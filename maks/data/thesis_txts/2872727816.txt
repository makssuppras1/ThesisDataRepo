Automated Construction of Modular Building 
Components through Fabrication Information Modeling 
and 3D Printing 
 
Casper A. Jensen 
 
Keywords:  
Additive Manufacturing (AM), Learning Factory for Construction (LFC), Fabrication Information 
Modeling (FIM), Robotic Assembly, Modular Construction, Automation in Construction, Digital 
Workflow 
Abstract 
The construction industry's digital transformation is impeded by fragmentation between design, 
production, and assembly. To overcome this, the industry is beginning to develop systems that use 
standardized data pipelines to connect digital designs with the physical world. This thesis addresses this 
challenge within a 1:50 scale Learning Factory for Construction (LFC) by pursuing two interconnected 
objectives: first, developing a tangible Lean construction game featuring high-fidelity modules to 
enhance hands-on learning, utilizing Additive Manufacturing (AM); and second, integrating and 
validating Fabrication Information Modeling (FIM)-driven workflow for the fabrication and automated 
robotic assembly of such modules. This thesis demonstrates a robust and efficient robotic assembly 
pipeline capable of assembly, disassembly, and reassembly with rapid recovery from simulated hazards. 
However, the findings also identify the AM stage as a significant bottleneck to the interoperability of 
the entire system, due to lengthy fabrication (~63 hours per module set) and inherent material 
inefficiencies from needed support structures and purging between tool changes. The primary 
contributions of this work are a digital, FIM-driven workflow connecting AM to construction, utilizing 
BIM and IFC data for both fabrication and assembly; a set of performance metrics for these processes; 
and the development of a scalable pedagogical toolkit for teaching modular design and automation in 
an LFC. Ultimately, the findings of this thesis highlight a promise of an integrated digital construction 
pipeline but underscore the critical need for improvements in fabrication speed and process robustness 
to enable an interoperable workflow and scalable deployment.

1 Introduction 
The construction industry is navigating through a critical transformation, shifting from traditional, 
labor-intensive practices toward a more digital future framed within the context of Construction 4.0. 
Key technologies such as Building Information Modeling (BIM), robotics, and Additive Manufacturing 
(AM) have demonstrated immense potential to enhance precision, reduce waste, and accelerate project 
timelines [1]. Despite these promises, construction has been slower to adopt these technologies 
compared to sectors like logistics [2]. The primary barrier to widespread adoption is not the potential 
of any single technology, but the challenge of fragmentation. The lack of standardized, integrated 
workflows connecting digital design directly to physical fabrication and assembly remains a significant 
hurdle, hindering the industry's ability to capitalize on these innovations. 
Learning Factories for Construction (LFCs) have emerged as key environments for tackling the 
challenge of integrating these complex digital workflows. By providing a controlled setting for hands-
on, experiential learning, LFCs allow researchers and professionals to test, validate, and refine the 
socio-technical processes required for modern construction [3]. 
While many LFCs and Lean construction simulation games provide valuable training in process 
optimization, those involving physical assembly often use simplified components designed for a single 
assembly method. Although such simplification is sufficient for demonstrating automated robotic tasks 
that rely only on basic bounding box data, it hinders the pedagogical potential of the modules 
themselves. It prevents learners from engaging with the more fundamental principles of building design, 
such as spatial layout, systems integration, and construction sequencing, which are inherently absent 
when using simplified blocks to represent building components. 
This thesis confronts these challenges directly, building upon the foundational work of Teizer et al. 
[4], to advance LFC workflows. Whereas prior research demonstrated robotic assembly with basic 
geometric modules made of LEGO, this project increases the complexity in two ways. First, it leverages 
AM to fabricate high-fidelity modules that represent entire room units, designing them to be suitable 
not only for robotic assembly but also for effective, hands-on learning. Second, it advances the data 
pipeline by replacing pre-programmed routines with a more standardized data-driven methodology 
utilizing Fabrication Information Modeling (FIM). This thesis, therefore, documents the initial 
development and evaluation of a design-to-assembly pipeline, assessing its resource consumption and 
potential to enhance the LFC.

1.1 Research questions 
To guide this investigation and systematically address its primary objectives, the following research 
questions have been formulated. 
RQ 1: To what extent can an integrated workflow, leveraging a Fabrication Information Model 
(FIM) and 3D printing, support the manufacturing and automated assembly of modular building 
components within a Learning Factory for Construction (LFC) context, and what are its implications 
for efficiency, sustainability, and pedagogical value? 
RQ 2: How can a standardized data pipeline, from FIM (using IFC from BIM) to robotic control be 
designed and implemented to manage the fabrication and assembly of complex, multi-part modular 
building units? 
RQ 3: What are the quantifiable performance metrics of this automated workflow, and what are the 
primary challenges affecting its robustness and scalability? 
RQ 4: In what way can the resulting physical scale model (1:50) and its underlying processes 
function as an effective pedagogical tool within an LFC to teach the principles of modular design, 
systems integration, and automated construction? 
1.2 Contributions 
This thesis makes the following primary contributions: 
C 1: The development of a modular Lean construction game at a 1:50 scale, serving as a detailed 
proof-of-concept for an educational Learning Factory for Construction (LFC) environment to 
provide a tangible and tactile educational experience for students new to the concept of prefabricated 
construction. 
C 2: The development, demonstration, and quantitative evaluation of a standardized, digital, data-
driven workflow for fabrication and automated modular construction, comprising:  
(a) The successful fabrication of complex, high-fidelity modules via Additive 
Manufacturing. 
(b) The successful validation of a FIM-driven automated assembly sequence. 
C 3: A systematic analysis of waste-mitigation strategies for multi-material Fused Deposition 
Modeling (FDM) 3D printing, resulting in a quantifiable framework for optimizing material 
efficiency.

Literature review 
2.1 Lean construction simulation games 
Lean construction simulation games are widely used in construction education to immerse students 
in realistic scenarios where Lean principles are applied in practice. Rather than learning purely through 
lectures, students enact processes or solve puzzles that embody Lean ideas, which make abstract 
concepts concrete. Such games are designed with clear pedagogical goals: they typically require teams 
of learners to coordinate tasks, identify and eliminate waste, and balance flow and value, effectively 
“learning by doing.” For example, Herrera et al. combine a traditional lecture with three simple Lean 
games called (“Broken Square”), (“Make-a-Card”), and (“Work-Sampling”) and find that the games 
significantly improve students’ grasp of Lean construction principles. Overall, simulation games allow 
participants to see and experience Lean concepts in action, making the learning process more engaging 
and developing skills like communication, collaboration, and systems-thinking [5]. 
Illustrative examples of Lean construction games highlight these benefits and the integration of new 
technologies. Villego® is a hands-on Lego-based simulation of the Last Planner System (LPS). In a 
recent study, students worked in teams to build a small Lego house first using traditional scheduling 
and then using LPS; this exercise was enriched with BIM models, augmented-reality (HoloLens), and 
a virtual-reality (Oculus) view. The technology enhancements provided real-time feedback on progress: 
for instance, a live webcam tracked construction progress and data collectors recorded idle time and 
material waste. The result was a measurable Lean improvement, overall build time was shorter, idle 
time on-site fell, and rework decreased. Learners reported that the AR interface was intuitive for 
understanding sequence and layout, whereas full immersion in VR was more challenging but helped 
improve spatial orientation [6]. 
A fully digital counterpart is the multiplayer VR serious game by Jacobsen et al. [7]. In this game, 
each player assumes a trade role (carpenter, plumber, electrician) and works together to assemble tasks 
in a virtual job site. Two versions of the scene are played: a “messy” version lacking Lean organization 
and a “Lean” version with a pre-defined sequence of work and clear workstations. All game objects 
automatically log interactions, enabling detailed run-time data analysis. Jacobsen’s team found that the 
Lean version was completed in roughly half the time of the messy version, demonstrating the flow 
improvements Lean methods bring. The real-time data gave immediate feedback on principles like 
workflow balance and takt time. The authors note that this VR approach felt more realistic than a board-
game-style simulation and allowed participants to “eliminate waste, allow collaboration, and optimize 
quality” under remote conditions, although the set-up does require investment in headsets and capable 
hardware. 
More complex physical simulations focus on holistic process improvement. A great example is the 
"Lean School" at the University of Valladolid, where participants engage with a simulation that begins 
with an inefficient, unbalanced production line. During several iterative rounds of production, the 
participants utilize Value Stream Mapping (VSM) and spaghetti diagrams to identify waste; physically 
reconfigure the layout; balance workloads; and standardize work. This resulted in a measured reduction 
in both factory space and lead time [8]. This type of activity is ideal for practicing collaborative 
methodologies and allows participants to gain not only technical skills but also valuable managerial and 
soft skills, including logistics, cross-disciplinary communication, and collaborative problem solving [3, 
9]. 
Beyond specific games, recent literature stresses how such activities reinforce a spectrum of skills. 
Herrera et al. show that different game types target different Lean skills, communication and 
transparency (“Broken Square”), pull thinking (“Make-a-Card”), and process flexibility (“Work-
Sampling”) and that combining lecture plus game significantly deepens understanding of all Lean 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
principles [5]. Similarly, the 5S-organized workstations and rapid takt in Jacobsen’s Lean scene make 
benefits immediately tangible [7]. Technology integration is now central to modern Lean games. 
Researchers layer BIM, VR/AR or IoT sensors onto classic simulations to visualize sequences, 
automate time capture, and provide real-time feedback [6, 7]. Teizer & Chronopoulos include such 
games with full Learning-Factory-for-Construction environments that extend the same ideas to include 
robotics and cloud-connected progress-tracking loops [3]. Their morphology shows how future 
educational spaces can close the feedback loop between Lean planning, production tracking and data-
driven improvement. 
Despite their promise, Lean construction games come with challenges. Physical games need space, 
materials and trained facilitators, while bespoke VR games require costly hardware and technical 
support. Moreover, if a game’s complexity exceeds students’ practical experience, learning can stall, 
underscoring Herrera et al.’s recommendation to tailor each game to the cohort’s background [5]. Even 
advanced simulations cannot capture every nuance of a live site, and participant numbers are still limited 
by hardware availability. Consequently, educators must balance realism, cost and scalability when 
adopting Lean construction games [5-7]. Taken together, these classroom-scale games offer a rapid, 
low-cost gateway to experiential Lean learning. The next level is to embed the same principles in large-
scale, cyber-physical testbeds — Learning Factories for Construction (LFCs) — that extend the concept 
across entire production chains. 
2.2 Learning Factories in Construction (LFC) 
Where simulation games are discrete pedagogical events, Learning Factories (LFs) are persistent, 
reconfigurable, and cyber-physical environments designed to blend theory with practice at a near-
industrial scale [10]. Building on the game-based concepts discussed in Section 2.1, the construction 
sector is now developing Learning Factories for Construction (LFCs) to accelerate innovation and 
address the industry’s productivity and skills gaps [11]. LFCs serve not only as spaces for advanced 
training but also as crucial testbeds for research, allowing for the iterative prototyping, testing, and 
verification of emerging technologies in a controlled setting. 
The research function of LFCs allows for the evaluation of Industry 4.0 technologies before field 
deployment. For example, Teizer et al. [4] investigated robotic arms within a BIM-driven workflow for 
modular assembly, demonstrating a repeatable assembly workflow under controlled conditions. 
Similarly, Teizer and Chronopoulos [3] explored the application of Automated Guided Vehicles 
(AGVs) to streamline material handling, identifying both improvements and areas requiring further 
development in interoperability and autonomy. The LFC concept also extends beyond production into 
building performance management; in one study, a university campus with over two hundred IoT 
sensors acted as an LFC to test and potentially predict building behavior. This "living lab" demonstrated 
that an IoT-managed HVAC system could achieve energy consumption savings of 10-15% and was 
used to develop AI-driven predictive models for CO2 and temperature levels [12].  The value 
proposition of LFCs can be effectively summarized through adapted dimensions derived from the CIRP 
Learning Factory morphology framework [10], as showcased in Table 1. 
The effectiveness of LFCs lies in their integration of proven pedagogical practices at scale. Project-
Based Learning (PBL), a core principle of the LFC teaching approach, has proven to develop essential 
professional skills by having students solve complex real-world engineering problems that lack a single 
correct solution [13]. Within the LFC, advanced technologies in the facility’s "technology stack" 
amplify this learning. BIM-enabled platforms allow student teams to collaborate in a manner that 
mimics Integrated Project Delivery [14], [15]. while VR can enhance engagement in safety training or 
improve understanding of complex operations within a risk-free, controllable and modifiable 
environment [16, 17]. This integration of site-based experiential learning helps bridge theory and

Although there are many potential benefits, there are also challenges to be solved, such as achieving 
realism when scaling for larger cohort groups, formulating timely and measurable education outcomes, 
and making sustainability an intrinsic part of the curriculum [19]. A systematic review of VR training 
noted there was an ongoing need for more studies measuring long-term retention of knowledge [20]. 
Additionally, protecting the value of the capital-intensive LFC over time is another challenge. Focus 
should be placed on designing for changeability, modularity, and interoperability so the facility can 
embrace new technologies and/or changes in learning objectives without excessive cost [9]. LFCs 
provide an important conduit between innovation and practice, allowing manufacturers and industry 
partners to create and grow a solution to meet the unknown levels of productivity, workforce and 
digitization change required by the modern construction industry.

2.3 Robotics in construction 
Over the past several years the construction sector's search for higher productivity, better safety, and 
environmentally sustainable practices have led to a substantial interest in robotics and automation. 
Multiple robotic solutions, involving various robotic systems such as manipulative robotic arms, 
Autonomous Guided Vehicles (AGVs), Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), and more, have been 
researched and introduced to some extent on construction sites. These technologies have tremendous 
potential, but at this time it is unclear how and to what degree these technologies will be adopted and 
integrated within the near future of the industry. Some of the ongoing issues related to technologies 
such as interoperability, adaptability, and reliability are ongoing challenges, suggesting there will be a 
continual requirement for formal experimentation and formal case studies before significant 
consideration of making it widely available. 
2.3.1 
Robotic arms and manipulators 
Robotic manipulators, such as those manufactured by KUKA or Universal Robots, have gained 
significant role in automating labor-intensive construction tasks like bricklaying, assembly, and 
prefabrication. The DFAB HOUSE project at ETH Zurich has served as a great building-scale 
demonstrator, showcasing the capabilities of digital fabrication to enhance precision, reduce labor 
demand, and significantly cut material waste compared to traditional methods. However, the project 
also underscored a primary challenge: effectively aligning the perfect digital project model with the 
inherent inaccuracies of real-world jobsite conditions, a gap that still required substantial manual 
intervention during assembly [24].  
A central challenge for construction robotics is transitioning from the controlled environment of a 
factory to the unstructured and chaotic worksite. Research by Giftthaler et al., explored the "In Situ 
Fabricator," a mobile robotic system, addressed this by performing complex digital fabrication tasks at 
a 1:1 scale directly on-site, including building brick walls and reinforcing concrete structures. This work 
highlighted the immense potential for mobile robots to increase accuracy and reduce manual labor for 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
construction [25]. The challenge of mobility was also addressed by the mobile manipulator developed 
by Štibinger et al., which used a wheeled base and a 7-DOF arm to autonomously locate, grasp, and 
place construction blocks in an outdoor setting. This system was designed to be self-contained and not 
reliant on external positioning systems, making it robust to varying light conditions and suitable for 
both indoor and outdoor use [26].  
Achieving precision in such dynamic environments heavily relies on advanced perception systems. 
Many systems create high-accuracy local reference frames to bridge the gap between the digital model 
and the physical site. One effective method is using vision-based metrology with fiducial markers (e.g., 
AprilTags or ArUco-markers) placed strategically on-site. This allows a robot to use a single camera to 
rapidly determine its precise position and orientation relative to the building components and the overall 
structure, enabling autonomous assembly with sub-centimeter accuracy [27]. The system developed by 
Štibinger et al. also demonstrated robustness through a combination of LiDAR for coarse localization 
and vision for fine alignment [26].  
Beyond assembling uniform blocks, a significant hurdle is the manipulation of large, heavy, and 
irregularly shaped materials. Mascaro et al. demonstrated a solution with HEAP, a robotic walking 
excavator capable of autonomously handling boulders weighing several hundred kilograms. Instead of 
relying on predefined shapes, the system uses a LiDAR-based mapping and object segmentation 
pipeline to identify and plan collision-free grasps for these unique, raw materials found on-site. This 
points toward a future where robots can utilize locally sourced, unprocessed materials, increasing 
sustainability and flexibility [28].  
The application of robotic manipulators also extends to specialized, heavy-duty construction tasks. 
For high-rise steel construction, automated robotic crane systems have been proposed, featuring 
manipulators with specialized end-effectors for aligning H-beams and performing automated bolting 
operations [29]. The control of these more advanced large-scale machinery presents unique challenges 
not found in smaller electric manipulators, including nonlinearities system variables like pressure loss, 
and time delay, which complicate an otherwise sequential operation. Testbeds using hydraulic dual-arm 
systems have been developed to quantify these dynamics and create intelligent support systems for 
operators [30]. A different approach involves fitting existing machinery for remote operations in 
hazardous environments. Sasaki et al. developed a lightweight, portable pneumatic robot arm system 
designed to be easily installed in the cab of any conventional construction machine, allowing an operator 
to control it safely from a distance [31].  
The evolution of robotic arms in construction, as demonstrated by these cases, clearly shows a 
trajectory from pre-programmed automation towards perception-driven autonomy. While early projects 
like the one showcased by Teizer et al. succeeded in highly controlled settings with predefined motions 
[4], research from projects like the In Situ Fabricator and HEAP consistently flags the need for systems 
that can adapt to the variability of a real construction site [25, 28]. The main takeaway from these studies 
is that robust performance hinges not on the manipulator alone, but on the integration of advanced, 
multi-sensor perception systems (LiDAR, 3D vision), real-time feedback, and adaptive control 
algorithms that enable robots to understand, adjust to, and act within their ever-changing environments 
[26-28]. A summary comparison of the robotic arm and manipulator systems discussed is presented in

2.3.2 
Autonomous Guided Vehicles (AGVs) 
AGVs have been utilized for some time, primarily for moving material, with the intention to improve 
logistics, reduce inefficiencies, and improve safety in the work environment. In their research, Bock 
and Linner [32] acknowledged several implementations of AGVs on a construction site and highlighted 
their advantages, such as reduced delays for repetitive transportation tasks and increased safety on site. 
A primary challenge hindering the broader use of AGVs has been achieving reliable navigation in 
dynamic and unstructured construction environments. Traditional AGVs relied on fixed physical paths, 
such as buried inductive wires, painted lines or magnetic tape, which are reliable but inflexible [33, 34]. 
Modern systems have shifted towards "free-ranging" AGVs that use techniques like laser-based 
triangulation or, more recently, Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) to navigate based on 
the natural features of the environment, though these can struggle with the dynamic nature of 
construction sites [33].  
In a Learning Factory study, Teizer and Chronopoulos investigated AGVs for moving prefabricated 
elements in a smaller-scale setting that simulated the logistics between an off-site factory and an on-
site assembly area. The setup used a cloud-based information model and IoT technology to track 
progress and control the flow of materials. While the results were promising within this controlled 
environment, the researchers remained cautious about scalability in real-world scenarios [3].  
As the industry moves from single-robot solutions to multi-robot systems, the focus has shifted to 
the development of sophisticated Fleet Management Systems (FMS). A notable real-world example is 
the FMS developed by Obayashi Corporation for automating material transport in high-rise building 
construction. This system uses a centralized control server to manage a fleet of AGVs in coordination 
with automated elevators and cranes, with reliable communication being the main challenge addressed 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
through the use of 4G/LTE networks. A key complexity in this high-rise context, not typically found in 
manufacturing, is the challenge of vertical transportation, which requires the FMS to schedule and 
interface with shared resources like automated elevators to move materials between floors [35]. Such 
systems often rely on centralized, cloud-based architecture for real-time tracking of materials and 
overall system coordination, providing the data management and scalability needed for complex 
projects like prefabrication transport [36]. 
Efficiently coordinating a fleet requires advanced scheduling algorithms to solve the complex, NP-
hard problem of allocating multiple tasks to multiple AGVs. Research has explored classic optimization 
techniques, such as improved ant colony algorithms, to minimize the total time required to complete all 
tasks (i.e., the makespan) while managing path conflicts and time constraints [37]. More recently, Deep 
Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has emerged as a powerful technique for real-time scheduling in 
dynamic environments. A primary advantage of the DRL approach is its ability to handle the inherent 
uncertainty of a construction site, allowing the system to make robust real-time decisions even when 
faced with unexpected traffic congestion or the arrival of new, high-priority tasks [38]. Beyond 
scheduling, the specific challenge of path planning in large and cluttered construction sites has been 
addressed with hierarchical planning algorithms. One such approach uses a two-level method where a 
global 'coarse planner' first identifies an optimal, collision-free route across the site, which is then 
refined by a local 'precise planner' that generates a stable trajectory considering the vehicle's current 
situation and limits, such as maximum acceleration and jerk tolerances, ensuring stability for heavy-
load transport [39]. 
Overall, the evolution of AGVs in construction is moving from isolated vehicles to fully integrated 
logistics systems. Communication and control architecture is a key area of development, with research 
exploring both centralized cloud-based FMS [36] and decentralized approaches. The latter leverages 
technologies like Mobile Edge Computing (MEC) to overcome the communication delays (latency) 
inherent in pure cloud systems [40, 41]. By processing data closer to the robots, MEC enables the high-
speed, low-latency communication required for real-time negotiation and collaborative decision-
making among multiple AGVs [41]. The ultimate goal is the creation of a data-driven, interconnected 
ecosystem where AGVs, machinery, and digital models operate in seamless coordination, often 
leveraging the Internet of Things (IoT) for system-wide optimization [42, 43]. 
2.3.3 
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) 
Most of the ongoing work looking to adopt unmanned aerial vehicles for construction tasks is 
primarily focused on site management, inspection and safety. Howard et. al. [44] propose that UAVs 
provide the opportunity to relay high-fidelity visual information on work tasks and site conditions 
allowing for better identification of hazards and safety interventions to improve overall safety. Full 
integration is likely still a challenge due to regulatory issues, privacy/publicity concerns, limited range 
and complications from irregular weather. 
Recent literature reviews have associated the primary uses of UAVs in construction with four key 
areas: (a) Progress Monitoring, (b) Quality and Geometric Control, (c) Safety Management, and (d) Site 
and Logistical Management [45]. The workflow for most of these applications is consistent and follows 
the same process, which includes data acquisition, 3D reconstruction, and analysis [46]. The most 
common UAV use-case is for project progress monitoring, which is achieved through photogrammetry. 
For photogrammetry, UAVs execute an automated flight path to collect overlapping photographs of the 
construction site - typically hundreds or thousands. The images are stitched together to form a dense 
3D point cloud, or 3D textured mesh that shows the project in the "as-built" state [46]. The use of drones 
and Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAV) has been increased in recent years for surveying, facility 
management and other relevant fields. However, more recently, the technological progress in the design 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
and navigation of low-weight and autonomous drones and UAVs have resulted in their more practical 
and cost-effective operation in the fields of architectural engineering and construction management and 
monitoring. This research presents a framework for the development of a fully automated smart 
construction monitoring and reporting system based on real-time data obtained from drones and UAVs. 
The data in terms of drone images from multiple locations and point clouds (from 3D scanning of 
construction site) can be used to construct a 3D model using the photogrammetry techniques. This so-
called \"drone model” can be compared to BIM model at various construction stages to monitor the 
construction progress. Beside construction scheduling and costing, this comparison can be expanded to 
include real-time recording, reporting, billing, verification and planning. Using the example of a case 
study construction project, the effective use of drone data is demonstrated in terms of smart construction 
monitoring and comparisons between drone model and BIM model. It is shown that this fully automated 
system can significantly reduce the effort required in traditional construction monitoring and reporting 
procedures. The system not only provides convenient and smart ways of site supervision and 
management but also results in better operations, planning and effective on-site adjustments [47]. This 
as-built model can then be automatically compared against the "as-designed" Building Information 
Model (BIM) to evaluate physical progress and the identification of any deviations [48]. While we can 
create a 3D mesh, the quality of this data is not guaranteed. For instance, Hamledari et al. [49] observed 
that motion blur on the images can limit the 3D reconstruction quality and affect the reliability of as 
built 4D BIMs to track schedules and costs, respectively. 
For inspection and quality control, UAVs are particularly effective for accessing hard-to-reach 
locations [50]. Beyond visual confirmation, advanced computer vision and deep learning models, often 
based on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), are being used to automatically analyze the collected 
imagery for defect detection. For example, deep learning algorithms can process orthomosaic maps of 
bridge decks created from UAV images to automatically detect, segment, and map cracks, allowing for 
more efficient structural health monitoring [51]. Similarly, for safety management, Gheisari and 
Esmaeili [52] observed that UAVs improve the accuracy and speed of data collection for everything 
from proactive site inspections to real-time situational response, although they also noted that 
challenges with payload limitations and flight time remain. For site and logistical management, novel 
hybrid systems are being developed. One such system is a UAV-RFID platform, where a drone 
equipped with an RFID reader can autonomously fly over a site to locate and inventory tagged materials 
and equipment, achieving a localization accuracy of 94% in experimental settings [53].  
Ultimately, the greatest value of UAVs is unlocked when they are incorporated into a larger 
Construction 4.0 digital ecosystem [54]. The data collected by UAVs are not to be seen as isolated 
reports but rather should be considered as vital inputs to other advanced technologies. For example, 
UAV generated models may inform immersive AR and VR experiences for remote site walkthroughs, 
or their data can be combined with information from on-site Internet of Things (IoT) sensors to create 
a comprehensive digital twin of the construction site. The possibilities are many and pave a way for a 
more holistic and data-driven approach to project management [54]. 
 
2.3.4 
Challenges and future directions 
The research highlighted across manipulators, AGVs, and UAVs indicates considerable momentum 
for construction robotics but also highlights several problems that persist and are interrelated, that will 
need to be solved to allow for widespread effective adoption. The single biggest challenge is the 
transition from controlled predictable laboratory settings to the unstructured and dynamic world of an 
active construction site. Even though many robotic systems were shown to work effectively in specific 
aspects, in general their performance is heavily reliant on a controlled environment as well as a 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
predetermined scope of work that cannot be applied to a real-world project. Mobile manipulators must 
contend with uneven terrain and irregular materials [28], AGVs will need to navigate unplanned 
obstacles that will inevitably be present from other construction workers and materials [34], and UAVs 
will need to deal with environmental stressors like poor weather while also accounting for data quality 
challenges such as motion-blur [48].  
In order to move forward, the future direction should be to transition from strict, pre-programmed 
automation to flexible, perception-driven autonomy. The evidence is clear that it is no longer enough 
to program a robot to follow a path. The next generation of construction robots will be more based on 
data and perception, using sensor suites such as LiDAR and 3D scanners, and algorithms that can 
perceive, reason and understand their environment in real time. These will include deep learning 
algorithms for AI-based grasp planning for manipulators [28]; deep reinforcement learning for AGV 
scheduling in stochastic environments [38]; and computer vision algorithms that automatically identify 
defects from UAV imagery [51]. 
Furthermore, the full potential of construction robotics will not be realized by deploying isolated 
solutions, but by creating an interconnected ecosystem of systems. This points to a second major 
challenge: interoperability. To enable true site automation, it is crucial for manipulators, autonomous 
guided vehicles (AGVs), and unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) to communicate and collaborate easily 
with each other and a central digital backbone. As a good example, Fleet Management Systems (FMS) 
are being developed to help coordinate operations of AGVs with automated cranes and elevators [35], 
at the same time integrating data from UAVs and Internet of Things (IoT) sensors and Building 
Information Modeling (BIM) to form a comprehensive Digital Twin of the project [54]. The full 
realization of this vision will require the establishment and use of standards for communication and 
open data platforms, so that data can flow with ease between different hardware platforms and software 
applications.  
This technological evolution also requires a new "socio-technical" channel of development. As 
robots become more commonplace in undertaking daily work activities and processes, robust safety 
procedures, updating regulatory and liability frameworks, and new qualification standards become 
essential. The human workforce will require new training programs for operating, supervising, and 
collaborating with these increasingly autonomous systems. Therefore, realizing the promise of robotics 
in construction hinges on a holistic approach that simultaneously advances technological capabilities in 
adaptability and interoperability while co-developing the regulatory, safety, and workforce structures 
needed to support them. 
2.4 Additive Manufacturing (AM) in construction 
The field of Additive Manufacturing (AM) (or 3D printing) is quickly becoming a transformative 
technology for the construction sector. AM can deposit materials layer by layer to print in 3D based on 
digital models, allowing for increased productivity, reduced waste, and expanded design possibilities. 
In addition, AM can be used to remove existing structures, in the construction process, while at the 
same time, the range of printable materials keeps growing, everything from cement-based slurries and 
thermoplastics to advanced composites, metals, and even biologically sourced materials. However, 
despite its promise, the current application of the technology is met with a variety of technical, material, 
and practical challenges. The upcoming sections will focus on descriptions of common AM techniques, 
the materials that are currently being implemented into the construction process, and a summary of 
recent research, while also addressing both the opportunities and challenges in an evolving field. 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
2.4.1 
Additive Manufacturing techniques in construction 
There are a variety of AM techniques available, and the choice of technique will depend on scale, 
materials and specific application; each of these methods is implemented via one of two primary robotic 
form factors, either large scale gantries, which can move an extruder over a fixed area, or multi-axis 
industrial robotic arms, which offer somewhat more geometric flexibility [55]. The primary techniques 
include: 
Extrusion-based 3D Concrete Printing (E3DCP): The most common method of large-scale 
construction. In E3DCP, a traditional robotic or gantry-based 'extruder' is used to dispense a 
cementitious material, layer-by-layer, in accordance with a digital path [56]. A variant of E3DCP, called 
Contour Crafting, uses a similar extrusion methodology; however, it also incorporates automated 
trowels to smooth each layer deposited, resulting in a better surface finish and dimensional accuracy 
[57]. E3DCP, in principle, has significant potential to build complex geometries while saving time on 
construction formwork and human labor; however, there are a number of substantial challenges to 
overcome and refine. The ability to produce a successful print depends on the material properties, in 
terms of ability to deliver to the nozzle (pumpability), ability to be deposited cleanly (extrudability) and 
the capacity for a fresh deposited layer to suspend additional layers without collapse (buildability) [58]. 
For example, it is important to note the buildability of the material is very time dependent, and potential 
failure modes include: separation of layers, slumping and material collapse; which can lead to damage 
to the build, or greater management issues further down in construction workflow (e.g. needing to 
rectify damaged components). E3DCP, like most layered additive methods, results in an end product 
with anisotropic structural properties, meaning the material is much weaker between the layers of the 
print from the actual bead direction, a concern for structural applications [58]. 
Shotcrete 3D Printing (SC3DP): shotcrete 3D printing is also classified as an injection method of 
AM [55], whereby a robotic manipulator uses pneumatic methods to spray concrete onto a surface. 
SC3DP has considerable control over the thickness of concrete application layer and geometric shape, 
however, the robot needs to be calibrated precisely in regard to spray angles, spray pressures and 
distances to ensure the spray conformed correctly and produce strong interlayer bonding. 
Wire and Arc Additive Manufacturing (WAAM): WAAM can be classified as a Directed Energy 
Deposition (DED) method and is typically used for nodal printing. Designed specifically for metallic 
materials like steel, aluminum, etc. WAAM uses a welding tool that is generally mounted on a robotic 
arm. The welding tool uses wire feedstock and utilizes welding methods to melt the wire feedstock, 
depositing it in layers as directed. WAAM has considerable potential for producing structurally 
optimized and complex metallic components while vastly minimizing material wastage in additive 
construction. Constraints of WAAM, however, include managing thermal stresses that can cause 
material distortion, as well enabling uniform metallurgical properties throughout the 3D printed object 
require perfect control through the WAAM process. Similar to WAAM, other forms of AM for metals 
exist, including the use of Powder Bed Fusion (PBF) methods such as Selective Laser Melting (SLM). 
WAAM is ideal for large structural components and PBF processes are suited towards much smaller, 
and generally geometrically complex components, such as custom structural nodes, with high accuracy 
compared with WAAM [55].  
Large-Format Polymer and Composite Extrusion: In addition to concrete and metal, extrusion is 
also utilized for large-format polymer printing, commonly referred to as Big Area Additive 
Manufacturing (BAAM). BAAM utilizes a gantry or robotic system to extrude thermoplastic polymers, 
possibly reinforced with short carbon or glass fibers, into relatively large, lightweight components [59, 
60]. BAAM can produce non-structural or semi-structural items with a high geometrical complexity, 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
including building façade panels, interior features, or integrated formwork for concrete pouring [59, 
61]. 
Particle-bed Printing (Binder Jetting): This process selectively binds layers of powdered material 
(such as a sand-cement mixture or polymers) using a liquid binder sprayed from a print head. While 
this technique allows for high levels of detail and geometric complexity, it has mostly been used for 
smaller-scale prefabricated components or architectural models [55]. However, large-scale systems like 
the D-Shape process have been developed to print large building elements from sand [57].  
Multi-Material and Multi-Color Additive Manufacturing: An emerging frontier in construction 
AM is the ability to print with multiple materials or colors within a single process [62]. This is often 
achieved through advanced printheads equipped with multiple nozzles to deposit different materials 
side-by-side or through co-axial extrusion, where one material is extruded around another in a core-
sheath structure [62]. This advanced technique could allow for the fabrication of components with 
integrated functionalities, for example, combining a dense, structural material with a porous, insulating 
material in one continuous print [61]. This opens the door to creating Functionally Graded Materials 
(FGMs), where properties change gradually across a component, or to producing visually complex 
parts, such as the multi-color air-diffusion systems developed by Schork et al., offering a new level of 
performance and aesthetic optimization [62, 63]. 
2.4.2 
Materials used in Additive Manufacturing 
Planning, selection and preparation of materials is essential for the success of additive manufacturing 
(AM) in construction, considering that the fabrication process places unique demands on the properties 
of the material that is printed with. Common materials include: 
Concrete and cement-based composites: Concrete remains the most researched material for 
construction AM due to its versatility and low cost [64]. Extrusion-based 3D concrete printing (E3DCP) 
requires highly customized mixes optimized for a balance between competing properties: the mix must 
be fluid enough for pumpability but cohesive enough for extrudability and possess enough yield stress 
for buildability, where fresh layers can support the weight of subsequent ones without collapsing [58]. 
This balance is often achieved through a carefully controlled water-to-cement ratio and a suite of 
chemical admixtures, including superplasticizers to enhance flow, viscosity modifying agents to 
improve shape stability, and accelerators or retarders to precisely manage setting time [58]. Recent 
research has focused on integrating industrial by-products like fly ash and silica fume to reduce the 
carbon footprint of concrete mixes [64]. However, these binders can alter the fresh-state properties, 
requiring careful optimization to maintain printability while achieving desired structural performance, 
which is often anisotropic (weaker between layers) due to the nature of the layered process [58].  
Metals and metal alloys: Metal AM processes, primarily Wire and Arc Additive Manufacturing 
(WAAM), typically use steel, aluminum, and titanium alloys to create structurally efficient components 
with complex geometries [55, 65]. While offering high strength-to-weight ratios, these processes face 
significant challenges in managing the thermal cycles. The intense heat from welding can cause thermal 
distortion and generate residual stress within the material, often necessitating post-processing like 
machining or heat treatment to ensure dimensional accuracy and mechanical integrity. Furthermore, the 
rapid heating and cooling rates inherent to the process result in unique microstructures and potentially 
anisotropic grain structures, which directly influence the final mechanical properties of the component 
[65].  
Polymers and composites: Polymers are widely used in construction AM for non-structural 
applications like complex formwork, architectural screens, and high-performance façade panels [61]. 
Large-format techniques like Big Area Additive Manufacturing (BAAM) often use thermoplastics such 
as Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene (ABS), which are reinforced with short carbon or glass fibers to 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
increase strength and stiffness [59, 60]. The development of bio-based composites, using polymers like 
PLA with wood-fiber fillers, offers a more sustainable alternative, though challenges with moisture 
sensitivity and mechanical strength remain [66]. Advanced applications include the use of translucent 
polymers like PETG to create multi-color, functionally complex components such as integrated air-
diffusion systems for building interiors [63].  
Clay and earthen materials: Driven by an interest in sustainability and local sourcing, AM with 
natural clay and ceramics is a growing field. The process requires careful control of material rheology, 
moisture content, and shrinkage behavior to successfully print objects [67]. While aesthetically 
versatile, the resulting components typically have low mechanical strength, limiting their use in load-
bearing applications unless they undergo energy-intensive post-processing such as firing in a kiln.  
Multi-material systems: An emerging frontier is the area of Multi-Material Additive 
Manufacturing (MMAM), where it is possible to utilize multiple materials in a single print. This may 
be accomplished with multi-nozzle printheads, or by co-axial extrusion to fabricate integrated 
components, such as printing structural concrete alongside lightweight insulating foam, or embedding 
strands of steel reinforcement directly inside a concrete element while it is being fabricated. MMAM 
facilitates the utilization of Functionally Graded Materials (FGMs), where properties change across a 
component, offering a new level of performance and optimization that is impossible with traditional 
methods [62]. 
2.4.3 
Challenges and future directions 
Additive Manufacturing (AM) is poised to significantly impact the construction industry, moving 
beyond novel prototypes to become a viable method for producing building components and systems. 
However, for AM to transition from experimental use to widespread adoption, several persistent and 
interconnected challenges must be addressed. 
To begin, the primary challenge lies at the intersection between material science and structural 
performance. The printability of materials including concrete is a balancing act of fresh-state materials' 
properties such as pumpable, extrudable, and buildable conditions [58]. The main challenge is 
maintaining robust mechanical performance while in the hardened state. Addressing this concern is an 
area of high research value. One significant consideration associated with the method of layer 
fabrication will always be related to the anisotropic properties of printed materials, where the interlayer 
bonds are weaker than the material within the layers.  
Anisotropy creates a significant issue for both concrete [68] and metals from WAAM, which create 
grain structure that is not ideal due to thermal cycles [69]. In addition, this issue is directly related to 
how to provide structural reinforcement during the AM process. While embedding steel and printing 
with steel during the printing process are under investigation [62], the ability to seamlessly incorporate 
reinforcement to meet structural engineering requirements while not disrupting the automated 
construction process remains a substantial challenge. 
Second, ensuring the quality, consistency, and accuracy of large-scale printed components is 
paramount. Most current AM systems operate in an open-loop fashion, where the machine simply 
executes a pre-programmed path. Deviations in material behavior or environmental conditions can lead 
to geometric inaccuracies or defects. The future direction is therefore a shift towards closed-loop 
process control. This would involve real-time monitoring and in-situ sensing with advanced data 
frameworks like Fabrication Information Modeling (FIM), which could enable the robotic system to 
identify errors and correct them as they happen [56]. In addition to verifying the fabrication progress, 
the post-fabrication quality of components must also be determined. For this to happen, considerable 
Non-Destructive Testing (NDT) techniques that are able to identify internal inconsistencies, like voids 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
or delamination that cannot be identified on the outside, must be used (like ultrasonic or radiographic 
scanning) [70]. 
Looking forward, the future of AM in construction lies not just in printing existing designs more 
efficiently, but in creating high-performance, multi-functional building systems. The development of 
Multi-Material Additive Manufacturing (MMAM) is key to this vision, enabling the printing of single 
components with integrated functions, such as a wall section that combines a structural core with an 
insulating layer [62]. This concept is evolving into 4D Printing, which is defined as the 3D printing of 
"smart materials" that are programmed to change their shape or function over time, the fourth 
dimension, in response to an external stimulus. These stimuli include heat, light, moisture, or pH, with 
the implication being that components could subsequently vary depending on the environmental 
conditions to which they respond. The idea of a stimulus-response is enabled through additive 
manufacturing by using advanced materials, such as shape-memory polymers (SMPs) which return to 
a predefined shape when heated, or composites with the ability to self-heal [71, 72].  There are a 
multitude of applications that this technology could have, such as building components that self-
assemble, adaptive building skins that respond to changing weather conditions or deployable emergency 
shelters [72]. Ultimately, AM is pushing the boundaries of what is possible, from creating these adaptive 
systems and functionally graded facades [61] to exploring In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) by 
printing structures with local materials like soil or even lunar regolith [73]. To realize this potential, 
future efforts must focus on holistically addressing the challenges of material development, process 
control, and regulatory standardization. 
2.5 Digital Twins and integrated data environments  
As construction technologies such as robotics and additive manufacturing have become 
commonplace, there is a need for a unified digital framework to manage and integrate them. 
Accordingly, Digital Twin (DT) emerged as the digital spine of the modern construction site and 
factory. 
2.5.1 
Defining the Digital Twin in construction 
A Digital Twin is much more than a digital 3D image, it is a dynamic virtual model of a physical 
asset, system, or process, where the virtual model is continuously updated using current data from its 
physical counterpart [74, 75]. More specifically, the attribute of a true Digital Twin is automated, bi-
directional information sharing: the physical assets share data with the virtual model, which provides 
commands or insights back to inform the physical assets [74]. While a Building Information Model 
(BIM) is often a foundational component or prerequisite, a BIM model by itself is not a Digital Twin. 
A BIM is a rich but static data repository, whereas a Digital Twin is a "living" model that evolves in 
real-time as the physical object changes [76, 77]. The goal is to create a high-fidelity digital replica that 
can be used for monitoring, simulation, analysis, and control throughout an asset's lifecycle. 
2.5.2 
The Digital Twin ecosystem 
The creation of an effective Digital Twin is dependent upon an ecosystem of integrated technologies 
to process the acquisition, management, and application of data [77]. 
Data Acquisition: The live connection to the physical world is made with the process of capturing 
data, including a range of sensor inputs and reality capture technologies. This includes, but is not limited 
to, the use of IoT sensors for environmental data, laser scanners and UAV photogrammetry for 
geometric data, and RFID or GPS trackers for location data [77]. 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
Data Management: The immense volume of data collected requires robust management systems. 
Cloud computing platforms provide the scalable storage and processing power needed, while specific 
data construction methods are required to structure and query the complex datasets generated by a 
construction project or manufacturing workshop [78]. 
Data Application: The true value of the Digital Twin is realized when the data is used to generate 
insights. This is done through Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) for predictive 
analytics, physics-based simulation to test "what-if" scenarios, and Augmented and Virtual Reality 
(AR/VR) for immersive visualization and interaction with the virtual model [77]. 
 
2.5.3 
Applications across the construction lifecycle 
Design Phase: DTs can facilitate advanced performance simulation, permitting designers to test a 
building's energy efficiency, structural integrity or occupant flows before construction begins [75]. 
Construction Phase: This is where DTs directly support automated manufacturing. They can be 
used for real-time progress monitoring, managing logistics by tracking materials and equipment, and 
controlling robotic systems [75, 79]. For example, a DT can be used to simulate a robotic workcell to 
ensure collision-free paths for robotic arms or manage the operations of an AGV fleet. In AM, a DT 
can simulate a print to predict defects and then monitor the process in real-time to ensure quality. 
Operation & Maintenance Phase: Once the construction phase is over, the DT continues to exist 
as a tool for facility management. It can be used for space management, monitoring energy use, and 
using predictive maintenance strategies through analyzing sensors data to help anticipate equipment 
failures before they occur [75]. 
 
2.5.4 
Challenges and future directions 
Although Digital Twins hold considerable promise for the construction industry, their large-scale 
implementation presents distinct challenges or barriers. These barriers are frequently discussed as a 
mixture of process barriers (lack of standardization and clear contractual frameworks), technology 
barriers (interoperability, data security and cost), and people barriers (lack of skilled labor and resistance 
to change) [80]. The anticipated future research objectives are to overcome these challenges by creating 
systems that are smarter and can work together more effectively. A focus amongst that work is the 
development of the "semantic" Digital Twin, which focuses on allowing the data represented within the 
twin to be machine-readable and therefore have unambiguous meaning, leading to easier 
implementation of more advanced reasoning and automation with AI [81]. The ambition is for a 
paradigm called Digital Twin Construction (DTC), whereby the Digital Twin is the primary, 
authoritative source of truth, managing and coordinating the entirety of the automated and human 
activities of a project from design to fabrication and assembly [82]. 
2.6 Societal, ethical, and sustainability considerations 
As the construction industry transitions into greater levels of automation, robotics, and additive 
manufacture, there are important societal, ethical, and sustainability implications that need to be 
addressed. Ultimately, it is important to recognize the broader level of digital transformation, looking 
not only at the technology itself but to be mindful of the societal, environmental, and governance [83] 
transition as well. This requires a proactive approach to managing the impacts on the workforce, 
developing robust ethical and regulatory frameworks, and ensuring that technological advancement 
aligns with long-term sustainability goals. 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
2.6.1 
Workforce displacement and employment Impacts 
The most significant societal issue associated with automation is workforce displacement [84]. For 
instance, a role that is labor intensive and repetitive is a prime candidate for automation [85]. However, 
looking at work experience is more complex than this. Studies suggest that automation works best when 
it focuses on tasks that workers experience negatively, such as labor and ergonomically intensive, 
unsafe, or high stress tasks and keeping the positive aspects of the job, such as social interaction, 
problem solving, and variation in tasks [86]. 
However, even with a human-centric approach, the introduction of new technologies like AGVs can 
create a disconnect between the goals of management and the concerns of the workforce. Some studies 
suggest that workers are mainly worried about whether the new technology systems are safe and usable, 
whereas management have focused on productivity or cost savings [87]. This shows that purely defining 
and implementing a technology agenda will not work in practice. Getting the workforce to embrace or 
accept a new working system requires effective management changes and should include a participatory 
process that engages and communicates with the workforce to integrate the technology and related 
processes into their work on how to effectively use the new technology and communicating with the 
technology in the workplace [87]. Certainly, some jobs will become obsolete and commissioned for 
automation, however the automation agenda will also create new jobs that require a higher level (or 
different) skill with the working technology (programming, data points, robot maintenance etc.), 
therefore require strategic responses to the impacts of these transitions on the workforce [1]. 
2.6.2 
Skills development and workforce upskilling 
Assessing the issues and challenges of workforce change to automation in the construction industry 
requires a proactive and structured approach to skills development. This involves both upskilling - 
enhancing the skills of the existing workforce to use new digital toolkit in their current roles - and 
reskilling - training the workforce in entirely new competencies suitable to the emerging job roles 
caused by the shift in technology [88]. The competencies required will not only be technical in nature, 
and although digital literacy in aspects such as BIM, data analytics, and robot operation will be vital. 
Research highlights that the skills valued by the workforce are soft skills such as collaborative working, 
cross-disciplinary work, complex problem solving, and the ability to adapt to constant state of change 
[89]. Considering the future workforce with rapidly changing roles due to an evolving workplace, and 
the requirement for a culture of lifelong learning [88].  
To provide this training, Learning Factories for Construction (LFCs) have sufficient evidence to 
show as a means of providing experiential learning, and allow participants to gain hands-on skills with 
new automated technologies workflows that require collaborative use of technology or collaborative 
smart construction [3, 4]. On the corporate level, some larger organizations have started to implement 
their strategies which include in-house training programs, appointing "BIM champions", or digital 
leaders to promote awareness and knowledge sharing [90]. Nevertheless, part of the challenge is the 
reluctance of seasoned employees to transition from the old ways of working to the new digital 
technologies [90]. Moreover, these corporate strategies will not be viable for the Small and Medium-
sized Enterprises, who make up a notable chunk the construction industry, as they often do not have the 
facilities or resources, to develop new training program specific to the entirely new methods of 
technology taking place in their organizations [91]. The same challenges exist for the operatives on site 
who need task-specific training to operate digital tools at the sharp end of construction. To address this, 
research points out the need for tailored BIM learning models designed specifically for site operatives 
with the aim of ensuring that the benefits of digitalization can be achieved during the tangible work of 
construction [92].  
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
All these points highlight the need for coordinated, industry-wide initiatives, often led by 
professional and governmental bodies, to ensure that upskilling opportunities are accessible to all 
stakeholders in the industry. Finally, offering training by itself is not enough; it must be evaluated. A 
formal evaluation framework, such as the Kirkpatrick model, provides a useful framework to evaluate 
skills interventions at four levels: (a) Reaction of participants; (b) Learning and Knowledge levels; (c) 
Behavior changes on-the-job; and (d) Result in the project outcomes [93]. It is critical to apply such 
frameworks so that the skills development investments deliver results that matter to the workforce and 
their industries. Adopting such frameworks is essential to ensure that the investments in skills 
development deliver tangible and meaningful results for both the workforce and the industry. 
2.6.3 
Ethical frameworks and regulatory challenges 
The pace of technological change is often faster than the development of the ethical and regulatory 
frameworks to govern it [2]. The use of autonomous systems on construction sites raises important 
issues related to safety, accountability, and liability. Additionally, data-based technologies like digital 
twins and IoT sensor networks create issues of ethics regarding data privacy, ownership, and security. 
A comprehensive governance framework is required to ensure that sensitive information about workers 
and projects is handled responsibly [56]. 
The role of the construction client is also critical in overcoming regulatory and contractual barriers. 
Traditional models often penalize the risk-taking associated with new technologies. Forward-thinking 
clients can act as drivers of innovation by adopting contracts and project delivery models that encourage 
and reward the use of digital tools and automation, thereby helping to bridge the gap between 
technological potential and practical implementation [94]. 
2.6.4 
Environmental sustainability and life cycle impacts 
While automation and AM provide unique possibilities for sustainability, careful consideration is 
required. AM can help mitigate material waste by using design for AM methods and eliminating 
formwork requirements, while using industrial by-products in concrete mixes that can help reduce the 
overall carbon footprint of the base material [64].  
In a similar manner, Design for Disassembly methodologies could be integrated with robotic 
fabrication methods in a way that supports the circular economy [4]. Yet, these advantages must be 
balanced against the increased consumption of energy used by robotic equipment and by the data 
center’s powering the digital workflow. As a result, a comprehensive Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is 
required to show that there is, in fact, a net benefit [83]. That said, verification of the long-term 
durability and performance of new material types, such as bio-based composites [66] and earthen 
materials [67], will also be required to understand sustainability claims over the entire lifecycle of the 
building. The goal should be to leverage automation in ways that support not only efficiency, but to 
build even greater sustainability into the building starting at the construction phase through to 
deconstruction. 
2.6.5 
Challenges and future directions 
The emergence of digital transformation in the construction industry has caused numerous 
opportunities and challenges for industry participants. Converting from the current research-oriented 
experimentations to normalizing fully integrated digital tools and systems in the mainstream 
construction site requires a more expansive view that relates to not just the technology, but also its 
impact on the workforce, social practices, and environment. [83].  
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
A primary challenge is managing the socio-technical transition of the workforce. The narrative has 
matured beyond a simple fear of job displacement to a more nuanced, human-centric vision where 
automation is designed to augment human capabilities—eliminating tasks that are unsafe and 
monotonous while preserving those that workers find valuable, such as problem-solving and social 
interaction [86]. Nevertheless, a gap exists between the aspirations of management regarding 
productivity and the worries of the workforce about safety and usability [87]. The future direction must 
be toward participatory change management with workers involved directly in the implementation of 
new technologies. This must be matched by a commitment to lifelong learning and development of not 
only the digital competencies required to operate with new systems, but also the soft skills, such as 
collaboration and adaptability, which will characterize the new construction profession [88, 89].  
In addition to the human element, there is a need for agile governance and ethical frameworks. The 
pace of technological advancement in respect of robotics, AI, and data collection is currently outpacing 
the capacity to develop the corresponding standards, building codes, and regulations to govern them 
[2]. This generates uncertainty and can stifle innovation. Additionally, data-rich tools such as digital 
twins will lead to significant ethical questions, concerning data privacy, ownership, and algorithmic 
accountability, which will also need to be addressed [56]. The construction client will be a key 
stakeholder in overcoming these challenges. By moving away from traditional, risk-averse procurement 
models, the more forward-thinking clients will construct contracts that can create a culture for adopting 
and rewarding new technologies, thereby helping to pull innovation into the industry [94]. 
Finally, while new technologies offer pathways to greater environmental sustainability, their benefits 
are not automatic. Additive manufacturing can drastically reduce material waste [64], and robotic 
systems can enable design for disassembly to support a circular economy [4]. However, these gains 
must be weighed against the potential for increased energy consumption from robotic equipment and 
data centers. The future of sustainable construction will therefore depend on the widespread use of 
comprehensive Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) to verify that new, automated processes deliver a net 
positive environmental impact over their entire lifecycle [83]. Ultimately, the successful and equitable 
future of construction depends not on technology alone, but on the parallel development of a skilled 
workforce, robust governance, and a steadfast commitment to holistic sustainability.

2.7 Synthesis and identified research gaps 
The review of the literature indicates that the construction industry is transitioning away from 
traditional methods of construction that rely on labor and materials to integrated, data-driven, and 
automated delivery methods. This shift, often framed in the realm of Construction 4.0, does not rely on 
a single technology, but rather the confluence of several. One pivotal environment that has developed 
to test, validate, and teach these approaches is Learning Factories for Construction (LFCs). LFCs have 
emerged as crucial environments for testing, validating, and teaching these new methodologies by 
providing a controlled setting to bridge the digital and physical realms. In both learning environments 
like LFCs and on project sites, the inclusion of advanced technologies like Robotic manipulators, 
Autonomous Guided Vehicles (AGVs), Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), Additive Manufacturing 
(AM) and Digital Twins have the potential to define the future of construction, driving substantial 
improvements in productivity, operational effectiveness, and project-wide communication. However, 
the literature also revealed some gaps and challenges that continue to delay the widespread adoption of 
these technologies. These gaps can be summarized below: 
• 
The physical-digital gap in robotics: While robotic systems show immense potential, a 
primary challenge is translating instructions from a perfect digital model into precise actions in 
a variable physical environment. This requires robust perception systems, adaptive control, and 
better interoperability between different robotic platforms and digital models [26, 28]. 
• 
Material and process control in AM: The success of AM is fundamentally dependent on 
material science. Key challenges include balancing the fresh-state properties of materials (e.g., 
printability) with their hardened-state performance, overcoming structural weaknesses like 
anisotropy, and integrating reinforcement into the automated process [58, 68]. 
• 
The lack of a standardized data workflow: There is a critical need for a standardized data 
pipeline that connects design (BIM) directly to fabrication and assembly (AM and robotics). 
Many current workflows rely on custom scripts and intermediate software, which creates a 
bottleneck and limits interoperability [56, 82]. 
• 
The need for integrated, hands-On training: As technologies become more complex and 
integrated, there is a corresponding need for training environments where students and 
professionals can gain practical skills and understand the challenges of implementing these 
interconnected systems [3, 88]. 
Therefore, to address the identified research gaps, this thesis presents the design, implementation, 
and evaluation of an integrated workflow within a 1:50 scale Learning Factory for Construction. The 
project's primary objectives are:  
1. To develop a modular Lean construction game that serves as a functional learning factory, 
enhancing the educational experience for new audiences by providing a tangible understanding 
of modern prefabricated construction. 
2. To test and validate an integrated, FIM-driven automated assembly workflow. The 
system's performance will be measured through metrics such as assembly speed, motion 
efficiency, failure rate, and interruption handling.  A central model will supply both the IFC 
data for robotic sequencing and the geometric data for fabrication.

3 Methodology 
This section describes the systematic method used to design, implement, and evaluate an automated 
modular construction workflow within a scaled-down Learning Factory for Construction (LFC). In 
particular, the research concentrates on the core process pipeline from digital design to physical 
realization and utilizes Building Information Modeling (BIM), Additive Manufacturing (AM), and 
robotic manipulators for automated assembly. The methodology is specifically oriented to generate the 
data necessary to evaluate the workflow's sustainability and efficiency.  
3.1 Overall approach and workflow 
The process described above follows a fully data-driven workflow that subsequently turns a 
conceptual digital design into a physically constructed module. This design-to-assembly processing 
pipeline (illustrated in Figure 1) consists of six stages: 
1. Digital design: The process begins in the digital realm with the development of a 1:50 scale 
multi-room building model in Autodesk Revit. This stage focuses on defining the macro-level 
spatial relationships, the geometry of the modular interfaces, and the data-rich environment 
necessary for the subsequent automated stages. 
2. Data preparation: The verified BIM model serves as the single source of truth. Geometric 
data is exported in two formats: Industry Foundation Classes (IFC) files are parsed to extract 
the spatial coordinates and metadata required for robotic path planning, while Standard 
Tessellation Language (STL) files are generated to serve as the printable meshes for the additive 
manufacturing stage. 
3. Digital fabrication: The individual building components defined in the STL files are 
physically fabricated using a Prusa MK4S Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) printer. This 
stage focuses on the translation of the digital model into a physical artifact. 
4. AGV-Based material transport: Following fabrication, a TurtleBot 4, acting as an 
Autonomous Guided Vehicle (AGV), handles material transport. Leveraging the ROS 2 
Navigation Stack within a pre-mapped environment, the AGV autonomously navigates from 
the printer station to the assembly area, delivering the physical modules. 
5. Assembly: In the final stage, a Universal Robots UR5e robotic arm follows an assembly 
sequence. Relying on the information and data derived from the BIM model and a vision-based 
system to identify and locate components, the robot performs the physical ergonomic 
movements to traverse printed modules into their final assembly configurations; embedded 
magnetic connections helped adjust for slight misalignment errors. 
6. Data logging: All previous actions included several stages of continuous data logging. 
Important performance indicators such as print time, energy consumption and waste filament 
and robotic accuracy measurements for placement, are documented for evaluating the 
workflow's efficiency, sustainability, and scalability in the Results chapter.

3.2 Conceptual design and BIM Modeling 
The conceptual design of the modules was driven by two primary goals: first, to create a set of 
components suitable for an integrated and automated fabrication-to-assembly workflow; and second, to 
deliver an engaging Learning Factory for Construction (LFC) context where participants could physically 
investigate fundamental building systems. A 1:50 scale model was developed in Autodesk Revit to serve 
as the single centralized source of truth, defining every spatial relationship, interface geometry, and data 
point required for both the robotic processes and fabricated interfaces. 
 
Figure 2. Conceptual visualization of the modular elements assembled into a simplified dormitory

3.2.1 
Technical design principles for automation 
The project is based on a modular dormitory construction, which was established in six "macro" 
modules: Residential Rooms, Toilet Pods, Staircase & Elevator, Kitchen & Common Area, Utility Room, 
and Storage & Garage. A key technical principle was the standardization of the primary building module. 
Most modules were designed to share the same envelope of 90×90×60mm, a constraint imposed by the 
robotic arm's gripper limits. By having the modules share the same dimensions, the entire automation chain 
was streamlined as similar robotic pick-and-place logic could be deployed for every module regardless of 
the condition of the module. Figure 2 shows a conceptual layout of how these modules are aggregated to 
form the ground floor of the dormitory building. The modeling was structured as a two-stage digital 
==PAGE:28==
27 
 
workflow. In the first stage, the high-level geometry, including the main bounding boxes and spatial 
hierarchy, was modeled in Revit, thereby producing necessary IFC files that contained the essential 
coordinates and metadata for robotic path planning and assembly sequencing. The base models were then 
exported as STL files and imported into Blender for fine-tuning. In Blender, the smaller interface details, 
such as the precise geometry for magnetic connectors, alignment pegs were added and refined for 
printability.  
3.2.2 
Pedagogical design as a Learning Factory 
Concurrent with the technical requirements, each module was designed as a stand-alone learning unit to 
provide participants with a tactile introduction to modern construction principles. 
The residential room serves as the main education module. People can engage with first-fix services 
installing 3D-printed piping-systems for wastewater, hot and cold-water and closed loop floor heating 
circuits. Following this, the assembly and installation of multi-layer floor system, including insulation, 
screed and flooring, installation of toilet modules with ventilation and supply and position furniture, thereby 
mimicking real-world construction sequence in a simplified manner. 
The toilet pod module is based on a commercially available prefabricated bathroom unit, introducing 
learners to a widely adopted modular solution. At a 1:50 scale, participants can physically grasp the concept 
of off-site prefabrication by snapping the self-contained pod and its plumbing manifold into the main 
residential unit. 
The utility room module is designed to immerse participants in MEP (Mechanical, Electrical, and 
Plumbing) fundamentals. They can trace the routing of district heating through a heat exchanger and buffer 
tanks, follow the logic of supply and extract HVAC ductwork, and learn the difference between domestic 
hot water and closed-loop heating circuits. 
Finally, the staircase & lift core, kitchen & common area, and storage & garage modules encourage 
holistic building layout and design thinking. Participants can arrange these macro-modules to create 
different building configurations, reinforcing the system-level planning essential to coherent spatial design 
while simultaneously observing the automated system's ability to reliably assemble their custom layouts. 
3.2.3 
Modeling workflow 
The digital modeling workflow is divided into two main stages — Revit and Blender — each playing a 
critical role in preparing the modules for both additive manufacturing and robotic assembly.  
Revit stage  
The modeling process starts with Autodesk Revit, where every module is modeled by centering around 
a standardized bounding box of 90×90×60mm, to retain compatibility with the robotic gripper. The stage 
includes the most obvious geometry, such as the integration of door and window openings. After modeling 
in Revit, the model is exported in two formats. The IFC file represents the bounding box’s geometric and 
spatial metadata and will be used for extracting global coordinates, orientation and alignment information 
for robotic Assembly. The second export is the STL file that serves as the foundation of the 3D print and 
contains the model’s print mesh. The resulting digital model from this stage, defining the bounding box for 
both fabrication and robotic alignment, is illustrated in Figure 3.

Blender stage 
Once exported, the STL files can be imported into Blender to enhance and refine the module mesh. At 
this stage, smaller interface details such as geometry for magnetic connectors, alignment pegs, and ArUco 
marker sockets were added as shown in Figure 4. Boolean operations were used to create accessory slots 
or cavities, and displacement maps were used to embed surface textures, such as brick facades or wooden 
floor designs, into the mesh geometry. These additions, refinements, and enhancements were baked into 
the mesh structure, with the intention that the MK4S printer would subsequently translate the work into a 
successful print. The models also underwent significant refinement in Blender to prepare them for the 
printing process. This involved not only minor mesh repairs but also strategic geometry simplifications and 
part orientation to minimize support structures, ensuring both dimensional accuracy and material efficiency. 
As a final quality control step, all finished models were validated for mesh integrity to ensure they were 
manifold (i.e., watertight), correcting any issues like inverted normals that could cause errors during slicing.

Connection strategy. 
In order to ease robotic handling and to account for minor robotic imprecision, each interface rests on 
embedded magnets. Eight 2mm-diameter cylinder discs are printed in place at the roof and floor corner 
locations so that the modules can stack like building blocks. Meanwhile, stronger rectangular magnets in 
the side walls stabilize ground-floor units against a steel assembly plate and provide lateral connectivity, 
too. Shallow pyramidal "pikes" on the roof fit with corresponding sockets below in the next story, allowing 
a room to slide into position if the UR5e sets it down a millimeter off-center, whereafter the magnetic pull 
completes the seating. An ArUco-code holder is modelled into the front corner so the overhead camera can 
track the unit through the process of assembly. 
Interior fit-out. 
Assembly follows a set sequence that reflects actual construction logic, although it is adjusted for the 
scaled-down system. First, a plumbing fixture with three-pipes, domestic hot-water, cold-water and 
wastewater, is magnet-docked to the interior rear wall. Once mounted, it also acts as a locking mechanism 
that keeps the bathroom pod from drifting around once it is fastened into place. The pod's ventilation 
exhaust gets aligned with a small circular cut-out on the top of the wall of the room, which provides for a 
ventilation duct to pass into the hallway slab above. The floor itself is modular. The heated layer has damp-
proof membrane, insulation, and heated water pipes for floor heating (the closed loop heating is colored 
coded orange) which is to be placed onto three locator pegs mounted on the base shell so the floor tiles are 
restrained and would not slide around after installation. A printed screed middle layer and a wood-finish 
top layer complete the floor sandwich. The design and resulting print of this floor system are detailed in

3.2.9 
ArUco marker fabrication for vision tracking 
To enable vision-based pose detection during robotic assembly, physical ArUco markers were created. 
The primary methodological challenge was to create markers that were not only readable by the system but 
also could physically fit onto the 3D printed modules without being too intrusive. The result was a custom 
fabrication process of each individual marker rather than simple paper printouts. 
 
Digital design and automated generation 
 
The marker design was a multi-stage digital process focused on repeatability and precision. 
Geometric design: Each marker was modeled in Blender as a three-part assembly to maximize visual 
contrast. This included: 1) a thin white skirt to frame the marker and separate it from its surroundings; 2) a 
solid black baseplate; and 3) the unique white ID pattern, extended 0.5mm from the base for clear definition. 
Automated pattern generation: To ensure consistency and eliminate manual error, the visual patterns 
for the markers were generated programmatically. A custom Python script (shown in Listing 1) utilizing 
the OpenCV ArUco module was written to create high-resolution (1080×1080) grayscale images for each 
required ID from a 6×6, 250-marker dictionary. These images were then used in Blender as displacement 
maps, where the software interprets pixel brightness to geometrically extrude the white pattern from the 
black base. This automated pipeline from a script to a 3D model ensured every marker was perfectly formed 
according to the ArUco specification. 
Automated modeling of ArUco Markers: Subsequently, a second script (shown in Listing 2) utilizing 
Blender's Python API (bpy) automated the 3D modeling process. This script programmatically constructed 
the three-part geometry of the marker, including the white base skirt and the black interface plate. It then 
automatically imported the corresponding grayscale image generated by the first script and applied it as a 
displacement map, geometrically extruding the white pattern from the black base. This two-script pipeline 
from a numerical ID to a finished 3D model ensured every marker was perfectly and consistently modeled 
to the required  specificity of the assembly setup.

Physical fabrication and material considerations 
 
Moving from the digital model to the physical 3D printed object has required significant thought around 
the printing method and materials required to create the necessary visual clarity. 
 
Printer and material selection: The markers were printed on a Prusa MK4S equipped with a Multi-
Material Unit (MMU3) to handle the dual-color requirement. A fine 0.25mm nozzle was used to ensure 
high horizontal resolution and crisp edge definition between the black and white elements. Both the 
baseplate and pattern were printed in PLA, chosen for its printability and rigidity. 
Managing color contamination: A significant challenge in the multi-color printing was color bleeding. 
To maintain the contrast necessary for reliable machine vision, a substantial amount of filament needed to 
be purged between tool-changes. So called wipe towers were utilized between each color change. The 
necessary purge volume needed was especially high, going from black to white. While this strategy 
increased material waste, it was a necessary trade-off to guarantee the operational integrity of the vision 
system. 
The final printed markers, shown in Figure 13, exhibited excellent visual clarity and mechanical 
robustness. By designing dedicated sockets for these markers in the main building modules, they were 
seamlessly integrated into the system. This method provided the robotic assembly system with the ability 
to adapt dynamically based on real-time vision feedback, a critical step away from hardcoded sequences 
and toward a more flexible and intelligent automation workflow.

3.3 Additive Manufacturing (3D Printing) 
3.3.1 
Overview of equipment and materials 
The additive manufacturing part of the project was completed with a specific hardware setup, shown in 
Figure 14, printing with materials chosen to fulfill the requirements of the project in terms of precision, 
durability and reliability. 
 
Printing hardware: The primary fabrication tool was a Prusa MK4S printer, equipped with a Multi-
Material Unit (MMU3) for dual-color and multi-material prints. Two different brass nozzles were used: a 
0.4mm high-flow (HF) nozzle for standard components and a 0.25mm nozzle for high-detail parts like 
==PAGE:39==
38 
 
ArUco markers. The printer was housed in a standard enclosure to maintain a stable ambient temperature 
as well as simultaneously holding the filament roles in place. 
Build surfaces: Two distinct build plates were used depending on the material. For the vast majority of 
prints using PLA, a smooth PEI (Polyetherimide) sheet was used, as its surface provided excellent adhesion 
and a glossy finish. For components printed in PETG, a satin powder-coated steel sheet was utilized instead. 
A smooth PEI plate would be damaged by PETG’s inherent adhesive properties. The lightly textured 
surface of the satin plate was better suited. 
Filaments: The primary material utilized was Prusament PLA, selected for its high degree of 
printability, dimensional stability, low warping characteristics, and predictable performance. Its inherent 
higher reliability compared to other printer materials help streamline the troubleshooting during the iterative 
design phase. PETG was used selectively for components requiring clarity, such as windowpanes. 
Embedded components: The design methodology relied on the in-print embedding of N52 
Neodymium (NdFeB) magnets to provide the connection mechanism for the modules. Both cylindrical (Ø2 
mm) and rectangular magnets were used to achieve the desired vertical and lateral connection forces. 
 
Figure 14. The 3D printing hardware setup. An overview of the Prusa MK4S printer in its enclosure, with 
the satin print bed and filament spools placement (left image) and a close-up view of the printer extruding 
filament (right image). 
3.3.2 
Slicing, calibration, and print settings 
All digital models were translated to machine instructions (G-code) through a slicing operation using 
PrusaSlicer. To ensure quality and maintain uniformity and repeatability among the multiple parts, three 
dedicated printer setting profiles were developed, to the specifications and demands of the project’s 
modules. They can be categorized as Standard Profile, Quality oriented Profile and detail-oriented Profile. 
Slicer profiles 
Standard profile: For most structural components, a profile utilizing the 0.4mm nozzle with a 0.2mm 
layer height was employed. This profile used a 15% gyroid infill pattern, offering a good balance between 
structural integrity, material usage, and print speed. 
High-quality profile: For components where surface finish was important but extreme detail was not, 
such as the textured facade panels, a profile with the 0.4mm nozzle and a finer 0.1mm layer height was 
used to minimize visible layer lines. 
==PAGE:40==
39 
 
High-detail profile: For parts requiring maximum planar fidelity, such as the ArUco markers, a specific 
profile was used with the 0.25mm nozzle and a 0.12mm layer height. A critical modification to this profile 
was required to ensure reliability during multi-material printing: the ‘ramming speed’ parameter was 
manually lowered in the filament settings in the slicer software. The default ramming speed used by Prusa 
for purging filament into the wipe tower created a flow rate that exceeded the capacity of the small 0.25mm 
orifice, leading to frequent clogs. By tuning this parameter, these failures were prevented. It is noteworthy 
that typically, Prusa's predefined slicer settings work well. However, since the ‘ramming speed’ parameter 
is categorized as a filament setting rather than a printer setting, it is not prone to changing automatically 
between nozzle installations. 
Calibration and tolerances 
Before each print job, the MK4S executed an automatic first-layer calibration procedure to guarantee 
bed adhesion and material deposition accuracy. However, despite that, there was still a noteworthy amount 
of planar tolerance of approximately ±0.125mm, which significantly hindered the original idea of using 
purely press-fit sockets for magnet-embedment. 
In-print assembly for magnet insertion  
To address the tolerance issue, the process adapted to include a manual in-print assembly step instead. 
A M600 (pause-at-height) command was inserted into the G-code at the specific layer when the magnet 
cavities were fully formed, but still open, by using the slicer. The printer would automatically pause, 
allowing for the manual insertion of magnets into the open cavities. After the operator resumed the job, the 
printer would then continue printing, effectively sealing the magnets inside the part. Thereby completely 
bypass the issue of press-fit tolerances and successfully securing each magnet in the component, a process 
visualized in Figure 15. 
 
Figure 15. An operator places a magnet into a printed socket during a mid-print pause (left image). 
The print continues, permanently embedding the component and ensuring a secure fit (right image).

equipment shown in Figure 16, was established for the data collection of each print job.

log real-time energy use. The readings were summed over the total time for each print and reported in

Material usage and waste: All utilized printing materials were recorded using a high-resolution digital

Data logging and post-processing: This raw quantitative data was systematically logged in a

3.4 Automated assembly 
The automated assembly phase utilizes a custom software framework developed in Python, tailored 
specifically for the construction scenario presented in this thesis. The physical hardware includes a 
Universal Robots UR5e arm equipped with an OnRobot RG2 parallel gripper, with visual feedback 
provided by an overhead RGB-D camera. The following subsections describe the operational principles 
and components at an architectural level. Helper functions, secondary checks, and low-level URScript 
commands are omitted for clarity. The overall sequence logic of the framework is visualized in the

3.4.2 
Part identification and pose estimation 
After system initialization, component detection and pose estimation are performed on-demand when 
the robotic task planner requires information about specific components. Vision processing is handled by 
the detections/marker_detector.py module, following a three-stage process: 
Marker detection: The system leverages OpenCV's 6x6 ArUco dictionary to identify markers in 
camera frames received via a dedicated camera thread. This computer vision procedure detects visible 
ArUco markers by returning their unique IDs and the 2D pixel coordinates of marker corners. 
Pose estimation: For each detected ArUco marker, the system applies the cv2.solvePnP algorithm. This 
function computes the marker's three-dimensional position and orientation (pose) relative to the camera's 
optical frame, based on known marker dimensions and the detected 2D pixel coordinates. It outputs a 
translation vector (t_cam) and rotation vector (r_cam). 
Coordinate transformation: The pose derived relative to the camera frame is then transformed into 
the robot's coordinate system. This is accomplished by applying the pre-loaded calibration data (rotation 
matrix R_ZERO and translation vector TRANSLATION), converting marker positions into accurate robot 
workspace coordinates. The marker detector returns each marker’s ID together with its three-dimensional 
pose expressed in the camera coordinate frame. Later in the process, the pose is adjusted by the transforms 
defined by R_ZERO and TRANSLATION. 
3.4.3 
Task planning, motion control, and alignment 
Once component poses are known, the robotic system initiates task planning and execution using a state 
machine coupled with motion primitives: 
Task planning logic: The list of high-level states, search → pick → swing → place → swing_back, is 
declared in configs/construction_config.py, while the finite-state-machine that iterates through those states 
for each part is implemented in construct/construction.py. That loop polls perception results, updates the 
current state variable, and calls the corresponding motion primitive for the active state. 
Motion execution: The robot_controller/rc.py module encapsulates low-level URScript commands 
behind concise helpers. Its primary Cartesian primitive, movel(pose, acc, vel), issues linear moves in task 
space, while translate_tool(dx, dy, dz) applies small relative offsets to the current tool pose. The RG2 gripper 
is actuated through gripper_width(width_mm) together with a digital-output toggle that opens or closes the 
fingers. When the global _ENABLE_OBSTACLE_AVOIDANCE flag is active, the makerAvoidmovement() 
routine inserts intermediate lift-up and set-down waypoints so the tool rises vertically above the workspace 
before any horizontal motion and descends only after clearing obstacles. 
Component alignment: All grasping and placement motions are executed with a fixed, vertical tool 
orientation: zero pitch, zero roll, and a fixed yaw. Because the parts used in the testbed are rotationally 
symmetric in the horizontal plane, no explicit vision-derived yaw correction is required; correct alignment 
is achieved by maintaining the top-down approach and precisely controlling the XY position.

3.5 AGV-Based material transport 
To fully realize the automated "design-to-assembly" workflow envisioned for the Learning Factory, a 
key requirement is the autonomous transport of 3D-printed modules from the fabrication area to the robotic 
assembly station. The successful integration of an Autonomous Guided Vehicle (AGV) would be necessary 
to bridge the gap between the digital fabrication and robotic assembly stages of the process. This section 
outlines a proposed framework for integrating an AGV system into the LFC in the future. 
3.5.1 
Operational requirements 
Based on the specific constraints of the LFC environment and the nature of the fabricated components, 
the selected mobile platform must meet these core operational requirements: 
 
Payload and form factor compatibility: The AGV must be capable of carrying the largest fabricated 
modules, such as the Kitchen & Common-Room, which has a footprint of 211×90mm. It must provide a 
stable, flat top surface for the manual placement and secure transport of these components. 
ROS 2 native integration: To ensure seamless integration with the broader LFC ecosystem, the 
platform must natively support the Robot Operating System (ROS). This is essential for leveraging 
established software packages like the Navigation Stack (Nav2) for path planning and the SLAM Toolbox 
for mapping. 
Sufficient navigational sensing: The AGV must be equipped with the necessary sensors for reliable 
localization and navigation in a semi-structured laboratory environment. A 2D LiDAR is the minimum 
requirement for creating an occupancy grid map and performing robust localization. 
==PAGE:45==
44 
 
Physical footprint and maneuverability: The vehicle's dimensions must be compact enough to 
navigate the intended pathways between the 3D printer station and the UR5e assembly platform without 
causing obstruction or requiring excessive clearance. 
Extensibility for future research: The platform's architecture should be extensible to support future 
investigations into multi-agent systems and swarm robotics, a key research trajectory for the LFC. 
3.5.2 
Evaluation of candidate AGV platforms 
To select the most appropriate AGV for the defined operational need, a comparative analysis of several 
available robotics development platforms was conducted. The evaluation focused on platforms ranging 
from educational-grade robots to small-scale industrial AGVs, assessing their features against the project's 
requirements for payload, sensing, software integration, and physical footprint. The platforms reviewed, 
shown in Figure 19. Candidate AGV platforms considered for the logistics system, include the iRobot 
Create 3, the TurtleBot 4, the ROSbot 3, and the husky from Clearpath Robotics. 
 
iRobot Create 3 
The iRobot Create 3 serves as an accessible entry point, based on the consumer-grade Roomba® robot 
vacuum cleaner. It includes a basic sensor suite with an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU), optical floor 
tracking, and seven pairs of IR proximity sensors. While it offers a sufficient payload capacity of up to 9 
kg and can be programmed via ROS 2, its primary limitation is the lack of a pre-installed LiDAR sensor. 
This necessitates a separate purchase and significant integration effort to enable the SLAM and autonomous 
navigation capabilities required for the project. 
TurtleBot 4 
The TurtleBot 4 is built directly upon the iRobot Create 3 base but is sold as a fully assembled and 
integrated system. Its main advantage is the inclusion of essential sensors for autonomous navigation right 
out of the box, including a 2D LiDAR and an OAK-D Spatial AI stereo camera. It maintains the 9 kg 
payload capacity of its base and is ideal for learning ROS-based applications. The only practical issue noted 
was the height of the standard platform, which is easily modified to suit project needs. 
ROSbot 3 
The ROSbot 3 is a custom 4-wheel, open-source platform designed for research and education. It is 
well-equipped with a LiDAR, an RGB-D camera, and an IMU. However, it presents a significant practical 
challenge for this project: its chassis does not have a flat top surface, which would require the design and 
fabrication of a complex custom mount to carry the building modules. It also does not specify a payload 
capacity and lacks a joystick controller for easy teleoperation. 
Clearpath Husky 
The UGV platforms from Clearpath Robotics, such as the Husky, represent a higher, industrial-grade 
tier. These systems are highly customizable and offer substantial payload capacities of up to 75 kg and 20 
kg, respectively. While powerful, their primary limitations are their physical size and cost. The Husky, for 
example, is nearly a meter long, and the platforms are priced in the range of $15,000 to $35,000 USD, 
making them unsuitable for the scale and budget of the current scope of this LFC implementation.

3.5.3 
Platform justification 
Based on the comparative evaluation, the TurtleBot 4 was selected as the most suitable platform for this 
theoretical implementation. It represents the optimal balance of capability, cost, and out-of-the-box 
readiness required for the project. While the iRobot Create 3 offers a cost-effective base, it lacks the pre-
integrated LiDAR sensor essential for autonomous navigation, which would require significant additional 
setup time and integration effort. Conversely, the ROSbot 3, while well-equipped with sensors, has an 
impractical physical shape that would necessitate a complex, custom-designed mount to carry the project's 
modules. The larger industrial platforms from Clearpath Robotics, while highly capable, were deemed 
unsuitable because of the high cost; however, Clearpath is still a potential future candidate if the LFC grows 
in scope. The TurtleBot 4, therefore, stands out as the ideal choice. As a fully assembled system built upon 
the Create 3 base, it provides the necessary LiDAR and stereo camera sensors seamlessly integrated for 
immediate use with the ROS 2 Navigation Stack. This out-of-the-box functionality minimizes hardware 
integration challenges, allowing the research to focus directly on the development and validation of the 
autonomous logistics workflow itself. 
3.5.4 
Proposed framework for implementation of the TurtleBot 4 AGV system 
To support automated material flow within the Learning Factory for Construction (LFC), 3D-printed 
modules would be transported from the fabrication area to the robotic-assembly station by a TurtleBot 4 
running ROS 2. The proposed workflow would be built on three main packages, each shown as a swim-
lane in Figure 20. Swimlane flowchart of proposed AGV  
• 
slam_toolbox: For offline mapping and pose initialization 
• 
Nav2: For online planning, control and recovery 
• 
LFC coordination: A custom package with nodes focused on interactions with the UR5e and 
3D-printer 
The AGV would rely on a pre-generated 2-D occupancy-grid map of the laboratory. 
During commissioning the operator would teleoperate the robot through all relevant zones while the 
[sync_slam_node] from the open-source slam_toolbox package records [/scan (LaserScan)] and [/tf (transform 
frames)]. When coverage is judged complete, the map data is exported as (map.pgm) (bitmap) and 
(map.yaml) (metadata). In Figure 20 this entire routine occupies the left-hand swim lane. Once the map is 
available it would be re-loaded, and the Adaptive Monte-Carlo Localization (AMCL) node, managed by 
Nav2, continuously correlates live scans with the static grid. Successful localization, confirmed in [RViz2], 
is represented in the figure by the decision diamond “Pose stable?”; if the covariance remains high the 
==PAGE:47==
46 
 
process routes back to re-initialization before any navigation attempt can begin, ensuring the AGV never 
drives on an uncertain pose estimate. 
Navigation, coordination, and task execution 
The AGV would enter its Navigation, Coordination and Task-Execution phase once a print job finishes. 
A custom coordination node called [lfc_print_monitor] would subscribe to [/print_status] and would look up 
the next delivery pose in its job table. The pose is stamped in the map frame and dispatched to the 
[/navigate_to_pose] action as a PoseStamped goal. 
Utilizing Nav2, the Behavior-Tree navigator contacts [planner_server] (Nav2’s global-planner node). If 
the planner returns FAILED, the recovery server runs its cost-map-clearing or spin-in-place behaviors 
before re-planning. A Yes outcome feeds velocity commands from Nav2’s local tracking node 
[controller_server] to [/cmd_vel].  
Upon arrival the command [/aruco_pose] would be published. This event would trigger the UR5e to pick-
and-place the modules. When the arm picks the module the [lfc_print_monitor] node would issue 
[/follow_waypoints] action that references (charging_waypoints.yaml). Nav2 would then guide the base to the 
dock and then afterwards it is ready to loop back to the next print-completion. 
Multi-agent extension and swarm robotics 
The proposed system would be intentionally designed to be expandable from a single robot into a 
coordinated fleet. Introducing more TurtleBot 4 units would allow multiple transport tasks to run in parallel, 
increasing the factory's efficiency and providing backup in case one robot needs maintenance. ROS 2 is 
built to support this kind of expansion. To make this work, each robot on the network would need a unique 
identity. This would be achieved using a built-in ROS 2 feature called "namespaces," which would act like 
a digital name tag for each AGV, ensuring that internal communications would be kept separate, preventing 
any confusion or crossed signals on the shared network. With multiple robots running, a coordination 
strategy would be needed to manage them. This could be handled by a central "Task Dispatcher," a simple 
software program that would act as a fleet manager. This dispatcher would maintain a list of available 
delivery jobs and assign them to the nearest available robot, ensuring the fleet works together efficiently. 
Finally, to ensure safety, the robots would need to avoid colliding with one another. This would be 
accomplished by having each AGV constantly share its location with the rest of the fleet. From the 
perspective of any single robot, the other AGVs would simply appear as moving obstacles to be navigated 
around, a task handled automatically by their onboard navigation systems.

4.1.3 
Environmental and sustainability assessment 
In accordance with the sustainability metrics of the evaluation framework, this section examines the 
material and energy footprint of the fabrication process. 
Carbon footprint breakdown 
• 
The total carbon-equivalent footprint of the production of the finished LFC assembly was 
4,234.7 g CO₂e. The carbon footprint is based on two main sources: electricity consumption for 
powering the 3D printer, and emissions from the production of raw materials including PLA 
filament and embedded NdFeB magnets. In terms of the overall carbon footprint, the source 
contributions within the carbon footprint breakdown reveal that material production is the highest 
contributor to the total environmental footprint.  
• 
Energy consumption: The total energy consumption for all print requests was 5.652 kWh. The 
Danish energy mix is an emission factor of approximately 138 g CO₂e per kWh, and thus, the 
electricity use alone on the 3D printer accounts for 780.0 g CO₂e or 18.4% of the total carbon 
footprint for the fabrication. 
• 
Material production: The remaining 3,454.7 g CO₂e (81.6%) in the production emissions are 
accounted for lifecycle emissions from manufacturing the PLA printer filament and in the 
manufacturing of the neodymium magnets embedded in the components. 
 
This distribution reveals one of the key findings of the analysis, that although energy efficiency in 
the process of printing is important, the choice of raw materials has significance to the overall 
environmental footprint of the fabricated components. 
Analysis of material and filament waste 
Over the entire project, the combined amount of filament used in the final parts and pieces equals 1201.2 
g, while the process waste (e.g., supports, skirts, purge towers) accounts for 176.2 g. This means that of the 
1377.4 g of filament consumed in total for the successful parts, 12.8% was process waste, while the 
remaining 87.2% (1201.2 g) became part of the final components, which is a relatively significant number 
and points to a challenge in the FDM printing of these complex models. Reviewing the component level 
data in Table 6, Table 7 and Table 8 shows that the waste generation is certainly not uniform and is very 
much concentrated on two types of prints. 
 
==PAGE:52==
51 
 
1. Parts with complex geometries: The shell for the "Staircase & Elevator" module, for instance, 
generated over 31 g of waste, primarily due to the extensive support structures required for its 
overhanging features. 
2. Multi-material prints: Components involving multiple colors or material types, such as the two-
layer flooring in the "Utility Room," necessitated significant filament purging to ensure color 
fidelity, contributing disproportionately to the total waste volume. 
This indicates that while AM is often touted for reducing waste compared to subtractive manufacturing, 
the specific design geometry and use of multi-material systems can introduce significant inefficiencies that 
must be considered in the design phase. It should be emphasized that the values reflected in this chapter are 
limited to the successful prints that contributed to the final assembled model. These values do not account 
for any material or energy spent on the earlier, iterative design and testing stages. There were numerous 
prototypes printed to test tolerances, the mechanics of joints, and the design of pieces. There were also 
dozens of failed prints due to many an issue including nozzle clogs, warping, and miscalibration of the 
printer (more details can be found in Section 4.2.4). Even though these are not included in the footprint of 
the final product, they certainly influence the impact of the research project's true, whole footprint. 
4.1.4 
Multi-material printing waste optimization 
While the overall material waste from support structures was managed through careful design, the 
fabrication of multi-material components presented a significant challenge to the project's sustainability 
goals. Components requiring multiple colors in a single layer, such as the dual-color ArUco markers, 
necessitated frequent filament changes. The standard method for preventing color contamination when 
switching between these colors, is the use of a "wipe tower”, a disposable block printed alongside the model 
onto which the nozzle purges old filament. Initial tests confirmed this was highly inefficient; the wipe tower 
often contained a mass of plastic waste comparable to the functional part itself, while also adding 
considerable time to the print. This baseline level of waste was deemed unacceptable, prompting a 
systematic approach to optimize waste production in the fabrication process. To address material waste, a 
series of increasingly sophisticated optimization techniques were developed. These interventions, which 
could be applied in combination depending on the model's geometry, are detailed below. 
 
1. Wipe tower without sparse layers: The initial optimization involved printing with "no sparse 
layers" on the wipe tower. By skipping sparse layers—meaning the layers without a tool change—
the tower is not built up continuously with the model, but only on the layers where color changes 
occur, thereby the size and material consumption are significantly reduced. This approach generally 
also reduced the overall print time. 
2. Snug supports: To reduce waste from support structures, the default support style was replaced 
with "snug" supports. This slicer feature generates support with a more efficient interface, reducing 
the total volume of material required for printing overhangs. 
3. Custom G-code for dynamic temperature purging: For the components that still required a wipe 
tower, a technique was developed to make the wipe tower as small and material-efficient as 
possible. The key to reducing the necessary amount of filament that needs to be purged between 
color changes. To achieve this, a custom G-code macro (illustrated in Listing 3) was developed to 
make each purge move more effectively by increasing the nozzle temperature by 20°C specifically 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
during the purging moves on the wipe tower. This higher temperature increases purging efficiency, 
flushing out residual color in the nozzle with less needed extruded material. 
4. Wipe to infill: The final substantial optimization eliminated the external wipe tower completely 
by utilizing the slicer's "Wipe to Infill" feature. This method directs purged filament into the 
model's internal infill structure, where it remains hidden from external view. By removing the need 
for an external tower, material waste and printing time due to travel moves were dramatically 
reduced. This method, however, requires sufficient internal infill volume within the printed part, 
and thus was not applicable to small or thin-walled components, which continued to require the use 
of a traditional wipe tower or alternative methods. The cumulative impact of these optimizations is 
visualized in Figure 21 for different parts printed.

4.1.6 
Fabrication challenges and mitigations 
The iterative footprint detailed in the previous section was a result of numerous technical challenges 
encountered during the fabrication campaign. These issues, common in (FDM) printing, necessitated both 
slicing adjustments and design revisions. This section provides a detailed analysis of the primary printing 
challenges, and the mitigation strategies developed to overcome them. 
Nozzle clogs and extrusion issues 
Consistent material extrusion is an absolute necessity for good print quality, and issues in this area were 
a recurring source of failures. 
Problem: Frequent partial or full nozzle clogs were observed, particularly when using the high-
resolution 0.25mm nozzle. This was often manifested as under-extrusion (thin, weak layers) or a complete 
stoppage of filament flow, leading to nozzle clogs and subsequently failed prints. 
Root cause: The primary cause was traced to an insufficient melt flow rate for the given volumetric 
printing speed, especially during operations requiring fine detail and frequent retractions, such as the 
printing of ArUco markers. The smaller orifice of the 0.25mm nozzle was much less forgiving of even 
minor variations in filament diameter or flow variation. A second distinct cause was heat creep, particularly 
during low-flow operations like printing at very fine layer heights or using the "ironing" feature. In these 
cases, the slow movement of filament allowed heat to travel too far up the "cold end" of the extruder, 
causing it to soften prematurely and jam the gear mechanism. 
Mitigation strategy: The mitigation strategy was twofold, depending on the specific printing operation. 
To ensure a consistent melt flow rate during general printing, the temperature was increased by 10°C from 
the default printing profile to ensure a fully molten state and consistent flow. Conversely, to combat heat 
creep during specific low-flow situations like ironing, the temperature was slightly lowered to keep the 
filament solid until it reached the melt zone. Furthermore, a minimum layer height of 0.07mm was 
established as a baseline. To proactively prevent clocks and physical jams, a "cold pull" technique was 
regularly employed to purge the nozzle of any hardened residue before printing. This maintenance 
procedure involves partially melting filament in the nozzle and then rapidly pulling it out after it has cooled 
slightly, a process which extracts burnt-on carbon and filament stuck to the nozzle's interior walls. 
==PAGE:56==
55 
 
Dimensional inaccuracy and component interfacing 
Achieving the precise tolerances required for snap-fit joints and interfacing components was a 
significant design challenge. 
Problem: Early prototypes using press-fit designs for embedding magnets were unreliable. The inherent 
dimensional variance of FDM printing (±0.125mm) meant that sockets were often too tight, preventing 
insertion, or too loose, compromising the connection. 
Root Cause analysis: This issue stems from the fundamental nature of FDM, where the final dimension 
of a feature is influenced by factors like filament shrinkage, extrusion width, and small variations in 
machine movement. Designing for a perfect "net fit" is impractical without extensive, part-specific 
calibration. 
Mitigation strategy: The design philosophy was shifted from post-print assembly to in-print 
fabrication. By programmatically inserting M600 (pause-at-height) commands into the G-code, the printing 
process was halted at a specific layer. This allowed for the manual insertion of magnets into precisely 
printed cavities. The printer would then resume, encapsulating the magnets directly within the component. 
This strategy completely eliminated the tolerance issue, guaranteeing a secure and perfectly aligned 
magnetic connection while also reducing secondary assembly time. 
Bed adhesion failures and warping 
Problem: Several long prints, particularly prints with low amounts of flat surface area touching the print 
bed, failed due to warping, where the corners of the print lift off the build plate. This not only ruins the 
component but also poses a risk of collision with the print head. 
Root cause: Warping is the result of thermal contraction. As the upper layers of the print are extruded, 
they cool and shrink, creating a force that cumulatively pulls the still-warm lower layers up and away from 
the build surface. This situation is exacerbated significantly if the print experiences poor adhesion of the 
first layer, mostly due to microscopic layers of material, dust, or skin oils from fingerprints on the build 
plate preventing a strong bond. 
Mitigation strategy: Bed preparation and material-specific hardware selection routine were adopted. 
Nearly all of the prints produced were done using PLA, so a smooth PEI sheet was used for the build plate, 
which provided excellent adhesion for this material. The PEI sheet was cleaned with isopropyl alcohol to 
remove residual oils and dust thoroughly prior to every major print. When printing the limited number of 
components in PETG (for example, windows), a satin-blasted powder-coated sheet was used as the lightly 
textured surface is better for releasing the more adhesive PETG material after completion. To reduce 
thermal contraction on parts with small footprints or sharp corners, a "brim" as a slicer setting was utilized. 
A one layer of extra perimeters around the bottom of the print. The brim significantly increases the surface 
area of the first layer and therefore provides additional adhesion to hold the corners down during cooling. 
Finally, allowing the part to return to room temperature before removing it significantly reduces the risk of 
destroying the bottom surface of the print when retrieving it. 
4.1.7 
Scalability and feasibility analysis 
The last metric of evaluating the fabrication process is related to scalability and practical potential if 
these modules as a LFC. The quantitative and qualitative results from earlier sections provide information 
on the limitations associated with the current FDM-based manufacturing method. 
==PAGE:57==
56 
 
Fabrication time: The biggest constraint for scalability is speed of production. As shown in Table 5, 
the total fabrication time for the core modules of the LFC assembly was 63 hours, 11 minutes. The duration 
is significant for a small single set of 1:50 scale components; thus, this specific single-machine FDM 
process does not represent a valid way to produce at high speeds and efficiencies. If multiple Lean 
construction games utilizing these modules were to be made, where dozens or hundreds of modules might 
be required, a timeline of 63 hours to fabricate a single “set” would not be feasible. In order to achieve 
scalability from this point, a fabrication plan must begin to include parallelization of the fabrication, such 
as a "print farm" with a team of printers working at the same time, or a move to completely different large-
format additive manufacturing technologies that operate with higher deposition rates. 
Process robustness: Apart from the speed of a process, the robustness of a process defines the feasibility 
of an automated process. The sum of 34 failures and redesigns described in Section 4.1.5 provide a direct 
indicator of the lack of automation robustness in the current fabrication workflow. As previously 
mentioned, part of a truly scalable and autonomous system would be a zero-failure or near-zero-failure 
automated process to be profitable or advantageous to use. Yet, the results confirm that the current process 
is fundamentally reliant on the operator’s experience when diagnosing problems and remediating failures. 
Scale and LFC context: While the findings point to limitations for production, the process is highly 
appropriate within its intended Learning Factory for Construction (LFC) context. The current 1:50 scale 
was selected largely to accommodate the UR5e robotic arm's gripping limit. However, the small size of 
certain components, especially door hardware, wall socket blanks, and other fine fixtures, presents a 
potential challenge. It is projected that participants in the Lean construction game could find these items 
difficult to manipulate, even with the aid of embedded magnets.  While moving to a 1:25 scale would 
significantly exacerbate the issue of fabrication time, the resulting improvements in user handling and 
design fidelity could justify this trade-off for the specific educational goals of the Learning Factory context.  
In summary, while the FDM process proved highly effective for creating customized, high-fidelity 
components within this research context, the findings regarding fabrication time and process robustness 
indicate significant challenges to its direct scalability for real-world construction manufacturing. The 
technology, as implemented here, is best characterized as a powerful and appropriate tool for prototyping 
and hands-on education within a small-scale Lean construction game. However, there are clear 
opportunities for future enhancement through up-scaling.

(κ) of 0.20 m⁻¹. The path efficiency's tight inter-quartile range (33.1%) also indicates that these efficient 
paths were followed consistently. This confirms that lifting modules from an open assembly is a 
geometrically simpler task, allowing the robot to follow more direct, smoother trajectories.

Construction and reconstruction phases require more complex paths: Both the CON and RE-CON 
phases show lower median path efficiency (≈62%) and, in the case of the initial build, higher curvature 
(0.31 m⁻¹). This suggests that the motion planner generated more circuitous routes to navigate around 
existing modules and hazards to achieve final placement.  
Reconstruction shows highest path variability: The RE-CON phase has the widest IQR for path 
efficiency (59.7%), indicating significant variation in the directness of its movements. This aligns with the 
productivity findings and suggests that while some reconstruction movements are direct, others require 
significant detours to ensure correct placement and avoid collisions in a crowded workspace. 
Overall, motion quality is highest during the unconstrained disassembly phase. The geometric penalties 
associated with fine alignment in the construction phases result in less efficient and more complex robot 
paths.

5 Discussion 
This chapter synthesizes and interprets the findings from the implementation and evaluation of an 
integrated workflow for the automated manufacturing and assembly of modular building components. The 
research was guided by the primary objective of assessing the feasibility, efficiency, and pedagogical value 
of a Fabrication Information Model (FIM) and 3D printing-driven process within a Learning Factory for 
Construction (LFC) context. The discussion connects the quantitative and qualitative results from the 
additive manufacturing and robotic assembly phases back to the initial research questions, contextualizes 
the findings within the existing literature, explains the limitations of the current setup and explores the 
potential future implications of this work. 
5.1 Interpretation of key findings 
RQ 1: To what extent can an integrated workflow, leveraging FIM and 3D printing, support the 
manufacturing and automated assembly of modular building components within a LFC context, and 
what are its implications for efficiency, sustainability, and pedagogical value? 
 
The findings confirm that an integrated FIM and 3D printing workflow can indeed support automated 
manufacturing and assembly within an LFC. The research demonstrated the successful fabrication of 
complex modules using FDM 3D printing, and the subsequent validation of the robotic assembly process 
using simplified modules of same bounding box dimensions provide a tangible proof-of-concept for a 
complete digital design to production and assembly pipeline. However, the implications for efficiency and 
sustainability of this implementation are nuanced. 
Efficiency — While the robotic assembly phase was efficient (e.g., disassembly throughput of 79.2 
modules/hour), the overall workflow is bottlenecked by the lengthy additive manufacturing process. The 
63-hour fabrication time for a smaller set of modules suggests that the current FDM-based setup is not 
viable for high-volume production but is well-suited for the prototyping and educational purposes of a Lean 
construction game.  
Sustainability — The sustainability assessment reveals a substantial trade-off. On one hand, AM offers 
the potential to reduce waste compared to standard methods as no formwork or mold is needed. On the 
other, the process in this thesis generated significant waste from necessary supports and purging, and the 
iterative nature of the design development carried a substantial "footprint of innovation." The finding that 
material production accounted for over 81% of the total CO₂e footprint underscores the need for a holistic 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), to ensure that the benefits of automation are not offset by increased energy 
and material consumption.  
Pedagogical Value — The project's success in creating high-fidelity, tactile modules supports the 
potential pedagogical value of this approach. The resulting 1:50 scale models provide a rich, hands-on 
learning tool that can make abstract concepts like systems integration, MEP routing, and modular design 
principles tangible for students, directly addressing the need for more experiential gamified learning 
environments as highlighted by the literature on LFCs [3, 4, 11]. 
==PAGE:69==
68 
 
RQ 2: How can a standardized data pipeline, from FIM (using IFC from BIM) to robotic control be 
designed and implemented to manage the fabrication and assembly of complex, multi-part modular 
building units? 
The research was successful in designing a standardized data pipeline that bridges the gap between 
digital design,  physical production, and assembly. The workflow, which leverages IFC for assembly data 
and STL for fabrication, demonstrates a practical solution to the problem of interoperability. By maintaining 
a single source of truth for the system from a centralized BIM-model and ensuring consistent coordinate 
systems and part identifiers, the pipeline minimizes the need for manual data translations. The use of 
IfcOpenShell to parse IFC files for robotic waypoints exemplifies the kind of standardized  approach needed 
to create a larger-scale FIM-driven system of systems in the future.  
RQ 3: What are the quantifiable performance metrics of this automated workflow, and what are the 
primary challenges affecting its robustness and scalability? 
The research has provided quantifiable metrics for both fabrication and assembly. The fabrication 
metrics (print time, energy use, waste) and assembly metrics (cycle time, path efficiency, recovery 
overhead) offer a baseline for future research. The primary challenges affecting robustness and scalability 
were: 
Fabrication Inaccuracy — The ±0.125mm tolerance of the FDM process was a significant hurdle, 
necessitating a redesign of the connection strategy. This highlights the persistent "physical-digital gap" 
discussed by Graser et al. [24] in the context of the DFAB HOUSE, where perfect digital models must be 
reconciled with real-world imperfections.  
Fabrication Speed — As discussed, the slow speed inherent to the utilized AM technique (FDM-
printing) is the single greatest barrier to scalability. This finding reinforces the need to explore alternative, 
higher-deposition-rate AM technologies for any application beyond educational prototyping.  
Operator Dependence — Despite the high degree of automation, the process was not entirely 
autonomous. The need for manual intervention for tasks like magnet insertion and operator expertise 
required to diagnose and mitigate print failures indicate that the system's robustness is still heavily reliant 
on human knowledge.  
RQ 4: In what way can the resulting physical scale model (1:50) and its underlying processes function 
as an effective pedagogical tool within an LFC to teach the principles of modular design, systems 
integration, and automated construction? 
The 1:50 scale model can serve as an effective pedagogical tool with its values lying in its ability to 
bridge theory and practice in several ways: 
Tangible Learning — By allowing students to physically handle and assemble modules, the model 
transforms abstract BIM models into a concrete, tactile experience. This "learning by doing" approach is 
central to the LFC philosophy and has been shown to be more effective than purely lecture-based methods 
[5].  The models effectively function as a Lean Construction game, similar to the Lego-based simulations 
described by Dallasega et al. [6], but with a higher degree of fidelity and integration of robotic hardware. 
Systems Thinking — The multi-component nature of the modules, with their modular plumbing, 
flooring, and structural elements, encourages students to think holistically about building systems while 
==PAGE:70==
69 
 
also introducing them to disciplines involved in construction, such as carpentry, plumbing, and electrical 
work. 
Understanding Automation — The automated assembly process itself is a learning opportunity. Students 
can observe robots’ movements, understand the role of vision systems and fiducial markers, and appreciate 
the challenges of robotic precision. This direct exposure to Industry 4.0 technologies is a key value 
proposition of LFCs [10].  
5.2 Implications for education, industry, and sustainability 
Although conducted at 1:50 scale and within a controlled laboratory, the research offers a few insights 
that may guide subsequent work in construction automation and education.  
Educational use-cases: The printed modules and their IFC-driven assembly routine demonstrate how 
learners can move from screen-based BIM exercises to hands-on exploration of tolerances, sequencing, and 
basic fabrication. Because the artifacts require no specialized tools beyond a desktop and a FDM 3D-printer, 
they could be replicated in other teaching labs at a minor cost. A formal evaluation of learning outcomes, 
however, is still required. 
Workflow design for SMEs: By relying on open-source IFC parsing (IfcOpenShell) and commodity 
hardware, the prototype has a relatively low-barrier path for small firms or research groups that wish to 
experiment with design-production-assembly pipelines without much investment. Whether this approach 
scales to heavier components or alternative machines remains untested. 
Sustainability considerations: The material-intensive nature of FDM-printing, together with longer 
print times, should remind practitioners that automation does not automatically equate to environmental 
benefits. Any attempt to transfer this workflow to industry should therefore begin with a cradle-to-gate life-
cycle assessment and an exploration of lower-impact feedstocks or higher-throughput AM processes. 
5.3 Limitations of the Study 
It is important to acknowledge this research has many limitations, which gives context to the findings 
and provides avenues for potential future work. 
Scale: The 1:50 scale of the project, while necessary for the available robotic equipment, may not fully 
capture the complexities of full-scale construction. Issues like material handling of heavy components, 
structural stability, and the physics of larger assemblies are not addressed. 
Controlled environment: The experiments were conducted in a controlled laboratory setting. The 
robustness of the robotic assembly system was not tested against the greater variability and unpredictability 
of a real construction site, which would include dust, uneven lighting, and unplanned obstructions. 
Specific hardware: The findings are specific to the Prusa MK4S printer and the UR5e robotic arm. 
Different hardware would likely yield different performance metrics and perhaps will present a unique set 
of challenges. 
Lack of AGV integration: The proposed AGV-based material transport was a theoretical framework 
and was not physically implemented. The full integration of an AGV would introduce additional 
complexities related to fleet management, communication, and system coordination that were not explored 
in this research. 
==PAGE:71==
70 
 
No Pedagogical Evaluation: Although the project developed a detailed pedagogical oriented toolkit, 
this research did not formally evaluate its educational effectiveness. No data from pre/post-tests or learner 
feedback was collected to empirically validate the impact of the scale model as a learning tool. 
Separation of Game and Automation: While the thesis presents both a Lean construction game with 
high-fidelity modules and a validated automated assembly workflow, these two core components were not 
fully integrated in the final experiment. The robotic assembly tests were conducted using simplified 
modules, not the complex pedagogical modules designed for hands-on learning 
Human-in-the-loop: The fabrication workflow still required human intervention for several steps, 
including loading necessary printer material/color, inserting magnets, and the detachment of support 
material when finished. The system contains branches where human intervention is needed, therefore the 
overall system cannot be presented as fully autonomous. 
5.4 Future research directions 
Based on the findings and limitations of this research, there are several directions to be taken for future 
research. 
Integration of the Lean game and automated assembly: To create a more cohesive and autonomous 
learning experience, future work should focus on integrating the manual Lean Construction Game with the 
automated assembly workcell. This could involve developing scenarios where students first manually 
arrange a digital building layout, and then the robotic system autonomously scans, recognizes, and 
constructs their design utilizing the high-fidelity modules. This would provide a direct and powerful 
feedback loop, allowing learners to see the tangible results and challenges of their planning decisions being 
executed by an automated system. Following the robotic placement of the main modules, students could 
then assume the roles of tradespeople—like carpenters, plumbers, and electricians—by manually installing 
the finer interior components such as doors, floor panels, furniture, and electrical sockets. 
Increase level of  autonomy: The system cannot be considered fully autonomous currently, as the 
fabrication workflow relies on human intervention for several critical tasks. As such, a significant avenue 
for future work lies in increasing the level of autonomy in the fabrication workflow to minimize the need 
for human intervention.  
Upscaling: A logical next step would be to replicate the workflow at a larger scale (e.g., 1:25 or even 
1:10) using a larger robotic arm and a large-format AM printer. This would allow for a more realistic 
investigation of structural connections and material handling challenges. 
Full AGV integration and swarm robotics: A critical future direction is to implement and expand the 
proposed AGV framework, evolving the current linear process into a dynamic, multi-agent swarm robotics 
system. The initial step involves implementing and testing the proposed AGV framework to create a fully 
automated logistics chain, which requires research into multi-robot coordination and fleet management 
within the LFC. This would then be scaled into a parallel production environment with multiple 3D printers, 
a fleet of AGVs, and several robotic assembly stations operating in tandem. Building on the proposed AGV 
framework's "Task Dispatcher" concept, a central Digital Twin could orchestrate the entire factory, 
allocating fabrication and assembly tasks to available machines in real-time to optimize material flow and 
minimize idle time. Research in this area could address the challenges in interoperability between 
heterogeneous systems, dynamic scheduling, and resilient control in response to real-time events.

6 Conclusion 
Building upon prior research of Teizer et al. [4] to advance a Learning Factory for Construction (LFC), 
this thesis presents the integration of Fabrication Information Modeling (FIM) and Additive Manufacturing 
(AM) in a LFC. This was achieved through two interconnected objectives: first, the development of a 
tangible Lean construction game featuring high-fidelity, 1:50 scale modules that replicate prefabricated 
rooms, using FIM; and second, the validation of a data-driven, automated assembly sequence where a 
robotic manipulator assembles modules of the same scale using data derived from the same FIM model. 
This thesis successfully addressed these objectives, confirming the viability of employing a standardized 
data pipeline, leveraging IFC data directly from a central FIM model, to effectively control additive 
manufacturing and robotic assembly processes. The AM stage, though capable of producing high-quality 
modules, highlighted a critical bottleneck in terms of interoperability, requiring nearly 63 hours of print 
time for a small set of modules. Conversely, the robotic assembly component of the pipeline showcased 
robust performance, accurately and efficiently assembling complex modules without manual intervention 
and recovering from simulated safety hazards in under 3.5 seconds, though this was validated using test 
modules which, while sharing the same footprint, were simplified and lacked some of the complexities of 
the pedagogical models. 
Quantitative analysis of the workflow underscored areas for improvement, particularly in fabrication 
speed, material waste reduction, and scalability. The exploration and validation of waste-mitigation 
strategies in multi-material Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) 3D printing provided actionable insights 
and a quantifiable framework for future optimization. 
From an educational perspective, the detailed modular designs developed throughout this thesis provide 
a solid foundation for creating tangible and interactive learning experiences. While practical testing and 
validation of these pedagogical approaches remain a future research direction, the established modular 
framework inherently supports future expansion and adaptation to various educational as well as technical 
contexts. 
In conclusion, while the current workflow validates a promising path forward for modular automated 
construction, realizing broader industry-scale adoption requires further investigation into additive 
manufacturing as a technology, standardization of data exchange, and refinement of process robustness. 
Future research should continue to optimize these aspects.
