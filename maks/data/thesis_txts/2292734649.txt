Abstract
In the early 90s the ﬁrst size eﬀects were discovered for indentation problem in Stel-
mashenko et al. 1993 and Ma and Clarke 1995. Here it was found that for problem
sizes below about 100 µm a signiﬁcant increase in the hardening measures was ob-
served. Since then several attempts have been made to make analytical formulations
accounting for the size eﬀects. One widely used framework is from the ﬁeld of Strain
Gradient Plasticity, where gradients of strain is an essential part of the material mod-
els. Today many areas of the size eﬀects have still not yet been investigated. For the
indentation problem good numerical solutions for comparison with experimental and
analytical solutions were still not yet found which motivated this thesis.
This thesis gives numerical solutions to the classical indentation problem studied
for a single grain visco–plastic crystal material in 2D. A ﬁnite strain version of strain
gradient crystal plasticity model is used for the material model. Two crystal structure
cases are considered; a Hexagonal Closely Packed crystal oriented in the (0001) direc-
tion with basal slip and a Face–Centered Cubic crystal oriented in the (110) direction
with slip in the {111} planes.
The solutions veriﬁes the gradient eﬀects by comparing to conventional plasticity
solutions. This comparison is made both on a macroscopic level, where hardening
measures are compared, and on a microscopic level, where the crystal deformation
of the material is investigated along with the density of the Gemoetric Necesarry
Dislocations. Also the inﬂuence of material rate dependency on the hardening mea-
surements is analysed in the attempt to verify the existence of a characteristic velocity
for indentation problems, as were discovered for other visco–plastic models in Nielsen,
Niordson, and Hutchinson 2012 and Nielsen and Niordson 2012.

Preface
This master thesis was prepared at the department of Mechanical Engineering at the
Technical University of Denmark in fulﬁllment of the requirements for acquiring the
Master of Science in Engineering degree in Engineering Design and Applied Mechanics.
The project was supervised by associate professors Christian Frithiof Niordson and
Kim Lau Nielsen.
Prerequisites
This thesis aims to explain the theory on an understandable level for readers with
basic knowledge within plasticity. The reader is besides should have some knowledge
within the ﬁeld of Strain Gradient Plasticity and crystal plasticity.
Also a basic
knowledge within Finite Element Modelling is expected.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Since the early 90s several experimental investigations have conﬁrmed size–dependence
of material behaviour for small scaled problems. For indentation problems, experi-
ments shows a signiﬁcant increase in the hardness measurements for small scales,
where some of the earliest results conﬁrming this can be found in Stelmashenko et al.
1993 and Ma and Clarke 1995. Here a clear increase in hardening was seen for test
scales below about 100µm. To ﬁnd the cause of these size eﬀects one must look at the
micro-mechanic behaviour of metals, where the general accepted reason for the size
eﬀects is the geometrically necessary dislocations (GNDs). These stored dislocations
build up in areas of large slip gradients. As a result this theory led to a further de-
velopments within the ﬁeld of Strain Gradient Plasticity (SGP) to account for these
size eﬀects.
Some of the earlies formulations of SGP was proposed back in 1984 in Aifantis
1984. These earlier formulations did not, however, account for higher order stresses
and are therefore called lower order theories. Higher order theories were introduces
several years later in for instance Fleck and Hutchinson 2001, Gudmundson 2004,
and Fleck and Willis 2009 containing higher order stress quantity work conjugates to
strain gradients. A ﬁnite strain version of the formulations in Fleck and Hutchinson
2001 is proposed in Niordson and Redanz 2004 and these formulations together with
the crystal plasticity formulations of Niordson and Kysar 2013 will be the base for
the material model used in this thesis.
The growing interest in size eﬀects is partly a result of the development within
material science, where thin coatings on a micro-scale now plays a major part in the
material industry. This leads to a wish for better predictions of material behaviour on
these scales where conventional plasticity models will not be suﬃcient. Since there is
yet no good numerical investigations of the size eﬀect for indentation problems, this
is the main motivation for this thesis.
Another motivation is found within the numerical complications of visco–plastic
models. For a power–law visco–plastic deﬁnition it is found that the visco–plastic
exponent has a major impact on the stability of the numerical calculations. Here a
smaller visco–plastic exponent leads to lesser stable numerical calculations. For met-
als the visco–plastic exponent will typically be within the range [0.005-0.02] where
it becomes diﬃcult to obtain a stable solution. In Nielsen and Niordson 2012 and
Nielsen, Niordson, and Hutchinson 2012 it is predicted that a characteristic velocity
exist, where the material hardening measurements will be rate–independent. The

the existence of such a characteristic velocity for the considered indentation problem.
1.1
Numerical tools
The numerical calculations in this project are made by the use of a handed–out
Fortran code. This code is made from scratch by Christian F. Niordson from DTU
mechanical department with the use of a skyline solver developed by Erik van der
Giessen in 1980. The code is from handout already modiﬁed to solve a crystal plastic
indentation problem. As a part of this project, further modiﬁcations have been made
in order to optimize the code for solving the current problem as described in Chapter
4. For one of the modiﬁcations, the structural analysis software of the commertial
program Ansys is used.
1.2
Thesis overview
Chapter 2 covers the mathematical background of the material model used in this
thesis. The chapter will gradually build the material model up, by ﬁrst intruding
the mathematical framework of strain gradient plasticity for small strain, hereafter
introducing the crystal plasticity formulations and ﬁnally by introducing the ﬁnite
strain formulations. In the end the numerical framework is described used in the
Finite Element Model.
In Chapter 3 the investigated problem is described. Here the assumptions are
listed and the input data is deﬁned. This is followed by a description of what inves-
tigations will be made and what results are expected from these investigations. This
chapter also covers more in depth theory of the slip systems, the GND’s, the size
eﬀects, the characteristic velocity and more.
Chapter 4 describes the implementations made during this thesis to improve the
model.
In Chapter 5 all the results concerning the hardening curves are presented and
discussed. Here results will be presented and compared for conventional plasticity and
for strain gradient plasticity. This includes size eﬀect investigations and the search
for the characteristic velocity.
The rest of the results will be presented and discussed in Chapter 6. These are
all results on a micromechanic scale, where the focus will be on the dissipative size
eﬀects for strain gradient plasticity.
Finally Chapter 7 contains the closing remarks for this thesis. Here the thesis
conclusion is found along with further work.

3.1
Assumptions
To make the problem numerical solvable and to ease the solving process diﬀerent
assumptions are made. The main assumptions are stated below whereas other as-
sumptions will be stated later during this chapter.
The indentation problem can be modelled in 2D
Due to the symmetry of the problem, it is assumed, that the problem can reasonable
be modelled as a 2D problem assuming plain strain in the x3 direction. This assump-
tion will require some extra assumptions to account for crystallographic slip in the
x3–direction. For more on this see Section 3.2.1.2.
The wedge is made from an inﬁnite hard crystal
Here it is assumed that no deformation will occur on the wedge during indentation.
This is a reasonable assumption since the indenter will be made from a material, typ-
ically diamonds, where the material hardness is signiﬁcantly higher than the material
of the considered domain, hence the deformation of the indenter should be negligible.
The domain consists of a single grain crystal
This assumption makes it possible to compare with single grain crystal experiments.
No slip on the edges of the wedge
Hereby assuming inﬁnite friction between the wedge and the domain. As a conse-
quence, as soon as the domain comes in contact with the wedge it will be constrained
in the x1-direction. The implementation of friction between the wedge and the do-
main is saved for further work.
Flat initial contact area between the wedge and the indenter
To avoid singularities, the contact area at the beginning of the indentation is assumed
non–zero. This initial contact area 2a0
c is assumed to cover the four top center nodes
of the numerical mesh in the Finite Element Model.
The inﬁnite domain can be modelled as a ﬁnite domain
For the numerical calculations the domain is modelled as a semicircle with radius
r0. For the results to be representable as to modelled an inﬁnite domain, one must
therefore assure, that the boundary eﬀects are small enough to not have any inﬂuence
on the results. For instant one must assure that no slip occurs on the edges of the
domain due to boundary reaction forces.
The material is assumed perfectly plastic
Meaning no conventional hardening of the material. Hereby the slip resistance g(α)
will be constant equal to τ (α)

The material is assumed to be elastic isotropic

3.3.2
The characteristic velocity
The characteristic velocity for rate–sensitivity materials was ﬁrst time observed in
Nielsen and Niordson 2012 for a slap model in shear deformation and again for mode
I crack growth in Nielsen, Niordson, and Hutchinson 2012.
The characteristic velocity is a speciﬁc rate, where the material macroscopic hard-
ness does not depend on the material rate sensitivity. Besides the two cases conﬁrm-
ing the existence of a characteristic velocity, Nielsen, Niordson, and Hutchinson 2012
predicts that this rate will exist for a broad range of problems with rate–dependent
materials. They argue that the characteristic velocity should always exist, since a
monotonically behaviour of the rate–sensitivity is expected at all rates does not depen-
dent on the other material parameters. This means that the hardness of the material
should always be either monotonically decreasing with an increasing rate–sensitivity
or monotonically increasing with an increasing rate–sensitivity. For this to apply the
hardening as a function of the rate for diﬀerent rate–dependencies must always have
a speciﬁc rate, the characteristic velocity, where the values are exactly the same.
It has not yet been tested if the characteristic velocity exist for the considered
indentation problem.

3.3.3
Geometric Necesarry Dislocations
During the deformation, dislocations can be trapped within the material for two
reasons; the Statically Stored Dislocations (SSDs) and the Geometrical Necesary
Dislocations (GNDs).
The SSDs occurs under homogeneous deformation where dislocations are stored
due to inclusion and random trappings. This will result in the usual hardening ob-
served in metals.
The GNDs on the other hand will occur due to inhomogeneous deformation. Here
dislocations are stored to accommodate plastic strain gradients. The GNDs was ﬁrst
introduced by Nye 1953 and are illustrated nicely for edge and screw deformations in
Arsenlis and Parks 1999. For the 2D model only edge dislocations will be considered.
These edge dislocations can be explained by a simple example. Here four rectangu-
lar blocks of a crystallographic lattice with an eﬀective slip system allowing horizontal
slip are considered on top of each other as illustrated in 3.8. Now diﬀerent shear forces
are applied to the blocks, such that the bottom block undergoes a single dislocation
motion, the second block undergoes two dislocations and so on as also illustrated in
the ﬁgure. By now comparing the left side edge dislocations with the right side, it

4.1.1
The updated mesh
In the updated mesh the geometry of the domain is changed from a rectangular section
with the dimensions a0 and b0 to a semicircle with radius r0, see for instance Figure 3.1
(b). Since the code is only build for quadrilateral elements, the outer edge of the
domain will be made by linear connections of points placed in the distance r0 from
the semi circle center. A ﬁner mesh therefore means a domain closer to a perfectly
==PAGE:57==
4.1 Mesh improvement

round semicircle, see Figure 4.2 (a) and (c). To avoid problems in the semicircle
center with the quadrilateral elements there will be a transfer from the outer areas
where the elements should simulate a round domain to a completely rectangular area
when approaching the center of the semicircle. The center rectangular area will be
made of elements with a ﬁxed aspect ratio of 1 (cubic elements), see Figure 4.2 (b).
The number of elements in the ﬁxed aspect ratio area will be directly determined by
the number of elements along the outer periphery of the semicircle to ensure the right
connections of the elements. More on this after describing the mesh input parameters.
The updated mesh for two grid resolutions can be seen in Figure 4.2.
The mesh input parameters for the updated mesh is reduced to the following three
parameters:
• nel: The number of elements in the top layer of the domain along the diameter
of the semicircle. This must be an even number in order to ensure symmetry.
• nel_0: The number of elements along the outer periphery of the domain. This
number should also be even.
• fak: Faktor which determine the element size increase when moving away from
the center of the semicircle outside the rectangular area.
As mentioned the number of element rows and columns (Fortran name row and
col) in the rectangular area will be determined by the value nel_0. The number
of columns will here be row = nel_0/4 if nel_0/2 is an even number and row=
(nel_0+2)/4 if nel_0/2 is an odd number. The number of rows will then have to be
row = nel_0/2 - col. These deﬁnitions gives a rectangular area as close to half a
cubic as possible when having a ﬁxed aspect ratio of one in this area. To ensure better
aspect ratios of the outer elements for especially the coarse meshes, the placement of
the element nodes along the periphery of the semicircle are determined by the ratio
row/(col/2). The eﬀect of this can be seen in the coarse mesh in Figure 4.2 (c). For
the cases where the ratio is one (when nel_0/2 is an even number) the nodes will be
placed in the same mutually distance along the semicircle periphery.
If one is to change the initial contact area, given by location of the four top
centered nodes, or if extra reﬁnement is needed close to the indenter the fak–value
can be changed. Since the value determine the increase in element size away from
the rectangular area the rectangular area can be made smaller simply by increasing
this value. To ensure proper element aspect ratio one should change nel accordingly.
For a generally mesh reﬁnement the parameters nel and nel_0 should be increased.
Here it is found, for fak=200, a ratio nel/nel_0 close to 2 gives aspect ratios close
to one for all the elements in the mesh.

4.3
Iteration reduction
As described in Section 2.2.1 the slip rate ﬁelds for each slip system is solved by an
iterative process from Fleck and Willis’ minimum principle I. Each iteration step is
a heavy computational process, therefore the overall computational time is strongly
dependent on the number of iterations needed for each increment. In order to decrease
the computational time, two implementations have been made in this Thesis in an
attempt to reduce the number of iterations. The implementations have been made,
so that the user can change the input ﬁle to decide which of these tricks to apply, if
no trick should be applied or of both should be applied at the same time, see A.2.0.2.
The two implementations will be documented and discussed in this section.

For each increment n the iterative process uses the calculated values of the slip rate

This trick was also implemented with success in the 3D void problem by Kim Lau
Nielsen, where it was found to reduce the overall number of iterations with more than
1/3 of the iterations when applying this method alone.
After implementing this method for this problem it at ﬁrst glance seems like the
number of iterations saved were not signiﬁcant and would for some increments lead
to even more iterations. This can be explained with the large changes in values when
the contact area is updated. By applying a gradient guess based on previous values
the initial guess for these increment will be worse than just using the last solution.
Due to the time limits of this thesis a further analyse of the convergence has not
been made for the gradient guess and is therefore saved for future work. Based on the
ﬁrst observations, it is chosen not to use the gradient guess when solving the problem.
4.4
Chapter concluding remarks
Of the implementations made, the updated mesh and the iteration reduction by im-
plementing Julie’s trick was found to be the most inﬂuential.
By updating the mesh it was found that good numerical results could be obtained
with a relatively coarse mesh.
By implementing Julie’s trick the number of iterations were greatly reduced for
each incremental step with up to 44% of the iterations saved, and hereby making
signiﬁcant savings on the computational time.
By updating the constraints of the boundaries with the super–element method it
was found, that the code could handle larger indentations before crashing. At these
large indentations the results were not representable for a indentation problem in an
inﬁnite domain, and therefore the update was found not to improve the results.

In this chapter the results of all the analyses including macroscopic hardening of the

scale and the macroscopic hardening eﬀect, the total slip (see the intro of Chapter 6)

on the micro–scale and the stationary phase of the hardening curve. Based on this,

Chapter concluding remarks
The total slip, as well as the individual slip and densities of the GND’s, was in this
chapter analysed for diﬀerent cases.
For ϕ = 5◦, clear slip edges with high slip gradients were found for the HCP and
FCC conventional cases leading to high accumulations of GND’s in slim lines along
these edges. For ϕ = 45◦, FCC conventional case slip edges were still found but with
lower slip gradients compared to the slip itself. This led to a more defuse GND density
ﬁeld with high density accumulations stretching far within the slip ﬁelds instead of
being found just at the edges.
For all the conventional cases the self–similar eﬀect was found as expected.
By applying a dissipative length scale, in all cases led to overall more defuse slip
and density ﬁelds. This was explained by the quadratic deﬁnition of the eﬀective
slip from Equation (2.22), where the gradients of the slip gives a contribution when
applying a length scale. For the slip ﬁelds this results in a reduction of sharp edges
where high slip gradients were found. A higher dissipative length scale resulted in
even more defuse slip and GND density ﬁelds.
For the FCC cases (ϕ = 5◦and
ϕ = 45◦) it was checked and found, that the total slip for LD ̸= 0 in fact converges
towards the conventional solution for higher indentation depths.

CHAPTER 7
Concluding remarks
7.1
Conclusion
As one of the main aims of the thesis, the inﬂuence of size eﬀects was shown. Both
on the micro–scale and for macroscopic material hardening, the size eﬀects was found
to have the greatest impact on the results for the smallest scales and as the scale
increases, by increasing the indentation depth, the solutions was found to converge
towards the conventional plasticity solutions. For the macroscopic hardening, the
inﬂuence of the size eﬀect was found leading to an increase in hardening. Thereby
the numerical results follow the tendency found in experimental investigations of
indentation problems. On the micro–scale a dissipative length scale contribution was
found to give an overall more defuse slip ﬁeld. Here the impact were especially seen
for slip edges with high slip gradients, leading to high accumulations of the GND
densities along these edges. By increasing the dissipative length scale the GND’s
were found to accumulate over wider areas within the slip ﬁelds.
This eﬀect was
shown for the FCC and HCP crystal case for a indentation wedge angle of ϕ = 5◦
and for the FCC crystal case for an increased indentation angle of ϕ = 45◦.
The aim to conﬁrm the existence of a characteristic velocity for indentation prob-
lems was less successful. Here the existence of a characteristic velocity could not
be conﬁrmed nor denied despite several attempts to improve the numerical results.
Instead ranges were given based on the numerical results where the characteristic
velocity is expected to be found if existing. This was done for the HCP crystal case
with a wedge angle of ϕ = 5◦with and without dissipative length scale contributions.
Besides these two main aims of the thesis, several other investigations have been
carried out. The self–similarity eﬀect of indentation problems for conventional crystal
plasticity, when keeping the rate of indentation (change in indentation depth per
contact area) constant, was investigated. Here a clear connection was found between
when the self–similar eﬀect began (the inﬂuence of the initial contact area have become
suﬃcient small) and when the macroscopic hardening measurements became constant.
The self–similar eﬀect was seen for all considered cases with conventional crystal
plasticity.
The macroscopic rate–dependency inﬂuence for diﬀerent visco–plastic exponents
was also shown, where a high visco–plastic exponent led to a high rate–dependency
on the material hardening measurements.

compared to the total slip were not as clear, leading to a more defuse GND density
ﬁeld.
7.2
Further work
The next natural step after this thesis would be to set up experiments for the crystal
cases considered, in order to conﬁrm the numerical solutions of this thesis.
Beside this, further implementations could be made to improve the numerical
model. Some suggestions would be:
• Implement the ﬁnite strain formulations for the energetic gradient contributions
in Flecks and Willis’ Minimum Princple I.
• Parallelization of parts of the code for multiple core calculations for even more
computational time savings.
• Implementation of slip between the wedge and the domain.
• Implementation of grain boundary eﬀects for poly–grain crystal investigations.
For further numerical investigations one could:
• Look at even more diﬀerent wedge angles. Here an angle of 90◦is suggested for
investigations of a ﬂat punch indentation.
• Investigate other crystal cases.
• Find the ranges of a possible characteristic velocity for the FCC crystal case as
well.
• Look at diﬀerent crystal orientations for the HCP and FCC crystal case by
changing the eﬀective slip angle θ.
• Investigate contour plots of other parameters on a micron scale. Here σ22 for
instance can be investigated, which is an expression of the hardening at the
contact area.
Finally it is suggested that more investigations should be made on the Gradient
Guess trick for iteration reduction to conﬁrm or deny the usefulness of the trick for
this problem.

The numerical solution procedure to solve the problem follows these 10 steps:
1. Deﬁne parameters
2. Deﬁne mesh
3. Prepare system of equations to solve for the displacement rate ﬁeld (D-equations)
and the system of equation to solve for the slip rate ﬁelds (E-equations)
4. Initialize, build and solve system of D-equations
5. Update parameters
6. Compute reaction forces
7. Initialize, build and solve system of E-equations
8. Update parameters
9. Check for convergence – if not reached, return to step 7.
10. If convergence is reached update parameters. Start next incrementation step at
step 4

APPENDIX E
Code clean–up
The handed out code has been used to solve various types of problems. For each
problems diﬀerent parameters and subroutines are needed, and therefore a new type
of problem means adding parameters and subroutines leaving some of the old ones
unused. Many of these unused parameters are still found in the code along with many
old parameters previously used for debugging. As a part of the understanding process
of the code, a general clean–up has been made to get rid of all unused parameters
and sub–routines.
The clean–up also aims to make the code more readable by merging some of the
subroutines into fewer source ﬁles, by adding headings and comments during the code
and by simplifying the code whenever it is found possible.
This Appendix chapter documents the clean–up process made during this master
thesis.

Reducing the number of source ﬁles means tracking of less ﬁles and can give a better
overview of the code. Here it was found that many of the source ﬁles contained only
a single subroutine but had similar purposes to other subroutines.
To reduce the number of source ﬁles, some of the source ﬁles with a similar purpose
is merged into a single source ﬁle. An illustration of the merged source ﬁles can be
