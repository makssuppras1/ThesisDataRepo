Master Thesis
Abstract
Increasingly more tactile sensors are being integrated into visual robotic grasping
systems to increase performance, adaptability, and reliability.
In this thesis, a
tactile-visual pick-and-place method using a robotic manipulator is designed. The
aim of this thesis is, to determine the possible advantages of visual-tactile fusion
and identify and measure the performance of the designed system.
First, a basic system is created that is able to grasp static objects of a known loca-
tion. After which it is upgraded by the implementation of a visual object detector
and the possibility to fuse width sensor measurements obtained by a tactile gripper
and a static camera. Overall, 184 pick-and-place experiments were conducted which
aimed to provide insights into the overall grasping success rate, time required, and
sensor fusion performance.
The experiments demonstrated an overall grasping success rate of 73%, or 86%
when excluding errors caused by the Inverse Kinematics (IK) solver. It also shows
that sensor fusion can achieve a more accurate width estimation compared to the
width measurement of a singular sensor. This, however, depends on the charac-
teristics of the object that is being analyzed. Lastly, the final system design offers
the possibility to easily expand its functionality by introducing more features in the
future, such as a tactile-visual object classification algorithm.

Introduction
In recent years more and more robotic manipulators are deployed in the industry,
services, and inspection tasks. [1] This trend is caused by the current shift to In-
dustry 4.0. Among other things, Industry 4.0 requires production lines to increase
their flexibility and offer individualization on demand. [2] In order to become more
adaptable to the environment, the human perception system offers a convenient
model as an example.
Humans rely on their five senses vision, hearing, touch, olfaction, and taste in order
to interact and explore their environment. [3] When trying to perceive an object a
human will use their vision to classify it and estimate its location. However, when
humans encounter diﬀiculties in distinguishing objects visually, tactile sensing can
help to extract characteristics such as size, weight, and texture. [3][4] Nonetheless,
to humans, sight is generally considered more valuable than touch, which might
be the main reason that touch in the industrial environment has not received the
attention it deserves. [5]
Up to now, visual sensors in robotics have proven to be an effective low-cost method
that is able to extract important visual features such as pose, color, and shape.
Nonetheless, vision-based sensors reach their limits in strong dynamic environments
as their usability is limited to a low sample rate, high sensor delay, and uncertain
measurements. [6] Additionally, grasping systems that rely on solely visual infor-
mation can cause potential damage in force-sensitive tasks, as the visual sensor can
be occluded by the robot arm, or can lack the required image quality due to a lack
of luminosity. [1][7]
Accordingly, in a search of a more adaptable solution, the integration of tactile
sensors into a robotic arm has become a promising option. In doing so, a robotic
grasping system can be able to discriminate among object surface textures, stiffness,
shape, and temperature. [8] The additional extracted feature can then be used to
increase the grasping performance or help classify the object type. However, it is
not straightforward to integrate a tactile sensor as the spatial-temporal patterns of
feature space from multi-modal sensor data are different. [4] Therefore, making it
is diﬀicult to fuse the data acquired from both sensors.
1.1
Project Scope
Although, tactile-visual sensor fusion has been a highly researched field for many
years, there are still challenges that need to be investigated. Accordingly, this re-
search will concentrate on solving the following three research questions:
1. Does a visual-tactile grasping system outperform a visual-only grasping sys-
tem?
2. What indicators are used to measure grasping performance?
3. What additional information does a tactile sensor provide in comparison to a
solely visual grasping process?
Based on the defined research questions, the research objective is defined as follows:

1.2
Sustainability
The aim of the project is to implement a grasping controller for a tactile robotic
gripper which will be merged with a visual path planning controller of a robot arm.
The final system will thereby use sensor fusion to pick up an object from an unknown
location and reduce the amount of uncertainty of the grasping process.
The formulated research goal can be broken up into several research aims. A list of
the research aims can be found below:
• To build an algorithm that is able to fuse tactile-visual sensor data which is
able to provide additional information to the process
• Identify advantages of using a visual-tactile approach compared to a solely
visual approach
• Identify performance indicators in order to evaluate the grasping performance
• To attach the gripper to the robot arm and build an interface that allows
control of the robot arm as well as the gripper
During the course of the project a robotic gripper provided by the company Graspian
Aps will be attached to a 7 Degrees of Freedom (DoF) Franka Emika Panda robot
arm. The hardware will then be wired to a centralized computer running Robot
Operating System (ROS) Noetic and an algorithm is designed that is able to detect,
locate and grasp a static object without damaging it.
Hereby the algorithm will be extended step by step with increasing diﬀiculty. Firstly,
the location of the object is known to the system. Therefore, hardcoded coordinates
can be used to navigate the end effector of the robot arm to the desired location,
after which a grasping command is sent, so that the object can be placed in a new
location. Secondly, the camera will identify the object’s pose and share the infor-
mation with the robot arm which will then grasp the object. This also means that
a new object detector needs to be programmed as the current one is dependent on
an April tag. Finally, the system will be expanded by implementing sensor fusion
techniques such that the uncertainty of the grasping process is reduced or enhanced
information about the grasped object is acquired.
Zooming out into a bigger picture, this project will contribute to the work of other
projects focusing on the area of neural robotics. The general idea is, that the system
that is built during this master thesis will be integrated with a classification model
built by Belén Castellote López that uses the same gripper as in this thesis. For
more information concerning future research please see chapter 6.4.
1.2
Sustainability
When researching new methods, ideas, and systems it becomes increasingly impor-
tant to think about sustainability. It is important because it should be in everyone’s
interest to preserve the quality of life as human beings and the diversity of life on
Earth. The United Nations (UN) has therefore defined seventeen goals to guide
towards a more sustainable world. [9] Those goals are used as an inspiration to

demonstrate how tactile-visual sensor fusion for a robotic grasping process can sup-
port sustainable development.
Overall three sustainability goals are supported by implementing visual-tactile sen-
sor fusion for robotic grippers:
1. Goal 8: Decent work and economic growth
2. Goal 9: Industry, Innovation, and Infrastructure
3. Goal 12: Responsible Consumption and Production
Naturally, innovation in an area will cause this area to be used more frequently. In
this case, more robotic grippers will be deployed in industrial production lines, thus
freeing some workers from repetitive and physically exhausting tasks. Ideally, they
will be promoted instead to a production line supervisor which is controlling the
robotic grippers. Therefore, this trend will support goal 8 by creating decent work
for all.
Additionally, as mentioned in the Background, the trend toward Industry 4.0 re-
quires the need for more flexibility and individualization on production lines thus
increasing the need for robotic manipulators as well. In other words, it will support
goal 9 by supporting sustainable industrialization as well as fostering innovation.
Lastly, fewer robotic arms need to be built as with the new system one arm is more
adaptable than before and thus is able to manipulate more products than before. As
a result, more sustainable production patterns are created which in turn supports
goal 12.

2.1
Design of a Model-Free Predictive Control Network
As mentioned the system that is designed during this project is partially based on
a previously written master thesis named ”A Model-Free Predictive Control Frame-
work for Trajectory Optimisation with a Collaborative Robot” written by Christian
Kampp Kruuse in 2022. [10] Accordingly, it is important to give a short overview
of the designed system and the work that has been done.
The project has implemented two adaptive joint level controllers, one being a model-
based Model Reference Adaptive Controller (MRAC) and the other being a model
free Active Inference Control (AIC) for a Franka Emika Panda robot manipulator.
Both controllers’ implementations were based on [11], but instead of the references
passed to the controllers being constant position references in joint space, time-
varying position and velocity references in joint space are used.
Finally, testing
their relative performance showed that the AIC managed to generally outperform
the MRAC in terms of position reference tracking in task space. [10]
Additionally, the project has implemented a predictive Kalman filter and a TRAC-
IK solver which converts joint space references generated by the predictive Kalman
filter to joint space position references. Although the end effector was able to track
the target, even when the end effector occluded the target object from the camera,
it was still found that the implemented IK-solver started to jitter when it received
multiple requests to find joint space solutions to the same task space pose. This
problem was also observed during this project and is discussed further in Section
5.3.2. [10]
All in all, the system designed was built on a ROS framework which uses two
computers to handle communication between different nodes. The system serves
as a starting point for the system built in this project but was exposed to severe
changes as well. Most importantly, the final system built for this project does rely
on one computer instead of two and has completely removed the implementation of
the Kalman filter, as in this project the objects will remain static, thus no predic-
tive pose estimation needs to be done. Furthermore, as the AIC outperformed the
MRAC, it is used as well for this project.
In theory, any controller that is able to handle time-varying position and velocity
references in real time is suitable for this project. Consequently, using an AIC is
not a necessity for satisfactory performance.

2.2
Tactile Sensors
Tactile sensing is the process of detecting and measuring a given property of a con-
tact event in a predetermined area and subsequent pre-processing of the signals at
the sensor level itself. [8]
Tactile sensors are becoming more relevant over the years as they bring certain ad-
vantages with them that other sensors, such as cameras, do not offer. One straight-
forward advantage is that their performance does not depend on their environment.
[12] Tactile sensors also have the ability to discriminate among surface textures,
stiffness, and temperature. They could even sense incipient slips. [8] Nonetheless,
even though tactile sensing is gaining much more interest than before, it is still lag-
ging behind in comparison to visual sensing. One reason is that in order to achieve
a high spatial localization accuracy, more taxes need to be added to a sensor, which
in return will increase the wiring necessary and thus complicates integration onto
robot end-effectors or other systems. [13] Therefore, it is also preferred to integrate
tactile sensors into systems that are mechanically easier to build. For example, for
robotic grippers, two-fingered grippers are preferred as their mechanical design is
less complex in comparison to end-effectors with more fingers. [14] Another exam-
ple are the fingers of a three-fingered gripper which needs more DoF resulting in
a more obscured structure. [15] By now many tactile sensors have been reported
in the literature exploring different methods of transduction. Some of the methods
to highlight are Resistive/Piezoresistive, based on Tunnel Effect, Capacitive, Opti-
cal, Ultrasonic, Magnetic, and Piezoelectric. [8] The most widely used methods for
robotic applications will be elaborated on in the rest of this section.
Resistive tactile sensors measure the change in resistance of a material for the de-
tection and measurement of contact forces. The change of the resistance hereby
depends on the contact location, the contact force, or the contact pressure. [16] Re-
sistive sensors typically involve two conductive sheets where one is carrying a voltage
gradient. Once pressure is applied and both sheets come into contact the second
sheet will serve like the slider in a linear potentiometer which makes it possible to
find the contact point. However, the clear disadvantages are that it is possible to
only locate one contact point at a time. [8]
Piezoresistive touch sensors are made of materials whose resistance changes with
force/pressure.
[16] It is also the sensor type that has been integrated into the
robotic gripper used in this thesis. Generally, resistive tactile sensors are easy to
construct, their complexity does not increase with the surface area and they can be
produced using inexpensive materials. Nonetheless, they have high power consump-
tion, and integrating multiple sensors does increase the system’s complexity. [16]
Another sensor type that is worth mentioning is the capacitive sensor. It consists
of a plate capacitor, in which, the distance between plates can be changed by an
applied force resulting in a changed capacitance. [8][16] They can be made rela-
tively small so that they are combined into dense sensor arrays. Sometimes they are
employed in robotics to measure contacts over large areas of a robot’s body. [16]
This can be useful when designing a collaborative robot that needs to detect humans
when working side by side. They are able to detect applied normal or tangential
forces but are not able to distinguish between either of them. [16]

Finally, the optical sensor is another alternative for tactile sensing in robotics. Op-
tical techniques may be used to monitor a deflection of a membrane and thereby
provide an output that varies with pressure. [5] The paper [15] provides an exam-
ple of how an optical sensor could be constructed. It uses an inflatable latex film
as the outer surface and a built-in depth camera to detect the deformation of the
elastic inflatable film. Optical-based tactile sensors are flexible, sensitive, and fast
but heavily reliant on the manner in which the membrane distorts as pressure is
applied. [5][16] The construction of an optical sensor is also relatively labor inten-
sive in comparison to the construction of resistive or capacitive tactile sensors. [16]
The gripper used in this thesis actually has one optical sensor per finger which is
intended to detect object slippage. However, due to time issues, they are not being
used during this project.
2.3
Robotic Grasping with tactile and/or visual sensors
Visual-tactile learning dates back to 1984 when it has been used to create surface
descriptions of primitive objects. [13] It has gained interest over the recent years as
the robotic grasping process is facing problems that cannot be solved using visual
sensors only. The application areas in which tactile sensors are being deployed vary
greatly as it is possible to apply them for various use cases. Accordingly, some of
the most relevant ones will be mentioned here.
For example, some studies have used tactile sensors to detect object deformation
as well as object slippage in order to reduce the number of times that an object is
dropped during the grasping process. [14] Similarly, [17] implements tactile sensors
to a grasping system to reshape a 4-fingered robotic hand after making contact with
the object thus increasing the contact area of each finger to the object and therefore
stabilizing the grasp itself. Others use tactile sensors to assist in classifying objects
that cannot be identified using visual sensors only. [14][18] Or to the extreme, [19]
uses tactile sensors only to decide what type of object is being grasped. For this,
tactile sensors were used for contact detection, whilst proprioceptive information
composed by the position of the three fingers from the robotic hand and orientation
was used for object recognition.
Tactile sensors have also been used to design a position adjustment strategy when
grasping objects, improving repeatability and correctness. [20] In the case of a dim,
reflective, smokey environment causing a visual sensor to become ineffective, [15]
has proposed an optical tactile sensor with a large detection range that is able to
detect objects by switching between searching and grasping modes.
Yet again, in another study, it was even attempted to design a full body tactile sens-
ing augmented robot arm that should be deployed for collaborative tasks so that
they can achieve tasks without dealing damage to objects and human workers. [21]

Usually, the sensor fusion process can be categorized into a three-level model dis-
tinguishing low, intermediate, and high-level fusion. [22] The level of sensor fusion
increases with the manipulation that has been done on the data. For example, low-
level sensor fusion combines several sources of raw data in order to produce new
data that is more informative than before. High-level fusion, however, will focus on
complex decision-making that is based on data from several experts. [22]
In general, research has been made on fusing specific raw sensor data and on design-
ing whole frameworks to fuse a wide variety of sensor data. As one can imagine, in a
robotic system, measurements could be retrieved from overhead vision, eye-in-hand
vision, proximity, tactile array, position, force/torque, cross-fire, over-load, and slip-
sensing devices, to name a few. Accordingly, [23] proposes a general approach to
data fusion by segmenting robotic sensors into four stages of data acquisition:
1. Far Away
2. Near To
3. Touching
4. Manipulation
The segmentation made it possible to only access specific sensor data thus improving
performance. Unfortunately, implementing such a system requires a complex system
architecture including multiple units and databases. Appendix A.1 shows the system
configuration proposed by [23]. However, as the implementation would go beyond
the scope of this project it was decided to implement sensor fusion on a smaller scale.
For this project, it is required to identify a data type or measurement which can be
extracted by the gripper and camera. However, both sensors actually vary greatly
in the type of information that they are able to extract meaning they overlap in only
a few measurable data types. This is the reason why, in the end, the width seemed
to be a logical choice for an object feature that can be attempted to be fused within
the time frame of this thesis. It is also desired that the fusion method calculates
a new width estimate based on the certainty of the measurement of each sensor.
Accordingly, an applicable method to provide an estimation would be the Bayesian
approach to statistical inference using conjugate prior distributions. The approach
will shortly be elaborated on in the following section.

A common approach to detect circular objects on an image is by the application
of the RDP algorithm. The RDP algorithm approximates a curve as a series of
points and reduces the number of points. [28] In general, the algorithm will start by
drawing a line between the start and end points of a contour. From there it will try
to find the next point which lies the furthest from the drawn line. It will then draw
a new line to that point if the distance lies within a given threshold after which the
same process is repeated again. It will stop doing so once no eligible point lies in
between the drawn line. Then it continues to draw a new line again until it has
iterated through the whole contour.
As a rectangle can be described with fewer data points than a circle, one can use the
number of data points needed by the RDP algorithm to distinguish between both
of them. Appendix A.2 shows a step-by-step illustration of the functioning of the

3.2.1
Robotic Operating System
As partially already mentioned, ROS has been used as a base framework for this
project. ROS is an open-source middleware that uses software frameworks for robot
software development. ROS can be configured on Ubuntu, Debian, and ROS 2 on
Windows. It also includes low-level device control, hardware simulators, and package
management. Furthermore, ROS includes tools and libraries for compiling, writing,
and executing code on multiple computers. [34] ROS is able to disassemble a robotic
application into different parts called nodes. The nodes will then communicate with
each other over the ROS framework based on a Subscriber/Publisher method.
More precisely, each node is able to constantly publish data to a ROS topic over
the ROS framework. Hereby, a ROS topic is an address where data can be stored.
Other nodes can then extract only the information needed for their specific use case.
A Subscriber/Publisher system is more scalable and versatile as it is not necessary
for the nodes to know one another’s identity. [35]

Design and Implementation
The final goal of this project is to design a system that uses tactile-visual sensor fu-
sion in order to reduce the uncertainty of a grasping process by providing additional
information in an effective manner. Designing such a system is not straightforward
as multiple components/nodes need to be integrated into a communication network.
Therefore, three milestones are defined that add an increasing amount of complexity
to the system by giving more requirements that need to be fulfilled.
Section 4.1 to Section 4.4 will elaborate in detail on the systems that have been de-
signed for this project and how each of them has been implemented. Additionally,
there will be diagrams illustrating, in a simplified way, the decision-making process
and information flow between each node.
4.1

The first system aims to provide a robust framework that will make it easier to
increase its complexity. Therefore, the system’s elemental goal is to pick up a static
object from a known location. In other words, the camera will not be used in this
system, instead, the robot arm will receive instructions on where to go and when to
grab meaning that the system will not be based on any type of feedback. Overall,
three nodes, namely the supervisor, the controller, and the gripper, are used to
accomplish this. Figure 5 gives an overview of how they communicate with each
other.

instructions, send over the topic, remain the same.
The updated supervisor will now start off by sending an initializing command to
the gripper as well as the camera which will then reply as soon as they are ready to
receive commands. Then the supervisor will wait for the pose coordinates of the first
object. Those coordinates are found by the camera and published on a separate topic
called ”/objCoordinates”. Once they have been received, the supervisor will execute
a pick-and-place scenario in the same manner as in the first system. Afterward, it
awaits new object coordinates.
4.2.2
The Object Detector
The object detector’s task is to identify the objects positioned on the conveyor belt
and share their pose with the supervisor. In order to accomplish this, the library
Open Source Computer Vision Library (OpenCV) has been used. OpenCV is an
open-source computer vision and machine learning software library. It is mainly
aimed at real-time image processing as it attempts to offer a common infrastructure
for computer vision applications. [36]
Image preprocessing

first remove noises and irrelevant areas from the process. Furthermore, it is assumed
that the system will only be deployed in static environments where the position of
the conveyor belt and camera remains static. This assumption makes it possible to
apply some permanent changes to the raw image.

only the area of the black conveyor belt is visible while everything else is overlapped

Object filtering
The features extracted during the image processing step are then used to filter out
some of the objects which are not of interest to the object detector. More specifically,
the following filters have been used:
• A filter that is based on the horizontal position of each contour thus removing
objects located at the start or end of the conveyor belt
• A filter that is based on the area of each contour which removes objects larger
than a certain area
• A filter which is based on the vertical length of each contour thus removing
objects that cross a certain height
• A filter that is based on the horizontal and vertical position of each contour
removing objects that are close to the base of the robot arm
The applied filters made sure that most of the time the objects found on the con-
veyor belt are actually objects and not occlusions by humans around the work area
or the robot arm itself.
Visual object orientation detection
Some of the objects that are being tested for the system have a rectangular shape.
That also implies that the orientation of the gripper with respect to the orientation
of the object has a large influence on the performance of the grasp. As a result, a
system has been implemented that uses the contours of the objects to extract the
orientation of each rectangle.
Firstly, the object needs to be analyzed on whether it is a circle or not. For this, a
function called ”approxPolyDP” provided by OpenCV can be used which uses the
RDP algorithm to approximate the contour of an object. As described in Section
2.5, the algorithm will require more data points in order to approximate the con-
tour of a circle than of a rectangle. Accordingly, only the object whose number of
required data points for a contour approximation lies below a certain threshold (for
this project the threshold was set to 10) will be fed into a function that calculates
the object’s orientation. If the object’s length of the contour approximation is larger
than ten, its default orientation will be set to 0.0.
The base algorithm used to extract the orientation has been designed by [37] but
slightly adjusted to fit the requirements of the system. The implementation uses

4.4
Tactile object detection
During the process of designing and testing the system, it has occurred multiple
times that the gripper has tried to grab an object but while closing the fingers the
object slipped away and rolled slightly to the side. It also happened that the ob-
ject fell down while it was being transported as the gripper’s grasp was not strong
enough to actually hold the object in place.
However, a lost object was impossible to detect by the camera’s object detector
as the robot arm itself was occluding the view of the camera. Additionally, the
supervisor is not constantly updating the pose of the object, instead, it is retriev-
ing one pose estimation by the visual object detector which it will then move to.
Consequently, the system needed to be expanded with a feature that is able to
verify if an object has been grabbed and if the object fell down during the trans-
portation process. Therefore, it was decided to implement a tactile object detector
that is able to use the tactile sensor measurements of the robotic gripper to verify
if an object is attached to the fingers during the grasping and transportation process.
In order to realize such a system, one needs to monitor if a taxel is currently mea-
suring any force. Therefore, the gripper node has been expanded with a function
called ”isObjAttached” that is being activated while the gripper is closed. The func-
tion takes in an array of fourteen tactile measurements and iterates through them,
verifying that there is a taxel measuring a force larger than 0.005. If during the last
twenty measurements, ten of them have registered an object the function will return
true, telling the gripper that an object is currently attached.
Unfortunately, each taxel is influenced by process and environmental noise resulting
in unreliable registered force fluctuations making it necessary to monitor a series
of measurements. It is not uncommon that if the pressure sensor of the taxel is
activated only slightly that the actual pressure measured will jump in between val-
ues, one of them being 0.0 Newton.
Thus it was required to track a history of
measurement values so that fluctuations can be filtered out. As a consequence, the
implementation had a negative effect on the grasp as it prolonged its duration. For
more information concerning the time required, see section 5.3.4. Lastly, the gripper
node sends two different messages to the supervisor if the method ”isObjAttached”
returned false.
”No object found”
The message is sent when a grasp is being performed, however, once it is finished no
object has been detected as the object most probably slipped away, the arm missed

the object, or the pose estimation failed.
”The object fell down”
The message is sent when a grasp has been performed and an object has been reg-

4.4.1
The Gaussian
After this project is finished it will be merged with a classification algorithm that
is able to provide information about the type of the object at each position on the
conveyor belt. It will also be possible to differentiate between similar-looking ob-
jects such as full and empty cans using visual-tactile sensor fusion. Nonetheless,
merging both projects would exceed the scope of this thesis which is why a more
straightforward method of visual tactile sensor fusion was chosen.
As described in Section 2.4, sensor fusion refers to systems that use information
acquired from multiple sensors to enhance system performance. [38] This project,
attempts to use sensor fusion to reduce the measurement uncertainty of an inter-
secting feature, measurable by both systems, namely the camera and the gripper.
In this case, this intersecting feature is the object width. It is arguable if the grip-
per’s width measurement is actually connected to the tactile sensor as the gripper
retrieves the measurement by reading a value of the motor’s odometer. Nonetheless,
the position at which the fingers stop, when grasping an object, is dependent on the
accuracy of the tactile sensors as the grasping process comes to a stop once a certain
pressure is reached (see Section 3.1.3).
Before a system is implemented, it is crucial to decide based on which factors the
measurements should be fused. There exist many different approaches how to fuse
sensor data which vary greatly in level of complexity. In this case, a system is de-
sired that is able to take the certainty of the measurement into consideration such
that uncertain measurements are trusted less. Consequently, fusing sensor data,
based on Bayes’s theorem, was the most fitting method.
As described in Section 2.4, using this method makes it possible to calculate two
Gaussian distributions which will be merged into a new one. Hereby the variance of
each Gaussian distribution is representative of the uncertainty of the measurement
and the mean is representative of the average measured value.
In order to realize the new system, a new ROS-node has been introduced to the
network with the sole purpose to retrieve both measurements, calculate a better
estimate and visualize it. Because the node is working with Gaussian distributions,
as a naming convention, it will be referred to as ”the Gaussian” for the rest of this

5.1
Experimental setup
The experimental setup chosen to test the system, that was designed for this project,
aims to provide information concerning its performance. The objects chosen to test
the system have already been described in chapter 3.1.4.
During the design of the system, it was noticeable that the gripper’s performance
is reliable on the object’s location on the conveyor belt. Accordingly, four testing
areas have been introduced from which objects will be grabbed. Each area is 30cm
by 19.2cm whereas area 1 is located to the left of the robot arm and area 4 is located
to the right of the robot arm. It is not possible to use the whole conveyor belt as
the robot arm is not able to physically reach the far left side.
Overall, three sets of experiments have been designed. Experiment set 1 (Section
5.3) focuses on grasping a single object placed on the conveyor belt, experiment set
2 (Section 5.4) targets multiple objects placed on the conveyor belt, and experiment
set 3 (Section 5.5) tries to test fragile objects that also have not been tested before
and thus require a certain sensibility of the gripper.
5.2
Performance metrics
Before the experiments can be designed it is important to define factors on which
the system’s performance will be measured. In order to do so, one has to look at
the reasons why robots are being deployed in the industry in the first place. Most
of the time, a robot offers higher reliability as well as consistency. Robots are also
faster and once acquired, cheaper to keep running. Finally, a robot can work nearly
without interruptions.
Therefore, one metric that will be tracked is the time needed to pick and place
an object. More precisely, an automated timer will start once all the nodes are ini-
tialized and stop once the gripper opens when placing an object. If the object would
fall down during the process the timer would keep running as it did not successfully
finish its task. Depending on the application area, the system performs satisfactorily
if the task does not require longer than 20s −60s
Next, it will be tracked if the task has been completed successfully or failed. If the
task failed, a reason will be provided in the form of a short note. As the success
quote might depend on other factors, namely the object or the area the object has
been placed in, they will be tracked as well. The system has a satisfactory success
percentage if more than 80% of the experiments finish successfully.
The last metric that is tracked is the object width. This metric is established to
test the performance of the Gaussian node. Accordingly, it is decided to track the

and how can it be improved in the future?
First of all, an object needs to be grasped at its widest position to get an accu-
rate width measurement. This is relatively simple for rectangular objects, thus the
low error on the wood block, but more diﬀicult on round objects. Here the gripper
needs to be positioned perfectly, which however is not given all the time. The object
detector will only share one coordinate with the robot arm and assumes that the
object will not move while the gripper approaches it. Obviously, there is no guar-
antee for this especially since round objects might roll away when even the slightest
vibration is felt. So fixing the position of the round object might already improve
the measurement substantially.
Secondly, the gripper’s accuracy is dependent on the softness of the object. As al-
ready mentioned in section 4.4.1, softer objects will be squeezed more before the
width measurement is published, thus the larger error of the sponge. To fix this,
one needs to characterize the object type and consequently adjust the grasping force
beforehand. This idea will be elaborated on in more detail in the upcoming section
5.5.
Finally, the gripper width sensor/odometer should be calibrated again. As all mea-
surements are lower than estimated, it is possible to partially correct them by adding
an amount to each measurement. In this case, around 2.9mm at most as this was
the lowest measurement error.
Also, the camera has a substantial measurement error due to mainly two reasons.
The first reason is that the camera needed to be positioned far away from the con-
veyor belt as otherwise, it would have been within the reach of the robot arm. As a
consequence, objects placed on the conveyor belt are seen as relatively small inside
of the camera’s frame. Even though the BlueFox3 camera offers a high resolution,
each object’s width in pixels will still be low making it more diﬀicult to retrieve
good measurements.
Implementing a second camera closer to the conveyor belt
might therefore improve the measurements.
Second and lastly, as described in section 4.2.2, multiple filters are applied to the
image frame in order to detect the contour of an object. Some of them are dilation,
erosion, and Gaussian filter methods to remove background noise. Even though
those methods are extremely effective to filter out objects of interest, they also fal-
sify the actual image. Especially, dilation methods tend to round off contours which
is no problem for round contours but critical for any other. This also is the main
reason for the large measurement error on rectangular objects. To fix this error, the
object detector needs to be further refined, or potentially other more viable image

Bad width estimate for rectangular objects
When analyzing the result it becomes obvious that the calculated estimated width
for rectangular objects is actually worse than the width measurements obtained by
the gripper. In order to explain how this is possible, one has to look at the variances
obtained by the camera. As just mentioned, the far placement of the camera will
result in a small pixel width for each contour. This means that width measurements
are influenced less by small environmental changes such as reflections or vibrations,
thus producing a consistent width output. Primarily, this behavior might seem good
to the reader but looking back at the design of the Gaussian node in section 4.4.1,
it becomes evident that this is not the behavior desired for the system to work.
More clearly, if the Gaussian retrieves width measurements that are consistent, it
will calculate a mean with a variance that approaches zero. This also means that
the width measurement of the camera is trusted more shifting the estimated width
towards the measurement of the camera.
However, the width measurements re-
trieved by the camera for rectangular objects, are actually far from the truth but
the Gaussian still trusts them due to the low variance.
This behavior clearly shows a major weakness of the sensor fusion approach taken.
Once the gripper is able to characterize objects, thus using individual variances per
object as well, the behavior will become less problematic but still persist. Therefore,
it is suggested to use another closer camera to estimate the width of the object so
that it will be easier for the camera to detect fluctuation in the measurement.

Discussion
The following section will give an overview of the possible application areas of the
built tactile-visual grasping system in the industry (Section 6.1) followed by its
limitations (Section 6.2). As mentioned in the Literature review many other papers
have built similar but not identical systems using visual-tactile grasping methods.
Accordingly, this section will provide an abstract comparison to some of the ideas
that have been developed by other researchers (Section 6.3). Lastly, possible future
steps to further develop and improve this system are given (Section 6.4).
6.1
Potential application areas
Considering the autonomous grasping system build during this project, one of the
most straightforward application areas is to deploy it on a production line where
objects need to be picked and placed. This however can be done by many other
robotic grippers which do not require tactile sensors. Therefore, one has to specialize
further.
Accordingly, as also tested in the third experimental series, deploying the system in
an environment where fragile objects need to be to be manipulated can be especially
useful. For example, this could be a production line that is manipulating organic
products such as fruits or vegetables. Other objects could be ones that can easily
crack if too much pressure is applied namely thin glasses, circuit boards, ceramics,
or porcelain.
Generally, the built system also offers a higher degree of adaptability in comparison
to most production lines deployed in the industry. Many production lines currently
can only work with one specific material in particular as each step of the pick and
place procedure is hard coded. This system, however, could potentially grab much
more objects which only need to fulfill the limitations given. It could also filter
out objects of specific characteristics such as too-large objects, and circular objects,
or fix the orientation of wrongly placed objects. Even though filtering in this way
would require some more programming, the basic detection methods have been
implemented already. Additionally, the node framework that has been built makes
it easy to integrate new functionalities as the supervisor can control all other nodes
as a centralized entity.
6.2
Limitations
As with every other system also the one built during this project does have some
limitations. Some of them have been already mentioned but this section is intended
to provide them in a more general way.
First of all, the object that is being grabbed is limited to a given size. In gen-
eral, one can say that the maximum width of an object should not be larger than
10cm, as well as the minimum height, should not be less than 4cm, the maximum
height should not be more than 8cm. If any of those object characteristics are vio-
lated the gripper might get stuck or will be unable to grasp the object in general.
There are already some filters inside of the object detector that prevents the robot
Page 41 of 45
==PAGE:56==

manipulator from detecting those objects, although the object height will always
remain a problem due to the missing depth recognition of the camera. Additionally,
the objects are limited by their weight. Even though there have not been any ex-
periments to test this limit, it is certain that if the weight of an object will increase
enough the gripper will fail to lift it, as it no longer offers enough grip and/or force.
It is recommended to only lift objects which weigh below 1kg.
Subsequently, the object needs to fulfill certain visual aspects. More specifically, the
object cannot be black or translucent. This is because the object detector is only
able to detect objects due to their color contrast compared to the black conveyor
belt. If the contrast is not given the object might not be visible at all or its contour
will fluctuate in size greatly causing the center of mass to shift rapidly as well as
falsifying the width measurement of the object detector.
Next, certain aspects of the environment cannot be changed. More specifically, as
it should be clear to the reader, the hardware and software need to remain the same
because the system has been built specifically for that. However, this project also
assumes a static position of each node, thus one cannot change the relative position
of the camera, the conveyor belt, or the robot arm. Otherwise, the object detector
would analyze the wrong region and possibly send the wrong object coordinates to
the supervisor.
Finally, environmental conditions such as lightning conditions, fog, or dust in the
air are expected to remain on a similar level as during the experiments. If lightning
conditions change objects might become more reflective or not visible at all thus
creating occlusion for the object detector. The same principle goes for a change in
the amount of fog or dust inside of the air.

A lot of research about grasping objects using tactile and/or visual sensors has been
done in recent years. Nonetheless, since the grasping process can specialize in many

significantly, as a region of 6x6 areas needs to be searched one by one. Furthermore,

force thus being unable to safely manipulate fragile objects.

6.4
Future Work
In the future, it is possible to further increase the performance of the system pro-
posed in this project as well as implement more features. The main improvements
that can be done concerning the system’s performance focus on the time required
and the object detection.
As mentioned in Section 5.3.4, the time required to pick and place objects is unsat-
isfactory. The reason for this is because of the way that some processes need to be
finished first before others can start. Generally, this feature provides more reliability
and safety although it is not necessary to use it for all processes. For example, one
could try to constantly retrieve tactile sensor measurements instead of requesting
them only when they are required. Sending those requests slows down the grasping
process significantly which forces the supervisor to wait until the data is received.
Furthermore, the transportation process can be accelerated as well. Currently, the
robot manipulator is required to pass multiple waypoints which do not let the arm
continue if the error margin between each waypoint coordinates has not been below
a specified distance. This feature is again a necessity during specific phases of the
program like when the end-effector is approaching an object or dropping it down.
However, it is being used too frequently when an object is transported from one
location to another.
Lastly, another student called Belén Castellote López is working in parallel to
this project on her master thesis called ”Vision-tactile object classification through
FasterRCNN and machine learning algorithms for robotic grasping”. She has de-
signed a system that is able to classify objects based on their shape and object

The idea is that after both projects will end, they will be merged into one system.

Future Work
This way it should be possible to classify objects that are placed on the conveyor
belt and adjust certain parameters, like the variance of the Gaussian or the force
at which an object is grasped. It should also be possible to differentiate between
objects that visually look similar to each other but have different properties. This

7
Conclusion
In this thesis, a tactile-visual sensing system has been designed with the aim to re-
duce the uncertainty of the robotic grasping process. Multiple hardware components
were combined to build the system, namely a tactile gripper provided by Graspian,
a collaborative Emika Franka Panda robot arm, and a BlueFox3 mono camera. Us-
ing a ROS based interface each component was then assigned to a ROS-node which
allowed the construction of a network where a supervisor, hosted on a university
computer was able to navigate and control each process in a decentralized manner.
Furthermore, width measurements by the camera and gripper were collected by a
fourth ROS node called ”the Gaussian”, which calculated a new width estimate of
the objects grasped.
In order to test the system’s performance, three experimental series consisting of
overall 184 pick-and-place trials, are designed. Each of these aimed to provide data
concerning specific areas of interest. More specifically, experimental series 1 focused
on grasping single objects placed on the conveyor belt, experimental series 2 focused
on grasping multiple objects, and the last experimental series focused on grasping
fragile objects. The five objects available for grasping during the first and second ex-
perimental series were a tennis ball, a softball, a Christmas ornament, a woodblock,
and a sponge. The objects available for grasping during the third experimental se-
ries were a banana, an egg, and a tomato.
The experimental series showed that the system is performing robustly with an
average success rate of 81.33% in specific areas of the conveyor belt, namely areas 2
to 4, and on all objects with an average success rate of 87.85% except for the sponge.
The most common observed failure reason was due to the TRAC-IK solver as it was
unable to find joint solutions for specific end effector locations. However, failures
that occur because of the IK-solver fall out of the scope of this research thus it was
decided that they are excluded from the results. This consequently increased the
average success rate of all areas to 85.5%. The sponge failed mainly because it was
unstable and sometimes too soft to be detected by the tactile sensors of the gripper.
Furthermore, fusing tactile-visual width measurements gave a more accurate width
estimate for round objects but less accurate estimates for rectangular ones. The
reason for this can be traced back to the low variance of the camera. The low vari-
ance resulted in the Gaussian node believing that the width measurement of the
camera is of high accuracy. This, however, was not the case, instead, the camera
was not detecting small variations in the measurement caused by the far position of
the camera with respect to the objects and the image processing techniques used.
Finally, implementing tactile sensors made it possible to detect a lost object which
as a consequence improved the system’s performance.
