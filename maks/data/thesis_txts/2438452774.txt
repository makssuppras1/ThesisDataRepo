Abstract
Even though vibrations are present in everyday life and are often fundamental for the human
being, they are not so desired in the ﬁeld of mechanical engineering. In fact, in general, they
often lead to undesired eﬀects such as noise or even the catastrophic failure of the structure
subjected to them. For this reason during the last decades’ methods to identify the structural
behaviour of a structure have been developed. One of the techniques used to identiﬁes modal
parameters, natural frequencies, damping ratios and mode shapes of the structure is called
Experimental Modal analysis also known as EMA.
The aim of this thesis is therefore to present the theory behind EMA and its application to
ﬂexible structures. The techniques are applied at ﬁrst to a small windmill structure in the
laboratory using diﬀerent source of excitation such as an impulsive hummer and a magnetic
shaker. The results are used to understand the behaviour of the structure and to validate a
mathematical model.
The concepts are at the end applied to a real industrial case, a ﬂywheel energy storage system
of Watts Up Power -Mærsk.

1. EXPERIMENTAL MODAL ANALYSIS
1
Experimental Modal analysis
The focus of Experimental Modal Analysis, EMA, is to identify natural frequencies, mode
shapes and damping ratios of a particular structure using experimental vibration measurements.
The fundamental physical phenomenon used to predict the dynamical behaviour of a structure
and to determine its modal parameters is that If the system driving frequency matches the one
of resonance the amplitude of the vibration increases and the response shifts by 180 degrees.
In the context of this thesis, experimental modal analysis will be used to predict and understand
the system behaviour and to verify and validate a mathematical model. A third goal that can
be achieved using EMA is to determine the durability of the design under certain conditions or
for diagnostic and maintenance.
The three central assumptions that need to be considered while conducting the experiments
are presented.
• The structure can be modelled as a lumped-parameter model
• The structure has a linear behaviour satisfying the properties Homogeneity, additivity
and shift invariance.
• The damping ratio of the system is less than 0.5
The assumption of linear behaviour is fundamental. In fact in signal processing is a standard
approach to break down a signal in simple components, process them individually and reunite
the results. This divide and conquer strategy is called superposition, and it is valid just if the
system is linear. Therefore a complicated problem can be braked down in many easy ones just
if this critical assumption is valid.
1.1
Theory Behind EMA
To understand the theory that lies behind EMA it is crucial to recapitulate some concepts of
Statistic, Vibrations and digital processing theory. These concepts will be used to deﬁne the
idea of the response of a system. It will be shown that if the reaction of a system is determined,
then the whole behaviour of the structure can be obtained.
1.2
Impulse
The introduction to this chapter introduces the concept of superposition that breaks down the
signal into simple components called impulses. The subdivided signal is then analysed and
reassembled using the convolution math. At ﬁrst, it is therefore essential to deﬁne what an
impulse is and what are its mathematical properties. Physically an impulse can be interpreted
as a large force suddenly applied and removed to a structure. This force generates a change in
the momentum of the structure itself.

The sampling and analysing process can lead to two common errors in digital processing:
Aliasing and Leakage.
Aliasing
Aliasing is connected with the sampling rate. If it is too slow to catch the details of the analogue
signal the digital representation will cause high frequencies to appear as low ones. To avoid
aliasing Shannon sampling theorem is commonly used. This theorem states that ∆t(Sample
rate) must be chosen small enough to provide at least two samples per cycle of the highest
frequency to be calculated. Often an antialiasing ﬁlter (a low pass ﬁlter is used). What it does
is cutting the frequencies higher than about half the max frequency of interest denoted by ωmax
(Nyquist Frequency).
Leakage
Another common error that might occur while sampling is leakage. Leakage arises when the
signal is cut unproperly. In fact, a periodic signal is sampled over a ﬁnite time N. The best
way to prevent this would be to cut oﬀthe signal in one of the multiples. Unfortunately, there
is no way of doing this, and the risk of cutting in mid-period is high. This causes erroneous
frequencies to appear in the digital representation because the digital Fourier transform of the
ﬁnal length signal assumes that the signal is periodic within the sample record length; this
means that the signal will leak into some ﬁctitious frequencies.

2. MATHEMATICAL MODELS
2
Mathematical models
In the following sections, a mathematical model for the small windmill presented in section
1.3.1 is built and validated. The assumption used to simplify the model are:
• The masses are considered as lumped masses. This means neglecting the rotational de-
grees of freedom and the inertia of a rigid body.
• The mass of the beams is added to the one of the platforms and tip masses of the blades.
• A linear spring stiﬀness is assumed for the beam bending. Therefore the deﬂection of the
beams has to be small compared to the beam length.
• The model can be analysed in the XY-plane. In fact, the stiﬀens in the z-direction and
y-direction is large and therefore the relative displacement is neglectable.
• The mass and inertia of the accelerometers mounted on the tip masses of the blades are
neglected
• Each blade has a beam that extends after the tip mass. The inertia and the mass of these
beams are neglected.
Eliminating some of the assumptions mentioned above it is possible to subdivide the modelling
into two sub-models.
The ﬁrst model considers all the assumption listed above and is called Lumped Model because all
the masses are considered as lumped masses. Comparing the experimental and analytical results
it will be possible to see that this model is not suﬃcient to describe the physical properties of
the structure.
Therefore a second model that eliminates the assumptions of lumped masses has been investi-
gated. It considers the Inertia of the platforms and the tip masses of the Blades and is called the
Inertia model. This model was further studied eliminating ﬁrst the assumption that neglects
the masses of the accelerometers and then the one that neglects the ending part of the blades.

6. EXPERIMENTAL AND ANLAYTICAL RESULTS COMPARISON
6
Experimental and Anlaytical Results Comparison
To explain the behaviour of the obtained natural frequencies, it is better to describe at ﬁrst
the mode shapes related to them. From ﬁgure 32 almost all the modes calculated using the
mathematical model are matching the experimental ones. The only mode that presents large
discrepancy is the ﬁrst one. This is because the coherence associated with this mode is almost
always close to zero as can be seen from ﬁgure ﬁgure15 and ﬁgure 16. It has been tried to
increase the accuracy of the coherence without succeeding. For low frequencies, the coherence
is always close to zero.
It is now possible to proceed and compare the experimental natural frequencies with the ones
obtained analytically. To see the discrepancy between the analytical and experimental value the
percentage error is calculated. Table 10 presents all the obtained frequencies. From the results,
it can be seen that, excluding mode 1, the frequencies related to the mode of the structure
are the one with the smallest error while for the ones related to the blades the discrepancy
assumes a high value. This error is drastically reduced increasing the complexity of the model.
Unfortunately passing from the lumped model the error of the last two frequencies slightly
increases. Furthermore, the fourth frequency pass from an error of the 39 % to one of the 70
%.
The reason behind the inaccuracy of the ﬁrst natural frequency lies again in the coherence of
the experimental data while the inaccuracy of the blades resides in the fact that the system
might be non-linear. In fact, when the system is placed in resonance, the blades are starting
moving with an high amplitude.
Even though the error is quite high, it can be seen that overall it tends to decrease increasing
the complexity of the model.

7. CONCLUSIONS AND SUGGESTION FOR FUTURE WORK
7
Conclusions and suggestion for future work
Through this thesis, it has been possible to learn and apply theory and techniques behind the
experimental modal analysis. These tools have been used ﬁrst to understand the behaviour of a
ﬂexible and low damped structure and after to the industrial case of a ﬂywheel energy storage
system.
The experiments have been used to understand the physical behaviour of the structure and to
validate mathematical models that describe it.
Furthermore conducting experiments on ﬂexible structures, it has been possible to realise how
non -linearity increases the complexity of data extraction. As a future project, it would be
therefore beneﬁcial to create a non-linear model of the windmill structure. This model should
aim to ﬁt the experimental natural frequencies presented in section 6 with better accuracy.
