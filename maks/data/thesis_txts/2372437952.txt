Abstract 
Different products of dry-aged cod were developed with reduced salt content and added pure ethanol 
or alcoholic beverages (ouzo, Pernod and gin). Dry-aged cod was produced from thawed raw cod by 
brining for 24-48 h at 2 °C and dry-aging in semipermeable polymer film bags (Tublin 5, Tub-Ex, 
Denmark) for 72 h at 2 °C. Different sample sizes as well as brine compositions and application methods 
were tested. Microbiological, sensory and chemical changes were evaluated in the products over time. 
Sensory analyses were carried out to assess the effect of salt reduction and addition of alcohols. The 
highest scores in overall impression were given to products with anise-flavoured beverages. Beverages 
induced changes in colour and shine of the products especially when alcohols were added together 
with the brine. Evaporation via absorbent pad to the headspace mitigated these unfavourable effects. 
Products with ethanol or beverages also maintained their quality over time. Products with reduced salt 
to 1.8 % WPS gave better overall sensory scores than products with 2.5 % WPS or higher. 
Pure ethanol and alcoholic beverages were used in the products with the aim to inhibit growth of 
naturally occurring bacteria and inoculated human pathogen Listeria monocytogenes. In challenge 
tests, a significant (P<0.05) antimicrobial effect of pure ethanol and Pernod was established against 
naturally occurring bacteria, lactic acid bacteria and L. monocytogenes. Conducted Bioscreen C assay 
indicated stronger antimicrobial effect of pure ethanol against Lactobacillus sakei (MIC=48.4) than 
against L. monocytogenes (MIC=93.0). Chemical changes induced by alcohols were not observed. 
Alcohol preservation of dry-aged cod showed positive antimicrobial effects and positive sensory 
properties. Before being able to reduce salt in commercial seafood products by replacing some of it 
with alcohols, more research must be carried out to document ethanol’s antimicrobial effect against 
relevant human pathogens, most importantly against Clostridium botulinum.

1. Introduction 
Seafood is contributing to food supply throughout the world by being a source of food, nutrition, 
income and livelihoods for hundreds of millions of people. Nowadays even more people are turning to 
seafood as a healthy alternative for red meat (Huss 1994). The world fish supply has steadily increased 
since 1950 (Figure 1). However, since 1990 the rise in capture fisheries production has slowed down 
and aquaculture has increasingly contributed to the total supply. The world capture production was 
93.4 million tonnes and aquaculture production 73.8 million tonnes in 2014 equalling 167.2 million 
tonnes fish supply in total. Most of this fish (146.3 million tonnes) went for human consumption but 
some also for non-food uses. Of the fish not used as food, 76 % was reduced to fishmeal and fish oil 
and the rest was utilized for a variety of purposes. Utilization of the by-products is becoming an 
increasingly important industry. Per capita food fish supply in 2014 was 20.1 kg measured as whole 
fish weight (FAO 2016) (Appendix 1). 
 
Figure 1. World capture fisheries and aquaculture production 1950-2014 (FAO 2016). 
Seafood has an important role as a source of proteins, minerals, essential trace elements, fat-soluble 
vitamins and essential fatty acids (EFAs), predominantly long-chain omega-3 polyunsaturated acids 
(PUFAs). Many studies suggest that aquatic food and omega-3 PUFAs have beneficial effects on human 
health (Ryder et al. 2014). However, consumption of seafood can also lead to food safety hazards, 
caused by e.g. microorganisms or toxins. Elevated level of substances used for preservation of seafood 
can also create risk of chronic diseases (Ryder et al. 2014; WHO 2012).  
Nowadays noncommunicable diseases (NCDs), also known as chronic diseases, are the primary 
contributor to mortality and morbidity in the world. Elevated sodium consumption has been associated 
==PAGE:11==

with several NCDs including stroke, cardiovascular diseases and hypertension (WHO 2012). Today, over 
99.2 % of the adult population in the world are consuming more sodium than is physiologically 
necessary. This is the case also in Denmark, where the population is consuming much more than the 
current World Health Organization (WHO) recommendation on sodium consumption for adults, which 
is 2 g sodium/day (equivalent to 5 g salt/day) (Mozaffarian et al. 2015; WHO 2012).  
Interventions to reduce the burden of NCDs are highly cost effective (WHO 2012). In Denmark it has 
been estimated that a reduction of the daily intake of salt by 3 g per person could prevent 1300 cardio 
vascular fatalities annually and reduce healthcare costs by 1-2 billion DKK (Fødevarestyrelsen 2016). 
Processed and ready-to-eat (RTE) foods contribute approximately 80 % of the salt that the population 
consumes (James et al. 1987). Based on that background, authorities and retailers are increasingly 
challenging the food industry for development and production of foods with a reduced content of salt. 
Yet, reduction of salt in seafood is challenging. It requires knowledge about changes in sensory 
properties and the risks related to growth of spoilage and pathogenic microorganisms (WHO 2013). 
2. Objective 
The present study was part of a larger Danish research program with the overall objective to develop 
seafood products with improved human health effects, sensory quality and food safety. The specific 
objective of the present project was to develop and evaluate salt-reduced dry-aged cod products by 
using pure ethanol or alcoholic beverages (ABs) as alternative types of preservation. Within the 
project, alcohols were used to replace some of the salt in dry-aged products and the changes in 
microbiological, chemical and sensory properties were assessed over time. The study discusses the 
following questions. 
• 
Whether ethanol and pure ethanol and ABs can be used as alternative types of preservation 
to replace some of the salt in seafood? 
• 
How do ethanol and ABs affect to the sensory, chemical and microbiological quality of the final 
product? 
• 
Can ethanol and ABs be used as effective and affordable preservatives in industrial production 
of safe and high quality seafood? 
3. Literature review 
3.1. Health effects of seafood 
Seafood has a crucial role as a source of nutrients. Many compounds with high nutritional value are 
only found in seafood. Seafood provides high-quality protein, minerals, essential trace elements, fat-
==PAGE:12==
Literature review 
 
3 
 
soluble vitamins and essential fatty acids (EFAs), principally omega-3 PUFAs. Human, and mammals in 
general, can synthesize saturated and unsaturated fatty acids, but are unable to synthesize PUFAs 
efficiently (Luten 2009). 
EFAs cannot be synthesized efficiently in the human body due to the lack of the desaturase enzyme 
(Figure 2). The enzyme inserts a double bond to the carbon chain to form omega-3 PUFAs. Both, 
eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), can be synthesized in the liver, but this 
occurs at a very low rate. 0.01–8 % of alpha linolenic acid (ALA) can be converted to EPA, and even less 
to DHA (Flock et al. 2013). Due to low conversion efficiency, consumption of seafood, naturally rich in 
EPA and DHA, can bring beneficial health effects. 1-30 % of the total lipid content in fish consists of 
PUFAs (Love 1988). In the aquatic environment, these fatty acids are produced originally by 
microphytes and then transferred efficiently through the food chain via zooplankton to fish (Vlieg & 
Body 1988). The lipid content of in wet tissue of Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) is approximately 0.7 % 
in white muscle and 1.8 % in dark muscle (Love 1988). 
 
Figure 2. Classes of essential fatty acids and their sources (Higdon 2003). 
Many studies suggest that aquatic food and omega-3 PUFAs have beneficial effects on human health. 
The studies include clinical trials in humans, together with supportive retrospective, ecological, 
==PAGE:13==
Literature review 
 
4 
 
metabolic and experimental animal studies (Ryder et al. 2014). According to European Food Safety 
Authority (EFSA) relationships have been established for DHA and its effect on blood triglycerides, 
blood pressure, brain function and vision (EFSA Panel on Dietetic Products & Allergies 2011). There is 
also convincing evidence that seafood consumption reduces the risk of death from cardiovascular 
diseases (CVDs) and that consumption by women reduces the risk of suboptimal neurodevelopment 
in their children (Ryder et al. 2014). 
3.2. Health effects of salt-reduced food 
Salt, primarily consisting of sodium chloride, has been used by human for thousands of years. It has 
traditionally been used to preserve foods. Seafood, being susceptible to microbial activity, has been 
often preserved by salting. Salt has provided a way for people to extend shelf-life of foodstuffs and use 
them during the times of food shortage. Salt gives also positive sensory properties to the food and 
saltiness is recognized as one of the basic human tastes. Salty flavour of food is easy to get used to, 
and therefore it is easy to add more salt to the food than is needed (He et al. 2011; Mozaffarian et al. 
2015).  
After it was discovered that salt could prevent growth of microorganisms in food, the use of salt 
increased rapidly. The use of salt peaked at the time of the industrial revolution in the 1870s and it had 
a major economic importance as a traded and taxed commodity (He et al. 2011). Invention of freezer 
and refrigerator reduced the use of salt. Nowadays the consumption of salt is increasing again due to 
the popularity of highly salted processed foods. It has been estimated that as much as up to 80 % of 
the salt consumption today is from processed foods (He et al. 2011; James et al. 1987). 
The large increase in salt intake is rather recent from the evolutionary point of view. It creates a major 
challenge to the human excretory system to get rid of these large amounts of salt through the kidneys. 
Kidneys are unable to filtrate high amounts of salt which leads to gradual rise in blood pressure and 
thereby increasing the risk of CVD and renal disease. CVDs alone contribute 62 % of all the strokes, 49 
% of the coronary heart disease and can also lead to myocardial infarction (MI) or heart failure (He et 
al. 2011). It has been estimated that a reduction of 6 g/day in salt intake would prevent approximately 
2.5 million deaths worldwide (He & MacGregor 2003). 
Nowadays, over 99.2 % of the adult population in the world are consuming more sodium than is 
physiologically necessary. This is the case also in Denmark, where the population is consuming much 
more than the present World Health Organization (WHO) recommendation on sodium consumption 
for adults, which is 2 g sodium/day (equivalent to 5 g salt/day) (Mozaffarian et al. 2015; WHO 2012). 
Nowadays, the salt intake in many countries is between 9 and 12 g/day (WHO 2013).

3.2.1. Salt reduction in Denmark 
Due to the negative health effect of high salt consumption, many countries have adopted coherent 
and workable strategies to reduce salt intake. Denmark subscribes to the Nordic Plan of Action on 
better health and quality of life (Nordic Council of Ministers 2006). The overall ambition behind the 
action plan is to ensure better health and quality of life on equal terms for all Nordic citizens through 
diet and physical activity. The document points out that intake of salt should be maintained or reduced, 
depending on the specific national context. 
In 2008, the Danish Veterinary and Food Administration, in collaboration with the National Food 
Institute, drew up guidelines that included a recommendation on how to cut down the use of salt in 
canteens. In the meantime, the Ministry of Food, Agriculture and Fisheries targeted an overall salt 
reduction benchmark of 16 % over a 4-year period, leading to a reduction in the population average 
daily salt intake by 2014 to 5 g for women and 6-8 g for men. Population salt intake levels were 
estimated in baseline assessment in 2008 via dietary records and survey data. The results indicated 
salt intake of 7.3 g per day for women and 9.8 g per day for men. Earlier results from a dietary survey 
done in 2000–2002 demonstrated a reduction in salt intake of 10-15 %. Despite the reduction, actual 
salt intake levels in 2008 were estimated to be higher as salt added at the table was not considered in 
the survey (WHO 2013). 
Considering the reductions in population salt consumption targeted for 2014, an extension was made 
to assess the impact of the salt strategy (WHO 2013). The current strategy consists of: a.) monitoring 
an evaluation, b.) industry involvement, c.) labelling and d.) consumer awareness. The policies and 
actions taking place are described in the end of the document (Appendix 2). 
Denmark, Norway, Iceland and Sweden have agreed on a common Nordic food labelling symbol, 
known as the Keyhole (Figure 3). The Keyhole was implemented in 2009 after being established earlier 
by Sweden. Finland does not currently use the Keyhole symbol, but the authorities are considering the 
possibility of including Keyhole’s criteria in the existing Finnish nutrition labelling scheme, the Heart 
Symbol. The Keyhole label makes it easier for consumers to identify products that are good for their 
health. In Denmark, the Veterinary and Food Administration is responsible for administering the 
Keyhole label. The labelling is on a voluntary basis. 
To be able to place the Keyhole label on a product, it must meet the requirements set by the scheme. 
The requirements include criteria regarding how much fat, sugar, salt, dietary fibre and wholegrain 
may be present in foods with the Keyhole symbol. These conditions are based on the Nordic Nutrition 
Recommendations, which are based on scientific research (Nordic Council of Ministers 2014). The 
Keyhole scheme applies to a variety of packed and unpacked foods which are separated in 33

semi-preserved fish products. The criteria states that to qualify for the Keyhole label the marinated or

the fish per 100 g of product. Maximum levels for sugar is 5 g / 100 g of product and for salt 3 g / 100

3.3. Seafood safety and quality 
Along with the health benefits of seafood, there are a number of associated hazards affecting to the 
safety of the products. Food safety can be described as the use of resources and strategies to ensure 
that foods are properly produced, processed, stored and distributed so they prevent foodborne illness 
and are safe for consumption (Raspor et al. 2016). Unsafe food leads to a cycle of disease and 
malnutrition, particularly affecting babies, young children, elderly and the ill (WHO 2015). The exact 
number of food-borne diseases in the world is unknown. This is mainly because many of the illnesses 
are not reported to public health authorities (Ryder et al. 2014). However, WHO estimates that 
foodborne and waterborne diarrhoea alone kills approximately 2 million people annually (WHO 2015).  
Different biological, chemical or physical hazards, with potential to cause adverse health effects, are 
associated to seafood. The biological hazards, in which the present study focuses on, consist of 
pathogenic bacteria, viruses and parasites (Ryder et al. 2014). The introduction of these food safety 
hazards can occur at any stage of the food chain and adequate control throughout the food chain is 
essential. To control these hazards, all seafood companies within European Union (EU) must 
implement hazard analysis and critical control points (HACCP) and appropriate prerequisite program  
(Raspor et al. 2016). 
The concept of quality has been given many definitions according to different ways of interpretation. 
The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) provides a broad definition of quality and 
explains it as “the totality of features and characteristics of a product, process or service that bear on 
its ability to satisfy stated or implied needs” (FAO 2004). Regarding food, quality can refer e.g. to 
==PAGE:16==
Literature review 
 
7 
 
rheological, nutritional, microbiological, sensory properties or factors set by different standards 
(Lawless & Heymann 2010). The present study focuses on the seafood quality mainly from 
microbiological and sensory perspective. 
Sensory quality of a products is affected by many characteristics related to the design and development 
of the product. These can include flavour, colour and texture of a product. Product development aims 
to maintain attractiveness of the product through improvements of the sensory properties of a 
product. This can be challenging as consumers have a wide range of preferences regarding optimal 
sensory quality (Lawless & Heymann 2010). 
Sensory quality of a product is also affected by microbial activity. Spoilage bacteria can make the 
product unfit for human consumption because of off-flavours created by the metabolic activity of the 
microorganisms. Another type of microorganism affecting the quality of a product are pathogenic 
bacteria. Pathogenic bacteria can cause disease or illness and should not be present in a product in 
critical concentrations within the shelf-life of the product (Ryder et al. 2014). The concentrations of 
spoilage and pathogenic bacteria limit the shelf-life of the products due to spoilage or safety concerns 
(Figure 4).

3.3.1. Microbiological spoilage 
The US National Research council estimates 25 % of the world’s food supply to be lost due to 
microbiological spoilage (Anonymous 1985). This is the case especially with seafood since (i) fish 
==PAGE:17==
Literature review 
 
8 
 
muscle may contain a natural population of psychrotolerant spoilage bacteria with the capability to 
grow rapidly at low temperatures, (ii) fish flesh has high pH, high free amino acid content and presence 
of trimethylamine oxide (TMAO), (iii) fish from wild catch has low uniformity, and (iv) fish has high 
levels, up to 10-108 cfu/g, of specific microorganisms in the intestine which can contaminate the fillets 
during processing (Dalgaard 2000; Huss et al. 1997). 
Naturally contaminated fresh fish from temperate or cold waters develop off-odours just after 8-14 
days at 0-2 °C due to growth of psychrotolerant bacteria. Many of these bacteria can carry out 
anaerobic respiration where they use TMAO as electron acceptor and form trimethylamin (TMA). TMA 
has an unpleasant ammonia-like odour of decaying fish, increases pH of the product and causes 
sensory rejection in critical concentrations (Howgate 2010). 
On the other hand, some tropical fish have shelf-life of more than 30 days at 0-2 °C. Even though warm 
waters typically result in higher concentrations of microorganisms on fish than temperate waters, the 
microbiota in warm water fish is low in psychrotolerant bacteria, resulting in low microbial activity at 
low temperatures (Dalgaard 2000; Dalgaard 2006). Microbiota in fish varies also, not only depending 
on water temperature, but also between freshwater and marine species. For example, Vibrio spp. and 
Photobacterium spp. are typical marine bacteria which require salt and therefore are not as often 
found on freshwater fish (Dalgaard 2006). 
The microorganisms in seafood can be divided into “spoilers” and “non-spoilers”. This general pattern 
of microbial activity and growth in seafood during the storage period has been named the specific 
spoilage organism (SSO) concept (Figure 5). In fresh and lightly preserved seafood SSOs are usually a 
small part of the total microbiota. Yet, SSOs often grow faster than the rest of the microbiota. 
Microbiological spoilage is due principally to metabolism of non-protein nitrogen (NPN) compounds 
by SSOs leading to production of off-flavours, and not because of proteolytic activity (Lerke et al. 1967).  
The metabolites produced by the SSOs depend on the type of the spoilage bacteria and include often 
TMA, hydrogen sulphide (H2S) and biogenic amines (Dalgaard 2006). Knowledge of the SSOs, their 
growth and metabolites have also allowed development of predictive models and therefore prediction 
of shelf-life under particular storage conditions (Dalgaard 2000). 
==PAGE:18==
Literature review 
 
9 
 
 
Figure 5. Specific spoilage organism (SSO) concept showing changes in total viable counts (TVC), SSOs and metabolites 
produced by SSOs and the index for chemical spoilage (Dalgaard et al. 1993). 
Determination of shelf-life is vital for setting up processing and distribution conditions, assuring 
compliance with guidelines and agreements, and prevent complaints by consumers. In determination 
of shelf-life, objective sensory methods, such as the Torry scheme and the quality index method (QIM), 
are combined to instrumental methods. The same principles can be used in quality control and seafood 
inspection. Instrumental methods, often measuring microbiological, chemical or biochemical 
properties of a product, should be casually related to sensory changes (Dalgaard 2000). 
Typical bacteria responsible for seafood spoilage are Pseudomonas spp., H2S-producing bacteria, 
Shewanella spp., Enterobacteriaceae spp., Photobacterium phosphoreum and lactic acid bacteria 
(LAB). In modified atmosphere packaging (MAP) packed cod typical spoilage organisms are P. 
phosphoreum and LAB (Dalgaard 2006). 
3.3.1.1. Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) 
LAB consists of a large group of Gram positive, catalase and oxidize negative cocci and rods that 
produce lactic acid as one of the main metabolites of the sugar fermentation (Figure 6). All lactic acid 
bacteria grow anaerobically, but unlike many anaerobes, LAB are not sensitive to oxygen. Thus, LAB 
are aerotolerant anaerobes, meaning that they can grow with or without oxygen (Leroi 2010).

The genera of LAB consist of Lactobacillus, Enterococcus, Lactococcus, Leuconostoc, Pediococcus and 
Streptococcus genus. One of the main differences between these subgroups is in the metabolites 
produced through fermentation of sugars. Homofermentative LAB produces only lactic acid as 
fermentation product whereas heterofermentative LAB produces ethanol, carbon dioxide and lactate. 
Only genera of Lactobacillus and Leuconostoc belong to the heterofermentative group (Madigan et al. 
2003). 
LAB are found in the general environment and are present in many food products such as dairy and 
meat. Most of the LAB obtain their energy through metabolism of sugars and therefore they prefer 
habitats with sugars (Madigan et al. 2003). However, they can be found in environments with very 
little sugars, such as from seafood. Humans have used LAB for natural fermentation of foodstuffs for 
thousands of years which has led to development of many new and microbiologically stabilized 
products. The low pH achieved due to the lactic acid production in the sugar fermentation is one of 
the most desirable side effects of LAB growth. However, LAB growth in fish does not always lead to 
acidification. If acidification process takes place in a food product, it is inhibiting growth of other 
microorganisms including the most common human pathogens (Leroi 2010). 
3.3.2. Microbiological safety 
Many biological hazards need to be considered when microbial safety of seafood is evaluated. The 
microbial risks are influenced by bacteria, viruses and parasites. The present study focuses on the risks 
created by bacteria. 
One of the main risks in RTE products is the growth of the human pathogen Listeria monocytogenes. 
RTE products have typical product characteristics and storage conditions which are often inadequate 
to prevent growth of this psychrotolerant pathogen. Another potential risk is created by growth of 
==PAGE:20==
Literature review 
 
11 
 
toxin forming pathogen Clostridium botulinum. The bacteria are gram positive and anaerobic spore 
forming. Cl. botulinum is divided into several phylogenetic groups and the isolates are grouped to 
classes A-G based on the type of neurotoxin they produce. The non-proteolytic form in class E is 
naturally occurring in marine environment and therefore an important concern in aquatic food. 
Occurrence of Cl. botulinum cannot be conveniently prevented but growth and toxin formation must 
be done by applying storage temperature <3.5 °C, heat treatment at 90 °C for 10 min or at 121 °C for 
2.4-3 min “botulinum cook”, or by adequate combination of preservation and temperature (Huss 
1997). 
Vibrio spp. occur naturally in seawater and therefore are not usually a concern in catch from 
freshwater. However, V. cholera is known to habit freshwater environments. The genus Vibrio contains 
80 species, 12 being infectious to human and three, namely V. parahaemolyticus, V. cholera and V. 
vulnificus, having of major importance in fish and seafood. Microbiological criteria for Vibrio spp. are 
not included in EU regulation 2073/2005. Seafood-borne disease caused by Vibrio spp. is common in 
Japan, Asia and US. Disease is often caused by consumption of raw or insufficiently cooked seafood 
and bivalve molluscs during summer months when water temperatures are high.  Vibrio spp. are heat 
sensitive and growth can be inhibited by low temperatures (Ryder et al. 2014). 
One of the main safety aspects in aquatic food is related to the growth of L. monocytogenes. The 
bacterium is on focus in the present study and therefore discussed further in the following section. 
3.3.2.1. Listeria monocytogenes 
L. monocytogenes is a psychrotolerant and halotolerant bacterium causing the infection, listeriosis. It 
also survives in acidic environments. The bacterium is a gram-positive, short and non-spore-forming 
rod (Figure 7) (Brock et al. 2003). L. monocytogenes isolates can be divided into three different genetic 
lineages and multiple subtypes. Lineage 1 including serotypes 1/2b, 3b, 4b, 4d and 4e is most of the 
times involved in human disease, however all variants of the bacterium should be considered as 
potentially pathogenic (Ryser & Marth 2007). 
L. monocytogenes is widely present in general environment meaning that seafood can become 
contaminated by L. monocytogenes at any stage during production or processing. It can grow in a range 
of 1 °C – 45 °C, but is inhibited by cooking or hot smoking at above 70 °C. Its halotolerance can reach 
up to 10 % NaCl in a product and therefore often not prevented in lightly preserved seafood (Farber 
and Peterkin (2000) in Mejlholm (2007)). Hence, methods which usually slow microbial growth, such 
as refrigeration are ineffective. Therefore common sources of listeriosis outbreaks are lightly 
preserved foods such as meat, dairy and fish products that are stored for long period in refrigerator 
temperature (Brock et al. 2003).

Literature review

Listeriosis has the highest mortality rates among babies, elderly and persons with reduced immune 
defence including pregnant women. The relative susceptibility compared to healthy adults is highest 
among people with transplants followed by acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) patients, 
perinatal and neonates (Ryser & Marth 2007). Listeriosis is a rare disease but it accounts for 3.8 % of 
foodborne disease hospitalizations, and 27.6 % of foodborne disease deaths (Painter & Slutsker 2007). 
In the EU 2206 human cases were reported with a mortality rate of 17.7 % in 2015. Most of the 
reported deaths (270 deaths) in 2015 due to zoonoses within EU were resulted in by literiosis (EFSA 
2016). Possible symptoms of the disease are fever, muscle pain, diarrhoea in pregnant women and it 
can lead to preterm delivery, abortion or stillbirth. In elderly and babies, possible symptoms are sepsis, 
meningitis, pneumonia and infections. In healthy adults, the symptoms are diarrhoea and fever. 
Listeriosis is diagnosed by isolation of the pathogen from a normally sterile site e.g. from blood or 
cerebrospinal fluid. The treatment is done by antibiotics (Ryser & Marth 2007).

Listeriosis is caused by high concentrations of L. monocytogenes and therefore its growth in aquatic 
food is more problematic than its occurrence (FAO/WHO 2004). The food safety criteria for RTE foods 
is based on Commission Regulation 2073/2005. The regulation differentiates RTE foods that are able 
or unable to support growth of L. monocytogenes. In foods that support growth of L. monocytogenes 
it must be documented by the producer that the critical limit of 100 cfu/g is not exceeded within the 
shelf-life of the product. If not documentable, L. monocytogenes must not be present in the product 
at the time of production. This means five, negative, 25 g samples.  In foods that are unable to support 
growth, it must be documented that (i) pH ≤ 4.4, (ii) aw ≤ 0.92, (iii) pH ≤ 5.0 and aw ≤ 0.94, or (iv) shelf 
life is below five days. If the regulation is not met or no documentation has been done, the food cannot 
be sold in EU. Predictive microbiology models can be used to document the control (European

Parliament 2005; European Parliament 2007). Many countries e.g. Australia, New Zealand and US have 
zero-tolerance, stating that L. monocytogenes should not be present in the RTE foods (samples of 25

A number of enumeration methods for L. monocytogenes have been developed. According to Nordic 
Committee on Food Analyses (NMKL) enrichment is done in Half-Fraser broth and plating on: (i) Agar 
Listeria according to Ottaviani and Agosti (ALOA), (ii) Listeria monocytogenes blood agar medium 
(LMBA), or (iii) Chromogenic Listeria Agar. Confirmation can be done by blood agar, biochemical tests 
or typing (Nordic Committee on Food Analysis 2010). Other valid enumeration methods are described 
in US Food and Drug Administrations (FDA) Bacteriological Analytical Manual. The methods include use 
of Oxford Agar, Modified Oxford Agar (MOX), LPM and PALCAM. The confirmation according to NMKL 
and FDA can be carried out by use of blood agar (ß-hemolysis), biochemical tests or typing (Hitchins et 
al. 2017).  
Prevention and control of L. monocytogenes is essential in aquatic food. This can be carried out by: (i) 
reducing occurrence of the pathogen after processing by implementing good management practices 
(GMP) and good hygiene practices (GHP), (ii) inactivating the pathogen in packed products by e.g. heat, 
high pressure or bacteriophages, (iii) reducing or preventing the growth of the pathogen by adequate 
product formulation, bio-preservation (LAB) or MAP, and (iv) delivering dietary recommendations and 
information to risk groups (Ryser & Marth 2007).

3.4.1. Temperature 
Temperature is the most important parameter to control microbial growth in seafood. In addition, the 
parameter is exposed to the great risk of variation at different stages of seafood production and supply 
chain. The growth rate is at its maximum at the optimum temperature of the specific microorganism. 
Chill temperatures favour growth of psychrotolerant microorganisms including spoilage and 
pathogenic bacteria. Temperature has also an effect on other preserving parameters, e.g. CO2 solubility 
in water phase is enhanced at low temperatures (Sivertsvik et al. 2002). Temperature has also different 
effect depending on the type seafood. This can differ depending on species (Figure 10), or depending 
on the level of processing (Figure 11). The latter figure describes temperatures relation to relative rate 
of spoilage (RRS). RRS is defined as the shelf-life at 0°C divided by the shelf-life at t °C (Dalgaard 2002).

3.4.3. pH 
pH is a measure of hydrogen ion activity. To avoid denaturation of intercellular proteins, bacterial cells 
must maintain their pH above a critical limit (Montville & Matthews 2007). To maintain the intercellular 
pH, bacteria pumps ions in and out of the cell. pH is a very important environmental factor affecting 
on microbial growth. In food products pH is often controlled by weak organic acids. When the bacterial 
cells are exposed to low pH in a food product, the undissociated form of the organic acid can easily 
pass the cell membrane. Once penetrated the cell membrane, the acid dissociates releasing hydrogen 
proton into the cell. This makes the cytoplasm acidic. As a response, the cell tries to neutralize itself by 
pumping the hydrogen ions out. The transportation of the hydrogen ions requires energy and 
therefore reduces the amount of energy available for cell division and growth. Drastic drop in pH can 
suspend the growth or kill the cell (Gould 2000). The equation below describes the equilibrium reaction 
of lactic acid dissociation [Eq. 5].

[Eq. 5] 
Since the undissociated form of organic acids is the most effective antimicrobial form, it is important 
to know their amount when planning preservation. The concentration of undissociated organic acids 
depends on the logarithmic acid dissociation constant (pKa) and the acidity of the food. The 
concentration of undissociated organic acids is greater than the dissociated form when pH is below 
the pKa value. Therefore, organic acids are more effective antimicrobials in acidic environments. 
3.4.4. Ethanol 
Alcohols are organic compounds containing one or more hydroxyl groups (–OH) in their structure. 
Many alcohols have shown antimicrobial properties with, ethanol, isopropyl alcohol and n-propanol 
being the most widely used. They display antimicrobial activity against vegetative bacteria, viruses, 
and fungi, but not against spores. The antimicrobial mechanism of alcohols is based on their ability to 
==PAGE:27==
Literature review

rapidly denaturise proteins. Alcohols can go through the cell wall of an organism, denaturise the 
protein inside the cell and in this way, kill the organism (Best et al. 1988; McDonnell & Russell 1999). 
Even many alcohols are strong antimicrobials, many of them are poisonous to human. Therefore, 
ethanol, being consumable by human, has can be used in food preservation (Insel et al. 2004). 
Ethanol, often abbreviated as EtOH, is the primary type of alcohol and predominant in ABs. It is a 
volatile, flammable and colourless liquid. Its full chemical formula is C2H6O (Figure 12)  (National Center 
for Biotechnology Information 2017). Ethanol has several uses; it has long history as part of beverages, 
it can be used as antifreeze, antiseptic, fuel, solvent and preservative (Insel et al. 2004). In the present 
study, when the term “alcohol” is used, it refers to ethanol.

In beverages ethanol is usually consumed due to its psychoactive effects causing pleasant euphoria. 
Ethanol is a small molecule and consequently, it requires no digestive breakdown in the human 
digestive system to be absorbed. Alcohol has also unusual ability to cross blood-brain barrier. The 
barrier is a highly selective semipermeable membrane barrier that prevents the passage of many 
compounds from the blood into the brain (Insel et al. 2004). 
In recent years, natural antimicrobials have become increasingly popular in the food industry and 
research. Also, food-grade ethanol has showed up in research due to its antimicrobial action, well-
known history as part of beverages and its ability to evaporate to the headspace. Antimicrobial activity 
of vapours of ethanol has been in focus mainly in products from plant origin e.g. grapes, cherries, 
apples and pomegranates (Kapetanakou et al. 2015). The experiments have led to development of new 
patents e.g. Antimold and Negamold (Freund Corporation 2017). Ethanol has also been applied in 
coatings and edible films to preserve e.g. frankfurters. Also other application methods like dipping and 
spraying have been reported to significantly decrease growth of spoilage and pathogenic organisms in 
foods such as fruits and bakery products (Kapetanakou et al. 2016). Some of the concerns in the use 
of ethanol in food products are related to the antimicrobial effectiveness, economic viability, changes 
in sensory and chemical properties as well as public opinion (Kapetanakou et al. 2015; Kapetanakou et 
al. 2016).

3.4.5. Dry-aging technology 
Aging is a processing technique traditionally used to produce a high-quality beef products to high-end 
customers. The process can be defined as holding a product for a period to improve its palatability 
(Campbell et al. 2001). Attributes such as tenderness, flavour, and juiciness appear in the product due 
to a structural breakdown of muscle by proteolytic enzymes. In general, there are two types of aging 
methods. In traditional dry-aging, the products are unpacked and stored in carefully controlled 
conditions of temperature, relative humidity and airflow. These parameters need to be balanced and 
monitored to inhibit microbial growth and minimise weight loss. The weight losses during dry-aging 
can be up to 10 % in beef (Warren & Kastner 1992). The second type of aging is vacuum or wet-aging 
where the product is kept at refrigerated temperatures and packed in non-permeable package 
(Campbell et al. 2001; Kim et al. 2016). 
Until the development of vacuum packing in 1960s the only aging option was to dry-aged the meat. 
Vacuum packing displayed benefits over traditional dry-aging due to its ability to prevent weight loss 
and extend shelf-life without considerable loss of palatability (Minks & Stringer 1972). However, it has 
been shown that the dry-aging process is typically resulting in products more tender than wet-aging. 
Warren and Kastner (1992) compared dry-aged and vacuum-aged beef strip loins and found dry-aged 
products more beefy, tender and having more brown-roasted flavour than not aged or vacuum-aged 
products. 
Despite positive sensory effects of dry-aging, it is not a widely-used processing method because of the 
time used for drying, costs and weight loss. Due to recent technological inventions, new patents have 
emerged to support the method. One of them concerns the polymer film Tublin (Tub-ex, Denmark) 
using combination of polymers creating a single layer of material. Due to its molecular structure, the 
bag lets moisture to evaporate from the dry-aged product. The bag lets oxygen in while it keeps other 
particles in the air out. This prevents flavour contamination and makes it possible to carry out dry-
aging in normal refrigerator conditions. It is claimed by the producer that Tublin is also suitable for 
drying fish (Tub-ex 2017b). However, compared to the number of studies conducted on dry-aged beef, 
dry-aging of fish has not been the subject for much scientific research. 
3.5. Sensory evaluation of seafood 
Sensory evaluation is defined as the scientific method used to evoke, measure, analyse and interpret 
reactions to characteristics of food as perceived through the senses of sight, smell, taste, touch and 
hearing. Most sensory characteristics can only be measured purposefully by humans. In most of the 
cases human sensory data provides the best models for how consumers are expected to perceive and 
react to food products in real-life. Yet, advances have been made in the development of instruments 
==PAGE:29==
Materials, experimental design and methods 
 
20 
 
that can measure individual quality changes. The present study is however focusing on the sensory 
properties perceived through human senses (Huss 1995). 
The sensory analysis process can be divided into three steps: (i) detection of a stimulus by the sense 
organs, (ii) evaluation and interpretation by a mental process, and (iii) the response of the person to 
the stimuli. Consumers do have various individual preferences and experiences regarding food. This 
can be related to multiple things: location, traditions, availability and frequency of consumption. 
People can also differ widely in their response to colour and sensitivity to chemical stimuli (Huss 1995). 
It is important to be conscious of these differences when selecting the people for sensory panel. 
The sensory analyses of a food can be divided into two groups: discriminative tests and descriptive 
tests. Discriminative testing is used to assess if a difference exists between samples. In this type of test 
the panellists are ranking samples according to their characteristics. Descriptive tests are used to 
profile the characteristics and intensity of the differences (Huss 1995). Sensory analyses can be divided 
also into objective tests, which are not influenced by personal feelings or opinions, and subjective tests, 
which are influenced by personal feelings, tastes and opinions. Objective analysis is recommended to 
do with panels consisting of few trained assessors, while subjective analysis can be carried out with 
panels consisting of consumers or panellists without much experience (Lawless & Heymann 2010). 
Scaling forms the basis for the sensory method of descriptive analysis. Scaling is used in sensory 
evaluation to describe the perceived intensity of a sensory experience or the degree of liking or 
disliking for some experience or product. The judgments are often converted to numerical values to 
quantify sensory experiences. By doing this, sensory evaluation becomes a quantitative science subject 
to statistical analysis, modelling and prediction (Lawless & Heymann 2010). In the present study, 
sensory properties of seafood products were evaluated by using descriptive, numerical, semi-objective 
sensory scale.

4.2.3. Sensory analyses 
The aim of the sensory analyses was to determine the sensory characteristics of salt-reduced dry-aged 
cod treated with different strengths of brine and ABs. Two different sample sizes, 100 g (± 10 g) slices 
and 330 g (± 80 g) fillets, were used in the analyses. In these studies ABs were added to the brine. Two 
analyses were carried out, namely (i) preliminary sensory analysis, and (ii) final sensory analysis. The 
dry-aged cod products were evaluated by an untrained panel. The evaluation in each analysis was 
carried out once right after dry-aging. The sensory panel were chosen from the DTU students and 
employees and included six persons in the preliminary sensory analysis and eight persons in the final 
sensory analysis. The procedure used to prepare the brines is explained in section 4.3.1.1. 
Prior to the sensory analyses, the fillets were taken from the dry-aging and cut into small approximately 
10 mm x 10 mm pieces. In the preliminary study the samples were put in numbered, 140 mm x 140 
mm, white weighing boats where the panellists could take three pieces on their own plates. In the final 
sensory analysis, the samples were put in white, 75 mm x 75 mm, numbered weighing boats. Three 
pieces of fish were put on each boat. The boats were placed on big aluminium trays, allowing panellist 
to have their own samples. The samples were prepared in the morning and the analyses took place in 
the same day afternoon. The prepared samples were kept in ice in the preliminary sensory analysis 
and in the cool room at 2 °C in the final sensory analysis until the start. The panellists were provided 
water, spitting cup and apple slices to have between the samples. Water and apple slices were 
provided to refresh panellists’ taste buds.

4.3.1. Sample preparation 
The section describes the methods used to produce dry-aged cod.  
4.3.1.1. Brine preparation 
Brine preparation started by adding sodium di-acetate (Macco Organiques, Vallyfield, Quebec, Canada) 
in approximately 37 °C deionized water. Stirring was applied by a beater. Then sodium chloride (Akzo 
Nobel Inc., Jozo, Denmark) was weighed and added to the solution. After this the lactic acid (80 % w/w) 
was added by a syringe. After the ingredients were well mixed, more deionized water was added to 
give the solution its final volume. 
pH of ready brines was controlled by transferring a sample into a 1 ml cuvette and using pH meter 
(PHM250 Ion Analyzer, Radiometer, Copenhagen, Denmark). If adjustments were needed 2 M or 5 M 
NaOH and 2 M HCl were added until the desired pH was reached. The brines were produced at DTU 
Food in the sensory laboratory. Ingredients and equipment that were necessary to move between 
pathogen and sensory facilities were disinfected with ethanol every time moved. The brines were 
stored in covered bottles, beakers or canisters at 2 °C. 
4.3.1.2. Thawing and brining 
The samples were thawed in plastic bags in room temperature for 3-4 h in all but two experiments. In 
the final storage trial the fillets were thawed overnight at 2 °C to achieve optimal quality, and in the 
preliminary storage trial the samples were thawed 10-15 min in a warm water bath. While the samples 
were thawing the equipment, including cutting board, knife and working surface, were cleaned with a 
==PAGE:48==

detergent and disinfected with ethanol (96.3 % ABV). Thawed fillets were cut in desired sizes, weighed 
and put individually into plastic bags for brining. 
Based on the sample weight, the predetermined amount of brine was calculated. The brine was 
weighed on a scale and added in the plastic bags with samples. Excess air was squeezed out and the 
mouth of a bag was wrapped around the samples. Bags were placed in a Styrofoam box and it was 
made sure that the brine is evenly distributed among the samples to ensure even absorption of the 
brine. In the storage trial, multiple samples were placed in same bag and therefore the bags were 
placed in 10-15 l buckets to facilitate optimal coverage by the brine. The bags were marked with 
corresponding codes and moved into 2 °C for 24-48 h depending on the experiment. 
4.3.1.3. Dry-aging 
Marinated samples were transferred in 200 x 400 mm dry-age bags made of thin, 0.04 mm, polymer 
(Tublin 5, Tub-Ex, Denmark). Additional information on the dry-age bag is provided in the end of the 
document (Appendix 10). In some experiments the samples were placed in the bags by using a special 
funnel to avoid grease, brine and water contaminating the sealing surface. This was done to facilitate 
proper welding at the time of vacuum packing. In some cases, the welding surface was cleaned with 
paper towels prior to vacuum packing.

4.3.2. Inoculum preparation 
A total of ten isolates were studied including (i) four strains of L. monocytogenes (94-203D, 95-54A, 
95-442A, 94-167B) from seafood (cold-smoked salmon, cooked shrimp, surimi in brine and seafood 
salad), (ii) four isolates of Lb. sakei (LA-5, A1, MAP23-1, F46-4) from seafood (brined and drained 
shrimp in MAP and cold-smoked Greenland halibut), and (iii) two strains of P. phosphoreum (P-0, P-
100) from intestines of cod. Each isolate was transferred from a culture collection kept at -80 °C and 
sub-cultured individually in VM (Dalgaard et al. 1994) for P. phosphoreum at 15 °C, in BHI (Oxoid 
CM1135) for L. monocytogenes at 25 °C and in APT (Difco 265510) for Lb. sakei at 25 °C. The isolates 
were sub-cultured for 24 h. Then 10, 100 and 1000 µl of each strain were transferred to fresh medium 
with adjusted characteristics. In this medium organic acid and salt content were adjusted to 
correspond to the same level as in the experiment. The absorbance was measured at T0 and followed 
up by repeated absorbance measurements. The pre-cultures were kept at 15 °C for 2-3 days. The pre-
cultures were harvested in late exponential growth phase. This was defined as increase in absorbance 
of 0.05 to 0.2 at 540 nm (Novaspec II, Pharmacia Biotech, Denmark). Harvesting was done by 
transferring 1 ml of each strain into same tube. 
The cell-density (cfu/ml) of the inoculum was determined by microscopy (BH-2, Olympus Optical, 
Japan). The counting was done for tenfold dilution.  The calculation of the cell-density assumes that 1 
cell per field of view corresponds to approximately 106 cfu/ml. An average value from 10 randomly 
picked fields of view was used. More precise concentration of the inoculum was determined by plate 
counting on VM for P. phosphoreum, on BHI for L. monocytogenes and on NAP for Lb. sakei. As needed, 
the inoculums were diluted in physiological water (0.85 % NaCl) prior to inoculation of the samples.

4.3.3.1. Preparation of the culture media for microbiological analysis 
Different types of media were used in the present study to measure growth of microorganisms. The 
media had to contain compounds required by the microorganisms to grow. Some media were non-
selective favouring growth of many types of microorganisms, while other media were selective and 
supported growth of specific microorganisms. 
The media were prepared according to the specific protocols (Appendix 11). Physiological salt peptone 
(PSP) media was used in dilutions and kept in cooled incubator. The media for plating were cooled, 
depending on the media, to 45-50 °C in water bath, necessary supplements were added, and the media 
were poured on sterile plates in laminar flow cabinet. The media were applied approximately 20 ml 
per plate - enough to cover the bottom of the plate. The plates for spread plating were done before 
the samplings and the plates for pour plating were prepared at the time of sampling. The plates for 
spread plating were left into laminar flow cabinet to solidify overnight with the lids closed. Some 
plates, which were used in the same day, were left with the lids slightly open to speed up the drying 
process.  
4.3.3.2. Plating 
For each sample, 20 g of the fish was taken into a stomacher bag (BagPage, Interscience, France). The 
exact weight of the sample was recorded and then the bag was filled with chilled PSP until 10-fold 
dilution was achieved. Then the bag was taken to the stomacher (BA 7021, Seward Stomacher, 
Worthing, UK) for 60 sec homogenisation. Two types of plating techniques were used: (i) spread plating 
(Figure 21), and (ii) pour plating (Figure 22).

Drygalski spatula. The plates with 500 µl samples were dried briefly in a laminar flow cabinet to get rid

possible to estimate from the previous plate counts. More diluted plates were usually needed towards

The instrument uses a ceramic sensor made of zirconia with external and internal electrodes made of

platinum electrodes interact with the oxygen molecules as oxygen ions flow between the electrodes

4.3.4.3. Water activity 
Water activity (aw) of the samples was measured with device (CX-2, AquaLab, Decagon, US). The device 
determined the dew-point temperature of the samples with stainless steel mirror and infrared beam. 
The mirror in the chamber, against the sample, is repeatedly cooled and heated so the dew is 
continually forming and evaporating. Each time dew forms on the mirror, the device measures the 
temperature of the air and of the sample. The dew-point temperature was translated into water 
activity by the device (Decagon Devices Inc. 2012). 
For the calibration of the device calcium carbonate (CaCO3) and distilled water were used before 
analysing the samples. The homogenized samples were placed into plastic cells used by the device and 
let to warm up in the room temperature. Prior to measurement the cells were placed in warming unit 
(2112R, AquaLab, Decagon, US) for 10 min to reach stable (25 °C) temperature. The temperature of 
the device was controlled by water. The water temperature was adjusted by cooling bath (CBN 8-30, 
Heto-Holten Ltd., Allerød, Denmark) and thermostat (HMT 200, Heto-Holten Ltd., Allerød, Denmark). 
Cooling bath allows the temperature to be adjusted even lower than the surrounding room 
temperature. After 10 min warming the edges of the cells were cleaned with a tissue and the cells were 
placed in the reader and the results were recorded.

The aim of Phase III and IV was to obtain a method to produce high and low salt products needed in

The aim of the sensory analyses was to determine the sensory characteristics of salt-reduced dry-aged 
cod treated with different strengths of brine and ABs. 
5.3.1. Preliminary sensory analysis 
The results from the preliminary sensory analysis indicated positive sensory responses in treatments 
2 and 3 with slightly diluted brine and in treatments 8 and 9 containing anise-flavoured ABs (Table 14). 
The highest overall sensory score (6.17) was attained from treatment 2. The treatment contained 
brine which was diluted by 20 % water. This was followed by even more diluted treatment 3 with 40 
% water, and treatment 8 with 50 % water and added ouzo. 
The most diluted treatments 4 and 5 gave the most fishy odour to the samples (Table 14). Treatment 
6, which included pure ethanol, had interestingly the least fishy odour and least odour of ABs. This 
indicates ethanol’s ability to neutralize off-odours without giving odour of ethanol. The treatment 9 
with Pernod gave the highest score regarding the odour of ABs. 
The results indicated relatively even scores in appearance, excluding treatment 5. Also, the texture of 
treatment 5 was evaluated harder than others. This became visible as low scores in texture where 
score 1 equalled “hard and score 9 “soft” (Table 14, texture). This was also regarded in the comments 
by the panellists describing the product as hard and dry. This was because treatment 5 consisted of 
thin tail part and therefore was dried faster than others. It is also likely that it absorbed the brine more 
efficiently than others and therefore resulted in higher concentration of salt leading to high scores in 
saltiness. To obtain comparable results, the tail parts were excluded from the further experiments.

caused due to high volume of ABs.

6. Discussion 
Many studies have shown that the population nowadays consumes more salt than is physiologically 
needed. Much of this salt originates from RTE and processed foods, including aquatic food (He et al. 
2011). High salt consumption has been related to many chronic diseases. Interventions to reduce the 
burden of these diseases are highly cost effective (WHO 2012). Therefore, it was investigated whether 
or not salt can be reduced in dry-aged cod products by replacing some of it with alcohols. 
6.1. Effects of salt-reduction, alcohols and sugar on sensory properties  
Dry-aged cod tested by Royal Greenland Seafood Ltd. contained 3.5 % sugar of the sample weight, and 
hence would have provided typical characteristics to the product also in this study. However, sugar 
was reduced to 1.5 % in the final storage trial due to feedback from the final sensory analysis. It is 
important to notice, that the final sensory analysis was carried out with low salt and therefore may 
have given more space for sugar to be sensed. Sweetness of the samples became visible in the 
comments given by the panellists (section 5.3.2). As the salt in the final storage trial ended up to be 
higher than expected, more sugar could have had an impact e.g. by increasing sweetness and 
neutralizing some acidity. Previous studies related to effects of sugars on sensory properties of fish 
were not found.  However, in a previous study, sugars were shown increasing sweetness in mildly 
acidic foods, such as in lemon beverages and white cake, but not in foods with high acidity (Cardello

Previous research on the effects of flavoured alcohols on fish was not found. However, positive 
sensory effects of flavoured ABs on pork steaks were observed by Kapetanakou et al. (2014). In their 
study odour, colour, overall appearance and taste of pork steaks exposed to vapours of whisky, 
brandy, tsipouro, raki and ouzo were evaluated. They found that on the third day of the trial, control 
treatment without added ABs showed significantly (P<0.05) higher scores compared to the treatments 
with ABs. However, the pork steaks exposed to ABs showed significant (P<0.05) stability compared to 
the control treatment and maintained high sensory scores on 16th day of the trial. The present study 
supports and augments the previous findings by showing that the flavoured ABs maintain positive 
sensory properties over time regarding flavours but may reduce appearance e.g. shine of the products 
(section 5.5.4, Figure 41). Loss of shine and unnatural colour induced by flavoured ABs was considered 
negative by the sensory panel. 
Suzuki et al. (2004) used ethanol vapour treatment created from alcohol powder to inhibit senescence 
of harvested broccoli. Their results showed that the control treatments without alcohols turned yellow 
at day 3 whereas the treatments with ethanol vapour maintained their colour until day 5. They 
concluded that alcohol was effective for prolonging the shelf-life of broccoli due to supressed ethylene 
production by inhibiting oxidase activity. The present study conflicts with the previous findings by 
showing that colour and texture can be affected negatively by the ABs. However, it is important to 
acknowledge that some of the used ABs in the present study had distinct colour from pure ethanol 
and therefore easily influenced cod which has naturally white flesh. The colour is not necessarily an 
issue with other types of seafood e.g. with salmonids, where the colouring effect of ABs could be 
mitigated by the natural pink colour of the flesh. Pure ethanol did not influence the colour of the 
products. 
Regarding the texture, Saltveit (1989) reported that tomato discs, exposed to a high concentrations 
(>200 µl/g fresh weight) of ethanol, appeared to have structural changes in cell membranes resulting 
in enhanced permeability and denaturation of proteins. Deleterious effects of ethanol were also 
reported by Jackson et al. (1982). However, damaging effects were only observed at concentrations 
of 12- to 200-fold higher those found naturally in flooded plants. This data cannot be related to most 
of the experiments in the present study as the previously reported concentrations causing damaging 
effects were greater than 20 % ethanol of the sample weight. However, the observations from final

sensory analysis (section 5.3.2) support the previous findings. Too much alcohols were added to the 
treatments 4, 5, 6 and 7 which resulted in loss of the shine that was present in the control treatments. 
One of the major concerns regarding ethanol treatments is whether they may induce off-flavours to 
the products (Plotto et al. 2006). In the preliminary sensory analysis, it was observed that treatment 
6 with pure ethanol had the least odour of fish (section 5.3.1, Table 14). The ethanol treatment 4 had 
also little alcohol odour in the final sensory analysis (section 5.3.2, Table 15). This indicates that 
ethanol could possess properties that are neutralizing off-odours. However, when the sensory 
changes were assessed over time in the final storage trial, it was observed that after four weeks of 
storage, the odour of ethanol was possible to distinguish (section 5.5.4, Figure 38). This on the other 
hand, designates that the positive sensory effect of pure ethanol in the beginning may end up having 
negative sensory effects in the end. However, if used in RTE foods, the actual shelf-life of the product 
is likely to be over by the time the ethanol starts to give off-odours. 
Effects of ethanol in various fruits and vegetables, e.g. in cherries, pomegranate arils and tomatoes 
has been previously studied (Bai et al. 2011; Beaulieu & Saltveit 1997; Kapetanakou et al. 2014; 
Kapetanakou et al. 2015; Plotto et al. 2006). However, the previous research regarding the sensory 
effects of pure ethanol on fish products is limited. However, Kapetanakou et al. (2015) found that the 
ethanol vapour likely had a limited influence on overall cherry aroma, and no off flavours were 
detected from ethanol treated cherry flesh. The present study extends the previous findings by 
displaying that pure ethanol mitigates the off-odours in the beginning but it is possible to recognize in 
fish after four weeks of storage (section 5.5.4, Figure 38). It is known from chicken products that 
ethanol can accumulate to the headspace alongside other volatile compounds (Eilamo et al. 1998). 
This accumulation is assumed to be caused by LAB and coliform bacteria. LAB was detected in the 
present final storage trial even though growth of this bacteria did not take place in any of the 
treatments. Coliform bacteria, including genera Enterobacter, were not enumerated in the present 
study. However, they could have been present and enumerated on non-selective plates. As the 
storage trial was long (44 days) and relevant bacteria were present in small quantities, it is possible 
that accumulation of the volatile compounds took place and consequently made it possible to 
distinguish ethanol odour during the last weeks of the trial. 
Specific concentration of bacteria needed to produce ethanol was not found from the literature. Thus, 
this may depend on multiple factors such as species, time given for the metabolites to accumulate and 
growth environment including available nutrients. However, it has been recorded that from 1 mole of 
glucose, heterofermentative LAB can produce approximately 1 mole each of lactate, CO2, and either 
acetic acid or ethanol (Fugelsang & Edwards 2007).

6.2. Microbiological changes in the developed products 
In the present study, antimicrobial effect of ethanol was established in two challenge tests and in a 
liquid broth study carried out by Bioscreen C. Antimicrobial effect of ethanol has been established in 
previous studies. Kapetanakou et al. (2014) studied microbial properties of pork meat stored in MAP 
and were exposed to vapours of whisky, brandy, tsipouro, raki and ouzo which were added on cotton 
cloths 10 % of the sample weight. All applied ABs were effective (P<0.05) against LAB, Pseudomonas 
spp. and Brochotrix thermosphacta. Barker and Park (2001) investigated sensitization of L. 
monocytogenes to low pH, organic acids, and osmotic stress by ethanol and found out that the killing 
of the pathogen was enhanced by the addition of 5 % ABV ethanol. The findings from the present 
challenge tests and Bioscreen C assay corroborate and extend the previous research by showing 
significant (P<0.05) inhibition of L. monocytogenes in treatments with ABs compared to the control 
treatments without ABs. 
In the present final storage trial, slightly stronger antimicrobial effect was observed in TVC in the 
treatment 4 with Pernod compared to the treatment 3 with ethanol (section 5.5.2, Figure 31). Similar 
observations were done by Kapetanakou et al. (2014) when studying antimicrobial effect of brandy 
and distillery ethanol on RTE pomegranate arils. They reported stronger antimicrobial effect on 
vapours of brandy compared to vapour of pure ethanol suggesting existence of additional 
antimicrobial compound in brandy such as esters or aldehydes. These compounds are extracted along 
with ethanol during distillation process (Panosyan et al. 2001). 
In the present preliminary storage trial, it was observed that as P. phosphoreum grows, the pH 
increases correspondingly. These observations corroborate with the previous studies where the 
growth of the microorganism was associated to the rise in pH. This is mainly due to reduction of TMAO 
to TMA (Dalgaard et al. 1996; Howgate 2010). This was observed clearly in treatments 1 and 5, when 
changes in TVC were compared to the changes in pH (section 5.1, Figures 23 and 24). 
In the Bioscreen C assay it was observed that ethanol and Pernod had stronger inhibition against Lb. 
sakei than L. monocytogenes (section 5.4). However, similar studies where these microbes would have 
been assessed against ethanol in broth were not found. The Bioscreen C assay faced also some 
challenges. The biggest drawback in the assay was the turbidity caused by Pernod and BHI. The broth 
became more and more turbid when as Pernod was added. The same effect was not observed with 
Pernod and APT broth. Similar effects were not found from other studies. To efficiently assess Pernod’s 
effect against L. monocytogenes in broth, other types of medium needs to be tested. This could be 
done with e.g. buffered Listeria enrichment broth (BLEB) or Half Fraser Broth (Hitchins et al. 2017; 
Nordic Committee on Food Analysis 2010).

6.3. Effects of brining, alcohols and dry-aging on chemical properties 
In the present final storage trial, it was observed that while WPS, DM, pH and aw remained relatively 
unchanged during the experiment, organic acid concentrations were much higher in the end 
compared to the concentrations in the beginning. Lactic acid concentrations had gone from 
approximately 10000 ppm to 14000 ppm while acetic acid had risen from approximately 5000 ppm to 
7000 ppm within all the treatments (section 5.5.3, Tables 21 and 22). 
In the previous study, evaluating effect of alcohol vapours on microbial, physiochemical and sensory 
characteristics of pork meat stored in different MAP conditions, Kapetanakou et al. (2014) observed 
significant (P<0.05) increase of acetic acid during storage at 4 °C within control treatments in MAP (40 
% CO2:30 % O2:30 % N2) containing no ABs. The final concentration of acetic acid in samples exposed 
to vapours of ABs were significantly lower (P<0.05) that those in controls. The formation of acetic acid 
was connected to metabolic activity of LAB, which was likely inhibited by alcohols. On the contrary, 
the increase in acetic acid was not observed in any of the treatments under 20 % CO2:80 % O2. The 
latter observation supports hypothesis that the oxygen content in the MAP was the factor effecting 
on the accumulation of acetic acid. In the study, it was suggested that high oxygen could hinder the 
growth of homoformative LAB strains. 
The present study supports and refines the previous findings. It is important to notice that the 
research carried out by Kapetanakou et al. (2014) included 10 % ABs of the sample weight. (1 ml ABs 
and 10 g sample). However, the ABs were not added based on their alcohol concentration. Each of the 
used ABs contained 36-41 % ABV, thus giving pure ethanol concentration of 3.6-4.1 % per sample. In 
the present study, slightly less pure ethanol was used (3.5 ml/100 g). Also, the substrate and 
atmosphere were different than in the previous study. Hence, it is possible that metabolic activity of 
heteroformative LAB occurred in both, alcoholic and non-alcoholic treatments in extent, which 
allowed accumulation of acetic acid. LAB were present in the products used in this study, but they 
were inactivated by ethanol. It remains unclear whether LAB could produce enough organic acids in 
these conditions to be able to explain the increase in the organic acids during the storage. The 
reported increase of organic acids during the storage may be also attributed by better distribution of 
the of the brine compounds in the product during the storage. 
A 10 % increase in dry-matter was often observed between brined and dry-aged products (section 
5.2.1, Table 10). The difference in the dry matter content was obtained using 20 mbar vacuum, 
allowing a large area of the product to be in contact with the packaging film. This combined to effective 
ventilation introduced by an electric fan resulted in dry matter content of approximately 30 %. Similar 
observations were done by Warren and Kastner (1992) when they compared weights of dry-aged and

vacuum-aged beef and reported weight losses up to 10 % in dry-aged steaks compared to the vacuum-
aged products with nonpermeable packaging. 
In both present storage trials, it was observed that when alcohols were included in the package, 
significantly lower oxygen composition than was observed compared to the control treatments 
without alcohols. When average oxygen composition in the control treatments was approximately 
0.10 %, the treatments with alcohols had 0.00 % oxygen. In addition, a test was carried where ethanol 
preserved fish was packed into a normal atmosphere (21 % oxygen) and the gas composition was 
measured after two days. The results indicated 14.4 % oxygen composition in the packaging. The 
ethanol did not seem to influence the carbon dioxide content. 
Later, it was discovered that the used zirconia sensor in the gas analyser was heated up to 700 °C to 
obtain a sufficient oxygen ion conductivity. This resulted in combustion of ethanol if it was present in 
the atmosphere. As non-oxidizing compound, ethanol used the available oxygen in the burning 
process. Consequently, the sensor showed a lower O2 levels (Dansensor 2014). Gas analyser, equipped 
with electrochemical sensor which is not cross-sensitive to alcohol, would have been proper 
instrument to accurately measure oxygen composition in MAP. 
A systematic review and meta-analysis on consumer acceptance of reformulated and salt-reduced 
food products was carried out by Jaenke et al. (2017). The aim of the study was to determine to what 
extent foods can be reduced in salt without unfavourable effect on consumer acceptability. Random 
effects meta-analyses conducted on salt-reduced products showed that salt could be reduced by 
around 40 % in breads and around 70 % in processed meats without significantly impacting consumer 
acceptability. Results varied for other products. The observed results from the present study support 
the previous findings by establishing a connection between high overall sensory score (section 5.3.2, 
Table 15) and reduced salt content (section 5.2.2, Table 11).  
7. Conclusion 
In the present study, different dry-aged cod products with reduced salt and added pure ethanol and 
ABs were developed. Significant (P<0.05) antimicrobial effect induced by pure ethanol and Pernod 
against naturally occurring bacteria, LAB and the human pathogen Listeria monocytogenes was 
established in the study. Conducted Bioscreen C assay indicated stronger antimicrobial effect of pure 
ethanol against Lactobacillus sakei (MIC=48.4) than against L. monocytogenes (MIC=93.0). However, 
more research is needed to establish ethanol’s antimicrobial effect against specific microbes and to 
determine adequate application volume.

Conclusion 
 
80 
 
Sensory analyses were carried out to assess the effects induced by salt reduction and addition of 
alcohols. The highest scores in overall impression were given to products with anise-flavoured ABs. 
However, the alcohols were shown insufficient to add together with brine due to their negative effects 
on colour and shine of the products. However, alcohols showed better applicability when evaporated 
to the headspace through absorbent pad. Alcohol preserved products displayed stability regarding 
sensory scores over time and they had ability to neutralize off-odours. 
Chemical properties did not differ over time between dry-aged cod products with or without alcohols. 
Salt was reduced to under 3 % in the developed products thus qualifying them for the Keyhole label. 
The products with reduced salt to 1.8 % WPS gave better overall sensory scores than products with 
2.5 % WPS or higher. These results are also supporting manufacturers to make reductions in salt when 
reformulating food products and consequently contribute to a healthier food supply. 
Tublin 5 dry-aging bags showed good performance during the study and facilitated fast drying. Packing 
the products to 20 mbar vacuum and giving them dry-aging period of 72 h at 2 °C with ventilation 
resulted in approximately 10 % increase to dry matter content. These products showed positive 
sensory characteristics, e.g. regarding taste and texture.  
In the brining and dry-aging trials, variability in WPS and organic acid concentrations was observed.  
Possible reasons for this variability are e.g. uneven distribution of brine, or unrepresentative samples 
taken from the fish. It was observed that brining carried out with whole fillets left the inner parts of 
the fillets often unaffected by the brine, whereas thin tail parts were heavily affected by the brine. 
However, brining carried out by smaller slices resulted in more even distribution.  
Affordability of ethanol preservation depends on the types of alcohols being used. The volume needed 
to obtain sufficient antimicrobial effect depends on the ABV of the used beverage. Pure ethanol has 
the most potential as antimicrobial, but it does not have flavouring. On the other hand, flavoured 
beverages, such as ouzo and Pernod with approximately 40 % ABV, showed positive sensory effects in 
the present study, but they had to be used in higher volume to obtain the same antimicrobial effect.  
The study showed that ethanol has potential to be used as an alternative type of preservation in salt-
reduced seafood products against L. monocytogenes, LAB and unspecified naturally occurring 
bacteria. The limitations in use of ethanol as preservative relate to its effects on shine and colour, 
antimicrobial abilities against relevant human pathogens, and physical properties such as fast 
evaporation and flammability. To properly implement alcohol preservation in seafood the limitations 
regarding sensory, antimicrobial and chemical abilities must be understood and respected.

8. Perspectives 
Before being able to replace salt with alcohols in commercial seafood products, more research must 
be carried out to document ethanol’s antimicrobial effect against relevant human pathogens, most 
importantly regarding Clostridium botulinum. The pathogen was not included in the present study, 
even though it possesses high importance regarding safety of lightly preserved seafood. Its occurrence 
in foods cannot be prevented but its growth and toxin formation must be prevented. If the dry-aged 
product is stored in 3.5 – 10 °C, it must possess product characteristics having pH below 5 or WPS 
above 3.5 % to be able to prevent growth of Cl. botulinum. Neither of these characteristics are present 
in the currently developed salt-reduced products. Thus, ethanol’s antimicrobial effect in combination 
with relevant product characteristics (e.g. reduced salt, lactic acid, acetic acid and pH) against Cl. 
botulinum must be well documented before alcohol preservation can be commercially applied to 
aquatic food. Optimal application ratios between salt and ethanol need also to be research further as 
based on the present study it cannot be said how much salt is possible be replaced by alcohols. 
Even though cod is established as a well-known product in Nordic countries, the dry-aging method in 
fish is new to many people. Hence, certain product characteristics are, and are not expected by the 
consumers. For example, the texture of dry-aged cod is close to cold smoked salmon and therefore 
can be expected by some consumers. On the other hand, having the texture of cold smoked salmon 
in lean white fish can be unexpected by many. Another concern from the consumer perspective could 
be the presence of alcohols. Based on individual preferences and ethics the alcohols can possess 
positive or negative values. Hence sensory properties and public opinion regarding alcohol 
preservation could be further experimented by e.g. sensory evaluation carried out by consumer panel. 
Predictive models were available to predict growth of LAB and L. monocytogenes. However, limitations 
regarding the predictive models used by the FSSP software were met during the project. One of the 
limits was encountered when the growth of LAB and L. monocytogenes was predicted in low pH of 5.4, 
and the model allowed minimum pH of 5.6. Inactivation models are available for L. monocytogenes. 
However, they are not included in FSSP which would have been useful in the present study. In addition, 
the obtained MIC values from the present study could be used in future development of predictive 
models including added effect of ethanol. 
Based on the experience from brining and dry-aging trials, a systematic approach is needed when 
aiming to obtain certain product characteristics. Many attributes e.g. brine composition, brine 
dilution, pH adjustment, sample size, brining method and brining time have an effect on the final 
product characteristics giving multiple options for a product developer and therefore making the 
development process time consuming. By changing one factor at the time, the effect of the specific 
==PAGE:91==
Perspectives

factor is easier to evaluate than changing multiple parameters at once. This is important to 
acknowledge when planning future brining trials. In addition to this, the relation between sugars, 
flavoured alcohols and salts was shown to be important in production process regarding sensory 
properties and careful adjustments between these parameters are needed to achieve optimal sensory 
properties. 
To be able to obtain good sensory and antimicrobial properties and possibly reduce costs of alcohol 
preservation, an option could be to have pure ethanol with an added flavoured extract. In addition to 
affordability, also flexibility could increase as the type of flavouring can be chosen freely. In addition 
to these, the volumes being added to a product would be smaller than by using a beverage with 40 % 
ABV. This aspect is important when using absorbent pads with limiting liquid holding capacity.
