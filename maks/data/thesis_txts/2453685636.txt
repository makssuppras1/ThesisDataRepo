Abstract
Diabetes is a serious chronic disease, which globally aﬀects more than 400 million patients - and
the number is constantly increasing. Patients suﬀering from type 1 diabetes require delivery
of exogenous insulin to survive.
Although, there are many great insulin therapy solutions
available on the market there are still some challenges connected to these such as managing
the overall glycemic control. Beta cell therapy is a possible way to overcome this challenge and
several studies have already shown promising results. One of the major obstacles in cell therapy,
however, is the hypoxic environment the therapeutic cell are exposed to upon transplantation. In
this study, hydrogels composed of thiolated hyaluronic acid (HA), 8-arm-Poly(ethylene glycol)-
Acrylate (PEGA), and calcium peroxide (CPO), were manufactured to obtain an oxygen releasing
hydrogel system. HA is a hydrophilic natural polymer that like the synthetic PEGA is known
for its biocompatible and non-immunogenic properties.
Hydrogels containing 0, 7.5, and 30% CPO were successfully manufactured and the presence
of CPO was conﬁrmed by FTIR and Alizarin Red. An oxygen release study was conducted to
evaluate the oxygen release potential of the hydrogel systems. Here it was found that hydrogels
containing 7.5% CPO released oxygen for at least 10 h, whereas hydrogels containing 30% CPO
released oxygen for at least 30 h. The hydrogels were characterized in terms of their mechanical
and rheological properties. All three hydrogel conditions were found to be injectable and suitable
for beta cell therapy in terms of their Young’s modulus. Additionally, CPO was found to increase
the gelation process. Insulin secreting INS-1E reporter cells were successfully encapsulated to
investigate their viability and functionality. The viability study indicated that CPO increased cell
survival of the encapsulated clusters, most likely by providing oxygen; however, the functionality
study indicated impaired insulin secretion upon encapsulation in CPO containing hydrogels.

Dansk Resumé
Diabetes er en alvorlig kronisk sygdom, der globalt rammer over 400 millioner mennesker - og
antallet er konstant stigende. Patienter, der lider af type 1-diabetes, kræver eksogent insulin
for at kunne overleve. Selvom der er mange gode behandlingsmuligheder tilgængelige for denne
patientgruppe, er der stadig udfordringer forbundet med behandlingen, såsom styring af den
overordnede glykæmiske kontrol. Betacelleterapi er en mulig måde at overvinde denne udfordring
på, og ﬂere studier har allerede vist lovende resultater. En af de største hindringer i celleterapi
er imidlertid det hypoxiske miljø, som de terapeutiske celler udsættes for ved transplantation.
I dette studie blev hydrogeler, lavet af thioleret hyaluronsyre (HA), 8-arm-Poly(ethylenglycol)-
Acrylat (PEGA) og calciumperoxid (CPO), fremstillet for at opnå et iltfrigivende hydrogel-
system. HA er en hydroﬁl naturlig polymer, der ligesom PEGA er kendt for sine biokompatible
og ikke-immunogene egenskaber.
Hydrogeler indeholdende 0, 7,5 og 30% CPO blev fremstillet, og tilstedeværelsen af CPO blev
bekræftet ved brug af FTIR og Alizarin Red. Et oxygen release studie blev udført for at evaluere
hydrogelernes iltfrigivelsespotentiale.
Her blev det fundet, at hydrogeler indeholdende 7,5%
CPO frigav ilt i mindst 10 timer, mens hydrogeler indeholdende 30% CPO frigav ilt i mindst 30
timer. Hydrogelerne blev karakteriseret i forhold til deres mekaniske og reologiske egenskaber.
Alle tre hydrogelsystemer viste sig at være injicerbare og egnede til betacelleterapi med hensyn
til deres Young’s modulus.
Derudover viste det sig, at CPO forøgede geleringsprocessen.
Insulinsekreterende INS-1E reporterceller blev indkapslet for at undersøge deres viabilitet og
funktionalitet. Et viabilitetsstudie indikerede, at CPO forøgede celleoverlevelse af de indkapslede
celle-clusters, men funktionalitetsstudiet indikerede derimod nedsat insulin secernering efter
indkapsling i CPO-indeholdende hydrogeler.

Introduction 1
Diabetes is a serious disease which aﬀects more than 400 million patients world wide - and the
number keeps increasing [World Health Organization, 2016], [American Diabetes Association,
2018]. In 1980 4.7% of the world population suﬀered from diabetes, whereas this number was
increased to 8.5% in 2014 [World Health Organization, 2016].
Patients suﬀering from type 1 diabetes require exogenous delivery of insulin to survive. Today
there are many diﬀerent varieties of insulin therapies available which are becoming better and
better.
However, there are still some challenges connected to the existing therapies such as
managing the insulin dosing and thereby maintaining the overall glycemic control [Rezania et al.,
2014]. Poor glycemic control is associated with long-term complications such as cardiovascular
diseases, diabetic nephropathy, retinopathy, and peripheral neuropathy [Cleary et al., 2005],
[Michael J. Fowler, 2008]. The World Health Organization stated in 2012 that 3.7 million people
that year died from diabetes or diabetes-related complications [World Health Organization,
2016]. In addition to the increasing fatality number, diabetes is also associated with tremendous
economical costs. All this makes diabetes a major public health problem that is in need of a
solution!
A solution to this increasing problem may be found at the crossroad of cell therapy and tissue
engineering. By combining these ﬁelds it may be possible to not only treat but also cure diabetes
and thereby decrease or maybe even remove this problem entirely from the medical landscape. By
improving the glycemic control the before-mentioned complications associated with diabetes can
be minimized [Willi et al., 2010]. The following sections seek to describe the important factors
to consider when applying cell therapy and tissue engineering for the treatment of diabetes.
1.1
Insulin
Insulin is an endocrine hormone produced by pancreatic beta cells. The mature insulin molecule
consists of two polypeptide chains - an A-chain and a B-chain - that are connected by two
disulphide bonds.
The A-chain is composed of 21 amino acids and the B-chain contains 30
amino acids. Insulin regulates the carbohydrate metabolism by promoting uptake of glucose
from the blood and into diﬀerent tissues via the glucose transporter protein GLUT4 [Huang og
Czech, 2007], [Walsh, 2003].
After a meal, the blood glucose increases and stimulates an immediate pancreatic insulin release
from the storage granules of the beta cells. This is also called the ﬁrst phase secretion of insulin.
As previously mentioned, insulin has a dimeric structure, however, it is synthesized as one single
polypeptide chain called pre-proinsulin. As illustrated in ﬁgure 1.1, pre-proinsulin is composed
of 4 units; signal sequence, A-chain, B-chain, and C-chain [Walsh, 2003].

In the endoplasmic reticulum the signal sequence is enzymatically removed before the now
proinsulin is packed in vesicles and fused with the Golgi apparatus. After budding oﬀfrom
the Golgi apparatus proinsulin is proteolytically processed in the vesicles by removing the C-
chain allowing the A- and B-chain to cross-link to yield the mature insulin protein. Insulin is
then stored in the vesicles and are upon transportation to the cell membrane ready for the ﬁrst
phase insulin secretion [Walsh, 2003].
When insulin is secreted it travels via the blood to its target tissues. Even-though diﬀerent cells
in the human body express insulin receptors, the main target cells of insulin are the skeletal
muscle ﬁbres, adipocytes, and hepatocytes.
The insulin receptor is an integral glycoprotein
composed of four amino acid chains; two α-chains and two β-chains. Both α-chains are located
on the extracellular side, whereas the two β-chains reside both on the intra- and extracellular
side of the membrane. The binding of insulin to its receptor initiates diﬀerent complex signal
transduction processes in the diﬀerent target cells. In brief, insulin stimulates glycolysis and
glycogenesis in the liver. In the muscle tissue, insulin stimulates glycolysis, glycogenesis, protein
synthesis, and increases glucose and amino acid uptake from the blood into the cells. Lastly,
insulin stimulates glucose uptake and lipogenesis in the adipose tissue [Walsh, 2003].
1.2
Diabetes
Diabetes is as stated previously a possibly fatal disease that aﬀects millions of people world wide.
It is divided into two 3 major types of diabetes; type 1 diabetes, type 2 diabetes, and gestationel
diabetes (GD) [American Diabetes Association, 2018].
Type 1 diabetes is a common and chronic autoimmune disease which leads to destruction of the
insulin producing beta cells. This autoimmune destruction results in an almost complete loss
of the patient’s insulin production, making patients dependent on exogenous insulin for survival
[Quiskamp et al., 2015],[Burrack et al., 2017]. The autoimmune destruction of the pancreatic beta
cells is mediated by auto-reactive T-cells. Type 1 diabetes is often diagnosed in the childhood

Type 1 diabetes accounts for approximately
5-10% of the total cases of diabetes [American Diabetes Association, 2018]. Some patients are
genetically predisposed to type 1 diabetes, other predisposing factors are, however, not fully
understood yet. Type 2 diabetes, on the other hand, is associated with lifestyle and obesity and
accounts for the majority of the total diabetes cases. Patients suﬀering from this disease have
reduced responsiveness to insulin in the target cells – a state called insulin resistance. Type
2 diabetes does not directly lead to loss of insulin production. However, at the initial stage
of the disease, the beta cells secrete an increased amount of insulin to maintain blood glucose
homeostasis. The increased secretion of insulin will eventually lead to exhaustion of beta cells
and as a result reduced or loss of insulin production [Quiskamp et al., 2015], [World Health
Organization, 2016]. Even though type 1 and 2 diabetes are diﬀerent diseases the ﬁnal outcome
is the same; insuﬃcient uptake of glucose to the tissue and thus hyperglycemia (chronic increase
of blood glucose concentrations).
Hyperglycemia may acutely lead to ketoacidosis, which if
not treated, is life-threatening and on the long term may lead to secondary side eﬀects such
as organ dysfunction and cardiovascular diseases [Quiskamp et al., 2015]. GD is like type 2
diabetes deﬁned as glucose intolerance and is strongly associated with obesity. This type of
diabetes is detected at the second or third trimester of pregnancy and aﬀects up to 5% of all
pregnancies in the United States [American Diabetes Association, 2018], [Bellamy et al., 2009].
The glucose homoeostasis of the patient is restored to normal non-pregnancy values after the
delivery. However, the women diagnosed with GD are in an increased risk of developing type 2
diabetes in the future [Bellamy et al., 2009].
This project will focus on type 1 diabetes, hence the following sections will mainly be described
in relation to this speciﬁc disease.
1.2.1
Current Treatment
As stated in the beginning of section 1, the current treatment to type 1 diabetes is exogenous
delivery of insulin by subcutaneous administration. This treatment, however, demands constant
attention and compliance in order to keep the blood glucose in balance and the daily glucose
ﬂuctuations of a diabetic patient remain higher compared to healthy individuals [Wilson et al.,
2015]. As stated previously glycaemic unbalance is associated with diﬀerent fatal diseases and
complications.
Because of the problems associated with the current treatments, there is an
ongoing search for alternative and improved methods to treat diabetes. One of the promising
methods that have been highly investigated is cell therapy by beta cells or pancreatic islets [An
et al., 2017].
1.3
Cell Therapy
In cell therapy the cellular material is implanted or injected into a patient to treat a speciﬁc
disease. It may be used as a treatment for several diﬀerent diseases, such as Chron’s disease,
type 1 diabetes, osteoporosis, and Parkinson’s disease [Van Blitterswijk og De Boer, 2015],
[Hasany et al., 2018]. Even though cell therapy has shown promising results, there are some
issues connected to this method. One of the problems associated with cell therapy is the low
therapeutic eﬀects [Haraguchi et al., 2012]. If the cells are delivered by syringe based delivery

due to mechanical shear stress introduced upon injection [Agashi et al., 2009]. Hence, if the cells

This challenge, however, can be overcome by either using

with immuno-protective systems, or tissue engineering [An et al., 2017], [Ludwig et al., 2017].
1.3.1
Cell types
There are diﬀerent cell therapy approaches when it comes down to the type and source of cells.
One approach is islet transplantation from a deceased donor to a diabetic patient. Pancreatic
islets are located in the endocrine compartment of the pancreas that among other cell types
contains four hormone producing cells; alpha cells (glucagon), beta cells (insulin), delta cells
(somatostatin), and PP cells (pancreatic polypeptide). The insulin producing beta cells comprise
the majority of the total amount of islet cells [Lumelsky et al., 2001], [Dolenšek et al., 2015]. Islet
transplantation has shown a successful control of the glucose levels; however several drawbacks
are associated with this type of cell therapy. These include shortage of transplantable islets
and the inability of islets to be expanded in vitro [Wilson et al., 2015], [Beck et al., 2007].
Additionally, there are further complications associated with this method given that the donor
islets are foreign to the patient.
Hence, a life-long immuno-suppression must be taken into
account and compared with the potential beneﬁts when considering this treatment [Beck et al.,
2007]
Diﬀerentiated stem cells are also an option for cell therapy. A stem cell is an unspecialized
cell that by cell division is capable of renewing itself or diﬀerentiating into a more specialized
cells with new phenotypes and functions [Van Blitterswijk og De Boer, 2015]. As stem cells can
proliferate and renew themselves it is possible to expand them in vitro. After cell expansion, it
is possible to aﬀect and control the diﬀerentiation into tissue-speciﬁc cells. For these two reasons
stem cells may be very useful in many cell therapy applications [Van Blitterswijk og De Boer,
2015], [D’Amour et al., 2006].
There are diﬀerent stages of stem cells. The most unspecialized stem cell type is the totipotent
stem cell, which has the ability to divide and form the entire human organism. A totipotent
stem cell can diﬀerentiate into pluripotent stem cells, which are the cells inside of the blastocyst
and are able to give rise to the three germ layers of the body. When pluripotent stem cells
are cultured they are called embryonic stem cells (ESC). Diﬀerent studies have previously
reported diﬀerentiation from human or mouse ESC to insulin producing cells [Assady et al., 2001],
[D’Amour et al., 2006], [León-Quinto et al., 2004]. ESC is harvested from blastocysts generated
by in vitro fertilization and hence the research is controversial [Quiskamp et al., 2015]. Another
way to obtain pluripotent cells without harvesting ESC from the blastocyst is to re-program
specialized cells back to the pluripotent state by genetic engineering. These de-diﬀerentiated
cells are called induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSC). IPSC’s may be generated by adenoviruses
that encode and express genes such as Nanog, Oct4, and Sox2, which regulate the pluripotency
and are critical for the re-programming. By using iPSC reprogrammed from patient-derived
cells for cell therapy it may be possible to eliminate the need for immunosuppressive drugs since

1.4
Tissue Engineering
Because of the before-mentioned problems (section 1.3), cell therapy alone may not solve the
problems associated with type 1 diabetes.
However, a solution may be found by combining
cell therapy with tissue engineering. In tissue engineering cells are seeded on or encapsulated
in a three-dimensional (3D) construct made from biomaterials.
These constructs may be
used as carrier systems for the cells upon transplantation by providing physical support and
protection. By incorporating essential growth factors or speciﬁc stimuli the construct may also
serve as a template for cell interaction and control the fate of the cells.
Depending on the
intended application, these constructs may positively aﬀect the diﬀerentiation, proliferation, and
vascularization [Van Blitterswijk og De Boer, 2015], [Haraguchi et al., 2012], [Derakhshanfar
et al., 2018]. Furthermore, an ideal construct should protect the transplanted cells from the host
immune response and create a transplantation environment that promotes survival of the cells
[Weber et al., 2009]. Figure 1.2 illustrates the diﬀerent factors that are important to consider in
tissue engineering.

implanted construct. Because of this, factors, such as VEGF, that are stimulating angiogenesis
are highly studied [Weber et al., 2009].
As ﬁgure 1.2 illustrates there are several diﬀerent techniques to generate constructs for tissue
engineering. Some of them are 3D-printing, electrospinning, and cross-linking of hydrogel systems
[Van Blitterswijk og De Boer, 2015]. By using the additive fabrication technique, 3D-printing,
it is possible to create very well-deﬁned constructs.
A detailed model is ﬁrstly created in
a computer-aided design software before a given material is printed in the X-Y-Z-plan [Van
Blitterswijk og De Boer, 2015]. A sub-group of 3D-printing is 3D-bioprinting. This method is
an actively studied fabrication technique that combines printing of polymers and cell-solutions.
Like basic 3D-printing this allows high fabrication precision but furthermore high control of cell
distribution [Derakhshanfar et al., 2018].
Electrospinning is a widely employed ﬁber production technique that may be used to fabricate
tissue engineering constructs. In electrospinning a polymer solution is pumped to a ﬂat-tipped
needle and an electrical force is applied to the needle and a collector platform. This results in
an ejection of a continuous polymer jet from the needle that forms a polymer ﬁber mesh at the
collector platform. Depending on conditions such as distance from the needle to the collector,
ﬂow rate and voltage the ﬁbers produced by electrospinning can vary in size from nm scale to
µm scale [Bhardwaj og Kundu, 2010], [Van Blitterswijk og De Boer, 2015].
This Master thesis will focus on hydrogels as a carrier system for cells, hence the following
sections seek to give an in-depth description of the principle of hydrogels, their properties and
the many diﬀerent materials that may be used to generate this type of construct.
1.5
Hydrogels
It is possible to fabricate constructs by cross-linking polymers into a highly hydrated 3D network
called hydrogels. Hydrogels have the ability to absorb and contain a high amount of water -
in their hydrated state the mass of water is typically much larger than the mass fraction of
polymers. The ability to absorb water is dependent on the amount of hydrophilic groups of the
polymer [Mahinroosta et al., 2018], [Ahmed, 2015]. A lot of attention has been given to hydrogels
in the recent years, due to their broad application portfolio within the ﬁeld of biomedical
engineering.
Hydrogels may be manufactured to be injectable, self-healing, conductive, or
environment responsive and can, therefore, be used in a wide range of applications [Mahinroosta
et al., 2018]. By using in situ forming injectable hydrogel systems it is possible to minimize
transplantation morbidity and make the therapy less invasive to the patient [Thambi et al.,
2017].
Hydrogels can be categorized based on the type of cross-linking used in the system.
There
are two overall categories, namely chemical and physical cross-linked networks. The chemically
cross-linked systems are based on covalent bonds, whereas the physically cross-linked network
is based on transient junctions such as polymer entanglements, ionic interactions, or hydrogen
bonds [Ahmed, 2015]. When manufacturing hydrogels it is important to consider which materials
and properties are suitable for the intended application.

1.5.2
Properties
One of the most essential things to consider when working with cell therapy and tissue
engineering is biocompatibility. When the construct are meant for transplantation they must be
noncarcinogenic, nontoxic, nonantigenic, and nonteratogenic [Van Blitterswijk og De Boer, 2015].
As both HA and PEGA are considered to be biocompatible materials they are good candidates
for hydrogel fabrication.
Other properties to consider are the mechanical properties of the hydrogel.
The mechanical
properties play a crucial role in successful performance of hydrogels in tissue engineering
applications [Mehrali et al., 2017]. Toughness and Young’s modulus varies to a great extent
among diﬀerent types of tissues in the human body.
Therefore, to yield a more native-like
microenvironment for the encapsulated cells, one should assess and optimize the mechanical
properties to match the application. The Young’s modulus of an average pancreas is reported
to be ≈3 kPa [Sugimoto et al., 2014]. This means that a carrier system for pancreatic beta
cells should approximate this value for optimal support. Cell constructs with insulin secreting
cells are often implanted under the kidney sub-capsule [An et al., 2017], however, the mechanical
properties of this site are not reported in any literature.
The mechanical properties of HA
cross-linked with 8-arm-PEGA was reported by [Hasany et al., 2018] to have a low compressive
modulus and might, therefore, be a good candidate for beta cell therapy.
Depending on the intended application stability in terms of degradation is important to consider.
If the intention is to develop a retrievable and immuno-protective hydrogel long-term stability
and low degradation is critical [An et al., 2017]. However, if the hydrogel only serves as a carrier
until the transplanted cells are integrated in the tissue a lower stability is necessary.
For cells to survive in an encapsulated system like a hydrogel it is important that they are
able to interact with the surrounding tissue and environment. For optimal cell viability and
function, porosity and interconnectivity of the hydrogel are important properties to consider
and characterize.
The interconnectivity describes how well the pores of the construct are
connected. A hydrogel needs to approximate 100% interconnection to fully allow ﬂuid exchange
[Van Blitterswijk og De Boer, 2015], [Hasany et al., 2018]. It is essential that nutrients can
reach the encapsulated cells and that the metabolic waste products are removed. For diabetes
treatment it is likewise essential that the secreted insulin is able to reach the intended receptor
cells [Van Blitterswijk og De Boer, 2015], [Hasany et al., 2018], [Evron et al., 2018]. The pore size
aﬀects what is able to exit and enter the system. Depending on the cells used for encapsulation the
hydrogels should be able to protect the cells from the patients immune cells while simultaneously
allowing the exchange of nutrients, waste products, and hormones to maintain both cell survival
and function [An et al., 2017]. Lastly, the encapsulated cells must be supplied with suﬃcient
oxygen as it is critical for both cell survival and function [Colton, 2014].
1.6
Oxygen demand
As stated above, encapsulated cells must have access to oxygen to survive and function. The
oxygen partial pressure (pO2) at the surface of an average islet should be approximately 35
mmHg for maintaining full viability of all cells and around 45 mmHg for maintaining full
functionality throughout the islet [Colton, 2014].
Pancreatic islets receive up to 15% of the

1.6.1

One way to yield an oxygen releasing hydrogel is by incorporating solid calcium peroxide (CPO)

reported an oxygen release from the CPO laden gelatin methacryloyl (GelMA) hydrogel over 5
days [Alemdar et al., 2017]. Solid peroxides, such as CPO, decompose upon contact with water
to release oxygen [Camci-Unal et al., 2013] (see following reactions).

However, the intermediate of this reaction is H2O2, which may have unwanted side eﬀects on the

is often used as a catalyst. Catalase has a high turnover number and catalyzes the conversion
of H2O2 into oxygen and water. It is found in almost all living organisms and protects the cells
from reactive oxygen species that otherwise would cause oxidative damage [Camci-Unal et al.,

2.2
Incorporation of CPO
To investigate if CPO was successfully incorporated into the hydrogels and for how long it
stayed in the system, Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) and Alizarin Red were
used. Additionally, an oxygen release study was conducted to assess the oxygen release potential
of the hydrogels. All three experiments were carried out on hydrogels containing three diﬀerent
concentrations of CPO; 0%, 7.5%, and 30% (w/wHA) fabricated according to section 2.1 and
ﬁgure 2.1A.
2.2.1
Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy
FTIR may be used to examine the chemical interactions of a material by investigating the
vibrational freedom of molecules, which the material is composed of [Petit og Madejova, 2013].
In this project, FTIR was used to identify the presence of CPO and its derivatives to track its
removal from the cross-linked hydrogel system.
Hydrogels containing 0%, 7.5%, and 30% CPO were made according to section 2.1 and incubated
at 37°C in DPBS (technical replicates = 4). The hydrogels were analyzed at day 1, 3, 5, and
7, where they were washed in DPBS prior to lyophilisation for 24 h. The FTIR spectra of each
hydrogel condition were obtained using Spectrum 100 FTIR spectrometer (PerkinElmer, MA,
USA). Before the samples were analyzed, a background subtraction (no samples) was conducted
to remove noise introduced from the background. The FTIR spectra were recorded from 4000-530
cm-1. The FTIR spectrometer was set to 16 scans at a resolution of 4 cm-1. After recording the
spectra they were all baseline-corrected and normalized using PerkinElmer Spectrum software.
For comparison, an FTIR spectrum was obtained for pure CPO.
2.2.2
Alizarin Red
Alizarin Red staining was used to qualify the calcium present in the hydrogels.
As CPO is
composed of calcium, Alizarin Red was used to indicate successful CPO incorporation.
As
Alizarin Red reacts with and stains calcium the color formation was only an indication of CPO
and not necessarily a correlation.
Alizarin Red staining was conducted on 10 µL hydrogels (ﬁgure 2.1A) incubated at 1, 3, 5 and
7 days in DPBS at 37 °C (technical replicates = 3). A 2% (w/v) Alizarin Red solution was
prepared by dissolving 1 g of Alizarin Red (Sigma Aldrich, Germany) in 50 mL calcium-free
DPBS. The pH was adjusted to 4.2±0.1 using 1 M HCl and the solution was subsequently
ﬁltered through a 0.45 µm syringe ﬁlter. Prior to the staining, hydrogels were washed twice with
calcium-free DPBS to avoid contamination of any calcium released during incubation. 20 µL
Alizarin Red dye was added on top of each hydrogel before they were incubated without light
at 37 °C for 1 h. After incubation, all hydrogels were washed twice with calcium-free DPBS and
incubated without light in DPBS overnight to remove excess dye until the 0% CPO hydrogels
were transparent. The hydrogels were imaged by bright ﬁeld microscopy the following day. The
color intensity of the microscopic images was measured using Image J 2.0 and used for further
comparison of the three conditions.

2.3.3
Scanning electron microscopy
The morphology and pore size of the three diﬀerent hydrogel conditions were assessed using

beam. The size of the interaction volume depends on the material and the acceleration voltage
of electrons.
A low acceleration voltage will give information about the surface topography,
whereas a high acceleration voltage gives information about the sample composition[Goldstein
et al., 2003]. 200 µL hydrogels containing 0, 7.5, and 30% CPO were prepared according to section

DPBS and incubated for 14 hours to allow swelling of the system. After incubation, the hydrogels
were washed twice with Mili-Q water prior to lyophilisation for 24 h. The lyophilized hydrogels
were cut with a scalpel to visualize the inside of them. As the hydrogels were not conductive,
they were coated with gold particles using a Q150R Plus Rotary Pumped Coater (Quorum
Technologies, UK). To analyze the pore morphology of the hydrogels, they were investigated
using SEM-Quanta FEG-250 (FEI Company, OR, USA) operated with an acceleration voltage
of 4 kV and a spot size of 2.5. The pore sizes were determined from eight images per hydrogel
condition using Image J 2.0 software.

Cluster formation
Cell clusters were formed from single INS-1E cells to mimic the nature of pancreatic islets.
The cluster formation was done by seeding 7.5×106 single cells in 10 mL growth medium in
a nonadherent 6 cm petri dish before adding 10 µM Rock-Inhibitor (Roche, Germany) and 50
µg/mL DNase (Sigma Aldrich, Germany). Rock-Inhibitor was used to maintain beta cell function
and enhance survival [Ghazizadeh et al., 2017], whereas DNase was used to avoid any DNA from
aﬀecting the clustering process, cluster size, and cluster morphology. The cells were incubated
at 37°C, 5% CO2, and 90% humidity at 70 RPM for two days to obtain ≈800 islet equivalent
(IEQ)/mL. IEQ is a standardized volume measurement of islets, where 1 IEQ corresponds to an
islet diameter of 150 µm [Ramachandran et al., 2015].

Oxygen consumption
The oxygen consumption rate (OCR) of naked and encapsulated INS-1E clusters was measured

OCR was used to investigate the oxygen consumption of INS-1E clusters.
Micro Oxygen
Monitoring System (Instech Laboratories, PA, USA) and Instech Viewer software were used
to conduct the OCR measurements, where the oxygen concentration was measured based on
ﬂuorescence quenching. Each sensor was calibrated by a two-point calibration. 0% O2 calibration
was obtained by adding 180 µL sodium hydrosulﬁte (≥82%, Sigma Aldrich, Germany) dissolved
in deionized water 12% (v/v) to each sensor chamber. A 19.6% O2 calibration was obtained by
addition of 180 µL freshly prepared and pre-heated RPMI 1640 medium to each chamber. The

Glucose solution (Sigma Aldrich, Germany), and 20 mM HEPES buﬀer solution (Sigma Aldrich,
Germany). The medium was left in the sensor chamber for 45 min with stirring to ensure that
the equilibration of oxygen was reached. INS-1E clusters were counted on an Automatic Islet Cell
Counter (Biorep Technologies, FL, USA) and 500 IEQ were transferred to each sensor chamber
and used for the OCR measurement of naked clusters (n=3, with 4 technical replicates each
time).
2.6
Cell encapsulation
Two days after cluster formation INS-1E clusters were counted by an Automatic Islet Cell
Counter and encapsulated with a cluster density of 10 IEQ/µL in hydrogels containing either
0%, 7.5%, or 30% CPO. Cell clusters were mixed with HA prior to the addition of CPO and
8-arm-PEGA. The cell-polymer solution was deposited in the desired casting molds and left
to cross-link according to section 2.1. Hydrogels were incubated in growth medium containing
100U/mL catalase prior to the cellular experiments.

2.7.2
Live/dead staining
Live/dead staining is an assay that may be used to visualize and discriminate viable cells
from dead cells. Calcein-acetoxymethyl (calcein-AM) is a lipid-soluble non-ﬂuorescent molecule
that can passively enter through the cell membrane of living cells and be converted into the
lipid-insoluble green ﬂuorescent calcein molecule. This conversion is catalyzed by intracellular
esterases of viable cells which means that the green color formation only appears in living cells
[Neri et al., 2001]. Ethidium homodimer-1 (EthD-1) is a membrane-impermeable red ﬂuorescent
molecule that binds to DNA. As EthD-1 cannot cross the membrane it only stains the DNA of
cells with disrupted cell membranes - hence, dead cells [Sigma-Aldrich].
LIVE/DEAD Viability/Cytotoxicity Kit (Invitrogen Thermoﬁsher, MA, USA) was used to carry
out the live/dead staining. A fresh dye solution consisting of RPMI 1640, 5 µg/mL calcein-AM,
and 5.1 µg/mL EthD-1, was prepared before use. Encapsulated clusters were washed with DPBS
before each hydrogel was removed from its mold and stained with 20 µL dye. For comparison,
≈100 IEQ naked clusters were transferred to a 48 well plate and likewise washed and stained
with 100 µL dye. The encapsulated and naked clusters were incubated without light at 37°C for
20 minutes before they were imaged by Nikon inverted Spinning Disk confocal Ti2 microscope
(Nikon, Japan) and Nikon NIS Elements Imaging Software (Nikon, Japan). Calcein was excited
at a wavelength of 488 nm, whereas EthD-1 was excited at 517 nm. To yield 3D images of the
clusters at 4X and 20X magniﬁcation, the z-stacks were chosen to be 2 µm and 1 µm, respectively.
Additionally, distribution of the encapsulated clusters was visualized at day 1 at a wavelength
of 488 nm using 2 µm z-stacks over 300 µm.

2.8.1
Insulin quantiﬁcation
The secretory outputs were analyzed for insulin using a luminescence oxygen channeling
immunoassay (LOCI). The insulin quantiﬁcation was conducted by the Novo Nordisk Reseach
Bioanalysis department and will thus not be described in detail in this Master thesis. In brief,
LOCI is a bead-based immunoassay that works by adding anti-insulin antibodies conjugated
acceptor beads and biotinylated anti-insulin antibodies (binding to a diﬀerent epitope on insulin)
to the sample.
After incubation for 1 h, streptavidin-coated donor beads are added to the
samples. Antibody conjugated acceptor beads, biotinylated antibodies, and streptavidin-coated
beads form a complex together with insulin, that upon illumination triggers a release of singlet
oxygen from the donor beads leading to chemiluminescence from the acceptor beads.
The
chemiluminescence can be measured by a plate reader, where the amount of generated light
is proportional to the insulin concentration. The lower detection limit of this assay is 5 ng/mL.

Luciferase qualiﬁcation
To qualify the amount of released luciferase the ﬂow-through was collected from the perifusion

Nano-Glo Luciferase Assay Substrate was mixed with Nano-Glo Luciferase Assay Buﬀer to reach

analyzed by their luminescence intensity using Spectramax M4 Microplate Reader (Molecular
Devices, CA, USA). The luciferase measurements were plotted against time and normalized to
the number of clusters. To compare the amount of luciferase produced during the 20 mM glucose
challenge the AUC from 120 - 215 min was calculated for each condition (naked, 0, 7.5, and 30%
CPO). Lastly, the oﬀ-set delay of luciferase secretion from encapsulated clusters was calculated
and compared to the luciferase secretion oﬀ-set of naked clusters.

DNA quantiﬁcation
To normalize the results obtained from perifusion the assayed INS-1E clusters were collected
from the perifusion column and washed with Hank’s Balanced Salt Solution (HBSS). Before
DNA quantiﬁcation, the clusters were lysed by adding 400 µL lysis buﬀer containing 0.2% (v/v)
Triton X-100 (Sigma Aldrich, Germany) and 1 M ammonium hydroxide (Acros Organics, Fisher
Scientiﬁc). Then clusters were mechanically lysed by Tissue Lyser instrument (Qiagen, Germany)
(3 min, 20 Hz) and afterwards sonicated using Branson Digital Soniﬁer (Buch & Holm, Denmark)
for 2×30 sec at 40 kHz.
The double stranded (ds) DNA was quantiﬁed using the ﬂuorescence-based Qunat-iT PicoGreen
dsDNA assay kit (Invitrogen Thermoﬁsher, MA, USA). The ﬂuorescent probe PicoGreen may
bind to dsDNA to form a luminescent complex that enables quantiﬁcation of dsDNA [Dragan
et al., 2010].
The provided TE Buﬀer was diluted in DNAase-free water to reach a 1X TE buﬀer solution.
DNA standard was prepared by diluting the provided Lambda DNA in 1X TE buﬀer to reach a
DNA concentration of 2µg/mL. The DNA standard was used to prepare a standard curve ranging
from 1 to 600 ng DNA/mL for quantiﬁcation of the sample DNA. After lysing, the samples were
spun down at 10,000×g and 5 µL of the supernatant was loaded in a 96-well plate (3340, Corning,
NY, USA) together with 95 µL 1X TE buﬀer. 100 µL PicoGreen reagent was added to each well
prior to incubation at room temperature without light for 3 min. The samples were excited at

Results and Discussion 3
The aim of this thesis was to develop an oxygen releasing hydrogel suitable for beta cell therapy.
To assess whether this aim was met, hydrogels containing 0, 7.5, and 30% CPO were characterized
in terms of their CPO content, oxygen releasing properties, mechanical and rheological properties,
and morphology (track 1). To investigate if the above-mentioned hydrogel conditions allowed
optimal survival and function the cells, viability and functionality of the insulin secreting INS-1E
clusters were likewise characterized (track 2).
Hydrogels with a volume of 10, 20, 30, and 200 µL were successfully manufactured and used
for both hydrogel characterization and cell studies. The following sections seek to present and
discuss results obtained during this Master thesis.
3.1
Incorporation of CPO
FTIR spectroscopy and Alizarin Red staining were used to investigate the incorporation of CPO
and its stability over time. All data are presented for hydrogels containing 0, 7.5, and 30% CPO
after 1, 3, 5, and 7 days of incubation.
3.1.1
FTIR
The FTIR spectra obtained from hydrogels containing 0, 7.5, and 30% CPO are presented in
ﬁgure 3.1A and B. To conﬁrm the presence of 8-arm-PEGA in the cross-linked network, the
obtained spectra were compared to the ﬁndings of [Hasany et al., 2018], who previously have
chemically characterized the same cross-linked thiolated HA-8-arm-PEGA system. The presence

caused by C-O stretching vibrations and was likewise observed by Hasany and coworkers from the
cross-linked hydrogel, pure thiolated HA, and 8-arm-PEGA [Hasany et al., 2018]. In hydrogels
containing 7.5%, the same double peak was observed, however, at day 1 and day 3 the left and
right part of the peak were equal in size. Day 5 and day 7 were similar to the observations

3.1.2
Alizarin Red
To further asses the incorporation of CPO, Alizarin Red staining of hydrogels containing 0, 7.5,
and 30% CPO was conducted at day 1, 3, 5, and 7 (ﬁgure 3.2A). Hydrogels containing 0%
CPO appeared, as expected, transparent at all time points. Both 7.5% and 30% CPO were
stained red at all four time points and calcium was observed to be homogeneously distributed
throughout the entire hydrogel (ﬁgure 3.2A). Homogeneous distribution of CPO is necessary to
obtain an even oxygen release throughout the system, which is vital for the cell viability and
functionality of all encapsulated clusters. The color did not vanish during the 7 days, indicating
that calcium was still present in the hydrogels after one week of incubation. This observation was
in alignment with the ﬁndings of the FTIR analysis. As stated previously, CPO is converted into
calcium hydroxide and since the FTIR spectra (ﬁgure 3.1B) indicated that calcium hydroxide was
formed and possibly interacted with the gel matrix, the stained hydrogels were most likely due to
hydrogen bonds or electrostatic interactions between the hydrogel matrix and calcium hydroxide.
The color intensity was found to be highest for hydrogels containing 30% CPO compared to
hydrogels containing 7.5 and 0% CPO (ﬁgure 3.2B), indicating, like the FTIR spectra (ﬁgure
3.1B), that the CPO concentration was successfully increased from 7.5% to 30%. To further
investigate if or when the saturation of CPO in the system is reached, CPO concentrations
below and above 30% could be assessed with Alizarin Red and FTIR.
The results obtained from FTIR and Alizarin Red both suggested that CPO was successfully
incorporated in the hydrogel matrix and that CPO was continuously converted into calcium
hydroxide, which then remained in the hydrogel system. The FTIR results further indicated
that CPO was present for at least 3 and 5 days of incubation for hydrogels containing 7.5 and
30%, respectively. Additionally, the FTIR spectra indicated that the hydrogels were degraded
over time. A degradation study could be conducted by assessing the material weight loss over
time to further examine the degradation of the system.
Since both FTIR and Alizarin Red
experiments were conducted only once with 4 technical replicates each they need to be repeated
to fully conclude on the CPO incorporation.

Another reason for the short oxygen release could be explained by the diﬀusion of oxygen in
water. After the ﬁnalized experiment, it was observed that the hydrogels were ﬂoating at the
top of the 3 mL DPBS. As mentioned before, oxygen diﬀusion is limited in water and it could,
therefore, be speculated that the oxygen release was greater and longer than reported in ﬁgure
3.3B. The FTIR results (ﬁgure 3.1A) indicated that CPO was present for at least 3 and 5 days
for hydrogels containing 7.5 and 30% CPO, respectively. Therefore, it is likely that the relatively
short oxygen release was a consequence of the experimental setup.
A way to overcome the
problem connected to the ﬂoating hydrogels could be to cast them in a mold to prevent the
bouncy of the hydrogels, and thereby maintaining the proximity to the sensor throughout the
release study. In this experiment the hydrogels were cast in syringes as illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1
and transferred to the vial when they were fully cross-linked. By keeping the hydrogels in molds
might, however, inﬂuence the oxygen release, as some parts of the hydrogels would be covered.
Another way to investigate if the hydrogels still produced oxygen after 10 and 30 h, could be
to place an additional sensor spot at the top of the liquid. In this way, it would be possible to
detect oxygen produced by the ﬂoating hydrogel.
To further prolong the relatively rapid oxygen release presented in this study, matrix
hydrophobicity/hydrophilicity and core/shell properties might be considered. HA is a highly
hydrophilic polymer [Necas et al., 2008], which makes it a good material candidate for hydrogels,
however, its hydrophilic nature results in relatively rapid release of oxygen [Hoare og Kohane,
2008]. Using hydrophobic polymers or introducing hydrophobic elements in the construct could
remarkably decrease the oxygen release rate [Gholipourmalekabadi et al., 2016]. Hydrophobic
elements could, however, compromise the hydrogel properties such as cell-material interactions
[Van Blitterswijk og De Boer, 2015]. Another way to slow down the oxygen release could be to
introduce microcapsules or core/shell structures. CPO might be incorporated in microcapsules
or cores composed of hydrophobic polymers such as Poly(Lactic-co-Glycolic Acid) (PLGA)
or Poly(L-Lactide-co-ϵ-Caprolactone) (PLCL) to obtain a controlled and sustained release
[Gholipourmalekabadi et al., 2016],[Xu et al., 2019]. By creating a controlled release it would
be expected that the instant bulk release of oxygen (ﬁgure 3.3A) would decrease and instead be
released throughout the whole incubation period. To provide an optimal environment for the
therapeutic cells around the hydrophobic core or capsules a hydrophilic polymer such as HA
might be used as a lubricant shell material.
To compare the diﬀerences between the oxygen release reported from hydrogels containing 0, 7.5
and 30% CPO, the AUC was calculated from the release curves (ﬁgure 3.3B). As indicated by the

fracture, and hence makes it more robust and easier to handle. CPO signiﬁcantly increased both

interaction between calcium hydroxide and the cross-linked polymer matrix. As reported from
both the FTIR and Alizarin Red data, calcium hydroxide was expected to chemically interact
with the system. If the interaction was, as speculated previously, due to hydrogen bonds between
HA and calcium hydroxide, it could be expected that these bonds intensiﬁed the cross-linking of
the system and hence increased the ultimate stress and toughness of the CPO laden hydrogels.
The strain breakage of a hydrogel determines how compressible the system is. The strain breakage
of all three hydrogel conditions was reported to be 40-55%, which means that they can be
compressed to approximately half of their original size before breakage. The strain breakage of
hydrogels containing 7.5 and 30% CPO were found to be signiﬁcantly larger than for hydrogels
containing 0% CPO.
3.3.2
Rheological properties
The rheological properties for each polymer solution (0, 7.5, and 30% CPO) were evaluated in
terms of their gelation point and shear thinning properties (ﬁgure 3.5A and B).
The gelation point of a polymer solution is the onset of gelation and marks the transition from
liquid to solid. At this point, the polymer solution is no longer a liquid but at a solidlike gel
state. The gelation points are reported in ﬁgure 3.5A to be 530±73, 134±33, and 41±4 s for
polymer solutions containing 0, 7.5, and 30% CPO, respectively. These results demonstrated
that CPO decreased the gelation time. CPO is unstable in aqueous solutions and decomposes
into H2O2 and calcium hydroxide (section 1.6.1, reaction (1.1)). H2O2 might have induced the
cross-linking process of thiolated HA and 8-arm-PEGA by oxidizing the thiol groups on HA,
which could explain the eﬀect of CPO on the gelation point. The decrease in gelation point time
could likewise be explained by cross-linking of calcium hydroxide. Calcium hydroxide might as
speculated previously introduce hydrogen bonds between itself and HA, and might thus have
decreased the gelation time.
The shear thinning properties of the polymer solution were evaluated to assess the injectability of
the systems. An injectable hydrogel system minimizes transplantation morbidity; thus, making
the cell therapy less invasive to the patient [Thambi et al., 2017]. Figure 3.5B illustrates the
viscosity as a function of shear rate. From this ﬁgure, all three conditions were observed to
exhibit shear thinning properties, as their viscosity decreased with increasing shear rate. At low
shear rates, an increase in viscosity was observed for all conditions, which might be explained by

3.7
Functionality
A perifusion set-up was used to characterize and compare the functionality of both encapsulated
and naked INS-1E clusters. Furthermore, the set-up was used to assess if CPO, as hypothesized,
had any positive eﬀect on cell functionality. As stated in section 2.4, INS-1E clusters secrete
luciferase 1:1 with insulin, thus luciferase was used as an indicator of cell functionality. Luciferase
qualiﬁcation has diﬀerent advantages compared to insulin quantiﬁcation, such as sensitivity and

Functionality
Figure 3.13A illustrates luciferase secretion normalized to number of clusters. The clusters were
treated with three diﬀerent input solutions: 2 mM glucose, 20 mM glucose, and 20mM glucose
supplemented with IBMX and forskolin (positive control).
The naked clusters exhibited, as
expected, a transient ﬁrst phase and a smaller, but sustained second phase during the 20 mM
glucose challenge. As described previously in section 1.1, the ﬁrst phase of insulin secretion
occurs immediately after high glucose stimulation when cells release their insulin from the
readily releasable storage granules. Glucose does not only stimulate an immediate release of
insulin, but also insulin production. The second phase is the sustained secretion of the newly
synthesized insulin plus transport and release of granules stored in the reserve pool [Hou et al.,
2009]. Encapsulation of clusters, however, caused a more blunted ﬁrst-phase peak compared
to naked clusters. An explanation to this could be found in the 3D structure of the hydrogel.
Since the clusters were distributed in a 3D micro-environment, it could be argued that the
glucose reached the encapsulated clusters at diﬀerent time points and thereby gave rise to a
broader and blunted ﬁrst-phase peak. The onset of luciferase secretion from encapsulated clusters
was similar to the onset of naked clusters, however, the maximum luciferase concentration
was decreased and delayed.
This observation was seen from both 20 mM glucose challenge
and the IBMX/forskolin challenge. Both the limited secretory output in response to glucose
stimulation and the blunted ﬁrst-phase peaks have previously been reported in the literature, as
a consequence of encapsulation. Buchwald and coworkers reported limited insulin secretion and
blunted ﬁrst-phase peak, in response to encapsulation of murine and human islets in calcium
cross-linked alginate micro-beads [Buchwald et al., 2018].
Figure 3.13B illustrates the diﬀerence in percent of AUC from 120-215 min of the encapsulated
clusters compared to the naked clusters.
Compared to naked INS-1E clusters, encapsulated
clusters were found to secrete 50±23%, 20±8%, and 14±9% luciferase when encapsulated in
hydrogels containing 0, 7.5, and 30% CPO, respectively.
The luciferase secretion of clusters
encapsulated in CPO laden hydrogels was found to be signiﬁcantly lower than the naked
clusters. It was expected that the CPO containing hydrogels would positively aﬀect the cluster
functionality compared to hydrogels containing 0% CPO. However, the trend observed from
both ﬁgure 3.13A and B indicated the opposite. A reason why CPO negatively aﬀected the
luciferase secretion, might be that the cells were negatively aﬀected by the incorporation of CPO
itself, burst release of oxygen, or the production of H2O2. The viability study (ﬁgure 3.10 and
ﬁgure 3.11) did, however, not indicate any impaired cell viability due to CPO incorporation. An
important factor to consider when comparing the functionality study to the viability study is lack
of catalase in the KRBH buﬀer solution (used for perifusion). The functionality study was run for
almost ﬁve hours and the clusters encapsulated in CPO containing hydrogels might, thus, have
been negatively aﬀected by H2O2 produced during the experiment. However, as the perifusion
was carried out at day one and with a continuous ﬂow of 100 µL/min it could be argued (when
looking at the release study ﬁgure 3.3B) that only a small amount of H2O2 would be present
in the hydrogel during the perifusion experiment. Another factor to consider when comparing
the functionality results to the viability results, is that the viability studies were carried out
on 10 µL hydrogels, whereas the functionality study was conducted on 30 µL hydrogels (ﬁgure
2.1). Increased size of the construct increased the incorporated amount of CPO. The cells might,
therefore, have been negatively aﬀected by the increased burst release of oxygen. To investigate

hydrogels it might be possible to increase the secreted amount of insulin and decrease the oﬀ-set

decreasing the encapsulation diameter from 1800 µm to 700 µm [Buchwald et al., 2018].

Conclusion 4
Cell therapy has shown promising results for treatment of type 1 diabetes. However, injection-
based cell administration has shown low viability rates post transplantation. To overcome this
challenge the aim of this thesis was to develop an oxygen releasing hydrogel system suitable for
beta cell therapy. To reach the aim and test the hypothesis of increased viability and functionality
due to CPO incorporation, the experiments were run in two tracks - hydrogel characterization
(track 1) and cell viability and functionality (track 2).
Hydrogels consisting of cross-linked
thiolated HA and 8-arm-PEGA were successfully manufactured and used for both tracks.
For track 1 the incorporation of CPO was evaluated based on FTIR and Alizarin Red, which
both indicated that CPO was successfully incorporated. Furthermore, FTIR and Alizarin Red
demonstrated that calcium hydroxide remained in the system during incubation, most likely due
to hydrogen bonds or electrostatic interactions. The oxygen release potential of the hydrogels was
evaluated by a release study, which showed an oxygen release of at least 10 and 30 h for hydrogels
containing 7.5 and 30% CPO, respectively. The mechanical properties of the hydrogel systems
were characterized by a compression test, which indicated that the three hydrogel systems were
suitable for beta cell therapy in terms of their Young’s modulus. CPO was found to increase
toughness, ultimate stress and strain breakages, thus indicating, like FTIR and Alizarin Red, a
more cross-linked system. All three hydrogel conditions were found to be injectable and CPO
was found to aﬀect the gelation point, most likely due to the oxidizing eﬀect of H2O2.
For track 2 the viability was evaluated by live/dead staining and bright ﬁeld images.
The
viability study showed that CPO minimized the formation of necrotic centers after three days.
These results indicated that CPO provided suﬃcient oxygen for the INS-1E cells to survive at
least 3 days of incubation. However, as the study was carried out under normoxic conditions,
the experiment needs to be repeated under hypoxic conditions to mimic the environment of the
transplantation site. To investigate if CPO aﬀected the cell functionality in terms of insulin
secretion a perifusion experiment was conducted.
This study showed that all encapsulated
clusters were functional, but indicated that CPO negatively aﬀected insulin secretion.
This
might be due to a more cross-linked hydrogel system or that the experiments were run without
catalase to convert the oxidizing molecule H2O2 into oxygen and water.
In conclusion, the results of this Master thesis suggest that CPO containing HA-8-arm-PEGA
hydrogels have promising properties, which may be useful in beta cell therapy and in overcoming
the hypoxia challenge at the transplantation site. However, the cell functionality in the CPO
laden hydrogels needs to be further improved for optimum cell therapy.

Future Perspectives 5
This study presents promising results for beta cell therapy and for overcoming the challenge of the
initial hypoxic environment occurring upon transplantation. However, more research is needed
to fully optimize the hydrogel system. Firstly, the oxygen release needs to be fully characterized
and optimized to release oxygen for one or more weeks to match the vascularization process in
vivo. As stated previously, this could be obtained by introducing a hydrophobic core element
for CPO incorporation to the hydrogel and thus prolong the oxygen release. Furthermore, the
eﬀect of CPO on cell survival needs to be further characterized in hypoxic conditions, since this
Master thesis only characterized cell survival in normoxic conditions.
Even though HA and PEGA were chosen for their desired properties such as high
biocompatibility, it is still important to investigate their aﬀect on a foreign body reaction. A
foreign body reaction leads to ﬁbrotic encapsulation of the implanted foreign construct and may
negatively aﬀect the mass transfer through the construct [An et al., 2017], and thus impair
cell functionality and viability. Furthermore, An and coworkers reported that the size of the
construct aﬀects the foreign body reaction in terms of ﬁbrosis - thicker constructs (diameter 1.3
mm) showed less cellular overgrowth compared to thin constructs (diameter 0.5 mm) [An et al.,
2017]. Therefore, it is also important to consider the size of the hydrogels to ﬁnd the optimum
construct for transplantation both in terms of diﬀusion of nutrition/waste and the host foreign
body response.
Lastly, a swelling and degradation study should be conducted to characterize the hydration
capability and the degradation proﬁle. The ECM is naturally a highly hydrated environment,
thus the hydrogel system needs to mimic that for optimum function. If the hydrogel system is not
suﬃciently hydrated, the mass transfer and thus cell viability and functionality might become
adversely compromised. By conducting a degradation study it is possible to characterize the
stability of the material over time, and thus investigate if it matches the intended application.
