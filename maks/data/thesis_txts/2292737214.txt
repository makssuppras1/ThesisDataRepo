Abstract 
With the circular economy gaining momentum as the premier business model, companies are beginning 
to embrace the Cradle to Cradle ® (C2C) vision of a world without waste. Precious resources have the 
potential to maintain their value long after their intended use has expired as long as there is a design 
framework for upcycling them back into their natural cycle.  
Carlsberg recently launched the Carlsberg Circular Community which has the ambition to create Cradle-
to-Cradle® certified products. In doing so, they envision a world where resources can be upcycled rather 
than eventually going to waste.  There are three Carlsberg packaging solutions being evaluated from a 
C2C perspective, including the aluminium can. As has been examined and proven in previous Life Cycle 
Assessment (LCA) studies on the aluminium can, energy consumption during primary aluminium 
production is the main hotspot in the life cycle. Therefore, recycling aluminium is taking priority to 
reduce greenhouse gas emissions and the reliance on primary aluminium. These criteria for energy 
usage and recycled material are directly quantified in the C2C certification scheme in two categories: 1) 
material reutilization and 2) renewable energy and carbon management. This study examines the C2C 
requirements of these two categories and integrates the quantifiable measurements into an LCA which 
identifies and quantifies the environmental impact savings of each possible certification combination. 
The goal is to determine which C2C category should be prioritized in Carlsberg’s wish to improve the 
sustainability of their packaging solutions in the UK market.  
A baseline study of the aluminium can manufactured by Carlsberg UK considering a worst case with 0% 
recycled content and 0% renewable energy will provide a benchmark for each category. This will allow 
trends to be developed when the performance of each category is increased individually and through 
the various certification level combinations. 
The results of the C2C requirements modeled into the LCA scenarios show that material reutilization 
should be prioritized by the Carlsberg Circular Community due to larger improvements of the associated 
environmental impacts and a direct correlation between recycled content and total energy demand. 
Furthermore, the comparative analysis shows that a high level of certification is not necessarily going to 
perform better than a lower level from an LCA perspective. 
In the end, a list of specific actions will be suggested to Carlsberg related to short and long term 
objectives.

1 Introduction 
Beer is a social beverage. When friends, colleagues or teammates meet at the end of the day, they 
usually sit together, relax and enjoy a refreshing beer. They will talk about work, sports or the latest 
movies. One topic they probably do not think to discuss is sitting right in there hand: aluminium cans. 
They might admire the design of the label or feel good holding a cold one on a hot summer day. But they 
probably will not wonder what becomes of the can when they throw it away. They might not realize that 
they hold a valuable resource that can be recovered, recycled and used again and again.   
1.1 Cradle to Cradle 
Our world is starting to lose its resources. As the human population increases, demand for consumer 
goods increases and thus the supply of raw materials used to produce these goods decreases. This is 
very noticeable in food and beverage packaging as it is something used by everyone on a minute to 
minute basis. The industrial revolution introduced the idea of linear consumption which is a flow of a 
product from raw materials, to manufacture, to use and to disposal. The new reality is that a more 
sustainable way of developing products is necessary. The shift is in the form of the circular economy 
 
which is “an industrial system that is restorative or regenerative by intention and design” (EMF, 2014) 
and turns the disposal phase into a recycling loop. 
Cradle to Cradle® Design Framework (C2C) was developed by Michael Braungart and William 
McDonough as a new way of thinking about the resources we use in our products. They envision a 
consumer world that acts as nature does where “everything is effectively food for another organism or 
system.” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 3). Rather than using an item once and throwing it away, C2C creates a new 
system which turns that waste into its original biological or technical nutrients that can be infinitely 
recycled. A C2C system centers around three main principles for the design of products and services: 
eliminate the concept of waste, use renewable energy and celebrate diversity (C2CPII, 2013, p. 4) 
 
Eliminate the Concept of Waste 
1.1.1
The first principle redefines how we view waste. When we finish using an on-the-go product like the 
aluminium can, our first thought is to throw it away at the earliest convenience. Waste is viewed as just 
that: waste. C2C challenges this notion by claiming that “waste equals food” (McDonough & Braungart, 
2002). The resources used in the life cycle stages as well as the product itself should be designed to feed 
other systems. All materials should be safe for society and the environment and there should be an 
effective system in place to recover those materials at disposal and bring it back into the biological or 
technical cycle, illustrated in Figure 1. This is a promotion of a concept known as upcycling which is 
recycling without the material losing its value. If the biological and technical nutrients in a product are 
mixed to the point where it cannot be separated, that product can only be downcycled as it cannot 
return to its original cycle and will no longer have the same quality and value it once had (McDonough & 
Braungart, 2002).

Use Current Solar Income 
1.1.2
The second principle emphasizes that quality matters for the source of energy to all life cycle processes. 
An ideal C2C product system utilizes energy exclusively from renewable sources such as solar, wind, 
hydropower or biomass. This eliminates the burning of fossil fuels and the emission of carbon into the 
atmosphere. A C2C product would properly manage the energy emissions from its processes and 
connect it directly into the biological sphere. 
 
Celebrate Diversity 
1.1.3
The final principle focuses on the fact that there should not be a “one size fits all solution.” A C2C design 
should be creative and support local biodiversity, leaving a beneficial social, ecological and cultural 
footprint (C2CPII, 2013, p. 4). 
 
C2C Certification 
1.1.4
Cradle to Cradle developed a set of criteria for labeling a product as an official “Cradle to Cradle 
Certified™ Product.” These criteria are organized into 5 categories: material health, material 
reutilization, renewable energy and carbon management, water stewardship and social fairness. A 
product is assessed and graded for each category. The grade is organized into 5 levels: basic, bronze, 
silver, gold and platinum. A product will receive a certification based on the minimum level of 
achievement on the scorecard (C2CPII, 2013, p. 10). As an example, a product has been assessed with 
the scores shown in Table 1.

Material Health 
1.1.4.1
Material health is an assessment of the material selection in a product. It follows a series of rating 
systems to ensure that the selected materials are safe for use and are healthy enough to upcycle.  
The first step is to generate a bill of materials list.  It is a guide to identify “the chemicals present in 
those materials that will be assessed for their potential to impact human and environmental health” 
(C2CPII, 2013, p. 18). A C2C certification requires that all materials used in a product system are 
declared. 
The second step is to identify the appropriate metabolisms of each material. As stated earlier, materials 
are recovered and brought back into either the biological or technical cycles. The certification process 
identifies the matching cycle for each material in the product and highlights those which cannot be 
returned to either. 
Next is to determine if there is any use of banned chemicals. There is an extensive list for all industries 
which outlines the chemicals which should never be knowingly used in a product. By conducting this 
assessment, it places responsibility on the suppliers to understand the chemical composition of their 
products (C2CPII, 2013, p. 21). Banned chemicals include e.g. cadmium, lead, chromium and mercury. 
When dealing with recycled content, it is necessary to conduct analytical testing on the material if there 
are concerns about possible contamination. For metals, testing is not always necessary as the 
determination of the alloy provides the chemical composition (C2CPII, 2013, p. 25). 
C2C uses material ingredient data to assess each material in the product. This assessment is done using 
a chemical hazard rating system, risk assessment rating system and cyclability rating system. The 
chemical hazard rating system is traffic light color coded to identify how hazardous the material is for a 
certain endpoint (i.e. climate change). The ratings are outlined in Table 2.

After the bill of materials is completed, each homogeneous material has to be classified as percentages 
into one of four categories: 
 
Recyclable material 
 
Compostable material 
 
Recycled material 
 
Rapidly renewable material 
The individual percentages of recyclable material and compostable material are summed to give the % 
of the product considered recyclable or compostable. In order for a material to be considered recyclable, 
it must be able to “be recycled at least once after its initial use phase” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 50) or separable 
into individual components. 
“The individual percentages of recycled material and rapidly renewable material within each 
homogeneous material are multiplied by the percentage of those materials within the overall product 
and summed” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 50) in order to give the % of recycled or rapidly renewable content in the 
product. A recycled material is classified according to where in the value chain it is collected relative to 
the consumer. Post-consumer scrap is collected after the use phase. Pre-consumer scrap is collected 
prior to the use phase and is material from outside the applicant’s manufacturing facility. 
If eligible to be awarded the gold level, the company is required to develop a “nutrient management 
strategy for the product including scope, timeline and budget” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 51). The goal is to place 
more responsibilities on the company itself in creating the appropriate environment to maximize the 
recovery and recycling potential of its products. 
If eligible to be awarded the platinum level, the company will have to show that the product is 
successfully reentering the biological and technical cycles. This can be done through collection programs 
either by the company or its partners (i.e. municipality, distributors, etc.). 
 
Renewable Energy and Carbon Management 
1.1.4.3
The second principle of the C2C certified product standard is use of solar energy income which is a 
category in itself for the certification process. The end goal is for a product to positively impact the 
environment with energy usage being a prime example of an area to improve. This is because of the 
carbon emissions associated with energy consumption. When fossil fuels are burned for energy, carbon 
is released into the atmosphere which has a direct impact on climate change. The C2C assessment 
quantifies the amount of energy usage and emissions for electricity and on-site CO2 emissions. The 
product is graded based on the percentage of renewable energies and direct onsite emissions used 
throughout the manufacturing step. An overview of the requirements for each certification level is 
provided in Table 7.

The assessment process begins with a “facility level audit of energy use and emissions” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 
55). This is to establish a quantified baseline so the manufacturer knows where they currently stand. 
Calculations are conducted on only the final stage of manufacturing rather than all product relevant 
processes. This is to set a standard for all manufacturers on similar processes.  
1.1.4.3.1 Electricity 
The first step of the calculation is purchased electricity.  This includes “the percent on-site renewables 
and percent renewables purchased from the grid and/or compliant renewable energy certificates (RECs) 
sources” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 55). This requirement also stipulates that if any purchased heat that is sourced 
directly from a utility, it is to be included in the on-site emissions calculation rather than purchased 
electricity. When reporting this figure, some renewable energy that is already a standard with the local 
grid mix does not count towards the requirement. There are exceptions such as if “the applicant is 
participating in a voluntary green pricing program  or has verified that their utility is delivering 
renewable energy which may be claimed by the utility customer without being double counted 
elsewhere in the system” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 58). When exploring offset projects, the Clean Development 
Mechanism (CDM) recommends using ones which address, for example, increased electricity generation 
from existing hydropower stations through decision support system optimization (United Nations, 
2011).

1.1.4.3.2 On-Site Emissions 
Secondly, the carbon emissions from on-site operations of the final manufacturing process are 
calculated. This includes all “on-site fuel such as gasoline for company-owned vehicles and propane 
when attributable to the product” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 55). These are considered to be originating from 
non-electrical resources such as on-site natural gas. When exploring offset requirements, C2C 
recommends that “the given percentage of on-site emissions be compensated for by the purchase of 
offsets or via use of renewables such as biomass on-site that results in avoided emissions from non-
renewable sources (C2CPII, 2013, p. 61).  
Products are rated based on how far it has progressed in achieving 100% renewable energy. Each level 
of the rating system represents an increasing percentage of renewable energy. As a product progresses, 
more is required. Once the basic level has been achieved, a clear strategy for increasing renewable 
energy usage and improving carbon management must be presented. The silver level and above 
requires a calculation of the purchased renewable energy or offset renewable energy projects. Once the 
platinum level has been achieved, C2C requires a plan to optimize the strategy when the next 
application is submitted. It is also required that “at least 5% of the embodied energy associated with the 
product from Cradle to Gate is covered by offsets or otherwise addressed” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 64).  
 
Water Stewardship 
1.1.4.4
Stewardship is defined as “the activity or job of protecting and being responsible for something” 
(Merriam Webster, 2015). Naturally, this is easily applied to sustainability and the protection of natural 
resources. Up to this point, it is clear that C2C is heavily anchored around creating a management 
system which maximizes the potential for protecting, recovering and recycling resources. 
Water stewardship is a part of C2C certification as an assessment of a company’s management and use 
strategies of the effluent from production and manufacturing. The certification levels are a progression 
of the optimization of product-related process chemicals in the effluent. At the bronze level, a facility 
level audit would have been completed. At the platinum level, all water leaving the facility is of drinking 
water quality. 
 
Social Fairness 
1.1.4.5
The value chain for a product involves a large amount of stakeholders, both internally and externally. 
Decisions that the company makes regarding the development, production, sales and collection of a 
product will impact employees, customers, consumers, suppliers and the environment. In order to 
maintain a sustainable business operation, it is important for the company to remain ethical and fair to 
all those involved. The last category in the Cradle to Cradle certification is “a qualitative measure of the 
impact a product’s manufacture has on people and communities” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 9). 
1.2 Life Cycle Assessment 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) provides a comprehensive overview of the environmental performance of 
products and systems. It allows the user to identify opportunities for improvement at specific points of 
the life cycle. This is an ideal tool to use because it can compare various scenarios for a single product.  
 
 
==PAGE:15==
9 | P a g e  
 
According to ISO 14040-44 standards (ISO 14044, 2006), LCA is structured into a series of 4 steps: 
1. Goal and scope definition 
2. Life Cycle Inventory (LCI)  
3. Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) 
4. Life Cycle Interpretation 
The goal and scope will establish the breadth, depth and detail of the study to ensure a clear objective. 
The inventory analysis is the data gathering process and involves research and calculations to quantify 
all relevant inputs and outputs of the product system. When the product system has been modeled with 
the relevant data, the impact assessment can be conducted. It will evaluate the potential environmental 
impacts based on the selected impact categories (e.g. climate change) and quantifies the impacts in 
terms of category indicator results (e.g.  kg CO2 equivalents) . Finally, the results are interpreted by the 
user to use in the decision-making process of product and system development (ILCD Handbook, 2010). 
1.3 Carlsberg 
Since being founded in 1847, Carlsberg has evolved into the 4th largest brewer in the world. To put 
things in perspective, Carlsberg’s reach extends across 150 markets featuring over 500 beer brands. 
With such a large reach, it is easy to imagine how much beer is produced by Carlsberg. But to give a 
precise number, it is over 36 billion bottles sold in 2013 alone (Nielsen, 2014). 
Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) has been a part of the Carlsberg Group strategy since 2008 with 
growing influence over the years. CSR provides a vision for the company to “Grow Responsibly” in 
regards to “contributing to a healthy, sustainable environment and supporting the communities in which 
[they] operate” (CSR, 2014). They focus on 3 key areas: energy, water and sustainable packaging. 
Considering the 36 billion bottles sold last year, a very large quantity of packaging must have been 
produced in order to meet the demand. But what happens when the supply of virgin resources used to 
produce the packaging begins to decrease? 
Carlsberg CSR recognized 2 key trends regarding their vision and packaging. First, the pressure on 
resource productivity is rising. Second, key enablers are coming into place.  Resource scarcity is a 
growing reality and, from a business perspective, is causing price volatility on the materials needed to 
manufacture the packaging. The question that arises is how can the depleted resources be replenished 
or replaced. This is the context where the Carlsberg Circular Community plays its role. 
In 2014, Carlsberg launched a “collaborative platform where Carlsberg and suppliers work together to 
eliminate the concept of waste,” (CSR, 2014) based on the circular economy concept. In order to combat 
resource scarcity, the solution is to rethink the way products are designed utilizing the Cradle-to-Cradle 
(C2C) framework. C2C examines the materials that are used in a product and defines how they can be 
used after the product reaches the end of its life; “the right material in the right place at the right time” 
(McDonough & Braungart, 2002). This supports the reuse of materials and the C2C guideline that “waste 
equals food.”

1.4 Aluminium Life Cycle 
Aluminium is the most abundant metal in the Earth’s crust, making up roughly 8.1% by weight (EAA, 
2014 a). Quantity aside, aluminium has unique intrinsic properties which make it an ideal material for 
packaging because, among other things, aluminium is formidable, an excellent barrier, corrosion 
resistant and visually appealing (EAA, 2014 b).  
There are two different types of aluminium in life cycle assessment: primary and secondary. Primary is 
aluminium which is produced from mined materials. Secondary is aluminium which has been recycled 
and reprocessed. 
 
Primary Aluminium 
1.4.1
The life cycle begins with mining the aluminium from the crust. The raw material is called bauxite, which 
is “composed of aluminium hydroxide compounds, plus silica, iron and titanium oxides as the main 
impurities” (EAA, 2014 a). Alumina, the primary material for producing aluminium, is extracted from the 
bauxite using the Bayer chemical process and then converted into primary aluminium through 
electrolysis. The next stage is semi-fabrication which involves rolling, casting and extrusion to form the 
components of the product. The final products are then manufactured, used and recycled. The majority 
of these products are used in everyday life. Figure 2 shows the distribution of end-use products made 
with aluminium in Europe in 2013 (EAA, 2014 b). Figure 3 visualizes the life cycle process.

Figure 2: End-use products made with aluminium 
Mobility makes up the majority of use as aluminium is the desired metal for automobiles and planes due 
to its high mechanical strength. Construction uses aluminium for its high tensile strength in various 
building materials and decorum. It is also used in many consumer goods such as cell phones, home 
appliances and bicycles. For packaging, aluminium is an ideal material because of its durability, 
appealing look and it provides a perfect seal for preservation. Basically, aluminium is everywhere and 
there are many opportunities to continue its life after its use is over. In fact, it is estimated that 
approximately 75% of the aluminium manufactured worldwide is still in circulation since production 
began in the 1800s! (EAA, 2014 a). 
 
Secondary Aluminium 
1.4.2
The high rate of circulation comes down to the most important characteristic of aluminium: it maintains 
quality and can thus be recycled infinitely. Because it doesn’t corrode or rust, there is an environmental 
and economic incentive to collect scrap aluminium and either recreate the product or produce 
something new. For the environment, it satisfies the C2C dream of saving resources through recycling 
and reducing waste through collection. For the economy, the industry saves money by recycling. Every 
tonne of recycled aluminium saves 4 tonnes of mined bauxite, 9 tonnes of CO2 emissions, 95% less 
energy than producing primary aluminium and 97 % reduction of Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions 
(Every Can Counts, 2011).

1.5 Packaging Waste Management 
Recycling is not possible without a structured recovery system. There needs to be an infrastructure 
which can maximize the return rate and remain convenient to consumers. The EEA proposes six topics of 
recycling legislation which can be improved (EAA, 2014 c). These targets all fit the C2C vision for the way 
products and systems are designed 
 
Promote more ambitious recycling targets 
1.5.1
It begins with setting goals to perform higher than the status quo. All recyclable materials should be 
treated the same and have the potential to reach 100% recycling.  If more and more materials are 
recovered, landfills could eventually become obsolete. 
 
Improve sorting and collection processes 
1.5.2
Since waste contains many useful materials which can be recycled, an improved process is needed in 
order to recover them. This involves “promoting the uptake of existing separation technologies, but also 
developing further innovative separation technologies” (EAA, 2014 c). In many cases, this can’t be done 
without the products being redesigned with the C2C standards to ease the dismantling process in 
making the materials more recoverable. 
==PAGE:19==
13 | P a g e  
 
 
Better and transparent reporting 
1.5.3
Proper recycling is reliant upon the focus of the collection process. As will be seen in the UK case study, 
focusing on recycling of specific materials is a better method for streamlining the process than simply 
“collection for recycling” (EAA, 2014 c). 
 
Better definitions of recycling 
1.5.4
C2C calls for the upcycling of materials after initial use. In the common system, many materials are 
recycled but ultimately downcycled. EAA calls for a distinction to be made “between endless recycling in 
a closed material loop and recycling which ultimately results in the degradation of the collected 
materials” (EAA, 2014 c). 
 
Better monitoring of the export of metal scrap 
1.5.5
In the current system, EU countries have their own unique collection scheme which does not allow for 
scrap from one country to be recycled in another country, specifically outside the EU. The EAA calls for a 
better tracing system to recover used aluminium that leaves its country of origin. On a global scale, they 
want best practices in Europe to be implemented in developing countries. 
 
The application of the correct LCA methodologies 
1.5.6
As will be seen in this study, understanding the recycling system requires a better assessment of the 
environmental impacts from the materials and proper modeling of the end-of-life stage. The integration 
of C2C requirements allows for this study to highlight the potential impacts and view the reductions 
when less is sent to landfill. 
1.6 Deposit Schemes 
Deposit schemes are an incentivized program which puts a small additional fee on beverage products 
with the amount determined based on packaging material and container size (Gandy et al 2008, p. 4). 
The retailer pays the small fee to the beverage company, and then the consumer pays the fee to the 
retailer and is reimbursed when the empty container is returned. This system encourages consumers to 
recycle as the financial incentive is a motivator for human behavior. 
Many EU countries have a successful deposit scheme in place. As an example, Denmark achieved a 
metal beverage container return rate of 84% in 2007 (Gandy et al 2008, p. 17). There are several keys to 
successfully implementing a deposit scheme which can be narrowed down to three topics: partnerships 
with parties in the value chain, reasonable deposit values and national bar codes. 
 
Partnerships with Parties in the Value Chain 
1.6.1
A deposit scheme can only be effective if all members of the value chain are a part of the system with 
clearly defined roles as outlined in Table 8 (Gandy et al 2008, pp. 6-8).

Reasonable Deposit Value 
1.6.2
If a deposit scheme is going to be successful, the value of the returned beverage container has to be 
reasonable and realistic. It begins with providing enough incentive to participate. If the consumer sees 
value in taking the time to return their beverage containers, they will feel motivated to gather the 
refund. If the refund is high, the motivation will be high. In order to be reasonable, the deposit has to be 
lower than the cost of manufacturing so fraudulent behavior is avoided i.e. producing new containers 
simply to collect the deposit (Gandy et al 2008, p. 16). 
Many countries base the deposit value on the size and packaging type. In Denmark, beverage containers 
are typically refunded for 1 DKK if less than 1 L and 3 DKK if greater than 1 L. In Germany, the deposit is 
standardized as 25 Euro cents regardless of size.  
 
National Bar Codes 
1.6.3
One of the issues in Europe is ensuring that the deposit value remains within the loop it was originally 
charged to. This means that the containers are designed to be country specific. A can purchased in 
==PAGE:21==
15 | P a g e  
 
Denmark can only be refunded if it is returned in Denmark. This prevents fraud across borders. In some 
cases, a returned can will be accepted but no refund will be given. 
Not all European countries utilize a deposit scheme and thus have lower return rates. For Carlsberg, this 
means that several of their markets have room for improvement from a C2C perspective. One of these 
markets is the UK. 
 
The UK Collection Scheme 
1.6.4
The UK is an example of a country where the recovery process is inefficient. Collection of household 
waste is occurring on a daily basis however the packaging materials are not properly collected based on 
type, they are merely bundled together with any material considered to be recyclable (Gandy et al 2008, 
p. 34). The UK lacks a unified system for resource recovery from county to county and the majority of 
households do not have access to curbside collection (Gandy et al 2008, p. 43).  Legislators created a 
system of regulations to make recycling and recovery a requirement for companies. Every year, a 
company has to provide evidence that they fulfilled their recycling and recovery obligation in the form of 
Packaging Recovery Notes (PRN) which is awarded by an external auditor based on the tonnes of 
packaging material reprocessed. PRNs are treated as market shares and can rise and fall in value. The 
generated revenue is “meant to be reinvested into the reprocessing industry, to fund the commissioning 
of further equipment, so that increasing targets can be met” (Gandy et al 2008, p. 34). However, this 
system proves to be a disadvantage because it creates confusion due to the “disjuncture between the 
packaging waste that is generated and the targets for recovery which are set for each organization” 
(Gandy et al 2008, p. 43).  
Despite the lack of a structured deposit scheme, there are still several voluntary schemes to aid the 
collection process. One example of a voluntary scheme is “Recycling Zones”. Recycling bins are placed in 
high-traffic locations such as shopping centers and stadiums where consumers can toss their waste into 
pre-sorted categories (aluminium, paper, plastic, etc).  
Carlsberg UK is an active part of the community as they wish to engage consumers to voluntarily return 
their used beverage containers (UBCs). One initiative they are involved in is called Every Can Counts® 
whose mission is to “help people recycle the drinks they use when they are at work, college or just ‘out 
and about’” (Every Can Counts, 2011). They provide over 4000 recycling points across the UK which 
eases the recycling process as the distance between recycling zones is decreased, which helps raise the 
return rates.  
However, there is still room for improvement as the UK return rates remain far behind Carlsberg’s other 
markets in Western Europe. In terms of C2C, this means that there is still potential to grow and provide 
higher usage of secondary aluminium.

2.1  Goal of the Study 
 
Purpose 
2.1.1
This study seeks to examine Carlsberg’s aluminium beverage can using a combination of LCA 
methodology and C2C product certification requirements with the following objectives: 
1) Set a benchmark by assessing the environmental impact of a baseline scenario; 
2) Model the material reutilization and renewable energy and carbon management requirements 
in LCA; 
3) Conduct a scenario analysis where the environmental impact associated with the different levels 
of C2C certification are assessed and compared. 
Primary packaging is considered to be the materials which come into direct contact with the product. In 
this case, the primary packaging includes the can body, the lid and the label. Together, the objectives 
will provide a “before” and “after certification” assessment of the primary packaging solution. 
Objective 2 only covers a portion of the entire C2C certification scope. The reason is because material 
reutilization and renewable energy and carbon management are quantifiable criteria. The other points 
are qualitative in the sense that they relate to the level of intention for strategic development. 
Objective 3 only covers the certification steps from bronze to platinum. This is because the basic level is 
considered to be an entry level into the C2C assessment and only provides an initial screening of a 
product system. 
 
Intended Application of the Study 
2.1.2
This study will be applied in the following ways: 
1) To investigate the potential benefits of increasing the amount of recycled material versus the 
renewable energy usage used to produce the aluminium can;  
2) To provide Carlsberg with the environmental impact difference between the C2C certification 
levels allowing them to decide which aspects of C2C should be prioritized in the certification 
process.

2.2 Scope of the Study 
 
The Functional Unit 
2.2.1
The function of the aluminium beverage can is to contain the required volume of beer, which is typically 
either 25, 33, 44 or 50 cl. This case study will consider the 33 cl can. Since cans and beer are both 
produced in large quantities, the functional unit has to properly reflect the impact and thus the 
measurement will be calculated per 1 hl. 
The model will be designed with the produced can as the reference point for all inputs and outputs. 
Therefore, the functional unit will be represented as the containment of 1 hl of beer in 33 cl aluminium 
cans. 
By calculating per hl as opposed to per can, the results can be comparable to other packaging types. The 
EPD of Carlsberg’s beer declared their functional unit to be per 100 L (EPD, 2014). Detzel & Mönckert 
(2008) declared 1000 L as the functional unit. In general, it is common practice to model beverage cans 
in mass quantity. 
 
System Boundaries 
2.2.2
Carlsberg conducts their own environmental assessment of their specific products using a custom made 
LCA tool which accounts for all processes. They need to track the performance of each aspect of the 
product: the packaging (primary, secondary, tertiary and quaternary), the transportation, the disposal 
system as well as the beverage itself.  
C2C’s certification scheme defines their scope with some limitations which will impact how this study 
will be conducted. A C2C certification is not applicable to food and beverages due to the fact that they 
are not the aspect of the product which can be recycled. It is only the packaging which can be developed 
to be continuously recyclable. Therefore, this study will not be considering the beer production or 
anything beyond the primary packaging solution. The model will only take into account the used 
beverage container and the lacquer used for the label. Furthermore, although transportation can 
contribute to on-site emissions and renewable energy usage, it will not be considered in the report 
because there was not enough information available to justify consideration (e.g. company vehicle 
usage). The introduction provided details about collection systems and deposit schemes utilized in the 
UK. This system will not be specifically modeled but collection is considered in the end-of-life process. 
The EcoInvent database includes collection in the aggregated process of secondary aluminium 
production. It will also be assumed that in a closed-loop system, the percent recycled content is equal to 
the percent recycling rate with the remainder going to landfill.  The modeled life cycle processes are 
shown in Figure 4. 
The can body and lid manufacturing process as well as the final can production results in scrap being 
output from the system. Although this scrap could be recycled and included in the secondary aluminium 
production, the model will consider that all primary aluminium produced will be utilized in the 
manufacturing.

Allocation 
2.2.3
Allocation was carried out based on the ISO-14044 standards. Since this is a reuse/recycling model, 
special considerations needed to be made.  There are two methods which can be utilized to design the 
recycling system: closed-loop or recycled content.   
A closed-loop system feeds the material back into the same system without consideration for utilization 
in other products (can to can). It emphasizes the replacement of materials as avoided production 
meaning that “metal losses have to be replaced by virgin material” (da Silva et al 2010, p. 25). In this 
approach, the amount of avoided primary production cannot exceed the amount of secondary 
aluminium scrap collected. 
A recycled content approach allows for surplus secondary aluminium to leave the system burden-free to 
be utilized in other product systems. This requires the designer’s need for a waste management 
perspective because of the potential impacts from increasing the need for secondary aluminium. If one 
industry requires more of the recycled material, it could potentially be depriving another industry where 
it would be more economical (da Silva et al 2010, p. 25).  
The EAA recommends using the closed-loop approach as the end-of-life model because it focuses on the 
burdens within the singular system (EAA, 2014 c). 
PE International also recommends the closed-loop approach because “when the material is recycled 
without changes to inherent properties, it is treated as if it replaces virgin materials, thus allowing for 
preferential treatment” which is the principle of closed-loop methodology (Stichling & Nguyen-Ngoc, 
2009, p. 12). 
 
Time Coverage 
2.2.4
The study is conducted based on the data most recently available. All Carlsberg data including can 
weights and energy consumptions are measured from 2014. The raw material data are representative of 
the average European situation, as is taken from PE International based on a 2009 study (Stichling & 
Nguyen-Ngoc, 2009). The deviation of five years from the referenced year is acceptable.

Geographical Coverage 
2.2.5
The study is a general assessment on the Carlsberg UK market.  All datasets considered are either 
European standards or specific to the UK. For example, electricity is generated from hydropower for the 
entire UK.  
 
Technological Coverage 
2.2.6
All physical processes will be considered to be the industry standards for producing aluminium beverage

Life Cycle Impact Assessment Methodology 
2.2.7
The analysis of the GaBi results requires a selection of LCIA methodology to translate the inventory data 
into potential environmental impacts. This report will utilize ReCiPe 2008, one of the common 
methodologies used worldwide. ReCiPe examines impacts at two levels: midpoint and endpoint. ISO 
14044 standards require “that the characterization factors be used on environmental mechanisms that 
link (man-made) interventions to a set of areas of protection (Goedkoop et al, 2008). Endpoint focuses 
on human health and damage to ecosystems at the end of the mechanism. Midpoint analyzes the 
emissions of hazardous substances and extraction of natural resources and is positioned as a midway 
point. ReCiPe utilizes 21 total impact categories (18 at midpoint and 3 at endpoint) which are outlined in 
Figure 5 taken from (Goedkoop et al, 2008).  
 
Figure 5: ReCiPe 2008 impact categories (midpoint and endpoint) 
One of the advantages of ReCiPe is that the impact categories can be aggregated into a singular score 
using normalization and weighting in order to make all the results comparable. The weighting system 
provides three perspectives to account for the uncertainty associated to an LCA study: individualist, 
==PAGE:26==
20 | P a g e  
 
hierarchist and egalitarian. Choosing a perspective is dependent upon the interested time-frame. 
Individualist considers the short term (e.g. 50 years), egalitarian considers the long term (e.g. 500 years) 
and hierarchist considers a middle ground (e.g. 100 years). A hierarchist perspective is ideal for this 
study as it is considered by ReCiPe to be associated with common policy principles. This singular score is 
ideal for the scenario analysis in the results section as it will provide a simple ranking system to 
determine which of the C2C requirements should be prioritized. 
 
Limitations and Assumptions 
2.2.8
As a project indirectly related to Carlsberg and its suppliers, not all information was readily available. 
Therefore, the study was conducted with some limitations.  
 
Data 
2.2.8.1
The majority of the data utilized is a combination of information from PE International and Carlsberg. 
However, several of the chosen datasets for Carlsberg UK’s site and the recycling process were unable to 
be verified. After speaking with Carlsberg CSR, they were not able to provide specifics as to, for example, 
the specific type of boiler used for heat production from natural gas. In cases like this, the best available 
dataset was selected based on previous studies and the descriptions from EcoInvent. 
 
Materials 
2.2.8.2
In reality, a Cradle to Cradle assessment is a comprehensive review of all aspects of a product. In 
conducting this assessment, the bill of materials is the first piece of information collected from suppliers 
and lists the specific materials used throughout the entire process. In many instances, this information is 
kept confidential by the company or EPEA. Therefore, a complete bill of materials was not available for 
this study. The chosen materials are based on best available information and do not provide an exact 
representation of the Carlsberg aluminium can life cycle. 
 
Cradle to Cradle Certification 
2.2.8.3
The process of obtaining a C2C certification is rather long and requires a significant investment. As 
outlined in the introduction, there is a large amount of details that go into assessing a product and 
revolves around building a long-term strategy. In that sense, C2C is as much a business decision as it is 
an environmental assessment. This case study does not have access to the resources needed to give an 
exact assessment of the 5 certification categories. Therefore, all C2C criteria which are utilized in the 
LCA are based on a best interpretation of the process.  
 
Material Reutilization Score 
2.2.8.4
The material reutilization score formula is reliant upon having the full bill of materials. This includes not 
only the aluminium and lacquer but also all chemical additives that are used in the processing. The lack 
of access to these specifics places a limit to the study of this formula as only the recycled content of the 
aluminium and lacquer can be assessed. In reality, the score would account for all of the materials 
included in the bill of materials.  
 
Renewable Energy Usage 
2.2.8.5
Renewable energy usage examines the amount used throughout the entire manufacturing process. The 
best interpretation of this is a one for one replacement of mega joules between renewable and non-

renewable energy. The challenge comes from the reality of trying to eliminate non-renewable energy 
usage. Current infrastructure does not allow for all forms of energy to be obtained from renewable

3 Data Collection and Modeling 
The model was developed using the GaBi V.6 LCA software. The primary feature will be a single model 
for all scenarios. Using the parameter function, the values for recycling content and renewable energy 
percentage can be adjusted. This will provide flexibility for making quick changes to the model and a 
simplified process for acquiring the results and making direct comparisons.  
3.1 
Modeling the Aluminium Life Cycle 
The life cycle was modeled according to the traditional stages outlined by the European Aluminium 
Association and explained in the introduction, Figure 3. Several of the key processes were aggregated 
into a singular process to keep the focus on the production of the body, lid and the completed can.  
3.1.1 Can Body and Lid Production 
The early stages of the life cycle (mining and alumina production) are aggregated into the primary 
aluminium processes within the model. This makes it unnecessary to model so the impact is not counted 
twice.  
The body and lid of the beverage can are modeled with five aggregated inputs and shown in Figure 6: 
1. Primary Aluminium 
2. Aluminium Sheet Rolling 
3. Electricity from Hydropower 
4. Heat from Natural Gas 
5. Heat from Wood  
Primary aluminium contains all the processes from raw material to production required to produce 1 kg 
in an ingot form. In order to form the ingot into the beverage can components, the total mass required 
needs to be rolled using a hot reversing mill which reduces the thickness to the desired measurement 
(Stichling & Nguyen-Ngoc, 2009, p. 21). In the LCA model, the mass flow for primary aluminium and 
aluminium sheet rolling are equal. This allows the system to compute the correct amount of material 
which has to be rolled.  
The energy processes are modeled based on data collected from PE International. According to their 
assessment of body and lid production, renewable energy is primarily sourced by hydropower and non-
renewable is sourced by a combination of natural gas, crude oil and nuclear. In order to simplify the 
scenario analysis, the model accounts for hydropower as the only electricity input. This assumption is 
made based on the fact that the can components are produced in the UK where all electricity is from 
100% hydropower (EcoInvent Centre, 2007, p. 98). 
For heat energy, the input depends on the component of the beverage can. According to PE 
International, the body is produced using mostly natural gas (Stichling & Nguyen-Ngoc, 2009, p. 35). 
Without a percentage breakdown of the other non-renewable materials, it will be assumed to be 100% 
of the input.  The report also states that the lid is produced using mostly raw oil. Therefore, it will be 
assumed that light fuel oil accounts for 100% of the non-renewable energy usage for the lid. 
==PAGE:32==
26 | P a g e  
 
 
 
Figure 6: GaBi screenshot of the body and lid production process 
In order to conduct a scenario analysis for heat usage, a renewable option was created to proportionally 
change with the non-renewable option based on the percent of renewable energy. One of the preferred 
alternatives to fossil fuels is heat generated from biomass. According to EcoInvent, wood energy 
provides a “reduction of greenhouse gas emissions, since carbon contained in the burned wood and 
hence emitted as CO2 was taken from the atmosphere during the growth of the trees” (EcoInvent 
Centre, 2007, p. 107). 
3.1.2 Beverage Can Production 
The process following the body and lid production is to combine them into one beverage can. In the 
GaBi model, this is set up as an open process which flows the two separate mass flows into one. Since

this is considered to be the final product, the process is fixed as the point of reference for scaling within 
the model, see Figure 7.  
This is also the step that introduces the lacquer into the model. According to data received from 
Carlsberg, the body and lid use an internal coating for the barrier between the metal and the beverage 
as well as an external coating for the label. Earlier it was explained that the lacquer accounts for 
approximately 3.2% of the total weight of the can. The mass flows for the three paints are estimated 
amounts as the exact numbers were not made available. Therefore it will be assumed the acrylic and 
polyester are equal flows and the epoxy is a lesser amount. Epoxy accounts for approximately 20% of 
the lacquer mass (Fujisawa et al, 2000, p. 2). The values are listed in the appendix

3.1.3 Carlsberg UK 
After the can has been manufactured, it is sent to Carlsberg’s brewery in Northampton for filling and 
distribution, Figure 8. From a modeling perspective, the only inputs in this stage are the energy 
consumptions. As was the case for modeling the energy usage in can body production, the same rule 
applies to energy usage at Carlsberg UK. The primary input for renewable electricity is hydropower and 
the primary input for non-renewable heat is natural gas. The secondary option for heat is wood energy 
which will vary proportionally to natural gas depending on the scenario. The output for the process is

3.1.4 Aluminium End-of-Life 
At the end-of-life stage, the UBC needs to be flowed separately depending on the end of life destination, 
Figure 9. The metal itself is 100% recyclable and has the purpose of replacing the primary aluminium 
used in the initial production. This is done using an avoided production process. The open process is 
called Avoided Primary Aluminium and utilizes a one-for-one replacement between the primary and 
secondary mass flows. Whenever a unit of secondary aluminium is produced, a credit of primary 
aluminium is negated from the system. 
The amount of secondary aluminium entering this process is split depending on the desired recycled 
content of the beverage can. If it is to be counted as recycling credit, it is directed to the avoided 
process. The remaining amount of secondary aluminium is directed to landfill. The landfill model 
accounts for the impact from landfilling aluminium in a one-to-one ratio, just as the process for 
modeling the aluminium sheet rolling compared to the amount of primary aluminium.  
The lacquer used is also accounted for in an incineration process. During recycling, the can is pretreated 
to remove as much paint as possible to minimize the amount of impurities added to the recast 
aluminium. This is done through a heating process at approximately 600C (Li & Qiu, 2013, p. 1). The 
model keeps the three resins separate and removes them from the total weight of the beverage can.

considered for the lacquer.

increased can size. One of the standards in the UK is the 50 cl can. It requires a larger amount of 
aluminium for the body but the lid remains the same size. Therefore, only minor adjustments to the 
GaBi model needed to be made in order to satisfy the increased material requirements.

PE International. The adjusted inputs and outputs for the 50 cl can are displayed in the appendix. 
Since the size of the can is increased, the amount of lacquer used for the label is increased as well. This

5 Discussion 
This section will discuss the results obtained in relation to the three objectives stated in the goal of the 
study, as well as the results of the sensitivity analysis. Furthermore, the process of modeling the C2C 
requirements into the LCA and the perceived limitations to combining the two will be discussed as well 
as how the results can be applied to Carlsberg and how they should approach future development.  
5.1 
Review of Objectives 
5.1.1 Baseline Assessment 
The initial assessment of the aluminium beverage can considered the recycled content to be 0% and 
renewable energy usage to be 0%. In reality, this would not be the situation. This merely provided the 
worst-case and served as the benchmark for the selected scenarios.  
The findings from increasing recycled content and renewable energy with the benchmark confirmed the 
following: 
1. Recycled content reduces impacts at a higher rate than renewable energy. 
2. Shifting from natural gas to wood energy causes burden shifting from climate change to human 
toxicity. 
When the recycled content is stable and the renewable energy increases the human toxicity impact 
actually increases. This could be attributed to burden shifting after the introduction of burning wood 
into the life cycle which releases more emissions of 1,4 dichlorobenzene. In fact, the emissions to air of 
biotic carbon dioxide increases significantly as the wood energy heating process is integrated, see Figure

By comparing the increasing recycled content with the increasing renewable energy it appears recycled 
content is a stronger indicator of environmental performance. However, this is not representative of the 
actual situation for two reasons: 
1. There is no Carlsberg can in the UK market with a recycled content of 0%. 
2. When the recycled content is increased, the total life cycle energy required decreases as well. 
The second point is reflected by the EAA’s estimation that recycled aluminium requires 95% less energy 
to produce than primary aluminium (EAA, 2014 a). 
So in order to properly understand the impacts from the aluminium can from the C2C and LCA 
assessments, all possible certification combinations need to be examined. 
5.1.2 C2C Categories and How They Can Be Reflected in LCA 
It is important to assess the C2C categories and explain the differences between C2C and LCA. In a 
previous study, it was determined that C2C and LCA are inherently complementary as C2C focuses on an 
ideal future and LCA focuses on the short-term and the current systems. However, parts of it can be 
assessed together if a clear trade-off exists. In this case, “LCA should be applied as a reality check” in 
order to confirm the C2C design and is directly applicable to energy and material consumption (Bjørn & 
Hauschild, 2012).   
C2C should not be regarded as solely a “far-sighted methodology”. The C2C requirements were 
developed as milestones towards an ideal sustainable world. They are not meant to be viewed as a 
platinum-or-bust mentality but rather as a guide to aid companies in building towards a future oriented 
towards product quality and innovation. It is true that the infrastructure for certain requirements may 
not exist at the time the LCA and C2C assessments are conducted but the assessments are not meant to 
be completed once. C2C supports continuous improvement and should be revisited constantly.  
The five categories are considered to be equal in the eyes of C2C with each carrying equal weight to 
provide the final certification grade. But when you assess them on an individual basis, they can be 
organized into different levels. When packaging is assessed, it has different levels based on how much 
contact it has with the product. Primary is directly in contact (e.g. the aluminium and lacquer) and 
secondary contains the primary packaging (e.g. plastic wrap). The C2C categories can be seen through 
the same way of thinking. Therefore, the categories can be organized into two levels of C2C certification: 
1) Product/Material 
2) Process/Company 
Material health and material reutilization is essentially the product itself. Renewable energy and carbon 
management, water stewardship and social fairness are all processes which support the creation of the 
product. Based on the results of the LCA, this difference in categories could support the assertion that 
recycled content is a higher priority than renewable energy because it is directly related to the product 
itself. It could even be said from the C2C vision that the product/material is “doing the right things” and 
the process/company is “doing it the right way.”

5.1.3 
Included Categories 
The material reutilization formula was developed to grade a product based on the materials chosen in 
the design phase. With a completed bill of materials, it has a quantifiable impact value which makes it 
possible to model the recycled content percentage and the material selection. The first variable 
examines the recyclability of a material. Based on the results of the material health, the assessment 
determines a numerical value for each material of how recyclable it is. This is viewed as an external 
assessment similar to material health and is not quantifiable in the LCA itself. But if it is done with the 
C2C assessment as a supplement, the materials selected in the model will reflect the recyclability values 
of the C2C grade. However, the second variable, recycled content, can be modeled directly into the LCA 
as explained earlier. Therefore, it made sense to base the study around the recycled content of the 
product rather than the entire C2C requirement because the LCA of the recycled content supports the 
decisions made in the strategic aspect of C2C.  
That being said, traditional LCA has inherent limitations regarding material reutilization. A traditional 
LCA only considers the product life as a snapshot. It is measured as one life cycle. This conflicts with C2C 
because C2C considers the “defined use” of the material and is meant to be continuous life cycles, not a 
single snapshot. Furthermore, there have been open discussions regarding how the end-of-life of 
recycled materials should be modeled. This study was done using the substitution method, a 
recommended practice from the EAA and utilized in other studies including Detzel & Mönckert (2008) 
and Leroy (2009). In a platinum rated C2C system, this 1 for 1 replacement of primary for secondary 
aluminium makes sense. However, the LCA does not account for the quality of the aluminium over the 
course of a continuous life cycle. A recent study by Bala Gala et al (2015) developed a new method for 
calculating the environmental credits during the end-of-life recovery process. They argue that “the 
simple substitution factor can lead to a misrepresentation of reality and, in particular, to an 
overestimation of the environmental credits associated with recycling practices” (Bala Gala et al 2015). 
So the reality is that the quality of the aluminium could potentially be degrading over time and the 
traditional LCA would not be accounting for it. Bala Gala et al (2015) introduced a quality parameter into 
the typical calculation of recycling credits which is multiplied by the percentage of virgin material in the 
average market mix. This factor needs to be further developed for each type of material as the quality 
value was virtually 1 for aluminium. This could very well prove different based on the Løvik & Müller 
(2014) study on aluminium impurities, discussed in section 5.2. 
Renewable energy and carbon management is virtually a direct trade-off of renewable and non-
renewable energy utilized in the manufacturing process. However, the applicability of the results is 
determinant on the infrastructure available in the chosen location. If Carlsberg UK were to begin utilizing 
wood chips to replace natural gas for heating, it would be assumed that it would not be a seamless 
transition from one to the other. It would be a gradual replacement over time as the infrastructure is 
developed. So in this sense, C2C and LCA can work well together in forecasting the future impacts of the 
product. C2C provides the strategic milestones and LCA provides the potential impacts when the 
milestones are reached. Furthermore, Figure 23 represents the total amount of energy required for each 
scenario and separates each total based on whether it is renewably or non-renewably sourced. The 
main issue seen is that even at 100% renewable energy, there is still non-renewable energy being

utilized. This is because the existing infrastructure still requires some non-renewable sources in order to 
function properly and C2C requirements only consider the manufacturing stage rather than the entire 
life cycle. In the 100% scenario with only wood energy being utilized for heat, the chosen dataset

5.1.4 
Excluded Categories 
Material health is an assessment of the material selection and determines which should be removed 
from the system. From a C2C perspective, the grade is given based on how many of the X rated 
materials were removed. From an LCA perspective, the model formulation is determined based upon 
the bill of materials. If the two were to be combined, a C2C assessment of the materials would need to 
be conducted first before generating the LCA to determine how the model will be created. In this case, 
the LCA could provide separate assessments of the before and after to determine if the changes to the 
material selection really improved the environmental impacts of the product. So while the material 
changes could be reflected in an LCA, the scope of this study does not include changes to the product 
composition. Therefore, material health was excluded. In a future study, it is recommended to base the 
LCA on the bill of materials and conduct a comparative assessment with and without the X rated 
materials. 
Water stewardship challenges manufacturers to take responsibility for their water consumptions, 
treatment and discharge quality. From a C2C perspective, a grade is given based on the implementation 
status of optimizing effluent and waste flows from the production site. This involves an assessment of 
the process chemicals utilized in manufacturing. An LCA can utilize this information once all chemicals 
are known. Similar to material health, a combined assessment would utilize the C2C assessment first 
followed by the LCA assessment. A future study could take this into consideration. 
Social fairness is slightly more challenging to model as C2C defines it as a purely qualitative measure. It is 
the exception that proves that C2C and LCA are differing in scope. C2C focuses on product quality and 
innovation while LCA focuses on environmental sustainability. However, it would be recommended to 
revisit the applicability of social fairness in LCA as S-LCA is further developed.

This may be due to the fact that the aluminium can has such a high level of recyclability. Many cans that 
are currently on the market contain a recycled content of at least 70% (Novelis, 2015). But it shows that 
there could be an inherent flaw in the way C2C awards its certifications.  
If Carlsberg were to prioritize renewable energy over recycled content, they could conceivably achieve 
gold despite the fact that they are impacting the environment significantly more than if they prioritized 
recycled content over renewable energy. This could potentially lead to a system of bad faith as 
companies see that receiving a gold certification could be the result of a decision based solely on 
financial investment. If they could achieve gold by only increasing renewable energy and it also costs 
less than increasing recycled content, they would choose the cheaper route despite the difference in 
environmental impact. 
Despite this possible drawback, the aluminium can is a unique case in that it is already a high performing 
product based on the impact assessment. Furthermore, C2C’s vision of a sustainable future sets its 
sights on not only the long term but the short and medium term as well. So if a product were to receive 
this lower gold certification, the company would have two years to improve by “making successive 
design-based decisions that will move [them] towards a more sustaining future” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 3). This 
is why the C2C design framework is meant for continuous improvement. Even if the first assessment 
leaves room for improvement, it helps set into motion the necessary objectives to reach the next level 
for all five categories. 
5.2 
Applying the Results in Reality 
C2C provides a vision for the future to design products so that all resources are returned to either the 
technical or biological cycle. It’s a perfect world scenario which is extremely difficult to develop at this 
point in time. This makes it challenging to apply the results of this study without considering how the 
value of the aluminium beverage cans will change over time. 
C2C envisions the can maintaining its value as it is continuously upcycled. Every time it is used, the 
secondary aluminium is recycled and replaces the need for a certain percentage of primary aluminium 
with its recycled content slowly increasing. However as the infrastructure will currently allow, it is highly 
doubtful that the aluminium would in fact maintain its value. The reason for this is the composition of 
the product itself. 
The body and lid of the can are composed of different types of aluminum alloy with variations in added 
metals, primarily magnesium or manganese. The lid contains higher levels of magnesium in order to 
make it stiffer than the body (Stichling & Nguyen-Ngoc, 2009, p. 24). Furthermore, the UBC becomes 
contaminated during the end of life phase with “impurities such as other metals or glass from 
commingled collection systems, dirt or titanium dioxide particles from the lacquer” (Løvik & Müller, 
2014). This means that unless the UBCs are thoroughly treated to completely separate these materials, 
they will accumulate over time, contaminating the pure aluminium. In the current system, “the 
concentration of impurities in remelted material is adjusted by diluting with primary aluminium or 
higher quality scrap” (Løvik & Müller, 2014). If primary aluminium is necessary in order to compensate 
for the accumulation of impurities, then the reality is that the C2C platinum grade is not possible for the 
==PAGE:56==
50 | P a g e  
 
can as currently manufactured and recycled. Although, this is more quantifiable in a closed-loop system 
as this study was conducted. In an open-loop system, UBC scrap could be used for other purposes, not 
solely a can for can replacement. There would still be impurities but on a smaller scale than if it was 
closed-loop. 
So what does this mean for Carlsberg? Carlsberg has already taken the first steps in building towards a 
C2C future by examining their products specifically and driving consumer awareness. However, an 
optimized product is only as valuable as the infrastructure will allow. It was established that return rates 
in the UK are lower than the EU average and the collection system in the UK bundles all recyclable 
material together which, based on the study by Løvik and Muller (2014), means that the UBCs are 
already prone to contaminants from other packaging material. While the system is not currently ideal 
for maximizing recycled content, there is potential for significant improvement. For example, with 
regard to the delacquering process, a vacuum pyrolysis treatment could be introduced which in one 
study resulted in a decoating rate of almost 100% (Li & Qiu, 2013). If all of the resin used for decoration 
were removed prior to remelting, contaminants such as titanium dioxide would no longer be a concern 
in the long-term quality of the aluminium. 
Starting with the Every Can Counts initiative, aluminium cans are already being voluntarily separated 
from other types of UBCs. But in order to build the infrastructure necessary to not only increase recycled 
content but also improve UBC treatment during recycling, Carlsberg UK and the Circular Community can 
take the lead in working with the members of its value chain to rethink not only the design of the can 
but also the way the can is processed during the end of life.  
5.2.1 Nutrient Management Strategy 
The Carlsberg Circular Community already provides the vision for developing sustainable packaging with 
its suppliers. One of the next steps should be to develop a nutrient management strategy and this is 
where the C2C milestones really help in achieving the “platinum world” that for now is only a wish. C2C 
intends to “challenge manufacturers to take more responsibility for creating the infrastructure and 
systems necessary for recovering and recycling materials” (C2CPII, 2013, p. 51). This goes beyond 
increasing the return rates through deposit schemes or improved separation during collection. 
Partnerships with waste management specialists will need to be developed in order to explore the 
treatment of the collected UBCs and evaluate how the can could be redesigned to eliminate the 
possibility of impurities accumulating in the aluminium during its continuous life cycle.  
Carlsberg can also utilize the EAA’s recommendations for recycling legislation which were outlined in 
section 1.5. A nutrient management strategy would include most of the six topics in that it would begin 
to design the infrastructure to allow for more ambitious recycling targets through improved sorting and 
collection processes. This would benefit Carlsberg UK by creating a collection system which does not 
bundle all recyclable materials. 
Carlsberg UK already has several initiatives in place to develop these topics. They signed onto the UK 
government’s Waste, Resources and Action Programme (WRAP) which seeks to eliminate waste through 
packaging reductions and use alternative disposal options to landfill (Carlsberg UK, 2015). Furthermore,

Carlsberg is developing a new version of their custom LCA tool which will provide customized 
assessments of their specific products, including recycled content and renewable energy usage. It will 
provide scenario-based analysis similar to those conducted in this study (CSR, 2014).

6 Conclusions/Recommendations 
The objective of the study was to conduct a scenario analysis based on LCA of the possible C2C 
certification combinations of material reutilization and renewable energy and carbon management. 
Using this information, Carlsberg can understand the environmental impacts associated with their 
aluminium beverage cans and begin to make a strategy for prioritizing which category can be improved 
the most while providing the biggest difference in impact. 
This study resulted in the following conclusions: 
 
Increasing recycled content provides more improvements to environmental impacts than 
increasing renewable energy usage 
 
Receiving a gold certification is not necessarily better environmentally than a bronze or silver 
 
The quantifiable C2C requirements can be modeled in LCA and provide checkpoints for each 
certification milestone 
 
While C2C examines the continuous recycling loop, LCA can only provide snapshots of the loop 
and not the future impacts associated with accumulation. 
 
100% recycled content is not guaranteed to provide continuous upcycling due to the possibility 
of impurities accumulating over time 
 
A nutrient management strategy needs to address the treatment of aluminium during the 
recycling process 
 
Wood energy is a good alternative to natural gas but should be explored further due to the 
increased levels of human toxicity 
While this study was being conducted, Carlsberg UK’s aluminium beverage cans were being assessed by 
the Cradle to Cradle Products Innovation Institute. They were awarded a bronze ranking for the size 44, 
50 and 56.8 cl cans. The certification will expire on 17th January 2017 at which time Carlsberg can apply 
to recertify. Details of the certification have not been disclosed to the public (CSR, 2014). 
Furthermore, the results lead to the following recommendations for Carlsberg: 
 
Prioritize increasing recycled content over the next two years 
 
Explore replacements of the decorative lacquer used to make the label 
 
Work with waste management specialists for the purpose of developing improved treatment 
techniques for packaging materials 
 
Develop a long term strategy for increasing renewable energy usage with local energy providers 
 
Develop a nutrient management strategy before the current certification expires in 2017 
 
Increase membership in the circular community to include collection and recycling centers, local 
municipalities and consumers to explore ways to improve the return rate in the UK
