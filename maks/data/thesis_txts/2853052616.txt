Abstract 
This thesis contributes to the field of Design for Sustainability Transitions by examining how transi-
tion theories can guide transition projects through practical design support, and the role designers 
play in this process.  
A systematic literature review identified 71 tools for strategic, tactical, operational, and reflexive 
transition governance, discovering an underrepresentation of tools for monitoring and evaluating 
existing efforts. An empirical study of a transition project in the Danish public waste management 
system identified a key opportunity to strengthen monitoring and evaluation practices, enabling lo-
cal actors to assess their collective capacity to contribute to broader transition objectives based on 
experiences from their projects. 
By developing and testing an Urban Transformative Capacity Assessment Procedure within an 8-year, 
circularity-focused transition project, this study introduced and evaluated a novel reflexive tool to 
help local actors strengthen self-awareness of their ability to contribute to transition projects. The 
tool allows practitioners to reflect on how project experiences relate to systemic, transformative 
capacity factors in their local urban environments. This serves as a first step to guide further action 
to strengthen their capacity to facilitate change.  
Initial findings indicate that designers play a role not only in facilitating strategic, tactical, and oper-
ational governance processes but also in enabling reflexive practice to build collective self-awareness 
of existing capacities. Designing structured learning processes that simultaneously monitor and eval-
uate transition efforts through “doing-by-learning” highlights an expanded role for designers in sus-
tainability transitions. This role goes beyond the “learning-by-doing” functions commonly discussed 
in the literature, which focus on the early stages of transition projects.

Glossary 
The glossary refers to terms commonly used in the thesis and their meanings in this context. 
Sustainability transition: A non-linear, multi-dimensional, co-evolutionary process in socio-technical 
systems that engage a diverse range of actors in pursuing long-term normative sustainability goals. 
Socio-technical system: The networks of various actors, artefacts and institutions within e.g. 
techno-scientific communities, cultures, markets, industries, and political institutions, which collec-
tively support societal functions such as electricity, transportation, and food systems. 
Regime: The set of semi-coherent rules that shape and sustain a particular socio-technical system. 
These rules are reflected in cognitive beliefs, social norms, regulations, institutional arrangements, 
and technologies. 
Niche:  Protected spaces where new practices, ideas, and technologies can emerge and gain mo-
mentum. These are temporary environments that allow innovations to develop without the immedi-
ate pressures of the dominant regime. 
Landscape: The broader societal context that indirectly shapes socio-technical systems, which in-
volves shocks (e.g. pandemics or wars) and slow-moving macro-developments such as shifts in cul-
tural values, political ideologies, demographics, and macroeconomic conditions. 
Transition Management: A prescriptive governance framework designed to guide the natural pro-
cesses of learning, searching, and experimenting in transitions  – supporting the strategic, tactical, 
operational, and reflexive clusters of activities essential to navigate transitions. 
Transition projects: A project that spans multiple years and involves various actors, phases, and 
initiatives making them larger than typical innovation projects and explicitly oriented towards con-
tributing to transition contexts. Due to their long-term orientation, these projects are often under-
taken as hybrids, combining elements of experimentation and practical implementation, emphasiz-
ing both learning and practical outputs. 
Local projects: Smaller innovation projects that may address broader transition objectives but are 
often practical experiments rooted in place-based settings that reflect local initiatives. 
==PAGE:6==
5 
 
Practitioners: Individuals engaged in local transition initiatives, coming from diverse backgrounds 
and organizations. They are defined by their actions and contributions toward shared objectives ra-
ther than by specific disciplines. 
Participants: The specific participants, which have been involved in applying or testing a specific 
prototype within the thesis project.  
Actors: Actors are groups or organizations from public, private, or civil society sectors that actively 
engage in transitions – and transition projects. They contribute to and influence change and are 
characterized by the role they take and power they wield in shaping sustainability transitions. 
Urban Transformative Capacity: The collective ability of the stakeholders (actors) involved in urban 
development to conceive of, prepare for, initiate and perform path-deviant change towards sustain-
ability within and across multiple complex systems that constitute the cities they relate to. 
Local urban environments: The place-based settings where local projects take place. These involve 
various local actors who partake in efforts toward sustainability transitions, often defined by specific 
geographic boundaries, like city or jurisdictions limits – such as local waste jurisdictions.

1.1 Research Motivation 
As climate change intensifies, ecosystems degrade, and global inequalities grow, supporting the tran-
sition to a sustainable and just future is more important than ever. This transition requires extensive 
changes to the systems we've relied upon throughout history, yet these systems often resist change 
due to their high inertia and systemic lock-ins (Markard et al., 2020). Promising innovations fre-
quently encounter resistance from e.g. vested interests, established habits, and sunk investments, 
hindering the adoption of more sustainable ways of life. And while some innovations do occur, they 
tend to be incremental and reinforce existing trajectories, risking falling short of long-term sustain-
ability goals in favour of short-term stability (Geels et al., 2023). 
To address this resistance to path-deviating change, a growing field of research on sustainability 
transitions has emerged, focusing on the dynamics and pathways for accelerating societal shifts to-
wards sustainability (Köhler et al., 2019; Markard et al., 2012). Within this field, frameworks like the 
multi-level perspective on socio-technical systems (Geels, 2019) and the study of technological in-
novation systems (Markard, 2020) have become popular tools for analysing societal transitions and 
informing national strategy and policy processes. However, applying these frameworks to local sus-
tainability projects operating within transitions remains a practical challenge. 
Transition Management, a complexity-based governance approach, offers a notable contribution to 
support sustainability projects. The participatory approach aims to facilitate and accelerate sustain-
ability transitions by guiding the natural processes of envisioning, learning, and experimenting, 
deemed critical for transitional progress (Kemp et al., 2007; Loorbach, 2010). Following their idea 
that transitions can be guided, recent research in design for sustainability has emphasized the posi-
tive role that design, and designers can play in transitions, particularly in supporting the intermedi-
ate governance processes discussed in transition management (Gaziulusoy & Öztekin, 2019).  
While design has traditionally focused on products, its role has expanded to address complex socio-
technical issues, incorporating methods from complex systems theory, sustainability sciences, and 
social innovation (Dorst, 2019). This role includes designing not just sustainability-oriented products 
but also the systems they exist within and the processes that support their transitions, reflected by 
the emerging research area Design for Sustainability Transitions (Ceschin & Gaziulusoy, 2019).  
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
Recent Design for Sustainability Transitions research has examined the role design professionals play 
in developing collective visions and scenarios for urban sustainability projects (Gaziulusoy & Ryan, 
2017; Irwin, 2015), co-designing transition pathways in policy processes (Hyysalo et al., 2019) and 
designing guidelines for socio-technical experiments (Ceschin, 2014). 
However, despite these advances, the field of designing for sustainability transitions is still emerging, 
with limited integration into mainstream practice. The methods and tools to support this approach 
are still developing, and there is a growing but limited understanding of how they can be applied in 
large-scale transition projects (Von Flittner et al., 2022). Designers involved in these projects cur-
rently have few means to integrate transition theories effectively, although transition theories are 
argued to be growing in popularity within systemic innovation programs (Kjøde, 2023). 
Therefore, this thesis seeks to build upon recent research in Sustainability Transitions and Design for 
Sustainability Transitions, aiming to further explore how design practice can support ongoing transi-
tion projects. Through an action research approach within a national project-partnership contrib-
uting to a transition in Denmark, it seeks to contribute to the identification and development of 
viable methods and tools that can help local practitioners guide their transition projects towards 
achieving long-term sustainability goals. 
1.2 Context of Study 
Sustainability transitions encompass many socially constructed normative pathways towards what 
can be perceived as a sustainable future. Among these, the circular economy is often highlighted as 
a key pathway (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017). It aims to shift away from the linear take-make-waste 
model, prevalent since the mid-20th century, by promoting the recirculation of products and mate-
rials by design (Aggeri, 2021; Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019). The circular economy focuses on 
the extension of product lifecycles, service-oriented business models, and closed-loop material sys-
tems, to decouple value creation from resource extraction (Bocken et al., 2016). In Europe, it has 
become central to the European Green Deal, featuring in action plans aimed at improving resource 
management (European Commission, 2020). 
However, Denmark faces significant challenges in this transition. The country’s resource footprint in 
2024 suggests that if all nations followed Denmark's consumption patterns, nearly five planets' 
worth of resources would be required (Earth Overshoot Day, 2024). Additionally, Denmark's 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
circularity rate is at a low four percent, and it holds a global record in municipal waste production at 
845 kilograms per capita per year (Bruna Alves, 2022; Hofman-Bang et al., 2023).  
To address these challenges, Denmark's Action Plan for Circular Economy, also known as the National 
Plan for Waste Prevention and Resource Management, outlines the current national strategy to im-
prove waste prevention and resource recirculation by 2032 (Danish Ministry of Environment, 2020). 
Traditionally overseen by the Environmental Agency of Denmark, the current action plan also in-
cludes a collaborative approach with the project-partnership Circular Economy Beyond Waste 
(CEBW), which aims to support the circular transition at a local level. 
CEBW is an 8-year long EU-funded LIFE Integrated Project, led by the Central Region of Denmark. It 
constitutes a partnership of 33 public organizations, including national authorities, regional admin-
istrative units, municipalities, and local utility companies, who all share responsibilities related to 
the collection and management of municipal waste.  
With a mission to support the implementation of the Danish Action Plan for Circular Economy, the 
project-partnership has established 26 individual projects, also called actions, aiming to contribute 
to achieving the targets outlined in the national action plan. It is the collective management of these 
projects that will be the primary unit of analysis in this thesis. 
1.2 Research Focus and Goals 
Circular Economy Beyond Waste provides a relevant entry point to dive into an ongoing transition 
process and explore the mechanisms influencing the transition towards circularity from the perspec-
tive of the public waste management sector in Denmark.  
Additionally, as an 8-year mission-driven transition project involving a consortium of actors, actions, 
and phases, it serves as a relevant unit of analysis to examine the implications of such large coali-
tions, when mobilizing for collective action in a large-scale transition process. 
Finally, the project provides an ideal space for experimentation, allowing the exploration of various 
methods and tools from both sustainability transitions and design for sustainability transitions stud-
ies to determine how transition theories can support the participating organizations and practition-
ers within CEBW in achieving their long-term goals.

The primary goal of this study is therefore twofold and will be to:  
1. Examine to what extent the existing transition approach in CEBW can be supported by tran-
sition theories, as well as practical methods and tools connected to these theories 
2. Develop a practical design support for transition projects, such as CEBW, and provide tangible 
recommendations to enhance the overall impact of the project going forward 
1.3 Research Approach 
Following a theoretical clarification of common analytical frameworks used to characterize transition 
processes, this study will conduct a systematic literature review to identify relevant methods and 
tools that can support large-scale transition projects. An empirical analysis of the CEBW transition 
project will be presented through a qualitative case study that applies three frameworks from sus-
tainability transitions theory to understand its transition context, assess its existing governance ap-
proach, and identify enablers and hindrances to its progress. 
Based on insights from these studies, specific processes where transition tools can be introduced 
will be pinpointed, as the foundation for developing a practical design support. In this regard, a tool 
to enable reflexive self-awareness among local actors regarding their capacity to contribute to tran-
sition projects and broader transition processes will be developed. The tool guides practitioners to 
reflect on how current project experiences relate to urban transformative capacity factors within 
their local urban environment and provides means to monitor changes in these capacities over time. 
Finally, the applicability of the tool will be discussed through an evaluation with practitioners in 
CEBW, gathering feedback on its usability and usefulness. Based on this input, recommendations will 
be made for refinement and future research to further explore the tool's potential to support local 
capacity building, as a prerequisite for local actors to contribute to transition objectives. 
In the following pages, the design research methodology behind this approach and the research 
methods conducted throughout the project will be elaborated in further detail.

2.1 Design Research Methodology 
To achieve the stated research goals, this thesis follows the Design Research Methodology (DRM), 
which is a design research framework developed by Blessing & Chakrabarti (2009). The framework 
provides an iterative and structured approach to conduct design research with the aim of generating 
and validating means to support design in practice.  
It consists of four iterative stages, as outlined below: 
• Research Clarification 
The research clarification stage (RC) covers activities related to the identification and refinement of 
the research problem in focus. This involves reviewing existing literature to understand the current 
state of research on the subject and to identify gaps that the research project will address. The pur-
pose is to outline an overall research plan that is both realistic and worthwhile. It includes the de-
velopment of research questions, identification of relevant topics to explore, expected areas of con-
tribution, and deliverables to be completed within a given time frame for a research project. 
• Descriptive Study I 
The Descriptive Study (DS-I) builds upon the initial insights gathered in the RC. It aims to expand on 
the initial understanding of the research field through activities such as reviewing literature, under-
taking empirical research or both, depending on the comprehensiveness of the study. These insights 
are gathered as descriptive factors to the existing situation suitable to be addressed during the later 
stages of the research project. 
• Prescriptive Study 
The Prescriptive Study (PS) applies insights from DS-I to develop a design support aimed at improving 
the existing situation. Deliverables in this stage involve defining design requirements, conceptualiz-
ing the design support, developing prototypes, and outlining an introduction and evaluation plan to 
properly test the applicability of the intended design support.  
• Descriptive Study II 
Following PS, the second Descriptive Study (DS II) reintroduces the empirical study, but this time to 
evaluate the impact and effectiveness of the design support. The aim is to evaluate the design 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
support in use and to identify necessary adaptations for future use. It involves implementing the 
support in a real-world setting, collecting data on its performance, and comparing this data to the 
initial situation, to detect if improvements have been made. The DS-II makes it possible to discuss 
whether the research goals have been met at the end of the project. 
The PS is closely connected to the DS II. The two stages often go back and forth in an iterative man-
ner, as feedback provides means for refinement and further adaptation of the design support and 
vice versa. 
2.2 Research Questions and Study Type 
Six research questions have been formulated to guide the planning of the research project. These 
questions are structured around the four stages of the DRM. An overview of the questions and their 
connection to research approaches and empirical methods is presented in Figure 1. 
These four stages in DRM can be combined in multiple ways depending on the type of research 
questions formulated, and the required effort to answer them. For example, in some cases, a liter-
ature review can be sufficient, while in other cases, this has to be combined with an empirical study 
to sufficiently answer one or more research questions. 
Considering the focus of the study and given the opportunity to conduct research on a real-world 
project on a topic, where an understanding of the existing situation is limited, the type of design 
research project selected is Type 5 in the DRM framework. The focus of such research project is 
explained as the “development of support based on a comprehensive study of the existing situation” 
(Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009, p. 62) 
This type of design research involves: 
• A review-based Research Clarification (RC) 
• A comprehensive Descriptive Study I (DS I) 
• A comprehensive Prescriptive Study (RS) 
• An initial Descriptive Study II (DS II)

2.3 Descriptive Research Methods 
While review-based research projects are based on the review of existing literature, a research pro-
ject involving a comprehensive study also involves empirical studies, where evidence is produced by 
the researcher. 
To ensure proper collection of the evidence necessary to answer the research questions in such 
studies, it is important to use appropriate descriptive research methods to structure the process 
accordingly. This section provides a brief overview of the additional data collection methods applied 
throughout the four stages of the research project. Please note that their specific application in the 
respective empirical and review-based studies performed in this project will be presented when 
those studies are presented. 
2.3.1 Literature Reviews 
Literature reviews in this study were used during the RC and DS-I stage of the research project. Their 
purpose has been to expand on the initial understanding of the research questions and critically 
review studies from various schools of thought. 
Literature reviews have contributed to address the first two research questions: 
• Q1: What defines sustainability transitions and what are their characteristics? 
• Q2: What methods and tools can be employed to support transitions? 
Following Campbell et al. (2023), and considering the transdisciplinary nature and vast scope of sus-
tainability transitions as a research field, the literature reviews in this thesis has primarily aimed to 
grasp the 'big picture'. As a result, they involve broader-focused and more exploratory research pro-
tocols covering a wider area of search. This stands in contrast to the narrower, more tightly focused 
scope typically found in literature reviews. 
This family of ‘big picture’ literature reviews primarily distinguishes between scoping and mapping 
reviews. Scoping reviews are geared towards addressing open-ended research questions, aiming to 
quickly explore and clarify key concepts and definitions within literature on a broader topic (Arksey 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
& O’Malley, 2005). They follow an inductive approach, often without predefined coding, allowing for 
a more explorative, and descriptive data extraction from the literature retrieved.  
In contrast, mapping reviews tend to focus on closed research questions using predefined classifica-
tion and a deductive structure, examining more papers, but extracting less data from each paper. 
They also tend to quantify and visually map specific aspects within a given topic, presenting the 
findings through visual summaries like tables or graphs (Campbell et al., 2023, p. 5).  
Scoping reviews, which tend to be used more exploratively, are often used as precursors to more 
systematic literature reviews (Munn et al., 2018). A preliminary scoping review considering the char-
acteristics of sustainability transitions was conducted as the first part of this thesis. The insights from 
the review informed subsequent, comprehensive empirical and literature studies. It was used to 
inform protocols and define research strings, inclusion-exclusion criteria and the coding of a map-
ping review focused on scanning papers for methods and tools connected to transitions theory that 
can be employed in transition projects, such as CEBW. 
The mapping review was structured following Biolchini et al. (2005), which describes a systematic 
three-step approach for literature reviews including the planning, execution, and analysis of results. 
The purpose of each step is outlined below: 
1. Planning: Research objectives are listed, and a review protocol is defined. 
2. Execution: Identification, selection, and evaluation of retrieved abstracts using inclusion-ex-
clusion criteria. 
3. Result analysis: Extraction and synthesis of data from the papers selected. 
This protocol can be used to define the focus of the literature review and establish suitable search 
strings, inclusion-exclusion criteria, and coding for e.g. identified methods and tools. 
The protocol for the preliminary scoping review will be presented in Section 3.1, while the protocol 
of the systematic mapping review will be presented in Section 4.1.1.

2.3.2 Case Studies 
Parallel to the literature reviews, a case-specific perspective was included in the descriptive stages 
of the study to investigate the current governance approach employed to facilitate transition pro-
jects, and assess current contributions towards the transition processes they take part in. 
Case studies have been contributing to answer the first three questions: 
• Q1: What defines sustainability transitions and what are their characteristics? 
• Q2: What methods and tools can be employed to support transitions? 
• Q3: What enabling and hindering mechanisms do practitioners working in transition pro-
jects experience? 
To answer these questions, a qualitative and exploratory case study approach, following guidelines 
from Flyvbjerg (1998), was employed. These case studies refer to the examination of one or more 
examples of a wider phenomenon with an emphasis on ‘letting the phenomena explain itself’ before 
comparing this to the available evidence on the topic. The strength in qualitative case studies lies in 
combining structural-historical and actor-oriented analysis, to obtain a deeper understanding of the 
existing situation (Flyvbjerg, 1998. p. 7).  
This type of study involves conducting open-ended interviews, to investigate shared patterns, values 
and experiences among actors around a given topic, which in this case involved interviewing local 
practitioners on their projects and experiences from their participation in CEBW.  
Furthermore, to investigate the structural-historical context of the project, historical documents 
were collected, such as the EU LIFE-IP project application, previous and current national plans and 
articles from professional media outlets covering recent controversies and developments in the 
waste and resource management sector in Denmark. 
An elaboration of protocols for interviews and document analysis will be presented in Section 4.2.1. 
2.4 Action Research Methods 
Later research stages follows an action-oriented research approach, focusing on applying research 
to address real-world problems - testing its effectiveness in doing so, as a way to contribute to sci-
entific knowledge. These methods adopt a cyclical, interactive process in which study participants, 
concerned with a particular issue, are actively involved as co-researchers in a series of research 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
events. In these events, research interventions are implemented and assessed within their real-
world context to address the problem at hand, thereby contributing to the scientific understanding 
of problem-solving in these settings (Coughlan & Coghlan, 2002). 
2.4.1 Participant Validation 
Towards the end of DS-I, a collaborative participant validation method was employed to verify iden-
tified gaps and needs in the transition project. According to Lindheim (2022), participant validation 
research methods involve conducting validation meetings and sharing your results at intermediate 
stages of research processes, “inviting the informants to validate and discuss your analysis and in-

can assist its specified users in achieving specified goals in a specified context. To do this, the intro-

methods evaluation included reviewing task outputs, conducting brief post-task interviews for

3.1 Origins of Sustainability Transitions 
The field of sustainability transitions formally emerged as an interdisciplinary research area in the 
late 2000s, following an increasing awareness of the need for radical systemic changes to address 
global environmental challenges, especially climate change (Markard et al., 2012). This period saw 
the formation of scientific communities such as the Sustainability Transitions Research Network 
(STRN), which has since grown to include over 3,000 scholars.  
The origins of the field trace back to earlier research on technological, social, and institutional 
change theories. This section offers a brief historical overview of sustainability transitions, highlight-
ing key concepts and common theoretical frameworks, while also comparing their similarities, dif-
ferences, and relevance to the goals of this thesis. 
A scoping review was conducted using the search databases DTU FindIt, Scopus, and Google Scholar. 
Search strings combined keywords such as "sustainability transitions," "socio-technical transitions," 
and "transitions in socio-technical systems." Following the initial search, three key literature reviews 
(Köhler et al., 2019; Loorbach et al., 2017; Markard et al., 2012) were identified. These reviews have 
been primary sources for retrieving significant contributions within the research field over the years. 
3.1.1 Transitions in Socio-Technical Systems 
In the 1990s, scholars like Rip, Kemp, and Schot pioneered research on how complex, large-scale 
technological change occurs and how they can be reoriented towards sustainability. Drawing upon 
evolutionary economics, the sociology of innovation and institutional theory, their work introduced 
concepts that have since been further developed into frameworks used to analyse and guide transi-
tion processes (Markard et al., 2012).  
Focusing on systems that integrate social, technological, and institutional components, they laid the 
foundation for understanding how novel technologies diffuse and become alternatives to en-
trenched technologies in sectors such as energy, transport, and agriculture. These studies empha-
sized that transitions require more than technological innovation - they also involve shifts in social 
practices, regulations, and markets, making transitions deeply multi-dimensional in their progres-
sion (Kemp, 1994; Rip & Kemp, 1998; Schot et al., 1994).  
Transition studies rely on the concept of socio-technical systems, which integrate both social and 
technical aspects during the creation, diffusion, and use of innovations. These systems describe the 
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
networks of various actors, artefacts and institutions within e.g. techno-scientific communities, cul-
tures, markets, industries, and political institutions, which collectively support societal functions 
such as electricity, transportation, and food. A central argument is that it is the co-evolution of these 
dimensions - where changes in one influence and are shaped by others - that drives change in socio-
technical systems over time (Geels, 2004). 
3.1.2 Regimes, Niches and The Evolving Landscape 
A key concept in socio-technical transitions theory is the socio-technical regime, which refers to the 
set of semi-coherent rules that shape and sustain a particular socio-technical system. These rules 
are reflected in cognitive beliefs, social norms, regulations, institutional arrangements, and technol-
ogies, forming stable, dominant structures across technological, cultural, political, scientific, market, 
and industrial domains of a socio-technical system. Regimes develop in response to societal chal-
lenges and become increasingly embedded and institutionalized over time. While this creates sta-
bility, it also generates resistance to radical change. Consequently, regimes reinforce certain logics 
that guide innovation efforts along established development paths, rather than fostering radically 
new ones. By focusing on incremental improvements to existing technologies and practices and di-
recting resources to maintain the status quo, regimes create systemic lock-ins and path dependen-
cies. Examples of such lock-ins include sunk investments, economies of scale, cognitive biases, tech-
nology-dependent habits, and vested interests in existing policy networks, making deeper structural 
changes intentionally difficult (Geels, 2002; Rip & Kemp, 1998).  
A frequent argument used to study socio-technical regimes is that, while their incremental improve-
ments can lead to environmental gains of up to a factor of two, deeper changes to their underlying 
structures - referred to as transformative changes - can achieve improvements of a factor of ten 
(Elzen et al., 2004). This makes understanding regime lock-ins crucial, as overcoming such barriers is 
essential to enable the kind of radical shifts needed to tackle environmental and societal challenges 
on a larger scale. Early transitions research aimed to understand how such path-deviating regime 
shifts might occur, investigating the factors that influence the destabilization of incumbent regimes 
and the emergence of new ones (Kemp, 1994). In this context, the derived concepts niche and land-
scape were introduced as relative counterparts to the regime, helping to understand how external 
pressures on a regime can induce regime shifts (Rip & Kemp, 1998).  
==PAGE:28==
27 
 
Niches are described as spaces where new practices, ideas, and technologies can emerge and gain 
momentum. These are protected, temporary environments that allow innovations to develop with-
out the immediate pressures of the dominant regime, also referred to as the selection environment. 
Consequently, niches tend to emerge in geographic areas (e.g. off-grid) or certain application do-
mains (e.g. alternative cultures). This protection enables the formation of innovation trajectories 
with potential to challenge the established regime-driven pathways. They are described as seedbeds 
for alternative innovations, allowing them to eventually destabilize and replace one or more ele-
ments of incumbent regimes (Geels & Raven, 2006; Smith & Raven, 2012). 
Landscapes refer to the broader societal context that indirectly shapes socio-technical systems. Un-
like regimes and niches, which are influenced by direct actions from internal system actors, the land-
scape involves shocks (e.g. pandemics or wars) and slow-moving macro-developments such as shifts 
in cultural values, political ideologies, demographics, and macroeconomic conditions. These auton-
omous factors are typically beyond the immediate control of individual actors or groups but exert 
pressure on regimes and niches. The landscape serves as the overarching context, shaping the ex-
ternal pressures that either restrict or facilitate transitions (Geels, 2005a). 
3.2 The Multi-Level Perspective 
Building on the early conceptualization of socio-technical niches, regimes, and landscapes, the 
Multi-Level Perspective (MLP) integrates the concepts into a framework that explains how their in-
teractions can both enable, and hinder regime shifts within socio-technical systems. Particularly ad-
vanced by Geels (2002, 2005a, 2011, 2019), the MLP has become a widely used tool for analysing 
and characterizing sustainability transitions (Köhler et al., 2019). For example, the MLP has been 
applied to study historical transitions, such as the shift from horse-drawn carriages to automobiles 
(Geels, 2005b), as well as contemporary transitions like the shift to low-carbon electricity mixes in 
Germany and in the UK (Geels et al., 2016).  
The MLP examines transitions by investigating interactions across three distinct analytical levels, 
which indicate varying degrees of dominance, scale and structuration within society (Geels, 2011): 
niches (micro-level) - where radical innovations emerge; regimes (meso-level) - the stable configu-
rations of technologies, institutions, and practices; and the landscape (macro-level) - representing 
the broader context that evolves slowly but exerts pressure on both niches and regimes. 
==PAGE:29==
28 
 
As depicted in Figure 2, transitions occur when niche innovations align with landscape pressures, 
opening windows of opportunity to challenge or replace the dominant regime (Geels, 2019). More-
over, The MLP draws on the multi-phase concept (Rotmans et al., 2001), which divides transition 
dynamics into four phases.  
 
Figure 2 – Visual depiction of the MLP on socio-technical transitions (from Geels (2019)) 
According to Geels (2019), these phases can be described as:  
• An experimentation phase: Radical innovations develop through trial and error, as actors 
from various socio-technical dimensions experiment with novel technologies or practices. 
This phase addresses growing misalignments between the socio-technical landscape and the 
regime. It is marked by high uncertainty, frequent failures, high costs, isolated experimenta-
tion, and limited cultural acceptance. 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
• Stabilization phase: Some innovations gain a foothold in niches, supported by improved re-
source flows, network formation, and shared learning. This phase is characterized by the 
emergence of dominant designs and vision articulations. In turn, resistance from established 
regime actors intensifies as these innovations gain momentum. 
• Diffusion and disruption phase: Successful innovations expand into mainstream environ-
ments, driven by performance improvements and increasing landscape pressures on the ex-
isting regime. This phase involves clear power struggles, competition, and cultural debates 
as new innovations challenge and start disrupting the regime. 
• Institutionalization phase: The new system becomes embedded in regulations, user habits, 
and professional practices, gradually replacing the old regime. Over time, this new regime 
stabilizes and, like the previous regime, may become resistant to change. 
Throughout these phases, transitions can advance, stall, or reverse due to uncertainties, shifting 
landscape developments, and complex feedback loops. This process is inherently non-linear, involv-
ing momentum shifts, delays, and resistance from vested interests, often taking decades to unfold. 
These factors reflect the uncertainty from ongoing tensions between forces of stability and change, 
which influence both the pace and direction of transitions (Rotmans et al., 2001). 
MLP also argues that the progression of transitions depends on the timing and nature of interactions, 
including whether niche innovations are sufficiently developed when landscape pressures intensify 
across multiple domains of socio-technical systems, and whether they align with or compete with 
their respective sub-domains. This creates various types of transition patterns, such as internal re-
gime transformations, market competition, whole-system substitutions, reconfigurations and mer-
gers, as well as regime collapse followed by realignments (Geels & Schot, 2007). 
3.3 Strategic Niche Management  
Strategic Niche Management (SNM) complements the Multi-Level Perspective (MLP), highlighting 
the role of niche development in sustainability transitions. Introduced by Hoogma, Kemp, and Schot 
(2002; 1998), SNM investigates the creation and development of niches for promising innovations 
through experimentation. Geels and Raven (2006; 2008) further elaborated on these ideas, investi-
gating how radical innovation emerge within protected niches and cluster into pathways capable of 
shifting and transforming existing regimes. Therefore, SNM examines the experimental, pilot, and 
==PAGE:31==
30 
 
demonstration projects that facilitate the practical development of these pathways over time. It 
studies the co-evolution of technologies, user practices, and regulatory structures within niches, to 
understand how they ultimately support broader socio-technical transitions. 
According to Geels & Raven (2006), there are three key internal niche processes, which drive and 
stabilize niche development over time: 
• Articulating expectations: Polling expectations and setting clear visions (goals, require-
ments) for innovation activities to guide development and attract continuous support. 
• Building networks: Bringing together diverse actors (e.g., companies, policymakers, users) 
to support the innovation and share resources (funding, knowledge, projects etc). 
• Facilitating learning: Gaining insights from successes and failures to improve both the inno-
vation and the broader social and technical context (1st and 2nd order learning). 
As illustrated in Figure 3, niches operate on two levels: the local level, which involves specific pro-
jects, and the global level, encompassing broader communities working on similar innovation topics. 
Positive projects outcomes from local projects might create a reinforcing feedback loop: refining 
learning, adjusting expectations, and attracting new actors, which shapes innovation trajectories, 
creating shared rules and heuristics for the further development of the niche. Negative project out-
comes risk doing the opposite, slowing down the niche processes as a result. 
Later refinements to SNM consolidated three properties of niche development: shielding, nurturing, 
and empowering. These developments also highlighted two types of niche development strategies, 
to empower niche actors (Smith & Raven, 2012):  
• Fit-and-conform - where niches adapt to the existing selection environment by aligning 
with current markets, regulations, and societal expectations. 
• Stretch-and-transform - where niches aim to politically reshape the selection environment 
by pushing for changes in regulations, market conditions, and societal norms. 
Finally, the concept of shielding has expanded to include not just protective, passive environments 
but also consider active measures, such as targeted public policies, strategic research, subsidies, and 
political support, which actively creates favourable conditions for niche innovations to gain traction, 
and eventually challenge dominant regime structures (Raven et al., 2016).

3.4 Transition Management 
While SNM and the MLP provide insights into how local innovations pressure regimes, critics argue 
that their focus on bottom-up experimentation overlooks societal power dynamics and the role of 
governance, policy frameworks, and strategic visioning. More specifically, it criticizes SNM for as-
suming niche innovations to develop autonomously, downplaying the importance of national poli-
cies and coordination in guiding such processes (Berkhout et al., 2004). 
==PAGE:33==
32 
 
Transition Management (TM) responds to the critiques of SNM and the MLP, particularly regarding 
the need for a stronger focus on the role of governance and strategic coordination in shaping sus-
tainability transitions. It elaborates on the Multi-Level Perspective (Rip & Kemp, 1998), and Complex 
Systems Theory (Kauffman, 1995).  
Scholars such as Loorbach, Rotmans, and Kemp (2007; 2001) advanced TM by emphasizing the role 
of reflexive governance when managing co-evolutionary processes - a governance approach that is 
adaptive, iterative, and guided by continuous learning. This includes a learning philosophy based on 
learning-by-doing and doing-by-learning, where practical experience informs strategic adjustments 
in a recursive manner through small steps of searching, learning and experimenting. 
The TM approach is rooted in the dynamics of complex societal systems and includes several key 
tenets for guiding transition processes: It emphasizes the importance of long-term thinking (at least 
25 years) to inform short-term actions, the acknowledgment of uncertainty and need for flexible 
objectives, and the creation of protected spaces for the experimentation and formation of niche 
trajectories. Additionally, it highlights that self-governance through multi-actor participation across 
multiple domains and levels is essential for fostering shared understanding, mobilizing networks, 
and ensuring political and social support during transitions (Loorbach, 2010). 
To operationalize the principles of TM, the approach prescribes a structured and iterative process 
known as the Transition Management Cycle shown in Figure 4. 
The TM cycle aligns different types of governance activities - strategic, tactical, operational, and re-
flexive - in a continuous, iterative loop. Each activity informs the others, ensuring that short-term 
actions are aligned with long-term goals and goal adjustments are made based on real-world learn-
ing outcomes from experiments (Loorbach, 2010): 
• Strategic activities occur at the societal level, focusing on cultures. It involves participative 
problem-structuring, envisioning long-term sustainability goals, and mobilizing networks, 
bringing together key stakeholders, who seeks to steer transitions. 
• Tactical activities take place at the subsystem level, aiming to translate strategic visions into 
coalitions, developing scenarios to explore possible transition pathways, and creating de-
tailed transition agendas with a focus on enabling structural change. 
==PAGE:34==
33 
 
• Operational activities focus on short-term actions, mobilizing actors to implement projects 
and coordinated experiments that test new technologies, policies, and practices across mul-
tiple domains, providing practical insights for strategic transition efforts. 
• Reflexive activities ensure continuous monitoring and evaluation of the above activities, pro-
moting learnings from projects and broader societal developments to reflect upon, adapt 
and refine the earlier outcome of both operational, tactical and strategic activities. 
 
Figure 4 - The Transition Management Cycle (From (Loorbach, 2010). 
In addition to the TM cycle, key concepts in TM include transition arenas, which are central in facil-
itating strategic activities with diverse actors. The arenas are conceptualized as collaborative spaces 
for frontrunners and key actors to come together, co-create visions, identify challenges, and set long-
term goals. The aim is to foster cooperation and build networks, empowering innovative and influ-
ential actors to drive transition processes (Loorbach, 2010).  
Furthermore, transition scenarios aim to explore diverse future pathways toward achieving long-
term goals, helping stakeholders anticipate potential challenges and coordinate their transition 
agendas, even in uncertain conditions (Sondeijker et al., 2006). Finally, transition experiments rep-
resent coordinated efforts to test innovative technologies, policies, and practices in real-world set-
tings, explicitly aligning them with specific transition goals. By combining multiple experiments, the 
objective is to broaden, scale, and deepen their impact, ensuring that short-term actions effectively 
contribute to systemic, long-term transition (Van Den Bosch & Rotmans, 2008).

3.5 Technological and Mission-Oriented Innovation Systems 
The fourth strand of research on sustainability transitions to sustainability transitions is the concept 
of Technological Innovation Systems (TIS), which emerged in the 2000s, building on theories of tech-
nological systems (Carlsson & Stankiewicz, 1991) and systems of innovation (Edquist, 1997).  
TIS focuses on how networks of actors - such as firms, research institutes, and governments - influ-
ence the generation, diffusion, and utilization of technologies (Carlsson et al., 2002). Unlike the 
Multi-Level Perspective (MLP), which examines broader socio-technical niche-regime interactions, 
TIS focuses on the structural components, such as actors, institutions, networks, and infrastructure, 
along with system functions (Hekkert et al., 2007). It investigates the conditions that enable or hin-
der technological development. These functions include entrepreneurial activity, knowledge diffu-
sion, the mobilisation of resources, market formation, and the creation of legitimacy, among others 
(Bergek et al., 2008). 
In recent years, the TIS Framework has expanded from a focus on technological development to 
Mission-Oriented Innovation Systems (MIS), which respond to specific societal challenges related to 
e.g. climate change or circular economy goals (Hekkert et al., 2020). MIS shifts the focus from specific 
technological innovations to a broader, goal-driven approach, aligning innovation activities with so-
cietal missions instead. According to Elzinga (2023, p. 5), a MIS reframes the innovation system to 
encompass "all actors, organizations, institutions, networks, and technologies that play a role in 
driving solutions that may contribute to mission completion". MIS also draws upon recent exnovation 
studies, considering how the targeted decline or destabilization of outdated systems can enable the 
adoption of new solutions (Markard, 2020). Consequently, a MIS might integrate multiple innovation 
and exnovation efforts to achieve a mission objective. 
MIS assessments are structured into three main analytical steps, also depicted in Figure 5: 
1. Problem-solution diagnosis: Focus is on the mission’s societal problem, framing the multiple 
existing problem-solution directions, their interactions (as neutral, contesting or collabora-
tive) and their maturity. 
2. Structural analysis: MIS introduces the mission arena, defining the actors, institutions, net-
works and infrastructure involved in the mission’s programming and performance structure 
and how these interact with each other. The programming structure consists of the collection 
==PAGE:36==
35 
 
of actors that deliberately formulate, operationalise, and govern efforts to support the mis-
sion, while the performance structure refers the broad set of actors, networks, technology, 
and institutions involved in the development and diffusion of solutions. 
3. Functional analysis: This step assesses how key processes affect the acceleration of solutions 
and the destabilization of regime barriers. In addition to traditional TIS functions, MIS con-
siders programming functions to align solutions with the mission and expands performance 
functions to include both innovation and regime destabilization activities.

and hindrances for solution acceleration and regime destabilization). (From Elzinga et al. (2023)

transition dynamics. It focuses on the shielding, nurturing, and empowering properties of niches, 
and the role these play in helping local innovation projects gain wider support. It emphasizes on 
protected spaces and how internal processes, such as facilitating learning, building networks, and 
articulating expectations, reinforce projects outcomes leading to niche development.

TM contrasts with SNM utilizing a top-down approach. Rather than viewing transitions as emergent 
phenomena that create their own trajectories, TM argues that short-term experimentation efforts 
must be tactically coordinated to ensure that they follow long-term societal goals. It emphasizes on 
how the interconnection between strategic, tactical, operational, and reflexive governance activities 
actively steer sustainability transitions. Consequently, it also highlights the political nature of transi-
tions, where coalition-building and actor agency play an important role in shaping the pace and di-

and open-ended objectives due to the uncertainties inherent to transitions. 
TIS does not introduce concepts like niches and regimes, but focuses on innovation systems, consist-
ing of networks shaped by actors, institutions, and infrastructures. It examines how these compo-
nents support or hinder innovation, analysing them through systemic functions - such as entrepre-
neurial activity, knowledge development, and market formation - to identify systemic drivers and 
barriers to their progress. While SNM and TIS share similarities in the way they address radical, 
emerging innovation communities, SNM primarily focuses on the early phases of transitions (niches). 
In contrast, TIS examines system functions across the entire innovation system life cycle, focusing on 
removing systemic barriers, and improving conditions during multiple phases. 
MIS extends TIS by focusing on innovation systems with mission-oriented goals, addressing persis-
tent societal challenges, and considering multiple trajectories rather than focusing on one specific 
technology. It emphasizes coordination across innovation trajectories and, like TM, the importance 
of normative directionality in steering efforts toward achieving societal missions. Like TM and SNM, 
MIS acknowledges the need for experimentation and social learning in transitions but extends this 
focus to include both innovation and exnovation activities. 
3.7 Relevance to Thesis 
In summary, this thesis aims to explore how theoretical frameworks from sustainability transitions 
research can support transition projects, such as CEBW.  To achieve this, the thesis will conduct an 
empirical analysis informed by the MLP, TM, SNM, and MIS frameworks, along with a systematic 
literature review to identify relevant methods and tools that apply these frameworks in practice. 
In the empirical study, the frameworks have been applied as follows:

38 
 
• 
The MLP framework will be used to provide an initial overview of niche-regime-landscape 
dynamics in Denmark’s waste system, examining how landscape pressures affect the regime 
and identifying the niche trajectories and pathways responding to these forces. 
• 
The TM framework will be used to analyse the governance processes and past decision-mak-
ing shaping the current state of CEBW, with a focus on how actors have navigated across 
strategic, tactical, and operational levels to date. 
• 
The MIS framework, supplemented by SNM, will focus on assessing system functions (e.g., 
knowledge development, resource mobilization) to identify systemic enablers and hin-
drances. The analysis will highlight how functional dynamics of structural components - both 
internal ‘niche’ processes and external factors - influence CEBW’s momentum. 
The purpose of applying the frameworks has been to evaluate the transition project through three 
lenses: its transition context, governance approach, and the systemic factors influencing its ability 
to achieve long-term goals. This evaluation serves as a first step towards identifying the most appro-
priate tools to introduce to support transition projects, such as CEBW. 
3.8 Other Schools of Thought 
While frameworks such as MLP, SNM, TM, and TIS/MIS have shaped much of sustainability transi-
tions research, alternative approaches have emerged that offer both complementary, conflicting, 
and more specialized perspectives (Köhler et al., 2019). For instance, Coenen et al. (2012) emphasize 
the importance of geography in transitions, arguing that transitions are heterogeneous, place-based 
and shaped by local conditions such as regional policies and capacities. Moreover, Jørgensen (2012) 
introduces the concept of arenas of development, where different actor-worlds shape transition tra-
jectories through conflict, debate, and negotiation. Unlike the MLP's hierarchical levels, this presents 
a flat ontology, emphasizing the contested and often controversial nature of transitions. Another 
actor-oriented perspective comes from Avelino & Wittmayer (2016), who examine power dynamics 
between diverse social groups - such as governments, businesses, and civil society - exploring how 
their interactions influence transitions. Additionally, Kivimaa et. al. (2019) highlights the role of in-
termediaries in facilitating collaboration, mediating between these social actor groups, and bridging 
knowledge gaps between different sectors to support smoother transitions.

3.8 Conclusion to RC 
Sustainability transitions are non-linear, multi-dimensional, co-evolutionary processes that engage 
a diverse range of actors in pursuing long-term sustainability goals in socio-technical systems. They 
unfold over decades and are driven by experimentation, reflexivity, and open-ended goals, making 
them uncertain and politically contested by nature. 
This section has described and compared four key frameworks within the field of sustainability tran-
sitions to understand these processes - MLP, SNM, TM, and TIS/MIS. While they share common aims, 
they differ in their origins, focus, and core concepts. These differences create areas of overlap but 
also highlight how the frameworks can complement each other, making them suitable for analysing 
different aspects of CEBW and its transition context. 
The Multi-Level Perspective (MLP) explains transitions by examining interactions between niches, 
regimes, and the landscape, focusing on how landscape pressures create opportunities for niche 
innovations to disrupt established regimes in socio-technical systems.  
Strategic Niche Management (SNM) emphasizes internal niche processes within protected spaces 
that foster innovation, particularly through the shielding, nurturing, and empowering of niches.  
Transition Management (TM) provides a governance approach for steering transitions by coordi-
nating strategic, tactical, operational, and reflexive activities, aligning short-term actions with long-
term goals through a participatory process of searching, learning, and experimenting.  
Technological Innovation Systems (TIS) analyse how networks shaped by actors, institutions, and 
infrastructure drive the generation, diffusion, and phasing out of technologies. Mission-Oriented 
Innovation Systems (MIS) expand this focus to consider mission-oriented innovation systems, incor-
porating mission framings and multiple innovation and exnovation trajectories.

4.1 DS I - Literature Review 
This section provides an overview of methods and tools that can be applied in transition processes 
to support them. The section is based on a systematic literature review, which reviews past contri-
butions connected to transitions theory that present prescriptive approaches to support transition 
governance activities, such as visioning, experimenting and learning. First, the section outlines the 
systematic review process, covering selection of keywords, criteria for inclusion and exclusion, and 
the approach used to map and classify the methods and tools. Second, it provides an overview of 
the identified methods and tools and describes different clusters based on function commonality. 
For each cluster, the key characteristics are compared and described. 
The section is primarily concerned with answering the following research question(s): 
• Q2: What methods and tools can be employed to support transitions?

4.1.1 Review Protocol 
The systematic review protocol was designed to only include relevant methods and tools in the re-
view by developing an appropriate search string, and inclusion-exclusion criteria. It also aimed to 
categorize methods and tools connecting their purpose to the TM cycle (Loorbach, 2010), focusing 
on how they intend to support various transition governance activities within this cycle. The final 
search string, shown in Figure 6, retrieved 703 papers from the SCOPUS database, along with one 
additional paper from SciSpace.ai using the prompt "tools for managing transitions". 
 
Figure 6 - Keywords and filtering process of retrieved papers from SCOPUS 
==PAGE:44==

The review included only papers that explicitly presented or applied methods or tools related to 
transition concepts, such as sustainability transitions, socio-technical systems, socio-technical tran-
sitions, or innovation systems. The screening process was conducted in three rounds: 
1. Abstract review: Screening of 704 papers by reading abstracts. 
2. Introduction and conclusion review: Deeper screening of 82 papers by reviewing introduc-
tions and conclusions. 
3. Full paper review: 16 papers were fully reviewed, with an additional 28 papers identified 
using Wohlin’s (2014) snowball sampling method (screening references and citations). 
Papers that were purely descriptive, tools highly specialized for specific fields (e.g., medicine, food), 
or meta-research methods were excluded during abstract review. Thereafter, the retrieved papers 
were scanned for tools, and the papers with tools explicitly connected to transition theories were 
included in the study. A review of 44 papers identified methods and tools supporting transition pro-
cesses. Some focused on specific tools. Others presented systematic methods or toolkits. Although 
tools can be considered task-specific and methods as broader, structured approaches involving mul-
tiple tasks, this review describes them as tools unless explicitly referred to as methods. 
4.1.2 Classification 
The tools were classified with respect to the different levels of governance activities depicted in the 
transition management cycle, as described in section 3.6.  
• Strategic activities: Tools that assist in structuring of complex problems, envisioning and the 
establishment of multi-actor networks. 
• Tactical activities: Tools that help translate long-term visions into coalitions and actionable 
transition agendas, which develop and target images of desirable futures. 
• Operational activities: Tools that guide short-term actions helping to mobilize actors and co-
ordinate projects and experiments, testing solutions and gathering practical insights to refine 
and inform future strategic and tactical processes. 
Additionally, the classification distinguishes between active (learning-by-doing) and reflexive (doing-
by-learning) aspects of transition governance.  This is based on Loorbach’s (2022, p. 2) description 
of these processes as the “collective process of ‘learning-by-doing’ and ‘doing-by-learning.’” 
==PAGE:45==
44 
 
• 
Active (learning-by-doing): Focuses on forward-looking action and experimentation, where 
knowledge is gained through the practical implementation of initiatives. 
• 
Reflexive (doing-by-learning): Focuses on gaining knowledge by evaluating past actions to 
adapt and refine future strategies, improving ongoing efforts based on lessons learned. 
Finally, tools classified under each governance level were grouped into subcategories, clustering 
those having similar purposes. 
4.1.3 Findings 
A total of 71 tools were identified in the literature review, supporting various functions in transitions 
governance. A list of these tools and their mentions is summarized in Table 2. Key sources include 
the Visual Toolbox for System Innovation (de Vicente, 2016) and the Workbook for Transition Makers 
(Silvestri et al., 2020), which compile tools to assist practitioners in mapping, analysing, and facilitat-
ing systemic change. Similarly, The Transition Design Framework (Carnegie Mellon University, 2024; 
Irwin, 2018) offers tools for addressing wicked problems, co-creating long-term visions, and devel-
oping systemic interventions 
Other notable contributions include: 
• Pereno & Barbero (2020), which compares tools from Systemic Design to key system innova-
tion strategies from transition studies, such as sharing visions and enhancing niches. 
• Gaziulusoy & Öztekin (2019) discussing design methods for scenario development, stake-
holder engagement, pathway creation, and niche management within the Design for Sus-
tainability Transitions research field.  
• Halbe et al. (2020) and de Koning et al. (2021), which both emphasize the importance of 
participatory modelling, listing several methods to structure stakeholder engagement across 
different phases of transition governance. 
Additionally, the most frequently cited method, Participatory Backcasting from Quist & Vergragt 
(2006) has been further developed by various authors to better align action with long-term goals. 
Likewise, Technology Roadmapping (Phaal et al., 2004), has become a popular approach for refining 
broader objectives and linking these to specific interventions at the micro-level.

4.1.3.1 C1: Actor-Oriented Analysis and Building Multi-Actor Networks 
The first cluster focuses on how individuals and social groups influence the pace and direction of 
transitions, using stakeholder mapping and strategic participation methods to identify, select, and 
engage key actors in addressing societal issues during the early stages of network formation. 
G1.1 - Mapping Actor Relationships  
This subgroup involves tools for identifying stakeholders with vested interests in a societal issue, 
ranging from gaining an initial overview of relevant actors involved in a societal issue to broader 
mapping exercises aiming to better understand their interconnections.  
The Actor Tree from de Vicente (2016) helps practitioners identify actor groups and clustering indi-
vidual actors within them. Other tools focus more on the relations between actors. Silvestri et al. 
(2020) and Halbe et al. (2020) highlight Social Network Analysis as a method for understanding ac-
tors’ roles, relationships, and positions through their interactions within networks. Similarly, de Vi-
cente’s (2016) Stakeholder Universe tool aids in examining existing connections among actors, but 
also puts emphasis on identifying the areas that might be prone to change. Irwin (2018) also em-
phasizes mapping connections between actors through Mapping Stakeholder Relations. It focuses 
on identifying lines of conflict and agreement, to highlight where actors have opposing or shared 
interests, clarifying points of future contention or collaboration. 
G1.2 - Analysing and Comparing Actor Perspectives 
This group of tools focuses on analysing the deeper motives, attributes, and roles of specific stake-
holders on a given topic, rather than their interconnections and relationships. Here, de Vicente 
(2016) offers tools such as Enlarged Empathy Mapping (5) and Credential Cards (6), which help cre-
ate detailed profiles of individual actors to better understand their attributes, capabilities, and per-
spective on specific challenges. Another tool from the same toolbox, Stakeholder Mapping (7), helps 
compare actors in terms of their interests, expertise, and relevance, clarifying power dynamics and 
different stances on a societal issue.  
Silvestri et al. (2020) introduces the Power-Domain Mapping (8) tool, enabling practitioners to ana-
lyse the organizational background of actors and discuss the types of power they wield (e.g., inno-
vative or reinforcing) during transitions. Silvestri et al. (2020) also presents the Multi-Actor 
==PAGE:50==
49 
 
Perspective Tool (9), which further explores how organizational contexts shape the logics of certain 
actor groups in socio-technical systems (e.g. state, market or civil society). 
G1.3 - Engaging and Facilitating Collaboration in Multi-Actor Networks 
This subgroup facilitates the early formation of large multi-actor networks through co-creation and 
participatory design, engaging proactive frontrunners and ensuring balanced participation, when 
developing shared understanding on objectives. The Ontological Variety in Participation tool aims to 
prevent selection bias in strategic foresight processes by ensuring a balanced representation of per-
spectives and interests (Pereno & Barbero, 2020). Additionally, Structured Dialogical Design presents 
a systematic method to manage interactions and strengthen dialogue among diverse actor groups. 
It uses specialized protocols to collect and visualize participant responses, ensuring all perspectives 
are represented (Pereno & Barbero, 2020).  
Halbe et al. (2020) introduces methods to improve participation for integrated knowledge develop-
ment. For example, the Rural Participatory Appraisal offers guidelines to help stakeholders work 
together in creating visual maps and diagrams to identify and prioritize problems. Another method, 
Companion Modelling, uses role-playing games combined with modelling techniques to simulate 
complex systems. This approach allows participants to explore different perspectives, test various 
scenarios, and engage in ongoing discussions and decision-making. 
4.1.3.2 C2: Systems-Oriented Analysis and Problem-Structuring 
The second cluster builds on the first by offering tools that enhance the understanding of problems 
using a systems-oriented approach. This includes identifying root causes, recognizing feedback 
loops, evaluating multi-domain implications in socio-technical systems, and analysing how societal 
issues evolve over time to understand the emerging trends shaping socio-technical systems. 
G2.1 - Analysing the Underlying Causes of Societal Issues 
Looking into the complexity of societal issues, this subgroup examines tools that reveal the underly-
ing dynamics of seemingly surface-level challenges. Irwin (2018) presents Mapping Wicked Prob-
lems, a tool to connects visible consequences of problems to their root causes across five system-
level domains, using visual maps to link surface-level issues to deeper systemic causes (Carnegie 
Mellon University, 2024). Mitake et al. (2020) describes a similar tool, Fault-Tree Analysis, which 
outlines the underlying causes of a target problem in a hierarchical structure. Likewise, de Vicente 
==PAGE:51==
50 
 
(2016) introduces Fishing for Barriers, an adapted Ishikawa Diagram that maps problems and multi-
ple possible root causes, distinguishing itself by framing these causes as ‘bottlenecks’ to better con-
textualize them as issues within broader transitions. 
Causal Loop Diagrams maps recursive cause-and-effect relationships instead of identifying root 
causes. Both Halbe et al. (2020) and Wilkerson & Trellevik (2021) highlight its value in visualizing 
dynamic system patterns, identifying feedback loops, and locating leverage points to disrupt prob-
lem-reinforcing mechanisms in complex systems. Finally, the Uncovering Systems Tool, takes an in-
stitutional perspective on problem definition, examining how problematic practices connect to dom-
inant structures and cultures within subsystems. It offers a structured approach to reflect on how 
these elements interact to reinforce institutional patterns (Silvestri et al., 2020). 
G2.2 - Exploring Broader Systemic Influences Shaping Problems 
This subgroup broadens the investigation of complex problems by situating them within their sys-
temic contexts. It highlights the need for a multidisciplinary, multi-dimensional, and multi-level ap-
proach to understand how socio-technical systems shape these challenges. 
The System Demarcator helps defining system boundaries considering sectoral, geographical, and 
institutional factors, stressing the importance of setting boundaries early for effective modelling (Sil-
vestri et al., 2020). Various approaches then guide systems modelling. The Pentagonal Problem tool 
encourage self-assessment across five key areas to build an understanding from one perspective 
before engaging multiple perspectives in comprehensive mapping tools (de Vicente, 2016). Con-
versely, Pereno & Barbero (2020) highlights Gigamapping, as a multidisciplinary tool that combines 
modelling techniques from different fields to create a multi-dimensional visual representation of 
complex systems. Similarly, the Rich Picture technique uses visual diagrams to capture diverse un-
derstandings of an issue and its socio-political context, to make complex relationships more visibly 
understandable (de Koning et al., 2021; Halbe et al., 2020). Pereno and Barbero (2020) also discuss 
the Holistic Diagnosis method, which starts by analysing quantitative energy and matter flows in 
systems to later explore the socio-political factors affecting them. 
Additionally, De Vicente (2016) and Silvestri et al. (2020) describe two variations of a Multi-Level 
Perspective Tool, which help explore the niche-regime-landscape levels and their interactions across 
socio-technical system dimensions to identify tensions and opportunities. Similarly, the Transition 
==PAGE:52==
51 
 
Model Canvas simplifies the MLP by presenting its core concepts with the visual format of the busi-
ness model canvas, to make an accessible tool for practitioners familiar with such formats (van 
Rijnsoever & Leendertse, 2020). Gorissen et al. (2016) flips this around and integrates the business 
model canvas within the MLP to create Transformative Business Models, offering a framework for 
contextualizing business model innovation within transition processes.  
Another set of tools focuses on framing problems at different levels of abstraction. Joore & Brezet 
(2015) introduce the Multi-Level Design Model, which links design phases - such as problem defini-
tion - to change processes across four scales: products, product-service systems, socio-technical sys-
tems, and societal systems. Similarly, Irwin (2018) discuss the Social Pathway Matrix, a tool that 
breaks down problems by levels of involvement, from individual actions to cross-sectorial collabora-
tion, and by scale, from specific projects to broader cultural contexts. Finally, the 4 Levels of Govern-
ance tool connects problem definition to strategic, tactical, operational, and reflexive governance 
levels, exploring how policymaking practices at these levels contribute to societal issues (Silvestri et 
al., 2020). 
G2.3 - Assessing Past and Future Trends Shaping Current Patterns  
By tracing the impact of past developments to explain emerging trends, this subgroup uncovers how 
socio-technical regimes and niches evolve, highlighting how forces of stability and change have 
gained momentum over time. Both the Mapping the Evolution of a Wicked Problem tool from the 
Transition Design Framework (Carnegie Mellon University, 2024) and the Trajectories of Change tool 
from de Vicente (2016), use a historical perspective to understand system evolution, emphasizing 
how past insights can guide future visions and inform present-day intervention.  
The Context Map broadens the focus to ongoing macro-trends across multiple domains, to explore 
how possible future developments can shape and influence innovation efforts (de Vicente, 2016). 
Irwin (2018) refers to a similar tool for contextualizing wicked problems in evolving socio-technical 
landscapes, the STEEP/PESTEL model, which examines how they are shaped by macro-environmen-
tal trends. Finally, The X-Curve Framework engages practitioners in discussing how current trends 
and future developments may impact socio-technical systems, but does so, by explicitly focusing on 
emerging patterns of both build-up and breakdown (Hebinck et al., 2022; Silvestri et al., 2022). 
 
 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
4.1.3.3 C3: Collectively Envisioning and Imagining Distant Futures 
The third cluster of tools helps stakeholders align on desirable futures before planning long-term 
strategies. By exploring and testing future scenarios, these tools help prioritizing strategic direc-
tions based on current knowledge of what is feasible and desirable. 
G3.1 – Developing Shared Narratives for a Desirable Future 
These tools help stakeholders collaboratively imagine and co-create shared narratives and outline 
detailed scenarios for reaching long-term objectives. 
The Ocean of Opportunities ideation tool helps identify current market gaps as a preliminary step 
in envisioning processes (de Vicente, 2016) The Advanced Design model integrates design methods 
with anticipatory processes to develop concepts based in future scenarios, rather than focusing on 
gaps in existing markets (Pereno & Barbero, 2020). 
The Visual Story tool uses 'media headlines' to encourage practitioners to creatively express desired 
outcomes and key milestones through words and drawings, providing a simple yet imaginative way 
to explore desirable futures (de Vicente, 2016). Insights from such tools can serve as inputs for more 
advanced methods like Virtual City Experimentation, which synthesizes contributions from visioning 
workshops turning them into detailed urban scenarios. These normative scenarios visually depict 
immersive, long-term urban futures through design vignettes that integrate insights from various 
disciplines (Gaziulusoy & Ryan, 2017b; Ryan et al., 2016).  
Similarly, Sustainable Futures Scenarios provides a method for co-developing long-term visions by 
integrating exploratory and normative scenario techniques with participatory systems modelling. 
Like Virtual City Experimentation, it uses detailed design vignettes but combines this with quantita-
tive model simulations to help participants better anticipate trade-offs between different scenarios, 
evaluating their impact on long-term futures (Iwaniec et al., 2020). 
Alternatively, instead of focusing on detailing a single lens, Developing Long-Term Future Visions uses 
multiple lenses to describe potential futures. To consider the wide-ranging implications, the tool 
invites participants to explore future scenarios across different scales - from households to cities and 
the planet as a whole (Carnegie Mellon University, 2024; Irwin, 2018). 
 
 
==PAGE:54==
53 
 
G3.2 – Evaluating the Desirability and Plausibility of Visions 
This subgroup focuses on testing the consistency and plausibility of existing visions and scenarios, 
offering insights for decision-making within strategic planning processes. 
The Causal-Layered Analysis Game engages stakeholders in immersive roleplay, where participants 
adopt diverse perspectives to explore a scenario across four layers - litany, systemic causes, 
worldviews, and underlying myths - uncovering deeper insights to refine scenarios before strategic 
decisions are to be made (Heinonen et al., 2017). Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis takes a more pre-
dictive approach, enabling stakeholders to semi-quantitatively evaluate and compare scenarios us-
ing pre-negotiated criteria, providing a structured and transparent approach for decision-making 
under uncertainty (Halbe et al., 2020).  
Fuzzy Cognitive Mapping, combines expert insights with local knowledge, enabling participants to 
map key variables and relationships, assign relative strengths, and simulate changes to assess plau-
sibility (Halbe et al., 2020). Cross-Impact Analysis evaluates how these variables interact to ensure 
consistent scenarios, while System Dynamics Modelling uses quantitative models to simulate future 
scenarios, revealing plausible side effects, trade-offs, and long-term consequences in the form of 
changes in stocks and flows of a system (Halbe et al., 2020; Iwaniec et al., 2014).  
Two newer approaches, Vision Cooperation Analysis and Branching Point Analysis, investigate sce-
narios by analysing for potential affinities among different niche trajectories. The former highlights 
potential alignments for collaboration by analysing for complementarities in their developed visions 
(Wesche et al., 2024), while the latter provides a framework to evaluate different pathways to clarify 
possible diverging or converging outcomes as niches progress (Lovell & Foxon, 2023). 
4.1.3.4 C4: Aligning Long-Term Visions with Short-Term Action 
Once long-term goals and visions are established, the fourth cluster of tools focuses on translating 
broader policy agendas and future scenarios into actionable strategies, detailing the steps required 
to move from the present to the desired future. 
 
 
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
G4.1 – Backcasting: Connecting Long-Term Goals with Ongoing Changes 
This subgroup elaborates on the Participative Backcasting method by Quist & Vergragt (2006), en-
couraging actors to work backwards from a desired future to identify intermediate objectives and 
agree on short-term actions. This process is implemented in various ways but emphasises on creat-
ing process-type scenarios, adding pathways to the more content-related scenarios in C3. 
The Double-Flow Scenario Method integrates backcasting and forecasting through visual two-way 
scenario maps, enabling organizations to see how their current actions influence certain trajectories, 
connect short-term changes to broader long-term societal goals, and identify pathways to achieve 
their desired outcomes (Gaziulusoy et al., 2013). Similarly, the Future Radars tool (50) developed by 
de Vicente (2016) combines backcasting and forecasting using a visual mapping approach, encour-
aging participants to outline pathways and then qualitatively assess their feasibility and impact, help-
ing them prioritize the most desirable options. The Transition Scenarios method combines backcast-
ing with TM concepts to create a framework that considers multiple futures, and the structural 
changes needed to achieve them. It helps actors navigate uncertainties by exploring various path-
ways toward long-term goals based on niche trajectories, alongside their barriers and drivers, before 
selecting specific strategies (Sondeijker, 2009). 
Kok et al. (2011) also merge backcasting with forecasting methods, such as exploratory scenarios. 
Their Exploratory-Backcasting Scenario approach emphasizes the importance of forecasting before 
backcasting to enhance the credibility and outcomes of the backcasting process. This is also reflected 
in the Backcasting & Assessing the Present and Three Horizons methods, providing ways to assess 
the current state of a system against an envisioned future. They focus on what to retain, discard, 
strengthen, and keep from the present before backcasting the intermediate actions needed to reach 
a future setting (Carnegie Mellon University, 2024; Irwin, 2018; Sharpe et al., 2016).  
Finally, The Mid-Range Pathway Creation Toolset specifically focuses on detailing pathways for inter-
mediate objectives targeting long-term vision. It incorporates co-design elements when backcasting 
- such as personas, visual maps, and game design - to foster collaboration among diverse actors 
during the planning and detailing of strategies and interventions in policy-making processes (Hyysalo 
et al., 2019). 
 
 
==PAGE:56==

G4.2 – Coordinating Strategies and Interventions Across Scale and Time 
While backcasting outlines the overarching pathways to achieve long-term goals, a complementary 
set of tools focuses on detailing intermediate steps, clarifying necessary actions, and assigning re-
sponsibilities for implementation. These tools draw upon Technology Roadmapping, designed to 
help organizations visualize and adapt to evolving relationships between technology, organizational 
aims, and societal trends. Its flexible, multi-layered structure has been argued as effective in captur-
ing the complex co-evolutionary processes of transitions, when diverse actors are involved in plan-
ning disruptive socio-technical change (Phaal et al., 2004).  
Recent transition studies have elaborated on Technology Roadmapping, now referring to Socio-tech-
nical Roadmapping - a visual tool that connects long-term goals with the micro-level developments 
necessary to drive structural changes. Here, Mitake et al. (2020) and de Vicente (2016) combine 
socio-technical roadmapping with forecasting-backcasting methods to help actors organize the mi-
cro-level developments - such as in skills, resources, and knowledge - necessary to achieve interme-
diate objectives tied to future scenarios. Other examples include Letaba & Pretorius (2022), who 
aligns the format of socio-technical roadmapping with the domains, phases, and levels of the MLP. 
Moreover, Greenaway (2024) emphasizes the importance of agency and provides a tool to evaluate 
which actors to engage to implement the outlined changes in roadmaps with Stakeholder Transitions 
Analysis Roadmapping. 
Finally, both Four Quadrants of Change from de Koning et al. (2021) and the Designing System Inter-
ventions tool from the Transition Design Framework (Carnegie Mellon University, 2024), introduce 
matrices for generating diverse, interconnected strategies that address issues from multiple angles 
across levels of agency and domains. These tools do not coordinate actions over long periods like 
roadmaps, but they expand the scope of action by providing a holistic, multi-scalar mapping of com-
plementary strategies contributing to similar objectives. 
G4.3 – Evaluating Transition Plans and Interventions 
The final subgroup assesses whether the proposed transition strategies align with expectations, fos-
ter a shared vision for long-term sustainability, outline alternative pathways, and establish clear 
plans to achieve key milestones and targets, to ensure stakeholder commitment. For example, 
McDowall (2012) introduces criteria for Technology Roadmap Evaluation that encourage reflection 
on whether policy roadmapping processes effectively address attributes like credibility, desirability, 
==PAGE:57==
56 
 
utility, and adaptability. Miedzinski et al. (2022) presents the Science and Technology Roadmap As-
sessment Framework, which systematically evaluates roadmap outcomes with a focus on evaluating 
the presence of roadmap features argued as critical sustainability transition policy instruments.  
Finally, Pahker et al. (2024) propose an adapted Transitions Delphi survey method to assess the de-
sirability of different strategies by gathering diverse expert knowledge on various interventions 
based on the MLP, evaluating feasibility, and reaching consensus among actors on the most desirable 
courses of action. 
4.1.3.5 C5: Mobilizing Actors, Executing Projects and Managing Experiments 
The fifth cluster focuses on evaluating existing transition efforts and developing strategies to mobi-
lize actors in experiments across diverse settings, enhancing learning outcomes through social learn-
ing and reflexivity, and embedding these results within broader strategies and policy frameworks. 
G5.1: Managing Radical Experiments and Fostering Social Learning 
This subgroup provides guidelines to improve the learning outcomes from radical niche experiments. 
Early contributions from SNM such as the Strategic Niche Management Workbook, compile lessons 
from past niche developments and provide design criteria for success at three levels: managing pro-
jects, forming niches, and facilitating regime shifts (Weber et al., 1999).  
Later works, like the Steering Transition Experiments Framework by Van Den Bosch & Rotmans 
(2008) provides guidelines for transition experiments, which address societal challenges and explore 
alternative pathways. The framework provides strategies for deepening context-specific learning, 
broadening experiments across settings, and scaling them into dominant ways of thinking. Similarly, 
the Design Framework for Social Learning Situations offers criteria for structuring experiments to 
maximize learning outcomes, focusing on creating conditions that support social learning and shared 
understanding within multi-actor networks (Bos et al., 2013).  
The Handbook for Scaling Up PSS integrates SNM with design theory, providing visual tools to man-
age niche processes. These tools help to align expectations, design series of experiments, mobilize 
networks, and monitor progress (Ceschin, 2012, 2014; Vezzoli et al., 2008). Finally, the Six Systemic 
Strengths by de Vicente (2016) serves as an ideation tool for brainstorming activities to reinforce 
niche processes, focusing on creating strategies for the shielding, nurturing, and empowering prop-
erties of the niche-networks involved in experiments. 
==PAGE:58==
57 
 
G5.2: Evaluating the Systemic Conditions for Experimentation 
This final subgroup evaluates the capacity of multi-actor networks in supporting ongoing experi-
ments. Building on the tools and guidelines from Subgroup 5.1, this reflexive governance cluster 
assesses experimental setups as a preliminary step to identify areas needing further attention, in 
order to create optimal conditions for experiments contributing to transitions objectives. 
For example, Transition Waves by de Vicente (2016) applies the concepts of deepening, broadening, 
and scaling from Van Den Bosch & Rotmans (2008) as reflection prompts for actors to address the 
strengths and weaknesses of their projects across six systemic elements of niche management. Tran-
sition Strategy: Niche Management Framework provides exercises for assessing initiatives through 
different phases of the transition process, helping niche actors evaluate progress, anticipate future 
niche-regime configurations, and identify further intervention (Manders et al., 2018). 
Finally, Bos & Brown (2014) offer a systematic self-assessment and monitoring tool for evaluating 
the Organisational Capacities for Transition Policy Programs (71). The tool helps assess the capac-
ity of organizational structures and processes that affect an organization’s ability to contribute to 
transition policy programs. It offers a benchmarking procedure to track progress and supports the 
development of adequate capacity-building strategies. 
4.1.4 Discussion and Reflections 
The five tool clusters - actor-oriented analysis, systems-oriented analysis, envisioning distant futures, 
aligning long-term visions with short-term actions, and mobilizing actors and managing experiments 
- connect to the core transition activities of TM. These tools encompass the iterative activities of 
understanding complex problems, planning strategies, and extracting lessons from experiments.  
Their commonality lies in their intention to bridge diverse perspectives and facilitate collaboration 
within multi-actor networks across different governance stages. Here, participatory and systems-
based modelling helps actors build a shared understanding, align on future visions, and explore mul-
tiple scenarios before opting for strategies. Often used in multidisciplinary contexts (e.g., Gaziulusoy 
& Ryan, 2017; Heinonen et al., 2017), these tools guide actors in co-creating robust understandings 
of problems and systems (e.g., Wilkerson & Trellevik, 2021) and co-developing desirable transition 
pathways (e.g., Gaziulusoy et al., 2013; Hyysalo et al., 2019). These forward-looking processes sup-
port dialogue, inform decision-making, and encourage creative thinking to translate diverse problem 
==PAGE:59==
58 
 
framings into strategic goals, tactical strategies and operational projects. They help actors navigate 
the uncertainties and tensions between competing pathways, reflecting the political nature and nor-
mative aspects of sustainability transition processes. 
While many tools are oriented towards strategy development, others prioritize monitoring and eval-
uating governance activities. These tools provide criteria, protocols and exercises for assessing policy 
instruments (e.g., Miedzinski et al., 2022), niche experiments (e.g., Manders et al., 2018), and insti-
tutional capacities for transition governance (e.g., Bos & Brown, 2014). Unlike the forward-looking 
tools, these reflexive tools focus on evaluating past actions, enabling practitioners to refine future 
strategies by incorporating lessons learned in their ongoing efforts. This duality of the tools highlight 
different facilitation approaches and roles in TM governance, which prompts a discussion on what 
necessarily characterizes the practitioners, who employ these tools. For instance, methods that help 
facilitate participative workshops require different practical skills than the reflexive methods which 
systematically evaluates the learnings from these workshops.  
On another note, when reviewing Table 2 - reflexive tools are underrepresented at the operational 
and tactical levels. This indicates a possible bias towards "active experimentation" and forward-look-
ing aspects like planning, and less attention for reflexive tools, focusing on monitoring and evaluating 
experiments. However, this imbalance may also result from the literature review’s keyword selec-
tion, which did not specifically target terms related to monitoring or evaluation. 
Finally, some tools serve multiple purposes, creating overlap in their classification. For instance, the 
systems-oriented modelling tools can both map challenges and simulate future scenarios, offering 
support in both problem structuring and tactical scenario planning. Similarly, causal-layered analysis, 
in this case used for scenario testing, is also valuable for structuring complex problems.  
Despite these overlaps, the classification provides a useful framework for understanding the distinct 
roles of tools and selecting appropriate ones to implement based on the governance stage a partic-
ular initiative currently operates in. However, future research should evaluate the various purposes 
that these tools can serve beyond the intended use described in the papers from which they were 
retrieved.

4.2.2 Findings: Multi-Level Perspective 
CEBW operates within Denmark's socio-technical waste system, addressing the growing complexity 
and volume of household and commercial waste in society. Initially shaped by consumption growth, 
urbanization and environmental awareness, the system has since evolved in response to several 
niche developments and shifting landscape pressures. The full MLP analysis is found in Appendix A. 
The study reviewed Hansen’s (2019) historical depiction of the Danish waste sector and covers the 
strategic shifts in its waste management practices over time, while also discussing tensions in recent 
media coverage. The screening of niche-regime-landscape interactions are shown in Figure 7. 
 
Figure 7 – Screening of niche-regime interactions preceding the existing state inspired by the Mapping the Evolution of Wicked 
Problems tool from the Transition Design Framework (Carnegie Mellon University, 2024). A larger version of the model can be 
found in Appendix A. 
To summarize the MLP study, the Danish waste system has evolved from private landfills to a decen-
tralized, publicly regulated municipal regime, dominated by recycling and waste-to-energy (WtE) in-
cineration, with reuse and waste prevention as niche practices in comparison. Although some waste 
fractions are gradually being privatized, local municipalities and public utility firms predominantly 
manage waste under national legislation, driven by public waste fees and overseen by the Danish 
==PAGE:64==
63 
 
Environmental Agency, with European targets currently providing the primary strategic direction. 
Recent media coverage reveals how Denmark’s waste system faces significant tensions between its 
established incineration practices, and the increasing EU-driven pressures to prioritize recycling, re-
use, and waste prevention. While these waste strategies all aim to reduce reliance on incineration, 
coordinating their pathways within a decentralized waste system remains complex. Challenges arise 
from local autonomy, strict environmental regulations, uncertainties surrounding the implications of 
waste sector liberalization, and ongoing political debates regarding the benefits of each approach.

while also delineating the future timeline of the project. The process is visualised in Figure 8.

Strategic processes 
When reviewing the strategic processes that preceded the project, many decisions were made in 
policy arenas beyond the direct influence of the project’s actors. Negotiations within the European 
Commission set a 2018 recycling target of 65%, followed by national policy processes that led to the 
Circular Economy Action Plan (Danish Ministry of Environment, 2020).  
With this as the framework, internal problem structuring and goal setting of CEBW involved national 
authorities and two regions, with the Central Region of Denmark taking the lead due to its experi-
ence in CE projects and EU administration, and because national authorities were unable to at the 
time (Contributor D). The two regions each launched a pre-project phase reaching out to local actors, 
including municipalities, utilities, and private recyclers, to identify relevant actors, and understand 
local agendas and needs (B). Open seminars were held to explain the challenge and possible pro-
jects. This was followed by interviews with potential partners to explore the themes these actors 
stressed as important and to identify synergies between national and local agendas (B).  
As a result, while the formal backbone of the project remains to implement the national action plan, 
the mobilization of actors leading up to the coalition’s formation also highlights elements of local 
autonomy and self-governance. Several contributors suggest that the later coalition was also influ-
enced by visions from local actors in regions, municipalities, and utilities. In particular, the project’s 
informal strategy has been to strengthen waste prevention efforts, emphasizing repair, reuse, and 
prevention. This approach aligns more closely with the waste hierarchy model and contrasts with 
the national action plan's focus, argued to primarily promote recycling (A, B, C, E). 
As Contributor B explains:  
"The strategy is inherent in the action plan, as the circular economy plan sets the broader objectives. 
However, there are also goals within the CEBW project that address the limited focus on prevention 
in Denmark, largely due to the dominant growth paradigm. This focus is less emphasized in the na-
tional action plan, so it's a focus area we've chosen to prioritize ourselves." (B) (Translated) 
Another strategic objective discussed during goal setting was inspired by past circularity projects and 
the need to strengthen the capacity to facilitate circular innovation in the public waste management 
sector. Challenges include fragmented efforts across jurisdictions, varying local ambitions, contex-
tual differences, and regulatory barriers that hinder adopting higher steps in the waste hierarchy (A, 
==PAGE:66==
65 
 
B, C). Consequently, a second goal to establish cross-municipal networks and build up capacity was 
incorporated into the tactical development of the project (B, E). 
Tactical processes 
The tactical phase of project coordination involved organizing thematic meetings, where local actors 
were grouped by ideas, potential synergies and waste fractions in focus. The main goal was to foster 
encourage them to collaborate to create their own projects aligned with the action plan's broader 
leverage points. Coordinating actors took a facilitating role, providing guidance and new ideas when 
needed (B). However, they also developed their own transversal projects focused on network for-
mation, capacity building, and institutional governance, and made the decisions on which projects 
to include in the project application (B, C). 
The process has been described as an iterative, fuzzy "ping-pong" exchange, where project descrip-
tions, following the EU-LIFE IP formula, have been refined through back-and-forth document collab-
oration between actors (C, E), while coordinating actors monitored that the projects met the neces-
sary application requirements (A). This process had to adhere to strict EU-LIFE IP procedures, to 
ensure that all leverage points from the national strategy was formally addressed (D).  
Altogether, this has led to the selection of a range of dispersed subprojects, varying by focus, geog-
raphy, and waste fraction. They are organized into clusters characterized by their position in the 
waste hierarchy. Each project has been divided into three phases: experimentation, replication, and 
dissemination, to ensure resources for each phase (A). Additionally, cross-cutting projects have been 
included, such as a network- and capacity building project (C1), described as the "motor" of the 
initiative (A). This project aims to create a shared learning environment (E) and support the subpro-
jects, as they progress through the eight years, most of them are planned to be operating (B, C, E). 
Operational process 
To conclude, many strategic and tactical negotiations were made prior to the project’s current state. 
CEBW has entered its operational stage, with local actors actively executing projects. The first phase, 
focuses on demonstrating solutions to problems identified during tactical planning, and is nearing 
completion for most initiatives. The project has shifted to a governance structure, where each pro-
ject and its participants are responsible for meeting their own goals, as outlined in the application, 
while the coordinating actors primarily monitor and provide support as complications arise. 
==PAGE:67==
66 
 
Particularly through iterative capacity-building efforts to address common needs emerging within 
each project phase. Presently, initiatives are moving towards a short reflexive phase, where inter-
mediary learnings are to be consolidated from the first phase. In the upcoming phases, projects are 
expected to further test and replicate solutions in different contexts, such as other municipal juris-
dictions, and share lessons learned in broader settings (A). 
4.2.4 Findings: Mission-Oriented Innovation System 
The third lens qualitatively assesses CEBW's functioning within a mission-oriented system (MIS) aim-
ing to minimize waste incineration. Although CEBW doesn’t cover the entire system, its complexity 
– involving 26 initiatives and 33 partners - makes it a valuable unit to understand the dynamics in 
the system. Enablers and hindrances to progress mentioned in interviews were mapped to the func-
tions of a MIS. Drawing upon sections 4.2.2 and 4.2.3, the mission framing focuses on waste preven-
tion and materials recirculation from direct reuse to recycling solutions. Figure 9 highlights the in-
volved actors, as public utilities, municipalities, and to a lesser extent knowledge institutions, NGOs, 
and social enterprises. The programmatic structure is led by regions, authorities, and an NGO, with 
institutional initiatives complementing three main clusters guided by the waste hierarchy. Steering 
and political committees connect the project to political systems and private actors, while a media 
team supports dissemination. The EU administration ensures documentation and compliance.  
The full overview of drivers and hindrances - internal and external to the project - are listed in Ap-
pendix A. This section presents the key findings from the functional analysis. 
Functional Analysis 
Despite CEBW's formal goal of implementing the action plan, most participants viewed the national 
strategy as unambitious, outdated, and ineffective in guiding local circularity efforts. They found the 
leverage points unclear or repetitive of previous strategies (A, B, C, E, G).  
While some municipalities pursue ambitious circularity objectives, others stick to national targets, 
often constrained by sunk investments in Waste-to-Energy (WtE) plants with long payback periods 
(A, G, H). One municipality has set higher recirculation targets (e.g., 100%), exceeding European 
goals (H), making the national targets appear more as a baseline for varying levels of circular ambi-
tion across local waste jurisdictions.

“In reality, the action plan was already unambitious when it came out. And the sub-projects know

This view coincides with a scepticism towards recycling. Some associate recycling with green growth 
focused on consumption and economic growth, which they find unsustainable (C, B). Others link it

most municipal waste fractions (A, G). In this regard, Contributor A notes a shift in orientation within

explore reuse and waste prevention strategies as alternatives. 
“… we need to talk more about prevention and try to challenge the projects that work with recycling.

happened, also in terms of how we approach this in the project” (A) (Translated) 
The divergence from the action plan and orientation towards higher levels of the waste hierarchy 
occurs within a decentralized waste system, where coordination across jurisdictions is limited (B, C, 
E). Contributor B describes municipalities as separate "kingdoms" with distinct cultures and prac-
tices, created by geographical silos that hinder cooperation and knowledge sharing. A recurring issue 
is the tendency to "reinvent the wheel", where solutions implemented in one jurisdiction tend to be 
recreated rather than replicated (A, B, C, F, G). This lack of coordination and awareness of related 
projects has, in some cases, allowed external developments to overtake past initiatives, limiting the 
learning potential and legitimacy of projects (A, B). 
In addition, limited public-private collaboration is also described to present challenges. Strict public 
tenders - due to legal concerns over competition (H, F) - and regulatory disputes over the point at 
which waste fractions transfer ownership (G, J) has created legal obstacles for some initiatives. One 
result has been that private recycling companies, initially involved in the CEBW partnership, left 
prematurely, leading to an underrepresentation of private actors in the project (A).

Another factor is the strict legislative framework and the priority for municipalities and local utilities 
to sustain existing waste services. These services are tied to politically determined public waste fees 
presenting legal obligations. Without resources allocated for innovation in these fees, municipalities 
and their utilities see this as a natural limitation on their ability to pursue innovative projects along-
side their regular operations (B, E, H).

“While they (ambitious employees within utilities) may want to pursue innovation projects (circular-

focused on duties, responsible for timely bin collection during a snowstorm.” (B) (Translated) 
This makes it difficult to justify pursuing circular initiatives, as limited resources and expertise - e.g., 
employees being novices with innovation practice, but excelling at operations - are available for in-

tributor C and J refers to a tendency to pursue "low-hanging fruits" and the enactment of incremen-
tal efforts, with an example from Contributor J prior to their enrolment in CEBW: 
“…When I came to Renosyd, the idea for the garden waste project was something like this: We would 
go home to one of us who was skilled in things like brushwood fences, compost, and beekeeping, 
invite 20 people over, and have some food, and that was basically the extent of what we could come 
up with.” (J) (Translated)

taining commitment to local circularity projects (H), this is more of an exception than norm. At the 
other end of the spectrum, innovation efforts are described as driven by motivated individuals who 
must persuade their management to back their initiatives, rather than these efforts being the result 
of local political strategies (B). For example, Contributor J describes how an internationally recog-

the challenge of executing cross-organizational innovation initiatives, and the limited experience 
among project managers in facilitating collaboration in these projects (B, E). 
A factor mentioned to help address capacity deficiencies among local municipalities and public util-
ities is the ability to attract funding, and the availability of external financial instruments, for example 
through the EU. These resources have been mentioned as critical to support more radical circularity 
initiatives (B, C, E, G). It has enabled projects that shift focus from recycling operations to upstream 
waste prevention, including investigations into behavioural change in households and cultural mind-
set shifts around waste (H, J). In this regard, although the action plan is criticized for lacking ambition 
towards these initiatives, it is also considered critical for strengthening their legitimacy within local 
municipalities and utilities. 
For instance, Contributor E explains: 
“This is where it becomes paradoxical, because the action plan may have served more as a tool for 
legitimacy with foundations or grantors than as a steering tool for us … And then comes the challenge 
afterwards of figuring out how to demonstrate that our projects were actually designed to imple-
ment the plan…” (E) (Translated) 
By establishing legitimacy at the EU level - particularly through financial programs like EU LIFE, which 
are only accessible through national action plans - it attracts external funding. This, in turn, is argued 
to create legitimacy, even for initiatives like waste prevention and capacity-building that aren’t di-
rectly tied to the action plan (E). Here, an argument is that securing competitive pan-European grants 
legitimizes circularity initiatives, as the funding supports not only circularity efforts but also other 
local political agendas, such as job creation, mobilizing greater financial capacity overall (C).

Identified Factors within the Internal Project Processes 
A closer examination of CEBW’s internal processes, supported by these grants, reveals several factors 
perceived to positively influence the ability to minimize the incineration of waste. This includes fos-
tering a sense among individuals of belonging to a larger movement (F, I), and the opportunity to 
build relationships with like-minded individuals and mentors from outside own organizations (H, J). 
This draw parallels to CEBW’s intent to strengthen networking and capacity-building activities across 
local waste jurisdictions for actors with circular ambitions (A, B, C, D). 
In addition to co-financing salaries, valuable resources include cross-project cluster meetings, webi-
nars, seminars, and workshops designed to facilitate knowledge sharing among project managers 
(B, D, E, H, J), social activities aimed at building mutual trust and strengthen information sharing 
across organizational divides (B, E), and the pooling of resources for joint upskilling efforts (A, B, E, 
F). Additionally, the prioritization of resources for one-on-one sparring sessions and the use of con-
sultants to support projects facing greater capacity deficiencies are stressed (A, B). The dedicated 
media team is also highlighted as important for raising awareness of results by channelling local 
project outputs through a shared platform with more traction (J, F).  
Furthermore, in terms of facilitating learning processes, the notion of utilizing bottom-up experi-
ments in local settings, to identify not only opportunities, but also institutional barriers, has been 
mentioned as a potential positive output from CEBW (B, C, G) - for instance, to inform wider policy 
arenas on regulatory barriers emerging in local circularity projects (A, C, E). There are, however, also 
instances of projects appearing more as investment projects than learning experiments (A, I), indi-
cating that this focus is not necessarily covered in all the outlined subprojects.  
When investigating the negative factors influencing CEBW, a frequent mention is the loss of 
knowledge when managers leave projects prematurely (A, B, E, H, F, I, J). For example, some project 
managers left prior to the execution phase, leaving limited knowledge on past decisions for subse-
quent managers, resulting in less motivation, knowledge of, and ownership over projects they did 
not help plan (E, F, H). This is further complicated by the rigidity and strict amendment procedures 
from the EU, making it difficult to alter project descriptions (A, D).

4.3 DS I - Comparative Discussion 
This section consolidates key insights from the literature review and empirical study. First, it com-
pares the TM approach with the current governance approach in CEBW, highlighting similarities, 
differences, and opportunities. Second, it validates the empirical findings with input from partici-
pants to pinpoint the most relevant needs for CEBW. Finally, it discusses the overall potential for 
implementing transition tools in CEBW, concluding the findings from DS-I. 
The section is primarily concerned with answering the following questions: 
• Q4: What methods and tools are most relevant to apply in CEBW?

4.3.1 Discussion of Transition Approach  
When comparing the case study findings with the tools identified in the literature, shown in Figure 
10, it becomes apparent that many tools focus on strategic and tactical processes, which are not 
within the scope of the current governance stage in CEBW. However, when contrasting the tools in 
the TM cycle with earlier project formation processes, similarities emerge, particularly in the coor-
dinators' efforts to identify needs and incorporate multiple perspectives before setting objectives.  
Strategic and Tactical Processes 
A similarity is the effort to identify and mobilize ambitious individuals within organizations to drive 
circular initiatives in public waste management and encourage them to collaborate to formulate 
their own initiatives. One difference is that, unlike the participatory methods in the literature, the 
coordinating actors opted for one-on-one interviews with individual actors early on. This differs from 
the collective participatory methods typically advocated for problem-structuring and goal setting in 
TM. These interviews may have allowed for more in-depth discussions and facilitated easier inter-
views. However, this approach may have led to a longer process, concentrating the understanding 
of different agendas in the hands of a few individuals responsible for translating a large amount of 
knowledge into a shared strategic direction on behalf of multiple actors.  
In contrast, a participatory process for problem-structuring, visioning, and backcasting might be 
challenging to organize, but it could offer an opportunity to mediate differing perspectives early on, 
integrate knowledge, and foster social learning about the agendas of local actors. However, the link 
between pre-decided leverage points in the national action plan and the goals of CEBW may reduce 
the relevance of TM tools supporting strategic processes. Nevertheless, given the presence of local 
ambitions, autonomy and self-governance, such tools may facilitate collaboration, help build rela-
tionships and mitigate the tendency to reinforce cultural silos across municipal divides. 
Operational Processes 
The operational stage of the project shows similarities to the Transition Experiments Framework 
from Van Den Bosch & Rotmans (2008). This framework provides guidelines to deepen experiments 
by maximizing learnings within each setting, broaden experiments by learning across multiple set-
tings, and scale up experiments by embedding them in dominant discourses and ways of thinking. 
==PAGE:77==
76 
 
 
Figure 10 - A visual overview of tools aligned with the current governance activities within CEBW 
 
==PAGE:78==
77 
 
Their guidelines align with the demonstration, replication, and dissemination phases of CEBW, which 
focus on identifying best practices, replicating them in multiple contexts and disseminating results 
in broader settings. The difference may be that the Transition Experiments Framework place a 
stronger emphasis on maximizing reflexive learning outputs, whereas CEBW project phases lean to-
wards maximizing practical innovation outputs. Nevertheless, one could argue that securing learning 
and achieving innovation inevitably are two processes that mutually influence each other, which 
reflects the iterative processes of learning-by-doing and doing-by-learning from TM. However, be-
cause some projects are perceived more as investments than experiments, certain components of 
CEBW lean more toward the practical side of innovation. Consequently, the project could have ben-
efited from TM tools to guide the design of its projects, to explicitly emphasize their dual role as 
both projects and experiments aiming to generate practical results, but also knowledge output. 
With that said, the project does incorporate reflexive monitoring and evaluation activities, with C1 
tracking for intermediate capacity needs and C2 monitoring for institutional enablers and barriers 
identified in local projects. This focus on assessing efforts to identify intermediate capacity needs 
and institutional barriers align with tools in G5.2, which provide tools to assess the systemic condi-
tions for experiments, as a prerequisite to improving these conditions. The difference may be how 
capacity needs and barriers are identified through interviews, which focus on creating a space where 
project managers can openly discuss their challenges in trust (B, C). In contrast, the methods in G5.2 
provide assessment procedures, exercises and checklists for actors to self-assess and collectively re-
flect on systemic factors contributing to challenges. These tools take a systemic, collaborative and 
systematic approach, while the approach in CEBW puts more emphasis on facilitating dialogue, 
building relations and empowering project managers to voice concerns through active listening (E).  
In this regard, mentions of limited resources allocated for reflection and systematic knowledge man-
agement highlight opportunities to improve monitoring and evaluation. Optimizing these processes 
could help ensure that valuable insights from local projects and CEBW, as a transition project, are 
systematically captured for easier replication and dissemination in later phases.

To conclude DS-I, participant validation was conducted to uncover the most relevant needs in CEBW. 
The session was conducted with input from the lead actors (Contributors A and B), who were as-

and challenges. This process helped validate findings and prioritize key factors for the project’s suc-
cess. Furthermore, by presenting the identified tools, it also helped determine relevant tools to in-
troduce. Figure 11 shows the results from an exercise, where Contributors A and B reviewed factors 
identified in Section 4.2.4. They prioritized the factors they found most important for achieving the 
overall goals and evaluated their perceived capacity deficiency in addressing these factors in CEBW.

A key discussion point from the session was that the responsibility for selecting and using TM tools 
now largely falls on individual project managers, rather than Contributor A and B, as the centralized 
project management currently only have a supporting role. Therefore, the introduction of TM tools 
requires the individual managers to be receptive to their use, and aware of their relevance: 
“What’s unique about working on a project like this, which is already defined, is that certain aspects 
are beyond our control. If one of the sub-projects needs to change direction, the initiative must come 
from them. They have to take the necessary steps. While we can provide input centrally, we depend 
on them to be open and receptive to our suggestions.” (A) (Translated) 
This challenge is further complicated by the insight that the views of subproject needs vary signifi-
cantly due to differences in professions, timelines, and their place-based context, making it difficult 
to determine when the use of a specific tool is relevant and sought for in a local context: 
“…Tools can help, but the tools needed vary greatly depending on the context of each project. It’s 
crucial to understand that no two situations are alike. All the projects we work on face different 
challenges, and their needs are unique.” (B) (Translated) 
However, both Contributor A and B pointed out the variation in capacities across local waste juris-
dictions and how well individual projects have been integrated into their respective organizations as 
a fundamental issue among many local projects. These projects require collaboration among multi-
ple actors within different local political environments, leading to varying levels of organizational 
commitment towards initiatives, which complicate their coordination. Contributors A and B stressed 
the differences in the support these projects receive from their multi-organizational settings. Con-
tributor B, for instance, noted the large variation in project integration across organizations:

“It varies greatly how well projects are integrated within organizations. In some cases, individual 
employees drive the projects… We've organized activities to manage complex projects in organiza-
tions that simply want them completed so employees can return to their regular duties. The depth of 
project integration varies greatly across organizations…” (B) (Translated) 
This sentiment was echoed by Contributor A, who emphasized the challenge of integrating circularity 
initiatives into the structure of public waste organizations and the need to develop strategies to ad-

“…The real challenge is transitioning from a project to something that becomes part of the organi-

mon shortfall in many places.” (A) (Translated) 
Consequently, to conclude the comparative discussion on how TM tools could be relevant to CEBW 
in its current stage, there is an opportunity to further strengthen the project’s existing monitoring 
and evaluation processes. These processes focus on empowering project managers to voice their 
challenges as a way to identify capacity needs and institutional barriers.  
A complementary approach would be to combine these efforts with the tools outlined in G5.2, which 
provide systematic methods for assessing the systemic conditions that influence local projects and 
their ability to contribute to broader transitions. 
These tools can help address the challenge of integrating local projects into institutional structures 
by offering systematic methods to monitor deficits in systemic conditions and assisting local project 
managers in articulating how these deficits impact their projects. This awareness serves as a crucial 
first step in developing strategies to address these systemic issues. Subsequent steps may involve 
using additional TM tools to further integrate circular practices into the institutional frameworks of 
local waste jurisdictions, tailored to the specific deficits identified in the local context. From a 
knowledge perspective, the tools from G5.2 also provide a way to systematize and validate the learn-
ing outcomes of CEBW, which is highlighted as a high-deficit factor in Figure 11, in addressing this 
challenge. Finally, from the perspective of transitions and design research, CEBW provides an oppor-
tunity to address an underrepresented area of transition management tools - specifically the moni-
toring and evaluation of projects and experiments. It allows for the elaboration of existing studies 
by applying assessment tools in real-world settings where they are needed.

4.4 Conclusion to DS - I 
The literature study systematically reviewed 71 tools that support governance activities in transition 
processes, classifying them into strategic, tactical, and operational clusters. These tools included 
participatory methods for problem-structuring, collectively envisioning desirable futures, and plan-
ning strategies and experiments. It also included reflexive tools for evaluating projects and experi-
ments and adjusting strategies based on their learning outcomes. The study highlighted the dual 
need for active experimentation (learning-by-doing) and reflexive evaluation (doing-by-learning) for 
efficient transition governance, while also noting an underrepresentation in reflexive tools for mon-
itoring and evaluating ongoing efforts. 
The empirical study applied three analytical frameworks - multi-level perspective, transition man-
agement, and mission-oriented innovation systems - to analyse the CEBW project. Through docu-
ment analysis it conceptualized the Danish waste system as a decentralized, yet publicly regulated 
municipal regime, with tensions between incineration, recycling, and waste prevention pathways. 
Through interviews it positioned CEBW in the operational stage of the TM cycle, focused on imple-
menting a national action plan, while also exploring higher levels of the waste hierarchy. Finally, the 
study identified hindrances, including fragmented municipal coordination, organisational commit-
ment and circular innovation capacity deficiencies, while also noting enablers regarding cross-juris-
dictional networks, local ambition, capacity-building and the ability to attract external grants. 
The comparative discussion compared CEBW’s strategic, tactical and operational processes with the 
tools identified in the literature, while also identifying three key clusters of opportunity: anticipating 
policy shifts and their legal implications, building up the organisational capacity to facilitate circular-
ity projects, and coordinating cross-project collaboration beyond municipal divides.  The relevance 
of applying systemic assessment tools, such as G5.2 from the literature review, was discussed, as 
these could help address gaps in CEBW’s ability to integrate circularity initiatives within local, mu-
nicipal structures. These tools offer systematic methods to assess the conditions for local projects to 
contribute to transitions, helping to identify strategies to strengthen the implementation of circular-
ity initiatives in local institutions, systematize learning outcomes from CEBW and elaborate on a set 
of reflexive tools currently underrepresented in transitions literature.

5.3.1 Iteration 1.1: Comparison and Selection of Method(s)  
First, the three tools from G5.2 were compared based on the design requirements to determine 
which one was most relevant for further development. The three tools are as following: 
Tool 1: Transition Waves 
The Transition Waves tool, part of the Visual Toolbox for Systems Innovation, is designed to help 
practitioners self-assess the strengths and weaknesses of their projects from a systems perspective 
while they are operating. Building on the Transition Experiments Framework by (Van Den Bosch & 
Rotmans, 2008) the tool divides projects into six systemic elements. These elements are then sys-
tematically evaluated across three contextual perspectives - deepening, broadening, and scaling up 
(as described in Sections 4.1.3.5 and 4.3.1). The tool is shown in Figure 12. 
To do this, it uses reflexive question prompts followed by fuzzy semi-quantitative assessments of a 
project's current performance (high, mid, or low). This serves as a preparatory step before develop-
ing strategies to address performance issues. For example, for the vision element, a deepening per-
spective question might be: "Do participants in the project share a long-term sustainability vision?". 
For the stakeholder element, a broadening perspective question could be: "Have you sought part-
ners outside your own network?" (de Vicente, 2016). 
==PAGE:88==

Figure 12 - Transition Waves (de Vicente, 2016) 
Tool 2: Transition Strategy: Niche Management Framework 
The Transition Strategy: Niche Management Framework, developed by Manders et al. (2018) pro-
vides five qualitative assessment exercises with systemic indicators for practitioners to evaluate their 
initiatives - ranging from experiments, pilot tests, to larger living labs. This framework is structured 
based on the multi-phase concept introduced in the MLP.  
The individual reflection exercises are related to transition phases like experimentation, niche for-
mation, niche growth, niche empowerment, and ultimately regime stabilization. For example, as-
sessment 3 (see Figure 13) asks practitioners to assess ongoing niche growth - the transition from 
clusters of initiatives to more established fields and learning communities - using indicators from 
strategic niche management, including network formation, learning processes, and expectations 
alignment. Other exercises focuses on examining the characteristics of experiments (1), opportuni-
ties for clustering (2), possible fit-conform and stretch-transform strategies (4) and current degree 
of regime institutionalization of the experiments (5).

The three tools discussed all aim to help practitioners to self-assess ongoing efforts and indicate 
areas of improvement before creating strategies to either reinforce niche processes or build up the 
organizational capacities to contribute to broader transition programs. The two different framings 
comes from the two transition frameworks they are primarily based on: Strategic Niche Manage-
ment (de Vicente, 2016; Manders et al., 2018) and Transition Management (Bos & Brown, 2014), 
which depart from experimental bottom-up and strategic top-down processes, respectively. 
Both Transition Waves and Transition Strategy use systemic indicators to evaluate project experi-
ments, but their focus differs. Transition Waves (TW) emphasizes on "transition experiments," while 
Transition Strategy (TS) contextualise niche development using the "multi-phase" concept from the 
Multi-Level Perspective (MLP). Both tools focus on niche dynamics and encourages practitioners to 
assess how existing experiments might cluster, develop, scale, and can be integrated within socio-
technical systems. On the other hand, Organizational Capacity Assessments (OCA) examines how 
novel sustainability practices become embedded within organizational structures. It helps practition-
ers evaluate how attributes such as strategies, skills, culture, and resources in organizations, are cur-
rently in place to support the novel practices they seek to introduce. 
When comparing the tools with the design requirements, as shown in Table 7, OCA is more tailored 
towards monitoring than TS and TW given the emphasis on tracking progress of capacity building 
strategies over time. All tools provide means for self-reflexiveness through interactive exercises, fo-
cus groups and self-evaluation prompts (e.g. questions or forms). In this setting, OCA puts more 
emphasis on systematizing and analysing the data from these reflexive sessions for wider knowledge 
dissemination. However, TW and TS can be used by multiple types of actors compared to OCA, which 
is tailored towards one type of actor (municipality). TW and TS also use systemic indicators at the 
level of institutions/regimes based on transition theories, while OCA considers attributes at the level 
of individual organizations. On the other hand, OCA takes are more place-based approach, bench-
marking different local municipalities to each other. Finally, all tools focus on empowering niche ac-
tors working with novel practices through either group exercises or focus groups to voice needs and 
challenges. However, OCA and Transition Waves specifically puts emphasis on discussing areas with 
low performance, as a diagnostic feature. None of the tools directly prescribe subsequent action, 
but they all focus on identifying areas of intervention, as a preliminary exercise.

5.2.2 Iteration 1.1: Literature Snowballing for Recent Elaborations 
Before developing the first prototype, a brief review was conducted to assess whether recent re-
search could provide better insights into how multiple local actors (e.g. within a municipality) con-
tribute to transitions. The review searched for broader and more systemic perspectives on local 
place-based capacities rather than focusing solely on individual organizations, as discussed in Sec-
tion 5.2.1. This review used the snowball approach (Wohlin, 2014), which was also applied in the 
literature review in section 4.1. It primarily involved screening papers that cited Bos & Brown (2014) 
retrieving two additional papers. These papers referred to the same framework, which introduced 
the concept of "urban transformative capacity", providing a means to assess the ability of local urban 
environments to facilitate radical change (Brodnik & Brown, 2018; Wolfram, 2016).  
As a place-based and issue-driven concept, Wolfram (2016) defines the concept in this way: 
“…the collective ability of the stakeholders involved in urban development to conceive of, prepare 
for, initiate and perform path-deviant change towards sustainability within and across multiple 
complex systems that constitute the cities they relate to.” 
The framework consists of 10 interdependent components of Urban Transformative Capacity (UTC), 
suggesting a total of 60 qualitative capacity factors for conducting path-deviating change to address 
a sustainability issue in a local urban environment. The components distinguish between compo-
nents related to forms of agency and interaction, types of development processes, as well as two 
relational scales between these components. The individual components are shown in Figure 15.  
Within this line of research, recent contributions seeks to operationalize the framework to develop 
collective strategies for improving urban transformative capacities in cities. For example, Brodnik & 
Brown (2018) uses the framework to characterize strategies for building urban transformative ca-
pacity across three institutional phases (introduction, diffusion, establishment), based on how well 
the UTC factors have integrated into local institutions.  
Additionally, when looking at recent elaborations from Wolfram (2019), the framework has been 
applied to qualitatively assess the transformative capacity of different local urban environments 
ranging from larger towns to metropolitan cities, such as Seoul in South Korea, from the perspective 
of different actors (e.g. practitioners, researchers). 
==PAGE:94==
93 
 
In this regard,  Wolfram et al. (2019) calls for the adoption of novel techniques for transdisciplinary 
self-assessments among urban actors that facilitate collective learning and help establish baselines 
for transformative capacity building within and across the organizations, who aim to facilitate change 
in a local urban environment (Wolfram, 2019, pp. 444–445).

Discussion of Relevance 
When analysing the UTC factors in Wolfram (2016) there are parallels to the factors identified in the 
empirical study, as well as the functions identified in the literature review.  
For instance, when examining the agency-oriented factors (C1, C2, C3), one factor focuses on how 
well different actors are included in local governance activities, such as whether civil society and the 
private sector are engaged with governmental actors in the deliberation of action. When considering 
the factors from DS-I, the issues with underrepresentation of private actors and limited public-pri-
vate collaboration is tied to this factor. It also relates to the tool focused on balancing representation 
in strategic processes (G1.3). Another factor includes whether governance structures help build so-
cial and political capital. This relates to CEBW's efforts to build trust across organizations. It also ties 
to the lack of political support in some municipalities.  
 
==PAGE:95==
94 
 
On the process-oriented side (C4, C5, C6, C7), factors include whether there is an explicit future vi-
sion, and whether it is motivating. This draw parallels to some municipalities developing long-term 
visions, compared to those following national strategies finding less local ambition. It also links to 
the practice of "envisioning" in TM and the tools to facilitate collective envisioning (G3.1). There are 
also factors considering whether plans are in place for reaching future goals, whether there is diverse 
experimentation, and whether these experiments are coupled to form systemic alternatives. This 
ties into the tools discussed in the literature, which focus on developing scenarios (G3.1, G4.1) and 
coordinating (G4.2) and designing experiments (G5.1). It also relates to the tendency to “reinvent 
the wheel” mentioned in CEBW. There are also factors that focus on reflexivity (C8) and monitoring 
to track progress and ensure local actors have the skills to evaluate and adjust their actions. This 
relates to G5.2, and the tool being developed in this thesis. Finally, the relational dimensions (C9, 
C10) links the above factors, based on how capacity building activities connect across scale and dif-
ferent forms of agency. 
In conclusion, many elements of the UTC framework align with the MIS factors identified in the em-
pirical study and the tool functions from the literature review. The framework acts as a bridge be-
tween potential capacity gaps and, to some extent, the TM tools that can help address them. More-
over, the framework considers local urban environments as a whole, rather than focusing on indi-
vidual organizations and their specific attributes. This systemic, transdisciplinary approach helps to 
better to meet the design requirements that OCA has not yet fulfilled regarding the use of systemic 
indicators and representation of multiple actors. 
Therefore, as part of tailoring the Organizational Capacity Assessment (OCA) method, the Urban 
Transformative Capacity (UTC) framework has replaced the original organizational attributes with 
capacity factors related to local urban transformative capacity.  
This approach reflects the idea that capacity building occurs both within and across organizations. It 
emphasizes the importance of assessing local urban capacities through diverse perspectives. And it 
applies systemic indicators to monitor and evaluate the collective ability in these environments to 
contribute to national strategies and transition projects, such as CEBW.

5.2.3 Iteration 1.1: Urban Transformative Capacity Assessment Procedure 
Building on the discussions in Sections 5.2.1 and 5.2.2, the Urban Transformative Capacity Assess-
ment Procedure has been developed. Adapted from the OCA framework, this tool uses UTC factors 
to guide actors through reflexive self-assessments considering local urban transformative capacities, 
evaluating the impact on their ability to contribute to transitions. 
Iteration 1.1 included two key activities: an initial self-evaluation form and follow-up session with a 
facilitator ‘experienced’ in transition theories. These activities are designed to help local actors as-
sess the transformative capacities based on existing project experiences, linking them to systemic 
factors to highlight any gaps or weaknesses. The insights from this tool intend to guide capacity 
building and institutional governance initiatives in transition projects like CEBW, introducing transi-
tion theories to help evaluate local project experiences and inform local capacity building. 
Self-evaluation Form 
A key element of the procedure is the self-assessment form, which was distributed to practitioners

•

•

5.2.4 Iteration 1.2 Refinement: Follow-up session – Factor Cards and Scoping Model 
In all three tests, participants completed the same assessment form, but the follow-up session for-
mat was adjusted iteratively based on observations and feedback. 
The test with Partner 1 provided the following insights for further refinement: 
• Participants found it difficult to recall the specific questions and reasoning behind their an-
swers in the form a week later. The spider chart did not effectively trigger past reflections, 
and reviewing all the bar charts was time-consuming. 
• The form's questions were complex and nuanced, leading to confusion as participants felt 
they could be interpreted in multiple ways, when the scope was not fully understood. 
• Facilitating reflection was difficult without aid. Asking for general feedback on the form often 
led to comments about the form's usability rather than discussions on how participants' ex-
periences connected to UTC factors. It was also challenging to explain theoretical concepts 
without clear definitions or examples readily available as a facilitator. 
• There were too many factors to discuss, and more time was spent revisiting results and clar-
ifying concepts than exploring participants' experiences with the indicators, which left them 
feeling unheard. 
To address the reflection barriers identified in the first test, the follow-up session format was revised. 
Although the time gap between completing the form and the follow-up session could not be 
changed, "Factor Cards" with simplified descriptions of the UTC factors were introduced to help par-
ticipants quickly revisit each factor. Initially presented as post its in MIRO, a graphically revised ver-
sion of the cards is shown in Figure 17. Additionally, a visual representation of the scope - the local 
urban environment - was created to clarify that focus was not on project setups or organizations, 
but on their relationship to the local urban environment they were part of. This ‘Scoping Model’ is 
shown in Figure 18. Finally - during the follow-up session, less time was prioritized for general re-
flections with the Spider Chart, and only high and low factors in the Bar Charts were examined. 
Instead, as a follow-up exercise, participants were asked to read through the factor cards, while con-
sidering current challenges and opportunities, and then select the 3 to 5 factors they found most 
relevant for further discussion with the facilitator.

5.2.5 Iteration 1.3 Refinement: Follow-up session – Effect-Cause Mapping 
After applying revisions from iteration 1.2, testing with Partner 2 revealed new insights: 
• 
The time for reviewing the factor cards was too short - more time was needed for participants 
to recall the UTC factors properly. 
• 
While the follow-up session prompted extensive reflections on current experiences and chal-
lenges, discussions often went off-topic, making it difficult for the facilitator to follow the 
shifts from one factor to another. 
• 
The participant didn’t always specify which factor they were discussing, which complicated 
the post-session process of connecting experiences to specific capacity factors. 
In response, an exercise was developed to help guide the reflection of participants regarding the 
UTC factors. This exercise led participants through a structured process, asking them to explain why 
certain factor cards were important, exploring their choices in more depth. It was inspired by the 
cause-effect tools from G2.1 that focus on systematically uncovering issues by investigating the un-
derlying causes behind their visible consequences (or effects). 
==PAGE:103==
102 
 
The step-by-step process guided the facilitator and participants through the following activities: 
1. Review the UTC factors by reading through the factor cards and discard those that are not 
relevant after the initial review (the facilitator disengages the session) 
2. From the remaining cards, select the 3 to 5 UTC factors that are most relevant to discuss, 
focusing on both challenges and opportunities (the facilitator reengages) 
3. Place one of the selected factor cards at the top of the Effect-Cause Map, as shown in  
Figure 19. 
4. Discuss the immediate cause (the specific challenge or opportunity) that made the factor 
card important and write it below the card. 
5. Identify and add one or more underlying causes related to this immediate cause in the sub-
sequent sections of the model, using arrows to show connections. 
6. Repeat steps 3 to 5 for each of the 3 to 5 selected factor cards. 
The exercise was introduced with Partner 3 to assess if it would help participants clearly explain why 
they considered certain factor cards relevant and to visually connect these choices to their underly-
ing causes. It is intentionally named "Effect-Cause Mapping" due to its focus on starting with the 
broader capacities (UTC factors) and then narrowing down to context-specific challenges and their 
underlying causes, ultimately contributing to these capacity deficits. The goal was to test whether 
this structured approach would lead to clearer reasoning and encourage deeper reflection on the 
local dynamics at play influencing the chosen UTC factors. 
5.3 Current Version of the Tool 
To conclude the PS, the final iteration consolidates insights from previous tests and an expert review 
(see Section 6.2.1 for elaboration). Combined with feedback from Partners 1 and 2, the following 
key observations and suggestions were considered for the final iteration: 
• Framing the procedure solely as a way to identify capacity deficits, without linking it to the 
goal of accelerating sustainability transitions, may unintentionally disempower local actors 
and give the impression that their existing efforts are inadequate.

5.4 Conclusion to PS 
The PS developed and refined a procedure to help local actors assess how their project experiences 
relate to the transformative capacity of their local urban environment. It elaborates on an analytical 
tool to monitor and evaluate organizational capacities for transition policy programs. By combining 
it with the Urban Transformative Capacity Framework (UTC) the procedure helps local actors con-
sider how institutional structures, extending beyond individual organizations, influence their ability 
to contribute to transition objectives. Using an iterative prototyping approach, the procedure, in-
cluding activities such as self-assessment forms, follow-up sessions, and structured exercises were 
tested and refined with local actors contributing to a national transition project. The final iteration 
of the tool introduces a workshop format designed to engage a diverse group of actors in assessing 
and collectively reflecting on the transformative capacity of their local urban environment, serving 
as a first step toward developing local transformative capacity-building strategies.

6.1.1 Purpose of Study 
The goal of this evaluation study is to assess the design support, identify areas for improvement, and 
validate the tool’s usefulness and usability for its target users. The hypothesis is that an Urban Trans-
formative Capacity Assessment Procedure will offer a structured process to help practitioners reflect 
==PAGE:111==

local urban environments. The approach intends monitor for deficits and their improvements and

6.2.1 Observations and Facilitator Self-Evaluation 
Although participants completed the form independently, observation and facilitation was possible 
in the follow-up sessions. All participants, and especially Contributors K, struggled to recall responses 
a week later, when relying solely on the facilitator and visual aids like the spider- and bar charts. 
Initially, reflection prompts unintentionally shifted focus to issues like understanding theoretical con-
cepts, language barriers, and form usability rather than the UTC factors. However, factor-specific 
tasks and interactive exercises helped guide dialogue and reflection in later iterations, refocusing 
attention on capacity-building factors instead of tool usability.  
For instance, in the Effect-Cause Mapping exercise Participant M identified both vertical and hori-

the facilitator, sometimes resulting in intricate explanations. Allowing participants to review simpli-

the facilitator an opportunity to better prepare tasks and exercises. Finally, having two participants 
encouraged debate and differing perspectives, while one-on-one interviews created a more inti-
mate, almost therapeutic setting that allowed for more personal and vulnerable reflections.

Two other themes emerged around the need for clarity in the procedure’s purpose and scope, em-

6.2.1 Expert Review 
As a final evaluation, key insights on language barriers, collective reflection and dialogue, and pur-
pose and scoping were presented to the external expert N. The aim was to gather expert feedback 
and discuss their experience in developing similar self-assessment procedures.  
In this regard, N has co-developed an interactive learning platform for companies to assess their 
circularity readiness. This step-by-step process enables companies to self-assess their current CE 
readiness levels across the value chain, discuss identified strengths and weaknesses, develop tar-
geted action plans to address gaps and seize opportunities, and later on monitor for changes in read-
iness from these actions. 
The key insights and suggestions from the external review included: 
• Language barriers: To balance theoretical soundness with clarity, assessment questions were 
systematically revised by research experts and then refined through feedback from target-
group allies (e.g., industry experts). For terms identified as particularly challenging, defini-
tions, glossaries, and examples were developed and tested for better understanding. 
• Dialogue and collective reflection: N recommended one-day workshops to support the self-
assessment process, with facilitators introducing its purpose and content, clarifying theoret-
ical aspects, and guiding dialogue in topic prioritization and action planning. A side-effect is 
that participants find mutual support and networking opportunities across organizations. 
• Purpose and scoping:  N’s procedure addressed scoping challenges by prompting participants 
to define their assessment scope at the outset, and "strengths and weaknesses" phrasing 
was used to avoid negative connotations associated with readiness deficits. 
Following Contributor M's reflections on the usefulness of UTC factors in facilitating multi-actor dia-
logue, N also confirmed that the readiness indicators had helped structure discussions within 
==PAGE:119==
118 
 
companies by providing a reliable, scientifically validated baseline for discussing next steps. Addi-
tionally, N described how their platform allowed users to browse tailored tools, such as MECO tables 
and circular strategy scanners, to inspire further action. It also provided cross-organizational bench-
marking, similar to the OCA framework, and included measures to assess potential biases in self-
assessments by analysing the diversity of disciplines involved. Contributor L emphasized this feature 
as essential for ensuring scientific validity. 
6.3 Discussion and Outlook 
The initial evaluation of the Urban Transformative Capacity Assessment Procedure (DS-II) shows 
promising potential for promoting practitioner self-reflection and awareness of how current project 
experiences relate to urban transformative capacity factors. Both Contributors L and M expressed 
interest in additional testing for further refinement. However, the evaluation also highlights key ar-
eas for improvement. 
This includes further testing activities related to: 
• 
Refining the assessment form and factor cards to improve accessibility of UTC terminology by 
revisiting questions and reflexive prompts and incorporating practical examples, visualisa-
tions, definitions, glossaries, and an introductory theoretical session before self-assessment. 
This aims to test whether these changes can help practitioners better understand the terms, 
which are currently perceived as complex and distanced from everyday practices. 
• 
Implementing the procedure in group settings with multiple actors to increase representa-
tiveness of the forms and to introduce transdisciplinary discussion, testing the procedure’s 
effectiveness in fostering collective reflection, bridging diverse perspectives, and creating a 
shared understanding of viable capacity-building strategies. A proposed workshop format, 
described in Section 5.3, has been developed to facilitate this. 
• 
Clarifying the purpose statement and introducing deliberate scoping activities to determine 
if these adjustments can reduce negative connotations on capacity building and better scope 
the self-assessment process in future iterations through scoping exercises. 
It is also important to note that the initial test of UTC factors included only 38 of the 60 total factors, 
leaving room for further testing to evaluate the complete framework. Furthermore, UTC is an 
==PAGE:120==
119 
 
emerging scientific field within sustainability transitions research and there are examples of recent 
authors, who has further adapted the UTC factors (Glaas et al., 2019). A more comprehensive liter-
ature study on the field, could be conducted to consolidate the final selection of relevant factors. 
Additionally, the rating tool (see Figure 14) used by Bos & Brown (2014) and adapted by Brodnik & 
Brown (2018) offers a ‘level of institutional integration’-oriented rating scale for UTC factors in the 
self-assessment form. Future iterations could test the implementation of this scale in comparison to 
the used Likert-type scale. It could start with a few factors before tailoring a scale for all factors. 
Testing the rating tool, could determine if it helps in defining the scope of assessment and provides 
more representative results for benchmarking across local urban environments. 
Finally, as Contributors K argued, the self-assessment procedure requires follow-up activities to 
guide further action, and it cannot inform next steps on its own. Building on Contributor M’s sugges-
tion to collaboratively prioritize the most important factors to address and drawing on Contributor 
N’s step-by-step self-assessment process, an area for improvement in the procedure could be to test 
the self-assessment procedure in a broader format of activities. This would involve adding follow-up 
exercises focused on identifying most viable UTC factors to address, browsing relevant tools for in-
spiration (e.g. TM tools), and guided co-creation of local capacity building strategies.

7.1 Discussion of Research Goals 
This thesis set out to examine how transition theories can support transition projects, exploring the 
role designers play in applying these theories in the projects through practical design support, to 
strengthen their contributions towards transition objectives. It seeks to contribute to the emerging 
field of Design for Sustainability Transitions, aligning with recent discussions on the evolving role of 
designers in driving forward sustainability transition initiatives. Gaziulusoy & Ryan (2017a) first dis-
cussed this topic by mapping the specific roles of designers in a sustainability transitions project to 
three governance stages in TM - strategic, tactical, and operational - originally outlined by Loorbach 
(2010). They argue that designers have multiple interdisciplinary roles, related to the participatory 
inquiry, process and output of these stages, shown in Figure 20. 
 
Figure 20 – Listed transition activities and the specific roles of designers mapped to each governance stage - strategic, tactical, and 
operational - as discussed by Gaziulusoy & Ryan (2017a) in a case study on what they refer to as a sustainability transition project, 
which developed collective visions and pathways towards low-carbon futures for Australian cities in 2040. 
 
==PAGE:126==
125 
 
Most recent, Von Flittner et al. (2022) discuss three case studies where sustainability transition 
methods and theories were introduced in the early stages of transition projects to mobilize actors. 
In line with Gaziulusoy & Ryan (2017a), they argue designers can facilitate participatory inquiry, 
visually communicate alternative futures, formulate pre-negotiated scenarios, conduct human-cen-
tred research, support active experimentation, and navigate uncertainty and complexity. Von 
Flittner et al. (2022) also argue that designers' ability to adapt theories and methods into practical, 
designerly tools makes transitions research more accessible to a wide range of practitioners - in-
cluding innovators, businesses, and policymakers - creating shared entry points into complex tran-
sition processes helping to engage all relevant actors (Von Flittner et al., 2022, p. 176) 
A comparison of the case studies and this thesis project reveals similarities in how both investigate 
the practical adaptation and application of transition theories within transition projects. However, 
there are differences in the specific transition activities examined and the roles the designers play. 
For instance, both Gaziulusoy & Ryan (2017a) and Von Flittner et al. (2022) focus on the early, con-
ceptual stages of transition projects - emphasizing problem-structuring, network formation, collec-
tive envisioning, and scenario planning. This thesis examines transition projects in operation, with 
practitioners already executing planned projects and experiments. As a result, the transition activi-
ties requiring support, the designer's role in providing that support, and the types of designerly tools 
developed to facilitate it have all varied. For instance, the intention in this project has been to sup-
port reflexive activities. In this setting, the designer's role has centred on facilitating learning and 
reflection to improve practitioners' self-awareness on how institutional structures, extending be-
yond individual organizations, influence their ability to contribute to transition objectives. 
This approach led to the development of a tool introducing transition theories through structured 
self-assessment and facilitated evaluation sessions, rather than through forward-looking processes 
focused on active experimentation. Therefore, this thesis emphasizes the "doing-by-learning" over 
"learning-by-doing" within the TM cycle. These reflexive activities are not represented in Figure 20, 
nor covered in the case studies by Gaziulusoy & Ryan (2017a) and Von Flittner et al. (2022). In this 
regard, its findings suggest that the designer's role extends beyond strategic, tactical, and opera-
tional activities to also include enabling reflexive activities for monitoring and self-evaluation. These 
activities are important as they legitimize the strategic and tactical processes, they originate from, 
increasing the likelihood of follow-up transition projects emerging again in the future.

7.2 Discussion on Learning Objectives 
This section briefly discusses whether the learning objectives have been met. 
1. Explain the origin and the key characteristics of sustainability transitions as a scientific 
research field 
This learning objective was addressed in Chapter 3, where the origins of the research field were 
discussed to define key characteristics. This included an initial focus on technological regime 
shifts, niches and landscape pressures, and the introduction of the multi-level perspective and 
multi-phase concept. Ultimately, sustainability transitions were defined as non-linear, multi-di-
mensional, co-evolutionary processes that engage a diverse range of actors in pursuing long-
term normative sustainability goals. 
2. Characterize transition processes through the most commonly applied sustainability 
transitions frameworks 
This learning objective elaborated on the first objective, discussing how multiple analytical 
frameworks can be used to frame how transitions come about. More specifically, the Multi-Level 
Perspective (MLP) was compared alongside other analytical frameworks, such as Strategic Niche 
Management (SNM), Transition Management (TM) and the Mission-Oriented Innovation System 
framework (MIS) adapted from Technological Innovation Systems (TIS). The characteristics de-
scribed from each framework have been gathered in Chapter 3 (RC). 
3. Utilize transition theories to analyze relevant aspects of an existing transition project 
contributing to one or more trajectories of change towards sustainability 
The analytical frameworks introduced in Chapter 3 (RC) were applied in Chapter 4 (DS-I) in an 
empirical study analysing the CEBW as a transition project. This analysis involved briefly examin-
ing the transition process it contributes to using the MLP to identify transition pathways, such as 
recycling and waste prevention. The TM framework was used to assess past and present govern-
ance activities, while the Mission-Oriented Innovation System MIS framework helped identify in-
ternal and external factors - both positive and negative - affecting the project's progress. 
 
==PAGE:128==

4. Compare relevant methods and tools to manage transition processes and assess their 
relevance within a given transition project 
This objective was met by conducting a literature review in Chapter 4 (DS-I) alongside the empir-
ical study to identify tools related to the analytical frameworks discussed in Chapter 3 (RC). These 
tools were classified according to the governance stages outlined in TM. The review also identi-
fied functional themes within these tools, which were later explored in a comparative discussion, 
linking them to governance stages and clusters of opportunities identified in the CEBW project. 
5. Utilize one or more methods and tools to develop a design support that strengthens the 
contribution of a transition project 
This learning objective was met through an investigation of tools from the literature review in 
Chapter 5 (PS). A specific group (G5.2) was selected and analysed to identify features relevant to

Assessment Procedure, providing design support aiming to strengthen CEBW’s contribution to 
broader transition objectives through reflexive governance. 
6. Introduce a design support in one or more areas of a transition project in practice 
This learning objective was met by introducing the UTC Assessment together with three different 
partners in CEBW, who are actively contributing towards transitioning local waste systems to-
wards circularity. It was introduced with practitioners from utility firms and an NGO, who used it 
to reflect on existing efforts, connecting their experiences to the broader UTC factors. This proto-

7. Evaluate the usefulness of the design support with their applicants in a transition project 
This learning objective was covered in Chapter 6 (DS-II), where the developed UTC Assessment 
Procedure was evaluated using mixed empirical methods during prototyping sessions. Although 
this evaluation showed promising results, it also identified three areas needing improvement, 
which were critical in order to properly validate the usefulness of the procedure in the future.

7.3 Conclusion 
Supporting the transition to a sustainable and just future has never been more important. This thesis 
aimed to contribute to the rapidly growing field of research focused on understanding how sustain-
ability transitions can occur. Drawing on recent research in Design for Sustainability Transitions, the 
thesis examined the extent to which sustainability transition projects can be guided by transition 
theories through practical design support to strengthen their respective contributions.  
As a core result, an Urban Transformative Capacity Assessment Procedure has been developed and 
introduced within an existing transition project supporting the shift to a circular waste management 
system in Denmark. This design support aims to assist practitioners in transition projects by helping 
them monitor and facilitate reflexive self-awareness considering their collective ability to contribute 
to broader transition objectives. Based on an initial evaluation of the procedure, evidence suggests 
that this tool can help practitioners articulate how difficulties in their local projects relate to the 
broader transformative capacity of their local urban environment. This awareness serves as a first 
step towards building up the local capacity to contribute to sustainability transitions.  
Finally, the findings of this thesis suggest that the designer's role in transitions goes beyond strategic, 
tactical, and operational tasks to include the design and facilitation of reflexive activities. These ac-
tivities foster collective awareness and help monitor and evaluate the impact of transition projects 
within their local place-based settings. It helps legitimize the extensive strategic and tactical pro-
cesses from which these large-scale projects originate and are therefore important to consider for 
similar projects to emerge in the future. 
7.3.1 Summary 
This thesis followed the Design Research Methodology, which introduces four distinct stages of de-
sign research. This considers the Research Clarification (RC), where the research topic was outlined; 
Descriptive Study I (DS-I), which involved examining the current situation; the Prescriptive Study 
(PS), dedicated to developing and implementing practical design support; and Descriptive Study II 
(DS-II), which assessed how effectively the design support met its intended objectives. 
Considering a transition project in the Danish public waste sector, the goal of the thesis was to: 
1. Examine to what extent the existing transition approach in CEBW can be supported by tran-
sition theories, as well as practical methods and tools connected to these theories 
==PAGE:131==
130 
 
2. Develop a practical design support for transition projects, such as CEBW, and provide tangible 
recommendations to enhance the overall impact of the project going forward 
During RC, the thesis started out by identifying and comparing four common analytical frameworks 
within the scientific field of sustainability transitions to understand the characteristics of transition 
processes. This included the Multi-Level Perspective (MLP), Strategic Niche Management (SNM), 
Transition Management (TM) and Mission-Oriented Innovation Systems (MIS), which refers to a re-
cent adaptation of the more common framework Technological Innovation Systems (TIS). While 
these frameworks share areas of overlap, they were found to complement each other, making them 
suitable for defining keywords for a literature study and for empirically analysing different aspects 
of a particular transition project and its broader transition context. 
DS-I began with a systematic literature review that identified and classified a total of 71 tools argued 
as relevant for supporting transition governance activities at the strategic, tactical, and operational 
levels of TM. These tools included participatory methods for problem structuring, envisioning desir-
able futures, planning strategies and designing experiments. The review also identified reflexive 
tools for evaluating ongoing projects, to guide the adjustment of projects and strategies based on 
intermediate learning outcomes. The literature study highlighted the dual need for active experi-
mentation (learning-by-doing) and reflexive evaluation (doing-by-learning) in transition manage-
ment. In this regard, it identified an underrepresentation of tools for reflexive governance, which 
supports the monitoring and continuous evaluation of projects and experiments. 
In parallel with the literature review, an empirical study applied the MLP, TM and MIS frameworks 
to analyse the EU-LIFE Integrated Project, Circular Economy Beyond Waste (CEBW), as a transition 
project contributing to the transition of a socio-technical waste system. The MLP analysis identified 
tensions among three circularity pathways - focused on incineration, recycling, and waste prevention 
- within a decentralized, publicly regulated waste management regime gradually shifting from mu-
nicipal control to partial privatization. In this context, the consortium of local actors in CEBW cur-
rently operates at the operational level of TM – and although its strategic goals formally focus on 
implementing a national strategy promoting recycling, it was found that it informally follows a waste 
hierarchy promoting waste minimization, demonstrating signs of self-governance and autonomy 
within the broader transition. 
==PAGE:132==
131 
 
When examining the transition project as a MIS, the study identified several hindrances, including 
limited directionality from the national strategy, fragmented municipal coordination, issues with or-
ganizational and political commitment, and local capacity deficiencies for circular innovation initia-
tives, especially for waste minimization. However, it also noted enablers such as cross-municipal 
learning networks, dedicated capacity-building, presence of local ambition and a developed local 
ability to attract pan-European grants, pushing the transition project forward. 
A comparative discussion examined CEBW’s transition governance approach alongside the tools 
identified in the literature review, identifying three clusters of opportunities for practical tool intro-
duction. This was done through participant validation of the findings from DS-I with CEBW project 
managers and included: anticipating future policy shifts and their legal implications, supporting local 
capacity building to facilitate circularity projects, and coordinating cross-project collaboration be-
yond municipal divides. The relevance of introducing systemic project assessment tools from the 
literature review for monitoring capacity deficits was discussed as particularly relevant. These tools 
could help monitor and create awareness on gaps in CEBW’s partners' ability to integrate circularity 
practices within the local waste management structures. 
The PS introduced and refined a procedure enabling practitioners to self-assess the transformative 
capacities in their local urban environments, in this case referring to local waste management sys-
tems. This procedure was inspired by an analytical tool that uses self-assessment forms followed by 
interviews to monitor and investigate organizational capacities to contribute to broader transition 
policy programs, serving as a first step toward developing adequate capacity-building strategies. 
Acknowledging that local waste management systems extend beyond individual organizations, this 
procedure was adapted to align with systemic assessment indicators from transition theories. It in-
corporates a framework for urban transformative capacity (UTC), which refers to the collective ability 
to conceive, prepare, initiate, and implement path-deviating changes toward sustainability in local 
urban environments. The systemic indicators, or transformative capacity factors, from UTC replaced 
the organizational attributes used as indicators in the original assessment procedure. 
As a result, the Urban Transformative Capacity Assessment Procedure was developed and intro-
duced in CEBW through an iterative prototyping process, adapting the procedure based on practical 
feedback. This involved testing the extent to which an introductory self-evaluation form, presenting 
==PAGE:133==
132 
 
the UTC terminology, and a reflexive follow-up session would assist local practitioners build aware-
ness of how their project experiences relates to the broader transformative capacity of their local 
urban environment. In this regard, while the follow-up session was initially unstructured, later design 
iterations introduced design objects, such as simplified UTC factor cards and interactive exercises to 
ease the practical accessibility of the UTC terminology and guide structured reflection. 
In DS-II, the initial evaluation of the Urban Transformative Capacity Assessment Procedure showed 
evidence of the tool facilitating self-reflection and awareness among practitioners in considering 
broader transformative capacity factors. However, the evaluation also highlighted areas of improve-
ment requiring more testing: the refinement of the assessment form and factor cards to ease prac-
tical accessibility of UTC terminology; implementing the procedure in group settings with multiple 
actors to increase representativeness of forms and introduce transdisciplinary debate; and explicitly 
clarifying its purpose alongside the introduction of scoping activities before assessment. 
To address these areas for improvement, the final version of the procedure presents a structured 
workshop format with a step-by-step approach that guides local practitioners in assessing their col-
lective capacity to drive urban transformative change through facilitated reflection activities. 
The initial test covered only 38 of 60 UTC factors, highlighting the need for further testing to evaluate 
the full framework. Additionally, the current Likert-type rating scale could be tested against an 'in-
stitutional integration' rating tool from the original procedure. Finally, adding action-oriented follow-
up activities and bringing together actors to prioritize relevant UTC factors, browse transition tools, 
and co-create capacity-building strategies could strengthen the self-assessment procedure’s role in 
guiding further transformative capacity building in local urban environments. 
To conclude the thesis, its findings contrast recent research investigating the role of designers in 
large-scale transition projects. It suggests a new educational design role, focused on enabling reflec-
tion and collective awareness, fostering a “doing-by-learning” logic complementing the forward-
looking “learning-by-doing” approach discussed in the available literature. The findings underline 
the importance of reflexive activities in transition governance to evaluate the impact of large-scale 
transition projects. These activities serve as instruments for specifying further action and can poten-
tially legitimize the formation of future long-term, large-scale capacity building projects.
