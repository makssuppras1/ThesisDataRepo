Abstract
This thesis is an investigation of plasmonic modes in diﬀerent MoS2 monolayer structures using
density functional theory and linear response theory. MoS2 nanoparticles are used as a catalyst
for removing sulphur from oil products and it has recently been proposed that a combination of
diﬀerent MoS2 phases can be used as a transistor. The motivation behind the study is therefore
to better understand the physics behind such MoS2 nano structures. The starting point is an
investigation of the inﬁnite T- and ZT-phase slabs. A single damped plasmonic mode related to
intraband transitions is found for both phases. The following step is to investigate an interface
where the H-phase is combined with the ZT-phase. Three such structures are investigated and
it is found that they all have metallic states which are localized right in the boundary between
the two phases. The plasmonic modes of two of the structures are investigated and it is found
that the metallic boundary states give rise to plasmonic modes. A linear dispersion is found for
the boundary plasmon in the ﬁrst interface consistent with the mode being conﬁned in 1D. The
ﬁnal investigation is on a 4 atoms wide triangular nanoparticle of the H-phase. Three kinds of
states are found in the region which would be inside the band gap of the pure H-phase; edge
states on the sulphur dimer, delocalized edge states, and corner states. Of these three kinds of
states, plasmonic modes related to the ﬁrst and third group are found on the sulphur dimer.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The following thesis is an investigation of the plasmonic modes found in diﬀerent variations of
the MoS2 monolayer using density functional theory (DFT) and linear response theory. MoS2
is one of the transition metal dichalcogenides which have gotten massive attention from the
scientiﬁc community during the last few decades. It is a natural continuation of the interest
which graphene sparked earlier as the ﬁrst real 2D material.
The bulk MoS2 consists like
graphite of stacked layers where it is relatively easy to separate a single monolayer and thereby
get a 2D material. The monolayers which results from this are semiconducting and placed in
a triagonal prismatic H-phase as seen on Figure 1.1. By doping the structure either with an
electron beam or by adsorbing lithium atoms, one can create another phase of the monolayer
which is metallic. This octahedral phase is known as the T-phase and can be seen on Figure
1.2. Some recent investigations[1] of the T-phase shows that the structure of perfect symmetry
tends to be unstable and that a restructuring occurs which forms zigzag chains of molybdenum
atoms, see Figure 1.3. This new restructured phase is called the ZT-phase and has a very small
band gap of 0.022 eV[1].
The H-phase has metallic states on the edge[2] which gives rise to edge plasmons[3]. The plas-
monic modes of the T- and ZT-phase has, to my knowledge, not been investigated previously
and will be the starting point of the investigations. The following step will be to investigate in-
terfaces where the H- and T-phase are combined. Such interfaces have recently been investigated
experimentally[4] in the prospect of using the T-H-T interface as a transistor where the T-phase
can work as contacts for the source and drain having an atomically ﬁne matching to the H-phase.
The results are very promising which motivates for a better understanding of these interfaces.
The plasmonic modes will be investigated along with the eﬀects on the quantum states when
the two phases comes into contact. It is the hope that the preliminary investigations on the
simple monolayers will make it easier to interpret the results obtained on the more complicated
interfaces.

MoS2 nanoparticles are used to a large degree as a catalyst for hydrogenating sulphur containing
molecules from oil production and has also shown potential as a catalyst for the hydrogen
evolution reaction[5]. The ﬁrst process is obviously important for environmental reasons and the
second is important in the context of storing solar energy in hydrogen. Both of these chemical
processes occur on the edge of the particles and therefore involves the metallic edge states.
Motivated by this, an investigation is made on such a triangular nanoparticle to see whether the
properties of the H-phase edge states are replicated on the edge of the triangle.
The general motivation for the project is to a large degree academic since the main objective
is to gain a better understanding of these structures which have proven to be very useful in
diﬀerent areas of physics.
This does not mean that plasmons cannot be used for practical
applications. Because the plasmonic modes can couple to optical ﬁelds and conﬁne them to a
very small length scale, they play an important role when investigating materials for optical
cables in microelectronics[6].
Another application, which is related to the coupling between
plasmons and light, are the materials used in solar cells[7]. The adsorption of the cell can be
improved by using a material with surface plasmons and the required thickness of the material
can then be signiﬁcantly reduced.
The thesis is divided into the following chapters;
Chapter 2 presents the theoretical background needed to describe the plasmons quantum me-
chanically.
Chapter 3 describes the theory behind the computational methods which have been applied
and how these methods work.
Chapter 4 presents the parameters of the response function calculations and explains how they
should be converged.
Chapter 5 presents the investigations of the T- and ZT-phase.
Chapter 6 presents the investigations of the interface structures.
Chapter 7 presents the investigations of the triangular nanoparticles.
Chapter 8 sums up the ﬁndings of the project and suggests some further investigations.

the starting point for understanding how a quantum mechanical system responds to a small
time-dependent perturbation such as an electric ﬁeld.

3.1
Density Functional Theory
DFT is a computational scheme for solving the many-body Schr¨odinger equation. Since the
nuclei move much slower than the electrons, they can be viewed as being static. This is known
as the Born-Oppenheimer approximation. It results in an electronic Hamilton which can be
solved separately.
The basic foundation of DFT is the Hohenberg-Kohn theorem[12] which
states that the external potential, vext, and thereby the ground state of the electronic problem
is uniquely determined by the ground state density, ρ. This means that the ground state energy
of the system can be written as a functional of the density,

The response function calculation needs to be converged wrt. a number of parameters. Conver-
gence in generally considered achieved when the eigenenergy of the ﬁrst plasmon mode is stable
within 10 meV. There is, however, other parameters which need to be taken into account. This
is elaborated in the explanation below. The response calculation runs in three steps; ﬁrst, the
standard DFT ground state calculation which yields the KS states and eigenvalues, secondly,
the response function is constructed, and ﬁnally, the dielectric matrix is calculated from the
response function and is diagonalized to obtain the plasmon modes.
The parameters of the calculations are as follows;
• vac - The vacuum thickness between identical images.
This is required since periodic
boundary conditions are used. We use a truncated Coulomb potential to minimize how
much vacuum we need. The details are explained below.
• k-points - The k-point sampling of the Brillouin zone in the ground state calculation.
• TF - The Fermi temperature of the ground state calculation. This is converged wrt. the
total energy of the ground state. The concept of the Fermi temperature is explained below.
• εgs
cut - The plane wave (PW) cut-oﬀenergy of the ground state calculation which deter-
mines how many PWs we use to expand the KS-orbitals and thereby how well they are
represented.
• εres
cut - The PW cut-oﬀenergy of the response function which determines how many G-
vectors we include in the response matrix of eq. (2.4.2). The cut-oﬀenergy is equal to
the maximum allowed energy of a transition. Since the convergence of this parameter is
slower for larger plasmon energies, we consider both the ﬁrst plasmonic mode and the
macroscopic EELS of the system in order to ensure a good convergence.
• nbands - The number of bands included in the response function. This is simply chosen
such that we include all bands up to the chosen cut-oﬀenergy.
• η - The broadening parameter of the response function which is crucial in order to resolve
all the plasmon modes. This must be converged together with the k-point sampling since
a ﬁne η will require a ﬁne k-point sampling. The choice of η is based on whether or not
all the plasmon modes are present and the eigenvalue curves look reasonably smooth more
than it is converged wrt. to the plasmon energy. More on this below.

• ftol - The threshold for transitions. Only transitions where fnk−fmk+q > ftol is included.
This means that we will allow for a transition to occur if one of the states can be considered
occupied and the other unoccupied. The parameter is necessary since we don’t have integer
occupation numbers which is related to the concept of the Fermi temperature.
As mentioned previously (eq. 3.4.2), the response function is evaluated on a non-linear frequency
grid. This gives 3 more parameters,
• ωmax - The largest frequency point which is set equal to the cut-oﬀenergy.
• ∆ω0 - The smallest frequency spacing of the frequency grid.
• ω2 - The linearisation constant of the frequency grid. As ω2 goes to inﬁnity, we approach
a linear grid.
The Wiegner-Sietz truncation of the Coulomb potential is a way to avoid the repeated images
in interacting through the otherwise long range Coulomb interactions. It simply truncates the
Coulomb potential at a distance of half the unit cell size. This oﬀcourse means that our vacuum
thickness cannot be smaller than the width of our structure in any given direction. Otherwise
we would truncate the Coulomb interactions between the two ends of our structure as well.
The Fermi Temperature is a way to compensate for only having a discrete k-point grid.
It means that we smear out the occupations of each state which is similar to having a ﬁnite
temperature of the system. This parameter should therefore always be considered together with
the k-point sampling. It is most important for metallic structures where the occupation should
vary smoothly going from an occupied part of the band to an unoccupied part of the band.
When the calculation is run through, we extrapolate all the KS eigenvalues back to 0 Kelvin.

imaginary part of the dielectric function which consists of delta functions at all the single particle

Chapter 5
Metallic Mono-layer
The ﬁrst structure which is investigated is the metallic phase of the MoS2 monolayer. The T-
phase is set up in a simple unit cell of one molybdenum atom and two sulphur atoms whereas the
ZT-phase demands a unit cell of 2-by-2 molybdenum atoms to break the symmetry. Both the
T- and ZT-phase have been investigated in a previous study[19] and the structures are therefore
simply taken from this. A short explanation on how these structures where obtained is provided
here;
The T-phase calculation was converged wrt. cohesive energy and yielded the following calcu-
lational parameters; a grid spacing of 0.14 ˚A, a vacuum thickness between layers of 12 ˚A, and
a k-point sampling of (12,12,1). Finite grid mode was used because this method tends to give
the most realistic structures. Total energy calculations where made on diﬀerent sizes of the unit
cell and the result where ﬁtted to a 3-order polynomial. The structure was relaxed wrt. the
inter-atomic forces for each size of the unit cell until the forces were below 0.05 eV/˚A. This gave
a lattice parameter of 3.18 ˚A using the PBE functional.
The ZT-phase was obtained from a relaxation of the T-phase in the before mentioned 2-by-2
unit cell. The calculational parameters of the T-phase calculations was used but the k-point
sampling was changed to (6,6,1) k-points since the unit cell is twice as big in both directions.
Since the symmetry is broken, we needed to relax both the inter-atomic forces and the unit cell
stresses1 simultaneously to obtain the most realistic structure. The UnitCellFilter function of
ASE makes this possible but we had to use plane wave mode for the calculation. A plane wave
cut-oﬀof 800 eV was used. This cut-oﬀhas been shown to be suﬃcient by M. Pandey. The
result is a structure where the Mo-Mo bond is 3.28 ˚A along the ﬁrst lattice vector (x-axis) and
3.27 ˚A along the second lattice vector.

Chapter 6
Interface
Investigations on an interface between the T- and H-phase MoS2 has emerged recently in the
literature[4,20]. In this project, we study a slab where each phase is 4-atoms wide, see Figure
6.1. Preliminary investigations are made on larger slabs as well which have up to 8-atoms of
each phase.
Two diﬀerent kinds of boundaries between the two phases are investigated. The starting point
of this was the article by Lin[21] where phase transitions in MoS2 are investigated. This study
deﬁnes three kinds of boundaries, α, β, and γ, which can be seen on Figure 6.2. The α-boundary
is the boundary where a sulphur atom is ’missing’ along the border and the γ-boundary is the
one where an extra sulphur atom has been added. The β-boundary is more complex because it
not only compresses the rows of Mo-atoms but also realign them at the border. Because of this
complexity we have excluded the β type boundaries from this study. This gives 3 structures to
study; the α −γ interface, the α −α interface, and the γ −γ interface.
The α and γ boundaries can furthermore be divided into two types each. The type is dependent
on how the triangles of the H-phase is oriented at the interface. The 4 types of boundaries can be
seen on Figure 6.3. The sulphur atoms belonging to the H-phase is marked by red and represent a
sulphur atom both above and below the molybdenum layer whereas the sulphur atoms belonging
to the T-phase is marked by blue and is placed either above or below the molybdenum layer.
The triangles of the H-phase is furthermore marked by a red area. The 3 investigated interfaces
have the following boundaries; γ1 −α2, α1 −α2, and γ1 −γ2 as shown on Figure 6.4.

Chapter 7
Triangular Structure
A triangular structure of the H-phase MoS2 is the ﬁnal structure which is investigated. The
investigation is motivated by the fact that MoS2 nanoparticles are known to take this form[22].
We hope to recover some of the edge states and plasmons found on the edge of the H-phase. This
will mean that the physics happening at the edges of the triangular nano particles can be (and
has been) described by considering a single edge. The triangle is put in a hexagonal unit cell to
conserve the 3-fold symmetry in case of unwanted interaction between the images. A relaxation
of the structure is made using a real space grid to represent the wave functions and a grid spacing
of 0.14 ˚A was used as this was found to be suﬃcient previously. All calculational parameters
can be found in Appendix F.7. The relaxed triangle can be seen on Figure 7.1 and 7.2. The
distance between the Mo-atoms are around 3.0 ˚A in the central region and the distance between
the S-atoms are around 3.2 ˚A in the center and around 2.2 ˚A at the edges where S-dimers are
formed. The normal distance between Mo- and S-atoms in the H-phase which is 3.18 ˚A and 3.13
˚A. This means that the central region resembles the pure H-phase except that the Mo-atoms
have moved a little closer together resulting in the S-atoms moving a little further apart.

Chapter 8
Summary and Outlook
The plasmonic modes of 3 diﬀerent kind of MoS2 nano structures have been investigated. The
investigations also considered the electronic band structure and the localization of the wave
functions for these structures.
The ﬁrst structure, which was investigated, was the metallic T-phase monolayer of MoS2. This
revealed a single plasmonic mode related to two intraband transitions in the bands crossing the
Fermi level. The mode was strongly damped for large q-values which resulted in a plasmon dis-
persion quite far from the expected square root q-dependence. It is interpreted as the monolayer
behaving quite diﬀerently from a simple free-electron metal.
The ZT-phase was investigated next, being the more stable version of this octahedral monolayer.
The calculation predicted a metallic structure, even though, we know the structure to be slightly
semi-conducting. This results in a very damped plasmonic mode for low q-values which can be
related to intraband transitions in the metallic bands and perhaps interband transitions in the
small band gap between the Γ and M points. The macroscopic EELS was calculated and could
be used to compare with experimental investigation and as a way to identify the ZT-phase.
After the investigations on the simple monolayers, we turned our attention to the more complex
interface structures which consists of a combination of the H-phase and ZT-phase. 3 diﬀerent
interfaces was investigated; the α −γ, α −α, and γ −γ interface. All of these three structures
resulted in metallic states in the boundaries between the two phases. The α −γ structure has
a metallic state in both boundaries, the α −α structure has one in the α1 boundary, and the
γ −γ structure has one in the γ1 boundary. This is quite interesting in the context of electronic
transport through such an interface where these boundary states may play an important role.
The plasmonic modes was investigated for the α −γ and α −α interfaces.
For the α −γ
interface we found a relatively nice plasmonic mode in the γ1 boundary which even showed a
linear dispersion. The α −α interface had a very damped plasmon in the α1 boundary and a
relatively stable mode in the ZT-phase. This shows that some of the metallic boundary states
does indeed result in plasmonic modes localized in the boundary.
The ﬁnal investigation considered a triangular nanoparticle of the H-phase MoS2. The states
found inside the band gap could be characterized into three groups; the edge state on the sulphur
dimer, the delocalized edge state, and the corner state. The two edge states are consistent with
the ﬁndings of Bollinger[2] but the corner states have, to my knowledge, not been described
earlier. These corner states may have an impact on the electronic properties of the nanoparticles.
When the plasmonic modes where investigated, we found that transitions between the edge states
on the sulphur dimer and the corner states resulted in plasmons. A comparison with the edge
state found at the sulphur dimer of a inﬁnite H-phase stripe shows that the plasmonic energy
is only half of what we would expect. We must therefore conclude that the small size of the
particle means that the modes are fundamentally diﬀerent from the previously described edge
modes.
42
==PAGE:49==
The most obvious continuation of these studies would be to make response calculations on the
last interface as well and to make calculations using a ﬁner k-point sampling. This might make
it easier to resolve the diﬀerent plasmonic modes. Another obvious continuation would be to
do a response calculation on the triangular structure with a smaller η which may resolve some
more modes. It would also be interesting to investigate larger triangles but this would require
devoting a massive amount of calculational resources.
Some further investigations could be to calculate the band structure of the interface structures
more carefully since the location of the band will have great importance for the electronic prop-
erties. One could for instance do a GW or HSE calculation to obtain a more realistic picture. It
could also be interesting to do some experimental investigations on these structures, for example
create an EELS to compare with the calculations. Making a scan with a highly conﬁned electron
beam, it would be possible to create a spatially resolved EELS[24] which could be compared with
calculations using the method described by Andersen[18]. In that way, one could investigate if
plasmons located in the boundaries can be found experimentally as well. A ﬁnal interesting in-
vestigation would be of the transport properties of the interfaces. Such calculations are already
in progress and will reveal much more information on how the interfaces can be used in electronic
devices.
