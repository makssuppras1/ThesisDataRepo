Summary (English)
Independent component analysis (ICA) is a widely used blind source separation
method for decomposing resting state functional magnetic resonance imaging
(rs-fMRI) data into latent components. It can however be challenging to obtain
subject-speciﬁc component representations in multi-subject studies. Indepen-
dent vector analysis (IVA) is a promising alternative approach to perform group
fMRI analysis, which has been shown to better capture components with high
inter-subject variability. The most widely applied IVA methods are based on
the multivariate Laplace distribution (IVA-GL), which assumes independence
within subject components coupled across subjects only through shared scaling.
In this study a more natural formulation of IVA based on a Normal-Inverse-
Gamma distribution (IVA-NIG) is proposed, in which the components can be
directly interpreted as realizations of a common mean component with individ-
ual subject variability. The performance of IVA-NIG compared to IVA-GL and
similar decomposition methods is evaluated, through the application of three
types of simulated data, on real task fMRI data and on resting-state fMRI
data. The results show that IVA-NIG oﬀers superior detection of components
in simulated fMRI data. On real fMRI data with low inter-subject variability it
was found that all methods identify plausible components that are very similar.
On resting-state fMRI the model identiﬁed resting-state networks, which was
used to investigate changes in the expression of common resting-state networks
due to eﬀects of multiple sclerosis. To summarize, the investigations applied in
this work indicate that IVA-NIG may prove to be a useful analysis technique
which can adequately take subject variability into account and with a natural
interpretation of the parameters.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) is a widely used neuroimaging
modality, which measures brain activity through changes in blood ﬂow employ-
ing the coupling between neuronal activation and cerebral blood ﬂow. It is a
non-invasive methods used to study both the healthy and diseased brain. By
correlating distant brain regions, fMRI is most often used to infer brain connec-
tivity either between selected brain regions or at the whole brain level. Mul-
tivariate decomposition methods, also known as blind source separation (BSS)
techniques, can be used to decompose the fMRI signal into latent components,
which reveal brain regions with high degree of signal synchrony (functional con-
nectivity).
Independent Component Analysis (ICA) is probably the most applied BSS tech-
nique, and is based on estimations of independent activity patterns from linearly
mixed fMRI signals (McKeown et al., 1998). In multi-subject studies, however,
it can be challenging to obtain the subject-speciﬁc component representations
(time courses and spatial maps) (Michael et al., 2014). In group ICA, a group-
level PCA step is performed on subject datasets which are temporally concate-
nate in order to detect shared spatial components, and the subject-speciﬁc rep-
resentation are subsequently obtained using back-reconstruction (Erhardt et al.,
2011) or dual-regression (Beckmann and Smith, 2004). Despite being a widely
used method, it still had some disadvantages; the group-level PCA step may
restrict signal distribution and hence have diﬃculty in capturing inter-subject
variability and the back-reconstruction can be unstable.
Independent Vector Analysis (IVA) has been suggested as an alternative way to
perform multi-subject BSS (Kim et al., 2006), and studies have shown that it is
better at capturing spatial inter-subject variability when the subject variability
is high (Dea et al., 2011; Ma et al., 2013; Michael et al., 2014). In IVA, the
components are random source vectors, which are assumed independent of each

other within a dataset, just as in ICA. However, in IVA the multivariate source
distribution causes the vectors to exhibit dependence across subjects (Lee et al.,
2007, 2008; Ma et al., 2013).
1.1
Problem statement
The overall aim of this study is to investigate and characterize inter-subject
variability in multi-subject fMRI data. Therefore, the IVA model is investigated
and a novel model using a diﬀerent source distribution is proposed.
The multivariate Laplace distribution, which is the most common source dis-
tribution for IVA, can be derived by considering an a priori independent zero
mean multivariate Normal distribution over subjects, with variance drawn from
a Gamma distribution.
The posterior source distribution is then formed by
marginalizing with respect to the variance, which causes dependency between
subjects to arise in the source distribution.
Instead of assuming coupled scaling across subjects, it is hypothesize that a
more natural assumption is that the source vectors exhibit variability around
a common mean.
Therefore, this work presents an alternative parametriza-
tion with mean drawn from a Normal distribution and scale drawn from the
inverse-Gamma distribution. The source distribution can then be formed by
marginalizing with respect to both the mean and scale which leads to a Normal-
Inverse-Gamma (NIG) source distribution. This model is denoted IVA-NIG. In
contrast to the multivariate Laplace distribution the mean has a natural inter-
pretation in the NIG distribution. Given that the Laplace source distribution
only considers the scale of variability across subjects, the sign of individual vox-
els can change over subjects without inﬂuencing the likelihood. Using the NIG
prior this behavior is discouraged as this would increase the variability across
the mean. In summary, the multivariate Laplace distribution focuses on vari-
ance dependency with a consistency in modulation, whereas the NIG source
distribution will capture consistency in the activation patterns.
1.2
Objectives
The overall objectives of this thesis are to:
• Formulate and implement the IVA-NIG model.
• Evaluate the performance of IVA-NIG compared to similar decomposition
methods through the application of three types of simulated data.

• Perform a comprehensive investigation of the eﬀects of IVA-NIG compared

• Investigate the models abilities to decompose task fMRI.

2.1
Magnetic resonance imaging
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is a medical imaging modality that can cre-
ate images of the body. This safe and non-invasive method, which oﬀers detailed
views of the brain tissue, is essential in diagnostics and treatment evaluation.
Mostly, the magnetic properties of hydrogen is utilized, because of its high
abundance in water and fat in the human body. The hydrogen atom possess
a nuclear spin angular momentum since it only consists of a single proton. An
MR-scanner consists of a strong static magnetic ﬁeld, B0, a radiofrequency ﬁeld,
B1, and linear gradient ﬁelds, G. The nuclear spins of protons will be randomly
oriented in the absence of a magnetic ﬁeld and hence the net magnetic moment
will be zero (Nishimura, 2010). However, when applying a strong magnetic ﬁeld
to nuclear spins, all magnetization vectors in the body will align in the direction
of B0, also called in the longitudinal direction, creating a net magnetic moment,
M0. The equilibrium condition is depicted in Figure 2.1b. The nuclear spins
will precess around the magnetic ﬁeld at the Larmor frequency, ω. The Larmor
frequency is expressed as

is dependent on the ﬁrmness of the tissue and the mobility of the molecules.
The time constant describing the return to equilibrium are called relaxation
time constants. T1 describes the relaxation in the longitudinal direction, where
the signal is exponentially increasing corresponding to the return of the net
magnetization and the alignment of the vectors. T2 describes the relaxation in
the transversal direction, where the signal is exponentially decreasing due to the
dephasing of the nuclear spins. Another relaxation constant, T∗
2 , also describes
the decay of transversal magnetization, but not only due to dephasing but also
due to local ﬁeld inhomogeneity. The contrast and weighting of an image is
determined by which settings of sequences are used in the acquisition. The echo
time (TE) describes the time between the emission of the radiofrequency pulse
and the recording of the signal. The repetition time (TR) is the time between
the radiofrequency pulses.
By varying these settings and obtaining diﬀerent
contrasts of the images, the diﬀerences in tissues are enhanced and more easily
recognized (Nishimura, 2010).
The changes in the magnetization occurring after the excitation is turned oﬀ
induces a voltage over a receiver coil. The emitted signals from the oscillating
protons can be used to reconstruct an MR image. However, spatial informa-
tion is needed in order to map the oscillations. Through application of linear
gradient ﬁelds, G, control of the phases and frequencies of the oscillations can
be achieved. So, magnetic gradient coils are introduces in order to distinguish
signals generated from diﬀerent spatial locations (Nishimura, 2010).

2.1.1
Functional MRI
Functional MRI (fMRI) measures brain activity through changes in blood ﬂow
employing the coupling between neuronal activation and cerebral blood ﬂow. It
is a widely used method, since it provides non-invasive images of brain activ-
ity with good spatial resolution and relatively good temporal resolution. The
most common contrast is the blood-oxygen-level dependent (BOLD) contrast,
discovered by Ogawa in the 1990s (Ogawa et al., 1990, 1992). Here, it is as-
sumed that increased blood ﬂow (and hence increased amount of oxygenated
hemoglobin) follows increased brain activity. Simply deﬁned, the BOLD signal
measures the total amount of changes of deoxygenated hemoglobin over time
(Kim and Bandettini, 2010).
Oxygenated hemoglobin behaves diﬀerently from deoxygenated hemoglobin due
to diﬀerent magnetic properties. Oxygenated hemoglobin is diamagnetic mean-
ing that it has no unpaired electrons and zero magnetic moment.
Whereas
dexoygenated hemoglobin is paramagnetic meaning with that it has unpaired
electrons and strengthens the magnetic ﬁeld causing a shortening in the T∗
2 re-
laxation time. Thus, more signal can be seen on T∗
2-weighted images when the
blood is highly oxygenated (Huettel, 2009).
2.1.1.1
Resting state fMRI
Experiment using fMRI often includes block design or an event-related design
with a task which is hypothesized to stimulate a certain activation in the brain.
However, fMRI while the subject is not performing any speciﬁc task in the MRI
scanner, i.e. is at rest, has increased in popularity in the last decades. Here,
diﬀerent brain regions with synchronous BOLD signal changes can be identiﬁed.
Since no external stimuli is applied, the BOLD changes are believed to be part
of intrinsic operations or stimuli-independent processing in the brain (Huettel,
2009). Usually, rs-fMRI signals are low-frequency osciallations within the range
of 0.01-0.1 Hz.
The ﬁrst resting-state connectivity study was performed by
Biswal et al. (Biswal et al., 1995). By correlating distant brain regions, rs-fMRI
is most often used to infer brain connectivity either between selected brain
regions or at the whole brain level, and it can be used to describe functional
connectivity diﬀerences between subject groups.
Certain brain regions become more prominent at rest. It has become clear that
the default mode network has an increase in activity during resting conditions
and activation decreases while performing a certain tasks (Vincent et al., 2007;
Damoiseaux et al., 2006). Another resting state network is the sensorimotor
network, which is related to the sensorimotor system (Cruz Gómez et al., 2013).

2.2
Multiple Sclerosis
MS is an autoimmune, neurodegenerative disorder aﬀecting the central nervous
system (CNS). The disease has a prevalence of approximately 1 per 1000 persons
in Denmark (Bladbjerg et al., 2012) with an age of onset between 20 - 40 years.
It is characterized by inﬂammatory demyelations and axonal degenerations in
the white matter of the brain, spinal cord and optic nerve (Porth, 2011). This
leads to conduction abnormalities such as decreased conduction velocity and
complete blockage of the conduction of the signal. The lesions, or plaques, can
happen in any location of the CNS, and the lesions can lead to great physical
disabilities. Neuroimaging using MRI is highly useful, since the lesions of MS
are macroscopically visible. The pathogenesis is thought to have both genetic
and environmental inﬂuences (Porth, 2011).
2.2.1
Fatigue
Fatigue aﬀects 75-95% of MS patients and is characterized by an overwhelming
sense of exhaustion, lack of energy and tiredness.
It can occur at any time
during the illness and many MS patients report fatigue as the most disabling
symptom causing the greatest impair to their quality of life (Braley and Chervin,
2010). However, despite the severity of the condition, the concept of fatigue
is vague and the term can be perceived diﬀerently by diﬀerent individuals -
after all, almost everyone has experienced fatigue at some point in their lives
(Johnson, 2008). Therefore, it is important to be able to assess and diagnose the
condition reliably for patients with MS - both for treatment reasons as well as
to improve the understanding of the condition within the social and professional
environment of the patient. Several ways to assess fatigue exist - however, it
is diﬃcult to measure it clinically and there is no deﬁnitive measure (Johnson,
2008).
2.2.1.1
Measuring Fatigue
Several scales for characterizing the disabilities caused by MS exist.
One of
these is the Expanded Disability Status Scale (EDSS) developed by Kurtzke in
1983. This is a widely used standard scale which accounts for all neurological
functions of the patients. The scores ranges from 0 to 10, where 0 indicates
healthy conditions and 10 indicates severe disability.
The scientiﬁc Fatigue
Scale for Motor and Cognitive Functions (FSMC) was developed by Penner et
al. in 2005 (Penner et al., 2005) for patients with MS in order to describe the
fatigue severity. It is a self-rating questionnaire about everyday life problems
connected to fatigue covering both motor and cognitive functions.

3.1
Statistical Parametric Mapping
Statistical Parametric Mapping (SPM) is a voxel-based approach developed by
Friston et al. for performing functional analysis of the brain (Friston et al.,
2007). Parametric statistical models are assumed at each speciﬁc voxel using
the general linear model (GLM) described in section 3.6.1. This mass-univariate
approach is used for modeling and statistical inference of the fMRI data. The
statistical parameters are collected in a map of the brain, statistical parametric
maps (SPMs). Statistical inference can then be made on the basis of the SPMs
by utilizing random ﬁeld theory combined with the GLM (Friston, 2003).
3.2
Preprocessing of the data
Preprocessing is important in order to reduce any undesired processes and vari-
ability in the signal and hence improve the quality of the signal and prepare it for
statistical analysis. Since the BOLD signal is very small compared to the inten-
sity of the MR signal, preprocessing with diﬀerent spatio-temporal transforma-
tions is required for optimal analysis and for increasing the functional resolution
(Huettel, 2009). An essential part of analyzing fMRI data is the assumption that
the location of each voxel stays constant over time. This, however, is not always

In order to ensure that each voxel in a single subject is relatively consistent

to know the corresponding neuroanatomy of a speciﬁc activation. Therefore,
coregistration to a structural image is needed. For multi-subject comparisons
a common reference space was achieved by performing normalization of each

3.2.1
Slice acquisition time correction
Slice timing correction is performed because the data is acquired one slice at a
time, i.e. each slice is acquired at diﬀerent times. This phenomna results in a
mismatch between the acquisition timing of the diﬀerent slices, and this can be
problematic when analyzing event-related fMRI where the time points of events
are important and assumed to be acquired at the same time in all voxels (Roche,
2011). In order to correct this, slice timing correction can be applied (Henson
et al., 1999). Here, the data in the diﬀerent slices are interpolated to match
the timing of a chosen reference slice (Russel Poldrack, 2011). The order of the
collection of the slices is essential for performing correct temporal interpolation.
In this project, the ﬁrst slice was chosen as a reference slice and the time series
were shifted using sinc-interpolation (Ashburner et al., 2013).
3.2.2
Motion correction
Some degree of head movements always occurs during scanning - even such
a motion as swallowing can aﬀect the fMRI data greatly. This can result in
a mismatch in the location of the brain compared to the subsequent images
acquired at diﬀerent time points. A given voxel can suddenly contain signal from
both gray matter and tissue from the ventricle. The outcomes of the movements
are often most visible at the edges of the brain. This is because of the large
intensity change of a voxel containing no brain tissue to suddenly becoming
active signal-wise (Huettel, 2009). Head movement can also cause a spin history
eﬀect causing voxels to not correctly reﬂect their respective tissue. This occurs
because protons with a diﬀerent excitation can move into voxels in a neighboring
slice (Russel Poldrack, 2011). Since the motion concerns the movement of the
brain on a large scale it is usually referred to as bulk motion.
In order to
reduce the motion eﬀect, the images in the time series are often realigned to a
common reference image using standard motion correction techniques. These
techniques typically assume that the movements can be described using a rigid
body transformation (six-parameter) - meaning that the shape and size of the
brain cannot change but the position of the brain can. The movements are then
described by a combination of three translations (movement of the brain along
each x−, y−and z−axes) and three rotations (turning of the brain around an
axis) (?Russel Poldrack, 2011).
In this project, the optimal rigid body transformation-parameters were deter-
mined by a cost function using the least squares approach. A Gaussian smooth-
ing kernel with a full-width-half-maximum (FWHM) of 5 mm was applied to the
images before the transformation-parameters were estimated in order to mini-
mize potential noise eﬀects within the images. The reference image was then
registered to the mean of the time series in order to gain the most precise results.

The original data were resampled in order to estimate the values obtained by the
optimal transformation. Here, a second order degree B-spline interpolation was
applied. Afterwards, the realigned images were visually inspected in FSLView
using the time series option in order to check that no severe residual motion
eﬀects was observed.
3.2.3
Distortion correction
Geometric distortions can appear due to inhomogeneities in the main magnetic
ﬁeld often seen at the air-tissue interfaces, such as at the sinuses or ear canals.
When the spatial information is encoded, the local ﬁeld inhomogeneities cause
errors in the location of the anatomical structures and signal attenuation due
to T∗
2 decay. The spatial distortions occur along the phase encoding direction,
usually along the anterior-posterior axis, and they can cause alignment-issues
in relation to the structural images. One way to correct the distortions is by
using a ﬁeld map, which describes the B0 ﬁeld (Jezzard and Balaban, 1995).
This can be done by acquiring images with reversed phase-encoding. A map is
then created describing the distance each voxel has been shifted. This ﬁeld map
can be applied to obtain the original location of each voxel (Russel Poldrack,
2011). In this project, the FSL TOPUP tool was used (Andersson et al., 2003;
Smith et al., 2004), which is part of the FMRIB Software Library (FSL) created
by Stephen Smith in 2000. Following the distortion correction, the images were
inspected as to see if the operation has proceeded as expected and ensured that
no new artifacts had been introduced from the computed ﬁeld map.
3.2.4
Functional-structural coregistration and normalization
When analyzing functional data, it is important to be able to relate the activa-
tion to an anatomical region in the brain. However, both the anatomical contrast
and the spatial resolution are low in fMRI data. Therefore, coregistration to a
structural image is needed, where the functional data is mapped to an anatom-
ical image with high resolution. The size, shape and gyral anatomy of the brain
can also diﬀer greatly across diﬀerent people. This can cause diﬃculties when
comparing activations between diﬀerent subjects. In order to accommodate for
this eﬀect when performing inter-subject comparisons, spatial normalization can
be applied, where each individual brain is transformed to have the same shape
and size as the others (?). This is done by translation, rotation, scaling and
shearing the images and by low dimensional non-linear warping Ashburner and
Friston (2005).
Prior to normalization, certain preprocessing steps are required. A bias ﬁeld
correction was applied in order to correct low-frequency variation in the signal

their cause: Intensity variations due to MR physics are usually spatially smooth,
while intensity variations due to the properties of diﬀerent tissue types contain

Despiking
A data-drive wavelet-based method was applied for despiking motion artifacts
(Patel et al., 2014). A primary motion artifact is the misalignment of the brain
position, where the correction can be performed as previously described. Sec-

eﬀects. These artifacts are often observed as non-stationary events, which can be
detected and despiked from the time series. This wavelet-approach was applied

used. The process was subsequently evaluated by examining diﬀerent peaks in
the time series before and after despiking.

3.2.6
Spatial smoothing
Spatial smoothing can be applied in order to improve the signal-to-noise ratio
(SNR) for signal with large spatial scale, although it can happen at a cost of the
spatial resolution if used unwisely. Smoothing is usually performed by utilizing
a Gaussian ﬁlter, which spreads the intensity of a voxel to the adjacent voxels
and hence removes the high-frequency information. The amount of smoothing
a Gaussian ﬁlter can perform is often described by the width of the distribution
measured in millimeters at half of the maximum value (Russel Poldrack, 2011).
Spatial smoothing should be performed using the matched ﬁlter theorem, i.e. at
the same width as the width of the signal of interest. Because of spatial inter-
subject variability, the activation region is rarely located at exactly the same
voxels across subjects. Therefore, the SNR can be improved if the ﬁlter matches
the size of the expected area of activation (Huettel, 2009). Another advantage
of smoothing is that the errors will be more normally distributed (because of the
central limit theorem), and hence have a positive eﬀect on statistical analysis
(Friston, 2003). In this project, spatial ﬁltering was performed at 6 mm FWHM
(i.e. about two voxels in size).
3.2.7
Nuisance Variable Regression
Several factors may be present in fMRI data, which can induce an unwanted
eﬀect when performing statistical analysis. Therefore, these factors can be re-
gressed out using the GLM. These nuisance factors include low frequency scan-
ner drift, aliasing from the pulse and respiration, movement artifacts and spin
history eﬀects.
Low-frequency drift is usually present in the time series caused by either phys-
iological or scanner noise. These eﬀects were removed by including regressors
containing a cosine transform based high-pass ﬁlter with cut-oﬀfrequency of
1/128 Hz.
Both respiration and the cardiac cycle can cause a signiﬁcant amount of phys-
iological noise in the signal. The heart rate is faster than the repetition time
of the acquisition which can cause aliasing to the lower frequencies with the
measured spectrum. Respiration and the susceptibility variations in the lungs
can cause small disturbances in the magnetic ﬁeld within the brain tissue (Raj
et al., 2001). A possible solution is to monitor the heart rate and respiration
during scanning, such that the physiological disturbances can be modeled and
corrected in the signal retrospectively. One way of correcting these eﬀects is
using the RETROICOR method (Glover et al., 2000), where the physiological
noise is modeled as a basis-set of sines and cosines. These basis-sets represents

3.3
Decomposition methods
Univariate and hypothesis-driven approaches, such as the general linear model
(GLM), measures similarities between the observed voxel-wise BOLD signal and
a ﬁxed hypothesized canonical hemodynamic response function (HRF) (Huettel,
2009). However, since variations in the HRF sometimes occur across subjects,
the univariate models may not have the capacity to fully describe the BOLD
signals (Lee et al., 2007). Therefore, a variety of data-driven approaches have
been introduced in fMRI data analysis providing more ﬂexibility in the data
processing. Some exploration techniques partition the fMRI data into sets of
components that reﬂect the intrinsic structure of the data.
Multivariate decomposition methods, also known as blind source separation
(BSS) techniques, can be used to decompose the rs-fMRI signal into latent
components, which reveals brain regions with high degree of signal synchrony
(functional connectivity) and estimate broad patterns of common brain acti-
vations. Two decomposition methods are employed in this work: Independent
Component Analysis (ICA) and Independent Vector Analysis (IVA). Since both
of these methods utilize principal component analysis, this technique will be
described initially.
3.3.1
Principal Component Analysis
Algorithms for data reduction are often applied in order to simplify complex and
high-dimensional fMRI data while still preserving most of the variation in the
original data. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is one of the most common
methods ﬁrst developed by Pearson in 1901 (Pearson, 1901). Initially, the com-
ponent containing the most variability in a dataset is identiﬁed. Hereafter, the
component which accounts for the largest amount of the remaining variability is
identiﬁed - this methods is called the deﬂation approach (Hyvarinen, 2009). The
==PAGE:43==
3.3 Decomposition methods
19
process is repeated until a certain amount of components have been extracted.
The rest of the variation in the data is discarded. Singular value decomposi-
tion (SVD) can also solve the PCA problem eﬀectively given the right scaling
and projection (Coombe, 2006). The extracted components are orthogonal, i.e.
uncorrelated with each other, since each component is deﬁned in relation to
the previous component. The amplitude of the BOLD signal is relatively small
compared to the noise, which may result in a high amount of noise reﬂected by
the ﬁrst couple of components.

source components which can be estimated using various assumptions between
the relation of the underlying components (McKeown et al., 2003). It can be

networks in the brain are statistically independent. Independent Component
Analysis (ICA) is probably the most applied BSS technique (Comon, 1989; Bell
and Sejnowski, 1995), and is based on the estimation of independent activity
patterns from linearly mixed fMRI signals.

Group ICA
McKeown et al. (1998) introduced spatial ICA of a single subject. Multi-subject
studies allows for the possibility of identifying group trends or mutual activa-
tion patterns across subjects. Here, however, it can be challenging to obtain
the subject-speciﬁc component representations (time courses and spatial maps)
(Michael et al., 2014). In group ICA, the subject datasets are temporally con-
catenate in order to detect shared spatial components, and the subject-speciﬁc
representation are subsequently obtained using back-reconstruction (Erhardt
et al., 2011) or dual-regression (Beckmann and Smith, 2004). In this way, the
permutation problem of matching components across subjects is solved. This
approach is called Group ICA (GICA) and is implemented in the Group ICA of
fMRI Toolbox (GIFT, 2000). Spatial stationarity is assumed in GICA, but no
assumption is made concerning temporal consistency across subjects (Michael
et al., 2014).

3.3.5
Optimization
Anderson et al. (Anderson et al., 2012) showed that optimization techniques
considering Hessian information oﬀers better convergence than the natural gra-
dient (NG) algorithm, which is currently implemented in the GIFT toolbox
versions of IVA-L and IVA-GL (denoted IVA-L: NG and IVA-GL: NG).
Limited memory Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shanno (L-BFGS) solves uncon-
strained nonlinear optimization problems utilizing a quasi-Newton technique
which approximates the BFGS algorithm using only a limited amount of mem-
ory (Liu and Nocedal, 1989). This method is applied to both IVA-NIG and our
versions of the IVA-L and IVA-GL algorithm (denoted IVA-L: L-BFGS and IVA-
GL: L-BFGS) using the minFunc-implementation by Mark Schmidt (Schmidt,
2005).

3.5.1
Regional Homogeneity
Local functional connectivity can be described by the homogeneity of the ﬂuc-
tuations of the resting state BOLD-signal in neighboring voxels (Dogonowski
et al., 2013). When analyzing the regional homogeneity, a spatial smoothing
step during the preprocessing can lead to undesired bias. Therefore, the spatial
smoothing step in Figure 3.1 was omitted during the preprocessing of the fMRI
data for this analysis.
The Kendall’s Coeﬃcient of Concordance (KCC) measure was used to assess
the regional homogeneity. A cube consisting of 3 × 3 × 3 voxels was extracted
and the KCC-value was calculated and assigned to the centre voxel. Each time
point was assigned a rank between 1 and the number of time points depending
on signal intensity. The KCC-value was then calculated as

2005). Here, 100 of the previous corrections are used to form a low-rank Hes-

interpolation/extrapolation.

score function. Therefore, sICA with the Infomax algorithm was applied.

5.1
Simulations
In the ﬁrst two simulations, the IVA algorithm was applied directly to the data
without performing an initial subject-level PCA step, hence the correct subspace
is used under the assumption that PCA has worked perfectly. PCA reduces the
data to a subspace, and if this subspace does not suﬃciently describe the data,
the IVA algorithms can never produce successful separation. By not doing the
initial PCA reduction step, only the performance of the IVA models are taken
into account when evaluating the solutions.

5.1.4
Discussion of simulations
The implementation of the IVA-NIG algorithm was validated through applica-
tion to NIG-distributed data, where IVA-NIG was superior to the other models
in terms of both the correlation and the ISIjnt measure. The ISIjnt measure
describes the estimated time courses compared to the true values of the mixing
array, whereas the focus in this project mainly lies in estimating the correct
spatial components. The fact that IVA-NIG also performed well on data with
Laplace-distributed sources indicate that it is good at source separation and can
account for variations in diﬀerent types of data.
IVA-L: NG did not obtain good results when applied to Laplace-distributed
data, which was unexpected. The great diﬀerence between IVA-L with L-BFGS
and natural gradient optimization could indicate that either the optimization
procedure or the GIFT-implementation of the procedure cannot handle this par-
ticular type of Laplace-data. As described previously, the GIFT-implementation
sometimes allows the cost function to increase and diverge. IVA-L: L-BFGS did
however obtain good results, and this indicates a correct implementation of the
algorithm.
More realistic datasets were generated where inter-subject variability was intro-
duced as spatial rotations, translations and shearing of the components across
subjects. The detection power of IVA-NIG was better than the other models,
when looking at the ROC curves and calculating the relative performance based
on AUC scores. However, all models performed well in terms of the Pearson
correlation. IVA-L: L-BFGS had superior detection power compared to NG op-
timization, but only slight diﬀerences could be seen between the two methods
for IVA-GL, which still outperformed the IVA-L algorithm. This corresponds
well to previous studies of IVA-GL.
Spatial ICA obtains the highest Pearson correlation between the true compo-
nents and the estimated components.
The IVA algorithms introduce a bias
towards zero, since we assume a priori that the mean of the distribution is zero.
Group ICA does not make any assumption concerning the a priori mean of the
components across subjects, and will therefore not have this bias. By introduc-
ing this bias towards zero, the model will be less inﬂuenced by noise and due
to reduced variance of the sources. In this way, it will be less volatile around
the true value, and hence maybe provide more stability. However, the Pearson
correlation will usually be larger for sICA compared to IVA, because the acti-
vation values of IVA will always experience a slight bias towards zero compared
to the true values. Although, the sequence of the voxels will still be correct for
IVA, so measurements such as AUC will not be inﬂuenced by this. This means
that IVA can perform better in the presence of noise at detecting and estimating
whether a certain voxel is activated compared to sICA. This hypothesis could

5.2.2
Parameter selection for IVA-NIG
The inﬂuence of the choice of shape and scale parameter for the inverse-Gamma
distribution was investigated. 20 components were estimated by IVA-NIG with
10 diﬀerent a0 and b0-values. The components were matched across runs by
estimating the correlation coeﬃcient between the mean component of the ﬁrst
run and the mean component of each individual run. This made it possible to
calculate the correlation coeﬃcient of a given component across subjects. It
was hypothesized that by changing the assumed shape of the inverse-Gamma
distribution describing the variance across subjects would result in more or less
subject variability in the estimated components.
Two diﬀerent experiments were performed. First, a0 was kept at the constant
value of 1, while b0 was varied from 0.1 to 10.000. The correlations coeﬃcient
for each component at the diﬀerent b0-values can be seen in Figure 5.7. When
inspecting each individual correlation curves, no systematic eﬀect was observed.
The same was done, where a0 was varied from 10−3 to 80, and b0 was varied
such that the mode was kept constant at a value of 1. When calculating the
between-subject correlation coeﬃcient, no signiﬁcant eﬀect was observed over
this range of parameter values. Based on these ﬁndings, it seems that the prior
distribution of the variance does only very weakly inﬂuence the results and that
IVA-NIG mainly learns from the data. This means that the choice of using
an uninformative prior with a wide variance and a mean of zero for the model
described in section 3.3.4 was an adequate decision.

5.2.4
Discussion of task fMRI data
When analyzing the stability of the IVA-NIG algorithm by initializing with a
random unmixing array ten times, the cost-function converged to almost the
same value indicating that it is a stable method.
The IVA algorithm based
on the multivariate Laplace distribution showed that the cost function utilizing
L-BFGS optimization converged to a lower value compared to natural gradient
optimization. It was also seen that when initializing in a Gaussian solution,
natural gradient provided a better optimization than IVA-L: NG. This exper-
iment suggests that it is better to use the L-BFGS optimization procedure.
When evaluating the parameter selection for IVA-NIG regarding the prior of
the inverse-Gamma distribution, no eﬀect was seen when changing the shape
and scale of the distribution. This indicates that IVA-NIG mostly learns from
the data.
The task-related component was identiﬁed, and IVA-NIG, IVA-GL: L-BFGS
and sICA produced similar results, both visually as well as when looking at the
Pearson correlation of the components. However, the optimization procedure
of IVA-GL: NG was halted at a suboptimal minimum, and hence its results
was diﬀerent from the others. More inter-subject variability was seen in the
components estimated by group ICA, which probably can be explained by the
back-reconstruction step. Generally, this experiment showed that when applied
to real task fMRI data, IVA-NIG produced very similar components and results
which are comparable with results from both IVA-GL and group ICA. This can
be attributed to the simple block design motor task exhibiting limited between-
subject variability. It was also seen that the implementation of the IVA-GL
algorithm from GIFT using the natural gradient method tended to get stuck
in local minima and it is therefore suggested that using L-BFGS optimization
oﬀers more stable convergence.

Conclusion
In this work, an IVA algorithm based on the NIG source distribution was im-
plemented and its performance was evaluated. The algorithm was compared to
IVA-L and IVA-GL through application of simulated data with NIG-distributed
sources and data with Laplace-distributed sources. Here, the IVA-NIG model
performed well on both types of data. The models were also evaluated through
application of simulated fMRI-like data with spatial inter-subject variability
obtained through rotation, translation and shearing of the components across
subjects.
IVA-NIG obtained better detection of components as assessed by
the AUC-score both for components with lower and higher spatial inter-subject
variability. When applied to real task fMRI data, the components identiﬁed
by IVA-NIG were comparable to results obtained from both IVA-GL and group
ICA, which can be attributed to the simple block design motor task exhibiting
limited between subject variability. The stability of the IVA-NIG algorithm was
investigated and the cost function converged to almost the same values with
diﬀerent starting points indicating stability with respect to local minima. The
selection of parameters for the prior inverse-Gamma distribution of the vari-
ance was also investigated, and it was concluded that the parameters only very
weakly inﬂuenced the results in the observed interval.
It was also observed that the implementation of both IVA-L and IVA-GL al-
gorithms from GIFT using the natural gradient method tended to get stuck in
suboptimal minimum values. The L-BFGS optimization was therefore imple-
mented as an alternative method, since it oﬀers more stable convergence. This
eﬀect was most prominent when applied to the task fMRI data. The decomposi-
tion methods were also applied to rs-fMRI data from MS patients with diﬀerent
degrees of fatigue and from healthy controls. A regional homogeneity analysis
was performed, however, no signiﬁcant clusters were found. Common resting-
state networks were identiﬁed using group ICA and IVA-NIG, which were ana-
lyzed for abnormal activity. A few signiﬁcant clusters in diﬀerent parts of the

Outlook
IVA-NIG may prove useful for identifying component vectors from fMRI data as
an alternative to IVA-L and IVA-GL, in cases where interpretability of the mean
component is desired and components are expected to have variability around a
common mean. If one has a prior expectation of the mean of the time series, it

speciﬁc traits.

Abstract—Independent component analysis (ICA) is a widely
used blind source separation method for decomposing resting
state functional magnetic resonance imaging (rs-fMRI) data into
latent components. However, it can be challenging to obtain
subject-speciﬁc component representations in multi-subject stud-
ies. Independent vector analysis (IVA) is a promising alternative
approach to perform group fMRI analysis, which has been
shown to better capture components with high inter-subject
variability. The most widely applied IVA methods is based on
the multivariate Laplace distribution (IVA-GL), which assumes
independence within subject components coupled across subjects
only through shared scaling. In this study we propose a more
natural formulation of IVA based on a Normal-Inverse-Gamma
distribution (IVA-NIG), in which the components can be directly
interpreted as realizations of a common mean component with
individual subject variability. We evaluate the performance of
IVA-NIG compared to IVA-GL and similar decomposition meth-
ods, through the application of two types of simulated data and
on real task fMRI data. The results show that IVA-NIG offers
superior detection of components in simulated fMRI data. On
real fMRI data with low inter-subject variability we ﬁnd that all
methods identify plausible components that are very similar.
I. INTRODUCTION
Resting state functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (rs-
fMRI) has increased popularity in the last decades and is used
to study both the healthy and diseased brain. By correlating
distant brain regions, rs-fMRI is most often used to infer brain
connectivity either between selected brain regions or at the
whole brain level. Multivariate decomposition methods, also
known as blind source separation (BSS) techniques, can be
used to decompose the rs-fMRI signal into latent components,
which reveals brain regions with high degree of signal syn-
chrony (functional connectivity). In BSS, the observed fMRI
data is assumed to be composed of a mixture of latent source
components which can be estimated using various assumptions
between the relation of the underlying components [1].
Independent Component Analysis (ICA) is probably the
most applied BSS technique, and is based on estimations
of independent activity patterns from linearly mixed fMRI
signals. In multi-subject studies, however, it can be challeng-
ing to obtain the subject-speciﬁc component representations
(time courses and spatial maps) [2]. This can be solved
by temporally concatenate subject datasets and subsequently
obtain subject-speciﬁc components using back-reconstruction
[3] or dual-regression [4]. This approach is called Group
ICA (GICA) and is implemented in the Group ICA of fMRI
Toolbox (GIFT, http://mialab.mrn.org/software/gift).
Independent Vector Analysis (IVA) has recently been sug-
gested as an alternative way to perform multi-subject BSS [5].
In IVA, the components are random source vectors, which
are assumed independent of each other within a dataset,
just as in ICA. However, in IVA the multivariate source
distribution causes the vectors to exhibit dependence across
subjects [6]–[8]. The multivariate Laplace distribution, which
is the most common source distribution for IVA, can be derived
by considering an apriori independent zero mean multivariate
Normal distribution over subjects, with variance drawn from
a Gamma distribution. The posterior source distribution is
then formed by marginalizing with respect to the variance,
which causes dependency between subjects to arise in the
source distribution. Instead of assuming coupled scaling across
subjects, we hypothesize that a more natural assumption is
that the source vectors exhibit variability around a common
mean. Therefore, we suggest an alternative parametrization
again based on an independent Normal distribution, but with
mean drawn from a Normal distribution and scale drawn from
the inverse-Gamma distribution. The source distribution can
then be formed by marginalizing with respect to both the mean
and scale which leads to a Normal-Inverse-Gamma (NIG)
source distribution. In contrast to the multivariate Laplace
distribution the mean has a natural interpretation in the NIG
distribution. Given that the Laplace source distribution only
considers the scale of variability across subjects, the sign of
individual voxels can change over subjects without inﬂuencing
the likelihood. Using the NIG prior this behavior is discour-
aged as this would increase the variability across the mean.
In summary, the multivariate Laplace distribution focuses
on variance dependency with a consistency in modulation,
whereas the NIG source distribution will capture consistency
in the activation patterns.
Previous studies comparing IVA and GICA have demon-
strated that IVA is better at capturing spatial inter-subject
variability when the subject variability is high [2], [8], [9].
In resting state fMRI, a high degree of spatial and temporal
variability can be expected since the subject do not follow a
speciﬁc task [2].

using only a limited amount of memory [13]. This method is
applied to both IVA-NIG and our versions of the IVA-L and
IVA-GL algorithm (denoted IVA-L: L-BFGS and IVA-GL: L-
BFGS) using the minFunc-implementation by Mark Schmidt
(http://www.cs.ubc.ca/ schmidtm/Software/minFunc.html).
D. Simulations
We generated two synthetic experiments in order to test the
individual models (IVA-NIG, IVA-GL, IVA-L and spatial ICA
(sICA)) abilities to decompose data from mixed sources.
1) Simulations on NIG distributed data: In order to validate
the implementation of the model, component data drawn from
the assumed source distribution, i.e. NIG-distribution, were
generated with the hypothesis that IVA-NIG should outperform
the competing models. The experiment had 30 subjects, 3000
voxels and the mixing matrix was drawn from a normal
distribution. We generated 5 different components sampled
from the NIG distribution with voxel- and component-speciﬁc
means and voxel- and component-speciﬁc variances drawn
from the inverse-Gamma distribution with a = 3 and b = 1.
Each dataset was generated with added Gaussian noise at 10
different contrast-to-noise ratios (CNR).
2) Simulated fMRI data: Erhardt et al. [3] introduced
a
MATLAB
toolbox,
SimTB,
used
for
simulations
of
fMRI datasets under a model of spatiotemporal separability
(http://mialab.mrn.org/software/simtb/index.html). This tool-
box is used in various studies for testing the performance of
IVA [2], [8], [9]. We used SimTB for simulating spatial maps
with inter-subject variability. The simulation parameters for the
spatial maps were based on the study by Dea [9]. Here, 23
different spatial components for 12 subjects were simulated
with voxel dimensions 148 × 148. The inter-subject spatial
variability was modelled by rotations, translations and spread
of the components with the following parameters: Component
number 1-13 had translation as N(0, 1), rotation as U(−2, 2)
and spread as U(0.8, 1), while component number 14-23 had
translation as N(0, 1), rotation as N(0, 1) and spread as
U(0.8, 0.85). Note that the components are divided into high
and low variability. The time courses were simulated as 23
different ﬁrst order harmonics with frequencies in the expected
range of rs-fMRI data (0.01 −0.1 Hz) with 500 time points.
Each dataset was generated with added Gaussian noise such
that the CNR was 0 dB.

E. fMRI data
The different models were applied to data from a simple

processing of the data was performed using standard SPM12
preprocessing steps and included temporal slice timing, motion
correction, normalization and smoothing by a 5 mm full width
at half maximum isotropic Gaussian kernel.
F. Model evaluation
Two methods were applied in order to evaluate the per-
formance of the models. The Pearson correlation between
the true and the corresponding estimated component was
calculated. In order to evaluate the detected activations, the
receiver operation characteristics (ROC) curve was generated.
The true simulated component map was masked at a given
threshold, where the amount of activated voxels, Nactive, and
the amount of deactivated voxels, Ndeactive, were computed.
While varying the threshold of the estimated component map,
the number of voxels that were inside the mask (Ntrue) and
outside the mask (Nfalse) were computed. The probability of
detection is deﬁned as Ntrue/Nactive and the probability of
false alarm is deﬁned as Nfalse/Ndeactive.
