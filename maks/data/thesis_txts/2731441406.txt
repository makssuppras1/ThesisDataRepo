Abstract 
The number of indoor playgrounds in the Scandinavian countries is rising. Knowledge of 
evacuation behaviours from indoor playgrounds is limited or non-existent. The many children in 
an indoor playground are a vulnerable group in society, and the buildings containing the indoor 
playgrounds need to be designed with a special focus on evacuation due to the social bonds 
that the children and their families will have.  
The main content of this thesis is based on Pathfinder simulations of an existing indoor 
playground in Denmark with a capacity of 800 occupants. Twelve scenarios were carried out to 
simulate different effects of social bonds, the impact of door choice, and the number of children. 
Average movement time is found through 100 runs with variation in occupant speed. 
Simulations showed that the social bond and the exit choice significantly influenced the 
movement time. The likely scenario of families finding each other before evacuation and then 
using the main entrance took five times as long as individual evacuation to any door. The 
movement times were increased by about 6 minutes if 2/3 of the occupants chose to evacuate 
through the main entrance where their shoes were located and up to about 8 minutes if the 
evacuation was also with a social bond and not individually. 
Increasing the number of children did have an impact, but depending on door choice the impact 
was either an increase or a decrease of under one minute. Adding time in the play equipment to 
simulate more prolonged evacuation from these areas did not result in significantly longer 
movement time. Simulations with meeting points did not have faster movement times compared 
to simulations where families found each other without meeting points.  
The results show that the safety margin between RSET and ASET most likely doesn’t exist or is 
minimal if the evacuation simulations do not use individual evacuation and free door choice.

Der bliver bygget flere og flere indendørs legeland i Skandinavien. Viden om evakuerings fra 
disse er begrænset eller ikke eksisterende. De mange børn, som et indendørs Legeland er 
beregnet til, er en sårbar gruppe og designet bør have fokus på evakuering med de sociale 
bånd som børnene og deres familier har.  
Hovedvægten i dette Master i brand speciale er baseret på Pathfinder simuleringer af et 
eksisterende indendørs legeland i Danmark med en maksimal personbelastning på 800 
personer. Tolv scenarier er valgt til at simulere de forskellige effekter af sociale bånd, dør valg 
og antal børn. Gennemsnitstid er fundet ved 100 gennemløb med variation i person hastighed.  
Simuleringerne viste at sociale bånd og valg af exit påvirkede bevægelsestiden væsentligt. 
Scenariet, hvor familierne fandt hinanden før de påbegyndte deres evakuering, og hvor mange 
brugte hovedindgangen tog 5 gang så lang tid som individuel evakuering frit til alle døre. 
Bevægelsestiden blev forøget med ca. 6 minutter hvis 2/3 evakuerede via hovedindgangen, 
hvor alles sko var placeret, og op til lidt under 8 minutter hvis denne evakuering blev foretaget 
med sociale bånd og ikke derved ikke individuelt. 
Tilføjelsen af flere børn til scenarierne ændre bevægelsestiden, og alt efter om opsætning af 
dørvalg, var dette enten en forøgelse eller en mindskning på lidt under et minut. Tilføjelse af 
ekstra ventetid i legeredskaberne for at simulere, at der her var længere 
evakueringsstrækninger, resulterede ikke i betydelig forøgelse af bevægelsestiden. 
Simuleringer af mødepunkter viste ikke en hurtigere bevægelsestid sammenlignet med 
simuleringer hvor familierne fandt hinanden uden mødepunkter. 
Resultaterne viste at sikkerhedsmargin mellem RSET og ASET højest sandsynligt ikke findes 
eller er meget små, såfremt simuleringerne ikke er sat op til individuel evakuering frit til alle 
døre.

1. 
Introduction 
The number of indoor playgrounds in the Scandinavian countries is rising. Knowledge of 
evacuation behaviours from these indoor playgrounds is limited or non-existent.  
The many children on the indoor playground are a vulnerable group in society, and the buildings 
containing the indoor playgrounds need to be designed with a special focus on evacuation due 
to the social bonds that the children and their families will have with each other.  
An increasing amount of data has been collected in recent years about the evacuation of 
children in schools and kindergartens. This data is useful, but the evacuation from an indoor 
playground evacuation is more complex with the many children not knowing each other or the 
surroundings. The children are not in a familiar building where they have yearly evacuation drills 
with a teacher. Most are in the building with parents and siblings, having a social bond with 
these in evacuation. Their speed will be affected by the playground equipment and unfamiliar 
surroundings. Some children might have been taught to evacuate by themselves, but others will 
have been taught in school to wait or find an adult before starting evacuation.  
For the adults in the building, the situation is also complex. They will be there because of their 
children but will often be in a sitting area without visual contact with their children playing. A 
recent study [1] found that many parents look for and find their children before evacuating 
themselves. These social bonds affecting the evacuation are more comparable with the 
evacuation of a shopping centre than a school but are, in fact, unique. 
In an indoor playground, the children and their parents must leave their shoes at the main 
entrance. Many might not exit the building before these shoes have been collected. This will 
result in longer evacuation time due to large queues at the main entrance. The question of 
egress doors used during the evacuation is therefore highly relevant. 
Indoor playgrounds are unique buildings. The layout is like no other typical building. The 
playground equipment is not like outside equipment. There will be 2-4 story high mazes with 
transparent nets around. They consist of easy obstacle courses, soft stairs, slides, and nets to 
cross. Other areas are enclosed trampolines or enclosed areas for ball play. Most areas are 
connected at the first-floor level by corridors enclosed by a net. Around and under the 
playground areas and corridors are areas with seating for the children and their parents. These 
seating areas look like they might not have been there originally but have been added as more 
parents choose to stay with their children during their visit. 
Indoor playgrounds are often located in old factory buildings that have been changed into 
activity centres for children.  
In Denmark, evacuation drills are not mandatory in indoor playgrounds. Planned evacuation 
drills with sufficient occupants are challenging because the children and their parents are paying 
consumers. Having enough occupants participate in a fire evacuation drill with little or no 
knowledge about the building is complex, and data about social bonds, total evacuation time, 
and premovement time has not yet been obtained.

1.1 Motivation and background 
Making the many new indoor playgrounds as safe as possible is an interesting field. The task 
seems to be based on very little literary information. The job is also complex because indoor 
playgrounds are not easily comparable to pre-defined usage categories or strategies in the 
current Building Regulation 2018 (BR18) [2]. Therefore, using computer simulation to make 
more data about indoor playground evacuation seems an appropriate choice. With more data, 
new indoor playgrounds can be made safe based on the actual occupants and their behaviour.   
The RISE report “Brannsikkerhet I lek- og aktivitetssenter” [3] from 2019 analysed the safety 
level for indoor playgrounds in Norway and Sweden and found that evacuation strategies for 
indoor playgrounds generally did not consider the children’s behaviour, social bonds, the effect 
of playground material, or the playground layout. The authors found that international research 
on the evacuation of children had been carried out, but not in a setting such as an indoor 
playground. They analysed existing playgrounds and their fire strategy reports and found that 
they needed deeper and better analyses in the future. 
Making new research on the evacuation of indoor playgrounds based on data from actual 
evacuations or realistic evacuation drills is difficult because yearly evacuation drills are not 
mandatory. Physical data collection in an indoor playground has been done at least one time for 
a master’s thesis by Maria Bernard Riise [1]. The data was not based on an evacuation drill but 
did result in data on children’s movement speed within playground equipment. The experiment 
was conducted during the Corona shutdown, and only 14 children participated. The drill had 
children from 4 to 11 years old and was conducted in an indoor playground in Odense called 
“Leo’s Legeland”. 
A master’s thesis from 2021 by Svenbjörg Sara Baldursdóttir [4] investigated how playground 
equipment affects evacuation. It looked at the difference in movement speed between adults 
and children. The data on children’s movement speed within playground equipment [1] was 
used, and the simulation used the same geometry and indoor playground in Odense.  
Most simulations were done with 100 occupants, and only two were done with the permitted

1.2 Danish legislation 
Buildings in Denmark are all given a “usage category” to determine the necessary fire protection 
required to comply with the Danish Building Regulations (BR18), Chapter 5 – Fire [9]. There are 
six categories in total. An indoor playground will usually be placed in usage category 3. In this 
category, the occupants are awake but unfamiliar with the building and its egress routes. This 
category is designed for occupants who can bring themselves to a secure place. Furthermore, 
buildings in the category will be used by many people and will have rooms for more than 50 
occupants.  
Using prescriptive solutions, usage category 3 does not always require an automatic fire alarm 
system, such as in usage category 6 (kindergartens, hospitals, and nursing homes). However, a 
manual alarm system is required if more than 150 occupants use the rooms. A similar 
requirement exists for schools in usage category 2.  
If the room or building is used in a way that compromises evacuation due to diminished visual 
clarity, the requirement is changed from a manual to an automatic alarm system. Indoor 
playgrounds are usually built with automated fire alarm systems. 
For an indoor playground in usage category 3, the alarm system will have a spoken message as 
part of the warning signal according to the prescriptive solutions for BR18. According to chapter 
8 in the guideline for BR18, chapter 5 [10], and to DBI Guideline 024 [11], a single repetition of 
the warning message must have a maximum playtime of 20 seconds. Normally, an attention 
signal will be preceded within 4-10 sec by a spoken message with a maximum duration of 10 
seconds. 
In the prescriptive solutions, the required number and placement of emergency exits are the 
same in all three usage categories (2, 3 and 6), where children are normally present. If the room 
has more than 150 occupants, the primary doors must have a minimum free width of 1.2 
metres, and all doors must open in the direction of the evacuation flow. Secondary exits must 
have a minimum free width of 0.77 cm. The total free width of all egress exits from a room must 
be at least 1 cm per person.  
The maximum allowed walking distance to the nearest exit in usage category 3 is 30 metres 
according to the prescriptive solutions to BR18 [2]. In 2014, when “Leo’s Legeland” was built, 
the prescriptive requirement was 25 metres.

1.3 Purpose and research questions 
This thesis aims to investigate the evacuation of an indoor playground by looking at social 
bonds, the number of children, playground equipment, and door choice. This thesis elaborates 
on the information acquired in the two earlier master’s theses about indoor playgrounds [1] [4]. 
The same case building, geometry, and maximum occupancy will be used. In this thesis, 
simulations will all be conducted in Pathfinder with a full occupant load of 800 persons and a 
mixed distribution of adults and children.  
Other studies [4] [12] have calculated evacuation time inside playground equipment as a 2D 
model (one level) with adult movement speed or as a 3D model using fictive geometry data. 
This thesis will combine speed data gathered about children with requirements for exits and 
distance from the standard, DS/EN 1176-10:2009, for fully enclosed play equipment [13] 
The simulation model in Pathfinder will have only one level, and when possible, use a “waiting 
period” that equals the min/max distance and min/max speed of the children. This defined 
“waiting period” can be used from the early design phases of a new building project, where 
playground equipment is not detailed.  
The thesis considers and investigates the following questions: 
  
How does the choice of egress door influence the evacuation time? 
o In the 2021 thesis, all simulations used random door choice. This thesis will have 
both simulations with free choice and with 2/3 of the occupancies using the main 
entrance. Is there a significant difference in evacuation time between the two 
strategies? 
 
How does the number of children affect the evacuation time?  
o Indoor playgrounds have summer camps and rooms dedicated to birthday parties. 
When these events occur, the ratio of children to adults will be higher than if all 
occupants consist of individual families. Is the evacuation affected by this change in 
ratio? 
 
How is the evacuation time affected by the family bond between the occupants? 
o Will the evacuation time change if the parents find their children and move towards 
the egress doors together (compared to standard evacuation calculation, where all 
occupants move to the exit alone)? 
o Will the evacuation time change if the parents and children have a shared 
understanding of a place to meet before moving towards the egress doors together 
(compared to standard evacuation calculation, where all occupants move to the exit 
alone)?

1.4 Limitations and author´s remarks 
This thesis deals with the evacuation of children and their parents in an unfamiliar setting of an 
indoor playground. The focus is on evacuation. Finding the available safe egress time is not part 
of this thesis. The thesis is also not about designing outdoor playgrounds. 
The results and conclusion only apply to the actual building being investigated. Changes to 
geometry, number of occupants, and size of playground areas will affect the evacuation times. 
The findings of the thesis, however, could be used as a guideline and inspiration for other 
indoor playgrounds.   
The playground equipment has been modelled as one level and as rooms with doors. A 
simulation with 3D playground equipment would be more accurate. The simplified standstill time 
used has the advantage that it can be used in designing an indoor playground building from the 
early design phases, where there will only be a 2D plan and not a 3D model available for the fire 
engineer.  
The functions used in Pathfinder to simulate social bonds are limited. The variations and 
scenarios have been adjusted to fit the possible input that can be chosen in Pathfinder.  
The waiting period before movement cannot be used on some members of a group and not 
others. All members of a group can only have the same behaviour. Therefore, simulations 
considering social bonds will be without a “waiting period” in playground equipment.  
The gathering points, which in Pathfinder are called Waypoints, are not “stronger” than the 
social bond in “movement groups”. As a result, some families will meet before they enter a 
waypoint, and some families will not have a “tight” group evacuation.    
It should also be mentioned that the user manual for Pathfinder [14] describes that specific data 
about grouping is generally unavailable in validation studies. Therefore, it is highly 
recommended to run simulations with and without grouped occupants to identify the impact of 
occupant groupings on evacuation times [14].

2. 
Fundamentals and data 
The theoretical background, fundamental input and data needed for investigating the research 
questions will be presented in this chapter. 
When working with fire safety and evacuation, the two essential terms are Required Safe 
Egress Time (RSET) and Available Safe Egress Time (ASET). 
RSET is defined as the time it takes from a fire starts until all occupants have evacuated the 
building (or evacuated to a safe place). The RSET is also referred to as the evacuation time.  
ASET is defined as the time from the fire starts until the conditions in the room/building become 
untenable. Untenable conditions can be caused by radiant heat, low visibility, toxic gases, etc. 
In general, ASET must be higher than RSET to have a secure building.  
The subject of ASET will only be described briefly in this thesis, and instead, the focus is on 
RSET for an indoor playground.

2.2 Detection time 
The first component/ time period in an evacuation is the detection time. For an indoor 
playground, there will typically be an automatic fire alarm system. With an automated alarm, the 
detection time typically depends on the type of detectors, the size of the fire, and the ceiling 
height.  
The standard detector type is an optical smoke detector, and the ceiling height in “Leo’s 
Legeland Odense” is approx. 7-7.5 metres (area with play equipment) according to the architect 
cross section drawing, which is an appendix to the building permit [16]. The detection time for a 
specific fire can be found in a FDS model, but this has not been made for this building. 
An indoor playground building in Malmö, Sweden, of similar size for 800 occupants did have an 
FDS model. In the fire strategy report [12], the ceiling height was generally defined as 5 metres, 
and the detectors were optical smoke detectors. The FDS simulation had ten detectors. The 
design fire was a 10 MW fire with a “fast” fire growth (NFPA). For this building, the detection 
time was 80-120 seconds. 
2.3 Warning time 
After the detection of a fire, the warning period starts.  
The warning time with a spoken message and an attentive signal will be approximately 20 
seconds. 
2.4 Reaction and decision time 
The reaction and decision period starts after having heard the warning signal or seen signs of a 
fire. As mentioned previously, the reaction and decision time is unique for an indoor playground. 
However, assessment of the reaction and decision time for an indoor playground can be based 
on e.g., the Danish building regulations (BR18), surveys/interviews, and evacuation drills made 
in schools. 
2.4.1 
Reaction and decision time according to BR18 
Chapter 8 in the guideline for BR18, chapter 5 [10], does not define a reaction and decision time 
for an indoor playground. The two categories that come closest are a shopping mall with a 
reaction and decision time of two minutes (120 seconds) and an educational building with a 
reaction and decision time of one minute (60 seconds).  
For all buildings, Chapter 8 in the guideline for BR18, chapter 5 [10], defines that if the fire is 
within the same room as the occupants, the reaction and decision time is reduced to one minute 
(60 seconds) and the warning time is reduced to 0 minutes. 
2.4.2 
Reaction and decision for the parents 
The reaction and decision time for adults have been widely resurged. There is however not 
much literature about reaction and decision time for the parents in an indoor playground.  
The RISE report “Brannsikkerhet i lek- og aktivitetssenter” from 2019 [3] presented relevant 
available literature about children’s and adults’ behaviour during an evacuation of indoor 
playgrounds. A report by N.R. Johnson, W.E. Feinberg, and D.M Johnson was used for the 
reaction and decision time for adults. This report describes a social bond between adults in 
evacuations. The article was based on interviews with survivors after a fire in 1977 at Beverly 
Hills Supper Club and showed that occupants would evacuate in groups rather than individually. 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
9 
 
 
A staff survey done for “Leo’s Legeland” by Riise [1] shows that being part of a group, 
specifically a family, affects the evacuation. The survey found that 63 % of the parents began to 
look for the children and not for the egress doors. The questions can be seen in Figure 2.2. 
 
Figure 2.2 – Reaction patterns among parents and responsible adults [1]. 
Riise suggested that a pamphlet received on arrival with instructions on how to react in the 
event of a fire and where to find the egress door could improve the evacuation time. 
In the master’s thesis by Baldursdóttir [4], the concept of searching time was described but not 
used because of a lack of experimental data. The “searching time” is the added time between 
the parent´s decision to leave the building and before they moved towards an egress door. 
It could be assumed that the reaction time is short. Nothing should motivate a parent more than 
the safety of their children. A time period between reaction and moment towards the exits 
seems relevant for an indoor playground.  
2.4.3 
Reaction and decision for children  
The reaction and decision process is different for children. Several studies have been done 
about the evacuation of children in schools [17] [18] [19] [20] [21] [22] [23] [24] [25]. These 
studies looked into movement time and reaction and decision for the children. They describe 
that children do not have a fully developed brain and will most likely not react as adults. They 
are vulnerable due to reduced cognitive and physical capabilities [18], resulting in limited 
abilities for assessing emergency situations and self-rescue skills. [25]

2.5 Pre-movement time  
The mentioned components/periods of “detection time”, “warning time”, and “reaction and 
decision time” give in total the pre-movement time. In this thesis, the pre-movement time does 
not include search time. Search time is simulated as part of the movement time. 
The conservative pre-movement time would be based on the above-mentioned maximum times 
and thereby as: 
- 
120 seconds detection time (playground building in Malmö [12]) 
- 
20 seconds warning time (DBI 027 [26]) 
- 
120 seconds reaction and decision time (BR18, BRV 8, time for a shopping centre [10]) 
Resulting in a pre-movement time of 120+20+120 = 260 seconds (4.3 minutes).

Alternatively, it could be as little as 60 seconds because the indoor playground building mainly 
consists of one big room where the occupants quickly smell and see a fire. Thereby following 
BR18, BRV 8 guidelines [10] and not adding detection and warning time. 
The pre-movement time could also be even less with highly motivated parents and children 
reacting in 46 seconds (Time from unannounced school drill of the not prepared last child in 
Table 2.2). 
2.6 Movement time

this period is, depends on the speed the occupants. 
Adults and children don’t have the same movement speed. There is a difference in both mean 
speed and in minimum and maximum speed. These differences will influence the evacuation of 
an indoor playground.

2.7 Choice of exit 
In indoor playgrounds, it is a requirement that children and their parents leave their shoes at the 
main entrance. Many might not exit the building before these shoes have been collected. 
Literature about school evacuations also shows that time is used to put on shoes and outdoor 
clothing before evacuation [20] [22] [24]. 
Much literature shows that many people evacuate using the door they know from entering the 
building and not through the nearest egress door. As described in the SFPE Guide to Human 
Behaviour in Fire [27], people who are not familiar with a building but members of the public 
may be more likely to attempt to leave by the route they entered unless they are directed 
otherwise by signs or systems. This choice of exit is seen in surveys and reflected in the Danish 
building regulations. 
2.7.1 
Employee Survey Leo’s Legeland 
In the master’s thesis by Riise [1], a survey involving staff at Leo’s Legeland in Denmark, 
Sweden and Norway was conducted. Regarding how many evacuated through the main 
entrance at the most recent evacuation, 31 % answered 50-75%, and 38% answered more than 
75 %.  The main entrance is observed as a main point for evacuation. 
Being part of a group and, specifically, a family affects the evacuation. This was confirmed in 
the survey [1] where 63 % answered that the parents began evacuation by looking for their 
children, not the exits. The children’s behaviour shows that only 44 % of them searched for the 
exit independently. 
In the master’s thesis by Maria Bernard Riise [1], it was suggested that a pamphlet received on 
arrival with instructions on how to react in the event of a fire and where to find the egress door 
could improve the evacuation time.

2.7.2 
Danish Building Regulations 
Chapter 8 in the guideline for BR18, chapter 5 [10], describes that for evacuation calculations, 
the primary entrances should be dimensioned for 2/3 of the total occupancy because people 
often seek the exit they know will lead to safety. This rule applies to all types of buildings. To 
deviate from this rule, the other exits must be made more attractive by means such as: 
• 
Egress doors have glass so that the outside can be seen. 
• 
Regular evacuation exercises. 
• 
People are familiar with building and use the egress system daily. 
• 
The building has staff that appoints the exits in an evacuation.  
 
Leo´s Legeland in Odense don’t have glass doors, regular evacuation drills or customers that 
use the egress system daily. 
2.8 Available Safe Egress Time – ASET 
The Available Safe Egress Time (ASET), i.e., the time until untenable conditions, for an indoor 
playground could be found through a fire and smoke simulation in FDS. An FDS simulation has 
not been made for “Leo’s Legeland Odense”. When identifying untenable conditions, three 
parameters are usually studied: The visibility, the temperature, and the heat radiation. 
The earlier mentioned indoor playground building in Malmø [12], where untenable conditions 
were found using an FDS model, had an ASET of 550 seconds with a ceiling height of 5 metres. 
The building had fire ventilation (roof lights) and no sprinkler system, just like Leo’s Legeland in 
Odense. The first acceptance criteria to fail was reduction of visibility (<10 metres).

3. 
Methodology and simulation setup 
Using the data from chapter 2, the case building containing an indoor playground is modelled in 
an evacuation program. The choice of programme and the setup of the programme will be 
presented in this chapter.  
3.1 Pathfinder 
Evacuation models can be made in many different programmes, each having unique 
specialities. The evacuation models can be made in a range between very simple movement 
models to complex ones depending on space representation, route choice and movement input. 
The program Pathfinder, version 2023.2 [14], [34], [35], will be used to study evacuation in the 
indoor playground. Pathfinder is an agent-based evacuation simulator where the occupants are 
represented as an upright cylinder on the movement mesh. Each occupant calculates 
movements independently.  
The program uses a continuous space model. A 3D triangulated mesh representing the 
geometry and not a cell division, facilitates continuous movement throughout the model. This 
model type enables the occupants to be located anywhere and the geometry to be more 
detailed and have curves if needed.  
Pathfinder’s steering mode will be used for the simulation. Steering mode bases occupant 
behaviour on interaction and the simulations are not based on calculation of specific flow rates 
[35]. The method considers the physical boundaries and collisions at queues located at doors 
and stairs. The algorithm of Pathfinder ensures that the occupants, to some degree, will change 
direction during evacuation if, for example, there is a queue and another exit without a queue is 
available. 
The Pathfinder model and simulations will use the inputs described below. The default settings 
will be used unless they are described otherwise. 
3.2 Average movement time 
The simulations will be without the pre-movement time and only be the movement time found in 
each scenario as the last occupant leaves the building. For some of the scenarios in this thesis 
the movement time includes time for searching before evacuation. In some studies, this would 
be part of the reaction and decision period, but in this thesis, the search time is part of the 
simulation and is considered part of the movement time. 
Each simulation is initially run a minimum of 10 times to evaluate on the movement time. As the 
coefficient of variation in all scenarios is more than 5 % and the variation spread at the same 
time is more than 10 seconds, 100 simulations were run for each case.

The children’s age, and thereby, their speed and behaviour, are based on a site visit and on 
“Leo’s Legeland” homepage [36].  
On the homepage of Leo’s Legeland, there are advertisements for Clubs for summer (6-9 years) 
and after-school time without parents (4-7 years). The age group of 4-9 is directly mentioned, 
but they also advertise birthday parties, typically for preschool children and, therefore, up to 12 
years old. Children younger than 4 years old might be present, but likely not playing alone and 
can be carried by a parent in an egress situation. Children older than 12 might accompany a 
younger sibling but will not be the typical occupant. The age group is chosen as 4-12 year-olds. 
Families in this thesis will be modelled as approximately 43 % with one child, 42 % with two 
children and 15 % with three children based on the statistics in section 2.9 on page 17. Only 
one parent will sometimes accompany the families’ children on a trip to an indoor playground. 
This was also seen on the site visit in “Leo’s Legeland Odense”. However, Martin Sommerlund, 
regional manager for “Leo’s Legeland”, describes the typical family in their playgrounds as 
having both the mother and father present. No statistics have been available about the 
numbers. The families in this thesis will have 50 % of the families with one adult and 50 % with 
two adults. Having more of the 800 occupants being children will provide a conservative 
estimation of the evacuation time as the children have a slower movement speed. 
To situations will be simulated to investigate the research question about the effects of more

3.5 Occupant size 
The occupant size in this thesis is based upon the guideline for BR18 [9], being an ellipse that is 
510 mm by 430 mm. Pathfinder does not use ellipses, and each occupant will be a cylinder with 
a diameter of 510 mm. At congestions, the occupants will be able to turn and walk sideways 
with a reduced diameter of 430 mm. In Pathfinder, this is programmed with a reduction factor of 
0.843. The height will be the standard Pathfinder value of 1.83 metres. 
The children will not be modelled with a diameter smaller than the adults. With the uncertainty in 
the distribution and size of today’s children, the choice of making them the same diameter as 
adults has been made. The children will take up more space in queues and go through doors 
more slowly. The evacuation time will be longer with a conservative and bigger size for the 
children.  
3.6 Occupant speed 
The walking speed for children will be modelled based on the results from the study by 
Hamilton, Lennon and O’Raw from 2017 [18], [19]. This was a large study, and the results were 
for children of the same age group of 4 to 12 years as in “Leo’s Legeland”. See also section 
2.6.2 on page 12. Therefore, the mean walking speed for children will be 1.46 m/s.  
All literature investigated for this thesis describes that younger children move slower than older 
children. The studies shown in section 2.6.2 on page 12 have higher or lower speeds than 1.46 
m/s depending on which age group participated in the studies. For studies of only younger 
children between 3-7 years [21], [22], [30], the mean speed was lower (0.81 m/s, 0.84 m/s and 
0.86 m/s, respectively). The mean speed was higher for studies with older children between 6-
12 years [17] (1.6 m/s). These studies indicate that the speed for a group with a mix of both 
younger and older children should be between 0.81 m/s and 1.6 m/s, which 1.46 m/s conforms 
with. 
The occupant speed for children within playground equipment will not be modelled as a 
changed speed. From section 2.6.3 on page 13, the average speed within playground 
equipment was between 0.4 m/s and 1.5 m/s.

3.7 Family Groups 
Searching for family members before exiting will be simulated. Based on the date from the 
survey by Riise [1] (section 2.7.1 on page 16), parents and children are often searching for each 
other instead of evacuating individually immediately. Therefore, the Pathfinder function “grouped 
movement” will be used to simulate social bonds. This function defines a group with a group 
leader and plain members. The members will not go to an exit but to the leader. They will leave 
for the same exit when all group members are together (connected group). The realistic 
scenario is that the parents will find the youngest child, not vice versa. Therefore, one of the 
children is defined as the leader. The leader can be given a “slowdown value” to decide if this 
occupant will move for a few seconds towards the exit before waiting for the members, however, 
this is not done in the simulations. The child (sometimes placed within the playground area) will 
not move before the other family members arrive. 
The group will have a “maximum distance” value that defines how much distance there can be 
between occupants to define the group as connected. The distance of 1,0 metre is used, and 
the group moves towards the exit if all members are within this distance of each other. Social 
distancing between group members is, by default, not enforced.

3.8 Go to waypoints/meeting points. 
Inspired by the idea of a pamphlet with instructions (cf. section 2.4.2 and 2.7.1), a concept of 
meeting points is simulated. The parents would, on arrival, be shown a map of six meeting 
points. The pamphlet and staff would explain that each family should choose a point as their 
base and evacuation meeting point. The point would be marked on the floor with a colour to be 
found easily. Near each point are sofas and seating areas where the parents could relax and 
have their bags.  
The children should be told about this point before leaving for the many play areas. Meeting at a 
specific point would replace the standard procedure, where parents and children would need to 
look at several play areas and sofa areas in search of each other.   
The nearest member of the “movement group”/family needs to get to the waypoint for the 
concept to work and the families not to meet and then move to the point together. It is 
impossible to ensure this 100 %, because the function “movement group” is the strongest. 
Different slowdown times for the leader have been tested to ensure the most realistic flow. 
Ensuring that not too many groups had the leader reach the egress door before the group 
reaches him/her. The different setups can be seen in Table 3.3. Setup B, with a smaller 
deviation and group flow looking reasonable, have been chosen to be compared with the other

4.1 Choice of scenarios 
The scenarios have been chosen to answer the following research questions:

How does the choice of egress door influence the evacuation time? 
 
-  How does the number of children affect the evacuation time?

How is the evacuation time affected by the family bond between the occupants? 
 
All simulations will be carried out with the maximum number of allowed occupants in the building

simulations will randomise occupant behaviour/speed, and the movement speeds will be as 
described in section 3.6. 
4.1.1 
How does the choice of egress door influence the evacuation time?

4.2 Number of simulations and the effect on movement time 
The number of simulations is highly relevant, with a large spread in the standard deviation, 
coefficient of variation and mean movement times. Results for 10, 50 and 100 simulations have 
been compared and can be seen in Table 4.2. 
The confidence interval number gets smaller with more simulations; the largest change is from 
10 to 50 simulations. The confidence interval only changes from an interval of 7-23 seconds (50 
sim.) to an interval of 5-15 seconds (100 sim.) An increase in simulations would therefore not 
change the confidence interval or movement time significantly.  
For scenarios with free door choice (X.1 and X.3), the median only changes with 0-4 seconds 
for an increase from 50 to 100 runs. For scenarios with a door choice of 2/3 to the main 
entrance (X.2 and X.4) the median changes slightly more, with 2-10 seconds. These intervals 
seem to be acceptable for the scenarios investigated and the total movement times that are 
compared.  
The coefficient of variation is between 8 and 29 % and generally doesn’t get smaller with more 
simulations. The variation caused by the randomised speed distribution is more than a few % 
because the children have a larger speed range than adults (0.18 - 4.41 m/s) and because the 
evacuation distance also varies from a few meters to over 25 meters. The highest coefficient of 
variation is found in the scenarios with free door choice, where queue time is a minor part of the 
total movement time.

5.2 The effect of exit choice - Comparison individual evacuation 
To look at the research question about the effect of door choice, the simulations in scenarios 
1.1-1.4 are compared. Scenarios 1.1 and 1.3 have free door choice and all 11 egress doors can 
be used. As expected, these simulations have the fastest movement times with about 2 minutes 
(108-124 seconds).  
Scenarios 1.2 and 1.4, where 2/3 of the occupants evacuate through the main entrance are 
significantly slower. The two scenarios are very similar and have movement times of around 8 
minutes (481-486 seconds). This type of evacuation is relevant for most buildings with 
occupants who are unfamiliar with the egress system. For an indoor playground where all 
occupants (adults and children) leave their shoes at the main entrance, finding the added 
movement time for this route choice is especially interesting. For individual evacuation, with 
many using the main entrance this was an increase of up to 350 % or 6 minutes  
(378 seconds). Therefore, the effect of exit choice plays an important role in the total movement 
time.  
All movement times and comparisons can be seen in Table 5.1 on page 37. 
Boxplot of scenarios 1 can be seen in Figure 5.14. Scenarios with 2/3 evacuation to the main 
entrance (scenarios 1.2 and 1.4) have a larger spread of results but still a lower coefficient of 
variation. The movement times are significantly longer than those for free door choice. 
 
Figure 5.14 – Boxplot scenarios 1 
Extra time for evacuation within playground equipment doesn’t seem to be a critical factor. In 
Scenario 1.3 and Scenario 1.4, children in playground equipment have been given the very 
conservative profile of waiting up to 45 seconds, thereby having a speed as low as 0.4 m/s for 
up to 18 metres. With extra evacuation time within playground areas, the time is increased 
slightly for free door choice and decreased a little for 2/3 through the main entrance. The time is 
changed by 5-16 seconds. As seen in the boxplot in Figure 5.14, “the boxes” (first quartile to 
third quartile) overlap, which indicates that the difference is insignificant. 
The slight time decrease for scenario 1.4 seems justified as the prolonged evacuation from the 
playground areas results in later arrival at the queue areas at the main entrance. The time spent 
in queues is thereby smaller. Not having an exact playground geometry or knowing of exactly 
how much time to add when deciding on the evacuation strategy does not seem critical when 
the time added to the total movement time is only 5-16 seconds.

5.9 General discussion 
Having looked at all the simulations and compared the relevant scenarios to each other, the 
research questions have been answered. Many of the results were as expected. Meeting points 
were unexpectedly not a faster way to evacuate the 800 occupants. Meeting points might still be 
an improvement for places with smaller amounts of occupants, but not for Leos Legeland 
Odense. The effect of an increased number of children was as expected for scenarios with 
individual evacuation to any door. Still, it was surprising that the combination with changed door 
choice or delay in play equipment resulted in faster or similar movement times.  
In this thesis, the focus has been on the movement time. However, the total evacuation time/ 
required safe egress Time (RSET) is relevant when investigating the indoor playground safety 
level. The pre-movement time must be added to the simulated movement times to find the 
evacuation time. As described in Chapter 2, the pre-movement time consists of detection time, 
warning time and reaction & decision time. This time period could be as long as 260 seconds if 
the general guidelines are used, and the movement time starts when the warning time and 
reaction & decision time are over.  
For individual evacuation, this results in about 6 minutes (108+260=368 seconds) for 
evacuation to any door and 12.5 minutes (486+260=746 seconds) if the main entrance is used 
by 2/3 of the occupants. For evacuation with social bonds, the evacuation time is increased to 
7,5 minutes (198+260=458 seconds) and 13.5 minutes (542+260=802 seconds).  
The available safe egress time (ASET) has not been found for the case building (Leos Legeland 
Odense). It could be assumed to be similar to the earlier mentioned indoor playground in Malmø 
and be around 9 minutes (550 seconds). Compared to this ASET time, all results from scenarios 
where many used the main entrance showed a critical safety level between ASET and RSET. 
The pre-movement time might be estimated to be shorter because the occupants can quickly 
see and smell a fire, and the parents are highly motivated to react & decide. If the time of 46 
seconds is added, the evacuation time would be 214 seconds shorter.  
For individual evacuation, this results in a time of about 9 minutes (532 seconds), where the 
main entrance is used by 2/3 of the occupants. For evacuation with social bonds, the 
evacuation time is 4 minutes (244 seconds) and about 10 minutes (588 seconds). This pre-
movement time would bring the evacuation time under 9 minutes for all but the scenario with 
social bonds and queues at the main entrance. Generally, the scenarios where the evacuation 
is 2/3 through the main entrance are still critical or have a minimum safety margin.   
Looking at the high coefficient of variation (19-29%) for evacuation to any door, these scenarios 
should have a larger safety margin. The scenarios are much more likely to have a movement 
time on a day of a fire that could be as much as the maximum times and thereby 1.5 - 4 minutes 
slower.  
Adding the possibility of a blocked door would increase the movement times and lower the 
difference between evacuation time and critical conditions even further. Time for putting on 
shoes and overcoats would also increase the movement time as documented in literature about 
school evacuations. [20] [22] [24].

The results presented in this thesis have limitations and uncertainties. The input for the 
simulations could have been done differently. The starting points, the number of children in 
each area and the construction of each family had to be chosen, and a different setup could 
result in different outputs.  
To answer the research questions and test meeting points, two functions in Pathfinder were 
combined, and the result wasn’t as realistic as hoped. Generally, the function works, but all 
groups did not evacuate together, and all runs had a few children evacuated alone late in the 
simulations, giving uncertainty to the results about meeting points.  
The output clearly showed that the queue areas were essential to the movement times. The 
results would have been quite different if fewer occupants had been put in the simulations. Most 
days, a typical indoor playground will have fewer visitors, resulting in smaller queues and faster 
movement times. With 800 occupants, the results are conservative but cover the most critically 
allowed situation.

6. 
Conclusion 
The total movement time for an indoor playground was investigated using evacuation models in 
Pathfinder. Multiple scenarios examined the influence of social bonds, the number of children, 
time in playground equipment and door choice.  
Simulations showed that the social bond and the exit choice significantly influenced the 
movement time. The likely scenario of families finding each other before evacuation and then 
using the main entrance took five times as long as individual evacuation to any door. Increasing 
the number of children did have an impact, but depending on door choice, the effect was either 
an increase or a decrease. Adding time in the play equipment to simulate more prolonged 
evacuation from these areas did not result in significantly longer movement time. Simulations 
with meeting points did not have faster movement times compared to simulations where families 
found each other without meeting points. Many people moving in opposite directions in small 
areas make this a slower way for families to meet and evacuate. 
The safety level in an indoor playground was very much affected by changing the simulations 
from individual evacuation to groups with social bonds. Exit choice even significantly affected 
the safety level for individual evacuation. The results show that the safety margin between 
RSET and ASET most likely doesn’t exist or is minimal if the evacuation simulations do not use 
individual evacuation and free door choice.  
The movement time could probably be improved with more training of the occupants or with a 
lower limit for the maximum number of occupants. 
The study had the following findings and answers to research questions: 
Choice of egress door and the effect on evacuation time 
The effect of exit choice plays an essential role in the total movement time. If 2/3 of the 
occupants choose to evacuate through the main entrance where their shoes are located, the 
evacuation time is increased significantly. 
The movement times were increased by about 6-8 minutes. 
• 
For individual evacuation, with many using the main entrance, this was an increase of 
350 % or 6 minutes (378 seconds). 
• 
For group evacuation, with many using the main entrance, this increased by  
173 % or 5.7 minutes (344 seconds) compared to groups going to any door and  
402 % or 7.2 minutes (434 seconds) compared to individual evacuation to any door. 
 
Increased ratio of children and the effect on the evacuation time 
The effect of an increased number of children is seen to play a minor role but still a role in the 
total movement time.  
The two numbers of children were based on the most realistic ratios of children to adults that a 
typical family day (53 %) and a day with birthday parties or camps (64 %) would have. 
The movement times were increased/decreased by a little under a minute. 
• 
For an increased number of children evacuated to any exit, this increased by about 
46-47 seconds. 
• 
For an increased number of children, with many using the main entrance, this was a 
decrease of 38-51 seconds. 
 
==PAGE:78==

Simulating family bonds and the effect on the evacuation time? 
The effect of family bonds also plays an important role in the total movement time. 
The simulation where families evacuate together significantly influences the movement time with 
about 1 – 1.5 minutes. 
• 
For groups with social bonds and evacuated to any exit, this increased by  
83 % or 1.5 minutes (90 seconds). 
• 
For groups with social bonds and many using the main entrance, this increased by 
12 % or 1 minute (56 seconds) compared to individual evacuation and many using the

• 
Having meeting points doesn’t result in a faster evacuation than groups finding each 
other inside play equipment. The increase was 21-23 seconds.  
 
 
7. 
Outlook 
More knowledge about the evacuation of indoor playgrounds is needed. The following 
investigations are recommended after doing this thesis and reading the available data.  
• 
More interviews should be done with employees and parents who have experienced an 
actual evacuation from an indoor playground. They should be asked the following. 
o 
Could an evacuation game for the children be implemented and give value? 
o 
Would pamphlets, signs or meeting points give value in their opinion?  
o 
Would thought-through seating areas around the play areas change the evacuation? 
o 
Could the warning message be improved? 
o 
Do the parents train their children for evacuation situations? 
• 
More simulations need to be made. 
o 
What would change if the children were given a smaller diameter based on research 
from school evacuation drills? 
o 
What would change if the time for putting on shoes and overcoats is added to the 
simulations? 
o 
The function of groups can be set up in different ways. Would other setups change 
the evacuation time?  
o 
Would the evacuation time change if the playground equipment was modelled in 3D?
