Wind energy MSc - Thesis
Abstract
The roughness induced by the corrosion in steel surfaces, such as the monopiles from
the oﬀshore wind energy industry, can initiate the process of corrosion fatigue, thus reducing
the lifetime of the structure. In this project, the eﬀect of this surface roughness induced
by corrosion is evaluated, using diﬀerent formulas to assess it. In Section 1 of this project,
the roughness parameters applied are displayed. Section 2 deals with the diﬀerent formulas
to obtain the elastic stress concentration factor (SCF) (Neuber, Arola-Ramulu and Cheng),
as well as the equations needed to obtain the eﬀective fatigue SCF with them. A formula
from a DNV recommended practice used to evaluate the roughness in welds is also used to
evaluate its validity in a corroded sample.
In Section 3, a method to produce a ﬁnite element model of a corroded sample from CT-
scans is developed, using diﬀerent commercial software. First the steel surface is extracted
in Avizo, it is prepared for ﬁnite element modelling in Geomagic Wrap, and ﬁnally imported
in Abaqus, where the ﬁnite element analysis is carried out. The elastic SCF is extracted
from diﬀerent notches, as well as the notch root radius of the notches, and the eﬀective
fatigue SCF is calculated. The roughness parameters of the same sample are obtained with
a Python script, and the eﬀective fatigue stress concentration is calculated using Neuber,
Arola-Ramulu and the formula from the DNV standard. It is observed that Arola-Ramulu
yields the best agreement with the ﬁnite element analysis, underestimating slightly the eﬀect
of the roughness with a deviation of -0.53%, the DNV formula underestimate it further with
an error of -3.42%, and Neuber, with a deviation of 15.79%,is not able to predict it correctly.
In order to check the validity of these equations, artiﬁcial samples are produced and
evaluated in Section 4.
First, diﬀerent samples with a rough surface with a sinusoidal
shape are analyzed, and it is seen how as the ratio between the amplitude and wavelength
increases the accuracy decrease. Cheng formula yields the best results for the eﬀective fatigue
stress concentration, and Arola-Ramulu initially underestimate it but later increase its value
exponentially as the ratio is increased, increasing the error too. Similarly, the relation from
the DNV standard underestimate the eﬀect of the roughness but ends up increasing its value,
and it is seen that only the depth of the notches aﬀects in this case. The eﬀect of the thickness
of the sample evaluated is assesed too, observing that the elastic SCF increase as the sample
is thinner. Finally, more samples are created with a roughness produced by a superposition
of diﬀerent sinusoidal proﬁles. The results from Arola-Ramulu are improved, with similar
results to Cheng equation. This analysis has to be extended further, with samples with a
roughness produced with sinusoidal curves in two directions, in order to observe if the results
with Arola-Ramulu keep on improving.

Introduction
1.1
The oﬀshore wind energy industry
Since the ﬁrst oﬀshore wind turbine was placed in 1991, the oﬀshore wind industry has
not stopped growing. The ﬁrst oﬀshore turbine was installed in Nogersund, Sweden, and
was a single small turbine, with a capacity of 220 KW. In the same year, the ﬁrst oﬀshore
wind farm was built in Vindeby, Denmark, consisting of 11 turbines of 450 KW, resulting
in a total installed power of 4.95 MW [2]. In 2019, Europe already had 22,072 MW of in-
stalled capacity in oﬀshore wind farms [3]. Oﬀshore wind energy was originally developed
in Europe, but nowadays other markets are opening to it, such as the United States, China,
Japan, Vietnam, Korea and Taiwan [4]. It is still a technology in development, and as the
knowledge in the industry is increased new wind farms can be placed in deeper waters or fur-
ther away from the shore. These new wind farms come with increased costs, but is expected
to see a reduction in the cost of the energy produced with them as more wind farms are in-
stalled [5], in order for this new wind farms to be price competitive with other energy sources.
The main advantage of moving the wind turbine to oﬀshore locations, compared to the
traditional onshore wind farms, is that the winds are usually higher, with lower turbulence
and shear, thus increasing the energy production in comparison with onshore sites. The
regulation in oﬀshore also allows to have bigger turbines, increasing the production, and
the resistance of the stakeholders is usually lower. The main disadvantages of oﬀshore wind
farms are the increased costs of having to introduce structures to maintain the turbines
standing in the sea, as well as the increase in the rest of the installation costs, such as cables
or electric substations, due to have to install it oﬀshore. The cost of repairing the turbines is
increased too, and as the turbines are less accessible, the windows to repair them are more
narrow, thus increasing the downtime of the turbine in case of failure and therefore this can
cause temporal reductions in the energy production.
The diﬀerent marine substructures to maintain the wind turbine can be divided in two
big categories: ﬁxed and ﬂoating. Among the ﬁxed turbines, the most common system uti-
lized is the monopile [3], a single beam connecting the tower of the turbine to the seabed.
This system is the most simple one, and can be used in relatively shallow waters. In more
deep waters other types of structures can be used, such as jackets, tripodes, or tripiles. In
case of having shallow waters, but complications to install a monopile due to, for example,
problems with the soil, it is also possible to use gravity based structures [6, 7].
Floating wind turbines are a very promising technology, that will allow to allocate wind
farms in places where ﬁxed ones are not feasible, but it is still inmature. Only 45 MW
were installed by 2019 in all Europe, with 274 MW already commissioned and planned to be
installed before 2022 [3]. Despite this, ﬂoating wind turbines are expected to develop further
in the following years, reducing costs and becoming economically viable as the oﬀshore wind
industry continue its development.

1.2
Corrosion fatigue
1.2
Corrosion fatigue
Oﬀshore steel structures, such as the ones sustaining the wind turbines, can suﬀer from
corrosion as they are placed in an aggressive environment. In this report, steel structures
produced with steel with strength class S355 and higher are considered, with approximately
0.2% of carbon content. Corrosion fatigue (CF) is a failure mechanism driven by a synergy
between the presence of an aggressive environment and cyclic loads [8], which in the case
of oﬀshore wind turbine substructures are the harsh environmental conditions given by the
sea and the loads produced by the wind and waves [9], respectively. CF can be fundamental
when assessing the lifetime of an structure, as, if not correctly protected, the material can
suﬀer a reduction in its fatigue strength [8]. This can cause huge losses in the industry,
due to the failure of these structures. In order to reduce these losses, diﬀerent protection
methods, such as cathodic protection, are applied to the oﬀshore structure [10].
In metals, fatigue failure is usually initiated in the surface of the structure, induced by
cyclic loads in it. In wind turbines, the structures are expected to last between 20 and 25
years, and therefore these structures have to sustain high cycles counts. In these cases, the
fatigue failure is produced with small stresses compared to the yield stress of the material,
but high number of cycles, at least 10000, and it is called high cycle fatigue (HCF). The cyclic
loads induce the movements of dislocations, that enable intrusion-extrusion mechanisms in
the surface of the steel and end up producing cracks in it. A macrocrack then evolves due
to an stress concentration in the surface, that could be due to a notch in the structure or
the presence of defects in the material [10]. In the case of CF, this notch could actually be
formed by pitting corrosion of the material, a localized attack that creates a deep pit in its
surface. These pits are the worst case scenario when it comes to fatigue assessment, as they
can cause severe problems in the structure even though the material removed is very small
compared to other types of corrosive attacks [8]. This type of localized attack can be caused
due to inclusion of diﬀerent elements in the alloy forming the material, or due to breakdown
of the protective ﬁlm that many materials have, such as the alumina (aluminium oxide) in
the aluminium, due to the deformation of the material produced by the cyclic loads [8, 10].
Rough surfaces, such as surfaces that have been corroded, also originate stress con-
centration due to its complex geometry, thus reducing the fatigue lifetime of the material.
Evaluating this stress concentration is the main topic that is developed in this thesis. Some
research dealing with the topic of CF from a fracture mechanics perspective have already
been done, analyzing the pits produced by pitting corrosion and evaluating the reduction
of the fatigue lifetime of steel, and it is still a hot spot of international research, but this is
beyond the scope of this report.
Diﬀerent standards to assess the fatigue life of marine structures have been developed
for almost 50 years, since the ﬁrst standard for fatigue design was published in 1974, and they
are constantly updated as more data is available [11], but there is still a lack of information
regarding the eﬀects of CF in the oﬀshore structures. Also, many operating oﬀshore wind
turbines are designed using standards derived from the oil and gas industry, with diﬀerent

1.3
Roughness eﬀect
The aggressive environment produces a generalized corrosive attack in the material if it
is not suﬃciently protected, which induces an irregular and rough surface. This rough sur-
face induce stress concentration, initiating the cracks that originate the fatigue failure, and
therefore the fatigue lifetime of the structures is reduced by it. An equation to obtain the
reduction of the fatigue lifetime in steel with the surface roughness produced by corrosion is
to the best knowledge of the author not available in the open literature, but some researches
have been conducted dealing with the eﬀect of the roughness produced by other means, such
as machined surfaces. For this reason, the theoretical framework developed for machined
surfaces is presented in this section, assuming that it can be applicable to corroded surfaces.
Neuber [12] elaborated an expression able to predict the elastic stress concentration
factor (SCF) as a function of the average roughness of the surface as well as the notch root
radius. The elastic SCF is deﬁned as the factor that is used to multiply to the nominal stress
in locations with complex geometry, such as notches, considering that the deformation pro-
duced by it is only elastic, with no permanent deformation. Taylor [13] showed that diﬀerent
surface roughness proﬁles produced by machining of the material aﬀect to the fatigue limit.
Following a similar approach to Neuber, Arola [14] estimated the elastic stress concentration
of machined surfaces implementing diﬀerent roughness parameters and obtained from it the
eﬀective fatigue SCF. The eﬀective fatigue SCF can be deﬁned as a factor that has to be
multiplied to the nominal load when assessing the fatigue lifetime in a surface with complex
geometry. The results of the eﬀective fatigue SCF showed that as the roughness average is
increased the HCF lifetime is reduced, and these results were checked with real fatigue tests.
A surface roughness factor parameter has also been developed [15], where the fatigue
strength of a low alloy steel in a marine environment can be modiﬁed as a function of the
ten point height of the surface.
This surface roughness factor is used to assess directly
the reduction in the fatigue strength of a specimen, compared to a case where the surface
is completely smooth. The expression for the surface roughness factor is later adapted to
use the average roughness of the surface [16], as it is a more convenient parameter to measure.
Itoga [17] demonstrated that the surface roughness aﬀects to the stress-number of cy-
cles curve (S-N curve) of steel, where an empiric relation between the number of cycles that
the material can have before failure and the cyclic load applied is presented. The study
demonstrated that the surface roughness decreases the lifetime of the steel, by reducing the
transition stress in a step wise S-N curve. In new researches, the reduction of fatigue re-
sistance due to the eﬀect of surface roughness has also been observed [18]. The eﬀect of
other roughness parameters, such as the distribution and shape parameters (skewness and
kurtosis, respectively [19]) has also been reported to be indicative of the fatigue strength

Det Norske Veritas, in the recommended practice for oﬀshore steel structures [21], use

Cheng [23] developed a method to obtain the elastic SCF in a machined surface with a
regular roughness, without the need to obtain the notch root radius, assimilating the surface
to a sinusoidal curve. The notch root radius is a parameter key to deﬁne the elastic SCF, and
is used to represent the curvature in the notch where it is maximum. Later, the validity of
his approach for machined surfaces was corroborated and conﬁrmed with real fatigue tests,
as well as with ﬁnite element analysis [24].
Procedures to reproduce the eﬀect of the aggressive environment in a laboratory have
been tested, in order to manufacture samples with a roughness that simulates the surface
roughness produced by corrosion [18]. The aim of these procedures is to make it easier to
obtain samples that are equivalent to the ones that can be obtained in marine structures,
but in shorter time spans. This can increase the number of samples available for fatigue
tests, and therefore help to increase the knowledge in this topic faster.
In this project, a set of the proposed equations will be used to evaluate the eﬀective
fatigue SCF of a corroded sample with the surface roughness parameters. The results are
compared with ﬁnite element analysis simulations in order to evaluate their validity, and a
procedure to obtain the ﬁnite element model from computerize tomography scans (CT-scans)
is developed. The aim is to check if the equations provided can be used in a case where the
roughness is produced by a corrosive attack in the surface of steel, such as the one that
the marine structures in the oﬀshore wind industry can suﬀer. The diﬀerent equations are
actually used to assess the eﬀective fatigue SCF in other cases, such as machined surfaces
or welds, but are considered here to check their validity in a corroded sample.

2.3.2
Preparing the surface for meshing with Geomagic wrap
The surface is introduced in Geomagic wrap, in order to prepare it for meshing in Abaqus.
First, some boundaries are created in the model, separating the diﬀerent plain faces and the
corroded surface. These boundaries are created to avoid that the following steps produce any
modiﬁcation in the separation between the diﬀerent faces of the sample. In order to do it,
the Curves Extract tool, under the Curves tab, is used. The curve sensitivity, separator sen-
sitivity and minimum area are set as 60, 70 and 500, respectively. The curvature sensitivity
is a parameter that determines the relative ﬂatness allowed between two surfaces to consider
them parts of the same region, increasing the number of regions created as this number is
increased. The separator sensitivity represents the width of the separation between regions,
and the last parameter indicates the minimum area that a surface must have in order to
determine it as an individual surface delimited by a curve.
Diﬀerent regions appear in the corroded surface, so they have to be manually put to-
gether. This step can be avoided by decreasing the curve sensitivity, but then some of the
plain surfaces might not be considered as individual regions. The diﬀerent regions that ap-
pear on the corroded surface are merged using the Merge Regions function and selecting
them manually. The curves are then extracted using the default parameters, and ﬁnally
converted into boundaries, using the convert to - boundaries tool, again under the Curves
tab.
This process also helps to prepare the analysis in Abaqus, as it assures that the diﬀerent
plain surfaces have patches following their borders, so the sets and surfaces required there
are bound to the geometry instead of to the mesh, facilitating the mesh sensitivity study
with diﬀerent discretization levels.
After that, the number of polygons that deﬁne the model has to be reduced to facilitate
the processing of the data. These polygons are deﬁned in Geomagic Wrap as triangles formed
by the intersection of three line segments [32]. These triangles form the faces of tetrahedrons,
that in the end form the mesh of the model. This reduction is done with the decimate tool,
in the polygons tab. The polygons are reduced stating a percentage of polygons that have
to remain after the decimate, set as 25% in this case. If the number of polygons is decreased
further, the accuracy of the mesh would be reduced. The Fix Boundaries function is also
applied in this step, in order to assure that the previously created boundaries remain the
same after the reduction of elements.

The surface is ﬁnally imported in Abaqus as a part, joining all the patches of the sample
generated in the same part during the import. The model is not scaled, so it uses pixel as
its length unit, and this unit will be used for the rest of the analysis in Abaqus. As Abaqus

conversions are produced before introducing them in the software.
A linear elastic and
isotropic material is created, with a Young’s modulus of 210 GPa and poisson ratio of 0.3, in
order to represent a steel. A section is created with this material and assigned to the part,
and then the volume of the sample is obtained, in order to later obtain the load applied to
the sample. An independent instance is then created, where the mesh can be produced. The
boundary conditions and the load are directly applied to the instance without the need of
the mesh, in order to be able to repeat the simulations with diﬀerent mesh discretization
levels. A step is created, in order to set the load and the stress analysis. The step used in this
project is set as static, linear perturbation, in order to set a static stress analysis. This type
of step allows to introduce the required stress and boundary conditions, and ignore the eﬀect
of time dependent material properties [33]. When the jobs with the diﬀerent simulations are
created, the default parameters are used, except in the precision tab, where the nodal output
precision is set as full.

To calculate the elastic SCF, the stress obtained from Eq. 17 is applied to one of the
plain faces of the models, as seen in Fig. 5 and it becomes the reference stress. The stress is
then analyzed in the surface, the maximum values are extracted, and divided by the reference
stress to obtain the elastic SCF. As the reference stress is the one applied to the sample,
it is important to set it up properly. Since the surface is rough the section is not constant
along the length of the sample. If the load is applied as an stress in one of the plain faces,
the results of the SCF in the sample depend on the area of the surface where it is applied.
Thus, if the area where the load is applied is modiﬁed, the stress analysis of the model will
give diﬀerent results. In a more standard case, where no roughness in the corroded surface
is present, this eﬀect is not observed as the cross section area is constant along the length
of the sample. In this case, if the stress is applied in the opposite surface of the one where
it is initially applied, the results are exactly the same, as the area of the surface where it is
applied is the same, and therefore it is equivalent to the initial case. But in the case of a
corroded surface, when the load is applied in the opposite surface of the model, instead of
having the same results they will change according to the area of the surface, having bigger
stress in the case where the surface where the stress is applied is bigger, and therefore the
SCF calculated is higher too, even if in reality it is not increased. To avoid this problem, it
is decided to calculate a mean section of the sample, with the following expression, in which
the length of the sample is deﬁned in Fig. 5:
Asample = Vsample/Lsample
(18)
A load that apply a force in the surface equivalent to the applied stress in that mean
section is calculated, and applied as a tensile force in a master node situated in one of the
plain surfaces. The master node is created as a point part, as seen in Fig. 4, and the rest of
the nodes in the surface are attached to it as constrained, limiting the displacement in the
three directions.
The irregular surface created by the corrosion of the steel, displayed in Fig. 4 also
generates another issue for the application of the load. Since the thickness of the sample
is variable along the section where the load is applied due to the surface roughness, the
force applied is not equally distributed. This cause that a higher load is applied where the
thickness is higher, producing some perturbations in the results of the rest of the sample.
This eﬀect is considered to be small and neglected in this report, but in order to have more
accurate results in Abaqus this issue should be solved. In order to reduce this eﬀect in the

2.3.3.3
Mesh sensitivity study
In order to determine the reﬁnement of a mesh, the seeds applied to it can be modiﬁed.
The seeds are marks in the diﬀerent edges or curves deﬁning the geometry of the model that
are used to create the mesh, and therefore the number of seeds determine the total number
of elements of the mesh. If the size of the seeds is decreased, the number of seeds in an
edge is increased, thus increasing the number of elements present in the mesh and therefore
increasing its reﬁnement. Diﬀerent seeds are applied to produce a mesh sensitivity study,
and observe the necessary reﬁnement of the mesh. The element used for meshing is a second
order tetrahedron, and the type of solid element selected is C3D10. This element type is
chosen as it is suitable for meshing complex shapes and producing static stress analysis in
them, and also because other types of elements, as wedge elements, might give issues to mesh
the imported .stp ﬁle. The C represents that the element is used for analysis of continuum
stress or displacement, the 3D represents that is a structure with three dimension, and the
10 indicates the number of nodes that it contains [1]. The ﬁrst 4 nodes are located in the
vertices of the tetrahedron as seen in Figure 7, while the remaining nodes are located in
the centre location of the edges [35]. As it is a second order element, there is no risk of
hourglassing in the structure [1].

2.3.4
Stress analysis
In order to avoid boundary eﬀects, the points close to the borders of the sample are not
taken into account for the analysis. For a node to be considered valid to look out for an
elastic SCF it should be at a distance of every border of at least the mean thickness of the
sample, as previously stated.
To extract the diﬀerent elastic SCFs, the Create display group tool is used, selecting
approximately only the evaluated area.
The maximum value of the stress in the main
direction, in this case σ22, is obtained, and the coordinates of the node are evaluated, in
order to make sure that it is not out of the area that is evaluated. The stress in the 22
direction is used in this analysis, as it is the correspondent to the direction where the load is
applied (Y axis), as seen in Fig. 5. The quality of the mesh is checked again, to make sure
that the nodes evaluated are not inside of any distorted element. If the node evaluated is
unable to pass any of this two checks, it is removed from the display group and not considered
in the analysis. After a point is extracted, it is eliminated from the display group in order
to ﬁnd a new maximum value in the sample, and the process is repeated until 10 points are
obtained. Finally, the stress concentration can be obtained by dividing the diﬀerent values
of the stress extracted from Abaqus by the reference stress applied, 150 MPa.

Discussion
4.1
Corroded sample
In all the results discussed, the values numerically obtained from Abaqus are assumed
to be the most reliable, and therefore they are used as a benchmark for the rest of the results
obtained. If fatigue tests are conducted, their results would be the ones used as a bench-
mark to evaluate the eﬀective fatigue SCF, but this is out of the scope of this thesis. The
elastic SCF is obtained with a high level of accuracy with Arola-Ramulu equation (-1.8%),
according to Tab. 5, but the result is slightly lower than the one obtained through ﬁnite
element analysis with Abaqus. Therefore, the SCF in the sample is underestimated using
this method. Using Neuber equation, the elastic SCF is no longer underestimated, but the
diﬀerence with the Abaqus results is too large (89.87%) to consider this a valid approach to
obtain the elastic SCF in this particular case. The issue behind this overestimation might
be the assumption that the parameter λ can be considered to be 1
Similarly, the eﬀective SCF, displayed in Tab. 6, with Arola-Ramulu is sligthly under-
estimated (-0.53%), but very close to the results from Abaqus, while the results obtained
with Neuber (15.79%) are too far to be considered valid. Again, the issue causing this big
error can be the assumption of λ = 1.
The result using the DNV standard equation is also very similar to the one obtained
with Arola-Ramulu, but the eﬀective SCF is actually underestimated further (-3.41%). As
this equation comes from a design standard, it is expected to overestimate the eﬀect of the
roughness, in order to make sure that the predictions are always on the safe side. One of
the reasons why this value is being underestimated is that the DNV formula compares the
eﬀective fatigue SCF with a case where the average roughness of the sample is 3.2 microns,
not with a completely smooth case. Because of this, the eﬀective fatigue SCF calculated
with it should be lower than the other cases evaluated, as those are relative to a completely
smooth surface. This underestimation in the DNV standard might still be valid, as the S-N
curve that is later used to assess the fatigue lifetime of the steel corresponds to a surface with
an average roughness of 3.2 microns. Since the S-N curve used is obtained with a steel with
the same average roughness that is used as a benchmark in the DNV standard equation, the
eﬀect of this underestimation might be corrected when obtaining the fatigue lifetime. This
can be checked by doing a fatigue test on the sample, and evaluating its fatigue lifetime with
the mentioned S-N curve, but this is not investigated in this project.
Another reason causing this underestimation could be the fact that the formula is de-
veloped for a surface that has a roughness approximately regular, without huge variations in
the pits depth, and thus it only uses the roughness average as an input parameter to estimate
the eﬀect of the surface roughness. On the other hand, Arola-Ramulu includes a term that
can reﬂect the eﬀect of the irregularities in its equation. The second part of the equation (Eq.
9), with the relation between the peak to valley height and the 10-point height, can somehow
account for the fact that the surface roughness is not regular, with some notches and peaks

The ﬁrst thing that should be noted about the results in Tab. 7 is the fact that the peak
to valley height and the 10-point height have the exact same values. In the end, these two
parameters measure the same thing in the rough surface, but the 10-point height includes the
5 maximum peaks and minimum valleys, instead of just picking one of each. As the surface is
regular following a sinusoidal shape, both the maximum peak and lowest valley are repeated
along its length, as seen in Fig. 9a, and thus the 10-point height use ﬁve times the same
values used for the peak to valley height, therefore having the same result. Also, since the
proﬁle used to obtain the roughness is known to be sinusoidal, the maximum height can be
set as equal to the amplitude and the minimum equal to minus the amplitude. Therefore, the
peak to valley height can be set as twice the amplitude. If Eq. 4, which deﬁnes the 10-point
height, is used taking this into account, the result obtained is again twice the amplitude,
and thus it can be concluded that both roughness parameters are the same for any given case.
Similarly, Eq. 3 is not really needed to calculate the roughness average of these samples.

Conclusions
• A method to obtain a ﬁnite element model from a set of images obtained with CT-
scans containing the surface of a corroded sample is developed and successfully used
to conduct a stress analysis.
• Arola-Ramulu provides the best agreement to predict the eﬀective fatigue SCF in the
corroded sample, with a deviation of -0.53% from the ﬁnite element analysis results.
The DNV standard equation underestimates the eﬀective fatigue stress concentration,
but also provides acceptable results, with a deviation of -3.41%, even though only the
roughness average is used to calculate it.
• Assuming valid the approximation of λ = 1, Neuber is not able to predict correctly
the eﬀective fatigue SCF, overestimating its value both for the corroded sample and
the artiﬁcial ones.
• The eﬀect of the notch root radius has been proven to be fundamental to calculate the
eﬀective fatigue SCF, and, due to the uncertainty that it has in the method applied,
new methods to calculate it more accurately should be considered.
• The thickness of the sample evaluated aﬀects to the elastic SCF induced by the surface
roughness by increasing it as the sample becomes thinner, and therefore the same eﬀect
can be expected in the eﬀective fatigue SCF.
• Cheng equation has proven to give decent results of eﬀective fatigue SCF in samples
with a roughness produced by a simple sinusoidal shape, with increasing error as the
ratio between the amplitude and the wavelength increases.
• In cases where the roughness can be characterized with a single sine function, the eﬀec-
tive fatigue SCF calculated with Arola-Ramulu is heavily dependent of the amplitude
of the sine, while in Cheng and Neuber the wavelength becomes more important.
• Arola-Ramulu provides the closest agreement in the results when curves formed by
superposition of multiple sines are used to create the rough surface in artiﬁcial samples,
followed by Cheng.
• The DNV standard equation has diﬃculties to predict properly the eﬀective fatigue
SCF in simple cases, such as roughness characterized by a sinusoidal curve, as it only
relies on the roughness average and does not take into account the notch root radius.

Future work
Fatigue tests have to be performed, both with corroded samples and artiﬁcially gener-
ated samples, in order to corroborate the results from the ﬁnite element analysis, as well as
the diﬀerent equations implemented to obtain the eﬀective fatigue SCF.
It has been observed the eﬀect of the thickness in the artiﬁcial samples, increasing the
elastic SCF obtained with ﬁnite element analysis, and therefore the eﬀective fatigue SCF. In
the corroded sample, the thickness used is the one obtained by default from the CT-scans,
but it might not be enough to consider negligible the thickness eﬀect in the SCF. Therefore,
this analysis could be improved by modifying the thickness of the sample evaluated, and
producing a convergence analysis to make sure that the thickness is big enough to avoid any
bias in the results.
The importance of the notch root radius is also showcased in this report. A diﬀerent
approach to obtain this parameter is proposed, but it has not been used in this project.
Using the Python code [39], the coordinates of the points of the surface of the model can
be extracted, and introduced in Matlab, where a contour plot can be produced with it. The
coordinates obtained with Python, as well as the contour plot produced with it are displayed
in Fig. 31. There, knowing from the ﬁnite element analysis where the SCFs are located, it
is possible to extract the coordinates of the surrounding points, and obtain a more precise
notch root radius. By doing this, the uncertainty introduced by the mesh discretization of
the model is eliminated. The main problem of this method is that, since the model analyzed
in Abaqus is not exactly the same that is introduced in Python, as seen with the deviation
analysis of Fig. 15, a new uncertainty is introduced in the obtention of the notch root radius.
It is also possible that the notch is not located exactly in the same point in the ﬁnite element
model, and therefore obtaining this parameter can be more complicated. It should also be
noted that, as seen in Fig. 31a, there are some voids in the coordinates extracted, as the
coordinates of all points of the surface are not obtained with the Python script. The contour
plots is obtained interpolating with these coordinates, and therefore it can also contain some
deviation caused by these voids. Because of these reasons, this method is discarded in this
project, but could be implemented if the deviation between the model in Abaqus and the
coordinates extracted in the Python script is reduced further.

These coordinates extracted can also be used to improve the results with Neuber equa-
tion. In this report, the assumption of λ = 1 has been considered to be valid, as it is diﬃcult
to measure the relation between the distance of the notches and its depth. In a more simple
case, as the one done in this report with samples generated with a single sinusoidal proﬁle,
the ratio can be easily obtained, as the distance between notches and their depth is equal
to the wavelength and the amplitude of the sine, respectively. Using fast Fourier transform
in 2D (2D FFT), with the already implemented 2DFFT function in Matlab, it is possible to
obtain a set of sines that represents the whole surface of the model, and therefore it would be
possible to obtain a more accurate approximation of the λ parameter for this concrete case.
The main issue with this approach is how to calculate the eﬀect of the diﬀerent wavelengths
and amplitude of the superposed sines to obtain the ﬁnal ratio between them.
Following this approach to obtain the 2D FFT, the diﬀerent wavelengths and amplitudes
could be used to evaluate the elastic SCF of the surface using Cheng equation, without the
need for neither roughness parameters or the notch root radius. The main issue with this
approach is that the eﬀect of the sinusoidal functions in both directions have to be evalu-
ated, so Eq. 13 needs to be updated. Also, one of the advantages of using this equation is
that to obtain the elastic SCF the notch root radius is not needed, but it is needed to evalu-
ate the eﬀective fatigue SCF, so the uncertainty induced by it is still included in this method.
It has been proved that the results using Arola-Ramulu equation are improved in a case
where more that one sinusoidal curve is used to produce the rough surface, when compared
with the more simple case with only one sine. This analysis should be developed further,
with a a bigger average roughness range, as well as with curves in two directions to produce
a 2D rough surface more similar to a real corroded surface, and the results of the diﬀerent
equations should be compared in order to evaluate their validity.
