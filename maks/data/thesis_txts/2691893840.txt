Abstract
Wearables and other mobile IoT sensing devices are becoming incredibly important, with
applications ranging from simple entertainment to industrial solutions. The networks these
devices are typically a part of, due to strict requirements for reliability and low power con­
sumption, typically depend on low­power wireless protocols for data collection. Indus­
trial IoT networking solutions, such as the IEEE 802.15.4 Time­Slotted Channel Hopping
(TSCH) [1], offer a very high degree of reliability; however, they assume static sensor
nodes and, thus, provide no particular mechanisms to support mobile nodes.
This report tackles this problem and introduces mobility support in IEEE 802.15.4 TSCH
networks. The main disadvantage in the case of mobile nodes is potentially long re­
association times due to scanning random radio frequencies for EBs that advertise a
network, which could possibly be avoided if the mobile node exploits information it has
already learned about the network during a previous association. This report focuses on
such previously gathered information to implement mechanisms that manage to foster
faster re­association.
While there are cases where the proposed solutions do not offer an improvement over
default TSCH behaviour, the results achieved indicate that the solutions are significantly
effective in a wide array of scenarios and help mobile nodes re­associate to networks
faster.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Wearables and other mobile IoT sensing devices are becoming incredibly important, with
applications ranging from simple entertainment to industrial solutions. They are now con­
sidered a critical factor in improving functionality, tasks and processes in the context of
many scientific fields.
The networks these devices are typically a part of, require a stack very different from the
traditional internet, due to the devices’ strict requirements for reliability and low power
consumption. As such, the communications in the IoT typically depend on low­power
wireless protocols for data collection; most notably with Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE).
However, BLE is not a reliable wireless solution, as it is prone to collisions. Moreover, BLE
does not really support intermittent connectivity, as it assumes that the central node (i.e.
typically, a smartphone) is always in close proximity to the mobile sensor nodes. This
assumption however overlooks the requirements for a very wide array of applications
where nodes are expected to be joining and leaving frequently.
On the other hand, industrial IoT networking solutions, such as the IEEE 802.15.4 Time­
Slotted Channel Hopping (TSCH) [1], offer a very high degree of reliability; however, they
assume static sensor nodes and, thus, provide no particular mechanisms to support mo­
bile nodes.
If this shortcoming can be addressed, ensuring faster joining times for mobile nodes, it
means avoiding consuming a significant amount of energy, thus leading to longer battery
life and greater autonomy.
1.2
Thesis Objectives
The goal of the project is to introduce mobility support in IEEE 802.15.4 TSCH networks.
As mentioned above, the key challenge for achieving this is the slow joining time of the
TSCH protocol. Due to intermittent connectivity, mobile sensor nodes are expected to
join and re­join the TSCH network several times. The aim of this effort will be to develop
mechanisms for re­joining a TSCH network quickly and efficiently. More specifically, these
mechanisms should, as a first priority, reduce the join time of default TSCH and, if possible,
be comparable to the state of the art in terms of reliability without introducing as much
complexity.
We are therefore aiming for:
• a small code footprint
• avoiding extra packet overhead
• unaffected or improved reliability
The end result should also mean reducing energy consumption.

Background

2.2.1
Devices in the (Industrial) IoT
Modern industrial environments require a very high degree of control and scalability. This
is achieved by using sensing devices and actuators in numerous points throughout an in­
dustrial process, operated and monitored either manually or automatically. In most cases,

this reason, it is imperative that they are part of a well­designed network [10]. WSNs made
up of low­power embedded devices ­ called motes (used interchangeably with nodes in
this work to represent low­power devices used in the IoT), or resource constrained de­

Challenges of the IoT
These devices, while enabling the implementation of a great variety of applications in a
cost efficient, highly autonomous and scalable manner, entail development difficulties.
Code footprint, code execution memory and storage space are examples of such diffi­
culties that need to be taken into account. Most commonly, WSNs are subject to energy
constraints [3], [2], running on a battery that might be difficult to replace (due to technical
knowledge, or simply the man­hours or the complicated terrain involved). Depending on
a specific device’s resources, there might be a need for sacrificing features (e.g. secu­
rity, compatibility with common internet application standards, or even battery life) for an
application to be deployed without issues.
Furthermore, because these devices are not intended to be constantly supervised and
managed but are expected to function by themselves, it’s often the case that failures may
occur, either because the hardware is defective, or even due to outside influence such
as radio interference or multi­path fading. Failing to function and communicate efficiently
with one another means that the nodes of a WSN waste a lot of extra energy, on top of
impacting the performance of the system.
One hugely important cause of unreliability and failed communication between nodes in
the IoT is clock drift. Since many operations in the context of lower level layers are time­
sensitive (such as radio controls), there arises a need for motes to be able to execute
certain tasks within very specific time frames, without necessarily being aware of the
state of other motes. This requirement is actually critical for energy­saving operations
as well. The most common example would be the transmission of a packet, where the
receiving mote needs to wake up its radio at the same time another mote is supposed to
send a signal, thus minimizing the radio’s energy consumption. To achieve this task syn­
chronization, every mote has an internal clock [4], configured so that it keeps an ideal or
reference time. Electronic clock oscillators however have imperfections that result in said

time may differ, resulting in a drift between pairs of motes as well. As such, IoT layers

That being said, industrial networking standards have greatly developed over the past

with protocols such as TSCH which is the focus of this work. Since 2013, IEEE 802.15.4
has been extended further by [10], which introduces support for IPv6 frames over the
already specified protocols.

2.2.2
The TSCH Mode of IEEE 802.15.4
The IEEE 802.15.4 standard introduced TSCH, or Time Slotted Channel Hopping [6],
a medium access control protocol that focuses on ensuring low­power consumption by
avoiding interference and is therefore indispensable to resource constrained devices.
Aside from the low energy cost, TSCH is simple and reliable. It overcomes interference
by handling a mote’s radio with a schedule based approach that avoids collisions. The
nature of the schedule itself is not defined in the default TSCH specification, but is left to
the implementing entity’s discretion. This entity (or ”LLC”, i.e. ”Logical Link Control” as
it’s defined in the IEEE 802 standard [6]) can be another layer protocol. This work utilizes
two of these LLCs and their respective schedules, which will be discussed later.
In order to join a TSCH network, a node has to listen for EB (i.e. Enhanced Beacon)
broadcasts from other nodes that have already joined, which share information about the
network and advertise its presence. It should be noted that in the IEEE 802.15.4 standard,
EB broadcasts occur at implementation­specific intervals and channels. Once a mote has
joined, they may start communicating with their ”neighbors” using their links on the times
and channels (thus ”Channel Hopping”) allocated by the established schedule, remaining
synchronized based on the time of the coordinator, which is passed down the leaf nodes
of the network following a Direct Acyclic Graph (DAG).
The TSCH Schedule
The schedule itself is a construct that defines the rules by which the nodes in the network
can use the data­links. It splits the passage of time into timeslots, that are grouped into
slotframes. These slotframes are repeating and represent the behaviour of the network
Reliable Mobility in Industrial IoT Networks
5
==PAGE:14==
within a certain period. The schedule’s cells indicate whether a member of the network
is supposed to use the radio to transmit any pending packets, listen for any incoming
packets, or simply do nothing, as shown in figure 2.6.
A timeslot, usually with a duration of 10ms, is sufficient time for a mote to transmit a
MAC frame of maximum size to a neighbour and receive an acknowledgement, along
with encrypting and decrypting said frame. A ”scheduled cell” [1] is an element of the
schedule defined by:
• a slotOffset, that points to the index of a corresponding timeslot in the slotframe,
• a channelOffset, which is a parameter that translates to a frequency in a pseudo
random hopping manner.
Each scheduled cell in the slotframe represents a unique link between different nodes
in the network, or a broadcast cell. Having different cells for each radio link during a
timeslot, and depending on the scheduling algorithm used, it’s possible for either a single
or multiple links to be in use. Motes may have dedicated slots where only a single one is
allowed to transmit in a specific frequency, or shared ones where multiple motes transmit
in parallel. For shared slots, CSMA/CD back­off is used to resolve contention.
Due to the channel hopping nature of TSCH, timeslots may be repeating over time, but that
does not mean that the same timeslot/cell translates to the same channel frequency every
time we iterate over a slotframe. The reason for this is that the protocol also uses a total
counter of the number of slots elapsed since establishing the network. This monotonically
increasing counter is the Absolute Slot Number (or ASN) and it can be calculated based
on the time that the network has been active with this equation:
ASN = (k ∗S + t)
(2.1)
with k being the slotframe cycle (or how many times we have iterated over the slots of
the slotframe) , S is the size of the slotframe, and t the slotOffset, pointing to the current
position in the current iteration over the slotframe.
The ASN together with the channelOffset are used in order to determine which fre­
quency is supposed to be used at any given point, using the equation:
channel = hoppingSequence[(ASN + channelOffset)%hoppingSequenceLength] (2.2)
where channel is the physical layer channel that will be used by the link in this particular
moment in time. The channels translate to actual frequencies as defined in [6]. The
ones available for use to a mote are stored in hoppingSequence, which is a list of length
hoppingSequenceLength (this value might vary for different motes).

This, as mentioned above, has the effect of channel hopping. Even if the same link ­

Again, as the authors of [11] and [12] explain, when a transmission fails it’s more efficient
to change frequencies, so TSCH is very successful in mitigating interference and multi­
path fading.
Network LLCs may employ slotframes of different numbers or sizes to accomplish different
goals. A single network may rely on multiple slotframes at the same time to organize
communication for different groups of nodes, control node radio duty cycle activity, or
just configure network traffic for different functions. A mote may have scheduled cells
concerning it in more than one slotframes, but it’s not necessary to participate in all of
them. Slotframes can be modified, added or removed on the fly, though that requires the
coordinator recalibrating the current state of the network. Lastly, in the case of conflicts,
there are rules in place that resolve them. For example, transmissions have higher priority
than receptions, and slotframes with a smaller ”handle” (registered in the schedule earlier)
have priority over higher handles.

Synchronization
As mentioned in 2.2.1, low power WSN devices require a high level of synchronization
in order to ensure correct communication, as well as achieve standard­specified goals.
TSCH networks address this requirement by using a mechanism that attempts to keep
associated nodes synchronized despite clock drift.
Each node in the network has to periodically make sure that it is synchronized to an­
other node (and correct its clock in case the reference time has drifted), while also make
other neighbor nodes aware of its own clock time. This ”time­source” neighbor that each
node refers to for synchronization is usually defined by the LLC mentioned in 2.2.2. Ad­
ditionally, TSCH nodes add timing data to all transmitted packets, including data and
ACK frames and therefore neighbors resynchronize very frequently. Generally, this is
done by computing the difference between the time a frame was expected to arrive at
the receiver and the actual arrival time. Then, either the receiver updates its own clock
(Frame­based synchronization) or notifies the sender upon replying with an ACK frame
(Acknowledgement­based synchronization). Synchronization can also also done by ex­
changing EB broadcasts, as they also include timing information.

2.2.3
LLC defined TSCH specifications
It is stated in the IETF tech report [1] concerning the TSCH environment and goals, that
the IEEE 802.14.4 standard leaves many details unspecified and up to the purview of any
LLC paired with the TSCH protocol.
• The scheduling of periodic EB broadcasts to advertise the presence of a network,
the information included in them, or its rules for joining.
• Actions taken after the join process and the network ”bootstrap”.
• The time­source neighbor to refer to for synchronization.
• The duration of a timeslot and guard times.
• Policies for:
– Building and maintaining communication schedules.
– Accommodating the traffic of an RPL­enabled network.
– Organizing schedule traffic according to the need for data propagation.
– The priority of packets based on protocol needs.
• Handle the resources allocated for communications, e.g. buffer space, rate limiting,
re­transmissions for fault tolerance etc.
And notably,
• How to handle topological changes and the mobility of nodes.
This means that TSCH is flexible, very optimizable and easy to merge with protocols in
other layers of a stack, despite different requirements.
Of course, almost all of these specifications can be implemented by a variety of existing
LLCs. For example, the 6TiSCH Working Group [10] provides a default specification for a
minimal schedule scheme for networks that do not have one already implemented, while
in Orchestra [20] nodes compute their own schedule, with different slotframes for specific
functions (as shown in fig. 2.4).
That being said, mobility support for TSCH nodes has not been addressed by any es­
tablished, popular stacks so far, which ­ the authors of [14] and [15] demonstrate ­ can
have significant impact on the network. A standardized approach to tackling the overhead
and reliability issues of mobile nodes would surely benefit all TSCH networks and enable
many industrial processes to increase effectiveness even more.
2.2.4
Mobility Aware Solutions
Few specifications that consider mobility in their networks exist at all (for example, [16]
briefly mentions mobile devices in an RPL topology), while there have also been pro­
posed solutions for mitigating the mobility problem. [19] proposes a router synchronization
scheme that enables mobile nodes to ”roam”, i.e. move away from the range of a border
router, and seamlessly enter the range (and subnet) of another without desynchroniza­
tion through the synchronization of the routers themselves. Meanwhile, the authors of [18]
propose a new protocol, based on TSCH, that drastically changes the association process
by introducing new frame types modifying acknowledgements and having certain frames
transmitted on a fixed channel, effectively making association deterministic and faster.

These solutions have impressive results, but they either assume that a mobile node will
always be within the range of a backbone router related to the network ([19]), or that
industrial processes will be able to change their existing network stacks in order to replace
their TSCH implementation with one that alters resource­consumption and network traffic

Contribution
This project tries to reflect on the shortcomings of the aforementioned solutions ­ and
TSCH itself, and proposes an elegant approach to mitigating the issues that mobile nodes
introduce to a TSCH network, effectively improving reliability (and as a side benefit, energy
consumption).
Furthermore, this approach is presented as a possible network­agnostic TSCH feature,
instead of assuming network structure or introducing a new protocol. This way, including
this functionality in an existing network is just a matter of updating the current TSCH
implementation.
Additionally, the implementation of this idea takes advantage of existing TSCH compo­
nents and exploits unused resources, to improve upon the original TSCH, without intro­
ducing traffic or interference, and without affecting energy consumption. All this, with a
small code footprint.
This is achieved by introducing two new mechanisms. The first is an ASN Tracking Pro­
cess, that keeps increasing the mobile node’s current ASN with respect to the network
even after disassociation. The second is an EB broadcast Channel Calculation Mecha­
nism that tries to anticipate the channel on which future EBs will be transmitted, based on
the network’s expected ASN. The Channel Calculation Mechanism comes in two varia­
tions, each relevant to different circumstances.
In the course of this project, various operation scripts where also developed, that enhance
TSCH development, testing and experimentation when working with the Contiki­NG oper­

nisms proposed in this work can be used to generate a substantial amount of data, which
can in turn be exploited using machine learning techniques for a different number of tasks.

implement a mechanism that accurately keeps track of the ASN, i.e. the ASN Tracking
Process, or ATP for short.

3.5
Technical Difficulties
During development a number of factors were identified that made implementation difficult
and stalled the process. To begin with, as stated in 3.4.1, Contiki­NG provides the rtimer
library for scheduling of real­time tasks. The Contiki­NG documentation explains that
because the system OS supports only one active rtimer at a time, any functionality that
requires more than that is not feasible. Unfortunately, TSCH is a protocol that’s indeed
implemented using an rtimer for timing of slot operation and scheduling of various threads.
This means than in order to actualize the ATP, it was impossible to introduce a new rtimer

Evaluation
This section goes through the procedures used to test the implemented features during
development, as well as identify possible bugs and evaluate the end result of the proposed
mechanisms. The motivation behind these experiments are also explained. Finally, the
results of those experiments are presented.
4.1
Testing Suite
As discussed in 3.2 and 3.3, a suitable test­bed of physical hardware devices was not
available in order to conduct real­world experiments. For that reason, the Cooja simulator
provided by Contiki was critical in order to actualize the scenarios for proper testing in a
well­controlled environment.
The concept of a testing process is not standardized when developing services and ap­
plications for embedded devices, but the effectiveness of a test depends on the coverage
and scalability of the test cases. During the course of this thesis, the testing process was
refined to the following methodology:
1. Compile implemented mechanisms using Contiki­NG for different supported plat­
forms.
2. Set­up Cooja simulation experiments, controlled using the simulator’s test script
function to create complex scenarios. The test script was written in Javascript and
saved as part of the simulation file and is responsible for managing the simulated
nodes’ locations, as well as how much time they should spend in and out of range
of the coordinator, allowing them to become mobile.
3. Run each simulation scenario multiple times, using a different random­seed each
time in order to obtain statistically significant results. The simulations are run in bulk
using a Python script that manages simulation specific parameters, like the random
seed that was just mentioned, as well as the network schedule, which implementa­
tion of TSCH to run etc.
4. Save the simulation log files for later analysis after the bulk simulation experiments.
The log files are also organized saved and stored in an organized folder structure
using the scripts referred in steps 2 and 3.
5. Analyze the log files corresponding to each scenario to obtain different evaluation
metrics. This is done using a second Python script that adapts to the result folders’
structure and generates results for each simulation after the fact, finally combining
statistics and producing a clear comparison between different implementations.
6. Rationalize the experiment results and judge the effectiveness, benefits and disad­
vantages of the proposed mechanisms.
4.2
Test Configurations
During the implementation phase, the TSCH settings described in 3.1 were used for most
of the testing. For the evaluation phase, various other values were explored. At the same
time, aside from TSCH, the configurations of the testing environment were chosen as
such:

• 25 different random seeds were used for each scenario to ensure statistically sig­

4.3
Experiments
The experiments for evaluating the new mechanisms were designed with reliability in mind
as the most important criterion. This means the implementations will ultimately be judged
on the degree to which the network loses packets which should have otherwise been
delivered to their destination. For this goal, according to [15], the most important measure
is the downtime of mobile nodes while in range of the coordinator. Since there is no way
for nodes out of range to exchange messages anyway, it’s essentially the re­connection
time, measured from the moment that a node enters radio range until the moment an EB
is received, that needs to be examined. This length of time is the only variable interval in
the node downtime in the following experiments, which ensures that the results obtained
from the testing process will indeed represent an accurate evaluation of the systems.

Discussion
In this section, the experiments’ results are interpreted with the aid of the generated met­
rics, statistics and graphs.
Beginning with a cursory look at table 4.1, we can see that a higher Count score usually
corresponds to a high Improvement score, while it is also apparent that either implemen­
tation is not necessarily effective in all scenarios, as it seems there are enough cases
where the default TSCH nodes had lower downtimes that the improvement score ended
up negative.
This is expected, since ­ considering Contiki­NG’s randomized broadcasting and scanning
implementation ­ randomized processes sometimes result in extreme cases of high per­
formance. Essentially, vanilla nodes sometimes get ”lucky” and re­connect very quickly.
At the same time, since the CCM can only offer an estimation of the ASN of the next
broadcast, there are cases where this mechanism produces wrong estimations that cause
the node to miss EBs. This can possibly happen multiple times during scanning and result
in longer downtimes.
An example of this can be seen in the following figure 5.1 that show that default TSCH
can sometimes outperform even a more sopisticated approach.

success. Both implementations are schedule­agnostic and can retain their effectiveness

5.1
Future Work
Despite the success of the proposed mechanisms in this thesis, there are various other
factors to consider in order for them to actually be included to TSCH as viable features,
meaning there is still work that can be done.
To begin with, there are bugs in the implementation of the proposed mechanisms for
Contiki­NG, related to timing issues and code that was not properly inserted into the ex­
isting TSCH implementation. These details most likely affect performance and in some
corner cases that were not properly addressed, may even result in nodes failing. Obvi­
ously, these issues need to be tackled with a software developing mindset as opposed to
academic research. ATP+CCM especially stands to be improved much more if the timing
problems are solved.
Additionally, TSCH has been a popular industrial IoT protocol for a long time, and the
work that has been done to improve the original IEEE 802.15.4 standard is extensive,
as explained in 2.1. Modes like Adaptive Channel Selection and updating the channel
hopping sequence in mobile node are techniques used to circumvent interference from
heavily used frequencies, which is not something the work done in this thesis takes into
account. Of course, since the changes to the original TSCH code were kept to a minimum,
it’s possible that the proposed mechanisms will be viable choices in networks where this
is done gradually, but they might require fine­tuning for them to work flawlessly in all
scenarios. The same holds true for other features that have been designed for TSCH.
Moreover, the testing process designed to test this work can also be refined further. While
the results presented here are statistically significant and generated through a highly ran­
domized process, further statistical analysis can provide more confidence. It is also worth
it to consider using other baselines to test against. On top of that, the testing process in­
troduced in this work generates a substantial amount of data related to the performance of
TSCH in various scenarios. It is possible to use this data to create machine learning mod­
els that will be able to predict the performance of TSCH implementations in experiments
with completely new setups.
Finally, the designs of the proposed mechanisms themselves have room for improvement.
While the ATP is effective in allowing a mobile node to utilize previously gathered infor­
mation, there might be other data worth exploiting, such as information provided by other
protocols such as RPL. As for the CCM, it is possible for it to be improved further by us­
ing techniques that can more accurately predict the ASN of future broadcasts, such as
optimization or machine learning algorithms.

Conclusion
During the course of this thesis, two mechanisms were developed for the purpose of
enabling mobile TSCH nodes to re­associate faster, resulting in more reliable TSCH net­
works that can be used for a wide variety of industrial processes. This was achieved while
keeping the numerous restrictions of IoT devices in mind and with little alterations to the
original TSCH design included in the IEEE 802.15.4 standard.
The test experiments that were run with the implementation of these mechanisms, when
compared against default TSCH behaviour, were largely positive. There were however
limitations, as some results from the same experiments indicated that the proposed mech­
anisms are not effective in all TSCH networks. Nevertheless, the results show that in most
cases downtime of all mobile nodes can be decreased by at least 10%, with some extreme
cases where the improvement is much greater. It’s therefore up to prospective developers
of IoT applications to judge whether using these novel features could benefit their stack
or not.
Of course, this thesis is not the definitive solution for faster re­association of mobile nodes.
From a developer standpoint there is still much work for the implementation to be per­
fected, while the designs of the mechanisms themselves could possibly be improved even
further. It is however proof that TSCH can be optimized in more ways than just choosing a
schedule or fine­tuning different configurations, which could motivate research for further
improvements of the protocol overall. Ideally, this thesis can also serve as a guideline
for future academic work that aims to improve the reliability of mobile nodes in a TSCH
network.
