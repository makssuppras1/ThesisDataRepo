ABSTRACT
ABSTRACT
From 2009 - 2014, Danish municipalities spent around 18 billion DKK on renewals and 
construction of primary school buildings. Several studies in the recent years have shown 
an association between poor indoor climate and pupils’ school performance. However, the 
Mass Experiments from 2009 and 2014 showed that the indoor climate in Danish primary 
schools had not improved in the intermediate period. 
Based on existing databases and information gathered from municipalities and primary 
schools, this project investigated associations between school building typologies, energy 
performance, perceived educational environment, ventilation principle and renovation 
initiatives. Data from Danish Ministry of Education, containing the register of all primary 
schools, was matched to BBR-property information from Ministry of Housing, Urban and 
Rural Affairs. Energy labels from the Danish Energy Agency and a selection of perceived 
educational environment data from Danish Centre of Educational Environment were 
matched to the primary schools. In order to identify performed renovation initiatives, 
questionnaires to the municipalities and primary schools were prepared and processed.
Six main building typologies based on year of construction were investigated; village school, 
storey school, aula school, comb school, open plan school and project work school. The 
results showed significant associations between building typologies and other parameters. 
A higher percentage of buildings with energy-efficient energy labels (A-C) was found, 
the newer the typologies were. Further, a higher percentage of pupils in newer schools 
were satisfied with the indoor climate. A lower percentage of pupils were satisfied with 
the indoor air quality in schools with mechanical exhaust, compared to pupils in schools 
with balanced mechanical ventilation or natural ventilation (manually through windows or 
valves). A higher percentage of pupils in schools with natural ventilation found that they 
had difficulty to concentrate.
Renovation of 125 schools showed that the most common initiatives were regarding artificial 
lighting systems and windows, followed by ventilation systems and renewable energy. The 
results suggested that the municipalities have been more inclined towards financially viable 
renovation solutions rather than exclusively improving the indoor climate. No significant 
association between renovation initiatives and building typologies was found. The 
construction and renovation investments showed large differences for each municipality. 
Likewise, the investment and background for renovating varied for each school.
It was found that there is a potential for developing a platform based on existing databases 
supplemented by data collected from municipalities, to identify the most effective methods 
for improving indoor climate and energy consumption in Danish primary schools. Such 
a platform will be useful in relation to development of a renovation strategy towards 
successful renovations in the future, which would benefit municipalities, professionals and 
last but not least the schools and pupils.

RESUMÉ
I perioden 2009-2014 investerede de danske kommuner omkring 18 milliarder kroner 
på renovering og opførelse af folkeskolebygninger. Flere studier gennem de senere år 
har vist en sammenhæng mellem dårligt indeklima og elevernes præstation. Dog viste 
Masseeksperimenterne fra 2009 og 2014, at indeklimaet i de danske folkeskoler ikke var 
forbedret i den mellemliggende periode. 
Dette speciale undersøgte sammenhænge mellem folkeskolers bygningstypologi, 
energimæssige ydeevne, elevevaluering af undervisningsmiljø, ventilationsprincip og 
renoveringstiltag. Undersøgelserne var baseret på eksisterende databaser og oplysninger 
indsamlet fra kommuner og folkeskoler. Undervisningsministeriets register over folkeskoler 
blev sammenholdt med BBR-ejendomsinformation fra Ministeriet for By, Bolig og 
Landdistrikter. Energimærker fra Energistyrelsen og udvalgte data fra Dansk Center 
for Undervisningsmiljø omhandlende elevernes evaluering af undervisningsmiljøet for 
de enkelte folkeskoler blev undersøgt. For at kortlægge udførte renoveringstiltag blev 
spørgeskemaer til kommuner og grundskoler forberedt og behandlet.
Seks overordnede bygningstypologier baseret på byggeår blev undersøgt: landsbyskolen, 
etageskolen, aulaskolen, kamskolen, åbenplanskolen og projektarbejdsskolen. Resultaterne 
viste signifikante sammenhænge mellem bygningstypologier og forskellige parametre. Der 
blev fundet, at en højere procentdel af bygningerne med energieffektive energimærker (A-
C) tilhørte de nyere typologier. Endvidere var en højere procentdel af eleverne i nyere skoler 
tilfredse med indeklimaet. En lavere procentdel af eleverne var tilfredse med luftkvaliteten 
i skoler med mekanisk udsugning, sammenlignet med elever i skoler med balanceret 
ventilation eller naturlig ventilation (manuelt gennem vinduer eller udluftningsventiler). 
En højere procentdel af eleverne i skoler med naturlig ventilation havde svært ved at 
koncentrere sig. 
Renoveringstiltag af 125 skoler viste, at de mest almindelige tiltag var renovering af 
belysningssystemer og vinduer, efterfulgt af ventilationssystemer og vedvarende energi. 
Resultaterne antydede, at kommunerne har været mere tilbøjelige til at udføre økonomisk 
rentable løsninger, snarere end udelukkende løsninger, der kunne forbedre indeklimaet. 
Der blev ikke fundet nogen signifikant sammenhæng mellem renoveringstiltag og 
bygningstypologier. Anlægs- og renoveringsinvesteringerne viste store forskelle for de 
enkelte kommuner. Ligeledes varierede investeringerne og baggrunden for renoveringerne 
for hver enkelt skole. 
Det blev erfaret, at der er et potentiale for at udvikle en platform baseret på eksisterende 
databaser suppleret med indhentet data fra kommunerne, for at identificere de mest 
effektive metoder til at forbedre indeklima og energiforbrug i danske folkeskoler. En sådan 
platform vil være nyttig i forhold til udvikling af en renoveringsstrategi for vellykkede 
skolerenoveringer i fremtiden, hvilket ville gavne kommuner, fagfolk og sidst men ikke 
mindst skoler og elever.

INTRODUCTION
01. Introduction
The Danish Government established a quality improvement fund of 50 billion DKK in 2007. 
The funds should be spent in the period 2009 - 2018 on investments in better physical 
environment of citizen-focused service areas among other things. An amount of 22 billion 
DKK was dedicated to the physical environment in day-care centres, primary schools as well 
as leisure and elderly areas [www.fm.dk]. Even though the municipalities spent 17.75 billion 
DKK on renovation and building new buildings for primary schools in 2009 - 2014 [www.ing.
dk], the Mass Experiments from 2009 and 2014 showed that the indoor climate in Danish 
primary schools had not improved in the intermediate period [Menå & Larsen, 2010] 
[Clausen et al., 2014]. This is unfortunate, as several studies in the recent years showed 
that there is an association between poor indoor climate and pupils’ school performance 
[Bakó-Biro et al., 2001] [Wargocki & Wyon, 2007] [DCUM, 2013].
Generally, there is a maintenance backlog among Danish primary school buildings. The 
school buildings are in various conditions; few are new, some are old and have gone through 
several rounds of renovations and continuous maintenance throughout the years. Others 
are old but have had no renovation and limited maintenance. In addition to the general 
need of renovation and maintenance, the upgrades of the schools are often motivated by 
the desire to reduce the energy consumption for heating and ventilation. 
At this present stage, there is no public database of school building characteristics, however, 
it is evaluated that there is a need for a platform to share knowledge and experience in 
order to effectively improve school buildings. The developed results are meant to be used 
in future investigations, as a stepping stone to identify the most effective methods for 
improvement of the indoor climate and energy consumption in Danish primary schools 
according to the conditions of the buildings. 
01.1 Danish Primary Schools
Danish primary schools are among the schools in Europe, where the pupils spend most 
time. The school year lasts typically 40 weeks [www.uvm.dk], and since the new school 
reform of year 2014 was introduced, the pupils are having longer school days than before. 
The pupils in 0th - 3rd grade shall spend at least 1,200 hours in school per year, the 4th - 6th 
grade at least 1,320 hours and the 7th - 9th grade at least 1,400 hours in the school per 
year [www.uvm.dk]. This corresponds to 14 %, 15 % and 16 % of the time in a year. Taking 
into consideration that a person sleeps approximately 8 hours every night, the time spent 
in school corresponds to 21 %, 23 % and 24 % of the waking hours in a year. The pupils’ 
wellbeing in school is of greater importance now than ever. 
The general purpose of Danish primary schools has changed in the last century in accordance 
with the development of the society. The primary school used to ensure a basic education, 
where for many, this was the only education they would get. Nowadays, the primary school 
is the first step into further education and has developed into a modern knowledge-based 
society school. In 2011, the Danish Government introduced increased investments in 
research and education, as they set an objective that in the year 2020, 95 % of the pupils in 
the primary school shall pursue secondary education, 60 % a higher education and 25 % a 
longer higher education  [Regeringsgrundlag, 2011]. 
The pupils’ wellbeing as well as their learning ability and health during the school day are 
affected by a considerable number of factors. Teachers, classmates, socioeconomic status, 
motivation, intellect, friendship, teaching, teaching aids, diet, exercise, hygiene as well as 
the physical environment including temperature and indoor air quality, are a few of the 
factors that may impact the general wellbeing of children. It is generally difficult to meet

all of these needs for each child, as every person is an individual being with an individual 
background. However, there are still issues that can be addressed to promote pupils 
wellbeing, learning and health. One of those is improvement of indoor climate conditions 
in classrooms. 
A study performed by Kjeldsen et al., (2013), where 17 % of Danish primary schools were 
investigated, showed a statistically significant association between the indoor air quality 
and the absence due to illness as well as the pupils’ results in national tests. The indoor 
air quality in the classrooms was provided by the type of ventilation system in the school. 
The analysis showed that the schools with the highest scores in the national tests had a 
ventilation system with both mechanical exhaust and supply, the schools with mechanical 
exhaust had the second highest scores and the schools with natural ventilation had the 
lowest test results. 
As stated previously, two experiments called the Mass Experiments were conducted in the 
years 2009 and 2014. The experiments focused on investigating the indoor environment 
in Danish schools. The experiment from 2009 concluded that 56 % of the participating 
schools (330 schools) had a CO2 concentration above the allowed 1,000 ppm limit stated by 
the Danish Building Regulations [BR10] [Menå & Larsen, 2010]. The experiment from 2014 
showed no improvements of the indoor environment in the meantime, as it was concluded 
that 60 % of the participating schools (262 schools) had a CO2 concentration above 1000 
ppm. [Clausen et al., 2014]. Figure 01.1 shows the distribution of CO2 concentrations in the 
two experiments. 
Figure 01.1: The results of the CO2 concentration in the Mass Experiments 2009 and 2014 [Clausen et al., 
2014]
The analysis of the two Mass Experiments showed further that there was a significant 
association between the presence of a ventilation system and the CO2 concentration. The 
CO2 concentration in classes with mechanical ventilation (either balanced or exhaust only) 
was lower than classes with natural ventilation, as the average of the CO2 concentration 
for balanced ventilation was 1,099 ppm, for mechanical exhaust 1,380 ppm and for natural 
ventilation 1,813 ppm [Clausen et al., 2014].
The Mass Experiment from 2009 was besides Denmark also conducted in Sweden and 
Norway at the same time. As mentioned, Danish schools had inadequate ventilation in 
56 % of the measured classes, whereas Sweden and Norway had inadequate ventilation 
in 16 % and 21 % of the cases, respectively [Menå & Larsen, 2010]. Sweden and Norway 
have higher demands for the ventilation rates in schools compared to Denmark [Boverkets 
byggeregler] [TEK 10]. This was also reflected in the Mass Experiment. An effort is needed 
to renovate and improve Danish primary schools, so that the conditions can be optimal for 
learning, health and general wellbeing.

01.2 Danish Energy Strategy
The overall energy strategy of the Danish Government is that Denmark should be fossil-free 
in the year 2050. Until then, different milestones have been defined, as the plan is that half 
of the traditional electricity use shall be covered by wind energy in the year 2020. By the 
year 2035, the electricity and heat supply shall be covered by renewable energy sources 
[Klima-, Energi og Bygningsministeriet, 2012].
In order to meet this ambitious energy goal, an effort needs to be made in several areas, 
including the building stock. Today the building stock represents around 40 % of the total 
energy use. Among the building stock, existing buildings constitute a large part whereas new 
buildings constitute a relatively small part. This means that it is necessary to renovate and 
energy optimise existing buildings to reach the goals. [Klima-, Energi og Bygningsministeriet, 
2012].
Because of the Danish Energy Strategy it is relevant to investigate how Danish primary 
schools have been renovated and which impact these renovations have had on the indoor 
environment and energy consumption. This knowledge can be used to ensure that Denmark 
can meet the strategy of being fossil-free in the year 2015.
01.3 The Socioeconomic Consequences of Poor 
Indoor Environment in Schools
Not only does poor indoor environment quality in the schools affect the wellbeing and 
learning ability of the pupils, it also has an impact on the socioeconomic balance in general.
 
As stated previously, better air quality in classrooms leads to improved pupil performance. 
This will not only benefit the pupils but also the national economy, as international research 
has shown that better performing pupils become more well educated and productive adults 
[Slotsholm, 2012].
 
The Danish Rational Economic Agents Model (DREAM) analyses Danish economy in the 
long term, and has investigated the socioeconomic consequences of improving the indoor 
climate in Danish primary schools. By use of the results of other studies, three overall effects 
were evaluated by improving the indoor climate: the effect on the length of pupils’ further 
education, the effect on the sick leave among the teachers and the effect on the pupils’ test 
scores and thereby their productivity. The three experiments showed that 4 % of the pupils 
that would have started in the optional 10th grade will begin another education instead. 
There would be fewer days with sick leave among the teachers, which would give an annual 
saving on the primary government budget of 50 million DKK (at 2011 level). Further, pupils 
influenced by a better indoor climate would on average score 10 PISA points higher, which 
is assumed to increase the growth in labour productivity by 0.17 percentage point. When 
combining these three experiments, a calculation performed by DREAM shows that the 
annual improvement of the primary government budget balance would be 121 million DKK 
(at 2011 level) [DREAM, 2012].

02. The Danish Building Regulations
02.1 Historical Development of the Building 
Regulations
This section presents major changes in the Danish Building Regulations with regards to 
energy and indoor climate. An overview of the development in requirements is given 
in order to illustrate what can be expected of buildings constructed under the different 
generations of regulations. 
The first Danish Building Regulations was from 1961 and its purpose was to set standards and 
requirements for new buildings to ensure good quality of the buildings. The first regulations 
were based on good building practice and included requirements for transmission heat loss 
of the building components [BR61].
Demands concerning energy consumption were first introduced into the Building Regulations 
of 1977 [BR77] after the oil crises in the 1970’s. In 2006 a supplement to BR95 [BR95] 
introduced a method to calculate the total energy frame of a building. [Ea Energianalyse et 
al., 2008]. BR95 also states requirements to the minimum ventilation rate [BR95].
In 2008, the Danish Government decided to develop a strategy for energy efficient 
buildings to reduce the energy consumption in new buildings by 75 % in 2020 compared 
to 2006. The energy frame was to be gradually lowered from 2010 to 2020. The current 
Building Regulations of 2010 [BR10] includes stricter demands regarding energy frame and 
introduced two new voluntary low-energy classes: Low-Energy Class 2015 (LEK2015) and 
Building Class 2020 (BK2020). The low-energy classes are expected to be implemented as 
requirements in 2015 and 2020, respectively [www.ens.dk].

02.2 Renovation Classes in BR2015
Until now, the requirements in the building regulations concerning existing buildings are 
limited to component requirements. The component requirements describe maximum 
U-value and linear loss when replacing components in the building envelope. In connection 
with rebuilding and renovation, the requirement for insulation applies if the initiative is 
financially viable [BR10].
The new Building Regulations of 2015 (BR15) is expected to introduce the concept of 
an optional renovation class. The purpose of the optional renovation class is to provide 
an alternative to the building component requirements in larger renovation projects. If 
the building meets the energy frame for existing buildings, equivalent to that used in the 
energy labeling system, the optional renovation class will allow reductions in relation to 
the component requirements. The renovation class will provide more freedom to building 
owners and designers that are ambitious about energy efficiency. For instance, it will be 
possible to choose better insulating windows and to insulate the roof extra against reducing 
the insulation thickness in the facade [SBi 2015:06].

For district heating, the primary energy factor is reduced to 0.8 in LEK2015, corresponding

03. Indoor Environment Quality
The indoor environment quality is the quality of a building’s environment in relation to 
the occupants of the building. A good indoor environment should ensure the health 
and wellbeing of the occupants. The indoor environment quality is affected and thereby 
determined by many factors. The factors can be divided into four main categories: the 
building envelope, the outdoor conditions, the occupancy and the building services, see 
figure 03.1.

03.1 Indoor Environment Requirements and 
Recommendations
The Danish Building Regulations [BR10] takes the indoor environment quality into account by 
defining requirements and recommendations for the thermal comfort, air quality, acoustics 
and lighting conditions, when building new buildings or maintaining and renovating existing 
buildings. There are different requirements according to the usage of the building. The 
Danish Building Regulation refers to several standards for detailed specification of the 
requirements. 
As the specific indoor environment requirements and recommendations regarding the 
schools are not directly relevant for this project, but nonetheless important to be aware of, 
these will only be reviewed in a very general manner, to give a brief overview. 
The assessment of the indoor air quality is often connected with CO2 concentration. The CO2 
concentration is among other things a by-product of humans’ metabolism, and is inevitable 
in the air. CO2 is not harmful to health, but is a measurable indicator of how well a room is 
ventilated and how polluted the indoor air is with the less measurable bio-effluents. When 
the ventilation rate per person increases, the indoor CO2 concentration decreases. The 
emission from humans depend on their age, size and activity level, and are often denoted 
in parts per million (ppm). The outdoor CO2 concentration is approximately 380 - 400 ppm 
[Petersen, 2011]. As mentioned previously, the maximum allowed CO2 concentration in 
schools is 1000 ppm in longer periods [BR10].
For thermal comfort the Danish Building Regulations [BR10] refers to DS 474, Code for 
Thermal Indoor Climate [DS 474]. Requirements are given for the air temperature, surface 
temperature, air velocity, air turbulence intensity and relative humidity. The air quality is 
defined by minimum ventilation rates as well as a maximum CO2 concentration level along 
with requirements to the emittance of pollutants from building materials. The acoustic 
requirements concern both the building installations and the adjacent surroundings. The 
lighting conditions are defined in regards to both the minimum daylight level and the 
artificial lighting, where DS 700, Artificial Lighting in Workrooms [DS 700] must be followed. 
For the new Building Class 2020, the requirements regarding CO2 concentration and daylight 
are tightened. 
Besides the Danish Building Regulations, several other standards set different 
recommendations to the indoor environment. These are not required by law, but give 
guidance to obtaining a satisfying indoor environment. 
The Danish Standard DS 3033, Voluntary Classification of the Quality of the Indoor Climate 
in Residential Houses, Schools, Childrens’ Day-care Centres and Offices [DS 3033], provides 
a voluntary classification of the indoor climate. Based on nine indoor environment 
parameters, the building can be classified within five quality categories, from A++ being the 
best to C being the worst. The nine indoor environment parameters are ventilation rate, 
CO2 concentration, thermal conditions, radon, formaldehyde, particles, moisture/mould, 
daylight and artificial light and lastly acoustic conditions. 
The Danish Standard DS/EN 15251, Indoor Environmental Input Parameters for Design and 
Assessment of Energy Performance of Buildings Addressing Indoor Air Quality, Thermal 
Environment, Lighting and Acoustics [DS/EN 15251], defines input parameters for designing 
buildings. Nevertheless, it is often used as a guideline for how the indoor environment 
should be. The standard defines four quality categories, where category I is the best and 
category IV is the worst. The building is qualified based on parameters related with the

The Danish Ministry of Education (UVM) has in the year 2001 implemented a law regarding 
pupils and students teaching environment [LOV nr 166]. It states that the pupils and 
students have the right to a good teaching environment and that teaching should take 
place in a safe and healthy environment. Further, it is stated that the teaching environment 
should promote the opportunities for development and learning and therefore also 
includes the physical- and aesthetical environment.  As the pupils in the schools occupy 
in an environment, where the teachers are working, they are indirectly covered by the 
working environment law from the Danish Working Environment Authority (AT). [LBK nr 
1072]. The law covers many aspects e.g. the work place design, rest periods, the execution 
of the work. Both laws require healthy and safe environments, however none of these 
explain how this should be conducted and fulfilled.
03.2 Indoor Environment and its Effect on Learning 
Ability
The indoor environment in general has a larger impact on children than adults because of 
their physiology. Children breathe a larger volume of air in relation to their body weight 
compared to adults. Further, their organs are still developing. [Mendell & Heath, 2005].
 
As mentioned earlier, several studies showed that the classroom environment had 
an effect on the learning ability of children. Wargocki & Wyon, (2007) performed two 
intervention experiments that investigated the performance on schoolwork when 
classroom temperatures and classroom ventilation rates were moderately raised for one 
week. The experiments were performed with children in the age 10 - 12 years, and showed 
that their performance in different aspects of schoolwork improved significantly when the 
temperature was reduced from 25 °C to 20 °C.  When the ventilation rate was increased 
from 5.2 l/s to 9.6 l/s, the pupils’ schoolwork performance increased. Both experiments 
showed that the pupils improved their speed at completing the tasks along with insignificant 
changes in error rates.
 
Bakó-Biró et al., (2011), performed a similar experiment in England where classroom 
ventilation was investigated in 8 primary schools. An intervention was performed in 16 
classrooms for three weeks, where the ventilation rate was altered from the existing 1 l/s 
per person to 8 l/s per person.  More than 200 pupils performed computerised performance 
tasks, which showed that they were significantly faster and gave more accurate responses 
with the high ventilation rate compared to the existing. The investigation provided strong 
evidence that low ventilation rates reduce the pupils’ memory, concentration and attention, 
and thereby reduced their learning ability.
Toftum et al., (2015), performed a study investigating the associations between learning and 
ventilation mode, which showed that pupils in schools with balanced mechanical ventilation 
or mechanical exhaust typically scored higher in the national test scheme compared with 
pupils in schools with natural ventilation by manual window opening. Further, widespread 
measurements showed that the CO2 concentration was lower in classrooms with balanced 
mechanical ventilation or mechanical exhaust.

04.1 Energy Service Company Projects
Energy Service Company (ESCO) projects are a fairly new concept in Danish context. The 
concept relies on a private ESCO operator undertaking to implement (and possibly finance) 
an energy optimisation of a building or another facility, and accept the risk whether the 
customer will get a lower energy bill or not. The ESCO projects breaks with the traditional 
way of implementing energy renovations, as the risk of the investment will be transferred 
to the private party, who in return gets to decide what needs to be performed in order to 
live up to the guarantee [Pietras et al., 2013].
The main point of the Danish ESCO model is, that the client does not buy a particular 
solution, but a certain energy performance, saving or energy service. Examples of this can 
be to maintain a certain level of indoor climate (temperature and air exchange) at a certain 
price that should be lower than the so far paid, provided that the quality does not change 
essentially. The ESCO operators will therefore typically be in charge of the operation of the 
energy saving solutions, so that they can live up to the guarantee of the lower energy costs 
to the client [Pietras et al., 2013].
ESCO project are typically divided into four phases, see figure 04.2.
Figure 04.2: Four phases of ESCO projects
04.1.1 ESCO Projects in Danish Municipalities
ESCO projects in Danish municipalities however differ from the above presented, as the 
municipalities do not use the third party financing. It has not been in the interest of the 
municipalities to use the third party financing as they have their own credit facilities, where 
they can borrow money at lower interest rates than the ESCO operator can offer. The ESCO 
contracts in Danish municipalities are therefore based on the municipalities financing the 
improvements themselves, while the ESCO operator conducts the solution and guarantees 
a certain reduction of the energy costs and consumption cost. If the guaranteed savings are 
not achieved, the ESCO operator pays the municipality the difference. If higher savings than 
the guaranteed is achieved, the municipality and the ESCO operator share the savings. Most 
often, the contract between the municipality and the ESCO operator includes the possibility 
that the municipality can terminate the cooperation after each phase, as a cooperation that 
lasts 10 - 20 years, can be hard to predict and therefore risky. This gives the municipalities a 
greater sense of security [SBI 2013:10].
The municipalities’ motivation for engaging in ESCO projects are among others, that there is 
a lack of internal resources to carry out energy renovations and it is a large task to implement 
energy efficiency in all of the municipal buildings. Further, there is a certainty that the 
planned energy savings are implemented due to the coverage of the contracts. Particularly 
small and medium sized municipalities have used ESCO projects, as their internal capacity 
is low compared to the larger municipalities. The ESCO projects should however not be 
considered as a goal in itself. If the municipalities implement energy savings otherwise in 
the buildings, there is not the need for ESCO projects, but as stated many municipalities 
have not felt that they had the internal resources to do so [SBI 2013:10].

05. School Building Typologies
The manner in which Danish school buildings are built has developed over time. Teaching 
principles evolve and new methods are introduced, which results in new demands to the 
physical framework of the school. As described in [Hviid et al., 2014], the Danish schools can 
be divided into six main building typologies: village school, storey school, aula school, comb 
school, open plan school and project work school. These typologies express the building 
types that are typical for a given time period for new-built schools. The time intervals are 
indicative and some buildings may differ from the norm. Several schools have since their 
construction expanded and may be a combination of several building typologies [Hviid et 
al., 2014].
According to Realdania et al. (2010), the school building stock can roughly be divided into 
three thirds. The first third consists of storey schools, the second consists of aula school 
and comb school and the last third consists of open plan schools and project work schools. 
Note that a different division in typologies is used and that the village schools are omitted 
in the study by Realdania.

later on.

06. Ventilation Principles
A brief overview of the most common ventilation principles in Danish primary schools 
[Hviid et al., 2014] is given in this section, as they will be discussed later in connection with 
the results. Details like VAV, CAV, throw length, pressure drops, aggregates, ducts, controls, 
sound propagation, filters etc. will not be addressed, as these are beyond the scope of this 
project. 
06.1 Natural Ventilation
Natural ventilation uses natural forces (temperature and wind) to supply and remove fresh 
air to and from a building. The air is exchanged through designed inlets and outlets in the 
building envelope such as windows and air intake valves. Natural ventilation can include 
either manual or automatic opening of windows. The principle may save energy (electricity) 
and space for duct systems and air handling units. However, it is challenging to control and 
to ensure the desired air exchange rate, and there may be large heat consumption along 
with possible draught problems [Hviid et al., 2014].
An explanatory diagram of natural ventilation is shown in figure 06.1. This ventilation 
principle is most common in the village school, storey school, open plan school and project 
work school.
Figure 06.1: Principle of natural ventilation
06.2 Mechanical Exhaust Ventilation
Mechanical exhaust ventilation has air intake through windows or valves and air outlets 
through an exhaust fixture. Mechanical exhaust ventilation can also include preheating 
at the air intake by heating coils or behind radiators. The exhaust can be positioned at 
different suitable places depending on the room geometry. The solution is rather cheap, 
but it may be difficult to ensure the desired air exchange rate due to lack of control of the 
supply air, draught problems and large heat consumption [Hviid et al., 2014].
An explanatory diagram of mechanical ventilation with only exhaust is shown in figure 06.2. 
This ventilation principle is most common at the comb school.

06.3 Balanced Ventilation
Balanced mechanical ventilation has two fixtures: One where the outside air is brought into 
the building, the other exhausting the indoor air, which results in balanced airflows. The 
fixtures can be positioned at different locations depending on the room geometry: centre 
of the room either in the ceiling or near the ceiling, or by or in the wall. Balanced mechanical 
ventilation can be centralised or decentralised, each system both has advantages and 
disadvantages in regards to service and space use. The systems can ensure the desired air 
exchange rate under all weather conditions and if designed correctly causes no draught 
problems. Further the heat consumption is low, as a heat exchanger recovers the heat from 
the exhaust air to the inlet air [Hviid et al., 2014].

ventilation principle is most common at the storey school and aula school.

the ventilation can either be balanced centralized or decentralized or exhaust only. The

07.1.5 Statistics Denmark (STDK)
In order to get an overview of the extent the municipalities have invested in the Danish 
primary schools in the last 10-year period, Statistics Denmark was contacted. Statistics 
Denmark is the central authority on Danish statistics, and collects and compiles the statistics 
on Danish Society.
Data was received on the municipalities’ investments in the primary schools, from the year 
2007 until 2014, and for the budgets in 2015. [REG31] [BUDK32]. Due to the municipality 
merging in 2007, the accountings before this year are not comparable to the years after, 
and therefore this project will only look at the accountings from 2007 and onwards. The 
accountings for the primary schools can be divided into two overall subcategories: ART and 
DRANST, which again can be subcategorised further. ART is a subcategory that specifies the 
municipalities’ expenses and income by their “kind” such as salary, services, purchase of 
goods, in general the use of resources. [OIM, 2014]. DRANST is a subcategory that specifies 
the municipalities’ expenses and income by their “type” such as operation, interests, 
constructions etc. [OIM, 2014].
Two subcategories in the DRANST dataset would be suitable for estimating what the 
municipalities have spent on renovation of primary schools: The operational accounts and

the construction accounts. The difference between these two accounts can be hard to 
tell, and municipalities can register the different projects at different accounts, as it often 
depends on the specific situation. A general approach is to consider if the cost makes a 
substantial change in the usual expenditure of the institutions, and if the work involves 
significant changes in the existing buildings or facilities. If this is the case then the costs 
shall be charged the construction account, but if not it should be charged the operational 
costs [OIM, 2014].

07.3.1 Method for Determining School Building Typologies
Schools built in the same period and with the same general building practice often share 
the same challenges regarding energy and indoor climate. An important step in this project 
was to establish a method to determine the building typology of primary schools.
 
The building typologies were based on construction year as described in [Hviid et al., 2014], 
see section 05. Note that the 10-year gap between 1980 and 1990, which was not assigned 
a typology, was divided equally between the open plan school and the project work schools. 
The time intervals were as follows:
•	
Village School (Before 1880)
•	
Storey School (1880 - 1930 and 1945 - 1960)
•	
Aula School (1930 - 1945)
•	
Comb School (1960 - 1970)
•	
Open Plan School (1970 - 1985)
•	
Project Work School (1985 - now)
The majority of the schools consist of several buildings, built in different periods of time. 
Therefore the typology of the individual buildings in a school may vary. A school may consist 
of several building typologies, for instance, if originally constructed as a village school before 
1880 and then expanded in the 1960’s. Such is the case for the majority of the primary 
schools. The data to be investigated in this project was, however, given per school, and it 
was therefore necessary to determine a single general typology for each school in order to 
process the data (energy labels, indoor climate perception and renovation initiatives).
==PAGE:54==
43
METHOD
The building typologies were determined based on the years of construction and the 
building areas (BBR-area) given in the BBR-data. The area of the buildings at a school were 
added for each of the six typologies to determine, which building typology within a school 
that represented the prevailing building typology. The ratio of this area, Amax, to the total 
area of the school was then determined, Amax,%, and used to specify the typology of the 
school. 
 
Example: A school consists of two buildings with different building typologies, 5,000 m2 
storey school and 10,000 m2 open plan school. The open plan school would be the dominant 
typology as Amax is equal to 10,000 m2, resulting in an Amax,% of 67 %.

A preliminary analysis of the DCUM data was carried out for the results containing the 
responses from all 590 primary schools. The reason for maintaining the division in these 
three groups was to evaluate if the pupils’ perception of their educational environment 
depended on their age, and if the data could be combined for later processing. The initial 
observations showed significant association between the replies and grade intervals 
(p<0.05). Note that the number of responses varied for each question. A summary of the 
association is presented, see the full results in Appendix C.1.
Academic learning environment
•	
Pupils in 0th - 3rd grade had most interest in learning (85 %). No significant deviation in 4th - 6th and 
7th - 10th grade pupils’ interest in learning (around 80 %).
•	
Pupils in 0th - 3rd had most difficulty in concentrating (38 % found it easy to concentrate in most 
or all subjects). 
•	
Pupils 4th - 6th grade (59 %) in found it easier to concentrate than pupils in 7th - 10th grade (56 %).
•	
The older the pupils, the fewer agreed that they did well academically (82 %, 74 % and 70 %).
Physical health
•	
Nearly 40 % of pupils in 0th - 3rd often had headaches. Pupils in 7th - 10th grade (18 %) had more 
frequent headaches within the last two weeks than the pupils in 4th - 6th grade (12 %).
•	
Pupils in 0th - 3rd and 7th - 10th grade less often felt fresh, and more often tired than pupils in 4th 
- 6th grade.
•	
The older the pupils, the higher absence from school.
Indoor climate: Lighting conditions
•	
The pupils in 4th - 6th grade (81 %) were more satisfied with the lighting conditions than the 
7th - 10th grade (75 %). Less pupils in 0th - 3rd grade found that there was sufficient light in the 
classroom (64 %).
Indoor climate: Air quality
•	
Pupils in 7th - 10th grade (46 %) were less satisfied with the air quality than those in 4th - 6th grade 
(62 %).
•	
Less 7th - 10th grade pupils described the air as fresh, more as dense or heavy, than 4th - 6th grade.
•	
The older the pupils, the more found that there was a bad smell in the classroom (6 %, 8 % and 
20 %). 
•	
The older the pupils, the less answered that there was an agreement about when to open the 
windows (28 %, 24 % and 18 %) and the fewer answered they did not know (24 %, 20 % and 13 
%). 
Indoor climate: Thermal indoor climate
•	
The older the pupils, the more were dissatisfied with the temperature (62 %, 59 % and 42 %).
•	
The older the pupils, the less found the temperature to be comfortable and more that it was too 
cold, too warm, changing or that there was a draught.
•	
More pupils in the 7th - 10th grade (28 %) than in the 4th - 6th grade (16 %) found the possibility to 
regulate the temperature poor.
It was decided, that it was not possible to combine data from 0th - 3rd grade with data from 
other grades, as the questions were not directly comparable due to the phrasing. Generally, 
the pupils in 7th - 10th grade were more critical than the pupils in 4th - 6th grade. However, 
it was found that despite the differences in the results from 4th - 6th and 7th - 10th grade, 
when viewing the results on a building typology level, were up to 2 percentage point and 
it was concluded, that these data could be merged for further investigations. This was an 
advantage as the number of schools represented would increase and the results be more 
robust.

08.6.4 Renovation Initiatives and Perceived Educational 
Environment
This section describes the perceived educational environment in comparison to the 
performed initiatives. The aspects of the educational environment that were included were 
indoor air quality, thermal indoor environment and lighting conditions. 
The DCUM data was used to describe the overall perceived indoor climate of the school, 
since it was not known in which specific buildings the pupils were located. Schools where 
the extent of the renovation initiatives was limited to a few rooms were disregarded, since 
the renovation initiatives were evaluated to have little influence on the overall indoor 
climate. Only the renovations that affected a larger part of the school will therefore be 
presented in this section. 
Indoor Air Quality
Renovation initiatives that may influence the indoor air quality are primarily those regarding 
the ventilation system and windows. Installing a new ventilation system, replacing an older 
model or changing filters may lead to higher air intake. Replacing windows with more air 
tight windows could reduce the infiltration. 
The municipalities indicated in the questionnaires that renovation initiatives regarding 
ventilation and windows were performed on 22 schools within the last ten years. Figure 
08.47 shows the perceived indoor air quality for these schools distributed by three 
combinations of initiatives. Note that other initiatives might be performed on these schools.
Figure 08.47: Perception of air quality distributed by three combinations of renovation initiatives performed on

The schools that had performed initiatives regarding ventilation had the highest percentage 
of pupils satisfied with the indoor air quality, followed by the schools that had performed a 
combination of windows and ventilation initiatives. The schools where fewest pupils were 
satisfied with the indoor air quality were those that had performed renovation initiatives 
regarding windows only.
The perceived air quality is influenced by the condition of the buildings and need of renovation 
as well as the type of ventilation system installed and geometry of the classrooms. A good 
evaluation of air quality can also mean that there was not that large a need for renovating.

08.6.5 Summary of Results
The results showed that most renovations were performed on an entire wing or building. 
In general, the most common renovation initiatives concerned lighting and windows. The 
results suggested that the less comprehensive renovation initiatives with higher financial 
viability were more common. There were more renovation initiatives in the period from 
2010 until present than in the period 2005 - 2009. This distribution suggests a delayed effect 
of the quality improvement fund in the year 2010. The most common renovation initiatives 
varied when compared to the extent of the renovations. The majority of the renovations 
were not performed in connection with maintenance and only in few cases it was indicated 
that other factors had an influence.   
The investments in school renovations showed large variations for the individual schools and 
municipalities. Comparing the renovation investments with the construction investments 
from Statistics Denmark, showed no compliance. 
No significant association between renovation initiatives and building typologies was 
present, However, a significant association between the extent and building typologies was 
present. It could be seen that the number of renovation initiatives performed on entire 
schools decreased the newer the building typology.
The schools that had performed initiatives regarding ventilation had a higher percentage 
pupils satisfied with the indoor air quality, followed by the schools that had a combination 
of both ventilation and window renovation. The individual cases showed some similarities.
The schools that had performed a combination of windows, ventilation and heating 
renovation had a higher percentage of pupils satisfied with the thermal environment. The 
case with heating renovation had the next highest percentage. However, these cases were 
based on limited data. The individual cases showed no real similarities.
No association between the perceived lighting conditions and renovation of lighting system 
was found. The renovations may impact the energy consumption more than the indoor 
climate as a large part of the lighting conditions are due to geometry of room and windows.

09. Discussion
The objective of this project was to establish a database of the Danish primary school 
buildings and to identify the most frequent renovation initiatives performed within the last 
ten years regarding energy efficiency and indoor climate. Based on existing databases and 
information collected by questionnaires, the project investigated the association between 
building characteristics, energy performance, perceived educational environment, 
ventilation principles and renovation initiatives. At this time, no database describing the 
condition of Danish primary school buildings exists. A platform to share knowledge and 
experience in renovation of school buildings would be beneficial, as part of a renovation 
strategy towards successful renovations. In order to reach the goals of the Danish Energy 
Strategy and improve the indoor climate in Danish primary schools, we see a potential 
in using existing databases along with information regarding performed renovations. This 
would benefit municipalities, professionals and last but not least the schools and pupils.
The majority of schools were distributed in six general building typologies based on a 
classification scheme suggested in a previous study describing general building characteristics 
[Hviid et al., 2014]. The developed method for determining building typologies resulted in 
the following distribution; 4 village schools, 272 storey schools, 40 aula schools, 213 comb 
schools, 241 open plan schools and 51 project work schools. The limitations in this method 
will be discussed in section 09.1 Study Limitations.
It was possible to find energy labels for 1,206 schools. Overall, the results regarding energy 
performance showed a connection between the year of construction and the distribution 
of energy labels by the schools’ BBR-area. As expected, the percentage of BBR-area with 
energy efficient energy labels (A-C) generally increased the younger the buildings. This 
corresponds to the development in the building regulations concerning energy consumption 
and the growing awareness on energy efficiency after the energy crises in the 1970’s. Some 
older buildings had a higher percentage of energy efficient energy labels. This may be due 
to renovation initiatives performed in connection to either maintenance or to save energy 
and reduce operational expenses. 
An important aspect of the project was to map school renovations. For only 6 % of the 
BBR-area, it was indicated in the energy label database that “significant renovation” was 
performed within the last ten years. However, it was questionable if the energy labels 
had been updated since the renovation, as required, and how the term was interpreted. 
Therefore this information was not used.
Based on 729 schools, the results showed a significant association between energy labels 
and building typology. The newest typology, project work school, had the highest percentage 
of energy efficient energy labels. This typology is the only one to be influenced by the 
restrictions on energy frame in the building regulations as the time interval is from 1985 to 
present. The oldest typology, village school, had the lowest percentage of energy efficient 
energy labels but was only based two schools. The intermediate typologies had somewhat 
identical distribution of energy labels and no systematic deviation was found. This may be 
due to renovation initiatives and uncertainties in determining the building typology.
An energy label is a description of the energy related quality of a building and is not 
influenced by user behaviour, but depends on several factors such as the overall condition 
of the building, installations and geometry. The distribution of energy labels by BBR-area 
showed that more buildings with balanced ventilation had an energy label from A-C. This 
may be because it is more common in newer buildings to have balanced ventilation and 
therefore better energy performance.

Unfortunately, it was not possible to investigate the association between renovation 
initiatives and energy labels, since several of the energy label reports were dated before 
the year of renovation. Building owners are in principle obliged to update the label after 
major alterations.
 
The initial results based on 590 schools showed a difference in the perceived indoor climate 
depending on the age of the pupils. The data from 0th - 3rd grade were not directly comparable 
with that from 4th - 6th and 7th - 10th grade, and were excluded from other analyses. However, 
a distinct difference in the perception of the educational environment of the pupils in 0th 
- 3rd grade compared to 4th - 10th grade was that nearly 40 % of the pupils in 0th - 3rd grade 
often had headaches and 12 % found it difficult to concentrate, which was substantially 
higher than for the other two grade intervals. This may be due to other factors, such as 
noise or general wellbeing in the school, which was beyond the scope of this thesis. 
The pupils in 7th - 10th grade were more dissatisfied in their perception of the educational 
environment compared to the pupils in 4th - 6th grade, except regarding the general interest 
in learning, where the results were identical. A higher percentage of the pupils in 7th - 
10th grade were dissatisfied with the indoor air quality, thermal environment and lighting 
conditions and a higher percentage of the pupils found it difficult to concentrate and 
disagreed that their academic performance was well or very well. The responses regarding 
physical health were also more critical in 7th - 10th grade compared to 4th - 6th grade. A reason 
for the general difference in the perceived indoor climate in the two age groups might be 
differences in user behaviour. When the pupils are younger a teacher may be responsible 
for them to go outside and open the windows during recess. This corresponds with the fact 
that a lower percentage of the older students answered to have an agreement about when 
to open windows in the classroom. Secondly, the ability to perceive the indoor climate 
evolves and therefore it is possible that the older pupils are more critical in their perception 
of the indoor climate, however, still not aware of how to improve it. 
Based on 260 schools, a significant association was found between building typology and 
perceived educational environment. The village school had only 7 pupil responses and 
was therefore omitted. The questions regarding general interest, concentration, academic 
performance and absenteeism showed similar results for each building typology. Generally, 
a higher percentage of pupils satisfied with the air quality and thermal environment was 
found, the newer the building typology, with exception of the storey schools. The difference 
between the intermediate typologies (storey school, comb school, open plan school) was 
however not that large. The project work school had the highest percentage of pupils 
satisfied with the indoor air quality and thermal environment (64 % and 57 %, respectively,) 
and the aula school the least (46 % for both indoor air quality and thermal environment). 
92 % of the project work schools had balanced mechanical ventilation, whereas the aula 
schools had 62 % natural ventilation and 38 % balanced mechanical ventilation (based 
on 172 schools). This suggests that the differences in pupil perceptions were due to the 
ventilation type rather than the building typology. The results regarding lighting conditions 
showed similar distribution, however the differences were not that large between the 
different typologies.
Three types of ventilation systems were considered; balanced mechanical ventilation, 
mechanical exhaust and manual opening of windows or valves (natural ventilation). 
Through online questionnaires the schools indicated the main ventilation type. Based on 
172 schools, no significant association between main ventilation type and building typology 
was found. Balanced mechanical ventilation was most common in newer building typologies 
but generally rather frequent in all typologies. It should be noted that the distribution of 
ventilation types by building typology varied from the distribution presented in a previous 
==PAGE:104==
93
DISCUSSION
study [Hviid et al., 2014]. This may be because renovations and installations of new systems 
were not included in the previous study or due to uncertainties related to the method for 
determining the building typology.
The results showed that pupils in schools with mechanical exhaust as the main ventilation 
system were less satisfied with the air quality in the classrooms compared to pupils in 
schools that had balanced and natural ventilation. The Mass Experiments showed that 
there was a significant association between measured CO2 concentration and applied 
ventilation principle. The average CO2 concentration for balanced ventilation was 1,099 
ppm, for mechanical exhaust 1,380 ppm and for natural ventilation 1,813 ppm [Clausen et 
al., 2014]. Contrary to expected, the results showed that the indoor climate in schools with 
natural ventilation was perceived equally well as in the schools with balanced ventilation. 
It should be noted that the DCUM questionnaires on educational environment were 
answered during different seasons and represented both summer and winter situations. 
Further, it is possible that the pupils were located in other parts of the school with a 
different type of ventilation than the main ventilation type. However, the results showed 
that pupils in schools with natural ventilation had most difficulty in concentrating and 
evaluated their academic performance lower. Similarly, other studies showed that pupils 
in schools with balanced mechanical ventilation had significantly higher achievement 
indicators than pupils in schools with natural ventilation [Toftum et al., 2015]. A reason for 
poorer academic achievement with this ventilation type may be lower ventilation rates and 
noise from the outdoors when opening windows. The schools with mechanical exhaust and 
balanced ventilation showed similar results in the pupils’ evaluation of their performance, 
unlike other studies that found that pupils in schools with mechanical exhaust (and natural 
ventilation) had lower scores in national tests compared to balanced ventilation [Kjeldsen 
et al., 2013]. 
The results concerning thermal comfort by ventilation type showed no large difference 
between building typologies.
The general renovation analyses in this study were based on 125 schools. The results 
showed that almost half of the school renovations concerned an entire building or wing, 
followed by the entire school and lastly only a few rooms. A renovation initiative performed 
on an entire school can be expensive and comprehensive, and many municipalities may 
not have the necessary funds and are perhaps more inclined to perform initiatives related 
to general maintenance of the buildings. Further, the renovation demand might not be 
present in the entire school, as the schools often consist of several buildings from various 
years of construction. A reason for the small number of renovation initiatives regarding 
few rooms can be the relatively small benefits concerning energy savings compared to the 
investment. Lastly, the municipalities may not have the same overview of smaller initiatives 
compared to more extensive renovation initiatives. 
Overall, primarily lighting systems and windows were renovated. Renovation of ventilation 
systems was the third most common initiative. The primary energy factors may have an 
influence on the choice of renovation initiatives, as electricity weighs 2.5 times the heating 
energy. The results show that focus may not be on improving the indoor climate, but 
more on the financial viability in relation to the pay-back time. The results also showed 
that renewable energy such as solar power was the fourth most common initiative, which 
supports the conclusion of focus on financial viability.
In 2005 - 2009, fewer renovation initiatives were performed compared to 2010 - 2015, 
especially the initiatives regarding the extent of entire schools. It is possible that this is a 
delayed effect of the quality improvement fund, which should be spent in 2009 - 2018, but 
may also be because several municipalities were not able to retrieve information this far 
back. There was no association between the extent of renovation initiatives and year of 
renovation.

The background for 37 % of the school renovations was maintenance. For 11 % of the 
schools it was indicated that other factors influenced the renovations including PCB, 
mould, fungus or injunctions from the Danish Working Environment Authority. Further, for 
more than half of the schools it was not indicated whether other factors influenced the 
renovations. In other words, these are all reasons that require renovation to make the 
building usable, and thereby not a planned renovation. This supports the perception of the 
potential need to develop an adequate database to use for the schools and municipalities, 
to improve the school buildings in general, so unplanned and very urgent renovations may 
be avoided. In contrast some schools stated that the renovations were made because it was 
part of an ESCO project.
The inventory of the municipality construction investments showed that Region Hovedstaden 
had higher expenses on primary schools than the other regions, both in total and when 
normalised by BBR-area of schools, number of inhabitants and number of children in the 
age 6 - 15 years. In 2007 - 2015, Hovedstaden had a total investment of 5,866 DKK/m2 
school, where the other regions had a more equal distribution, as the total investment in 
the same years were between 2,788 - 2,511 DKK/m2 school. It appeared that København 
(Copenhagen) and Gentofte were the municipalities that especially contributed to the high 
investments in Region Hovedstaden. Reasons for this can be found in that Copenhagen has 
many newcomers, also children, every year, and the tendency is still rising. Gentofte has 
had a great focus on renewal of their primary schools with the project SKUB. 
Based on 100 schools, the results on the investments in the renovations regarding energy 
and indoor climate showed large differences in the size of investments, as they ranged from 
19 DKK/m2 school to 8,460 DKK/m2 school. However, this does not necessary imply that the 
schools with the smallest investments are in the worst state of condition, as these may not 
need further renovations. Also at a municipality level, the renovation investments showed 
large differences. There was no compliance between the renovation investments and the 
construction investments. The construction investments in general were larger since they 
include e.g. new buildings, as mentioned.
Based on 77 schools, no significant association between building typologies and the 
renovation initiatives were found.  In contrast, there was a significant association between 
the extent of the renovations and the building typologies. The number of renovation 
initiatives performed on entire schools decreased in newer building typologies, whereas 
the renovation initiatives performed on a few rooms increased. The distribution of the 
extent according to the building typologies is reasonable, as it would be assumed that the 
newer the buildings, the lower the renovation demand. No village school was represented. 
The number of aula school and open plan schools were limited.
 
The renovation initiatives were compared to the perceived indoor air quality, thermal 
environment and lighting conditions. The selected cases contained renovation initiatives 
that might influence these three aspects of the indoor environment. The comparison of the 
perceived indoor air quality showed that the schools that had renovation of the ventilation 
had a higher percentage of satisfied pupils, followed by the schools that had combined 
renovation of ventilation and windows. The isolated cases showed some similarities.
The comparison of the perceived thermal environment showed that the schools that had 
the renovation combination of windows, ventilation and heating had the highest percentage 
of satisfied pupils, followed by the schools that only renovated the heating system. The 
isolated cases showed no real similarities. 
No association was found between perceived lighting conditions and renovation of lighting 
systems. Renovation of lighting systems may impact the energy consumption more than the 
actual lighting conditions, as these renovations could vary from changing lighting fixtures to

installing automatic control systems. The daylight conditions, which depend on geometrical 
factors, would perhaps influence the perceived lighting conditions more than the artificial 
lighting conditions. 
For all of the comparisons, it will not only apply that the performed renovations would have 
an impact on the perceived educational environment, as it will also depend on the state of 
the buildings. A good evaluation of the perceived indoor climate can also mean that there 
was not a need for a renovation. The renovation initiatives carried out on a building or wing 
might not also represent the actual responses of the pupils, as these could be located in 
different parts of the schools with poorer or better conditions.
09.1 Study Limitations
As any study, this study had its limitations due to different aspects of the study design as well 
as the data collection. Further, there were limitations in the data from existing databases.
A limitation in this project was the determination of building typologies. Danish primary 
schools often consist of multiple buildings from different years and therefore may vary in 
building practice and functional layout within the same school. A previous study divided 
the Danish primary school building stock into six building typologies by time intervals [Hviid 
et al., 2014]. The method for determining building typologies used in this thesis was based 
on that study. Since the vast majority of the schools consist of multiple typologies, it was 
chosen to assign building typologies by the prevailing typology, which should represent at 
least 75 % of the school based on BBR-area. The 75 % was decided based on the observed 
influence of including/excluding schools on results, supplemented by a number of random 
samples based on satellite images of the schools. This was in order to obtain a larger data 
foundation, however, had the disadvantage of larger uncertainties in the building typologies. 
46 % of the schools had a prevailing typology above 90 %, 15 % of the schools had between 
75-90 %. The remaining 39 % of schools were not matched to a building typology and thus 
omitted from the investigations. 
There was a distinct difference in the representation of the different building typologies 
and especially the number of village schools was limited. This was due to the defining year 
intervals, where in some periods fewer school buildings were built. The need to match data 
from various sources reduced the number of schools in the analyses. However, the relative 
distribution more or less remained. The year intervals of the typologies were meant to be 
indicative and therefore there may be uncertainties at the transitions between intervals 
where the building typologies were not entirely correct. Transitions between building 
typologies are more likely to happen gradually.
There are limitations connected to the building typologies themselves since they do not take 
into account renovations or replacement of technical installations. Instead they relate to 
the original building and architectural expression, and may be misleading when considering 
the energy consumption and the perceived indoor climate, since several alterations could 
have been performed since the construction. 
Another limitation to this study was regarding the use of data from the BBR-register and the 
energy label database. As some schools could not be identified in BBR, there were doubts 
whether the data was up to date. Further, several of the energy label reports stated that the 
BBR-area differed from the actual heated area of the buildings. For several energy labels, 
the BBR-area differed distinctly from the area stated in the BBR-register, assumed to be due 
to merging of several buildings in one energy label. In some cases, the date of the energy 
labels was prior to the renovation indicated by the municipalities, meaning that the energy 
label had not been updated as required.

There were also limitations regarding the DCUM data. Due to the way the schools were 
registered in the database, it was not possible to differentiate between sub departments of 
some schools, which could therefore not be matched to building typologies. The data was 
based on questionnaires answered in the school years 2012  - 13, 2013  - 14 and 2014 -15 
and from various seasons. 
Generally, there was a difference in the resources that the municipalities were able to 
allocate to retrieve the information about school renovations. 77 municipalities declined 
participating in the project due to lack of resources and because the data was not 
immediately available. The municipalities that completed the questionnaires had different 
levels of information available about the renovations of the primary schools. Several 
municipalities stated that this information was not catalogued in a database, and therefore 
the completed questionnaires were based on what could be remembered or quickly 
retrieved from the archives. Some municipalities responded that information was lost in 
the municipality merging in 2007. 
The collected questionnaires showed a difference in the level of detail of the answers, as 
well as misinterpretations. A couple of municipalities completed the questionnaires by 
stating all of the performed renovations and the extent of these in one row for each school. 
This means that it was not possible to differentiate between the extent of each renovation 
initiative and it was chosen to assign the initiatives to the largest extent. Likewise, several 
municipalities stated the total renovation investment for each school, and not for each 
individual initiative. 
If a school had been renovated multiple times and the questionnaires were completed in 
one row per each school, it was not possible to associate the specific renovation with the 
correct year, and the number of renovations was uncertain. This was interpreted from the 
authors to be a substantial source of error for the project, and therefore these schools were 
excluded from the analyses. This concerned 38 schools out of the total 198. Furthermore, 4 
schools were excluded, as it was not indicated which renovations were performed. 
Another uncertainty lies in the year of renovation, as this could be interpreted. The 
renovation period may be longer in comprehensive renovations and may last more than 
one year. The stated year of renovation could be the initiating year or the finalisation year. 
This would result in a misleading distribution of the renovations according to year. The 
insecurity in the year of renovation may also affect the results regarding the association 
between renovation initiatives and perceived educational environment. The effect of the 
renovation might not be perceptible in the school at the stated year of renovation.
A general limitation of the project was that the renovation demand of the school buildings 
was not identified, only the performed renovation initiatives. Further, it was not possible to 
map the effect of the renovation on energy consumption and indoor climate, as sufficient 
data prior to the renovations were not obtained.

09.2 Further Studies
Due to the time limit of this project it was not possible to realise all ideas. If similar studies 
were to be conducted, the following points could be interesting to investigate further and 
resolve limitations in this project.
•	
Improve the design of the municipality questionnaires to be more detailed, while 
attaching an exact explanation of the intention in order to avoid misinterpretation.
•	
Include authorities to support the collection of renovation data from municipalities and 
schools, making them allocate the necessary resources, and thereby obtain a sufficient 
database for further analysis.
•	
Develop the building typology method to take into account the renovations and 
installations (require identification of these).   
•	
Investigate the municipalities’ motivation for performing school renovations (e.g. 
financial gain, green profile, general financial ability, maintenance, injunctions, etc.)
•	
Further examine the investments in renovation initiatives regarding indoor climate and 
energy consumption, to get an overview of the precise investments in this area. 
•	
Map the effects of the renovation initiatives. Gain more knowledge on the indoor 
climate, energy consumption as well as user behaviour before and after a renovation.
•	
Validate the data collection of perceived educational environment by measurements 
on e.g. representative schools before and after renovation.     
•	
Investigate planned renovation initiatives and determine the general renovation 
demand of the schools.  
An interesting aspect to investigate is the actual possibility of establishing a database 
containing basic information on buildings and installations, building energy consumption, 
perceived indoor climate and performed renovations in Danish primary schools. The 
analyses presented in this thesis were based on existing comprehensive databases and 
collection of additional information, which forms a substantial data foundation, despite the 
mentioned limitations.
•	
The BBR-register could be expanded to include building installations, performed 
renovation initiatives and state of conditions, which could be registered continuously 
(information regarding additional insulation of building envelope has recently been 
added) 
•	
The energy labelling scheme describes the energy related quality of buildings 
(disregarding user behaviour) and could be used in connection to the database. It is 
however important that the labels are updated to correspond with alterations in the 
buildings. 
•	
Optimally, a system gathering the actual energy consumption would be more accurate 
as it also takes into account the user behaviour. This would require more maintenance 
and at least yearly updates. It could be implemented by law, that all public building 
should report this every year in a database in order to reach the goals of the Danish 
Energy Strategy. 
•	
The government could incorporate questionnaires regarding the perceived educational 
environment in the mandatory educational environment evaluation (UMV), to be 
completed by pupils in primary school. This could be a cost-effective method of 
gathering important information. The existing tool Termometeret could be a way of 
gathering the information.

•	
Overall, it was discovered that it was not as simple as expected to gather information 
from the municipalities regarding renovation of school buildings. The municipalities 
could do a greater effort of registering and systematising performed and future 
renovations. However this would have to be introduced on a higher level of decision, 
due to the limited resources.
The benefit of the building database would be for municipalities and schools to be able to 
share experiences in building renovations based on similar projects. This could provide a 
step towards renovations at a higher quality. At the same time they would be easier and 
cheaper than before, as many initial investigations could be omitted. Such a database could 
be implemented not only for primary schools but for all public buildings as well as private 
buildings, where the level of required information could be adjusted according to the usage.

CONCLUSION
10. Conclusion
It was possible to categorise the majority (60 %) of the Danish primary school buildings by 
six typologies; village school, storey school, aula school, comb school, open plan school 
and project work school based on year of construction. The associations between building 
typologies, ventilation types, energy labels, perceived educational environment and 
performed renovation of Danish primary schools were investigated. 
Generally, an improvement in the percentage of energy-efficient energy labels was seen 
the newer the buildings, corresponding to the growing awareness on energy efficiency 
since the energy crises in the 1970’s. A significant association between building typologies 
and energy labels was found. The newest typology, project work school, had more energy 
efficient energy labels than the oldest typology, village school. The intermediate typologies 
had similarly distributed energy labels, presumably due to renovations. The distribution of 
energy labels showed that more buildings with balanced ventilation had an energy label 
from A-C, which may be because several newer typologies had balanced ventilation.
Likewise, a significant association between building typology and perceived educational 
environment was found. A higher percentage of pupils were satisfied with the air quality 
and thermal environment in the classrooms in newer building typologies. No significant 
association between building typology and main type of ventilation was found. It was 
found that pupils in schools with mechanical exhaust were less satisfied with the indoor 
air quality and temperature compared to the schools with balanced mechanical ventilation 
and natural ventilation (manually through windows or valves). Pupils in schools with natural 
ventilation found it most difficult to concentrate.
Renovations of 125 schools showed that the most common renovation initiatives were 
regarding artificial lighting systems and windows. The third most common initiative was 
ventilation and fourth was renewable energy. The initiatives suggested that the municipalities 
had a focus on financial viability when renovating school buildings, and not on improving 
indoor climate alone. No significant association between renovation initiatives and building 
typologies was found. The most common renovation extent was an entire wing or building. 
A significant association between the extent of the renovation and the building typology 
was found. The number of renovation initiatives performed on entire schools decreased 
in newer building typologies. The school renovations had different backgrounds, such as 
maintenance or more urgent matters such as PCB, mould, fungus, or injunctions from 
Danish Working Environment Authority. 
Some associations were discovered between performed renovation initiatives and perceived 
educational environment. It was seen that the perception of indoor climate varied between 
the individual cases. A limitation in this project was that the current renovation demand for 
each school was unknown.
The project shows a potential for developing a common platform based on existing 
databases, to be used by schools, municipalities and building developers, to identify the 
most effective methods for improving indoor climate and energy consumption in Danish 
primary schools. Several aspects influencing the indoor climate and energy consumption 
should be investigated further. A limitation in the project was the limited resources the 
municipalities could allocate for participating and the amount of available data. Further, 
the building typology method used had some limitations and could be further developed, 
or other methods of classification could be investigated. The classification should not 
be defined only by year of construction but include renovation demand and building 
installations.
