Original project plan (04.08.2015) 
The master thesis project consists of two main parts. In the first part, laboratory analysis will 
be carried out on samples collected from two full-scale biogas plants taking part in the project, 
in order to define their exact composition. This set of data – covering a period of three months 
– will then be gathered and visualized, in an attempt to define the trends and characteristics of 
the full-scale biogas production processes in question. The second part of the project involves 
the computer modelling of the processes taking place at the above biogas plants, based on initial 
composition and operation data received from the sites. The outputs of the simulations will then 
be compared to the biogas plants’ data, and in light of the findings, several sensitivity analyses 
will be executed. Ideally, the conclusion of the project shall include – based on the data 
comparison and sensitivity analyses – recommendations on how the production processes 
followed by each plant could be further optimized, keeping in mind the sustainability and 
economic feasibility of the processes.  
A detailed description of the biogas production processes – pursued by Lemvig Biogasanlæg 
A.m.b.A. (Lemvig) and Linko Gas A.m.b.A. (Linko Gas) – will be defined, including process 
flowcharts. All potentially available information about plant setup and operation, substrate 
composition and other relevant parameters will be enclosed to the description. Samples from 
both sites will be collected, twice every week for three months, involving 9 reactors (4 and 5, 
respectively), industrial and manure input lines within the plants. These samples will then be 
analysed for total solid (TS) and volatile solid (VS) content, volatile fatty acids (VFA) and 
alcohols, total soluble sugar content, total nitrogen (T-N) and total phosphorous (T-P), ammonia 
nitrogen content (NH4-N) and last, but not least total lipids. Along with the samples, digitally 
collected process data will be extracted from both external parties, to cover the period between 
2013 and 2015. Once all the above data will have been gathered, the results will be visualized 
graphically and analysed for comparison with modelling results. 
Modelling of the two full-scale biogas production systems – utilizing the Biomodel created by 
Angelidaki et al. (1993, 1999) – will take place using the information collected from the plants, 
both readily available and resulting from laboratory analysis. The operation of the nine reactors 
for which data will have been collected, will be simulated for three months, taking into account 
the actual operation conditions that apply in the full-scale production during that period. The 
outputs of model simulations will then be compared to the biogas plants’ data, and the 
==PAGE:4==

conclusion on how well the simulations cover the results of actual operations will be made. 
Based on the findings, further sensitivity analyses will be run, using those data that were 
collected apart from the nine reactors. Inputs to reactors, thus inocula will be varied and 
simulated in different compositions, to find potentially more optimal alternatives for feeding 
schedules and initial substrate compositions. Further recommendations will be made on the 
sustainability and (economic) feasibility of the production systems at the two plants, along with 
any other aspects of their processes that might be deemed relevant during the preparation of the 
project. 
Revised project plan (15.01.2016) 
The master thesis project was organised into three main phases of work. In the first one, 
approximately from August to December 2015, laboratory analyses had been carried out on 
various substrate and reactor samples provided by Lemvig. In doing so, complete 
characterisation of the substrates and reactor contents was done, on one hand to generate 
material for a future central database of sample characteristics and on the other, to provide input 
parameters for the third or modelling phase.  
The second phase, running from October to December 2015, covered the implementation and 
further development of the 1998 version of the BioModel tool in a joint MATLAB-Excel 
computer software platform. The aim here was to create a successor for the model that matches 
its scientific content well, and offers more or improved functionalities, according to the current 
needs in modelling anaerobic digestion processes. Detailed research was done on historical and 
contemporary models of anaerobic digestion, as well as the programming language of 
MATLAB, in order to better understand the computational trends in dynamic modelling and 
create a tool that can successfully combine the advantages of both softwares. 
Finally, during the last phase, computer simulations were carried out from mid-December 2015 
to January 2016, using the new implementation of the BioModel tool and phase one data both 
as parameter inputs and as reference. In preparation for this phase, simulations involving 
previous studies and done with the 1998 version of the BioModel were used to validate the new 
model implementation, highlighting the key similarities, differences and points for 
improvement. The project work ends with a written thesis report, documenting the individual 
stages and aspects of the work process.

Brief auto-evaluation of the project process (21.01.2016) 
Due to certain obligations of the company representative, Linko Gas eventually could not 
provide material and data input for the project, as opposed to the original plan. Hence, already 
in the early phase of work, the amount of processable samples and consequently the diversity 
of full-scale process information available was reduced to those provided by Lemvig. 
Laboratory analyses were also prolonged, following an initial period of good progress with pH, 
volatile fatty acid, total and volatile solid, and ammonia nitrogen measurements. The remaining 
experiments required longer preparation times, special pre-treatment of samples, equipment 
that could not be used without supervision or a mix of the above.  
Once the schedule for the analytical part of the project was under control, it also turned out that 
model development requires more time and attention than previously assumed, even to achieve 
a satisfactory level of usability. By the end of the thesis project, it became evident that it was 
the most energy-intensive task. This also slightly shifted the preliminary project focus: from 
using simulations to find the most ideal feeding routines for a plant, to using them for model 
diagnostics. The cumulated result of the above discussed challenges was that neither multiple 
simulations for a given scenario, nor detailed sensitivity analyses could be performed, 
decreasing the reliability of the simulation results. However, through the constant use of the 
BioModel, key areas of issues and behavioural imbalances could be located, setting the path for 
a structured development process in the months following the submission of the thesis. In light 
of the project re-evaluation, socio-economic and environmental aspects of were not covered on 
purpose, as they were found to fall outside the boundaries relevant for consideartion.

ABSTRACT 
In order to prepare the full characterisation of animal manure and industrial waste substrate 
samples and reactor content samples provided by Lemvig Biogasanlæg A.m.b.A (Lemvig), 
analytical determinations were done under laboratory circumstances. Experimental data 
originating from the measurements was compared to published research conducted previously. 
Parallel with the analyses, a new and enhanced implementation of the 1998 BioModel 
(Angelidaki et al., 1999) was created, using a combination of the MATLAB and Microsoft 
Excel software platforms. Following the initial model validation using manure, pure substrates 
and a co-digestion case study published earlier, computer simulations were run with input 
parameters defined from the outcome of the Lemvig substrate characterisation. Results were 
compared quantitatively and visually to those of reactor content analyses. Relative methane 
yield simulations of the new and original edition of the BioModel showed close alignment for 
manure and pure substrates, however when contrasted, the co-digestion case study and Lemvig 
data simulation results differed significantly on biogas-related calculations. As a conclusion, it 
was found that the gas-forecasting module of the newly implemented BioModel requires 
adjustment, in order to be successfully applied for the simulation of certain co-digestion 
processes, similar to the original model version. In addition, suggestions were made on how to 
further model improvement, with several aspects of the tool proposed for revision.

1 INTRODUCTION 
There has been a rapid development in the field of computational tools in the past few decades 
(Lin & Wooley, 2005; Montuschi et al, 2014; Simonet et al, 2015; Gibbs, 2015), bringing about 
a radical change in how data is processed today. With more and more people gaining access to 
these powerful resources and the collective intelligence gathered by society through sharing 
their experience on platforms like the internet (Schilling, 2013), it is clear that this trend is about 
to continue. Considering that this vast amount of information is gradually becoming available 
to virtually anyone, and that individuals can interact with it in different ways, one can rightfully 
assume that future implications of such progress are innumerable, both from a scientific 
(Wolfram, 2005; Szalay & Gray, 2006) and social (Manyika et al, 2011; European Big Data 
Value Partnership, 2014) perspective.  
To offer an example, modelling of industrial processes and the generation of simulation data 
using software technology – as opposed to relying purely on expensive experimental 
information when preparing quantitative technological and economic forecasts – are fields that 
benefited from these technological advancements to a great extent (Matzopoulos, 2011). 
Considering the speed at which results can be generated or tested; the complexity of problems 
that can be solved or the level of investment and operation cost-reduction that such models 
potentially offer, the efficiency is far greater than can be achieved by means of doing 
experiments only. Thus being more conscious in involving mathematical models and 
computational tools in decision-making, while keeping the amount of experiments and pilot 
tests at the level of model validation (Fig. 1) has a positive effect on various aspects of industrial 
process planning. 
Besides their successful application in manufacturing and chemical process synthesis and 
design (Schneider Electric, 2015), models also have a well-established scientific background 
when it comes to simulating environmental processes (Bennett et al, 2013), natural resources 
and the role these play in renewable energy (renergy) production  (Donoso-Bravo et al, 2011). 
However, for a better understanding of their relevance in providing stakeholders with reliable 
information about ongoing and future trends in such areas, it is important to see how the field 
evolved throughout the years, ultimately to become a useful companion for those concerned by 
the productivity, as well as the sustainability aspects of energy systems.

1.1 The evolution of mathematical modelling: a renergy context 
1.1.1 Brief history 
Since the middle of the 20th century, when Marion King Hubbert introduced his theory about 
the peaking nature of the extraction dynamics of non-renewable fuels (Hubbert, 1956), 
forecasting and empirical models have been playing a significant role in natural resource 
estimation. Hubbert pointed out that fossil fuels previously deemed to be in great abundance 
were in fact reaching a point, after which their fraction available for exploitation started to 
decline, eventually to reach zero at some point in the future. To do that, he had collected a vast 
amount of historical production and statistical data for various fuels, examined the individual 
trends, compared them and then prognosticated what the short- and long-term production curves 
of these would look like. Although the contribution of Hubbert’s 1956 paper to how modern 
resource planning in energy management works is indisputable, nevertheless it did not consider 
the ecological implications of the use, as well as the depletion of the fossil fuels covered.  
During the Cold War, however, when the increasing levels of greenhouse gas emissions to the 
atmosphere began to raise international concerns about global warming, anthropogenic factors 
==PAGE:14==
3 
 
changing environmental and climatic conditions became a popular topic. Extensive research 
conducted by Callendar (1961) or early models like those reviewed by Charney et al (1979) 
successfully linked the changes experienced in usual seasonal and geographical weather 
patterns to the constantly increasing emissions originating from burning fossil fuels and other 
human activities. Despite the apparent differences in their approaches, they were all based on 
interdisciplinary data collected over past decades, and their forecasts convincingly converged 
upon a common conclusion: namely that average global temperatures are increasing alarmingly. 
With the spread of computer technology and the worldwide adoption of the Kyoto Protocol 
(UNFCCC, 1997), these formerly separate aspects of energy generation have finally become 
much simpler and more important to be studied in context than ever before. In effect, as opposed 
to merely serving the scientific needs of a purely academic society, models created since the 
1990’s have gradually evolved into being guiding and advisory tools for stakeholders working 
in or with the industry worldwide (Bozinis et al, 1996; Nakata, 2004; NREL, 2006; Gonçalves 
et al, 2010; Großmann, 2014), at all levels of the planning process (Heaps, 2012; Schneider 
Electric, 2012). So far this trend has been experienced in various fields of energy system 
management (Zeng et al, 2011), ranging from regulatory compliance to research and 
development, and more recently with regards to the integration of renergy in conventional 
energy production as well (Bayar, 2013; Morales et al, 2013; DNV GL, 2014). 
1.1.2 Modelling bioenergy systems  
Alternative fuels and their share from the total energy mix receive ever-growing interest 
nowadays, due mainly to their vital role in reducing global fossil fuel consumption and 
decreasing the extent to which humankind is polluting its natural environment (EEA, 2015). 
Among the most prominent of these fuels are the so-called biofuels, including all kinds of liquid 
and gaseous resources produced by the biochemical conversion of organic material. In contrast 
with other sources of renergy, like wind or solar which have gained significant popularity in 
electricity production and developed rapidly in recent years (FS-UNEP, 2015; Randall, 2015), 
the increase in the number of biofuel investments is not as clearly visible. Yet the fact that they 
offer a virtually constant supply of electricity, heat and transportation fuels, while the annual 
production of wind turbines and solar panels is limited to the amount of windy and sunny hours, 
makes biofuel-derived energy a highly relevant part of the future energy mix.  Furthermore, in 
a 2013 article Caspeta and his co-authors argued that despite common belief about the 
competition between biofuel and food production, there are numerous ways to exploit its 
==PAGE:15==
4 
 
benefits without exposing human or animal alimentation to risks, which is also very much in 
line with contemporary thinking. 
One example they mention is through the utilisation of second generation biofuels, which are 
highly capable of transforming the current state of the industry, given the technological and 
logistical background needed for this transformation is provided. In anaerobic digestion (AD), 
which is a branch of biofuel production and is centred mainly on the controlled degradation of 
organic wastes or slurries by anaerobes to generate biogas (Parkin & Owen, 1986; Mata-
Alvarez et al, 2000), this involves several aspects. Among others, ensuring appropriate biogas 
production control and stability measures, and monitoring key biochemical parameters are the 
more technical ones, but the management of substrates and the generated fuel or the economy 
of the process are also essential. Fortunately enough, all these areas of interest have been well 
documented over the course of the past few decades (Graef & Andrews, 1974; Boe et al, 2008 
and 2010; Ward et al, 2011; Madsen et al, 2011; van Foreest, 2012), offering a wide range of 
information about the promises and pitfalls in each. In her comprehensive study about remote 
monitoring and real-time controlling options in biogas production, Boe (2006) emphasised that 
many, if not all of these fields are interrelated. This means, in conjunction with other 
implications that proper digestion conditions can directly influence biogas production 
efficiency and the speed at which process imbalances can be tackled or measures can be taken 
against accidents, in either case with a significant effect on the costs of operation and 
maintenance.  
Given the apparent complexity of the biogas process, it thus became evident that there is a 
pressing need for generally applicable tools helping researchers and plant operators in their 
work with process development and control: in short, models of the complete AD dynamics. 
Since the early 1970’s, many of these were proposed around the world, with a clear tendency 
to improve from simpler to highly advanced ones over time (Lyberatos&Skiadas, 1999; 
Lauwers et al, 2013). Compared to the early years of modelling AD, when Andrews first alone 
(1969), and later with Graef (1971) introduced their concept about the production rate-limiting 
acetoclastic methanogenesis step and substrate inhibition, it is interesting to see where 
technology has evolved in a bit less than half a century.

1.2  Dynamic models in AD 
1.2.1 BioModel 
In 1993, after several simple or intermediate models describing certain aspects of the AD 
process by means of generalisations (only first-order kinetics) and simplifications (2-3 
microbial groups considered), the first complex model (the BioModel) able to give a good 
overview of most process variables, was introduced by Angelidaki and her colleagues (Tomei 
et al, 2009). Apart from accounting for enzymatic hydrolysis (glucose and protein) and four 
different microbial degradation steps, two inhibitory effects (VFA, acetic acid) and the pH and 
temperature effects on microbial growth, it limited the use of first-order equations to calculating 
the hydrolytic step, and was now applying Monod-type kinetics for a more accurate bacterial 
growth calculation (Angelidaki et al, 1993). A few years later the model was significantly 
updated, including a total of eight microbial groups (those being glucose, amino acid and lipid 
acidogens, LCFA, propionic acid, butyric acid and valeric acid acetogens, and acetoclastic 
methanogens), the decay of cell mass into consumable substrates, ammonia nitrogen as a 
growth-limiting co-substrate and two new types of inhibitors: namely ammonia affecting the 
growth of acetoclastic methanogens and the concentration of long-chain fatty acids influencing 
all microbial process steps (Angelidaki et al, 1999). Similar to the first published version, 
however, the conversion of hydrogen and glycerol were still not considered as separate elements 
of the model, due to their conversion dynamics being different from those defined in the model. 
Instead, they were accounted for by being combined with other sections of calculations, in order 
to keep the mass balance. Yet another important aspect of the BioModel is that its parameters 
were expressed in terms of gram component per litre substrate (g/L): the preferable way of 
characterising inhomogeneous substrates with high fibre and low water content, according to 
Lübken et al (2007) and Lindmark et al (2012). In addition to the fact that the model was 
specifically developed for cases when manure was co-digested with other substrates 
(Angelidaki et al, 1997; Pozuelo, 2001), it proved to be a suitable tool for simulating the 
anaerobic digestion of agricultural and many industrial wastes. In spite of its numerous 
advantages over its precursors, the BioModel was not developed markedly post its 1999 update, 
thus it could not reach a wider range of users and remained solely in the use of researchers. 
1.2.2 Anaerobic Digestion Model No. 1 
The year 2002, however, marked the introduction of the currently most widely applied 
Anaerobic Digestion Model No. 1 (ADM1), which was the result of an international 
==PAGE:17==
6 
 
cooperation between significant members of the relevant scientific community (Batstone et al, 
2002). Since then, it has been implemented in numerous studies concerning the simulation and 
control of anaerobic digestion at wastewater treatment plants (WWTP) (Bernard et al, 2005; 
Shang et al, 2005) and in several continuously stirred tank reactor (CSTR) setups (Kalfas et al, 
2006; Silva et al, 2009; Taruyanon & Tejasen, 2010; Rivera-Salvador et al, 2014). Some notable 
novelties of the new model were the introduction of competitive substrate inhibition, due to 
butyric and valeric acid both serving as feed for the same microorganisms, and turning 
inorganic nitrogen into a substrate-dependent limiting factor for microbial growth rate (Tomei 
et al, 2009). As opposed to the units of measurement in previous works, the ADM1 was first to 
use a kg-COD/m3 basis for most parameters, with the exception of inorganic carbon and 
nitrogen units (kmole-C/m3 and kmole-N/m3, respectively). The authors argued that this did not 
cause significant difficulties in conversion to more commonly applied units (like mg/L), and 
was in line with their attempts to consolidate the number of units used in various branches of 
the anaerobic digestion industry, in order to make ADM1 more accessible for non-scientific 
users (Batstone et al, 2002).  
1.3  State of the art in the mathematical modelling of AD processes 
Although the development of the ADM1 did not halt and it is constantly being improved by 
additions of new functionalities (the latest official version was made available in early 2015), 
it will probably not be the last model proposed for describing the anaerobic digestion process. 
One potential reason for this is the evident split between unresolved issues (Batstone et al, 2015; 
Flores-Alsina et al, 2015) calling for more detailed models, while the fact that complex tools 
seem to fall short of satisfying the needs of users without a sufficient scientific background 
implies the opposite. Already now, specialised and simplified tools exist that support the 
decision-making process of those planning to work with anaerobic digestion on a farm or small-
scale. According to Kythreotou et al (2014), in such cases, the main drivers for evaluating 
process feasibility are usually the estimated investment and operation costs, while the detailed 
knowledge of the biochemical process is not necessarily desired.  
It is nevertheless crucial not to treat the issue with Ockham’s razor, meaning that a model should 
not only be selected for use, because it is able to reduce an otherwise complex system to a 
simple form. In fact, the scientific approach at all times must be to find the easiest way for 
expressing the true relationship between elements of the anaerobic digestion process, without 
compromising the reliability of the model results. Gaining a better overview of the system, its 
interconnectedness and underlying mechanisms, and applying the newly acquired knowledge 
==PAGE:18==
7 
 
in the formulation of the model is one way to do it. This eventually leads to the so-called white-
box models (see Fig. 2): tools that make use of the full spectrum of available scientific 
information and generate satisfactorily accurate simulations, regardless of the substrate 
characteristics used at the start.  
Simply put, such models contain all the necessary aspects of a complex system to make 
forecasts based on historical data inputs, yet they also require large amounts of input parameters 
to be processed when implemented on a computer. This makes them harder to control and run 
longer or slower than generally desirable, depending on the build of the software and the 
capabilities of the machine hardware infrastructure, of course. 
 
Figure 2: The three types of mathematical models used to compare the complexity of 
dynamic systems (adapted from Kalmykov & Kalmykov, 2015 and Madsen et al, 2015)  
From a practical viewpoint, grey-box models that combine the use of theory with empirical 
parameters appear to be favoured over white-box models, especially in applied science (Brus, 
2005; Cameron et al, 2011). Similar to the BioModel and ADM1, they are based primarily on 
proven scientific concepts, but their algorithm can include estimated values or the results of 
historical measurements as well. As a consequence, a partial loss of model precision and 
reduction in the scope of applicability must be expected, however the upside is that handling 
the model becomes easier and the computational time decreases appreciably. Enabling biogas 
and wastewater plant operators, along with other personnel at various levels of the process 
==PAGE:19==
8 
 
management, to exploit the benefits offered by such tools is mutually beneficial. On one hand, 
their hands-on experience with the anaerobic digestion process provides a source of valuable 
feedback for further model optimisation, and in return, they receive an instrument that makes 
their work more efficient and less time consuming.  
1.4  Concluding remarks 
It can be seen that whether the amount of detail or an improved usability is the decisive factor, 
model selection does, and will essentially differ. So far, there is no ultimate choice ready to 
answer the questions of all parties involved in the development or use of AD models, and no 
significant change should be expected in these terms, until a radical turn in the definition of 
model dynamics makes their expression more transparent and computationally less demanding. 
With or without a Kuhnian paradigm shift (Kuhn, 2012), progress is kept constant nonetheless. 
Recent reviews about the technological development in WWTP management (Olsson et al, 
2014) and current trends in anaerobic co-digestion practices (Mata-Alvarez et al, 2014) suggest 
that the field is receiving more attention than seen previously and the diversity of AD simulation 
tools and applications in wastewater (WW) and sewage slurry (SS) treatment is getting broader 
by the year. For one branch of modelling AD systems, this is quite promising.  However, it is 
still unclear which model or models will describe an equally relevant field of biogas generation, 
notably the anaerobic treatment of solid or semi-solid wastes, with a satisfactory level of 
precision. The statistics collected by Mata-Alvarez et al (2014) indicate that the types of wastes 
biogas plants combine as substrates nowadays are many, with more and more animal manure, 
agro-industrial and municipal solid waste used. In Denmark, for example, due to historical 
reasons (Lafferty&Ruud, 2009; Møller et al, 2008) and an ambitious Green Growth agreement 
in place (Danish Parliament, 2009), the same trend in the AD of organic wastes is documented 
(Al Seadi, 2015). With further increase forecasted for the years to come, more innovative biogas 
plants expected to be built (IEA, 2015) and a potential shift of the role of biogas in the overall 
energy systems (Persson et al, 2015; Dong Energy, 2015), the spread of AD in Danish and other 
agricultural and industrial applications is accelerating. The question is, to what extent can 
mathematical models and software tools support this expansion, in order for biogas to become 
a key source of renergy.

Considering the aspects of AD modelling outlined in the previous paragraphs, the objectives of

2.2 Data and sample collection 
Samples and digitally available process information needed for experiments and process 
simulation were kindly supplied by the Lemvig plant. Samples of the raw slurry (a mixture of 
cattle, pig and mink manure) from farmers and mixed industrial waste streams (see Demazel, 
2008; Lemvig, 2015 for reference on composition) from companies working in partnership with 
the plant were taken once a week for a period of sixteen consecutive weeks and frozen to avoid 
decomposition. At the same time, reactor samples were prepared and preserved in a similar 
manner, covering the same period and originating from Lemvig’s four interconnected 
thermophilic reactors. Substrate samples were provided in singlets, in 250 ml containers (non-
acidified), while reactor samples were duplicates: a 10 ml sample, acidified with 1 ml of 34% 
phosphoric acid and a 250 ml, non-acidified sample from each week of sampling. The reason 
for acidification in this case was to examine the effect of a weakly acidic pH range on the 
volatile fatty acid and free ammonia nitrogen content of samples, in comparison to that of non-
acidified samples. 
Production data regarding pump feeding volumes, reactor temperatures and pH, retention times, 
gas production and other relevant operational information were retrieved from the plants’ 
automated logging system and sampling history, covering the period of sample collection and 
six months prior to that. Regarding the information gathered from data logs, the frequency of 
data points varied between two minutes (for information about gas composition) and thirty 
minutes (for pump feeding, reactor temperature and biogas production). Supplementary data, 
on the other hand, about the fraction of total and volatile solids in the substrates and reactor 
contents, the concentration of total volatile fatty acids and free ammonia in the reactors, reactor 
pH (measured manually) and finally the fraction of nutrients in the digestate were extracted 
from weekly historical sampling records. 
Following the collection period, samples were sent to DTU and kept frozen at -20 degrees 
Celsius until the start of their component analyses, the list of which can be seen in Table 2. All 
124 available reactor samples were analysed for their VFA and NH4-N content, while pH was 
only measured in the 250 ml samples, since the acidification of the 10 ml samples made it 
irrelevant. Data collected from these analyses was used as a reference: with the substrate sample 
characterisation process explained briefly in the introduction and providing inputs for the 
model, the outcome of simulations was compared to the analytical results from reactor samples.

Short-chain or volatile fatty acid (VFA) concentrations of the samples were analysed similar to

analyses and otherwise forming part of the VS calculation, were not taken into consideration 
during the process, due to their low significance.

Determination of the amount of nitrogen in the samples was according to APHA et al. (1999).

respectively. Quantification of the nitrogen content of the different sample extracts – 
temporarily bound in the form of ammonium borate – was carried out by back titration, using a 
0.1 M hydrochloric acid (HCl) solution. All samples were analysed in duplicates.  
Due to the fact that both TKN and inorganic ammonia concentrations were given on the basis 
of nitrogen mass units, and as it can later be seen, the BioModel interface also requires that 
form to be kept, no conversion to ammonia equivalents was necessary.

2.5 BioModel framework  
2.5.1 Model description and analysis 
Using the BioModel tool for the simulation of Lemvig’s anaerobic co-digestion process 
required the deeper understanding of the underlying model dynamics and interactions between 
its different subunits. Hence, in order to support its detailed description, the flowsheet of the 
model is presented in Fig. 3, based on the description by Angelidaki et al (1993, 1999). 
As seen in the visual representation, the flow of the BioModel AD process can be distributed 
into five functionally and chronologically separate phases. In the first phase titled “INPUTS”, 
complex substrates are described in terms of their main organic and inorganic constituents. 
Carbohydrates, proteins and lipids constitute the organic fraction, which later degrade into fatty 
acids, CO2 and other , while the inorganic fractions consists of NH4+-N, P, carbonate (CO2), 
hydrogen sulphide (H2S), anions (A-) and cations (Z+). This kind of description is essential for 
further calculations, given the production and conversion of intermediates are, to a great extent, 
influenced by the initial concentration of these components. By assuming that carbohydrates 
are present in the form of glucose, proteins in the form of gelatine and lipids in the form of 
glycerol trioleate (GTO), the model offers the possibility to use the results of common analytical 
measurements as input values, making quantification more straightforward. In addition, it 
accounts for the decay of dead bacterial cell mass as well, assuming that such biomass 
decomposes into carbohydrates and proteins and adding to the amount of these initial organic 
fractions. This step, however, is not shown in the flowsheet. 
The second or “HYDROLYSIS” phase of the process introduces the enzymatic degradation 
(yellow circles) of carbohydrates and proteins to either inert material, or soluble glucose and 
amino acids convertible to intermediates. The hydrolysis follows first order kinetics. In Fig. 3, 
Carb.in and Prot.in stand for inert glucose and gelatine, while Carb.s and AA represent soluble 
glucose and amino acids, respectively. Following the enzymatic hydrolysis, the 
“ACIDOGENESIS” phase is where soluble glucose, amino acids and GTO are consumed by 
acidogenic bacteria (green circles) and converted into VFAs and alcohols. As seen in the 
flowsheet, glucose is converted to acetic, propionic and butyric acid, while glycerol – the 
intermediate of GTO acidogenesis – is turned into propionic acid, under the assumption of 
instant degradation. Amino acids, on the other hand, can be converted to all three acids 
mentioned above, with the inclusion of valeric acid. As indicated in the flowsheet, butyric and 
valeric acid can be present both with their n-forms and isoforms. Alcohol formation is not 
==PAGE:30==
19 
 
shown in this visual representation. An important aspect of the extent of conversion in this 
phase is the converse effect it has on the hydrolysis step described previously. If the 
concentration of VFAs increases above a certain level, the self-regulatory mechanisms of the 
system inhibit the further hydrolysis of the initial organic compound, in an attempt to reinstate 
the process equilibrium. However, if the VFA production exceeds the buffer capacity of the 
overall system, acidification might take place, leading to process failure and the death of pH-
sensitive microorganisms, such as methanogens. This regulatory effect is included in the model. 
Moreover, LCFA inhibition is also included (in the form of oleate inhibition), affecting every 
process phase in the model. According to Ahring et al (1992), if the amount of oleate is kept 
below 0.1 g/L, or at least 0.5 g/L, GTO degradation can continue at a normal level and no 
inhibition should take place. These inhibition expressions included are non-competitive, with 
the exception of the inhibition on LCFA acetogenesis, where Haldane-type substrate inhibition 
is defined. 
Moving on, phase four explains the “ACETOGENESIS” step of the model AD process. Here, 
propionic acid, butyric acid, valeric acid and LCFA are converted by acetogens to acetic acid, 
while hydrogen (H2) and traces of CO2 (not shown in the flowsheet) are also generated. As 
opposed to the degradation of the VFAs, H2 is converted to methane (CH4) faster, hence this 
step was merged with the VFA degradation in the equations that describe the model. 
Nevertheless, Fig. 3 shows H2 formation as a distinct step in the process flow. Acetogenesis is 
affected by the total concentration of acetic acid, and similar to the hydrolysis step, is subject 
to inhibition when this concentration exceeds the system buffer capacity. Acetic acid inhibition 
is represented by a non-competitive expression. In the final phase of the AD process, the model 
considers “METHANOGENESIS” as a means of producing CH4 and CO2 from the acetic acid 
and H2 available. So far, it could be seen in the flowsheet that all bacterial groups – with the 
exception of amino acid degraders – utilise ionised ammonia for cell growth (green arrows), 
which applies to acetoclastic methanogens as well. For this reason, NH4+-N was introduced as 
a co-substrate in the model. Despite this similarity, nevertheless, methanogens are arguably the 
most sensitive bacterial group among the ones involved in the AD system described by the 
BioModel, and as such, free NH3 inhibition is a major threat for their existence. Through the 
affection of methanogens, an increase in free NH3 concentrations can trigger the accumulation 
of VFAs, which eventually inhibits the enzymatic hydrolysis and reduces the system pH. In an 
optimal situation, the cycle ends with the reduction of free NH3 (due to the decreased pH) and

thus the recovery of the system. Still, a change beyond buffer capacity can have terminal 
effects on digestion, which the model is able to predict. 
2.5.2 Model implementation  
After the detailed description of model dynamics, a few words must be given to the computer 
application of the BioModel, allowing insight into the simulation part of the project. Initially, 
the model was written in the Delphi (Pascal) programming language for application on 
Microsoft Windows systems, with the first available version released in 1998. Although 
accessible for only a limited audience, this program already had a point-and-click, visual 
interface and could handle up to eight digestors, connected in a user-defined order (Fig. 4). 
Setting up the software began with the specification of simulation parameters and the definition 
of the inoculum and substrate compositions, and continued with running the simulation. Both 
the substrate and simulation sheet of the model are shown in Fig. 5. Once the simulation was 
done, calculated results had to be exported in a delimited file format (Fig. 6, top left), which 
then was arranged in Microsoft Excel to different rows and columns (Fig. 6, bottom right). 
Depending on the complexity, one such round of simulation and the subsequent data edition 
usually took between 10-20 minutes. Besides the time-intensive simulation process, model 
handling also offered some difficulties regarding the inflexibility of organising data entries. In 
early 2014, nevertheless, the official support of the Microsoft Windows XP operation system 
ended, which was the last platform where the original BioModel could be run. For the sake of 
continuity and in an attempt to further upgrade the model, the software was translated to the 
language of MATLAB®, an engineering software package developed by The MathWorks, Inc 
(Fig. 7).  
By involving Microsoft Excel (Excel) in this transformation, parameter entry and manipulation 
became significantly easier, as data exchange between the two products is already well-defined 
(see Fig. 8 and The MathWorks, Inc., 2015 for reference). Finally, with the new structure, an 
enhanced and automated visualisation of the simulated results became possible, enabling the 
user to get an instant view of the simulation without having to edit the file or transform the data.

3.3 Model validation 
Before using the BioModel to simulate the Lemvig AD system, it was necessary to prove that 
the new implementation of the model is producing valid results. To do that, simulations 
previously run with the Delphi version were compared to those of the MATLAB-Excel version 
and based on the findings, interpretations were made on model reliability.   
3.3.1 Comparison of simulated co-digestions with pure substrates 
During the first case of validation, the following AD system was simulated in both the 1998 
and 2015 edition of the BioModel. Given the analytical characterisation of an inoculum and a 
manure substrate (data not disclosed), and assuming manure to be digested in a 2880 m3 
anaerobic reactor, fermentation was simulated for a period of 30 days, with a hydraulic retention 
time (HRT) of 15 days. This was also equivalent to feeding 8 m3 fresh manure to the reactor 
every hour of the day, while removing 8 m3 of effluent just after the feed. With a half-hourly 
logging frequency and taking the last 12 hours of the digestion as a reference, the basis for 
comparing relative CH4 productivity – that is, the amount of CH4 generated per unit of reactor 
volume per time, expressed in nL/L/day – was created. At the end of the 30th simulated day, the 
digestion was continued with the addition of 10 g/L of glucose, gelatine or GTO, in order to 
simulate carbohydrate, protein and lipid co-digestion processes, respectively. From this point, 
the three different co-digestion scenarios were simulated parallel for 108 days, totalling the 
number of days in 120. Declaring the reference CH4 production a theoretical 100% (as it was 
constant during the 12 reference hours), the relative increase of CH4 production from the three 
co-digestion scenarios was compared for simulations done with both models and is shown in 
Figure 10. The different curves represent the reference period of only manure digestion (yellow 
lines) and the co-digestion periods with pure glucose (green lines), gelatine (brown lines) or 
GTO (blue lines) added to the simulation. 
As it is seen in the graph and also emphasized by the numbers written between the curves in 
bold, the difference between the simulation results generated with the two alternate BioModel 
softwares is very small, with a clear tendency of the 2015 version forecasting more methane 
generation. However, as there was no experimental data involved in this experiment, it was 
only proof that the model dynamics in the newer software resemble those of the older version 
very well, making the new BioModel eligible for being considered for further analysis.

of the BioModel operate within the same tested framework as the 1998 one, but when used to 
forecast production for a co-digestion setup with practically determined input parameters, it

be tested, before the 2015 edition could be applied for the simulation of the Lemvig case.

what took place in the model. At the beginning, the same initial inoculum was chosen as in the 
case of the second validation, and an averaged mix of the substrate characteristics available 
from laboratory analyses for the period of 20.04.2015 to 03.08.2015 was made. This averaged 
feed was used as the only substrate, in order to run the first cycle of simulation from 20.10.2014 
to 19.08.2015. The choice of starting date was due to it being exactly six months before 
20.04.2015 or the first day with an experimentally evaluated sample composition, making for 
enough time to reach a stable state with the model before laboratory data are included. The end 
date, however, was chosen arbitrarily, but making sure that it was at least one HRT after the 
last day (03.08.2015) with available sample information. This initial simulation cycle then 
prepared the inoculum for the second cycle that was now only running from 20.10.2014 to 
31.03.2015, until the last day before the experimental period. Once the simulation was done, 
the digestate composition of the last time point was taken as inoculum and the following manure 
substrate composition schedule (Table 6) was defined for the different periods simulated 
between 20.04.2015 and 03-08-2015.

be introduced to the process as well, based on the industrial waste characterisation data also 
available from experimental analysis. Utilising half-hourly readings provided by the Lemvig 
plant, several distinct feeding periods were established, based on the average feed amounts in 
those periods. Co-digestion simulation was eventually run using experimental manure and 
waste characteristics and close-to-reality feeding patterns, whose results were at the last stage 
compared to the monitoring information gathered from Lemvig. The visual comparison 
between the simulated and monitored gasrate, along with the CH4 and CO2 fraction of the 
produced gas can be viewed in Fig 13. and 14.

4 CONCLUSION 
4.1 Achievements 
When evaluating the overall results of the project, account must be taken of both successes and 
failures. Present study managed, for example, to provide new parts for a growing database of 
substrate characterisation data. The part of the project to characterise various manure and 
industrial waste samples, in an attempt to generate more inputs for subsequent simulations, can 
also be listed among the successful aspects. Unfortunately, some experiments were not carried 
out as opposed to the original project plans, due to either a lack of time, substrate or process 
knowledge. Information thus not generated was substituted by estimations and assumptions, 
which is a usual part of data processing. Despite minor aspects missing, the experimental part 
of the project can be considered a success, and the BioModel implementation in the joint 
MATLAB-Excel platforms was also a significant part of the work done. Although the 
simulation of a full-scale biogas production process did not yield overall satisfactory results 
(due to an overly complex setting and presumable deficiencies in the gas calculations of the 
enhanced BioModel), a host of valuable information has been generated on model dynamics 
and issues to be solved, which can greatly contribute to the future development of the model.  
4.2 Future work 
Much has been achieved with the BioModel during project execution, however, many new 
aspect surfaced during the five months of the work. The inclusion of a parameter estimation 
module is an important improvement to be made, and so is the implementation of a so-called 
memory effect in the model. This covers the inclusion of a coefficient or set of coefficients that 
regulate the speed at which the system is reacting to inhibition or food depletion effects, and 
also the speed of recovery from such events. By the inclusion of such a factor, model response 
to smaller changes could be made less significant, and also the growth of microorganic 
communities could be better simulated. An equally relevant improvement for the success of the 
above mentioned is a delay factor for substrate mixing. In real-life scenarios, the addition of 
new substrate to the fermentation broth does not mean that this new material becomes readily 
available for the bacteria. A time factor could be developed, accounting for the lag between 
substrate addition and reaching a fully mixed state.  
Further differentiation of microorganisms should also be carried out, going from the currently 
available 8 microorganic groups to a more diverse community. This could be done by collecting 
==PAGE:50==
39 
 
genetic material information about the separate species present in analysed samples, and also 
the segmentation of the groups by means of changing their robustness. A system based on Gibbs 
Free Energy utilisation differences also could be an option. Special features regarding substrate 
depletion and new inhibition effects should also be considered. Comparing the model with other 
tools currently available, looking into how they are built up and consulting their developers 
might lead to an increase in the amount of related discussions the scientific community of AD 
can have. Apart from newly implemented features, the revision of existing issues, like those 
involving differential equation calculations or the incorrect definition of certain equations in 
the new BioModel implementation must be considered, too.

APPENDICES 
A.  Plant overview of Lemvig Biogasanlæg A.m.b.A 
The experimental part of present thesis was realized through a partnership between the 
Bioenergy Group of DTU and Lemvig Biogasanlæg A.m.b.A. (Lemvig plant). The biogas plant 
is situated in the northern part of the Jutland peninsula, Denmark, south of its eponymous city 
in the centre of Lemvig municipality (Lemvig, 2015). Since it began operation in 1992, the 
Lemvig plant has been collecting livestock manure from almost 80 farms, along with a variety 
of industrial side-products and wastes from their partners, eventually for these to be degraded 
by anaerobic bacteria under controlled thermophilic circumstances and converted into biogas. 
In all cases, this biogas is then burnt in gas engines – located either on-site or off-site – to 
produce electricity, while the waste heat generated during the process is used for reactor and 
district heating. Approximately 0.7% of the total amount, however, is deemed surplus biogas 
and needs to be torched every year, avoiding the overload of engines or exceeding storage 
capacity.  
Besides making their revenues by selling electricity to the grid and providing heating for over 
3000 consumers through their partnerships with Lemvig Varmeværk A.m.b.A. and Klinkby 
Kraftvarme A.m.b.A., the Lemvig plant has an important ecological and societal role as well. 
During the anaerobic conversion of the substrates mentioned above, they produce a natural 
fertilizer in the form of the reactor digestate, which contains a low amount of residual infectious 
organisms – owing mainly to the thermophilic treatment conditions (Smith et al, 2005) – and 
has a reduced odour, compared to the input substrates (Det Økologiske Råd, 2012). In 
comparison, the material is claimed to have a lesser concentration of xenobiotic compounds as 
well, like Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs) that usually originate from industrial 
processes involving fossil fuels, and can pose mild to severe health risks once spread into the 
freshwater reserves. It is due to the activity of the thermophilic anaerobes that the amount of 
such substances can be efficiently reduced (El-Hadj, 2006), while their bioconversion of 
organic nitrogen to biologically more available, inorganic nitrogen facilitates the nitrogen 
absorption of plants, upon applying the digestate as a fertilizer on farmlands (Møller, 2006).

while a more detailed plant structure can be found in Appendix B.
