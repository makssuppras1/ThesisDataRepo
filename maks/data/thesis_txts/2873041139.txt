Abstract
Six of the nine planetary boundaries have already been transgressed, and the global
population is projected to rise significantly toward 2050. Current trajectories pose a severe
threat to the stability of Earth’s systems. Excessive consumption in high-income countries
is a major driver of environmental degradation, while many nations continue to struggle
with meeting fundamental human needs. This raises a critical question: how can the
needs of billions be met without further compromising Earth’s systems?
This study presents a method to analyze human well-being in its multivariate form in re-
lation to various environmental footprints. Development trajectories from 2000 to 2020
across 52 indicators of well-being outcomes from the Social Progress Index (SPI) are
used to cluster 173 countries. Multivariate Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE) is
applied to include as many countries as possible and to explore the potential for extend-
ing the dataset with additional indicators of material well-being, typically unavailable in
global analyses due to data limitations. The resulting well-being clusters are compared
with consumption-based environmental footprints, including greenhouse gas emissions,
blue water use, biodiversity loss, and NOx emissions.
Nine well-being clusters are identified, spanning multiple world regions. The approach
effectively groups countries with similar levels and trajectories over time in indicators of
basic and societal human needs, revealing distinctions not captured by GDP or the Hu-
man Development Index. Significant within-cluster variation in environmental footprints is
observed, allowing identification of countries that exhibit relative environmental efficiency
in achieving well-being compared to countries of similar development. Additionally, sev-
eral clusters overlap in terms of GDP and environmental impact, suggesting that some
countries may already possess the means to substantially improve well-being outcomes.
Further investigation into the political and cultural characteristics of these clusters may
help identify pathways toward more sustainable development in well-being.

Introduction
In the context of escalating environmental degradation and social inequalities[1, 2], the
concept of planetary boundaries has gained prominence as a critical framework to guide
sustainable development. This concept describes critical thresholds in Earth’s biophys-
ical and biochemical systems, identified by Rockström et al. (2009) and further refined
by Steffen et al. (2015), within which humanity can sustainably develop [3, 4]. Crossing
these thresholds risks destabilizing Earth’s systems, triggering irreversible environmental
changes detrimental to human well-being. The framework includes nine planetary bound-
aries – climate change, biosphere integrity, land-system change, freshwater change, bio-
geochemical flows (nitrogen and phosphorus cycles), ocean acidification, atmospheric
aerosol loading, stratospheric ozone depletion, and novel entities – 6 of which we are
currently exceeding, highlighting the urgent need for transformative sustainability policies
[5]. The planetary boundaries provide the environmental ceiling within which sustainable
development must occur, informing Raworth’s (2012) framework of the safe and just op-
erating space, also referred to as the ”doughnut”. While planetary boundaries identify
critical environmental thresholds beyond which Earth’s system stability is threatened, the
safe and just operating space complements this by adding an inner social foundation,
reflecting essential human needs such as health, education, and equity [6]. This under-
scores one of the core challenges of our time: how to improve human well-being without
destabilizing Earth’s systems.
Traditional measures of development, such as Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and the
Human Development Index (HDI), have been widely used to assess and compare na-
tional progress. However, these indicators have been criticized for their limited scope,
particularly their insufficient consideration of environmental sustainability and the multidi-
mensional nature of well-being [7, 8, 9]. In response, several indicators have emerged
combining measures of well-being and sustainability in various ways [10]. Indices, such
as the Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI), have been developed to address well-being
in its multidimensional form, but the multivariate approach is often challenged by missing
data from developing countries [11]. This represents a gap in research as the as the low-
and middle-income countries are typically of high interest in studies of well-being [12].
The Social Progress Index (SPI) time series covers 52 indicators of various well-being
outcomes over time. All indicators in the SPI time series cover at least 95 % of geo-
graphic regions [13], this study aims to address the aforementioned gap by imputing the
remaining missing values.
To this end, Multivariate Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE) is employed to handle
missing data. This approach enables the inclusion of a broader set of countries in the
analysis while preserving the uncertainty associated with the imputed values. Additionally,
imputation facilitates the integration of supplementary indicators related to material living
conditions, which are often excluded from global studies due to data limitations. These
indicators enhance the alignment between the data and Decent Living Standards (DLS) –
a set of universal minimal material requirements that are essential for human flourishing
[14]. The benefits and potential drawbacks of this data-extension are assessed.
A key aim of the study is to develop a method to cluster countries based on shared patterns
of multivariate well-being over time, offering a new perspective from which the environ-
mental footprints of countries can be assessed. By applying the method, cross-regional

The well-being clusters are compared to four environmental indicators from the Global

Finally, the study identifies countries that demonstrate relative environmental efficiency
– those with lower per capita environmental impacts, given their level and trajectory of
development, while controlling for affluence. These countries may provide insight into
potential pathways for development that supports human flourishing without necessitating
increased GDP or environmental impacts.

Background
Human well-being is a multifaceted concept influenced by numerous factors that affect
the quality of life (QOL) within a country. Well-being depends on objective and subjective
factors; and the means required to achieve well-being can vary from one country to the
next [17]. In spite of this complexity certain needs are universal and fundamental to hu-
man well-being. These needs have been formulated in various ways, with probably the
most well known being Maslow’s pyramid of needs, which places needs in a hierarchy of
basic-, social- and moral needs, with the basic needs (also referred to as material needs)
being foundational for the other needs. While Maslow’s basic needs are indeed necessary
for survival it has been recognized that any fundamental need that is not satisfied results
in a form of poverty [17]. This recognition has led to the development of several theo-
ries complimenting each other through different approaches: Doyal and Gough’s theory
emphasizing the interdependencies between individual and societal needs; Max-Neef’s
theory outlining the linkage between fundamental needs: (Subsistence, Protection, Af-
fection, Understanding, Participation, Leisure, Creation, Identity and Freedom) and their
satisfiers, which can be both material and immaterial [17]; and finally the capabilities ap-
proach of Sen and Nussbaum, which in Nussbaum’s version outlines a set of capabilities
that are central for humans to be able to flourish and that any human being should be
entitled to (life; bodily health; bodily integrity; Senses, imagination and thought; emotions;
practical reason; affiliation; other species; play; and control over one’s environment) [18,
14]. Together these theories underline the importance of a multivariate approach to mea-
suring well-being.
While not all needs or capabilities are material-dependent, some are; subsistence for in-
stance requires consumption of food and materials for shelter [17]. This forms the basis
for the definition of Rao and Min’s Decent Living Standards (DLS), a set of minimal ma-
terial requirements that are instrumental for human flourishing [14]. Connecting poverty
eradication to natural resource consumption, the DLS provide a list of goods that are ”nec-
essary and indispensable, or globally desired” both on household- and societal scale to
facilitate well-being [14].

Vita et. al. links consumption to Max-Neef’s fundamental human needs in a subjective
and objective assessment. They find that the needs subsistence and protection have
the largest carbon footprints globally, and demonstrate that many needs, when measured
objectively, show thresholding trends which indicate that needs can be satisfied at certain
levels of consumption [19]. With offset in the DLS, studies have estimated the energy and
material consumption required to provide DLS globally [20, 21]. These studies show that
on a global scale we already posses the necessary resources to provide decent living for
all [22]. These findings highlight the social and environmental inefficiencies of the current

Studies of how well-being can be achieved at different levels of consumption play an

In international rankings and scientific studies countries are commonly compared interna-

development in well-being. Using 52 indicators of well-being over a 21-year period, coun-
tries are grouped according to their development trajectories. These clusters facilitate a
separation of wealth and well-being and can be applied in the context of any environmen-
tal footprint. Through analysis of the clusters, pathways may be identified toward resource
efficient development in well-being.

3.1
The Social Progress Index time series
The Social Progress Index (SPI) time series is a dataset built on 52 indicators of social
progress for 170+ countries covering the period from 1990 to 2020. These indicators re-
flect outcome-based measures rather than economic inputs, intentionally excluding direct
economic indicators such as GDP. The SPI organizes its indicators into three distinct di-
mensions of social progress: Basic Human Needs (BHN), Foundations of Well-Being
(FWB), and Opportunity (OPP), each designed to address specific guiding questions
[7].(1) Does a country provide for its people’s most essential needs? (2) Are the foun-
dations in place for individuals and communities to improve and sustain their well-being?
(3) Do all individuals have the opportunity to reach their full potential? The 52 indicators
are aggregated into a composite measure, the Social Progress Index, which summarizes
a country’s social progress as a single score. However, this study focuses on the indi-
vidual indicators themselves, rather than the aggregated index, in order to capture the
underlying patterns and variations in social progress over time.
As can be seen in Table 3.1 the SPI data contains two versions of the indicators: indicators
and scaled indicators. The scaled indicators are scaled to range between historical or
theoretical worst- and best-case scenarios. Scaled indicators are inverted if higher values
reflect lower social progress [13], the effect of this can be seen in Figure C.1 in Appendix C,
where almost all correlations are positive. To ease interpretation, the scaled indicators are
used in the study and referred to as indicators from here on. The indicators are presented
in Table 3.2 with a given reference number (Ref.) to ease visualizations. Note that Ref.
0-3 are descriptive variables used to identify observations.
The SPI time series includes some imputed values, which have been estimated using a
consistent and transparent methodology. Details of this imputation process can be found
in the section titled “Imputations Prior to Calculations”. in [36]. These imputations are
accepted as observations for this study. Not all missing values are imputed via their
methodology. The distribution of remaining Non Available data (NA) can be seen in Fig-
ure 3.1. The figure shows how the NAs are mostly concentrated in specific countries. For
some countries data is missing for specific indicators for a couple of years (or all years).
An overview of the countries with the most NAs is presented in Table 3.3. The countries in
with many missing values and fewer than 100,000 inhabitants in 20201 are excluded from
the study. Furthermore, to focus on the countries that can provide insight into DLS and
development, the countries from Table 3.3 that are consistently rated high (HDI>0.7) or
very high (HDI>0.8) in the human development index (HDI) from 1990-2020 are excluded
from the study. These countries are Brunei Darussalam, The Bahamas, and Grenada
[37].
Thus, the countries left are Micronesia (FSM), Tonga (TON), Kosovo (KSV), Belize (BLZ),
St. Lucia (LCA), St Vincent and the Grenadines (VCT), and South Sudan (SSD). For these
countries Figure 3.1 shows that the missing values continue throughout the observation

3.2
Basic human needs extension
While the SPI data does cover outcomes in several dimensions of well-being, aligning well
with the premise of the capability approach and DLS, it lacks the ability to measure the ma-
terial means required to reach some of those outcomes [14]. To mitigate this disconnect
an enrichment of the basic human needs dimension of the SPI data has been compiled2.
This extension aims to bridge the gap between well-being outcomes and qualitative mea-
sures of material inputs. While limited in scope relative to SPI, which also covers more
societal and political aspects of well-being, focusing on basic human needs is justified, as
these needs are the most material-intensive needs to satisfy on a global level [19].
In Table C.1 the extended data set is presented, where it can be seen how the measures
of outcomes from the SPI are being supplemented with material measures such as Nutri-
tional deficiencies with Protein Supply and Calorie Supply, or Share of Slums which adds
a measure of the quality of housing - an aspect missing in the SPI data. The extended
BHN data will not be the main focus of the study, but is included to illustrate how measures
of material means may influence studies of well-being.
The correlation matrix of the extended BHN data (Figure 3.4) shows that strong correla-
tions exist between most of the indicators. The reader should be aware that the added
indicators are not scaled and inverted like the SPI indicators, which explains the negative
correlations of Share of Slums and Intentional Homicide Victims.

Environmental and socio-economic data
The environmental analysis of this study is based on the EE-MRIO model, GLORIA [16,
15]. EE-MRIO models combine economic production data and international trade data,
they are usually structured between final demand, the end of the value chain where a com-
modity is finally consumed, and intermediate demands, nodes along the value before final
consumption. Last, they integrate the environmental impact of each step of production
along value chains (detailed per country and sector), informing about all the environmental
impacts embodied along value chains. Via the Leontief inverse, environmental footprints
can be calculated for any final demand [38, 39]. The accounting framework used to cal-
culate these environmental footprints is called Consumption Based Accounting (CBA),
which, from a given final demand, calculates all the environmental impacts caused by the
entire supply chain required to fulfill that demand. Through this method, all environmen-
tal damage related to a product is assigned to the country where that product is finally
consumed.

4
Methodology
To arrive at the results of study, several processes are needed as illustrated in Figure 4.1.
First, missing values are imputed; then several clusterings are generated based on a num-
ber of imputed sets; these are combined into one unified clustering; and finally a statistical
analysis is performed on the identified clusters. All of these steps can be executed in a
variety of ways, and this section aims to argue for and present the selected approach.
Figure 4.1: Overview of the methodology of the study. 35 imputed data sets are generated
in total.
4.1
Imputation of missing values
Missing data is a common problem in many fields of research. A simple solution to this
problem is Complete Case Analysis - also known as listwise deletion - where all obser-
vations containing missing values are deleted from the study [42]. This approach can be
wasteful, as both unobserved and observed values are lost in the process, and it may
cause biases if the data is not missing completely at random [43]. Applying Complete
Case Analysis in this study would reduce the number of countries included to 52 out of
173, which would be a drastic reduction in the scope of this work.
Alternatively Available Case Analysis can be employed, where the variables containing
missing values are deleted. Yet again, this requires completely random missing data [42].
A different approach is single imputation, where a missing value is filled in by, for example
the mean of the variable it belongs to. With this method, all observed data is retained
and can be used for inference, but the uncertainty of the missing value is ignored, which
may lead to wrong conclusions [42]. To retain as many countries as possible and retain
the uncertainty of the missing data as well, Multivariate Imputation by Chained Equations
Clustering Development Trajectories
17
==PAGE:26==
(MICE) is employed in this work1. This method generates multiple complete datasets –
i.e., without missing values – that can be used for further analysis while accounting for the
uncertainty of imputed values [44]. MICE, also known as ”fully conditional specification”
[44], operates by specifying a conditional imputation model for each variable with missing
data and iteratively drawing from and updating these conditional models until convergence
is achieved [45]. Figure 4.2 illustrates one iteration of the algorithm using a selection of
models. The initial step in mice is a random draw from observed values, rather than mean
imputation as depicted in the figure [45].
Figure 4.2: Illustration of the imputation process using MICE. The figure shows a single

4.1.1
Longitudinal data
As shown in Figure 4.2, MICE conditions imputations on values from other variables within
the same row, assuming independence between rows. In this study, countries are treated
as two-dimensional observations – indicators measured over time – representing a lon-

predictive power from the time dimension and can lead to inaccurate estimates of the
variance [47].
As living conditions within a country typically show limited year-to-year variation, surround-
ing years of a missing value are expected to provide substantial information. For longitu-
dinal data, it is recommended by Van Buuren (2018) to convert the dataset to wide format

longitudinal structures [45, Ch. 11]. As all data points in this study are time-aligned, the
dataset is converted to wide format before proceeding. Table 4.1 and Table 4.2 illustrate

4.1.2
Setting up the imputation model
While MICE yields statistically more valid imputations, it comes at the cost of higher com-
plexity [42]. The following section details the relevant modeling options and provides
justification for the selected approach, which is implemented using the mice-package.
Missingness mechanisms
When working with missing data there are three structures of missing data to be aware
of: Missing completely at random (MCAR), Missing at random (MAR), or Missing not at
random (MNAR) [45, Ch. 1]. MCAR is the case where the probability of a value being
missing is equal over the entire data set,
P(mis|Yobs, Ymis, ψ) = P(mis|ψ).
(4.1)
Where Yobs denotes the observed data, Ymis the missing data, and ψ are parameters inde-
pendent of the data describing the missingness. MAR is when the probability of being
missing is the same within groups of observed data. In other words the missingness
can be explained by the observed data
P(mis|Yobs, Ymis, ψ) = P(mis|Yobs, ψ).
(4.2)
The final structure, MNAR, is the most complicated structure because it deals with
the case where the probability of data being missing is dependent on unobserved
data, i.e. the expression
P(mis|Yobs, Ymis, ψ)
(4.3)
cannot be simplified. Van Buuren describes this with the analogy of a weight that produces
more errors when heavier objects are weighed [45, Ch. 1, 2, 6].
Given the presence of many highly correlated variables (Figure C.1 in Appendix C), the
availability of observations from other years for most cases, and a low overall fraction of
missing values (7%), the MAR assumption is considered acceptable. Consequently, it
is not necessary to expand the dataset or implement additional steps in the imputation
model to obtain valid imputations [45, Ch. 6].
The form of the imputation model
As previously mentioned, MICE fits a separate model for each incomplete indicator in the
dataset. In this case, all indicators to be imputed are numeric, furthermore the SPI indica-
tors have been normalized to a 0–100 range. Given the large number of indicators with
missing data and the relatively low level of missingness, predictive mean matching (PMM)

is used to impute all indicators. PMM is considered more robust to model misspecification
and is well suited for bounded variables, where standard distributional assumptions may
not hold [45, Ch. 3].
PMM works by fitting a model to the indicator with missing data,

to all the predictions of the observed countries. The five observed countries with closest
predictions are then selected as donors to the missing country. A random draw from the
observed values of the set of donors is inserted at the missing value [45, Ch. 3].

Number of imputed sets
To incorporate the uncertainty of missing values MICE produces several imputed sets, i.e.

In this study, the primary aim of the well-being data is to identify clusters that can be applied
to interpret patterns in other datasets. This analysis relies on point estimates rather than
the variance of the estimates, which would be more critical in statistical inference within
the well-being data itself.
Given the diminishing gains in statistical power from additional imputations [45, Ch. 2],
and the absence of planned inference or p-value calculations on the imputed data, m is
set to 35. This represents a compromise between preserving variation and maintaining
computational feasibility on a standard laptop.
4.1.3
Imputing completely missing variable-years
The extended data set contains data from multiple sources, and most of these sets have
more missing data than the SPI time series. Most of the variables are unproblematic to
impute with the presented method. However, for Share of slums another approach has to
be used since only every second year is observed (see Figure 3.5). Regarding imputations
Share of slums is therefore divided in two – observed years and missing years. Observed
years are mostly observed, but there may be some countries with missing values; missing
years are years where Share of slums is completely unobserved in all countries. Observed
years are imputed with the same methodology as described in the previous sections.
However, when there are no observations, as in the missing years, the wide format cannot
be used because it is impossible to fit a model to unobserved data. The missing variable-
years will therefore be left unimputed if inserted in mice.
To solve this problem a second imputation is performed using the already imputed sets.
First the sets are converted back to long format, then, for each of the m = 35 imputed
sets, Share of slums is imputed once. Several attempts to impute it using mice were
developed, but the variation, or rather the lack thereof, in Share of slums is not captured
well. Share of slums is observed to have incremental change happening over each two
year period. Since it is unlikely that large fractions of a country’s population moves in
and out of slum conditions from one year to the next, it is expected that the truth of the
missing values lie somewhere close to the mean of their surrounding observations. To
impute Share of slums we propose a linear model with interactions assigning specific
slopes to each country. Besides the interaction between Country and Year, an interaction
with Child stunting, which is the indicator that is most correlated with Share of slums, is
also included. Predictions with added noise are used as in PMM to find the five closest
observations and sample one of those as the imputed value. Since this method does not
include the uncertainty of the model parameters it is slightly inferior to PMM in terms of
preserving the uncertainty in the data. It can be argued, however, that this uncertainty is
to some extent included through the imputation of the observed years, see Figure 4.5. All

4.2
Clustering
Now 35 complete data sets have been constructed, how these 35 sets are condensed
into a final clustering is detailed in the following. The task of clustering is inherently un-
supervised in the sense that no definitive ground truth exists. As such, evaluation is not
a matter of objective correctness, but rather of whether the clustering is interpretable and
capable of providing meaningful insight. This section describes the selected approach
and provides the rationale behind its choice.
The clusters should divide the countries based on their development within all well-being
dimensions. A key element is that the clusters consider how countries have developed
over time as well as their level of development, so countries within the same cluster have
had similar living conditions throughout the time series. Ideally the clustering should be
able to distinguish countries with different development within the different dimensions of
well-being. A country with rising indicators within BHN but stagnating FWB, should be put
in a different cluster than a country where both dimensions are rising. The distance mea-
sure controls how the countries are compared, and the choice of measure is described in
Section 4.2.2.
Since cluster assignments may vary across imputed datasets, ensemble clustering meth-
ods are employed to determine the final clustering. Each of the m imputed datasets is
clustered independently, and the results are then aggregated using a consensus function
to produce a single, unified clustering [48].
4.2.1
Dimensionality reduction through Principal Component Analysis
For each of the imputed sets the dimensionality of the data can be reduced via Princi-
pal Component Analysis (PCA). This reduces noise and concentrates the information into
principal components (PCs) – new variables defined by the underlying correlation struc-
tures of the data. Through PCA similarities and differences stand out more clearly and

4.2.4
Clustering Validity Index
The clustering validity index (CVI) is used to determine how many clusters there should
be. A review by Todeschini et. al. (2024) [54] compares 68 different CVIs and this will
form the basis for our selection.
The field of cluster validity indices (CVIs) can be narrowed by focusing on pairwise distance-
based indices – those that require only a distance matrix as input. The C-index belongs
to this category and has been identified as one of the better-performing indices in this
class [54]. While other indices demonstrate higher precision, the C-index is less prone
to underestimating the number of clusters. In the referenced study, it more frequently
overestimates than underestimates the true number of clusters, but does so with greater
balance compared to higher-precision indices, which tend to consistently underestimate
[54].
For this study, a higher number of clusters is preferred over too few. Subsequent analysis
will reveal if any clusters are similar. In contrast, too few clusters risk obscuring distinct
patterns of well-being development, potentially diluting insights due to high within-cluster
variance.

squares model. By acknowledging that variance varies between countries, more robust
estimates and confidence intervals can be achieved. Denoting year as y and country as
c the model assumes the footprint can be described by the expression
log(footprint) = β0 + β1y + β2c + β3yc + ϵ
(4.12)

(4.13)
where ϵ ∼N(0, σ2/wc).

6
Discussion
This section evaluates the developed method by first discussing the chosen imputation
and clustering approach as well as the resulting clusters. This is followed by a brief remark
on the included data. The results of the environmental analysis are then examined in
relation to existing literature, and finally, the study’s limitations and suggestions for future
research are presented.
6.1
Imputations
MICE is employed to ensure data completeness for the clustering process while limiting
the number of discarded countries. The imputation model has been carefully designed to
generate realistic and and reliable imputations in all indicators. Years of tertiary school-
ing and Species protection are the two indicators with the highest proportion of missing
data in the SPI time series. Their imputations are shown in Figure 6.1 and Figure 6.2.
A key strength of MICE is its ability to preserve the uncertainty associated with missing
values. The degree of uncertainty varies depending on how strongly the indicator corre-
lates with other indicators; Species protection is not as strongly correlated to the rest of
the data as Years of tertiary schooling is. Therefore the variance of the imputations in
Species protection is greater. This highlights that single imputations may not reflect the
true values, however, generating multiple imputed datasets allows the model to account
for the inherent uncertainty. Nevertheless, anomalies may exist in the true values, such
as country-specific deviations from general correlations, which are unlikely to be captured
by the imputation model. Figure 6.2 shows that Years of tertiary schooling is generally
imputed in accordance with the observed values, but for Montenegro and Seychelles a
minor discontinuity can be observed where the imputations meet the observed values.
This discontinuity may be due to structures not included in the imputation model; or it may
be that the lighthouse approach, used to set up lag and lead structures in the predictor
matrix, is not the correct fit for these countries.
The lighthouse approach is the result of an exploratory process. Ideally, the entire time
series of a country would be used to impute the missing values [47], but that led to failed
convergence of several indicators. Several lag and lead ”patterns” (as in Figure 4.4) were
developed and assessed for convergence along with a visual inspection of the imputa-
tions. Although not theoretically optimal, of all patterns tested, the lighthouse approach
generated the best results. Better imputations could potentially have been achieved by
implementing specific models for each indicator. However that would be a very time con-
suming endeavor with the number of indicators in this study.

PCA and Euclidean Distance
PCA is applied to capitalize on the strong correlation structures shown in Figure C.1,
and thereby reduce the impacts of noise and redundancies in the data. By reducing the
number of dimensions, the underlying differences between countries become clearer. In
doing so, however, some information is lost depending on how many PCs are included.
The presented approaches establishes a boundary to ensure that at least 80% of the
original information is included in the clustering. This requires 8 PCs for the SPI based
clustering. The influence of the number of included PCs on the optimal number of clusters
is illustrated in Figure 6.4. It shows that the resulting clustering is sensitive to how many
PCs are included. It can also be seen that between 8 and 19 clusters, the C-index is gen-
erally lower. The optimal number of clusters predominantly varies in this range, with some
exceptions, depending on the number of PCs. Computing the mean of the C-index across
varying numbers of included PCs, as seen in Figure 6.5, demonstrates that the validity
of the different numbers of clusters has little variation and four local minima in this range
(at 9, 11, 14, and 17). Given the tendency of the C-index to overestimate the number of
clusters [54], a solution that indentifies 9 clusters is considered reasonable. Hence, the
boundary of 80% resulting in 8 PCs is also considered appropriate. To achieve 90 % re-
tention of information, 17 PCs are required1. Other approaches to retain more information
in the clustering process were attempted; these are briefly described in Appendix B.
After conducting the PCA, the dissimilarity between the countries is measured with the Eu-
clidean Distance. Figure 6.6 shows the sum of all the distance matrices2 used to compute
the clustering. This view of the clusters shows similarities and contrasts. For instance, it
can be seen that the pairwise distances between the countries in clusters C02 and C03
are small. However, C02 is more similar to C05, whereas C03 is more similar to C06. The
contrast between C05 and C06 is clear. A different distance measure could be selected
or designed to ensure even sharper contrasts between all clusters, bringing the discus-
sion back to the compromise between precision and interpretability. Advantages of the
Euclidean Distance is that it does not require arbitrary choices. It is easier to interpret
compared to several other distance measures, and it is compatible with Ward’s linkage
function which is used for the clustering in this study [50].
Hierarchical Clustering
The pairwise distances between countries inform the hierarchical clustering algorithm
which divides the countries into clusters and determines an appropriate number of clus-
ters.
The combination of distance metric, linkage function, and CVI has a significant impact on
the final outcome of the clustering. Each of these components offers a variety of method-
ological choices, leading to a wide range of possible combinations [50, 55, 54]. The se-
lected approach reflects the result of an exploratory process where the combination of the
Euclidean distance, Ward’s linkage, and the C-index was found to produce consistently
interesting results. Compared to other configurations, the optimal number of clusters was
more often in a range that balanced interpretability and precision. Other attempted con-
figurations of linkage functions and CVIs frequently tended toward either the minimum- or
maximum allowed number of clusters (the range was defined from 2 to 172).

Hierarchical clustering is useful when the number of clusters is unknown, however,the pro-

”greedy process” where with each step of the algorithm, the optimal choice is selected.
Due to the choice of Ward’s linkage function, the optimal choice is the combination of
two countries or clusters, that causes the smallest increase in within-cluster SSQ. Thus,
the algorithm follows a path of optimal steps, but it never re-evaluates its path [60]. The
CVI can identify the optimal clustering on the path. However, further optimization of the
9-cluster solution using an alternative non-hierarchical clustering algorithm may be able
to produce a more refined outcome of the 9 clusters.
Consensus function
To combine the clusterings identified for each of the imputed sets the Mirkin metric from the
field of ensemble clustering is applied [56]. As described in Section 4.2.5 it measures the
total number of different country-pairs between clusterings. The clustering with the lowest
overall Mirkin metric is selected as the final clustering. This approach is called ”best-of-K”
and it assumes that the clustering which minimizes the Mirkin metric is found among the
35 clusterings [56]. However, there is a chance that it might not be. For the SPI clustering,
9 of the clusterings are identical, 10 clusterings differ by 1 country; and 3 clusterings differ
by 2 countries from the final clustering. Thus, 22 out of 35 clusterings differ by two or
less countries, it is therefore considered unlikely that a better clustering could be found by
moving countries between clusters, as the most likely moves seem to have been tested.
It is not impossible that a better partitioning exists though. Several methods to identify
the optimal clustering are described by Vega-Pons and Ruiz-Shulcloper (2011) [56]. The
variation in the 22 similar clusterings is presented in Table 6.2.

6.2.3
Extending the data
The SPI time series has an objective outcome based approach to well-being [61]. The
questions are designed as the following ”How many years have the average citizen at-
tended school?” rather than ”How many schools are there per person?”. The approach
of the SPI tells a lot about the conditions in which individuals exist but it lacks percep-
tion of the individual ”Am I satisfied with the education?”. Including subjective indicators
in assessments of well-being is important because there may be a disconnect between
objective living conditions and perceived QOL [62]. On the other hand, answering the
question ”How many schools are there per person?” could provide insight to the DLS
[14]. Thus, the assessment of well-being through SPI is incomplete both in terms of the
material and the subjective dimension of well-being.
The extended BHN data combines the measures of social outcomes from SPI with more
measures of the material satisfiers to achieve well being. Data to measure all material
requirements of the DLS is not currently available for the time- and geographical scope
of this study. The variables that have been included exhibit higher amounts of missing
values and strong correlations with the SPI indicators.
Clustering the countries with the extra variables demonstrated that a stronger relationship
may be found between environmental impacts and well-being by including more indica-
tors of material well-being. However, for the countries with low well-being, for which the
extended BHN data was originally designed, the results indicate the opposite. This is
potentially caused by the missing values in the extended BHN data playing a larger role
when only considering BHN indicators.
The clustering process showed sensitivity to the input data in all cases. This suggests that
a different clustering of the countries might be as correct as the SPI clusters presented
in this study. Since the quality of these unsupervised clusterings depends on their ability
to provide insight into a specific analysis, the appropriateness of the clustering is consid-
ered depending on the point of interest. In this study the clustering process is tailored
to the SPI data, for which a robust clustering has been identified. Figure 6.7 shows the

6.3
Quality of environmental data and models
The GLORIA model supplying the environmental data to this study relies on a finite set of
sectors that aggregate the production of multiple products, and uses averaged values for
countries. It is a model, and to each entry in the model there is an uncertainty. In sectors or
regions where data is scarce, these uncertainties are higher, meaning that contributions
from small industries and low-income countries are often more uncertain. The data is,
however, consistently validated against other sources so that unrealistic estimates are
recognized and improved [16]. Lenzen et al. (2022) suggest to exclude results for the
countries Ethiopia, Zimbabwe, and Belarus (and Tanzania in the year 2013) as these do
not adhere well to other sources. These countries are included in this study, and therefore
we advise that their environmental results are considered with this limitation in mind. Since
these countries are not outliers in any of the environmental indicators, the inclusion of them
should not affect the overall results significantly. The validation of the GLORIA model also
provides an argument for including the outliers presented in Figure 3.7.
Generally the environmental data is described well by the regression models applied in
this study with only minor deviations from the underlying assumptions of normality. The
residuals of the country model for Biodiversity Loss show larger deviations from normality
implying a potential underestimation of the confidence intervals for some countries. Like-
wise, the cluster model for Biodiversity Loss may be affected by the outliers as the random
effects show some deviation from normality, which may cause a slight overestimation of
the expected values for cluster C07 and C08, potentially causing more countries to appear
efficient in this indicator.
Aside from the environmental data, the SPI data also has 4 environmental indicators:
Species protection, Deaths from outdoor air pollution, Particulate matter (2.5) pollution,
and Deaths from lead exposure. Furthermore there are indicators related to water con-
sumption: Improved sanitation, Improved water source, No access to a handwashing
facility, and Deaths from unsafe water, sanitation and hygiene. These indicators are re-
lated to some of the environmental footprints that the clusters are compared to. This may
by some be considered a limitation. However, excluding these indicators would be a sig-
nificant limitation to the measure of well-being [18]. In addition, the environmental data
measures the environmental impacts caused by consumption, whereas the indicators of
SPI measures the level of experienced environmental degradation. There is a clear dis-
tinction here, potentially highlighting the disproportionate effects of environmental change
on communities with low environmental footprints [1]. Including both perspectives can
therefore be justified, as they represent complementary dimensions of the problem.
6.4
Relation to literature
Understanding the relation between living conditions and environmental degradation is
an important task if the goal is to establish a society in which human well-being can be
maintained within the boundaries of the Earth. How we measure development ultimately
has an impact on policies that are implemented. The SPI clusters provide a new way
of comparing countries by their level and history of development. This section aims to
position the findings in relation to relevant literature.
Figure 5.3 shows that both BHN and FWB have generally had a rising trend from 2000
to 2020. Opportunity (OPP) however, which aggregates indicators of: equality, personal
rights, freedom and advanced education is not rising at the same rate. The mean trend in
56
Clustering Development Trajectories
==PAGE:65==
OPP for all clusters shows slow development during studied period as global stagnation
has been observed over the past decade for these indicators [61]. These aspects of well-
being seem not to be as affected by changes in consumption, see Figure 5.8, this is similar
to the conclusions found by Vita et al. (2019) [19]. While there may be small connection
between environmental footprint and OPP, it is likely governed by structural, cultural and
historical factors. The study of this dimension of well-being may benefit from a longer
time scope to be able to grasp significant structural or cultural change within countries
and clusters.
The clusters have been used to assess the connection between well-being, GDP and
environmental footprints. This relationship has been found to depend significantly on the
selected environmental indicator, highlighting that studies of decoupling should integrate
a wider range of environmental indicators to avoid unintended trade-offs. Goodwin et al.
(2024) connects consumption footprints from GLORIA, enriched by other sources, directly
to the planetary boundaries and assesses countries in a sectorial and regional framework
[31]. A similar assessment could be conducted for the clusters to provide a more complete
perspective on the environmental impacts of well-being.
As several of the clusters overlap in per capita footprints (Figure 5.14), but scores differ-
ently in well-being (Figure 5.3), it suggests that increases in well-being may be achievable
without increases in environmental footprint/cap. This assumes, however, that such a
transition is possible across cultures and societal structures. Investigating the differences
between, for instance low-footprint countries of C02 or C03 and high-footprint countries
of C01, might reveal the possibility or the inhibitors of such a transition. In contrast, in-
creases in GDP/cap is linked to increasing per capita footprints of GHG and NOx for most
clusters. This suggests that a needs oriented distribution of resources may lead to more
environmentally efficient increases in well-being than economic growth, a topic discussed
in more detail in other works [22, 23].
Large within-cluster variation is observed for all environmental footprints, and some of this
may be explained by the within-cluster differences observed in well-being. A deeper study
of the clusters on indicator level could provide insight into what may cause the variation
in environmental footprints. Furthermore, pairing this study with data of political history,
climatic factors, international conflicts etc. could provide valuable insight to the charac-
teristics of the clusters and what may cause some countries to be more environmentally
efficient than others in spite of having similar well-being development.
The proposed decoupling index can compliment assessments of well-being decoupling by
placing environmental impacts in relation with a more nuanced perspective on well-being.
This study presents a list (Table 5.3) of efficient countries with decoupling index < 0.75 in
all environmental footprints, which contains countries that may be unexpected in such a
list, for example Poland with its mostly fossil-based energy production [63]. This is likely
a result of using CBA to account for environmental footprints, as many of the highlighted
countries seem to have a lower household final consumption expenditure per capita com-
pared the other countries of their cluster [64]. After all, reductions in consumption is a very
effective way to reduce environmental impacts without unintended environmental trade-
offs [65]. The finding that the environmentally efficient countries from clusters of lower
development are achieving relatively high well-being compared to their clusters seems
contradictory given that basic needs, also referred to as material needs, are not fulfilled
in these countries. These needs are fundamental to well-being and are also the largest
contributors to the carbon footprint [17, 19]. It is, however, supported by the findings of
Aguilera et. al. that decoupling of well-being, GDP and GHG-emissions is more common

in lower-income countries [32].
The list of efficient countries and the decoupling index invites further research. While
the index may reveal more sustainable ways to generate well-being, it does not address
absolute environmental sustainability, which should be a main target in identifying devel-
opment pathways [5]. Aguilera et.al. argues that no country has achieved high human
well-being within the safe and just operating space by showing that the boundary has been
transgressed for climate change in all high well-being countries [32]. While they recog-
nize it as a limitation, their assessment is based on production-based estimates, which
do not reflect the emissions embodied in the consumption that facilitates well-being in the
countries. Based on estimates of the remaining GHG-budget from [66], an allowance of
0.4 t CO2e/cap·yr as of 2024 corresponds to a 50% probability of limiting global warming
to 1.5 ◦C by 2050 [32, 67]. Under this threshold, none of the countries in this study –
regardless of their level of well-being – have operated within the GHG-budget.
The clusters of this study and the decoupling index can in their current form assist in
reducing the impacts of development, but further work is needed to identify how they
might be used to address absolute sustainability.
6.5
Limitations
The environmental indicator used to measure BL may not represent the full picture. First,
it is based on land use and may overlook other contributors to loss of biodiversity; second,
the indicator used from the GLORIA model may not be the correct one to use in a CBA-
framework. The specific indicator used for this study is named ”land_threats” in the model
output, as opposed to ”land_threats_all”. It is assumed that this may be the distinction
between domestic and total (incl. spillover) impacts. However, we have not able to clarify
the difference from the relevant documentation [38, 15, 16]. Given that the error was
identified late in the process, time has not allowed further investigation or recalculation of
the results.
The decoupling index should be understood as a relative index that compares countries
of similar living conditions. In its current form the decoupling index says nothing about
whether acceptable living conditions have been achieved, nor anything about whether
efficient countries operate within planetary boundaries.
The time scope of this study is limited to 2000-2020 in order to include the variables of
the extended BHN data. However, considering that both the environmental data and the
SPI time series range from 1990-2020, further studies should aim to include the full range
of the SPI time series. Given more time, this aspect would have been addressed in the
present study.
To a limited extent, the clusters are based on simulated data rather than observed data.
The SPI time series contains missing values, some of which are imputed by default [13],
with the remaining gaps filled using MICE. Care has been taken to avoid unrealistic as-
sumptions about the missing values and to incorporate the associated uncertainty of these
into the clustering process. Although the impact of imputations on the final clustering is
assumed to be minimal, as discussed in Section 6.2.2, a complete dataset would enhance
cluster robustness. The influence of the default imputations of the SPI data has not been
evaluated in this study.
Due to the low amount of missing data in the SPI indicators the MAR assumption can be
justified for the SPI clusters. However the MAR assumption has not been quantitatively
evaluated for the extended BHN data, where a higher fraction of the data is missing. A
58
Clustering Development Trajectories
==PAGE:67==
sensitivity study of the imputations and the resulting clusterings could have been con-
ducted to have more certainty in the assumption.
In addition to HDI and GDP, the clusters could have been assessed in comparison with
a wider variety of well-being indicators. Comparing the clusters with more indicators, for
instance the Happiness Index, MPI or even the Social Progress Index score, would have
clarified the contribution of the clusters to field of human well-being.
Another limitation is that well-being is not exactly equal between the countries within a
cluster. This challenges the interpretation of the results, because the clusters may contain
countries of decreasing well-being among countries of increasing well-being, or countries
that excel in different aspects of well-being. While overall the clusters capture similari-
ties in trends and values, within-cluster differences at indicator level remain. However, a
similar critique could be directed towards the human development index or the practice
of comparing countries regionally. The clusters present a new perspective from which
countries can be compared and the results should be interpreted with awareness of the
limitations of this perspective.
6.6
Future work
This section summarizes suggestions for future work.
• The SPI clusters and the subsequent analysis, as presented in this study, can be di-
rectly expanded to the years 1990-2020 through the same method and with existing
data. This may improve insight related to long term development.
• A deeper investigation into the clusters and the within-cluster variation in environ-
mental footprints is needed. These differences may stem from factors such as prod-
uct preferences, inequality, or geography. Assessing the clusters in relation to so-
cial and historical data could offer explanations for the environmental efficiency that
some countries exhibit in achieving well-being.
• In addition, further analysis of the extended clustering may yield results that can
complement the present study.
• The scope of the environmental analysis could be extended to be compatible with
the planetary boundaries to certify that efficient countries are indeed performing
better across all impact categories.
• To measure DLS more data on material living conditions is needed. Furthermore,
the approach by which these indicators are included should be considered due to
high correlations among the indicators. Depending on the goal of the study, it might
be better not to include everything in one clustering.
• Based on the progress in the field that relates consumption to the satisfaction of
needs and environmental impacts [14, 19, 68] the idea of a Human Needs Extended
Multiregional Input-Output Model (HNE-MRIO) can be conceived. This could be
paired with the clusters to relate the amount of material well-being that is consumed
in a country to the amount of well being that is achieved, potentially revealing fulfill-
ment efficiencies or inefficiencies of products or policies.

7
Conclusion
The present study has developed a method to cluster 173 countries based on multivariate
trajectories of development in human well-being. Nine clusters are identified from 52
indicators of well-being from the Social Progress index (SPI) time series over a 21 year
period.
To maximize the scope of the study, Multivariate Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE)
is employed to impute missing values. To retain the uncertainty of the missing values
in the clusters, a consensus function is applied to combine the results of 35 different
imputed datasets. Thus, bridging the gap between ensemble methods and imputations
and demonstrating an approach to extract more information from global datasets with
missing values.
Through the developed approach, countries that exhibit similarities in well-being develop-
ment are identified and grouped in clusters, some of which span multiple regions of the
world. The approach is able to separate countries not only by differences in indicators
of basic human needs, but also by indicators of higher order needs, such as for instance
Freedom of discussion. In comparison with traditional socio-economic indicators, HDI
and GDP, several of the clusters overlap confirming the common criticism of these, by
showing that a multivariate approach to well-being can reveal details, that are likely to be
overlooked with the traditional measures.
Besides socio-economic indicators the clusters are also evaluated in the context of four
environmental footprints (Green house gasses, Biodiversity Loss, Blue Water Consump-
tion, and NOx) in a consumption based framework. Similarities in environmental footprints
and GDP are found between clusters, suggesting that some countries may have the po-
tential to substantially increase their well-being without necessitating increased per capita
GDP or environmental footprints. Within clusters, the environmental footprints can vary
significantly, indicating once again that similar well-being development can be achieved
with significantly different impacts on the environment per capita. This promotes the no-
tion of environmentally efficient countries - countries that are able to generate well-being
with a limited consumption of resources compared to their peers.
A multivariate decoupling index is proposed, through which Bangladesh, Burundi, Croatia,
Hungary, Latvia, Poland, Slovakia, Uruguay, and Ghana are identified as particularly effi-
cient in all tested environmental impacts given their GDP and level of well-being. A study
of the political and historical context of these countries, and the clusters in general, could
reveal important insight and guide policymakers toward a more sustainable approach to
achieving well-being.
This work underlines the importance of separating well-being from wealth, and demon-
strates how the complexity of today’s problems can be harnessed through existing meth-
ods. Further work should continue to grapple with the multivariate natures of well-being
and environmental degradation.

B
Additional comments
In the imputation of the data some additional variables are included, that are not used at
any other point in the study. These variables were at the time of imputation thought to be
useful, but as the project developed they were removed from the study, either due to the
quality of the variable or if the variable was unfit for the scope of the study. Although they
were not used further they were imputed among the other variables.
Universal health coverage from SPI been inserted in the BHN data to replace ”Essential
health coverage” as both of these measure the same unit, UHC, which is more well-
observed in the SPI time-series.
The weight-parameters of the WLS model used to model countries could have been es-
timated using Maximum likelihood, instead of using observed variance. This would be
expected to improve the quality of the models. Furthermore the assumption of indepen-
dence between observations is unlikely in a time series study. This assumption could
have been included by modelling correlations among observations, but
In developing the method for clustering, several approaches were attempted. Several
combinations of linkage function and CVIs were tested for hierarchical clustering, but
completely different clustering methods were also implemented. These are summarized
below.
• Multi-view clustering: Instead of reducing dimensionality with PCA, multi-view clus-
tering divides the data into many smaller ”views” containing fewer variables. Each of
the views are then clustered and the final clustering across all views is identified via
a consensus function [69]. We attempted an approach that used Round-Robin Fea-
ture set partitioning to separate the views [70]; a hierarchical clustering approach
to cluster each view; and then we applied the Mirkin metric twice to combine the
results of the views first and then the results of the imputed sets. This method was
not selected as it was found to be very sensitive to the selected number of views.
• CPCA clustering: An approach that mimics the process of a K-mean algorithm, but
PCA reconstruction errors are used instead of distances to the mean [71]. This was
implemented as well, but it was found too sensitive to the initial guess, to be applied
in this study.

predictions <- rnorm(n=nrow(newdata),

sample_closest <- function(a, B, k = 5) {
closest <- B[order(as.matrix(abs(B - a)))][1:k]
sample(closest, 1)
}
# Apply the function to each value in the predictions
slumImp <- sapply(predictions, sample_closest,
B =

Transformed variables and the visiting sequence (Variables not included in the
final study)
To properly impute the economic- and energy related indicators from the IEA dataset, indi-
cators not included anywhere in the clustering or analysis, we found that POP, GDPPPP,
TESJ and TFCJ should be log-transformed before imputation. Both versions of the vari-
ables are kept in the dataset during imputation, but only the transformed variables are
included in the predictor matrix. The original variables are imputed by a process called
”passive imputation” where they are transformed back after their log-transformed coun-
terparts have been imputed using pmm [45, Ch. 6]. Passive imputation is implemented
via the ”meth” argument.
Per default mice imputes the variables from left to right across the data set. To ensure
that the relationship between the original and transformed variables are kept, an extra visit
to the original variable is added in the end to passively impute them based the imputed
value of the transformed variable. The is specified using the ”visitSequence” argument to
mice[45, Ch. 6].
