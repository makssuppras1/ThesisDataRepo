Abstract 
Anaerobic Digestion (AD) is a biological process used to treat residual biomasses, which is 
gaining attention in the last decades since it produces a renewable energy source (CH4) and a 
fertilizer (digestate), reducing the need for fossil fuels and enhancing nutrients recovery. Today, 
the number of biogas plants is increasing around the word, and many different biomasses (e.g. 
sewage sludge, manure, organic industrial waste, wastewater, food waste etc.), are used as sole 
or co-digestion substrates. Some of these biomasses (i.e. protein-rich) can cause major 
operational problems to the biogas plants due to the ammonia toxicity, resulting in a significant 
reduction of the methane production. However, protein-rich biomasses need to be treated and 
have an high methane potential, and thus they could cover the increasing need for cost-effective 
digestion substrates. Bioaugmentation is an interesting option to overcome ammonia inhibition 
on biomethanation processes, since the addition of specialized methanogens microbial 
communities used to high levels of NH3 allows to establish a microbiological domino effect, 
with the creation of a functioning and equilibrated syntrophic process that leads higher methane 
production efficiencies.  
Good results have been obtained at a lab scale, but in order to understand the applicability of 
this technology at larger scales it is important to understand its environmental and economic 
costs. In order to do so, it is necessary to define the steps of the process, the material and energy 
flows involved and the reference period of 2018 and country (Denmark or Italy).  
The data on the process are the ones obtained in the outgoing research at DTU Environment 
under the project framework ForskEL (program no. 2015-12327) ‘‘MicrobStopNH3-Innovative 
bioaugmentation strategies to tackle ammonia inhibition in anaerobic digestion process. 
This works aims to perform an environmental and cost assessment of bioaugmentation 
strategy as compared with the plant running at “inhibited steady state”. The temperature 
condition is also considered, with the mesophilic case being consistently more positive than the 
thermophilic one, where ammonia inhibition has a stronger impact. 
The environmental focus in on climate change considering avoided emissions due to 
substituted energy production and the results show a better outcome for bioaugmentation, more 
notable in the Italian case. The economic analysis in done on operational costs and revenues and 
it results in greater incomes in the bioaugmented case. 
A sensitivity analysis is performed to see how the results change based on different biogas 
losses from the reactor, CHP efficiencies, tax conditions and using future forecasts.

1 Introduction 
Biomethanation is a process by which organic material is microbiologically converted under 
anaerobic conditions to biogas (Angelidaki et al. 2011). Anaerobic digestion is the 
implementation of this natural process in artificial systems; it is an important treatment option 
for organic waste streams characterized by the production of biogas and digestate. The first is a 
mixture of methane and carbon dioxide, which can be used to generate electricity, heat, or as a 
substitute for natural gas and transportation fuel, while the organic solid residue can be used as 
fertilizer due to its high nutrients content (Kougias and Angelidaki 2018). This process allows 
also to reduce the volume and mass of the treated organic waste and provides its stabilisation and 
hygienization before land application.  
Biofuels and waste currently contribute for 11% of the word total final consumption of 
energy and the rate of growth is impressive, with around 30% of the increase in renewables 
consumption expected to come from modern bioenergy in the forecast period of 2018-23 
(International Energy Agency (IEA) 2018b). In Europe, biomass currently accounts for 2/3 of 
renewable energy and will play a key role in achieving the target approved by the renewable 
energy directive of 20% of final energy consumption based on renewable sources by 2020 
(Hijazi et al. 2016; European Parliament and Council of the European Union 2009). In Denmark, 
it has been a political priority from the mid-1980s to increase the exploitation of biogas, 
especially from livestock manure; it was part of the new energy framework and a measure to 
address the issue of nitrate leaching from agricultural land (Raven and Gregersen 2007). The 
Danish Energy Agency (DEA) reports the presence of biogas plants spread throughout the 
country as shown in Figure 1, the majority of biogas plants are manure-based agricultural plants, 
with manure being set to be at least 75% of the feedstock material; biogas is promoted through 
public subsidies according to its use (Danish Energy Agency 2018). Most of the biogas produced 
is used to generate electricity in gas engines, or for the production of electricity and heat in 
combined heat and power plants (CHP).  
Anaerobic digestion is a sensible process and one of the main problems that some reactors 
face is the one of ammonia inhibition, which can slow down the process leaving a relevant 
portion of the substrate’s methane potential unexploited (Hansen, Angelidaki, and Ahring 1998; 
Fotidis, Karakashev, and Angelidaki 2014). This is especially true with plants that deal with 
substrate with high protein content, such as food waste, organic fraction of municipal solid waste, 
slaughterhouse waste, poultry manure, mink manure, etc. One of the possible solution to the 
==PAGE:10==

ammonia inhibition problem is to implement bioaugmentation with enriched ammonia-tolerant 
methanogenic cultures; this approach has been proved successful on a laboratory scale (Fotidis, 
Treu, and Angelidaki 2017). It is relevant to assess the environmental and economic costs of this 
procedure in order to compare it to other strategies and evaluate its feasibility.

1.1 Anaerobic Digestion  
Anaerobic degradation is a complex multistep process and is made possible by the combined 
action of three microorganisms groups: primary fermenting (hydrolytic–acidogenic) bacteria, 
anaerobic oxidizing (syntrophic–acetogenic) bacteria, and methanogenic Achaea. These are 
active at different steps of the organic matter degradation, have specific physiology and optimum 
conditions, and are connected by a number of microbial interrelationships and dependencies 
(Angelidaki et al. 2011). Figure 2 shows a schematic representation of the process steps, with the 
metabolic pathways and generated products. 
 
Figure 2: Key steps in the anaerobic degradation process (Angelidaki et al. 2011). 
 
1.1.1 Process steps 
Hydrolysis is the first step of the anaerobic digestion process, where complex polymeric 
organic compounds are broken down into soluble monomers through a wide range of 
depolymerisation and solubilisation processes (Angelidaki et al. 2011). The reactions are 
generally mediated by extracellular enzymes, called hydrolases, which are produced by a 
multitude of bacteria and are usually available in the substrate. Hydrolysis may be the rate-
==PAGE:12==
 10 
limiting step of the whole process for high concentrations of particulate organic matter like 
manure, sewage sludge and crop residues (Tong and McCarty 1991). 
In the second step of fermentation, the monomers formerly produced are reduced in the 
absence of inorganic electron acceptors (sulphate, nitrate or oxygen). The most abundant 
substrates for fermentation are monosaccharaides and amminoacids; the main products are 
volatile fatty acids (that give this phase also the name of acidogenesis), acetic acid, hydrogen and 
carbon dioxide. The active microorganisms are a very heterogenic group and they can follow 
different pathways depending on intermediates accumulation (Angelidaki and Batstone 2011). 
Acetogenesis is the third step of anaerobic degradation, when volatile fatty acids are 
converted to acetate and hydrogen. The microorganism responsible for the reaction are strictly 
anaerobic and the conversion process they foster has unfavourable energetics, so they rely on the 
hydrogen consuming methanogens to keep the products concentration low, establishing a 
syntrophic relationship called interspecies hydrogen transfer (Stams and Plugge 2009). 
The fourth and final step of anaerobic digestion is methanogenesis, with the production of 
methane from acetate and/or from carbon dioxide and hydrogen, formate, alcohols, and 
methylated C1 compounds (Thauer et al. 2008). Methanogenic microorganisms responsible for 
this transformations belong to the Archaea domain, they are strictly anaerobic and have an 
unique metabolism that involves a number of specific enzymes and coenzymes (Deppenmeier 
2002). They are very sensitive to the ambient conditions of pH and temperature, and tend to have 
slower growing rate; hence sometimes methanogenesis is considered the rate-limiting step of the 
entire process (Demirel and Scherer 2008). Methane formation can follow three metabolic 
pathways: aceticlastic methanogenesis develops the production of methane and carbon dioxide 
from acetate, in hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis carbon dioxide is reduced to methane, while 
methylotrophic methanogenesis converts methylated C1 compounds to methane. The main 
source of methane is acetate, which typically gives rise to 70% of the total production, while the 
remaining 30% is formed through the other pathways (Angelidaki et al. 2011; Conrad et al. 
2010). 
 
 
1.1.2 Process factors 
The performance of the anaerobic digestion process depends on several factors, such as 
nutrients availability, temperature, pH and inhibition. They can influence each other and effect 
the reactions in different ways and at different steps. 
==PAGE:13==
 
11 
Nutrients availability is important in order to supply the microorganisms with all the 
elements to build their cells, but since very little biomass is synthetized during anaerobic 
digestion, this is rarely an issue (Angelidaki and Batstone 2011). The nutrients presence is 
usually measured through the carbon on nitrogen ratio C/N, which can be properly balanced 
through co-digestion to avoid overloading or ammonia inhibition (Joan Mata-Alvarez et al. 
2011). Micronutrients are also necessary for the internal processes in very small concentrations; 
monovalent and bivalent cations (Calcium, Magnesium, Sodium, and Potassium) can stimulate 
the fermentation step, while heavy metals like Nickel and Copper are contributing to microbial 
growth (Angelidaki and Batstone 2011). 
Temperature is a fundamental condition parameter that influences both physicochemical 
parameters and microbiological processes. Increased temperature decreases viscosity and 
improves mass transfer, gas-liquid transfer, diffusivity and solubility; it also influences 
thermodynamic and equilibrium coefficients. Microorganism have optimal operating 
temperatures that delineate three generalized microbial operating regimes: thermophilic (40-
60 °C), mesophilic (25-40 °C) and psychrophilic (0-25 °C). The growth rate follows the 
behaviour shown in Figure 3, with exponential increase to the optimum and sequential rapid 
decrease (van Lier, Rebac, and Lettinga 1997). 
Biogas plants usually operate at either mesophilic or thermophilic conditions; higher 
temperature foster higher biogas production rates and shorter retention times, but the process has 
poor process stability and is more sensible to ammonia inhibition, while mesophilic reactors 
behaviour is well understood.

thermophilic methanogens (van Lier, Rebac, and Lettinga 1997)

The digestion process can also be affected by inhibition, the restriction of biological 
processes at different levels caused by the adverse effect of various substances. Some 
compounds like detergents, cyanide, antibiotics and xenobiotics are biocidal inhibitors that are 
responsible for irreversible reactive toxicity (Batstone et al. 2002).  
Biostatic inhibition is nonreactive and usually reversible and acts on the specific intracellular 
conditions required by microorganisms (redox potential, pH and total salts). pH inhibition is very 
important and is connected with ‘acid overload’ with a self-reinforcing pattern; the process can 
often continue at a slower rate due to the high buffering capacity of ammonia. This new 
condition is characterized by high volatile fatty acids (VFA) concentration and low methane 
production yield and the balance reached is referred to as “inhibited steady state” (Hansen, 
Angelidaki, and Ahring 1998; Angelidaki and Ahring 1994).  
Inhibition can be caused also by organic overload in systems fed readily degradable organics. 
Biostatic inhibition can originate from drops in the thermodynamic yield caused by accumulation 
of products; hydrogen inhibition is the most common of this type (Angelidaki and Batstone 
2011). 
 
1.1.3 Ammonia inhibition  
The presence of ammonia in anaerobic reactors originates from the feedstock but is also 
created during the degradation process of proteins, urea and other nitrogen-rich molecules 
(Yenigün and Demirel 2013).  
Unionised free ammonia nitrogen (FAN) is reported to be the most toxic form of total 
ammonia (NH3 + NH4
+) and the one responsible for inhibition of methanogens Archaea. The 
presence of free ammonia increases the maintenance energy requirements because it can rapidly 
cross cell membranes and consequently affect intracellular pH, deplete intercellular potassium 
and inhibit specific enzyme reactions (Kadam and Boone 1996; Yenigün and Demirel 2013). Its 
concentration depends on total ammonia, and increases with temperature and pH, that influence 
the dissociation equilibrium (Chen, Cheng, and Creamer 2008; Rajagopal, Massé, and Singh 
2013). 
Many solutions have been suggested to address ammonia inhibition. Dilution is reported as a 
simple way to reduce the concentration of Nitrogen in the feedstock, but causes reduction of the 
overall AD efficiency and increase of digestate volume and consequent treatment and 
transportations costs (Fuchs et al. 2018). Co-digestion with carbon-rich substrates is presented as 
a promising solution to increase the C/N ratio, but depends on the local availability of these 
materials that can’t always be ensured in the quantities necessary to allow continuous manure 
==PAGE:15==
 
13 
digestion. The use of food crops like maize and corn as anaerobic digestion substrate is also 
going to be limited in the future to avoid competition with food (Tilman et al. 2009). Moreover, 
anaerobic co-digestion is a sensitive process and C/N ratio is not the only parameter to keep in 
consideration (Joan Mata-Alvarez et al. 2011; J. Mata-Alvarez et al. 2014). Temperature and pH 
can also be adjusted to reduce the free ammonia concentration in the reactor: lower temperature 
allows better control of the process but reduces the productivity and increases the reactor 
retention time, pH adjustment can reduce FAN concentration but requires the use of chemical 
with additional costs and punctual control can be challenging (Hansen, Angelidaki, and Ahring 
1998; Fuchs et al. 2018). 
Another proposed option is the addition of inert material (clay, activated carbons, zeolite, 
biochart) to immobilize the microorganism, reducing inhibition and stabilizing the process 
(Cuetos et al. 2017; Mumme et al. 2014; Angelidaki, Petersen, and Ahring 1990). Ionic 
exchangers or adsorbants have also been added in order to remove sulphide that would otherwise 
act synergistically with ammonia (Hansen, Angelidaki, and Ahring 1998; Chen, Cheng, and 
Creamer 2008; Ardolino, Parrillo, and Arena 2018; Borja et al. 1993). In both cases, the material 
cost and possible change in reactor operation discourage these solutions. 
It has been observed how the microbial community is able to adapt to high FAN levels; 
however acclimatization can be a long process requiring up to two months and requires gradual 
increase and control on the process parameters that can’t always be obtained in a full scale 
reactor (Rajagopal, Massé, and Singh 2013). Bioaugmentation can be performed in order to 
obtain the positive results in a shorter time and with more control, with the addition of already 
acclimatized microorganisms that boost the activity of the microbial community in the digester. 
 
1.2 Bioaugmentation 
Bioaugmentation is an interesting option to overcome ammonia inhibition on 
biomethanation processes, since the addition of specialized methanogens microbial communities 
used to high levels of NH3 allows to establish a microbiological domino effect, with the creation 
of a functioning and equilibrated syntrophic process that leads higher methane production 
efficiencies. 
Bioaugmentation was first introduced in wastewater treatment as a process which attempts to 
improve treatment by increasing diversity and/or activity through direct introduction of specific 
microorganisms in a biological system (Stephenson and Stephenson 1992). Bioaugmentation has 
been considered an interesting solution to aid anaerobic digestion, as a mean to recover from 
organic overload, to improve hydrolysis of lignocellulose and lipids’ reaction rates, to decrease 
==PAGE:16==
 14 
the lag phase in the start-up phase of the process and to tackle ammonia inhibition (Costa et al. 
2012; Peng et al. 2014; Fotidis et al. 2014).  
One main concern with this technology is the risk of washout, when the bioaugmented 
organisms and desired properties aren’t able to establish themselves in the reactor in a short time, 
with the consequent unsustainable need of continuous supplement (H. B. Nielsen, Mladenovska, 
and Ahring 2007). There are also some claims on the inherent need of bioaugmentation, since it 
is known how the microbial community would eventually adapt to the new conditions through 
acclimation (Chen, Cheng, and Creamer 2008).  
Nevertheless the study of this recover opportunity for ammonia inhibition has been carried 
on to better understand how it effects anaerobic degradation and to develop the best acclimation 
and biological mixture (Tian et al. 2017, 2018), since promising results in terms of methane 
production increase were found (Fotidis et al. 2017). These results were obtained at a laboratory 
scale, and to understand the applicability of bioaugmentation in a real case some considerations 
in terms of environmental impact assessment as well as of economic feasibility should be done.

Aim and objectives 
This works aims to perform an environmental and cost assessment of bioaugmentation to 
alleviate ammonia inhibition on anaerobic digestion. The data on the process (feedstock 
characteristics, retention time, methane production) are the ones obtained in the outgoing 
research at DTU Environment under the project framework ForskEL ‘‘MicrobStopNH3-
Innovative bioaugmentation strategies to tackle ammonia inhibition in anaerobic digestion 
process”. Moreover, some important assumptions need to be made to model a possible up-
scaling of the process, using technical specifications and data from the literature. Some of these 
parameters can actually vary as various conditions are considered, making the final result only as 
reliable as the uncertainties associated to them. Hence, a sensitivity analysis was performed to 
identify the parameters with the most effect on the assessments results. 
This work considers a medium size reactor with a volume of 4000 m3, where the main 
feedstock is manure and the generated biogas is combusted in a combined heat and power unit 
(CHP). The produced electricity and heat are used to fulfil the internal needs and the surplus is 
sold to the grid and injected in the district heating network. The plant is thought to be located in 
Denmark, so all the considered prices and the substituted energy mixture are referred to this 
country, while Italian data are used to create a case for comparison. 
The analysis aims to compare the case of a plant working with a reactor at regular regime 
with no inhibition, in “inhibited steady state” and one where the bioaugmentation strategy is 
implemented; the three cases are developed at mesophilic and thermophilic conditions. 
 
1.3 Environmental performance 
In order to evaluate the environmental performance of the bioaugmentation strategy, its 
positive effect on biogas generation and consequent energy production is balanced with the risks 
connected with the enriched mixture creation and additional losses from the digester. A Life 
Cycle Assessment approach has been applied, with focus on the environmental category of 
climate change. This is well developed and one of the most commonly used since green house 
gases (GHG) are a matter of concern in the international community; anaerobic digestion is also 
particularly important from a global warming point of view, for its contribution to GHG 
emissions, savings from fossil fuel substitution and soil improving effects (Møller, Boldrin, and 
Christensen 2009).

1.4 Economical feasibility  
The cost assessment is done to evaluate the expenses associated with the bioaugmentation 
strategy compared to the additional revenues from the increased productivity of the plant. Since 
there is no need to change the plant’s main characteristics, it is assumed that capital investments 
and construction cost are the same. In addition, the environmental impact from construction and 
demolition of a biogas plant are comparably small, especially if the lifetime of the plant is longer 
(Mezzullo, McManus, and Hammond 2013; Hijazi et al. 2016). 
Hence, this analysis focuses on the operational costs and revenues and how they change at 
different methane production and with the additional request from the bioaugmentation step. 
The economical analysis focuses on the additional costs due to: 
- 
sterilization in order to account for the benefits of thermophilic conditions, 
- 
enriched consortia production (chemicals, electricity, personnel) for the bioaugmented 
case. 
The main revenues are the ones derived from the electricity inserted in the grid and possibly 
the heat transported due to district heating.

because manure is a very pure and active substrate.

comply to the sanitation requirements from the European Regulation on Animal By Products 
(European Parliament and Council of the European Union 2009). This treatment consist of 
keeping the waste at 70°C for one hour and has been chosen as the most commonly used, easily 
implemented and with the most data available (Grim et al. 2015). This step is not needed in the

anaerobic conditions and the microorganisms are producing biogas and stabilized organic matter. 
The process is performed in a mixed reactor with a retention time and consequent volume 
dependent con the working temperature. The digester’s conditions need to be closely monitored 
in terms of temperature, pH, VFA, ammonia concentration, mixing and methane production. Its 
operation requires electrical and thermal energy, with the consumption of a portion of the 
produced one. 
The produced biogas is sent to the successive CHP, where it is burned for the production of 
heat and power. This is not the only treatment option for biogas; in fact, the mixture can be 
upgraded into 95% pure methane and introduced in the natural gas grid to be used for other uses 
elsewhere. The combustion option is still the more common and widespread, and that is why is 
the one considered in this work. 
The energy comes from the methane and can be calculated through its lower calorific value, 
13.9 kWh/kg, ISO 6976 (International Standard Organization (ISO) 2016); this allows to keep 
into account the energy used for the evaporation of water and to maintain a conservative 
approach. Then the efficiencies proper to the specific combustion unit are applied to obtain the 
produced electrical and thermal energy. These are going to be considered as the main source of 
revenue, once the internal needs are fulfilled.  
The engine needs some air to provide the oxygen for the combustion process and will 
produce flue gas; this mixture must be treated by an air pollution control unit prior release into 
the atmosphere (Fan et al. 2018). This step’s construction cost and impact and operational costs 
are not addressed by this analysis, since on one hand the treatment unit would be designed for 
the higher biogas production and would simply work at lower rates in case of inhibition, with no 
difference between the scenario analysed, and on the other hand the operational costs of this step 
are low and can be neglected if compared with the other contribution. The atmospheric 
emissions from the combustion exhaust are considered as source of green house gases. 
The digestate produced in the anaerobic reactor can be used as fertilizer or soil amendant, 
but it usually undergoes some kind of treatment first. The digestate treatment can be performed 
relying on various processes and technologies, depending on the results that needs to be achieved, 
with higher costs the more concentrated the final product is, the longer distance it will have to 
travel. This step is kept outside the system bounderies because of the variability of treatment 
==PAGE:24==
 22 
options and considering that the amount would be the same with or without bioaugmentation, 
even if the quality will change, with a more stable product in the first case. This choice is on the 
caution side, since a possible positive impact for bioaugmentation is ignored. 
In the scenarios where bioaugmentation is performed, an additional process step is 
considered: the acclimation and cultivation of the enriched culture. The main inputs are 
inoculum, i.e. active biomass from the anaerobic reactor, and basal anaerobic medium. The 
bacteria consortia first undergoes stepwise acclimation in a small scale container and are 
subsequently cultivated in a dedicated fed-batch reactor according to the feeding curve described 
in Appendix A (Tian et al. 2017). Chemicals are needed as source of ammonia, carbon and 
nutrients. The enriched culture is continuously produced, in order to have a portion always ready 
to use in the main reactor when inhibition starts kicking in.  
1.6.2 Functional unit 
In order to perform a LCA analysis it’s fundamental to define a functional unit, which 
specifies the function of the product system being studied and is then used as reference for the 
collection of input and output data and following calculations (Hauschild and Barlaz 2010). In 
this study one ton of manure per day had been considered (after the optional pre-treatment stage). 
The reference countries are Denmark and Italy and the data are from 2018 or the most recent 
available. 
The choice is done from a waste management point of view, as the input material to be 
treated. This also eliminates the allocation problems related to the different products of a biogas 
plant that will arise if the functional unit would be a MJ of electrical energy out of the system. 
1.7 Environmental assessment 
The environmental assessment is a procedure whose goal is to understand the environmental 
impact associated to a project, looking at its emissions, waste production, land use, and so on. 
This work follows a Life Cycle Assessment approach, and the main category analysed is the one 
of Global Warming. The main contributions to the greenhouse effect produced by the process are 
evaluated and compared to the ones from the avoided electrical and thermal energy production.  
The anaerobic digestion process produces methane, which contributes to global warming if 
released in the environment, so one of the main impacts is biogas loss from the reactor. The 
uncontrolled methane emissions from losses are calculated as 1% of the total biogas produced, 
so they are higher for the cases that present higher production rates (Börjesson and Berglund 
2007). This parameter has an important effect in the overall Global Warming Potential as will be 
analysed further in the sensitivity analysis. 
==PAGE:25==
 
23 
The exhaust fumes from the CHP unit are another source of GHGs: some unburned methane 
is emitted as well as nitrogen oxides and carbon monoxide from the combustion processes. The 
combined Global Warming Potential is calculated using the emission data for a reference biogas 
plant found in EASETECH and reported in Table 1, Appendix A (M. Nielsen et al. 2010; 
SPIRIT - informacné systémy a.s. n.d.). 
Avoided emissions associated with the substituted electricity and thermal energy production 
are calculated referring to the most recent mean data in Denmark and Italy from 2016 
(International Energy Agency (IEA) 2018a). These savings are proportional to the biogas 
production and are the main contribution towards a less impacting process. This approach 
compares electricity and heat from anaerobic digestion with what is happening now in the 
reference countries. The positive effect of this renewable energy source are going to seem less ad 
less as the overall electricity and heat mix are increasing their low carbon sources, but this needs 
to be balanced with the other beneficial services supplied by biogas plants like organic waste 
treatment and energy safety. This aspect will be further analysed in the future scenario analysis.

1.8 Economical assessment 
For the economic part, it was performed the balance between the additional revenues from 
the higher biogas and consequent energy production in the bioaugmented cases and the expenses 
needed to acclimate and grow the enriched consortia. 
This analysis focuses on a fist evaluation of possible operating expenses and revenues, and 
doesn’t pretend to be a detailed and financially accurate depiction of a real case. It was assumed 
to apprise the working expenses and not the capital investments, because of the variety of plant 
configurations and the fact that plant construction has not significant contributions towards the 
environmental impacts, when compared to the use phase of a biogas plant (Mezzullo, McManus, 
and Hammond 2013). 
The results are reported in Euro, and when a unit price was gathered in Danish Krone the 
exchange rate  0.134009 €/DKK was used (European Central Bank 2018) to go back to the 
European currency. 
The revenues from electricity sold to the grid were calculating using electricity prices for 
non household consumers, with reference to Denmark and Italy in 2018, from the Eurostat 
database nrg_pc_205 (Eurostat 2018). The revenues from heath sold in the district heating 
system are calculated using the unit price of 0.041 €/kWh, as reported in Regulation and 
==PAGE:28==
 26 
planning of district heating in Denmark (Danish Energy Agency 2017). This value is referred to 
the thermal energy leaving the plant, so there was no need to consider the losses in the network. 
The evaluation of the expenses arising from the acclimation and cultivation of the enriched 
culture was performed through the prices of the chemicals used as carbon and ammonia source 
and to create the Basal Anaerobic Medium. It has been assumed that the inoculum initially used 
is collected from the plant itself, so it has no economic burden. 
In the mesophilic scenarios the additional step of pasteurization has been evaluated in order 
to take into account the benefit of thermophilic conditions of performing sanitation in the main 
reactor.  
Pasteurization consist of keeping the material at 70°C for at least one hour and it’s the 
standard method used for sanitation, that is required under EU law in order to reduce the amount 
of pathogens and prevent the spread of infections for category 3 animal by-products (European 
Commission 2011). The burden of the pasteurization step was evaluated as portion (8%) of the 
thermal energy produced in the plant used for pasteurization instead of being sold to the district 
heating network (Grim et al. 2015). 
The following paragraphs describe how the costs and revenues are calculated; they are then 
summed in order to obtain the final net income.

Results and discussion 
The implementation of bioaugmentation strategies to overcome ammonia inhibition on 
biomethanation processes is an interesting solution for anaerobic digestion. This work aims to 
perform an environmental and economic assessment. The results presented in this chapter are 
obtained according to the model assumptions and calculations previously described. First the 
main outcomes are presented and discussed. Then the effect of tax inclusion, percentage of 
losses and efficiencies are shown and the future scenario analysed. The complete numeric results 
are reported in Appendix B. 
1.10 Environmental performance 
The environmental assessment results in the category of climate change are expressed in 
terms of Global Warming Potential, measured in kg CO2 equivalents, which are associated to the 
treatment of one ton of wet waste entering the plant, the chosen functional unit. According to this 
indicator, the best option would be the one with the lower GHG emissions. The results can be 
negative values since the contributions of avoided electricity and heat production are subtracted 
from the ones associated with the anaerobic digestion process.

1.12 Sensitivity Analysis 
1.12.1 Biogas losses 
The effect of the percentage of biogas losses is analysed because of the uncertainty 
associated to it and its significant effect on the global warming indicator. The reference values of 
1 %  is chosen after looking at the literature (Börjesson and Berglund 2007), but is still a strong 
assumption since it is hard to have real life measurements and it can vary a lot depending on the 
plant. The sensitivity analysis looks at the effect of reducing or increasing the value by 50%. 
Air emission from anaerobic digestion are proven to be one of the main impact sources in 
the global warming category, making it a technical priority but also a needed point to take into 
account in policies and LCA considerations. The Danish EPA fixed 3 % as the upper limit for 
biogas losses for a plant to be authorized, so this value is also used in this sensitivity analysis as

Conclusions 
The present study conducted an environmental and economic assessment of bioaugmentation 
as a strategy to overcome ammonia inhibition on anaerobic digestion processes. The option to 
employ this solution to mesophilic and thermophilic reactor was analysed in terms of its effect 
on climate change and economic revenues, based on its positive results in increase methane 
production registered in laboratory experiments. 
Looking at the results it is possible to conclude that bioaugmentation is a feasible solution, 
especially at mesophilic temperatures, since the improvement in methane production is translated 
in less impact on climate change and increase in revenues. In fact, the outcomes are positive in 
terms of both environment and economic consideration.  
The comparison between mesophilic and thermophilic condition shows a clear preference 
towards the medium temperature from the single parameters analysed. Nevertheless, it is not 
possible to draw a definite conclusion since other positive aspects of higher rates and smaller 
volumes couldn’t be taken into account in this analysis. 
The reference to two European countries makes the results not universally applicable, but 
allows to understand the effect of substitute energy, environmental policies and prices on the 
final results. The application in Italy seems more convenient from the Global Warming indicator, 
but it needs to be balanced to the not certain presence of a district heating network and the 
specific focus on manure digestion. 
It is crucial to understand the effect of assumption on the results, especially in the 
environmental assessment. In fact, it is possible to witness a drastic increase of impacts with 
more biogas losses, while the effect of substituted electrical and thermal energy will change in 
the future.  
Finally, it is important to underline the significant effect of process definition and 
boundaries: substrate transport, digestate treatment and final destination are not considered, as 
well as the opinion of stakeholders.

Future perspectives 
This work focused on the environmental impacts and economic feasibility of 
bioaugmentation assuming that the methane production was the same obtained at lab scale. 
Hence, even if the results give a positive outcome, it would be important to proceed with other 
experiments to have more reliable data to base this kind of analysis on. The idea of developing a 
pilot scale experiment is actually discussed in the same framework project where the used data 
were obtained. 
A deeper understanding of the strategy’s environmental impact could be achieved if other 
impact categories are considered. Terrestrial and aquatic eutrophication is affected by the 
Nitrogen contained in the digestate and then released in the environment; the use of water and 
land is important when we look at different technical option within anaerobic digestion, that can 
effect the resources depletion indicator. 
The cost assessment performed in this project is limited to operational cost, while a more 
punctual analysis needs to take into account the needs of capital investments and the return rates 
in time. An actual feasibility would go more in depth and benefit from direct contact with 
construction, management and consulting companies. 
This work focuses on manure as substrate and, even if it is an important organic waste in 
terms of amounts and contamination potential, it is not the only one and the option of co-
digestion with food waste and microalgae could be looked at. In fact, promising results are 
obtain concerning the digestion of microalgae using the ammonia tolerant bioaugmentation 
strategy (Mahdy et al. 2017; Tian et al. 2018). 
In a life cycle prospective, it is additionally important to underline the effect of all the 
process steps, even the ones kept outside the project bounderies in this work, for example the 
impact of substrate transport and digestate treatment. The option to use the biogas in a different 
way, for example through biogas upgrading, would also affect the main parameters and final 
results.
