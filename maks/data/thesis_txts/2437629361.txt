Periodic Reactive Separation
Abstract
In recent years, periodic operation of various processes has regained industrial and academic interest.
By moving away from the classical steady operation, signicant improvements have been reported in
theoretical studies. With newly presented realizations of the periodic operation for distillation columns,
a few industrial columns are today operated in this manner.
Recently obtained patents show that the industry is now looking into the possibility of using
the periodic operation in another well known process intensication, reactive separation. Before this
combined process intensication can be introduced on an industrial scale, a range of tools will be
needed. This includes robust design-methods, rigorous models and tting control systems.
In this work, design of reactive periodic separation has been subject of a theoretical study. The
two state-of-the-art design methods, available in the litterature for this unit operation, by Patruµ et al.
[1] and Eriksen [2] respectively, have been investigated with respect to their applicability, features and
nally compared.
Through a range of case studies, including DME, MTBE and methyl acetate, the design algorithm
by Patruµ et al. [1] was found to greatly overestimate the number of reactive stages. This was found
to be due to a simplifying assumption of constant reaction rate on each tray throughout the reactive
section. On the other hand, the design algorithm by Eriksen [2] would result in a much more accurate
number of needed reactive stages, but was found to be limited to binary element (ternary component)
and single feed systems.
In this work, the design algorithm by Eriksen [2] has been expanded to include multi-element and
multi-feed systems. Furthermore, the algorithm has also been expanded to account for feeds that are
not at its bubble point. The design algorithm applicability has been veried through three case studies
of increasing complexity; reproduction of a design of a binary element, single feed case of MTBE, a
multi-element, single feed case of MTBE with a feed impurity, and nally a multi-element, multiple
feed case of methyl acetate.
As an addition to the design algorithm, it has furthermore been expanded to include a driving
force design procedure for binary element systems. This has been done using a procedure similar to
the one suggested for pure cyclic distillation by Nielsen et al. [3]. Through open-loop and closed loop
simulations for the production of MTBE, it has been found that column operated at maximal driving
force results in a minimized energy exchange within the column and a better robustness towards feed
ow changes compared to a reference design, as also previously reported for the driving force designs
in other types of equipments.

Periodic Reactive Separation
Resumé
I de seneste år har periodisk drift af en række processer genvundet industriel og akademisk interesse.
Ved at bevæge sig væk fra den klassiske stabile drift, er der i teoretiske studier blevet rapporteret om
signikante forbedringer. Med nyligt præsenterede realiseringer af denne periodiske drift i distillations-
kolonner, er der i dag nogle få kolonner i verdenen der benytter denne drift.
Nyligt opnåede patenter viser at industrien nu er begyndt at undersøge mulighederne for at benytte
den periodiske drift i en anden velkendt process-intensicerende enhedsoperation, reaktiv separation.
Før dette kan blive introduceret på en industriel skala, vil det være nødvendigt at have en række
værktøjer til rådighed. Dette inkluderer robuste design-metoder, præcise modeller og passende kontrol-
systemer.
I dette værk, er design af reaktiv periodisk separation blevet undersøgt igennem et teoretisk studie.
De to state-of-the-art design metoder der er tilgængelige i litteraturen, af Patruµ et al. [1] og Eriksen
[2] henholdsvis, er blevet undersøgt med hensyn til deres brugbarhed, funktioner og til sidst blevet
blevet sammenlignet.
Gennem en række case-studier, inkluderende DME, MTBE og methyl acetat, blev det fundet at
design algoritmen af Patruµ et al. [1] har en tendens til at overestimere antallet af reaktive bunde. Det
blev konkluderet at denne overestimering primært stammer fra antagelsen af en konstant reaktions
rate på hver bund i den reaktive sektion. Derimod ville design algoritmen af Eriksen [2] resultere i et
meget mere præcist antal af reaktive bunde, men ville kun være brugbar for design af binære element
systemer (ternære komponent) og enkelt-føde systemer.
I dette værk er design algoritmen af Eriksen [2] blevet udvidet til at inkludere multi-element og
multi-føde systemer. Ydermere er den også blevet udvidet til at tage højde for fødestrømme som ikke er
ved dets bobblepunkt. Brugbarheden af design algoritmen er blevet vericeret gennem tre case studier
af stigende kompleksitet; reproduktion af design for et binær-element, enkelt-føde system for produktion
af MTBE; et multi-element, enkelt-føde system for produktion af MTBE med tilstædeværelse af en
inert føde-urenhed, og til sidst et multi-element, multi-føde system for produktion af methyl acetate.
Som en ekstra tilføjelse til design algoritmen, er den ydermere blevet ekspanderet til at inkludere
en driving-force baseret design metode for binær-element systemer. Dette er blevet gjort ved at bruge
en metode lignende den som blev anvendt af Nielsen et al. [3] for cyklisk distillilation. Gennem åben-
løkke og lukket-løkket simuleringer er det blevet konkluderet at designet der drives ved maksimal
driving-force har en minimeret energi-udveksling i selve den reaktive distillations-kolonne, samt en
bedre robusthed overfor føde-ow forstyrrelser, hvilket er samme fordele som er blevet rapporteret for
driving force design i andre typer af udstyr.

Introduction
In recent decades, a strong focus has been on reducing utility usage, and especially in the industry,
the focus has been on moving towards more sustainable processes. In 2014, the industry accounted
for 36% of the total energy consumption in the world. Chemical and petrochemical industry itself has
in the last four decades gained share of the total energy consumption, from 6% to approximately 10%
in 2017 [4]. A lot of this energy still comes from non-renewable energy sources, which poses several
problems in the long run.
Despite the fact that the chemical and petrochemical industry has grown in share over the last
decades [4], a lot of proposals have been made to reduce both utility usage and capital cost; popularly
known as Process Intensications (PI). As dened by Stankiewicz and Moulijn, Process Intensication
is "any chemical engineering development that leads to a substantially smaller, cleaner, and more
energy ecient technology" [5]. It has been estimated that PI has the potential of increasing the energy
eciency in petro- and bulk chemical production by 20% within the next 30 years [6]. Furthermore,
some of these processes have also shown to be easier to operate, reducing ow-sheet complexity and
enhancing process safety.
Some of the most promising process intesication technologies that have been proposed in the last
decades includes micro-reactors, dividing wall columns, membrane distillation, reactive separation and
periodic separation [7]. Despite the great advantages of each of these technologies, the dissemination
of them varies greatly. This is, among other things, due to lack of knowledge about the new processes,
their reliability and the lack of good design tools [8].
Reactive separation, which has been called the front-runner of process intensication, was already
implemented on commercial scale in the mid 1980s, and showed great potential to reduce both capital
and operational costs, by integrating reaction and separation into one unit operation. The savings
showed to be up to 80% with respect to operational costs and at a capital cost only posing 20% of
the conventional process [9]. With more than 150 reactive distillation columns in operation worldwide
[10], reactive distillation is by far the most widespread reactive separation technique in the chemical
industry.
Over 800 patents and 1100 journal articles have been published on reactive distillation,
describing over 235 dierent reactions since the 1980s [11], resulting from both large academic and
industrial interest. Especially in the production of the bulk chemicals like ethers and esters, reactive
distillation has provided a much more convenient and ecient process in comparison to the conventional
processes. A selection of chemicals produced by reactive distillation today include methyl-t-butyl ether
(MTBE), methyl acetate (MeOAc), dimethyl ether (DME), tert-amyl methyl ether (TAME) and ethyl
t-butyl ether (ETBE).
In later years, periodic distillation, also known as cyclic distillation, has gathered both industrial
and re-emerging academic interest. Periodic distillation divides the conventional continuous operation

mixing of tray-liquids. By doing so, the distillation can be operated using a signicantly lower utility

This process intensication was already suggested in 1961 by Cannon [12]. Due to the lack of trays
that were capable of realizing this operation, the technology newer caught on industrially. In 2012
however, Maleta [13] presented a patent on a tray that could be used to realize the cyclic distillation
concept on industrial scale. As of today, only a few industrial applications of cyclic/periodic distillation
have been reported in the litterature, including the Lipnitsky Alcohol Plant in Ukraine, that processes
20,000 L/day of alcohol [8]. However, the periodic distillation shows great potential, as 40-55% of all
operational costs in the chemical industry originates from distillation operation [14, 15]. Furthermore,
over 40,000 distillation columns are in operation today [16], making this process intensication widely
applicable.
In the following sections, these two process intensication technologies are described in more detail,
providing the background, methodology, applicability and limitations for each process.

Reactive Separation
The primary task for reactive separation is to combine reaction with separation. One of the main
motivations for doing so, lies in the fact that high utilization of raw materials is crucial in the production
of bulk chemicals, with narrow prot margins.

Two main types of reactive distillation exist today; homogeneous and heterogeneous catalytic distil-
lation. Homogeneous reactive distillation requires one or more extra separation steps after the column,
to recover the catalytic material from the reactive column, whereas the heterogeneous reactive distil-
lation column can be used independently, as the catalytic material will be xed in the column.

He took the rst patent on, what today would be denoted a reactive distillation column, for the
production of esters. 60 years later, in 1983, the rst large industrial reactive distillation was taken
into operation by Eastman Chemical, for the production of methyl acetate [6]. This case has been the
text book example of reactive distillation for decades, due to its signicant improvement compared
to the conventional process. The methyl acetate reaction used in conventional commercial production

and reactants respectively. Due to the continous separation, the products will be removed in the top
and/or bottom of the column, thus driving the reaction equilibria further towards the desired products
[19].
It is however, depending on the reaction, also possible to operate only using a reactive rectifying or
stripping section, using either a complete reboiler or total reux respectively, which allows the reactant
to be the high or low boiling compound also.

1.2
Periodic Separation
Periodic distillation, has been known since a group of Cannon [12] studied various processes operated
cyclically in the 1950s, including liquid-liquid extraction, particle separation, adsorption and distilla-
tion [12]. Especially cyclic distillation, also known as periodic distillation, has caught academic and
industrial interest the later years.
Cyclic distillation divides the continuous vapour and liquid ows in a conventional distillation into
two ow periods; a vapour ow period and a liquid ow period. Except from this and another design
of the internal trays, a cyclic distillation column is constructed in the same manner as a conventional
continuous distillation column. Due to this similarity to the conventional column, the cyclic distilla-
tion is relatively easy to implement in current columns that are operated as conventional distillation
columns. The only things that needs to be changed are the column internals (i.e. trays) and the
controller system operating the feed, reboiler and condenser.

1.3
Periodic Reactive Separation
In recent years, a hybrid between the two processes, reactive separation and periodic separation, has
been explored, where the separation is distillation. This has been denoted catalytic/reactive cyclic
distillation. The hybrid is still a novel process equipment, that has only been briey touched upon
in the literature [1, 2]. The only change this hybrid is introducing, is the introduction of catalytic
material on each tray in a cyclic distillation column, which allows chemical reactions to take place.
Thus, from a construction point of view, this process equipment will be fairly easy to realize.
Initial theoretical studies have shown to reap the benets of each process intensication, utilizing the
benet of combining reaction with separation, and signicantly reducing utility usage/number of needed
stages by operating the separation periodically. Furthermore, periodic reactive separation may have
the potential of broadening the applicability of reactive separation to a selection of slower reactions,
as the residence time may be controlled easier with the cyclic operation, than with conventionally
operated reactive separations.
There are no reports on catalytic cyclic distillation columns in operation today, however patents
have been granted for catalytic cyclic distillation for Akzo Nobel Chemicals [28], indicating it might
be in operation within a foreseeable future.
With the increasing number of advantages of this hybrid, comes a greater complexity, which needs to
be accommodated in the design, modelling and generation of tting control systems, before this process-
equipment can be introduced on an industrial scale. Today, only two design methods are available in the
literature, which are fairly limited in their application range due to strongly simplifying assumptions.
Furthermore, the designs that are obtained from the methods are not optimized with respect to utility
consumption and controllability. Finally, they lack the exibility for designing columns with several
feeds which is commonly used industrially for conventional reactive distillation. All these limitations,
are very likely to aect the industrial desire to implement this new combined unit-operation. Thus,
these deciencies need to be addressed before the reactive cyclic distillation can be widely implemented
in industrial scale.

Problem statement
This masters thesis is a theoretical study of designing the hybrid unit operation, periodic reactive
separation. More specically, this study will be based entirely on distillation as the separation.
The objective of this project is to investigate the currently available design methods, investigate
their applicability, and investigate the possibility of expanding them to designing columns with multiple
feeds. Finally, it is sought to introduce a driving force based design method to the periodic reactive
separation, obtaining a optimal design with respect to utility usage and controllability.
This is done through case-studies of several industrially common reactive distillations, including
MTBE, DME and methyl-acetate. The results are furthermore compared to the ndings from simple
reactive distillation, to point out possible advantages and disadvantages by making the hybrid between
reactive and cyclic distillation.

Modelling of reaction
In order to form the theoretical basis for the literature review in the next chapter, the two common
reaction modelling approaches are now briey covered. Typically, when modelling a reaction, there are
two ways of calculating the extent of reaction; one that assumes chemical equilibrium and one that
assumes the reaction to follow a reaction rate expression.
In this chapter, the two methods are briey presented, including their advantages and disadvanta-
ges, as these two approaches results in signicantly dierent design algorithms, which will be covered
in the next chapter. As this thesis will only cover reactions occurring in the liquid phase, this will also
be the scope in this chapter.

To satisfy the equations, i.e to solve the equilibrium system, NC −NR intensive properties must

accommodate this, a number of transformations have been suggested to describe the simultaneous

The assumption of chemical equilibrium can however also give some valuable insights in the nature
of the reactive mixture, as it can be decoupled from factors like catalyst amounts.

When designing and modelling reactive distillation, it is crucial to nd out which depth of modelling
that will be required to obtain reliable results without having to increase modelling complexity more
than necessary. This has been discussed by Keller [20], where he suggests that chemical equilibrium
modelling might be the optimum solution in design of reactive distillation, based on the available design
algorithms in the literature and the costs of kinetic parameter estimation (see Figure 8).
Figure 8: Modelling depth versus costs for design of reactive distillation suggested by Keller [20].
He suggests to only use modelling like rate based modelling (non-equilibrium stage model) in
the rigourous simulation [20], after having obtained the rst preliminary design using the chemical
equilibrium assumption. Following the result from the simulation, one should then adjust the design
accordingly, if not fullling the separation and reaction specications.
The chemical equilibrium modelling also oers the advantage of detaching the needed catalyst
amount from the initial design algorithm, and thus also reducing the design complexity. This way, a
minimum number of reactive stages can be estimated and used as an initial design estimate and/or as
a screening method for process feasibility.
The conclusion by Keller, based on conventional reactive distillation, might however not be correct
for reactive cyclic distillation, as this hybrid unit operation might allow a range of slower reactions to
be carried out in a reactive distillation, where chemical equilibrium will be a too rough assumption.
Also, as will be seen in following literature review, reaction rates may have a signicant inuence on
the feasibility of the design, when reaction rates are low.
Due to these ndings, both modelling methods will be represented in the following design review.

3.1
Reactive distillation
As described by Malone and Doherty [32], the design phase of a reactive distillation column can
be divided into the following subtasks; (1) feasibility study, (2) conceptual design, (3) equipment
selection and hardware design and (4) operability and control. Especially the feasibility study phase
has attracted much attention in the literature, where many screening methods have been suggested,
with varying degrees of complexity and dierent assumptions. As summed up by Almeida-Rivera et al.
[33], only a small number of the design methods, that focus on the conceptual design (number of stages,
reboil ratio, reux ratio etc.) have been suggested in the literature. The few methods have, however,
been subject to a lot of development.
The most dominant design method for reactive distillation is the xed-point method, that estimates
the number of trays, reboil/reux ratio, location of feed stage etc. as one would typically associate
with column design methods. Apart from the xed-point method, there are other methods availible in
the litterature that adresses some of the same process parameters, including: conventional graphical
methods, mixed-integer non-linear programming (MINLP), orthogonal collocation on nite elements
(OCFE) and mixed-integer dynamic optimisation (MIDO) [33].
The last three are optimization design methods, typically optimizing column sizing with respect to
annualized costs, using a process superstructure model. Here, the algorithms have the opportunity of
searching a design space by varying several design parameters, including number of stages (stripping,
reactive and rectifying), feed location, reboiler duty etc. This results in considerable large computa-
tional times, as the optimization requires many iterations to obtain an optimized or sub-optimized
design.

To optimize the design, all three optimization methods requires an initial guess of the design. If this
guess is too far away from the optimal design, this may lead to convergence problems. To deal with this,
OCFE has been developed. OCFE transforms the discrete number of stages into a continuous variable.
Furthermore, the stage temperatures and compositions are also treated as a continuous variable as a
function of position in the column, which results in easier convergence [33].
Whereas MINLP can only be used for optimizing the steady state operation in the conventionally
operated reactive distillation column, MIDO can be used to optimize with respect to the dynamic
behaviour of the column as well, where the column controllability will have an inuence on the optimizer
objective, promoting column designs that will be easier to maintain in stable operation.

Reactive cyclic distillation
Due to the relatively new concept of reactive cyclic distillation, the availability of design methods
is very limited. Two design methods are currently available in the literature, one equilibrium based
approach and one rate based approach. These two are now reviewed individually. It is worth noting
that both methods are quite similar to the xed-point methods from the design of reactive distillation,
by designing from a specied bottoms composition and working up through the column.

3.5
Concluding remarks
From this chapter, it can be concluded, based on the design frameworks that have been suggested for
conventionally operated reactive distillation, that there are two distinct ways to approach the design
challenge of reactive cyclic distillation, a rate-based design and a equilibrium based design.
Both
approaches typically assume that all stages are reactive, and therefore requires iterative addition of
non-reactive stages above and below the reactive section to approach full conversion.
Seen in the light of this, the design method by Patruµ et al. [1] for reactive cyclic distillation, has
the interesting feature of providing a design with both the number of non-reactive stripping stages
and reactive stages, without any iterations. Only the number of non-reactive rectifying stages is to be
iterated upon, until the desired distillate purity is obtained.
The design method by Eriksen is more similar to the previously suggested design methods for CPE
based designs, by Pérez Cisneros et al. [35]. Here, the number of reactive stages is calculated directly,
whereas the number of non-reactive stripping stages and rectifying stages aren't addressed.

Design, simulation and dimensioning implementation
In order to investigate the rate based and the equilibrium based design algorithms, both design methods
are implemented in MATLAB R2018a, alongside simulation scripts and dimensioning algorithms. The
implementations of the algorithms are in this chapter described in further detail, as this forms the
basis for the ndings and design approaches used in this thesis. All code used in this project can be
found in Appendix E.
4.1
Backwards integration design
The backwards-integration method, used in both the rate based and equilibrium based design algo-
rithm, has been implemented in MATLAB and covers both the dierential mass balances, describing
the vapour ow period and algebraic equations describing the liquid ow period. The algorithm struc-
ture can be seen in Figure 25. The vapour ow and liquid ow mass balances are divided into two
functions VFP.m and LFPr.m respectively, where VFP.m integrates the VFP mass balances bac-
kwards in time, from t = tvap to t = 0 for N trays, using a variable-order Adams-Bashforth-Moulton
PECE solver, known as the ode113 solver in MATLAB. The reverse liquid ow mass balances, calcu-
lating compositions at the end of the vapour ow period (denoted (V)) from the compositions of the
tray below at the end of the liquid ow period (denoted (L)), are included in the function LFPr.m,
where the number of trays are expanded from N to N + 1.
In the work by Patruµ et al. [1], they only carry out the backwards-integration for the stripping
section, thus the system of equations will only expand with the mass balance equations for the stripping
section.
In the design algorithm used by Eriksen [2], he designs the complete column, including rectifying

DME
For the DME system, the feed stream is pure component A (methanol). The design target is here
to obtain an almost pure bottoms product of component C (water), where a mixture of non-reacted
component A (methanol) and the product component B (DME) is drawn from the column as the dis-
tillate product. Because DME is much more volatile than methanol, it will be easy to separate the two
after the reactive column, obtaining an almost pure product DME, and a pure methanol stream that
can be recycled to the reactive column. The most signicant eect of the introduction of separation
in this reaction, is the removal of water, that pulls the reaction towards the product. As the excess
methanol is drawn from the column in the distillate product, methanol will mainly be transported
upwards the column. To maximize methanol's exposure to catalytic material, the methanol feed is
therefore introduced at the bottom part of the reactive section. Note also that the column is designed
to be operated under a pressure of 10 bars to increase the column temperature, and thus also the rate
of reaction. The reaction rate at atmospheric pressure would simply require too much catalyst. For
this case, the Wilson equation is used to calculate liquid activity coecients, whereas an ideal vapour
phase is assumed.
MTBE
For the MTBE case, both components A and B (isobutene and methanol) are in the feed stream to the
column. It is here decided to operate with an excess of component A with the molar ratio of 0.7:0.3
(isobutene:methanol), as excess isobutene will be much easier to separate and recycle to the column
compared to excess methanol. The design target is here to obtain almost pure excess isobutene as
the distillate product, and a mixture of non-reacted methanol and the product MTBE as the bottoms
product. The major motivation behind combining reaction and separation here, is the utilization of
heat of reaction for enhancing separation, resulting in reduced utility consumption. Due to the relative
volatilities of the reactants it is common for conventionally operated reactive distillations with two feed
locations, introducing isobutene in the lower part of the reactive section and methanol in the upper
part of the reactive section. It is however chosen in this case to locate the feed of the two components at
the same stage, in the middle of the reactive section. Also for this case, the Wilson equation is used to
calculate liquid activity coecients and an ideal vapour phase is assumed. Note that MTBE forms an
azeotrope with methanol with a mole fraction of MTBE of approximately 65 mole-%. This azeotrope
will however not pose a problem when operating with a relatively high conversion of methanol.
Methyl acetate
In the methyl acetate case, the two reactant components A and B (methanol and acetic acid), are
introduced in stoichiometric proportions. The design target is here to obtain an almost pure distil-
late stream of methyl acetate with small residues of methanol. For the bottoms product, a stream of

acid in the upper part of the reactive section and introducing methanol at the lower part of the reactive

column design. For this case, NRTL is used to calculate liquid activity coecients, whereas an ideal

5.4
Concluding remarks
From these three major reactive distillation cases, DME, MTBE and methyl acetate, the applicability of
the design method by Patruµ et al. [1] has been evaluated. It has been seen that the estimated design for
the DME case does not dier signicantly from the actual design, whereas the design method predicts
too large numbers of reactive stages for both the MTBE and methyl acetate cases, in comparison
to what are actually needed. This overestimation origins primarily in two major assumptions in the
method by Patruµ et al. [1], judging from these three cases.
Firstly, the stage composition in the upper part of the stripping section, predicted by the backwards-
integration method, is assumed to be equal to the composition in the complete reactive section. From
the mass balance simulations for the three cases, it has been shown that this feature is only partly
present in the DME case, and not existing in the MTBE and MeOAc reactive cyclic distillation.
This leads to a non-constant reactive prole over the column, that contradicts with the assumption
of constant reaction rates on each stage that equals a mean reaction rate, that was obtained in the
backwards-integration design method by Patruµ et al.
Furthermore, the mean reaction rate, predicted by the backwards-integration is not necessarily
representative for the real reaction rates at the stages located around the feed stage, as the introduction
of fresh feed can change the composition signicantly. The eect of this was not as clear in the DME
case, as it was in the MTBE and methyl acetae case. In the DME case, pure methanol would be
introduced on a stage with only approximately 20 mole-% methanol, but the composition on this stage
would not change signicantly. This is due to the high reux ratio (RR > 40) used in the DME case,
that increases the molar holdup on each stage, reducing the impact of introducing am amount of feed,
that will be small in comparison. This eect was also demonstrated by reducing the boil up ratio to a
third, where the design method by Patruµ et al. would begin to overestimate the number of reactive
stages for the DME case as well.
The changes in compositions on the feed stage in both the methyl acetate case, and especially in the
MTBE case, are very signicant, which results in an underestimation of the reactant concentrations
on the reactive stages, and thus also an underestimation of a mean reaction rate on each stage. This
faulty estimation is evident in the large overestimations of the number of reactive stages. By using
this design method, a large part of the design method thereby becomes trial and error in mass balance
simulations, iterating many times on both number of reactive stages and rectifying stages, before a
feasible design is obtained.
From these case studies, it is evident that the design method by Patruµ et al. has some issues
with predicting the number of reactive stages when operating with lower reux ratios, but also with
reactive systems, where the composition in the reactive section varies signicantly. With this reux
ratio restriction, the design method results onlyin feasible designs when operating with large liquid
61
==PAGE:73==

holdups. As discussed by Okasinski and Doherty [44], some reactive distillation processes will also have
an maximum reux ratio, which could cause some further restrictions for the design method by Patruµ
et al. The design method does however predict the number of stripping stages with high accuracy for
all cases.
Based on these observations, it could be benecial to revise the design method for the reactive
section, where the assumption of constant composition throughout the reactive section is relaxed, and
thereby allowing for more precise predictions of reactive stage numbers. Furthermore, it would also be
benecial to be able to design the rectifying section, using the backwards-integration, instead of pure
trial and error, as this also contributes with a high number of mass balance simulation iterations, and
thereby takes a signicant time to obtain the design.
From the hydrodynamic calculations, used for dimensioning the columns, it can be concluded that
there will be a practical maximum of the vapour ow period duration tvap.
For long vapour ow
periods tvap, the vapour velocity will be very low, resulting in potential weeping. One can prevent this
by reducing the fraction of hole-area, α, but for very low values of α, mixing problems may occur.
Thus, depending on the chosen operating boil up ratio, one should be careful not choosing a vapour ow
period duration that is so long that α needs to be less than a given limit, say 1%, to prevent weeping.
Compared to the conventionally operated reactive column, where one can adjust the liquid holdup on
each stage and thereby easily adjust the residence time on each stage, this may actually indicate a
little less exibility in the periodic operation. Here, the only way to adjust residence time is to adjust
the duration of each cycle. One cannot change the liquid holdup for each stage separately, as all liquid
will be drained from each stage for each liquid ow period. This must however be examined further in
experimental studies before before solid conclusions can be drawn on this, as the hydrodynamics used
for dimensioning of the columns in this thesis, are strongly simplied.
As the reactive stages will, by far, be the most expensive stages in the column, it will be crucial
to obtain a correct number of reactive stages from a given design method. The accuracy of predicting
the number of reactive stages may be improved in the equilibrium based design method by Eriksen,
and it is therefore decided to investigate this method further in the next chapter.

Equilibrium based design
In this chapter, the applicability of the equilibrium based design method by Eriksen [2] is investigated.
The method is investigated for three cases, MTBE, MTBE with reactant impurity and methyl acetate.
Both the MTBE and the methyl acetate reactions are typically quite fast, but limited by their reaction
equilibria, thus the assumption of CPE will be tting in these two reactions.
To be able to design the cases with MTBE including reactant impurity and the case of methyl
acetate, the design method must be expanded to support multi-element and multi-feed systems. This
expansion is presented in the rst section of this chapter, alongside an model expansion to relax the
assumption of the feed to be saturated liquid at the introduction to the column.
The design algorithm is then used to reproduce the MTBE case, without reactant impurities,
originally shown by Eriksen [2].
Following this, the two new cases of MTBE with feed impurity
and the production of methyl acetate case are examined using the expanded design method. The
thermodynamic properties and equilibrium constant expressions used in these cases can be found in
Appendix A and Appendix B respectively. Unless otherwise stated, the column pressure is set to 1
atm.
All designs are conrmed by simulation, using the procedure presented in Chapter 4, where the
element and component composition proles are obtained. The designs and simulation results are
summarized in Section 6.5
Note that all designs obtained in this chapter assume that all stages are reactive (including reactive
reboiler), and furthermore that the element based heat of vaporization is constant and composition
independent.
6.1
Expansion of design method
In the following section, the design method is expanded to accommodate the design of more com-
plex reactive columns. First, the reasoning behind each part of the expansion is described, starting
with the multi-element expansion followed by multi-feed and nally the relaxation of the feed quality
assumption. The complete column model and design algorithm is hereafter summarized.

presented in Section 3.4.1, this is relatively easy, as the system of equations is simply expanded to

Driving force design
It is now sought to apply the driving force approach to the design of reactive cyclic distillation. The
driving force approach has so far been applied to a range of processes, including conventional non-
reactive distillation, reactive distillation and cyclic distillation. In all cases, the design operating at
the maximum driving force has the lowest sensitivity to feed disturbances, maximized sensitivity to
controlled variables and furthermore a minimized utility consumption within the column (reboiler and
condenser) [51, 52, 53].
First, a driving force based design methodology is suggested for the cyclic reactive distillation, and
subsequently applied on the case of MTBE. The performance of a non-optimized design and the driving
force design, for the case of MTBE, are then evaluated side by side, on a range of performance criteria,
including a sensitivity analysis of the column operation, controller performance and energy exchange
within the column.

before a disturbance in the feed will be picked up in the bottoms product temperature/composition.
The reason behind this, is the cyclic operation where the mass transfer down the column is solely
controlled by the liquid ow periods, where the tray liquid is transported one tray down per cycle.
Thus, for a feed tray location of NF, it will take NF −1 cycles before the disturbance will reach the
reboiler. On the other hand, the disturbance will be observed almost instantaneously in the distillate
product, as the mass transfer up through the column is only limited by the vapour ow. This can
result in the two control loops to act as disturbances to each other, which will make the controller less
eective. One way to reduce this time-lag, may be to move the control from the reboiler to a tray

7.3
Concluding remarks
In this chapter, the driving force design approach has successfully been adapted to the cyclic reactive
distillation. It has furthermore been shown that it is possible to obtain a driving force design, that
exhibits a range of the same advantages, as previously demonstrated for conventional non-reactive
distillation, conventional reactive distillation and cyclic distillation.
This includes both minimized
energy exchange within the column and enhanced robustness towards feed ow uctuations, shown in
both open-loop and closed-loop simulations.
For the binary element case of MTBE, the total reduction of energy exchange within the column
was shown to be reduced by 12.3%. With a discrete-time PI control structure, employed on the MTBE
case, controlling the bottoms and distillate temperature at the end of the vapour ow period, by
manipulating the vapour ow from the reboiler and the reux ratio respectively, it was observed that
the driving force design could reduce the total sum of absolute error (SAE) with approximately 50%
for feed ow uctuations of 1%. However, for feed composition uctuations of 1%, the driving force
design showed not to be the optimal design, as the conventional design would result in both lower SAE
and total variation of the manipulated variable (TV).
It has furthermore been observed that a control structure, using the bottoms composition/tempera-
ture as the controlled variable, may not be optimal, as the cyclic behaviour can result in a large time-lag
of up to several cycles (depending on the feed tray location) before an eventual feed disturbance will
be picked up in the bottoms measurement.

Summarized design algorithm
Based on the expansion of the CPE based design method in Chapter 6 and the ndings in Chapter 7,
a complete design method is now presented, resembling the algorithm for conventional reactive dis-
tillation. The design method presented in Chapter 6 is utilized for designing the reactive section of
the column, including the driving force design for binary element systems. In this suggested design
method, it is assumed that all stages are reactive (including reboiler).
The suggested design algorithm is divided into four tasks, and is resembling the design procedure
presented by Mansouri [52];
 Task 1: Problem denition
 Task 2: Transformation to elements
 Task 3: Identifying key elements
 Task 4: Reactive section design, using backwards-integration
Now, the complete design method is presented in detail, where the ve tasks are presented indivi-
dually.
8.1
Task 1: Problem denition
First, the design problem objective must be dened. The column pressure, number of feeds, reaction(s)
and present components are here specied alongside feed composition(s) and product purity objectives.
This may also include a certain conversion specication. Vapour-liquid equilibria data must here be
retrieved for each component, alongside equilibria constant(s) for the reaction(s).
8.2
Task 2: Transformation to elements
The NC components must now be transformed into NE elements. Following Gibbs phase rule, as
presented in Chapter 2 on page 12, the number of elements NE is given as the dierence between the
number of components and the number of reactions, i.e. NE = NC −NR.
When the elements have been identied, the formula matrix A is written, with the elements as
rows and components as columns.
Note any non-realizable elemental composition-spaces, if an element is only present in the reaction
scheme in conjunction with other elements. As an example, the element reaction BC+A −−⇀
↽−−AB+C,
requires that WB ≤WA + WC, as the element B is only present in conjunction with either A or C.

8.3

of elements is equal to two, these are identied by plotting the binary T-W l-W v CPE curve, where the
light key is identied as the element with the lowest element bubble point. For multi-element systems,
the rules by Jantharasuk et al. [41] are used:
Rule 1: Elements contained in the lightest component should not be assigned to be the heavy key
element, nor non-key heavy element.
Rule 2: Elements contained in the heaviest component should not be assigned to be the light key
element, nor non-key light element.
Rule 3: Key elements should be contained in both distillate and bottoms products.

Discussion
With the presented design algorithm in Chapter 8, it has been shown that it possible to obtain pre-
liminary designs for multi-element and multi-feed reactive cyclic columns. Also, for binary element
systems, it has been shown that it is possible to obtain a design that operates at the maximum driving
force, that will be more robust towards feed-ow uctuations, and will have a minimized reboiler and
condenser duty.
However, the proposed design algorithm has a range of limitations and simplifying assumptions.
A selection of the most dominant are now summarized in this chapter, and the eects of them are
discussed.
9.1
Assumption of only reactive stages
In the suggested design algorithm, one of the major limiting assumptions is the assumption that
all stages are reactive (including reboiler). Only with the presence of non-reactive stages, one can
approach complete conversion for the reactive column for many reactive systems. Here, little to none
separations are needed after the reactive column, which is one of the most signicant benets of
choosing the reactive distillation column over conventional reactor-distillation setups in the rst place.
As an example, the MTBE column, designed in Chapter 6 has a bottoms product stream with a
residue of methanol. To obtain full conversion of methanol, one will need to add non-reactive stages
to the bottoms part of the column, to strip the methanol from the MTBE. Furthermore, as to the
authors knowledge, it is not practical to load the reboiler with catalytic material. Thus, the design
algorithm presented in this thesis should not stand alone.
The design algorithm may however pose as an initial design with respect to the number of reactive
stages. One could then add non-reactive stages to the initial column design obtained in this algorithm.
By iteratively adding non-reactive stages above and below the reactive section, one could then obtain
a complete cyclic reactive distillation column. This procedure is resembling the one that has been
used for conventional reactive distillation previously [52]. Here, the addition of non-reactive stages
could look like the one presented in Figure 64 on the following page, which resembles the procedure of
Mansouri [52].
One could also consider the possibility of using a backwards-integration method for components,
resembling the procedure used by P truµ et al. [8] for cyclic distillation, to design the non-reactive
rectifying and stripping sections. One would then still use the chemical phase equilibrium assumption
for the reactive section, but enable one to design the complete column, including the non-reactive
sections, without having to do any iterations.
This could be done by specifying the desired product purities and conversion. Then by doing the
backwards-integration for non-reactive stages until a certain criteria is met, one could possibly obtain

9.2
Assumption of constant element vapour ow
Another major assumption made in the design of the reactive cyclic distillation, is the assumption of
constant element vapour ow. As has been stated earlier, this does not even correspond to a constant
molar overow assumption. Depending on the reactive system, this may lead to either over- or under-
estimated vapour ows. Thus, to get a more precise design from the design algorithm, this assumption
should be relaxed.
This could be done by linking the element vapour ow rate bV to energy balances instead. However,
when designing the column using the chemical phase equilibrium (CPE) assumption, it is not directly
97
==PAGE:109==
Periodic Reactive Separation
possible to calculate the reaction rate on each stage, which means that it is not possible to calculate
the heat of reaction of each stage. Thus, it will only be possible to include heat of vaporization in the
energy balances.
Therefore, even by including energy balances to calculating the element vapour ow, this may still
result in signicant deviations from the generated design and to the real design performance. This
will especially be dominant for reactions like the MTBE synthesis, where the heat of reaction is very
signicant.
On the other hand, the preferable degree for the heat of reaction, for reactive distillation, is mo-
derate. Thus, by neglecting the contribution of heat coming from the reaction, this may not be an
entirely bad assumption for these ideal reactive systems.
9.3
Other design alternatives
From the two major limitations presented above, it is obvious to consider other design-method alter-
natives that are capable of relaxing these assumption. Also, both the design algorithms by Patruµ
et al. [1], and the one suggested in this thesis, may require iterative addition of non-reactive stages.
This may lead one to consider optimization based design methods like mixed-integer linear program-
ming (MILP) and mixed-integer dynamic optimization (MIDO), that also rely on iterating on various
process-design variables. This method could however be able to optimize the column with respect to
costs and controllability for instance.
This may require a little more computational eorts, but can result in an economically optimized
design, which may not be the case for the two other design methods. As this method has already been
successfully applied in conventionally operated reactive distillation, it is an obvious possibility for the
cyclic reactive distillation as well.
The implementation of MILP/MIDO as a design method requires the process model to be set up
as a process model superstructure, and to be easily solvable for both pseudo steady state and dynamic
operation. The model used in this work, by Patruµ et al. [1], has in fact been implemented as a process
model superstructure, but currently it is still a computationally heavy task, that will result in the
MILP/MIDO optimizer to be unacceptably slow.
One must therefore look into revising this implementation for speed-enhancements, in case it will
be needed to apply any form for optimization based design method.
The design tool suggested in this thesis may however reduce this computational time, as it will be
able to provide a MILP/MIDO solver with a good initial guess, that will help the optimizer to converge
to a global optimum faster and/or easier.

Conclusion
In this work, the state of the art design algorithms for periodic reactive distillation have been reviewed
in detail, and their applicability have been tested through a range of case studies, including DME,
MTBE and methyl acetate.
Through the case studies, it has been found that the design algorithm by Patruµ et al. [1] have a
tendency of greatly overestimating the number of reactive stages. The reason for this was found to be
the simplifying assumption of constant reaction rate on each tray throughout the reactive section. The
design algorithm would therefore come up with unfeasible designs. The design algorithm by Eriksen
[2] was also investigated and showed to produce a more accurate number of needed reactive stages, but
the design algorithm was found to be limited to binary element (ternary component) and single feed
systems, which restricts the applicability greatly.
The design algorithm by Eriksen [2] has in this work been expanded to be applicable for multi-
element, multi-feed and systems where the feed is not necessarily saturated liquid. By relaxing these
assumptions, the design algorithm has been successfully applied on more advanced cases, including
design of a MTBE column with feed-impurities and the production of methyl acetate. The results
have been compared to the results obtained for conventionally operated reactive distillation columns,
and has shown that the periodic operation indeed enhances the operation, enabling one to operate
with a signicantly lower utility consumption or reducing the number of stages with more than 30%.
This design algorithm is applicable to any reactive system where the extent of reaction is limited by
its chemical equilibria.
A driving force based design algorithm has furthermore been suggested for binary element systems,
simultaneously designing a column that will have the minimal energy exchange within the column and
has a minimized sensitivity to feed ow changes, thus forming a reactive column that will be easier
to control. Through a case study of MTBE, it has been shown that the driving force design results
in easier control for feed ow uctuations, in comparison to a benchmark design. Here, a discrete
time PI-controller was used, and showed that the total sum of absolute error (SAE), for the design
operating at the maximum driving force, was approximately 50% lower than for the benchmark design.
Furthermore, it was shown that the energy exchange within the column could be reduced by 12.3% in
comparison to the benchmark design.
The suggested design algorithm does however not consider non-reactive stages. Thus, a subsequent
design algorithm, addressing the number of non-reactive stages, will be needed in addition to this
design algorithm, before one can design generic periodic reactive distillation columns, that will be able
to approach full reactant conversions.
Finally, by consulting hydrodynamics for dimensioning periodic reactive distillation columns, through
the range of case studies, it has been discovered that there may be a practical maximum of the vapour

studies, before before solid conclusions can be drawn on this.
