Abstract
This master’s thesis is titled ’Optimization of Damping in Flexible Rotor-
Bearing Systems via Controllable Fluid Film Bearings’. The aim is to increase
the damping of a given rotor-bearing system, using tilting pad journal bearings
(TPJB) with controllable hybrid lubrication.
This is carried out building
mathematical models of the ﬂexible rotor-bearing system, in order to predict
its lateral dynamics.
In this report, important theory behind the ﬂexible rotor-bearing system is
presented. Among 4 diﬀerent types of TPJBs, the best bearing conﬁguration
is identiﬁed under passive condition. This bearing conﬁguration is then used
for optimizing the use of controllable lubrication, in order to minimize the
vibrations of the rotor-bearing system. In the optimization of the controllable
lubrication, injection ﬂow and power loss are also taken into account.
It is concluded that the best bearing conﬁguration under passive condition is
the 4-pad TPJB with load between pads (LBP). When using this conﬁguration
it is found that the optimal injection setup is using only the two bottom pads
for controllable lubrication, while the two top pads remain conventional tilting
pads with no injection holes.
At the critical speed of approximately 2700 RPM, the lateral displacement
of the simulated industrial compressor is reduced by 62 % using an injection
pressure of 20 bar (0.2 l/min per pad).
At 15 000 RPM the vibrations are
reduced by over 30 % using 140 bar injection pressure (16.7 l/min per pad).
This gives an increase in power of 46.7 % when compared to a conventional
TPJB. The vibrations at high rotational speeds can be further reduced using
a higher injection pressure.
However, this leads to an unrealistically high
power consumption. By combining the 20 bar at 2700 RPM and the 140 bar
at higher speeds, the amplitude limit of the API standard is respected in the
entire operational range, fulﬁlling the objective of the use of the controllable
lubrication i.e. maximum amplitude smaller than 22 µm.
It is also found that the controllable lubrication can signiﬁcantly alter the mode
shapes and damping ratios of the industrial compressor, hereby improving the
lateral dynamics of the rotor-bearing system.

Resumé
Dette master projekt har den danske titel ’Optimering af Dæmpning i Fleksi-
ble Rotor-Leje-Systemer via Regulerbare Hydrodynamiske Lejer’. Formålet er
at øge dæmpningen for et givent rotor-leje system ved at benytte vippeskolejer
med kontrollerbar hybrid smøring. Dette er opnået ved at opstille matema-
tiske modeller af det ﬂeksible rotor-leje system, for at forudse dets laterale
dynamik.
I denne rapport er vigtig teori bag det ﬂeksible rotor-leje system præsenteret.
Iblandt 4 forskellige typer vippeskolejer identiﬁceres den bedste lejekonﬁgu-
ration under passive betingelser. Denne leje konﬁguration bruges videre til
at optimimere brugen af kontrollerbar smøring, for at minimere vibrationerne
af rotor-leje systemet. I optimeringen af den aktive smøring tages også ind-
sprøjningsﬂowet og eﬀekttabet med i betragtningen.
Det konkluderes at den bedste lejekonﬁguration under passive betingelser er
vippeskolejet med 4 sko og belastning mellem skoene. Ved at benytte denne
lejekonﬁguration er det optimale indsprøjtningssetup identiﬁceret som brug
af aktiv smøring i de to nederste sko, mens de to øverste sko fungerer som
almindelige vippesko uden indsprøjtningshuller.
Ved den kritiske hastighed på ca. 2700 RPM er den laterale forskydningen
af den simulerede industrielle kompressor reduceret med 62 % ved at benytte
et indsprøjtningstryk på 20 bar (0.2 l/min per sko). Ved 15 000 RPM reduc-
eres forskydningerne med over 30 % ved at benytte et indsprøjningstryk på
140 bar (16.7 l/min per sko). Dette giver en stigning i eﬀekt på 46.7 % sam-
menlignet med et almindeligt vippeskoleje. Vibrationerne ved de høje rota-
tionshastigheder kan reduceres yderligere ved at benytte et højere indsprøjt-
ningstryk. Dette fører dog til et urealistisk højt energiforbrug. Ved at kom-
binere 20 bar ved 2700 RPM og 140 bar ved højere rotationshastigheder er
amplitudegrænsen fra API standarden respekteret i hele det operative om-
råde, hvilket opfylder formålet med brugen af den kontrollerbare smøring dvs.
maksimum amplitude mindre end 22 µm.
Det er også konstateret at den kontrollerbare smøring betydeligt kan ændre
egensvingningsformer og dæmpningsforhold af den industrielle kompressor, og
herved forbedre de laterale dynamikker af rotor-leje systemet.

1 Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In modern society a continuous demand to the industry of increasing produc-
tion and simultaneously reducing power loss exists. This makes considerable
demands to machine elements towards eﬃciency. To achieve this, the machine
elements have to withstand larger operational speeds, higher loads, higher
temperature ranges etc. For that reason, a great amount of resources and en-
ergy are put into research of ways of improving machine elements worldwide.
Especially bearings are of great interest, as there is room for improvement due
to power loss and instability at high operational speeds.
Journal bearings are a type of bearing that is widely used due to its high
load carrying capacities. One type of journal bearings is the tilting-pad jour-
nal bearing (TPJB), illustrated in ﬁgure 1.1, which is used due to its good
dynamic stability properties. Within the last few decades a new idea of using
controllable hybrid lubrication in the tilting pads has been developed and in-
vestigated. The idea of using controllable lubrication in the tilting pads was
ﬁrstly presented by Santos [1] in 1994. By using controllable lubrication, a
further increase in the damping and stabilization can be obtained.
The deﬁnition of the controllable hybrid lubrication is described by Santos
and Svendsen, 2017, [2] as follows:
"When the hydrostatic and the hydrodynamic lubrication are simultaneously
combined in a ﬂuid ﬁlm bearing, one refers to the hybrid lubrication. While the
hydrodynamic lubrication regime is fundamentally driven by the journal angu-
lar velocity, the hydrostatic lubrication regime is driven by an external source
of pressurization, i.e., a high-pressure pump. Oriﬁces and pockets are normally
machined over the bearing surface and directly connected to the high-pressure
unit by means of pipelines. The pair of oriﬁces is circumferentially machined
around the bearing surface, normally 180 deg from each other. Through the
oriﬁces, the pressurized lubricant reaches the bearing gap, inﬂuences the lubri-
cant ﬁlm pressure responsible for the separation of the rotating surface from
the stationary one, and carries the journal loads. When part of the hydro-
static pressure is dynamically modiﬁed by means of hydraulic servosystems,
one refers to the controllable lubrication. Such a lubrication regime is realized

by further including a servovalve between the high-pressure unit and a pair of
oriﬁces. Through the servovalve’s input signal, the lubricant pressure and ﬂow
through the pair of oriﬁces are electronically modiﬁed, generating controllable
lubricant ﬁlm pressures and hence controllable forces. The controllable lubri-

This project is a further theoretical investigation and optimization of the dy-

1.2
TPJB and Rotor Programs
The investigation and optimization of the controllable hybrid lubricated TPJB
is carried out using a TPJB and a rotor program for analyzing mode shapes,
damping ratios, and unbalance responses.
The TPJB program was initially written by Ilmar Ferreira Santos in 1998
for his paper; ’Tilting-Pad Journal Bearings With Electronic Radial Oil Injec-
tion’ [3].
In 2005 the program was modiﬁed by Bo Uldall Kristiansen as part of his
master’s thesis at DTU [4]. In this project the mesh routine was improved to

In 2016 Mik Mathias Mønsted improved the program further during his mas-

1.3
Project Description
The title of this project is ’Optimization of Damping in Flexible Rotor-Bearing
Systems via Controllable Fluid Film Bearings’. The aim is to make a theo-
retical parameter study of the inﬂuence of controllable hybrid lubrication on
the damping eﬀects of a tilting pad journal bearing in a rotor-bearing system.
This parameter study is conducted using the TPJB and rotor programs.
The project is carried out as follows:
• Verify the dynamic coeﬃcients from TPJB program using Someya et al.
[6] as reference.
• Refactor the rotor code in order for the program to be more eﬃcient and
easier readable.
• Add oil ﬂow and shear stress calculations for the pads in the TPJB
program.
• Add unbalance response to the rotor code.
• Simulate the rotor-bearing system with bearings in passive condition (no
controllable lubrication) in order to determine the TPJB conﬁguration
that is to be optimized.
• Make simulations that sweeps over pressure of the controllable lubrica-
tion in order to determine optimum for chosen TPJB.
In order to fulﬁll the above listed goals, the theory behind the TPJB and rotor
is examined in the following chapters.

2.2
Modiﬁed Reynolds Equation (MRE)
Reynolds equation was ﬁrst derived by Osborne Reynolds in 1886 [8], and is
today well-known and widely used in theory of lubrication. Reynolds equation
is used for determining the pressure distribution in ﬂuid ﬁlms.
In 1998 Santos and Russo [3] presented a Modiﬁed Reynolds Equation (MRE)
for determining the pressure distribution in the ﬂuid ﬁlm of an controllable
lubricated TPJB. The following derivation of the MRE is made based on this
article.
In ﬁgure 2.2 a section of the journal and pad surface is illustrated. Here the
moving curvilinear coordinate frame ¯Bj(¯x, ¯y, ¯z) is deﬁned, in which the MRE
is derived. The ﬁgure also shows ﬂuid ﬂow kinematics, velocity proﬁles and
no-slip boundary conditions.
In order to derive the MRE from Navier-Stokes
equation, the following assumptions are made:
• The lubricant is a Newtonian and incompressible
• The ﬂow is laminar
• The pressure gradient in the radial direction is negligible
• The body forces and inertia are negligible

When modeling the complete set of Dynamic Coeﬃcient, the perturbation

is to be assembled, the matrices have to have extra space allocated for the pad
DOFs. This increases the computational time compared to the reduced dy-
namic coeﬃcient method. However, with the current eﬃciency of computers
this is not a problem.

Solve
In order to solve the global equation of motion for the rotor-bearing system,
an eigenvalue problem needs to be solved. There are two ways of setting up
an eigenvalue problem. The normal eigenvalue problem is set up on the form:
Aφ = λφ
(3.27)
whereas the generalized eigenvalue problem is set up on the form:
(A −λB) φ = 0
(3.28)
In the above λ is the eigenvalues, φ is the eigenvectors and A and B are
matrices set up from the global equation of motion.
From the eigenvalues
the damped and undamped natural frequencies and the damping ratio can
be found for the rotor-bearing system. The mode shapes of the rotor-bearing
system are found by normalizing the eigenvectors.

mented. This was done in order to ensure that no errors due to precision of the
calculations would occur. The three methods are presented in the following.

3.6. NATURAL FREQUENCIES AND DAMPING RATIO
33
3.5.4
Solving in MatLab
All three methods were initially solved in MatLab with the command eig.
Due to construction symmetry around the center node of the rotor, the mode
shapes should also be symmetrical around this center node. However for the
two methods solving the generalized eigenvalue problem, the mode shapes were
not symmetric around the center node on the rotor.
The MatLab command eigs was then used instead. This command allows
for specifying that only a given number of the lowest modes are to be calcu-
lated. This increases the calculation precision on the lower modes, which are
of interest in this project, instead of MatLab trying to obtain good precision
of all modes.
Using this command it was found that the mode shapes of all three methods
were identical and symmetric around the center node of the rotor, as desired.
However, when using this command, MatLab kept printing warnings saying;
’singularities/badly scaled’.
It was therefore found that the most numerical robust method for solving
the eigenvalue problem was method 0 using the MatLab command eig(A).
This method was used throughout this project.

Number of Pad DOFs
The TPJB program allows for 3 pivot DOFs; rotation, radial deﬂection and
tangential deﬂection (α, ξ′, η′). In preliminary analysis it was found that the
pivot primarily deforms in the radial direction, with a tangential pivot defor-

the forces acting on the pad always to be perpendicular to the pad. As a re-
sult of this, all simulations were only run with 2 pivot DOFs; rotation, α, and
radial deﬂection, ξ′.

The ﬂow proﬁle used to simulated the ﬂow from the injection oriﬁce was cho-
sen as the Hagen-Poiseuille ﬂow proﬁle. This ﬂow represents the reality more
correctly than the 2D circular Gaussian ﬂow proﬁle. The 2D circular Gaussian
ﬂow proﬁle was implemented in case the TPJB program experienced conver-
gency issues with the large pressure gradients. However, this was not an issue
during this project.

5 Passive Bearing Selection
In this project four diﬀerent conﬁgurations of the tilting pad journal bearing
(TPJB) is analyzed; 4-pad and 5-pad bearings both with load-between-pad
(LBP) and load-on-pad (LOP). The goal with this project is to optimize the
controllable hybrid lubrication of the bearings, in order to achieve the best
possible operational conditions.
However, before this is done it is important to identify the bearing with the
best operational condition without injection holes for the controllable lubrica-
tion (passive condition). This is done in order to ensure that if the controllable
lubrication of the bearing fails during operation, the rotor-bearing system will
not break due to instability or the like.
In this chapter, the bearing with the best operational conditions in passive
condition will be identiﬁed. This is carried out by analyzing Campbell dia-
grams, stability plots, mode shapes and unbalance responses.
5.1

5.2
Mode Shapes
In order to investigate the damping of the 4-pad TPJBs further, the mode
shapes are plotted at the critical speeds. At these speeds it is preferred to
have damping ratios ζ above 0.3. In the plotted mode shapes red illustrates
nodes with TPJBs, blue illustrates nodes with discs and black illustrates or-
dinary shaft nodes. The mode shapes are normalized in all directions.
In ﬁgure 5.5 the mode shapes for the 4-pad TPJB with LBP are illustrated.
Figure 5.5(a) and 5.5(b) are backwards and forwards motion of the same mode,
both occurring at critical speed 2500 RPM. The largest displacement takes
place in the center of the shaft, meaning that it is pure bending. In the bear-
ing node, illustrated in red, the displacement is very small, which does not
allow for much damping. The damping for both the backwards and forward
motion i approximately 0.07, which is very low. However, it is damping issue
like this that is to be solved using controllable hybrid lubrication.
The modes in ﬁgure 5.5(c) and 5.5(d) are very similar, again indicating that
it is the backwards and forwards motion of the same mode. In the middle of
the rotor a nodal point exist with close to zero displacement and the largest
displacement of the mode is at the rotor ends. This allows the bearing to
provide more damping. The damping ratio is approximately 0.4 for both the
forward and backward mode which is above the preferred value of 0.3.
In ﬁgure 5.6 the mode shapes for the 4-pad TPJB with LOP are illustrated.
It can be seen that the mode shapes are not symmetric in the XY -plane, as
was the case for the 4-pad TPJB with LBP.
For both mode shapes, the displacement in the center node of the rotor is
approximately zero, and the largest displacements are found in the rotor ends.
This means that the vibrations in the nodes with TPJBs is relatively large,
allowing the bearing to provide damping.
For the mode shape at 8650 RPM, ﬁgure 5.6(a), the damping is ζ = 0.236, and
for the mode shape at 10 900 RPM, ﬁgure 5.6(b), the damping is ζ = 0.370.
Both damping ratios can be concluded to be in the preferred range of 0.3.
In the previous section it was argued that two critical speeds would occur
under 2900 RPM. However, due to the program convergens issues it was not
possible to know anything about these mode shapes and their damping ratios.

sonable. The pads at 45° and 135° (the two pads at the top of the bearing)
has a larger oil inlet ﬂow than the pads at 225° and 315°. This is due to the
external load casing the shaft to be closer to the bottom of the bearing. For
that reason the gap between the journal and the pads are larger at the top of
the bearing allowing more oil to pass by.
The power loss per pad, ﬁgure 5.10(b), also increases as the rotational speed
increases. On average the power loss of the pads are 100 W at 2000 RPM and
4500 W at 15 000 RPM. The power loss for the pads at 225° and 315° (bottom
pads) are larger than the pads at 45° and 135°. This is due to the pressure
being higher at the bottom pads causing a larger power loss.

5.5
Conclusion
In this chapter the four TPJB conﬁgurations (4-pad LBP, 4-pad LOP, 5-pad
LBP, and 5-pad LOP) were investigated as conventional TPJBs without con-
trollable lubrication. This was done in order to identify the bearing conﬁgu-
ration to be optimized further.
The two bearings with 5 pads were early discarded, as they had modes lying
along the 1x line.
The two bearings with 4 pads had very similar operational conditions. How-
ever, when analyzing the Campbell diagrams it was found that the 4-pad
LOP bearing had a mode lying along the 1x line in the interval of 9000 RPM
to 11 000 RPM. For the mode shapes, it was found that the 4-pad LBP bear-
ing had symmetrical mode shapes in the XY-plane opposite to the 4-pad LOP
bearing. Further it was possible to simulate the 4-pad LBP bearing at lower
rotational speeds than the 4-pad LOP bearing.
It is therefore concluded that the bearing having the best operational con-
ditions without controllable lubrication is the 4-pad TPJB with LBP. This
bearing will therefore be analyzed and optimized further.

6 Varying Injection Pressure
In the previous chapter the 4-pad TPJB with LBP was found to be the best
without controllable hybrid lubrication. This bearing conﬁguration is therefore
used for ﬁnding the optimal injection pressure of the controllable lubrication.
Each pad in the bearing only has a single injection hole, located at the center
of the pad.
In this chapter, the controllable lubrication is injected in diﬀerent pad com-
binations. The Campbell diagram, stability map, and unbalance response are
then used to analyze and minimize the vibrations of the rotor-bearing system.
The injection pressure is varied in the range of 20 bar to 300 bar and compared
to results from the conventional TPJB. The pads where the injection pressure
is not varied are working as conventional tilting pads with no injection holes.
All unbalance responses plotted in this chapter can be found in A3 size in
Appendix D.
6.1
All Pads
For the ﬁrst setup, the injection pressure is varied in all pads simultaneously.
Intuitively, using controllable lubrication should improve the damping of the
bearing, leading to smaller vibrations.
In ﬁgure 6.1 the Campbell diagram and stability map are plotted.
The Campbell diagram shows that the modes for injection pressures 100 bar
and 140 bar are lying closely along the dashed 1x line (synchronous speed).
This is not preferred as these modes will be excited by the rotational speed.
The modes for the remaining injection pressures have an acceptable distance
to the 1x line.
The stability map shows that the rotor-bearing system does not become un-
stable for any injection pressures, as all graphs are above zero. At the higher
rotational speeds, when looking at the mode closest to zero, the damping ratio
increases as the injection pressure increases, which is favorable.

6.5
Conclusion
In this chapter the injection pressure of the controllable hybrid lubrication
was varied for diﬀerent injection setups.
It was seen that mode shapes can be changed signiﬁcantly using controllable
lubrication.
The best setup was found to be using controllable lubrication in the two bot-
tom pads, while the two top pads remain conventional tilting pads with no
injection holes.
When using the controllable lubrication, the injection pressure has to be var-
ied as a function of the rotational speed in order for the displacements to be
minimized. As the optimal injection pressure at a given rotational speed is not
the same when minimizing the displacement of the shaft end and the center
disc respectively, a compromise has to be found.
At the critical speed of approximately 45 Hz, the displacement was reduced
from 47 µm at no injection, to 18 µm at 20 bar injection pressure (0.2 l/min).
This is a reduction of 62 % leading to the amplitude limit of the API standard
being respected.
At high rotational speeds it was found, that in order to obtain the smallest vi-
brations, the injection pressure had to be as large as possible. At 15 000 RPM
and 300 bar injection pressure the vibrations of the shaft end was reduced by
over 70 %. However, at this pressure each of the bottom pads uses an injec-
tion ﬂow of 69.3 l/min. This resulted in a total power consumption of 91.4 kW,
which is an increase of over 400 % when compared to a conventional TPJB.
By reducing the injection pressure to 140 bar it was possible to reduce the
injection ﬂow to 16.7 l/min and more importantly reducing the total power
consumption to 26.4 kW. This is an increase in power of 46.7 % when com-
pared to a conventional TPJB. However, the displacements are still reduced
by more then 30 % and are within the amplitude limit of the API standard.
Finally it was found that if one of the two bottom injection nozzles stop work-
ing, it is still preferable using controllable lubrication in one of the bottom
pads. Which bottom pad to use does not matter, as their unbalance response
was found to be the same. However, some of the displacements could not be
reduced as much, but it is still an improvement compared to using a conven-
tional TPJB with no controllable lubrication.
Through all simulations it was found that using a wrong injection pressure
or injection setup could not only increase the vibrations, but also make the
rotor-bearing system approach instability. However, using the right setup can
reduce vibrations signiﬁcantly.

7 Conclusion
During this master’s thesis the overall goal was to increase the operational
range of a given ﬂexible rotor-bearing system from 12 000 RPM to 15 000 RPM.
This was carried as a theoretical investigation and optimization of the dynamic
properties of the controllable hybrid lubricated tilting pad journal bearing
(TPJB). For doing this, a mathematical model of the ﬂexible rotor-bearing
system was built, in order to predict its lateral dynamics.
Initially the dynamic coeﬃcients of a conventional TPJB (with no control-
lable lubrication) were veriﬁed using Someya et al. [6] as reference. In general,
the simulations showed deviations below 2 %.
In the TPJB program, oil ﬂow and shear stress calculations were added. These
calculations were veriﬁed for a conventional TPJB with no controllable lubri-
cation. The ﬂow continuity showed deviations of less than 0.5 %.
Unbalance response was added to the rotor program. This allows for predic-
tion of the rotor-bearing system’s lateral dynamics.
The rotor program was then refactored and combined with the TPJB pro-
gram in order to simulate the ﬂexible rotor-bearing system.
A qualitative analysis based on Campbell diagrams, stability maps, mode
shapes, unbalance responses and bearing energy consumption identiﬁed the
best passive bearing conﬁguration as the 4-pad TPJB with LBP.
This bearing conﬁguration was used for a theoretical investigation and opti-
mization of the dynamic properties of the controllable hybrid lubricated TPJB.
The injection pressure was varied in the range of 20 bar to 300 bar and com-
pared to results from the conventional TPJB for diﬀerent injection setups.
The best setup was found to be using controllable lubrication in the two bot-
tom pads, while the two top pads remain conventional tilting pads with no
injection holes.
• At the critical speed of approximately 2700 RPM = 45 Hz, the dis-
placement was reduced by 62 % using an injection pressure of 20 bar
(0.2 l/min).

• At 15 000 RPM the vibrations were reduced by over 70 % using 300 bar
injection pressure (69.3 l/min). However, this resulted in a power in-
crease of over 400 % when compared to a conventional TPJB. This is an
unrealistic industrial application.
• At 15 000 RPM the vibrations were reduced by over 30 % using 140 bar
injection pressure (16.7 l/min). This is an increase in power of 46.7 %
when compared to a conventional TPJB.
By combining the 20 bar at 2700 RPM and the 140 bar at higher speeds, the
amplitude limit of the API standard is respected in the entire operational
range.
Through all simulations it was seen that mode shapes and damping ratios can
be changed signiﬁcantly using controllable lubrication. Using a wrong injec-
tion pressure or injection setup could not only increase the vibrations, but
also make the rotor-bearing system approach instability. However, by using
the right injection pressure and setup, damping can be increased and conse-
quently vibrations reduced signiﬁcantly.
In summary, reducing lateral vibrations under controllable lubrication with
20 bar (0.2 l/min) is a very realistic task, but using 140 bar (16.7 l/min) is still
an unrealistic industrial application.
In this framework, active lubrication
(feedback controlled) could be a possible solution to the problem and should
be investigated in the future, keeping pressure, ﬂow and power loss under
reasonable values.

8 Future Work
Based on this master’s thesis there is a lot of possibilities for future work. In
the following, some ideas will be presented.
Experimental Validation
Through this master’s thesis it was found that a 4-pad TPJB with LBP was the
best bearing conﬁguration. Further it was found that the smallest vibration
occurred with the injection setup using only the two bottom pads for control-
lable hybrid lubrication, while the two top pads were working as conventional
tilting pads with no injection holes. It would be interesting to investigate this
experimentally and verify the results found theoretically in this project.
Controller
It was found that in order to minimize the vibrations in the whole operational
range, the injection pressure had to be varied as a function of the rotational
speed. A next step could therefore be to design a controller for the rotor-
bearing system, both theoretically and experimentally.
Hole Conﬁguration
In this project, the controllable lubrication was injected through a single injec-
tion hole, placed in the center of the pad. However, it would be interesting to
increase the number of injection holes in the pads, and optimize on the placing
of these holes. Maybe by using more injection holes, the diameter of the holes
could be smaller, thereby reducing the amount of oil and power needed for the
controllable lubrication.
Discharge Coeﬃcients
When the hole size is increased to a certain point, some of the assumptions
used to set up Reynolds Modiﬁed Equation does no longer apply. It is therefore
necessary to use a discharge coeﬃcient in order to correctly model the amount
of ﬂow coming into the pad from the injection nozzle. This ﬂow from the
injection nozzle could both theoretically and experimentally be investigated,
in order to determine the correct discharge coeﬃcient. This would allow the
user of the TPJB program to use larger hole diameters for the controllable
lubrication.

DYNAMICS
2.6.1
General
Note: Refer to API Publ 684 for more information on rotor dynamics.
2.6.1.1
In the design of rotor-bearing systems, consider-
ation shall be given to all potential sources of periodic forcing
phenomena (excitation) which shall include, but are not lim-
ited to, the following sources:
a. Unbalance in the rotor system.
b. Oil-ﬁlm instabilities (whirl).
c. Internal rubs.
d. Blade, vane, nozzle, and diffuser passing frequencies.
e. Gear-tooth meshing and side bands.
f. Coupling misalignment.
g. Loose rotor-system components.
h. Hysteretic and friction whirl.
i. Boundary-layer ﬂow separation.
j. Acoustic and aerodynamic cross-coupling forces.
k. Asynchronous whirl. 
l. Ball and race frequencies of rolling element bearings.
m. Electrical line frequency.
Note 1: The frequency of a potential source of excitation may be less
than, equal to, or greater than the rotational speed of the rotor.
Note 2: When the frequency of a periodic forcing phenomenon
(excitation) applied to a rotor-bearing support system coincides with
a natural frequency of that system, the system will be in a state of
resonance. A rotor-bearing support system in resonance may have
the magnitude of its normal vibration ampliﬁed. The magnitude of
ampliﬁcation and, in the case of critical speeds, the rate of change of
the phase-angle with respect to speed, are related to the amount of
damping in the system. 
2.6.1.2
For the purposes of this standard, a resonant condi-
tion of concern, such as lateral and torsional critical speeds,
are those with an ampliﬁcation factor (AF) equal to or greater
than 2.5 (see Figure 1.2-1).
2.6.1.3
Resonances of structural support systems that are
within the vendor’s scope of supply and that affect the rotor
vibration amplitude shall not occur within the speciﬁed oper-
ating speed range or the speciﬁed separation margins (SM)
(see 2.6.2.10). The effective stiffness of the structural support
shall be considered in the analysis of the dynamics of the
rotor-bearing-support system (see 2.6.2.4d).

2.6.2
Lateral Analysis
2.6.2.1
Critical speeds and their associated AFs shall be
determined by means of a damped unbalanced rotor response
analysis. 
2.6.2.2
The location of all critical speeds below the trip
speed shall be conﬁrmed on the test stand during the mechan-
ical running test (see 2.6.3.1). The accuracy of the analytical
model shall be demonstrated (see 2.6.3).
2.6.2.3
Before carrying out the damped unbalanced
response analysis, the vendor shall conduct an undamped
analysis to identify the undamped critical speeds and deter-
mine their mode shapes located in the range from 0% – 125%
of trip speed. Unless otherwise speciﬁed, the results of the
undamped analysis shall be furnished. The presentation of the
results shall include: 
a. Mode shape plots (relative amplitude vs. axial position on
the rotor).
b. Critical speed-support stiffness map (frequency vs. sup-
port stiffness). Superimposed on this map shall be the
calculated system support stiffness; horizontal (kxx), and ver-
tical (kyy) (see Figure 1.2-2).
Note: For machinery with widely varying bearing loads and/or load
direction such as overhung style machines, the vendor may propose
to substitute mode shape plots for the undamped critical speed map
and list the undamped critical speed for each of the identiﬁed modes.
2.6.2.4
The damped unbalanced response analysis shall
include but shall not be limited to the following:
Note: The following is a list of items the analyst is to consider. It
does not address the details and product of the analysis which is cov-
ered in 2.6.2.7 and 2.6.2.8.
a. Rotor masses, including the mass moment of coupling
halves, stiffness, and damping effects (for example, accumu-
lated ﬁt tolerances, ﬂuid stiffening and damping).
b. Bearing lubricant-ﬁlm stiffness and damping values
including changes due to speed, load, preload, range of oil
temperatures, maximum to minimum clearances resulting
from accumulated assembly tolerances, and the effect of
asymmetrical loading which may be caused by gear forces,
side streams, eccentric clearances, etc.
c. For tilt-pad bearings, the pad pivot stiffness. 
d. Support stiffness, mass, and damping characteristics,
including effects of frequency dependent variation. The term
“support” includes the foundation or support structure, the
base, the machine frame and the bearing housing as appropri-
ate. For machines whose bearing support system stiffness
values are less than or equal to 3.5 times the bearing oil ﬁlm
stiffness values, support stiffness values derived from modal
testing or calculated frequency dependent support stiffness
and damping values (impedances) shall be used. The vendor
shall state the support stiffness values used in the analysis and
the basis for these values (for example, modal tests of similar
rotor support systems, or calculated support stiffness values). 
Note 1: The support stiffness should in most cases be no more than
8.75 × 106 N/mm (5 × 106 lb./in.).
Note 2:   Guide lines are used to deﬁne whether or not bearing sup-
port stiffness should be considered. While modal testing of the
actual bearing support system would be preferred, an analytical
analysis (such as FEA) is permitted.
e. Rotational speed, including the various starting-speed
detents, operating speed and load ranges (including agreed-
upon test conditions if different from those speciﬁed), trip
speed, and coast-down conditions.
f. The inﬂuence, over the operating range, of the hydrody-
namic stiffness and damping generated by the casing shaft
end seals.
g. The location and orientation of the radial vibration probes
which shall be the same in the analysis as in the machine.
2.6.2.5
In addition to the damped unbalanced response
analysis requirements of 2.6.2.4, for machines equipped with
rolling element bearings, the vendor shall state the bearing
stiffness and damping values used for the analysis and either
the basis for these values or the assumptions made in calculat-
ing the values.
2.6.2.6

the train shall be included in the damped unbalanced response
analysis (that is, a train lateral analysis shall be performed).
Note: In particular, this analysis should be considered for machinery
trains with rigid couplings.

2.6.3
Unbalanced Rotor Response Veriﬁcation 
Test
2.6.3.1
An unbalanced rotor response test shall be per-
formed as part of the mechanical running test (see 4.3 of the
applicable chapter), and the results shall be used to verify the
analytical model. The actual response of the rotor on the test
stand to the same arrangement of unbalance and bearing
loads as was used in the analysis speciﬁed in 2.6.2.9 shall be
the criterion for determining the validity of the damped
unbalanced response analysis. To accomplish this, the
requirements of 2.6.3.1.1 through 2.6.3.1.6 shall be followed.
2.6.3.1.1
During the mechanical running test (see 4.3 of
the applicable chapter), the amplitudes and phase angle of
the shaft vibration from zero to trip speed, shall be recorded.
The gain of any analog recording instruments used shall be
preset before the test so that the highest response peak is
within 60% – 100% of the recorder’s full scale on the test-
unit coast-down (deceleration).
Note: This set of readings is normally taken during a coastdown,
with convenient increments of speed such as 50 rpm. Since at this
point the rotor is balanced, any vibration amplitude and phase
detected should be the result of residual unbalance and mechanical
and electrical runout.
2.6.3.1.2
The location of critical speeds below the trip
speed shall be established.
2.6.3.1.3
The unbalance which was used in the analysis
performed in 2.6.2.9, shall be added to the rotor in the loca-
tion used in the analysis. The unbalance shall not exceed 8
times the value from Equations 1.2-2a or 1.2-2b.
2.6.3.1.4
The machine shall then be brought up to the trip
speed and the indicated vibration amplitudes and phase shall
be recorded using the same procedure used for 2.6.3.1.1. 
2.6.3.1.5
The corresponding indicated vibration data taken
in accordance with 2.6.3.1.1 shall be vectorially subtracted
from the results of this test.
Note: It is practical to store the residual unbalance vibration mea-
surements recorded in the step at 2.6.3.1.1 and by use of computer
code perform the vectorial subtraction called for in this paragraph at
each appropriate speed. This makes the comparison of the test
results with the computer analysis of 2.6.2.9 quite practical. It is nec-
essary for probe orientation be the same for the analysis and the
machine for the vectorial subtraction to be valid.
2.6.3.1.6
The results of the mechanical run including the
unbalance response veriﬁcation test shall be compared with
those from the analytical model speciﬁed at 2.6.2.9. 
2.6.3.2
The vendor shall correct the model if it fails to meet
either of the following criteria:
a. The actual critical speeds determined on test shall not devi-
ate from the corresponding critical speeds predicted by analysis
by more than 5%. Where the analysis predicts more than one
critical speed in a particular mode (due, for example, to the
bearing characteristics being signiﬁcantly different horizontally
and vertically or between the two ends of the machine), the test
value shall not be lower than 5% below the lowest predicted
value nor higher than 5% above the highest predicted value.
Note: It is possible, particularly on electric motors, that the vertical
and horizontal stiffness are signiﬁcantly different and the analysis
will predict two differing critical speeds. Should the operating speed
fall between these critical speeds, these two critical speeds should be
treated separately, as if they resulted from separate modes.
