Abstract
This thesis study the Extended Zero-Derivative Principle as a method of approximating the slow invari-
ant manifolds of dynamical systems that exhibit slow-fast dynamics. The Extended Zero-Derivative
Principle is presented along with the Fenichel theory for singularly perturbed systems.
A simple chemical system is introduced and the ability of the EZDP to approximate the slow dynamics
of the system is tested. The manifolds provided by the EZDP is then used to create a system with a
reduced number of variables and the dynamics of this system is compared to the original system.
Another model is then introduced where special solutions called canard solutions are present. The
dynamics of the EZDP manifolds are then studied in an area near the canard solutions. In this area
the theory behind the Extended Zero-Derivative Principle is not applicable. In this area a connection
between the EZDP manifolds and the canard solution is observed.
A nal model is then introduced, where mixed-mode oscillations occur for certain parameter values.
The EZDP manifolds for this system are then examined in the area where the small oscillation of the
mixed-mode oscillation occur.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The benets of creating mathematical models of chemical and biological processes are many. In biology
the models can be used to gain increased understanding of the underlying mechanisms of the system
and predict the behavior of the system, when conditions are changed. In chemistry the models can
be used to predict optimal species concentrations in order to optimize yield, reaction time or quality
of the the end product. It is therefore essential to develop mathematical models of the biological and
chemical systems that can reliably approximate experimental data.
The kinetics of biological and chemical systems are modeled using dierential equations. Biological
and chemical systems often consist of a vary large number of species and reactions. This will result in a
mathematical model with a large number of dierential equations and variables. Large sets of complex
dierential equations are computationally very expensive. It is therefore of interest to develop and
use methods of reducing the number of variables in such systems, however this must be done without
compromising the accuracy of the original model.
The kinetics of the mathematical model often happen on dierent time scales and it is sometimes
possible to separate the dynamics of the model into two separate time-scales.
In such cases it is
possible to consider the dynamics of the system on a fast time-scale and a slow one. If the separation
between the time-scales is great enough then on the fast time-scale the slow dynamics are so slow
that they can be considered constant and on the slow time-scale the fast dynamics are so fast that
they are considered instantaneous. In some models where the fast dynamics all tend towards a lower
dimensional manifold after some time. In these models the dynamics of the system can be conned
within these manifolds. The dimension of the systems can then be reduced by only considering only
the dynamics of attracting invariant manifolds and this will in turn lead to a computationally less
expensive model.
One approach to approximate these invariant lower dimensional manifolds is to look at the zero set of
the time-derivative of dierential equations. The latest iteration of this method is called the Extended
Zero-Derivative Principle, or EZDP for short, and is presented in [4].
1.1
Objective of the thesis
This thesis will implement the Extended Zero-Derivative Principle method numerically and analyze
the methods ability to approximate invariant manifolds of systems that exhibit slow-fast dynamics.
The EZDP method is introduced in [4] and it is in this paper that this thesis has it's base.
The thesis has two objectives. The rst one is to provide a numerical implementation of the EZDP
and analyze how well the EZDP manifolds can approximate the slow dynamics of slow-fast systems.
The second objective is to explore behavior of the manifolds produced by the EZDP in the areas where
the theory behind the EZDP is not applicable.

2.1
Fenichel theory
Singular perturbation theory is the theory concerning problems involving a small parameter ϵ, which
cannot be approximated by setting the parameter equal to zero. This is in contrast to the regular
perturbed problems where an approximation of the system can be reached simply by setting the
parameter ϵ equal to zero [15]. Fenichel theory tries to explain the behavior of singular perturbed
systems with a parameter that is suciently small, i.e. 0 < ϵ ≪1. The theory of this section is based
on the theory presented in [6]. The systems analyzed will be on the following form.

The Fenichel theory proves the existence of a manifold that describes the slow ow of a system with
slow-fast dynamics if certain criteria are met. There are several methods to computationally approxi-

2.2.4
Ghosts
In the previous it has been established that the Fenichel manifold for a system on the form (2.4) will
have time-derivative that is very close to 0, which should mean that the zero set of the time-derivative,
i.e. f(k)

case for the full zero set. The zero set for the time-derivative will contain an approximation for the
Fenichel manifold for the system, but it may also contain spurious solutions which will be referred to
a as ghosts. In this section it will briey be discussed how such ghosts can appear.
Considering a system on the form (2.5) in the case where n = 1. Then the zero set of the k-th time-
derivative of the fast variable will be f(k)(x, y, ϵ) = 0. Looking at the case of ϵ = 0 and using the
relation of (2.12) the following holds

(2.54)
This means that the solution to f(k) = 0 is the union of f = 0 and ∂xf(k−1) = 0. For ϵ > 0 the rst
part will become an approximation of the Fenichel manifold, while the later will become a ghost. For
the equation of g(k)(x, y, ϵ) = 0 the relation from (2.12) will again be used and the following will hold
for ϵ = 0
g(k)(x, y, 0) = ∂xg(k−1)(x, y, 0)f(x, y, 0)
(2.55)
The zero set for this equation again consists of two parts, which is f = 0 and ∂xg = 0. As before the
rst part will be an approximation of the Fenichel manifold for ϵ > 0, while the other one will produce
ghosts.

2.3
ILDM
The Intrinsic Low-Dimensional Manifold method is one of the more popular methods for nding ap-
proximations of the Fenichel manifolds for slow-fast systems. The method was rst introduced in [10].
The method assumes a N-dimensional system on the form
˙z = F(z),
z ∈RN
(2.56)
For this system the Jacobian at the point x, J(x) = DF(x), will have N eigenvalues and corresponding
eigenvectors. It is assumed that all of the eigenvalues are dierent such that the eigenvectors form a
tangent space to RN at the point x. The eigenvalues at the point x will be named λp(x) for p = 1...N
and their corresponding eigenvectors will be named vp(x). If the eigenvalues are complex, then only
the real part will be considered. The eigenvalues are then ordered in the following fashion
Reλ1 ≤Reλ2 ≤... ≤ReλN−1 ≤ReλN
(2.57)
with equality between two eigenvalues only occurring when the eigenvalues are pairs of complex conju-
gates. The idea of the ILDM is that if there exists a spectral gap between a pair of eigenvalues at some
k, i.e. that Reλk ≪Reλk+1, then the system consists of k fast variables and N −k slow variables.
The ILDM is then dened as the manifold with dimension N −k where the fast component of F in
the eigenvector base vanishes. If V is dened as matrix where the eigenvectors are the columns and
α = (α1, α2, ..., αN)T then F can be dened as
F = V α
⇔
α = V −1F
(2.58)
The ILDM is then dened from α1 = ... = αk = 0. For a system on the form (2.5), where the spectral
gap corresponds to ϵ, then the ILDM approximates the Fenichel manifold to an order of O(ϵ), which is
at least the same order of magnitude as the second time-derivative any given variable using the EZDP.

Chapter 4
Canards
The model in the previous chapter displayed slow-fast dynamics, but it eventually came to rest at a
stable x point. A lot of interesting real world problems that display slow-fast dynamics will not reach
a steady state, instead they can display what is called relaxation oscillations. Systems that display
relaxation oscillations will typically follow an attracting manifold of the system until it reaches a fold
of the system and then jumps to another attracting attracting manifold. This can happen if the slow
manifold of the system is S-shaped. This type of behavior happens a lot in real world problems in both
biology, chemistry and electronics.
Some special cases of slow-fast systems exist where the trajectory of a system does not jump imme-
diately when the fold of the slow manifold is reached. Instead the trajectory passes through the fold
and follows the repelling part of the manifold for some time. Such a trajectory is called a canard.
Canards have been shown to have a connection dynamics of slow-fast system. Some systems exhibit
what is called mixed-mode oscillations, where a solution trajectory alternates between frequent small
amplitude oscillations and a large amplitude relaxation oscillation. The number of small oscillation for
the trajectory is linked to the number of canards and the position of the the trajectory compared to
the canard solutions.
In this chapter a model will be presented where such canards appear both on the critical manifold of
the system and also exclusively on the Fenichel manifold for the perturbed system. The theory used
in this chapter will be based on [9].
The model that is to be examined in this chapter is a model with three variables on the form

Bursting model
The previous model introduced folded nodes and canard solutions and their eect on trajectories
of slow-fast systems. However the model had no global return mechanic which means that once a
trajectory leaves the unstable manifold it will diverge to innity. Even though the model is educational
it does not have any direct relation to real world problems. The model that will be examined in this
chapter is a model that simulates what is called electrical bursting. Electrical bursting is dened as
electrical pulsing for a given amount of time followed by a period of inactivity. This form of behavior
can be observed in several biological systems like hormone secreting cells [1] and in particular pancreatic
β-cells [3]. An example of this bursting can be seen in gure 5.1, which is a recording of the voltage
in a GH4 pituitary cell. This form of electrical bursting is modeled in [13] using a model that exhibits
mixed-mode oscillations.
Mixed-mode oscillations are when a trajectory in a dynamical system alternates between frequent and
typically small amplitude oscillations and less frequent larger amplitude relaxation oscillations. Looking
again at gure 5.1 it is not dicult to envision that the bursting phenomena can be approximated
using mixed-mode oscillations.

5.2
Orbit segment approximation
A similar approach to the one used in the previous chapter can be used to approximate the slow
manifold of the system at a plane. However it is not obvious how this plane should be oriented and
where it should be located. Since the mixed-mode oscillations happen near the folded node of the
critical manifold of the system it is natural for the plane to go through this point. The orientation
of the plane is chosen to be parallel to the (V, n)-plane. The reason for this is the orientation of the
mixed-mode oscillations of simulations of the system. Looking back at gure 5.5 it can be seen that
the oscillations of the trajectory that occur near the folded node are predominantly in the V and n
directions. Comparing this to the model of the previous chapter, where the oscillations predominantly
happened in the x and z directions, means that the plane most comparable to the one used in the
previous model should be one parallel to the (V, n)-plane.
The next step is determining the initial conditions for the orbit segment method. The initial conditions
need to be suciently far away from the fold of the system and they need to start near the attracting
part of the slow manifold. Figure 5.8 shows an example of how the initial conditions could be selected.
However a much larger range of initial conditions is used in order to make sure the entire slow manifold

Chapter 6
Discussion
The application of the Extended Zero-Derivative Principle on singularly perturbed systems has been
explored in this thesis and the Fenichel theory has been presented in order to dene the properties of
slow manifold of singularly perturbed dynamical system. This sets the stage for the presentation of
the Extended Zero-Derivative Principle proposed in [4]. The relevant theorems of [4] and their proofs
are reviewed and a similar method called ILDM is also mentioned.
A model for a simple chemical system is then introduced with the intention of exploring how the well
the manifolds created using the EZDP method can approximate the slow dynamics of the system. The
system is considered for two set of parameters and the system displays dierent dynamics for each of
them. It is shown that for the rst set of parameters the slow dynamics of the system is on a one-
dimensional manifold, whereas the slow dynamics are on a two-dimensional manifold for the second
set of parameters.
A numerical method of calculating an EZDP manifold in Matlab is presented, where the contour
function is utilized to nd the contours of the derivative function that are equal to zero.
This is
done for a number of cross-sections of the 3-dimensional phase-space and the contours are then pieced
together to form the EZDP manifold for the full system. This method proves to be eective, but
does come with limitations. The method will produce a collection of points and not a true manifold.
In the thesis this becomes an issue when trying to determine the distance between the manifold and
simulations of the system. However since the number of points used to represent the manifold can be
increased at the expense of computation time and storage space it is not considered a major issue. On
the other hand if the system allows the EZDP manifold to be constructed analytically then this would
be preferable.
The EZDP manifolds are then constructed for the system for dierent order of dierentiation and for
each of the variables of the system. The second parameter set, where the slow dynamics are located
on a two-dimensional manifold is rst considered.
Using trajectories of simulations of the system
as representations of the slow manifold of the system, it is shown that increasing k in the f(k)
i
= 0
manifolds improves the approximation of the slow manifold of the system. It is then proposed that
the complexity of the system can be reduced by replacing the ODE system with three variables with
a DAE system with two variables and the algebraic equation f(k)
i
= 0. The DAE system is solved
using the ode15s solver in Matlab. It is shown that the best variable to replace with the algebraic
equation is the [A] variable, however using the f(3)
1
= 0 equation in the DAE system yields poor results
since the ode15s solver has diculty evaluating near the sharp bends of the f(3)
1
= 0 manifold. Using
one of the other equations in the DAE system yields better results and as a whole the DAE system
yields results very close to the original system. The one downside to using this method however is
the dependence of the initial conditions. This is due to the fact that the EZDP produces ghost that
have no dynamical signicance for the system. Initial conditions placed on these ghosts will therefore
yield very poor approximations of the original system. For the rst set of parameters, where the slow
manifold of the system has been shown to be one-dimensional, largely the same approach is used. The
==PAGE:74==
63
only twist is that the one-dimensional EZDP manifold is constructed using the intersection of two
dierent two-dimensional EZDP manifolds. This does however present a numerical challenge since the
detection of the intersection between the two EZDP manifolds can be dicult if the two manifolds are
almost parallel to each other. A suitable intersection is found and in a similar fashion to the other
parameter set and a DAE model is the constructed. This time with one variable and two algebraic
equations. This DAE model is found to also be a very good approximation of the original system.
Having shown that the EZDP can be used to approximate the slow manifold of a chemical system and
be used to create a system with fewer variables that exhibits the same dynamics as the original system,
then the next step going forward would be to apply it on a more realistic model with a lot of variables.
Reducing the number of variables in such a system would greatly reduce the computation time needed
to simulate the system
A new and more complex model is then introduced in order to study the behavior of the EZDP
manifolds where the Fenichel theory brakes down, namely near the fold of the critical manifold of the
system. First however the concepts of folded singularities and canard solutions are introduced, since
these eect the behavior of the system near the fold of the of the system. In this context the two
primary canard solutions named γs and γw are introduced and it is established that the parameter µ
controls the number of secondary canards solutions on the slow manifold. A method for approximating
the slow manifold of the system is then introduced called the orbit segment method. This method has
been shown to approximate the slow manifold of the system very well even when near the fold of the
system. The method uses a lot simulations of the system with various initial conditions to approximate
the slow manifold of the system. This can be seen as an expansion of the approach used in the simple
chemical system model, where simulations of the system was also used as an representation of the slow
manifold of the system. The orbit segment method is implemented in Matlab and results are found
equal to the ones found presented in [9]. The EZDP manifolds for the system is then constructed and
the argument is made for the usage of only the f(k)
3
= 0 manifolds. The possibility that the f(k)
3
= 0
and f(k+1)
1

forward. It is shown that the EZDP manifolds are a good approximation of the slow manifold of the
system when suciently far away from the fold. The dynamics of the EZDP manifolds near the folded
node is then studied in the (x, z) -plane at the folded node of the system. It is shown that the EZDP
does not approximates the slow manifold of the system very well when in the vicinity of the fold.
The parts of the EZDP manifolds in this area are mostly ghost branches. However the two primary
canards γs and γw are found to always be located on all of the EZDP manifolds. Another result of
studying the EZDP manifolds in this area is the realization that they display dierent dynamics for
odd and even time-derivatives. In the thesis the focus is on the odd integer k f(k)
3
= 0 manifolds,

EZDP manifolds it can be observed that γw jumps from one ghost branch to another at odd integer
values of the parameter µ. This is also the parameter values of µ at which new secondary canards are
created. The jump is however only present if the f(k)
3
= 0 manifold has large enough k in relation to
the µ value. The connection between the ghost branches of the EZDP manifolds and the appearance
of secondary canards indicates that the EZDP manifolds does in fact detect some of the dynamics
near the fold of the system. The signicance and validity of this discovery would be something to
explore going forward, since the theory behind the EZDP does not predict this and as such it might
be something that only holds for this specic system. On the other hand if this is a general attribute
of the EZDP manifolds then the reason for this should be explored.
Lastly a model is introduced that models the bursting displayed by hormone secreting cells. In this
model mixed-mode oscillations can be observed. This model has a return mechanic for the trajectories
of the system unlike the model in the previous chapter. First the critical manifold of the system is

found numerically. It is then shown that folded node is located near the small oscillation of the mixed-
mode oscillation trajectory of the system. This supports the assumption that the small oscillations of
the mixed-mode oscillation is governed by the dynamics of the slow manifold near the folded node. A
method of automatically detecting the number of small oscillations in the mixed-mode oscillations in
Matlab is then introduced. The method is not perfect however and detection of incorrect number of
oscillation have been observed in when working the method, but for the purpose of detecting a general
trend in the number of oscillations for varying parameters the method works suciently well. It is then
shown that by varying the parameter gK the number small oscillations in the mixed-mode oscillation
changes.
More specically the for gK < 6.3nS there are no small oscillations and the number of
oscillations increases as gK approaches approximately 3.6nS. An approximation of the slow manifold
of the system near the folded node is then attempted using the same strategy employed in orbit segment
method in the previous chapter. However the choice of initial conditions for the simulations are not
constrained by anything other than being located on the attracting part of the critical manifold and
being suciently far away from the folded node. A thing worth exploring would be to more narrowly
dene which set of initial conditions for the orbit segment method that detects all of the slow manifold
on the plane near the folded node. The orientation of this plane is another thing that is not properly
justied. It is selected to be parallel (V, n) -plane, but the argument could be made that the plane
should be tilted such that the plane and the fold are orthogonal at the folded node, since this is the
case in the previous model. However this would require a rewrite of the Matlab-code that generates the
EZDP manifolds, since in its current form it cannot generate manifolds on tilted planes. The attracting
manifold found using the orbit segment method behaves as expected and increasingly spirals around
the folded node as the gK parameter is decreased. Unlike the previous model it is not possible to infer
the unstable part of the slow manifold using symmetry and because of this the canards solutions cannot
be detected visually. Finally the EZDP manifolds are constructed for the system and for k = 2 and
k = 3 some similarities between the manifolds of this model and the previous can be observed. Namely
that for k = 2 the EZDP manifold of both systems has a saddle shape and for k = 3 both EZDP
manifolds has two saddles that creates a funnel. The f(3)

the funnel on the EZDP manifold both shift downward for increasing gK, but it is not clear why this
reduces the number of oscillations in the mixed-mode oscillation. The dynamics near the folded node
is then examined on the (V, n) -plane with the orbit segment manifold as reference. On this plane
it is observed that the ghost branches near the folded node does not contain a common point near
the folded node for all of the EZDP manifolds. This means that there is not a point corresponding
to γw from the previous model. However it can be observed that as the parameter gK is varied the

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The use of the Extended Zero-Derivative Principle is analyzed in this thesis. The thesis has intro-
duced Extended Zero-Derivative Principle method presented in [4] and briey explained the underlying
Fenichel theory.
The EZDP has been tested on a model for a simple chemical system and it has been found to approx-
imate the slow manifold of the system very well. It has also been demonstrated that the method can
be used to reduce the number of variables of the system and it has been shown to approximate the full
system very well. It is also shown that more than one variable can be reduced by using intersections
of dierent EZDP manifolds and still yield good approximations of the full system
The behavior of the EZPD manifolds has been explored near the fold of systems, where the theory of
[4] and [5] brakes down. It has been shown that the EZDP generally does not approximate the slow
dynamics of the system very well in these areas, but the results also show that some dynamics such as
the primary canard solutions of model (4.6) are located on the EZPD manifolds.
The work with the (5.1) model has shown that it is not clear how much the of the dynamics are
detected by the EZDP manifolds near a fold, but it is shown that the EZDP manifolds share many
similarities with EZDP manifolds from (4.6) near the fold.
Finally, the results of the work is discussed and suggestions to further work is proposed.
