Abstract
With the growth of maritime traffic and the complexity of ship behavior, predicting ship
trajectories has become a practical and important task to improve maritime safety. In this
thesis, I explore the use of AIS data and deep learning methods for ship trajectory predicôÙ
tion. After cleaning and preprocessing the original AIS dataset, I convert ship positions
into gridôÙbased sequences and extract key features such as longitude, latitude, ground
speed and ground heading.
To improve the prediction performance, I propose a hybrid deep learning model called
MSCNNôÙTransformer. The model combines a multiôÙscale convolutional neural network
to capture local spatial features and utilizes the Transformer encoder to learn long range
time dependencies. Compared to benchmark models such as CNN, GRU, LSTM, and
their Transformer variants, my proposed model shows higher accuracy and stability in
both singleôÙstep and multiôÙstep prediction.
Experiments are conducted on real AIS trajectories, and the results show that the MSCNNôÙ
Transformer model is effective in predicting the future position and motion state of ships.
This study demonstrates the potential of combining spatial and temporal modeling for use
in real ship monitoring systems.
Keywords: AIS data, vessel trajectory prediction, MSCNNôÙTransformer, deep learning,
multiôÙscale CNN, Transformer, maritime safety

2.2
Introduction to the AIS System
The Automatic Identification System (AIS) is a navigation aid system that contains rich
navigational information about vessels. It consists of shoreôÙbased stations, satellite terôÙ
minals, and onboard transceivers. According to the International Maritime Organization
(IMO), since 2004, all passenger ships and other vessels with a gross tonnage (GT) of 300
tons or more are required to be equipped with AIS devices[22]. The main purposes are
to improve maritime navigation safety, enhance navigational efficiency, and support marôÙ
itime traffic control operations. Currently, major coastal areas and port waters in China
are already covered by AIS networks.
AIS plays a key role in vessel monitoring and traffic management, offering the following
primary functions:
(1) Information exchange between vessels. AISôÙequipped ships continuously broadcast
critical navigational information such as vessel ID, motion parameters, geographic posiôÙ
tion, and voyage status over Very High Frequency (VHF) radio, in coordination with the
Global Positioning System (GPS). These messages are sent to nearby vessels, shore
stations, and satellites at intervals typically no longer than 6 minutes. In return, the vessel
also receives AIS messages from surrounding ships. This efficient communication helps
support collision avoidance decisions.
(2) ShoreôÙbased vessel surveillance. Maritime authorities can monitor the realôÙtime moveôÙ
ment of vessels passing through coastal waters via AIS shore stations[23]. In addition

4.4.1 Overfitting Handling
To improve the generalization ability of the MSCNNôÙTransformer model and reduce the
risk of overfitting on the training data, we employed several regularization strategies during
training.
First, Dropout layers were applied at multiple points in the model architecture. In particôÙ
ular, Dropout was used after the multiôÙhead selfôÙattention outputs within the Transformer
encoder as part of the residual connection, as well as after the fully connected layers near
the output. These Dropout layers randomly deactivate a portion of the neurons during
training, which helps prevent the model from relying too heavily on specific features in the
training data. In our implementation, dropout rates of 0.1 and 0.5 were used respectively
in the encoder and dense layers.
In addition to Dropout, we also used early stopping as a practical method to prevent overôÙ
fitting. During training, if the validation loss did not improve for a fixed number of epochs,
the process was automatically terminated. This prevents the model from continuing to
train once it starts to overfit the training data.
Although the model does not explicitly include L2 regularization (weight decay), the comôÙ
bination of Dropout and early stopping provided sufficient regularization effect. The genôÙ

6.1
Conclusions
With the continuous expansion of the scale of shipping and the constant improvement
of the level of intelligence, how to efficiently and accurately predict the future trajectory
of the ship has become a key issue to ensure the safety of maritime traffic and optimize
the scheduling of shipping routes. This paper focuses on the processing and modeling
of AIS trajectory data, proposes and constructs an MSCNNôÙTransformer model based on
the fusion structure of multiôÙscale convolution and Transformer to predict the ship trajecôÙ
tory in singleôÙstep and multiôÙstep, and systematically compares and evaluates it with the
traditional timeôÙseries model and the main conclusions are as follows:
(1) A trajectory coding scheme combining spatial grid and feature embedding is proposed.
Aiming at the sparsity and time series imbalance of AIS trajectory data, this paper introôÙ
duces the trajectory cleaning and spatial projection mechanism, combines grid coding
and physical feature extraction, and uniformly converts the shipãs historical trajectories
into structured sequences, which provides a normalized feature base for the subsequent
model input.
(2) The structure of MSCNNôÙTransformer prediction model is built. The model extracts
local spatioôÙtemporal features through multiôÙscale convolution, and introduces a multiôÙ
attention mechanism to capture longôÙterm dependencies, which realizes the synergistic
modeling of local and global information. Among multiple timeôÙseries prediction models,
MSCNNôÙTransformer shows strong advantages in both prediction accuracy and stability,
especially maintaining low prediction errors in complex segments such as sharp turns and
sudden speed changes.
(3) A complete model evaluation system and visualization comparison framework are conôÙ
structed. Combining the singleôÙstep and multiôÙstep prediction results, this paper comôÙ
prehensively analyzes the prediction performance of the six models in terms of multiôÙ
ple dimensions, such as MSE, MAE and other error indicators, trajectory offset graphs,
speed heading graphs, and prediction error over time graphs. The experiments show
that MSCNNôÙTransformer not only has lower average error and error fluctuation range,
but also has better error control ability and time robustness in multiôÙstep prediction.

6.1.1
Outlook
On the basis of this study, there are still a number of subsequent directions worth digging
deeper into.
The first is the data aspect. With the continuous accumulation of AIS data, the number of
ship trajectory points grows exponentially, and how to control the computational volume
while maintaining the data accuracy is an issue that must be considered when the subseôÙ
quent model is applied on the ground. On the other hand, the existing model mainly uses
singleôÙmodal data such as trajectory, speed and heading, and in the future, we can try
to introduce multiôÙmodal information (such as meteorology, hydrography, radar images,
port announcements, etc.) to further enhance the modelãs ability to perceive the complex
scene through information fusion, and to improve the robustness of the prediction.
The second is the further improvement of the model structure. Although the MSCNNôÙ
Transformer model proposed in this study performs well in a number of indicators, there
is still room for optimization. In the future, more efficient attention mechanisms, residual
structures or lightweight neural networks can be introduced to reduce the inference cost
while guaranteeing the accuracy and provide the possibility of practical deployment of the
model.
The third is the time scale refinement analysis. The current research focuses on the preôÙ
diction under fixed time interval, but in practical application, ships may face the demand of
dynamic interval and multiôÙscale decisionôÙmaking. In the future, we can consider shifting
from ãcontinuous predictionã to ãtimeôÙadaptive predictionã to explore the optimal modeling
strategy under different prediction durations and frequencies.
