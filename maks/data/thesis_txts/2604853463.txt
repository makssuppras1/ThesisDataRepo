Abstract 
A series of laboratory experiments were performed to investigate the swapping in the marine environment using a high-
pressure cell followed by gas chromatography analysis. Methane hydrate was formed using coarse/fine-grained 
unconsolidated silica sand in the presence of the surfactant SDS, the amino acid L-Methionine as kinetic promoters and low 
dosage alcohol as inhibitor Methanol; in different concentrations and saturation. The percentage of methane hydrate which 
was established has been quantified and showed that kinetic promoters do not affect the hydrate saturation level as it is 
diminished by inhibitor presence, but only changes the formation kinetics. The hydrate saturation was positively correlated 
with the growth kinetics which for higher induction times, higher gas uptake was observed as higher hydrate saturation as 
well, which is controlled mainly from the saturation degree of the porous medium together with the surface chemical activity 
at the gas-liquid interphase. 
CH4-hydrate was formed from gaseous CH4 and in the presence of small concentrations of different surface-active chemicals 
to mimic the hydrate reservoir. Methane hydrate formation was followed by a swapping procedure applied to the already 
formed methane hydrate by injecting pure CO2. Swapping leads to the creation of mixed hydrates as well as pure CO2 
hydrate besides existing methane hydrate. Pure CO2 was injected into this reservoir to initiate the CH4-CO2 exchange 
scenario and pressure-temperature with chromatography analysis were carried out. The CH4/CO2 mixed hydrate formed 
under marine reservoir conditions between CH4-hydrate and CO2 interface. After a swapping period of 24 hours, a gas 
analysis was carried out to measure the change in CO2 and CH4 concentration in the vapor phase at the start of injection and 
after the soaking period to calculate the number of liberated moles of CH4 and stored CO2. The effect of surface-active 
chemical change in concentration, initial hydrate saturation on methane recovery, and CO2 storage potential was also 
analyzed. CO2 injection into natural gas hydrate reservoirs is proposed as a novel and environmentally friendly technique 
to produce natural gas with simultaneous CO2 storage where disturbance of geological settings is not an issue. This method 
is limited by the mass transport barrier created by hydrate film formation at the liquid-gas contact area. The very low CO2 
diffusivity through hydrate film formed at this interface causes a delay in CH4-CO2 replacement and leads to low 
replacement rates and CH4 recovery. The results showed that 5 wt. % Methanol was the best-performing chemical among 
selected ones to enhance CH4 production and CO2 storage efficiency, as it enhanced gas diffusion through hydrate film by 
acting as an anti-agglomerant in the pure CO2 hydrate layer, as well as improving the driving force between the CH4/CO2 
hydrate phase boundary. The presence of a low concentration of methanol also reduced the risk of hydrate reformation 
compared with SDS and L- Methionine. A low saturation degree favors this exchange scenario since the grain-coating 
hydrate morphology favors the replacement in the interphase due to the higher specific surface area. The elevated 
temperature favors the replacement process by providing a higher driving force in the system due to the higher pressure 
difference between the bulk hydrates equilibrium pressure difference. Additional CH4 production can be facilitated with 
stepwise pressure reduction below pure CH4-hydrate equilibrium pressure and above CO2-hydrate equilibrium, such that 
methane hydrate dissociation leads to CO2 hydrate reformation released from mixed hydrate dissociation. In the presence 
of methanol, the impact of the anti-agglomeration effect during induced hydrate reformation by depressurization results in 
higher CH4 recovery compared to promoter presence. 
For the fine porous medium, the hydrate saturation specifies the surface area of the hydrate that controls the gas release and 
the dissociation rate.  For a higher specific surface area as a result of a higher induced hydrate saturation, the dissociation 
metrics are higher. The grain-coating hydrate morphology is porous due to the water-to-hydrate volume expansion that leads 
to imperfections in the micro-pore scale. Trapped gas and liquid from increased capillary forces inside these imperfections 
together with promoter presence leads to enhanced hydrate formation. The dissociation is an endothermic phenomenon that 
can induce secondary hydrate nucleation as it absorbs energy from the surroundings. Therefore, promoted reformation from 
the released gas trapped in the micropore-scale imperfections results in the mass transfer limit of the gas which is seen as 
lower dissociation metrics, while inhibited reformation by low dosage methanol leads to higher dissociation metrics.

It is generally known regarding the practices of general medicine, which have been expanded from industrial to developing 
nations, life expectancy has been significantly increased from the early 50’s. Economic development in the world leads to 
large demographic shifts for poorer regions. [1] This statistic trend is illustrated from population growth data comparison 
among low-income countries and high-income countries in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1. Population growth prospects from 1950 to 2100 of high-income countries (left) compared to low-income countries (right) projected growth. 
Reproduced from Parant A.(2019)[2]. 
 
1.1.1 Population Growth Trend 
 
The world population is expected to grow up to 10 billion within 2050 regarding Figure 2, with contribution mainly from 
the less developed countries in the African and the Indian continent. [3] Already populous countries, which are in transition 
from developing countries to industrial ones, through manufacturing and to some extent due to human knowledge the human 
growth is driven. Other geographic locations such as the Middle East and Southwest Asia majorly contribute to this 
incremental trend, opposite to the stable trend of the population increase among many western countries, excluding the 
United States of America, Mexico and Brazil. [2]

1.1.2 Energy Sources and Consumption Statistics 
 
A surge in the world population combined with a growing middle class is expected to lead to stepped-up energy 
consumption. 
 
Figure 3. World energy consumption from 2010 to 2050. Reproduced from U. S. E. I. Administration (2019) [4]. 
As it is comprehended from Figure 3, world energy consumption rises nearly 50% between 2018 and 2050, with almost all 
of the increase occurring in non-OECD countries. World energy consumption totals 911 quadrillion British thermal units 
(Btu) in 2050, which 287 quadrillion Btu consumed by Organization of Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) 
countries, while 624 quadrillion BTU is the consumption in the non-OECD countries. China and India are considered the 
primary contributors to future growth in the world energy demand, together with countries from Africa and the Middle East 
due to fast-paced-population growth with access to domestic resources, illustrated in Figure 4, [4]. 
 
Figure 4. Regional energy consumption projection of non-OECD. Reproduced from U. S. E. I. Administration (2019)[4]. 
The current production of fossil fuels such as coal, oil and gas follows a constant declining trend for the last decades, 
contrary to the continuously up-trending renewable energy sources such as wind, solar, hydro, geothermal, and nuclear. 
However, the producible amount, with these sources and the known new technologies, is far below the energy demand

CO2 per unit of produced energy, replacement of the coal with natural gas can be a path to follow for CO2 increasing 
emissions mitigation. Conventional methods are used to produce most of the natural gas demand, while unconventional 
methods start to gain prominence in recent years. Based on the current consumption rate, an average estimate of the recovery 
from both conventional and unconventional gas could cover the energy demand for above 200 years. Among the known 
unconventional methods of gas exploration, such as tight gas and shale gas, a new rising form is on the rise and lately 
studied; namely Natural Gas Hydrates (NGH)[6]. 
 
1.2 Natural Gas Hydrates Fundamentals 
 
Natural Gas Hydrates, known also as clathrate hydrates, are non-stoichiometric ice-like crystallized natural gas resources 
which have been stable for million years. They exist usually in permafrost regions or deep ocean sediments. Their diverse 
geographical distribution, along with their existence in vast amount reserves, compared with the amount both of 
conventional and unconventional resources, marks them as an interesting research topic.[7] Hydrates are formed by a 
combination of water molecules and low molecular weight gases under low-temperature and high-pressure conditions. Light 
gases such as methane (CH4), ethane (C2H6), carbon dioxide (CO2), and nitrogen (N2) can be hydrated with water.[8] It is 
==PAGE:15==
 
4 
 
conservatively estimated that the reserves of NGH are double of all fossil fuel sources [9]. Hydrates are quite a promising 
research topic in many industrial applications, such as natural gas storage and transportation and carbon capture, due to their 
good storage capacity, aiming to the improvement of world energy supplies along with their expanded application in 
industry. The United States, China, Japan and Russia have mainly contributed with field tests, achieving progress in the 
exploitation of hydrates[10], while various research groups have been focusing on different topics within the NGH study 
field. Thermodynamic and kinetic properties of NGH in different porous systems, but also their production and 
environmental impact have been the focus in this research field throughout the years.[11] 
1.2.1 Hydrates Composition and Structure 
 
Hydrates’ structure can be described as ice-like solids. The water molecule acting as the host lattice encapsulates the guest 
species of the gas molecule. The stability of the hydrate structure depends on the size fit amongst the gas molecule and the 
lattice. Three main forms of different lattice cavity diameter are existing: cubic structure I (sI), cubic structure II (sII), and 
the hexagonal structure (sH).[12]

The hydrate structure is dependent on the guest size molecule which occupies the water cage cavities. Regarding the stable

while for a gas hydrate formed by a mixture, the largest molecule will occupy the largest cages, and accordingly the smaller 
molecules will occupy the smaller cages.[14] As each structure consists of guest species occupying the water cage cavities, 
the ratio of the host species molecule to the guest molecule represents the hydration number. This is a number indicating 
how many water molecules are required to encapsulate in their cage one gas molecule. From the aforementioned structures, 
sI has small to large cage ratio of 1/3, while the sII ratio is equal to 2. The guest species are typically categorized based on 
their chemical characteristics, with the group of hydrophobic non-polar molecules to be the most prominent. CO2 is one of 
the exceptions with water-soluble acid gas behavior.[15] 
 
1.3 Formation of Gas Hydrates 
 
Natural gas hydrates can be formed either biogenically either under thermodynamic conditions by hydrocarbon gas forms. 
Biogenic hydrates are emerging from methane generating microbes. Natural gases, whose origin is restrained by 
thermogenic processes, are formed by the decomposition of the organic matter in the sediments. Therefore, thermogenic 
natural gas hydrates are expected to have higher hydrocarbon content with ethane, propane whereas biogenic hydrates will 
have higher methane purity. A favorable set of conditions needs to be met for gas hydrate formation, most often under high 
pressure and low temperature. Under these circumstances, a host lattice of the water molecule and the guest gas molecule 
will coexist and may lead to the formation of gas hydrate. Mixtures of methane and ethane form sI structure gas hydrates 
with stability at higher pressures and lower temperatures, found at higher depths than pure hydrates. The most prominent 
gas hydrate structures are sI and sII, while sH structures have been newly discovered with at least three new clathrate hydrate 
structures. Nitrogen and oxygen exhibit double occupancy of the large cavity in sII. Multiple occupancies of water cavities, 
by hydrogen and methane, have been confirmed. A complex structure consisting of stacks of sH and sII has been explored, 
while there are reported also tetragonal and trigonal hydrate structures.[16] 
 
1.3.1 Nucleation and Growth of Gas Hydrates 
 
The formation of gas hydrates can be generally subdivided into three stages; one stage named nucleation, the second one 
known as induction time, and the third named as growth. Nucleation is occurring when the liquid phase is saturated and the 
first crystal is formed, however, it is not easily distinguished in an experimental system since it is a random micro-scale 
process, not detectable macroscopically. Subsequently, the induction time follows, and this is identified with a higher gas 
consumption rate as it is depicted in Figure 7. Agglomeration, where liquid phase molecules and gas molecules form clusters 
via a stochastic process initiates the nucleation stage when the crystal reaches a critical size. This stage is thermodynamically 
stable even though it does not form a solid stable hydrate. More precisely, this is comprehended more like a metastable 
period where there is an interval stability range instead of an absolute stability point, resulting in a stochastic occurrence of 
this stage relative to the system.[17] The end of the nucleation period along with the induction time is marked when the

where the density of gas molecules is higher than in the gas-free phase. With time the gas consumption rate will decrease 
and finally stop due to lack of gas or liquid phase.[16]

1.3.2 Gas Hydrates Formation Kinetics 
 
An important factor in hydrate formation kinetics is the liquid phase. The history of the liquid phase is known as the memory 
effect and this is correlated with a liquid molecule previously engaged in a hydrate’s crystal lattice. There is a tendency for 
faster engagement in the lattice structure from this kind of molecules causing a faster formation.[18] The way that liquid 
droplets entrapped into crystalized solid hydrate molecule are via mass transfer phenomena of gas molecules from the vapor-
liquid interphase to the center of the droplet. Thus, the liquid droplet is exposed to the gas from the interphase to the center. 
The dissolved gas molecules in the liquid phase can be diffused back out from the liquid droplet due to heat transfer 
phenomena as the removal of residual heat. Therefore, a previously engaged liquid droplet changing to crystallized gas 
tends to help the nucleation since the mass transport is better due to better water activity, and the heat transfer from the 
droplet is accelerated.[19]  
 
1.4 Gas Hydrates Dissociation 
 
The stability of hydrates is related to the interfacial area between liquid and gas, water composition, gas composition, the 
mass, and the heat transport properties between phases except for the pressure and temperature conditions. To change the 
solid hydrate molecule into the initial separate gaseous and liquid state the energy provided needs to be high enough since 
when the lattice is formed energy is released as an exothermic reaction, resulting in the lowest possible free energy state. 
The hydrogen bonds created during formation will be destroyed by the energy in the endothermic reaction. The kinetic 
decomposition depends on the same factors for the stability or the formation.[20]

1.5 Inhibitors and Promoters 
 
Some chemicals are known to inhibit the formation of hydrates while others are known to promote hydrate formation. Pure 
water is considered always as a reference, while the other liquids are compared to pure water behavior. Inhibitors include 
saline water, alcohols, and glycols. Inhibitors are utilized for flow assurance in the fields by avoidance of hydrates formation 
by shifting the equilibrium curve to operational pressure and temperatures where no formation occurs.[21] The salts lower 
the solubility of the gas in the liquid phase and by Coulomb forces created by water dipoles, resulting in more difficult 
engagement of water to hydrate lattice. Alcohols and glycols work similarly, but there is a competence for the hydrogen 
bonding of the hydroxyl group and the water molecules. 
Promoters include chemicals such as sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) and amino acids (AA). SDS increases the hydrate 
nucleation rate as it reduces the interfacial tension between hydrate and liquid, as well as the hydrate growth rate by 
increasing the contact surface area of hydrate and the gas-liquid interphase area. Similar behavior display amino acids with 
the advantage of being greener chemicals.[22]

1.6.2 Production by Dissociation 
 
Production of methane trapped in hydrate structures can occur either by the dissolution of NGH or with an injection of a 
more thermodynamically stable hydrate former that will allow the replacement process with CH4. Pressure, temperature, 
and composition are the factors defining the hydrate stability. In case that the reservoir conditions alter and move out the 
stability zone by crossing the equilibrium line, gas recovery via dissociation is possible[27], as presented in Figure 11. Gas 
can be recovered from gas hydrates by inducing dissociation with depressurization, thermal stimulation, and thermodynamic 
inhibition. Depressurization requires the removal of all reservoir fluids, thermal stimulation is conducted by hot water/steam 
injection, in situ combustion or radiation and chemical stimulation can be performed by alcohols, glycols, and salts 
respectively. A combination of the aforementioned methods is generally the case for the real production strategy scenarios 
to utilize a combined effect of the methods[28]. Based on simulation studies is shown that depressurization is the most 
promising technique considering economics as well. The addition of heat demands energy production and inhibitors 
injection is increasing the cost. At the top of all these drawbacks, the produced water and sand are extremely high, and with 
a high risk of geomechanical losses[29].

1.7 Hydrate Formation in a Porous Media 
Gibb’s phase rule has many degrees of freedom that must be specified to achieve equilibrium in a hydrates system. In a 
system containing liquid water, gas, and hydrate, the number of phases in equilibrium can be several as a result of adsorbed 
phases on solid surfaces, such as hydrate crystals and the porous medium grains. For pressure and temperature known the 
system is over-determined and equilibrium cannot be reached. Hence, the hydrate in porous media will be under different 
semi-stable hydrate compositions[34]. 
The hydrate formation is also connected to water activity quantified by the chemical potential of water[35]. The water 
activity is reduced with adsorption onto hydrophilic surfaces and partial ordering. The capillary forces in porous media 
affect also the water activity by creating inhibition of hydrate nucleation compared to bulk medium, illustrated in Figure 14. 
Natural gas hydrate formed in the interstitial pore space of a porous medium is different from those of bulk hydrates. 
Depression in water activity is due to higher capillary forces and the presence of dissolved anions/cations occurring from 
salts. Consequently, the hydrate equilibrium (Hydrate-Liquid-Vapor) will vary as a function of porous sediment properties. 
The particle size of the porous medium is the determining factor together with the correlated properties for the hydrate 
equilibrium. Pore size defines the porosity, intrinsic and relative permeability, capillary forces, and specific surface area 
(SSA) of the grains. It is well known that fine-grained media are dominated by higher capillary forces and bigger SSA 
==PAGE:23==
 
12 
 
resulting in lower permeabilities. Hence, the hydrate equilibrium will result in a different hydrate formation/dissociation 
pattern governed by particle size together with water content in the pore space[36]. 
 
 
Figure 14. Illustration of the different water layers in pore space with water the wetting phase and bounded water layer concerning the water activity. 
Reproduced by Clennell (2006)[35]. 
The thermodynamic effect of undercooled water inside a porous system, unrelated to supercooling which is as a consequence 
of nucleation kinetics, is observed. Water tends to freeze at a lower temperature than in bulk conditions due to the curved 
ice-water interfaces. Thus, the free energy of pore ice is increased relative to bulk ice and this effect is crucial for very fine-
grained porous media[33]. 
The mobile phases in a porous medium will be rearranged into an equilibrium system with the minimum surface energy in 
various local spots where the nucleation of hydrate will occur. Therefore, the localities may interact with neighboring 
localities causing a minimum surface energy rearrangement. These local stable state phases may occur in a discontinuous 
way affecting the hydrate saturation and phase distribution as a function of capillary pressure, wettability, initial phase 
saturations, and saturation history due to the hysteresis phenomenon[37]. In the presence of ice in porous media, most grains 
are coated with a thin film of water that remains unfrozen[38]. 
1.7.1 Hydrate Growth Pattern in a Porous Media 
There are different theories concerning the hydrate growth pattern in a porous system, based on the interactions among 
hydrate and an unconsolidated packing of mineral grains, as the systems investigated in this thesis. The hydrate can form at 
the grain contacts and have a cementing behavior or in the center of the pores, partially supporting the medium[39]. 
Experiments with synthetic porous media by glass micromodels indicated that hydrate preferably grows in the center of the 
pores rather than on grain surfaces for water wet grains. The hydrate forms preferentially first at the surface of the hydrate 
reactor and then gradually grows inward. The hydrate tends to cement the glass beads together and then occupy the pore 
gradually. This is because the wall of the vessel reaches the required formation temperature first. Furthermore, the surface 
of the reactor can provide the over-critical dimension crystal nucleus, which can overcome the energy of the liquid interface. 
When the hydrate crystal nucleus of over-critical dimension forms, the surface contact process turns into the diffusion 
process of the hydrate’s thin interphase membrane[40]. Some NMR studies of hydrate growth similar to deep-sea 
environments suggest that the growth of hydrate is mainly pore-filling[41].

promoted in bigger pores. The inhibition of the hydrate growth in the smaller pores is based on the capillary pressure effect

1.7.2 Conditions for Growth Pattern in a Porous Media 
The hydrate distribution when the hydrate is partially saturating a porous media results in the reduction of effective porosity 
and relative permeability of the fluids as well. Hydrate distribution is a function of initial water distribution in a porous 
system. It is generally suggested that a more even hydrate distribution can be obtained after a second hydrate formation. 
This is a result of improved chemical active water distribution after the dissociation of hydrate which offers more evenly 
distributed nucleation sites for hydrate formation. 
Gas hydrates in a porous media formed within two conditions of the system. The excess water systems and the excess gas 
systems. The excess gas system is utilized in this thesis for the conducted experiments. An excess gas system is governed 
by water availability. In a porous hydrate system with excess gas, there us excess water which is, however, unavailable for 
hydrate formation. This is due to water-wet grain surfaces with bounded unavailable water coating the grains. Furthermore, 
reactive excess water may be present in an excess gas system when no contact with the gas is allowed due to capillarities of 
the small pores[35]. Hence, it is implied that the kinetics of the hydrate formation and the final hydrate saturation is closely 
correlated to the initial water distribution. The rate of hydrate formation is expected lower for a smaller gas-water interfacial 
area corresponding to heterogeneous water distribution.

transfer scenarios in the pore space network. Two mechanisms are supporting the mass transfer scenarios during hydrate

2. Literature Review  
In this chapter, several experimental and theoretical studies will be presented focusing on methane recovery from gas 
hydrates by CO2 exchange and the effect of excess water on hydrate formation, CO2 exchange, and dissociation will be 
covered. Moreover, the effect of depressurization as a production technique will be discussed. 
2.1 Depressurization 
NGH are cage-like crystalline compounds formed by the van der Waals-forced inclusion of natural gas molecules in 
hydrogen-bonded water molecules under high-pressure and low-temperature conditions [29][45]. NGH are considered as 
potential clean energy in the future with up to 230 gas hydrate deposits, distributed evenly across the world in marine or 
permafrost environment. These deposits remain untouched and hold a reserve estimated to be twice the known fossil fuel 
available[9], [24], [46]. To produce methane from a gas hydrate reservoir, several techniques have been suggested including 
depressurization[47], thermal stimulation[48], and chemical inhibitor injection[49]. In comparison, depressurization is 
considered as the most cost-effective method to be commercially applied. Many depressurization techniques are suggested 
to optimize gas production, including constant rate depressurization [50], multistage depressurization [51], cyclic 
depressurization [52],[53], slow stepwise depressurization[54],[55], and depressurization combined with gas injection 
[56],[57]. However, thermal stimulation has significant heat loss, and depressurization may induce hydrate reformation 
during the exploitation process and plug the production pipeline. Further, thermodynamic inhibitor stimulation by methanol 
(MeOH) or glycol may pollute the environment [7]. As all of the above production techniques are based on the 
decomposition of methane hydrate, catastrophic sediment failures can be triggered with an additional risk of methane release 
to the atmosphere, accelerating the greenhouse effect due to rapid hydrate decomposition. Methane is considered a potent 
greenhouse gas, absorbing infrared radiation about 25 times more efficiently than carbon dioxide [58], [59]. Thus, there is 
a need to explore more viable production technologies, and carbon dioxide sequestration coupled with methane recovery 
may be economically attractive with the advantage of greener technology. 
Studies on operations of depressurization with systems saturated with hydrate, liquid water, and gas suggest that the hydrate 
decomposition and recovery behaviors are strongly correlated to the ice formation phenomenon as a function of production 
pressure, depressurization rate, saturation, physical properties of the sediment, and ice formatted location [60], [61]. Frozen 
gas hydrate deposits are free of water whereby the hydrates can be sustained for a long period even under equilibrium 
conditions. This special phenomenon is known as the self-preservation effect due to ice hindering of hydrate 
dissociation[62], [63]. The underlying mechanism of self-preservation is that there is an ice layer covering gas hydrates that 
can prevent the diffusion of the gas hydrate molecules, controlling further the subsequent dissociation of hydrate [63]–[65].  
2.1 CH4-CO2 Exchange 
Utilization of CO2 in natural gas hydrate production by CH4-CO2 rf has been introduced in the 1980s suggesting methane 
recovery from hydrate reservoir and simultaneous CO2 sequestration in the formation of CO2 hydrate [66], [67]. In 2012, 
an in situ gas hydrate exploitation field test based on the CH4-CO2 exchange was successfully performed on the Ignik Sikumi 
field. A binary CO2 gas mixture was injected into the hydrate-bearing sandstone formation and CH4 was produced, while 
CO2 was sequestered in the formation. This suggests the feasibility and commercial viability of the swapping method for 
simultaneous methane recovery and carbon dioxide capture [68], [69]. Subsequently, producing CH4 from NGHs by gas 
swapping has gained attention as an important energy resource together with the mitigation of greenhouse gas, CO2, in the 
atmosphere, one of the major global environmental issues that concern humans, while the stability of the formation is not 
disturbed [70], [71]. 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
16 
 
Apart from promising carbon-neutral CH4 production, the process is sustainable in terms of heat requirement, as the heat 
released for CO2 hydrate formation is 57.98 kJ/mol while the heat required for CH4 dissociation is 54.49 kJ/mol [72]. This 
approach is based on phase equilibria among CH4 and CO2 hydrates. CO2 hydrate is more stable than the CH4 hydrate under 
the thermodynamic conditions under which most hydrate deposits exist. CO2 as the preferred guest species in hydrate 
enclathration was experimentally demonstrated by Ohgaki [67] in a mixed CH4-CO2 hydrate system at 280 K. Gibb’s free 
energy for the exchange reaction is negative, and thus, the thermodynamic feasibility of the spontaneous replacement 
reaction of CH4 hydrate by CO2 is justified [73]. 
 
Methane recovery efficiency was found to be driven by guest species fugacity, where the use of liquid CO2 leads to higher 
methane production [30],[31]. The replacement efficiency was studied on the CH4-CO2 process utilizing liquid CO2 and 
proposed as a more suitable alternative than gaseous CO2 [31], [74], [76]–[78], while similar approaches with CO2 emulsions 
showed the most favorable efficiency despite the uncertainties about optimal CO2 emulsion conditions [79]. Stanwix et al. 
[80], reviewed pure CO2 replacement experiments reporting up to 50% CH4 recovery. The replacement efficiency below 
the freezing point is lower due to the ice layer formation during the replacement reaction, resulting in further hindering of 
gas diffusion [78], [81], [82]. 
 
2.3 Limitations of CH4-CO2 Exchange  
The proposed method can be limited by the contact area between injected CO2 and CH4 hydrate. Another limitation is met 
under excess water conditions, whereby the injected gas will form a hydrate with water before the replacement reaction 
occurs, limiting the reaction rate. The presence of this outer layer from CO2 hydrate formation in the gas-liquid interface 
acts as a diffusion barrier, hindering the mass transfer of guest species and water from further replacement reactions [83], 
[84]. 
 
Surfactants were also reported to reduce the surface tension at the gas-liquid interphase, which improves the mass transfer 
during hydrate formation[85]–[87]. Hence, considering the improvement of the porous system due to surfactant presence 
on mass and heat transfer in the hydrate formation process, many studies focus more on the improved interaction among 
porous media properties and surfactant in the hydrate formation rate[88]–[90]. Amino acids are greener potential alternatives 
to currently used kinetic promoters such as surfactants. Most amino acids are soluble in water, non-toxic in nature, and bio-
friendly. The utilization of amino acids to gas hydrate formation is very recent, and their behavior is not well understood 
[91],[92]. Recent research has shown that hydrophobic amino acids like L-Methionine have similar promotion capabilities 
as SDS at a similar concentration range of 500-2000 ppm [91]. 
 
Alcohols are known to work as hydrate inhibitors to prevent hydrate formation during oil and gas pipeline transportation. 
The mechanism for this inhibition is based on the change in chemical potential of water, due to the disruption of the water 
hydrogen bonding network in the presence of the strong dipole moment of the alcohol hydroxyl group. Due to the strong 
polar nature of the alcohol methyl group, they have a strong affinity towards water, thus leaving less active water for gas 
hydrate formation. However, methanol is reported to have an additional effect on the surface tension of a gas-liquid 
interface, where for a small amount of methanol in water, a molecular dynamics study displayed a reduction in surface 
tension [93]. Some studies report that methanol acts as a methane hydrate promoter increasing hydrate stabilization when 
used at lower concentrations [94]. Propane gas formation was found to be promoted in the presence of low concentration 
(0.25-1.00 wt.%) methanol at 253K [95]. Another prospect is alcohol cosurfactants as an aid in micro-emulsion formation 
resulting in lower interfacial tension, where MeOH is proposed to be effective. York et al. [96] showed that small amounts 
of MeOH cosurfactants may affect hydrate anti-agglomeration. Therefore, the presence of MeOH may lead to synergetic 
effects for less CO2 hydrate formation with excess water as well as a more efficient replacement process by the disruption 
of the diffusion barrier. However, considering the low rate of the CH4-CO2 replacement process which results in low 
==PAGE:28==
 
17 
 
exchange efficiency, it can be considered as a not commercial and viable method. Thus, there is a need for further 
investigation of the recovery in the replacement process with new approaches to exceed the limitations of the method. A 
new approach was suggested as a combination of CH4-CO2 replacement with the addition of hydrate thermodynamic 
inhibitors and alcohols, accelerating the decomposition of the methane hydrate [97]. 
2.4 Study Objectives  
This study investigates the chemical enhanced swapping in the marine environment using a high-pressure cell (HPC) 
followed by gas chromatography (GC). Methane hydrate will be formed using various sizes of unconsolidated silica sand 
in the presence of the surfactant SDS, the amino acid L-methionine and low dosage alcohol inhibitor Methanol; in different 
concentrations and saturation. The percentage of methane hydrate established will be quantified to evaluate the chemical 
effect. Methane hydrate formation will be followed by a swapping procedure applied to the already formed methane hydrate 
by injecting pure CO2. Swapping would lead to the creation of mixed hydrates as well as pure CO2 hydrate besides existing 
methane hydrate. Additional methane production will be investigated with stepwise pressure reduction between methane 
and CO2 equilibrium pressure. Gas samples both after swapping and stepwise pressure reduction technique will be collected 
for GC analysis, to study the molar composition of CH4 & CO2 in the vapor phase. 
 
The objective of the study include:  
 
To study the effect of chemicals; surfactant (SDS), amino acid (L-methionine) and low dosage methanol 
concentration and its initial saturation volume during the CH4-hydrate formation in coarse-grained unconsolidated 
silica sand.   
 
To study the effect of chemicals; surfactant (SDS), amino acid (L-methionine) and low dosage methanol 
concentration and its initial saturation volume during the hydrate swapping in unconsolidated silica sand.   
 
To study the temperature effect of pure CO2 gas chemical assisted swapping on methane production and CO2 
storage. 
 
To study the stepwise pressure reduction technique after swapping on methane production and additional CO2 
storage. 
 
To study the effect of different pore size silica sand along with the chemical effect on gas hydrate saturation and its 
dissociation.

3. Materials and Methods  
The laboratory work, data acquisition and analysis have been conducted at the Centre of Energy Resources Engineering at 
the Technical University of Denmark. The utilization of the experimental materials, setups, processes as well as, methods 
are described thoroughly in this chapter. It should be noted, that the main experimental study is focused on the effect of the 
porous medium grain size on the hydrates formation, dissociation, as well as in the swapping process followed by stepwise 
depressurization. In this study, it is investigated the methane hydrate formation and CH4-CO2 exchange in unconsolidated 
porous media with or without hydrate former. CO2 rich gas injection into natural gas hydrate reservoir is proposed as a 
novel technique to produce natural gas and simultaneously storage CO2 without disturbing the geological settings. This 
method is limited by the mass transport barrier created by hydrate film formed at the liquid-gas interface. The very low CO2 
diffusivity through these films causes a delay in the CH4-CO2 exchange. Few simulation studies in the literature suggest 
that adding a small concentration of surface-active chemicals can improve the diffusivity through hydrate film. It is 
important to investigate this phenomenon through experiments to check whether methane recovery and CO2 storage 
efficiency can be improved.

experimental procedure helps to acquire an overview of the experimental steps as it is presented in Figure 19 below.

3.5.1 Sand Pack and Hydrate Reactor Preparation 
 
Prior to the start of each experiment, the HPC is cleaned initially with deionized water, then with pure ethanol, and lastly 
dried with laboratory air. Afterward, it is covered with dry clean laboratory tissue to keep it clean and dry. Meanwhile, the 
sand that needs to be used is placed to a heating oven at 90oC for at least 24 hours before the start of the experiment, to 
ensure no moisture or contaminants before each experimental run. When the sand was cooled off at room temperature, the 
desired amount of the sand was weighted with a Mettler Toledo laboratory scale with an accuracy of ±5 g. Followingly, the 
desired amount of promoter solution is weighted after its density measurement with a densitometer to achieve the targeted 
saturation. The density of the solution is required for determining the solution weight that must be added and mixed with 
the sand pack. After mixing well the sand with the promoter solution in a big plastic basin, the saturated sand pack is placed 
carefully not to lose a big portion of sand and solution in the hydrate reactor. Then, the promoter saturated porous system is 
pressed with a flat metal sheet to achieve a flat top surface. This aims for sand pack height measurements, quantifying 
volume values needed for saturations and gas mole quantifications. Lastly, prior to the safe closure of the HPC, the porous 
==PAGE:33==
 
22 
 
saturated medium screenshot was used for comparing the sand form after every experiment, as it is expected to differ from 
the hydrate experiment. 
3.5.2 Hydrate Formation Procedure 
 
This section describes the experimental procedures for primary methane hydrate formation and the secondary methane 
formation after dissociation, aiming to create more uniformly distributed hydrate. All the hydrate formation experiments 
under this study occurred in an excess methane gas system with the promoter solution as the limiting phase. 
3.5.2.1 Primary Hydrate Formation Procedure 
 
Following the preparation standard operation procedure, as described in section 3.5.1, the hydrate reactor is closed and 
vacuumed from the outlet valve for some minutes to ensure no presence of air inside the porous-promoter system. Afterward, 
the CH4 gas canister is connected to the HP-cell’s inlet valve for methane injection. Prior to CH4 injection, the system is 
purged two times with the methane gas. For the purging, the outlet valve is closed and the system is pressurized to low 
pressure, approximately 10 bar. Then, the inlet valve is closed and the outlet valve is opened to reach atmospheric pressure 
at ambient temperature. This procedure ensures the absence of air in the reactor, aiming to have pure consistency of the 
desired methane excess gas environment when starting the primary hydrate formation. Followingly, the reactor was injected 
from the inlet valve with pressurized CH4 to the experimental pressure at the range of 85 bar at 25oC. Then, the inlet valve 
was closed, the reactor was disconnected from the gas cylinder and isolated with no interaction for some hours, for the 
methane gas get dissolved in the promoter liquid phase. To form hydrates in the system, the cooler temperature was lowered 
to reach the CH4 stability zone. The stability conditions for each experiment were determined with the aid of CSMGem 
knowing the system saturation. Hydrate formation started spontaneously when the temperature was brought towards gas 
hydrate equilibrium conditions and was detected as an abrupt decrease in pressure profile from a sudden increase in CH4 
consumption. Moreover, the temperature spiked during hydrate formation as it is an exothermic reaction. 
3.5.2.2 Hydrate Dissociation Procedure 
 
The primary hydrate formation has typically lasted 24-48 hours until no visible changes could be observed in pressure and 
temperature profiles. The system was then reheated at 25oC to induce the hydrate dissociation in an isochoric environment. 
As the temperature increased, the system goes above the thermodynamic hydrate stability zone and starts to dissociate. The 
pressure returns to the initial range before the start of the first cooling cycle suggesting that methane is released during the 
dissociation allowing the pressure to return to its initial range. 
3.5.2.3 Secondary Hydrate Formation Procedure 
 
After the first cooling-heating cycle of the system, it is cooled down again within the hydrate stability zone, inducing 
secondary hydrate formation in the system. This step helps to create more uniformly distributed hydrate saturations due to 
the hydrates’ memory effect with the promoter solution in the porous system since the induction time in the reformation of 
the same structure hydrate varies. Consequently, the hydrates start to nucleate faster and in more spots starting from the 
previous ones. Another factor that may contribute to higher and more uniform hydrate saturation is the better free gas 
dissolution in the liquid phase. 
 
 
==PAGE:34==
 
23 
 
3.5.3 CH4-CO2 Exchange Procedure 
 
When the methane hydrate formation has ceased, as there is no or low and slow CH4 gas consumption, then the pure CO2 
cylinder is connected in the experimental setup through the inlet valve, while the outlet valve is closed. As the methane 
hydrate formation stability pressure is known from the data recorder, the pure carbon dioxide is injected at higher pressure, 
ensuring its liquid state in the range of 60 bar at a constant temperature from the previous step, 1oC. To ensure that there is 
no remain CH4 excess gas in the system, the same logic flushing procedure is followed as described in the primary hydrate 
formation stage. Note that the removal of the free gas phase is performed above the range of the methane hydrates’ 
equilibrium pressure. Furthermore, after two times flushing a gas sample is collected from the outlet valve for gas 
chromatography analysis, helping to quantify the initial amount of moles and consistency in the free gas. Then, the outlet 
valve of the reactor is closed, while the inlet valve is disconnected from the gas cylinder. 
3.5.4 CH4-CO2 Swapping Stage 
 
Following the gas exchange procedure, the system is left for the swapping process among the two gases, at a constant 
temperature range of 1 degree Celsius. The CO2 tends to compete with CH4 in the sandy porous system under the presence 
of a promoter for hydrate formation. The diffusion is the most dominant force providing CO2 to the methane hydrate reaction 
sites, leading to dissociation of hydrates during the replacement process of CO2-CH4. All generated and recorded pressure 
and temperature data were recorded during the process. Under experimental conditions, the pressure decreases as the 
swapping reaction proceeds. When the pressure was stabilized for a long time (≈24 hours) the swapping reaction was 
assumed to be complete. 
3.5.5 Depressurization Stage 
 
Depressurization is following, aiming to increase further the concentration of CO2 in the CH4-CO2 mixed hydrate. This 
helps to investigate the possibility of safely methane production with carbon dioxide sequestrated in the sandy porous 
system, under the presence of promoter. To perform the depressurization, the outlet valve of the setup was opened, and after 
collecting a gas sample for mole fraction analysis by gas chromatography, the pressure was quickly reduced to a predefined 
value, keeping always the temperature of the circulating cooling fluid at 1oC. At this point, another gas sample was collected 
for better quantitative comparison between free gas consistencies after and before every depressurization step. In such a 
way, the CH4 recovery and CO2 storage can be quantified. Some of the experiments were conducted with fewer and bigger 
stepwise pressure reduction steps to investigate the pressure driving force effect on methane recovery and CO2 storage. 
Some of the experiments were performed with depressurization until all hydrates dissociated, while others were kept above 
CO2 hydrates’ equilibrium pressure. 
3.5.6 Self-Preservation Stage 
 
Hydrates remain stable like ice at atmospheric pressure and below zero temperature. This effect is known as the self-
preservation effect on hydrates. As the last step before terminating the experimental run, the system temperature is targeted 
at -2oC. When the pressure and temperature are stabilized, then the system is quickly depressurized and the reactor content 
is pictured with height measurement after opening. This stage helps to see the effect of hydrate formation on the porous 
system under the presence of a promoter. However, due to pre-followed steps hydrate might not be seen. This depends on 
the kind of sandy porous sediment besides promoter saturation, as well as the depressurization steps and its pressure levels. 
For instance, an experimental run with coarse sand, high methanol saturation, and stepwise depressurization above CO2 
hydrate equilibrium pressure, after opening did not display hydrate formed. Consequently, the self-preservation process is 
==PAGE:35==
 
24 
 
crucial to determine some qualitative conclusions about the explored methane production technique and the effect of the 
porous system, promoter, and its saturation. More specifically, experimental runs with a study on the effect of the sand 
under the presence of promoters on self-preservation were performed as well. For the sake of this, some experiments instead 
of opening after subzero cooling, they were remained closed after depressurization to atmospheric pressure level in the 
temperature of -2oC. After a predetermined waiting time, 12 hours for all this kind of runs, and GC analysis, the reactor was 
opened and the result was again pictured. In such a way, the recorded pressure differences and temperatures can provide the 
number of moles released during dissociation for the gas CO2-CH4 mixture consistency, as well as the dissociation behavior 
that can be studied.

4. Results and Discussion 
In this section, the experimental collected and calculated results will be presented and further discussed concerning the CH4 
hydrate formation both in coarse and fine sand and the CH4-CO2 swapping results in the hydrate-bearing coarse sediments 
followed by depressurization and self-preservation will be evaluated, as well. 
4.1 Chemical Assisted CH4 Production and CO2 Storage in Coarse Sediment 
A series of experiments were performed to study CH4-CO2 exchange with unconsolidated porous medium and hydrate 
formers, including alcohol (methanol, MeOH), the amino acid (L-methionine) and the surfactant (sodium dodecyl sulfate, 
SDS). The unconsolidated sediment used was silica sand with 0.9-1.6 mm particle size (Coarse Sand) to mimic methane 
hydrate in coarse sediments. A high-pressure cell was used to measure methane gas hydrate formation in porous medium 
followed by CO2 injection into the methane hydrate-bearing sediment. Experiments were performed in multiple steps 
identified below. At the start and end of each step, a gas sample was collected for gas chromatography (GC) analysis. GC 
analysis provided information about the moles of CH4 and CO2 gas in the vapor phase. The difference in the mole fraction 
of each gas in the vapor phase provides insights about the mechanisms involved in that phase. The effect of hydrate former 
correlated to the concentration and initial water saturation, change in temperature and hydrate saturation on CH4 recovery 
and CO2 storage was studied. 
4.1.1 Role of Chemical Hydrate Former on CH4 Hydrate Formation and CH4-CO2 Exchange 
Alcohols and salts act as hydrate inhibitors which cause a delay in hydrate formation. However, the addition of low methanol 
concentrations to water reduces the chemical potential of water through dilution as methyl dipole moments disrupt the 
hydrogen bonding. The dipole moment of methanol comes from a high charge on the hydroxyl group and a smaller charge 
on the methyl group. The low charge of the methyl group and its large size result in a surfactant-like behavior when the 
aqueous phase is exposed to the hydrate former. This behavior hinders the formation of hydrate film in the interfacial region, 
broadens the interface between water and former contact region and thus reduces the interfacial energy between CH4 and 
the water. Hence, the transport of methane into the water phase is increased, resulting in possible mass transport rate-limited 
hydrate formation beyond the solubility peak of methanol[98]. Another similar prospect is offered with alcohol cosurfactants 
as aids in micro-emulsion formation by lowering interfacial tension, where low amounts of MeOH are proposed to be 
effective for anti-agglomeration [96]. Consequently, this behavior could result in better synergetic effects for the hydrate 
formation as well as the replacement process by preventing hydrate reformation. Amino acids and surfactants are not 
chemicals that affect the thermodynamic state of hydrate formation, but they are kinetic promoters. SDS and methionine 
are faster kinetic formers due to the quicker agglomeration of hydrate nuclei during hydrate formation. Hence, SDS, 
methionine and low amounts of MeOH result in lower water affinity towards other water molecules and consequently lower 
surface tension in the water/hydrate former interface. This effect results in a faster CH4 hydrate formation. 
The presence of hydrate inhibitors such as MeOH and sodium chloride (NaCl) [99] shifts upward the hydrate equilibrium 
curve of CH4 and CO2 hydrate. The difference in equilibrium pressure between CH4 and CO2 at a given temperature is 
considered to be an extra thermodynamic driving force behind CH4-CO2 swapping. Table 6 provides the calculated change 
in driving force from Table 4 and Table 5  (ΔP = Peq, CH4 – Peq, CO2) in the presence of various concentrations of hydrate 
inhibitors (MeOH and NaCl). The P, T calculations concerning the hydrates in the presence of different chemicals is 
illustrated in Figure 22.   At 0℃ and below, the presence of an inhibitor improves the driving force as ΔP increases for all 
concentrations for a given inhibitor compared to the pure water case. Above 0℃, it was found that 5 wt. % methanol is most

same temperature. As the temperature increased from 1℃ to 4℃, the driving force increased from 21.54 to 27.58 bar, 
thereafter starting to decrease. Therefore, based on CSMGem software calculations in this study, 5 wt. % MeOH and a 0℃-

4.2 CH4-Hydrate Formation in Coarse Sediment 
Initially, some CH4 hydrate formation experiments under the presence of SDS and L-methionine were performed to confirm 
that the kinetic promoters (SDS and L-methionine) do not affect hydrate saturation as they do not affect the thermodynamic 
equilibrium conditions but only the hydrate formation kinetics. 
Experiments with low dosage methanol were performed to investigate its effect on the CO2-CH4 replacement process. It 
should be noted that for the experimental series with 5 wt.% MeOH present, the targeted experimental temperature was as 
per SDS, L-methionine and water experiments, while the effect of pressure driving force at different temperature was 
investigated in the CO2-CH4 replacement process as well (based on the observed trend from CSMGEM tabulated data in 
Table 6). Thus, the effect of methanol in hydrate saturation and growth profile during CH4 hydrate formation can be 
evaluated along with the other utilized chemicals. 
The initial fluid saturation, the sediment characteristics and the formation kinetics induced by the hydrate former and porous 
media presence determines the hydrate saturation and specifies its morphology. Coarse sediments have mostly pore-filling 
hydrates, while veined type hydrates are mostly met in fine-grained porous media. Undrained heating and cooling cycles 
during the hydrate formation stage result in induced gas hydrates dissociation and reformation, which in turn favors evenly 
homogeneous gas hydrates distribution[103]. Therefore, multiple heating and cooling cycles were performed to increase 
hydrate saturation and improve hydrate distribution within porous media.

4.2.4 Analysis of Role of the Chemical in CH4-hydrate Formation

4.3 CH4 Recovery and CO2 Storage at same initial water saturation 
The presence of chemical could have an influential role during the CH4-CO2 replacement process as the chemical can change 
water chemical potential, influence driving force during CH4-CO2 swapping and alter the hydrate morphology. Methane 
recovery from the CH4-CO2 exchange process is a function of the residual water saturation after the induced methane hydrate 
formation since the remaining active water determines the CO2 hydrate formation which in turn controls the CH4-CO2 
replacement. Hence, the hydrate saturation is an important parameter for the replacement process. Qing et al. [74] studied 
CH4-CO2 exchange using liquid CO2 and found that the replacement of CH4 hydrate decreased with an increase of hydrate 
saturation, but it also increased with an increase in initial water saturation. Therefore, quantified results are collected to gain 
a better understanding of the effect of the chemical on CH4-CO2 exchange in a porous system. The behavior of each chemical 
can be evaluated by the comparison of the CH4 recovery and CO2 storage efficiencies among each chemical type. 
The hydrate saturation degree and its morphology control the hydrate specific surface area. Regarding the same grain-sized 
porous medium, pore-filling hydrates have lower specific area compared to grain-coating type hydrate. The high CH4-
hydrate specific area can enhance the contact area between CO2 and CH4-hydrate, which in turn could lead to better CH4 
recovery due to a different mass transfer scenario. In this section, the effect of different chemicals on the CH4-CO2 exchange 
will be discussed. Table 11 summarizes the experimental values and the calculated results for the CH4-CO2 replacement key

the gas-liquid interphase. Therefore, the addition of a promoter indicates higher CO2 gas consumption. However, the 
promoter-driven enhanced kinetics of pure CO2-hydrate formation favor thermodynamically CO2 to be engaged in hydrate 
than participate in the replacement process due to higher water activity and lower surface tension in the gas-liquid interphase 
provided from the promoter. As a consequence higher CO2 storage is observed but in a dense interfacial area between the 
gas-liquid contact area. 
Furthermore, the CH4 recovery ratios in Table 11 are negative under promoter presence which suggests that additional CH4-
hydrate reformation occurred. This phenomenon is a consequence of the limited CO2-gas diffusion into the CH4-bulk 
hydrate phase from enhanced pure CO2-hydrate formation kinetics. Consequently, the amount of CO2 gas that was diffused 
into the CH4-bulk hydrate through the pure CO2-hydrate barrier was lower as a combined result of promoted formation 
kinetics leading to a denser and thicker barrier which in turn limits the amount of CO2 diffused further from it. Therefore, 
the CH4 gas released driven from the CH4-CO2 replacement process is trapped beneath this barrier and it is possible to 
reform CH4 hydrates due to higher selectivity compared with gas swapping in the enclathration process. Thus, it can be 
claimed that the presence of promoters results in higher CO2 storage efficiency mainly driven from pure CO2-hydrate 
formation with additional hindering of the CH4-CO2 replacement efficiency and induced CH4-hydrate reformation.

To explain the occurring phenomena in the chemical assisted CO2 injection in the CH4-hydrate sediment experimentally 
examined, the occurring phenomena in the presence of promoter compared to water will be described in an illustrative way 
based on Figure 21. In the CH4-hydrate bearing-sediment with the presence of water after CO2 injection, a pure CO2-hydrate 
starts to form in the contact area of gas and excess active water due to higher CO2 selectivity for hydrate formation than 
CH4-CO2 swapping. This process creates a pure CO2-hydrate layer which is considered as a general interfacial barrier for 
further gas diffusion and CH4-CO2 exchange into the deep bulk CH4-hydrate phase. Beneath the pure CO2-hydrate the CH4-
CO2 exchange results in a mixed CH4-CO2 hydrate. The mixed hydrate composition is governed by the diffusion rate of 
CO2 into the deeper CH4- bulk hydrate phase through the interfacial barrier, which is correlated to the thickness of the barrier 
and the kinetic effect of the chemical. The presence of promoter (SDS, Methionine) results in a thicker pure CO2-hydrate 
layer compared to the barrier in the presence of water, since the water activity is increased from the promoter effect of lower 
interfacial tension. Hence, it is expected that the CH4-CO2 replacement under the promoter presence occurs at a slower rate 
compared to water as a consequence of the denser barrier existence. Therefore, the limitation of the CH4-CO2 exchange via 
slower diffusion through the denser barrier results in CH4-dominated mixed hydrate with promoter presence compared to 
CO2-dominated mixed hydrate for water case. 
On the contrary, the presence of low dosage MeOH (5 wt.%, Swi≈ 45% ) in the system subjected to CH4-CO2 exchange, 
shows promising results in terms of storage and recovery efficiencies compared with promoter cases tabulated in Table 11. 
The role of the inhibitor is to affect the thermodynamics of the hydrate formation by increasing the free energy of the system 
and hence make the energy requirements for hydrate formation higher. This, concerning the pore-scale level it is achieved 
by the higher interfacial energy in the gas-liquid interface where hydrate formation occurs. Considering a system where 
CH4-hydrate has formed in the porous media with low dosage MeOH presence, the CH4-hydrate saturation is expected lower 
compared to the water or kinetic promoter system as a consequence of the alcohol inhibition effect. This is confirmed by 
the calculated CH4 hydrate saturation in with observed lower hydrate saturation (SH≈ 28%) for the MeOH case. However, 
the MeOH presence leads to a disruption in the barrier layer by inhibiting pure CO2-hydrate formation in the contact area 
of the injected CO2 gas-liquid interface. The result is a less dense and thicker pure CO2-hydrate layer compared to promoter 
cases. Therefore, the diffusion of CO2 into the deeper CH4-bulk hydrate phase is possible favoring the CH4-CO2 exchange 
by a more efficient replacement process. Consequently, the CH4 release efficiency will significantly increase compared with 
the water case and no observed CH4- hydrate reformation compared with promoter cases, while the CO2 storage efficiency 
will be approximately the same compared with promoter presence systems. However, the advantage of the inclusion of low 
dosage inhibitor in the system (5 wt. % MeOH), is that the CO2 is mainly stored in the bulk hydrate driven by the CH4-CO2 
replacement process compared with promoter cases, resulting in greater CO2 storage and CH4 recovery with the absence of 
CH4-hydrate reformation. The systematic of the CH4-CO2 exchange in MeOH presence is illustrated in Figure 31.

4.3.1 Saturation Effect on the CH4 Recovery and CO2 Storage  
Methane recovery from the CH4-CO2 replacement process is controlled by the residual water saturation after methane 
hydrate formed and consequently the hydrate saturation. Thus, for the same chemical that provides the most efficient result 
as discussed in section 4.3  (5wt. % MeOH), experiments of CH4-CO2 swapping with varying water saturation were 
performed to investigate which system results in the best recovery ratios. Hence, quantified results are collected to gain a 
clear understanding of the effect of the initial water saturation and consequently the residual water saturation from the CH4-
hydrate formation step on CH4-CO2 exchange in a porous media. The behavior can be evaluated comparing the CH4 recovery 
and CO2 storage efficiencies for different initial water saturations. 
In this section, the effect of saturation on the CH4-CO2 exchange will be analyzed. Table 12 summarizes the experimental 
data and the calculated results regarding CH4-CO2 exchange key ratios, concerning the swapping process for varying initial

Experiments 8 to 10 investigate the effect of a low, medium and high initial water saturation initially on hydrate saturation 
and morphology; and finally their role on CH4-CO2 replacement. Table 12, shows that the porous system with medium 
MeOH saturation (Exp 8) exhibits the highest hydrate saturation, while for low (Exp 10) and high (Exp 9) initial water 
saturation the final hydrate saturation is at the same range. It is expected that with less initial water saturation the morphology 
of formed CH4-hydrate will be different compared with the medium and the high saturation cases. The lower the initial 
water saturation (Exp 10), the lower the amount of free active water in pore space for CH4-hydrate formation. Therefore, 
the morphology of CH4-hydrate is considered grain-coating since the active water with water-wet porous media assumption 
is mainly in contact with grains. On the other hand, for the other cases (Exp 8 and 9) it is considered pore-filling CH4-
hydrate formation. The bulk hydrate phase morphology defines the contact area between its area, the liquid residual water 
and the vapor (CO2) interface. The specific surface area in a grain-coating hydrate system is higher compared with pore-
filling analog[105]. Consequently, the hydrate morphology and the resulting specific surface area have a determinant role 
in the CH4-CO2 exchange since they specify the contact area for the replacement process.

4.4 Depressurization enhanced CH4 Recovery and CO2 Storage  
Depressurization can improve further the CO2 storage and CH4 recovery since induced dissociation of CH4, CO2 bulk 
hydrates and mixed hydrate may favor the CH4-CO2 replacement process. In an unconsolidated porous coarse-grained 
medium after the CH4-hydrate formation stage, the CH4-CO2 replacement forces the system to a new equilibrium state 
mainly controlled from the chemical type, initial water saturation, induced methane hydrate saturation and the temperature 
of the exchange process, as was explained in section 4.3 . The stepwise depressurization determines which hydrate could 
dissociate according to the thermodynamic calculations for the equilibrium pressure from CSMGem (Table 4, Table 5) and 
the experimentally obtained equilibrium pressures for CH4-hydrate. The dissociation of CH4 or CO2 hydrate could create an 
additional driving force for the CH4-CO2 exchange. Therefore, for the above-analyzed experiments (Exp 5 to 12) 
depressurization close or below CH4-hydrate but above CO2-hydrate equilibrium pressure was performed. Moreover, further 
depressurization close or below CO2-hydrate equilibrium pressure was performed and together with GC sample collection 
the impact of the depressurization step was analyzed as described in section 3.6  (Equations (19)-(27) ). Hence, the quantified 
moles' number at the start and the end of the depressurization steps provide an understanding of the impact of the pressure 
relief steps.

As was discussed in section 4.3 , the chemical presence together with the induced hydrate saturation controls the CH4-CO2 
exchange, while the elevated temperature can help the process with an increased driving force. The presence of promoter 
(SDS, Methionine) favors the creation of a dense barrier of pure CO2 hydrate, hindering the CH4-CO2 exchange and resulting 
in a CH4 dominated mixed hydrate. On the contrary, the presence of MeOH as an anti-agglomerant reduces the pure CO2 
barrier which leads to better CH4-CO2 replacement and a CO2 dominated mixed hydrate. Therefore, the state of the system 
after the CH4-CO2 swapping stage is crucial for the following depressurization steps since the level of the depressurization 
will determine which hydrate dissociation will be induced that in turn controls the CH4-CO2 exchange.  
From Table 16, the evaluation of depressurization above or close to the CO2-hydrate equilibrium pressure shows that it 
results to further CO2 storage (positive %) and CH4 recovery (positive %). Considering the system state from the swapping 
stage under promoter presence, there is mainly a dense pure CO2 barrier where the CO2 was stored and a CH4 dominated 
mixed hydrate as a consequence of the dense barrier and limited exchange. Hence, the depressurization below PCH4, final but 
above PCO2, eq will induce the dissociation of these layers. Consequently, as the system is forced in an equilibrium state less 
than the swapping stage the free energy is increased. This free energy will be decreased preferably by CO2 hydrate 
reformation as a more stable hydrate former compared to methane. Similar behavior is observed also with MeOH presence 
and depressurization above or close to the CO2-hydrate equilibrium pressure. 
There are further CO2 storage and CH4 release both for promoters and MeOH systems. This can be explained as a result of 
the complete dissociation of the pure CO2 layer which initiates the dynamics of the system again. Regarding promoters' 
presence, the depressurization induced total dissociation of the dense pure CO2-barrier will initiate the CH4-CO2 replacement 
again. Thus, further CO2 will be stored and further CH4 will be released primarily from the CH4-dominated mixed hydrate. 
The behavior for the MeOH systems resembles the promoters' one with the difference of the non-dense barrier and the CO2-
dominated mixed hydrate impact on the efficiencies. The CO2 storage and the methane release are lower generally compared 
with promoters as a consequence of the CO2 dominated mixed hydrate presence that lowers the driving force for further  
CH4-CO2 replacement process compared to the CH4-dominated mixed hydrate. However, it should be mentioned that the 
comparison is qualitative since the depressurization step history is not the same. The removal from the free gas phase during 
the shut-in period of the pressure relief action affects the free gas phase consistency. The less the number of depressurization 
steps, the less the rejection of CO2 gas from the free gas phase which can favor the CH4-CO2 replacement. 
It is obvious the stochastic behavior of the depressurization as a function of the step defined pressure level. Fewer steps 
result in a system with a free gas phase that consists of more CO2. This can lead to a better CH4-CO2 exchange scenario 
induced by the pressure relief level always in conjunction with the promoter/methanol presence, their saturations and the 
temperature. Both promoters and methanol systems favor further CH4-CO2 swapping below CH4-hydrate equilibrium 
pressure as a consequence of the additional driving force. The system from the pressure relief dynamically restarts as the 
driving force is provided from the induced dissociation of the pure CO2 barrier and the mixed CH4-CO2 hydrate. Zhao et al.

4.6 Gas Hydrate Formation in Fine Sediments 
Over the past years, hydrate formation dynamics in marine sediments have drawn the attention of extensive research to 
understand the role of the porous medium presence in hydrate formation mechanisms. The effect of an additional surface 
on formation dynamics, such as a porous medium, was originally investigated by Cha et al. [111], reporting that large 
surface areas of interactive surfaces and the surface orientation affect the thermodynamics and the kinetics of formation 
mechanism. The hydrate nucleation processes in porous media depend on the solubility of the gas available phase, as a 
function of the presence or the absence of the hydrate phase. The calculated solubilities of methane gas over a range of 
temperature and pressure in hydrate nucleation processes under porous media presence according to Zatsepina et al. [112]–
[114]indicate great alteration by the presence of hydrate phase, while the dissolved gas in the aqueous phase can lead to 
hydrate crystallization. Tohidi et al. [115] showed experimentally by glass micromodels, that hydrates can form in synthetic 
porous media without available free gas phase. They reported also pore-filling hydrate morphology while cementation of 
grains was observed only with small grain size. Chuvillin et al. [116], [117] proved that temperature and cooling cycles 
affect the water-ice ratio in gas-saturated media that changes the thermodynamics of hydrate since only a part of the pore-
water transforms to hydrate. For partially water-saturated, CH4-hydrate bearing fine sand (Ottawa sands) Waite et al. [118] 
found gain-coating hydrate morphology which cements sediment grains as well. According to Spangenberg et al. [119] 
experiments with glass bead model hydrate formation in pore space, showed that pore-filling hydrate can be formed from 
the upward transportation of the dissolved CH4 in water inside the hydrate stability field. Madenna and Linga et al. [120], 
[121] with gas hydrate formation in variable silica bed size reported massive hydrate accumulation firstly in a thin film 
interface at the gas-liquid contact area of the CH4 bubble inside the pore-space and followingly their massive growth. Thus, 
hydrate formation mechanisms and distribution are influenced by sediment grain size except for the thermodynamic 
conditions. 
In the following sections, the analysis for the CH4 and the CO2 hydrates formation will be discussed for experiments with 
fine sand (Table 3, 0.212-0.300 mm) to evaluate the effect of the particle size on the resulting hydrate saturation when 
compared with coarse sand (Table 3, 0.900-1.600 mm). Furthermore, the chemical effect on the bulk hydrates formation in 
the fine sediment will be again discussed. Lastly, the chemical effect together with the grain size will be analyzed for the 
self-preservation stage.

promoters’ cases. Besides, the formation kinetics are affected by the inhibition where lower hydrate saturation is faster 
compared with the promoters’ analog. Comparing the induction times for the coarse-grained size porous medium from 
Figure 30 with Figure 40 it can be claimed that there is a positive correlation between the hydrate saturation and the kinetics. 
The higher the saturation the higher the induction times concerning the same chemical but different sand particle size. The 
hydrate formation induced saturation and its distribution is determined from the active pore-water. As it has previously 
discussed for the fine sand big amount of water is bounded in the solid interface of the grains as a result of the higher specific 
surface area of this porous medium compared to the coarse one. Hence, for the fine sand, the hydrate formation is prone to 
start on that gas-liquid interface as a thin hydrate film which thickens gradually with time. This thickening of the hydrate 
film together with the hydrate growth in the pore-space decreases the pore diameter which in turn increases capillary forces 
and together with the liquid volume expansion and the liquid phase migrates upwards. Consequently, the formed hydrate 
tends to fill all the pores in the silica sand bed with further cementation of the particles together[125], [126]. Considering, 
the relative permeabilities in the smaller pore space of the fine sand and the aforementioned hydrate formation mechanism 
leads to lower relative permeabilities when compared with coarse sand bed and thus the kinetics of the formation will be 
prolonged.

volume. The initial dissociation of pore-filling hydrates in large pores (coarse sand) is slower than hydrates in small pores 
(fine sand) with the same hydrate saturation as a result of less specific surface area. However, the contribution of the heat 
and mass transfer together with the permeability alteration affects also the dissociation kinetics. The endothermic feature of 
hydrate dissociation can induce secondary hydrate nucleation as a consequence of the drop of temperature in the 
surroundings which in turn could slow down the diffusion rate of the gas liberation induced by dissociation [124]. The 
Joule-Thompson cooling effect induced by the fast initial gas release rate due to gas expansion can reduce effective 
permeability which in turn slows down the dissociation rate according to Moridis et al. [128]. Therefore, the obtained 
profiles are following the literature trends for the dissociation kinetics where they suggest an initial fast diffusion 
dissociation stage that gradually decreases as the mass transfer is hindered by the heat transfer and the effective permeability 
alteration. 
Evaluation of Table 20 and Figure 41 shows that there is a positive correlation between the hydrate saturation and the CH4 
release. The higher the hydrate saturation the higher the number of moles that are released during the dissociation stage. 
This is attributed to the fact that for the same porous medium with the initial water saturation where the induced hydrate 
saturation is higher as a result of the chemical presence, leads to higher hydrate surface area resulting in faster dissociation 
kinetics and consequently higher gas release for the same time duration (12 hours). A similar trend is observed for the 
dissociation rate which for higher hydrate saturation follows a higher dissociation rate in the fast diffusion dissociation stage 
(90 mins). Therefore, the chemical presence affects the induced hydrate saturation during the hydrate formation stage. Thus, 
for the same porous medium, it specifies the specific surface area of the hydrate that controls the gas release and the 
dissociation rate. For instance, for SDS-500 ppm presence (EXP 1), the gas release (0.268 moles) is at the same order as in 
the methionine experiment (EXP 3 with 0.265moles) where similarly induced hydrate saturations (SH≈42 %) was observed. 
These results align with some similar studies on the effect of the silica sand and the hydrate saturation on the gas hydrates 
dissociation [126], [129], [130].

5. Conclusion 
High-pressure cell and gas chromatography analysis with various chemicals and coarse silica sand were utilized to study 
initially the formation of gas hydrates and subsequently the CH4-CO2 exchange with further stepwise depressurization. For 
the hydrate formation in the coarse sand porous medium, methane gas was utilized with kinetic promoters (SDS, L-
Methionine) and low dosage methanol as the chemical solutions at different saturation levels.  
5.1 CH4-Hydrate Formation in Coarse Sand 
It was confirmed that the presence of the kinetic promoters does not affect the thermodynamic equilibrium of the CH4 bulk 
hydrate phase but only the methane hydrate growth kinetics. On the contrary, the presence of the inhibitor (methanol) even 
at low dosage changes the thermodynamic equilibrium of the hydrate phase mainly observed as increased equilibrium 
pressure for the same formation temperature. Furthermore, it was shown that there is a trade-off between formation kinetics 
and gas uptake regarding the chemical presence, as a lower induction time translates into a lower gas uptake which in turn 
results in lower hydrate saturation. 
It can be stated that adsorption and surface tension control the gas uptake since they determine the gas phase in the solid-
liquid-gas interphase where the nucleation of the hydrate starts in the pore system. For decreased adsorption, the nucleation 
temperature can be delayed and that requires further driving force for the initiation of the nucleation process. This is 
accomplished by lower temperature, but it results in lower gas uptake for the same experimental temperature. Moreover, 
the surface tension depends on the chemical nature of the molecule. During the hydrate growth process in the gas-liquid 
contact, the surface tension between the gas and the liquid solution determines the water activity. Lower surface tension, as 
a result of reduced water activity by disruption of the hydrogen bonding between gas-liquid, leads to lower induction times 
and in turn, lower gas uptake resembled into lower hydrate saturation. 
5.2 Chemical Assisted CH4-CO2 Swapping in Coarse Sand 
Analysis of the CH4-CO2 exchange, after the CH4 hydrate formation, with pure CO2 injection, was performed to investigate 
the swapping process potential in the porous medium under the presence of the chemicals. Experiments showed that the gas 
uptake is correlated to the promoters or inhibitor presence. Comparing a system that is saturated with water and one saturated 
with a small concentration of promoter (SDS, L-Methionine), it was found that the promoter presence enhances the uptake 
of free available gas molecules with no observed selectivity between CH4 and CO2 molecules. 
Regarding the case with no chemical presence (water), the controlling factor for the selectivity among the two gas molecules 
is the driving force supplied by the initial pressure concerning the equilibrium hydrate formation pressure of either molecule. 
It is shown that after the CO2 injection methane is released, observed as an increase in the CH4 moles in the free gas phase, 
while the CO2 to a greater extent is decreased in the gas phase. 
The addition of a promoter results in a much more significant CO2 decrease; however, the increased CO2 storage is limited 
in the CO2-bulk hydrate phase formed by the enhanced formation kinetics in the gas-liquid contact area. Consequently, a 
dense interfacial layer is expected to be formed. That layer acts as a barrier to the CH4-CO2 replacement process since the 
CO2 molecules are unable to diffuse further deep into the CH4-bulk hydrate phase. Additionally, this dense barrier with the 
synergetic effect of the enhanced formation kinetics due to the promoter presence leads to limited gas diffusion of the CH4 
that was swapped by CO2 which results in CH4-hydrate reformation without CH4 recovery. 
The addition of a low dosage inhibitor (Methanol) has a different effect compared to a promoter presence. The role of an 
inhibitor is to reduce the gas hydrate formation by a disruption of hydrogen bonding in the gas-liquid contact area. The 
result is that the CO2 barrier interfacial layer created by the CO2 injection is to a great extent limited when compared with 
==PAGE:80==
 
69 
 
a promoter system. The enhanced diffusion of CO2 into the deeper CH4-bulk hydrate phase favors CH4-CO2 swapping with 
more efficient CO2 storage and CH4 recovery. The amount of CH4 significantly increased when compared with the water 
system; the amount of CO2 stored in the CH4-bulk hydrate is greatly increased as well, compared with a promoter system. 
The effect of initial water saturation on the CH4-CO2 swapping was studied with the low dosage methanol system as the 
most promising and efficient system in terms of CO2 storage and CH4 recovery when compared both with a promoter and 
water system. The CH4 recovery efficiencies are correlated with the hydrate saturation degree as a function of the initial 
water saturation which in turn specifies the hydrate morphology. Pore-filling hydrates are an outcome of high initial water 
saturation, while low Swi results in grain-coating hydrates. Since the morphology specifies the contact area for the gas 
exchange efficiency it can be stated that grain-coating hydrates provide more contact are via their bigger specific surface 
area for the gas exchange. Furthermore, the lower resulting hydrate saturation for the same hydrate morphology favors the 
replacement process due to better gas connectivity in the pore-space. Lastly, the temperature effect is prominent by the 
higher thermodynamic driving force induced by the bulk hydrate equilibrium pressure between the two components of the 
system. 
5.3 Depressurization Enhanced CH4 Production and CO2 storage in Coarse Sand 
Further CO2 storage and CH4 recovery induced by depressurization stochastically as a function of the step defined pressure 
level. Fewer steps result in a system with a free gas phase that consists of more CO2. This can lead to a better CH4-CO2 
exchange scenario induced by the pressure relief level always in conjunction with the promoter/methanol presence, their 
saturations and the swapping temperature. Both promoters and methanol systems favor further CH4-CO2 swapping below 
the CH4-hydrate equilibrium pressure as a result of the additionally provided driving force. The system from the pressure 
relief dynamically restarts as the driving force is provided from the induced dissociation of the mixed CH4-CO2 hydrate, 
favoring the CO2-hydrate reformation and further CH4 recovery in the presence of methanol occurs due to the anti-
agglomeration effect during the hydrate reformation. 
5.4 CH4 , CO2 Hydrates Formation and Dissociation in Fine Sand 
Methane hydrate formation in fine sand displayed the same correlation as in coarse sand concerning formation kinetics. The 
higher the gas uptake together with higher induction times results in lower hydrate saturation. It is shown also that higher 
hydrate saturation is a consequence of longer induction times concerning the same chemical but smaller grain size. Since 
the hydrate occurs in the gas-liquid interfacial area, in the pore-space of a fine-grained system this specific surface area is 
bigger and thus the nucleation sites for hydrate formation are more than the coarse-grained porous analog. 
The chemical presence affects the induced hydrate saturation during the hydrate formation stage. Thus, for the same porous 
medium (fine sand), it specifies the specific surface area of the hydrate that controls the gas release and the dissociation rate 
with the self-preservation effect holding.  For a higher specific surface area as a result of a higher induced hydrate saturation, 
the dissociation metrics are higher. 
The grain-coating hydrate morphology is porous due to the water-to-hydrate volume expansion that leads to imperfections 
in the micro-pore scale. Trapped methane gas and liquid from increased capillary forces inside these imperfections together 
with promoter presence leads to enhanced hydrate formation. The dissociation is an endothermic phenomenon that can 
induce secondary hydrate nucleation as it absorbs energy from the surroundings. Therefore, promoted reformation from the 
released gas trapped in the micropore-scale imperfections results in the mass transfer limit of the gas which is seen as lower 
dissociation metrics. 
A similar but more pronounced trend is observed regarding CO2 gas in the presence of promoters (SDS, Methionine) that 
higher hydrate saturation leads to lower dissociation metrics. This can be attributed to the higher gas volume expansion

the pore space. Hence, the gas diffusion is further limited by the mass transfer hindering induced by the hydrate reformation 
which is reflected in lower gas release and dissociation rate. On the contrary, MeOH with the anti-agglomeration effect 
results in the opposite trend. For MeOH presence and CH4 gas, the dissociation metrics are lower despite the higher hydrate 
saturation when compared with the CO2 analog. The higher CO2 hydrate saturation is translated into more porous hydrate 
morphology due to higher CO2 volume expansion during the water to hydrate conversion. This in turn increases the 
imperfections but also the surface area and considering the inhibition effect during the hydrate reformation induced by the 
dissociation, it results in higher dissociation values.

A. Solutions preparation 
 
The solutions preparation was performed in 1000 ml of demineralized water assuming the density of distilled water 1g/ml 
then the targeted concentration can be achieved by the following equation.

dissolved in the solution and Vt is the final total volume of the solution assuming deionized water is used. Thus, for a 500 
ppm solution in a 1000 grams water solution, it is required 0.5 g of the kinetic promoter to be dissolved in water.

B. CSMGem 
CSMGem is a thermodynamic equilibrium software tool for incipient hydrate calculations designed by Colorado School of 
Mines. For known initial water saturation and the hydrate former concentration for a given temperature, it calculates the 
equilibrium pressure for the CH4 or CO2 bulk hydrate phases. 
C. REFPROP 
REFPROP from NIST is a thermodynamic equilibrium software tool for pure gases, liquids and mixtures that calculates 
many thermodynamic properties for known substances. It was utilized to calculate VLE for pure CO2 and the compressibility 
factors for CH4-CO2 gas mixture compositions acquired from GC analysis.
