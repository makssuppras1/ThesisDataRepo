Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate application of topology optimization
to control the sound pressure ﬁeld generated by a plate subject to a time har-
monic excitation. This is done in a multiphysical ﬁnite element analysis combi-
ning acoustics and structural mechanics through an elastic-acoustic-coupling. The
focus of the study, is to optimze the design of the excited mindlin plate on three
diﬀerent parameters: Thickness distribution and material composition varying be-
tween two materials of the plate, and the distribution of the excitation pressure.
The optimization is done at a ﬁxed excitation frequency or a frequency range and
is directed against optimizing the design of the plate and excitation pressure. Akl
et al. (2008) optimize the coupling between a mindlin plate and an acoustic cavity.
This thesis takes this as starting point and further applies material composition
as a design variable and enable optimization for multiple load cases. To further
control the pressure distribution and power ﬂow in the cavity a objective that
maximizes and minimized the output in speciﬁc parts of the cavity (Jensen 2017).
A study of this objective is carried out for two diﬀerent open cavities. One for a
minimization and maximization of the output in two degrees of freedom in oppo-
sed corners in the top of a cavity that is open in the top. Another cavity with two
holes in the side, one in the bottom and one in the bottom, here the output in
degrees of freedom for each hole is either maximized or minimized.

Introduction
This thesis explore the possibilities of utilizing topology optimization on a plate
structure to control the sound pressure generated by a vibrating plate. Noise is a
signiﬁcant concern when designing structures whether it is components for large
airplanes, cars or small hearing aids. Historically the evolution of mechanical de-
signs has been driven by the human intuition but the vastly growing engineering
ﬁeld of topology optimization is through computer aid opening up for unrestri-
cted design freedom. This is made possible by combining mechanics, variational
calculus and mathematical programming.
For a acoustic-mechanical coupling between a mindlin plate and a acoustic
cavity as are used in this thesis a topology optimization study has been carried
out by Akl et al. (2008). The objective function of the article is stated to work
towards minimizing the coupling of the system by minimizing the work done in the
degrees of freedom of the coupling. This thesis will take the achievements of this
as starting point and will investigate the possibilities of further optimization using
the this objective function, by applying material composition as a design variable
and enable optimization for multiple load cases to provide a robust method to
optimize for a frequency range. Further an objective enabling optimization towards
minimizing or maximizing the sound pressure in speciﬁc degrees of freedom in
the cavity is applied (Jensen 2017). To enhance the optimization possibilities the
distribution of the excitation pressure on the plate is implemented as a design
variable. To facilitate power ﬂow in the system a Sommerfeld radiation condition
is implemented as boundary condition.
The thesis is organized in four parts. It begins by introducing the theory behind
the physical system, hereafter the design variables introduced and the sensitivities
are calculated in the second part. Then the two objective functions are introduded
and ﬁnally a study of the two is carried out.

4
Optimization
The ﬁeld of structural optimization includes both topology, material and shape
optimization. This thesis utilizes a distribution optimization method to solve both
sizing and topology optimization problems. Two sizing problems are addressed;
one works to ﬁnd the optimal distribution of material through variation of the
thickness distribution of a linear elastic plate, the other works to ﬁnd the optimal
distribution of excitation pressure on the plate to optimize a given objective.
Topology optimization problems concern where to put the material and in great
extent where not to put the material. Here it is used to ﬁnd the distribution of
two materials to optimize the objective function. This is solved using a density
based approach where the material properties of plate elements are deﬁned as an
interpolation between the two given materials. This way the topology optimization
problem also take the form of a sizing problem. All design domains are ﬁxed and
known a priori and the optimization is done on a macroscopic level not concerning
the microstructures. As optimization problems typically contain a large amount
of design variables use of an eﬃcient optimization procedure is crucial. Through
the thesis a gradient based iterative optimization scheme known as the Method of
Moving Asymptotes (MMA) by Svandberg (1987) utilized in Svandberg (2002):
is used to ﬁnd the design variables. It is important to mention that this method
performs a search for minima, but there is no certainty that this is the global
minimum.

Sensitivity
In gradient based topology optimization the sensitivity is an essential tool as it
provides the gradient that indicates the change of the objective with respect to a
element wise change of the design variable. It is calculated by partial diﬀerentiation
of the objective with respect to the elements of the design variable. When calcu-
lating the sensitivities the Adjoint Method is used. This is a mathematical trick
used to eliminate computationally cost-heavy parts of the sensitivity calculation.

4.1.2
Variable Thickness
Changing the thickness distribution of the plate will change the stiﬀness and mass
distribution and thus the motion, momentum and natural frequencies of the plate.
When optimizing a plate subjected to a time harmonic excitation these are crucial
properties.
4.1.2.1
Interpolation

4.1.3
Material composition
Much like it was the case for change in thickness distribution, a variation of the
material through out the plate will change the stiﬀness and mass distribution of
this, which eﬀects the motion, momentum and natural frequencies. Therefore a
optimization of this is relevant.
4.1.3.1
Interpolation
In this case two materials with same Poisson’s ratio are chosen. Therefore the
diﬀerent material parameters of the two materials are Young’s modulus E and
the density ρ. Optimization by varying between two materials can be done by
using the design variable ξ ∈[0, 1]. When the two materials has the same Pois-
son’s ratio ν, a Rational Approximation of Material Properties (RAMP) or SIMP
interpolation can be used to express the density ρ and Young’s modulus E. For
both interpolations a penalization of intermediate values of the design variable can
be used to achieve 0-1 solutions, this can be applied to make the mixing of the
two materials unproﬁtable and in other cases where interpretation of intermediate
values lacks physical meaning. As the density of mixed materials is a linear com-
bination of the two, the linear interpolation of ρ will correspond to the physical
density. On the other hand the stiﬀness is not necessarily a linear combination of
the stiﬀness of the two materials, therefore penalizing the interpolation of Young’s
modulus promotes a physical realizable isotropic material.(Bendsøe & Sigmund
2003, p.6-8, p.64, p.119)
For designs where a two-material structure is desired for the optimization the
RAMP model can be said to be more physical than the SIMP model, as the
SIMP model always will violate the Hashin-Shtrikman bounds for small values
of the design variable, while the properties of the RAMP interpolation can be
chosen such that the Hashin-Shtrikman bounds are satisﬁed (Bendsøe & Sigmund
2003, p.64). Due to this the Young’s moduli in the present optimization model are
chosen to be represented by the RAMP interpolation, while the physical densities

Objective 1: Extension of optimization on ob-

A topology optimization study using the ﬁnite element model of the mindlin plate
coupled to a closed acoustic cavity has previous been carried out by Akl et al.

of the coupling between the structure and ﬂuid domains. This is done by mini-
mizing the summarized amplitude of the degrees of freedom wise multiplication of
the values for the transverse displacement of the plate w and the pressure in the
related degree of freedom in the acoustic domain.
The optimization is done with thickness as a design parameter, and it is chosen
to optimize for a oscillating force in the transverse direction exciting the plate in
the middle. Because the natural frequency changes with change in plate thickness
Akl et al. (2008) has chosen to ﬁt the excitation frequency to the new natural
frequency at every iteration.
Akl et al. (2008) is chosen as a basis of this work, and their method utilized
on the optimization problem, deﬁned in the following equation (5.1.1), to explore
the possibilities of further development.
In this section an optimization of the same objective function will be done with
both thicknesses, two materials and a combination of the two as design parameters.
As ﬁtting the excitation frequency to a natural frequency every iteration can lead
to the natural frequency just being moved back and forth rather than a diminish
of the response, it is chosen to instead optimize for a frequency range near the
given natural frequency to increase the robustness in the optimization. To be able
to compare the results to Akl et al. (2008) the same dimensions and material
parameters has been used.

Discussion
If one chooses to optimize towards minimizing the pressure in one degree of fre-
edom it is advantageous for the optimization to minimize the total energy of the
system. If a minimization only in one degree of freedom is desired and not in the
rest of the system, one should choose another degree in the system to maximize.
As the system is linear, the pressure diﬀerence between two part of the cavity will
be directly proportional to the pressure in the cavity. This means that for a negati-
ve objective an increase of the pressure in the cavity will lead to a lower objective.
This leads to the tendency of the optimization to maximize the pressure in general,
which is not the intention with the objective. A solution to this problem could be
to apply a objective function that evaluates the relative diﬀerence. When the opti-
mization works towards maximizing the pressure, it will work towards maximizing
the displacement. This can be done with a design with a natural frequency at the
optimization frequency, and lead to cases where the full use of mass constraint is
not proﬁtable.
Including both scaling of pressure and stiﬀness in the optimization can lead to a
system that is sensitive to optimization parameters as tmin, tmax and {f}0, as the
system can become badly scaled both when a high force is applied on low stiﬀ-
ness and low force on high stiﬀness, which leads to numeric errors. This problem
enhanced when the optimization works towards maximizing the total pressure, as
this can lead to elements with the lowest stiﬀness being exposed to the maximal
force.
Even though penalization has been applied the results of the optimizations show
a grayscale design of the material composition. This issue could be solved by im-
plementation of a projection ﬁlter such as Heaviside.
For the open cavity the problem is neither symmetric nor proportionally dam-
ped. This means that the direct method must be used to solve the system and
to construct the frequency response plots, for large systems this is can become a
computationally heavy and time consuming process.

8
Conclusion
The coupling between the plate and acoustic medium, which is the objective used
in Akl et al. (2008) has successfully been carried out with the thickness of the
plate as design variable. Further a application of the material composition of the
plate has been added as design space and a optimization of multiple load cases
has been carried out to minimize the objective within a frequency range. A mass
constraint has been applied, so that the optimized solution provides a design that
is a prioritized balance between the two design variables. These applications lead
to a improved result compared to the one achieved by Akl et al. (2008).
To further control the pressure distribution and power ﬂow in the cavity a
objective that maximizes and minimized the output in speciﬁc parts of the cavity
A study of this objective is carried out for two diﬀerent cavities. One for a
minimization and maximization of the outout in two degrees of freedom in opposed
corners in the top of a cavity that is open in the top. Another cavity with two
holes in the side, one in the bottom and one in the bottom, here the output in
degrees of freedom for each hole is either maximized or minimized.
The studies of this show that a plate excited by a time-harmonic excitation
pressure can be topology optimized to to control the generated pressure and power
ﬂow in an acoustic cavity. The optimization is here carried out three design ﬁelds:
Thickness distribution, material composition and excitation pressure distribution.
For a negative objective, as can be the case for Objective 2, an increase of the pres-
sure in the cavity will lead to a lower objective. This leads to the tendency of the
optimization to maximize the pressure in general, which is not the intention with
the objective. The possibility of improving the objective function to one evaluating
the relative diﬀerence rather than the numeric could be done by investigated. To
solve the producibility problem of the material composition of the plate due to
grayscale an optimization with a projection ﬁlter such as Heaviside could done.
When varying both the stiﬀness and pressure the system becomes sensitive to the
optimization parameters as tmin, tmax and {f}0
