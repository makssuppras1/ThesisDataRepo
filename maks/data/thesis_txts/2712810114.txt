Abstract
Roll motion is one of the most important motions to consider in sea keeping, but also one of the
hardest to predict due to the roll damping, that is highly influenced by viscous forces. The roll
damping is most often predicted from semi-empirical formulas, developed for conventional cargo
ships. Wind turbine installation vessels have very different characteristics from conventional
ships, and it is questionable if the semi-empirical methods are valid for this ship type.
This thesis investigates the roll damping of wind turbine installation vessels by use of CFD
and experimental tests. Model tests have been carried out under different loading conditions,
firstly to validate the CFD model under different conditions, and secondly to gain a deeper
understanding of the influence of the vertical center of gravity on the roll damping. A high
center of gravity is a distinct characteristic of wind turbine installation vessels. The analysis is
solely based on the ship Wind Orca, owned and operated by Cadeler A/S. It was found that the
roll damping decreases significantly for high centers of gravity, and that a certain threshold for
significant changes in the damping might exist.
Different damping models and their reliability are compared, such as linear damping, quadratic
damping and the methods provided by ITTC 2011 [1], which includes Ikeda’s method. It was
found that a quadratic model provides a good estimate of the roll damping, and that Ikeda’s
method also performs fairly well, while the barge model recommended by ITTC 2011 [1] over-
estimated the damping.
The concept of adding bilge keels to the hull, to increase the roll damping, is investigated. While
relatively large bilge keels was chosen, it was found that the bilge keels increased the damping
significantly and the concept could potentially extend the vessels’ operational weather window.
The likelihood of waves coinciding with the natural roll frequency however is low in the current
operational areas.
It was found that the computational expense related to free roll decay simulations is high, and
a method to perform the simulation in 2D is proposed.
However, it does not yield similar
results in the current state, but it reduces the computation time vastly. Full scale simulations
are performed to investigate potential scale effects, which indicate significantly smaller damping
than at model scale, but is not investigated in depth.
Keywords: Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), Hydrodynamics, Roll Motion, Roll Damping,
Wind Turbine Installation Vessels, Sea Keeping, Digital Twin, Free Roll Decay, Model Tests.

Resumé
Skibsrulninger er en af de vigtigste bevægelser at betragte i analyser af sødygtighed, men sam-
tidig en af de sværeste at forudsige på grund af rulledæmpningen, som i høj grad er påvirket
af viskose kræfter. Rulledæmpningen bliver oftest beregnet ud fra semi-empiriske formler, ud-
viklet til konventionelle fragtskibe. Vind-installationsskibe (WTIV-fartøjer) har meget forskel-
lige karakteristika end konventionelle skibe og det er diskutabelt om de semi-empiriske formler
er gældende for denne skibstype.
Denne afhandling undersøger rulledæmpningen af vind-installationsskibe ved hjælp af CFD og
eksperimentelle test. Der er blevet udført model forsøg under forskellige lastekonditioner, først
og fremmest for at validere CFD modellen, of dernæst for at opnå en dybere forståelse for indfly-
delsen af massemidtpunktets vertikale position på rulledæmpningen. Et højt massemidtpunkt er
et markant karaktertræk for vind-installationsskibe. Analysen er udelukkende baseret på skibet
Wind Orca, ejet og opereret af Cadeler A/S. Det blev opdaget at rulledæmpningen mindskes
markant for et højt massemidtpunkt og at der muligvis eksisterer en bestemt grænse for hvornår
dæmpningen markant begynder at ændre sig.
Forskellige dæmpningsmodeller, og deres pålidelighed, er sammenlignet, herunder lineær dæmp-
ning, kvadratisk dæmpning og dæmpningsmodellerne præsenteret i ITTC 2011 standarden [1],
som inkludere Ikeda’s metode.
Det blev fundet frem til at en kvadratisk dæmpningsmodel
giver et godt estimat af rulledæmpningen og at Ikeda’s metode også præstere forholdsvist godt,
hvorimod pram-metoden, anbefalet af ITTC 2011 [1], overestimerer dæmpningen.
Der bliver undersøgt et koncept af at tilføje kimingskøle til skroget, for at øge rulledæmpnin-
gen. Mens det skal tages i betragtning at den undersøgte kimingskøl var relativt stor, forøgede
kimingskølene dæmpningen væsentligt, og konceptet vil potentielt kunne udvide skibenes oper-
ationelle vejrvindue. Sandsynligheden for sammenfald mellem skibets naturlige rullefrekvens og
bølgernes frekvens er dog lille i de nuværende operationelle områder.
Det blev konstateret at det beregningsmæssige omfang af at simulere rullehenfald er stort og
der blev derfor foreslået en metode til at foretage simuleringen i 2D. Metoden, i dens nuværende
form, resulterer dog ikke de samme resultater, men reducere beregningstiden markant. For at
undersøge eventuelle konsekvenser af nedskaleringen til model skala, er der blevet udført fuld-
skala simuleringer, som umiddelbart indikerer at dæmpningen er væsentligt mindre end i model
skala, men dette er dog ikke en dybdegående undersøgelse.
Nøgleord: Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), Hydrodynamik, Skibsrulninger , Rulledæmp-
ning, Vind-installationsskibe, Sødygtighed, Digital Twin, Frit Rullehenfald, Model Forsøg.

1 Introduction
In the global transition to green energy the offshore wind energy sector is experiencing an
immense growth. With the increased interest in offshore wind comes the need for wind turbine
installation vessels. These vessels are used to install the wind turbines and are characterized by
having 4-6 legs which can be lowered down to the sea floor and jack-up the vessel, such that you
have a fixed platform to install from. A wind turbine installation vessel is shown in Figure 1.1.
Compared to conventional cargo ships, these vessels are characterized by being much wider,
relative to their length and by having a higher center of gravity and a shallower draught. These
characteristics affects not only the dynamic behavior of the ship but also the prediction of the
ship’s motions, in particular the ship’s roll motion. Usually when performing a motion analysis
of a vessel, using either strip- or panel code methods, a damping coefficient of the roll motion is
estimated by semi-empirical formulas known as Ikeda’s Method. While Ikeda’s Method is fairly
reliable for conventional ships, its validity is questionable for box-shaped vessels like barges and
wind turbine installation vessels (Standing, [2], Kawahara [3]).
This thesis aims to develop a numerical model to estimate the roll damping coefficient using
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) in the commercial software STAR CCM+. At the ex-
pense of computation time, CFD is a highly sophisticated tool at solving a variety of advanced
fluid problems. The roll damping will be extracted by simulating roll tests in the time-domain.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
The thesis aims to verify the model directly against a wind turbine installation vessel through
experimental model tests in an established water tank. This way the roll damping and the
performance of the CFD model can be investigated under different loading conditions.
In situations where a ship is particular exposed to large roll motions, it is common to equip the
hull with bilge keels which increases the roll damping significantly. However, it is not commonly
seen in the context of wind turbine installation vessels, but if shown effective, adding a bilge keel
could potentially extend the operational weather window for the vessels. Hence, the influence
of adding a bilge keel in this context will be investigated.
1.1
Problem Statement
The above outlined project description can be shortened down to the following objectives:
• Develop a CFD model which can simulate the vessels dynamic behavior in time and extract
roll damping coefficients.
• Conduct model tests to validate if the CFD model’s predictions are reliable and investigate
the accuracy of the CFD model.
• Compare results with current methods for estimating roll damping (e.g. Ikeda’s method)
and evaluate the respective methods’ reliability.
• Investigate the influence of adding a bilge keel, and discuss its justification on wind turbine
installation vessels.

2 Theory
This chapter will briefly summarize the necessary theory to understand the subsequent chapters
of the thesis. It is assumed that the reader will have a background from mechanical engineering
either within the field of CFD or maritime engineering and naval architecture. Therefore, the
theory chapter is divided into two parts, one accounting for maritime engineering, focusing on
roll motion, and one accounting for CFD.
2.1
Roll Motion of Ships
2.1.1
Ship Stability
The term ship stability refers the ship’s ability to maintain or regain its natural upright position
in the water, when subjected to rotations. This is a fundamental topic explaining the nature of
ship rolling. This section will focus on the stability with regards to roll motion.
Consider a ship in calm water and perfectly trimmed such that it has zero heel (no rotation
about the longitudinal axis). In this position the ship is subjected to a gravity force from its
own mass, working in the center of gravity (COG), and a buoyancy force, working in the center
of the submerged part of the vessel, called the center of buoyancy (COB). At zero heel the two
forces are exactly vertically aligned, making this the ships natural ’rest’ orientation. When the
ship is heeled with a certain angle about the water line the COB changes and creates a restoring
moment about the COG, which will force the ship back towards the natural position at zero
heel, see Figure 2.1.
An important term in ship stability is the metacentre, MT . As seen in Figure 2.1 the metacentre
is a point, defined as the intersection between the ships vertical centerline and the line of the
buoyancy force. The distance between the COG and the metacentre is given as

This section will explain the damping, both from a theoretical and practical point of view and
discuss how the non-linearity is dealt with. Until now roll motion has been denoted as η4, which

When solving the equations of motion the damping coefficient is usually calculated by use of
strip- or panel code methods, which is based on potential flow theories. This can predict the
ship motions in waves to a fairly good accuracy for the motions pitch, heave, sway, yaw and
surge. Solving for the damping in roll however does not work well with potential flow, due to
significant effects of viscous forces, causing skin friction and eddy making, whereas eddy making
is the most dominant, and is therefore an important phenomenon to capture. The eddies are
formed at the turns of the bilge1, and appendages on the hull, and is illustrated in Figure 2.2.

Free roll decay tests
Roll damping can be obtained experimentally either by forced roll or by free roll decay. Forced
roll tests offer the opportunity of measuring roll damping at any combination of frequency and
amplitude, but it also requires a substantially more extensive test setup and it is therefore rarely
done. In free roll decay tests the ship is inclined to an initial heel angle and then released to
roll freely and the decay in the oscillating roll curve is observed, hence the name of the test.
The big flaw of this setup is that the model will always roll at the natural frequency, thus the
damping cannot be examined at other frequencies. Contrary to forced roll it is however a much
more simple setup. In free decay tests the ship is often restrained from sway and yaw motions
to avoid the effects of horizontal motions. In such case the roll axis is taken through the center
of gravity.

2.3.3
Wall Treatment
Walls are a source of vorticity and accurate prediction of the flow near the wall is therefore
essential. Walls are usually applied a no-slip condition meaning that due to the viscosity of the
fluid the velocity is zero at the wall. This results in a thin viscosity-affected layer, in which
the velocity profile changes from zero to the free-stream velocity, known as the boundary layer.
Depending on the Reynolds number the boundary layer can either be laminar or turbulent. For
high Reynolds numbers the flow is turbulent, for which the the boundary layer is characterized
by unsteady swirling in the flow direction. Figure 2.3 shows the velocity profile of a turbulent
boundary layer.
Figure 2.3: Velocity profile of turbulent boundary layer
In ship rolling the damping is usully dominated by the viscous effects and it is especially im-
portant in that type of problem to accurately predict the boundary layer. To do this several
approaches exists. One approach, called low y+ wall treatment, is to resolve the boundary layer
directly by applying a very fine mesh near the wall, called prism layers, which is gradually
coarsened away from the wall. This is computational expensive and therefore it is common
to use the approach high y+ wall treatment instead, which estimates the boundary layer from
algebraic relations based on the assumed distribution of velocity, temperature, and turbulence
quantities across the layer. The non-dimensional wall distance y+ gives an indication of how
well the turbulent velocity profile in the viscous sublayer of the surface is resolved. If the y+
value is below 1 the boundary layer is well resolved, meaning that the solution of the boundary
layer is independent of the mesh resolution. The All-y+ wall treatment model uses blended wall
functions and automatically decides which wall treatment to use, based on the y+ value. If
y+ is below 1 it will use the low-y+ model and if it is above 30 it will use the high-y+ model.
If the value is in-between it will perform an interpolation between the two models. Despite
the interpolation, one should generally strive for either keeping the y+ values below 1 or above
30. The blended wall treatment makes the all y+ wall treatment suitable for a wide range of
near-wall mesh resolutions.
The y+ value gives an indication of how well the turbulent velocity profile in the viscous sublayer
of the surface can be resolved. It is a convenient way to check if the mesh at the surface is of a
sufficient resolution, or if refinement is needed. When dealing with turbulent flow and transition
modelling the y+ value should be 1 or less, in order for the solution of the boundary layer to be
independent of the mesh resolution (Langtry [11]).

2.3.4
Finite Volume Method
Finite Volume refers to the discritization scheme used to solve the governing equations. It uses
the integral form of the conservation equations, as stated in Section 2.3.1 as its starting point.
The solution domain is subdivided into a finite number of control volumes, and the conservation
equations are applied to each control volume. There are many other discritization schemes, such
as Finite Element and Finite Difference, but the Finite Volume Method is one of the most used
methods for CFD codes (Ferziger [12]). It is also the one provided by the commercial software
package STAR-CCM+, used in this project.
2.3.5
Multiphase - Volume of Fluid
Multiphase flow is the flow and interaction of several phases within the same system where
distinct interfaces exist between the phases. The term ‘phase’ usually refers to the state i.e.
solid, liquid, or gas, but may cover a wider range of fluid characteristics in CFD modelling. There
are generally two approaches to multiphase modelling, namely the Eulerian approach and the
Langrangian approach, which have different characteristics. This project will use the Eulerian
approach with the Volume of Fluid method (VOF). The Volume of Fluid Method predicts the
distribution and the movement of the interface of immiscible phases, thereby capturing the
interface.
It is therefore very useful for maritime applications, where water and air can be
considered immiscible. The modelling approach assumes that the mesh resolution is sufficient
to resolve the position and the shape of the interface between the phases. The distribution of
phases and the position of the interface are described by phase volume fractions, which is defined
as

is the total volume of the mesh cell. Thus a volume fraction is calculated for all cells, and the

2.3.6
Dynamic Fluid Body Interaction and Overset Meshing
Dynamic Fluid Body Interaction (DFBI) makes it possible to model the dynamic interactions
between a fluid and a rigid body that is free to move. By applying mass and inertia properties
of the rigid body the equations of motion of the body is solved about its COG, taking into
account the body’s interaction with the fluids and resulting forces and moments from this.
Including the motion of a body however requires the mesh to be modified. This can be handled
by Mesh Morphing or Overset Meshing. In morphing the mesh is stretched to accommodate the
movement of the body, and the interfaces between the cells in the mesh are preserved. This only
allows for small movements as the cells otherwise will experience excessive stretching. Overset
meshing allows for large body motions. The domain is divided into an overset region, containing
the body, and a background region and a mesh is created for each of them. The overset region is
placed inside the background region and interfaces between the regions’ mesh cells are created.
The overset region is fixed to the body, which is free to move based on the equations of motion
and will therefore follow its motions. Every time it moves new interfaces will be created between
the overset- and the background mesh cells.

the time step too large will result in inaccurate or wrong results and in worst case numerical
instability, making the calculation ’blow up’.
Choosing a time step too small will result in

2.3.8
Convergence Studies and Validation
To make sure that the solutions are accurate and independent on mesh resolution and time step,
convergence studies should always be performed. In a convergence study the mesh and time step
is gradually refined (independent of each other), and the influence on the variable of interest
is observed. Eventually the solution will converge as the mesh or time step is refined. The y+
value is used to check the initial mesh resolution of boundary layers along with the Courant
number to check the initial time step used, to ensure a good starting point for the convergence
study.
While CFD is a highly sophisticated tool one should still take care in accepting results without
further validation, especially when used for new applications.
Therefore validation through
experimental tests is often required in order to fully rely on the solutions, as will be the case for
this project.

Model Construction
To build a successful model many factors had to be taken into account, for which the most
important will be explained in this section. First it will be defined what constitutes a successful
model. In general, in order for a model to be a success it must be able to replicate the scaled
dynamic properties of the full scale ship for a known and realistic loading condition, as well as the
environment surrounding the ship. As this definition implies, one must be able to have knowledge
of the models dynamic properties, e.g. mass distribution, within a reasonable tolerance. Only
then will it be possible to model a digital twin and replicate the results in CFD. In addition
to that a number of low-practical criteria had to be met for the model to be a success in this
particular situation:
• It had to be done within the time frame of this thesis, effectively meaning that it should
take no more than 4 weeks to build, to allow time for the other essential parts of the thesis.
• The total costs should be within the budget provided by OSK-ShipTech and DTU (22,000
DKK)
• The materials used should offer sufficient mechanical strength and a smooth surface to
ensure a correct near-wall boundary flow.

Optimally the hull would have been machined by a CNC milling machine, but since no CNC
milling machine was available it had to be done in another way. This raised the problem of
how the complicated double-curved shapes of the hull could be achieved by hand. Inspired by
the Strip Theory the hull was divided into a number of strips, where the amount of geometrical
change between each strip would be limited.
All strips would then be glued together and
the difference between them evened out by sanding. Some of the considerations behind these
processes is elaborated below.
Ideally the strips should be cut out by a CNC machine. Laser cutting and water jet cutting was
considered. Laser cutting was technically not possible for the plate thickness used (100 mm)
and water jet cutting was not available in due time for the project. Therefore, the strips were
sawed on a bandsaw manually. A reference line for sawing was important and was created by
laser cutting pieces of paper 1:1 of the outer profile of each strip and was then used to mark up
the profile on the SIKA blocks.
Since one sides of each strip was used as a reference line in shaping, it was important that the
strips were glued correctly together and thus a method for avoiding slight rotation or shifting of
the strips were needed. The deck of the model represented a flat surface all along the ship and
could be used as a reference for ensuring rotational alignment. To ensure alignment in vertical
and transverse direction a steel rod was used as core in the ship. In each strip there was drilled a
hole in the center, with the diameter of the steel rod. Once sliding the strip on, it would be fixed
in transverse and vertical direction. The rod had several other advantages. It would increase
the strength of the model against bending, which was critical in ensuring the model would reach
the testing basin unharmed and without chance of deformation. Also the rod served as handles
for carrying the model from one place to another. In each end of the rod a 150 mm thread was
made to tighten the strips together during the gluing process, and hold the pressure to ensure
a strong binding of the glue. In order for the rod to fulfil its purpose it was essential that the
hole in the strips would be drilled exactly at the right location. To ensure that a fixture was
made for the bench drill, such that the hole would always have the same position, relative to
two reference surfaces (the deck and the port side wall).
Several glues were considered, and two glues were tested on a small scale, namely the Combiflex
524 and a water based glue called Casco Contact 3880.

3.3.1
Mass and Inertia Properties
Since the loading conditions were designed based on the base loading condition at design draught,
which comes from a real loading condition in salt water, scaling it down and testing it in a basin
with fresh water introduces some dilemmas. Due to the lower density of the fresh water the
draught becomes larger in model scale, and thus the hull area that contributes to damping
becomes larger. This was compensated by reducing the total loading, while maintaining the
same COG, until the same draught was obtained hence the conditions for the hydrodynamics
around the hull would correspond.
The reduced loading would decrease the inertia of roll,
and thereby affect both roll period and damping. To compensate for this, weights were moved
outwards from center in transverse direction, until the same inertia was obtained (initially done
in CAD software). Then the dynamic properties would still correspond to those from salt water.
Practically the mass was checked by loading the ship until it would be at design draught, 6.875
cm. Simultaneously the weights’ positions were adjusted to give zero trim. At design draught the
displacement should be 69.93 L at model scale corresponding to 69.76 kg in fresh water, assuming
that the hull shape of the model is true to the CAD. The obtained loading condition was then
modelled in CAD, from which the mass was evaluated as 66.50 kg, meaning a difference of 3.26
kg. Since the mass of the bare model from the CAD software was only based on densities of the
most obvious materials, being the SIKA foam, EPS foam and the steel rod, it did not include
additional weight from the epoxy coating and glue. From an estimate of the material usage this
would sum up to 3.3 kg (1.8 kg epoxy, 1.5 kg glue). There are also uncertainty of hull shape,
draught mark readings, and from above estimate of material usage comes also evaporation during
hardening processes. From these uncertainties the discrepancy seems reasonable in size and the
assumption of correct hull shape is maintained. Since inertia properties would be calculated
from the CAD software it was important to correct the mass in the CAD for this discrepancy.
The mass of the bare hull was therefore user-overwritten with the discrepancy of 3.26 kg.

Notes on Data Processing for Decay Tests

further analysis. The time series data from the free decay is processed in the following way:

2. All data up until the roll is initialized is discarded, as well as the first cycle of the roll, to
rule out transient effects of initializing the flow2.
3. Time have been reset to start the time after the first half roll cycle.

ing average starts to drift significantly away from 0◦, the time is cut off and all subsequent
recording is discarded. In practice the cut-off time was standardized to either 30, 25 or 20
seconds.
5. The mean of the remaining roll cycle have been subtracted from all data to account for an

4 CFD Modelling
The primary focus in the CFD modelling have been to create a model that would be able to
reliably predict the roll motion and roll damping at a high level of accuracy. The reliabilty is
measured against the experimental results and therefore the model should be a replication of
the experimental setup. This chapter will describe how the setup is modelled in CFD. Modelling
of the bilge keel is also described in the end of the chapter.
The CFD modelling have been done using the commercial software package STAR-CCM+,
provided by Siemens. This is one of the most widely used industry-leading commercial CFD
software packages, which combines the CFD modelling with pre- and post processing (mesh
generation and visualization) into one program.
The computational time of practical CFD
problems quickly becomes extensive and the use of supercomputers becomes necessary, as was
the case for this project. Computations for this thesis have been carried out on the cluster system
Niffleheim, which is a supercomputer located at DTU Department of Physics, and consists of
24,560 CPU cores with a total peak performance of more than 1,785 TeraFLOPS1.
4.1
Overall setup and procedure
This section will describe the overall setup of the model, and the general concepts of the model
that are necessary to understand for the individual subsequent sections, including the work-
procedure that have characterized the process.
Focus have primarily been placed on simulating in model scale to have the best premises to repli-
cate the model tests. Once the CFD model is properly validated there are multiple possibilities
of scaling it.
The model uses the overset method to take care of the meshing of the ship’s motions. Therefore
the model consists of two regions; a background region and an overset region.
The model
uses the Volume of Fluid model to model the interface between water and air. The simulation
domain was modelled with an approach of making it as much alike to the basin conditions as
possible, to have the best conditions for replicating the results, but with a few simplifications
to limit the computational time. The basin from the model tests was 35 m long, but was only
modelled as 6 m to reduce the computational time. This would increase the risk of influence
from reflecting waves, which was handled at the boundaries, discussed later in Section 4.3. The
upper structure on the experimental model is not included in the CFD model, again to limit
the computational time. The influence of drag on the structure from the air was investigated
and was found insignificant. This is not accounted further for here, but a short presentation
on the study can be found in Appendix E.2. Implementing these simplifications, the simulation
domain is defined with dimensions (L x B x H)=(6 x 3 x 1.2) m, and is shown in Figure 4.1,
where also the overset region is seen.
To easily simulate different draughts and heel angles, the CFD model is designed so that these
can be given as input values, without changing other parameters.

4.2
Physics
The following models are used:
• Space: three dimensional
• Time integration solver: Implicit unsteady, 2nd-order temporal discretization
• Material: Multiphase, including multiphase interaction
• Multiphase model: Volume of Fluid (VOF), including VOF Waves model
• Turbulence: Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes
• Turbulence model: SST (menter) k-ω
• Wall treatment: All y+
• Gravity
• Overset multiphase conservation
• Occasionally: Adaptive time-step
The motion of the ship is modelled with the DFBI solver, using overset meshing, which activates
the 6-DOF motion solver. Per standard, unless otherwise specified, all six DOF’s are allowed.

Time stepping
As well as for the mesh, also the time step was subjected to a convergence study, explained later

occasionally. When used it was specified to have a target mean CFL number of 0.8 and a target
maximum CFL number of 5, while also having maximum and minimum time step sizes specified.
These CFL conditions showed to give similar results to that of the converged time step.

to resolve the flow. Therefore, another volume refinement was added around each bilge keel,
in which the isotropic cell size was reduced to 20% of the overset region base size, equating to
an absolute cell size of 3 mm. Each refinement covers a cross-sectional area of 6 x 4.5 cm and
extends 1.5-2 cm outwards from the edge of the bilge keel, thus a quite limited area, to keep
the additional computational cost at a minimum. The mesh around the bilge keel is seen in
Figure 4.7a.
A finer mesh, combined with higher flow velocities caused the convective Courant numbers to
increase, and consequently a smaller time step was needed. Due to the fluctuations in the flow
velocities, the Courant numbers change quite significantly during a roll cycle, meaning that the
time step needed at the high velocities, could be much larger when velocities are small, such as
when the roll amplitude peaks. Therefore, it is obvious to use adaptive time stepping, which can
reduce the computational time significantly, while also automatically give an idea of the time
step size needed to resolve the flow. Note that the minimum time step size of the adaptive time
stepper, should be reduced for the bilge keel model.

5.1
Convergence Study
Convergence studies are performed to ensure that the solution is independent from the dis-
cretization of time and space, and should always be performed. The sequence of which time
step and mesh is studied is however a dilemma, because the solution is dependent on both time
step and mesh. The finer the mesh, the smaller the time step. Therefore instead of starting
out by a coarse mesh and going finer, this analysis will start out with a very fine mesh, and
study how coarse it can be before it will have an impact on the results. Convergence of the
time step is studied first, on basis of the fine mesh, which will ensure that the subsequent mesh
convergence is independent of the time step size. A different approach, which bypasses the time
step convergence study, is to use adaptive time stepping. The convergence study for this project
was based on the first and more manual approach. The convergence study was performed on
the loading condition with the lowest COG, LCOG2, because this would have the highest roll
angular frequency, resulting in the highest velocities, hence the most demanding loading condi-
tion to simulate. Initially, a guess on an appropriate base size was made, which was then varied
±25% and ±50%. The initial base size was 0.04 m, which means that the time step convergence
should be performed on a mesh with base size 0.02 m (-50% of initial base size). All cell sizes
throughout the mesh was defined relative to the base size, thus the entire mesh would be scaled
accordingly.
The convergence level will be evaluated by inspecting the parameter of interest, which is the
damping ratio. But since the calculation of the damping ratio does not include the roll period
or the variation in time, the roll decay curve will also be studied as a means of evaluating the
convergence level.
While studying the convergence on the parameters of interest, other parameters should be
monitored simultaneously, such as the convective CFL-number and the wall y+ values. The
CFL number is monitored in time with max-reports of 6 different planes, which reports the
maximum value in each plane throughout the simulation. y+ values are first monitored with
a max-report of the hull surface, and secondly by visual inspection of the distribution and
location of high y+ values. If they only occur in small areas they may not pose a problem, but
if they stretch over larger areas that are important to the flow, they may influence the solution
significantly.

test and low COG tests, that could well explain this difference. In Chapter 4 it was explained that
the boundaries in the wave direction was applied damping, to avoid reflecting waves. This was
however only done in practice for the loading conditions with low COG, because the reflecting
waves was not observed problematic at the loading conditions with high COG. Therefore the
higher amplitudes could be a result of reflecting waves returning from the boundaries. It was
unfortunately not possible to repeat the simulations with damping on the boundaries within
the time frame of this project. It must however be stressed that damping should be applied at
the boundaries, as the pressure outlets have shown not to work as intended, and that the wave
damping should extend as far as possible, while still maintaining a safe distance to the vessel.

6 Expanding the Model
While the previous two chapters have covered the setup and the validation of the model, this
chapter will look into further development of the model with the goal of obtaining the damping
with different approaches.
Two topics will be treated, namely full scale simulations and 2d
simulations, whereas the first have been intended throughout the thesis, but was given the least
priority, and the latter is beyond the original scope of the thesis, but could show great potential
for the future applicability of using CFD for predicting roll damping.
6.1
Going Full Scale
While everything till this point have been done in model scale, full scale is still the real use
scenario, hence the damping at full scale is of great interest.
The experimental model was
chosen of a sufficient size, such that the lack of scaling viscosity would have little influence on
the damping. Still, as explained in Theory Section 2.1.3, it has been shown that the skin friction
accounts for 8-10% of the damping for a 2 m long model and only 1-3% for a full scale ship.
This section will investigate the influence of viscous scaling effects, when going from model scale
to full scale. It will only be investigated for the loading condition LCOG2.
When the purpose is to investigate the viscous effects of the scaling, the scaling can either
be done by only scaling the viscosity itself, such that it would have the relative viscosity that
would correspond to full scale, or it can be done by scaling as it occurs in reality, where the
viscosity is kept unchanged, but all geometric relations and mass properties are scaled. It was
decided to go with the last option, as this would also give insight into the time scaling and
hence roll period at full scale, which would be an easy parameter to compare with real full scale
observations. The CFD model was much the same and only a few relations were changed. The
CAD model was exchanged with a full scale CAD model, and all volume refinements, origins of
planes, reference coordinate systems and mesh configurations were scaled with the scale factor.
Mass and moments of inertia was scaled according to Equation (2.20) and the solver time step
size was scaled according to Equation (2.21), Section 2.2. It was however expected that the
thickness of the prism layers would need to be adjusted, because the thickness of the viscous
boundary layer would not increase by the scale factor. Due to the higher surface velocities, it
was expected that the thickness would increase though. To investigate this effect 3 prism layer
configurations were simulated:

2. Prism layer thickness: 0.15 m, time step: 0.006 s
3. Prism layer thickness: 0.075 m, time step: 0.006 s

6.2
2D Slice
After having the CFD model (model scale) applied on different loading conditions, it was clear
that even though the accuracy is very high, so is the computational cost. For reference, a 20 s
simulation of the normal ship without bilge keel and loading condition low COG2 would take
47 hours, running parallel on 112 cores, distributed over 2 compute nodes (56 cores per node).
One of the common characteristic of wind turbine installation vessels is a uniform hull shape
along most of the ship’s length, which is likely to be responsible for most of the roll damping.
With a high computational cost of the 3D simulation, and a uniform shape that (postulated)
stands for most of the damping, it is proposed to perform the simulation only on the cross section
in two dimensions (2D), which would reduce the computational cost vastly. In this section the
potential of this proposal will be investigated as an initial study. The study will be bases on
model scale, loading condition Low COG 2.
The cross section can be modelled in 2D in three ways. The first way is to create the 2D mesh
from scratch, which will not have a thickness, and equations are only solved in 2 dimensions.
This would be the most optimal way, had it not been for the limitations of STAR-CCM+’s 2D
mesher, which does not include the trimmer mesher in 2d. Instead a quadrilateral mesher is
available, which unfortunately meshes quite differently from the trimmer mesher, and thus this
approach was not a viable solution. The second way is to generate a thin slice, that is modelled in
3D, and then applying symmetry condition the the boundaries in the 3rd ’undesired’ dimension.
The computational expense of solving the 3rd dimension is still present, but it is heavily reduced,
as the cells does not propagate in the 3rd dimension. This is an agile option that allows for the
same mesh configurations with the trimmer mesher and allows for easy adjustment of the mesh.
The third way was to convert the existing 3D mesh into a 2D mesh, which STAR-CCM+ has
an implemented function for. It is probably the easiest but also the least agile posibility as it
does not easily allow for mesh changes. However, since the mesh was proved well functioning at
this stage, this was the chosen approach.
The obtained decay curve from the original CFD model in 3D is directly dependent on the length
of the ship, relative to the mass properties. This dependency is however not included in a 2D
simulation, which calls for the need of transforming the mass and moment of inertia to quantities
per unit length, to be able to expect similar decay curves. This however is not straight forward,
as the stern and the bow’s contributions to the damping cannot be included, but neither can
their contributions to mass be neglected nor separated from the total mass and inertia. To solve
==PAGE:74==
60

this problem a solution is proposed. It is assumed that the damping is uniformly distributed
along the entire length of the ship, and that this can be represented by the mid-section of the
ship. To obtain the same damping, the wetted surface area of the mid-section must be the
same, thus the draught in the 2D slice, must be the same as in 3D. The draught however is
directly reflected by the mass of the ship and just dividing the mass with the length of the ship
would result in a too shallow draught. Therefore the mass must be corrected, to give the same
draught. To expect the same roll behavior the moment of inertia also needs calibration, due to
the increased mass. To correct mass and moment of inertia the following procedure is proposed:

7 Bringing the Ship into a Sea State
In this chapter the damping will be put into context, by applying the previously obtained damp-
ing values in a hydrodynamic motion study, from which maximum roll angles are calculated con-
sidering different combinations of sea states and damping models. From an industry perspective
the the roll motions are usually calculated with commercial software, such as NAPA, DNV’s
HydroD or Ansys’ Aqwa. Common for all these programs is that they have several possibilities
of specifying the roll damping. The most common options are Ikeda’s method, user-specified
linear damping or quadratic damping. The performance of these models will be compared and
discussed. The hydrodynamic motion studies are done using the commercial software HydroD,
which is a part of DNV’s software package SESAM and is developed for stability and motion
study on ships and offshore structures. The chapter will start by shortly outlining how the ship
is modelled in HydroD. For further details on the modelling in HydroD, refer to the user manual
of HydroD [14]. The ship was modelled in full scale under the loading condition LCOG1.
7.1
HydroD Modelling
Prior to starting an analysis in HydroD, the hull is discritized into a finite element model (FE)
using the pre-processing program GeniE. In GeniE the generated mesh is applied hydrostatic
pressure loads, which is essentially just a pre-definition of which side of the panels the hydro-
pressure will work on. The FE-model is then imported into HydroD and used as a panel model.
7.1.1
Environment
In HydroD the environment is first defined. A set of wave directions are given, of which the most
important for the roll instance is 90◦(beam sea). A wave spectrum is then defined, which in
short is a distribution of wave energy density for a spectrum of wave frequencies. Wave spectra
are defined as a combination of the significant wave height Hs and the zero-upcrossing period
Tz, and can be defined in several ways. The wave spectrum was defined using the JONSWAP
wave spectrum with a significant wave height of 3.5 m and different Tz values. A duration of
the sea state is specified, which is used for predicting the motions using short term statistics.
The duration was specified as 10,800 s, corresponding to three hours. Lastly as part of the
environment the water depth, density and viscosity is defined.
7.1.2
Stability Properties and Hydro Models
A set of heel angles is specified which is used to calculate the intact stability of the ship, e.g. the
roll GZ curve, which expresses the restoring moment as function of the heel angle. That is used
to calculate the linearized restoring coefficient C44. The mass is then specified along with the
COG and the moment of inertia in all three rotational DOF’s. From that the ship is balanced
and the natural draught, trim and heel is calculated by HydroD. While HydroD’s calculations
is mainly based on a panel code method the viscous roll damping is calculated using a strip
method. The hull is discretized into a number of strips in the longitudinal direction, and the
panel model will be used as a basis for generating the strips. The roll damping is then calculated

7.1.3
Roll Damping Model
The wave damping will always be calculated from potential flow using the panel model. The
viscous roll damping can be calculated either from a specified Morison model, a roll damping
model, global quadratic coefficients, or a user-specified damping matrix. If the roll damping
model is chosen the damping will be calculated as outlined in ITTC 2011 [1], which follows
Ikeda’s method if the hull type is specified as conventional, and the simplified barge method if
the hull is specified as a barge. Furthermore, when using the roll damping model, it is specified
whether the flow is laminar of turbulent, the presence of forward speed, and if the solver should
use stochastic linearization. Linearization is performed, because the equations of motion are
solved in the frequency domain, for which the program uses a linear solver. Thus the non-linear
damping needs to be linearized into an equivalent linear damping to solve for the roll response.
When the ship is brought into a sea state, which is a random process, the best method of
linearizing is stochastic linearization. Since the roll damping is dependent on roll amplitude,
which is not known prior to solving the equations, HydroD’s solver will guess on a roll amplitude
and iterate as described in the Theory chapter, Section 2.1.3.
If the damping is specified with global quadratic coefficients, only the quadratic coefficient B2 can
be given as input, when the equations are solved in the frequency domain (Wadam solver). The
quadratic damping is linearized with stochastic linearization to solve in the frequency domain.
For more details, refer to the manual of HydroD’s solver Wadam [15]. The following damping
models was investigated: roll damping model, conventional (Ikeda’s method), roll damping
model, barge (ITTC 2011, barge), global quadratic damping, and linear damping matrix.

This discussion brings a question mark to the validity of today’s state of the art roll damping
prediction methods, the ITTC 2011 Ikeda’s methods for conventional ships and barges, and to
whether they are a conservative choice of damping model. Especially the part that questions
the conservatism of the models opens a discussion on operational safety at sea for the vessels
that are currently operating.
On this topic some additional comments will be added.
The
conditions for the short term analysis was chosen as a combination of realistic conditions and
extreme conditions that would trigger large motions. From the scatter diagram of The North
Sea, it is clear that zero up-crossing periods of 8.5 s or larger is very rare for any value of Hs
and the scale of the scatter diagram only goes to 10.5 s. Therefore, the largest differences under
realistic conditions is at 8 s. The relative deviations are still large, but the absolute deviations
are limited. The loading conditions was chosen with a low, but still realistic COG, such that the
natural period would be closer to realistic wave periods, thus triggering large motions. However,
based on information from Cadeler A/S, most loading conditions have higher COG than this,
hence increasing the distance between the natural period and the wave periods that are likely to
occur. Summing up, this investigation shows that the roll damping methods provided by ITTC
2011 overestimates the roll damping for the investigated wind turbine installation vessel Wind
Orca, especially the barge model. It also shows that the likelihood of the conjunction of the roll
natural period, and wave periods likely to occur, is small due to the high COG of wind turbine
installation vessels.

8 Conclusion
The overarching aim of the thesis was to develop a CFD model that could predict the roll
damping of wind turbine installation vessels. To validate CFD results, model tests had to be
conducted and for that purpose a model scaled 1:80 of Cadeler’s Wind Orca was build and tested
under various loading conditions. The results from the model tests was generally deemed to be
of a high quality and to be considered a reliable benchmark for validation.
A CFD model was build in the program STAR-CCM+, which was done in model scale, as a digi-
tal twin of the experimental setup, to create the best conditions for replicating the experimental
results. A convergence study on time step and mesh was conducted, which showed that a fine
mesh and a small time step is necessary for obtaining a converged solution. The CFD model
was able to replicate the experimental results to a high level of accuracy for all investigated
loading conditions. It was found that wave damping must be added to the boundaries in the
wave-propagating direction, to avoid effects of reflecting waves. To answer the overarching aim
of this thesis, it must be concluded that CFD certainly can be used as a method for obtaining
reliable predictions of the roll damping.
With a general accordance between the experimental- and CFD results, it was found that the
damping increases non-linearly with the roll amplitude, and experimental results indicated that
the damping might increase for small amplitudes, though this was not validated with the CFD.
It was shown that the damping was significantly higher at the loading condition with the lowest
center of gravity, indicating that vessels with high center of gravity, such as wind turbine instal-
lation vessels, have a smaller roll damping. This finding is in good alignment with the findings
of Kawahara [3], who came to similar conclusions when investigating a wide-breadth-shallow-
draught pure car carrier.
The influence of adding a bilge keel was investigated to get insights into the benefits of the bilge
keel itself and the use of CFD for the purpose. A modular bilge keel was designed and tested
experimentally, and a slightly modified bilge keel was modelled in CFD. While the CFD was
able to accurately replicate the experimental results, the computational time was significantly
larger, due to the need of a finer mesh near the bilge keels. The bilge keel showed a significant
increase in the damping, with the potential of reducing roll angles between 18-40%. It must
however be stated that the investigated bilge keel was quite large in comparison with other bilge
keels, and it has not been investigated if such a large bilge keel in reality is practical.
While the model was build in a certain size, following IMO recommendations, to avoid significant
viscous scaling effects, an initial investigation of this effect was carried out by extending the
CFD model to a full scale simulation. This revealed significant influence on the roll damping,
which was much smaller in full scale. Since the full scale model was not subjected to a complete
convergence study, the results are not fully reliable, but strongly indicate that the viscous effects
of a 2 m long model are significant, at least for this ship type. Though it was not possible to
investigate further within the time frame of this thesis, this should be investigated in depth.
Different approaches to describe roll damping from experimental results have been investigated,
by analytically solving for a 1-DOF free roll decay in the time domain, where it was found that

and it is concluded that this is inappropriate to be used for wind turbine installation vessels.
From the results it is difficult to conclude on the reliability of Ikeda’s method, due to the
variation in the deviations. Lastly, on the roll damping, it must be concluded that applying a
simple damping of 5-10% of the critical damping is a bad and non-conservative approximation,
and should be avoided. The damping models’ influence on the roll angles is quite significant
when the encountered wave frequency is near the natural frequency, but becomes less important
when those are distanced from each other.
The natural period of wind turbine installation
vessels are typically relatively long, due to the high center of gravity. Therefore, the likelihood
of experiencing such long wave periods is small in protected seas and coast-near areas where the
vessels typically operate. Therefore, the choice of damping model might not in practice mean
that much, nonetheless it is important to keep this in mind for future wind farm projects.
Extreme value predictions was also performed on the case with bilge keel, which support the
conclusions on the models’ reliability from the original ship. Furthermore, it shows that the
increased damping with bilge keel makes a significant impact on the extreme value predictions
of the roll angles, which were reduced significantly close to the natural period, though again
with a limited effect outside this region. Considering the societal needs and forecasts of the
increase in offshore wind, the wind farms are likely to move to more open oceans and, with the
introduction of floating wind turbines, possibly deeper seas, which might increase the likelihood
of wave periods near the vessels’ natural period. Furthermore, to meet the future demands, it is
crucial to maximize the vessels’ operational weather window. In this light, the presence of bilge
keels on this vessel type is not a distant thought. It is from these investigations not possible to
provide a solid justification of the use of bilge keels on wind turbine installation vessels, though
the results definitely provide sufficient reason to justify a deeper analysis of the concept.
Due to the high computational costs, it was investigated if the simulations could be performed
in 2D. It was found that while it reduces the computational time vastly, the current 2D models
deviates too much from 3D, and further calibration of the model is needed for this concept
to be viable. It could however prove as an time-efficient approach, especially for initial design
purposes, such as investigating and optimizing design of bilge keels and hull shapes in general.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
% ------------------- RAW DATA PROCESSING SCRIPT -------------------------
% This script analyzes the raw data files from repetitive tests. it will
% sort out all data prior to the roll motion begins incl. the first roll
% cycle. Also it will subtract the mean of the part of the time series of
% interest, such that mean is in zero, and the different dataseries are
% comparable.
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------
clc
clear all
close all
% Importing data:
data1=importdata('FileName_test1.txt');
data2=importdata('FileName_test2.txt');
data3=importdata('FileName_test3.txt');
t_cutoff = 20; % Time period of decay to consider
% DATA SERIES 1:
t1=data1.data(:,5);
% time series data
roll_1=data1.data(:,9);
% roll amplitude data [deg].
[roll_1,zeta1,Phi1]=ResetDecay(roll_1,t1,t_cutoff); % processing
% DATA SERIES 2:
t2=data2.data(:,5);
% time series data
roll_2=data2.data(:,9);
% roll amplitude data [deg]
[roll_2,zeta2,Phi2]=ResetDecay(roll_2,t2,t_cutoff); % processing
% DATA SERIES 3:
t3=data3.data(:,5);
% time series data
roll_3=data3.data(:,9);
% roll amplitude data [deg]
[roll_3,zeta3,Phi3]=ResetDecay(roll_3,t3,t_cutoff); % processing
% Exporting processed data to text files:
Rolldata = table(roll_1,roll_2,roll_3);
writetable(Rolldata,'Rolldata_COND_X.txt','Delimiter','\t');
Dampingdata_log = table(zeta1,zeta2,zeta3);
writetable(Dampingdata_log,'Dampingdata_log_COND_X.txt','Delimiter','\t');
Dampingdata_ext = table(Phi1,Phi2,Phi3); %*** (see comment)
writetable(Dampingdata_ext,'Dampingdata_ext_COND_X.txt','Delimiter','\t');%***
% *** Only used if data processed with Himeno's method
%% ------------------------ FUCNTIONS ------------------------------------
function [roll,zeta,Phi]=ResetDecay(roll,t,t_cutoff);
[maxval,idxmax]=max(roll);
[minval,idxmin]=min(roll);

% starts positive or negative
roll=-roll(idxmax:length(roll));
else
roll=roll(idxmin:length(roll));
end
[pks, idx] = findpeaks(roll,'MinPeakProminence',2);
% Identifying peaks
% and their indexes, which in fact is Min values
roll = -roll(idx(1):length(roll)); % Sorting everything until first peak

E CFD - Modelling and Results
E.1
k-ω versus k-ε
The two turbulence models performance was investigated by applying each of them in the CFD
model.
In Figure E.1.1 they are compared against experimental measurements at the base
loading condition at design draught, 5.5 m. It is seen that both models perform quite well, but
that k-ω is a bit closer to the experimental data. Note that this was done at an early stage in
the project, where the COG from experiments was not yet known, and the convergence study
was not yet finalized, which explains why even the k-ω deviates more than what has previously
been presented.
Figure E.1.1: Roll decay for the k-ω and k-ϵ turbulence models compared against experi-
mental measurements.
E.2
Influence of air on upper structure
The influence of air on the upper structure on the experimental model was investigated by
modelling the structure in CFD. To do this the domain and the overset region was extended in
the z-direction. The resulting mesh is seen in Figure E.2.2. As seen the domain is extended quite
significantly, and consequently the number of mesh cells increases well. Hence, the computation
time was significantly longer. The decay curve from the simulation, compared to the simulation
without the structure, is shown in Figure E.2.3. It is seen that the two curves are coincident for
most of the decay, and deviations are only visible at the peaks in the beginning of the decay. It
is noticeable that the maxima and minima actually is larger with the structure. This does not
make particular sense, as the air should add additional damping to the system. Due to the small
differences, this was not investigated further. Due to the relatively small deviations, especially

Matlab script containing the solver:
function dx = rollEoM(t,x,I44,a44,b44,B1,B2,B3,c44)
% This function solves the equation of motion for roll of the vessel in regular
% waves
F4tt = 0;%interp1(dur,F4mw,t); % Interpolating the excitation moment function
% at the time integration steps of the solver
% Creating the system of two first order non-linear differential equations
% from the initial second order differential equation
dx(1) = x(2);
% TURN ON 8 order poly-fit:
%dx(2) = ((1/(I44+a44)))*(F4tt-b44*x(2)-c44*x(1)); % Linear damping
dx(2) = ((1/(I44+a44)))*(F4tt-(B1*x(2)+B2*abs(x(2))*x(2))-c44*x(1)); % Quadratic
% damping
%dx(2) = ((1/(I44+a44)))*(F4tt-(B1*x(2)+B2*abs(x(2))*x(2)+B3*x(2)^3)-c44*x(1));
% Cubic damping
dx=dx';
