Abstract 
Since 2010, the bike carriage on the S-trains in the Greater Copenhagen Area has been free of charge. This 
new offer became very popular among the passengers in the S-trains, and quickly after the implementation 
of the free bike carriage, the number of bicycles as well as the number of passengers began to increase.    
The advantages of the combined use of bikes and public transport is as follow: bike is flexible and fast 
transport mode in the congested cities, while public transport has advantages on the long-distances. The 
combined use of bike and train is contributing to more flexible door-to-door solution that public transport 
alone cannot give to its users. Moreover, this combination transport mode has a potential to reduce the 
use of private car, thus, less congested cities.  
However, bike has the problem with availability at activity-end of a trip, i.e. from the S-train station to 
activity, for example workplace or shopping center. Therefore, the share of bike trips from (or to) the  
S-train station at activity-end is lower compared to home-end (i.e. from home to the S-train station). The 
size of this problem has been reduced when the Danish railways allowed the free bike carriage on the 
suburban S-trains. 
This project analyzes the change of the travel patterns among the inhabitants living in the Greater 
Copenhagen Area where the suburban S-trains are operating. The existing body of knowledge about the 
combined use of bikes and public transport is very limited, which makes this project unique.  
In this thesis, the passengers’ travel patterns before and after 2010 were analyzed. The analyzes were 
based on the Danish National Travel Survey (TU) data that contains detailed data information of the trips 
made by individuals with residence in DK. The analyzed time period is 2008-2013 and the data sample 
contains 52,811 trips in the Greater Copenhagen Area.  
The descriptive analyzes revealed that the main travel motive of the S-train passengers is work and leisure, 
as well as there are more women than men and more young people in age 16-32 among the S-train users. 
Besides, working commuters represent a major part of the passengers that are taking bike to and from the 
S-train station (52% at the home-end and 60% at the activity-end), probably because those type of 
passengers are seeking the most seamless transport solutions that can be comparable to private car.  
The popularity of bike in combination with public transport is high among the passengers in the S-trains and 
less popular among the metro passengers, since the Copenhagen Metro has time and route restrictions 
with regard to bike carriage as well as a bicycle ticket is required. The share of bikes at the home-end 
among the train passengers in the Greater Copenhagen Area is more than two times bigger compared to

the share of bikes at the activity-end, however, the difference in the bike shares at the home-end and the 
activity-end is lower among the S-train passengers, which is one of the indicators of the success of DSB’s 
free cycle strategy. According to the TU-data, the majority of the S-train users cycle to the S-train station if 
it is located further than 1.7 km from home but within a distance of 3.5 km. If the distance is longer than 
3.5 km, most of the S-train passengers choose bus as an access (or egress) mode to the S-train station.  
The analyses have also showed that the share of bike trips to and from S-train station has increased both at 
the home-end and the activity-end after 2010, while the share of bus and car trips to and from S-train 
station has decreased after the strategy implementation, especially at the home-end, which also indicates 
the success of the cycle strategy.  
The estimation results have revealed that the free bike carriage has a significant impact on the probability 
of taking the S-train in combination with bike, while it is insignificant for the two other combination 
transport modes, S-train and walk, and S-train and other transport modes (primarily car), which was 
expected. The proximity from home to the S-train station plays an important role while choosing one of the 
three modes of combination transport, and the higher proximity is associated with lower probability of 
taking the S-train in combination with other acces modes. The estimation results have also revealed that 
living in the apartments as well as being couples with and without children is associated with the lower 
probability of taking the S-train regardless access mode. 
The main conclusion that can be drawn from this thesis is that the inhabitants living in the Greater 
Copenhagen Area have changed their travel patterns caused by the DSB’s free cycle strategy. The people 
began to use bike more as an access and an egress mode to and from the S-train station, and the 
probability of taking the S-train with bike has increased after the implementation of the strategy. However, 
the free cycle strategy did not increase the probability of taking the S-train.

1 Introduction  
Six years ago, the Danish Railways (DSB) abolished bicycle ticket on the S-train and introduced free bicycle 
carriage on the suburban S-trains. Quickly afterwards the initiative became very popular among the S-train 
users, which is reflected in the number of passengers taking the S-train with and without bicycles that has 
been growing since the strategy was implemented.  
 
Figure 1-1. DSB S-train’s marketing campaign from 2010.  
Before the implementation, DSB made improvements of the accessibility to the railway stations for cyclists. 
Furthermore, parking facilities for bicycles have been upgraded and extended. Without those changes and 
improvements, the free cycle strategy could not be received as well among all S-train passengers (with and 
without bikes) as it has been according to the results of the customer satisfaction survey made by DSB 
(DSB, 2015b).  
The free cycle strategy makes it easier to choose environmentally friendly transport mode such as public 
transport and bicycle for everyday activities. In the past several years there have been many investments in 
the bicycle infrastructure including parking facilities and super bicycle highways. Public transport has also 
been updated with several purpose to increase number of users in the public transport network. All those 
improvements can help municipalities to get healthier citizens and better environment with less congestion 
in cities and less traffic noise (Transportministeriet, 2013). It is not surprising that the free cycle strategy 
has received a big attention from the politicians including the Danish Ministry of Transport, since the 
inhabitants in the Greater Copenhagen Area were offered a new flexible transport solution that is not 
existing in other places in the world (Transportministeriet, 2013).

Since the introduction of the free cycle strategy, DSB has conducted various behavior campaigns, which aim 
was to teach passengers travelling with bicycles how to act at platform and aboard at the S-train. 
Moreover, DSB has evaluated the free bike carriage by interviewing the passengers with and without bikes. 
However, no statistical analyses have been made of the strategy with regard to the changes of the 
passengers’ travel behavior before and after the strategy implementation. This thesis is contributing to a 
new knowledge about the strategy’s impacts and its importance for the Danish society. The free cycle 
strategy is unique in itself because only the Danish Railways are offering its passengers free bike carriage 
with almost no time or route restrictions. On a global scale, the knowledge is also very limited, which also 
makes this thesis exceptional.  
1.1 Problem statement 
This particular thesis analyzes the changes in the S-train passengers’ travel patterns in the Greater 
Copenhagen Area before and after the implementation of the free cycle strategy using the Danish National 
Travel Survey (TU-data) provided by DTU Transport. More specifically, the project’s focus is to examine the 
importance of the cycle strategy with regard to the probability of taking the S-train including the S-train in 
combination with bike. The project is limited to the Greater Copenhagen Area, thus, home municipality of 
the respondent in the TU-data is located within this area. The analyzed time period is from 2008-2013.  
Following hypotheses has been formulated during the upstart phase of the project:  
1. It is expected that the share of bike trips from home to the S-train station and from the S-train 
station to activity has increased after 2010.  
2. It is also expected that some of the passengers switched from bus (or car) to bike as a feeder 
transport mode to S-train station.  
3. Furthermore, it is likely that the demand for the S-train trips has increased after the 
implementation of the free cycle strategy, which is reflected in the larger number of trips in the S-
train.

2 Background  
The aim of the following section is to introduce a combined transport mode (bike and public transport) and 
describe its main characteristics, including its benefits over private car mode as wells as its drawbacks. The 
section is structured as follows.  Section  2.1 summarizes main characteristics of the combined use of bike 
and public transport. Section 2.2 describes various strategies in promotion of the multimodal transport. The 
implementation of the free cycle strategy as well as the description of the Greater Copenhagen Area are 
presented in Section 2.3. The last section, Section 2.4 presents existing literature on the combination use of 
bicycle and public transport.     
2.1 Combined use of bike and public transport 
Denmark has an old tradition for cycling, especially in the major cities as Copenhagen, which is officially 
called as the first Bike City in the world (The official website of Denmark, 2015). The Danes use bicycles not 
only for sport activities, but also (and mostly) for everyday activities such as getting to job or going for 
shopping or other leisure activities (Politiken, 2012). 
Bike is a fast and flexible mode of transport especially at relatively short distances, while public transport 
has its strength in long-distance trips (Transportministeriet, 2013). The combination of those two transport 
modes is contributing to environmentally friendly and more flexible door-to-door transport solution, also 
over longer distances. If used as a combined transportation form, bike and public transport, can contribute 
to lower the overall congestion in cities (Transportministeriet, 2013) as this combination transport mode 
can be a good alternative to private car (Martens, 2004). 
The combined use of bike and public transport for one trip refers to so called bike-and-ride (Martens, 
2004). The bike-and-ride can take several forms: the bike can be used as a feedering mode for access trips, 
for egress trips or for both trips. Bike-and-ride can help solving one of the main problems in public 
transport: the accessibility of railways stations and bus stops (Martens, 2004).  
Bike-and-ride benefits over the private car can be categorized as environmental and social benefits 
(welfare) (Martens, 2004). The environmental benefits include reduced use of fossil fuels, reduction in air 
and noise pollution. In addition, bike-and-ride can contribute to lower congestion level and it can limit the 
need for park and ride facilities at railway stations (Martens, 2004). The social benefits include the 
improvement of the economic performance of public transport and as results, the potential attraction of 
new customer groups (Martens, 2004). This implies, however, that the public transport expands its capacity 
to keep the “old” passengers and to attract the “new” ones; otherwise, the public transport network can 
get congested, thus, reduced mobility of the travelers and as a result, decrease in the demand.

In Denmark, 27% of the passengers in public transport are cycling to a railway station, while only 8% of the 
passengers are using bike from the station to the destination point (Transportministeriet, 2013). Bus as a 
feedering mode is used by 20% of the passengers on the way to the station and 18% from the station to the 
destination. However, bus is more popular among the passengers living more than two km from the 
railways station, while the bike is more preferable on the distances less than two km from the station 
(Transportministeriet, 2013). This phenomenon is known as an asymmetry problem: private access modes 
such as car or bike are usually only available at the home-end trips1, but not at the activity-end trips (Keijer 
& Rietveld, 2000). Since the availability of bike at the activity-end trips is limited, the share of walk and bus 
is higher at the activity-end trips than at the home-end trips.  
Even though the availability of bike at the activity-end trip is limited, the bicycle has some alternatives such 
as:  
 
bike sharing system at railway station, 
 
the owner’s second bike is parked at the railway station, and  
 
bike carriage on train. 
These three alternatives are usually associated with high costs for the passengers using those services; and 
therefore, it can be difficult to find a good alternative for bike at the activity-end trips (Keijer & Rietveld, 
2000).  
On contrast to many other countries, Denmark has a unique offer to the cyclists on the suburban trains in 
Copenhagen (S-trains) – bike carriage on the S-trains is free and the offer is even available in rush hours 
(Nørreport station2 is an exception because of limited capacity of the station). Therefore, the bike carriage 
on trains can be a good alternative to bus or walk at the activity-end of trips.

One of the practices is focusing on marketing and communication of the bike in combination with public 
transport. At the most general level it includes global strategy and campaigns that provide necessary 
information about the intermodality. Public transport tickets should be integrated with bike-and-ride 
activities including bike carriage on public transport and bike sharing system at train stations. Another good 
practice is to provide the citizens a journey planner that can compare different transport modes with each 
other, which in some cases can encourage people to choose bike instead of public transport or car.  
Another good practice is focusing on bike carriage on public transport. However, the 24 countries that had 
participated in the survey have different regulations depending on the kind of bicycle (conventional, folded, 
electrical etc.). Regulations also vary among public transport modes. For instance, most of the European 
cities do not allow bringing a bike aboard busses. However, there are several exceptions including 
Copenhagen, for example lines 150S or 500S.   
Bike carriage on commuter trains and light rails is allowed in the most countries, but usually with some 
time or/and route limitations. Many transport authorities prohibit bike carriage during peak-hours due to 
capacity problems. For instance, in Copenhagen, it is not allowed to travel with bike in metro during 
morning rush hours (from 7.00 to 9.00) and evening rush hours (from 15.30 to 17.30) (Wonderful 
Copenhagen, 2015). The problems with capacity can be managed by vehicle adaption allowing users to

combine bike with public transport on their daily activity trips such as trips to work or education

Besides time limitations, there can be several route limitations of bicycle carriage on public transport even 
though they are not common in Europe. In Copenhagen, it is forbidden to bring a bike aboard S-train at 
Nørreport station during peak-hours on weekdays (Wonderful Copenhagen, 2015).  
Bike facilities at stations are very important elements in the combination trips. Especially, security-watched 
rooms/areas are popular among the passengers because they feel more confident to leave their bikes at 
stations. Repair services can also help in assisting bike-riders and stimulate the use of the combined bike-
public transport (Greenways Product, 2014).  
Bike sharing system can be an alternative to bike carriage on public transport. The system can be managed 
by railway operator, private operator or public authority.  
It can be concluded that the promotion of bike-and-ride need to involve different promotion strategies 
(marketing, infrastructure and vehicle adaptations, and facility services at stations) and most importantly 
cooperation among different transport authorities, train operators and municipalities that can support all 
those strategies.  
An example of the bike-and-ride project in Europe is Bike-Train-Bike project 
(BiTiBi), which has started in four cities in Europe including Barcelona and Milan. 
The project has many partners all over Europe, also in Denmark.  
The project is inspired by the Dutch model and it seeks to solve the problems with 
parking spaces at train stations, ineffective fare system or the lack of it as well as 
absence of bicycle friendly access to railway stations and lack of “last mile” transport option at the activity-
end trips (BiTiBi, 2015). One of the BiTiBi project goals is to substitute car trips with the combined transport 
(train and bike). Especially, the project is focusing on the home-end and activity-end trips made by bicycle.

make a comparison between those two countries by looking at their bike-and-ride promoting strategies. It 
should be noticed that later in the project only data from Denmark will be used for the analyses, while the 
knowledge about the promotion of the bike-and-ride use in the Netherlands will be used as an inspiration

The Netherlands  
The Netherlands has a unique cycling culture, which is one of the most famous in the whole world (News, 
2013). The country has more bicycles than people living in the country (Nederland Fietsland, 2016); and it 
has a great infrastructure for cyclists with their own traffic lights and intersections. The cycling has a special 
attention from the transport planners; however, they also understand that the cycling cannot carry the 
whole sustainable transport development.  
The Netherlands promotes the combined use of public transport and bike by the nationwide bike sharing 
system called OV-fiets (Openbaar Vervoer) bike meaning “public transport bike” in Dutch (AmsterdamTips, 
2015). The system has been introduced in 2002 and since, it has been a big success among its users, 
especially among the business commuters, as their share is almost 50% (Ministry of Transport/ Public 
Works and Water Management, 2009). The OV-fiets is owned by the Dutch Railways (Nederlandse 
Spoorwegen) that can offer to the users of the system low prices. The OV-bicycle can be rent for €3.5 (26 
DKK) per day if the user has a yearly subscription that costs €10 per year (75 DKK) (NOS, 2015).  
One of the key factors of the OV-fiets popularity is availability at railway stations, low cost, integrated 
charge system with the public ticket and no own risk of bicycle theft. The Dutch Railways has also plans to 
expand the bike sharing system at big bus stations and city centers (Ministry of Transport/ Public Works 
and Water Management, 2009).  
According to the Dutch survey about the OV-fiets conducted in 2011, 54% of the city residents began to use 
train more because of the OV-fiets and 46% switched from bus/tram to bike for the “last mile” for the 
egress trips (Nanninga, van Zeebroeck, & Imbert, 2014).  
The Netherlands is also known for its bicycle parking policy. This policy is mainly focusing on parking 
facilities at big transport hubs, e.g. railway stations (see Figure 2-2 from Den Haag). The bicycle parking 
facilities can be found everywhere in the Netherlands, and in the last few years there have been huge 
investments in the parking facilities for the bicycles (Godefrooij, 2012). The quality of the parking facilities 
can be evaluated based on user needs such as ergonomics, weather protection, protection against theft 
and vandalism, free or low cost for parking, proximity to railway station (Godefrooij, 2012).

Denmark 
Denmark has also a long tradition of cycling and it has its own cycling strategy called “Denmark on your 
bike!” published by the Danish Ministry of Transport in 2014. The key point of the strategy is promotion of 
cycling for everyday activities and for leisure (Transportministeriet, 2014). The strategy is also pointing out 
the importance of safety and education. Cycle highways are popular in the country and the government 
allocated resources for building new cycle superhighways and better parking facilities for bikes.  
As in the Netherlands, Denmark has also a bike-sharing system called “Bycyklen”, which means “city bike” 
in Danish. The system was launched in 1995 in Copenhagen, and was one the first large scale bike-sharing 
system in the world (Wikipedia, 2015a). Due to its inefficiency, the bike-sharing program was abolished in 
October 2012, but two years after a new program has been introduced in Copenhagen, because of the 
pressure by the Danish Cyclists Federation and the tourist organization Wonderful Copenhagen (Wikipedia, 
2015a). The Bycykler is delivered by GoBike, the company that also had delivered the bike-sharing system in 
Barcelona, Utrecht and Stavanger. The Danish Railways (DSB) is one of the business partners in the Bycyklen 
project, and members of DSB Plus customer program can save 10 DKK per reservation of bicycle (Bycyklen, 
2014).  
This new bike-sharing program is offering white electrical bicycles featured with GPS navigator. The bicycles 
can be rented for 25 DKK (€3.35) per hour with a regular user account and for 6 DKK (€0.80) per hour with a 
monthly subscription that costs 70 DKK (€9.4) per month (GoBike, 2015). It is also possible to pre-book the 
bicycle for a fee of 10 DKK (€1.34). The pre-booking is free for the members of the DSB Plus customer 
program. Compared to the Dutch OV-fiets, the City Bikes in Copenhagen is more expensive and less 
popular, which can be caused by the fact that the bicycles can be carried for free on the suburban trains in 
Copenhagen.

2.4 Previous scientific work about bike-and-ride 
The next sub-section describes the existing literature about the combined use of public transport with bike. 
This literature research will be used as an inspiration to the analysis in the particular project.  
Most of the existing work has focused on the access transport modes to railway stations with either a 
modelling approach (Brons, Givoni, & Rietveld, 2009; Debrezion, Pels, & Rietveld, 2009; Givoni & Rietveld, 
2007) or a descriptive approach (Keijer & Rietveld, 2000; Martens, 2004). However, the body of knowledge 
on bike-and-ride trips is very limited.  
The next section summarizes the main conclusions from the existing literature about bike-and-ride and bike 
as a feeder mode and a combined mode with public transport.  
In general, the level of bike-and-ride is higher in the countries with well-developed bicycle networks and 
facilities (Martens, 2004). For instance, Denmark and the Netherlands have many similarities with regard to 
the bike culture, and both countries has a high bicycle share of all trips – 26% in the Netherlands and 19% in 
Denmark (Ministry of Transport/ Public Works and Water Management, 2009)7.

Travel purpose 
The main trip purposes of bike-and-ride are work and education, and other everyday activities. Train as a 
mean transport mode in the combination with bike is more important for the commuters travelling to 
work, while the combined use of bike and bus is more important among students. This means that the 
commuters are usually overrepresented among bike-train passengers and the students are 
overrepresented among bike-bus users (Martens, 2004).  
The share of bike-and-ride is significantly lower at the activity end trips. Data from the Netherlands shows 
that the share of bike trips at the activity-end can be four to nine times lower compared with the home-end 
trips (Martens, 2004). According to the same data, the usage of bike in access trips is highest among the 
commuters travelling to work or education by train as mean transport mode; while the highest level of bike 
usage in egress trips is among the students travelling by bus.  
Share of bike in access trips  
According to the previous research, the share of bike in access trips to suburban train was 22% in 
Copenhagen in 1993, while in the egress trips from station to the final destination the bike share was 
around 4%. The bicycle share in access trips to regional train is a bit higher in the Netherlands compared to 
Copenhagen – 30% and 25% accordingly. For the busses, the bicycle share in access trips is much lower for 
both countries: 14% in the Netherlands and 12% in Copenhagen.  
Travel distance 
The relationship between type of public transport and level of bike-and-ride can be explained by travel 
distance (to railway station or bus stop) as one of the key factors (Martens, 2004). Faster types of public 
transport such as railway attract passengers from a lager distance because of higher travel time savings; 
thus, longer access and egress distances. Data analysis from the Netherlands, Germany and UK has shown 
that two thirds of all cyclists using trains as a main mode travelling more than two km to the nearest 
railway station. In the Netherlands, 79% of all home-end8 trips start within a distance of 5 km from the train 
station, and 80% of all activity-end trips end within a distance of 5 km from the train station (Keijer & 
Rietveld, 2000).

Summary of Background  
The level of bike-and-ride is correlated to the overall use of bike in a country. The bike-and-ride trips can be 
an alternative to private car or to public transport in areas with low population density. The share of bike-
and-ride relates to type of public transport: faster types of public transport attract more bike-and-ride 
users compared to lower quality types of public transport. The distance is another factor that has an impact 
on the level of bike-and-ride use: faster modes of public transport may attract passenger from an area of 
up to 5 km, while for lower types of public transport the distance is significantly shorter – up to 3 km.  
Bike-and-ride’s main issue is the asymmetry problem, since bicycle and other private access modes are 
usually only available at the home-end trips and not at the activity-end trips (Keijer & Rietveld, 2000). That 
is the share of bike at activity-end trips is significantly lower compared to the share of bike trips at home-
end. DSB’s cycle strategy makes it possible to reduce the size of the asymmetry problem, since the people’s 
travel behavior changes. Moreover, the strategy affects the number of changes between bus and train, 
since this number will be reduced as people will make changes between bike and train.  
The experience from the Netherlands shows that the bike-sharing system at railway stations, the OV-fiets, 
is contributing to higher bicycle share in egress trips. Denmark has another strategy to promote combined 
use of bike and public transport – through the free cycle strategy that allows the S-train passengers bring 
their bike for free on S-train, also in rush-hours. Both countries are trying to promote the combined use of 
bike and public transport, since it can be an alternative to private car, thus, more environmental benefits.

3.1 Definition of home-end and activity-end  
In this thesis, home-end trips are defined as access or egress trips to or from train station from or to home, 
so one of the trip ends is home (see the figure below).  Activity-end trips can also be either access or egress 
trips to or from train station, but from or an activity, which is not home based. An example of the home 
based trip is a trip from home to office. An activity-based trip can be a trip from office to shopping mall.  
In this particular project, this definition is necessary since the TU-data9 (the Danish National Travel Survey 
data) used in this thesis contains information about the whole round trip (from home to activity and from 
activity to home). This modeling approach is called the home-activity modeling approach, in which both the 
home-end and the activity-end taken into account (Hoogendoorn-Lanser & van Nes, 2005). Another 
modelling approach uses access and egress trips without distinguishing whether or not a trip end is the 
traveler’s home or activity address which can be useful in discrete choice model (Hoogendoorn-Lanser & 
van Nes, 2005).

3.2 Treatment effect 
The treatment effect is used to describe some outcome of interest that has been affected by some 
treatment (Newey, 2007). In this particular project, the outcome of interest is the choice of taking the S-
train or not, while the treatment is represented by the DSB’s free cycle strategy. Technically, the outcome 
of interest is the left-hand side variable (dependent variable) in statistical model and the treatment effect a 
right-hand side variable (independent variable). In general, the analysis of the treatment effect is useful 
since it can be used in prediction of the possible consequences of the effect or policy (Angrist & Pishke, 
2009).  
The treatment effect in this study is described as a binary variable, 𝐷𝑛= {0,1}, where n represents 
individual. 𝐷𝑛 is equal to 1 if an individual has been treated, and 0 if not. In the data, used for the analysis, 
the dummy variable called “After 2010 Dummy” corresponds to the treatment effect of the cycle strategy, 
and it is equal to 1 for all the individuals after 2010 (2010-2013). In the analysis, it has been assumed that 
the inhabitants in the Greater Copenhagen Area began to change their travel behavior during the first year 
of the implementation. 
Two other variables are also necessary in explaining the treatment effect. The first one, 𝑌𝑛0, describes the 
potential outcome of interest when individual n is not treated (𝐷𝑛= 0); the other variable denoted by 𝑌𝑛1 
also describes the potential outcome of interest but only occurs when individual n is treated (𝐷𝑛= 1).

3.3 Discrete choice models 
The section gives a short introduction to the types of discrete choice models used in the thesis. Those 
models are logistic regression model and multinomial regression model. 
Discrete choice models are models that is used to predict the probability that an individual choose one 
alternative among a finite set of alternatives. A decision maker can be an individual (e.g., a train passenger), 
a group of individuals (e.g., a family travelling for leisure), a corporation (e.g., a train company), a 
government, etc. A choice set of alternatives should has following characteristics to be within a discrete 
choice framework (Train, 2009c):  
1. Mutually exclusive, which means that choosing one alternative automatically implies not choosing 
any of the other alternative.  
2. Exhaustive, which implies that all possible alternatives are included in the choice set. 
3. Finite, meaning that the number of alternatives must be finite.  
 
Attributes or characteristics of the alternatives are usually derived from the revealed preference data (in 
this case, TU-data), which is type of data that is based on the actual travel behavior. In model developing 
phase it is important to include the attributes that could be important for an individual when choosing a 
transport mode.

The demand can be inelastic, unitary elastic and elastic. Inelastic demand means that the demand does not 
change with price change, thus, greater potential of consumer surplus (Economics, 2015). Unitary elastic 
demand is a situation where there is an equally proportional change in demand because of price change –

product. If the demand is elastic, it implies that consumers are not willing to pay a higher price for the 
product and as a result, the demand will fall more than proportion.  
 
3.5 Reported statistics 
A variety of statistical tests can be applied to an estimated model. Following section briefly introduces the 
statistics reported in the project.  
3.5.1 Signs of parameters 
It is most common to examine sign of the estimated parameters with regard to theory or intuition. For 
example, it is expected that travel time or cost variables to have negative sign of coefficients, implying that 
the utility of a transport mode decreases as travel time and/or cost increases. This also implies that the 
choice probability of the corresponding mode will became lower.  
3.5.2 Standard error  
Standard errors are stated in brackets for all parameters in all models. Standard error measures data 
variation in the dataset. If a standard error is close to zero it implies that the data points tend to be very 
close to the mean value of the dataset, while if the standard error has a high value it indicates that the data 
points have a wide range of values.  
3.5.3 Log-likelihood 
The log-likelihood of the model can be used to compare the models. However, the log-likelihood values 
cannot be used alone because these values express a function of parameters. Higher log-likelihood values 
are better, since the log-likelihood function has to be maximized.

Summary of Descriptive Statistics  
The descriptive statistics has shown that there are more women in the sample than men. Among the S-train 
passengers, there are even more women than men – almost 60% of the S-train passengers are women. 
According to the analysis, the average income of the S-train users is lower compared to the sample, which 
can be explained by the fact that the young people in age 16-32 are contributing for a large part of the 
passengers in the S-trains as well as there are many singles and couples without children take are taking the 
S-train. The level of education in the sample is relatively high – over the half of the people in the sample are 
highly educated, which is also the case for the S-train passengers.   
The availability of cars offers people the opportunity to choose this transport mode as the primary 
transport mode on the trip or as an access mode to train stations. The analysis has shown that the majority 
of people living in Copenhagen (over 50%) owns a single car in the household, around 19% owns two cars 
and around 2% owns three or more cars, and one third does not have a car. Among the S-train users, the 
majority (46%) do not have a car in the household, and about 44% own one car and the rest of the 
households have two or more cars. The lower car ownership among the S-train travelers suggests that the 
people using the S-train has relatively low income compared to the sample. The bike ownership in the 
sample as well as among the S-train users is very high and it has been over 73% in 2008-2013.  
The relatively high car ownership in Copenhagen is also reflected in relatively high number of trips made by 
car as a main transport mode - the majority of all trips in the Copenhagen Area is made by car, while walk 
and bike accounts for around 18% and 20% of all trips, respectively. The public transport including the S-
train, metro and other trains is used in around 11% of all trips.  
The main travel motive of the inhabitants in Copenhagen is leisure and errand, while most of the S-train 
users are taking the S-train to work or to leisure activities. The working commuters represent a major part 
of  the passengers that are taking bike to and from the S-train station (52% at the home-end and 60% at the 
activity-end), probably because those type of passengers are seeking the most seamless transport solutions 
that can be comparable to the private car.  
Bicycle as an access and egress mode to S-train station 
The combination of bike with public transport is specifically popular among the passengers in the S-train 
and less popular among the metro passengers (probably due to time and route restrictions for bike carriage 
in metro and a charge for bike carriage), while walking is the most important transport mode both at the 
home-end and activity-end. According to the TU-data, the bike share is more than two times higher at the 
home-end than at the activity-end, since bike is not always available on the trip to the activity, thus, 
==PAGE:56==
Anna Ildutova  
March 2016 
DTU Transport 
Page 55 of 98 
 
availability problem of the bike. However, the extent of the problem is less among the S-train passengers 
than for the whole Copenhagen Area, which can be caused by the free cycle strategy in the S-train.  
The majority of the S-train users take the bike if the S-train station located further than 1.7 km from home 
but within a distance of 3.5 km. If the distance is longer than 3.5 km, most of the S-train passengers choose 
public bus as an access (or egress) mode. The changes in travel behavior can also be observed by analyzing 
at the confidence interval of the average distance travelled by bike at the activity-end –there is more 
variation in data after the strategy implementation, specifically after 2012.  
The share of bike trips to and from S-train station at the activity-end has increased after 2010 especially for 
the distances larger than 2 km.  
Time trends  
The time trend analysis has revealed that the car ownership among the S-train passengers has decreased 
after the strategy implementation (44% before 2010 and 51% after 2011), which can be indicate that some 
of the S-train users have abolished their cars. This trend is not general for the Greater Copenhagen Area.  
Among the S-train passengers, the bike-and-ride trips became more popular after 2010, thus, higher 
market shares of those types of trips compared to the number of bike-and-ride trips in combination with 
other public transport modes such as other trains than S-train, bus or metro. This can be another indicator 
of the success of the free cycle strategy.  
In addition, the number of bike trips to and from S-train station has increased both at the home-end and 
the activity-end after 2010, while the number of bus trips and car trips to and from S-train station has 
decreased after the strategy implementation, especially at the home-end.   
According to the TU-data, the share of bicycles carried on S-train has increased from 16% to 46% in the 
time period 2009-2013.  
 
The observed trends in the data will be further investigated by applying discrete choice modeling to analyse 
if the free cycle carriage on the S-trains and other factors have had a significant effect on the S-train use.

5 Model results 
The chapter is organized as follows: first, the explanatory variables are introduced; then, model 
specifications and models results from the logistic and multinomial regression models are presented as well 
as the interpretation of the results.  
The models have been estimated using the same dataset that contains of 52, 811 trips. The modelling 
approach applied in the logistic regression models as well as in the multinomial logit models is tur-
modelling approach. The analyzed time period is 2008-2013.  
All the models contain the distance variable and the year variables – these are described below. Another 
variable that is specifically interesting, an interaction variable between the distance and the year dummy, is 
only included in the logistic regression model.  Moreover, some of the models include socio-economic 
characteristics such as gender, income, age, family type, accommodation type, education and two weather 
variables (maximum temperature and number of rainy days up to trip).  
Each trip in the TU-data has weights that guarantee that the sample is representative to the whole 
population in Denmark. The models in this project are estimated with and without weights; however, the 
model results were not specifically different from each. The results presented in the following section 
derives from the weighted models, while the results of all non-weighted models are presented in Appendix.   
5.1 Dependent variables  
The dependent variable in the logistic regression model describes whether the trip was made by the S-train 
or not. In the multinomial logistic model, there are three response variables that represent three 
alternatives with the S-train as the main train mode. Those alternatives are S-train in combination with 
walk, S-train in combination with bike (bike-and-ride trip) and S-train in combination with bus.  
In both models, the response variable is discrete, because the purpose of the models is to analyze whether 
the event occurred or not, such as choice of transport mode.  
5.2 Explanatory variables  
The explanatory variables are the variables that can explain or cause the effect, in this case, choice 
between S-train (in the logistic regression model) and other transport modes. Those variables are 
introduced below.

5.2.1 Distance from home to the nearest S-train station 
One of the most important variables in the model, the distance variable, describes the proximity from 
home to the nearest S-train station (at the home-end). This attribute is assumed to be more important than 
the proximity from S-train station to activity (e.g. workplace) in the modelling context, since the choice of 
the main transport mode (S-train) will most likely be made before the person leaves his or her home.     
The distance variable has been transformed using the logarithmic function, since the distribution of the 
original distance variable is skewed. The logarithmical transformation makes the skewed distribution less 
skewed, thus, more normal, which gives a better model fit (see figures below).  
 
Figure 5-1. The histogram of the distance variable (in km): left – the original variable; right – logarithmical transformation. 
 
5.2.2 Treatment effect 
The dummy variable called “After 2010 Dummy” derives from the year-variables and the variable takes 
value 1 if the year is equal or greater than 2010 and represents the treatment effect of the free cycle 
strategy. As mentioned, it has been assumed in the analysis that the passengers had adapted their travel 
behavior within the implementation year, since the free cycle strategy was introduced in January 2010. In 
this project, this variable represents the treatment effect of the cycle strategy.  
5.2.3 Interaction variable 
Exposure to the intervention (also called as the interaction variable) (Heinen, Panter, Mackett, & Ogilvie, 
2015) was defined as the logarithm of the distance from home to the nearest S-train station multiplied with 
the “After 2010 Dummy” variable (the treatment effect). The variable is also interesting, since it indicates if 
the distance became more or less important after implementation of the free bike carriage. This variable is 
only included in the logistic regression model, and in the sensitivity analysis of the MNL model.

5.2.4 Other control variables 
Other control variables included in the models, are the socio-economic characteristics of individuals as well 
as the weather conditions that could be specifically relevant for the trips made by bike. The socio-economic 
characteristics included in the models are gender, income, age, family type, accommodation type, 
education. Moreover, two weather variables (maximum daily temperature and number of rainy days up to 
trip) are used in the statistical models.  
The other types of the control variables in this study are the year-dummies (2008-2013), which are included 
in the models because they can capture the general trends. For instance, those variables capture the 
growth in the population in the Greater Copenhagen Area as well as the development in the number of 
trips made by S-train and by bike.  
 
5.3 Logistic regression – Probability of taking the S-train 
In the logistic regression model, the probability of taking S-train is estimated as a function of the 
explanatory variables such as the proximity to the S-train station (also called distance variable), the year-

as follows: the first model includes the distance and the year-dummies including the “After 2010 Dummy”

characteristics of an individual as well as the weather variables; the third model adjusted for distance, years 
and interaction; and the fourth model is an expansion of the third model – with only difference that Model 
4 adjusted for the socio-economic characteristics and the weather. The model specifications for the logistic

8 Discussion  
The results of the project are based on the TU-data containing the information about the trips made by the 
inhabitants in the Greater Copenhagen Area. The TU-data is used in many Danish projects in the 
transportation sector; however, the detail level of the data is very limited in the project’s context, since the 
number of trips with the S-train is small compared to the other modes of transport, for instance, car. The 
extent of the problem is even larger for the bike-and-ride trips in combination with the S-train, and 
thereby, bigger uncertainty of the analysis where those trips are included as it is a case with the MNL 
models where one of the alternatives represents bike-and-ride trips in combination with S-train.  
Besides the limited size, the data is missing information about trip prices or generalize cost that is expected 
to be one of the most important factors in the transport choice modelling. For instance, it could be valuable 
to see if the reduced price of the S-train trip for the passengers with bikes had contributed to the higher 
probability of taking the S-train among the bike-and-ride users (the new ones as well as the existing 
customers). However, this type of data was not available in the TU-data. 
The estimated models are based on the assumption that all the independent variables are exogenous 
meaning that they are not correlated with the random error term in the model and the independent 
variables are not affected by each other in the model, thus, no correlation. As mentioned, the estimated 
model does not have a trip price attribute or a generalized cost attribute, and therefore, the error term in 
the model can be correlated with the unobserved attribute, which can have an impact on the model results 
(Reed College, 2015).  
The models were estimated using weights available in the TU-data. In the model estimation, it has been 
assumed that the weights are correct and they can scale to the whole population level in Denmark. The 
estimated models with and without weights did not gave marginal differences in the results, however, the 
use of the weights reduce the uncertainty in the models all things being equal.   
All discrete choice models were estimated based on the assumption that the proximity from home to S-
train station is important factor when choosing transport mode. The variable that describes the proximity 
to S-train station is not linear in attributes because of its skewed distribution. The variable has been tested 
both as linear in attributes and as part of the original variable where only the proximity less than 3 km was 
added into the model. All tests have shown more or less the same results with the proximity being 
significant at 10% level, which confirms the robustness of the results.  
The free cycle strategy can be considered being a natural experiment because all inhabitants in the Greater 
Copenhagen Area were exposed to the new reform without knowing about it in advance, which is a big

benefit.

the Greater Copenhagen Ares have changed their travel patterns after 2010. The share of bike-and-ride 
trips at the home-end and the activity-end has increased, and according to the analysis, the new trips by 
bike derive from bus and car, so the bus trips and car trips to and from the S-train station was replaced by 
trips by bike. This is in line with the results of the customer satisfaction survey that DSB conducted in 2012-

9 Conclusion 
Since 2010, the Danish railways have introduced free bike carriage in the S-trains in the Greater 
Copenhagen Area. The implementation process was almost seamless and it has resulted in the passenger 
growth as well as increase in the number of bicycles carried on the trains. In addition, the Danish 
Government has made investments in bicycle parking facilities at train stations in an effort to promote the 
bike-and-ride with public transport.  
Existing knowledge about the combined use of bike with public transport is very limited, but the interest of 
the topic is growing. This particular master thesis has contributed to the understanding of the changes in 
travel pattern among the S-train passengers caused by the free cycle strategy. The project’s findings 
indicate that the combination of bike with public transport is specifically popular among the passengers in 
the S-train compared to the other modes of public transport. In general, the bike-and-ride trips can have 
the availability problem of the bike, especially at the activity-end. However, the size of the problem is 
smaller among the S-trains passengers, since their freely can bring their bikes on the S-trains and make the 
“last- mile” of the trip by bike.   
The analyses have shown that most of the bike-and-ride users in combination with S-train cycle to the S-
train station if it is located between 1.7 and 3.5 km from home. For the longer distances, most of the 
passengers choose bus as an access (or egress) mode. After the implementation of the free cycle strategy, 
the popularity of the bike-and-ride trips among the S-train passengers became higher compared to other 
modes of public transport (other trains than S-train, bus or metro), which is reflected in the higher market 
shares of those types of trips before and after 2010.  
The estimation results have revealed that free bike carriage on the S-trains has not significant impact on 
the probability of taking the S-train. The proximity from home to S-train station is important factor while 
choosing the S-train – this attribute is highly significant and it is associated with lower probability of taking 
the S-train, as expected.  
However, the free cycle strategy has significant impact on the probability of taking the S-train in 
combination with bike – the probability of taking the S-train with bike became higher after 2010. The 
proximity from home to the S-train station plays a significant role while choosing one of the three modes of 
combination transport, S-train with walk, S-train with bike and S-train with other transport modes 
(primarily car) – the higher proximity is associated with lower probability of taking the S-train in 
combination with other modes of transport. The estimation results also reveal that living in the apartments 
==PAGE:77==
Anna Ildutova  
March 2016 
DTU Transport 
Page 76 of 98 
 
as well as being couples with and without children is associated with the lower probability of taking the S-
train regardless access mode (walk, bike or bus).  
The main conclusion that can be drawn from this thesis is that the inhabitants living in the Greater 
Copenhagen Area have changed their travel pattern because of the DSB’s cycle strategy. The people began 
to use bike more as an access and egress mode to and from S-train station, however, the passenger growth 
in the S-trains since the 2010 caused by the general trends in the society, and not by the free cycle strategy. 
The free bike carriage has increased the probability of taking the S-train in combination with bike, but all 
things being equal the free bike carriage has no impact on the probability of taking the S-train.
