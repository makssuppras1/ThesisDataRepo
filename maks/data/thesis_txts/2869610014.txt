Abstract
Achieving net­zero emissions by 2050 requires high efforts for the electrification of energy
consumption, but also it demands the decarbonization of the hard­to­abate sectors that
are challenging to electrify, such as heavy transport. This thesis examines the Danish
energy system, focusing on the production of renewable fuels under different land use
patterns. By employing Geographic Information System (GIS) for gathering and manag­
ing data and the Balmorel­Optiflow sector­coupled modeling framework, the study inves­
tigates how different land­use patterns influence the availability of lignocellulosic biomass
and CO2 emissions.
The research focuses on biofuels,e­fuels, and e­biofuels. While biofuels are the least ex­
pensive to produce, their dependence on biogenic carbon intensifies the competition for
biomass resources among different sectors. In scenarios without land­use change mea­
sures, biomass is also allocated to BECCS, which emerges as the most cost­effective
approach for achieving negative emissions, a key factor for offsetting emissions from in­
dustry and CHP production that rely on fossil fuels when integrating output from VRES.
The three land­use change measures are modeled in the Optiflow model: the extrac­
tion of carbon­rich soils, the establishment of protected forests, and the conversion of
agricultural land into productive forests. With a cap on emission reduction via land use
measures set at 6.5 Mtons per year, the cost­optimal solution is achieved by diverting
0.45 Mha of agricultural land (18.2% of the current agricultural area) for productive forest
establishment. This approach not only enhances biomass availability and delivers cost­
effective CO2 sequestration but also reduces biomass transport costs when forests are
strategically located near renewable fuel production facilities.
The optimal renewable fuel mix for decarbonizing heavy transport is found to comprise
bio­jet fuel, ammonia, and e­jet fuel produced via e­methanol upgrading.
Furthermore, even though targeting additional protected forest areas and the extraction
of carbon­rich soils is not the cost­optimal solution, it still results in a 17% reduction in total
system costs compared to the scenario with no land­use changes, while simultaneously
contributing to European biodiversity protection targets, increasing danish protected area
by 61500 ha.

Introduction
According to the revised pathway for net zero emissions published by the International
Energy Agency (IEA) in the World Energy Outlook 2022 [1], in the Net Zero Emissions
(NZE), in 2050 electricity will become the ”new oil”. This means that according to the
scenario, electricity will have a dominant role in the final energy consumption, replacing
fossil fuels.
However, there are sectors for which it is not feasible or cost­effective to electrify directly
with the technologies currently available or likely to be commercialized in the coming
decades. Energy­intensive industries, requiring high­temperature heat, together with the
segment of the transport sector which comprehends aviation, maritime transport, and
heavy trucks are planned to be decarbonized via modern bioenergy, Hydrogen, and car­
bon capture and storage. Also, district heating, fueled by modern bioenergy, is used as
an asset for supplying a part of the building’s energy demand.
In the NZE scenario, hydrogen production is expected to become nearly fivefold from
today to 2050 and the bioenergy supply, limited by sustainable potential, is expected to
increase by 2.5 times.
Electrofuels, e­biofuels, and biofuels will be used as assets to link the power and the hard­
to­abate sector, electrifying them indirectly. Biofuels are produced starting from biogenic
carbon, electrofuels, instead are produced via carbon or nitrogen sources together with
hydrogen, for the fuel to be carbon neutral, hydrogen must be made via electrolysis, using
electricity produced from renewable sources. The carbon source of the electrofuels can
either come from carbon capture or biomass, in that case, e­biofuels are obtained[2].
Carbon capture technologies can capture the CO2 either from point source emitters or
directly from the air, with Direct Air Capture (DAC) technologies[3].
Although it’s proven that these technologies will play a role in the energy transition, the
path is not free of obstacles.
Historically, biofuels have been cheaply produced from food crops, however, they are
being phased out in the European Union (EU) due to issues connected to land use change
and sustainability [4]. Advanced biofuels based on lignocellulosic biomass show potential,
but at the state of today, no commercially operational production exists. [5] The potential
development of electrofuels is highly dependent on the price of electricity and on the
availability of capacity for the production of carbon­neutral power.[2]
To address these uncertainties, many analyses have been conducted studying renewable
fuels in energy system models, evaluating different pathways with different geographical
scopes.
For the transport sector, the main outcomes of the analyses show that a transition to low­
carbon fuels is necessary for the system to meet the carbon neutrality targets. The most
suitable options identified are biofuels, power­to­liquid fuels, and hydrogen­based liquid
fuels[6]. However, many of the illustrated decarbonization pathways encompass the use
of high biomass potentials[2] [7], there is still a big uncertainty regarding the sustainable
availability of the resource[6].
Regarding biomass availability [4] points out that, for lignocellulosic feedstock, scarcity is
a cost driver. Moreover, including alternative uses of biomass rather than the production

of biofuels, shows that, when low carbon sequestration potential is available, the biomass
is used for industry and as an asset for flexibility for the combined production of heat
and power. From this competition, an opportunity cost arises. These results show that a
sector­coupled approach to modeling is necessary for taking into account the competition
between sectors for the use of biomass and its relative costs.
Moreover, it is inferred that modeling of land­based carbon capture and sequestration
measures like afforestation and reforestation has been overlooked. It is shown that these
measures could play a role in carbon capture and sequestration [8] at lower costs than in
other sectors contributing also to multiple targets like biodiversity preservation and safe­
guarding of the water streams[9]. However, it is also true that these measures could alter
the biomass potentials, which is considered an exogenous factor in the analyzed studies.
This study aims to include a representation of the land­use, agricultural, and forestry sec­
tor to sector coupled energy system modeling framework, in order to study the effect of
different land­use patterns on the availability of lignocellulosic biomass and on carbon
dioxide emissions.
The study will focus on the country of Denmark and two models will be used: the energy
modeling framework Balmorel and the network model Optiflow[7]. The analysis will in­
clude the power sector, district heating and individual users, and the industry sector of
the chosen country.
The land­use sector and the renewable fuels production chain will be handled with a
municipality­level geographical resolution. The choice of high geographical detail has
been done to account also for the transport costs of the feedstock, which, in the case of
biomass represent a high cost per energy value [7].
1.1
The Danish Case
Denmark has been an early leader in decarbonization, in 2022, a net zero target has
been announced for 2045 and the government is aiming at a 110 % emission reduction
for 2050, to the 1990 level[10]. Due to its ambitious targets, the country was chosen as a
case study; moreover, biomass plays a significant role in the Danish energy supply.
In 2019, the Total Energy Supply (TES) of the country has been covered by 39% from
Renewable sources [11], including wind, solar, biofuels, and waste. The latter category is
the second energy source in the mix, providing 175 PJ. This share of renewables in the
TES has significantly increased in 2023, reaching the value of 48.9%. The category of
biofuels and waste provided 210 PJ of TES.
In 2019, the majority of the bioenergy consumed was represented by primary solid biofu­
els; they include fuel wood, wood chips, wood pellets, and straw, and they covered 75%
of the bioenergy consumption. Other types of sources are Municipal solid Waste (12%),
Biogas (7.5%), Biodiesel, and Biogasoline (5% in total). [12]
Primary solid fuels have been mostly used for Combined Heat and Power (CHP) and heat
production plants (127 PJ), particularly for replacing fossil fuels for district heating. They
have also been used for residential applications, with quite a stable trend in consumption.
The consumption in the industry sector, instead, has been marginal.
In 2018, it was agreed that by 2030 minimum of 90% of Danish district heat should be
based on other sources than coal, oil, and gas. The measure is reflected in the con­
sumption trends, as these fossil sources have been declining greatly and substituted with
biomass. [12]

Denmark, as a country, plans to expand renewable fuel production. In the final update
of Denmark’s National Energy and Climate Plan [10], it’s stated that Denmark aims to
become a net exporter of green energy including hydrogen and power to X fuels. Power
to X production of renewable fuels will also be enhanced to comply with ReFuelEU Avi­
ation [13], Maritime regulation [14] and the Renewable Fuels of Non­Biological Origins
(RFNBO) EU regulation [15].
Denmark is also planning on managing its land to pursue targets regarding biodiversity or
emission reduction from the Land Use, Land Use Change and Forestry (LULUCF) sector.
This is likely to require a change in the land use patterns of the country whose impact on
the energy sector is yet to be determined.
In fact, according to the revised regulation of emissions by sources and CO2 removals
by sinks in the land sector, Denmark is allowed to reach her targets for CO2 emissions
reduction with the contribution of a maximum of 14.6 million CO2 credits from LULUCF in
the period 2021­2030 [16].
These credits are planned to be reached via multiple measures, which could be permanent
changes (including afforestation and managed carbon­rich soils) or temporary changes
(like improving manure management or improved crop rotation) [10].
The Danish Council on Climate Change report on future Danish land use [9], aims to
shed light on how the use of land in Denmark will change to comply with different cli­
matic requirements, such as the Danish Climate Act and the EU’s biodiversity strategy.
The result of the analysis presents three scenarios, aiming at different targets such as
emission reduction from land, protection of biodiversity, and requirements for the water
environment. In all these three scenarios the result is that achieving those targets brings
numerous benefits and that the cost is quite low compared to the price of emission reduc­
tions in other sectors; however, the studied measures require subtracting land from other
sectors, mainly the agricultural one.
Although the Danish Council on Climate Change identified the extension of the areas and
their future scope for all the analyzed scenarios, which specific areas will be subtracted
from the agricultural sector, to serve other purposes have not been officially identified
yet and are still open for discussion between the government and the interested sector
players.
Synergies between the agricultural and forestry sectors and the energy sector could play
an important role in the definition of such areas, but currently, the multipurpose aspect of
land has been overlooked when talking about energy system analysis.
This analysis aims to shed light on the topic, using a high­resolution geospatial represen­
tation of the Danish land, to understand how future land use patterns can affect the energy
sector, starting from the case of the estimation of the impact on biomass availability.
1.2
Representing Land in Energy System Models
To better choose how to structure the analysis, a literature review has been conducted to
analyze the land­energy nexus in energy system modeling frameworks. It is inferred that
the way land is modeled in energy system frameworks is project­specific.
A common approach is modeling land implicitly, as a constraint for the potential of the ca­
pacity for technologies like wind turbines and PV panels, the technologies are given a land
use factor per capacity unit and constraints are placed on the maximum area available
[17] [18] [19].

This approach, however, doesn’t characterize land in the model, not allowing it to ad­
dress the synergies and competition of the energy sector with other sectors, such as the
agricultural sector.
To address coss­system dynamics, different approaches for integrated analysis have
been developed.
One is linking different models that cover different aspects, like energy, economy, or hy­
drogeology for the land part [20] [21].
To avoid the link between different models, another approach is to exploit the flexibility
of energy system models as tools to describe also other dynamics, like the ones of the
Climate­Land­Energy­Water nexus [22]. Specifically, the energy systems model OSe­
MOSYS has been widely used in the literature for analyzing the nexus in different case
studies, also regarding bio­energy and renewable fuels [23] [24].
Another option to assess the links between different sectors is using integrated assess­
ment models, which avoid the need for soft­linking separate complex models. This is the
case of the MUSE framework, which has been enhanced with a specific model section
dedicated to the agricultural, forestry, and land use sectors. In [8] the framework is used
for assessing, along with the biomass potentials, the role of reforestation alongside de­
carbonization measures in the energy system in achieving carbon mitigation pathways,
for the case study of Brazil.
Among the copious reasons for developing an agricultural and land use sector model part,
the authors mention the big uncertainties related to the estimates of biomass potentials
and the environmental impacts of using such a resource (e.g. indirect land use change,
the increased land use competition); also, the intent of deepening this research field is to
mitigate the uncertainty regarding costs and risks concerning measures like biochar, soil
carbon sequestration and reforestation/afforestation as large scale emission mitigation
tools.
Despite the advantage of providing a comprehensive overview by integrating multiple sec­
tors within a single model, Integrated Assessment Models (IAMs) often lack sufficient geo­
graphical and temporal resolution. The MUSE framework is a global model, which divides
the entire globe into 28 geopolitical regions.
Given the objectives of the analysis, a combined representation of multiple sectors is
essential while also ensuring the flexibility to achieve high geospatial and temporal reso­
lution.
To meet these requirements, the sector­coupled energy modeling framework Balmorel
[25] has been selected for its flexibility. It allows for the direct integration of multiple ’add­
ons,’ enabling a multi­sectoral analysis without the need for soft­linking additional models.
For this study, the energy modeling framework has been co­simulated with the network
model Optiflow [7], which has been extensively used for research on renewable fuel pro­
duction [26] [2].
This network model allows for the easy integration of additional processes, making it
straightforward to expand the analytical boundaries to include other sectors, such as the
land­use sector while maintaining high geographical and temporal resolution.

2
Methodology
The framework chosen for the analysis is Balmorel, which was hard­linked with the net­
work model Optiflow.
In Optiflow, the renewable fuels and renewable gas production chain, the utilization and
storage of carbon dioxide, DAC technologies, and the part of the land use regarding the
lignocellulosic feedstock for biofuels are modeled.
Balmorel optimizes the supply of the exogenously provided demands for power and district
heating. For the analysis, three ”add­ons” of the model are used; specifically hydrogen,
industry, and individual users.
The hydrogen add­on accounts for the balance of the hydrogen production and consump­
tion, the industry add­on includes the industrial demand for high­temperature heat in the
simulation, and the individual­users add­on accounts for the heat demand of the areas
not connected to district heating.
2.1
GIS Analysis
Given the model’s high geographical resolution, extensive data research has been nec­
essary. To streamline this process, GIS has been employed for data management, as it
has been proven in the literature to be a beneficial practice [27].
For the analysis, the open­source software QGIS[28] has been used in combination with
Phyton to handle layers of diverse types like shapefiles or raster files.
2.2
Balmorel
Balmorel is an open­source, partial equilibrium, energy system optimization modeling
framework [25].
The model entails a comprehensive representation of technical components in the current
energy system and allows simultaneous optimization of both investments and operational
decisions for dispatch. It optimizes the conversion of energy sources to electricity and
heat, the optimal capacity of storage and power transmission.
The objective function of Balmorel is to maximize social welfare. Balmorel can be run
in several modes economic dispatch, unit commitment, discrete binary investments for
economy of scale, and myopic or perfect foresight within a year and between years. In
this analysis, the framework is used with a perfect foresight approach within the year of
optimization.
The framework version used for this analysis is the same used for the study of Kountouris
et al. [29], and the data forming the model are available at this repository [30].
2.3
Optiflow
Optiflow is a spatiotemporal network optimization model, which may represent any net­
work e.g. energy, mass, monetary, or emissions.
Optiflow is an open­source tool that can model networks with user­defined spatial and
temporal resolution [7]. This particular feature is quite important for the scope of the
Analysis of the impact of Danish land use patterns on renewable fuel production and CO2 emissions
5
==PAGE:16==
analysis, where distributed resources like biomass need to be studied in an energy system
with high Variable Renewable Energy Sources (VRES) penetration.
Optiflow can perform optimization based on a multi­criteria approach, however, in this
specific case, the model is hard­linked with Balmorel, therefore, the two models share the
objective function which is to minimize the total system cost.
Optiflow is formulated as a generalized network model, based on node­arc relationships.
The processes are modeled as nodes, connected through flows as the arcs [7]. A process
can represent any physical or mathematical conversion process, flows can represent any
physical quantity and its properties, including monetary or emissions flows.

2.5
Geographical and temporal Resolution
The focus of this study is 2050. Four significant weeks were selected: S02, S15, S28,
S41; for each week the following hours were considered: T073, T079, T085, T091, T097,
T103, T109, T115, T121, T127, T133, T139. These data were then weighted on the year
to obtain timesteps for the whole extension of the simulation. The choice of these weeks
and hours is done since these timesteps appear to be representative of the whole year
[31].
To account for power imports and exports, Sweden, Norway, and Germany are modeled
in the Balmorel, together with Denmark. The geographical resolution in Balmorel is power
market regions, which for Denmark results in two areas DK1 and DK2. Since renewable

Since the model deals with distributed resources like biomass, for which the transportation

Having different geographical resolutions in the two models implies a simplification, how­

The technologies modeled in Optiflow are very innovative and, in many cases, not com­
mercially available at the state of today; therefore a green field approach is used to han­

is assumed that no capacity for renewable fuels production is considered to be already
installed in Denmark. Instead, a brown­field approach is used for the energy generation
technologies managed in Balmorel, meaning that the existing capacities of generation
technologies are included in the simulation, and their use can be dispatched by the model,
in accordance with technology lifetime constraints.

that were produced on the land, need to be imported. If imports of cereals are explicitly
modeled in the analysis, with a demand to supply, for the rest of the goods the term
Cimports,agriculture,y accounts for the imports.
Cimports,agriculture,y is computed as shown in Equation 3.5. In the equation:
• Vagri¬cereals,2020 is the gross value of the agricultural output excluding cereals in 2020
[38],
• β corresponds to 1+20%, 20% is the estimated cereal price percentage increase
estimated by IPCC for 2050. [44], it is assumed to hold true also for the other types
of food,
• γ corresponds to 1+10%, being +10% the cost percentage difference between im­
ported goods and domestically produced ones, which is assumed to be equal to the
value computed for wheat[49], [38],
• δ corresponds to 52% which is the share of agricultural land, that is not dedicated
to cereals farming.

To understand why the emissions don’t even out in the scenarios where land use solutions

sensitivity analysis is performed on the Biodiversity+CO2 Scenario with fossil fuels alter­
natives, varying the price of natural gas by +/­50%.
As shown in Figure 5.11, at low gas prices, the system becomes carbon neutral since the
natural gas is used for boilers and CHP production technologies. On the contrary, for the
case with the highest gas price, the solution is carbon negative and the CHP production

6
Discussion
6.1
Discussion of the Assumptions and Future Work
In all the scenarios conducted in this analysis, the energy production from VRES covers
a high share of the demand. However, the production from technologies like photovoltaic
panels or wind turbines is not dispatchable and depends on time variables. It must be
taken into account that, for representing the 2050 year in the model, some representative
weeks and hours have been chosen, it is important to highlight that, the outcome of this
analysis could be modified under different time­step choices.
Agricultural patterns and efficiencies are assumed to remain unchanged, and the de­
mands for cereals and wood products are held constant. However, these parameters
could be influenced by shifts toward alternative forestry and agricultural practices such as
increased plant­based production. Moreover, not all food produced in Denmark in 2018
was consumed domestically [21]. The demand figures are based on historical domestic
production levels, which include exports whose future trends are uncertain. Additionally,
since 85% of Denmark’s cereal and grain consumption is used as animal feed [57], a shift
towards a higher share of plant­based agriculture might affect not only demand and land
potentials for non­farming uses but also manure availability.
For this analysis, manure was treated as an exogenously determined potential, however,
the livestock sector also affects land use and GHG emissions. Future work should con­
sider incorporating the livestock sector into the model.
The different geospatial resolutions of the two models introduce approximations, particu­
larly since electricity, hydrogen, and heat production are not accounted for at the munic­
ipality level, this prevents this analysis from accounting for the transport costs of these
carriers. Furthermore, point source CO2 managed in Balmorel and then passed to Opti­
flow, was assumed to remain in the same municipalities in 2050 as historically observed.
Changes in the level of detail within the Balmorel model could therefore modify these
outcomes.
Biogas production is not considered cost­effective in this analysis. However, since only
CO2 emissions were accounted for, including other greenhouse gases in the analysis
might alter the results. For example, improvements in manure handling, such as biogas
production, offer additional climate benefits [16] that were not modeled here but could
influence the findings.
The costs associated with diverting agricultural and forestry land for alternative uses en­
compass both the direct costs of the implemented measures and the opportunity cost
related to the value of the sector being replaced, as illustrated in Equations 3.1 and 3.2.
It should be noted that a significant portion of the agricultural sector is not explicitly mod­
eled but is instead represented through cost parameters, whereas the energy sector is
modeled in greater detail.
The optimization results are driven more by energy­related factors, such as biomass avail­
ability, than by agricultural factors. In particular, the analysis shows that converting agri­
cultural land to productive forests is very profitable. Moreover, the locations where land­
use changes are implemented are heavily influenced by the renewable fuels production
chain; for instance, most of the new productive forest is located near the municipalities
Analysis of the impact of Danish land use patterns on renewable fuel production and CO2 emissions
35
==PAGE:46==
where biofuels can be produced. It cannot be ruled out that incorporating greater detail
in modeling the agricultural sector could change these results.
It must also be considered that many of the obstacles to applying land use change are not
economical, instead, they regard social and cultural aspects which are outside the scope
of this analysis.
Geological sequestration of carbon dioxide and biochar production do not appear to be
cost­effective CO2 sequestration measures in this analysis. However, since some emis­
sion sources—such as those from the livestock sector—are not included, additional se­
questration capacity and methods might be required if these emissions were considered.
CO2 transport is not used in the scenarios. In this analysis, the modeled way of trans­
porting carbon dioxide is via trucks, however, also the option of pipe transport is possi­
ble. This last solution is optimal for medium to long­distance transport, it implies lower
transport costs per kilometer and transported units but requires investment costs for the
infrastructure[26]. In future work, also this technology could be investigated.
It is important to note that the sequestration rate of newly implemented land use change
measures depends not only on the initial and final land covers but also on how the mea­
sures are maintained and managed [41].
In this analysis, land use change measures are assumed to be permanent, following the
assumptions made by the Climate Council [9]. The CO2 sequestration rates applied here
are taken directly from the same report [9]; however, sequestration rates for land use
change measures could also be quantified with higher detail by considering changes in
carbon pools (such as aboveground biomass, below ground biomass, dead organic mat­
ter, and soil organic carbon) [8]. In particular, for new productive forests, factors like the
forest’s production rate or age can influence the carbon stock of the biomass pools and
thus modify the CO2 reduction obtained per hectare. This aspect has been overlooked in
the current analysis and could be explored in future work.
One of the primary challenges encountered was the limited availability of data for the
agricultural and forestry sectors. Expanding this type of study to a broader geographical
scale would necessitate access to a comprehensive and consistent database. The FAO
GAEZ Data Portal [58] is one such resource, although it does lack certain data that were
required for this analysis, such as land costs and data for the forestry sector.
This study did not employ clustering methods for the modeled municipalities in Optiflow;
the decision was made to account for distances and resource transportation with a high
level of detail, which significantly increased the computational demands. For future work,
clustering techniques could be used to group land with similar characteristics (such as
land prices or agricultural efficiencies) into clusters. This approach would help balance
the level of detail with computational efficiency, particularly if the geographical scope of
the analysis were to be extended.

7
Conclusions
Among the energy sectors analyzed, heavy transport emerges as challenging to decar­
bonize, due to the lack of cheap alternatives to fossil fuels. To meet the fuel demand of
this sector, the most cost­effective mix of renewable fuels consists of:
• Bio­jet fuel and biogasoline,
• Ammonia,
• E­jet fuel produced via e­methanol upgrading.
Although biofuels are less expensive than their e­fuel counterparts, they rely on biogenic
carbon, a resource that could also be used for other purposes, like BECCS.
Results from the Base Case indicate that BECCS is the most economical method for
achieving negative emissions, which are essential to counterbalance emissions from sec­
tors such as industry and CHP production from fossil fuels, when integrating the VRES.
This suggests that biomass potential cannot be fully allocated to biofuel production.
When integrating land­use measures in the model, the cost­optimal solution is found in
the CO2 Case where 0.45 Mha of agricultural land (18.2% of the current agricultural area)
is diverted for productive forest establishment. The key benefits of this measure are that:
• It increases biomass availability, enabling the development of a renewable fuel pro­
duction chain based on biofuels.
• It provides CO2 sequestration at a lower cost compared to alternatives like DACCS
or BECCS, to offset positive emissions from the industrial sector and CHP produc­
tion when VRES integration is required.
The majority of land­use change implemented establishes productive forests close to the
municipalities where renewable fuel production facilities can be located. This planning ap­
proach allows to reduce the influence of transport costs on the total costs of the renewable
fuel production system.
By moving away from the strictly cost­optimal solution of the CO2 scenario and imposing
constraints on the area allocated to new protected forests and carbon­rich soil extraction
in the Biodiversity+CO2 scenario, the target is achieved with minimal impact on the en­
ergy system and renewable fuel costs. Moreover, this scenario results in a 17% reduction
in total system costs compared to the Base Case, while also contributing to the EU Bio­
diversity Strategy targets by increasing Danish protected areas to 10% of the total land
area [59].
Scenarios incorporating land use measures yield carbon­negative outcomes. This pool
of negative emissions could be crucial in offsetting emissions from the livestock farming
sector, which could be considered in future analyses.
