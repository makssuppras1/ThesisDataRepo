Abstract
The present work focuses on designing, implementing, and testing a prototype mediВӯ
cal device (MyoВӯFES). The aim of this project is to develop a neurorehabilitation device
that can help caregivers assist stroke survivors during their recovery phase. This project
started with an idea, followed by a literature review and a final prototype. Safety and regВӯ
ulations were also addressed to take the prototype to a further stage of development.
A functional electrical stimulator (FES) is a device that can deliver the electrical stimuВӯ
lation through a pair of electrodes placed above the targeted muscle,thereby inducing a
functional movement. Many systems have been used to activate (or trigger) the FESНҫ
such as a foot switch, a button, a brain computer interface signal, or an electromyogram
(EMG) signal. Specifically, EMGВӯtriggered FES systems use the EMG signal as a switch
to activate the functional electrical stimulation. Hence, when a predefined EMG threshold
is achieved, the FES modules generates the electrical signal that stimulates the target
muscle. By contrast, EMGВӯmodulated FES systems can deliver an electrical stimulation
that is proportional to the EMG envelope signal. The developed prototype can deliver
an EMGВӯamplitude controlled biphasic pulse train that is used to stimulate wrist extenВӯ
sion muscles. Thus, the developed prototype senses the EMG envelope signal from the
extensor carpi radialis muscle to control the output waveform amplitude of the functional
electrical stimulator module. This feedback system is an essential part of the rehabilitaВӯ
tion output and can be the key to faster and more effective stroke recovery. Additionally,
the prototype device has been designed to be fully portable and batteryВӯpowered, and it
has been tested on a test group to evaluate the output of this study.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Stroke is a neurological disorder caused by an impaired oxygen supply to the brain. This
can lead to cell injury and death of brain cells, which is the main source of motor deficits
[1] [2]. Stroke risk factors include hypertension, smoking, diabetes, and cholesterolemia.
Based on the mechanism of impaired oxygen supply to the brain, strokes can be classified
as hemorrhagic and ischemic [2]. Furthermore, based on the time following a stroke
onset, postВӯstroke phases can be classified in: acute (< 3 weeks after stroke onset),
subacute (3 weeks up to 6 months), early chronic, and late chronic (> 18 months) [3]. As
a result, stroke lead to lifetime conditions such as motor impairment, loss of muscle tone,
and muscle weakness which leads to difficulties to perform activities of daily living (ADL).
Hemiparesis, namely unilateral paresis, is the motor weakness of one entire side of the
body, and it is one of the serious complications of strokes. Among stroke survivors, more
than 80% of suffering from hemiparesis during the postВӯstroke acute phase, while 40% of
postВӯstroke survivors suffer from hemiparesis in chronic phase [1]. In addition, 50 % of
those stroke survivors suffer from upper limb paresis [4].
As a result, there is a high demand for solutions aiming to restore motor functionalities in
postВӯstroke patients. Many techniques have been used for postВӯstoke recovery such as
robotic assistance units, virtual reality (VR), and brain computer interface (BCI) [5] [1]Нҫ in
particular functional electrical stimulation (FES) showed how repeated muscle activation
may improve volitional motor control of the upper limb in post hemiparetic stroke patients
[6]. Neuromuscolar electrical stimulator, in particular FES, has been used for years for
both stroke rehabilitation and spinal cord injury rehabilitation [1]. With FES, an electrical
current delivered on the targeted muscles through surface electrodes induces a muscle
contraction that aims to produce a functional movement (e.g. holding or grasping).
1.1
Aim of the project
The aim of this pilot study is to develop an FES device controlled with volitional musВӯ
cle activity, that can be an effective rehabilitation device for stroke patients. This study
has been inspired by existing EMGВӯtriggered FES devices (Chapter 5.1), which use the
EMG signal as a trigger to deliver the FES therapy. In other words, such devices use the
EMG signal to activate the electrical stimulation above a preВӯdetermined EMG threshold
(e.g. 75% of maximum volitional muscle response [7]). The benefits of this type of therВӯ
apy reside in the brain plasticity mechanism: It is well established that the human brain
can in fact вҖқreprogramвҖқ itself, changing and arranging neural pathways [8]. Studies have
shown that, among other postВӯstroke rehabilitation therapies, electromyography (EMG)Вӯ
controlled FES improves motor functions of stroke patients affected by hemiparetic arm

injured motor cortex area. In addition, Previous studies have shown the efficacy of EMGВӯ

1.2
Objectives and Project Outcomes
The objectives of this study are:
вҖў Develop an effective batteryВӯpowered FES device to assist stroke patient during the
recovery phase.
вҖў Generate a FES output waveform with variable amplitude, fixed frequency, and fixed

2.1
Stroke and Motor Disability
Stroke, also called brain attack, is a neurologic deficit resulting from an impaired blood
flow to the brain tissues [2] and is one of the major causes of mortality in the United States,
reaching 5.5 million deaths worldwide in 2016 [9][10]. In addition, the World Health OrgaВӯ
nization (WHO) forecasts that strokes are expected to increase in Europe by 30% in 2025
[11]. Strokes can lead to death or, most commonly, postВӯstroke motor deficits followed
by deficits of cognition, sensation, and speech [2][12][13][10]. There are two main types
of strokes: hemorrhagic and ischemic. Ischemic strokes are the most common, accountВӯ
ing for 87% of strokes and are caused by a disruption of blood flow in a cerebral vessel
following thrombosis [2]. Risk factors of ischemic strokes include high blood cholesterol,
smoking, hypertension, and age. On the other hand, hemorrhagic strokes occur when
there is a decreased blood flow to the brain caused by a spontaneous rupture of a blood
vessel. This results in a haemorrhage in the brain tissues that can cause edema and
compression of other brain tissues [2].
Upper limb unilateral paresis (also known as hemiparesis) is the most common deficit after
a stoke and affects more than 80% of stroke survivors in the acute form and 40% in the
chronic form [1]. Clinical manifestations of upper limb motor impairment include muscle
weakness, impaired motor control, and changes in muscle tone, thus, impaired patients
have difficulties performing activities of daily living (ADL) including reaching and holding
objects [1]. It is worth mentioning that stroke recovery follows a nonВӯlinear logarithmic
pattern (Fig. 2.1) and can take months or years [1] [14], but the most important part of
the recovery phase takes place 3 months after the stroke onset and this early recovery
phase is largely dependent on learning adaptation strategies [1].

surface electrodes, it is also important to understand the role of the skin during the FES
therapy. Therefore, this chapter focuses on analysing the human skin model and the

2.5
Muscles and Skeletal Muscle
Movement is a distinct characteristics of all living things. Muscular tissue is the foundation
of movement and muscle cells, the building blocks of muscles, are devices that convert
chemical energy, in the form of adenosine triphosphate (ATP), into mechanical energy of
contraction. Depending on location and function, muscle tissues can be divided into skeleВӯ
tal, cardiac, and smooth muscle [22]. The most relevant characteristics that differentiate
muscular tissues from other tissues are the following:
вҖў Responsiveness: When muscle cells are stimulated by neurotransmitters, they reВӯ
spond with electrical changes across their membrane. It is a characteristic of all
living cells, but muscles cells developed it to the highest degree [22].
вҖў Conductivity: The electrical change mediated by neurotransmitters triggers an exВӯ
citation wave that travels along the muscle fiber leading to muscle contraction [22].
вҖў Contractility: Muscle fibers shorten when stimulated, pulling on tissues and bones
to create the movement of the targeted body part [22].
вҖў Extensibility: As muscle cells contract, they are also able to stretch again after a
contraction [22].
вҖў Elasticity: The ability of muscle cells to return to its original resting length when
tension is released [22].
For the purpose of this project, only skeletal muscles are considered relevant.
2.5.1
Microscopic Anatomy of Skeletal Muscles
Usually attached to one or more bones, skeletal muscles are defined as voluntary striated
muscles because they are subject to conscious control and exhibit light and dark transВӯ
verse bands (striations) . They are also called muscle fiber as skeletal muscles can reach
3Вӯ30 cm length and 100Вӯ500 Вөm in diameter [22]. Moreover, muscles are surrounded by
connective tissues named endomysium, perimysium, and epimysium. The endomysium
surrounds muscle fibers, perimysium ties together fibers into fascicles, and the epimyВӯ
sium wraps the entire muscle [22]. These connective tissues are connected to tendons
collagen fibers and those are anchored to the collagen of the bone matrix. Therefore,
when muscle fibers contract, they hold and pull bones, ultimately moving the body. The
building block of muscles are muscle fibers and have several sausageВӯshaped nuclei.
The cytoplasm of such cells is called sarcoplasm and accommodates long protein bunВӯ
dles called myofibrils. Each myofibrils incorporates several protein microfilaments named
myofilaments [2]. Myofilaments are the following:
вҖў Thick filaments: Each filament is made of molecules of myosin. Myosin is a molecule
shaped like a lipid with a head and two tails.
вҖў Thin filaments: Each filament is made of molecules of actin.
вҖў Elastic filaments: Made with a protein called titin, those filaments form the Z disc
structure which helps to keep thick and thin filaments aligned.
By sliding one on each other, actin and myosin (also called contractile protein) are reВӯ
sponsible for shortening muscle fibers. Each segment of a myofibril from Z disc to Z disc
is called Sarcomere and it is the functional unit of a muscle fiber. When actin and myosin
filaments overlap, the sarcomere shrinks causing Z discs to move towards each other [2].

2.5.3
Muscle Contraction and Relaxation
The process of muscle contraction and relaxation can be divided in four main phases: (1)
excitation, (2) excitationВӯcontraction coupling, (3) contraction, and (4) relaxation [22].
1. Excitation: During the excitation phase, action potentials of the nerve fiber lead to
action potentials of the muscle fibers.
2.
ExcitationВӯContraction Coupling: This refers to the event that links the excitation
of the sarcolemma with the contraction of the muscle fibers. Once the sarcolemma
is excited by a muscle action potential (MAP) [15], voltageВӯgated calcium (Ca2+)
channel opens (across the TВӯtubule network) releasing calcium and thereby allowing
actin filaments to bind with myosin heards [22].
3. Contraction: In this phase, muscle fiber shorten or develop tension without shortenВӯ
ing.
4. Relaxation: After muscle contraction, the muscle fibers relax and return to their
normal resting length.

2.5.5.1 Muscle fatigue and electrical stimulation
Muscle fatigue, as defined by Edwards in 1981, is the inability to generate the expected
force. It can also be defined as:
вҖқany reduction in the maximum force generating capacity, regardless of what type of work
is being done.вҖқ Вӯ BiglandВӯRitchie et aI. (1986) [15].
Muscle fatigue may be induced by a series of events involving the excitability of the motor
neuron, the propagation of the action potential, and the excitationВӯcontraction coupling.
In addition, muscle fatigue may also be affected by intracellular calcium exchange impairВӯ
ment.[15]. It should be noted that skeletal muscle fibers are вҖқdynamicвҖқ entity that is related
to the motor usage of the unit and the neural input. Prolonged training (weeks/months)
at low frequencies (10Hz) with constant electric stimulation can lead to a tranformation of
skeletal muscle fibers, increasing metabolic capacity, muscle fatigue resistance, and capВӯ
illary density. [15]. Depending on the frequency of stimulation of electrical pulses trains at
a magnitude that can activate the motor neuron, different types of fatigues phenomenon
can be seen [15]:
1. Low frequency fatigue (Edwards et. al., 1977): occurs with 10Вӯ20 Hz electrical pulse
trains. A deficit in excitationВӯcontraction coupling may contribute to this effect.
2. High frequency fatigue: occurs with 20Вӯ100 Hz electrical pulse trains. At this point,
muscle action potentials (MAP) propagation may fail.
3. Very high frequency fatigue (studied by Bowman and McNeal (1986)): occurs with
100Вӯ10000 Hz electrical biphasic stimuli. From 2000 Hz up to 10000Hz, Axons stop
firing after seconds after the initial stimulation [15].

3.1.1
Components of an FES System (Neuroprosthesis)
The basic components of an FES system are electrical stimulator, electrodes, sensors,
and orthosis (in some cases) [26].
Electrical Stimulator
An electrical stimulator is an electronics device responsible for generating electrical disВӯ
charges that induce muscle contraction. Each electrical stimulator channel consists of a
cathode and an anode electrodes used to deliver stimulation pulses. Electrical stimulator
can have more than one channel to stimulate different muscle groups [26].
Stimulation Electrodes
Different types of electrodes can be used to deliver the electrical stimulation. Each elecВӯ
trode type can offer advantages and disadvantages in terms of usability, flexibility, and
cost [26]. There are mainly three types of stimulation electrodes with different levels of
invasiveness:
1. Implanted electrodes: Electrodes surgically implanted into the body and therefore,
they are suitable for longВӯterm use. Implanted electrodes can be placed close to a
nerve to improve the selectivity of the targeted muscles. The typical current used
to produce muscle contraction using implanted electrodes is in the range of 25 mA
[27].
2. Percutaneous electrodes: Typically in the form of wires that penetrate the skin in
close proximity to the targeted motor neuron. Like the implanted electrodes, perВӯ
cutaneous electrode can reach a high selectivity but are often used for short term
FES applications. The typical current used to produce muscle contraction using
percutaneous electrodes is in the range of 25 mA [26].
3. Transcutaneous (surface) electrodes: Surface electrodes do not require surgery and
can be self adhesive or require adhesive tape to be placed on the skin. Therefore,
transcutaneous electrodes are easy to reposition but they require a higher stimulaВӯ
tion current to produce muscle contraction (<120 mA) [26]. The higher stimulation
current is caused by the higher impedance of the surface electrodes, that in turn,
can be reduced by applying gel on the skin surface [21]. Although simpler and safer
to use than implanted and percutaneous electrodes, surface electrode do not proВӯ
vide the best muscle excitation, especially for deep muscles (i.e. muscles far below
the skin) [21] [26].
Transcutaneous Electrode Size
Different electrode sizes can be used depending on the size and location of the targeted
muscle. Large muscle groups, like quadriceps (i.e. knee extensors) require large 10x5 cm
electrodes or several 5x5 cm electrodes located at different muscle heads. On the other
hand, smaller electrodes can be used when when stimulating afferent nerves (e.g. for
the flexion reflex) [16]. Small electrodes can suffer from the fat layer underneath the skin
as the current tends to spread within the fat layer, preventing the activation of the motor
nerves [28]. It can be concluded that in the upper extremities (especially for the hand
movement) electrode size has an influence on the selective stimulation of muscles that
are close together. In general, smaller electrode can achieve a more selective activation
of different muscles [16].
FES electrode placement
The target muscle should be identified before placing the FES electrodes. The first FES
electrode is placed over the muscle belly, whereas the second electrode is placed over
the distal portion of the muscle [29].

Pulse Amplitude

3.2.5

Cyclic stimulation is referred to when the stimulation is passively applied to the muscle
without any active involvement from the subject, whereas triggered stimulation is electrical
stimulation delivered by volitional activity from the subject (e.g. EMG activity, pushВӯbutton,
insole sensor, etc.) [35]. An example of FES therapy using cyclic mode stimulation would
be 6s FES ON and 9s FES OFF repeated for 30 minutes [35].
3.2.6

Assessing how long the stimulation should be applied was also a topic of this study. Article
reported 6 to 10 second stimulation OFF and 9 or 10 second ON for both cyclic and
triggered electrical stimulation [36] [35]. According to their experience, physiotherapists

while the OF time should be double or triple the ON time (e.g. 15Вӯ30 s.) because paretic
muscles need more time to recover [34].

3.3
FES Вӯ Targeted Muscle Groups
It is worth mentioning that FES for stroke rehabilitation can be broadly classified into two
categories depending on the targeted muscle. Classes of post stroke FES rehabilitation
are upper limb and lower limb rehabilitation. Lower limb FES is mainly focused on restorВӯ
ing gait and foot drop [30], whereas upper limb FES rehabilitation is mainly focused on
restoring hand functions.
3.3.1
FES for lower limb rehabilitation
Stroke often leads to lower limb impairment that often leads to weakness and impaired
coordination. Therefore, many stroke survivors are unable to perform coordinate and
precise movement during the swing and gait phase [44] or have difficulties lifting the front
part of the foot or toes [30]. Rehabilitation strategies focus on improving strength and
coordination, controlling hip and ankle to mitigate the fear of falling, and overВӯground gait
training [44] [30]. FES delivered in the lower limb, often triggered by external devices or
volitional control, helps to regain freedom of movement [30] [31]. Muscles groups involved
in FES for lower extremity rehabilitation are often ankle plantar flexors, knee extensor
muscles, ankle dorsiflexors [45] [46] [30] [47].
3.3.2
FES for upper limb rehabilitation (SCI vs. Stroke rehabilitation)
Since this study focuses on upper limb rehabilitation, it is essential to highlight muscles
involved during the upper limb FES rehabilitation therapy. A key part of this analysis is
understanding what type of hemiparesis (i.e. unilateral paresis) is commonly caused by
strokes and what type of upper limb impairment stroke survivors have. It has been found
that even though SCI patients and stroke patients share unilateral paresis, there is a subВӯ
stantial difference between the two conditions, therefore the FES therapy is fundamentally
different among SCI and stroke patients [48] [49].
The recovery of upper limb functions is unbalanced between the two groups, and in stroke
survivors it is strengthened more in the flexors than in the extensors. Flexors are the musВӯ
cles that let us close the hand, and the extensor muscles are the muscles that let us open
the hand [49]. In other words, it is often seen that stroke survivors recover their flexor
muscles first then the extensor muscles, therefore the focus of the therapy would be on
hand opening, wrist flexion, and fingers relaxation [48]. In short, stoke patients recover
hand closing much more faster than hand opening, whereas SCI patients have difficulties
closing their hand and grasping objects. Therefore, SCI FES therapy is more focused on
flexors muscles (hand/finger flexion) and regaining muscle strength[48][49]. Many articles
that use FES for upper limb paresis often focus on posterior forearm muscles that provide
finger and wrist extension (See Table 3.1) such as: extensor carpi radialis (ECR) longus
and brevis, extensor carpi ulnaris, extensor digitorum cummunis, and extensor pollicis
longus and brevis [50][7]. Table 3.2 summarises all muscle groups used for upper limb
FES rehabilitation. Figure 3.4 shows superficial muscles of the posterior forearm used for
upper limb recovery FES in stroke patients.

3.4
Outcome Control Вӯ ARAT test
Widely used to assess the upper extremity function of postВӯstroke patients, the Action
Research Arm Test (ARAT) quickly became important to investigate and quantify stroke
recovery [55]. The ARAT is a wellВӯdesigned, efficient, comprehensive, and feasible clinical
quantitative method that has been widely used in stroke survivors. The upper limb is
mainly used for grasping, reaching, and object manipulation, hence a proper evaluation
of the upper extremity following a stroke is essential for addressing the functional use of
the limb [55]. Despite being used for assessing other upper limb impairment diseases,
such as Parkinson and multiple sclerosis, the most effective use of ARAT has been seen
in stroke [55]. The ARAT is grouped into 19 subtests which include grasp (6 items), grip (4
items), pinch (6 items), and gross arm movement(3 items). Each item is rated on a fourВӯ
point scale (from 0 to 3) where the lowest possible grade means no movement, and the
highest grade (three) indicates that the movement is performed naturally [55] [56]. The
maximum ARAT score is 57 which is equivalent to the sum of each of the 19 tests score,
performed without any limb impairment (e.g. no aids). Examples of ARAT functional
movement are grasping and lifting a 10 cm2 block or a 2.5 cm2 block. Gross movement
test include touching the back/top of the head and mouth [56]. One disadvantage of the
ARAT is that scoring is subjective and is determined solely on the clinicianвҖҷs interpretation
of the patientвҖҷs ability to perform the task [56]. Also, the lack of a standardised materialвҖҷs
weight can also bias the ARAT result [56]. In the literature review, it has been found that
the ARAT has been one of the most used tests to assess the FES outcomes. Among
reviewed articles, it can be concluded that FES (cyclic or triggered) therapy provides a
significant improvement in the ARAT. Findings can be found in Table 3.1.
3.5
Why does FES work?
The goal of FES is not only to help stroke (or SCI) patients to complete a functional moveВӯ
ment, but its main goal is to restore the motor capability of patients by restoring the brainвҖҷs
motor pathways. This process of вҖқrestoringвҖқ, вҖқregainingвҖқ, or вҖқrewiringвҖқ electrical connecВӯ
tions inside the brain is called brain plasticity, and it is one of the most fascinating facts of
our nervous system.
3.5.1
Neuroplasticity
Brain plasticity, also called neuroplasticity, is our brainвҖҷs ability to adapt based on experiВӯ
ence [8]. Even if the process of neuron creation (neurogenesis) stops after birth, scientists

believe that our nervous system is capable of creating new connections, electrical pathВӯ
ways, and even вҖқfabricatingвҖқ new neurons. There are two main types of brain plasticity:
вҖў Functional plasticity: Ability of the brain of relocating brain functions from the damВӯ
aged ares to the undamaged ones.
вҖў Structural plasticity: Ability of the brain of changing its physical structure following
learning.
It is worth mentioning that even though plasticity occurs throughout a lifetime, young brains
are more sensitive and capable of better adaptation compared to вҖқolderвҖқ brains. Also,
neuroplasticity has its own limitationsНҫ after a stroke onset, healthy areas of the brain may
take over the functions of damaged areas, but for certain areas, this process may not
resolve in a full recovery [8].
3.5.2
FES and Stroke Recovery
Following a stroke, survivors experience a lack of motor abilities (depending on the site
of the stroke). This is due to the necrosis of brain cells, thus it is believed that process of
neuroplasticity induced by FES therapy may help to restore motor pathways. It should be
mentioned that nearly all the movement is generated in the spinal cord, but motor control is
held by the cortical activity [34]. FES therapy, both triggered and cyclic, is able to produce
positive changes in stroke patients affected by upper limb impairment [48](Table 3.1).
FES stimulation induces brain plasticity by stimulating the ascending pathways through
the muscle fibres [57]. In addition, somatosensory inputs (e.g. visual stimuli) to the motor
cortex are a key factor of motor learning and control, thus they play a crucial role in the
recovery process [58]. In particular, EMGВӯtriggered electrical stimulation in stroke patients
with hemiparetic arm have shown activation of motor areas in the brain [7]. In the study
carried by von Lewinski et. al. [7], a Functional Magnetic Resonance Image (fMRI) of
the brain, with and without EMGВӯtriggered stimulation, shows how the activation in the
sensorimotor area is expanded during the EMGВӯtriggered stimulation. In this study, during
the electrical stimulation without EMG, seven patients received passive finger extension,
while during the EMGВӯtriggered session, the electrical stimulation was controlled by the
muscle activity to produce functional movement (the EMG threshold was set at 75% of
maximum EMG activity for each muscle) [7]. Targeted muscle groups for both groups were
wrist and finger extensor muscles: extensor carpi radialis, extensor digitorum, extensor
pollicis longus and brevis. The fMRI recordings in Fig. 3.6 show how EMG triggered
electrical stimulation can expand the activation of the sensorimotor area [7]. In addition,
studies have shown that during an EMGВӯFES therapy session, levels of OxyВӯHb in the
damaged motorВӯcortex area increases. By contrast, conventional electrical stimulation
without any volitional control did not show any significant increase in OxyВӯHb [8].
Following those findings, we believe that Functional electrical stimulation with volitional
electromyography control can induce positive results in the postВӯstroke recovery process
of the upper extremity.
3.6

Electromyography
Biomedical signals are typically acquired from an organ or apparatus that represents a
variable of interest. Such signals are normally a function of time and they can be deВӯ
scribed in terms of amplitude, phase, and frequency. The electromyogram (EMG) is a
biomedical signal that measures the potential difference generated by muscle contraction
and represents the neuromuscolar activity [59]. This signal is sometimes referred to as
myoelectric activity. As electrical impulses can travel through the tissue, the EMG signal
can be acquired from electrodes placed above the muscle of interest. Moreover, if EMG
electrodes are placed on the skin (surface electrodes), they can also collect unwanted
signals from different motor units (cross talk [60]) and capture noise [59]. The EMG sigВӯ
nal is the direct representation of the electrical activity of muscleвҖҷs motor units and it can
be divided in:
вҖў Intramuscular EMG: This invasive technique requires an intramuscular needle elecВӯ
trode (Fig. 6.1) containing two fine wires. This electrode is placed inside the muscle
of interest [60].
вҖў Surface electromyography (sEMG): This nonВӯinvasive technique requires surface
electrodes (Fig. 6.1) placed above the targeted muscle. This technique is preВӯ
ferred when analysing superficial muscle activation [60]. Moreover, such electrodes
need an electrolyte gel to ensure proper performances and reduce electrodeвҖ“skin
impedance [61].
EMG signals can be used for generating control commands for rehabilitation devices or
human computer interfaces (HCI) [60]. This chapter will mainly focus on surface EMG as
it has been used for the experimental setup.

4.2
Noise and EMG signals
The EMG signal is often masked by noise or artefacts generated by various factors includВӯ
ing electrodes, movement, electromagnetic noise, acquisition site, etc... . Those artefacts
should be eliminated in order to extract the pure EMG signal needed for the analysis[60].
The amplitude range of EMG signals before amplification is 0 to 10mV and therefore can
be easily masked by electrical noise [59]. Noise affecting EMG signals can be classified
in:
вҖў Electrodes and electrodes noise: As other types of electrical equipment, electrodes
also have вҖқintrinsic noiseвҖқ with frequency components from 0 Hz to several kHz [60].
For nonВӯinvasive EMG recordings, Ag/AgCl (silver / silver chloride) electrodes with
size 10x1 mm are typically used because of their signal to noise ratio [60]. Our
electrodes have an Ag/AgCl sensing area of 8.9 mm2 [63].
вҖў Electrical equipment noise: Inhered noise produced by electronic equipment that
can not be eliminated [59].
вҖў Ambient noise: Caused by electromagnetic radiation. This type of noise can have
an amplitude 1 to 3 orders of magnitude greater than EMG signals [59].
вҖў Motion artefacts: Caused by the electrodes setВӯup that create irregularities in the
data. Motion artefacts can be caused by electrode interference and electrode cable.
вҖў Cross Talk: It is defined as undesired EMG signal form a muscle group that is not
currently monitored. Cross talk can be minimised by carefully choosing both elecВӯ
trode size and interВӯelectrode distances (generally 1Вӯ2cm ). This phenomena can

State of the Art
Many FES devices has been proposed to restore upper limb functionality for postВӯstroke
patients. Those devices can be mainly divided into two categories, depending on how
the electrical stimulation is deliveredНҫ in cyclic electrical stimulator (e.g. passive) devices,
the electrical stimulation is applied without any active involvement from the subject [35]
whereas in triggered devices, the electrical stimulation is activated by the subject using
sensors or manual switches [35]. In addition, FES devices using EMG signal as a control
signal to deliver the electrical stimulation can be either EMGВӯtriggered or EMGВӯcontrolled
FES devices [69]. EMGВӯ triggered devices activate the FES stimulation when an EMG
signal threshold is reachedНҫ by contrast, EMGВӯcontrolled FES devices deliver an electrical
stimulation that is proportional to the EMG signal (Fig. 5.1) [69].
Noma et. al. [5] developed a NMES for upper limb with triggered stimulation by a manВӯ
ual foot switch. For the wrist and finger extension movement the patientвҖҷs arm is fixed
to an arm support. The electrical stimulation was delivered using the ESВӯ530 (ITO Co.,
Tokyo, Japan) highВӯvoltage stimulator delivering a fixed 50Hz, 50Вөs twinВӯpeak monophaВӯ
sic pulsed current. When the foot switch device is pressed, the stimulation is delivered
with an intensity corresponding to 50% of the full wrist and finger extension range of moВӯ
tion [5]. This treatment was delivered to 15 postВӯstroke patients with chronic upper limb
hemiparesis for 60min each days for two weeks with a total of 12 training sessions. The
FuglВӯMeyer assessment (FMA) showed a significant improvement (preintervention: 49.2 Нҫ
postintervention: 54.9) on motor functions as well as the ARAT test (preintervention: 27.7
Нҫ postintervention: 32.4) demonstrating the benefits of only two weeks of FES treatment
[5].

Buck Converter
Similar to the boost converter, a buck converter (Fig. 6.10) also uses an inductor, a diode,
a switch, and a capacitor to modify the source voltage. However, instead of вҖқboostingвҖқ
or increasing the voltage of a voltage source, the buck converter acts by reducing or
вҖқsteppingВӯdownвҖқ the voltage source [83].
The operation mode of the buck converter is similar to the boost converter:
Referring to Fig. 6.10, when the Mosfet Q1 is conducting, the current flows through the
inductor and the energy is stored. During this stage, the diode D1 is not conducting.
When the Mosfet is switched off (i.e. the circuit is open), the energy that was previously
stored in the inductor flows through the capacior. The diode D1 is conducting during
this stage [83]. Therefore, the insertion of a capacitor creates an LC filter that removes
switching harmonics and leaves only the DC component to the output [83].

Arduino controlled HВӯbridge
An HВӯbridge (Fig. 6.13) has been used to obtain a biphasic wave with controllable pulse
width and frequency. The principle behind an HВӯbridge is fairly simpleНҫ Four switches are
switched on and off in pairs to redirect the current flow across the load. As can be seen
in Fig. 6.13, if the switches S1 and S3 are closed, the current flows towards the positive
terminal of the load. By contrast, if S3 and S2 are closed, the current flows towards the
negative terminal of the load. Therefore, the HВӯbridge is the optimal choice for our applicaВӯ
tion as it can provide a positive and negative wave by switching on and off four switches.
In realВӯlife applications, such switches are typically replaced BJTs (for low power applicaВӯ
tions) or, most commonly power MOSFETS, that can easily drive higher loads [83]. For
our application, we chose the Texas Instruments L293D [90] dual channel HВӯbridge motor
driver (Fig 6.12) that is typically used for robotic applications. This integrated circuit (IC) is
designed to provide bidirectional drive voltages up to 36V DC and can be controlled using
the 3.3V or 5V PWM output of an Arduino. Taking Fig. 6.12 as a reference, the ENA pin
on the IC controls the on/off state of the IC, logic pins IN1 and IN2 control the positive or
negative output wave, VCC2 is the external input voltage (36V DC max.), and VCC1 is
the 5V internal logic circuitry supply voltage. We only used one side of the L293D such
that outputs OUT1 and OUT2 were connected to the body.

Experimental Procedure
The experimental procedure can be divided into four sections: electrode placement, EMG
threshold calculation, FES threshold calculation, and treatment.
Electrode placement
The experiment starts with positioning the electrodes above the muscle of interest ensurВӯ
ing good contact between the electrodes and the skin. We suggest shaving the area to
ensure good adhesion of the electrodes to the skin. For the wrist extension movement,
electrodes were placed above the extensor carpi radialis that can be palpated inferoposВӯ
teriorly to the subjectвҖҷs elbow [98].
EMG threshold calculation
After electrode placement, the EMG signal has to be interpreted and analysed to deВӯ
tect the threshold above which the FES stimulation is activated. Five measurements of
10 seconds each have been taken to assess the EMG threshold value. The calibration
sequence is initiated with a Bluetooth command, the subject is asked to perform wrist
extension at maximum strength. This process is repeated 5 times and data are collected
on an SD card. Subsequently, the EMG signal is processed with a moving average filter
to eliminate unwanted background noise and the EMG threshold is calculated based on
the 60% maximum EMG value of each calibration procedure. The mean of Five different
threshold values is then calculated to extract the final threshold value that will be used
during the experimental procedure.
The operator can also choose to calculate the threshold value based on different percentВӯ
age of the maximum EMG value (e.g. 30%, 40%, 60%, 75% ...)

FES module calibration
The FES calibration phase is done to ensure that the output voltage does not exceed the

the output voltage. Once the desired wrist extension angle is reached, the operator can
decide to stop the sequence manually with the push button. The FES voltage value is
then recorded. The FES voltage value can then be used in the therapy code to set the
maximum voltage that the FES module should reach. Although the frequency and pulse
width of the stimulation pulse can be modified, we decided to keep them constant with a
value of 50Hz and 300 Вөs.

Electrical Safety and Regulations
The present work shows the design, implementation, and testing of a medical device.
Even if the proposed device is just a prototype, and it is far from being marketed as a
biomedical device, it is important to address safety requirements because they are esВӯ
sential to admit the device on clinical trials, and test it on human beings. This chapter is
focused on medical devices safety and it can be a source of inspiration for later developВӯ
ment stages of the device.
The medical device industry rose exponentially during the past decade because of new
technologies and researches in the healthcare industry. Because of this increase in popВӯ
ularity, there was the need of applying uniform rules in order to obtain high standards
of safety and quality, leading to protect the health of patients and users [103].
After
several scandals regarding the medical device industry, such as the Bonelock Acrylic
bone cement and the Poly Implant ProthГЁse breast implant [104], governments decided to
strengthens their regulations to meet the desired health and quality requirements. NowaВӯ
days, many organisations are responsible for enforcing such regulations and to protect
and promote human health. Depending on the county in which a medical device will be
marketed, there are different organisations in charge of assessing medical device regulaВӯ
tions, the most famous of which are EU and FTAНҫ EU, for the European market, and FDA,
for the US market [103]. Furthermore, before placing a medical product on the European
market, third party organisations designated by EU countries called notified bodies, are
accountable for assessing the conformity of medical products. This project developed in
Denmark aims to be marketed in both EU and US countries and therefore, should follow
both EU and FDA medical device regulations.
The developed device falls into the active biomedical devices category, and therefore,
electrical safety should be taken into account. The European commission defines active
biomedical devices as those devices whose operation depends on an electrical source([105]
section 3.1 4). This raises the concern about the electrical safety of active biomedical deВӯ
vices that should not be harmful and, according to the FDA definition of a biomedical
device, active biomedical devices are intended to be used to cure, mitigate, treat, or preВӯ
vent diseases in man or other animals [103]. In addition, the EU definition of biomedical
device emphasises the вҖқintended useвҖқ of such devices, that, in short, is what the device
is meant to do[103].
Medical devices design should address safety factors, including the minimisation of huВӯ
man errors during the device operation [103].

roughly 1/2D = 1/(2*0,0003) = 1666Hz should be reached. The Arduino is capable of
delivering digital outputs of +5V up to 4MHz (way higher than the required frequency)
[67], but the L293D was not fast enough to вҖқkeep upвҖқ. However, on page 6 of the L293x
datasheet [112], it is indicated the optimal switching parameters for input IN1 and IN2 (the
ones that control when the output is zero, positive, or negative). In theory, this IC should
be able to switch at 5kHz but in reality, some ICs switching at such high frequencies
(above 1kHz) were distorting the output waveform. Clearly, we pushed the L293D to its
limits since this IC was not designed to reach such high frequencies. Figure 11.1 shows
how the output of the IC looks like when programming the Arduino to drive the L293D to
generate an output biphasic wave of 300Вөs. As can be seen in Fig. 11.1, the IC does not
even follow the вҖқcommandsвҖқ of the Arduino and it tries to keep up, generating an abnormal
output waveform. This output waveform remains almost unchanged if a lower pulse width
is reached. Moreover, we tested this concept on different L293D and the result maybe
even worse, meaning that some ICs have voltage spike at the beginning or end of the
square wave.
On the other hand, only few L293D presented this problem. In fact, after testing some
ICs we found the ones working properly. Figure 11.2 shows a 300us pulse waveform at
50Hz recorded from the output of a working L293D. However, the biphasic wave in Fig.
11.2 is not perfect, and has some distortion in the transition between the positive phase
and negative phase. This slightly вҖқdistortedвҖқ wave was considered acceptable for our
application.
Another limitation of the L293D was its output voltage. In fact, FES devices can reach an
output voltage >100V [6] and our design lacks of вҖқflexibilityвҖқ since the maximum voltage
we can achieve is 64V peakВӯtoВӯpeak. Therefore, future implementations of this device
could include a wellВӯdesigned HВӯbridge using four Mosfets. One design idea would be
to design the HВӯbridge usin two IRFN540N NВӯchannel Mosfet on the lower side, and two
IRF9540 PВӯchannel Mosfet that can both sustain a drain source voltage (VDSS) of 100V
and Вӯ100V. Alternatively, to reach a VDSS of up to 200V, the IRFB4020PBF NВӯchannel
mosfet and FQPF7P20 PВӯchannel Mosfet can be used.

Another limitation of our design is the SparkFun MyoWare Muscle Sensor. This muscle

to avoid such issues, we decided to not using the cable shield and instead using the
MyoWare Muscle Sensor itself. In addition, the MyoWare was kept away from the Arduino.
Furthermore, muscle electrodes are not very easy to place and they loose their stickiness
after after the third time the electrodes are repositioned. As a final note, the SparkFun
MyoWare Muscle Sensor suffers from amplifier saturation This means that the CMOS
amplifier used in the muscle sensor tends to slowly recover when saturated (e.g. if the
input signal is too high). This can be the case when, by moving the electrodes in place or
when moving the EMG sensor, a high amplitude signal is generated (e.g. noise). Such
noisy signal can saturate the CMOS amplifier leading to a slow recovery of the amplifier
and inaccurate measurements. To avoid this behaviour, SparkFun suggested adjusting
the amplifier gain, but this can ultimately lead to a loss of information due to a lower
amplitude output signal. Finding the gain вҖқsweet spotвҖқ is a matter of trial and error and
it should be calibrated on each measurement, increasing the therapy time. In addition,
we used an optocoupler between the Arduino and the muscle sensor to avoid powerline
interference. We suggest avoiding using this MyoWare Muscle Sensor as it is clearly not
optimal for medical applications due to its high inhered noise and its proneness to motion
artefactsНҫ not including that the вҖқRawвҖқ signal cannot be amplified. At the end we decided
to avoid using the MyoWare cable shield, and instead we used the вҖқstandaloneвҖқ MyoWare

Chapter 12
Conclusions
The purpose of this project was to develop and test a FES with EMG biofeedback. The
proposed device achieved its main goal of controlling the FES with an EMG signal and a
flex sensor placed on the subjectвҖҷs wrist. The FES module can generate a biphasic output
waveform at 50Hz and 300 Вөs pulse with and the maximum output voltage achievable by
the FES module is 64Vpp. From the results obtained from a test group we can conclude
that the device achieved its main purpose as it showed that our concept of controlling the
FES output waveform amplitude with an EMG signal is feasible. However, since we could
not connect the FES output to a human arm, we do not know how the muscle will react to
such therapy in real life. Future researches will be focused on testing the safeness of the
functional electrical stimulation on a test group. In addition, we do not also know how the
device behaves when electrodes are connected to a post stroke patient. Depending on the
stroke onset we predict that the EMG threshold might be modified therefore, we assume
that, depending on the severity and time after the first stroke onset, the EMG volitional
activity of stroke subjects might change. Inductively, we predict that stoke patients in
the early recovery phase will have a lower EMG signal amplitude. Therefore, our initial
assumption of setting the EMG threshold to 50% (or 30%) of the maximum EMG activity,
might not work as intended. We do not exclude the possibility to modify the EMG threshold
to meet 20% or even 10% of the maximum EMG activity.
In addition, some stroke patients that during the early stroke recovery phase do not have
residual EMG activity will not take advantage of this therapy. For those patients. we
expect that this device may be effective in later stages of stroke recovery, perhaps once
some muscular activity will be recovered.
The beauty of the human body is unpredictable, non linear, and incredibly difficult to
model. Therefore, there is always some degree of uncertainty when developing a medical
device. The most important question we would like to address inВӯvivo would be: Will this
device work for postВӯstroke patients? Will this therapy be effective? In concussion, we
are confident that this project will be a source of inspiration for future researchers and
hopefully help stroke patients live a more enjoyable life.
