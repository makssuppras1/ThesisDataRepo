Abstract
Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs), introduced in 2017, is a powerful
technique for solving known ordinary and partial differential equations.
How-
ever, in many real-world applications, the underlying dynamics are only partially
known, which makes PINNs less applicable. Universal Physics-Informed Neu-
ral Networks (UPINNs) are a recent (2022) advancement that allow solving hid-
den discovery problems by combining PINNs with Universal Differential Equations
(UDE). While UPINNs have shown promising results, the framework is still in its
infancy and research is sparse. This thesis aims to contribute by exploring the
capabilities and limitations of UPINNs in a variety of settings, including the Lotka-
Volterra system, SIR model, Burgers’ equation, and Navier-Stokes equations for
incompressible fluid flow. Further, a real-world example of using PINNs to model
plasma transport in a tokamak reactor is considered.
The results demonstrate that UPINNs are capable of modelling hidden dynamics
in partially known systems, often yielding solutions comparable to regular PINNs
applied to the full system, even when data is sparse and/or noisy. Furthermore,
the approximated hidden dynamics are accurate enough for symbolic regression
frameworks like SINDy and PDE-find to recover the hidden equations.
The study also highlights a tendency for UPINNs to estimate known dynamics
that are insufficiently constrained, or to introduce new ”phantom” dynamics that
does not exist in the true system but do not alter the solution either. Issues that
were evident in inverse problems and systems with several governing equations.
Moreover, while shown to be feasible, disregarding inductive biases can lead to
less robust models and hinder extrapolation capabilities.

below linked GitHub repository. The repository also contains the model files for
all results presented in this thesis.

Introduction
Solving differential equations (DE) is central task in science and engineering, as
Ordinary and Partial Differential Equations (ODEs and PDEs) are fundamental
tools in modelling of dynamical systems. It is often more natural to formulate
a system in terms of its dynamical behavior rather than its static properties, as
many fundamental laws of physics describe or constrain how a system changes
(e.g. conservation, interaction, motion etc.).
Traditional solution methods, such as analytical tools (e.g. Computer Algebra
Systems (CAS)) or numerical schemes like Runge-Kutta for ODEs and finite dif-
ference/element (FDM/FEM) methods for PDEs, are well established and have
proven effective in many applications. However, most of these methods require a
fully known model, which might not always be available. If model parameters are
unknown, additional assumptions and estimation techniques may be required. In
addition to the forward problem of solving DEs, this adds the challenges of the
inverse problem, where parameters of a model with known symbolic form are
partially or fully unknown, and hidden discovery, where model dynamics are only
partially or not at all described, simultaneously raising the issue of model inter-
pretability.
Forward Problem: Solve differential equations with sufficient initial/boundary
conditions to predict the system’s behavior over the domain.
Inverse Problem: Use observed data from the system to identify unknown pa-
rameters.
Hidden Discovery: Detect and learn residuals of dynamical system through data-
driven methods.
Interpretability: Uncover governing equations from data to provide model inter-
pretability.
These 4 challenges have gained significant attention in the last decade within
Physics Informed Machine Learning (PIML) [1, 2], a research area within the
emerging subfield of Deep Learning known as Scientific Machine Learning (SciML).
Traditional machine learning methods are often purely data-driven black-box tech-
niques, usually requiring loads of data. Contrary, scientific computing is mostly
driven by physical modelling and numerical discretization techniques with little to

no use of data. SciML is a hybrid approach that aims to leverage data in combi-
nation with physical knowledge to solve scientific problems.
Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) [3, 4, 5] were a key development
within SciML. Using neural networks as a surrogate, PINNs embed differential
equations into the training process, thereby imposing physical constraints while
also leveraging data. Since their introduction in 2017, PINNs have been thor-
oughly studied in the context of solving forward and inverse problems.
Universal Physics-Informed Neural Networks (UPINNs) are a recent (2022) ad-
vancement in the field of PINNs. In an attempt to combine PINNs with Universal
Differential Equations (UDE) [7], UPINNs add a trainable neural network to the
diffential equations, allowing the model to learn functional representations of the
residual dynamics while simoultaneously solving the DE. Thus UPINNs can po-
tentially solve both forward, inverse and hidden discovery problems in a unified
framework.
1.1
Previous Work
Universal Physics-Informed Neural Networks stand out in the landscape of PIML
methods, as they combine the flexibility of neural networks with prior physical
knowledge, while leveraging data to simultaneously solve partially known systems
and discover their hidden dynamics.
UPINNs combine Universal Differential Equations (UDE) [7] and Physics-Informed
Neural Networks. Aknowledging the concept of UDEs, it is relevant to establish
why UPINNs are not UDEs. Both use a universal approximator (neural network)
to model the residuals of differential equations. UDEs use traditional numerical
solvers to simulate the observed data based on the current state of the system.
The discrepancy between simulated data and true observations is then computed
and used to train the residual network. By contrast, UPINNs leverage a solution
surrogate neural network to solve the differential equation directly, and incorporate
the residual network in the physics-informed loss computation of the surrogate.
This allows information from the data to train the solution surrogate and indirectly
train the residual network as well.
Podina et al. initially introduced UPINNs as “A PINN Approach to Symbolic Differ-
ential Operator Discovery with Sparse Data” [6]. In 2023 they released a revised
paper, in which the term Universal Physics-Informed Neural Networks (UPINNs)
first appeared [8]. The paper primarily serves as an introduction of the UPINN
framework, highlighting its potential rather than providing a comprehensive anal-

ysis. The authors demonstrate examples of UPINNs for solving hidden discovery
problems on the Lotka-Volterra system, Burgers’ equation and a Cell Apoptosis
model. However, while not comprehensive, only the Lotka Volterra model is ex-
plored in any significant detail. The authors aknowledge that their paper is not yet
”camera-ready”, thus it should be considered a work in progress, which leaves
room for further exploration and study of the UPINN framework. This paper is the
primary source of inspiration for this thesis.
In the context of hidden discovery and model interpretability, the SINDy and PDE
find frameworks [9, 10] have been used to determine governing equations directly
from data. Being purely data-driven, these methods rely solely on observed data
and interpolation techniques to infer the governing equations. UPINNs do not find
governing equations directly, but learns a functional representation of the differ-
ential equation residuals in the form of a neural network.
Another promising emergence in the context of model interpretability are Kol-
mogorov Arnold Networks (KAN) [11]. A recent (2024) neural network architec-
ture, that has also made its appearance in the field of PINNs as PIKANs [12]. Us-
ing learnable activation functions, KANs have shown examples of learning highly
interpretable computational chains in some applications.
With UPINNs being a functional extension of PINNs, much of the previous work
and research done to enhance the performance and training of PINNs [5, 13, 14,
15, 16, 17, 18] might also be relevant for UPINNs. These works are referred
throughout section 3.1. Note, despite its name, Extended PINNs (XPINNs) [19]
are unrelated to the concept of UPINNs.
During the project period, Podina et al. released a new paper [20] discussing the
use of UPINNs with dimensional analysis and the symbolic regression framework
AI Feynman [21, 22] for recovering hidden equations. As of writing, March 2025,
the AI Feynman framework is exclusive to macOS and Linux-based environments.
1.2
Scope of the Thesis
In simple terms, the focus of this project is to study the UPINN framework on a
variety of ODE and PDE systems. Many physical systems involve processes that
are not well understood or fully captured by the current models, and being able
to use data-driven techniques to discover these hidden dynamics is a significant
advancement.
This report serves as a study on the use of UPINNs to discover hidden dynam-
ics on a selection of problems from the literature, and aims to provide insight into

UPINNs capabilities and limitations. The problems examined are systems com-
monly used to test capabilities of PINNs, and include the ODE systems of the
Lotka-Volterra predator-prey model and the epidemic SIR model, as well as the
PDE systems of Burgers’ viscous equation and Navier-Stokes equations for in-
compressible fluid flow.
In addition to estimating hidden dynamics, the report also investigates the use
of symbolic regression (SR) techniques to recover hidden equations of the, by
UPINN, discovered dynamics using SINDy and PDE-find [9, 10].
All experiments are conducted using the Python implementation of the Torch frame-
work [23]. A comprehensive highperformance machine learning library with a wide
range of tools and utilities for neural network training and evaluation, also provid-
ing easy access and usage of the GPU accelerated CUDA toolkit1.

2
Neural Networks
In recent years, the concept of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has gained significant at-
tention by the general public, particularly with the emergence of generative- and
large language models such as ChatGPT [24, 25], DALL-E [26, 27], Stable Diffu-
sion [28] and several others. AI is commonly conceptualized as an abstract and
highly complex computational system capable of independent thought and intelli-
gent reasoning in response to its received input. However, fundamentally, these
models are nothing more than large intricate mathematical functions known as
neural networks.
This chapter provides a detailed overview of neural networks. It starts by intro-
ducing the concept of an artificial neuron, followed by outlaying the role of neu-
ral networks as universal function approximators and the model training pipeline,
including the automatic differentiation-based backpropagation algorithm that en-
ables efficient fitting of these models. Finally, a selection of common and relevant
neural network architectures are presented.

2.4
Universal Approximation Theorem (UAT)
Having established what a neural network is, it seems appropriate to motivate why
they are useful at all and how their use is justified.
The holy grail is the Universal Approximation Theorem, stating;
”A feedforward neural network with a single hidden layer containing a finite number
of neurons, is capable of approximating any continuous function defined on a
compact domain, with arbitrary accuracy, provided enough neurons.”
Formally, if f : Rd →Ro is a continuous function defined on a compact subset
Ω⊂Rd and ϵ > 0, then there exist a single layer neural network fθ with parameters
θ such that,

followed by an independent generalization to all squashing activation functions by

holds for any non-polynomial activation function [34].

2.5
Model Fitting - Training
Motivated by the universal approximative capabilities of neural networks, the next
question is how one gets the network to actually approximate the desired function.
The process of tuning the networks parameters to make it fit a particular function,
is commonly known as training. This section is a step by step outline of the general
idea for the neural network training process. The structure is as follows:
• Weight initialization: Find a starting point for the optimization process.
• Loss function: Define the objective of the optimization process.
• Gradient-based optimization: Adjust the weights to minimize the loss func-
tion.
• Gradient computation - Backpropagation: Compute gradients efficiently
for gradient-based optimization.
2.5.1
Weight initialization
Apart from the network architecture itself, the weights are what defines the neural
network. These weights can be thought of as small dials that are tuned during the
training process, such that the network approximates the desired function.
As will become apparent, training is an iterative process where weights are con-
stantly adjusted based on their current value. Subsequently, the weights must be
assigned initial values before training. The choice of initial weights can signifi-
cantly impact convergence of the training process, as well as the networks ability
to approximate the target function accurately.

2.5.3

Having defined the goal of the training process, the next step is to devise a strategy
for adjusting the weights of the network as to minimize the loss function. Generally,
the loss is a complicated and non-linear function of the network weights, hence
analytically obtaining the optimal set of weights is intractable.
Therefore, optimization is typically carried out using an iterative gradient based
algorithm. The perhaps most simple of such algorithms is Gradient Descent
(GD).

2.5.4
Gradient computation - Backpropagation
Gradient-based optimization algorithms require gradient computation of the loss
function with respect to the network parameters, i.e. weights and biases. As gra-
dients are used for every weight update and directly influence the steps, thereby
also convergence of the optimization process, it is crucial that they are computed
efficiently and with high precision. Specifically, the gradient of the loss function
with respect to the network weights is needed.
Symbolic differentiation, either manually or by CAS, provide exact symbolic rep-
resentations of gradients. However, neural networks typically comprise long com-
positional chains of functions, which, due to the product rule for derivatives, makes
the symbolic representation of gradients grow exponentially, making it computa-

Numerical differentiation techniques, such as finite differences method, usually
only require that the loss function can be evaluated. However, they are approxi-
mate and can be computationally expensive due to multiple evaluations of the loss
function for each parameter. Furthermore, the limited precision of floating-point
arithmetic can introduce numerical instability in these methods.

which provide exact gradients without relying on full symbolic representations.

loss function with respect to the network output, is a vector. Starting from the
left results in repeated matrix-matrix products, while starting from the right means
repeated matrix-vector products. Thus for neural networks, the latter is typically
faster as it requires less operations. These two methods are known as Forward-
mode and Reverse-mode AD, respectively.

Learning, this reverse-mode AD is known as Backpropagation [44]. Its introduc-
tion allowed efficient training of deep neural networks and remains state of the art
for computing gradients in training deep learning models.

2.6.1
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)
Convolutional Neural Networks are a class of models particularly well suited for
tasks involving signal processing. They extract features from an input signal (e.g.
an image) by convolution with a kernel (filter), typically small like 3 × 3 or 5 × 5
(see figure 2.4). Traditionally, such operations relied on handcrafted kernels (e.g.
Sobol, Ridge, Gaussian, etc.) designed to capture specific features like edges,
corners, textures etc. CNNs automate this process by learning kernels directly
from data.
A convolutional layer typically comprise multiple kernels, each extracting different
features from the input. The output, often referred to as a feature map, can be
passed through activation functions and additional convolutional layers to extract
more complicated features. Depending on the task, the final feature maps may
be flattened and passed through a FNN to make a prediction, e.g. classifying the
contents of an image.
An inherent advantage of CNNs is translation invariance, meaning features can be
detected regardless of position in the signal. Contrary to FNNs, where each input
has its own set of weights, CNNs share weights across the input using kernels,
making them more efficient for large inputs like images. Consequently, convolu-
tional layers can also handle inputs of arbitrary size.

Residual Neural Networks (ResNet)
Residual Neural Networks [37] (not to be confused with Recurrent Neural Net-
works (RNNs)) introduce the concept of skip connections, a direct connection be-
tween two layers bypassing one or more intermediate layers. Formally, if fθ is a
neural network (of arbitrary architecture), a residual block is defined as,
R(x) = fθ(x) + Wx,
(2.20)
where W is a weight matrix representing a linear mapping projecting the input x
to match the dimension of the output of fθ. For equisized input and output, He
et al. [37] found the identity mapping to be sufficient.
The idea behind ResNets is that the desired output of a layer is (potentially) only a
small deviation from the input. Consequently, the network can learn the residual,
i.e. the difference between the input and output. This approach means the net-
work can focus on learning small adjustments rather than trying to reconstruct the
entire output from scratch. This results in network layers with fewer parameters,
allowing many ResNet blocks to be stacked while maintaining efficient training.
Additionally, skip connections allow direct flow of gradients through the network,
mitigating the vanishing gradient problem.

Kolmogorov Arnold Networks (KAN)
Kolmogorov Arnold Networks are a recent (2024) advancement in neural network
architectures, introduced by Liu et al. [11]. While related, the idea behind KAN is
fundamentally different from that of traditional neural networks, as it arises from

3
Universal Physics-Informed Neural
Networks
Universal Physics-Informed Neural Networks (UPINNs) [8] is a recent (2023) ad-
vancement in the field of Physics-Informed Machine Learning (PIML), attempting
to generalize the idea of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs). The PINN
framework is a technique for training neural networks to solve differential equa-
tions with a surrogate by utilizing domain knowledge and data. While PINNs have
shown great results in solving both forward and inverse problems, its formulation
assumes that the underlying physics, i.e. the governing equations, are complete.
UPINN relaxes this assumption by modelling residuals of the governing equations
with a neural network.
This chapter initially outlines the formulation of the PINN framework in detail, along
some important advancement and results made in recent years regarding training
and optimization of PINNs. It then proceeds by extending to the UPINN frame-
work.
3.1
Physics Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)
Since its introduction by Raissi et al. in 2017, Physics-Informed Neural Networks
(PINNs) [3, 4, 5] have enjoyed great attention from the SciML community. Unlike
many traditional numerical methods that solve differential equations on a set of
discrete points in the domain, PINNs use a surrogate network to approximate the
solution of a differential equation, thus the approximate solution exist continuously
on the domain and can be evaluated at any point.
It is important to note that PINNs, as the name suggests, are Physics-Informed,
not Physics-Enforced. This means the network is not (necessarily) strictly con-
strained to satisfy the governing equations but rather encouraged to do so.
A key insight is that, unlike traditional data-driven machine learning tasks that rely
on labelled input-output pairs, PINNs do not require knowing the true solution at
the training points. If derivatives of the surrogate can be accuratly computed, the
residuals of the differential equation can be evaluated at any point on the domain.
While real world data from the system might be sparse, noisy or even unavailable,
sampling collocation points in the domain and evaluate the differential equa-
tion residuals comes ”only” at computational cost. With the constantly increasing

3.1.2
Activation functions in PINNs
As mentioned in section 2.3, choosing an appropriate activation function is crucial
when designing a neural network. Considering the usecase of PINNs, the activa-
tion function should preferably be smooth and differentiable, as training requires
higher order derivatives of the network.
Despite the popularity of ReLU and its variants in many deep learning applications,
due to its cheap computational nature, its not widely used in physics-informed
neural networks. PINNs usually require computation of higher order derivatives
and the second derivative of ReLU vanishes, making it challenging to capture
non-linear dynamics. Instead, infinitely smooth functions such as the sigmoid and
hyperbolic tangent functions are often preferred in PINNs. A brief elaboration of
selected activation functions in the context of PINNs can be found in appendix
B.1.

Adaptive sampling
While collocation points are initially sampled in an even manner, resampling parts
of or even the entire set of collocation points during training can be useful. This is
due the spectral bias of the problem formulation. Spectral bias refers to the fact
that neural networks tend to fit lower frequency functions better higher frequency
ones. Sampling densly around high frequency regions can help inform the net-
work to better capture those features. Generally, it is not known a priori where
these regions are. An obvious solution is to simply increase the number of collo-
cation points across the entire domain. However, this is a highly inefficient use of
collocation points and comes at the cost of significantly increased computational
cost, especially for high dimensional problems.
Adaptive sampling techniques aim to leverage information acquired during train-
ing to strategically refine the collocation points, by targeting regions in which some
predefined criterion is met. Two (three) such methods are Residual-based Adap-
tive Refinement sampling (RAR) [55] and Residual-based Adaptive Distribution
sampling (RAD and RAR-D) [16], both of which use the PDE residuals as the
criterion for refinement.
RAR is a greedy algorithm that iteratively refines the set of collocation points by
periodicly adding new points in regions with high PDE residuals. Practically, this is
done by evenly sample a set of candidate points across the domain, then greedily
add the N points with largest residuals to the set of collocation points.
RAD resamples all collocation points based on the distribution of the PDE resid-
uals. The algorithm works by evenly sampling a set of candidate points across
the domain, then compute the PDE residuals and sample a new set of colloca-
tion points without replacement from the distribution defined by p(x) ∝
εk(x)
E[εk(x)] + c,
where ε is the PDE residuals and k, c ≥0 are tunable hyperparameters.
RAR-D is a hybrid of RAR and RAD, adding new points sampled according to the
RAD scheme.
The authors of [16] found that RAD method using a quasi-random sampling scheme
consistently outperformed non-adaptive sampling methods as well as RAR and
RAR-D. They also discovered that k = 2 and c = 0 usually yields good results.
Resampling all collocation points radically change the loss landscape, which can
cause optimizers such as L-BFGS to crash or diverge. The authors propose a
training strategy employing more stable optimizers such as Adam or AdamW for

3.1.5
Regularization
Overfitting is a classic problem in machine learning, and neural networks are no
exception. It often occurs if the network is too complex and begins to memorize
the training data rather than learning underlying generalizable patterns. Weights
of large magnitude can be an indication that overfitting is present in the model.
Expression (2.15) shows that gradients through a NN layer are directly dependent
on the weights, thus large weights can destabilize the training process, making
gradients, and thereby weight updates, explode or vanish.
Regularization is a technique constraining the magnitude of the weights, directly
imposed in the loss function. The most common regularization schemes are L1
and L2 regularization, penalizing the weights based on the respective norms. Us-
ing L2 as an example, the regularization loss is,
Lreg(θ) = λreg∥θ∥2

L2 regularization is also known as weight decay, as it effectively seek to decay the
weights towards zero. Many machine learning frameworks, like PyTorch [23], in-
corporate weight decay in the optimizers and automatically applies it to the weights
during training.
This issue can also be addressed with gradient clipping, i.e., thresholding the
gradients during backpropagation.
3.1.6
Learning rate scheduling
The learning rate (lr) is a hyperparameter controlling the step size of the optimizer
during weight updates. High lr accelerates convergence through larger steps, at
the risk of overshooting the minimum. Contrary, low lr provide stability, but may
require more epochs to converge or get stuck in local minima. Adaptive tuning be-
tween these tradeoffs can be employed during training to speed up convergence
while retaining stability. This is the concept of learning rate scheduling. Common
strategies include periodical reduction of the lr by a predetermined factor every
set number of epochs, or when the loss stops decreasing. More sofisticated ap-
proaches adjust the lr based on the loss, gradients or other metrics, allowing more
responsive tuning of the lr.

formulation of the BVP. Note that the weights θF and θG of the residual networks

θF and θG are trained to approximate unknown operators, that are potentially non-
linear and differential. Podina et al. [8] suggest imposing an inductive bias on
these networks by passing them inputs thought to be part of the unknown opera-
tors, such as partial derivatives of ˆuθu evaluated at the collocation points, as these
can be accuratly computed using backpropagation. Note that this methodology
connects the computational graphs of the residual networks with the solution net-
work, making weight updates interdependent. As data is only evaluated by the
solution network, this connection is crucial if the residual networks are to learn
directly from the data as well.
UPINNs generalize the idea of PINNs by extending the frameworks flexibility. This
generalization does not only allow solving BVPs, but facilitates integration of data
in combination with known physics to approximate a functional representation of
the hidden parts of the system. This is particularly useful in cases where underly-
ing physics are not fully understood and relevant data is available.
While not guaranteed or thouroughly investigated, some of the results and tech-
niques outlined in section 3.1, developed and studied for PINNs, may also be
relevant for UPINNs.
UPINNs have yet to be fully explored. This project aims to contribute to the under-
standing of this novel extension of the PINN framework, by investigating its abili-
ties regarding approximation of the hidden parts of a system. While the formula-
tion (3.12) assigns neural network representations to both the PDE and boundary
conditions, this project will focus on problems related to the PDE part, i.e. F.

Symbolic Regression
Approximating a functional representation of a system is a powerful tool. Trans-
lating it into a symbolic expressions is even more valuable. Symbolic regression
is the data-driven process of obtaining governing symbolic expressions from ob-
served data.
In the context of UPINNs, the unknown parts of the system are approximated by
neural networks. If these approximations are sufficiently accurate, they can be
used to densely sample the unknown dynamics within the domain. From this,
symbolic regression methods may be used to find the underlying symbolic ex-
pressions.
This project utilize the symbolic regression frameworks of SINDy for ODEs and
PDE find for PDEs. Thus, the following section provide a brief introduction to
these methods.

The Navier-Stokes equations are a set of non-linear PDEs that describe the mo-
tion of fluid flow. This project considers the 2D incompressible fluid flow equations
given by,
ut + λ1(uux + vuy) + px −λ2(uxx + uyy) = 0,

(6.3)
ux + vy = 0,

where (u, v) is the velocity field, p is the pressure, and the parameters λ1 and
λ2 are the convective scaling factor and kinematic viscosity respectively. The
last equation is the incompressibility constraint, expressing divergence-free flow.
Note, for the purposes of this project, there are no boundary conditions, implying
the system is exclusively constrained by the data and system dynamics.
The solution consists of the velocity field (u, v) and the pressure p. The pressure is
only constrained by its gradients, thus p can only be determined up to an additive
integration constant if not constrained by absolute measurements. The specific
instance of (6.3) considered, is the example from [5], which describe the flow
around a cylinder, specifically the turbulent wake flow behind it. The full dataset
consists of 1000000 simulated measurements of u, v and p at steady state, that is,
the vortex shedding has stabilized. The domain is (x, y, t) ∈[1, 8]×[−2, 2]×[0, 20]
and the specific system is (6.3) with λ1 = 1 and λ2 = 0.01.
With inspiration from [5], the incompressibility constraint is strongly enforced with
an ansatz, by letting the surrogate estimate (ψ, p) with u = ψy and v = −ψx rather
than (u, v, p). This ensures that any approximation is trivially divergence-free as,
ux + vy = ψyx −ψxy = 0.
To simplify visualization, u and v are illustrated by the vorticity field ω = vx −uy
rather than seperately.

7
Hidden discovery with UPINNs
Having introduced the set of test problems, and verified that regular PINNs can
solve forward and inverse problems on these systems, this chapter focus on using
UPINNs to solve hidden discovery problems, i.e. detecting and learning residual
dynamics from data and partially known systems.
These 4 systems are explored in the order presented in chapter 5. The aim is to
explore the UPINN idea and its capabilities to discover and model hidden dynam-
ics in a variety of settings. Further, symbolic regression, by means of SINDy and
PDE-find, is applied to learn equations from the discovered dynamics.
Some of the following sections explore hyperparameter tuning. In these cases, in-
termediate results are not necessarily applied chronologically. Checking all com-
binations of hyperparameters and architectures is a combinatorial problem that is
not feasible to solve exhaustively in general. Keeping a baseline model that is
varied in each experiment, i.e one-factor-at-a-time search, makes a better foun-
dation for evaluation and comparison. While this does not guarantee that the
combination of the marginal best models will be the overall best, it serves as a
good foundation for understanding how the model behaves when applied to the
problem at hand. Additionally, the main goal is not to find hyperoptimal models,
but rather to explore the capabilities of UPINNs.

7.1.5
Brief note on interpretability using KANs
Neural networks are often criticized for their black-box nature. Although they can
approximate any function arbitrarily well, they lack capabilities in providing insight
into the underlying dynamics that they model. KANs are different in this context,
as they approximate a computational chain of activation functions, potentially al-
lowing for better interpretability. Figure 7.4 shows two instances of a [2,2,2] KAN
with gridsize 5 trained as the residual network in the baseline setup, aiming to ap-
proximate the interactions βxy and −γxy. Appendix A.2 provides a more detailed
explanation of how KANs can approximate interactions.
While human interpretation of the learned activation functions in figure 7.4 is not a
formal scientific measure, the input layer of the left KAN, though not perfect, could
arguably resemble linear functions with the second layer showing quadratic like
structure. Such a KAN is able to represent the product xy as shown by equation
A.2 in Appendix A.2. Applying the build-in symbolic regression tool in the Python
KAN library did not yield these results, but rather more complicated expressions.
Arguably, the learned activation functions in figure 7.4 are more interpretable for
humans than the learned weights of a regular FNN. The right KAN in figure 7.4
could perhaps be interpreted as the case presented in equation A.1 in Appendix
A.2, allbeit if one did not know the true terms, this seems like a stretch.
Note, this serves merely as an example of the interpretability that KANs could
potentially provide, rather than a formal in depth analysis of the architecture as
that is beyond the scope of this project.

The PDE-find model uses a function library containing up to 2nd order derivatives

8.4
Model setup
A central advantage of PINNs is the ability to use densly sampled collocation
points to evaluate the PDE residuals across the entire domain. However, the ge-
ometric terms of the PDE in (8.1) are only given on a discrete grid in the domain,
which also make computing gradients with automatic differentiation challenging.
To overcome this, these terms are approximated with FNNs with 2 layers of 20
neurons each and Tanh activation function. This allows for gradient computation
and continuous evaluation of the PDE residuals. The neural network approxima-
tions can be seen in figure C.6 in Appendix C.
The loss in this model consists of 2 terms, the PDE loss and the zero flux loss at the
center of the plasma, ρ = 0. The data loss remain constant as it is approximated
already by the above mentioned FNNs.
The models will use FNNs with 2 hidden layers of 20 neurons each and Tanh
activation function, for each of the profiles of D, V and S. Training is done for
100000 epochs using Adam optimizer with learning rate 1e-3.

9
Discussion of results
UPINNs are a novel deep learning approach to hidden discovery problems, com-
bining the concepts of physics-informed neural networks and universal differential
equations. By utilizing data, UPINNs solve incomplete differential equations while
simultaneously estimating the missing terms.
Having conducted a range of experiments with UPINNs on a variety of problems,
this chapter discuss the obtained results and their implications. While UPINNs
showed promising results in a variety of settings, certain limitations emerged that
may be addressed in future work.
9.1
Undesired estimation
Throughout chapter 7, it became evident that UPINNs have a tendency to make
undesired estimation. This would often not affect the estimated solution, but could
lead to inaccurate parameter estimates, unnecessary complexity in model resid-
uals and hinder interpretability using symbolic regression frameworks.
UPINNs, in their general formulation, are highly flexible, which comes at the risk of
estimating known dynamics, if not sufficiently constrained. Subsequently, UPINNs
should be used with caution and careful consideration as to prevent these issues.
As shown, incorporating inductive biases on the hidden dynamic structure and
leveraging additional data can help mitigate these challenges. If left completely
unconstrained, the residual network can overfit the PDE loss, artificially lowering
it and halt training, rather than capturing the true hidden dynamics.
9.1.1
Undesired estimation of known dynamics
In section 7.1.3, it was seen that UPINNs ability to simultaneously solve hidden
discovery and inverse problems simultaneously depends on the form of the un-
known dynamics. The results indicated that insufficiently constrained systems
allow UPINNs to estimate dynamics that are already known, while terms with un-
known parameters may only be partially estimated. Consider splitting the first term
of the first equation in the Lotka-Volterra system into two fractional terms,
dx
dt = α1x + α2x −βxy,
α = α1 + α2.
As α is unknown and treated as a trainable parameter, the residual network is free
to estimate α2x −βxy rather than just −βxy. Section 7.1.6 confirmed existence
90
Universal Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Hidden Discovery
==PAGE:101==
of this behavior with results from SINDy, which was able to extract the part of the
known dynamics estimated by the residual network. While this does not prevent
obtaining a solution network with low error, it is undesired behavior, leading to
inaccurate parameter estimates that are not unique, while the residual network
becomes unnecessarily complicated and less interpretable. This also explains
why fixing δ in the second equation and using a shared residual network allowed
accurate estimation of α. By constraining the residual network to model γxy, the
shared structure naturally enforce βxy in the first equation, ensuring that α can be
accurately estimated.
It was tried adding a regularization term on the residual network output in the
loss, which improved the results when no residuals were present but worsened
them otherwise. Small residuals are not necessarily desirable, as they may arise
naturally when modelling known terms with unknown parameters. The overall
issue is that no constraints are in place to inform the network of existing terms
with unknown parameters. No solution was found to this issue, but it is a clear
limitation of the current UPINN formulation.
9.1.2
Phantom estimation
In the section 7.2, it was seen that a UPINN model which trivially enforced net zero
flow in an SIR model with resusceptibility, estimated a flow that was not present in
the data. However, the solution network still remained accurate, suggesting that
the residual network estimated a ”phantom” flow - a flow which does not exist, but
does not change the solution either.
This may be explained by considering how the dynamics of an SIR model would
respond to an artificial flow from R to S and I. It will inevitably result in an in-
creased number of susceptibles and infected, inducing natural flows from S to I
and I to R by the known dynamics, cancelling out the artificial flow and leaving
the overall dynamics unchanged. While regularizing the residual network output
did not reduce estimation of known dynamics in the Lotka-Volterra system, it did
reduce the phantom flow in the SIR model.
Although this behavior was only observed in the SIR model, it could potentially
occur in other systems with several equations as well. This is a clear example
of the residual network performing undesired estimations that do not affect the
solution.

91
==PAGE:102==
9.1.3
Residual estimation by solution network
In the Navier Stokes experiments of section 6.4, it was seen that the model strug-
gled to estimate the pressure when not constrained by data. It became evident
that the residual network was partially estimating the pressure term. This is per-
haps not surprising, as the pressure was unconstrained by data and its partial
derivatives appeared in the PDEs as free terms, which is also the case for the
residual network. Subsequently, the residual network prediction and pressure
part of the solution network got entangled, leading to wrongly estimated pressure
and residuals, allbeit, they were collectively accurate which allowed the velocity
field to be estimated accurately.
This further emphasizes the need for additional constraints on the known dynam-
ics to prevent the residual network from estimating them.
9.2
Inductive bias
The UPINN results presented in [8] used inductive priors on the residual networks.
That is, assumptions regarding the structure of the hidden dynamics were im-
posed on the model.
For the Lotka-Volterra system, the assumption is that the hidden dynamics are
proportional between the two equations, directly coupling them through a train-
able scalar parameter. Section 7.1.4 showed that this assumption consistently
lowered the model error for a range of combinations of noise and sparsity levels.
Further, section 7.1.3 showed that it also enabled the model to estimate one of the
parameters in the known dynamics, while also accurately estimating the unknown
dynamics, partially solving the issue discussed in section 9.1 by introducing a di-
rect dependence between the hidden dynamics of the two equations.
For Burgers’ equation, this included passing partial derivatives to the residual net-
work. In section 7.3.2, it was demonstrated that a UPINN model can be trained
without this inductive bias and still obtain accurate results. Subsequently, in sec-
tion 7.3.3, it became apparent that such a model fails to be a surrogate of the
true dynamics. This suggests that the neural networks were unable to learn the
underlying differential operator, and merely served as an instance specific guide
to the solution network during training. In contrast, using the suggested inductive
bias allowed solving the problem with a data-free PINN and allowed extrapolation
beyond the training data.
While the model without inductive bias did yield a residual network suitable for
direct use in post training purposes, section 7.3.4 showed that it can be used to
92
Universal Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Hidden Discovery
==PAGE:103==
recover the governing equations using PDE-find. More on this in section 9.3.
If the goal is solely to obtain the solution, inductive biases may not be necessary,
and the model is still able to obtain good results. However, for extrapolation or
tasks requiring knowledge of the differential operator, they can be crucial.
The motivation for testing the removal of inductive biases, is that prior knowledge
of unknown dynamics can generally not be assumed available. While feasible in
simpler problems, like Burgers’ equation, to pass all possible first and/or second
order partial derivatives to the residual network, it quickly becomes infeasible for
larger problems like the Navier-Stokes equations considered in section 6.4. That
system in particular, with 3 states and 3 domain dimensions, results in 9 partial
derivatives of first order and 18 of second order. Computing every single one of
these on all collocation points in every epoch quickly proved computationally in-
feasible. However, the experiments showed that good results can still be obtained
by only passing the states and tempospatial positions to the residual network.
9.3
Equation recovery
Being able to model residuals using known dynamics and available data to ob-
tain a surrogate solution is a powerful tool - recovering the governing equations,
even more so. This was demonstrated for all of the tested systems, with varying
degrees of success, using the SINDy and PDE-find frameworks.
In all cases, the correct equations were recovered in many of the tested scenarios.
For the Lotka-Volterra system, the model using a shared residual network, i.e.
inductive bias, could be used to correctly identify the equations in both sparse and
noisy data. Without the inductive bias, it was still able to recover the equations
but struggled with the highest noise levels and sparsest data.
A quite surprising results was recovering the correct form of the hidden dynamics
in Burgers’ equation using data from a single time point with 100% noise using
the inductive bias model. Despite the extreme noiselevels, the structure of the
sharp wave interface remained clear enough for the residual network to model
it sufficiently accurate for PDE-find to determine the correct form of the equation.
This was not possible without the inductive bias, highlighting its significance. Even
so, without inductive bias, it was still possible to recover the equation with data
from 3 time points with up to 10% noise, and also using a single noiseless time
point.
Notably, the found equations of Burgers’ were consistently estimated with similar
scalar value, which deviated from the true value significantly. This suggests that
Universal Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Hidden Discovery
93
==PAGE:104==
the data resolution at the sharp wave interface was insufficient for UPINN to ac-
curately capture the extremity of the dynamics. However, it was accurate enough
for PDE-find to recover the correct form of the equation. Furthermore, it was seen
that a regular PINN can accurately estimate the true scalar value, ultimately lead-
ing to an accurate recovery of the equation.
Navier Stokes equations posed a more challenging problem - attempting the re-
cover a total of 4 second order terms in the two equations, without inductive bias.
Even so, the correct equations were recovered on noise-free data in all tested sce-
narios except the most sparse with 100 datapoints. Further, some of the terms
could be recovered with 1% noise with 1000 and 2000 datapoints, but never all
of them. With further refinements to the model, training process and tuning of
PDE-find, it may be possible to recover the correct equations in all scenarios.
Collectively, these experiments showed that UPINNs have the potential to facili-
tate physics informed data-driven discovery of governing equations in sparse and
noisy data settings, where fully data-driven methods may struggle.
9.4
Future work
The experiments conducted in this thesis have only scratched the surface of UP-
INNs potential, and the results have indicated multiple directions for future work.
The perhaps most immediate would be to investigate undesired estimation and the
use of inductive biases in further detail, finding better ways to prevent the resid-
ual network from estimating known dynamics. Notably, identify methods that can
effecttively penalize or completely constrain the residual network from estimating
forms of known dynamics with unknown parameters.
Systems with multiple equations in generel, would be an interesting topic for fur-
ther investigation, as results suggested that UPINNs can be too flexible compared
to the constraints in these cases. Future work could investigate the use of addi-
tional inductive biases to address these issues.
Results also showed that combining UPINNs with symbolic regression can re-
cover governing equations in many cases. Future work could further investigate
the use of PySINDy or apply other symbolic regression frameworks, such as PySR
[59], AI Feynman [22], gplearn or others. Improving performance on noisy and
sparse data using models with fewer or no inductive biases, would be a valuable
contribution.
Regarding the plasma physics problem of chapter 8, the experiments showed

promising results, but further work is needed to obtain a more accurate model.
Future work could focus on a more thorough investigation of PINN optimization
techniques and hyperparameter tuning. Unfortunatly, the limited time scope of
this thesis did not allow for such an investigation to be conducted.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to investigate the use of Universal Physics-Informed Neural
Networks (UPINNs) for discovering hidden physics in dynamical systems. The
first part established the mathematical foundation of neural networks, covering
artificial neurons, activation functions, the training process and various network
architectures. This was followed by an introduction to regular PINNs, along with
techniques that have shown to improve their performance, ultimatly leading to
the formulation of UPINNs and the second part of the thesis, which focused on
applying UPINNs to a variety of ODE and PDE systems.
Both regular PINNs and UPINNs have been applied to a selection of ODE and
PDE systems, including the Lotka-Volterra predator-prey model, the SIR epidemic
model, Burgers’ equation and the Navier-Stokes equations for incompressible
fluid flow. The results demonstrate that UPINNs are well capable of modelling
the residuals of partially known systems, even when data is sparse and/or noisy.
Additionally, the neural network approximations of the hidden dynamics were so
precise that the symbolic regression frameworks of SINDy and PDE-find could, in
many cases, accurately recover the hidden equations from a set of densely sam-
pled residuals. Additionally, it was shown that UPINNs can perform well without
the inductive bias suggested by the original UPINN paper [8]. Allbeit, it improves
convergence, robustness to noise and generalization.
It was demonstrated that the high flexibility of the general UPINN formulation,
allow the residual network to perform undesired estimation of insufficiently con-
strained known dynamics, or even introduce non-existing ”phantom” dynamics
that do not alter the solution. This issue was particularly evident in inverse prob-
lems and systems with several equations. A fundamental challenge of this be-
havior is that the solution is often unaffected, making it difficult to detect. In these
scenarios, imposing inductive biases on the model can be crucial for obtaining
meaningful and consistent results.
Many of the well studied techniques for optimizing the training process and con-
vergence rate of regular PINNs were found to have a less significant effect on UP-
INNs. Notably, the PDE residual modelling was enhanced in some cases when
applying softadapt weight loss balancing and/or residual based adaptive distribu-
tion (RAD) refinement on the collocation points during training.
96
Universal Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Hidden Discovery
==PAGE:107==
While the tested architectures, including FNNs, ResNets and KANs (and com-
binations of them), often performed similarly, the standard feed-forward neural
network was found to be the most reliable and robust choice. The most signif-
icant improvements were achieved with wider networks, adaptive sampling and
weight loss balancing, but did dependent on the specific problem.

B.1
More on activation functions in PINNs
ReLU
The Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) is simple and computationally efficient due to
its piecewise linear nature. By outputting exactly 0 for all negative inputs, ReLU
can introduce sparsity in the network, essentially deactivating neurons that may
not contribute to the output, which in turn could indicate that a simpler model is
sufficient.
As the first derivative of ReLU vanishes for all negative inputs, if a neuron is acci-
dentally deactivated it cannot be reactivated, a phenomenon often reffered to as
the dead ReLU problem. This can slow down or even halt training, as the weights
of the affected neurons will not be updated.
Leaky ReLU
Leaky ReLU is a variant of ReLU that is designed to rectify the dead ReLU prob-
lem by introducing a small slope for negative inputs. This retains much of the
computational effenciency and ensures non-zero gradients for all inputs, prevent-
ing neurons from deactivating permenantly. The slope is typically kept relatively
small, e.g. 0.01 [61], but is a hyperparameter that can be tuned to the specific
application.
SiLU (Swish)
The Sigmoid Linear Unit is a smooth approximation of ReLU, differentiable ev-
erywhere. It avoids the dead ReLU problem by having non-zero gradients for all
inputs. This smoothness is appealing in the context of PINNs, which often require
higher order derivatives.
SiLU is computationally more expensive than ReLU due to the exponential oper-
ation, but is used in practice due to its smooth properties.

Sigmoid
The sigmoid function is a classic activation function, squashing the input between
0 and 1 in a saturating manner. Despite its popularity in the early era of neural net-
works, the sigmoid function has fallen out of favor due to zero-centered activation
functions converging faster.
The squashing nature also means that it suffers the vanishing gradient problem,
as gradients approach 0 for numerically large inputs.
Tanh
The hyperbolic tangent function, is also a squashing function, but with a range of
]−1, 1[. In contrast to the sigmoid function, tanh is zero-centered, making it suitable
for problems where the solution is expected to be symmetric or oscillatory, which
is quite common in dynamical systems.
Tanh is the most used activation function in PINNs. It has shown empirical success
in many applications and was also the activation function used in the original paper
on PINNs [5].
Sin
The sine function is periodic and infinitely smooth, thus potentially useful for prob-
lems with periodic solutions. Unlike sigmoid and tanh, the sine function does not
suffer from vanishing gradients for large inputs due to its periodic nature, and does
not saturate either.
However, due to lack of evidence regarding its effectiveness and poor ability to
generalize, it is rarely used in practice.

Example: UPINN for Burgers' equation
The following code snippet is an example of setting up and training a UPINN model using the
framework. The example trains a UPINN model for Burgers’ equation with unknown convection
term using a FNN with 8 hidden layers of 20 neurons each with Tanh activation functions. It
employs SoftAdapt with β = 0.1 and loss weighting every 100 epochs and alternate training with
Adam and L-BFGS optimizers for 1000 epochs each followed by RAD refinement of collocation
points. Further, it use the inductive bias of passing the solution network output and its partial
derivatives as input to the residual network. Notice how this inductive bias is implemented by
overwriting the F_input method.
Note that the class BurgerData is a custom class formatting the data for the Burgers’ equation into
the correct format. The class is not included in the code snippet but can be found in the provided
