Abstract
This thesis presents a rheological evaluation of biologically based concrete composite suited for
extrusion 3D printing. The novel material comprises a biopolymer binder, which was used as an alter-
native cementitious binder for printable concrete. The bio-based concrete mix requires speciﬁc rheo-
logical properties that allow easy extrudability and good layer-by-layer buildability through thixotropic
behaviour and thermoplasticity of the material. Initially, by altering mix design and subsequently, its
experimental testing, ranges of material compositions suited for 3D printing were identiﬁed in terms of
binder concentration, binder to sand ratio and temperature of the material during printing. The work pre-
sented in this thesis provides information on how rheological characteristics affected the suitability of the
material for extrusion-based 3D printing, as well as the quality of the printed object. Furthermore, a mate-
rial thixotropic behaviour and temperature dependency was assessed by oscillatory rheometer testing and
results were evaluated in terms of material complex modulus development. Additionally, the inﬂuence
of binder concentration was comparatively evaluated from penetration test results and indicated faster
stiffness increase with the increase of biopolymer concentration. Finally, the correlation between the
quantitative and qualitative results helped to formulate a printability characteristic of bio-based concrete
composite, which was validated by 3D printed organically shaped object.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Research motivation
In the past decades, 3D printing manufacturing has been developed rapidly and expanded to several
industries including the construction industry. Introducing additive manufacturing and 3D printing tech-
nologies in the construction area have resulted in several advantages compared to the traditional building
methods. 3D printing provides an economical advantage by reducing building time and human resources
at the construction site [20] and by building without formwork can save up to 50 % in cost compared
to cast concrete [21]. Additionally, complete digitalization of construction can improve the current en-
vironmental issues resulting from concrete production. Due to the shape optimization and precise mate-
rial placement, topological optimization, the material waste and amount needed for construction can be
reduced considerably and consequently resulting in the reduction of CO2- emissions from concrete pro-
duction [21]. Moreover, 3D printing of cement-based materials provides architectural freedom in term
of formwork free structure, which creates a possibility of designing complex shapes and textures[22].
Although there are clear beneﬁts of incorporating 3D printing technology into the building industry there
remain some difﬁculties and challenges that hinder its widespread use. The challenges mainly comprise
the material performance and compatibility with the 3D printer. The printable concrete must be ﬂuid
enough to be extruded by the printer but also rigid enough to retain its shape and to support subsequent
layers without collapse [21]. These speciﬁc rheological properties for printable concrete composite re-
quire an excessive usage of additives, which increases the cost of the structure as well as the impact
on the environment. Even though that material consumption is reduced, the printable material usually
requires higher cementitious content in the mixture due to the reduced aggregates grain size [21]. As
a result, the positive aspect for the environment by material saving is balanced out by a larger cemen-
titious material usage. The constant development of digital fabrication methods and development in
construction material science is needed to improve the 3D printing performance of concrete, its struc-
tural stability and environmental impact. In recent years, a huge amount of research has been carried
out, to optimize the concrete for the 3D printing process, however, the main problem remains. Cement
used as the main binding material for 3D printing concrete continues to be the biggest contributor to the
emission of greenhouse gases due to its large-scale use [23]. Though there are trending improvement
of partially replacing cement by different additives such as ﬂy ash, silica fume, slag, etc., the share of
cement in material composition still prevail. There is, therefore, a great need to ﬁnd alternative ways for
sustainable concrete production that balance societal demands, environmental condition and technical
requirements in 3D printing. Ongoing research on DTU Civil Engineering has investigated the potential
of using biologically based binding material for concrete in constructional 3D printing [1]. The novel
material consists of biopolymer resourced from industrial waste products such as animal tissues and
bones and is used as a complete replacement of cementitious material. The biopolymer binder acquires
a rapid change in viscosity due to its thermoplastic hardening, which indicates favourable property for

Chapter 1. Introduction
3D printing. The biopolymer concrete composite, further referred to as BPC, comprises the concrete
composite consistency with polymer thermoplastic behaviour. Together with its more environmentally
friendly production, biopolymer binder shows the potential for a substitution of the traditional cement-
based binder. Although the researcher has reviewed the general characteristic of the material and its
mechanical performance [24], its rheological properties and suitability for 3D printing manufacturing
and has not been yet investigated in details.
1.2
Project objectives
Given the potential of using biopolymer concrete in additive manufacturing, this study aimed to inves-
tigate the printability performance and rheological properties of the BPC suited for extrusion-based 3D
printing. The main objectives are listed as follows:
1. Identiﬁcation of suitable concentrations and temperature ranges for good printability of biopoly-
mer concrete.
2. Determination of rheological characteristics of printable BPC as potential criteria for formulation
of suitable material properties for extrusion-based 3D printing.
The outcome of this study will provide the identiﬁcation of ranges for optimal mix design of BPC
suited for 3D printing together with the rheological formulation of this novel material.
1.3
Outline
This thesis consists of 5 chapters in total, details of which are provided below:
Chapter 1 gives a brief introduction to the background and motivation of this study.
Chapter 2 provides background on 3D printing manufacturing and extrusion-based system in the con-
struction industry and review necessary theory to understand the rheology and rheological characteristic
of printable materials such as concrete and hydrogels.
Chapter 3 reviews the current trends in the materials used for 3D printing as well as common testing
methods evaluating the suitability and characteristic of printable concrete material.
Chapter 4 describes the methodology for printability assessment of BPC as well as investigation
of rheological characteristics of the suitable composite. The chapter concludes on material printing
suitability that combines quantitative experimental evaluation and rheological characterization.
Chapter 5 summarizes the present research work and discuss brieﬂy suggestion for future research
development.

Research background
Formulation of the material properties suited for the 3D printing process is based on the understanding of
a general principle of 3D printing manufacturing and the fundamental theory of Rheology and Rheology
in 3D printing. This chapter provided a literature review on the aforementioned topics with a deeper
study of the concrete and polymer rheological properties needed for 3D printing.
The ﬁrst section discusses 3D printing technologies and constructional 3D printing, mostly following
A. Perrot [21]. In the second section, some general principles of Rheology are introduced based on T.G.
Mezger [6] and the third section covers the rheological properties of concrete and follows closely N.
Russel and A. Perrot [25, 8, 26]. The last part focuses on the rheology of hydrogels in 3D printing,
mainly reviewed from H. Li and T. Jang [27, 28].
2.1
3D printing process
The goal of this section is to review 3D printing technologies with special emphasis on 3D printing
in the construction industry. Firstly, general characterization of 3D printing is introduced followed by
summarization of different printing processes. Afterwards, an extrusion-based printing process in the
construction industry is presented in more details.
2.1.1
3D printing technologies
3D printing is a process of fabrication of object through the deposition of material from a 3D data
model. There exist many deﬁnitions or terms to describe 3D printing, such as additive manufacturing,
digital fabrication or rapid prototyping [21]. Compare to traditional manufacturing techniques, digital
fabrication allows for the fast and efﬁcient production of more complex objects. With its ability to
directly fabricate 3D object from the digital model the manufacturing process requires less time, less
energy and producing less waste. It is considered as the industrial revolution since the mid-1980s and
has been widely used in the automotive, aerospace, biomedical and food industry [21].
The general principle of 3D printing can be used for different materials and different manufacturing
processes. Firstly, the 3D object is created by the software. The ﬁle is exported in a speciﬁc format and
converted into a list of commands for the 3D printer to carry on. After the establishment of the list of
instruction for a 3D printer, the process of printing can start. There exist many different types of 3D
printing technologies. The American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) for example distinguish
7 different categories [29]:
• Material extrusion: It is used to print multilayered pattern by extruding material through a noz-
zle. It is the most widely used system and the least expensive one. This method is commonly used
for plastic material also called Fused deposition modelling (FDM). FDM builds an object by heat-
ing and extruding thermoplastic ﬁlaments following the prescribed path. The main material used

by this system is a polymer. However, in recent years the composite material such as concrete,
ceramics and clay have been extruded using this 3D printing technique as well.
• Binder jetting: Or powder bed printing, is a process when liquid binders are deposited on the
layers of powder grains. The binder acts as an adhesive between the powder layers. Binder jetting
is used for a variety of materials such as metals, ceramics and polymers.
• Powder bed fusion: During this process powder layer is ﬁrstly deposited and afterwards agglom-

• Direct Energy deposition: It is a more complex printing process and covers several different types

• Materials jetting: This process is similar to the extrusion process where drops of materials are

light.
• Photopolymerization: A liquid photopolymer used for creating object layer by layer is hardened
by selective exposure to light (UV). This process is called polymerization and exposed areas to
light cures or solidiﬁes.
• Sheet lamination: This method mostly uses sheets of metal that are thermally bond together by
using ultrasonic welding or adhesive. The sheets of metal are sequentially layered and the object
is deﬁned by its thickness.
For purpose of this thesis, the focus will be only on the extrusion-based printing process and material
rheological properties suited for this type of printing techniques.
2.1.2
Additive manufacturing in construction
In the last few years, new technologies of 3D printing have been developed for the construction indus-
try as well. The construction industry is one of the least automated industries and the introduction of
3D printing can lead to many advantages such as reduction of construction cost, time, material waste,
energy and at last more architectural freedom for fabrication of complex structures [30, 21]. Traditional
3D printing techniques are mainly based on thermo-dependent properties of materials to achieve a fast
solidiﬁcation and rapid printing rate. The same principle cannot be directly applied for the printing of
cement-based materials and technology need to be adjusted to suit the concrete printing [21].
Extrusion-based printing system
The extrusion-based method is the most common printing method used in construction. This method,
used for printing concrete, was ﬁrstly developed in the early 1990s by Professor Khoshnevis [31] and is
widely used nowadays by companies such as XtreeE [32], Construction 3D [33] and Contour Crafting [4]
[21]. During the extrusion/deposition process, the structure is created by depositing fresh cementitious
material layer-by-layer along a deﬁned path [34]. Material is extruded by a nozzle mounted on a robotic
control system of different sizes. In the extrusion method concrete is ﬁrstly mix traditionally and after
has to transported to the printing head. The two most common types of transportation can be applied:
transportation systems by a pump connected to the printing head and a tank mounted directly on the
printing head. Both systems have their limitations. In the case of pump the consistency of concrete can
be limited to ensure concrete ﬂowability for pumping and in the case of the tank solution the amount

2.1.3
Challenges of 3D concrete printing
Like with every system, constructional 3D printing has as well some limitations and challenges that have
to be overcome for its successful implementation. The following sections discuss these challenges in
more details.
Reinforcement in the 3D printed structure
One of the aspects that should be a focus on, is the reinforcement of structures to provide good me-
chanical properties and improvement in structural integrity. The conventional reinforced structure is not
possible to produce by extrusion printing and new techniques need to be explored. There are already
some solutions such as the use of ﬁbre reinforcement which could improve ductility and brittleness of
concrete [22] or the use of an additional robot to place the reinforcement into the speciﬁc voids in printed
structures [20].
Architectural limitation
Even though 3D printing allowing for more architectural freedom there are some limits in terms of shapes
as well. Due to the material low green strength after extrusion, a fabrication of signiﬁcantly overhanging
parts are not possible to print and additional supports need to be used [20, 21].
Cementitious material compatible with 3D printer
3D printable concrete material may be the most important limitation for 3D printing in construction.
Cementitious material for 3D printing has new demands compare to traditionally cast concrete. The
main difference is due to the loss of formwork. Without the formwork, the shape of the object is fully
deﬁned by material rheology and fresh properties. Material has to acquire sufﬁcient yield stress to retain
its shape after deposition and to withstand subsequently deposited layers without any major deformation
of the bottom layer. At the same time, composite has to be pumpable to the printing head and extrudable
from the printing nozzle [20]. It is therefore important to understand the rheology of concrete material
and its application in mixed design propositions.
2.2
General principal of Rheology
Rheology is a branch of science that studies the ﬂow and deformation of matter [6]. Rheology covers
many materials properties but in general, it describes how materials respond to a force. This force is
usually described in terms of stresses, which is the ratio of the force to its surface of application. The
response of materials to this force is either its deformation, strain or rate of deformation, strain rate [6].

Viscoelasticity
Rheology differs between ﬂuids with an ideal viscous behaviour and soft solids with ideal elastic defor-
mation. Between these two extremes are substances with viscoelastic behaviour [6]. Viscoelasticity is
a combination of viscous ﬂow behaviour and elastic deformation behaviour. Viscoelastic ﬂuids do not
ﬂow until critical stress is reached τc. This stress is called yield point. Below this point, ﬂuids exhibit
solid-like behaviour with elastic deformation (shear strain) γ:
γ = s/h

Elasticity:
G = τ/γ
(2.4)
To sum it up, viscoelastic material at ﬁrst acts as solid and its behaviour can be described by Law of
Elasticity (2.3), and after exceeding the yield point, material ﬂows and Law of Viscosity (2.2) is applied.

2.2.2
Flow behaviour and Flow curve
Different materials have different ﬂow behaviour under shear. The ﬂow behaviour can be represented by
two types of diagrams: Flow curve and Viscosity curve as illustrated in Figure 2.4.
Figure 2.4: The ﬂow curve (left) and viscosity curve (right) of ideally viscous ﬂuid [6].
Flow curve results from plotting shear stress τ versus shear rate ˙γ. The viscosity curve is afterwards,
calculated by applying the Law of Viscosity (2.2).
If the shear stress changes linearly with the shear rate the material is considered as ideally viscous
ﬂuids also called Newtonian ﬂuids. In this category liquids such as water, oils, syrups, etc. can be placed.
They are characterised by constant viscosity independent of shear rate. Shear stress for Non-Newtonian
ﬂuids varies non-linearly with shear rate. Many non-Newtonian materials are viscoelastic, combining
initial elastic response and late viscous response [6].
Among the non-Newtonian ﬂuids, two main behaviour are observed: shear thinning and shear thick-
ening [7]. When the viscosity decreases with increasing shear rates this behaviour is called shear-
thinning. Shear-thinning behaviour can be caused by the structural reorganization of the ﬂuid molecules
due to ﬂow. Taking as an example, polymer entangled chains align due to the shear what results in
lower resistance to the ﬂow and a decrease in viscosity. In the shear-thinning category are for example
shampoos, toothpaste, glues, polymer solutions and polymer melts.
As the opposite to shear-thinning is shear-thickening behaviour. In this case, the viscosity increases
with the increasing shear rate. As the best example of shear thickening behaviour is a mixture of corn
starch and water. When shearing the solution act as solid but once the shear stops the solution acts like a
liquid.

Time-dependent behaviour - Thixotropy
Thixotropy is a reversible, time-dependent change in viscosity when the material is subjected to shear
stress. With higher applied load the viscosity decreases [6]. Thixotropy can be, therefore, deﬁned as
structural decomposition when load/stress is applied and structural regeneration upon reduction of load-
/stress [35, 36]. The viscosity of the material depends on the time applied shear stress and the time ma-
terial needs to recover to its initial state. Therefore, thixotropy is usually presented as a time-dependent
parameter [6]. As an illustration, a time-dependent viscosity behaviour is shown in Figure 2.6.

The ﬁgure presents viscosity behaviour before, during and after shear application. The ﬁrst part of the

thixotropy property of materials for printability assessment.
Temperature dependent behaviour
Deformation and ﬂow of some materials are besides time-dependency also temperature-dependent. Typ-
ical temperature-dependent behaviour is softening or melting of material when heated or solidiﬁcation,
crystallization or cold gelation when cooled down [6]. Viscosity is usually evaluated by a temperature-
dependent viscosity curve during the test with a constant share rate and linear increase of temperature
over time [6]. The viscosity of the material can either be decreasing due to softening or increasing during
the cooling. Temperature dependant behaviour plays a crucial role in the 3D printing of polymers melts
and can improve the printability of other materials as well.
2.2.3
Rheological measurement
Shear viscosity can be measured by subjecting materials to certain stress and measuring the resulting
strain or vice versa. There exist different types of measuring method ranging from simple tests deﬁning
ﬂowability of material up to the very speciﬁc measuring systems. This section will review some of these
methods.
Rotational test with spindle
The simple rotational test can be performed by using a cup with a spindle. Material is subjected to con-
stant shear rate (speed) and from the ﬂow resistance of the sample, the amount of torque (force) needed
to turn the spindle is measured. Afterwards, the viscosity can be calculated from the data provided. Vis-
cosity can be either calculated for a speciﬁc shear rate or over the range of shear rates to generate a ﬂow
curve. However, this type of test provides just relative viscosity value because the viscosity is correlated
to the size and geometry of the apparatus [6].

Rotational viscometers
Rotational viscometer works on the same principle as measuring test with a spindle. However, the
viscosity is given in absolute value due to the narrow shear gap of the measuring device. Viscometer can

2.3
Rheology in 3D printing
The goal of this section is to review the importance of the rheological properties of the materials used for
3D printing. For this purpose, the rheological characteristic of printable materials will be analyzed with
an introduction to the rheology of concrete and polymers/hydrogels and with a more detailed characteri-
zation of rheological parameters required for 3D printing.
2.3.1
Rheology of concrete
The concept of the rheology of concrete is important in the characterization of fresh concrete paste
behaviour. This chapter presents an overview of the rheological properties of fresh concrete and the
parameters that inﬂuence them.
In the terms of rheology, concrete in the fresh state can be considered as elastic-visco-plastic material.
Only when it overcomes the critical threshold value, yield stress τc, it ﬂows [21]. Below the yield stress
the cement materials deform elastically displaying elasto-plastic behaviour with elastic modulus E in
compression and shear modulus G in shearing [7]. The elastic stiffness modulus can be deﬁned as a ratio
of yield stress to the corresponding critical deformation in compression εc or shear γc:
E = σ0/εc
G = τ0/γc
(2.9)
After reaching the plastic criterion the cement-based materials displaying visco-plastic behaviour.
The most common models to describe the behaviour of cementitious materials for shear ﬂow are the
Bingham model (2.5) or Herschel-Bulkley model (2.6) [21].

Evolution of cement paste
Fresh cement material changes its chemical, physical and rheological properties over time in order to
fully gain strength and rigidity. This change in material characteristic is connected with the evolution of
yield stress in several stages [8].
At rest, after concrete mixing, disperse cement particles starting to ﬂocculate. Flocculation is the
process during which the particles of the dispersion form clusters due to colloidal attractive forces (elec-
trostatic interaction), creating a network of interacting particles (Figure 2.10 b) [21, 8]. Cementitious
material display at this stage initial yield stress γc0 and initial elastic modulus Gc0. At rest, internal
structure is build-up and hydration products nucleate, self-organize, at pseudo-contact points between
cement particles. This nucleation changes the colloidal interaction between cement particles into high
energy interactions creating a solid bridges (Figure 2.10 c). Elastic modulus and yield stress are increas-
ing. However, this process is reversible and when sufﬁcient shear stress is applied the structure can be
broken up and rebuilding again at rest [8]. This type of behaviour correspond to thixotropic characteris-
tic of cementitious materials. During the last stage the number and size of solid bridges increases to the
point they cannot be broken. Therefore, this process is irreversible with sudden increase in yield stress
and elastic modulus (Figure 2.10 d) [2].
Figure 2.10: Development of network of interacting particles. (a) Disperse cement particles (b) Flocculation of
cement particles. (c) Nucleation of hydration products at pseudo contact points. (d) Creation of solid bridges [8].
The increase in yield stress is usually considered linear at the early stage [26]. Accordingly, Roussel

where τ0,t=0 is material yield stress at the beginning of resting period and t is duration of resting period.

Factors affecting rheology of concrete
Concrete is a combination of a variety of particles. The properties of each ingredient, presented in the
mix, together with mix design and consistency inﬂuence the rheological behaviour of fresh cement paste.
Besides these factors, the rheology of concrete is changing over time with reversible and irreversible
processes as explained in the previous section. Altogether, the rheology of concrete is inﬂuenced by a
combination of many factors, some of which are outlined below:
• Particle packing: In general, a concrete mixture is designed with the maximum possible amount
of solid particles. The yield stress increases with the higher volume fraction of solid particles [17].
16
==PAGE:33==

On the other hand, a higher amount of particles results in higher viscosity and consequently to the
decrease of workability. Therefore, the solids concentration in the concrete mix must be optimized
in order to be easily transported, placed and ﬁnished [25].
• Aggregates shape and grading: The rough and angular aggregates create more voids and increase
internal friction in the concrete mix. As a result, more ﬁne aggregates and paste are required to ﬁll
up the voids. A well-graded set of aggregates results in good workability by helping to ﬁll up the
voids in a systematic manner [25].
• Water/cement ratio: The water/cement ratio inﬂuence both the yield stress and the plastic vis-
cosity. An increase in water content results in higher ﬂuidity and a decrease in internal friction.
Therefore, with higher w/c ratio viscosity is decreasing. However, a decrease in cohesion can lead
to segregation of the material [2].
• Mineral admixtures: There is a great variety of admixtures replacing the amount of cement to im-
prove the concrete properties. Their internal structure, particle size distribution and intermolecular
forces greatly inﬂuence the concrete behaviour [2]. For example, replacing concrete with ﬂy ash
will generally decrease yield stress. On the other hand, it can either increase or decrease its plastic
viscosity depending on ﬂay ash properties and interaction with cement particles [37]. Silica fume,
Nano-silica and limestone ﬁller can be used as supplementary cement material and can contribute
to better workability and strength improvement by modifying the packing density of cement grains
[38].
• Chemical additives: Superplasticizer, Retarder, Accelerator and Viscosity modifying agent affect
the properties of fresh cement paste signiﬁcantly. They inﬂuence ﬂow characteristic, setting time
and yield strength differently, depending on their molecular structure and chemical conﬁguration
[38]. Superplasticizers are used to reduced the water content while maintaining the same level
of workability. Retarder delays the hydration of cement by creating the insoluble layer around
cement particle. As oppose to retarding effect, accelerators are used to shorten the setting time and
to acquire enough strength right after deposition. Viscosity modifying agents are water-soluble
polymers enhancing dimensional stability and increasing plastic viscosity. They directly control
the ﬂow and rheological performance of cement paste [38].
The required mix design and workability of cement paste depend on the application for which concrete is
used. Different demands are for the traditionally cast concrete, different for self-consolidating concrete
and different for concrete used for 3D printing.
2.3.2
Rheology of cement-based materials for 3D printing
One of the main characteristics of the 3DCP is the absence of form-work. This creates new demands on
concrete behaviour in the early stage. There is a strong relationship between the rheological properties
of concrete and the required properties of fresh concrete for 3D printing. In the next section, the neces-
sary properties for the good printability of concrete are going to be presented together with the factors
inﬂuencing them.
During the stage of pumping, extrusion and deposition, concrete has to acts as a ﬂuid and right
after it has to exhibit the behaviour of solid to retain its shape and to sustain its weight and the weight
of the next layers [26]. This transition from liquid to solid state is the main characteristic of fresh
concrete suitable for 3D printing. In terms of rheology, concrete must exhibit visco-plastic behaviour
at the stage of pumping and low critical yield stress τc is required to ensure sufﬁcient ﬂowability and
extrudability. After deposition, materials show elasto-plastic behaviour when the yield stress of the
printed layer has to be bigger than the applied stresses. The stresses applied on the layer (i.e. gravity-
induced stress) gradually increase with the increasing number of printed layers and therefore the yield

stress and shear modulus should increase as well with time, to avoid bottom layers deformation [2]. As
mentioned before, there are several stages of yield stress evolution of concrete: ﬂocculation, structural
build-up and irreversible structural build-up. During the 3D printing process, cement particles ﬂocculate
after mixing and deﬂocculates during the pumping and extrusion. Low initial yield stress is favourable for
easier extrusion. Right after deposition, the material ﬂocculate again, exhibiting thixotropic behaviour.
The clusters of cement grains induce structural build-up and with nucleation of hydrates create solid
bridges that increase elastic modulus, developing a green strength [2]. At this stage, the process is still
reversible and can be broken by sufﬁcient shear stress. The material with increased yield stress can
support the subsequently deposited layer and minimized deformation. After this stage, there is a rapid
increase of strength during the irreversible structural build-up. The development of yield stress in 3D
printing is illustrated in the Figure 2.11.
Figure 2.11: Development of yield stress during the printing process [9].
A quality of 3D printing, called printability, can be described by terms extrudability and buildability
[39]. Both properties depend on the rheological properties of fresh cement paste and are described in
details below.
Extrudability
Extrudability describes the ability of the material to pass through the nozzle and extrude continuous
ﬁlament [39]. In addition to the pumping material through the nozzle, the extrusion of material has to be
consistent without blocking, segregating and bleeding. Extrudability is directly linked with the material
consistency and overall workability of the mixture. Initially, the material needs to be ﬂuid to be pumped
through the nozzle. This means that the material viscosity and yield stress has to be as low as possible
[8].
One of the factors affecting the extrusion of material is the particle size distribution [39]. In general,
the raw material should have a round shape and ﬁne particles in order to better control extrudability and
avoiding blockage for a speciﬁc water/binder ratio [38]. Several amounts of trials have been carried out to
ﬁnd the most suitable mixture for 3D printing. Malaeb et al. [40] observed that increasing the amount of
cement and decreasing the amount of sand display better extrudability. He also recommended a mixture
18
==PAGE:35==
2.3 Rheology in 3D printing
with a ﬁne aggregates-cement ratio of 1.28 and a ﬁne aggregate-sand ratio of 2. The maximum size of
aggregates should be 1/10 of the diameter of the printing nozzle. Perrot [26] suggested the maximum
size of aggregate of 2 mm for a small nozzle diameter of 9 mm together with a 3:2 sand-binder ratio. A
suitable mix can be further optimized by additives such as superplasticizers, retarders and accelerators.
The size and shape of the nozzle can have an effect on extrudability as well. In general, the smaller
size of the nozzle requires higher ﬂowability of material. Moreover, the nozzle can be build up with
additional trowels attached to the nozzle from the sides or from the top as proposed by [40]. The trowels
can inﬂuence the printing quality and stability of the printed layer.
Buildability
The characteristic of a material to be able to print layers on top of each other without layer deformation
is called buildability [26]. Buildability can be quantiﬁed as the number of ﬁlament layers that could be
build up and the level of deformation of the layers[39]. After the extrusion, the material needs to retain
its extruded shape and resist deformation due to self-weight and the weight of the layers subsequently
deposited. In contrast to the low viscosity and yield stress during the extrusion process, the printed layer
has to reach adequate yield stress right after deposition. In addition to that, while developing enough
stiffness at an early stage, the material has to still be capable to bind the adjacent layers [38].
In general, there are two types of failure analyzed in the literature: the plastic collapse and elastic
buckling [21, 26]. Plastic collapse occurs when the gravity-induced stresses in the bottom layer reach the
material yield stress. Elastic buckling is the main concern of slender vertical structures. The instability
under the structures owns weight is controlled by the change in the elastic modulus with testing time
[21].
The material stability can be improved by the mix design. More favourable results were achieved
by a high content of ﬁne aggregates together with the inclusion of ﬁbres [40, 2]. In addition to the
concrete mixture, the important factor inﬂuencing the buildability of concrete is the inaccuracy of the
layer placing, which could lead to eccentricities. Even the small misalignment of printed layers can lead
to buckling instability of the whole structure [21].
Open time / Time window
Time plays an important role in the 3D printing process. There are contradictory demands on material
and time for printing. The time when the material is printable, deﬁned as open time, should be long
enough for pumping and extrusion of material but short enough for better buildability [2]. Another term
used with respect to time is the time window. The time window is referring to the optimal time between
printed layers. The material should have enough time to develop sufﬁcient yield strength but not too long
to avoid the creation of cold joints. Therefore, it is important to ﬁnd the optimal time window between
the deposition of layers [21].

Rheology of polymer gels and its 3D printing
The widely used material for extrusion-based 3D printing are polymers. There exist many different types
of polymer suited for 3D printing. One of the recently used polymers in 3D printing are polymers gels,
hydrogels, mostly used for bioprinting [28]. This section provides a basic characteristic of gels and their
rheological properties with respect to 3D printing.
Polymer gels consist of crosslinked polymer networks and behave as viscoelastic solids [41].Polymer

bonded the gel is considered a strong gel and crosslinks can not be reformed if broken. On the other
hand, weak gel such as colloidal gels or biopolymer gels consists of physical entanglement of polymer
chains, which can be broken and reform again. The structure of gels and its rheological characteristic is

Similarly, as for other printing material, a successful realization of hydrogel printing depends heavily

3.2
Printability assessment of concrete for additive manufacturing
As the concrete is the most used material for consturctional 3D printing, its fresh properties for compati-
bility with 3D printing need to be analysed. Printability of concrete can be asses by material workability,
extrudability and buildability of the materials. All of the mentioned parameters are depending on and
inﬂuence by the rheological properties of concrete in the fresh state. There exist several methods for
measuring and assessing printability and rheology of concrete from simple slump tests up to advanced
rheological measurement devices (rheometers). Traditional methods such as slump test, ﬂow table test
and penetrometer test can be used to evaluate the workability of concrete and are prescribed by various
standards [51, 16, 52]. These methods, however, do not measure speciﬁc physical parameters of the
material and therefore, more sophisticated rheological measurement methods have to be used to conduct
properties such as shear stress, shear rate and viscosity. For this purpose, speciﬁc rheometers are used.
Additionally, extrudability and buildability can be determined by using the visual inspection method and
quantiﬁcation of extruded ﬁlaments [39]. Several different measuring methods currently used are listed
and described in this section.

Rheometer measurement
Rheometer has been used to quantify direct rheological property measurements such as yield stress and
plastic viscosity. There exist many tests for measuring different rheological parameters. Typical rota-
tional rheometer for concrete is very similar to the rheometer for ﬂuids such as polymers or oils where

ter: parallel plates, coaxial and vane [25]. Parallel plates rheometer consist of two discs one of which
rotate and second remain stable. Coaxial apparatus consist of cylinder mould and smaller cylinder which
is immerse into the material and one of the cylinders rotates. The vane rotational rheometer consist of
cylindrical container and rotational vane [25]. All of them are based on controlled speed of rotated shear
surface. Resistance of the material is measured by a torque on the surface. After measuring of torque
at different speed the torque and speed are plotted and the yield stress and viscosity are determined by
regression analysis [25]. Le T. et al. [39] used vane apparatus determining shear strength from maximum
torque. The same type of vane apparatus was used by Perrot [26] in his studies of structural build-up of

To characterize extrudability and buildability of concrete the manual simulation test of 3D printing can
be conducted and printed samples are after visually evaluated. The printing process can be simulated by
using the modiﬁed caulk gun as printing devices or by compress air gun, which was used by Dedenis et

of the printing process, the actual 3D printers can be used as an experimental device to evaluate the
printability of concrete and as a validation tool of the previous experimental results.

Extudability as an ability to pump concrete to the nozzle and after extruding a continuous ﬁlament can
be evaluated by the extrusion process and the inspection of printed ﬁlaments. Le T. et al. [39] evaluated
extrusion by printing 9 mm wide and 300 mm long ﬁlaments with a total length of 450 mm. Test was
evaluated as YES or NO depending if it was possible to extrude material successfully without blockage
or disruption. The extruded ﬁlaments can by after compare and evaluated by qualitative ranking in terms
of continuity and conformity [46].
Buildability
Buildability can be estimated by horizontal build test with measurement of the sample height or the
number of layers printed with minimum deformation [2]. However, it has not been speciﬁed the allowed
degree of deformation and therefore, the level of accepted deformation is evaluated intuitively. Another
way how to measure buildability is imitating the loading of layers on the sample material. Perrot et.al
[26] in their studies of structural build-up used two parallel plates between concrete sample to simulate
the loading of the deposited layers. The top plate was loaded by 1.5 N and recorded time between each
loading monitored the average building rate. The building rate ranged from 1.1 to 6 m.h−1. Plastic
failure was then detected by displacement of the upper plate and by monitoring surface fractures.

The concentration of BG dissolved in water is given in weight to volume (w/v) percentage concentration.
That is, BG will be given in units of grams (g) per 100 millilitres of water. Binder to the sand ratio (b/s)

composite can be written as:

100g sand

composites following nomenclature was proposed:
60BG40B30

4.2
Vicat apparatus/Open time
Vicat apparatus is usually used for measuring the setting time of the material. However, for the purpose
of this thesis, it was more relevant to evaluate an open time of the printable BPC. Open time refer to
the workability time limit of printable concrete due to the thixotropic and thermoplastic behaviour of the
material. Open time usually occurs earlier than the setting time of the material and has an inﬂuence on
extrudability and buildability criteria (layer time) as a result of workability loss that occurs over time.
However, the results of Vicat testing are not directly helpful in characterizing the speciﬁc open time due
to the thermoplastic nature of the material. Material thermoplastic hardening depends on heat losses after
extrusion which are inﬂuence by the thickness of the sample and surface area exposed to the ambient air.
For this reason, it was not possible to directly compare the stiffness development of the printed ﬁlament
and the tested samples of the Vicat test considering different shapes of tested samples. Results of the test
were, therefore, used for comparative evaluation between different concentrations of BG in the mixture.

Temperature sweeps
For evaluating temperature-dependent viscoelastic behaviour of the BPC composite the temperature
sweeps were carried on. The oscillatory shearing was performed with the constant amplitude and fre-
quency and with graduate temperature change. As a result, the temperature-dependent function of a
complex modulus was analysed. The test consisted of several steps for evaluating different rheological
characterization of BPC composites.
Before the graduate change of temperature, the material was tested with the constant strain of 0.002%
and constant temperature of 50◦C for 15 minutes to evaluate the thixotropic behaviour of the sample. To
characterise the structuration of thixotropic materials at rest, ﬁrstly, a well-deﬁned initial state has to be
achieved. For this reason, samples were pre-sheared at high strain for 30 seconds to break down any
structural build-up of material. The value of the pre-shear strain was chosen from the amplitude sweeps
test results, which are presented in follow up section.
As a second part of the test, the temperature-dependency was analysed by gradually decreasing tem-
perature at the constant low strain of 0.002%. Similarly, as for thixotropic measurement, a pre-shear of
15% for 30 seconds was applied before the temperature change. This step simulated the condition for
concrete under a certain shear rate during the extrusion process. Afterwards, the material was cooled
down from the initial 50◦C to the 20◦C with a constant gradient of 1.5◦C per minute.
Additionally, two steps were added to the testing. At the beginning of the test, the material was left at
rest for two minutes to reached a prescribed temperature of 50◦C. Another step was added at the end of
the temperature decrease. Measurement after continued for another 20 minutes in a constant temperature

It was observed that all samples exhibited a shear-thinning behaviour, where the complex modulus
was decreasing with increasing shear strain due to the breakage of weak physical bonds like hydrogen
bonds under large strain. For 60BG and 150BG paste the elastic region is not clearly visible and γL can
be assumed at the low value of 0.002 %. For the concentrations 80BG and 100BG, LVE region was more
noticeable with a limiting strain value of 0.003% and for 120BG the so-called plateau value reached up
to 0.008 %.
Contrary, the highest and the lowest concentrations of BG resulted in the highest G∗at low applied
strain and within LVE region. Concentration 80BG and 100BG showed very similar results across the
entire testing and the lowest value of G∗in LVE region was observed for 120BG. There is no clear
tendency for G∗values with respect to the amount of bone glue in the mixture. The unusual behaviour
can indicate the measurement error due to the very small resulted values but more detailed knowledge of
the molecular structure of the material would be necessary to know to understand material behaviour at
low strains. On the other hand, it was observed that with increasing shear strain the complex modulus
decreases more rapidly for lower concentrations. The slope of the complex modulus, therefore, indicates
that BPC with a higher concentration shows a weaker shear-thinning behaviour than the one with a lower
bone glue concentration.

Conclusion and recommendations
5.1
Conclusion
The overall aim of this research was to design and evaluate 3D-printable biologically-based concrete
composite through an investigation of its rheological properties necessary to ensure extrudability and
buildability of the material. The material consists of biopolymer binding material (bone glue) dissolved
in water and mixed with ﬁne aggregates.
Firstly, the optimal mix design suited for extrusion-based printing had to be identiﬁed. This was
performed via quantitative investigation of key parameters such as polymer binder concentration, binder
to sand ratio and material temperature of a mix design. The testing was conducted by caulk gun extrusion-
based printing of 4 subsequently deposited layers. The printability assessment resulted from fulﬁllment
of extrudability and buildability requirements.
Furthermore, the rheological characteristics of suitable BPC paste were measured by an oscillatory
rheometer. Oscillatory rheology determined general rheological characterization of novel material and
provided insight on the response of the material to the temperature change. The results were evaluated by
complex shear modulus and loss factor and possible correlation with extrusion test results were assessed.
Additionally, the inﬂuence of binder concentration on the workability and stiffness of the material
was measured by the Vicat apparatus. However, due to the shape dependency of thermoplastic hardening,
the results were only comparatively investigated between different binder concentrations.
The main conclusions drawn from the performed study are elaborated in the following sections:
1. Based on experimental ﬁndings reported in Chapter 4, biologically-based concrete material can
be considered as a suitable material for extrusion-based 3D printing. Due to the easily control-
lable thermoplastic hardening of bio-polymer binder, BPC acquires sufﬁcient stiffness right after
extrusion.
2. The printing quality was mainly inﬂuenced by the b/s ratio and material temperature during the
printing. The b/s ratio between 0.4-0.35 provided the widest range of good quality printed spec-
imens. Whereas with higher or lower ratios the printability was limited to speciﬁc temperature
and binder concentration. The change in buildability for some of the samples was observed in
temperatures between 30-40 °C. The inﬂuence of higher temperatures on buildability was not that
signiﬁcant and contributed mostly to workability improvement of the material.
3. The rheometer test results conﬁrmed the predicted shear-thinning viscoelastic behaviour of the
composites and structural build-up by thixotropic and thermoplastic material characteristic.
4. Based on the temperature sweeps analysis of 40B50 composite, a signiﬁcant increase of complex
modulus was observed when temperature dropped below 30 °C for all concentrations. Accord-
ingly, the rheological characteristic that allowed for a BPC paste to be extruded was identiﬁed as
63
==PAGE:80==

shear strain between 60 - 200 kPa. It has to be noted that this evaluation is only valid for the 0.4
b/s ratios and any change in mix consistency may result in different conclusions.
5. The rheological measurements give some useful results for stiffness development with respect to
the temperature change but for stating the inﬂuence of bone glue concentration in an early stage of
hardening, the results are not that clear. The reason might be that the strain applied is simply too
small, and hence measurement resulted in an error. To conﬁrm or discard this hypothesis, further
testing should be done and a deeper study is needed to explore the exact mechanism of the BPC
composite.
6. At last, thermosetting and thermoplastic properties of BPC allows to build more geometrically
complex structures with overhangs without additional support as it was proved by 3D printed
object, using proposed suitable BPC mix.
5.2
Recommendations for future work
The BPC is a novel material and further investigation of its properties would be necessary to complement
its rheological evaluation from this study. Successful development of printable material requires inter-
disciplinary research of material fresh and hardened properties, suitable printing system and effective
printing parameters.
As the suitable range of printable concrete mixture has been proposed based on rheological character-
ization of material, the next step would be an evaluation of mechanical properties for those composites.
As previously investigated by J. Christ [24] the reference BPC composite 150BG33B showed compa-
rable strength magnitude to cementitious concrete. On the other hand, this composite did not pass the
extrudability test in this work. It is, therefore, out of interest to assess the mechanical properties of
identiﬁed printable composites to ﬁnd an optimum mix design suited for 3D printing.
Moreover, from the evaluation of stiffness dependency on the temperature, the printing setting could
be adjusted to further improve the printability quality of the material. In particular, it would be interesting
to test different time gaps between new layer printing to see to which extend it would improve the plastic
deformation of the layers.
The obtained results created a pathway towards the possibility of completely replacing cement mate-
rial with a more sustainable binder in 3D printing manufacturing. The proposed material cope effectively
with the demands of the printing process and continuation of this research in the directions listed above
will help to adopt this novel material in 3D printing resulting in lower environmental impact and higher
degrees of freedom in shape design.
