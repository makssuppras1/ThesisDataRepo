Abstract
In a world, that robots make an appearance into the everyday life and with an exponential incre-
ment in the industry, the safety of the people and the robots itself is becoming more crucial and
more demanding. The current thesis project focuses on the successful motion control of a three
wheeled differential-drive mobile robot and its ability to drive itself in an environment avoiding ob-
stacles. This project investigates different methods of fuzzy logic control in order to decide the
optimal one. Moreover the importance of a good simulation is highlighted, because it reduces
the waste of valuable resources (hardware, working hours), as it gives a good preview of the be-
haviour of a motion control algorithm. Feedback motion control with P controllers and line-drive
algorithm was used for motion control. Fuzzy logic control with a rule base of 63 rules was used for
the implementation of obstacle avoidance algorithm. Two types of membership functions were
used both for input fuzzification (measure distance sensors) and output defuzzification (velocities
of wheels), each one having three crisp values. First type uses linear membership functions and
the second uses smooth Gaussian ones. The two types were applied into the same fuzzy rule base
for the simulation and the results were compared concluding that smooth membership functions
present a slight faster performance. In addition more experiments with different scenarios of obsta-
cles were tested and succeeded except from the case of U-shaped obstacle, where modifications
of fuzzy rule base were required. For the initial experiments two simulators were implemented one
in Matlab, where it takes into account only kinematic constraints and the second was V-rep that
simulated both kinematic and dynamic constraints. After the validation that the results agreed
V-rep was used as the sole simulator for further experiments.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The application of mobile robotics in the industry is continuously becoming more important [Neh01].
Mobile robots are useful for conducting monotonous tasks, accessing environments that are dan-
gerous for humans (e.g. radiation, underwater, space) and reducing costs in industry. At the
moment mobile robots are widely used for surveillance, inspection and transportation tasks.
With this widespread use of mobile robots, it is to expect that the usage of these devices in the
daily life will be more usual in the future. This is the reason why some safety precautions have to be
implemented. Firstly it must be assured that no probable singularities occur during the function of
the robot (calculation of kinematics or dynamics), which are a direct threat to human lives (very
large velocities may deploy at the motors and this can lead to human injuries [Sic+10, p. 116]).
Secondly it should avoid the collisions with obstacles in its workspace. For a successful operation
of a mobile robot in an environment, proper navigation and planning are crucial. This is done
by making a path planning which guides the long-term operation of a mobile robot completing
a task [SNS11, p. 257]. Even if the robot possesses the perfect a priori mapping of its operating
environment, it is still impossible to predict every single change that could happen. One paper
investigated the case of mobile robots working in a warehouse for placing materials [Fai+13] and
examine the feasibility of this argument. For example maybe someone misplaced a box inside the
workspace of the robot or even someone is accidentally standing in its workspace risking his own
life. Obstacle avoidance algorithm is designed for preventing these situations, by reading real-time
sensor values, it can react in real-time and modify its trajectory in order to avoid collisions.
1.1
Overview
A lot of research has been conducted in this field and several approaches have been proposed.
One approach [ISA11] suggests the algorithm of ant colony implemented and calculated by Cel-
lular Automata theory. The application of a potential field is also very common, because it has
the advantage of driving the vehicle to the goal without using pure obstacle avoidance algo-
rithm [Zha+13]. Nevertheless, it presents a weakness when taking into account dynamic obstacles
because the expansion of the field may be smaller than the velocity of the moving obstacles.
Furthermore this technique presents a weakness when it anticipates U shaped obstacles, as the
robot will be trapped inside it (called local minimum in the literature) and solutions have been
suggested for this problem [Gue+16]. Another recent method uses Rapidly-exploring random tree
[LY15], which presented a problem when the robot crossed between two obstacles. A different
approach uses potential function (field) that drives the robot to target while at the same time an
artificial neural network collects data for locating obstacles [LCW12]. The last method is the ob-
stacle avoidance by fuzzy logic controllers which is used a lot because it is not solely dependent
on mathematical model of the controlled process and it is quite flexible [Fai+13]. Furthermore this

1.2
Aim of the project
The main aim of the project is:
• Creation of a model of differential-drive mobile robot
• Implementation of an algorithm for motion control which is able to drive the mobile robot in
an environment without obstacles into a target position with a precision of error distance less
than 0.01m and error orientation less than 0.02rad
• Simulation of the previous algorithm applied on the model first in Matlab considering kine-
matic constraints. Then the simulation of the same algorithm and model in simulator V-rep
according to dynamic constraints besides the kinematic ones. The two results are going to
be compared and evaluated for further validation.
• Implementation of an obstacle avoidance algorithm with fuzzy logic control.
• Simulation of obstacle avoidance algorithm along with the motion control, tested in an envi-
ronment with obstacle. The simulation will be conducted in Matlab according to kinematic
constraints only and then in V-rep with both kinematic and dynamic constraints. The results
will be compared and evaluated.

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical background of
mobile robotics
In the field of mobile robotics, one of the most crucial topics is the kinematics of the mobile robot.
Hence it is very important to describe the mechanical behaviour of the robot model and imple-
ment it in appropriate simulators. This stage can test the feasibility of the proposed concept, before
proceeding to the stage of hardware building, saving financial and labour resources. For example,
if if high acceleration is applied on the wheels of the robot, then the reaction will lead the robot
to perform wheelie.
A mobile robot moves with respect to surrounding environment, so to measure the position of the
robot, its motion should be integrated over time. Other challenges e.g. slippage of the wheels
can insert inaccuracy into the model of the mobile robot.
The first stage in acquiring the kinematics of a mobile robot is to examine each wheel of the robot
separately according to the total motion of the mobile robot. This includes the contribution but also
the constraints it inserts into the system. The motion will be examined in a global reference frame
and additionally in robot’s local reference frame. Combining kinematics and constraints, someone
can define the possible paths, trajectories and total mobile robot model’s maneuverability[SNS11,
p. 48].
The mobile robot is expressed as a rigid body with the wheels attached to it, which moves on a
horizontal plane. Three dimension are required to describe the position of the robot: two for 2-D
position in plane (x, y) (width and length) and one which refers to robot’s orientation (θ) along
the vertical axis, which is orthogonal to the horizontal plane. At this moment any internal wheel or
joint inside the robot is ignored.
As far as concerning to the reference frames, a relationship between the global reference frame
of the environment and the local reference frame of the robot needs to be established. Figure
2.1 illustrates this. The two axes Xl and Yl define the global reference frame on the current plane
from the origin point (0, 0, 0). Next a point P is chosen as the origin point of a reference frame,
which is going to be the local reference frame defined by the two axes XR and YR

2.2
Sensors of mobile robots
A mobile robot uses a variety of sensors which are separated into two big categories: propriocep-
tive or exteroceptive and passive or active [SNS11, p. 89]. The sensors which measure the internal
states of the robot are classified as proprioceptive e.g. motor speed. The sensors which measure
states that belong to the environment of the robot are classified as exteroceptive. Furthermore the
sensors are additionally classified as passive when they just receive environmental energy in order
to measure it e.g. CCD or CMOS cameras. On the contrary when they emit an amount of energy
into the environment and they measure the reflected signal coming back from it, they are called
active sensors [SNS11, p. 90] e.g. ultrasonic sensors, laser rangefinders.
For obstacle detection and avoidance, a mobile robot uses active ranging sensors. Two time-of-
flight active ranging sensors are: the ultrasonic sensor and the laser rangefinder[SNS11, p. 104].
These systems use the propagation speed of sound (ultrasonic sensors) or electromagnetic wave
(laser rangefinders). The equation that describes the travel distance of a sound or electromagnetic
wave is
d = c · t
(2.8)
where d is the traveled distance including round-trip, c the speed of the specific wave propagation
and t the time of flight. Concerning to ultrasonic sonar the propagation speed of sound is usually
considered to be c = 300 m/s and the propagation speed of electromagnetic wave referring
to the laser rangefinder is considered to be c = 3 × 108 m/s. Hence the time travel of electro-
magnetic wave is a million times faster than sound wave. It is obvious that laser rangefinder uses
equipment which is more complex and advanced, so its price is higher. There are some factors
that affect greatly the good operation of time-of-flight range sensors:
• the precise determination of the time when the reflected signal is received.
==PAGE:18==
8

• inaccurate measurement of the time-of-flight. This happens more often with the laser range
sensors.
• the dispersion of the transmitted beam, this problem happens mainly with the ultrasonic sen-
sors.
• possible surface absorption of the wave from the target object or specular reflections which
produce a weak reflected signal.
• the speed of the mobile robot as a transmitter and also the speed of the target object in
case it is a dynamic object.
2.2.1
The ultrasonic sensor
The ultrasonic sensor [SNS11, p. 105] transmits a packet of pressure waves and it measures the time
which is necessary for this wave packet to reflect on the target object and return to the receiver
of the sensor. The distance d is calculated according to
d = c · t
2
(2.9)
as obtains only the ”one way trip” of the transmitted wave packet. The speed of sound c in air, is
calculated according to
d =
√
γ · R · T
(2.10)
where γ is ratio of specific heats, R is gas constant, T is temperature of gas in which the wave
packet travels, measured in degrees Kelvin.
When air is at standard pressure (101 325Pa [DP08]) and its temperature is 20 ◦C, the speed of
sound is approximately c = 343 m/s. A series of sound pulses, which can be considered as the
wave packet are emitted. An integrator begins to count in value and measures the time interval
from the transmission of the packet until the detection of the reflected signal, this happens In order
to obtain a valid echo signal and there is a threshold value which determines if the received signal
is the expected echo. This threshold is decreasing in time, as the echo signal the longer it travels,
more its amplitude reduces because of dispersion. On the other hand when the waves packet is
being transmitted for a short amount of time the threshold is being set very high. This happens as
an insurance that the receiver will not misinterpret the just transmitted signal as echo. The previous
process about the input and output signals, is depicted in 2.4. A transducer will ring for up to
several milliseconds and it is used to drive the blanking time of the ultrasonic sensor. The blanking
time creates a problem in case that there is an object very close to sensor, because the transmitted
wave packet reflects back very quickly and it may be subject to the blanking time. Thus if the echo
signal arrives at time out of blanking time and the trigger of receiver is set correct, the sensor will
produce a digital signal and it will measure the distance according the integrator value.

The ultrasonic wave operates at a band of frequencies 40kHz-180kHz. A piezo or electrostatic
transducer generates this wave. For measuring the echo signal, the same transducer is used again.
Different input and output devices are used to reduce the blanking interval time according to the
need each time. The appropriate frequency is selected each time according to the requirements
of the robot application. For example if the application requires a longer range detection then
lower frequencies are preferred to operate, even though this fact bears the consequence of a
longer post-transmission ringing and as a result longer blanking intervals. The majority of ultrasonic
sensors used in mobile robot industry feature an operating range of 12cm to 5cm, with an accuracy
between 98% and 99.1% and some specific units achieve a resolution of approximately 2cm. One
more disadvantage of using ultrasonic sound is the fact that sound propagates in cone formation
and the opening angles of these ”rays” vary between 20 ◦and 40 ◦. As a result the sensor is not
able to acquire depth data points but just regions of a specific depth. This feature makes ultrasonic
sensor useful to detect an object and measure the distance until up to this but not to acquire
point measurements. Moreover, the ultrasonic sensor is based on perpendicular reflections of the
sound wave which reflect back from an sound reflective material. Therefore when the transducer’s
angle doesn’t send sound pack waves at perpendicular angles in relative with target position, the
sound pack waves may reflect coherently away from the sensor and this problem is of course
more intense when the robot is moving. It is understandable, that the performance of the sensor
varies according to an imminent coherent reflection of the sound wave. In addition, the acoustic
properties of the material of the target object, affect performance of the ultrasonic sensor a lot.
For example if the material is e.g. fur, foam, cotton, it absorbs the sound waves instead of reflecting
back to the receiver of the sensor. Lastly a major drawback of using ultrasonic sensor is its slow cycle
time. For example, if the target object is 3m away from the sensor, then 20ms are necessary for
the sensor to realize the existence of the object. This inserts the problem when someone uses an
application with 20 ultrasonic sensors and each one emits sound pack waves sequentially, in order
to minimize possible interference between the different sensors. Subsequently the duty cycle of
the whole system rises up to 0.4s adding an important performance factor in the system. Because
of the probable scenario that robot is moving at a high speed then the sensors may not have the
required time to detect the surrounding environment and lead to unwanted collisions.

2.2.2
Laser rangefinder (time-of-flight, electromagnetic)
Laser rangefinder [SNS11, p. 108] presents some advantages over ultrasonic sensors. This comes
from the fact that laser light is used instead of sound. A transmitter illuminates a target area with
a collimated beam e.g. laser and a receiver detects the part of light which is coaxial with the
transmitted beam. A mirror, which is operated by a mechanical mechanism, sweeps the covered
area (a plane or three dimensional) with the light beam.
As in ultrasonic sensors, the electromagnetic ones transmit a pulsed laser and then they measure
the travel time and acquire the distance until the target object according to formula 2.9. However
the disadvantage of this method is that the electronics which are capable to operate properly
within picoseconds are pretty expensive. Another method measures the difference of the beat
frequency of a frequency modulated continuous wave (FMCW) and its received echo. A third
method, called Phase-shift measurement, is based on measuring the phase shift of the echo signal.
In phase-shift measurement, the applications uses near-infrared LED or laser source. This transmit-
ted light ”ray” reaches a point P in the environmental area. When the surface presents a roughness
which is larger than the wavelength of the light, this will provoke diffuse reflection and the light re-
flects at an isotropic way. The wavelength of the used infrared light is 824 nm, so most of the
usual surfaces will work as diffuse reflectors, except from the case that the object is highly polished
one. The part of the transmitted light that reflects back as echo, will return almost parallel to the
track of the transmitted one for distant objects. Hence the 100% amplitude modulated light of a
known frequency is transmitted and then its echo is received. The light wavelength is calculated
according to the equation

2.3.1
Open loop control(trajectory-following)
In this method [SNS11, p. 81], the trajectory is divided in motion segments of defined shapes, e.g.
straight lines and curves. The problem then minimizes to precompute a smooth trajectory divided
in lines and circles, which will be able to drive the mobile robot into the desired destination with the
efficient success. Because of the lack of any feedback control in this strategy, it is called open-loop
motion control. There are some disadvantages in this method:
• Considering all the limitations and constraints of the velocity and acceleration of the robot,
it is a complex task to compute a smooth trajectory.
• The robot is not able to correct its trajectory, if there is a distortion in the system e.g. a structural
anomaly on the floor, which will lead it away from its target position
• As it was told the trajectory of the robot is not consistent but it is made of different segments
(lines, curves). This inserts the factor in the system of violent instant accelerations. Besides

2.4
Fuzzy logic controllers
A fuzzy controller is a self-regulating mechanism that controls states according to a desired be-
haviour. Digital computers can tell difference between true and false. On the contrary, fuzzy
logic allows a graduation between these two states. This concept has a lot of applications in real
life as it presents two main advantages.
• Fuzzy logic uses mainly if-then rules and the rules could contain easily comprehensible words
as ’very high’, ’high’, ’low’, ’very low’.
• A fuzzy controller can function with multiple inputs/outputs and they can be appropriately
combined by these if-then rules using simultaneously operators and, or.

3.2.2
Physics dynamics engine
Another important characteristic of the simulator is the choice of the right physics dynamics engine.
These physics dynamics engine are[cop17a]:
• Bullet physics library, which is an open-source physics engine that simulates 3-D collision de-
tection, rigid and soft body dynamics (soft body dynamics is not supported in V-rep). Its use
is more widespread in games and visual effects for movies.
• Open dynamics engine (ODE), which is an open-source physics engine that it includes two
main features, rigid body dynamics and collision detection. This engine is also more widespread
in games industry.
• Vortex dynamics, which is a closed-source, commercial physics engine. The big advantage
of this engine is that it produces high fidelity physics simulations: Vortex dynamics is suitable
for real-world parameters of physical parameters. It is very realistic and it is used for industrial
and research applications.
• Newton dynamics, which is a cross-platform life-like physics simulation library. Its solver doesn’t
use traditional numerical iterative methods or linear complementarity problems algorithms,
but it has the advantages of their stability and speed concerning to the implemented cal-
culations. Hence this engine is suitable for real-time physics simulation. At the moment V-rep
provides only a BETA version.
According to the previous points, the most reliable physics engine that will be used in the simulator
is Vortex dynamics and it is selected according to the documentation of V-rep. It produces high
fidelity physics simulations and it is suitable for real-world physical parameters. Secondly there has
been research in this field, that it proves its reliability for using it[Erl05]. The efficiency of physics
engine can be seen in [ETT15].

robot in an area that is 0.3 m away from the target and the angle difference will be 0.2 rad from
targeted orientation. To be more specific this point acts as a ’bait’. After another algorithm (line-
drive) is implemented that drives the robot with a steady linear velocity, but the angular velocity is

angle. As a result, the robot drives forward and turns into the line and stops as soon as the position
acquires the desired accuracy which in this case is 0.01 m and 0.02 rad. The control law then just
regulates the angular velocity.

3.4.4
Fuzzy rule base
The fuzzy rule base was mainly inspired by [Shi+18] for the case that all the sensors detect an
obstacle at some distance. It was concluded that if the algorithm would consider only this case,
it would create problems in the procedure of avoiding the obstacles, especially in the case that
the obstacle was a small pillar, so it would never cover the area of all three sensors leading to a
failure. Then it was realized that the fuzzy logic controller should activate when either one or two
random sensors or all of them would detect one or several different obstacles. Hence the 27 fuzzy
rules cases were enriched by 36 more empirical fuzzy rules, 63 in total covering all the probable
scenario-cases that the robot can face. The used type of inference rules for the experiment is
Mamdani [SHP95] and more information about this are referred in section 2.4.5.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
Motion control and obstacle avoidance have attracted a lot of attention of scientific community
the last years. The objective of this thesis project was to implement a model of a differential-drive
mobile robot, a feedback motion control and an obstacle avoidance algorithm based on fuzzy
logic control. The next step was to test them in simulations and investigate the behaviour. The key
points of this procedure are described as:
• Successful implementation of the differential drive mobile robot model.
• Choice of the suitable simulator according to literature review for simulating kinematic and
dynamic constraints of the experiment
• Implementation and test of the feedback motion control in Matlab according to kinematic
constraints, where it succeeded with the necessary precision. On the other hand V-rep pre-
sented the weakness of the algorithm as it simulates all the dynamic constraints. The algo-
rithm was then corrected with the insertion of line-drive algorithm. After that both simulators
succeeded in the completion of the experiment and the difference in the results was rational
because of V-rep simulating the dynamic constraints.
• Successful simulation of active ranging sensors (ultrasonic/laser rangefinder) both in Matlab
and V-rep with the necessary modifications for being inputs into the fuzzy logic control system.
• Successful connection of V-rep with Matlab in a synchronous mode protocol in order to de-
ploy the fuzzy logic control mechanism with appropriate Matlab toolbox.
• First implementation of the obstacle avoidance algorithm using linear membership functions
and testing it in both Matlab and V-rep. Comparison of the two results and validation that
they agree with slightly different results due to simulation of dynamic constraints in V-rep.
• Second implementation of the obstacle avoidance algorithm using Gaussian smooth mem-
bership functions and testing it in both Matlab and V-rep. Comparison of the two results
and validation that they agree with slightly different results due to simulation of dynamic
constraints in V-rep.
• Comparison of the two previous implementations of the algorithm (linear and smooth gaus-
sian membership functions). Choice of the implementation with smooth membership func-
tions as it is faster according to the results of both simulators.
• Implementation of an algorithm that successfully switches the control of the mobile robot
between the two control systems (fuzzy logic and feedback motion control), in order to pre-
vent the robot from inserting a state that continuously switches between the two systems and
leading the simulation in an endless loop.
==PAGE:78==
68
5 Conclusion
• After the validation from initial experiments that both simulators are in agreement accord-
ing to the results, V-rep is selected as the sole simulator for the implementation of further
experiments that cover different scenarios the mobile robot could anticipate (two obstacles
forming a tunnel, wall). Successful operations in most scenarios even with a wide U-shaped
obstacle. Collision with a narrower U-shaped obstacle as the available space for evading
maneuvers is limited.
• Modification of the fuzzy rule base in order to make more aggressive maneuvers and success
of the robot to avoid the collision with the narrower U-shaped obstacle.
The followed methodologies are in compliance with published literature. The personal motivated
modifications which were made for the fuzzy logic controller justify the claim that it is a quite flexible
system which gives the ability to overcome a lot of different challenges in the demanding fast-
paced world of industry.
5.1
Recommendations for future work
The establishment of the experiment gives opportunities to investigate further features and con-
duct some improvements on the algorithm in order to find more applications in the real industry
by being able to cope with more challenges.
A very interesting paper work [NC18] suggests the detection and avoidance of dynamic (moving)
obstacles. As it is described in the paper the sensors first observe and track for four time-steps the
position of the obstacle and stores it in the memory. Then it absolute subtracts the most recent data
position from the oldest one and it decides its probable direction. This result and the measurement
distances are fuzzified to a fuzzy control system in order to regulate properly the speed of the mobile
robot and temporarily change the target position of the robot. For this implementation 9 ultrasonic
sensors are used covering the front face of the robot in a wide range of 180◦. The information
about the fuzzy rule base is not clear and it is just mentioned that 103 fuzzy rules are used as an
optimal set from a total of 219 = 524288 probable fuzzy rules. Moreover the information about
membership functions is insufficient requiring. It is required more research on this subject, even
though the results of the paper seem quite promising.
Another aspect that needs consideration is the response of the angular velocity of the mobile
robot, being jerky. The deviation is rather small (0.1 rad/s) and it didn’t affect the experiment.
Nevertheless there are applications where high accuracy and smoothness are required for their
success. It is suggested the further investigation upon this by testing of some stochastic adaptive
systems on the system (e.g. Kalman filter as estimator of the disturbance with a linear quadratic
controller).
