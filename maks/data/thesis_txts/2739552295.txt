Abstract
This master’s thesis was prepared at the Department of Applied Mathematics and Computer Science
at the Technical University of Denmark to fulfill the requirements for acquiring a master’s degree in
Electrical Engineering.
The thesis provides a comprehensive pipeline for developing AI applications for the MAX78000FTHR
development board, a specialized board designed for Edge AI solutions. It utilizes the CNN accelerator
of the MAX78000 microcontroller featured on the board. The focus of the study is to assess the perfor-
mance and energy eﬀiciency of the edge device while running AI applications under various clock speeds,
operating modes, and input methods. Experiments are conducted to measure inference time, power,
and energy consumption under different configurations. Development environments are established for
training and synthesizing the models.
The results indicate that higher system clock frequencies increase power and energy consumption,
and vice versa. Additionally, utilizing the onboard camera to generate inputs for the CNN accelerator
substantially increases power and energy consumption compared to using pre-made samples. Various
operating modes are also tested, demonstrating the potential for energy savings in low-power configu-
rations.
While the MAX78000FTHR proves to be a suitable platform for developing AI applications, future
work should explore optimizations, including utilizing the MCU’s RISC-V core, and make comparisons
with other platforms.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In recent years, artificial intelligence (AI) has grown in popularity and has had a significant impact
in numerous areas such as healthcare, transportation, manufacturing, etc. While AI applications have
commonly been processed in the cloud, the demand for having these applications processed at the edge
is rapidly growing. Edge Computing is the practice of processing data closer to the data source, such
as IoT devices or local edge servers, eliminating the need for centralized cloud servers. The advantages
of local data processing are, for instance, improved speed, reduced bandwidth usage, lower latency,
increased reliability, and enhanced privacy. It also offers a way to become independent from costly
cloud services. Edge Computing has facilitated the move of AI processing to the data source, which has
helped a new concept to rise known as Edge AI, which leverages the benefits of Edge Computing. This
development has significantly influenced the progress in creating edge devices with specialized hardware
for eﬀiciently handling Neural Networks (NN) and Machine Learning (ML) tasks. The emergence of
AI accelerators has greatly improved the processing of data, and having them integrated into MCUs
like the MAX78000 and MAX78002 from Analog Devices or the STM32N6 from STMicroelectronics
unlocks new possibilities for deploying AI applications at the edge. While AI acceleration is not a new
idea and has been widely utilized in high-end GPUs in graphic cards, such as those made by NVIDIA,
introducing AI accelerators into smaller edge devices is a newer trend that has quickly picked up speed.
AI accelerators play an important role in meeting the growing demand of deploying AI applications at
the edge as they provide the necessary computational power while also maintaining energy eﬀiciency.
[1] [2] [3]
This study delves into a specific Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) accelerator integrated into
the MAX78000 MCU, which is featured on the MAX78000FTHR development board. CNN accelerators
are commonly used for image and video processing tasks such as image classification, object detection,
and facial recognition. These tasks involve complex operations like convolution, pooling, and activations.
However, the CNN accelerator maximizes performance by parallelizing the computations. Evaluating
the power and energy eﬀiciency of such systems as the MAX78000FTHR is vital to understanding
their potential in real-world applications.
This thesis explores and assesses the performance of the
MAX78000FTHR development board for embedded AI, focusing on inference time, power consumption,
and energy usage across various settings and configurations. Detailed objectives are further elaborated
in section 1.1.
1.1
Objectives
The primary goal of this thesis is to examine the capabilities of the MAX78000FTHR development board
from Analog Devices and assess its performance and energy eﬀiciency when running AI applications.
The focus is to understand how various factors such as system clock frequencies, operating modes, and
input methods influence the inference time, power consumption, and energy usage.
To achieve this, the following sub-objectives are outlined:
• Establish environments for developing and deploying AI application onto the MAX78000FTHR
development board. This involves:
– Creating a Windows Subsystem for Linux(WSL2) on the provided Lab PC to facilitate
compatibility with the necessary toolkits for training and synthesis.

– Configuring two virtual environments in the WSL2 distribution: One for the model training
environment and one for the model synthesis environment.
– Modifying the derived C-code from the synthesis to allow adjustments of system clock set-
tings, selection of various operating modes, and choosing between using inputs from pre-made
samples or the camera. Utilizing the onboard camera adds a practical dimension to the study.
• Record and assess the inference time, power consumption and energy usage. This involves:
– Using a power analyzer to measure the power and energy consumption of the AI applica-
tions across various system configurations, including multiple system clock frequencies and
operating modes.
– Configuring a timer in the MCU of the development board to measure inference times.
• Evaluate the impact of the system clock frequencies, operating modes, and input methods. This
involves:
– Investigating how the various system clock frequencies, operating modes, and the choice of
input method affect the inference time, power consumption, and energy usage of the system.
– Comparing, analyzing, and identifying which system configurations provide the best bal-
ance between energy eﬀiciency performance when running the chosen AI applications on the
device.
1.2
Limitations of the Study
This study solely evaluates the energy eﬀiciency and performance of the MAX78000FTHR development
board and the CNN accelerator within the MAX78000 MCU. It is important to note that the following
limitations apply:
• The scope was limited to working with basic CNN models suitable for the device, excluding more
complex models.
• Only the MAX78000FTHR was tested. Comparisons with other platforms were not made.
• Two input methods were used: pre-made samples and live camera capture inputs.
• A decision was made to use only the Arm processor and exclude the RISC-V core due to time
constraints and the need for additional hardware to properly program and evaluate the RISC-V.
As a result, the potential contributions of the RISC-V were not examined.
• Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) was utilized, but other optimization techniques, such as
pruning, were not explored.

CHAPTER 2
General Overview
This chapter provides the general concepts and technologies that support the deployment of AI appli-
cations at the edge.
First, a general overview of Edge AI is provided, emphasizing its advantages in enabling real-time
decision-making on resource-constrained edge devices. After that, the structure and functionality of
Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) are explained in more detail, offering a thorough understanding of
artificial neural networks (ANNs) and the transition to DNNs. The chapter then delves into the imple-
mentation of convolutional neural network (CNN) accelerators in microcontrollers, with a focus on the
MAX78000 MCU as is featured on the MAX78000FTHR development board, detailing how its CNN
accelerator functions and operates, and how it optimizes AI processing at the edge. This chapter also
examines the energy eﬀiciency of Edge AI deployments, which is a crucial consideration for sustainabil-
ity and scalability. Finally, related work on the MAX78000FTHR and similar devices is reviewed to
provide context for the current state of research and practices.
This chapter establishes the groundwork for the subsequent chapters, where these concepts will be
implemented and evaluated within the context of the MAX78000FTHR platform.
2.1
Edge AI
Edge AI is the practice of implementing artificial intelligence algorithms directly on edge devices, which
brings the system closer to the source data and makes it independent from cloud infrastructure. There
are many advantages to this, and in the last couple of years, this has resulted in a rapidly growing
tendency to transcend from cloud-based AI to edge-based AI. [2] [4]
Exploring the advantages of edge-based AI highlights the potential for real-time data processing, as it
enables immediate decision-making as data is processed locally at the source. This is desirable for time-
sensitive applications such as autonomous vehicles, healthcare devices, industrial automation, industrial
IoT, and real-time video analytics. With the rampant spread of edge devices, an incomprehensible
amount of data is being produced. As a result, there is a growing demand for localized processing to
reduce latency issues and minimize the need to transmit large amounts of data to the cloud. Reducing
the transmissions to the cloud also increases security and privacy, as the data is not exposed outside
the local network or even the edge device itself.
This also makes compliance with data protection
regulations more comprehensible. Another contemporary and popular advantage that emerges with
edge computing is power optimization, as processing data on edge devices is more energy-eﬀicient than
edge-to-cloud-and-back transmissions, which is especially beneficial for battery-powered devices. A key
advantage from an industry point of view is the potential for reduced costs by avoiding expensive cloud
services and resources. Other savings can be found where edge devices take on some of the previous
human responsibilities and tasks. [2] [5]
Edge AI architecture commonly includes edge devices, sensors, cameras, servers, and sometimes
cloud integration when unavoidable. In Figure 2.1, an example of a simplified Edge AI architecture is
presented, illustrating a typical data flow while also suggesting the reduced reliance on the cloud. [2]
The emergence of AI-capable edge devices, such as the Google Coral, NVIDIA Jetson Nano, Arduino
Nano 33 BLE Sense, and the MAX78000FTHR, has played a massive role in enabling this tendency
of moving data processing to the edge, particularly the ”device edge”. AI accelerators, like the inte-
grated CNN accelerator on the MAX78000 MCU, enable complex and eﬀicient inference tasks. However,

2.2.1
Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs)
ANNs, a subset of machine learning, form the core of deep learning. ANNs mimic human brains by
imitating how neurons collaborate to recognize phenomena, weigh options, and reach conclusions. NNs
consist of layers and nodes (neurons), one input layer, one or multiple hidden layers, and an output
layer. Each node connects to one or multiple other nodes and has an associated weight and a threshold.
If the output of a node exceeds its specified threshold, that node is activated to send data to the next
layer. If the output is below the threshold, the node sends no data to the following network layer. A
NN depends highly on good training data to learn and improve accuracy. One can train the models
in a supervised or unsupervised manner. In supervised learning, the models analyze labeled data sets
designed to train models to classify data or predict outcomes. Supervised learning encompasses two
primary types of problems: classification and regression.
In unsupervised learning, models analyze
unlabeled datasets to discover hidden patterns without human supervision. The three main tasks done
by unsupervised learning models are clustering, association, and dimensionality reduction. [6] [7] [8]
To provide an example, one of the most basic ANN structures in this section is considered, namely the
feedforward network. Despite its simple form, this structure is a robust network for both classification
and regression. Figure 2.2 below illustrates a feedforward network with two hidden layers.
Figure 2.2: Example of a fully connected ANN structure (feedforward network) with two hidden layers
The input layer passes raw data from the dataset into the network for feature processing. Each node
in this layer represents a specific feature present in the data. The purpose of the two hidden layers is
to systematically extract more intricate and abstract features from the given input. In the first hidden
layer, each node is connected to every node in the input layer, creating a fully connected layer, and
processing is done to the data received from the input layer. The second layer is also fully connected,
as each node in this layer is connected to every node in the first hidden layer. Further processing is
done to the data now received from the first hidden layer. The output layer produces the final output

of the network, which, e.g., could be a label in a classification model or a predicted value in a regression
model. This depends on the specific application of the network. [9]
Each connection in the network is assigned a weight, a real-valued number, that determines the
influence of one node on another. These weights are adjusted during training to minimize error and
improve performance.
Apart from the first layer, each node applies an activation function to the
weighted sum of its inputs to determine whether to pass on the information, acting as a switching
mechanism. If the input of the activation function is greater than a specific value, then the output of
the function switches state, e.g., from 0 to 1, −1 to 1, or 0 to greater than 0, and thereby mimicking
the activation of a biological neuron. Refer to Figure 2.3 for an illustrative view of a node with multiple
weighted inputs, showing that the sum of the inputs produces a result for the activation function. [9]
Figure 2.3: Illustration of a node with multiple inputs

An ANN can be in either a learning state or a normal operating state.
When the network is
being trained, it is in the learning state.
After the training, the network is put into the normal
operating state where the data flow propagates from the input to the output through the hidden layers,
indicated by the direction of the arrows in Figure 2.2. A network needs to be trained so the weights are
properly modified to enhance the performance of the network, enabling it to predict the output correctly.
The feedback process algorithm to determine the correct weights is called backpropagation, which, in
summary, compares the output of the network with the expected output, using the difference (e.g.,
the error) to update the weights in the network, propagating backward from the outputs to the inputs
through the hidden layers. This way, the network ”learns” over time, epochs, or iterations, consequently
reducing the error. Once the network has been trained, it has acquired the ability to process novel and
unseen inputs and subsequently predict correctly with a certain level of accuracy. [12] [13]
2.2.2
Convolutional Neural Networks
As described in the previous section, ANNs usually comprise an input layer, one or two hidden layers,
and an output layer. These networks are often suﬀicient for relatively simple tasks, such as binary
classification. However, for tasks such as image processing, CNNs are a good choice for utilization.
==PAGE:15==
2.2 Neural Networks
8
These types of networks often feature a more complex hidden layer structure with different types
of layers, such as a convolutional layer, max-pooling layer, dense layer, and other distinctive types. If
properly designed, these extra layers help the model understand the tasks better, ensuring high accuracy
and performance. Figure 2.7 illustrates a fully connected CNN structure. Refer to Figure 2.10 for more
detailed example. [14]
Figure 2.7: Example of a DNN structure with four hidden layers
CNNs are particularly well-suited for data tasks such as object detection, facial recognition, and key-
word detection. Keyword detection may sound odd, but utilizing an approach where the Mel Frequency
Cepstral Coeﬀicients (MFCCs) are generated for each audio sample makes this possible. The MFCC
captures the shape of the power spectrum, which is used to create a spectrogram of the audio sample.
CNNs are highly appreciated for producing very accurate recognition results. It is also advantageous
that they eliminate manual feature extraction, as the features are ”learned” directly. Similar to ANNs,
CNNs consist of an input layer, hidden layers, and an output layer. However, CNNs can have hundreds
of layers, with each layer having learned to detect different features of an image or spectrogram. The
nodes of CNNs have weights and biases assigned to them, just like ANNs. Training CNN models can be
computationally heavy, and the use of graphical processing units (GPUs) is often a necessity to handle
the workload. [15] [16] [17]
When image data is fed into a CNN, it is segmented into smaller parts using filters. These parts are
then processed and forwarded through the network rather than an entire image represented as one set
of numbers. Filters vary in size and complexity. Each output of a convolved image is passed forward
to the next layer for further processing. Initially, filters extract simple features like edges or brightness
in the image but then gradually advance to more complex features that identify unique characteristics
that ultimately define the object to be recognized. There are different types of filters with different
weight values for different purposes. They each highlight different features or patterns.
In a CNN, the following operations are commonly used: Convolution, Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU),
Pooling, and Classification. These operations are generally repeated across many layers, with each layer
identifying different features of the object. Further explanation of these operations is provided in the
following sections. [18] [19]
2.2.2.1
Convolution
Convolution is the process where a filter is applied to and across an input (e.g., an image) or feature
map, performing element-wise dot product operations to produce a new output. The filter sliding over
==PAGE:16==
2.2 Neural Networks
9
the input is a two-dimensional (2D) array of weights. The reason for doing convolution is to extract the
features from the input image or feature map. The different filters help the network identify features like
edges, lines, corners, curves, shapes, patterns, and other characteristics necessary for the recognition
task. Figure 2.8 shows a filter being applied to a window slice, computing their dot product as follows:
1·1+0·2+1·3+0·4+1·5+1·6+1·7+0·8+1·9 = 31 The result is then fed to an output matrix. The
filter will then move by a stride, repeatedly applying the process until it has moved across the entire
input and filled out the output matrix. The filters, which are also known as kernels or feature detectors,
can vary in size, though 3x3 and 5x5 are the most common, and the weights that characterize them are
fixed as they move across an input. However, the weight values are adjustable between layers. After
every convolution operation, a CNN applies a ReLU transformation to the input, which introduces
non-linearity to the model, to help detect complex patterns and relationships. [17] [20]
Figure 2.8: Example of a filter being applied to a window slice
Some parameters, specifically hyperparameters, must be set to fixed values before training. These
parameters have an impact on the volume size of the output, and they include the number of filters to
use, the stride, and whether zero-padding should be introduced or not. The number of filters directly
affects the depth of the output. For instance, when using three filters, three features are extracted,
resulting in a depth of three. The stride is the distance a filter moves per computation, e.g., one pixel
or more. The larger the stride, the smaller the output. Zero-padding is commonly introduced when a
filter does not fit the input image, setting the elements that fall outside the input matrix to zero. [17]
2.2.2.2
ReLU
ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit function) is a widely used activation function that is applied to the output
of each convolution operation. It replaces all negative values in the input with zero, introducing non-
linearity. This allows for the model to learn more complex patterns for complex tasks. Without an
activation function like the ReLU, the model would not be able to learn non-linear relationships. [17]
2.2.2.3
Pooling
Pooling layers perform dimensionality reduction of the input by using down-sampling methods like max-
pooling or average-pooling. The aim is to reduce the spatial size of the input, decreasing the number
of parameters and computational workload while retaining the most essential information. Figure 2.9
shows a max-pooling process with a 2x2 window where the input is divided into non-overlapping regions,
and the maximum of each region is fed forward to the pooled output. If average-pooling were utilized,
the average of the region would be fed to the pooled output instead. Similarities are drawn to the
==PAGE:17==

10
convolution layer process, where a filter travels across the entire input, but the pooling filter differs as
it does not contain any weights. [17]

2.2.2.4
CNN Example Walk-through
Figure 2.10 summarizes what has been explained in the previous CNN sections. It shows an image of
Tweety from Looney Toons being used as input to the CNN for feature extraction and classification. The
input image goes through two convolutional layers and two max-pooling layers. The first convolution
sweep of the input image results in a set of feature maps (the blue layers) representing the different
features detected. ReLU is then applied to the feature maps before being forwarded to the first pooling
layer, in which the feature maps undergo max-pooling, reducing their dimensionality. The output of
this is pooled feature maps (the green layers) that are reduced in size but preserve the essential features.
The pooled feature maps are forwarded to the second convolutional layer, where another convolution
sweep is performed, further extracting complex and abstract features from the image, resulting in a
new set of feature maps (the yellow layers). Again, ReLU is applied to the feature maps before being
passed on to the second pooling layer, in which they undergo max-pooling for dimensionality reduction,
producing pooled feature maps (the orange layers). The pooled feature maps are forwarded to a flatten
layer, which converts the 2D arrays into a 1D array by flattening them. The flattened 1D array is then
used as the input for the fully connected layer, which then performs the final computations to correctly
classify the input image based on the extracted features. The probabilities for each class are computed
in the fully connected layer using a softmax function, which simply put, converts input values to values
between 0 and 1. The sum of these values must equal 1, and the class with the highest probability score
is considered the identified class. In Figure 2.10, Tweety has the highest probability score and is thus
the identified cartoon character. [17] [18]

2.3
AI Accelerators in Microcontrollers
The rapid development of AI accelerators for embedded systems has brought forward new and exciting
possibilities for implementing complex AI applications onto edge devices such as the MAX78000FTHR.
AI accelerators are specialized hardware components explicitly designed to optimize and accelerate the
performance of machine learning tasks, particularly those involving complex deep learning algorithms,
which are often composed of many linear algebra computations (matrix and vector operations) and are
highly amenable to parallelization. These accelerators are tailored to eﬀiciently handle the intensive
computations needed to run DNN models. Some are designed for a specific purpose, while others have
general functionalities.
For example, the MAX78000 MCU features an integrated CNN accelerator
specifically designed to handle CNN models. [21] [22] [3]
This study primarily focuses on the MAX78000FTHR development board, which features the
MAX78000 MCU with the integrated CNN accelerator.
This CNN accelerator is elaborated on in
section 2.3.1 without delving into other types of AI accelerators.
2.3.1
The integrated CNN Accelerator in the MAX78000
The MAX78000 MCU comprises two main functional parts: the embedded part and the CNN accelerator.
The top-level architecture is illustrated in Figure 2.11. The CNN accelerator is designed to be energy
eﬀicient and can execute AI inferences while spending only microjoules of energy, making it ideal for
Edge AI applications such as keyword detection and face ID recognition.
It comprises 64 parallel
processors with 512 KB SRAM-based storage, enabling parallel and real-time updates to the NN. Add
to this 432 KB of memory for weights, 2 KB of memory for biases, and 384 KB of working storage, which
is also called Tornado Memory. Each processor is equipped with a pooling unit and a convolutional
engine with dedicated weight memory and can support 1-, 2-, 4-, and 8-bit weights. The 64 parallel
processors are organized into four processor quadrants, each with 16 processors that share common
controls. Every quadrant is divided into four processor groups, each with four processors that share
32 KB of data memory. Each quadrant can function as a slave to another group or independently. Refer
to Figure 2.13 for an illustration of a processor quadrant. Data is read from the SRAM linked with each
==PAGE:19==
2.3 AI Accelerators in Microcontrollers
12
processor and written to any data memory located within the CNN accelerator. The CNN accelerator
has been designed to handle up to 32 layers. [23] [24] [25]
Figure 2.11: The architecture of the MAX78000 microcontroller
Figure 2.12 shows how a processor element in the CNN accelerator works. When a processor reads
data from the associated data RAM it initially performs pooling if enabled. This process happens as
part of the data fetching and is not performed as a separate step. After the first stage, the pooled
data is stored in the compute cache, allowing the convolution engine to operate with multiple pieces
of data simultaneously. The compute cache is a buffer that facilitates seamless exchange between the
convolution engine and the memory interface, holding the elements in storage for as long as they are
required by the convolution engine. The convolution engine convolves the input with a filter stored
in the weight memory. The dimension structure of the dedicated weight memory of each processor
is 768x72. This word size of 72 bits is determined by the number of bits in a 3x3 filter. As each
weight in the filter is 8 bits, the total number of bits in a filter is 3 · 3 · 8 = 72, meaning that every
processor has the capability to access 768 3x3-filters. The output of the convolution engine is a sum of
products, and since the product of two 8-bit numbers gives a 16-bit result, the maximum value of each
product is 216 −1 = 65535. The maximum sum is then 9 · 65535 = 589815, which bitwise requires at
least log2(589815) ≈19.13 = 20 bits. But taking the risk of overflow into account, the output of the
convolution engine has been sized with 26 bits. Optionally, bias can be added to the result. The final
result of the process is sent to a bus where it can be summed with the result from other processors. [26]
Figure 2.12: Simplified processing workflow of the CNN accelerator
Figure 2.13 illustrates the architecture of a processor quadrant. Before delving into what happens
in the processor quadrant, it is helpful to recall that a node in a particular layer receives the activations
from the nodes in the preceding layer, which usually comprises multiple channels. The node then weights
these activations using the convolution filter, adds up the weighted activations, and then self-activates
the resulting sum. In the MAX78000 MCU, each processor manages one input channel. Therefore, if
the previous layer has four channels, the current layer will use all four processors in a processor group,
==PAGE:20==
2.4 Energy Eﬀiciency in Edge AI
13
where each of the processors performs a single convolution. The convolutions are then summed up
before the activation is performed, and the result hereof is stored in local memory. If the previous layer
comprises more than four channels, then the results from multiple processor groups in the processor
quadrant must be combined, and it may even be necessary to combine results across quadrants. Each
processor quadrant has a sum-of-products block that combines the results from all four processor groups
in its quadrant and shares the sum with the other quadrants in the CNN accelerator. This allows each
quadrant to receive a partial result from every other quadrant. In theory, the results from 64 processors
in the previous layer can be combined. However, the CNN accelerator of the MAX78000 supports a
multipass mode, allowing the utilization of a multipass accumulator to hold an intermediate result while
more channels are convolved. Therefore, the system is not limited to only 64 channels but can instead
process up to 1024 virtual channels, making the CNN accelerator a very flexible piece of hardware that
is capable of a variety of AI applications. [26]

The MAX78000FTHR provides a platform for developers to leverage the capabilities of the MAX78000

to be trained and synthesized with dedicated toolkits like the ai8x-training and ai8x-synthesis toolkits

Energy Eﬀiciency in Edge AI
Edge AI brings processing closer to the data sources. Therefore, the data does not have to be sent to a
data center for processing, which reduces latency and optimizes energy eﬀiciency. Power consumption
is a crucial factor in Edge Computing and Edge AI, as it can allow systems to be powered by small
batteries and operate for extended periods without having to replace or recharge their batteries. The
MAX78000 ultra-low power microcontroller and its integrated CNN accelerator have been designed
to support such power-optimized applications at the edge. It offers various configuration options to
help facilitate the development of low-power applications by adjusting oscillators, clock sources, and
operation modes, which are configured based on the requirements of the target application. [3] [27]

4.1
Model Selection & Training
Given the limitations of edge devices, in terms of restricted computational power and memory capacity,

4.1.1
CNN Model Selection
The constraints of the MAX78000FTHR influenced the selection of CNN models, as careful consideration

4.1.1.1
catsdogs
The catsdogs model is a specialized binary classification model designed to classify images into two

sification network for AI85 hardware like the MAX78000 MCU. The network architecture comprises
six convolutional layers, followed by a fully connected layer, and leverages ReLU activation and max-
pooling. It takes images of size 128x128 pixels with three color channels (RGB). It gradually reduces
the spatial dimensions while increasing the number of channels. In the end, the network uses a fully

ilarities with conventional CNNs like LeNet, AlexNet, and VGG, but is adapted for deployment onto

The cifar-10 model is a specialized multi-classification model designed to classify images into 10 cat-

labeled dataset containing images of the animals mentioned, like e.g., the cifar-10 dataset provided
by Swaroop Kumar on Kaggle (https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/?search=cifar-10). Note that

utilized to generate the model, as it provides the Python code for creating the AI85NASCifarNET
classification network tailored for AI85 hardware. This network architecture consists of several convo-
lutional layers, each followed by batch normalization, ReLU activation, and max-pooling, and it ends
with a fully connected layer to classify the input into 10 categories. The network takes images of size
32x32 pixels with three color channels (RGB). The network structure is based on conventional CNN
architectures, but unlike the catsdogs model in this study, it was optimized with the help of Neural
Architecture Search (NAS) for eﬀicient inference on the MAX78000 MCU. A NAS script is provided
by the ai8x-training toolkit, and to briefly elaborate, NAS is utilized to automatically find the most
suitable neural architecture for a given set of requirements.

4.1.2
Training & Quantization
The models chosen models were re-trained using the ai8x-training toolkit, which is built explicitly
around conventional machine learning frameworks like PyTorch and TensorFlow and is intended for
developing deployable models for development boards powered by the MAX7000 or MAX78002 MCUs.
During the training process, the goal is to optimize the weights to minimize the output error. The time
required to train a model depends on the complexity of the network, the type of processor, and the
graphics card available. [36]
When training a network with the ai8x-training toolkit, the initial step is to activate the virtual
training environment. Then, the cuda-check.py script should be executed with the following command,
(ai8x-training) $ python check_cuda.py, to make sure that hardware acceleration is available and
recognized by PyTorch. Additionally, CUDA availability should also be verified by executing the follow-
ing command in the terminal: nvidia-smi -q. The main training software in the ai8x-training toolkit

is the train.py script. This script drives training aspects such as model creation, checkpoint file creation,
model save, and status display. Models that fit into the weight memory of the specified MCUs are kept
in a folder in the ai8x-training directory called models, and they rely on the hardware operators of the
MCUs, which are defined in the ai8x.py script. The scripts folder, which is also in the ai8x-training
directory, contains bash files that configure various aspects of the training process, including specifying
which model should be trained, how many epochs should be used, and which MCU is the target device,
among other parameters. These scripts also handle the placement of the checkpoint files into the log
directory. The training script, train.py, utilizes a distiller framework, which has been modified to have
it specifically tailored for the designated MAX78000 and MAX78002 MCUs. Knowledge distillation
is a method that involves transferring knowledge from computationally heavy models to simpler ones
without sacrificing accuracy and reliability. The ai8x-training toolkit supports utilizing multiple GPUs
for distributed training. However, since there is only one GPU at disposal for this study, it was not
possible to take advantage of this feature. By default, many systems impose limits on the number of
open file descriptors, so it is important to check the ulimits and adjust their values properly. [37]
In this study, Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) is utilized, as recommended by the ai8x devel-
opers, to achieve better quantization performance. QAT is controlled by a policy file placed inside the
policy folder in the ai8x-training directory. QAT is a technique that aims to enhance the performance
of DNNs when they are later quantized and deployed onto constrained hardware, like the MAX78000
MCU. During QAT, the model’s weights and activations are reduced in precision, transitioning from
32-bit floating point values to 8-bit values. This reduction helps minimize the model size, memory
usage, and inference time. However, this can often lead to a notable drop in accuracy, especially when
working with complex models, but when utilizing QAT, the model is trained with quantization in mind,
making it better equipped to maintain its accuracy. [37]

hardware.

results. The cnn.c file contains low-level control code to operate the CNN accelerator hardware on the

configurations (if chosen), among other things. For this study, the generated C-code from the synthesis

4.3
C-code Modifications & Application Deployment
The ai8x-synthesis toolkit provided a good foundation for deployment. However, as the study aimed
to explore some of the more advanced capabilities of the MAX78000FTHR development board and the
CNN accelerator, additional modifications to the C-code were necessary to align with the thesis objec-
tives. The catsdogs and the cifar-10 applications both needed testing with the synthesis-derived input
samples as well as image captures from the onboard camera. Integrating the camera required setting up
the camera driver, and aligning the image processing steps with the model’s input requirements. Besides
being configured to run with and without the camera integration, the applications were also configured
to run with three different system clock frequencies: 100 MHz, 60 MHz, and 7.3728 MHz.
Manual
switching between the system clock frequencies was necessary between deployments. Furthermore, the
application code was modified to change operation mode every 4 seconds.
The following bulleted list outlines some of the code modifications done to the applications:
• The onboard camera was configured for use, which involved setting the camera frequency, creating
an input buffer for the camera image, writing an additional load_input() function for the camera
image captures, and creating a capture_process_camera() function for capturing and storing
images in a buffer, and process them appropriately.
• An alarm handler function was added to manage the switching between operation modes. The
alarm was set to be triggered every 4 seconds by the RTC (real-time clock).
• A couple of macros were added and configured to toggle and switch between different features.
The macros are the following:
– #DISABLE_GPIO:
This macro is activated when the camera is not in use, which is when the synthesis-derived
input sample is used as input to the CNN. When the camera is used, the GPIOs are not
disabled, because the camera utilizes some of them. It is common practice to disable the
GPIOs to save power.
– #USE_ALARM:
This macro is used to toggle between using the alarm handler or not, which enables the
switching between different operation modes.
– #DELAY_IN_SEC:
The value of this macro determines the duration between each operation mode.
– #USE_CAMERA:
This macro controls whether the camera or the sample input is being used to feed the CNN
input.
• Several modifications were made inside the main(), including 1) initialization of the camera if the
specified macro has been set, 2) a couple of if-statements that determine the operation modes of
the board based on the value of a dedicated variable, 3) clock-setting configuration, and 4) turning
the camera on and off between each inference to conserve power.

The inference time was recorded using a dedicated timer in the MCU, with results printed to the
terminal after each inference. The rest of the measurements were obtained using the power analyzer
detailed in section 3.2.4, and the following metrics were recorded:
• Average power consumption during idle, processing (including inference), inference only, and the
entire iteration.
• Energy power consumption during idle, processing (including inference), inference only, and the
entire iteration.

6.2
Interpretation of Measurement Results
The measurements of power and energy consumption, as well as the recorded inference times, for the
catsdogs and cifar-10 models running on the MAX78000FTHR, provide valuable insights into the impact
of the various board configurations such as: varying system clock frequencies, operation modes, and
input methods (sample vs. camera). By comparing the results, it is possible to highlight the influence
of these factors on power consumption and energy usage.
6.2.1
Impact of System Clock Frequency
The obtained results indicate that higher clock speeds result in increased power and energy consumption.
For example, when looking at table 5.1, with a system clock frequency of 100 MHz in ACTIVE mode for
the catsdogs example using the pre-made sample as input, the average power and energy consumption
for the entire ACTIVE mode iteration is 0.050 W and 85 µWh. In comparison, with a system clock
frequency of 60 MHz, the average power and energy consumption for the entire ACTIVE mode iteration
==PAGE:69==
6.2 Interpretation of Measurement Results
62
is 0.046 W 77 µWh. Finally, with a system clock frequency of 7.3728 MHz, the average power and energy
consumption for the entire ACTIVE mode iteration is 0.040 W and 71 µWh.
The results also suggest that lower clock speeds result in increased inference time. However, the im-
pact is not significant enough to counteract the reduction in power and energy consumption. Therefore,
based on these results, lower clock speeds generally result in decreased power and energy consumption.
The same trends are observed for the other models as well. One noticeable deviation to this, though,
is that with the lowest system clock frequency while using the camera, the energy consumption is high.
This is likely due to increased inference time, resulting in the camera being on for a more extended
period.

Utilizing the camera to generate inputs to the CNN accelerator naturally leads to increased power and
energy consumption compared to using the pre-made samples. When the camera is turned on, the
system has to power and operate the camera module, which consumes additional power. Furthermore,
capturing images in real-time requires the system to handle a significant amount of raw data and convert
it to the appropriate format, leading to increased power consumption. It is possible to turn the camera
on and off between inferences, so the differences in power and energy consumption between using the

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The main focus of this thesis was to examine the capabilities of the MAX78000FTHR development board
and evaluate its performance and energy eﬀiciency while running AI applications. The study examined
the impact of deploying two distinct AI models, catsdogs and cifar-10, onto the board, utilizing different
clock speeds, operating modes, and input methods (pre-made samples vs. camera-generated inputs).
The research provided valuable insights that enhanced the understanding of how AI application perform
on the MAX78000FTHR
Different environments were configured for training and synthesizing the models, including config-
uring a Linux subsystem for using the ai8x toolkits. This comprehensive setup ensured the smooth
development, testing, and deployment of the AI applications used in this study, and allowed for a
complete development pipeline.
The experimental results indicated that higher clock speeds led to faster inference times, but also
increased power and energy consumption. Lower clock speeds resulted in longer inference times but were
more energy eﬀicient. However, longer inference times are not necessarily a bad thing. Furthermore,
utilizing the camera to generate inputs, instead of using the pre-made samples, noticeably increased the
power and energy consumption.
Additionally, the different operating modes (ACTIVE, SLEEP, LOW-POWER, MICRO-POWER,
AND STANDBY) impacted the power and energy consumption as well. However, they did not have
much effect on the inference time. The results showed that low-power modes reduced power and energy
consumption, making them attractive for low-power applications such as in IoT or battery-powered
devices.
The choice to utilize the onboard camera of the MAX78000FTHR added a practical, use-case di-
mension to the experiments. This offered a more comprehensive evaluation of the development board.
While the thesis did not include the RISC-V core on the MAX78000FTHR, future work can further
explore its potential. Moreover, comparisons with other AI-specialized microcontrollers would provide
deeper insights into deploying energy-eﬀicient AI at the edge.
In conclusion, this study demonstrated that the MAX78000FTHR is a feasible platform for deploy-
ing AI at the edge. The thesis results offer valuable guidance for future developments in AI application
development, particularly for the MAX78000FTHR, while also highlighting the need for ongoing re-
search. The findings are relevant for developers working with or interested in creating AI applications
for edge devices.
