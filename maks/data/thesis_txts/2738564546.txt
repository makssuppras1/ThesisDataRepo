Abstract
PtX can serve as an energy carrier between the electricity and hard-to-abate sectors and
can de-fossilize future energy systems. This study investigates the role of CO2 infras-
tructure when optimizing renewable fuel production using the Balmorel-Optiflow energy
system model. The Balmorel model, containing the electricity and district heating net-
work, is co-optimized with the OptiFlow model, including fuel production pathways. This
study investigates Danish fuel production in 2050, with Norway, Sweden, and Germany
included in the electricity system to enable transmission. A high spatial and temporal res-
olution is applied to study possible locations of renewable fuel production facilities and
routes of CO2. The results showed a 50-50 split in produced fuels between e-biofuels
and e-fuels. The origin of the CO2 is based on the point source availability, as Direct
Air Capture (DAC) is utilized only when point source CO2 cannot fulfill the demand. The
required CO2 infrastructure and location of fuel production facilities is influenced by the
CO2 point source availability, demand for district heating (DH), location of CO2 storage,
and cost of CO2 transport.
1
Introduction
If the global temperature rises above 1.5◦C,
climate hazards will present risks to ecosys-
tems and humans [1]. To mitigate climate
change, the European Union recommends
CO2 reductions of 55% in 2030 [2] and
90% in 2040 compared to 1990 levels [3]
to reach climate neutrality in 2050. The re-
duction levels are established in the Fit-for-
55 package and the 2040 Climate Target
Plan (CTP), both conducted by the Euro-
pean Union [[2], [3]].
It is efficient and feasible to mitigate emis-
sions by electrifying sectors and convert-
ing the power sector to produce electricity
from renewable energy sources [4]. Elec-
trification has been greatly implemented in
the residential and service sectors and is
being implemented in the light vehicle sec-
tor. This conversion has been more chal-
lenging for the heavy transport sector and
the industry [5].
However, to reach cli-
mate neutrality in 2050, achieving zero or
negative emissions for all sectors is neces-
sary. This establishes a need for e-fuels,
e-biofuels, and biofuels, as these can link
the power and hard-to-abate sectors. Sev-
eral studies have been conducted on how
to implement carbon-neutral fuels in future
energy systems to reach carbon neutrality
[[6], [7]]. The net-zero energy outlook for
2050 carried out by the International Energy
Agency (IEA) establishes that consumption
from low-emission fuels such as hydrogen-
based fuels, hydrogen, bio-methane, syn-
thetic methane, and bio-liquids must in-
crease from a current level of 1% to 20%
in 2050 [6].
Another way to mitigate greenhouse gases
(GHG) for the hard-to-abate sectors is to
store captured CO2 underground. The CO2
can be captured directly from fossil fuel
emitters, biogenic CO2 emitters, or by DAC.
The biogenic and DAC capture will lead to
negative CO2 emissions, whereas the fos-
sil CO2 capture will lead to zero emissions
[8].
A report conducted by the European
Commission establishes that 245.3 Million
Tonnes per Annum (MTPA) CO2 emitted in
Europa must be captured and stored under-
ground in 2050 under the Fit-for-55 scenario
[9].
Other studies concerning CO2 infrastruc-
ture investigate the feasibility of Carbon
Capture, Utilization, and Storage (CCUS)
for specific sites and technologies [[10],
[11], [12]]. Feasibility studies do not model
the infrastructure and technologies in an
energy model context, as they only con-
sider the technology in question. The bene-
fit of analyzing the technologies in an en-
1
==PAGE:8==
ergy system model is that it deems alter-
native investments, which is why this study
chooses energy system modeling over fea-
sibility studies.
A study conducted by Bramstoft et al. [13]
examined the future role of renewable fu-
els in Denmark using the energy system
model Balmorel with the OptiFlow add-on.
The model had a high geographical reso-
lution and investigated the advantages and
drawbacks of producing gas/fuels at a small
scale locally or at central large-scale facil-
ities. In a model without Carbon Capture
(CC) as an investment option, they found
that it was economically optimal to have
local biomethane production if there is a
high gas demand and central production of
biomethanol if there is a high demand for liq-
uid fuels. Lester et al. [14] investigate differ-
ent fuel pathways for Denmark in 2050 us-
ing Balmorel with the OptiFlow add-on in a
Danish case study. In this study, no CO2 in-
frastructure was available, and it was found
infeasible to capture and utilize the CO2 for
fuel production. Instead, they found that the
carbon source of the future renewable fuels
originates from biomass.
This article examines the research gap of
CO2 infrastructure in renewable fuel pro-
duction pathways for future energy sys-
tems.
It is desired to model which role
CO2 infrastructure can play using an en-
ergy model with a high spatial and tempo-
ral scope.
The reason for implementing
high spatial and temporal scope is to study
the connections of CO2 transport routes
and interactions with the DH network. The
factors influencing the routes and trans-
port amounts will also be examined in re-
lation to the locations. The modeling frame-
work utilized in this study is the implementa-
tion of Balmorel to cover the electricity and
DH generation and the OptiFlow add-on to
cover renewable fuel production.
2
Technology Descriptions
Carbon is the building block of most renew-
able fuels suitable for heavy transport, ex-
cept nitrogen-based fuels.
If the carbon
originates from biomass, it is called biofu-
els. If hydrogen is added to the biomass, it
is called e-biofuels. Otherwise, if the carbon
originates from captured CO2 and is com-
bined with hydrogen, it is called electrofuels
(e-fuels). Biofuels, e-biofuels, and e-fuels
can be produced in various ways depend-
ing on the technology. A description of the
fuel production pathway for the different fu-
els is described in appendix A.1, A.2, and
A.3.
2.1
Carbon Capture
There are five prevalent ways to capture
CO2: pre-combustion, oxyfuel combustion,
chemical looping combustion, DAC, and
post-combustion. The first three technolo-
gies require a modification of the emitting
production facility and are not considered
in this study.
DAC can be located any-
where, as it extracts CO2 from the air.
The DAC technology is cost, energy, and
resource-intensive because it has to extract
0.04%−vol CO2 of the air. The applied DAC
technology in this study is solid absorption
with low-temperature regeneration type (S-
DAC), as it has a simpler integration with the
DH network.
The other investigated way to capture CO2
is post-combustion at point sources. The
CO2 can be extracted from the flue gas
in numerous ways. The market-dominant
technology used in this study is the amine
CC technology. The amine CC technology
can be integrated into existing CO2 emit-
ting facilities. The point sources included
in this study are combined heat and power
plants (CHP), industrial factories, municipal
waste incineration plants (MWI), and bio-
gas upgrading plants. The amine CC tech-
nology will need to be configured differently
for CHP plants and cement factories, as
the flue gas from the CHP plant contains
10 −15% −vol CO2, and flue gas from ce-
ment factories contains 20−30%−vol CO2.
If the CO2 is not emitted when burning fos-
sil fuels in an industrial facility but rather
captured, the CO2 accounting can be re-

duced or zero if stored long term.
If the
CO2 is not emitted when burning sustain-
able biomass in a CHP but rather captured,
the CO2 accounting can be negative when
stored long term. Therefore, the CO2 uti-
lized to produce electrofuels has to be bio-
genic to be net zero when combusted in the
heavy transport sector.
2.2
Infrastructure
To obey transportation standards, the CO2
must be compressed or cooled after cap-
ture, depending on the transportation type.
The CO2 can be transported by ship, truck,
onshore and offshore piping. Intermediate
storage is necessary if the CO2 is trans-
ported by ships or trucks. Transporting CO2
by truck makes sense if the amount is small
or the distance between two locations is on-
shore and limited. Transport by ship is cho-
sen if the amount is medium to large and
the locations make sense in a routing net-
work. Onshore piping is used for large on-
shore amounts, and offshore piping is used
for large offshore amounts.
3
Methodology
The energy system models chosen for this
study are Balmorel and Optiflow. Balmorel
and Optiflow can be run separately or in
co-simulation.
This report utilizes the co-
simulation to benefit from the synergies of
electricity, DH, and hydrogen modeling in
Balmorel and the renewable fuel modeling
in Optiflow. Two different add-ons are used
in Balmorel: hydrogen and industry.
The
hydrogen add-on carries out the hydrogen
modeling in Balmorel.
The industry add-
on facilitates the electrification of the indus-
try sector with increased electric demand to
produce heat.
3.1
Balmorel
Balmorel is an international, partial equilib-
rium, open source, bottom-up model [15].
Equilibrium models explain the behavior of
demand, supply, and market prices of the
whole or partial economy.
The markets
considered in this study are electricity, dis-
trict heating, and hydrogen.
Bottom-up
models analyze specific investment options
depending on the included energy technolo-
gies, which means that alternative options
are considered.
Balmorel can be used as a scenario tool
[15].
Scenario tools often use time-steps
within a year and combine the results of that
year into a scenario of 20-50 years. Bal-
morel is an operation and investment op-
timization tool that optimizes the operation
of technologies in a given system and de-
termines the investments in each of them
[15]. The combination of the three function-
alities enables Balmorel to make scenarios
of new production and consumption path-
ways of technologies that are not yet fully
developed and broaden the perspective of
new possible pathways.
Balmorel is chosen as the model, allowing
for high spatial and temporal resolution [15].
It is crucial to have high spatial resolution
when a part of the objective is to deter-
mine the location of CO2 infrastructure, in-
fluenced by the demand for district heating
and the CO2 availability from point sources.
When analyzing PtX from renewable en-
ergy production, it is essential to have a high
temporal resolution to represent the daily
and seasonal variations. Balmorel is cho-
sen rather than, e.g., the TIMES or Energy-
PLAN model that also optimizes the trans-
port sector because these models can only
optimize on a national level [16]. It is cru-
cial to capture the transmission possibility,
as Denmark has a high degree of electricity
trade with the surrounding countries [17].
3.2
Optiflow
Optiflow is also a spatiotemporal,
par-
tial equilibrium, open-source optimization
model. OptiFlow is better at waste/energy
recycling integration and fuel production,
as it allows for multiple in- and outputs for
a given technology [18].
In the OptiFlow
network model sources, technologies, con-

Assumptions and data
4.1
Spatial and temporal resolution
In this study, it has been chosen to include
four weeks: S08, S22, S36, and S49, with
every third hour. This configuration is cho-
sen because it is a balance of high tem-
poral resolution and reasonable processing
time. The implications and justifications for
this choice are illustrated in appendix C.1.
Denmark is chosen as the case study coun-
try, as it has high GHG targets [19], re-
stricted biomass availability [20], high share
of renewable energy [21], and extensive en-
ergy trade with the surrounding countries
[17].
Denmark is of particular interest for
CO2 infrastructure, as it, with its many pos-
sible CO2 storage facilities, is designated as
a CO2 transit country under all scenarios
in ”Shaping the future CO2 transport net-
work for Europe” conducted by the Euro-
pean Union [9].
The renewable fuel pro-

Scenarios
To investigate under which circumstances
CO2 from point source capture is deemed
a viable option, five scenarios are created,
with increasing availability.
Scenario 1,
named NoFossil, represents a future where
fossil CO2 emission is prohibited in Den-
mark, and therefore, no sources containing
a fossil CO2-part is included.
Scenario 1
assumes that heat pumps and excess heat
from renewable fuel production will fulfill the
heat demand, which means that biomass-
fired CHP plants are not included. Scenario
2, named NoFossil-CHP, contains the same
elements as scenario 1, but with the belief
that biomass-fired CHP plants will still op-
erate in 2050. Scenario 3 is called Fossil-
CHP because emissions containing fossil
CO2 are allowed, but the fossil part must
be stored underground in geological stor-
age facilities.
Scenario 4, named Fossil-
Offshore, is similar to scenario 3, but it is as-
sumed that the public opinion is against on-
shore CO2-storage, and the CO2 will need
to be stored offshore. Scenario 5, named
Import, is built upon the fit-for-55 D1 sce-
nario conducted in the EU CO2 transport
report, where 245.3 MTPA of CO2 needs
to be stored in 2050 across EU [9].
The
numbers are based on already proposed
projects until 2035, and after 2035, it follows
the need for CO2 storage established in the
fit-for-55 package. In the Import scenario,
119.2 MTPA is transported through Jutland
to storage facilities in the North Sea. Ad-
ditional 2 MTPA is transported to Lolland-
Falster and 10.5 MTPA of CO2 to Copen-
hagen. The scenarios are described in ta-
ble 6, where BIO stands for biogas up-
grading and MWI for municipal waste in-
cineration plant. The Fossil-CHP scenario
is used as the reference scenario, as this
has medium availability of CO2 from point
sources.

Fuel production
The exogenously defined fuel demands are
consistent across the scenarios. This op-
timization model configuration implies that
the same share of e-biofuels and e-fuels is
valid for all scenarios.
The e-biofuel pro-
duction pathway opted for in all scenarios is
the upgrading of biomethanol to jet fuel, and
the e-fuel production pathway is the combi-
nation of hydrogen and CO2 in the Fisher-
Tropsh process.
In the biomethanol up-
grading process, 36.79 PJ of jet fuel is pro-
duced. The e-fuels produced are 9.89 PJ jet
fuel, 3.40 PJ diesel used for road transport,
and 20.16 PJ diesel used for sea transport.
The fuel shares result in a total production
of 36.79 PJ e-biofuel and 33.45 PJ e-fuels.
Since the same amount of e-fuels is pro-
duced by the Fisher-Tropsh process in all
scenarios, the required amount of CO2 for
the process is the same. Even though the
amount has to be the same, the origin of the
CO2 can be differentiated. The results show
that the maximum available CO2 from point
sources is utilized to produce e-fuels across
scenarios.

6.3
CO2 infrastructure
The significant deviation between scenarios
is the need for CO2 infrastructure. Since the
entire amount of CO2 from point sources is
utilized, the required CO2 infrastructure will
increase with increasing availability.
The
results show that it is not economically vi-
able to capture CO2 from biogas upgrading
plants, as it is not opting for this solution in
any scenario. Due to CO2 capture from bio-
gas plants and DAC being the only possible
CO2 sources in the NoFossil scenario, there
is no need for CO2 infrastructure in this sce-
nario, as DAC can be located anywhere.
In the NoFossil-CHP scenario, CO2 is cap-
tured from biomass-fired CHP plants. How-
ever, as many CHP plants are located in the
five largest district heating areas, there is
no need to transport the captured CO2. Of
the total available CO2 from CHP plants of
1.27 MTPA, close to 0.3 MTPA of those are
transported. Of the around 0.3 MTPA CO2
transported, 0.1 MTPA is transported from
Kalundborg to Copenhagen, 0.1 MTPA from
Herning to Aarhus through Silkeborg, and
0.1 MTPA from Randers to Aarhus.
In the Fossil-CHP, Fossil-Offshore, and Im-
port scenarios, a total point source amount
of 3.66 MTPA CO2 is available for storage
and utilization. Of the 3.66 MTPA CO2 avail-
able, 1.13 MTPA is of fossil origin and is
exogenously set to be stored, leaving 2.53
MTPA of biogenic CO2 to be used for re-
newable fuel production.
2.82 MTPA of
CO2 was needed for fuel production, which
means that DAC must produce 0.29 MTPA
of CO2 in the three fossil scenarios.
As

investigate the required CO2 infrastructure,

6.4
Sensitivity analysis
The sensitivity analysis investigates the im-
pacts of the assumptions made to construct
the five scenarios. The sensitivity analysis
is based on the Fossil-CHP scenario, which
is the medium CO2 availability scenario. A
sensitivity analysis called ”sun” is created to
investigate the implications of having a high
share of PV and a low share of wind in the
Danish electricity system. A large capacity
of PV for Denmark was opted for in the opti-
mization, as a maximum capacity of 55 GW
was allowed in the five scenarios.
In the
”sun” sensitivity analysis, only a maximum
capacity of 10 GW is allowed. The 10 GW is
chosen as it is estimated in IDA’s energy vi-
sion 2050 that only 6.4 TWh PV will be pro-
duced in 2050 [40], which corresponds to
5.6 GW assuming 1140 FLH. To give some
margin, it is chosen to restrict the capacity
to 10 GW rather than 5.6 GW. The applied
electricity demand of Denmark is lower than
the demand listed in IDA’s energy vision
[40]. Therefore, the INDIVUSERS add-on
in Balmorel is activated in the ”sun” sensitiv-
ity analysis to simulate a higher electric de-
mand. The add-on appends more heating
demand for individual heating users by the
power-to-heat conversion.
The estimated
electricity distribution for the ”sun” sensi-
tivity analysis is compared with the Fossil-
CHP scenario in figure 12.
Figure 12: Production share of electricity in
”sun” sensitivity [-]
Figure 12 shows that the PV share is de-
creased from 85% to 31%, and the onshore
wind is increased from 8% to 62% of the to-
tal electricity production. The onshore wind
is increased over the offshore wind, as it has
a high maximum capacity and a lower Lev-
elized Cost of Energy (LCOE) [41].
Another parameter that could influence the
results is the availability of biomass. The
biomass input competes with the CO2 in-
put as the carbon source for fuel production,
meaning that the amount of produced e-
biofuels influences the amount of produced
e-fuels. A total availability of 54 PJ straw is
assumed for Denmark in the scenarios. In a
study conducted by Bentsen et al., they es-
timate that the straw potential of Denmark
is between 2.9-3.5 Tg/y [42]. The 2.9-3.5
Tg/y corresponds to 42.05-50.75 PJ, using
a calorific value of 14.5 GJ/ton [43]. The
lower bound estimated by Bentsen et al. is
around 22% lower than the potential used
in this study. Therefore, a sensitivity analy-
sis called ”straw” is constructed where the
straw potential in each municipality is de-
creased by 25%.
Cost estimates of the e-fuel and e-biofuel
production pathway can influence the CO2
infrastructure and the location and share of
e-fuels. A study conducted by the European
Technology Platform for Zero Emission Fos-
sil Fuel Power Plants estimates that the

7
Discussion
7.1
Model validation
The results are validated by comparing
them with historical data and other liter-
ature.
It was described in the introduc-
tion how little research has been conducted
on the CO2 infrastructure in energy system
modeling. Therefore, it will be impossible to
validate if the results of this study are similar
to others regarding CO2 infrastructure path-
ways. However, comparing it with other re-
newable fuel and energy production path-
way studies will be possible. The electricity
production composition of the investigated

scenarios has a share of around 85% PV
power of the total electricity production in
the Danish Energy system, In contrast, the
total wind production only constitutes 7.8%.
In 2022, the PV share of the total Danish
electricity production constituted 6.3%, and
wind 54.2% [45].
Since much technological development can
happen from 2022 until 2050, it is relevant
to compare the electricity composition with
other projections for 2050.
A case study
of the renewable fuel production pathways
conducted by Lester et al. has estimations
for the Danish energy system with 2050 as
the base year [14]. Lester et al. find that PV
constitutes around 20%, whereas wind con-
stitutes 77% of the total production. When
comparing the production shares, it is es-
sential to compare demand, as this can shift
the shares. In the study by Lester et al.,
they used fuel demands of 10 PJ for mar-
itime, 58 PJ for aviation, and 80 PJ for road
fuels. As a result of this, the fuel demand
was significantly higher in the 2050 case
study compared to the demands used in this
report.
Lester et al.
investigate the fuel
production pathways, where CO2 can be
captured and used for fuel production, but
no storage or infrastructure is considered.
With this model, they find that three out of
four scenarios produce no e-fuels with CO2.
Only the no biomass import scenario, where
biomass importation to Denmark is forbid-
den, results in e-fuel production. Further-
more, there was no requirement for storage
of fossil CO2, which means that the fossil
CO2 from Aalborg Portland was used to pro-
duce e-fuels. With the deviating assump-
tions between this study and the study con-
ducted by Lester et al., it is challenging to
validate nor reject the findings of high CO2
utilization in this report.
7.2
Discussion of results
The ”sun” sensitivity was constructed to in-
vestigate the PV vs wind electricity pro-
duction share implications.
The sensitiv-
ity analysis showed that the electricity pro-
duction composition was not sensitive to
the amount of transported CO2 from point
sources to utilization facilities. It is there-
fore assumed that the CO2 infrastructure
and location of fuel production facilities will
not change significantly if different electric-
ity production technologies are applied than
the ones opted for in the five scenarios.
In 2050 energy system studies of Denmark
conducted by Lester et al.[14], the DEA [46],
and IDA [40] they all foresee that biofuels
or e-biofuels will supply the future renew-
able fuel demand.
They estimate that e-
fuels will only be produced in scenarios with
low biomass availability. The restricted e-
fuel production is inconsistent with the re-
sults found in this report since e-biofuels
composed 52% of the total fuel production
and e-fuels composed 48% in all scenarios.
However, none of the energy system anal-
yses considered CO2 infrastructure; they
only assumed local PtX production based
on CO2 availability in the concerned area.
In the world energy outlook, the IEA has cre-
ated a scenario called Net Zero Emission
(NZE), where they project that 5.7 million
barrels per day (mb/d) of biofuels and 5.6
mb/d of low-emissions hydrogen-based fu-
els will be produced in 2050 [47]. The 50-
50 split in fuel production between biofuels
and e-fuels estimated in the world energy
outlook is more consistent with the results
found in this report.
The scenario and sensitivity analysis found
that the CO2 availability and the transport
cost were crucial parameters in determining
the location of fuel production facilities and
the required CO2 infrastructure. With this,
it becomes essential to critically assess the
availability of biogenic CO2 from biomass-
fired CHP plants. The cost estimation for
biomass-fired CHP plants assumed a price
doubling due to the reduced FLH. In the op-
timization of the district heating production,
it was found that the only fuel input for CHP
plants was municipal solid waste, yet little
heat was produced from wood-pellet-fired
boilers. It is, therefore, unclear to which ex-
tent heat-producing units will be biomass-
fired in future energy systems.

==PAGE:21==
Since the CO2 transport cost is a sensi-
tive parameter, special attention needs to
be drawn to this.
The analysis assumed
that the costs increase linearly with the
amount and distance.
However, a study
by the Joint Research Center of the Euro-
pean Commission showed that this relation
is non-linear [48]. The study illustrated that
the transport costs decreased exponentially
with increasing mass flow rates, implying
that the transport amounts would benefit
from the economics of scale. The study also
showed that transport costs decreased with
the transport distance, but not as rapidly as
for the mass flow rate. A yearly amount of
2.5 MTPA of CO2 and a distance of 50 km
was used to estimate the onshore transport
cost in the study. The results showed that a
transport amount of 2.5 MTPA or higher was
only achieved for the routes between import
locations and storage facilities in the Import
scenario. By assuming a yearly transport
amount of 2.5 MTPA, it is therefore likely
that the CO2 transport cost was set too low.
Contrary to this, many routes were longer
than 50 km, indicating that the cost estimate
could have been too high.
7.3
Future work
The yearly amounts of transported CO2
could be divided into ranges for the CO2
transport costs to account for the eco-
nomics of scale.
Concerning this, mak-
ing a lower threshold for the investment in
pipelines could be relevant, as it is eco-
nomically viable to transport CO2 by truck
for small yearly amounts and flow rates.
It was not chosen to implement the eco-
nomics of scale in the Balmorel-Optiflow
modeling framework as it would make the
problem a mixed-integer problem, signifi-
cantly increasing the complexity and pro-
cessing time. However, if implemented, it
would reduce the uncertainty of the CO2
transportation cost.
The error made by exogenously defining
the CO2 potential of an uncertain production
unit, such as biomass-fired CHP plants, can
be avoided by coupling the CO2 emitted to
the endogenously optimized production of
the plant. The coupling of the operation of
the CHP plants will be a relevant and es-
sential addition to the current work of this
report, and it will give additional insights into
the CO2 availability in the future energy sys-
tem of Denmark.
As the CO2 transport amount was given per
km, future studies should estimate the dis-
tances between point sources, production
facilities, and storage locations using a GIS-
based geological tool instead of Google
Maps.
With this implementation, estimat-
ing the storage space needed for the dif-
ferent fuel production facilities and identi-
fying whether the DH areas have the re-
quired storage space would clarify the pos-
sible transport routes even further.
This report exogenously defines that the
system must be net zero in 2050 and that
fossil CO2 emitted from point sources must
be stored.
Currently, the model is forced
to store the fossil part of the emitted CO2,
as it has no economic incentive to store it.
Instead of this setup, it would be interest-
ing to couple the CO2 in OptiFlow with the
CO2 budget and taxes in Balmorel and esti-
mate the level of the CO2 tax, which guaran-
tees that the fossil CO2 will be stored. Fur-
thermore, including an economic incentive
to store a part of the biogenic CO2 would be
interesting. The economic incentive could
represent a carbon credit that fossil CO2
emitters can purchase to offset their emis-
sions. By this inclusion, the carbon credit
market and renewable fuel production will
compete for the biogenic CO2 source and
potentially prolong the lifetime of biomass-
fired CHP plants.
8
Conclusion
The required CO2 infrastructure for creat-
ing future renewable energy systems was
investigated using the Balmorel-OptiFlow
model with Denmark in 2050 as the under-
lying basis of the study. In the analysis, it
was found that 52% of the fuel production
originated from biomass, and 48% of the
15
==PAGE:22==
fuel produced used CO2 as the carbon feed-
stock. When CO2 from point sources was
available, it was preferred over DAC, even
though it came with an additional transport
cost. DAC only fell in the missing amount to
reach the production level. It was found that
there was a strong relationship between the
DH sector and the transport sector, as the
excess heat from the fuel production could
be used in the DH network. Therefore, large
amounts of fuel were produced in areas with
high demand for DH. However, the entire
demand was not determined solely by the
DH demand, as the distances to straw po-
tentials, point sources of CO2, and storage
locations also influenced the location of fuel
production facilities.
With an increasing amount of available
CO2, the requirement for CO2 infrastruc-
ture increased too, meaning that the CO2
point source availability is a crucial param-
eter of this study.
The storage locations
and the amount needed to be stored also
influenced the amount of CO2 transported.
More CO2 was transported when offshore
storage was the only possibility. The sensi-
tivity analysis found that the total amount of
transported CO2 was sensitive to the CO2
transportation cost. When decreasing the
transport cost by 30%, the amount of CO2
transported from point source to utilization
facility increased by 60%, as more CO2 was
transported to the large DH areas for heat
utilization. On the contrary, the transported
amount of CO2 decreased by 11% when the
CO2 transport cost increased by 30%, as
more CO2 was used locally to produce e-
fuels.
Compared with other literature and histor-
ical data, the optimized electricity produc-
tion share of PV and wind deviated from
the work of others.
A sensitivity analysis
investigated the implications of the skewed
electricity production shares. When chang-
ing the production share of wind and PV, it
was found that it increased the transported
amount of CO2 between point source and
utilization facility with only 9%. Another un-
certain parameter of this study is the avail-
ability of straw in Denmark, which was in-
vestigated in a sensitivity study.
It was
found that a 25% decrease in the straw po-
tential only led to a 0.04% decrease in CO2
transported from a point source to utiliza-
tion.
It can, therefore, be concluded that
neither the electricity production composi-
tion nor the straw potential changed the re-
sults of required CO2 infrastructure signif-
icantly.
On the other hand, it was found
that the CO2 availability, location of stor-
age facilities, demand for DH, and the CO2
transport cost influenced the location of re-
newable fuel production facilities and the re-
quired CO2 infrastructure greatly.

Biogas Plants
Biogas is produced in biogas plants by anaerobic digestion, which means that organic
matter is decomposed in an oxygen-free environment. The output of a biogas plant is a
gas consisting of 50 −75% methane (CH4) and 25 −50% carbon-dioxide (CO2) together
with small fractions of hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, hydrogen sulfide, and ammonia and
digestate. A biogas plant can either be fuelled with biomass from agriculture or wastew-
ater from wastewater facilities. In this study, only biomass-fueled biogas plants are con-
sidered. In principle, all agricultural products can be used in agricultural biogas plants.
However, for the biomass to be sustainable and non-competing with feedstock, only ma-
nure, deep litter, organic waste, and grass are considered in this study. The biogas output
is directly proportional to the input feedstock’s Dry Matter (DM) content. See table 10 for
the correlation between DM content and methane output.

The biomass input often consists of multiple feedstocks, which means that a mass balance
of the different input substances is necessary to calculate the biogas output. The biogas
output also depends on the reaction tank’s processing time and the biomass loading rate.
[34]
The biomass feedstock for the biogas plant is often transported by trucks but can be
transported by pipes, which is seen for substances with a low DM content. In this study,
only transport by truck is assumed. The biomass is unloaded into pre-storage tanks.
Depending on the initial composition of the feedstock mixture in the digestion reaction
tank, the new feedstock can be stored in the pre-storage tank for hours or days. The
feedstock is added to the digestion reaction tank in small amounts several times during the
day because the Danish digestors are continuous stirred-tank reactors (CSTR). A small
amount of the digested biomass is removed in connection with the inflow of new biomass.
The processing time of biomass in Danish CSTR is between 60 and 100 days. After the
digestion reaction tank, the digestate gets pumped into the post-digestion tank, where
additional biogas is produced. The digestate is then treated to reach the desired water and
sulfur content and can be used as fertilizer. The gas collected from the digestion reaction
tank and the post-digestion tank can either be burned directly at the facility to produce heat
and electricity or be upgraded to methane that can be injected into the Danish natural gas
grid. The digestion reaction tank needs to be heated to 35−40◦C in a mesophilic digestion
process and 50 −55◦C in a thermophilic digestion process. The digestion reaction tank
is heated with excess heat from the upgrading process for new combined biogas and
upgrading plants, but an extra boiler is needed for standalone biogas plants. [34]

Biogas Upgrading Plants
Biogas can be upgraded by removing the CO2 gas content in upgrading facilities. It is
expected that all new biogas plants in Denmark will have upgrading facilities, which means
that 80% of the produced biogas in 2030 will be upgraded to biomethane. When the CO2
is removed from the upgrading facility’s biogas mixture, it can be emitted or captured.
Until recently, CO2 has been emitted from all upgrading plants in Denmark. Due to its
lack of propane, biomethane has up to 10% lower heating value than natural gas. To
properly bill the customers, Danish legislation decides that the quality of the biomethane
shall be measured before entering the natural gas grid. Therefore, additional measuring
equipment must be installed at upgrading facilities. [34]
The three common biomethane upgrading units are amine scrubbers, water scrubbers,
and membrane separation units. The amine scrubbing technology is dominating the Dan-
ish market. In amine scrubbing, the amines absorb the CO2 and H2S from the biogas, sep-
arating the methane from the substance. The CO2 and H2S are released when the amines
are regenerated in the stripper. The amines regenerate by adding heat of 120 −150◦C.
The heat is added either by the burning of natural gas or biomass. Due to the consumption
of fuels, it is crucial for the economy of the upgrading plant that a large part of the heat is
reused. The amine upgrading process happens under a pressure of 1-3 bar, establishing
the need for an electric compressor. There is a need for 0.6 kWh of heat per Nm3 biogas
and 0.12 kWh of electricity. [34]
In water scrubbing, the water is sprayed or bubbled through the biogas, as the water ab-
sorbs CO2 and H2S in this way. This process increases the pressure to around 6 bar,
making it unnecessary for the methane to be compressed further to comply with the natu-
ral gas grid standards. The methane slip equipment must be added to the facility to keep
the methane slip below 1% for water scrubbers. The waste gases’ methane can be oxi-
dized through thermal regeneration or catalytic oxidation. There is a need for 0.2 kWh of
electricity per Nm3 biogas upgraded for the water scrubbing upgrading facility. [34]
In membrane separation, the CO2 and methane are separated by the membrane. There
are three stages in the membrane separation. The first step is letting the substance
through a filter to remove water and oil. The next step is removing H2S using activated
carbon. The last step removes the CO2 through the membrane. There is a need for
around 0.3 kWh of electricity per Nm3 biogas upgraded for the membrane separation.
The membrane separation technology is less utilized in Denmark due to its higher OPEX
costs than the two scrubbing upgrading technologies. [34]
The CO2 stream from the upgrading facilities can be utilized if the CO2 is further purified
for the water scrubbing and membrane separation. After the purification (not needed
for amine scrubbers), the CO2 must be dried, compressed, and possibly cooled to be
transported to a storage or utilization facility. [34]
Thermal gasification
Thermal gasification happens by adding heat to solid biomass with restricted access to
oxygen. The types of biomass applicable for thermal gasification are wood, wood pellets,
straw, wood chips, and, in general, solid biogenic residues from agriculture and industry.
The products of the thermal gasification are CO, H2, CO2, CH4, H2O, and a small amount
of longer hydrocarbon chains. Byproducts of the reaction are S8, N2, Cl2, tar, and dust.
The gas is cleaned from dust through washing and cleaned from tar through separation or
conversion. It can also be cleaned from S8 and Cl2 if necessary for further gas processing.
21
==PAGE:28==
The gas from thermal gasification can be used in various ways, such as directly producing
heat and electricity in a boiler or being upgraded to Synthetic Natural Gas (SNG) or liquid
biofuels. [34]
The thermal gasification process can happen through direct or indirect gasification. Direct
gasification is commonly used by small facilities to produce low-quality gas. In this pro-
cess, the gas is created through partial combustion, where heat comes from combustion
and is mixed with air. Due to the high nitrogen content in the air, the gas will contain a
large amount of N2, making it unsuitable for further upgrading to SNG or biofuels. In the
indirect gasification process, heat is added to the process either by a gas or a movable
bed concept. Therefore, the heat is formed outside the gasification process, which allows
for the products and byproducts to be separated. CO2 or steam can be added to the
indirect gasification process, fully converting the entire biomass to the desired products.
[34]
Thermal gasification, BioSNG
The most common type of gasification for BioSNG production is the Circulating Fluidised
Bed (CFB) reactor. In the CFB, solid particles, such as sand, circulate with the gas created
by heating the biomass. The sand becomes liquid when blown through the gas with
ambient air. In this fluidization, the pyrolysis, drying, and oxidation-reduction processes
are mixed, which gives control over the output products. The CFB process occurs at
around 800◦C, which outputs a gas with a high methane content. [34]
After the gasification, all molecules except the syngas (CO, and H2) need to be cleaned
from the gas. Depending on the method applied the undesired gases can be removed by
physical or chemical absorption. The catalytic methanation of the syngas can then take
place. The methanation process produces heat, so the CFB plant is often located next
to the methanation plant, as the heat can then be reused. The methanation of syngas
is an exothermic reversible reaction, which happens through the help of a catalyst. The
maximum conversion of syngas into methane and water happens at around 350◦C for
most catalysts. The most common catalysts are Ni-based, but other noble metals such
as Ru, Rh, and Pt can also be applied. The main reaction for the conversion of syngas
into methane is illustrated below. [49]
CO + 3 H2
CH4 + H2O
Thermal gasification, Fisher Tropsh
Like the thermal gasification used to produce BioSNG, the syngas can be used for further
upgrading. In the Fisher Tropsh process, the syngas is used to produce Naptha, jet fuel,
and diesel biofuels. The temperature and pressure determine the share of produced fuel
types, which can be varied to match the desired outcome. Entrained flow reactors are
expected to be used from biomasses for the Fisher Tropsh syngas process. However, it
is currently only employed on large for coal. The advantage of producing biofuels through
the entrained flow reactor is that it produces much cleaner syngas with reduced output of
methane and tar. It is problematic if the CFB process produces a high share of methane
if the goal is to produce Fisher Tropsh fuels because only the syngas can be converted
to longer carbon chains. The entrained flow reaction is partly a combustion and partly
a gasification process. The process happens in a pressurized environment at high tem-
peratures above 1000◦C or even at 2000 −3000◦C if the solid biomass is torrid, which
increases the conversion efficiency. [34]

E-Biofuels
Methanation with hydrogen
There are two possible methanation ways to upgrade biogas into methane; catalytic
methanation and biological methanation. If the biogas is upgraded to SNG through cat-
alytic methanation, the CO2 captured from the upgrading plant produces more methane.
In biological methanation, hydrogen reacts with CO2 during biogas production. As the
hydrogen is added to the biomass conversion process, microorganisms in the substance
act like a biological catalyst and enable the following reaction. [34]
CO2 + 4 H2
CH4 + H2O
The hydrogen can be added to the conversion in the biogas digester, which is called
23
==PAGE:30==
an in-situ process. Otherwise, the hydrogen can be added to the raw biogas in another
tank with separate microorganisms. Another possibility is that the CO2 from the upgrading
facility can be added together with hydrogen and microorganisms in a separate tank. The
addition of hydrogen or CO2 in an external tank is called ex-situ biological methanation.
Both in-situ and ex-situ, the gas requires post-treatment to reach methane levels that fulfill
requirements for the Danish natural gas grid. Furthermore, the in-situ process requires
the hydrogen to be treated separately post-processing, and then the hydrogen is recycled
to be reused. The in-situ process has lower capital investment costs. However, currently,
it is only tested on a pilot scale, and it is difficult to control, as the process is highly depen-
dent on the biomass input. The ex-situ process has higher capital investment costs, but
there is full control of the methanation process. The methane content of ex-situ processes
is around 95%-97%, and they only need limited CO2 removal. The methane content in
the in-situ process is currently 80%, which means that it needs substantial CO2 removal
to comply with requirements for the Danish natural gas grid. [34]
In catalytic methanation, the CO2 captured from the upgrading facility of biogas plants or
other CO2 emitting units is mixed with hydrogen to produce SNG. The catalytic methana-
tion process is highly exothermic and releases heat. The process has the highest con-
version rate at temperatures around 200◦C if the pressure is around 1 bar and 300◦C if
the pressure is around 30 bar. Ni-based or Ru-based are the most promising catalysts for
the methanation process. The oxides used to support the Ni-based catalysts are Al2O3,
TiO2, ZrO2, and CeO2. [50]
Catalytic hydropyrolysis
In catalytic hydropyrolysis, e-biogasoline and e-biojet can be produced by the conversion
of biomass with hydrogen. The hydrogen is added to a catalyst with solid biomasses,
such as wood, wood pellets, wood chips, and straw. The process happens at around
375−475◦C and at 10 to 30 bar. Different renewable fuels can be produced depending on
the feedstock and plant configuration. The technology is still in the experimental stage and
needs to be fully optimized, but it is expected to mature in the coming years. The output
of the catalytic hydropyrolisis is e-biofuel, which in most instances is e-biojet, which can
be further distilled to produce e-biogasoline. [34]
The biomass and hydrogen are added to a CFB reactor containing a catalyst. After this,
the char and catalyst are removed by a cyclonic separator. The gas produced in the
reactor is transferred to a hydroconversion reactor, where more hydrogen is added to
convert the gas into liquid oil and an aqueous substance. The aqueous and oil phases are
then separated using a condensing technology called a separator. After the separator, the
e-biofuel is in liquid form. When the e-biofuel is in liquid form, it can be further hydrotreated
and distilled to produce e-biogasoline. The uncondensed gas and the aqueous phase can
be steam reformed to produce hydrogen and energy required for the process in a feedback
loop. As hydrogen is added to the process two times, a significant amount of hydrogen
is needed for the process, and it is vital that hydrogen can be fed back into the process.
[34]

E-methanol synthesis
E-methanol can be produced in various ways, but all pathways have carbon and hydro-
gen as feedstock. E-methanol can be produced directly from captured CO2 and hydrogen.

The overall reaction scheme is seen below.
CO2 + 3 H2
CH3OH + H2O
Even though this is the overall reaction scheme, the reverse water-gas-shift reaction also
happens in the reactor. The reaction of CO2 and hydrogen into methanol happens at
around 200 −300◦C and 50-100 bar. There are two types of methanol reactors: boiling
water and tube-cooled reactors. Heat is released as steam in the boiling water reactor,
whereas in the tube-cooled reactor, heat is released as heated water. The investment
costs of tube-cooled reactors are less expensive than those of boiling water reactors, but
the heat released as steam in the boiling water reactor is more valuable than that of heated

A third way of producing e-methanol is to co-electrolyze CO2 in a solid oxide electrolysis
cell (SOEC). In the SOEC, CO and H2 can be co-produced with steam and CO2. The

CO2 + H2O
CO + H2 + O2

The last pathway is the conversion of bio-methane into syngases through a steam methane

CH4 + H2O
CO + 3 H2
After the methane is converted into syngas, the gases can be upgraded to methanol
through methanol synthesis, as described for the other pathways. [34]
E-methanol upgrade
The methanol produced from the methanol synthesis can be further upgraded to e-gasoline
and e-jet fuel through conversion to olefins and then further to e-jet fuel and e-gasoline
fractions. This conversion is, however, almost solely seen for fossil fuels currently. Methanol
synthesis is an exothermic reaction that produces heat in a 1.74 MJ/kg methanol conver-
sion. The process is split into two stages. In the first stage, methanol is converted to
dimethyl ether and water over a non-zeolitic catalyst. In the second stage, the mixture is
converted using a zeolitic catalyst, like H-ZSM, to produce e-gasoline and e-diesel with
water as a byproduct. [51]

Fisher Tropsh
The Fisher Tropsh process to produce e-fuels uses the same Fisher Tropsh process as
the thermal gasification Fisher Tropsh process. The only difference is that the syngas for
the reaction originates from the reverse water-gas-shift reaction of captured CO2 and H2.
In the Fisher Tropsh process, the syngas produces syncrude or Fisher Tropsh wax, which
can be hydrocracked and upgraded to e-diesel, e-jet fuel, and other hydrocarbons. Recent
experiments show direct utilization of the CO2 in Fisher Tropsh processes instead of first
upgrading it to CO. However, this is in the very early development stage and, therefore,
not discussed further in this section.
As mentioned, the CO2 and H2 are utilized to produce syngas through the reverse water-
gas-shift reaction. The water from the reaction can be fed back to the hydrogen produc-
tion. The reverse water-gas-shift reaction requires heat and happens under temperatures
between 350 −600◦C and low pressures, but it depends on the catalyst used. As shown
by the arrow in the reverse water-gas-shift reaction, the reaction is reversible, so the max-
imum CO obtained from the reaction is around 80%. Therefore, there will always be some
CO2 left in the gas, which would need to be cleaned. There are also experiments on the
electrochemical conversion of the reverse water-gas-shift reaction, which is only proven
on a small scale. Since hydrogen is needed in the reverse water-gas-shift reaction and
in the Fisher Tropsh process, the ratio of H2 to CO2 needs to be around 3:1 for the entire
process. [34]
The Fisher Tropsh process is not completely selective in its output fuels and, therefore,
produces a variety of paraffin, olefins, and alcohols. However, the choice of catalyst and
operating conditions can somewhat influence the share. Iron and cobalt are the most
promising catalysts for the production of e-fuels.
The reaction scheme of the production of paraffin, olefin, and alcohol from syngas by the
FT process is shown below. [34]
(2n + 1)H2 + nCO −→CnH2n+2 + nH2O
(2n)H2 + nCO −→CnH2n + nH2O
(2n)H2 + nCO −→CnH2n+2OH + (n−)H2O
The Fisher-Tropsh process is exothermic, which means that the heat produced from this
process can be used in the production of syngas. Even after utilizing the heat for syngas
production, excess heat is still available for export to the district heating network. The
maximum amount of excess heat is estimated to be around 25%. [34]
Ammonia synthesis
Ammonia is produced from N2 and H2 through synthesis. The most known synthesis
process to produce ammonia is the Haber-Bosch process. Currently, the hydrogen for
ammonia production is produced from the steam methane reforming of natural gas. In
order to produce green ammonia, it is necessary to capture and store the CO2 emitted
during the steam methane reforming of natural gas or to use bioSNG or hydrogen pro-
duced through electrolysis. Nitrogen is obtained through an Air Separation Unit (ASU).
The ASU uses cryogenic distillation to separate argon, oxygen, and nitrogen in ambient
air. The process compresses and dehydrates the air and then chills it through a heat
exchanger to liquefy the gas. When the substance is liquid, it is possible to separate the
liquid nitrogen from the liquid oxygen and argon in the distillation process. [34]

The Haber-Bosch process is exothermic. The high amount of heat produced is used to
generate steam. In order to convert the nitrogen and hydrogen into ammonia, the pres-

loop cycle, the pressure usually drops to around 10 bar, which needs to be compressed

A.4
Direct Air Capture
S-DAC
The S-DAC adsorption process leads the airflow through a filter that binds the CO2. When
the filter reaches saturation, the process of separating the concentrated CO2, called the
desorption process, is initiated. The desorption process works by adding heat of around
85 −100◦C and reducing the pressure. Moisture is often absorbed from the air and needs
to be separated downstream by condensing the gas. The S-DAC cycle requires around
7.2 GJ energy to capture 1 tonne of CO2. The CO2 of the S-DAC cycle has a 98 −99.9%
purity. Due to the reduction of the working sorbent, some of the sorbent needs to be
replaced as the operation is running, which imposes an extra OPEX cost. [8]
L-DAC
The L-DAC adsorption works by directing the airflow through a potassium hydroxide (KOH)
capture solution, which, together with an air contactor, allows the CO2 to be chemically
absorbed. The desorption process happens by adding heat to the calcium regeneration
of 850 −900◦C. The high temperature needed for the concentrated CO2 to be released
can only be achieved by burning methane with current technologies. The L-DAC process
uses 366 kWh of electricity, 5.66 GJ of methane, and 5 kg of make-up limestone and KOH
to produce 1 tonne of CO2. [8]
A.5
Amine post-combustion technology
In the amine CC technology, the CO2 in the flue gas is absorbed by circulating the flue gas
in an alkaline solution. During absorption, heat will be released, increasing the flue gas
temperature with 25−35◦C, and it fosters a need for additional cooling. The concentrated
CO2 is released through the desorber, adding low-pressure steam of around 3-5 bara and
130 −150◦C. The steam will be added by a re-boiler. Re-boilers are already included in
CHP plants but must be added to an amine CC facility for industrial factories. The amine
technology uses 2.5-3.5 GJ of steam and 20-40 kWh electricity per tonne of CO2 captured.
[8]
Before entering the amine cycle loop, the flue gas needs to be around 30−40◦C and clean
from pollutants such as SO2, HCl, and NO2. Flue gas must be without pollutants since they
will react with the amines and create irreversible absorption or desorption. The irreversible
reaction can lead to heavy consumption of the amine solution, corrosion, and chemical
27
==PAGE:34==
waste. However, some of the pollutants are already cleaned from the flue gas in a pre-
treatment process at the existing emitting facilities, as NO2 has a high greenhouse gas
potential. Small amounts of pollutants and amine degradation products will inevitably build
up over time, raising the need for an amine reclaiming unit. The reclaiming unit can either
be a thermal process requiring steam at around 6-10 bara and caustic solutions or an ion-
exchange process requiring caustic solutions, sulfuric acid, and substantial amounts of
water. To avoid excessive water usage, most vendors use the thermal reclaiming process.
The reclaiming unit introduces the need for 0.2-1.6 kg amine consumption and 0.1-0.5 kg
caustic soda consumption per tonne of captured CO2. [8]
A.6
Hydrogen production
A hydrogen plant consists of an electrolyzer system, a balance of the plant, control sys-
tems, and civil infrastructure. The electrolyzer system consists of several electrolyzer
units, each consisting of a number of stacks of cells, together with a balance of that sys-
tem consisting of piping and connectors. The balance of the plant includes the supplies
and technologies for possible storage or transportation. The plant supplies needed to
operate are demineralized water and nitrogen for safety purposes. The control system is
all components involving the automation of the plant. [34]
Currently, there are three dominating electrolyzers: Alkaline Electrolysis Cell (AEC), Pro-
ton Exchange Membrane Cell (PEMEC), and Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cell (SOEC). AEC
is the most mature technology of the three. AEC uses potassium hydroxide as the liquid
electrolyte, the chemical solvent that facilitates the conversion of water into oxygen and
hydrogen. In the AEC, Nickel is used as a catalyst in some configurations or more earth-
rare metals such as ruthenium or iridium. Even though Nickel is more abundant than the
two others, it is expected to become more scarce in the coming years; therefore, Ni-plated
carbon steel is replacing Ni in bipolar plates, electrode supports, and even as a catalyst,
but it requires improved coatings of the carbon steel. Other less expensive catalysts such
as Raney Ni, Ni-, Fe and CU-containing alloys are also being deployed for some facilities.
Since AEC has a liquid electrolyte, it is necessary to install gas separators, scrubbers,
and purifiers. AEC has a footprint of 25 m2/MW and a stack lifetime of 70,000 h. [34]
The PEMEC has higher current densities than AEC, resulting in a lower footprint of 10
m2/MW, yet PEMEC has a stack lifetime of 55,000 h. The PEMEC uses a solid electrolyte
for the process and uses platinum and iridium as catalysts. Titanium is used for bipolar
plates and porous transport layers. The challenge of using these three materials is that
all of them are scarce, and it is, therefore, difficult to source materials in the future and for
large-scale projects. [34]
The SOEC has a low stack lifetime of 21,250 h and the highest footprint of the three, which
is 30 m2/MW. Contrary to the two others, SOEC almost solely uses cheap, abundant
materials. SOEC uses Zr, Fe, Mn, Ce, La, or Y materials. The SOEC electrolyzer also
uses Ni and Co, which are more costly and scarce but in much lower quantities, e.g.,
compared to AEC. The conversion of water into hydrogen and oxygen happens at high
temperatures of around 550 −850◦C, which makes it the most efficient electrolyzer of the
three. It is difficult for the SOEC electrolyzer to upscale the stack sizes due to ceramic
technology, which needs improvements. However, much research is going on for SOEC,
and it is already on the MW scale, which gives hope for the prospects of SOEC. [34]

A.7
Transportation of CO2
Liquefaction
For CO2 to be transported by truck or ship, it must be liquefied. In standard conditions,
the CO2 is compressed to around 15-20 bar and chilled to around −30 to −25◦C, which is
in the liquid phase for CO2. CO2 can be liquefied using a closed or open loop liquefaction
method. Open loop liquefaction methods have the advantage that they do not need a
working fluid to cool the CO2, yet they have high CAPEX costs, which make them unsuit-
able for small amounts, such as CO2 from biogas upgrading plants. Before entering the
liquefaction, it is essential that the CO2 is cleaned from impurities such as H2S, moisture,
and VOC as they can be fatal for equipment and safety. In the closed loop method, the
CO2 first comes through the compression train, then the pre-cooler, drier, ammonia cy-
cle, and finally through the distillation column. An alternative working fluid in the closed
loop is propane. In the compression train, the CO2 is compressed and cooled at several
stages, depending on the desired pressure and temperature of the outlet. After every
compression stage, the gas is cooled in intercoolers, and flash separators remove water.
Potential impurities in the gas are then removed. Now, the gas is cooled to 25◦C in a
cooler. After this, the gas enters the ammonia cycle loop, which changes the CO2 phase
to liquid. Lastly, the CO2 is purified even further in a distillation column. [8]
In the open loop cycle, the CO2 is compressed, cooled, and liquefied without a working
fluid like ammonia. There are two common open-cycle methods: the valve design and
the turbine design. In the valve design, the gas is first compressed and then transferred
to the cooling unit. After cooling, the CO2 is compressed during several stages to reach a
pressure of 85 bara. The CO2 is liquefied using cooling water in the last compressor stage.
Then, the pressure of the CO2 is raised again to match transport specifications using a
valve. After this, the liquid CO2 is separated from gaseous CO2 in a flash column. Lastly,
CO is removed from the CO2 in a CO stripper. In the turbine design, the compressor in
the valve design is substituted with a turbine. The turbine design enhances the possibility
of electricity production. However, the CO2 must be in the vapor phase. Due to the need
to expand the CO2 before entering the cycle, the turbine design is much more expensive
than the valve design and is. [8]

Intermediate storage
In connection with a liquefaction plant for CO2 transportation via ship or truck, an interme-
diate storage unit is necessary. The storage tanks can be either cylindrical or spherical.
The spherical tanks can contain double the pressure with the same wall thickness as the
cylindrical tanks. However, they are more complex to design and, hereby, more costly.
The cylindrical tanks can be vertical or horizontal, where the vertical structure takes up
less footprint but is slightly more costly. Different materials are utilized depending on the
pressure and temperature. If the stored CO2 has a high pressure of 45 bar and tempera-
ture of 10◦C, the material used is forged carbon steel. For 15 barg and −29◦C conditions,
carbon steel with vacuum or polyurethane insulation is utilized. If the pressure is seven
barg and temperature is −49◦C either carbon manganese steel, stainless steel, or low-
temperature steel can be utilized. In order to maintain the pressure in the tank, a small
amount of CO2 is allowed to be boiled off. The boiled-off CO2 can either be returned to
the liquefaction unit or locally liquefied by a refrigerator compressor. [8]
Trucks
The standard conditions for transporting liquid CO2 by truck are 15-18 bara and −30 to −
25◦C. Transporting CO2 by truck is widely applied today, especially for breweries and other
CO2-consuming factories. The standard road tanker for CO2 can transport 25-30 tonnes
and be loaded in around 45 minutes. Due to the size limit of the truck, the maximum size
that would make sense to transport is around 25−30 t.CO2/h. CO2 from large CHP plants,
which can emit around 100 t.CO2/h, and it would therefore not make sense to transport by
truck. Truck transportation has low CAPEX but high OPEX compared to the other types
of transportation. The only input needed for the truck transportation is fuel for the truck,
which for a 25 km distance will result in emissions of less than 1% for a transportation
cycle.
Ships
Only limited amounts of CO2 are currently transported by ship. The current ships only
have a capacity of 1000-2000 m3 but are expected to grow to 10,000 m3 in size in the
coming years. The CO2 will need to be in the liquid phase for transportation by ship, but
unlike truck transportation, there is not one standard. The CO2 can either be transported
at low pressure of around 6-8 bara and −50◦C, or medium pressure of 15-18 bara at
−30 to −25◦C, or at high pressure of 40-50 bara at 5 −15◦C. Lower-pressure tanks will
require more insulation due to low temperatures, and higher-pressure tanks will require
less insulation, but increasing the need for higher-pressure design. The CO2 will have a
density of 1150 kg/m3 at low pressure, 1070 kg/m3 at medium pressure, and 800-900
kg/m3 at high-pressure design. The fuel input for the ship is 90 kWh/day for a 4,000 m3
vessel and 180 kWh/day for a 10,000 m3 vessel.
For CO2 to be transported by ship, it is required that there must be a functioning termi-
nal with intermediate storage, pumps, and loading arms. If the CO2 is shipped from an
ending onshore pipeline, there would also need to be a liquefaction facility. As the CO2 is
compressed to some extent to be transported by pipeline, the liquefaction facility will be
cheaper and consume around 1/3 of the energy that a liquefaction plant at a point source
facility would do.

CO2 pipes
CO2 can be transported in all phases in onshore and offshore pipelines, yet if the distance
is more than 400 km, the CO2 must be transported in the dense phase. CO2 pipelines
are applicable if the transported amount is large, e.g., from large emitters to storage, a
terminal, or a utilization facility. The CO2 can either be transported in gas (25-35 barg,
+5 to + 25◦C), liquid (15-30 barg, −28 to + 5◦C), dense (80-110 barg, +5 to + 25◦C)
or supercritical (>75 barg,> 31◦C) phase. If CO2 is transported in liquid form, it must be
reheated to around 5◦C to prevent the soil around the pipe from freezing. The supercritical
phase is very unstable and can easily change the phase along the pipeline, so extra safety
concerns must be considered.
Due to the high density of CO2 in the liquid phase (1100kg/m3), the pipeline’s diameter
is decreased, resulting in lower CAPEX. The OPEX is also relatively low since there is a
reduced need for reciprocating pumps for pipelines in the liquid phase. However, the liquid
phase is less stable than, e.g., the gas and dense phase, which means that extra safety
concerns must be considered to prevent the formation of dry ice in the case of an outage.
The density of CO2 in the dense phase is also relatively high (800 −1000kg/m3), which
reduces the CAPEX, but it has a higher need for reciprocating pumps, which increases the
OPEX. The gas phase has a density of < 1000kg/m3, which increases the CAPEX cost as
the pipeline’s diameter increases significantly. Another challenge with transporting CO2
in the gas phase is the pressure drop along the pipeline, which needs to be increased
more frequently with compressors than in the other phases, and it results in higher OPEX
costs for compressors. The supercritical phase is a mixture of the gas and liquid phase,
which increases the CAPEX and OPEX for compressors.

C.1
Implications of the temporal resolution choice
With the four-week setup and 56 hours per week, there are 224 time steps for each vari-
able. The number of variables increases the number of iterations at most by n·m
ϵ , where n
is the number of variables, m is the number of constraints, and ϵ is the tolerance of the dif-
ference of objective function value between two iterations [52]. The number of iterations
is proportional to the processing time, which means that the processing time is higher with
more variables. The implications of choosing this setup are investigated by analyzing the
electricity and district heating demands and the solar and wind variations. The possible
yearly time steps of 8736 hours in Balmorel are decreased to 224 hours by replicating the
first hour of the week to the two following ones, and the fourth hour is replicated to the
two following ones, and the pattern follows this way. Week eight of the year is replicated
to the first 13 weeks, week 22 to week 14-26, so this pattern continues. The replicated
data is plotted with the original data to display the differences.
