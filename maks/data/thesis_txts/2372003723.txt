Abstract
This report looks into the modal damping of a ﬂoating wind turbine, with the goal of
determining the damping ratios dependency on loading conditions.
The analysis is built on a lab test of a triple spar ﬂoater design, tested under diﬀerent
load conditions, using wind and wave loading. Using data obtained from a Qualisys
motion capture system the 6-DoF of the tower base was obtained, these being Surge,
Sway, Heave, Roll, Pitch and Yaw. By converting the collected data from the time
domain to the frequency domain the natural freqencies of the system is obtained.
The free decay test of the system is analysed and it is found that the ﬂoater has non
linear damping.
Using Operational Modal Analysis, the modal damping is estimated for the ﬂoater’s
Surge, Heave, Pitch and Yaw motion. However do to time series being shorter than
recommended the results have not been found to be completely stable.
From the
estimated it was possible to visualise a clear tendency for the damping ratio to increase
as the sea states increases in severity.
Finally a method for implementing the found damping ratios in a linear frequency
domain model is proposed. The results from the model show that a linear model is not
fully capable of recreating the lab measurements. The errors in estimation is believed
to be caused by non linear behaviour, not captured by the model.

0.1
The Project
In the latest years wind turbines have established them self as one of the most eﬃcient
and reliable forms of renewable energy. Starting out with the construction of onshore
turbines that proved the concept, there are now also several oﬀshore wind farms, pro-
ducing electricity for the Danish power grid.
In the oﬀshore market the mono pile foundation is completely dominating the market,
with its easy installation and structural reliability. However, the mono pile has the
very intuitive problem that it is not possible at large water depth, about 45m [1], as
the dynamic responses, and installation cost, in both cases become too great. But as
always if there is a problem or in this case a limit, there are people who have ideas
for getting around it. The idea here being that the turbine itself does not have to be
directly connected to the seabed, but instead could be ﬂoating and simply be held in
place by mooring lines. Many diﬀerent types of ﬂoaters have been pitched, but so far
no design has proven to be superior. And there is therefore still a lot of research going
into diﬀerent types of ﬂoaters to determine the structural, dynamical and also equally
important economical properties.
This report will look into the dynamical properties of a triple spar ﬂoater, designed by
the University of Stuttgart, with a centrally placed tower. The ﬂoater itself is to be
held in place by mooring lines. For the analysis a scaled model of 1:60 was tested by
[2], under a variety of diﬀerent wind and wave loads. A picture of the ﬂoater can be
seen in Figure 1.
This report is aiming to determining the modal damping or the turbine, both us-
ing traditional means of free decay test, and by the use of the acquired data from [2] to
make a Operation Modal Analysis (OMA) of the system. And use the results from the
OMA to create a physical link between real world test and numerical modelling. By
making an OMA there is made a correlation between sea states and modal damping
parameters, a correlation that is not possible with standard free decay tests.
The concept of OMA has been around for many years but has so fare not seen extensive
use. However, in the last decade it has been slowly gaining ground due to. increased
computational power and quality of test equipment. What OMA should be capable
of is to determine frequency, damping ratios and modeshapes, for modes that are not
possible to test independently.
This report is parted in to four chapters each looking into diﬀerent aspects of the
project, and a partial conclusion will be made at the end of each, except for the last
chapter which will contain the ﬁnal conclusion for the project, the chapters are as
follow.
Test Campaign: This chapter looks into the actual lab test of the ﬂoating wind
turbine, the needed theory and process for data assembling is described. natural fre-
quency and preliminary damping ratios is also determined here.
Operational Modal Analysis: This chapter descries the theory behind OMA, and
OMA analysis is carried out on the lab test data, to determine frequency, damping

Chapter 1
Test campaign
When a new structure is to be build, whether this be a apartment, dam, bridge or an on
shore wind turbine, there is often only made a numerical analysis as practical model test
are expensive and have high demands for testing equipment and facility. The numerical
analysis is here much easier as only a computer is needed and there most likely already
is a program in the ﬁrm that is capable of calculating the structural response of the
system to a satisfying degree. Model test is therefore just an unnecessary expenditure.
But these numerical models are only reliable if there already have been made a lot of
model test for these structure types and the behaviour is well known. When looking
into a ﬂoating wind turbines, which is a relatively new structural area, it is necessary to
carry out model tests and see if a numerical model actually catches all of the turbines
behaviour. So with the goal of obtaining new information for ﬂoaters the test campaign
for the Triple Spar Floater was carried out.
1.1
Test Campaign Theory
The analysis carried out in this report is built on data obtained from a Triple Spar
Floater (TSF) test campaign carried out in 2016 by Fjelstrup & Larsen [2].
This
chapter will describe the theory needed to understand the obtained result of the test.
The test set-up itself will be described, and a preliminary structural analysis for the
OMA results to be compared with, will be carried out.
1.1.1
Time and frequency domain
When working with structural data you have two options either to work in the time
domain or to work in the frequency domain. One domain is not superior to the other
but there are advantages to working in each of them. The main advantage of the time
domain is that it is easily relatable, at a given time t there is a displacement x, velocity
v and an acceleration a. One can therefore easily show and understand how a structure
behaves over time.
The frequency domain on the other hand can not tell us the deﬂection at a given time
but can tell us how much a given frequency is represented in a signal, by plotting the

1.1.2
Filtering
When dealing with measurements it is always impotent to determine whether or not
ﬁltering is necessary. Usually there will always be some degree of noise in the signal
as, electrical components used for measuring the structural response, all have some
degree of noise in the measurements. Noise, can lead to false responses data, especially
when looking at the data at a PDS plot where false peaks might occur because of the
sampling frequency, inbreed noise signals in the apparatus and the electric signal itself,
along with responses form other modal responses than the one of interest. To ensure
that these ”false” signals are not analysed the signal can be ﬁltered to ensure that only

Chapter 2
Operational Modal Analysis
As the name Operational Modal Analysis implies, the analysis is/can be made under
normal operational conditions. Meaning that is it not necessary to decommission a
structure for a while to subject it to known forces and from there get force responses,
which is the standard way of going about the analysis. OMA gives the opportunity
to estimate the dynamic properties while keeping the structure in commission. For
the purpose of making OMA of the turbine there is used test data where the model
is tested under conditions that correspond to diﬀerent operational stages it could be
subjected to in real life.
There is however a number of assumptions that has to be made when using OMA.
1. The forces are assumed to be Gaussian distributed.
2. The response is assumed to be stationary and ergodic.
3. The system is assumed linear, with low damping ratio, below (10-15%).
4. Mode shapes are geometrically orthogonal.
What these assumptions mean and what inﬂuence they have is described later in this
Chapter:
OMA can be carried out in both the Time Domain, and the Frequency Domain. with
each domain having a number of diﬀerent identiﬁcation algorithms. In this report there
will be explained and made use of the time domain algorithm named, The Eigensystem
Realization Algorithm (ERA).
There exist other types of OMA, examples of these being the Stochastic Subspace
Identiﬁcation (SSI) and the Frequency-Domain Decomposition (FDD), among others
that could also be used, but these will not be looked into here, for more information
on these there is refereed to [11].
The OMA methods have already been used in dynamic analyses, examples of these are
that the ERA has been used to determined the dynamic response of a ship haul [12],
and FDD has been tested by [13] to see if the dynamic property of a spar ﬂoater, could
be determined from a numerical model. In all cases OMA proved to give very useful
results.

states that for a large number of independent and random variables the sum of the
variables will approach a Gaussian distribution, as the number of variables increases

2.1.2
Stationary and Ergodic
The OMA assumes that the loading is stationary and Ergodic.
A stationary loading does not mean that the loading is constant at all times but rather
refers to the probability distribution of the loading ensemble being independent of the
time [3]. Meaning that the probability distribution taken from t1 to t2 would be the
same as one taken from t2 to t3.
A ergodic process it when the average of a single time series is the the same as the
ensemble averages.

2.2.1
Elongation of the signal
As the length of the test signal was not of the desired length it was tested whether or
not it was possible to elongate the signal in any way. Diﬀerent approaches was tested
but none of them were found to be useful.
Copying or Mirroring the signal
It was tested if it was possible to copy the signal and place it at the end of the signal,
thereby creating a signal that had double the length. It was also tested if the signal
could be mirrored at the end to ensure no discontinuity, which the copying could result
in. Neither of these procedures was successfully as there is gained no new information.
the procedures simply resulted in a repetitive correlation.
Placing diﬀerent test in end of each other
With the help of the QuLAF model (see section 3.1.1) it was tested whether or not
it was possible to place diﬀerent test samples at the end of each other in that way
ensure that there was new information. This procedure was not successfully. And
it is believed that the cause is that the lack of connection between the diﬀerent test
gives a discontinuity in the response from the diﬀerent modal response resulting in the
correlation function, not having the sought after free decay.

2.3
OMA Free decay
The OMA theory was originally made to evaluate the system parameters from free
decay tests. This section will look into whether or not the result from OMA is the
same as the results from the logarithmic decrement.
When using OMA theory to determine the frequency and damping ratios, the initial
step of determining the correlations are simply skipped, e.g. starting from equation
2.30 in the ERA.
When running the OMA it is of importance to take a suitable number of rows (s)
in the Hankel matrix (which is not really a Hankel matrix when only one signal is
used). Also the start time, sample frequency, and number of columns in the Block
Hankel matrix inﬂuence the results. By not taking all logged data but skipping some,
the computation time could be highly reduced, if all data points were used the Matlab
terminated the calculation because of the size of the data. In the end it was decided to
use every ﬁfth of the logged data, resulting in a time step of dtOMA = 5dts = 0.0313s
which gives about 650 sample points over one Surge oscillation, and about 120 in the
Pitch oscillation which should be more than enough, it was however found that tak-
ing fewer points gave a less stable result. For both the Surge and Pitch decay there
was tested what was the optimal test length (number of columns in the block Hankel
matrix).

2.4.5
Source of Problems
Time series
The length of the time series is most likely the largest source of error and determi-
nation problems. The 10 min long time series is not long enough to have a suﬃcient
representation of the structural response. This results in the CF being unclear and
determination harder. Optimal the test series should have been about 9 hours long
and should at least have been half an hour.
Gaussian distribution
The OMA technique demanded that the loading should be Gaussian distributes, mean-
ing a white noise loading on the structure. This was not upheld because of the loading
only coming from the wave and the wave spectrum leaving ”holes” in the frequency
spectrum where no load was present.
Damping
The non-linear damping of the system which is clearly found/seen from the damping
ratios determined from the free decays, also makes the determinations harder. As it is
noted that one of the initial assumptions is that the system is linear, which it is clearly
is not.
The larger the damping ratio is, the faster the response of the CF decays, a large
damping ratio would ensure that there was not enough signal available to analyse as
was seen for the decay test for the Pitch with wind, in Figure 2.6. This is quite possibly
the same eﬀect that makes the Pitch modal parameter impossible to determine in the
sea states with wind

2.6
Part Conclusion
From the roughly estimated damping ratios found from the logarithmic decrement, it
was determined that the total length of the data signals used for OMA analysis should
be about 9 hours, or at least half an hour. However the available data series was only
10min long. Meaning that the test data is not optimal for OMA analysis.
The OMA assumes linear damping which from the logarithmic decrement was deter-
mined not to be the case. The loading is assumed to be Gaussian, meaning white
noise loading, as the loading is comping from only the waves and wind, the white noise
loading is not obtained.
From the ERA test of the free decay is was determined that the ERA was very inﬂu-
enced by what peak the analysis was started from and of which order the ERA was
solved. The frequency could be determined with much precision and high stability.
Whereas the damping ratio was very dependent on the dimension and the starting
peak. From the decay test it is also found that problems can arrive if the decay is not
centred around zero as was found from the wind surge decay. Finally it was found that
for very short OMA signals, as was the case for the Pitch decay in wind, it was not
possible to determine the frequency and damping ratio. This was most likely caused
by a too high damping ratio.
Using the ERA it was, for the sea states without wind, possible to determine the
Surge, Sway, Heave and Yaw. for almost all sea states. But with some of the values
not being determined for certain sea states and thereby missing a value for some sea
states.
With wind it was not possible to determine the Pitch decay, as was also found for the
ERA of the free decay analysis. This is believed to be because of the damping ratio
being too large, not leaving enough decay in the CF to be analysed
From the mode shapes it was observed that both a Surge* and Sway* motion was
found, the (*) indicating that they where a combination of the true Surge and Sway,
as seen in Figure 2.11. This was in line with the responses found from the STD analysis
in the previous chapter, where it was found that the Sway motion was very prominent.

The modal participation was not usefull for determining real mode from the noise
modes, the reason for this most likely being that the length of the signal was not long
enough.
From using the damping ratios determined from the ERA, the damping was plot-
ted as a function of the standard deviation. From these plots there was seen a clear
increase in the damping ratio as the STD increased. For the Pitch and Heave it was
found the misaligned waves decreased the damping ratios, whereas the reverse was
observed for the waves. Inclusion of wind was found to increase the damping ratio for

Numerical Modelling
Numerical modelling have many advantages over lab test, as they are much faster to
perform, cost less as fewer man hours are generally used, and there is no cost for
rent of equipment. But all these savings rely on the assumptions that the numerical
model is capable of calculating all relevant structural responses. It is therefore of great
importance to have the numerical model validated with some real life responses.
This chapter will look into how the damping ratios, found in the previous chapters,
can be incorporated in a numerical model, and test whether the model ﬁnds responses
that concur with the responses found in the lab test.
3.1
Numerical model
The numerical model used is the Quick Load Analysis for a Floating foundation
(QuLAF) which is an extension of the QuLA program for pile foundations. The QuLA
model is thoroughly described in [17] and the QuLAF model is developed by Antonio
Pegalajar-Jurado at DTU Wind, a short presentation of the program will be given
here.

3.5
Part Conclusion
The linear QulAF model is found not to have the same damping ratios as found in the
ERA analysis. The already implemented damping in the QuLAF model comes from
the potential damping calculated in WAMIT, and some structural damping (estimated
with Reyleigh damping).
A method for adding additional damping where the found damping ratios from ERA
could is taken from modal parameters back to the system damping matrices. This was
done by picking out the speciﬁc mode shapes related to the natural frequencies. This
method however imposed a problem in the sense that there was not imposed propor-
tional damping. This resulted in damping being added across the modes as seen in
table 3.1. For getting around this problem it was proposed to take the cross additions
into account by solving four equations with four unknowns. Thereby gaining the in-
tended addition of damping.
From the RAO the addition of damping gave a large reduction in response ampli-
tude. It was also observed that the increased damping widen the peak responses. as
was to be expected from general damping theory. The increased damping was found
to make the Surge less dependent on the Pitch, while the Pitch became more depen-
dent on the Surge. The Heave and nacelle acceleration was also found to have much
inﬂuence form Surge, beside that there was also found that the Nacelles RAO did not
ﬂatten out but had several ”humps”. One of these ”humps” that was seen in there
RAO was also observed in the PSD from the test data, indicating that the RAO is
correct.
With the damping ratios determined from the ERA analysis implemented in the
QuLAF model, There was found that the Surge and Pitch responses from the test
data was eﬀected by the low frequency Surge mode in a much higher degree than what
was estimated in the QuLAF model. By ﬁltering out the responses blow 0.1Hz the
Surge responses was found to be more identical. The reason for the QuALF model
not catching this response is believed to be caused by non linear responses that is not
implemented in the model.
The Heave is found to be comparable but with the QuALF model ﬁnding larger re-
sponses especially for the higher sea states. For SS9 where the Heave natural frequency
is inside the wave spectrum the QuLAF ﬁnds much larger responses than what is found

from the lab data. This is caused by a much more resonant response in the QuLAF
model than what is seen in the lab test data.
The nacelle acceleration in general is found to be estimated pretty accurately there is

coming from tower modes that is not incorporated in the QuLAF model.
As it was not possible to determine the Pitch damping ratios form the ERA anal-

The QuLAF model is not found to perfectly describe the response of the turbine.

4.1
Next Test Campaign
As there have been stated earlier in the report there are quite a few factors that in-
ﬂuence the quality of the results gained from the OMA analysis, much of the trouble
originating in the fact that the data was not originally collected for the purpose of use
in OMA. This section will therefore come with some recommendations for what should
be done in the next test-campaign to ensure increased quality from the OMA.
Start with Freedecay and FFT analysis
Start of the test campaign should be to estimate the damping ratios and frequencies
though simple free decay test, from where the damping ratio and frequency can be
estimated, this will give a god idea of how long the test series should be to provide
good results.
Length of Test
As has been stated several times throughout the report, the data set is much shorter
than what is recommended for use in OMA. Increased length of the time series would
be likely to decrease the noise so that frequencies, damping and mode shapes could be
more precisely determined.
The estimate of the damping ratios and frequency found from free decay can be used
to determine the time length using the methods described in section 2.2
Measurements
Measurements should be of same unit, it is possible to measure in both displacement
and accelerations, and then convert one into the other, by taking the integral of deriva-
tive, but it is very likely that information will be lost in the transformation. For this
reason it was chosen not to use diﬀerent measurement types in this project. Likewise
measurement of degrees are not optimal unless one has a clear idea of how one would
translate them to displacements or acceleration, as was done in this report.
The recommendation would therefore be to use accelerometers measuring 3-DoF, and
place these on top of each spar, one at the bottom of the turbine and one on the

added on the tower depending on how ﬂexible it is to get more tower modes. From

Start with commercial program
This might seem like an obvious choice when doing any kind of project, but it was

seeing if there are peaks at the natural frequencies and also seeing how dominant the
noise is in the signal. The SSI on the other hand can give estimations of the damping
ratios, However like the ERA there is problems with determining stable damping ratios
for the short signal.

4.3
Conclusion
There was in this report only worked with the Surge, Sway, Heave, Roll, Pitch and
Yaw responses of the ﬂoater, several other measurement were made but were not used
for analysis in this report. The motions were captured by a Qualisys system.
The natural frequencies of the ﬂoater were obtained from the frequency domains PSD
plot, ﬁnding that fsurge = fSway = 0.049Hz, fY aw = 0.11Hz, fpitch = froll = 0.27Hz and
fheave = 0.46Hz.
Using the logarithmic decrement method for free decays, the damping ratio was found
to be non-linear for the Surge and Pitch mode, with the damping ratio increasing for
larger displacements. The rest of the modes were not tested using free decay.
The STD of the ﬂoater showed that the ﬂoater initiated 3-d responses, for 2-d loading
indicating that the mooring of the system was not completely symmetric. This eﬀect
was also observed in the mode shapes found from the ERA analysis where the Surge
and Sway mode were found to be a combination of the two.
The ERA analysis could for most load cases determine the natural frequencies with
high accuracy, whereas the damping ratios was a lot less stable meaning high uncertain-
ties in the estimations. For the load cases with wind it was not possible to determine
the damping ratio for the Pitch, which is believed to be because of a too high damping
ratio for the mode, caused by the aerodynamic damping.
Most problems in the OMA estimations are believed to come from the time series being
too short. These short measurements result in a high noise level in the measurements.
The measurement used was of a length of 10min where as the theory estimated that
at least half an hour was necessary.
The estimated damping ratios showed a clear increase of the damping ratio as the
sea states, and thereby the STD, increased. There was however also seen some points
that deviated from the trend, some sea states where the determination was impossible,
these errors are believed to be caused by the too short time series, and the non linear
damping.
Inclusion of wind load was in Surge and Yaw seen to increase the damping ratio,
indicating that the aerodynamic damping had a positive eﬀect on the system. For
misaligned waves there was found an increase of the Surge’s damping ratio, whereas a
decrease was observed in the Pitch.
The proposed method for implementing the estimated damping ratios into the QuLAF
model, gave an easy conversion of the found damping ratios into a frequency inde-
pendent system damping matrix C. The method was however very simpliﬁed and
the procedure resulted in damping increase across the modes, as the method did not
produce proportional damping. These cross relations should therefore be taken into
account when adding damping.

From the comparison of the QuLAF estimates and the lab results it was found that
the QuLAF model under predicted the Surge and Pitch motion, the Heave response

The errors from the QuLAF model are believed to come from the fact that the model

It is believed that better and more reliable result can be estimated from the OMA
