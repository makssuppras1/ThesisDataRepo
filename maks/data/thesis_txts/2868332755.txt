Summary
Reconstructing visual stimuli from neural signals offers new opportunities to decode
brain activity and develop advanced brain-computer interfaces.
This thesis evalu-
ates a two-stage pipeline designed to transform EEG signals into coherent visual
reconstructions by mapping them into the CLIP embedding space and refining these
mappings for image generation via Stable Diffusion. The research provides a compre-
hensive examination of alignment and reconstruction quality, incorporating previously
unexplored metrics and analyses across subjects and stimulus categories.
Reproducing the methodology of Li et al. [1], this study matched their reported
zero-shot retrieval performance for the best subject while significantly extending the
analysis to address inter- and intra-subject variability. The results highlight substan-
tial differences in alignment quality across subjects, most likely shaped by neural
signal variability and individual differences in response to stimuli. By organizing the
original stimulus labels into categories using the WordNet hierarchy, this work further
analyzed the semantic alignment achieved within the CLIP space, revealing strong
category-level consistency for objects such as animals and foods but challenges with
semantically overlapping or underrepresented categories.
In the second stage, embedding refinement enabled reconstructions that, while
varying in quality, demonstrated the ability to capture the broad semantic context of
the original stimuli across all subjects. While the images lacked precise visual details,
they reflected general categories and distinguishing features, such as consistent align-
ments within familiar object classes. The findings illuminate the trade-offs between
achieving semantic coherence and fine-grained visual fidelity, particularly given the
reliance on MSE loss for refining embeddings in the provided diffusion architecture.
This trade-off, alongside the observed variability across subjects and categories, points
to the need for strategies that balance these objectives more effectively.
By reproducing state-of-the-art methods and providing deeper insights into sub-
ject variability and category-level performance, this thesis advances the understanding
of EEG-based visual reconstruction pipelines. It highlights both their potential and
the challenges of creating robust and precise reconstructions, suggesting directions
for future work to enhance alignment quality, generalization to unseen stimuli, and
semantic precision.

Introduction
The human brain’s remarkable ability to perceive and interpret the world has long
inspired advancements in neuroscience and artificial intelligence. Our ability to de-
rive meaning from complex visual stimuli, generalize across unfamiliar scenarios, and
reconstruct these experiences internally represents the pinnacle of cognitive sophisti-
cation. As researchers seek to combine human cognition and computational models,
leveraging brain activity to guide AI-driven interpretations has become an exciting
frontier. Visual Stimulus Reconstruction, in particular, aims to translate neural sig-
nals into coherent visual reconstructions, bridging neuroscience and AI to unlock new
possibilities in brain-computer interfacing.
Zero-shot learning reflects a systems ability to infer and generalize to unseen data
by drawing on prior knowledge. This capability mirrors human cognition: we often
understand novel concepts by extrapolating from related experiences. In the context
of visual stimulus reconstruction, zero-shot learning holds immense potential. A ro-
bust pipeline should ideally reconstruct both familiar and unfamiliar visual stimuli.
Recent breakthroughs have introduced new opportunities - among these, CLIP
[2] and Stable Diffusion [3] are particularly notable. CLIP aligns visual and textual
data, providing a holistic, multi-modal understanding of the world, while Stable Diffu-
sion generates detailed, coherent images based on CLIP embeddings of textual input.
These models, inherently designed to generalize beyond their training data, offer a
foundation for reconstructing visual stimuli from neural recordings.
The current state-of-the-art (SOTA) in this field is exemplified by the Visual Stim-
ulus Reconstruction pipeline introduced by [1], a significant contribution presented
at NeurIPS 2024. This pipeline utilizes CLIP to align neural signals to a semanti-
cally meaningful space, and refining them for image generation via Stable Diffusion.
Figure 1.1 illustrates their process: EEG signals are aligned to the CLIP space and
subsequently refined - enabling both Zero-Shot-retrieval and Image Reconstruction.
Figure 1.1: Visual Stimulus Reconstruction model by [1], as adapted in this thesis.

1 Introduction
A key challenge lies in balancing semantic alignment and embedding precision.
While alignment ensures that EEG embeddings retain high-level conceptual mean-
ing, precision dictates their accuracy for generating coherent images. Achieving both
simultaneously requires addressing subject-specific and stimulus-specific variability.
Neural encoding differs across individuals, as does the semantic ambiguity of certain
stimuli categories, introducing variability into the alignment and reconstruction.
Furthermore, zero-shot learning poses its own challenges. CLIP’s robust embed-
ding space enables generalization across modalities, but it remains unclear to what
extent a Visual Stimulus Reconstruction pipeline can leverage this capability when
translating noisy EEG signals into semantically meaningful embeddings. Understand-
ing how well the pipeline generalizes to unseen stimuli and categories is critical to
extending its utility beyond tightly controlled experimental datasets.
This thesis builds upon the work of Li et al. [1] (2024), reproducing their results
to validate the pipeline’s robustness while addressing unexplored aspects of its per-
formance.
The core contributions of this research lie in advancing the evaluation
frameworks and analyzing alignment quality. Specifically, novel metrics and visual-
izations are introduced to examine semantic alignment and embedding precision, the
variability across subjects, and the generalization to unseen stimuli. This evaluation
aims to deepen the understanding of how EEG signals interact with AI-driven visual
reconstruction systems and where potential improvements can be made.
With that, the research questions guiding this thesis are as follows:
Research Questions
On Semantic Alignment:
RQ1 How effectively can EEG signals be mapped onto semantically rich visual
embedding spaces like CLIP?
RQ2 How do trade-offs between semantic alignment and embedding precision
influence reconstruction quality?
On Variability in Performance:
RQ3 What role do subject-specific and stimulus-specific factors play in align-
ment quality and reconstruction performance?
On Zero-Shot Generalization:
RQ4 To what extent can the Visual Stimulus Reconstruction pipeline gener-
alize to unseen stimuli through zero-shot learning?
By addressing these questions, this thesis aims to enhance the understanding of
how human brain activity can be mapped onto semantically meaningful spaces for
reconstructing visual stimuli, highlighting both the potential and challenges of this
emerging field.

Background and Related Work
2.1. Electroencephalography (EEG)
Electroencephalography (EEG) is a widely used, non-invasive technique for measuring
the brain’s electrical activity, by detecting voltage fluctuations generated by ionic
currents within the neurons of the brain.
One of the most significant benefits of
EEG is its exceptional temporal resolution, which captures neural dynamics on the
order of milliseconds [4], providing a real-time representation of neural activity. The
method involves placing electrodes on the scalp to capture these electrical signals,
providing information on the brain-activity, that directly translates to specific areas
of the human brain.
2.1.1. Advantages and Limitations of EEG
EEG presents several distinct advantages over other neuroimaging techniques such
as Magnetoencephalography (MEG) and functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging
(fMRI). It is relatively small, portable, and cost-effective, making it highly versatile
for various applications, from basic research to clinical practice. Unlike MEG, which
requires a magnetically shielded room and expensive equipment, or fMRI, which in-
volves complex machinery and high operational costs, EEG can be employed in more
practical and less controlled environments. However, EEG is not without its limita-
tions. The spatial resolution of EEG is lower than that of fMRI [5], which can provide
detailed images of brain structures and activity. Furthermore, EEG signals are sus-
ceptible to noise, making them challenging to interpret. Thankfully, various filtering
and artifact removal techniques can help alleviate these issues.
Regardless, EEG
remains one of the most accessible and widely used neuroimaging tools worldwide [6].
2.1.2. EEG Measurement and Configurations
EEG measurements are conducted using electrodes arranged in specific configurations
on the scalp. The most common system is the International 10-20 system, where elec-
trodes are positioned based on the relative distances between anatomical landmarks:
the nasion (front of the head), inion (back of the head), and the left and right preau-
ricular points (sides of the head). More advanced configurations, such as the 10-10 or
10-5 systems, offer higher spatial resolution by adding more electrodes. The number
of channels, or electrodes, used in an EEG recording can vary, typically ranging from
32 to 256, depending on the desired resolution and the specific research or clinical
application, for Visual Stimuli Datasets, the most typical configuration is the 10-10

As a data-structure, each electrode is sampled at a given sampling rate, resulting in

2.2. Visual Stimulus Reconstruction
Visual stimulus reconstruction experiments involve recording brain activity as par-
ticipants are exposed to a series of curated images, patterns or textures. Some ex-
periments choose to incorporate other modalities, such as an audible cue as to what
they’ll be looking at [8, 9], or giving the participant instructions of imagining a spe-
cific object rather than visually presenting it to them [10]. During the experiment,
the brain activity is measured – early renditions of these experiments were typically
performed using fMRI [11, 12], however, due to the aforementioned disadvantages,
and in line with the increasing interest in these studies, recent experiments have
gravitated toward the use of EEG caps. Though the current goal of Visual Stimulus
Reconstruction is to recreate the experienced stimuli through various deep learning
methods, its origins can be traced back to early brain pattern-information analyses
and visual stimuli classification [13, 14, 15, 16].
2.2.1. Key Parameters in EEG Data Acquisition
During the data acquisition, three key parameters are of primary importance, namely
the Spatial- and Temporal resolution of the signal, as well as the Stimulus Onset Asyn-
chrony (SOA).
As previously mentioned, the number of EEG channels determine the spatial res-
olution of the recorded brain activity. More channels allow for a finer mapping of the
activity within the brain, however, there’s also a direct linear correlation between the
number of channels and the size of the dataset in terms of gigabytes.
Meanwhile, the sampling rate affects the temporal resolution of the recorded signals.
Higher sampling rates capture more detailed neural activity, essential for identifying

fast and subtle changes in brain responses.
Finally, SOA, understood as the time interval between the onset of one stimulus
and the onset of the next, plays a significant role in the temporal dynamics of EEG
signals. Research shows, that while it takes the brain between 50-120 ms for the
recognition of an object [17], further time is needed for the brain to completely process
what it has seen. Crucial EEG decoding features can be extracted within specific time
windows after stimulus onset, such as classification tasks performing best on features
320-480 ms after stimulus onset [18], while relevant decoding features are even present
at 550 ms after stimulus onset [19]. Meanwhile, research indicates that the brain can
continue extracting essential features of a stimulus, even after encountering several
subsequent stimuli [1]. Datasets with carefully controlled SOA are better suited for
capturing these critical neural responses - and while the higher the better, there exists
a bottleneck in the fact that larger SOAs are typically associated with less collected
data, as lengthening the SOA simply lengthens the data collection time quite linearly.
2.2.2. Datasets and the Experimental Setup
The origins of the Visual Stimulus Reconstruction paradigm can be traced back to
brain-decoding datasets, such as Kriegeskorte et al. (2009) [13], Carlson et al. (2013)
[14], Cichy et al. (2014) [15] and Kaneshiro et al. (2015) [16]. Though originally devel-
oped for brain pattern-information analyses, these datasets were later adopted as clas-
sification tasks, while Kaneshiro et al. lived a second life as an image-reconstruction
task, fetching fairly impressive results [20]. These early datasets were small in size,
consisting of 24 to 92 unique images, typically featuring objects super-imposed onto
gray backgrounds, with a colored border indicating a corresponding object category.
While these datasets started the era of Visual Stimulus Reconstruction, they were
later criticized, with papers commenting on the drawbacks of datasets of such size –
stating that the limited amount of unique stimuli will lead to significant over-fitting,
with models learning to reconstruct images almost perfectly. Particularly due to the
models repeated exposure to identical images, thereby reducing the dataset’s utility
for generalization to new stimuli [21].
Subsequent datasets rapidly expanded in scale by incorporating EEG data into
three major pre-existing image databases — ImageNet (2009) [22], MSCOCO (2015)
[23], and THINGS (2019) [24] — each offering distinct advantages.
ImageNet:
The largest manually annotated image-text database, comprises 14 mil-
lion images across 21,000 categories. In addition to its extensive categorization, over
a million of these images include bounding-box annotations, enabling precise object
localization. In the context of Visual Stimulus Reconstruction, ImageNet has served
as the foundation for the most widely used EEG-to-image dataset: PeRCeiVe Lab
EEG-ImageNet1 (2019) [25] and its subsequent, unoﬀicial expansion EEG-ImageNet2
(2024) [4].

The two EEG-ImageNet datasets build upon ImageNet, but differ in scope. EEG-
ImageNet1 was collected from six subjects, each shown 2,000 images organized into
40 broad ”super-classes”, based on subsets of the original 21,000 ImageNet categories.
These classes spanned general categories, such as Animals, Transportation, House-
hold Items, Food etc. – Building on this, EEG-ImageNet2 expanded the number of
classes from 40 to 80 by refining some of the broader categories in EEG-ImageNet1
into more specific subclasses. For instance, the Musical Instruments category was
subdivided into instruments such as accordions, cellos and flutes. This dataset was
collected from a new participant pool of 16 subjects, each shown 4,000 curated im-
ages. Both datasets were originally designed with classification tasks in mind, with
images selected to minimize background complexity and maximize object recogniz-
ability. Regarding the experimental setup, each class was presented in blocks of 50
consecutive images - with each image being displayed for 500ms - while EEG signals
were recorded at a 1000 Hz sampling rate, using 128 and 62 channels respectively.
Critically, neither dataset includes predefined train/test splits, a decision criticized
within literature, as it increases the likelihood of overlapping neural data between
training and testing phases due to the block-based presentation structure [26].
Microsoft’s Common Objects in Context (MSCOCO):
A database with over
330,000 complex images and 1.5 million object instances in 80 categories. A distin-
guishing feature of MSCOCO is its focus on contextual understanding. Images are
annotated not only with object labels but also with bounding-boxes, object segmenta-
tions, as well as detailed captions of the scene and interactions among objects. Brain-
to-Image datasets targeting complex scene reconstruction, such as Natural Scenes
(2022) [27] and the AllJoined1 Dataset (2024) [17], typically rely on the MSCOCO
database.
The AllJoined1 Dataset is a dataset developed as an EEG adaptation of the pop-
ular fMRI Natural Scenes Dataset (NSD). While NSD is a large-scale dataset built
from over 70,000 natural scene images sourced from MSCOCO, it is not suitable for
this thesis due to the aforementioned disadvantages of fMRI. AllJoined1 reformulates
the dataset, by repurposing a subset of 960 images from the NSD shared1000 for
EEG-based experiments.
The dataset comprises EEG recordings from 8 subjects,
each viewing the same set of 960 images presented 40 times across multiple sessions.
Stimuli were displayed for 300 ms followed by a 300 ms blank screen, with data col-
lected using a 64-channel cap at a 1000 Hz sampling rate. By leveraging the visual
complexity and rich annotations of MSCOCO, AllJoined1 is well-suited for research
into neural decoding of naturalistic scenes. However, the dataset has not yet had
time to be utilized within literature, while its relatively small size limits its broader
adoption, posing challenges for generalizability and benchmarking. Despite these lim-
itations, AllJoined1 remains a valuable resource, particularly in its alignment with
MSCOCO’s focus on contextual understanding and complex scenes.

THINGS:
Containing around 26,000 images across 1,854 everyday object cate-
gories, the THINGS database is designed specifically for cognitive neuroscience re-
search. Usually used to study human perception, memory, and object categorization.
The dataset is annotated with rich metadata, including object names, typicality rat-
ings, and various perceptual features, making it a valuable resource for studies explor-
ing cognition and visual recognition. In contrast to MSCOCO, THINGS is limited
by being very object-forward, with images being cropped to put focus on the object,
while making the background minimally distracting. From the THINGS database,
two prominent EEG datasets have emerged, namely THINGS-EEG1 (2022) [28] and
THINGS-EEG2 (2022) [29].
Both datasets share key methodological similarities: they use a rapid serial visual
presentation (RSVP) paradigm to display a large number of images in quick succes-
sion, while EEG data was collected at a sampling rate of 1000 Hz using 64-electrode
caps. Despite these commonalities, the two datasets differ in their scale, experimental
protocols, and aims, which reflect contrasting trade-offs between dataset size, cogni-
tive engagement, and experimental structure. THINGS-EEG1 is the larger of the two
datasets, recording neural activity from 50 participants, each viewing 12 images of
each of the 1,854 concepts, while 200 unique images were selected as validation images.
Data was collected with a SOA of 100 ms, of which 50 ms corresponds to an image,
followed by 50 ms blank screen. Meanwhile, THINGS-EEG2 offers a more structured
approach to EEG data collection by splitting the 1,854 concepts into 1,654 training
concepts, with 10 images per concept, and 200 unique test concepts, enabling the
dataset to be used for zero-shot analyses. Each training image, is shown 4 times per
participant, while the test images are shown 80 times each, a decision which might
cause the later test EEG measurements to be influenced by prior familiarity with
the images [29]. THINGS-EEG2 doubles the SOA to 200 ms, while having a much
smaller subject-pool of 10 subjects.
2.2.3. Modeling Approaches and Performance
Previous work in Visual Stimulus Reconstruction can be broadly categorized into
three main architectural approaches: Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), Vari-
ational Autoencoders (VAEs) and Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs).
It should be noted that comparing performances across papers is a diﬀicult task within
Visual Stimulus Reconstruction. Early papers base their results on classification re-
sults and their Inception Scores, with some focusing solely on human qualitative
analyses - while later papers put more emphasis on generating visually similar images
as the target, basing their results on Cosine Similarity and the Structural Similarity
Index (SSIM). Meanwhile, papers utilize various datasets of incomparable class-size,
making accuracies misleading.

2 Background and Related Work
2.3. Zero-Shot Learning
Zero-Shot learning (ZSL) is a machine learning paradigm that enables models to gen-
eralize beyond predefined categories by recognizing or inferring unseen classes. Unlike
traditional supervised learning, which relies on extensive labeled datasets, ZSL trains
models to capture dense, information-rich embeddings that encode broader semantic
relationships [43]. By aligning inputs (e.g., images, texts) with high-level represen-
tations in a shared latent space, ZSL allows models to leverage the structure and
interconnectedness of data across different modalities [2]. Thus, rather than learning
direct mappings to existing classes, ZSL encourages models to develop a more holistic
view of the world. This broader understanding enables them to recombine and apply
elements from seen examples, thereby facilitating reasoning about novel data during
inference.
Key applications of zero-shot learning include zero-shot classification and retrieval.
In these tasks, the model encodes novel, unseen data and compares it to previously
unseen samples from another modality to identify the most similar labels or data
points within the shared latent space. This is made possible by the model’s holistic
understanding of the data, which allows it to generalize beyond the training exam-
ples. While, previously mentioned papers such as Li et al. [1] utilize techniques such
as Stable Diffusion to build on these principles, extending zero-shot learning from
retrieval tasks to more complex applications, such as zero-shot image generation.
2.3.1. CLIP: Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining
Perhaps the most influential approach to zero-shot learning is OpenAI’s CLIP (Con-
trastive Language–Image Pretraining) by Radford et al. (2021) [2], which exemplifies
the use of a shared latent space to align textual and visual data. CLIP is trained on
a large-scale dataset of image-text pairs, enabling it to associate images with descrip-
tive text and perform tasks such as zero-shot classification and image-text retrieval
without requiring task-specific fine-tuning.
The key to CLIP’s success lies in its contrastive loss function. This loss function is
designed to bring the embeddings of matching image-text pairs closer together in the
latent space while pushing apart the embeddings of mismatched pairs. During train-
ing, the model optimizes this objective by maximizing the cosine similarity between
correctly paired samples while minimizing it for incorrect pairs. The architecture
of CLIP consists of two separate uni-modal encoders: a vision encoder, based on
models like ResNet [44] or Vision Transformers [45], and a text encoder, based on a
transformer architecture. These encoders project their respective inputs into a shared
latent space, where the alignment of images and text takes place. By learning to en-
code both modalities into a unified representation, CLIP extends the capabilities of
zero-shot learning, demonstrating strong generalization across a wide variety of tasks.
This makes it an essential tool for both classification and retrieval applications, as
well as a foundational component for more advanced models like Stable Diffusion.

2.3.2. Stable Diffusion and CLIP Embeddings in Image Generation
Stable Diffusion [3] (2022) represents a groundbreaking advancement in text-to-image
synthesis, combining the generative power of diffusion models with the semantic rich-
ness of CLIP embeddings. By refining the image generation process through latent
diffusion models (LDMs), Stable Diffusion achieves a remarkable balance between
computational eﬀiciency and high-fidelity image synthesis, setting a new standard in
generative modeling.
At the heart of Stable Diffusion is the use of a latent space for generation. Instead
of operating directly in the high-dimensional pixel space, which is computationally
intensive, Stable Diffusion compresses images into a lower-dimensional latent represen-
tation using a pretrained autoencoder. This latent space captures essential semantic
and perceptual information while removing unnecessary pixel-level details, allowing
for eﬀicient training and inference. The denoising process is guided by cross-attention
mechanisms that condition the model on text inputs, ensuring the generated images
align with the provided prompts.
Crucially, Stable Diffusion employs CLIP embeddings of these textual inputs, to
guide the visual content. A pretrained text encoder, based on a CLIP transformer,
converts the input prompt into a sequence of high-dimensional embeddings that rep-
resent the semantic meaning of the text. These embeddings were originally trained
on the extensive LAION-400M dataset [46] (2021), enabling the model to interpret
and generalize across a wide variety of text-image pairs. During the diffusion process,
these text embeddings are integrated into the UNet backbone through multi-head
cross-attention layers, ensuring the generated images closely align with the intended
semantic context.
Over time, advancements in Stable Diffusion have further enhanced its capabili-
ties. Later versions, such as SDXL [47] (2023), utilize an even more powerful CLIP
ViT-H/14 encoder trained on the larger LAION-2B dataset, an english subset of
LAION-5B [48] (2022), improving the model’s generalization and ability to handle
complex, nuanced prompts. This iterative refinement has expanded the versatility of
Stable Diffusion, enabling it to excel in applications ranging from creative art gener-
ation to photorealistic imaging.
Unlike earlier generative approaches, which often relied on class-conditioned train-
ing or lacked robust semantic grounding, Stable Diffusion leverages CLIPs multi-
modal understanding to excel in zero-shot scenarios. The model can generate images
for prompts it has never encountered before, thanks to CLIPs ability to encode di-
verse, abstract textual descriptions into a shared latent space with visual data. This
capability dramatically expands the applicability of generative models, enabling ro-
bust generalization to novel tasks without requiring task-specific fine-tuning.

2.4. Leveraging CLIP for Image Reconstruction via Stable Diffusion
Building on the principles of zero-shot learning and the multimodal capabilities of
CLIP, Stable Diffusion can generate images not only from textual prompts but also
from visual inputs. Within CLIP’s shared latent space, textual descriptions and visual
representations encode the same underlying semantic information. This alignment
makes it possible to use image embeddings, derived from the CLIP image encoder in
SDXL, as direct prompts for Stable Diffusion’s generative process without requiring
additional adaptation.
To explore the effectiveness of this approach, images from a personal library were
encoded into CLIP embeddings using the image encoder integrated into SDXL. These
embeddings were then used to reconstruct the images through Stable Diffusion. Fig-
ure 2.2 (next page) showcases the results, comparing original images (top rows) to
their corresponding reconstructions (bottom rows). The dataset used for this evalu-
ation is entirely personal and independent, ensuring no overlap with the LAION-5B
dataset that this specific CLIP model was trained on. This avoids potential data
leakage and provides an unbiased evaluation of the model’s reconstruction capability.
The reconstructions demonstrate that Stable Diffusion effectively captures the
essential characteristics of the original images. Key attributes such as objects, com-
position, color, texture and object placement are faithfully preserved, with minor
variations in finer details, a testament to how densely packed the CLIP embeddings
are. One consistent observation is that left/right orientation appears to be a chal-
lenge for the model, as some reconstructions exhibit mirrored versions of the original.
Despite this, the overall fidelity highlights the strength of the CLIP embedding space
in preserving semantic and perceptual information across modalities.
This finding is particularly relevant for applications seeking to incorporate alter-
native data modalities, such as EEG signals, into the CLIP space. The ability of
Stable Diffusion to generate accurate outputs without fine-tuning suggests that align-
ing EEG-derived embeddings with CLIP representations could enable effective visual
reconstruction. By leveraging the pre-trained capabilities of Stable Diffusion, this
approach bypasses the computational and time-intensive requirements of model fine-
tuning, making it a highly eﬀicient solution for cross-modal generation tasks.
These results reinforce the versatility of Stable Diffusion in utilizing multimodal
embeddings. While traditionally applied to text-to-image synthesis, its capacity to
handle image-to-image tasks further highlights the robustness of the CLIP-guided
latent space. This demonstrates the potential for expanding Stable Diffusion’s ap-
plications to tasks such as EEG-based visual reconstruction and beyond, where em-
beddings from diverse modalities can guide the generative process, through accurate
alignment with the already existing CLIP space.

This chapter provides an in-depth analysis and exploration of the dataset that will be
used for investigating the cognitive visual interpretation within the setting of Visual
Stimulus Reconstruction. As described in the literature review (Section 2.2.2), the
THINGS database and its associated EEG datasets, THINGS-EEG1 and THINGS-
EEG2, offer a rich resource for studying the neural correlates of human visual percep-
tion. Among these, THINGS-EEG2 [29] is particularly well-suited for the objectives
of this study due to its unique design, which facilitates zero-shot analyses - helping to
investigate the brain’s ability to generalize beyond previously encountered categories,
a central focus of this thesis.
With a primary focus on the EEG signals across subjects, and the target Image
embeddings within the CLIP space, this chapter will additionally detail the EEG
preprocessing steps.
3.1. The THINGS-EEG2 dataset
The provided dataset comprises EEG recordings from 10 healthy adult participants
(8 female, 2 male) with a mean age of 28.5 ± 4.0 years. All participants had normal
or corrected-to-normal vision and provided informed consent prior to the experiment.
Each participant underwent four experimental sessions, resulting in a total of 82,160
image trials per subject. As reported by the authors, this extensive sampling ensures
a high signal-to-noise ratio and allows for robust modeling of neural responses to vi-
sual stimuli [29].
During each session, participants viewed images from the THINGS database pre-
sented using a Rapid Serial Visual Presentation (RSVP) paradigm.
Images were
displayed centrally on a screen for 100 ms, followed by a 100 ms blank screen, result-
ing in a 200 ms Stimulus Onset Asynchrony (SOA). To maintain fixation, a bull’s
eye target was present at the center of the screen throughout the experiment, with
participants being instructed to keep their sight fixated on it. The experiment was
structured to include 16,540 training trials (1,654 object concepts, 10 images per con-
cept) and 200 test trials (1 image per zero-shot concept). Test images were repeated
80 times per participant, while each training image was shown 4 times. To ensure
the participants attention to the stimuli, the participants were instructed to press a
button when presented a specific image, which appeared occasionally throughout the
experiments.

3.2. Preprocessing
The preprocessing of the EEG data is directly borrowed from Li et al. [1], involving
multiple sequential steps to ensure data quality, reduce noise, and prepare the data
for downstream modeling and interpretation. The steps include channel selection,
epoching, baseline correction, frequency downsampling, multivariate noise normal-
ization (MVNN) and proper data sorting.
All preprocessing is handled using the
MNE-library in python - one subject at a time.
In order to retain full spatial resolution of the signal, all 63 EEG channels were
loaded into MNE. The continuous EEG signal was then segmented into epochs corre-
sponding to the full 200 ms between visual stimuli, by utilizing the stimulus channel
(64th channel). The epochs were then extracted over a time window of [−0.2, 1.0]
seconds, encompassing one pre-target stimulus and 4 subsequent post-target stimu-
lus periods. This step ensures that the entire neural response to a visual stimulus is
captured, as critical features may persist even after subsequent stimuli, as discussed
earlier and previously attributed to [1]. Baseline correction was performed by sub-
tracting the average pre-stimulus activity (from −0.2 to 0 seconds) from each epoch
- before removing the pre-stimulus period from the signal. This step helps eliminate
slow drifts of the EEG signal, typically caused by the skin [49], and other offsets in
the signal, providing a normalized reference point for the neural responses.
To reduce data dimensionality and computational load, the EEG signal was down-
sampled to a frequency of 250 Hz. This downsampling rate avoids aliasing wrt. the
Nyquist theorem [50], while retaining the most essential brain frequency bands; delta
(1–3 Hz), theta (4–7 Hz), alpha (8–12 Hz), beta (13–30 Hz) and gamma (30–100 Hz),
while optimizing processing eﬀiciency.
Multivariate Noise Normalization (MVNN)
was applied to enhance the signal-to-noise ratio of the EEG data. The covariance ma-
trices were computed per epoch, then averaged across image repetitions, before their
inverse square roots were used to whiten the data. Thus reducing noise correlations
between EEG channels, and standardizing the data for subsequent analysis.
The EEG epochs were then sorted according to images, and reshaped into a stan-
dardized format: Images × EEG repetitions × EEG channels × EEG time points.
Ensuring that data from different sessions are aligned and comparable. The prepro-
cessed data from all sessions was merged into the test and training partitions. By
default, a maximum of 20 repetitions per image condition was used for test data,
while 2 repetitions were used for training data. EEG repetitions were shuffled across
sessions to prevent session-specific biases. Finally, the data was saved in a structured
format, ready for downstream analyses. With 63 channels, and 1000 ms of data at a
250Hz sampling rate, a single preprocessed datapoint takes the form: 63 × 250.
Figure 3.1 illustrates the effect of preprocessing on a single EEG datapoint. The raw
signal shows noise and large amplitude fluctuations, reduced after preprocessing.

Methods
This section provides a detailed explanation of the Visual Stimulus Reconstruction
pipeline used within this thesis, adapted from the approach by [1], as described in
Section 2.2.3. Unlike the broader discussions of existing work provided earlier, this
section focuses on the specific implementation and experimental design employed in
this study. It begins with a detailed overview of the Visual Stimulus Reconstruction
pipeline, including in-depth descriptions of its architecture and model components.
Finally, the experiments carried out to validate the pipeline, along with the evalu-
ation metrics used to assess semantic alignment and low-level visual similarity are
explained.
While the core codebase was borrowed from the implementation by [1], signifi-
cant modifications were made to restructure, debug, and ensure functionality of the
pipeline.
The primary contributions of this thesis lie in the analysis and results,
where novel plots, evaluation metrics, and visualizations were developed to better
understand and assess the aligned embeddings and their reconstructed images.
4.1. Visual Stimulus Reconstruction Pipeline
The pipeline proposed by Li et al. [1] is comprised of two stages with separate, but
overlapping goals. The first stage aims to achieve a broad semantic alignment be-
tween the EEG embedding space and the CLIP embedding space. To this end, it
employs an α-weighted loss function that combines a CLIP loss and a Mean Squared
Error (MSE) loss. The CLIP loss is the primary driver in aligning the EEG embed-
dings with the high-level semantic representations of the target CLIP embeddings,
ensuring that the two spaces are conceptually compatible. While the MSE loss serves
as a complementary component, guiding the embeddings to more precisely resemble
the target embeddings - crucial for accurate image reconstruction.
In contrast, the second stage focuses exclusively on fine-tuning the embeddings for
precision. Here, only the MSE loss is used, as the goal is no longer broad alignment but
achieving highly accurate, one-to-one correspondence between the EEG and image
embeddings.

4 Methods
4.1.1. Stage 1: Adaptive Thinking Mapper (ATM)
The first stage of the Visual Stimulus Reconstruction pipeline employs the Adaptive
Thinking Mapper (ATM), as dubbed by the original authors of [1]. The ATM is
designed to map raw EEG signals into semantically meaningful embeddings within
the CLIP space, using an α-weighted loss function - combining a contrastive CLIP
loss, with a mean squared error (MSE) loss to ensure semantic alignment within the
space and accurate embeddings, respectively.
The main purpose of Stage 1 is an
alignment to the CLIP space, in which the authors found α = 0.9 to work best, while
also validated as suitable through pilot experiments conducted for this study (Ap-

ms sampled at 250 Hz). Additionally, subject-specific information is provided as a

an embedding layer, a Temporal-Spatial convolution module, and a MLP projector -
shown in Figure 4.1 and explained in detail below.

4.1.2. Stage 2: Diffusion U-Net
The second stage of the Visual Stimulus Reconstruction pipeline involves a Diffusion
U-Net, which refines the embeddings generated by Stage 1 to closely match the target
CLIP embeddings. Built on the principles of diffusion probabilistic models, this net-
work employs a hierarchical encoder-decoder U-Net structure with skip connections,
allowing for eﬀicient and detailed denoising of noisy target embeddings.
Overall Architecture:
The Diffusion U-Net follows a classic encoder-decoder de-
sign, with noisy embeddings zt ∈R1024 as the input. The encoder progressively com-
presses the input through a series of fully connected layers with hidden dimensions,
[1024, 512, 256, 64]. Each encoder layer performs a sequence of linear transformations,
normalization, activation (SiLU), and dropout. The compressed representations are
then passed to the decoder, which reverses the process to reconstruct the target embed-
dings by expanding the dimensionality back to the original dimension, [64, 256, 512,
1024]. Skip connections between corresponding encoder and decoder layers ensure
that fine-grained information is retained throughout the process. This hierarchical
design enables the model to capture both high-level structure and intricate details
necessary for precise embedding refinement.
Handling Time-Step and Conditional EEG Inputs:
To integrate temporal
and contextual information, the model leverages time-step and conditional EEG em-
beddings at each stage of the encoder and decoder. The scalar timestep, t, sampled
during training for each instance, is encoded into a high-dimensional representation
through a sinusoidal positional embedding followed by a linear projection, produc-
ing an embedding of size 512. Similarly, the conditional EEG embeddings, i.e. the
embeddings produced within Stage 1, are linearly projected to match the hidden
dimensions of each of the layers within both the encoder and decoder, ensuring com-
patibility with the encoder and decoder layers. These embeddings are added to the

4.2. Main Experiments and their Metrics
The experiments designed to evaluate the Visual Stimulus Reconstruction pipeline
focus on two central tasks: Zero-Shot Image Retrieval and Zero-Shot Image Recon-
struction. These tasks assess the effectiveness of embedding alignment across both
stages of the pipeline. Stage 1 aims to achieve a broad semantic alignment between
EEG signals and the CLIP embedding space, creating embeddings that capture the
general semantic structure of the input signals. Stage 2 builds on this foundation, re-
fining these embeddings to achieve precise one-to-one correspondence with the target
embeddings, facilitating accurate image reconstructions through SDXL. Furthermore,
evaluations are conducted at both the inter- and intra-subject levels, examining how
consistently the pipeline performs across individuals and within the same subject,
providing a comprehensive understanding of the alignment’s robustness.
4.2.1. Stage 1: Zero-Shot Image Retrieval
The first task, Zero-Shot Image Retrieval, evaluates the embeddings produced by
Stage 1 by testing their alignment with the zero-shot image embeddings in the CLIP
space. By leveraging cosine similarity, the model identifies the image embeddings
most similar to the trained EEG embeddings. This enables both image retrieval and
category classification, providing insights into the semantic structure of the aligned
space. Importantly, inter- and intra-subject evaluations are performed to assess how
well the alignment generalizes across subjects and individual trials.
To interpret the results, image labels have been grouped into categories using the
WordNet hierarchy, as detailed in subsection 3.3.2. These categories provide a means
of assessing the degree of semantic alignment achieved by Stage 1. Success at the
==PAGE:40==
28
4 Methods
category level indicates that the embeddings capture meaningful semantic groupings,
while success at the image level demonstrates a more refined alignment capable of dis-
tinguishing individual stimuli. Both levels of evaluation are crucial for understanding
the extent of semantic alignment and its practical implications for downstream tasks.
Performance is evaluated using a range of metrics.
Accuracy is measured for
both image- and category-level classification, with top-5 accuracy providing an addi-
tional measure of retrieval robustness. Subsets of the zero-shot test CLIP embeddings
are used for K-way accuracy calculations, simulating more constrained classification
scenarios. The analysis is further broken down by category to identify patterns of
strength or weakness across semantic groups. Confusion matrices and heatmaps are
generated to visualize these trends, offering a detailed perspective on retrieval perfor-
mance.
4.2.2. Stage 2: Zero-Shot Image Reconstruction
The second task, Zero-Shot Image Reconstruction, evaluates the refined embeddings
produced by Stage 2. These embeddings are used to generate images through a pre-
trained SDXL model, with the goal of producing visually coherent and semantically
accurate reconstructions of the original stimuli. The Stage 2 fine-tuning focuses on
minimizing the Mean Squared Error (MSE) between the target and aligned embed-
dings, ensuring a closer match for reconstruction. Inter- and intra-subject evaluations
are performed here as well, examining whether the fine-tuning process can reliably
adapt to variations in input across different individuals and trials.
The evaluation of the reconstructed images is conducted on two levels. At the em-
bedding level, cosine similarity and MSE are used to assess the degree to which the
Stage 2 embeddings align with their corresponding target embeddings. These metrics
provide insight into the semantic and numerical accuracy of the aligned embeddings
and their consistency across subjects.
At the image level, reconstructed images are evaluated for their similarity to the
original stimuli. Average color channel correlation and local binary pattern (LBP)
correlation are used to quantify similarities in color- and texture-distribution, respec-
tively - plots showing such distributions for example-images for both color and LBP
can be found within Appendix A.2.2. Additionally, the images are subject to visual
inspection to qualitatively assess their fidelity and semantic relevance.
Together, these tasks highlight the progression of alignment achieved across the
two stages. Stage 1 focuses on embedding the EEG signals into a semantically mean-
ingful space, while Stage 2 refines these embeddings to enable accurate reconstructions
of the original images. The inclusion of inter- and intra-subject evaluations ensures
that the findings provide a robust understanding of the pipeline’s performance and
its adaptability to individual variability.

5.2. Stage 1: Discussion
The primary goal of Stage 1 was to align the EEG embedding space with the se-
mantically meaningful CLIP space, forming the foundation for reconstructing visual
stimuli in Stage 2, as the EEG embeddings will guide the utilized diffusion process.
By leveraging the rich structure of CLIP, Stage 1 seeks to create a semantically orga-
nized embedding space that can effectively encode high-level information about the
stimuli.
Importantly, this alignment requires balancing broad semantic alignment
with embedding precision, which is why a combination of CLIP and MSE losses is
employed. Zero-shot retrieval serves as an intermediate metric to assess how well
the EEG embeddings align with this space, providing insights into the structure and
functionality of the embeddings before progressing to fine-tuning in Stage 2.
The PCA results provide a preliminary visualization of the alignment achieved
in Stage 1. Figure 5.1 shows that the EEG embeddings of all subjects are tightly
clustered in the lower-left corner of the PCA space trained on the target CLIP em-
beddings. While this clustering suggests consistency across subjects, it also reflects
the limited alignment breadth, as the embeddings fail to span the full variance of
the target space. The first two principal components explain only 7.33% of the vari-
ance, which constrains the interpretability of these results and highlights the need for
quantitative metrics like retrieval accuracy to gain deeper insights. Subject-specific
PCA plots (Figure 5.2) reveal similar limitations, with stacked categories rather than
distinct clusters as seen in the original CLIP space (Fig. 3.6) - further indicating that
category-specific semantic distinctions are not fully captured. However, the overlap
between train and test points in Figure 5.3 is a positive outcome, emphasizing that
the model generalizes well to zero-shot test embeddings without overfitting to the
training set.
The retrieval accuracy metrics provide a quantitative measure of the alignment
quality and are particularly valuable in comparing these results to prior work. The
model achieves an average zero-shot retrieval accuracy of 19.7%, as indicated by Fig-
ure 5.4 and Table 5.1, far exceeding the random baseline of 0.5%. However, subject
variability is notable, with subject 8 performing best at 28.5% accuracy, closely match-
ing the results reported by Li et al. [1] in both accuracy (28.64%) and across the re-
maining metrics. Notably, [1] reports only the best-performing subject in their paper,
omitting details about variability across other subjects. This omission is critical, as
the variability observed in this study (e.g., subject 2 performing at 12.5%) highlights
significant differences in alignment quality across individuals. Such variability could
stem from differences in EEG signal quality, individual cognitive processes, or even
subtle inconsistencies in how subjects respond to stimuli. Reporting only the best
subject obscures these nuances, which are crucial for understanding the robustness
of the alignment process. Further analysis of the Top-5, 2-Way, 4-Way, and 10-Way
accuracies (Table 5.1) reveals additional patterns. Subject 8 performs best in accu-
racy, Top-5, and 10-Way metrics, while subject 10 performs best in 2-Way and 4-Way

5.3. Stage 2: Results - Accurate Zero-Shot Reconstruction
We now shift our focus to the results of the second stage of the proposed Visual
Stimulus Reconstruction pipeline. The primary objective of Stage 2 is to transition
from Zero-Shot Retrieval to Zero-Shot Image Reconstruction, refining the embeddings
to enable precise image generation through Stable Diffusion. To achieve this, Stage
2 replaces the combined CLIP and MSE loss of Stage 1 with an exclusive focus on
the MSE loss - though it is also a necessity for the Diffusion UNet. This change
prioritizes precision over the stability of the embedding space, optimizing the EEG
embeddings for one-to-one correspondence with their target CLIP embeddings. As
a result, the performance of zero-shot retrieval is expected to degrade due to the
tradeoff in embedding stability.
The results presented in this section evaluate the pipeline through both quantita-
tive metrics, aiming to assess the alignment and precision of both embeddings and
generated images, as well as qualitative analyses of the generated images. Other re-
sults, such as retrieval scores and confusion matrices, are less relevant in this stage
due to the shift in focus, but are still included for reference in Appendix A.3.3.

5.4. Stage 2: Discussion
The transition from Stage 1 to Stage 2 represents a pivot in objective within the
proposed pipeline, shifting the focus from broad semantic alignment to precise recon-
struction of visual stimuli. Stage 1 established a foundation by aligning the EEG
embedding space with the semantically rich CLIP embeddings, facilitating meaning-
ful zero-shot retrieval.
However, Stage 2 intentionally shifted priorities, replacing
the combined CLIP and MSE loss with an exclusive focus on MSE. This adjustment
aimed to refine embeddings for precise one-to-one correspondence with their target
CLIP embeddings, enabling accurate image reconstruction through Stable Diffusion.
The inherent trade-off, sacrificing the broader stability of semantic alignment for finer
precision, permeates the results, revealing both the promise and complexity of this
approach.
Taking a starting point within the PCA plots (Figures 5.10 and 5.11), the evolving
dynamic of the embedding space across stages is illustrated. In Stage 1, embeddings
were tightly clustered within a constrained region of the CLIP space, suggesting con-
sistent alignment across subjects, but limited variance within the space. In contrast,

5.5. Overall Discussion
This thesis evaluates a two-stage pipeline for reconstructing visual stimuli from EEG
signals, focusing on embedding alignment in Stage 1 and precise reconstruction in
Stage 2. The results highlight both strengths and limitations of the approach, em-
phasizing the challenges of balancing semantic alignment and embedding precision.
In Stage 1, the alignment of EEG embeddings with CLIP embeddings demon-
strated the feasibility of mapping neural signals into semantically rich visual spaces.
Metrics such as retrieval accuracy and PCA visualizations indicated that the aligned
space retains meaningful semantic structure.
However, variability across subjects
and categories underscores the challenges in creating a robust and consistent align-
ment. Certain categories, such as Animals and Foods, exhibited strong performance,
suggesting that well-defined semantic boundaries facilitate alignment.
Conversely,
overlapping or sparsely represented categories, such as Fruits and People and Body
Parts, presented diﬀiculties, often resulting in misclassifications or reduced alignment
quality. These results highlight the need for improved techniques to handle variability,
whether arising from the inherent properties of EEG signals, subject-specific neural
encoding, or stimulus complexity.
Stage 2 shifted the optimization objective from broad alignment to precise re-
construction, focusing exclusively on MSE loss. While this approach successfully en-
hanced embedding precision, the results reveal a trade-off: semantic coherence often

diminished as embeddings became more aligned in terms of MSE. High-performing
reconstructions captured fine-grained details but lacked clarity in semantic context,
whereas low-MSE reconstructions sometimes sacrificed both semantic and visual fi-

that fine-tuning mitigated some individual differences at the cost of broader alignment.
The persistence of variability in reconstruction performance across categories further
illustrates the nuanced challenges of refining embeddings to balance semantic richness

The interplay between semantic alignment and embedding precision is a recurring
theme throughout the results. Metrics such as Cosine Similarity and MSE reveal com-

Zero-shot generalization emerged as a key strength of the pipeline, particularly in

ability to generalize across diverse stimuli.
Overall, the results demonstrate the potential of this two-stage approach while

stimulus-specific variability, underscore the complexity of this task.

5.6. Future Work
Building on the findings of this thesis, future research can address critical challenges in
balancing semantic alignment and embedding precision to enhance visual stimulus re-
construction pipelines. In Stage 1, the dual-loss approach combining CLIP and MSE
losses demonstrated effectiveness for initial alignment. However, Stage 2 relies on a
diffusion U-Net architecture that is inherently constrained by its exclusive use of MSE
loss for noise prediction. This highlights the potential to explore refinements that do
not depend solely on generative architectures. For instance, focusing on improving
the alignment achieved in Stage 1 through extended training, advanced regulariza-
tion techniques, or additional intermediate steps to fine-tune the embeddings could
mitigate the trade-offs observed in semantic coherence and local precision. Such ap-
proaches may even allow for reconstruction pipelines that rely exclusively on Stage 1,
bypassing the need for subsequent generative modeling altogether.
Given the strong reliance on CLIP embeddings, future work should also investi-
gate improved strategies for aligning EEG signals with the CLIP space. The pipeline’s
ability to generalize to zero-shot stimuli is a promising strength, but the uneven per-
formance across categories suggests that further refinements are needed. For example,
expanding datasets to include a broader diversity of stimuli or employing augmenta-
tion techniques could enhance the robustness of alignment and retrieval processes.
Additionally, understanding and addressing structural biases within the CLIP space
- such as the embeddings acting as ”hubs” or category overlaps - may help mitigate
misalignments and improve zero-shot performance for underrepresented or semanti-
cally ambiguous categories.
Lastly, the substantial variability across subjects and stimuli highlights an oppor-
tunity to develop methods that dynamically adjust to individual differences. While
the models are already subject-specific, further tailoring of the alignment process,
such as through adaptive loss weighting or subject-specific regularization techniques,
could help account for variability in EEG signal quality or cognitive encoding strate-
gies. These refinements would not only improve performance consistency but also
increase the robustness of the pipeline in real-world applications.
By addressing these challenges and extending the current framework, future work
can advance the development of visual stimulus reconstruction pipelines that are more
precise, robust, and capable of translating neural signals into coherent and accurately
reconstructed visual representations.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated a two-stage pipeline for reconstructing visual stimuli from
EEG signals, grounded in state-of-the-art models such as CLIP and Stable Diffusion.
Through careful evaluation and extension of the analyses outlined by Li et al. [1],
this work not only reproduced key findings but also provided new insights into the
alignment of EEG signals with multi-modal embedding spaces and the reconstruction
of visual stimuli.
In Stage 1, the alignment of EEG embeddings with the semantically rich CLIP
space demonstrated results on par with the best subject reported in [1] for zero-shot
retrieval. However, this thesis went beyond the original study by analyzing inter- and
intra-subject variability, revealing critical nuances in alignment performance across
individuals. While the original work presented results solely for the top-performing
subject, this thesis underlined the significant variability between subjects, highlighting
factors such as EEG signal fidelity, cognitive processes, and individual neural encod-
ing that influence alignment quality. Additionally, this thesis applied the WordNet
hierarchy to group the original labels into categories, enabling a deeper exploration
of the semantic alignment within the embedding space. This categorization allowed
for more structured evaluations, revealing patterns of alignment that were previously
unexamined, particularly in category-level distinctions where semantically distinct
groups like Animals and Foods excelled, while overlapping groups like Fruits strug-
gled.
Stage 2 aimed to translate these aligned embeddings into visual reconstructions, by
further fine-tuning the Stage 1 embeddings via a diffusion-based architecture. While
image reconstructions did not achieve pixel-level fidelity, all subjects were able to pro-
duce reconstructions that were semantically coherent in terms of the general category
of the visual stimuli. High Cosine Similarity reconstructions, in particular, captured
broad semantic categories, with notable consistency across subjects for specific items
like jellybeans and categories such as animals. These reconstructions, while lacking
fine-grained detail, often preserved semantic context, demonstrating the model’s abil-
ity to translate neural signals into meaningful visual approximations. This outcome
represents an encouraging step toward combining neural signals and visually gener-
ated representations, with the potential for further refinement in future work.
The analysis of Stage 2 also highlighted inherent limitations of diffusion models
as an alignment method, particularly their reliance on MSE loss for noise prediction.

6 Conclusion
While MSE fine-tuning enabled the model to capture subtle visual features, it often
came at the expense of semantic coherence. The study identified a core trade-off
between embedding precision and semantic alignment, underscoring the challenges
of balancing these objectives in generative architectures. However, the results also
suggested that further refinement of embeddings within Stage 1, without transition-
ing to generative models, or the incorporation of additional fine-tuning steps, could
mitigate these trade-offs and enhance both alignment and reconstruction.
Through this investigation, the thesis advanced the understanding of multi-modal
pipelines for visual stimulus reconstruction by evaluating the relationships between
alignment, variability, and reconstruction quality. By reproducing and extending the
findings of [1], it provided a more comprehensive view of the variability across sub-
jects and categories, while offering new methodologies to interpret alignment in the
CLIP space.
In conclusion, while challenges remain in achieving precise and semantically co-
herent reconstructions, this work highlights the potential of EEG-based pipelines to
approximate visual stimuli.
By addressing limitations such as subject variability,
embedding refinement, and the trade-offs inherent to current architectures, future re-
search can build on these findings to develop more robust and accurate reconstruction
pipelines, paving the way for deeper integration of neural signals and computational
models.

A.3.4. Category wise reconstructions
To complement the quantitative evaluations, this section provides a qualitative anal-
ysis of the reconstructed images, highlighting the model’s performance across individ-
ual categories. Figures A.19–A.31 contain 14 plots, each corresponding to one of the
object categories observed during data collection. These plots offer a detailed view of
the reconstruction quality and how it varies across subjects.
Each plot contains five rows of randomly sampled images from the respective cate-
gory (fewer if the category contains fewer images). The first column shows the original
target stimuli viewed by the subjects during data collection, serving as the ground
truth. The subsequent 10 columns display the reconstructed images for each subject,
allowing for direct visual comparison of reconstruction fidelity across individuals.
In the right-most column of each plot, four bars per row provide a summary of
key quantitative metrics for the reconstruction of the target image. These metrics
include average scores for color, LBP, Cosine Similarity and MSE, represented in their
respective colors as described earlier. The bars show the mean across all subjects,
with standard deviations, worst-performing, and best-performing subjects indicated
by colored dots.
This combination of qualitative and quantitative measures offers an in-depth per-
spective on the reconstruction performance, revealing not only how well the recon-
structed images align with the target stimuli but also the variability across subjects
and categories.
