Abstract
In this Master’s thesis, the role of context in deep learning for image analysis was investigated
by employing an experimental approach. Firstly, the overall research topic is motivated by
issues of data hungriness in deep learning and the potential negative effects of spurious cor-
relations in the backgrounds (contexts) in images on deep learning approaches. Additionally,
deep learning models may generalize poorly when trained on data without context, as ex-
emplified by the BugNIST 3D detection task. To address this, the context free to in context
domain adaptation task was formalized, and a simple data augmentation approach to solve
it was proposed. This approach involves adding random Perlin noise around the object(s) of
interest in order to improve robustness to changes in the context. Notably, it was found that
applying this approach with a deep learning model for the BugNIST detection task greatly
improved detection performance in mixtures of bugs compared to the reference results. Be-
yond addressing the domain adaptation task, I propose the object score as a measure of
the degree to which objects of interest are used for prediction in deep learning classification
models. The object score was successfully used in multiple scenarios in order to quantify
model behaviour. Furthermore, I propose that promoting invariance to changes in the con-
text could lead to improved models by reducing the potential negative effects of context in
images. Motivated by this, the semantic alignment loss was developed, based on existing
methods for domain adaptation. It was found, that while the results were promising, the
effect of this method was statistically inconclusive. Finally, the effect of context in images on
model performance was quantified by training models on context free and in context images
respectively, as well as by measuring the effect of spurious correlations in the data. It was
concluded that context generally had a negative effect on model performance, and that the
models were highly susceptible to spurious correlations in the data.

Introduction
Deep learning (DL) models for image analysis have the capability of solving exceedingly
complex vision tasks. Progress in research, computing, and availability of data have lead
to great advances in the technology and an ever increasing amount of use cases. A key
advantage of DL models is that they are able to learn complex patterns from large amounts
of data. However, this advantage is directly linked to data hungriness, which is one of the
greatest drawbacks of DL [Marcus, 2018]. That is, DL models need large amounts of train-
ing data to succeed. Furthermore, the large learning capacity of DL models means that
they are very likely to learn spurious correlations between the input and output, especially
when the amount of data is low [Marcus, 2018][Lapuschkin et al., 2019]. Context in images
can contain such confounders, as the context represents the space of possible backgrounds,
settings, and conditions that the subject of the image is found in. In an extreme case, mod-
elling the natural variation in the context could require both larger amounts of data and a
more powerful model than if there was no context in the images. These observations leads
to the following avenues of inquiry that will later be formulated as research questions: Can
the influence of context on DL models be quantified? Furthermore, can information of the
context be leveraged to improve the outcome of DL models? Finally, can DL models trained
on images without context be successfully adapted for prediction on images with context?
In this thesis, the relationship between the context in images and the behaviour of DL
models will be investigated. In the following, a theoretical motivation of the problem(s) that
will be addressed in this thesis will be provided (section 1.1). Then, the research questions
and general approach of the thesis will be presented in (section 1.2). On a practical note,
please refer to section 6.1 and section 6.2 for an overview of the abbreviations and notation
introduced in the thesis,
1.1
Motivation
In this section, DL models for image analysis will be introduced, and the focus of the thesis
will be presented and motivated. This is followed by an introduction to related work on the
subject of context in images.
Convolutional neural networks (CNN) are central to many modern DL architectures for image
analysis, and variations of this architecture are widely applied for most image analysis tasks,

including classification, detection, and segmentation [Lecun et al., 1998][Liu et al., 2022b].
In recent years, improvements to model training schemes, data augmentation, and the archi-
tecture itself has lead to many advances with "ConvNeXt V2" being the current state of the
art (SOTA) CNN architecture for image classification [Liu et al., 2022b][Woo et al., 2023].
A neural network (NN) architecture competing with CNNs is the vision transformer (ViT),
which sacrifices computational efficiency compared to CNNs to achieve SOTA performance
on large scale datasets or with large scale pretraining [Dosovitskiy et al., 2021]. Furthermore,
CNNs have several inductive biases that ViTs avoid [Liu et al., 2022b][Dosovitskiy et al.,
2021]. Despite the potential shortcomings of CNNs compared to ViTs, the main focus in this
work will be on CNNs. This is motivated by the properties of CNNs, which I will expand
upon in the following, as well as their widespread adoption.
The inductive biases of CNNs include translation invariance from weight sharing in the
spatial dimensions and hierarchical features from increasing the number of channels with
the network depth (multichanneling) [Wang and Wu, 2023]. These properties make CNNs
specifically suited for image analysis, where e.g. translation invariance can be beneficial for
detection and segmentation. Furthermore, the weight sharing property of CNNs make them
highly efficient and well suited for smaller datasets and real time applications. In 3D image
analysis, CNNs are often more practical than ViTs, since large scale datasets are currently
scarcer and harder to obtain than for 2D image analysis. Finally, it can be reasoned that
in order for a given model to achieve e.g. a higher classification accuracy, the model must
learn to ignore confounders. In large scale datasets, the potential issues of context in images
may therefore be negligible. Based of these factors, this thesis will mainly focus on CNNs
trained on small to mid scale datasets.
In general, CNNs encode an input image into an embedding space.
These embeddings
are commonly referred to as features and are dependent on a certain localized window of the
input image. The size of this window is given by the receptive field of the CNN, which is
typically more than the entire image, although the effective receptive field may be smaller
than the window [Luo et al., 2016]. The features are then decoded into a prediction (segmen-
tation/detection/classification). In the case of classification, the decoding step is typically
performed using a feed forward neural network (FFN). Thus, information from the entire
image is used to generate a prediction. That is, the receptive field of the output classification
is effectively the entire image, irrespective of CNN architecture. This means that both the
object(s) of interest (OI) and the context are used to generate a prediction. The pixels of
the input image leading to a given prediction can be analyzed using various methods such as
saliency mapping / LRP and Grad-CAM [Simonyan et al., 2014][Selvaraju et al., 2019][Bach
et al., 2015]. Crucially, these methods can reveal what information in the input image lead
to the prediction, e.g. whether it was the context or the OI. Methods such as these may
reveal that the model is ill fitted and has learned to generate predictions from spurious cor-
relations (confounders) in the context such as watermarks [Lapuschkin et al., 2019]. Since
there is currently no way to explicitly control what a given model focuses on beyond the
training data itself, the typical solution is to gather more data in diverse settings and hope
that the model learns to distinguish the OI rather than the contexts. In 3D imaging, this

1.1.1
Related Work
Information about the context in images can potentially improve DL models. For instance, in
a study on improving CNNs for leaf disease classification, the performance of the investigated
models were significantly improved by removing the context (foreground/background) of the
images [KC et al., 2021]. In another study concerning the effect of background removal
for classification and segmentation performance on fashion data with CNNs and ViTs, a
significant improvement was observed for most models, especially shallow ones, when training
and evaluating on images with the background removed [Liang et al., 2023]. However, the
success of these approaches depended on the fact that the context could be successfully
removed from the images for both training and inference. This may not always be feasible
as filtering out the background may be as hard as the given image analysis task itself. In
order to circumvent this, viewing the context free (CF) images and in context (IC) images as
different domains, a model could be trained on the CF domain and adapted to the IC domain.
This could have several benefits, the first being improved performance by eliminating noise
and confounders from the the training data [KC et al., 2021]. Secondly, this would allow
the OI to be captured independently, making training data easier to obtain and label. This
problem is an instance of domain adaptation (DA) for covariate shift, where covariate refers
to the input. The CF to IC DA problem will be referred to as CF-IC for brevity. However,
CF-IC can be said to be a special case of DA, since the OI in the input does change between
domains. The BugNIST volumetric (3D) detection dataset is an instance of CF-IC, where
bugs are captured individually in the training data and appear in mixtures with other bugs
for inference [Jensen et al., 2024]. Motivated by this, the CF-IC problem will be investigated
using BugNIST and a novel 2D CF-IC classification dataset.
Contextual information has been shown to improve object detection models by leveraging the
context around the OI [Mottaghi et al., 2014][Shivdeo et al., 2021]. For other problems, the
contextual information may worsen models in the case of spurious correlations in the context
[Lapuschkin et al., 2019].
Post hoc methods to identify confounders in the context and
unlearn unwanted model behaviour have been developed on the basis of Spectral Relevance
Analysis (SpRAy) [Lapuschkin et al., 2019][Anders et al., 2020]. The main idea of SpRAy is
to first obtain model explanations in the form of the relevance of pixels wrt. the output and
then cluster the explanations to investigate model behaviour and identify potential outliers
[Lapuschkin et al., 2019]. Confounders such as watermarks and other artifacts may then be
identified from the outliers, which can then be used to unlearn spurious correlations [Anders
et al., 2020]. However, the context may carry information that can be exploited beyond
image artifacts, and can therefore not be easily removed or identified. Motivated by these
observations, I will provide an investigation of how context impacts model performance, and
of whether steps can be taken to minimize the potential negative effects of context.

1.2. Approach
1.2
Approach
The main research question that will be addressed in this work is stated in the following:
Can a deep learning model trained on images of out-of-context objects be successfully
applied for detection/segmentation/classification on in-context objects?
My first step towards addressing this research question will be to formalize and explore
the aforementioned CF-IC DA task. Then, I will find and/or create datasets that enable
experiments with this task. In order to measure whether a given method to apply a DL
model trained on CF images to IC images was successful, I plan to measure the impact
of context on performance. In particular, I define that a successful CF-IC model will not
experience a significant drop in performance when applied to CF images compared to IC
images. This can be tested by constructing a dataset where the context in each image can
be added/removed. The primary suggested method to address the CF-IC problem will be
a data augmentation technique that adds random contexts (backgrounds) around the OI.
Furthermore, I will present and apply tools to investigate model behaviour including saliency
mapping and the object score.
Beyond quantifying the impact of context on models and addressing the CF-IC problem,
I will investigate whether context can be leveraged to improve the performance of models
in general.
To this end, I will introduce the semantic alignment loss function, inspired
by existing methods for DA, that aims to promote invariance to changes in the context.
This suggested method will be tested through experiments to investigate its effect on model
performance and whether it alleviates problems such as low amounts of data and confounders
in the context.

Data
In this chapter, the two main datasets used for experiments in this thesis are presented. The
datasets consist of 2D and 3D images respectively. Furthermore, each image in the datasets
has a corresponding class label and a segmentation mask for the object(s) of interest. In
the following sections, the preparation procedure, origin, and summary statistics for each
dataset will be presented.
2.1
Husky/Wolf 2D Dataset
The ImageNet classification dataset consists of 1.3 million natural images with 1000 different
classes [Deng et al., 2009]. Two of the 1000 classes are "Siberian Husky" and "Grey Wolf".
These animals are similar in appearance, as they have similar snouts, stature, ears, etc. How-
ever, the context in the image can provide strong clues towards either class. For instance,
huskies are more likely to be in urban environments and to appear together with humans,
and wolves are more likely to appear behind a fence or in a nature setting. These charac-
teristics make the Siberian husky versus wolf classification problem (HW problem/dataset)
an interesting case study of how deep learning models potentially leverage the context in
images when generating predictions. Therefore, for the purposes of this project, a small
binary classification dataset for these classes was derived from the ImageNet dataset with
segmentation masks for the objects of interest in the images.
2.1.1
Object of Interest Segmentation
The objects of interest in the HW problem, e.g. the huskies and wolves, were efficiently
segmented using the "segment anything model" (SAM) [Kirillov et al., 2023]. To ensure
consistency in the segmentation masks, it is assumed that there is only one object of in-
terest in each image, as there may be multiple animals in a given image. Furthermore, the
segmentation masks were made for the purpose of keeping as much as possible of what was
determined to be the main subject of the image while avoiding loss of detail. SAM enabled
a high degree of detail in the segmentation masks, and examples of this object of interest
segmentation is found in Figure 2.1.

2.2. BugNIST 3D Dataset
2.2
BugNIST 3D Dataset
BugNIST is a volumetric (3D) dataset for classification, detection, and segmentation [Jensen
et al., 2024].
BugNIST consists of 12 classes of bugs both scanned individually and in
mixtures and stored in volumes. Since the objects of interest (bugs) in BugNIST appear
both individually and in mixtures, e.g. in a context, it is an example of domain shift, where
the object(s) of interest appear out of context in the source domain (training data) and in
context in the target domain (evaluation data).
For the purposes of this project, the individual scans in BugNIST will be used for studying
classification models, and the scans of bug mixtures will be used strictly for studying the
performance of detection models under the domain shift from scans of individual bugs to
scans of mixtures of bugs [Jensen et al., 2024]. Furthermore, the BugNIST volumes are
available in different resolutions. The 128 × 128 × 128 resolution dataset, denoted BugNIST
×128 in original paper, will be used for all intents and purposes. For brevity, I will refer to
BugNIST×128 as "BugNIST".
2.2.1
Object of Interest Segmentation
In order to get accurate segmentation masks of the bugs in the individual scans, a similar
method as that of Jensen et al. will be employed. Without altering the scans, the largest
connected component in each scan was found by connecting voxels that differ in intensity by
no more than δ units. The value of δ was manually tuned to obtain good quality segmentation
masks for a sample of the dataset. Then, outliers in terms of the size of the masks were
identified, and the value of δ was manually tuned again to obtain good quality segmentation
masks for the outliers. Samples of the segmentation masks obtained using this method are
shown in Figure 2.3. This method was chosen as it fairly reliably captures high fidelity
details such as legs, antennae, and wings of the bugs.

3.1
Domain Adaptation
In this section, I will provide an outline of what DA entails and how it relates to the CF-IC
learning task. Furthermore, methods to approach the given problem will be presented.
DA is a task in machine learning where the goal is to apply a model fitted to data from a given
source distribution (source domain, SD) on data from a different target distribution (target
domain, TD) [Kouw and Loog, 2019]. The change in the data from the source distribution
to the target distribution is referred to as the domain shift. Furthermore, this domain shift
can be categorised depending on where the change occurs. A common case is covariate shift
where the change occurs in the input data [Liu et al., 2022a]. Therefore, the CF-IC learning
task is an instance of DA under covariate shift. DA under covariate shift will be referred to
as DA for brevity. The difficulty of a given DA problem is heavily dependent on the specifics
of the domain shift, e.g. the relationship between the SD and the TD. For example, the
SD could be drawings of cars and bicycles, and the TD could be natural images of cars and
bicycles. In this particular example, the distributions are clearly related in the sense that
the object(s) of interest that the images are meant to convey remain the same, but they
are represented in different styles. Therefore, the domain shift in this example refers to the
change in style in the images from drawings to natural images. The CF-IC problem, which
will be formalized in the following, is particularly interesting in terms of its difficulty, since
the representation of the OI remain unchanged in the domain shift
3.1.1
Context in Images
When working with image data, it is often useful to distinguish between the natural image
domain, which refers to images of the real world, and other image domains. Removing the
background of natural images arguably alters them such that they no longer belong in the
natural image domain. However, since the OI came from the natural image domain, and
since neutral backgrounds exist in the real world, it could be argued that CF images also
belong to the natural image domain. Since the OI of the images remains the same, we can
assume that there is a certain overlap between the natural image domain and the CF image
domain. Furthermore, assuming that one domain is a subset of the other, the CF image
domain must be a subset of the natural image domain, since not all natural images have a
neutral background. Thus, the domain shift between the natural image domain and the CF

image domain refers to adding and removing the background from the image, and the CF
image domain is a subset of the natural image domain. Figure 3.1 visualizes the CF image
domain as a subset of the natural image domain, and the domain shift in the form of adding
or removing the context (background).
The special case of DA problems where the SD is a subset of the TD is referred to as partial
DA. In the CF-IC problem, CF images are the SD, and IC (natural) images are the TD.
Therefore, CF-IC is an instance of partial DA. For a classification CF-IC problem, the true

CF-IC is said to be class preserving. Furthermore, in the case of classification, a successful
CF-IC model must be invariant to any changes in the context. However, the property of
invariance to context may also be beneficial in general, as it could improve robustness to
noise and confounders. This insight will be used to investigate whether models improve in
general by promoting invariance to context.

3.1. Domain Adaptation
3.1.2
Supervised Domain Adaptation
DA often appears in one of three main tasks: Supervised DA (SDA), semi-supervised DA
(SSDA), and unsupervised DA (UDA), which differ in the availability of labels in the TD
[Motiian et al., 2017][Saito et al., 2019][You et al., 2019][Liu et al., 2022a]. In SDA, the
TD samples are fully labelled, and the domain shift can therefore be directly modelled. In
SSDA, the TD samples are scarcely labelled, and in UDA, the TD samples are not labelled.
For example, the TD is assumed to be fully labelled in the HW CF-IC problem consid-
ered in this work, so it is an instance of SDA. Approaches to SDA problems often involve
a contrastive loss function [Motiian et al., 2017]. The contrastive loss operates on feature
representations produced by the model. In the case of classification, it aims to increase
similarity of features stemming from images of the same class (semantic alignment) while
decreasing similarity of features stemming from different classes (separation) across domains
[Motiian et al., 2017]. The purpose of semantic alignment is to promote invariance to the
domain shift, while the purpose of separation is to improve classification performance in
the TD [Motiian et al., 2017]. The main component of the contrastive loss is a similarity
measure (function) s : Rn →R. Formally, given feature vectors a, b ∈Rn from the SD and
TD respectively, the similarity of the vectors is given by s(a, b). The similarity measure can
then be used as a loss function to increase/decrease similarity between vectors by switching
the sign of the result. In particular, semantic alignment will be utilized as a loss function in
this work (section 3.4).
Another approach to SDA, and DA in general, is data augmentation. Data augmentation ap-
plies class preserving transformations to the data, which can effectively increase the amount
training data in the SD and the TD [Shorten and Khoshgoftaar, 2019]. This is a standard
practice that has been shown to improve the generalization performance of models, and can
therefore improve DA performance [Shorten and Khoshgoftaar, 2019]. For instance, con-
sider a trivial DA problem where the SD is RGB (full color) images and the TD is grayscale
images. This can be fully solved by augmenting the SD images with an RGB to grayscale
transform.
Thus, DA problems can be approached by applying data augmentation that
models or mimics the given domain shift. This idea will be further explored and tested for
the CF-IC problem.

3.2.1.1
Residual Network (ResNet)
For classification experiments in both 2D and 3D, a Residual Network (ResNet) CNN model
will be used [He et al., 2015]. The ResNet model was chosen as it is both relatively perfor-
mant and well covered in the literature. Furthermore, the flexibility in number of layers and
other hyperparameter make them well suited for problems with different levels of complexity,
as with low or high amounts of data. The main distinguishing feature of ResNets over earlier
CNNs is that they employ residual connections over blocks of e.g. convolutional layers. A
residual connection adds the output of an earlier layer to that of a later layer, which enables
deeper and more accurate models (Figure 3.2) [He et al., 2015]. For this reason, residual
connections are now adopted in most, if not all, modern DL models for image analysis.
The core operations in ResNet apart from residual connections are convolution, activation
(non-linearity), pooling, and batch normalization. The convolutional layers are found with
different kernel sizes and strides, and the pooling layers include max- and global average
pooling. The activation function makes the network non-linear, and therefore enables it to
learn non-linear functions. In this work, commonly used rectified linear unit (ReLU) func-
tion is used for the activations. The max pooling layers are used to downscale intermediate
features while preserving the most prominent features. The global average pooling layer
is found before the linear classification layer at the end of the network, and aggregates all
image features into a single feature vector by averaging. Finally, the batch normalization
layers normalize intermediate outputs within the network by computing statistics over train-
ing batches, while running estimates of the statistics are kept for normalization at inference
time.
ResNets with L convolutional/linear layers are commonly denoted ResNet-L and the num-
ber of layers is typically changed by varying the number of residual blocks in the network
(Figure 3.2). Furthermore, all of the components of the ResNet model directly translate to
3D by adding a spatial dimension to all operations. A 2D ResNet-18 model is visualized in
Figure 3.2.

3.3
Saliency Mapping
DL models often suffer from being so complex that their behavior cannot be easily explained.
This problem is known as explainability and is an active area of research. An approach to
addressing explainability is to develop models that are intrinsically explainable. Another
approach is to develop post hoc methods for better understanding the behavior of existing
models. For instance, when developing deep learning models for image analysis tasks, it can
be useful to know which features of the input images contributed to producing a given output.
Saliency mapping in deep learning for image classification aims to visualize what pixels in
an input image lead to a given class prediction. Saliency mapping, as a broad term, does not
refer to a specific technique. However, many saliency mapping methods are gradient-based.
The simplest gradient-based method, referred to as vanilla saliceny mapping, computes the
gradient of the output softmax probability with respect to the input [Simonyan et al., 2014].
The magnitude of the gradient at the pixel level can then be interpreted as the importance
of the pixel in terms of producing the output. There exists many methods that build upon
this idea, hence why the base method is referred to as vanilla. Alternative methods, such as
LRP which addresses the potential shortcomings of vanilla saliency mapping, may provide
less noisy or more human understandable saliency maps [Bach et al., 2015]. For the pur-
poses of this work however, "vanilla saliency mapping" and "saliency mapping" will be used
synonymously.
A formal definition of the saliency map will be provided in the following. Consider a clas-
sification problem with C classes. Given an input vector x ∈Rn and a classification model

Saliency mapping and similar visualization methods are often used as a qualitative measure
of the behaviour of a given model. In particular, saliency mapping can reveal unwanted
behaviours such as spurious correlations learned by the model, which can then inform im-
provements for subsequent iterations of the model. This use case is typically heavily reliant
on human interpretation of the saliency maps. Therefore, quantitative measures of how well
models align with expected and wanted behaviour could be of great use. The object score
is a quantitative measure developed in this work, based on the "relevance score (average
outside-inside relevance ratio)" introduced by Lapuschkin et al, that addresses this notion

3.6. Perlin Noise
3.6
Perlin Noise
In this work, Perlin noise is used to generate random backgrounds/context as a form of data
augmentation in order to improve domain adaptation performance of deep learning models
[Perlin, 1985][Bae et al., 2018]. In the following section, I will provide a brief introduction
to the Perlin noise algorithm and an examination of the theoretical properties of the noise
images produced by the algorithm. Then, the use of Perlin noise as data augmentation over
simpler noise generating algorithms such as uniform noise is motivated. Finally, a description
of the practical application of Perlin noise in this work is given in subsection 3.6.2.
3.6.1
Theory
Perlin noise is a noise generating algorithm invented by Ken Perlin [Perlin, 1985], and is often
used to randomly generate textures or terrain in computer graphics. It works by constructing
a signal with a number of random points with random values (white noise) and interpolating
the values between them. In 2D, the random points are specified by a lattice with a random
unit gradient vector at each point. Then, the value for each point (pixel) in the image is
given by the interpolated dot products of the offsets from the four nearest lattice points
with the gradients in these lattice points. With this method, noise with different frequencies
can be generated by specifying the spacing between lattice points. Importantly, Perlin noise
generalizes to any number of dimensions. Furthermore, the algorithm is relatively efficient
and produces visually appealing results, making it widely adopted in computer graphics.
Perlin noise is often used to generate fractal Perlin noise. The main idea of fractal Perlin noise
is to generate a number of Perlin noise signals with different frequencies and amplitudes and
add them together. The heuristic for choosing the frequencies and corresponding amplitudes
is the key to generating the desired outcome. A commonly used heuristic is to start with low
frequency Perlin noise and then iteratively generate Perlin noise with double the frequency
and half the amplitude. This process can be repeated indefinitely, which is why it is referred
to as fractal. However, for practical applications the process can be halted when the desired
level of detail is reached. Additionally, a hard limit is reached when the spacing between
lattice points is one pixel, where the Perlin noise corresponds to white noise. In general,
given any number of dimensions and a finite lowest unit of measurement, the lowest possible
spacing between lattice points is given by this unit of measurement, e.g. pixels, voxels, etc.
The purpose of fractal noise is incorporate multiple levels of details into the result, which
is particularly useful for generating e.g. terrain since realistic hills and valleys have bumps
and holes when you look closely.
3.6.1.1
Properties
We have now seen how Perlin noise is produced, and how it can be used to generate interesting
textures. In order to motivate its usage for this work, I will examine the properties of Perlin
noise compared to natural images, other types of noise, etc. To this end, the 2D Fourier
transforms (FT) will be used [Hoggar, 2012].
The theoretical basis for the FT is that
any signal can be decomposed into a sum of sinusoids (waves) with different frequencies,

CNN classification model is fixed to a ResNet-18 model (subsubsection 3.2.1.1, Figure 3.2).
Furthermore, three datasets are derived from the HW dataset, referred to as HWA, HWB,
and HWC respectively (section 2.1). HWA is the unaltered dataset, HWB is HW images
where the context is replaced with the mean of the OI in the HW dataset, and HWC is HW
images where the context is replaced with Perlin noise. Four samples from the validation
split of these datasets is shown in Figure 3.7.
Due to the complexity of determining optimal training schemes for each dataset, a minimalist
training setup is used for most experiments, as described in the following. All models were
trained until convergence with early stopping (maximum 1000 epochs). For optimization,
the AdamW optimizer was used with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999 and with a varying weight decay
(w.d.) parameter [Loshchilov and Hutter, 2017]. The initial learning rate was set to 10−4
and was reduced by a factor of 0.1 if no improvement was observed with a patience of 40
epochs. The criteria for early stopping was set to the following: No improvement in the
validation loss being observed with a patience of 75 epochs or the learning rate being less
than 10−6. Furthermore, the weight decay parameter was individually tuned to minimize

Impact of Context & DA performance

3.7.1.2
Impact of Confounders in the Context
In order to quantify the impact of confounders on CNN classification models, two experiments
are conducted on the HWA dataset. In the first experiment, the model is trained normally,
exactly as in the experiments described in subsubsection 3.7.1.1.
Then, predictions are
generated for 183 images from ImageNet containing humans and no dogs/wolves, and the
distribution of the predictions is reported [Deng et al., 2009]. The purpose of this experiment
is to test whether humans or human in environments are correlated with the prediction
"Siberian Husky". In the second experiment, a model is trained with an artificial confounder
in the form of a "watermark". The watermark is the letter "W", and it is added to 50% of
the images with wolves in the training set (see Figure 6.2). The model is then evaluated on
the test images in two ways: In the first, the model is evaluated normally, so no watermarks
are added to the images in order to measure how well the true signal from the OI has been
learned. In the second, watermarks are added to all evaluation images, denoted by "HWA
+ Confounder" in order to measure how well the model has learned to ignore the artificial
confounder in the context.

3.7.2.1
Classification
The purpose of this experiment is to investigate the effect of adding Perlin noise around
the OI (bugs) in the individual bug scans on classification models. Therefore, the model
choice is fixed to a 3D ResNet 18 classifier, which is also used in the BugNIST paper [Jensen
et al., 2024]. Then, a model is trained on volumes with Perlin noise and one is trained
without it. Both models are trained for a total of 200 epochs. For optimization, the Adam
optimizer is used with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, and an initial learning rate of 10−4, which
is reduced to 10−5 after 150 epochs [Kingma and Ba, 2017]. For evaluation, each model is
tested on volumes with and without Perlin noise, and the object score is measured along with
classification metrics. This experiment will test whether Perlin noise degrades or improves
classification performance of 3D models. Additionally, it will test the effect of evaluating on
the opposite data than what each model was trained on. Finally, the overall behaviour of
models will be assessed qualitatively with saliency mapping on volumes with and without
Perlin noise (section 3.3). The classification data split made for this project was created
independently from that of the original BugNIST paper, so while the original results are not
directly comparable to mine they will be still used as reference [Jensen et al., 2024].
3.7.2.2
Detection
The purpose of this experiment is to investigate the effect of adding Perlin noise around the
OI (bugs) in the training data for segmentation/detection in mixtures of bugs. Here, the
U-Net baseline as presented in the BugNIST paper is used for direct comparison (subsec-
tion 3.2.2) [Jensen et al., 2024]. The validation and test sets are the same as in the BugNIST
paper. Two models are trained with Perlin noise added around the OI. One is trained on

individual bugs, and one is trained on synthetic mixtures of bugs as in the BugNIST paper.
The synthetic mixtures are created per batch by splitting the batch in two and merging one
half into the other, if the objects do not overlap too much. This is repeated for the half
that the other was merged into until the batch cannot be split anymore. This way, volumes
with different amounts of bugs are represented in each batch, and the synthetic mixtures are
unique in each batch. Furthermore, the loss for the background class pixels is weighted by
0.1 to emphasize correct bug segmentation over background segmentation. For evaluation,
the segmentation masks are thresholded and converted to bounding boxes by identifying
blobs using connected components for each class as in the BugNIST paper. The hyperpa-
rameters for converting the segmentation masks to bounding boxes have a noticeable impact
on detection performance. For comparison with the original results, these hyperparameters
are left as is, since we are trying to measure the effect of Perlin noise. However, alternative
results are also presented, where the hyperparameters are further tuned on the validation
set in order to better sanitize the bounding boxes. These hyperparameters include the con-
fidence threshold for classifying a voxel as one of the bug classes versus the background and
the number of times binary erosion is applied to the thresholded segmentation masks.

Discussion
5.1
The Role of Context
The effect of context in images on the behaviour of CNN models was investigated through
a majority of the proposed experiments. In the following, my overall findings from these
experiments will be presented. First, the results of the individual experiments will be inter-
preted and discussed. Then, these findings will be compounded for an overall assessment of
the role of context in the investigated datasets and models.
The impact of context on model performance was directly quantified on the HW dataset,
where it was found that context may negatively impact model performance, especially when
reducing the amount of training data (section 4.1, Figure 4.1, Figure 4.2). Furthermore,
adding an artificial confounder to the training images did not significantly impact the model
performance on the unaltered test set. However, the performance of these models was very
negatively affected when adding the confounder to the images in the test set. This sug-
gests that the models simultaneously learn the spurious correlations and the true signal in
the training data. This is corroborated by the fact that the models preferentially predict
"Husky" on images of humans, even when the context is removed in the training data. The
fact that models trained without context prefer the "Husky" class when the models have not
directly seen humans during training is a surprising result. I hypothesise that this is mainly
caused by errors in the segmentation of the OI. Another contributing factor could be the
distribution of color in the images. This was superficially tested by generating ∼104 predic-
tions on images of flat, random RGB colours for models trained with and without context
(HWA and HWB). Then, the distribution of the intensities in the green channel grouped by
class was presented (Figure 6.3). The result suggests that both models preferentially predict
"Wolf" when the intensity of green increases. This could be explained by wolves often being
pictured in nature, and errors in the segmentation masks, where e.g. foliage obscuring the
OI in the foreground was not labelled as context. Alternatively, this could be explained by
the lighting and intrinsic of the camera being correlated with each class.

it was found to be highly susceptible to spurious correlations. Furthermore, the difficulty of
the BugNIST detection problem supposedly stems from the introduction of context [Jensen
et al., 2024]. Therefore, I conclude that context in images negatively impacts model per-
formance on the investigated datasets, and that spurious correlations in the training data
significantly affects model behaviour. However, I was unable to determine a concrete source

5.2
CF-IC Domain Adaptation
The main research question posed in section 1.2 is addressed by the experimental results
(Figure 4.2). Specifically, the CF-IC results for the HW dataset along with the BugNIST
classification and detection results contribute to answering this research question. The HW
CF-IC results alone suggest that the answer is mixed, since the best performing CF-IC
model, which was trained with Perlin noise, did not achieve as good performance as training
on HWA directly (Figure 4.1). However, training with Perlin noise (HWC) substantially
improved CF-IC performance compared to training with a neutral background (HWB) (Fig-
ure 4.1a). It can therefore be concluded from the HW CF-IC results that Perlin noise is
a positive step towards successfully applying a model trained on CF images on IC images.
Considering the BugNIST detection results, adding Perlin noise around the OI greatly im-
proved CF-IC detection performance (Table 4.2, Table 6.1). Although the CF-IC detection
results cannot be directly compared to those of a detection model trained directly on IC
volumes, this implies that a model trained on CF volumes was successfully applied for IC
volumes. Compounding the results from the HW case and the BugNIST case to answer the
main research question, it is concluded that certain instances of the CF-IC DA task can be
successfully solved by augmenting the context with Perlin noise.
While augmenting the context with Perlin noise was shown to work well for the CF-IC DA
task, I hypothesise that this is not the only type of randomly generated noise/texture that
would work well. As suggested in subsubsection 3.6.1.1, Perlin noise is able to recreate some
of the characteristics of natural images, at least to a higher degree than white noise or neutral
backgrounds. However, noise generating algorithms similar to Perlin noise, such as "value
noise", may yield comparable results. Additionally, it may be possible to directly model, and
thereby randomly sample from, the context (backgrounds) of e.g. natural images. This could
be achieved using an unsupervised learning approach, such as a Gaussian mixture model,
in order to model the distribution of a given set of images in the frequency domain. Then,
a random frequency image could be sampled from the modelled distribution and converted
back to the pixel representation of the image using the inverse DFT. A major disadvantage
of this is that a sufficiently large amount of representative data would be needed to model
this assumed distribution. In contrast, Perlin noise can be applied "a priori" as was done in
this work.

5.3. Semantic Alignment Loss
5.3
Semantic Alignment Loss
The SA loss was proposed in this work to investigate the effect of promoting invariance
to context in images (section 3.4). The experimental results suggest that the SA loss may
slightly improve classification performance. The observed effect of the SA loss was most
prominent on the smaller 1
4HWA dataset and when introducing an artificial confounder to
the training and evaluation set (Figure 4.2, Figure 4.5). However, the observed performance
increase of the SA loss on the full HWA dataset was negligible. In conclusion, while the results
are mixed, the SA loss shows some promise for improving model performance. I hypothesise
that potential improvements in model performance of the SA loss can be attributed to an
improved robustness to spurious correlations, as was observed in the artificial confounder
experiment.
For the purposes of this project, the context removing transformation used with the SA loss
was fixed to a simple blurring algorithm with different strengths. This relaxed the definition
of an image being "context free" in favour of making the SA loss less harsh in practice.
However, this transformation may have a significant effect on the efficacy of the SA loss.
Therefore, it could be interesting to explore whether alternative methods for removing the
context, such as replacing it with a random flat colour, would improve the effect of the SA
loss. Expanding upon this, multiple context removing transformations could be applied on
a given image yielding multiple SA loss terms, which could then be averaged to obtain a
better estimate of the SA loss. These ideas for potential improvement could be interesting
to explore in future work.
5.4
Deep Learning in 3D
While the practical implementations of CNN models in 2D and 3D respectively is compa-
rable, the behaviour, performance, and prerequisites for models in 3D compared to 2D are
decidedly different. For instance, there has been shown to be a significant benefit in model
accuracy when applying deep learning models for medical semantic segmentation tasks on
3D volumes compared to 2D slices [Avesta et al., 2023][Shivdeo et al., 2021]. The authors
attribute the increased performance of the 3D models to their ability to leverage more con-
textual information than the corresponding 2D models [Shivdeo et al., 2021]. However, 3D
models are much more demanding in terms of memory than 2D models, limiting their po-
tential complexity [Avesta et al., 2023]. Furthermore, 3D data is generally much harder to
come by than 2D data, which is a major limiting factor for DL applications. For instance,
large scale pretraining is very commonly used for 2D DL models, which is often not feasible
for 3D models, although advances are being made [Wu et al., 2023]. These factors place 3D
models at a severe disadvantage over 2D models for many practical use cases. Developing
more efficient, e.g. less data hungry, methods for DL in 3D may therefore be very useful.
In BugNIST, volumes with mixtures of bugs are exceedingly hard to label compared to
volumes of individual bugs, where simple hand-crafted methods for e.g. segmentation are
sufficient (subsection 2.2.1) [Jensen et al., 2024]. In order to develop models for segmenta-
tion/detection in mixtures of bugs, it is therefore more practical to train models on volumes

individual bugs that are easier to label and obtain data for overall. In general, the workload
for labelling and data gathering for 3D DL models could be reduced by enabling models to
learn from simpler data that is more easily obtained, as in BugNIST where the OI can be
more easily scanned and labelled individually. The BugNIST detection results show, that the
simple data augmentation scheme of adding Perlin noise around the OI in individual scans of

found to improve model performance, when the amount of training data was low on a 2D
dataset. It could be interesting to investigate whether SA loss also improves model perfor-
mance in 3D models, as it could then lighten the need for training data. Overall, the methods
proposed in this work could potentially be applied to make DL in 3D image analysis more
efficient, and thereby more viable. I find that this is an interesting direction for future work.
5.5
Object Score
Throughout the experimental results, the object score has been used to quantify the degree
to which the OI contributed to a given classification outcome. But what is the significance
of a high object score? An object score of ∼1 means that pixels pertaining to the OI are
much more salient than those pertaining to the context wrt. to the output classification.
As described in section 3.3, a saliency score with |si| > 0 in a pixel means that a small
perturbation to that pixel will change the classification score. Therefore, if the object score
is ∼1, small changes in the context are unlikely to change the classification outcome. In
other words, models that achieve a high average object score are more robust to changes in
the context compared to models that achieve a low average object score. Since the CF-IC
domain shift only involves a change in the context, it is expected that models that achieve a
high average object score will perform better in the CF-IC DA task. This is corroborated by
the CF-IC results for training with Perlin noise in both 2D and 3D (Figure 4.1, Figure 4.7,
Table 4.1). In particular, in the case of the BugNIST classification task, the low observed
object score revealed that the model trained without Perlin noise was ill fitted, and therefore
susceptible to changes in the context. From these outcomes, I conclude that the object
score is an accurate quantitative measure of robustness to change in the context, as it
was successfully applied as an indicator for how well models would adapt to change in the
context, as seen in the CF-IC experiments. Additionally, optimising for the object score when
developing DL models for image analysis in general could yield more explainable models.

In this work, Perlin noise was to randomly generate features that filled empty space in the
context, or replaced it entirely. This was shown to successfully improve CF-IC DA per-
formance. Therefore, it could be interesting to investigate whether Perlin noise, or similar
approaches, could improve performance for other methods in image analysis, where infor-

5.7
Limitations
There are several limitations to this work. These mainly stem from the many limiting, but
deliberate, choices that were made at different points in order to properly scope the work.
Firstly, the majority of the experiments were only carried out on the HW set and with a
fixed CNN model (ResNet-18). This severely limits the scope of the conclusions that can be
made from this study. For instance, it is unclear whether the observed effects of e.g. the SA
loss persist for other datasets and other CNN architectures at different scales. To address
this, the proposed experiments carried out in this work should be repeated for different
datasets and models. In particular, fixing the classification model for the HW experiments
to a ResNet-18 model resulted in apparent overfitting in some instances, which suggests that
a simpler model would have been more appropriate (subsection 6.4.1). However, fixing the
model allowed for direct comparison of different interventions (experiments) on a relative
scale as intended. Overall, fixing the dataset and the CNN models allowed for investigation
of a wider range of interventions in the form methods and experiments in exchange for a
more limited scope.
Another limitation of this study is the quality of the HW dataset.
The most pressing
issue with the dataset is that there may be multiple OI (animals) that fit the class label
in each image, which proved an issue for OI segmentation (subsection 2.1.1).
This was
shallowly circumvented by assuming that each image only contained one OI (husky/wolf),
when creating the segmentation masks. A serious consequence of this is that the OI may be

These errors may have severely impacted the effect of the SA loss, as the SA loss may
then have counteracted the CE loss for learning discriminative features between the OI in
images where the OI was falsely labelled as "context". However, since the models trained
and evaluated on CF images generally outperformed the corresponding models trained and
evaluated on IC images, the OI that were correctly labelled seem to be sufficient for successful
classification. Finally, the HW dataset is limited by its scale. This introduces a substantial
amount of statistical error in the evaluation results for both the validation- and test set,

5.8. Conclusion
5.8
Conclusion
In this Master’s thesis, an experimental approach to investigating the role of context in deep
learning was proposed. The context free to in context domain adaptation task was introduced
and formalized, and both 2D and 3D image datasets representing this task were gathered
for experiments. I propose simple data augmentation approach for solving this task, which
randomly generates context in images with Perlin noise. From experiments with both 2D
and 3D CNN models for classification and detection tasks, I conclude that models trained on
images of out-of-context objects can be successfully be applied for classification/detection on
in-context objects using this approach. Notably, I found that the proposed method greatly
improved detection performance on BugNIST compared to the reference results.
Furthermore, I propose a quantitative measure, namely the object score, of the degree to
which deep learning classification models rely on the context versus the objects of interest
for prediction. I find that the object score is useful for investigating the properties of, and
identifying potential problems for, 2D and 3D deep learning classification models.
Several experiments aimed at quantifying the effect of context in images on model perfor-
mance was carried out. I conclude, that context may negatively impact model performance,
and that confounders in the context strongly affect the behaviour of models on the inves-
tigated dataset. In order to investigate whether the negative effects of context could be
curbed, the semantic alignment loss function was developed. I conclude that while the se-
mantic alignment loss improved model performance in several experiments, the effect was
found to be statistically inconclusive.
Finally, I found that the 2D classification dataset used for several experiments in this work
had major issues in quality and scale. Therefore, it is suggested that the proposed exper-
iments should be repeated on a revised and version this dataset and/or on other similar
datasets.
Overall, I conclude that the proposed approach(es) for investigating the role of context in
images for deep learning provided compelling insights, that could be useful in improving
deep learning models for 2D and 3D image analysis.

6.3.1
Feature weights
The weights w for the features at each layer are hyperparameters that can be freely chosen depend-
ing on the desired outcome. For the purpose of dissuading a given model from learning features
pertaining to the context of an image, the weights should reflect which features belong to the con-
text. The weights could then be defined from a segmentation mask of the context. However, the
receptive field for each feature grows with the number of layers, so each feature pertains to multiple
input pixels. A safe approach is to downscale the segmentation mask to the corresponding height
and width dimensions of the features at each desired layer and use the resulting binary mask as
weights (Figure 6.1). This way, the majority of the pixels within the receptive field of the chosen
features pertain to the context, and we therefore avoid discouraging features pertaining to the object
of interest. This method also seemed to produce the best results from my own brief investigation.
