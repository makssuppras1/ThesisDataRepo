Abstract 
 
ABSTRACT 
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate whether the use of external shading as 
a passive means of cooling is a beneficial solution to meet the current demands to 
satisfy both the thermal and visual comfort of occupants in office spaces. This was 
done by conducting both building energy modelling (BEM) simulations and 
daylight simulations for a generic shoebox office using various external and 
integrated solar shading systems.   
The study found that the solutions from the thermal and the visual assessment were 
contradictory and in fact none of the chosen shading scenarios satisfied all of the 
chosen design values at the same time. As a result of this finding, the report 
highlighted a range of additional steps which must be taken to achieve a more 
wholesome understanding of the effects these various shading types can have on 
both the visual comfort, thermal comfort and energy consumption of office 
buildings. The aim of the suggested future steps was to create optimisations in 
regards to both the geometry and the control of each shading type and these 
optimized shading devices may provide a more fair evaluation of the relative effect 
of the devices. 
In total, the study found that both external and integrated solar shading solutions 
could serve as a passive means of providing thermal comfort in office spaces when 
installed and controlled correctly. Still, none of these solutions created desirable 
daylight conditions. However, it must be underlined that the findings of this thesis 
are not conclusive as optimal solutions since each shading type must be found to 
make fair comparisons.

Introduction 
1.1. Background 
For centuries, window design has been a core element in building design. Le 
Corbusier once said: “The history of architecture is also the history of windows” [1]. 
The first known glazed windows were found in Rome and can be traced back to 
around 100 BC, yet glazed windows in dwellings were not common until the 14th 
century. The greatest advancements in window design occurred during the 19th 
century. In 1880, the American engineer William Jenney build the First Leiter 
Building, which was the first building with exterior load-bearing columns. By 
making the façade non-load-bearing, this innovation made it possible to build 
offices with large glazing ratios [2]. During the last two decades, extensive attention 
has been payed to reduce the energy use in buildings. Due to subsequent 
increases in insulation efficiencies combined with these large glazing ratios, 
overheating has become a prime concern in modern buildings [3]. Thus, the need 
to adjust the thermal climate in buildings has increased along with the advances in 
energy efficiency. 
 
 
Figure 1 - Window design through history according Bill Addis [2]  
 
==PAGE:12==

Introduction 
The thermal environment in buildings can be adjusted by either active means, such 
as mechanical ventilation and cooling, or passive means, such as natural ventilation 
or solar shading. Solar shading can be installed either internally, externally or as an 
integrated part of the glazing unit. However, multiple studies have illustrated a 
significant increase in shading efficiency by using external shading devices rather 
than internal solutions. The advantage of external and integrated solar shadings is 
that they block out solar heat gain before it enters the room, thereby reducing the 
need for mechanical cooling [5] [6].  
While shading devices may reduce overheating and lower the cooling demand of 
buildings, they also have significant negative impact on the daylight provision in 
the zone and on the view towards the outdoor surroundings. This effect can be 
reduced by using dynamic shading systems combined with glazing units with a 
high light transmittance (LT). In theory, this combination creates good protection 
from excess solar heat gains, while allowing high quantities of daylight into the 
building, when the outdoor illuminance levels are low.  
Due to the uncertainties and the long-term cost associated with repair and 
maintenance of external shading devices, simpler solutions with low maintenance 
costs such as solar control glazing units are sometimes chosen instead. While 
dynamic shadings provide low LT when drawn and high LT when they are not, solar 
control windows provide a moderate LT during the entire year. In essence, the 
latter also blocks the daylight during hours with low external illuminance levels. 
Hence, trade-offs concerning the indoor environmental quality (IEQ) in buildings 
can be registered for both solutions.  
 
 
Figure 2 - Four categories of indoor environmental quality  
 
The quality of the indoor environmental quality may influence the perceived 
comfort in a space. The IEQ consists of four components: Thermal-, visual-, 
atmospheric-, and acoustic climate. However, this thesis solely focuses on the 
thermal and visual climate. The effects of the IEQ can be divided into two areas, 
namely perceived indoor climate and human health. To highlight the 
consequences of poor indoor environment quality on health, wellbeing and 
productivity in offices, the World Green Building Council (WGBC) published a 
report in 2014 compiling results from various international studies on the matter. 
Among other things, the report indicated that ideal illuminance levels at the 
workspace could increase the productivity of employees with up to 18 %. The

Other studies also show that Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD), also known as 
winter depression, can be directly correlated with geographical latitude. According 
to Mullins et al., this correlates with the fact that the yearly solar exposure is reduced 
the further you get away from the equator. A higher rate of people with SAD is 
hence registered in the northern regions. In Denmark, approximately 5 % of the 
population is diagnosed with SAD, while it is estimated that more than 40 % of the 
population suffer from SAD-symptoms in various degrees [7].  
In 2001, the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL) in the USA published a 
paper based on a nationwide two-year probability-based survey in which they 
reported that the respondents spent an average of 87 % of their time in enclosed 
buildings, comprising 22 % at an indoor workplace, and about 6 % of their time in 
enclosed vehicles. The study added that the variation in the responses was fairly 
small despite geographical location and demographics. It must be noted that the 
report underlined that the majority of this time was spent sleeping [8]. Still, the 
SmartMarket Report found that offices are the top sector considered in relation to 
reported health issues caused by indoor conditions [9]. These findings highlight 
the importance of designing buildings with IEQ in mind.

An increased focus on the indoor climate in buildings can potentially also yield 
financial benefits. The World Green Building Council has estimated that 
approximately 90% of the business operation cost during the lifetime of a building 
may be accounted for in staff costs, including salaries and benefits [6].

1.2. Problem definition 
With the advancement of modern architectural design practices, the question 
raised is whether the use of external shading as a passive means of cooling is a 
beneficial solution to meet the current demands to satisfy both the thermal and 
visual comfort of occupants in office spaces. The following questions will help 
clarify the issue: 
 
Can the effects of various shading devices on the indoor thermal 
environment be correctly modelled in the simulation program IDA ICE? 
 
Is the shading effect of external shading sufficient to minimize the risk of 
overheating in office buildings with a high window-to-wall ratio? 
 
What are the quantitative consequences of using external shading on the 
daylight provision in office buildings? 
 
How does the choice of solar shading influence the energy consumption 
of an office building?  
This project mainly focuses on the influence of various solar shading devices on the 
building’s energy demand, the thermal environment, and the visual comfort. Only 
the aspect of daylight provision will be considered concerning the visual comfort. 
As a simplification, the influence of daylight levels on the energy use of artificial 
lighting is not considered. Only very simplified considerations concerning the view 
out and the draught rate in the zone will be included in this study. Other topics 
such as maintenance of shading devices, life-cycle cost, acoustics, environmental 
impact, etc. is outside the scope of this project. It should be noted that assessing 
the potential trade-offs concerning both maintenance and the life-cycle costs is an 
essential element in the holistic assessment of the potential of using solar shading 
in office buildings.

Methodology 
The project is comprised of two separate case studies, which in themselves are 
unrelated. The results from Case 1 will however be applied in the discussion of the 
results acquired in Case 2. Each case is researched using different approaches, why 
two separate methodologies will be introduced in the following sections. 
2.1. Case 1 approach 
To ensure that the results acquired through building performance simulations 
(BPS) are relatively realistic, the first phase of the project consisted of validating the 
newest release of the software IDA ICE 5.0 beta 20. The validation was carried out 
by comparing field measurements of an office space with simulation results from a 
virtual replicate.  
2.1.1. Field measurements 
Measurements were conducted at an office space on the third floor of building 118 
at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The experiment was conducted in a 
set of intervals. In the morning, the entire room was flush ventilated by opening the 
door towards the corridor and opening the window in both the investigated office 
and the adjacent office (see Figure 7). The room was ventilated during the morning 
until a target indoor air temperature of 21°C was reached. During the ventilation 
period, the shading was fully drawn with a slat angle of 90° to avoid additional heat 
from direct solar irradiation. On certain days, it was not possible to achieve the 
desired starting temperature due to high outdoor temperatures. In these instances, 
the room was ventilated in 1-2 hours, until the indoor air temperature reached a 
relatively steady state. Hereafter, the shading was set to one of the states listed 
below and the conditions in the zone was measured in one-hour intervals.  
 
State 1: No shading drawn 
State 2: Venetian blinds fully drawn with a slat angle of 0° 
State 3: Venetian blinds fully drawn with a slat angle of approximately 45°

2.1.2. Simulation approach 
A virtual replicate of the office, hereafter referred to as Office 260, was modelled 
in the building energy modelling (BEM) software IDA ICE version 5.0 beta 20. Zone 
dimensions and other detailed specifications can be found in the appendix.  
The entire project was divided into 20 individual simulations comprised of a 
ventilation period followed by a measurement period according to the structure of 
the field measurements. The starting temperature was set to match the air 
temperature at that given timestep according to the field measurements 
regardless of the final temperature of the previous simulation. This was done in 
order to avoid potential errors or unwanted variations at the start of each 
ventilation. The ventilation flow rate from the window opening was likewise 
adjusted to ensure that the start temperature of each measurement period 
matched that of the field measurements. The results from each simulation are 
combined and illustrated as one simulation in the results for simplicity.

Furthermore, the effect of the window size on both thermal and visual comfort was 
investigated by modelling two different window sizes. The window size is defined 
by a window-to-wall ratio (WWR) excluding the area from the window frame. In a 
study in 2016, Francesco Goia researched the optimal WWR for different 
orientations in 4 different European climates. He concluded that the optimum 
could be found at a range between 30-45 % [13]. According to the Selection 
Guideline from MicroShade, it is only advised to use of MicroShade products in 
zone with a window-to-floor ratio of WFR = 25% [14]. In the case of this shoebox 
model, this value corresponds to approximately WWR  ≅ 50 %. Therefore, the first 
shoebox was modelled with a WWR of 40 % and the other shoebox was modelled 
with a WWR of 50 %. This results a total of 120 simulation scenarios. 
Draught risk simulations require complex computational fluid dynamics (CFD) 
modelling. This type of simulation is extremely time consuming and is highly 
sensitive to input variations. Hence no CFD simulations have been made as part of 
this study. Instead, it is assumed that the draught rate is below 20 % if the cooling 
capacity of the ventilation system is below 55 W/m2 as described by Nickel [15].  It 
must be noted that this simplification is fairly rough. The actual conditions will 
depend on a range of factors such the placement and amount of diffuser. It is hence 
not implied that a draught risk higher than 20 % will occur if the cooling capacity 
exceeds 55 W/m2. Rather, it is implied that the chances of reaching a draught risk 
above the design value are rather low if the cooling capacity is below 55 W/m2.

Nickel proposes that the ventilation should be supplemented with other cooling 
systems such as a chilled beam or a radiant cooling ceiling if the design value is 
exceeded. Nickel likewise alternatively argues that ventilation supplied through the 
perforations in an acoustic ceiling may provide ventilation with a cooling capacity 
up to 90 W/m2 while providing a draught risk below 20 %, [15] 
 
2.2.2. Daylight simulation 
The daylight simulations are made using the Three-Phase method and is calculated 
in Radiance through the Rhino/Grasshopper plugin Procedural Compute. The 
annual daylight availability (DLA) in the zone is calculated based on the daylight 
metric defined in EN17037:2018. 
The reflectance of each surface is chosen according to BR18 §379-381 (see 
appendix). As a simplification, both windowpanes and shading devices are defined 
as bidirectional scattering distribution function (BSDF) files rather than being 
modelled 1:1 as geometry. Radiance parameters and other details concerning the 
model setup are listed in the appendix. 
The activation and rotation control of the shading devices are based on output files 
from the BEM simulations. In Radiance, each shading state needs a separate BSDF 
file. As a simplification, the angle of each shading state is rounded up to the nearest 
10, though IDA ICE simulates the rotation of the lamellas dynamically. 
The daylight study consists of 60 simulations, comprising of the 10 simulations 
listed in Table XX with their two respective window sizes in three orientations. As 
for the building energy modelling, the northern orientation is neglected, as solar 
shading usually is not needed to the north.

3.1. Analysis 
The purpose of conducting a model calibration was to assess whether the 
simulation tool IDA ICE 5.0 beta was suited to simulate the effects of solar shading 
devices on the thermal climate of an office space. When comparing the field 
measurements with the computed results, the simulation results proved to be in 
close alignment with the actual conditions (see Figure 11). Overall, the mean 
variation was 0.9°C, however the divergence from the mean value was rather high. 
When excluding the ventilation periods from the calculation, the largest 
divergence was reduced from 8.1°C to 2.7°C. From these results it can thus be 
concluded that the program is better suited for simulating the effect of shading 
devices than that of natural ventilation.

There are a range of probable reasons which could explain the poor computational 
replication of the field measurements in regards to the periods with flush 
ventilation. First of all, IDA ICE uses a rather simplified method for natural 
ventilation calculations. In this specific evaluated model, one-sided natural 
ventilation has been used. Air flows through openings toward the façade for this 
type of ventilation are calculated by dividing a given window into two separate 
sections of equal size. One section is considered as an inlet area whereas the other 
area is considered exhaust area. It is always assumed that the same air volume 
enters the zone as exits it. The air flow is determined as a combination of 
predefined pressure coefficients, wind speed and wind direction given in the 
weather file. The pressure coefficients are determined by whether the building is 
considered to have an exposed, semi-exposed or sheltered location and takes the 
height of the modelled building body is also taken into consideration [18]. 
However, the program does not take detailed air flow patterns inside or outside 
the zone into account. A more accurate modelling of the natural ventilation air flows 
could have been achieved using computational fluid dynamics (CFD), but CFD 
simulations require great computational power and are very time consuming. 
Furthermore, though CFD calculations strive to depict realistic air flow conditions 
and patterns, this type of simulation is highly sensitive to variations in the input data 
[19].  
Another apparent explanation for the poor replication of the natural ventilation is 
inaccuracy of the collected weather data. As described in Section 2.1.2, the 
weather data used for the calibration model is taken from the weather station 
located on building 119, (B119) at DTU, whereas the measurements have been 
conducted in building 118, (B118). The total height difference between the two 
buildings is 4.9 meters and the buildings are placed approximately 22 meters from 
one another. With this in mind, it must be assumed that B118 blocks the wind at 
the weather station, when the wind comes from a southern or southwestern 
direction. These conditions would have direct influence on the computed 
ventilation rate during the flush ventilation periods.

The third observation was that the air temperature dropped during the night in the 
simulations, while the air temperature generally increased slightly during the night. 
To avoid excess heat before the first measurement period of each day, the shading 
was drawn at either state 2 or state 3 during the nights. As no solar irradiation is 
present during the night, potential defects in the weather data has little effect on 
the observed conditions. In this situation, two possible conditions may play a 
significant role in the temperature decrease observed in the simulations: 
 
1) Non-adiabetic conditions 
2) Poor replication of the effect of thermal masses 
 
In IDA ICE, it is always assumed that conditions beyond the internal surfaces are 
completely adiabatic, why no heat flux is calculated through the internal surfaces. 
However, this might not be the case in reality. The three primary conditions which 
might have created influential heat fluxes between Office 260 and the surrounding 
areas are occupant presence, window openings, and active radiators. It may be 
assumed that there were no occupants present during the night, but it was 
observed that some offices had open windows during the night on certain days. 
Whether the radiators were turned on in adjacent areas during the measurements 
is uncertain. 
The next uncertainty is in regards to thermal mass. As explained in Section 2.1.2, 
each simulation consisted of a period of flush ventilation and a measurement 
period. During the two weeks before the flush, the air temperature was kept steady 
so that the starting temperature for each simulation was correct. However, this 
method results in low surface and construction temperatures which may very well 
explain the temperature drops.

4.3. Partial conclusion/Discussion 
In terms of thermal comfort, the superior solutions was given in Scenarios 6a, 4, 2, 
and 3 respectively, as these solutions proved to be able reduce the cooling 
demand to a such a degree that draught rates below 20 % could be achieved even 
with large glazing ratios. At the same time, these solutions proved to reduce the 
energy demand of the zone by 3-9 % towards west, 12-17 % towards south and 5-
8 % towards east relative to the energy use from the Baseline scenario. The study 
highlighted that solutions using the lowest possible air flow rates which allows 
compliance with the overheating design value are relatively sensible to changes. 
Hence the optimal solution in terms of overheating, draught, energy consumption 
and robustness should be found somewhere in between the minimal and the 
maximum versions. 
On the other hand, the best solutions in terms of daylight provision and weighted 
shading activation hours were presented in the Baseline scenario and Scenarios 2a 
and 1 respectively, while none of the other solutions created conditions which 
satisfied the design value given in BR18 and EN17037. 
When taking both these aspects of indoor environmental quality into 
consideration, it is evident that each recommendation contradicts one another. As 
such, this study has highlighted the high complexity of creating building designs 
aiming to create optimal indoor environmental quality. The results also highlight 
the complexity of creating building performance simulation models that can give 
exact indications of the effect of shading devices. More detailed parameter 
variations for each shading type must be performed to create a holistic understand 
of its potential. Parameter variation concerning the shading geometry may include 
optimisation of slat depth, slat distance, slat angle, and shading material. When 
investigating the effect of the MicroShade film, each of the 5 glazing combinations 
suggested by the company should be considered individually [14]. Parameter 
variations concerning the control signal may include setpoint variations and 
alternative rotation segments. Attention should also be payed to the parameters 
influencing when the shading is deactivated, namely illuminance thresholds, 
maximum air speeds and other relevant weather indicators. Such parameter 
variations could be facilitated through multi-objective parametric algorithms such 
as Galapagos or AUTOMOO. However, the specifications of such algorithms 
should be thoroughly investigated prior to the simulations.

5. DISCUSSION 
 
 
==PAGE:44==
 41 
Discussion 
In this chapter, topics such as the general validity of assumptions, methods and 
result interpretations will be discussed. Suggestions will likewise be made to how 
the topic can be further investigated in the future and which aspects could help 
raise attention on the complexity of indoor environmental design. 
5.1. Daylight simulation 
While the software used for the building energy modelling was calibrated through 
field measurements, it was not possible to do the same for the daylight simulation 
program Procedural Compute. In the daylight provision metric described in 
EN17037, DLA, the illuminance level in each node in a given grid is calculated for 
each hour of a year. As such, field measurements of the indoor illuminance levels 
should be carried out for an entire year to have enough data for a manual DLA 
calculation. Furthermore, separate measurements should be carried out 
simultaneously across the entire room and the measurement nodes should be 
placed in a grid. As the measurement duration for such an experiment exceeded 
the standardized period for a 30 ECTS master’s thesis, this option was not a 
possibility. Alternatively, the simulation tool could have been calibrated against a 
simulation in another validated daylight software. As this method is not based on 
actual data, such calibration would not serve to determine whether the achieved 
results were realistic all the same. One might argue that this uncertainty affiliated 
with the simulation software might explain why the case of WWR_50 Scenario 6 did 
not comply with the design value even though the design of the room followed the 
recommended guidelines by MicroShade [14].   
5.2. Sensitivity 
As the results in building performance simulations are highly sensitive to variations 
in input parameters, a detailed sensitivity analysis could have provided greater 
robustness in the results. In 2017, the Danish Building Research Institute (SBi), the 
Danish Technological Institute (TI), and the two consultancy offices NIRAS and 
MOE created a list of input parameters and ranked them according to their relative 
influence on the results of building energy models. Especially relevant to this study 
is the installed cooling capacity (W/m2), the window-to-wall ratio (%), the internal 
gains (W/m2), hereunder the concurrency factor of the specified occupation, and 
the activation setpoint for external shading (lux) [12]. Other important parameters  
include the depth of the room, the louver control rotation segment and the chosen 
material reflectance chosen for the daylight simulation. 
5.3. Repair and maintenance 
A vital issue which has not been further researched in this thesis due to time 
constraints is that of repair and maintenance costs. According to the informant from 
Blendex, the largest solar shading providers and repair company in Denmark, the 
uncertainties about the repair and maintenance is the main reason why people 
choose solar coated glazing units and increased ventilation flow rates over external 
shading solutions.  
==PAGE:45==
 
 
42 
Discussion 
InSync Solar documents the most common repairs in external shading devices 
concern the cables and the motors. Though most motors only have a warranty for 
5 years, InSync Solar has registered operation motors, which lasted 25 years of use. 
Over time, the cables pulling the shading fabric or slats may gradually loose 
tension. This may cause the fabric to become loose, which reduces the shading 
efficiency of the product. In worst case, the fabric might get stuck as a consequence 
of loose cables [23]. To avoid this phenomenon, Blendex advises annual 
inspections of the external shading [21]. One of the most important consideration 
in shading control design is the maximal wind speed as high wind gusts over longer 
periods could tear screen fabrics or bend slats. According to InSync Solar, external 
shading devices should be retracted if wind speeds above 16 m/s occur [23]. 
However, modern screen products with a zip-edge function can withstand wind 
speeds of 20 m/s according to Blendex [21]. The Blendex informant highlights that 
the greatest problems with external shading devices occur when the annual 
inspection is neglected, as annual inspections allow smaller problems to be 
adjusted before the reparation costs accumulate. 
In the contrary, the only maintenance costs associated with integrated solutions 
such as solar-control glazing units and the MicroShade film is window cleaning. As 
such, these solutions demand less attention and result in substantially fewer costs 
during the building operation. Still, the study illustrated that solutions with solar-
control glazings had a relatively larger energy demand compared to solutions with 
external shading. When looking at both thermal comfort, maintenance costs and 
energy demand the solutions using MicroShade perform relatively, as the product 
calls for low maintenance while creating favourable conditions in terms of thermal 
comfort and energy demand. Even so, the findings illustrated that very large 
glazing ratios were required to create sufficient daylight conditions using said 
product. 
Detailed life-cycle cost analysis is thus needed to quantify the economical 
advantages and trade-offs caused by external solar shading. Such calculations are 
beneficial as they may highlight both initial costs, maintenance costs and costs 
related to relative reduction in the energy consumption of a given building.

Conclusion 
The purpose of this thesis was to investigate whether the use of external shading 
as a passive means of cooling would be a beneficial solution in order to meet the 
current demands and satisfy both the thermal and visual comfort of occupants in 
office spaces. This was done by conducting both building energy modelling (BEM) 
simulations and daylight simulations for a generic shoebox office using various 
external and integrated solar shading systems. The shoebox represented a generic 
section of a landscape office space with four workstations.  
Before analysing the effects of solar shading via the shoebox model, a model 
calibration experiment was conducted to verify whether the chosen BEM tool IDA 
ICE 5.0 beta 20 was suited for the project. This was done by conduction field 
measurements of the thermal climate in an office space at the Technical University 
of Denmark for a week. The experiment consisted of 20 measurement periods 
where the state of the external shading device was altered at the start of each 
period. A virtual replicate of the field experiment was then modelled in IDA ICE 
and the results were compared. The model calibration illustrated that the software 
was able to compute relatively reliable results when looking specifically at the 
thermal conditions in relation to external shading devices. The mean deviation 
relative to the field measurements was 0.7°C. Though the software proved to 
provide relatively reliable results regarding external solar shading, the model 
calibration illustrated that the replication of natural ventilation in the software was 
inadequate. This defect was however deemed of lesser importance in relation to 
this study, as it is customary to ventilate office spaces mechanically rather than 
through natural ventilation. 
The shading device study consisted of two different shoebox models with a 
window-to-wall ratio of 40 % and 50 % respectively. 10 different scenarios with 
various external or integrated shading devices was simulated for each shoebox 
model. Each scenario was then simulated in both eastern, southern, and western 
orientation. Three variables were investigated in the BEM simulation: Overheating, 
draught risk, and energy consumption. The design values for overheating were 
considered met if only 100 hours of year exceeded operating temperatures of 26°C 
and only 25 hours exceeded the upper temperature limit of 27°C. The approach of 
the simulation was to create one simulation which met the overheating design 
value exactly and then another simulation where only 20-30 hours exceeded 26°C 
to create greater robustness in the results. This amounted to 120 BEM simulations. 
The study found that the four solar shading solutions which were best at 
maintaining the desired temperature interval without creating undesirably high risk 
for draught were respectively: 
1. Scenario 6a: 3-pane lowE glazing with MS-F 
2. Scenario 4: Dynamic horisontal louvers 
3. Scenario 2: Venetian blinds with a slat angle of 45° 
4. Scenario 3: Screen with LT = 0.25 
The study found that all of these solutions performed well in terms of thermal 
comfort for window-to-wall ratios of 40 % as well as 50 %. However, conclusions 
concerning their performance for higher window-to-wall ratios cannot be made 
solely based on the results in this study. These solutions proved to reduce the 
energy demand of the zone by 3-9 % towards west, 12-17 % towards south and 5-
8 % towards east relative to the energy use from the Baseline scenario. On the other 
hand, both Scenario 2a with venetian blinds with a slat angle of 0° and Scenario 6 
==PAGE:48==

using 2-pane lowE glazing with MS-F increased the energy demand towards east 
and west according to the results. 
The daylight provision met the design value threshold if the indoor illuminance 
level exceeded 300 lux in half of the zone for half of the daylit hours on an annual 
basis. The simulations were conducted using the Radiance three-phase method 
and entailed a total of 60 daylight simulations. The study found that the three 
solutions which performed best in terms of daylight provision were respectively: 
1. Baseline: No shading, glazing LT = 0.64 
2. Scenario 2a: Venetian blinds with a slat angle of 0° 
3. Scenario 1: No shading, glazing LT = 0.55 
None of the other solutions created daylight conditions which satisfied the design 
value. The results in this study clearly illustrates the negative effect of solar shading 
devices on the annual daylight availability in office spaces. The study also 
highlighted that the negative effect on the daylight conditions created by the 
shading was larger for external shading than for the integrated solutions in most 
instances.  
The study found that the solutions from the thermal and the visual assessment were 
contradictory and in fact none of the chosen shading scenarios satisfied all of the 
chosen design values at the same time. As a result of this finding, the report has 
highlighted a range of additional steps which must be taken to achieve a more 
wholesome understanding of the effects these various shading types can have on 
both the visual comfort, thermal comfort and energy consumption of office 
buildings. The aim of the suggested future steps was to create optimisations in 
regards to both the geometry and the control of each shading type and these 
optimized shading devices may provide a more fair evaluation of the relative effect 
of the devices. 
In total, the study found that both external and integrated solar shading solutions 
could serve as a passive means of providing thermal comfort in office spaces when 
installed and controlled correctly. Still, none of these solutions created desirable 
daylight conditions. However, it must be underlined that the findings of this thesis 
are not conclusive as optimal solutions for each shading type must be found to 
make fair comparisons.

DRY2013 weather file 
The building energy model has been simulated using the DRY2013 weather file, 
according to BR18 §386. DRY2013 is a design reference year based on weather 
data from the period 2001-2010 measured by the Danish Meteorological Institute. 
[25] The DRY2013 weather file includes the following measurements: 
 
Temperature (°C)  
 
Relative humidity (%)  
 
Wind speed (m/s)  
 
Wind direction (°)1  
 
Atmospheric pressure (hPa)  
 
Global radiation (W/m2)  
 
Cloud cover (%)  
 
Sea temperature (°C)  
 
Diffuse irradiance (W/m2)  
 
Illuminance (lux) 
The data set does not contain average values from continuous measurements 
during the given 9 years. Rather, the design reference year aims to represent typical 
weather conditions in Denmark. As such, the weather data is constructed from 12 
representable months from the period 2001-2010. [26][27]
