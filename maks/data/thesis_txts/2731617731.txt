Abstract 
Starfish is a possible new source of omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids which is essential for maintaining 
human health. Current fishing practices include the storage of starfish in an empty tank on the fishing boat 
for several hours after capture. Omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids are prone to oxidation and 
preservation techniques at the initial processing steps are required to maintain a high quality. 
This thesis investigates the most efficient method to prevent oxidation of starfish oil extracted from the 
starfish species Asterias rubens. These methods include storage of starfish in seawater immediately after 
capture, a non-seawater treatment with a dipping-based strategy at return to harbour using a Duralox 
MANC-213 dipping solution containing a mixture of antioxidants (tocopherols, ascorbic acid, rosemary 
extract, and citric acid), and different storage temperatures (cold and ambient storage). The samples were 
evaluated over a 4-day period. The level of oxidation was evaluated by determination of peroxide values 
(PV) and free fatty acid content. High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) was applied for evaluation 
of naturally occurring tocopherols within the starfish oil. 
The outcome of the thesis suggests that all proposed methods improve the quality of the starfish oil. 
Storage in seawater was shown to delay the onset of oxidation with an initially lower peroxide value. The 
antioxidant dipping inhibit oxidation with higher initial peroxide value that remains stable over time. Low 
storage temperature improved oxidative stability regardless of handling after capture. The most efficient 
method is proposed as storage in an antioxidant treatment immediately after capture in combination with 
low storage temperature. The findings of this thesis show the potential in implementing preservation 
methods to enhance the oxidative stability of starfish oil during processing as a result of the initial handling.

1 Introduction  
The nutritional benefits of omega-3 fatty acids, particularly eicosapentaenoic acid and docosahexaenoic 
acid are essential for maintaining human health. Traditionally, the source of these beneficial 
polyunsaturated fatty acids has been fish or oil derived from fish (Chaudhary, et al., 2021). However, as the 
global population increases there is a growing demand for omega-3 fatty acids through sustainable means 
as to avoid overexploitation of the oceans (Jacobsen, Let, Nielsen, & Meyer, 2008). This has prompted the 
search for alternate sources. 
Starfish has shown to be a promising candidate. Current applications involve the use of starfish in lieu of fish 
meal for animal feed, but the omega-3 PUFA-rich oil has the potential to serve as a reliable and alternative 
source of omega-3-rich oil for human consumption (Sørensen, Getachew, & Jacobsen, Starfish (Asterias 
rubens) as a New Source of Marine Lipids: Effect of Season, Size and Oil Extraction Methods, 2022). 
Preliminary experiments involving the extraction of oil from starfish meal have produced results that 
necessitate a critical evaluation of the current practices regarding the initial stages right after harvest on the 
fishing boat and at the fishery to ensure the best product quality (Papakonstantinou, 2023). 
The thesis aims to investigate different methods for prohibiting the deteriorating oxidation processes of the 
lipids in the starfish. These include submersion in water, antioxidant application, and different storage 
temperatures and storage time. The quality is assessed by evaluating critical quality parameters including 
free fatty acid content, peroxide values, and tocopherol content.

2 Background  
2.1 Starfish 
Asteroids or more commonly known as sea stars or starfish are part of the taxonomic phylum 
Echinodermata. Starfish are closely related to sea urchins, sea cucumbers, sand dollars, brittle stars, and sea 
lilies since they share the same phylum. The common denominator of this group is their endoskeleton 
which is comprised of calcareous ossicles (Blowes, et al., 2017). These ossicles can project outwards as 
spines on the backsides of the animals. Because of this physical attribute the group got the name 
echinoderm which means “spiny skin” (Ruppert, Fox, & Barnes, 2004). 
2.1.1 
Physiology 
Asteroids are typically known for their five arms, which gives its characteristic star shaped look, but some 
species have more than five arms. The arms of an asteroid are called rays and protrude outward from the 
central disc. Located in the centre of the central disc on the underside of the asteroid is the mouth. A grove 
extends outwards from the mouth towards the tip of each ray. In this grove the tube feet are located, and 
they are referred to as podia. Asteroids also have sensory tube feet which are placed at the tip of the rays. 
On the top side on the central disc the asteroids anus is located (Ruppert, Fox, & Barnes, 2004) (Santos, 
Haesaerts, Jangoux, & Flammang, 2005). 
The asteroid’s tube feet are used for locomotion and are part of an intricate system called the water-
vascular system (Santos, Haesaerts, Jangoux, & Flammang, 2005). Besides locomotion the water-vascular 
system is also used for respiration by circulating gas (Ruppert, Fox, & Barnes, 2004). All the tube feet on a 
ray are connected to the radial canal by lateral canals. The radial canal is present in all the asteroid’s rays 
and runs from the tip of the ray to the central disc where it is connected to the circumoral ring canal. Each 
individual tube foot is connected to the ampulla, which is a muscular sac in the perivisceral coelom 
(Ruppert, Fox, & Barnes, 2004). The perivisceral coelom also contains a high volume of coelomic fluid, 
constituting approximately 20% of the total starfish body mass. This fluid serves as a transport system for 
metabolites, nutrients, signalling molecules, and immune system components in the form of coelomocytes 
that partake in the early repair phase during arm regeneration. The coelomic fluid content fluctuates 
depending on feeding, capture method, and if the starfish has sustained any injuries (Shabelnikov, Bobkov, 
Sharlaimova, & Petukhova, 2019). Also, the sexual organs, the gonads, are found within the perivisceral 
coelom along with the digestive system, the pyloric caeca (Wilkie & Carnevali, 2023). 
2.1.2 
Habitat and growth dynamics 
Asterias rubens also referred to as the common European starfish can be found in the waters from the arctic 
part of Norway all the way to Senegal along with the Mediterranean Sea (Guillou, Joly-Turquin, Leyzour, 
Pernet, & Dubois, 2012). It can be found in all Danish waters and is the most common starfish in Limfjorden 
(Holtegaard, Gramkow, Petersen, & Dolmer, 2008). A. rubens can be found along the coast in the littoral 
zone all the way down to a depth of 200 meters. This adaptability is advantageous, since their main prey 
Mytilus edulis (blue mussels) form extensive beds ranging from low intertidal to shallow subtidal zones 
(Saier, 2001). A. rubens is an extra-oral feeder that attaches its tube feet to the shells of bivalves allowing 
them to exert enough force to open the shell and initiate extra-oral digestion (Saier, 2001).

page 7 of 31 
 
The growth pattern of A. rubens is highly plastic, which allow them to adjust as a response to environmental 
fluctuations by shrinking and increasing in size. Growth dynamics are influenced by seasonal changes with 
elevated growth in summer and reduced or negative growth in winter. It is also affected by habitat 
characteristics with intertidal populations being notably smaller compared to subtidal populations, which 
may hint at a potential migration of starfish from one habitat to another over time (Guillou, Joly-Turquin, 
Leyzour, Pernet, & Dubois, 2012). Other factors include availability of food, which is also seen for sea 
urchins, and lowered salinity, emersion, and wave exposure leading to decreased feeding rates (Guillou, 
Joly-Turquin, Leyzour, Pernet, & Dubois, 2012). As such size cannot be used as an indicator of starfish age 
besides when used for juveniles who exhibit continual growth for at least one year. However, if food 
conditions are poor the juveniles enter a “waiting stage” where growth is delayed for several months 
(Guillou, Joly-Turquin, Leyzour, Pernet, & Dubois, 2012). 
When being removed from their aquatic habitat and into the air, the starfish’s hydraulic system drains. This 
reduction as a result of stress leads to a reduction in mass. This is however reversible by resubmission in 
water, but the time required for recovery and rehydration varies with larger starfish experiencing a bigger 
challenge compared to their smaller counterparts (Burgaard, Carstensen, Fuhrman, Saurel, & O'Neill, 2023). 
Burgaard et. al. compared the mass of starfish collected by handpicking, trawling, and a new hydrodynamic 
catching method and found that handpicking resulted in the least loss of mass due to a gentler approach 
(Burgaard, Carstensen, Fuhrman, Saurel, & O'Neill, 2023).  
2.2 Lipids 
Lipids are long aliphatic chains with a carboxylic acid functional group at the end. Lipids mainly occur as 
complex compounds called glycerollipids, which is an esterification of the carboxylic group on the fatty acid 
to a glycerol. Two groups of these are the triglycerides and the phospholipids (Frankel E. N., 1998) 
(Chaudhary, et al., 2021). Lipids can either be saturated or unsaturated with the unsaturated group being 
further divided into monounsaturated and polyunsaturated lipids. Monounsaturated fatty acids (MUFAs) 
are characterised by having a single double bond while polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) feature two or 
more double bonds separated by a methylene group (Frankel E. N., 1998). Lipids abundant in unsaturated 
fatty acids are commonly called oils and remain liquid at room temperature due to their low melting point 
(Chaudhary, et al., 2021).  
PUFAs are essential fatty acids meaning they are not synthesized by humans but instead must be consumed 
from food. Eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA, C20:5 n-3) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA, C22:6 n-3) are two well-
known omega-3 PUFAs that contribute significant health benefits in adults such as lowering the risk of 
cardiovascular disease and diabetes, anti-inflammatory effects and improved brain functions (Sioen, 
Devroe, Inghels, Terwecoren, & De Henauw, 2010) (Jacobsen, Let, Nielsen, & Meyer, 2008) (Arab-Tehrany, et 
al., 2012) (Farvin, Grejsen, & Jacobsen, 2012). Adequate intake during pregnancy and lactation period 
supports the neural and visual development in babies and reduces the risk of premature birth (Garcia-
Moreno, Damberg, Chronakis, & Jacobsen, 2017) (Sioen, Devroe, Inghels, Terwecoren, & De Henauw, 2010). 
The food source most abundant in these omega-3 PUFAs are fish and other seafood in particular oily fish 
such as salmon and halibut, which contains 13.5 and 11.7 grams of lipids per 100 grams of fish, respectively 
(Chaudhary, et al., 2021) (Arab-Tehrany, et al., 2012). However, despite the many nutritional benefits, 
consumption does not meet the recommended omega-3 PUFA intake in most European countries (Sioen, 
Devroe, Inghels, Terwecoren, & De Henauw, 2010) (Jacobsen, Let, Nielsen, & Meyer, 2008). At the same 
==PAGE:9==
 
 
page 8 of 31 
 
time an increasing population increases the demand for seafood which the oceans cannot supply leading to 
concerns about overexploitation. This scenario has initiated the exploration of alternative omega-3 PUFAs 
(Sioen, Devroe, Inghels, Terwecoren, & De Henauw, 2010). 
Previous studies done by Sørensen et al. have shown that A. rubens can be used as a sustainable raw 
material in the production of omega-3 PUFA rich oil. Analysis of the extracted oil have shown that the 
starfish have a lipid content of about 105 – 150 g/kg of dry matter and that the oil contains phospholipids, 
EPA, and DHA (Sørensen, Getachew, & Jacobsen, Starfish (Asterias rubens) as a New Source of Marine 
Lipids: Effect of Season, Size and Oil Extraction Methods, 2022). Another study by van der Heide et.al. found 
the fat levels to be lower and with monthly fluctuations at 53.0 - 9.24 g/kg of dry matter. Smaller starfish (0 
- 7.5 cm) was found to have lower yearly fluctuations in lipid content compared to medium (7.5 - 15 cm) 
and large starfish (>15 cm) (van der Heide, Møller, Petersen, & Nørgaard, 2018). These variations in fat 
content were considered to be related to accumulation of lipids in either the gonads or the pyloric caeca 
depending on reproductive phase and possibly environmental factors.   
2.3 Oxidation of lipids 
Lipids may undergo oxidation processes that significantly impact their quality. PUFAs are highly prone to 
oxidation because of their unsaturated nature (Garcia-Moreno, Damberg, Chronakis, & Jacobsen, 2017) 
(Jacobsen, Let, Nielsen, & Meyer, 2008). Lipid oxidation causes a range of adverse effects such as changes in 
the functional (e.g., nutritional value and shelf-life) and sensory characteristics (e.g., formation of off-
flavours, texture, and colour) (Bayram & Decker, 2023)  (Frankel E. N., 2005). Such changes in quality are 
often unacceptable and oxidation may even lead to the formation of carcinogenic substances (Cheng & 
Ockerman, 2004) (Pop & Mihalescu, 2017). There are a few forms of oxidation that affects lipids. The main 
form of lipid oxidation is by the process called autoxidation. Photooxidation (catalysed by ultraviolet light) 
and lipoxygenase are two other types and these only differ from autooxidation in the initial step (O'Connor 
& O'Brien, 2006) (Frankel E. N., 2005).  
Autoxidation happens through a free radical chain mechanism which can be divided into three steps: 
initiation, propagation, and termination. Each of the three steps are dominated by an action that defines 
the step (O'Connor & O'Brien, 2006) (Frankel E. N., 2005).  
During the first step (initiation) the unsaturated lipids are destabilised through radicalisation. The 
radicalisation can happen as a result of an interaction with compounds such as free radicals, metal ions, and 
heat (Cheng & Ockerman, 2004) (O'Connor & O'Brien, 2006). The compound that causes this destabilisation 
are called initiators (I•), because they initialise the process by stripping a hydrogen atom of the unsaturated 
lipids (LH) to create free radicals of the unsaturated lipids (L•). 
For the second step (propagation) the free lipid radicals (L•) react with oxygen to produce peroxyl radicals 
(LOO•). The peroxyl radicals (LOO•) can thereafter react with lipids (LH) to create lipid hydroperoxides 
(LOOH) and free lipid radicals (L•).  
In the third step (termination) the produced radicals react with one another to produce lipid polymers. 
Depending on what types of radicals have been created there will be a variety of different polymers.

The hydroperoxides (LOOH) formed during the propagation step are also called primary oxidation products. 
They are often odourless and tasteless but can be used to assess the extent of lipid oxidation (O'Connor & 
O'Brien, 2006). The primary oxidation products react further to form secondary oxidation products 
including various carbonyl compounds (e.g., aldehydes and ketones) that contribute to off-flavours and 
other undesirable sensory characteristics of oxidised lipids (Aubourg, 2000) (O'Connor & O'Brien, 2006). 
The oxidation rate depends both on the lipids present, types of initiators, and environmental factors such as 
the salt content, temperature and pH (Osborn-Barnes & Akoh, 2003) (Frankel E. N., 2005). Amongst metal 
ions iron and copper stand out as primary initiators of lipid oxidation. The metals are typically found bound 
to proteins within the biological tissue, however processing such as harvesting, slaughtering, salting and 
cooking release them and they can engage to form free radicals (Bayram & Decker, 2023) (O'Connor & 
O'Brien, 2006) (Frankel E. N., 2005). 
The oxygen concentration needed for lipid oxidation depends on the food matrix, since oxygen has a greater 
solubility in non-polar than in polar solvents (O'Connor & O'Brien, 2006). The reactive oxygen species 
singlet oxygen is very electrophilic and therefore will react more readily with the unsaturated lipids. It may 
be formed from triplet oxygen (O'Connor & O'Brien, 2006). 
2.4 Antioxidants 
To inhibit lipid oxidation antioxidants are applied as they possess several mechanisms that act upon both 
the initiators and the oxidation steps (Pop & Mihalescu, 2017) (Bayram & Decker, 2023).  
Primary antioxidants act as free radical scavengers and are characterised by their ability to delay the 
initiation step and interrupting the propagation step by donating hydrogen atoms to the lipid radicals 
(Osborn-Barnes & Akoh, 2003) (Frankel E. N., 2005). The lipid radicals then become nonradical species with 
no further oxidising abilities. Their antioxidant ability is determined by their reduction potential, structure, 
and energy of the antioxidant radical. Notably, phenolic antioxidants exhibit reduced energy dynamics in 
their radical form due to resonance delocalization (Bayram & Decker, 2023). However, due to hydrogen 
donation these free radical scavengers are depleted over time and lose their ability to protect unsaturated 
fatty acids (Bayram & Decker, 2023). Tocopherols represent examples of primary antioxidants (Osborn-
Barnes & Akoh, 2003) (Bayram & Decker, 2023).

Secondary antioxidants work by inhibiting the initiators of the lipid oxidation (Frankel E. N., 2005). Metal 
chelators create a steric effect to prevent direct contact between transition metals and hydroperoxides, vary 
the reduction potential of transition metals and reduces metal ion solubility (Tunieva, Nasonova, & 
Revutskaya, 2023) (Bayram & Decker, 2023). As oxygen scavengers, secondary antioxidants prevent the 
formation of peroxides by quenching singlet oxygen (Osborn-Barnes & Akoh, 2003) (Tunieva, Nasonova, & 
Revutskaya, 2023). Furthermore, secondary antioxidants are characterized by a substantially lower redox 
potential than the primary antioxidants and may partake in antioxidant regeneration by thermodynamically 
reducing oxidized free radical scavengers back to their original form (Bayram & Decker, 2023). Examples of 
secondary antioxidants include citric acid (Osborn-Barnes & Akoh, 2003). 
The use of synthetic antioxidants is associated with potential mutagenic and carcinogenic effects, which 
raise health concerns (Aubourg, 2000) (Bayram & Decker, 2023). Recognizing a growing consumer 
preference for clean labels, a shift towards natural antioxidants is seen (Bayram & Decker, 2023). Natural 
antioxidants have GRAS status (“generally recognized as safe”) and offers a safer and more appealing 
alternative in lipid preservation strategies (Frankel E. N., 2005). 
2.4.1 
Tocopherol 
Tocopherols belong to the vitamin E family and are common antioxidants applied for their chemical stability, 
widespread availability, and low cost (Bora, Tongbram, Mahnot, Mahanta, & Badwaik, 2022) (Pop & 
Mihalescu, 2017). Tocopherol is comprised of four homologues (alpha, beta, gamma, and delta) with the 
variations between them depending on the number and position of methyl groups in and around the 
chromanol ring (Bora, Tongbram, Mahnot, Mahanta, & Badwaik, 2022) (Frankel E. N., 2005). Alpha-
tocopherol is the most naturally occurring form, consists of a tri-methylated 6-chromanol ring, and exhibits 
remarkable antioxidative properties by being the beat singlet oxygen quencher out of all the tocopherols 
(Frankel E. N., 2005). Both beta- and gamma-tocopherol are dimethylated 6-chromanol rings, whereas 
delta-tocopherol is a monomethylated 6-chromanol ring (Bora, Tongbram, Mahnot, Mahanta, & Badwaik, 
2022). Tocopherols are lipid-soluble, but their hydroxyl group and phytol chains provide them with 
amphiphilic properties (Bayram & Decker, 2023). Fats and oils are found to naturally contain 1-2% non-
glyceride components which include tocopherols (Frankel E. N., 2005). 
Tocopherols are highly potent antioxidants and since they are thermally stable a significant proportion (60-
70%) remains in oils after processing (Jacobsen, et al., 2001) (Frankel E. N., 2005). They are classified as 
primary antioxidants and react directly with free radicals of PUFAs hence preventing the propagation of free 
radical chain reactions (Bora, Tongbram, Mahnot, Mahanta, & Badwaik, 2022) (Jacobsen, et al., 2001) (Let, 
Jacobsen, Pham, & Meyer, 2005) (Kanarovskii & Yaltychenko, 2024). This process involves the transfer of a 
hydrogen atom from the hydroxyl group on the chromanol ring to the peroxide radical of PUFAs. The 
resulting tocopherol radical is resonance stabilized inside the chromanol ring and the tocopherol radical can 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
page 11 of 31 
 
form self-coupling products (dimers and trimers) (Bora, Tongbram, Mahnot, Mahanta, & Badwaik, 2022). 
The effectiveness of tocopherols as antioxidants can be attributed to their reaction rate being 100,000 times 
faster than lipids when dealing with lipid peroxy radicals (Bora, Tongbram, Mahnot, Mahanta, & Badwaik, 
2022). Tocopherols may also react with singlet oxygen and other reactive oxygen species by two-electron 
transfer to irreversibly produce tocopherols quinones (Bora, Tongbram, Mahnot, Mahanta, & Badwaik, 
2022).  
2.4.2 
Ascorbic acid 
Ascorbic acid, commonly known as vitamin C, is a water-soluble molecule featuring a lactone ring, two 
enolic hydroxyls, and both a primary and secondary alcohol group (Santos, et al., 2022) (Gegotek & 
Skrzydlewska, 2022) (Bayram & Decker, 2023). As a primary antioxidant it is capable of donating hydrogen 
atoms forming a relatively stable ascorbyl radicals due to low electron potential and resonance stability 
(Santos, et al., 2022) (Let, Jacobsen, Pham, & Meyer, 2005). Ascorbic acid donates electrons from the 
double bond between the second and third carbon and therefore acts as a reducing agent (Santos, et al., 
2022) (Gegotek & Skrzydlewska, 2022). It also acts as oxygen scavenger and metal chelator (Let, Jacobsen, 
Pham, & Meyer, 2005) (Frankel E. N., 2005). Finally, it has shown to partake in antioxidant regeneration 
(Cheng & Ockerman, 2004) (O'Connor & O'Brien, 2006). However, while it acts as an antioxidant at higher 
concentrations it has the potential to exhibit prooxidant behaviour at lower concentrations in the presence 
of metals (Frankel E. N., 2005). 
2.4.3 
Rosemary extract 
Rosemary extract has emerged as a potent antioxidant with additional antimicrobial activity (Tunieva, 
Nasonova, & Revutskaya, 2023) (Jaswir, Che Man, & Kitts, 2000). It has shown to have the same high radical 
scavenging activity as found in the synthetic antioxidant butylhydroxytoluene (BHT) and alpha-tocopherol 
(Tunieva, Nasonova, & Revutskaya, 2023). The extract contains various components with antioxidative 
properties. The polyphenols carnosic acid and carnosol are capable of scavenging peroxyl radicals, aromatic 
transformations, and formation of metal complexes (Tunieva, Nasonova, & Revutskaya, 2023) (Frankel E. N., 
2005) (Kim & Choe, 2018). Carnosic acid consist of three six-membered rings including a dihydric phenolic 
ring and a free carboxylic acid, and carnosol is a derivative of carnosic acid containing a lactone ring (Frankel 
E. N., 2005). Other compounds found in rosemary extract, such as rosmarinic acid, hesperidin, and 
isoprenoid quinones, contribute to its antioxidant profile by scavenging free radicals and acting as chelating 
agents for reaction oxygen species (Tunieva, Nasonova, & Revutskaya, 2023). 
2.4.4 
Citric acid 
Citric acid is one of the earliest antioxidants used for prevention of lipid oxidation and it is still used due to 
being economic (Frankel E. N., 2005). It is a secondary antioxidant and hence does not have free radical 
scavenging activity (Bayram & Decker, 2023). Instead it retards oxidation through metal chelation and 
oxygen scavenging (Pop & Mihalescu, 2017) (Bayram & Decker, 2023) (O'Connor & O'Brien, 2006) (Frankel E. 
N., 2005). It combines the photo-oxidative metal ions with free radicals of carboxyl or hydroxyl groups, 
thereby effectively quenching catalytic oxygen (Singh, Kaldate, & Bisht, 2022) (Osborn-Barnes & Akoh, 
2003). Besides the application as antioxidant, it is also widely used as acidifier, flavouring agent, and 
stabilizer (Singh, Kaldate, & Bisht, 2022).

2.5 Research objectives and hypothesis 
The aim of this project was to investigate the quality of oil extracted from starfish and to determine what 
affects the quality of the oil after capture of starfish.  
The research goals listed below were created to answer the project aims and hypothesis.  
• 
Analyse key quality parameters of extracted starfish oil such as free fatty acid content, peroxide 
value, and tocopherol content for various storage conditions and times.  
 
• 
Evaluate the key quality parameters of extracted starfish oil for various storage conditions to 
determine the best handling practices. 
 
• 
Develop a recommended guideline for best handling of starfish for optimal quality of starfish oil. 
This guideline is based on the findings of the key quality parameters but should also take into 
consideration the practical possibilities.

3 Methodology 
3.1 Conceptual experimental design 
3.1.1 
Preliminary visit to the fishery 
A preliminary visit to the fishery was conducted to gain an understanding of the regular procedures and 
limitations during starfish capture on the fishing boats. Another objective for the visit was to evaluate the 
possibilities of treatment methods during catching of starfish to improve the oxidative stability of the 
starfish lipids already on the boat. This visit established the parameters for the project. 
The fishing boats at the fishery were primarily used for mussels and later their purpose was expanded to 
include starfish. When the fishermen were out catching starfish, their primary target was starfish and

3.1.2 
Intended full project design 
During catching the starfish should be divided into two groups (Figure 3). One group should be stored under 
the regular conditions. These were in open air with no form for cooling in the holding containers. The 
second group should be stored in seawater. When the fishing boat returns to the harbour with the starfish a 
sample from each of the treatments on the boat is collected and frozen.  
The starfish that was stored in seawater at the boat should be divided into five groups: transportation, 
stored for two days at cold temperature, stored for four days at cold temperature, stored for two days at 
room temperature, and stored for four days at room temperature.  
The starfish that was stored at regular conditions on the boat should be treated by four different methods: 
antioxidant dipping with Duralox MANC-213 (tocopherol, ascorbic acid, rosemary extract, and citric acid), 
antioxidant dipping with Herbalox (rosemary extract), antioxidant dipping with tocopherol and ascorbic 
acid, and seawater dipping. Each of the different group are than divided into five groups: transportation,

3.3 Treatment of the starfish 
The starfish have been stored in different medias, at different temperatures, and for different periods of 
time. This was performed to investigate the effects of the different treatments on the quality of the lipids in 
the starfish.  
3.3.1 
Antioxidant dipping

without water or cooling. These conditions were kept for the duration of the fishing trip. After the boat 
returned to harbour the starfish were divided into six groups: RT-F, AO-T.REF, AO-2-COLD, AO-4-COLD, AO-2-

box containing cooling with elements.

3.4 Analysis of starfish 
To evaluate the quality of the starfish oil the starfish were first pretreated by mincing to a homogenous 
mass and thereafter the lipids were extracted using the Bligh & Dyer method with a reduced amount of 
solvent. For characterisation and quality determination of the extracted oil peroxide value, free fatty acid, 
and tocopherol was measured by different techniques described below.  
3.4.1 
Pretreatment 
The frozen starfish were cut into smaller pieces and refrozen in liquid nitrogen. The starfish pieces were 
thereafter minced to a homogenous mass using a Waring 8011 laboratory blender (Merck, Darmstadt, 
Germany). The minces starfish samples were thereafter individually packed into plastic bags and refrozen 
(−40 °𝐶) until extraction. 
3.4.2 
Oil extraction and oil content 
The extraction was performed on the minced starfish in accordance with protocol FF-109.09 “Content of 
oil” from the National Food Institute at DTU. The extraction was performed in duplicates. 
For each extraction 15 g of sample were weighed and 20 mL methanol were added to the sample. The 
samples were thereafter placed in an ice bath to keep them cool. Chloroform was added to the sample, and 
it were mixed using a T25 digital ULTRA-TURRAX® homogenizer (IKA-Werke GmbH & Co., Staufen, Germany) 
at a speed of 15,000 rpm. After 30 seconds another 15 mL of chloroform were added to the resulting 
extraction broth. 15 mL of deionised water were added after another 30 second and after a total of 90 
seconds of mixing the extraction were stopped. The extraction broth is centrifuged for 10 minutes at 2800 
rpm and 18 °𝐶. The lipid extract was collected. 
The oil content of the lipid extract was determined gravimetrically by evaporation of the chloroform. 
3.4.3 
Peroxide value method ferric thiocyanate by spectroscopy 
The analysis was performed in accordance with protocol FF-121.03 “Peroxide value” from the National Food 
Institute at DTU.  
Two solution was prepared beforehand, ammonium thiocyanate and ferrous chloride. The ammonium 
thiocyanate solution was made by weighing of 3.0 g of ammonium thiocyanate and dissolving it in 10 mL of 
deionised water. It is bubbled with nitrogen for 5 minutes and thereafter stored cold and dark. The ferrous 
chloride solution was made by weighing of 0.25 g of ferrous sulphate heptahydrate and dissolving it in 25 
mL of deionised water under constant stirring. Meanwhile 0.20 g of barium chloride dihydrate were 
dissolved in 25 mL of deionised water and added to the ferrous sulphate solution. 1 mL of 10 M 
hydrochloric acid was added to the solution, and it was thereafter placed cold and dark for 1 hour. It was 
then filtered and bubbled with nitrogen for 5 minutes. It was stored cold and dark.  
A lipid extract quantity of 0.5 – 2.1 g was weighed and dried to complete dryness with nitrogen. It was 
thereafter redissolved in 10 mL of chloroform-methanol solution (7:3 v/v). 50 µL of ammonium thiocyanate 
(30% w/v) and ferrous chloride (0.016 M) was added to the redissolved extract. The solutions were 
thereafter placed in dark for 5 minutes and afterwards the absorbance was measured at 500 nm. The 
spectrometer used was a UV-Vis spectrometer UV-1280 (Shimadzu Scientific Instruments, Columbia, MD, 
USA).  
==PAGE:20==
 
 
page 19 of 31 
 
3.4.4 
Peroxide value method iodometric by titration 
The analysis was performed in accordance with protocol FF-110.06 “Peroxide value” from the National Food 
Institute at DTU. 
A lipid extract quantity of 8 – 10 g was weighed, and 15 mL of acetic acid was added to it. The solution was 
aerated with nitrogen for 5 minutes and afterwards 1.2 mL of saturated potassium iodide was added to the 
solution. The solution was placed in the dark for 5 minutes and afterwards 40 mL of deionised water and 2 
mL of 0.5% starch solution was added. The solution was titrated with 0.002 M sodium thiosulphate until the 
water phase was colourless.  
3.4.5 
Free fatty acids 
For the quantification of free fatty acids in the samples the protocol FF-112.07 “Free fatty acids” from the 
National Food Institute at DTU were used with a change in the lipid extract quantity used to 5 g. 
25 mL of ethanol (96%) and 5 drops of phenolphthalein indicator were added to each sample. The samples 
were titrated with a 0.1 M sodium hydroxide solution until a colour change was observed from yellow to 
orange. 
3.4.6 
Tocopherol 
The analysis was performed in accordance with protocol FF-113.07 “Tocopherols” from the National Food 
Institute at DTU. 
A lipid extract quantity of 1.5 - 2.1 g was weighed and evaporated to complete dryness with nitrogen. The 
sample quantity was determined based on the detectable area for the peaks. The sample was thereafter 
redissolved in 1 mL heptane and transferred to HPLC vials. 
The samples were run on the high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) system Agilent 1100 (Agilent 
Technologies, Palo Alto, CA, USA) with an autosampler. The column used was a Spherisorb (3 μm silica 
beads, 4.6 mm x 150 mm, Waters, Dublin, Ireland) with the pre-column Spherisorb (5 μm silica beads, 4.6 
mm x 10 mm, Waters, Dublin, Ireland). A fluorescence detector (G1321A) was used for detection of the 
peaks. The detector was setup with an excitation wavelength at 290 nm and an emission wavelength at 330 
nm. The mobile phase was a mixture of heptane and isopropanol (100:0.4 v/v) which was run isocratic with 
a flowrate of 1 mL/min. The sample injection volume was 20 μL. The tocopherol peaks were identified by 
comparing the retention time with a standard (613424, Calbiochem) for the α-, β-, γ-, and δ-tocopherol 
peaks. 
3.5 Statistical Analyses 
All samples were done in duplicate. Statistical analysis was performed with Excel (Microsoft 365, version 
2311). Results depicted graphically shows the mean with error bars of the standard deviation. For 
comparison of more than two datasets single-factor analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used. Student’s t-test 
was used to compare two data sets and as a post hoc test for the ANOVA. A p-value ≤ 0.05 was considered 
as statistically significant for all the tests.

4 Results and Discussion 
4.1 Peroxide value 
The peroxide value (PV) analysis was used to quantify the amounts of peroxides in the samples. This was 
done by oxidising the ferrous ions (Fe2+) to ferric ions (Fe3+) by the peroxides present in the samples. The 
ferric ions react with the thiocyanate ions to create a red ferric thiocyanate complex. The samples are 
measured using spectroscopy for the red complex. For this study it was used to assess the quality of the 
extracts, since a high number of peroxides present in the samples are a result of oxidation hence leading to 
a lower quality extract.  
The PV was measured over a period of four days for the different treatment groups (Figure 6). A pattern 
seems to be forming when comparing the seawater treatment (WT) with the antioxidant treatment (AO). 
The seawater treatments had a PV on day 0 at 21.96 ± 5.51 meq LOOH / kg oil and the cold stored samples 
(WT-COLD) showed a significant increase in PV over the four days, but the samples stored at room 
temperature (WT-ROOM) showed no significant increase over the four days. The antioxidant treatment (AO) 
on the other hand had a lower PV on day 0 at 16.56 ± 3.73 meq LOOH / kg oil and the PV on the following 
days seemed to have a stable trend for both storage temperatures (COLD and ROOM). Although, this could 
not be supported statistically since day 4 of the cold storage (AO-COLD) and day 2 of the room storage (AO-
ROOM) was significantly different from the rest of the days within the grouping.   
When comparing the same days across the different treatment groups (WT with AO) of same storage 
condition all the days was found to be statistically significant. Due to this finding, it seemed that the 
antioxidant treatment would be the better method of preserving the quality of the starfish.

4.3 Tocopherol 
The tocopherol analysis was a HPLC method used to quantify the types and amounts of tocopherols in the 
samples. In this project it was used to see the effect of the antioxidant and the tocopherols present in the 
starfish. 
The starfish analysed during this project naturally contained α-tocopherol this was observed in the seawater 
treated (WT) samples, since the other forms of tocopherol was not present. The content of α-tocopherol in 
the samples seemed to be relatively stable and vary between 15 to 25 μg / g starfish with a clear outlier for 
the antioxidant treatment with cold storage for two days (AO-2-COLD) with a higher concentration in the 
samples (Figure 13). Statistical analysis showed that the only group to be significantly similar was seawater 
treatment with storage at room temperature for two days (WT-2-ROOM).  
There were some minuscule peaks for β-tocopherol in all the samples that was dipped in antioxidant but 
what stands out was the large peak that appeared on day 4 of both the samples that was stored at room 
temperature (Figure 14). This large peak would most likely not be β-tocopherol but another compound that 
had the same retention time. Since this peak was only precent in the day 4 samples at room temperature it 
can be assumed to be a lipophilic compound that was created in the starfish under a possible 
decomposition process.

4.4 Further discussion 
The different analytical methods each had different finding and they will be summarised here.  
The spectrometric method generally showed high PV for all samples. The results also showed an upward 
inclining trend for WT-COLD and WT-ROOM, while the AO-COLD and AO-ROOM groups showed a stable 
level. Because of a dissimilarity in the data the PV was investigated and showed an inverse relationship 
between the quantity of lipid extract and the corresponding measured PV. This finding was rather 
problematic since it means that the PV could potentially be chosen and gives the data a level of uncertainty. 
After this was discovered a fixed quantity of lipid extract was weighed every time for the spectroscopic 
method to counter the uncertainty under the assumption that the slope does not change. All the samples 
treated with antioxidant had a fixed quantity of lipid extract. Therefore, the PV measurements for these 
samples may appear to be precise but may not necessarily be accurate.  
The iodometric method with titration showed a different tendency than the ferric thiocyanate method and 
that was that the seawater treatment at day 0 had a much lower PV than the day 0 regular treatment. This 
means that storage of the starfish in seawater at the boat had a positive effect on the quality of the oil. 
While this result was not based on a proper sample amount, it does show promise and it would be 
recommended to validate this experiment. This finding can be supported by (Burgaard, Carstensen, 
Fuhrman, Saurel, & O'Neill, 2023) that found that the catching process was very stressful experience for the 
starfish and that they could use a recuperating period in water.  
For the FFA neither of the treatments, seawater or antioxidant, had an effect on the formation of FFA. Only 
time and temperature were important. The best thing to is to store the whole starfish cold (2 °𝐶) then they 
could potentially be stored for up to four days without an increase in FFA content. But are they stored at 
temperatures closer to room temperature then an increase would measurable before the second day.

The tocopherol results did not show any clear changes in the tocopherol concentrations that could be 
assigned to oxidation. The fluctuations in the samples were too random. This could also just mean that the 
samples were not stored for long enough to see a change in the antioxidants.  
The fishing boats used for catching starfish was not originally intended for starfish but was intended and 
used for mussels. Later when starfish became an interest the fishing boats use was expanded to include 
starfish. Since the vessels was not designed or intended for starfish originally an obvious question would be 
are the boats equipment and the fishermen’s methods sufficient in handling of starfish and to preserve 
their freshness? The PV results from the iodometric method would indicate that storage of the starfish in 
seawater even if it was a cramped bucket was better than storing them out of water. So, it should be 
possible to preserve the freshness of the starfish on the boat by filling the holding container on the boat 
with water.  
When deciding on which of the three antioxidants to use during the project Duralox MANC-213 was chosen 
because of the synergistic interactions between the different antioxidants in the solution (Sørensen, et al., 
2023). Several places in the literature have proven synergistic effect between different combinations of 
tocopherol, ascorbic acid, rosemary extract, and citric acid (Bayram & Decker, 2023) (Frankel E. N., 2005) 
(O'Connor & O'Brien, 2006) (Jaswir, Che Man, & Kitts, 2000) (Garcia-Moreno, Damberg, Chronakis, & 
Jacobsen, 2017). Due to the synergy between the antioxidants the potency of the solution is increased, 
since the multicomponent antioxidant solution can prevent oxidation at several steps (Osborn-Barnes & 
Akoh, 2003). This also allows for a reduction in the antioxidant concentration to achieve the same effect as 
when applying only one antioxidant. Since the starfish oil already contains alpha-tocopherol, the Duralox 
MANC-213 solution works in synergy with the naturally present antioxidants to improve the oxidative 
stability of the starfish oil (Bayram & Decker, 2023).

5 Conclusion and Future Perspectives 
The implementation of preservative steps during the capture of starfish was found to improve the quality of 
extracted starfish oil regardless of method.  
The seawater treatment had a larger effect on the PV due to being implemented at an earlier stage of the 
process. The starfish are easily stressed during capture and need a recuperation period to return to normal. 
This could easily be achieved by using the seawater treatment, but the treatment is not halting the 
oxidation but rather delays the onset of oxidation. Therefore, the starfish should be processed within 24 
hours.  The antioxidant also had an effect on the PV by halting any further oxidation but since it was 
implemented at a later stage of the process the PV was already high. The antioxidant should be used if the 
starfish are not processed within the first 24 hours to ensure the quality of the oil.  The most ideal solution 
is to combine the two methods. That way there will be an early intervention and the PV will be kept stable 
at that value for a prolonged time.  Another parameter that influences the oxidative stability of the starfish 
oil is the storage temperature. For both methods the storage temperature had a significant impact on the 
rate of oxidation. When the samples were stored cold (2 °𝐶) there could not be detected any development 
in the FFA content.  
For further research on this topic an investigation into other antioxidants and their oxidative effects in 
starfish. This project used an antioxidant solution with multiple antioxidants that work in synergy. It could 
be seen that the antioxidant had an effect, but it is not known whether a single antioxidant created the 
effect or if it was the cocktail. Different antioxidants should be investigated for their different synergies.  
In the literature it is described that the starfish vary with the seasons. It should be investigated if these 
seasonal variations affect the oxidative stability of the starfish and have an impact on the preservation 
methods.  
In conclusion the implementation of oxidation inhibiting steps has demonstrated promising results in 
enhancing oxidative stability. Future studies should be used to better understand starfish and how they 
should be treated to create a consistent product.
