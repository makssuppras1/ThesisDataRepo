Abstract 
During the past decades, male fertility has been decreasing, and incidences of reproductive 
dysfunctions increased. Sexual differentiation in the male fetus is hormone dependent, and 
androgen activity is vital for masculinization and thus susceptible for interferences. The fetus 
is especially susceptible to exposure by anti-androgens during this period of gestation, and 
thus, such exposure is associated with malfunctions. OPFRs have been suggested to exert an 
anti-androgenic effect and might thus be linked to the decline in male fertility. In this study, 
eleven OPFRs were analysed for their anti-androgenic effects. 
The H295R assay and the androgen receptor transcriptional assay were applied to assess the 
androgenic activity of the relevant chemicals. For the H295R assay, the steroid hormones 
testosterone and 17β-estradiol were quantified using an immunoassay. The androgen receptor 
transcriptional assay was applied to measure antagonistic activities against the agonist R1881.  
No major effects on the steroidogenic pathway were observed for OPFRs. A decreasing trend 
of testosterone is observed, caused by TPHP, and an increasing trend of 17β-estradiol is 
indicated, caused by TMPP, EHDPP and CDP. The comparison to published literature 
showed inconsistencies in effects on steroidogenesis. Antagonistic activity on the androgen 
receptor was observed for ten out of eleven OPFRs, varying in potency. The highest potency 
was observed for TDCIPP (EC50 6.7 µM) and no effect was observed for DPHP. The results 
further indicate that aryl-substituted OPFRs exhibit anti-androgenic activity.

INTRODUCTION 
Male fertility has been decreasing throughout the past 50 years. (Carlsen et al., 1992) This 
trend is manifested in the reduction of sperm quality, as well as reproductive dysfunctions 
observed as hypospadias, cryptorchidism and increased testicular cancer. (Carlsen et al., 1992, 
Meeker and Stapleton, 2010) The cause of the decline in male fertility is suspected to be, 
among others, environmental pollutants with endocrine activity such as oestrogenic or anti-
androgenic activity. (Carlsen et al., 1992, Pflieger-Bruss et al., 2004) Especially, exposure to 
endocrine disrupting chemicals (EDCs) during fetal development and childhood can affect the 
hormone responsiveness in adulthood. (Sharpe, 2006) Examples of well-known EDCs are 
phthalates, chlorinated hydrocarbons and polychlorinated biphenyls. (EPA, 2012, Pflieger-
Bruss et al., 2004) Other chemicals, like flame retardant additives, are also suspected to cause 
endocrine disruption. Examples hereof are polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDEs) and 
organophosphorus flame retardants (OPFRs). (Meeker et al., 2009, Meeker and Stapleton, 
2010) PBDEs have been widely used the past decades until their restriction in the EU between 
2003-2008 with the Directive 2003/11/EC and Directive 2002/95/EC. The U.S. has 
voluntarily discontinued the use of PBDE because of their persistence in the environment, 
bioaccumulation and endocrine activity. (Meeker et al., 2009, Meeker and Stapleton, 2010)  
Ever since the phaseout, OPFRs have been a high-production-volume-chemical as an 
alternative to PBDEs as flame retardants. (Reemtsma et al., 2008) They are now found at 
higher concentrations in indoor air and dust compared to PBDEs. (Van der Veen and de Boer, 
2012) Meeker and Stapleton (2010) detected OPFRs in more than 96% of dust samples in 50 
U.S. houses. The diffusion of OPFRs from the host material to the environment is furthermore 
indicated by the detection of these chemicals in both wastewaters, drinking water and indoor 
and outdoor air. (Reemtsma et al., 2008, Van der Veen and de Boer, 2012) It has been 
suggested that a correlation exists between increased OPFRs levels and decreased semen 
quality in men. (Meeker and Stapleton, 2010) 
Objectives 
Since OPFRs are in widespread use and classified as (re-)emerging pollutants, a thorough 
profiling of the endocrine activity of these chemicals is required. (Reemtsma et al., 2008) The 
objective of this thesis project was to investigate the hazardous effects of eleven OPFRs as 
anti-androgens. These eleven OPFRs were: TEHP, TPHP, TCEP, TDCIPP, TMPP, DPHP, 
EHDPP, CDP, V6, TIPPP, TCIPP. The effects were investigated on the interference with the 
steroidogenesis and the androgen receptor activity. The goal was to present a concentration-
effect relationship and obtain EC50 values, to be able to compare the potency of OPFRs. The 
chemicals were tested on the H295R cell line, measuring the concentration of the hormone’s 
testosterone and 17β-estradiol. The anti-androgenic effect on the androgen receptor were to be 
tested on the CHO cell line, an androgen receptor gene reporter assay.  
The main hypothesis for this project is as follows:  
 The eleven chosen OPFRs are expected to exert an anti-androgenic effect.  
1. OPFRs affect the hormone synthesis of testosterone and 17β-estradiol (in vitro). 
2. OPFRs demonstrate an antagonistic activity on the androgen receptor (in vitro)

LITERATURE 
As the endocrine system is complex, it is important to understand the basic mechanisms on 
which interference of chemicals can occur. This section aims to present key information on 
the endocrine system, endocrine disrupting chemicals and current knowledge on OPFRs. 
The Endocrine System 
The endocrine system consists of endocrine glands that produce hormones which are excreted 
to the blood. They travel through the blood bound to protein carrier molecules, to a distant 
target site and act on either cytoplasmic-, membrane- or nuclear receptors. Hormones control 
the homeostasis of the body, reproduction, metabolism, growth and development. Notable on 
hormones is their ability to exert an effect at very low concentrations, in the nanomolar range. 
(Silverthorn, 2014, De Coster and Van Larebeke, 2012) The primary endocrine glands include 
the following: The pineal gland, hypothalamus, posterior and anterior pituitary, thyroid and 
parathyroid gland, pancreas, adrenal glands and the sex organs, being the ovaries and placenta 
for females and testes for males. Secondary endocrine glands are the heart, the liver, the 
stomach, the small intestine, the skin and adipose tissue. (Silverthorn, 2014) 
Androgen Synthesis 
Hormones can be divided into three classes: proteins/peptide hormones, amine hormones and 
steroid hormones. The class of steroid hormones include the major sex hormones: oestrogens, 
progesterone and androgens. These are mainly produced in the ovaries and testes. 
(Silverthorn, 2014) Testosterone, being the main natural androgen in the human body, is 
primarily produced in the Leydig cells within the testes. It is to a smaller extent produced in 
the adrenal cortex, where the production is controlled by adrenocorticotrophic hormone 
(ACTH) and corticotropin released by the anterior pituitary. The steroidogenesis of the 
H295R cell line, originating from a human adrenal carcinoma, produces both the androgens as 
well as corticosteroids. The focus of the following passage relates to the androgen synthesis, 
but effects on the corticosteroids can interfere with the androgen synthesis. (OECD, 2011) 
Cholesterol is the precursor for testosterone and other androgens like dihydrotestosterone 
(DHT) and androstenedione. The synthesis of steroid hormones involves enzymes in the 
smooth endoplasmic reticulum and the mitochondria. (Rang et al., 2012, Miller and Auchus, 
2011) The rate limiting step of the steroidogenesis is the delivery of cholesterol to the inside 
of the mitochondria, which is facilitated by the steroidogenic acute regulatory protein (StAR). 
Enzymes involved in steroidogenesis are mainly of the families cytochrome P450s (CYPs) 
and hydroxysteroid dehydrogenases (HSDs). The initial formation of cholesterol to 
pregnenolone is catalysed by P450 side chain cleavage enzyme, CYP11A1. This is the 
enzymatic rate limiting step. (Miller and Auchus, 2011) The steroidogenic pathway is 
demonstrated in figure 2, at the end of the literature section. The formation of testosterone can 
occur through two pathways. From pregnenolone, steroid synthesis can proceed through the 
∆5-pathway, involving the metabolites 17-hydroxypregnenolone, dehydroepiandrosterone and 
androstenediol. The ∆5-pathway predominates in humans. Testosterone synthesis can also 
proceed from progesterone through the ∆4-pathway, involving 17-OH progesterone and 
androstenedione. (Miller and Auchus, 2011) The enzyme aromatase, CYP19, can catalyse the 
==PAGE:13==
- 3 - 
 
conversion of either androstenedione or testosterone to the oestrogens estrone or 17β-
estradiol, respectively.  
The steroidogenesis is regulated by the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis. The male 
reproduction is thus controlled by hormones excreted from the hypothalamus and the anterior 
pituitary, which are located in the brain, and the testes. The production of the steroid 
hormones in the testes is regulated by the hormones and the negative feedback loop. The 
hormone produced and released in the hypothalamus is the gonadotropin-releasing hormone 
(GnRH), which acts on the anterior pituitary to stimulate the release of gonadotropins. Two 
gonadotropins are produced in the anterior pituitary: The follicle stimulating hormone (FSH) 
and the interstitial cell-stimulating hormone (ICSH), also called the luteinizing hormone 
(LH). FSH stimulates the Sertoli cells, which support the development of spermatozoa in the 
spermatogenesis. The ICSH stimulates Leydig cells to secrete the androgen testosterone. The 
activity of testosterone is the maintenance of spermatogenesis by promoting the maturation of 
spermatozoa. In puberty, testosterone is responsible for maturation of sex organs and 
development of secondary sex characteristics. (Rang et al., 2012)

Androgen MoA 
Steroid hormones are transported through the blood bound to carrier proteins and leaves the 
bloodstream by simple diffusion through the cell membrane. (Silverthorn, 2014) Testosterone 
==PAGE:14==
- 4 - 
 
is metabolised at the target tissue to the active metabolite DHT, which has a genomic effect. 
The mode of action of androgens is the modification of gene transcription by binding to either 
cytoplasmic or nuclear receptors in the target cell. The hormone-receptor complex thus acts as 
a transcription factor for gene transcription and initiates/inhibits production of proteins upon 
binding to DNA. (Silverthorn, 2014, De Coster and Van Larebeke, 2012) Xenobiotic 
androgens can exhibit similar effects and thus affect the feedback mechanism and decrease 
gonadotropins, and thus also testosterone. This can eventually lead to infertility. (Rang et al., 
2012) An anti-androgenic activity is also seen from oestrogens and other so-called anti-
androgens. These compounds can, among others, inhibit gonadotropin secretion or compete 
with androgen receptors on the target cell. (Rang et al., 2012) 
Anti-androgenic Effects on Male Fetus 
The human fetus is sexually indifferent until gestational week seven, where the fetus as a 
default develops into a female. Development along the female pathway is largely hormone 
independent, whereas the male development is hormone dependent. For the male 
development pathway, the activation of the Sry gene on the Y chromosome in gestational 
week seven, initiates the formation of testes. (Sharpe, 2006, Hughes, 2001) Following the 
formation of the testes, the process of sexual differentiation is dependent on hormones 
produced by the testes; anti-Müllerian hormone, insulin-like factor 3 and testosterone. From 
gestational week 8-9, fetal Leydig cells initiate the production of testosterone. (Hughes, 2001) 
Because the male development is hormone dependent, a male fetus is more susceptible to 
endocrine disruption than females. The masculinisation process is thus affected when changes 
in hormone levels are occurring. The androgen testosterone is distributed through the 
bloodstream and converted to the more potent 5α-dihydrotestosterone (DHT) on androgen 
target tissue. This conversion is facilitated through the enzyme 5α-reductase that is expressed, 
among others, in the brain, the skin and the reproductive tract. (Sharpe, 2006) An effect on 
testosterone levels or on the androgen receptor, could lead to genital malfunctions like 
hypospadias and cryptorchidism, or male reproductive disorders like testicular cancer or low 
sperm count. (Sharpe, 2006)  
Cryptorchidism is a condition seen in males, where one or both testes fail to descent to the 
bottom of the scrotum. This condition is correlated with the loss of spermatogenic cells and 
thus the loss of spermatogenic capacity, possibly leading to infertility. (Skakkebaek et al., 
2016) Hypospadias is a penile congenital malformation, where the urethra opens somewhere 
other than the tip of the penis. The possible cause of both genital malfunctions could be 
exposure to anti-androgens during the critical window of male sex differentiation between 
gestational week seven and fifteen. Androgen activity is vital for masculinization. 
(Skakkebaek et al., 2016) The malfunctions are typically linked to disorders in testicular 
differentiation, testosterone synthesis, conversion of testosterone to DHT or androgen 
receptor action, leading to under-masculinization. (Skakkebaek et al., 2016, Gray et al., 2006)  
Endocrine Disrupting Chemicals 
Humans and the environment are exposed to large amounts of chemicals. An emerging group 
of chemicals that are of concern for human health as well as the environment are the 
==PAGE:15==
- 5 - 
 
endocrine disrupting chemicals (EDCs). (Monneret, 2017) The definition of EDCs by the 
World Health Organization (WHO) is the following: “An endocrine disruptor is an exogenous 
substance or mixture that alters function(s) of the endocrine system and consequently causes 
adverse health effects in an intact organism, or its progeny, or (sub) populations”. (WHO, 
2012) This definition is widely applied in human and environmental risk assessment and 
adopted by the European Commission, EC, European Food Safety Authority, EFSA, and by 
the European Chemical Agency, ECHA. (Fuhrman et al., 2015, European Chemical Agency, 
2017) The adverse effects on the endocrine system can affect the endocrine glands, hormones 
or receptors and thus interfere with the regulation of growth, development, reproduction, 
metabolism, immunity and behaviour. (Monneret, 2017) 
A well-known chemical class with endocrine disrupting activity are the compounds PBDEs, 
which have both a xenoestrogen and an anti-estrogenic activity. The agonistic xenoestrogens 
are found to have a similar binding mode to 17β-estradiol. In men, 17β-estradiol inhibits the 
anterior pituitary and the release of gonadotropins and thus inhibits the production of 
testosterone. (De Coster and Van Larebeke, 2012) 
Many chemicals like polychlorinated biphenyls and a variety of pesticides exhibit an anti-
androgenic effect. Anti-androgens oppose the effect of androgens by blocking the androgen 
receptor (AR), which inhibits the effect mediation of androgens. (Gray et al., 2006) Of 200 
tested pesticides, 66 showed an anti-androgenic activity on the CHO cell line. (De Coster and 
Van Larebeke, 2012) For example, the organophosphorus pesticide fenthion displays the 
same anti-androgenic activity as potent AR antagonist hydroxyflutamide (OHF) applied 
against prostate cancer. (De Coster and Van Larebeke, 2012) The mode of action of an anti-
androgenic effect can occur through a receptor mediated mechanism or hormone synthesis 
disruption.  
EDCs are widespread and found in various materials, and there are thus multiple routes of 
exposure. Epidemiological studies show increased incidences of reproductive disorders 
correlated with ECDs. Examples of reproductive disorders are breast, prostate and testes 
cancer and decreased fertility. (De Coster and Van Larebeke, 2012) Other chemicals, like the 
OPFRs, have been linked to cause other reproductive dysfunctions like hypospadias and 
cryptorchidism. Several of the OPFRs have been found to possibly be reproductive toxins 
(TCP, TCEP, TPHP, TDCIPP). Although the use of TCEP has been largely phased out, it is 
still found in building materials and is suspected to reduce fertility in humans by e.g. reduced 
sperm density and motility. (De Coster and Van Larebeke, 2012, Hou et al., 2016) Also, 
TPHP have been associated with altered hormone levels and decreased sperm concentration. 
(Meeker and Stapleton, 2010) TDCIPP levels in dust is correlated with increase prolactin in 
males and testicular cancer. (Hou et al., 2016) 
Organophosphorus Flame Retardants 
Flame retardants (FR) are widely applied in both building materials, furniture, electronics and 
textiles and many other materials. (Van der Veen and de Boer, 2012, Hou et al., 2016) The 
addition of FRs serves the prevention or the delay of ignition and combustion processes. In 
2007, 1.8 million tons of FRs were consumed in Asia, Europe and the U.S.. 20% of the 
==PAGE:16==
- 6 - 
 
European consumption of FRs are the organophosphorus flame retardants. (Yasin et al., 2016, 
Van der Veen and de Boer, 2012) The use of organophosphorus compounds as flame 
retardants dates back to the 1970s, but only in recent years have the OPFRs receivd more 
attention in terms of usability and production. (Reemtsma et al., 2008) Since the phase-out of 
PBDEs in the early 2000s, OPFRs were applied as an alternative flame retardant and the 
production increased. (Reemtsma et al., 2008)  
Organophosphorus flame retardants are synthetic phosphoric acid derivatives and can be 
divided into three sub-categories: Chlorinated OPFRs, aryl substituted OPFRs and alkyl 
substituted OPFRs. (Hou et al., 2016) This study focuses on eleven OPFRs, shown in table 1, 
where four of the compounds are chlorinated (TCEP, TCIPP, TDCIPP, V6), one is alkyl 
substituted (TEHP) and five are aryl substituted (TPHP, TMPP, DPHP, CDP, TIPPP) or both 
alkyl and aryl substituted (EHDPP). The characteristic of the OPFR molecules are the 
phosphate esters with different substitutions. The properties are variable between OPFRs, but 
the log Kow values were consistently found to be positive. Variability is also seen in solubility 
and vapour pressure. (Van der Veen and de Boer, 2012, Reemtsma et al., 2008) The 
properties of chemicals can indicate where exposure to the environment and organisms take 
place. Positive log Kow values demonstrate a lipohilic character of the compound, leading to it 
likely accumulating in soil and sediments. Water soluble compounds are likely to be found in 
aquatic environments and eventually drinking water. The vapour pressure indicates how likely 
it is for the compound to be emitted to the air and thus expose humans through indoor air and 
dust. (Hou et al., 2016) Depending on the properties of the chemical, OPFRs can leak out to 
the environment by either volatilization, abrasion or leaching.  
The mechanism of action of OPFRs as flame retardants differs between the type of 
substitution. Chlorinated OPFRs act in the gas phase, where Cl-atoms removes H+ and OH- 
radicals from flammable gas, which diminishes the combustion. The non-halogenated OPFRs 
operate in the solid phase of the material. When the material is heated, the phosphorus creates 
a polymeric phosphoric acid which forms a char layer that prevents oxygen from entering the 
material and thus extinguishes the fire. (Van der Veen and de Boer, 2012)

External and Internal Exposure 
To assess the extent and relevance of exposure to humans and potential toxicity of OPFRs, the 
external as well as the internal exposure is presented in the following section. The external 
exposure is assessed by looking into occurrences and fate of OPFRs in the environment. The 
internal exposure is assessed looking into adsorption, distribution, metabolism and 
elimination (ADME) in the human body. 
The high-volume production of OPFRs has led to increased findings of these chemicals in the 
environment. Up-concentration of OPFRs is especially seen for airborne particulate matter in 
the indoor environment. (Reemtsma et al., 2008) Meeker and Stapleton (2010) detected 
OPFRs like TDCPP in over 96% of samples of house dust in the U.S. The more volatile 
compounds like the halogenated alkyl phosphates are found in higher concentrations in indoor 
air. Furthermore, OPFRs have been detected in surface water, groundwater and drinking 
water. It is indicated that wastewater treatment plants, WWTP, are able to eliminate OPRS 
only to a limited extent. Especially chlorinated compounds are not eliminated in the WWTP. 
(Reemtsma et al., 2008) 
The internal exposure to the human body has been investigated using human blood, urine and 
breast milk samples. Eleven OPFRs have been determined in human blood, seven of which 
are examined in this study (TCEP, TCIPP, TDCIPP, EHDPP, TPHP, TEHP, TMPP). EHDPP, 
TCIPP and TPHP were detected in 90% of human blood samples from participants in one 
study at average concentrations of 1.22, 0.71, 0.43 ng/mL, respectively. (Zhao et al., 2016) In 
urine samples of children, the same OPFRs as in the blood samples were found. Additionally, 
eleven metabolites were found in the urine, including DPHP. DPHP was found as frequently 
as in 94% of samples. DPHP is the metabolite of both EHDPP and TPHP and potentially 
==PAGE:19==
- 9 - 
 
other aryl-OPFRs. (He et al., 2018) The investigation of OPFRs in human breast milk 
detected TCIPP, TCEP, EHDPP, TDCIPP and TMPP. 
The internal exposure and biological fate of OPFRs is determined by ADME of the chemical 
inside an organism. The processes in ADME control the internal exposure when the organism 
is externally exposed to a chemical that can be absorbed. OPFRs are found to absorb through 
the skin, through ingestion and inhalation. (Hou et al., 2016) The most important route of 
exposure is inhalation of indoor air and dust particles. Furniture and building materials 
contain especially high concentrations of OPFRs that are emitted to the air. Ingestion of 
OPFRs can occur through drinking water contaminated by washed out OPFRs from several 
sources to the WWTP. Also, fish can bioaccumulate OPFRs from water and sediments, which 
then enter the human body through ingestion. (Sundkvist et al., 2010) A third exposure 
pathway is the OPFRs applied in food packaging, e.g. the OPFRs used as plasticizers. Uptake 
through the skin occurs with dermal contact to materials containing OPFRs. An example is 
TDCIPP that as a lipophilic compound is shown to enter through the skin. (Hou et al., 2016) 
The metabolism of chemicals inside the body largely determines the internal exposure and the 
toxicological effects. The metabolism of xenobiotics occurs mostly in the liver through phase 
I and phase II biotransformation. Reactions in phase I are oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis, 
hydration and dehalogenation. Phase II mainly consists of conjugation like sulfation, 
glucuronidation and amino acid conjugation. The purpose of biotransformation of xenobiotics 
is to increase the polarity for easier elimination of metabolites through the urine. (Timbrell, 
2008) OPFRs are transported through plasma and are metabolised both through phase I and 
phase II mechanisms. OPFRs are generally found to be metabolised and eliminated rapidly in 
rats. (Hou et al., 2016) Phase I is driven by cytochrome P450 (CYPs) enzymes that catalyse 
hydroxylation, carboxylation and dealkylation of OPFRs. Phase II includes processes like 
glutathione and sulfate conjugation catalysed by glucuronyl transferase (UGTs), 
sulfotransferases (SULTs) and glutathione S-transferase (GSTs). It is furthermore suggested 
that OPFRs accumulate in inactive tissue like adipose tissue and hair. (Hou et al., 2016, 
Timbrell, 2008)  
Studies in rats and human in vitro models have looked into type-specific metabolism of 
OPFRs. Halogenated OPFRs are transformed by the reaction of oxidative dehalogenation and 
O-dealkylation during phase I to create diesters and hydroxylated metabolites. Phase II occurs 
with direct substitution of Cl-atoms with glutathione conjugation. Aryl and alkyl OPFRs 
undergo reactions like hydroxylation, dihydroxylation and carboxylation, where alkyl OPFRs 
additionally undergo O-dealkylation during phase I metabolism. Phase II metabolism consists 
of glutathione conjugation on hydroxylated metabolites. (Hou et al., 2016) 
Relevant Literature on Toxicological Effects  
The following section seeks to give an overview on the current knowledge on the anti-
androgenic activity of OPFRs studied in vitro. The focus was on effects on steroidogenesis 
measured on the H295R cell line. Additionally, effects on the androgen receptor were 
investigated and relevant literature presented. However, the literature on this topic is limited. 
==PAGE:20==

Effects on steroid synthesis  
Effects on hormone synthesis was investigated by Liu et al. (2012) applying the H295R assay. 
The following OPFRs were analysed using the concentration range from 0.001 to 100 mg/L: 
TCEP, TCIPP, TDCIPP, TPHP, TMPP. The results show that all of the tested OPFRs 
increased levels of both testosterone and 17β-estradiol. TDCIPP showed the highest potency 
with a significant increase in hormone levels at 0.01 mg/L. Additionally, TPHP and EHDPP 
were found to have a stimulating effect on progesterone production. (Hu et al., 2017) 
All the mentioned OPFRs were found by Liu et al. (2012) to up-regulate the mRNA 
transcription of the enzyme CYP11A1. Furthermore, an up-regulation was observed for the 
gene CYP19A1 by TCEP, TDCIPP, TPHP and TMPP and the gene 3βHSD2 by TCEP, 
TCIPP, TDCIPP and TMPP. (Liu et al., 2012) Also, TPHP and EHDPP are found to increase 
the expression of 3βHSD1. (Hu et al., 2017) Another study performed by Zhang et al. (2017) 
found an up-regulation of the genes CYP17 by TDCIPP, CYP21 by TDCIPP and TEHP, and 
3β2HSD by TEHP and TCIPP.  
Down-regulation of the genes SULTE1 and SULT2A1 was observed after exposure to TCEP, 
TCIPP, TDCIPP, TPHP and TMPP. (Liu et al., 2012) These genes encode for 
sulfotransferases which are involved in the phase II metabolism. (Hou et al., 2016) 
Transcription of CYP17 was down-regulated by TCIPP. (Liu et al., 2012) Both CYP17 and 
CYP21 were down-regulated by TMPP, TPHP and TCEP, as well as CYP11B1 by TMPP and 
TPHP. Additionally, the expression of StAR was decreased by TPHP and TCEP. TPHP also 
inhibited the expression of 3βHSD2 and CYP11A1, and TDCIPP inhibited the expression of 
CYP11B1. (Zhang et al., 2017) Not affected were CYP21A1, 17βHSD1 and 17βHSD4. (Liu 
et al., 2012) To give an overview, all known effects of OPFRs on mRNA gene transcription 
involved in steroidogenesis are gathered in figure 2. This figure focuses mainly on the 
synthesis pathways of testosterone and estradiol and only mentions the pathway of gluco- and 
mineral-corticoids. 
Effects on androgen receptor 
The study of Kojima et al. (2013) observed AR antagonistic activity for TDCIPP, TPHP and 
TMPP, with TDCIPP being the most potent antagonist and TMPP the least. The experiment 
was performed with the CHO-K1 cell-based reporter gene assay and the application of DHT 
as the agonist for androgenic activity induction. AR antagonism was also found for TDCIPP 
in the study of Reers et al. (2016). No antagonistic effects were measured for TEHP, TCEP 
and TCIPP. (Kojima et al., 2013) This is partly confirmed by the findings of Reers et al. 
(2016), who observed no AR antagonism for TCEP and TCIPP. Contrary to Kojima et al. 
(2013), TMPP was found to have no AR antagonistic effect. Furthermore, no AR agonistic 
effect has been observed. (Reers et al., 2016) A third study by Weiss et al. (2011) detected 
moderately anti-androgenic activity as antagonism towards AR for TCIPP and TEHP. The 
authors performed the androgen-responsive transactivation bioassay to obtain these 
results.(Weiss et al., 2011)

H295R Assay 
The potential effect on the steroidogenesis was assessed using a human adrenal cortico-
carcinoma cell line, called the H295R steroidogenic assay. The H295R assay is applied for in 
vitro testing of chemicals that are suspected to interfere with the steroidogenesis and affect 
production of testosterone and 17β-estradiol. The H295R cell line derives from human 
adrenocortical carcinoma cells and is able to produce all important adrenal steroid hormones. 
The assay is described in detail in the guidelines provided by the Organisation for Economic 
Co-operation and Development (OECD). (OECD, 2011)  
The assessment of a potential anti-androgenic effect on H295R cells started with a profiling 
experiment of all OPFRs. 17β-estradiol and testosterone levels were measured for the 
exposure concentrations 10, 25 and 50 µM. Additionally, three experiments were conducted 
to measure the concentrations of 17β-estradiol and testosterone applying seven concentrations 
in the range 0.8 to 50 µM to create a full concentration-response curve. Cell viability was 
tested in three separate experiments, applying the concentration 10, 25, 50 and 100 µM. All 
experiments were performed using triplicates of each concentration. 
Cultivation of Cells 
The human adrenocortical carcinoma cell line (NCI-H295R subtype) was received from the 
American type culture collection (ATCC, CRL-2128). H295R Cells were defrosted from 
stock at passages between 4-5 and denoted passage 0. They were cultured in Dulbecco′s 
Modified Eagle′s Medium (DMEM-F12) without phenol red (Thermo Fisher, Paisley, UK) 
containing glutamine medium and HEPES buffer. The media was supplemented with 1% ITS 
+ premix (containing insulin, human transferrin and selenous acid; Corning, USA) and 2.5% 
NU-serum (Thermo Fisher, Paisley, UK). Cells were incubated at 37°C with a humidified 
atmosphere of 5% CO2. Passaging of cells by trypsinization was performed when achieving 
approximately 95% confluency in the cell culture flask. Cells at passage between four and ten 
were applied for hormone analysis, whereas lower or higher passages were in some cases 
used for cytotoxicity testing. 
Cell Exposure 
Performing the H295R assay, H295R cells at a concentration of 3 x 105 cells/mL and a final 
density of 3 x 105 cells/well, were placed in a 24 well plate (Cellstar Greiner bio-one, Austria, 
cat. no. 662960) and incubated at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere of 5% CO2 for 24 hrs. The 
OPFRs were added using a digital dispenser (Hp D300 Digital Dispenser) and the cells were 
exposed for 48 hrs, incubated at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere of 5% CO2. The final 
exposure concentrations for the first profiling of all OPFRs were 50, 25, 10 µM for all test 
chemicals dissolved in DMSO and EtOH. The final exposure for the following three 
experiments were 50, 25, 12.5, 6.3, 3.1, 1.6 and 0.8 µM. The solvents DMSO and EtOH were 
kept at ≤0.1% in the respective media in all solutions. All concentrations were tested in 
triplicates. Following exposure, the supernatant was collected in new 24-well plates and 
stored at –80°C until hormone analysis. For every conducted experiment, a quality control 
plate was prepared.

Quality Control of Cells 
The quality of cells was determined using a quality control plate (QC), where cells were 
exposed to the positive controls forskolin and prochloraz. The exposure concentrations of 
forskolin were 1 µM and 10 µM, which induce production of testosterone and estradiol. 
Prochloraz was added at the concentrations 0.3 µM and 3 µM. The measured effects were 
inhibition of testosterone and estradiol production. All incubations were performed as 
triplicates. Specific criteria for expected levels have been defined in the OECD test guideline 
456. The limit of qualification (LOQ) for testosterone and estradiol is 100 pg/mL and 10 
pg/mL, respectively. The performance criteria were evaluated using the QC plate for each cell 
batch. Thus, the basal production of testosterone should be ≥5 times the LOQ, and for 
estradiol ≥2.5 times the LOQ, to be able to detect changes in hormone levels. The induction of 
hormone production by 10 µM forskolin should increase testosterone by ≥1.5 times the LOQ 
and estradiol by ≥7.5 times the LOQ. Inhibition by 1 µM prochloraz decreases both 
testosterone and estradiol by ≤0.5 times the LOQ. Although 3 µM of prochloraz was applied 
instead of 1 µM, the same or higher inhibition is expected. 
Cell Viability Assay 
Cytotoxicity testing was performed on the eleven OPFRs. This was done to assess the 
concentration which does not decrease cell viability by more than 20% compared to the 
solvent control. TEHP was dissolved in 0.1% EtOH, whereas all other OPFRs were dissolved 
in 0.1% DMSO. Both EtOH (0.1%) and DMSO (0.1%) were applied on each plate as solvent 
controls and results were normalized to these. The cytotoxicity of each chemical was tested 
by applying the CellTiter-Glo 2.0 assay (Promega, Madison, USA). The Celltiter-Glo 2.0 
luminescence cell viability assay determines the amount of ATP present, which is 
proportional to the number of metabolically active cells. The CellTiter-Glo 2.0 Reagent lyses 
the cell and oxidizes luciferin, which is catalysed by luciferase, ATP, Mg and oxygen. The 
oxidised luciferin creates a luminescence signal. 
The cells were plated in clear bottom 96-well plates (Corning, USA) at a concentration of 7.9 
x 105 cells/mL to obtain the same cell density applied for the 24 well plates. (OECD, 2011) 
The plates were incubated for 24 hrs at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere of 5% CO2. All of 
the eleven OPFRs were plated at the concentrations 10, 25, 50 and 100 µM and incubated for 
48 hrs. 100 µL of the CellTiter-Glo 2.0 Reagent (Promega, Nacka, Sweden, cat. no. G9242) 
was added to each well and luminescence was measured on a luminometer (EnSpire 
multimode plate reader, PerkinElmer). 
Quantification of Steroid Hormones 
The method for the steroid hormone quantification of 17β-estradiol and testosterone was 
performed by an enzyme immunoassay (EIA). Caymans testosterone EIA kit and estradiol 
EIA kit were applied. (Cayman chemical, Item No. 582701 and 582251). The manufacturer 
terminated the production of the estradiol EIA kit during the project and limited the number 
of samples analysed for estradiol production. This assay is based on the competitive binding 
of the hormones estradiol and testosterone and the hormone-acetylcholinesterase conjugate 
(the hormone tracer) to the antiserum. The concentration of the hormone tracer is kept 
==PAGE:25==
- 15 - 
 
constant, whereas the hormone concentration varies in the samples. Thus, the hormone tracer 
that binds to the antiserum is inversely proportional to the concentration of the hormone. The 
antiserum-hormone complex binds to a mouse monoclonal anti-rabbit IgC antibody that was 
previously attached to the wells, see figure 3. The quantification of the antiserum-hormone 
complex is measured by adding Ellman’s reagent, which initiates a reaction creating a yellow-
coloured substance that absorbs at 412 nm. The intensity of the colour is proportional to the 
amount of hormone tracer bound to the antibody, which is in turn inversely proportional to the 
hormone concentration. The limit of detection lies at 80% B/B0, the ratio of the absorbance of 
a sample to the maximum binding, which lies around 20 pg/mL for estradiol and 6 pg/mL for 
testosterone. 
The supernatant obtained from H295R cell exposure to OPFRs were analysed for estradiol 
and testosterone. Standard solutions of the two hormones were diluted to obtain a range of 
concentrations to create a standard curve. The standard curve was applied for the calculation 
of the hormone concentration in the samples (4000, 1600, 640, 256, 102.4, 41, 16.4, 6.6 
pg/mL for estradiol and 500, 250, 125, 62.5, 31.3, 15.6, 7.8, 3.9 pg/mL for testosterone). 
Standards and samples were added in duplicates to the 96-well plates delivered with the EIA 
kit, coated with mouse monoclonal anti-rabbit IgC. 50 µL of the tracer was added, followed 
by 50 µL of the antibody solution, whereafter the plate was placed on an orbital shaker for 1 
hr at room temperature. The wells containing testosterone were mixed on an orbital shaker for 
2 hrs. To remove unbound reagents, the wells were washed five times with 200 µL of the 
wash-buffer solution. The Ellmann’s solution was prepared immediately before use by 
dissolving the freeze-dried Ellmann’s reagent in 50 mL ultrapure water and 200 µL of the 
solution was added to each well. After 1 hr on an orbital shaker, covered with aluminium foil, 
the absorbance was measured on a spectrophotometer (EnSpire multimode plate reader, 
PerkinElmer) at a wavelength of 412 nm. Absorbance of the B0 wells (blank subtracted), the 
maximum binding wells, were between 0.3-1.0.

Androgen Receptor Transcriptional Assay 
The anti-androgenic activity on the androgen receptor was investigated by using the AR 
STTA assay, a reporter gene assay that is able to detect agonistic and antagonistic activity. 
The assay utilises a derivative of the Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cell line, the AR-
EcoScreen cell line (AR-Eco™). This cell line has been genetically modified by inserting the 
wild-type human AR receptor gene, a firefly luciferase reporter gene and a renilla luciferase 
reporter gene. The principle of this assay is the activation or inhibition of reporter genes 
following the activation or blocking of the AR. Upon binding of a chemical to the AR, the 
receptor-ligand complex translocates to the nucleus where it binds to DNA and activates the 
expression of luciferase through the firefly luciferase reporter gene. The presence of luciferin 
creates a luminescence that can be measured. The renilla luciferase reporter gene 
distinguishes between antagonism and cytotoxicity of the chemical. (OECD, 2016) 
The potential AR antagonistic effect of all OPFRs was tested in this assay. This experiment 
was conducted once with concentrations ranging from 0.4 µM to 100 µM, each concentration 
performed in triplicates. Data on three additional experiments were obtained from Anne 
Kjerstine Rosenmai, who previously had performed this assay with the same settings. Cell 
viability was performed three times applying the same exposure concentrations in triplicates.  
Cultivation of Cells 
The CHO cell line was received from the Japanese Collection of Research Bioresources 
(JCRB) cell bank (Sekisui XenoTech, Germany, Cat. No. JCRB1328) and cultivated in 
DMEM-F12 growth media w/o phenol red (Thermo Fisher, Paisley, UK), containing 
glutamine medium and HEPES buffer. The media was supplemented with 5% FBS-serum 
(Thermo Fisher, paisley, UK), 1% penicillin/streptomycin (Thermo Fisher, Grand Island, NY, 
USA), 200 µg/ml zeocin (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA) and 100 µg/ml hygromycin 
(Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA). Performing the assay, cells were plated with an assay 
media.  The assay media contained a medium without the presence of androgens or other sex 
steroid hormones in the serum that can disturb the measurement. The media consisted of 
DMEM-F12 w/o phenol red, containing glutamine medium and HEPES buffer, supplemented 
with 5% charcoal stripped serum (DCC) (Sigma-Aldrich, St Louis, USA) and 1% pen/strep. 
(Thermo Fisher, Grand Island, NY, USA) Cells were incubated at 37°C with a humidified 
atmosphere of 5% CO2. The cells were trypsinized when the confluency reached 
approximately 90-95% and cells between passage 5-15 were applied for the assay. 
Cell Exposure 
CHO cells at a concentration of 9 x 104 cell/mL were placed in 96-well plate (Corning, NY, 
USA) and incubated at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere of 5% CO2 for 24 hrs. The cells were 
exposed to the OPFRs for 24 hrs, incubated at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere of 5% CO2. 
The OPFRs were added using a digital dispenser (Hp D300 Digital Dispenser). The exposure 
concentrations were 100, 50, 25, 12.5, 6.3, 3.1, 1.6, 0.8 and 0.4 µM for all test chemicals 
dissolved in DMSO and EtOH. The solvents DMSO and EtOH were kept at 0.1% in the 
respective media in all solutions. All concentrations were tested in triplicates. All wells were 
incubated with 0.1 nM of the agonist Methyltrienolone (R1881, PerkinElmer, Skovlunde, 
==PAGE:27==
- 17 - 
 
Denmark) together with the OPFRs to test for potential antagonism. On day three, the dual-
reporter assay, Dual-Glo luciferase assay (Promega, Madison, USA), was applied to measure 
luminescence. 100 µL of the Dual-Glo luciferase reagent, consisting of Dual-Glo luciferase 
buffer and Dual-Glo luciferase substrate, was added to each well followed by luminescence 
measurements of firefly luciferase activity on a luminometer (EnSpire multimode plate 
reader, PerkinElmer). Afterwards, 100 µL of Dual-Glo Stop and Glo reagent, consisting of 
Dual-Glo Stop and Glo buffer and Dual-Glo stop and Glo substrate, was added to measure 
renilla luciferase activity. This measurement accounts for the cytotoxicity of the chemicals at 
the present concentration.   
The data of previous experiments received from Anne Kjerstine Rosenmai were measured on 
a different luminometer, Lumistar Galaxy (BMG Lab Tech). 
Cell Viability Assay 
In addition to the cytotoxicity testing with renilla luciferase activity, cell viability was tested 
using the CellTiter-Glo 2.0 assay (Promega, Madison, USA). The cells were plated in white 
96-well plates (Perkin Elmer, USA) at a concentration of 9 x 104 cell/mL. The plates were 
incubated for 24 hrs at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere of 5% CO2. All eleven OPFRs were 
plated at the concentrations 100, 50, 25, 12.5, 6.3, 3.1, 1.6, 0.8 and 0.4 µM and incubated for 
24 hrs. 100 µL of the CellTiter-Glo 2.0 Reagent (Promega, Nacka, Sweden, cat. no. G9242) 
was added to each well and luminescence was measured on a luminometer (EnSpire 
multimode plate reader, PerkinElmer). This procedure was repeated three times. Only the 
results for cytotoxicity obtained from the CellTiter-Glo 2.0 assay were analysed and shown in 
this report. 
Statistical Analysis 
Data were plotted and analysed using GraphPad Prism and Microsoft Excel and are presented 
relative to the solvent control. Normality of distribution and homogeneity of variance was 
tested on raw data on GraphPad Prism to ensure normal distribution. Nonlinear regression fit 
was applied to obtain EC50 values. The hormone concentrations were calculated using 
nonlinear regression fit of the standard curve. A one-way ANOVA analysis was performed, 
followed by Dunnett’s multiple comparisons test that determines differences between exposed 
groups to the solvent control group. Differences with p < 0.05 were considered significant.

RESULTS 
Effects on Hormone Synthesis 
The aim of this project was to perform a profiling experiment of all 11 OPFRs with three 
exposure concentrations to evaluate their effect on synthesis of testosterone and 17β-estradiol. 
Additionally, OPFRs with measurable effects were to be analysed in three experiments with a 
full dose-response exposure scenario in the H295R assay. All four experiments were 
performed during this project. However, the cultivation of H295R cells was challenging with 
regard to contamination incidents and insufficient growth. This led to delays in the 
experimental work. Thus, testosterone levels have only been analysed for two out of four 
experiments. Additionally, the termination of the production of Cayman’s estradiol EIA kit by 
the manufacturer led to only seven analysed OPFR samples.  
Quality Control of Hormone Production 
To ensure sufficient hormone production for the detection of any changes in hormone levels, 
QC plates were included for each experiment. Assessment of the QC plate is essential for the 
approval of the following measurements of the same experiment. Two QC plates were 
analysed for hormone concentrations; the first experiment was analysed with respect to both 
testosterone and estradiol and the second was analysed only for testosterone. Table 3 shows 
the results of all QC analyses as well as the performance criteria of the H295R assay. The 
basal production of testosterone seen in the solvent control (SC) on the QC plate of the first 
experiment was 1865 pg/mL, see graph A in figure 4. 10 µM forskolin induced a 3.2-fold 
increase in testosterone production compared to the reference. The inhibition by 3 µM 
prochloraz led to 0.12 testosterone relative to the SC. These findings coincide with the 
performance criteria of ≥ 1.5 times the SC for the induction of testosterone and ≥ 0.5 times the 
SC for the inhibition. The estradiol analysis of the first experiment showed a basal production 
of 329 pg/mL, see graph B in figure 4. The induction by forskolin increased estradiol 6.6 
times relative to the SC and prochloraz reduced levels by 0.69. This does not meet the 
performance criteria of an estradiol induction of ≥ 7.5 the SC and an inhibition of ≥ 0.5 the 
SC. However, the results show a clear trend towards the ability of the cells to react to these 
positive controls.

Effects on Androgen Receptor Activation 
The activation of AR transcription was measured by exposing AR-EcoScreen cell line (AR-
Eco™) to eleven OPFRs together with 0.1 nM of the synthetic AR agonist R1881.  
Cytotoxicity in CHO Cells 
Cytotoxicity was tested by applying the same concentrations that were tested in the assay, 
ranging from 0.39 to 100 µM. The results are shown in figure 9. Six of the OPFRs are 
cytotoxic at the concentrations 50 and 100 µM. These are TPHP, TDCIPP, TMPP, EHDPP, 
CDP and TIPPP. V6 exhibits cytotoxicity only at 100 µM exposure concentration. The 
concentrations leading to cytotoxicity are marked with a C in the concentration-effect curve in 
figure 10, to distinguish effects due to cellular downregulation or cell death. The OPFRs that 
did not show cytotoxicity at or below 100 µM are TEHP, TCEP, DPHP and TCIPP.  
AR Antagonism  
The concentration-effect curves of the different OPFRs are seen in figure 10. The last plot 
shows the potent antagonist hydroxyflutamide (OHF), the positive control which gave rise to 
an EC50 of 25.5 nM. Furthermore, eight of the OPFRs demonstrate a dose response 
relationship; TPHP, TCIPP, TIPPP, TMPP, EHDPP, CDP, V6 and TDCIPP. Six of these 
OPFRs exhibit cytotoxicity at 50 and 100 µM. DPHP shows no antagonistic effect on AR. 
TEHP and TCEP show a significant decrease in the luciferase signal at 50 and 100 µM 
exposure concentrations, meaning a competitive binding against the agonist and thus blocking 
the androgen receptor. Higher concentrations should be analysed to be able to create a 
concentration-effect relationship. The EC50 values of the OPFRs are shown in table 4, as well 
as the LOEC values for all OPFRs. Since it was not possible to create EC50 values for all 
OPFRs, LOECs are another useful measure of comparing the potency of different compounds. 
The most potent are thus TDCIPP, TMPP, EHDPP, TPHP and CDP with the LOEC values of 
0.8, 1.6 and 3.1 µM, respectively. The EC50 values are similar between the compounds and 
vary between 6.7 to 10.3 µM. TDCIPP displays the lowest EC50 value of 6.7, however the 
larger standard deviation adds uncertainty to the result as the curve fit was poor. TIPPP, 
TCIPP and V6 show moderate potency to inhibit AR transcription compared to the other 
OPFRs. DPHP, on the other hand, shows no antagonistic effect. TEHP and TCEP 
demonstrate the highest LOECs of 50 µM and are consequently the least potent AR 
antagonists.

DISCUSSION  
Anti-androgens may exercise effects on hormone synthesis as well as hormone receptors. 
Since multiple studies indicate that male fertility is decreasing, it is of special interest to 
investigate chemicals that could have such effects. The examination of disruptive effects on 
androgen synthesis and on the androgen receptor is important because these are key 
mechanisms for interruption in male reproduction, especially during fetal development. Other 
chemicals than OPFRs have already displayed such behaviour. Examples hereof are the 
formerly intensively used brominated flame retardants, PBDEs, which have found to be 
potent AR antagonists. (Vinggaard et al., 2008) The following section aims to discuss the 
trends indicated by the present study and to compare them to published literature on the same 
topic. 
Effects on Steroid Hormone Synthesis 
One of the mechanisms of anti-androgens is the interference with the steroidogenesis, which 
thus can affect the androgen concentration. The analysis of hormone production by H295R 
cells exposed to OPFRs, seeks to investigate possible effects on the steroidogenic pathway. 
The results presented in the previous section indicate that no major effects from the studied 
OPFRs can be detected on the synthesis of the hormones testosterone and estradiol. When 
analysing the results, it is important to bear in mind that only two separate experiments were 
analysed for testosterone production, meaning that the current findings can only indicate 
effects and no definite conclusions can be drawn. Nevertheless, the trend of testosterone 
levels is seen as a decrease caused by TPHP, TIPPP and TMPP. The chemicals TMPP, 
EHDPP and CDP are found to increase estradiol levels. Only seven out of eleven compounds 
from one experiment were analysed for estradiol concentration. The remaining OPFRs 
(TDCIPP, TCIPP, V6, TCEP, DPHP) did not seem to induce a change in hormone levels. 
Although no clear effects were found for testosterone and estradiol, it is recommended to 
analyse the remaining samples of the three independent experiments already performed, 
including a statistical analysis, to be able to draw conclusions. As for now, the current results 
could indicate that interference with the steroidogenesis is not the main mode of action 
regarding the anti-androgenic activity of OPFRs.  
The observations found in this study appear to contrast the findings of Liu et al. (2012), where 
TCEP, TCIPP, TDCIPP, TPHP and TMPP are seen to increase levels of both testosterone and 
estradiol. We did not observe any clear trends where testosterone levels increased as a 
function of OPFR concentration. The basal hormone production of the H295R cell line stated 
by Liu et al. (2012), were around 1600 and 150 pg/mL for testosterone and estradiol, 
respectively. The current experiment showed some variation between solvent controls, but the 
average basal production was 1297 ± 324 and 347 ± 42 pg/mL for testosterone and estradiol, 
respectively. Variations in hormone synthesis within and between studies may be affected by 
the application of different cell passages, as it is known that biochemical fluctuations can be 
linked to the cell cycle and increasing age of the cell culture. (Hecker et al., 2006) 
Specifically, the H295R cell line has shown a decrease in hormone production after passage 
ten of the cell line, which demonstrates the alteration in hormone production with increasing 
==PAGE:40==
- 30 - 
 
age of cells. (Hecker et al., 2006, OECD, 2011) Additionally, the difference in the applied cell 
density in wells can affect the variability. Liu et al. (2012) seeded 200,000 cells/mL, whereas 
we applied 300,000 cells/mL. The use of a cell density of 300,000 cells/mL or higher, could 
either lead to an increase or decrease in hormone production. An increase could be seen from 
more cells producing hormones. On the other hand, cells at higher densities can decrease and 
alter production patterns of hormones. (OECD, 2011)  
The concentration applied varies between the mentioned study and the present experiment. 
Whereas we mostly applied 10, 25 and 50 µM, Liu et al. (2012) applied 0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1, 
10, 100 mg/L. Converting our concentrations to mg/L, we have tested concentrations ranging 
from 3 to 30 mg/L. Interestingly, they found an increase of hormone levels by exposure to 
TDCIPP at concentrations as low as 0.1 mg/L. Overall, they measure effects at concentrations 
lower than the ones tested in this experiment.  
The indicated increase in estradiol levels for TMPP, EHDPP and CDP could indicate an 
overall up-regulation of the steroidogenesis. However, testosterone levels are either 
unaffected or slightly decreasing for the mentioned compounds. TMPP measurements suggest 
a decline in testosterone levels and could as followed suggest an up-regulation of aromatase 
(CYP19) which converts testosterone to estradiol. It was observed by Liu et al. (2012) that 
TMPP indeed upregulated the mRNA expression of CYP21. (See figure 2 in the introduction) 
On the other hand, the same study detected an increase of testosterone by TMPP. EHDPP and 
CDP could potentially affect the inhibition of estradiol metabolism, thus steroid sulfatases. It 
has not been investigated for these two OPFRs, but Liu et al. (2012) found SULTE1 to be 
down regulated by TCEP, TCIPP, TDCIPP, TPHP and TMPP. It would thus be interesting to 
further explore the effects of OPFRs on steroid metabolism through sulfotransferases.  
As opposed to the findings of Liu et al. (2012), our results indicate a slight decrease in 
testosterone levels when exposing the cells to TPHP, TIPPP and TMPP. Since only 
testosterone and estradiol were measured, it is challenging to draw any mechanistic 
conclusions. Possible explanations could be up-stream effects on the enzymes involved in 
steroidogenesis. This could potentially be performed by either the down-regulation of StAR, 
as indicated for TPHP by Zhang et al. (2017), or by inhibition of any enzyme involved in the 
∆4 -pathway and ∆5 -pathway. Zhang et al. (2017) suggest that TPHP down-regulates mRNA 
expression of CYP11A, CYP17 and 3β-HSD that are all involved in the mentioned pathways. 
The same authors suggest that also TMPP down-regulates the expression of CYP17. On the 
other hand, CYP11A and 3β-HSD are found to be up-regulated by TPHP and TMPP. (Liu et 
al., 2012, Hu et al., 2017) Also, an up-regulation of CYP19 could lead to a decrease of 
testosterone production, because testosterone is transformed into estradiol. This is indicated 
by Liu et al. (2012) for TPHP and TMPP. An increase in the metabolism of testosterone and 
other steroids could affect the testosterone levels as well. Liu et al. (2012) found both TPHP 
and TMPP to down-regulate SULTE1 and SULT2A1, which are involved in steroid 
metabolism. This would not correlate with a decrease in hormone levels, as indicates by our 
findings, but would rather lead to an increase of hormone levels. The somewhat inconsistent 
findings between these three independently published studies and the present study could 
indicate that the working assay is difficult to pursue in a reproductive manner. 
==PAGE:41==
- 31 - 
 
The overall trends in the effects of OPFRs on steroidogenesis gene expression found in recent 
literature of three independent studies, showed that the majority of OPFRs up-regulate the 
enzymes CYP11A, 3β-HSD and CYP19. A few OPFRs are detected to down-regulate CYP17 
and CYP21. Information on the effect on gene expression can reveal where the OPFRs act on 
the steroidogenesis pathway. Furthermore, chemicals can indirectly interfere with the 
steroidogenic pathway by affecting co-factors such as NADPH and cAMP and by affecting steroid 
metabolism. The subject has not been studied intensively and the results of the current study 
indicates inconsistencies compared to previous findings. Thus, more work is vital in order to 
try to confirm the effects of OPFRs on the hormone synthesis.  
 
Antagonistic Effects on Androgen Receptor  
The androgen receptor was found to be affected by eight out of eleven tested OPFRs with 
different potencies, as shown by the derived EC50 and LOEC values in table 4. TDCIPP was 
identified to be the most potent of eleven OPFRs, yielding an EC50 value of 6.7 µM. This 
compares well to the findings of Kojima et al. (2013), who detected TDCIPP, TPHP and 
TMPP to have an antagonistic effect on AR. The mentioned study described the potency on 
AR with RIC20 values, the 20% relative inhibitory concentration. It shows 20% of the 
antagonistic activity against 0.1 nM DHT. The RIC20 values for TDCIPP, TPHP and TMPP 
were 1.9 ± 1.5 µM, 17 ± 3 µM and 24 ± 9 µM, respectively. The RIC20 values, also termed 
EC20, were for the present study estimated to be equal to LOEC values or slightly higher for 
TDCIPP, TMPP, TIPPP and V6, see table 4 in the previous section. Detection of TDCIPP as 
the most potent OPFR, with an EC20 around 1.6 µM, correlates with the findings of Kojima et 
al. (2013). Nevertheless, one of the four experiments for TDCIPP measurement performed in 
this study, yielded higher values compared to the three other experiments and increases the 
standard deviation substantially. Those values are thus suspected to be outliers. The EC20 
values for TMPP and TPHP stated by Kojima et al. (2013) showed higher concentrations than 
what is estimated in the present work. The EC20 for TMPP is approximately 3.1 µM. Thus, 
the potency is observed to be higher in the current findings compared to 24 ± 9 µM found by 
Kojima et al. (2013). The same was seen for TPHP. Furthermore, we observed moderate to 
weak AR antagonism by TCIPP, TEHP and TCEP, although Kojima et al. (2013) did not. The 
antagonistic effects caused by TEHP and TCEP were measured at exposure concentrations of 
50 µM. Kojima et al. (2013) treated cells with concentrations ranging from 0.1 µM to 30 µM 
and thus did not detect the antagonistic abilities of TEHP and TCEP.  
Weiss et al. (2011) detects moderate AR antagonism of TCIPP and TEHP, which complies 
with the moderate effect on AR activity observed in this study. Furthermore, the effect of 
TDCIPP as a potent AR antagonist is detected by Reers et al. (2016). The same study found 
no antagonistic effects on AR by TCEP, TCIPP and TMPP. However, Reers et al. (2016) 
applied 1 nM R1881, while the current study applied 0.1 nM. The application of an agonist 
concentration of 1 nM causes less sensitivity of the assay compared to an assay where 0.1 nM 
androgen agonist is used. It is harder to compete against a potent agonist when it is present at 
a higher concentration (10-fold higher in this case). This could explain why they did not 
==PAGE:42==
- 32 - 
 
observe AR antagonism of TCEP, TCIPP and TMPP whereas the current results showed an 
inhibitory effect on androgen receptor activity. 
When we looked at anti-androgenic effects for compounds with structural similarities, we 
found that aryl substituted OPFRs showed higher potencies as AR antagonists compared to 
chlorinated substances. Also, our results indicate a trend where mainly OPFRs with aryl 
substitutions affected hormone levels, i.e. anti-androgenic activity. However, this is not 
reported in other available literature. In the mentioned studies, both chlorinated and aryl 
substituted OPFRs showed anti-androgenic activity, and no consistent trend with regards to 
overall structure was reported. This goes to show that this type of studies are highly complex, 
and that more work is needed in this field before definite conclusions about the effects can be 
drawn and effect predictions can be made on the ground of OPFR structures.  
Data quality 
The quality of the data presented in this study are assessed using performance criteria stated 
in the OECD guidelines for the H295R Steroidogenesis Assay. As shown in the results 
section, the quality control plates performed with each experiment did not entirely meet the 
criteria. In general, the QC plates demonstrated that H295R cells are capable of responding to 
known inducers and inhibitors of steroidogenesis. The first testosterone QC plate analysis of 
the second experiment showed the poorest results, see table 3 in results. A second testosterone 
analysis on the same QC plate was performed to either verify or disprove the first 
measurement. The results showed different values, which almost fulfilled the performance 
criteria. It indicated that the cell culture applied in this experiment after all performed as 
desired. However, since a mistake must have occurred during the analysis of the QC plate, the 
analysis of the entire second experiment may be affected by the same mistake and data should 
be re-analysed. The data of the second experiment presented in this work should be handled 
and understood with caution. Notably, reviewing figure 7, EHDPP, CDP, V6, TIPPP and 
TCIPP demonstrated no effect on testosterone levels, essentially no standard deviation and 
almost the same testosterone production level for the solvent control.  
Limitations 
In vitro study designs have both advantages and disadvantages, which we aim to highlight in 
this section. Specific challenges working with the H295R assay are that antibiotics are not 
applied and thus the cell line was susceptible to contamination. We had several incidences of 
biological contamination during the initial phase of the experiments. The contamination was 
expressed as turbid cell culture media. Furthermore, it was observed that the cell culture at 
times grew slowly with no recognizable pattern between passages. Thus, it did not seem as if 
the slow growth was related to the cell age. Also, the OECD guidelines on the H295R assay 
that were mostly followed in this work, are only guidelines and thus not a criterion for 
working with H295R cells. This supposedly induces methodological variations between 
laboratories and introduces variations between studies. Not accounted for in this work were 
effects on gluco- and mineral-corticoids in the H295R assay. This cell line stems from adrenal 
tissue and produces the mentioned hormones. Effects on these could ultimately affect the 
levels of testosterone and estradiol. By excluding this pathway, a possible causation of effects 
==PAGE:43==
- 33 - 
 
can be overlooked. On the other hand, the advantage of H295R cells is the high production of 
hormones, so detection of both testosterone, estradiol and potentially progesterone is possible. 
Challenges working with in vitro experiments in general are the determination of the exact 
cell exposure. The concentration inside the well is not necessarily the same as the intra-
cellular concentration. Depending on the chemical (and the Kow), some substances adhere 
strongly to the surface of the equipment and the surface of cells, which must be accounted for. 
Therefore, we most likely underestimate the effect of a substance. This leads to the question 
of how to measure the concentration inside the cell and suggests that further work should be 
applied within this topic. 
Another major drawback of in vitro studies is the difficulty in extrapolating any results to 
effects in humans. In vitro experiments do not yield information on the absorption, 
distribution, metabolism and excretion of chemicals, and thus the extent of the actual effects 
in humans cannot be foreseen. Also, it is challenging to analyse chemicals that need metabolic 
activation with in vitro studies, with the exception of testing the metabolites of parent 
compounds. Furthermore, effects on the feedback mechanism of steroid hormone synthesis, 
the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis, which can have a major impact on hormone levels, can 
only be detected in intact animal systems. On the other hand, combining in vitro data with 
physiologically based kinetic modelling makes exposure reconstruction possible. Exposure 
assessment by reverse dosiometry can thus aid the risk assessment without the application of 
in vivo studies. Also, in vitro studies are especially applicable for screening of chemicals, as it 
is relatively fast and simple compared to animal studies. The ability to discriminate between 
effects and cytotoxicity is another key advantage of in vitro studies. 
Some of the studies presented in the literature section showed that OPFRs are rapidly 
eliminated in rats. Although OPFRs may thus be rapidly eliminated in humans as well, it may 
still lead to a potential risk in humans if continuous exposure occurs. Most of the OPFRs 
examined in this project have frequently been found in human matrices like blood, urine or 
breast milk. (Zhao et al., 2016, He et al., 2018, Sundkvist et al., 2010) The highest 
concentration of an OPFR (EHDPP) found in blood was 3.37 nM. EHDPP, found in this study 
to be one of the most potent AR antagonists, has a LOEC of 3.13 µM. This is a 1000-fold 
difference between the exposure concentration and lowest observed effect concentration. 
Although this indicates that the exposure level is safe for EHDPP in this case, toxicity could 
potentially be much higher considering the exposure of several chemicals at the same time. 
Orton et al. (2014) found that a variety of AR antagonistic chemicals have the ability to create 
a joint effect at very low concentrations when exposed together. Mixture effects of several 
chemicals are observed at individual concentrations below their LOEC values (Orton et al., 
2014), and thus even low exposure concentrations of individual anti-androgenic chemicals 
can be of concern. This effect is especially prominent when considering the susceptibility of 
the male fetus during sexual differentiation. Joint effects of anti-androgens have shown 
alterations of the anogenital distance in rats when exposed during gestation. (Hass et al., 
2007) Thus, the exposure to chemical cocktails of anti-androgens during pregnancy could 
potentially lead to an effect of the male fetus and on male fertility.

CONCLUSIONS 
This project aimed to investigate the anti-androgenic effects of organophosphorus flame 
retardants on steroidogenesis and androgen receptor. We could confirm the hypothesis that 
OPFRs exert an anti-androgenic effect when tested in vitro. This effect is expressed by the 
antagonistic activity on the androgen receptor caused by ten out of eleven OPFRs, varying in 
potency. The highest potency was observed for TDCIPP (EC50 6.7 µM) and no effect was 
observed for DPHP. These results were found to correlate well with published literature. We 
could, however, not confirm our hypothesis that OPFRs interfere with the hormone synthesis 
of testosterone and 17β-estradiol in vitro. The reason for this is that we were not able to 
perform statistical analysis, since there was not sufficient time to perform the required number 
of measurements. However, a decreasing trend in testosterone levels was observed, caused by 
TPHP, and an increasing trend in 17β-estradiol levels was indicated, caused by TMPP, 
EHDPP and CDP. Inconsistencies are seen regarding effects on steroidogenesis, both between 
published literature as well as when comparing literature studies to the present results. We 
found a trend for aryl substituted OPFRs, rather than chlorinated OPFRs, to exert an anti-
androgenic effect. This is, however, not supported by literature.  
 
PERSPECTIVES 
As there are uncertainties regarding the results of our first working hypothesis on the effects 
on the steroidogenesis, more studies are needed to reach definite conclusions. Since the 
results obtained in this project are inconsistent with published literature, several laboratories 
should ideally repeat the exposure experiment of OPFRs conducted on H295 cells. This could 
hopefully lead to a clear understanding of the mechanism of action involved in the effect of 
OPFRs on steroidogenesis by getting reproducible results. The focus of further studies could 
thus be to look into several metabolites and the gene expression of enzymes involved in the 
steroidogenic pathway.  
A natural progression of this work is to analyse the mixture toxicity of OPFRs, also including 
other known anti-androgens that humans are commonly exposed to. This would provide 
information about synergistic or antagonistic effects of multiple chemicals in a mixture. 
Also, to enable the further use of in vitro studies in risk assessment, focus should lie on 
physiologically based kinetic modelling and biomonitoring, where relevant data on human 
exposure, like blood samples, should be gathered. 
A recent interesting study by Thomas and Dong (accepted manuscript 2019) discovered a 
novel mechanism for endocrine activity that occurs through binding to the membrane 
androgen receptor, ZIP9. This mAR mediates rapid testosterone-dependent signalling, and 
some pesticides, including prochloraz, were found to act as androgen antagonists in the same 
ways as for the nuclear androgen receptor. This leads to blocking of the mAR signalling. 
Further research should be undertaken to explore how suspected anti-androgens, like OPFRs, 
act on the membrane androgen receptor and thus further explore the MoA. (Thomas and 
Dong, 2019)
