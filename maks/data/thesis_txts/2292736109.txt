ABSTRACT 
 
The present work investigates the mechanical and thermo-chemical pretreatments efficiency on 
lignocellulosic material. Lignocellulosic material is known as a recalcitrant part for anaerobic 
digestion due to its structure and chemical composition. The experimental setup consisted of batch 
and CSTR reactors both operating under thermophilic conditions. The feedstock of the reactors was 
cattle manure and on a second phase, cattle manure co-digested with mechanically or 
thermochemically pretreated digested biofibers. Mechanical pretreatment was obtained using two 
heavy plates, which contact surface was made of different types of material. Thermo-chemical 
pretreatment was achieved using alkaline solution: the base NaOH was incubated with biofibers at 
different temperature and time in order to the lignocellulosic structure to be broken. The experiment 
is thus subdivided into a batch assay and a subsequent continuous mode reactor configuration. 
During the whole experimental period daily measurements were taken in order to control and 
monitor the state of the process.

Chapter 1: Introduction and research objectives 
Anaerobic digestion (AD) process is an extensively applied method for manure management and 
treatment. It presents many advantages compared with other alternative processes such as aerobic 
digestion, alkaline treatment and composting. The main advantages of anaerobic digestion are odors 
minimization, effective reduction of pathogens, low energy demands and biogas production (Chen 
et al., 2008). In addition, in manure digestion systems, the nutrient recovery makes the digested 
substrate a qualitative soil fertilizer. Moreover, it was shown on a life cycle assessment that biogas 
production from manure has the highest reduction effect on greenhouse gas emissions compared 
with the processes for other biofuels production (Thyø & Wenzel, 2007), so this makes the process 
interesting for global warming mitigation. Despite the environmental advantages, the economic 
viability of centralized biogas plants based on manure alone as influent substrate is difficult due to 
the low methane yield per volume unit of manure (Gerin et al., 2008). In addition, in Denmark 
approximately 40 million tons of manure is available each year for bioenergy production (Biswas et 
al., 2012). Nevertheless, less than 10% of this amount is utilized and the biogas plants are currently 
focused on the co-digestion of livestock manure with industrial wastes. These wastes typically 
originate from the food industry and are good candidates for biogas production due to their high 
methane yield, but unfortunately they are not available in high level so as to cover the whole 
demand. Due to this obstacle, possible ways to improve the economy are investigating. The interest 
is mainly focused on finding alternative biomasses or trying to increase the overall process 
efficiency.  
As, the biogas sector in Denmark is mainly based on manure digestion, this substrate is of highly 
interest. The dry content of livestock manure is mainly consisted of lignocellulosic materials. The 
efficient utilization of this feedstock is known to be limited due to the presence of lignin. 
Subsequently, by the end of AD, in the solid part of the digestate is still available a significant 
amount of degradable organic matter that is not converted into biogas. The efficient exploitation of 
this material can positively result in increasing the process viability. 
In the present study, it is investigated the possibility of use the undigested manure biofibers as 
alternative feedstock for biogas production. Nevertheless, the digestion of this recalcitrant biomass 
is not efficient without application of pretreatment method. So, alternative pretreatment methods are 
applied in order to disrupt the biofibers tissue and permit easy access of microbes to the degradable 
components of lignocellulose.

Aims and objectives 
The aim of the project is to evaluate the efficiency of mechanical and thermo-chemical pretreatment 
methods on the anaerobic biodegradability of lignocellulosic residues as the biofibers, for 
enhancing biogas production.  
For the experimental setup, batch and continuous stirred tank reactors (CSTRs) were used. Firstly, 
the efficiency of mechanical and thermo-chemical pretreatment methods was evaluated in batch 
experiments. Also, batch assays were conducted to define the biochemical methane potential of 
cattle manure. Then, CSTR reactors were operated, digesting biofibers pretreated with the most 
efficient mechanical and thermo-chemical pretreatment methods according to the results of the 
batch assays.

Chapter 2: Literature review 
2.1 Anaerobic digestion 
AD is a biological process in which a complex flora of different microorganisms operating under 
anaerobic conditions can convert organic polymeric substances to mainly methane and carbon 
dioxide. The process is mostly subdivided in four stages: hydrolysis, acidogenesis, acetogenesis and 
methanogenesis. Figure 2.1illustrates on a schematic way the general pathways of an anaerobic 
process.  
 
  Figure 2.1 Schematic metabolic pathway on anaerobic digestion process. (Salminen and Rintala, 2002) 
Depending on the operational parameters, the process can be also subdivided on mesophilic and 
thermophilic condition of wet or dry system. For lab scale project thermophilic conditions are 
preferred due to the higher organic matter degradation, particularly when operating with fibers, 
higher pH, higher methane yield and lower HRT, thus operating time (Moset at al., 2015). 
2.1.1 Hydrolysis 
Carbohydrates, protein and lipids are the most abundant polymeric compounds in manure; fibers are 
mainly made of lignocellulose. During hydrolysis different groups of fermentative bacteria can 
degrade the complex polymeric compounds into oligomers and monomers. In this primary step, 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
lipids are hydrolyzed to Long Chain Fatty Acids (LCFA) and glycerol, proteins to polypeptides and 
amino acid and carbohydrates to sugars and alcohols (Salminen and Rintala, 2002). 
2.1.2 Acidogenesis 
During the second step, the organic compounds that were produced in the hydrolysis (amino acids, 
sugars and glycerol) are degraded into volatile fatty acids (VFA), hydrogen, carbon dioxide and 
alcohols. Ammonia is created as by-product from the fermentation of amino acids. The main part of 
the organic substances, approximately 70%, will be degraded by the fermentative bacteria to 
methanogenic substances, such as acetate, CO2 and hydrogen, and the rest 30% of the organic 
substances will be transformed into other lower fatty acid and alcohols.  
2.1.3 Acetogenesis 
During the third step of anaerobic digestion, VFAs and alcohols are oxidized to acetate, carbon 
dioxide and hydrogen. Acetate is produced mainly by acetogenic bacteria, which are obligated H2 
producers. A combination of factors such as linear metabolism and inhibition at high concentration 
level to these bacteria, makes partial H2 pressure an important parameter for the process. 
(Angelidaki, 2011). 
2.1.4 Methanogenesis 
The fourth and last step of the anaerobic degradation is known as methanogenesis. After this step, 
the initial substrate is converted into methane and carbon dioxide, ending on a complete 
mineralization. Methanogenesis can be separated in two metabolic pathways, depending on the type 
of compound utilized. The main part of methane production (70%), due to acetoclastic 
methanogenesis, is formed from acetate and the rest 30% from H2/CO2 or formate due to 
hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis pathway. 
Methanogenic microorganisms belong to the domain of Archaea. According to modern molecular 
biology, Archaea are not classified as bacteria due to their differences in physical properties.

the organic material when the chemical composition is known. In fact under such conditions and 
only with adding of water as external source, the organic matter is completely converted to methane 
and carbon dioxide.

2.2 Biofibers on AD 
According to the goal of the Danish governmental program ‘Green Growth’, the utilization of 40% 
of total manure for energy purposes should be reached by 2020. This corresponds to an increase in 
biogas plant capacity by 8-fold, that practically means the construction of more than 70 new 
centralized biogas plants of the largest scale (Danish Government, 2009). Nowadays manure is the 
main substrate and it will play an important role even to realize the governmental program. Thus an 
economically feasible operation of manure-based biogas plants is a prerequisite in order to fully 
implement the targets of this program. Under an economic point of view, a biogas yield of more 
than 30 m3 per m3 feed is needed to compensate for the transportation costs (Biswas et al., 2012). 
The biogas yield of manure in centralized biogas plants is often lower as the organic matter content 
in manure is typically less than 10%.  Moreover, a large fraction as 40-50 % of total solids are 
fibers, resulting to methane yields that amount only 50% of the methane potential. In fact only a 
part of the fibers is degraded producing a methane yield of 0.2-0.25 m3/Kg VSadded compared to the 
0.4-0.45 m3/KgVSadded of the theoretical methane yield, using cattle manure as substrate (Boe and 
Angelidaki, 2008). Thus, in order to improve the biogas production, there is a need to exploit the 
unused methane potential of manure. Different methods for improving biogas production were 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
investigated ranging from pretreatment of the feedstock to changing on the reactor configuration. 
For what concerns the pretreatment methods, these mainly focus on the alteration of substrate’s 
structure; as the aim is to change the biomass properties by preparing the lignocelluloses for 
enzymatic attack.  
Biofibers are lignocellulosic materials and thus are mainly composed by three major fractions: 
cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin. Most of the biofiber lignocellulose structure consists of 
holocellulose, which is a core of carbohydrates. This part matters up to 63-78% of the biofiber 
structure (Crawford, 1981). After holocellulose the second major constituent of biofibers with 
matters up to 15–38% of the lignocellulose structure is lignin, a natural and very stable binder 
which wraps the holocellulose fraction (Sarkanen and Heregeret, 1971). Lignin is a complex 
polymer composed mainly by three types of aromatic alcohols defined as phenylpropanoid. The 
final physico-chemical structure of lignocellulose gives to this complex structure protection on the 
living plant against physical impacts and protection against microbial attack. Natural degradation of 
lignocellulose is a slow process that involves different microorganisms, each one with special 
abilities to produce enzymes that mainly attack the holocellulose part. The main obstacle in the 
degradation process is the lignin which acts as a barrier for the microorganisms’ accessibility to 
different carbohydrates found in the lignocellulose. Another obstacle in the degradation process is 
attributed to the cellulose crystallinity in some regions of the fibers structure. In general, the 
microbes can more easily degrade the amorphous cellulose part. 
The improvement of the biogas yield of manure has been previously investigated via the application 
of mechanical, chemical, biological, mechanical and thermal pretreatment methods. The 
aforementioned methods disrupt the recalcitrant material in order to make the holocellulosic part 
accessible for the hydrolytic enzymes produced by bacteria that are able to degrade cellulose and 
hemicellulose. Enzymes acts on available cellulose. The effectiveness of pretreatment may 
therefore be due to the generation of available cellulose chain end and decrease the cellulose 
crystallinity. Comparing the different pretreatment methods, it was found that thermochemical 
treatment was the most promising techniques for increasing the biogas yield (Angelidaki and 
Ahring, 2000). 
2.3 Pretreatment methods 
Pretreatment is a key step in to achieve an efficient conversion of lignocellulosic materials into 
more degradable constituents by making them accessible to the microorganism enzymes (Figure 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
2.2). It is basically a prehydrolysis step that prepares the biomass to the enzymatic hydrolysis. The 
main characteristics of lignocellulosic substrate, that make them hardly degradable, are the 
crystallinity of cellulose, cellulose surface area, degree of cellulose polymerization and the lignin 
protection. One of the main goals of the pretreatment methods is to attack the lignin barrier. Lignin 
is tightly bounded to the carbohydrate polymers, wrapping the holocellulose part and protecting the 
biomass from the microbial enzymes attack. It is a natural seal which is not easily biodegradable 
and blocks the way to the enzymes, to convert the carbohydrates into fermentable sugars (Mosier et 
al., 2005). ). So, the disruption of lignin is highly important for the success of the pretreatment 
method. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 2.2 Schematic efficient pretreatment on lignocellulosic material. 
In recent years, pretreatment technologies have been specialized in each lignocellulosic substrate. 
As a result, it is very important to take the right decision to select the correct one among the 
available pretreatment methods. Parameters that should be taken into consideration are for example 
based on the type of biomass, moisture content, time and energy requirement for the process, 
improvement of enzymatic attack on the cellulosic material, inhibitory effects and limited 
production of waste compounds. 
Basically the pretreatment method can be subdivided into physical, chemical, physiochemical and 
biological treatment. In the next subchapters it will be further presented only the physical and 
chemical methods, as these technologies were used in the present Thesis.

2.3.1 Physical pretreatment 
Physical pretreatments are a group of different type of procedures that act mainly on the size 
reduction with simultaneously increasing of functional surface. They can be subdivided into 
mechanical or non-mechanical (e.g. steam explosion) pretreatments. Mechanical pretreatment will 
be described thoroughly, as they were used on the present study. 
Biological degradation of lignocelluloses is a slow and hard process. Mechanical pretreatments act 
mainly to enhance the accessibility of microorganisms to the degradable organic fraction by 
increasing the surface area and reducing the cellulose crystallinity. In this way, the holocellulosic 
part is hydrolyzed more quickly and with higher yields. The size of the fibers is the most important 
parameter for what concern the degradability of the fibers. In general, by reducing the biofibers 
size, the methane potential of the pretreated manure is increasing compared to the corresponding 
one of the untreated manure. Researchers reported that macerated manure to size smaller than 0.35 
mm had a methane potential of 20% higher than untreated manure (Angelidaki and Ahring, 2000). 
If the size range is between 1–2 mm the increase reported was 16%. However, decrease of the size 
from 20 to 5 mm does not significantly increase the methane potential of manure (Angelidaki and 
Ahring, 2000). Moreover size reduction can lead to another advantage, as it is the reduction of the 
digestion time by 23-59% (Hartmann et al., 2000). Different types of machines are available in 
order to decrease the plant tissue and achieve the aforementioned sizes. After chopping the biomass 
particles with size of 10-30mm can be reached; instead after grinding or milling 0.2-2mm of particle 
size can be achieved (Gonzalez-Fernandez et al., 2015). As it was previously reported, the size 
reduction process is very energy consuming and about 33% of the total electrical demand on a plant 
is applied in this step (Kratky and Jirout, 2010). As a consequence, the energy requirement, in 
relation to the final particle size and the characteristic of the initial feedstock, is one of the main 
parameters to be evaluated during pretreatment process management. Moreover, on the choice of 
the right machine to utilize, moisture content of the fiber materials play an important role. If 
feedstock has moisture content above 20% (wet) colloid mills and extruders can be efficiently 
utilized; if instead the water content is under 15% (dry), in this case hammer and knife mills can be 
utilized. As rule, energy requirement increase with higher moisture content and lower final particle 
size. Apart from the well-established methods which efficiently reduce the size of the biomass, 
alternative methods are also available to pretreat recalcitrant biomass. The utilization of heavy 
plates was found to be a promising pretreatment technique for biogas enhancement (Tsapekos et al., 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
2014). The combination of different materials on the surface of metal plates, managed to efficiently 
shred grass silage in order to prepare it for biogas production. The examined pretreatment 
techniques showed positive results on the biomethanation process and the biogas production was 
increased from 8 to 25%. So, this method can be examined as an alternative solution to pretreat 
recalcitrant biomass as the biofibers. 
2.3.2 Thermo-chemical pretreatment 
Chemical pretreatments include the use of acids, bases, ionic liquid and oxidants (Zheng et al., 
2013). It is a well-known and diffuse technique utilized in particular to enhance cellulosic ethanol 
production. Both alkaline and acid pretreatment can be used in order to pretreat biofibers, to the 
final goal of making lignocellulosic biomass more degradable to microorganisms. Feedstocks on 
which chemical treatments were effectively applied were mainly agricultural and forest residues. 
Chemical methods are often used together with thermal pretreatment. Several studies reported an 
increase of biogas production after chemical and thermo-chemical pretreatments of several kinds of 
lignocellulosic substrates. 
Acid pretreatment can be applied on a wide range of conditions; concentrated acid (30-70%) and 
low temperature (40 °C) or dilute acid and high temperature (230 °C) with both organic and 
inorganic acids. Sulfuric acid (H2SO4) is shown to be one of the most utilized and effective acids. 
Although the concentrated pretreatment is considered to be more efficient than the dilute acid, due 
the danger of presenting toxicity and corrosive problems, the diluted acid pretreatment is wider 
applied for lignocellulosic biomass pretreatment (Zheng et al., 2013). However, diluted acid 
pretreatment was found on some researches to have negative effect on methane potential 
(Antonopoulou et al., 2010). Therefore acid pretreatment conditions such as acid concentration, 
temperature and residence time should be selected properly to reduce the formation of inhibitors 
(e.g. H2S, when H2SO4 is used). 
The alternate solution of chemical pretreatment is to use a base instead of acid. Alkaline 
pretreatment can be achieved utilizing bases as ammonium, sodium hypochlorite, ammonium 
hydroxide, sodium hydroxide and calcium hydroxide. This kind of pretreatment lead to increase of 
accessible surface area, solubilization of lignin and alteration of lignin structure (Zheng et al., 
2013). The utilization of NaOH is known to be one of the most promising alkaline treatments 
(Zheng et al., 2009), since positive results reported by several researches. As it was shown in the 
past, NaOH can efficiently break down the bound between lignin and holocellulose and decrease 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
the degree of polymerization and crystallinity of cellulose (Fan et al., 1987). Moreover, it is a well-
known method that has been extensively used for several years even on pulp and paper industry 
(Zheng et al., 2013). Furthermore, the application of steam treatment with NaOH has been reported 
to increase biogas production up to 50% on sorted municipal solid waste (Wang et al., 2009). 
Higher methane production using 4% NaOH at 55° C for 24h was achieved with higher dissolution 
of lignin, compared to acid pretreatment (Monlau et al., 2012). Treating corn stover with 2% NaOH 
at 20° C for 3 days and a moisture content of 90%, reached an enhancement of 73% of biogas 
production, with concomitant 45% shorter digestion time (Zheng et al., 2009). Possible adverse 
conditions can result by the use of NaOH; the pretreatment can cause Na+ ion inhibition of 
methanogenesis. Moreover, the release of Na+ in the environment with the effluent might lead to 
soil salinization and water pollution. 
Mechanical and thermo-chemical pretreatments pose the ability to enhance significantly the biogas 
production. Alternatively, the combination of different categories of pretreatment methods was also 
examined in the literature. However, when mechanical and chemical pretreatment methods were 
combined, they did not lead in higher enhancement of biogas production (Angelidaki and Ahring, 
2000). To sum up, a number of alternative pretreatment methods are available in order to prepare 
the biomass for bioenergy production. Consequently, the selection of the proper one is of primary 
importance for a successful biological process.

3.2 Experimental setup  
3.2.1 .Batch experiments 
The biochemical methane potential (BMP) is used in order to investigate the ultimate methane 
potential of the biomass. The experiment was conducted using glass bottle reactors. The working 
and the total volume of the bottles were 164 and 547 ml respectively and thus, the headspace was 
383 ml. Batch reactors were filled with inoculum, water and substrate. Moreover, batches 
containing only inoculum were used as blanks, in order to calculate the biogas production from the 
inoculum and reactors with glucose were used as control. 
According to the organic matter content of the biofibers (0.192 gVS/g), the substrate quantity that 
should be added in the batch reactors was calculated as 2.31 g of wet fibers. The Inoculum to 
Substrate ratio (ISR) was set up to be equal to 2. Thus, the VS of inocula in the bottles were 
measured to be 0.89 g. After the filling procedure and in order to ensure the anaerobic conditions, 
all bottles were flushed with gas mixture, containing 80% nitrogen and 20% carbon dioxide. Then, 
the bottles were sealed with rubber stoppers and aluminum screw caps. Subsequently, all batches 
were incubated under thermophilic conditions (55 °C) as depicted in Figure 3.1. The experimental 
==PAGE:21==

setup of the batch series is shown on Table 3.4. Batch bottles were manually shaken daily in order 
to mix the substrate and avoid the creation of dead zones. In order to check the reproducibility of 
the test, the batches were run on triplicates. The methane production in the batch reactors was 
measured two or three times per week and degassed regularly, in order to avoid the creation of 
overpressure in the headspace. Subsequently, the degassed amount of biogas was taken into account 
for the calculations. The measurements of methane production were stopped when the methane 
production was stabilized in the experiments.

3.2.2. Continuous mode experiments 
For the continuous mode experiment, four CSTR reactors were used. The selected three 
pretreatment methods (i.e. the ones that were found to be more efficient according to the results 
from the batch experiment) were tested on continuous mode operation. One reactor was fed with 
untreated biofibers and kept as control reactor for the evaluation of the pretreatment efficiency. The 
reactors had a total volume of 3.5 L and a working volume of 2.4 L, respectively. The hydraulic 
retention time (HRT) was kept constant at 15 days and so, 160 ml of feed was daily fed to each 
reactor. The feeding was twice per day using peristaltic pumps. The reactors operated under 
thermophilic conditions with a constant temperature of 53 °C using thermal jackets. Each reactor 
was equipped with a magnetic stirring device to keep the substrate homogeneous and to avoid the 
creation of dead zones. The influent bottles were also equipped with stirred bars in order to keep the 
solution homogeneous. In Figure 3.2 is schematically illustrated the configuration of a CSTR 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
reactor system. The biogas production was measured daily by the water displacement method using 
calibrated gas meters and gas counters (Angelidaki et al., 1992). During the experiment the 
following parameters were measured: pH, VFA, biogas production, methane content in biogas, 
TKN content, N-NH4 content, TS and VS of the substrate and VS reduction. 
For the start-up of the experiments, the working volume of the reactors was filled with 2.4 L of 
inoculum. During the start-up period the reactors were fed only with cattle manure (Period 1). Then, 
after the initial period and when the biogas production was stabilized, the feedstock composition 
changed (Period 2). Biofibers were introduced in the feed representing 30% of the organic matter 
content and subsequently the share of cattle manure was 70%, as reported in Table 3.5. 
The Organic loading rate (OLR) was 1.94 gVS/L/day during the period 1. During period 2 the OLR 
was gradually increased to 2.54 gVS/L/day. Concerning the feedstock characteristics, the organic 
matter of feedstock in period 1 was 29.12 gVS/kg and 37.85 gVS/kg during period 2.

3.4.4. VFA determination  
Volatile fatty acids were determined by gas chromatography using GC Shimadzu GC-2010. (Figure 
3.8) The samples were obtained directly from each reactor, using a 100ml syringe; then 3.0 mL of 
distilled water and 3.0 mL of sample were collected in a plastic tube. The excess of sample 
collected were store at -20 C. The sample in the plastic tube was acidified with 400 μL of a 34% 
H3PO4 solution, for moving the VFAs in their acidic form and to eliminate microbial activity. Then, 
the acidified solution was centrifuged at 13400 rpm for 10 minutes. Then, 1.0 mL supernatant 
sample was stored in a GC glass vial, with the addition of 100.0 μL internal injection standard 
solution (1.1 mM 4-Methyl valeric acid). Samples were taken in duplicate and were stored in a 
freezer at -22 °C until analysis by Gas Chromatography. 
The Gas Chromatographer, GC Shimadzu GC-2010, was equipped with Flame Ionization Detector 
(FID) and a capillary column (ZB – FFAP, 30 m, 0.53 mm I.D x 1.0 μm) in order to separate the 
compounds. The liquid sample was injected into the GC Shimadzu GC-2010 and the determination 
of the VFAs was achieved by a linear calibration curve obtained by standards made from a 
concentration of 5 to 1500 mg/l. As a result, acetic acid, n- and iso-butyric acid, n- and iso-valeric 
acid, propanoic acid and n-hexanoic acid were detected.

3.4.5. Biogas production 
Biogas production was measured daily by the water displacement method (Angelidaki et al., 1992). 
Each reactor was connected with a gas meter. The amount of the produced biogas was entered into 
the gas meter column displacing equal amount of water. The gas meters were equipped with an 
electro-optic sensor. When 100 mL of biogas entered into the gas meter the displaced water was 
reaching the sensor and the entrapped biogas was released. Simultaneously, the sensor was sending 
a signal to the connected gas counter. 
3.4.6. Biogas composition  
For the continuous mode experiment the methane content in the produced biogas was determined by 
gas chromatography using GC Shimadzu, equipped with a packed column for compound separation 
and a thermal-conductivity detector (TCD). The injector and detector temperature is set to 50 °C. 
The oven temperature is set to 50 °C. Hydrogen was used as the carrier gas. The sample injection 
volume was 0.5 mL. The linear calibration curve was obtained by standard gas, which contained 
30% N2, 30% CO2 and 40% CH4, in samples of 0.2 mL, 0.5 mL and 1.0 mL. This calibration curve 
was used to determine the percentage of methane and carbon dioxide in the samples. In fig 3.9 is 
possible to see the calibration curve and the peaks corresponding to N2, CH4 and CO2.

3.4.7 Determination of structural carbohydrates and lignin 
Structural carbohydrates and lignin in solid biomass, as biofibers, can be determined by conducting 
a strong acid hydrolysis using H2SO4 (Sluiter et al., 2011). 150 mg of biofibers were weighted on an 
analytical balance. The sample was then transferred into a tube and 1.5 ml of H2SO4 was added. 
After 60 minutes of digestion at 30C, in which the sample were stirred every 10 minutes, 42ml of 
water were added. The sample was then autoclaved for 60 minutes at 121°C and filtered. 1 ml of 
filtered sample is move into HPLC vial for the determination of structural carbohydrates: the 
holocellulose fraction: cellulose and hemicellulose. The materials on the filter were used for 
determination of lignin. Filter paper after vacuum filtration was first dried and then incinerated.

3.4.8 Determination of COD 
Chemical oxygen demand (COD), is the amount of O2 required to oxidize organic compounds, and 
some inorganic such as ferrous iron and sulfide, present on a sample. It is determined 
experimentally by measuring the amount of chemical oxidizing agent needed to completely oxidize 
a sample. 
The destruction takes place at 148oC for 110 minutes in sulfuric acid with silversulfate as a catalyst, 
according to standard methods (APHA, 1998). The Cr2O7
2- is reduced to 2Cr3+ while organics 
compound are oxidized. The remaining Cr6+ is determined, on a second step, by titration with 
ferroammoniumsulfate (FAS) added redox indicator 1,1 0-fenantrolin, that shifts from clear 
greenish to rusty red when Cr6+ is reduced. The COD results for the samples are based on the 
amount Cr6+ that has been reduced due to destruction, in fact at beginning a specific amount of 
reagent is added. Controls and blanks are made in every run. For each run FAS is determined with a 
separate dichromate solution. During COD analysis, 90-95% of the organic matter is oxidized 
(Angelidaki, 2011). COD results for manure, are useful coupled with VS values. Theoretical 
COD/VS ratio for carbohydrate is 1.19 g-COD/g-VS. In the samples different values denote 
presence of different compounds such as lipids, proteins and lignin.

4.1 Batch assays

differently pretreated biofibers and also, cattle manure. During the batch experiments, blank 
reactors containing only water and inoculum were installed in order to estimate the methane

biofibers were undergone to different mechanical and thermo-chemical pretreatment methods 
before setting up the batch assays. Thus, the main aim of the batch assays was to determine the most 
efficient pretreatments which would lead to the highest methane production enhancement. The 
experiment lasted for 28 days, as after that period the methane production was ceased. All the

4.2 Continuous mode experiments  
The continuous mode experiments were conducted in order to evaluate the biogas and methane 
production of biofibers co-digested with cattle manure under practical process. Moreover, another 
goal was to evaluate the efficiency of chosen pretreatment methods compared to the untreated 
biofibers. The whole experiment was divided in two phases, in the first, the feedstock contained 
only cattle manure and in the second, the feedstock was a mixture of cattle manure with biofibers. 
The results from the data analysis, which were recorded and measured during the whole 
experimental period, are given in the next two subchapters. The results are presented as a 
comparison to the untreated fibers. 
For the first 3 HRT the reactors were fed only with cattle manure, so that microbes could adapt to 
the substrate and reach stable conditions. Figure 4.11 depicts the daily data of biogas production. R1 
had a slightly higher biogas production compared to R2 during the start-up period. Nevertheless, the 
average biogas yields did not differ significantly.

process stability during the experiment. It is known that an increase in TVFAs concentration is an 
indicator of process instability or inhibition in particular for acetogenic producing bacteria and 
methanogenic process. Thus, TVFAs are measured during the anaerobic digestion process as 
indicator of process instabilities (Angelidaki et al., 2011). Nevertheless, it is assumed that no 
significant problems in the process were presented. Concerning R1, TVFAs concentration was 
always below 1g/L, and almost constant, except for the last days of the experiment. Probably, 
further continuation of the experiment would present more significant problems in the AD, as the 
trend of TVFA was increased gradually.

Chapter 5: Conclusions and future work   
5.1 Conclusions 
The purpose of this present work was to evaluate the efficiency of pretreatments of biofibers 
materials came from already digested manure. Batches and continuous mode experiments were 
conducted to meet this goal.  
The results, obtained from the batch experiments, showed increased process performance efficiency 
by pretreating the biofibers. 
From the results of the continuous mode experiments it can be concluded that pretreated biofibers 
lead to higher methane production. Thermo-chemical pretreatment contributed in the achievement 
of more enhanced biogas production compared to the mechanical pretreatment methods. Moreover 
reactors treating mechanically pretreated biofibers showed higher accumulation of undigested 
material. This can suggest that while using mechanical pretreatment the solid contribution of the 
fibers in the influent feedstock should be less than 30% in order to avoid inhibition. 
The batch experiments presented higher methane enhancement compared to the continuous 
experiments using certain pretreatment methods. This can be attributed to the nature of the substrate 
used which is quite recalcitrant. 
Generally, it can be concluded that in this work the thermo-chemical pretreatment was more 
efficient compared to mechanical pretreatment. Nevertheless, both of them contributed in higher 
methane yields compared to the untreated biofibers. 
 
5.2 Future work 
As future work the following issues could be addressed: 
 To test different pretreatment methods, especially in continuous mode experiments, since 
there is a lack of information in the literature. 
 To apply the pretreatments used in the present work in other lignocellulosic substrates. 
 To characterise the microbial community populating the biogas reactors treating 
lignocellulosic substrates. 
 To examine potential improvement in the process performance by alternating other 
operational parameters (i.e temperature, longer HRT etc).
