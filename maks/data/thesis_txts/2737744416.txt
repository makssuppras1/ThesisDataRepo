Abstract
One of the greatest challenges our society faces today is the fight against cli­
mate change, the main driver of which is the release of greenhouse gases, more
specifically, CO2. This can potentially be mitigated by directly targeting anthro­
pogenic CO2 emissions. One way to do this is to directly capture the CO2 from
industrial processes with heavy emissions, with the utilization of carbon capture
technologies. One important process step in the carbon capture value chain is
intermediate storage. This process step was the focus of this study.
A thermodynamic model was made of a storage tank that should store liquid CO2.
The design of the tank, storage period, storage pressure and various other param­
eters were investigated, and a techno­economic analysis performed. The storage
tank receives liquid CO2 from a liquefaction plant at a given pressure and should
store it for a set time period, before delivering the liquid to a road carrier.
It was seen that any change in design parameters has minimal affect on the lev­
elized cost of storage, furthermore, that the operational expenditure of a stor­
age tank has little influence on the overall cost. The only design parameter that
showed any reduction in the overall cost was the material selection of the tank,
when changed from stainless steel to carbon steel, the overall cost decreased by
62.76 %.
Additionally, the difference in initial investment based on the desired storage pe­
riod, showed that investing in a smaller tank to accommodate 24 hour storage has
a lower levelized cost of 28.79 DKK/tCO2, than a larger tank that accommodates a
120 hour storage period, which has an overall cost of 38.79 DKK/tCO2. However,
investing in the larger tank to start, gives the opportunity of operational growth,
and a larger quantity can be stored daily, which over time lowers the levelized
cost, for the trade­off of a shorter storage period.
Additionally, it was seen that the levelized cost of storage is very low in comparison
to the levelized cost of other process steps within the carbon capture value chain.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
One of the greatest challenges our society faces today is the fight against cli­
mate change, an unfortunate side effect of our technological and societal ad­
vancements. Climate change is the cause of various irreversible damage to our
planet, such as severe weather changes, water and food scarcity, and declining
biodiversity [1]. The main driver of climate change is global warming, which is de­
scribed as the increase in Earth’s average surface temperature. This increase is
largely caused by greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions into the atmosphere, which
has seen constant escalation since the industrial revolution [2, 3]. The primary
contributor to this greenhouse effect is Carbon Dioxide (CO2), which constitutes
approximately 75% of total GHG emissions [3]. Due to this, CO2 concentration in
the atmosphere is at an all­time high at 423 ppm [4] compared to pre­industrial
levels of 280 ppm [5].
Understanding the sources and sectors contributing to these high CO2 levels is
critical. The continuous increase in industrialization and urbanization has been the
main driver for the rise in energy demand, and the energy sector is consequently
responsible for the highest global CO2 emissions as global energy production is
still heavily reliant on fossil fuels. The power sector is responsible for a total of
14.65 GtCO2 or approximately 42.16% followed by the industry sector with 9.15
GtCO2 or 26.33% as of 2022 [6], with transport related emissions not far behind.
As energy demand continues to increase with a growing population, these sectors
hold a large potential to reduce anthropogenic CO2 emissions. Direct CO2 capture
with the utilization of Carbon Capture (CC) technologies could play a critical role
in reducing emissions from these sectors. While fossil fuel based energy could
to some extent be mitigated with the replacement of renewable energy sources,
electrification and increased energy efficiency, other sectors, mainly industrial pro­
cesses such as the production of cement, steel, iron, and chemicals have CO2 as
a direct by­product. The emissions from these sectors are difficult to mitigate, as
they show technological and economic challenges [7].
Recognising the severity of the climate crisis, the Paris Agreement was estab­
lished in 2015. This legally binding international treaty on climate change, seeks
to limit the global average temperature increase to 2 °C above pre­industrial lev­
els, with efforts to limit it at 1.5 °C [8]. Additionally, this treaty seeks to lower
emissions by 43% by 2030 [8], this underscores the importance of green tech­
nology initiatives like CC technologies. Furthermore, the Net­Zero Industry act
(NZIA) has set a goal of net zero GHG emissions by 2050 through investments
in green technology such as CC technologies. The NZIA has set a target of 50
MtCO2 of annual CO2 storage by 2030 to facilitate Carbon Capture and Storage
(CCS) [9]. As of 2023 the total annual CO2 capture capacity was just over 45
MtCO2, while the required amount is 1.2 GtCO2 captured annually to reach goals of
net zero emissions by 2050 [10].

CC technologies are expected to account for approximately 12 % of the cumula­
tive emissions reductions in the energy sector through 2050, with 14 Gt of nega­
tive emissions from Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage (BECCS), which
compensates for higher emissions from other processes in the energy sector. The
incorporation of CC technologies are subsequently thought to be an integral part
of reaching the goals of the Paris Agreement and are of great interest as they allow
for continuing utilisation of existing facilities (e.g. fossil fuel based power plants) as
well as emission reduction from industries that experience challenges with tech­
nological advancements (e.g. production of cement, steel, iron and chemicals)
[7].
With these ambitious goals and the technology still under development [11, 12],
it is imperative to improve on the technology. As previously mentioned the total
annual capture capacity is just over 45 MtCO2, with a goal of 7.6 GtCO2 captured
in 2050 [11], if the annual capture capacity stays constant, that only amounts to
1.2 GtCO2 captured from now until 2050. This certainly highlights the importance
of improvement and innovation as well as reducing costs related to the CC value
chain to scale up capacity to meet these goals, as CC technologies are deemed
to be far too expensive [13].
A significant portion of the existing literature and research primarily emphasises
the initial and final stages of CC, as well as pipeline transport. Thus, there is a no­
table gap in literature regarding the process steps in between, with many articles
failing to address the concept of intermediate storage altogether. It is important
not to overlook intermediate storage, as it serves as a vital link between the con­
tinuous CO2 capture process and the intermittent transfer via ships or vehicles.
Even in systems that depend on pipeline transport, there is a necessity for inter­
mediate storage to accommodate fluctuations in CO2 volumes and unexpected
changes in capacity throughout the CC value chain [14].
1.2
State of the Art
This section gives an overview of state­of­the­art­technology for relevant process
steps within the CCUS value chain. The inclusion of the preceding and succeeding
process steps to the intermediate storage is to get a comprehensive overview of
the boundary conditions for the intermediate storage, and as a result identify the
requirements for this process step.
The structure of this section is as follows: In Section 1.2.1 a brief description of the
process as a whole is given. This is followed by Sections 1.2.2, 1.2.3 and 1.2.4,
in which the transportation methods, pressurization and liquefaction process, and
end­purpose, respectively, are described. These steps influence the requirements
for the intermediate storage process. The process step of interest is then finally
described in Section 1.2.5.
1.2.1
The Carbon Capture Value Chain
The process flow of CC technologies can be categorised as Carbon Capture and
Utilization, and Carbon Capture and Storage, together they form Carbon Capture
Utilization and Storage (CCUS). This combined process flow is illustrated in Fig­
ure 1.1. Initially, a CO2 source is required, which is subsequently captured and

separated using technology that is compatible with the source type and concen­
tration of CO2. A stream of concentrated CO2 is then purified and pressurized by
compression and/or liquefaction. Pure and pressurized CO2 is then transported
using various methods to its end­purpose. The addition of intermediate storage
is in many cases placed before and after transportation. The focus of this study
is on onsite intermediate storage (marked with a blue box in Fig. 1.1). This is

1.2.2
Transport
After purification and pressurization, the CO2 is transported to its end­purpose
destination, whether that be utilization or sequestration. The different modes of
transport are through pipelines, shipping, road and rail, with pipelines and ship­
ping being the most widely used methods. All of these transportation methods
have their advantages and disadvantages and different requirements for the CO2
conditions, however it is optimal to increase the density with compression and/or
liquefaction to reduce the transportation costs. Solid CO2 (dry ice) has high den­
sity as well, but is impractical, thus solid state transportation is not considered a
viable option [15].
Following is a sectioned overview of each of these methods.
Pipeline Transport
For large scale operations pipeline transport is considered to be the most com­
mon and viable option, and is favored for longer distances but it is most feasible
for large amounts of CO2 for shorter distances [15, 16]. Pipeline transport is a
very well established transportation method for CO2, especially in North America,
where it is mainly utilized for Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) [17, 7].
Pipeline transport operations where phase change may occur are not optimal, thus
pipeline transport is often operated above critical pressure to avoid this. That is
at pressures greater than 74 bar and a temperature higher than 31 °C, typically at
pressures between 80 bar and 150 bar [15].
Liquid and gaseous CO2 at lower pressures are additional options for pipeline
transport [15]. However, as aforementioned it is desirable to increase the density

to reduce transportation costs, thus gaseous CO2 will not be of interest in this
study. Liquid CO2 is a viable option for pipeline transport and is thought to be a
safer option, as the pipeline has a much lower operating pressure, but in return
has a higher investment cost due to this and the need for insulation layers [16].
For both methods of pipeline transport it is of interest to limit the water content
by dehydrating the CO2 before transportation, to avoid corrosion and formation of
hydrates. Much higher restrictions are set for liquid CO2 due to low temperatures
and the risk of ice formation and operational problems [15].
Shipping Transport
Shipping transport is well established technology for similar processes, and is
commonly used for liquefied natural­ and petroleum gas (LNG and LPG).
There are three types of tank structures for liquid gas transport ships: pressure,
low temperature and semi­refrigerated.
Most small gas carriers are pressure
types to prevent the gas from boiling under ambient air conditions, while low tem­
perature types are used for larger operations to minimise tank size restrictions
and still keeping the gas liquefied. Semi­refrigerated are used for tanks that are
designed specifically to transport CO2, as they take into account the combined
conditions of temperature and pressure needed to keep the CO2 in a liquid state
[18].
This is thought to be a feasible transportation method for long distances, even
more so than pipeline transport [7], for medium to large amounts of CO2, but there
has been some experience in the past with smaller operations as well.
The standard transport conditions are in the range 15 bar to 18 bar and ­25 °C to
­30 °C, however there have been some considerations of using LPG transporta­
tion ships which could transport CO2 at 7 bar and ­50 °C. While transportation
at 7 bar has the potential for significant cost reduction [19] it may not be optimal
due to the high density of CO2 compared to LPG [15]. Additionally, transportation
in the 15 bar to 18 bar range is a far more advanced technology and will likely
be selected for upcoming projects, and with further efforts transportation at 7 bar
could become a safe and viable option at a large­scale [19].
Ship transportation is in addition to this a more flexible solution than pipeline trans­
port and has the opportunity for growth which could be converted into a pipeline
network in the future [7].
Road and Rail Transport
CO2 road carriers are widely used as a transportation method of liquid CO2, with
capacities between 25 m3 and 30 m3. The standard conditions for transportation
is between 15 bar and 18 bar and ­25 °C to ­30 °C and the tanks are usually
insulated to maintain these conditions [15].
Rail transport is not commonly used, and is mainly used for small scale operations,
and there are no examples of large amounts of CO2 transported via rail, and it is
not considered a feasible option in Denmark [15].

1.2.3
Pressurization and Liquefaction
After the captured CO2 has been separated and purified it goes through a pressur­
ization cycle. The CO2 is either compressed with a multi­stage compression cycle
with intercooling, to a desired pressure; or liquefied at a lower pressure using a
refrigeration cycle and then pumped to a desired pressure.
As previously mentioned, higher pressures are preferred for pipeline transport and
this is achieved through multi­stage compression with intercooling.
However, for ship­ and road transportation the preferred conditions are liquefied
CO2. To achieve these conditions, the CO2 goes through a multi­stage compres­
sion cycle with intercooling to reach a suitable pressure for liquefaction. After
compression, any remaining impurities are removed and the CO2 is finally passed
through a refrigeration cycle to reach liquid state [15].
Liquefaction system can be quite energy intensive due to the refrigeration cycle
and it is desirable to minimize the energy consumption as well as keeping the
liquefaction cost low [20]. Some studies have shown that the cost of liquefaction
decreases with an increase in delivery pressure [20, 21], making it interesting to
study in combination with intermediate storage and transportation.
1.2.4
Utilization and Sequestration
Captured CO2 can ultimately be either utilized or sequestered. There are around
230 MtCO2 used annually, the majority of which is utilized for fertilizer production,
EOR and the food and beverage industry. All of these are categorized as direct­
use, while CO2 utilization holds a lot of potential for fuel, building materials, and
chemical production. All of these forms of utilization have purity requirements for
the CO2, however the food and beverage industry holds the strictest requirements
of 99.9 % purity [22, 23].
For sequestration the options that hold the largest capacities and are most promis­
ing are injections into geological formation. This includes depleted oil and gas
fields and deep saline aquifers [7]. In both of these cases the CO2 is injected at
high pressure into a geological formation of porous rocks, typically deeper than
800 m below Earth’s surface, and is then covered with a barrier on top to prevent
leakage [7, 24].

1.2.5
Intermediate Storage
This section introduces the concept of onsite intermediate storage within the CCUS
value chain, and is the process step of focus in this study. Intermediate storage
plays a critical role in bridging the gap between the continuous process of CO2
capture at source sites and the batch­wise process of road ­ and shipping trans­
portation. The CO2 is typically stored in liquid form, at an elevated pressure and
low temperature to increase the density. A brief summary of typical storage oper­
ations is given. It is followed by a section on material selection and sizing.
In this process step, CO2 should enter a storage tank in liquid form, and should
be able to be stored from between 1 and 5 days. The outlet conditions depend on
the requirement of the following transportation method.
Storage Operation
The operation of a storage tank is simple, it consists of, filling up the tank, emptying
the tank, and heat input from the environment.
The storage tank is filled up with a continuous stream of liquid CO2 from a lique­
faction process. This leads to an increase in the liquid level within the storage
tank and compression of the vapor phase above the liquid. To prevent excessive
6
Intermediate storage of CO2 for CCUS infrastructure
==PAGE:21==
pressure build­up, this vapor is let out of the tank via a valve and re­circulated
back to the liquefaction process as the tank is being filled. Additionally, heat input
from the environment will cause the liquid CO2 to vaporize, this vapor is let out of
the tank via a valve as well, and re­liquefied, to maintain the pressure­ and tem­
perature level within the tank. The storage duration then varies between operation
facilities, with offloading ranging from once every few days to several times a day
[26].
The storage tank is emptied by a road carrier that is operated at conditions de­
scribed in Section 1.2.2 . Loading time on to the carrier is minimized with transfer
at high flow rates, typically 20 t/h [15]. The removal of liquid CO2 from the storage
tank will reduce the pressure in the tank if not compensated by feeding CO2 vapor
into the tank simultaneously. This vapor is then circulated back to be re­liquefied
as the tank is filled up again.
Storage Conditions
Typical onsite storage conditions are set to match the operating conditions of the
road carriers, between 15 bar and 18 bar and ­25 °C and ­30 °C, with some facili­
ties opting for storage at higher or lower pressure levels [15]. Liquid CO2 at higher
pressures has a lower density and consequently less can be stored. However, an
increase in pressure does allow for an increase in temperature, thus lowering the
difference in temperature inside and outside the tank. A lower temperature differ­
ence does result in less liquid CO2 boiling off. Additionally it is optimal to fill the
tank up as much as possible to keep boil off to a minimum, as it requires more
energy input and time to heat up a larger volume, for the liquid to start to vaporize.
Typical maximum fill levels of pressure vessels for liquid CO2 storage ranges from
93% to 95%, and boil off rates are between 0.06 %/day to 0.22 %/day according
to storage tank manufacturers[27, 28].
Storage Tank Shapes and Materials
For liquid CO2 storage and similar processes the most commonly used storage
tanks are pressure vessel [29] and cryogenic storage tanks [27, 30, 31]. Different
types of storage tanks offer varying advantages and considerations in terms of
thermodynamics, economics, and practical implementation. The most common
shapes of storage tanks are cylindrical and spherical. Even though spherical ves­
sels require a thinner wall than cylindrical vessels for the same internal pressure
and have a reduced surface are, cylindrical vessels are preferred over spherical
ones, as they are easier to manufacture and install, cheaper to construct and can
be arranged in a more efficient manner. Now, cylindrical vessels can be either
horizontal or vertical, with horizontal vessels being more common[29].
The material selection does not vary much, the most common material for the inner
vessel is stainless steel [27] or carbon steel [28]. The wall thickness of the vessels
does increase with an increase in pressure, however a wall thickness greater than
0.04 m is not recommended [20]. This does limit the maximum size of the vessels
as their operating pressure is increased. Table 1.2 shows the maximum vessel
size at different operating pressures. The insulation material most commonly used
is polyurethane foam, with a thickness ranging from 0.15 m to 0.2 m [28], or perlite

1.3
Scope
This project focused on onsite intermediate storage within the CCUS value chain.
In this process step a storage tank receives liquid CO2 from a liquefaction process
and should deliver liquid CO2 at a pressure suitable for road transportation. The
baseline system investigated in this project was the storage of liquid CO2 from a
liquefaction plant that delivers 20 ktCO2/year. The baseline system was investi­
gated for 6 different pressure levels and a storage period of 1 and 5 days. The
liquid CO2 was then delivered at storage pressure. A techno­economic analysis
was conducted for all scenarios to investigate how both capital­ and operational
expenditure varies with changes in the system. An alternative system layout was
made, this requires the CO2 to be delivered at a suitable pressure for road trans­
portation, for all operating pressures above the normal operating pressure, that
is. The alternative system was investigated for 5 different pressure levels as well
as a storage period of 1 and 5 days. And finally a techno­economic analysis was
conducted. It was investigated whether the design of the tank had any affect on
the capital­ and operational expenditure, that is, material selection, dimensions,
wall thickness and ambient temperature. The project aimed to investigate the
following overarching research question:
How do variations in operating conditions and tank design impact the capital­ and
operational expenditure of the system, and what are the main trade­offs?
To answer the research question, the following sub­questions were addressed:
• How does the boil off rate vary with an increase in pressure and change in
other parameters?
• How does the tank design impact the capital­ and operational expenditure?

Discussion
This chapter includes a discussion on the limitations of the modeling, as well as
thoughts on some results. Firstly the assumption of pure CO2 is discussed, fol­
lowed by the validity of the scaling exponent used. This is followed by a discus­
sion on the fill­up and emptying time being neglected in the modeling. Following
is a short discussion on the limitations of high­pressure storage and the results
of alternative tank design compositions are discussed. Finally, a short discussion
on facility growth opportunities from initial investments, followed by thoughts on
future work.
Impurities
For the modeling, it was assumed that the CO2 was rid of all impurities due to the
high requirements set for utilization and sequestration. Although the standards are
not as high for sequestration, facilities are expected to deliver food­ and beverage
grade quality liquid CO2 [26]. Liquid CO2 that contains some impurities causes
a two phase region to open up, large amounts of impurities increase this region,
more so if the properties are very different. This causes the bubble and dew
point to be further apart at low temperatures and consequently requires higher
pressures to reach a liquid state, which in turn would require a higher operating
pressure for storage. However, at a requirement of 99.9 % purity, any additional
impurities are unlikely to affect the behaviour of the liquid, and storage conditions
should be similar to that of pure CO2 [46]. Pure liquid CO2 is thought to be sufficient
enough for the modeling, as 0.01 % of any impurity is not thought to affect the
storage conditions drastically.
Scaling exponent
The scaling exponent used to evaluate the PEC of other sizes of tanks was cal­
culated using Equation 2.10. It was determined based on two known sizes and
their corresponding PECs, which makes the exponent valid between those two
sizes, or from 100 m3 to 196 m3, this range was stretched to 50 m3 to 300 m3.
Other scaling exponents are suggested in literature [42], or α = 0.3 for sizes up
to 150 m3 and α = 0.65 for volumes beyond that. These scaling exponents were
evaluated and results are shown in Figure 4.1, this is done for a 59 m3 tank where
α = 0.17 and α = 0.3 are compared and for a 294 m3 tank where α = 0.17 and
α = 0.65 are compared, they are designated as 1 and 2, respectively. These tank
sizes are the ones used for 24 hour and 120 hour storage at a 15 bar storage
pressure. The alternative scaling exponent for the smaller tank shows a 6.2 %
decrease in LcoS, which is a more substantial reduction than observed with the
change in cost of liquefaction and maintenance cost performed in the sensitivity
analysis (Fig. 3.9). The larger tank, however, has a 80.3 % increase in LCoS with
the alternative scaling exponent, which is a more substantial effect than seen with
any parameter varied in the sensitivity analysis (Fig. A.10).
These results highlight the significant impact of scaling exponents on the LCoS.

The fill up­ and emptying time was neglected in this study as the main focus was
on the storage conditions themselves. That is, it was assumed that the storage
tank would receive a pre­determined amount of liquid CO2 each day and it should
be stored for either 24 hours or 120 hours. This does not reflect real life conditions,
and does not represent the total amount lost through vaporization as accurately
as possible. Emptying the tank, ideally does not take a long time and the flow rate
is set as high as possible to minimize this, however, due to that, some of the liquid
vaporizes and replaces the CO2 gas in the truck, which is let out and circulated
back to be re­liquefied. This amount is expected to be greater than the amount
lost through heat ingress [15]. For storage at 15 bar and 24 hours, this would
be 3 % of the liquid amount that is sent to the road carrier, this fraction however
increases with an increase in pressure due to the smaller difference in densities
of the gas and liquid phases [15].

Limitations of High Pressure Storage
The high pressure storage options looked promising at first, with the decrease in
liquefaction cost, as the main operational expenditure of storage tank is the re­
liquefaction of BOG. However, due to these tanks being so well insulated, this
loss is fairly minimal and has little to no affect on the LCoS. Even when combined
with the LCoL, the 15 bar storage still has the lowest overall cost, this is purely
due to the PEC of the tanks. The higher pressure limits the size of the tank quite
substantially as well, which is undesirable, the upfront cost is very high and if the
facility grows in size, additional tanks would have to be purchased due to their
size limitations, it is essentially not optimal.
Tank Composition
The tank sizes provided by [26], did not come with any additional information other
than the material most likely being stainless steel. It was consequently assumed
that the tank from the manufacturer would be stainless steel as the main vessel, a
perlite insulation and an aluminum outer jacket, and that the L/D ratio was 3. This
composition was then varied in Section 3.3. It was found that the L/D ratio has little
to no affect on the LCoS, this is expected as the BOG loss has a minor influence
on the LCoS. This is however worth knowing, as it deems it unreasonable to order
storage tanks in specific L/D ratios, which would presumably increase the PEC.
The main difference was seen in the material selection, a carbon steel tank has
a significantly lower PEC, which reduces the LCoS by more than half. This how­
ever is based on a material factor provided in literature [43], if it is accurate and
applicable to real life is debatable. The carbon steel vessel does have a slight
increase in BOG loss, but as has been established the re­liquefaction cost is so
low that it might be worth looking into if carbon steel tanks do in fact have a lower
PEC. The downside to carbon steel tanks is that they are more prone to corrosion,
which could consequently lead to a higher maintenance cost. This was investi­
gated with a sensitivity analysis and with an annual maintenance cost of 10 % the
LCoS is 11.12 DKK/tCO2, which is still 61.4 % lower than the LCoS for stainless
steel tank. It is worth noting that pure CO2 is not corrosive, but any impurities that
are present could cause corrosion in the material.
It was assumed that the ambient temperature was 25 °C to start, and it was ex­
pected that a lower temperature would have some effect, this however was not
the case, due to the heat of vaporization of liquid CO2 being so high in compar­
ison to the heat ingress the tank experiences at any temperature. It is however

Larger Tank to Start
While the results for 120 hour storage periods have a higher LCoS than the 24 hour
storage period model due to the larger tank investment, these facilities have an
opportunity for growth. The facility could potentially deliver up to 300 tCO2/day from
the liquefaction plant to the storage tank, for the trade off to only store for 24 hours
at a time, but it would bring the LCoS down. The liquefaction cost alone would
increase by about 30 %, however the LCoS could potentially be brought down to
7.82 DKK/tCO2. This is in the case of 15 bar storage with a 120 hour storage period
going to a 24 hour storage period. This would also allow for a gradual growth,
while investing in a smaller tank to start would not. To accommodate growth when
starting out with a smaller tank, another one would have to be purchased which
increases the CAPEX and LCoS, while the OPEX and total amount of liquid CO2
stored are gradually growing to bring the LCoS down again.
Future Work
Following are some ideas that could be of interest to investigate further in any
possible future work
It would be of interest to investigate an integrated approach of the liquefaction,
intermediate storage, and transportation processes, while also considering the
revenue generation from sequestration. This would involve a detailed analysis
of the liquefaction process across various pressure levels with the possibility of
district heating integration coupled with a more dynamic assessment of boil­off
rates during tank filling, storage period, and emptying.
Examine CO2 storage at 7 bar, similar to current practices for LPG ships.
Evaluate the feasibility of high­pressure vs. low­pressure onsite storage for pipeline
transportation, considering that pipeline transport typically requires CO2 in vapor
phase and high pressures.
Further investigation into transportation methods, the transportation cost is quite
high in comparison to the liquefaction and storage cost. Can this cost be reduced
in any way? Is one method better than another?

Conclusion
A thermodynamic model of a storage tank that should store liquefied CO2 was
made. The storage pressure was varied as well as the tank design, storage pe­
riod and ambient temperature. Initially the CO2 should be delivered at storage
pressure. Alternatively, the CO2 should be delivered at 15 bar, this requires the
liquid stored at higher pressures to be throttled down and separated.
The results show that despite the liquefaction cost decreasing with an increase in
pressure, it is not significant enough to outweigh the substantial increase in the
PEC with higher operating pressures. While the boil­off rate is lower at higher
pressures, it is a minor contribution to the operational expenditure. It was quite
apparent that storage at higher pressures was not optimal in any case and was
therefore disregarded for any additional modeling and calculations.
The boil­off rate does experience some changes when the L/D ratio and ambient
temperature are varied, however they show no significant changes in the LCoS.
The same conclusion can be made regarding the change in vessel material to
carbon steel, the change in the boil­off rate is minimal, however the capital ex­
penditure decreases dramatically, or by 62.76 %, while the re­liquefaction cost
only increases by 11.05 %.
Two different storage periods were investigated, 24 hours and 120 hours. The
shorter storage period achieves the lowest overall cost of 28.79 DKK/tCO2 and the
120 hour storage period has an overall cost of 38.79 DKK/tCO2. However, an initial
investment of a larger tank gives the opportunity for growth, as was discussed in
Chapter 4. The LCoS could be brought down to 7.82 DKK/tCO2 for the trade off of
a shorter storage period. These results are for a stainless steel tank. Opting for
a smaller tank to start, to satisfy a 24 hour storage period, would only double the
LCoS at best if the liquefaction plant were to deliver double the original amount of
liquid CO2.
A sensitivity analysis was performed as well, and the model showed most sensi­
tivity to the lifetime of the storage tank as well as sensitivity to the nominal discount
rate and inflation rate. While the cost of liquefaction and maintenance had minimal
affect. When combined with the levelized cost of liquefaction and transportation,
the sensitivity to these parameters becomes almost negligible and the sensitivity
is the largest for cost of transportation and liquefaction.
The LCoS is very low in comparison to the LCoL and LCoT, with LCoT being the
highest at 400 DKK/tCO2 to 460 DKK/tCO2. The LCoS is mainly related to the capital
expenditure and the amount stored, making it most optimal to store as much as
possible per tank, as the operational expenditure is to low in comparison to have
any real affect.
