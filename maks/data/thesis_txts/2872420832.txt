Abstract
The increasing prevalence of distributed energy resources and the electrification of loads, such as
heat pumps and electric vehicles, present significant phase imbalance challenges in low-voltage
distribution grids.
This thesis utilizes high-resolution, per-phase smart meter data from 9
Danish substations over an 8-month period to analyse and profile the distinct characteristics
of voltage and current unbalance. This work is part of the 3PhaseInsight project, which aims
to optimize grid capacity through enhanced monitoring.
Using detailed three-phase Power Flow models, this study first quantifies the impact of phase
imbalance, showing that asymmetry contributes to more volatile voltage and current magni-
tudes. Additionally, the percentage increase in energy losses due to unbalance, Efi, ranges from
2.8% to 5.4% across the studied substations. Phase imbalance is then characterized using
two key metrics: the standard, symmetrical component-based Voltage Unbalance Factor (VUF)
and a novel, scalar-based Current Unbalance Ratio (CUR), which is defined in this thesis to
be robust in grids with bidirectional flow of power. A feature-based classification using charac-
teristics of Severity, Duration, Distance, and Problem Phase is then performed to identify key
operational patterns.
The results quantify the distinct nature of the two metrics: while VUF levels typically remained
low (0.2-0.4%), significantly below regulatory limits, CUR values showed significant variation
(20-60%). The classification analysis further revealed that voltage unbalance is a cumulative,
distance-dependent phenomenon, with the most severe events being transient and occurring
at the feeder ends. In contrast, current unbalance was found to be a local, behavioural phe-
nomenon, which is highlighted by the dual nature of sustained CUR events: they can signify
either a state of near-perfect balance from symmetrical loads or one of extreme unbalance from
a dominant, isolated single-phase load.
The intrinsic divergence between voltage unbalance as a structural grid issue and current un-
balance as a local, behavioural issue indicates that they are distinct phenomena. This finding
implies that distribution system operators must employ separate mitigation strategies to ef-
fectively address both types of unbalance. Ultimately, this work underscores that access to
high-resolution, per-phase data is the essential prerequisite for accurately diagnosing, profiling,
and managing phase imbalance in modern LV grids.

Introduction
The rise in electricity consumption driven by the adoption of consumer technologies such as heat
pumps and electric vehicles (EV) is expected to impact both the transmission and distribution
grids in a number of ways. The rapid adoption of distributed generation (DG) is introducing
new challenges to the low-voltage (LV) distribution grids. With the increasing complexity of
local electrical grids[1], issues such as power imbalance, voltage stability, congestion, power
quality and phase imbalance are becoming more prevalent and increasingly problematic[2]. In
order for a distribution system operator (DSO) to combat all these issues in the LV grid, data
availability is now more critical than ever.
Of the many emerging grid challenges, phase imbalance presents a particularly difficult one for
DSOs to manage. At its core, it is an unbalanced distribution of voltage or current among the
phases of a three-phase system, where a deviation in either magnitude or phase angle from a
symmetrical state contributes to the imbalance. The difficulty in managing it stems from the
fact that access to detailed, per-phase meter data is scarce[3]. This lack of data creates an
uncertain environment where most operators have little information on the operational state of
phase imbalance or whether they are complying with regulatory limits. Imbalance can also be
quite sensitive to geographical location and type of consumer profile, meaning different parts of
the grid are impacted in unique ways. This makes it a complex issue for DSOs, who are under
increasing pressure to optimize grid performance while integrating new technologies and loads.
Although effective mitigation measures exist, the lack of sufficient, high-resolution data makes
it difficult to effectively define, monitor, and manage the grid’s balance state.
Most prior studies on phase imbalance – typically framed as voltage or current unbalance –
examine its fundamental origins, grid efficiency impacts, and mitigation techniques.
Com-
mon causes include unbalanced single-phase loads and high penetration of distributed energy
resources such as photovoltaic (PV) systems, which introduce asymmetries in low-voltage dis-
tribution networks[2],[4]. The consequences of phase imbalance can be severe: Dong et al. [5]
found that these include increased active power losses and line congestion, whereas Ul-Haq et
al. [6] mention extra heating effects and system vulnerability to faults. At the component level,
these consequences can lead to the partial or complete damage of essential electrical equipment
[7]. For instance, Woolley et al. [8] highlight that three-phase motors are particularly vulnera-
ble, experiencing increased heating, noise, vibration, and a loss of efficiency under unbalanced
conditions.
The traditional mitigation strategy is phase reconfiguration, a physical intervention where util-
ity crews redistribute loads from overloaded phases to underloaded ones. More advanced meth-
ods for optimized reconfiguration can be found in academic literature such as Gandomkar in [9],
where a genetic algorithm is implemented to perform optimal phase swapping on a feeder level,
and Fang et al.[10] implementing a statistical approach due to the often data-scarce situation
in most LV networks. While other methodologies, such as per-phase reactive power control[11],
dynamic EV phase switching[12], and active power compensation using battery energy stor-
age systems[13] exist, their practical implementation hinges on a common prerequisite. The
effectiveness of all these advanced strategies is fundamentally dependent on the availability of
high-resolution, per-phase data, which remains a significant challenge in most LV distribution
grids. In response to this "data gap," some research has focused on Power Flow methods for
limited metering[3] or optimizing meter placement[14]. However, despite these efforts, phase

• Which electrical phenomena or consumption patterns are most strongly correlated with

Background
Managing a larger electrical network is a task that requires continuos monitoring and coordi-
nation. For all such power grids it is essential to have a stable voltage source and operational
elements such as transformers, lines and circuit breakers[16]. Most larger grids are based on
Alternating Current (AC) and here it is also imperative to keep the grid frequency within op-
erational limits. Beyond these fundamental parameters, a plethora of sub-parameters must be
monitored to maintain regulatory compliance and ensure safe grid operation.
Generally, the operation of a power grid is split into two domains based on the voltage level -
Distribution grids (<50 kV) and transmission grids (>50 kV). The operation of a distribution
grid is fundamentally different than that of the transmission grid. Where a transmission system
operator (TSO) might focus on system-wide issues such as power balance, frequency control,
voltage/reactive power control, inertia, and black start, a DSO has other concerns. For a DSO,
these concerns are primarily related to local voltage quality. This focus is associated with issues
such as voltage magnitude variations, harmonics, flicker, and voltage unbalance. Power losses
and line current are also important factors, as they directly affect the efficiency of distribution
grid operation. While frequency plays a role, it is typically regulated at higher voltage levels,
and maintaining it within operational limits is primarily the responsibility of the TSO.
While many operational concerns apply across the distribution level, LV grids in particular
stand out due to their unique structure and management. The scale and available resources of
DSOs can vary widely. In several European countries, both nationwide operators and numerous
smaller DSOs serving relatively few consumers coexist[17]. Historically, the flow of electricity
in these LV grids was unidirectional towards the end consumers in a simple, radial topology.
This meant that the day-to-day operation was rather steady, with the main focus on traditional
grid reinforcement.
Today, however, the LV grids are far from purely passive and unidirectional, marking a signifi-
cant departure from their historical steady-state operation. This is largely due to the introduc-
tion of consumer distributed energy resources (DERs) and more complex consumption profiles
from units like EVs and heat pumps. Simply put, more electricity is flowing both in and out on
end-user LV lines and other parts of the LV grid. Therefore, DSOs are now faced with several
new challenges that require a more active operational role. This complexity is amplified when
these new loads and generators are not distributed evenly amongst the three phases, which
directly threatens the fundamental principle of a balanced network.
2.1
A Network Built to be Balanced
Modern electric power systems are supplied by means of three-phase AC generators. In an
ideal situation, the loads in the system are balanced with identical impedances in all three
phases[18]. This particular polyphase system architecture was adopted since the three sinusoidal
signals deliver an accumulated constant flow of power and for the material efficiency, where the
amount of copper required per kW transmitted is optimized[19]. Figure 2.1 shows a simple
representation of how the three signals are distributed in a balanced three-phase power system.

However, in real-world asymmetrical power systems, this ideal condition of
perfect balance is never fully achieved.

Note the direction of rotation. Since phase B is initially defined as lagging phase A by 120° and

The phasor domain becomes especially handy when dealing with an unbalanced three-phase

Connection Configurations
Another key aspect of three-phase systems and their balance is connection types. Most common
are Delta (∆) and Wye (Y) connections. In Figure 2.3, the concept of these configurations
is illustrated. The most typical configuration found in distribution systems is the Delta-Wye
Figure 2.3: Common 3-phase connection types (Delta and Wye).
connection, where the Delta winding is on the HV-side and the Wye winding is on the LV-
side of the transformer1. Understanding this configuration is critical, as it determines how
phase imbalance propagates between the different voltage levels (e.g., from the LV to the HV-
side).
Generally, the Delta HV-side can contain the imbalance by circulating in the Delta
loop, and therefore phase imbalance originating from HV-side will have less of an impact on
the LV-side. In essence, the LV-side is shielded from phase imbalance occuring on HV-side.
However, imbalance originating from the LV-side can affect the balance in the HV-system.
This propagation occurs because the Delta-connected HV side lacks a neutral conductor and
therefore has no path for the zero-sequence currents that arise from the unbalance.
2.2
Regulation
For phase imbalance specifically, there are certain regulatory limits that network operators
have to comply with. Generally, voltage unbalance plays a bigger role in regulation around the
world; however, current unbalance is also present and is often monitored, even if not always
directly regulated with explicit limits.
2.2.1
Voltage Unbalance
When examining voltage unbalance in terms of regulation, DS/EN 50160[20] becomes ex-
tremely relevant. According to this standard, the values of the negative phase sequence com-

component for 95% of the time during each period of one week. The values should be based on
10-minute mean r.m.s. values and should apply under normal operating conditions. This can

can be stated as:

2.2.2
Current Unbalance
Some jurisdictions, such as Denmark, have regulations for current unbalance at the consumer
level. The Danish standard [21] states that a consumer’s current unbalance should not exceed
20% of their delivery current. This rule is moderated by two absolute limits: an unbalance of
up to 16 A is always permitted, while an unbalance exceeding 300 A is never allowed. For a
typical 230 V supply, the 16 A allowance effectively serves as the practical limit for installations
up to 55.2 kVA.
Notably, these Danish requirements for consumer installations focus exclusively on current un-
balance magnitude. Consumers are not held to any specific voltage unbalance limits, primarily
because VUF is more difficult for them to document.
Unlike VUF, which depends on the
grid’s short-circuit power at the point of common coupling, current unbalance is a more direct
measurement of a consumer’s own installation.
It is also important to distinguish responsibility: unlike voltage unbalance, which is a direct DSO
responsibility, current unbalance originates at the consumer level. However, the consequences
of high current unbalance, such as increased losses and stress on equipment, primarily affect the
DSO’s distribution network. Therefore, monitoring and managing sources of current unbalance
remains a critical task for ensuring efficient and stable grid operation.
2.3
Causes of Phase Imbalance
The most traditional cause of phase imbalance in LV grids is the uneven distribution of single-
phase loads.
This issue stems from historical installation practices where the allocation of
residential connections across the three-phase network was not always carefully managed. For
instance, a case study by the Danish Safety Technology Authority (SIK) highlights a common
scenario where installers preferentially connect loads to Phase A (L1) due to familiar wiring
standards, creating a structural imbalance from the outset[7].
This long-standing problem is now significantly amplified by the widespread adoption of high-
amperage single-phase loads, such as EV chargers and heat pumps. Unlike smaller appliances,
these devices introduce a substantial asymmetry into one phase. Simulation-based studies have
quantified this impact, showing that placing EV charging stations on a single phase can raise
voltage unbalance to a level 3.44 times higher than for randomly distributed charging[22]. This
issue is also compounded by predictable temporal patterns, with EV charging creating load
peaks overnight and heat pump consumption increasing during colder months.
Adding to this complexity is the rise of single-phase DG, particularly residential PV systems.
When installed unevenly, these systems inject power asymmetrically. This generation is unco-
ordinated and intermittent, following solar irradiance rather than grid needs. During periods of
high production and low local consumption, this can lead to significant reverse flow of power.
For instance, a stochastic analysis by Schwanz et al. demonstrated that high penetrations of
PV systems are likely to contribute over 1% to voltage unbalance, and in some grids, can even
cause it to exceed the 2% regulatory limit[23].
Finally, phase imbalance can originate from the physical grid infrastructure itself. A persistent,
underlying imbalance can be caused by asymmetries in the distribution lines, where the non-
symmetrical arrangement of the conductors results in slightly different phase impedances. A

more direct and severe cause is an equipment fault, such as a blown fuse on one phase, which
creates an immediate and acute asymmetry.
The combined consequences of these factors are significant, including severe voltage drops
that cause end-user equipment to malfunction and thermal stress that can damage cables and
distribution cabinets[5],[7]. Given this diverse set of causes – from load distribution to grid
hardware – understanding their collective impact on a specific network is a complex challenge
that requires a detailed network model.
2.4
Power Flow Model
The analysis of phase imbalance in this thesis is based on the results from a detailed Power Flow
model. Given time-series power data for each load and generator, the Power Flow solution yields
the complete voltage phasors for every bus and allows for the calculation of the corresponding
bus injection current phasors (both magnitude and angle).

3
Methodology
This chapter details the complete methodological framework developed for the analysis of phase
imbalance. The methodology is presented in two main parts. First, it establishes the funda-
mental metrics used to quantify both voltage and current unbalance. This includes presenting
the standard, sequence-based metric for voltage and defining the novel, magnitude-based metric
for current developed for this thesis.
Second, it outlines the "profiling" framework used for classification. This includes the process
for defining imbalance instances, the selection and definition of the 4 core analytical features
(Severity, Duration, Distance, and Problem Phase), and the heuristic strategies used to define
the categorical bins for each feature. This comprehensive methodology provides the foundation
for the results presented in Section 4.
3.1
Defining Phase Imbalance
Phase imbalance in LV distribution systems can manifest through unequal voltage or current
across the three phases. Consequently, these two primary quantities are typically used to assess
this phenomenon. This section provides the formal definitions for the specific indicators used
to quantify each type of imbalance.
3.1.1
Voltage Unbalance Factor
Voltage unbalance is often the primary concern for distribution system operators, as it di-
rectly affects power quality and the performance of end-user equipment. Voltage unbalance
can be broadly described as unequal magnitudes or phase angles among the phase voltages of
a polyphase system[6].
Several formulas exist for quantifying voltage unbalance, but the most rigorous and widely
accepted is based on symmetrical components. Here, voltage unbalance is defined as the ratio
of the negative-sequence to the positive-sequence voltage component magnitudes[24], formally
expressed as the Voltage Unbalance Factor (VUF):

magnitude and phase angle and is used throughout this work as the primary metric for voltage-

Current Unbalance Ratio
Although it may seem natural to define current unbalance using a similar approach based on
symmetrical components, doing so introduces challenges in systems where current flow direc-
tion varies dynamically. In such real-life networks, the complex interaction between loads and
distributed generation can lead to unusual angular relationships between the three-phase cur-
rents, where they deviate significantly from the ideal 120-degree separation. As demonstrated
in Appendix A, this can cause the positive-sequence component magnitude, |I1|, which forms
the denominator of the standard Current Unbalance Factor (CUF), to momentarily collapse
towards zero. The resulting division by a near-zero value creates extreme spikes, making the
CUF an unstable and misleading indicator in such scenarios. A thorough case analysis of this
phenomenon can be found in Appendix A.

Other Features
In addition to the 4 core features, several secondary features were considered during the ex-

vs. production), and a localization factor (L). This factor was defined to quantify the correla-

attributes were not part of the initial data batch provided by 3PhaseInsight and are therefore
not included in this thesis. While not part of the primary classification model, these features
provide further insights and may hold explanatory power for future analyses.

Duration Bins
Event durations are grouped into 5 qualitative categories:
Short:
A single 15-minute interval.
Medium:
30 to 60 minutes.
Long:
1 to 2 hours.
Very Long: 2 to 6 hours.
Sustained:
Longer than 6 hours.
These categories were deliberately chosen to distinguish between different types of imbalance
phenomena. The Short category is essential as it represents the highest data resolution (∆t =
15 min) and serves as the closest proxy for aligning with regulatory VUF standards (10 min.
r.m.s readings). The Medium category is designed to capture intermittent unbalances from
temporary faults or short-term load asymmetries. The Long and Very Long categories identify
imbalances that follow predictable daily cycles, such as those caused by evening load peaks
or daytime solar production.
Finally, the Sustained category isolates chronic, long-lasting
problems that may point to fundamental issues like persistent load imbalances or structural
asymmetries in the network.
Distance Bins
To enable consistent comparison across substations of varying physical sizes, the Distance from
the transformer is binned into relative segments. For each grid, a reference distance, Lmax, is
first established, defined as the Distance to the single bus located furthest from the transformer.
The bins are then defined as percentages of this specific Lmax value:
LV Trafo:
The LV transformer bus itself (L = 0).
0–25%:
Buses in the first quarter of the total feeder length.
25–50%:
Buses in the second quarter of the total feeder length.
50–75%:
Buses in the third quarter of the total feeder length.
75–100%:
Buses in the final quarter, i.e., closest to the feeder end.
This "per-substation" binning method was selected after considering other approaches. An
initial attempt using equal-frequency binning (quantiles) was dismissed because it created arbi-
trary and uneven groupings in grids where the number of buses was not divisible by the number
of bins, which skewed classification results. A "per-feeder" normalization was also evaluated,
where each bus’s distance was made relative to the end of its own specific feeder branch. While
this offers a highly localized perspective, the chosen "per-substation" method was deemed su-
perior for this analysis. By using a single Lmax value for the entire grid, the resulting bins retain
a clearer link to the absolute physical distances (in km) and provide a more stable, system-
wide view. Initial tests showed that this simpler approach did not produce drastically different
results, making it the more robust and interpretable choice. The per-feeder methodology could
be useful for analysing larger or more complex radial LV grids in future work.
Problem Phase Categories
This feature is inherently categorical and does not require numerical binning. It consists of the
three discrete values corresponding to the phase most affected: Phase A, Phase B, or Phase C.

Results
This chapter presents the key findings derived from the Power Flow simulations, with the
central goal of characterizing and contrasting voltage and current unbalance across the 9 LV
distribution grids. The analysis aims to move beyond simple metrics to build a detailed profile
of how imbalance events manifest, ultimately revealing the fundamental differences between
VUF and CUR.
To achieve this, the analysis is presented in three main parts. It begins by quantifying the
overall impact of asymmetry, comparing the results of a perfectly balanced system with those
from an unbalanced system to quantify the overall impact on grid losses and volatility. Next,
the temporal behaviour of the two primary imbalance metrics, VUF and CUR, is examined
across all 9 substations to identify baseline trends and outliers. The chapter culminates in a
detailed classification analysis, which uses descriptive statistics and heatmaps to explore the
relationships between key features – such as Severity, Duration, Distance and Problem Phase
– and expose the distinct operational signatures of voltage and current unbalance.
Dataset and Example Substation
The analysis in this thesis is based on a high-resolution time-series dataset provided by the
3PhaseInsight project. The dataset consists of per-phase smart meter measurements for voltage
magnitudes, active power (P), and reactive power (Q). For the Power Flow analysis, the P and
Q measurements serve as the primary inputs to model the corresponding asymmetrical loads
and static generators. These are then assigned to buses within the grid model, where each bus
represents a physical connection point, such as a distribution cabinet. The measured voltage
magnitudes are set aside because the simulation calculates the full complex voltage phasors
(including both magnitude and angle), which are essential for the symmetrical component
analysis used to determine the VUF. The data was recorded over a 8-month period, from
September 2023 to May 2024, with a temporal resolution of ∆t = 15 minutes.
The dataset covers 9 distinct LV distribution grids, each distinguished by a unique substation.
In fact, they are all in such a radial topology where a substation is connected to a 20/0.4 kV
transformer for each substation grid. To provide context on their physical differences, the key
topological characteristics of these substation grids are summarized in Table 4.1.

outcome of this analysis, highlighting that VUF and CUR capture distinct aspects of grid
asymmetry.

Overall, the VUF classification results demonstrate that VUF is a complex phenomenon with
characteristics that are strongly tied to the grid’s physical structure. The key findings can be
summarized as follows:
1. VUF is a cumulative, distance-dependent effect: The Severity of voltage unbalance
consistently increases at greater distances from the transformer, reflecting the physical
impact of line impedance.
2. Severe events are transient and distant: The most significant VUF events (Elevated
and Very High Severity) are typically short in Duration and tend to occur farther from the
transformer. This profile suggests they are linked to grid disturbances, like load switching,
at the most vulnerable points of the feeder.
3. A stable, low-level unbalance is common: The aggregated data reveals a common
profile of sustained, low-Severity unbalance. This likely represents a grid’s persistent,
baseline asymmetry under normal operating conditions.
Taken together, these findings suggest that managing VUF requires a dual approach: mitigating
short-lived, high-Severity events at the vulnerable ends of feeders, while also addressing the
persistent, low-level unbalance inherent in the system’s overall structure.

DISCUSSION
June 20, 2025
5
Discussion
The results presented in the previous chapter provide a detailed profiling of phase imbalance.
This section now discusses the practical implications of these findings, the limitations of this
study, and potential avenues for future work. The discussion begins with the complex relation-
ship between imbalance and grid losses.
From a practical standpoint, a primary motivation for DSOs to manage phase imbalance is its
direct impact on network efficiency, specifically through increased energy losses. To quantify
this effect, this thesis utilizes the percentage increase in energy losses due to unbalance (Efi).
The analysis confirms this impact is significant, showing that asymmetry is responsible for
an additional 2.8% to 5.4% in system losses compared to an ideal, balanced case (Table 4.2).
However, while this system-wide relationship is clear, a more detailed analysis found no direct
local correlation between unbalance indicators VUF and CUR at a specific bus and the energy
losses in its immediate surrounding lines. This suggests that losses from unbalance are a com-
plex, distributed phenomenon rather than a localized one. The composition of the calculated
losses – which includes both physical resistive losses and modelling artifacts from zero-sequence
elements (detailed in Appendix C) – may also contribute to this weak local correlation. There-
fore, while Efi is a robust indicator for the overall impact on efficiency, local VUF and CUR
are better suited as indicators for other power quality issues than losses.
Another practical application of this research is the development of a data-driven tool to guide
electricians when connecting new single-phase loads. The Problem Phase feature was designed
as a first step in this direction, offering a flexible way to identify the most consistently stressed
phase at any level of the grid. However, for practical guidance, this feature has a significant
limitation: it identifies which phase deviates most but does not specify the nature of that
deviation. For example, it does not distinguish between an over-voltage and an under-voltage,
or an over-loaded and an under-loaded phase. This means an installer would know which phase
has a problem, but not whether to add or remove load to help solve it. This ambiguity arises
from both calculation methods – the VUF-based problem phase is derived from the complex
phasor deviations (∆VA, ∆VB, ∆VC), while the CUR-based version uses deviations from an
average current scalar. One could revert to simpler, purely scalar-based methods. From a DSO
planning perspective, this would mean bypassing the unbalance metrics and using the direct
power flow voltages or currents to identify the phase with the lowest load. From an installer’s
field perspective, the equivalent and traditional way would be using a voltmeter at the point of
connection. While both approaches are straightforward, they rely on incomplete data, ignoring
the crucial phase angle information required to understand true voltage unbalance. Therefore,
a truly robust recommendation tool would need to bridge this gap, likely by combining both
simple magnitude data and the more complete, phasor-based unbalance information.
The basis for the entire phase imbalance profiling and classification analysis is the three-phase
Power Flow. As with any model, its accuracy is dependent on the input data. The raw smart
meter readings presented several challenges, and within the scope of this thesis, basic data
cleaning measures were taken. These included removing empty data chunks (such as the first
two weeks), filling NaN measurements using values from the same time on the previous day,
and correcting feeder fuse sizes. Concurrently, other teams within the 3PhaseInsight project
identified more complex issues, notably "phase twisting" (incorrect phase labels at the meter),
and developed a sophisticated "Data Preprocessor" pipeline to produce a fully corrected dataset.

Due to the project timeline, the analysis presented in this thesis was conducted using the
initially cleaned data, before the final phase-corrected dataset from the advanced preprocessor
became available. According to the developer of the Data Preprocessor, instances of phase
twisting were rare in this initial dataset; nevertheless, this limitation must be acknowledged.
It is expected that using the fully corrected data would likely result in slightly more balanced
grid profiles, but this is not anticipated to change the fundamental conclusions drawn about
the distinct behaviours of VUF and CUR.
Finally, given the large-scale ambitions of the 3PhaseInsight project, the scalability of the pre-
sented methodology is a critical consideration for its practical application. The analysis in this
thesis was performed on an initial dataset of 9 substations. However, the full scope of the
project involves a much larger dataset, with a subsequent data batch including over 830 sub-
stations. The Power Flow simulation itself, implemented using the Python-based PandaPower
library, required approximately 1.5 hours of computation time per substation for the 8-month
period on a standard workstation. While feasible for this study, extrapolating this performance
to the full dataset of over 830 substations would equate to more than 1,200 hours – or nearly 52
days – of continuous computation. This clearly demonstrates that the Power Flow calculation
presents a significant bottleneck for large-scale deployment. Therefore, while the analytical
framework is robust, applying it system-wide would necessitate significant code optimization
or the use of high-performance computing (HPC) resources.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to address the challenges of phase imbalance in modern, complex LV grids
by leveraging high-resolution, per-phase smart meter data. By establishing a comprehensive
analytical framework, this work has demonstrated that it is possible to move beyond simple,
system-wide averages and create a detailed profile of imbalance events, revealing the distinct
behaviours of voltage and current unbalance.
The analysis yielded several key findings, including a quantified impact of asymmetry: unbal-
anced systems exhibit greater volatility in voltages and current magnitudes, and the percentage
increase in energy losses due to unbalance, Efi, ranges from 2.8% to 5.4% across the studied
substations.
A central contribution of this work was establishing a clear framework for quantifying phase
imbalance. This involved applying the standard, symmetrical component-based VUF, while
also defining a novel, scalar-based CUR, which proved to be a more stable and robust indicator
for current unbalance in these networks. The application of these distinct metrics was critical,
as it revealed their inherently divergent characteristics: while VUF levels typically remained low
(0.2-0.4%), well below regulatory limits, CUR values showed significant variation (20-60%).
The subsequent classification analysis explained this divergence: voltage unbalance was shown
to be a cumulative, structural issue with severity increasing with distance from the transformer.
In contrast, current unbalance was found to be a local, behavioural phenomenon driven by
specific load activity, with no strong correlation to distance. This difference is also clear in
their behaviour over time: the most severe VUF events are typically transient, short-lived
disturbances, whereas sustained CUR events can signify two opposite extremes – either the
presence of a balanced, symmetrical load or that of a dominant, isolated single-phase load.
The fundamental conclusion of this thesis is that voltage unbalance and current unbalance are
not interchangeable proxies for one another; they are distinct phenomena with different causes
and system-wide impacts. This has significant practical implications for DSOs. Managing volt-
age unbalance requires addressing the grid’s physical structure, such as line impedances and bus
placement, while managing current unbalance necessitates a focus on specific, local customer
load behaviours. Therefore, the development of effective, data-driven mitigation strategies will
benefit greatly from the continued rollout of high-resolution, per-phase monitoring systems.
Such infrastructure can provide valuable insights to support the efficiency, stability, and relia-
bility of future distribution grids.

It is worth noting that an alternative method exists for calculating the complex currents, by
using the apparent power (Si) and voltage (Vi) at each bus via the formula Ii = (Si/Vi)∗.

(Ybus) and is generally less sensitive to potential numerical instabilities that can arise in the
individual power and voltage outputs of the simulation.
Nevertheless, defining a symmetrical component-based Current Unbalance Factor (CUF) be-
comes feasible. However, as mentioned in Section 3.1.2, the nature of current injections in mod-
ern LV grids—with the risk of phases rapidly switching between consumption and production—
causes transient spikes in the negative sequence component. This makes the CUF an unstable
and often misleading indicator of the underlying unbalance. Figure A.1 illustrates a typical
example of this volatile behaviour.
Throughout the time series, several of these spikes are found and are consistent throughout
the substations. The largest one occurred on 8 October 2023 at 12:30 and happening on
the bus identified as ’386119’. This will be the topic of the following case study, showcasing
how these large CUF spikes can occur from Power Flow results based on real-life data. These

While this analysis clearly demonstrates how the CUF becomes unstable—via a collapse of the
positive-sequence component caused by a specific alignment of phasors—the underlying root
cause of this specific phasor behaviour warrants further investigation. The issue could originate
from several sources, which were outside the scope of this work to diagnose definitively:
1. Anomalies in the input data: There could be inherent errors or rare conditions in
the raw smart meter readings that trigger this behaviour, such as the discussed "phase
twisting" that already was identified, see Section 5.
2. Numerical instability: The power flow solver itself may have numerical instabilities
when encountering certain edge cases in load or generation profiles.
3. Calculation method instability: The method used to back-calculate the complex
currents from the simulation’s voltage results (I = Y V ) could be sensitive to small errors
or assumptions regarding symmetrical impedance profile.
Determining the precise origin would require a dedicated sensitivity analysis. However, the key
conclusion remains: the symmetrical component-based CUF is an unreliable indicator for this
type of network, justifying the use of the more robust, magnitude-based CUR for the main
analysis in this thesis.

Zero-Sequence Losses
During the thesis period, a meeting with the data-providing DSO (Radius) highlighted that
zero-sequence losses are considered an important factor in low-voltage (LV) grid efficiency. In
distribution networks, zero-sequence currents and the resulting losses can contribute signifi-
cantly to overall energy inefficiency. It is therefore relevant to analyse the presence of zero-
sequence currents, I0, and their associated energy losses, E0,loss, using the available per-phase
current data.
Complex currents are required in order to quantify their zero-sequence elements, and they are
extracted from the Power Flow with the same methodology as in Appendix A. I0 is obtained
using the standard Fortescue transformation matrix from Eq. (2.3), e.g.:
I0 = 1
3 (IA + IB + IC)
(C.1)
This is applied grid-wide, such that every bus has a single I0,b phasor describing the zero-
sequence current injection. The flow of I0 through the resistance of the network conductors
results in active energy losses (E0,loss). Since losses occur in the lines, not at the buses, the nodal
zero-sequence current I0,b must be distributed to the lines connected to each bus, resulting in
I0,l (zero-sequence currents in lines). The complex currents obtained through the admittance
matrix (see Appendix A) only provide complex current injections at the buses, but not in the
lines, and thus a different approach to distribute I0 amongst line must be taken. This distribu-
tion is done proportionally, based on the total current magnitude carried by each line relative
to the total current activity at the bus. The phase current magnitudes (|Il,A|, |Il,B|, |Il,C|) are
directly provided by PandaPower, as of Table 2.1, and thus is very reliable. The distribution
process is defined in three steps.

Here, the zero-sequence losses seem almost non-existent and therefore the impact of the zero-
sequence element for grid efficiency would be very limited. This situation was found to be
consistent across all 9 substations, and therefore the analysis of zero-sequence losses were moved
out of the main thesis.
The underlying reason for these very low zero-sequence losses could stem from different sources:
1. Errors from complex current calculations: What might have caused same problems
in the CUF described in Appendix A, could be the same thing that is happening for
the zero-sequence losses. If the complex currents are unfeasible, any following sequence-
component-based analysis becomes faulty
2. Problems from zero-sequence parameters: As described in Appendix B, the input
value for the parameter r0, Ω/km is based on an approximate value from standards. This
parameter is directly proportionate with the zero-sequence losses, however it is not the
driving factor, which is |I0|2. Therefore, it is less likely that the low magnitudes directly
stem from the fixed zero-sequence resistance R0.
In summary, while the initial motivation for this analysis came from a meeting with the DSO,
where zero-sequence losses were highlighted as potentially important in LV grids, the results
presented here indicate that their impact appears minimal across all substations. However, due
to several uncertainties—such as the assumptions behind current reconstruction, estimation of
R0 values from line lengths, and the inherently small magnitudes of the calculated losses—it is
difficult to confidently assess whether the results reflect the true scale of zero-sequence energy
losses. For this reason, and to avoid over-interpreting potentially unreliable values, these losses
were ultimately excluded from the main results chapter. Still, the analysis provides value by
clearly documenting the assumptions made and suggesting that, under these conditions, zero-
sequence effects may play a limited role in the overall loss profile.
