Abstract
Recently, the communications world witnessed rapid developments with the growing
number of users and an increased demand for higher data quality at lower costs. Moreover,
with the limited bandwidth, the spectrum eﬃciency of communication systems has been
improved by adopting some advanced technologies. The next generation of telecom networks
(ﬁfth generation or 5G) is the latest step in moving forward from the cellular 3rd generation
3G services. It was standardized by the 3rd Generation Partnership Project 3GPP.
In order to meet the requirements of 5G for the International Mobile Telecommunication
(IMT-2020) networks, which have been speciﬁed by the International Telecommunication
Union (ITU), Mobile Network Operators (MNOs) need to develop and deploy new Radio
Access Technology (5G NR). In this thesis, we will present Mobile Network Planning based
on the Propagation of Radio Waves in Wireless Communications Systems. This thesis con-
sists of two-parts, theoretical and practical, in the ﬁrst part, we will propose an introduction
to Mobile Communication Systems, including clariﬁcation of the requirements of mobile sys-
tems, such as Equipment Mobile (ME), Base Station (BS), Controller Station (MSC). In
this section, we will focus on the Propagation of Radio Waves in Wireless Communications
Systems where we will explain in detail The System Budget, Antenna Gain, The Path Loss
and Millimeter-Wave Propagation Modeling for 5G Mobile Networks ......Etc.
In the practical part, we programmed MATLAB code which used propagation models
to estimate coverage and capacity of a planned network, without planner calculation. This
thesis aims to apply the MATLAB code in order to identify the best positions for base station
installation, in case new base stations are required. This methodology can save planner time
and eﬀort by creating optimum coverage while minimising loss, this is essential to keeping
up with the ever increasing data demands of today.

Chapter 1

Introduction
Over the past two decades, wireless communication has developed at an exceptionally
quick pace to have a productive and completely digital future.5G networks are the result of
this development, a summit of mobile communication technology was introduced to meet the
increasing demand for high speed, high capacity data traﬃc, which was growing signiﬁcantly,
where studies indicate the total mobile traﬃc will increase by a factor of 5 between 2020 and
2024. Global mobile data traﬃc will reach 136 exabytes per month by 2024 according to
Erickson forecasts. In order to meet the new generation (5G) requirements, mobile operators
need to develop and deploy new Radio Access Technology (5G NR), in other words, redesign
wireless communication networks.
However, designing wireless communication networks requires a detailed understanding
of Propagation of Radio Waves in Wireless Communications Systems. The two basic chal-
lenges facing the network designer are multi-path fading of the radio link and interference
from other users in the cellular reuse environment. This thesis discusses the basic concept
of the cellular system, 5G Key Technologies Requirements for Radio Access, Propagation
Mechanisms in mobile communication (Reﬂection, Diﬀraction, Scattering, Refraction) and
the eﬀect of the surrounding environment on the path loss. Before the programming can
be started, a detailed understanding of propagation models is required, in order to choose
the right propagation model considerations must be given to Functional design, Accuracy
Validity, Simplicity, Ease of use and Computational Requirements

1.1
The fundamental diﬀerences between mobile and ﬁxed radio
communications
Mobile communications have a set of requirements that distinguish them from ﬁxed
communications, these diﬀerences can be summed up as follows:
• Mobile communication systems require a portable feed source (battery).
• Mobile communication systems need antennas for both the mobile station and base
station which should be suitable for frequencies within the required range (24 - 40
GHz).
• Each user is assigned two radio channels in order to secure communication with the
Duplex system, this allows the user to hear and speak at the same time.
• Each mobile station (the mobile) has a special number in the cell’s memory, and this
number is unique to each user.
• The mobile station and the base station shall be in constant and automatic communi-
cation using special protocols.
• The mobile user can move in the area of network coverage without missing service.
1.2
Evolution of Cellular Technologies
Mobile operators and companies were encouraged to develop new technologies due to
the need for service compatible transmission systems and very high increase in telecom cus-
tomers (and by extension mobile users).
Generation refers to the change in nature of service compatible transmission technology and
new frequency bands[15]. A new mobile generation has always appeared every 10 years since
the ﬁrst generation system in 1981, which oﬀered only analog voice services where FDMA
was used as the access technique of 1G RAN.
Later in 1992, the second generation (2G) of cellular networks was introduced, provid-
ing digital voice services and Short Message Service (SMS) text messages. (GSM) was the
most common system and widely used technology which was based on Frequency Division
Multiple Access (FDMA) and Time Division Multiple Access (TDMA) schemes.
TDMA/FDMA technology in second generation requires large frequency gaps between
the channels to avoid interference, therefore third generation (3G) was introduced, promising
higher capacity and data rates. 3G systems supports voice, low speed data, and enable a
number of data services which use code division multiple access (CDMA) allowing for a wide
variety of services such as e-mail, video-streaming and Global Position System (GPS) naviga-
tion. Universal Mobile Telecommunication Systems (UMTS) were the most common systems
in 3G, which were based on Wide Code Division Multiple Access Scheme (WCDMA). Signif-
icant growth in data traﬃc led to the introduction of 4th (4G) cellular networks in 2002 such

Why New Radio Access Technology (5G NR)
Recently, the demand for digital content and services on mobile networks has continued to
rise signiﬁcantly. This demand is increasing, not only due to an increase of users, but of a

Access Technology (5G NR). According to the latest versions [4][5] from the 3GPP, there
are two diﬀerent paths towards 5G development and deployment: Stand Alone (SA), and
Non-Standalone (NSA). NSA requires deployment of a new base station (BS) or so called
gNB ”Next Generation NodeB”. Therefore, the goal of this thesis is to ﬁnd the best location

1.5.2
Cellular networks
A cellular network or mobile network is a communication network where the last link is
wireless (RAN). In order to cover a speciﬁc area, the network is distributed over land areas
called "cells", normally, three cell sites or base transceiver stations (B). Each base station
provides the cell with the network coverage which can be used for transmission of voice,
data, ....Etc. Each cell typically uses a diﬀerent set of frequencies from neighbouring cells
to avoid interference between each other. Using these principals, the coverage areas can be
divided into smaller forms and the frequencies divided into smaller geographical areas, by
doing this, it is possible to support a larger number of users and it becomes possible to use
the available frequencies in other cells as long as these cells are not near to each other. This
principle is known as "frequency reuse" as the available frequency spectrum is divided into
frequency blocks, then these blocks can be placed within cells, and each cell can use a range
of frequencies that diﬀers from the other cell.

1.5.3
Cell Types
• Macro-cell: Covers a large area where their diameter reaches 6 miles or even more
based on the technology used (2G, 3G, or 4G). Macro-cells are mainly used in an open
area (rural area) with high-power transmitters and receivers.
• Micro-cell: Covers a small area where their diameter reaches 0.5 miles and are used in
an urban area with low-powered transmitters and receivers.
• Pico cell: Typically covering a small area, used to extend coverage to indoor areas such
as in-building oﬃces, shopping malls, train stations, ....etc. where signals do not reach
well.
The advantages of small cells size are Increased user capacity, increased number of handovers
per call, and lower power consumption in mobile terminal.
1.5.4
RF Planning
RF Planning is how to assign frequencies, ﬁnd best transmitter locations, and deﬁne
the parameters of a wireless communications. RF planning is a very important approach
in cellular communication systems, and it’s required in order to provide adequate coverage
and capacity for the services. The RF plan has two objectives, coverage and capacity, which
are the backbones of mobile communication systems. Detail in network planning section
(Chapter 4).
• Coverage relates to the geographical area within the system where the service must
be available at all locations where users are.
• Capacity relates to the capability of the system to serve their users ( how many users
within one cell can use the allocated spectrum ).
Capacity and coverage are interrelated. To extend the coverage, capacity has to be decreased,
and vice versa, to increase capacity, coverage will have to be sacriﬁced.

2.1
Overview
Radio wave propagation is the way radio waves travel or propagate when they are
transmitted from one point to another (point to point or point to multi-points ) or even
into various parts of the atmosphere and aﬀected by the medium in which they travel.
Even travelling through diﬀerent volumes in the earth’s atmosphere exposes radio waves to
a myriad of eﬀects such as reﬂection, refraction, diﬀraction, absorption, polarization, and
scattering.
Not all wireless communication systems require the existence of a line of sight in order
to be linked between two or more, for example, television station receivers or radio stations
are not required to achieve mutual vision with transmitting stations, because the nature of
the waves used in such systems is characterized by their ability to spread in all directions.
In a cellular communications system, there is often no direct line of sight between the
transmitter (Bs) and receiver (ME). As a result of the nature of electromagnetic signals and
their behaviour of propagation, these signals are subjected to factors like dispersion and
reﬂection. This results in receivers picking up signals from multiple directions, a phenomena
known as multi-path propagation.

2.2
Propagation Mechanisms
In a mobile communication system, the design of propagation models allows optimiza-
tion of cell coverage area while minimizing inter-cell interference. In order to design successful
wireless communication systems (Mobile communication systems), we have to understand
and analyse the propagation mechanisms of radio waves. This section presents some basic
concepts of Propagation Mechanisms without going into details and derivations.
2.2
Propagation Mechanisms
2.2.1
Reﬂection
Reﬂection occurs when the radio wave impinges upon an obstruction that is much larger
in size compared to the wavelength of the signal where the wave cannot penetrate, thus
changing the wave’s path (for example, reﬂections from earth and buildings). The reﬂected
signals may interfere with the original signal constructively or destructively.
Figure 10: Reﬂection of radio wave oﬀsurface [4]
2.2.2
Refraction
Refraction is the change in direction of a wave passing from one medium to another.
In other words, refraction describes the behaviour of a radio wave when it travels through a
medium of diﬀerent density as the waveform is bent and so it passes through it. Refraction
is a big problem for long-distance where the bending in the waveform increases proportional
to distance travelled.

Diﬀraction
Is the bending of the wave around an object that intercepts its path, and that occurs
when the radio path between sender and receiver is obstructed by an impenetrable body,
this aﬀects signal strength or may block it from getting to the receiver. Diﬀraction explains
how the radio signals can travel and reach the receiver in the urban and rural environments

Scattering
Scattering of a wave occurs when the medium in which the signal is transmitted contains
small objects with a high-density relative to the wavelength and also when the number of
obstacles is quite large, which leads to the splitting and reﬂection of the wave in multiple
directions, this leads to the disappearance of the main wave.

2.3
The Basic Concepts of Antenna
2.3.1
Deﬁnition
Brieﬂy, Antennas convert electrical energy into electromagnetic waves (radio) in the
case of emission (transmitting antenna), and vice versa in the case of receiving (receiving
antenna). The physical nature of the antennas has a direct relationship with the frequency
and reception of the waves. In this section, we will point out some of the fundamentals
concerned with managing wireless networks without studying the installation of antennas in
detail.
2.3.2
Antenna Gain
Antenna gain is deﬁned as ”the ratio of the power required at the input of a loss-free
reference antenna to the power supplied to the input of the given antenna to produce the
same ﬁeld strength or the same power ﬂux density at the same distance and in the desired
direction”.
Usually, antenna gain is expressed in decibels and refers to the direction of
maximum radiation [16].
In other words, it is a measure of the antenna’s potential to concentrate or receive
electromagnetic energy in/from a speciﬁc direction. Antenna gain mostly depends on the
shape of the antenna that controls the beam width, which is measured in degrees horizontally
and vertically.
For example, for an antenna that broadcasts in the range of 30 degrees, the emission
reaches a distance higher than an antenna that broadcasts in the range of 360 degrees (
Omnidirectional- Antenna) because the concentration of the emissions in the ﬁrst antenna
is higher than that of the second for the same power.

Line of Sight LOS
The line of sight is a straight imaginary line that provides a clear view between the
transmitter and the receiver or any two points, and it is one of the most important points
that must be taken into account in wireless networks. For clariﬁcation, your line of sight is
an imaginary line that stretches between your eye and the object that you are looking at.

2.3.5
Fresnel Zone
Fresnel is the area around the line-of-sight in which radio waves propagate after leaving
the antenna.
Radio waves do not actually travel in straight lines, instead the radio-frequency power
propagates in an area (ellipsoid) where he LOS is the axis, forming a wave ﬁeld called the
Fresnel Zone (ﬁgure (16)). The obstructions that are located in the middle of the transmitter
and the receiver cut oﬀthe Fresnel region and cause the waves to be reﬂected or refracted
away from the receiver or are absorbed by these objects, this may lead to the wave weakening
or disappearing according to the percentage of intersection of these objects with the Fresnel
region. So,the design of the link shall be such that the radius of the Fresnel zone remains
approximately free of obstructions within Natural conditions.

Millimetere-Wave Propagation
Millimetere-wave (mmWave) communication is one of the most important key tech-

rates, latency, coverage, capacity, and reliability, using a large number of available spectrum
resources.
Millimetere wave historically, was used only by selected authorities such as governmental
bodies. Nowadays, in order to meet the increasing need for data traﬃc and high speed which
is growing signiﬁcantly, the wireless industry / Mobile Network Operators (MNOs) is moving
to its ﬁfth generation (5G) of cellular technology that will use millimetere wave (mmWave)
frequencies. This is partially due to semiconductor technology advances and decreasing cost.
However, 5G millimeter wave usage by mobile network operators hosts a number of
challenges.
According to [18] [19] Millimeter wave path loss and shadowing are greatly
impeded due to the reﬂection of buildings as well as absorption and scattering caused by
rain, haze and fog besides that, implementation of millimeter wave is not easy.
2.5.1
Technical Challenges in Implementation of 5G Millimeter Wave
According to [9] [18] [19] technical challenges facing the implementation of 5G millimeter

1. Channel Propagation
mmWave technology requires new channel models to facilitate real system design in 5G.
According to [9] most channel modelling methods can be classiﬁed into two main cat-
egories, analytical modelling and ray-tracing-based modelling. Channel Propagation
will be seen in detail later in the 3rd chapter (propagation models).

The antenna is one of the main components in cellular communication systems. Al-
though, there are many type of advanced antennas that had been proposed and used
in previous generations none of them are designed speciﬁcally for millimetere wave,
which is was characterized by low cost, reduced size, light weight and high gain. So, a
new antenna needed to be analysed and developed for millimetere wave systems.
According to[19] it was necessary to operate 60 GHz antennas with constant gain and
good eﬃciency for frequency range 55-67 GHz. There was another solution proposed
which was beam-forming of 60 GHz using switched beam array or phase array. Re-
searchers proved that beam-forming antenna could provide result around 14 dBi with
20 degree half power beam width (HPBW), at the same time MIMO antenna (4x4)
have an average conversion loss up to 10.6 dB for all 4 channels.
MIMO with beamforming will be seen in detail in the practical chapter (Network

3.1
Overview
Designing a mobile communication network (wireless communication networks) requires
a detailed understanding of radio propagation models over a particular terrain.
In general, they aim to provide an accurate estimate of the mean received power and a
calculation of the path loss for a speciﬁed frequency band based on some detailed geographical
information about the environmental eﬀects.
There are a lot of models to estimate the path loss between the transmitter and the
receiver in mobile radio systems. Broadly speaking, these models can be divided into the
following three main categories[16].
• The empirical models: Models based on experimental measurements and results,
like the Bullington model, Hata model, and Lee model.
• The deterministic models: which are based on the geometrical structure of building
blocks, streets, like the ﬁrst simple basic model.
• The physical-statistical models: which utilise a combination of deterministic and
statistical models of diﬀerent parameters like building height and road width.
In this chapter, we try to deﬁne and analyse some of the most important propagation
models, and how they compare to the 5G Propagation Model Requirements.

3.5
5G Propagation Model requirements
MmWave technology is a completely new frontier for cellular communication / mobile
communication systems, due to the large bandwidths at these high frequencies which provide
extremely high data rates and signiﬁcant increases in capacity. On the other hand, using
these high bands (mmWave) lead to increased propagation loss and susceptibility to blockage,
further discussed in the sections (1.4 and 2.5.1).
Several various requirements are foreseen for 5G mmWave which lead to novel propa-
gation scenarios that must be modelled, in addition to what was previously mentioned in
section (1.4) the requirement of 5G for the IMT-2020.
In order to design a realistic 5G system with mmWave, the designer must consider prop-
agation scenarios that include environments like big cities with high population densities,
which contain shopping malls, huge companies with a lot of indoor oﬃces, stadiums, Hospi-
tals,.....etc. Due to the variety of eﬀects these structure types impose, models would need to
increase the granularity of urban area types to include:
• outdoor-to-outdoor (O2O): in rural areas and highways.
• outdoor-to-indoor (O2I): indoor coverage requirements for urban and dense urban ar-
eas.
• indoor-to-indoor (I2I): micro or pico cell could be used for shopping malls, indoor
oﬃces, and stadiums,.......etc
These new propagation scenario challenges, in addition to implementation challenges (sec-
tion 2.5.1), set the requirements for the 5G propagation models.
According to [20], 5G
propagation models requirements can be described as the following:
1. Applicability to Dual-Mobility Channels
Ensures that the channels evolve smoothly without discontinuities when devices move
or turn around.
2. Applicability to Frequency -Agile Channels
In 5G cellular access links, the propagation model should be able to cover a wide
frequency range from 0.5 to 100 GHz, (whilst also taking into consideration the fact
that the attenuation is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent at 100 GHz compared to attenuation at
1 GHz).
3. Applicability to Massive-Antenna Channels
As Massive MIMO is one of the key requirements of 5G for the IMT-2020, 5G tech-
nologies aggressively exploit multiple antenna transmission techniques like spatial mul-
tiplexing and spatial division multiple access. Most of these techniques, like massive
MIMO with beam-forming will use high accuracy spatial properties of the radio chan-
nel, especially for high carrier frequencies in the mmWave range. Narrow beams will
be required to compensate for various losses resulting from propagation characteristics
in millimeter-wave range.

3.6
Millimeter-wave Propagation Models
As discussed in the previous section, new channel models are urgently required in or-
der to facilitate realistic system design in 5G. This section reviews the basic concepts of
the mmWave channel propagation characteristics for developing and deploying 5G wireless
communication systems.
According to [21], mmWave channel modelling methods could be classiﬁed into two
categories:
• Analytical modeling:
This method is based on measured information or statistical characteristics of the sce-
narios.
The statistical parameters would factor in a number of parameters, which
include the number of paths, root means square (RMS), total path loss, and shadow-
ing, therefore, this method is called the analytical modelling method.
This method is expressed by a ﬁxed collection of parameters without considering en-
vironment characteristics ( environment eﬀects).Hence, neglecting environment eﬀects
could cause error in the analytical results.
• Ray-tracing-based modelling:
Depend on calculating of signal paths in a speciﬁc environment. As discussed in section
(3.5) the directional antenna and beam-forming antenna are used to ﬁnd the best path
with a minimum loss. Ray tracing is one promising solution that can rapidly adapt to
catch the main paths in changing environments. This provides reliable characterization
for multi-path radio channels near buildings.
The ray tracing-based models could also be used in massive MIMO in order to get the
channel characteristics which could be more complex with a larger number of array
elements for antennas.
High frequencies (mmWave ) cause more reﬂection and less diﬀraction, and environmental
eﬀects are neglected in analytical modelling, therefore, ray-tracing-based modelling is more
suitable for mmWave Propagation.

Discussion
In large-scale models the two free-space reference distance path loss models (CI and

used which corresponds to the data.
The CI and CIF models depend on fundamental principles of wave propagation, dating
back to Friis and Bullington, where the PLE parameters provides an accurate calculation
for path loss according to the environment, equation (27).

3.8
Summary
Much research has provided a comparison of large-scale propagation path loss models
with existing models [10] [22] [23] [24]. All of this research is in agreement, that the CI
and CIF models oﬀer computational simplicity, have very similar ﬁtnesses, exhibit more
stable model parameter behaviour across frequencies and distances, yield smaller prediction
error in sensitivity tests across distances and frequencies, and provide superior sensitivity
performance. The CIF model has been proved to be appropriate for indoor modelling, while
the CI model is more suitable for outdoor environments (both for urban macro-cell (UMa
)and urban micro-cell (UMi)), furthermore it is applicable to both single and multi-frequency
cases. Free space path loss in the ﬁrst meter of propagation ranges between 32 and 72 dB
from 1 to 100 GHz. As a result, a close-in (CI) free-space reference distance path loss model
is the best solution for designing a 5G wireless network in terms of ease of implementation,
accuracy in performance, applicability in covering wide frequency ranges (1 to 100 GHz),
applicability in dual-mobility channels, applicability to massive-antenna channels, and could
be implemented for outdoor-to-outdoor and outdoor-to-indoor scenarios. In short, the CI
model is the most suitable model for meeting 5G propagation model requirements.
3.8
Summary
In this chapter, we have provided an overview of basic concepts of propagation models
i.e theoretical models (free space model and plane earth loss model). We examined and
compared propagation models , where we started with some important applied models which
are used for coverage estimation and calculation in the mobile radio networks, such as Hata
model, COST 231-Hata model, and Ibrahim and Parsons Model. We found that although
the Hata model is suitable for mobile radio stations and widely used, it is not applicable
to mmWvave propagation due to the limitation in frequency range (150 to 1,500 )MHz.
We also found that other applied models and even the state-of-the-art propagation models
such as WINNER/IMT Advanced,COST 2100, and IEEE 802.11 could not fully satisfy 5G
requirements. Therefore, new propagation models are required in order to design a realistic
5G system with mmWave technology, in this ﬁeld, an overview of mmWave requirements
and implementation challenges have been provided. This overview informs how to choose
the right model framework which can fulﬁl the requirements with minimal complication.
Finally, we provided a discussion about mWave channel modelling methods, i.e. ana-
lytical modelling, ray-tracing-based modelling and large-scale path loss models. Where we
ﬁgured out that Ray-tracing-based modelling is more suitable for mmWave propagation than
analytical modelling due to the fact that mmWave frequencies result in more reﬂection and
less diﬀraction.
As for large-scale path loss, we have provided a detailed study and comparison of three
large-scale propagation path loss models, i.e., the ABG, CI , and CIF models, over the
microwave and mmWave frequency bands. Here we concluded that the CI and CIF models,
are superior to the ABG model in both performance and accuracy over wide frequency range
and that CI is more suitable for outdoor environments for both urban micro-cell (UMi )and
urban macro-cell (UMa). As a result of measurements and discussions, the CI model is the
most suitable model which could fulﬁl the 5G propagation models requirements.

The 5G spectrum is a range of radio frequencies in the sub-6 GHz range and the

Ac-

4.2
Coverage Planning
Coverage relates to the geographical area within the system where the service must be
available at all locations where are. The existing network topology typically LTE, aims to

NR with mmWave propagation, providing max coverage will not be possible anymore, due
to the fact that higher frequencies are also associated with higher radio channel attenuation,
limiting the network coverage. Instead to achieve maximal coverage , several alternatives
will emerge in order to compensate for the coverage limitation, such as beam-forming and
advanced multi-antenna transmission/ reception.

4.4.1
Omnidirectional antenna
An omnidirectional antenna is an antenna without directional pattern, which radiates its
power out in all directions in a plane (circular pattern) or in a given plane with a directional
pattern in any orthogonal plane such as dipoles and collinear antennas.
Omnidirectional antennas are rarely used in a mobile network, with exceptions in special
cases such as indoor coverage.

4.4.2
Sectorized antenna
A sector antenna or sector panel is a specialized antenna usually used for outdoor
coverage in mobile network systems where wide coverage areas are desired.
Typically, the mobile network is distributed over land areas called "cells", normally, each
three cell sites are covered by one base stations. Where these base station are equipped with
3 directional antennas (typically radiating in a 140 degree angle).
As a base station (3 directional antennas) provides coverage and capacity, a mobile net-
work designer must consider factor in many variables to properly implement these directional
antenna ( how they are built, conﬁgured, categorized, and their performance).
• Directional antennas usually are built from an array of dipoles placed in front of a
shaped reﬂector.
• The antennas performance is determined by shape and size of the reﬂector.
• A directional antenna can be categorized by its azimuth plane 3-dB beamwidth, direc-
tional angle at 60-, 90-, and 120 degree sectors (which are the most generally available).
• Sectors are usually deployed higher up on the base station and may have side lobe and
front-to-back ratio requirements associated with them.

5.3
Energy Optimization
The rapid growth and demand for information and communication technology (ICT)
systems have led to a signiﬁcant increase in power consumption and CO2 emissions. This,in
turn led to an increased interest in energy eﬃciency (EE) and how mobile networks can
consider this with respect to future mobile communication systems.
Typically, the main power consumption for mobile networks occur in the RAN part
(Fronthaul), where the base stations (Antennas) consume a lot of power to radiate the
signal.
Studies show that the RAN consumes 70 to 80 percent[8] of the energy within a mobile
network, therefore RAN is a good starting point to investigate energy optimization in mobile
networks.
5.3.1
Energy Consumption in Base Stations
Nowadays most mobile networks are deploying a so-called hierarchical cell structure
(HCS). The HCS uses a Macro-, Micro-, and Picocell style structure in a similar vein to 5G
technologies. While the HCS is a great solution for increasing coverage and capacity of the
network, a side eﬀect of this implementation may be that, the energy consumption might
increase as well [12].
Usually, base stations (for all Generations) are composed of the same basic elements. With-
out going into details, macro BS’s power ampliﬁers are the components with the highest
energy consumption. This is due to BS’s operating principles, where they are deployed for
peak traﬃc volume and therefore are always on. Figure (46) shows the power consumption
of the main components of typical Macro and Micro base stations at peak load.
Figure 57: BS power consumption at peak load [12]
According to [13], in wireless access networks (RAN) the traﬃc proﬁle can be modeled
as a periodic sinusoidal proﬁle, where the traﬃc proﬁle during the day time period has a
higher value, and the traﬃc proﬁle during the night time period has a lower value.
As
for weeks vs weekends, ﬁgure(47) clearly shows that weekday traﬃc proﬁles are high whilst
weekend traﬃc proﬁles are low.

To summarize, the infrastructures of access networks are underutilized during the night
time and the weekend period. This is a good starting point in order to optimize energy
consumption in mobile networks ( infrastructures of access networks).
5.3.2
Energy Savings through Dynamic BS’s Switching
Energy Optimization technologies in Mobile Networks is a rather large topic in itself.
This section presents a brief overview of Energy Savings through Dynamic BS’s Switching
Technologies, where a speciﬁc idea will be discussed based on what was concluded in previous
bodies of work exploring Energy Optimization in Mobile Network [12].In general saving
techniques using Dynamic BS’s Switching can be classiﬁed in three main algorithms ( Traﬃc
adaptation , Coverage and power adjustments, and Cell coordination).
5.3.3
Traﬃc Adaptation
The idea here is to switch base stations oﬀ(could be one sector only) if they are not nec-
essary at speciﬁc times based on a given traﬃc proﬁle, where the cell capacity is underutilized
(such as the night time period and weekend period).
5.3.4
Coverage and Power Adaptation
Usually, adjusting the transmission power of the BSs is common way for coverage opti-
mization, this method can be used as an energy consumption optimization strategy.
The idea is to reduce transmission power (decrease the cell edge ) where the cell capacity is
under-utilized based on a given traﬃc proﬁle. This requires a cooperation of neighbouring
BS’s, where they can adjust their coverage in areas where the base stations are turned oﬀin
order to compensate for the missing coverage.

Cell coordination
Similar to the previous algorithm the idea is to turn oﬀspeciﬁc base stations and the
missing coverage will be compensated by neighbouring BS’s. The diﬀerence here is, in this
method the coordination occur between two or more neighbouring BS’s which may already
cover the same area ( in a HetNet style topology), while in the previous method, the coor-
dination occurred between neighbouring basic base stations.
summary: Based on previous discussions we conclude that, the power consumption
in infrastructures of access networks can be optimised through turning oﬀsome BSs in a
speciﬁc area at a speciﬁc time based on individual traﬃc proﬁles.

Conclusion
The 5th generation of cellular networks is a signiﬁcant and ambitious project that aims
to meet the expectations of customers of the future. Where users are expecting high-speed
(up to 1 Gbps) internet, even while traveling at high speed (500 km/h). However, 5G comes
with some lofty goals, promising a 100 Mbps capacity available everywhere, including rural
areas and open areas.
This thesis has presented a comprehensive study and analysis of the propagation models,
basic parameters of antennas together with propagation mechanisms, fundamental concepts
of network planning (coverage/capacity planning) as a theoretical study. In the practical
part, a new design of mobile antennas ( Phased Array 8 by 8 Phased Array) has been
presented along with a MATLAB program used in conjunction with a simulation suite to
model and optimize network design for 5G requirements.
Through study, analysis, and comparison we conclude that 5G generation with new radio
waves (millimeter waves) requires new propagation models, so called mmWave propagation
models, research shows that close-in (CI) is more suitable for outdoor environments for
both urban micro-cell (UMi )and urban macro-cell (UMa). Simulations back up this claim,
showing that the CI model is the most suitable model which could fulﬁll the 5G propagation
model requirements, and the desired results as we concluded in the theoretical study are
correct and congruent.
The prediction of the coverage and capacity proved that the created programs as a simulation
tool are intelligent in terms of saving time and eﬀort for the network designer. As for network
planning, the results indicate that the new frequency bands ( 3.5 and 3.7 GHz) can achieve
5G goals in terms of increased data rates, and improved coverage.
On the other hand,
providing 100 Mbps capacity everywhere requires a huge number of BS’s due to the short
inter-site distance (ISD).
Finally, a new idea has been proposed in terms of energy savings through dynamic BS’s
switching, where simulation proved that 10 percent of total consumed power can be saved
during the night time period by turning oﬀsix out of the nineteen BS’s.
