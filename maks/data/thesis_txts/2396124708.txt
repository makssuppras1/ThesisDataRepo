Chapter 1
Introduction
After the invention of blue LED, a new generation of white light source became
available. Nowadays the white LED has already been successfully commercialized
and mass produced. However, there are drawbacks of these white LEDs. Mainly
two methods to generate white light, one is mixed-color white light, the other
is phosphor-converted white light. For mixed-color white light, the drawback
is that the behaviour of generated light for illumination is poor and the color
rendering index (CRI) is low as it covers only limited range of spectrum. For
phosphor-converted white light, which is a layer of phosphor that can be excited
by blue light and emit yellow light with a blue LED chip, it has a higher CRI but
due to the lifetime of the phosphor layer, the color of the light will become blueish.
Also since the phosphor layer consists rare-earth element, mass production can
be a bottleneck in the future. Thus an alternative needs to be proposed. It is
found that 6H silicon carbide heavily doped with boron and nitrogen can emit
yellow light that covers a wide range of spectrum when excited by a blue light.
Thus the investigation of increasing light extraction eﬃciency (LEE) of silicon
carbide is important. The aim of this master project is then to increase the LEE
by applying photonic crystal grating on silicon carbide.
1.1
SiC Overview
Silicon carbide (SiC) is a semiconductor material with wide indirect bandgap, and
is the only stable IV-IV compound [1, 2]. With its unique material properties, SiC
can be used for manufacturing devices for working under harsh environment, such
as high-temperature, high-power or high-frequency, etc. Although this summary
of material properties of SiC can be found in many papers, one shall not omit it
as this gives prominence to the potential of this material and why SiC has been
interested and deeply studied for decades. Compare to Si, SiC has high thermal
conductivity, high electric ﬁeld breakdown strength and high saturated electron
drift velocity. Compare to other compound semiconductors, the very advantage

of SiC is that, similar to Si, it has a think layer SiO2 with thickness of about
1nm as native oxide[3]. This property of SiC is important as it gives possibility
to apply SiC to MOS-based device. However, all these properties that make SiC
unique and promising, make it full of challenge as well from manufacturing and
processing point of view.
In power electronics application, although Si is still a dominant material, SiC and
other wide band gap semiconductors are considered as new generation material
due to the need for improving energy eﬃciency, i.e, reduction of energy loss, as
well as the capability of working at high temperature and frequency. In recent
decades, with development of crystal growth technique of SiC such as epitaxial
growth, the product quality has been signiﬁcantly improved, which gives better
performance of devices based on SiC.
At present, SiC based devices are applied in various ﬁelds, for instance, general
lighting (LEDs), astronomy (SiC optics [4]), nuclear technique (diﬀusion barrier
[5]), energies (solar cell) and so on. Summary of advantages of SiC based devices
compared to nowadays Si based devices is shown below:
• SiC can sustain much higher voltages and carry much higher currents,
which results to the shrink of devices size and reduction of the resistive
losses.
• SiC has higher thermal conductivity (4.9W · m−1/K for 6H SiC [2]), which
results to a lower heating rate and a reduction of cost and complexity for
cooling.
• SiC can operate at high temperature up to 400 ◦C (150 ◦C for Si).
• SiC can achieve fast switching due to its low switching losses, therefore
systems based on SiC devices can be cheaper and more eﬃcient.
• SiC has a higher radiation hardness compare to Si [6], it can be used as a
shield for nuclear related application.
1.2
History
It has been more than one century that SiC was synthesized, as well as natural
SiC crystal was found. It is believed that J¨ons Jacob Berzelius from Sweden
was the ﬁrst one who successfully synthesized SiC. Berzelius was born in the
parish of V¨aversunda in ¨Osterg¨otland in Sweden, not far away from Link¨oping,
at where nowadays the centre of SiC research in Sweden, University of Link¨oping,
is located. Although he is more well-known by his contribution of discovering
Cerium (Ce), Selenium (Se), Thorium (Th) and, the very important one, Silicon
(Si) [7]. In 1824 he published one paper that assumed the existence of chemical

that is believed to be the ﬁrst synthesized SiC sample.
In 1890, when Edward Goodrich Acheson attempted to synthesize artiﬁcial dia-
mond, he ended up synthesis of SiC, which he called as carborundum [9]. Acheson
then patented this process in 1896 [10] and made SiC for abrasive application.
In Acheson process, four chemical reactions occur:
C + SiO2 −−→SiO + CO
SiO2 + CO −−→SiO + CO2

Years later, in 1905, French chemist Henri Moissan, who is known for his work of
isolation of ﬂuorine, ﬁrst found natural SiC crystal in a meteorite [8], therefore,
natural SiC was named after him as moissanite.
Figure 1.1 is the photo of
moissanite under 14x magniﬁcation.
Not long after the discovery of moissanite, in 1907, Henry Joseph Round observed
yellow, green and amber light emitted from the cathode after applying voltages to
SiC crystal, and this is the ﬁrst light emitting diode invented in the world [12].
This experiments was then repeated successfully by O.V. Lossev from former
Soviet Union later in 1923 [13].
The interest of SiC started to grow in 1950s, when Jan Anthony Lely of Philips
Electronics presented a new method of growing high quality SiC crystal. How-
ever, this interest soon dropped due to the problem of the purity of SiC, and the
improvement and success in Si technique [14]. High-purity SiC growth technique,
such as epitaxial SiC growth on Si substrate was achieved in 1970s to 1980s, but
still, SiC as a semiconductor material was not as popular as Si. Until 1989, with
a breakthrough of SiC growth technique, Cree Inc. was founded and produced
the ﬁrst SiC based blue LED [8]. This sparked the evolution of SiC, and it is still
under way until today. Moreover, in August 2010, Cree successfully produced
6” 4H-SiC wafers. With improvement of semiconductor producing technique,
and development of devices, the interest of SiC as semiconductor material will
remain.

1.3.1
Basic building blocks
In SiC crystalline form, a silicon atom has four covalent bonds which bonded to
four neighboring carbon atoms (or vice versa). The formed structure is tetrahe-
dron (Figure 1.2), and is the basic building block for all SiC crystals.
1.3.2

In material science, the phenomenon that a solid material crystallizing in dif-
ferent forms is called polymorphism. Until 2006, more than 250 polymorphs of
silicon carbide was identiﬁed [15]. However, due to the unique property of SiC
crystal structure, the term polytypes was deﬁned later, where the close-packed
layers are identical, but diﬀer in stacking orders.

By the law of closed spherical packing, all the SiC polytypes crystallize in a way
that identical layers stack in diﬀerent order with respect to the shift of relative
positions of each layer. All these bilayers of SiC exhibit same hexagonal frame,
seen as spheres with identical diameter form a layer. The shape of the frame
and relative position of each layer can be seen in Figure 1.3. The shift of relative
position of each layer is to ﬁll the ‘void’ in the neighboring layer as a result of
closed spherical packing. In total there exist three inequivalent relative positions,
respectively referred as A, B and C. By stacking these three layers repetitively,
diﬀerent polytypes can be formed. Moreover, no neighboring layers with the
same relative position are allowed, i.e, if the relative position of the ﬁrst layer
sits at A, then the next layer can only sit at B or C. Hence no stacking order such
as AAB, ACC or ABCC is allowed. The stacking orders of three main polytypes,
3C, 4H and 6H are ABC, ABCB, ABCACB, respectively. The number 3, 4, 6
give the information of the number of layers that repeated as a ‘unit layer’ [16].

2.1
General semiconductor theory
2.1.1
Classiﬁcation of materials
In general, materials are classiﬁed as insulators or dielectrics (e.g. quartz or SiO2,
ceramics), conductors (metals) and semiconductors depending on their electri-
cal conductivity value. Moreover, semiconductors can be classiﬁed as elemental
semiconductors (Si, Ge), compound semiconductors (SiC, GaN, ZnO) and semi-
conductor alloys. To characterize the electrical property of the material, the ﬁrst
macroscopic parameter is the conductivity

2.1.2
Energy band
Shown in Figure 2.1, in quantum mechanics, for an isolated atom, the allowed
energy levels for the electrons are discrete and can be determined by solving the
Schr¨odinger equation. If a molecule with multiple atoms is considered, by Pauli
exclusion principle, two levels cannot have the same energy, single-atom levels
split to satisfy the principle. Moreover, if a 3D crystal is considered, one has

a continuous band of allowed energy deﬁned over the whole crystal structure

The highest energy band is called the conduction band and is limited by the
vacuum level. The band below the conduction band is called the valance band.
The separation between conduction band and valance band, Eg, is called the
band gap. The conduction band is considered almost empty while the valance
band is almost ﬁlled with electrons. Transition of electrons from the valance
band to the conduction band can be achieved when being excited as a result of
interaction with other charge carriers, photons or phonons, etc. The transition
process will generate an electron-hole pair, and when they recombine, the energy
is released.
2.1.3
Direct and indirect band gaps
Semiconductor material are always classiﬁed to two diﬀerent types, the direct
band gap material and the indirect ones. For the direct band gap semiconduc-
tors, as the name states, the maximal-energy state of the valence band and the
minimal-energy state of the conduction band are situated in same direction of
ﬁrst Brillouin zone (same crystal momentum) while for indirect band gap semi-
conductors, the crystal momentum (k-vector) of the valence band maximum and
==PAGE:19==

11
the conduction band minimum are diﬀerent. Thus the generation and recom-
bination process of electron-hole pairs in direct band gap material and indirect
band gap material are diﬀerent.
2.2
Photoluminescence
2.2.1
Overview
The deﬁnition of luminescence can be concluded as light emission from objects
not resulting from heat (If the light emission process is involved with heating
then it is called incandescence). Photoluminescence, is then one of many forms
of luminescence. Depending on how it is caused, luminescence can be classiﬁed
as (included but not limited to):
• Photoluminescence
• Electroluminescence
• Chemiluminescence
• Mechanoluminescence
• Crystalloluminescence
Photoluminescence, as it has a preﬁx photo-, is caused by photoexcitation, i.e,
absorption of photons/electromagnetic waves. Key parameters to classify pho-
toluminescence phenomenon are for instance, band structure of the crytalline,
energy of exciting photons, etc. Two subclasses of photoluminescence are ﬂuores-
cence and phosphorescence. The diﬀerence between these two forms of photolu-
minescence is the “waiting time”, which is the time interval between absorption
of photons and light emission, as a result of relaxation process. Depending on the
property of the material, the delay of light emission may vary signiﬁcantly, from
femtoseconds to milliseconds, and in some cases can be minutes to even hours
[23]. Figure 2.2 illustrates the ﬂuorescence and phosphorescence process, the
diﬀerence between these two forms of photoluminescence can be clearly viewed.
Moreover, the impurity in the material also eﬀects the photoluminescence pro-
cess.
Figure 2.3 shows the photoluminescence phenomenon of diamond with
diﬀerent impurities.

Chapter 3
Simulation
This chapter covers the simulation model and methodology for calculating the
eﬀect of photonic crystal structure on the surface of SiC sample to the far-ﬁeld
radiation pattern and light extraction eﬃciency enhancement.
The software
used for simulating was Lumerical FDTD Solutions which is based on the ﬁnite-
diﬀerence time-domain (FDTD) method. Due to the large amount of simulations
needed to be run, HPC cluster of DTU Computing Center was used to reduce
the time needed for each simulation.
3.1
Overview
3.1.1
Simulation construction
Figure 3.1 shows the overview of simulated structure and the objective tree.
The red slab is SiC material with a span of 20 µm × 20 µm and thickness of 1 µm
while the small light blue cylinders with a hexagonal period inside the red slab are
the photonic crystal structure, i.e, holes by removing the bulk material. In this
simulation, the material is deﬁned by the refractive index [31], thus the light blue
cylinders have a refractive index of 1 as air. As for the refractive index of SiC,
it is more complex and will be discussed later in this chapter. The source used
for simulating is an electric dipole source which can be treated as an abstracted
point light source, presented as the blue arrow. The orange hollow box is the
deﬁned simulation area with dimension of 8 µm × 8 µm × 1.5 µm. There are two
analysis groups called far ﬁeld and dipole power in the objective tree, which
are groups of monitors that record the calculated electromagnetic ﬁeld data for
further calculation of far-ﬁeld radiation pattern and light extraction eﬃciency
enhancement. The parameters of the constructed structure, such as period of
photonic crystal structure, diameter and height of the cylinders, position of the
source and monitors, dimension of simulating area and so on, can be set through
the objective tree.

3.1.2
FDTD
Finite-diﬀerence time-domain (FDTD) is a numerical analysis method for mod-
elling electrodynamics and it is a subclass of grid-based diﬀerential numerical
modeling methods (Finite diﬀerence method) [32]. The method is to consider a
space as a ﬁnite set of voxels. For an electromagnetic wave in the space, each
voxel has a dielectric constant depending on its material property, as well as the
value of E(⃗r) and H(⃗r) ﬁelds components. Thus the propagation of an electro-
magnetic wave in the space can be simulated by updating the value of E(⃗r) and
H(⃗r) ﬁelds components of each voxel in small time intervals. By increasing the
==PAGE:25==
Contents
17
number of voxels, i.e, the resolution, the simulated result is expected to converge
and match the result in reality. Moreover, since FDTD is a time-domain method,
the result from one single simulation can cover a wide frequency range.
3.2
Simulation parameters
3.2.1
Refractive index
In Lumerical FDTD Solutions material database, there is no pre-deﬁned SiC
model. To deﬁne the SiC material, one can import a text ﬁle with information
of material refractive index. The ﬁle has a format of three columns which are
wavelength, real part of refractive index and imaginary part of refractive index,
respectively. First the refractive index data was found in [33]. However, since it
has been twenty years after the book published, there is improvement in fabricat-
ing and measuring method and the doping concentration of the sample described
in the book is much diﬀerent from the sample used in this project. Thus a mea-
surement of refractive index was proposed and done by using ellipsometer in the
cleanroom at DTU Danchip.
Figure 3.2 illustrate the plot of refractive index as a function of wavelength for
both data from the literature and data from the measurement. It can be seen
that there is an obvious diﬀerence between two sets of data which may lead to a
very diﬀerent simulation result. Although the newly measured refractive index
data can be inaccurate, still it is better to choose this one to deﬁne the material
for simulation instead of data from literature.
3.2.2
Boundary condition
Since the simulation area is ﬁnite, it implies that a boundary condition setting on
the edge of simulation area is necessary. In Lumerical FDTD Solutions, diﬀerent
types of boundary condition are:
• PML: Perfectly matched layer [34] boundary. Fully absorbing boundary.
• Metal:
Metal boundary, also called Perfect Electric Conductor (PEC)
boundary. Fully reﬂecting boundary.
• PMC: Perfect Magnetic Conductor boundary. Similar to Metal boundary,
fully reﬂecting.
• Periodic: Periodic boundary. Used for periodic ﬁeld and structures.
• Bloch: Bloch boundary. Similar to Periodic boundary, but used when there
is a phase shift from one period to another.

The PML boundary was used on all sides in all simulations in this project. The

the photonic crystal structure is periodic, is because the Periodic boundary will
apply not only the periodicity of structures, but sources as well. Due to the

generated by spontaneous emission are incoherent, thus the Periodic boundary
should not be used here. Later in this chapter, the coherence between sources
and how to simulate an incoherent source will be discussed more in detail.
3.2.3
Analysis group
There are two diﬀerent analysis groups as shown in Figure 3.1, the far ﬁeld
and dipole power analysis group.
The far ﬁeld analysis group locates above
the surface of the sample and contains one frequency-domain ﬁeld monitor and
one index monitor.
The index monitor simply records the data of refractive
index of the its location. The frequency-domain ﬁeld monitor records all three
components of output E(⃗r) and H(⃗r), as well as the output power. It also gives
the standard Fourier transform of the recorded data. The dipole power analysis
group is located around the dipole source, and contains six frequency-domain
ﬁeld monitors which form a “box” (each monitor is one surface of the cube)
outside the dipole source. All the data recorded by these two analysis group
will be used for the calculation of far-ﬁeld radiation pattern and light extraction
eﬃciency enhancement.
3.3
Simulation methodology
3.3.1
Sources
For photoluminescence, the emitted photons are generated by the radiative re-
combination of electrons and holes. This process is called spontaneous emission
and the direction, phase and polarization of each emitted photon are all random,
i.e, incoherent. Theoretically this process must be explained with quantum me-
chanics, but due to the fact that Lumerical FDTD Solutions is just a Maxwell’s
equations solver, and the excitation/emission process are not concerned here in
fact, it is possible to treat the generated light classically. Thus a point electric
dipole source is used in this simulation.
Since FDTD is a coherent method for simulation, to simulate an unpolarized,
incoherent, isotropic dipole source, three separate simulation with diﬀerent dipole
orientations along x/y/z axes are needed (shown in Figure 3.3) [35]. The ﬁeld
intensity of this dipole source can be calculated from the ﬁeld intensity of dipoles
with diﬀerent orientations.

3.3.2
Symmetry folding operation
Due to the symmetry of hexagonal lattice, one can take advantages to reduce
the number of simulation needed.
As shown in Figure 3.4(A), the unit cell
can be represented as a diamond, the light blue area is the photonic crystal
structure. The unit cell is then divided into eight triangular areas labeled from
1 to 6 and A, C. Triangles labeled with numbers can be considered equivalent

center of the circle (light blue area). For triangle A and C, they have the same
rotational symmetry. Thus the simulation area needed is reduced to triangle 1
and A. Moreover, the triangle 1 and A have the symmetry themselves as shown
in Figure 3.4(B), and this will further reduce the simulation area. In Figure
3.4(C) and (D), it shows the that the simulation result of a dipole located at
position X can be used as the result of a dipole at positions O after applying
the symmetry to “fold” the unit cell and the number of simulation needed is
reduced from 16 to 2. If orientation of dipole source is considered, the reduction
of number of simulation is then from 48 to 6.

Chapter 7
Conclusion and outlook
The goal of this dissertation was to design, fabricate and characterize the eﬀect
of photonic crystal for enhancing the light extraction eﬃciency of 6H f-SiC. Due
to the fact that the sample used in this project was a 325 µm thick substrate
and there exists multiple optical modes in this sample, optimizing the photonic
crystal structure is extermely hard to achieve. The reason is that the photonic
crystal has a complicated modiﬁcation of the light propagation direction changes
for individual mode parameters, for diﬀerent modes, the optimal photonic crystal
structure is diﬀerent [40].
Reducing the thickness of devices can help reduce total number of optical modes,
and this has been achieved by Wierer et al who reduced the device thickness
down to 700 nm by using a laser-liftoﬀtechnique [41].
Thus optimization of
photonic crystal structure parameters can contribute to increase the behaviour
of the structure.
The work done by Wierer et al was mainly about controlling the light propaga-
tion direction to increase light extraction eﬃciency [41]. The other idea for the
same purpose can be the usage of photonic crystal for inhibiting undesired light
emission in the material. This idea was proved by Susumu Noda and Masayuki
Fujita in [42]. Moreover, non-radiative surface recombination process at the air
holes of the photonic crystal can be a problem. The solution may be to introduce
quantum nanostructures [43]
All mentioned above requires a good theoretical knowledge of related ﬁeld. For
this project, the simplest way to improve the behaviour of the photonic crystal
is to optimize the fabrication process.
Another point to consider is that the
fabricated structure has a depth of 360 nm, on a sample of 325 µm, which is only
about ∼0.1% of the sample thickness. Hence the reduction of sample thickness
should also be done in order to increase the behaviour of the photonic crystal
structure.

System operation 
 
1. Check the fiber and power connection 
1.1. Input arm of the goniometer (Fig.1a) should be connected to a laser diode (Fig.1b) (for PL 
measurement) or a Xenon lamp (Fig.1c) (for reflectance and transmittance measurement) via 
a dedicated black SMA fiber. To connect to the 375 nm laser, use a SMA2FC/PC adapter. 
And the black SMA fiber can be connected to the Xenon lamp directly. 
1.2. Output arm of the goniometer (Fig.1d) should be connected to Instrument System 
spectrometer CAS140 by using a fiber bundle (higher stability). Remember to choose the 
correct connection part for both goniometer and CAS140 when using a fiber bundle. 
1.3. Make sure that all the fiber and fiber bundle are not bended too much. 
1.4. Check the electrical power connection for the optical spectrometer, laser or Xenon lamp, 
goniometer, and computer. Turn on the power for all the equipment. To turn on the Xenon 
lamp, first turn on the power at the backside of the machine, then press the lamp button on 
the front side, finally open the shutter below the lamp button. Please allow a 25minutes 
warm-up time for the Xenon lamp. Please note that the power switch of the laser is on the 
backside of the laser diode controller and the power of the goniometer is on the backside of 
the machine. 
2. Preparation 
2.1. The sample stage can be adjusted in a range of 6.5mm (Fig.2a). Insert the small device 
made out of copper with two pins on one side (Fig.2b). You might have to lower / adjust the 
table a little bit to insert one side into the hole of the goniometer shaft (exact center). Now 
raise the table again until it hits the two pins (is stopped by the two pins) in the exact center 
position. The stage top surface is now at the focus phase. Push "SET" twice on the digital 
height meter (Fig.2c) as ‘zero’ position. 
2.2. Measure the sample thickness by using the digital height meter. Place the sample on the slit 
of the stage (face up). Lower the stage by the value of the sample thickness to make the 
sample surface at the focus phase of the system. If the sample is too small to sit on the slit. 
Use the two half-2inch Si wafer (Fig.2d) to hold the sample. In this case, further lower the 
stage by the thickness of the Si wafer which should be marked on the wafer box. 
2.3. There are two knobs (Fig.2e) on each side of the machine which are used to control the two 
arms. The small knob is used to fix the position of the arm and the big knob is used to fine 
adjust the arm position in step of 0.1°. 
2.4. To rotate the arm to certain position. Turn on the power on the backside of the machine and 
press ‘’OFFSET’’ on the goniometer (Fig.2f). ’’0’’ and ‘’0’’ should be displayed. Unplug the 
stick (Fig.2g) on the backside of the main rotation disc. Release the small knob and rotate the 
arm to the certain position, tighten the small knob (NOTE: do not tighten it too much, 
otherwise it will break the bear inside). Tune the big knob to fine adjust the position of the 
arm.  
2.5. Please be aware, big knob is ONLY used to do the fine adjustment, during the tuning of big 
knob, always keep an eye on the bottom left part of the rotation disc where you can see a 
small wheel disc mark with two of 20° (Fig.2h). Try to keep the little silver dot in the middle as 
much as you can. Make sure the silver dot in the middle do never go over either end of the 
wheel which marked with 20°, in which case you will break the goniometer unrepeatable. 
==PAGE:81==

3. Measurement 
3.1. To measure the transmittance or reflectance, by using the software ‘SpecWinLight’, measure 
a spectrum directly from input arm to output arm through the stage slit as a reference first. 
Then put the sample on the stage and measure either transmitted or reflected spectrum. The 
transmittance or reflectance of the sample will be the ratio of measured transmitted or 
reflected spectrum to the reference spectrum. 
3.2. Due to the limit from the machine design, the reflectance of the sample can be measured 
from 6° to 90°. Remember to adjust the stage height accordingly (described in 2.2). 
3.3. For angle-dependent photoluminescence, the laser excitation can be either on the backside 
or on the surface of the sample. 
3.4. During the measurement, try to avoid touching the fiber and fiber bundle, do not bend the 
fiber and fiber bundle too much when rotate the arms. 
4. Finish 
4.1. Turn off the laser: tune the knob on the laser controller all the way to the left, you can see the 
displayed power will become zero. Then switch off the key and finally switch off the laser

off the power. 
4.2. Turn off the spectrometer. 
4.3. Remove the sample from the sample stage. 
4.4. Release the two small knobs, and move the two arms back to upper middle place where you 
can plug the stick to fix the position of the two arms. 
4.5. Turn off the digital height meter and the goniometer power. 
4.6. Close the SpecWinLight and logoff the computer. Remember to save the measured spectra 
before close the software.

in place)  
ST(λ) = the signal with the Transmittance Source/Switch in the Comparison position (with the 
sample in place)

before close the software.

The integrated output fiber of 375-nm laser is a FC/PC fiber and all the other fibers used in

the dedicated fibers connected to the backside of the microscope and to the bottom of the

1.2. White light source should be connected to the backside of the microscope (Fig.1d) via a 
special fiber bundle. 
1.3. The output arm of the microscope (Fig.1e) should be connected to the optical spectrometer 
via a SMA fiber. 
1.4. Make sure that all the fiber and fiber bundle are not bended too much. 
1.5. Check the electrical power connection for the optical spectrometer, laser, white light source, 
and computer. Turn on the power for all the equipment. Please note that the power switch of 
the laser (Fig.1f) is on the backside of the laser diode controller. 
2. Sample observation 
2.1. Place the sample on the sample stage and turn on the power of the white light source. 
2.2. Set the pole on the backside of the microscope (Fig.2a) to the right position, then you can 
see white light come out of the objective and illuminate on the sample surface. 
2.3. Set the two poles on the front side of the microscope (Fig.2b) to the right position, then you 
can observe the sample through the eyepieces. Only use the knob on the microscope 
(Fig.2c) to adjust the height of the microscope to find the focus. Sample stage can be moved 
in X-Y plane in a certain range by tuning the two knobs on the stage (Fig.2d). Please be 
aware, the height of the stage can also be adjusted via the bottom jack (Fig.2e), however, it is 
not recommended to do that in normal measurements. 
3. Measurement 
3.1. To turn on the laser, first switch the key on the front side (Fig.3a), wait 10 seconds and then 
tune the knob beside the key (Fig.3a) clockwise to turn up the laser power (the unit is mW). 
Please allow 10 minutes warm-up time in order to get a laser beam with a stable output 
power. 
3.2. Set the pole on the backside of the microscope to the opposite position. In case of surface 
excitation, you can see laser beam will go through the object and illuminate on the sample 
surface. In case of backside excitation, you can see laser beam go through the sample stage 
and illuminate on the backside of the sample. 
3.3. Set the two poles on the front side of the microscope to the opposite position, so the 
luminescence emitted from the sample will be detected by the spectrometer. Do not try to 
observe the sample through the eyepieces during the measurements in case of backside 
excitation. Look at the laser beam in a straight direction is extremely dangerous to your eyes. 
3.4. Now it is ready to acquire a spectrum. Open the software ‘SpecWinLight’ on the computer, 
set all the parameters (wavelength range, integration time and number, resolution, etc) and 
acquire the photoluminescence spectrum. Background signal level will be taken and saved 
automatically every time before you change the integration time.

backside. 
4.2. Turn off the white light source. 
4.3. Turn off the spectrometer. 
4.4. Close the SpecWinLight and logoff the computer. Remember to save the measured spectra
