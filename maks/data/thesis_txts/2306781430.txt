Abstract
This projects is a study of the 1,2,3-Conjecture and related problems. The main problem is the
coloring by sums-problem: We want to assign a weight to every edge in a graph G, such that for
any pair of adjacent vertices u, v in G the sum of the edge-weights of the edges incident to u is
different from the sum of the weights of the edges incident to v. If this is satisﬁed we say that the
weights induce a proper vertex-coloring by sums. The problem is to determine which weights
we need in order to do this. We may also want to assign weights to both the edges and vertices
such that the sum of the weights of the edges incident to u and the weight of u is different from
the sum of the weights of the edges incident to v and the weight of v. A graph is called k-weight
chooseable if for any assignment of k-element lists lei to the edges ei there is a way to assign edge-
weights inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums such that for any edge ei the weight assigned
to ei is in the list lei. A graph is called (m, k)-total weight chooseable if for any assignment of
m-element lists lvi to the vertices and k-element lists lei to the edges ei there is a way to assign
edge- and vertex-weights inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums such that for any edge, ei, and
any vertex, vi, the colors assigned to ei and vi are in the lists lei and lvi respectively.
This project studies known results in the subject and provides new study concerning cographs.
Examples of the previously known results that appear in this thesis are: For any graph with
no component isomorphic to K2 there is a way to assign weights 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 to the edges such
that the assigned weights induce a proper vertex-coloring by sums [M. Kalkowski; M. Karonski;
F. Pfender: Vertex-coloring edge-weightings: Towards the 1-2-3-conjecture, Elsevier, Journal of
Combinatorial Theory, series B 100:347-349, 2010]; Every graph is (2,3)-total weight chooseable
[T. Wong; X. Zhu: Every graph is (2,3)-total weight chooseable, Combinatorica 36 (1):121-127,
2016]; Complete graphs, complete bipartite graphs and trees are 3-weight chooseable [T. Bart-
nicki; J. Grytczuk; S. Niwczyk: Weight Choosability of Graphs, Journal of Graph Theory Volume
60, Issue 3, pages 242-256, March 2009]. The investigation concerning cographs shows that every
2

cograph with no K2-component is 3-weight chooseable.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This project uses standard graph theory notation which is explained in Chapter 2. Unless otherwise
stated, graphs in this project are simple, ﬁnite and undirected.
Graph coloring is a major subﬁeld of graph theory. The basic idea is to assign colors to the el-
ements of some set such that some speciﬁed subsets do not contain two elements with the same
color. The best-known example is probably the problem of vertex-coloring, where we want to
assign a color to each vertex in such a way that any two adjacent vertices have different colors.
How many colors are needed? One could ask an analogous question for edge-coloring: How many
colors do we need to color the edges of a graph such that no two edges sharing an end-point have
the same color? These problems are fairly easy to understand, but in general difﬁcult to solve. The
main type of coloring studied in this project is called vertex-coloring by sums and is a bit more
complicated: Here we want to assign a weight (a real number) to every edge such that for any
two adjacent vertices, u, v ∈V (G) the sum of the weights of the edges incident with u is differ-
ent from the sum of the weights of the edges incident with v. If this is the case we say that the
edge-weighting induces a proper vertex-coloring by sums. Here the question is which weights
we need. In 2004 Thomason, Karonski and Luczak conjectured that there exists a weighting of
5
==PAGE:6==
the edges of any connected graph with at least three vertices with weights from the set {1, 2, 3}
inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums [11]. This conjecture is known as the 1,2,3-Conjecture
and is still open. The motivation for this conjecture was the study of the irregularity strength of
graphs. The irregularity strength of a graph, G, is the smallest k such that there is a weight func-
tion, w : E(G) →{1, 2, ..., k} inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.
Compared to the problem of vertex-coloring and edge-coloring this problem is more difﬁcult to ap-
proach since we are coloring vertices indirectly. Furthermore, it is impossible to adjust the weight
of a vertex without adjusting the weight of another vertex at the same time. Also, the problem is
not monotone in the sense that it does not behave in a nice way if we, for example, add a new edge.
More formally: if H is an induced subgraph of G and w : E(H) →{1, 2, 3} is a weight func-
tion inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums of the subgraph H, then there is no general way
to extend w to a weight function inducing a proper vertex-coloring of the whole graph G because
of the edges between H and G −H. Similarly if w is a weight function for G inducing a proper
vertex-coloring by sums of G there is no general way to adjust w to a weight function for H that
induces a proper vertex coloring by sums since removal of the edges between H and G −H will
change the vertex-weights/colors in H.
A relaxation of the coloring by sums-problem is where the weight function also assigns a weight
to each vertex. Here we want to assign weights to both the edges and the vertices such that for any
two adjacent vertices, u, v ∈V (G) the sum of the weights of the edges incident with u and the
weight of u is different from the sum of the weights of the edges incident with v and the weight
of v. In general, this problem is easier than the original problem of coloring by sums since we
can now adjust the weight of one speciﬁc vertex without changing the weight of any other vertex.
In 2010 Przybylo and Wozniak conjectured that the edges and the vertices of any graph may be
assigned weights from the set {1, 2} such that for any two adjacent vertices, u, v ∈V (G) the sum
of the weights of the edges incident with u and the weight of u is different from the sum of the
weights of the edges incident with v and the weight of v [15]. This conjecture is also open and is
6
==PAGE:7==
known as the 1,2-Conjecture.
A generalization of the coloring by sums-problem is its associated list version. This is described as
follows. To every edge, e, we assign a list, Le, of weights, and our weight-function, w, now has to
satisfy w(e) ∈Le for every edge e (in addition to inducing a proper vertex-coloring of the graph).
If there exists such a weight function inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums for any assign-
ment of k-element-lists, Le, e ∈E(G) we say that G is k-weight chooseable. This list-version of
coloring by sums is called weight-chooseability. It is easy to see that this is a generalization of
the coloring by sums-problem (the assignment of the same list of weights to each edge is just one
example of a list assignment).
This project is inspired by the 1,2,3-Conjecture and is a study of some of the known results re-
garding vertex-coloring by sums. A lot of the project is actually about the associated list-problem.
It will always be stated from where precisely the results are taken. The proofs of some of the
results may vary from the proofs in the original versions as there are added details in this project.
The main goal of this project has been to study known results and write them down in a slightly
different way. (Some of the papers that were studied were originally written in a very minimalistic
way, as is often the case with articles in scholarly journals. In this project the goal was to rewrite
these proofs in a way that includes all details. This made some of the proofs longer, but hopefully
a bit easier to follow.) If the proofs vary in a signiﬁcant way this difference will be described. If
a result has no direct reference it is because it has not been found in any of the material used for
this project. The second goal of this project has been to think about new ideas to tackle the the
1,2,3-Conjecture and related problems. We now give an outline of the whole thesis:
Chapter 3 - Notation and deﬁnitions introduces the notation and the deﬁnitions used in the the-
sis. Both the deﬁnitions and the notation are standard in graph theory and can be found in various
textbooks and scholarly papers.

1.1
Historic overview
This section follows [16] and seeks to give a historic overview of the progress on the 1,2,3-
Conjecture.
As mentioned the 1,2,3-Conjecture was formulated in [11] in 2004 by Karonski, Luczak and
Thomason. In the very same paper they proved that there exists a ﬁnite set of real numbers that can
be used to assign weights to the edges of any graph with no K2-component so that the resulting
vertex-weighting by sums is a proper vertex-coloring. This paper also shows that if (Γ, +) is a ﬁ-
nite abelian group of odd order k and if the vertices of a graph, G, with no component isomorphic
to K2, can be colored properly with k colors, then there exists a weight function, w : E(G) →Γ,
inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums of G. We give a short list of similar results. Let G be a
graph with no K2-component and let k ≥3 be an integer:
1. (Karonski, Luczak and Thomason [11], 2004) If χ(G) ≤k for k odd, then there is a
weight function w : E(G) →{1, 2, ..., k} inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.
2. (Duan, Lu and Yu [6], 2010) If G is k-colorable for k ≡0 mod 4, then there is a weight
function w : E(G) →{1, 2, ..., k} inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.
3. (Duan, Lu and Yu [6], 2010) If the minimum degree in G is at most k −2, then there is a
weight function w : E(G) →{1, 2, ..., k} inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.
4. (Duan, Lu and Yu [6], 2010) Assume that G is connected. If G has an odd number of
vertices or if there is a proper χ(G)-coloring where one color-class has even size, then there
is a weight function w : E(G) →{1, 2, ..., k} inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.
5. (Lu, Yang and Yu [14], 2009) If G is 2-connected , k-colorable and the minimum degree of
G is at least k+1 for k ≡2 mod 4, then there is a weight function w : E(G) →{1, 2, ..., k}
inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.

==PAGE:10==
These results reﬂect the early approach to the problem which was to relate the problem to the
chromatic number. Progress on the problem for general graphs can be seen in the following list.
Let G be a graph with no K2-component:
1. (Addario-Berry, Dalal, McDiarmid, Reed and Thomason [1], 2007) There is a weight
function w : E(G) →{1, 2, ..., 30} inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.
2. (Addario-Berry, Dalal, McDiarmid and Reed [2], 2008) There is a weight function w :
E(G) →{1, 2, ..., 16} inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.
3. (Wang and Yu [17], 2008) There is a weight function w : E(G) →{1, 2, ..., 13} inducing a
proper vertex-coloring by sums.
4. (Kalkowski, Karonski and Pfender [9], 2009) There is a weight function w : E(G) →
{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6} inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.
5. (Kalkowski, Karonski and Pfender [10], 2010) There is a weight function w : E(G) →
{1, 2, 3, 4, 5} inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.
The last result with edge-weights 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 is the strongest result obtained to date and its proof
is given in section Towards the 1,2,3- and 1,2-Conjecture (Theorem 3.2.1).
It is easy to ﬁnd graphs for which two edge-weights do not sufﬁce, for example K3, but one may
investigate for which graphs the edge-weights 1, 2 do sufﬁce. A complete classiﬁcation of these
graphs do not yet exist. Actually, Dudek and Wajc proved in [7] that it is NP-complete to determine
whether or not a graph can be properly colored by sums with weights 1 and 2. Only partial results
are known in this subject. Let G be a graph with no K2-component:
1. (Chang, Lu, Wu and Yu [5], 2011) If G is bipartite and d −regular for d ≥3 then there is
a weight function w : E(G) →{1, 2} inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.

2. (Lu, Yu and Zhang [13], 2011) If G is either bipartite and 3-connected or has minimum
degree at least 8χ(G), then there is a weight function w : E(G) →{1, 2} inducing a proper
vertex-coloring by sums.
3. (Khatirinejad et al [12], 2012) If all cycles in G have length divisible by 4, then there is a
weight function w : E(G) →{1, 2} inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums.

2.1
Basic graph theoretical deﬁnitions and notation
A graph G = (V (G), E(G)) is an ordered pair of two ﬁnite disjoint sets V (G) and E(G). An
element in V (G) is called a vertex and an element in E(G) is called an edge. Every edge is
associated with two vertices x, y ∈V (G) that are called the ends of the edge. We say that an edge,
e ∈E(G) with ends x, y ∈V (G) joins x and y. Two vertices can be joined by more than one
edge. If this is the case we talk about multiple edges. A loop is an edge which joins a vertex to
itself. A graph is called simple if it contains no loops nor multiple edges. If G is simple we also
write e = xy when e is an edge joining the vertices x and y. We will use the term multi-graph to
emphasize that there can be multiple edges. The degree or valency of a vertex x is deﬁned as the
number of edges which are associated with x (loops count for two edges). For a vertex v ∈V (G),
we let N(v) denote the set of vertices in G adjacent to v. The vertices in N(v) are also called the
neighbors of v. Two vertices are called adjacent if there is an edge joining them. A vertex, u, is
12
==PAGE:13==
incident with an edge, e, if e has u as one of its ends. An edge, e, is incident (or adjacent) with
another edge, e′, if e and e′ have an end in common. If v ∈V (G) is a vertex we call the set of all
vertices incident to v the neighborhood of v (a vertex in this set is also called a neighbor to v).
The closed neighborhood of v is the set consisting of v together with the neighborhood of v.
An isomorphism f between two graphs G and H is deﬁned as a bijection between the vertex sets
of the graphs; f : V (G) →V (H), such that any two vertices u, v ∈V (G) in G are adjacent if and
only if f(u) and f(v) are adjacent in H. If such an isomorphism exists we say that G and H are
isomorphic.
Let us look at two examples of graph families: We let Kn denote the simple graph with n vertices,
where any pair of vertices is joined by an edge. This is called the complete graph with n vertices.
A graph which is isomorphic to a complete graph is sometimes called a clique. A set of vertices
in a graph where no two vertices are adjacent is called an independent set. We let Kn,m denote
the simple graph where the vertex set consists of two disjoint sets A and B, with n and m vertices
respectively, and where every vertex in A is joined to every vertex in B by an edge, and where
there is no edge between any pair of vertices (x, y) where x and y belong to the same set A or B.
This is called the complete bipartite graph with n + m vertices (see Figure 2.1). More generally
we let Kn1,...,nk denote the simple graph where the vertex set consists of k disjoint sets, A1, ..., Ak,
with n1, ..., nk vertices respectively, and where every vertex in Ai is joined to every vertex in Aj,
j ̸= i by an edge, and where there is no edge between any pair, (x, y), of vertices where x and
y belong to the same set Ai. This is called the complete multipartite graph with n1 + · · · + nk
vertices (see Figure 2.2). Both complete graphs and complete bipartite graphs are special cases
of multipartite graphs. A multipartite graph is a simple graph, G, where the vertex set consists
of k ≥1 disjoint sets, A1, ..., Ak, such that there is no edge between any pair, (x, y), of vertices
where x and y belong to the same set Ai. In the special case where k = 2, G is called bipartite.

A path is a graph with vertices {v1, v2, ...vn} and edges {v1v2, v2v3, ..., vn−1vn}. If 2 ≤n and we
add the edge vnv1 we obtain a cycle . The length of the path with vertices {v1, v2, ...vn} is n −1,
and the length of the cycle with vertices {v1, v2, ...vn} is n. A path of length n −1 has n vertices
and is denoted Pn. A cycle with length n is also called an n-cycle. A graph H = (V (H), E(H))
is called a subgraph of G = (V (G), E(G)) if V (H) ⊂V (G) and E(H) ⊂E(G). A subgraph
H ⊂G of a graph G is a an induced subgraph of G if, for any pair of vertices x, y in V (H) we
have that xy is an edge of H if and only if xy is an edge of G.
If G is a graph and A ⊂G is a subgraph, then G −A is the graph obtained by deleting all vertices
and edges in A from G, and whenever we delete a vertex we also delete all edges having that vertex
as an end. If E is a set of edges in G we let G−E denote the graph obtained from G by deleting all
the edges in E (we do not delete any vertices). We also write G −v and G −e instead of G −{v}

and G −{e} when deleting a single vertex v or a single edge e.
We say that a graph G is connected if every pair of vertices in G is joined by a path in G. A
connected component or just a component of a graph G is a maximal connected subgraph. We
say that a graph G is 2-connected, if G has more than two vertices and for every vertex v ∈V (G),
we have that G −v is connected. More generally a graph G is said to be k-connected if G has
more than k vertices and if for any set A ⊂V (G) consisting of at most k −1 vertices, we have
that G −A is connected. For example K3,3 is 3-connected, and K5 is 4-connected, See ﬁgure 2.1.
A subdivision of a graph G is a graph obtained from G by replacing some (or all) edges of G by
paths with the same ends. Informally we can say that we are inserting vertices on edges. If we
have inserted k vertices on an edge, e, we say that e has been subdivided by k vertices. See Figure
2.3 for an example of a subdivision.

2.2
Graph-coloring
Here follows a quick overview of the coloring problems investigated in this project. The deﬁnitions
will be repeated later on.
A proper vertex-coloring of a graph G = (V (G), E(G)) is a map, ω : V (G) →S (where S
is some set of colors), assigning to each vertex v ∈V (G) a color ω(v) ∈S, such that any two
adjacent vertices have different colors. We also use the formulation that ω colors V (G) properly.
The minimum number of colors needed to color the vertices of a graph G properly is called the
chromatic number of G and is denoted χ(G).
15
==PAGE:16==
Instead of coloring the vertices we can also speak of a proper edge-coloring of a graph G =
(V (G), E(G)): A proper edge-coloring of a graph G = (V (G), E(G)) is a map, ω : E(G) →S
(where S is some set of colors), assigning to each edge e ∈E(G) a color ω(e) ∈S, such that
any two adjacent edges have different colors. We also use the formulation that ω colors E(G)
properly. The most important type of coloring for this project is called a proper vertex-coloring
by sums and is deﬁned as follows. Let f : E(G) →R be a map from the set of edges E(G) to
the real numbers. For any edge e we call f(e) the weight of edge e. Deﬁne ω : V (G) →R as
ω(v) = P
e∈E(G) incident to v f(e). For any vertex v ∈V (G) we call ω(v) the induced vertex weight
of v. If ω colors V (G) properly we say that the pair (ω, f) is a proper vertex-coloring by sums.
In this project we also study coloring by sums where the weight function assigns weights to both
the edges and the vertices. That is, f : E(G) ∪V (G) →R and ω : V (G) →R is now deﬁned as
ω(v) = P
e∈E(G) incident to v f(e) + f(v). These induced vertex weights ω(v), v ∈V (G) are called
total vertex weights and the the function, f, is called a total weight function.
To a vertex-coloring problem we have an associated problem called the list version. This is deﬁned
as follows. To every vertex, v, we assign a list, Lv, of colors, and our coloring-map, ω, must now
satisfy that ω(v) ∈Lv for every vertex v. If such a proper coloring-map exists for any assignment
of lists, Lv, v ∈V (G) of size k we say that G is k-list colorable. We also have a list version
associated to the problem of coloring by sums deﬁned as follows. To every edge, e, we assign a
list, Le, of weights, and our weight-function, f, now has to satisfy f(e) ∈Le for every edge e. If
there exists such a weight function which in addition induces a proper vertex-coloring by sums for
any assignment of lists, Le, e ∈E(G) of size k we say that G is k-weight chooseable. This list
version of coloring by sums is called weight-chooseability. We also have a list-version associated
to total weight functions: To every element, z in V (G) ∪E(G), we assign a list, Lz, of weights,
and our total weight-function, f, now has to satisfy f(z) ∈Lz for every z ∈V (G) ∪E(G). If
there exists such a weight function which in addition induces a proper vertex-coloring by sums for
any assignment of lists, Lz, z ∈V (G) ∪E(G) of size k when z is an edge and of size m when z

is a vertex we say that G is (m, k)-total weight chooseable.

3.2
Towards the 1,2,3- and 1,2-Conjecture
This section presents some of the best known results in relation to the 1,2,3-Conjecture and the
1,2-Conjecture. The following theorem is the best known towards the 1,2,3-Conjecture. Here the
proof has been extended to include multigraphs and not only simple graphs as in [10]. Also, a lot
of details have been added and reformulations have been made to make the proof easier to follow.
Theorem 3.2.1 [10] Let G = (V, E) be a multigraph without loops and without components with
only two vertices. There exists a weighting ω : E(G) →{1, 2, 3, 4, 5} of the edges, such that the
induced vertex weights by sums properly color V (G).
Proof This proof shows a recursive way of ﬁnding appropriate edge-weights. Let |V (G)| = n
and let d(v) denote the degree of v ∈V (G), that is, the number of edges incident to vertex
v. Furthermore let D(v) denote the number of vertices adjacent to v ∈V (G). Clearly we can
21
==PAGE:22==
assume that G is connected by arguing component-wise. If |V (G)| ≤1, then we are done, and
the case |V (G)| = 2 is excluded by assumption. So we can assume that |V (G)| ≥3. We seek
an ordering of the vertices {v1, v2, ..., vn} such that vn has at least 2 neighbors in G and such that
for i = 1, 2, ..., n −1 vertex vi has at least one neighbor vj, j > i. This ordering is found in the
following way. Start with some vertex having at least 2 neighbors and call it vn. The ordering is
made recursively from vn and down, so assume that vk, vk+1, ..., vn have been deﬁned. Since G
is connected then unless the ordering is complete, there is some non-ordered vertex, v, which is
adjacent to some already ordered vertex. Deﬁne v = vk−1. Proceeding in this way we achieve the
desired ordering.
We will recursively construct a sequence of edge-weight functions fi : E(G) →{1, 2, 3, 4, 5}
for i = 1, ..., n where each fi+1 will be a modiﬁcation of fi and where fn will be our ﬁnal edge-
weighting. Also, for any v ∈V (G) let fi(v) be the induced color of vertex v when the edges
are weighted by fi, that is, the sum of the fi-weights of the edges incident to v. We start out by
setting f1(e) = 3 for all e ∈E(G). In the rest of the proof it should be implicitly understood
that for i = 2, ..., n fi(e) = fi−1(e) for each edge e ∈E(G) unless otherwise stated. For every
i < n we will assign a set of two colors W(vi) = {w(vi), w(vi) + 2} with w(vi) ∈{0, 1} mod 4
and deﬁne the functions f1, f2, ..., fn−1, such that for every edge vjvi with 1 ≤j < i, we have
W(vj) ∩W(vi) = ∅and fn−1(vi) ∈W(vi). When this is done we need to consider vn separately
and deﬁne fn.
For any pair of adjacent vertices vb, vc ∈V (G) we choose one speciﬁc edge connecting them and
call it vbvc. We will only modify the weights of these "speciﬁc" edges in the rest of the proof. We
start by looking at the vertices vi, i < n. Recall that f1(e) = 3 for all edges, e, so f1(v1) = 3d(v1).
We pick W(v1) = {w(v1), w(v1) + 2} such that f1(v1) ∈W(v1) and such that w(v1) ∈{0, 1}
mod 4. This is of course always possible. We proceed recursively so assume that we have picked
W(vi) for all i < k where k is some natural number satisfying 2 ≤k < n. In addition assume the
following:
22
==PAGE:23==
(a) For all i < k we have W(vi) = {w(vi), w(vi) + 2} for some positive number w(vi) with
w(vi) ∈{0, 1} mod 4.
(b) fk−1(vi) ∈W(vi) for i < k.
(c) fk−1(vkvj) = 3 for all edges, vkvj, with j > k.
(d) For i < k we have that if fk−1(vivk) ̸= 3, then either fk−1(vivk) = 2 and fk−1(vi) = w(vi),
or fk−1(vivk) = 4 and fk−1(vi) = w(vi) + 2.
We will explain how to assign W(vk) and deﬁne fk such that the above assumptions also hold
when k is replaced with k + 1. If vivk ∈E(G), i < k, we will choose fk(vivk) to be one of
the values fk−1(vivk) −2, fk−1(vivk) or fk−1(vivk) + 2. In what follows we explain how we
make the appropriate choice. If vivk ∈E(G) for i < k we can either increase fk−1(vivk) by 2
if fk−1(vi) = w(vi) or we can decrease fk−1(vivk) by 2 if fk−1(vi) = w(vi) + 2, and we can
deﬁne fk in this way such that fk(vi) ∈W(vi). Of course it is also possible to leave edge-weights
unchanged. Let s be the number of neighbors vi (i < k) of vk such that fk−1(vi) + 2 ∈W(vi) and
let ℓbe the number of neighbors vi (i < k) of vk such that fk−1(vi) −2 ∈W(vi). In total we get
s + ℓ+ 1 = d + 1 different choices for fk(vk). Note that all these choices have the same parity.
In addition to this we also allow ourselves to possibly alter the weight fk−1(vkvj) by ±1, where
j > k is minimal with the property that vk and vj are adjacent. This way fk(vk) can take all integer
values in an interval [a, a + 2d + 2] for some natural number a. We want to adjust the weights
(deﬁne fk) and assign w(vk) ∈{0, 1} mod 4, deﬁning W(vk) = {w(vk), w(vk) + 2}, such that
the following holds:
1. fk(vi) ∈W(vi) for 1 ≤i ≤k.
2. w(vi) ̸= w(vk) for vivk ∈E(G) with i < k.
3. Let j > k be minimal such that vkvj ∈E(G). Either fk(vk) = w(vk) and fk(vkvj) ∈{2, 3}
or fk(vk) = w(vk) + 2 and fk(vkvj) ∈{3, 4}.
23
==PAGE:24==
Note that since we must have w(vi) ∈{0, 1} mod 4 for all i ≤k condition (2) is equivalent to
W(vi) ∩W(vk) = ∅. Each w(vi) for vivk ∈E(G) with i < k "blocks" both w(vi) and w(vi) + 2
in [a, a + 2d + 2] as values for fk(vk). Hence condition (2) can "block" at most 2d values for
fk(vk). Note that by (c) we have that fk−1(vkvj) = 3 where j is as in (3). Note also that any of
the values in [a, a + 2d + 2] for fk(vk) different from a and a + 2d + 2 which can be obtained by
deﬁning fk(vkvj) = 2 (respectively fk(vkvj) = 4) can also be reached by deﬁning fk(vkvj) = 4
(respectively fk(vkvj) = 2). Hence condition (3) can only "block" the values a and a + 2d + 2
as values for fk(vk). Hence at least one value remains free for fk(vk) and we choose w(vk) and
deﬁne fk accordingly.
By the above procedure we can assign all the sets W(vi) for i ≤n −1 and deﬁne all the functions
f1, f2, ..., fn−1 without any conﬂict. Now we need to consider vn separately and deﬁne fn.
This time we don’t have an extra "forward" edge to use, but we also don’t have to worry about
later vertices. If vivn ∈E(G) we can, as above, either add or subtract 2 to or from fn−1(vivn)
while keeping fn(vi) ∈W(vi). These adjustments give a total of D(vn) + 1 ≥3 possibilities
(all of the same parity) for fn(vn). If the smallest of these options gives color a ∈{2, 3} mod 4
to vn, then choosing the lowest possible weight on each edge incident with vn and deﬁning fn in
this way yields a proper vertex-coloring of G (for all v ∈N(vn) we will have fn(v) ∈{0, 1}
mod 4). If, on the other hand, a satisﬁes a ∈{0, 1} mod 4 and there is some vertex vi ∈N(vn)
with w(vi) ̸= a, then picking the higher weight on vivn and the lower weight on all other edges
incident to vn and deﬁning fn in this way yields a proper vertex-coloring with fn(vn) = a + 2 (for
all v ∈N(vn), v ̸= vi, we have fn(v) ∈{0, 1} mod 4, while a + 2 ∈{2, 3} mod 4 satisﬁes
a + 2 ̸= fn(vi) = w(vi) + 2). If a satisﬁes a ∈{0, 1} mod 4 and w(vi) = a for all vi ∈N(vn),
then picking the higher weight on at least 2 edges and deﬁning fn in this way gives a proper vertex-
coloring (fn(vn) ≥a+4 while f(v) ≤a+2 for all v ∈N(vn)). We complete the proof by setting
ω = fn.

The next theorem was until recently the best known in relation to the 1,2-Conjecture. The cur-
rently best now result is in [18] and is shown later in this project (Theorem 3.4.17). As in the case
with Theorem 3.2.1 this proof is extended here to include multigraphs and not only simple graphs
as in [8], and reformulations have been made to make the proof easier to follow. Also here, the
most signiﬁcant difference between the proof in this project and the proof in [10] is that here we
deﬁne a sequence of weight functions f0, f1, ... leading to the ﬁnal weight function fn whereas in
[8] the authors repeatably redeﬁne their weight function.
Theorem 3.2.2 [8] Let G = (V, E) be a multigraph without loops. There exists a total weighting
ω using weights 1, 2, 3 on the edges and weights 1, 2 on the vertices, such that the induced total
vertex weights by sums properly color V (G).
Proof This proof shows a recursive way of ﬁnding appropriate edge- and vertex-weights. Let
|V (G)| = n and let d(v) denote the degree of v ∈V (G), that is, the number of edges incident to
vertex v. Clearly we can assume that G is connected by arguing component-wise. If |V (G)| ≤1
we are done, so we can assume that |V (G)| ≥2. We order the vertices {v1, v2, ..., vn} arbitrarily.
We will recursively construct a sequence of edge- and vertex-weight functions fi for i = 1, ..., n+1
using weights {1, 2, 3} on the edges and weights {1, 2} on the vertices, and where each fi+1 will
be a modiﬁcation of fi. The weight-function fn+1 will be our ﬁnal total weighting. Also, for any
v ∈V (G) let ψi(v) be the induced color of vertex v when the edges and vertices are weighted
by fi, that is, the sum of the fi-weights of the edges incident to v plus the fi-weight of v. The
ﬁnal color-function, φ : V (G) →N, is recursively deﬁned as we go through the list {v1, v2, ..., vn}
25
==PAGE:26==
and the modiﬁcations of the weight-function will guarantee ψn+1(v) = φ(v) for all v ∈V (G).
We start out by setting f1(e) = 2 for all e ∈E(G) and f1(v) = 1 for all v ∈V (G). In the
rest of the proof it should be implicitly understood that for i = 2, ..., n + 1 fi(e) = fi−1(e) and
fi(v) = fi−1(v) for each edge e ∈E(G) and each vertex v ∈V (G) unless stated otherwise. For
any pair of adjacent vertices vb, vc ∈V (G) we choose one speciﬁc edge connecting them and call
it vbvc. We will only modify the weights of these "speciﬁc" edges in the rest of the proof. For v1
we have ψ1(v1) = 2d(v1) + 1 and we choose φ(v1) = 2d(v1) + 2 = ψ1(v1) + 1. We proceed
recursively so assume that we have picked fk−1 and φ(vi) for all i < k where k is some natural
number satisfying 2 ≤k ≤n. In addition assume the following:
1. ψk−1(vi) = φ(vi) or ψk−1(vi) = φ(vi) −1, for i < k.
2. φ(vi) ̸= φ(vj) if vivj ∈E(G) and i, j < k.
3. fk−1(e) = 2 for every edge e incident with vk and vj, j > k.
4. fk−1(v) = 1 for all v ∈V (G).
We will explain how to deﬁne fk and φ(vk) such that the above assumptions also hold when k is
replaced with k + 1. Assume that vk has d neighbors among the vertices {v1, v2, ..., vk−1}. For any
edge e = vivk, for i < k we have the following possibilities for deﬁning fk(e): We can keep the
"old" weight fk(e) = fk−1(e) or either, deﬁne fk(e) = fk−1(e) + 1 if ψk−1(vi) = φ(vi) −1, or
fk(e) = fk−1(e) −1 if ψk−1(vi) = φ(vi). Let s be the number of neighbors vi, i < k, of vk where
ψk−1(vi) = φ(vi)−1 and let ℓbe the number of neighbors vi, i < k, of vk where ψk−1(vi) = φ(vi).
Now we have every integer in the interval [2d(vk) + 1 −ℓ; 2d(vk) + 1 + s], where s + ℓ= d, as
a possible value for ψk(vk). Vertex vk is adjacent to precisely d vertices vi with i < k and the
interval [2d(vk) + 1 −ℓ; 2d(vk) + 1 + s] contains d + 1 integers. Thus at least one value ("color")
is from the interval is different from all the φ(vi), i < k and vivk ∈E(G). We choose such a color
ψk(vk) and deﬁne fk accordingly. We also put φ(vk) = ψk(vk). We continue recursively in this
26
==PAGE:27==
way, deﬁning f1, f2, ..., fn and φ(v1), φ(v2), ..., φ(vn), untill we have processed every vertex. Now
G has the following properties:
(a) Every vertex v ∈V (G) has ψn(v) = φ(v) or ψn(v) = φ(v) −1.
(b) No adjacent vertices have the same ﬁnal color assigned.
(c) fk(e) ∈{1, 2, 3} for all e ∈E(G).
(d) fk(v) = 1 for all e ∈V (G).
If v ∈V (G) has the property that ψn(v) = φ(v) −1, we deﬁne fn+1(v) = fn(v) + 1. Now fn+1
induces a proper vertex-coloring of G.

As we saw previously (Lemma 3.1.2) complete graphs can be properly colored using edge-weights
1, 2, 3. An obvious question is then: are the algorithms in Theorem 3.2.2 and Theorem 3.2.1 using
more than the edge-weights 1, 2, 3 on complete graphs? This question is investigated here. Note
that there are some choices to be made during the execution of the algorithms. That is, as described
in the proofs of Theorem 3.2.1 and 3.2.2, the algorithms are not completely deterministic. How
these choices are made will determine which edge-weights we use. This is discussed below in the
analysis of each algorithm.
We start by considering the algorithm from Theorem 3.2.2. This algorithm was constructed in
relation to the 1,2-conjecture [8]. Nevertheless, we consider it in relation to the 1,2,3-Conjecture
in this analysis. The algorithm uses edge-weights 1, 2, 3 and vertex-weights 1, 2, so the question is
whether or not the algorithm uses different vertex-weights. The claim is that unless we are dealing
with K3 or K5 the algorithm is not wasteful (that is, the same weight is assigned to all vertices) if
the choices in the algorithm are made in a proper way. This is shown below.
Consider the general complete graph with ordered vertices {v1, v2, ..., vn}. The algorithm starts
by putting all edge weights equal to 2 and all vertex-weights equal to 1. Furthermore we have that
ψ1(v1) = 2d(v1) + 1 = 2n −1 and φ(v1) = 2d(v1) + 2 = ψ1(v1) + 1 = 2n. The next 3 steps in
the algorithm are forced:
• In step 2 we must choose φ(v2) = 2d(v2) + 1 = 2n −1 leaving all edge weights unchanged.
• In step 3 we must choose φ(v3) = 2d(v3) = 2d(v3) = 2n −2 changing the edge-weight
f3(v2v3) = 1. Note that this shows that the algorithm is wasteful if n = 3, since we need to
deﬁne f4(v1) = 2.
• In step 4 we must choose φ(v4) = 2d(v4) + 3 = 2n + 1 changing the weights f4(v1v4) =
f4(v2v4) = 3.
Note that after step 4 all the vertex-weights are 1 and ψ4(vi) = φ(vi) holds for i = 1, 2, 3, 4. Hence

the algorithm is not wasteful if n = 4. No matter how we make the choice in step 5 we will get
a vertex vj, j < 5 where ψ5(vj) = φ(vj) −1. This shows that the algorithm is wasteful if n = 5
since we will have to deﬁne f5(vj) = 2.
Now, how should we proceed from step 4 in general? We start with the case where n > 4 is an
even number. If this is the case we can make the following choice for vℓ, ℓ> 4:
• If ℓis odd deﬁne φ(vℓ) = 2d(vℓ) + 2 −ℓ= 2n −ℓchanging the edge-weights fℓ(vivℓ) = 1
for all i < l.
• If ℓis even deﬁne φ(vℓ) = 2d(vℓ) + ℓ−1 changing the edge-weights fℓ(vivℓ) = 3 for all
i < l −1.
When n is even these choices will clearly lead to ψn(v) = φ(v) for all vertices v. This means that
the algorithm is not wasteful.
Now let us look at the case where n > 5 is an odd number. In this case we do the following:
• Deﬁne φ(v5) = 2d(v5) −1 = 2n −3 changing the edge-weights f5(v1v5) = f5(v2v5) = 1.
• In step 6 we then deﬁne φ(v6) = 2d(v6)−4 changing the edge-weights f6(v3v6) = f6(v4v6) =

• If ℓis even deﬁne φ(vl) = 2d(vl) + 2 −ℓ= 2n −ℓchanging the edge-weights fℓ(vivℓ) = 1
for all i < l.
• If ℓis odd deﬁne φ(vℓ) = 2d(vℓ) + ℓ−1 changing the edge-weights fℓ(vivℓ) = 3 for all
i < l −1.
When n is odd these choices will clearly lead to ψn(v) = φ(v) for all vertices v. This means that
the algorithm is not wasteful.

Now we consider the algorithm from Theorem 3.2.1. This algorithm uses edge-weights 1, 2, 3, 4, 5.
The claim is that this algorithm will always use weight 4 or 5 for some edges on complete graphs.
It might also be interesting to investigate whether or not it always uses more than 3 weights (except
for the case with K3). This could be another topic to look into. This is not included here and we
will just show that the algorithm will always use weight 4 or 5. Consider the general complete
graph with n > 2 vertices and vertex order {v1, v2, ..., vn}. The algorithm starts out by putting
all edge-weights equal to 3. Here we only consider the case where 3n −3 ≡1 mod 4. The
analysis in the other cases are similar and rather tedious to do and are therefore omitted here. We
note that f1(v1) = 3n −3 and deﬁne W(v1) = {3n −3, 3n −1}. Recall that if we are in some
step k of the algorithm and change an edge-weight of an edge of type vmvk for m < k we never
change this weight again, so in step 2 we cannot deﬁne f2(v1v2) = 5. Hence in step 2 we can
only choose f2(v2v3) = 2 or f2(v2v3) = 4. Consider the case where we deﬁne f2(v2v3) = 2 and
W(v2) = {3n −4, 3n −2}. In this case we cannot change the weight of either v1v3 or v2v3 in
step 3 since this will lead to edge-weights 5 or 4. We cannot change the weight of edge v3v4 either
since the current color of v3 is congruent to 0 mod 4. This excludes the choice of f2(v2v3) = 2.
Now we consider the case where we deﬁne f2(v2v3) = 4 and W(v2) = {3n −4, 3n −2}. Then in
step 3 we must change the weight of edge v2v3: f3(v2v3) = 2 and we cannot change the weight of
v1v3. When we make these changes the color of v3 is 3n−4 and as before since 3n−4 mod 4 = 0
we cannot deﬁne f3(v3v4) = 2 or f3(v3v4) = 4. This excludes the choice of f2(v2v3) = 4 and
completes the case where 3n −3 mod 4 = 1. As mentioned above the other cases are similar and
omitted.

3.5
Split-graphs
This section investigates vertex coloring by sums for so-called "split-graphs". A split-graph is a
graph consisting of a clique together with an independent set and an arbitrary collection of edges
between the clique and the independent set. Note that if there is a vertex, v, in the independent
set which is joined by an edge to all the vertices in the clique we can move v to the clique and
still have a split-graph. So when we investigate split-graphs we can assume that there is no such
vertex v in the independent set. In this case we also say that the clique is maximal. We say that a
graph, G, is (n, m)-split if its vertex set can be partitioned into an n-vertex maximal clique and an
m vertex independent set. See Figure 3.12 for an example of a split-graph.
Figure 3.12: An example of a graph which is (4, 3)-split.
It is easy to prove that these graphs satisfy the 1,2,3-Conjecture:
Lemma 3.5.1 Let G be an (n, m)-split graph which is not isomorphic to K2. There exists an
edge-weighting, f : E(G) →{1, 2, 3}, inducing a proper vertex coloring of G by sums.
Proof Order the n vertices of the maximal clique by their degrees: v1, ...vn, where i > j implies
d(vi) ≥d(vj). Note that none of the m vertices in the independent set is joined to all the n vertices
of the clique since otherwise our clique would not be maximal. Deﬁne the weight function, f, on
the clique as we did in Theorem 3.1.3. Then the the weight of f(vj) is strictly increasing in j. We
61
==PAGE:62==
then extend f to a weight function g : E(G) →{1, 2, 3} by assigning the weight 1 to all the edges
between the clique and the independent set. Since the vertices in the clique were ordered by their
degree, g(vj) is strictly increasing in j. Furthermore each vertex in the clique has weight at least
n −1, and if the weight of a vertex in the clique is exactly n −1 then this vertex has no neighbors
in the independent set. On the other hand, any vertex in the independent set has weight at most
n −1. This means that our induced vertex-coloring by sums is proper.
□
We can prove that split-graphs with small independent set are 3-weight chooseable:
Lemma 3.5.2 Let G be a graph which is (n, m)-split which is not isomorphic to K2. If m ≤2
then mind(G) ≤2.
Proof If m = 1, G cannot contain an induced subgraph isomorphic to P4, and in this case we are
done by Lemma 3.4.12 and Theorem 3.4.15. So assume that m = 2. The proof is by induction on
the size of the clique, n. It is easy to check that the assertion holds for n = 1, 2, 3, 4. We proceed
to the induction step and assume n ≥5. Let {v1, v2} be the independent set. Any vertex, v, in the
clique in G can either be joined to neither of v1 and v2, joined to both or joined to only one of v1
and v5. Since n ≥5 then by the pigeonhole principle at least two vertices in the clique in G has
the same closed neighborhood. Now we are done by Theorem 3.4.7 and the induction hypothesis.
□
Note that this proof is not very general since the non-trivial part besides the use of Theorem 3.4.7
is checking the induction basis by brute force. If one hoped to show that any graph, G ̸= K2,
which is (n, m)-split satisﬁes mind(G) ≤2 with this approach, one would need to check all the
(n, m)-split graphs where n ≤2m in the induction basis.
It might be interesting to investigate for which split graphs there is a weight function using only
weights 1,2 and inducing a proper vertex-coloring by sums. This could be a subject for further
