Abstract
Accurate modeling of sea states is essential for predicting the dynamic responses of mar­
itime structures exposed to wave action.
The present work combines several data sources and modeling approaches in order to
investigate the performance of ship­response prediction models induced by waves. To
do so, data from ERA5, a climate reanalysis database that includes coherent and high­
resolution sea state estimates, was employed. This information was implemented on
wave parametric models and combined with DNV software­calculated transfer functions
and real onboard measurements from an operating transatlantic vessel.
Various parametric wave spectral models such as Bretschneider and JONSWAP were
implemented to analyze how different modeling strategies affect the spectral shape of
directional waves, as well as the ship responses, focusing mainly on the heave acceler­
ation. A sensitivity analysis on the usage of mean instead of the peak wave direction,
the directional spreading parameter, and the peakedness factor of the JONSWAP model
were performed. This analysis aimed to make these parametric approaches resemble as
much as possible the one derived from ERA5 to enable a more realistic representation of
the sea state.
Directional wave spectra and the transfer functions were the basis for a model that in­
corporates the Doppler shift effect due to the forward motion of the ship. This allows
for the distinction between the absolute and the encounter domain, using the latter for
the estimation of the ship response. The results were then compared with the real mea­
surements, allowing evaluation of the impact of previous modeling choices on the model
performance.
A high variability in the model behavior depending on the input parameters was shown,
highlighting the importance of initially adjusting them appropriately. Moreover, it was ob­
served how the Bretschneider model, even if simple, gave reasonable results, especially
when implementing a bimodal approach.
All this contributes to improving the understanding of how wave spectrum parameteriza­
tion affects ship response predictions, supporting a more reliable design and analysis of
offshore and maritime structures. The findings are broadly applicable, contributing to en­
hanced performance, safety, management, and durability of floating and fixed installations
such as ships, offshore platforms and wind turbines.

1
Introduction
The precise modeling of sea states is essential for the understanding and prediction of the
dynamic responses of ocean structures, such as vessels, offshore platforms, and other
maritime installations.
For decades, the classical approach to obtaining ocean­related information relied on vi­
sual inspections, CTD instruments, satelite­based altimetry, research vessels, tide gauges,
and especially wave buoys. The latter are floating devices equipped with various sensors
that register motion induced by waves, allowing for the extraction of relevant information
about the sea state (Holthuijsen, 2007). Thanks to networks such as the ODAS (Ocean
Data Acquisition System) buoys, these devices have played a key role in meteorological
forecasting systems, the validation of numerical models, and the monitoring of sea con­
ditions. Their operation involves measuring the response of the floating elements and
transforming this data into wave spectral representations.
In this context, the Wave Buoy Analogy (WBA) describes how navigational ships can act
as wave buoys by replicating their function, measuring the ship’s dynamic responses and
transforming them into sea state information (Nielsen, 2008b). This approach enables
real­time estimation of the sea state precisely at the ship’s location.
The estimation of sea state using data from moving ships has gained increasing relevance
in recent years, especially following the development of the concept of collaborative wave
sensors, introduced in works such as (Nielsen, 2018). This approach integrates several
observational means in a complementary manner, combining heterogeneous platforms.
Doing this, a potentially more accurate sea state estimation can be obtained, as the un­
certainty associated with each individual platform may be reduced.
When introducing a factor such as the forward motion of a moving ship, additional con­
siderations must be taken into account when processing the measured spectra. Sev­
eral studies in the specialized literature address this issue, including (Dam Nielsen & of
Denmark., 2005), (Nielsen, 2008a), (Nielsen & Stredulinsky, 2012) and (Nielsen & Dietz,
2020), among others. Although these approaches have certain limitations compared to
more traditional methods discussed earlier, they are considered a useful complement to
classical measurement­based techniques, as they provide real­time spatial and temporal
information by leveraging the wide availability of operating vessels at sea.
1.1
Project scope and objectives
The scope of this project is to implement idealized parametric wave models, such as
Bretschneider (Bretschneider, 1952) and JONSWAP (Hasselmann et al., 1973), and an­
alyze how decisions made during the parameterization process affect both the directional
wave spectrum and the dynamic response of the ship. A preliminary study of wave direc­
tionality trends in the North Atlantic Ocean has also been carried out, providing a broader
oceanographic context of wave directionality and supported by the observations detailed
in (Goharnejad et al., 2022).
In the present work, information extracted from the ERA5 database (Hersbach et al., 2023)
is combined with transfer functions provided by Wasim, a software developed by DNV
(DNV, 2023), as well as real dynamic response measurements obtained from an in­service
transatlantic: a 2800 TEU Panamax container ship (Nielsen et al., 2024).

One of the main objectives of this study is to understand the fundamental differences be­
tween parametrized spectra, which represent the sea in a simplified manner, and those
obtained from databases such as ERA5, which offer a more complex and realistic repre­
sentation. This allows for a critical evaluation of the advantages and limitations of each
approach, particularly regarding their application in operational contexts. To address this,
a sensitivity analysis is first conducted to properly adjust the input parameters of the ide­
alized wave models so that they closely resemble the ERA5 directional wave spectra.
Additionally, it is aimed to analyze the hydrodynamic response of the floating ship un­
der the aforementioned spectral excitation. This analysis is approached by the using the
transfer functions or Response Amplitude Operators (RAOs), that allow the transforma­
tion from wave movement into ship responses. To do so, a detailed analysis focusing
on the heave acceleration of a ship is performed, assessing the performance of the in­
duced response models by comparing them with the measured ones from the previously
mentioned vessel.
From this spectral modeling, the variations in the spectral shape of the response are as­
sessed, as these variations play a key role in decision making during the design stages of
ships and other floating structures, which are typically subjected to strict safety regulations
regarding operability under severe sea state conditions. Consequently, the evaluation of
the performance of the developed models was performed by comparing their estimations
with measured data, allowing an accuracy and applicability evaluation in real­world sce­
narios.
Another key goal of this study is to analyze the implication of the Doppler effect and how
it affects the structural responses of the vessel. Understanding this phenomenon allows
for a more reliable estimation of the dynamic environment and enhances the accuracy of
the hydrodynamic analysis. To address this, the analysis was performed using NetSSE
(Mounet & Nielsen, 2025), a Python package that provides tools to account for the Doppler
shift induced by the vessel’s forward motion along its route. This effect is crucial for cor­
rectly interpreting the wave spectrum as observed from the moving vessel, enabling the
distinction between the absolute wave spectrum and the encounter spectrum experienced
onboard. By incorporating the Doppler shift, the methodology facilitates a direct compar­
ison between parameterized spectra and those derived from ERA5 data with the actual
measurements taken on board.
This work is mainly limited by the large number of uncertainty sources throughout the en­
tire modeling process of waves and ship responses. The information extracted from the
ERA5 database presents temporal and spatial deviations relative to the analyzed vessel,
as well as inherent uncertainty linked to the extracted data itself, from the spectrum esti­
mation process to the calculation of integral wave parameters. Moreover, the employed
transfer functions, as previously mentioned, are computed using a software that intro­
duces simplifications and assumptions, which generate uncertainty because they do not
fully represent the real ship­wave interaction. Additionally, measured ship responses con­
tain a degree of uncertainty, as factors related to motion sensors can cause measurement
errors. Finally, all modeling processes contribute some error, as grid adaptations, inter­
polations, and other correction procedures related to the modeling must be performed.
It is worth mentioning that the results obtained are inherently linked to the particular
dataframe used, subjected to spatial and temporal variability, and limited by the char­
acteristics of the analyzed vessel, including its geometry and loading conditions.

2
Theoretical background
Waves in the open ocean are highly irregular and by studying wave records, this irregu­
larity, both in space and time, is clearly observable. However, by analyzing time windows
of approximately half an hour, it is possible to observe that the sea tends to maintain a
characteristic appearance, allowing it to be considered statistically steady or stationary
(Lewis, 1989). Moreover, analysis on wave recordings have shown that wave records
tend to follow an approximately Gaussian or normal probability density function. This
statistical behavior enables the application of probability theory, statistical methods, and
Fourier analysis to study ocean waves, paving the way for developing models based on
these techniques.
Storm waves are generated by the interaction between wind and the sea surface. Friction
between air and water, as well as the local pressure differences caused by the airflow over
the wave surface, play a crucial role in wave generation. Several studies on wind­wave
interaction, such as (Korvin­Kroukovsky, 1967) or other ones summarized on (Nielsen,
2023) indicate that storm wave systems result from numerous independent and localized
interactions occurring across space and time. Each of these local events adds a small
disturbance to the existing wave field.
Under the the hydrodynamic theory of surface waves, when considering simple gravity
waves, it is assumed that wave crests are straight, infinitely long, parallel, and equally
spaced, and that wave heights remain constant (Airy, 1845). Waves propagate in a direc­
tion that is strictly perpendicular to the wave crests at a uniform speed, referred to as the
celerity, where it is important to mention that it is the wave form that advances, not the wa­
ter particles themselves. These waves are idealized as inviscid and incompressible, and
the pressure at the free surface is assumed to be constant and equal to the atmospheric
pressure. A two­dimensional reference frame is typically used, leading to the so­called
potential flow theory.
The motion of fluid particles under these idealized conditions can be described using a
scalar quantity known as the velocity potential, denoted by ϕ. This function is defined
such that its derivative in any direction yields the velocity component of the fluid in that
direction. The velocity potential satisfies Laplace’s equation due to the incompressibility
of the fluid and can be used to derive other important wave characteristics. By introducing
several simplifications, (Lewis, 1989) rewrites the expression for the velocity potential:

The energy in a train of regular waves consists of kinetic energy, associated with the orbital
motion of water particles, and potential energy, resulting from the variations in water level
at wave troughs and crests. This kinetic energy can be derived from the previously defined
expression for the velocity potential.
Over short periods of time, and neglecting wave breaking, one wave system can prop­
agate through another without significant alteration, meaning that the principle of super­
position is a fairly accurate approximation of reality (Goda, 1985). Using Fourier integral
techniques, any local wave system can be resolved into a sum of regular wave compo­
nents with different wavelengths and directions (Dean & Dalrymple, 1994). This short­
term descriptive model enables the use of mathematical tools to represent irregular seas
at a given time and location as a superposition of multiple wave components.
Figure 2.1: The principle of superposition: The sum of the component waves determine
the system of waves. Extracted from (Pierson et al., 1958)
So, it is possible to represent a sea state through the superposition of individual harmonic
waves with different frequencies and directions (Pierson et al., 1958). Assuming a Gaus­
sian distribution and a stationary state, statistical methods become useful for character­
izing the wave field. One key tool for this purpose is the wave energy density spectrum,
which represents how the energy (or surface elevation variance) is distributed across dif­
ferent frequencies.
In practice, wave spectra are derived from data series of recorded water surface mea­
surements, measured using buoys, laser sensors, or radar systems (Heathershaw et al.,
1979) and (Elachi & Brown, 1977). From these, Fourier Transformation based spectral
analysis is used to decompose the signals into their frequency components.
These energy density spectra are valuable because they condense complex datasets into
concise representations containing all the statistical information required to define a sea
state that are widely used in oceanography and maritime engineering. The shape of the
spectrum reveals a lot about the visible characteristics of wave systems. To quantify this,
spectral moments are defined as shown in Equation 2.2 (Holthuijsen, 2007), which de­
scribe not only the total energy but also how that energy is distributed across the frequency
range.

2.1
Wave spectrum: Idealized parametric wave spectra
As mentioned before, wave energy density spectra are very useful for describing sea
states, as they condense statistical information, such as the integral wave parameters,
that are frequently used in ocean engineering. When only wave parameters are available,
it is possible to use certain tools to model sea states with simple methodologies, allowing
the description of energy distribution as a function of frequency with a reduced number of
parameters, thus avoiding the need for extensive time series. Several idealized paramet­
ric spectra exist, such as Bretschneider (Bretschneider, 1952), Pierson­Moskowitz (Pier­
son & Moskowitz, 1964), JONSWAP (Hasselmann et al., 1973), Torsethaugen (Torsethau­
gen & Haver, 2004) or the Ochi 6­parameter model (Ochi & Hubble, 1976) among others.
However, these models are point spectra that define a unidirectional wave system, where
waves propagate along a single direction. This assumption corresponds to long­crested
waves, characterized by long, parallel wave fronts aligned in one direction. In reality, more
complex wave behavior is commonly observed, as real waves consist of multi­directional
wave fronts, producing a more complex and three­dimensional behavior known as short­
crested waves. These waves exhibit irregular and discontinuous crests due to the super­
position and interference of wave fronts with different frequencies and directions.
In the following subsections, the mathematical definitions of these idealized parametrized
wave spectra will be discussed from both long­crested and short­crested perspectives.

be mathematically described using previously defined point spectra in combination with

2.1.3
Multimodal wave spectra
A multimodal wave spectrum is generated by combining several individual spectra, each
representing a different component of the wave system, such as local wind seas and dis­
tant swell components. This approach allows for a more realistic and detailed description
of sea states, as multiple wave systems with distinct characteristics often coexist, espe­
cially in open seas, where different meteorological conditions overlap. Each individual
spectrum can be defined using the previously mentioned parametrized models, such as
Bretschneider or JONSWAP, adjusting the parameters to match the desired conditions.
This multimodal approach is based on the principle of linear superposition, whereby the
total energy of the spectrum is obtained by summing the individual contributions of each
wave system, each with its own energy, frequency, and direction. When all these com­
ponents are combined, a compound spectrum is formed, more accurately capturing the
complexity and variability of a real sea states.
2.2
Transfer functions
Transfer functions are mathematical tools used to describe how a system responds to a
given input. They establish a relationship between an input (such as a force or excitation)
and an output (such as displacement or acceleration) in the frequency domain.
The concept was first developed in electrical and communication engineering during the
20th century, where it was necessary to define how input signals in electric circuits were
transformed into output signals (Rice, 1949). The fundamental principle is that, for linear
and stationary systems, any input can be decomposed into a sum of sinusoidal signals
(using, for example, Fourier transforms). Since the response of such systems to a sinu­
soidal input is another sinusoid of the same frequency (but with different amplitude and
phase), the transfer function captures this relationship.
Years later, the concept was extended to other engineering fields such as structural me­
chanics and dynamics, as well as naval architecture and ocean engineering (St Dinis &
Pierson Jr, 1953). When ships are subjected to wave­induced forces, they experience

2.3
Response spectrum
As presented earlier, the fundamentals currently used in ship motion analysis are based
on work originally developed in electronics, electrical, and communication engineering.
These principles have been successfully extrapolated to ocean engineering through the
theory of linear superposition ().
This linearization in ocean wave studies can be mainly justified due to the relatively small
amplitude of ocean waves compared to their wavelength and the water depth (St Dinis &
Pierson Jr, 1953). Under these conditions:
• The Navier–Stokes equations for fluid motion can be linearized.
• Non­linear effects, such as wave­wave interactions, wave breaking, and strong ver­
tical accelerations, can be neglected.
This linear approach enables the application of linear superposition theory, treating the
ocean wave system as a sum of multiple sinusoidal components with different frequen­
cies, directions, and phases (Goda & Suzuki, 1975). This allows each component to be
analyzed individually and later recombined to obtain the overall wave field.
However, ocean engineering presents certain peculiarities not found in electronics or com­
munication fields:
• Ocean waves are functions of both space and time.
• Both the ship and the wave field are in motion, meaning there is a relative displace­
ment between them. The ship is typically considered to move with a relative velocity
with respect to the wave field. This leads to phenomenon called the Doppler effect
(Christian, 1842), which will be addressed later.

These peculiarities introduce additional complexity to the previously described simplified
wave theory. One practical and reasonable way to manage this is to model ocean waves
as a random, Gaussian, stationary, zero­mean process. From this perspective:
• Although the ocean surface elevation varies with time and space, its statistical struc­
ture (mean, variance, frequency spectrum) is assumed to be constant over short
periods of time and within a local spatial range.
• In a zero­mean Gaussian process, the wave field can be fully characterized by its
variance and spectral distribution, which describe how wave energy is distributed
over different frequencies and directions.
These wave spectra allow for a compact, practical, and efficient statistical description of
ocean wave fields and their effects on marine structures.
2.3.1
Ships at zero­forward speed
When ships are completely stationary (i.e., when the forward speed is zero), there is no
relative motion with respect to the wave field. This allows the wave field to be considered
stationary, which simplifies the application of the principle of linear superposition. As ex­
plained earlier, the response of a floating body can be computed using the RAO and input
data, in this case, wave­related data. Since this analysis is performed in terms of energy
(or variance) spectra rather than direct motion amplitudes, the relationship between the
wave spectrum and the response spectrum is given Equation 2.22 (Nielsen, 2023).
SR(ω) = |RAO(ω)|2 · Sη(ω)

This squared relationship arises because energy is treated as a signal property and its
spectral contribution is proportional to the square of its amplitude. Therefore, even though
the system response is linear, the energy of that response depends on the squared magni­

4.1
Time series into spectral density
As already mentioned in subsection 3.1.2, the measured ship responses are time­dependent
accelerations (heave, pitch, roll, surge, sway, and yaw). These responses were recorded
continuously with a recording interval of 0.2 seconds on each sample, and as previously
stated, each sample corresponds to a 25­minute interval (Nielsen et al., 2023).
Since the main objective is to analyze the responses in the frequency domain, it is neces­
sary to apply a mathematical transformation to obtain the Power Spectral Density (PSD)
of the recorded signals. Several concepts are important in this context:
• Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT). This is the fundamental mathematical approach
to transform a signal from the time domain into the frequency domain (Oppenheim
et al., 1999). A direct computation of the DFT has a computational complexity of the
order O(N2), which can become inefficient for long signals.
• Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). This is an algorithm to efficiently compute the DFT de­
veloped first by (Cooley & Tukey, 1965), with a computational complexity of O(N log N).
Due to its efficiency, it is widely used in digital signal processing, especially when
large volumes of data need to be processed.
• Welch’s Method. This is a technique developed on (Welch, 1967) for estimating the
PSD that reduces the variance of the estimate by dividing the signal into overlap­
ping segments, applying a window (a weighting function used to smooth extreme
values) to each segment, performing the Fourier transform on each, and finally av­
eraging the results. This method reduces spectral noise at the expense of frequency
resolution.
In this study, a FFT­based approach is adopted, similarly to (Nielsen et al., 2023), as com­
putational efficiency and direct applicability to long discrete signals are desired. To carry
out the transformation, the Python package scipy.signal is employed. This package
offers, among other signal processing tools, spectrum calculation optimization methods,
including the csd() function (SciPy Community, 2023), which is used to compute the
cross­spectral density by using the previously mentioned Welch’s Method.
To perform this transformation, several decisions were made:
• Sampling frequency: The previously mentioned recorded interval of 0.2 seconds is
used, corresponding to a sampling frequency of 5 Hz.

4.2.2
Cut­off frequency determination
When performing a spectral transformation from an absolute frequency domain fo to an
encounter frequency domain fe, the selection of an appropriate cut­off frequency is crucial
to ensure an accurate representation of the wave energy without unnecessarily increasing
computational cost. The cut­off frequency corresponds to the point beyond which the
energy density of the spectrum becomes negligible.
Due to the Doppler effect, previously addressed on subsection 2.3.2, frequencies in the
absolute and encounter domains may differ and must be adjusted based on the naviga­
tional parameters of the vessel, following what is known as the Doppler shift phenomenon.
Simplifying the relationship by assuming on the cut­off frequency that fe,max = f0,max can
lead to an underestimation of the response energy, particularly at high vessel speeds and
under beam to following sea conditions.

off f0,max = 0.5Hz. Ignoring this effect would result in discarding energy­carrying wave
components, leading to inaccurate spectral results.
In this analysis, the cut­off encounter frequency was estimated using the Doppler shift
relationship shown in Equation 2.23, which relates the absolute cut­off frequency, vessel
speed, and relative wave heading. This approach ensures that the computational effort
is optimized while maintaining the required spectral accuracy by preserving the energy
content within the transformed frequency range.
4.2.3
From wave spectra to response spectra: NetSSE
The estimation of sea state using data from moving ships has gained increasing relevance
in recent years, especially following the development of the concept of collaborative wave
sensors, introduced in works such as (Nielsen, 2018). This approach integrates several
observational means in a complementary manner, such that when heterogeneous plat­
forms are combined. Doing this, a potentially more accurate sea state estimation can be
obtained, as the uncertainty associated with each individual platform may be reduced.
In this context, the NetSSE project (Mounet & Nielsen, 2025) was developed as an initia­
tive aimed at creating engineering tools to compute reliable sea state estimations based
24
Sensitivity to wave spectrum shape in estimation of wave­induced responses of ocean structures
==PAGE:33==
on vessel data frameworks. This approach takes advantage of the increasing availability
of commercial ship sensors, which register several variables such as accelerations, ve­
locities, and structural deformations. Although this data is continuously generated on a
massive scale, its full potential is still not fully exploited. However, thanks to the ongoing
digitalization of the maritime sector, this information is increasingly leveraged for energy,
safety, and operational purposes.
NetSSE is developed in the widely known and used Python (Python Software Founda­
tion, 2023) environment, allowing users to experiment and develop high­quality sea state
estimation and prediction models. Here, model quality is understood as a measure of
precision, accuracy, reliability, and data availability.
In the present work, the NetSSE­developed tool resp_spec_2d was employed. This tool
constitutes the core of the workflow used to estimate ship responses induced by an initial
wave spectrum. It enables the estimation of a response spectrum from a bidimensional
wave spectrum and a set of transfer functions expressed on the same directional and
frequency grid, using the algorithm developed by (Nielsen et al., 2021).
One of the most relevant functionalities of this package within the NetSSE project is its
capability to perform the transformation from absolute to encounter spectra, particularly
important when analyzing moving ships affected by the Doppler shift. This algorithm pro­
vides an energy­consistent conversion, enabling physically realistic estimations of mo­
tions, accelerations and sectional forces (Mounet, 2023).
The algorithm works by going through a range of encounter frequencies and wave direc­
tions. For each combination, it determines the absolute frequency that corresponds to
the given encounter frequency, taking into account the relative angle between the wave
propagation direction and the ship’s heading. Depending on whether waves approach
from the bow, stern, or beam, the algorithm considers whether the mapping between en­
counter and absolute frequencies is one­to­one (1:1), where each encounter frequency
corresponds to a single absolute frequency, or one­to­three (1:3), where multiple absolute
frequencies can lead to the same encounter frequency.
In each case, it evaluates the wave energy spectrum at the transformed frequency, ap­
plies the appropriate Jacobian to conserve energy during the change of variables, and
accumulates that energy into the resulting spectrum. This yields a one­dimensional spec­
trum in the encounter domain, representing how the ship experiences the wave field in
motion, and it can be directly used to compute spectral responses such as motions or
loads.

As discussed in subsection 2.1.1, in order to construct parametrized wave spectra based

However, not all parameters can be reliably derived through such straightforward means.
For those cases where a clear analytical relationship is not available or where the pa­

adopted to identify and tune the most suitable input variables. This trial­and­error ap­

resulting spectra provide an accurate representation of the wave field under study.

which will be performed to evaluate how the selection of different parameters affects the

• Usage of the mean wave direction or the peak direction of incoming weaves. This

• Spreading parameter of the directional wave spectra. This analysis was performed

• Peakedness parameter of the parametrized JONSWAP spectrum.

concentrated around a single peak frequency and a dominant direction, defined by the
peak wave direction Dp. The directional spreading is governed by a spreading parameter
s = 1, which determines the energy distribution around the dominant direction.
This unimodal approach assumes that wave energy is focused and gradually dispersed
according to the chosen spreading function. For this particular dataset, the selected pa­
rameters provide a wave model that closely resembles the corresponding directional spec­
tra from the ERA5 reanalysis database.
It is important to note that this parametrization has been chosen specifically for the current
dataset, and the conclusions derived from this configuration may not be directly transfer­
able to other sea state conditions or databases.

6
Conclusions and future work
In this project, a thorough analysis of ship responses under several sea states in
the North Atlantic Ocean was undertaken, exploring various methodologies to charac­
terise the sea state. These included both the direct use of directional wave spectra ex­
tracted from the ERA5 database (Hersbach et al., 2023) and simplified models based
on parametrized spectra. Each method presents benefits and specific limitations, espe­
cially concerning the reliability of the sea representation and the required computational
resources. Therefore, both approaches are subject to several sources of uncertainty,
from the spatial resolution of the atmospheric­oceanic model to the transfer functions used
to estimate the dynamic responses of the ship (DNV, 2023), as well as the implications
and constraints of the simplifications applied in this study.
First, the analysis of predominant wave directions in the North Atlantic reveals a
strong influence from atmospheric patterns, particularly the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO),
which undergoes seasonal variations that affect the westerlies and, in turn, wave di­
rections. In winter, waves tend to come from the west­northwest, while in summer,
west­southwest components are more common. These patterns, consistent with ERA5
data and supported by studies such as (Goharnejad et al., 2022), are crucial for planning,
managing, and operating maritime routes, as understanding dominant wave directions
enhances the safety and efficiency of marine transport.
Secondly, ship response models were developed using directional wave spectra directly
obtained from the ERA5 database. This approach allows the inclusion of detailed informa­
tion about wave energy distribution across frequencies and directions, resulting in a more
realistic sea state representation compared to integral­parameter­based models, which
offer a more simplified approximation.
In general, using the full directional spectrum leads to more accurate results, as it
better captures the complexity of real ocean wave fields. However, this comes at the cost
of higher computational demands, requiring more memory and longer processing times.
Additionally, transfer functions introduce uncertainty, since they were computed using
DNV software, which, despite being technically reliable, involves simplifications that may
not fully reflect real vessel behavior.
The spatial resolution of the ERA5 data also represents a key source of uncertainty.
Due to the grid size, it is not possible to obtain precise information about the vessel’s exact
location, which introduces marginal errors in the wave conditions applied to the model.
This becomes particularly relevant in areas with steep spatial wave gradients or during
localized storm events that generate abnormal wave conditions.
Thirdly, several parametric models were used to estimate ship responses based on
idealized wave spectra, providing an alternative to the full directional spectrum. These
models are based on mathematical functions that represent the wave energy distribu­
tion through adjustable parameters.
One main limitation of these parametrized spectra is the need for an initial adjustment
phase to fit the parameters to the specific dataset used. In this study, a sensitivity anal­
ysis was conducted on several spectral parameters (e.g., using peak wave direction
instead of mean, directional spreading parameter, and the peakedness parameter of the
JONSWAP spectrum) to better match with the ERA5 spectra. Additionally, a comparative
Sensitivity to wave spectrum shape in estimation of wave­induced responses of ocean structures
47
==PAGE:56==
study was carried out to determine whether the JONSWAP or a simpler Bretschneider
spectrum better reflected the measured responses.
In fact, using such a simple model as Bretschneider, representing more developed
seas, yielded reasonably good results, particularly when sea states were clearly unidi­
rectional and unimodal in the frequency domain. Even in some cases with multimodal
characteristics, the unimodal approximation still demonstrated a fair ability to reproduce
ship responses.
However, to represent more complex scenarios characterized by coexisting wave com­
ponents from swell and wind, an additional bimodal parametric model was implemented.
This required more input information, increasing both the complexity and uncertainty
of the analysis. Nonetheless, this added complexity was reflected in improved model
performance. Still, under highly complex conditions, such as multimodal spectra with
multiple swell and wind systems, the models were not fully capable of accurately repro­
ducing the vessel’s responses.
There are still several aspects that require from further study. Firstly, a deeper ap­
proach complementary to the already performed analysis with the NIAE indicator should
be employed. This indicator gives an overall fitness measure of two input spectra. How­
ever, this fitness is comprised of some aspects: energy quantity and energy distribution
along the frequency, or both. That is why it would be very appropriate to develop a com­
plementary approach to look at these two aspects and quantify their individual fitness.
This indicators, together with the overall NIAE indicator, could give more information
about the reasons behind the performance of the models.
Secondly, quite simple models have been employed to represent directional wave spectra.
Employing more complex models such as the Torsethaugen (Torsethaugen & Haver,
2004) or the Ochi 6­parameter model (Ochi & Hubble, 1976) could be implemented, as
well as more complex spreading functions that could represent more accurately the
directional distribution of energy as a function of frequency.
Another possible interesting implementation would be to develop an even more paramet­
ric model by employing parametric transfer functions, such as the ones proposed in
(Jensen et al., 2004). Even if this approach would introduce more uncertainty into the
model it would be a useful implementation, making models adaptable in case of changes
in the ship’s geometrical conditions.
Finally, from an industry­implementation perspective, it would be relevant to develop
an adaptive hybrid approach to this problem. This strategy would consist of detecting
the complexity of the wave spectra, and in case of having a dispersed or multimodal
sea state, the whole directional spectra from ERA5 would be used. In simpler situations,
with unimodal or bimodal wave spectra, parametric models could be employed, reducing
computational requirements without losing precision in the estimation.
In summary, this study has shown the advantages and limitations of using both full direc­
tional spectra and simplified parametric models to estimate ship responses. While each
approach involves different levels of complexity and uncertainty, both can be valu­
able depending on the sea state and available resources. However, several aspects
of the methodology could be improved to enhance accuracy, which opens the door to
future developments and refinements.

parametrized models apparently perform better than models using the whole directional
spectra extracted from the ERA5 database, which at first sight does not make sense, as
parametrized wave models are simplifications of the ERA5 ones. In the following sections,
several cases will be studied to look for the reason of this phenomenon.

section B.1. A subset of these cases is analyzed individually to uncover anything that
