Abstract 
Hydraulic models are a commonly used tool to assess flood hazard, which is a key component in 
determining flood risk. In Denmark, these 2D hydraulic models are typically driven by high-reso-
lution, high-quality data, e.g., bare-earth elevation data from a high-resolution LiDAR-based DEM, 
design storms based on SVKs expansive rain gauge network etc. With increasing urbanization in 
developing countries comes an increasing demand for pluvial flood modelling, but flood model-
ling progress in some parts of the world suffers from data scarcity for various reasons. 
In this thesis, several free, global datasets were reviewed, and a smaller selection of these datasets 
were analysed further, to determine their viability in urban pluvial flood modelling. The focus 
was primarily on 3 data types: digital elevation models, precipitation data, and imperviousness. 
Each individual dataset was compared to a high-quality, high-resolution counterpart through a 
series of comparative analyses in a case study area in Odense. Finally, a selection of datasets were 
used in a simple Mike 21 flood model setup. 
Two global, satellite-based DEMs in 30x30 meter resolution, NASADEM and Copernicus’ GLO30 
DEM, were compared to Danmarks Højdemodel, a LiDAR-based DTM from Styrelsen for Data-
forsyning og Effektivisering (SDFE). Elevations in GLO30 were found to be closer to the SDFE 
DEM’s elevations compared to NASADEM. However, a higher correlation was found between ma-
jor flow paths in NASADEM and the SDFE DEM compared to GLO30. A blue spot analysis showed 
that both global DEMs had 3-4 times larger total sink volume within the project area likely be-
cause of the coarse resolution and surface noise from buildings and treetops/canopy.  
Two global precipitation datasets, ERA5 and MSWEP, were analysed and compared to historical 
data from the Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) and Spildevandskomiteen (SVK). Cumulative 
rainfall in the global datasets were both lower than data from DMI. Extreme Value Analysis of the 
datasets was used to create IDF curves, however, the resulting IDF curves were limited by the 
temporal resolutions of the datasets. Multiple Linear Regression was used to create IDF curves 
that cover a range of intensities for duration from 10-2880 minutes. MSWEP IDF curves were 
closest in comparison to SVK equivalents and were used to create Chicago Design Storms. 
A global impervious surface map in 30x30 meter resolution was analysed and compared to high-
resolution imperviousness data from SCALGO. Comparing the imperviousness after aggregating 
to 210x210 meter resolution showed higher discrepancies between the datasets in urban areas 
than non-urban areas. Additionally, a tendency was found in the global dataset to overestimate 
imperviousness in urban areas by ~20% for the case study area in Odense. 
Finally, simple Mike 21 flood model setups were used to test the GLO30 DEM, MSWEP rain data, 
and the global imperviousness dataset with mixed results. The GLO30 DEM, without modifica-
tions, produced hazard maps incomparable to results from the base model. Modelling with 
MSWEP derived CDS rains resulted in severe overestimation of flood extents. Modelling with the 
global imperviousness dataset resulted in flood extents very similar to the base scenario after 
reducing imperviousness of urban areas by 20%.  
Based on the above findings, GLO30 was found not viable for pluvial flood modelling at the scale 
used in this project. MSWEP was successfully used to create IDF curves and CDS rains in this pro-
ject but with a simplified approach. With adjustments, the global imperviousness dataset was 
found viable for the simple flood modelling used in this project, although the tendency to overes-
timate imperviousness in urban areas should be explored further.

1 Introduction 
Flooding from extreme rain events is a large societal problem and will continue to be a problem 
in the future, as future climate projections show an increased frequency and potency of extreme 
weather events (IPCC, 2012).  
In 2012, the IPCC published the SREX – the Special Report on managing of the Risks of Extreme 
Events and Disasters to Advance Climate Change Adaptation. Figure 1-1 is an illustration of the key 
concepts of the report, one of which is how disaster risk is determined by the exposure and vul-
nerability to extreme weather and climate events. In essence, flood risk is determined by 3 driving 
factors: hazard, vulnerability, and exposure.  
 
Figure 1-1. From IPCC's SREX (Special Report on Managing of the Risks of Extreme Events and Disasters to Advance Climate 
Change Adaptation). The illustration shows key concepts of the report (IPCC, 2012). 
In scientific circles, a widely accepted definition of risk is that it is a function of exposure, hazard, 
and vulnerability (Kron, 2005). For this project, it is important to define exactly what this means 
in a flood modelling context. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) uses the 
following definitions in the SREX, below paraphrased to a flood context (IPCC, 2012): 
• 
Exposure is the presence of people, resources, infrastructure, livelihoods etc. affected by 
a climate extreme. 
• 
Hazard is the climate extreme/event, in this case pluvial floods. 
• 
Vulnerability is how susceptible an area or community is to being affected by a climate 
extreme. 
The above-mentioned three factors are what constitute the overall risk, i.e., risk is the product of 
exposure, hazard, and vulnerability. More specifically, the overall flood risk can be seen as the 
economic costs that result from a given area/community and its exposure and vulnerability to a 
flood (hazard). 
In Denmark, flood risk is managed on a municipal level. In 2017, The Water Pollution Committee 
of The Society of Danish Engineers released guidelines on how to determine service levels for 
water on terrain (Spildevandskomiteen, 2017). The municipality determines service levels for 
water on terrain based on water depths and frequency, either on a total municipal level or locally. 
A typical approach to determine this service level is through hydraulic modelling, where 2D flood 
models are used to map the potential flood hazards. The risk is determined by calculating EAD 
(expected annual damage) to assets and the service level is thus a socio-economic balance.

Key input data for these hydraulic models are bare-earth high-resolution elevation models, de-
sign storms, imperviousness, etc. – all data types that are readily available in Denmark. 
Data scarcity is a problem in developing parts of the world, where high-resolution DEMs and his-
torical rain data may be unavailable for various reasons (Ekeu-wei & Blackburn, 2018). With in-
creasing urbanisation comes an interest in urban flood modelling and risk management, which is 
why an increasing availability of openly available global datasets may be of interest in data scarce 
regions (Courty et al., 2019; Ekeu-wei & Blackburn, 2018; Lindersson et al., 2020; Q. Sun et al., 
2018). 
The purpose of this thesis is to evaluate global datasets and their applicability for pluvial urban 
flood modelling specifically. This thesis focuses mainly on the hazard and vulnerability aspects of 
pluvial flood modelling by assessing the performance of global and free elevation, rainfall and 
imperviousness datasets in a flood modelling context. The focus is therefore on the usability of 
the data for this specific purpose, hence the absence of exposure in Figure 1-1.  
Various analyses with relevance to flood modelling will be used to assess how well the open data 
performs in comparison to high-resolution data from a well-known case study area in Odense, 
Denmark. The analyses were performed to shed light on the following objectives: 
• 
How well can urban areas be represented using satellite-based elevation data? 
• 
At which scale does the difference between global DEMs and high-resolution DEMs be-
come irrelevant? 
• 
Can global imperviousness data be used to represent the actual imperviousness of the 
project area? 
• 
How much do design storms created from global data deviate from design storms created 
from high-resolution data? 
• 
How do modelled flood extents and depths compare when using: 
o High-resolution LiDAR and global satellite-based elevation data?  
o SVK CDS rains and CDS rains derived from global rainfall data? 
o High-resolution imperviousness data from SCALGO and global imperviousness 
data?

2 Theory 
This section covers theory relevant to the thesis project with a focus on flood risk in general, flood 
modelling approaches and uncertainties, and flood model complexity/detail. 
2.1 
Assessment of flood hazards 
There are various approaches to determining flood risk, where most approaches require some 
form of computer modelling for hazard mapping. Model complexity can vary depending on what 
the objective is and the quality of the available data. 
With an increase in model complexity comes the burden of requiring more and higher quality 
data. Table 2-1 is an excerpt from Henonin et al. (2013) that shows a number of modelling ap-
proaches with increasing complexity. This complexity comes both in the form of the data require-
ment as well as the model results (Henonin et al., 2013). 
Table 2-1. Different modelling approaches with increasing complexity from left to right. From (Henonin et al., 2013).  
 
All the model types mentioned above can be highly data dependent and the results are only as 
good as the quality of the input data. The individual models can of course vary in complexity, but 
flood models usually require high-resolution elevation data, e.g., LiDAR-based DEMs, measured 
catchment parameters relating to infiltration, imperviousness etc., and a large historical rain data 
record. The best models are calibrated and verified with measured data.  
The simplest 2D flood models need three primary components: elevation data, rainfall, and im-
perviousness data. Replacing these components with global dataset counterparts adds additional 
uncertainty to the model results: 
• 
Most global elevation data are surface models with generally higher elevations and lower 
accuracy, especially in forested areas (Uuemaa et al., 2020). Lower accuracy as well as 
elevation differences may affect flow paths and volumes in the model. 
• 
With regards to rainfall input, a long time series is preferable. Although in a fluvial con-
text, Apel et al. (2004) suggests that a short time series, e.g., 35 years, adds a lot of uncer-
tainty to the risk assessment due to the potential absence of less frequent events (Apel et 
al., 2004).  
• 
Impervious surfaces are key input data for the generation of runoff in hydrological models 
(Butler & Davies, 2018). The overall imperviousness and local imperviousness will affect 
flood results by either over- or underestimating floods depending on the quality of the 
data.

To summarize, there is uncertainty associated even with the most complex models. The quality 
of the input data heavily affects model uncertainty and accuracy, which should be kept in mind 
when choosing the global datasets for further analysis and modelling.  
Furthermore, although there are uncertainties associated with the hazard mapping and vulnera-
bilities, damage calculations add an additional uncertainty to the results. A study by Zhou & Arn-
bjerg-Nielsen (2018) for a case study area in Odense showed that the uncertainty stemming from 
hazard is overshadowed by uncertainties related to the calculation of adaptation costs etc., after 
committing to a decision of action (Zhou & Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2018). 
2.2 
Extreme Value Analysis and Chicago Design Storms 
Extreme Value Analysis, also referred to as frequency analysis, is a commonly used tool within 
hydrology to assess the probability of occurrence of future extreme weather events (World 
Meteorological Organisation (WMO), 2009).  
WMO defines a series as a “convenient sequence of data, such as hourly, daily, seasonal or annual 
observations of a hydrological variable.” There are primarily two types of series used in frequency 
analysis: annual maximum series (AMS) and partial duration series (PDS). 
An annual maximum series (AMS) is a series where the event with the highest magnitude is ex-
tracted for each year in a given series. The method is useful for datasets already in an annual 
format but has the disadvantage of only including the maximum event of each year. The magni-
tude of an event extracted from one year may fall below the magnitude of the 2nd highest event of 
a different year, thus leaving potentially extreme events out of the series (World Meteorological 
Organisation (WMO), 2009). 
A partial duration series (PDS) is a series divided into events based on the exceedance of one or 
more pre-determined threshold values. An event definition is used to divide the series into events 
– in this case, for extreme rainfall, the event definition is the exceedance of a certain rain rate for 
a certain amount of time. A sharp event definition is needed as to properly divide the series into 
events to avoid potentially chaining events together. 
IDF curves (Intensity-Duration-Frequency) can be derived from a partial duration series by rank-
ing rainfall depth across several durations in a descending order (Chow et al., 1988). Return pe-
riods are calculated by dividing the data period of the series in years with the rank of the event. 
The curves are aptly named as a single curve represents an expected average intensity over a 
number of durations for a given return period.  
Design storms are storm events specifically designed for dimensioning sewer systems (Chow et 
al., 1988). Design storms are typically derived from IDF relationships. An example is the Chicago 
Design Storm (CDS), which are synthetic rain storms first introduced by (Keifer & Chu, 1957). The 
special properties of a CDS rain result in a single, synthetic storm event that, for a given return 
period, covers all possible durations within the total duration of the storm.  
CDS rains are commonly used for dimensioning purposes as input in dynamic models. Specifically 
in Denmark, CDS rains serve as input and design criteria for an intermediate calculation level 
when used for dimensioning. When dimensioning more complex sewer systems, historical rain 
series are preferred (Spildevandskomiteen, 2005).

3 Review of open data 
The following subsections serve as an overview of various global data sets that are available for 
free, with a primary focus on elevation models and rain data. Not all data is equal as the various 
data sets may have different coverage, resolutions, accuracy etc. The datasets will be assessed 
from a perspective where usage in flood modelling is the end goal. 
Elevation datasets were found using search engines and by cross referencing which datasets were 
used in previous articles and projects such as those in the following section 3.3. Additionally, 
(Ekeu-wei & Blackburn, 2018) was used for an initial overview of available global DEMs as well 
as projects in which they were used.  
Initial sources of rain data were found in (Lindersson et al., 2020), which is a collection of global 
datasets specifically for the study of floods and droughts. Assessments of additional global rainfall 
datasets can be found in (Q. Sun et al., 2018).

making elevation models with coarse vertical resolutions less desirable. Out of the 30x30-meter 
DEMs, only 2 products have float-based vertical resolutions (NASADEM, COP-DEM).  
NASADEM is a refined version of the original SRTM data using modern processing tools, aiming 
to provide a better product with less voids than the SRTM DEM. There is very limited information 
available on NASADEMs float-based elevation product (Buckley et al., 2020). Elevations are ref-
erenced to a different datum compared to the integer version and files are provided in .hgts-for-
mat, thus requiring additional processing before use and comparison (see appendix H, section 
“NASADEM” for more details on this subject).  
Copernicus DEMs contains 3 different products: GLO90, GLO30, and EEA10. Copernicus DEMs are 
developed from the same data as WorldDEM, the TanDEM-X mission (Airbus Defence and Space, 
2020). The first two products are global and in 90- and 30-meter resolutions, respectively. Alt-
hough it is available 10x10-meter resolution as well in the EEA10 product, this version only co-
vers the EEA-39 area, i.e., EEA members and collaborators. As such, the 30x30-meter version is 
of high interest as it has global coverage. 
The ASTER DEM has received criticism for its elevation voids and lower accuracy compared to its 
counterparts (Ekeu-wei & Blackburn, 2018), especially in areas with high forest covers (Courty 
et al., 2019). 
WorldDEM is a commercial alternative to the free global datasets available in up to 5x5 meter 
resolution. An accuracy assessment of the 12x12 meter version, using the Runway Method, shows 
a higher vertical accuracy than ALOS World 3D (Becek et al., 2016), a terrain model known for its 
low uncertainty and high accuracy when compared to similar products (Uuemaa et al., 2020). 
Because of their superior resolution, coverage, and availability, Copernicus’ GLO-30 DEM and 
NASADEMs integer-based DEM were chosen for further analysis. For more technical details on 
Copernicus DEM and NASADEM products, please see appendix H.

3.4 
Overview: Similar projects using open data 
The SRTM digital elevation model is by far the most commonly used DEM for flood modelling 
purposes.  
In a study by Patro et al. (2009), the 90x90 meter version of SRTM was used to create refined 
cross sections of the Mahanadi River basin for use in hydrodynamic modelling (Patro et al., 2009). 
In a similar fashion, and on a similar scale, SRTM was used by Wang et al. (2012) to model glacial 
lake outburst floods in Tibet (Wang et al., 2012). Lewis et al. (2013) used SRTM upscaled to 
900x900 meter resolution to create a storm surge inundation model for northern part of the Bay 
of Bengal (Lewis et al., 2013). 
Another DEM, ASTER, was evaluated in a number of studies (Jarihani et al., 2015; Tarekegn et al., 
2010; Wang et al., 2012). Jarihani et al. (2015) used ASTER and SRTM to model a stretch of the 
Thompson River in Australia. The study by Tarekegn et al. (2010) shows ASTER as a viable ele-
vation source but only after reconstructing large parts of the Ribb River in the DEM. 
Courty et al. (2019) explore the potential of another global DEM, ALOS World 3D, by comparing 
flood modelling results to similar models using SRTM and ASTER elevation data (Courty et al., 
2019). In all aspects, AW3D seems to perform better than SRTM and ASTER, with ASTER being 
the worst performing of the 3 DEMs.  
Yan et al. (2015) explore the potential of low-cost data, i.e., global, openly available datasets is 
explored (Yan et al., 2015). In this study, the discussion also revolves around modelling of large-
scale flood plains in a fluvial context. A key point of the study is that the modelling of low-fre-
quency events tends to cover some of the lesser inconsistencies in DEMs, thus arguing that DEM 
accuracy potentially matters less with more extreme events. Additionally, the authors of the study 
argue that the global satellite-based DEMs are useful for what they are, but that it is up to the 
individual modeller to assess if the DEMs can produce flood model results that are useful in the 
given context.  
What the beforementioned studies all have in common is that the models are of fluvial flooding, 
i.e., when a water body such as a river overflows onto the surrounding land. The modelled fea-
tures such as lakes and rivers are large and easier to identify in DEMs with coarse resolutions. 
Additionally, the modelling scale in the above studies are vastly different to what one would ex-
pect from a pluvial flood model.  
Finding projects where open data was used for pluvial flooding, i.e., floods caused by rainfall in-
dependently from overflowing water bodies such as rivers, has been a much bigger challenge.

4.1.1 Denoise algorithm 
Since COP-Dem and NASADEM are digital surface models, elevations include objects such as trees 
and buildings. In an attempt to smoothen elevations in green areas to reduce the number of false 
sinks reported in the blue spot analysis and produce flow paths closer to the SDFE DEM, a denoise 
algorithm was applied to one of the global DEMs. 
Current algorithms exist that are able to turn digital surface models into bare-earth digital terrain 
models (Pijl et al., 2020; Vosselman, 2000), although these algorithms are primarily used for pro-
cessing high-resolution LiDAR data. The fine elevation details needed for these tools to filter ob-
jects are not present in the lower resolution, openly available, global DEMs such as COP-DEM and 
NASADEM. 
==PAGE:18==

A mesh denoising algorithm was tested on COP-DEM that works by iteratively checking the ori-
entation of each face’s normal to its neighbours’ as illustrated in Figure 4-3  (X. Sun et al., 2007). 
A face is the “cell” between 4 vertices, i.e., the fictive cell that depicts the slope between 4 terrain 
points in the DEM. 
If the angle between two face normals exceeds a pre-determined, user-chosen threshold value, T 
(corresponding to the cosine of the max. allowed angle between the normals), the vertices remain 
unadjusted. If the angle is below the threshold value, vertices are adjusted to smoothen the sur-
face of the DEM (Stevenson et al., 2010). 
 
Figure 4-3. An illustration showing how Sun's mesh denoising algorithm works (Stevenson et al., 2010). The red vertices 
are adjusted as the angle between the neighbouring faces’ normals is below the threshold value. The white vertices remain 
in place as the angle exceeds the threshold value, allowing the algorithm to preserve sharp features such as ridges. 
The threshold value is therefore essential to the denoising results. The study by Stevenson et al. 
(2010) shows that the most optimal threshold settings depend on the topography but can typi-
cally be found in the range T = 0.87-0.99, while number of iterations should be in the  
range n = 1-10. For COP-DEM, the threshold value T was set to 0.90 and number of iterations was 
set to 10. 
4.1.2 Blue spot analysis 
A blue spot analysis was completed on both the chosen global DEMs and the LiDAR DEM for com-
parison. Blue spots (or sinks) are indentations in the terrain where water may collect during rain-
fall.  
The blue spots will be filtered based on the following criteria: 
- 
They must be larger than 1,500 m2 and deeper than 1 m to filter sinks possibly stemming 
from noise. 
- 
To analyse sinks within urban areas, only sinks within an urban mask are considered. 
Edge cases, i.e., sinks that fall both inside and outside of the urban filter, must have at least 
50% overlap with the urban filter. 
Blue spot volumes, locations, and areas were calculated and compared between global DEMs and 
the LiDAR DEM. The aforementioned variables as well as overlap percentage between sinks in 
each global DEM compared to the LiDAR DEM will be used to assess how well blue spots are rep-
resented in the global DEMs. 
The mask was created from imperviousness raster data and watersheds vector data and applied

4.1.3 Flow path analysis 
A flow path analysis was completed using a version of each DEM that has its indentations filled. 
The D8 flow modelling algorithm was used to determine the flow direction for each cell in the 
raster after which the flow accumulation tool from ArcMap’s Spatial Analyst toolbox was used to 
determine flow paths.

much upstream area that flows into any given point in the terrain model. This will be used to 
compare each of the DEMs to the SDFE Odense DTM by analysing how each flow path compares 
with the flow paths in the SDFE DTM.

4.4.1 Elevation data 
The hydrologically corrected SDFE DEM is used as input elevation data for the base Mike 21 
model. A hydrologically corrected DEM ensures more realistic flow paths and serves as the best 
possible point of comparison for the flood model scenarios tested in this project. 
The global DEM used in model 4, originally in 30x30 meter resolution, was downscaled to 5x5 
meter grid spacing without interpolation. This means that a 30x30 cell with a single elevation 
value in the original DEM now consists of 6x6 cells with 5-meter grid spacing, all with the same 
original elevation value of the 30x30 version of the DEM. Boundaries in the model are closed 
which means that no water flows out of the model. 
4.4.2 Infiltration parameters and maps 
Imperviousness data will be used to create the infiltration maps for the modelling setup. Infiltra-
tion maps are gridded data where cell values are infiltration rates. These infiltration rates depend 
on the degree of imperviousness, where impervious cells will have lower infiltration rates and 
pervious cells will have higher infiltration rates.  
The infiltration rate in impervious cells reflects the capacity of the sewer system. Danish design 
standards state that combined sewer systems must be full running no more than once every 2 
years (Spildevandskomiteen, 2005). A simple approach is used to calculate the sewer system ca-
pacity using the SVK spreadsheet and an assumed rain duration equal to the time of concentration 
for the catchment of 30 minutes. The constant rain intensity for a 30-minute 2-year rain event in 
Odense is ~7 μm/s. Assuming that the Odense sewer system is perfectly capable for such an event 
results in a 25.2 mm/h infiltration rate. 
Infiltration rates in pervious parts of the project area will depend on soil parameters. Like most 
of Denmark, the dominating soil type in the project area is moraine clay, which is a clay, sand, 
gravel, and rock mixture that is not entirely impermeable despite the clay contents. A homoge-
nous infiltration rate for compacted sandy soil of 45 mm/h is assumed, based on Horton final 
infiltration capacity reported in (Pitt & Lantrip, 2000).

6.1 
Raster-based comparison between SDFE LiDAR DEM and satellite-based DEMs 
COP-DEM and NASADEM are both digital surface models (DSM), meaning that the recorded ele-
vations include structures and vegetation such as buildings and treetops. This is detrimental to 
flood modelling, where bare-earth elevations are preferred to properly depict flow paths and ter-
rain indentations. Danmarks Højdemodel (distributed by SDFE) is a digital terrain model (DTM) 
with bare-earth elevations. Elevations will therefore differ between COP-DEM/NASADEM and the 
SDFE DEM.  
To show these differences, each global DEM was subtracted from a 30x30 m aggregated version 
of the SDFE DEM. The DEMs were reprojected and aligned with the SDFE DEM and a 3x3 neigh-
bourhood moving average was performed on the elevation difference to eliminate noise. Results 
can be seen in Figure 6-1.

frequency of the datasets and to be aware of possibly incorrect data due to increasing/on-going

6.3.2 Chicago Design storms (CDS) 
Figure 6-16 shows a comparison between the MSWEP T10.5 and T42 CDS rain and equivalent 
return period CDS rains generated using the SVK spreadsheet. It is important to note that the IDF 
curves used to generate the CDS rains pictured below are vastly different and that the MSWEP 
data has not gone through the same statistical treatment as the SVK data. 
The resulting CDS rains have very high peak intensities and high fall-off as distance (time) from 
the peak increases. This is a result of the regression used to derive the alpha, theta, and nu-values 
that are needed to calculate the stepwise intensities of the design storms. The theta-value takes 
into account the characteristic bend in IDF curves that results in lower intensities for shorter du-
rations (Spildevandskomiteen, 1999). Since the MSWEP IDF curves do not bend at lower dura-
tions, the resulting theta-value is near zero, which results in a very high peak intensity.

7 Discussion 
This section serves mainly as a direct discussion of the results from the previous section. Addi-
tionally, limitations of the analyses and the project are discussed, as well as future prospects in 
the form of improvements and further analyses that could be completed to explore the potential 
of open data. 
7.1 
Global DEMs in 2D flood models 
The results of the flow path analysis showed an overall agreement between major flow paths in 
the satellite-based DEMs and the SDFE LiDAR DEM with a few exceptions. Part of the reason is 
the fact that larger features such as streams are easily identifiable on all of the DEMs despite the 
resolution. Analysing upstream area in the analysed DEMs by using SDFE sinks as a common 
buffer area confirmed this to some extent, where correlation was found between upstream area 
in the satellite-based DEMs and the LiDAR DEM. The analyses showed that NASADEMs flow paths 
were more similar to flow paths in the LiDAR DEM, whereas COP-DEM had slight discrepancies 
in the major flow paths. 
However, the fact that NASADEM is integer based and its vertical resolution is in whole meters is 
a disadvantage. The blue spot analysis revealed far fewer sinks in NASADEM compared to COP-
DEM. However, both DEMs had an overall much larger total sink volume 3-4 times higher than 
the volume in the LiDAR DEM. The denoising revealed that a large portion of the sink volume in 
COP-DEM (20%), at least, could have been false positives stemming from noise. The sink volume 
is impactful on the flood modelling results as rainwater in the dynamic model will be retained in 
these larger volumes, limiting both the overall rainwater travel and severely limiting the accuracy 
of flood extents and depths.  
To summarize, the global DEMs were useful for overall flow path mapping. However, the noise 
from buildings and canopy results in poor flood model results. Removing noise from a DEM of 
such coarse resolution is difficult as the origin of the noise can be hard to determine. Although 
major flow paths were similar between datasets, small scale water routing and flow paths on ter-
rain appear to be problematic in the flood models, with very few local flood similarities between 
the COP-DEM model and the SDFE model.  
Until free global DEMs are offered in a higher resolution and perhaps even as bare-earth models, 
modellers could consider using the WorldDEM product as a commercial alternative. Airbus re-
cently released WorldDEM NEO, a 5x5 meter global DEM with very high accuracy (Airbus Defence 
and Space, 2021). The product is available both in DSM and DTM versions. 
7.2 
Global rainfall data in 2D flood models 
Both MSWEP and ERA5 were successfully used to create IDF curves. There are a few differences 
between the produced IDF curves, however, with clear differences in scale and shape. It should 
also be underlined that the precipitation data used to generate MSWEP and SVK IDF curves have 
undergone very different statistical treatments, and that the comparisons in this project should 
be treated with that in mind.  
As mentioned in a previous section, traditional IDF curves have a characteristic shape with a bend 
in the curve at low durations. Creating such a curve shape from the global datasets is simply not 
possible mainly as a result of the low temporal resolution. However, the overall shape of the ERA5 
IDF curves were closer to the traditional shape than MSWEP, with a curve bend that occurs at 
~200 minutes the IDF curves from raw data. The scale of the MSWEP dataset was closer to DMI’s 
==PAGE:51==
 
Exploring the potential of open data in flood models 
23.01.2022 
 
Page 45 of 79 
historical data and the regression resulted in curves with a level slightly lower than the regional 
IDF curves based on historical data from SVK rain gauges, at least for durations above 10 minutes. 
The IDF curves made it possible to create Chicago Design Storms. One problem with the method 
used in this project stems from the multiple linear regression and the resulting low intensity data 
points. The absence of more realistic low intensity data points results in high peak intensities in 
the CDS rains after parametrisation. Introducing a set of artificial lower-duration intensities to 
the IDF curves could solve this problem but was outside the scope of this project, and the concept 
was not explored beyond this initial thought. 
Applying the MSWEP CDS rains in the flood model resulted in overestimated flood extents, 
depths, and volumes. This was also expected as the derived design storms had very high peak 
intensities. The flood model setup uses flat infiltration rates on a local cell basis, which means that 
the model only generates runoff if the precipitation in the cell exceeds the infiltration rate. As the 
SVK CDS rains generally have more balanced levels across individual time steps, the total gener-
ated runoff is lower. Although fewer time steps of the MSWEP CDS rain exceed the threshold in-
filtration value, the peak is high enough to result in a higher runoff generation.  
7.3 
Global imperviousness data in 2D flood models 
The analyses of the global imperviousness dataset showed promising results in terms of repre-
senting both urban and non-urban areas. Despite the differences between the global dataset and 
SCALGO especially in urban areas, the modelled flood extents were very similar when comparing 
the base flood model and the global imperviousness flood model.  
In this case study for Odense, the global imperviousness map tends to overestimate impervious-
ness. The analysis of urban areas in this project revealed an approximate overestimation of 20% 
compared to high-resolution data from SCALGO. However, in data scarce countries there is no 
high-quality imperviousness dataset to compare to. The amount of adjustment needed could per-
haps be reasonably guessed by comparison to satellite photos. Alternatively, the global dataset’s 
tendency to overestimate imperviousness should be evaluated further by completing compara-
tive analyses similar to the one completed in this project on different case study areas.  
7.4 
Limitations of the data, further analyses, etc. 
Rain data 
Ideally, the initial analysis of the rain data should have been more thorough. Additional neigh-
bouring grid points could be used to check for data anomalies such as differences in scale/cumu-
lative rainfall. Comparison across multiple grid points would have made the analysis more robust, 
thus potentially strengthening the final choice of global rain data set for use in the flood model. 
Additionally, the overall analysis of the global rain data would have benefited from a comparison 
with known historical events. 
IDF curves created from the ERA5 dataset showed potential in terms of shape but were problem-
atic in terms of scale. Additional analyses could discover the potential of scaling the dataset. 
Digital Elevation Models 
Various other tools are available to assist with denoising and removing treetops/buildings from 
digital surface models. Further exploration of and experimentation with these tools could lead to 
a terrain model far more suitable for flood modelling. Denoising followed by more aggressive 
tools/algorithms designed to remove objects such as trees and buildings could perhaps improve 
the major flow path discrepancy in COP-DEM as described in section 6.1.3. However, the noise 
==PAGE:52==
 
Exploring the potential of open data in flood models 
23.01.2022 
 
Page 46 of 79 
and coarse resolutions in the global datasets may simply not make it possible to obtain close to 
true elevations no matter how the data is treated/modified. 
Raster alignment 
Most of the raster-based analyses in this project are based on a direct comparison of one or more 
DEMs. With raster data from different sources and different projections and datums, the individ-
ual cells may not line up. The default projections of most of the global datasets result in uneven 
grid spacing at northern and southern latitudes, differing between datasets. All datasets were 
reprojected to ETRS 1989 UTM Zone 32N which is the primary projection for Denmark, with even 
30-meter grid spacing. During the reprojection process, the datasets were aligned with and 
clipped to the extents of the original SDFE LiDAR DEM for perfect cell alignment.  
The above-mentioned method allows for cell-by-cell comparisons and has the added benefit of 
easing the modelling process, as the model results will be aligned and easier to compare. How-
ever, the alignment and reprojection process essentially modifies the original data by warping 
elevations and grid placements to fit the SDFE DEM. As such, the method is expected to add un-
certainty to results derived from the following analyses.  
Urban mask 
Initial attempts at creating an urban mask used Copernicus’ global land-cover map (Buchhorn et 
al., 2020), which contains land cover classification in 100x100 meter resolution. The coarse res-
olution did not properly filter large waterways which would have affected the urban blue spot 
analysis negatively. Instead, an urban mask was developed from imperviousness data from an 
earlier remote sensing project. The resulting mask has missing urban areas due to the slightly 
dated imperviousness data – in hindsight, it would have made sense to develop and use an urban 
mask based on SCALGOs high-resolution imperviousness data. The urban mask had been used for 
a number of analyses relatively late into the project at the point where it was decided to use 
SCALGO’s imperviousness data as a baseline for comparison with the global dataset. 
Flood model complexity and uncertainty 
It is imperative to point out that the Mike 21 flood model setup used in this project is of a relatively 
simple nature.  As mentioned in section 2.1, far more complicated models exist. However, there 
is a discussion to be had about the quality of the input data and the expected output of a model. 
The specific use case is worth to keep in mind – since this project used a simple comparison of 
flood extents and overall usability of the global datasets, a simple model setup will probably suf-
fice. On the contrary, if the aim of the project was to calculate EAD and to map inundated build-
ings, a higher level of detail would be beneficial.  
Although not directly quantified in this project, using global datasets is expected to add to the 
overall uncertainty of the flood model results. However, the increased uncertainty from hazard 
and vulnerability that may come from using global datasets could potentially be much less im-
portant if the hazard maps were used for socioeconomic decision making (Zhou & Arnbjerg-
Nielsen, 2018).

8 Conclusion 
The research and analyses of this project leads to the following main conclusions: 
• 
The flow path analysis showed that there was correlation between flow paths in the global 
DEMs and the SDFE LiDAR DEM. Urban areas are represented in the global DEMs, but 
surface elevation models of urban areas are limited by the presence of buildings and other 
unknown objects that affect the recorded elevations. 
• 
Large flow paths with 1000 ha upstream area were found to be very similar between the 
datasets, where only little difference was found between datasets at the larger scale. How-
ever, the flood modelling results show large differences in the small scale, as overall flow 
routing leads to Odense fjord, but local flow routing and flood extents, when comparing a 
global DEM to a high-resolution counterpart, are incomparable. 
• 
The global imperviousness dataset was found to overestimate imperviousness in urban 
areas in the case study area by ~20%. There were a few missing larger impervious areas 
in the dataset, but despite not being completely updated, the dataset was found applicable 
for simple pluvial flood modelling. 
• 
It was possible to create IDF curves and Chicago Design Storms (CDS) with the global rain 
data, although the shapes of the resulting design storms differ from the high-resolution 
SVK counterpart. Because of the way CDS rains are parameterized, peak intensities of the 
global CDS rains are higher, but the intensities leading up to and from the peak are lower.  
• 
Flood modelling with the global datasets gave mixed results: 
o Flood extents and depths in the model with the global DEM were very different 
from the base scenario to such a point that the results are virtually incomparable. 
The unreliable flood results may to be a combination of low resolution and too 
different local elevations in the GLO30 DEM compared to the SDFE LiDAR DEM. 
o Using the MSWEP derived CDS rains as input data results in overestimated flood 
depths and flood extents. This is caused by a combination of the regression used 
in this project and the low temporal resolution of the analysed rainfall datasets 
which gives high peak intensities with the Chicago Design Storm formula. 
o Modelling with the global imperviousness dataset resulted in very similar flood 
extents and depths to the base model after slight adjustments to the data. Whether 
this adjustment is necessary in other regions should be explored further. 
To summarize, the global datasets showed mixed potential in terms of applicability for flood mod-
elling. The ideal data for flood modelling is a bare-earth digital elevation model with high accuracy 
and high vertical and horizontal resolution. Although the satellite-based DEMs have come a long 
way since the SRTM DEM was first introduced, their use in urban flood modelling may be limited, 
at least in default/unmodified form. The global DEMs may be too coarse/noisy for modifications 
to be able to reflect close to true elevations but can perhaps be improved with other methods. 
The global rainfall data was successfully used to create design storms after employing a regres-
sion method to extend the IDF curves to lower durations. The method used in this thesis resulted 
in high peak intensities. The temporal resolution of the currently available global rainfall datasets 
limits the usability of the raw data without additional processing.  
Despite the differences in imperviousness recorded in the global dataset vs. the high-resolution 
counterpart, similar flood extents were obtained after adjusting the global imperviousness data. 
The dataset was found viable for flood modelling in the scale applied in this project.

C Method, further explanations 
This section expands on some of the methods outlined in section 3 by providing more detailed 
step-by-step descriptions of the individual processes, such as the reprojection/alignment pro-
cess. 
Reprojection and alignment 
Many of the analyses in this project are centered around the direct comparison of one or more 
digital terrain model rasters or raster by-products from the analyses themselves. 
To perform proper cell-by-cell comparisons of the raster data, each terrain model must be repro-
jected and aligned in such a way that their individual cells line up and are of the same size. There-
fore, each global DEM will be reprojected and aligned with the SDFE Odense DTM. 
The original SDFE Odense DTM is in 5x5 meter resolution and will be aggregated to 30x30 meter 
resolution to match the Copernicus terrain model (COP-DEM) and NASA terrain model 
(NASADEM). COP-DEM and NASADEM are then reprojected and aligned with the 30x30 meter 
SDFE DEM using a ‘nearest neightbor’ resampling method. Figure 0-1 shows the general work-
flow for alignment and reprojection of each DEM. 
 
Figure 0-1. GIS workflow for aligning the digital terrain models using QGIS. 
After reprojection and alignment, the aligned COP-DEM and NASADEM are subtracted from the 
30x30 SDFE raster. The resulting raster’s cell values are equal to the difference between the SDFE 
DTM and the respective DEM for comparison. The difference, after eliminating noise with a 3x3 
neighbourhood averaging method, will be used to visualize key differences between each global 
DEM and the SDFE Odense DTM. 
Blue spot analysis 
A blue spot analysis was completed by filling indentations in the aligned versions COP-DEM and 
NASADEM. A selection of tools in ArcMap’s Spatial Analyst toolbox enables the calculation of sink 
volumes and maximum depths of sinks in the terrain. An overview of the GIS workflow is shown 
in Figure 0-2. The method results in a vector layer with sinks/blue spots within the project area 
that contains information for each sink such as volume, area, maximum depth etc. 
Since COP-DEM and NASADEM are digital surface models (DSM), treetops and canopies are ex-
pected to affect the amount and location of sinks. A blue spot analysis focusing only on urban 
areas in Odense will be completed.

Blue spot analysis of urban areas 
An urban filter/mask was created from 2 layers provided for this project: 
- 
Raster of imperviousness in the Odense area 
- 
Vector of watersheds (to exclude streams etc. from the urban mask) 
The imperviousness raster was reclassified to assign cells with the value 1 where imperviousness 
is >= 10% and 0 when below. The reclassified raster was then converted to vector format, keeping 
only the polygon representing imperviousness >= 10%. The imperviousness polygon was then 
clipped to the extent of the watersheds polygon, yielding the final urban mask. With urban filtered 
sinks, the goal of this analysis is to find out if the global DEM sinks within the urban confinement 
have better overlap with SDFE sinks compared to sinks that fall outside of the urban filter. 
Since some sinks may fall on the edge of the urban mask, a criterion to be included in the urban 
area is defined: sinks must overlap 50% or more with the urban mask to be included in the anal-
ysis.

cells that flow directly into that cell.

Rain data access 
ERA5 
Data access 
ERA5 rain data is accessed from the Copernicus’ Climate Data Store (CDS) website (Hersbach et 
al., 2018). As mentioned earlier, initial subsetting is done directly on the website. Large scale rain 
rate files (hourly precipitation in mm/h) can be downloaded from 1979 to present day at a max-
imum of 120,000 items at a time. Hourly data from 1979 to 2021 is approximately 384,000 items, 
hence the data should be split into 10-year intervals when downloaded from the website. 
Data pre-processing 
The ERA5 netCDF files are processed into a single .csv file using a slightly modified version of a 
Python script from (Kluz, 2021). The resulting .csv file contains hourly rain rates in mm/h from 
1979 – present.  
MSWEP 
Data access 
MSWEP data is accessed via invitation to a Google Drive folder and the 3-hourly dataset consists 
of more than 100,000 individual files (more than 250 GB of data). While Google Drive does have 
compression- and download capabilities, compressing and downloading such a large number of 
files at once is not supported by the Google Drive interface.  
To download the files efficiently, the GloH2O website refers to a tool called rclone (Rclone, 2021). 
Rclone is an open-source command line program that is able to interact with more than 40 differ-
ent cloud storage products on behalf of the user. After an initial setup process (recommended 
guide: link), folders in the Google Drive folder can be accessed and synchronized/copied to a local 
drive. The tool allows for various sorts of filtering on a folder, filename, and filetype basis. With a 
command line-based interface, an example of interaction with the program is seen below:  
rclone sync -v --exclude NRT/ --exclude Daily/ --drive-shared-with-
me GoogleDrive:/MSWEP_V280 c:/temp/MSWEP_V280 
 
The above example synchronises all files from a shared drive (GoogleDrive:/MSWEP_V280) to a 
local folder (C:/temp/MSWEP_V280) with the exception of folders called NRT and Daily. Inclusive 
and exclusive filters must be used exclusively. Having both inclusive and exclusive filters requires 
a filter list – see the rclone documentation for more information. 
Data pre-processing 
The MSWEP netCDF files are processed into a single .csv file with a Python script. The resulting 
.csv-file contains 3-hourly rain rates in mm/3h from 1979 – present.

H In-depth review of select open data and technical information 
This section serves as a slightly more in-depth review of the global DEM and precipitation prod-

Selection of openly available digital elevation models for further analysis 
Copernicus’ GLO-30 DEM and NASADEM were chosen for further analysis based on their high 
accuracy, resolution and spatial coverage. The following sections highlight key performance pa-
rameters and expands on the reasoning behind the choice. 
Copernicus’ GLO-30 (COP-DEM) 
The Copernicus DEM is a byproduct of another DEM called WorldDEM. The WorldDEM model was 
developed in a Public Private Partnership between Airbus Defence and Space and the German Aer-
ospace Centre based on satellite data from the TanDEM-X Mission which launched in 2010. 
WorldDEM is a commercial product whereas the Copernicus DEM products are openly available.

NASADEM 
NASADEM is a refined and re-processed version of the original SRTM data. SRTM (Shuttle Radar 
Topography Mission) was originally flown in February 2000 with the goal of covering 80% of the 
earth’s landmass (between -60 and +60 degrees latitude) to create digital elevation models of the 
earth’s surface (Buckley et al., 2020).  
The goal with NASADEM was to utilize modern processing algorithms to refine the original SRTM 
data. NASADEM primarily improves upon the SRTM DEM’s numerous voids, especially in moun-
tainous areas, as well as artifact reduction. Remaining voids are filled with data from ASTER 
GDEM and ICESat which were unavailable at the time. SRTM and NASADEM share the same geo-
graphical extent, but NASADEM’s overall coverage was increased in the void-filling process.

of EGM96 as the integer product)(Buckley et al., 2020). Additionally, tiles are provided in .hgts-

by creating a related header (.hdr) file that contains information in regards to grid size, bands, 
bits, resolution, a coordinate reference point etc. (link), however this was deemed beyond the 
scope of this project and the integer version will be used for further analysis instead. 
Selection of openly available rain data for further analysis 
ERA5 and MSWEP were chosen for further analysis as they have some of the highest temporal 
resolutions of the global precipitation datasets. Both datasets cover similar periods in time from 
1979 until present which, combined with the temporal resolution, potentially makes it possible 
to calculate extreme statistics, IDF curves, and CDS rains for use in flood models.

ERA5 
The ERA5 dataset is published by the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts 
(ECMWF). The dataset does not only contain precipitation data but rather a large number of dif-
ferent climate variables, such as temperature, cloud formation, wind etc. In this case, the ‘large 
scale rain rate’ variable of the dataset is of particular interest as it represents the hourly rainfall 
rate (mm/h) in grid points that cover a 30x30 km area.  
ERA5 is a reanalysis product where model data and observed data are combined into a global and 
consistent dataset by reanalysing historical observations using modern climate models, which 
makes it possible to have global data from periods in time where measuring equipment was not 
globally widespread (Hersbach et al., 2018). 
Currently, the dataset covers the period from 1979 – present with the goal of covering the period 
from 1950 and onwards, eventually. Key technical data about the ERA5 dataset can be seen in 
Table 0-4.  
MSWEP 
MSWEP (Multi-Source Weighted-Ensemble Precipitation) is a relatively new precipitation prod-
uct developed by GloH2O, containing precipitation data from 1979 until present time. Compared 
to similar products, it is unique in the sense that it combines rain gauge, radar, and reanalysis 
data to provide a global, reliable precipitation product (GloH20, 2021). 
The MSWEP products merge data from model-based products such as ERA5, CMORPH, and JRA-
55 with satellite-based products such as GridSat and GSMaP (Global Satellite Mapping of Precipi-
tation) and determines merging weights of each dataset using rain gauge precipitation data from 
76,747 gauges globally (Beck et al., 2019).
