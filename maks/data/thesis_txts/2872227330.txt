Abstract
Phytoplankton are vital organisms that perform multiple ecosystem functions. Their abil­
ity to photosynthesize, sequester carbon, and regulate climate makes them essential to
oceanic and global processes. Understanding the drivers behind phytoplankton variabil­
ity is therefore crucial — not only for their survival, but for the broader ecosystems and
species that depend on them.
This study investigates how large­scale oceanographic processes influence phytoplank­
ton dynamics in the Northeast Atlantic, focusing on two unique locations: the Rockall and
Porcupine Banks. Using satellite­derived data from the Copernicus Marine Service, the
effects of sea surface temperature, wind stress curl (WSC), and the strength and position
of the North Atlantic Subpolar Gyre (NASG) were analyzed in relation to three phytoplank­
ton groups: diatoms, dinoflagellates, and haptophytes.
Statistical analyses performed included Generalized Additive Models (GAMs), Analysis
of Variance (ANOVA), Tukey’s Honest Significant Difference test, Analysis of Covariance
(ANCOVA), spatial autocorrelation (Moran’s I), and climatological mapping. The results
revealed statistically significant relationships between both WSC and NASG index with
all three phytoplankton groups. Additionally, spatial differences were identified between
the Rockall, Porcupine, and a reference region (BBA), though dinoflagellates showed no
significant regional variability.
This study highlights the importance of large­scale oceanographic drivers and bathymetric
features in shaping phytoplankton distribution. These findings enhance our understanding
of the physical­biological coupling in the Northeast Atlantic and its implications for higher
trophic levels.

Introduction
Phytoplankton are among the most important organisms on Earth. These microscopic
and diverse primary producers form the base of the marine food web, drive global bio­
geochemical cycles, and play a central role in regulating the climate. Without them, life
as we know it — including human life — would not exist. Understanding how, where,
and when phytoplankton proliferate, and the environmental processes that govern their
dynamics, is therefore of fundamental ecological and climatological importance.
Phytoplankton dynamics are shaped by a range of interacting processes operating across
multiple spatial and temporal scales — from local biological interactions to basin­scale
oceanographic drivers. For example, fish species such as blue whiting (Micromesistius
poutassou) — both commercially valuable and ecologically significant in the Northeast
Atlantic — depend indirectly on phytoplankton through the marine food web. Variations
in phytoplankton abundance and distribution can thus cascade through the ecosystem,
influencing the productivity of higher trophic levels.
This thesis investigates phytoplankton variability through a case study of two ecologically
important regions in the Northeast Atlantic: the Rockall Bank and Porcupine Bank. These
shallow submarine plateaus, located off the west coast of Ireland, lie at the confluence of
subpolar and subtropical waters. This confluence creates oceanographically complex and
biologically productive conditions. The regions are critical spawning and feeding grounds
for species like blue whiting, underscoring the importance of understanding the drivers of
primary production in these areas.
Several studies — including [1] and [2] — have shown links between sea surface temper­
ature, wind stress curl (WSC), and the North Atlantic Subpolar Gyre (NASG) index with
chlorophyll­a and diatom abundance in waters surrounding Rockall and Porcupine Banks.
Others, such as [3], have explored these physical drivers’ impacts on higher trophic levels.
While some research — e.g., [4] — has hinted at key dispersal and productivity relation­
ships in the area, no study has directly examined the relationship between temperature,
WSC, NASG dynamics, and phytoplankton group composition at Rockall and Porcupine
Banks.
Given their location near the boundary of the NASG and their exposure to variable wind
forcing, Rockall and Porcupine Banks offer ideal natural laboratories for assessing how
physical oceanographic conditions shape phytoplankton variability. This study focuses
on three phytoplankton groups — diatoms, dinoflagellates, and haptophytes — each with
distinct traits and ecological preferences. These groups respond differently to environ­
mental drivers such as temperature, nutrient availability, and water column stratification.
The central aim of this thesis is to examine how oceanographic variability influences phyto­
plankton abundance and community structure in the Rockall and Porcupine Bank regions.
The following hypotheses are tested:
H1: The abundance of phytoplankton is influenced by sea surface temperature in the
Rockall and Porcupine Bank regions.
H2: The abundance of phytoplankton is influenced by the magnitude of the NASG in the
Rockall and Porcupine Bank regions.
H3: The abundance of phytoplankton is influenced by wind stress curl in the Rockall and
Porcupine Bank regions.
H4: The abundance of phytoplankton is influenced by bathymetry in the Rockall and
Porcupine Bank regions.

Ireland in the Northeast Atlantic. Their complex topography plays a significant role in

2.1.1
Rockall Bank
Rockall Bank is a relatively isolated seabed plateau, approximately 300 km long and 100
km wide, see figure 2.1. Its central area has depths less than 200 m, while the edges of
the shelf descend to around 400 m. At the center of the bank lies the small rock formation
from which the bank takes its name.
The western side of Rockall Bank has a gradual slope, whereas the eastern side is notably
steeper, see figure 2.2. To the south, the slope is approximately 0.04, with the seafloor
descending from 300 m to 2200 m. Further north, the slope reaches 0.08 between depths
of 1000 m and 1500 m [5].
2.1.2
Porcupine Bank
Porcupine Bank lies southeast of Rockall Bank and is less topographically distinct, see
figure 2.1. It shares similar bathymetric characteristics, with shallower regions on top and
steep slopes along the flanks. The depth ranges ranges between 250­400 m. The shal­
lowest part of Porcupine Bank is roughly 145 m.
At the northern and western edges of Porcupine Bank there is a shelf break at approxi­
mately 500 m depth. The slopes are steep, over 3°, and narrow towards Rockall Trough,
see figure 2.2. Towards the southeast the slopes are less steep. The general slopes of
Porcupine Bank are low, typically below 0.5° [6].
2.1.3
Rockall Trough
Between Rockall and Porcupine Banks lies the Rockall Trough, a deep­water basin ex­
tending along the continental margin, see figure 2.1. Despite the steep slopes adjacent
to Rockall Bank, the deepest areas are generally found on the eastern side of the trough,
see figure 2.2. The eastern slope ranges from 0.04 to 0.06, increasing northwards to
around 0.1, after which it becomes more irregular [5].
Several seamounts are located within the Rockall Trough, including: Rosemary Bank, a
broad seamount with a diameter of approximately 65 km and a summit at 500 m depth;
Anton Dohrn, a circular seamount around 35 km wide with steep slopes and a minimum
depth of 600 m; Hebrides Terrace Seamount, an elongated feature connected to the
continental slope.
2.1.4
Internal Waves and Ocean Dynamics
The complex bathymetry of the region contributes to the generation of internal waves,
especially internal tidal waves. These are formed when tidal and wind forces displace
water masses along density gradients, creating oscillatory wave motions within the water
column. When these waves interact with seafloor features, they can break and create
intense turbulence, enhancing vertical mixing in the ocean [5].
In areas with strong stratification and variable topography, such as Rockall Trough, tidal
currents are not vertically uniform. Internal tidal waves in this region can have vertical am­
plitudes up to 20 m and displace the seasonal thermocline by approximately 25 m. These
waves are believed to originate in the deeper parts of the trough when strong diurnal and
semi­diurnal currents coincide.
Model simulations predict tidal currents with maximum speeds around 16 cm s−1, gener­
ally flowing parallel to the slopes [5].
2.1.5
Water Masses and Circulation
Several water masses enter the Rockall Trough from different origins: Warmer, saltier wa­
ters from the Subtropical Gyre (including the Gulf Stream) cross the Rockall Plateau and
flow through the trough. Cooler waters from Iberia flow northwards along the continental
slope. At depths of approximately 700 m in the southern trough, a strong anticyclonic
current (∼5 cm s−1) has been observed. Below 1500 m, circulation patterns appear to

Waters surrounding Rockall Bank tend to have higher density, while fresher, cooler wa­
ters on the bank itself may indicate upwelling or differential winter cooling. Water masses
also tend to diverge around the bank [5].
A typical CTD profile from the Rockall Trough shows a seasonal thermocline above a

Atlantic Waters (ENAW), while below 1750 m, Labrador Sea Water becomes dominant.
Winter convection penetrates beyond 600 m depth, making the region an important site
for ocean­atmosphere heat exchange [5].
2.1.6
Eddies and Mesoscale Activity
Wind stress in the region peaks during winter, while eddy kinetic energy (EKE) is highest
in spring. Drifter data reveal a high abundance of mesoscale eddies, particularly around
Anton Dohrn. These include: Small eddies with periods of 1–3 days; Larger eddies with
10­day periods and speeds up to 25 cm s−1; A major westward­drifting eddy (∼1.4 km/­
day) near Rosemary Bank, believed to originate from Norwegian Sea Deep Water over­
flow near Wyville Thomson Ridge [5].
2.1.7
Topographic Constraints and Mixing
As currents move between Rockall and Porcupine Banks via the narrower Rockall Trough,
flow speed increases due to constriction. This enhanced velocity, combined with complex
topography, generates additional turbulence and mixing. Such mixing contributes to a
more homogeneous water column and supports biological productivity.
2.1.8
Ecological Importance
Rockall Bank, Porcupine Bank, and the Rockall Trough are ecologically significant. The
region hosts the spawning grounds, which can be seen on figure 2.3, of blue whiting, a
specie belonging to the cod family, (Micromesistius poutassou).

Blue whiting is a key mid­trophic species that feeds on zooplankton in its larval stage and
krill and amphipods in adulthood [7]. It is often found to be in competition with herring and
mackerel, who share a similar throphic level in the marine food web.
Blue whiting is also a valuable fishery resource, with an estimated market size of 3.09

subpolar gyre and the fluctuations in the blue whiting [9] [10].
2.2
Phytoplankton Dynamics
In the ocean there are a multitude of microorganisms. These organisms, invisible to the
naked eye, play a crucial and indispensable role for all organisms on Earth. Many of
these microorganisms have the ability to perform photosynthesis, which is the biochemical
process of transforming carbon dioxide, CO2, into oxygen O2, by the means water, H2O
and sunlight and they are called phytoplankton. Phytoplankton absorb CO2 and H2O from
the surrounding water and through the energy obtained by sunlight, glucose (C6H12O6)
and oxygen (6O2) is produced. The biochemical process of photosynthesis is expressed
as given by the description in 2.1.
6CO2 + 6H2O + light ⇀
↽C6H12O6 + 6O2 + 6H2O
(2.1)
Looking at the processes described in 2.1 it is clear that the process is reversible due to
the symbol of ⇀
↽, meaning the process can occur in both directions. The opposite process
of photosynthesis is respiration. All organisms from plants to animals, from fungi to hu­
mans, perform respiration. Respiration in organisms which undergo phtosynthesis occurs
when the energy from sunlight is sufficiently low to not trigger the pigment of chloroplast,
and it therefore occurs typically during the night, or periods of few solar hours. However, it
is only organisms that have light­capturing organelles which can undergo photosynthesis.
The organelle, chloroplast and their associated pigments,i.e. chlorophyll a, chlorophyll b,
Carotenoids, Phycobilins and Bacterioclorophylls, is what drives the photosynthesis.
Phytoplankton can vary greatly in size, ranging four orders of magnitude, from 104 to 100
µm 1 as seen on figure 2.4.
This range is proportionally the same as the average male human height to the entire
length of Manhattan ­ a drastic difference in size. Phytoplankton therefore come in many
different sizes all having a specialized strategy to survive in the ocean environments. Phy­
toplankton are generally positioned at the bottom of the marine food web and are termed
primary producers due to their ability to fixate sunlight and perform photosynthesis. Higher
trophic levels, species higher up in the marine food web, are depended on this ability and
they would not exist if the phytoplankton does not. The marine food web depend on the
phytoplankton to harvest energy from the sun, amass nutrients and thereby creating and
injection of food into the marine food web. Thus, due to this dependency, the phytoplank­
ton are placed at the bottom of the marine food web [11]. On figure 2.5 it can be seen
how the different trophic levels are depended on each other.

2.2.1
Haptophytes
Haptophytes are a diverse and ecologically significant group of phytoplankton that con­
tribute substantially to marine primary production, accounting for an estimated 30–50%
of chlorophylla in the ocean. Among the most well­known haptophytes are the coccol­
ithophores, a subgroup characterized by their production of calcite plates known as coc­
coliths. Figure 2.6 illustrates the cellular morphology of a representative coccolithophore.
Coccolithophores play a key role in the global carbon cycle by fixing atmospheric CO2
through both photosynthesis and calcification. These two processes occur within the cell,
suggesting a complex metabolic regulation. Upon death, the calcified coccoliths of these
organisms often sink and contribute to deep­sea sediments, forming limestone deposits
over geological timescales. This biological carbon pump effectively sequesters carbon
and influences atmospheric CO2 levels. Among coccolithophores, Emiliania huxleyi is

2.2.2
Dinoflagellates
Dinoflagellates is a vast and various group of microorganisms which generally have a
size ranging from 15 to 400 µm although most are found at the smaller end of the spec­
trum. Dinoflagellates can be found in ocean and neritic waters and belong to the Kingdom
Chromista. As primary producers the dinoflagellates play a vital role in the marine, and
global, systems.
Dinoflagellates come in various shapes and forms. A defining feature of all dinoflagellates
is their mobility, which is made possible by the means of their two flagella. These flag­
ella enable a spiralling swimming motion that allows dinoflagellates to adjust their vertical
position in the water column. This ability to move gives the dinoflagellates an ecologi­
cal advantage in stratified waters, where vertical migration, movement towards nutrients
and light, is favorable. All thoguh all dinoflagellates have two flagella, the configuration of
these remain unique to the specific dinoflagellate specie. The two flagellates have differ­
ent functions and are located differently on the dinoflagellate. On figure 2.8 it is possible
to see the two flagellates: The transverse flagellum and the longitudinal flagellum [15].
The transverse flagellum encompasses the dinoflagellate. The longitudinal flagellum

[15].
Dinoflagellates are often classified into two broad morphological groups: Thecate di­
noflagellates and nonthecate dinoflagellates. Thecate dinoflagellates have a tough cell
wall, which is composed of interlocking cellulose plates located beneath the pellicle, see
figure 2.8. These plates provide protection and may assist in flotation, but these plates
limit the dinoflagellates ability to ingest prey. As such thecate dinoflagellate are primarily
autotrophic and rely on photosynthesis. Contrary to thecate dinoflagellates, the nonth­

on low nutrient conditions where other phytoplankton might not thrive. Additionally, some
colorless thecate dinoflagellates can neither undergo photosynthesis nor predate upon

of nutrients have fewer implications for the dinoflagellates than for other phytoplankton
groups [15].
Dinoflagellates are also key contributors to harmful algae blooms (HABs). Some species
can produce toxins which can prove harmful to marine life and humans. Their ecological
success ­ drive by mobility, trophic versatility, and tolerance to low­nutrient environments ­
makes dinoflagellates one of the most dynamic and ecologically important phytoplankton
groups in the ocean [15].
2.2.3
Diatoms
Diatoms are among the most important planktonic organisms in the ocean, playing a key
role in both the marine food web and global climate regulation. Their distinctive morphol­
ogy and ecological behavior make them some of the most impactful primary producers in
marine systems [16].
Diatoms are unicellular or colonial photoautotrophic microalgae, typically ranging in di­
ameter from 15 to over 400 μm, although a few species are smaller or significantly larger
[15]. They are commonly found as single cells, but many species form colonies, either sus­
pended in the water column or attached to solid substrates [16]. These resilient microor­
ganisms inhabit a wide variety of environments—from sea ice and deep marine habitats
below the photic zone to moist terrestrial ecosystems. In many aquatic systems, diatoms
dominate the microalgal community, often outcompeting other microalgae and cyanobac­
teria. This competitive advantage is likely due to their unique physiological and metabolic
traits [16].
A defining structural feature of diatoms is their siliceous cell wall, known as the frustule,
which encases the cell like a protective shell. The frustule is made of biogenic silica and
has a glass­like appearance. It is composed of two overlapping halves: the larger ep­
itheca and the smaller hypotheca, similar to the lid and base of a petri dish. Figure 2.6
illustrates a simplified diagram of the centric diatom Coscinodiscus, showing the overlap­
ping valves.
The surface of the frustule consists of small depressions and raised points, often in sym­
metry. The depressions and raised points of the valves increase the surface area and
also increases the strength of the cell wall. For many diatoms species the growth occurs
by elongation of the two valves and therefore a strong cell wall is important [15].
Diatoms exhibit a wide variety of shapes, each adapted to specific environmental con­
ditions and optimized for a high surface­area­to­volume ratio. Internally, they contain

cytoplasm, a central vacuole, cell sap, a nucleus, and numerous small chromatophores.
The cell sap within the vacuole is thought to be less dense than seawater, contributing to

Diatoms typically reproduce asexually through mitotic cell division. Under optimal con­

and large­scale blooms—especially during the spring, a phenomenon known as the spring
bloom. During cell division, the protoplast enlarges, and the nucleus and cytoplasm divide.
The two original valves separate, with each daughter cell inheriting one of the parent’s
valves as its new epitheca. A smaller hypotheca is then synthesized. Over successive
divisions, this asymmetrical inheritance leads to a gradual reduction in average cell size.
This shrinkage cannot continue indefinitely. When a critical minimum size is reached, the
protoplast escapes from the frustule, and the frustule is discarded. The protoplast, now
called an auxospore, expands and generates a new, full­sized frustule, allowing the cycle
of asexual reproduction to begin anew [15].
It is estimated that diatoms contribute approximately 20–25% of global primary produc­
tion and about 40% of marine primary production—roughly equal to the total productivity
of all the world’s rainforests combined. As such, they play a critical role not only in the
carbon and silicon cycles but also in sustaining marine food webs. Due to the density of
their frustules, diatoms tend to sink through the water column, transporting carbon and
nutrients to the seabed. This sinking flux helps connect surface processes with benthic
ecosystems, reinforcing diatoms’ pivotal role in biogeochemical cycling [16].

The thermohalice circulation is what drives the ocean circulation. The thermohaline circu­

and pressure. These changes arise from freshwater fluxes; which include evaporation,
precipitation, river discharge etc.
The main determining components of seawater’s density is the temperature and salinity
as pressure varies little on a global scale. The principle of the thermohaline circulation is

3.5.2
Akaike Information Criterion (AIC)
To find the most accurate function for prediction whilst minimizing the risk of under­ and
overfitting two methods were used, AIC and CV. AIC is given by
AIC = 2K −2 ln (Likelihood)
(3.8)
Where K is the number of parameters a model uses and likelihood is a measure of how
likely an observer is to observe their data given the specific model used. As such the AIC
is a metric which informs one of how well a model balances complexity and fit. Preferably,
one wants as low a number as possible when using the AIC. Thus, if model A’s AIC = 10
and model B’s AIC = −10, model B provides a better balance of complexity and fit.
Minimizing the complexity also minimizes the risk of overfitting, and therefore model B
has a lower risk of overfitting than model A. However, minimizing the complexity does
increase the risk of overfitting and thus a balance is required. To ensure such a balance
was achieved the a cross validation was performed.
3.5.3
Cross Validation
There exist many different cross­validation methods. One widely used approach is k­
fold cross­validation. The idea behind k­fold CV is to randomly split the dataset into k
equally sized groups, or folds. In each of the k iterations, one of these folds is set aside
as the test set, while the remaining k−1 folds are combined to form the training set. A
model is trained on this training data and then evaluated on the test fold by comparing
the model’s predictions to the actual values, typically using a performance metric such as
the residuals or root mean squared error (RMSE). This process is repeated k times, with
each fold serving once as the test set. The final model performance is usually assessed
by averaging the error metrics across all k iterations. Comparing the RMSE of the training
and test data can give keen insights into the accuracy of the model. If the RMSE of the
Oceanographic Modulation of Phytoplankton Dynamics
31
==PAGE:40==
training and test data is equal, or the difference is very small, the model fits well and there
is no under­ or overfitting. If large differences are present the model under performs and
the risk of under­ or overfitting is high [30].

Analysis of covariance (ANCOVA) is used to test if there are differences in mean between
different parts of a dataset. From the dataset used in this thesis, differences in mean is
tested between Rockall/Porcupine and BBA. To test these differences, a linear regres­
sion is made based on Gyre, WSC, Temp and Year and the regions. An entire dataset is
made, and it is ensured that the datapoint’s collected from the three regions receive the
corresponding Region­index. Based on the data, an overall linear regression is made to
test if the linear regression of each input variable can explain the pattern existing. Fur­
thermore, a linear regression is made on the data of each region. Based on these fits, the
slope and intercept of the linear regression, statistical tests are performed to distinguish
if there exists significant differences between these regions.

Discussion
5.1
Ocean and weather conditions
5.1.1
North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO)
The Rockall and Porcupine Banks are influenced by complex oceanographic and atmo­
spheric interactions shaped by both local bathymetry and broad climatological patterns.
One of the most significant of these patterns is the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO), which
regulates wind patterns, sea surface temperatures, and ocean stratification.
From Equation 2.14, it follows that an increase in pressure must either cause the subpolar
gyre to shift or increase the geostrophic currents. Since gyres are bounded by landmasses
and typically shift over much longer timescales than NAO fluctuations, it is more likely that
the gyre remains stationary while the geostrophic currents intensify.
A negative NAO index indicates a weaker­than­usual pressure difference between the
Azores High and the Icelandic Low; in some cases, the pressure over Iceland may even
exceed that over the Azores. This reduces the strength of the prevailing westerly winds
across the North Atlantic, leading to a less zonal and more meridional atmospheric circu­
lation that often permits increased cold air advection into Europe.
Dynamically, this weakening of the pressure gradient results in reduced geostrophic wind
speeds. However, localized increases in surface pressure over Iceland may cause air
convergence at lower levels and subsidence aloft, rather than divergence. Although air
is more compressible than seawater, pressure­induced changes in air density at synoptic
scales are typically small. Instead, wind stress (τwind), as shown in Equation 2.9, is
primarily controlled by wind speed and air density. Therefore, any local increase in τwind
during a negative NAO phase is more likely associated with changes in wind speed and
atmospheric stability than with compression effects alone.
Furthermore, Hátún et al. [2] found a strong correlation between a positive NAO and an
intensified subpolar gyre index. A positive NAO indicates a greater pressure difference
between the Azores High and the Icelandic Low, strengthening the westerly winds. As
these winds intensify, the wind stress on the ocean surface similarly increases, fostering
enhanced Ekman transport from the North Atlantic Subpolar Gyre (NASG). This link be­
tween NAO and the NASG is well established. The increased Ekman transport delivers a
greater concentration of nutrient­rich water into the Rockall­Porcupine system, influencing
phytoplankton growth and local ecosystem dynamics.
5.1.2
El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO)
El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO) is an important regulator of oceanographic and cli­
matological conditions. ENSO can be found in three phases, El Niño, La Niña and netural,
which is in the middle of the two former phases. On figure 5.1 ENSO is illustrated. During
El Niño, the SST are above­average in the central and eastern part of the tropical Pacific
Ocean. Over Indonesia, rainfall is reduced, while rainfall over tropical Pacific Ocean in­
tensifies. The easterly winds, which normally blows from east to west along the equator,
weakens or even changes direction. During La Niña the SST cooles in the central and
eastern part of the tropical pacific Ocean. Rainfall intensifies over Indonesia and it be­
comes drier over the central and eastern tropical Pacific Ocean. The westerly winds along
the equator intensifies. In the neutral phase neither of these two phases occur. However,
a warming or cooling of the SST can occur, but ENSO remains neutral, if the atmosphere

Calculations of Ekman transport velocity, presented in Appendix C, yield an average value

stratification, and seawater density. As wind stress increases, vertical eddy viscosity is
expected to increase proportionally, thereby deepening the Ekman transport layer.

meaningful regional differences in phytoplankton abundance remains unclear. Additional

5.2.2
Temperature and phytoplankton effects
As shown in Figure 3.5, the Rockall and Porcupine Banks are significantly shallower than
the BBA region. Naturally, a shallower water column implies a reduced vertical extent
between the sea surface and the seafloor boundaries. These boundaries strongly influ­
ence the temperature gradient within the water column. Consequently, assuming similar
boundary conditions, a shallower water column tends to exhibit a lower temperature gra­
dient compared to a deeper water column.
Supporting this, seafloor temperature data and detailed calculations of vertical tempera­
ture gradients are provided in Appendix D. On an annual scale, this thermocline struc­
ture can be disrupted by processes such as internal tidal waves, wind stress curl (WSC),
and Ekman transport. Additionally, the waters above Rockall Bank are characterized
by cooler and fresher conditions, which [5] attribute to upwelling phenomena around the
bank. This upwelling not only disrupts the development of a stable thermocline but also
brings nutrient­rich waters to the surface, potentially explaining the strong biological sig­
nals observed over Rockall Bank.
Therefore, these climatological temperature maps may also serve as useful indicators of
the prevailing temperature gradients over the Rockall and Porcupine Banks.

5.2.3
Gyre and phytoplankton effects
Diatoms and haptophytes show statistically significant differences in abundance between
Rockall/Porcupine and BBA (Table 4.3), whereas dinoflagellates do not. This may be ex­
plained by the mobility of dinoflagellates, which employ diurnal vertical migration (DVM)
as a survival strategy. By migrating downward during the day toward the nutrient­rich
thermocline—beyond the reach of many predators and often undetectable by satellite
sensors—dinoflagellates reduce predation risk while accessing essential resources.
Table 4.12 reveals a highly significant relationship and strong explanatory power of the
subpolar gyre for diatom abundance across all locations, highlighting the gyre’s crucial
role in nutrient supply [2]. The gyre facilitates nutrient exchange between the thermocline
and mixed layer through vertical and horizontal flows, supporting diatoms and haptophytes
(notably coccolithophores), which depend heavily on silicate and carbonate availability,
respectively.
This nutrient exchange has important ecological implications, particularly in early spring
when phytoplankton growth is often nutrient­limited despite increasing sunlight. Strong
gyre­induced mixing can deliver nutrients earlier and more efficiently, leading to an earlier
and more intense spring bloom. Therefore, the subpolar gyre’s mixing processes are key
to the timing and success of this foundational seasonal event. Investigating the Gyre vari­
able in Table 4.4, we observe that the Intercept is negative for all phytoplankton groups,
while the coefficients for Gyre, RegionPorcupine, and RegionRockall are positive. This
indicates that phytoplankton abundance increases with increasing NASG strength, within
the observed data range.

NASG conditions are associated with lower phytoplankton biomass and higher zooplank­

greater than the area investigated in this thesis. In the analysis presented in table 4.4 only
Rockall, Porcupine and BBA have been investigated. In their paper a much greater area
were investigated. From this it follows that Rockall and Porcupine banks play significant
effect on the phytoplankton abundance ­ enough to oppose the general trend found in [3].

5.3
Higher Trophic Levels and Marine Food Web
Marine organisms exhibit distinct habitat and dietary preferences, which shape the struc­
ture of the local food web. Based on the relative abundance data shown in Figure E.1
(Appendix E), the trophic pathways in this ecosystem can be outlined as illustrated in Fig­
ure 2.5. According to this, the copepod zooplankton primarily feed on diatoms and, to a
lesser extent, dinoflagellates. Non­photosynthetic dinoflagellates are capable of predating
on diatoms, haptophytes, and even smaller dinoflagellates. Given the high relative abun­
dance of diatoms and haptophytes, it follows that species feeding on these groups—such
as dinoflagellates and copepods—would be prominent in these regions. Higher trophic
levels then prey on copepods, continuing the food chain.
One ecologically and economically important species in this system is the blue whiting (Mi­
cromesistius poutassou), which spawns around Rockall Bank and relies on copepods for
food during its early life stages. Blue whiting must contend not only with fishing pressure
but also competition for food from species like herring and mackerel, as well as predation
by larger fish such as mackerel and hake, which themselves are prey for even higher
trophic predators [35].
A relevant case study by [2] investigated Atlantic mackerel spawning west of Ireland be­
tween May and July. During this time, their preferred prey, the copepod Calanus fin­
marchicus, is scarce, prompting mackerel to migrate northward into the Icelandic Basin
[10]. In contrast, blue whiting spawn earlier—from February to March—while still nour­
ished by their egg yolk for about 8 days before hatching [36]. This timing closely aligns
with the onset of the spring phytoplankton bloom (March 30 to July 9) [23].
According to the match­mismatch hypothesis1, blue whiting’s earlier spawning may confer
a reproductive advantage over mackerel by ensuring their larvae encounter higher zoo­
plankton abundance during critical growth phases.
Blue whiting spawn near Rockall Bank, a region that exhibits significant oceanographic
variability under different circulation regimes (see Figure 3.2). These fluctuations likely
affect phytoplankton dynamics, cascading through the food web from primary producers
to zooplankton, and ultimately to blue whiting and their predators. As Table 4.4 shows,
wind stress curl (WSC) and the subpolar gyre index exert statistically significant influ­
ences on these regions. This aligns with earlier research highlighting the importance of
these oceanographic processes in regulating phytoplankton productivity and, by exten­
sion, higher trophic levels [9, 10].

5.6
Higher Sampling Frequency
The sampling frequency used in this thesis was approximately 3.86 · 10−7 hz. This low
frequency is highly valid for capturing large scale processes ­ processes which have been
studied in this thesis. However, Rockall Bank, Rockall Trough and Porcupine Bank are
naturally affected by processes which occur on timescales lower than what has been in­
vestigated in this thesis. One of these processes are tides. Rui Cereja et al. found in their
study that not only did tidal processes explain 50 % of the chlorophyll­a variations, but tidal
processes similarly explained the variability found in the phytoplankton community com­
position [38]. Rockall Bank, Rockall Trough and Porcupine Bank are highly influenced by
tidal processes. The area experience two high tidal periods every 24 hours and 50 min.
These high tidal periods can have a drastic effect on the phytoplankton community. As
previously stated in section 2.1, the typography of the area investigated varies drastically.
With steep slopes, seamounts and tidal processes it is evident that tidal internal waves
will emerge, especially during summer where stratification is strong. These internal tidal
waves, when breaking, will mix the water column drastically and bring nutrient rich wa­
ters to the surface. This process will help phytoplankton species which are not suited for
nutrient­depleted environments like diatoms. The breaking of internal waves will allow for
a new ”injection” of nutrients would could sustain a community of diatoms.

under nutrient­depleted conditions. This means that haptophytes could survive during

5.7
Uncertainties and applicability
The data used in this thesis carry considerable uncertainty. Appendix F provides maxi­
mum uncertainty plots and results from Monte Carlo simulations illustrating this variability.
As shown in Figure F.1 (Appendix F), all phytoplankton groups exhibit high uncertainty
levels, which substantially affect the reliability of the statistical analyses presented here.
Consequently, the interpretability and applicability of the derived models are limited.
To ensure robust conclusions, only relationships with strong statistical significance and
relatively low associated uncertainty should be considered reliable. These relationships
carry a reduced risk of Type I errors (i.e., falsely rejecting a true null hypothesis).
Another important assumption made throughout this work is that satellite­derived surface
concentrations of phytoplankton represent their distribution throughout the entire water
column. This assumption is idealized and often inaccurate, as phytoplankton blooms fre­
quently occur below the surface, beyond the detection depth of satellites. Therefore, a
significant portion of vertical variability remains unobserved, since satellite measurements
lack information on the third spatial dimension—depth.
5.7.1
Monte Carlo Simulation
To address data uncertainty, Monte Carlo simulations can be applied to statistical tests,
incorporating variability into the analysis. Appendix F presents an example of a simple
Monte Carlo simulation performed on the dataset.
While Monte Carlo methods can be extended to higher­dimensional spatial grids, their
computational cost increases substantially. Some statistical computations in this thesis
required several hours to complete, so Monte Carlo uncertainty analyses should be ap­
plied judiciously, especially when computational resources are limited.
Ignoring uncertainty may lead to misleading conclusions. Observed values can vary ran­
domly within their uncertainty bounds. Not all values within these bounds will support
statistically significant relationships; some may even contradict them. Therefore, it is
critical to assess the probability of obtaining a significant relationship given the data’s
uncertainty—particularly in marine remote sensing applications, where large uncertain­
ties are common.

6
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the following hypotheses:
H1: The abundance of phytoplankton is influenced by the temperature of the occupied
water in the Rockall and Porcupine Bank regions.
H2: The abundance of phytoplankton is influenced by the magnitude of the NASG in the
Rockall and Porcupine Bank regions.
H3: The abundance of phytoplankton is influenced by the magnitude of wind stress curl
(WSC) in the Rockall and Porcupine Bank regions.
H4: The abundance of phytoplankton in the Rockall and Porcupine Bank regions is
influenced by bathymetry.
A series of analyses were conducted to examine potential patterns and relationships be­
tween these oceanographic processes and the response of various phytoplankton groups.
Regarding H1, no clear or consistent relationship was found between sea surface tem­
perature (SST) and phytoplankton abundance. However, temperature gradients across
Rockall and Porcupine Banks did reveal spatial patterns that warrant further investiga­
tion. Due to time limitations, these patterns were not explored in depth. Thus, while this
study does not identify a significant relationship between SST and phytoplankton abun­
dance, future work should consider subsurface temperatures and vertical stratification,
which may more directly influence phytoplankton distribution.
In contrast, H2 and H3 yielded more conclusive results. Both Generalized Additive Mod­
els (GAMs) and ANCOVA analyses showed statistically significant relationships between
phytoplankton abundance and both the NASG index and WSC. Among the tested vari­
ables, the NASG showed the second­highest explanatory power in the GAMs, indicating
a strong influence on phytoplankton dynamics. WSC had a smaller, yet still statistically
significant, effect. Therefore, I conclude that phytoplankton abundance in the study area
is strongly influenced by the NASG and moderately influenced by WSC.
Finally, analyses related to H4 revealed robust support for the influence of bathymetry.
ANOVA and post­hoc Tukey tests indicated highly significant differences in phytoplank­
ton abundance between Rockall/Porcupine Bank and the surrounding basin (BBA) across
most phytoplankton groups. Additionally, the GAMs demonstrated a strong relationship
between bathymetry and phytoplankton abundance, with high explanatory power. These
results support the conclusion that bathymetry exerts a substantial influence on phyto­
plankton distribution in the region, likely by shaping local hydrography and nutrient dy­
namics.

Given that uncertainty estimates are available for each individual data point across space

Specifically, a 4D simulation matrix CMC can be constructed as:
CMC(i, j, k, n) = Normal
(
µ = P(i, j, k), σ = Q(i, j, k)
)
where P(i, j, k) represents the matrix of observed values, Q(i, j, k) the corresponding un­
certainty matrix at each spatial­temporal location (i, j, k), and n is the number of Monte
Carlo iterations (the fourth dimension). In other words, for each grid cell, a normal distri­
bution is defined, from which n samples are drawn iteratively.
Monte Carlo simulations were used to assess the robustness of the statistically significant
relationships observed in this study. By generating random samples and repeating the
statistical analyses, the simulations test whether these significant patterns could arise by
chance, thereby evaluating the reliability of the results.
Running a sufficient number of iterations (e.g., 10,000) allows estimation of the probabil­
ity that each cell’s statistical test is significant. Figure F.2 shows the results of a single
Monte Carlo simulation of Spearman’s Rank Correlation Test between dinoflagellates and
temperature, with 10,000 iterations. The simulation is fixed in space at a single point on
Rockall Bank.
Figure F.2: The non­exceedance probability of the p­values from a Spearman’s Rank
Correlation Test at a single spatial point on Rockall Bank.
The figure illustrates the non­exceedance probability (cumulative distribution function) of
the p­values from the test. It shows that the probability of finding a statistically significant
relationship between dinoflagellates and temperature at this location is small.
If a statistically significant relationship (e.g., p < 0.05) is observed in more than 95% of
the iterations, one can conclude that the result is robust to uncertainty and likely reflects
a true underlying pattern. Based on this simulation, no significant relationship appears
to exist between dinoflagellates and temperature at the examined point on the Rockall
Bank.
