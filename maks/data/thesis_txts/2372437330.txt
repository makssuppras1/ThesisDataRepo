Abstract
Keywords: Float glass, composite, structural glass, mechanically reinforced glass beam, reinforcement, ad-
hesive, ductility, 4-point bending, fracture of glass, tensile strength of glass, load-carrying capacity, post-
breakage capacity, yielding trajectory, shear, FE-model.
Highlights
• 4-point bending tests with varying distance between the loads were successfully performed on me-
chanically reinforced glass beams.
• A FE-model was established in Abaqus/Explicit.
Eﬀort was made in obtaining a quasi-static
response with ductile behaviour, as in the experiments, and reliable crack patterns.
• Through the experiments the beams achieved ductile post-breakage behaviour, and bending were
dominating the experiments in regards to crack formation and failure.
• Investigation of the crack patterns revealed no tendencies in connection to the eﬀect of shear.
• The FE-model was compared to analytical and experimental results and a satisfying accordance to
the analytical predictions was revealed.
• The inﬂuence of shear may have been present in regards to the tensile strength of glass, where the
tallest beams yielded the lowest strength.
The use of glass has through the last couple of years developed progressively, and glass is nowadays applied
as structural load-carrying elements in a variety of constructions. Research is developing rapidly, yet the
knowledge of the brittle material is still insuﬃcient. This raises the demand for further investigations to be
performed in the ﬁeld. Especially the unexplored ﬁeld of taller cross-sections, where more shear is induced,
is of great interest.
Glass has a linear elastic behaviour until the ﬁrst crack emerges and thereby ultimate failure occurs. To
ensure safety, glass elements are frequently over-dimensioned. In order to overcome the brittle nature of
glass and to compensate for over-dimensioning the mechanically reinforced glass beams are evolved. The
concept of the beam is corresponding to that of reinforced concrete, where a brittle material and a ductile
material are composed, to beneﬁt from both.
Several studies have been performed in regards to the reinforced glass beam exposed to pure bending, where
design formulas already are derived. However, the knowledge of the structural behaviour of reinforced glass
beams subjected to shear is non-existent, hence the purpose of the project is to investigate the inﬂuence
vii
==PAGE:8==
viii
of shear through numerical analyses and physical experiments. This is done by comprehensive analyses of
the beam behaviour obtained from both analytical calculations, FE-modelling and conducted experiments.
A FE-model of the beams was modelled in Abaqus/Explicit to account for non-linear eﬀects when glass
fracture initiates.
Subsequently by application of a cracking model implemented in Abaqus/Explicit
it was possible to investigate the progressive crack propagation. Careful consideration was dedicated to
obtain the most optimal model that approaches a quasi-static response.
Diﬀerent mesh schemes and
mass scaling was applied and great eﬀort was made to keep the computational costs low and still achieve
reliable results.
It was discovered that this was a diﬃcult process causing necessity for compromises.
Despite this the ﬁnal model obtained a favorable crack pattern and the beam exhibited great post-breakage
capacity. Additionally a satisfying accordance to analytical predictions were achieved, where the analytical
predictions were obtained from already deduced design formulas.
Concurrently experiments on similar mechanically reinforced glass beams were performed. The experiments
consisted of 4-point bending tests on 32 test specimens. The beams were constructed of three diﬀerent
heights of glass (100 mm, 150 mm and 200 mm) in order to detect the eﬀects of the greater magnitude
of shear force present in the taller beams. The beams were tested with three diﬀerent distances between
the loads to investigate the inﬂuence of increasing the shear/moment ratio by decreasing the moment
contribution.
The experiments revealed that a safe post-breakage ductile behaviour were obtained for the beams. Fur-
thermore bending was dominating the crack patterns and failure of the beams. Strain gauge measurements
provided a detailed image of the behaviour before and after initial fracture of glass and it was clear that
local yielding in steel was accomplished. Deviations between experimental and analytical/numerical re-
sults were evident, mostly for the ﬁrst cracking condition, and for the yielding trajectory. Regarding the
higher initial glass breakage load investigations revealed that this was caused by the assumption of a tensile
strength of glass equal to 45 MPa, where the actual strength of the glass in the beams exhibited greater
magnitude. Determination of the strength of glass showed that the largest strength was obtained for the
beams with the smallest height, which indicated that the higher amount of shear in the tallest beams may
have inﬂuenced the strength. Deviations between the numerical and experimental yielding trajectories
were present, where it was argued that strain-hardening might have occurred, resulting in a higher yield
strength than the one obtained from tensile tests, probably due to the high load level and rapid increment
of strains during ﬁrst fracture.

Resumé
Keywords: Uhærdet glas, komposit, konstruktionsglas, mekanisk armeret glasbjælke, armering, lim, duk-
tilitet, 4-punkts bøjning, glasbrud, trækstyrke af glas, lastbærende kapacitet, efter-revne kapacitet, ﬂy-
dekurve, forskydning, FE-model.
Highlights
• 4-punkts bøjningsforsøg med varierende afstande mellem laster var succesfuldt udført på mekanisk
armerede glasbjælker.
• En FE-model var etableret i Abaqus/Explicit. Der blev fokuseret på at opnå et quasi-statisk respons
med duktil opførsel, som i eksperimenterne, og troværdige revnemøsntre.
• Gennem forsøgene opnåede bjælkerne duktil opførsel efter glasset revnede, og bøjning var dominerende
i forsøgene med hensyn til revnedannelse og kollaps.
• Undersøgelse af revnemønstre afslørede ikke nogle tendenser i forhold til eﬀekten af forskydning.
• FE-modellen blev sammenlignet med analytiske og eksperimentelle resultater og en tilfredsstillende
overensstemmelse til de analytiske forudsigelser var opnået, hvoraf det således var muligt at validere
modellen.
• Indvirkningen af forskydning har muligvis været til stede i forhold til styrken af glas, hvor de højeste
bjælker havde den laveste styrke.
Brugen af glas har gennem de seneste år udviklet sig progressivt, og glas anvendes nu om dage som
bærende konstruktionselement i mange forskellige konstruktioner. Forskningen er i hurtig udvikling, men
kendskabet til det skøre materiale er stadig mangelfuld. Dette kræver at yderligere undersøgelser af emnet
udføres. Især det uudforskede område med højere bjælketværsnit, hvor mere forskydning er til stede i
bjælkerne, er interessant.
Glas har en lineær elastisk opførsel, indtil den første revne fremkommer og derved ultimativ kollaps indtræf-
fer. For at garantere sikkerheden er glaselementer ofte overdimensionerede. For at overkomme glassets
skøre natur og for at kompensere for overdimensionering, er den mekanisk armerede glasbjælke udviklet
gennem tidligere forskning. Bjælkens koncept er tilsvarende armeret beton, hvor et skørt materiale sam-
mensættes med et duktilt materiale og derved får en sikkerhedsmargin.
Mange undersøgelser er udført med hensyn til armerede glasbjælker udsat for ren bøjning, hvor design-
formler er udledt.
Kendskabet til den strukturelle opførsel af armerede glasbjælker, der udsættes for
ix
==PAGE:10==
x
forskydning, er imidlertid ikke-eksisterende, og derfor er formålet med projektet at undersøge indﬂydelsen
af forskydning gennem numeriske analyser og fysiske eksperimenter. Dette gøres ved omfattende analyser
af bjælkeopførslen fra både analytiske beregninger, FE-modellering og udførsel af eksperimenter.
En FE-model af bjælkerne blev modelleret i Abaqus/Explicit for at tage højde for ikke-lineære eﬀek-
ter, når glasset bryder, hvilket gør det muligt at undersøge revnemønstre. Omhyggelige overvejelser var
dedikeret til at opnå den mest optimale model, der nærmer sig et quasi-statisk respons. Forskellige mesh
og masse-skalering-faktorer blev anvendt, og der blev ydet en stor indsats for at holde beregningsomkost-
ningerne lave og stadig opnå pålidelige resultater. Det var tydeligt, at dette var en vanskelig proces, der
forårsagede nødvendigheden af kompromiser. Den endelige model opnåede et gunstigt revnemønster, og
bjælken udviste kapacitet efter brud. Der blev desuden opnået en tilfredsstillende overensstemmelse til
analytiske forudsigelser, hvor de analytiske forudsigelser er baseret på tidligere udledte designformler.
Samtidig blev der udført eksperimenter på mekanisk armerede glasbjælker. Eksperimenterne bestod af
4-punkts bøjningstest af 32 testprøver. Bjælkerne blev konstrueret af tre forskellige højder af glas (100
mm, 150 mm og 200 mm) for at påvise indvirkningen af den større mængde af forskydning, der er til
stede i de højere bjælker. Bjælkerne blev testet med tre forskellige afstande mellem belastningerne for at
undersøge indﬂydelsen af at forøge forskydning/moment forholdet ved at reducere momentbidraget.
Forsøgene viste at der blev opnået en sikker duktil adfærd efter brud af bjælkerne. Desuden dominerede
bøjning revnemønstrene og kollaps af bjælkerne. Strain gauges viste et detaljeret billede af opførelsen før
og efter indledende brud på glas, og det var evident, at lokal ﬂydning opstod i stålet. Afvigelser mellem
eksperimentelle og analytiske/numeriske resultater var tydelige, primært for første revnebetingelse, og for
den opnående ﬂydekurve. Med hensyn til den højere belastning ved første revne, viste undersøgelser, at
dette skyldtes antagelsen om glassets trækstyrke svarende til 45 MPa, hvor den virkelige styrke af glas i
bjælkerne var af større værdi. Bestemmelsen af styrken af glas viste, at den største trækstyrke blev opnået
for bjælkerne med den mindste højde, hvilket tydeliggjorde, at den højere mængde af forskydning i de
højeste bjælker kan have påvirket styrken. Afvigelser mellem de numeriske og eksperimentelle ﬂydekurver
var til stede, hvor det blev vist, at der kunne forekomme tøjningshærdning af stålet, hvilket resulterede i
en højere ﬂydespænding end den, der blev opnået for trækprøver.

Introduction
Introduction to the project
Glass is the oldest man-made material and for centuries it has adorned façades on buildings. As con-
structions have developed the past years the use of glass has evolved and nowadays architecture and
construction have raised a more complex use of glass as a structural load-carrying material. Through the
last two decades technology has developed fast, and with a proper analysis, glass is now often utilized as
a load-carrying structural element in constructions. Certain precautionary measures must be taken when
working with glass which often result in over-dimensioning the glass elements. This raises the demand for
comprehensive investigations of the behaviour of the material under diﬀerent circumstances.
Glass has a very high strength in compression which makes it an obvious material as structural element.
Furthermore, glass exhibits favorable properties in relation to stiﬀness, durability and a high melting point.
Another very important property of glass is the resistance to corrosion and other harmful substances.
However, as any other material glass has disadvantages, which includes a relatively low tensile strength
and low toughness combined with a completely elastic behaviour. This deﬁnes glass as a brittle material.
Certain circumstances have to be considered when working with such a brittle material and this is one
of the greatest concerns and challenges in the application.
The aim is to take advantage of the high
compressive strength without reaching the low tensile strength exhibiting brittle fracture.
In order to accomplish this a new phenomena is developed; the mechanically reinforced glass beam, where
safety is ensured by reinforcement. The mechanically reinforced glass beam is created to compensate for the
low tensile strength and to utilize the high compressive strength of glass. It is constructed as a composite
beam consisting of glass and steel, where the concept of combining a brittle and ductile material is similar
to the concept of the well-known and well-used concrete reinforced structural elements. When considering
bending, the glass carries the compressive forces and the steel carries the tensile forces, consequently the
steel is yielding after fracture of glass commence. A plastic behaviour of the composite beam is then
obtained which causes a build-in warning and by that it is possible to replace a broken glass beam before
it crushes. This provides the structural glass element with a safe post-breakage behaviour causing the
necessity for over-dimensioning to diminish.
Nowadays constructions are primarily consisting of heat-strengthened glass, fully tempered glass or a
combination of these in laminated glass panes.
However the mechanically reinforced glass beams are

1.1
Material Properties
Glass is deﬁned as a ceramic, which is a nonmetallic, inorganic and amorphous solid prepared by heating
and subsequently cooling.
Glass is a purely elastic material characterized by its brittleness and high
compressive strength. In addition to the high compressive strength another advantage is the ability of glass
to resist corrosion, which makes the material beneﬁcial as construction material. However a sensitivity
to small cracks that occur during the manufacturing process makes the tensile strength unreliable and
rather low. The glass referred to throughout the report is soda-lime-silica ﬂoat glass, which primarily
consists of 69-74 % SiO2, 5-14 % CaO and 10-16 % Na2O. When producing ﬂoat glass, see Figure 1.1,
these raw materials are blended and subsequently melted in a furnace that reaches temperatures of up to
1550◦C. A continuous pouring of the glass to a molten tin bath at around 1000◦C is then performed. Tin
is optimal for this process because it appears in its liquid physical state at a large range of temperatures.
The glass is ﬂoating on the tin creating a smooth surface that ensures an optimal transparency of the glass.
Gradually the glass is cooled down and when it exits the tin bath the temperature is decreased to 600◦C.
Subsequently the glass enters the long oven, named the annealing lehr, where the glass is transported

After the ﬂoat glass is produced as illustrated in Figure 1.1 it is possible to strengthen the glass by
further heating of the material to a temperature above the glass transitions temperature Tg = 575◦C,

chosen to use ﬂoat glass throughout the project.

is to fracture. The performance curve for steel and glass are plotted together, where it is seen that steel

The failure criterion of glass is deﬁned by the Maximum-Tensile Stress Criterion by Rankine
[Chen & Hal, 2007, p. 88]. The criterion states that failure of a brittle material occurs when the maximum
principal stress reaches the value of the maximum tensile strength of the material, in the case of glass this
is the tensile strength ftg = 45 MPa.
Another important aspect when using glass as load-carrying element is the inevitable surface ﬂaws that
weakens the structural performance of the material. According to Griﬃth [Griﬃth, 1920], fracture of glass
is not started from an intact glass surface, but from ﬂaws that already exist in the glass, the so-called
Griﬃth ﬂaws.
It is not always possible to detect these ﬂaws, but very high stress concentrations are
produced in the ﬂaws and the glass is weakened [Haldimann et al, 2008, p. 55]. These ﬂaws inﬂuences the
tensile strength of glass and previous research has shown that the strength can vary between 20-100 MPa
[Nielsen, 2009, p.7], which is a noticeable variation. The diﬀerence between the actual strength and the
theoretical is explained by Griﬃth as microscopic defects sporadic spread at the surface of glass. When
glass is subjected to load tensile stress concentrations emerge around the ﬂaws and from these stresses
initial breakage of glass initiates. During the manufacturing and the transportation of glass the defects
develop. The fracture strength of glass is dependent on the ﬂaw depth, where a large depth causes a
decreased strength. Since the ﬂaws are sporadic distributed across the surface of glass, the possibility of
detecting a large critical ﬂaw is greater for a larger specimen size.

The Concept of the Mechanically Reinforced Glass Beam
The concept of the MRGB is a quite newly developed approach for utilizing glass. The objective of the
MRGB is to develop a post-fracture redundancy, which is accomplished by attaching reinforcement to the
tensile face of glass. The reinforcement acts as a bridge for cracks appearing in the tensile zone of glass,
where the tensile forces are transferred from the glass to the reinforcement by shear through the adhesive
layer. This feature enables the beam to continue carrying load after cracking of the glass and a ductile
behaviour is obtained for the beam. A principal sketch of the reinforced monolithic ﬂoat glass beam is
illustrated in Figure 1.3. The MRGB consists of ﬂoat glass and reinforcement, connected by an adhesive
layer.

Certain safety concepts have to be applied when glass is used as a structural element in building structures.
The objective is to apply precautionary measures to minimize the possibility of complete failure of the
beam. The main challenges when working with glass is the brittle nature and the fact that no built-in
warning for the failure of glass exits. A perfect composite action between glass and steel is what deﬁne
the safety concepts, where glass is the main load-carrying member and activation of steel primarily occurs
when the glass fails [Louter et al, 2011a].
The concept of the MRGB, where the performance of a structural element consisting of a brittle material
enhanced by an implemented reinforcement, is familiar to that of reinforced concrete. Glass and con-
crete are both brittle materials that exhibit high compressive strength and low tensile strength. However
signiﬁcant diﬀerences between the materials are present and they should be accessed by diﬀerent meth-
ods. Regarding design concepts, upon cracking, concrete is accepted in the Serviceability Limit State
(SLS), while cracks in glass will be in the Ultimate Limit State (ULS). Furthermore the reinforcement
method for the two materials are very diﬀerent. Figure 1.4 illustrates the concept of the MRGB, where
(a) presents the cross-section for the MRGB. Glass fracture of the MRGB is shown from the side in (b).

When the glass fails only the reinforcement is carrying the tensile stresses. Consequently the degree of
reinforcement and the usability of the adhesive layer is signiﬁcant for the post-breakage behaviour of the
MRGB. If tensile stresses are not transferred suﬃciently or if the amount of reinforcement is not adequate
the ductile behaviour might be absent. In connection to this four diﬀerent failure modes are identiﬁed
associated with reinforced glass beams in relation to pure bending.
• Anchorage: Typically characterized by delamination of the adhesive, where failure of the adhesive
layer occurs before the reinforcement has yielded. The failure often develops after cracking of the
glass.
• Over-reinforced: Crushing of the compression zone occurs before the reinforcement has yielded.
Failure due to over-reinforcement is unlikely to happen because of the high compressive strength in
glass.
• Normal-reinforced: A perfect composite action between glass and steel is obtained. The reinforcement
yields after the glass has cracked and before the compression zone has failed.
• Under-reinforced: The glass and the reinforcement fails together and the maximum load capacity is
equal to the capacity of the glass beam without reinforcement.

1.3
Design Formulas
As mentioned the behaviour of the MRGB is analogous to that of reinforced concrete and certain design
formulas exist. These design formulas have been developed in previous research by [Nielsen & Olsen, 2010]
and [Louter, 2011]. The formulas describe the behaviour of the MRGB acting in pure bending, which can
be subdivided into three stages. These stages and the design formulas will be described in the following.
In relation to pure bending the cross-section of the MRGB is analyzed as a composite and it is assumed
that the adhesive layer has no thickness. Furthermore it is assumed that the glass has a perfectly linear
elastic behaviour and that the steel exhibits elasto-plastic properties. Finally a rigid connection between
glass and steel is assumed. The three stages are listed below.
• Un-cracked stage: The glass and reinforcement work together as an absolute composite in the linear
elastic range. The strength of glass is the controlling parameter until initial failure of glass.
• Cracked stage: In the cracked-stage cracks starts to propagate in the tension zone. The load-carrying
mechanism is composed of the compression zone carrying all compressive forces and the reinforcement
carrying all tensile forces. The compression zone remains intact and the reinforcement behaves linear
elastically.
• Yield stage: The deformation of the steel is changing from linear to plastic in this stage resulting in
a ductile post-breakage response. Glass is already extensively cracked and existing cracks starts to
grow into the un-cracked compression zone.
The three stages are presented in Figure 1.6(a), 1.6(b) and 1.6(c), where the cross-section for the composite
beam is illustrated in the un-craked, cracked and yield stage along with the strain distribution. Furthermore
the moment-curvature plot for each stage is presented in [Nielsen, 2009].

Dimensioning of Test Specimens
In the previous chapter the properties of ﬂoat glass have been introduced along with the concept of the
MRGB. Throughout the following section the aim is to determine the dimensions and properties of the
elements included in the composition of the MRGB to be investigated through experiments. Preliminary
analyses have been performed in order to create the most optimal design of the beam. This includes
preparatory calculations considering the height of the glass, dimensions of steel and type of the adhesive
layer. The main purpose is ideally to design the beam as normal-reinforced and to avoid anchorage failure.
Furthermore it is important that the MRGB is constructed as a perfect composite, where the adhesive layer
transfers the shear stresses induced in the glass to the steel and the steel then carries the tensile stresses
after initial cracking. Finally it is desired to achieve a ductile post-breakage capacity of the MRGB.
2.1
Design of Glass
The objective of this work is to investigate the behaviour of the MRGB in shear. Therefore three diﬀerent
heights of the glass are chosen, since a taller cross-section increases the shear force when subjected to load-
ing. The dimensioning of the glass is performed by assuming the dimensions and subsequently investigate
if these are suitable for the experiments. It is desired to investigate monolithic ﬂoat glass beams because
monolithic glass ensures a more even distribution of the applied load compared to laminated glass. The
height, width and length of the three diﬀerent beams are 100 × 19 × 1000 mm, 150 × 19 × 1000 mm and
200 × 19 × 1000 mm, now referred to as 100-beams, 150-beams and 200-beams respectively.
To determine if the dimensions are suitable for the experiments, analytical and numerical calculations are
performed. The analyses are based on the principal stresses because the failure criterion of Rankine is
based on these as described in Section 1.1.

3.2.1
The Process of Gluing
Before starting the composition the beams are marked with a left and right side as well as a front and
back side. In this way it is easier to observe where the cracks are formed and it is easier to locate any
defects. Before the steel is glued to the glass it is sandblasted on the edge facing the adhesive to ensure
a proper fastening of the steel. The attachment of the steel to the glass is conducted in continuation to
sandblasting as no dust or ﬁnger prints are supposed to ﬁx on the surface. The glass is investigated at
both edges and the edge with the best polishing and less ﬂaws are chosen as the tensile edge. Just before
the gluing starts both glass and steel are cleaned with acetone in order to remove dirt that might inﬂuence
the bonding. When using epoxy diﬀerent precaution measures need to be considered, thus the gluing is
performed in a fume cupboard with special gloves produced for gluing and following security guide-lines
provided from DELO R⃝.
The adhesive is applied to the steel in zigzag-pattern in order to prevent air voids from being trapped
inside the glue and it is aimed to apply the glue in a height of 1 mm. After the glue has been applied to
the steel, the glass is placed on the steel. Subsequently two supports on each side of the beam are placed
in order to keep the glass straight when the glue is curing. The beam does not need to be subjected to
further weight because the dead weight of the glass itself compresses the elements. The manufacturer of
the adhesive claims that a tensile shear strength of 22 MPa for a glue height of 1.6 mm is obtained after a
curing time of 72 hours and an E-modulus of 1.7 GPa is obtained after 7 days [DELO, 2017]. As described
in Section 2 the preferred properties for an adhesive is a high tensile shear strength and a low stiﬀness.
The beams are left in the fume cupboard for curing in at least 72 hours in order to obtain the maximum
tensile shear strength and too long curing time is avoided to ensure a low E-modulus. The exact curing
times for each of the beams are illustrated in Appendix C.4.
During the process of gluing diﬃculties regarding the slope of the beams and problems with air voids in
the glue evolved. At ﬁrst the gluing was performed in a fume cupboard, in which a slope of the base
was detected, consequently it resulted in a small slope of the beams, that mostly leaned against the front.
Furthermore this cupboard was quite small, and it was hard to see if any of the beams had air voids
trapped inside the glue. Another cupboard was then utilized, where the composites turned out to be more
straight and it was easier to prevent air voids in the adhesive. The gluing process is presented in Figure
3.2, where Figure 3.2(a) illustrates the application of the glue in zigzags and Figure 3.2(b) shows the setup
with the supports used during the time of curing.

Loading
The aforementioned Instron bending test machine is used to test the beams in 4-point bending and the
load is applied as a displacement with constant rate. The test machine measures the displacement of the
entire setup, thus it is necessary to correct the results in order to determine the actual displacement of the
test specimen.
Since the strength of glass is strongly time-dependent as described in Section 1.1 it is desired that the ﬁrst
crack in the glass occurs after approximately one minute. Though it is possible to convert the measured
load-level to an equivalent one-minute strength, and therefore it is not necessary that each experiment
lasts exactly one minute.
As mentioned before the tests are performed as displacement-controlled and the loading rate of the test
machine is determined based on the expected displacement of the test specimen. To determine this a
150-beam tested in series B is chosen because it is the intermediate beam. The total displacement of the
beam at ﬁrst crack is determined from Equation (2.3.4) to 0.90 mm. Thereby it is decided to conduct the
test at a rate of 1 mm/min so ﬁrst crack appear within 1 minute. With this rate it was realized during
the experiments that ﬁrst crack initiated after approximately three minutes. As an equivalent one-minute-
strength can be computed this is not an important factor and the rate is not corrected throughout the
experiments.

Numerical Investigation
This section deals with an exploratory study of the beam behaviour when investigated as a FE-model.
Dimensions of glass, steel and adhesive are determined in Section 2, where also the glass in the un-cracked,
linear stage, was investigated by use of the ﬁnite element software Abaqus/Standard.
It is of great
importance to obtain knowledge about the post-crack behavior of the MRGB, therefore a new analysis is
performed. The beam is modelled as a composite beam with adhesive and steel attached to the bottom
face of glass, so it is possible to investigate the global behaviour of the composite beam. The un-cracked
linear stage is normally characterized by full interaction between the reinforcement and the glass. Since
cracks are not propagated in the glass before the tensile strength is exceeded, Abaqus/Standard is used
for this purpose to perform a static analysis. Non-linear eﬀects arise when cracks starts to propagate and
therefore the following investigation is performed in the dynamic solver Abaqus/Explicit. By accounting
for crack opening and propagation it is possible to investigate the post-cracking structural behaviour.
Challenges arise when using the dynamic solver and special consideration is required to approach a quasi-
static solution. The global response of the beam is compared to the analytical behaviour and validated
according to this. With a well-established and accurate numerical model it is possible to compare results
to the experimental obtained results. Since the numerical model considers all secondary eﬀects, such as
shear deformations and non-linear eﬀects in the steel during yielding, it provides a more detailed image of
the behaviour. Therefore numerical modelling has many advantages and with a proper establishment it is
very beneﬁcial compared to analytical calculations.
Careful considerations is given to the explicit dynamic modelling as it is aimed to obtain a quasi-static
response and not a dynamic. Throughout the investigation it is seen that compromises with mesh, time and
mass scaling need to be taken in order to achieve an economical solution which approaches the quasi-static
response. All possible eﬀects are diﬃcult to evaluate, therefore the investigation is only exploratory.
Numerical modelling of mechanically reinforced glass beams has been investigated before, where an exhaus-
tive research of the inﬂuence of diﬀerent parameters have been performed in [Bedon & Louter, 2014a]. In
this project only a few parameters are investigated, those which are not investigated are based on previous
research, primarily [Bedon & Louter, 2014a], [Pelfrene et al, 2013] og [Bedon & Louter, 2014b].

Brittle Cracking was originally developed to use for concrete.
Though previous investigations reveal that it is useful for other brittle materials, like glass, if it is well-

where the failure stress and the fracture energy are implemented. Fracture energy is deﬁned as the amount

4.1.3
Quasi-static Response
As mentioned the dynamic solver Abaqus/Explicit is applied to the analysis, since non-linear eﬀects
appear when the glass starts to develop cracks. As the solver normally is used for dynamic problems
eﬀort is made in order to obtain a quasi-static response, so the simulation reproduce the experiments. The
load is applied as displacement-controlled with smooth amplitude, 225 mm from the symmetry-line. The
displacement is not the important factor for the structural performance of the beam if applied properly.
If the displacement is applied so slowly that the response approaches quasi-static, it does not change the
results, only the length of the yielding trajectory. Additionally it is necessary to have a displacement that
can cause fracture of the glass. An investigation of the inﬂuence of the displacement on the structural
response is performed in Appendix D.1.1.
From Equation (2.3.4) the total beam displacements after yielding are estimated to 4.15 mm, 2.60 mm and
1.87 mm for a 100-beam, 150-beam and 200-beam respectively. Thereby the applied displacements are 4
mm, 3 mm and 2 mm to ensure a suﬃcient amount of yielding and still keep computational costs low.
Other values of the displacement are investigated but it is concluded that it only inﬂuenced the length of
the yielding trajectory, within the tested limits.
The time step size in the explicit analysis is controlled by the smallest element in the mesh and not the
average size of elements as in the implicit analysis. This makes the computational costs very extensive for
dynamic analyses and calculations are slowed down signiﬁcantly compared to static analyses. The running
time is increased when all spatial eﬀects are accounted for. The time period of the model need to be of
the same magnitude as in the experiments to obtain a quasi-static solution, but this is computationally
impractical to model as it would require a very large number of increments. Therefore it is of great interest
to speed up the calculations but without compromising the quasi-static response or reﬁnement of mesh.

mass scaling, which is an automated procedure in Abaqus where the density of the smallest elements in

and it demands the user to be aware. In order to obtain accurate and trustworthy results extreme mass
scaling is not always beneﬁcial and it is necessary to be very careful when applying it. The change of
the mass when applying mass scaling causes the internal velocity, wave speed, to speed up and thereby
approach a dynamic solution. Thus the model demands an increased time period in order to approach the
quasi-static solution again. Thereby the total time period needs to be increased before the real time from
the experiments again are reproduced in the simulation.
The time period is the total time duration of the model and should reﬂect at least the experimental time
duration, and when applying mass scaling a longer duration is required to obtain a quasi-static response,
as explained above. Herein the main problem of explicit dynamic modelling emerges; the objective is to
have the shortest time period, where it still corresponds to the experimental duration. Additionally the
inertia forces should still be negligible so the quasi-static solution is valid.

response where inertial forces are negligible. This is done by tentative checking the energy balance through-

4.2.2
Troubleshooting
In the following a comprehensive discussion of the results and approach is performed and the ﬁnal choice
of parameters is described.
Careful consideration has been made about the balance between the mass scaling and mesh in order to
obtain satisfactory accurate results with an economical solution.
If mass scaling was increased it was
seen that distortion of the elements in the model were increased and large pieces were deleted from the
mesh. In contrast to this, less mass scaling increased the simulation time exponentially. When the element
size were decreased it was seen that ﬁner crack patterns were obtained. Awareness about the signiﬁcant
increase in computational cost and running time, when element size decreases even by a small factor, was
the limiting factor. Due to limitations in the time schedule of the project a limited mesh pattern have
been applied and further investigation into this ﬁeld is recommended. By modelling with ﬁner meshes
it is possible to achieve a much ﬁner crack pattern. The application of diﬀerent mesh schemes resulted
in changes in the crack pattern as visualized in Figure 4.9, where crack patterns for two diﬀerent mesh
schemes are presented. The change in crack patterns caused by the change in the mesh scheme is due to
element deletion, where mesh dependency always will be present.

mesh schemes lead additionally to great element distortion and thereby greater changes in the structural
response.

no further analysis is performed with this amount of elements. It could have been beneﬁcial to perform an
analysis where the elastic stage is performed in a standard static step and then imported to the dynamic
step. The excessive computational costs would then have been reduced allowing the mesh to be optimized
and reﬁned or mass scaling reduced further.

Partial Conclusion
With the numerical modelling performed in the previous it is revealed that it is possible to obtain a
post-crack behaviour of the brittle material glass. Eﬀorts are made in choosing the right mesh scheme,
approaching quasi-static response, applying mass scaling and hence ﬁnding a satisfactory balance between

Finally the FE-model is validated to analytical predictions from Section 2, consequently it is concluded that
the numerical simulations exhibit good correlation with the analytical predictions for all beams modelled,

modelling. It might be possible to obtain similar results with satisfying accordance to analytical predictions

Throughout the experiments certain tendencies of the behaviour of the MRGB subjected to 4-point-
bending are identiﬁed.
All beams showed extremely ductile behaviour except one beam, which failed
shortly after ﬁrst crack appeared before obtaining the yielding trajectory (B150_2). The three stages are
easily recognized on the load-displacement plots, despite a few beams which reached yielding just after
ﬁrst crack causing the un-cracked stage to be almost absent. A couple of the beams had a smaller crack
in the elastic phase, which did not aﬀect the stiﬀness of the beams (B150_1 and C100_3). The chosen
design of the MRGB determined in Section 2 causes the beams to be at risk of anchorage failure according
to Equation (1.3.12). During the experiments it is observed that this is not an issue and it supports the
statement that the formulas for anchorage failure, based on the design formulas, are very conservative.
The inlet to the linear elastic curve from each structural response is due to settlements of the experimental
setup in the test machine during loading and it has no inﬂuence on the stiﬀness of the beams. It is seen
that the beams within the same series have a fairly equal behaviour, though minor deviations are found in
the stiﬀness and more excessive deviations are seen in the initial breakage load, Fcrack, and corresponding
displacement, ucrack. This is explained by the tensile strength, which is strongly dependent on impurities
in the glass and may vary signiﬁcantly. The tensile strength have been measured in the interval of 20-100
MPa [Nielsen, 2009, p.7]. This explains the diﬀerence in the initial glass breakage load for beams tested
in the same test setup. As expected it is noticed that taller beams have a greater stiﬀness, due to the
amount of material. Diﬀerences in the experimental bending stiﬀness are observed and some beams have a
noticeable deviation from the average, which is explained by experimental insecurities. Further discussions
of the stiﬀness and reasons for the deviations are performed in Section 5.2.

increasing load. The deviation between the results is approximately equal to 10 %. The experiments were
performed in the same loading rate and initial breakage of all beams occurred after ≈180 seconds. Since
the deviation between the test strength and the one-minute strength is of equal magnitude it is irrelevant
if the results are based on the equivalent one-minute strength or the equivalent "three-minute strength",
as obtained in the experiments.
Investigation of Table 5.3 and Table 5.4 clarify tendencies in the test strength. If the test strength is
compared for the 100-beams tested in series A, B and C no clear correlation is shown. The same tendency
is observed for the 150-beams tested in A, B and C. However if the 100-beam, 150-beam and 200-beam are
compared in series A, a clear decrease in the test strength is observed for an increase in beam height and
thereby an increase in shear force. Additionally this accounts for series B and C. This may be caused by
the increase in shear for the taller cross-section. Thus clear correlation is seen between strength and shear
force when the beams are compared across heights, but not when compared across test series. It is noted
that for 200-beams only two comparable series are tested, so on the basis of this it is diﬃcult to clarify
all tendencies. The higher test strength of the smaller beams also explain the higher deviation between
experimental and numerical results for Fcrack which is observed for the smaller beams, Table 5.2.
The determined values of the test strengths are of relatively higher magnitude than the assumed 45 MPa
and this can explain the diﬀerence between numerical and experimental results. Some of the extra strength
might have been gained by adding the reinforcement with the adhesive layer. The epoxy closes small cracks
and impurities in the glass when it is applied to the bottom face. This provides the glass with additional
strength [Louter, 2011] though it is only a small contribution.
The lowest value for the real strength is found to be 50 MPa for a 200-beam test and from this it is
concluded that the assumption about a tensile strength of glass ftg = 45 MPa is acceptable as it is a

5.2.2
Investigation of Yielding Trajectory
In the previous section it is shown that an adjustment of the tensile strength improves the analytical
predictions and satisfying results were obtained. However the adjustment of the strength did not change
the yield force, which is relatively low compared to the magnitude of the experimental yield force, see
Table 5.2. It is investigated if the deviation is related to the increasing amount of shear in the beams.
In order to do so a yield force investigation is assessed in Appendix E.8, where the aim is to ﬁnd the
V/M ratio between shear force and moment and then ﬁnd the corresponding deviation ∆y between the
analytical yield force Fyield,a and the average experimental yield force Fyield,exp,av. The analytical yield
force is determined by use of the design formulas in Section 1.3 based on pure bending. When the height
of a beam increases, the capacity is increased and a higher shear force is registered and consequently a
higher V/M ratio. When the shear force increases it is expected that the analytical calculations results
in a higher deviation to the experimental research because the analytical calculations only account for
bending and not for shear. Therefore it is expected that an increase in V/M ratio causes an increase in
the deviation between the experimental and analytical yield force.

modelled with a width of 19 mm for all the materials, which also applies for the analytical calculations. The

is modelled with a uniform rectangular cross-section, where all materials have the same width of 19 mm.
This makes a diﬀerence in the numerical and analytical results compared to the experimental, because the
additional steel in the experiments may add extra yield strength to the beams. Furthermore the measure-
ments of all specimens revealed that the average height of the steel was 6.2 mm instead of 6 mm which is
declared by the supplier. It has been demonstrated through previous research in [Martens et al, 2016] and
[Louter et al, 2011a] that the reinforcement percentage has a great inﬂuence on the structural response of
reinforced glass beams. A higher percentage has a favourable eﬀect on the beam behaviour, where a higher
bending stiﬀness and post-breakage strength is achieved. To investigate the inﬂuence of the dimensions of
steel, a width of 20 mm and a height of 6.2 mm are implemented in the FE-model presented in Section 4.
Furthermore analytical calculations are performed by use of the new dimensions. Figure 5.16 presents the
structural response for B150_S along with the numerical and analytical results.

It is also of great importance to investigate the time aspect of the crack propagation (models and videos are

occur at the same time and only minor cracks are developed later. This is not the case for the experiments,
where only one crack is appearing at ﬁrst and a greater propagation of cracks are noticed during the

the value of the fracture energy parameter Gf, which can change the amount of cracks. It is very diﬃcult
to obtain similar crack patterns, but the accordance may have been better with another value of Gf. The
complication in obtaining similar crack patterns is probably caused by the mesh dependency, which make
the crack patterns very sensitive to the mesh and may change if the size of the elements or organization
are changed. Furthermore cracks in the experiments can occur caused by impurities in the glass or the
adhesive which is not accounted for in the numerical model. In the numerical model it is assumed that
the adhesive layer behaves linear-elastically, but it might have plastic properties in reality, which aﬀects
the crack patterns too. By increasing the time in the time period in the FE-model, cracks developed more
slowly and smooth throughout the response and not all cracks appeared at once. As also mentioned in
Section 4.1.3 this highlights the importance of increasing the time period when mass scaling is added. Less
energy is thereby released.
In the experiments it was observed that the initial crack developed several horizontal cracks throughout
the test. During the plastic deformation of the beams, cracks propagated from the main crack to smaller
horizontal cracks slowly but steady across the length of the beam. The new horizontal cracks formed from
the existing cracks diminished the height of the compression zone. These cracks propagated over a long
distance up to 300-400 mm. The amount of horizontal cracks in the numerical model is almost non-existing
and only some cracks developed shorter horizontal cracks from it, see Figure 5.19(b).
No cracks were detected outside the area of constant moment for both experimental and numerical crack
pattern. At some of the experimental beams cracks developed at the support due to stress concentrations
induced by the fork-bearings. Crushing of the glass were also observed at the load point due to stress
singularities, otherwise the glass were intact outside the loading area. The area of constant moment in
4-point bending is where the maximum moment appears, whereas the maximum shear appears outside
this area. This might explain that bending is the dimensional factor and that stresses are higher inside
the loads. The experimental crack patterns from all the beams are displayed in Appendix E.3.
Based on the comparison between numerical, analytical and experimental results by observing the response
it is concluded that a satisfying correlation is obtained, but only few similarities between the crack patterns
are achieved. Additional eﬀort in the work and investigation of the parameters and a ﬁner mesh scheme
may provide better results. It is also crucial to the diﬀerences in crack pattern that the FE-model assumes
completely symmetrical behaviour but as described in Section 5.1.2 it was only the experimental 100-beams
which actually revealed symmetrical behaviour. Therefore deviations between the experiments and the
numerical model are present.
Based on the crack patterns obtained by experiments and by numerical modeling it has not been possible
to detect any clear correlation to the inﬂuence of shear.

5.3
Source of Errors
There are many factors that can aﬀect the beam behaviour and several errors and uncertainties exist in
experiments, including deviations in the design of the beams and the experimental execution.
Related to the beam design, surface ﬂaws and bad polishing interrupts the intact glass surface. Even
small ﬂaws and impurities, that can not be detected by the naked eye can inﬂuence the strength and
the appearance of cracks. The conditions of the glass elements used for the experiments are presented in
Appendix C.5. The composite beam also showed deviations mainly due to diﬃculties in the composition,
consequently a varying height of the adhesive layer is measured. The height of the adhesive does not have a
noticeable inﬂuence on the global behaviour of the beam and anchorage failure is still prevented. However it
is a problem when the adhesive layer is of unequal height throughout the length of the beam, see Appendix
C.6 for average heights of the adhesive. The conditions of the composite beam is also important in regards
to air voids in the adhesive and sloping beams, see Appendix C.5. Other issues are related to the test
conditions and curing time of the adhesive layer, see Appendix C.4, where it is concluded that properties
of the adhesive may not have been reached. Earlier investigations show that longer curing time is needed
before reaching the given properties from the supplier. In this research the curing time was at least 72
hours, but since the adhesive is not tested it is possible that 72 hours is not a suﬃcient curing time. It was
not possible to obtain an equal curing time of 72 hours for all beams. Consequently properties as tensile
shear strength may vary, if it is not fully gained after 72 hours and deviations occur when some beams
have cured for a longer time. The epoxy has a height of 0.5 mm instead of 1.6 mm, which might cause a
lower tensile shear strength, as the value only is provided for this height of the adhesive [DELO, 2017].
Mounting of the strain gauges is a diﬃcult procedure which demands a lot of precision. The measurements
solely depends on the strain gauges and how they are mounted and therefore it is possible that some
deviations will be present in these measurements.
Even though the beams, potentiometers and supports were mounted equally some uncertainties exists
with the positioning of the test machinery. Additionally it is observed that the machine loaded unevenly
causing the cross-head to start loading in the right side of the beam before the left side. There are also
some general uncertainties related to the use of potentiometers and it is observed during the experiments,
that they are extremely sensitive. Finally it was noticed, after the execution of some experiments, that the
base of the support fork bearing was pressed together. The test conducted on the 200-beams measured
loads of almost 110 kN. This is a fairly high load causing the soft material in the fork bearing to undergo
permanent deformations.
5.4
Partial conclusion
Throughout the experiments satisfying results of 30 test specimens tested in 4-point bending have been
achieved where it has been demonstrated that the reinforced glass beams have a ductile behaviour and
exhibit good post-breakage behaviour. Diﬀerent researches have been performed in order to investigate
the inﬂuence of shear. Clear correlation between height of glass and strength was observed and it might be
caused by the increased shear force in taller beams. Regarding the test series A, B and C it was diﬃcult
to conclude anything speciﬁc caused by lack in test results and inconclusive tendencies. It was concluded
that more investigation and experimental testing is needed. Eﬀort have been made in investigating the
crack pattern, but the results did not reveal any clear tendencies regarding the inﬂuence of shear.

Conclusion
Throughout the work of this thesis a comprehensive study of the behaviour of the mechanically reinforced
glass beams in shear has been completed. The study was divided into three main parts; analytical predic-
tions, numerical modelling and experimental tests, where it was aimed to investigate the global behaviour
of the mechanically reinforced glass beams. The dimensions of the test specimens and the test setup was
chosen to ensure that increased shear forces were present in the beam. This enabled the exploration of the
beam behaviour under the impact of shear.
From the analytical predictions, achieved by application of the design formulas, the behaviour of the beams
were determined in relation to bending. From here it was evident that only one beam height was designed
as being normal-reinforced, while the remaining beams were under-reinforced.
A FE-model of the beams were modelled in Abaqus/Explicit to account for non-linear eﬀects after the ﬁrst
crack evolved. An extensive eﬀort was made to obtain the most reliable model with a quasi-static response,
by attempting to use various mesh schemes and diﬀerent values of mass scaling. The model revealed a
satisfying post-breakage behaviour. It was concluded that, by comparison to analytical predictions from
the design formulas, a good correlation between the results were obtained. Eﬃciently causing the model
to be veriﬁed against analytical calculations. It was also observed that the numerical model provided a
more detailed image of the behaviour compared to the analytical.
A post-breakage ductile behaviour were achieved for the beams experimentally tested in 4-point bending
and a good correlation between the beams in each series with the diﬀerent heights were achieved. Pre-
damage outside the area of constant moment, where the maximum shear is present, were applied to a test
series. It was concluded that it was possible to maintain the post-breakage capacity even though the beam
was damaged before loading, since it did not aﬀect the global response of the beams signiﬁcantly.
The experimental test results were compared to the numerical model and the analytical predictions. The
comparison revealed deviations between the experimentally and numerically obtained results regarding
the bending stiﬀness, the initial glass breakage load and the yielding trajectory. As deviations in bending
stiﬀness were small and sporadic, it might have been due to experimental uncertainties. In order to clarify

the deviation at ﬁrst cracking condition an investigation of the strength were performed. The test strength

Comparison of the experimental and numerical/analytical results also revealed extensive deviations in the
yielding trajectory. Based on exhaustive investigations, hypotheses were outlined to clarify reasons for the
deviations. The FE-model was modelled with smaller dimensions of the steel than the actual dimensions
used in the experiments. By increasing the reinforcement percentage in the numerical model, corresponding
to the actual dimensions, it was concluded that the deviation between numerical and experimental results
decreased.
Additionally it is argued that the determined yield strength may have been closer to the
ultimate strength of steel due the strain-hardening. By estimating a lower and a upper limit based on the
experimentally obtained compressive strain in glass and the height of the compression zone it is evident
that the yield strength was of higher magnitude than the tensile steel test indicated. It was concluded that
the higher obtained yield strength in experiments caused the extensive deviation. Furthermore the V/M
ratio between shear force and moment was examined as a function of the deviation between the yield force
from experiments and from analytical predictions. From this it was observed that the highest deviation
in yield forces were found for the tallest beams. This result indicates that the response from the beams
were inﬂuenced by the presence of shear, since the analytical calculations are based on bending and the
deviation to these are greater for a larger amount of shear. However the examination was based on few
measuring points and more data is needed in order to quantify any certain tendencies.
Through experiments it is concluded that bending was the determinate factor in regards to crack devel-
opment and failure mechanisms. The numerical and experimental crack patterns were compared and it
was concluded that the FE-model from Abaqus/Explicit was successfully modelled to achieve reliable
crack patterns. Nevertheless the occurrence of diﬀerences was inevitable. A greater amount of cracks was
obtained in the numerical model compared to the experiments. This may be caused by the fracture energy
parameter Gf, where further investigation is needed. Regarding the numerical modelling it was evident
that the simulations caused extensively computationally costs and it was concluded that more eﬀort in
ﬁnding the right balance between the investigated parameters would lead to more satisfying results.
Summarizing all the results obtained through this work it is revealed that more research is needed in this
ﬁeld. It is concluded that taller beams may be aﬀected by the an increased amount of shear, but it was not
possible to determine any conclusive tendencies when comparing across the test series, nor was it possible
to establish tendencies in the crack pattern regarding the inﬂuence of shear. Based on this investigation the
formerly derived design formulas are still applicable for the purpose of designing mechanically reinforced
glass beams even when increased shear is present. However, additional research may reveal tendencies
leading to modiﬁcations of the formulas.

Proposal for Future Work
Ideas to additional work in the ﬁeld are discussed in the following. Due to limitations of the project many
aspects of the mechanically reinforced glass beam have not been investigated. It is recommended that the
absence of these are investigated in future work in order to improve and continue the work performed in
this project.
It is recommended to examine the composition of the MRGB. In this project the composition of the
MRGB was challenging and it was diﬃcult to obtain an equal height of the adhesive. Thus it could be
interesting to investigate the eﬀect of the height of the adhesive for the structural behaviour of the MRGB.
In correlation to this a rethinking of the practical composition of the MRGB could be advantageous for
future investigations in order to obtain a composite beam as uniform and homogeneous as possible.
All tests were performed in a Instron-machine with a load capacity of 100 kN. As the 200-beams with a
distance of 620 mm between the loads were able to carry 112 kN, according to the design formulas, these
beams were not able to fail in the testing machine and pre-damage experiments were performed instead. It
is of great interest to test the 200-beams in series C as the other beams. Testing of more beams in diﬀerent
heights would be valuable, since some of the investigations revealed inconclusive results. This highlights
the importance of testing more series in order to clarify how great the inﬂuence of shear is on the results.
It was stated that pre-damage did not diminish the post-breakage behaviour of the beams. Only two
beams were tested for pre-damage, and it is of interest to investigate this further.
The beams were
only pre-damaged with a crack outside the constant moment and it could be interesting to investigate
the outcome of pre-damage applied inside the area of constant moment, which may aﬀect the structural
response of the beams in another way. The fact that pre-damage do not aﬀect the post-breakage behaviour
is very relevant regarding constructions. This allows the structure to be damaged, unloaded and loaded
again without losing capacity. This is very useful as it is not always possible to detect and repair the
cracks immediately after occurrence, thereby the knowledge of pre-damage provides the structure with an
extra safety margin. This knowledge is of great interest and therefore additional research in this ﬁeld is
deﬁnitely needed.
In relation to FE-modelling the time limit of the thesis was the determinate factor. Further development
of the models may reveal improved correlation to the experimental results. This regards mesh reﬁnement,
application of mass scaling factor and time period. For further investigation it is recommended to examine
the fracture energy parameter and include a more detailed behaviour of the adhesive as this will inﬂuence
the obtained crack pattern.
Finally it is recommended that more research is performed on the impact of shear. Additional tests may
reveal more evident results and by these it might be possible to make modiﬁcations to the design formulas
for pure bending so these accounts for the contribution of shear.

C.1
Pre-damage Test Setup
Due to limitations in the test machine, which only were able to load up 100 kN 13, it is not possible to test
the 200-beams in series C as they theoretically will reach an initial breakage load of 112 kN. Accordingly
it is chosen to test these beams in another way denoted series D.
The aim of test series D is to investigate the inﬂuence of cracks outside the constant moment area, where
the maximum shear force appears. Therefore pre-damage was applied to the beams. This was done by
drilling a hole in the tension zone with a length of 2/3 of the width of the beam. The hole was drilled
outside the constant moment area in order to ensure that the crack would be formed outside the area where
the maximum shear force is present. By drilling a hole in the glass the boundary stresses are increased up
to a maximum of three times the value of the applied stress [Sandford, 2003, p. 39]. Therefore there is a
high probability of crack formation originating from the hole when the beam is loaded. After the hole was
drilled, the beam was placed in the 4-point bending machine. The smallest possible distance of 190 mm
between the loads was chosen. The loads were then placed 95 mm on each side of the drilled hole and the
beam was compressed asymmetrically until the ﬁrst crack appeared. The test setup for the pre-damage of
the beams D200_1 and D200_2 is illustrated in Figure C.1.

C.3.1
Inverse Analysis
The aim of an inverse problem is to determine the unknown factors that produced a known set of results.
Section C.3 describes how stress-strain performance curves for steel were obtained through experiments.
Thus the objective of this section is to use these results to reconstruct the yield strength and true plastic
strain of steel and implement these in Abaqus/Standard.
Firstly the steel specimen illustrated in Figure C.5 is modelled in Abaqus/Standard. Then a trial and
error method is utilized, where a value of the yield strength of steel and a corresponding true plastic strain
is guessed. Subsequently the stress-strain curve from the model is checked against the stress-strain curves
obtained from the experiments. Modiﬁcations to the ﬁrst guess are made until a ﬁtted curve is obtained.
When a satisfactory ﬁt is achieved, the yield strength and the plastic strain can be implemented as material
parameters in the other numerical models presented in Chapter 4.

C.9
Calibration of Potentiometers
A calibration of the potentiometers is necessary before the beams are tested. The following section brieﬂy
describes the potentiometers and how the calibration is performed.
In the test setup two potentiometers of the type TRS 50 are used. These are connected to a data logger
that collect the data during the duration of the experiment. The data logger provides the potentiometers
with a voltage Usupply around 5 V. This voltage is reduced by a displacement in the potentiometer deﬁned
as U. The voltage varies linearly with the displacement in the device. When the potentiometer is fully
compressed it returns a voltage of U ≃0 to the data logger and when it is uncompressed it returns a
voltage of U ≃Usupply.
In order to perform the calibration the potentiometer is positioned in a setup where it is possible to create a
known displacement by use of a digital vernier caliper and subsequently read oﬀthe corresponding voltage.
From this a linear correlation is obtained. Likewise the supply voltage is noted. Figure C.12 illustrates
the test setup for the calibration.

E.1
Processing of the Results
The following section provides a detailed description of how the data is processed after testing of each
specimen. The outcome of the test from the test machine and the potentiometers is two separate .xlsx
ﬁles. The two data-ﬁles are compiled into one .xlsx ﬁle, where the data from the potentiometers and the
test machine are located in diﬀerent sheets. Thus all the measurements and information regarding each
test specimen are gathered in this ﬁle. The measured data are placed in Annex. The two displacement
gauges have been calibrated according to the standard procedure for this equipment, see Appendix C.9
for the calibration and further description of the potentiometers. Processing of the data is performed
in Matlab where the script Data-processing.m completes all the calculations accompanied by some simple

E.4
Photoelasticity of Glass
An experimental method for determination of the stress distribution in glass is photoelasticity.
This
method provides a satisfying insight in the stress concentrations developed in the glass during loading,
thus it is possible to observe the critical stress points [Aben & Guillemet, 1993]. In order to examine the
stress trajectories in the MRGB two pieces of polaroid ﬁlm is mounted on each side of the beam during
loading and the stress distribution is observed and photographed during the test.
Figure E.7 illustrates the stresses in the glass for beam B100_3 just after loading has started, where it is
seen that the stresses in the MRGB are formed between the fork bearing and just under the load. The
stresses are quite evenly distributed between those points. Subsequently Figure E.8 illustrates the further
development of stresses in the glass, where it is seen that stress concentrations are developed at the two
skrews from the fork bearing. The stresses in the beam just before the ﬁrst crack appear is presented in
Figure E.9. The stress distribution after the ﬁrst crack has appeared and just before the second crack
has formed is illustrated in Figure E.10. Finally the stress trajectories after the second crack occurred
are illustrated in Figure E.11. For comparison, the numerical crack pattern with Von Mises stresses for a
B100-beam is presented in Figure E.12. The modelling of the FE-model is described in Section 4.
