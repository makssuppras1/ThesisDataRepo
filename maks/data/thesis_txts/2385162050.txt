Impacts of global warming on plankton dynamics
by Camila SERRA-POMPEI
Direct impacts of temperature derived from climate change are still not well under-
stood at the planktonic community level. Changes in community composition can
alter ecosystem processes such as carbon sequestration or transfer to higher trophic
levels. In this master thesis I will assess how effects of temperature at the cellular level
can scale to ecosystem processes. I start with the hypothesis that a rise in temperature
will increase primary production, but will also enhance the effects of the microbial
loop, which will reduce carbon export to depth and transfer to upper trophic levels.
To tackle this issue, I ﬁrst performed a literature review of the temperature effects of
global warming on the plankton community. Next, I developed a trait-based model of
the pelagic microbial community, where effects of temperature on traits are explicitly
represented. The biological model was then implemented in a 1D physical model to
simulate different trophic scenarios. I indeed ﬁnd that an increase in temperatures
together with limitation of resources reduces carbon export and trophic transfer efﬁ-
ciencies of ecosystems. While when resources are sufﬁcient, temperature has an over-
all positive effect in these processes.

1.1
Introduction
Aquatic systems are currently in a transition state due to, among others, climate change.
Some of the main impacts are caused by a generalized rise in temperature, with di-
rect consequences both in organism’s physiology and the physical environment (e.g.
variations in the mixed layer depth or its stability), the latter altering in-situ resource
availability. Direct effects of temperature partly results in an acceleration of metabolic
activities responsible for both growth and maintenance. How the interplay between
resource availability, organisms functional responses and consequent interaction be-
tween groups remains complex. Arising questions such as whether a shift in species
will occur, how will primary production respond, and how will the upper trophic
levels and the biological pump be affected.
There are two main reasons to worry about how climate change might alter plank-
ton community. In a ﬁrst instance, phytoplankton is the base of the food web, any
change on its structure might affect the trophic transfer toward upper levels, having
a consequence in ﬁsh stocks or other marine-associated macro fauna. Second, plank-
ton are key players in the draw-down of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere toward
the ocean depth, as phytoplankton contributes to approximately 50% of atmospheric
carbon ﬁxation, adding to the biological pump.
Here a literature review is presented with the aim to capture the impacts that have
been observed on the pelagic planktonic community, and associated biogeochemical
processes, due to global warming. Impacts of climate change other than tempera-
ture are omitted, as it is not the main interest of this project. The review is divided
between indirect and direct impacts of global warming on marine unicellular organ-
isms. We ﬁrst make a brief introduction to ecosystem processes. Next, observations
mainly attributed to indirect effects of temperature are presented. Then, direct effects
of temperature are explained of the following carbon pools and physiological and
ecosystemic processes: primary production, including photosynthesis and nutrient
uptake; secondary production, with respiration and predation; bacterial production
and interactions with the dissolved organic matter pool; and ﬁnally vertical carbon
export to depth.

1.2
Energy ﬂuxes in the ecosystem
Organic carbon is ﬁrst introduced in the system through photosynthesis by primary
producers. Gross primary production (GPP) is the sum of carbon ﬁxed by all the
organisms of an ecosystem, where part of this energy is respired by autotrophs them-
selves for maintenance of its metabolism. Net primary production (NPP) is the differ-
ence between GPP and respiration of these autotrophs, and so is the net gain of carbon
from the ensemble of primary producers of the ecosystem, and is consequently the
energy available for all the upper trophic levels (consumers), which are heterotrophs
(herbivores, carnivores and detritivores). Another group of heterotrophic organisms
is formed by bacteria, which feed on dead organic matter and are able to convert it
back to inorganic compounds (remineralize), which can ﬁnally be utilized again by
autotrophs. In each organism, part of the available energy is used for metabolic main-
tenance (i.e. respired) and is dissipated as heat in the environment, which makes it
unavailable for other organisms. This loss of energy partly determines trophic trans-
fer efﬁciency between two trophic levels, and how much of the energy available in the
ﬁrst trophic level reaches higher ones. Another exit of carbon from aquatic systems
is its export towards the deeper layers. Export efﬁciency is tightly linked to NPP and
respiration rates among others. Temperature inﬂuences all of these processes, but each
can respond in a different way, meaning that a warmer environment could disrupt the
current balance of ecosystems.
1.3
Indirect impacts of temperature
The observed negative relation between Chl-a and sea surface temperature (SST) has
often been attributed to the intensiﬁed stratiﬁcation that comes with the rise in atmo-
spheric temperature. In the tropics and mid-latitudes, this physical factor exacerbates
the already present nutrient stress, negatively affecting productivity (Doney, 2006).
Conversely, microalgae in polar regions could be beneﬁted, since a shoaling of the
mixed layer (MLD) can increment the mean integrated PAR. Boyce, Lewis, and Worm
(2010) gathered in-situ globally distributed chl-a measurements and satellite data of
the upper ocean from over the past century, and centered on the correlation between
chl-a and MLD, SST and wind intensity at 10m. In concordance with Doney (2006),
and attributed to the same cause, negative primary production (PP) trends were found
at low latitudes, and a strong positive trend at higher ones. They however concluded
that overall chl-a has been declining at a rate of about 1% per year relative to the global
median chlorophyll concentration. Behrenfeld et al. (2006) also noted a prolonged de-
cline in NPP derived from chl-a satellite data during the last years also attributed to
intensiﬁed stratiﬁcation.
In high latitudes other factors can alter MLD stability and light availability, among
those, sea ice thawing plays one of the most important roles in the transition that po-
lar oceans are experiencing. A decrease in the ice cover can trigger phytoplankton
blooms beforehand due to light readily available sooner in the season, or extended
bloom duration due to to longer free-ice periods (Kahru et al., 2011). On the other
hand, longer periods of surface waters being exposed to the atmosphere together with
the higher concurrence of storms, leads to a deepening of the MLD reducing over-
all production (Montes-Hugo et al., 2009). In western Antarctic Peninsula, Venables,
Clarke, and Meredith (2013) noted that deepening of the winter mixed layer due to
reduced sea-ice diminished the magnitude of the phytoplankton spring bloom (even
though the MLD when the bloom occurred did not vary). Such was attributed to a

less stable water column, and so less light and iron available in the mixed layer. Still,
impacts of climate change in polar regions are diverse and also diverge among regions
(Deppeler and Davidson, 2017).
Nevertheless, Chl-a time series should be carefully treated, as chlorophyll is not
necessarily a good proxy of biomass or production, since phytoplankton can accli-
mate. When it comes to Chl-a, cells can photoacclimate, that is, pigments can be
up- or down-regulated depending on light availability (Geider, MacIntyre, and Kana,
1996), which is predicted to vary with the previously mentioned changes in MLD.
Behrenfeld et al. (2016) revised chl-a satellite data and found that the main observed
changes in chl-a with temperature were due to photo-acclimation, and that akin was
not necessarily accompanied by an increase/decrease in biomass or primary produc-
tion. By applying a new algorithm that considered photoacclimation, they concluded
that more than 85% of the chl-a observed anomaly was due to this factor, and not
to proportional variations in production. Metabolic processes are often omitted in
plankton models, and examples like Behrenfeld and colleagues’s article (2015) rises its
importance. In fact, despite agreements for some regions, overall primary production
predictions largely vary and even show opposite signs, where some predict an overall
decrease in PP (e.g. Marinov, Doney, and Lima 2010; Steinacher et al. 2010) or increase
(e.g. Sarmiento et al. 2004).
Reduction in cell size has been another subject of debate, where the concern comes
from the fact that a reduction in cell-size of the phytoplankton community can nega-
tively affect the ﬂow of energy both up on the food web and down to deeper layers.
Regarding this matter there are two parties that differ in the cause. On one side, size-
temperature theory declares that phytoplankton will decrease by a mean of 2,5% per
each degree of increase (Atkinson, Ciotti, and Montagnes, 2003), whereas others de-
clare that direct effects of temperature are not the cause, but rather the intensiﬁed
nutrient limitation due to MLD variations. Maranón et al. (2012) arrived to the latter
conclusion by means of a meta-analysis of the phytoplankton size structure for cold,
temperate and warm waters, and refused the size-temperature theory, stating that
”temperature won’t have a single universal effect on phytoplankton cell size”. To this
article a response was made by López-Urrutia and Morán (2015), where they revised
the exact same data-set used by Maranón et al. (2012), and declared that these authors
failed to properly separate the effects of temperature and resources in their analysis,
and concluded that the higher the temperature, the higher the contribution of smaller
cells to total biomass.
All in all, effects of global warming seem to affect primary production in different
ways depending on the region, being some of them negatively affected, as increased
stratiﬁcation exacerbates nutrient competition and limitation in areas that already suf-
fer these conditions annually (e.g. subtropical gyres). Conversely, other regions could
have their net production enhanced as better conditions for growth can be achieved,
such is the case of polar regions and for examples increased light availability. There
is also a generalized consensus that it is most probable that a warmer environment
comes with a decreased cell-size community, even though the reason being unclear.
Nonetheless, the majority of studies mentioned up until now mainly center on indirect
effects that come with global warming, i.e how the physical environment is being al-
tered, and which are the consequences of such environmental disturbances to biomass
an production of microalgae, being direct effects of temperature often omitted.

1.4
Direct impacts of temperature
In this section we will go through the main processes responsibled for ecosystem func-
tioning and how temperature affects them at different levels. We ﬁrst brieﬂy review
effects of temperature on metabolic rates in general, and a broad theory used to inter-
pret and model these effects: metabolic theory of ecology. Next we review primary,
secondary and bacterial production and associated processes. Temperature effects on
carbon sequestration is ﬁnally reviewed.
1.4.1
Metabolism
Metabolism is the set of chemical reactions involved in the processing of energy and
materials to maintain life. Organisms take resources from the environment; convert
them to biologically available forms; allocate this energy to enhance ﬁtness for the con-
straints imposed by the environment as to ensure survival, growth and reproduction;
and ﬁnally excrete altered forms back to the environment. Both autotrophic and het-
erotrophic planktonic organisms, due to their hectothermic character, can be affected
by temperature to a greater or lesser degree. Response of single species differ to the
response of the bulk community. As a result of evolutionary adaptation, each species
possess a temperature optima for growth and for other physiological traits, and so
effects of temperature on growth are featured by an unimodal curve (Edwards et al.,
2016). On the other hand, when taking the whole community level, maximum growth
rates respond to temperature with a Q10 of approximately 2 (Eppley, 1972; Edward
et al, 2016). In biology, the change in rate of reactions of metabolic processes are of-
ten described by the Q10 temperature coefﬁcient, factor by which a rate of a reaction
increases with a 10 ◦C increase in temperature.
Interestingly, biological processes in the cellular level also have differing temper-
ature dependencies. In microbes for example, maximal growth rate could be settled
by the biosynthetic rate, i.e. the rate at which biomass is transformed for cell growth.
This maximal growth rate however only applies when resources are plenty, which
is rather the exception than the norm in natural environments. Consequently, when
resources are limiting, temperature dependence of growth is settled by the balance
between temperature dependence of the biological process responsible for the uptake
of the limiting resource and for maintenance. In marine microbes, such could trans-
late into four main processes: photosynthesis, diffusive uptake of dissolved particles,
uptake and ingestion of food, and respiration.
Metabolic theory of ecology
Metabolic theory of ecology (MTE; Gillooly et al. (2001) and Brown et al. (2004)) has
attempted to cover the theoretical lack of understanding that temperature has on
metabolic processes. MTE relates organisms’ metabolism with cell size and tempera-
ture. To describe the effects of temperature the Van’t Hoff-Arrhenius equation (Arrhe-
nius, 1915) is used:
B = b0e−E/kT
Where B is a given metabolic rate, b0 is a normalization factor which depends on body
size, E is the activation energy of the given process, T is the absolute temperature (in
Kelvin), and k the Boltzman constant. Relation of this equation with the Q10 factor is
discussed in Gillooly et al. (2001).
An statement that has become of interest in plankton ecology is the prediction
made by MTE, where heterotrophic processes respond differently to autotrophic ones.

MTE predicts an activation energy of 0.65 eV for heterotrophs, while for autotrophs
the activation energy is close to 0.32 eV (Allen, Gillooly, and Brown, 2005). Conse-
quently, heterotrophic processes would respond more strongly to temperature than
autotrophic ones. This assumption has subsequently been applied to ecosystem pro-
cesses such as metabolic balance of the oceans (López-Urrutia et al., 2006), which is
the ratio of ecosystem respiration rate to gross primary production; prey-predator in-
teractions (López-Urrutia, 2008); or carbon export to depth (Cael and Follows, 2016).
Recently however, this assumption has been reanalyzed by Chen and Laws (2017),
who compared estimates of activation energies of phytoplankton (sorted by taxon)
and heterotrophic protists from two experimental data-sets. Their results show that
activation energies of phytoplankton strongly depended on community composition.
In the pooled data-set, activation energies for phytoplankton was underestimated due
to differential growth rates of taxa (slow growing cyanobacteria v.s. fast growing di-
atoms). Individual median activation energy values from experiments did not differ
between phytoplankton and heterotrophic protists. The authors suggested that esti-
mations of activation energies from ﬁeld data-sets could be biased by effects of covari-
ates (e.g. resources availability or thermal acclimation).
1.4.2
Primary production
Maximum growth rate of the bulk phytoplankton community is exponentially and
positively correlated with temperature, where Eppley (1972) found a Q10 = 1.88 by
compiling data of marine phytoplankton from temperate and tropical waters. This
Q10 value was later conﬁrmed by Bissinger, Montagnes, and Atkinson (2008) who ex-
tended the data-set. Nevrtheless, growth is ruled by many other metabolic processes,
and maximum growth rate is only achieved when resources are optimal, which is
most often not the case in natural systems. In phytoplankton, growth depends on
the uptake of light (through photosynthesis) and nutrients (through diffusive uptake).
Hence in this section we will go through the different effects that temperature has on
these processes.
Photsynthesis
Light–limited growth is much less sensitive to temperature than at saturating condi-
tions. Initial slope of the photosynthesis-irradiance curve is temperature insensitive
(Post, Wit, and Mur, 1985), while maximum photosynthetic rate (i.e. Rubisco capac-
ity), is temperature dependent, with a Q10 of approximately 2 (Davison, 1991). In
natural environments, if not limited by light, photosynthetic rate tends to be limited
by Rubisco activity (Rogers and Humphries, 2000). Photosynthesis can be divided in
two processes: light and dark reactions. The light reaction converts light into reduc-
ing power and metabolic energy. These are then used in the dark reaction to ﬁx CO2
and convert it to organic matter. Carbon dioxide is ﬁxed in the Calvin cycle. The ﬁrst
step of this cycle is the carboxylase reaction catalyzed by the enzyme Rubisco, where
CO2 is reduced. The Rubisco enzyme (ribulose bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase)
has a dual function: oxygenase-carboxylase; the balance of which is established by the
pO2/pCO2 ratio in the enzyme active site: high ratios promote the oxygenase function
and low ones promote the carboxylase function. The oxygenase function is known
as photorespiration and forms CO2, resulting in considerable carbon losses in plants.
Nevertheless, the role of photorespiration is mainly to release excess reductants as-
sociated with the photoreactions, and have little to do with organic production or
==PAGE:18==

and litterature review
decomposition (Falkowski and Raven, 2013). Photorespiration tends to be more sen-
sitive to temperature than carboxylation, hence, at high pO2/pCO2 quantum yield
would actually decline with temperature, whereas when pO2/pCO2 is low, quantum
yield is practically temperature insensitive (Farquhar, Caemmerer, and Berry, 1980),
value then mainly depending in pigments concentration. To ensure the dominance
of the carboxylase function, algae have carbon-concentrating mechanisms to maintain
an elevated CO2 in the cells where Rubisco is located (Raven, 1997).
Nutrients uptake
Nutrient uptake by osmotrophs is limited by the rate at which diffusion delivers
molecules to the cell-surface, which scales inversely with the squared radius of the
cell (Munk and Riley, 1952; Kiørboe, 2008). Consequently, larger cells are far less efﬁ-
cient at acquiring nutrients than smaller cells. Temperature effects on nutrient uptake
rates are often either not represented, or are included in maximum uptake rates with
a Q10 ≈2 (Geider, Maclntyre, and Kana, 1998), and in the half-saturation constant
parameter of the michaelis-menten (Michaelis and Menten, 1913), with a smaller tem-
perature dependency (Shuter, 1979). At the individual level, nutrient limitation has
been shown to lower temperature optima by 3 to 6◦C (Thomas et al., 2017), which im-
plies that nutrient limitation makes species more vulnerable to warming, signiﬁcantly
reducing species range limits to temperature.
Modeling approaches
Effects of temperature in distinct physiological processes of phytoplankton have been
incorporated in models before (Shuter, 1979; Geider, MacIntyre, and Kana, 1996; Gei-
der, Maclntyre, and Kana, 1998), yet few have actually applied them to the context
of climate change.
Among those, Dutkiewicz, Scott, and Follows (2013) included
phytoplankton functional types in a marine ecosystem model, and coupled it to an
earth system model. The aim was to study how biogeography and productivity could
change in a warming climate. Their simulations showed local invasions of some phyt-
plankton types, which outcompeted the previously abundant type, and changed the
biogeographic ranges of the modeled plankton populations. The outcome was that
direct (increased metabolic rates) and indirect (change in resource availability due to
stratiﬁcation) impacts of temperature compensated each other on globally integrated
production basis. Regionally speaking however, impacts were much more complex
and strongly depended on resource supplies, being biomass, primary production and
export ﬂux decreased in warmer regions ( -40◦- 50◦) and increased in colder regions.
Toseland et al. (2013) combined different approaches (metatranscriptomes, biogeo-
chemical data and a model), one of them being a model of phytoplankton cell physi-
ology, to examine how temperature affected resource allocation and metabolism at a
global scale. Their model resolved cell size and resource allocation to photosynthe-
sis and biosynthesis, which largely inﬂuence cellular stoichiometry. They concluded
that temperature inﬂuenced phytoplankton metabolism almost as signiﬁcantly as nu-
trients and light. Especially translation of proteins (synthesis of proteins from ribo-
somes) seemed to be strongly affected, and noted a latitudinal gradient in the relative
amount of ribosomal proteins (biosynthetic apparatus in the model), where an in-
cremented amount of ribosomes would partly compensate the reduced translational
efﬁciency at low temperatures. Next, with the use of the physiological model, which
depicts optimal cellular allocation to structure, photosynthetic and biosynthetic ma-
chinery (under nutrient-replete conditions and exponential growth), they performed

1.4.3
Secondary production
Int his section w revise the main processes that characterize secondary production.
First in a physiological level for respiration. Second we go through how temper-
ature can affect prey-predator interactions. Finally, we describe effects observed in
mixotrophic organisms.
Respiration
Reactions involved in the provision of energy induce proton and electrons transfer
from a reduced substrate (derived internally for pure autotrophs and externally for
heterotrophs) to a proton and electron acceptor (Del Giorgio and Williams, 2005). Or-
ganic matter is the common donor of electrons and protons; and oxygen is the pre-
dominant proton acceptor. From an ecological point of view, this kind of respiration
is important as it provides energy for most heterotrophic organisms, partly shape the
organic and inorganic pools, and that it is the major sink of organic carbon in the en-
vironment. Overall respiration process can be reduced to two main stages: the Krebs
cycle and the terminal oxidation system (TOS). The former generates carbon dioxide
and a number molecules of reduced nucleotides (three NADH and one FADH2). The
latter are then delivered to the TOS. In the ﬁnal stage, oxygen is reduced to water
by means of the electrons and protons associated to the reduced nucleotides. The re-
sultant electron ﬂow allows formation of metabolic energy in the form of adenosine
triphosphate (ATP). By increasing anzymatic activity, respiration rates scale with tem-
perature with a Q10 close to 2.
Ecosystems can be net sinks or sources of CO2 depending on how much of the
ﬁxed carbon is respired, term also referred as metabolic balance. If there is a higher
amount of carbon being respired than ﬁxed, the ecosystem is completely heterotrophic.
Such can happen when the heterotrophic community subsists out of carbon that was
previously ﬁxed, for example when dissolved organic matter (DOM) accumulated
in the system, fueling bacterial production. López-Urrutia et al. (2006) showed that
metabolic balance of the oceans was inﬂuenced by temperature, where higher tem-
peratures resulted in oceans less efﬁcient at capturing CO2. More ﬁxed carbon being
respired means less carbon being exported to deeper layers, as a consequence, the
biological pump could be negatively affected.
Prey-predator interactions
Phytoplankton biomass is a balance between: growth (controlled by resource avail-
ability), and death (lysis or predation). Predation can impose a strong control on phy-
toplankton biomass (Banse, 2013), and in some areas losses by grazing can represent
up to 4/5 of the total primary production (Landry and Calbet, 2004). This temperature
difference has been shown to alter several predator prey-interaction, and has been hy-
potheiszed to be one of the main drivers that allows phytoplankton bloom formation.
The latter was concluded by Rose and Caron (2007), which measured the response to

temperature of protists separating them by feeding mode (auto- or heterotrophs) and
then the latter were divided depending on their prey type (bacterivores vs herbivores).
Heterotrophs presented higher Q10 than autotrophs and therefore at temperate tem-
peratures, maximum growth rates of herbivores were much higher than their prey.
In contrast, at temperatures below 5◦C, maximum growth rates of grazers were less
than half than the phytoplankton ones. Therefore, low temperatures constrain het-

Some authors stated that difference in temperature dependencies between auto-
and heterotrophs could increase predation pressure, reducing phytoplankton stand-
ing stocks (e.g. Boyce, Lewis, and Worm 2010). Nutrient availability seemed to be
a determinant factor to answer this question, as predation pressure indeed increased
when nutrients were plenty in the environment and conversely, did not change when
nutrients were limiting primary production (Chen et al., 2012; O’Connor et al., 2009).
O’Connor et al. (2009) performed a mesocosm experiment under two resource sce-
narios to observe the microbial food web response when being limited or not by
any resource. They observed that a rise in temperature increased the heterotrophic
biomass relative to autotrophic biomass, consistent with the difference between res-
piration and photosynthesis-limited metabolism. So there was an increasing pressure
from grazers and consequent reduction of phytoplankton biomass even thought there
was a greater per capita primary production. They argued that nutrient availability
determined whether total biomass of the food-web would increase or decrease. Thus,
in regions where there is nutrient limitation, cells would present a higher resilience
to warming because consumer production would be restricted (as PP did not signiﬁ-
cantly increase). Yet, in regions where resources are not limiting, primary production
would increase, leading to a rise in secondary production and so consumers control,
which would ﬁnally end up reducing overall biomass of algae. That is, overall pro-
duction would be higher, but autotrophic biomass would be less due to predation.
Nevertheless one problem of mesocosms is the effect of trophic cascading, becoming
difﬁcult to predict overall biomass. Yet, all the studies agree on the shift towards a
more heterotrophic ocean. Chen et al. (2012) attemped to answer the hypothesis that
trophic transfer efﬁciency (ratio of herbivory to phytoplankton growth) will increase
with temperature due again to their different temperature dependence. The result
again depended on the trophic state of the system. Transfer efﬁciency increased in
eutrophic environments due to higher production of prey, whereas decreased in olig-
otrophic scenarios, due to primary production being limited by nutrients and herbi-
vores biomass being reduced at higher temperatures.
Mixotrophy
Mixotrophy is the ability of some protists to perform both photosynthesis and phagotro-
phy. Due to their dual capability, mixotrophs are able to thrive in more stable envi-
ronments such as summers in temperate regions or permanently stratiﬁed regions
(Mitra et al., 2014). This kind of trophic strategy allows an increase in trophic trans-
fer efﬁciency (Ward and Follows, 2016), mainly by phagotrophy alleviating nutrient
limitation, and not so much by its contribution of carbon. This short-cut from nu-
trient regeneration to primary production, allowing persistence of primary produc-
tion from bacterivory, results in a more efﬁcient food-web, playing an essential role
in regulation of carbon ﬂow and the biological pump (Mitra et al., 2014). As for tem-
perature, contribution of heterotrophy relative to autotrophy in mixotrophic growth
seemed to increase. Wilken et al. (2013) investigated how mixotrophs adjusted their
autotrophic and heterotrophic metabolism to higher temperatures. They did not ﬁnd

1.4.4
Bacterial production
Bacteria are the link of the microbial food web, as it is able to mineralize nutrients,
entering them to the available inorganic pool for phytoplankton growth and therefore
closing the loop of the food web (Azam et al., 1994). Heterotrophic prokaryotes need
organic carbon and nutrients to grow, the former can come from the dissolved or the
particulate pool. Particulate matter has to be hydrolyzed by extracellular enzymes,
transforming it to dissolved carbon just in the outer part of the cell wall, and then be
diffused towards the interior through the permeases. In the process there is a leakage
that contributes to the DOM pool. As for nutrients, bacteria can take either from the
organic pool (urea, nitrogen-containing reduced carbon substrates such as dissolved
free aminoacids (DFAA) or proteins also referred as dissolved combined aminoacids
(DCAA)), DFAA seemingly supporting the highest fraction of bacterial growth ( 40%).
On the other hand, heterotorphic prokaryotes can also incorporate inorganic nutrients,
mainly ammonium and nitrate for the nitrogen forms. Nitrate is more energetically
demanding for bacteria, as they will have to reduce it to ammonium inside the cell
before assimilation. Still, bacteria can effectively compete with phytoplankton for ni-
trate, and can therefore be both a net producer or a sink of nutrients.
There is a tight coupling between bacteria and algae, as the substrate from which
they live mainly comes from those. However, even thought concentration of microal-
gae can vary by orders of magnitude in time and space, concentration of bacteria
do not differ much between biomes, suggesting there are some factors keeping its
biomass in check. These factors have largely been discussed and distinct hypotheses
arise, differing on whether the control is bottom-up or top-down imposed. Pomeroy
and Deibel (1986) observed in Newfoundland (cold temperate region), that the spring
phytoplankton bloom was not followed by a bacterial bloom, and deduced that pho-
tosynthesis, and so phytoplankton growth, was less constrained by low temperatures
(<4◦C) than bacterial growth, allowing a large export of POM to the deep or to be read-
ily available for other organisms such as metazoans or other phagotrophs which com-
pete with bacteria for detritus. The authors also related this processes to be precisely in
areas that supported large ﬁsheries. Later, Pomeroy and Wiebe (2001) discussed that
growth rates of bacteria largely varied between a gradient of temperature and sub-
strate, being signiﬁcantly inhibited at very low temperatures, nonetheless, if enough
substrate was added, such effects were not observed. Based on some of these obser-
vations, Nedwell (2000) argued that the combined effect of temperature and substrate
was in fact a result of the response of nutrient uptake speciﬁc afﬁnity to temperature.
He attributed this decrease in afﬁnity to a stiffening of the membrane lipids when
temperature was below the optimum, and so decreasing the efﬁciency of transport
proteins from the membrane.
Bacterial production (BP) and bacterial respiration (BR) respond in the same way to
temperature, hence bacteria growth efﬁciency (BGE=BP/(BP+BR)) should remain the
same even if there is a temperature rise. Nevertheless, different response of BP and BR
have been observed in warm oligotrophic regions (Del Giorgio, Cole, and Cimbleris,
1997). López-Urrutia et al. (2006) later demonstrated by using metabolic theory and
comparing it with datasets of natural BP and BR that effects of temperature on BGE

in microbial food web processes, and showed that warming increased both bacterial
respiration and production, the latter doing it at a higher rate than its grazers, resulting
in a decreased proportion of bacterial biomass being grazed and so an overall higher
bacterial biomass in the system, with possible consequences on other processes such
as degradation of particulate organic matter (POM) and consequently weakening the
biological pump.
Dissolved organic matter pool
Dissolved organic matter (DOM) is a composition of diverse bioelements, where plank-
ton organisms are the main source of it, coming as by-product of cellular metabolism
(e.g. exudates, sloppy feeding or bacterial enzymatic hydrolysis), of cell lysis and
decomposition, and also from direct solubilization of POM (mainly driven by phys-
ical and thermodynamic processes). Different pathways produce different kinds of
DOM, some being considerably different from the initial cellular composition and
others maintaining the previous stoichiometry. DOM is therefore often characterized
based on its bio-availability, separating three main pools differing by their turnover
times: (1) labile DOM, lasting from minutes to days, and representing less than 1% of

weeks to months, and hypothesized that bacteria do not consume it because of nu-
trient limitations being a poor growth substrate, or that its consumption will depend
on the bacteria community present at the moment, and so sometimes delaying its
consumption; and (3) refractory DOM (rDOM), that lasts from years to millennia sur-
passing turnover rates of the oceans. rDOM is one of the largest carbon pools, and
its long turnover time suggests that it can provide little or none of the nutrient or en-
ergy required for bacterioplankton growth. There seems however to be a trend where
DOM produced in eutrophic systems is more biologically reactive than the one pro-
duced in oligotrophic ones, the reason remains unclear, yet one possible explanation is
that DOM produced in high-nutrient environments possess a greater nutritional value
(Cotner and Biddanda, 2002). Thingstad, Hagström, and Rassoulzadegan (1997) cre-
ated a model that captured interaction between bacteria, phytoplankton and bacteri-
vores as to understand observed accumulations of DOC in the environment. Where
bacterial biomass is kept in check by competition with phytoplankton when being
nutrient-limied, and by predators when abundant. Such model allowed to analyze
how DOC production and consumption varied depending on nutrient availability. Fi-
nally, semilabile DOM is also interesting, as it has been observed that some bacteria
in summer subsist of DOM that was produced during winter, and such fact can also
allow the possibility of a net heterotrophic community during some periods, i.e. be-
ing the production of heterotrophic prokaryotes higher than of primary producers at
a given time (Azam et al., 1994).
Lastly, during the last years TEP gained importance since it plays an important
role for the biological pump due to its hight C:N ratio and capacity to stick together
and form POM to further sink. Biermann, Engel, and Riebesell (2014) observed an
increase in TEP production with rising temperature; such was attributed to a higher
excretion of DOM by plankton when temperature rises while being nutrient limited,
as the ﬁxed carbon cannot be synthesized. An increase in temperature seems to lead
to higher TEP formation and ﬂocculation, which consequently leads to an increased
export ﬂux. Nonetheless, amounts of TEP highly depends on degradation rates from
bacteria.
1.4.5
Carbon sequestration
Export production was predicted to decrease globally by 6%. This conclusion was
reached by Bopp et al. (2001), who by means of coupled ocean-atmosphere biogeo-
chemical models that included plankton dynamics, attempted to predict regional and
global changes in marine productivity due to climate change. With their model they
separated the changes in export production due to light, temperature and nutrient
supply. Nertheless, they concluded that direct effects of temperature did not seem to
have any effects with the exception of latitudes beyond 70◦. And that it was mainly
physical factors such as nutrients supply, that reduced productivity in the tropics and
mid-latitudes, but that such would be compensated by increased photosynthetic efﬁ-
ciency due to temperature in spring and summer, mainly in mid- and high latitudes,
enlarging the growing season, and so increases the export in the latter.
Export efﬁciency (ef: deﬁned as the fraction of PP exported from the euphotic
layer) and temperature have been shown to be negatively correlated. Laws et al. (2000)
suggested that ef-ratio is shaped by PP and temperature of the mixed layer, mainly
driven by different temperature sensitivities between autotrophs and heterotrophs by
means of a detailed model of the planktonic system, supporting the results with obser-
vations of the export efﬁciency together with physical and biochemical factors. Later,

1.5
Conclusion
Overall impacts of climate change widely vary between ocean biomes and in a latitudi-
nal gradient. Alterations of the physical environment such as variation in the MLD can
promote or reduce primary production by either an intensiﬁcation of nutrient stress
or conversely, an increase in light availability and therefore promotion of carbon ﬁx-
ation. Direct effects of temperature on phytoplankton have been widely recognized,
where most of the metabolic rates in phytoplankton increase by a Q10 of 2 with the
exception of the photosynthetic rate and nutrient uptake. As for heterotrophic organ-
isms, Q10 are not so well established, and largely vary across the literature. It seems
however that these organisms tend to respond more steeply to temperature than pho-
toautotrophs, leading to an increment in overall heterotrophy of the system. This
imbalance between trophic levels seems to be the main driver of further impacts, such
as a modiﬁcation in the carbon ﬂow through the food-web, increasing respiration,
with consequent diminished export towards higher trophic levels. Changes in size
or higher respiration rates by bacteria can also dampen export ﬂux towards deeper
layers. Nevertheless all these processes also vary depending on environmental condi-
tions, such as resource availability or whether the organisms are at the higher or lower
extreme of the temperature gradient. Finally, export ﬂux towards the ocean interior
seems to be diminished, whatsoever the way TEP works still remains fairly unknown.

2.1
Introduction
Phytoplankton are responsible of about 50% of atmospheric carbon ﬁxation, and are
key players of the biological pump, that is, the biotic draw-down of carbon dioxide
from the atmosphere to the deep ocean. Through primary production, phytoplankton
fuel the whole marine food-web, so any change in their growth can potentially cascade
to higher trophic levels. Efﬁciencies of carbon sequestration and transfer to higher
trophic levels are tightly linked to the rate at which carbon is ﬁxed, respired, and de-
graded in the ecosystem. As temperature affects most biological processes (Gillooly et
al., 2001), global warming is expected to alter organisms’ performance and emergent
ecosystem functioning. Over the last decades, an observed decline in chlorophyl has
been associated to climate change (Boyce, Lewis, and Worm, 2010), even though very
much debated (Mackas, 2011; McQuatters-Gollop et al., 2011; Behrenfeld et al., 2016),
and with recognized regional differences (Doney, 2006). The main factors responsible
for this decline were associated to temperature-driven changes in the physical envi-
ronment. The main example being a reduction in the nutrient supply of the photic
layer due to a stronger temperature-induced stratiﬁcation. On the other hand, direct
effects of temperature tend to be interpreted or modeled as an exponential function
of temperature independently of growth being limited by resources or not (Arrhe-
nius, 1889; Taucher and Oschlies, 2011). This is however not the case, as response to
temperature vary among metabolic processes (e.g. Allen, Gillooly, and Brown 2005;
López-Urrutia and Morán 2007; Edwards et al. 2016).
It is well known that temperature affects plankton metabolism (Eppley, 1972; Raven
and Geider, 1988; Geider, Maclntyre, and Kana, 1998). Temperature increases metabolic
rates by accelerating enzyme activities and so protein production, hence, almost all
vital metabolic processes are likely to be altered. Operating temperatures of most bi-
ological processes range from 0oC to 40oC and vary among species. Performance of
species with temperature is described by a left skewed unimodal curve, which peaks
in the optimal temperature for growth (Edwards et al., 2016). When coupled together,
these curves form the temperature response of the bulk community, which increases
exponentially with temperature for most biological rates (Eppley, 1972). Interestingly,
biological processes in the cellular level also show different temperature dependen-
cies. Resource-limited growth responds to temperature according to the dependency

tend to be considered simply as an exponential positive increase in growth. In mod-
els, the way temperature effects are implemented, signiﬁcantly changes models output
(Taucher and Oschlies, 2011). On the other hand, others have used Metabolic Theory
of Ecology (Brown et al., 2004) to explain observed patterns at the ecosystem level,
nevertheless, this theory has assumptions that might not be applied in the plankton
community context.
Metabolic theory of ecology (Gillooly et al., 2001; Brown et al., 2004) has stated that
heterotrophic processes (i.e. respiration) respond more strongly to temperature than
autotrophic ones (Allen, Gillooly, and Brown, 2005). This is due to different rate-
limiting biogeochemical reactions, where ATP synthesis drives heterotrophic ones,
and Rubisco carboxylation autotrophic ones. This hypothesis has however been re-
cently discussed by Chen and Laws (2017), who found that estimated activation en-
ergies of phytoplankton were underestimated due to differing growth rates in taxon,
and that when analyzed individually, activation energies between auto- and heterotrophs
did not signiﬁcantly differ. Also, the activation energy of 0.32 eV for Rubisco carboxy-
lation (which reﬂects maximum photosynthetic rate) was estimated from terrestrial
vascular plants. Rubisco carboxylation and so carbon ﬁxation is controlled by the
ratio of pO2/pCO2 in the active site, which balances the dual activity of Rubisco (pho-
torespiration v.s. carbon ﬁxation). Unlike most terrestrial plants, algae possess carbon-
concentrating mechanisms to maintain an elevated CO2 in the cells where Rubisco is
present, ensuring the dominance of the carboxylase function (Raven, 1997) over pho-
torespiration. Which means that high CO2 concentrations in the Rubisco uptake site
attenuates effects of temperature on photorespiration, approaching the maximum rate
of carboxylation, which has an activation energy close to 0.65 eV (Bernacchi et al.,
2001), i.e. the same as for heterotrophic processes. Nevertheless, the assumption of
differing temperature dependencies for heterotrophs and autotrophs has been used
to explain biogeochemical processes such as phytoplankton blooms (López-Urrutia,
2008), metabolic balance of the oceans (López-Urrutia et al., 2006) or carbon export to
the deep ocean (Cael and Follows, 2016), all properly ﬁtting with ﬁeld observations.
If, in the case of protists, autotrophic and heterotrophic processes do not differ in their
temperature response, such would suggest that there might be other factors masking
temperature effects, as had already been shown in López-Urrutia and Morán (2007),
where substrate limitation in bacteria reduced temperature effects on bacterial pro-
duction but not in respirtion, explained differences observed in growth efﬁciencies.
To examine the effect of the various temperature scaling relations inherent in a
planktonic ecosystem, we developed a trait-based model of the pelagic microbial com-
munity. The model treats effects of temperature explicitly for each of the physiolog-
ical processes responsible for plankton growth. We ﬁrst developed a cell-model of
heterotrophic bacteria and mixotrophic protists. Next, we scaled the cell models to
a community level by allowing several individuals to interact through competition
and predation. The biological system is implemented in a realistic physical model of
two trophic scenarios (oligotrophic and eutrophic). We start by hypothesizing that
temperature will increase primary production, but also enhance bacterial production,
reducing carbon export to depth and to higher trophic levels. This document discusses

We work under the hypothesis that a rise in temperature will increase primary
production, but will also enhance the effects of the microbial loop, which will reduce
carbon export to depth and trophic transfer to upper trophic levels. All these pro-
cesses are tightly linked to the availability of resources and light, which determine
abundance, trophic strategies (i.e. autotrophs, mixotrophs, heterotrophs) and the cell
sizes of dominant organisms in space and time, i.e. seasonal succession. To capture
this fact, a system of inﬁnite diversity (SID; Bruggeman and Kooijman 2007) is im-
plemented. The SID model allows a continuum in traits, which recreates a deﬁned
number of species representative of each trait combination. In our model, species in-
teract through competition and predation, deﬁning the winning traits at a given time
and place. Light afﬁnity and maximum growth rate are the two traits considered for
a continuum. Both are subject to the trade-off: investing in light reduces investments
in growth and viceversa. Hence at the extremes of this traits gradient one can ﬁnd or-
ganisms specialized in growth (r strategists; MacArthur 1962), characteristic of envi-
ronments abundant in resources; and on the other side, organisms highly competitive
under scarce resources (in this case for light) that are able to subsist at the carrying
capacity imposed by the environment (K strategists). Three cell size classes are con-
sidered: 0.5 µm (∼10−8 µgC), ∼1 µm (10−5 µgC) and ∼100 µm (10−2 µgC), based on
the prey-predator mass ratio of 1:1000. Following Andersen et al. (2016) the smallest

2.2.3
Temperature dependencies
Temperature dependencies of each physiological parameter follow an exponential in-
crease deﬁned by the Q10 coefﬁcient (Fig. 2.2). Note that we are considering commu-
nity level rates, and so we consider exponential growth with temperature, and not the
unimodel curve corresponding to single-species. Biosynthetic activity (i.e. maximum
growth rate) varies according to the upper layer of growth of the bulk community (Ep-
pley, 1972), here assumed to be Q10 = 2. Respiration rates also scale with a Q10 close
to 2 deﬁned by the rate of ATP synthesis (Shuter, 1979; Allen, Gillooly, and Brown,
2005). We consider that overall growth is limited by biosynthetic rate, and so maxi-
mum uptake of resources are not considered. We assume afﬁnity for light (αL) to be
temperature independent (Post, Wit, and Mur, 1985; Edwards et al., 2016). Tempera-
ture dependence of uptake of dissolved compounds tends to either not be represented,
or to be as slightly less affected by temperature in the half saturation constant (Shuter,
1979; Pomeroy and Wiebe, 2001). Nevertheless, uptake of dissolved matter tends to
be limited by diffusivity. Hence, we argue that as the viscosity of sea water doubles

2.2.4
Physical set-up
The system was coupled to the physical model GOTM (Burchard, Bolding, and Vil-
larreal, 1999) by means of The Fortran-based Framework for Aquatic Biogeochemical
Models (FABM; Bruggeman and Bolding, 2014), which allows an implementation of
the biochemical model in a 1D water column model (GOTM) with realistic values. For
such we used two test cases, one from a shallow productive area from L4-station in
the English Channel (ﬁg. C.2), and another from a permanently stratiﬁed region in
the North Paciﬁc (ﬁg. C.3), referred as L4 and Ows respectively from now onward.
Running period of L4 and Ows scenarios were of 9 and 8 years correspondingly. As
this time is too short to obtain convergence, approximate values at the end of each run
were used as input for a new run until an acceptable periodic trend was obtained. To
avoid extinction of species, a minimum background concentration of 1 10−7 µgC L−1
was set in a way that, if biomass of generalists became inferior to this values, growth
term was set to zero, avoiding further decreases. This value is nevertheless extremely
small, and is unlikely to affect dynamics of the system. The system has an open bottom
boundary with an input rate of nutrients deﬁned as VN,bottom = v(Nb −N) to avoid
accumulation, where v is the input rate equivalent to 0.5d−1, Nb a ﬁxed concentration
(150µgN L−1). Sinking POM also leaves through the bottom boundary at a rate equiv-
alent to the sinking velocity, set at 10m d−1. Since we seek to observed direct effects
of temperature on organisms, without the physical environment being altered, an in-
crease in temperature was not forced in the physical environment itself, but only in
the biological system, so only biological features are affected by higher temperatures.
The forcing was of a +3◦C increase in temperature.

2.3.1
Optimization
To provide a ﬁrst understanding of the effects of temperature on plankton dynam-
ics we ﬁrst performed a simpliﬁed steady-state optimality analysis of the cell model.
We looked for the investments that maximized growth for several combinations of
resources with temperature. In the current model, plankton are ruled by the limit-
ing resource, and so we separate carbon from nitrogen resources. Note that mortality
and predation are not included in this optimization problem, which simply focuses on
the division rate of cells. Under saturating conditions of resources, and as expected
with the increase in maximum growth rate, division rates vary both with temperature
and investments in biosynthesis (ﬁg. B.1). Growth is much higher at higher tempera-
tures and biosynthesic investments. For the values where respiration rate overcomes
biosynthetic rates, growth is negative.
When carbon-limited, a rise in temperature has two implications. First, maximum
growth rate increases, which means that organisms that were at an optimal balance
in their investments in light and growth, are given a larger margin to invest in photo-
synthesis, resulting in a higher growth. Second, respiration losses also increase, which
increases the minimum amount of resources needed to have a positive growth (R*,
Tilman 1982). Consequently, under this threshold, where the functional response is
dominated by afﬁnity and not maximum growth rates, higher optimal investments
are not able to keep up with higher respiration rates, resulting in a reduction (nega-
tive) growth rate where the difference between R* of both temperatures occurs (ﬁgures
B.3 and B.4).
Under nutrient limitation, since the model does not contemplate respiration losses
under this condition (see Eq.2.13 that respiration only accounts for in the carbon side),
all other factors kept the same, division rates of cells increase with rising temperatures
(ﬁg. B.2). Nevertheless, this is the most idealized case, and temperature is likely to
change other factors such as predation, mortality rates or resources availability. Also
note that under nutrient limitation, investments are kept at its minimum, meaning
that organisms mainly invest in growth as carbon is not the limiting factor.

2.4
Discussion
How differing effects of temperature scale from an organism’s fundamental processes
to its growth rate, and so to the emergence of community and ecosystem processes re-
mains unclear. Identiﬁcation of ecosystem-level interactions as a result of direct effects
==PAGE:41==
2.4. Discussion
29
of temperature will allow a better interpretation of the impacts of global warming in
the microbial community of the oceans. To tackle this issue, we developed a simple
model of the pelagic microbial community, where community composition emerged
from environmental conditions and inter- and intra- speciﬁc interactions, shaping re-
sulting energy ﬂows of the ecosystem. We started by hypothesizing that a warmer
environment will increase primary production, but also enhance the effects of the mi-
crobial loop, reducing carbon export to depth and to higher trophic levels. In a broad
sense, we do indeed show that in absolute terms, this hypothesis cannot be refuted
for oligotrophic environemnets. In eutrophic systems however, all three processes ap-
pear to increase with increasing temperature. From a relative point of view, higher
temperatures results in ecosystems that are less efﬁcient in exporting carbon to depth,
whereas trophic transfer efﬁciency depends on the trophic state of the system, increas-
ing for resources-sufﬁcient areas, and decreasing for regions scarce in nutrients. Here
we discuss the main results obtained, next we examine the model performance rela-
tive to real data and highlight the limitations of the model.
Metabolic balance - Ecosystems are less efﬁcient at retaining ﬁxed carbon, due
to the differential effects of temperature on resources-limited growth and respiration,
since a higher proportion of the ﬁxed carbon is respired. This balance could be shifted
if, for examples bacterial biomass decreased relative to cooler scenarios. This is not
the case, as an increase in bacterial biomass is an evident trend in all the simulations.
Interestingly, these results are in agreement with those reported by López-Urrutia et
al. (2006) who scaled the metabolic balance of the oceans by means of a simple model
derived from the metabolic theory of ecology (Brown et al., 2004), under the assump-
tion of heterotrophs responding faster to temperature than autotrophs. They then
compared the results with real data of primary production and respiration rates of
the oceans. We obtain the same trend through different assumptions, implying that
metabolic balance can also be affected by limitation of resources together with tem-
perature. This becomes of importance in systems that are net sources of CO2 during
given periods of the year (e.g. see ﬁgures D.1, D.2, or D.6); where a period can last for
over one month or more. Taking into account that oligotorphic open oceans are about
40% in area of all the oceans, this might actually contribute to the green house effect.
Carbon export efﬁciency - Carbon export efﬁciency (ef-ratio) towards deeper layers
decreases with increasing temperature, independently of absolute export trends. Total
vertical export of organic carbon follows living biomass trends, that is, increases when
resources are not limiting and decreases when by whichever factor living biomass di-
minishes. For an oligotrophic situation, our model predicts a considerable decrease
of up to 30% relative to the base-line scenario. Reduction in the ef-ratio is due on one
hand to increased respiration rates that remove organic carbon from the system; and
on the other hand to higher hydrolysis rates, where a higher proportion of the incom-
ing carbon is recycled in the surface layers. Such inverse trend of the export efﬁciency
with temperature is again in agreement with predictions made through MTE on the
ef-ratio (Cael and Follows, 2016), which found reduced export efﬁciencies with increas-
ing temperatures by comparing data and a simple model, whose main assumption is
the differential temperature dependence between autotrophs and heterotrophs.
Primary production - Total NPP follows growth rates and biomass of generalists,
increasing with temperature when resources are abundant and decreasing in limiting
situations. Our model however heavily underestimates NPP when comparing with
other annually integrated values (Field et al., 1998). This could be due to the trade-off
==PAGE:42==
30 Chapter 2. Effects of temperature on plankton community dynamics: scaling from
the individual to the ecosystem level in a climate change setting.
between maximum growth rate and light investments. By doing so, we eliminate one
of the main characteristics of some of the main r-strategists: diatoms. Since invest-
ments in light at the beginning of the spring blooms come in detriments of growth,
diatoms, which thrive precisely at this time of the year and are characterized to efﬁ-
ciently grow at low light levels, cannot emerge in our model. Also, when nutrients
are strongly limiting, investments go to growth and not to light, which considerably
reduces potential capacity for carbon ﬁxation. Nevertheless, at least for station L-4,
biomass trends relate fairly well with real data (not provided here), where the spring
bloom occurs late in the season, around April, and then a second bloom thrives again
in July-august-September. Size-classes do not correspond however, but this might as
well be due to the model only representing two size groups of generalists.
Competition for nutrients and community composition - Nutrient limitation and
so competition can increase just by a rise in temperature per se (without affecting the
physical environment). Following competition theory (Tilman, 1982), organisms with
the lowest R∗, that is, organisms which need the lowest amount of resources as to
maintain a positive growth, will win, excluding all the others in cases of low resources.
Following this principle, in the Ows scenario where nutrients are the limiting factor
for the 3 cell groups, bacteria should be the sole survivor due to its reduced size (Kiør-
boe, 2008). Nevertheless, generalists still persist in the system as phagotrophy alle-
viates nutrient stress. Hence, increased nutrient limitation could potentially promote
a shift towards cells with higher investments in phagotrophy. In fact, the sensitivity
analysis for food afﬁnity (Fig. D.1 and D.5), shows that lower food-afﬁnities result in
complete exclusion of the large generalist. Wilken et al. (2013) found that contribu-
tion of photosynthesis to growth decreased, resulting in mixotrophic organisms be-
ing more heterotrophic with rising temperatures. In our model we cannot compare
this trend, as balance between autotrophy and heterotrophy is a result of resources
availability (since we have a ﬁxed food uptake). But, if higher temperatures were to
increase heterotrophy, this would be further enhanced by the increased nutrient limi-
tation induced by temperature on the community performance. Shift of mixotrophic
organisms towards heterotrophy results both in a reduction in NPP and an increase of
predation (that is, if enough prey are available).
Species that had a minor role due to constraints could gain importance in a warmer
environment due to the positive effects of temperature on growth. The increase in the
average photosynthetic growth rate is mainly due by a higher biomass of organisms
investing in light at the extreme of the trait-distribution (ﬁg. ??), since growth con-
straints on the maximum growth rate are alleviated by temperature. Which means
that slow growing species, which tend to be more competitive for resources, could do
better in a warmer environment.
Finally, we do observed a shift toward dominance of smaller cells. This is however
due to enhanced nutrient competition, where bacteria always do better, and small gen-
eralists tend to increase in expense of the larger ones when nutrients are limiting.
Trophic transfer efﬁcieny - Trophic transfer efﬁciency between trophic levels de-
creases when nutrients are limiting, and practically do not change when resources
are sufﬁcient. Such results partly agree with Chen et al. (2012), who observed essen-
tially the same transfer efﬁciency for a range of trophic conditions. In their experi-
ments reduced transfer efﬁciency comes from a decrease in zooplankton biomass, it is
however unclear what promoted this reduction. In our case, reduction of predators
comes from an enhanced nutrient limitation in generalists, which partly compete with
==PAGE:43==

smaller cells for the same nutrient pool (unlike in Chen et al. (2012)). This ﬁnding sup-
ports the hypothesis that in oligotrophic environments, higher temperature will not
reduce standing stocks at least from predation, contrary to what others stated (Boyce,
Lewis, and Worm, 2010). On the other hand, Chen et al. (2012) also found an increased
transfer efﬁciency when resources were sufﬁcient, which differs from our results, that
show practically no change in transfer efﬁciency. This is however because they in-
creased temperature while holding chlorophyll constant, whereas in our model prey
also increases with higher temperature, meaning that, if there was any strengthening
of predation in the prey population, it was compensated by bottom-up processes. This
ﬁnding is supported in O’Connor et al. (2009) in their mesocosm experiments. Also, in
absolute terms of biomass, the larger generalist always decreases or remains the same,
which can be translated in food availability for higher trophic levels, which would
remain the same or see a decrease in their prey, with negative effects. All in all, in-
creased predation pressure due to temperature only works in eutrophic environments
but not in oligotrophic ones. Nevertheless, regional differences, specially oligotrophic
areas being the ones in the warmest environments where top-down control tends to
be the strongest, it seems that no effects will occur. While in eutrophic ones, more
representative of temperate and colder regions, could see a decrease in biomass but
with higher productivity and trophic transfer efﬁciency, which would actually have
an overall positive effect for the food web.
An interesting aspect to note is that a decrease in transfer efﬁciency could result in
an increase in the transfer efﬁciency towards the detritivorous pathway (e.g. for larger
metazoans or the benthic community). Nevertheless, in our model, this does not trans-
late into greater export, but rather more being recycled at surface. This suggests that
the microbial pump (Legendre et al., 2015) might gain importance under a warming
environment, especially if bacterial activity increases and its activity results in produc-
tion of refractory DOM, which could keep carbon in the system (Jiao et al., 2010). In
our model however, DOC seems to be underestimated. A study in the English Chan-
nel station E1 (Williams, 1995), a bit farther from the station we are modelling, found
an accumulation of DOC of 34 mmol m−3 just after the spring bloom, which is much
higher than what we obtain here, probably because DOC is only leaked when be-
ing nutrient limited (explaining the accumulation during the second bloom), whereas
plankton in general also leak passively, even if not nutrient-limited. Also other major
players contributing to the DOC pool are missing, such as copepods.
Model limitations
This model is an extremely simpliﬁed version of the microbial community, which only
includes some of the main players of the biogeochemical cycles here analyzed. To be-
gin with, diatoms are not present. Diatoms emerge mainly at the beginning of the sea-
son when nutrients are plenty and light is still low. Due to their size and fast growth,
they are the main responsible for blooms formation in spring and highly contribute to
carbon export.
Diazotrophs, organisms that are able to ﬁx atmospheric nitrogen, are also not in-
cluded. These organisms have been shown to play an important role in extremely
oligotrophic areas. If nutrient competition were to increase, conditions would favour
these kind of organisms. Regarding export to depth, another organisms that has a
key role are copepods. These metazoans would fall out of the microbial community
strictly speaking, nevertheless, they play a key role in some carbon ﬂuxes, such as car-
bon export or inputs of DOM by for example, sloppy feeding, which has been said to
be one of the main contributors of DOM accumulation at surface (Jumars et al., 1989).

the individual to the ecosystem level in a climate change setting.
In the bacterial parametrization, we consider the polymeric pool to be semi-labile and
the monomeric pool to be labile. Degradation of organic matter highly depends on
the composition of the latter, which makes these interactions highly complex. Also
phytoplankton lysed cells go directly to POM, whereas it is estimated that 34% is ac-
tually readily available for bacterial uptake (Billen and Fontigny, 1987). Despite its
simplicity, this model seems to properly capture observed trends of processes such as
vertical carbon export or primary production with temperature.
2.5
Conclusion
Increasing temperatures together with limitation of resources reduces carbon export
and trophic transfer efﬁciencies of ecosystems. In the individual level, resource lim-
itations prevent anabolic processes (uptake of resources coupled to biosynthesis) to
increase with temperature at the same rate as catabolic processes (respiration). When
resources are sufﬁcient, overall primary production and carbon export to depth in-
crease, whereas trophic transfer efﬁcieny depends on the performance of the higher
trophic level considered. In seasonal environments, an enhanced growth during the
spring bloom depletes nutrients earlier in the season, which enhances competition for
nutrients, shifting community composition towards smaller cells.
Effects of temperature on physiological processes have been studied for over a cen-
tury, nevertheless, we argue that interpretation of data related to global warming has
been mislead by effects of resource limitation. Here, by explicitly representing effects
of temperature on traits responsible for growth, we show that patterns in the indi-
vidual level can be scaled to observed trends in the ecosystem level, and that species
interactions can actually exacerbate nutrient limitation by increasing competition just
by temperature effects on physiology. We anticipate combined direct and indirect ef-
fects of temperature to negatively affect ecosystem efﬁciencies by enhancing sinks of
carbon from respiration relative to production.
