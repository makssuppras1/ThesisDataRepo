Abstract
The thesis investigates deep reinforcement learning, specifically Proximal Policy Optimization,
on online scheduling to minimise the schedule’s makespan. Invalid action masking was gradually
applied with potential-based and auxiliary reward shaping to assess their individual and com-
bined effect on the performance of the deep reinforcement learning algorithm. Hyperparameter
tuning using Bayesian Optimization and Hyperband was implemented to find the optimal con-
figuration of hyperparameters, including the neural network architecture. The PPO algorithm
was trained and tested on three flow shop production layouts with two, four, and eight stages.
The study found that invalid action masking was necessary for the PPO algorithm to solve the
scheduling problem. Furthermore, potential-based and auxiliary rewards shaping was found to
improve the performance of PPO, resulting in the algorithm outperforming the shortest pro-
cessing time heuristic for the two and four-stage layouts. However, the advantage of using deep
reinforcement learning was only slight. One may argue that the SPT heuristic would be used
in a practical setting as it is easier to implement, more transparent in its decisions, and does
not require training. In the eight-stage production layout, the PPO algorithm did not perform
better than the shortest processing time heuristic with neither invalid action masking or reward
shaping.

Introduction
1.1
Smart Manufacturing
Industry 4.0 entails the integration of digital technologies into industrial processes. The ad-
vancement of Industry 4.0 allows for large-scale real-time data to be collected from sensors
and networked machines. Furthermore, the emergence of artificial intelligence (AI) and deep
learning to derive and analyze knowledge from real-time data lays the foundation for intelligent
manufacturing. [73] Key components of Industry 4.0 are the Internet of Things (IoT), Cloud
Computing. and Big Data Analytics. [24]
Smart manufacturing is a manufacturing process managed through networked data. [74] Manu-
facturing data is essential for smart manufacturing and goes through several steps. First, man-
ufacturing data is collected from a multitude of sources. Among these sources is the Internet of
Things (IoT). For IoT, data from wireless connected manufacturing equipment is continuously
collected through smart sensors, Radio Frequency Identification (RFID), and other communica-
tion devices. The sensing devices allow real-time monitoring and reporting of equipment health
and operational status. Second, the large volume of data collected by the IoT is then stored
in data structures. Cloud Computing allows cost-effective, shareable, scalable data storage and
access through cloud-based servers. Third, Big Data Analysis, e.g., deep learning or artificial
intelligence, is used to derive knowledge and rules from the large volume of data. Real-time
data collection and analytics can help manufacturers adjust to changes and drive decisions on
whether to modify processes or equipment. [25]. A prerequisite for utilizing real-time data col-
lection and big data analytics is automated manufacturing processes and wireless interconnected
machines. The fundamental idea of smart manufacturing is to use accumulated manufacturing
data to make informed decisions to increase production efficiency. [74].
The ability to dynamically respond to real-time changes in the production environment presents
promising production scheduling opportunities. A manufacturing environment contains various
sources of uncertainty, including machine breakdowns and manufacturing time. A previously
optimal production schedule might become sub-optimal or infeasible as these uncertainties are
realized. [73] Thus, it may be advantageous to exploit real-time data and analytics to dynami-
cally allocate tasks to resources in the face of unforeseen disruptions. A concept that may meet
these criteria is Online Scheduling. The online scheduling technique does not prepare a schedule
beforehand but makes real-time allocation decisions as dynamic events occur. [26]

1.2
Online Production Scheduling
Production scheduling is a crucial tool for better resource utilization and productivity in man-
ufacturing. Production scheduling is allocating machines to process jobs at specific times. The
purpose is to create a schedule that optimizes an objective function, such as minimizing the
makespan or cost of completing the jobs. The objective is achieved by communicating to the
manufacturing facility when a job should be processed at which machine. Production schedul-
ing reaps many benefits, including change-over or machine setup reduction, cost reduction, less
inventory, and higher customer service levels. [1].
Production scheduling is a complex optimization problem historically solved through mixed-
integer programming (MIP), constraint-based optimization, heuristics, and various other com-
puter science techniques [1] [2]. The manufacturing industry often utilizes production schedul-
ing modules within Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems [3]. The MIP and heuristic
approaches are, for example, leveraged in the Production Planning and Detailed Scheduling
(PP/DS) features of the Advanced Planning Module in SAP Integrated Business Planning (IBP)
[63] [62]. However, these approaches are computationally difficult and may be incapable of han-
dling the complexities and uncertainties of a real production environment. [73]
Robust scheduling is another approach to handling production uncertainties. In robust schedul-
ing, the manufacturer forecasts potential disruptions and accommodates these uncertainties into
the production schedule. Thus, if these events come to pass, then the production schedule should
be able to handle them. However, the production schedule will be less effective if these events
do not occur. [75]
Online scheduling is performed with incomplete knowledge of the problem instance, i.e. future
disruptions or uncertainties. Information about the production state or input data is provided
gradually to the algorithm. Thus, task assignments must be executed without knowing the
complete problem instance.
Partial solutions are calculated as new information is acquired.
Online scheduling is the intersection between deterministic and stochastic scheduling. In offline
or deterministic scheduling, one has complete knowledge of the problem instance beforehand
and is therefore not subject to uncertainties. In stochastic scheduling, incomplete knowledge is
represented by random variables drawing from a probability distribution. Online scheduling can
never perform better than offline, deterministic scheduling, where the complete problem instance
is known in advance.[26] Online scheduling is advantageous as it often mirrors real-world pro-
duction environments where uncertainties are gradually realized throughout the manufacturing
process and immediate decisions must be made with only sparse information. [75]

1.3
Deep Reinforcement Learning
Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) offers an alternative approach to the traditionally used pro-
duction scheduling methods. Reinforcement learning is modelled as a Markov Decision Process
where an agent learns to make optimal decisions by interacting with its environment.
The
learning agent observes the state of the environment and subsequently chooses an action. Based
on the decisions or actions that the learning agent takes, the environment will respond with a
reward or penalty and a new state. The agent’s goal is to learn a set of behavioural policies to
maximize its cumulative reward over time. Reinforcement learning is distinct from other forms
of machine learning as it learns through trial and error and delayed rewards. [64]
Deep reinforcement learning integrates deep neural networks with reinforcement learning and
has shown promising results in many fields, such as robotics. The policy can be represented as a
deep neural network with the state as an input and a probability distribution over actions as an
output. Deep reinforcement learning has been studied in production systems due to its ability
to produce robust production schedules in complex and uncertain manufacturing environments
without complete knowledge of the system dynamics. Its ability to update its choices based
on real-time feedback makes it suitable for production scheduling, as much uncertainty occurs
through delays, breakdowns, and other sudden changes to the state of the production line.[31]
Production environments are often complex, and training the learning agent can be computa-
tionally expensive [76]. Action masking and reward shaping techniques are leveraged to improve
the performance and efficiency of the deep reinforcement learning approach.
Many decisions or actions may be infeasible in a complex production environment, e.g., the
agent cannot assign a job to a busy machine. An abundance of invalid actions may slow down
the agent’s learning process. Action masking limits the number of actions the agent can take to
a set of valid actions based on the state of the environment. [56] Thus, the algorithm’s efficiency
increases as action masking removes the need to explore unfeasible actions while ensuring the
real-life practicality of the learned policy.[57]
Reward shaping involves feeding the learning agent additional sub-rewards to guide it towards a
desired behaviour, which can accelerate the learning process. [38] [39] Since production schedul-
ing to minimize makespan will only provide sparse rewards, e.g. the total makespan at the end
of the simulation, reward shaping is a suitable tool for improving the learning process. Careful
consideration of the reward-shaping structure must be given to not introduce biases into the
algorithm’s training. The reward structure should align with the underlying objective of the
production schedule. [40]

1.4
The Purpose of the Paper
To improve performance and efficiency, the project aims to apply deep reinforcement learning
with action masking and various reward-shaping techniques on a multi-stage, flow shop produc-
tion scheduling problem. The paper will address the following research questions:
1. How does production scheduling benefit from deep reinforcement learning?
2. To what extent can action masking improve the performance of deep reinforcement learn-
ing for production scheduling?
3. How can reward shaping improve the performance of deep reinforcement learning for
production scheduling, and which reward functions are most effective?
The remainder of the report will use the following structure:
• Section 2: A literature review of current research on deep reinforcement learning for
online scheduling, invalid action masking, and reward shaping.
• Section 3: A description of the problem setting and formulation
• Section 4: A description of the solution method used
• Section 5: A presentation and discussion of the experimental design and results
• Section 6: Main findings, limitations and future research potential within deep reinforce-
ment learning for online scheduling.

Literature Review
2.1
Online Scheduling
The paper From rescheduling to online scheduling by Dhruv Gupta et al.
investigates the
shortcomings of traditional event-triggered rescheduling in a chemical process and how online
scheduling can be used to address them. Dhruv Gupta et al. state that a predefined schedule
can become sub-optimal in the face of realized uncertainties or disruptions requiring a reactive
rescheduling procedure.[16] The paper shows that accounting for schedule uncertainties can par-
tially or fully mitigate their effect. However, the paper also argues that small delays are likely
not to be observed, which may render the schedule sub-optimal or infeasible. [17] Through
numerical experiments, the paper showed that accounting for newly available information about
delays, new production orders, changing demand, etc., can lead to better performance than
accounting for uncertainty in the production schedule as a built-in leeway. Furthermore, the
experiments showed that periodic rescheduling yields better results than waiting for a reschedul-
ing event trigger. Periodic rescheduling contrasts with current rescheduling methods in which
exceeding a certain deviation measure triggers a rescheduling. [18] Thus, the paper highlights
the importance of accounting for newly available information when scheduling. The need for
an adaptable scheduling technique in the face of new information motivates the necessity of
researching online scheduling. [19]
Dhruv Gupta et al. showed the necessity of online scheduling and accounting for new infor-
mation.
The paper ”Scheduling Algorithms: Challenges Towards Smart Manufacturing” by
Abebaw Degu Workneh et al. is a literature review, critically analyzing existing production
scheduling algorithms from the viewpoint of smart manufacturing. The paper highlights the
challenges and benefits of using different algorithms for online scheduling in a complex, chang-
ing production environment enabled by Industry 4.0 technologies. [73]
The paper argues that production environments in practice need an efficient and adaptive al-
gorithm to keep up with the changes. The application of meta-heuristics and mathematical
programming is limited in real time due to the computation time for finding an optimal solu-
tion. [77] Furthermore, they are underperforming when increasing the complexity and scale of
the scheduling problem. [73]
The paper states that deep reinforcement learning has proven effective for online scheduling for
several reasons. First, it communicates with the dynamic production environment and learns
from the feedback data or trial-and-error to optimize its scheduling strategy. Second, it requires
much offline training but is effective when executing its policy. Third, it is more adaptable
than classic dispatching rules. Compared to reinforcement learning, the main advantage of deep
reinforcement learning is that it can simplify the complex model to a neural network instead of
5
==PAGE:16==
a Q-table, allowing for continuous state spaces and high dimensions. However, since the neural
network functions as a black box, it is unknown or difficult to asses how the DRL will act in
uncertain situations. Furthermore, for policy gradient methods, there is no guarantee of finding
an optimal solution and designing a reward structure is challenging. [76]
The paper found that adaptive scheduling using reinforcement learning or deep reinforcement
learning would be the most well-suited approach for dynamic, uncertain environments, as in real
industrial environments. [78]
2.2
Deep Reinforcement Learning
Abebaw Degu Workneh et al. recommended using reinforcement or deep reinforcement learning
for online scheduling. The paper ”Playing Atari with Deep Reinforcement Learning by Mnih et
al. investigates the application of deep reinforcement learning in playing various Atari games [49]
comparing its performance to reinforcement learning. In the paper, the intelligent agent learned
by interacting with the Atari emulator or environment. At each time step, the agent would
observe the visual image of the screen as its input state, represented by a vector of pixel values.
Subsequently, the agent would select an action from a discrete list of legal, possible moves in the
game. The environment or emulator would respond with a reward depending on the changed
game score and a new state.[50] Mnih et al. tested the deep reinforcement learning approach on
seven different Atari games to ensure the robustness of the approach. They compared it to the
best-performing reinforcement learning algorithms. The paper received state-of-the-art results
on six of the seven games while performing better than a human expert on three. The paper
highlights how a deep reinforcement learning agent can learn intricate patterns and behaviour
from performing tasks in a complex environment and outperform typical reinforcement learning
and human expertise. [51]
Production environments are often large and complex.
In addition, the deep reinforcement
learning agents require intensive training, which is time-consuming [76]. Techniques like action
masking may speed up the learning process of the agent. In the paper ”A Closer Look at In-
valid Action Masking in Policy Gradient Algorithms”, Shengyi Huang et al. provide a deep
dive into how invalid action masking works in policy gradient algorithms like Proximal Policy
Optimization (PPO). The paper studies the effect of four strategies to avoid sampling invalid
actions: invalid action masking, naive invalid action masking, masking removed, and invalid
action penalty. [56]
In invalid action masking, one replaces the logit of masked actions with a large, negative num-
ber. When re-normalizing, the action probability distribution and gradient of the logit for the
masked action is very close to 0. Naive invalid action masking follows the same principle as
6
==PAGE:17==
invalid action masking. However, the gradient of the logit is not replaced with 0, i.e. the gradi-
ent is updated with the ”non-masked” probability. In masking removed, the learning agent is
trained the same way as in invalid action masking, but the masking feature is removed when
executing the policy. Lastly, in an invalid action penalty, one gives the learning agent negative
rewards for choosing invalid actions. As the agent tries to maximize its cumulative reward, it
will avoid choosing invalid actions. [58]
The approaches were tested on multiple test instances of a simple strategy game with clear in-
valid actions, such as walls blocking the player from moving. The paper concluded that invalid
action masking resulted in the fastest convergence and scaled well when the action space and
the number of invalid actions increased. Furthermore, the three remaining strategies failed to
perform similarly to invalid action masking. [59]
Thus, the paper found invalid action masking to be the most effective technique to avoid sam-

3.1
Problem Description
The purpose is to create a simulation that schedules a set of jobs to a set of machines at specific
times to minimize the total makespan of the schedule. The production environment consists of
a multi-stage, flow-shop production line where each stage contains multiple machines. The pro-
duction layout is shown in figure 1. At the beginning of the simulation, the production receives
a production order with a list of jobs that must be produced. In this simulation, the production
order consists of 30 jobs. Each job must go through one machine at each production stage in
order before it is finished. The model contains no restriction on which jobs must be processed
at each machine, meaning a job can be assigned to any available machine within its production
stage. A machine can only be assigned another job once it has completed processing its current
assignment. Thus, there are no machine-specific queues. However, there are buffers before each
production stage. A buffer is a waiting position when finishing a production stage and waiting
to be assigned a machine in the next stage, e.g. if the machines in the next stage are busy. The
buffers contain no dispatching rules such as First-In-First-out.
Figure 1: Four-stage flow shop production layout of the scheduling model.
Once a machine begins manufacturing a job, two time components occur:
processing and
sequence-dependent setup times. Setup time is needed to configure the settings of a machine
when switching from one job to another. The machines’ setup times are sequence-dependent as
they are asymmetrical between jobs, meaning the setup time is not the same when switching
from job 1 to 2 and from job 2 to 1. A machine will maintain its most recent configuration until
a new job is assigned. Thus, the machines do not lose their setup when idle. The setup time
depends on the assigned machine, its current job setup, and the assigned job. The processing
time depends on the assigned machine and job. Both time components are exponentially dis-
tributed around their mean with a minimum allowed time equal to half of their mean.

4.1
Deep Reinforcement Learning
4.1.1
Reinforcement Learning
In reinforcement learning, the algorithm learns through trial and error. Reinforcement learning
simulates how an intelligent agent learns to make decisions by interacting with its environment.
[52] An environment is a world where the agent exists, defining the rules and consequences of
the agent’s decisions. [53] In the case of this thesis, the environment is the production schedul-
ing environment described in section 3.1. The learning agent’s actions are assigning a job to
a machine. Besides the agent and its environment, reinforcement learning contains three more
elements: a reward signal, a policy, and a value function.[65]
Every time the learning agent interacts with the environment, the environment will respond
with a reward, e.g. increments in the time period. The received reward signal depends on the
action or assignment made by the agent in the specific state of the environment. The agent’s
actions may indirectly influence future reward signals by changing the state of the environment,
e.g. allowing for shorter setup times. The agent aims to maximize the cumulative reward signal.
[65] [53]
The policy is the agent’s learned behaviour, meaning it is a mapping between the state of the
environment and the action taken by the agent in that state. The purpose is to learn a policy
that maximizes the cumulative reward. Therefore, if an action is followed with an immediate
low reward, then the agent may change its policy not to select that action when finding itself in
the same state again and vice versa. [65]
The value function determines which actions are preferable in the long run in contrast to the
reward signal, which only considers the immediate interaction. The value function outputs the
expected value of a state, meaning the discounted, cumulative reward expected in the future
after being in that state. Thus, a state might result in a low immediate reward, but it can have
a high value if it leads to more preferable states where higher rewards can be extracted.[66]
Assigning a job to a machine with a high manufacturing time might be advantageous if the ma-
chine setup allows for a shorter manufacturing time in the future. Moreover, selecting an action
with a high immediate reward may close future opportunities available to the agent. When the
agent selects an action, it must have foresight and consider indirect and delayed consequences.
[67] In reinforcement learning, actions are chosen based on value, not reward. [66]
The trial and error process enables the reinforcement learning agent to learn. The learning
agent is not instructed on which actions to take but is guided through rewards and penalties.
The goal is for the learning agent to discover by itself a policy or behaviour that will maximize
16
==PAGE:27==
its cumulative reward in the long term. Learning through trial and error and delayed rewards
are two characteristics that distinguish reinforcement learning from other branches of machine
learning. [52] [68]
The environment is modelled as a Markov Decision Process in reinforcement learning. Thus, in
discrete time steps, the agent obtains an information signal containing the current state of the
environment, st ∈S, and the realized reward rt ∈R. Here S is the set of possible states. Based
on the state of the environment, the agent performs an action, at ∈A(st), where A(st) is the set
of possible actions in state st. The action prompts a new state, st+1, and reward, rt+1, in the
next step. Thus, three signals pass between the learning agent and the environment: the action,
state, and reward. Figure 2 shows the interaction between the agent and the environment in a
high-level reinforcement learning setup. [69]
Figure 2: Overview of the agent-environment interaction in reinforcement learning
After each time step, the agent will map and update the probability of taking a specific action,
at = a, in a specific state, st = s. The probability mapping is also known as the agent’s policy
denoted πt(a|s). The agent’s learned experience will change its policy depending on the rein-
forcement learning algorithm used. The time steps are not necessarily fixed intervals but are
successive stages or moments of decision-making. Thus, there can be multiple steps within a
time period. [69].
Reinforcement learning is faced with the challenge of balancing exploration and exploitation.
Exploitation is when the agent capitalizes on their past experiences by selecting an action that
has proven to yield a high reward for the specific state. In opposition to exploitation is explo-
ration. Exploration is when the agent chooses a previously unselected action for the specific
17
==PAGE:28==
state to explore whether it would yield a higher reward. The agent must exploit its experience
to accumulate higher rewards and explore to find more advantageous actions and make better
decisions in the future. The agent must balance exploration and exploitation and not solely
pursue one. Typically, the exploration rate is high at the beginning of the learning process,
and progressively, the agent exploits favourable actions. Furthermore, in stochastic problems,
the learning agent must select an action multiple times to gain an accurate assessment of the
expected reward. [70]
Reinforcement learning contains several hyperparameters that affect how the agent makes de-
cisions. First, the learning rate, denoted by 0 ≤α ≤1, is the stepsize parameter of the value
function by limiting the update to the value function. A high learning rate can result in faster
convergence, but the solution might be unstable due to the excessive policy updates and vice
versa. Thus, the learning rate influences the relative importance of the algorithm to new or old
learning. [71]
Second, the discount factor, denoted by 0 ≤γ ≤1, determines the present value of future
rewards so the agent can compare the long-term benefits of its possible actions. The discount
factor influences the importance of immediate rewards against long-term rewards. [72] A high
γ value prefers long-term rewards, while a low value prefers immediate rewards. [53]
4.1.2
Deep Reinforcement Learning
Deep reinforcement learning refers to when a deep neural network (DNN) is incorporated into
the learning agent in reinforcement learning. [52] Deep reinforcement learning has proven ad-
vantageous when dealing with large, unstructured environments by detecting complex patterns.
Combining reinforcement learning with a neural network results in the ability to learn and
generalize over a large amount of data. The neural network output is an action probability
distribution with the environment’s state as an input, referred to as the policy, πθ(a|s). Thus,
the neural network predicts the action that will lead to the highest cumulative reward from
the current state of the environment. [54] Figure 3, shows the high level architecture of deep
reinforcement learning.
18
==PAGE:29==
Figure 3: Overview of the agent-environment interaction in deep reinforcement
learning.
Neural networks operate as a value function approximator, predicting the value of a state. Fur-
thermore, for large-scale problems, using neural networks is beneficial compared to traditional
reinforcement learning approaches. Updating and looking up values in a Q-table can become
infeasible for large state and action spaces, especially for continuous spaces. The neural network
approximates the value function through coefficients or weights that establish the relationship
between input and output. As the neural network is trained on samples from the state and
action space, the weights are adjusted according to gradients with less value prediction error,
i.e. the expected against the actual value. Thus, as training commences, the neural network of
the deep reinforcement learning algorithm should be able to estimate the value of a state and
produce an action probability distribution that reflects the best long-term actions. [55]
4.1.3
Proximal Policy Optimization
In 2017, OpenAI introduced a new policy gradient method for reinforcement learning called
Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) in their paper ”Proximal Policy Optimization Algorithms”.
In the past, gathering decent results from policy gradient methods has been challenging. The
algorithms are susceptible to the stepsize: if it is too small, then progress is slow, and if it is
too large, then the policy can have a significant drop in performance due to destructively large
updates. PPO alternates between sampling feedback data from interacting with the environ-
ment and optimizing a surrogate objective function. Furthermore, PPO introduces a clipping
function to the surrogate objective that disincentivizes the algorithm from making excessively
large policy updates. [27] The literature review in section 2 showed that PPO has proven ad-
vantageous for previous implementations of deep reinforcement learning in online production

4.2
Invalid Action-Masking
A complex environment may have constraining rules that inhibit specific actions as they vio-
late said rules. Thus, the learning agent may sample an action from a sizeable discrete action
space based on the learned policy, which is invalid or infeasible. The set of valid actions that
the learning agent can use depends on the state of the environment. An action valid in one
state does not necessarily mean it is valid in another. Invalid actions lead to inefficient action
sampling, resulting in higher computation times and a slower learning process. Invalid action
masking is a technique that limits the number of actions an agent can perform in a state to a
set of valid actions. [60]
A policy is often represented as a neural network in deep reinforcement learning. The neu-
ral network outputs logits are converted to an action probability distribution or policy, π(a|s),
using a softmax function. The algorithm then samples an action from the action probability
distribution. Invalid action masking masks the logit of an invalid action by setting the logit to
a large negative number. Thus, when the action probability distribution is re-normalized, the
logit corresponding to an invalid action is close to zero. Furthermore, the gradient of the invalid
action masking will also be zero. Masking the invalid actions for a state improves the agent’s
learning, as it does not need to explore infeasible actions. [61]
4.2.1
Implementation of Invalid Action Masking
In this paper, invalid action masking is applied in four instances. First, if a machine is busy,
all assignments to that machine will be masked until it has finished processing its current job.
Second, if a job is being processed on a machine, then all machine assignments for that job will
be masked until it has been processed on its current machine. Third, if a job has completed a
production stage, all assignments for the job to the machines in that stage and those below will
be masked for the rest of the simulation. Thus, if a job is complete, then it cannot be assigned
to any more machines. Fourth, assignments that do not fit the machine stage and the job status
will be temporarily masked. If a job is ready for stage three, assignments to stage 4 will be
masked until the job is ready for stage 4.
4.3
Reward Shaping
The reward structure of the proposed model can be characterized as sparse. A penalty is only
incurred in the environment with increments in the time period, meaning the resulting reward
will be 0 for many actions. Moreover, the penalty may not directly result from the agent’s most
recent action. If rewards are scarce in an environment, the learning agent may take a long time
to recognize states that can yield high rewards. [40] To circumvent the reward sparsity and
21
==PAGE:32==
accelerate the agent’s learning process, one may manipulate the reward signal through reward
shaping. [7] However, utilizing reward shaping may introduce bias into the agent’s policy if the
new reward signal does not align with the overall goal of the problem. [40]
Reward shaping is a supplement to the original reward of the environment and can be represented
in equation 4.6.
r′ = r + f
(4.6)
where r is the expected or original reward signal from the environment, f is the supplementary
reward from the reward shaping, and r′ is the resulting shaped reward feed to the agent. Thus,
the policy will be optimized after maximizing the cumulative shaped reward. [8]
This paper will investigate the effectiveness of several types of reward shaping. First is a teacher
policy where the agent attempts to learn from a predefined heuristic. Second, the agent receives
a reward signal when completing auxiliary tasks.
4.3.1
Teacher Policy: Shortest Processing Time
Policy transfer methods involve conveying knowledge from a predefined ”teacher policy” to the
learning agent. In this instance, the teacher policy is the shortest processing time heuristic. The
SPT heuristic selects the job-machine assignment which has the shortest processing time. The
teacher policy may be transferred to a learning agent by manipulating its reward signal through
reward shaping. The reward signal will be designed to reward the agent for following the teacher
policy’s recommended action. Thus, when the agent selects an action, it will be punished if it
does not choose the SPT assignment. Having the learning agent mimic the teacher’s policy may
speed up the learning process and potentially teach a better policy.[7] The high-level concept of
how the agent interacts with the environment and the teacher policy is shown in figure 4.

4.4
Hyperparameter Tuning
Deep reinforcement learning algorithms’ performance and learning process are sensitive to their
hyperparameter settings. Hyperparameter tuning involves searching for the optimal configu-
ration of hyperparameters. Bayesian Optimization and Hyperband (BOHB) is a simple and
effective hyperparameter optimization method which combines the benefits from both Bayesian
Optimization (BO) and Hyperband (HB). [46] This paper will utilize the BOHB hyperparam-
eter tuning method as described in the paper ”BOHB: Robust and Efficient Hyperparameter
Optimization at Scale” by Stefan Falker et al.
Hyperband works by randomly sampling a set of hyperparameter configurations from their spe-
cific search range.
The acquired set of hyperparameters is subsequently tested on the deep
reinforcement learning algorithm on a limited budget of computational resources, e.g. a number
of training iterations. Hyperband utilizes successive halving by periodically ranking the con-
figurations based on their performance, where the η−1 best-performing configurations continue
24
==PAGE:35==
simulating. Successive halving is repeated until the maximum budget is reached and one hyper-
parameter configuration remains. Thus, hyperband’s successive halving continuously allocates
more resources to better-performing configurations. by iteratively eliminating underperforming
configurations early. Hyperband assists in quickly finding a well-performing set of hyperparam-
eters. [47]
According to the paper by Stefan Falker et al., BOHB enhances hyperband by replacing the
random sampling of hyperparameter configuration with a model-based search, i.e.
bayesian
optimization. Bayesian optimization creates a probabilistic model to estimate the behaviour of
the objective function using observed data points. The probabilistic model guides the search
for hyperparameter configurations more likely to yield well-performing results. Once a new set
of hyperparameters has been chosen and evaluated, BOHB will update its probabilistic model
with the new data points. Thus, bayesian optimization is used at the beginning of the hyper-
band procedure to select new configurations based on the previously evaluated hyperparameter
configurations. [47] [48]
4.4.1
Tuning of Hyperparameters
In this paper, BOHB will be used to tune the hyperparameter configuration of the PPO al-
gorithm.
The BOHB tuning algorithm samples a number of hyperparameter configurations
within a specified search range and tunes them over several training iterations in the production
scheduling environment. The following hyperparameters, earlier presented in this paper, will be
subject to tuning:
1. Learning Rate (α)
2. Discount Factor (γ)
3. Clip Parameter (ϵ)
4. General Advantage Estimation Parameter (λ)
5. Entropy Bonus Coefficient (c1)
6. Value Function Loss Coefficient (c2)
Apart from the hyperparameters mentioned above, the neural network’s architecture will also be
subject to tuning. The paper ”What Matters In On-Policy Reinforcement Learning? A Large-
Scale Empirical Study” by Marcin Andrychowicz et al. [35] found that experimenting with the
capacity of the network leads to better performance of the PPO algorithm. The neural network
will be tuned in terms of the number of nodes in each layer and the number of layers together
with the other hyperparameters.
25
==PAGE:36==
To match the environment’s reward structure and PPO’s objective, the hyperparameter con-
figurations are evaluated on the reward per episode, where BOHB will try to maximize this
objective. The final set of hyperparameters after the last round of successive halving will be
used during the training process of the learning agent. The search range of the hyperparameters
will be based on suggestions from the ”Proximal Policy Optimization Algorithms” paper by Ope-
nAI [30] and ”What Matters In On-Policy Reinforcement Learning? A Large-Scale Empirical
Study” by Marcin Andrychowicz et al [36].

Simulation Process

0 as described in section 3.2. The state is normalized between 0 and 1 when fed to the learning

5.3
Summary of Results
The experiments found that layouts A, B, and C could only solve the scheduling problem with
invalid action masking. The PPO algorithm’s policy likely incentivises invalid actions if they
yield a higher reward than alternative valid actions. The algorithm may get stuck without in-
valid action masking to stop the policy from repeatedly selecting the same invalid actions in the
next step.
The experiments found that reward shaping is beneficial for the performance of the PPO algo-
rithm in layouts A, B, and C. Moreover, a combination of invalid action masking, potential-based
reward shaping, and auxiliary reward shaping generally had the most stable performance across
all experiments. The PPO algorithm with invalid action masking and reward shaping had a
lower average relative gap to the optimal makespan of the test instances than the shortest pro-
cessing time heuristic for the two and four-stage layouts. However, the advantage of using deep
40
==PAGE:51==
reinforcement learning was only slight. Thus, in a practical setting, using the SPT heuristic
might be more advantageous as it is easier to implement and is more transparent in its deci-
sions. Furthermore, the SPT heuristic does not require training and hyperparameter tuning.
The PPO algorithm applied to the eight-stage production layout, layout C, did not perform
better than the SPT heuristic in any scenario.
The performance of the PPO algorithm diminished as the problem instance or layout became
larger and more complex. Since the same amount of training episodes were used for all layouts,
one may argue that the agent did not have adequate time to learn the problem instance for
layout C.
The PPO algorithm’s performance varied during the execution of the scenarios. Furthermore,
the received rewards during training in appendix 6.1.1, 6.1.2, and 6.1.3 show large fluctuations in
performance even towards the training process’ end. An explanation for the instability could be
the uncertainty around the setup and process times. The setup and process times were sampled
from an exponential distribution around their mean with a minimum time value equal to half
their mean. In addition, there was no maximum limit to their values. Thus, the performance
of the PPO may experience significant fluctuations due to the uncertain nature of the setup
and process times. Furthermore, if an assignment results in an unexpectedly long setup and
process time, the agent might disincentivize that choice in future steps, even if the expected
manufacturing time was advantageous.

6
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to investigate the use of deep reinforcement learning on online scheduling
problems. In the experiments of the thesis, the proximal policy optimization algorithm was
gradually combined with invalid action masking, potential-based reward shaping, and auxiliary
reward shaping to show their individual and combined effects on the algorithm’s performance.
To investigate how deep reinforcement learning performs under different levels of complexity,
the experiments were conducted on three flow shop production layouts consisting of two, four,
and eight stages.
The experiments found that invalid action masking is necessary when using deep reinforcement
learning in online scheduling, as the algorithm could not solve the problem without it. Neglect-
ing to use invalid action masking may result in the learning agent’s policy repeatedly selecting
invalid actions if they yield a better reward than alternative valid actions. The two- and four-
stage production layouts show that a combination of invalid action masking, potential-based
reward shaping, and auxiliary reward shaping improves the PPO algorithm’s performance and
outperforms the shortest processing time heuristic. However, the advantage of using deep rein-
forcement learning is slight. It can be argued that the shortest processing time heuristic would
be used in a practical setting since it is easier to implement and does not require training. In
the eight-stage production layout, the PPO algorithm did not perform better than the shortest
processing time heuristic with neither invalid action masking or reward shaping.
A limitation of this study stems from the performance comparison only being done on a set of
three layouts. It is difficult to conclude the general performance of deep reinforcement learning
in online scheduling from the experiments. Furthermore, the thesis did not assess whether the
layouts’ design was more favourable towards the shortest processing time heuristic.
The production scheduling model proposed in the thesis did not include specific deadlines for
finishing each job, which does not correspond to a real-world setting. Furthermore, the model
did not consider cost or energy factors when making the schedule. Including these factors may
have provided a more realistic setting for the experiments, which could have translated into
more practical managerial insights.
The study only considered one potential-based reward-shaping function and one auxiliary reward-
shaping function. In future research, one may study a more comprehensive selection of functions,
e.g. based on job completion, machine utilization, or energy consumption. The experiments in-
cluded a hyperparameter tuning of the number of hidden layers and nodes in the neural network.
In future research, one may benefit from a more systematic and in-depth approach to optimiz-
ing the neural network, including the activation function. Lastly, future research may benefit
from investigating how to practically deploy deep reinforcement learning in a real manufacturing
environment regarding system integration and data collection.
