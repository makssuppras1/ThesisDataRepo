Abstract
The indigenous microbiota of the gastrointestinal tract has within recent years proven to
have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on human health. Still, much uncertainty exist concerning
the detailed mechanisms of how these eﬀects are exerted, partly due to lack of in situ
experiments. In this study we present two complementary qPCR based methods designed
to evaluate growth and physiology of bacteria in situ. One method estimates bacterial
growth rate based on the ratio of genes near the origin of replication versus the genes
near the terminus (origin/terminus ratio), whereas the second method estimates growth
and physiology based on the ratio of 16S rRNA transcripts versus 16S rRNA genes (16S
ratio) which is normalised to P. ﬂuorescens. The two methods were evaluated in vitro on
three organisms with relevance for the human intestine: E. coli K-12 MG1655, E. faecalis
DSM 2570, and B. adolescentis DSM 20083. Additionally, we sampled and performed
deep sequencing of DNA and RNA of the bacterial communities throughout the intestinal
tract of two rats, which illustrated that the bacterial microbiota varied greatly both be-
tween individuals and between compartments. Overall, our study demonstrated that the
combined method showed potential for being an easy, cheap, and robust tool useful for
providing a comprehensive interpretation of growth, physiology and abundance of a single
species growing in situ.

Introduction
The indigenous microbiota inhabiting the human gastrointestinal tract (the gut micro-
biota) is incredibly diverse, complex, and one of the most dense bacterial environments
on Earth. More than 1000 diﬀerent bacterial species have been identiﬁed[24, 11], and it is
estimated that the intestine is the habitat of around 3 · 1013 microorganisms in total [90].
The gut microbiota is known to inﬂuence human health in several ways: they help us digest
the food we eat, produce vitamins that we can absorb, modulate our immune system in a
beneﬁcial way, and protect us against opportunistic pathogens [44].
Our knowledge of how the microbiota aﬀects our health has expanded immensely within
the last few decades, mainly due to methods such as metagenomic sequencing and metabolic
proﬁling of human faecal samples [67]. We now know that the bacteria inhabiting our body
has an inﬂuence on severe diseases such as colorectal cancer, obesity and its associated
metabolic disorders, insulin sensitivity, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, hypertension, in-
ﬂammatory bowel disease, asthma, atopy, hormone signalling eﬀecting our mood and
behaviour, as well as autism [72, 78].
Metagenomic analyses are most often based on deep sequencing of bacteria from faecal
samples with the attempt of mapping and identifying relative abundances of individual
species. Most often though, the microbiota proﬁles in faeces are not representative of
community proﬁles from other parts of the intestine. Furthermore, the relative abundance
measure of a certain species does not always provide knowledge of the activity, growth
and physiology of that species.
This has been exempliﬁed in various studies, e.g. Korem et al. [54]: here the bacterial
growth rate – estimated using the origin/terminus gene ratio – was seen to correlate
with inﬂammatory bowel disease and type II diabetes, while the relative abundance of
bacterial species could not be correlated with disease. Other studies have used 16S rRNA
gene sequencing to map the bacterial proﬁle through the intestinal tract, which diﬀered
from the faecal microbiota both in composition, diversity and density [107, 100]
Since the interaction between the gut bacteria and the host takes place through the entire
7 metres and ∼300m2 surface area of the gastrointestinal tract [107], knowledge about
growth and physiology of species at the various sites is crucial when analysing the causal
relationship between gut bacteria and human health. A robust method for examination
of the eﬀect of a single species of interest is often desired, e.g. when testing the eﬀect of a
probiotic species. In such a case, next generation sequencing (NGS) of an entire bacterial
community does not completely ﬁt the need, and a cheaper and more simple platform
would therefore be desirable.
2.1
Aim
The purpose of this study was to develop a qPCR based method to estimate bacterial
growth and physiology in situ by using the origin/terminus ratio in combination with a
relative 16S rRNA/rDNA ratio (16S ratio). Together these methods should complement
each other leading to a combined approach for a robust measurement of cellular state.
Last, we wanted to sequence the bacterial communities throughout the intestinal tract of
two rats to illustrate the importance of developing methods to monitor bacterial species
in situ. For a schematic overview of the current study, see Figure 1 on the following page.

2.3
Applications and advantages
The potential applications of the qPCR methods developed in this study includes both in
vitro as well as in situ studies in animal models. The main advantage of the method is that
cheap and easy testing can be performed for a single species growing in situ without having
to analyse the entire community e.g. by NGS. Some examples include; testing a probiotic
strain for its presence at diﬀerent locations down the intestinal tract; the ability of a
strain to persist in the mucus layer and adhere to epithelial cells; the eﬀect of a prebiotic
substrate on the growth of a probiotic bacteria; or to evaluate the eﬀect of two or more
4
==PAGE:11==
species on each others growth, e.g to examine the eﬀect of a probiotic strain on the growth
of a pathogen, or the eﬀect of an antibiotic treatment. Furthermore, using a normalisation
bacteria such as P. ﬂuorescens makes it possible to estimate the real bacterial count in
a compartment by normalising to the bacterial count of P. ﬂuorescens before and after
nucleotide extraction steps.
This way bacterial abundances can be compared between
diﬀerent sites, thereby providing additional information.

Motivational background
In this section the theoretical background motivating the work conducted in this study
will be presented. It includes a general presentation of the diversity and complexity of the
bacterial communities living in the human gastrointestinal tract, a short presentation of
the three organisms used: E. coli K-12 MG1655, E. faecalis DSM 2570, and B. adolescentis
DSM 20083, as well as examples of where our method for estimating bacterial growth and
physiology could be applied to improve the research ﬁeld.
3.1
The diversity of the human gastrointestinal tract
One of the major issues when researching the correlation between the intestinal microbiota
and human health is that the community proﬁles diﬀer immensely between individuals and
between the diﬀerent compartments. These variances can be explained by the changes in
the physiochemical composition and nutrient availability down the intestinal tract. The
upper parts of the gut are dominated by low bacterial load, low pH, presence of bile and
oxygen, as well as high nutrient availability, whereas the lower parts has higher bacterial
density, higher pH, low levels of bile and oxygen, lower supply of nutrients, and higher
ratio of host-indigestible polysaccharides [67] (see Figure 5 on the next page).

Much research is focussed around correlating bacterial proﬁles to human phenotypes. For
instance the ratio of Firmicutes:Bacteroidetes which has been seen to vary greatly between
individuals, and is suspected to be related to a variety of gastrointestinal disorders [67].
However, due to a great variety of diﬀerent bacteria being able to ﬁll the same function
in a community, e.g. production of certain short chain fatty acids, correlations often show
to be weak when only looking at the abundance of species. On the contrary, correlations
between functional modules present in bacteria often has been shown to be stronger. As
an example of this, a study found that a signiﬁcant enrichment in aspartate biosynthesis
was observed in males, and that the presence of starch degrading enzymes was correlated
to old age [9].
These types of studies are usually based on faecal samples from smaller groups of individ-

could be obtained by having more diverse study populations, as well as studying bacterial

3.2
Probiotics and prebiotics
Probiotic bacteria are deﬁned as: ”live microorganisms, which when consumed in ade-
quate amounts, confer a health beneﬁt on the host” [79]. Species from Lactobacilli and
Biﬁdobacterium are the most prevalent consumed probiotics and occur naturally in foods
such as milk and vegetables [40]. It has been shown that probiotics can have an inﬂuence
in; counteraction of opportunistic pathogens; amelioration of bowel disorders; allergy; lac-
tose intolerance [79]; colon cancer; as well as eﬀecting metabolic diseases such as obesity
and type II diabetes [56].
These eﬀects occur due to several functions exerted by the probiotic such as: interaction
with dendritic cells; maintenance the mucosal barrier, for instance by inducing mucus
production or enhancing tight junctions between epithelial cells; and by production of
antimicrobial compounds [86].
One of the ways to ensure and/or stimulate the growth of probiotic strains is to co-deliver
speciﬁc carbohydrates that are not digested by the host but selectively by the bacteria of
interest. Such carbohydrates are termed prebiotics [39]. Testing the eﬀect of a prebiotic
on selective stimulation of a probiotic is not a trivial task, since bacteria in the intestine
live in a complex environment where composition of both nutrients and other bacteria
varies.
Most studies with prebiotic and probiotics are therefore initially conducted in
vitro, followed by in vivo studies in model animals such as rats or mice. The method
developed in this study has the potential of being used to examine the in situ growth
and physiology of for example a probiotic-prebiotic combination diﬀerent places in the
intestine.

3.4
Intestinal bacteria and their competition for nutrients
The gastrointestinal tract is an extremely competitive environment consisting of a complex
community of around 3 · 1013 microorganisms in total [90], where approximately 1000
diﬀerent species has been identiﬁed [24]. One of the things determining the success of a
gut bacteria is its ability to compete against other species over nutrients. One strategy is
to rapidly be able to adapt to changes in both nutrient availability and composition. This
is mostly relevant for bacteria residing in the small intestine. In the colon, nutrients are
more scarce and consist mainly of non-digestible carbohydrates. Furthermore, the colon
is one of the most dense bacterial environments on earth and being able to compete for
nutrients is crucial for survival [11].
One of the main survival strategies, is to be the best among the total community to
utilise one or a few particular substrates. From this, it follows that the concentration
of this substrate is controlling the population size of that species. Substrates can either
be ingested by the host organism, or it can be by-products from synthesis of dietary
substrates performed by other organisms. This yields a wide variety of diﬀerent substrates
and therefore gives rise to the large diversity found in the intestinal environment [35].
Two species competing for the same limiting nutrient are normally not able to co-exist
because the species with the highest growth rate will out-compete the other, except if the
less competitive species has another advantage such as being able to adhere to epithelial
cells. Due to the complexity of this environment, implantation with a new species in the
intestine is not easily accomplished. However, when removing a species from the intestine,
the concentration of the limiting nutrient that normally supported this species rises. This
in turn gives room for growth of another bacterial population that also has the ability to
utilise this substrate [35]. These phenomenons supports the idea of co-delivering prebiotics
together with probiotics.
One of the ways in which the intestinal community can be altered is by administration
of antibiotics. In a study by Tulstrup et al. [97], this eﬀect was examined. It was ob-
served that the total bacterial load in faecal samples increased during antibiotic treatment
with amoxicillin, cefotaxime and vancomycin. The three antibiotics also decreased the
alpha-diversity and changed the composition of bacteria during treatment. For instance,
after amoxicillin or vancomycin administration the relative abundance of Firmicutes and
Actinobacteria were reduced while the relative abundance of Proteobacteria increased, es-
pecially the Enterobacteriaceae including E. coli [96]. Cefotaxime treatment was seen to
signiﬁcantly increase the relative abundance of Biﬁdobacteriaceae.
Some studies show that an alteration in the microbiota during antibiotic delivery can
be detected in up to two years post treatment (see Figure 9 on the next page). Often
the bacterial proﬁle is changed such that an increase is seen in bacteria with antibiotic
resistance, and/or that the proportion of anaerobic bacteria decreases (which was seen
post clindamycin treatment). Anaerobic bacteria are the main producers of volatile fatty
acids which are known to maintain gut health [46].

Theoretical background
In this section, the underlying theory supporting the usage of the origin/terminus and the
16S ratios as a measure for cell growth and physiology will be presented. This includes an
overall introduction to cell growth control, the control mechanisms of DNA replication and
ribosome synthesis, as well as a discussion of uses and limitations regarding the methods.
4.1
Bacterial cell-growth control
Among the most important attributes for single cell organisms are their ability to respond
rapidly to environmental shifts. Kjeldsgaard, Maaløe and Schaechter (1958) [51, 88] was
some of the ﬁrst to investigate how bacteria react on, and respond to their environment.
Two important studies were performed with Salmonella typhimurium and Escherichia coli
as models:
In one study, cell size was studied in relation to growth rate by subjecting bacterial
populations either to a shift-up (transfer from minimal media to a richer media), or a shift-
down (transfer from a richer media to a minimal media) (see Figure 10 on the following
page). In both experiments it was observed that regardless of the previous growth rate,
RNA content and cell mass responded immediately to the shift, whereas DNA synthesis
and cell division rate responded later. This study provided knowledge about the detailed
mechanisms underlying how cells increase in size during shift up: ﬁrst they synthesise
more RNA and protein, then when the cells have reached a certain mass, DNA content
increases due to initiation of DNA replication. When DNA replication is completed the
10
==PAGE:17==
cells starts to divide. The opposite eﬀect was seen for cells during shift-down: cell size and
RNA concentration respond immediately, followed by a decreases rate in DNA synthesis
and cell division [51].

In the other study [88], it was observed that the physiological state of a cell (size and
composition) primarily depended on the speciﬁc growth rate. This meant that only the
growth rate permitted by the medium where the cells were grown inﬂuenced the state of
the cell, and not the speciﬁc chemical composition of that medium. Following this they
concluded that at a given temperature, the average cell mass, RNA, DNA and the number
of nucleoids in each cell, increased exponentially with the growth rate [88] (see Figure 11
on the next page).
These two studies elucidate that synthesis of RNA and DNA as well as cellular mass
are largely correlated to growth rate in bacteria growing in balanced and pure cultures.
Furthermore they illustrate that cells are capable of rapidly adapting to nutrient shifts,
by changing the cellular macromolecular composition. Since most of the RNA in a cell
is rRNA (∼80%) [13], the concentration of ribosomes in a cell also correlates to growth
rate. This means that the cellular content of rRNA and DNA can be used as a measure
of bacterial growth rate under the right conditions, which is what we in this study take
advantage of.

always initiated, which is called the origin of replication, or OriC [43]. DNA replication
is performed in a bidirectional manner, beginning from the OriC in both directions, and
ending at the opposite site called the terminus (see Figure 12 on the following page).

takes place several times before the ﬁrst replication event has been terminated. Due to the
nature of the regulation mechanism for DNA replication, all OriC present in the cytoplasm
of a cell is initiated at the same time. This means that the amount of OriC in a cell most
often will be 2n, where n = 0, 1, 2, 3... [91] (see Figure 12 on page 12).
The initiation of DNA replication is thus tightly regulated and has been found to only
occur once a certain cellular mass per origin has been reached and is independent of the
cellular growth rate. The coupling between cell mass and DNA replication lies at the DnaA
protein, where an accumulation of DnaA bound to ATP in the cell during steady-state
growth, as well as an increase between DnaA bound to ATP over DnaA bound to ADP
(DnaA-ATP/DnaA-ADP ratio), determines when initiation occur [83, 31]. Furthermore,
since cell mass is linearly correlated to the growth rate [51] the number of initiation origins
must also be correlated to growth rate.
4.3
The origin-terminus ratio - uses, limitations and advantages
Using the origin/terminus ratio as a measure of growth rate has some limitations. Due
to the small diﬀerences in ratios of bacteria growing in vivo (usually between 1-2), the
method must be quite accurate in order to properly separate ratios of cells with low growth
rates.
As an example, lets look at the diﬀerence in origin/terminus ratio between cells doubling
every 120 minutes compared to every 180 minutes. If it takes 40 minutes to replicate
the entire E. coli genome, then in the ﬁrst population the average ratio will be 1.33
(40/120 · 100% = 33% is in the process of DNA replication, and has an origin-terminus
ratio of 2. This means that 66% of the population are not in the process of DNA replication
and therefore has a ratio of 1. This gives a total ratio of (0.33 · 2 + 0.67 · 1) = 1.33. The
population with a doubling time of 180 minutes will have a ratio of 1.22 (do the math).
13
==PAGE:20==
Considering the uncertainty regarding the method, detection of the diﬀerence between
these ratios in practise will be challenging.
One of the most important precautions to take is a rapid deactivation of the replisome at
sampling time in order to stop the replication fork. Otherwise DNA replication continues
until the terminus is reached giving an origin/terminus ratio of 1.
Other issues regarding this method includes mechanisms that potentially could destroy
the correlation between ratio and growth rate. For instance if something is stopping the
replication fork of bacteria while they are replicating their DNA (as is the case with some
antibiotics [52]). In such a case, cells would not grow but still have a high origin-terminus
ratio.
In a study by Korem et al. [54] next generation sequencing was used to ﬁnd the ori-
gin/terminus ratio. Compared to this approach, a qPCR method has both advantages
and disadvantages. The main disadvantages are that primers for each bacteria of interest
have to be developed and tested for speciﬁcity. Second, the method has diﬃculties de-
tecting diﬀerences between strains, since primers bind on regions that are shared between
closely related organisms. Third, the genome of the bacteria of interest has to be fully
sequenced in order to ﬁnd genes in the origin and terminus regions that can be used as a
template for the qPCR. However, the availability of whole genomes will also be necessary
for optimal quantiﬁcation using the sequencing approach.
One of the main advantages with the qPCR based approach is that when the method is
validated for use in a bacteria of interest, testing it in situ would be both cheaper and
faster compared to sequencing. For this reason, the qPCR method mostly has applications
when testing a single or a few bacteria. For instance a probiotic bacteria and its response
in growth rate to diﬀerent prebiotics. If diﬀerences between strains needs to be measured,
insertion of speciﬁc genes in the origin and terminus regions could be used as template in
the qPCR in order to separate these strains.
4.4
Ribosomes
4.4.1
Structure and function
Ribosomes are nucleoproteins build from both ribosomal RNA (rRNA) and ribosomal
proteins (r-proteins). In bacteria, ribosomes consists of two subunits: the large 50S subunit
(LSU) (S refers to Svedberg units which is a sedimentation rate), and the small 30S subunit
(SSU). Together these two subunits form the entire 70S ribosome (see Figure 14 on the
next page).
In E. coli the ribosome contains 52 diﬀerent r-proteins and 3 rRNA’s. The rRNA’s are
translated together as one transcript (30S). After transcription, 30S rRNA is processed to
form the three units: 16S rRNA, 23S rRNA, and 5S. 16S becomes part of the SSU whereas
5S and 23S becomes part of the LSU [104, 13] (see Figure 14 on the following page).
Due to their role in protein synthesis, ribosomes are present in all cells, and the secondary
structure of rRNA is highly conserved between all species [13]. However, the 16S rRNA
contains several hypervariable regions (see Figure 15 on the next page). These regions
have been used to assign the phylogenetic relationship between species, and can also be
used as templates for designing organism speciﬁc primers. In this study we use organism
speciﬁc primers to calculate the normalised ration of ribosomes in a cell.

4.4.2
Regulation
From a cellular point of view, proteins should only be synthesised when they are needed,
such as during fast growth where the cells are rapidly dividing. Therefore, the synthesis
of rRNA and r-proteins is tightly regulated. One of the most important control mech-
anisms regarding ribosomal concentration lies at the level of rRNA transcription [74].
Other factors contributing to the regulation during diﬀerent growth conditions include;
the transcription rate of r-protein genes; the translation rate of r-protein mRNA; as well
as the ribosomal degradation rate. This section will give an overview of these regulation
mechanisms and how they work in correspondence.
4.4.2.1
The concentration of ribosomes determines protein synthesis rate:
It has been shown that during medium and fast growth (µ > 0.5 generations/hour), all
ribosomes are occupied in performing protein synthesis, and since the rate of enzymatic
activity of the ribosomes remains fairly constant, it is the concentration of the ribosomes
in a cell that determines the rate of protein synthesis. [88, 38].
However, exceptions has been observed at very low growth rates, where for instance bacte-
15
==PAGE:22==
ria such as E. coli can have a certain amount of non-occupied ribosomes. This mechanism
makes the bacteria able to rapidly adapt to a changing environment (for instance a rapid
increase in nutrient availability) [74]. Ribosomes can be inactivated by a dimerisation
event of two 70S ribosomes to form one 100S ribosome, due to binding of the ribosomal
modulation factor (RMF) [99]. The concentration of RMF increases during the transition
phase from exponential to stationary growth, resulting in a greater amount of non-occupied
ribosomes and a decrease in protein synthesis. If growth rate again should increase, RMF
is rapidly released from the ribosomes (within 1 minute). This dissociates the 100S ribo-
somes, followed by an almost immediate increase in protein synthesis rate [103].
4.4.2.2
Regulation of rRNA transcription - the stringent response:
As men-
tioned, one of the most important regulatory mechanisms for controlling the concentration
of ribosomes in a cell, is to control the transcription rate of rRNA. This control mechanism
is part of the stringent response, activated during amino acid starvation or cellular stress,
and which has been extensively studied in E. coli (see Figure 16).

4.4.2.3
Regulation of r-proteins:
Even though
most of the ribosomal regulation lies at regulating the
levels of rRNA, both transcription and translation of
r-proteins can inﬂuence ribosomal concentration [73].
During most growth conditions (medium-high growth)
r-protein synthesis is subjected to translational con-
trol of r-protein mRNA, and the promoters of r-protein
genes are constitutive. Only at very low growth rates
is transcription of r-protein genes rate limiting for ribo-
some synthesis (µ < 0.5 generations per hour) [73, 38].
The genes encoding the r-proteins are scattered into at
least 20 diﬀerent operons all over the genome of E. coli.
The translation of the mRNA from these operons are
regulated by a negative feedback mechanism where an r-protein from a given operon acts
as a translational repressor for all the genes present in its own operon [73] (see Figure 18).
The r-proteins capable of this negative feedback inhibition, binds to regions of the mRNA
which are homologous to the regions where they bind the rRNA once they are assembled
into ribosomes. However, they bind with lower aﬃnity to the mRNA, thereby making
sure that inhibition of their own translation only occurs when they are not assembled into
ribosomes. In this way, the synthesis of r-proteins are indirectly regulated by the stringent
response; since a decrease in rRNA levels, leads to higher amount of free r-proteins [74].
Together, the regulation of r-protein synthesis and the regulation of rRNA synthesis, en-
sure that all the components needed to build ribosomes are present in the correct amounts
during diﬀerent growth conditions.
4.4.2.4
Degradation of ribosomes, rRNA, and r-proteins:
Stable RNA’s, such
as rRNA’s and tRNA’s, are termed stable because they in general, are considered to be
much more stable than mRNA’s. However, stability depends to a large extend on the state
of the cell, and has shown to be correlated to growth rate. At low growth rates, newly
synthesised rRNA which is not incorporated into ribosomes, and therefore in excess, can
reach degradation rates of up to 70%. In exponentially growing cells, more of the newly
synthesised rRNA is incorporated into ribosomes and the degradation rate decrease to
approximately 10% [38]. Studies has shown that this shift in degradation rate changes
rapidly as soon as nutrients starts to become limiting [29]
Even though rRNA is more stable than mRNA, free ribosomal proteins are even more
stable.
This mechanism facilitates fast adaptation to changes in nutrient availability,
because r-proteins needs to be ﬁrst transcribed and then translated before they can be
incorporated into ribosomes, whereas, rRNA rapidly can be transcribed and be ready for
use. The molecules are most stable when assembled into ribosomes [29] (see Figure 19 on
the next page).

Halvorsen [66], it was shown that during nitrogen starvation in E. coli, ribosomes was
degraded, and in non-growing cells they showed that the degradation rate of both rRNA
and r-proteins incorporated into the ribosomes was approximately 5% /hour. However,

of time when entering stationary phase, the ribosomes are diluted leading to a decreased
amount in each cell [51].
4.5
16S rRNA/rDNA ratio as an estimate for growth and physiology
As discussed, ribosomal content and therefore also the ratio between 16S rRNA transcripts
and 16S rRNA genes, is fairly proportional to growth rate in balanced cultures except at
very low rates [74]. However, in more complicated settings, ribosomal content is not always
directly correlated to growth rate but instead reﬂects the environmental conditions, the life
strategy, and/or the history of the cell. Therefore it provides a more complex interpretation
of cellular state, compared to the origin/terminus ratio.
To illustrate this point some examples where ribosomal concentration was not directly
correlated to growth rate will be presented here:
Bacteria previously grown at low growth rates was observed to lose rRNA slower, compared
to bacteria grown in a batch culture at a higher growth rate when both cultures were
exposed to a starvation environment [75].
Heat treatment of Salmonella typhimurium
was seen to cause an extensive loss of ribosomes leading to cell death. However, carbon-
starved and magnesium-supplemented S. typhimurium, maintained stable levels of rRNA
19
==PAGE:26==
and survived much better [95]. Studies with E. coli have shown that phosphate starvation
was leading to an extensive loss of ribosomes, also followed by cell death [27]. In the
genera: Synechococcus and Prochlorococcus, it was seen that ribosome amount and growth
rate was linearly correlated at intermediate growth rates. However, at very high growth
rates, ribosomal levels started to decrease [16].
Last, in another study the ribosomal
concentration was seen to increase for certain species when these were entering a dormant
stage. Apparently to prepare for a rapid return to a vegetative state [16].
Additionally the amount of ribosomes has been found to vary between species even when
these grow at the same rate. Therefore, when employing ribosomal concentration as an
evaluation of bacterial growth, information regarding the ribosomal concentration during
diﬀerent settings of the respective species must be taken into consideration [16].
In our study we apply both the origin/terminus and a normalised 16S ratio, making it
possible to get a more complex interpretation of the cellular state. .

Related work
Several methods have proven useful for estimating growth of bacteria in situ. Examples
include; high throughput sequencing of the intestinal communities; dilution of a non-
replicate marker; radioisotope techniques; viable cell count by plating; the origin/terminus
ratio; the concentration of ribosomes; and the ratio of rRNA/rRNA genes (16S ratio). This
section will focus on presenting studies where the origin/terminus ratio or the ribosomal
concentration was used.
More ribosomal studies than origin/terminus studies will be
presented due to the higher complexity of this method, and because more studies have
been performed.
Not because the origin/terminus method is less important.
In this
study we propose to use both methods for a more robust measure of bacterial growth and
physiology.
5.1
Studies using the origin-terminus ratio
The origin/terminus ratio has been used for many diﬀerent types of experiments as a
measure for bacterial growth rate. For example to examine the growth rate of temperature
sensitive strains when shifting from a restrictive to a permissive temperature by inserting
prophages near the origin and terminus regions [50], or by qPCR to evaluate the eﬀect of
certain proteins on DNA initiation by using mutant strains [71].
The origin/terminus ratio has also been found using high throughput sequencing. Here
the DNA sequences from a species was mapped to its genome.
The number of reads
mapping to the origin versus the number mapping to the terminus region was used as an
estimate for origin/terminus ratio. In a study by Korem et al. [54] it was shown that the
origin/terminus ratio of bacterial communities from faecal samples correlated to speciﬁc
phenotypes in humans, e.g. inﬂammatory bowel disease and type II diabetes, whereas the
abundance measures of the bacteria alone did not.
5.2
Ribosomal studies
5.2.1
16S rRNA/rDNA ratio studies
In a study by Campbell et al. [18] 16S rDNA as well as 16S rRNA was sequenced from
coastal bacteria. For most bacteria, the relative abundance measures obtained from DNA

samples, were seen to correlate to the relative activity, found based on the RNA samples.
For other taxa however, it was seen that some of the rare bacteria (low abundance in
DNA) were had very high activity (high relative RNA abundance), and that this activity
decreased as these bacteria became more abundant [18]. In another paper the 16S ratio
was used to compare bacterial activity down sediment layers, and a change in RNA proﬁle
was observed down the layers, indicating a diﬀerence in activity for the speciﬁc bacteria
in relation to their location [53].
The 16S ratio has also been applied to examine bacterial intestinal communities, e.g. in
the study by Perez-Cobas et al.
[81].
Here, the community from faecal samples was
evaluated with 16S rRNA and rDNA sequencing and was compared before, during, and
after diﬀerent antibiotic treatments. Here it was seen that both the relative abundances
as well as the relative activity was altered during treatment, which is important when
evaluating the real eﬀect of antibiotics on the microbiota.
5.2.2
Comparing ribosomal content between diﬀerent sites in situ
The ribosomal content in cells has been used to evaluate the state of diﬀerent sub-
populations from the same species in the study by Poulsen et al. [80]. Here hybridisation
of rRNA target probes were used to measure the quantity of rRNA in E. coli BJ4 in situ
in a streptomycin-treated mouse model, by epiﬂuorescense microscopy.
Using this approach two diﬀerent variants of E. coli BJ4 was observed: a slow growing
variant residing in the lumen, and a faster growing variant in the mucus. When these
two sub-populations were grown in vitro, they both fell under the same standard curve
correlating ribosomal content with growth rate. However, when examining them in situ,
it was seen that the slow growing variant of the lumen had a higher ribosomal content
than predicted from the standard curve. This phenomenon was explained by the release
and rapid cessation of growth of the faster growing cells in the mucus. Due to the rapid
cessation of growth the ribosomal dilution was minimal, and with no or little degradation
of ribosomes, a high cellular concentration was maintained. Based on this discovery, a
mean retention time of the population within the mucus was estimated [80]. This study
showed that due to the high stability of ribosomes, the amount present in a cell cannot
always be used as a direct measure of growth and/or activity.
It has also been proposed that due to the diﬀerences between degradation rate of pre-
rRNA’s in fast versus slower growing cells, the presence of the pre-rRNA’s could be used
as a measure of growth rate due to a rapid breakdown of these after cessation of cellular
growth. This was studied by Licht et al. [58] as a follow-up of the previously described
study. Here it was found that, like the with rRNA, the pre-rRNA not always correlated
with growth rate either, due to a mechanism where the pre-rRNA was accumulating in
the slower growing variant of the lumen.
Other studies support the observation that bacteria in the lumen often have static growth,
whereas those living in the mucus layer often have a higher growth rate. Since a signiﬁcant
part of the eﬀects exerted by bacteria in the intestine occurs via adherence to the mucus
layer or host epithelial cells, it is important to be able to characterise these populations
[86], which is what the method developed in our study aims to achieve.

5.2.3
Finding the real ribosomal concentration in cells
Sometimes it can be desirable to ﬁnd the real ribosomal concentration in cells.
In a
study by Zhen et al. [106] P. ﬂuorescens 16S rRNA was used as a standard to calculate
the real ratio of 16S rRNA/rDNA in diﬀerent E. coli samples. Known concentrations of
P. ﬂuorescens 16S rRNA and rDNA was spiked to E. coli samples after the cell lysis step.
After extraction of DNA and RNA and reverse transcription of RNA to cDNA, qPCR was
performed on the samples and the initial concentrations of DNA and cDNA was found.
Based on the percentage of DNA and cDNA loss for P. ﬂuorescens, the initial ratio of 16S
rRNA and rDNA in E. coli could be estimated. Using this approach, the diﬀerential loss
occurring during the lysis step of E. coli was not taken into account. However, they found
their results to be comparable to the levels of ribosomes found by other studies for E. coli
growing in stationary phase E. coli (3 · 104 copies/cell).
In the present study, the goal was not to ﬁnd the real 16S ratio of the bacteria, but rather
to calculate a normalised ratio that could be used to compare samples, and where repro-
ducible results could be obtained. This simpliﬁed the procedure, because large biases often
occur during extraction of DNA and RNA from a cell, for instance due to diﬀerence in
DNA and RNA that gets extracted during cell lysis, during nucleotide extraction steps, or
in the eﬃciency of reverse transcription of RNA to cDNA [47]. P. ﬂuorescens was consid-
ered a good candidate since this bacterium and Pseudomonas in general are uncommon
in the adult intestine (has been shown to be transient in some neonates [6]), and because
it had been tested together with E. coli in the study described above.

Real-time quantitative PCR (qPCR) can be used to
quantify the initial concentration of RNA, DNA or pro-
tein in a sample. It is used for a variety of analysis such
as: gene expression analysis, microRNA analysis, SNP
genotyping, and protein analyses [4].
In qPCR, an amplicon of interest is ampliﬁed in each
cycle in the same manner as in a classic PCR, which
consist of three phases: melting, annealing and am-
pliﬁcation. In a classic PCR the concentration of the
amplicon can be detected after all cycles are completed
(usually between 25-35), whereas in qPCR the concen-
tration of the amplicon is detected after each ampliﬁ-
cation cycle via a ﬂuorescent signal [4].
The qPCR can be divided into two phases: the ex-
ponential and the non-exponential plateau phase. In
the exponential phase, all components of the PCR are
present in abundance such as nucleotides and poly-
merases, whereas in the second phase of qPCR, the
signal reaches a plateau due to depletion of PCR components.
At some point during
the exponential phase, the ﬂuorescent signal in the sample rises above baseline noise to a
detectable value. The cycle where this signal appears is called the threshold cycle value
(Ct), or sometimes quantiﬁcation cycle value (Cq) [2] (see Figure 20).
6.1.1
Determination of the Ct value
The Ct value can be determined in diﬀerent ways depending on the platform and analysis
method used. When the Ct value has been determined, it can then be used to quantify
the initial amount of DNA in a sample (by using a standard), or can be used to compare
initial ratios between samples [2].
In this study the LightCycler 480 instrument and software was used to calculate Ct values.
This platform calculates the Ct value from an absolute quantiﬁcation analysis, where the
log-linear phase of the ﬂuorescent signal curve is used, either by a ﬁt point or a second
derivative maximum method, based on user choice [59] (see Figure 21 on the next page).
The ﬁt point method uses the cycle where the ﬂuorescent signal is ﬁrst detected, whereas
the second derivative maximum estimates the part of the log-linear phase that indicates the
maximum acceleration of the ﬂuorescence and uses the shape of the curve as estimation
of the initial concentration of DNA [59].
In this study we used the second derivative
maximum method to estimate Ct values, since it has shown to provide more accurate
results [60].

6.1.2
Estimating PCR eﬃciency
During each cycle of the exponential phase of the qPCR (see Figure 20 on page 23) all
the template DNA should in theory be ampliﬁed, yielding a copy concentration after each
cycle of N0·2c+1, where N0 is the initial DNA copy number, and c is the cycle of the qPCR.
However, in reality all the DNA templates are not always perfectly copied in each cycle,
depending on the primer binding eﬃciency, the formation of primer dimers, unspeciﬁc
priming, and the presence of inhibitors [59]. Using a ﬁxed value of 2 (perfect amplicon
doubling during each cycle) for each primer and amplicon has shown to be well supported,
especially when the real eﬃciencies does not diﬀer to much. However, not accounting for
diﬀerences in qPCR eﬃciency can lead to either under- or overestimating fold diﬀerences
calculated between samples [84].
One of the most common methods to estimate eﬃciency is to make a standard curve with
serial dilutions. The eﬃciency is then calculated as: Dilution1/slope. An example is given
of the E. coli origin eﬃciency in Figure 22 on the next page. Here, 5-times serial dilutions
was made and the calculated eﬃciency therefore became: 51/2.4145 = 1.95 or to get it in
percentage (5(1/2.4145) −1) · 100% = 95%. In theory the primer eﬃciency should be 100%,
corresponding to a perfect doubling of the template at each cycle. However, the eﬃciency
seen is usually between 90-110% [4]. When using the primer eﬃciency to calculate the
initial DNA concentration from a qPCR run, we will use the numerical values (e.g. 1.95)
instead of the percentage (e.g. 95%). When referring to eﬃciency values elsewhere, the
percentages will be given.
Another way to estimate the eﬃciency is to use a linear regression method. In this anal-
ysis, the log-linear part of the slope of the curve created in the exponential phase of the
PCR is used to ﬁnd primer eﬃciency (see Figure 20 on page 23). The program: LinReg-
PCR can perform this analysis based on raw data from LightCycler480 (linregpcr.nl [84]).
LinRegPCR calculates the eﬃciency for each well in the qPCR, performs a quality check,
and outputs the mean value for each primer pair and amplicon.

The performance of LinRegPCR has been evaluated and compared to the classical serial

6.1.3
Parameters inﬂuencing PCR eﬃciency
The main parameters inﬂuencing PCR eﬃciency has been shown to be the speciﬁc primers
and amplicon used, and to a lesser extent the DNA concentration in the samples, or the
Ct value.
Therefore when optimising qPCR eﬃciency, one must focus on the primers
and amplicons. Undiluted cDNA samples could also inﬂuence the eﬃciency, especially in
higher cycles, due to residues from the reverse transcriptase reactions, and cDNA should
therefore be diluted [49].
6.1.3.1
Primer and amplicon design:
When designing primers there are important
guidelines to follow; all primers must bind speciﬁcally to the target DNA region, and
unspeciﬁc binding should be minimised; primers should have minimum tendency to form
hairpin structures and primer dimers; and the melting temperature (tm) of two primers
in a reaction should be within 5℃. Online tools can be used for primer design such as
primer3.ut.ee [5].
In qPCR, primers are recommended to be 18–24bp in length, and the amplicon is rec-
ommended to lie within 50–150bp, and have a GC content of around 50-60% to ensure
maximum product stability. 100 pg - 1 ug of genomic DNA can be used as input, or cDNA
generated from 1pg-100ng of RNA. Excess template may reduce qPCR eﬃciency. In some
cases it may be important to treat RNA templates with DNaseI to ensure that no genomic
DNA is left [4].

6.1.4
Fluorescent signal detection and melting curve analysis
Several ﬂuorescent PCR chemistries exist and the two most widely used are the TaqMan
probe and the SYBR green. Both use the Taq polymerase and both have advantages and
disadvantages (see Figure 23).
Figure 23: Left: TaqMan probe binds speciﬁcally to amplicon. When the reporter and the quencher
are separated by the DNA polymerase a ﬂuorescent signal appears. Right: SYBR green bind to

The TaqMan probe binds speciﬁcally to the DNA amplicon, and yields a ﬂuorescent signal
when cleaved by DNA polymerase. The TaqMan therefore ensures that the signal detected
in the qPCR is due to speciﬁc ampliﬁcation of the amplicon. Furthermore, it can be used
in multiplex assays were two diﬀerent DNA templates are ampliﬁed in the same well, by
using diﬀerent dyes. Advantages of multiplexing is reduction of reagents and minimisation
of pipetting errors. The disadvantages of the TaqMan probe is its higher cost as well as
the requirement of speciﬁc probes for each primer pair [4].
The SYBR green emits a ﬂuorescent signal when bound to any dsDNA and is therefore
less robust towards unspeciﬁc priming. This could especially be a problem when using
input DNA containing many diﬀerent species such as faecal samples. On the other hand
the SYBR green is cheaper and easier, since speciﬁc probes are not needed. To decrease
the risk of unspeciﬁc binding, the highest possible annealing temperature permitted by
the primers should be used in the PCR.

6.1.5
Reverse transcription of RNA
To quantify the levels of RNA, reverse transcription into cDNA must be performed. Two
methods exist for reverse transcription of RNA into cDNA: one-step and two-step. In the
one-step, the RNA is added directly to the PCR mix and reverse transcribed during the
qPCR. In the two-step, RNA is reverse transcribed before addition to the PCR mix. The
advantage of the one-step is that it contains less steps and therefore is faster, whereas the
advantage of the two-step is that less of the total sample is used as input in the qPCR, and
therefore is preferable when samples are scarce. Furthermore, with the two-step method,
the sample can be diluted before going into the qPCR thereby minimising inhibitors in
the well [2]. In our study we only use the two-step method.
6.2
Next Generation Sequencing with Ion Torrent
Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) is a high throughput technique where millions of
reads can be sequenced in the same run. This type of sequencing has many applications,
including bacterial community analysis from diﬀerent environments such as soil, or the
intestine. In this study we used the Ion Torrent platform to perform high throughput
sequencing of DNA and RNA samples taken down the intestine of two rats.
Figure 25: Schematic representation of the sequencing work ﬂow for Ion Torrent. First a library
is produced with the sequences of interest containing the Ion Torrent sequencing adapters. The
fragments then become clonally ampliﬁed on Ion Sphere particles by emulsion PCR. Ion Sphere
particles containing the ampliﬁed template are then applied to the Ion Torrent chip. The chip is
placed to the Ion PGM (personal genome machine), and the sequencing run is set up. Output ﬁles
containing the sequencing results are provided in standard ﬁle formats. Last, downstream data
analysis carried out [1].
The Ion Torrent workﬂow can be seen in Figure 25.
A library containing adapters is
prepared and clonally ampliﬁed on sphere particles. These particles are then applied all
over the chip in wells. At each cycle the wells are ﬁlled with a ﬂuid containing one of the
four nucleotides. If the nucleotide is incorporated into the growing DNA strand by DNA
polymerase, a proton will be released. This creates a change in pH of the well, which is
sensed and translated into a digital signal. The nucleotides are washed away and a new
nucleotide is added to the chip. The digital signal created with the change in pH is then
used to create the sequencing reads for each sample.
Each sample contains a barcode, and can therefore be separated during down stream
data analysis steps [1]. Down stream analysis of NGS data contains the following steps:
trimming oﬀadapter sequences, trimming of the 5’ and 3’ end of the reads (these are often
of poor quality), removal of bad quality reads, reads with too many unknown base pairs,
and reads that are too short.

Materials and Methods
7.1
Finding the origin of replication
High throughput sequence analysis was used to ﬁnd origin and terminus regions on the
three species: E. coli K-12 MG1655, E. faecalis DSM 2570, and B. adolescentis DSM
20083. GC-skew analysis was also used to estimate the origin region: The number of
G’s on the plus strand subtracted by the number of C’s, divided by the total bp. The
range where the outcome switches from positive to negative corresponds to the origin of
replication [37]. And last, the DoriC database was used to estimate the origin region [36]
which uses comparative genomics technique to estimate the location of the origin region.
Work performed by Damian Pichta [82].
7.2
Primer design for qPCR
The overall idea of the qPCR approach for measuring origin/terminus ratio was: 1) design
speciﬁc primers for the origin and the terminus regions for each bacteria, 2) perform qPCR
with origin and terminus primers on samples taken at diﬀerent growth rates, 3) calculate
the origin/terminus ratio from the qPCR run based on the Ct value obtained with the
primers, and 4) make a standard curve where origin/terminus ratio was correlated to
growth rate. The method is visualised in Figure 2 on page 3.
Several parameters were taken into consideration when designing the primers: 1) origin
and terminus primers must be located in the origin and the terminus regions on the
genome, 2) amplicon size should not exceed 200bp (for maximum eﬃciency in the qPCR),
3) primers must have high speciﬁcity for the target species, 4) primers may not exhibit
unspeciﬁc binding to other places on the target genome, 4) primer eﬃciency should be
as high as possible, 5) for samples were results are to be compared, such as calculation
of ratios, primer eﬃciency and amplicon size should be as similar as possible to minimise
bias. Furthermore, in case the primers would not be speciﬁc enough for detecting the
species of interest in a community sample, probes were also designed in case it became
necessary.

with BLAST [3] and RDP [101] to compare phylogeny results.

7.7.4
Statistical analysis
Diversity analysis was performed with QIIME [19]. Alpha diversity (the species diversity
and relative abundance in each sample) was analysed by rarefaction analysis for the Shan-
non index, Chao-1, and number of observed species in each sample [65]. The rarefaction
analysis calculates the species diversity in the diﬀerent samples, and rarefaction curves
can be used to evaluate whether sequencing has been deep enough, by plotting the species
abundance for a subset of samples, thereby generating a curve that eventually will reach a
plateau. The Shannon index (H′) takes the species abundance in each sample into account
and is positively correlated with species richness. Chao-1 estimates the ”real” number of
species in a sample [21].
38
==PAGE:45==
Beta diversity analysis (comparison of the species present in the diﬀerent samples) was
performed using weighted and un-weighted UniFrac (takes the phylogenetic relationship
between species into account. Weighted: with abundance, un-weighted: without abun-
dance), Bray Curtis (takes abundance into account), and Abund Jaccard (takes abundance
and presence/absence of the species into account) to create Principal Coordinate Analysis
(PCoA) plots. PCoA plots are made creating an n-1 dimensional space of the distances
between all samples, where n=number of samples [8]. PCoA were visualised in a three di-
mensional plot using the three dimensions explaining the highest percentage of the variety.
For the analysis, the samples were normalised to use 40.000 reads per sample (number of
reads for the samples with fewest reads).
Two tailed T-tests assuming unequal size and variance between groups was used to com-
pare the species diversity between samples, and the statistical signiﬁcance for Shannon
index values between samples, to give a measure of the diﬀerence in the overall number of
observed species present from one group of samples to another.
Spearman Correlation Coeﬃcients were used for all correlation analyses of datasets as-
sumed to be non-normally distributed [93], and was calculated using excel.

8.2
Testing primer speciﬁcity
The developed origin and terminus primers were tested for unspeciﬁc binding to their
respective genomes with PCR following gel electrophoresis. Only single bands appeared
on the gel, indicating a single target template. The 16S rRNA gene primers for E. coli,
E. faecalis, and B. adolescentis had previously been tested in [14]. E. coli origin and
terminus primers were further tested on faecal samples with varying E. coli concentra-
tion and was found to be able to detect concentrations down to 0.003% at an annealing
temperature of 68℃. However, since diﬀerent polymerases were used for qPCR and PCR,
41
==PAGE:48==
the annealing temperature could not be directly transferred but was tested again in the
qPCR, where it was shown that all primers were capable of binding at least up to 58℃.
E. coli and E. faecalis origin and terminus primers were tested on 36 faecal samples from
rats that had been treated with antibiotics/control rats, in order to test for speciﬁcity
(B. adolescentis was not present in the samples, and therefore not tested). For each sample
the origin/terminus ratio was calculated for E. coli and E. faecalis. E. faecalis showed
reasonable ratios (between 0.8-1.5), whereas E. coli primers gave ratios between 0.5-7000,
indicating unspeciﬁc binding to species other than E. coli. Therefore, new primers were
developed for E. coli (the ones listed in Section 7.2.1 on page 28), and ratios were now
seen to range between 0.02-6, again indicating unspeciﬁc binding. It was decided not to
try and optimise further on E. coli primers until it was veriﬁed that the origin/terminus
ratios calculated from pure cultures gave reasonable results.
An in silico analysis of the 16S P. ﬂuorescens primers were performed with BLAST where
speciﬁcity was seen for other Pseudomonas species besides P. ﬂuorescens. However, since
Pseudomonas species in general are uncommon within the intestinal environment [6], it
was decided to continue using the P. ﬂuorescens primers. It was also seen that primers had
potential for non-speciﬁc binding to bacteria from other genus’. Therefore, P. ﬂuorescens
primers were tested on 6 of the faecal samples (mentioned above) with PCR followed by
gel electrophoresis. This revealed a band in 5 out of 6 samples. Since no Pseudomonas
species were detected in the samples from high throughput sequencing analysis (data from
[96]), it was assumed that primers bound non-speciﬁc to non-Pseudomonas species. It
was decided to continue using the developed primer pair for monitoring 16S ratios in pure
cultures, and when this method had been veriﬁed, perform further primer optimisation to
make P. ﬂuorescens genus speciﬁc. Speciﬁc suggestions to how this could be performed
will be described in Future Work in Section 10.1 on page 63.
Since probes had also been developed for the origin and terminus regions of E. coli,
E. faecalis, B. adolescentis, and 16S rRNA regions of P. ﬂuorescens, it could be suggested
to use these to increase speciﬁcity.

Expected theoretical values were calculated for the three bacteria using the following logic:
assuming that it takes 40 minutes to replicate the genome of E. coli, the mean ratio for a
culture growing at a speed of 1 generation/hour would be: 1.66 (40min/60min · 100% =
66% having a ratio of 2, whereas the rest would have a ratio of 1, giving: 0.66·2+0.34·1 =
1.66).
Assuming that the replication fork proceeds with approximately the same speed in all
organisms ([17]), bacteria with smaller genomes would be expected to have lower ratios

8.7.5
Relative 16S rRNA/rDNA ratios
To evaluate the relative activity of the species present in the diﬀerent samples, 16S
rRNA/rDNA ratios were found by dividing the relative RNA abundance to the relative
DNA abundances of individual species (see Figure 40 on the next page).
In rat A it was observed that the amount of species with ratios > 1 were higher in the
upper than in the lower gut (p = 0.014). This observation was not signiﬁcant for rat
B (p = 0.5). The highest relative RNA/DNA ratios were observed for the two species:
Peptostreptococcaceae(f) (154) and Uncultured Clostridium(g)(205) in the jejunum of rat
A. It was therefore examined whether these higher ratios were an indicator of an observed
higher abundance further down the intestine. The species from Peptostreptococcaceae(f)
had a relative abundance of 38% in the ileum of rat A, compared to 1% in ileum of rat B.
However, further down the intestine the abundance of the species decreased in rat A and
no signiﬁcant diﬀerence was observed between rat A and rat B (p = 0.32).
This observation could indicate that the species from Peptostreptococcaceae(f) resides in
the ileum of rat A, perhaps by adhering to the mucus layer (high abundance of this species
in the mucus sample), where it then slows down its growth rate. In order to perform a
proper analysis of such a phenomenon a more comprehensive method where the bacterial
count is also taken into consideration, would be needed.
The two very abundant Bacilli species in rat A from: Lactobacillus(g) and Turibacter(g)
had approximately the same RNA/DNA ratios for both rats in all compartments (ratio:
0.9-2.9), and were not signiﬁcantly more abundant in the lower gut of rat A than rat B
(Lactobacillus:(p = 0.83) and Turibacter(p = 0.37)). Therefore it was hypothesised that
the reason for the higher relative abundance in rat A, was not caused by a higher growth
rate of these species, but rather that the environment in the upper gut of rat A facilitated
less competition from other species. For instance if the upper gut of rat A was more acidic
and had a higher oxygen concentration, since the two Bacilli are facultative anaerobes
and also have a high tolerance for low pH.
This sequencing study was an illustration of how diﬀerent the intestinal community can
look between individuals, both in terms of diversity, relative abundance and activity, most
likely driven by the diﬀerences in the physiochemical environmental between individuals.
It stresses the importance of examining the bacterial proﬁle throughout the intestinal
tract, since the gut microbiota seen in faeces only was shown to be representative of the
lower gut but not the upper gut.
Last, interesting hypothesis can be made from relative abundance analysis regarding the
overall gut community. However, when the goal is to understand how a single species is
growing, it would be better to use a more focussed tool such as the one developed in this
study. This way a more robust measurement of bacterial growth and physiology can be ob-
tained due to applying both the origin/terminus and the 16S ratio. Furthermore it would
be possible to ﬁnd the real abundance measure by normalisation to P. ﬂuorescens, thereby
making comparisons between compartments more true. For instance it would be interest-
ing to ﬁnd out if the species from Peptostreptococcaceae(f) was present in high numbers,
or if the reason for the higher relative abundance was due a generally low bacterial load
in the upper gut regions of rat A.

9
Discussion
In this section the key results from this study will be discussed and strengths and draw-
backs of the two proposed methods to estimate growth and physiology will be evaluated.
This will be followed by a general discussion of the impact of this study on the basis of
the high throughput sequencing result of the bacterial communities from two rats.
9.1
Comparing the two methods - strengths and drawbacks
Since the origin/terminus and the normalised 16S rRNA/rDNA ratio measures were de-
signed to complement each other, this section will be dedicated to comparing them. Ini-
tially it was expected that the origin/terminus ratio would provide a snapshot of the
cellular growth rate, and to be less sensitive towards slow growing cells. The 16S ratio was
expected to be a more sensitive measure at low growth rates, as well as provide a more
complex illustration of cellular growth and physiology.
To examine the diﬀerences between the two methods, the growth curve from the single
media experiment of E. coli (see Figure 29 on page 44, top) was divided into ﬁve growth
phases: exponential, transition phase, early stationary phase, late stationary phase, and
O/N culture. The average ratios in these phases were plotted in a histogram. For com-
parison the ratios from the multiple media experiment for E. coli was plotted as well (see
Figure 41).
Figure 41: Histogram showing the normalised 16S and origin/terminus ratios at diﬀerent growth
rates. Left: the growth curve of E. coli was divided into ﬁve phases and the average ratios and
growth rates were calculated based on OD600nm. Right: average of the two technical replicates
from the multiple media experiment.
60
==PAGE:67==
Figure 41 on page 60 illustrated visually that the origin/terminus ratio seemed to respond
faster to the change in growth rate and correlated quite well in both types of experi-
ments (SCC multiple media = 0.86 (p < 0.001), SCC single media = 0.60 (p = 0.03)).
The normalised 16S ratio correlated well in the single media experiment (SCC = 0.96
(p < 0.01)). However, the ribosomal content tended to decrease more gradually than the
origin/terminus ratio when cells were reaching stationary phase. In the multiple media
experiment correlation between 16S ratio and growth rate was less evident (SCC = 0.32
(p = 0.184)), and an especially large diﬀerence was observed between the LB samples and
the samples from other media.
This diﬀerence seen between the single and multiple media experiments, could be due to
the long storing of the multiple media samples (> 1 year at -20℃). This could lead to
diﬀerences in cell lysis potential and/or breakdown of DNA and RNA (e.g. by activity
of non-inactivated DNAses), which could explain the generally higher RNA/DNA ratios.
Furthermore, these mechanisms could be inﬂuenced by media type which could explain
the low correlation between growth rate and 16S ratio in the multiple media experiment.
A separate experiment with E. coli in exponential and O/N growth in LB media showed
16S ratios comparable to the single media experiment: exponential mean from three tech-
nical replicates: 4.3. O/N mean from ﬁve technical replicates: 1.4.
9.1.1
Pitfalls and uses - origin-terminus ratio
When evaluating the utility of the origin/terminus ratio as a measure of growth it was seen
that for E. coli and B. adolescentis, all experiments showed fairly good correlations (SCC
between 0.60-0.86 (p < 0.05)) and SCC 0.78-0.98(p < 0.05)) respectively. However, more
data should be generated for B. adolescentis to back up this observation. All E. faecalis
experiments on the other hand showed poor correlations. An explanation could be that the
origin and terminus primers didn’t bind to the the origin and terminus regions. However,
the results obtained from the sequencing analysis was not convincing either. The reason
for this was unclear since other studies successfully have correlated origin/terminus ratio
of E. faecalis to its growth rate [54].
It was observed that care must be taken when interpreting a single origin/terminus ra-
tio without context, due to several factors such as: ratios from qPCR generally being
lower than the ratios found with sequence analysis, ratios deviated from the theoretical
origin/terminus ratios, and that ratios in some cases got below 1. Therefore, ratios should
only be compared when samples are treated alike, is analysed with the same method, and
when using the same primer pair.
9.1.2
Pitfalls and uses - 16S ratio
Unlike the origin/terminus ratio, the 16S ratio was seen to decrease more slowly when cells
were entering stationary phase, indicating that the ribosomal content not only depended
on the immediate growth rate but also where a reﬂection of the previous growth rate. This
observation makes sense since ribosomal concentrations is a sum of both the synthesis rate
of the rRNA and r-protein as well as the degradation rate, and because ribosomes are fairly
stable molecules. Additionally, we know from the literature that ribosomal concentration
can be a result of many factors such as life strategy of the cell, or environmental conditions
[16].
61
==PAGE:68==
Therefore, when using the 16S ratio as a measure of cellular growth and physiology, it
is important to keep this in mind as well as primarily use the ratio to compare samples
taken from the same type of experiment and from the same organism.
9.1.3
Pitfalls and uses - the combined method
The main strengths of these methods are that, when used together they increase the
sensitivity especially of slow growing cells as well as provides a combined illustration
of both the growth and the physiology of a species.
Because they provide a targeted
analysis they are especially useful when examining a single species in diﬀerent settings.
As an example it could reveal if a bacteria that is now growing slowly (by examining the
origin/terminus ratio), has had high growth rate in the near past (by examining the 16S
ratio).
Some of the main disadvantages are, that in order to measure the origin/terminus and 16S
ratio in a complex environment, primer pairs have to be optimised for exact speciﬁcity
to the species of interest. Furthermore, it will be diﬃcult to separate individual strains
of the same species, since the genomes between strains often are very similar. This could
be overcome by inserting a unique gene in the origin and terminus regions of the bacteria
of interest. However, it would be diﬃcult to insert a gene within the 16S rRNA regions,
due to the risk of disrupting ribosomal function. Therefore, the method is most suitable
when the goal is to measure the growth of strain where not many other strains within this
species are present in the gut.
9.2
Evaluation of the experimental set-up
In this study the bacteria were grown as batch cultures from exponential to stationary
phase, which does not completely reﬂect their natural growth within the intestinal en-
vironment. The small intestine, the caecum and the upper part of the colon (proximal
colon) resembles to some extend a continuous ﬂow culture with variable transit times (ap-
proximately 4-6 h in the small intestine up to 60-70 h in the colon [61]). The last part of
the colon on the other hand is closer to resembling a batch or a fed-batch culture [62].
Additionally, the environment in the intestine diﬀers from our batch experiments e.g. by
being more anaerobic and having a higher concentration of bile and other molecules such
as those produced by the complex bacterial community. I order to perform more accurate
experiments, in vitro studies in an artiﬁcial intestinal system or in vivo animal studies
should be performed.
9.3
Sequence analysis evaluation
Several important points were obtained from the sequence analysis; 1) the diﬀerence in
abundance, relative activity, and diversity of the gut microbiota of diﬀerent animals (rat
A and B) was relatively high. Even for individuals living very close together (e.g. shares
a cage); 2) this diﬀerence was only signiﬁcant in the upper gut of the rats (stomach,
duodenum, jejunum, and ileum) p < 0.001, and not in the lower gut (caecum, colon,
faeces) p = 0.05 −0.66; 3) in both individuals, the microbiota of the upper gut diﬀered
signiﬁcantly from the lower gut, both in relation to abundance and diversity measures
(p < 0.01);
62
==PAGE:69==
4) the bacterial community in faeces, caecum, and colon was quite similar. Therefore,
faecal samples might be representative for the whole lower gut region but a less accurate
illustration of the bacterial community proﬁle in the small intestine, which is in correspon-
dence to the ﬁndings in other studies[67]; ﬁnally 5) sequencing and relative abundance
analysis gives a good overview of the bacterial proﬁle throughout the intestinal tract.
However, when the goal is to analyse a single species, the method developed in this study
is better suited because it provides a more robust targeted analysis.
By analysing the relative 16S ratio, it was seen that the species with high activity was not
necessarily those that were highly abundant, and vice versa. Information about activity
could be useful when evaluating for instance the eﬀect of the growth of a probiotic on
a prebiotics substrate or the eﬀect of bacterial growth after an antibiotic treatment. It
would especially be interesting to be able to examine the activity of bacteria residing in
the mucus versus the lumen, since mucus associated bacteria resides for a longer period
of time, thereby interacting to a greater extend with host cells [107], compared to the
bacteria in the lumen that passes by more quickly [61]. In this study, samples were taken
from the mucus layer of both the small and large intestine. However, only the RNA sample
of the small intestine of rat A contained enough nucleotides for sequencing.

Future work
In this section experiments that should be performed before the method is mature for
usage in situ will be presented. Suggestions to how standard curves could be generated
in a way that validates the method as a universal tool for diﬀerent types of bacteria will
be given. Last, it will be proposed how experimental set-ups could look in order to study
the growth of intestinal bacteria in situ.
10.1
Primer speciﬁcity
Optimisation should be performed of the E. coli origin and terminus primers to make
them organism speciﬁc in order to use them for detection in community samples. To do
this primers should be ﬁrst tested in silico with BLAST, both against the entire database
and against speciﬁcity for binding to other regions on the E. coli genome. This should
be followed by PCR on a variety on pure cultures of E. coli, and a qPCR of a variety of
faecal samples where the origin/terminus ratios should be veriﬁed to lie within reasonable
ranges.
P. ﬂuorescens primers should be optimised to become speciﬁc for Pseudomonas in com-
munity samples, since they were found to bind unspeciﬁc to something not being Pseu-
domonas (see Section 8.2 on page 41). The ﬁrst step could be to modify the Pseudomonas
speciﬁc primers developed by Widmer et al. [102]. This primer pair was tested on pure
Pseudomonas samples, pure non-Pseudomonas samples and soil samples. Primers showed
to bind to all Pseudomonas samples and none of the non-Pseudomonas samples. One
suggestion could be to use the reverse primer from Widmer et al. to test together with
the forward primer from our study, since an in silico analysis with BLAST revealed only
a small overlap between the species that could bind non-speciﬁc to these two primers. To
test for unspeciﬁc binding, PCR could be performed on faecal samples from rats followed
by gel electrophoresis.

The developed primers for E. faecalis showed to be species speciﬁc in community samples.
However, they were not able to detect the origin/terminus ratio in pure cultures and should
therefore be optimised.
10.2
Standard curves
A new multiple media experiment could be performed for the three bacteria, since current
samples were suspected to be partly destroyed during storage. Samples should be taken
both in the experimental phase of growth as well as O/N. Two samples should be taken
at each sample point: 1 in RNAprotect for the 16S ratio, and 1 heat inactivated sample
for the origin/terminus analysis.
Experiments could be performed that are closer to resembling the in vivo environment.
For instance a continuous growth experiment for E. coli where the cell grows at a ﬁxed
growth rate and samples are taken periodically. Mainly to examine how the 16S ratio
changes and when it becomes stable. Such an experiment could be performed for e.g.
multiple media types or in aerobic/anaerobic growth to monitor the diﬀerence in 16S ratio
in the cell at diﬀerent growth rates.
New single media experiment with B. adolescentis.
Having standard curves for three
diﬀerent bacteria with distinct properties, supports the usage of the method as a universal
tool.
10.3
Validation studies in situ
A new sampling experiment throughout the intestine of rats could be performed where
more mucus samples should be included, since it was experienced that nucleotide extrac-
tion from mucus was challenging. Extra caecum samples should also be taken, in order
initial tests by PCR with P. ﬂuorescens 16S primers to validate that no unspeciﬁc priming
occurs, as well as validate the presence of the bacteria of interest. After validation the
samples should be spiked with P. ﬂuorescens followed by RNA and DNA extraction and
qPCR analysis. Such an experiment could provide information regarding bacterial growth,
physiology and abundance of the species of interest in the diﬀerent compartments, as well
as monitor the diﬀerence between mucus and lumen.
To validate the usage of the method as a tool for being able to measure the eﬀect on
antibiotic treatment on bacterial growth, qPCR with E. coli and E. faecalis origin and
terminus primers on antibiotic treated and control rats from the study by Monica Tulstrup
[96] could be interesting. It was seen from that study, that the relative abundance of E. coli
increased during the ﬁrst days of antibiotic treatment and it would therefore be interesting
to test whether this increase could be monitored by a change in origin/terminus ratio.
To monitor the eﬀect on the 16S ratio as well, we could ask Monica Tulstrup [96] for some
of the non-puriﬁed faecal samples still left from the study of the antibiotic treated rats.
These should be spiked with P. ﬂuorescens prior to nucleotide extraction and qPCR 16S
ratio analysis could reveal if the normalised 16S ratio responds to changes in growth of
E. coli and E. faecalis during antibiotic treatment.
It would be interesting to set up an in vivo experiment where a probiotic strain of interest
is delivered to a mouse/rat model animal. Perhaps with co-delivery of a prebiotic substrate
versus a control substrate. Faecal samples could taken e.g. at day 1, 3, and 5. When the

animals were sacriﬁced samples should be obtained from the various sites throughout the
intestinal tract (including mucus), and 16S and origin/terminus ratios should be found
based on qPCR. Such an experiment would yield knowledge regarding the growth of the
bacteria of interest both in terms of the growth in the diﬀerent locations of the intestinal
tract, as well as the bacterial count, and it would provide information about the usefulness
of the prebiotic substrate to support the growth of the speciﬁc bacteria.
10.4
Validation studies in vitro
Alternatively the method could be tested in vitro, e.g by growing B. adolescentis with one
or more prebiotic substrates to see which substrate best promote growth. Then the best
or two of the best substrates could be used in the in vivo study described above.

Conclusion
In this study the origin/terminus and the normalised 16S rRNA/rDNA ratios were eval-
uated as a measure of growth and physiology for three diﬀerent bacteria with relevance
to the human gastrointestinal tract: E. coli K-12 MG1655, E. faecalis DSM 2570, and
B. adolescentis DSM 20083. It was seen that the origin/terminus ratio was a good mea-
sure of immediate growth rate, whereas the 16S ratio was seen to be dependent on both
the immediate and the previous growth rate. From deep sequencing of the bacterial com-
munity throughout the intestinal tract of two rats, it was seen how diﬀerent the bacterial
proﬁle could look with regards to species diversity, relative abundance and activity, both
between gut regions and between individuals.
Metagenomic sequencing studies provide a good illustration of the bacterial diversity of
the gut microbiota. However, in order to test the causal relationship between the gut
microbiota and human health, experiments where bacteria grow within a model organism
is necessary to evaluate their eﬀect. To perform such an experiment, the method developed
in this study has demonstrated it’s potential as an easy, cheap, and robust platform for
testing a speciﬁc bacteria of interest, once the set-up for this bacteria has been developed.
