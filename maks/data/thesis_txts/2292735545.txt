Abstract
Our modern world is facing a global challenge; the demand for finite and scarce resources keeps growing. 
This challenge creates a need to develop new innovative materials, which optimize the use of resources. 
Really ApS is developing a composite material (CRC), where the key component is discarded textiles. 
This composite seems intuitively sustainable as it is transform waste to a valuable material, but does it 
actually have a better environmental performance than existing materials? 
This study quantifies the environmental impact of a shelf with CRC compared to a Montana shelf with 
medium density fiberboard (MDF) and natural fiber reinforced composites (NRC). Furthermore, it 
also investigates whether there is a market for a Montana shelf produced with green materials. 
Life cycle assessment with an attributional approach was applied to quantify the environmental perfor­
mance shelf with the involved materials. All life cycle stages are included to enable a holistic view of the 
environmental impacts. 
The results obtained from the ReCiPe methodology indicated that MDF is the best performing ma­
terial, whereas the CRC is the worst performing material, and the rest of the material performs in the 
range between MDF and CRC. However, the primary data provided from Really ApS for the composite 
manufacturing stage is from a small scale test production, which might contribute to the high environ­
mental impact of this material.  To stimulate a large scale production, it was investigated how a reduc­
tion of the energy consumption for the most energy intensive production process (airlaid) influence the 
results. With 80% reduction of energy consumption reduction CRC has comparable results wiht MDF. 
It has not been possible to evaluate if a 80% energy reduction is  realistic when going from a small scale 
production to a large scale production, so it difficult to evaluate the enviromental impact of  CRC for 
with a large scale production. 
Market assessment indicates that there is a potential market for Montana shelf with green material. 
Environmental aspects of a shelf is valued by potential end-users, however it is the not the most critical 
factor for the potential end-users - price and design have a significant influence. The potential market 
for Montana Raw shelf with green material depend highly on price and design.

1	INTRODUCTION
1.1	Introduction
Finding new ways to optimize the use of resources serve a great focus in our modern age, as the growing 
demand of finite and scarce resources puts increasing pressure on the fragile environment. The Earth 
can absorb a certain degree of impact without lasting damage, however, current human activities surpass 
this threshold and contribute to the decrease of the quality of the world we live in and threaten future 
generations’ needs (European Commission 2015). This problem is shaping our modern world and has 
created a paradigm of sustainable thinking in product development.
Materials play an important role in resource efficiency as they derived from natural resources. A growing 
demand of materials increases the depletion rate of natural resources. Therefore, new materials that uses 
the natural resources more efficiently must be developed; a point of view shared, adopted and promoted 
by many government and intergovernmental institutions. For example, the Danish government advo- 
cates for waste recycling, regarding waste as valuable resources that could be used as new material for 
another product (Danish Ministry of Environment 2013). Moreover, there is a growing field of using 
renewable resources to make new alternative material such as natural fiber reinforced composite or 
transforming waste to new material.
Many companies consider and focus on sustainability when developing new products or business strat- 
egies. To support this, new methods are being developed to guide green product development and 
thereby develop products with lower environmental impact. There are many different tools and indi­
cator to assess and benchmark environmental impacts of different product and systems e.g. Life Cycle 
Assessment (LCA). Knowledge gained from applying these tools and indicators can be used to integrate 
environmental considerations in different types of decisions made by business, policymakers and in­
dividuals (Finnveden et al. 2009). Consumers has an increasing interest to know how the product is 
made, where it is sourced from and what the environmental impact of the product is throughout its 
entire life cycle. This creates incentives to assess the environmental performance of a product. Knowl- 
edge about the environmental performance of a product might increases the speed to market as Bovea 
& Vidal (2004) states “Knowledge regarding the environmental impact of the materials typically used in a 
specific in- dustrial sector is a key factor to enable companies to improve their products and production pro­
cesses from an environmental point of view and thus accelerate their entrance into the emerging market for 
green products”.

2.1	Literature Review 
There are a limited numbers of studies which have focused on the application of LCA to furniture. The 
LCA of furniture can be categorized in residential and commercial (office) furniture. This literature 
review will only look into residential furniture as it is within the scope of this study.
There are no studies applying LCA to assess furniture with NRC or with other materials of green char­
acter in the scientific literature, which is a motivation for conducting this study.
The majority of the LCA studies of residential furniture are wood based furniture. Mirabella et al. 
(2014) investigated the environmental performance of a wooden school desk which has been subject 
for eco-design and has integrate environmental considerations in the design phase. The results of the 
study shows to which extent eco-design has improved the environmental performance of the product. A 
wardrobe built from Medium density board was assessed using LCA by Iritani et al. ( 2014) to establish 
sustainability strategies to decrease the environmental impacts of the product. González- García et al. 
(2011)  evaluated several wood based product among them was furniture as an environmental criteri­
on for eco-design. However only global warming potential was calculated without considering other 
environmental iompacts/problems. González-García et al. (2012) did an hotspot analisys for a set of 
wooden childhood furni- ture applying LCA. The hotposot analysis was used to define the best design 
criteria for eco-design. Environmental impact of materials and process commonly used in the wood 
based furniture industry was analyzed by Bovea & Vidal (2004) to establish a  material selection criteria 
to reducethe environmental impact.
To sum up, the vast majority of the studies includes hotspot analysis, many of them are cradle to gate 
disregarding usage and end of life (Mirabella et al. 2014; Iritani et al. 2014; González-García et al. 2011; 
Bovea & Vidal 2004). Some studies presented comparative analysis of different products to evaluate 
their environmental performance. Many suggestions for improvement were based on eco-design. .
As none of the LCAs reviewed studies analyzed NRC and cotton based textile fiber reinforced compos­
ite,a literature review of LCA of bio- composite and cotton based textile fiber reinforced composite will 
be presented in the following paragraph.

LCA of recycled textile waste
Woolridge et al. (2006) conducted a LCA of reused and recycling textile waste compared to manufac- 
turing of virgin material. The study was a streamlined LCA with focus on energy footprint. The results 
showed that the reused cotton only use 2,7% of the energy required to manufacture virgin material.
Zamani et al. (2014) performed a LCA of textile recycling using different recycling technologies and 
com- pared them to incineration with energy recovery. The results indicated that environmental burden 
is de- creased when recycling textile instead of incineration considering carbon footprint.
The two studies do not depict the full picture as they each only consider one aspects of environmental 
impact (carbon footprint). However, the results showed that recycling is more environmentally benefi­
cial when considering this specific environmental problem.
LCA of textile based bio-composite with recycled plastic
Few LCA studies have been carried out of recycled textile fiber based composite.
Vidal et al. (2008) conducted a LCA study of bio-composite obtained from recycled polypropylene (PP) 
and high density polyethylene (HDPE) combined with risk husk and recycled cotton liners. The results 
showed that environmental impact is reduced significantly compared to same bio-composite made with 
virgin PP and HPDE.
Rajendran et al. (2012) performed a LCA study of bio composite with flax+ recycled plastic and glass 
fiber + recycled glass compared to same bio- composite with virgin plastic. The materials are applied to 
non–automobile structural application and to automobile application. In the case of non-automobile 
applications the bio-composite with recycled plastic has a clear advantage over bio-composite with vir­
gin plastic. For the case of automobile applications bio-composite with virgin plastic is a much better 
option than bio-composite with recycled plastic. 
Both studies, demonstrate a reduction of the environmental burden when using recycled plastic in 
composite materials.

2.2.1	Production Process of CRC 
The current production of CRC is a small scale test production, it is manufactured by an airlaid process, 
see Figure 2 for an illustration of an airlaid process. The collected prewashed discarded textile from 
industrial laundries is grinding in to small fibers, the textile fibers are fed to the airlaid process together 
with bico- fiber. The textile fiber and bico-fiber are mixed by air. The heat from the airlaid process acti­
vates the outer sheath of bi-co fiber and binds the cotton fiber together with bi-co fiber superficially. In 
this state the panel has a low density and is porous. Afterwards the panel is pressed with a temperature 
which activates the core of bi-co fiber and binds the polymer and cotton stronger together. The output 
of this process is the final CRC, which have  a smooth plan surface. See Figure 3 for an overview of the 
production processes.

2.2.2	Circular economy  and closed loop concept 
Really ApS has incorporated the concept of circular economy in their business. Circular economy aims 
to keep products, components and materials at their highest value and utility, thus waste is regarded as 
valuable resources and able to be a part of production again or of input to a new product network. To 
enable reuse and recycling of waste, products must be designed for disassembly and repurposing(Ellen 
Macarthur  Foundation  n.d.).
Really ApS is working with a closed loop system, where they have a ‘take back’ system for furniture 
with their composite. A ‘take bake’ system is a system, where Really ApS recollect the disposed shelves, 
the shelves can be processed and use as feed material tfor new CRC. Figure 4 illustrates the closed loop 
con- cept of Really ApS. As mentioned, Really ApS is selling their composite in a business to business 
market, which might makes it difficult to control the treatment of the composite. They devise guide­
lines of how to treat the composite, so it is able to be a part of the production chain again. From there, 
it is up to the collaborating companies whether to follow those guidelines. However, Really’s collaborat­
ing companies are all interested in incorporating the sustainability aspect in their business in one way 
or another, so the likelihood of the collaborating companies following the guideline is high, thus the 
disposed products can be processed again to new  composites.

2.4	NRC 
There is a growing interest for NRC from industry, which is caused by an increasing environmental 
concern and regulations driving industries to search for more sustainable materials (Srebrenkos- ka et 
al. 2008). 
Reinforcement in NRC are typically plant fibers such as jute, hemp, ramie, kenaf, flax etc. (Fowler et al. 
2006). Matrices may be polymers which can be  fossil derived or biological de- rived. The main function 
of the matrices is to provide solidness to the composite.
At the present time matrices of fossil based polymer are the most common applied, there are a growing 
interest in substituting it with biological derived polymer (biopolymer) made of renewable resources 
such as starch, sugar corn etc. The main benefit of biopolymer is that they are able to degrade complete­
ly in soil or by a composting process, they do not release any toxic or noxious substances  and they do 
not originate from renewable resources (Srebrenkoska et al. 2008; Truss 2011).
The automobile industry has been the main driver for the market of bio-composite, currently several 
automobile parts are manufactured using natural fiber reinforced bio-composite (NRC) to produce 
lightweight non-structural component to reduce fuel consumption as substitute to glass fiber and car­
bon fiber (Faruk et al. 2014), see Figure 7. The use of NRC for technical application has been subject 
for extensive research in Europe (Brouwer 2000), which shows that there is a great interest and potential 
for the NCR. According to Faruk et al.( 2012) NCR are bound to be used in more and more applica­
tions, especially in Europe, caused by a rising pressure from legislation. Besides the automobile industry 
various market segments are continuously seeking for innovation methods to exploit bio-composites 
(Robertson 2013).

2.5.2	Virgin polymer and recycled polymer 
As mentioned Really Aps works with  a closed loop approach, where shelves  are recycled and used as 
new material to produce new CRC, hence a polymer which is able to be recycled should be chosen. 
Op- posed to thermoset, thermoplastic polymer is able to be reworked and reprocessed, and thereby 
is recyclable (Callister 2007), hence thermoplastic polymer should be used over thermoset. Really ApS 
uses bico-fiber which consist of PP and PE (both thermoplastic plastics). The two types of plastic are 
commonly used in many applications and NRC. Van de Velde & Kiekens (2001) investigated which 
thermoplastic polymer is the most suitable for flax fiber reinforced polymer which is also the most en­
vironmental friendly polymer. The results showed that PP is the best performing polymer in a flax fiber 
reinforced bio-composite, however the results also showed that PE is suitable. 
Plastic is used to provide solidness for the composite, however Young’s modulus of plastic has influence 
of  the overall Young’s modulus of the composite. The Young’s  modulus of  bio-composite are calculated 
according to the following equation. 
ECompo=EFiber*VFiber*FFiber+EPlastic*VPlastic*FPlatic 
Where EComp is the Young’s modulus  of biocomposite, E descriebes the Young’s modulus, V is the 
volumen of material and F is the fraction of the material. Plastic has a general much lower Young’s 
modulus  than natural fiber, Young’s modulus for PE and PP is approximately 0.8 Gpa and 1.5-2 GPa 
respectively, which is significantly lower than flax (Engineering Tool Box n.d.). Plastic’s contribution to 
the overall will in  general be of minor degree.  It is estimated that with both types of plastic the final 
Young’s modulus of NRC is comparable with CRC (see Table 5). As PE and PP is recyclable and studies 
have shown very promising results for the technical performance of recycled PE and PP (Boldizar et al. 
2000; Srebrenkoska et al. 2009; Peijs 2003) both PE and PP are selected as they both suitable for flax

2.5.3	Bio-polymer 
The main benefit of biopolymer is that they are able to degrade completely in soil or by a composting 
process, they do not release any toxic or noxious substances  and they do not originate from renewable 
resources (Srebrenkoska et al. 2008; Truss 2011). They can either be generated from biological systems 
(i.e plants and microorganism) chemically synthesized from biological materials ( e.g starch, sugar, nat­
ural fats)(Flieger 2003). There is a growing interest in bio-polymer as they are derived from renewable 
resources and serve as an alternative to traditional crude-oil based polymer. There exists a wide range of 
bio-polymer, however the majority of the bio-polymer are still in the development stage and not com­
mercial available(Fowler et al. 2006). One of the main challenges with biopolymer is the price, however, 
the price of bio-polymer will decrease as utilization  of bio-polymer will increase due to economic of 
scale (La Mantia & Morreale 2011). 
PLA is a promising bio-polymer: Van de Velde & Kiekens (2002) investigated the mechanical proper­
ties of a flax based bio-composite with different type of bio-polymers. The study showed that PLA is 
a suitable biopolymer in a flax based bio-composite. Furthermore, PLA is also commercially available 
and produced in large scale (Van de Velde & Kiekens 2002; Fowler et al. 2006). However, the price

bio-polymer is not currently relevant and therefore disregarded in this study..

3.1	A Brief Introduction to LCA Theory 
There are many methods to evaluate the environmental impact of different systems, such as life cycle 
assessment (LCA), Strategic Environmental Assessment (SEA), Material Flow Analysis (MFA) etc. In 
this study LCA  is the applied tool to assess the environmental impacts as it is broadly applied by aca­
demia and industry and it is a standardized method defined in ISO 14044:2006 and ISO 14040:2006 
(Finnveden et al. 2009). In addition, LCA is the most recognized and accepted assessment method at 
international level (Bovea & Vidal 2004). 
LCA is a method to assess potential environmental effects considering all life-cycles of a product, i.e. 
raw materials extraction, manufacturing, usage and end of life in a cradle to grave approach.  LCA is an 
extensive assessment,  it systematically quantifies all the emissions including all aspects of the natural en­
vironment, human health and resources (ISO 2006b; JRC European Commission 2011).The particular 
attributes of LCA is the focus on a product’s life cycle perspective, which provides a holistic approach 
that brings the environmental effects into one consistent framework (Guinée 2001). This framework 
contribute to avoid burden-shifting, which resolves one  environmental problem while creating others 
(Finnveden et al. 2009; Guinée 2001). This makes LCA an essential and powerful decision support tool, 
supplementing other tools in making the usage and production of a product more sustainable (JRC 
European Commission 2011). There are two different types of LCA approaches – a consequential and 
an attributional LCA. A consequential approach seeks to describe the effect of changes within the life 
cycles of a product while an attributional approach seeks to describe the environmental properties of a 
life cycle of a product. Thereby the choice of LCA approach depends on the decision context.  
A LCA study is applicable when (Guinée 2001)
• Analyzing the origin of problems related to a given product
• Comparing between different products
• Comparing alternatives of a given product 
• Designing a new product
The framework of LCA consists of four phases; Goal and Scope definition, Life Cycle Inventory Anal­
==PAGE:30==
20
 Chapter 3 THEORY AND METHODOLOGY
ysis (LCI), Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA), and interpretation, see Figure 6 (JRC European 
Commission 2011). 
The phase of Goal definition and scope definition contain the reasons for conducting the LCA study; 
the intended audience and the intended application. In addition, the system boundaries and functional 
unit are defined (ISO 2006b). The results of LCI are given by compiling the input (resources) and out­
put (emissions) in relation to the functional unit of the product throughout the life cycle stages. LCIA 
aims to evaluate and understand the magnitude of the potential environmental effects of the study. In 
the interpretation phase, the results from LCIA are evaluated in relation to the goal and scope definition
 
Conducting a LCA is an iterative process, since some issues cannot be addressed or touched upon ini­
tially. These issues will be addressed, improved or revised later on as knowledge about the study increases 
while working with it (JRC European Commission 2011).
The results of LCIA are given in impact categories either in Midpoint level or in Endpoint level. Mid­
point level is a problem orientated approach, which translates the environmental impacts to environ­
mental categories e.g. ecotoxicity, human toxicity and fossil depletion etc. Endpoint level is a dam­
age-oriented approach, which translate the environmental impacts into matters of concern such as 
human health, ecosystem damage and resources depletion (PE Int. 2015). The Midpoint level results 
have a lower uncertainty compared to Endpoint level results. However, it easier to understand the End­
point level results for decision makers, as there are much fewer categories to relate to (PE Int. 2015).
Even though LCA is a standardized method described in ISO 14040:2006 and 14044:2006 there is 
room for the practitioner to make choices which might affect the results. Therefore further guidance is 
necessary to assure the quality and support consistency (JRC European Commission 2011), hence this 
LCA study will be conducted according to the ISO 14040:2006 and ISO 14040:2006 and guided by 
ILCD handbook. If there are deviations from the ILCD Handbook it will be mentioned throughout 
the study. 
The two most widespread kinds of software for conducting a LCA are GABi and SigmaPro. In this study 
GaBi is chosen because of the accessibility from university. The database EcoInvent 2.2 by PE interna­
tional is used as it is the largest database and has a strong popularity by LCA practitioner.

3.2.2	Scope Definition 
A functional unit defines and quantifies the qualitative and quantitative aspects of the function of a 
product. One of the main purposes of the functional unit is to provide a reference of which inputs and 
outputs are normalized. The functional unit is vital as it guarantee the comparability between different 
products. The reference flow fulfills the functional unit; it relates to all other input and output flows. 
(European Commission 2010; ISO 2006b)
 The main function of the Montana shelf is to storage and display goods, the shelf modular and fits to 
the rest of the Montana shelf system. The secondary function is the aesthetic of the overall look, which 
should be in line with the design aesthetic of Montana and easy to maintain. 
The functional unit is a Montana shelf with outer dimension 35 cm X 35 cm X 38 cm that fulfill the 
minimum mechanical properties of a conventional Montana shelf with same outer dimensions. 
It should be noted that Montana shelf with MDF shelf has a thickness of 12 mm while Montana with 
CRC and FRC has a thickness of 8 mm.  The outer dimensions enable the shelves to fit to the rest of 
the system, which is the core concept behind Montana shelf system. 
The reference flow is one Montana shelf of type 6161 with outer dimension 35 cm X 35 cm X 38 cm.
3.2.2.1 Product System Model
To perform a LCA it is necessary to establish a product system model. A product system model is a 
collection of unit processes of elementary and product flows, and can perform one or more defined 
functions. Which stage of the life cycle and which processes are in included in the analyzed system, are 
defined by the system boundary. A precise definition of system boundary ensures that all the relevant 
processes are taken into account in the modeled system and thereby all related potential environmental 
impacts are appropriately covered. The product system model are modeled in regard to a cradle to grave 
approach - where every life cycle stage is considered. 
When conducting a comparative study, the processes which are the same for all materials can be ne­
glected. Productions steps and packaging of the Montana shelf are excluded as it is assumed to be the 
same process for every composite material.The product system model for every material is available in 
3.3 on page 27. 
3.2.2.2 Cut-off Criteria 
Any process which account below 1% percentage of the environmental impact are cut off. Evaluation of 
these process are based on qualitative assessment – the magnitude and the type of processes are typically 
taken into account when qualitatively evaluating the potential cut off processes. Furthermore, a prag­

3.2.2.3 Solving Multi-functional Process
According to ISO 14044:2006 there is a hierarchy of solving multifunctional processes. The first ap­
proach is to subdivide the multifunctional process and thereby elude (cutting free) the actual process. 
The second approach is to expand the system, where substitution is included. The third approach is 
allocation, where the multifunctional process is allocated by a physical causal relationship between the 
different products or functions. 
This study is a micro decisions level support hence it will make an insignificant change in the background 
system, therefore an attributional approach is applied. According to ILCD Handbook, multi-functional 
processes should be allocated with regard to physical causality or to economic value. 
In this study, a combination of system expansion and allocation is performed, however the system ex­
pansion procedure has the highest priority. System expansion is disregarded, when it is to hard to pre­
dict the substitution of a multi-functional process,  as it increase to the uncertainty of the study. System 
expansion is carried out in the end of life stage as the method to manage waste is very settled and will 
not change that significantly in the future, which decrease uncertainties related with system expansion 
The allocation is performed with regard to mass and economic value in this study. when assessing the 
agriculture stage of  NRC. 
3.2.2.4 Data Quality  
To ensure the data quality, the data must be representative, according to  ILCD handbook representa­
tiveness is “the ability of inventory data to describe the emissions and environmental impacts of the system” 
(European Commission 2010). Data representativeness consists of three components; technology, geo­
graphical and temporal representativeness. All three components are interrelated and have to be taken 
into account and fulfilled in the used data. For a comparative study a balanced representativeness is 
critical, if not it might distort the results
ISO 14040 differentiates between primary and secondary data. Primary data is directly related to the 
specific product system, while secondary data is based on national and international database, scientific 
papers and research groups. To assure that a LCA study to be fully representative, primary data must be

used. However, collecting primary data is a lengthy process and not all producers are willing to release 
there data, so aiming for only primary data is usually insufficient. A LCA which use primary and sec­
ondary data can still be representative, though the uncertainties of the results increase with the use of 
secondary data as the data is generic. 
The foreground system typically uses primary data provided by the producer and the supplier as the 
foreground system is controllable by producer. However, if no or few primary data are available, generic

3.2.3	Life Cycle Impact Assessment 
Life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) evaluates the significance of potential environmental impacts by 
converting the results from the inventory to potential damages to the environment. The procedure of 
this assessment links the inventory data with specific environmental impact category indicators to fa­
cilitate the understanding of the potential damage caused by the impact from the product-system (ISO 
2006a), see Figure 8 on page 26  for the relation between LCI parameters, Midpoint indicators and 
Endpoint indicators. 
The LCIA methodology ReCiPe is chosen as it offer results at both Midpoint and Endpoint level, an 
overview impact categories are given in Appendix I on page 1.The Endpoint results are of great im­
portance, as the intended audience is non- technical hence Endpoint level results make it easier to un­
derstand. Furthermore, ReCiPe methodology is commonly used by LCA practitioners.  The Midpoint 
results represent a single environmental aspect, while the endpoint results represent an area of protec­
tion. There are 18 impact categories at Midpoint level, these impact categories are further converted and 
aggregated at Endpoint level into 3 areas of protection (Huijbregts M. & Struijs J. 2012), see Table 7 on 
page 27 for an overview of Midpoint impact categories and Damage categories at Endpoint. The end­
point approach has a longer cause effect chain from the emitted emission to areas of protection, which 
cause a higher uncertainty  than the midpoint results, in addition the models to convert the midpoint 
to endpoint results are not well developed, see Figure 7 on page 26(Buchgeister 2012). 
==PAGE:35==
25
The Midpoint and Endpoint results are characterized to quantify the degree to which each pollutant 
contributes to the different environmental impacts. These impact scores are expressed in individual 
metric specific for the impact category, which make it difficult to interpret the scores. The characterized 
impact scores can be normalized to make it easier to interpret the results, as all the impact is expressed 
in same unit -person equivalent  
According to ISO 14044 (ISO 2006) characterization is mandatory, while normalization is an optional 
step. In this  study the results are normalized to better compare and interpret the results. 
Normalisation of Midpoint and Endpoint results are deployed with three different cultural perspectives, 
where uncertainties have been incorporated. The cultural perspectives represent a set of subjective choic­
es on issues like time horizon, assumed manageability etc. The three cultural perspectives are 
• Individualist (I): Based short termed interest. 
• Hierarchist (H):  Based on the most common policy principles with respect to time frame and 
other.
• Egalitarian (E): Based on long term with precautionary principle thinking
The hierarchist is often considered at the default perspective to be implemented as it seeks consensus 
and is often used in scientific models. For this reason the hierarchist view will be applied in study 
(RIVM,CML, PRe Consultants, Radboud Universiteit 2015). 
The endpoint results can be weighted and grouped into a single score. However, the nature of the 
weighting put more emphasis on one category and makes the results subjective and difficult to see what 
is behind that one score. All in all a single score is easy to interpret and compare, which makes it an easy 
method to use for communication of the results to the decision makers, but at the same time the results 
will lose their transparency and have a higher uncertainty.

When materials are recycled,  the production of  a new material of the same kind is avoided. The avoid­

credit of recycling process is calculated by the following formula.  
Environmental credit = x*REC + (1-x) *Q* VIR
Where x is the proportion of recycled material on the average market mix, (1-x) is the proportion of 
virgin material in the average market mix, Q is the quality factor of recycled materials vs. virgin material, 
REC is the environmental load of the recycling process and VIR is the environmental load of the virgin 
material. 
The common replacement approach is 1:1 substitution (Gala et al. 2015), while this formula take into 
consideration the degradation of recycled material by introducing a quality factor. The value of Q-fac­
tor should be based on recycled material’s technical performance and the level of contamination in the 
material. The Q-factor is directly linked to the cycles of recycled material; the recycled material becomes 
downgraded the more time it is recycled (Gala et al. 2015). The degradation of recycled material (Q-fac­
tor) is highly depending on the type of material. 
Q-factors CRC, MDF and FRC

5	INTERPRETATION  & 
DISCUSSION
In this chapter, the results obtained in previous chapter are interpreted and discussed .Uncertainty has 
great importance of a LCA study and might affect the outcome of the results, especially in a compar­
ative study where one scenario is compared to another scenario. The uncertainty of the study will be 
discussed including the data quality. The modeling of the models will also be discussed as this influences 
the results as well.
5.1	Baseline scenario
The results (see Figure 12 on page 41) of the baseline scenario showed surprisingly that the bio-com­
posites (FRC and CRC) do not perform better than MDF, which  is the best performing material. ReaA 
is the worst performing material when looking at the baseline scenarios. 
MDF consist mainly of materials which are residual and waste product from wood production, which 
give them a quite low environmental burden. Production of CRCs is very energy intensive and both 
CRCs (ReaA and ReaB) contain fossil based material (plastic). These two factors contribute to the high 
environmental impact of the CRCs.  
All the FRCs perform environmentally better than the CRC but worse than MDF. FRC with virgin 
plastic (Flax/PE and Flax/PP) has the highest impact compared to the FRC with recycled plastic (Flax/
REPE and Flax/REPP).  FRCs perform environmentally better than CRCs despite the fact that they 
contain a higher amount of fossil based material. The reason for this is that the production of FRCs is 
not as energy intensive as CRCs. In the coming section the results will be discussed in depth.
5.1.1	Single score
The life stage which is has the biggest burden is material production, this apply for every material. 
For CRC, the composite production account for a major impact, and besides EOL the other life cycle 
stages are insignificant. EOL also have the biggest environmental gain compared to the other material. 
The environmental gain at EOL is strongly related to how the recycling process is modeled. There is an 
avoided production of virgin material in recycling process of EOL. This avoided production of virgin 
composite will utilize a significant environmental gain as the virgin composite production accounts for 
a major environmental impact.

5.3	Increasing Recycling Rate at EoL 
As seen on the results –Figure 21 on page 52 increasing recycling rate in EOL decreases the environ­
mental impact.
It is interesting that with 100% recycling all the materials have more or less same environmental score. 
This is caused by the method used to model recycling in EOL. (Gala et al. 2015) proposed a method to 
model recycling in EOL in attributional LCA, which was used in this study. The method is described in 
the following formula (see 3.3.1 on page 28  for a walk through of the formula)
Environmental credit = x*REC + (1-x) *Q* VIR
iThe environmental credit of recycling process is made from the environmental impact from avoided 
virgin production and avoided recycled production. The ratio between avoided virgin and recycled pro­
duction depends on the average market mix of virgin and recycled material. The amount of avoided vir­
gin material depends on the Quality-factor of the recycled material compared to its virgin counterpart.
In the case of CRC and FRC the Quality factor was estimated to be 0.9 for all the materials, thus by a 
recycling rate of 100% there is an avoided virgin production of 90%. As the Endpoint results for CRC’s 
and FRC’s have shown, the composite production accounts for the majority of the environmental im­
pact for both materials (see Figure 13).Therefore, with an avoided virgin composite production of 90%

5.8	Modelling of EOL
As seen on the results from the Figure 21 on page 52 and Figure 21 on page 52, the method by 
Gala et al. (2015)used to model recycling has a great influence on the EOL results. In this section, the 
weaknesses of this formula are discussed. The formula is given below. 
Environmental credit = x*REC + (1-x) *Q* VIR
The formula assumed that is possible to recycle every material to a new same material, and Q-factor is 
introduced to compensate for the degradation of material by recycling process. However, in reality, it is 
not always possible to recycle all material into new same material, which is a weak point of this formula. 
Materials are often used in combination with other materials and it might not be possible to separate 
two different kinds of materials in the recycling process. Therefore it might not be impossible to recycle 
every material it into new same material, e.g. MDF.
According to Gala et al. (2015) the Q-factor should be based on technical performance of the recycled 
material relative to the technical performance of the virgin material. It is not mentioned which specific 
aspects of technical performance should be used to define the Q-factor. This can be critical, as  the level 
of decrease of the technical performance is not necessarily the same for every technical parameter (e.g. 
Young’s modulus and tensile strength).
There is not always  available data about how the average market mix of recycled and virgin content, 
which increase the uncertainty of a study.
It is important to model virgin material (VIR) and recycled material (REC) with the same degree of 
detail for not giving an environmental favor to one of the parameters, as they have great influence on 
the outcome of the results. This specific problem is illustrated in the case of this study; REC is modeled 
very simple while VIR is modeled with a higher degree of detail - consequently REC has a lower envi­
ronmental impact. Therefore, if the recycled content of average market (x) is high, the environmental 
gain is low. 
The use of this  method (Gala et al. 2015) in this study has increase the uncertainty of the study. This 
method should be used with carefulness to decrease the uncertainty of a study.

1. Define Product-Market Boundaries and Structures
	
2. Identify and describe end-users
	
3. Value added chain
	
4. Evaluate the key competitors
	
5. Forecast the market and growth trend
The first step is to define product- market boundaries and structures in order to frame the product 
which is examined and to create an overview of the market boundaries.  This is realized  by first looking 
at the broad perspective of the product – which general need is satisfied by the product.  Subsequently 
the product types which fulfill the general need is identified, hereafter the examined product is identi­
fied within the generic product market.
End-users are identified and described to obtain an understanding of the target group. To classify the 
end-users the typical characteristics of end-users (such as age, gender, income and occupation) are 
identified to construct the end-user’s profile. It is identified which parameters (price, design etc.) have 
importance for end-users when purchasing a product. 
Value added chain along the supply are defined to give an overview and to gain an understanding. 
Key competitors are evaluated to analyze the industrial landscape that a company operates within.  Por­
ter’s Five Forces and Strategy Canvas are the models used to evaluate the key competitors.
Porter’s Five Forces are used to assess the competitive force in the market and it provides an overview of 
the market forces which influence the competition between the analyzed company and its competitors. 
The forces are ‘Threat of new entrants’, ‘Threat of substitutes’, ‘Bargaining power of supplier’ and ‘Bar­
gaining power of costumers’,  analysis of these four forces enables an evaluation of the competitive

7	CONCLUSION & 
RECOMMENDATIONS
This study quantified and compared the environmental impact of Montana shelfs with MDF, CRC and 
FRC, respectively. No previous research has been conducted on this subject. 
The LCA results showed that MDF and all FRC is environmentally superior to CRC. The environmen­
tal impact of MDF only accounts for approximately ¼ of the environmental impact of ReaA, while the 
environmental impact for the worst performer, FRC (Flax/PE), accounts for ½ of the ReaA’s environ­
mental impact. These results show that ReaA has a high environmental impact compared to MDF and 
FRCs. The environmental load of ReaB is 10% lower than that of ReaA, which means that replacing 
virgin bico fiber with recycled bico fiber is environmentally beneficial for Really ApS.  
MDF consists of resin, waste, and residual products from wood production, which cause a low environ­
mental load as waste and residual products have a low burden. 
The discarded textile and the production processes give rise to a high environmental impact.  Cotton is 
a very burdensome material; despite being recycled cotton it still cause  high environmental load. The 
current production of ReaA is a small-scale test production, which might induce the high impact of the 
production processes, as the production processes is not optimized and thereby have a large energy con­
sumption. Large-scale production was simulated to investigate how it would affect the environmental 
impact of the ReaA; energy consumption of the air-laid process was reduced. With a reduction of energy 
consumption by 80% for the air-laid process, ReaA would have comparable performance to MDF. This 
indicates that ReaA has a potential to perform environmentally better than it does now. However, it 
was not possible to evaluate whether the energy consumption reduction of 80% is realistic when going 
from a small-scale to a large-scale production. Hence, the potential environmental performance of ReaA 
highly depends on the future production set-up and the efficiency of the large-scale production. To 
obtain a more realistic result of ReaA’s environmental load, it is necessary to use production data from 
a large scale production. 
Future waste management and the closed-loop concept was investigated to evaluate its influence on the 
results. The results showed that an increasing recycling rate at EoL decreases the environmental burden, 
thus, it is environmental beneficial to recycle waste. With 100% recycling of waste (closed-loop con­
cept), FRCs and CRCs have similar environmental impact, and the impact is lower than MDF (with 
current waste scenario). Therefore, if the closed-loop concept is implemented fully, the CRC is perform­
ing better than MDF, although only slightly.

 Chapter 7 CONCLUSION & RECOMMENDATIONS
Future scenario of recycled content of a material in average market mix The results for increasing re­
cycling content of material in an average market mix showed that with an increasing recycled content 
in an average market mix the environmental load is increased. The increase of recycling content in an 
average market mix is not desirable, as it will worsen the result. 
The market for Montana Raw with CRC was investigated to identify whether there is market for it. 
The market assessment indicated that the potential end-users are environmentally conscious and have a 
generally positive view on shelves made of sustainable materials. The sustainable aspect of a shelf is not 
a critical factor for end-users when purchasing a shelf; design and price have a large influence on their 
choice. If there is market for Montana Raw with CRC. depends highly on whether the price and design 
fit the end-users’ needs and wants; the environmental aspect of Montana Raw serves as is a competitive 
advantage.  It was identified that the value propositions of CRC and Montana Raw were not aligned – 
Montana does not put emphasis on the sustainable aspects of CRC.  Montana Raw will have a stronger 
competitive advantage if Montana incorporate the sustainable aspects into their shelf, as none of their 
key competitor offer this value. 
All in all this study indicated that Montana shelfs with MDF is more environmental friendly than 
Montana Raw shelfs with CRCs or FRCs. However, considering that the production data for CRC is 
from a small-scale production, the environmental impact of CRC in the case of large-scale production 
might be much lower. There is a potential market for Montana Raw with green materials, the end-users 
value environmental considerations, however it is not the decisive factor that influence the end-users’ 
decision making. Design and price is the most significant factors for end-users, so the potential market 
for Montana Raw with CRC is determined by design and price of Montana Raw. 
Recommendation 
The LCA study provides a foundation for a comparison of the environmental performance for MDF, 
CRC, and FRC included in the production of Montana shelfs. However, the biggest limitations of this 
study are the available data and validation of obtained data. To obtain a more realistic result, the data 
quantity and quality should beimproved. The following aspect need to improved. It is recommended to 
improve the following aspects; 
Data for CRC’s production process is critical for the results of this study, as the production processes 
account for a major part of the environmental contribution. The used data for production processes, 
which is the best available data, is from small-scale production. It is recommended to use data from the 
future large-scale production as to give a more accurate comparison between CRC, MDF, and FRC. The 
production process of MDF and FRC is modeled for a large-scale production with the use of a generic 
process from EcoInvent.     
The paint and varnish applied to MDF is calculated from a production sheet for Montana shelfs, how­
ever, the units were given in minutes, so assumptions were made to convert minutes to liters of paint 
==PAGE:97==
87
and varnish. These values need to be validated as paint and varnish contribute to a large part of MDF’s 
environmental impact. 
The production of FRCs is modeled according to the production of FRC for a car door panel as there 
are no commercial examples of FRC used in furniture production. The production of FRC for furniture 
might be different, which will influence the results. Moreover, the ratio between flax fiber and plastic 
might also be different in FRC for furniture production. It is recommended to use future data for FRC 
applied on furniture. It should also be investigated if FRCs need a surface treatment.  
The study has shown that modeling of EoL has a significant influence on the overall results. The model­
ing of the recycling process for a material is modeled in a simple manor due to lack of data. It is recom­
mended that the recycling process should be modeled more detailed and modeled specifically for each 
material. The Q-factor for each material should be investigated by experiments. 
A new EcoInvent database (EcoInvent v.3) has just been released. It is recommend to use this database, 
as it is the newest database and, hence,has newer data. 
From the finding of the market assessment it is recommended that Montana should emphasis on the 
sustainability aspects of the CRC, as this value is not offered by any of its key competitors and it will 
give them a competitive advantage.  The current target group is quite broad, it is recommended to have 
a clear target group as it will make marketing strategy more focused.  Once the target group is refined a 
new questionnaire should sent to the new target group to identify there taste and values.
