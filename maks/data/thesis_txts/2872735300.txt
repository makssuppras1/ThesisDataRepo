Abstract (English)
The following masters thesis explores improvement to data collection methods, tools and acces/
sibility in the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED) regarding anxiety detection and reduction.
Current approaches can be lacking in regards to invasiveness or cohesiveness for aggregating
objective physiological data. This project sets out to contribute in this area by developing and
evaluating a prototype for a centralized platform or field deployable solution to enhance data
gathering and accessibility, with focus on eye/tracking technology. How can such a solution be
developed and what are the needs and requirements in a pediatric setting.
The key outcome developed is a field/deployable prototype, hosted and on a Raspberry Pi 5 that
integrates with Pupil Labs’ Neon eye/tracking glasses. The system provides users remote control
access over recording sessions and event registration to help enhance data quality and reduce
invasiveness through the remote access. The system was tested and ran at Herlev Hospital’s
PED to asses real/world viability and usage at parallel studies ongoing during this thesis.
The findings confirm the technical feasibility of using a portable, standalone system that’s able
to help the methodology of data gathering at the parallel studies in the pediatric environment.
However hurdles and challenges still exist regarding usability and possible future adoption.
Usability evaluations and field testing revealed practical challenges such as “technoference”
caused by the distance a mobile device creates during patient interactions. Further is a reduced
flexibility or speed for more descriptive annotations of events compared to pen and paper.
In conclusion, the work validates the potential of using a portable system with eye/tracking
technology as a usable tool and with value to pediatric anxiety research. It showcases that while
practical integration is possible, many considerations are required of the clinical environments
and human/computer interaction to be effective. Future development for the specific tools now
revolves much around trying new avenues to improve user experience, the introduction of multi/
modality and more integrated deployment in the hospital environments existing infrastructure.

Abstrakt (Danish)
Det følgende speciale udforsker forbedringer af dataindsamlingsmetoder, værktøjer og
tilgængelighed på Pædiatrisk Skadestue (PED) vedrørende angstregistrering og /reduktion.
Nuværende tilgange kan mangle i forhold til invasivitet eller gnidninger ved aggregering af
objektive fysiologiske data. Dette projekt sætter sig for at bidrage på dette område ved at
udvikle og evaluere en prototype til en centraliseret platform eller felt operationel løsning til at
forbedre dataindsamling og tilgængelighed, med fokus på eye/tracking teknologi. Hvordan kan
en sådan løsning udvikles og hvad er behovene og kravene i et pædiatrisk miljø.
Det centrale resultat der er udviklet er en feltudplacerbar prototype, hostet på en Raspberry Pi
5, som integrerer med Pupil Labs’ Neon eye/tracking briller. Systemet giver brugere fjernadgang
til kontrol over optagelsessessioner og hændelsesregistrering for at hjælpe med at forbedre
datakvaliteten og reducere invasivitet gennem fjernadgangen. Systemet blev testet og kørt på
Herlev Hospitals PED for at vurdere anvendelighed og brug ved parallelle studier, der foregik
under dette speciale.
Resultaterne bekræfter den tekniske gennemførlighed af at bruge et bærbart, selvstændigt
system, der er i stand til at hjælpe metodologien for dataindsamling ved de parallelle studier i
det pædiatriske miljø. Dog eksisterer der stadig hindringer og udfordringer vedrørende bruger/
venlighed og mulig fremtidig adoption. Brugervenlighedsevalueringer og felttest afslørede
praktiske udfordringer såsom “technoference” forårsaget af den afstand, en mobilenhed skaber
under patientinteraktioner. Yderligere er der en reduceret fleksibilitet eller hastighed for mere
beskrivende annoteringer af hændelser sammenlignet med pen og papir.
Konklusionen er, at arbejdet validerer potentialet i at bruge et bærbart system med eye/tracking
teknologi som et anvendeligt værktøj og med værdi for pædiatrisk angstforskning. Det viser,
at selvom praktisk integration er mulig, kræves der mange overvejelser af de kliniske miljøer
og menneske/computer interaktion for at være effektiv. Fremtidig udvikling for de specifikke
værktøjer ville omhandle nye veje til at forbedre brugeroplevelsen, introduktionen af multi/
modalitet og mere integreret indsættelse i hospitalsmiljøernes eksisterende infrastruktur.

1.1 Context and Motivation
Anxiety is a familial feeling for most people in an unfamiliar environment or when presented
with unknown territory. It is therefore to be expected that children at hospital settings or
arriving at the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED) often are faced with such emotions.
Hospitals such as Herlev Hospital in Denmark and their PED of course try to mitigate such
feelings and provide good experiences for their patients. However long waiting times in peak
periods or the unknown environment can be more challenging to comprehend or handle for
children and provide a challenge or source of this anxiety.
Upon arrival at the hospital, children and guardians go through a structured process. This
revolves around by first being triaged to determine urgency of care. They are then in most cases
taken to a waiting area if not in need of immediate medical attention. This waiting period varies
often without any precise indication of waiting times. Lastly followed by an examination by a
doctor which usually results in being discharged with the possibility of hospitalization.
Anxiety can be present throughout the hospital experience and there has been researched
various ways to detect, reduce or make interventions to help improve this area. However, many
approaches to detect or monitor can be restrictive to the child or limited because of requirements
of the technology or other limitations. Despite research in the area is there no perfect or perfect
way to reduce anxiety or measure and define a current child’s exact feelings.
This report sets out to try and improve some of these challenges like data gathering techniques
and utilizing less invasive or restrictive data collection devices. Further looking into not only
data collection but also simple monitoring of patients and event registration. And combining
these elements in a cohesive and reliant manner.
The project and report work is also heavily centered and focused around eye/tracking
glasses from Pupil Labs [1] in regards to design and implementation of working prototype.
This gives the report a another focal point other than general theoretical solutions with
specific implementation challenges, and insights particularly to what certain data collection
technologies offers.

Existing solutions and research explore mitigations and improvements to handle anxiety via
anxiety/reducing games, monitoring through wearable wristbands and other sensors and other

Recieve and aggregate anxiety/related data from various sources in children’s emergency care

2.1 Anxiety
Anxiety is a quite fundamental emotional response to perceived threats and can be
characterized by heightened nervousness, apprehension or worry. It is a function to prepare
individuals for potential dangers but with the caveat of excessive anxiety potentially interfering
with daily functioning [2]. It can be distinguished from fear conceptually and physiologically
despite the terms being used interchangeably. Anxiety is more focused around the future and
a more long lasting response for more loosely defined threats. Fear on the other hand is more
centered present and immediate threats and is a more short/lived response to more clearly
defined threats [3].
There are further subdivisions for anxiety in regards to classifications, disorders or how an
individual is affected by anxiety [4]. One of these is the classification of Trait anxiety and State
anxiety[5]. Trait anxiety is if ones personality and how they view the world may be defined
by anxiety. And State anxiety simply refers to anxiety only occurring in response to stressful
circumstances. Further are there anxiety disorders such as panic disorder, social anxiety disorder
and many more [4]. This paper will focus more on general anxiety or state anxiety and that found
typically of pediatric patients. It’s still however important being able to differentiate these other
types and distinctions for proper understanding and evaluation.
2.1.1 Anxiety in Pediatric Emergency Departments
Like mentioned is anxiety a normal thing and it’s natural to have such emotions in an
environment such as hospitals [6]. Hospitals unfortunately are something that is correlated
with disease, pain or discomfort in many different ways [7]. But no matter potential correlations
or not, children have less experience in these situations and less control over their given
circumstances and are therefore also more easily intimidated by the unknown situation [8].
Anxiety can derive from many places during the timeline for a visit to the hospital and from
many of the surrounding factors. A hospital visit will typically involve the child having a problem
in need of examination. The problem in itself can induce anxiety or not knowing what and when
the next thing will happen, the various waiting times and much more. For specifics refer to
Section 2.3 on specifically Herlev PED as a reference on what a hospital visit can look like.
As described it’s a very common thing for children to experience anxiety or fear in hospitals.
Therefore is it also important being able to ascertain this information in a manner that’s is
reliable and helpful for medical staff. And that hopefully can give a clearer image of a childs
situation aswell as what might be affecting it like parents, fear of the unknown, pain or
something else.

2.1.2 Assessing Anxiety, Fear and Pain subjectively
Assessing anxiety, fear and pain in children is a significant challenge due to the subjective
nature of determining a patients current state of mind. There doesn’t exist a flawless objective
method that gives a perfect number for how an individuals anxiety, nor for any other emotion[9].
This of course isn’t solely isolated to children but can be further a challenge because of pediatric
patients inherent reduced experience in such manners as one example.
Self Assessment
One of the more famous and widely used methods for determining specifically pain is the pain
scale by Wong Baker [10]. This scale is to help base the pain of an individual on their own opinion
for where they feel they are on the scale and what is representative of their pain. An isolated
pain scale of just giving a number from 1/10 can also be used, but with the potential drawback of
a more vague reference point for the patient. That’s why the Wong Baker pain scale is especially
useful as it gives something for the pediatric patients to reflect themselves in.
Figure 1: Pain scale [10], [11]
At Herlev PED they use the Wong Baker pain scale but also have experimented with use of
a fear scale [12] to measure fear or anxiety as part of current studies done alongside this
masters thesis, refer to Figure 2. This is to expand on the pain scale and not just ask about
the pain but also the situation or general emotions surrounding being at the hospital. The
two measurements can then help further define a broader picture of the child’s situation in
collaboration with everything else. Just like the Wong Baker scale is this also based on faces to
give a reference point a child can reflect in and more easily determine what they think correlates
with their feelings.

2.1.3 Interventions
As anxiety in the hospital is a common and often unavoidable experience for children is it a risk
that cannot be easily eliminated or transferred. Because of this, it becomes essential to focus on
managing and reducing anxiety as effectively as possible. Acknowledging that some amount of
anxiety is likely to be present and implementing tools and strategies to help the children adapt
to the situation. This can have many forms and be approached or handled throughout the whole
process or journey of a child going to the hospital [13].
One way to conceptualize these strategies is by dividing them into three categories, based on
when and how they are primarily implemented: preventive interventions, situational (in/the/
moment) interventions, and post/event support, refer to Figure 3.
Figure 3: Simple intervention categorization
Preventive Interventions are proactive measures aimed at reducing anxiety before escalation.
The goal is creating an environment less intimidating and more predictable for children [14].
• Environmental design: Calming colors, child/friendly decorations, interactive distractions.
• Educational preparation: Videos or booklets explaining hospital procedures.
• Parental coaching: Guidance for parents on how to prepare children and support them.
Situational Interventions are interventions that are more prevalent during the hospital visit.
These can happen while in the waiting rooms or during examinations, to actively mitigate
anxiety real time and help calm down a child [15].
• Distraction techniques: Use of tablets, games, VR headsets, Clown visits or toys to draw
attention away from the hospital environment.
• Emotional regulation tools: Breathing exercises, guided imagery, or calming audio.
• Sensory tools: Fidget toys, stress balls, or weighted blankets.
Postevent Support is a lesser part but for after the specific visit or during the end of these.
These interventions or actions can help avoid fear related to future hospital visits.
• Reflective Conversation: Praise and explanation of what occurred to frame the visit positively.
• Feedback collection: Allowing children to express how they felt and what could be improved.
• Follow/up materials: Sending home a summary of the visit, with coping strategies.
These are all just a few examples and especially the list of situational interventions is quite
long with various approaches and tools involved. Nonetheless is it important to understand and
accertain these types of ways to handle anxiety and fear in the PED to ensure proper treatment.
Also note that the timing in Figure 3, can also be viewed as start or end of a hospital visit.
This paper will have more focus on how measurements or access to new insights have potential
to help evaluate or improve these interventions.

2.2 Technologies for biological measurements
There has been developed a multitude of ways in medicine to capture biometrics and other
assessments of the human body. These are to help diagnose and get insights into the wellbeing
and state of individuals. Many of them can be used isolated or in conjunction with each other,
and these can further help ascertain the emotional state of a subject, and in this context if they
are experiencing anxiety. The list of these approaches and technologies are long and each area
of the human body can have a multitude of specific measurements or characteristics to measure.
A broad view encompassing many of the primary areas can be seen in Figure 4 and these along
others has been grouped and described in this section according to related areas.
Figure 4: Overview of biometric indicators for assessing anxiety / ungrouped
Physiological Measures
As mentioned and shown in Figure 4 is there much to measure and detect in regards to the body.
Some of the primary physiological signals are those related to the nervous system and more
specifically the Autonomic nervous system (ANS) gives well/established indicators of stress and
anxiety [16]. When an individual is anxious or stressed the Sympathetic nervous system (SNS)
or sympathetic part of the nervous system is activated. This leads to measurable changes in
heart rate, sweat gland activity, respiration and other body functions.
Heart Rate and Heart rate variability  (HRV): The heart is a source of key physiological
measures. Anxiety often leads to elevated heart rate and a reduced HRV [16], which reflects
activity in the SNS. This can be measured using wearable sensors like chest belts or
smartwatches and similar, with varying invasiveness like pulse oximeters attached to the finger.
Respiration: Changing breathing patterns, hyperventilation, irregular breathing and shallow
breaths are also patterns or responses related to stress and anxiety [17]. Therefore these can
also be relevant metrics which can be measured in various ways. Respiratory rate as an example
can measured manually or via respiratory belts or measured indirectly via other measures.
Blood Pressure: When measuring blood pressure is it a common problem that the individual
being stressed or anxious can lead to elevated blood pressure, Hypertension. It’s therefore
important for the individual to be relaxed when during procedure to get more truthful values.
This of course showcases that blood pressure is an indicator of stress but measuring it with
cuffs and blood measuring equipment is problematic and impractical to do continuously [18].
Skin Conductance: Electrodermal activity (EDA), also called Galvanic skin response (GSR), is
measuring skin conductance, which rises with increased sweat gland activity, which is linked
to sympathetic nervous system arousal. EDA is a sensitive and real/time measure which can be
measured either with electrodes on fingers, palms or feet or via certain wearables [19].

Temperature: The core body temperature isn’t what’s focus in this regard, but the consequence
of constricted blood vessels which reduces blood flow to the skin, and thereby the temperature
of the skin, peripheral vasoconstriction. The SNS has this response which skin temperature
sensors, which can be on wearable devices, can capture these changes. But this measurement
or indicator is of course susceptible to other factors like room temperature, exercise, etc. [20]
Hormonal Indicators: Anxiety is associated with elevated cortisol levels and also fluctuations
in other hormones that are stress/related. These measurements however usually require saliva,
blood or urine samples and are therefore more invasive and disruptive than other methods [21].
Neurological Measures
Another more specific area to measure activity and signals is the brain. This can allow for a direct
window or view into cognitive or emotional processes.
Electroencephalography: Electroencephalogram (EEG) records electrical activity from the brain
via specific patterns of the brain neurons. This can be increased beta activity or altered frontal
asymmetry. These markers can reflect heightened arousal and be observed in stressed or
anxious states [22]. The process requires electrodes to be placed on the scalp and be connected
to a machine which is impractical and can restrict movement and limit practicality.
Functional NearInfrared Spectroscopy  (fNIRS): fNIRS measures changes in the blood
oxygenation in the brain cortex, particularly in regions involved with emotion regulation. These
areas show characteristic changes during stress or anxiety. It can provide an alternative to the
EEG that is generally easier to wear and tolerate, allowing for better use in more natural settings,
though still requiring a headband or sensors to be worn [23].
Behavioral and Expressive Measures
There is now covered many essential and internal measurements for anxiety, but it also
manifests externally through facial expressions, body movements or vocal characteristics
Facial Expression, Movement, and Posture: The human mind is capable of reading and
understanding facial ques for emotional state and this is also possible through computer vision
[24]. Changes in emotional state can lead to widened eyes, tightened lips or furrowed eyebrows.
Movement patterns may also change like fidgeting, restlessness, changes in posture or crossing
ones arms and much more [25].
Voice and Speech: The responses to stress and anxiety can also include changes in voice
parameters such as pitch, tone, speech rate or voice tremors. People that are anxious can tend
to speak faster, softly or with discernable hesitation. Voice analysis can allow for detecting and
analyzing these changes and in a more passive and non/disruptive manner [26].
Ocular Measures
Vision and eye/movement is another window into a details and insights about a person’s internal
state. Eye/tracking technologies have become more popular in research to help quantify aspects
such as emotional and attentional responses in individuals [27].
Eyetracking Technology: There is a few technologies and methods for doing eye/tracking
related measurements. These primarily include infrared light and high/speed cameras to monitor
gaze direction and movement. These can be positioned stationary or be screen/based or be more
mobile with wearables like glasses or being head/mounted [28].

Gaze and Attentional Biases: Anxiety is associated with attentional biases, meaning a
stronger fixation on certain elements—especially emotionally prominent or threatening stimuli.
Eye/tracking provides an objective way to quantify these biases. One example is studies
observing that socially anxious individuals have a tendency to show prolonged fixation on
negative facial expressions [29]. Further also having a faster initial attention towards threat
cues and need to identify the types of threats and more similar tendencies [30].
Pupil Dilation and Stress Response Beyond gaze, pupil size is also an indicator. Pupils dilate
as part of the body’s stress response, driven by activation of the SNS. In controlled lighting can
larger pupil diameters indicate increased cognitive load or emotional strain. Measurement of
fluctuations in pupil diameter, known as pupillometry, alongside other measurements can help
support interpretations of measurements.
Saccadic and Oculomotor Patterns: In extension to all the other metrics is saccadic eye
movements, rapid shifts of gaze between points, also capable of providing insights into internal
states [31]. Anxious individuals can exhibit quicker or more erratic saccades, shortened fixation
durations or delayed disengagement from stimuli. These are correlated with gaze but differs and
shows unique details. Lastly is blinking frequency which is also measurable and can be elevated
during high cognitive load and can be another metric to help discern anxiety or stress [32].
It varies based on technologies but modern eye/tracking setups can offer unobtrusive and
mobile solutions which makes them flexible and usable in dynamic environments.
Wearable Technologies
Many of the mentioned biometric markers, bodily measurements or technologies are heavily
situated to the medical field, hospital or psychiatric settings. Further are they often difficult to
move and requires a stationary and controlled setting. In extension to this are there varieties of
machines and technologies able to do the measurements. Some of these include technologies
that are inexpensive, mobile and accessible to more people.
Smart watches, wristbands and smart rings: These are some of these potentially more
accessible technologies. These can be able to monitor elements like for example heart rate,
HRV, skin temperature, sleep patterns, Blood oxygen saturation  (Sp02) physical activity,
Electrocardiography (ECG) and more. Of course these values and measurements can be subpar,
differ per model and be less accurate than more expensive and medically graded devices or
wearables build specifically for such measurements like Empatica E4 [33].
Other: The list of technologies is quite encompassing and probably without end, this Section 2.2
has gone through things mounted to fingers like rings and EDA sensors, wrist with watches or
bands, arms for blood pressure, head with eye/tracking and neurological measurements, chest
for heart and much more.
Many of these technologies and measurements vary in a range of places like what they are
capable of providing insights into, ease of use, invasiveness, but what’s common for all is how
they are able to give a more objective image of an individual. Utilizing these and preferably
in conjunction with each other can allow for better insights during medical procedures. A
subjective minor comparison of these technologies can be found in Section A.8.1 developed
during research.

Some of the recent research showcases and highlights the big influence of physical design
on anxiety reduction. Goel et al. [14] conducted a pilot study investigating the perceptions
of children and parents undergoing outpatient gastrointestinal procedures. The findings
showcased and demonstrated design elements like nature murals, colorful decor, comfortable
furniture and access to entertainment (television, natural light) greatly played a part in reduction
for perceived anxiety. Despite these environmental interventions and perceived improvement,
physiological measurements like HRV and Skin conductance response (SCR) showed limited
differences across spaces, suggesting that while design offers emotional support, real/time
physiological monitoring could still play a crucial complementary role to offer proper capture of
emotional state.
Parallel technological approaches is another topic aiming to directly manage or detect anxiety
in children. Mihandoust et al. [38] evaluated pre/visit virtual tours of healthcare facilities, with
the hypothesis that environmental familiarity could alleviate some of the anxiety. However,
their findings ended inconclusive in regards to measured anxiety between the child groups, but
some control group parents reported lower anxiety. The study concluded that that the exposure
to the virtual facility tours wasn’t greatly impacting the psychological measures related to
preoperative anxiety levels. Another avenue is, interventions blending biofeedback and sensory
engagement which some of demonstrates more consistent success. Savaş et al. [39] is one
of many VR/based games (BioVirtualPed) for which this specifically is integrating breathing
biofeedback. This led to significantly lower pain, fear, and anxiety levels during port catheter
needle insertion. Similarly, Orgil et al. [40] refined a treatment protocol combining VR immersion
with HRV biofeedback, achieving positive outcomes in pre/ and postoperative anxiety reduction.
Adding to the broader view, Li et al. [41] conducted a systematic review mapping technologies,
including wearables, biofeedback devices, and interactive robots, aimed at children’s stress
management. Their review highlights the value of real/time feedback systems that adapt
dynamically to physiological and emotional states. This is as well as the value of personalization
based on individual child characteristics. Multisensory engagement—using visual, auditory,
and tactile stimulus was also shown to keep attention and promote emotional regulation.
It was emphasized that interventions combining multiple methods or being multimodal, such
as HRV biofeedback integrated with interactive narratives, achieved more consistent success
than single/modality approaches. These findings can be said to align with trends in VR/based
and wearable biofeedback solutions and suggest that future pediatric approaches gains from
prioritizing adaptability and multimodality.
Real/time physiological monitoring has a range of studies and implementations and usages of
technologies. Redd et al. [42] is one of these examples of demonstrating that using wearable
devices like the Empatica E4 wristband [33] allowed for reasonably accurate prediction of
emotional dysregulation in children, with individualized models reaching over 80% accuracy
in their study. Constantin et al. [43] further explored the dynamic interplay of HRV between
children and their parents during pediatric venipuncture, emphasizing the potential for co/
regulation effects, how parent and child affect each other, and validating HRV as an accessible
biological marker. Mentioning a different monitoring technology, Sriranjan et al. [44] examined
EEG delta–beta coupling as a biomarker of shyness and anxiety in pediatric surgical settings,
identifying neural signatures associated with heightened stress. The findings of the study
2 Background and Related Work

==PAGE:27==
show the possibility of using brain/based monitoring to complement cardiac or electrodermal
measures in future anxiety detection frameworks.
Attention/based monitoring methods, more specifically eye/tracking, have also been validated
or showcased for their value for pediatric anxiety and emotional regulation assessing. Nozadi et
al. [45] is one of these examples that showcases that eye/tracking methodologies are functional
when determining emotional attention biases in preschool/aged children. Complementarily,
Piechaczek et al. [46] found that adolescents with major depression exhibited difficulties in
emotion regulation tied to attentional focus patterns, as captured through eye/tracking. These
are just a few findings but collectively support eye/tracking as a potential for less invasive,
informative method for capturing real/time emotional and cognitive states which can be relevant
to anxiety detection.
Further, technology platforms designed for pediatric care is a way to gain insights into
physiological monitoring in clinical workflows. Arjoune et al. [47] developed StethAid, an AI/
powered digital auscultation system, listening to the internal sounds of the body, tailored for
pediatrics, capable of real/time respiratory and cardiac anomaly detection. Although focused
on auscultation, their work demonstrates the feasibility of building platforms that aggregate,
process, and visualize physiological signals, an approach directly relevant to anxiety monitoring.
There are many of these advancements, and many more still not covered here but there can
still be seen potential for opportunities in the areas and room for improvement in the literature.
This in regards to further integration with broader physiological and behavioral datasets.
Further real/time aggregation and contextualization of multiple data streams (e.g., HRV, GSR,
gaze behavior, environmental factors) which seems to be an emerging area of interest or
study but still not widely done. Additionally, social/environmental influences such as parental
verbal interaction, demonstrated by Wauters et al. [48], can significantly modulate children’s
emotional experiences. Integrating these streams, or including caregiver behaviors and much
more represents and interesting and promising direction.

The architectural design exploration has listed and described various key parameters that shape

5.3.3 Event Management System
The event management system provides the ability to mark significant moments during eye
tracking sessions, creating a synchronized timeline of events with the recorded data. Because
the way events work on the pupil labs glasses has this part been implemented in a specific way
that cater and supports this setup. The glasses doesn’t have uniquely identifiable events (also
described as annotations) in it’s data, it only retrieves string of text. It then stores this merely as
a .txt file with lines of text equivalent to each event retrieved. There is further accompanied
by a .time file for which has a timestamp line corresponding to each of these events.
This means that the event management on this system can enrich the overall quality of events
but is still limited to only sending text to the actual pupil labs device. This is done by, when
starting recording via this system, sending an initial human readable identifier based on the
following structure {adjective}-{animal}-{timestamp}. Any events later send during this
recording time is then saved on the local device with the primary structure of {Unique id,
timestamp, event text, recording id}. These two parts allows for handling the session
information so it’s identifiable with the initial human readable id on the stored recording as the
connecting link. It’s then also possible once a recording is done also to easily extract a PDF that’s
based on a recordings data, and also retrieve or view earlier recordings events. This is done by
storing values in a local .json file and retrieving these as needed. Lastly is quick events and
custom events are handled the same way just as a text string.

Connection controls
The initial feature a user will interact with is the connection controls. This core feature simply
takes a button press to request the backend to connect or disconnect to the Neon glasses.
Once a connection is established, current connection status will be displayed alongside battery
status and some extra details in a details window. The state of connection is handled globally to
make it accessible for the other components. The global access is further used to lock or disable
functionality as it wouldn’t be usable until the connection has been established.

Recording controls
The recording controls is the primary interactive element for users of the application, alongside
event/management, for the MVP. This allows for remote control of starting and stopping
recordings. It’s a simple start and stop button with some timing functionality that gives a time
of current recording length. This isn’t exact, regarding most time data is based on current time,

Event controls and event history
The events controls are closely knitted function to the recording controls and only properly

5.4.2 Monitoring Page Features
While the recording page is focused on data capture, the monitoring page serves as a proof/
of/concept for real/time data visualization. The page is centered around providing live, visual
feedback from the Neon glasses’ sensors.
RealTime Data Streams
First aspect is simply turning on the data streams via the StreamingControls that starts and
stops the two types of streams, gaze and eye/events. This component is responsible for turning
on the streams on the backend and making the data accessible on the frontend.
The monitoring streaming is utilizing a WebSocket connection to receive the gaze and
eye/event data. The flow of information is handled by a set of custom React hooks

responsible for subscribing to the data streams, parsing the incoming JSON payloads, and
managing the state for each data type. This approach cleans up and isolates the logic, keeping
the UI components focused on the data presentation.

5.5 Communication overview
It should now be more clear some of the specifics of how each functionality works or

system to function properly is there utilized various approaches including, HTTP requests,
HTTP continous/long polling, WebSockets and MJPEG streaming over HTTP, refer to Figure 36.
These are all handled by the backend as the central orchestrator managing neon connection,
translating data streams and handling frontend requests.
Figure 36: Frontend solution structure and overview
The methods described are used to create a cohesive and flexible solution that is functional,
however not all these approaches are equal in performance, flexibility and more. The HTTP
polling is essentially a way to morph ordinary web requests into a type of continuous connection
by doing repetitive requests. This is unlike WebSockets who simply establishes this tunnel
connection once and only sends out updates as needed. Websockets are more efficient with
many various optimizations and features but can have an initial bigger overhead to implement.
The reason for keeping the polling functionality was it being used as an initial approach and it
functionally still working. There also were some problems when trying to move more elements
over to WebSocket which resulted in keeping the original approach. However the polling
approach was also chosen in regards to handling potential connection or network problems and
problematic websocket connections, to ensure a simple and functional baseline method.
Further we have simple HTTP requests used whenever a new user action is initiated to send
commands or requests to the backend. The stateless nature is of course optimal for the type

5.7 Integration Challenges and Solutions:
During development, several challenges were encountered and resolved in one way or another.
Some through changing approach or mindset, trying other approaches or looking at the problem

Raspberry pi selfhosted network
Like mentioned in Section 5.6 did the raspberry pi not end up using it’s self/hosted network
during the field tests and final implementations. This was due to the neon/device phone having
trouble communicating on the network. Other network communication on non neon/phone when
pinged and other stuff seemingly worked fine when on the RPI’s network, but the neon phone
didn’t want to play nice here.
There were done three different attempts at fixing this problem. 1. was fixing it or try
another linux approach. The other approach was to try and create the network not using
network manager, the default network managing service on the RPI. This was attempted with
Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol (DHCP) and daemon alongside some other linux specific
functionality and commands. This is done in a range of older tutorials for the RPI but in the
end didn’t work out very well and was eventually scraped. There were then looked into to try
and change some of the settings but this didn’t change much or work. A final of the bigger
hypothesizes tested were the problem being lack of internet access. But an Ethernet connection
didn’t change anything, other than allow web/browsing online of course.

with the network configuration, but it was a meaningful and interesting learning experience.
Video Stream Approach
To handle the video streaming of both eye and scene videos did two primary problems occur. The
first was simply how to handle the high amounts of 200hz data which was solved by reducing
data rate for the frontend solution, as user needs didn’t require this high eye/camera frequency.
The second element was simply how to transfer the video data from backend to frontend. There
were tested and played around with HTTP Live Streaming (HLS), WebRTC, RTSP and WebSocket.
In the end the choice were MJPEG because of it’s flexibility and support on most devices.
Websocket quickly were discarded as a contender as to not disrupt or add more complexity to
existing websocket data and websocket simply not being intended for video data specifically.
WebRTC seemed quite promising in it’s real/time video peer to peer functionality. But in regards
to it’s setup complexity and intended use/case for peer to peer communication like Zoom or
other similar programs were decided not to be used as it didn’t seem to fit as well. RTSP is
already supported and implemented in neons own neon/monitor solution [65] and were played
around with but seemed a bit more complex and problematic to implement.
Lastly were HLS considered and tested with seemingly fine results. HLS allows for adaptive
data rates and is used widely across both video and streaming services. The approach works
by breaking down contents into smaller segments that are downloaded or played in sequence.

However, despite some of it’s benefits, MJPEG ultimately provided the best balance of simplicity,
compatibility, and performance for the requirements of this eye/tracking video streaming
application. The MJPEG implementation proved effective for delivering both eye and scene
camera feeds to the frontend with minimal latency while maintaining broad device compatibility.
State management
A further challenge was managing state across the system which is composed of the three
distinct parts: the Neon glasses, the backend server, and the frontend client. Each component is
the “source of truth” for different information, the glasses for the raw sensor data, the backend
for the session state, and the frontend for the user interface state.
The core problem is mostly ensuring that the frontend’s view of the system is accurate and
representative of the backend’s and sensor’s true state. For example, if a user clicks “Start
Recording,” the UI should not update until it receives confirmation from the backend that the
command was successfully relayed to the Neon device and etc.
Initial implementations could have problems with UI might update optimistically before the
backend had confirmed an action, leading to a desynchronized state. The solutions for these
two was simply ensure only updating based on successful data transfer. Also only sending out
fragmented data updates for changed data, UI design instead of the whole data each time gave
problems. This is in the case of missing the update it won’t be resend and therefore is lots on
the frontend. The solution is just sending all the data as the small optimization of sending only
the updates for less frequent data updates didn’t make sense and gave bigger overhead.

through various means. There were of course also many other problems and challenges

connection stability, data synchronization, responsive UI design, error handling and typing
across solutions. But the ones mentioned here encapsulates some of the bigger or more

6.4 Usability Evaluation
With performance and general functionality testing covering overall system validation, is there
also done some usability evaluation to ensure it’s actually understandable to users. This has
been conducted alongside the primary intended users, the research nurses, involved in the
parallel pediatric studies alongside their testing and data collection. The test consists of two
parts, a set of 10 questions to evaluate the system more qualitatively and the standardized
System Usability Scale (SUS) to capture a more quantitative measure of usability. Please refer
to Section A.6 for actual results and answers to questions.
System Usability Scale (SUS)
The SUS test is 10 questions where the users states if they agree with a given statement. These
are both positive and negative and with a specific calculation ends in a final score between
0/100. The two users doing the test had given a score of 75 and 45, which results in an average
and final score of 60. According to various sources, also in healthcare settings [80], is an
average score 68 , and an acceptable but with room for improvement score of 51/68.
In general these results aligns with the amount the system was used by each respective nurse
and also the fact that one of the nurses had an aforementioned technical problem during
one sessions, Section 6.3. Also not both nurses had been given equal or the same amount of
introduction, in parts because of “rush” at certain field tests. And lastly a general “feel” from the
nurses in regards to self/proclaimed tech/savviness.
The primary outliers were in regards to confidence in using the system and requirements or
want for external help. This will be expanded in the qualitative parts but it’s a clear indicator
that the testing and usage of the system should have included a better introduction to it.
Qualitative Feedback
The questionnaire consisted of another 10 questions directed at getting feedback on using the
solution. It provided deeper insights into the “why” behind the SUS scores and more specifics
not captured otherwise. It was done orally and as objectively as possible, answers found in
Section A.6. The key themes that emerged from these questions and discussions are as follows.
1. Workflow integration friction: digital vs. analog pen and paper
2. The “digital barrier” with phones during patient interaction
3. User interface and interaction improvements
4. System speed and reliability
These already align very much, like expected, with the problems and experiences found during
the field tests, Section 6.3. But it were with the these questions the problems could be a little
more expanded upon and there were added some further insights.
The key takeaway from workflow integration was a combination between the digital patient
barrier, the speed of which events could be documented and at what level of detail. Having a
habit or current routine of being fairly descriptive meant the quick events quickly fell short.

Results summary
The results from the testing helps validate the prototype as a promising and functional

validation during field tests at location of Herlev Hospitals PED to thoroughly verify reliability.

Ultimately, the research demonstrated that while the technical implementation was successful.

the digital barrier to patient interaction, possibly improved input methods for detailed events,
and ensuring flawless system reliability to build and maintain usage trust. A list or depiction in
regards to requirements completion can be found in Section A.2.5.

7 Discussion
Analysis / Evaluation / Reflections
This project originally set out to investigate the feasibility and aspects of a centralized data
platform and areas surrounding it. This led to the development and testing of a solution to the
pediatric environment for monitoring of data related to anxiety in the PED. The outcome was a
final field deployable prototype that could potentially help and be a part of parallel studies data
collection for anxiety in pediatric patients. The findings from this process gives insights into
the technical viability, practical implications and potential of such a tool in similar research. This
section will evaluate these outcomes against the original goals, contextualize it and describe
limitations and future directions of the work.
7.1 Evaluation of Research Aims and Outcomes
The primary objective of this thesis was the creation of a system with focus on improving data
aggregation and accessibility for specifically anxiety research and measurements. The goal
quite early also wanted to look more into the registration of anxiety but this was quickly less
centralized. Focus was shifted more towards to the data gathering, neon glasses integration
and other related goals. These choices meant narrowing down the core focus to a clear and
concise area to ensure a proper and well defined project not grasping over more than what’s
achievable. Further is the wants for defining and creating design choices for a fully integrated
hospital system and field deployable prototype to envelop a complete and fulfilling system.
This has been achieved to a satisfactory manner on many accounts through the prototype
deployed on the Raspberry Pi. The system is capable of integrating directly with the neon eye/
tracking glasses, operate and be utilized directly inside the environment of Herlev Hospitals
PED. This successful deployment demonstrates setups that are less restrictive, non/invasive
and not bound to using a computer or other more lab/based procedures.
Another key part of the solution is the potential for improved quality during data collection and
enhanced approach. Providing remote control over recording sessions and implementing real/
time event registration showcased a way to reduce invasiveness during procedures, Section 6.3.
This with the event registration integrating directly into the recordings gives the possibility for
less laborious effort on data alignment later and more aligned or uniform event descriptions
with the use of the system.
The prototype also set out to allow monitoring in addition to the data collection. The recording
interface was tailored the research nurses needs and the parallel studies, while the monitoring
interface where to showcase live data visualization. This was achieved through the live
visualization of gaze and eye/event data serving as proof/of/concept for clinical or similar
application. This specifically showcases the potential and concept for a dual or multi/purpose
platform or solution by demonstrating this accessibility to the data alongside the recording
functionality.

7.2 Scientific Context
The findings of this project is meant to contribute the research and conversation of technology/
driven solutions in the pediatric environment. There is a wide range of studies in the area varying
in approach, technologies and age groups. Central for all is how they underscore the importance
and value of improving and getting a better understanding of the subject. Some of the examples
is ones such as Goel et al. (2024) that highlights the physical environment on mitigating anxiety,
which I also myself could both feel and see in Herlevs PED. However they also highlight the
difficulty in capturing corresponding physiological changes and measurements where a tool like
the one made in this thesis can demonstrate ways to improve this.
The solution or work done further aligns with some of the trends or work done by others like
the embodied technologies trends found by Li et al. (2023) literature review. Other solutions in
similar but different areas with VR and biofeedback games like Savaş et al. (2024) showcases
the interactive aspects where this specific solution on the other hand touches less on this part.
But a relation on this could be achieved with integration of such a biofeedback system in either
a lighter or more combined manner. An integrated platform or solution capable of handling such
systems or technologies could help manage, compare and increase accessibility to the data and
hopefully thereby increase overall value of the work.
Lastly to quickly mention is in regards to eye/tracking as primary data source. Much literature
supports such work and the value of it like Nozadi et al. (2023) and Piechaczek et al. (2022)
at least in more controlled and defined settings for assessing attention or emotional regulation
for children or adolescents as some examples. So moving the technology out of the lab and into
more active and dynamic environments can help test and showcase a potential for more real/
world data gathering and if its matureness is there yet.
7.3 limitations
Despite some successful outcomes, is it also important to acknowledge and point out the
limitations and setbacks of the project. A technical perspective is in regards to the processing
power of a Raspberry Pi. The RPI was sufficient on the simpler features and most of the
current scope but also showcased limitations for larger volumes of data and network handling.
Management of a more multimodal device or more functionality would require offloading
responsibilities to a more central solution. Further in this area was the aspects of errors
during field testing Section 6.3. The current implementation is limited to the information and
integration the eye/tracking glasses offer. For this limitation is certain errors or information not
accessible and thereby reduces flexibility and reliability of such a solution. So a solution would
always be limited by what the integrated or connected hardware/systems can offer.
Something more practical and more approachable is in regards to the projects methodology
or process. The project in itself had setbacks like other projects which affected approach and
development, but despite these would still contain other limitations. The field tests, while
extremely valuable, were done on a smaller scale with feedback primarily consisting of two
research nurses. A larger study would be needed to generalize these findings more and give a
more complete and correct image. Further was the interesting aspect of “technoference” during
the field tests and digital barrier the phone could create during patient interactions. This was
considered and thought about in regards to the possibility of an iPad or other types of devices.
But despite time, money and feasibility were it still very interesting to see this want or need of

8 Conclusion
This thesis were developed with a set of goals and objectives surrounding the challenges
of anxiety detection and reduction in the pediatric environment. These were in regards to
feasibility of improving existing data collection methods, creating a platform for data gathering
and monitoring all while having a focus on eye/tracking. The last addition was the potential for
further biometric technologies to be integrated like wearables into the final solution.
This has been achieved successfully through the development of a functional and field/
deployable prototype running on a Raspberry Pi. This solution is capable of being setup in a
quick and efficient manner in a pediatric patient room at the PED and integrate into eye/
tracking glasses. This integration allows for recording and event management to improve data
gathering quality and reduce invasiveness during procedures. The solution has demonstrated
improvements to aspects of manual methods with potential for future refining and adjustments
to even better align with user needs.
The prototype further expands on these aspects with it’s dual/purpose system capabilities
through it’s proof/of/concept monitoring functionality. Hereby supplementarily allowing
insights into eye/tracking real/time data visualizations alongside video streams of eye and scene
cameras. Unfortunately were other biometric technologies or tools not successfully integrated
but still tested and considered in regards to final implementation architecture and structure.
The solution has through field testing showcased it’s applicability in a real/world scenario
and the potential of similar technologies to be integrated or deployed in similar structures or
manners. In doing so the tool contributes a tangible way for on/site testing, remote access
and proof of capability outside a controlled or lab constricted environment. There is however
still many avenues to improve and explore further for the solution. These include addition for
multi/modality, new functionality for analysis of data and the monumental task of integration or
validation for a full hospital integration or system with it’s many regulations and requirements.
This area is a topic most can relate to, as anxiety in general and visiting a hospital both are
universally a part of everyone’s life. It’s therefore also logical why understanding these feelings,
especially for pediatric patients who can have a tougher time at unknown environments, can
bring value and benefit. So solutions like this and research and work in this area has importance
based on this simple logic. It’s through this continuos work that existing and varying approaches
continues to propel forward the current knowledge and tools available to allow for reaping even
greater benefits down the line. All to improve and help what is there today.

A.1 Literature Review
Literature review to find relevant literature and studies in regards to anxiety detection and
reduction in children’s emergency care departments. Focused on systems and technologies.
Refer to Section 2 and Section 2 for further details. The process has been depicted in Figure 47
and described in steps and more in/depth details below.
A.1.1 Research methodology and search strategy
This literature review was done in the period from 27th January to 1st April in correlation with
this masters thesis. The method has been primarily inspired by PRISMA [37] guidelines.
The primary approach has been to utilize a well defined search query within the “DTU Findit”
database [81]. The DTU Findit database has an extensive coverage and access to research
papers and journals from a large collection of academic institutions over the world.
A more complete, comprehensive and widely covered systematic review would also ensure more
sub queries and extra extensions like more databases. This has been excluded to maximize the
value of the review of the current articles found. Further literature or academic content have
been discovered or found via general desk research outside of this review.
A.1.2 Identification
The search query has been centered around the topic of anxiety and detecting it, in the context
of hospitals and in regards to children. This has further been adjusted to focus more on the
planed tools and technologies for the project area. Refer to Table 12 for final search string.
Anxiety AND (detect* OR register OR registration OR asses* OR measur* OR monitor)
AND (pediat* OR hospital* OR “PED” OR “Pediatric emergency department”)
AND (Child* OR kid* OR “pediatric patient”)
AND ((NOT “Trait anxiety”) OR (NOT Trait) OR (NOT disorder))
AND ( (“eye/tracking” OR “eye tracking” OR “biometric markers” OR “gaze tracking” OR “eye movement analysis”
OR “eye monitoring” OR “eye motion” OR “ocular tracking” OR “visual tracking”)
OR (“wearable” OR “sensor” OR “biometric” OR “physiological signal” OR “skin conductance” OR “heart rate
variability”))
Table 12: Final Literature Review Search String
A.1.3 Screening and Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria
Initial filtering is based on excluding articles older than 5 years to ensure relevance and
currentness. Also articles not accessible online or requiring purchase from external supplier were
excluded from the initial collection of papers.
After the initial rough filtering have the papers been manually gone over via reviewing abstracts
and titles. Then afterwards moved onto more detailed review.

Persona 1: Dedicated Nurse Researcher
Name
Signe Jensen
Role
Researcher and nurse
Collaborating with Herlev Hospital PED and
DTU. Not tech/savvy or familiar with software
development.
Background
Signe is running a study on anxiety in children at the PED. She has experience
with various research methodologies and data collection tools but many of
these can be cumbersome or disruptive in a busy clinical environment.
Goals related
to system:
• To reliably collect eye/tracking data from children.
• To time/stamp specific events during the recording sessions.
• To easily manage recording sessions with minimal technical fuss.
• To have a straightforward way to access or note down logged event data
associated with each recording for later analysis alongside the eye/tracking
data.
Needs from
system
• Simplicity & Speed: “I need to set up and start a recording efficiently. The
PED is unpredictable, and I can’t spend twenty minutes troubleshooting.”
• Reliability: “The data capture should be dependable. Losing a session’s data
due to something going wrong is a unwanted.”
• Ease of Event Logging: “Marking events should be simple. I need predefined
buttons for common events and a quick way to type a unique note.”
• Real/time Feedback: “I need to see that the glasses are connected and
recording, and preferably a sense of what the scene camera is capturing.”
• Portability: “The whole setup should be easily transportable.”
Potential
Pain Points
• Complex setup procedures for eye/tracking equipment.
• Difficulty synchronizing manually noted events with recorded data.
• Technical glitches interrupting data collection.
• Cumbersome interfaces that are hard to use.

1. Introduction
The following Privacy Policy describes how this research project “Improving Anxiety Detection
and Reduction in Children’s Emergency Care Waiting Rooms” collects, use and store personal
data. The project is done in collaboration with DTU and Herlev Hospital. This is done to ensure
proper privacy for participants in accordance with GDPR regulations and danish law.
2. Data Controller
The data controller for this research project is:
• Institution: Technical University of Denmark (DTU)
• Address: Anker Engelunds Vej 1, 2800 Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark
• Contact: For questions regarding this research and data processing, please contact Tobias
Olrik Birck Kristensen, at s195458@student.dtu.dk.
3. Data Collection and Purpose
The purpose of data collection is to conduct research on the detection and reduction of anxiety
in pediatric patients. The data is to be used exclusively for research.
The following types of pseudonymized data is collected:
• Biometric and Eye-Tracking Data: This includes pupil dilation, gaze direction, fixation
duration, saccades, and blink patterns collected via Neon eye/tracking glasses.
• Video Data: Video streams from the eye/tracking glasses’ world view and eye cameras
is recorded.
• Event Data: Timestamps of specific events that occur during the study, as logged by the
research nurse (e.g., “Doctor arrives”).
Data is pseudonymized. No directly identifiable personal information (such as names or personal
identification numbers) is stored with the research data.
4. Legal Basis for Processing
Processing of data for the research is done with explicit consent provided by particpants and
parents or guardians. Consent can be withdrawn without stating reason.
5. Data Security and Storage
The implemented solution and is to be designed and done in a secure and technically safe
manner. This includes proper data encryption, access control, network configuration and data
storage. All these being either done locally or isolated.
6. Data Retention and Sharing
Data stored in regards to this study is stored only for duration necessary to do the project.
Remaining data is handled by DTU and ongoing research projects. There is no third/party data
sharing and the data is only accessible to DTU and involved staff at Herlev Hospital.

SUS Score Calculation Method
The System Usability Scale (SUS) uses a standardized scoring method to convert responses into
a score between 0 and 100:
1. For odd/numbered questions (1, 3, 5, 7, 9): Subtract 1 from the user’s response
2. For even/numbered questions (2, 4, 6, 8, 10): Subtract the user’s response from 5
3. Sum all the adjusted scores from steps 1 and 2
4. Multiply the total by 2.5 to get the final SUS score

A.9.1 Raspberry pi machine setup  Linux machine
This guide is based around a Raspberry Pi Compute Module 5 and Raspberry Pi
Development Kit for Compute Module 5, Figure 38. This differs to an ordinary raspberry 5 in
certain aspects. The compute module 5 utilizes an IO board for USB,MicroSD, M.2/SSD and other.
Please refer to raspberry pi’s official documentation for most up to date instructions and tools.
Documentation: www.raspberrypi.com/documentation/
Tutorials: www.raspberrypi.com/tutorials/
Rasperry Pi CM5 devkit unboxing + setup~: youtu.be/iq_PMx70CTU/
A.9.1.1 Installing OS
An ordinary raspberry pi utilizes aMicroSD card to store the operating system of the raspberry
pi. ThisMicroSD card can be flashed with the OS or have it installed via network or one of the
other approaches.
The Compute Module 5 can be instantiated in a few ways not including theMicroSD card
approach according to personal tests and research, I’m unsure if it’s possible to store the OS
after instantiating the Pi in one of these other ways. The ways to do this instead is via:
• usbboot
• USB
• SSD
Flashing an image to a compute module can be found descriped in the following raspberry pi
documentation. usbboot: www.raspberrypi.com/documentation. Important note, a screwdriver
connecting the two shown pins or a jumper is needed to disable eMMC boot, Figure 62, .
Figure 62: Jumper location on raspberry pi compute module 5, IO board
USB/boot generally requires a Compute with onboard embedded MultiMedia Card (eMMC)
storage, the eMMC flash storage comes in 0GB (Lite), 16/64GB solutions.
Alternativelly is installing the OS on a USB stick or an SSD and then install either of
these into the IO board. Note: installing the OS on a USB means running the OS on that
specific usb, alternatively move the installation to another place afterwards like SSD. Refer to
Section A.9.1.1.1 for details on using Raspberry Pi Imager

A.9.1.2 Remote access
There are three primary ways to connect to a raspberry pi remotely
• SSH
• VNC Virtual Network Computing. cross-platform screen sharing system
• Raspberry Pi Connect Raspberry pi hosted solution to access device from anywhere
Ensure to enable the specific options in on device configurations.

A.9.1.3 WiFi and local hotspot and internet settings
Ensure when installing the antenna for the module 5 is connected to compute module on the
board and the case has the antenna attached.
There are two approaches to doing network management on the rapsberry pi.
• DHCP daemon
• Network Manager
The initial approach tried to use DHCP but the newest version of raspberry PI can and uses
Network manager. It’s possibly to use either the CLI, NMCLI or the build in GUI to configure this.

npm install

bash

2
python main.py
1
cd frontend

1
sudo ./setup.sh
bash
