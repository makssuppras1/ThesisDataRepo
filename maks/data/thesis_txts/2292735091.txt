Abstract 
Nitritation, i.e. the conversion of ammonia to nitrite, conducted by ammonia oxidizing bacteria 
(AOB) presents a shortcut in conventional nitrogen removal from wastewater. Its merits entail 
lower aeration demand compared to conventional treatment and an influent stream for anaerobic 
ammonium oxidation (Anammox). However, AOB are reported as a main producer in wastewater 
treatment of the undesired nitrous oxide (N2O). In a previous study N2O production was lower in a 
sequentially fed Sequence Batch Reactor (SBR) than in a sequentially aerated SBR. 
The aim of this thesis was to further investigate the effects of sequential feeding in SBRs on 
nitritation performance and N2O production. The process was monitored by nitrogen conversion 
performance (ammonium removal efficiency, nitritation, and nitratation), community composition 
of AOB and nitrite oxidizing bacteria (NOB), and N2O production.  
Two SBRs (NG1 and NG2) were operated with sequential feeding for 4 months. The ammonium 
removal efficiency was 82% ± 14% (NG1) and 86% ± 11% (NG2) during the operational period.  
Halfway into the operational period the ammonium loading rate (ALR) was increased 2-fold in line 
with the air flow rate, which increased nitritation efficiency, reaching 85% ± 24% (NG1), 77% ± 
17% (NG2). The bacterial community composition changed from initial activated sludge with a low 
AOB abundance to a composition where AOB practically comprised the entire bacterial 
community. N2O production decreased, from 0.64% ± 0.16% ΔN2Oprod/AOR (NG1), 0.97% ± 
0.25% ΔN2Oprod/AOR (NG2) to 0.38% ± 0.23% ΔN2Oprod/AOR (NG1), 0.30% ± 0.080 % 
ΔN2Oprod/AOR (NG2) despite the increased abundance of AOB suggesting microbial adaption to 
the condition (high NO2- concentration and limited DO). The sequential feeding and the SBR 
configuration resulted in a specific N2O pattern, in which N2O production increased during feeding, 
due to increased pH.

1 Introduction. 
Anthropogenic nitrogen pollution has great impact on the coastal areas and is greatest in coastal 
waters where agricultural activity and urbanization is strongest (Howarth & Marino 2006). 
Increasing anthropogenic nutrient input, including nitrogen, to aquatic environments has vast 
consequences, as nitrogen in the marine environments often is the main limited nutrient source for 
algae growth, and can thus result in eutrophication potentially leading to large algae blooms and 
oxygen depletion. This has large social and economic impact especially on fish and shellfish 
production, and on the recreational value of coastal waters (Howarth & Marino 2006). Reduction of 
nutrients including nitrogen from urban- and industrial life is essential and necessitates wastewater 
treatment (WWT). Wastewater treatment plants (WWTP) play an important role in removal of 
nutrients that would otherwise lead to eutrophication of coastal waters. The nutrients’ impact on the 
water environment has led to a stricter regulation of discharge. According to Danish national laws, 
total nitrogen in the effluent must not exceed 8 mg/l (BEK nr 1448, 11/12/2007, § 17). Nonetheless, 
on a wider geographic scale large amounts of nitrogen is discharged to the surrounding waters 
despite WWT. The point source, which includes wastewater treatment plants accounts for at least 
25% of the nitrogen, discharged in Europe (Donnelly et al. 2013) (Figure 1).

Nitrogen removal before discharge to the marine environment is mainly conducted by conventional 
nitrogen removal in which ammonium entering the WWTP is sequentially converted; first through 
nitrification to nitrite and nitrate followed by denitrification to dinitrogen gas (N2). However, 
innovative approaches are emerging, in which cost and environmental impact reductions in form of 
energy and space savings are prioritized. One of these innovation approaches involves a shortcut in 
the regular nitrification/denitrification steps – and is referred to as partial nitrification or nitritation. 
Ammonia oxidizing bacteria (AOB) oxidizes ammonium to nitrite, which can be denitrified or be 
used as influent for an anaerobic ammonium oxidation process (anammox). Nitritation is thus a 
desired process. One of the engineered systems where nitritation has successfully been operated are 
sequencing batch reactors (SBR). These impose dynamic changes in operation as all processes are 
contained in one reactor under pseudo batch conditions. High AOB activity is requires while nitrite 
oxidizing bacteria (NOB) should be inhibited. Promotion of AOB and inhibition of NOB demands 
control strategies involving limiting oxygen availability while sustaining excess ammonium. This 
leads to reactor conditions with low dissolved oxygen (DO) concentration and high nitrite 
concentrations. However, under these conditions AOB are found to produce nitrous oxide (N2O) 
which is a 300 times more potent greenhouse gas than CO2. This compromises the environmental 
sustainability of the system. N2O emission from WWTP has previously been underestimated and is 
a newer concern, why mitigation strategies to minimize emissions of N2O are in its infancy. 
Investigation in this field has greatly increased, and as knowledge increases, new gaps are revealed. 
This thesis among other things endeavours to investigate some of these gaps.

1.1 Aim and objectives 
This thesis is based upon a previous master’s thesis (Mavrovas 2014) in which a sequential aeration 
SBR and a sequential feeding SBR were operated and compared. The findings in that study revealed 
that lower N2O emissions were seen in the reactor operated with sequential feeding while higher 
N2O emissions were found in the sequential aeration regime. Therefore, sequential feeding was 
chosen for further investigations.  
The aim of the thesis is to investigate the effects of sequential feeding in an SBR on partial 
nitrification performance, microbial community and nitrous oxide production. 
The objectives of this project are to: 
 Operate to SBR reactors as replicates to achieve stable nitritation performance, in which the 
nitrite accumulated equals the ammonium removed.  
 Evaluate which operational parameters are most influential on the nitritation performance. 
Investigate changes in the ammonium oxidizing bacterial (AOB) and nitrite oxidizing 
bacterial microbial communities. 
 Relate changes in the AOB and NOB communities to the nitrogen conversion performance. 
 Monitor nitrous oxide production during the operation and compare if the production 
changes over the operational period. 
 Investigate the nitrous oxide emission pattern during one cycle, and relate the operational 
conditions’ effect on the nitrous oxide production pattern. 
 
In the work done by Mavrovas (2014) granulation was a major focal point. In the present project, 
particle size is monitored to register changes, but no further investigations or strategies to enhance 
granulation are made in the project.

2 Literature review 
2.1 Conventional nitrogen removal 
The nitrogen entering WWTP from municipal wastewater is mainly in the form of ammonium 
(NH4+) (Henze 2008). The preferred end-product in the removal of ammonium in wastewater 
treatment is dinitrogen gas (N2) as this is a nonreactive gas and the main compound in the 
atmosphere. The nitrogen cycle has been driven by microorganisms through the last ~2.7 billion 
years (Canfield et al. 2010) and WWTP take advantage of these nitrogen removing microorganisms. 
Biological nutrient removal (BNR) systems are the most widespread operations of conventional 
nitrogen removal (van Loosdrecht 2008; Claros et al. 2013). Briefly, BNR consist of a two-step 
process – Nitrification followed by denitrification. In nitrification ammonia (NH3) is oxidized to 
nitrite (NO2-) which is further oxidized to nitrate (NO3-). Denitrification follows with a sequential 
reduction of nitrate to N2.  
Chemolithoautotrophic organisms are the main organisms to mediate nitrification. They obtain 
energy from metabolic oxidation of nitrogen-species (as inorganic compound) and use carbon 
dioxide as their carbon source (Ekama & Wentzel 2008; Sinha & Annachhatre 2007). Nitrification 
is stimulated through sufficient aeration and alkalinity. Denitrification is mediated by anaerobic 
heterotrophic microorganisms. Denitrification is therefore stimulated by non-aerated bioreactors 
and addition of organic carbon as electron donors (Chandran et al. 2011).  
 
In recent years, attention has been drawn to cost reduction in WWT, especially in the form of 
reduction of energy and space needed. In addition, awareness of ecological footprints such as 
greenhouse gas emissions is increasing. In wastewater treatment those emissions can be indirect, 
from the carbon dioxide equivalents of energy production, or direct, from emissions of methane 
(CH4) as a consequence of anaerobic sludge digestion and nitrous oxide (N2O) from nitrogen 
removal, and mainly during nitrification (Rodriguez-Caballero et al. 2014; Mutlu 2015). 
Energy reductions can lead to financial advantages as well as a lower carbon footprint (Sinha & 
Annachhatre 2007; De Kreuk & De Bruin 2004). This has boosted the development of new 
concepts in microbial treatment processes for nitrogen removal with a focus on cost efficiency and 
high performance processes, and present-day wastewater treatment practices could to be improved 
through the introduction of new microbial treatment techniques (Sinha & Annachhatre 2007).

2.2 Innovative nitrogen removal 
One such improved biological nitrogen removal process, which can fulfil cost savings, is complete 
autotrophic nitrogen removal (CANR). It is a process which uses a shortcut in the nitrogen cycle 
compared to conventional biological nitrogen removal (BNR) (Domingo-Félez et al. 2014; Mutlu et 
al. 2013).  
The basis of CANR builds on partial nitrification, also called nitritation. Nitritation is mediated by 
ammonia oxidizing prokaryotes (AOP), which through metabolic oxidation converts ammonium 
(NH3) to nitrite (NO2-) by the reduction of oxygen (O2). The produced nitrite (electron acceptor) can 
be utilized by anammox bacteria (AnAOB) in the anaerobic oxidation of ammonium to N2 and this 
process is referred to as anammox (Mutlu et al. 2013; Winkler et al. 2011; Gilbert et al. 2013). The 
second step in nitrification is thus not necessary and the additional aeration used for the oxidation of 
nitrite to nitrate can be saved. In addition, the denitrification step is removed with the consequence 
that the additional organic carbon source needed for heterotrophic denitrifiers is dispensable. This 
makes it a most desired reaction and anammox has therefore been implemented in full scale 
wastewater treatment systems with success, especially in systems with high ammonium loading and 
low organic carbon loadings such as side stream processes (Sinha & Annachhatre 2007; Kotay et al. 
2013). 
The nitrite source needed for anammox can be produced from nitritation in a separate tank to be 
continuously fed to the non-aerated anammox reactor (Winkler et al. 2012; Kotay et al. 2013). 
Partial nitrification is thus very important to control in order to have a substrate for the anammox 
process. The challenge is to keep the second step of nitrification from occurring, as this requires the 
inhibition of nitrite oxidizing bacteria (NOB) (Xu et al. 2012). 
The merits of nitritation include decreased energy consumption obtained through the lower aeration 
necessity and its use as substrate for the anammox process. This makes it a desired system from a 
sustainability perspective.  
 
2.3 Process description of nitrification  
The main metabolic pathway in nitrification is a serial two-step biological process. In the first step 
ammonia is oxidized to nitrite, followed by the second step, in which nitrite is further oxidized to 
nitrate (cf. Figure 2) (Vázquez-Padín et al. 2010).  
The two steps in nitrification are driven by different guilds of microorganisms. Ammonia oxidizing 
prokaryotes responsible for nitritation consist of three groups: Ammonia oxidizing bacteria (AOB),

heterotrophic ammonia oxidizers, and ammonia oxidizing archaea (AOA). Since no clear evidence 
has been found to suggest AOA or heterotrophic ammonia oxidizers being of significant importance 
in conventional activated sludge processes (Kampschreur et al. 2009) only AOB will hereafter be 
mentioned of the ammonia oxidizing prokaryotes.

The nitrification process requires a significant amount of oxygen per mole of ammonium removed, 
while the biomass yield is low and alkalinity is reduced by the production of hydrogen ions (Figure 
2B) (Wiesmann 1994; Sinha & Annachhatre 2007). In addition to generating energy from ammonia 
oxidation, ammonia is assimilated by the cell. Furthermore, the fixation of CO2 uses 80% of the 
energy generated via substrate oxidation. As a result, the growth yield of nitrifiers is low (Sinha & 
Annachhatre 2007). 
2.3.1 Microorganisms responsible for Nitritation - Ammonia oxidizing bacteria  
Ammonia oxidizing bacteria convert free ammonia (NH3) and not the ionized form as the unionized 
form is easier to transport across the membrane (Sayavedra-soto & Arp 2011). Genomes of AOB 
from four different ecotype environments (freshwater sediments, sewage/wastewater, soils, and 
marine environments) all encoded four specialized proteins to perform oxidation of NH3: Ammonia 
monooxygenase (AMO), hydroxylamine oxidoreductase (HAO), cytochrome c554 and cm552. The 
genes encoding these proteins (amo, hao, cycA, cycB) are present in nearly identical multiple 
copies, although in different number and chromosome location in Betaproteobacteria AOB 
(Sayavedra-soto & Arp 2011). In the well studied N. europaea, 2 very similar copies of the amo 
operon have been reported by Chain and co-workers (2003). 2-3 complete amo operon copies were 
reported to be found in the genera Nitrosomonas sp. and Nitrosospira sp (Norton et al. 2002). 
The membrane bound enzyme ammonia monooxygenase (AMO) catalyses the oxidation of NH3 to 
hydroxylamine (NH2OH). Oxidation of NH2OH to NO2- follows and is catalysed by the periplasmic 
enzyme hydroxylamine oxidoreductase (HAO), (Figure 2). This final oxidation to NO2- releases 
==PAGE:19==
9 
 
four electrons that are channelled first through the periplasmic cytochrome c554 and thereafter 
through the membrane bound cytochrome cm552 to the ubiquinone pool, in which cm552 serves as a 
quinone reductase. From there two electrons are used for further oxidation by AMO, while the other 
two electrons proceed to electron transport processes (Sayavedra-soto & Arp 2011; Wong et al. 
2003). 
The AOB genera mainly found in WWTP are Nitrosomonas, Nitrosospira, Nitrosovibrio and 
Nitrosococcus (Wong et al. 2003), with Nitrosomonas-like and Nitrosospira-like populations as the 
most dominant AOB populations in wastewater treatment systems (Kowalchuk & Stephen 2001). 
In a study by Bin et al. (2011), the authors showed that there was a major change in the microbial 
community structure using conventional activated sludge as seed sludge for the enrichment of 
aerobic nitrifying granules. The AOB community at the end of the experiment showed only 40% 
similarity to the initial AOB community in the inoculum.  
 
2.3.2 Microorganisms responsible for Nitratation – Nitrite oxidizing bacteria 
Nitrite oxidizing abilities are found in morphologically and phylogenetically diverse lineages, and 
NOB species include Nitrobacter, Nitrospira and Nitrococcus (Wong et al. 2003; Starkenburg et al. 
2006; Starkenburg et al. 2011).  
In nature, there is a very tight link between AOBs and NOBs as they are almost always found in the 
near proximity of each other, and they are widely distributed in most environments.  
Nitrobacter has commonly been used as model organism in many pure culture studies. However, 
Nitrospira has recently been found to be very abundant and numerically dominant in activated 
sludge (Schramm et al. 1999). Nitrobacter is a K-strategist with high growth rate but low substrate 
affinity, whereas Nitrospira is an r-strategist with high substrate affinity, high oxygen affinity and 
low maximum growth rate (Nowka et al. 2015; Starkenburg et al. 2011). It is important to approach 
a NOB inhibition strategy by taking into account that they have different life strategies. Depending 
on which genera is mainly abundant in a given system, the inhibition strategy could vary. 
In WWTP lineages of NOB both Nitrobacter and Nitrospira sp. have been found that are not 
strictly chemolithoautotrophs but can also use simple organic compounds such as pyruvate, acetate 
and formate for mixotrophic growth (Daims et al. 2011).

2.4 Engineering Partial nitritation 
2.4.1 Sequencing batch reactors 
Wastewater treatment operations are highly engineered systems. Through process design and 
operational conditions, the nitritation process could be enhanced. Such a technique, which have  
been reported to successfully achieve nitritation are SBRs (Guo et al. 2010; Li et al. 2011; Vázquez-
Padín et al. 2010; Xu et al. 2012; López-Palau et al. 2011; López-Palau et al. 2013). 
One of the advantages of SBRs is that the whole biological treatment is contained in one reactor 
(Xu et al. 2012). The treatment is performed in cycles, which is divided into phases. One cycle 
consist of a fill phase, where the influent runs into the reactor (the rest of the cycle resembles batch 
conditions). Then follows the reaction phase, a settling phase to keep the biomass in the reactor, and 
a discharge phase where the effluent is removed (Qin, Liu, et al. 2004). This creates dynamically 
changing conditions for the microorganisms in the system. 
SBRs have been shown to reach better nitritation rates compared to continuous stirred tank reactors 
which contain less dynamic changes (Fux et al. 2003; Terada et al. 2013). 
In SBR systems, granulation has been shown to occur successfully (Qin et al. 2004; Liu & Tay 
2004; Liu 2006; Morgenroth et al. 1997). These are all properties of high value in the attempt to 
reduce space in WWTPs.  
2.4.2 Parameters influencing successful nitritation operation  
To archive nitritating conditions only, inhibition of NOB activity and selection of AOBs are 
important. Thermodynamically, ammonia oxidation is more favourable than nitrite oxidation 
(ΔGº´= -275 kJ/mol (AOB) versus -75 kJ/mol (NOB)), which result in a higher cell yield and a 
higher maximum growth rate of AOB (Mutlu 2015). However in natural environments, nitrite rarely 
accumulates because nitrite oxidation exceeds ammonia oxidation. This is probably due to a high 
substrate affinity of NOBs and a low Smin (minimum substrate concentration capable of supporting 
steady-state biomass) value and a high utilization rate (Sinha & Annachhatre 2007).  
Changing the rate-limiting step from ammonia oxidation to the nitrite oxidizing step involves the 
understanding of the different parameters that affect the growth rate of the two groups of nitrifiers. 
It is also important to keep in mind that when dealing with complex communities, generalizing one 
parameter’s effect on AOB growth over NOB growth can be too simplistic, due to variations 
between and within lineages and strains. Mutlu (2015) sums up interesting findings where variances 
within NOB and AOB overlap the variance between the two functional guilds. One example is that 
in some NOB the maximum growth rate (due to mixotrophic growth) reaches the same level as 
==PAGE:21==
11 
 
AOB maximum growth rate. Such variance overlaps were also described in studies investigating 
oxygen affinity both within and between AOB and NOB lineages. Differences in the biomass 
structure could explain the differences in the described results. For example could mass transfer 
limitations influence growth results and oxygen uptake, if comparing suspended cells to cell 
aggregates or granules (Mutlu 2015). The inconsistency reported about the parameters effect on 
AOB and NOB are important to keep in mind, when evaluation of important parameters for 
enhancing nitritation and inhibit nitratation are never the less attempted: 
1) 
Dissolved oxygen (DO) concentration is the very basis for the oxidation processes. 
Both AOB and NOB use oxygen as their electron acceptor. However, AOB seems to be more robust 
to low DO concentrations compared to NOB. Hanaki et al. (1990) showed that although NO2- 
production was reduced under low DO concentration, AOB dominated over NOB due to higher 
growth yield. Furthermore, they found that ammonia oxidation was more robust to lower DO 
concentrations than nitrite oxidation (Figure 3a) (Hanaki et al. 1990). Hence, under oxygen 
deficiency NOB activity is significantly more affected than AOB activity. It should be kept in mind 
that there can be differences between different strains in both AOB and NOB, as well as it has been 
found that NOB as well as AOB have been shown to acclimate to limited DO conditions (Park & 
Noguera 2004). Particularly when the solid retention time (SRT) is high, which gives time to 
manifest changes of different affinities (Mutlu 2015). Yet DO concentration is a key variable, which 
affects both AOB and NOB as either promoter or inhibitor, effecting selection of the biomass, as 
well as potentially drives nitrous oxide emissions. It is therefore an important parameter.  
2) 
pH is another important parameter that influences the activity of AOB and has a 
significant influence on the selection of AOB over NOB. As AOB utilize NH3 and NOB utilize 
HNO2, the main effect is associated with the chemical equilibriums of NH4+/NH3 (ammonium/free 
ammonia) and HNO2/NO2- (free nitrous acid/nitrite). Sinha & Annachhatre (2007) reviewed the 
literature on pH, free ammonia (FA) and free nitrous acid (FNA) and found pH to be a decisive 
parameter in NOB activity inhibition. Nitrite accumulation was best achieved at pH in the range of 
7.5-8.5.  
The fraction of available NH3 versus NH4+ is calculated from the pKa value of ammonium (Eq. 3) 
(Hvitved-Jacobsen et al. 2013) and depending on pH, the fraction of ammonia/ammonium varies. 
At pH 8 the fraction of ammonia/ammonium is 5.75 %, at pH 7.5 it is 1.82 % whereas at pH 6.9 it is 
0.46 %. This implies that at higher pH more substrate is available for the ammonia oxidizing 
microbial community.

Anthonisen and co-workers (1976) investigated different FNA and FA concentrations and their

model containing 4 zones each with different FA and FNA boundary ranges. Zone 1; at high pH and 
high FA concentration there is inhibition of AOB and NOB. Zone 2 and 3 are the normal

High free ammonia (FA) concentrations have been shown by Claros et al. (2010) not to be a

even though it is not due to inhibition of high FA concentrations, the activity of AOB is severely 
inhibited at high pH (>8.5) and more than at lower pH (<6) (Claros et al. 2013). 
In conclusion, the optimum pH to achieve high AOB activity and low NOB activity seems to be 
slightly different in the different studies, but common for all the studies is that optimal nitrite 
accumulation is around pH 7.5 – 8.5. In this range NOB are slightly inhibited by FA concentrations, 
ammonia substrate is readily available for AOB and there is not an inhibition from FNA on AOB.  
However, according to Sinha & Annachhatre (2007), several investigations have reported an 
acclimatization of NOB to high FA concentrations after long periods of exposure, which reversed a 
nitrite build-up. Such findings underline the importance of obtaining several operational parameters 
to select AOB over NOB in order to achieve long-term partial nitrification.   
3) 
Temperature in the range of 5-15° C, nitrite oxidizers have a higher specific growth 
rate than ammonium oxidizers. However, the reverse is true at temperatures above 15° C (Figure 
3c). In the SHARON process (Single reactor system for High activity Ammonium Removal Over 
Nitrite), high temperature (25-35° C) is used in order to achieve a maximum specific growth rate of 
AOB, which exceeds that of NOB. Under these conditions it is possible to reduce the solid retention 
time (SRT) enough to achieve NOB washout while AOB are kept in the system (Hellinga et al. 
1998; Blackburne et al. 2008).

select for AOB over NOB.

successful nitritation operations thus require multiple specific conditions, which necessitates close 
monitoring as a variety of factors can affect the stability and performance of the system.

2.5 Nitrous oxide production and emission from biological nitrogen removal 
Perhaps the most important challenge in achieving nitritation is that the optimal conditions for the 
process i.e. low DO and high concentrations of NO2, are also the conditions that can promote 
nitrous oxide (N2O) production (Figure 4) (Domingo-Félez et al. 2014; Kampschreur et al. 2009). 
Thus the merits of the system could be unfavourable for greenhouse gas emissions, as N2O is 300 
fold more potent than CO2 (Kampschreur et al. 2009).   
 
Figure 4: The main factors that lead to N2O emissions from a WWTP. N2O emissions can occur from the nitrification 
processes and denitrification processes. In a partial nitrification system it is the nitrification stage that is of importance. Low 
O2 and accumulation of nitrite have in particular been found to increase N2O emission. The figure implies the factors that can 
lead to low DO concentrations and high nitrite concentrations by arrows, yet these are also the main factors of importance in 
order to maintain for partial nitrification (Kampschreur et al. 2009). 
 
2.5.1 Nitrous oxide production from Ammonia oxidizing bacteria’s alternative metabolism. 
AOB are a major contributor to nitrous oxide emissions in WWTP (Schreiber et al. 2012; Law et al. 
2012). It is not always that AOB solely oxidizes ammonia to nitrite and alternative pathways occur, 
in which the end product is nitrous oxide instead of nitrite. There are generally two pathways from 
which AOB produce nitrous oxide (Figure 2). 
The first is called nitrifier denitrification. Here NO2- is reduced to nitric oxide (NO) catalysed by the 
enzyme NirK, which is followed by further reduction of NO to N2O through the membrane-bound 
enzyme cNOR (cytochrome c dependent nitric oxide reductase) (Figure 2). 
==PAGE:25==
15 
 
The second is hydroxylamine oxidation in which NH2OH is oxidized to NO catalysed by HAO, 
followed by an unidentified Nor, potentially c554, which reduces NO to N2O (Figure 2). 
Nitrifier denitrification and hydroxylamine oxidation produce N2O through different mechanisms 
despite the closely related enzymes in the two pathways. 
(Schreiber et al. 2012).  
It is in particular the changes between aerobic and anaerobic/anoxic conditions that induce N2O 
production. Chandran and co-workers (2011) examined the increase or reduction of ammonia 
conversion pathways during different conditions (Figure 2). In the transition from oxic to anoxic 
conditions all pathways are reduced with the exception of the reduction of NO2- to NO (carried out 
by NirK), yet NO is not further reduced to N2O. The proposed reason for the increase in NO 
production arise from the biochemistry of the pathways. When conditions turn anoxic there is 
presumably still excess reducing equivalents from the electrons channelled to the cytochrome pool 
in the step where hydroxylamine is oxidized to nitrite (cf. section 2.3.1) (Chandran et al. 2011; Yu 
et al. 2010).  
As conditions change from anoxic to oxic if excess ammonium concentrations are present, the 
specific substrate consumption rate of ammonia oxidation will be very high. The transient response 
will be a short period of NH2OH accumulation. However, it is proposed that to keep NH3 uptake 
rates high  and avoiding a self-inhibiting effect due to NH2OH accumulation, the hydroxylamine 
oxidation to NO and N2O has been found to increase (Chandran et al. 2011; Wunderlin et al. 2012). 
It is however believed that under aerobic conditions with low DO and high NO2- concentrations 
N2O production particularly originates from nitrifier denitrification. (Schreiber et al. 2012; 
Wunderlin et al. 2012). Yet in most studies, they are not able to discriminate between the nitrifier-
denitrification and hydroxylamine oxidation. 
Despite the extensive work that has been done, the exact enzymes, mechanisms, and environmental 
conditions still need further characterization and exploration to be fully understood (Kozlowski et 
al. 2014; Mutlu 2015). 
 
On a side note NOB have also been found to use other alternative pathways during non-optimal 
conditions  and expression of the enzyme (NirK) catalysing reduction of NO2- to NO has been 
observed (Ahlers et al. 1990; Schreiber et al. 2012). However, it is not believed that NOB are 
significantly contributing to N2O emissions (Law et al. 2012).

2.5.2 Process conditions triggering N2O emissions from SBR reactors 
Control strategies to minimize N2O emissions is a subject, which rather recently has gained interest. 
The effect of different aeration and feeding configurations on nitrous oxide emissions have been 
investigated by several authors in partial nitrification SBRs (Rodriguez-Caballero & Pijuan 2013; 
Law et al. 2011; Gabarró et al. 2014; Kong et al. 2013). 
A characteristic pattern was observed in all these studies: Nitrous oxide was produced during both 
aerated and non-aerated phases. As aeration was turned off accumulation of aqueous phase N2O 
was observed. This is presumably because minimal transfer from liquid to gas phase occurs due to 
the lack of stripping from aeration. Consequently, when aeration is turned on, the N2O accumulated 
in the liquid is stripped resulting in N2O gas emission peaks in the start of every aerated period 
following a non-aerated period. The accumulation of N2O in the liquid phase during the non aerated 
settling period accounted for up to 94 % of the N2O emitted in the first 15 minutes of the aerated 
period (Law et al. 2011). In line with these findings, Rodriguez-Caballero & Pijuan (2013) found 
that N2O emitted in the initial peak during the first 5 minutes of a cycle accounted for 80% of the 
total N2O emitted during a cycle.   
Introduction of anoxic periods (stripping with N2) after the settling period, also produced this initial 
N2O emission peak, substantiating the assumption that stripping is accountable for the initial N2O 
peak (Rodriguez-Caballero & Pijuan 2013). In the same study, introducing anoxic periods in 
between aerobic phases resulted in a much smaller N2O peak. NO was on the other hand greatly 
increased in such anoxic phases in line with the investigations by Chandran and co-workers (2011). 
 
pH and thus the available NH3 concentration has been found to have an impact on N2O production 
(Law et al. 2012). In this study by Law and co-workers (2012), N2O emissions increased with 
higher NH3 concentration until pH 8.5 where N2O production dropped. They speculated that the 
higher emissions were mainly governed by a higher ammonium oxidation rate (AOR). 
It was also found that increasing the length of the feeding period mitigated N2O emission, possibly 
because the lower alkalinity introduced to the system per time resulted in a lower pH change (Law 
et al. 2012). 
N2O emission per ammonium removed varies between studies., partial nitritating SBRs, emissions 
have been reported to be 0.8% in laboratory-scale experiments (Rodriguez-Caballero & Pijuan 
2013) or 1% was reported (Law et al. 2011) and an investigation from a full scale plant reported 
emissions to be 1.7 % of the nitrogen load (Kampschreur et al. 2008).

3 Materials and methods 
3.1 Configuration of sequencing batch reactors 
I investigated nitritation performance, changes in the microbial composition and N2O production 
during sequential feeding in two sequencing batch reactors (SBRs) over a period of 4 months (121 
days). The two SBRs were named Nitritating Granulation reactor 1 (NG1) and Nitritating 
Granulation reactor 2 (NG2). Both reactors had identical configurations – they were cylindrical 
shaped with a reactor volume of 6.8 L and a height to diameter ratio (H/D) of 5.   
During operation, the working volume was 5 L and the volumetric exchange ratio (VER) was 50% 
in each cycle. Each reactor had a set of peristaltic pumps; one provided influent feeding (introduced 
2 cm from the bottom), while the other discharged effluent (30 cm from the bottom) (Figure 1). The 
reactors were semi-closed with lids and shielded from sunlight. Air was supplied by a 7.62 cm 
diameter flat aquarium fine bubble air diffuser (Penn Plax, NY, USA), placed at the bottom of each 
reactor, to promote the formation of small bubbles for homogeneous oxygen supply. The airflow 
was regulated by two flowmeters (0.06-0.6 L min-1, Platon, Cole-Parmer). Below the air diffuser, a 
44x8 mm magnetic, cylindrical stirrer bar was placed and mechanical mixing was provided through 
a magnetic lab stirrer (Heidolph MR 3000, Schwaback, Germany and AGIMATIC-S, Abrera, 
Spain), onto which the reactors were placed (Figure 5). 
Air supply, mixing, and actuation of pumps for fill and discharge was controlled by a 
programmable power strip EG‐PM2‐LAN (Gembird Software Ltd., Almere, Netherlands).

3.2 Reactor seeding and operation  
3.2.1 Inoculum and influent composition 
The two reactors, NG1 and NG2, were inoculated with pre-enriched biomass from a parent reactor. 
One month prior to inoculation of the reactors, biomass from the return activated sludge stream at 
Lundtofte WWTP (Mølleåværket) in Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark was kept in a parent reactor operated 
with constant aeration and fed with synthetic influent (same composition as fed to the SBRs, see 
below). Ammonium was the sole nutrient source and kept constantly in excess to enrich the 
nitrifying community. After a month, the biomass from the parent reactor was inoculated into the 
two SBRs. At the time of inoculation of the enriched biomass, the volatile suspended solids 
concentration (VSS) of NG1 and NG2 was 2.73 gVSS L-1 and 3.08 gVSS L-1, respectively (see 
below a description of the procedure to measure VSS). 
During the operational period, the 2 reactors were fed with synthetic wastewater as 
influent. The composition of the synthetic media was based on Vangsgaard et al. (2013), with only 
slight modifications: NH4HCO3 was added in the concentration 805.7 mg L-1 from day 0 to day 68, 
and 1611.4 mg L-1 from day 68-121, while the rest of the components was added in the same 
amount throughout the whole running period: KH2PO4 64.89 mg L-1, NaHCO3 800 mg L-1, 
MgSO4∙7H2O 751.16 mg L-1, CaCl2∙2H2O 451.66 mg L-1, EDTA 20 mg L-1, FeSO4∙7H2O 0.5 mg L-
1, ZnSO4∙7H2O 0.43 mg L-1. Ammonium bicarbonate (NH4HCO3) was the only nitrogen source and 
sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3) was added as the inorganic carbon source. The water used in the 
influent was tap water containing 319 mg L-1 HCO3- (Andersen 2006). The ratio of HCO3:NH4+-N 
was 7 to not impose inorganic carbon limitations, which could affect the nitritation process. 
3.2.2 Reactor operation 
The reactors were operated in continuous cycles, lasting six hours (360 min), and the hydraulic 
retention time (HRT) was 12 hours. One cycle consisted of an aerated react phase (320 min) 
comprised of five consecutive intervals of 1 minute feed followed by a 63 minutes inter-feed period 
(Figure 6). The name of each inter-feed refer to the feed they follow, thus, after Feed 1 follows 
Inter-feed 1 etc., ending with the last Inter-feed 5 after Feed 5. The react phase was followed by a 
non-aerated and unmixed period consisting of 30 minutes settling, 5 minutes decanting, and 5 
minutes idle (Figure 6).  
 
During normal operation, pH was not controlled but measured pH remained in the 
range of 6.9-8.5. The reactors were operated at room temperature, which varied between 20-27ºC. 
==PAGE:30==
20 
 
Monitoring of DO and pH was performed with FDO® 925 optical DO and SenTix® 980 pH 
sensors (WTW GmbH, Weilheim, Germany). A Multi 3430 multimeter (WTW GmbH, Weilheim, 
Germany) and the MultiLab User software was set to continuously log the data every 1 or 5 
minutes. 
 
From inoculation of the enriched biomass, the operation of the reactors lasted 121 days. The 
reactors were operated with different ammonium loading rate (ALR) and air flowrates. Based on the 
ammonium to air loading ratio, the operation can be divided into two distinct periods: Phase 1 and 
Phase 2 (Figure 6). Both phases were divided into two sub-phases. Each sub-phase is given a title in 
“…”, describing the main characteristics of that phase.  
Phase 1: 
Phase 1 (sub-phase) (day 0-56): “Operation with initial ammonium loading rate” (ALR of 1430 mg 
NH4+-N day-1, and a constant air flowrate of 0.15 L min-1)  
Phase 1b (day 56-67): “Drop in ammonium removal efficiency” (ALR: 1430 mg NH4+-N day-1 and 
air flowrate at 0.15 L min-1). The end of phase 1 was characterized by a major decrease in 
ammonium removal efficiency. This decrease was presumably the consequences of a biomass 
washout due to a power failure, which was followed by another washout of the biomass in NG1 
shortly after (within a few days). In both cases, the washed out biomass was returned to the 
respective reactors from the two separated effluent collection tanks.  
Phase 2. Ammonium loading was increased twofold and the air flowrate was increased. It was 
divided into:  
Phase 2a (day 68-78): “Adaption period to higher ammonium loading” (ALR of 2860 mg NH4+-N 
day-1, air flowrate at 0.25 L min-1)  
Phase 2b (day 78-121): “Balanced nitritation at high ammonium loading” (ALR of 2860 mg NH4+-
N day-1, air flowrate at 0.35 L min-1). 
 
The increase in air flowrate and ammonium loading was imposed to increase the AOB community 
abundance.  
Phase 1b and Phase 2a represent changing operational conditions in the reactors, whereas Phase 1 
and Phase 2b represent performance with stable operational conditions imposed. Therefore, results 
will mainly be reported for Phase 1 and Phase 2b.

3.3 Evaluation of system performance and performed analyses  
Evaluation of how the reactors evolve during operation was done by comparing changes between 
the two ALR conditions imposed (Phase1 – Phase 2b). The evaluation was based on different 
system indicators:  
 To evaluate the development towards stable nitritation, change in nitrogen species (NH4+, 
NO2-, NO3-) was used as indicators. Nitrogen conversion performance was measured daily 
over the entire operational period and more closely within cycles (In-cycles) both in Phase 1 
and Phase 2b. 
 To link the performance in nitrogen conversion to the microbial community, qPCR analysis 
was performed. In particular, AOB and NOB gene copies were quantified and compared to 
the total bacteria, as well as the functional gene coding for the enzymes involved in 
ammonium oxidation to hydroxylamine (amoA). Sampling of DNA for qPCR was 
performed during the entire operational period, and actual qPCR analysis was done on one 
occasion with all samples. 
 To evaluate the net N2O production and emission, N2O concentrations were monitored 
during several periods, each lasting multiple (minimum 4) cycles, during both normal 
operation in Phase 1 and Phase 2b, and during a cycle in NG1 after manipulation with high 
pH (Phase 2b). 
 Changes in biomass characteristics were followed on a weekly basis by particle size 
distribution (PSD) analysis and concentration of volatile suspended solids (VSS).

3.4 Online N2O monitoring and analytical methods for nitrogen species  
3.4.1 Nitrogen species analyses  
The nitrogen conversion performance of the two reactors was evaluated based on the concentration 
of the nitrogen species - NH4+, NO2- and NO3-. In order to distinguish between the nitritation and 
nitratation capability, NO2--N and NO3--N concentrations were related to the NH4+-N removed.  
For measurements of aqueous nitrogen species (NH4+, NO2-, NO3-), mixed liquor samples were 
taken in the end of Inter-feed 5, just before the settling period began. Samples were taken in either 
two consecutive cycles, or one sample per day. During in-cycle analysis, mixed liquor samples were 
taken every 10-20 minutes.  
Samples were filtrated through 0.45 µm filters (Satorius, Göttingen, Germany) before analysis.  
Concentrations of nitrogen species were determined with Spectroquant® Photometric Tests (Merck 
kGaA, Darnstadt, Germany). For NH4+-N, the 100683 and 114752 Ammonium Cell Tests and for 
NO2—N, the 114776 Nitrite Test and for NO3—N, the 109713 Nitrate Test was used. Also, for NO3—
N,NOx- was analysed with the help of an autoanalyzer (Auto Analyzer 3, SEAL Analytical), from 
which NO3- could be calculated from the difference in the NO2- measured in the spectroquant 
photometric test.

3.5 Quantification of AOB and NOB abundance via quantitative PCR 
3.5.1 Sampling 
DNA sampling was performed on a weekly basis. Depending on the VSS concentration at the time, 
the sample volumes ranged from 1-9 ml. The samples were centrifuged for 10 minutes at 10,000 
rpm. The supernatant was removed, and for the larger sample volumes the centrifugation step was 
repeated. Finally, the pellet was stored at -20°C until further analysis.  
3.5.2 DNA extraction  
DNA was extracted using FastDNA™ SPIN KIT (MP Biomedicals) extraction kit, using the 
protocol from the manufacturer. For cellular disruption, bead beating was done using the 
wastewater program configurations (6 m/s, 40 s) (FastPrep®-24, MP Biomedicals).  
==PAGE:38==
28 
 
3.5.3 Quantitative PCR 
DNA was quantified using a NanoDrop ND-1000 UV-vis Spectrophotometer (ThermoScientific, 
Rockwood, TN, USA). Samples were diluted to contain 2 ng DNA µl-1 with a minimum volume of 
100 µl. 
Quantitative PCR (qPCR) was carried out on all the extracted DNA samples according to protocol 
in appendix 1. The primers used were: Total bacteria (16S rRNA gene) eubacterial primers 1055F 
and 1392R; AOB (beta-proteobacteria 16S rRNA) CTO189F and ABC-RT1R; NOB (Nitrospira 
sp. 16S rRNA) Nspra-675F and Nspra-746R; NOB (Nitrobacter 16S rRNA) FGPS872F and 
FGPS1269R; and amoA (beta-Proteobacteria) amoA1F and amoA2R. On one occasion primers for 
nirK and nirS was used. 
Each reaction was performed in duplicates. qPCR was performed using SYBR green. qPCR and 
analysis of the data was done using Opticon Monitor™ software (BIO-RAD).  
The abundance of total bacteria (EUB 16S rRNA) was supposed to be constant in all samples, as 
they were diluted to 2 ng DNA µl-1. Presumably due to dilution errors, or uncertainty in the 
NanoDrop DNA quantification, the EUB 16S rRNA value was not constant. Therefore, the mean 
value, standard deviation and coefficient of variance were calculated for the EUB 16S rRNA values. 
As the dilution errors or quantification errors were similar for all primer qPCR runs, the coefficient 
of variance from EUB was used to establish the error range of the AOB, NOB, and amoA samples. 
Fractions of the AOB, NOB, and amoA copy number to total bacteria copy number were used to 
express estimations of the community composition.  
3.6 Biomass characterization 
Biomass concentration was monitored through measurements of Mixed Liquor Total Suspended 
Solids (MLSS) and Mixed Liquor Volatile Suspended Solids (MLVSS) using the standard method 
APHA 2540 (APHA 2011).  
The particle size distribution was measured weekly and estimated by laser diffraction (using the wet 
dispersion method with the Hydro 2000SM unit) applied with the Mastersizer 2000 software 
(Malvern Mastersizer 2000, Malvern Instruments Ltd., UK).

4.1.1 Reactor and operation overview

- Phase 2a (days 68-78) “adaption period to higher ammonium loading” (2860 mg NH4+-N day-1,

- Phase 2b (days 78-121) “balanced nitritation at high ammonium loading” (2860 mg NH4+-N day-

4.1.3 Dissolved oxygen and pH dynamics – operational parameter profiles  
The reactors were operated with cyclic configurations. Therefore, the dynamic behavior of a cycle 
was investigated. Typical DO and pH profiles for three representative cycles were characterized by 
a very low DO concentration of approx. 0.07 – 0.7 mg/L (Figure 3). However, a DO increase was 
observed at the end of each inter-feed. The increase in DO concentration was amplified throughout 
the inter-feed periods and the DO concentration was highest in the very end of Inter-feed 5 (Figure 
9). Ammonium was measured in excess at the end of Inter-feed 5, and the increase in DO was 
therefore at first sight not due to NH4+ depletion. When ammonium is oxidized, two protons are 
released which results in a decrease in pH as nitritation occurs (Figure 2B). In the representative 
cycles, pH ranged between 7.8 just after feed 1 to 6.9 in the end of the Inter-feed 5 (Figure 9). The 
increase in pH after a feed was due to the buffer system of bicarbonate and potassium phosphate 
added in the influent.

4.1.4 In-cycle nitrogen conversion dynamics  
To determine how nitrogen conversion evolved during a cycle, in-cycle measurements were 
performed in Phase 1 and Phase 2b. The typical bulk NH4+, NO2- and NO3- concentrations, as well 
as DO and pH profiles demonstrated how NH4+ was converted to NO2- and NO3- (Figure 10).  
In Phase 1, DO levels remained between 0.08 – 0.25 mg/L in NG1 and 0.08 – 1.14 mg/L in NG2, 
with the DO level increasing in the end of Inter-feed 5. pH increased during feeding (to 7.7 in NG1 
and 8.4 in NG2) and decreased (to 7.25 in NG1 and 7.4 in NG2) during the inter-feed periods 
(Figure 4). 
 
In both Phase 1 and Phase 2b high concentrations of NO2- and NO3- in the beginning of each cycle 
were due to the cyclic operation of the reactors. While NH4+ was removed due to nitrification 
during the previous cycles, NO2- and NO3- were transferred from the previous cycle as 
denitrification activity was minor or absent. NO2- and NO3- concentrations decreased after each feed 
due to dilution, while NH4+ peaks became smaller throughout the feeds. Each feed results in an 
increase in the volume in the reactor as half a liter of influent containing NH4+ was pumped into the 
system. Thus as the mass of NO2- and NO3- is the same right before and after the feed, the 
concentration will be lower after feed. For NH4+ the same mass was pumped into the system during 
each feed, and as the volume increased the concentration decreased. 
The representative cycle from Phase 1 (day 36) revealed that there was both AOB and NOB activity 
in both NG1 and NG2, since both NO2- and NO3- concentrations increased as NH4+ was removed 
(Figure 10). The concentrations of NO2- and NO3- from beginning to the end of the react phase were 
similar, yet, as the volume in the reactors increased from 3 to 5 litres, the mass of the two 
compounds increased during the cycle. 
In the representative cycle from Phase 2b, nitrogen species were only measured in two inter-feed 
periods, as the reactors had reached a more stable operation. In both reactors, hardly any NO3- could 
be detected, while NO2- concentrations ranged from 150 to 172 mgN/L. During Inter-feed 1 in NG1, 
16 mg NH4+–N/L was removed and 16 mg NO2-–N/L was produced. During Inter-feed 5 in NG1, 
the NO2- concentration accounted for 63% of the NH4+ removed. 
A similar pattern was observed in NG2, where NO2- accumulation accounted for 73% and 74% of 
the NH4+ removed in Inter-feed 1 and 5, respectively.

5 Discussion 
5.1 Long-term performance – reaching stable nitritation  
Although there was an increase in nitritation and a decrease in nitratation throughout the operational 
period, the ammonium removal showed scattered data until the end of the operational period 
(Figure 7). It can thus be suggested that the reactors were reaching steady nitritation conditions, but 
completely stable conditions were not achieved. This timespan for reaching steady nitritation 
operation in other studies operating autotrophic SBRs with bicarbonate as the only carbon spans 
broadly. 58 days was reported by Kong et al. (2013),  3 months (55% ammonium converted to 
nitrite, influent for anammox reactor) was reported by Law and co-workers (2011), and 4 months 
(98% ammonium converted to nitrite) by Rodriguez-Caballero & Pijuan (2013) while the longest 
period was reported by Okabe and co-workers (2011) with 680 days.  
 
5.2 Operational control parameters influencing nitritation performance 
5.2.1 ALR and OLR 
During reactor operation, the variables controlled to enhance nitritation (enhance process 
performance and select the desired microbes (AOB over NOB)) was ALR and OLR. Optimizing 
nitritation using online real-time oxygen measurements as a control variable has been reported by 
Yang and co-workers (2007), while Mutlu (2015) concluded that adjusting the overall oxygen 
loading relative to nitrogen loading yields better process performance (though it was a nitritating-
anammox system) over a longer period of operation of an oxygen limited system. To inhibit 
nitratation in NG1 and NG2, OLR were applied using a heuristic approach. The aim was to keep the 
ammonium removal efficiency a little below 100% to avoid ammonium limitation. The ammonium 
removal efficiency in the system was on average between 80-90 %, hence, high and balanced 
removal efficiency was achieved (Figure 8).  
 
5.2.2 DO  
The dissolved oxygen concentration increased in the end of each inter-feed (Figure 9). Interestingly, 
DO was detected in higher concentrations in the end of Inter-feed 5 compared to Inter-feed 1. 
==PAGE:60==
50 
 
An increase in DO concentration in the end of an aerated react phase was reported by Ma and co-
workers (2015). This was due to depletion of ammonium and the increase was thus an effect of 
AOB inhibition. However in the cycles represented in (Figure 9) ammonium was always found in 
excess and the final ammonium concentration in the end of the react phase was 55 mg NH4-N/L. 
The DO increase between inter-feeds thus cannot be due to ammonium depletion.  
A more possible explanation could be associated with the decrease in pH over the inter-feed periods 
(Figure 9), where pH was a factor of 0.96 lower in the end of Inter-feed 5 compared to the end of 
Inter-feed 1. Based on the pH value of 7.2 at the end of the Inter-feed 1 (Figure 9), the ratio of 
[NH3]/[NH4+] would be 0.9 % (Eq. 3) On the other hand, the ratio of [NH3]/[NH4+] would be 0.46% 
in the end of Inter-feed 5, where pH was 6.9. This decrease in the available substrate for AOB, 
could possibly inhibit the AOB activity. Meanwhile, NOB activity might be enhanced, since there is 
less competition for oxygen, and the true substrate (HNO2) for nitratating microorganisms would 
increase with lower pH. 
Increased NOB activity and decreased AOB activity would influence the DO concentration, due to 
the oxygen demand of the two processes. Nitritation requires 3.43 g O2/(g Noxidized), while 
nitratation requires 1.14 g O2/(g Noxidized). Nitratation thus demands less oxygen, and with a constant 
oxidation loading rate (OLR), the DO concentration would increase during a shift towards lower 
nitritation activity and higher nitratation activity. Vázquez-Padín and co-workers (2010) found that 
when available ammonia was close to depletion, the nitrite concentration decreased while the nitrate 
concentration increased. Thus, the observed increase in DO concentrations at the end of the inter-
feeds could originate from a shift in equilibrium from nitritation activity towards nitratation activity. 
However, any inhibition in ammonia oxidation was not supported by pH, since pH was inversely 
proportional with time throughout all inter-feed periods (Figure 9). 
The increases in DO seen in (Figure 9) are representative for most profiles over the operational 
period. 
In the present case DO did not exceed 1.5 mg O2/L. These small increases in DO did not seem to 
influence the NOB activity of nitratation, as that decreased over the operational period, and the 
NOB copy number from the qPCR data indicated that over the operational period NOB numbers 
decreased compared to AOB. 
 
==PAGE:61==

5.2.3 pH  
Representative pH profiles for three consecutive cycles (day 70-71) showed that pH decreased 
inversely proportional during the inter-feed period (Figure 9). Two protons are released during 
nitritation (Figure 2B) which is the cause of the pH decrease. During nitratation, H+ and electrons 
are equally generated and the pH is thus not affected by nitratation.  
If no biological reaction occurred and aeration was constant, a constant pH increase would follow. 
The aeration would induce CO2 stripping, which would shift the equilibrium of the bicarbonate 
buffer system (Eq. 12) resulting in the pH increase.

Conditions with constant aeration but no biological reaction was presumable not present in the 
system. The pH profile can therefore help distinguish whether nitritation occurs, or is inhibited. pH 
decreases inversely proportional in the profiles (Figure 9), indicating that nitritation was not 
inhibited. As a feed started, a sharp increase in pH was observed. This was due to addition of 
bicarbonate and potassium phosphate in the feed.  
 
In total, this suggests that it would be possible to run the reactors at a higher DO concentration i.e. 
1-1.5 mg/L without an increase in the NOB community as long as ammonium is not depleted. Such 
increased DO concentrations were used by Ahn et al. (2011) and Rodriguez-Caballero & Pijuan 
(2013) who successfully achieved nitritation.  
 
5.2.4 Sludge retention time 
Another control mechanism applied to increase nitritation was the sludge retention time (SRT), as 
previous suggested as a control mechanism by Hellinga et al. (1998); Blackburne et al. (2008). 
AOB have been shown to have a higher growth rate at temperatures above 15°C (Sinha & 
Annachhatre 2007) and the temperature in the reactors was always above 20°C. During Phase 1 
(day 21-35), SRT was actively controlled and set to 20 days to remove NOB and retain AOB. This 
SRT was in line with other studies. For instance, Law and co-workers (2011) used a SRT of 20 days 
at a temperature of 33°C. However, over the period in which active SRT control was applied, an 
increase in the ratio of Nitrobacter copy numbers to total DNA was observed (Figure 15).  
In the same period, MLVSS decreased (Figure 12), indicating a net biomass loss, which should lead 
to a higher ratio of NOB to total bacteria Yet, comparing the actual copy numbers/ng DNA, the

strategist and is reported to have a higher substrate affinity (0.12-0.22 mg N L-1) compared to the r-
strategist Nitrobacter (0.3-1.9 mg N L-1) (Blackburne et al. 2007). K-strategists would succeed 
under oligotrophic conditions, whereas r-strategists would dominate in environments with high 
nutrient load (Nowka et al. 2015; Starkenburg et al. 2011; Mutlu 2015). The nitrite concentration 
was much higher in the reactors compared to in a WWTP due to the operation towards partial 
nitrification and no denitrification. In a WWTP, there is selective pressure for high substrate affinity 
and not as much on fast growth rate. This would favor K-strategists. In the laboratory-scale SBRs, 
r-strategists with high growth rates would be favored, due to the short SRT control applied, rapid 
changing conditions and high substrate availability.

5.3 In-cycle nitrogen conversion dynamics  
5.3.1 Close up of nitrogen conversion - including what can be learned from the pH profile 
Nitrogen conversions were outlined by measurements within a cycle (Figure 10). Going from phase 
1 to 2b, there was a clear increase in AOB activity, as nitrite accumulation accounted for the main 
part of ammonium removed (Figure 10). 
In line with these observations, the fraction of AOB 16S rRNA copies to total bacteria also 
increased throughout the operational period (Figure 15). A major AOB increase was in particular 
observed after the ammonium and oxygen load was increased 2-fold going from Phase 1 to Phase 
2b. This increase in relative AOB abundance is consistent with the large increase in the ammonium 
removal rate obtained after the change in condition. This suggests that the increase in ammonium 
and oxygen loading enhanced the growth and thus the number of AOB cells (assuming that there is 
one copy of 16S per cell).  
The NOB were present in much lower copy numbers compared to AOB (Figure 15). Nitrobacter 
was present in highest numbers during Phase 1 but decreased in Phase 2b (Figure 15), which 
corresponded nicely with the decrease in nitratation activity from Phase 1 to Phase 2b (Figure 8). 
In general, the trends found in the qPCR data related well with the nitrogen conversion 
performance. In Phase 1b, a decrease in AOB, amoA and Nitrobacter copy numbers to total bacteria 
copy numbers was observed, indicating that the microbial community was affected by the reactor 
failure, and the decrease in ammonium removal efficiency reflected the decrease in the microbial 
community (Figure 7 showing AOR and Figure 15 qPCR). 
The system recovered and AOB increased through Phase 2a to Phase 2b (Figure 7 and Figure 15). 
The change from Phase 1 to Phase 2 results are in line with the trends from the nitrogen conversion 
data for the entire operational period, in which nitrate was detected in higher amount in Phase 1, 
and much less in Phase 2b (Figure 8). The data thus fit and support the overall performance pattern. 
==PAGE:64==
54 
 
 
Taking a closer look at the in-cycle from Phase 1, pH decreased until a shortly before the beginning 
of the next feed where it reaches a minimum from where it increased until the end of the feed 
(Figure 10). Such a local minimum in the pH followed by an increase (which was started before the 
initiation of the feed, and thus is not due to the influent input) has been referred to as the “Ammonia 
valley” (Guo et al. 2010). It indicates the point in which ammonia oxidation to nitrite stopped, and 
potential nitrate production increased. The valley results from the stop of protons provided from 
nitritation, which was accountable for the decrease in pH and the simultaneous CO2 stripping from 
the bicarbonate buffer system, which results in a pH increase (Eq. 12). The ammonium valley 
observed in the in-cycle from Phase 1 indicated that ammonium was limited at the end of each 
inter-feed, which should inhibit the nitritation performance (Figure 10). An increase in nitrate 
accumulation as ammonia was depleted was reported by Vázquez-Padín et al. (2010), underlining 
the importance of a proper ammonium loading rate as an important control parameter for nitritation 
performance. The ammonium loading rate is therefore an important operational parameter to take 
into account, also suggested by Daalkhaijav & Nemati (2014). In a sequential feeding reactor, it is 
important to have excess ammonium for the entire inter-feed period, as ammonia limitation is 
probably the most important factor for inhibition of nitritation performance.  
 
The pH profile in Phase 2b did not show the same ‘ammonium valley’ trend and pH decreased at 
the same rate during all inter-feeds (Figure 10). This indicates that even though nitrogen species 
were only measured in Inter-feed 1 and Inter-feed 5 the ammonium removal to nitrite was stable 
during all inter-feed periods.

Analysis and measuring errors were presumably accountable for the problematic results. The nitrate

(Figure 10).The ammonium could beside conversion into nitrite and nitrate be turned into nitrous

In theory, the nitrate could be removed by heterotrophic denitrifiers, and the presence of 
denitrifying organisms was indicated by the detection of the functional genes nirK and nirS in the 
reactors (day 77) (cf. section 4.3). nirK has been found in heterotrophic denitrifying bacteria 
(Throbäck et al. 2004) as well as in AOB (Kozlowski et al. 2014), whereas nirS is a clear 
identification method for heterotrophic denitrifying bacteria (Throbäck et al. 2004). The presence of 
heterotrophic denitrifiers is inevitable. Even in cases where no organic carbon was fed to the 
system, decay products from the autotrophic bacteria will be enough to provide an organic carbon 
source to retain them in the system (Mutlu 2015).

Yet, it does not seem very likely that high levels of denitrification occurs during the aerated period 
(where the nitrate removal was observed), while denitrification did not seem to occur in the non-
aerated period (where an increase in nitrate concentrations was observed). Even if there were anoxic 
micro niches in the particles/flocs, which could account for the denitrification in the aerated period, 
this could not account for the missing denitrification in the non-aerated period, as this should in 
theory provide the best conditions for anaerobic denitrification.  
 
This unreliability in the nitrate data, also meant that the discussion of pH indication of ammonium 
valleys and following increase in nitrate concentration could not be substantiated by the nitrate 
measurements. Still if the pH data is trusted, the profile suggested that ammonia was depleted at the 
end of each inter-feed, indicated by an “ammonia valley” pattern, which reveals that control of the 
ammonium loading rate to keep it in excess is essential to enhance nitritation.  
In conclusion, although the nitrogen species measurements, especially in phase 1, was associated 
with large variation and uncertainties, there was a clear pattern of improved nitritation over 
nitratation going from phase 1 to phase 2b, which most likely was due to the high ALR and OLR 
applied to the system. These two factors were most likely accountable for the increase in the AOB 
community by not limiting ammonium availability and increasing the growth of AOB by adding 
more oxygen to the system.  
 
5.4 Development of particle size distribution 
As the ammonium loading was two-fold increased, aeration was also increased to balance the 
ammonium removal efficiency. A side effect of the increase in aeration was increased shear force 
on the particles, as the shear force came from superficial upflow air velocity (Liu & Tay 2004). 
Shear force has been identified as one of the factors which are important for increasing granulation 
(Liu & Tay 2004; Liu et al. 2006). An increase in the particle size was observed in Phase 2b 
compared to Phase 1 (Figure 13), which could be due to the change in aeration. However it cannot 
be excluded that the particle size increased over time independently of the condition change. In 
biological systems, changes do not necessarily happen instantaneously and biomass development is 
a slow process. A control experiment should be conducted without imposing the condition change 
in aeration to elucidate what other factors have an effect on particle size. This would have 
demanded operation of an additional reactor.

5.5 Specific nitrogen conversion performance and gene abundance  
5.5.1 Biomass specific nitrogen conversion performance – true replicate reactors? 
The overall nitrogen conversion performance was relatively similar between reactors (Figure 11). 
Yet, the biomass concentrations were not (Table 6). Therefore, ammonium removal efficiency per 
gram VSS was calculated. During the transient nitritation phase (Phase 1), the biomass’ specific 
ammonium removal and thus reactor performance per biomass in NG1 and NG2 was similar in 
Phase 1 (Figure 11). However, this was not the case in Phase 2b (Figure 11), where the average 
specific ammonium removal efficiency per gram VSS was approximately two times higher in NG2 
compared to NG1. It should be mentioned that not all of the biomass necessarily was comprised of 
nitrifiers. Yet, the fraction of AOB from Phase 2b in both reactors revealed that most of the biomass 
was most likely nitritating microbes (Figure 15). This yielded two possible suggestions for the 
higher specific ammonium removal efficiency of NG2: The AOB in NG2 had a more amoA copies 
per cell and therefore a higher activity, or that there was a higher gene expression of amoA or even 
on proteome level the ammonium oxidizing activity was larger in NG2 compared to NG1.  
The first suggestion of a higher amoA gene abundance in NG2 compared to NG1 was not possible 
to confirm, as the relative amoA gene copy numbers was similar in both reactors in Phase 2b 
(Figure 15). Therefore, the difference in activity could not be explained based on the amoA gene 
abundance. The other suggestion of a difference in gene expression of amoA between NG1 and 
NG2 cannot be established with regular qPCR, which determines the presence of the genes, but 
would need to be investigated with reverse transcription qPCR to quantify the actual gene 
expression level, or even further investigations on proteome level. This would however demand 
different molecular techniques. 
The results above indicate that it is not necessarily optimal to correlate presence of gene-copies to 
nitrogen conversion activity. This is bias, which should be kept in mind as most of the results in this 
thesis is exactly based on relating nitrogen conversion with the gene copy abundance. 
The overall low abundance of amoA genes compared to the 16S rRNA AOB gene abundance 
(Figure 15) could suggest that the number of amoA detected or the abundance of 16S rRNA AOB 
was inaccurate. This will be discussed in the following section. 
5.5.2 Gene copy abundance related to cell number and primer inadequacy  
It is possible to relate AOB 16S rRNA gene copies to total bacterial cells. This is based on the 
general assumption that there is one copy of 16S rRNA in all bacterial cells. The ratio of AOB to 
total bacteria can be used to state how many of the total microbial community were AOB. One copy 
==PAGE:68==
58 
 
of 16S rRNA have been found in Nitrosomonas europaea (Chain et al. 2003) and Nitrosobacter sp. 
(Starkenburg et al. 2006).  
The same assumption is not accurate for amoA. According to Sayavedra-soto & Arp (2011), the 
genes encoding amo (the amoCAB operon) are found in different copy numbers in different AOB 
belonging to Betaproteobacteria. In the well-studied N. europaea, two very similar copies of the 
amo operon have been reported by Chain and co-workers (2003). Two to three complete amo 
operon copies were reported to be found in the genera Nitrosomonas sp. and Nitrosospira sp. 
(Norton et al. 2002). These populations and their close relatives have been reported to be the most 
dominant lineages found in wastewater treatment systems (Kowalchuk & Stephen 2001). 
The comparison of amoA gene copies to total bacteria 16S rRNA in a 1-to-1 ratio could therefore 
overestimate the fraction of the total bacterial community containing amoA genes. 
It is therefore peculiar that the fraction of AOB 16S rRNA copies is much larger than the fraction of 
amoA copies (Figure 15). All AOB should contain at least one, and normally 2-3 copies, of the 
amoA gene to be able to oxidize ammonia to hydroxylamine, and thus the number of amoA copies 
measured, should illustrate the maximum amount of AOB. The large difference between the 
functional gene and the 16S rRNA gene, especially in Phase 2b, is therefore caused by either lower 
efficiency of the used amoA primers, or the used primerset did not detect the AOB species favoured 
in Phase 2b. 
N. europaea should in theory be abundant in the reactors as described by Ahn et al. (2011); Kotay et 
al. (2013). This species is metabolically versatile and the bacteria are present in activated sludge 
reactors with a wide range of ammonium concentrations (Ahn et al. 2011). Nitrosomonas eutropha 
is another ammonium oxidizer, which could be present in high abundance in the system (Kong et al. 
2013; Ahn et al. 2011). They can be enriched in environments with high ammonium concentrations, 
and the species was reported to be dominating in partial nitrification reactors (Kong et al. 2013; 
Ahn et al. 2011). These two species are well studied and the amoA primer is supposedly designed 
with a high coverage for these species. However, it has been reported that amoA primers are 
generally inadequate to detect the variance in an increasing diversity of amoA genes (Meinhardt et 
al. 2015). This could support the hypothesis that the primers used in this thesis did not detect all 
AOB species favored in Phase 2b, resulting in the large gap between AOB 16S rRNA copies and 
amoA copies. However, if N. europaea and N. eutropha were abundant in the system then the 
primers would, as earlier mentioned, probably be suitable. It would be appropriate to investigate 
which species were present in the different phases. This could be done by cloning and sequencing.

5.6 Production of nitrous oxide 
5.6.1 Nitrous oxide production and the microbial community 
The net production of nitrous oxide compared to the amount of ammonium removed was higher in 
the transient nitritation phase (Phase 1) compared to the production in the stable nitritation phase 
(Phase 2b) (Table 7). This was seen in both NG1 and NG2. The total net nitrous oxide production 
was higher in Phase 2b compared to Phase 1. However, high ammonium removal rates is a main 
objective in nitrogen removal wastewater treatment the results of nitrous oxide production will 
therefore in overall comparisons (such as per cycles or average in each phase) be to related to the 
ammonium removal rates as reported by (Domingo-Félez et al. 2014; Rodriguez-Caballero & 
Pijuan 2013). When comparing nitrous oxide production within a cycle, such as between inter-feed 
periods, it is the total net nitrous oxide production that is reported, and the production is not related 
to the ammonium removal. 
Ahn and co-workers (2011) reported  that nitrous oxide emissions in a transient period towards 
partial nitrification (80 days) on average was 1.9% of the ammonium loading, while the average 
emission decreased to 0.57% of the ammonium loading in the following 190 days. Despite the fact 
that Ahn and co-workers (2011) reported nitrous oxide emissions, and I calculated nitrous oxide 
production, their findings, are in accordance with my results, suggesting that there is an increase in 
N2O production during transition periods or start-up phases compared to stable conditions in the 
bioreactors, where also adaption to high nitrite conditions and limited DO has happened. Adaption 
to high nitrite concentrations and low DO imply microbial selection. This indicates a possible 
change in the AOB strains that are dominant in the more stable Phase 2b. It was proposed by Terada 
and co-workers (2013) that during low ammonium load, K-strategist AOB strains (from the 
Nitrosospira lineage) were dominant, but with increased ammonium load the microbial community 
shifted towards r-strategist AOB strains (Nitrosomonas lineage). They also proposed that the 
predominant AOB community determines the nitrous oxide production potential, and that K-
strategists can be related to lower nitrous oxide production. In Phase 2b, it could be hypothesized 
that the same conditions found by Terada and co-workers (2013) were applicable. This could be 
further examined by either 16S rRNA identification or whole genome examinations. At the time of 
the nitrous oxide measurements, the fraction of AOB to total bacteria in Phase 2b in NG1 was 
approximately two times higher compared to Phase 1 (Figure 15). The fraction of amoA was also 
higher in Phase 2b compared to Phase 1, though in smaller factors than AOB 16S rRNA ( cf. 
section 5.5.2). In NG2, a similar pattern was observed. Assuming there are no active heterotrophic 
==PAGE:70==
60 
 
denitrifiers of importance in the reactors, these observations suggest that it is unlikely to be the 
number of AOB or amoA copies that determines the nitrous oxide production, but rather which 
strains they belong to, as suggested by Terada and co-workers (2013).  
5.6.2 Characteristics of N2O production during a SBR cycle with sequential feeding 
The representative cycle (Figure 16A) shows that positive net nitrous oxide production rates was 
found throughout the react phase, indicating that there was no net consumption of nitrous oxide 
(Figure 16A).  
Net nitrous oxide production includes the change in aqueous concentration over time as well as 
emission over time (Eq. 10) For the representative cycle, net nitrous oxide production per 
ammonium oxidized was calculated from (Eq. 10) (calculations not shown). Nitrous oxide 
emissions per ammonium oxidized was also calculated (Eq. 10) (calculations not shown). This 
showed that net nitrous oxide production per ammonium oxidized for the representative cycle was 
0.36% and the emission per ammonium oxidized was 0.55 % for the representative cycle. This was 
lower than what has been reported in other studies, where 0.8% (Rodriguez-Caballero & Pijuan 
2013) or 1% was reported (Law et al. 2011). However, it is uncertain to compare the results of other 
studies, as the configurations of the systems employed are different. 
A closer look at the net nitrous oxide production during a cycle showed a distinctive pattern found 
in the majority of the profiles measured and depicted in the representative cycle (Figure 16A). The 
initial peak, from the onset of the aerated period (Figure 16A) was proposed to relate back to an 
increase in liquid concentration during the non-aerated and unmixed (settling, draw and idle) period 
(Figure 6) as no emission occurs since KLa is zero (Eq. 10). The production in the non-aerated 
aerated period, thus determines the high initial nitrous oxide concentration in the beginning of the 
following aerated phase (Figure 16A). Similar pattern in aerated and unaerated periods is described 
by Rodriguez-Caballero & Pijuan (2013); Law et al. (2011). Rodriguez-Caballero & Pijuan (2013) 
reported that 60% of the total nitrous oxide production occurred during settling phase in a nitritating 
SBR reactor and that the associated emission occurring in the first few minutes after initiation of the 
aerated period accounted for 60-80% of the total nitrous oxide emission of the cycle.  
In this thesis due to the setup of the nitrous oxide sensor, it is only the first few minutes of the 
settling phase that can be interpreted, as the reactor was not homogenous due to lack of mixing 
(Figure 6: Overview of reactor operationFigure 6). The sensor was floating in the top of the liquid, 
and during the unmixed periods the measured nitrous oxide concentrations are not reliable or 
representative, as the biomass settles in the bottom of the reactor. 
==PAGE:71==
61 
 
The first few minutes of the settling period is on the other hand interesting because there is only 
nitrous oxide production and no emission due to lack of aeration, and further investigations into 
production during the settling phase could be interesting as the main production seem to occur from 
this period. 
 
5.6.3 Effect of operational condition on nitrous oxide production pattern.  
Another interesting observation in the sequentially fed reactors during a cycle is the secondary 
peaks in nitrous oxide production just after a feed (Figure 16). Ammonium was in excess and 
therefore the peaks are not caused by ammonium depletion in the inter-feed periods. Also, the pH 
profile revealed no ammonium valleys or changes in rate during an inter-feed period (Figure 16). 
However, as the feed started, pH increased rapidly (Figure 16). This change in pH affected the ratio 
between ammonia and ammonium, and caused a much higher increase in the ammonia relative to 
ammonium concentrations. An increase in pH has been shown to enhance nitrous oxide production 
(Law et al. 2011; Hynes & Knowles 1984). In these studies, an increase in pH was related to an 
increase in AOR and N2O production until pH values of 8 - 8.5, whereas higher pH than 8.5 
decreased AOR and nitrous oxide production. This indicates that the concentration of available 
ammonia is not the only determining factor for nitrous oxide production, as ammonia concentration 
increases with higher pH values. Severe inhibition of AOB activity has also been reported at pH 
values above 8.5 (Claros et al. 2013). Law and co-workers (2011) discus that an increase in AOR 
could result in a higher rate of available electrons (released as hydroxylamine is converted to nitrite, 
cf. section 2.3.1), which would more readily divert to the nitrifier-denitrification pathway, leading 
to an increase in nitrous oxide production. Alternatively, the authors suggest that the higher 
ammonium oxidation rate result in an accumulation of hydroxylamine, which could lead to 
hydroxylamine oxidation to nitrous oxide. These are the two main possibilities for nitrous oxide 
production during ammonium oxidation, and since it is not directly possible to discriminate 
between the two pathways, isotope analysis would be needed, such as the ones performed by 
Wunderlin and co-workers (2013) on activated sludge. 
Thus, the increase in pH in the beginning of a feed could be responsible for the increase in nitrous 
oxide production, but whether increase in AOR caused the higher production or not cannot be 
definitely concluded. 
Another condition that has been reported to increase nitrous oxide production from AOB is 
limitations in inorganic carbon availability (Jiang et al. 2015). Inorganic carbon was in this project 
==PAGE:72==
62 
 
provided with the feed, and applied in amounts that should not cause limitations. If emissions were 
due to inorganic carbon limitations, a different pattern in the net nitrous oxide production would 
presumably be observed. The nitrous oxide would instead be produced at the end of the inter-feed 
periods leading up to a feed, and decrease as inorganic carbon would be added in the feed. 
4.6.4 Mitigating N2O production and emission in a sequential fed SBR  
Due to these observations and hypotheses that increased pH would lead to higher net nitrous oxide 
production an additional experiment was conducted elucidating the effect of increased pH on net 
nitrous oxide production. 
The cycle with increased pH conditions, indeed produced more nitrous oxide compared to the 
representative cycle with normal pH conditions and the production did not linearly relate to increase 
in AOR as was suggested by Law and co-workers (2011). The fraction of ammonium removed 
which converted into nitrous oxide was 0.36% in the average cycle, while the fraction in the cycle 
with high pH was 0.81%. If the increase in AOR and increase in nitrous oxide production related 
linear then the fraction found in the experiment should be equal. 
Nitrous oxide emissions showed a similar trend where the fraction of nitrous oxide emitted per 
ammonium removed was higher in the cycle with high pH (0.83%) compared to the representative 
cycle (0.55%). 
The other factor which changes during feed which has been shown to increase nitrous oxide 
production was limitations in inorganic carbon concentration (Jiang et al. 2015). Such limitations 
however also resulted in decreased ammonium removal (Jiang et al. 2015). 
In the high pH experiment conducted in this thesis the inorganic carbon load was the same as in the 
representative cycle and the AOR increased. This substantiates that it was indeed changes in pH 
during the feed and not changes in inorganic carbon that influenced the nitrous oxide production. 
In a SBR with sequential feeding, increase in the pH during feed is inevitable when the pH is not 
controlled. The present observation mention above indicated that pH was influencing the nitrous 
oxide production in the reactors. These secondary peaks will therefore continue to occur, due to the 
continuous decrease and increase in pH. Increasing the duration of the feeding period and with that 
decreasing the feeding rate, and thereby applying  a relatively lower change in pH has been 
investigated as a mitigation strategy by Law et al. (2011). The authors showed that nitrous oxide 
production did decrease with longer feeding periods. However, this also caused decreased AOR. 
Increasing the feeding period could change the dynamic character of the SBR. Findings by Terada 
et al. (2013) suggested that the large ammonium concentration changes in a SBR selected for r-
==PAGE:73==
63 
 
strategist AOB species, which produced less nitrous oxide compared to the continuous fed reactor 
selecting for the K-strategist AOB species. The mitigation strategy applied by Law et al. (2011) did 
not take into account the long term microbial adaption to such changed conditions. This shows that 
mitigation strategies are not trivial when a high AOR and a low N2O production is wanted.

6 Conclusion 
Two lab-scale partial nitrification SBRs were operated with sequential feeding, and the effects on 
nitritation performance, the microbial community and the reactors’ nitrous oxide production were 
investigated. It can be concluded that: 
 
  High ammonium removal efficiency (82% ± 14% NG1 and 86% ± 11% NG2) and nitrite 
accumulation of the oxidized ammonium (85% ± 24% NG1 and 77% ± 17% NG2) was 
achieved. This nitritation level was obtained after 4 months of operation. 
 The two reactors reached similar ammonium removal efficiencies and nitritation 
performance. Yet in the last phase, the specific ammonium removal rate differed as MLVSS 
in NG2 was half the concentration as in NG1. In both reactors, the bacterial community 
consisted mainly of AOB. Further investigations into gene expression and species 
identification should be conducted to shed light on the difference in biomass activity. 
 The ALR was suggested to be the main operational parameters to control in order to 
enhance nitritation in this system. A two-fold increase in the ALR together with an increase 
in the airflow, to maintain a similar ammonium loading to oxygen loading, resulted in 
increased the average AOR: 1251 ± 396 (NG1), 1220 ± 246 (NG2) (mg NH4+-N/day) in 
Phase 1 and 2132 ± 246 (NG1), 2298 ± 187 (NG2) (mg NH4+-N/day) in Phase 2b. Increased 
nitritation performance was observed from 52 ± 22% (NG1), 65 ± 30% (NG2) in Phase 1 to 
85 ± 24% (NG1), 77 ± 17% (NG2) in Phase 2b. 
 The fraction of the total bacterial community content of ammonia oxidizing bacteria and 
nitrite oxidizing bacteria changed significantly from the WWTP’s activated sludge to the 
final composition in the two reactors. An enrichment of AOB was obtained and a change 
was observed in the abundance of NOB, from a majority of Nitrospira (known K-strategist) 
to a dominance of Nitrobacter (known r-strategist). 
 AOB abundance did not correlate with nitrous oxide production. The fraction of ammonium 
converted to nitrous oxide was higher in Phase 1 compared to Phase 2b, yet the abundance 
of AOB gene copies was lower in Phase 1 compared to Phase 2b.  
 Nitrous oxide production was observed in which an initial emission peak was observed in 
the beginning of the cycle followed by secondary peaks during subsequent feeds, a 
==PAGE:76==
66 
 
characteristic shape seen in the majority of the monitored cycles. The initial peak accounted 
for approximately on third of the total emission in a cycle. 
 The secondary peaks were proposed to derive from an increase in pH induced by 
bicarbonate in the feed, confirmed experimentally. 
 The increase in nitrous oxide increased more than the AOR increase indicating that the N2O 
production did not linearly relate to increases in AOR.  
 
In general, it was possible to run sequentially fed SBRs with high nitritation efficiency and a low 
production of nitrous oxide. A dominance of AOB in the microbial community mediated high 
nitritation, but higher abundance did result in higher nitrous oxide production. This indicated that 
microbial adaptions to high nitrite concentrations and limited DO caused lower nitrous oxide 
production. This adaption was hypothesized to relate to changes in the AOB species or strains 
dominating in the reactors, yet that would need further investigations to prove. Dynamic changes 
from pH during the sequential feeds seemed to increase nitrous oxide production. 
This shows that successful nitritation as a potential influent for ANAMMOX treatment can be 
operated in sequential fed SBRs with low nitrous oxide production and emission, but after 
achieving stable conditions. For further reduction of nitrous oxide emissions, proper mitigation 
strategies needs further investigations.

7 Future research perspectives 
The initial nitrous oxide emission peak, which relates back to production during settling and the 
initiation of aeration, would be interesting to further investigate, as this period is responsible for a 
major part of the nitrous oxide emitted. 
It could be interesting to get a better understanding of the production during the settling period. 
With the current reactor setup, monitoring in the settling period was limited by heterogeneous 
conditions. With the current reactor setup, the production during settling could be investigated by 
turning on mixing in short time intervals in order to achieve homogenous conditions. Over the 
period of the settling phase this would indicate whether the max production occurred during the first 
5 minutes or after 20 minutes. It would enhance appropriate control mechanisms to know if reduced 
settling could be applied to limit the N2O production. However, if the max production occurred in 
the very beginning of the settling period then reduced settling time would be pointless. 
It would be pertinent to look into gene expressions rather than only describing the system based on 
the genes present. This could for instance be interesting in order to establish the differences in 
biomass activity between my two reactors. 
It could be interesting to investigate on a species or strain level which AOB were present in the 
transient nitritation period (Phase 1) and in the stable nitritation period (Phase 2b) to substantiate 
the suggestions that it was microbial adaption (seen as change in species/strain dominance), which 
were accountable for the decrease in nitrous oxide production. A better understanding of changes in 
the microbial community and its adaption to operational conditions, could give valuable knowledge 
when evaluating whether short-term experiments, conducted to suggest mitigation strategies, are in 
fact reliable during long-term application.
