1 Introduction 
 
Diabetes Mellitus (DM) is a serious public health burden with increasing prevalence. In fact, the 
International Diabetes Federation (IDF) estimated that 463 million people suffered from diabetes 
by 2019. A number that is estimated to increase by 51% to reach over 700 million people by 
2045 ​[1]​ . Generally, there are two major types of DM: Type 1 diabetes and Type 2 diabetes. 
Type 2 DM is the most prevalent type of diabetes accounting for up to 90% of all diabetes 
worldwide according to the IDF ​[1]​. Furthermore, type 2 DM is characterized by hyperglycemia 
caused by peripheral insulin resistance in addition to relative hypo-insulinemia caused by 
dysfunctional beta-cells in the pancreas ​[2]​. Although the guidelines for treatment of type 2 DM 
published by different associations are not entirely consistent, they all agree on metformin being 
the first-line therapy ​[3]​. However, if therapeutic targets are not achieved with metformin, then 
type 2 DM patients are prescribed combinatorial-therapy with the addition of a second glucose 
lowering agent to metformin ​[3]​. 
 
 Incretin-based therapies are considered to be effective add-on therapies and are in fact 
recommended by the American Association of Clinical Endocrinologists ​[4]​. These agents 
include glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor (GLP-1R) agonists and dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP 
IV) inhibitors ​[3]​. GLP-1R has thus been an attractive target for drug development and an 
understanding of the receptor activation mechanism of GLP-1R could prove crucial in designing 
potent agonists. So far, there have been only one published Cryo-EM structure of the activated 
full length rabbit Oryctolagus cuniculus GLP-1R in complex with glucagon like peptide-1 
(GLP-1) (PDB ID 5VAI) ​[5]​. Nonetheless, the crystal structure of full human GLP-1R have been 
published but either in complex with truncated peptide agonists  (PDB ID 5NX2) ​[6]​ or in 
complex with Exendin P5 which is a biased peptide agonist (PDB ID 6B3J) ​[7]​. Additionally, the 
inactive structure of the trans-membrane domain (TMD) of human GLP-1R has also been 
published (PDB ID 5VEW) ​[7], [8]​. All these provided the opportunity to perform a comparative 
in silico ​study of full human GLP-1R  in both inactive state and active in complex with various 
analogues which could enable further understanding of key peptide-receptor interactions as well 
as a possible mechanism of receptor activation. 
 
 In this project, a combination of homology modelling  and molecular dynamics simulation was 
applied to shed light on the interactions between peptide agonists and GLP-1R. Furthermore, a 
machine learning based approach was used to map the complex changes in the intramolecular 
interaction profiles between the activated and inactivated GLP-1R.

2 Background  
This section will provide the theoretical background of GLP-1, GLP-1R and the important 
intermolecular interactions between them highlighted in literature. Additionally , a concise 
description of homology modelling and molecular dynamics simulation. Finally, the machine 
learning algorithm used in this project will be briefly introduced.  
2.1 Glucagon Like Peptide-1  
2.1.1 Derivation  
Glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) is a peptide hormone produced and secreted by L-cell in  the 
distal small intestine ​[9]​.  GLP-1 is derived from a larger precursor called proglucagon, which in 
addition to GLP-1 encodes the related glucagon-like peptide 2 and Glicentin. However, in the gut 
this proglucagon precursor is subjected to post-translational modifications which facilitates the 
production of GLP-1 which composes residues 78-107 of this precorsure.

2.1.2 Function 
GLP-1 is secreted in response to ingestion of meals rich in fats and carbohydrates ​[10]​. Upon 
secretion, it travels the blood stream until it reaches its target receptor, the GLP-1R ​[10], [11]​. 
Binding to its receptor, GLP-1 triggers insulin secretion in a glucose-dependent manner, 
decreases glucagon secretion, slows gastric emptying and increases satiety [3]. Therefore, GLP-1 
promotes control of blood glucose levels and appetite which provides an attractive therapeutic 
option for treatment of type2 DM and obesity. 
 
 
 2.1.3 Structure 
GLP-1 is composed of natural α-amino acids. It is initially produced as the biologically inactive 
GLP-1(1-36) and GLP (1-37) which are activated by N-terminal truncation into the two 
equipotent GLP-1(7-36) and GLP-1(7-37) respectively ​[12]​. The first one is more abundant in 
human circulation. However, the latter has the generic term of GLP-1 (henceforth will be the one 
referred to as such) . The secondary structure of GLP-1 is a straight α helix . Figure 2 (a) shows 
the primary structure of GLP-1 while (b) shows the α helix secondary structure of the isolated 
GLP-1 from the cryo-em structure of rabbit GLP-1R (PDB ID 5VAI) in complex with GLP-1.

 2.1.4 Limitations  
GLP-1 has a quite short half-life in the bloodstream which is approximately 1.5-5 minutes ​[13]​. 
Furthermore, GLP-1 is readily cleared from the plasma by renal filtration ​[14]​. Therefore, the use 
of GLP-1 as a drug is rendered limited and hence the need for modifications and development of 
analogues with better pharmacokinetic profile to overcome these limitations and come up with 
clinically viable GLP-1R agonists ​[15]​.  
 
2.2 Investigated Peptides 
In this study, three different GLP-1R peptide agonists have been investigated in addition to 
GLP-1, namely Liraglutide,G1 and Exendin peptide-5 (henceforth referred to as exendin-P5) 
[16]​[7], [17]​ . These peptides have been developed to overcome the poor pharmacokinetic profile 
of GLP-1 by extending serum circulation time, thus improving its half-life ​[15]​. A special 
consideration will be taken when evaluating the potency of these analogues to that of GLP-1. 
This consideration concerns a variation of GLP-1 potency reported in different literature. The 
reported potency varitates from pEC50 9.93 ± 0.14 reported in Levine et al.  ​[17]​ and pEC50 
10.26 ± 0.13. These two values are within the same statistical uncertainty range therefore only 
one of them will be used for comparison.  
 
2.2.1 Liraglutide 
Liraglutide is a GLP-1 analogue which has two main structural differences from GLP-1 
structure. The first one is the attachment of a 16 carbon fatty acid chain attached on Lys26 
through a gamma glutamate (γGlu) linker. This attachment enables Liraglutide to bind human 
serum albumin (HSA) which protects it from degradation by DPP IV and subsequently prolongs 
half-life to 11-15 h without compromising the potency ​[18]​. Indeed, the potency of Liraglutide 
measured at pEC50 of 10.17 ± 0.05 is comparable to that of GLP-1 measured at pEC50 of 9.93 ± 
0.14 ​[16]​ ​[17]​. 
The other structural difference is the mutation of Lys34 of GLP-1 into Arg34 in Liraglutide to 
prevent fatty acid attachment on this location in addition to Lys26 ​[18]​.  The resulting structure 
of Liraglutide is illustrated in figure 3.

2.2.2 G1 
G1 is a GLP-1 analogue reported by Levine et al. with an amino acid sequence similar to GLP-1 
[17]​. The only structural difference between GLP-1 and G1 is the addition of β-linked 
N-acetylglucosamine (O-GlcNAc) on Ser18 of GLP-1​[17]​. This modification has been reported 
to double GLP-1 half-life by inhibiting its cleavage by DPP IV without affecting the 
potency​[17]​. In fact,the potency of Liraglutide measured at pEC50 of 9.94 ± 0.13 is comparable 
to that of GLP-1 measured at pEC50 of 9.93 ± 0.14 ​[17]​. Figure 4 shows the chemical structure

2.3 GLP-1 Receptor  
GLP-1R is a membrane-bound class B G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) that is composed of 
463 amino acids ​[20]​. GLP-1R is expressed on the surface of specific cells in lung, brain, 
pancreas, stomach and large intestine ​[21]​. Structure-wise, GLP-1R resembles all class B GPCRs 
as it has an N-terminal extracellular domain (ECD). This ECD is 100-150 residues long and is 
connected to an integral transmembrane domain (TMD) composing of seven transmembrane 
α-helices (TM1-TM7) ​[20]​. TM7 is connected to an intracellular C-terminal α-helix (Helix 8).

2.3.1 GLP-1R Extracellular Domain (ECD) 
The structure of GLP-1R-ECD has been described in detail in literature [23][24][25]. The ECD 
consists of one α-helix and two antiparallel β sheets, these structures are connected to each other 
through loops. Each β sheet is composed of two β strands, β​1​ and β​2​ belongs to the first strand 
while β​3​ and β​4 ​belongs to the second strand [23]. The loop between the α-helix and the first β 
sheet is called loop1. β​3​ and β​4 ​are connected through a relatively long loop which is called loop2 
[23][24][25].  
 
These secondary structures  are interacting with each other. For instance, the side chain of Arg64 
from β​1​ is forming a network of noncovalent interactions with Leu50 from α, Asp53 from α, 
Pro54 from loop1 and Asp74 from β​2 ​[23].

2.3.2 GLP-1R Transmembrane Domain (TMD) 
The structure of GLP-1R transmembrane domain has been described in two different states of the 
receptor, namely activated and inactivated ​[23]​. Nonetheless, the TMD of GLP-1R has in general 
a structure typical of class B GPCRs in the sense that it has seven transmembrane α-helices 
(TM1-TM7) ​[20]​. Furthermore, the N-terminal of TM1 is connected to the C-terminal of ECD 
through a linker (henceforth referred to as ECD-TM linker) ​[6]​. Additionally, these seven TMs 
are connected to each other through three extracellular loops (ECL1-ECL3) and three 
intracellular loops (ICL1-ICL3) ​[6]​. TM1 is connected to TM2 through ICL1, TM2 is connected 
to TM3 through ECL1, TM3 is connected to TM4 through ICL2, TM4 is connected to TM5 
through ECL2, TM5 is connected to TM6 through ICL3 and finally TM6 is connected to TM7 
through ECL3 ​[6]​. Moreover, the intracellular part of GLP-1R, i.e Helix 8, is connected to TM7.

2.4 Peptide-Receptor Interactions 
 
In order to create a reference for comparison later in this study, major interactions between the 
investigated peptides and GLP-1R mapped in literature will be summarized in this section. 
 
The interaction profile between GLP-1 and GLP-1R has been the subject of many studies 
already, namely mutagenesis studies ​[24]​[20]​ , crystal structure studies ​[5]–[7], [25]​ as well as 
molecular modelling ​[23]​.  
 
Furthemore, the interactions between the peptide and the ECD have been discussed by 
Underwood et al.  ​[25]​ which solved the crystal structure of GLP-1 in complex with the ECD 
(PDB ID 3IOL), and Zhang et al. ​[5]​ which solved a cryo-electron microscopy structure of 
GLP-1 bound to active full GLP-1R (PDB ID 5VAI)  
 
Nonetheless, the cryo-em structure reported by Zhang et al. reported further interactions between 
GLP-1 and the TMD of GLP-1R including the ECLs, whereas Jazayeri et al. reported the 
interactions between Pep5, a truncated 10 residue peptide agonist with a structure comparable to 
GLP-1 (7-17) residues, and full length GLP-1R in crystal structure (PDB ID 5NX2) ​[6]​. 
Hereafter, Santiago et al. ​[23]​ built a hybrid model of full GLP-1R using the structures reported 
by Zhang et al. (PDB ID 5VAI) and Jazayeri et al. (PDB ID 5NX2) and used to study major 
interactions between GLP-1 and GLP-1R.

2.5 Homology Modelling 
 
Proteins are macromolecules composed of one or more polypeptide chains. Each of these chains 
is a polymer of amino acids linked together through an amide chain ​[26]​. While the sequence of 
these polypeptide chains could be determined through prediction of expression of gene coding, 
predicting the protein conformation resulting from protein folding, i.e the subsequent adaptation 
of a unique 3D structure necessary for protein activity, remains one of the major challenges in 
the field of structural biology ​[26], [27]​. 
 
Despite the fact that correct protein folding depends basically on its amino acid sequence as it is 
stabilized by weak interactions between the amino acids of this sequence ​[26]​, an unfolded 
polypeptide chain has an astronomically large degrees of freedom resulting in an astronomically 
vast conformational space ​[28]​. Therefore, if a protein is to fold to its correct conformation by 
random search through simple sequential sampling of all possible conformation, it will require 
more time than the age of universe itself ​[28]​. This is known as Levinthal’s paradox proposed by 
Cyrus Levinthal in 1969 ​[28]​. 
 
Whereas modern diffraction and nuclear magnetic resonance have enabled solving the 3D 
structures of protein with a good resolution at an increasing rate. These methods remain slow and 
relatively expensive. Hence, the development of a variety of computational structure prediction 
methods which enable construction of an atomic model of a target protein based only on its 
amino acid sequence ​[27]​. 
  
Among all current methods for computational structure prediction, comparative homology 
modelling is considered to be the most reliable to generate a 3 D model of a target protein ​[27]​. It 
builds on the fact that naturally occurring homologous proteins have similar 3D structures. 
14 
==PAGE:19==
Therefore, an atomic model of the 3D structure of a target protein could be built entirely based 
on its amino acid sequence and the 3D structures of homologous protein which have been 
determined by experiment ​[27]​.  
  
In general, homology modelling methods follow four sequential steps: template selection, 
target-template alignment, model building and model assessment ​[29]​. 
  
1. Template selection: 
  
The first step in homology modelling is to identify template structures to be used in comparative 
modelling ​[29]​. This is usually achieved by using the target protein sequence as query to search 
for templates in established structure databases such as the Protein Data Bank ​[29]​. Desired 
templates are usually ones with the highest possible sequence similarity. Therefore, comparison 
between target and template sequences is necessary for valid selection of proper templates ​[29]​. 
Template selection could also be performed through literature review ​[29]​. 
  
2. Target-template Alignment: 
  
Once proper templates have been selected, an alignment between the sequence of the target and 
those of the templates is necessary to initiate model construction. The quality of this pairwise 
alignment is vital for proper model construction. Therefore, a variety of alignment methods have 
been developed to ensure that proper target-template sequence alignment is attained even in 
cases where target-sequence identity is below 40% ​[29]​. 
  
3. Model Building 
 
After a proper alignment is achieved, a 3D model is usually built through one of three main 
methods: (1) modelling by assembly of rigid bodies, (2) modelling by segment matching or 
coordinate reconstruction and (3) modelling by satisfaction of spatial restraints  ​[29]​.  
 
The first one; i.e modelling by assembly of rigid bodies, relies mainly on dissecting the protein 
structure into conserved core regions, loops that connect them and the side chains of the amino 
acid backbone . However , the second method; i.e modelling by segment matching or coordinate 
reconstruction , employs the known coordinates of the template atoms to approximate the 
coordinates of the target atoms. The third method ; i.e modelling by satisfaction of spatial 
restraints, depends on developing a set of geometrical and optimization restraints based on the 
templates and then develops a 3D model of the target by minimizing the violation of these 
restraints ​[29]​.  
 
15 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
4. Model Assessment 
In general, evaluation of the constructed models is based on structural comparison between the 
3D structures of the target and templates based on sequence similarity as well as the validity of 
the energy profile of the constructed models ​[29]​.  
 
 
2.6 Molecular Dynamics Simulations MD simulations 
 
The atomic models obtained from X-ray crystallography, Cryo electron microscopy (Cryo-EM), 
nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR),  provide a relatively static view of complex molecular 
systems. However, proteins are indeed of a dynamic nature. The constituents of these systems 
are continuously interacting with each other as well as with their environment ​[30]​.  Therefore, 
in order to understand and study this dynamic nature of complex molecular systems, an ​in silico 
approach also known as Molecular Dynamics (MD) have been developed on the basis of 
classical mechanics to enable following the changs in positions and momenta of atoms in these 
complex systems as a function of time ​[30]​. MD simulations play therefore an essential role to 
form a bridge between the observations that was obtained from the experiments and the theories 
that can be derived from the observations.  
 
2.6.1 Molecular Mechanics MM 
The 3D geometry of a molecular system with an N number of atoms,  i.e the cartesian 
coordinates of the system atoms, has a 3N-6 degrees of freedom considering that the first atom of 
the system is frozen at the origin point of the cartesian system ​[31]​. This degree of freedom 
provides a geometrical flexibility of such systems​[31], [32]​. However, this geometrical flexibility 
is restricted by the energy of the system as the energy of a given molecular system is dependent 
on the geometry of the system, and vice versa​[31]​. This relationship between energy and 
geometry of molecular systems could be described mathematically through the function of 
Potential Energy Surface (PES)​[31]​. 
 
Solving the PES of a molecular system with a known geometry enables predicting geometrical 
changes in time.  Moreover, the PES of such systems could be solved by either a quantum 
mechanics (QM) approach using Schrödinger equation or a molecular mechanics (MM) 
approach using classical mechanics ​[32]​. However, while the QM approach is more accurate, it 
comes with an extremely large computational cost. Therefore, for the study of large molecular 
16 
==PAGE:21==
systems; such as proteins, MM provides the computational efficiency required for studying these 
systems in realistic time period ​[33]​.  
 
In molecular mechanics, a force field (FF) approach is applied to solve the potential energy 
function of the molecular system. This FF approach describes molecular systems to be composed 
of functional groups that are particles are described by experimentally fitted parameters to 
describe the energetic behavior that is dependent on their geometry. The behavior of these 
groups is presumed to be similar regardless of the molecule which enables the use of a large 
number empirical numbers to solve the PES function of the molecular system​[31]​. The energy of 
the system is hence calculated based on the contribution of energy from bonded interactions and 
energy from non-bonded interactions between these particles​[32]​.

2.6.2 Molecular Dynamics MD 
Molecular dynamics enable a time-dependent study of molecular systems. After setting the initial 
conditions of a molecular system, i.e the position and momenta of atoms at the beginning of the 
simulation, the evolution of the system through discrete time steps is studied by numerically 
integrating the equation of Newton's second law ​[34]​. This equation describes a relationship 
between the force acting on a particle, and the acceleration of this particle as the second 
derivative of the position vector.

2.6.2.1 Leap-Frog Algorithm  
 
In an MD simulation, the state of a molecular system, i.e the position and momenta of its atoms, 
is updated at a small time step by an algorithm which runs a numerical integration to solve 
equation (5)​[35]​. The time evolution of the considered molecular system can be  studied using 
the leapfrog algorithm derived from Taylor expansion to solve Newton’s equation in this 
project​[35]​. However, this algorithm is not self starting and requires the initial position and 
velocities of atoms in the molecular system and the initial velocities of the atoms​[35]​. Whereas 
the initial position of atoms in this study was determined from the simulated model, the initial 
velocities were assigned from Maxwell-Boltzman distribution at the simulation temperature​[35]​. 
 
 
2.6.2.2 Periodic Boundary Conditions 
 
An MD simulation is usually performed on a nanoscale system in a finitely small simulation box 
with approximately 100,000 atoms​[30]​. Therefore, in order to minimize the finite size effect and 
enable a more accurate approximation of the bulk phase, the simulation algorithm works to 
mimic the effect of creating infinite duplicates of the simulated system in each direction. These 
duplicates allow molecules to leave and enter the simulation box as they behave similarly 
meaning that if an atom leaves from one side of the main box then it will reenter the box from 
the opposite side​[32]​. 
 
2.6.2.3 NPT Ensemble 
 
MD simulation of molecular systems creates an ensemble of N number of partices with a 
characteristic set of parameters including pressure P, temperature T and volume V. These 
parameters are interrelated so it is possible to control some of them by scaling the others ​[34]​. 
However, when simulating the behavior of biomolecular systems, it is crucial to mimic 
biological conditions. Therefore, MD simulations of such systems are usually run using a 
constant number of particles N, constant pressure P and constant temperature .

2.7 Random Forest Classifier 
 
MD simulations generate large amounts of complex data which could be extremely hard to 
interpret by simple visualization of the results. However, computational data analysis provides a 
great opportunity to maximize the efficiency of data analysis by applying statistics-based 
algorithms which comprises the machine learning model ​[36]​. The output data are hence the 
subject of the machine learning model in order to reveal patterns within the data set by for 
instance classifying the data into different classes (labels) based on their associated features. 
Based on the knowledge provided to the model, machine learning could hence be categorized 
into supervised and unsupervised learning ​[37]​. 
  
Supervised learning is a machine learning model in which the model is trained using structured 
data meaning that the classes of the data as well as their features are provided to the model and 
then the model learns to map features and labels ​[38]​.  While in unsupervised learning the model 
is trained using unstructured data, then the model works to find the features of the data based on 
similarity, patterns and differences in order to define classes ​[37], [38]​. 
 
An instance of supervised learning is classification. In classification, the labels of the data are 
restricted to a limited set of values ​[39]​.  Different classes of data have then a distinct set of 
features which could be used to classify unlabeled data ​[39]​. For example in this project, the 
classes are binary and restricted to active and inactive conformation of GLP-1R and the different 
intramolecular interactions are the features with which the different conformations of the 
receptor could be classified into active and inactive. For the purpose of classification of data, 
proper feature selection and feature weighting is required ​[40]​. 
 
A Random Forest classifier grows a large number of uncorrelated​ ​classification trees using 
random selection of features to split each node of the classification tree. Each tree votes then for 
its class and the class with most votes is predicted for the model ​[40]​.  The random forest 
algorithm also measures the importance of individual features used at each node ​[40]​. The score 
of importance could hence be used for detection of important features ​[40]​. This characteristic 
will be used in this study to detect major changes in intramolecular interactions between active 
and inactive conformations of GLP-1R.

3.2 Molecular Dynamics Simulations 
 
Triplets of each system were simulated accounting to a total of 15 simulations using AMBER 
and the DTU chemistry in-house GPU cluster. Each simulation was given a system ID followed 
by a sequential number, for instance, the first replicate of the simulation of GLP-1 in complex 
with GLP-1R will be written as GLP-1_Run1.  
 
First, the systems were minimized with 10,000 minimization steps.The first 5000 minimization 
steps were performed using the steepest descent method then the conjugate gradient method was 
used for the remaining steps.  Afterwards, the systems were heated slowly using 150000 steps to 
303 K using a Langevin thermostat.  Hereafter, the systems were equilibrated with 8 
equilibration runs for 2 ns with a coupled Langevin thermostat to ensure constant temperature at 
303K, a coupled semi-anisotropic Monte Carlo barostat to ensure constant pressure at 1 bar. 
Additionally, the surface tension for the extracellular and intracellular interface was defined at 0 
dyne/cm in the x-y plane. Afterwards, the systems were subjected to two production runs each 
for 200 ns. The production runs were performed under the same NPT ensemble conditions of the 
equilibration run ​[32]​. The input files for each run are attached in (appendix A-D). 
 
3.3 Post-simulation Analysis Methods 
 
3.3.1 Heatmaps and Contact Maps 
An MD simulation output is a trajectory that is a time series of three-dimensional atomic 
positions. A single trajectory can be dissected into many frames depending on the time step 
between individual frames. For this project, trajectory strides were printed out during MD 
simulation every 0.2 ns then 10 strides were used to produce each frame of the trajectory 
resulting in a frame time step of 2ns.  
For reasons that will be discussed later in the section 4.1, the last 200 ns  (100 frames) of each 
trajectory were analyzed to investigate both analogue-receptor interactions (hereafter referred to 
as intermolecular interactions)  as well as changes in the receptor’s conformation  namely 
changes in TMD inter-helix interactions (hereafter referred to as intramolecular interactions). 
The simulated systems exist in a dynamic state meaning that a pair of residues that are 
interacting in a single frame could be either interacting or not in another one and since the 
systems are too complex to simply follow the interactions profile visually it was of uttermost 
importance to use mapping tools that could enable a two-dimensional representation of the 
27 
==PAGE:32==
interaction profile. The mapping tools used in this project are heatmaps for evaluation of 
intermolecular interactions and contact maps for evaluation of intramolecular interactions. 
A heatmap is a graphical representation of data where the individual values contained in 
a matrix are represented as colors ​[46]​.  
In this project, time is represented as colors in the heatmap where the number of incidences of a 
pairwise intermolecular interaction are counted as the number of frames in which the interaction 
appears (i.e the residues exist within an empirically chosen cut off distance of 3 Å). The higher 
the frame count for a specific pair of residues, the longer time these residues spend interacting 
thus the stronger the interaction is regarded. Interactions appearing in more than 75% of the 
frames are considered to be strong interactions and therefore firmly considered for further 
analysis, while interactions appearing in 25% to 75% of the total frames are considered as weak 
to medium interactions and investigated with much higher scrutiny. Interactions Appearing in 
less than 25% of the total frames are not shown and considered negligible for further analysis.

4.5 Conclusion  
 
The models of GLP-1R in complex with GLP-1, GLP-1R in complex with exendin-P5 and 
GLP-1R in complex with liraglutide could reproduce many of the important intermolecular 
interactions reported in literature. However, an error in modelling the peptide in the model for 
GLP-1R in complex with G1 lead to a faulty interaction profile that could not reproduce the 
important interactions reported in literature.  
 
Furthermore, the simulation results coupled with mutagenesis data shows that N-terminal 
residues His7*, Glu9*, Phe12*, Thr13*, Asp15* and Glu21* of GLP-1 are important for 
receptor activation. However, residues Phe28*, Ile29* and Leu32* are important for mediating 
the correct conformormation for binding.  
 
The major conformational change for GLP-1R activation is the outwards movement of 
cytoplasmic part of TM6. The results show that the TM6 helix disruption at the 
Pro358-Leu359-Leu360-Gly361 motif is important for the outwards movement of the 
cytoplasmic part of TM6. 
 
Rearrangement of the receptor hydrophobic core between TM3, TM5, TM6 and TM7 ,mediated 
by the interaction of Leu356 and Leu360 with the hydrophobic cluster of (composed of 
Gly248(TM3), Leu251(TM3), Ile323(TM5)  Phe324 (TM5), Asn320(TM5) Val327(TM5) and 
Ile328(TM5)) in addition to Pro358(TM6)-Gln394(TM7), is important for stabilizing the TM6 
helix disruption. Furthermore, this rearrangement breaks the hydrogen bond cluster between 
TM3,TM6 and TM7 at the bottom of the receptor (composed of Glu247(TM3), Thr353(TM6) 
and Tyr402(TM7) ) which allows the cytoplasmic half of TM6 to move outwards away from the 
helix bundle.  
 
Eventually, The Random Forest Classifier could detect the major changes in intracellular 
interactions.
