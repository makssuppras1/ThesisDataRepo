Abstract 
Off-target side effects remains one of the largest problems with using systemic anti-cancer drugs. 
Cancers are difficult to treat since they consist of non-foreign cells that closely resemble healthy 
cells. In order to minimize the side effects of systemic drug, the design can be changed with a short 
peptide, with a selective affinity towards cancer cells. 
A synthesis strategy for four different peptide-drug conjugates utilising known reactions, was 
planned and executed. Unfortunately there were problems regarding the de-allylation and 
cyclisation of one of the peptides, and the synthesis of this peptide was halted. The three other 
peptides were synthesised and conjugated to a cytotoxic drug, MMAE. Due to time constraints, it 
was only possible to make preliminary analysis of the conjugates for their biological activity against 
HeLa cells. 
The synthesised peptide-drug conjugates are planned to be analysed for cytotoxicity against non-
small-cell-lung-carcinoma as well as healthy cells in order to compare their anti-mitotic properties 
to each other and to the free drug. 
PDCs are a promising alternative to current cancer therapy, and it is envisioned that it is possible to 
optimise the peptide for affinity to cancer cells. The design and synthesis of four different peptides 
in parallel can be expanded to any peptides that have shown affinity to cancer cells, and it is 
possible to use the describe design to synthesis multiple different PDCs with different peptides, 
linkers and drugs. 
Although not all analyses were finished, they will help determine which conjugate will work best 
for targeted delivery of drugs with affinity for cancer cells.

Scientific Background / Introduction 
Cancer is one of the most widespread diseases in the world with as many as 18 mill. new case 
annually [1]. The treatment of cancers is comparatively difficult since there are no foreign entity in 
the body, and it is next to impossible to develop methods that are specific towards cancer cells. 
Recent studies aim to more selectively kill cancer cells, while sparring healthy cells. The most 
recent strategy to do so is by attaching a cytotoxic drug to a molecule that can find the tumour site 
[2]. 
The goal of this project is to design and synthesise multiple different Peptide-Drug Conjugates 
(PDC) using a solid phase synthesis strategy. The PDCs should have the property that they 
recognise and bind to specific proteins that are unique to or overexpressed on non-small-cell lung 
carcinoma cells (NSCLC). The linker between the peptide and the drug should be stable to the 
formulation and the extracellular environment, but cleavable in the presence of NSCLC. Finally, the 
drug used should be a cytotoxic molecule, that is so potent, that it can't be used in its free form, due 
to adverse effects. The synthesised PDCs were planned to be tested for their cytotoxic properties in 
a cancer cell line. 
In this report the theory of peptides and proteins, cancers and pharmaco-kinetic and -dynamic 
considerations will be described. Furthermore, the synthesis strategy will be described as well as 
key reaction mechanisms. The conducted experiments will be described and discussed, and a 
backup strategy in case the original plan wouldn't work was also described. Every synthesised 
compound is documented by NMR, HPLC and/or LCMS to ensure that the desired products are 
obtained in adequate purity. 
1-1 Peptides and Proteins 
Proteins are one of, if not the most important class of molecules in biology. Proteins are large 
structures made up of chains of amino acids. These chains can vary from a few amino acids (called 
peptides, <50 amino acids), to tens of thousands of amino acids (called proteins ≥ 50 amino acids). 
There are 20 naturally occurring amino acids, Figure 1-1, giving rise to a large diversity of 
structures and functions of the proteins. Compared to DNA, where the entire macrostructure is 
double helical, proteins' more diverse building blocks ensure that there is no single folding structure 
of all proteins. Instead each individual protein has a unique folding pattern, which is very difficult 
to predict a priori. Many proteins in the human body, however, can be divided into groups that have 
similar structure, folding, and, thus, function. 
==PAGE:9==
Page 9 of 110 
 
 
Figure 1-1: 20 Natural occurring amino acids 
Proteins have many different functions within the human body, ranging from catalysis to structure. 
For this project, the most important function of proteins is that they can recognize specific 
molecules and bind them selectively. Synthetically prepared proteins can be build from unnatural 
amino acids, and these can have other effects that the natural amino acids can't have. 
1-1-1 Solid Phase Peptide Synthesis (SPPS) 
One method for generating both natural and unnatural peptides or proteins is to use biological 
material. Microorganisms (bacteria/yeast/mammalian cells) are genetically modified to have genes 
that, through the central molecular dogma, code for the desired protein. This method is the most 
efficient in terms of yield and scale (e.g. insulin production), but is halted by the need to commit to 
one sequence. One problem with this method is that the protein sequence is (mostly) limited to the 
natural amino acids, whereas newer methods [3], can utilise unnatural amino acids, as well as 
capping with amines or acids [3]. 
As opposed to the biosynthesis, chemical synthesis is the preferred method for producing shorter 
peptides. This method makes use of sequential condensation reactions between an amine and a 
==PAGE:10==
Page 10 of 110 
 
carboxylic acid, (Figure 1-2) to make individual peptide bonds. Since all amino acids are 
polyfunctional, they need to be protected at each nucleophilic site. The conventional method to do 
this is to have a solid support at the C-terminal, which also acts as protection, while building 
towards the N-terminal. Each step starts by deprotecting the N-terminal amine, and attach it to the 
next amino acid, with a protected amine, and so forth. Every protecting group needs to be 
orthogonal to the deprotection of the terminal amine so that no unwanted reactions occur on the side 
chains. 
 
Figure 1-2: Condensation to form a peptide bond 
A problem that arises from chemical synthesis of peptides is that larger, protected peptides usually 
gets insoluble and rigid. In order to overcome these problems, the synthesis was to be carried out in 
the solid phase, so that the aqueous insolubility is an advantage and not a disadvantage. If the C-
terminal is a natural amino acid, it is usually possible to buy preloaded resin. This will be a so 
called Wang resin if the C-terminal is desired to be a carboxylic acid [4]. If the C-terminal amino 
acid is desired to be an amide, a Rink resin would be used. The Rink resin does not need to be 
loaded, and the SPPS starts by deprotection of the resin and follows with normal couplings [5]. 
 
Figure 1-3: Structure of a Wang resin and a Rink resin 
The first step of the peptide synthesis is the deprotection of the nucleophilic nitrogen, that is used 
for the formation of the peptide bond. This nitrogen is usually protected with a Fmoc-group. This 
protection is preferred because the deprotection of Fmoc is orthogonal to all other protecting groups 
in the peptide, including the solid support on the C-terminal. Amino acids are Fmoc-deprotected 
with the use of piperidine, or any other nucleophilic base. The mechanism of deprotection is shown 
in Figure 1-4. 
 
Figure 1-4: Mechanism of deprotection of Fmoc 
==PAGE:11==
Page 11 of 110 
 
The peptide bond has a large bonding energy, and in order to make it, the carboxylic acid needs to 
be activated. Water and hydroxide are very poor leaving groups, so most coupling reagents aims to 
make the oxygen a better leaving group. There are multiple reagents that can do this, including 
HATU, HBTU, PyBOP. DCC and DIC, and they all have different advantages and disadvantages 
[6], Figure 1-5.  
 
Figure 1-5: Structures of common coupling reagents 
HATU, HBTU and PyBOP all build on the aromatically extended 1-hydroxytriazole, 1-
hydroxybenzothiazole, which can be attached to the carboxylic acid, according to Figure 1-6. 
 
Figure 1-6: Coupling mechanism of benzotriazoles 
DCC, DIC and EDC are all carbodiimides, that similarly makes the oxygen into a good leaving 
group via Figure 1-7. 
 
Figure 1-7: Activation of the carboxylic acid using carbodiimides 
A problem that is encountered with carbodiimides is the formation of N-acylurea (Figure 1-8, A). If 
this happens, the carboxylic acid can't undergo the peptide formation, and is thus rendered useless. 
Another problem is that during the activation, another carboxylic acid can attack the active ester, 
yielding a carboxylic acid anhydride (Figure 1-8, B). The anhydride, while not inactive, makes the 
effective concentration of the active ester only half. In order to solve these two problems, additives 
such as HOBt is used. HOBt suppresses the formation of O-acylisourea by forming another active 
ester that is stable in solution (Figure 1-8, C). Also, these additives are better nucleophiles, making 
==PAGE:12==
Page 12 of 110 
 
the formation of the anhydride less likely. Due to explosive danger of HOBt and analogues thereof, 
the additive Oxyma (Figure 1-8, D) is now more widely used [7]. With the more stable active ester 
formed, the new peptide bond can be formed with the addition of an amine (Figure 1-8, E). 
 
Figure 1-8: A) Formation of N-acylurea. B) Formation of carboxylic acid anhydride. 
C) The use of HOBt as a stabilising agent. D) The use of Oxyma as a stabilising agent. 
E) Formation of the peptide bond 
Another reason why additives such as HOBt or Oxyma are used is that they minimize racemisation. 
Once the carboxylic acid is activated the proton at the anomeric carbon becomes more acidic by one 
of two paths. This proton can be removed during the reaction to form a enolate anion, which then 
can be protonated again to form a racemic mixture (Figure 1-9). With additives in the mixture, only 
a weak tertiary amine would be necessary in order to remove the proton of the carboxylic acid. This 
weak base will not be able to deprotonate the anomeric carbon and thus minimize the risk of 
racemisation [8]. 
==PAGE:13==
Page 13 of 110 
 
 
Figure 1-9: Pathways for racemisation 
Some of the most important distinctions between the coupling reagents is how to remove the side 
product after the reaction. In solution peptide synthesis, PyBOP is very attractive, since the side 
product is a phosphonium salt, which is not soluble in organic solvents, and thus can easily be 
filtered or washed off. For solid phase synthesis, this is not a desirable property. Since the growing 
peptide is not dissolved, the side product should stay in solution, and be washed away after the 
reaction. This makes HATU and HBTU attractive, since the benzotriazole usually is soluble. 
Regarding the carbodiimide, the side chains on the nitrogen can be changed so that the desired 
properties are achieved. A good example of this the use of DIC. Contrary to other coupling 
reagents, DIC is liquid at room temperature, meaning that it is easier to accurately make a stock 
solution. Furthermore, the urea derivative is also easy to dissolve compared to the urea derivative of 
DCC. 
In between each formation of the next peptide bond, there are a lot of washing steps to ensure that 
there is no remaining amino acids in solution. Afterwards the Fmoc group of the newly introduced 
amino acid can be removed, and the next cycle can start. 
Once all the desired amino acids have been assembled to constitute the resin-bound protected 
peptide, the peptide can be cleaved from the resin. This is usually done with the use of concentrated 
TFA, with other scavenger molecules (like phenol and TIPS, that quenches cations of the protecting 
groups present). The concentration needs to be high, in order to cleave every side chain protecting 
group [9]. 
If it is desired to obtain protected peptides to do further chemistry with, the resin needs to be 
cleavable in relatively mild conditions. The most used resin for this purpose is the 2-chlorotrityl 
resin. By using a 2-chlorotrityl resin ensures that the peptide can be cleaved from the resin using 
dilute acid, that does not deprotect the side chain protecting groups. 
==PAGE:14==
Page 14 of 110 
 
In order to start the SPPS, the resin needs to be preloaded. This pre-loading happens on a 2-
chlorotrityl chloride resin, and follows a SN1 mechanism as shown in Figure 1-10. Once the first 
Fmoc-protected amino acid has been loaded, normal peptide synthesis can be performed [10]. 
 
Figure 1-10: Preloading of 2-chlorotrityl chloride using a SN1 reaction 
The process of solid phase peptide synthesis is very repetitive, and cyclic. From the very start of 
solid phase peptide synthesis, the goal was to make a fast and simple method for synthesizing 
polypeptides. Since there is a small amount of building blocks (amino acids), the most effective 
way would be to make the whole process automated. Automated SPPS is done with a machine, that 
can perform one or multiple syntheses at the same time, by utilizing multiple reaction chambers. 
Since the overall process of peptide coupling is not dependant on which amino acids that is coupled, 
it is easy to program a synthesiser to follow procedure of wash, deprotect, wash, couple, wash, with 
the only parameter being changed is which bottle the synthesiser should take the amino acid for 
coupling from. The peptide assembly cycle is showed in Figure 1-11. 
==PAGE:15==
Page 15 of 110 
 
 
Figure 1-11: SPPS cycle of reactions 
Despite automated peptide synthesis being the superior choice for research purposes, there are still 
instances, where the automation is a disadvantage. There is a size limit for the reactors in the 
automated peptide synthesis, meaning that if larger amounts of the peptides are needed, automated 
peptide synthesis will not have the capacity to carry out the synthesis. In such cases it is necessary 
to use manual peptide synthesis, where the reactor can be a lot larger. Another disadvantage is the 
start up cost, associated with the automation. A synthesiser is a lot more expensive than the cost of 
individual reactors for manual synthesis, and is, thus, an investment for the amount for the time, that 
will be saved with automation.  
1-1-2 Protecting groups 
The activated amino acid, that is added to the reaction chamber during each cycle, is a very strong 
electrophile. In order to control the synthesis, it is necessary to limit the resin-bound peptide to only 
possess a single nucleophile: The N-terminal amine. This is done by protecting the would-be 
nucleophiles on the side chains of each amino acid. The protecting group needs to be stable to the 
SPPS cycle, meaning that they cannot be base-labile. Most strategies use acid-labile protecting 
groups, such as trityl (Trt) and tert-butyl (tBu), so that once the linear sequence have been 
synthesised, every protecting groups, along with the solid support, can be cleaved in one step. If, 
however, the peptide is desired to have more modifications than the linear sequence, more 
==PAGE:16==
Page 16 of 110 
 
protecting groups, that are orthogonal to both the SPPS cycle and the other side chain protecting 
groups are needed. These include, but are not limited to Allyl (All, cleavable by hydrogenation in 
presence of Pd[0]) and Acetamidomethyl (Acm, cleavable by oxidation) [11]. 
1-2 Cancer 
Cancer is not a modern ailment, but instead has afflicted all of humanity in all regions of the world. 
The first evidence of cancer is tumour masses found in prehistoric bones, with other evidence of 
cancer growth in mummies from Egypt and Greece [12]. In the modern understanding of cancer, it 
is not one disease but rather a collection of over 200 disease from all over the body. Cancer can 
show slow growth patterns which gives a symptom-free "recovery" and it can show rapid growth 
leading to death within a few months. Cancer can spread from tissue where it originated to other 
tissues, or it can be stationary [13]. 
With our modern understanding of cancer, all tumour cells needs to have acquired a set of six 
capabilities in order to be considered cancer [14]. The first is self-sufficiency in growth signals. 
Normal cells receive growth signals at transmembrane receptors, while tumours are able to generate 
many of their own growth signals, and is thus able proliferate unchecked via a positive feedback 
loop [14]. The second capability is that cancer cells needs to be insensitive to antigrowth signals. In 
healthy tissue, there are many different antigrowth signals that maintains the homeostasis, and 
prevent the cells from dividing too fast. Cancer cells can disrupting the pRb pathway, which leads 
to liberation of E2F, and the cell is allowed to proliferate [14]. The third capability is evading 
apoptosis. The reason why cancer cells can expand so much in numbers is not only explained by 
their increased proliferation. Cancer cells have mutations in a tumour suppressor gene [14]. The 
fourth is limitless replicative potential. Normal cells have a limit on how many times they can 
divide. If this was the case for cancer cells, they would die out within a few generations (Hayflick). 
Cancer cells can circumvent this limit by upregulating enzymes that extends the telomeres in the 
DNA [14]. The fifth capability is sustained angiogenesis. Because cancer cells have an increased 
metabolism, they need to have an efficient "supply line" to ensure enough oxygen and nutrients. 
They can alter the formation of new blood vessels by upregulating inducers and countervailing 
inhibitors [14]. The sixth capability is tissue invasion and metastasis. For tumours to succeed they 
need to send out cancer cells to new tissue to set up new colonies. They do this by upregulating the 
expression of cell-cell adhesion molecules like integrins and selectins [14]. 
These acquired capabilities makes designing drugs against cancers interesting and challenging. 
With blood vessels leading into tumours, and increased enzyme activity in and around cancer cells, 
drugs are usually given systemically. Cancer cells still looks a lot like healthy cells, in a sense that, 
even  immunomolecules, cannot distinguish between the two [15]. Systemically given drugs will 
move through the whole body and kill any cells it comes in contact with, and with cancers this is 
usually fine because most of the drug will go to tumour sites anyway. Such treatments are rough for 
the rest of the body, with many off target effects, but strong adverse effects are tolerated because of 
the lethality of cancers [16]. 
==PAGE:17==
Page 17 of 110 
 
Recent research have been focused on minimizing the side effects of chemotherapy by making the 
drug more selective towards tumour sites [17]. This includes making prodrugs, that are inactive in 
the administered form, but is activated by enzymes that are unique to or over expressed in cancer 
cells. This will limit the off target effects by a lot, since the drug will only be active at the site of 
action. Some of the latest FDA-approved drugs utilises these strategies, and the current state of the 
art with regards to chemo therapy is antibody-drug conjugates [18]. These use an antibody to 
specifically target a certain tumour antigen, that ideally is unique to cancer cells. The antibodies 
themself aren't cytotoxic, but they carry a drug, that is inactive while bound to the antibody, but 
becomes highly cytotoxic once released in the vicinity of cancer cells [17]. 
1-2-1 Mechanism of Monomethyl Auristatin E 
The drug that was chosen for the PDCs in this project was Monomethyl Auristatin E (MMAE, 
Figure 1-12). MMAE is a mitotic inhibitor, derived from the natural compound Dolastatin 10 
(Figure 1-12) [19]. 
 
Figure 1-12: Structures of Dolastatin 10, Monomethyl Auristatin E and Monomethyl Auristatin F 
Dolastatin 10 is a natural cytotoxic pseudopeptide isolated from a certain species of sea hare, 
Dolabella auricularia. Due to the difficulty of extracting large quantities of Dolastatin 10 from such 
small animals (~1 mg per 100 kg biomass) [20], multiple synthetic variants have been developed 
[21]. MMAE was developed from this natural product by changing the C-terminal amine 
(dolaphenine) to another aromatic system (norephedrine) and change the N-terminal from an imide 
to an amide, and the tertiary amine to a secondary amide. This would make the compound easier to 
synthesise, since it would only be 4 amide couplings. 
==PAGE:18==
Page 18 of 110 
 
The five parts of MMAE are called Monomethyl Valine, Valine, Dolaisoleucine, Dolaproline and 
Norephedrine. Although MMAE is about 200-fold less potent than Dolastatin 10 [20], it is usually 
preferred, due to the ease of production. Experiments of MMAE and other auristatins in human 
cancer cell lines showed that the drugs inhibited cell growth and causes cell death in sub-nanomolar 
concentration [22]. This large toxicity makes MMAE an attractive drug for PDCs. 
Both Dolastatin 10 and MMAE (and sister compound MMAF) acts as mitotic inhibitors, by 
inhibiting the polymerization of tubulin [19]. Tubulin is a small peptide (50 kDa) that forms 
polymers (microtubules) upon binding to GTP/GDP. The polymerization of tubulin is a dynamic 
process, the hydrolysis of the GTP/GDP pair determines if the tubulin dimers will be in an 
assembling state (β-tubulin bound to GTP) or disassembling state (β-tubulin bound to GDP) [23]. 
The dynamics of the microtubules is highly important part of mitosis, because the 
assembly/disassembly is linked to multiple phases of the chromosome separation within the mitosis. 
There are three different domains within the tubulin dimer, one where inhibitors can bind and 
inhibit the polymerization of the microtubules [24], one where the Dolastatins binds in the Vinca 
domain, within the β unit of one dimer and the α unit of the next dimer. High resolution crystal 
structures of the tubulin-MMAE complex reveals that the C-terminal end (Norephedrine and 
Dolaproline) have a large overlap with the H6-H7 loop of the β unit, while there is good π-stacking 
between bound GDP and the phenyl group of Norephedrine. Finally D179 and N329 interacts with 
the final two valines in MMAE [25]. These interactions, and a few more, can be seen in Figure 
1-13. 
==PAGE:19==
Page 19 of 110 
 
 
Figure 1-13: Binding site for MMAE within the Vinca domain in tubulins [25], with permission from the author 
There are a few things to keep in mind when designing drugs based on MMAE. First, there is no 
way for MMAE to distinguish between healthy cells and cancerous cells, meaning that MMAE 
cannot be used systemically as a free drug. MMAE needs to be conjugated to another molecule that 
can recognise and find cancerous cells, and only then release MMAE locally for apoptosis. Since 
the active site is crowded, there is no room for MMAE if it is conjugated. Furthermore, from 
studying the binding site within the Vinca domain, it can be rationalised that MMAE would be 
inactive if it is conjugated in either end, and, thus, the cleavage from the peptide needs to be 
traceless for MMAE still to be active. 
1-3 Pharmacokinetic and -dynamic considerations 
The effect of any drug is often related to its concentration at the site of action, so it would be useful 
to be able to monitor this concentration. Obviously, it is not possible or, at least very difficult, to 
directly measure the drug concentration inside tissue, but if kinetic homogeneity (Figure 1-14) is 
assumed, it would be possible to determine the dose that should be administered. 
==PAGE:20==
Page 20 of 110 
 
 
Figure 1-14: Relationship of plasma to tissue drug concentrations 
Pharmacokinetics is defined as the kinetics of absorption, distribution, metabolism and excretion of 
a drug (ADME), while pharmacodynamics describes the relationship between the concentration of 
the drug at the site of action and the resulting effect. This also takes the time dependence of the 
drug, for both absorption and metabolism into account. 
The ADME properties of larger peptide drugs are vastly different from that of small molecules [26]. 
The first major difference is the route of administration. Generally, peptide based drugs are not 
active following oral administration. This is mostly due to two reasons. First, there is a lot of 
enzymatic activity in the gastrointestinal tract, an activity that primary function is to digest and 
absorb proteins. This fact makes the gastrointestinal tract the primary site for the metabolism of the 
peptide drug. Second, once the drug has made it through the stomach and into the intestines, it 
needs to pass through the mucosa and epithelium, and into the blood. The gastrointestinal 
epithelium works as a barrier for any water soluble molecules to pass into the blood. Even though 
there are complications with oral delivery, it is the preferred route of administration, because 
ingesting a tablet is a lot easier for the patient compared to any form of injection. 
Recent studies have tried to make the bioavailability a lot higher for oral administration with the 
help of additives and vehicles, but no breakthrough has happened yet [27]. The most common 
routes of administration of peptides are then intravenous (IV), subcutaneous (SC), or intramuscular 
(IM) administration. Of these, IV (either bolus or constant infusion) usually gives the highest 
bioavailability, but this does not mean that it automatically is the better route. Compared to the 
other routes of administration, IV is the most difficult to perform, and is almost exclusively 
performed by a professional in a hospital setting, whereas oral, SC or IM administration can be 
performed by the patients themselves. Furthermore, the instant infusion of the drug will spike the 
drug concentration, and thus, potentially, give higher adverse effects. 
In order to control the time dependency of the plasma concentration, SC or IM administration can 
be used. SC and IM works similar, by making a depot of the drug somewhere in the tissue (skin or 
muscle), from where the drug slowly can be absorbed into the blood. These methods prevent the

spike in plasma concentration, and thus result in longer lifetimes and fewer adverse effect, but this 
comes with a potential cost of bioavailability compared to IV. 
Once the drug has been absorbed into the blood plasma, it will be distributed. The distribution is 
usually important to take into account, in order to avoid any tissue accumulation of toxic 
metabolites. In the case of peptide drugs, this is not an issue, because all metabolites of the peptides 
will be amino acids, which is useful for the body anyway. Even the pharmacophore MMAE is 
comprised of unnatural amino acids, which will be degraded into amino acids or excreted. 
Biodistribution studies are used for calculating the targeting of the drug. Once inside the body, the 
drug can be (largely) distributed either in the hydrophilic blood or inside hydrophobic fatty tissue. 
The volume of which the drug is distributed within is called the Volume of Distribution (VD), and is 
used to calculate the concentration of the drug in the tissue. For most protein drugs, the large size 
means that they are confined to the extracellular space, and thus a very small VD. If the apparent VD 
get higher (low blood plasma concentration) it means that the drug has moved into the tissue, and is 
located outside the systemic circulation. At this point studies are necessary to see if the drug has the 
desired pharmacological effect or use fluorophores or radiotracers to locate where in the tissue the 
drug is located. 
                                   
                                
Protein based drugs are by their very nature very easy to metabolize. The goal of metabolism is to 
break down the drug into smaller compounds that either can be reused or excreted. Proteins are 
broken down into their component amino acids by proteases. These amino acids can be reused. The 
excretion process usually starts in the liver, where the Cytochrome P450 (Cyp450) enzymes 
oxidises compounds that cannot be reused. The oxidation makes the compounds easier to excrete 
(via conjugation to e.g. glutathione, sulphation) from the kidneys or bile. 
In the example of this project, the cyclic peptide is made up of almost exclusively natural amino 
acids, which can be reused. The unnatural amino acids that are present are citrulline, which is used 
in the Arginine pathway, and can thus be reused. The amino acids of MMAE are slightly different.

2-1 Design 
The current state of the art for treatment of cancer is antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) with several 
FDA-approved drugs including brentuximab vedotin (Adcetris®) and ado-trastuzumap emtansine 
(Kadcyla®). While ADCs have their advantages, they still carry limitations such as low tumor 
penetration and high manufacturing cost [17]. 
An alternative to ADCs that aim to overcome these limitations is PDCs. The design of the PDCs are 
based on already existing and approved ADC drugs. A peptide drug conjugate is composed of a 
receptor targeting peptide with a cleavable linker. The cargo drug can be synthesized relatively 
easily and cheaply compared to ADCs. There are currently no PDC's with regulatory approval, but 
GRN1005 is in clinical trials for patients with solid brain tumours [32]. Although not in clinical 
trials yet, there are multiple other PDCs being synthesised and analysed, using different peptides, 
linkers and drugs [2, 33, 34]. 
2-1-1 Peptides 
The sequences of the peptides are based on known sequences, that have shown to target specific 
surface receptors of cancer cells. In this project four different cyclic peptides will be synthesised 
and tested. Peptide 1 uses an Arginine-Glycine-Aspartate (RGD) moiety, and is one of the most 
tested cyclic peptide [35]. This tripeptide was originally found as an important moiety for 
fibronectin to bind to the α5β1 integrins on the cell surface of mammalian cells [36]. Integrins have 
later been found to be closely associated to cellular functions of cancer cell, such as initiation and 
metastasis, and have thus been a focus for cancer studies [37]. RGD has been selected as one of the 
most attractive targeting ligands in PDCs, because it is recognized by 8-12 of the 24 known 
integrins [29], and is already used in some PDCs to deliver Doxorubicin to mice bearing human 
breast cancer cells [38]. With regards to which other amino acids could be incorporated into this 
peptide, a SAR analysis of the pentapeptide cyclo(RGDXY) has shown that the position next to 
aspartate, X, is mainly used for hydrophobic interaction and preferred substitutions are D-Phe or 
βNal. The position next to X, Y, is largely variable, and the side chain of Y is expanding out from 
the binding site of the integrins [39]. It was consequently decided to use this position for 
conjugation to the linker. 
The specific peptide that are planned to be analysed in this project is cyclo(RGDfE) for plan A, 
because of its side chain caboxylic acid or cyclo(RGDfK) for plan B, because of its side chain

2-1-2 Linker 
The linker that will be used for this project is the tripeptide Glu-Val-Cit-OH coupled to p-
aminobenzyl alcohol (PAB-OH). This peptide is an extension to a very commonly used linker; Val-
Cit-PAB-OH. This linker is commonly used because both the internal bonds (amide bonds), and the 
conjugation site to the drug (carbamate bond) are very stable to the extracellular environment, and 
the Cit-PAB amide bond is selectively cleaved by the enzyme Cathepsin B, which is over-expressed 
in cancer cells [40]. 
 
Figure 2-6: Cleavage of the Glu-Val-Cit-PAB linker 
The PAB-O-CO-NH-drug carbamate bond is then cleaved by elimination of cyclohexadiene-imine 
and decarboxylation of the carbamate, releasing the drug without any trace of the linker. The 
mechanism of the cleavage is shown in Figure 2-6 and Figure 2-7 [41]. 
 
Figure 2-7: Elimination and decarboxylation for traceless drug release 
The reason why Glu-Val-Cit is preferred to Val-Cit is that the latter is unstable in mouse plasma, 
due to extracellular esterases [42]. To ease the transition from in vitro studies to in vivo studies, the 
linker is changed to a Glu-Val-Cit-PAB-OH system, which is more stable in mice [41, 42].

2-1-4 Drugs 
There are many different types of drugs that could be used in PDCs, each having a different type of 
mechanism for how they bring about cell death. The most ideal type of drug for Targeted Drug 
Delivery (TDD) will be a drug that is so cytotoxic, that it cannot be used in its free form. This will 
make the use of PDCs more attractive, since it allows for the use of more potent drugs, without 
having (as) severe off-target side effects [16]. 
Drugs that were considered for this project included SN-38 (active form of Irinotecan), 
Thapsigargin, Mertansine, Cabotegravir, Monomethyl Auristatin E and Doxorubicin (all shown in 
Figure 2-10). Doxorubicin is a drug that is usually used in research regarding the selectivity of drug 
carrying vehicles, to compare the potency of the vehicle to the potency of other vehicles and the 
free drug itself. The other drugs were considered, because they are currently used, either as a 
prodrug, or as the warhead of ADCs. They are considered too toxic to be used without having them 
reversibly inactivated [43]. 
Monomethyl Auristatin E (MMAE) was selected, because of its strong cytotoxicity (LD50 of 1 
mg/kg, [44]), because it is already used in ADCs on the marked (Adcetris®) and because it would 
be elegant to have a peptide conjugated to a largely peptide-based drug. MMAE would be easy to 
conjugate to the linker, since the nucleophile is a secondary amine, which would form a very stable 
carbamate bond with the PAB-OH of the linker, compared to otherwise considered carbonate and 
maleimide bonds with an oxygen or sulphur nucleophile.

Once all four peptides have been cyclised, they will be activated so that they can react with the

chapter 1, the PAB-OH should be turned into an activated carbonate. This is done according to

carbonate on the C-terminal of the peptide, that can then react with MMAE. The reaction with 
MMAE would likewise be done according to literature [49], in the presence of a non-nucleophilic 
base (DIPEA) and HOAt. HOAt is an additive usually used in peptide synthesis to inhibit 
cyclisation and racemisation of the activated amino acid. In this reaction it is used as an additive to 
facilitate the carbamate formation [49]. 
With the drug conjugated to the peptide, the last step of the synthesis is to deprotect the entire 
molecule. This is done with the use of concentrated TFA and other scavengers.

2-2-4 In vitro analysis 
The in vitro tests are planned to measure both the toxicity of each of the PDCs and compare it to the 
toxicity of free MMAE. This would have been done similarly to other PDC studies [34]. For this 
method, cells would be harvested using trypsin to a well-plate to be incubated. Afterwards the cells 
would be treated with various concentrations of the different PDCs and free MMAE. After 
incubation a AlmarBlue™ assay would be performed. AlmarBlue™ would be added to each well, 
and after a short incubation period, fluorescence at λ = 590 nm would be measured. In living cells, 
AlmarBlue™ consist of a dye called resazurin. Resazurin is weakly fluorescent, but in living cells, 
resazurin is reduced to resorufin, which is highly fluorescent. The cell viability can be measured by 
how much resorufin is formed by measuring the fluorescents [50]. 
This experiment would be replicated for HeLa cells (cervical cancer cells [51]), HEK-293 (healthy 
human kidney cells [52]) and MCF-7 (Human breast cancer cells [53]) to compare the cytotoxcicty 
of each PDC in each types of cells.

Discussion 
In this section the achieved results will be described and discussed. Furthermore, there will be 
commented on the reactions and their results. Finally, the experiments that were not executed due to 
time constraints will be discussed as suggestions for further research. 
3-1 Preloading 
Before any of the peptides could be synthesised, a resin was needed to be prepared. It was necessary 
to be able to cleave the finished peptide from the resin under mild conditions, so that no other 
protecting groups would be removed.  
3-1-1 N-Fmoc-p-aminobenzyl alcohol 
Commercially available PAB-OH needed to be protected with an Fmoc group. This reaction was 
attempted twice, with different starting materials. In the first attempt, the reaction was attempted 
with Fmoc-Cl dissolved in dioxane, dropwise added to another solution of PAB-OH in dioxane. In 
the second attempt, the starting material was Fmoc N-hydroxysuccinimide ester, with the same 
equivalents and procedures as the first. Another difference was that the second attempt used freshly 
purchased PAB-OH, compared to an old bottle of the first attempt. Neither products of the 
syntheses were purified, but instead thoroughly washed with water. This would remove any 
unreacted PAB-OH, which was necessary to do before loading onto the resin. If there were any 
unreacted PAB-OH, the aniline type nitrogen would be a better nucleophile compared to the benzyl 
alcohol. This would cause the eventual coupling to MMAE to be very different, since the link is no 
longer a carbamate but a carbamide, which would not have the same drug release mechanism as the 
carbamate. Since the products were never purified, the final yield could only be determined as crude 
yield. The yield of the first synthesis was 4.3 g (12.46 mmol, 73 %) and the second synthesis was 
5.9 g (17.08 mmol, 95 %). The NMR of the product showed that only the desired compound was 
obtained, see section 7-2-1. 
==PAGE:35==
Page 35 of 110 
 
3-1-2 2-Chlorotrityl resin bound Fmoc-PAB-OH 
This reaction was attempted trice. The first attempt was done with the first batch of Fmoc-PAB-OH, 
while strictly adhering to the literature [45]. This meant that the Fmoc-PAB-OH, 2-chlorotrityl 
chloride resin and pyridine was added to THF and heated to 60 °C. The heating was done using a 
heating mantle with a reflux condenser to ensure that the THF did not evaporate from the system. 
Because the heating mantle was necessary, stirring had to be done with a magnetic stirring bar. This 
is a problem since a too fast stirring can destroy the resin beads, so very mild stirring was necessary. 
While this reaction was successful, with a loading measured by UV-Vis [54] to be 0.51 mmol/g, the 
reaction still needed to be repeated, because there were not enough loaded Fmoc-PAB to synthesise 
all four peptides. The yield, determined by the loading, was found to be 573.342 µmol (33.5 %). 
The second attempt was done with the second batch of Fmoc-PAB-OH which should be more pure. 
It was attempted to optimize the reaction by using DMF as a solvent instead of THF, because most 
SPPS reactions uses DMF as a solvent, and was expected that DMF thus would be a better solvent 
for the loading as well. Furthermore, it was attempted to perform the reaction without heating. This

After the cyclisation, the next step was to cleave the peptide from the resin. This was done with the 
same procedure as all of the small-scale reactions, treating the resin with HFIP:DCM (25:75) for 1 
h. However, when this was done with the first full scale reaction, almost no peptide was cleaved 
form the resin. When the cleavage procedure was repeated, a similar small amount of the peptide 
was cleaved off. The first reasoning for why this had happened, was that reaction was done with L-
Phe and not D-Phe. Short peptides are difficult to backbone cyclise because all of the peptides are 
pseudo double bonds, and all are in a pseudo trans state. This makes the cyclisation of 5 L-amino 
acids problematic[55]. There are workarounds for this problem, including using proline (pseudo cis 
state), using pseudo prolines or using D-amino acids. It was at this point the error of using L-Phe 
was discovered, and the synthesis restarted with D-Phe. 
Once the second attempt reached this step of the synthesis, there was, however, still a problem with 
the cleavage. A new hypothesis for why the cleavage did not work as well was proposed. It might 
be, that when the Pd catalyst was used in the de-allylation step, the Pd could have made an 
oxidative addition reaction on the 2-chlorotrityl resin, which later was quenched. This would mean 
that the resin at this point in the synthesis is a trityl resin rather than 2-chlorotrityl resin. This would 
further mean that the reason why the peptide is only sparsely cleaved is that the resin is not as 
susceptible to cleavage, and more acidic conditions are required. It was attempted to use TFA (5 % 
in DCM) instead to cleave the resin, and a lot more peptide was recovered by doing this.

Purification by HPLC: 
Once the peptide had been cleaved from the resin, it was to be purified using preparative HPLC. 
According to previous LCMS analyses, there was a better separation between the peaks when using 
a C4 column compared to a C18 column. This might be because the more lipophilic C18 column 
makes the protected peptide bind better to the column material, making the separation worse. This 
is in contrast to "normal" peptide synthesis with full deprotection upon cleavage from the resin, 
where there are no lipophilic protection groups to bind strongly to the C18 column material. The 
only C4 column that was available was a small one (30 × 150 mm), which meant that the 
purification needed to be done over two runs, where only half of the compound was loaded onto the 
column at a time. 
The two first runs resulted in with no significant peaks. This is probably because the peptide was 
loaded onto the column with a too high concentration of MeCN. Because of this, all the fractions 
with any UV activity was collected, pooled, diluted in H2O, AcOH and purified again. This time, 
there were clean peaks to be collected, however, upon UPLC and LCMS analysis, it was revealed 
that one of these peaks consisted of two co-eluting compounds, the fully protected monomer, and a 
dimer with three missing tBu. In order to separate these two compounds, a fourth HPLC was 
performed, this time using NH4HCO3 as an ion pairing agent. This would mean that the HPLC 
would be run at a higher pH, and thus the free carboxylic acids of the unwanted dimer would be 
deprotonated. This would mean the dimer would have a negative charge, and be better souble in 
water, making it leave the column faster than the desired product. This made it possible to isolate 
the desired product. Pure fractions with product were pooled and freeze dried, the overall yield of 
the synthesis was found to be 21 mg (4.8 %) equivalent to around 15 % pr. step. This very poor 
yield is probably because of the large amount of compound that dimerised and thus became 
unusable, and the fact that multiple purifications were necessary after the cyclisation, losing a lot of 
compound on the column. 
It was decided not to advance this synthesis anymore and focus on the three others with better 
yields. If this synthesis were to be replicated it would need to be from a lower loaded resin, in order 
to reduce the dimerisation. Another optimisation would be an alternative to eliminate the use of Pd 
for the deprotection of the α-carboxylic acid. It is not confirmed that the Pd was the reason for the 
difficulty of the cleavage from the resin, but it is probable. If the α-carboxylic acid was protected by 
another orthogonal protecting group like the silane based Tmsi or a photocleavable protecting group 
like pHP, the use of TFA for resin cleavage would not be necessary, and that would mean a lower 
risk of partial deprotection on the tBu groups.

4 
Conclusion 
The goal of this project was to chemically design and synthesize peptide-drug conjugates with four 
different cyclic peptides conjugated to a cytotoxic drug. A possible strategy for synthesizing these 
from individual peptides and readily available chemicals has been described. The project involved 
syntheses of PDCs involved 23 different intermediates and products. During the experimental work 
most of the intermediates have been isolated and all the isolated products have been characterised 
by mass spectrometry. In the performed syntheses most of the products have been purified with the 
use of HPLC. 
It was possible to synthesise three of the four described peptide-drug conjugates. The reason why 
the final PDC was not synthesised was unforeseen problems with de-allylation and cyclisation. If 
the synthesis of this peptide were to be replicated, a new strategy for how to make the final PDC has 
been described.  
The strategy for synthesising the PDCs was designed to conjugate the drug at the very last step 
before the complete deprotection. This allows for safer handling of the peptides, and also makes it 
easier to further the study of PDCs. Since the drug was added at such a late point, it is easy to 
change which drug to use. 
==PAGE:50==
Page 50 of 110 
 
Preliminary in vitro studies shows that the three synthesised PDCs have a strong effect on cell 
viability. Since the studies only were preliminary, the accuracy of the data is not great, which means 
that it is difficult to calculate the IC50 values of the PDCs. This means that more studies are 
necessary to compare the three synthesised PDCs. 
PDCs are a promising alternative to current cancer therapy, and it is envisioned that it is possible to 
optimise the peptide for affinity to cancer cells. The design and synthesis of four different peptides 
in parallel can be expanded to any peptides that have shown affinity to cancer cells, and it is 
possible to use the describe design to synthesis multiple different PDCs with different peptides, 
linkers and drugs. 
There are still many different peptides and different synthesis pathways which can be investigated 
for further research in targeted delivery of drugs by conjugation with cyclic peptides.

The peptides were synthesised by the solid phase method using the Fmoc strategy, starting with 
preloaded Fmoc-PAB-OH. Solid-phase peptide synthesis was performed on a Prelude Solid Phase

5-4 In vitro toxicity [56] 
Cytotoxicity against the human cell line HeLa was evaluated at 72 h using AlamarBlue (Thermos 
Scientific, Kansas, USA). The assay was performed in 96 well plates (Costar 3595, Corning, New 
York, USA), with an assay volume of 200 µL. HeLa cells were cultured at 37 °C in a 5 % 
CO2 incubator and seeded in the plate with a cell density of 1.5 × 105 cells/mL. After 24 h the cells 
were treated with 200 nL the compounds to a final concentration of 20, 10, 5, 2.5, 1.25, 0.625, 
0.3125 µM (dissolved in DMSO) using an Echo 550 liquid handler (Labcyte, California, USA) to 
dispense. After 72 h of incubation in a 5 % CO2 incubator at 37 °C, 20 µL of AlamarBlue™ were 
added to each well and the cells were further incubated under the same conditions for 5 h. 
Fluorescence (excitation: 544 nm, emission: 590 nm) was detected by a plate reader. Control wells 
were treated with DMSO only and used as the maximal cellular growth reference, while wells 
containing only media were used to perform the background signal correction. The inhibitor 
MMAE dissolved in DMF (10 µM as final concentration) and 0.1% DMF were use further controls.
