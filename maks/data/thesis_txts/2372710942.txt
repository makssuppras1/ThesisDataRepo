Abstract
The major role that renewable sources of electricity are playing and will play, as well as the
emergence of smart houses and distributed storage is creating a new paradigm: the distribution
level is becoming more and more central in the system, and the consumer is bound to play a
major role in the future energy system. However, due to a lack of possible application it is
still uncertain as to how the consumers could be involved in the most eﬃcient way. There is
a will for consumers to adapt the system, their raising awareness calls for local and renewable
generation, however the structure is not their to allow them to have a real impact. One of the
direction for consumer involvement is the creation of local market place for electricity trading
also known as peer-to peer electricity markets. This thesis proposes a design for a Peer-to-peer
electricity market with a focus on consumers’ expression of preferences.
This design relies on two major concepts: Multi-bilateral trading and product diﬀerentiation.
Multi-bilateral trading, allows to trade directly between producers and consumers while con-
serving the competitive aspects of a pool-based market place.
Product diﬀerentiation is a
framework for consumers to include preferences for non-monetary characteristics of electricity
in the economic dispatch. These preferences as mentioned could be toward local and clean
generation of electricity, but it could also be for reliability and quality of supply or for non
rational aspects as social relations.
This thesis explores the possibilities oﬀered by the proposed Multi-Bilateral Economic Dispatch
(MBED) from the expressed consumer preferences to a distributed implementation of taxes.
The model is also simulated against a proposed setup in order to understand the economical
impact of this framework, the critical parameters for its implementation and its eﬃciency to
answer to consumer preference and is resilience to consumer strategic behaviour.
In order to understand how this market could coexist with the current wholesale markets,
an interaction framework is created and the peer-to peer market is connected to a wholesale
market with a variable degree of coupling.
The underlying goal, is to see if these kind of
markets suﬀer from imperfect coupling and if the partition of the pool of participants into
smaller diﬀerentiated market can aﬀect those who stay in a pool-based market.
Overall it comes out that the product diﬀerentiation is an eﬃcient framework for consumers
to express their preferences as well as for them to have a signiﬁcant impact on the generation
mix. By creating diﬀerent clustered products, the MBED model, gives a separate value to each
of them based on the demand and supply of these products. If this ﬁxes the cost for consumers
to satisfy their preferences, it is also an incentive for new generation unit to help match these
preferences.
As for the interaction with the wholesale market, it appears that a coupling can eﬃciently reduce
cost on the P2P market while maintaining a high level of preference satisfaction. However, the
setup of the interaction is very sensitive and can quickly lead to market distorsion or loss of
eﬃciency, which plead for a stronger interaction framework for the community manager.
Keywords: electricity markets, economic dispatch, peer-to-peer markets, product diﬀerentia-
tion, Bilateral trading, ﬂexibility, Consumer involvement, consensus+innovations, distributed
optimization

Introduction 1
Growing climate and environmental concerns have led European countries under the push of
the European Union to increase rapidly their share in renewables energy generation. These
rapid changes are not without challenges: the generation mix becomes is ever more ﬂuctuating
and unpredictable, the generation is more distributed with more and more consumers turning
into prosumers and becomes less and less aligned with the classic top-down structure of the
grid. In parallel, the technological evolution in data acquisition (72% of European households
will equipped by smart meters by 2020[1]) communication and management, have rendered
possible smart housing technologies and internet of things (IoT) which combined with increas-
ing storage capabilities (EV, battery, heat storage. . . ) render the consumers more ﬂexible.
Overall the vertical structure of the system is evolving toward a ﬂatter structure where the
ﬂexibility is shared between generation and consumption as well as between transport grids
and distribution grid.
This duality is translated in the two main, opposite but not exclusive, directions for the
evolution of the electricity grid:
1. A call for ever more integration of electricity market and network
2. A need for more intelligence and ﬂexibility on the distribution level.
The ﬁrst direction remains in the old hierarchical, centralized structure and is being pushed by
the EU, with an increase of interconnection capacity and an ever-larger coupling of electricity
markets. The second direction, however, although agreed upon, is slowed down by a lack of
regulatory framework that renders business opportunities in this sector risky or not possible.
However, this regulatory framework is starting to appear: in February 2017 was enacted a law
which allows for “collective auto-consumption” [2]. This law opens the way for private man-
agement of micro grid as well as speciﬁc tariﬀs for consumers using Auto-consumption up to
100kW[3].
This framework opens the way for auto-consuming energy communities, deﬁned in the law as a
collective where “the supply of electricity is between one or several producers and one or several
and consumers linked together as a legal entity and whose output and injection points are on
the same low voltage branch of the public distribution grid ”. This kind of community requires
a market-like structure to arbitrate and value the participation of each consumer to it. This
market could be centralized with a managing agent that would oﬀer contract in the way of a
retailer, but it could also open the way for to beneﬁt from consumer ﬂexibility through a local
electricity market.

1.1
The stakes of product diﬀerentiation and
Peer-to-Peer electricity markets
The challenges introduced by renewable generation are hardly expressed by a single price as in a
pool-based market. For instance, the grid’s congestions are only considered at an international
level as price zones of wholesale electricity markets are usually the size of a country. The stamp
tariﬀprinciple, although socially valuable, has the disadvantage of not reﬂecting the true cost
of electricity consumption, cost that is becoming ever more variable at the distribution level
due to distributed generation. Additionally, other externalities, such as greenhouse gases are
not properly internalized: emissions of a power plant might not have the same impact in a
polluted area as it has in a non-populated area. This means that a uniform tax as the EU-ETS
might not be the most eﬃcient way to internalize emissions. The question of collective auto-
consumption raises another dimension, as a quantity produced inside the community will have
a diﬀerent value than the same quantity produced outside the community. Other consumer
oriented dimension such as reliability and quality of the supply could also be added
It appears that electricity can no longer be described as a homogeneous product and that it
can no longer be described only by the generation cost. This problem can be tackled by using
concept of product diﬀerentiation [4] also called multi-attribute trading[5]. This concept recog-
nizes electricity needs to be described by several parameters and that consumers have diﬀerent
preferences with regard to these parameters. Using product diﬀerentiation, means that market
participant will pay for aspects of the electricity product that are not related only to power
and energy. The goal here is to take the best advantage of prosumer and consumer ﬂexibility
to react on these externalities and preferences, and this is reached using an economic dispatch
optimization model. To take into account local aspects and individual preferences, the model is
based on Peer-to-Peer (P2P) trading as proposed in [6] or Bilateral trading which means that
instead of considering only the quantity it produces or consumes, each market participant will
trade directly with other agents
P2P trading as well as an economic theory is here the tool that enable product diﬀerentiation.
It is a framework that allows to involve directly the individual consumer and put him in a
position where he can inﬂuence the outcome of the market in all the dimensions of product
diﬀerentiation. A P2P model, is the step from a system-centred pool-based market to a con-
sumer oriented market. It opens the way for distributed optimization and micro-trades with
consensus through blockchain technology.
The preferences in this report are introduced as linear responses to multi-attribute parameters.
In practice product diﬀerentiation is already possible through bilateral contracts or PPA for big
consumers and producers. However, retailers begin to oﬀer product diﬀerentiation to household
consumers [7] [8] by gathering diﬀerentiated needs and negotiating bilateral contracts. These
oﬀers usually have an extra cost compared to regular oﬀers. This extra cost is the valuation of
the diﬀerentiation. In [7] the valuation for renewable energy is of about 15% of the price. In
literature, [4] uses block rate for product diﬀerentiation whereas in [9] product diﬀerentiation
is based on a reputation parameter that allows consumers to grade producers.

nalities. It can be applied for diﬀerent types of implementation as the consumer-side preference
framework as mentioned but could also be implemented to apply taxes or grid tariﬀs. This

Concretely using distributed optimization removes the need for a retailer.

Modelling framework 2
In this chapter, a model is introduced for Multi-Bilateral trading of electricity on a Peer-to-
Peer level using product diﬀerentiation. The goal is to organize trades of electricity within a
community in a way that minimizes cost of production (negative proﬁts of consumption) while
taking into account individually deﬁned preferences of each agents in the community.
This chapter focuses on setting up the parameters and constraints of the model that will
be called the Multi-Bilateral Economic Dispatch model (MBED). The concepts of agent
preferences and product diﬀerentiation will be introduced. In the whole chapter as well as
in the rest of the report, power is the quantity traded with the convention that a power P > 0
is quantity sold or produced and a power P < 0 is a quantity bought or consumed.

2.2
Model formulation
Deﬁnition: Peer-to-Peer
Peer-to-peer (P2P) is a term that originally comes from computer science to describe a com-
munication network on which each participant can establish a communication with any other
agent, and where all participant will be considered as equal. Peer-to-peer can be used to col-
lectively solve tasks in a distributed manner or to exchange information or data without any
intermediary. That is where is diﬀers the most from the client/host classical model: it allows
to get rid of any unnecessary intermediary.
When applied to electricity markets, this concepts aims at removing the layer of the market
operator, and allow peer to directly trade from producer to consumer. In such an approach
all consumers and producers regardless of their size are equal interlocutors and are part of a
multi-bilateral trading negotiations in which they play an active role.
In this section we present a mathematical formulation for a P2P electricity market stated as a
cost minimization problem with constraint.

Application framework 3
In this chapter we develop a setup for the MBED model to be simulated against. Some key
aspects are developed here as the modelization of ﬂexibility or the speciﬁcity of a P2P model.
3.1
Preliminary discussions
3.1.1
Assumption of geographical proximity
For this setup it has been chosen to implement the distance criteria. A P2P market is very local
market and the distance criteria will provide an interesting stimulation for such a geographical
community.
To match this criteria, the setup is build around two villages/neighbourhood
distant from each other by 10 km.
This assumption of close geographical proximity is not without consequences. It means indeed
that renewables generation asset will most likely see their generation series be very correlated.
To take that into account when building the model, an eﬀort is made to make sure that wind 3.2)
and solar (3.2.2) series are correlated among each other. This will allow to have a challenging
setup for the simulation where variations of total wind and solar generation in each village are
as high as possible which will give a better conﬁdence in the resilience of the model.
3.1.2
Price setting in a zero marginal cost environment.
It could be expected that a consumer-oriented P2P market would rely only on distributed re-
newable generation, whos marginal costs are near zero.
The question of price settings arises then as it would in any environment where marginal costs
tend to 0. If we consider a system with only zero marginal cost renewables, the prices will be
set up only by consumers’ utility function, which could if consumers play strategically end up
with prices converging to 0 or to inﬁnity depending on the level of generation compared to the
demand. As such the prices would be most often driven by the wholesale market prices which
act as price boundaries. Several solutions can be considered.
1. In a case with a lot of prosumers that alternatively sell or by electricity, they have no
interest in dragging the prices fully down when buying their electricity as it would also
lower their proﬁt when the situation changes and they start selling electricity. A game
theory approach of this system could lead to a price setting diﬀerent than wholesale prices.
2. In the case where the P2P system is a localized community (where trust is assumed), the
consumer of the community could agree on a non strategic behaviour and a price level to
play around
3. Preferences could be used to create virtual prices based on non-monetary characteristics
of generation assets

As this could be the topic of a whole other thesis, this topic is not further investigated
here. Instead, conventional fossil generators are introduced as price setters. The role of these
generators is just to give a signal of imbalances such that prices would rise as a result of a
renewable generation scarcity. As a result, little focus is given to modelling them realistically.

3.2.1
Wind turbines
For the simulations, the wind turbine generation data are from the Australian Electricity Mar-
ket Operator (AEMO) and where processed by Stefanos Delikaraoglou and Jethro Dowell for
[11]. It consists of one year of generation data of 22 wind turbines in south east Australia in
2012/2013 with a resolution of 5 min The resolution of the data was adapted to a one hour
resolution to suit the rest of the data available.
In [11], regions are represented of geographically close wind turbines, however the average
distance between turbines in those region is of an order of magnitude of a hundred of kilometers,
which does not allow for constructive simultaneous correlations. To elude the problem, sets of
wind turbine have been selected to form villages based on the value of their covariance as seen
in ﬁgure 3.1.
The wind turbines can thus be selected among two ’wind farms’ of 5 (n° 11-14-15-18-21) and 4
(n° 1-3-13-22) turbines which covariance matrix can be seen in 3.2
The other wind series will be used to model the wholesale market, where the geographical size
of the market reduces, de facto, correlation.
3.2.2
Solar photovoltaics and consumption
Household consumption and generation data are obtained from Ausgrid as detailed in [12]. It
consists of one year of consumption and rooftop solar PV generation of 300 household in New
South Wales, Australia for the same year as the AEMO wind data and with a resolution of 30
minutes. Similarly to the AEMO data, the resolution is adjusted to 1 hour.
Solar Photovoltaic
Similarly to the wind data, for a eﬃcient P2P setup, correlation of solar series is required. In
the Ausgrid data, for each consumer/PV producer, the post code is given which gives a solid

3.3
Modelisation of ﬂexibility
The modelling of consumer ﬂexibility is a sensitive topic for a small scale market. In order
to be able to respond to signals sent by the market such as perceived prices and to have a
signiﬁcant impact on the system with their preferences or else, individual consumers need to
be ﬂexible. Indeed, an inﬂexible consumer (with an inelastic demand) cannot react to prices,
and therefore has no conscious impact on prices and his preferences make little diﬀerence from
the system’s perspective. There are several ways a consumer can introduce ﬂexibility into his
household.
3.3.1
Impact of ﬂexibility on the market
The goal of introducing demand side ﬂexibility is to be able to balance the system more easily
to deal with the loss of ﬂexibility from the generation side. However depending on the way the
ﬂexibility is modeled, the capacity involved for ﬂexibility might not be used eﬃciently.
The main parameters of the ﬂexibility are the ones of the utility curve: Pn and Pn that deﬁne
the capacity of ﬂexibility and an and bn that deﬁne the price range and price elasticity of
demand response. If the two last parameters are not well scaled, the ﬂexibility capacity of an
agent might not have any impact on the system.
We assume in the following study that the power boundaries (capacity of ﬂexibility) are ﬁxed
and we try to look at the impact of parameter an and bn on the system.
For instance, lets look at the demand response of a single consumer that acts as a price taker for
diﬀerent deﬁnitions of his utility under a ﬁxed price distribution as in ﬁgure 3.5. This example
is in a pool-based market but the results would be the same in MBED setup. In the ﬁgure, the
price distribution is represented on the same plot as the utility curve although with an adapted
scale.

The best way to chose bn is such that the utility curve is centered around the price distribution.
Given that, an increase of the slope an will increase the probability of being elastic: p = P(Pn ∈

decrease in the slope will make the consumer reactive to price variations, it will also decrease the
said probability of being elastic, which means that it will increase the probability of producing
Pn or Pn where the consumer will no longer react to price variations. A balance has then to
be found between maximizing the probability of being elastic and maximizing the elasticity to
make the resulting power distribution as ﬂat as possible.
To sum-up, the best choice of an, bn parameters for the system (the one that oﬀers the most
ﬂexibility to the system) requires a precise knowledge of the market as well an eﬃcient price
forecasting capability.

for the system (at given capacity of ﬂexibility. For instance if a capacity of ﬂexibility is linked
to a high loss of comfort, then the consumer will only be willing to reduce his consumption
when the prices are very high. On the other hand, if the purpose of the ﬂexibility of a consumer
is reduce the electricity bill, then the needs of the society and of the consumer might become
aligned.
A study of the sources of ﬂexibility is therefore required to determine how the utility function
should be deﬁned.
The situation is a bit diﬀerent though if the consumers are price maker, meaning that it is
assumed that consumers have roughly the same behaviour and that a choice of one consumer
is representative of a larger group.

4.1.3
Vision of the preferences
As mentioned earlier, the preference criteria creates a diﬀerence between the real price, used for
actual exchange of money and the perceived prices used as a comparison tool between trades
by the agents. The question that arises then is: which one should we consider when calculating
the proﬁts or costs of each agents?
The MBED model presented with the introduction of bilateral trading and preference criteria
is a framework that allows for product diﬀerentiation. However this framework is not related
to a philosophy of market structure (distributed optimization vs centralized optimization -
free agents vs controlled agents) , it could be applied with very diﬀerent interpretation of the
preference criteria which could have a major inﬂuence on how we analyze its impact. Two
approach are presented here and will be used for the simulations:
1. The consumer-side awareness. In this case the preference criteria is introduced for
the consumer to express a will to consume an electricity product with certain charac-
teristics. It could be a will to consume locally in the case of a distance criteria or to
consume renewables in the case of emissions. However, the consumer will rarely express
these will directly in monetary terms. The monetary impact of the criteria (the term cn)
is instead more a tool to reach a bigger goal. This goal could be for instance to consume
90% of locally produced electricity or 70% of electricity produced from renewables.The
preference value can be chosen at will by the consumer.
Here we get hold of one of the main advantages oﬀered by product diﬀerentiation and
multi-bilateral trading that is to oﬀer a certain traceability of electricity. This traceability
is certainly not physical as electricity transport is governed by laws that are not handled
at this stage, but it is monetary. By allowing to pay diﬀerent prices for diﬀerent elec-
tricity products, the model allows the consumer to have an impact on the general mix.
Associating the mix of electricity he consumes to the one he bought through bilateral
trading is an easy and transparent reward for this impact.
In this case, the value of the preference has no absolute meaning other than through the
impact on the consumption mix that it generates. There is no will from the consumer
side to pay more than necessary. A case to understand this would be to consider a system
powered with 100% renewable. Here the value of the preference would have no impact on
32
==PAGE:41==
Challenges and opportunities related to peer-to-peer electricity markets
the consumption mix. A rational consumer would then set it to 0. The preference criteria
creates market for electricity products with diﬀerent characteristics and the impact of the
preference value is the result of demand and supply of this speciﬁc product. This opens
the way for a consumer that reacts dynamically to the state of the system. In other
worlds the consumer wants to reach his goal (of for instance 90% of local consumption
and uses the preferences to reach this goal in the most cost eﬃcient way in the long run.
As a consequence, the perceived prices should not be used to calculate cost/proﬁt of a
consumer/producer. The price that should be used here is the real price. An increase in
the preference criteria of a consumer will most likely result in an increase of his cost but
this increase should then be balanced by the corresponding increase in local consumption
(if that is the goal).
The preferences can be applied by both consumers and producers or by consumers alone.
2. The centralized implementation. Under this approach, the community observes a
certain cost to operate and wishes to integrate this cost into the model in order to make
it more eﬃcient and to make the agents responsible for it pay. It is about integrating
negative externalities as an additional cost.
A example for this approach, would be a privately owned micro-grid with possibly dif-
ferent voltage level with it’s own transformers. There is a cost in operating this grid
and using the transformers and the community of the micro-grid to implement a MBED
model to handle trades within the micro-grid. A preference criteria is implemented with a
uniform value chosen by the community, the preference payment is actually here a "tax"
that has to be paid on each trade. The particularity of this "tax" is that it is not paid
proportionally to the total power consumption but instead a speciﬁc tariﬀis applied to
each bilateral trade. This "tax" might be on top of the existing taxes or instead of it,
that is not the point of the discussion here.
As a consequence, it is to be understood that real prices here represent the price before
taxes and that perceived prices, that are prices after taxes, are the ones used to calculate
the cost of buying electricity for a consumer or selling it for a producer. A degree of
freedom is given to the system, to implement the tax payment only to consumers or both
to consumers and producers. The model however shows that these two approaches are
the same provided that the uniform preference value of the ﬁrst approach is double the
one of the second approach.
These two visions of the preferences will be crucial in the way we analyze the results of the
model.
In the rest of this chapter, the criteria used is the distance criteria.
Note that the distance used in the distance criteria is the euclidean distance.
It could be
argued that the euclidean distance between two agents has not necessarily a strong link to how
a trade between these two agents impact the grid usage. Two arguments are answered to this
interrogation:
• The distance criteria is a simple and transparent criteria that allows to study the impact of
such a framework. The same framework could then be applied with an "electric distance"
33
==PAGE:42==
Decentralized optimization in a peer-to-peer electricity market based on bilateral trades
that would be more realistic. The distance between agents would diﬀer from the euclidean
distance but the impact of the distances would be similar.
• An "electric distance" would be somehow linked to the euclidean distance. Indeed the
cost of transport of electricity other than losses on the lines are induced by the use of grid
infrastructure for instance for voltage changes through transformers. Now, the voltage
levels are increasing with the distance of transport (the losses over long distances make
high voltages more cost eﬃcient). In the end the cost of grid usage is positively aﬀected
by the distance. A linear approximation is a simple approach to describe it.

4.3
Impact of consumers’ decision
In this section, we try to understand how the P2P market will be aﬀected if the consumers are
given the possibility to choose their own preference value. We give up here the idea of a common
value of preferences. This means that the vision of preferences here is still the consumer-side
awareness where consumers try to increase their local consumption share at a certain cost.
A small modiﬁcation has been made to the setup in this case to avoid possibilities of arbitrage:
indeed when having diﬀerent preference values, the cost of trades due to preferences might no
longer verify a triangular inequality, meaning that a direct trade between two agents n and m
might be less proﬁtable for the system that agent n selling to a intermediary prosumer l and
prosumer l selling it back to agent m as illustraded in ﬁgure 4.11.
In such a case, the path n −l −m on the graph costs less than the path n −m and is therefore
more eﬃcient. However this goes against the purpose of preferences: when agent m is buying
from agent l he is actually buying from agent n but his preference value only applies to the
trade with agent l. Agent l’s zero preference value actually diminishes the impact that the
preference value of agents n and m have.

4.4
Intermediate conclusion
The expression of preference should it be by both consumers and producers or only by consumers
creates a price gap between the two villages, that tells us that it is harder to increase the
internally traded share in the village 2 than the village 1. It is in a way an implementation
of the Polluters Pay Principle: the consumers pay more for their electricity as they need to
import more than village 1 and thus they induce more grid usage costs.
From a diﬀerent perspective, dynamically the systems creates the incentives to reduce the
imbalances: the high prices in village 2 are all the more incentive to reduce the consumption,
or to build new generation unit that will also beneﬁt from the local scarcity.
An attention has to be paid to the possibilities of arbitrage when the preferences are not
homogeneous to avoid free riders to beneﬁt from the preferences of others at the expense of
their preference satisfaction.

Wholesale market interaction 5
The goal of P2P trading is not to go completely of the grid. First, because a P2P system is
not necessarily geolocalised and linked to a local micro-grid. The grid might be needed for
some of the trades within the community. Second, a connection to the gird and the wholesale
market might be proﬁtable as it can allow to use ﬂexibility capacities of the P2P market to
take advantage of price ﬂuctuations on the wholesale market. And lastly, full autonomy can be
very costly due to renewable generation ﬂuctuation and it might be less costly to rely on the
grid sometimes rather than investing in additional infrastructure (Batteries, generation unit...)
to ensure feasibility. In this section, is presented a way for the P2P market to interact with
nation-wide electricity markets.
It has been chosen that the P2P market will interact only with the day-ahead market.

5.2.1
The need of a community manager
Some of the reasons why individual consumers are not allowed to bid directly on the wholesale
market are ﬁrst, the technical diﬃculty to allow direct biding from millions of consumers
and second the computational challenge of optimizing a system with millions of agents. By
introducing a P2P market, these challenges do not magically disappear.
The individual
consumers are still not allowed to bid on the wholesale market. However in the same way
as it works for a retailer, the community of a P2P market can group in order to bid together
on the market.To address this, one of the agent, that we call a community manager, plays also
the role (along with his classic trading role within the community) of collecting bids of agents
on the wholesale market and biding for them.
It is emphasized here that the community manager is not an external agent but rather a non
proﬁt member of the community that can be played out by each agent turn by turn. This means
that the P2P-wholesale interface is just a way for the P2P system to trade on the wholesale
market all-together without any proﬁt seeking.
The structure of this interface can be seen in ﬁgure 5.1. Basically, the community manager
bids and buy power on the wholesale market at a price λt and reﬂects this price to prosumer
and consumer that ponder if they want to sell/buy electricity on the market. In practice the
community manager sets a preference similarly to the P2P agents that allows him to sell/buy
at prices that are diﬀerent than market price.

P2P-Wholesale market interaction 6
In this chapter is simulated the HUMBED model presented in chapter 5. The goals behind
these simulations are to study:
• how the interaction can be priced in order to have an eﬃcient P2P market while taking
advantage of the wholesale market ﬂuctuations.
• how the P2P market and the wholesale market impact each other.
The second goal implies a certain equivalency in terms of weight of each market. Indeed if we put
together a wholesale market and a single community as in chapter 4, the small communities will
behave as a price taker and will not aﬀect the wholesale market. However if lots of community
are created, an impact can start to appear. In the previous chapter, the model introduced
does not represent a multitude of communities although the modelling would be very similar, if
every community appears as a single independent market participant of the wholesale market.
It was indeed chosen to model only one community for several reasons:
1. The problem could become quickly very computationally challenging.
2. A scenario with several communities is not likely to see these communities being
independent.
3. In a lesser measure, a lack of viable data.
4. the setup is secondary to the model under study here.
The underlying assumption, here is that the behaviour of a multitude of communities can be
approximated by a single for the limited purpose of the present study. However, one of the
requirements for modelling a community cannot be upscaled correctly: the need for correlation
in the wind and solar series that represents the geographical proximity of the generation unit as
presented in chapter 3. This correlation is important as it is one of the big diﬀerences between
a local market as the P2P and a national market as the wholesale. It creates a lot of ﬂuctuation
and stress on the P2P market, the reaction to which is part of the study.
A system with a multitude of community would have these correlations and stresses inside each
community, but these correlations would not necessarily appear when connected to a wholesale
market as they would be evened out on all communities. However these correlations are needed
for pricing inside a community. There is therefore two opposite needs that are incompatible
with the idea of a single community.
It was decided to keep the single community with correlated generation while neglecting the
approximation made for this purpose.

6.1.3
Stakes of the interaction
Possible distortion
The interaction between the wholesale and the P2P market relies in the framework of the
community manager as introduced in section 5.2.
However, this framework oﬀers a lot of
possibilities as to what it could mean for the interaction. A ﬁrst example would be direct
access to the wholesale market for all consumers without any additional cost of preferences
(γnW = γWn = 0). In such a case the result would be that all agents would trade directly
with the wholesale market and no trades would happen within the community. Again a graph
approach is here instructive: the 3 agents example from 4.11 is adapted to include community
manager as seen in ﬁgure 6.1. For any two agents n and m, the path n −m will always bear
more additional preference costs than the path n −W −m which bears 0 additional costs.
Thus, all the trades will be made with the community manager and no trade will be made
directly inside the community. As a result preference are not expressed and the HUMBED
model functions as a pool-based market. In this case the presence of a community manager
completely diminishes the impact of preferences.
To avoid this there is a need for expression of preferences toward trading with the wholesale
market (γnW ̸= 0 or cl ̸= 0 and γWn ̸= 0). The extreme case with those preferences is when
the additional cost of trading with the community manager is so high that it never happens.
In this case the two markets (the P2P and the wholesale) are completely decoupled and the
community manager does not play any role.
Philosophy of the interaction
The goal is now to study what happens in between those extreme cases but ﬁrst to give a
meaning to the role of the community manger
To understand the meaning of those preferences , we rely on the two visions of the
preferences as stated in subsection 4.1.3, the Consumer-side awareness and the Centralized
implementation. Each of them would correspond to a diﬀerent philosophy for the interaction:
• The consumer-side awareness: Under this vision, the community manager is just a
communication agent, in the sens that he expresses no preferences :cW = 0. The meaning
of a trade with the wholesale market is decided individually by each agent by setting the
value of γnW = γ+

Intermediate conclusion
The coupling of the two markets is necessary from the perspective of a P2P market: the high
degree of correlation combine with the small scale of the system can imply a very costly supply
to keep the market decoupled. Alternatively, when coupling the two markets it appears indeed
that costs are reduced on the P2P markets for the consumers, but some of the producers can

Conclusion and Future works 8
This chapter proposes a conclusion of the thesis as well as an opening of Future possible works.
8.1
Conclusion
This thesis has presented a model for a local peer-to-peer electricity market based on product
diﬀerentiation: the Multi-Bilateral Economic Dispatch.
As mentioned in introduction the
objective was dual, with two geographic scale of study. The ﬁrst scale was very local with
a study of the impact of product diﬀerentiation inside a local community while the second
was national with a study of the impact of the interaction between local markets and global
markets. It appeared during the thesis that a temporal duality also made sense. The classic
static approach is to study the direct response of the model to a setup and its parameters,
while a more dynamic approach is to market participant let periodically react on the result
of the static approach and strategically respond to these results. Both approach matters and
bring signiﬁcant results in this thesis.
A framework that can be adapted to several approaches:
Although implemented for the purpose of a consumer-side awareness expression of preferences
the Multi-Bilateral trading with product diﬀerentiation framework can also be implemented
for a more centralized approach for instance for a tax payment partition system.
• In the consumer-side awareness approach, the consumer deﬁnes the preferences to
compare oﬀers from diﬀerent producers, but still uses real prices for proﬁt evaluation.
The preference cost ∆nm is virtual.
• In the centralized implementation, a centralized agent uses the framework of product
diﬀerentiation to implement a fair partition of tax payments based on bilateral trades.
In this approach, the tax payment is directly reﬂected to those who cost the most
externalities to the system, and the tax payment is included in the economic dispatch in
order to minimize those externalizes (in other words, a polluters pay principle generalized
to all kinds of externalities).
Two these two approaches can also be added the multitude of criteria that can be implemented
as the distance or the emission criteria but also an internet platform based ranking of all agents
by their peers.
This freedom of design is also a danger as, a more complex expression of preferences could
also mean a great ineﬃciency of the system to respond to those preferences, as it does in the
presented simulations.
A model that reﬂects the general preferences
Overall the model in the corresponding setup with the distance criteria has shown great response
to preferences with internally traded shares increasing rapidly for low value of preferences. More
interesting is the duality of response that the model has shown in the villages. Villages 1 and
81
==PAGE:90==
Decentralized optimization in a peer-to-peer electricity market based on bilateral trades
2 were created on purpose with an excess renewable generation in the ﬁrst and a lack in the
second. Although applying the same preference criteria, this diﬀerence has lead to a price gap
appearing between the two markets. If proﬁt rose with the preference value in village 2, it
had a negative impact on proﬁt in village 1. The behaviour appears quite similar to a nodal
pricing here: product diﬀerentiation distinguishes to types of products: electricity generated
in Village 1 and electricity generated in village 2. Each product has a demand and a supply,
which creates a local market and explains the price gap between the two villages. This hints
at an alternative structure for a P2P market: the Hybrid clustered P2P market. The market
participant are clustered into villages and a PED is ran inside the village coupled with a MBED
between the villages. This is further detailed in 8.2.
A stable dynamic behaviour
The product diﬀerentiation approach creates a very competitive system, when a lot of beneﬁt
can be achieved in terms of preference satisfaction by guessing the preference value of other
agents. However, when given the possibility to change freely (but rationally) their preference
value, no instability where observed, and the preferences of each village converge toward value
that reﬂected the scarcity state of the village.
The dynamic behaviour of the producers is also stable: the price gap creates a higher value for
generation in village 2 and thus an incentive for additional generation unit to be built in this
village, or simply for a generation unit from village 2 to migrate from the wholesale market to
the P2P market, thus reducing the imbalances and by that making the generation mix more
suited to the consumers preference. That is one of the great strength of the MBED model: it
allows to send signals such that consumers can change the sahpe of the system.
A challenging interaction
The scaling of the interaction cost appears to be a critical parameter of the interaction design:
if it is to low, the community manager can be used to short-circuit the preference cost of
internal trades. On the other hand if it is too high, the two markets will be decoupled and the
P2P market will not beneﬁt from cost reduction due to coupling. For another perspective, the
interaction cost ﬁxed by the consumer, also limits the size of the P2P market. If the interaction
cost set by consumers corresponds to a radius of 20km, they will never trade with someone
further than this distance inside the P2P market.
However, if scaled properly, the coupling of the two market allows to reduce costs eﬃciently in
both markets without a signiﬁcant loss of preference satisfaction.
Additionally, producers especially of the village 1 have a great interest in lowering their
interaction costs to make the wholesale market accessible. By doing so they create an excess
generation on the wholesale market and a lack of generation on the P2P market which
completely changes their behaviour. The satisfaction of preferences inside the P2P market
becomes 5-10% higher.
8.1.1
A model that can be distributed
The Consensus+Innovation algorithm of the chapter 7 shows that the HUMBED model can
be solved with distributed optimization to justify the name of P2P market. This algorithm is
although quite sensible and needs a proper tuning of the parameters that can be diﬃcult.

8.2
Future works
8.2.1
Upscaling the P2P market
As mentioned previously, upscaling the P2P model can quickly intractable as the complexity of
the problem is more than quadratic with the number of agents. This hints for the upscaling more
toward a multitude of small community rather than a single large one. The framework of the
community manager can already be applied to manage the interactions between communities.
A proper study of how this partition into smaller communities could aﬀect the optimal cost of
the system should be conducted.
8.2.2
Structure of the upscaled set of community
As mentioned in 8.1 one of the possibility to structure the upscaled model is to use the clus-
tering of agents to run a PED model on the very local level and run the P2P level on a higher
level, where the number of agents is reduced.
Another option is the Russian doll (ﬁgure 8.1) structure where a P2P market is run on the
very local level (for the scale of microgrid), another one is run on higher level (for the scale of
a small city) and so on...
These structures allow to limit the complexity of the model while still keeping a connex model
to keep the communities coupled.

Distributed optimization

the wholesale part and the P2P part shuld be investigated, as it represents a great opportunity
to lower computational time, increase the convergence speed, and reduce instabilities of the
iterative process.

C.1
Storage devices
The easiest way to introduce ﬂexibility and possibly with the biggest impact.
• Devices: Electric Vehicle, Battery...
• Decision parameter: For EV it is the driving pattern, for batteries there is none.
• Temporal relation: The battery is limited in energy capacity, meaning that the sum of
ﬂexibility use is bounded.
• Instantaneous ﬂexibility capacity: The ﬂexibility capacity on any given time step is
bounded by the power capability of the battery or the EV. Typically 1-5kW for an EV [18]
• Flexible energy consumption per cycle Of the order of 20-40kWh (20kWh for a
BMW i3 and 40kWh for a Renault Zoe).
• Link to utility: In the case of a battery, the link to utility is purely monetary. The sole
purpose of the battery is to make money through time arbitrage of market prices. An
additional gain of utility can be considered if the battery is coupled with a solar panel,
allowing the consumer to consume its own electricity. For the EV, the driving pattern is
an absolute need (an inﬁnite utility loss if not met). Other than that, the link to utility
is the same as for the battery.
• Losses due to ﬂexibility: Charging and discharging losses are applied, typically of the
order of 10-20%

• Decision parameter: The temperature of the device is bound within an acceptable

• Temporal relation: The temperature is the result of cooling in the past hours and
natural losses.
• Instantaneous ﬂexibility capacity: Bound by cooling capacity of the device. Typi-
