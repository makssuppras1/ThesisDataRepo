Abstract
The shipping industry significantly contributes to global greenhouse gas emissions. This
contribution could potentially be diminished by the ship’s sailing speed. Decreasing port
turnaround times is one way to facilitate this. Recently, there has been an increased inter-
est in Roll-on Roll-off (RoRo) ship operations, including how tugs transporting non-rolling
cargo can utilize dual cycling, thereby reducing the number of empty tug trips. Jia Beizen’s
study [1] models dual cycling on RoRo ships carrying homogeneous cargo using a Gen-
eralized Random Key Algorithm (GRKA). This thesis builds upon this article by introduc-
ing heterogeneous cargo and investigating how different cargo compositions of standard
trailers and roll trailers affect the potential for dual cycling to reduce turnaround time. To
explore this, a set of training and test instances are developed. The training instances
help identify the most effective of multiple restart strategies for minimizing turnaround
times with the GRKA The findings, when evaluating the test instances reveal that stan-
dard trailers benefit more from dual cycling than roll trailers. Thus, as the proportion of
roll trailers increases, the effect of applying dual cycling decreases. The algorithm ana-
lyzes an instance based on a predefined stowage plan. Therefore, investigating how to
construct a stowage plan supporting dual cycling may increase the effect of dual cycling
on the turnaround time.

Introduction
Maritime transportation accounts for over 80% of global trade by volume [2]. The shipping
industry is estimated to be responsible for approximately 3% of worldwide greenhouse
gas emissions[3]. According to the goals set by the International Maritime Organisation
(IMO) in 2018 and revised in 2023, the international shipping industry aims to reduce
greenhouse gas emissions by 20-30% in 2030 compared to 2008 levels. Close to 2050,
net zero greenhouse gas emissions are expected to be achieved [4].
Different initiatives can be implemented to achieve the goals set by the IMO. These
include, among others, replacing traditional ship fuels with alternative fuels or energy
sources and enhancing operational efficiency in ports. One method to accomplish this is
to reduce the time ships spend in ports, the so-called turnaround time. This allows the
ships to sail at a lower speed on their voyage without compromising their time sched-
ules. Lowering the sailing speed is an effective tool to decrease the ship’s greenhouse
gas emissions since fuel consumption increases with the cube of speed [1]. In addition to
this, reducing the turnaround time of ships in ports also benefits the ports themselves, as
they can service more ships in a shorter period of time and thereby increase their revenue.
A key element in ensuring high efficiency within a port is to leverage its equipment to the
fullest. For handling container ships, this includes having the quay cranes preform dual
cycling (sometimes called double cycling). This effectively reduces the number of crane
moves necessary when loading and discharging the ships, thus allowing the ships to leave
the port faster. Dual cycling ensures that a quay crane cycle includes both the loading
and unloading of containers from the ship. This is in contrast to the single cycle where
the quay cranes first unload the ship entirely and then load it afterward. This method,
however, causes the quay cranes to only carry a load half of the time, which is highly
ineffective in addition to taking up an unnecessary large amount of terminal space [5].
Another ship type, which likewise benefits from introducing dual cycling is the Roll-on Roll-
off (RoRo) ship. RoRo ships differ from container ships in that they mainly carry rolling
cargo or alternative cargo without wheels, which can be loaded onto a tug (a small, high-
capacity truck built for maneuverability) during loading and unloading. A typical RoRo ship
features multiple decks of varying heights, some of which are hoistable. The decks are
connected with internal ramps. Moreover, the ships are equipped with an external ramp
placed at the stern that can be lowered to connect the main deck with the quay. In addition
to this ramp, some RoRo ships also have a side ramp which allows the ship to be loaded
and unloaded from different access points (figure 1.1). RoRo shipping is frequently used
within Europe with the shipping company Det Forenede Dampskibs-Selskab A/S (DFDS)
currently operating over 30 RoRo shipping routes, with the majority being shuttle services
[6], where the ship sails between two ports and is completely turned over at each port.

This type of ship typically contains different types of cargo. Therefore, the tugs can attach
various equipment, thus allowing the same tug to transport multiple cargo types [8]. The
SAT trestle system accommodates transportation of standard trailers (figure 1.2a). This
system is highly efficient due to the SATs deck-locking feature, which makes additional
lashing of the trailers redundant (figure 1.2b). When transporting large, heavy, or irregular
cargo not contained within a standard trailer, a roll trailer can be used for storing the cargo.
Tugs can be equipped with goosenecks, which enable them to drive the roll trailers on and
off the RoRo ship (figure 1.2c). As opposed to the standard trailers being secured to the
deck by the SATs themselves, the roll trailers need additional lashing, when loading.
The tugs can utilize dual cycling by driving one type of trailer onto the ship for loading and
exiting the ship carrying a similar trailer type for discharging. Thus, when performing dual
cycling, the tugs are utilized to their fullest. This is compared to single cycling, where they
only carry a trailer half of the time.
The Roll-on Roll-off Dual Cycling Problem (RRDCP) for shuttle services has until now only
been modeled for homogeneous cargo. Therefore, this thesis paper aims to answer the
question:
To what extent does dual cycling affect the turnaround time of Roll-on
Roll-off ships operating in a shuttle service carrying non-rolling
heterogeneous cargo, handled using different tug equipment?
To address the query, a mathematical model and a heuristic have been developed and
evaluated on a set of predefined instances. The following chapter reviews work pre-
viously conducted on dual cycling on RoRo ships. Next, chapter 3 presents a visual
representation of the modeled system, followed by an explanation of the mathematical
model, highlighting its assumptions. In Chapter 4, the principles of the Generalized Ran-
dom Key Algorithm (GRKA) are presented, together with a simple example of how to set
up a decoder. This is in chapter 5 followed by both a thorough explanation and exam-
ple of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), the unconstrained
continuous optimizer applied in the GRKA. Chapter 6 details the principle of the devel-
oped problem-specific decoder used together with the GRKA. Chapter 7 describes the
instances used in the project. Next, chapter 8 determines the exact search strategy ap-
plied to the GRKA and analyses the effect dual cycling can have on the turnaround time
of RoRo ships. Following this chapter 9 sums up the main results obtained in the thesis.

2
Prior work
In recent years, RoRo ships have gained an increased interest in academic research.
This mainly focuses on creating stowage plans that ensure the stability and safety of the
ship or reduce the need for shifting (temporary relocation of blocking cargo not meant for
discharge at a port) [10, 11, 12].
While multiple models have been developed to handle dual cycling on container ships,
showing that it can reduce the number of quay crane cycles by approximately 30-40% [5,
13, 14, 15], the literature covering dual cycling on RoRo ships is spare [1].
The RRDCP has, however, been modeled by Jia B. et al., who developed both a mathe-
matical model and a new heuristic approach to minimize the makespan of the discharge
and loading operations, using discrete time steps [1]. The RoRo ship was modeled to sail
in a shuttle service carrying only homogeneous cargo (unaccompanied trailers), which
was moved in and out of the ship using multiple tugs.
The article models the ship decks independently of each other, which is a common way
of modeling RoRo ships [11, 16]. Since the decks are all connected, the loading and
unloading procedure can take place simultaneously on multiple decks independently of
each other, assuming that the internal ramps are accessible. In the article, the decks are
represented using equally sized slots. Each slot can contain a single trailer at a time.
Precedence rules stating when a specific slot can be loaded or unloaded, depending on
the occupancy of the neighboring slots, are included in the model to ensure adherence to
safety requirements.
The article develops a new metaheuristic, the GRKA, which obtains optimal or near-
optimal solutions to the in-article-generated RRDCP instances within a few minutes com-
pared to the days used when solving the mathematical model. This method iteratively
creates a random vector. The vector called a random key, dictates the sequence in which
to load and unload the slots. The objective value of the solution obtained, when evaluat-
ing the random key is then used as input to an unconstrained continuous optimizer, which
in turn outputs new random keys. The optimizer in the article proving most capable at
solving the instances is the CMA-ES.
The solutions obtained using the GRKA reduce the number of tug operations performed
when applying dual cycling on RoRo ship carrying homogeneous cargo by 15-35% de-
pending on the precedence rules used and the size of the ship deck. The larger the ship
deck, the more dual cycle operations can be performed.
This finding leads to the inquiry of whether dual cycling likewise could achieve a similar re-
duction in turnaround time when addressing the RRDCP for heterogeneous cargo. Thus,
setting the stage for subsequent analysis.

3
The Roll-on Roll-off Dual Cycling
Problem for heterogeneous cargo
Building upon the RRDCP detailed in [1], the problem is expanded to also handle het-
erogeneous cargo. A system divided into two different subsystems is constructed. One
manages the standard trailers, where the tugs are equipped with SAT trestles, while the
other handles roll trailers, where the tugs have goosenecks attached. The tugs can switch
between equipment, allowing them to move freely between the two subsystems, as illus-
trated in the figure below and further detailed in the following section. A larger version of
the figure can be found in the appendix A.
Figure 3.1: System flow detailing tug movements in the RRDCP for heterogeneous
cargo. Purple arrows indicate tugs unloading trailers, blue arrows show loading
operations, and grey arrows represent idle tugs on the quay. A description of the system
variables can be found prior to the table of contents.
3.1
Loading and unloading dynamics
When loading a standard trailer the tug is equipped with a SAT trestle, which can found in
the stowage area on the quay. By following the blue loading arrows in the SAT subsystem
of figure 3.1, we see that the tug drives from the stowage area to the quay loading area
(WaqS) where it connects with a standard trailer. From there, the tug has the possibility
of driving immediately onto the RoRo ship (yS), loading the trailer by disconnecting the
SAT trestle from the tug, and leaving both on the ship before driving to the stowage area
(WsaE), picking up a new SAT trestle. However, the tug can also remain idle on the quay
within the loading area (WqqSL).
Similar to [1], the number of tugs moving in and out of the deck is restricted to account
for the capacity of the main ramp, acting as the bottleneck in the system. This sometimes
prompts tugs to wait on the quay before driving onto the ship, thus ensuring adherence
to the capacity constraint. While allowed to wait within the loading (WqqSL and WqqGL),
unloading (WqqSU and WqqGU), and stowage areas (WqqAS) on the quay, the tugs cannot
stay idle on the ship, seeing as it has very limited operating space.
A tug unloading a standard trailer will likewise stay idle on the quay until the main ramp

has room for the tug to drive in and out of the ship. Following the purple unloading arrows
in the SAT subsystem, the tug drives empty (without a SAT trestle equipped) from the
stowage area to the ship (WasE). Picks up a standard trailer and the SAT trestle con-
nected to it, drives to the unloading area on the quay (xS), and disconnects the trailer
from the SAT trestle. Lastly, it will drive with the SAT trestle to the stowage area (WqaS),
leaving it there before picking up a new standard trailer to unload.
Instead of driving to the stowage area with the SAT trestle, the tug can also utilize the al-
ready attached SAT trestle to pick up a trailer for loading (WqqSUL). Similarly, after loading
a standard trailer, the tug is no longer equipped with a SAT trestle and can, therefore, pick
up a standard trailer for unloading, thus performing a dual cycling operation.
When in the stowage area, the tugs can also be equipped with goosenecks, enabling
them to transport roll trailers. When leaving the stowage area equipped with a gooseneck
the tugs can either drive onto the ship (WasG) or to the quay loading area (WaqG) to pick
up a roll trailer for loading. Unlike the SAT trestles, goosenecks are not detached from
the tugs when loaded. Thus, before finishing the loading procedure, the roll trailer has to
be secured to the deck through lashing.
Following the blue arrows in the gooseneck subsystem, loading a roll trailer involves the
tug picking it up from the loading area, driving it onto the ship (WqsyG), and lashing it (yG)
before the tug once again leaves the ship (WsqG or WsaG).
The unloading process in the gooseneck subsystem, indicated by the purple arrows, in-
volves the tug driving from the unloading area onto the ship (WqsG), unlashing a roll trailer
(WssxG), and returning with the trailer to the quay unloading area (xG).
As with standard trailers, dual cycling can also be performed when handling roll trailers.
After lashing a roll trailer, the tug can immediately drive to another roll trailer on deck and
remove its lashings before returning to the unloading area on the quay with it. It can then
detach the trailer from the tug and drive to the loading area picking up a new roll trailer
(WqqGUL) or alternatively drive to the stowage area (WqaG).
3.2
Integration of problem dynamics and assumptions into a
mathematical model
Below is a mathematical model detailing the problem presented. The precise definition
of the different sets and model variables can be found prior to the table of contents. Note
that the model operates in deterministic time steps, thus assuming tug operations taking
equal amount of time to complete. Also the model assumes that the ship is filled to full
capacity both when arriving at and departing from the port. This assumption is included
in the model to illustrate the full potential of dual cycling on the turn around time of the
ship. However, in reality, the RoRo ship capacity is seldom utilized to the fullest[17]. To
simplify the model, it is further assumed that all loading trailers are already at the port
and ready to be loaded when the RoRo ship arrives. Yet, in practice, cargo to be loaded
will often arrive throughout the day. Typically, the port establishes a cutoff time, set a
few hours before the ship’s departure. Cargo arriving after this cutoff time will not be
loaded onto the ship[18]. Using the same ship representation as in[1], the mathematical
model for loading and unloading heterogeneous cargo divides the ship deck into slots.
Thus assuming, that the cargo is similar equal size. According to DFDS a roll trailer has
dimensions 13.62 × 2.48 meters, while the dimensions of a standard trailer are 13.62 ×
2.46 meters [19], making this assumption realistic. Lastely, the model utilizes a stowage
plan with predetermined trailer-slot allocations, which is expected to be drafted before the
ship arrives at the port. Since the model does not optimize the stowage plan, reorganizing
the cargo on the ship deck could potentially decrease the ships turnaround time further.

The model’s objective is to minimize the total number of timesteps necessary to discharge
and load the RoRo vessel, as depicted in equation 3.1. This is equivalent to setting the
makespan greater or equal to the finishing time for loading and unloading each trailer, as
stated in equations 3.2 to 3.5.
The total number of tugs is constant throughout the entire operating time, which is en-
forced by equation 3.6. This constraint adds all the variables, except WqqGULt and WqqSULt,
together, at each timestep to account for the different tug movements. The two variables
are excluded seeing as the distance between the loading and unloading area is consid-
ered very small. Therefore, it is assumed that the tugs can move from the ship to both the
unloading and loading area within a single timestep. This allows tugs to be registered as
either moving into or staying idle within the unloading area, while simultaneously driving
to the loading area. Hence, the variables are excluded from the constraint to ensure that
the model does not count the same tug multiple times.
Every slot has to be loaded and unloaded only once during the entire makespan. To
ensure this, the constraints in equations 3.7 to 3.10 are included in the model.
Equations 3.11 and 3.12 make sure that only half of the tugs move into the ship and out
of the ship within the same timestep, in order to limit the traffic on the main ramp.
The next two constraints (equations 3.13 and 3.14) model the flow through the ship for
tugs carrying standard trailers and roll trailers, respectively. The first of the two constraints
models tugs within the SAT subsystem and states, that the number of tugs entering the
ship at time t −1 must also exit the ship at time t.
Roll trailers take additional time to load and unload, due to them having to be secured
to the deck. This factor is incorporated into the constraint controlling the flow on the ship
deck in the gooseneck subsystem. After lashing a roll trailer, a tug can either return empty
to the quay in the following timestep or drive to another roll trailer ready for discharging
and unlash it. However, the number of roll trailers unlashed in timestep t also depends on
the number of gooseneck-equipped tugs entering the ship without a load in the previous
timestep. Thus, equation 3.14 states that the sum of gooseneck-equipped tugs not car-
rying a load when entering the ship at time t −1, and those finished lashing a roll trailer at
the same timestep, equals the sum of gooseneck-equipped tugs leaving the ship empty
at time t and the number of tugs unlashing a roll trailer in the same timestep. Both con-
straints facilitate dual cycling, enabling tugs to transition from loading or lashing a trailer
in one timestep to unloading or unlashing another trailer in the next.
The following constraint in equation 3.15 ensures roll trailers have their lashings removed
just prior to being unloaded. Similarly, roll trailers need to be lashed immediately after
entering the ship, which is enforced by the constraint in equation 3.16.
Just as the flow on the ship deck has been defined, the flow on the quay must similarly be
outlined. This includes the flow in the stowage area, as well as the loading and unloading
areas for standard and roll trailers. Therefore, the constraints in equations 3.17 to 3.21
are included in the model.

• The first set of constraints in equations 3.22 to 3.25 specifies which slots must
have their trailers unloaded before discharging a trailer. These trailers must be
discharged before unloading the successor trailer, as they block the tug’s path into
and out of the ship.
If the trailer to be unloaded is a standard trailer, the preceding trailers must be dis-

• The second set of constraints in equations 3.26 to 3.29 dictates which slots have to

these need to be loaded prior to the successive trailer in order to ensure that the
tugs are able to leave the deck again.
When loading a standard trailer, the preceding trailers must be loaded strictly before
the successive trailer. However, for roll trailers, the preceding trailers must be loaded
two whole timesteps before the roll trailer. This is to account for the time it takes to
lash the roll trailer.
• The last set of constraints in equations 3.30 to 3.33 applies for trailers to be loaded.
This set details when at the latest the slot, which the trailer is loaded unto, has to be
cleared of its trailer meant for discharge. When a tug discharges a trailer, the tug

Generalized Random Key Algorithm [1]
The GRKA algorithm is a metaheuristic using random keys in addition to an unconstrained
continuous optimizer to investigate the search space looking for an optimum. A random
key is a vector consisting of randomly drawn numbers. A decoder is used to convert the
random key into a feasible solution to the problem such that the corresponding objective
value can be obtained. This value is then used as input in the unconstrained continuous
optimizer, which generates new random keys based on it.
Paramount for the method is, that any given random key can be translated into a feasible
solution. In addition, the decoder should be deterministic, ensuring that the same random
key always will result in the same solution to the problem.
If such a decoder can be developed, employing a random key approach for optimization
is often a suitable choice. This is especially true if the problem involves ordering. GRKA
and similar random key methods, have previously shown large capabilities for effectively
learning the order of operations compared to other frequently applied metaheuristics such
as simulated annealing, TABU search, and adaptive large neighborhood search [1, 20,
21].

6
Implementing the Generalized Random
Key Algorithm
When applying GRKA to opimize instances the main challenge is, as mentioned earlier,
defining a valid decoder. For the decoder to be valid, it is important that all random keys
can be translated into feasible solutions and that these solutions are obtained solely de-
terministic.
For this problem, the random key is defined as a vector with length equaling the number
of trailers in the instance needing to be discharged or loaded. Each element in the ran-
dom key is linked to a specific slot on the deck, with all slots having a trailer being both
discharged and loaded from them. Therefore, each slot is likewise linked to two random
key elements. The value of the random key determines the priority in which the corre-
sponding slot is loaded/unloaded. The lower the random key value, the higher its priority.
However, to ensure a feasible solution, the slot with the random key having the lowest
value and, thus, the highest priority is not guaranteed to be loaded/unloaded first.
A trailer can only be loaded onto or discharged from a slot if all the precedence rules
are adhered to. Therefore, only the random keys having the precedence rules of their
corresponding slot fulfilled, are considered. Of these, the slot with the highest priority is
chosen to have its trailer loaded onto it or discharged from the ship. After determining
the destination slot, the next step is to figure out, which tug should handle the loading or
unloading operation. Here, the rule is that the tug able to finish the task the fastest is the
one assigned it.
A dual cycle may be performed if a trailer is about to be loaded. Whether the tug drives in
a single or dual cycle, has to be determined prior to loading the trailer, due to gooseneck-
equipped tugs leaving the ship at different times depending on the type of cycle performed.
This strains the main ramp at different timesteps, possibly affecting the number of time
steps the tugs have to remain idle on the quay before loading the trailer.

Following this, a dictionary, slotdict, is defined, associating slot IDs with their respective
random keys and slots (line 4). The dictionary values include the slot name, operation
type (either loading or discharging a roll or standard trailer), the slot’s random key value,
and slot IDs for the slot predecessors, along with a list detailing when the precedence
constraint for each predecessor is scheduled to be resolved. Since no operations have
initially been planned, this value is set to infinity, preventing slots with predecessors from
having trailers loaded onto or discharged from them.
Lastly, an additional dictionary,
tugdict is defined that details the type of operation assigned to each tug and the time step
at which the tugs will finish their current task. Initially, no tasks are registered to the tugs,
and thus the finish times are all set to zero (line 5).
The decoder operates by assigning tasks to tugs one at a time until all tasks have been
handled, which is controlled by the while loop. After a task is allocated to a tug, it is
thus removed from the slotdict, such that the algorithm terminates when the dictionary is
empty (line 6). The process of assigning a task to a tug varies depending on the cycle
type performed. Thus, a Boolean variable, dual, is introduced to track whether the tug
operates in a single or dual cycle. Initially, the tug is assumed to perform a single cycle
(line 7). In line 8, the slots, together with their respective random keys, are filtered based
on whether the slot is accessible (or scheduled to become accessible) for either loading
or unloading tasks.
From the filtered list, the slot operation having the lowest random key value is chosen for
evaluation, and thus the corresponding Slot ID is saved. To determine which tug should
handle the task, the completion time for each tug is derived. This takes into consideration
when the tug will be able to take on the new task and the number of moves necessary to
complete this task based on the tug’s position when finishing its current task. It also takes
into account the number of tugs moving in and out of the ship at different time steps and
that the tug cannot load or discharge the trailer until all the precedence rules are adhered
to. The tug will stay idle on the quay until it is able to move onto the ship, place or pick
up the trailer, and drive out of the ship afterward without violating any of the constraints.
The completion time for the tugs is compared, and the tug able to load or discharge the
trailer the fastest is the one assigned the task line 9.
When assigning a tug to a trailer, the tugdict is updated to reflect both the operations per-
formed and when the tug will finish the task. Likewise, the lists detailing when the tugs
enter and leave the ship are also updated. After having determined how and when the
trailer is loaded or unloaded, the slotdict is updated by removing the entry containing infor-
mation about that specific trailer and destination slot (line 10). This update also includes
changing the time for when the precedence constraints involving the trailer’s loading and
discharge are resolved.
Line 11 checks if the extracted trailer is to be loaded, since this potentially could be fa-
cilitated through dual cycling. In order to preform a dual cycle, another trailer has to be
unloaded. Therefore a second trailer is extracted for evaluation as described in line 12.
The GRKA dictates that the optimizer should be solely responsible for determining the
order of tugs moving in and out of the ship as long as the solution is guaranteed feasible.
Thus, the next trailer to be handled in a potential dual cycle or alternatively two single
cycles would be the trailer with the highest priority having its precedence constraints fol-
lowed. However, this decoder only considers the random keys for discharge trailers of a
similar type as the loading trailer. If such trailers exist, the decoder will choose the one
with the highest priority to be evaluated, thus prompting the solution towards planning
more dual cycles. This method is favored over the typical approach due to it obtaining

The instances used to evaluate the developed models are based on the ones found in
[1]. Different types of instances are adopted, with the first ones entailing the layout and
precedence rules applied on actual ship decks, while the other ones are developed by
Jia Hansen describing the layout and precedence rules of two constructed ship decks.
Additionally, a small constructed toy instance is also examined.
The first deck by Jia Hansen consists of up to 13 rows and 7 lanes of 79 slots, each
containing a standard trailer or roll trailer. The second deck has a capacity of 113 slots
with a maximum length and width of 15 rows and 9 lanes. The toy instance has both 4
rows and lanes, resulting in 16 slots in total. All these instances neglect obstacles on
deck, such as pillars and ramps. If these were to be included, this would call for the need
to manually define the precedence rule in the surrounding area of the obstacles. Instead,
the two instances are analyzed using the two predefined precedence rules mp and mps.
The mp precedence rule ensures that a slot cannot be unloaded unless both the slot di-
rectly in front of it and to the front port side are already unloaded. If there is no slot to the

vering space for the tugs. While the mp rule occasionally demands the front starboard
slot to be unloaded, the mps rule will always require this in addition to unloading the front
and front port slots (assuming that the slots exist). Therefor this precedence rule ensures
more maneuvering space compared to the mp rule. Table 7.1 outlines the two precedence
rules applied during both loading and unloading.

Results
For all analyses performed, both on the training and test instances, a total of four tugs are
allocated to the deck. Furthermore, the initial random key generated for the CMA-ES is
drawn from a uniform distribution between 0 and 1, and evaluated using an initial step-
size of 0.1. Unless otherwise stated, the default parameter values listed in chapter 5 are
applied to the CMA-ES. When analyzing the training instances, all tests are performed
five times, whereas the test instances are evaluated only once. The GRKA, utilizing the
CMA-ES package in Python[23], is set to run for five minutes. In contrast, the mathe-
matical model, implemented in Julia and analyzed with Gurobi[24], operates for an hour.
All instances were tested on a Windows 10 Pro computer equipped with an Intel Core
i7-6700HQ processor and 16 GB of RAM.
8.1
Search method of decoder
As outlined in chapter 6, when the decoder encounters a trailer meant for loading, it
searches through the random key, looking for a trailer to discharge as part of a dual cy-
cle. Typically, a decoder is only expected to generate a feasible solution, not improve the
sequence dictated by the random key. Both decoder strategies have been evaluated on
the training instances, with the results depicted infigure 8.1.
During these tests, the same restart strategy used in [1] is applied, with CMA-ES restart-
ing every minute until a total runtime of five minutes is achieved. This approach minimizes
the risk of the optimizer becoming trapped in a local optimum.
Figure 8.1: Comparison of the decoder’s performance when actively searching for a
trailer to discharge in a dual cycle versus only having the CMA-ES determining the
loading and unloading sequence.

Across all test instances, the GRKA achieves similar or better objective values when the
decoder searches the random key to identify a trailer for discharge in a dual cycle. This
approach enables the CMA-ES to more easily generate random keys, which translate
into solutions having low objective values. That said, the method also diminishes the op-
timizer’s ability to accurately predict how a change in a random key affects the objective
value since the random keys have a reduced influence on the final load-unload sequence.
However, this fact does not seem to impact the CMA-ES particularly compared to the ben-
efit of obtaining a load-unload sequence, which utilizes dual cycling better.
Future analysis could investigate whether better objective values can be achieved by al-
lowing the decoder to perform the additional search only after the CMA-ES has terminated
and the best random key has been identified. This approach would ensure that the load-
unload sequence during the optimization phase is influenced solely by the random key.
This would allow the CMA-ES to more accurately predict how changes to the random key
affect the objective value.

For the previous analysis, the CMA-ES was executed for five minutes, with restarts occur-

RRDCP for heterogeneous cargo is investigated in the figure below. Here, the CMA-ES
is restarted a fixed number of times at equally sized time intervals, totaling a run time of
5 minutes.

RoRo ships.

From figure 8.2, it is apparent that most of the instances perform rather well when the
CMA-ES does not conduct any restarts. However, both instances, TM-79-mps-0.0 and
TRD-38-industry-1.0, perform best when the number of restarts are increased. This indi-
cates occasional difficulties for the optimizer to escape local minima.
Another widely used restart technique, potentially improving the GRKA performance for all
training instances is having the optimizer restart automatically after presumably reaching
a local optimum.
After restarting, it is considered a good measure to increase the population size, thereby
conducting a more thorough search and thus increasing the probability of extracting new
promising search points [25].
The CMA-ES package in Python supports this option. Parameters determining when to
restart include among others:
• The number of iterations performed since the last restart: After a sufficiently large
number of iterations, depending on problem dimension and population size, the
algorithm restarts, deeming it unlikely for the search point to improve function value
further.
• The extent of the multivariate normal distribution: When the distribution becomes
too small, search points extracted will likely not result in better function values due
to them being almost identical, effectively trapping the algorithm within that search
area.
• The span of function values obtained when evaluating extracted search points: If the
span of the obtained function values is very small, this suggests that the distribution
has settled into a local optimum, and the algorithm thus could benefit from analyzing
a different part of the search space.
In Figure 8.3, the automated restart technique is evaluated on the training instances with
various increases in population size. The figure shows that an increase in population size
by a factor of 2.5 generally leads to shorter turnaround times for the instances. When
comparing this restart strategy with having no restarts at all, see figure figure 8.4, it is ap-
parent that the turnaround times obtained with automated restarts equal or even improve
those obtained without employing restarts.

Test instance analysis
The test instances are evaluated using the restart strategy showing the most promise in
the previous section (automated restart with a factor 2.5 increase in population size after
each restart). Each instances is evaluated twice, with one evaluation allowing a combina-
tion of single and dual cycling, while the other only permits tugs to perform single cycling.
The evaluations are performed with both the mathematical model and the GRKA. The re-
sults, containing the number of time steps required to turnover the RoRo-ship decks, are
presented in Table table 8.1. Additionally, the table lists the relative difference in turnover
times when solving the instances with and without dual cycling.
Based on the evaluation of the test instances, it is apparent that the mathematical model
can only obtain feasible solutions for the smallest instances within the one-hour time limit.
This is likely due to the number of variables in the model increasing linearly with the up-
per bound. The upper bound is rather large, due to it equaling the number of time steps it
takes a single tug to unload and load the ship, assuming that it has to switch equipment
between operations, causing the model to increase in complexity. Therefore, for medium-
sized or larger ship decks, leveraging metaheuristics (GRKA) can be an effective way of
ensuring feasible solutions.
When comparing results obtained using the GRKA with those of the mathematical model,
it is evident that the heuristic can find feasible solutions to all instances within a five-
minute time frame. However, the GRKA rarely locates the global optimum. When running
the heuristic using only single cycles, the percentage deviation from the optimum value
is 2.64 ± 2.27% for the toy instances and 4.7 ± 2.17% for the real deck instances with 38
slots. This indicates that solutions obtained with the GRKA, allowing only single cycles,
are relatively close to the optimum value, although the percentage deviation increases
with deck size. A similar increase is observed when dual cycling is permitted. For toy
instances, the deviation from the optimum value becomes 5.06 ± 3.36 percent, while the
real deck instance with 38 slots, has a deviation of 9.36 ± 3.99 percent. Thus, when tugs
are allowed to perform dual cycling, it is more difficult for the GRKA to obtain near-optimal
solutions within the five-minute timeframe. Thus, as deck size increases, the turnaround
time obtained using the GRKA is expected to deviate further away from the optimal value.
This is especially true when instances allow the tugs to dual cycle.

For most instances, when having tugs performing both single and dual cycling, the turnaround
time decreases.
However, the effect dual cycling has on this value diminishes as the share of roll trailers
increases. The turnaround times for decks carrying only standard trailers while utilizing
dual cycling decrease according to GRKA with 5.25 ± 3.94% compared to the turnaround
time for the corresponding solely single cycling instances. For decks carrying only roll
trailers the change in objective value when allowing dual cycling is −0.23 ± 2.57%, which
is lower than the decrease obtained in the instances operating only with standard trailers.
The difference in turnaround time when performing dual cycling on instances consisting
of only standard trailers versus those only having roll trailers could possibly be explained
by the roll trailers benefiting less from dual cycling than standard trailers do. A dual cy-
cle with standard trailers can be performed in two timesteps, this compared to the four
timesteps, it takes the same tug to perform two single cycles. Thus, dual cycling with
standard trailers is up to 50% more efficient than single cycling.
However, roll trailers have dual cycles, taking up four timesteps, while a tug can perform
two single cycles with roll trailers in six timesteps. Therefore, dual cycling is only up to
33% more efficient than single cycling, which would account for dual cycling having a
smaller effect on the turnaround time for instances carrying only roll trailers.
Thus, when the share of roll trailers in the instances increases, the effect dual cycling has
on the turnaround time likewise decreases. An example of this can be seen in the L-113-
mp instances. When purely handling standard trailers, the effect of applying dual cycling
is measured at 5.5%. This decreases to 4.6% when 10% of the cargo is roll trailers. For
30% roll trailers, the value is 1.6%. For the instances having roll trailer occupying 50%
and the entire ship deck, this value equals 1.1%.
The above does explain the general tendency in the results. However, there are some
examples where the effect of dual cycling increases with the share of roll trailers. For
example instance M-79-mp-0.3 has a registered effect of dual cycling equaling 0.8. How-
ever, for M-79-mp-0.5 this value has increased to 1.5. This could be simply be to chance.
However, an explanation for this might also be, the fact that the decks are generated at
random. Decks carrying a mixture of standard and roll trailers would be expected to have
different optimal turnaround times based on the configuration of trailers on the deck. It is
likely that the deck generated, for instance, M-79-mp-0.5, supports dual cycling quite well,
while the deck for M-79-mp-0.3 does not. This would cause the GRKA to more easily find
solutions with a better dual cycling effect on the instance despite the share of roll trailers
having increased. The calculated effect dual cycling has on the turnaround time acts as a
lower bound for the true potential of applying dual cycling to RRDCP with heterogeneous
cargo. This is due to the fact that it is likely possible to generate non-random stowage
plans for ship decks supporting dual cycling more, which would result in better turnaround
times. Thus, it could be of relevance to investigate how different deck configurations affect
the turnaround time when performing dual cycling.

Conclusion
To assess the impact of dual cycling on the turnaround times of Roll-on Roll-off (RoRo)
ships in shuttle service carrying heterogeneous cargo, a Generalized Random Key Al-
gorithm (GRKA) was developed. This to overcome the fact that the mathematical model
could only find feasible solutions for the smallest instances within an hour of runtime. Af-
ter running the GRKA for five minutes on each of the 50 instances, the results show that
dual cycling has the largest effect on turnaround times, for RoRo ships when transporting
purely standard trailers. Compared to single cycling, dual cycling improved turnaround
times by 5.25 ± 3.94%. As the proportion of roll trailers on the RoRo ships increases,
the effect of dual cycling on turnaround times diminishes. This trend continues until roll
trailers constitute 100% of all transported trailers, at which point the effect of dual cycling
on the turnaround time, as measured by the GRKA, is −0.23 ± 2.57%.
Although the GRKA always find feasible solutions for the Roll-on Roll-off Dual Cycling
Problem (RRDCP) involving heterogeneous cargo, these solutions are often not optimal.
Negative improvements from dual cycling are attributed to the GRKA’s inability to solve
the RRDCP sufficiently fast, seeing as the turnaround time for instances only operating
with single cycling acts as an upper bound for the turnaround time obtained when allowing
dual cycling. Suggested future research could explore how adjustments to the stowage
plan, when handling both standard and roll trailers, could enhance dual cycling efficiency
and further reduce turnaround times. Thus, allowing the RoRo ships to sail at lower speed,
reducing their greenhouse gas emissions.
