Abstract
Bacterial communities within the extreme environments of hyperarid regions, such as the Atacama
desert, are known for being more enriched with mobile genetical elements (MGE). However, identifyôÙ
ing the relations of hosts and corresponding MGEs within bacterial communities has been a challenge.
Next generation sequencing (NGS) methods, such as Pacific Bioscience (PacBio) SingleôÙMolecule
RealôÙTime (SMRT) sequencer, can offer a solution using methylation motifs. Therefore, the purpose
of this study is to examine the host/plasmids relationship based on methylation motifs and using difôÙ
ferent methods to ascertain if the hosts in question can be distinguished as well as the examining
the biological contains of the identified plasmids through manual curation. The analyses were perôÙ
formed on a sample from a semiôÙtranslucent hypolith sampled in the Atacama desert. The paper
proves that using methylation motifs, it is possible to determine host/plasmids relationships as was
seen between hybrid host bin_3, PacBio host PB_bin_1 and associated plasmids as well as hybrid
host bin_8, PacBio host PB_bin_3 and associated plasmids. Based on the differences in methylation
motifs between the two host, there is an indication that the hosts are two different species of cyanobacôÙ
teria. However, it was not possible to prove this distinction between the hybrid hosts: bin_3 and bin_8
using plasmids as basis for differential abundance analysis. The curation of the MGEs, found gene
functions related to motility, DNA repair, environmental resistances, and plasmids specific genes as
was the goal. Most genes curated related to DNA repair and environmental resistances, which was
expected due to the nature of the sampled biome.

1
Introduction
Horisontal gene transfer (HGT) is the process where organisms share their genetical material, not
through evolution, but through several different mechanism. In prokaryotes, HGT is widely used in
the adaption to new situation such as exposure to antibiotics or exploit new resources, by dissemiôÙ
nation information to related organism[1]. One method of dissemination comes in the form of circular
extrachromosomal replicons called plasmids also known as extrachromosomal mobile genetical eleôÙ
ments(eMGE). Plasmids can mediate advantageous HGT between organism through conjugations[2].
Studies has found that bacterial communities within biomes located in extreme conditions are more enôÙ
riched with MGEs[3]. The Atacama region in Chile, South America is one such location and is known
for the lack of precipitation. Therefore, the region plays host to organism such as extremophiles, hyôÙ
polith, and photosynthetic bacteria conditioned to the extremes of the biome. These organisms can
be found underneath semiôÙtranslucent quartz rocks that allows for photosynthesis processes and proôÙ
tection against high UVôÙradiation, while still having increased moisture retention. The Atacama desert
has in recent years been the focus of xenobiological scientific studies related to climatic limitations to
life. Therefore, microbiotic communities such as hypoliths become candidates for studying adaptive
properties of organisms living in hyperarid environments and dessimination of eMGEs[4, 5, 6].
However, tracing relations of hosts and corresponding MGEs has been a challenge as in vitro methods
disconnects chromosomal DNA from the plasmid DNA making it difficult to determine origin[7]. Here,
next generation sequencing (NGS) methods can offer a solution using methylation motifs. Methylation
of nucleotides at specific binding domains allows the organism to regulate gene expressions. Which
domains that are methylated are often unique to a host. If similar methylation motifs can be found in
both plasmid and the potential host, it would be highly likely that the plasmid came from that host[8,
9, 10].

How NGSs can find methylation motifs is examplified with Pacific Bioscience (PacBio) sequencing
technique known as singleôÙmolecule realôÙtime (SMRT). The process of the technique is simplified in
Figure 1.1. SMRT exploits an anchored DNA polymerase and fluorescent dyed nucleotides. When
single stranded DNA is fed into the polymerase, it will attach a corresponding dyed nucleotides onto
the exposed DNA strand and emit a fluorescent signal. The signal length and intensity is interpreted
by a camera, whereafter a computer can determine what the sequence of the single stranded DNA
is. The interpulse duration (IPD) between signals can determine whether the nucleotide is methyôÙ
lated[11].
Figure 1.1: Visual presentation of SMRT sequencing. Using an anchored polymerase and fluorescent
dyed nucleotides, single stranded DNA can pass through the polymerase. Based upon the fluorescent
signal, the four nucleotides and if the nucleotide is methylated can be distinguished[12].
The primary purpose of this study is to examine the host/plasmids relationship based on methylaôÙ
tion motifs extracted from Interpulse Duration (IPD) values using PacBios SMRTLink software suite.
The secondary goal is to identify if the PacBio bins in question are from different organisms or the
same organism split across multiple bins using a dereplication, marker gene analysis and differential
abundance analysis. The final focus is examining the gene content in the plasmids, which has been
linked to the host via methylation motifs, with a primary focus on genes involved with survival in arid
environments, plasmid regulation, and mobile genetical elements. This will be done using databases,
annotation analysis, and manual curation.

2.1

The workflow used in this thesis follows a chronological workflow as presented in Figure ??. The
workflow includes five stages each adding to the amount of obtainable information. This section will
provide a deeper description of each of the steps, and elaborate the used methods.

2.2
Data
The data utilised in this study is raw hypolith samples reads from the Atacama Desert, sequenced
both on PacBio SMRT sequencer and Illumina Miseq[5]. Furthermore, the hybrid assemblies, where
Illumina reads get mapped to PacBio reads, was provided by Christian Arendt Rasmussen[13]. The
data was utilised in the downstream process shown in Figure ??, and all processes run on the server
platform, Incerta. The system was provided by the Department of Bioinformatics at the Technical
University of Denmark and maintained by system administrator Peter Wad Sackett (pwsa@dtu.dk).
2.3
Plasmid extraction
The PlasFlow v1.1[14] tool was utilised to predict and then separate plasmids from hosts in the two
assemblies. This was first done by removing sequences with a length of less than 1000 bp, whereafter
Plasflow outputs four different files. Three files were fasta format files containing sequences classified
as plasmids, host and unclassified respectivelyëƒ the last file was a tsv file containing sequence classiôÙ
fication. The fasta file containing the plasmid sequences was used as a basis for further examination.

2.4
Host/Plasmid relationship
For the identification of methylation motifs, PacBio SMRTLINK v8.0.0[15] suite was utilised. The first
step was to align the raw PacBio BAM file to the reference sequences from Christians Arendt RasôÙ
mussenãs PacBio assemblies. The plasmid identified in Plasflow was used as a reference in PBAlign
v0.4.1. The output was an aligned BAM file that could then be indexed using PBIndex v0.23.0ëƒ The
same process was done to the reference fasta using SAMTools v1.9 faidx[16].
The indexed BAM file was analysed for methylation modification using KineticsTools ipdsummery
v2.4[15] to determine the difference in interpulse duration (IPD) between the BAM file and the refôÙ
erence file. The reference file was, in this case, used to simulate a nonmethylated sequence. This
process producesd a gffôÙfile, which was fed into MotifMaker find to identify the methylation motifs
and summarising the data in a CSV file. The final step was to combine the CSV and gff file using
MotifMaker Reprocess, giving the final gff file containing the methylation motifs and the methylated
residues.
When analysing the relationship between hosts and plasmids, the methylation motifs of both were
found, and the results from Motifmaker was examined in order to identify overlaps in methylation
motifs.
2.5
Comparing hybrid assemblies to PacBio assemblies
The hybrid assemblies, created from both PacBio and Illumina reads, were proven to show more
completeness during the binning process compared to the individual processes[13]. Therefore the 19
bins from the hybrid assembly were compared with the three bins from PacBio.
The program dRep Compare v2.6.2[17] was used to examine the combined 21 PacBio and hybrid
bins without filtering. The bins underwent pairôÙwise comparison and then clustering, where each bin
in the input folder was compared with the other input bins. These bin pairs were defined as having bins
from the hybrid and PacBio assembly clustered together with an ANI of at least 95%. As confirmation
of the primary clustering results, each bin pairs were blasted against each other to see if the similarity
was between 95ôÙ100%. From the clustering and the average nucleotide identity (ANI), two sets of
distinct bins were identified and outputted as a primary clustering dendrogram. The last step was to
consult the winning genome diagram and determine if the hybrid bin showed a higher likelihood of
completeness, as assumed by Christian Arendt Rasmussen in his thesis[13]. The hybrid bins were
extracted from the clusters and used for further analysis.
2.6
Host diversity
In order to gain an understanding of the hostã association with the plasmids, the diversity of the hosts
were identified. The analysis of the diversity of the host were conducted using Genome Taxonomy
Database Toolkit v1.4.0 (GTDBôÙTK) using the subroutine classify_wf[18]. The first step was an idenôÙ
tification of marker genes using prodigal[19] and HMMER[20], which also does multiple sequence
alignment[21]. The next step was concatenating of marker genes and a filtering of the MSA. The
final step was to find the best placement in the GTDBôÙTK referenceôÙbased using gANI and Relative
Evolutionary Diversity (RED)[21].

A vital step to ensure continued work with the hostôÙlinked plasmids was to correlate methylation motifs
found using PacBio data to hybrid bins. It is assumed that only if the methylation motifs are compaôÙ
rable, the host association are reliable. This was done by comparing the sequences predicted as
plasmids by Plasflow with the methylaion motif of the hosts and isolating the contigs that occurs in

both. The hostôÙlinked plasmids that were able to be linked with the host, were used for further analysis.
As the hybrid bins contains the most complete information about the plasmids, it was examined
whether the hostôÙlinked plasmids were part of the hybrid bins. This was done by using blastn[22][23],
where the isolated Pacbio plasmids were used as query and the hybrid bins were used as subject.
Only contigs from the hybrid bins, which had a query coverage of at least 90% and a similarity of
99.0%, when compared to the PacBio plasmid contigs, were extracted.
The extracted plasmid contigs were analysed for completeness by isolating around 15% residues
from both ends of the sequence and then blasting the ends against each other. Any identical overlap
would indicate a complete plasmid. Else it was assumed that the plasmids were not complete.
2.8
Differential abundance in plasmids
When annotating metagenomic sequences, the metagenomics datasets often show underôÙsampling
and low signalôÙtoôÙnoise ratios. This is true for pathways, COGs, species, or any classification where
comparison can be conducted across samples. To determine if groups of microorganism are signifiôÙ
cantly differently abundant between biomes, a differential abundance analysis can be performed[24].
Analysing the gene abundance in the plasmids was performed by using phyloseq v1.34.0[25] and
DESeq2 v 1.30.16[26]. The workflow is described in the Functional annotation exercise from Course
22136 Metagenomics and Microbiome Analysis[27]. The META file describs the bins and OTU files
being the merged KEGG and KEGG pathway files.
2.9
Plasmid similarity
In order to understand the function of the extracted plasmids, it was initally examined whether the
extracted plasmids identified were already studied/known, by doing a comparison search using the
Plasmid Database (PLSDB)[28][29]. Mash Dist with a maximum distance of 15% determined if any
plasmids similar to the input plasmid exists in the database. Mash Screen with a minimum identity of
85% determined if the input sample contained known plasmids.
2.10

analysis. This analysis of the extracted hybrid plasmids was done in two steps. In the first step,

passed onto eggNOG emapper v2.0.6[30] for functional annotation. After annotation, the annotation
file was run through eggnog_parser v1.0.0[31] to extract and summarise the gene into Cluster of
Orthology Groups (COG). The functional classifications can be seen in Table 2.1.Thereafter, the data
as imported into excel and two sets of graphs was constructed. The first graph shows the distribution

Discussion
4.1
Data
This study focuses on the data analysis of a single sample from the coastal fog area of the Atacama
region in Chile[5]. One future prospect related to this project would be to apply elements of this
projects pipeline to other arid and hyperôÙarid samples both related to Atacama or locations such as
Sahara, Antarctica or the Australian outback with similar environments[59]. Being able to compare
samples could gain valuable insight into common traits and survival strategies for microorganism in
arid and hyperôÙarid environments.
4.2
Plasmid extraction
Plasflow identified plasmids in PB_bin_1 and PB_bin_3 as cyanobacteria, proteobacteria, and as of
unknown origin. These results are supported by Christian Arendt Rasmussenãs thesis, where similar
phyla were found through the plasflow process[13].
4.3
Comparing hybrid assemblies to PacBio assemblies
The expectation was to pair the three PacBio bins with three of the hybrid bins, as the plasmids was
expected to be sequenced and assembled by both methods. dRep paired two of the PacBio bins
with hybrid bins, resulting in the two pairs PB_bin_1/bin_3 and PB_bin_3/bin_8 with a high degree of
confidence in the ANI score in the primary clustering. Unfortunately, pairing PB_bin_2 with a hybrid
bin was not possible, with the closest pair being hybrid bin_8 with an ANI score of 80 %.
The inability of pairing PB_bin_2 with the hybrid bins could be due to the size. PB_bin_2 contains
only 0.312Mbp, compared to the genome size span of cyanobacteria of 1.9 to 9.6Mbp[60]. PB_bin_2,
therefore, is most likely not a complete assembly. The genome size of PB_bin_1 and PB_bin_3 is
3.6Mbp each and is thus 11.5 times the size of PB_bin_2 and well within the genome size of cyanobacôÙ
teria.
4.4
Host diversity
The result seen in section Table 3.1 implies that bin_3 and bin_8 comes from the cyanobacterium
family Chroococcidiopsidaceae. While it is not possible to determine if it is the same genus or species
cyanobacterium, Christian Arendt Rasmussens thesis[13] supports the notion that the two bins are
indeed cyanobacterium. If the genus and species were to be annotated, it would add to the conclusion
of whether the two bins are separate organisms.
4.5
Host/Plasmid relationship
The SMRTLink results show that it is possible to link plasmids to both the PacBio host and the hyôÙ
brid host from a methylation motif perspective. The motif link in bin_3 based on GATC, CAGCTG
and GAAGAGG shows a similar distribution across the hosts and plasmids, making it more likely to
originate from the same organism. Bin_8, similar to bin_3, show related fraction distribution across
the five shared pairs GATC, SAGCTS, GGYRCC, ACAYNNNNNNNTTTC and GAANNNNNNNRTGT.
The difference between the pairs found in bin_3 and bin_8 indicates that these are two different orôÙ
ganisms as methylation motifs are considered unique to an organism[61, 62]

The GATC motif found in both pairs and distributed in at least 83% of the contigs and genomes was
expected. Methylated GATC plays a crucial role in cyanobacteriums DNA Methyltransferase (MTase)
system regulating solitary MTase functions. Methylation of adenine in GATC inhibits adenineôÙspecific,
GATCôÙrecognition endonucleases from cleaving genomic DNA. In some species of cyanobacterium,
the GATC motif performs a DamôÙlike function as found in esherichia coli. The methylation of GATC by
the Dam protein allows the bacteria to distinguish between ãyoungã hemimethylated and ãolderã fully
methylated DNA. The difference in age help regulate the timing of DNA synthesis and determine the
strand used to repair mismatched bases[63].

The whole process of linking the PacBio plasmids to hybrid bins was to gain more complete data
using the provenly more complete hybrid assemblies.
The plasmids extracted by linking the PacBio plasmids to the hybrid bins was incomplete when blastôÙ

data left in the hybrid bins relating to the plasmids. However, the analysis will still function with incomôÙ
plete plasmids, and therefore, a method to complete the plasmids was deemed less critical to find.
Furthermore, it is not examined if the PacBio plasmids used to identify the hybrid bins was complete.
With more time, blasting the ends against each other would be a time efficient method to identify if
any recoverable plasmid data migth be present in the bins.
If the PacBio plasmid were to show completeness, a method to locate and recover the missing data
would include neighbouring contigs from the hybrid bins. Using the same blasting method on neighôÙ
bouring contigs could reveal regional of overlaps that could indicate a complete plasmid.
4.7
Differential abundance in plasmids
The differential abundance analysis for both the KEGG and KEGG pathway entries showed no signifôÙ
icant difference between bin_3 and bin_8. Therefore, it is not possible to argue that bin_3 and bin_8
are different when examining the plasmids, but based on the prior methylation motif analysis, it would
suggest that the two bins are different organisms. The lack of difference found in the differential abunôÙ
dance analysis could be due to the plasmids not containing a sizable amount of data to differentiate
the two bins.
Another explanation could be that there is no difference between the bins. The two bins could be the
same organism that have been clustered in to separate bins during the binning process. A way to
further examine whether bin_3 and bin_8 are different or the same organism, could be to perform the
same analysis on the complete hybrid bins instead of only on the plasmids. The potential increase in
data could result in more KEGG and KEGG pathway hits, resulting in a more solid data foundation to
perform the analysis.

The general lack of cyanobacterial samples could indicate a potential for discovering new plasmids

Manual Curation
As the full dataset consisted of 22 COG categories, it was necessary to narrow the space of the genes

Three of the four categories defined for manual curation was chosen based on the environment where

The final category, plasmid specific genes, was chosen to help validate whether the sequences
thought to be plasmids did include genes associated with plasmids. Here, genes associated with
TA systems would be expected as well as genes associated with CRISPRôÙCas.
Curating for MGEs was conducted to gain insight into what information the plasmids exchange. The
curation was conducted using transposons as this is one of the more common methods of horizontal
gene transfer[3].
4.9.3
Cell motility
As mentioned earlier, genes involved in chemotaxis or phototaxis would be present in the plasmids.
Indeed, these genes were found in a small subset of plasmids but not as ubiquitous as first thought.
Mainly chemotaxis genes are seen, whereas only one phototaxis genes is seen in one plasmid. It
could be that photosynthesis is less critical for these hosts and therefore results in fewer genes being
shared through plasmids. Another explanation could be that little movement is needed in hypolith
environments as the semiôÙtranslucent rocks allows cyanobacteria to find more permanent locations
through phototaxis, but beyond that, phototaxis is not an usefull function.
A few genes involved with surface adhesion and movement using filamentous haemagglutinin doôÙ
mains was observed in bin_3. Such genes are found to function as an adhesin in other organism and
could play a similar role in Cyanobacteria[66]. Adhering to surfaces could prevent the host from being
moved into unfavourable environments during the rain events seen in the coastal fog areas[5].

to find genes related to repairing DNA exposed to UV radiation.
The discrepancy between the lack of phototaxis genes and the plethora of UV DNA repair genes could

4.9.6
Plasmid specific genes
One type of genes expected to show up in plasmids is TA systems. These systems regulate the
viability of plasmids in hosts. Several different toxin and antitoxin were found, but none was annoôÙ

Three of the TA systems did not show a corresponding toxin/antitoxin even after the second curation.
There could be two explanations for the absence. It could be that the systems in question lost the
function during a mutation or replications error, most likely resulting in termination or growth inhibition
in any organism integrating or translating the plasmid. The other explanation could be misannotation
by eggNOG, and therefore, the information was lost in silico.
The isolated Cas1 gene in plasmid 188 was surprising, as Cas1 genes usually associate with Cas2
and other companion genes[36]. The lack of Cas1 companion genes in plasmid 188 could indicate
that eggNOG did not annotate the genes or that, in the creation of the plasmid, these genes were not
included. The genes could also be mutated to such a degree that eggNOG could not recognise them.

of CRISPRôÙcas system is associated with plasmids 191, as only type IIIôÙA or IIIôÙB contains these
subunits. In both systems, it would be expected that Cas7 would be present in some form, and
therefore, presenting the explanation that Cas10 possible has no function in this plasmid[36].

Conclusion
In this paper it was proved that linking host of PB_bin_1 and PB_bin_3 to plasmids is possible using
methylation motifs from PacBio sequencing. Further, it was possible to extract the identical plasmids
from the more complete PacBioôÙIllumina hybrid bin_3 and bin_8 assemblies using the methylation
motifs as a basis for extraction.
Based on the differential abundance and marker gene analysis, it was not possible to differentiate
the bin_3 and bin_8 hosts, even though dereplication analysis and methylation motif strongly indicate
this to be the case. Both host and associated plasmids was found to be Cyanobacteria which was
expected.
It was impossible to link the found plasmids to any known plasmids indicating the plasmids are unique
in this context. Analysis of the COG terms showed that the primary type of annotated genes links to
DNA repair supported by the manual curation of MGEs and DNA repair genes. The manual curation
expected to find many genes involved with motility, DNA repair, environmental resistance, MGEs and
plasmid regulation. However, the examination showed that only one per cent of genes was associôÙ
ated with motility and the main part was involved with chemotaxis and not phototaxis as predicted.
Genes related to environmental resistance and DNA repair were more numerous and were focused
on survival the arid condition present in Atacama.
