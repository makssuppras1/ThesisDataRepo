Abstract of thesis entitled
Semantics of space through embeddings and
visualization
Submitted by
Betlem AGUADO PÉREZ
for the degree of Industrial Engineering and Management
at Technical University of Denmark
in July, 2020
This thesis is an implementation of the PyTre Python package through three different
cases. This package provides a model that uses embeddings as a tool for categorical
variables treatment. Its main purposes are ﬁrst the dimensionality reduction that is
achieved compared with other methods (e.g. dummy variables) and second the mean-
ing that can give to these variables and the consequences that it generates, as semantic
similarity measurements. The three cases where this model will be used are a) for giv-
ing meaning to Copenhagen areas regarding the travel purposes that people that go
there have b) measure similarities between different zones of the city of Santiago de
Chile depending on human visual perceptions and c) to extract the relationship be-
tween the mobility characteristics of a city and its C02 emissions and how this relation-
ship helps in the prediction task.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
This thesis is an implementation of the idea presented in [12]. The main point there is
focused on solving the problem of modelers to ﬁnd the right representation of different
phenomena. Usually, the models seem to enforce a compromise between the true se-
mantics of a variable and its realization in practice. This problem is comparable to the
dilemmas that the natural language processing (NLP) ﬁeld has had until recently when
thanks to the developments in deep neural networks together with massive amounts of
data (i.e. the World Wide Web) it is possible to achieve results such as question answer-
ing, translation or next-word prediction. One of the key concepts that helped with this
is that of embeddings, which will be the main methodology used on the development
of this thesis, and further explained later.
To propose a solution to that problem, a method called Travel Behaviour embed-
dings was introduced, which is based on the NLP concept. The idea there is a model
that can encode categorical variables, dependently on the problem one is focusing on.
This method proposes an alternative way of managing categorical variables, it solves
the limitations of dummy variables of augment of dimensionality and enforced orthog-
onality, and is different from principal component analysis (PCA) in that PCA is a non-
supervised approach while this model is supervised. This model is therefore capable
to extract the semantics of a variable based on a given context. It is openly available to
use in the PyTre Python package
An application for this model could be to extract the semantics of the space. Ur-
ban areas can be deﬁned as geographical zones with a high density of human structures
such as houses, commercial buildings, roads, bridges, and railways. Beyond that, based
on the characteristics and distribution of these points of interest (POIs), and how people
perceive and interact within them, two areas can be given a degree of similarity if one
analyzes them from a speciﬁc perspective. Therefore, two areas could be considered
similar if the present infrastructure and the use people give to these areas are the same.
Intuitively, the presence of a university campus in a given area implies buildings with
libraries, lecture halls, residence halls, student centers, or dining halls and presence a

younger demographic. While each university campus may differ to some degree, cam-
puses from one university and different universities share many of their characteristics,
while they can’t act as a substitute for a commercial area. Consequently, in the absence
of these POIs, e.g. libraries and lecture halls, an area won’t be considered similar to
the other from an educational perspective. It is possible to deduce that human percep-
tions of similarity regarding geographical zones or another topic should be described
by semantic similarity measures.
In this way, the PyTre Python package will be used in this thesis in order to extract
the similarity and relatedness of different sets of geographical areas. The similarity
will be based on the closeness of two areas regarding a speciﬁc perspective. From this
perspective, meaning is given to each area by generating the embeddings that describe
them, and these results can be analyzed to calculate the similarities. Furthermore, since
the model can extract information dependent on a target, a prediction task will be done,
using the generated embeddings for training the prediction model.
1.2
Contributions
In summary, the main contributions of this thesis are as follows:
• The concept of embeddings is further explained and an overview of the imple-
mentation of the PyTre algorithm is presented in order to understand the method-
ology used in the tree applications accomplished throughout this thesis
• The ﬁrst application is done using information about travel purposes and charac-
teristics of different zones of the urban area of Copenhagen.
• The second application focus on the visual perception that people have in differ-
ent areas of Santiago de Chile.
• In the last application, the relationship between transportation characteristics of
different cities and the CO2 emissions generated in these cities will be measured
and used in a prediction task.

perceive their environment. Some studies have found meaningful links between per-
ceptions and actual use of public spaces [1]. A relation between perceived neighbor-

between perceptions and use of public spaces, the simple question that arises is what
generates these perceptions. There have been several studies that relate the presence of
speciﬁc elements with perceptions, such as density [3] or vegetation [8].
Following this, researches using the concept of embeddings has been used to de-
scribe spaces. Points of interest (POIs) are used to describe places in [19] or to predict

Chapter 2
Methodology
2.1
Word embeddings
The idea of word embeddings comes from a simple re-representation necessity. An
NLP system is a numeric machine that requires each word in a dictionary to match its
numeric representation. For this, dummy variables could be used, but the dimensional-
ity of such representation becomes too high. The goal of text embeddings algorithms as
Word2Vec [10] is to reduce the representation dimensionality while learning the seman-
tic distance between the words, something that is not possible with dummy encoding
since it is set equal between all the words.
The methodology of word embeddings is based on the utilization of a neural net-
work model, with the architecture of ﬁgure 2.1. The method consists of giving a word
represented as its dummy encoding vector (one dummy variable for each word in a
dictionary of dimensionality D), it gives a vector with the same dimensionality that
corresponds to the probability of each word in the dictionary being the next one. The
dimensionality of this vector is called embedding size.

A fundamental observation to do here is that semantically similar words often
have similar contexts (i.e., the words appear around them) in sentences. Therefore, its
embedding vectors should be similar, since they represent the probabilities of other
words being the next one. Moreover, it is possible to do some linear algebra on them.
A well-known formulation is King - Man + Woman = Queen. Essentially, the vector
King - Man corresponds to the concept of “crowning" (therefore Woman+crowning =
Queen). Figure 2.2 shows other examples of embeddings algebra.
2.2
Travel behavior embeddings
Differently to textual data, the goal of travel behavior embeddings is to explore the
large amount of categorical data that is often collected in travel surveys [12]. The model
presented can preserve the semantic distance from a variable relative to a certain choice
problem. It creates a mapping between a variable for which is wanted to ﬁnd an em-
beddings representation, and a target variable, as in ﬁgure 2.3. The mapping function
is called “PyTre Embeddings" because that is the name of the object in the proposed
PyTre Python package.

2.3
Model Input
The aim of using the PyTre Embeddings is to generate an output that is capable to
describe better the behavior of a categorical variable regarding a target. This target, in
the case of the study of this thesis, are speciﬁc characteristics that deﬁne a place from
a deﬁned perspective. Therefore, the categorical variable that is proposed to be given
a meaning will be zone and will be called X’s of the input. The different alternatives
that this zone variable will have are the smallest geographical divisions of the whole
geographical zone that a dataset consists of.
To describe these zones, variables that are related or descriptive of the target will
be used and will be called Y’s of the input. Usually, these descriptive variables will be
given as continuous variables, but for the model to facilitate the task of extracting in-
formation from them, a discretization will be done previously. This is, all the variables
that are given to the model as Y’s will be discretized. The discretization task will deter-
mine different categories of each variable depending on the maximum and minimum
values that are represented.
Finally, more variables can be given to the model as exogenous variables to give
extra context to create the embeddings. Variables used as exogenous will be given as
continuous values.

Chapter 3
Travel purposes representation
The goal of this section is to extract closeness between stations regarding the travel pur-
poses of people traveling there and the area characteristics where are located. To do so,
a dataset containing information of different zones from the urban area of Copenhagen
has been created and has been used as the input for PyTre model. This area includes
the city of Copenhagen, capital and most populous city of Denmark, and its surround-
ing municipalities. An embedding vector for each zone has been generated as output,
based on its characteristics.
3.1
Dataset
The created dataset includes information from 89 areas corresponding to the location
of the train and metro stations of the greater area of Copenhagen presented in ﬁgure
3.1, and the local train stations of line 910 (Jægersborg - Nærum).
Figure 3.1: Stations contained in the Copenhagen dataset

For each station, information from several sources has been retrieved to describe
them according to the purpose of the section.
In order to do that, the Danish National Travel Survey (TU) dataset [4] has been
used to extract information about the travelers in each station. TU provides a consis-
tent, statistical picture of transport in Denmark for Danish residents over 6 years of age.
It has been conducted throughout several years (current survey since 2006) and is the
primary source of knowledge about how Danish residents travel on foot and by bike.
TU can supply consistent knowledge about the purpose of single trips and describes
how individuals and transport patterns are related.
Regarding the surroundings of each station, information on the area where they
are located such as land use, type of buildings, number of restaurants, and so on has
been downloaded from Open Street Maps and Foursquare application programming
interfaces (APIs). For the stations located in the Copenhagen area, a radius of 500m
has been considered, while a radius of 1000m has been considered for the rest of the
stations. Due to overlappings of some areas that are closer than the radius set, Dyb-
bølsbro station, Vesterport station and København H (Copenhagen Central station) are
considered as one. The three stations located in Lyngby are considered one as well.
Finally, Nørrebro and Bispebjerg stations are also considered as one.
Furthermore, information about burglaries made in the municipalities where the
stations are located have been taken from Statistics Denmark [5].
The information on the variables in its original form is continuous. As explained in
section 2 the data has been discretized. A complete list with the 52 variables, what they
represent, sources, and categories in which they are discretized is presented in table
3.1.

3.4
Output
The output of this experiment are 89 vectors, one corresponding to each area, of dimen-
sion 20 that represent with continuous values the 52 discretized variables given to the
model.
Since the dimensionality of embeddings is higher than 2, it becomes humanly
impossible to directly visualize them. A common technique to use is called Multi-
dimensional scaling (MDS). In such a visualization, data is projected in 2D space by
maintaining the same vector-to-vector distances as in the original. Therefore the X and
Y axes have no speciﬁc meaning, only distances between every pair of points are rel-
evant. Figure 3.4 shows the MDS visualizations for all stations in the dataset. The
interpretation of such visualization needs to focus on the similarity between areas. If
two areas are very close, it means that, from the perspective of travel purposes and
place characteristics, they have a similar meaning.

3.5
Discussion of the results
In ﬁgure 3.4 can be seen that Copenhagen Central Station and Kogens Nytorv are both
far away from the others. Considering their special characteristics for being a mix of
touristic, commercial, and business areas this location in the plot can make sense. The
stations located on the left have some similarities across them, being popular zones in

Visual feeling of the place
For this application, zones from the city of Santiago de Chile has been considered. San-
tiago is the capital and largest city of Chile as well as one of the largest cities in the
Americas. In this case, the similarity between these zones will be analyzed regarding
people’s visual perception of them. This is, the embeddings will be generated based on
data about the feeling of people when they are presented images from these zones and
the features that are in them.
4.1
Dataset
Two of the datasets used in [13] are considered here. They will be merged, and after-
ward, be divided into the zones considered.
4.1.1
Perceptual indicators
The perceptual indicators belong to the Place Pulse 2.0 dataset [6]. This dataset contains
indicators of urban and suburban public spaces based on the responses of a survey re-
garding people’s perceptions around the world. Every respondent is presented with
two images taken at random from within a city and is asked to state which one ad-
justs better to a certain adjective. Note that responses are given concerning a speciﬁc
pair of images and a speciﬁc adjective. The dataset includes responses for the follow-
ing qualitative attributes: “Beautiful”, “Boring”, “Depressing”, “Lively”, “Safe”, and
“Wealthy”. The images were taken from Google Street View (GSV), thus, they gener-
ally show streets during the day that do not contain many pedestrians or cyclists.
4.1.2
High level features
To have contextual information about why people would have these perceptions of the
places, an analysis of what is in the images was done in [13]. For each of the images
from Santiago, semantic segmentation is done. The problem of semantic segmentation
is deﬁned as the assignation of each of the pixels in an image to one possible category,
where categories are predeﬁned and generally correspond to semantically meaningful
==PAGE:29==
16
Chapter 4. Visual feeling of the place
visual elements, like cars, buildings, or pedestrians. SegNet method [2] was used for
these purpose.
The dataset obtained after this process and used in this work is, therefore, a set of
22 features that state the weight of “Building”, “Car”, “Cyclist”, “Fence”, “Pedestrian”,
“Pole”, “Road”, “Sidewalk”, “Sky”, “Trafﬁc sign”, and “Vegetation” visual categories
in the upper and lower half of each image.
4.1.3
Final dataset
Both datasets mentioned above have been merged in a way that each row represents
an image, with a total of 120085 images, and the columns correspond to the 6 percep-
tual indicators and the 22 high-level features stated above. Furthermore, based on the
location of an image it has assigned to a certain zone within the city of Santiago. For
the model better performance according to section 2 the values of the indicators and
features has been discretized based on their value into min, low, med, high, and max
categories.
The geographical division of Santiago has been made according to the communes

to calculate the embeddings corresponding to each city. City embeddings can be con-

Discussion of the results
This case shows numerically that embeddings generated with PyTre can ﬁnd the re-
lationship between the encoding variables (X’s) and the target (Y’s) or perspective of
meaning that one decides to give them. Learning how mobility features are related
with the CO2 emissions from a set of cities, it is possible to generate city embeddings of
other cities, based on their mobility features that describe better their behavior respect
to C02 emissions, and therefore the prediction task of this variable shows better per-
formance when the city embeddings are used as explanatory variables instead of the
mobility features itself.
Furthermore, the model is also able to give a meaning where missing values are
and allows generating a value that can be used in the prediction task, being impossible
before.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Throughout the three cases where the PyTre model has been used to generate embed-
dings, it has been seen that interesting results are obtained. By using this methodology
to generate vectors that can give information about a geographical area based on a
target, new descriptive variables can be generated and implemented in other models.
The good thing about these new variables is that describe better a zone from a speciﬁc
angle. This angle depends on one’s decision of what is wanted to represent, maybe
something that adds value to the existing information and helps in future works.
As seen in the last application, this method can extract some information in zones
where poor data is available. Learning from zones where data is accessible, the model
can give meaning to missing values and thus, generate a value to replace the missing
spaces of other zones. Further research on this application should be done to state that
this theory is consistent with other experiments.
Furthermore, interesting future work on this line would be to do some algebra with
zone embeddings (e.g. from different cities). The two main problems for doing this are
1) the lack of a function that measures the accuracy of the embedding representations,
now it is only based on individual human perceptions and 2) the difﬁculty to obtain two
datasets from different cities with the same variables to make consistent comparisons.
