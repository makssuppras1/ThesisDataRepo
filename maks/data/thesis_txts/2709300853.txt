Abstract 
Through exploration of novel yeast for production of new exiting products, the non-saccharomyces yeast, 
Wicherhamomyces anomalus, is known to contribute to increased flavour complexity in cider, as well as 
other potential beneficial traits. Meanwhile the apple industry worldwide is known to produce vast 
amounts of the waste product apple pulp (apple pomace). Valorising this waste stream would increase the 
revenue, and this sustainable approach to utilizing the apple pomace would support a circular economy 
more than the current disposals of apple pomace. The work conducted in this project has the objective to 
characterize some of the fermentative properties exhibited by W. anomalus, when grown in an apple 
pomace derived media. 
In order to characterize W. anomalus, it has been grown under various conditions in the apple pomace 
derived media (CAP media) and under aerobic conditions in YPD media. The growth in YPD at 30 °C under 
aerobic conditions indicated µm = 0.47 h-1. This experiment in YPD suggested that the strain is Crabtree 
positive as it produced ethanol at roughly 0.3 g ethanol/g glucose, where no ethanol production was 
present in the aerobic fermentation in CAP media at 30 °C, which contain less sugars. The aerobic 
fermentation at 30 °C in CAP media had µm = 0.42 h-1, where at 20 °C had a µm = 0.215 h-1. 
When grown anaerobically in CAP media growth rates were found to be µm = 0.055 h-1, µm = 0.092 h-1 and 
µm = 0.134 h-1 for 15 °C, 20 °C and 30 °C respectively. The anaerobic fermentation at 20 °C indicated the 
highest ethanol yield producing 0.39 g ethanol/g sugar, where 15 °C and 30 °C produced 0.337 and 0.35 g 
ethanol/g sugar respectively. The glycerol productions were found to be 0.202, 0.227 and 0.213 g glycerol/g 
sugar for 15 ˚C, 20 ˚C and 30 ˚C respectively. 
In fermentations at 30 °C in CAP media with increasing glucose concentrations no effect of sugar 
concentrations with addition of up till 15% glucose were shown. An experiment carried out in CAP media at 
different ethanol concentrations indicated that growth was completely inhibited in the area between 50 g 
ethanol/L and 100g ethanol/L. 
From samples taken from aerobic and anaerobic fermentations in CAP media a plate inhibition assay 
indicated, that under the investigated conditions, there were no inhibition of L. brevis, when in contact with 
the filtered samples.

Introduction 
Cider is one of the oldest beverages found in the world together with the other major fermented products 
i.e. beer and wine. The word cider would originate from sicera, which is a word meaning “any fermented 
beverage that is not wine”. The general definition used to describe cider is, an alcoholic beverage obtained 
by fermenting apple juice (apple must), although cider is a term often used to reflect a large variety of 
fermented apple products1. 
In traditional cider making spontaneous fermentation was carried out by a variety of different organisms 
which included both Saccharomyces species and non-Saccharomyces yeasts2,3. Today the common way to 
produce most fermented beverages is to inoculate with a pure yeast culture, most commonly 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae (S. cerevisiae). Using a pure yeast culture will allow for greater control of the 
process, this gives a higher reproducibility and a more consistent quality (little to no sensory deviation in 
the final product)3,4. However, this pure culture fermentation would limit the complexity in flavour and 
unique aromas, which was formerly generated from the diversity of organisms found in the spontaneous 
fermentations3. This caused some producers to go back to the spontaneously fermentation providing more 
character and complexity at the cost of lower reproduceability3. 
Among the non-saccharomyces yeasts that potentially could be present in spontaneous fermentations are 
the yeast Wicherhamomyces anomalus (W. anomalus) formerly known as Pichia anomala4. W. anomalus is 
labled as a biosafety level 1 organism (by the European Food Safety Authority) meaning it is considered to 
be safe for consumption by healthy individuals. This is based on the fact that it is known to only have 
caused few cases of human infection and only in individuals with serious illness5.   
When looking at non-saccharomyces yeasts found in spontaneous fermentations some yeasts can be 
associated with off-flavour production and in some cases unacceptable flavours (phenol, mousy and 
vinegary) by e.g. Brettanomyces spp.4. In recent years the interest in finding non-saccharomyces yeasts with 
flavour enhancing effects, good aroma profile and other beneficial effects have increased3,4. A species such 
as W. anomalus is reported to increase flavour complexity when grown in coculture with S. cerevisiae4. 
Further W. anomalus is of interest since it has been reported to be a producer of Killer toxins (KTs) able to 
inhibit growth of different organisms6. 
Apple pomace (which is comprised of pulp, peel, seed and stem) is a residue (considered a waste product) 
which is generated through various industries e.g. apple juice, cider and jam productions7. This waste 
product is generated in such large amounts that in 2010 the global apple pomace generation was expected 
to exceed 3600 kilotons per year7. Traditionally apple pomace has been used as crude feed or forage 
==PAGE:8==
Page 7 of 66 
 
extender for livestock (to reduce waste processing costs) even though it is low in protein compared to 
other types of feed7. Apple pomace as a waste product is considered to contain high amounts of nutrients 
and by utilizing this large amount of by product would be valuable and reduce environmental impact7. 
This study will focus on characterizing the growth of W. anomalus (DK nr. 466) when grown in an apple 
pomace (apple pulp) derived media both aerobically as well as anaerobically. Other than growth, the 
production of ethanol and glycerol will be described by calculation of yield coefficients. Effects on sugar 
concentration and ethanol concentration will be investigated.

Theory 
When characterizing an organism, it can be a powerful tool to look at how the theory is used to describe 
how similar organisms work, since many of the systems and methods used to describe similar organisms 
will give an indication of how to understand the behaviour of the organism of interest. For the theory in the 
following chapter most will originate from studies conducted on S. cerevisiae as this is the most studied 
yeast there is8. This theory section will try and cover the relevant theory in order to better understand the 
experimental work conducted. 
 
Yeast 
Yeasts are eukaryotic organisms that along with organisms such as moulds and mushrooms fits into the 
diverse kingdom of fungi9,10. The shapes of oval, spherical or cylindrical is the common appearance taken by 
yeasts. Yeasts are single celled organisms, in that regard similar to bacterial cells, however yeast cells are 
typically of a much larger size, and this difference in size can therefore be used to microscopically 
distinguish between the two types of organisms. Additionally, yeasts hold noticeable signs of having 
internal cell structures, which the bacteria do not have (e.g. nucleus or cytoplasmic vacuoles) this is 
noticeable microscopically10.  
The term budding fungi is a term given to yeasts because they normally reproduce via a budding process11. 
Budding is a cell division type where a bud (small bubble looking bump) starts to emerge from the parent 
cell. Over time the bud grows larger and eventually separates itself from the parent cell10,11. Yeasts are fast 
growing organisms and are able to grow in different environments succeeding well in sugar-rich 
environments such as e.g. flowers and fruits10,11. 
Yeasts and here especially S. cerevisiae is of great importance in many biotechnological applications such as 
e.g. food (especially the beverage- and baking-industry) and biofuel production industries8,10. 
 
Yeast growth 
Yeast growth is important to characterize to be able to control and predict how a yeast behaves under 
various conditions. Underneath, the general growth model for yeast is explained. 
When a microorganism such as yeast is introduced into a new environment and the growth conditions for 
the organism is met then the organism begin to grow. These growth conditions can be many including the 
==PAGE:11==
Page 10 of 66 
 
water content, availability of nutrients (e.g. carbon source, nitrogen source and trace metals), the pH value 
and the temperature amongst others10,11. 
If the above-mentioned requirements are met, when yeast is introduced into a new environment then the 
growth of the yeast will follow a characteristic growth pattern represented by the following phases shown 
in figure 111. 
 
 
Figure 1: With time on the x-axis and yeast cell number on the log transformed y-axis, this figure displays the characteristic phases 
of yeast growth. 1 - lag phase, 2 – acceleration phase, 3 – exponential phase, 4 – deceleration phase, 5 – stationary phase, 6 – 
death/declining phase11. 
 
The different phases of yeast growth can be placed into 6 phases, and they are described as follows: 
Lag phase 
The lag phase is the initial phase where no growth is observed10. During the lag phase, the cells are 
adjusting themselves to the media and thereby activating the metabolism. This adaptation can vary a lot in 
the duration it takes, but factors that can influence this time could be related to e.g. nutrients, the type of 
organism, the age of the culture being inoculated and if there is a big difference from the conditions that it 
==PAGE:12==
Page 11 of 66 
 
had been growing in prior to the inoculation10,11. The lag phase will end as soon as the first cell division 
takes place11. 
Acceleration phase 
This phase is short, the growth rate is increasing until the organism reaches the exponential phase. 
Exponential phase 
In this phase the growth of the organism is exponential and at its maximal capacity, given the conditions. 
The growth rate will be constant during this phase and the cell will usually be found to be in its healthiest 
state of the growth curve10,11. 
Deceleration phase 
The exponential phase will continue until something in the environment will start to decelerate the growth 
rate. This can be due to multiple factors e.g. a reduction in nutrients does so exponential growth cannot be 
supported thereby reducing the overall growth rate10,11. Additionally, the exponential phase could end if 
the amount of a growth-inhibiting product becomes high enough to influence the growth rate. During the 
deceleration phase, the growth rate continuously decreases and leads towards the stationary phase11. 
Stationary phase 
During the stationary phase, the overall growth rate should be zero10. This is caused by a balance between 
the formation of new cells and the decay of cells causing the number of living cells to be constant11. When 
some cells die, and others grow at a balance with no net increase or decrease its described as cryptic 
growth10. 
Death phase/declining phase 
This phase is often initialised by depletion of the nutrient supply, which leads to an increasing rate of cell 
deaths that decreases the total number of living cells11.

Yeast metabolism 
When looking at growth parameters and available nutrient sources when trying to understand why certain 
compounds are produced or consumed, by the organism, its essential to know something about the 
metabolism. Since the parameters the yeast is grown under can change the production and consumption of 
different10. The metabolism can be divided into two major processes: anabolism and catabolism10. The 
anabolism uses energy to perform the biosynthesis of compounds necessary for cell growth. The 
catabolism, which will be the main focus of this section, is a process, where organic compounds (e.g. 
glucose) is broken down to provide the cell with the necessary energy to perform all the energy-consuming 
processes in the cell10. 
The catabolism is characterised by two metabolic pathways to generate the required energy from the 
available substrate through either fermentation or respiration. The choice of which pathways the cell will 
use for growth is determined by the present condition from the environment that the organism is in10. 
Yeasts usually fall into the category of being facultative aerobes, which are organisms growing both under 
aerobic conditions (in presence of oxygen) and anaerobic conditions (no oxygen present)10. However, 
typically when oxygen is present the preferred strategy for the cell is to use respiration instead of 
fermentation. This is due to the larger amount of energy that the cell can produce per glucose molecule 
from oxidative phosphorylation of ATP (38 ATP) compared to the substrate level phosphorylation of ATP (2 
ATP) from fermentation10,11. Besides the fact that the yeast achieves less energy under anaerobic 
conditions, fermentation as a metabolic option still provides enough energy to thrive10. 
Fermentation and respiration are similar at first as both initially starts by utilizing glycolysis to break down 
glucose and ending up with pyruvate. The difference is that under conditions that allow for respiration 
(availability of oxygen) the pyruvate is sent to the mitochondria to initiate the citric acid cycle (CAC) to 
produce more energy whereas under anaerobic conditions the pyruvate is broken down in the cytoplasm of 
the cell10,11.

Respiration 
As previously mentioned then during respiration the first step of glycolysis is the same, meaning steps 1-10 
in figure 2, which is true for respiration as well10. However, instead of converting pyruvate into products 
that are excreted from the cell (such as ethanol), then respiration utilises pyruvate to be oxidised into CO2 
through the CAC, figure 3 displays the steps from where pyruvate is converted into acetyl-CoA and enters 
the CAC10. The initial step is when pyruvate is decarboxylated to produce the energy-rich compound acetyl-
CoA, yielding CO2 and NADH. When acetyl-CoA is entering the cycle then 1 ATP, 3 NADH and 1 FADH2 is 
produced see figure 314. However, since each glucose molecule turns into 2 pyruvate molecules then the 
total yield is 2 ATP, 8 NADH and 2 FADH210. This means that a total of 4 ATP, 10 NADH and 2 FADH2 is 
produced from the glycolysis and CAC combined during respiration10. Earlier it was mentioned that 38 ATP 
is produced for each glucose molecule during respiration. The ATP molecules are generated by the proton 
motive force combined with ATP synthase. The generation of proton motive force is happening when 
protein complexes in the mitochondria membrane is transporting H+ ions out of the membrane by oxidizing 
NADH to NAD+ and FADH2 to FAD (using O2 for the oxidation)10. Another membrane protein called ATP 
==PAGE:16==
Page 15 of 66 
 
synthase which is driven by the proton motive force transports the H+ ions back into the cell and in the 
process turns ADP into ATP by binding phosphor. For each NADH molecule 3 ATP is created and for each 
FADH2 2 ATP will be created, thus allowing the sum of respiration to be 38 ATP per glucose molecule10.

Some of the intermediate compounds produced during the CAC are drawn off in small amounts and used 
for biosynthesis during cell growth. This is such as oxaloacetate and α-ketoglutarate, which are precursors 
for many amino acids10. 
 
Crabtree effect 
For S. cerevisiae the Crabtree effect can be described as repression of the respiration in the presence of 
sugars under aerobic conditions15. Further Crabtree effect is when alcoholic fermentation takes place under 
aerobic conditions16. This effect is seen when sugar concentrations are increased to values as to where the 
==PAGE:17==
Page 16 of 66 
 
respiratory chain is not able to oxidize the amount NADH that is produced during glycolysis and the CAC. 
This causes the cell to utilize the alcoholic fermentation to oxidize NADH and this way ethanol is 
produced16.  
 
Alternative carbon metabolism 
If the environment is depleted of simple sugars such as glucose or fructose the cell needs to use alternative 
methods to gain energy for growth, a way to get this is through the glyoxylate cycle10. 
The glyoxylate cycle is a variety of the of the CAC which allows a two-carbon compound such as ethanol, 
glycerol, lactate, acetate and fatty acids to be used as a substrate for growth10,17. When a two-carbon 
compound is used for growth the reactions from the glyoxylate cycle and the CAC are taking place in 
conjunction with one another10.   
When ethanol is utilized as a carbon source its believed to diffuse though the cell wall were its first 
converted to acetaldehyde, which is converted to acetate and finally acetyl-CoA which can enter the 
glyoxylate cycle17.  
 
High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) 
High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) is an analytical method that can be utilized for quality and 
quantity analysis of many different chemicals and pharmaceutical compounds (e.g. Proteins, amino acids, 
sugars and more)18–21.  
HPLC works in a similar way as other column techniques when separating a sample, e.g. size or charge, but 
it can be considered an enhanced version of these since the column material is more finely divided and 
therefore has more interaction sites18. This finer material division in the column makes it more difficult for 
the sample to pass through the column, and therefore, the application of pressure is required to achieve 
acceptable flowrates18. The result is a method with a high resolution and fast separation18. 
The stronger a compound binds to the column or the longer it is trapped the later it will be released and 
thus the separation happens (the faster the release the faster the retention time)18,22. Depending on the 
separation problem different mobile phases can be used to help the separation22. In common setups, then 
after the separation in the column a detector is placed which will measure the absorbance of the eluate at 
a specific wavelength18.  
==PAGE:18==
Page 17 of 66 
 
 
Once the sample is measured the HPLC result will provide a curve with peaks measured in absorbance at 
different retention times depending on when the different compounds were released from the column18.

Literature review 
 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
The yeast S. cerevisiae is the best studied eukaryotic organism with a fully sequenced genome8. Being this 
well researched makes S. cerevisiae a model organism and thus a valuable tool in the basic research of 
other organisms8,27. 
Depending on the strain S. cerevisiae there would be deviations as to which conditions are optimal for the 
organism. The studying of the yeast indicate that S. cerevisiae has optimal growth between pH levels of 4.5-
6.5 at temperatures of 25-35 ˚C and can grow in ethanol concentrations around 8-12%28. Some studies 
suggest that the optimal growth for S. cerevisiae has been found at pH 4 and temperatures of 33-34 ˚C29,30. 
When put in a media S. cerevisiae will utilize the simpler sugars first before it starts to consume the more 
complex sugars11. In S. cerevisiae the hexoses, glucose and fructose, is known to be transported through 
the membrane by a common membrane carrier31. When grown in a media with both glucose and fructose it 
is known to utilize both sugars at the same time. However, glucose is consumed at a faster rate than that of 
fructose, which indicates that glucose competitively inhibits the uptake of fructose by the membrane 
carrier31. 
As mentioned earlier in the theory section the respiration under aerobic conditions is much more 
favourable when looking at energy yield and the anaerobic fermentation. An aerobic chemostat cultivation 
by Hoek et al. 1998 found S. cerevisiae to have a µm of 0.42 h-1 when grown in a defined mineral media with 
vitamins and glucose at 30 ˚C32. Another study conducted by Paalme et al. 1997 indicated a µm of 0.44 when 
grown at 28 ˚C on glucose with addition of various nutrients33. 
On the contrary, when grown anaerobically, S. cerevisiae is known to produce ethanol in quantities up 
towards 90-93% of the theoretical maximum (theoretical maximum is 0.51 g ethanol/g glucose34), meaning 
around 0.46-0.475 g ethanol/g glucose utilizing the glycolytic pathway12. S. cerevisiae is a highly alcohol 
tolerant organism allowing it to ferment high alcohol products and is known to grow at 13% ethanol, but at 
14% no growth is detected35. According to Wang et al. 2004 the maximum specific growth rates (µm) of S. 
cerevisiae when grown anaerobically on glucose, fructose were 0.0945 h-1 and 0.08 h-1 respectively when 
grown at 15 ˚C in synthetically mixed “ideal” apple juice media36.

Research indicates that when S. cerevisiae is grown anaerobically the production of glycerol is known to be 
the second most produced byproduct (after ethanol)37. Under these anaerobic conditions, when no oxygen 
is present to oxidize NADH then there will be an excess amount of NADH formed during cell growth, here 
the formation of glycerol is a vital part of the reoxidation of NADH to NAD+37. The production of glycerol is 
very strain dependent where values between 4.2-10.4g/L have been observed by Radler et al. 198238.  
 
Wickerhamomyces anomalus 
The biotechnological relevant yeast species W. anomalus is reported to have multiple uses in different 
industrial applications such as environmental, industrial, medicinal and the food industry39. Here, the food 
industry, more specifically the industry of fermented beverages, is of interest. To be able to effectively use 
an organism for production purposes there are numerous things to consider and variables that needs to be 
known, this section will describe some of the previously reported information about W. anomalus. 
A guide made from Graham H. Fleet combines many works and mentions growth ranges of some 
parameters for W. anomalus40. This guide mentions environmental factors for W. anomalus to grow in the 
ranges of 3-37 °C, 2-10 pH and more40. A study from Romero-Gil et al. 2013 finds values for W. anomalus to 
be 29.3 ˚C as the optimal growth temperature, 38.2 °C as the maximum temperature and 2.8 °C as 
minimum temperature for cell growth41.  
The ethanol production of W. anomalus are not very different to what was mentioned above in the S. 
cerevisiae section, this is indicated by experimental data by Atitallah et al. 2020 indicates that W. anomalus 
has the capacity to produce 0.46 g ethanol/g of glucose which corresponds to just above 90% of the 
theoretical maximum. Furthermore, they find a µm of 0.39 h-1 when grown on date palm sap25. 
One of the things that make W. anomalus biotechnologically interesting is its ability to produce 
glycoproteins also known as mycocins or killer toxins (KTs), which is described many places in the 
literature6,42–47. The production of these KTs makes W. anomalus a killer yeast, other yeasts may be 
described as sensitive due to suffering from the killer action or neutral as they neither produce KTs or suffer 
the killer action6. W. anomalus was the first yeast found to inhibit both eukaryotic and prokaryotic 
organisms6. As an example, Junges et al. 2020 performed a study indicating inhibition of the gram-negative 
organism Acinetobacter baumannii by addition of supernatant from W. anomalus cultures6. Thus, there are 
different KTs produced with different modes of action, some mechanisms are known while others remain 
unidentified6. Among the KTs some of their mechanism has been proposed an example being glucanase-like 
==PAGE:21==

mycocins which exhibits the ability of hydrolysis of the cell membrane components β-1.3-glucan and β-1.6-
glucan causing cytoplasmic components to leave the cell and result in cell death6.

The amino acids present are the main source of yeast assimilable nitrogen (YAN)2,52. YAN is described as the

Low YAN values are known to cause slow or incomplete fermentations and, in some instances, increase the 
production of hydrogen sulphide (H2S)2,52,53. H2S is an off-flavour usually described to have an odour of 
rotten eggs or cabbage52,54. H2S is produced as an intermediate product in the synthesis of the amino acids, 
methionine and cysteine52. However, in fermentations with low YAN content, the organism lacks the amino 
acid precursors which reacts with H2S to produce methionine and cysteine then the excess H2S will be able 
to permeate the cell membrane52,54. YAN content is known to be low for fermentation of cider and thus 
nutrient addition is common48,52.

and niacin55.  
The minerals present in apple juice include iron (Fe), calcium (Ca), potassium (K), sodium (Na), magnesium

Cider production 
Considering that cider is a product invented a long time ago, there are multiple ways to produce said 
product2. One way of producing cider is shown in the diagram of figure 4 below. 
 
 
Figure 4: This figure displays a diagram of the traditional steps to produce cider according to a French cider style2,57. 
 
As displayed above the first step of cider making is the harvest of the apples. The storage of the apples is 
done for a maximum of up to 5 days2. This period of storage is carried out to mature the apples to ensure 
that as much of the starch is converted into sugars as possible, and further, it also increases the 
compatibility for an optimal pressing of apples2. 
Ciders are seldom a product achieved from a single apple cultivar and therefore tradition calls for a mixed 
blend of apples to allow for a more balanced cider in regard to parameters such as taste, smell and 
appearance2.  
 
==PAGE:24==
Page 23 of 66 
 
The sorting and washing of the apples have the purpose of removing poor quality fruit and foreign 
materials. These materials could be stones, soil and wood material which has an impact on the quality of 
the product2. The fruits with poor quality are rotten, and this decaying fruit is associated with off flavours. 
Furthermore, the rotten fruits are connected to mould contamination, and this can be a health hazard, 
since some moulds have the ability to produce mycotoxins which has a negative impact on human health2. 
After the sorting, the fruits that are of good quality will be milled. The mill is a machine where graters or 
coarse knives will break the whole apples into pulp without crushing the seeds2. The advantage of breaking 
the apples into pulp is that this allows for higher yields of apple juice (or apple must) in the press. The 
pressing itself can be done by many different press types the typical ones found are horizontal presses and 
continuous belt presses2. 
The press provides two products, the juice and the pomace. It is possible to add water to the pomace and 
repress it to extract more sugars. However, this will usually be added to the juice from the first extraction 
and water down the juice a bit2. 
Prior to the fermentation step, the juice can be treated with the addition of SO2. The purpose of SO2 
addition is that it exhibits antiseptic properties towards non-saccharomyces yeast and bacteria2. Other 
types of pre-fermentation treatment could be as keeving where yeast nutrients are reduced in the juice by 
binding it to pectin58. This is possible because enzymes in the apples are able to partially hydrolyse the 
pectin into pectin anions which has an affinity for positively charged calcium cations. This will create a gel 
consisting of pectin, calcium, nitrogen compounds (thiamine and asparagine) and yeast2,58. This lower 
availability of nutrients will cause a slow fermentation and result in a sweeter cider58. A way to get a higher 
amount of pectin is to leave the apple pulp for around 24 hours this will release pectin from the cell walls, 
and this way increase the amount of pectin in the juice when pressed58. 
The traditional (French) way of fermenting is by using the indigenous yeast (and other flora) available and 
usually not done with a starter culture2,57. There are a few fermentation phases, the first one being the 
oxidative phase where there is a large microbial diversity present which will make various volatile 
compounds such as esters that will have a large contribution to the aromatic quality of the cider2. Then a 
second anaerobic phase called the alcoholic fermentation (as earlier described in the theory section) where 
saccharomyces sp. will convert glucose into CO2 and alcohol2. A third optional phase is the malolactic 
fermentation where different organisms such as Lactobacillus sp., Pediococcus sp. and Oenococcus oeni are 
transforming malic acid (main acid type of the apple juice) into lactic acid through decarboxylation2,57. This 
can be desired for reducing the acidity for organoleptic quality purposes, however, this will also mean that 
the cider now has a higher pH which makes the product less microbially stable2. 
==PAGE:25==
Page 24 of 66 
 
 
After the fermentation, the cider is racked to separate the cider and the lees/sediment to stabilize the 
product. However, when needed, further stabilization can be reached by finning, centrifugation or 
filtration2. Finning is when proteins are added to react with polyphenols, this complex will sediment and 
entrap compounds as they do. Centrifugation and filtration are other ways of separating microbes and 
other compounds from the cider2. 
The ciders are then blended to get closer to the product that the cidermaker wants to achieve. English 
ciders use water if a dilution is needed to reach a certain alcohol percentage. Other additives could be 
colouring agents, preservatives (e.g. SO2), sugar or sweetening agents, these are all to reach the desired 
end product2. 
When the adjustment has been performed the bottling can be initiated. In figure 4 the carbonation is done 
in the bottle this is called prise de mousse in French and refers to a natural carbonation step where the 
yeast eats residual sugars to make CO2 and thus a natural effervescence2. This step can be performed after 
the fermentation or in the bottles followed by a maturation step2.

CAP media

from the media it was run through a coffee filter. The liquid was then mixed and poured into 2L bottles and 
sent to the service unit to be autoclaved. The autoclaved CAP media would then be stored at 4 ˚C until 
usage in the fermenters.

media before being added to the fermenter. Here solutions of 50 g ethanol /L, 100 g ethanol/L, 150 g 
ethanol/L and 200 g ethanol/L.

culture is far into the deacceleration phase transitioning into the stationary phase. This suggests that there 
had been a limiting factor in one of the nutrients of the CAP media, and it was not the sugar concentration 
in this case. Based on the literature review it could be suggested that it was the YAN concentration in the 
CAP media, however this is not clear since the media has not been further investigated for the available 
nitrogen, in this study. 
No glycerol peaks were detected by the HPLC this would suggest that the culture is not oxygen limited as 
described in the S. cerevisiae section glycerol is known to be produced to oxidize NADH when oxygen is 
scarce. 
When looking at the ethanol production then the first 15 hours of the fermentation no ethanol is being 
produced, apart from one point at 4 hours which could potentially be an outlier or error. The general trend 
however shows that there is no ethanol production given these conditions. When comparing this to the 
above fermentation in YPD at 30 °C (figure 7) where ethanol is produced it could suggest that the 
==PAGE:38==
Page 37 of 66 
 
conditions in the YPD fermentation allow for the Crabtree effect to be in action. The oxygen (through the 
filtered air addition of 50 ccm) added to the YPD fermentation, would seem to be too low when the sugar 
concentration is at around 18 g/L. However, in the CAP media with around 9-10 g/L of sugar and 50 ccm of 
air no ethanol is produced and therefore suggests that there is no Crabtree effect present. Dissolved 
oxygen measurement would have helped this explanation (but were not available), since if dissolved 
oxygen were low enough the culture would use the alcoholic fermentation to help oxidize the NADH when 
oxygen would not be present to do so. However, further studies would have to be conducted in order to 
say exactly when the Crabtree effect would be present.

The way the media has been created in this study would not be feasible to do in a big production, since the 
pulp is blended with water. As described in the literature review the apple pulp contains around 2-4% 
seeds. These seeds are known to contain a significant amount of the compound amygdalin (1-3.9 mg/g 
seed)61. One of the effects of amygdalin is that it is known to be toxic62. For this reason, blending the pulp 
might be undesired unless the seeds are removed first, which raises another problem (the removal of the 
seeds). However, if either another method is used or if the seeds could be removed, then homogenizing the 
pulp with a blending type operation could work. 
To further increase the nutritional value in relation to sugars, then perhaps it could be beneficial to perform 
an acid or alkaline pretreatment. This pretreatment could potentially increase the sugar yield by disrupting 
the complex carbohydrate of lignin, as described by Magyar et al63. 
The experiment performed in YPD hinted at a Crabtree effect happening when compared to the Aerobic 
fermentation in CAP media. This area would need to be investigated further in order to know what DO-
values would be required to not have the Crabtree effect at different sugar concentrations. This kind of 
information would be interesting to know if you are a business producing yeast for selling to cideries or if 
you were a cidery who wanted to grow a batch of yeast that could be used for inoculation. Here biomass 
would be important, therefore if sugar was too high and/or oxygen too low then the Crabtree effect would 
cause the carbon source to go into ethanol production instead of the desired biomass production.  
When grown in CAP media anaerobically, this study suggests that up towards 0.39 g ethanol/g sugar (76.5% 
of theoretical maximum) is produced as seen in table 3. These anaerobic fermentations in CAP media 
suggested that 20 ˚C would be a preferred fermentation temperature over 30 ˚C and 15 ˚C if ethanol yield 
==PAGE:54==
Page 53 of 66 
 
and glycerol yield is the desired outcome of the fermentation. The yields found for all fermentations might 
potentially be higher, since the values presented in this study is not considering the evaporation which is 
known to happen in these fermenters. This would also correspond well with the literature which found 
higher yields, however, the exact values would need to be found. The literature suggests that W. anomalus 
is a good ethanol producer with similar capabilities as S. cerevisiae.  
From the ethanol tolerance experiment it was clear that there was growth at 6.3% ethanol v/v (50 g 
ethanol/L) and at 12.7% (100 g ethanol/L) there was no growth. To find the precise alcohol concentration 
where the growth is completely inhibited further studies need to be conducted. However, comparing the 
results of this study to the literature for S. cerevisiae then W. anomalus does not have as high of an alcohol 
tolerance as S. cerevisiae. 
Since W. anomalus were able to survive high concentrations of sugar the strain could potentially be used 
for higher gravity production, which is a way to save money from a producer’s perspective. This allows the 
producer to use less volume under fermentation and then later dilute with water. 
To be able to say if the W. anomalus strain used in the study can produce KTs, more studies need to be 
conducted. Initially an investigation of the genomic data of the stain can be conducted in order to tell if 
some of the known KTs are present. Thereafter it needs to be investigated at which conditions they are 
produced. With knowledge of when these KTs are produced this could be utilized to create a more stable 
cider like product and depending on what organisms can be inhibited this could reduce or remove the need 
for the addition of SO2 as is currently used (described in the literature review).  
The use of W. anomalus as a substitute to S. cerevisiae is very dependent on which product is desired. As an 
example, then if a high alcohol product is wanted through fermentation, then S. cerevisiae seems to have 
an edge in surviving higher concentrations of alcohol. However, if some useful KTs can be produced by W. 
anomalus then this could be desired for product stability. It should be noted that if KTs are produced then 
this could potentially cause issues in the malolactic fermentation unless the organism used is not affected 
by the produced KTs. Future studies of interest could be creating an aroma profile of W. anomalus, to see if 
the produced aromas are desired or not.

Conclusion   
The data acquired during this study would suggest that W. anomalus can achieve similar growth rates as 
found in the literature with slight deviation. However, when grown aerobically at 30 °C in CAP media the 
growth rates were a little less than in the nutrient rich synthetic YPD media (at 30 °C aerobically), which 
were µm = 0.42 h-1 and µm = 0.47 h-1 respectively. When grown aerobically at 20 °C in CAP media the growth 
rate was µm = 0.215, approximately half of the 30 ˚C fermentation. 
When W. anomalus was grown anaerobically the growth rates were found to be µm = 0.055 h-1, µm = 0.092 
h-1 and µm = 0.134 h-1 for 15 °C, 20 °C and 30 °C respectively. The anaerobic fermentation at 20 °C had yield 
coefficients of 0.39 g ethanol/g sugar and 0,227 g ethanol/g sugar which were higher than at 30 °C and 15 
°C. This suggest 20 °C as a more optimal fermentation temperature than 30 °C and 15 °C, if the aim is higher 
production of ethanol and glycerol. 
When looking at the modified CAP media, the CAP media fermentations with increased sugar 
concentrations did not seem to have an effect on the growth of W. anomalus. However increased ethanol 
concentrations indicated an effect on growth. The growth at 50 g ethanol/L were present and there were 
no growth at 100 g ethanol/L, this suggested that the tolerance for ethanol can be found in this interval. 
The production of killer toxins was neither confirmed or denied in this study and will require more research 
to know if any KTs can be produced when grown in CAP media. 
Both literature and the results in this study would suggest that W. anomalus is a promising production 
organism, depending on the desired end product.  
Apple pomace derived media proved to be a promising source of nutrients (especially sugars) for W. 
anomalus growth and can be further enhanced by addition of other nutrients, however the precise 
nutrients were not identified in this study.
