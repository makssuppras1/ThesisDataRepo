Abstract
This master thesis aims at developing a standardized model of an energy source that
supplies heat to an energy system integrated with a thermal energy storage (TES)
which can be used for multiple applications. The study performed is related to the
International Energy Agency (IEA) Annex 30 project which has the purpose of eval-
uating how to improve the TES in terms of efficiency and flexibility and therefore the
model is built to reach this goal.
The model is developed in three different configurations: Bypass, Charge and Dis-
charge. The first analyzes how the model performs when the TES is not used while
the others study, respectively, the process of charging and discharging of the storage.
To these, is added a Standstill configuration that assesses the behaviour of the storage
when is considered separated to the rest of the model. For all the configurations is
developed a mathematical model which is implemented in the equation-solving soft-
ware EES and used to perform the calculations required.
The model is verified examining a specific case study composed of a solar heating
plant as energy source and an Organic Rankine Cycle as energy system integrated
with a tank as TES. The verification is performed ascertaining that the results ob-
tained are reasonable and respect the laws of thermodynamics and the constraints
fixed. Finally, the performance of the specific case study is assessed performing the
exergy, the economic and the exergoeconomic analyses to understand which improve-
ments are necessary.
This study demonstrates that the model developed works properly, since the case
study considered is verified, and that the analyses performed are helpful to understand
which improvements need to be done.

1.1
Background
In the last decades, renewables gained increasing importance in the energy sector.
This is due not only to the demand of clean energy needed to reduce the global warm-
ing and the pollution of the environment, but also because the costs of producing
energy from renewables are falling, making these sources more competitive in the
market [18].
In 2016, due to the boost of the solar photovoltaic (PV) market, renewables ac-
counted for almost two-thirds of the net new power capacity around the world with
almost 165 GW coming online. In one year, new solar PV capacity around the world
grew by 50%, reaching over 74 GW, with China accounting for almost half of this
expansion. For the first time, as it can be seen in Fig. 1.1, solar PV additions rose
faster than any other fuel, surpassing the net growth of coal. [2]
Figure 1.1: Electricity capacity additions by fuel, 2016 [2].
The growth of energy produced by renewables was accompanied by the drop of
auction prices which reached record-low of 3 cents per kilowatt hour. Low announced

The International Energy Agency (IEA) forecasts an expansion of renewable elec-

require a continuous amount of energy. This constraint can be overcome introducing
an energy storage which gives more flexibility to the energy system.
Renewable energy can be used to produce both thermal and electrical energy.

1.2
Thermal Energy Storage
With Thermal Energy Storage (TES) is indicated a technology that allows to store
thermal energy and utilize it when it is needed. Using this technology it is possible
to store thermal energy both by heating or cooling a storage medium and this energy
can be used later on for heating, cooling and power generation [7].
As explained in Chapter 1.1, the nowadays situation is presented with a picture
where on the one side there is a very strong demand for huge amount of thermal energy
and on the other side there is plenty of thermal energy available. Nevertheless, there
are gaps between the demand and the supply caused by the time difference between
thermal energy generation and consumption, by the difference of the costs of thermal
energy between peak and off-peak hours of the day and by the distance between the
source of thermal energy and the place of consumption. TES systems have one simple
purpose which is preventing the loss of thermal energy by storing excess heat until it
is consumed. In this way, TES can overcome the issues caused by the gap between
supply and demand aforementioned. [3]
1.2.1
Classification of Thermal Energy Storage
TES is by now a well known technology which has been studied and developed mas-
sively. It can be classified according to the way the material of the storage can reserve
heat. Therefore, it is possible to distinguish between the following TES [7]:
• Sensible Heat Storage
• Latent Heat Storage
• Thermo-Chemical Heat Storage
1.2.1.1
Sensible Heat Storage
Sensible Heat Storage (SHS) is the most common way to store heat and it consists of
storing thermal energy by cooling or heating either a liquid or a solid storage medium.
The heat transferred to the storage medium leads to the increase of its temperature,
thus the storage is based on the temperature difference in the material. This means
that SHS offers a capacity that is limited by the specific heat of the storage material.
[15] [7]
==PAGE:20==
4
1 Introduction
The main materials used in SHS are: water, thermal oils, molten salts, liquid
metals, earth materials and concrete blocks. SHSs are thermally stable at high tem-
peratures and they are usually low cost materials with the exception of liquid metals
and thermal oils. The main drawback of SHS materials is the temperature stability
during the discharge process in fact, as the thermal discharge continues, the outlet
temperature of the Heat Transfer Fluid (HTF) gradually starts decreasing with the
time. Compared to Latent Heat Storage (which will be presented in 1.2.1.2), specific
heat of materials is 50-100 times smaller and therefore the TES density is smaller. [3]
Among the SHS media mentioned, water has the highest specific heat, which
makes it the most favorable material for heat storage.
Moreover, water has the
particular behaviour of stratifying naturally because its density increases at lower
temperature. Therefore, the hot water flows to the top while the cold water remains
at the bottom, and the intermediate region is the thermocline. [7]
As previously explained, SHS systems are based on the temperature difference in
the material and, therefore, it is fundamental to reduce the heat losses as much as
possible. In order to minimize the thermal losses during storing, the heat transfer
medium is usually kept in storage tanks with high thermal insulation. SHS requires
large quantities and volumes of materials, as well as proper design due to low energy
density and the possibility to discharge thermal energy at constant temperatures. The
storage capacity and the efficiency are quite variable as they depend on the specific
heat of the storage medium and on the thermal insulation technologies that could be
quite varying. [7]
To summarize, SHS presents the following advantages and disadvantages.
Advantages:
• Simple application with available materials
• Long lifetime
• Cost-effective technology
• Capacity of storing heat for long time
Disadvantages:
• Large volume needed
• Geological requirements
• Heat losses to the ambient [7]

1.2.1.2
Latent Heat Storage
Latent Heat Storage (LHS) consists of storing thermal energy in a particular material
called Phase Change Material (PCM). This name comes from the fact that the storing
of energy occurs during the change of phase. LHS systems involve the following types
of phase-change: solid-liquid, solid-solid, gas-solid and gas-liquid. In order to explain
how LHS works, it will be described the solid-liquid case. Notice that the other cases
are analogous. [7]
In the solid-liquid LHS the chemical bonds in the PCM will start to break up
when the temperature increases above a certain point. Also at that point, the mate-
rial changes from solid to liquid because the material, in an endothermic reaction, will
absorb the heat. This point is called Phase Change Temperature (PCT). Similarly,
as the temperature decreases, the material will return to a solid state since the PCM
desorbs heat in an exothermic reaction. By controlling the temperature within a spe-
cific rate it is possible to store the energy used to alter the phase of the material. It
is possible to use LHS also to store cold simply reversing the process just explained. [7]
The storage capacity of a PCM is equal to the phase change enthalpy at the PCT
plus the sensible heat/cold stored over/under the whole temperature range of the
storage. Therefore the storage capacity is greater for this technology than for SHS.
This is due to the additional storage capacity associated with the latent heat/cold of
the PCT. [7]
Latent heat is 50-100 times larger than sensible heat. This implies that the energy
storage density of LHS materials near the PCT is very high. Moreover, using PCM
results in a compact TES system and the outlet of the HTF is steady during discharge.
However the main drawbacks of LHS materials are their poor thermal conductivity
and their corrosive nature. [3]
To summarize, LHS presents the following advantages and disadvantages.
Advantages:
• Small volumes
• High storage density
Disadvantages:
• Low thermal conductivity
• Corrosive nature of material [7]

1.2.1.3
Thermo-Chemical Heat Storage
Thermo-Chemical Heat Storage (TCHS) is based on storing thermal energy using
chemical reactions and its basic principle consists of two or more components com-
bined in a chemical compound. The compound is broken by adding heat and the
divided components are stored separately. When the storage is needed, the compo-
nents are reunited into a chemical compound and heat is released. The released heat
from the reaction constitutes the storage capacity. [7]
Thermo-Chemical reactions can be used not only to accumulate and successively
discharge cold and heat when needed, but also to regulate humidity in a variety of
applications. The efficiency of this technology is in the range of 75% to nearly 100%
and the TCHS materials have among the highest density of all storage media. One
of the main characteristics of TCHS is its ability to bridge long duration periods
between demand and supply, on the contrary of SHS and LHS which are most often
limited in time due to heat losses. [7]
To summarize, TCHS presents the following advantages and disadvantages.
Advantages:
• Long distance transport available
• High efficiency
• Highly compact energy storage
Disadvantages:
• Expensive
• Technically complex
• High capital cost [7]

research. These technologies are:
• Tank Thermal Energy Storage (TTES)
• Pit Thermal Energy Storage (PTES)
• Borehole Thermal Energy Storage (BTES)
• Aquifer Thermal Energy Storage (ATES)

Tank Thermal Energy Storage
Tank Thermal Energy Storage (TTES, shown in Fig. 1.3) is a reinforced concrete
tank, most of the times located underground, filled with water and connected to
charging and discharging loops.
Figure 1.3: Tank Thermal Energy Storage [12].
The structure of the TTES is made of concrete, steel (usually stainless) or fibre
reinforced plastic. The majority of the tanks has a structure built utilizing concrete
because this material has the advantage to support high loads. An additional liner
(made of polymer or stainless steel) may be mounted on the internal surface of the
tank. For what concerns the outside part of the tank, the walls are insulated to avoid
heat losses. The insulation materials used are foam glass gravel for the bottom walls
of the tank and expanded glass granules in membrane sheeting for the walls on top.
The outside of the TTES is an assembly of several layers. The design of this part
is more complex because on the one hand the envelope has to guarantee protection of
the thermal insulation from moisture penetration from the inside and the outside but
on the other hand, in case the thermal insulation is already wet, desiccation has to be
enabled. There are TTESs made for temperatures even higher than 120◦C which are
filled with water, because water has an excellent heat storage capacity. Moreover, it
is a really good heat transport medium and it is easy to be integrated in the hydraulic
system because it is chemically inoffensive and easily manageable. TTES has a heat
storage capacity between 60 and 80 kWh/m3.
1.2.2.2
Pit Thermal Energy Storage
Pit Thermal Energy Storage (PTES, shown in Fig. 1.4) consists of an artificial pool
filled with storage material and closed by a lid. The walls of a pit are usually naturally
tilted and they can be heat insulated and then lined with watertight plastic foil. The
storage is filled with water and a heat insulated roof closes the pit. The roof can be
floating on the water or it can be built like a self-supporting structure as a rugged
roof. Another possibility is to fill up the storage volume with gravel and water and
store heat in this mixture. In this case, the roof of the storage can be carried easily
==PAGE:24==
8
1 Introduction
by the gravel filling. With this kind of filling, PTESs have heat storage capacities
between 30 and 50 kWh/m3, equivalent to 1.3-2 m3 of water. The maximal stored
temperature for PTES is about 80◦C and it can be 5 to 15 m deep.
Figure 1.4: Pit Thermal Energy Storage [12].
1.2.2.3
Borehole Thermal Energy Storage
Borehole Thermal Energy Storage (BTES, shown in Fig. 1.5) is a storage technique
in which the heat is directly stored in the underground. The system is composed of
U-pipes, also called ducts, that are inserted into vertical boreholes to build a huge
heat exchanger. While water is running in the U-pipes heat can be fed in or out of the
ground. During charging, the flow direction is from the center to the boundaries of the
storage to obtain high temperatures in the center and lower ones at the boundaries
of the storage. During discharging the flow is reversed. The storage is constituted by
the ground volume heated.
Figure 1.5: Borehole Thermal Energy Storage [12].
==PAGE:25==
1.2 Thermal Energy Storage
9
BTES should be implanted in grounds with high thermal capacity and imperme-
ability. Depending on the heat storage capacity, the ground can be heated up to
80◦C. The size of the BTES has to be between 3 to 5 times higher than that of a
TTES to obtain the same heat capacity, because ground can store between 15 and
30 kWh/m3.
These storage systems should have a minimal volume of 20000 m3 of ground to
be energetically and financially interesting. Also, because of the lower charging and
discharging power, often a buffer storage has to be integrated into the system.
1.2.2.4
Aquifer Thermal Energy Storage
Aquifer Thermal Energy Storage (ATES, shown in Fig. 1.6) is a system in which
aquifers, naturally occurring self-contained layers of ground water, are used for heat
storage.
Heat is fed into the storage via wells and fed out by reversing the flow
direction. During charging periods cold groundwater is extracted from the cold well,
heated up by the heat source and injected into the warm well. In discharging periods
the flow direction is reversed. Warm water is extracted from the warm well, cooled
down by the heat sink and injected into the cold well. Because of the different flow
directions, both wells are equipped with pumps, production and injection pipes.
ATES systems have a good store capacity, which is between 13 and 40 kWh/m3,
but they are not easy to realize and they need a lot of maintenance and pre-investigations.
Nevertheless, if the conditions are favorable, payback times are typically short.
Figure 1.6: Aquifer Thermal Energy Storage [12].

1.3
Actual Applications of Thermal Energy Storage
In the previous paragraphs, the main types of TES were explained. It is important
to underline that, even though there are only four types of it, TES can be applied
in many different ways combining it with several types of energy source and energy
system.
In [23] is described the simplest TES system which is a Solar Water Heating Sys-
tem (SWHS). A SWHS consists of a solar collector placed upon the roof of a household
which absorbs the heat from the sun and stores it in a TES. The heat stored is used to
warm up the utility water when it is required. Installing a SWHS instead of heating
the utility water with electricity, oil or gas may be worthwhile because it reduces
both the heating bill and the CO2 emissions. [23]
In [24] is explained that to make the green transition happens, a source of elec-
tricity must be able to deliver the required energy:
• to the necessary extent
• without destroying the climate
• without excessive public opposition
• at an affordable cost
• when it is needed
Renewables are able to fulfill all the requirements just listed except for the last one
because of the variability of the energy production typical of this type of source. This
problem can be solved using a High Temperature Thermal Energy Storage (HTTES).
A HTTES is a storage system composed of a rock bed that can be integrated to a
power cycle (as illustrated in Fig. 1.7) in order to produce electricity when needed
using the heat stored in it.
Figure 1.7: High Temperature Thermal Energy Storage [24].

This system works in two modes: charging and discharging. When the storage
system is charged, hot air is circulated through the rock bed.
Instead, when the
storage system is discharged, hot air is circulated from the rock bed through the
Heat Recovery Steam Generator (HRSG) which replaces the boiler of a conventional
Steam Power Plant (SPP). The benefit of using a HTTES in a power cycle is that,
in addition to eliminating the issue of the variability of energy production, it can
provide system support regulating the voltage and the frequency. [24]
Many TES applications include a heat pump. An example of this type of appli-

building. During summer, when the solar gain is much higher than the heat demand
of the building, heat is charged from the buffer heat store into the ATES. The ATES
operates with one doublet of wells and is located below the building. In order to
reach a high usability of the ATES a heat pump is integrated into the system. The
remaining heat load is covered by a gas condensing boiler.
Figure 1.8: Solar Heating Plant with an ATES integrated with a Heat Pump [19].
Another application that includes a heat pump is described in [20]. The article
analyzes a heat pump for domestic hot water connected with low temperature district
heating. Various system concepts for integration of micro-booster heat pumps have
been analyzed. The best configuration turned out to be a directly connected system
with the hot water tank placed on the primary side. The advantage of this system is
its ability to store relatively low temperature water without any legionella problems

1.4
Goals and Research Questions
This project aims at developing a dynamic model of a generic energy system inte-
grated with a TES. This model can be utilized, as a base model, to evaluate how
different types of energy system can be integrated with different types of TES.
The evaluation of the goodness of the system considered will be done performing
an exergoeconomic analysis which consists of calculating and analyzing the cost of
the thermodynamic inefficiencies [13]. This type of analysis allows to assess how well
the system performs and understand where to improve it increasing efficiency and
flexibility of the entire system.
The study performed in this thesis is related to the IEA Annex 30 project (Ther-
mal Energy Storage for Cost-Effective Energy Management and CO2 Mitigation).
The purpose of the Annex 30 is to evaluate technically and economically the TES
systems within industry, power plants, industrial buildings, district heating and trans-
port in order to obtain higher energy efficiency and more flexibility [1]. This thesis
will answer to an overall research question and 3 sub-questions.
The overall research question is: ”Can the model be used to analyze the perfor-
mance of a system composed of a Solar Heating Plant (SHP) and an Organic Rankine
Cycle (ORC) integrated with a Tank Thermal Energy Storage (TTES)?”
The 3 sub-questions are listed below:
• How does the TTES behave over the time in the charge and discharge configu-
ration?
• Does the model give useful information to improve the case study analyzed?
• Which improvements can be applied to the case study?

1.5
Approach
This project will be realized starting from the development of a dynamic model of a
generic energy system integrated with a TES. The development of the model will be
done following a three-step process. First, the model is drawn schematically in order
to have an idea of how it works. Second, the mathematical model of the system is
written. Third, the model is implemented in a modeling software. The model will
be developed using EES (Engineering Equation Solver) which is a general equation-
solving program that can numerically solve thousands of coupled non-linear algebraic
and differential equations [22].
When the model is created it is necessary to verify it, namely, check if it respects
the thermodynamic laws and the constraints given. Thus, it will be selected a specific
SHP integrated with a TTES to be applied to the model and the model will be run
to see if the calculations are reasonable.
After the verification of the model is executed, the exergoeconomic analysis will
be performed in order to find where the thermodynamic inefficiencies are located.
Successively, different parameters (e.g. temperature, pressure, etc.) of the system
will be modified to find the best configuration.
The purpose of this model is to evaluate different combinations of energy source,
energy system and TES. Therefore, a future work can be to analyze different energy
systems integrated with different types of TES and see which combination gives the
best results.

2.1
Introduction
As already explained in Ch. 1.4, the main goal of this project is to develop a dynamic
model. Therefore, it is fundamental to understand what is a model and what is the
difference between static model and dynamic model.
A model is the representation of a particular system and it can be seen as the
decomposition of the system itself in many subparts. In this way the system is seen
as an amalgam of many parts and it can be analyzed easily studying every single part.
On the contrary of a static, a dynamic model is a model that depends on time.
Thus, when time goes by, the model gives different results. This behaviour is typical
of the TES where the temperature increases or decreases during the time. A model
can be considered dynamic also when the data are regularly updated meaning that,
when the time goes by, different values are taken for the same parameter and so the
results obtained are different for each time step considered. The model developed
that will be described in this thesis can therefore be considered dynamic for the rea-
sons explained above.
The development of the model consists of a three-step process:
• Draw the schematic model of the system
• Write the mathematical model
• Implement the model in a software

2.3
Mathematical Model
The Mathematical Model (MM) of a system that aims to produce energy is a set of
interdependences (that can include equations, inequalities, logical conditions, etc.)
which describes the properties, the functioning and the development of this system.
This is done decomposing the global optimization task of the system into several sub-
problems solved independently and then coordinated. [27]
In this project, the MM of the system consists of writing the set of equations that
describes the model itself. As already explained in 2.1, the model considered is a
dynamic model. Therefore, the effects of the decisions become visible not only in the
present but also during the time. This is solved developing several static sub-models
constructed for the respective periods, into which the calculated length of time has
been divided.
==PAGE:37==
2.3 Mathematical Model
21
The process applied to build the MM of the system consists of dividing it into all
its components and modeling each one by itself. Then, after all the single components
are modeled properly they are merged together.
The process of modeling of every component consists of writing all the variables
of the model and dividing them into known variables and unknown variables. Once
the number of unknowns is found, it is necessary to write the equations necessary to
find them remembering that the number of equation must be equal to the number of
unknowns. The equations used are generally the mass and energy balances but they
can be integrated with equations used to calculate the efficiency.
To all these equations just mentioned, must be added also the entropy and ex-
ergy balances. These two balances are not necessary to solve the model but they are
used to calculate the entropy generated by the irreversibilities and the exergy. The
entropy generated by the irreversibilities is necessary in order to understand if the
model works properly. In fact, it must be equal or higher than zero otherwise it does
not respect the second law of thermodynamics. The exergy balance, instead, is used
to calculate the specific exergy in each point of the model which is utilized to perform
the exergoeconomic analysis. Finally, the exergoeconomic analysis itself is performed
using the cost balance which links the specific exergy to its cost.
Before analyzing each component by itself it is useful to write and explain the
balances that will be used to find the unknown variables.
The balances are the
following:
• Mass Balance
• Energy Balance
• Entropy Balance
• Exergy Balance
• Cost Balance
It is important to underline that in all the balances and formulas used it is applied
a positive sign to all the elements in input and a negative sign to all the elements in
output.
Moreover, all the balances are expressed both time dependent and time indepen-
dent. The first case is applied to the Thermal Energy Storage (because it changes
during the time) while the second is applied to all the other components.

3.3
Exergy Analysis
3.3.1
Introduction
The Exergy Analysis is the evaluation of the exergy in the system taken into consider-
ation. As Explained in [14], the exergy is the maximum possible amount of technical
work that can be done by a given amount of energy under the conditions fixed by
its surroundings (namely, the environment which is also called Dead State). In other
words, exergy is the work that theoretically can be done by changing the state of
the system to the Dead State. Exergy (E) can be divided into four components as
expressed in Eq. 3.2:
E = Ekinetic + Epotential + Ephysical + Echemical
(3.2)
As written in [4], kinetic and potential exergies are equal to 0 for a system in rest
relative to the environment. This condition can be applied to the model studied. In
fact, even though the TES is dynamic, it is analyzed for each time step and there-
fore, if it is considered each time step by itself, the model can be considered static.
The chemical exergy is the exergy component associated with the departure of the
chemical composition of a system from that of the environment. Since in this model
there are no chemical reactions nor change of phase, it can be considered equal to
0. Therefore the exergy can be seen equal to the physical exergy. From now on, the
term ”exergy” is always referred to the physical exergy.
The Exergy Analysis consists of determining the Exergetic Efficiency of each com-
ponent which is calculated by means of Eq. 3.3:
ηexergy =
˙Eprod
˙Efuel
(3.3)
Where ηexergy is the Exergetic Efficiency, ˙Eprod is the exergy produced (namely
the exergy in output) and ˙Efuel is the exergy fuel (namely the exergy in input).
Moreover, since the exergy destroyed is calculated by means of the Exergy Balance
using EES, it is possible to calculate the exergy loss using Eq. 3.4:
˙Eloss = ˙Efuel −˙Eprod −˙Edest
(3.4)
The exergy loss and the exergy destruction (or exergy destroyed) are two compo-
nents of exergy with a specific meaning. Exergy loss is the exergy lost with material
streams or by heat transfer to the ambient at temperatures above the reference state
while the exergy destruction is caused by internal entropy generation in a component
or by heat transfer to the ambient at temperatures at the reference state [14].

3.4
Economic Analysis
3.4.1
Introduction
The economic analysis consists basically on the calculation of the Net Present Value
(NPV). The NPV is the difference between the present value of cash inflows and the
present value of cash outflows over a period of time and it is used in capital budgeting
to analyze the profitability of a projected investment [9].
The NPV is calculated by means of Eq. 3.24:
NPV =
N
∑
n=1
Cn
(1 + i)n −P0
(3.24)
Where Cn is the annual cash flow, P0 is the investment cost, i is the interest rate
and N is the lifetime of the system.
The annual cash flow is obtained subtracting the annual expenses and the annual
operation and maintenance (O&M) costs to the annual income. For this model the
expenses are due only to the electricity needed for the pumps. Therefore, they are
calculated multiplying the electricity price (in DKK/kWh) for the power required by
the pumps (in kW) and their working hours. The O&M cost is considered equal to 1%
of the investment cost [11]. The annual income is given by the sale of the electricity
produced by the system and thus it is calculated multiplying the electricity price for
the power produced and the working hours. The investment cost, instead, is given
by the sum of the price of all the components and the allowances (the cost of the al-
lowances is considered equal to 15% of the sum of the price of all the components [11]).

high cost of exergy destruction which entails to improve the efficiency.

ergy Source and low for the Energy System meaning that the cost of Energy Source
has to be reduced while for the Energy System it is necessary to improve the exergy
efficiency.

the specific costs of the fuel and the product of the Pump are very small. f in the
Pump is equal to 1 because the exergy destroyed and loss are equal to 0.

the fuel and the product of the TES are approximated to 0 and this would entail a 0
in the denominator. f in the TES is equal to 0 because ˙Z is very little compared to
the sum of exergy loss and exergy destroyed of the TES.
Comparing the Charge and Discharge configurations it can be noticed that the
values of r and f for a specific component are usually analogous for the different
configurations, namely a component has a similar magnitude of r and f no matter
in which configuration is considered. r is high for the Energy Source and the TES
meaning that these components must be improved. f is high for the Pump and the
Energy Source and low for the TES and so the cost of the first two components has
to be reduced while for the TES the efficiency must be improved.

CHAPTER 4
Discussion
4.1
Introduction
This chapter discusses the model built and the results obtained analyzing the specific
case chosen. The discussion is related to the unexpected results due to the assump-
tions made and to the way the model was built. Namely, the discussion will regard
the modeling process (studied in Ch. 2) and the case study (studied in Ch. 3).
4.2
Modeling
Building a model that can be used to analyze different types of Energy Source, En-
ergy System and TES is the main goal of this project. The main challenge was to
build a model that could be applied to as many cases as possible and so it was cre-
ated a standardized model. This entailed that many aspects could not be taken into
consideration because they would have been applied only to certain specific cases.
Moreover, other aspects were neglected to simplify the calculations that, otherwise,
would have been too complicated and would have required more time.
The calculation of the power required by the pump to send back the water used
in the TES or in the Energy System (depending on the configuration) was calculated
by the model in a way that can produce an unusual result. The model was created in
order to calculate the power of the pump necessary to compensate the rest of the sys-
tem. This means that the model calculates the work that the pump needs to support
the rest of the system according to the data chosen in the specific case. This can lead
to a very high value of work for the pump and there might be no such good pumps in
the market. Nevertheless, this issue can be solved considering that this work required
can be given applying more pumps.
For what concerns what happens inside the TES, the model was built in a way
that the parameters become constant when the tank is fully charged or discharged.
Namely, the temperature inside the TES and the losses become constant when the
charging/discharging process is complete. Nevertheless, after the completion of the
process, there are still losses due to the heat exchange between the tank and the en-
vironment. In other words, when the tank is fully charged or discharged, it works as
the Standstill configuration. Anyway, this aspect does not influence the calculations
==PAGE:92==
76
4 Discussion
performed since the author wants to study what happens in the TES between time
τ = 0 and the moment of complete charge/discharge.
In the two stratified configurations, the only mass flows considered were the water
coming inside or outside the tank and the losses to the environment. Since in these
configurations the tank is divided into several layers, which can be seen as smaller
volumes, it should be taken into account also the conduction of heat between the
adjacent layers. This was made in [6] which performed a more complete analysis of
the stratified tank. This aspect was neglected in this study in order to keep the model
simpler, but it can be taken into consideration to improve the model.
The last consideration regarding the model is that it was built only for sensible
storage and thus it cannot be applied for those TES that exploit a change of phase
to store energy. To do so it is necessary to consider in the Mathematical Model also
the latent heat. This can be a possible improvement for a future work.
4.3
Case Study
For the specific case study analyzed, which consists of a SHP as Energy Source, a
TTES and an ORC as Energy System, there are some assumptions made and some
aspects that were not taken into account that can influence the results.
In the Energy Source it was considered a specific solar irradiance for a specific
hour of the day and a specific period of the year. In order to have a more complete
study it would be interesting to consider the change of solar irradiance during the
day and seasonally which will be very useful when a specific plant is analyzed. In this
study the author did not make all these considerations because the purpose of the
case study was to verify the model and thus all these aspects were judged unnecessary.
Moreover, it should be considered also that the grade of the solar panel varies depend-
ing on the geographical coordinates of the plant studied. This aspect could not be
taken into account because it was not studied a specific plant and thus its position
is unknown. Finally, it was not considered that when the surface temperature of the
solar panel increases, its efficiency decreases but the author considered that taking
an average value would have been a good approximation.
In the TES the amount of losses due to the heat exchange between the water
inside the tank and the environment is very big. This is caused by the overall heat
transmission coefficient (U) selected. In fact, it was only considered the metal wall of
the storage without taking into account its insulation. Considering also the insulation
would entail a higher value of U and therefore a smaller amount of losses.

The Energy System was analyzed as a unique component even though it is actu-
ally composed of many elements. This was done to simplify the model, since there are
many types of Energy System. A way to improve the model might be to decompose
the Energy System into its components and study them separately. This would entail
a more complete analysis, especially the Exergy and Exergoeconomic Analysis which
give unusual results because of this reason.
Regarding the two stratified configurations, it must be noticed that adding an
infinite number of layers would entail that the layers are not discrete anymore and
thus this configuration would be equivalent to the non-stratified configuration. There
is a specific number of layers, for a specific volume of the tank considered, after which
the model is not discrete anymore. The author planned to find this number but un-

layers and therefore it was decided to avoid this calculation.
The specific case study considered, as it can be seen in the results of the Bypass

CHAPTER 5
Conclusions
5.1
Introduction
This chapter illustrates the conclusions of the entire study performed in this master
thesis which consist of answering to the research questions illustrated in 1.4. After
that, the author will explain some possible future works that can be performed by
means of the model built.
5.2
Conclusions
This thesis demonstrates that the model built allows to analyze the performance of a
system composed of a SHP and an ORC integrated with a TTES. in fact, the results
obtained in Ch. 3 are reasonable and respect the laws of thermodynamics and the
constraints of the system. Moreover, the model manages to perform also the Exergy,
the Economic and the Exergoeconomic Analyses and therefore it was demonstrated
that the model works properly.
The TES was studied with particular interest performing a dynamic model of
it. This study was performed building two configurations of the model: one for the
charge process and one for the discharge process. Moreover, it was also analyzed a
stratified case for both these configurations that studies what happens in each layer
in which the tank is divided. In the charge configuration the temperature inside the
storage increases due to the hot mass flow that comes inside it. The temperature will
increase until the tank is fully charged, namely when it becomes equal to the inlet
temperature. The losses increase over time and tend to stabilize when the storage is
close to be fully charged. In the discharge configuration the temperature inside the
storage decreases due to the hot mass flow that goes outside it. The temperature
will decrease until the tank is fully discharged, namely when it becomes equal to
the ambient temperature (because the tank exchanges heat with the ambient). The
losses decrease over time until being neglected when the storage is close to be fully
discharged. Finally, in the correspondent stratified configurations the layers in which
the storage is divided are charged/discharged starting from the first one on the top
of the tank until the last one on the bottom.
==PAGE:96==
80
5 Conclusions
For what concerns the analysis of the results found, the model gives information
about how to improve the system studied especially in the Exergy and Exergoeco-
nomic Analyses.
The Exergy Analysis, and specifically the exergy efficiency, pro-
vides information regarding the components that need to be improved. For this case
study the component that needs most to be improved is the Energy Source which
has very low exergy efficiency in all the cases that include this component. Moreover,
in the discharge case there is still room for improvement of the Energy System while
the Pump results always very efficient. Finally, the exergoeconomic ratios (r and f)
calculated in the Exergoeconomic Analysis indicate that the costs of the Pump and
the Energy Source are high and must be reduced while for the TES is necessary to
improve its efficiency.
The system used in the case study can be improved substituting the inefficient
components illustrated above with more efficient components. Another way of im-
provement is changing the HTF used in the system. Water has many advantages from
the availability and security point of view, but it has the disadvantage that works (at
ambient pressure) only in a range between 273.15 K and 373.15 K. Therefore, using
a different fluid, as for example molten salt, can improve the total efficiency.
5.3
Future Works
The model developed in this project, as explained, can be applied to many different
case studies and this lead to a variety of possible future applications. In this chapter
the author will illustrate some of them but the reader could for sure find many others.
When this thesis started, the goal was to make a comparison of the different types
of TES and assess which of them performs better for a specific case study. For a matter
of time, it was not possible to develop the dynamic model of all these TES. Therefore,
a future work could be to develop a dynamic model for the ATES, the BTES and
the PTES in EES and analyze their performances when applied to the same system.
In this way, it can be evaluated which type of TES performs better for a specific plant.
The model of the storage was developed only for a sensible TES. Since there are
applications of TES which involve a phase change in it a future work could be to build
the dynamic model that include this aspect. Thus the mathematical model should
be modified adding the component related to the latent storage.
Finally, since several configurations were analyzed (Bypass, Charge and Discharge)
it would be useful to merge them together creating a unique model that considers
all these cases. In this way it would be possible also to analyze a situation in which
the processes of charging, discharging and bypass of the storage happen at the same
time.
