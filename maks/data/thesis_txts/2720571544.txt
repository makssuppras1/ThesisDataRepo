Abstract
This thesis explores the problem of improper retirement planning and its effects on
individuals.
It aims to provide a solution by presenting a tool that can lay out an
optimized retirement plan based on a person’s ﬁnancial situation. The study focuses
on Danish pension schemes and regulation. The project utilizes methods from Opera-
tions Research. Matheuristics were investigated as a interesting solution to assumed
performance problems but was omitted from the ﬁnal tool.
The pension and tax rules are outlined as part of the knowledge basis for modelling.
The model was implemented using GAMS and solved using the CPLEX solver, with
data processing and analysis done using the Julia programming language. The exact
relationship between salary and disposable income is analyzed. An overview of opti-
mal contribution rates are also found and presented. A dataset provided by a pension
platform along with synthetic cases are used for analysis. Using the ﬁnal model, a
potential large gain of six digits across the lifespan is found for most users.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In this chapter introductory statements and motivation are presented.
First some
background motivation is given to clarify the domain and illustrate the level of impor-

approach of the project is summarized.
1.1
Motivation
We live in a world that is always accelerating. Each day production increases, GDP
mostly grows and people are more and more busy. The amount of people living in
afﬂuence is growing. Through technology and medicine we seem to be conquering
the world with ever-increasing life expectancy.
In all the hustle and bustle many
people subscribe to the idea of living in the now, carpe diem. While that might be
a great way to increase happiness in the short term, it also sets up a case for less
planning. The idea of planning less can have many branches, but one of the things
that gets de-emphasised is the importance of retirement planning. That is not to say
that people do not save up, they do, but it is not planned properly. Especially the part
of "saving down". Saving up is for most common knowledge, but dissaving is such
an unusual part of most people’s relation with money management. Most people are
aware of the importance of saving up for the autumn of life, but not planning properly
might lead to either under- or over-contribution.
We all have to die. Talking about death is usually a huge taboo. This leads to people
wanting to forget that they will die at some point. This has effects on how we think
about money and wealth throughout life. If one can not accept that things change
and that it all ends at some point, then we cannot plan ahead. Being scared of the
last part of life often makes people just save up a bunch of money - to be safe. This is
the case for over-contribution. Instead, asking some existential questions about how
one wants to manage one’s wealth throughout life might reveal that they are saving
up way to much, or too little.
Saving up for a sweet retirement after a hard work life is not wrong. It is just personal
preference. It is important to ask what one values the most. Is a young and adult life
with more ﬂexibility and opportunities while the body still has great mobility impor-
tant? Or does one prefer a slightly tighter ﬁnancial situation but then more security
and a sweeter retirement? By thinking about how one wants to live throughout life,
one can start making good choices that reﬂect one’s real preferences.

1.2
This Project
This thesis explores how to plan ahead by making a complete plan of the ﬁnancial
situation of the users. The plan will of course be based on a handful of assumptions,
but it will allow us to see what the effects of situational changes can be. Every country
has their own retirement regulations, so it is important to choose a focus. As this
thesis was completed at the Technical University of Denmark it is straightforward to
choose Denmark’s pension schemes and opportunities and Danish regulation as the
point of focus.
The goal of the project is to produce a tool that can look at people’s pension plans
and spot potential for improvement. It should be able to lay out an optimized plan. An
use case for this exists at pension ﬁrms, to allow for automated systems to ﬁgure out
which customers need the most support. The pension advisor can then also use the
plan as input in his or her advisement.
In the project we will be using methods from the area of Operations Research. These
methods will be presented in Chapter 3. The Danish pension and tax system will be
documented and explained in Chapter 2. Here each element, be it a type of tax t or
deduction, will be described. The model will be built in Chapter 4. First a extremely
simpliﬁed model is introduced to showcase what the problem and solution could look
like. After this, the elements from Chapter 2 is modelled using mathematical pro-
gramming.
At the disposal of the pension platform Penly, of which the supervisor Kourosh Mar-
jani Rasmussen is the CEO, a dataset has been provided. This dataset will be explored
in Chapter 5. The dataset will be utilized together with synthetic cases in Chapter 6
where we will analyse different effects and results obtained from the model. In Chap-
ter 7 we will wrap everything up, look at the ﬁnal output of the thesis and discuss
where to go from here. In Chapter 8 all variables and input parameters are listed in
table form.
1.3
The Approach
The approach of this project was ﬁrst to look into and make sense of the pension and
tax rules. When a good understanding of the interplay between the different entities
in the system was obtained, the modelling could commence. The model would start
as small as possible, and then gradually increase with ’features’. This allowed for
step-wise validation of the outputs and calculations.
The original assumption, or guess, was that the model would become unreasonable
slow when all the features were active.
From the start it was therefore the plan
to research the concept of matheuristics as initial indications showed a good match
between this problem and matheuristics. Further explanation will be presented in
Chapter 3.
The model was implemented using GAMS (General Algebraic Modeling System) and
solved using the CPLEX solver. While most of the time was used in the GAMS en-
vironment, a signiﬁcant amount of data processing and analysis was done using the
Julia programming language. Due to a delay of a couple of months with the dataset,
the model structures was pretty much set in stone before receiving the data. The
small inescapable mismatch between the input structures and data structures had to

Chapter 2
Model Environment
In this chapter an overview of the personal ﬁnance environment, in which the model
exists in, is presented. This environment includes work income, saving choices such
as private pensions and free means, public beneﬁts and taxation of all these.
The model should reﬂect the important elements of environment that makes a differ-
ence to a person’s personal ﬁnances. While the Danish welfare system includes a wide
range of beneﬁts and the tax system offers even more complexity through deductions
and incentives, it is not the goal of the model to include everything.
The main focus is on savings through the government pension schemes, private pen-
sion and free means savings as well as tax optimizations hereof. The model will there-
fore not take into account elements like mileage allowance, public child support and
many of the other elements one should consider in their real personal ﬁnances. The
model also makes a couple of primary assumptions on the users of the model:
1. Employed and have a well-deﬁned salary every year
2. Does not make use of any special early retirement offers offered to persons
physically worn out by work or sickness
3. Has a single job with no side businesses
4. Unmarried and lives alone
In regards to the ﬁrst assumption it should also be noted at the model works in mon-
etary present values, so inﬂation and increased productivity and the derived salary
increase from these are not included. This way the guesswork is reduced and no dis-
counting is required. Model-wise it is of no issue to have a changing salary though.
This could makes sense if seniority is applicable in the job.

Denmark there exists a multitude of sources from which pension is obtained:
• Public Pension

– Pension Addition (da: Pensionstillæg)

– Elderly Support (da: Ældrecheck)
• Private Pension

– Life Annuity Pension (da: Livrente)

The different private pensions have different advantages and as we will discover in
the next section, different tax incentives. While everyone is eligible for the public
pension as a citizen, the three different types are reduced based on different criteria
depending on a person’s own wealth and savings.
2.1.1
Public Pension
Here the three types of public pension are presented.
Basis Amount
The basis amount is 78 564 kr. per year if one earns less than 348 700
kr. per year. From 348 700 kr. the basis amount is decreased by 30% of the amount
above the limit until you reach 610 500 kr.
where the basis amount is completely
gone. Earning in terms of the basis amount is deﬁned (simpliﬁed) as income from
work, proceeds from selling stock options, the last of early retirement beneﬁts and
severance pay from transitioning to retirement [1].
Pension Addition
The second and potentially largest part of the public pension is
the pension addition. This is an extra allowance of 89 664 kr. per year. This addition
is reduced quite easily if one has private pensions. If one has income above 89 700 kr.
the addition is reduced by 30.9% and is completely gone if income is above 379 900 kr..
For the pension addition, income is here deﬁned as salaried work, interest, capital
and stock gains and ﬁnally payments from private pension [2].
Elderly Support
The third part of the public pension is only payed to the poorest
(by Danish standards) of retirees. The elderly support is payed out once per year and
amounts to 19 200 kr. Retirees are eligible for the support if their liquid wealth is less
than 95 800 kr. Same limit applies to singles and couples. If eligible, the support is
reduced by 1% per 518 kr. above 36 000 kr. in income. This means that it is gone if one
has an income larger than 87 800 kr. [3]. While the elderly support amounts to more
than 1 000 kr. each month, it is not included in the model as most of the clientele of
this model will not beneﬁt from it.
2.1.2
Private Pension
The rules regarding private pensions are a bit simpler as it is generally just invested
savings. However there are some characteristics that are worth mentioning. For all
types they are similar in that it is an invested savings account and they all have a
reduced tax called "PAL-tax" of 15.3% on investment returns.
Annuity Pension
As an employee at a company or other organisation one typically
has their pension savings through the company’s or organisation’s pension partner.
The pension is taken from the salary before taxation and goes to the savings account.

Here the pension partner will invest the money in a pool with other people also signed
up with the pension partner. Upon retirement the money is payed out on activation
and registered as income and therefore taxed appropriately for that year.
For an
annuity pension the payee is entitled to all the money saved up. Should the payee die
before all the money is payed out it goes to nearest relative. The payee can choose to
divide the principal in instalments for 10 to 30 years [4].
Life Annuity Pension
The life annuity is a bit different than the annuity. Saving up
is the same, but the idea here is instead that you pay in an agreed amount each year.
Upon activation of the payouts, the payee is then entitled to a proportional amount
the rest of the payee’s life. Upon death, the payee’s relative is not entitled to anymore
money. It is very similar to - and sometimes also called - a life long insurance. The
ones who die early during retirement has then payed for the ones who lived longer.
The pension ﬁrms use life tables to determine the amounts payed out, so that is should
net out with a margin. It should be noted that the pensions ﬁrms do offer insurance
products that guaranties payouts after death to the surviving relatives [5].
Lump-sum Savings
Lump-sum savings are different from the other two in that the
money is saved up after taxation. It has the same "PAL-tax" beneﬁt on investment
returns, but the contribution amount each year is limited to 5 500 kr. if there is more
than 5 years until the public pension age. If there is less than 5 years left the limit is
54 200 kr. As the money put into the savings account was already income taxed, the
payouts are not taxed again. The money can be payed out in one lump-sum or spread
out over multiple yearly instalments. This depends on the pension ﬁrm. One beneﬁt
of this type of private pension is that there is no reduction in the public pensions [6].
ATP
ATP is a mandatory pension scheme that employees of companies are enrolled
in. As payment is regulated one does not have much control over this pension except
when to start the payout.
The size of the savings is typically much smaller than
ones private pensions. For full-time employee they pay 1 135.8 kr. yearly, while the
employer pays 2 272.2 kr. yearly totalling the yearly contribution to 3 408 kr. [7].
2.2
The Danish Tax System
When earning a salary in Denmark it is reduced by a multitude of taxes before hitting
the bank account. The level of taxation is a theme for another project, but in this
section each relevant element is presented.
2.2.1
Tax rates
Rules of taxation is subject to change every year so for this project the rules used are
from 2022 [8]. With the newly elected government of 2022 a new taxation structure
were presented but as of writing it is yet to be implemented, and so this project will
not reﬂect on the undetermined structure.
A salary is taxed primarily by ﬁve elements:
• Labour Market Contribution (AM) (da: Arbejdsmarkedsbidrag): 8% taken before
computing the other taxes [9].
• Bottom tax (da: Bundskat): 12.09%

• Church tax (da: Kirkeskat): Depends on the municipality, but ranges from 0.4%

While the different taxes constitute most of the Danish tax system, it also has many
incentives through deductions. These are decided politically and allow for the gov-
ernment to steer people in some direction regarding their personal ﬁnances. While
not included in this model, an example of this is the "worksman deduction" (da: Hånd-
værkerfradrag), that was created to increase work for carpenters, painters, electri-
cians etc.
When that business was soaring throughout the corona years, the gov-
ernment removed the deduction as demand was self-sufﬁcient. In the following the
deductions that is included in the model will be presented [8].
Personal deduction (da: Personfradrag)
While not an incentive per say, the most
known is the "personal deduction" of 46 600 kr. per year, meaning you can make that
amount each year tax free.
Employment deduction (da:
Beskæftigelsesfradrag)
As a real incentive for
keeping people employed is the employment deduction.
Every person receiving a
salary that is taxed for AM receives a deduction of 10.65% of their salary after AM,
albeit with a maximum deduction of 43 500 kr..
Job deduction (da: Jobfradrag)
Per the same rules as the employment deduction
one also receives a job deduction of 4.50% of their salary above 208 700 kr., albeit
with a maximum deduction of 2 700 kr.. Considering the whole tax calculation, this
deduction is relatively small. It is included in the model nonetheless.
Deduction for annuity pension contribution (da: Maksimumgrænse for Rate-
pension og ophørende alderspension)
This deduction is one of the more interest-
ing one and is of great importance to high-earners. For contributions to one’s annuity
pension savings there is a deduction for everything contributed up until 59 200 kr.. It
is clear an incentive is at play here in that the government want people to save up for
their own retirement.

2.3
Danish Taxation of Free Means and Capital Gains
To explain the taxation of investments in Denmark two dimensions need introduction.
First is the two ways investment returns can be taxed and second is the different tax
environments related to investments.
2.3.1
Types of taxation
There are two ways to tax the returns that invested assets yield [11]. One is to tax
the returns when the asset is sold and the return is de facto realized.
This is the
realization-based approach (da: Realisationsbeskatning). An example can be buying
into a fund for 50 000 kr. and then sell out three years later at 60 000 kr.. The proﬁt is
10 000 kr. and this is taxable by some tax rate, which is explained in the next section.
The other way to tax returns is on ﬁxed day basis. With the unrealized value-based
taxation (da: Lagerbeskatning) all positions are marked on the last day of the year.
The unrealized proﬁts are taxable, while the unrealized losses carry a deduction. If
assets were sold within the year, the realized proﬁt is of course still taxed.
What type of taxation to use is dependent on the type of asset, rules relevant hereof
and the tax environment.
2.3.2
Tax environments
In simple terms there exists four tax environments:
• Income from shares (da: Aktieindkomst)
• Income from capital (da: Kapitalindkomst)
• Pension savings (da: Pensionsopsparing)
• Investment savings account (da: Aktiesparekonto)
Each different environment has different use cases and are taxed widely different.
Income from shares
Generally shares and some ETFs (Exchange Traded Funds)
and funds depending on the content are taxed using this environment. The tax is 27%
for gains up until the progressive tax limit (da: Progressionsgrænsen) of 57 200 kr..
Gains above this limit is taxed 42%. Married couples can pool their limits together.
The taxation approach; realization-based or unrealized value-based, depends on the
speciﬁc asset [8].
Income from capital
This category is typically used for some funds, bank deposit
interest, bond-based ETFs, many foreign non-whitelisted ETFs, real estate trusts and
synthetic fund products. The gains on the invested assets is taxed as if it was regular

==PAGE:19==
non-AM income, albeit with some deduction in the top tax, as explained earlier. The
gains are thus taxed using both bottom tax, municipal tax and top tax. The taxation
approach; realization-based or unrealized value-based, depends on the speciﬁc asset.
Pension savings
The returns of all investment account related to pension savings
ie. annuity, life annuity and lump-sum are all taxed using the PAL-tax of 15.3%. The
accounts are taxed using the unrealized value-based approach. Losses are transferred
to counter next years potential proﬁts, instead of resulting in a deduction.
Investment savings account
The newest environment is the investment savings
account. It is an isolated tax environment, where the account holder can invest in
stocks, funds and whitelisted ETFs. The concept is created to improve the investment
culture in Denmark, by having a beneﬁcial tax rate of 17%. The account currently has
a limit on deposit of 103 500 kr. plus the tax of the account from the previous year.
There is no limit on actual amount in the account, only how much was deposited [12].
As it is isolated from the rest of tax calculation, losses are transferred to counter the
tax of the potential proﬁts next year.
It should be noted that even though the limit is on the deposit, there is a catch. If
the assets rise in price above the limit, the person is not eligible for depositing. If
the person buys assets for 100 000 kr. and they rise to 105 000 kr., the person cannot
deposit the remaining 3 500 kr.. On the other hand if the persons buys for 103 500 kr.
and the assets fall in price to 100 000 kr., the person cannot deposit 3 500 kr. either
because the limit is already reached.

Chapter 3
Methods
In this chapter the methods and operations research theory used will be introduced.
In broad terms two methods; mathematical programming and heuristic approaches
have been considered for the ﬁnal model.
3.1
Mathematical Programming
In the industry and academic world of Operations Research (OR) one usually start
by formulating a problem using Mathematical Programming (MP). Several categories
exists in this ﬁeld such as Linear Programming (LP), Quadratic Programming (QP),
Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) and many more even nonlinear ones. What they
have in common is that a mathematical program is deﬁned using three very important
elements: Decision Variables, Objective Function and ﬁnally, the Constraints.
The
problems can vary greatly but some very popular ones are:
• Vehicle Routing: Given a set of destinations, vehicles and packages, how do you
create the optimal solution for getting packages moved while minimizing the
costs associated with the logistics.
• Worker Allocation: Given a workplace, be it a hospital or a bus company, some
shifts and jobs needs to be covered, but at the same time employees have rights
like maximum daily work, days off, overtime work and so on. A solution ﬁnds
balances in all this.
• Factory Planning: Given a factory with a number of machines, some different,
with associated startup times and costs, running costs, and production times,
how do you maximize the output of the factory while minimizing the costs.
These can all be formulated as MP problems. MP problems can be solved using gen-
eral purpose solvers like the Simplex Algorithm and with branch and bounding when
introducing integers. Now, solvers is a whole different ﬁeld which will not be dis-
cussed, but many implementations both commercial and open-source exists. How-
ever, when the size of the problem instances grow too large, or for some problems
not even especially large, the solving time increases exponentially and it becomes
infeasible to solve the problems in a usable time. In that case the OR practitioners
need other ways to solve their problems. This typically introduces approximations
of optimal solution ie. sacriﬁcing optimality for good enough solutions that solves in
reasonable times.

3.2
Matheuristics
3.2.1
Metaheuristics
The term metaheuristics comes from the term heuristics. In this ﬁeld a heuristic is
a "way", function or algorithm to obtain some desirable result. It is typically based
on experience or problem-speciﬁc knowledge. The preﬁx "meta" refers to the meta-
heuristic being a higher-level, more general guiding algorithm for obtaining the re-
sults - here being the solution to an optimization problem [14].
A large multitude of different metaheuristics have been designed and proposed over
the years.
Metaheuristics can generally be grouped into two types of algorithms:
single-solution and population-based. The single-solution usually works with a single
solution and traverses the solution space for improvements to the solution by itera-
tively changing a subset of variables in the solution. The population-based typically
works with a population or group of solutions and then uses ideas from nature to con-
struct new solutions from the population using crossover functions and mutations ie.
generating offspring from parents. While deﬁnitely not a thorough list, some popular
metaheuristics include [14]:
• Single-solution metaheuristics
– Simulated Annealing
– Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP)
– Tabu Search
– Large Neighborhood Search
• Population-based metaheuristics
– Genetic Algorithm
– Ant Colony Optimization
– Particle Swarm Optimization
While metaheuristics try to solve the same problem as mathematical programming,
they are not mathematical programs solved by a general purpose solver. Frameworks
do exist for metaheuristics in a variety of programming languages. However, a large
part of the actual algorithm is implemented as problem speciﬁc as possible in a "regu-
lar" computer program. These programs are typically written in a compiled language
like C, C++ or Julia for the best performance.
3.2.2
Mixing Metaheuristics and Mathematical Programming
So far mathematical programming and metaheuristics have been introduced. MP has
the advantage of solving for optimality, but breaks down for large problem instances.
Metaheuristics tries to ﬁnd good but not necessarily optimal solutions. When switch-
ing to a metaheuristic some of the strengths of the MP are lost, since the mathematical
model is disregarded. Many relations are easier to write as equations than through
swaps or other discrete actions the metaheuristic uses.

What if the two different approaches could be combined to ﬁnd even better and/or
faster solutions? This is where matheuristics come into play, right in the intersection
between MP and metaheuristics. There are different ways to achieve this, but a de-
cent approach is to use the MP model as a tool in the metaheuristic and build the
metaheuristic around the MP [15].
Within matheuristics the approach on how to build them can be grouped into three
groups [16]:
• Decomposition Approach: Here the metaheuristic traverses the search space by
breaking up the full problem into a number of subproblems. These can then be
solved by mathematical programming
• Improvement Heuristics: Here a heuristic is used to generate a solution and
then the MP is used to improve the solution.
• Column generation-based approaches: Here the full problem is reduced by the
use of relaxation and the reduced problems is then solved. The "column gener-
ation" refers to adding new variables (columns in standard form) to the solution
at each step by looking at the effect on the objective function (reduced cost)
[17].

paper [18] works with the problem of matching assets and liabilities, a particularly

assignments. They use a MIP through a matheuristic. More interestingly, they let the
program become a simheuristic utilizing Monte Carlo simulation to achieve a mea-
sure of reliability. The returns on the assets (in terms of actual returns and default
risk) and the requirements of the liabilities are stochastic by nature. A simheuristic
is capable of simulating and dealing with stochastic variables. The matheuristic from
the paper [18] is shown in Algorithm 1.

match all assets and liabilities. It does this by doing an inner loop, where it forces a

Monte Carlo simulation (the simheuristic bit) to estimate the probability of failing to
meet the liabilities with the matched assets. The new solution is chosen instead of
the incumbent if the risk is reduced or equal and the net present value is reduced (a
cheaper solution).

saved up until retirement. For the Annuity a number of choices has to be taken:
• The amount saved up each year
• The length of the payout ie. how many instalments. The retiree can choose from
10 to 30 years.
• When the pension starts paying out. Rules allow for starting three years before
public pension is granted. The retiree can also push it further back and wait.
This can have impacts on tax and public pension reduction.
It should also be possible to have multiple annuity pension. This introduces the set
nRP which is the identiﬁer for a given instance of an annuity pension.
Initially Annuity was modelled by using a binary variable for each instalment possi-
bility ie. yRPL, L = 10..30 and then using a matrix with corresponding rows describe
the payout strategy. The matrix was required to include the nonlinearities of the zero
edges. A row for the 10 year plan would be
RPMatrixL=10 = [0, 0, . . . , 0, 1
10, 1
9, . . . , 1
2, 1
1, 0, 0, . . . ]
The approach had a couple of issues. It did not payout the same every year when
including the gains or interest that such a pension depot has, as it does not take future
gains into account. Also, introducing a set of variables to deal with the choice of when
to start the pension increased the possible combinations drastically. Performance is
one thing, but the fact that the future was not considered to compute an annuity-based
payout was a problem. A different way of modelling was chosen instead.
Here a start and end is set with binary variables yRPStartt,nRP and yRPEndt,nRP .
While many instances (nRP ) are allowed, they are not mandatory. This means that it
is a solver choice to set a start, but subsequently must then also set an end. Based on
these two variables the variables yRPOutt,nRP is set, indicating that payout is needed
here.

4.2.2.2
Life Annuity Pension (LR)
The Life Annuity is modelled much like the Annuity but with the following changes:
• Removal of the end variable. Life Annuity is for the whole remaining life.
• For Life Annuity there is no limit for contribution (in our modelling) so that has
been removed.
• The payout has to start at the latest ten years after becoming eligible for payout
(tP ).
The speciﬁc formula for calculating Life Annuity payout is unknown and probably ﬁrm
speciﬁc. The concept is that some people live shorter than expected and theoretically
pays for the people living longer. For this model the calculation is constructed such
that the depot is emptied and payed out as an annuity until the old age. The old age
is set to the expected age. This assumes that the pool of Life Annuity contributor’s
contributions are normally distributed, which is a weak assumption.
The pension
ﬁrm also has to earn money, but that is modelled as a 0.25% premium on the return.
The consumption for the last year of the model is deﬁned as public pension plus the
Life Annuity. While the Life Annuity is emptied in that last year, it is supposed to be
interpreted such that the payout size would stay the same if the person lived longer.
This corresponds with the idea of taking money from the pool.

5.1.2
Mapping to the model
An issue arises in the data.
Each element in composedPensions correspond to an
agreement with a pension ﬁrm. Within these elements there is the list of pensioner
(en: pensions) which each correspond to a pension ie. an Annuity, Life Annuity or
Lump-sum.
The currently saved up amount is present in opsparing (en: savings)
in aftale (en: agreement), which is on the ﬁrm agreement level.
To know how
much is saved up in the individual pensions, some logic is required.
Here the list
udbetalinger (en: payouts) of each pension is used. The sum of each list of payouts
is used to estimate the fraction of the total sum of all payouts in each agreements.
The sum of payouts is not equal to the saved up amount since they are future esti-
mated payouts. It should be noted that from some ﬁrms the ﬁeld ekstraInfo (en:
extra info) is non-null and the saved up amount can be read. But due to a variety
of inconsistencies between ﬁrms, the proposed estimation above is used for all users
instead.
From the payout list the start and stop years of the payouts in the model are set.
The ﬁeld pensionType indicates the type of pension. While there were a lot more
consistency here, some parsing logic had to be written as well.
Mostly the ﬁeld
contained Ratepension (en: Annuity), LivsvarigPension (en: life-long pension) and
Aldersopsparing (en: Lump-sum pension savings) which maps perfectly to Annuity,
Life Annuity and Lump-sum. Sometimes it also had the OphoerendePension (en: ex-
piring pension) which is sometimes a word for Annuity so the mapping is made.
The type Kapitalpension (en: capital pension) is also observed. It is a type of pension
that is not offered anymore. It was originally a pension where one could contribute
money pre-tax, and then pay 40% upon payout [21]. Today it can be converted to an
Annuity or Life Annuity. Because it can be payed out as a lump-sum it maps better
to the model’s Lump-sum pension type. While the 40% tax is lost it does not really
have anything to do with the optimization. The mapping was made to move on with
the parsing.
For some cases the labels Pension or Ukendt (en:
unknown) was observed.
For
these, the mapping is estimated based on the periodeType (en: period type) and
udbetalinger (en: payout) years.

Technical note
The experiments were run using a combination of Julia scripting
and GAMS. GAMS has a Python library as part of the installation. Using the PyCall
functionality of Julia, it was possible to integrate the Julia scripting and GAMS. PyCall
is a library that makes it possible to run Python code inside the Julia runtime. This is a
very handy tool for Julia and allows for usage of the large Python package library. Julia
was chosen for an opportunity to create fast heuristics. Julia is just-in-time compiled
while Python is interpreted, which allows for a great difference in performance for
many cases. Since Python integrates heavily with C libraries for many compute heavy

6.1
Increasing Throughput of Experimentation Using High
Performance Computing
Before getting to the experiments and analyses, it is worth presenting how some of
these experiments were carried out. After ﬁltering the dataset there are 315 cases
to solve for. When just trying out a number of the cases "manually" using GAMS, the
running time varied quite a bit. Also, depending on which features were enabled the
running time would vary. It was decided that for the Standard Model the realization-
based savings would be disabled.
When the dataset was parsed by the programs developed in Chapter 5 the output was
a folder of ".jld" ﬁles. One for each user case. The ﬁles are a special Julia ﬁle format
that is derived from HDF5.
They could easily be loaded from the other programs
developed.
To deal with the 315 cases for which solving could take an unknown amount of time,
it was decided to use the High Performance Computing (HPC) services from DTU. A
script was written to take the folder of user ﬁles and partition them into jobs. 30
users per job seemed like a reasonable partition size. It is a balance between number

6.2
Obtainable Improvements for Users
This section describes the ﬁrst and simplest experiment.
The experiment will try

6.5
Simpliﬁcation of the Model
In previous sections like Section 4.2.9 and Section 6.4 references to the simpliﬁcation
of payment ﬂows were made. The problem is found through experience gained from
working with the model. When the solver tries to ﬁnd the most optimal solution with
w.r.t the objective function it only focuses on increasing the base consumption. In
reality, one usually sets up the pensions contributions only once or a few times at a
given job. This means that not only should the solutions be as optimal (w.r.t. base con-
sumption) as possible, but to be useful they should not change pension contributions
all the time. To ﬁx this the simpliﬁcation term is introduced in Section 4.2.9. In this
section the consequences and potential losses incurred will be examined.
The simpliﬁcation term works by penalizing the objective value for every change in a
pension contribution. Concretely this refers to the variables xRPint,nRP , xLRint,nLR
and xAOint,nAO. Intuition says that when not only optimizing for increased base con-
sumption, but also a plan with fewest changes then a reduction in the original goal -
base consumption - should be seen. At least the simpliﬁed solution would be upper-
bounded by the non-simpliﬁed solution’s base consumption.
This was put to the test for the same synthetic cases as seen in the previous analy-
ses. In Figure 6.9 the results are presented. Looking at the topmost plot we see a
comparison of the simpliﬁed and non-simpliﬁed. The difference is not easy to spot.
To make this more clear the difference between the two solutions for each salary is
found in the middle plot. The difference is computed as the non-simpliﬁed subtracted
by the simpliﬁed. As argued the simpliﬁed solution should never have a higher base
consumption than the non-simpliﬁed. This means that only positive differences should
be expected. Looking at the middle plot this is not the case however.
A possible explanation for this lies in the MIPGap tolerance. When the solver tries
to optimize a MIP problem, a certain tolerance is given.
This tolerance is known
as the MIPGap which is the gap between a solution and the upper-bound, typically
found using LP relaxation [26]. In this project the tolerance has been set at 0.001
ie. 0.1%. It could be set lower and still return solutions, but due to performance and
consistency concerns the number 0.001 was found by trial-and-error. When considered
the numeric size of this gap tolerance this could explain the negative differences. It
might be that the solutions found by solver in the simpliﬁed version just happened to
be better compared to the ones found with the non-simpliﬁed within this tolerance.
From the test runs it seems to demonstrate that the solutions found using simpliﬁca-
tion are not worse. This is a pleasant discovery. The next point is then to look at how
much simpler the newly found solutions are. To measure "simplicity" the measure of
changes or deviations from previous time step is used. For instance if xRPint,nRP is
different from xRPint−1,nRP then that is one deviation. Looking at Figure 6.9 again,
this time at the bottom plot the number of deviations are shown.
For the lowest
salaries the difference is negligible. With a lower salary also comes less ﬂexibility,
and the fact that there is not enough money to make use of all the pension scheme,
let alone multiple pension accounts. From 360 000 kr. and onward a tangible differ-
ence is observed. The regularization of the objective value seems to work as expected.
It reduces the number of changes with 54.3% on average (64.4% in median terms), and
in the best case 90.0%.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
In this ﬁnal and conclusive chapter a ﬁnal wrap up will be presented. We will look at
the takeaways of the research produced, notes on how the project as a whole went
and share thoughts on what could be done in the future.
7.1
Takeaways
In this thesis a fairly useful model has been developed. Given the required input like
salary and relevant ages the mixed integer programming model can create a plan
for the personal ﬁnances of the users. The model takes into account most of the im-
portant elements for computing tax correctly.
It also models the different pension
schemes and free means types available in Denmark.
Using the the Penly dataset
as benchmark a high level of gains are found to be achievable with the use of the
model. Further analysis lead to insights into the relationship of base consumption -
an important element of the model - and salary. The contribution rate is also exam-
ined. Here we ﬁnd a seemingly low rate, even extremely low for some salary levels.
The lack of stochasticity in the model is found to be to blame. Lastly, an effort was
made to simplify the solution the model produced. The simpliﬁcation term introduced
in the model is found to work great in reducing the number of changes in pension
contributions.
7.2
The Project
By experimenting with the parameters of the model various effects have been mea-
sured and discussed. As the focus of this was ﬁrst and foremost to develop a model
capable of solving the problem. The MIP implementation showed great results and
most of the project time was used to make it work, validate and improve the results.
Conducting proper experiments resulting in useful data for analysis was a priority.
The model works well and fast for most cases, even if the realization-based income
from shares and capital was disabled for the model used in the experiments.
The
original idea was to turn to a matheuristic approach when the model showed signs
of speed issues. In reality it became more interesting to examine the effects of the
elements of the model, as the realization-based income or the investment savings ac-
count could just be turned of. The results did not indicate a signiﬁcant reduction of
base consumption. The need for spending valuable time on inventing and implement-
ing a matheuristic did not seem to add more value than examining what could have
effects on the model.

7.3
Future Work
To increase the relevancy and user base of the model, implementing features for
couples would make sense.
Currently the model can only handle a single, unmar-
ried person. A handful pension and tax rules has certain overlaps when involving a
married couple. This creates potential for an even higher base consumption when
combining incomes. Another feature in the model that would be hugely useful is the
vectorization of the PensionConsumption input parameter (set to 80% in the standard
model). A staircase curve of higher granularity would enable users to tune the solu-
tion to speciﬁc periods in their life. The current split in working and retirement years
could be considered too basic.
In the future, research into the use of matheuristics for this model could be done.
This would enable a real use of the realizable income from shares, which could not be
reasonable utilized with the pure MIP implementation. To implement a matheuristic
most efﬁciently would also require a rewrite of the model from GAMS to something
like JuMP in Julia. This would skip the GAMS step and make Julia communicate di-
rectly with the solver. By using a matheuristic it could be possible to reduce the MIP
difﬁculty even further thereby rendering open-source solvers feasible. This would be
relevant if the tool were to be commercialized, as saving money on the expensive li-
cense would improve the business case. Since the model is already integrated through
Julia, it is not too far off being able to be wrapped in a web service and put online. By
creating a simple UI to set the parameters and show the results, the usefulness of the
model would increase greatly for people in Denmark.
