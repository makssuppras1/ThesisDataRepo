Abstract
Thermistor emulators are useful for functional tests of various subsystems in spacecrafts.
Achieving the right trade-off between range, resolution, and channel count is crucial to
build a thermistor emulator that is flexible and can be used across test campaigns. This
work presents a thermistor emulator with up to 16 channels, where each channel contains
4 temperature points. A channel be configured as any type of thermistor (NTC or PT)
regardless of device parameters. This is achieved by connecting thermistor resistors
externally from the main board, thus allowing each test campaign to use its own set of
resistors. Solid state relays are used, which provides optical isolation and low power
dissipation. The relays are organized into 16 strings, with each string containing 4 relays.
Strings can be serialized to create larger channels. The worst case parasitic resistance
introduced by the system is 1.979Ω, which translates to a temperature accuracy of ±1◦C.
The temperature value can be switched with a frequency up to 545.55Hz. The thermistor
emulator was specifically developed for Rovsing’s EGSE platform.
A Failure Mode and Effects Analysis was carried out, which found that no failure modes
in the implemented solution could lead to damage to the spacecraft under test.
The implemented solution acts as a prototype which can be used as a base-line design
to implement a production ready solution that will feature in Rovsings product line.

Introduction
Spacecrafts serve a wide range of purposes, from communication services and earth ob-
servation to interplanetary probes. Common between all is once they are launched, there
is no physical access to the spacecraft. Design flaws and equipment failure present a risk
of failure for the entire mission, as there is no option for carrying out repairs. Mission failure
is very costly considering the large costs involved in developing, building, launching and
operating the spacecraft. Not to mention the loss of valuable scientific data or services
the mission could have provided. Yet, it is reported that between 2000 and 2016 41.3%
of all small satellite launches resulted in partial or complete failure. The failure causes
are wide and vary from case to case. Some missions fail due to launcher failures, others
due to failures in various subsystems and finally a significant number of missions fail to
receive any signals from the spacecraft making it difficult to establish the cause of failure
[1]. While this statistic is for small satellite missions it still demonstrates the importance
of testing.
To ensure mission success, it is crucial to test and validate the spacecraft’s functions on
the ground prior to launch. For this reason, manufacturers create Assembly Integration
and Testing (AIT) plans early in the development process to ensure continuous testing.
During this process it is particularly important to do integration tests, i.e. validating that
subsystems function correctly when connected together. This is because many failures
are caused by errors at the interface between two subsystems. To achieve this, Electrical
Ground Support Equipment (EGSE) is used to emulate parts of the spacecraft that have
not yet been implemented. I.e. a solar array emulator EGSE can be used to do integration
tests even when the real solar array has not yet been installed. This allows for an iterative
testing process in which design and interface errors are caught early in the development
cycle [2]. This approach to testing is also known as Hardware In the Loop (HIL) testing,
and is widely used in other industries such as automotive and robotics.
However, such EGSE that emulate various subsystems must be sufficiently detailed to
mimic the actual behavior during flight as closely as possible. Ideally, EGSE would em-
ulate all parts of the environment in which the spacecraft will operate in. This includes
emulating effects such as temperature, which varies throughout the orbit of a spacecraft.
This thesis seeks to develop EGSE that can be used for thermistor emulation. Such
equipment facilitates a detailed emulation of various subsystems such as a solar panel,
a battery, or a science instrument. It allows spacecraft manufacturers to validate that the
spacecraft correctly reacts to various temperature conditions without having to subject the
spacecraft itself to such temperature conditions. This is especially useful to emulate fault
conditions and validating that the spacecraft can recover from such fault conditions.
An example of such fault conditions is an overheating battery, which the spacecraft must
be capable of shutting off. In fact, an overheating battery was partly the cause for the
SpriteSat earth observation satellite to fail (built by Tohoku University in Japan). The
charging system used in SpriteSat did not react to the battery reaching critical tempera-
tures, which lead to the battery failing. Ultimately, the entire mission failed [1]. This error
could have been corrected prior to launch if the SpriteSat battery charge/discharge system
had been tested using a battery emulator in conjunction with a thermistor emulator.

This thesis is done in collaboration with the company Rovsing who specializes in building
test equipment for spacecrafts. In this project, the equipment is referred to as a thermistor

emulation is the correct term, that is what will be used in this thesis.
1.1
Objectives
1. Derive the requirements and features a thermistor must fulfill to be used for
spacecraft testing and fit into Rovsing’s EGSE
Requirements are typically specified prior to development. However, that is not the case
in this thesis. Part of this thesis is to derive the requirements such that the developed
thermistor emulator may be used in Rovsing’s EGSE platform (named Power Discrete
Mainframe (PDM)).
2. Choose a design topology that can fulfill the requirements and features specified
A number of topologies must be explored, and a specific choice must be made in accor-
dance with requirements derived.
3. Carry out schematic design and layout implementation of the topology resulting
in a printed circuit board (PCB)
4. Analyze design using Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA)
A Failure Mode and Effects Analysis must be carried out, with the purpose of ensuring
that no failure modes may lead to damage to the spacecraft.
5. Get PCB manufactured and board assembled for a prototype device
6. Write embedded test code and carry out prototype test
7. Analyze test results and write list of possible improvements
1.2
Thesis Structure
Chapter 2 provides an overview of existing thermistor emulators.
Chapter 3 presents the required background knowledge to implement a thermistor emu-
lator. The chapter details various thermistor types and how they are interfaced with. In
addition, the EGSE platform in which the thermistor emulator must be integrated will be

concludes by reviewing the specified requirements.
Chapter 9 concludes the thesis.

Related Work
Equipment made specifically for the purpose of thermistor emulation is not very common
due to their specific use case. One method for implementing such a system is using a
programmable resistor module. These modules are generic test equipment, not made
specifically for spacecraft testing. They are typically implemented in PXI (PCI extension
for instrumentation) form factor (3U or 6U size). They are made from a chain of series
connected resistors that can be switched in and out to form a resistance. Typically, a
chain has 8 to 24-bit resolution. Existing programmable resistor modules often use reed
relays (e.g. see [3] and [4]). With these systems, one can trade higher resolution for
lower range and fewer channels. For this reason, they exist with a wide range of options,
a comparison of three different modules is shown in table 2.1.

A thermistor emulator for use in spacecraft testing require many parallel channels (more
than 12) and also a wide resistance range, ranging from few ohms to mega ohms. How-
ever, they do not require high resolution (see chapter 4). This makes the resistor modules
outlined in table 2.1 unsuitable. For the purpose of building a thermistor emulator, advan-
tage should be taken of the fact that a very low resolution is required.
Dedicated thermistor emulators are not widely available due to their very specific use
cases. However, the company Temis provides a dedicated thermistor emulation solution
[7]. This solution provides 24 channels, in which each channel has four temperature val-
ues. A channel can be configured as either NTC, PT2000, PT1000 or PT100 by setting
configuration jumpers on the printed circuit board. Furthermore, each channel has gal-
vanically isolated output. The solution is in 6U size. Information is not provided on the
values of the four temperature points [7].
So far, commercial solutions have been presented. Guangquan et al.(2014) present a
digital potentiometer based solution which has 32 channels. Each channel has a range
from 2kΩto 200kΩ. To interface with a digital potentiometer, a RS-485 bus is utilized.
Because digital potentiometers have low tolerance, calibration is required for this solution
[8]. While this solution provides many channels, it has a very limited resistance range,
which is not capable of emulating a NTC thermistor at very low temperatures.
Thermistor emulators are very application specific. While generic programmable resistors
have wide range and high tolerances, they have a lack of channels and are expensive.
Dedicated thermistor emulators are not widely available. They have a limited resistance
range, which means only specific thermistor types may be emulated. The goal of this
work is to implement a flexible thermistor emulator specifically for spacecraft testing that
can be integrated into Rovsings EGSE platform. By making the right trade-off between

3.1
Resistance varying temperature sensors
A device whose resistance change with temperature can be categorized as either a ther-
mistor or a Resistance Temperature Detector (RTD). The difference between a thermistor
and RTD comes down to the underlying material used to construct the device. Thermis-
tors are manufactured using composite materials, depending on the type of thermistor
this can be a metal oxide or ceramics such as barium titanate. RTDs are made from a
pure metal, typically platinum [9] [10].
3.1.1
Thermistors
Thermistors are further classified into Negative Temperature Coefficient (NTC) and Pos-
itive Temperature Coefficient (PTC). As their name suggest, a NTC exhibits decreasing
resistance with increasing temperature where a PTC exhibits increasing resistance with
increasing temperature.
NTC
NTC thermistors are made from metal oxides that are milled to a powder substance. Ex-
amples of metals used are manganese, iron, cobalt, nickel, copper, and zinc. The powder
substance is made into a solid by heating with temperatures above 1000◦C (this process
is called sintering). NTC thermistors are typically packaged in a surface mount package
or into probes. In a NTC thermistor, electrons are excited from valence band to conduc-
tion band by means of thermal energy. As temperature increases, more electron hole
pairs are created. With further increase in temperature, more electrons are excited to the
conduction band, which results in the resistance of the material decreasing. It should be
noted that a thermistor is not doped with impurities like typical semiconductors, instead
it relies on the charged carriers naturally present in the material, and thermal energy to
create electron hole pairs. For this reason, a thermistor is categorized as an intrinsic
semiconductor where typical semiconductors used for transistors are extrinsic [11] [9].
NTCs can typically measure temperatures in the range −50◦C to 150◦C, e.g. see [12].
NTC thermistors exhibit very non-linear relationship between resistance and tempera-
ture. Several models exist, the simplest of which is based on the β-equation (graphed in

3.1.3
Thermistor interface circuits
In order to use a thermistor, a circuit must be built around the thermistor, whose purpose
is to measure the thermistor resistance. By measuring the resistance and using the re-
sistance temperature characteristics of the chosen thermistor, the current temperature is
measured. One approach to measure resistance, is to inject a known current through the
thermistor and measure the resulting voltage drop across the thermistor.

Rovsing Power Discrete Mainframe
The thermistor emulator developed in this thesis must be integrated into the Rovsing
Power Discrete Mainframe (PDM). The PDM is a modular platform that can implement
a variety of functions. A backplane is used which can host “daughter cards”. The function
of the PDM is based on which daughter cards are installed. The PDM can host up to
16 daughter cards and is responsible for powering and communicating with these daugh-
ter cards. Examples of daughter cards are thermistor emulator, second level protection
units, switch matrices, loads, and others. The purpose of the PDM is to provide a flexible
platform in which custom EGSE solutions may be implemented based on the needs of
individual spacecrafts.
Mechanically, the PDM is a 3U rack with 16 slots in which daughter cards can slide in.
Electrically, the PDM contains a backplane in which the 16 daughter cards can be con-
nected to and a single controller card. The controller card contains a System on Module
(SoM) that is responsible for communication with the 16 daughter cards using UART. The
SoM contains a device with both a FPGA and microcontroller. In addition, the controller
card contains a SPI bus and I2C bus which can be used to implement housekeeping
functionality such as temperature sensing on daughter cards, EEPROMS and similar. An
ATX power supply is used for powering the PDM and provides 12V, 5V and 3.3V to each
daughter card and controller card.
Daughter cards are equipped with a Daughter Brain Card (DBC), this is a module con-
taining a STM32F4 microcontroller. UART and I2C interface is connected to the DBC,
thereby the DBC handles communication between a daughter card and controller card.
A diagram of the PDM is shown in fig. 3.5. Note that the controller card provides other
interfaces than the ones shown in fig. 3.5, such as programmable IOs (PIO), general
purpose pins for connecting to user interface elements on front panel and others. The
EEPROM can be used to store daughter card specific settings. The temperature sensors
are used to do temperature regulation within the PDM.
Communication with the PDM is via an ethernet interface on the controller card by send-
ing SCPI commands. SCPI defines standard syntax and commands used to control test
equipment [20]. Incoming SCPI are translated into UART commands by the controller
card and transmitted to the correct daughter card.

3.2.1
ASCII UART Interface
The ASCII based protocol is the simplest and sends one character (8 bits) at a time.
Thereby, commands are encoded using character arrays. To send a command first, its
name is sent followed by arguments given to the command. Command arguments are
separated by a space, a single command can have up to 10 arguments. The last byte in
a command is the carriage return.
[command] [arg0] [arg1] ... [arg9]\CR
The DBC stores incoming commands in a First In First Out (FIFO) data structure. When
there is content in the FIFO, the microcontroller will try to match the incoming command
with a pre-defined set of commands. Each command is associated with a callback function
(implemented as a function pointer). When a match is found, the associated callback
function of the command is executed. Arguments given in the command are passed onto
to callback function. It is up to the callback function to ensure that the user has entered
the correct number of arguments and that they are “legal” for a given command.
3.2.2
Binary UART Interface
A more efficient approach is to encode commands in binary rather than characters, which
allows for encoding the same information using fewer bits (up to 8 times fewer bits for
the same information). To correctly delimit two different packets, the binary protocol must
start each new packet with a “start of packet delimiter”, this is a single byte which is used
to indicate the start of a new packet. To determine when the packet ends, the length of the
packet must also be sent. Finally, each packet gets a sequence number, this is used for
error handling. The sequence number is incremented with each new packet. If a packet
is lost, it must be re-transmitted. The sequence number within a packet is used by the
receiver to identify which was the last packet it received correctly. The receiver can use

4.1
ECSS Requirements
The interface between a spacecraft and a thermistor is specified in standard ECSS-E-
ST-50-14C Discrete Interfaces. To ensure, the thermistor emulator is compatible with all
spacecrafts built according to ECSS, the thermistor emulator must comply with require-
ments outlined in the aforementioned standard. A thermistor emulator acts as a TSM
source circuit, and must therefore follow its characteristics. As mentioned in section 3.1.4,
two types of TSM circuits are specified by the standard, TSM1 and TSM2, the emulator
must be capable of behaving as either one. Based on the characteristics of TSM1 and
TSM2 source circuits (see table 3.2 and table 3.4) the thermistor must comply with the
following:
• Be capable of emulating both TSM1 and TSM2 source circuits i.e., both NTC and
PT thermistors
• Fully floating resistive output
• DC coupled
• Tolerant to fault voltages in the range −17.5V to 17.5V with a source impedance,
≥1kΩ
The ECSS standard simply requires the TSM source to be floating with respect to space-
craft chassis. For the purpose of building EGSE hardware, the output must also be float-
ing with respect to PDM ground. This will ensure the spacecraft under test is protected
against faults within the PDM.
4.2
Temperature and Resistance Range
A question arises of what the resistance range should be? The standard specifies +∞Ω
as the largest resistance for TSM1 source circuits. Infinite resistance is normally used to
signify an open-circuit, one may define a current level Imin such that any current lower
than this level is defined as open-circuit. The resistance that would cause a current ≤Imin
would then be defined as infinite resistance. However, in this scenario, the resistance
corresponds to a temperature. Another approach is therefore to define the minimum tem-
perature value the thermistor emulator must be capable of emulating (NTC thermistors
have the largest resistance at lowest temperature). To determine the resistance range,
one must therefore first ask what the temperature ranges the thermistor emulator must
be able to emulate.
The temperature experienced by a spacecraft depends on its orbit and material used for
its panels. The locations at which the most extreme temperature occur are on panels of
the spacecraft that either directly face the sun or directly face away from the sun. It is
14

==PAGE:26==
reported in [22] that solar panels experience temperatures between −80◦C to 100◦C in
low earth orbit and −180◦C to 100◦C in geostationary orbit. The majority of all other units
within the spacecraft must be kept at temperatures between −20◦C to 70◦C [23].
However, the emulator shall also be capable of emulating failure conditions, such as over-
heating instruments or short circuit failures. One failure mode that has potential for caus-

that a lithium-ion battery may reach temperature up to 600◦C.

Exploring Design Topologies
The main function of the thermistor emulator is to set a resistance given a temperature
input, one may describe this as digital-to-resistance conversion. This chapter presents
possible design topologies to understand if they fulfill requirements outlines in table 4.2
and if they are feasible solutions. This chapter only discusses high level architectural
design decision, implementation details of the chosen topology will be presented in chap-

5.1
Digital Potentiometer
Digital potentiometers perform the exact function that the thermistor emulator must do,
i.e. changes its resistance based on a digital input. Therefore, it is worth analyzing if such
a component could fulfill the requirements. Potentiometers are three terminal devices
with terminals A, B and W. The end to end resistance is connected between terminals
A and B. In traditional mechanical potentiometers, the W terminal is a physical wiper
which slides along a resistive material (typically graphite or a resistive wire). A digital po-
tentiometer is made from an array of discrete resistors that are switched in and out using
CMOS transmission gates, which act as the wiper terminal. The resistors are typically
made from trimmed polysilicon or as thin film resistors. The wiper position (e.g. switch
positions) is typically stored in a register. To change the wiper positions, a new value is
written into the wiper register over a digital interface, typically SPI or I2C. A diagram of a 4-
bit digital potentiometer is shown in fig. 5.1. Digital potentiometers made from polysilicon

transmission gate is not drawn.

5.3
Generic Switch Module
The programmable resistor has the drawback that resistor values are fixed. All space-
crafts must therefore use the same four resistance values and their corresponding tem-
perature points in their test procedure. One way to avoid this is to allow the resistors to
vary from spacecraft to spacecraft. With this approach, a spacecraft manufacturer must
first specify what type of thermistor must be emulated and its device parameters, secondly
they must define what temperature points they want. This information is then used to com-
pute the exact resistance values needed, and a corresponding resistor is then mounted on
an external PCB which is called a “switch load block”. This means that the resistors pop-
ulated on the thermistor emulator varies from spacecraft to spacecraft. Economically, this
is a more expensive solution, as extra technician time is required to populate the specific
resistors whenever a thermistor emulator must be built for a new spacecraft. However,
the advantage is that the exact temperature point desired by the spacecraft manufacturer
may be emulated by the system. Furthermore, the accuracy requirement is fulfilled by
choosing resistors with appropriate tolerances. The flexibility and modularity of the sys-
tem is thereby greatly increased.
Such a system can be seen as a generic switch module because it is simply a collection
of isolated switches with a standardized interface to connect a generic load across the
individual switches. For the same reason, this topology does not need separate resistors
for NTC and PT.
The switches are organized into “strings” of four switches, as shown in fig. 5.6. A “string”
refers to a group of four switches and its associated switch load block, as shown in fig. 5.6.
In this topology there is a switch for each temperature point, i.e. to implement four temper-

combinations of resistors were utilized to implement four temperature points, thereby only
needing two resistors for a single channel and thermistor type. While combination of re-
sistors are also allowed in this topology, four individual resistors are reserved for each
temperature point, in case a combination of two resistors do not add up to the desired re-
sistance value. This also makes it easier to implement the exact resistance requested by

based topology unsuitable for realizing a thermistor emulator.

6.1
Switch Technologies
The switches must be isolating, possible switch technologies are electromechanical re-
lays, reed relays, solid state relays and optocouplers. Common for all these switch tech-
nologies is that they provide isolation between the control signal and switch terminals.
An important parameter of these switches are their on-resistance (or contact resistance)
as they will determine the accuracy of the thermistor emulator. This is the resistance
across the switch terminals when it is in closed position. Since the thermistor emulator
has multiple switches in series (minimum of four, but could be more) the on-resistance
26
Implementation of Thermistor Emulator for use in Spacecraft Testing
==PAGE:38==
of the switches will add up and negatively impact the accuracy of the thermistor emula-
tor. To fulfill requirement on accuracy requirement #7, the on-resistance must not be too
large. There will be a total of 64 switches, therefore another important parameter of the
switches is the power required to keep the switch in the closed state. Physical dimension
is also an important parameter, as there must be space to place all 64 switches within the
dimensions of the board (see requirement #9A). Finally, cost is a significant parameter as
there are 64 switches in total, making them a major driver in the total cost of the developed
solution.
To summarize, the most important parameters of the switch are on-resistance, physi-
cal dimensions, power dissipation and cost. The switching speed is not as important as
aforementioned parameters but does impact the performance of the system. How large
loads the switches can tolerate is not an important factor in this design because the total
injected power in a thermistor resistor is guaranteed to be less than 1mW by the TSM
receiver circuit. The four different switch technologies will now be discussed in relation to
abovementioned parameters.
Electromechanical relays energize a coil, which creates magnetic flux that is used to me-
chanically move an armature, which in turn moves a contactor between on and off position.
Because they must mechanically move a contactor, they have switching times of around
5ms. The power dissipation to keep a relay in the energized state depends on the coil
resistance and the voltage applied across the coils, Prelay = V 2
coil
Rcoil . Typical coil resistances
are ≈100Ωfor relays that operate at voltages around 5V. The contact resistance of relays
depend on how the contacts are coated, typical coatings use a variety of silver alloys and
have a contact resistance of around 100mΩ. Due to being so widely used, the cost of
electromechanical relays is relatively low [34].
Reed relays are similar to electromechanical relays in that they use a magnetic flux gen-
erated from a coil to open and close the switch. The difference is in how the contactors
are moved. The reed relay does not use an armature, instead it uses a pair of ferro-
magnetic metal wires, also called reeds. At the presence of a magnetic field, the reeds
are pulled together entirely by the magnetic field to create contact. Because it does not
have to move an armature, the reed relay can provide faster switching time at around
0.5ms. Similar to the electromechanical relay, the power dissipation in a reed relay is de-
pendent on the voltage applied to the coil and the coil resistance. Because a reed relay
does not have an armature and the reeds being small, the overall size of the reed relay
is smaller than an electromechanical one. Reed relays may be slightly more expensive
than electromechanical relays [35].
Solid State Relay (SSR) use a light emitting diode (typically infrared) in combination with a
photodiode array, which drives the gate of an output MOSFET thereby it achieves optical
isolation. The output stage come in many variations, some have two MOSFETs in a
source coupled configuration to switch AC loads, other variations of the output stage are
with triacs, silicon controlled rectifier and more. For this project, a single output MOSFET
is sufficient because only DC resistive loads needs to be switched.
The switching time of a SSR is limited by be integration time of the photodiode array and
the size of the gate capacitance in the output MOSFET. Typical switching time is 0.5ms
to 2ms. When switching the SSR off, the photodiode does not need an integration time.
This leads to asymmetrical switching times between the closed and open state, with the
closed state switching time being higher. The power dissipation is mainly determined

6.2
Switch Drive Strategy
Since each string consists of four switches, a 4-bit flip-flop is used to store the position of
the switches within a string. A flip-flop with sufficient drive strength is chosen such that it
can directly drive the solid state relay from its CMOS output pin. The chosen SSR can be
turned on with a LED current as low as 0.55mA, however with such a low LED current, the
turn-on time of the switch increases significantly. A LED current of 2mA is chosen, this is
low enough that no extra buffering is required while also providing sufficiently low turn-on
time for the SSR.
A 4-bit shared data bus from the DBC is used to write data into the 4-bit flop-flops. The
flip-flop must also have the option to tri-state its output, this is important to ensure clean
transitions for channels that consist of multiple strings. To understand this, consider a
channel that consists of two strings, i.e. eight switches. Since the data bus is shared, the
switch positions must be written sequentially in the two strings. This means there will be
a time period in which the emulated temperature is incorrect and undefined. Specifically,
this happens from when the DBC has finished updating the first string until it finishes
updating the second string. This is illustrated in a timing diagram in fig. 6.3. A channel
consisting of two strings must update the channel resistance, so it must update the flip-
flops of the strings. To do this, it pulses the clock for the two strings sequentially. The
time period during the two clock pulses is where the channel resistance is in an undefined
state. This “dead time” is not a problem, however it is undesirable to have an undefined
state during transitions.
Figure 6.3: Timing diagram illustrating dead time. CP: Clock Pulse.
To overcome this, the flip-flop output can be tri-stated (turning off its corresponding switch)
and enabled again once all strings have been updated. By doing this, the emulated tem-
perature is still incorrect during the transition period, however it will be at a defined state
(the channel will have max resistance as all switches will be off). To achieve this, the
enable signal for each flip-flop must arrive at the same time, which can be realized by
placing all the enable signals in the same register bank within the microcontroller. This
Implementation of Thermistor Emulator for use in Spacecraft Testing

is illustrated in fig. 6.4, with the addition of enable signal, the channel resistance is in a
defined state during the transition period, where it will have maximum resistance.

Enable (active high).

string will have its own flip-flop, there will be a total of 16 flip-flops. Each flip-flop must have

connected to another CMOS pin is a function of the supply voltage Vdd, the IO switching
frequency fio, the internal pin capacitance Cint, external capacitance Cext and PCB trace
capacitance Cs [45].
IIO = Vdd · fIO · (Cint + Cext + Cs)

Software Implementation
The purpose of the software is to provide a user interface. A user must be able to con-
figure a thermistor emulation channel and control it through the software. Recall from
section 3.2 that communication to the PDM is done via ethernet, and that the controller
card is responsible for translating SCPI on ethernet interface into UART commands which
are sent to respective daughter cards. This project uses a different approach in which
communication is done directly through UART interface by using the ASCII based UART
command line interface. A PC is connected directly to the thermistor emulator by using
a USB to serial adapter. The ethernet interface is therefore not used. This approach is
taken because the controller card software responsible for handling ethernet and UART
interfaces is under development at the company. The commands implemented in this
project can be adapted to be based on the binary UART protocol.
The implemented software is structured into five layers, as shown in fig. 6.13.
Figure 6.13: Embedded software stack. Items marked with red are implemented for ther-
mistor emulator in this project, remaining are existing software components
The application layer implements the commands. It is built on top of a collection of existing
software components which are the Rovsing library, FreeRTOS, and hardware abstrac-
tion layer (marked with black in fig. 6.13). The internal data structure of the thermistor
emulator as well as operations on the data structure are implemented at the application
programming layer. The middleware contains the code to initialize the peripherals as well
as driver implementation. It is also at the middleware layer that GPIO pins are mapped to
their action. The lowest layer is the hardware abstraction layer, which maps peripherals
to their register addresses on the microcontroller itself.
Figure 6.14 gives an overview of the individual software components and how they inter-
act. Incoming commands on the UART interface are matched with a command look-up

hardware. A command will modify and/or retrieve information in the data structure based
on the command and inputs the user has sent. This is done via getters and setters. A

Bypass capacitors are used throughout the board to provide supply rail filtering. One cru-

this will lead to a short circuit between supply rails. The ATX power supply provides short
circuit protection, and the power supply will shut off or latch-off the supply until the fault
condition has been removed [46]. In case the short circuit protection does not activate
quickly enough, this can lead to a loss of the entire PDM.
However, the spacecraft will be protected by the isolation. Therefore, it is categorized with
severity level three. Supply rail short circuit also occurs as a failure mode in microcircuits
(such as flip-flop and decoder), these are also categorized as severity level three.

The standard defines a number of failure modes for microcircuit inputs and outputs. Ex-
amples are an input or output being stuck at logic 1, logic 0 or high impedance. In general,
failure modes in the flip-flop or decoder result in loss of controllability of the given string,
leading to incorrect temperature measurement at the receiver circuit. These failure modes
are therefore also categorized in severity level three.

Test # 2 - Practical Use Case
The second test setup matches how the system will be used in practice. This includes
setting up a thermistor channel as NTC or PT and connecting it to a TSM receiver circuit
and carry out measurements just like the spacecraft under test would do. To do this is a
measurement system is set up based on TSM1 receiver circuit but with a few modifica-
tions (see fig. 3.3).
In the test-setup, the divider resistance Rref is variable, instead of being fixed as is the
case in a normal TSM1 receiver circuit. For a fixed value of, Rref the measurement sen-
sitivity will drop as the channel resistance increases. The lower sensitivity will lead to
lower measurement accuracy. To avoid this, Rref is manually changed in between mea-
surements to match the order of magnitude of the expected channel resistance. With this
approach the measurement sensitivity is kept approximately constant and so is the mea-
surement accuracy. Rref is included in the test setup as a decade resistor box. Every time
Rref is changed, its value is measured using a four wire measurement. This is because
each resistor within the decade resistance box has a tolerance of, ±1% and accurate
knowledge of Rref is important to achieve high measurement accuracy. To conclude, this
test setup deviates from a normal TSM1 receiver circuits in two aspects:
1. Rref is changed based on knowledge of expected channel resistance
2. Whenever Rref is changed, it is measured using 4-wire resistance measurement,
The reason for doing this is to achieve as high a resistance measurement accuracy as
possible. This allows for identifying any unexpected parasitic resistances in the thermistor
emulator while simultaneously validating that the thermistor emulator interfaces correctly
with a TSM1 receiver circuit.

8.3
Test # 3 - System Speed
The third test shall measure how fast the emulator can operate, i.e., how long it takes
to switch from one resistance value to another. This will indicate the fastest tempera-
ture change that the system is able to emulate. To emulate a temperature change, the
user must send a command over UART to the DBC. The UART operates at a speed of
921600 baud
s , and the total time it takes to transmit the command over UART is denoted
tuart. Once the DBC receives the command, it must process the command and control
the GPIO accordingly. The DBC will drive the inhibit pin, this will trigger a clock pulse to the
flip-flop currently being addressed, after which it will store the new switch position value.
The amount of time from the DBC receiving a command until a flip-flop is updated is de-
noted tprocessing. Finally, the solid state relay will turn on or off based on the new positions
written into its corresponding flip-flop. This time delay is denoted tswitch. To conclude, the
total time tsys it takes to change the emulated temperature can be expressed as:
tsys = tuart + tprocessing + tswitch
(8.14)
This test shall measure the total time delay, and the individual time delay to determine
how fast the system can operate. This will be done using an oscilloscope.

The current system implements a ASCII based protocol. To increase system speed, a
binary UART protocol may be utilized instead. A binary protocol allows for a more dense
encoding of commands and argument. Consider sending the switch position 1010 using
the ASCII protocol vs. binary protocol. In the ASCII based protocol each bit is encoded
as an ASCII character which takes up 8 bits, therefore a total of 8 · 4 = 32 bits must
be transmitted. In comparison, a binary protocol would only need exactly as many bits
as required, in this case four. This means the latency from the UART transmission is
decreased, allowing for a higher system speed.
Implications of asymmetrical switching time
Consider a string which is currently in switch position S1 = 0111. A user now requests
the string to change into switch position S2 = 1011. The transition from S1 to S2 requires
two switches to change state. One switch must transition from 0 to 1 (switch-on), another
must transition from 1 to 0 (switch-off). Since the switch-off time is much shorter, the string
will assume the position Sx = 0011 in the time between a switch-off and switch-on. An
oscilloscope measurement confirms this as shown in fig. 8.13 which shows the transition
from S1 = 0111 to S2 = 1011. The “overshoot” is in fact caused by the string entering
the intermediate state Sx = 0011. It is an “overshoot” because the intermediate state has
more switches in off position, therefore a larger channel resistance leading to a larger
voltage drop across the string.
Figure 8.13: A string transitions from 0111 to 1011. The string assumes an intermediate
position 0011 due to asymmetrical switching times
The intermediate state is only present if the string contains switch transitions in both di-
rections (i.e. on to off and off to on). If all the switches change state in the same direction,
there will be no intermediate state as shown in fig. 8.14.

To generalize: Given a string that undergoes transition Ts = S1 →S2. If the transition
Ts contains switches that change from on to off and switches that change from off to on,
then the string will undergo an intermediate state Sx in which only the on to off switch
transitions has occurred. The correct transition is therefore: TS = S1 →Sx →S2. If the
transition only contains switch changes in one direction, i.e. only on to off or only off to
on, then there will be no intermediate state and the transition is: TS = S1 →S2.

Discussion
This chapter presented the results from four different tests with the goal of validating that
the developed system fulfilled requirements outlined in table 4.2. All requirements will
now be reviewed to check if they were fulfilled:
Requirement # 0: Support thermistor types: NTC and PT
This is validated in test # 2 in which both NTC and PT type thermistors channels were emu-
lated in and connected to a TSM receiver circuit. The test showed that it functions correctly
when connected to the receiver circuit. In addition, it validated that the on-resistance and
trace resistance are the only parasitic resistances present in the system.
Requirement # 1: Fully floating resistive output
A fully floating resistive output has been achieved by using solid state relays (CPC1002N).
This has not been tested directly, however it is UL certified to withstand isolation voltage
from input to output of up to 1500Vrms.
Requirement # 2: DC coupled
The topology switches a DC resistance.
Requirement # 3: Tolerant to fault voltages in the range −17.5V to 17.5V with a
source impedance, ≥1kΩ
This is validated in test # 4 in which a sequence of fault voltages were applied. It was
validated that the system was able to correctly recover from an overvoltage event.
Requirement # 4: Temperature- and resistance range
This is achieved through the chosen topology. The resistance range (and therefore tem-
perature range) can be as large as desired since resistors are populated through the

Electrical Ground Support Equipment play an important part in the development of a
spacecraft. They allow the testing process to start early in the development, ensuring
that design errors and mismatches between subsystems are identified before launch. To
achieve this, it is important that EGSE exhibits behavior that matches that of the emulated
system during flight. This work presents the development of a thermistor emulator to be
used in EGSE. Such a system allows for emulating the temperature various subsystem
experience during flight, such as a solar array, battery, and others. This enables more
detailed functional testing and therefore increases chances of a successful mission.
In this report the full development process has been presented, starting from deriving the
requirement specification to exploring design topologies, implementing a chosen topology
and finally testing the implemented system.
Various topologies were explored. It was found that off the shelf digital potentiometers do
not have sufficient tolerance and range to be used in the thermistor emulator. A better
design topology is based on a generic switch module, in which the resistors specific to
the spacecraft under test may be mounted.
The implemented thermistor emulator makes use of solid state relays to switch resistors.
They are organized in 16 strings, with each string containing 4 solid state relays. Strings
can be serialized to achieve more temperature points. The thermistor emulator was de-
veloped to be integrated into Rovsing’s Power Discrete Mainframe. The implemented
solution achieved a resistance accuracy of 1.979Ω, and a switching speed of 545.55Hz.
A Failure Mode and Effects Analysis was carried out, which found that no failure modes
in the implemented solution could lead to damage to the spacecraft under test.
Four tests were carried out, which validated that the implemented solution fulfilled all re-
quirements. The implemented solution acts as a prototype which can be used as a base-
line design to implement a production ready solution that will feature in Rovsings product
line.

Future Work
To make this design production ready, a few aspects of the solution still need to be im-
plemented. Firstly are housekeeping functionality such as temperature sensing (actual
temperature sensing around the board). The PDM makes use of temperature sensors on
each daughter card to regulate the temperature of the PDM. These temperature sensors
are accessed through SPI. The implemented solution in this work does in fact include such
SPI temperature sensors (four distributed across the PCB), however a software driver was
not written for them. A software driver for these temperature sensors must be written to
test the sensors. Similarly, the board features an EEPROM that is accessed through SPI.
However, this was also not tested. The EEPROM is used to store daughter card specific
information.
The implemented solution makes use of ASCII based UART interface. For a production
ready solution, this must be changed to a binary based UART protocol in order to com-
municate with the controller card within the PDM.
The layout of the PCB can also be made more dense. The current system still has ade-
quate space to implement more strings, which allows for more channels.
Finally, it would be useful to characterize the switching time of the solid state relays un-
der various loading conditions. The current tests did not measure the switching time for
different load resistors. Since the load resistance may change, from a few ohms to mega
ohms, it would be useful to test how that influences the switching time.
In, chapter 4 calculations for determining the necessary resistance accuracy were carried
out. This was done by calculating sensitivity of various thermistors. However, the sen-
sitivity of a PT100 was not included in this calculation. Had this been included, it would
significantly impact the range and the accuracy in the lowest resistance range. For future
revision, it should be considered if this must be included or not.

INTRODUCTION 
This document conducts a Failure Mode Effects Analysis on board level RO-124013 
Thermistor Emulator for Power Discrete Mainframe (PDM). RO-124013 consists of two 
boards, a main board and 16 switch load blocks that each contain a resistor network. 
Furthermore, the thermistor emulator features a microcontroller card that connects to the 
main board through a SoDIMM connector, this is referred to as Daughter Brain Card (DBC). 
In this document an FMEA is conducted on both the main board and 16 switch load blocks, 
but not Daughter Brain Card. The FMEA is conducted in reference to the spacecraft under 
test, therefore failure modes are evaluated to determine how they affect the spacecraft 
under test.  
The thermistor emulator fits into the PDM. Local effects are those that occur on either 
main thermistor emulator board or individual switch load blocks, equipment level effects 
are those that occur at the PDM.

PRODUCT 
The thermistor emulator fits into PDM as a daughter card that connects to the backplane of 
the PDM. The thermistor emulator receives command over Man Machine Interface (MMI) 
through a controller card that is also connected to the backplane of the PDM. The MMI 
commands are sent as USART commands by the controller card to the thermistor 
emulator. The USART interface exists between the daughter brain card on the thermistor 
emulator board and the controller card.  
 
Figure 1 System overview 
Thermistors are simulated thermistors by means of a resistor ladder 
where each resistor can be switched in and out using optically isolated 
switches. The resistor ladder and switches are organized in strings of 
four resistors and switches in series, forming a 4-bit channel. A string 
array is formed of 16 strings.  
Spacecraft under test connects to thermistor emulator through DSUB 
connector. The thermistor emulator connects to the PDM’s backplane 
through a daughter card connector.  
Furthermore, the thermistor emulator houses an EEPROM and several 
temperature sensors that are distributed across the board. These are 
connected to the controller through a SPI bus.

RULES AND ASSUMPTIONS 
The most severe failures are those that would cause major damage or even loss of people 
(i.e. rack operator) and spacecraft under test. Failures causing major damage or loss of 
entire rack or PDM are less severe. Finally, are those failures that only cause damage or 
loss for the thermistor emulator alone. This severity categorization is summarized in the 
table below.

5.1 
Scope of analysis 
The DBC is not included in the analysis, however the SoDIMM connector through which the 
DBC is connected is included in the analysis. 
The analysis is done on a flat hierarchical level i.e. the thermistor emulator is considered as 
one homogeneous system. However, to prevent the analysis from becoming too large or 
unstructured the analysis is split according to the sheets/pages in the schematic. One 
page/sheet in the schematic has its own FMEA worksheet table.  
The thermistor emulator contains 16 strings. These are 16 identical hardware blocks. FMEA 
is only written out for one such string but is applicable to all 16 strings.  
5.2 
Remark on denoting location of failure mode 
The FMEA does not consider combinations of failure modes. However, some failure modes 
are applicable to several locations/pins, e.g. the failure mode caused by a logic input being 
shorted to VDD on an IC may the same for several logic inputs. In this case the location 
field in the FMEA worksheet may state: “U1 Pins 1, 2, 3, 4”. This shall be read as “This failure 
mode occurs at U1 pin 1 or U1 pin 2 or U1 pin 3 or U1 pin 4”.

RESULTS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The analysis shows that there are no failure modes that can lead to damage to s/c, nor are 
there failure modes that can cause injury or death to rack operator, i.e. there are no failure 
modes with severity lower than three. The end effect of all failure modes with severity 3 
can be categorized in three categories.  
First are those that cause a loss of commandability of the simulated temperature. These 
are caused by failures in strings or address decoding logic.  
Secondly, are those that cause loss of communication between thermistor emulator and 
MMI. Majority of these failures are caused in the SPI CS decoding logic, however 
disconnection of daughter card connector is also a cause for this. It is worth noting that 
some failure modes in this category cause the MISO line to be driven at a fixed potential 
level. This causes erroneous data on responses from SPI devices. However, this failure 
mode impacts other daughter cards also connected to the PDM because the MISO line is 
shared across all daughter cards and can therefore cause bus contention. Hence failure 
modes causing the MISO line to be erroneous may originate from any daughter card 
connected to the PDM.  
Thirdly, are failures that cause short circuits between +3.3V supply and ground. These are 
caused by either short circuits in bypass capacitors or in case of short circuits within CMOS 
logic IC’s. Short circuit protection and over current protection circuitry within ATX PSU 
ensure that supply rails are turned off in events of short circuit and over current. This 
ensures that the PCB does not sustain permanent damage in the form of burnt traces or 
similar.  
The analysis brings forward two recommendations: 
Firstly, it revealed that the CS inputs on the temperature sensors are left floating in case 
the CS decoding logic is also floating. This is undesired as CS should be always kept at a 
defined potential level to prevent bus contention. To solve this, the analysis recommends 
placing pull up resistors on the CS inputs on all SPI devices.  
Secondly it revealed that the 3:8 decoder responsible for decoding CS signals had it enable 
inputs driven incorrectly. The decoder provides two active low enables and one active high 
enable. The decoder must only be enabled if the SPI transactions are intended for the 
current daughter card, this is checked by the comparator. Therefore, the comparator, 
which has active high output, drives the enable signal of the decoder. Currently, the output 
of the comparator is connected to the active low enable signal of the decoder, and the 
active high enable signal was connected directly to +3.3V. This would cause inverted 
enabling of the decoder. To solve this, the output of the comparator must be connected to 
the active high enable of the decoder and the active low enables must be connected to 
GND. This ensures that decoder is only enabled for SPI transactions intended for 
thermistor emulator
