Abstract
One aspect that the pharmaceutical industry requires from the companies operating
in the business and that is missing in most of the other commerces, is the Market
Access department. This section of a pharmaceutical company make it possible for
the products to arrive on the shelter of a drug store or in the inventory of a hospital.
But before that, the products undergo a thorough process that ensures rapid and
maintained access to the brand, at the right price to all appropriate patients who
can beneﬁt. Italy is one of the few countries where the role of the Market Access
in a pharmaceutical organisation is crucial more than in other European States. In
fact, in the approval process of pharmaceuticals are involved several stakeholders
both in a country and regional level. The overall process is already well structured
but what is missing is a complete overview of all the players involved in the network
that lies behind the scenes of the industry. The result of the analysis focuses on
three main points: (1) The amount of information shared by the Public institutions,
(2) the crucial role of the Media as a key stakeholder and (3) the role of Association
of patients as a pool of information that is currently not used in the national process
of approval of pharmaceutical.

The Pharmaceutical Industry
According to the European Federation of Pharmaceutical Industries and Associa-
tions (EPFIA), the pharmaceutical industry represents the key asset to scientiﬁc
and medical progress. In fact, research methods are promising new prospects on
innovative treatments widely available and accessible to patients. Furthermore, the
pharmaceutical industry is a key asset to the European economy as well (EFPIA,
2016). According to the EU Industrial R&D Investment Scoreboard in 2016, the
pharmaceutical and biotechnology R&D expenditures are 37.327 million of €, the
ﬁrst among the other industries in Europe. Following the Pharma and Biotech are
the Software & Computer Services Industry (8.189 € mln). Nevertheless, compar-
ing the global R&D growth (%) The Software & Computer Service industry has
the highest one-year R&D growth rate (12.3%) followed by the Pharma & Biotech
(9.8%). Figure 1 shows the bubble chart of the R&D expenditures in Europe (IRI-
Economics of Industrial Research and Innovation, 2016).

products are called orphan medicines since they are not developed by the phar-
maceutical ﬁrms that have little commercial interest under normal market condi-
tions in developing medicines intended for small number of patients. The European

2.2
The Market Access
The Market Access is the set of activities done to ensure a fair evaluation of the
medicines and the access to the pharmaceutical market. In fact, as the pharmaceu-
tical industry is one of the most regulated ones, the companies need an entity that
mediate the relationships among institutional actors as well as business partners and
general stakeholders involved. The main activities carried out by a market access
department employees in a company are:
• Presentation of the results of the analysis conducted on the pharmaceutical to
the regulatory institutions
• Negotiation of a fair price with the regulatory institutions according to:
– The number of eligible patients
– All the beneﬁts brought by the cure according to the diﬀerent parameters
depending on the type of pharmaceutical introduced.
– The availability of ﬁnancial resources allocated to the funding of the
medicines branch

tients, (2) External health economic consultants (3) Associations of doctors,
(4) Associations of pharmacists on a national level
• Presentation of the pharmaceutical to the regional authorities
• Cooperation with prescription centres (hospitals, local health authorities) in
order to identify the regional pharmaceuticals’ needs and demand.
To sum up, the market access is the entity that ensure the access of the pharmaceu-

Stakeholder Analysis in the Pharmaceutical In-
dustry Environment
Stakeholder Analysis is one important task that has to be performed especially from
global companies as the reputation and the image of the company represents a valu-
able asset and it is extensively inﬂuenced by the stakeholder perception. Especially
pharmaceutical companies can largely beneﬁt from good Stakeholder Management
practices. A pharmaceutical company’s stakeholder is any person or group of people
with which the company has, or wants to develop, a relationship. These parties have
an interest or "stake" in the company’s success.
The process of Stakeholder Management is composed of 3 major steps: (1) the
Identiﬁcation, (2) the Classiﬁcation and (3) the Understanding of the stakeholders.
This process leads to eﬃcient management practices that will help understanding
the attitude of the stakeholders towards new developments in the business or new
changes and ﬁnally getting their support ((Sara Grex, Søren Lybecker, 2013)). As
for a pharmaceutical company, there are several institutions or association that are
interested in the business of a pharmaceutical company and all of them can be set
in diﬀerent areas of competence of a pharmaceutical company. Figure 3 shows the
diﬀerent stakeholders (both internal and external) that aﬀect the pharmaceutical
market environment (Dogramatzis, 2001).
As shown in Figure 3, stakeholders can be categorised according to their position in
the supply chain. Firstly, the Inputting stakeholders that concern the ﬁrst part of
research and following up the approval of the pharmaceuticals. Secondly, the Me-
diators stakeholders that often represent the actual customers of a pharmaceutical
company. In fact, in most of the cases the marketing department cooperates with
doctors (prescribers) as well as pharmacists that, as a matter of fact, represent the
most important gate to access the patients. Thirdly, the consumers stakeholders that
concern mainly the patients but also the patients’ association that act as opinion
leaders. On top of that, it has to be taken into account the community of stakeholder

Network Mapping applied on Stakeholder Anal-
ysis
As explained in section 3, Stakeholder Analysis is a basic planning and management
tool that contributes to project design and policy reform by helping to identify and
analyse the stakeholders involved in a process and their interrelations.
Stakeholder Mapping is the best-ﬁt tool for a Stakeholder Analysis. Mapping the
stakeholders’ environment, provides an overview of the stakeholder landscape. It vi-
sualizes people and organizations likely to have an inﬂuence on the planned reform.
It maps out the relevant stakeholders and their interrelations. Some of the beneﬁts
that Network Mapping applied to stakeholder gives are:
• Get valuable information that can be used for strategic programme planning;
• Obtain important hints on the actors (enough information, missing informa-
tion, not considered by the programme or reform, etc.);
• To see potential cooperation partners that are disadvantaged, excluded or
marginalised and therefore need to be empowered;
• To draw basic conclusions about alliances, power imbalances and potential
conﬂicts among the various actors
Basic Stakeholder identiﬁcation and analysis gives a simple passive classiﬁcation of
the stakeholders. The stakeholder map corrects premature assumptions concerning
individual actors and the relationships between them. Seen in a diﬀerent dynamic
perspective, supposedly important actors may become less signiﬁcant or, on the
other hand, secondary stakeholder may be revealed as a key player in the network.

Process and Stakeholder Environment overview
A drug can be deﬁned as "any chemical agent that can alter process of living". Gen-
erally speaking, a doctor is interested in substances having a pharmacological eﬀect
in humans with tolerable adverse events. More speciﬁcally, a drug can aﬀect human
health and these eﬀects may not even be immediately evident, but can manifest af-
ter many years. On top of that, the Italian government is spending signiﬁcant funds
in order to provide the best free medical and pharmaceutical care. For the reasons
mentioned health care is regulated in a scientiﬁcally based manner. Figure 4 shows
the environment the pharmaceutical companies operate in. Looking at this model,
Figure 4: Pharmaceutical marketers in the centre of a strict regulatory environment
it is clear that the pharmaceutical companies operate in a strictly regulated environ-
ment characterised by National Laws, Lobbyists and pressure groups, International
directives and regulations and voluntary self-regulation (Dogramatzis, 2001).

Discovery and Development
This is the ﬁrst step of a pharmaceutical development, usually research on new
pharmaceuticals is carried on based on the needs of the market and speciﬁcally of
the patients. At this stage in the process, thousands of compounds may be R&D
potential candidates for development as a medical treatment. After early testing,
however, only a small number of compounds look promising and call for further
study (FDA, 2015).
Preclinical Studies
This phase is characterised by two types of research: (1) In Vivo and (2) in Vitro.
The ﬁrst one refers to tests conducted on a living organism.
In Vivo testing is
often employed over the in Vitro as it is better suited for observing the overall ef-
fects on a living subject. However, sometimes conclusion may be misleading, for
instance, a therapy may oﬀer short term beneﬁt but a long term harm.
In Vitro studies refers to a test performed in a controlled environment outside of a
living organism. One of the great weaknesses of the in Vitro tests concerns the fact
that is hard to replicate the precise cellular conditions of an organism.
Usually, preclinical studies are not very large. However, these studies must pro-
vide detailed information on dosing and toxicity levels which will be the base of the
decision if the drug should be tested in people (FDA, 2015).

Clinical tests
Once the Preclinical testing has been terminated, the Clinical tests phase takes place.
The therapy is therefore tested on people across 3 phases. However, researchers
design clinical trials in advance, to answer speciﬁc research questions related to
a medical product.
These trials follow a speciﬁc study plan, called a protocol,
that is developed by the researcher or manufacturer. Before a clinical trial begins,
researchers review prior information about the drug to develop research questions
and objectives. After that, the proper trials begin and are organised in 3 phases:
(1) 20 to 100 healthy volunteers take part of the tests, after phase 1 roughly 70 % of
drugs move forward to the next phase, (2) Phase 2 studies concerns several hundreds
of people aﬀected by the condition/disease and ﬁnally (3) Phase 3, studying 300 up

Market Access on a European Level
As the majority of the pharmaceutical companies is nowadays operating globally,
the commercialization of the medicines has usually a global scope. Therefore, a
great deal of the procedures for pharmaceuticals’ access is managed on an European
level. Nevertheless, there are several procedures that can be applied, depending on
the Marketing Authorisation (AIC) request and on the type of medicines considered.
The procedures further explained in the following paragraph are:
• Centralised Procedure
• Mutual Recognition Procedure (MRP)
• Decentralised Procedure
• National Procedure
6.1
Centralized Procedure
The "centralised procedure" for authorising medicinal products is laid down in Reg-
ulation (EC) No 726/2004 (European Commission, 2017a). This kind of procedure
leads to an AIC which authorises the commercialization of the pharmaceuticals on
an European level and therefore valid in all the EU countries. As the request of com-
mercialisation is a single application, the main advantage of this procedure clearly
concern time saving matters. The application is examined by the CHMP (Commit-
tee for Medicinal Products for Human Use) established by the European Medicines
Agency (EMA) which carries on technical and scientiﬁc assessments. Furthermore
the CHMP gives a favourable or unfavourable opinion on the application. When the
opinion is favourable, it shall include the draft summary of the product’s character-
istics, the package leaﬂet and the texts proposed for the various packaging materials
(Tomino, 2010). Appendix A shows the overall process graphically, listing the single
phases that are involved (European Commission, 2017a).

6.2
Mutual Recognition Procedure (MRP)
Basic arrangements for implementing the mutual recognition procedure laid down
in Directive 2001/83/EC. Since 1 January 1998, the mutual recognition procedure is
compulsory for all medicinal products to be marketed in a Member State other than
that in which they were ﬁrst authorised (Mueller, 2004). The Mutual Recognition
Procedure takes place when the a National Marketing Authorisation that is released

other members of the European Community, identiﬁed as Concerned Member States
(CMS) in order for the authorisation to be released also in their markets. This
procedure is mandatory if the same dossier presented to the national regulatory
institutions in the RMS is being evaluated in a CMS and is ﬁled by the same company
or from a subsidiary (European Commission, 2017c). Appendix B shows the overall
process.

Decentralised Procedure
The decentralised procedure was introduced by Directive 2004/27/EC (European
Commission, 2017b). Whenever the previous procedure is not feasible and the phar-

of the EU, the decentralised procedure is followed. In this case, a dossier is edited
which will be identical both for a RMS and the CMS. According to this procedure,
the applicant (pharmaceutical company) ﬁles the request in all the member states
simultaneously (Tomino, 2010). Appendix C shows the overall process.

National Procedure

the national territory. This procedure is also the ﬁrst part of a Mutual Recognition
Procedure as explained above 6.3. If the same application has been conducted in
another state by the same organisation or a subsidiary this procedure is clearly not
relevant nor feasible, it is instead recommended the Mutual Recognition Procedure.

• Missing Therapeutic eﬀect
• Risk/Beneﬁt ratio too high
• Diﬀerent quali-quantitative composition from the declared one
• Wrong information in the dossier

The Direct Distribution (Law 405/01) has mainly fund saving purposes. To get the
point of the institution of the Direct Distribution system it is necessary to take a step
back and deﬁne the normal distribution. Normally, medicines undergo a value chain
composed of the (1) producers (Pharmaceutical Companies), the (2) wholesalers

pharmaceutical is purchased by the pharmacies, the country refund the whole price

public health entities to distribute medicines directly. As a result, there is no need
neither of the wholesalers nor of the pharmacies.

medicines are distributed through these public health units which are scattered on
the territory and therefore cannot be easily reachable by every Italian dwellers. As

Authority. This last method enriches extensiveness of the distribution enabling
pharmacies to sell medicines on behalf of the Local Health Authority. At the same

macy one since the country pays a lower percentage for the distribution (roughly
10%). The previous section has presented the two kinds of funds that are managed
by the country. It has to be highlighted that the Pharmaceutical hospital expen-

The stakeholder matrix is a tool that helps the classiﬁcation of the stakeholders in

Is the parameter indicated in the x-axis and indicates the level of commitment a

the only absolute veto power is expressed formally by AIFA. However, other entities
have informal and indirect power that can inﬂuence the process on a time base.
Sometimes entities can slow down the process as they are not fully convinced about
the new technology. Conversely, other organisation may want to speed it up as they
think the new technology can fully satisfy their needs.

9.3
Network Analysis Metrics
There are diﬀerent way for assessing the centrality degree of a node in a network.
Below are listed three metrics which have been taken in consideration when analysing
the network of stakeholders.
9.3.1
Degree Centrality
Degree centrality is the simplest way of calculating the centrality of a node. It comes
from the assumption that central actors must be the most active in the sense that
they have the most ties other actors in the network or graph (Wasserman and Faust,
1994).
9.3.2
Closeness Centrality
The second metric to evaluate the centrality of a node in a network is based on
closeness or distance. The measure focuses on "how close an actor is from all the
other actors in the set of actors. The idea behind this metric is that an actor is
central if it can quickly interact with all others (Wasserman and Faust, 1994).
9.3.3
Betweenness Centrality
Betweenness Centrality is the measure of a node to be in the shortest path between
two other diﬀerent nodes. Whenever a node is in between two other nodes, this gives
him an edge as it can control the connection between these two nodes (Wasserman
and Faust, 1994).

deviation score and 3 connections ranked as "yellow", meaning that connection that
concern the patients associations are shared among the stakeholders.
The association of patients as explained above, do not have scientiﬁc knowledge
on pharmaceutical matters. However, they represent a key part of the process. In
fact, whenever the country is assessing whether to purchase a pharmaceutical or
not, various drivers are considered. Several of them takes into account not only the
life expectancy values, but also the life quality enhancement. It goes without saying
that these matters can only be fully understood by real patients who, then, has to
be widely included in the process.
