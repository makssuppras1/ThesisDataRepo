Abstract
This project aims at redesigning the root section of the OlsenWings 14.3m small scale
wind turbine blade. The root section of the blade has to be made smaller to fit the
required dimensions of a Vestas V-27 rotor. It is required to also smoothen the curve
of the edges of the blade from the root to the maximum chord section.
An FEM model for static analysis is developed to validate the available experimental
results of static test. Using this model, the deformations, stresses and failure charac-
teristics of the original blade are studied. The new root section that fits the design
requirements is created by the scaling approach after a few trials. A CAD model
of the new root section is made using SolidWorks. Numerical analysis is done us-
ing Abaqus to study the deformations, stresses and failure characteristics of the new
blade. It is seen that the new blade has higher stresses and deformations compared
to the original model, but it confirms to design specifications. Two critical regions in
the new blade were identified where numerical analysis predicts failure.
The bolt connections in the original blade are modelled in Abaqus and the stresses
on the bolt are studied. The new V-27 rotor has a new bolt connection and is also
modelled. These studies show that the new bolt connections have higher stresses than
the original model, but it is not found to cause failure in them. Preload curves are
plotted for both root connection bolts at the maximum load.

Preface
This thesis was submitted to the Department of Wind Energy, Technical University
of Denmark in partial fulfilment of the requirements for obtaining an MSc degree
in ”Engineering Design and Applied Mechanics” at Technical University of Denmark.
The work was done between 27th January and 24th August 2020, at DTU campus,
Lyngby and DTU campus, Risø.
The project was based on the design requirement of Olsen Wings A/S, on the 14.3m
wind turbine blade. This work was supervised by Philipp Ulrich Haselbach, researcher
at DTU Wind Energy. Peter Berring, senior development engineer at DTU wind
energy was co-supervisor for the project.
The Thesis is organised into four basic chapters.
The first chapter gives a basic
introduction into the topic and the design problem. The second chapter describes the
methodology followed and the tools used for this study. The third chapter presents
the important results and discussions.
The last chapter concludes the study and
presents inferences and insights into future possibilities for research in the area.

into new avenues. Wind energy is the fastest growing mode of electricity generation in

energy of wind into usable electricity using electric generators. A need to move into
wind energy is due the over-dependence on fossil fuels and the damage it causes in
the environment due to carbon emission. This is seen as a way to prevent the ter-
rible effects of carbon emission, like global warming, climate change, and extinction

A wind turbine tower consists of a steel tower with a rotor mounted with blades at the
top. The blades are a complex structures made out of composite materials. The blades

2.3.1
Introduction to FEA
FEA has gained huge importance in the past 50 years and is widely used in the in-
dustry now. FEA in simple terms is a method used to simplify a continuous physical
problem into a discrete mathematical model and to obtain the distribution of field
variable for e.g. stress in structures. It requires the user to provide the right bound-
ary conditions that most accurately defines the physical situation. FEA has helped
engineering to save huge amounts of time and resources as this computational tool
saved designers from the need to do destructive testing.

2.3.2
Overview of the method
The basic idea of Finite element method (FEM) is to replace a complicated prob-
lem with a simpler one. This of course has a problem as the results obtained are
approximate. The exact solutions of the actual practical problems are not in most
cases attained with the currently available mathematical tools. As the name suggests,
FEM considers the domain to be analysed as comprised of a series of sub-domains
called finite elements. These elements are connected using nodes. The solution at
nodes are obtained and interpolated into the whole node using interpolation functions
(called shape functions). Using the governing equation of the problem, a solution for
each element is obtained and they are assembled together using equilibrium equations
to get a full scale solution for the whole model. The more number of elements are
used, the more expensive the problem is computationally, and the more accurate the
solution is. A simple analogy of FEM may be described using a circle. A circle is a
continuous figure and can be thought of as an infinite sided symmetric polygon. See
figure 2.2. For a symmetric polygon, as the number of sides are increased to infinity,
it approximates to a circle. The perimeter of the circle equals the sum of sides of the
polygon, when the number of sides tends to infinity.
Figure 2.2: A circle may be approximated as assembly of finite number of triangles
in a polygon [[Rao04]]

2.3.4
General description of FEM
The general procedure of FEM can be outlined as a series of steps, as described by
[Rao04]. The actual physical model is first made into a geometrical model. This
causes a modelling error due to inability to capture the exact details in the model. In
many situations, the model is simplified for ease of modelling, leading to a deviation
from exact results. In the next step, the geometrical model is discretized into the Fi-
nite Element (numerical) model. This induces discretization errors, and the solution
causes numerical errors due to residuals in the solving process. This shows that the
finite element method is an approximation, and it introduces errors in each step. In
order to achieve results, it is important to keep the errors in each step as little as
possible, by abstracting and modeling the physical problem in the best way possible.
• Discretization - The structure to be studied is divided into a finite number of
sub-domains, in the best way possible to represent the physical problem.
• Interpolation function- An appropriate interpolation model is selected to find
the solution within the elements.
• Stiffness and load matrices in element - The governing equation is represented
in matrix form within the element. The stiffness matrix and the load matrix
are derived. This is done using equilibrium or variational principles.
• Assembly of global matrices - The element stiffness matrices are assembled at
the right nodes to obtain the global matrices, which can be represented in the
form shown as
[K]ϕ = P
(2.6)
where [K] is the global stiffness matrix. ϕ is the global matrix of displacements
at each node and P is the global load matrix on each node. [K] depends on the
type of element chosen, and the material properties.

• Computing stresses and strains - Using basic stress strain relations and compat-

nodes.

the size of these matrices depends on the type of element chosen for the discretization,
and the number of degrees of freedom. The type of element may be chosen based on
the structure to be discretized, and engineering judgement and intuition, for example:
a bar in tension may be analysed in 1 dimension (1D), 2D or 3D each being more
computationally expensive than the former, but using engineering judgement, it is
known that a 1D analysis is only necessary to get the closest solution.
Similarly,
adding more elements will capture the physical model better and more accurate results
are obtained but will lead to higher computational costs.

2.4.1
Introduction to composite materials
Composite materials belong to that class of engineering materials that are not homo-
geneous or isotropic. These materials have properties that can be tailored according
to the need of the application. They have important applications in aerospace indus-
try in the manufacture of lightweight structures and in the wind-energy industry in
manufacture of blades. This is due to their high strength to weight ratio. Typical
composite materials consists of two parts, the matrix and the reinforcement fibres.
The reinforcement may be made of glass or carbon fibres in most applications. The
matrix are usually made of resin, where mainly polyester and epoxy resin are used in
the wind industry. The properties maybe altered in different ways, changing compo-
sition of the matrix, the material of the fibres, the orientation of the fibres and so on.
This ability makes composite materials very versatile and easy to work with. The
analysis of composite materials are done in accordance with the composite laminate
theory.

2.4.3
Failure theories in composite materials
The purpose of failure criteria is to compare in-plane stresses on lamina level with
material properties (allowable strengths/strain values) to predict possible failure or
damage. For a typical isotropic material like steel, failure is usually guided by a sin-
gle parameter like yield strength. Hence von-Mises stress parameter was introduced.
However it is not possible for failure of composite materials to be decided by a single
parameter due to the presence of different layers each carrying different stresses in
different directions. Over the years, many failure theories have been developed based
on these criteria, and many have been proved to be good for certain classes of com-
posite material. Most experimental strength measures are based on uniaxial stress
states. Some of the most common failure theories used now are explained below. In
all mathematical notations [ZB09], 1 and 2 directions represent the longitudinal and
transverse directions respectively. The strength parameters are represented using hat

Meshing
Once the material properties are verified, the next step is to mesh the model. First
the element type is changed to continuum shell (SC8R). The study of this element
type is done in the previous section. It is to be noticed that to obtain proper results
from composite layups, the sections should have one element through the thickness.
If there are multiple elements across thickness, the individual ply results would not
make sense.
This is the other constraint to be kept in mind while meshing.
A
structured mesh technique is used for trailing and leading edges and spar caps. This
is indicated by the green colour 2.25. The region where the shear webs connects is
yellow, where there is requirement for a swept mesh.This is because a structured mesh
is not possible in a complicated geometry.
2.7.2.1
Structured vs Sweep Mesh
A structured mesh creates mesh elements based on simple predefined geometric topolo-
gies. Abaqus uses the shape of the geometry to create mesh elements of regular simple
shapes like squares and triangles. Structured mesh can be applied to regular shaped
2D regions or simple 3D geometries.

Swept mesh is used for complex geometries. This is done by Abaqus in two phases;
creating a mesh on one side of the region called the source side and then copying the
nodes of the mesh, one by one to the other side, known as target side.This can be
done along an edge, called a sweep path. It can be an edge or a spline. The swept
direction maybe chosen. Abaqus usually chooses the more complicated face as the
source side. There are two algorithms for generating a swept mesh, which are medial
axis algorithm and advancing front algorithm.
2.7.2.2
Medial Axis algorithm and Advancing front Algorithm
Medial axis algorithm uses a technique of dividing the geometry region to be meshed
into simpler regions and use structural mesh to create elements in each of the smaller
regions. It can generally create mesh faster than advancing front algorithm if the re-
gion. There is an option to improve the quality of mesh using reduce mesh transition
option.
Advancing front algorithm technique creates elements on the boundary of the struc-
ture and continues to generate quadrilateral elements as it goes symmetrically into
the element. Here the solids follow exactly the seeds into the model. Both algorithms
generate acceptable meshes, sometimes the advancing front algorithm produces ele-
ments with skew and a more uniform mesh. So for the model, a combination of both
algorithms has to be used.

2.8.3
Scaling: two approaches
Once the outer cross sections are scaled, there are two ways to proceed with the
project. As a first case, the inner cross sections maybe scaled with the same scale
factors. This will lead to the new model having reduced thickness compared to the
original model. In another approach, the the thickness may be maintained constant
and the inner cross sections are scaled down using a different scale factor. There
are many considerations to be made before choosing one approach among this. For
the first method, when the thickness decreases, it is expected that the deflections
and stresses would increase considerably, weakening the blade.
This would mean
each ply would have to be reduced in thickness, if the same material configuration
is used generated by the Blade Modelling Tool (BMT) [Ver16]. (Refer figure 2.30).
This would lead to practical problems while manufacturing and would be unrealistic.
On the other hand, keeping the thickness constant and scaling outer and inner cross
sections by different scale factors seems a more feasible modification. This ensures
that the the ply properties, layups and configurations to remain the same. The second
model is shown in figure 2.31. For this method however, the inner cross sections are
not scaled from the original cross-sections, but generated from scratch. Using the
Python and MATLAB scripts used before, the coordinates of the inner cross sections
are created again, such that the thicknesses remain constant. Both of these methods

The experimental tests and analysis of the original model has been done and the re-
sults have been documented previously. The static and dynamic testing was done by
[Xia18] et. al. The blade was subject to experimental modal analysis using accelerom-
eters mounted aligned to the WTB coordinate system. The blade was excited by a
hammer tip in flapwise direction. A static strength analysis of the original model was
also done The details of the test are mentioned in the above sections. Finite element
analysis of these two tests are done and the results compared in this report. This is
done so as to verify the vailidity of the FE model.
A modal analysis is conducted, using abaqus to obtain the first six natural frequencies
and mode shapes. This is compared with the results from experimental tests done
by [Xia18]. The blade is mounted on the stand and experimental modal analysis was
done using conventional methods like the hammer test.
Further, the static strength tests were conducted in the large scale test facility at
DTU Risø campus. The test blade was installed in a 2.5 meter stand and load was
applied at 4 locations on the blade, at 3.5m, 6.1m, 10m and 13m from the blade
root. This was modelled in Abaqus as described in the previous sections. Four load
cases were tested, for each configuration of blade. The load direction were changed
to represent each configuration, namely S2P, P2S, L2T and T2L. The letters indicate
the sides and edges of the blade. Suction, Pressure, Leading and Trailing edges. For
eg:- P2S represents loads from pressure side to suction side of the blade. The loads
for each configuration are also specified in the static test in [Xia18]. The loads are
not ramped but applied as an amplitude, with five steps of uniform increase of 20%.
The four load cases were modelled in Abaqus by rotation of the blade instance by 900.

3.1.5
Failure study along the blade length
Various failure theories for composite materials were described in the previous sec-
tions. Tsai-Hill and Tsai-Wu failure theories are used to predict the behaviour of
composite plate panels under loading and how close it is to failure. The values are be-
tween 0 and 1 and 1 indicates failure. Here, Tsai-Hill and Tsai-Wu failure parameters
are examined on the blade to get an overall idea of possible failure in the blade. In
the case of isotropic material, like Steel, it is easy to describe the behaviour of a com-
ponent using a single parameter, like von Mises stress. Typical failure is characterised
when the von Mises value goes higher than the unique yield strength of the material.
This is however not possible for composite materials, due to different ply layups of
materials, different material properties and them being non-isotropic. Failure theories
simplify the analysis of composite materials by bringing in a one-parameter estimate
of damage. Here, a set of tools are used to analyse the failure.
Firstly TSAIH and TSAIW values are plotted for the whole model to check if the
model holds without failure. The most critical regions, (the regions closest to failure)
is looked into with detail. A through thickness plot of the failure parameter is plotted
to look at the most critical material and ply in the model. A critical ply plot is also
plotted around the critical region. It is to be noted, that at the loading regions, the
field outputs do not give accurate results and maybe ignored. In a real world scenario,
blades do not undergo point loading, and the load by winds are more uniform and
distributed along the blade’s length.
Furthermore, using the path tool in Abaqus, 4 paths are created, one each on the
trailing (marked T) and leading edge (marked L), one on the pressure surface (marked
P) and another on the suction surface (marked S). This is done, because they are the
critical regions of the blade. Trailing edge damages are a major cause of blade failures
[EB16]. The Tsai-Hill and Tsai-Wu parameters are plotted along these paths. This is
done using the envelope option turned on, hence giving an overall picture of the layers.
Some observations can be made from the plots.
For each kind of loading, there
is a spike in the failure criteria values at the load locations, due to the presence of
loads there. Generally, P2S loads are considered more dangerous from the criteria
values.For P2S loads, Tsai Wu criteria predicts failure at 6.1 m, the location of the
second load. Tsai-Wu being a more conservative theory, predicts higher values than
Tsai-Hill theory. As discussed above, the field output values close to load points need
not be considered.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
The most important conclusions drawn from this project are listed below.
4.1
Conclusion
1. A valid FEM model of the Olsen Wings 14.3m wind turbine blade was developed
for static analysis. The experimental results of [Xia18] were validated with good
accuracy. Four load conditions were studied based on the pressure, suction sides
and leading, trailing edge directions.
2. The root section of the blade, up to 2.02 m from the root is designed to fit the
Vestas V-27 rotor. The required root dimensions are met.
3. The slope on the blade from the root to the maximum chord is smoothened.
The curves are studied. This leads to reduced stress concentration in the model.
Stress concentration depends on geometry and is seen to affect failure life. De-
creased stress concentration leads to better life.
4. Two critical regions identified where failure occurs by P2S loading, which are
the highest loads of all 4 load cases. The lower flange and the trailing edge are
the identified regions that fail. These are the regions under extreme loading
and may need to be redesigned.
5. Failure maybe attributed to decreased cross-section, which means less material.
The objective was to reduce the cross-section, hence the increase in stresses is
an expected result.
6. Bolt inserts are modelled with the original dimensions, the stresses on bolts are
studied. M16 8.8 bolts are used.
7. Bolt inserts are remodelled for new blade root. The V-27 rotor uses M20 8.8
bolts. The bolts now undergo higher stresses than before, but is still safe.
4.2
Scope for future work
The objective of the study was to re design the root section of the Olsen Wings 14.3m
wind turbine blade to fit a Vestas V-27 rotor. There are certain limitations for this
==PAGE:126==
106
4 Conclusion
project, for example, there was no study of life of the blade. Fatigue loads and load
history are required to estimate the life of a blade and design can be improved by
considering them. There is also scope for improvement of the design in certain areas,
with respect to the static analysis already conducted.
1. The new design of the blade was designed with the primary objective of fitting
into the new dimension requirements. So the failure criteria was considered
secondary. The blade can be improved by strengthening the regions that are
seen to be critical. Regions with a higher failure parameter (>0.7 for example)
have to redesigned with thicker walls, or by using stiffer material. Due to time
limitations, this could not be explored in this project.
2. The overall stresses in the new model are higher, this could be improved by
increasing the thickness of the whole model, this leads to creating new layups
and changing the whole design.
3. Load envelops can be developed to obtain performance in all directions for the
blade.
4. Fatigue characteristics have to be examined to get an overall idea of the per-
formance and life of this model. A range of moment loads or a moment load
history needs to be applied to the FE model to study the performance.
5. A moment load history also can be used to study the fatigue failure of bolts to
estimate the life of bolts in the new design.
