Abstract
Department of Electrical Engineering
Biomedical Engineering
Synthetic Aperture Vector Flow Imaging using a Row Column ultrasound array
by Lasse Thurmann JØRGENSEN
A method for acquiring full volumetric 3-D VFI at fast frame rates with a Row Column Ad-
dressed (RCA) transducer is presented. The method is demonstrated using a 62+62 RCA
prototype transducer, a transverse oscillation cross-correlation estimator and an interleaved
synthetic aperture imaging sequence. The volumetric images are constructed from eight de-
focused emissions and a total of 16 volumetric image pairs were used to perform the VFI.
For a pulse repetition frequency of 10 kHz the method yields new 3-D velocity estimations
at a rate of 19.5 Hz. The approach is investigated through measurements where the trans-
ducer was connected to the experimental imaging system SARUS, and in a simulation en-
vironment using Field II. Simulations of a parabolic ﬂow in a vessel beneath the RCA trans-
ducer yielded a mean standard deviation of (˜σx, ˜σy, ˜σz) = (2.6, 2.6, 0.7)%, and a mean bias
of ( ˜Bx, ˜By, ˜Bz) = −(2.6, 3.5, 0.1)% at the beam-to-ﬂow-angle of α = 90◦. Reducing the beam-
to-ﬂow-angle to α = 60◦increased the standard deviation to (˜σx, ˜σy, ˜σz) = (6.0, 6.3, 1.0)%
and the mean bias to ( ˜Bx, ˜By, ˜Bz) = −(4.2, 3.7, 6.0)%.
The simulations were performed
with a peak ﬂow velocity of 25 cm/s and a pulse repetition frequency of 10 kHz. Dur-
ing measurements the parabolic ﬂow was established using an in-house built ﬂow-rig. The
peak ﬂow velocity was here set to ∼10 cm/s and the pulse repetition frequency to 2
kHz. At α = 90◦, the measurements yielded estimates with a mean standard deviation of
(˜σx, ˜σy, ˜σz) = (6.3, 6.4, 1.3)% and a mean bias of ( ˜Bx, ˜By, ˜Bz) = (4.2, −3.9, 2.5)%. At α = 60◦,
the bias and standard deviation was respectively found to be (˜σx, ˜σy, ˜σz) = (7.2, 8.5, 1.4)%
and ( ˜Bx, ˜By, ˜Bz) = (−3.1, 2.1, −12.6)%. The results from the measurements are however
slightly ambiguous likely due to reverberations artifacts being present in the measured data
and technical issues with the RCA transducer. Nevertheless, the results altogether suggest
that full volumetric 3-D VFI can be performed on volumetric images acquired at a fast rate.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Purpose
The purpose of this project is to investigate the performance of a 3-D velocity estimator on
volumetric images acquired at high volume rates using a Row Column Addressed (RCA)
probe and Synthetic Aperture Imaging (SAI) techniques.
1.2
Background and motivation
Volumetric 3-D Vector Flow Imaging (VFI) with ultrasound necessitates 2-D transducer
probes as-well as acquisition-schemes capable of acquiring volumetric images at a high
frame rate (≥25 Hz for cardiac imaging [1]) [2]. 3-D VFI has been demonstrated in previ-
ous works, e.g. [3] [4], but the methods presented are often unable to cover the full volume
at the frame rate necessary for clinical use. In this work, a method for acquiring real-time
volumetric images, suitable for 3-D VFI, is presented. The method is performed both in a
simulation environment and experimentally, and a vector velocity estimator is applied to
the volumetric images to gauge the performance of the acquisition technique.
Velocity estimation with ultrasound systems is often the ﬁrst-choice in the assessment of
several vascular conditions [5]. Among the reasons are that the modality is non-invasive,
non-ionizing, relatively inexpensive and can perform the velocity estimations in real-time.
Most commercial available ultrasound scanners perform the velocity estimations along the
direction of the ultrasound beam, i.e., in the axial direction. What is estimated is, therefore,
the complex 3-D ﬂow’s projection onto an 1-D axis, and this is insufﬁcient to describe details
of the ﬂow that could potentially disclose the vascular conditions. Vector velocity estima-
tion techniques aim to achieve a better representation of the ﬂow. Several schemes have
been suggested for ﬁnding the vector velocity ﬁeld, but currently, the only method utilized
by commercially available scanners is the transverse oscillation (TO) method, developed by
Jensen and Munk [6] [7]. This method relies on generating oscillations transverse to the
beam direction, to increase the sensitivity to transverse motion. The method thus allows for
lateral and axial velocity estimations. The scanners that currently utilize this method can
only display the velocity in 2-D, but the TO method can be extended to 3-D by using 2-D
transducer probes. The ﬁrst 2-D transducers were made by addressing the elements in a
matrix grid [8]. Such transducers are referred to as fully addressed probes. In [3] the TO
method implemented using a Vermon 32 × 32 fully addressed probe to acquire 3-D velocity
vector estimations. Here ﬁve lines are beamformed in parallel to acquire 3-D velocity esti-
mations along a ﬂow line. One line is used to perform the axial velocity estimations, and
the remaining four lines are pairwise used to obtain the estimates of the transverse and ele-
vation velocity components. However, with the fully addressed probes, manufacturing and
processing challenges arise. The number of electrical connections and channels increases

quadratically with the side-length of the 2-D probe, and the amount of data to process be-
come computationally exhaustive. Row Column Addressed (RCA) probes overcome this by
addressing the elements in the 2-D array as row and column elements. The Row Column
addressing scheme thus reduces the number of independent channels needed for contacting
the elements from N × N to N + N, while still retaining some of the 3-D imaging capabil-
ities of the fully addressed probe, albeit with less resolution [9] [10]. The RCA array was
suggested as an alternative to the fully addressed probe in 2003 by Morten and Lockwood
[11], and the addressing scheme opens up for the possibility of 2-D probes with a large
footprint and a small element pitch.
An approach for acquiring 3-D ﬂow estimation with a 62 + 62 RCA transducer using the
TO method is presented in [4]. Again, ﬁve lines are beamformed in-order to estimate the 3-D
velocity vectors along a ﬂow line. Both works ([3] and [4]) present measurement systems
that acquire a limited amount of ﬂow lines within a speciﬁc time frame. We are chasing
improvements to volumetric coverage at a high frame rate in order to meet the demands of
clinical use.
SAI and plane wave imaging address the frame rate issue for the full volumetric cover-
age. Both methods construct volumetric images from an emission sequence that isoniﬁes the
volume from different angles. The acquisition techniques acquire volumetric images suit-
able for ﬂow estimation potentially at very high frame rates, and from these images, ﬂow
can be estimated continuously throughout the volume.
Fast plane wave acquisition schemes with a 128 + 128 RCA probe were investigated
in [12]. Here the ﬂow image quality was assessed by the contrast of the volumetric power
Doppler image. It was concluded that ﬂow imaging is feasible over a large volume, but with
high grating lobes levels. The large grating lobes may possibly be attributed to the fact that
the pitch of the transducer array used was around one wavelength, λ, of the emitted pulse.
To avoid grating lobes, it has been recommended by others that the pitch should be no more
than λ/2 when performing SAI/plane wave imagining [13] [14].
In this project, the volumetric images are acquired with an RCA transducer using a
sparse SAI scheme to achieve high frame rates. The TO approach, in combination with a
cross-correlation estimator, is used to perform, for the ﬁrst time, full volumetric 3-D VFI.
1.3
Structure of the thesis
The thesis is composed of the following chapters:
• Chapter 2 describes the fundamental concept of the RCA-probe, SAI, and the TO esti-
mator.
• Chapter 3 presents the experimental and simulation setup and the parameters chosen
for the acquisition and post-processing.
• Chapter 4 presents and interpret the results.
• Chapter 5 concludes the thesis and gives suggestions for further research.
The appendix includes additional results and the MATLAB code developed and used
for the project.

Chapter 2
Theory
2.1
Real-time volumetric imaging with Row Column probes
Real-time volumetric imaging requires 2-D transducer probes as-well as fast acquisition-
schemes.
The following sections will explore how the Row Column Addressed (RCA)
probes solves the hardware and data processing issues of the traditional 2-D probe by reduc-
ing the number of channels from N × N to N + N. It will also be explained how Synthetic
Aperture Imaging (SAI) systems can be used to acquire volumetric images at a rate, poten-
tially far exceeding the 25 Hz requirement for clinical use.
2.1.1
Row Column Addressed probes
As mentioned in the introduction, manufacturing and computational issues arise when the
probe is fully addressed. Making the connections and processing the measured information
of a probe of say 128 × 128 = 16, 384 elements could prove an insurmountable task. Also,
having 16, 384 interconnecting wires would result in a large and heavy cable, which would
be impossible to use handheld. As a reference the Vermon 32 × 32 fully addressed probe
has a cable diameter of roughly 2 cm. Assuming the proportions scales quadratically with
the side-length, a similar 128 × 128 probe would have a cable diameter of 32 cm. A larger
number of elements is, however, necessary to obtain the image quality similar to what is
achievable with the usual 1-D probes, which contains around 128 elements or more. Fraun-
hofer diffraction theory states that the imaging quality is linearly dependent on focal depth,
d, divided by the width of the aperture, w; i.e. the f-number (F#) [15]. The following equation
captures this dependency:
FWHM = λF# = λ d
w,
(2.1)
here the Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM) of the Point-Spread Function (PSF) is a mea-
sure of the spatial resolution, and the dependent variable, λ, is the wavelength of the emitted
pulse. The equation states that the resolution can be improved by either increasing the cen-
ter frequency of the emitted pulse or by increasing the width of the aperture. Increasing the
center frequency comes with the drawback of lower penetration depth since tissue atten-
uation increases with frequency [16]. Increasing the width of the 2-D matrix array would
improve the resolution and penetration depth but is for the reasons mentioned above, difﬁ-
cult to manufacture. It has therefore been necessary to develop new acquisition techniques
that utilize a reduced number of channels per side-length of the probe. One approach is to
use RCA-probes. Here the elements are connected along the rows and columns respectively
of the matrix array. The elements therefore form a larger line element. The line elements
are referred to as either row- or column-elements based on the orientation of the connection
(see. ﬁgure 2.2). This addressing scheme effectively turns the 2-D array into two orthogonal

A drawback of RCA-probes arises from the sharp cut-off at the ends of the long line el-
ements. The cut-off causes prominent edge-effects in the emitted ﬁeld, which can be seen
in the measured signal as so-called "ghost-echoes". These edge-effects have been investi-
gated by Rasmussen et al. [10] and can be reduced by applying a built-in weighting of the
responses from the elements constituting the line-element. Reducing the weighting towards
the ends of the line-elements reduces the edge of the aperture, which in turn suppresses the
edge waves. Edge-elements are usually added to the ends of the line-elements, such that
the effective surface area is maintained. This weighting of the edge-elements is referred to
as roll-off apodization (see ﬁgure 2.3).

2.1.2
Time-of-ﬂight calculations
The focused ﬁeld of the RCA-probe is obtained by emitting from the row-elements and
receiving with the column-elements, or vice versa. The transmitted ﬁeld can only be focused
in the plane of the transmitting elements. The transmitted ﬁeld is in the direction of the
receiving elements always a plane wave. The propagating wavefront has, therefore, the
geometric shape of a cylinder. By focusing the orthogonal line-elements during receive,
information of a speciﬁc point in the volume can be obtained through a modiﬁed delay-
and-sum beamformer [18]. Delay-and-sum beamformation is based on the summation of
the responses from each element at the time for which a back-scattered signal from a speciﬁc
point would reach the corresponding receiving element. This time is also referred to as the
Time-of-ﬂight (ToF). When the emission is focused, meaning that the focus point is in front of
the aperture, the ToF is the traveling time from the center of the emission to the focal point,
then to the beamformation point and back to the receiving elements. By calculating the
ToF as a function of the receiver element, the received signals can be delayed and summed
retrieve a beamformed image point.
Delay-and-sum beamformers are based on the assumption that the acoustic sources
are point sources; however, the cylindrical wavefront of the RCA-transducer, suggests the
sources are better perceived as line segments [10]. A focused emission using an RCA trans-
ducer is shown in ﬁgure 2.5. The ﬁgure shows the vectors used to calculate the ToF proﬁle.

2.1.3
Synthetic Aperture Imaging systems
SAI is an acquisition technique where volumetric images can be constructed from an emis-
sion sequence which isonﬁes the image region from different angles [19]. From each emis-
sion in the sequence, a Low-Resolution Volume (LRV) in the same ﬁeld of view (FoV) can
be acquired. A High-Resolution Volume (HRV) can then be constructed by summing all the
LRVs from the sequence.
The name "synthetic aperture" stems from the fact that each emission’s focal point can
be viewed as a synthetic sound source. The focal points constituting the emission sequence
can therefore be viewed as virtual source-points of a new imaginary/synthetic aperture. As
for real apertures, width, pitch, and weighting of this synthetic aperture must be considered
carefully. Each LRV is acquired by transmitting, from a virtual source-point, an unfocused
wave covering the image region and then beamforming the received data. Because of the
unfocused transmission, the LRV is only focused in receive [20]. Transmit focusing is es-
tablished by summing the LRVs from virtual source-point-emissions with different origins.
The result is an HRV that is dynamically focused in receive and transmit [20]. This two-way

2.2
Vector ﬂow imaging
Vector ﬂow imaging (VFI) has yet to be successfully implemented in 3-D on real-time full
volumetric ultrasound images. The bottle-neck is mainly related to the image acquisition
and to a lesser degree the velocity estimators since well-established 2-D VFI techniques, such
as the directional beamformation method [23] and the Transverse Oscillation (TO) method,
could with little to no modiﬁcations be extended to 3-D. In this project, the TO method
is used in combination with a cross-correlation velocity estimator to acquire the 3-D VFIs.
The following sections cover the theory behind this method, and discuss how the method
potentially could be used together with directional beamforming to increase the accuracy of
the velocity estimate.

The cross-correlation estimator described in this section only determines the velocity in
one dimension i.e., the beamformed line. The estimator performs best if the direction of the
beamformed line is parallel to the direction of the ﬂow. The ﬂow direction is for obvious
reasons not known a priori in vivo. The ﬂow direction can be estimated by calculating the
cross-correlation in all possible directions for each velocity estimate but this would be very
computationally intensive, especially in 3-D. Another way of determining the ﬂow direc-
tion is by estimating the velocity components in three orthogonal directions; the axial (z),
the transverse (x) and the azimuth (y) direction. Typically, only the axial velocity can be
estimated with cross-correlation estimator. This is because the axial oscillations from the
emitted pulse make the received signal sensitive to axial motion. In the other directions,
the estimator performs poorly, if the beam-to-ﬂow angle is too large. The estimator can,
however, be improved in the transverse/azimuth direction by inducing a transverse/az-
imuth oscillation. This is the objective of the TO method, which is the subject of the next
paragraph.
2.2.2

that oscillates in the axial direction to estimate the axial shift in lag and thereby the axial
velocity component. If a ﬁeld with a transverse oscillation could be generated, the transverse
velocity component could similarly be derived. The TO method is a method that synthesizes

3.3
Imaging sequence and beamformation
The HRVs were acquired by sliding 32 active transmitting elements across the aperture in
Ne = 8 steps of 1.12 mm (≈2.1 λ) while receiving with all 62 orthogonal elements. Each
emission was defocused corresponding to an F# of −1. The geometry of the emitted ﬁelds is
shown in ﬁgure 3.5.
The emissions used to generate two successive HRV pairs were acquired using an inter-
leaving sequence, which means ﬁring a sequence of repeated emissions. This allows for the
creation of two HRVs; one from the initial emissions and one from the repeated emissions.
The result is two HRVs separated in time by Tpr f = 1/ fpr f . This is because the LRVs be-
tween the HRVs are on average, separated by this time difference. Had the emission been
ﬁred in a one-by-one sequence, meaning that each emission was ﬁred once before moving
on to the next, the two HRVs would have been separated in time by NeTpr f . The maximum
detectable velocity is, therefore, less constrained by Ne when using the interleaved sequence.
The interleaving sequence is rigorously explored in [25].
A ﬁxed double peak apodization was used in the receive processing to generate the
transverse oscillation. The chosen double peak apodization consisted of two Hann win-
dows, with a width of 31 pitch, separated by a distance of 31 pitch. The velocity components
in both the transverse and azimuth directions were acquired by performing two measure-
ments where the transmitting and receiving aperture were interchanged. The axial velocity
component can be acquired from both measurements by applying the dynamic apodization
in the receive processing, with an ordinary window function (i.e., Hamming, Hann, Boxcar)
instead of the double peak apodization. Dynamic apodization means that the window is a
function of the imaging depth. The width of the window function is determined by holding
the F# and FWHM constant in (2.1).
The HRV were beamformed with a sampling spacing of (∆x, ∆y, ∆z) = λ/8. In con-
ventional ultrasound imaging, a sampling spacing of around λ/4 would ensure that the
Nyquist criterion was satisﬁed. However, because of the mixing of the RF signals in the re-
moval of the axial frequency component (2.21), the sampling frequency needs to be at least
doubled to satisfy this criterion.

velocity of the ﬂow is estimated. This is necessary because the signal from stationary tissue

on the whole, the best results are found at the lower pulse lengths of 2 to 4 cycles, and the

The fpr f was varied from 1 to 10 kHz for three different beam-to-ﬂow angles, α = (60, 75, 90)◦.
for α = (60, 75)◦the estimates starts becoming unreliable at fpr f values lower than 5 kHz.

resemblance to the true parabola and bias start to decrease. The sudden increase in bias at
α = 60◦occurs because the estimator at this point completely breaks down. The precision
of the estimator is maintained, if not improved, for the most part, when lowering the fpr f
value. This observation was found for all tested values of α. The estimator performed well
at α = 90◦for all values of fpr f . This suggests that it is the axial motion that reduces the
accuracy of the transverse velocity estimates.
Depth and rotation
Every combination of α = (60, 75, 90)◦, β = (0, 45)◦and depth = (20, 30) mm, was simu-

accuracy for the lateral estimates, ˜σxy and ˜Bxy, respectively ranged from 1.9 to 3.1% and -3.5

˜σxy. At a depth of 30 mm, this average add-on is increased by 110.9%, yielding ∆˜σxy = 4.1%.
The axial precision is only slightly reduced by the lowering of α and for all the combinations
of α, β and depth, ˜σz ≤1.1%. The axial accuracy is on the other hand more greatly effected
by the α value. At a depth of 20 mm, lowering α from 90◦by steps of 15◦adds on average
∆˜Bz = −2.65% to ˜Bz. Setting the depth to 30 mm increases this average add-on by 11.3%

that the axial estimates are less affected by this variable when compared to the lateral es-
timates. However, the axial bias is in general lower than expected. At β = 0◦, the linear
decrease in accuracy seen in the axial estimates can be seen in some of the lateral estimates
(see ˜By), but at β = 45◦, the bias of the lateral estimates remains relatively constant with
respect to α and the depth. Lastly, shifting the β from 0 to 45◦reduces on average the ˜σ by
6.7%. However, this small reduction might be misleading since some of the proﬁles between
the β are completely different and thus somewhat incomparable.
In conclusion, the lateral estimates were demonstrated to be reliable within a close range
of the transducer. Moving away from the transducer can reduce the accuracy and precision
rapidly if the beam-to-ﬂow angle, α, differs sufﬁciently from 90◦. The axial estimates have
high precision and are less dependent on the depth of the vessel, but the accuracy decrease
for smaller values of α.

Chapter 5
Discussion
Both the simulations and measurements demonstrate that real-time volumetric 3-D VFI is
feasible given that the probing area is within a close distance of the transducer, and the
pulse repetition frequency is relatively high. The parameter study showed that the precision
of the lateral velocities was very sensitive to the vessel distance to the transducer and the
beam-to-ﬂow angle, α. This suggests that further work is needed to sufﬁciently reduce the
inﬂuence of the axial motion, which is not entirely removed by the ﬁltration process of the
TO estimator.
The quality of the axial estimates was less affected by the depth of the vessel center, but
the results showed a high increase in bias for decreasing values of α. In previous work, it
has been shown that axial estimates can be performed with high accuracy and precision for
α = 60◦, in a similar setup [33]. The previous acquisition scheme was however designed
for greater depths, so volumetric images would likely yield poor lateral velocity estimates.
Nevertheless, it does emphasize that further work is needed to optimize the emission se-
quence for close range axial velocity estimations.
The measurements contained severe reverberation artifacts. These manifest themselves
most notably at the vessel border regions of the proﬁle. Aside from reverberations artifacts,
several other adverse factors were present during the experiment, which could play a role
in reducing the quality of the measurements. These factors include:
• Unresponsive transducer channels. Figure 5.1 shows the element responses of the used
RCA transducer. It can be seen from the ﬁgure that 8 of the 62 row-elements measures
nothing but noise.
• Curved aperture. The surface of the aperture was reported to be slightly curved. This
curvature wasn’t accounted for in the ToF calculations, which assumes a ﬂat surface.
• Imprecise vessel center. The transducer ﬁxture does not necessarily align the center
vessel perfectly with the center of the aperture. The center proﬁle was therefore located
via the axial CFM similar to the one shown ﬁgure 4.3a. At β = 45◦the two planes
(x, y, z)|x=0 and (x, y, z)|y=0 are guaranteed to intersect the vessel center. The proﬁle
through the vessel center was identiﬁed as the axial proﬁle which on average has the
highest peak velocity.
Lastly, there is always the risk of small air bubbles being present in the gel between the
transducer cover surface of the aperture. These bubbles could severely distort the image.
Despite these real and potential adverse effects, the estimator was able to provide lateral
velocity proﬁles, which distribution at the center of the vessel followed the theoretical mean
proﬁle closely. The axial proﬁles from the measurements had a low standard deviation but
with a large bias for α = 60◦.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
It was demonstrated that full volumetric 3-D VFI can be performed at high frame rates,
using a 62+62 RCA transducer, a TO cross-correlation estimator, and an interleaved SAI se-
quence. In the current setup, the method requires the vessel to be within close distance of
the transducer, and that a relatively high pulse repetition frequency is being employed in or-
der to accurately estimate the ﬂow velocity ﬁeld. Ideally, we would like to have accurately
estimated ﬂow with a peak velocity of around 1 m/s, but this was only shown to be achiev-
able at a beam-to-ﬂow angle of 90◦. Still, this is to the authors knowledge the ﬁrst time 3-D
VFI with full volumetric coverage has been implemented at frame rates suitable for clinical
use.
To improve the robustness of the estimator, it is recommended that future work focuses
on developing methods for reducing the inﬂuence of axial motion. Also, because axial ve-
locity estimates did not perform as well as expected, the method might beneﬁt signiﬁcantly
from optimizing the emission sequence for axial velocity estimations. Some of the limi-
tations of the setup might also be mitigated by "simply" improving transducer hardware.
Increasing the channel count of the RCA transducer would yield higher quality volumetric
images, and having an aperture with a larger footprint would allow for increased sensitivity
to transverse motion at greater depths.
These potential improvements can all be investigated through Field II simulations, mean-
ing that they can be assessed with existing software and technology.

Function library overview:
• ICORRVEL - Estimates the velocity using the cross-correlation approach.
• TOPREPH - Attenuate axial oscillations.
• DESIGNSEQUENCE - Sets up the emission sequence; deﬁnes the apodization win-
dow, calculates ToF, etc.

tance angle of the emission. Is primarily used to visualize the emitted ﬁelds.

• INTERSECTAABBRAY - Calculates a ray’s intersection with an AABB bounding box.
Is used to determine the length of the vessel.
• FIELDSIMULATION - Function performing the ﬂow simulation.
• PREPAREFILE - Restructure the SARUS parameter ﬁle.
Classes overview:
• TRANSDUCERPROPERTIES - Class object contain the transducer properties.
• FLOWRIG - A class object of the ﬂow rig.
The classes are used to keep track of the parameters of the simulation and measurements.
They also ensure the parameters only can be changed in a way that does not break the scripts
and functions.
Overview of the scripts:
• SUBMISSION SCRIPT - Sumbits ﬂow simulation jobs to the HPC cluster system.
• BEAMFORMATION SCRIPT - Performs the RCA beamformation.
• VELOCITY ESTIMATION SCRIPT - Performs the vector velocity estimations.
