Summary
For most robotics platform dealing with navigation and control, it is crucial to have
a way of determining the position of the robot at any time. This is for instance the
case when having a wheeled mobile robot moving objects between specific locations in
factories and warehouses.
While wheeled robots have the advantage of being equipped with wheel encoders,
which can give a relative estimate of the robot position in a local frame, this estimate
will eventually drift over time. In order to provide a global reference and compensate
for drift, extereoceptive sensors are needed to correct the robot state with respect to a
global map.
Traditionally, the most common sensors used for this purpose are laser range finders,
which have shown great performance in static environments over the years. Standard
laser range finders however comes at a high cost, and are limited to two dimensional space
which can cause problems when the environment is dynamically changing. Therefore this
thesis proposes an alternative method to the localization problem based on a standard
camera and low cost printable artificial landmarks, which can be flexibly placed in
the environment. The proposed method is implemented as a tightly coupled Extended
Kalman Filter (EKF), using the wheel encoders to predict the state of the robot and
subsequently correct the prediction based on visual observed landmarks.
In addition to the localization framework itself, a method for mapping the markers
based on pose graph optimization is proposed, which can be used to easily create a map
of the placed markers. This is done in an offline fashion, serving as a preprocessing step
before using the implemented EKF to provide long term continuous localization.
The thesis is focused towards a logistic application in an industrial setting, and
takes its basis in the pallet transporting robot, Robotize GoPal 400. For validation
purposes, the framework is first tested on a smaller robot platform, where it shows
capable of combating drift and provide accurate global localization, consistent with the
constructed map of artificial landmarks. Finally, the proposed framework is integrated
in the full system on the GoPal 400 and used to successfully pickup and deliver pallets
in an industrial setting.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
During the last decade the manufacturing and logistics industry have increasingly drawn
their attention towards streamlining of internal logistic flows using autonomous mobile
robots.
Common for these industrial systems are the desire to safely and efficiently
navigate a robot from one point to another. To achieve this the robot needs an accurate
estimate of its pose throughout the entire navigation and planning process. This is often
achieved through a separate step known as localization, which based on sensor inputs
continuously update the pose of the robot. For this purpose robotics systems are often
equipped with a variety of different sensors. In an ideal world a single of these sensors
would potentially be sufficient for estimating the robot pose (i.e. odometry through
integration of encoders).
However one thing all sensors have in common is, that in
reality they are subject to different kinds of noise, which would quickly deteriorate the
quality of such an estimation.
When relying only on proprioceptive sensors as for instance wheel encoders and an
IMU, the pose estimation will be subject to large drift over time (as proprioceptive
sensors are only capable of relative measurements), thus requiring exteroceptive mea-
surements in order to compensate for this. The most commonly used sensors for this
are laser and vision sensors. While laser range sensors are highly accurate at measuring
distance, they come at a high cost, are often limited to two dimensional space and can
only be used to match geometrical features, often subject to ambiguity. On the other
hand, cameras are relatively cheap and capture images of the whole three dimensional
space, enabling detection and matching of a wide variety of distinct image features in
the environment which can be used as landmarks for localization.
While the fusion of most proprioceptive and extereoceptive sensors can be used to
give a good estimate of the relative position of the robot, it does not in itself provide any
way to relate this position to a global reference, which is required for robot navigation.
One way to solve this problem is by using a global positioning system. However for most
indoor use cases, such system is not available, or requires major infrastructural change.
An alternative solution is thus to make the robot localize against a previously built map,
which representation for instance can be based on laser or landmarks features. This will
naturally require prior knowledge about the environment, which will in most industrial
scenarios be easily available.
There exist several mapping and localization frameworks based on natural landmarks,
which can be of great value when navigating in a new unknown environment. In an
industrial setting it is however often seen, that only a few objects remain static, while

others are moved around several times a day, causing confusion for existing robotics
systems. It thus becomes essential to distinguish between what features can actually be
trusted for reliable localization. This can be achieved by utilizing artificial landmarks,
which can be placed on known static objects. As opposed to natural landmarks, they
have the great advantage of being designed to be easily detectable, invariant to different
view conditions, and uniquely identifiable, thus avoiding explicit searching and matching
in descriptor space.
The use case for this project is the pallet transporting robot GoPal 400 from the
company Robotize which needs to navigate in factories and warehouses, often subject to
dynamic changes and ambiguous layouts in the environment. This can cause problems
for the existing laser based localization framework relying on more or less explicit static
maps which are unable to handle or detect major changes in the environment. Therefore
this thesis propose an alternative localization method, based on a visual sensor, in the
form of a camera, and artificial landmarks, which strives to serve as a robust and flexible
pose estimation framework to combat the above mentioned challenges.
The framework will be based on sensor fusion in the form of an Extended Kalman
Filter (EKF), taking advantage of both wheel encoders, IMU and camera. By using
a number of artificial landmarks in the form of ArUco tags, the framework will be
shown capable of effectively detecting the static landmarks in the environment and fuse
these measurements with existing onboard sensors in order to combat drift and provide
globally consistent map based localization, suited for long term navigation.
1.1
Related Work
With the growing focus on automation in the industry, the number of mobile robots
used for internal transportation is increasing and examples of large scale autonomous
warehouses have been implemented, such as seen for Amazon Robotics (formerly Kiva
Systems) [Dap12].
Many industrial localization framework however relies on additional infrastructure,
such as high reflective markers mounted in the ceiling as shown in [Ron+11], or magnetic
strips in the ground. Installation of such system require a lot of work, comes at a high
cost, and strongly limits the flexibility of the system.
This has led to implementations of laser based localization methods independent of
any additional infrastructure, such as shown in [Row+12] and [Vas+16], which are based
on the well known Monte Carlo localization introduced in [Del+99]. Other examples of
such systems is the EKF based laser scan line matching shown in [Sie+11]. One of the
drawbacks about these laser based methods is that they rely on the environment being
relatively static and are limited to observation in a two dimensional plane. Changes in
the environment such as the movement of boxes and pallets, often observed in a dynamic
factory environment, can thus for instance lead to errors in the localization.
==PAGE:21==
1.1 Related Work
3
While implementation of 2D pose estimation methods has seen a large growth in
the industry, the academic research within state estimation has to a large extend been
focused towards camera based state estimation in 3 dimensional space, needed for drones
and flying robots. The main solutions to this problem are based on Visual Odometry
(VO) and Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM).
Visual Odometry has found widespread use in multiple applications [For+17; EKC18]
and recently the inclusion of inertial measurements has vastly improved these methods
by introducing scale information in Visual Inertial Odometry methods (VIO), one of the
most well known being [Blo+15]. However these methods will still serve as pure relative
odometry whereas the proposed method in this thesis focus on visual localization which
does not have the same drawbacks.
In [Leu+13], the literature within VIO is divided into what is known as loosely-
coupled and tightly-coupled systems.
Loosely coupled systems tries to fuse different
sensor readings as independent pose estimations, whereas the tightly-coupled systems
incorporates the sensor states directly to take the correlation between the internal states
of the sensors into account.
The advantage of loosely coupled sensor fusion algorithms is that they are simple to
implement. A general framework for applying a loosely coupled Extended Kalman Filter
to a wide variety of sensors is introduced in [MS16], which relies on a simple holonomic
kinematic motion model.
On the other hand tightly-coupled sensor fusion algorithms are more complex but
have shown to be more accurate [Sca17b]. In [MR07] a tightly coupled visual inertial
EKF based algorithm was introduced, which used the IMU data for state prediction and
the camera to update the state based on tracked features. Later this work was extended
to also include mapped landmarks for planetary landing in [Mou+09]. In [Lyn+15] a
similar approach was used where feature descriptors were matched against a previously
build map of the environment, in order to fuse global pose information into a local state
estimator and prevent drift.
The proposed state estimation method introduced in this thesis, is strongly related
to how these VIO algorithms are implemented, however due to the nature of wheeled
robots, the prediction step can be carried out based on encoder readings, which can
be used directly for pose prediction (without the need to integrate accelerations and
velocities), making it less sensitive to noise.
SLAM algorithms deal with localizing and building a map at the same time. Ex-
amples of such systems are PTAM [KM07] and LSD-SLAM [ESC14].
Many SLAM
algorithms are however limited to single session localization and are not consistent with
respect to a global reference frame. In order to compensate for this, frameworks that per-
forms loop closure in order to recognize previously visited places in a created map, have
been implemented, such as in [Sch+17; ESC14; Mon15]. In this thesis it will however be
sought to create such a map in an offline fashion based on GraphSLAM as introduced
in [TBF05].

1 Introduction
A wide range of feature detectors and descriptors in order to robustly detect and
match features in an image has been developed over the years, such as [HS88; ST94;
Low04; BTV06]. However natural landmarks are not unique and given the known en-
vironment the robot operates in, the work presented in this thesis will rather rely on
creating a map based on artificial landmarks (also known as fiducials).
Fiducial markers are a kind of artificial key points, which can be uniquely identified.
Approaches based on planar markers are the most popular ones.
One of the main
advantages of these markers is that they have 4 outer corners, which is the minimum
number of keypoints needed for camera pose estimation. Some of the most used planer
markers for robot state estimation is AprilTags [Ols11] and ArUco tags [Gar+16], where
the latter will be used for this project.
A lot of fiducial based-localization systems have been targeted towards smaller scale
related to augmented reality or by only using the camera for pose estimation.
The
problem about these approaches are that they fail to provide pose estimation in cases
where no marker is visible or in case of motion blur. An example of such system is
presented in [Muñ+18], where localization based on ArUco markers is carried out.
[LL09] presents a SLAM algorithm for artificial landmarks and uses an EKF based
method to estimate the robot pose and landmark positions. A similar EKF-based SLAM
algorithm is applied to an indoor Blimp in [Yam+09].
[NBB15] proposes a method closely related to the visual-inertial frameworks in
[Mou+09] and [Leu+13], which uses artificial landmarks as features in the environment.
This state estimation algorithm is applied on a Micro Aerial Vehicle (MAV), and is as
such very similar to the method proposed in this thesis however the prediction model is
different.
1.2
Contributions
Based on the given introduction and motivation, the main contributions of this thesis
can be summarized in the following objectives:
• Derive and implement a tightly coupled extended Kalman filter, based on an odom-
etry driven prediction model fused with IMU and a landmark based camera update
model.
• Show how fiducial landmarks, such as ArUco tags, can be used as a robust, easy
to match way of extracting landmarks from an image and use these in the EKF.
• Provide a mapping framework, to create a map of markers in the environment.
• Set up a simulation environment in order to test the implemented EKF.
• Evaluate the implemented EKF based localization against ground truth measure-
ments and existing methods.

Thesis Outline
The thesis is structured as follows. Chapter 2 gives an overview of the overall system

The main focus of this project is thus the implementation of a robot state estimator
for a mobile robot based on visual markers. An overview of the system can be seen in
Figure 2.2. The estimator will be based on an Extended Kalman Filter (EKF), using
the encoders mounted on the wheels to predict the state of the robot and 3D landmarks

robustness and fast feature matching fiducial markers, in the form of AR tags, will be

2.1.1
Planning and Navigation
The planning and navigation block as shown in green in Figure 2.3, can be divided
into 3 sub blocks dealing with the navigation on different levels. First a global plan is
computed based on the current position of the robot and its goal position. Given a cost
map of the factory, a global plan can be computed as a graph search in the given grid
map of the factory, where each cell corresponds to a weighted edge depending on how
close it is to a given obstacle.
Having computed the overall plan, the robot needs to follow in order to reach the
goal, the local planner takes over and are responsible of the actual navigation of the
==PAGE:28==
10
2 Industrial Overview and System Structure
robot. As it navigates along the path the local planner will try to compute the optimal
path to follow in order to track the global trajectory while at the same time avoiding
obstacles. The GoPal is using laser scanners and 3D cameras to detect obstacles which
can be added to a local cost map, in order to take their position into consideration. An
example of a commonly used local planner, is the Dynamic Window Approach (DWA),
which was introduced in [FBT97]. Briefly explained it works by sampling the control
space of the robot and try to simulate all possible velocities forward in time to estimate
what would happen and score each trajectory with respect to a grid based cost function,
consisting of several objectives. The highest scoring velocity is then chosen and sent to
the motor controller. This procedure is then repeated until the robot reaches its goal.
The last block of the planning and navigation is the docking handler, which parks
the robot in a given pallet station after reaching the desired approach position in front
of the pallet station. The navigation and localization stack thus only need to get the
robot in front of the pallet station and then the docking handler takes care of the rest.
2.1.2
Localization
During the entire navigation process the robot needs an estimate of its actual position
with respect to a global reference and this is where localization becomes crucial. As men-
tioned earlier wheeled robots have the advantage of being able to estimate the relative
pose based on the odometry, however even a small error in heading would for instance
make the estimated position error grow unbounded over time due to the integration of
the measurements. Hence localization against a global reference is crucial. This is the
part of the system which is in focus in this thesis.
The current localization running on the GoPal 400 is based on laser scans and uses
the widely used Adaptive Monte Carlo Localization algorithm introduced in [Del+99],
also known as AMCL, which is based on a particle filter. Briefly explained, this algo-
rithm works by randomly sampling the motion model of the robot based on the previous
position. Each laser ray is then weighted with a certain probability given the sampled
position, and based on the most likely position the pose is updated. This algorithm how-
ever relies on a static map to match against, and in very dynamic factory environments,
this might not always be the case, and thus the reason why alternatives are sought.

Coordinate Frames
For the framework to be implemented in this work there will be four different refer-
ence frames, each with their own properties. The different frames together with the
corresponding transformations can be seen in Figure 2.5.
The coordinate frame of the robot will be denoted Base, and is rigidly placed at the
center between the two driving wheels on the robot. Furthermore a static transformation
exists between the robot base and the mounted Camera frame (T B
C ).
The odom frame is the odometry frame and intended to be used for the pure odometry.
The transformation T O
B can thus be computed based on the encoder readings in order to
make sure that the transformation will always be continuous which is often desired on
a local control level. As a consequence this transformation will drift over time, making
it useless for longterm navigation.
In order to ensure global consistency the reference frame Map is used, and is the frame
which will be used for global navigation. The transformation T M
B is thus meant to be the
true transformation of the robot base in the global reference frame. In order to assure
this the transformation T M
O will have to be corrected based on the global localization.
As this transformation should always represent the best estimate of the robot position
in the map frame, it is allowed to have large discrete jumps which can for instance occur
when new extereoceptive measurements are integrated into the localization framework.
The map frame thus serves as a long term global reference which might not be desired
to use directly for local navigation but is well suited for mitigating the drift for global
planning.

OptiTrack
In order to compare the implemented localization framework against ground truth data,
a number of the experiments have been carried out using the OptiTrack Motion Capture
System as can be seen in [Opt], which theoretically is capable of tracking 6 DOF of a
rigid body down to sub-milimeter precision with the right configuration.
The system works by placing a number of retroreflective markers on the object to
be tracked and a number of IR emitting cameras around the space in which the object
has to be tracked. The emitted IR light is thus reflected on the markers which can
be sensed by the surrounding cameras in order to compute their 2D position in each
camera.
These points can then be triangulated using the accompanying OptiTrack
motive software which triangulates the points in order to get the 6 DOF pose of the
tracked object in a global frame. The retroreflective markers placed on the robot can be
seen in Figure 2.4(a).

CHAPTER 3
State Estimation
Having given an overview of the overall system involved for the thesis, this chapters
looks closer into the state estimation of the robot and how this can be done using the
formulation of an Extended Kalman filter, which proves suited for the task, due to the
non-linearities of the modelled system. First an overview of the Extended Kalman Filter
approach will be given, followed by a description of the involved mathematical models.
Based on these the final estimator, to be implemented, is derived.
In order for the robot to perceive its environment, it is equipped with a number of
sensors, which can be used together with the model of the robot to estimate the state
of the robot. Neither the robot nor its sensors can be modeled perfectly and will to
some extend always be subject to a certain amount of noise, which will degrade the
available sensor information. Having multiple sensors, one solution to this problem, is
to apply multi sensor fusion in order to estimate the overall state of the robot. One way
to achieve this is by the use of a Kalman filter, which is an efficient and optimal sensor
fusion technique[Sie+11].

3.2.2.1
Camera Model
The sensor used for the extereoceptive measurements in this project, is a camera mounted
on the robot. To model the camera, a pin-hole model is used, as illustrated in Figure 3.3.
The camera itself is essentially a bearing sensor measuring angles of the light.

the optical center. The intersection of the optical axis with the image plane is referred
to as the principal point O. The focal length f is the distance between the optical center
and the principal point.

The above mentioned camera model, assumes the reprojection to
be a linear operation, meaning that straight lines in the world, will be reprojected as
straight lines on the image plane. However this turn out not to always be the case,
since wide-angle lenses will often cause radial distortion making straight lines curved.
Therefore the radial distortion should be taken into account in the model. To do this,
a non-linear function can be fitted to the radial distortion, as described in [Sie+11, Ch.
4.2.3.3].

3.4
EKF Divergence Handling
Since the measurement Jacobian Hk for the EKF, is a linearization of the mapping from
state to measurement around the estimated state, it is a first order approximation of the
model, which works well within small errors in the state. However if the error between
the estimated state and the actual state becomes large, the linearization might no longer
be valid, which in turn can cause the filter to diverge. This will also be the case if a one
to one mapping between the state and measurement does not exist, and the system as
such is unobservable [BC01].
It is thus desired to detect when this happens in order to prevent the filter from
diverging towards infinity. The way this is done in this project, is by exploiting the
Mahalanobis distance, which based on the covariance of the innovation and residuals,
can be used to compute how far the observed measurement is away from the expected
distribution of the measurements. The Mahalanobis distance can according to [Sie+11],
be written as:
dM = rT

where ri,k is the residual for the ith measurement at time step k and Σin,k the innova-

(3.57)

As will be shown in Section 4.2, the camera pose can be estimated from 4 point
correspondences. Given 4 corners from an observed tag, these can hence be used to
reinitialize the filter in order to avoid divergence.

CHAPTER 4
Feature Extraction and
Pose Estimation
In order to fuse 3D landmarks into the Extended Kalman filter as described in Chapter 3,
a robust method for finding correspondence between the landmarks and the 2D image
points is sought. While there has been a lot of research within matching of natural
features in the literature, there is always a risk of making a wrong match which hurts
robustness of the method. As robustness is of large importance in industrial scenarios
an alternative can be to use artificial markers which has been designed to be easily and
robustly detected and matched.
Some of the most used artificial markers are binary squared fiducial markers. The
two main advantages of theses types of markers are that they consists of four outer
corners which is the minimum number to obtain a camera pose and the fact that the
inner binary code of squares are good for applying different correction techniques which
allows robust tag detection [Ope15]. One such type of markers are ArUco markers, which
will be used here.
4.1
ArUco Markers
The ArUco library is open source software library implemented as part of OpenCV [Ope].
The library is implemented in C++ and comes with a number of functions for marker
detection, camera calibration and pose estimation.
4.1.1
Markers
An ArUco marker consists of an external black border and an inner region of white
squares that encodes a unique binary code, that is used to identify the markers. The
size of the marker determines the number of bits in the code, such that a 6x6 marker for
instance has 36 bits. The physical size of the marker can be chosen arbitrarily depending
on the use case. For this project the markers were chosen to be 0.16 meters wide, since
this was approximately the maximum size fitting on a single A4 page, while still leaving
some free white border for contrast.
==PAGE:48==
30
4 Feature Extraction and Pose Estimation
There exists different types of markers belonging to different dictionaries, which can
depend on the specific application. The dictionaries are defined by dictionary size and
marker size, where the dictionary size is the number of markers in the dictionary and
the marker size the number of bits in the marker. For this project the DICT_6X6_250
dictionary was used, consisting of 250 markers of size 6 time 6.
The number of bits in the marker, determines how many different combinations can
exists in the dictionary. The id of the tag is not equal to the binary code, but rather
the index in the dictionary [Ope15].

Parameters
While the ArUco library comes with several parameters, it is according to the documen-

of situations.”, such that it in general should not be needed to change the parameters,
except for the type of dictionary. From the experiments carried out for this project, no
issues with detection of the markers were experienced, and thus the standard parameters
was kept for the tag detection.
4.2
Pose Estimation
The problem of determining the camera position from a set of given 3D points is known as
the PnP (Perspective-n-Point) problem. A general solution for this problem, is applying
the DLT (Direct Linear Transform) algorithm as introduced in [AK15], which requires
a total of 6 point correspondences and also estimates the camera intrinsics. Having a
calibrated camera, it can however be shown, that the minimum number of points needed
to find an unique solution for the camera pose, is 4 points, which is exactly the case for
a detected marker with four corners. While the implemented EKF in this thesis relies
on a more tightly coupled approach, not directly dependent on solving this problem, it
will be used to recover from filter divergence and to construct the pose graphs used for
mapping, as described in Section 5.1.

CHAPTER 5
Mapping Framework
Before the ArUco Markers, introduced in Chapter 4, can be used as 3D landmarks to
correct the robot position, their absolute position with respect to the map frame must
be known in advance. It is thus required to create a map of the 3D landmarks first. One
way of doing this could be to simply measure the position manually, however this would
be a very cumbersome and has a large risk of mistakes. Therefore this section provides
an autonomous way to create a map of markers in the environment.
Mapping in an unknown environment is a bit of a chicken and egg paradox, since
accurate mapping of the environment is needed to get accurate localization, however in
order to map the environment in the first place, some kind of localization is needed. The
field dealing with this is known as simultaneous localization and mapping as described
in Section 1.1.
Many SLAM algorithms deal with the problem of creating a map online based on a
completely unknown environment. However for the implemented localization framework
in this thesis, the AR tags to be mapped are known in advance and the generated map
can as such be assumed to be static and sparse. Therefore the mapping process will be
carried out in an offline fashion by first creating a pose graph and then optimizing the
node positions in the graph based on a given cost function.
After having constructed the pose graph, it will be shown how this can be used to
identify the parameters of the odometric model in order to improve the odometry.
5.1
Pose Graph Optimizaiton
The used mapping method is based on pose graph optimization, which is also referred
to as GraphSLAM in [TBF05]. This method solves the full SLAM problem in an offline
fashion and has as such been kept completely separate from the state estimation and
localization method proposed in Section 3.3. The mapping process hence serves as a
preprocessing step, that in theory only has to be done once in order to make the robot
localize continuously in the build map.
The idea behind the GraphSLAM algorithm, as described in [TBF05], is to create
graph of robot poses and features, resulting in a sum of non-linear quadratic constraints
which can be solved in a maximum likelihood fashion, resulting in a sparse graph poste-
rior.

7.2
EKF Convergence
Having tuned the filter, the convergence of the filter was tested by applying a step to
each of the three pose states individually.
Since the SMR initially did not have an
IMU, these results were carried out using only the camera to correct the state, based
on the observed visual markers. Since the convergence of the state will depend on the
covariance of the current state and the covariance associated with the observation, it
is hard to say anything general about the settling time. Therefore these experiments
should rather be seen as a validation of convergence, rather than a method to determine
how fast the EKF converges.
Figure 7.4 and 7.5 both shows how the filter reacts when a noise step of 0.1 is
applied to the current state. To make them comparable the corresponding variance of
the state was also set to 0.1 respectively. As can be seen, the position converges fast
within a few samples whereas the heading takes a bit longer and converges within half
a second. The difference in x and y can be explained by the difference in accuracy of
the state estimation when moving sideways and in depth relative to the marker, as also
illustrated in Figure 7.2(a). Overall it can however be seen that the filter is capable
of handling minor noise steps and correct the states accordingly. Larger steps might
result in divergence of the filter, due to non-linearities of the EKF, which is undesired
behaviour and further examined in Section 3.4.

7.3
Drift Experiment
Since the main purpose of using a global localization framework is that it should be able
to combat drift in the odometry and make the position error stay bounded, this was
sought to be validated. In order to test these effects the robot was driven in rectangles of
approximately 10 times 3 meters, looping a total of 5 rounds counter clockwise, driving
a total distance of approximately 132 meters. A single tag was placed in each corner, to
be used by the EKF. First the pure odometry was compared against the odometry fused
with IMU data as shown in Figure 7.6(a). As can be seen, the pure odometry drifts quite
significantly, and the rectangles are almost offset with 90 degrees towards the end. The
odometry fused with the data from the IMU does a better job of keeping the heading,
however a relatively significant amount of drift in the heading is still observed.
When compared to the fully implemented EKF localization in Figure 7.6(b), it is
however clear, that it is capable of combating drift. The pose estimation of the robot
stays within a bounded rectangle for all 5 loops. It can be seen that it does drift slightly
when no tag is observed, since there are some quite clear correction step around the
middle on both of the long side, which is around 5 to 6 meters from the marker, the
robot is facing at that given time. It is clear how this drift is corrected when the tags
enter into the view again and thus these two plots show that the implemented EKF
based localization framework is capable of combating drift.

7.5
Marker distance
The previous experiment showed that the implemented localization framework is quite
accurate when markers are visible for the majority of the time. However as soon as no
markers can be detected, the robot has to dead reckon based on pure odometry. While
this is not a problem for small distances, the drift will eventually become so large, that
the error in the state estimation might cause the robot navigation to fail, or even make
the EKF diverge. It is thus sought to do a worst case analysis of how far the robot can
travel without observing a marker.
First a limit of how much deviation in state will be considered acceptable has to be
determined. To relate it to the factory setting and the application of navigating the
robot in front of a pallet station, it is decided that the robot should not be more than
half the width of an EU pallet off in x or y direction, which corresponds to 0.4 meters,
as this is approximately needed to be able to initialize the docking procedure.

based on the error in initial state and the uncertainty in the state estimate. As shown in

will just be added as an offset, it will have little influence in the propagating error
compared to the error in initial angle. Therefore the initial error in position is neglected
for this analysis. Looking at the estimated angle on the other hand, it was seen that

Large Scale Scenario
While the constructed maze was a more realistic scenario, compared to the other experi-

small spaces. In large factories the robot might have to drive around large floors and
still be able to localize continuously. In order to test the performance of the localiza-

around the upper floor of building 329 at DTU.
A total of 15 markers were placed around the floor and a map was recorded by
driving around the entire floor twice and finding the optimal tag positions using the pose
graph optimization methods. Another dataset used for evaluation was then recorded,
having the robot driving around the floor 3 times over a total distance of approximately
340 meters. The estimated trajectory using all 15 markers is shown in Figure 7.16(a).

attempted to drive the same route for each of the multiple laps. This way any drift would
become apparent if the trajectory of a single lap consistently deviated from any of the
others. However it can be seen that the trajectory stays continuously bounded without
any significant signs of drift between the laps. For comparison the pose estimated on pure
odometry has been plotted next to it, which drifts quite significantly over time. This
experiment hence shows that the framework is also able to provide drift free localization
on a larger scale.
To investigate how many markers were needed to achieve continuous operation, the
number of tags in the map was reduced and the dataset reevaluated. It was found that
the minimum number of tags needed to cover the whole area of the upper floor was 5.
when using only 4 tags, it was experienced that the localization could fail, making the
EKF diverge, which is an unintended behavior and should always be sought avoided. A
plot of the estimated path using only 5 markers compared to using all 15 are shown

marker is observed, however at some points this correction result in a jump of 1-2 meters,
which is not very desired when navigating the robot. The size of these jumps will however
depend on how much the odometry drifts. It was seen in Section 4.2, that the robot was
able to detect the tags at a distance of 5-6 meters. In between it is thus dead reckoning
completely based on odometry which gives no guarantee of the localization accuracy.
While the longest spacing between two markers in this experiment is approximately 35
meters, the plot shows that the markers should probably not be placed more than 10-15

7.8
Divergence Handling
As mentioned in Section 3.4, one of the problems about using an EKF, is that due to
linearization of non-linear functions, there is a risk that the state estimate will diverge,
if the state used for linearization is far from the true state. In order to test this, the
same dataset as for the large scale experiment was used, and only markers during the
first part of the trajectory was included in the map, as shown in Figure 7.17.
Since the robot is driving for a long distance without meeting any known markers,
the state will for a long period of time be estimated based on pure odometry and drift
away from its true value. As a consequence of this, the state starts to diverge as soon as
a known marker is observed again, and fused into the filter around a wrong linearization
point. This is shown in Figure 7.17(a), where it is clear that after the first round, the
state estimation is so far off, that it is completely useless.
As a fault detection method, the Mahalanobis distance is computed, and if it gets
over a certain threshold, the state is reset using the pose estimation algorithm introduced
in Section 4.2. By applying this to the same trajectory, the robot is able to detect when
it starts to diverge and correct the position respectively, as shown in Figure 7.17(b).
While this method does not completely eliminate the divergence, it is able to recover
from it, such that the path going between the markers stays bounded without drifting
over time.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In this thesis it was successfully shown how a full localization system based on 3D
landmarks, in the form of ArUco tags, could be used as an alternative to traditional
laser based localization. Motivated by the often dynamic industrial environments, where
traditional laser based navigation often become infeasible, the implemented solution was
shown able to combat drift in wheel odometry and continuously provide accurate state
estimation for the position of the robot, based on a few static fiducial landmarks.
This was achieved by first formulating a sensor fusion scheme based on an Extended
Kalman Filter, which was chosen due to the non-linearity of the system. By using an
odometry driven prediction model and measurements from and IMU and camera, it was
shown how these could be used for accurate pose estimation of the robot. Experiments
showed that the gyroscope from the IMU was able to partially correct the heading of
the robot locally, however in order to combat drift with respect to a global reference,
the camera was used as an extereoceptive sensor. The camera model was formulated
in a general way, in order to effectively fuse 3D to 2D point correspondences, between
landmarks and image points, directly into the filter.
Compared to other methods, the proposed localization method relies on relatively
cheap sensors and comes at a low cost. Furthermore through the use of squared fiducials
it has the advantage of being able to uniquely match features, avoiding the ambiguity
issues which often trouble traditional laser and camera based methods. Another ad-
vantage of using squared markers is that the four corners corresponds to the minimum
number of points needed to solve the projective-n-point problem, which can be used to
initialize the filter as well as re-initialize the state in case of divergence.
In order to provide 3D to 2D correspondences, the 3D poses of the landmarks had
to be known in advance, and thus a method for mapping the markers was found. Since
the markers were assumed to be placed on static objects in the environment, an offline
mapping method based on constructing a pose graph was proposed.
By using pure
odometry and estimating the relative position between the robot and observed tags, it
was shown how the uncertainty in the measurement could be used as soft constraints
in order to find the optimal tag positions minimizing the overall cost of the graph. A
number of experiments were carried out to evaluate the accuracy of the constructed
maps and it was furthermore shown how the pose graph approach could be used to
refine the systematic odometry parameters for the robot.
The proposed method was successfully implemented in ROS and tested in simulation,
==PAGE:94==
76
8 Conclusion
where it was verified how the fusion of ArUco tags into the EKF could be used to correct
the robot state in order to get pose estimation very close to the provided ground truth.
To test the implemented framework in real life, several experiments were carried out.
While the final goal was to have the framework running live on the Robotize GoPal 400,
most of the initial development were carried out on the SMR robot.
Evaluation of the framework against ground truth showed, that it was able to suc-
cessfully combat drift with an deviation in the estimated robot state of less than 10
centimeters in position and less than 0.05 radians in heading.
To compare it with a laser based method, a track was constructed where the frame-
work was evaluated against the AMCL algorithm, currently used on the GoPal 400. The
results showed that the proposed method was able to localize at comparable level to the
existing AMCL method.
Furthermore the framework was tested in a large scale scenario covering a distance
of more than 300 meters and it was once again shown how it successfully corrected the
robot state in order to combat drift. The proposed method is naturally limited by the
density of tags, since it will be dead reckoning on pure odometry in between observable
tags. While the density of tags depends on different factors such as the uncertainty
in odometry, it was found that the tags should be placed approximately every 10-15
meters apart in order to achieve continuous accurate localization. While the filter was
shown able to correct the position when a tag was eventually observed and even catch
divergence, such large jumps in the pose estimate should be sought avoided.
Finally, the framework was integrated on the GoPal 400 robot at the Robotize test
facility and tested in closed loop as part as the whole system.
Using the proposed
method the GoPal was able to successfully execute several orders covering a total driven
distance of 300 meters. This experiments showed that the the implemented framework
could directly replace the existing laser based localization in order to make the robot
successfully pickup and deliver pallets in a real industrial scenario.
Overall, the proposed method successfully showed a way to achieve robust low cost
localization on a mobile robot which can be easily implemented in an industrial setting
where traditional methods would otherwise fail.
8.1
Future Work
In the future, it could be interesting to look at how other visual features could be used
as extereoceptive measurements.
One of the possibilities could for instance be to include natural landmarks in the map,
such as corner features already existing in the environment. In this way it would only
be necessary to use the artificial AR tags in places, where the environment is changing
often and in more static environments, the natural landmarks could be used. In this case
a map of natural features would have to be created of the environment. One solution
==PAGE:95==
8.1 Future Work
77
for this could be to use the Maplab framework introduced in [Sch+17].
Another type of features, could for instance be lines in the environment, which could
be found by applying an edge detection algorithm to the image and compute the line
parameters by using a line fitting algorithm such as the Hough transform or RANSAC,
as described in [Sie+11, Ch. 4.7.2]. Based on these the lines could be matched using
the Mahalanobis distance and fused into the EKF, similar to what was done for laser
points in [Sie+11, Ch. 4.7].
In some cases it might not be needed to have accurate localization along the whole
trajectory of the robot, as long as it is made sure that the robot does not deviate too
much from its global path. One such case could for instance be if the robot was to drive
along a designated forklift lane, where it had to go straight until a turn was met. In
this case a local planner could be designed to control the robot in a more behavioral
fashion and locally follow a given road, while estimating the driven distance based on
the odometry. When a known landmark is met, the global localization would be able to
accurately correct the state of the robot. In this way the landmark would only be needed
in cases where accurate localization are crucial, such as when turning, parking etc. The
problem about this solution is that in case the robot misses an essential landmark, it
can eventually cause the robot to get lost, which should be handled in a reliable way.
While the proposed method relies only on a 2D camera as extereoceptive sensor,
lasers will often be available anyway on larger industrial robots such as the GoPal 400
for safety reasons. In this case it would be silly not to take advantage of the lasers to
localize with. It would thus be interesting to look into how the two methods can be
combined. A simple solution would be to for instance rely on the AMCL algorithm
when no markers were available and then when the robot enters a more dynamic area
or a marker is observed switch to the marker based EKF localization. A better solution
would however be to fuse the independent readings from laser and camera together in
a more tightly coupled way and make them complement each other in for instance an
EKF, in order to further improve the state estimation and make it even more robust.
