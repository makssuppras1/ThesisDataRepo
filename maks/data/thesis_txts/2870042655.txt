Sustainable Processing of Biogas Digestate
Using Pyrolysis and Gasification
Abstract
The sustainable management of biogas digestate remains a key challenge in bioenergy
production. This study explores the potential of thermal processing techniques, specifically
pyrolysis and gasification, to enhance the value of digestate while maximizing nutrient re-
tention in biochar. The primary objective is to develop a techno-economically viable plant
model that produces bio-fuels and generates biochar as a fertilizer substitute for Danish
agriculture.
The results indicate that gasification using Low Temperature Circulating Fluidized Bed
(LT CFB) technology effectively converts biogas digestate into syngas, biochar, and other
valuable by-products. The produced biochar retains essential nutrients such as nitrogen,
phosphorus, and potassium, making it a potential alternative to conventional fertilizers.
However, nutrient retention varies depending on processing conditions, necessitating a hybrid
approach that combines biochar application with liquid digestate to meet crop nutrient
requirements.
The economic analysis suggests that the proposed plant can be financially viable, with
a projected return on investment of 8.2 years under baseline conditions. The levelized cost
of methanol production is estimated to range between 86.4 and 89.6 EUR/MWhMeOH, in-
fluenced by the efficiency of biomass drying and hydrogen production costs.
While the
technology presents promising opportunities for biofuel and fertilizer production, economic
feasibility remains sensitive to energy prices and policy incentives.
Future research should focus on optimizing process conditions, addressing potential
environmental concerns such as emissions and tar formation, and refining biochar quality
to enhance its agricultural applicability. By integrating biofuel production with nutrient
recovery, this research supports the development of a circular bio-economy, contributing to
both renewable energy generation and sustainable agricultural practices.

Introduction
1
Introduction
Global warming is an issue of global scale. Since the goal of limiting temperature rise to
1.5 °C under the Paris Agreement is becoming increasingly more out of reach, countries are
seeking solutions to combat climate change [IEA, 2024b]. This led to global efforts, like tran-
sitioning from fossil fuels to renewable energy sources. Many technologies are being adopted
by countries, like the substitution of traditional power plants like natural gas and coal-fired
power plants for renewable energy sources like wind and solar power. Even though wind
and solar energy have become more popular among these options, bioenergy also contributes
significantly to the creation of renewable energy, offering a route to a sustainable future.
Bioenergy is an umbrella term for energy or fuels made from biological resources, such
as bioethanol, biodiesel, and biomethane. These fuels help to decarbonize the energy sector
by replacing conventional non-renewable alternatives. Since bioenergy is continuous and can
be stored for later use, it is a more predictable alternative to inconsistent renewable sources
like solar and wind. Because of this, bioenergy is especially useful in areas where energy
demands vary, like big cities. Additionally, by using agricultural waste and other organic
materials to produce energy, bioenergy helps the development of a circular bio-economy while
simultaneously decreasing waste and offering a sustainable energy source [IEA, 2023].
1.1
Anaerobic Fermentation
Biomethane is indistinguishable from ordinary methane. However, conventional methane
is often produced from natural gas, whilst biomethane is produced from bio-based resources,
like agricultural waste[IEA, 2020]. Most biomethane is captured using biogas plants that
operate through anaerobic fermentation, a biochemical process in which bacteria digest bi-
ological material, such as agricultural waste, under anaerobic conditions. The by-products
of this process are methane and carbon dioxide. The methane can be separated and used in
industry or fed into the natural gas network. Beyond energy extraction, anaerobic fermenta-
tion offers additional benefits, such as preventing carbon dioxide emissions, since biomethane
is carbon neutral [IEA, 2020]. The methane captured from biomass would otherwise be emit-
ted into the atmosphere, making the process especially important for carbon-rich materials
like manure. Methane is a very potent greenhouse gas, it is 28 times more potent than carbon
dioxide over 100 years. This is why it is crucial to minimize these emissions. A schematic of
an ordinary anaerobic digester is seen in Figure 1

1.2
Thermal Processing
Over the past few decades, research has focused on extracting energy from other biomasses.
Examples of these methods are pyrolysis and gasification; these are both forms of thermo-
chemical processing. Pyrolysis is derived from the Greek words for "fire" and "separation"
and involves breaking down biological compounds with the use of high temperatures (300-
1000 °C). Pyrolysis occurs in the absence of oxygen, initiating chemical reactions that pro-
duce syngas (mainly CO and H2), bio-oil, and biochar.[Alkharabsheh et al., 2021]

Gasification is another method of thermochemical process for converting biomass into
fuels, the main difference from pyrolysis is mainly in temperature and oxygen presence.
Gasification happens under oxygen-poor conditions (excess air ratio < 1), whereas pyrolysis
requires strictly oxygen-free conditions. Figure 4 shows an example of an updraft fixed-
bed gasification reactor, where gas moves upward through a char bed. While all gasification
reactor types mainly produce synthesis gas (syngas), they generate relatively smaller amounts
of biochar compared to pyrolysis. Synthesis gas or syngas is a mixture of carbon monoxide
and hydrogen gas. The mixture often also consists of carbon dioxide and methane. The gas
gets its name from its application in downstream processes where the compounds are often
used to synthesize other more complex hydrocarbons.

There have also been setups where the two technologies are combined into a so-called
two-stage gasifier. The main advantage of the two-stage gasifier is the near absence of tar
in the syngas [Birk et al., 2003]. This is especially beneficial for the potential downstream
processing using the produced syngas.
The Low Temperature Circulating Fluidized Bed Reactor (LT CFB) is another method
of thermal gasification.
The LT CFB was developed by Danish Fluid Bed Technology
in collaboration with the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and DONG Energy.
[Ahrenfeldt et al., 2013] In Figure 5 a schematic is seen of a LT CFB. The reactor uses
the components explained in the previous sections, namely the pyrolysis reactor and the
gasification reactor, only in this case, they are called chambers.
The key difference of the pyrolysis chamber compared to the screw pyrolysis reactor
seen in Figure 3 is that instead of a screw a circulating fluidized bed is used. Sand is used
as a bed material in the reactor, it also functions as a heat medium to transfer the heat
from one reactor to the other. [Kofler, 2023] The temperature of the LT CFB is significantly
lower than ordinary thermal gasifiers like the fixed bed reactor discussed in subsection 1.2.
This prevents the inorganic compounds in the ash from melting and agglomerating. Like the
technologies mentioned before, the LT CFB is also able to produce a nutrient-rich biochar
(or bio-ash), which can be used for agricultural applications. However, the main goal of this
reactor, like other gasifiers, is to produce gasses that can be used in downstream processes
or for combustion.

1.3
Biochar
Biochar is one of the main products from both pyrolysis and gasification. The biochar
consists mostly of stable carbon and inorganic substances such as NPK nutrients (Nitrogen,
Phosphorous, and Potassium). This makes biochar useful for agricultural use. To assist in
achieving climate goals, for instance, the Danish government intends to exploit biochar’s ca-
pacity to absorb carbon, serving as a carbon sink[Elsgaard et al., 2022] [Bruun et al., 2017].
The biochar’s NPK content, similar to the original feedstock, allows it to substitute for
artificial fertilizers, contributing to nutrient recycling. The feedstock type and processing
conditions, like residence time and temperature, significantly affect the biochar’s quality and
nutrient composition [Elsgaard et al., 2022].
In addition to nutrient benefits, biochar improves soil water retention due to its high
porosity, which is particularly helpful in sandy soils like those in Jutland, Denmark [Xu et al., 2011]
[Atkinson et al., 2010]. Although biochar’s carbon is stable, some degrades slowly over time,
which can benefit carbon-poor soils, especially outside of the already fertile regions such as
western Europe. A more deep dive literature review concerning the fertilizing abilities of
biochar can be found in section 2.

1.4
Biofuels
Syngas and bio-oil are products of gasification and pyrolysis, respectively, and can
be converted into more complex and energy-dense hydrocarbons (CxHy) that can be uti-
lized as fuels. There are methods, such as methanation, which create synthetic natural gas
(SNG) by synthesizing methane from hydrogen and a basic carbon source (CO2 or CO)
[Clausen et al., 2019]. Alternatively, methanol can be synthesized through methanol synthe-
sis. This method converts hydrogen and carbon monoxide into methanol (CH3OH), which
has a higher energy density than methane or hydrogen gas [C Cutler, 2024]. Using catalysts
like iron or cobalt, Fischer-Tropsch processes may transform syngas into liquid hydrocarbons
that are comparable to gasoline for an even higher energy density [Bezergianni and Dimitriadis, 2013].
Whilst the transition of electricity generation to renewable sources is going quite well in
Denmark, the transportation sector is lagging. In Figure 6, it can be seen that, even though
a decline is predicted in the future, the emissions from road transport are not declining
fast enough to reach the goal of a 70 % reduction by 2030. [IEA, 2024a] It is speculated
that green or bio-methanol can be used as an intermediate solution to reduce the green-
house gas emissions from the road transport sector as the electrification is catching up.

2
Literature Review
The main goal of this chapter is to find out if biochar produced from the pyrolysis or
gasification of biogas digestate contains enough nutrients to (partially) replace ordinary fer-
tilizer on Danish farm soils. First, according to the literature, the content of NPK (Nitrogen,
Phosphorous, and Potassium) compounds in biochar is highly dependent on the feedstock
of the pyrolysis plant. [Elsgaard et al., 2022] When comparing studies, it becomes apparent
that manure-based feedstocks contain, on average, more nutrients than wood- or straw-based
biochars. [Elsgaard et al., 2022] [Piash et al., 2021] [Novak et al., 2018] Pyrolysis conditions
have also been linked to influence the nutrient contents of the produced biochar. Since the
loss of organic material increases with increasing pyrolysis temperature, the concentration of
the compounds that are part of the ash-fraction of the biomass increases. [Bruun et al., 2017]
The goal is to provide the farmers with an equivalent product to the digestate they
provide to the thermal chemical plant. Therefore, it is important to consider the effects of
pyrolysis and gasification on the quality and nutrient-release characteristics of the biochar.
This chapter will use literature to obtain information about this matter and determine
whether biochar produced from biogas digestate will be a suitable replacement for artificial
fertilizer and the direct application of digestate.

2.1
Nitrogen
In ?? a trend can be observed when comparing the pyrolysis temperature and the ni-
trogen concentration. The higher the pyrolysis temperature, the lower the nitrogen concen-
tration gets. According to [de Oliveira Paiva et al., 2024], this happens for multiple reasons.
First, the hemi-cellulose and cellulose thermally degrade when reaching temperatures above
150 °C. This thermal degradation process becomes stronger when reaching 250-500 °C. This
then leads to higher volatilization of the nitrogen compounds.
When the pyrolysis conditions become more extreme, nitrogen losses increase, mainly
as gases, which significantly reduces the nitrogen content in the biochar. The availability
of nitrogen from the biochar is relatively poor.
It has been observed that, due to the
adsorbing characteristics of the biochar, the mineralized nitrogen from straw compounds
could adsorb to the carbon of the biochar. This immobilizes the nitrogen from the soil and
has negative effects on crop yield. However, it has been stated that these effects are only
visible in the short term and that nitrogen availability returns to normal levels after some
time. [Bruun et al., 2011]
Important to keep into consideration that a large fraction of the nitrogen compounds
are extracted during the dewatering of the biogas digestate before the pyrolysis process even
begins. In Table 2, it can be seen that the majority of the nitrogen compounds are removed
as part of the liquid fraction. It is, therefore, recommended to use the liquid fraction as a
fertilizer supplement to the solid (biochar) fertilizer to provide enough nitrogen for the soil.

This is
logical since manure itself is richer in phosphorous compared to plant feedstocks. According

to literature, the application of phosphorous through biochar brings multiple advantages,
namely the lower leaching losses of phosphorous due to the adsorbing abilities of biochar,
slower release characteristics, and the enhancement of endogenic phosphorous availability
in the soil.
These lower leaching losses prevent the wash-out of phosphorous and make
more phosphorous available in the long term. Also, biochar improves the nutrient release
characteristics of phosphorous, which benefits the availability of phosphorous in the soil by
influencing P-related complexation and metabolic effects. [Yang et al., 2021]
It has been discovered that the thermal processing of phosphorous-containing biomasses
affects its speciation. There have been experiments looking into this speciation and the
effect on (bio-)availability of the phosphorous from the biochar, which was produced from pig
manure under different pyrolysis temperatures. This research has shown that with increasing
pyrolysis temperature, less water soluble phosphorous was present in the biochar whilst the
relative concentration of slow release phosphorous increased. The relative concentration of
water soluble phosphorous (H2O −P and NaHCO3 −P) in biochar pyrolysed at 400 °C was
1.74% and 13.34% respectively. Whilst the relative concentration in the sample pyrolysed
at 600 °C equaled to 0.45% and 9.17% respectively.
However the relative concentration
of phosphorous-containing minerals (HCl −P) and stable organic phosphorous (Res −P)
increases with increasing pyrolysis temperature, 72.99% and 5.50% respectively at 400 °C
and 75.53% and 13.16% respectively at 600 °C. [Jiang et al., 2024]
Research has also looked into the effects of this difference in speciation on plant growth
and bio-availability (see Figure 8). This was done through the hydroponic application of
the biochar to a species of water spinach. The result of this experiment was that the plants
exposed to calcium phosphate performed better compared to the control group, which was
exposed to ordinary fertilizer. This analysis was based on measurements of plant roots, leaf
area, stem height, and fresh weight. [Jiang et al., 2024]

2.4
Application strategy
The Danish government has implemented legal application limits of nutrients to the
Danish soil to prevent over-fertilizing the soils and to prevent eutrophication. This limit
has been set to an average value of 30 kg-phosphorous/ha/y over three years. This limit is
only applicable for organic fertilizers which consist of <75% manure, biochar falls into this
category. [Amery and Schoumans, 2014]
This is important to keep in mind since this will most likely be the limiting factor when
substituting artificial fertilizer with biochar. Especially when high concentration phospho-
rous feedstocks are being used, like manure or digestate made from manure. However, when
the nutrient content of the biochar is compared to an ordinary artificial fertilizer (N:20%
P:3% K:10%), it can be concluded that the other nutrients fall short [Elsgaard et al., 2022].
In other words, the biochar can only be applied to the application ceiling determined by the
phosphorous concentration. The nitrogen and potassium content is not high enough to sup-
ply the soil with enough nitrogen and potassium. It will, therefore, be necessary to supply
the soil with additional fertilizer containing nitrogen and potassium to reach the optimal
concentration in the soil.
Since the concentration of phosphorous in the biochar is known as well as the application
limit, the next logical step is to find out how much farmland can be fertilized using the
biochar. Without going into detail on creating a mass balance, literature can be used to
determine the yield for the biochar production. For example, the biochar yield has been
found for a pyrolysis process looking into the biochar formation of manure digestate. In
this experiment, the pyrolysis temperature was varied, and it showed that the pyrolysis
temperature has a significant influence on the biochar yield. A pyrolysis temperature of 300
°C led to a yield of 55%, whereas a higher temperature of 1050 °C led to a yield of 38%.
[Bruun et al., 2017]

2.5.1
Water retention
Besides the excellent nutrient contents of (manure-based) biochars, biochar has effects
on the quality of the soil. For example, one of the benefits of the application of biochar on
soils is the increase in water retention. This is due to the high porosity of the biochar, which
enables it to hold water over a longer period. However, it has been found that the magnitude
of the effects is highly dependent on the type of soil on which the biochar is applied. So, it
has been found that sandy soils benefit the most from the biochar with an increase of 56.2%
of plant available water in the soil. Loamy and clayey soils profit less from the biochar with
an increase of 19.1% and 23.3%, respectively. However, it is presumed that the quality of the
biochar also affects water retention capabilities. For example, different pyrolysis methods
lead to varying porosities, which is speculated to partially determine the water retention
capabilities of the biochar. [Elsgaard et al., 2022]

2.5.2
Improving Soil pH
Soil pH is another metric to determine soil quality. Acidic soils have the effect that the
plants are not capable of absorbing nutrients at the same efficiency. [Elsgaard et al., 2022]
When biomasses are heated to high temperatures, above 400 °C, carbonates, carbonyl, and
functional oxygen group containing molecules are formed in the presence of alkali metals.
[Watanabe et al., 2014]. These compounds, in combination with acidic soils, have a liming
effect.
This means that the pH of the soil is increased by neutralizing the acids in the
soil. So, it has been found that the application of biochar on acidic brown soil led to an
increase in soil pH of 8,48% to 79,25%. [Dai et al., 2017] [Geng et al., 2022] However, the
increase of pH has also been seen to decrease the productivity of the plants. At higher pH
levels, the cation exchange capabilities of the soil increase, which affects the availability of
nutrients like phosphates, iron, boron, and zinc. It has been observed that a higher pH
lowers the availability of these nutrients. It has also been seen to increase the nitrification
of microbes, which limits the availability of ammonia in the soil. [Kocsis et al., 2022] It is
therefore recommended to apply the biochar to a certain extent to prevent the soil from
reaching very high pH values.
2.5.3
Carbon Sequestration
Next to the benefits to the soil quality, biochar application also has the added benefit
of storing carbon. Since the plants that make up the biomass for the pyrolysis reactor take
up carbon dioxide from the air during the lifespan of the plant, the biochar that will be
applied to the field will act as a carbon sink. The produced biochar consists of very stable
carbon, which means it will not break down and emit carbon back into the atmosphere.
[Li and Tasnady, 2023] The quality and thus the stability of the carbon is very important
for the long-term emissions from the biochar. Pyrolysis at lower temperatures results in
a less stable product, which will be more prone to degradation and will thus emit more
carbon in a shorter period. To optimize the process for carbon sequestration, the focus
should be on producing a stable biochar, which is done ideally at temperatures higher than
500 °C. [Joseph et al., 2021] The Danish government is also seeing the potential of biochar
and is investing in biochar with a planned e1.35 billion investment. This will make new
biochar-producing plants more attractive financially. [Cavallito M, 2014]

The research consists of a combination of a short literature review, a modeling part using
Aspen Plus, and a techno-economic analysis. The information gathered from the separated
parts will be combined to form a conclusion to find out if an economically viable plant can
be modeled that is also able to provide the farmers with usable biochar for their farmland.

Figure 9 shows schematically what the supply chain of nutrients and energy would look
like. As of today, Biocirc recycles the nutrients to the farmers by taking their agricultural
waste, like manure and straw, and returning the digestate to them to use as fertilizer. This
way, the company can extract some of the energy in the waste in the form of biogas. How-

the farmers need to be compensated in some way. Since the company was getting the agri-

solution is to give the farmers a product of comparable value as the digestate. Biochar is
a promising candidate for this solution. The idea is that the non-volatile nutrients are pre-
served in the biochar and will be returned to the farmers. The volatile nutrients, like the
nitrogen compounds, will be separated during the dewatering process. With the use of this
altered supply chain, the farmers will be left with a nutrient-rich fertilizer like the digestate
they used to get, and Biocirc will be able to turn the leftover carbon in the digestate into
green fuels to combat climate change.

3.1.2
Biomass Drying
In order to prepare the biomass for thermal processing, it is considered more efficient
to first dry the biomass. Therefore, it has been chosen to include a drying step before the
actual processing. The method of drying depends on the throughput and heat requirements
of the plant. Two methods were considered in the development of the plant, namely steam
drying and hot air drying. Steam drying yields often higher thermal efficiency at a higher
capital cost. Hot air dryers come with lower capital costs but use relatively more energy in
the drying process. Since the throughput of the biomass is known, at 28.6 MW, it is on the
smaller side compared to other biomass plants. It has, therefore, been decided to use the
hot air dryer instead to keep the capital costs low.

3.1.3
Low Temperature Circulating Fluidized Bed Reactor
The most essential part of the model is the Low Temperature Circulating Fluidized Bed
Reactor (LT-CFB). Since the reactions inside the LT-CFB are quite complex, the choice
was made to use thermal equilibrium reactions. In other words, that gas composition was
determined based on the elemental composition of the biomass and chemical equilibria.
[Butera et al., 2020a][Butera et al., 2020b] In order to make sure the reactions do not turn
exothermic nor endothermic, additional oxygen is added to the reactor. This leads to oxida-
tion of the biomass, which in its turn leads to the release of thermal energy. The model is
set up so that the net heat output of the reactor is equal to 0. This is done to ensure that
there is no external heat required to run the reactor.
As described in section subsection 1.2, the LT-CFB consists of a gasification chamber
and a pyrolysis chamber. The pyrolysis reactions are difficult to model accurately. One
possible approach would be to create a mass balance based on the biomass input, biochar
output, and gasification gas output. However, it is essential to know the composition of the
biochar since the composition of the gas is not known. The logical approach would be to use
literature values or experimental data. It is of great importance that the elemental compo-
sition of the biochar and biomass input are from the same study since one is the product of
the other. This turned out to be an issue since there was no literature data available at the
time that fit this requirement, therefore, another method had to be considered.

3.1.4
Partial Oxidation
Even though there is no tar present in the model, it is important to stay close to a
realistic instance for this plant. It is, therefore, necessary to include a partial oxidation
reactor in addition to the LT-CFB reactor. Here, even more oxygen is added to the mixture
to oxidize the tar to gaseous products. Additionally, the methane is oxidized in this step.
The temperature in the reactor is assumed to be 1200 °C. [Ji and Wang, 2021]. Oxygen is
added to the reaction based on the heat demand of the reactor. The reactor is assumed to
be adiabatic, thus, no heat input nor heat output is considered. To heat the reactor to the
operating temperature, some of the chemical energy from the gas is released as heat energy
by oxidizing it. This does mean, however, that some chemical energy is lost in this step.

3.1.6
Hydrogen Electrolysis
In order to improve the methanol yield of the plant, extra hydrogen can be added to the
syngas mixture since it is the limiting factor. There are many methods available to produce
hydrogen. However, since the main goal of the project is to create a clean product, all fossil-
based methods are not considered. Hydrogen electrolysis was the chosen option to work
with. Even though this is a costly way of producing hydrogen gas, it is also very flexible and
can run completely on renewable electricity to produce a net-green product. Even within the
realm of electrolysis, there are a multitude of technologies available, all with their advantages
and disadvantages. Alkaline electrolysis (AEC) is a proven technology that is relatively low
cost compared to other technologies like proton exchange membrane electrolysis and solid
oxide electrolysis. Its downside is the relatively low efficiency. Since it was estimated that
the plant would not be very large, the choice was made to use the AEC. It was not beneficial
to use a newer technology as it might increase the capital costs too much with only limited
gains. The hydrogen input was calculated based on the gasification gas composition and the
equations for the transformation of carbon dioxide and carbon monoxide to methanol, seen
in Equation 2 and Equation 3. This way, the produced hydrogen is just enough to convert
the gas into methanol without producing excess hydrogen gas.

Economic Analysis
In this section, the methods and assumptions for the economic analysis will be explained.
The economic analysis is an import part of the development of a plant. It shows the feasibility
of the plant when money plays an important role.
3.2.1
Approach
Plenty of information concerning the data for the plants can be found in a data bundle
provided by the Danish government. [Danish Energy Agency, 2017] The bundle consists of
many technologies in the realm of green fuels. Every chapter is a single, compact study look-
ing at the technology and determining the expected economic results of this technology in the
current time and the future. These technologies also include the thermal gasifier, methanol
synthesis plant, and the electrolyzer. However, the catalog describes them separately, not as
a whole plant.

It has, therefore, been decided to use a ’block’ approach. All the components of the
plant, seen in subsection 3.1, can be summarized in 4 blocks. Namely, the electrolyzer, the
hot air dryer, the thermal gasifier, and the methanol plant. These blocks correspond to
technologies mentioned in the catalog.
For the thermal gasifier, an alternative approach was used to better estimate the costs
of the reactor. Since the gasifier from the catalog is a different type of reactor, namely an
updraft fixed bed gasifier, it seemed unrealistic to use the same economic values for this

3.3
Nutrient Balance
To connect the nutrient analysis conducted in the literature review to the development
of the plant in the modeling section, a nutrient balance was created. This was done using
nutrient composition data provided by BioCirc. With the provided information, it would be
possible to determine the nutrient composition in key components in the system as well as
the concentration of nutrients in the biochar.
After obtaining this data, it would be possible to determine the area of farmland that can
be fertilized using the produced biochar. In [Amery and Schoumans, 2014], it is described
that, in Denmark, organic fertilizers have to abide by a phosphorous application limit to
prevent eutrophication. With the use of this information, it is possible to determine how
many hectares of land can be fertilized using biochar.
Since the nitrogen and potassium concentrations are also provided, it would be possible
to also calculate the amount of nitrogen and potassium applied to the field and determine if
this amount is sufficient, too little, or too much.

5.1
Suggestions For Future Research
Screw press
Initially there was no information available for the screw press in terms of the nutrient
concentrations and separation efficiencies from BioCirc. This led to forced but inaccurate
assumptions, for example, the mass balance of the screw press is of crucial importance and
has a large influence on the effectiveness of the plant. Initially it was assumed that only
purpose for the screw press is the dewatering of the digestate. So in the model development,
that was the only function of the component. However, later during the project it was clear
that the separation efficiency of the screw press is relatively lackluster. Experimental data
from BioCirc as well as literature data [Drosg et al., 2015] showed that roughly 50% of the
solids end up in the liquid digestate. Since neither of the sources indicates what the exact
carbon losses are, it is fair to assume that the carbon separation efficiency is roughly equal to
the total solid separation efficiency. In this case, more than half of the carbon is already lost
in the dewatering step of the process. section 4 describes that the carbon efficiency of the
plant is equal to 75-85% depending on the plant parameters. If the screw press losses would
be taken into account the carbon efficiency would be further lowered by about 50%. This
has consequences for the entire plant and the analysis. Since the feedstock flow of the plant
is halved, the sizing of the components is not accurate anymore and needs to be adjusted for
the new feedstock mass flow. Besides the remodeling of the plan, the consequences stretch
further to the economic analysis as well. Since the components need to be down scaled,
the prices of the components are altered as well. Most of the component’s attributes from
[Danish Energy Agency, 2017] are scaled using a scaling factor of 0.66. With the use of
this scaling factor, the price of components becomes more attractive when the scale of the
plant increases. However, it works the opposite way when the plant is down scaled. This
means that by downscaling the plant, it might not be an attractive business case anymore
because the CapEx and OpEx outweigh the revenue from the methanol sales. It is therefor
recommended that, in the case this report is used to develop a business case, that the results
need to be adjusted for the carbon loss in the screw press.
Low Temperature Circulating Fluidized Bed Reactor
In section 3, it was described that the modeling for the LT CFB had to be simplified due
to the lack of information available for the biomass conversion to biochar. Ideally, one would
model this conversion in order to simulate a realistic composition of the gasification gas and
biochar. It is therefore recommended that in the future, when more literature information
is available in terms of biomass and biochar characterization, a more complete model should
be developed. Since biochar as a topic is gaining more popularity, this kind of development
will likely continue in the future.
[202, 2024] Apart from the straightforward conversion
of biomass to biochar and gas, ideally, one would also include the tar as a component of
the products. In Figure 2, it is seen that the tar intermediate is an important step in the

Besides tar, there are many other compounds of interest in the biomass that were not

harmful substances like antibiotics and heavy metals. These can pose a serious danger when
they end up in air or waterways. Most pharmaceuticals are broken down at high temper-
atures; heavy metals, however, cannot be broken down. [Mgharbel et al., 2023]. However,
it is still important to measure the biochar for residues of both pharmaceutical residue and
heavy metals. In the process of pyrolysis and gasification, the total biomass in the biochar
fraction is smaller than at the start since most of the compounds are extracted in the gas.
This means that the relative concentration of these harmful substances is higher than in the
feed biomass.

Next to dangerous substances in the soil, there is also the risk of emissions of harmful
gasses, mainly ammonia.
When the digestate is dried, the volatile nitrogen compounds
will end up in the vapor phase. This vapor phase often ends up as exhaust gas, and the
ammonia will thus end up in the surroundings of the plant. Ammonia can be irritating or
even harmful in higher concentrations [Berg et al., 2021]. A possible solution could be to
introduce a closed loop hot air dryer, see Figure 29.

A closed loop air dryer would have the benefit that the harmful gasses would either

Methanol Synthesis
During the development of the simulation for the methanol synthesis part of the plant,
some difficulties were encountered in the software Aspen Plus. Since thermodynamic equi-
libria were used to determine the reaction speed and balances, not all of the reactants would
react immediately. This resulted in a large quantity of the reactants ending up in the prod-
uct stream. It was, therefore, decided to include a recirculation stream as can be seen in
Figure 10, to recycle most of the reactants to prevent waste. However, this resulted in one
of the reactants building up to very high levels and breaking the simulation. So, some of
the reactant had to be vented through a purge gas valve. To keep the simulation running,
the recirculation flow had to be limited to a modest 95%. This means that 5% of the mass
flow would be vented to the purge valve. Ideally, one would use a higher recirculation flow
to improve the efficiency of the plant. However, this was not possible due to the restrictions
of the software. In reality, it might be possible to achieve higher recirculation ratios, which
will improve the general efficiency of the plant and, most importantly, the carbon efficiency.
Heat Management
From this observation, it can be concluded that there is enough heat produced by the
process that the heat demand from other components can be covered without any additional
heat input. It would even be possible to move the heat demand line to the left as long as
the line remains under the heat supply. This opens the possibility that the excess heat is
now of higher temperature, which increases the value of the excess heat since it can be used
for industrial applications [Danish District Heating Association, ].
In Figure 19, it can be seen that with the use of a biomass that is higher in moisture
content, the heat demand of the plant is naturally higher. This can be explained by the
additional heat required for drying the biomass due to its higher moisture content. Important
to note is that, once again, the heat demand line does not extend past the heat supply line.
This means that this instance of the plant does not require any external energy input.
However, it must be said that this limits the potential of using excess heat for additional
income streams.
In Figure 16, it is seen that some of the heat that is recovered is integrated back into
the system to provide heat to the hot air dryer. However, the hot air dryer has exhaust gas
itself, and some of the heat in the gas might be able to be recovered as well. The LT CFB
and POX reactors do not have any waste heat streams, but they do suffer some chemical
energy losses. To reach the operating temperatures for the reactors, some of the gasses are
combusted to release energy to heat the reactor.

Heat Recovery
The results indicate that the use of both the high moisture content as well as the low
moisture content biomass produce excess heat, which presents an opportunity to explore its
applications. Typically, district heating or industrial heat are high on the list for utilizing
this excess heat. However, since the biomass input is digestate, the plant may be built near
a biogas facility. As explained in section 1, anaerobic fermentation requires external heating
to run efficiently, especially if the feedstock is not up to temperature.
One potential application is to use the excess heat to supply the biogas plant with the
necessary heat. BioCirc has provided the heat demand for the biogas plant, specifically 2.52
MW heat input. This value has been incorporated into the model using a heat exchanger,
which ensures that only excess heat at sufficiently high temperatures is utilized. This ap-
proach not only meets the heat demand efficiently but also enhances the overall energy
management of the plant. In Figure 20, it can be seen that there is enough heat available to
satisfy both the new external heat demand and the internal heat requirement of the system.
This approach might be more beneficial in case the plant is constructed close to the biogas
installation since there will be little heat loss associated since the distance will be marginal
in comparison to a district heating pipeline towards a city or town.
However, it is important to note that while this setup can improve efficiency, it may not
completely satisfy the heat demand under all conditions. Further research and optimization
are needed to fully realize the potential of this approach.
Figure 21 shows that there is insufficient heat available to make the plant self-sufficient
in terms of heat. This can be explained since there is already less heat available due to the
increased requirements for heat for the dryer, and there is insufficient heat available for the
digester. Aspen was able to determine the exact lack of heat and concluded it needed 0.9043
MW to match the required heat. It can still be decided to partially heat the digester using
this waste heat. This heat will just not be able to completely satisfy the demand.

Techno-Economic Analysis
Figure 27 shows that a large fraction of the levelized cost of fuel is due to the high
electricity consumption. This can be explained by the demand for energy-intensive green
hydrogen, which is used in the production of green methanol. Since the energy consump-
tion of the electrolyzer is always bound to the formation energy of hydrogen, 120 MJ/kg,
the process will always be energy intensive. [Hea, ] Since it was chosen to use an alkaline
electrolyzer (AEC), a mature and proven technology, for the plant, the efficiency of the hy-
drogen formation process is not optimal. [Jin et al., 2023] Especially when the technology
is compared to newer forms of electrolyzers like the solid oxide electrolyzers (SOEC), the
efficiency is relatively lackluster. The SOEC can achieve an electrical efficiency of up to
85% [Wang et al., 2021]; this would be able to cut the electricity by a noticeable margin.
However, since the AEC technology is relatively cheap in capital expenditure, does not rely
on precious metals, and has proven to be reliable, it has been deemed a good fit for the
plant. However, this does not exclude the other options from being taken into considera-
tion. Since the SOEC is still in development, it might be a more attractive option in the

consider whenever the SOEC is investigated is that to achieve the relatively high efficiency
of the SOEC, it needs to be supplied with ample heat. This heat needs to be at a very high
temperature +700 °C. In most cases, the high efficiency can only be achieved when the heat
for the SOEC is supplied by process heat in the downstream processes. This means that the
waste heat from the plant might be exclusively necessary to feed the SOEC, or the plant
might not make enough waste heat at all. In this case, it might not be an attractive solution

In order to validate the results of the economic analysis, it is important to compare
the key result, the price of the fuel, to the literature. The values for green methanol vary
wildly in literature.
This is because there are many terms to describe green methanol,
depending on the process that is behind the production of the fuel. E-methanol, for example,
is often referred to as methanol made with the (indirect) use of electricity and most often
non-biogenic carbon, so carbon obtained through carbon capture and storage, for example.
Bio-methanol is methanol that is produced with the use of biogenic carbon and sometimes
through biological means instead of catalytic processes.
The methanol produced in the proposed plant is something in between or both. Since
the methanol in the plant is produced with the use of digestate, which falls in the category
of biogenic carbon, it can be referred to as bio-methanol. However, since there is also an
electrolyzer involved in the production of the methanol, it can technically also be referred to
as e-methanol since some of the energy contained in the fuel originates from electricity that
was fed to the electrolyzer.
Since both e-methanol and bio-methanol are being sold at different prices, it is difficult
to estimate a price for comparison.

Nutrient Balance
subsection 3.3 indicated that the assumption had to be made that there is no nitrogen
left in the biochar. This assumption was made to make the nutrient balance simpler, even
though the result might not be completely accurate. The choice can be defended by the
fact that most of the nitrogen compounds end up in the liquid digestate; thus, the nitrogen
content of the biochar is of less importance in the grand scheme, see Table 16. However,
to create the most accurate result possible, one should model the nitrogen content of the
biochar as well. This can be done using a more complex simulation that incorporates the
nutrients as well or a more thorough literature research.
Important to note is that in the literature, it is also found that there is a nitrogen
application limit for the Danish soil, 140-170 kg N/ha/y. [Amery and Schoumans, 2014].
However, the nitrogen limit is high enough that the ceiling will not be reached by applying
biochar and liquid digestate.
In ?? was mentioned that the information concerning the nutrient composition provided
by Biocirc was not complete. The assumption had to be made that the screw press respects
the mass balance and thus the sum of the liquid and solid output would be equal to the input.
This is a fine assumption to make if the information is not available. However, it would be
more accurate to use the nutrient composition of all steams. So it is recommended to redo
the nutrient balance whenever the nutrient compositions of all the streams concerning the
screw press are available.
Also, this nutrient balance has now been calculated separately from the Aspen model.
This resulted in differences in mass flows and compositions between the two mass balances.
This is mostly due to one important assumption that was, in hindsight, an inappropriate
assumption to make. Namely, the lack of solid losses in the screw press in the Aspen model.
In the aspen model the screw press is only responsible for the de-watering of the digestate.
However, in reality the solid separation efficiency of a screw press is relatively poor when
compared to alternatives like a decanter centrifuge. [Cathcart et al., 2023]
Ideally, one would model the nutrients together with the mass and energy balance in one
go. This way, the mass balances will make more sense since the presence of other compounds,
like nutrients, might influence the reactions, separation efficiencies, and energy requirements
in the Aspen model.
One method to improve the nutrient retention of the dewatering step is to replace the
screw press with another method of dewatering.
Literature shows that a screw press is
a cost effective method to dewater biomasses cheaply. However, when the separation of
nutrients and total solids becomes more important, a decanter centrifuge becomes a more
attractive alternative. [Cathcart et al., 2023] Literature shows that the separation efficiency
for phosphorous is noticeably higher when a decanter centrifuge is used over a screw press.
55
==PAGE:60==

The same study shows that the separation efficiency for total solids and carbon is also
noticeably higher when a decanter centrifuge is installed. The carbon separation efficiency
for example can be increased from 30% to 75% when the screw press is replaced by a decanter
centrifuge. However, the improved separation efficiency comes at the cost of relatively lower
concentrations of nutrients and solids in the solid output. This is the case due to the worse
dewatering ability of the decanter centrifuge versus the screw press. The paper however
still recommends the screw press for fuel production due to the lower level of nitrogen in
the solid output. It is expected that the inert nitrogen will cause unnecessary up-scaling of
components due to the larger mass flow without benefit.
Another research also favors the decanter centrifuge over the screw press in terms of
nutrient and carbon separation.[Cathcart et al., 2021].
It is, therefore, recommended to
include a thorough examination of the different dewatering methods to ensure the optimal
retention of both carbon and nutrients.

6
Conclusion
This study investigated the sustainable processing of biogas digestate through pyrolysis
and gasification, emphasizing nutrient retention in biochar. The research aimed to develop a
techno-economically viable plant model capable of producing biofuels and generating biochar
as a fertilizer substitute for Danish agriculture. The findings indicate that gasification using
Low Temperature Circulating Fluidized Bed (LT CFB) technology effectively converts bio-
gas digestate into syngas, biochar, and other valuable by-products. The biochar produced
retains essential nutrients such as nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium, making it a poten-
tial alternative to conventional fertilizers. However, nutrient retention varies depending on
processing conditions, necessitating a hybrid approach that combines biochar application
with liquid digestate to meet crop nutrient requirements.
The economic analysis suggests that the proposed plant can be financially viable, with
a projected return on investment of 8.2 years under baseline conditions. The levelized cost
of methanol production is estimated to range between 86.4 and 89.6 EUR/MWhMeOH, in-
fluenced by the efficiency of biomass drying and hydrogen production costs.
While the
technology presents promising opportunities for biofuel and fertilizer production, economic
feasibility remains sensitive to energy prices and policy incentives.
Future research should focus on optimizing process conditions, addressing potential
environmental concerns such as emissions and tar formation, and refining biochar quality
to enhance its agricultural applicability. By integrating biofuel production with nutrient
recovery, this research supports the development of a circular bio-economy, contributing
to both renewable energy generation and sustainable agricultural practices. This approach
not only aids in reducing greenhouse gas emissions but also promotes the efficient use of
resources, aligning with broader sustainability goals.
