II 
Abstract 
Since the transport sector accounts for one of the highest shares of greenhouse gases (GHG) 
emissions, several existing proposals state the idea to control the by the transportation sector 
caused GHG emissions through an Emission Trading Systems (ETS). However, most existing 
approaches integrate GHG emissions through the fuel consumption and car registration. Such 
design entails that the GHG emissions are not assigned directly to individual’s trips, limiting 
the tracing of emissions in shared vehicles, shared rides and even public transportation that 
ultimately contribute to the sector’s total emissions. Blockchain has the potential to establish 
cost-effective, decentralized, privacy-aware, and cybersecure ETS implementation with an 
increase in security and transparency. This paper presents a new design of a user-centric ETS 
and its implementation as a carbon Blockchain framework for Smart Mobility Data-market 
(cBSMD). The cBSMD allows for the seamless transactions of token-equivalent GHG 
emissions when realizing a trip, or an emission trading action as well as the transaction of 
individual, service or system-wide emission performance data. Furthermore, an instance of the 
cBSMD implementation for the transactions of an ETS where all travellers receive a certain 
amount of carbon tokens, which are linked to the GHG price and a total emission cap. 
Travellers use their carbon tokens each time they emit GHG when travelling in a multi-modal 
network, purchase carbon tokens for a given trip when they have an insufficient token amount 
or sell when having a surplus of carbon tokens due to a lower amount of emitted GHG. This 
part of the cBSMD is then applied to a case-study of 24 hours of mobility of 3,186 travellers 
from Oakville, Ontario, Canada, in which the GHG emission trading system is showcased and 
the token usage as well as the emission performance analyzed.

1. Introduction  
 
The following section introduces the reader about the importance of the field of research, the 
purpose of the study and the methodology of the following study. 
 
1.1 Problem Statement  
 
Greenhouse gases (GHG) are necessary for sustaining life on Earth as they reflect most of the 
infrared radiation from the atmosphere back to the Earth. Without these gases the Earth’s 
surface temperature would be around -18°C instead of the current average temperature of 15°C 
[Jain, 1993]. However, the increasing global population and the consequently higher demand 
in nourishment, energy and transportation possibilities has resulted in a greater GHG emission 
and therefore in a stronger reflection of infrared radiation [Masson-Delmotte et al., 2018].  
Many governments have implemented Emission Trading Systems (ETS) in order to meet the 
Paris Agreement and Kyoto Protocol which strive for a constant reduction of the global GHG 
emissions. ETS promise a direct GHG quantity control mechanism where reduced emission 
allowances are allocated to emitting as well as possibly non-emitting stakeholders and traded 
through different possible schemes [European Commission (a)]. Therefore, the Kyoto Protocol 
has a strong potential to affect the carbon footprint of individuals, but it forces reductions in 
the production process rather than the consumption process [Pan et al., 2019]. The causality 
between number of cars and GHG emissions has a main impact on the global warming. During 
the last decades the number of motor-driven vehicles has continuously increased and more than 
1 billion vehicles are on the global streets nowadays [Davis and Boundy, 2019]. The result is 
that the transport sector accounts in many countries one of the highest shares of GHG 
emissions, for example, with 28.9% [United States Environmental Protection Agency, 2017] 
and 24.3% [EEA, 2016] in the US and the EU respectively. While some countries have tried 
to include maritime and air transport in its proposed ETS, the GHG share by the road sector is 
tremendous compared to these sectors (Figure A1) [IPCC, 2017]. Furthermore, in urban areas 
the pollution by the road sector is the highest due to its concentrated population (75% of all 
people in the industrialized and 40% in the developing countries) and the traffic situation 
propels people to a non-efficient driving behavior, which leads to higher GHG emissions 
[IPCC, 2017].  
Thus, some countries and cities have started to announce actions against fossil fuel vehicles in 
order to reduce and avoid GHG emissions from the road transportation sector [World Bank, 
2010]. Those actions, for instance, sanctions against older vehicles or the implementation of a 
==PAGE:9==
 
- 2 - 
carbon tax, whereby users have to pay an additional share per liter gasoline [Department of 
Finance Canada, 2018] are supporting the declarations aiming at fighting the global warming, 
as the Kyoto Protocol and the Paris Agreement.  
Exemplary to mention is Costa Rica and Norway, countries which aim to ban new sales of 
diesel and gasoline vehicles by 2021 and 2025, respectively [Spaen, 2019; Staufenberg, 2016]. 
Furthermore, particular cities, for instance, London, Los Angeles, Seattle, Vancouver want to 
meet national and international climate declarations such as the Kyoto Protocol and the Paris 
Agreement to decelerate the climate change as well as enhance the air quality in urban areas 
[Bendix, 2018]. As a consequence, some cities have taken already action and have been banned 
older cars from the city center. In Paris, for example, it is prohibited to enter the city center 
with a car manufactured before 1997 on weekdays [Bendix, 2018]. 
 
Nevertheless, not only petroleum-based engines are sources of GHG in the air, e.g. through 
weather-related circumstances like wind, the GHG amount in a city could be higher when an 
airport or harbor is around [McArthur and Osland, 2013]. Therefore, in many cities, the data 
on vehicular emissions used in pollution management is still inaccurate and often limited as it 
generally relies on fixed measuring stations which is incapable of detecting the direct 
source/emitter. Furthermore, other factors may substantially affect an efficient emissions 
management. Since the Volkswagen diesel emissions scandal, for example, it is known that the 
data from the test bench - and the resulting pollution badge - does not reflect the real emitted 
GHG emissions in daily life [Oldenkamp et al., 2016]. The emissions vary tremendously from 
driver behavior, the context of the trip, cold or warm start, and the condition of the vehicle’s 
parts as well as the condition of the road [Kam et al., 2012; Qian and Chung, 2011]. 
Consequently, there is currently no adequate program to monitor and control accurate emission 
data from the road transportation sector.  
However, existing proposals of user-based ETS approach a way to control the individual’s 
carbon footprint and use therefore the fuel consumption as a crucial factor [Li et al., 2019; 
Dobes, 1998]. This entails that the GHG emissions are not assigned directly to individual’s 
trips, limiting the tracing of emissions in shared vehicles, shared rides and even public 
transportation that ultimately (and increasingly) contribute to the sector’s total emissions. 
Moreover, existing proposals are not implying the time factor, thus the amount of GHG 
emissions cannot be assigned to individual trips.

1.2 Purpose of the Study 
 
The purpose of the study is to design and develop a user-centric emission trading and 
monitoring system in order to assign the individual with a personal GHG footprint instead of 
the control of emissions on a fuel and vehicle-based basis (3. Carbon Blockchain Framework 
for Smart Mobility Data-market). Hereby, the multi-modal aspect has to be highlighted to 
ensure a proper GHG emission monitoring as well as trading scheme. Thus, the proposed 
system takes into account the type of mode, the number of passengers as well as trip related 
data which affect the amount of GHG emissions. Consequently, the proposed system is 
designed to incentive individuals to reduce their personal GHG footprint. To ensure an 
applicable trading system, the blockchain technology is used to develop a cost-effective, 
decentralized, privacy-aware, and cybersecure ETS which increases security and transparency 
of transactions compared to existing systems.  
 
Consequently, the research question of the paper is the following:  
How can the blockchain technology help to monitor GHG emission records in vehicles, assign 
them to individual users and use them to reduce emitted GHG in a long-term perspective?  
 
1.3 Methodology  
 
At first, the theory of ETS and the blockchain technology are described to provide the reader 
the necessary background. Secondly, examples of blockchain implementations in the 
environmental sector as well as the mobility sector are shown to highlight the state-of-the-art.

type of users. Finally, the conclusion and future work section completes the study.

2. Background  
 
The background chapter describes the theory of ETS and blockchain to provide the necessary 
fundamentals for the later chapters.  
 
2.1 Emission Trading Systems 
 
The Kyoto Protocol was adopted with the target to extend the United Nations Framework 
Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) in 1997 in order to commit the state parties to 
actively reduce the GHG emissions during 2008 to 2012 [Watanabe and Robinson, 2005]. The 
protocol describes several mechanisms to propose member states actions to meet the 
determined GHG emission reduction, Joint Implementation (JI), Clean Development 
Mechanism (CDM) and an Emission Trading System (ETS) [United Nations Climate Change, 
2019]. Whereby, the ETS, as a market-based approach, is considered as the most effective and 
efficient political strategy to reduce GHG emissions [Daskalakis, 2013; Montagnoli and de 
Vries, 2010].  
In general, there are two types of trading systems, cap and trade and baseline and credit. A 
cap and trade system creates a supply and demand principle. Thus, quantities of future GHG 
emissions are distributed across individuals and each participant in the system receives a 
certain amount of emission allowances. These allowances can be traded as far as a user changes 
his environmentalism and produces less GHG emissions than his allowances permit. The 
baseline and credit mechanism contains no determined limit on emissions. However, the 
participants can receive rewards in form of credits (offsets) when they reduce their GHG 
emissions below a baseline level (usually based on historical data), which they can sell to other 
participants who need them to meet regulations [Chomitz, 1999]. However, in large ETS the 
cap and trade mechanism is primarily used to decrease the cap of allowances steadily and meet 
therefore GHG emission determinations [European Commission, 2008b].  
Therefore, many regions began to establish, under the terms of the Kyoto Protocol, an ETS. 
The first multi-industry ETS was introduced in the UK in 2002 [Nye and Owens, 2008]. After 
the start of multi-sector ETS was made, the European Union Emission Trading System (EU 
ETS) was established in 2005, as the largest ETS, which contains all EU countries [European 
Commission, 2008a]. The system governed by the EU was in place with an emission cap on 
fixed installations amounting to 2,084,301,856 EU allowances (EUAs), distributed to more 
than 11,000 heavy energy-consuming organizations in 2013 [European Commission, 2008a]. 
==PAGE:12==
 
- 5 - 
This cap is set to decrease by a linear reduction of 1.74% annually (and 2.2 % from 2021 
onwards) [European Commission, 2008b]. Thus, all low emitters, who reduced their GHG 
emission by implementing new technologies or changed procedures, can trade their surplus on 
GHG EUAs to other companies which have run out of EUA for this year. The annual, linear 
reduction of allowances incentives the participants to actively reduce the GHG emissions. 
Furthermore, ETS arouse more and more attention from the transportation sector. In 
combination with the three different streams of the transportation sector - the upstream, 
midstream, and downstream [Mock et al. 2014; Iankov and Zito, 2008] - new approaches have 
been made. The upstream contains the fuel producers, who are responsible for the fuel’s 
emission factor, which affects the amount of GHG emissions due to combustion. According to 
Grayling et al. the most effective and political accepted approach would be to include the fuel 
producers (upstream) in carbon trading mechanisms as the EU ETS [Grayling et al., 2006]. The 
midstream is accountable for the vehicle fuel economy. Winkelman et al. state that the vehicle 
manufactures (midstream) should be responsible for the GHG emissions which are produced 
through the sold vehicle [Winkelman et al., 2000]. Dobes, however, describes a permit scheme 
in which the vehicle users (downstream) are responsible for the emissions due to the use of 
combustion engines [Dobes, 1998]. As a solution, every user gets permits to refuel they car 
[Dobes, 1998].  
How ETS can be benefit from the blockchain technology is highlighted in 2.3.1 Blockchain in 
the Environmental Sector. Moreover, a more detailed approach to control CO2 emissions in all 
streams by Li et al. is described in 2.3.2.2 Blockchain-based Emission Trading Systems for 
Road Transport.  
2.2 Blockchain Technology  
 
Blockchain belongs to the superior Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT), which is 
characteristic to store, distribute and facilitate the exchange of value between users without a 
central authority [Beck et al., 2017]. Nevertheless, not all DLT systems using a blockchain 
(e.g. IOTA, Hashgraph). Those systems using Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) instead of blocks 
to decrease the computing power, the related energy consumption and the resulting costs. 
However, blockchain is the most suitable DLT in terms of asset transactions between users in 
the system [Collomb and Sok, 2016].  
==PAGE:13==
 
- 6 - 
Therefore, the following study considers blockchain for trading information and assets in the 
system. This mechanism is very applicable to develop a sufficient ETS on a user-based 
perspective.  
The most common example of trading, based on blockchain, is at the same time the invention 
of blockchain. The cryptocurrency Bitcoin was invented by Satoshi Nakamoto in 2008. The 
digital currency uses the underlaying blockchain technology to decentralize the transaction 
process. Therefore, no central bank or intermediaries are needed. Furthermore, the blockchain 
is formed by linked blocks which are in a chronological order and contain transactions 
[Nakamoto, 2008]. Those linkups between the blocks are permanently and cannot be tampered 
after the transaction was made. This mechanism, in connection to the consensus algorithm 
(2.2.6 Consensus Mechanism), which allows for the distributed flow of information, secures 
the transactions [Dapp et al., 2017]. The invention of Bitcoin as a cryptocurrency opened the 
door for other digital assets. Since the release of Bitcoin in 2009, more than 2200 other 
cryptocurrencies, altcoins (alternative variants of bitcoin), have been created [CoinMarketCap, 
2019]. 
However, after the crypto-market has been settled, the utilization of blockchain for other 
sectors was explored. As a result, the features of decentralization and tamperproofing are not 
anymore solely used for financial transactions. For instance, in the supply chain domain, in 
combination with RFID technology, to verify transactions from every step in the value chain 
to maintain the agreed, for example, quality, origin and storage period. The immutable ledger, 
fed with accurate data, can be seen by everyone who participates in the trade procedure, from 
producer through to the consumer [Tian, 2016]. Further implementations of blockchain can be 
seen in 2.3.1 Blockchain in the Environmental Sector and 2.3.2 Blockchain in the Mobility 
Sector. 
 
2.2.1 System Structures  
 
This chapter creates an overview about differences in types of networks (Figure 1) to 
understand the functionality of blockchain. 
In centralized systems, components are connected to one central authority that controls 
everything in this network [Kube, 2017]. Hence, the central point in this network sets up rules 
which are relevant for every other component. This entails that the network creates a 
dependency from every part to the main authority. If the central point does not work in a proper 
manner, all involved parties will be affected [Kube, 2017].  
==PAGE:14==
 
- 7 - 
In decentralized systems, the main nodes are connected to each other, but not one single 
authority has control over it. Therefore, it is a democratized system, if one node goes down, 
the system still works [Kube, 2017]. 
The distributed system is defined by components which are connected to each other, at least 
indirectly, without having a central element. Peer-to-peer (P2P) networks are a special kind of 
distributed systems; individual computers (nodes) give their computational resources directly 
available to the rest of the peers in the system [Kube, 2017]. All nodes in the system are equal; 
therefore, all of them are suppliers and consumers of the resources in the system. The concept 
behind a P2P system is, the bigger the system the larger and more powerful the system 
becomes. It is a true form of democratic network due to the fact that no single authority controls 
it [Kube, 2017]. 
 
 
Figure 1 Centralized, Decentralized and Distributed Network, own figure referring to [Eagar, 2017] 
 
2.2.2 Level of Permission  
 
Blockchain systems differ in the type of permission. In general, there are four different levels 
of permission which are composed of the level of entry (public or private) and the level of 
participation in the consensus mechanism (2.2.6 Consensus Mechanism) (open or closed) 
[Ølnes et al., 2017].   
 
a) Public closed systems are open for anyone. The participation in the consensus mechanism 
is restricted.  
b) Public open systems are fully open. Anyone can do transactions, query the ledger and 
participate in the consensus mechanism.  
==PAGE:15==
 
- 8 - 
c) Private closed systems are in full control of the owner. Access and the participation in the 
consensus mechanism are restricted.  
d) Private open systems control who can access the system. Participants in the system have 
no restrictions.   
 
Public blockchain systems are used in particular for purposes in which the users are not known, 
for example, for digital currencies. Therefore, in public open systems, all users are free to 
interact within the system (e.g. help verify transactions, running a node, submitting 
transactions). In public closes systems, the consensus mechanism is reserved for active nodes, 
thereby it is ensured that only nodes are involved in the process to write new blocks, which 
have the computing power. However, no access restrictions are in place.  
Private blockchain systems are mostly used by centralized organizations which use the 
blockchain technology for their own reasons [Novotny et al., 2018]. As a consequence, for 
instance, consumers, customers or business partners, can be invited or must be approved to 
participate in a private system, each participant has therefore a well-known identity. In private 
system the level of decentralization can be adjusted by the owner. Moreover, a private closed 
system can avoid consensus mechanisms which entails excessive energy consumptions, as 
Proof-of-Work (2.2.6.1 Proof-of-Work) due to the strict barrier of entry. 
2.2.3 Cryptography  
 
One reason why blockchain is considered for so many purposes is the cryptography which 
encrypts and decrypts information through complex mathematic peculiarities of large-scale 
numbers, primes, calculus and randomness [Diedrich, 2016]. Consequently, the information 
which has to be transferred are encrypted for the transfer and can only be seen by the intended 
receivers who decrypt the information. This entails that all participants in a blockchain system 
can be confident that records and transfers are secure and are not accessible and alterable by 
third parties [Kosba et al., 2016]. The in blockchain systems general used public-key 
cryptography (asymmetric cryptography) contains two different keys for the user, the public 
key and private key [Kosba et al., 2016]. Therefore, a combination of the user’s private and 
public key encrypts the information. In order to decrypt the transferred information by the 
receiver, the private key of the recipient and the sender’s public key has to be used. However, 
it is not possible to indicate which private and public key are related. As a result, the public key 
can be seen by anyone without giving unsolicited participants access to the private key. 
Moreover, the addresser can transfer information to another participant in the system with the 
==PAGE:16==
 
- 9 - 
certainty that no intermediary can intercept a piece of information due to the fact that only the 
eligible receiver can access the data [Kosba et al., 2016].  
 
2.2.4 Digital Signatures 
 
The public-key cryptography contains digital signatures. These signatures ensuring the validity 
of the displayed data. Therefore, the private key of the user is combined, through out an 
algorithm, to the signed data. As a consequence, the data itself is part of the digital signature 
and cannot be tampered without being detected by other participants in the system due to the 
fact that the merest change of the data would reshape the whole signature and lead to an 
disparate signature between start and end of transaction [Kosba et al., 2016]. The algorithm, 
which is involved in the signature process, make it easy for others to see that the right signature 
is shown, but impossible to forge it without the knowledge of the private key [Kosba et al., 
2016]. As an illustration, it is hard to show the full complex graph if just few points are known 
[Diedrich, 2016]. However, digital signatures are not used to encrypt something, they just use 
the principles of encryption. [Diedrich, 2016]. 
Nevertheless, if the private key’s information is not secreting anymore, the digital signature of 
the private key's holder can be imitated [Kube, 2017]. 
 
2.2.5 Hashing Data  
 
One of the most important blockchain features is the cryptographic hash value and can be seen 
as a digital counterpart of human fingerprints. Hence, it is essential to secure blockchain 
systems. Nevertheless, in blockchain systems and in particular P2P systems can contain a 
tremendous amount of transactions which must be identified uniquely and in a fast and easy 
way [Diedrich, 2016]. As a result, these digital fingerprints are used. Therefore, the input data 
are transformed by hash functions into a number of fixed lengths which could also be filled up 
with leading zeros to provide the required length (Figure 2). 
 
 
 
Figure 2 Calculate a hash value out of an input, own figure referring to [Kube, 2017] 
 
The by Diedrich [Diedrich, 2016] described properties of cryptographic hash functions are the 
following:  
 
==PAGE:17==
 
- 10 - 
a) Providing hash values for any kind of data quickly 
With the hash function it is possible to calculate hash values out of every kind of data, 
this is done quite quick to create a stable system. 
 
b) Being deterministic 
The hash function yields identical hash values for identical input data. Thus, 
discrepancies in the hash value must come from discrepancies of the input data. 
 
c) Being pseudorandom 
The from a hash function returned hash value changes unpredictable, when the input 
data are changed minorly. Furthermore, the hash value must always be a surprise and 
should not be predictable based on the input data. 
 
d) Being one-way functions 
It is not possible to trace the input value based on the output (hash value), this is caused 
through one-way functions. To take up again the fingerprint imagery, no one can say 
anything about a person based on the fingerprint. 
 
e) Being collision resistant 
Since the chance to receive an identical hash value for different sets of data is to be 
virtually zero, the hash function is collision resistant. A hash collision, when we get 
two identical hash values of distinct data, would be the same as two identical 
fingerprints. You have no chance to distinguish between them anymore. 
 
Due to the fact that hash functions can just handle one piece of data at a given time and a 
blockchain requires one single hash value for a large collection of transaction data, there are 
several hashing functions which can be applied [Diedrich, 2016].

2.2.6 Consensus Mechanism 
 
As mentioned in 2.2 Blockchain Technology, blockchain contains no middleman. 
Consequently, the users within the network need a proof, that the transaction of data is valid. 
==PAGE:20==
 
- 13 - 
Therefore, the blockchain technology uses consensus mechanisms to verify transaction through 
other nodes in the system.  
The following chapters explain three consensus algorithms, Proof of Work (PoW), Proof of 
Stake (PoS) and practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance (pBFT). 
 
2.2.6.1 Proof-of-Work 
 
PoW is a consensus protocol which makes it possible to bring thousands of nodes to agree on 
the world state [Diedrich, 2016]. PoW is used to verify transactions and create new blocks in 
the chain. Due to a reward system, usually the collection of the transaction fee, miners (nodes 
that are involved in the consensus mechanism) compete against each other to complete 
transactions [Vukolić, 2015]. This competition can be considered as a hard-to-solve 
mathematical puzzle and requires high computational power what leads to the fact that mining 
pools bundle the computational power of individuals to solve the puzzle faster [Schrijvers et 
al., 2016]. For instance, the hash function (2.2.5 Hashing Data), in this type of puzzle the node 
has to find the input by knowing the output. Moreover, the hash of each block contains the hash 
of the previous block, a chain accrues (Figure 8). This principle is necessary to ensure the 
transactions and prevent tamped data due to the fact that the hashes are connected to each other 
and would be reshaped when one piece of data in the system changes. When a node finds the 
random number to solve the puzzle (nonce) first, the transaction is written in the block and 
considered as verified. Moreover, the winner publishes with the block the nonce, which shows 
the other nodes that the puzzle was solved from the node. In the PoW principle nodes cannot 
agree, disagree or even negotiate on the validation of the transaction [Diedrich, 2016]. 
Therefore, it is more like every node want really to identify the right choice to be with all other 
nodes the world state [Diedrich, 2016]. 
Figure 8 The structure of blocks, own figure referring to [Yuan and Wang, 2016] 
==PAGE:21==
 
- 14 - 
The heaviest or longest chain is a principle to reflect the truth of the world state [Diedrich, 
2016]. The heaviest chain is the one which includes the most blocks consequently the most 
solved puzzles, the proof of the blockchain. All other chains with fewer blocks or just with 
another transaction order than the heaviest one will be rejected. 
However, the whole mining process takes a lot of money due to electricity consumption 
[Vukolić, 2015]. Therefore, alternatives are described in the following chapters.   
 
2.2.6.2 Proof-of-Stake 
 
PoS is a consensus mechanism that uses, instead of a race for the nonce, the power of 
punishment [Diedrich, 2016]. Thus, a miner who is not following the rules loses money, his 
stake [King and Nadal, 2012]. As in PoW, the rewards are given to nodes that create blocks, 
but the responsibility to create blocks and receive rewards are dished out via round robin. A 
violation is given whenever the behavior results in non-consensus, when the nodes cannot agree 
on the next block within time. In addition, the concept claims that a node mines or validates 
block transactions in relationship to the stake of credits/tokens holds [King and Nadal, 2012]. 
Therefore, the power within the network is distributed due to inequality [BitFury, 2015]. 
 
2.2.6.3 Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance  
The pBFT is part of the consensus layer assumes that a small number of unreliable or 
potentially malicious nodes can be tolerated in order to ensure the validity of a transaction 
[Castro and Liskov, 1999]. If the stake of dissidents is no more than 1/3, the correctness of a 
block can be guaranteed [Castro and Liskov, 1999]. In comparison to this traditional idea of 
the Byzantine Fault Tolerance, the pBFT adds the feature of primary and secondary replicas. 
Thus, the secondary replicas are responsible to check the validity and computing power of the 
primaries. Whenever a primary is compromised, the secondary replica can switch to another 
primary in order to ensure the transaction. Nevertheless, the pBFT algorithm is more suitable 
in trusted environments rather than in public open systems due to the fact that transactions are 
verified by individuals and signed by known validator nodes [Castro and Liskov, 1999]. In 
order to reach consensus and create a valid transaction a certain number of signatures have to 
be collected, minimum 2/3 of the participants in the consensus process [Castro and Liskov, 
1999].

2.2.7 Smart Contracts 
 
A smart contract is a precise legal, self-enforced agreement in which no human execution or 
interpretation is implemented [Clack et al., 2016]. The included parties in the smart contract 
have to select parameters what happen when the smart contract is fulfilled. All the set promises 
in a smart contract are specified in digital form, including protocols which define the required 
performances by the parties [Szabo, 1996]. As soon as the parties of the smart contract agree 
on the conditions, they sign the smart contracts cryptographically and start executing the set 
terms. The most important part in a smart contract is the binding part, therefore the terms of 
the agreement are realized automatically, without interruption and without wiggle room. In 
addition, it is mostly really precise and easier to understand what the agreement contains, 
without any loopholes [Diedrich, 2016]. The combination between blockchain and the contract 
program creates an unstoppable environment, in which no one can obstruct the transfer of data 
or money [Diedrich, 2016]. The smart contract which is stored in a blockchain has a unique 
address. Therefore, no changes to the contract can be implemented after the signatures by the 
parties are made. As a result, the decentralized code of the smart contract moves the data or 
money as soon as the condition of the agreement is fulfilled. Hence, the smart contract is the 
witness of an agreement, the facilitator in a blockchain [Clack et al., 2016]. Whenever the 
parties’ terms in the smart contract do not match, the transaction will be dismissed, or an option 
of renegotiation is given.

The following chapters provide an overview of the state-of-the-art approaches and

2.3.1 Blockchain in the Environmental Sector 
The blockchain technology makes also in the environmental sector a great stir. Therefore, 
several proposals emphasize the need of blockchain in the environmental sector, in particular 
the energy industry and ETS. 
Liss points out that the EU ETS (2.1 Emission Trading Systems) could benefit from the 
blockchain’s characteristics [Liss, 2018]. Several incidents resulted in theft of allowances and 
non-authorized persons gained access to accounts in the system. In addition, huge damages, 
around 5 billion euros [European Court of Auditors, 2015], were caused by a Value Added Tax 
==PAGE:23==
 
- 16 - 
(VAT) fraud. In those cases, traders could profit from taxes while trading EUAs. Europol 
estimates that up to 90% of some country market volumes were caused by these deceptions. In 
the so-called Missing Trader Intra-Community Fraud (MTIC) the traders abuse the zero rate 
of taxation on intra-community, cross-border trades [Liss, 2018]. In this way the buyer can 
purchase carbon allowances without paying taxes on it and can sell them within the same 
country, with added VAT. Instead of paying the extra earnings to the respective government, 
the tax-difference is transferred to an offshore bank account. As a final step the whole process 
has to be repeated as long the business exist, after the business closes, the trader will be the 
Missing Trader [Guegan et al., 2011]. Even though the fraud is known, the problem is not fully 
solved yet. According to the European Court of Auditors, around one third of all EU ETS 
member-states has not implemented the VAT reverse charge mechanism [European Court of 
Auditors, 2015].  
However, not only the security aspect would lead to the consideration of blockchain for the EU 
ETS. The trading system is centralized, with a database operated by the EU Commission, the 
Union Registry [Liss, 2018]. Nevertheless, the stored data does not monitor financial 
information nor prices of transferred EUAs. Therefore, nobody can trace back if the VAT was 
or was not included in the trade. Anyhow, this is caused by data protection policies, the national 
regulators are not allowed to access data from other member states [Liss, 2018]. As a result, 
even if there is a suspicious cross-border transaction, the authorities have no access to the 
financial information of the transaction and have to rely on external information.  
 
As a last main issue, Liss states the extraordinary surplus of allowances in the system. 
According to Healy et al., the surplus of 1.7 billion EUAs accounts was worth almost one 
whole year of emissions under EU ETS in 2017. This is caused by the fact that every year more 
EUAs are handed out than demanded as well as the blurred boundaries of EU ETS and the 
Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) [Liss, 2018]. Thus, an EU company can compensate 
or reduce their emissions in their plant in another non-EU country. As a result, those, under the 
Kyoto Protocol earned, international credits for reducing GHG can be claimed under EU law 
as credit for the EU ETS [Liss, 2018]. 
The blockchain technology can help in order to cope with the MTIC and other types of fraud 
in carbon trading schemes [Hyvärinen et al., 2017]. Therefore, the implementation of 
blockchain in public sectors as ETS could have a big impact [Government Office of Science, 
2016] Nevertheless, heretofore there are just theoretical approaches of blockchain 
==PAGE:24==
 
- 17 - 
implementations of blockchain implementations in carbon trading schemes [Pan et al., 2019; 
Liss, 2018; Li et al., 2019]. 
 
However, blockchain is not only for the industry sector interesting. Organizations like 
ClimateCoin tackle the above stated problems for ETS. They introduce tokens which represents 
carbon credits along with immutable records of environmental credit ownerships and 
utilization. Those tokens can be traded by individuals and companies [Climatecoin, 2017]. 
Through a purchase, 50% of the price is going to a CO2 fund. Therefore, a purchase supports a 
CO2 offset project and the certificate for the purchased carbon credits can be traded at a later 
point, when carbon credits may gain on value [Climatecoin, 2017].  
However, blockchain is not only an option for ETS. It could be also used in fully decentralized 
energy systems [Pan et al., 2018]. Thus, the blockchain technology could help to develop an 
infrastructure with an increased level as well as a higher efficiency factor of the renewable 
energy usage. Renewable energy must be used when generated, the unpredictable generation 
of energy, trough for example windmills, can end in a disconnection to the grid [Pop et al., 
2018]. Therefore, smart contracts (2.2.7 Smart Contracts) can help to distribute the level of 
energy within the system, without a third party. Hence, the consumers as well as the producers 
have a higher influence at the market, a direct communication could be established and the 
process of metering, billing and settlement simplified [Pan et al., 2018]. The first P2P energy 
trading system was established in the TransActiveGrid project in Gowanus and Park Slope, 
Brooklyn, New York, United States [Nguyen, 2016]. The system allowed producers of 
renewable energy a direct interaction with consumers. Thus, a household which uses, for 
example, solar power could sell the produced excess directly to another household. The project 
had to be ended due to the law which states that individuals are not allowed to sell electricity 
[Mengelkamp et al., 2018]. Nevertheless, with such a system the energy information is verified 
by other peers in the system, the real amount of generated energy, the origin as well as the 
current price is transparent. In conclusion, the blockchain can help to develop a transparent 
way to balance the energy level at the grid level and provides the user with information where 
the energy comes from [Pop et al., 2018].

an approach to compensate the total emissions of the transportation sector [Li et al., 2019]. 
Furthermore, the proposed scheme composes all three emission streams of the entities in the 
transportation sector, the fuel producers, vehicle manufactures and vehicle users (2.1 Emission 
Trading Systems). The government has to determine the total amount of permits and allocate 
the initial permits to all users in the system. Therefore, the blockchain technology supports the 
system in terms of trading, monitoring, reporting and verification the emitted CO2 within the 
regulated entities as well as between the entities. In addition, the use of the blockchain 
technology in general and smart contracts in particular is used in the BC-ECT-RT to reduce 
the administration costs enormously and to eliminate the need for a central authority [Li et al., 
2019]. 
The BC-ETS-RT merges the three different ETS approaches for the road transport sector while 
considering emission factor, vehicle miles and fuel economy [Li et al., 2019]. As a result, the 
fuel producers, vehicle users and vehicle manufactures do not just trade permits within their 
entity sector as it is usual in a conventional ETS (2.1 Emission Trading Systems). As seen in 
Figure 13, the government allocates the initial permits to all regulated entities. Furthermore, 
the framework illustrates the flow of permits within the system.  
 
 
Figure 13 Conceptual framework of the BT-ETS-RT by [Li et al., 2019] 
Therefore, the flow starts with the midstream, the vehicle manufacturers. All vehicle 
manufactures receives incremental permits which have to be assigned to new vehicles. These 
==PAGE:33==
 
- 26 - 
permits states how much vehicles a manufactured is allowed to produce per year. Thereby, it 
has to be distinguished between car models and their fuel efficiency. In other words, the car 
manufacturer can produce with the same amount of permits more efficient cars than cars with 
a higher CO2 emission rate. When a manufacturer runs out of permits a purchase of permits of 
another manufacturer with a permit surplus has to be taken.  
The second regulated entity is the downstream, the car users. With a purchase of a new car the 
assigned permits of the car are transferred from manufacturer to the buyer. However, the 
proposed stream considers existing vehicles as well. This entails that the government transfers 
reduced permits to the already registered cars according to the build year of the vehicle. 
Nevertheless, whenever the vehicle has to be refueled, the user, individual or transportation 
company, has to transfer their permits of the car to the fuel producer. This leads to the fact that 
the incremental permits from the purchase of the car or the afterwards allocated permits are 
sooner or later depleted. Whenever a car cannot be refueled the vehicle user has to buy permits 
of other users who have a surplus on their car’s permit balance. 
The end of the permit flow is at the third regulated entity, the fuel producer. Although the fuel 
producers emit CO2 as well, the BC-ETS-RT consider the last entity as collector. Thus, the fuel 
producers are not allowed to collect more from the user transferred permits than from the 
government stated. However, similar to the other streams the fuel producers have the option to 
purchase additional permits from other producers.  
Consequently, the BC-ETS-RT tackles every entity in the transportation sector and forces them 
to a more sustainable behavior. In order to decrease the usage of permits the car manufactures 
are encouraged to increase the fuel efficiency of the cars and the vehicle users may reconsider 
their travel activities. However, the fuel producers are limited in their actions. They could 
change the carbon intensity of fuels what would lead to a rise in the fuel price and therefore a 
higher number of required permits for the vehicle users [Graham and Glaister, 2002]. 
3. Carbon Blockchain Framework for Smart Mobility Data-markets 
 
The proposed carbon Blockchain Framework for Smart Mobility Data-markets (cBSMD) is 
built on the basis of the BSMD by López and Farooq (2.3.2.1 A Multi-layered Blockchain 
Framework for Smart Mobility Data-markets) and implements a user-centric GHG ETS by 
leveraging the conceptual ETS for the road sector proposed by Li et al. (2.3.2.2 Blockchain-
based Emission Trading System for Road Transport).  
==PAGE:34==
 
- 27 - 
According to the by Li et al. purposed BC-ETS-RT the fuel consumption is crucial for the 
needed numbers of permits and is connected to the registration of the car [Li et al, 2019]. The 
emissions are not assigned to individuals, therefore tracing emissions in shared vehicles, shared 
rides and even public transportation that ultimately (and increasingly) contribute to the sector’s 
total emissions is not possible. Consequently, the BC-ETS-RT displays the CO2 emission of 
the registered vehicle owner only, not from the actual user. Moreover, the time factor is not 
implied, thus the amount of GHG emissions cannot be assigned to individual trips.  
As a result, the proposed cBSMD extends existing state-of-the-art conceptual frameworks as 
the BC-ETS-RT by Li et al. with trip- and user-specific carbon token transactions. The 
considered GHG emissions are composed of Water vapor (H2O), Carbon dioxide (CO2), 
Methane (CH4), Nitrous oxide (N2O), Ozone (O3), Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), 
Hydrofluorocarbons (HCFCs and HFCs), Sulfur hexafluoride (SF6). Therefore, all GHG 
emissions are converted to carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e) to calculate with a consistent 
factor and to consider the impact of the different gases. As a consequence, the implemented 
carbon token is composed of the CO2e. 
This chapter presents the architecture of the proposed cBSMD along with the changes to the 
original layer structured BSMD in order to align them with the GHG emission trading here at 
stake.  
 
The first step is to define the level of permission to determine the involvement of the 
participants. Even though the original BSMD was set as a public closed system, the level of 
permission has to be reconsidered if the type is also suitable for the additional emission trading 
scheme and the involved transactions of tokens. Therefore, all four different options are 
considered (2.2.2 Level of Permission).  
Open systems consume a high amount of energy due to the great involvement of participates 
in the consensus mechanism [Vranken, 2017]. To go along with the eco-friendly idea of the 
purposed system, control GHG emissions, only closed systems are considered. Moreover, since 
the scheme is in particular for the public, participants should not have any restrictions in terms 
of access the ledger or do transfers within the system. This leads to the same type of permission 
level as the BSMD system contains: public closed.  
As a norm in blockchain systems [Yuan and Wang, 2016] the cBSMD is described as the 
BSMD in layers. As mentioned before the cBSMD is an expansion of the BSMD which uses 
the principles of the blockchain system and assigns personal GHG emissions to the users. The

3.2 The Identification Layer  
Nowadays, each person and each vehicle generate, while traveling, a certain amount of data. 
This data can be used by entities by way of example for research purposes, statistical 
evaluations or planning and operational processes. The initial approach of the BSMD 
distinguished therefore between metadata, static data and dynamic data which is stored in the 
identification of each node. The metadata is always accessible for other nodes in the system. 
As a result, another node can see the type of node (user, vehicle, entity) and the blockchain 
address to get in touch with this node. However, the metadata does not disclose any personal 
data and ensure the sole ownership of sensible data. Secondly, the static data is composed of 
all the information which does not change often. Typical static data is for example the 
information about the name, gender, address. For entities or vehicles, the static data consists, 
for instance, the company name or vehicle brand. Moreover, due to the presentation of a by a 
government authority issued document (e.g. birth certificate, passport) the authenticity of the 
node’s metadata is ensured and the static personal data (age, gender, address, etc.) verified. 
Finally, the dynamic data in the identification describes the gathered trip related information. 
In this dataset the cBSMD added the information about the GHG emissions of the trip as well 
as the equivalent amount of tokenized carbon credits. Furthermore, the emission trading 
scheme leads to additional information about executed transactions in the dynamic dataset, 
which includes data and time of the transaction, the sender’s and receiver’s blockchain address, 
if applicable a description with the vehicle ID, and the amount of transferred tokens.

geomask and GeoInd possibilities to set level of privacy to conceal or disclose the current 
location [López and Farooq, 2019].  This hybrid privacy approach can also be implemented in 
the cBSMD. However, for the cBSMD the LBS is not only important to share trip related

Although the GHG emission information has to be shared with other nodes, the level of privacy 
is not affected negatively. The personal data can be secured and the GHG emission 
consumption is anonymized due to the use of public accessible metadata instead of personal 
information.   
3.4 Communication Layer 
 
The cBSMD was built with Hyperledger Iroha. Therefore, the Peer Communication Service 
(PCS), an intermediary, is part of the cBSMD. The PCS establishes a communication channel 
between two nodes, whenever both nodes accept the requirements of sharing information. 
Thus, the PCS is responsible for the data transition from the entry point for users via the 
Multisignature Transaction (MST) Processor through to the Ordering Gate. The Torii, the 
entry point for users, as well as the MST Processor use the open source remote procedure call 
gRPC to connect the user with the system and other peers. In addition, the MST Processor 
exchanges the status of unsigned transactions among the users to reach the required quorum of 
signatures. Thus, each peer in the system receives messages from other peers’ trough a Gossip 
protocol. Since a distributed ledger does not have a central registry, the Gossip protocol 
establishes an epidemiologic communication service which distributes messages to nodes and 
their linked peers. Consequently, the PCS sends stateless but valid transactions to the ordering 
==PAGE:44==
 
- 37 - 
gate along other peers and can hide the complexity of interaction with the consensus 
implementation. Due to a fact that a single node has several communication channels, a hacker 
attack would have to intercept multiple communication connections before entering transferred 
data. In general, the PCS ensures a secure P2P data sharing system. 
 
3.5 Incentive Layer 
 
In order to incentive the participants, the incentive layer contains three different mechanisms.  
First of all, like the most blockchain systems, the involvement in the consensus mechanism 
(2.2.6 Consensus Mechanism). 
Secondly, the reduction of GHG emissions. The users can be incentive by the system, in terms 
of reduce the personal GHG footprint. Whenever a user decides to emit less GHG, he receives 
rewards in terms of his surplus what he can share and his saving respectively. In the other way 
around, a user has to purchase more tokens as offset for his extra GHG emissions. This 
mechanism should incentive individuals to reduce the personal GHG footprint while receiving 
rewards or reducing expenses.  
Finally, the sharing rewards which is already implemented in the BSMD scheme. Those 
rewards are given to users who are willing to share trip related trip information. For instance, 
an individual can share his trip data to an entity for MRV purposed and get paid by the receiver. 
 
3.6 Consensus Layer 
The consensus layer consists the cBSMD’s mechanism for the consensus process. This is in 
particular the Yet Another Consenus (YAC) which uses as basis the BFT (2.2.6.3 Byzantine 
Fault Tolerance) consensus algorithm with an added modular architecture as well as a simple 
implementation structure [Muratov et al., 2018]. YAC’s distinctive features are its scalability, 
low latency for transactions and high transaction throughput.

4.1 Implementation  
 
The trading system over the cBSMD is simulated on a physical network and with a blockchain 
composed of 3,186 passive nodes that represent users traveling in a day and 4 active nodes in 
charge of hosting and running the cBSMD. An i5 3.5 GHz × 4 and 8GB of RAM do every 
transactions of the active nodes while 4 t2.medium (Amazon cloud EC2 virtual machines with 
2cores at 3.1GHz and 4GB of RAM), runs 1 active node each. 
 
All users in the cBSMD are entitled to consume a certain amount of tokens. Thus, the 
assumption has to be made that all users have the same amount of carbon tokens at the 
beginning of the day, in this case all users receive 493.79 carbon tokens at midnight, which are 
added to the users’ wallets. As mentioned above this allocation for everyone could lead to fraud 
or unfair trades, that’s why reliable market schemes have to be considered and implemented in 
the future.  
==PAGE:47==
 
- 40 - 
In the cBSDM ledger it is recorded the amount of carbon tokens each user has at beginning of 
the day. Wallets in the cBSMD stores carbon tokens and can only be altered (add or remove 
carbon tokens) if a transaction request is sent and approved by the active nodes. Every carbon 
token transaction is always recorded in the cBSMD ledger. In the current status of the cBSMD 
the payment of carbon tokens, for the produced GHG, is done when the user finishes his trip. 
However, since the user behaviour is dependent on the allocation policy, the allocation of 
carbon tokens before the trip, as upfront costs, or flexible price schemes can be considered for 
future works.  
For paying the trip the user sends a transaction request to the active nodes where they inform 
that they are going to alter their wallet to pay the carbon tokens they spent in the trip. 
 
The transactions request contains the following information: 
 
a) Date: date of the transaction 
b) Sender: public ID of the node who made a trip and sends the carbon tokens 
c) Receiver: public ID of the node receiving the tokens. In this particular case study, it is 
the public ID of the agency who create the initial carbon tokens of the user. However, 
for the user-centric trading scheme it would be the public ID of the user who is receiving 
the carbon tokens  
d) Description: message attached to the transfer. This message may contain the produced 
GHG amount and a summary of the trip. 
e) Amount: the total number of carbon tokens. 
 
The lifecycle of a carbon token transaction request is depicted in Figure 22.  
At first, the transaction request is sent to one of the active nodes in where it is verified; (1) if 
the transaction is well formed (i.e., is written in a language readable by active nodes) and (2) 
if the user has enough carbon token for paying the trip. Since every carbon token transaction 
is recorded in the ledger the active nodes can easily verify if the paying node has enough carbon 
token in his wallet.  
==PAGE:48==
 
- 41 - 
 
If the transaction is well formed and pass the carbon token validation, it is sent into a pool 
where it waits to be grouped into a block by an active node. More than one active node can 
build blocks using the transactions from the pool. Once a predetermined number of blocks are 
created, the active nodes starts a consensus algorithm called YAC (3.6 Consensus Layer) to 
decide which block will be added to the blockchain. The blocks that were not selected by the 
consensus are discarded.  
All blocks added to the blockchain have the following information: 
 
a) Previous block: hash value that chains the block to the previous block (2.2.5 Hashing 
data) 
b) Transaction list: array of transactions. Blocks contains more than one transaction (3.4 
Communication Layer) 
c) Timestamp: current time when the block was created 
d) Signatures: signatures of active nodes, which voted for the block during consensus 
round

5. Conclusion and Future Work 
 
The carbon Blockchain framework for Smart Mobility Data-market (cBSMD) is designed to 
provide individuals the accurate GHG footprint from a multi-modal perspective, which is 
necessary to implement a user-based GHG trading scheme. The cBSMD uses the Blockchain 
framework for Smart Mobility Data-market (BSMD) as a basis to develop a user-based, 
tokenized GHG trading scheme. In addition to the existing BSMD, the proposed conceptual 
framework adds trip caused GHG emissions information to the trip data of the user. 
Consequently, a monitoring, reporting and verification of the emitted pollution can be ensured 
and utilized for the ETS.  
We demonstrate the usage of the cBSMD due to a simulation on a physical network with a 
blockchain composed of 3,186 passive nodes that represent traveling users during the day as 
well as 4 active nodes which are responsible for hosting and running the cBSMD. All users in 
the system are allocated with the same average amount of carbon tokens to cover their total 
emitted GHG. The allocated carbon tokens are reduced based on emitted GHG whenever a 
user completes their trip. The case study provides an overview about the distribution of carbon 
tokens during the day and did not include any ETS market setting and rules, individual behavior 
when dealing with such a market, nor alternative token (travel) charging scheme. Based on the 
sociodemographic data, patterns could be established and the production of GHG emissions 
and consumption of carbon tokens analyzed with cBSMD. Thus, the analysis gives insights 
into who consumed carbon tokens, when, where and for what mode or mobility service.

To be able to use the system in real-life cases, similar sociodemographic analysis can be used 
to ensure the operationalizability of different token allocation and trading schemes. 
Consequently, a market-ready user-centric ETS under cBSMD should be tested in different 
experiment and simulation studies for behavior, market, emission and scalability evaluation. 
This cBSMD demonstration showed its capability of materializing ETS strategies such that not 
only industries are forced to reduce their CO2e emissions, but also travelers should be 
stimulated to manage their own GHG footprint in their mobility patterns. 
To sum up, the study demonstrates a possibility how blockchain can help to assign GHG 
emissions to individuals as well as vehicles and implies therefore an accurate emission 
monitoring system. With, for example, a cap and trade market mechanism, these monitored 
GHG emissions can be controlled in the system and consequently decreased due to incentivized 
individuals who have to pay attention to their GHG footprint.
