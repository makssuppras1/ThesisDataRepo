Abstract
User queries in information retrieval are often ambiguous, making it challenging for systems to
identify a userÔÇÖs target from a single query. While recent dialogue┬¡based interactive retrieval sys┬¡
tems can clarify user intent, they are inefficient as they often lack an explicit strategy to ask the most
informative questions. To address this limitation, we propose SherlockLLM, a dialogue┬¡driven re┬¡
trieval framework that learns an optimal questioning strategy via Reinforcement Learning (RL)
and avoids the need for large┬¡scale annotated dialogue data. In our framework, an agent is trained
to generate a sequence of binary questions to efficiently narrow down the search space. To validate
our approach, we introduce a benchmark with both structured and unstructured tasks. Experimen┬¡
tal results show that SherlockLLM is a robust and efficient solution. On the structured tasks, its
performance matches strong baselines and approaches the theoretical optimal defined by binary
search. On the challenging unstructured task, our agent significantly outperforms these baselines,
showcasing its ability to learn a highly effective information┬¡seeking dialogue policy.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The fundamental goal of an information retrieval (IR) system is to bridge the gap between a userÔÇÖs
information need and the vast repository of available data. However, a significant and persistent
challenge lies in the inherent ambiguity of queries. User queries are frequently underspecified,
incomplete, or rely on contextual knowledge that is not explicitly stated. Traditional retrieval
systems, which are often designed for precise, keyword┬¡based, single┬¡shot interactions, struggle to
perform effectively under these conditions. They lack a mechanism to resolve ambiguity, forcing
the user into a frustrating cycle of query reformulation and trial┬¡and┬¡error.
This challenge is particularly acute in search scenarios where a user has a specific target in mind but
lacks the precise vocabulary to describe it. Consider the common experience of trying to find an
image of a celebrity whose name one cannot recall. A query such as ÔÇØthe actor from that recent sci┬¡
fi movie with the red planetÔÇØ is likely to yield thousands of irrelevant results from a conventional
search engine. In such cases, a simple, one┬¡shot query is often insufficient to guarantee the retrieval
of a specific target image. This highlights a critical need for systems that can move beyond static,
one┬¡shot retrieval and engage in a more dynamic, collaborative search process.
Dialogue┬¡based interactive retrieval offers an effective solution by enabling systems to clarify user
intent through multi┬¡turn interactions [1, 2, 3, 4]. As illustrated in fig. 1.1, although no precise
target queries are provided, an agent can engage in a conversation, asking targeted questions to
iteratively narrow down a large pool of candidates and pinpoint the correct item.
With the development of Large Language Models (LLMs), there has been a surge of interest in
using their powerful generative and reasoning capabilities for interactive retrieval tasks [5, 4, 6,
7]. A promising direction is to employ LLMs directly as dialogue agents that generate clarification
questions to interact with users [8, 6, 4]. However, the reasoning capabilities of LLMs do not
inherently translate into effective information┬¡seeking strategies. The reason is that the models lack
an explicit learning signal aligned with the objective of uncertainty reduction [9]. Consequently,
although LLMs can generate fluent and contextually relevant questions, these questions are not
optimized to be maximally informative. They do not effectively minimize the number of turns
required to identify the target.
Therefore, the central challenge and the primary focus of this thesis is to bridge this gap. We aim to
design a framework that explicitly trains an agent to generate a sequence of maximally informative
questions. The objective is to learn an optimal policy to minimize the number of dialogue turns
required for retrieval.
1.2
Problem Statement
Based on the challenge of LLM┬¡based interactive retrieval systems, this project aims to answer the
following questions:
ÔÇó RQ1: How can an agent be trained to generate a sequence of natural language binary┬¡choice
(yes/no) questions that are maximally informative, thereby effectively resolving the ambi┬¡
guity of user queries in an IR system?
ÔÇó RQ2: To what extent does the proposed agent improve the retrieval efficiency compared to
state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art LLMs?

1.3
Proposed Approach
We propose SherlockLLM, a dialogue┬¡based retrieval framework. We formulate the task as a se┬¡
quential decision┬¡making problem solved via reinforcement learning (RL). The objective is to learn
a policy that generates a sequence of questions to minimize the number of turns needed to achieve
a satisfactory retrieval outcome. We train an agent to interact with a retrieval environment, where
it receives rewards based on how effectively its generated questions reduce the candidate space.
Through this process, the agent autonomously learns a dialogue policy optimized for retrieval ef┬¡
ficiency.
To validate SherlockLLMÔÇÖs effectiveness and generalizability, we evaluate it on two distinct re┬¡
trieval tasks with both structured and unstructured data. For structured data, we introduce a bench┬¡
mark composed of two tasks: Guess Number and Guess Who. These tasks provide a controlled
environment for evaluating the agentÔÇÖs ability to filter and retrieve targets via dialogue. The agent
is guided by a reward function based on Expected Information Gain (EIG), and evaluates retrieval
efficiency using binary search as a reference for optimal performance. For the more complex task
of unstructured image retrieval, the agentÔÇÖs questioning policy is guided by image captions. Here,
the reward function is based on the rank of the target image in a text┬¡based search, directly opti┬¡
mizing for retrieval performance.

Background
This chapter introduces the technical foundation of this thesis. We begin by reviewing Transformer
architecture, which is the cornerstone of LLMs and VLMs. We then introduce the specifics of
LLMs, detailing their pre┬¡training and post┬¡training methods. Subsequently, we summarize CLIP
[11] and BLIP [12], which are relevant to multimodal retrieval tasks in this work. The discussion
then transitions to reinforcement learning, the training approach of our proposed framework. We
provide an overview of principles of RL and common algorithms, summarizing their architectures,
objective functions, and applications for fine┬¡tuning LLMs. Finally, we introduce the concept of
information retrieval. We first trace the evolution of IR systems, then discuss the role of LLMs in
query reformulation.
2.1
Transformer Model
Transformer architecture [13] represents a fundamental shift in sequence┬¡to┬¡sequence (Seq2Seq)
model design. Previously, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) [14] and their variants (such as
LSTMs [15]) were the major approaches for Seq2Seq tasks. These recurrent architectures ex┬¡
hibit significant limitations: (1) can not be trained in parallel, (2) have difficulties in capturing
long┬¡distance relationships, and (3) can lead to vanishing or exploding gradients. Transformer ad┬¡
dresses these challenges by replacing recurrence entirely with a self┬¡attention mechanism. This
design allows the model to process input tokens simultaneously. This capability enables mas┬¡
sive parallelization, significantly reduces training times, and provides a more effective method for
modeling global contextual relationships. Consequently, Transformer is the essential component
of modern large┬¡scale language models.
As illustrated in Figure 2.1, Transformer is an encoder┬¡decoder architecture. It contains two par┬¡
allel stacks that process input and output sequences. The process begins by converting inputs into
Embedding vectors. Positional Encoding is then added to these vectors to provide information
about token order. On the left, the encoder stack processes these inputs using Multi┬¡Head At┬¡
tention and Feed Forward layers to generate a rich contextual representation. On the right, the
decoder stack generates an output sequence. The decoder utilizes a Masked Multi┬¡Head Atten┬¡
tion layer to ensure that predictions for a given position depend only on preceding tokens. It also
uses the encoderÔÇÖs output to guide its predictions by Multi┬¡Head Cross Attention. Both encoder
and decoder stacks use Residual Connections and Layer Normalization after each sub┬¡layer. Fi┬¡
nally, the output from the decoder stack passes through a Linear layer and a Softmax to produce
the probability distribution for the next token.

2.1.2
Tokenization
Tokenization refers to the process of converting a discrete text sequence into the smallest units
(called tokens) processed by transformers. Tokenization is not simply splitting at the character or
word level, but rather at the sub┬¡word level, which balances between covering rare vocabulary and
maintaining vocabulary compactness.
2.1.3

2.1.4
Visual Transformer (ViT)
While Transformer was originally designed for Natural Language Processing (NLP), it has been
successfully adapted for computer vision tasks with Vision Transformer (ViT) [16]. A key chal┬¡
lenge is reconciling the TransformerÔÇÖs input requirement of a one┬¡dimensional token sequence with
the two┬¡dimensional structure of images. As shown in Figure 2.2, ViT addresses this by partition┬¡
ing an input image into a series of fixed┬¡size, non┬¡overlapping patches. Each patch is subsequently
processed into a one┬¡dimensional vector. This procedure converts the image into a sequence of vi┬¡
sual tokens that a standard Transformer encoder can process. The model then applies self┬¡attention
to compute relationships between all pairs of patches, thereby capturing global, long┬¡range depen┬¡
dencies across the entire image.
Following the architectural flow illustrated in Figure 2.2, the sequence of flattened patches is first
projected into an embedding space and adds spatial information to these patch embeddings. An
extra learnable [class] token is added to the beginning of the sequence. This complete sequence
is then fed into a standard transformer encoder. The final value of [class] token serves as the
representation for the entire image and is passed to a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) to produce the
class prediction.

2.2
Large Language Models (LLMs)
LLMs are developed using the transformer architecture and trained on massive corpora. This pro┬¡
cess allows LLMs to have broad world knowledge and sophisticated reasoning abilities. LLMs can
perform a variety of tasks, including text generation, question answering, translation, and dialogue,
through natural language understanding.
2.2.1
Training Objectives
LLMs can be broadly categorized according to their training objectives. Two dominant objectives
are used in practice, each associated with a distinct model architecture. These are the masked
language model (MLM) and the causal (or autoregressive) language model (CLM). While both are
based on transformers, they differ significantly in their structure, learning objective, and expected
use cases. Table 2.1 summarizes their differences.

Masked Language Model (MLM). The MLMÔÇÖs objective involves masking random tokens within
an input sequence and training the model to predict these masked tokens from the surrounding con┬¡
text [17]. This approach most commonly utilizes an encoder┬¡only architecture. In this configura┬¡
tion, the encoder stack processes the entire input sequence simultaneously in a non┬¡autoregressive
manner. This structure allows the model to attend to both left and right contexts, making it highly
effective for understanding┬¡based tasks such as classification, textual entailment, and information
extraction.
Causal Language Model (CLM). The CLMÔÇÖs objective is to train a model to predict the next
token in a sequence based exclusively on the preceding tokens. This process follows a left┬¡to┬¡right
directional flow. In contrast to encoder┬¡only models, CLMs primarily employ a decoder┬¡only ar┬¡
chitecture, as seen in models like GPT [18]. This architecture generates output tokens sequentially,
making it inherently suitable for generative tasks, including open┬¡ended text completion, question
answering, and dialogue systems.
In this work, we select a CLM (Qwen [19]) as the backbone for SherlockLLM. This choice is
motivated by the CLMÔÇÖs generation ability, a critical requirement for conversation with an agent.
2.2.2
LLM Training
Pre┬¡training. The initial stage in an LLMÔÇÖs lifecycle is pre┬¡training. During this phase, the learn┬¡
ing objective is derived from raw text without the need for manual annotation. The most common
task is autoregressive next┬¡token prediction, where the model learns to predict the probability dis┬¡
tribution of the next word given the preceding context. Although simple in principle, this objective
provides a powerful learning signal. When trained on sufficiently large and diverse corpora, it en┬¡
ables the model to capture complex syntax, semantics, and even higher┬¡order reasoning patterns.
By exposing the model to billions or trillions of tokens, it develops statistical associations that can
be repurposed for a wide range of downstream tasks.

2.2.3
Capabilities of LLMs
After extensive pre┬¡training and well alignment, LLMs exhibit a wide range of flexible capabilities.
These capabilities extend beyond core language tasks to encompass more complex functions that
require reasoning and high levels of adaptability.
ÔÇó Language Tasks: LLMs are capable of performing a wide array of traditional NLP tasks
with fluency and contextual awareness, such as text generation, translation, and summariza┬¡
tion.
ÔÇó Question Answering and Information Retrieval: LLMs integrate extensive world knowl┬¡
edge from their training data, which allows them to function as powerful knowledge bases.
They can directly retrieve information and answer factual questions through natural language
dialogue.
ÔÇó Complex Reasoning: LLMs demonstrate excellent reasoning abilities, including mathe┬¡
matical, logical, and commonsense reasoning required to understand real┬¡world situations.
The coherence and accuracy of their reasoning can be further enhanced through sophisti┬¡
cated prompt engineering techniques, such as Chain┬¡of┬¡Thought (CoT) [25] or Reflection
[26].
ÔÇó Zero┬¡Shot and Few┬¡Shot Learning: LLMs exhibit strong meta┬¡learning capabilities. They
can often perform tasks correctly without any specific training examples, relying only on a
natural language description of the task in a process known as zero┬¡shot learning. Their
performance can be further improved by providing a small number of examples within the
prompt, which is called few┬¡shot learning. This capacity for generalization makes them
exceptionally versatile tools.
2.3
Vision┬¡Language Models (VLMs)
VLMs are a type of deep learning models that jointly process images and text. They are designed
to integrate visual with natural language understanding. The primary objective of these models is
to enable AI systems to interpret and reason about visual content using language, and similarly, to
understand text in a visual context. A typical VLM architecture comprises two main components.
A vision encoder extracts key features like colors, shapes, and objects from images or videos, while
a language encoder captures the semantic meaning of text. Through training on large┬¡scale, paired
image┬¡text corpora, these models learn to map visual and textual semantics into a shared represen┬¡
tation space. This shared representation enables VLMs to perform a wide range of downstream
tasks, such as image captioning, Visual Question Answering (VQA), and image┬¡text retrieval. This
section introduces two influential VLMs, CLIP and BLIP, which form the basis for the methods
employed in this work.
2.3.1
Contrastive Language┬¡Image Pre┬¡training (CLIP)
CLIP is a transformer┬¡based VLM model developed by OpenAI. The model is pre┬¡trained on a
large┬¡scale image┬¡text corpus to align text and visual representations in a shared embedding space.
In this space, the embeddings of semantically similar text┬¡image pairs are close to each other, while
dissimilar ones are farther apart.
The CLIP architecture is composed of two distinct encoders, one for text and one for images.
The text encoder is a Transformer architecture that maps text inputs to high┬¡dimensional semantic
embeddings. The image encoder is used to encode image features into the same embedding space.
The image encoder can be a ResNet or ViT model, but the original study found the ViT to be
more computationally efficient [11]. Both embeddings are normalized and compared using cosine
similarity to measure correspondence.

2.4
Reinforcement Learning (RL)
RL is a learning method wherein an agent learns to make decisions through direct interaction with
an environment. This learning process can be formulated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP),
which provides a mathematical framework for sequential decision┬¡making under uncertainty.
An MDP is defined by the tuple (S, A, P(┬À), R(┬À), ╬│). Here, S represents the state space, and A
denotes the action space. The function P(┬À) specifies the state transition probabilities, while R(┬À)
defines the reward function. The discount factor, ╬│ Ôêê[0, 1], adjusts the present value of future
rewards. The interaction unfolds over discrete timesteps. At each timestep t, the agent observes a
state st ÔêêS and selects an action at ÔêêA. Consequently, the environment transitions to a new state
st+1 based on the probability P(st+1|st, at), and the agent receives a reward rt+1 = R(st, at). The
central objective in an MDP is to learn a policy ¤Ç that maximizes the cumulative reward the agent
receives over time. Solution methods for this problem are broadly classified into two categories.
Value┬¡based methods concentrate on learning value functions that estimate the desirability of states,
from which an optimal policy can be derived. In contrast, policy┬¡based methods directly search
for an optimal policy by adjusting its parameters. In this section, we introduce the representative

2.5
Information Retrieval (IR)
IR is finding information that satisfies an information need from within large collections based on a
userÔÇÖs query [38]. An IR system typically indexes a collection of documents using techniques such
as tokenization offline. The retrieval process begins with a user submits a query, the systemÔÇÖs query
processor processes it similarly, and retriever locates a set of potentially relevant documents using
16
A Conceptual Framework for Dialogue┬¡Driven Retrieval via Reinforcement Learning
==PAGE:26==
an inverted index. These candidate results are evaluated by a reranker module, which scores them
based on relevance metrics such as cosine similarity or probabilistic scores. Finally, the system
displays these results to the user as a ranked list, with the documents predicted to be most relevant
appearing at the top.
2.5.1
IR Methods
IR has evolved significantly from its early logic models to todayÔÇÖs deep learning models. The ear┬¡
liest retrieval model was boolean retrieval. Boolean retrieval model treats each document as a set
of words, which are then combined with queries using logical operators AND, OR, and NOT [38].
While intuitive and straightforward, Boolean retrieval does not support result ranking, limiting its
effectiveness in capturing partial relevance or prioritizing useful documents. In order to support
ranking the documents matching a query, the Vector Space Model (VSM) was developed. In VSM,
both queries and documents are represented as vectors in a high┬¡dimensional term space, and their
similarity is typically measured using cosine similarity. However, these vectors are often sparse,
especially for long documents, making meaningful similarity computation challenging. Therefore,
researchers developed various term weighting schemes, such as term frequency (TF) and TF┬¡IDF
(Term FrequencyÔÇôInverse Document Frequency), to balance term specificity across the entire cor┬¡
pus. Probabilistic retrieval models attempt to estimate the probability that a document is relevant
to a given query, denoted as P(R = 1|d, q), where R Ôêê0, 1. One of the foundational models in
this category is the Binary Independence Model (BIM), which assumes that term occurrences are
conditionally independent and that document relevance is independent of other documents. Build┬¡
ing on this framework, more sophisticated models were developed. One of the famous models is
Okapi BM25 [39], which takes into account factors such as term frequency and query weights.
In contrast, modern IR methods utilize neural networks to move beyond static matching. Cen┬¡
tral to this shift is the use of embeddings, which capture semantic relationships between words,
phrases, and entire documents. Transformer┬¡based models project queries and documents into a
shared embedding space where semantically similar pairs are closely located [17, 40]. This seman┬¡
tic approach addresses issues like high┬¡dimensional semantic relationships that traditional models
struggle with. Benefiting from advances in transformers, this evolution is shifting from unimodal
to multimodal retrieval, which integrates and searches across diverse data types, including images,
audio, and video.
2.5.2
LLMs for Query Reformulation
User queries in retrieval systems are often ambiguous or incomplete. Query reformulation, also
known as query rewriting or query expansion, is used to address this challenge. This process modi┬¡
fies or expands the original query to generate a new representation that is more effective for down┬¡
stream retrieval systems. Traditional query reformulation techniques relied on methods such as
spelling correction and thesaurus [41, 42]. With powerful language understanding and generation
capabilities, LLMs can interpret complex or ambiguous user inputs to mitigate the query┬¡document
mismatch problem.
Several methods utilize LLMs directly for query enhancement. For instance, Query2Doc [43]
employs an LLM to generate multiple pseudo┬¡documents for a given query. These generated doc┬¡
uments are then concatenated with the original query to improve retrieval effectiveness. Jagerman
et al. [44] investigated various prompt engineering strategies for query expansion and concluded
that CoT prompting yields the best performance. Agent4Ranking [45] presents a multi┬¡agent
system that introduces demographic and stylistic diversity into the reformulated queries. In a dif┬¡
ferent approach, DeepRetrieval trains an LLM using RL to generate augmented queries, achieving
superior results with a compact 3┬¡billion┬¡parameter model [46].
In conversational search, user intent often unfolds across multiple turns. LLMs excel in these
scenarios by managing context. They rewrite conversational inputs into complete, self┬¡contained
A Conceptual Framework for Dialogue┬¡Driven Retrieval via Reinforcement Learning
17
==PAGE:27==
queries. This allows standard non┬¡conversational retrieval systems to process them effectively [47,
2, 48]. Following this direction, ConvGQR [49] fine┬¡tunes a T5 model [40] with RL specifically
for conversational query reformulation.
Complementary to enhancing the query, other frameworks improve the information available for
generation. LLMs with Retrieval┬¡Augmented Generation (RAG) module integrate retrieval mod┬¡
ules to provide external knowledge that supports the generation process [50, 51, 52, 53, 54]. Be┬¡
yond query┬¡side modifications, LLMs can also expand documents to enrich corpus representations.
For example, Doc2Query┬¡T5 [55, 56] generates synthetic queries for each document and appends
them to the index, thereby making the document easier to retrieve.

This chapter presents the experimental framework designed to evaluate the efficacy of the Sher┬¡

4.1

We evaluate SherlockLLM on two data modalities in three tasks. In all settings, an episode ends
when the target is identified or the turn budget is exhausted.
4.1.1

This is a logical deduction task on a tabular dataset. In each game, a set of 100 consecutive integers

turns. This task is designed to test whether SherlockLLM can autonomously learn this optimal

the frameworkÔÇÖs ability to grasp the most fundamental principle of uncertainty reduction.

4.2.1
Oracle
We define an Oracle baseline to represent the theoretical upper┬¡bound performance of our tabular
tasks. The Oracle simulates a perfect decision┬¡making process. At each round, it exhaustively
searches the entire pool of single┬¡attribute questions and selects the one that yields the maximum
EIG. This question is the most informative one and, ideally, it halves the remaining candidate set.
This process continues until the target probability becomes 1.

4.3
Implementation Details
4.3.1
Model
We use Qwen┬¡2.5┬¡7B┬¡Instruct as the question policy. We select the instruction┬¡tuned version for
its superior ability to instruct following1. We selected the 7B parameter model after experiments
demonstrated that smaller variants (0.5B and 1.5B) consistently fail. A detailed performance anal┬¡
ysis of the smaller models is presented in section 4.5. Lacking the necessary reasoning capacity
for the task, these smaller models achieved a zero success rate. This outcome makes policy opti┬¡
mization impossible since the agent receives no positive learning signal. Furthermore, we utilize
a 4┬¡bit quantized version of the model to accelerate training and reduce computational overhead.
4.3.2
User Simulator
We use DeepSeek┬¡V3.1 to serve as the user simulator, responsible for answering questions. This
model was selected for two reasons. First, its state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art reasoning capabilities and high ac┬¡
curacy, verified in our preliminary tests, provide the agent with reliable and consistent feedback.
Second, a practical consideration is its APIÔÇÖs lack of a rate limit. This feature is important for our
training, which relies on running numerous episodes in parallel to gather diverse experiences.
Instead of a direct question┬¡answering format, we implement a more structured and efficient two┬¡
stage process. In this design, DeepSeek functions as a natural language parser. It converts the
agentÔÇÖs free┬¡form question into a structured key┬¡value attribute pair. For instance, the question ÔÇ£Is
the character a man?ÔÇØ is parsed into {gender: male}. A local logic module then determines the
final ÔÇ£yesÔÇØ or ÔÇ£noÔÇØ response by comparing this parsed attribute against the ground┬¡truth properties
of the target item.
This decoupled design yields significant efficiency and cost benefits. The mapping from a question
to its key┬¡value representation is deterministic and independent of the targetÔÇÖs properties, which
allows us to cache the parsed results. As the same questions are frequently asked across thou┬¡
sands of training episodes, this caching mechanism reduces redundant API calls. Consequently,
it accelerates training by mitigating request latency and substantially lowering API usage costs.
Furthermore, the key┬¡value pairs serve a dual purpose: they are directly utilized as keywords by
Retriever to filter the candidate pool, streamlining the overall framework.

Retriever

These values were empirically determined to yield optimal performance.

4.4
Metrics
To provide a comprehensive assessment of our agentÔÇÖs performance, we employ a set of metrics
tailored to each task category. For all tasks, we report the Success Rate (SR), which is the percent┬¡
age of dialogues that successfully meet the task┬¡specific completion criteria, and the Mean Turns
(MT), which measures the mean number of turns for all dialogues. For the CelebA Image Retrieval
task, we also report common retrieval metrics: Recall@K (R@K) for K=5, representing the per┬¡

to SR. Finally, we report the Median Rank (MedR) and the Mean Rank (MR) of the target image
across all trials.

4.5.1
Guess Number
We first evaluate SherlockLLM on the Guess Number task. The results, presented in Table 4.2,

The Oracle baseline, representing the theoretically optimal binary search, solves the task in a mean

our SherlockLLM (Qwen2.5┬¡7B + GRPO) exhibits strong performance. It reaches a near┬¡perfect
success rate of 0.99 with a mean of 7.62 turns, reducing the MT by 5.0%.
The performance of the base Qwen2.5 models without reinforcement learning fine┬¡tuning high┬¡
lights that strategic questioning is a non┬¡trivial skill. The smaller 0.5B and 1.5B variants show

Case studies.
For the Guess Number task, the dialogue examples (Figure 4.3) offer clear insights
into each modelÔÇÖs strategic approach. The optimal strategy for finding a number in a sorted range
of 100 items is a binary search, which requires a theoretical minimum of seven turns to guarantee
a solution.
Both the SherlockLLM agent, DeepSeek┬¡V3.1 and Qwen2.5┬¡32B baseline successfully identify
and execute this optimal binary search strategy. SherlockLLM consistently poses questions that
divide the remaining candidate space in half, such as ÔÇ£Is the target number less than or equal to
135?ÔÇØ and subsequently ÔÇ£Is the target number less than or equal to 160?ÔÇØ. It methodically narrows
the search range with high EIG questions and identifies the target in exactly seven turns, matching
the theoretical optimum. Similarly, DeepSeek┬¡V3.1 implements a perfect binary search, using
ÔÇ£greater thanÔÇØ queries to achieve the same effect and reach the solution in the optimal seven turns.
In contrast, Qwen2.5┬¡1.5B model employs an inefficient linear scan, sequentially checking fixed┬¡
size intervals. Although it correctly identifies the general range of the target on turn 9, the model
fails to narrow the search further and ultimately exceeds the maximum turn limit.
The untrained Qwen2.5┬¡7B model demonstrates a flawed and inefficient strategy. Despite starting
with reasonable questions, its policy is erratic and prone to logical errors. For example, in Turn 4,
it asks if the number is less than or equal to 121 when the established range is already [136, 157],
a query that provides no new information. The model also falls into repetitive loops (asking about
the number 144 three separate times). This combination of logical errors and redundant questions
results in a lengthy 15┬¡turn dialogue, which explains the poor MT in our quantitative results.
SFT agent learns to mimic actions from the training data, but it fails to optimize for turn efficiency.
In the initial phase (turns 1┬¡6), the agentÔÇÖs performance is optimal. It consistently poses a series
of questions with high or perfect EIG scores. However, the agentÔÇÖs strategy falters in the endgame
phase. At turn 7, instead of making a decisive guess, the agent asks other questions.

4.6.2
Image Retrieval Reward Signal
To optimize the training of our agent for the interactive image retrieval task, we investigated the
efficacy of two different reward signal formulations. Intuitively, if the similarity score between
the target and the query is higher, then it will rank higher. The objective of this experiment was to
determine whether a direct optimization of the targetÔÇÖs rank is more effective than using a implicit
signal based on text┬¡image similarity. All comparative experiments were trained on the 100┬¡image
dataset. Except for the definition of the reward function, all other conditions and training proce┬¡
dures remained consistent.
As Table 4.6 shows, both reward signals achieve an identical SR of 0.90. This indicates that both
formulations are feasible. However, we observe clear differences in efficiency and ranking quality.
The agent trained with the Rank┬¡based reward demonstrates superior efficiency. It requires an MT
of 6.15, a 6.1% reduction in conversational length compared to the 6.55 turns required by the
agent trained on the Similarity score. Furthermore, the Rank┬¡based reward achieves a MedR of
3.5, representing a 12.5% improvement for the Similarity┬¡based reward.
Interestingly, the Similarity┬¡based reward achieves a slightly better MR of 5.9. The Rank┬¡based re┬¡
wardÔÇÖs MR is approximately 7.6% worse by comparison. We hypothesize that while the similarity┬¡

4.7.2
Ranking Algorithm Comparison
The ranking algorithm is a critical component of our agent for the image retrieval task. It translates
the dialogue history into a ranked list of candidate images at each turn. We justify our choice of
algorithm by comparing our proposed method against two plausible and computationally efficient
alternatives.
Our primary ranking algorithm, detailed in Algorithm 2, operates on a cumulative basis. This
method is designed for high precision because it leverages all information gathered up to the current
turn. The main drawback of this approach is its computational cost. Recalculating similarities for
the entire keyword history at every turn can introduce significant latency. We therefore design two
alternative algorithms that improve efficiency by avoiding these historical recalculations.
Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF).
RRF [68] is an algorithm that combines multiple ranked lists
into a single, more optimal result. This method has seen wide application in modern retrieval
systems [69, 70]. The algorithm is entirely training┬¡free. It operates only on the rank positions of
items and does not require their underlying relevance scores. The RRF score for a candidate is the
sum of the reciprocal of its rank across all lists. A small constant k = 60 is typically added to the
denominator. The formula is given by the following equation.

The failure of the RRF method stems from its incorrect assumption that information from each turn
constitutes parallel and independent evidence. In our interactive scenario, the dialogue imposes
sequential, conditional constraints. For example, a query for ÔÇ£red hairÔÇØ after a positive response
to ÔÇ£is it a man?ÔÇØ implies a search for a ÔÇ£man with red hair.ÔÇØ The RRF fusion mechanism cannot
model this implicit AND condition. Instead, it simply combines the ranked list for ÔÇ£manÔÇØ with the
ranked list for ÔÇ£red hair,ÔÇØ failing to properly constrain the search space as the dialogue progresses.
The SSM method fails because it operates on the flawed assumption that similarity scores from
each turn are equally important and directly comparable. However, query terms vary in their se┬¡
mantics and difficulty, which leads to vastly different score distributions from the retrieval model.
Multiplying these scores, which are derived from different scales, distorts the true relevance of
candidates and produces unreliable rankings.

Discussion
5.1
Answering the Research Questions
This study tries to address query ambiguity through interactive dialogue. Specifically, we inves┬¡
tigate two core research questions. The first (RQ1) asks how an agent can be trained to generate
maximally informative binary (yes/no) questions. The second (RQ2) concerns the extent to which
such an agent can improve retrieval efficiency compared to state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art LLMs.
We proposed SherlockLLM to tackle these questions. SherlockLLM is a dialogue┬¡based retrieval
agent. The agent is trained via RL with a reward function based on EIG. Accordingly, Sherlock┬¡
LLM learns a policy that minimizes retrieval uncertainty through asking high EIG questions.
Our experimental results provide clear answers to both research questions. For RQ1, Sherlock┬¡
LLM demonstrates the ability to generate questions that are both goal┬¡directed and maximally
informative. In structured tasks such as Guess Who and Guess Number, it achieves near┬¡optimal
performance, requiring only one additional dialogue turn compared to the Oracle baseline.
For RQ2, the agentÔÇÖs advantage is most evident in the unstructured CelebA image retrieval task,
where the ambiguity is highest. When conditioned on natural language captions, SherlockLLM
outperforms all baselines decisively: it improves retrieval success rate over DeepSeek┬¡V3.1 by
47.5% and 91.7% on datasets with 100 and 500 candidate images, respectively, while reducing the
number of dialogue turns by 52% and 38.8%.
In summary, the findings affirm both research questions. SherlockLLM not only learns to ask max┬¡
imally informative questions (RQ1), but also achieves substantial improvements in retrieval effi┬¡
ciency and accuracy over powerful LLMs across both structured and unstructured settings (RQ2).
5.2
Key Insights
5.2.1
Reward Shaping Matters
Our experiments reveal that the design of reward signal plays a crucial role in the learning process.
Balancing Informativeness and Efficiency: The ablation study on reward components for the
Guess Who task (Section 4.6.1) highlights a crucial trade┬¡off. An agent trained with only an EIG
reward learned to ask informative questions but adopted a slow and deliberate strategy. Con┬¡
versely, an agent guided only by a step penalty became highly efficient but lacked the guidance
to formulate the most robust queries. The combination of EIG and step penalty yields the optimal
policy: a perfect success rate with a highly efficient 6.25 mean turns. This demonstrates that by
combining reward components, one can precisely shape an agentÔÇÖs behavioral preferences along
the dimensions of local information acquisition and global action efficiency.
Aligning the Reward with the True Objective: For the image retrieval task, we compare a direct
Rank┬¡based reward with an implicit signal based on text┬¡image similarity in Section 4.6.2. The
results reveal how the intrinsic nature of the signal alters the agentÔÇÖs strategic motivation. When
the reward is based on similarity, the agentÔÇÖs objective is to find keywords that maximize the text┬¡
image similarity score. This encourages it to discover the most unique and precise descriptive
features of a target, even if it requires more dialogue turns. This objective leads to a higher R@1
rate (0.25). However, this exploratory process came at the cost of overall efficiency, reflected
in a longer average dialogue. The study emphasizes the importance of aligning the reward more
directly with the retrieval objective.

Overall, our analysis shows that reward shaping determines the agentÔÇÖs behavior, strategy, and

5.2.2
RL Outperforms SFT
While SFT enables agents to mimic informative questioning behavior, it lacks an explicit mech┬¡
anism to determine when to stop asking and attempt a retrieval guess. In our experiments, SFT
agent was trained on synthetic dialogues constructed from optimal strategies. Quantitatively, it
underperforms RL┬¡trained agent across all evaluation metrics on our set. Qualitative analysis also
illustrates that although the early questions exhibit high EIG, the agent fails to learn a principled
stopping policy.
For instance, in Figure 4.4 (f), after asking four highly informative questions, SFT agent fails to
identify the target and instead resorts to brute┬¡force enumeration. This behavior suggests that the
agent has not learned to reason over accumulated evidence. Instead, it appears to imitate a fixed
guessing behavior seen during training, without understanding when and why to stop asking and
commit to a guess.
This failure arises because SFT is trained only to imitate surface┬¡level action sequences and does
not optimize for long┬¡term objectives such as minimizing dialogue turns. As such, its behavior
reflects pattern replication rather than interactive reasoning. In contrast, our RL┬¡trained agent
learns a policy that optimizes over complete interaction trajectories.
5.3
Limitations
Despite the promising results, several limitations should be acknowledged.
ÔÇó Constrained Action Space: SherlockLLM was restricted to generating binary questions.
While this simplifies the problem, it may cause the agent to overlook more efficient formats,
such as multiple┬¡choice questions.
ÔÇó Limited Scale and Scope: The evaluation is confined to three specific tasks and uses small┬¡
scale datasets with up to 500 images. The agentÔÇÖs scalability and its generalizability to larger
or different domains, such as text retrieval, have not been verified.
ÔÇó Dependency on Dataset Quality: The success of SherlockLLM in the image retrieval task
is highly contingent on the quality of annotations. In our experiments, the agent learns to ask
questions based on rich textual captions generated from 40 binary attributes. In real┬¡world
scenarios where metadata is rough or inaccurate, the agentÔÇÖs ability to learn an effective
questioning policy would be seriously hampered, as it would lack the necessary information
from which to draw meaningful inquiries.
5.4
Future Work
The limitations of this study suggest several promising avenues for future research to build more
robust and capable interactive retrieval agents.
ÔÇó Expanding the Action Space: The framework could be extended to a richer action space
beyond binary questions. One approach could explore multiple┬¡choice questions, which may
be more efficient for pruning the candidate space in certain domains.
ÔÇó Scaling and Generalizing the Framework: To validate the broader applicability of Sher┬¡
lockLLM, future efforts should focus on testing the system on large┬¡scale datasets and across
diverse domains.
ÔÇó Multi┬¡Modal Reasoning for Retrieval: To reduce the dependency on high┬¡quality meta┬¡
data for the image retrieval task, a compelling next step is to develop a multi┬¡modal agent.

Conclusion
The central challenge addressed in this thesis is the inherent ambiguity of user queries in IR sys┬¡

two data modalities to validate our framework: two structured data retrieval games and an image
retrieval task.

ÔÇó On the unstructured CelebA Image Retrieval task, where ambiguity is highest, SherlockLLM
