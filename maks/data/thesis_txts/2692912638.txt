Summary
With the ever increasing amount of Internet of Things (IoT) devices in the world,
one critical issue is often forgotten and that is security. One of the reasons for
this oversight is that manufacturers are competing to make the cheapest possible
devices. This has caused significant issues as failing to consider security has led
to compromised IoT devices attacking important internet infrastructure and
that seems unlikely to stop. With new IoT security standards regulations this
could soon change.
However, these standards can be convoluted and hard to understand. To combat
that, this thesis finds all the appropriate IoT standards and identifies the key
requirements from each. These requirements are then compared and used as the
foundation for a new framework. This framework aims to distill the knowledge
from these standards and present it in a manner that is easier to digest. The
framework can avoid all the exceptions that the standards have by only focusing
on a single type of device, namely consumer IoT gateways. The benefits from
only targeting consumer IoT gateways are significant as they are literally the
gateway to most other consumer IoT devices.
To validate the framework a case study is done with an IoT gateway from an
industry partner. This case study shows that the framework can be used both
proactively and retroactively to determine the security of an IoT gateway.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Over the past few years the growth of Internet of Things (IoT) devices has been
massive and the growth does not seem to be slowing down. In 2020, 11.7 billion
IoT devices can be found worldwide and in 2025, they are projected to reach
30.9 billion [1]. IoT devices now outnumber traditional connected devices in the
world, which are around 10 billion [1]. These IoT devices are increasingly being
used to control critical processes and gather sensitive data. They are used in
almost all industries, including healthcare and industrial automation.
This rapid increase in the number of IoT devices brings some challenges, most
importantly security. IoT devices are traditionally designed to be as cheap as
possible, with limited emphasis on security.
Frequently, as a means of cost
reduction, software updates are not available. This means that any discovered
vulnerabilities in such devices could be abused for years [2], [3]. Additionally,
the quantity of these devices make them prime targets for botnets which can
cause widespread damage to internet infrastructure, such as happened with the
Mirai botnet. Mirai managed to make wast swathes of the internet unreachable
for people in the United States and Europe in 2016 with a Distributed Denial
of Service (DDoS) attack [4].
With high profile attacks on IoT happening fairly regularly, consumers are in-
creasingly demanding that the products they buy are secure [5]. Regulators
are also taking notice and are starting to roll out security regulations for these
==PAGE:14==
2
Introduction
devices [6]. This is forcing companies to focus more on making their devices
secure. The problem is that proving that a device is secure can be challenging
and therefore companies are looking towards using IoT security standards to
aid them in their quest. That is easier said than done, as there is a significant
amount of security standards available and they can often be long and obtuse.
The requirements in each standard are often not the same and some make cer-
tain matters mandatory while other do not mention them. Additionally, there
are some standards that mainly target their own region, such as standards from
national standard organizations, which further complicates matters.
Another complication with these standards is that they often target the entire
IoT ecosystem which means that some of them cover devices ranging from smart
light bulbs to large industrial actuators. The security requirements for these
types of devices are clearly very different as the compromise of the latter could
cause serious harm while a compromise of the former is more likely a nuisance.
A standard that is supposed to cover such a wide range of devices most likely is
filled with exceptions for different types of devices. Luckily, most of the larger
standards only cover limited segments of the IoT ecosystem, such as consumer
IoT or Industrial IoT (IIoT). Even though that is the case, those standards still
have a fairly wide range of devices to cover and are still filled with exceptions
or even elaborate class systems. All these exceptions and edge cases can make
the standards hard to read and understand.
Develco Products is one of the companies that is looking for ways to make their
products more secure and they are the industry partner for this thesis. They
are a Business-to-Business (B2B) company that has been making white label
IoT products since 2007 [7]. Their products include various sensors that use
the most common communication protocols, such as Zigbee, Bluetooth Low
Energy (BLE) and Z-Wave. To interface with all these sensors, they offer an
IoT gateway, which is a device that allows users to setup automated routines,
such as scheduled control of lights and curtains. Additionally, the gateway can
be used as a security system and can be remote controlled from the internet.
1.1
Thesis Objectives
This thesis will focus on consumer IoT gateways as they are literally the gateway
to most consumer IoT devices. These devices are often constrained and have
limited security. They then communicate with an IoT gateway which connects
them to the internet. By securing the entry point to these devices, it is possible
to shield them from most remote attacks. Additionally, IoT gateways are usually
the only device that communicate with other IoT devices and the user then
==PAGE:15==
1.1 Thesis Objectives
3
communicates with the gateway to read information or send commands to them.
This also means that the gateways can be valuable targets for an attacker and
making sure they are secure should be a top priority for consumer IoT [8].
To contribute to the security of these consumer IoT gateways, this thesis seeks
to find all the key requirements in the largest and most influential consumer
IoT security standards. These key requirements are then compared between
the standards and will be used as the foundation for a framework that seeks to
lower the barrier to entry to security. The framework seeks to make it easier
for companies to secure their devices while also following all the relevant key
requirements from the major relevant standards. By tailoring the framework to
a single type of device, most of the pitfalls of the standards themselves can be
avoided.
Lastly, the framework needs to be tested on a gateway to validate it and to
find any limitations or issues. For that part, Develco Products has offered their
Squid.link gateway for the test. They are also interested in knowing how good
their security is and if they comply with all the requirements of the proposed
framework. They stand to gain in multiple ways as they are currently looking
for a good framework to evaluate the security of their IoT gateways and will
evaluate if this proposed framework suits their needs.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
This chapter presents a literature review of all the relevant IoT security stan-
dards that are in use today and are likely to become dominant in the field in the
near future. The chapter provides an overview of each of the relevant standards
with a short analysis of each. The standards are compared to each other and
the main requirements of each are extracted. The details of each individual
requirement are lastly compared between the standards.
2.1
Methodology
This section presents the research methodology for selecting the relevant stan-
dards to analyze further. The research methodology was based on the work of
Petersen et al. [9] and Wohlin [10].
2.1.1
Research Questions
The goal of the literature review is to identify and analyze all the IoT security
standards. To assist in selecting the relevant literature, the following research
questions were posed:
==PAGE:18==
6
Literature Review
• RQ1: What IoT security standards are the largest and most influential?
• RQ2: How do those standards differ?
2.1.2
Search Strategy
The literature search was initially conducted with Google Scholar and DTU
Findit. The search terms used were ”IoT Security”, ”IoT Security Standard” and
”IoT Standard”. They resulted in an abundance of articles about IoT security
standards, such that it was not feasible to read through them all. This meant
that some inclusion and exclusion criteria needed to be specified. The criteria
that was used were the following, where EC represents an exclusion criteria and
IC an inclusion criteria.
• EC1: Article does not reference or propose a standard
• EC2: Standard is not available in English
• EC3: Standard is not backed by a reputable entity
• EC4: Standard is platform specific
• IC1: Article proposes a standard about IoT security
• IC2: Standard is about consumer IoT device security
The initial pool of articles was first filtered by EC1, which proved to be a great
deal of articles. Then filtering away articles that were not in English and any
articles about standards that are not backed by any reputable organization or
entity, reduced the amount of articles further. Finally, all remaining standards
that were only designed for a specific platform, e.g. only for ARM based de-
vices, were also excluded. After applying all the exclusion criteria and then the
inclusion criteria on the remaining there was a handful of standards left. They
were then used for Snowballing in accordance with the works of Wohlin in [10],
which is a good way to discover relevant literature that did not turn up in the
initial searches. That resulted in an couple of more standards, since many of
the standards reference other standards.
2.1.3
Selection Process
This section details the process and reasoning for selecting the relevant stan-
dards from what was left in the pool of standards, identified in Section 2.1.2.

Two standards were selected based on the fact that they might become manda-
tory in the near future as that aligns with the thesis objectives. These standards
are ETSI EN 303 645 [11] and NISTIR 8259 [6], [12], [13]. Additionally, UL-
2900 [14] and the IoT Security Verification Standard [15] were selected for their

Interestingly, there was one framework that was referenced by most of the stan-

that was the IoT Security Compliance Framework (ISCF) from the IoT Security

An expert in the field of IoT Security Standards, Jeppe Pilgaard Bjerre from

agreed and helped gather additional sources of information with some of the
standards. He considered the EN 303 645 standard to be the most influential
and important for the future of IoT security. Additionally, he also confirmed
that the ISCF [16] is often considered to be some kind of pseudo standard, as it
was one of the first guidelines released about IoT security by a reputable entity.
For these reasons, the IoTSF is included in this literature review for the sake of
completeness.

2.2
Standards
This section aims to provide an overview of the standards that were selected
for further analysis in the previous section. It presents some background details

2.2.1
ETSI EN 303 645
The European Telecommunication Standards Institute (ETSI) is a European
Standards Organization established in 1988 by the European Conference of
Postal and Telecommunications Administrations after a proposal from the Eu-
ropean Commission. ETSI is responsible for the standardization of Information
and Communication Technologies (ICT) for the European Union (EU) [20].
ETSI EN 303 645 [11] is a European standard based on the technical specifica-
tion ETSI TS 103 645 [21]. The standard was released in June 2020. It specifies
high-level security and data protection provisions for consumer IoT devices, such
as connected children toys, IoT gateways, and connected appliances. It also has
some minor policy points that device manufacturers should set up.
The EU does not currently enforce this standard. It is up to individual member
states to decide if they will enforce it. Multiple countries are, reportedly, in
the process of doing so [22]. In accordance with the EU Cybersecurity Act, The
European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) could pick up this standard
and enforce it [23]. This is further supported by the statement from ETSI that
there has been significant uptake of the standard [24].
The standard consists of the following key requirements for consumer IoT de-
vices.
• No universal default passwords.
==PAGE:21==

• Implement a means to manage reports of vulnerabilities.
• Keep software updated.
• Securely store sensitive security parameters.
• Communicate securely.
• Minimize exposed attack surfaces.
• Ensure software integrity.
• Ensure that personal data is secure.
• Make systems resilient to outages.

2.2.1.1
ETSI TS 103 701
ETSI TS 103 701, v0.0.7, [25] is a draft document released in March of 2021.
It contains guidelines for how to assess if consumer IoT devices conform to
ETSI EN 303 645. A device that has been certified can then claim to be secure
according to the standard.
The compliance assessment with the document can be performed in-house, that
is by the company itself in order to self-certify.
Additionally, the document
can be used by a third party to certify that the device complies with the stan-
dard. Regardless, the entity performing the assessment is referred to as the Test
Laboratory (TL).
The assessment procedure starts by defining the Supplier Organization (SO).
The SO is usually the developer, manufacturer or vendor of the Device Under
Test (DUT). The DUT is the device that is being subjected to the assessment
on behalf of the SO.
The SO starts by completing the Implementation Conformance Statement (ICS),
which is a questionnaire which states the DUT’s features and capabilities. This
questionnaire is presented in Annex B in ETSI EN 303 645. That questionnaire
includes a table with a list of all the provisions in ETSI EN 303 645 and if the
provision is mandatory, recommended or conditional. The SO needs to fill out
==PAGE:22==
10
Literature Review
the table and state if and how they followed the provision. If they decide not
to implement a provision they have to provide a detailed rationale for that de-
cision. The same applies if the provision is conditional and the SO thinks that
the provision does not apply to the DUT.
The next step is for the SO to complete the Implementation eXtra Information
for Testing (IXIT). The IXIT contains additional information needed to perform
the assessment. It provides some design details to the TL and is the basis for the
gray-box testing methodology, which is where the tester has partial knowledge
of the internal structure of the DUT. The SO is allowed to provide references
to external documents to provide better context. The document needs to be
verified for completeness and consistency by the TL in coordination with the
SO before it is deemed acceptable.
The next step is for the TL to perform the assessment itself. It is up to the
TL to determine how the tests are designed and implemented. They are free to
use whichever methods they want as long as they go through all the test groups
provided in table C.1 in ETSI TS 103 701. Some of the test groups refer to best
practices, which are clearly stated to be non-exhaustive.
Finally after performing the assessment the TL will issue a verdict. The verdict
can be either ”Pass”, ”Fail” or ”Inconclusive”. To get a passing verdict all the
test criteria must have passed. To fail only a single criterion deems the DUT
a failure. An ”Inconclusive” verdict is given if none of the tests criteria have
failed and the TL deems that there was not enough detail provided in the IXIT
in order to perform the assessment.
The TL will also have to produce a document that shows their test methodology
and it needs to detailed enough to be reproducible [25].

2.2.1.2
Review
ETSI EN 303 645 is presented in a clear and precise manner. It focuses on a few
key requirements, each with more detailed provisions which aim to clarify the
requirement. The requirements are logical and tackle many of the most prevalent
issues plaguing consumer IoT devices. The upcoming compliance publication
indicates that the standard is still being worked on which is good for the future
of the standard. The standard does, however, have some drawbacks. The main
drawback is the standard’s lack of detail. Compliance with the standard might
need a significant amount of communication with the TL for clarifications. An
SO might need to begin the certification procedure multiple times because the
==PAGE:23==
2.2 Standards
11
Figure 2.1: Overview of ETSI TS 103 701 Assessment Procedure [25]
SO and TL interpret the provisions differently.
One aspect of the standard is especially troubling and that is the lack of hard-
ware requirements. There is no mandate or recommendation to use a hardware
root of trust for cryptography and the storage of credentials. It is mentioned
under one provision as an option without recommending it. This is probably
done in an attempt to not exclude any devices. The provisions in the standard
in its current form can be implemented retroactively in devices that have been
deployed, as long as they support software upgrades. This is probably by de-
sign. It would be unreasonable to release a standard that few currently deployed
devices could meet.
The standard is backed by the EU, which has a history of making consumer pro-
tection standards that over time become the de facto global standard. One such
example is the consumer device power efficiency regulation that mandated all
household appliances have an energy usage label on them. This has reportedly
reduced annual energy consumption by 33.5 TWh per year [26]. That is equiva-
lent to the annual energy consumption of Romania. It is therefore indisputable
that any EU regulations can have a big impact on any market.
Currently this standard is voluntary and it is up to the developers whether they
follow it or not. There are however some countries, (e.g. UK and Finland), that
have made this standard mandatory [22], [27]. This could change in the future
as the EU is reportedly working on introducing some of the requirements from

the standard into the Radio Equipment Directive (RED) [28], [29]. This was

The RED [29] establishes a regulatory framework for radio equipment for the

It is broad and encompasses a significant amount of connected

2.2.2.1
Review
The standard focuses mainly on pre-market activities such as identify expected
customers and define expected use cases. Some of those activities have no clear
reasoning behind them and are simply presented as a good idea or as a helpful
activity.
Furthermore, the provisions under those activities are mostly just
questions, such as should the device use a hardware based root of trust?, which
usually relies on a hardware security module. The standard names a few reasons
for that being a good idea with no clear requirement or recommendation to use
it.
The post-market activities use better reasoning when recommending the
provisions to developers, but it still has most of the same problems as the pre-
market requirements.
The first part of the standard, NISTIR 8259, does not have any requirements. It
just poses questions for the developer and explains why asking these questions
might be a good idea. No actual requirements are presented until the developer
explores the second published document, NISTIR 8259A, which presents the
requirements. The presented requirements also come with rationales and refer-
ences to other standards or frameworks on how to get more information on the
subject. The requirements and the rationale are good, but the requirements are
simply too few. There is a total of six requirements in that document. Of those
six, five refer to the ETSI standard and all six reference the IoTSF framework.
The NISTIR 8259B [33] draft makes up for some of the missing requirements in
the previous documents. It is only about policy recommendations and why they
are a good idea. Again, this document is too general and only has four main
requirements, further divided into subcategories. Because this document is still
a draft, the reference to other standards section is still empty. When NIST
publishes the final version of the document, it will most likely have references
to the ETSI standard for all the requirements.

The final two documents have no further requirements or recommendations.
The NIST 8259C [34] document aims to guide the users on using the previous

or provide many actionable examples. The document tries to make up for the
shortcomings of the previously mentioned documents. It mentions different fo-

The final document, NISTIR 8259D [35], is not useful for most organizations

The standard describes three different security levels. Each level is to describe

impact of someone compromising a smart light bulb is smaller than the same
individual being able to compromise a home security system. That means that
not all of the requirements in the list above apply to all the levels.
Level 1 requirements only target software vulnerabilities where an attacker is
not able to gain physical access to the device. This is only for devices where the
impact of a compromise would not have a high impact. These are devices that
store no sensitive information and where it is not possible to move laterally to

Another flaw with this standard is that it has limited policy requirements. Re-

other standards. The standard mainly focuses on the technological aspects of
security. This is a drawback since a big part of improving security are policy
changes [41].
In conclusion, the standard lacks some features but has nothing inherently
wrong. It lacks details regarding the compliance levels and leaves too much

2.3.2
Default Passwords
New IoT devices commonly use default username and password combinations,
such as ”admin, admin”. Default usernames and passwords are often not changed
by the user [43]. This could allow intruders to access devices and install mali-
cious software on them. The most famous example of this is the Mirai botnet,
which crippled parts of the internet in 2016 [4].
Most of the explored standards agree that this is unsatisfactory. Devices should
preferably force users to create a new username and password when the device
is first configured. Another acceptable method is to randomly generate unique
long passwords for each device. Being able to change the passwords is also rec-
ommended. Some standards such as ETSI EN 303 645 [11], say that networked
devices should implement rate limits on authentication attempts. NISTIR 8259
notably lacks a requirement for this.
2.3.3
Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) aims to improve security by requiring two
or more identification steps before granting access to a device or application. It
usually uses a single use generated code that needs to be provided in addition to a
standard password. By utilizing MFA, even when a user has an easily guessable
or reused a previously compromised password then an attacker can still not
gain access to a system. This is becoming increasingly more important as data
leaks are becoming more frequent. Attacks that use previously compromised
passwords are called credential stuffing [44], [45].
Of the explored standards only ETSI EN 303 645 mentions MFA as a way to
increase security, without presenting it as mandatory.

2.3.4
Input Validation
Validating the legitimacy of any input fields has been a long standing issue with
all kinds of devices and applications. It is been on the OWASP top ten list for
many years as one of the most common security vulnerabilities in the world [46].
An attacker will try and input some malicious input that will get the program
to do something unwanted. This is usually some code snippet that the program
will then execute. To prevent this all input fields need to be sanitized. This
is needed for both human interfaces and Application Programming Interfaces
(APIs).
All of the standards agree on this. They only vary slightly on the details but
otherwise their requirements are identical.
2.3.5
Secure Communications
When it comes to communicating sensitive data, it is important to do so in a
secure manner. That is where encryption comes in. All of the standards make
some kind of requirements on securing communications. Some are vague and
just require that devices communicate securely, such as both NISTIR 8259 and
UL 2900-1. The rest of the standards require that all traffic is encrypted, except
information that is in no way sensitive. An example of such information is the
temperature from a temperature sensor. In most situations, that information
could not be classified as sensitive.
2.3.6
Privilege Separation
A simple way to prevent the total compromise of a system is to have different
access levels. Services are then only given the minimum system permissions
they require to function. For example, a service that checks the weather forecast
should only be able to access the internet and should not be able to access any
files on the system. When a service gets compromised, the attacker can only
access a small part of the system. This prevents them from making changes to
big parts of the system, thus limiting the impact. This is similar to not allowing
users to have administrator or root access to systems.
All the standards agree that this is a requirement to have on systems that can
support it. IoTSF even specifies it as mandatory for class one devices.

2.3.7
Data Protection
It is important to store data securely, especially when it comes to the storage of
PII. The best way to ensure that the data cannot be extracted from the device
is to encrypt it. This helps against both software vulnerabilities and physical
attacks. For this to be effective, only the best and most up to date cryptographic
methods should be used.
All the standards agree on this. They do, however, vary slightly. Some place
more emphasis on Trusted Execution Environment (TEE) and other hardware
based methods of securing data. Others focus more on the protection of encryp-
tion keys. It is only NISTIR 8259 that lacks some details in regard to how the
data should be encrypted as it has no requirements for data encryption.
2.3.8
Personal Data Management
Devices need to be General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) compliant to
be legal in the EU. In broad terms, personal data should only be collected and
processed with the consumers consent. The user needs to opt-in to any personal
data collection. They should also be able to revoke their acceptance and also
delete their data at any time [47].
Most of the standards agree on this, except NISTIR 8259. However, they have
a generic requirement that states that all devices must follow local regulations.
That means that all devices sold in the EU need to comply with GDPR to meet
the standard. It is also important for devices to comply with GDPR from a
purely financial perspective, as a violation can result in a fine of 4% of global
company revenue [47].
2.3.9
Logging Security Events
It is important to be able to review incidents after the fact. Therefore it is
critical to be able to go through log files. This is especially important for security
events, such as logins. Furthermore, some kinds of log files can be helpful for the
manufacturer. These logs can help the manufacturer identify abnormalities that
could indicate a potential malware infection in multiple devices. They can then
send out updates to fix any detected vulnerabilities before becoming a nuisance.
That is why it is good to send anonymized data to the manufacturer. Both the
ETSI and NIST standards agree on this requirement. Additionally, they also
mandate that all types of collected telemetry data are clarified and justified to
the user. The user should also be able to turn off all types of telemetry data to

comply with GDPR.
2.3.10
Hardware Based Security
Cryptography is useless if the cryptographic keys are easily accessible.
It is
therefore paramount that they are stored in a safe and secure manner. The best
way to do that is to use specialized security hardware that is tamper resistant.
Extracting secrets from such hardware is difficult or, at least, requires extremely
specialized equipment and significant time.
UL 2900-1 is the only standard that does not mention any kind of hardware
security. The ETSI and NIST standards only mention Secure Boot as an exam-
ple of hardware based security systems and do not require it. The OWASP and
IoTSF standards go into more detail and require hardware security for devices
at certain compliance levels. Those compliance levels require that cryptographic
keys and functions utilize special security hardware such as Trusted Platform
Modules (TPMs) and Secure Access Modules (SAMs). The security hardware
should then be utilized to cryptographically verify the firmware before it is
started. Each stage in the boot sequence should verify the next before launch-
ing it.
Additionally, they require that the bootloader be stored in one-time
programmable memory. If any abnormality in the startup sequence is detected,
the startup should be limited to notifying the user of possible malicious activity.
2.3.11
Minimize Attack Surface
Gaining access to a system requires an entrance. The same applies to malicious
actors. They need to find a vulnerability somewhere in order to gain access.
For this reason, the majority of the standards require disabling of all the non-
essential services and ports. The fewer doors or ports on a system, the fewer
ways a malicious actor could gain entrance. Only the UL 2900-1 standard lacks
any requirements for this. The other standards vary in their strictness. ETSI
EN 303 645, for example, also requires that all hardware ports and interfaces
that are not uses should also be disabled. That means interfaces such as serial
ports need to be disabled.
2.3.12
Vulnerability Disclosure
Companies that create and develop IoT devices should implement a vulnerability
disclosure framework. They should have a policy for accepting vulnerability

disclosures.
These vulnerabilities should then be fixed in a timely manner.
This will help security researchers that find vulnerabilities in devices report
them to the company and help the company fix it. These policies also let the
security researchers know that it is alright to do security audits and checks on the
company’s devices. Many researchers are often scared to report vulnerabilities
they stumble across because they fear the company will sue them.
Most of the standards agree that this kind of policy is required in order to secure
IoT devices. The only exception is UL 2900-1. However, that is not notable due
to the lack of any policy requirements in the standard. NISTIR 8259 also does
not stress the importance of this policy requirement. It only mentions it in an
example for how to plan for the adequate support of customer needs.
2.3.13
Software Bill of Materials (SBOM)
In most modern systems, the amount of libraries and software frameworks is
often significant [48]. If there is a vulnerability found in any of the libraries in
use on a device it can cause the device to become vulnerable. Therefore, it is
important to keep track of what libraries and systems are in use on a device.
The version of the library is also very important. A SBOM is a good way to
keep track of that. It can simply be a list in a document. A more effective way
would be to sign up for notifications in multiple Common Vulnerabilities and
Exposures (CVE) databases. When a new vulnerability is reported for a library,
the team responsible for the device can get right to work updating the library
on the device. This will hopefully prevent any abuses of the vulnerability.
Most of the standards agree that this is required for the device to be considered
secure. Only IoTSF does not mention it at all. Otherwise, all the standards
agree with varying levels of details provided.
2.3.14
Lifetime support policy
It is obvious that no device is designed to last forever. Similarly, no device should
be supported forever. A support period is how long the device’s manufacturer
pledges to update the software of the device. This is most important when it
comes to updates that fix vulnerabilities. As such, consumers need to know how
long the device will remain secure. After the support period ends, the consumer
should replace the device.
All the standards agree on this, except OWASP ISVS. That should not be taken
significantly as that standard has almost no policy requirements, compared to
the other standards.

2.3.15
Incident Response Policy
An Incident response policy is a policy that clarifies the responsibilities of peo-
ple within a company in the event of an incident. This usually refers to a newly
discovered vulnerability. This kind of policy can prove invaluable when a vul-
nerability is discovered that is actively being exploited. In such a case each
minute can prove critical. It is therefore good to have an action plan ready. In-
terestingly the ISCF from the IoTSF has a significant amount of requirements
that normally fall under an incident response policy, but they simply refer to
them as something that should be part of a security policy. The other standards
do not mention this type of policy by name.
2.3.16
Risk Assessments
Risk assessments are a good way to determine the potential dangers to a product
or system. In this context, it mainly applies to developers conducting these
assessments on the impact if various systems are compromised.
Each standard requires some sort of risk assessment, except NISTIR 8259. The
OWASP ISVS also does not have strict risk assessment requirements. That is
mostly because that standard lacks many common policy requirements found
in other standards. Additionally, some standards even require that each device
needs a risk assessment based on the environment, such as UL 2900-1.
2.3.17
Penetration testing
Penetration testing is a simulated cyberattack, where penetration testers use
common methods and tools used by malicious actors.
Penetration tests are
tremendously useful and can result in the discovery of novel vulnerabilities. It
is one of the most extreme forms of security auditing as it can take an exceedingly
long time to complete. It will also require a professional penetration tester or a
company that specializes in it. Interestingly it is required by a single standard,
UL 2900-1. That is understandable because of the cost and time requirements
of such an endeavor. This is a measure that should only be taken if the security
of the device is critical.

2.3.18
Compliance
When it comes to standards, it is important to be able to comply with them in
an easy manner. Therefore, it is good to have some frameworks for claiming to
be in compliance. Some standards have a very detailed and good procedure to
do this, for example, ETSI EN 303 645. Their approach is extremely detailed
and leaves no doubt on how to proceed for the SO. Other standards do not have
such a good procedure. This is true for the NISTIR and OWASP standards. On
the other hand, the UL 2900-1 standard has no information on how to proceed
with compliance with a third party.
2.3.19
Compliance Classes
There is a wide variety of consumer IoT devices available on the market. They
differ significantly in capabilities and design. Therefore, it would make sense
that there would be different levels of requirements for each type of device. For
example, the impact of a compromised smart light bulb and a smart lock would
clearly be different. A compromised light bulb would only be able to toggle
the light, while a compromised lock could cause a house to be unlocked for a
malicious entity.
This is what compliance classes are designed to target. They provide a way
for standards to apply to multiple different types of devices based on their use
and capabilities. This feature is included in the two more detailed standards,
OWASP and IoTSF. The other standards do not include this. However, the
other standards could also have used it because they currently have provisions
that some constrained devices do not have to comply with. The definition of a
constrained device is not very clear either. It could lead to a situation where a
manufacturer manages to falsely convince the certifier that their device should
be tested as a constrained device. That way, they would not have to comply
with the more stringent requirements.
2.4
Discussion
Having looked through all the standards, it has become clear that they more
or less align. Implementing one standard should not prevent anyone from also
implementing another standard as there are no contradictions. Additionally,
most of them have the same key requirements and yet none of them have all
of them. This means that no single standard can be adopted that would then
automatically cover all the requirements in the other standards.

plans for regulations come to pass, is the ETSI EN 303 645 standard.
If a
company is just looking to comply with a single standard then that would be

Chapter 3
Proposed Framework
While demand for manufactured goods continues to increase globally, many
manufacturing companies aim to expand in multiple regions simultaneously. In
this context, it is crucial for such companies to ensure that regulations do not
threaten this expansion. This can cause companies to have to examine and re-
view all the regulations and relevant standards for each of their potential future
markets, which would cause them a significant amount of work before bringing
a device to the market.
If some new regulation came out that would force them to comply with a cer-
tain standard and they had decided not to do the research, they would have to
redesign their device. Otherwise, they would not be able to sell their device in
that region. Redesign and any kind of change to an existing product can cause
a chain reaction where one change leads to another. Each step of the manufac-
turing process might need to be changed, which can be incredibly expensive at
times. Such an approach might lead the company to choose to comply with ev-
ery reported and upcoming IoT security standard if they think that will be less
expensive. That might still not work as some regulations could unexpectedly
appear shortly after the device has entered the production phase.
In the context of the challenges explained above, IoT device developers could
benefit from a framework that will help them navigate this multitude of stan-
dards. A framework that allows developers to comply with all the key require-
ments of the major standards while maintaining flexibility.
This framework
==PAGE:50==
38
Proposed Framework
makes it easy for companies to adapt to any new standards and also upgrade
deployed devices. It will allow the developers to easily maintain the security of
any deployed devices.
Additionally, this framework does not need to have specific requirements for
different types of devices as it only focuses on non-constrained consumer IoT
devices. Therefore, complexity is reduced and readability increased. It could
even get more organizations to secure their devices as the barrier to entry has
been lowered.
The framework uses the key requirements identified in the previous chapter as
the foundation of the requirements it will present. It is broken up into four
sections based on the type of requirement, Software, Hardware, Policies and
Compliance. The order of requirements is the same within each section as the
order in the comparison table in the previous chapter (Table 2.3).
3.1
Software
Most systems use some kind of software framework and a multitude of libraries
for their operation [48]. It is important to consider what external software is
chosen for a new system as all parts of a system need to be secure. Each part
of the system needs to be analyzed for vulnerabilities and failure to do so can
lead to compromise and device hijacking. The flexibility of software is also its
biggest weak point and it needs be configured securely to prevent attackers from
taking advantage of it.
3.1.1
Software Updates
It is extremely hard to make perfect code. The chance that there is some bug
increases exponentially the larger the code base is. This is especially true for
code that uses external libraries for some functionality, the external libraries
often being open source. These libraries are often not looked at by the device
developers and are simply trusted to work and expected to be secure. If a vul-
nerability is found in such a library after the device has been sent to customers,
it is important that it can be fixed as soon as possible. Otherwise, if the library
is popular among many IoT device manufacturers then it could be a tempting
target for malicious actors. The attackers would then be able to possibly cre-
ate malware that could infect hundreds or thousands of devices. These update
features should be present on all internet connected devices. If this is not done
a widespread vulnerability could make it trivial for attackers to create a huge
==PAGE:51==
3.1 Software
39
botnet that could threaten large parts of the internet.
The key to flexible software is the ability to update software.
It allows the
developers to retroactively make deployed devices comply to any new standards.
It is also crucial when it comes to fixing any discovered vulnerabilities or bugs.
That is why it is critical to be able to update software, firmware and as many
parts of an IoT device as possible. That allows the device developers to fix
almost everything. There are of course some limits to what can be remotely
updated, such as boot loaders and other low level firmware.
Requirements
The device needs to have an update mechanism that can update all high-level
code as well as firmware. It should regularly check for any updates and imme-
diately apply any security related updates. Additionally, any update procedure
needs to be secure as it would be non-optimal if malicious actors could leverage
the update procedure to infect devices. That is why the device manufacturer or
developer must cryptographically sign all updates. This signature then needs to
be verified by the device immediately before applying the update. The update
file should also be encrypted in transit to prevent malicious actors from inter-
cepting them and gaining some insight from that which they could abuse. The
update procedure also needs to be robust and be able to handle failed updates.
If an update fails due to, for example, power loss the device should be able to
recover from that automatically. It should always have a backup partition that
it will boot into that in the event of a failed update. A known good backup
should always be stored on that partition. The device should only boot up into
this backup in the event of a failed update. Otherwise, this feature could be
abused by malicious actors to force the device to boot up into an older version
of the software that could have some vulnerabilities. The device should always
restart the update process if it boots into the backup software.
3.1.2
Default Passwords & MFA
Almost all devices that can contain some sensitive data have some sort of authen-
tication mechanism to protect it. That is the simplest way to protect systems.
However, this method is only as strong as the password. It is not enough to
only have strong passwords, they also need to be unique to prevent, for ex-
ample, an attack commonly referred to as credential stuffing. That is when a
malicious actor looks up the email address of owner of the device in previous
data breaches. There are lists available on the internet where anyone can search
==PAGE:52==
40
Proposed Framework
for email addresses and see if any password has been present in a data breach.
If the device owner tends to reuse passwords on multiple services then there is
a decent chance an attacker can find their commonly used passwords and use
them to login into the device. This practice of reusing passwords is done by
43-51% [49] of people online.
Other measures that improve security are MFA mechanisms. Common MFA
approaches require a user to type in a secondary code after successfully au-
thenticating with their username and password. This secondary code is often
generated in the form of a one time code that is generated from a seed and is
based on the date and time. This approach is commonly used by many large
websites and services, such as Google. They, for example, prevent credential
stuffing attacks, because they force the attacker to provide, for example, an
ever changing code that is generated on the user’s phone.
Additional measures that should be used to protect all types of authentication
mechanisms are rate limits. It is an effective way to defend against brute forcing
attacks. Brute force attacks is where an attacker tries random passwords or
passwords from a list of common passwords in the hope of one of them being
correct. This attack is usually automated and tries hundreds or thousands of
passwords per second. Rate limits usually disable a certain IP address from
trying to authenticate after a certain number of failed attempts, often three or
five. This IP address is then unblocked after a time limit, such as 30 minutes.
This time limit is critical for legitimate users that have simply forgotten their
password and want to be able to log into their device again. Rate limits are also
an effective way of dealing with the excessive resource strain which these kinds
of attacks can cause on a system.
Requirements
To have an acceptable level of password security, all default passwords should
be randomly generated. Randomly generated passwords are the most secure as
they will be almost impossible to guess based on word lists or common pass-
words.
Additionally, all new passwords that users change should be subject
to password strength tests with a minimum length as well as requirements for
upper and lower case characters.
There are existing tools such as password
strength checkers that are purpose built for this use case [50]. Recently some
password strength checkers have also started to check if the password the user
is creating is present in any public data leaks. The checker will then inform
the user that their password has been present in one or more data leaks. They
might even prevent the user from using the password entirely [44]. The best
known service that offers this is have i been pwned [51].

Lastly, no passwords should ever be stored in plaintext. The only acceptable
way to store passwords are with cryptographically secure hashing functions that
also use salting. This prevents malicious actors from gaining easy access user’s
credentials in the event of a data breach. It also slows any attackers down sig-
nificantly if they can extract the firmware of a device but cannot gain access to
the device itself.
3.1.3
Input Validation
Many systems allow users to type in their configuration settings and other cus-
tom information, such as the name of the user. These input fields can be abused
by typing in malicious strings that can result in code execution or other unin-
tended behavior. One of the more common attacks that abuse this, is called
SQL injection. That is when an attacker uses malicious input to execute SQL
queries and it can be used to extract information that was meant to be secret or
alter some information. These kinds of alterations can result in leaked credential
hashes that can then be brute forced on powerful computers. These credentials
can then be used to gain access to other parts of the system.
In some cases, insufficient input validation or sanitation can result in code ex-
ecution. If an attacker manages to do that, then they are often able to create
some kind of back door, which can then be used to move through the system.
Their goal at this point will be to gain further access to the system by searching
for vulnerabilities that allow them to perform any kind of privilege escalation
attack. They could abuse other insufficient input sanitation mechanisms, per-
haps on services that are not exposed to any user and are only accessible from
within the system.
Requirements
To defend against the aforementioned vulnerabilities, there needs to be input
validation and sanitation on all services that allow for user input, on both ex-
ternal and internal services. Sanitizing user input is relatively simple. The set
of required characters for each field should be determined, then only those char-
acters should be allowed. For example, if the field is for the user to enter their
phone number, then every character that is not a number should be discarded
before the string is processed. This could be done with Regular Expressions [52].
This should then be repeated for all other forms and input fields based on the
type of information requested.
Special care needs to be taken with APIs, as they can also be a type of input

field. Therefore, the same sanitation and validation needs to be done there as is
done with normal user fields. Additionally, the method or library that is used
to deserialize, for example, a JSON string should also be carefully checked for
these types of vulnerabilities.
3.1.4
Secure Communications
Keeping all communications to and from the gateway secure is instrumental in
keeping it secure. That is where encryption comes in, it is used to keep commu-
nication private and safe. It can protect from Man in the Middle (MitM) attacks
and prevent attackers from seeing sensitive data going to and from the gateway.
A lack of proper encryption could lead to attackers gaining access to some PII
or could reveal sensitive information about the user’s habits. Knowledge about
the user’s habits could alert the attackers to when the users are not present on
the premises and could allow them to select better times for, for example, break
ins.
Additionally, an attacker could be able to gain access to the local network
through poorly configured WiFi. Therefore, all the local communication should
be encrypted as it prevents local attacks from being able to intercept any user
credentials and gaining access that way.
Requirements
To prevent all these potential attacks the system needs to use up to date cryp-
tography in its encryption and only use standard protocols such as Transport
Layer Security (TLS) 1.2. Additionally, the system needs to disable any out-
dated protocols that are or could be vulnerable, such as TLS1 and TLS1.1, and
all communication protocols that have an encrypted alternative should use the
encrypted variant. Furthermore, the cryptography should be upgradeable, i.e.
a software update should be able to upgrade the protocols used. This should
be done if a vulnerability is found in one of the protocols. Additionally, the
protocol should be disabled, if possible, until a fix has been pushed.
These standard, up to date, protocols should be used for all external communi-
cation over the internet. Additionally, the system should do the same with any
local management and API interface. Having to use a self-signed certificate is
better than having no encryption as clients will be notified if the certificate has
changed in between connections.

3.1.5
Privilege Separation
Devices often run a plethora of services which do independent and dissimilar
tasks. These services require access to different peripherals and access levels.
Some might only need access to the internet, such as a weather forecasting ser-
vice, while others might need to be able to store some data and change user
permissions. Separating the services into discrete units makes it harder for an
attacker to gain complete control over a device if they manage to gain access to
a single part of the system. This means that an attacker can only get the same
access level as the service that they managed to breach.
This separation forces the attacker to move laterally on a system and try to
breach a different service that has more permissions than the initially compro-
mised one. This requires the attacker to succeed in finding multiple vulner-
abilities on a single device which is probably less likely than finding a single
one.
Requirements
All services should utilize the principle of least privilege. That means that each
service should only have the absolute minimum permissions that they require to
function properly. This is often easiest to implement by creating a user account
for each service and only giving that user account the privileges that the specific
service needs to function. Make sure that no service is running with the top
level privileges, such as root, if at all possible. Additionally, no service users
should be allowed to login, as that would create more avenues of entry.
3.1.6
Personal Data Management & Protection
Data is quickly becoming one of world’s most valuable commodity [53].
It
allows retailers to precisely target individuals or small groups with just the right
advertisements that could get them to buy their product. That is one of the
reasons why malicious actors have been increasingly targeting companies and
services that might contain large amounts of user data and PII. The attacker
could then sell any information to legitimate data brokers or more sinister data
brokers. Malicious actors have increasingly been using PII to tailor their scams,
such as phishing, to the target in order to steal from them or gain access to
systems.
All these factors mean that all kinds of data, but especially PII, is increasingly
becoming a target.
==PAGE:56==
44
Proposed Framework
It is not only malicious actors that could cause damage to an organization or
company in the event of a data breach. Fines imposed by regulatory entities by
way of their national and international regulations can cause financial damage.
The most well known is probably GDPR. It took effect in 2018 and requires that
any organization that collects data about users in the EU or European Economic
Area (EEA) keeps that data secure. The regulation mentions some additional
measures that mostly relate to the collection and processing of personal data. If
GDPR is violated, a fine can be imposed of up to 4% of global annual revenue
of the company or organization.
Additionally, GDPR puts big emphasis on the management of personal data
and consent when it comes to any data collection [47].
Requirements
All types of PII need to be stored encrypted and kept separated from any pro-
cesses or services that do not require access to it, similar to the requirements
in Section 3.1.5. When it comes to the GDPR, it states that adequate pro-
tective measures must be taken to safeguard PII. However, what is considered
adequate protection in the GDPR is not defined and the description is rather
vague. Therefore, the best course of action is to follow standards from the EU,
such as ETSI EN 303 645, when it comes to that. The main requirements are
that all types of data should be encrypted at rest, that is when the data is saved
to a persistent storage medium. Similarly, all communication containing PII
should be encrypted in transit, as was mentioned in Section 3.1.4.
Additionally, all PII collection should be strictly necessary and the user needs
to opt in to it. The users must also be able to opt out of any data collection
and should be able to withdraw consent at any time. Therefore, They should
be able to delete any PII on the device and also be able to reset the device to
factory settings.
3.1.7
Logging Security Events
No devices are perfect and they sometimes do strange and unexpected things.
Then, it is invaluable to be able to check what was going on in the device after
the fact. This is what logs are meant to do. They will periodically log the
state of the system along with any unexpected behavior. When it comes to the
security of devices, these unexpected behaviors can hint that something is not
right. Malware often leaves some traces and causes some disruptions, especially
when the malware is trying to gain further access to a device as it might be
trying to cause some issues with the currently running services which it can
==PAGE:57==

abuse.
Requirements
These logs are of limited use if they are only accessible from the device itself.
Therefore, any abnormal logs should be anonymized and sent to the developer’s
server. They should then monitor those logs for any abnormalities that could
indicate if there was malware present. They could then be the first to detect
if any malware could be targeting their devices. They would then be able to
start working on a patch that could fix the vulnerability and then send out an
update to all the devices, hopefully before much damage is done.
3.1.8
Minimize Attack Surface
Most new systems are based, at least, somewhat on old systems and that can
cause unneeded software to be included by default. This software often comes
in the form of services that are often enabled by default on, for example, Linux.
These services are often used during development and are therefore not disabled
initially.
Some of them could even have been turned on specially to aid in
development.
These services result in an increased attack surface as each additional service
means a potential extra point of entry. The chance of an unknown vulnerability
being able to affect a system naturally increases with each service running on
the system.
Requirements
To prevent this from happening, all the running services on the device need
to be carefully evaluated, to determine if they are necessary. All services that
are not strictly required for the operation of the device should be disabled and
preferably removed. Additionally, all services that are not required for the initial
setup and configuration of the device should be disabled until a user enables it.

3.2
Hardware
It is particularly tough to change hardware designs after the device has been
manufactured. The hardware requirements should therefore be considered early
in the design phase. When choosing the hardware of a new device, one of the
biggest concerns is the price of all the components. This is especially impor-
tant for constrained devices that are released in a competitive market segment.
However, consumer IoT gateways are not constrained devices and are usually
more expensive than simple sensors. Therefore, it is worth adding some extra
hardware to improve the security of the ecosystem as the relative price of the
gateways should not change much [54].
If the hardware of the device is not secure then the software that runs on top
of it can hardly be considered secure.
3.2.1
Minimize Attack Surface
Making a device harder to breach can be difficult. When an adversary gains
physical access to a device, one of their first actions is to disassemble the device
and extract any useful information. Information such as the type of computer
chip used and if the layout of the Printed Circuit Board (PCB) can tell them
anything.
Many devices also use some kind of communication protocols to
communicate with other board components. PCBs often have debug ports when
the device is being tested. This allows the developers to troubleshoot if there
are any issues with the device. Similar ports are often present on boards to flash
the chip. This is useful during the development of the device.
The problem arises when the devices are deployed to a user or customer while
still having these debug ports.
These ports can give a malicious actor easy
access to different parts of the device. The ports are sometimes even marked
clearly such that the malicious actor does not even need to guess the function
of a debug port or pad.
Requirements
An easy way to prevent easy access to device hardware is to remove all unnec-
essary ports and pads. If any pads are needed to, for example, initially flash
the device with the requisite firmware they need to be unlabeled.
This will
increase the amount of work needed to gather information about the device and
potentially discourage some malicious attackers.
Additionally, all debug options need to be turned off for common interfaces such
==PAGE:59==
3.2 Hardware
47
as Joint Test Action Group (JTAG). It can be used to extract the firmware of
a device and it can also be used for hardware debugging along with memory
extraction. That could result in the compromise of any private keys as well
as user credentials. JTAG can be turned off on most Integrated Circuits (ICs)
and after doing that, the only way to re-enable it is to flash new firmware on
the chip. This would overwrite any existing firmware and thus removing any
sensitive information. The JTAG interface can also sometimes only be enabled
through hardware changes, e.g. if certain pins are bridged. Those pins or pads
can often be resoldered to re-enable the interface and should therefore not be
considered adequate protection.
Other common vulnerable ports include a serial interface that gives access to
the command line interface. Access to that interface can allow an attacker to try
brute force attacks. Sometimes, the serial interface is only configured to allow
for serial communications from the device. This will often output any debug
messages (e.g. from the kernel) on the device and can give an attacker valuable
information. These types of ports need to be disabled in software.
3.2.2
Secure Communications
Most IoT devices consist of multiple ICs or chips that all have different features
and tasks. All these chips need to be able to communicate to be useful. There
is a plethora of protocols for these kinds of on-board communications. However,
most of them have either minimal or no security at all. Any new device can join
a bus and listen to all the communications happening on that bus. That means
that if any secret information such as any authentication information is being
sent on that bus then an attacker can easily see that. They can then possibly
abuse that information by imitating the device or snooping on all future network
traffic that is encrypted with that sensitive information.
Requirements
Therefore, all on-board communications that can include sensitive data should
be encrypted.
Some communications are, by their nature, not secret, such
as temperature readings.
On the other hand, some communications should
always only send encrypted information, such as any communications to a data
storage medium. This can be done in multiple ways, such as storing encrypted
information and only decrypting it during runtime.

3.2.3
Hardware Based Security
Hardware based security can prove to be invaluable when it comes to the security
of IoT devices. They can protect the device software from unwanted modifica-
tions and can also make sure only trusted code is executed. Additionally, they
are often designed with hardware attacks in mind, i.e. glitching attacks that are
meant to extract sensitive information. It is also harder to hijack the operations
of hardware, than software, for malicious purposes as hardware is less flexible
and often limited to a single purpose.
Security hardware can also be used to verify the integrity of the device, which
could then prevent any modified, and potentially compromised, devices from
launching.
Requirements
To utilize hardware based security correctly, the device needs to have special-
ized security hardware that is tamper resistant. This should preferably be built
into the chip, if that is possible for the device design, otherwise it needs to use
external ICs.
A TPM is a type of hardware component that is critical in keeping any system
secure as it is used both for securing cryptographic keys and verifying device
integrity [55]. It should be used in all the devices in the scope of this framework
and should be used in conjunction with secure boot, which allows only autho-
rized code to boot. The bootloader also needs to be stored in read only storage
so that it cannot be compromised [56]–[58].
3.3
Policies
Policies are an essential part of the security ecosystem. They often help maintain
the correct security mindset and many of them have been shown to increase the
security awareness in a company [41]. There are many policies that are directly
related to security and the responsibilities related to these policies. They all
have one thing in common, and that is that they all contribute to a better
security environment.

3.3.1
Vulnerability Disclosure Framework
When individuals in the ever increasing community of international security re-
searchers come across vulnerabilities in devices and services that they are using,
they will want to report the vulnerability to the owner of the device or service.
The problem is, that many security researchers worry about what the reaction
of the company will be. Some security researchers have been sued because they
reported vulnerabilities.
The solution to this is to make a public vulnerability disclosure policy that
is available on the company website. The policy details how the vulnerability
reporting process works and it also shows the security researchers that the com-
pany is willing to accept vulnerability reports and that the company will not sue
the researchers. Having a list of previous findings by security researchers, along
with their name, should also encourage others to report any reports they find. It
could also make them more inclined to test the company’s devices and services
in the future again. Some companies have even started offering financial rewards
for finding vulnerabilities with bug bounty programs, such as the one HackerOne
runs for multiple companies. Setting up such a system allows these companies
to use freelance experts at a fraction of the cost of hiring them directly. Ad-
ditionally, for every bug that is found and paid out, there might be countless
attempts from other security researchers that did not find anything [59]. As
only 13% of manufacturers have such a scheme, the majority of manufacturers
are missing out on the immense value given by such a scheme [27].
Requirements
The company responsible for the device needs to have a Vulnerability Disclo-
sure policy in place. It should detail how security researcher should contact
them regarding vulnerabilities and detail how the process works at that specific
company. All claims should be investigated that come through that contact
email and the researcher should be notified of the fact. The researcher should
also be notified when the issue has been fixed and preferably given credit for
the finding on the company website, if they want it [60]. Companies are also
advised to use the Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure (CVD) framework [61]
and also read through the recommendations from the IoTSF [62] for additional
recommendations and standardized processes. The CVD is a framework that
contains a set of processes for dealing with reports of vulnerabilities and their
remediation [61]. The framework is standardized by International Organization
for Standardization (ISO) and is used successfully by many large companies
around the world [11].

3.3.2
Software Bill of Materials (SBOM)
Most modern software projects use multiple third party libraries. The practice
has increased over the last few decades and significantly more software develop-
ers do it now [48]. This trend follows the increased availability and prevalence
of open source software. Keeping track of all these different components can
be a daunting task. Additionally, limited oversight can cause problems with
licensing as many open source software libraries have stringent requirements for
commercial use [65]. Having limited oversight over the different components of
any software can prove dangerous [65]. This is especially true for software that
is expected to have a long lifetime as some issues with component libraries will
eventually arise. Critical vulnerabilities can be detected in any of the compo-
nents which could lead to the entire software package becoming vulnerable.
Requirements
This framework requires that all software that is used by IoT devices has a list
of some kind, with a breakdown of what component libraries are used and in
which product. It also needs to include a version number. This requirement
is not limited to third party components, but also any components that could
be used across multiple devices or projects. Managing such a list can become
inconvenient as the amount of projects and the size of the code base increases.
Many dependency management systems allow their users to export most, if
not all, included libraries and components.
Using tools such as that is the
recommended way to produce these lists.
An automated system should be setup so that it periodically checks if any items
on the list have had any published vulnerabilities. There are multiple ways to
implement this as no single system fits all use cases. As an example, a system
could use the API that is available from NIST [66]. Another method could make
use of third party services that notify when a vulnerable component is detected
based on the supplied list. Most vulnerabilities are assigned a CVE name. It

3.3 Policies

allows people and computers to better track and exchange information about
the vulnerability.
When a vulnerability is detected, the developers responsible for the affected
devices should take a look at the vulnerability and try and determine the impact
of it. A good indicator of that is the Common Vulnerability Scoring System
(CVSS). That is a score that is given to a CVE by security analysts and it
is based on the impact and severity based on multiple factors. This score is,
however, not sufficient to determine the impact on a product such as an IoT
device. It depends on multiple factors such as where the software component is
used and how. Some exploits, for example, depend on specially crafted inputs
that can circumvent the input validation in the component. That kind of exploit
is likely not critical if the component only inputs from trusted sources.
This variability means that each vulnerability needs to be individually assessed
in order to determine how urgent it is to fix it.
All detected vulnerabilities should always be fixed at a reasonable pace. Some
vulnerabilities are discovered in places where they could not be utilized by any
external malicious actors. This is however not a good reason to leave them in
place, because they could be used by malicious actors for privilege escalation.
A malicious actor could gain access to systems on the device that they were not
meant to access, through some unknown vulnerability. Then, use that access to
gain access to the unpatched vulnerability and leverage that to gain more access
to the system. In summary, they could use a minor unknown vulnerability and
then leverage a known vulnerability that was deemed safe to gain complete
control of a device.
3.3.3
Lifetime Support Policy
It is a given that devices have a limited lifetime, but the lifetime from one device
to another can wary significantly. Additionally, some products are considered
obsolete after a few years and the company will no longer support them. This is
especially important when it comes to security updates as they tell the consumer
for how long they can reliably believe the device to be secure. The support
lifetime can also vary for each field. Some devices may receive feature updates,
where additional features are added or improved, for a couple of years. After this
initial period of support they will stop receiving these feature updates and will
then for a few more years only receive security and bug fix updates. Knowing
how long the device will receive updates helps consumers take informed decisions
and it will help them plan for the future.

Proposed Framework
Requirements
Information about the intended support life of each product should be pub-
lished.
It should clearly state for how long the device will receive updates,
with particular emphasis on security updates. If this information has not been
decided when the product launches, a statement about the minimum support

3.3.4
Incident Response
When a vulnerability or any kind of security incident happens it can be difficult
to figure out what to do in the heat of the moment. The stress level can be
high when a vulnerability has been reported, especially if it is being actively
exploited. This can lead to confusion and incorrect decisions. Other scenarios
can also be causing downtime or system unreliability, such as a Denial of Service
(DoS) attack. Having common scenarios and mitigation strategies thought out
beforehand can prove invaluable. Having clear mitigation strategies can reduce
the amount of time that needs to be spent on research during an incident that
could be better spent on mitigation.
Requirements
For these reasons, having an Incident Response policy is required. It should
include a few common scenarios, such as what to do in the event of DoS attack
on any central servers. Other time critical scenarios are a must, such as scenarios
where an actively exploited vulnerability is discovered. That type of scenario is
one where each minute of indecision can result in the compromise of multiple
other devices.
Each scenario should have a contact person listed that will be the main person
responsible for that type of scenario and that person should be among the most
knowledgeable about it.

3.4
Compliance
To increase the usefulness of the framework, it is important to have a way to
validate that the requirements are met. This section details the steps necessary
to verify the compliance to the framework. It will highlight a method that is
widely used in the industry to find security vulnerabilities and how that can be
used to check for compliance. This method will allow companies to focus on
what they do best and allow them to use expertise that already exists.
3.4.1
Penetration Testing
Penetration testing, or pentesting, as it is often referred to as, is a commonly
used tool to check the security of a system and identify vulnerabilities. It usu-
ally involves using methods that are typically used by malicious attackers to
compromise systems. They are often performed by third parties that have no
prior knowledge of the system that is being tested and they have to perform the
attack without any inside knowledge of the system. The pentester will then have
to operate solely on the information that they are able to gather themselves.
This is often referred to as black box testing. Alternatively, the tester can do
gray or white box pentesting, where gray box testing allows the tester to have
some inside knowledge of the system and white box testing is when the tester
has full knowledge of the system [67], [68].
Finding flaws in a system can be difficult for developers that have been work-
ing on the system for a long time, as they might believe certain segments of a
system to be secure without testing them. That is one of the reasons why it
can be beneficial having an external third-party perform a pentest with no prior
knowledge of the system as it can expose the blind spots that the developers
missed. These third parties are usually contractors or specialized security com-
panies, which usually translates to more detailed and skilled pentests.
However, before hiring an external company to test a system, the developers
should consider going over common security practices themselves beforehand.
That should catch the most low-hanging fruits and result in more value derived
from the pentest.
Requirements
External pentesting should be done on the device and it should seek to discover
if all the requirements listed in the framework are met. This should be a black-
==PAGE:66==
54

box exercise until no further information can be gained and then transform the
test into a grey-box exercise where the pentester can ask the developers of the
device for some basic insight into the device. Once that is done, the final step is a
white-box exercise to determine if the device meets the remaining requirements
that were deemed inconclusive.
The pentester must then supply the organization that commissioned the pentest
with a detailed list of findings and mitigation strategies [68]–[70].

The password was salted hashed with the
standard Linux hashing algorithm SHA512crypt. Since this algorithm is salted,
it is almost impossible to use any kind of lookup method, such as rainbow
tables, to check if this hash has been previously found. Therefore, the hash
must be cracked from scratch.
To make such an attack feasible on such a
computationally hard hashing algorithm, a Graphical Processing Unit (GPU)
is needed, preferably multiple. The author has access to two GPUs, a Nvidia
RTX2070 Super and a Nvidia GTX1660. These two GPUs can be used with
hashcat [83], a hash recovery tool, to try around 300000 hashes per second,
which means that they can check that amount of passwords per second for a
match in the shadow file.
Initially, a brute-force attack was executed, all alphanumeric passwords with
lengths from 1 to 6 were tested, without success. Testing longer passwords with
that keyspace is unfeasible so 7 to 8 character long passwords were tested with
the same keyspace as an MD5 hash. They also did not yield a match.
Finally, as a last resort a wordlist generated from all the cracked passwords found
in the have i been pwned database in 2018 was used [84]. A word combination
rule, called best64 [83], was also used to generate 64 candidates from each entry.
Nevertheless, this wordlist also did not find a match.
In conclusion, the gateway needs to force their users to create a username and
password when they first access the gateway. Alternatively, they could randomly
generate the username and password for the user interface and print it on the
label next to all the other information they have on the gateway. The API also
==PAGE:75==
4.3 Software

needs to have some kind of randomly generated authentication by default or be
disabled until the users create an authentication token from the management in-
terface. Additionally, they should setup some kind of MFA scheme for increased
security.
The password itself is secure but the way it can be generated multiple times is
not.

4.3.4
Secure Communications
The management interface of the gateway does not provide any encryption,
all the traffic to it uses plaintext HTTP requests. Additionally, all the API
communication is done unencrypted as well. This means that anyone on the
local network can see all the traffic that passes between the gateway and the
client. Which could include sensitive API requests, such as password changes
for various protocols.
To determine if any of the communication to external services are encrypted,
the traffic capture from Section 4.3.1 is analyzed.
It reveals that the only
TLS1.2 encrypted connection is to the domain fw.squid.link with the IP address
212.98.84.146. This connection is only established when the gateway checks for
updates.
Additionally, the gateway establishes an external connection to the domain
demo.smartamm.com which it sends data to every ten seconds throughout the
==PAGE:78==
66
Case Study
entire capture window. According to the developer guide, found in Section 4.2,
this domain is used for the proprietary SmartAMM protocol that Develco Prod-
ucts developed themselves. Finding any details about this protocol is tough, but
it appears to be used for configuring the device, as well as to upgrade it. The
connection that it initiates is just a standard TCP connection, with no TLS,
but it could have some encryption in the data fields themselves. However, in-
formation about this protocol is scarce so it is not possible to conclusively tell
if this communication is encrypted or not.
After making a DNS request asking about 0200000100016cb4-ce0460bd193a
d15fddf4.nabto.squid.link and getting a response IP, the gateway sends a
UDP packet to it. The packet contains a sequence that has both the device serial
number and the installation code in it. In the response from that server there
is another IP address and the gateway sends another UDP packet to that new
IP which includes the same aforementioned string. There are regular packets
that go back and forth between them after that. According to the developer
guide [72], this communication is using the Nabto protocol, which uses AES-128
symmetric encryption for all its communications [87].
The information contained in the DNS request could be intercepted since the
DNS protocol is not encrypted. That information could be abused since those
values might have been used to generate some passwords as is discussed in
Section 4.3.2 and is used for the default password for the WiFi. Additionally,
it is possible to connect to that domain through the Nabto protocol [87] and
create a tunnel to the gateway through the internet. That means that anyone
who intercepts this message as it travels through the local network and the
internet can simply connect to the gateway and begin making API requests.
This is further explored in Section 4.3.8
It is clear that the gateway does not meet the secure communication require-
ments detailed in the framework. First of all, it is not clear if the SmartAMM
protocol even uses encryption, which is a critical flaw for a protocol that is
used to control the device. Additionally, there is sensitive data sent through
the network in plaintext in the form of a DNS request.
It could have used
either DNS over TLS (DoT) or DNS over HTTPS (DoH) which are both stan-
dard alternatives to DNS that use encryption. Lastly, the gateway needs to
change the management interface and the API such that they use TLS for all
communications.

Using a port scanning tool such as Nmap [92], reveals that there are only two

been discovered. Running a version detection scan with Nmap [92] does not

searching for vulnerabilities using common databases is not useful and it is not
possible to say if this service is secure or not. This service allows other computers
on the local network to find the gateway by the hostname of the system. That
means that it is possible to navigate to, for example, gateway-6CB4.local without
knowing the IP address of the system.

4.6
Discussion
The case study has some limitations as only the black-box testing was done and
could therefore not determine if all the the framework requirements were met.
There were some potential issues that could have affected the outcomes of the
findings, such as the limited sampling rate of the logic analyzer that was used as
it can only sample communications with the max speed of 50MHz. This means
that any protocols that communicate at a faster rate cannot be detected reliably.
Additionally, the voltage of the data transmissions were right at the edge of the
supported range of the logic analyzer [102]. Both these factors could have led to
some protocols being missed when analyzing the pads in the Minimize Attack
Surface & Secure Communications section.
There are a few other avenues that could have been explored in the black-box
testing phase, given enough time. One of the unexplored potential avenues of
entry is the SmartAMM protocol. It seems to be used to configure the devices
and does not appear to use any kind of authenticaiton. This could mean that
if the protocol is reverse-engineered an attacker could easily gain access.
Another interesting avenue of attack could be to inject data directly into the
data bus between the communication modules, through the debug pads. These
communication buses are less likely to use input validation methods as all the
data on them is supposed to only be between known entities programmed by
Develco Products.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
This thesis has identified and explored all of the major consumer IoT security
standards. It has compared all the key requirements that were extracted from
those standards and used them as the foundation for a security framework fo-
cused on consumer IoT gateways. This framework has avoided many of the
pitfalls of the standards themselves by having a narrow focus area while tar-
geting one of the most important types of consumer IoT devices. Securing the
IoT gateways helps protect most of the other connected devices, which are of-
ten harder to secure. This framework is presented as a means to counter the
high barrier to entry of the security standards, which often contain hard to
parse information and are filled with exceptions. The framework also presents
a novel approach to compliance that utilizes existing knowledge in the security
industry, rather than constructing a rigid and unwieldy system, like some of
the standards do. By basing the framework on all they key requirements of the
standard, the framework complements their security rather than contradicting
them. This means that when some of the explored standards become mandatory
it will be straightforward for anyone that follows this framework to comply with
that standard. It should be simpler to follow this framework and then extend
that with any required standards in the future as this framework has a lower
barrier to entry while maintaining high security.
The presented framework was used in a case study on an IoT gateway from
Develco Products.
This case study showed that the framework can also be
==PAGE:92==

Conclusion
utilized in analyzing the security of an IoT device by checking if the device
fulfills the requirements of the framework. The case study found several areas
where Develco Products can improve their product and hopefully allows them
to permanently increase the security of their device.
Even though the framework presented in this thesis helps improve the security
of consumer IoT devices, there is much more that needs to be done in this
sector and focusing only on gateways is not enough. The rapid growth of IoT
devices around the world coupled with the lack of regulation and the demand
for low-cost devices means that security issues will inevitably continue to pop
up for years to come. There is no single solution that can fix these issues, but
more regulations are a good first step to encourage IoT manufacturers to have
consumers’ best interests at heart. Additionally, increased consumer awareness
regarding the security of their devices would further encourage the companies
to increase their device security.
