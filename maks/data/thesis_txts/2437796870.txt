Abstract 
 
In recirculated aquaculture systems (RAS), solid management is generally considered as being one 
of the most difficult tasks when ensuring good water quality. Solid management includes almost all 
parameters by contributing with organic matter, providing surface area for bacteria growth, 
reducing fish welfare, etc. Thereby, reducing solid level has the ability to improve overall system 
performance. Small solids (< 20 m) make up the largest proportion of particles present, and as 
they are the most difficult to remove, solid removal can be improved by increasing both the particle 
size and particle stability. Previous studies have shown that Guar gum has such an effect in flow-
through systems, but this has not yet been studied in RAS systems. Therefore, the aim of this study 
was to examine how Guar gum, added as a dietary binder affected the level of suspended particles 
and overall water quality when fed to rainbow trout in pilot scale RAS. An experiment including six 
identical, but separate, pilot scale RAS stocked with 19 kg/m3 juvenile rainbow trout (Oncorhyncus 
mykiss) was carried out. Three tanks were fed the control diet containing no binder, while the 
remaining three tanks were fed the diet with binder. Each system was operated with a total volume 
of 0.8 m3, 100 L/d of make-up water (MUW) and applying a constant daily feed load of 2 kg/m3 
MUW. Prior to the start of the experiment, 3 weeks were spent to achieve system stabilization, 
following an experimental period of 4 weeks. During the experimental period particles and 
microbial, chemical and biological water quality were monitored weekly. There was no effect of 
Guar gum inclusion on fish performance measured as the specific growth rate (SGR) and feed 
conversion ratio (FCR). Significantly lower particle numbers and larger particle surface area was 
seen in the group fed with Guar gum. There were no significant differences in biological or 
chemical oxygen demand, and no difference in suspended solids between the two groups. Bacterial 
activity was measured both as microbial hydrogen peroxide degradation and using BactiQuant®. 
There was no significant effect on bacterial level of the feed types measured with either of the two 
methods. In systems fed Guar gum, the solid production was significantly higher than in the control 
systems. Concentrations of TAN, NO2- and NO3- varied during the experiment. These variations 
were related to formaldehyde treatment of all systems, due to a sudden drop in appetite probably 
caused by an outbreak of white spot disease prompted by the parasite Ichthyopthirius multifilis. 
Based on the results from this study, the conclusion was that adding Guar gum to the feed could 
have a positive effect on water quality parameters in RAS.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
 
The global demand for fish products has been expanding through the last decades (FAO, 2016). 
Due to a limitation on the contribution from captured fish, on account of regulations and limited 
population densities, there is a need to look at alternative ways of meeting this demand. With their 
low water consumption and low environmental impact, recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) are 
in general regarded as a good sustainable alternative for producing fish (FAO, 2015).  
One advantage of recirculated systems is their low water consumption which, dependent on degree 
of recirculation, can be as low as 1 m3 per kg feed (FAO, 2015). Compared to more traditional fish 
rearing, such as flow-through systems, RAS have significantly reduced make-up flows, which result 
in accumulation of compounds such as organic matter and suspended solids (Colt, 2006).  
A broad variety of fish species are currently farmed in recirculated systems. As each species 
possesses its own specific requirements regarding water quality, and these requirements might also 
be dependent on the life stage of the fish, the focus here will be on rainbow trout (Oncorhyncus 
mykiss). A good overview over the specific water quality requirements for different fish species has 
been presented by Dalsgaard et al (2013). A modified version of this overview is shown in table 
1.1, where the most relevant parameters and their respective values in relation to rainbow trout 
rearing are listed.

In an indoor RAS, buildings are insulated to make temperature control easy. Minor differences in 
temperatures might occur, but they do not have a negative impact on the fish. In outdoor RAS such

as Model Trout Farms (Jokumsen and Svendsen, 2010), temperatures might be more prone to 
fluctuations, as they are more difficult to regulate in the outdoors.  
Oxygen in RAS is consumed by fish, nitrifying bacteria in the biofilter and degradation of organic 
matter (Lekang, 2008). Oxygen consumption might vary, depending on feeding regime, 
photoperiod, exposure to stress, etc. There are various methods for oxygen supplement in RAS. 
Depending on the stocking density, oxygen from the air might be sufficient. However, most 
commercial facilities add pure oxygen to ensure stable oxygen concentration (FAO, 2015).  
The pH is affected by the consumption of bicarbonate (HCO3-) in the biofilter by the nitrification 
process. Furthermore, CO2 deriving from fish respiration also affects pH. As pH affects the toxicity 
of ammonia, fluctuations might have a detrimental effect on the fish (Lekang, 2008). Therefore, it is 
important to control pH, and this is done by the addition of sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3).  
Ammonia is the end product of protein catabolism in fish. Ammonia is extremely toxic to fish and 
is therefore excreted, mainly through the gills and, to a smaller extent, through the urine. In water, 
equilibrium occurs between ammonia (NH3) and ammonium (NH4+)

The first step in the nitrification process is performed by the bacteria genus Nitrosomonas, whereas 
the second step is performed by the bacteria genus Nitrospira. As the two steps of nitrification run 
independently, and as fish have a low tolerance to both ammonium and nitrite, it is important to

(Lekang, 2008). Fish have a high tolerance to nitrate, which consequently can accumulate in the 
system and be balanced by the daily renewal of make-up-water (MUW).   
As seen above, several of the water quality parameters affect each other. This makes it a 
challenging task to operate a RAS, as changing one factor might affect several other factors.  
In commercial RAS, water quality parameters play an important role in ensuring optimal 
production. Badiola et al (2012) found that the most frequent water quality parameters to cause 
problems in commercial facilities were solid levels, ammonia and dissolved oxygen. Among these 
three, solid levels were found to cause the most problems as solids affect biofilter performance, 
hereby also affecting ammonia and nitrite concentration. Furthermore, degradation of solids causes 
oxygen consumption, thus minimizing the available oxygen for the fish and nitrifiers (Badiola et al, 
2012).  
 
As stated, waste is accumulating in recirculated systems. The main contributor to waste in a fish 
rearing system is fish faeces and any feed spill in the form of settleable and suspended 
particles/solids (Summerfelt, 1998). This is one of the downsides of such systems, since suspended 
particles are a main contributor of organic matter and they negatively influence fish survival 
(Stewart et al, 2006). Particles are also known to lead to imbalances in bacterial populations, since 
they serve as substrate for bacterial colonization, offering growing space and nutrients for bacterial 
growth (Ling and Chen, 2005; Pedersen et al, 2017; Becke et al, 2018). In its entirety, all this 
causes imbalances in the system. Suspended solids in RAS systems largely consist of organic matter 
primarily deriving from feed and faeces (Lekang, 2008). In commercial RAS, settleable and 
suspended particles are typically removed by a drum filter. These filters might be differently 
designed, the working mechanism is the same. In a drum filter the water is led through a fine mesh, 
typically 40 – 100 µm in size, which removes solids larger than the mesh size (Lekang, 2008). This 
allows only particles of a given size to be removed, whereas smaller particles seep through and 
remain in the system. Particles are also exposed to hydrodynamical forces, which might disintegrate 
large particles into smaller sizes (Wong and Piedrahita, 2000). Along with this, mechanical 
filtration also tends to break large particles into yet smaller particles (Dolan et al, 2013; Fernandes 
et al, 2015).  
In the present experiment, where pilot scale RAS were applied (detailed in section 2), no drum filter 
was installed due to limitations of space. Instead, each system was equipped with a swirl separator. 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
The principle behind a swirl separator (or hydrocyclone) is that particles are denser than water, and 
centrifugal forces will hurl particles out to the edges (see illustration in fig. 1.1). 
 
This will lead to stratification, forcing particles to settle out at the bottom while the purified water 
in the top enters back into the rearing system. The hydraulic load in a swirl separator is estimated to 
be 20 - 25 m/h (Lekang, 2008). The advantage of the swirl separator is that it is cheap and area-
intensive. The disadvantage is that it requires a uniform water flow for optimal efficiency. If the 
flow is higher than the filter is designed for, the particles will flow out of the unit with the outlet 
water in the center.   
One of the aims when operating a recirculated facility is to limit the fragmentation of larger 
particles, as this leads to microbial degradation and, to a lesser extent, leaching of soluble nutrients 
within the system (Stewart et al, 2006). Since it is easier to remove larger than smaller particles, it 
is desirable to remove as many large particles as fast and as gently as possible without causing any 
destruction of them (Unger et al, 2015). One approach is to use finer-sized mesh in the mechanical 
filtration; however, doing this will raise operating costs since the need for backwashing will 
increase (Cripps and Bergheim, 2000). Another approach is to increase the stability of the digesta as 
it moves through the digestion tract, making it less susceptible for disintegration when leaving the 
fish as faeces (Brinker, 2007; Unger et al, 2015). A few previous studies have tested different 
approaches to stabilizing the faeces. This has included the addition of cork and indigestible non-
starch polysaccharides (NSP) such as Guar gum to the feed (Brinker, 2007; Unger et al, 2015). 
The results have indicated that water quality parameters such as particles, total suspended solids

Dietary inclusion of binders is not a new phenomenon in aquaculture. Back in the 1980s binders 
were introduced to improve the consistency of fish feed and reduce feed wastage in moist and wet 
fish diets (Storebakken, 1985).  
One of the most commonly applied binders is Guar gum. The adhesive effect of Guar gum is related 
to its chemical composition. Guar gum is a linear polysaccharide (galactomannan) derived from the 
endosperm of the Indian cluster bean (Cyamoposis tetragonolobus). A large proportion of the 
annual Guar gum production is grown in India and Pakistan (Imeson, 2012). Chemically, Guar gum 
is composed of the two sugars galactose and mannose. The backbone is a linear chain of β 1,4-
linked mannose to which galactose are 1,6-linked at every second mannose which forms side 
branches (Imeson, 2012). A sketch of the chemical composition of Guar gum is shown in figure 1.2.

(PubChem, 2018).

Guar gum is widely used in industry as a thickener in cosmetics, sauces and salad dressings, as well 
as in the oil industry, where it is applied in well drilling. Guar gum is hydrophilic and has a high 
viscosity, which make it suitable for stabilizing products (Imeson, 2012). The industrial use of 
galactomannans (including Guar gum) is primarily a result of the ability of these substances to 
produce highly viscous aqueous solutions at relatively low concentrations (Imeson, 2012). 
Furthermore, another advantage of Guar gum is that it easily hydrates in cold water, making it 
suitable for use in fish production, where low water temperatures typically are observed. One of the 
advantages of including Guar gum in the fish feed diet is to increase the viscosity of the digesta and 
thus increase the stability of the evacuated faeces (Brinker, 2007). When Guar gum dissolves in 
water, the long mannan chain unfolds and an open, flexible, non-ordered conformation, termed a 
random coil, will form. When the concentration of Guar gum is low, the coils are separated and 
move around freely, but as the concentration of Guar gum increases (e.g., along the intestinal tract) 
coils will gather and create a cluster of polymers (Imeson, 2012). The size of this cluster determines 
the stability of the particle and reduces deformation. Viscosity is extremely sensitive to Guar gum 
concentration, making the right dosages important. A dependency between Guar gum concentration 
and viscosity has been found to be proportional to the fifth power, resulting in a 32-fold increase in 
viscosity when Guar gum concentration is doubled (Imeson, 2012). As a consequence, even small 
differences in the inclusion level have a major influence on the viscosity of the end product.  
 
Guar gum is a non-starch polysaccharide (NSP), which is a complex group composed of linked 
monomers of hexoses and pentoses (Storebakken, 1985; Imeson, 2012). The nutritional roles of 
these NSPs are dependent on the presence of the right enzymes in the gut of the fish (Sinha et al, 
2011). In carnivorous fish, enzymes such as β-glucanases or β-xylanases that digest NSPs are scarce 
or non-existent. Therefore, NSP in the fish diet will not be digested and does not serve as an energy 
source. Furthermore, too large an inclusion of NSP can lead to reduced nutrient digestibilities and 
resulting reduced growth (Sinha et al, 2011). As a consequence, there is an upper limitation on how 
large a proportion of NSP can be added to a diet without compromising the overall energy load of 
the feed, as NSP is not energy contributing.  
The effect of Guar gum on nutrient digestibility and its effect on the digestive tract have been 
examined in a few studies (Storebakken, 1985; Brinker and Reiter, 2011). At high inclusion levels 
(2.5, 5 and 10 %), a significant reduction in both protein and lipid digestibility, along with a 
reduced gastric transit, was observed for rainbow trout (Storebakken, 1985). A negative effect on 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
feed intake was observed at all inclusion levels, while the specific growth rate did not seem to be 
affected at inclusion of 2.5 % Guar gum. At these inclusion levels, Guar gum acts as a sequestrant 
component of the mixed micelles in the intestinal lumen (Storebakken, 1985). However, these 
inclusion levels were much higher than the inclusion levels observed in present feed types (Brinker 
and Reiter, 2011; Ekmann 2018). For rainbow trout, an inclusion level of 0.3 % was found to have 
no negative effect on protein or lipid digestibility, along with no significant negative impact on 
specific growth rate and feed conversion ratio (Brinker and Reiter, 2011). An advantage of Guar 
gum is that since it is not degraded, it accumulates through the intestinal passage (Brinker, 2005). 
The inclusion can thus be kept at a low daily level, which does not compromise energy content nor 
increase feeding cost too much but minimizes the risk of negative side effects.  
Based on previous findings, it therefore seems that the addition of Guar gum to the feed can have a 
positive effect on water quality parameters. However, no studies have examined the effect of feed 
binders in recirculated systems, where water is being reused and substances accumulate. This state 
of affairs prompted the background for this thesis, leading to the following aim.  
 
1.2 Aim  
 
The aim of this study was to examine how Guar gum, added as a dietary binder to a rainbow trout 
diet, affected the level of suspended particles and overall water quality when fed to simple, pilot 
scale RAS.  
 
1.3 Hypothesis 
 
The inclusion of binder was hypothesized to have the following effects:  
- 
Reduces the levels of particles in systems fed binder feed due to higher particle stability.  
- 
Increases the size of particles in systems fed binders due to higher stability of faeces.   
- 
Lowers levels of suspended solids due to settling of large particles in binder systems 
- 
Lowers levels of COD and BOD5 due to lower levels of organic matter  
- 
Lowers bacterial activity due to less available particles and substrate for adhesion

2 Materials and methods  
 
To study the effects of Guar gum on suspended particle levels and overall water quality in RAS, a 
4-week (16/02/2018 – 13/03/2018) exposure trial including two triplicate treatments (± binder) was 
carried out at the research facility at the section for Aquaculture at the Technical University of 
Denmark (DTU Aqua, Niels Juelsvej 30, 9850 Hirtshals, Denmark). The experiment was carried 
out in six separate but identical pilot scale RAS, each with a total volume of approximately 0.8 m3 
(fig. 2.1).  
Each system was composed of a rearing tank of 0.7 m3, a swirl separator, a pump sump and a 
moving bed biofilter. Each swirl separator was equipped with 2 L solid collectors to collect solid 
particles (see fig. 2.1).  
Biofilters were built for this experiment and had therefore not been used for other experiments prior 
to this one. Only the rearing tanks and swirl separators had been used previously for fish rearing but 
were cleaned. 100 L of water was discharged from each system, and an equal amount of municipal 
tap water (i.e., MUW) was added to all 6 RAS every morning. To compensate for evaporation, etc., 
additional water was manually added until a fixed point in the pump sump was reached. The

experiment. This was due to the fish losing appetite, accompanied by some mortality, in 4 out of 6 
systems. This presumably happened due to an outbreak of white spot disease caused by the parasite 
Ichthyopthirius multifilis (Heinecke and Buchmann, 2009). Formalin is the name for formaldehyde 
(H2CO) in aqueous solution, and although being phased out, formaldehyde is still applied as a

outbreak of what was considered to be white spot disease was observed in one of the two batches 
from which the fish originally derived. It is therefore plausible that this was the causative agent, but 
time constrictions did not allow for further diagnosis. However, the effect of formaldehyde on 
Ichthyopthirius multifilis was dependent on which life stage the parasite was in, and therefore

waste was detected two weeks after the first formaldehyde treatment, which made it necessary to 
treat all systems with formaldehyde again. The second formalin treatment took place on 28/02 (fig.

2.1 Daily management 
 
The fish were fed from 8.00 to 18.00 every day using belt feeders. The daily photoperiod ranged 
from 7.15 to 22.00. Large solids, particulate matter and faecals were collected in a solid collector 
placed at the bottom of the swirl separator (see fig. 2.1). Solid collectors were removed every 
morning and checked for uneaten pellets by draining the solid collector and pouring the contents 
into a plastic tray. Before draining, solid height in the solid collector was measured using endorsed 
rulers on the collectors. Pellets were manually counted, and there was no discrimination between 
half and whole pellets. pH was kept within the range 7.1 – 7.6 by occasionally adding sodium 
bicarbonate to compensate for alkalinity loss due to nitrification.  
Daily measurements of pH, dissolved oxygen and temperature were conducted in each system every 
morning at 7.30 during the whole experimental period using a portable multimeter (HACH HQ40d, 
Hach Lange, Germany). At the same time, TAN, NO2- and NO3- were measured by a test kit (Merck 
KGaA, Germany), using colour test kit for TAN determination and test strips for NO2- and NO3-. To 
measure the actual concentration, water samples were taken once a week, filtered (0.2 m filter) 
and stored at 5 C for later analysis in the laboratory. All measures were taken prior to water 
replacement. The water temperature ranged from 10 – 13 C during the trial period, and diurnal 
variations were less than 1 C. Oxygen concentration was maintained at 6.8 – 7.6 mg/L by aeration 
from a compressor. No pure oxygen was added to the systems. Dead fish were removed, weighed 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
on a scale and registered. Fish appetite was assessed daily by pushing a few feed pellets from the 
belt feeder to the water surface, while observing the activity of the fish.   
 
On every sampling day, 5 L of water was representatively taken from each systems pump sump and 
transferred to a 5 L plastic can. To avoid any potential degradation, all cans were placed in darkness 
at 3 C and stored for later analysis in the laboratory. 
 
2.2 Organic matter 
 Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD5)  
 
BOD5 (mg/L) measured the quantity of easily biodegradable organic matter contained in a water 
sample (Danish Standard, 1999). This was relevant to measure since the organic matter serves as a 
substrate for bacteria, and because bacterial degradation of organic matter consumes oxygen as 
indicated by the reaction:   
𝐶𝐻2𝑂2 + 𝑂2 →𝐶𝑂2 + 𝐻2𝑂 
 
The BOD5 was thus the amount of dissolved oxygen that bacteria use when they oxidize the organic 
matter. In an aquaculture system, the majority of organic matter is composed of protein, lipid and 
carbohydrate deriving either from feed or present in undigested form of faecal origin. The organic 
matter may be dissolved, suspended or particulate.  
In this trial, water samples for BOD5 analysis were taken once a week in the pump sump. 
Immediately after sampling, bottles were stored at 3C until later analysis in the lab was possible. In 
all systems, both BOD5 from raw and filtered (0.45 m) water samples were measured, and 
particulate BOD5 was derived as the difference between the two.  
 
Water samples on raw water were diluted 1:10 – 1:20 due to a high load of organic matter. Before 
samples were aerated, 2 mL/L Allylthiourea (C4H8N2S) was added to all samples to inhibit 
nitrification. After addition, all water samples were aerated for 15 minutes at a temperature of 20 
C. To hinder supersaturation, the aerated samples were placed in a thermobox for another 15 
minutes. Subsequently, stirred water samples were transferred to 3 x 250 mL bottles. Oxygen was 
then measured in one of the bottles using Hach-Lange HDQ40D sensors, which served as the 
control, while the two remaining bottles were sealed and placed at 20 C in the incubator in full 
darkness.

 Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD)  
 
COD (mg/L) measures the quantity of chemically degradable organic matter contained in a water 
sample. COD estimation followed the standard described in Danish Standard (2006).  Its relevance 
was correlated to BOD5 since COD measured the total amount of organic matter, whereas BOD5 
only estimated the amount of easily biodegradable organic matter within 5 days. The chemical 
oxygen demand expressed the theoretical oxygen demand consumed in a total chemical oxidation of 
the organic constituents in the water. The COD value depends primarily on how complete the 
oxidation is. Many organic compounds and most inorganic reducing agents were oxidized to 
between 90 – 100 % (Danish Standard, 2006). The principle was that samples were oxidized in a 
standard manner by digestion with sulfuric acid and potassium dichromate in the presence of silver 
sulfate and mercury (II) sulfate. In practice this was done using 13 mm barcoded vials from Hach-
Lange (Germany). COD was measured on raw and filtered (0.45 m) water samples that had been 
stored for a maximum of 1 month. These samples were conserved by 1 mL 4 M Sulfuric Acid 
(H2SO4) for raw samples, and 0.15 mL for dissolved samples, and stored at 3 C. Barcoded vials 
were automatically heated to 172 °C for 15 minutes using a Hach-Lange HT200 and after cooling 
read on a HACH DR 2800 spectrophotometer (Hach Lange, Germany).

 Biodegradability index 
 
This index was proposed by Srinivas (2008) and takes into consideration the ratio between BOD5 
and COD. According to this index, organic matter can be classified into three categories based on 
BOD5: COD respectively easily biodegradable (> 0.6), average biodegradable (0.3 - 0.6) and not 
easily biodegradable (< 0.3). The indexes were calculated for raw, dissolved and particulate water 
samples. 
 
2.3 Total Suspended Solids (TSS)  
 
Suspended solids were defined as the particles in a water sample that were retained after filtration 
through a filter. To determine the load of suspended solids, a water sample of known volume was 
filtered through a 1.6 m glass fiber filter (Whatman GF/A), which was afterwards dried and 
weighed (Danish Standard 2005).  
 
A blind test was weighed using 3 filters in a crucible, and total weight was registered. Before

2.4 Particles  
 
Particles between 1 and 30 m (hereafter determined as small particles) were measured using a 
Beckman Multisizer 4 Coulter Counter. The principle of this measurement is that particles are 
suspended in a weak electrolyte solution. Afterwards, they are drawn through two electrodes, as 
indicated by figure 2.4.  
 
 
Figure 2.4: Illustration of the Coulter Counter measure of particle size (Beckmann Coulter, 2009). 
The electrolyte generates a conductivity, which is applied to measure the size of the particles. The 
voltage applied in this device creates a “sensing zone”. When a water sample is drawn through the 
aperture, its particles displace its own volume in the conducting liquid. This momentarily increases 
the impedance of the aperture, producing a tiny proportional current flow that is directed to an 
amplifier and measured (Beckmann Coulter, 2009). In practice, a 2 mL water sample was 
transferred to a beaker and diluted in 98 mL milli-Q water. Then, the particle number was 
automatically counted 3 times in a row by the Coulter Counter, and the average of the readings was 
presented.  
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
Particles between 30 and 200 m (hereafter determined large particles) were measured using a 
single particle optical sizing (SPOS) with the AccuSizer 780/SIS from Nicomp (Nicomp, USA). 
The SPOS method measures the size of individual particles, hence the name. The principle behind 
this method is that an optical sensor emits a laser beam across the flow path. When no particles are 
passing through, a baseline voltage is released. Using vacuum, particles are drawn through the 
optical sensor. When a particle passes through, it casts a shadow over the detector, which eventually 
changes the output voltage. This change in magnitude depends on the size of the particle. This 
analysis method uses the “extinction mode” – the degree of light extinction is related to the particle 
size. In practice, a diluted 80 mL water sample was transferred to a beaker and placed on the 
Accusizer. The instrument automatically recorded a sample 3 times in a row, and then presented the 
average of these readings. The Accusizer has a built-in function, switching off the particle measure 
if the particle concentration is too high. As a consequence, it was necessary to dilute samples. The 
dilution factor varied from 2-8 during the experiment and this was included in the calculation of 
particle number. Since many analyses were made on the same day, and other samples had to be 
prepared, particles were not analyzed immediately after sample were taken. However, no particles 
were measured later than 5 hours after sampling. In the intermediate time, particle samples were 
stored at 5°C in complete darkness. Before analyzing, all particles were placed on a magnetic stirrer 
at a speed of 150 rpm to ensure homogeneity in the sample.  
On the 7th of March, a 24-hour particle experiment was performed. At every fourth hour a 
representative water sample was drawn from the pump sump of each system. Samples were taken at 
0800, 1200, 1600, 2000, 2400, 0400 and 0800. Once taken, samples were immediately stored at a 
temperature of 3 °C until they were analyzed. During this experiment no samples were analyzed 
later than one hour after they were taken.

2.5 Bacterial activity 
 Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) degradation 
 
Most aerobic organisms have a natural defence mechanism against hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), 
which they will degrade using the enzyme catalase (Hosetti and Frost, 1994). The degradation 
follows the reaction 
2𝐻2𝑂2 →2𝐻2𝑂+ 𝑂2 
As has been described previously, bacteria are naturally present in water samples. When bacteria 
experiences hydrogen peroxide (HP) a natural degradation process is started. This was used to 
assess the bacterial activity (HP-activity) in the present water samples in the same way as described 
by Hosetti and Frost (1994). HP-activity was measured as the sum of enzymatic degradation of 
H2O2. The degradation of HP can be described as a first order reaction by the exponential decay 
equation (Pedersen et al, 2006; Arvin and Pedersen, 2015; Tirado et al, 2018):    
𝐶𝑡= 𝐶0𝑒−𝑘𝑒𝑡 
Where C0 is the initial H2O2 concentration (mg/L) and Ct is the concentration (mg/L) at time t (in 
hours). In the equation, ke is the elimination rate constant describing the fractional change in 
concentration per hour (h-1) and thus how fast the degradation takes place. The higher the bacterial 
load, the faster the degradation of H2O2. This measurement method is based on Tanner and Wong 
(1998) with the same modifications as described by Pedersen et al (2012).  
 
Prior to analysis, water samples from each system were transferred to a water bath to ensure a 
temperature of 22°C. A 4A (i.e., four-fold the strength of the reagent used in Tanner and Wong, 
1998) reagent was made in advance, consisting of 1.2 g NH4VO3, 5.2 dipicolinic acid and 60 mL 
conc. H2SO4. Likewise, a 200 ppm H2O2 stock solution was made in advance from a 35 % standard 
solution. In the current setup, a total of 6 x 12 cuvettes (1x1 cm) were used. Before starting up, 0.2 
mL 4A reagent was transferred to each cuvette.  At time 0, a baseline sample was taken from all 
water samples and transferred to the first row of cuvettes. After 1 minute, hydrogen peroxide was 
spiked to the first sample. At 30 second intervals, H2O2 was spiked to one of the remaining samples, 
until all samples had been spiked with H2O2. Immediately after spiking, a water sample was taken 
from the respective sample and transferred to the belonging cuvette. During the following hour, a 
water sample was taken every 15 minutes and transferred to the remaining cuvettes. When the H2O2 
in the water samples came into contact with the 4A reagent an oxo-peroxo-pyridine-2,6-
dicarboxylato-vanadate chelate formed. This resulted in a yellow/orange coloration of the solution.

 BactiQuant®  
 
BactiQuant® (Mycometer, Denmark) is an indirect method for measuring the microbial enzyme 
activity approved by the United States Environmental Protection Agency (Mc. Kernan et al, 2012). 
It was originally developed for testing water quality in relation to human consumption but has since 
found widespread use in many other industries. It has also been found applicable for assessing 
bacterial activity in modern recirculated aquaculture systems (Tirado et al, 2017; Pedersen et al, 
2017).  The bacterial activity is measured by quantifying the enzymatic activity of hydrolase which 
is found in most bacteria (Reeslev et al, 2011). In practice, the microbial enzymes in a water sample 
hydrolyze a synthetic fluorescent enzyme-substrate, which releases fluorophores that can be 
quantified with a fluorometer.  A water sample of 10 mL was filtered through a 0.2 µm filter 
inoculated with the substrate. After filtration the filter was allocated at a fixed period of 30 minutes 
to react with the fluorescent reagent before measurement. After 30 minutes filters were flushed with 
a developer which stopped the reaction process. Then, the amount of fluorophores in the developer 
was measured on a fluorometer (BactiQuant ®, Mycometer, Denmark). The results were expressed 
in standardized BactiQuant® Values (BQV) corresponding to the amount of fluorescence in a 250 
mL filtered water sample, and incubated for 30 minutes at 23 C.

2.8 Ammonia-N 
 
Ammonia concentration (mg/L) in water samples was determined referring to the method described 
in Andersen et al (2004) and Danish Standard DS 224 (Danish Standard, 1975). Ammonium in 
reaction with salicylate-catalyst (reagent 1) and alkali-hypochlorite solution (reagent 2) form 
indophenol blue. The intensity of the blue color is proportional to the concentration of NH4+/NH3. A 
2.5 mL diluted sample was transferred to a test tube and 0.3 mL reagent 1 was added and mixed 
well. After mixing, 0.5 mL reagent 2 was transferred to the test tube and mixed by shaking the tube. 
Test tubes were immediately sealed after mixing and put aside in darkness. The reaction was 
complete after 1 hour, and all samples were measured immediately after this period. Absorbance 
was measured at a wavelength of 680 nm. The concentration of NH4+-N was calculated by the 
formula:  
 
[NH4

2.9 Nitrite 
 
Nitrite concentration (mg/L) in water samples was determined following the standard described in 
DS 223 (Danish Standard, 1991). Nitrite in an acid solution (pH 1.5 – 2) will react with 
sulfanilamide (C6H8N2O2S) to form a diazocompound which will connect to N-(1-
naftyl)ethylendiamin (C12H14N2) to form an azo dye. The absorbance of this compound can be 
measured by a spectrophotometer at a wavelength of 545 nm. A 2.5 mL water sample was 
transferred to cuvettes and 0.1 mL of diazotization reagent was added. Cuvettes were then capped 
and mixed well and left for reaction to take place for 5 minutes. Then, 0.05 mL coupling reagent 
was added, and probes were mixed well and left for 20 minutes. Samples were read no later than 2 
hours after the coupling reagent was added.

2.10 Total nitrogen and nitrate-N 
 
The procedure for determination of total nitrogen and nitrate-N follows the guidelines in DS/EN 
ISO 11905-1 (Danish Standard, 1997). The procedures are almost similar; the only variation is that 
5 mL total nitrogen samples were mixed with 1 mL kaliumperoxodisulphate (K2S2O8) oxidation 
solution and heated to 120 C for 1 hour. The oxidation leads to the formation of nitrate, and from

3 Results 
 
Data presented in this section was analyzed in accordance with the methods described in section 
2.11. All analyses were made at least in duplicate to minimize the impact of source errors. A 
maximum limit of 5 % deviation was applied, except for BactiQuant®, meaning that deviations less 
than this value were accepted, while higher deviations led to new analyses. The mean of duplicate 
values was used as the representative value except for BactiQuant® for which all values are shown.  
Data from particle samples was obtained in triplicate due to the technical instruments, as described 
previously. As formalin was applied twice during the experiment, and since this might have 
affected some parameters, the two treatments are indicated by arrows in the chosen presentations of 
the results. In the representation of BOD5, COD, TSS and H2O2, data from the second test sampling 
(16/02) was not included in the graph but as separate points. Due to their distinctive deviation, those 
values were considered outliers and therefore not included. The test size (N) for sludge production 
and feed loss was 138 observations in total. For TAN, NO2-, NO3-, TN, BOD5, COD, TSS, HP-
activity and particles N was equal to 24 observations in total. For ash, DM and BactiQuant® the test 
size was 12 observations in total.  For SGR and FCR the test size equaled 6. 
Some of the observations were analyzed by several statistic approaches; e.g., both by ANOVA and 
t-test. As a new ecosystem has to be established when starting up an RAS, some time was needed 
before equilibrium was reached. Consequently, some of the measures might have changed during 
the experiment and may thus be more relevant to analyze later in the experimental period than at the 
start. This was the reason for choosing several statistical tests. All systems were grouped based on 
the feed type they were fed, and all results thus comprise of three systems. The presented estimates 
in this section were only based on the four samplings, and does not contain the two test samples 
(fig. 2.3)

4 Discussion 
 
The aim of this study was to examine how Guar gum, added as a dietary binder, affected the level 
of suspended particles and overall water quality when fed to simple pilot scale RAS. Therefore, an 
experiment was conducted, examining how 0.2 % Guar gum inclusion in fish feed affected these 
parameters. The focus was primarily on the effect on organic matter, particle level and bacterial 
activity. In addition, concentrations of TSS, TAN, NO2-, NO3-, and TN were measured. The 
experiment was conducted in a pilot scale setup with 6 independent recirculated systems, where 3 
systems were destined as treatment systems assigned the binder diet, and 3 systems were control 
systems. This experiment was conducted during the startup phase of a recirculated system and was 
thus not expected to be stable. The results presented in this section is only based on last four 
samplings and does not contain the two test samples. 
 
4.1 Organic matter  
 
Easily biodegradable organic matter and total amount of total organic matter were measured as 
BOD5 and COD at raw, dissolved and particulate fractions. It was hypothesized that the load of 
organic matter would be lower in systems fed binder. Overall estimates for BOD5raw and BOD5part 
from systems fed binder were slightly higher compared to systems fed the control diet. However, 
was there not a systematic tendency for higher concentrations in binder systems (fig. 3.4 and 3.6). 
BOD5diss values were slightly lower in systems fed binder (fig. 3.5). For all three fractions the 
highest variations between replicate systems were observed in control systems. This observation 
could be prompted by the Guar gum, as a consequence of increased particle stability and thus 
reduced particle contribution of organic matter, thereby making binder system less prone to 
variations. CODraw, CODdiss and CODpart were higher in systems fed binder (fig. 3.7 - 3.9). 
However, neither of these differences were significant. Since there was no difference between the 
two feed types the hypothesis was rejected. There was a significant accumulation of all COD 
fractions over the 4 last sampling dates accompanied by a less, non-significant increase in the BOD5 
fractions. A similar evolution in organic matter was observed in both binder and control systems. 
Organic matter accumulated in the systems since it was difficult to remove and because of the reuse 
of water (Stewart, 2006; Tirado et al, 2017). The accumulation observed was in accordance with 
previous findings (Fernandes et al, 2015; Tirado et al, 2017). As previously described BOD5 is a 
==PAGE:51==
50 
 
representation of the most easily degradable organic matter present in a water sample and is the 
portion of organic matter that is most easily available for bacteria as energy substrate. 
On the other hand, constitutes COD all types of organic matter, including both easily and hard 
degradable organic matter, which for the latter is not easily accessible for bacteria. As BOD5 was 
continually used by bacteria, it did not accumulate in the system as opposed to COD where a 
significant accumulation was observed.  
Equilibrium concentrations did seem to be reached for BOD5raw and CODraw in the present 
experiment, but an extra measurement point would, however, have been desirable in confirming 
this. In a study by Tirado et al (2017), equilibrium concentrations of organic matter were reached 
after 30 days in an experiment with a similar set-up using, however, a fixed bed biofilter.  In 
comparison, the present experiment’s trial period lasted only 26 days, which could advocate that 
equilibrium was almost reached.  Both BOD5 and COD values for all three fractions were on 
average 2-4 times higher in the present study compared to the organic load observed in Tirado et al 
(2017). This was a surprise and occurred despite a lower feed load of 2 kg/m3 MUW was applied in 
the present experiment compared to the 3.25 kg/ m3 MUW applied in Tirado et al (2017). Also, a 
moving bed biofilter (MBB) was applied in the present experiment, and these types of filters in 
general result in a higher load of organic matter (Fernandes et al, 2017). Furthermore, biofilter 
elements derived from an established RAS and were expected to be matured, hence were nitrifying 
bacteria assumed to dominate over heterotrophic bacteria (Malone and Pfeiffer, 2006). In the 
biofilter only heterotrophic bacteria consumes organic matter, and as they were a minority in the 
start, this could imply the high organic load observed. This would also explain why the 
accumulation was not significant, as heterotrophic bacteria started to colonize and consume organic 
matter.  
 
A biodegradability index was calculated as the BOD5: COD ratio for raw, dissolved and particulate 
fractions (table 3.1). These calculations provided information in relation to the organic matter 
characterization in recirculated systems as described by Tirado et al (2017). The overall mean 
biodegradability obtained in this study averaged 0.4 for both binder and control systems and was 
thus classified as average biodegradability. Dalsgaard and Petersen (2011) were the first to apply 
the biodegradability index to characterize aquaculture organic matter in the water column in relation 
to dissolved and particulate biodegradability. In the present experiment, the corresponding ratios for 
dissolved and particulate ratios averaged 0.1 (BOD5diss: CODdiss) and 0.6 (BOD5part: CODpart) for 
==PAGE:52==
51 
 
both control and binder systems. This indicates that less easily biodegradable organic matter 
dominated in the dissolved fractions, whereas particulate fractions were dominated by easy 
biodegradable organic matter. In a previous study, Pedersen et al (2017) found a strong correlation 
between readily degradable organic particles and bacterial activity. Based on this, it therefore seems 
that conditions for bacteria colonization were favorable in the present experiment, as both sufficient 
surface area and abundant energy sources were provided. The results obtained in relation to 
biodegradability index were in contrast to findings by Tirado et al (2017), where both the dissolved 
and particulate fractions were dominated by less easily biodegrable organic matter during the start-
up phase of a RAS. The BOD5raw: CODraw ratio in the present experiment was double that observed 
by Fernandes et al (2017) in a study on the influence of fixed and moving bed biofilters on micro 
particle dynamics. Here a BOD5raw: CODraw of 0.2 was reported for a MBB but with the application 
of a drum filter. As particles were broken in MBB´s, such filters contribute to the production of 
small particles and as these were difficult to remove they accumulated in the system increasing the 
presence of more easily degradable organic matter. 
 
As described previously, formalin (formaldehyde) was applied to treat the systems. At the second 
test sampling (16/02) both BOD5 and COD at raw and dissolved level were affected by the addition 
of formalin. Formaldehyde is a simple aldehyde with the chemical formula (CH2O). In water, 
formaldehyde is degraded to CO2 and H2O. Formaldehyde at high concentrations had a bactericidal 
effect but could also serve as an easy biodegradable organic source for heterotrophic organisms 
such as Pseudomonas strains which have also been reported in RAS systems (Chongcharoen et al, 
2005). Since the chemical structure is relatively simple, it was expected that formaldehyde would 
influence the level of organic matter. It was also expected that formaldehyde would act as a carbon 
source for microorganisms present in the system as reported by Sortkjær et al (2008). 
Formaldehyde might also have disrupted some of the biofilm present on the surface of the biofilter 
elements, causing organic material in the form of dead microorganism to be released (Pedersen et 
al, 2007). This explains the high peak on the second sampling date (16/02), where the organic 
matter was the highest registered during the trial period. The second formaldehyde application was 
done after the fourth sampling, and seventh days prior to the fifth sampling. This might explain why 
no effect on organic matter was observed, as microorganism would have consumed it all. The 
reason for choosing formaldehyde as the chemical agent was the rapid degradation by the biofilter.

4.2 Particles 
 
Particle level was measured using two different instruments. Thus, particles were divided into two 
different size classes; i.e., small (< 30 µm) and large particles (> 30 µm), dependent on the 
respective method. Particle level was measured both as total particle number (TPN) measured as 
number pr. mL and total surface area (TSA) measured as µm2 pr. mL.  
TPN was hypothesized to be lower in systems fed binder, whereas TSA was hypothesized to be 
higher due to an increase in particle stability. For small particles there was a clear tendency towards 
higher values of TPN and TSA in the control systems (fig. 3.11). Although this effect was not 
significant, it indicates a reduced presence of fine particles in the binder systems. For large particles 
the results were opposite, i.e., TPN and TSA were significantly higher for systems fed the binder 
diet, indicating an increase in particle stability (fig. 3.12). As systems were operated identically, this 
difference was most likely caused by the binder. Although not all values were significant, there was 
a clear tendency towards a change in particle distribution in binder systems sustaining the initial 
hypothesis. These findings were confirmed by the particle size distribution data shown for the last 
sampling day (13/03), where a clear presence of fewer small and more large particles occurred in 
the binder systems (fig. 3.13).  
The findings in relation to the presence of more large particles were in accordance with Brinker et 
al (2005), where the inclusion of Guar gum (0.3 %) significantly increased the amount of large 
==PAGE:54==
53 
 
particles present in a flow-through fish farm. In flow-through systems, hydrodynamical forces are 
less definite compared to a RAS (Wong and Piedrahita, 2000), since water is not recirculated and 
thus passes through only once. Therefore, particles should in theory be less exposed to 
disintegration in such facilities (Lekang, 2008). The finding by Brinker et al (2005) is an indication 
that not only does inclusion of Guar gum influence the particle size distribution, resulting in larger 
particles; it also increases the stability of the particles present. According to Brinker (2007), the 
effect of Guar gum inclusion was more pronounced for large particles compared to small particles 
consistent with the present study where a significant difference between feed types was only 
observed for large particles. As small particles originated from disintegration of large particles, this 
was consistent with the observation of fewer large particles and more small particles present in 
control systems compared to binder systems. This also emphasized the increased stability of 
particles in binder systems. TPN values for large particles obtained in the present experiment were 
of the same order as TPN values (2.4 x 103 particles/mL) found in Fernandes et al (2017). 
 
Although systems were operated identically, variations in particle load were observed between 
replicate systems. This variation was most likely caused by multiple factors, some more substantial 
than others. Great formations of biofilm on the internal surfaces of pipes were observed. This would 
lead to a decline in water flow, reducing the effect of hydrodynamical forces and minimizing the 
disintegration of particles in the affected systems. In turn, this would cause a lower prevalence of 
particles in these systems. MBB are also known to produce fine particles (Ivanovic & Leiknes, 
2012; Fernandes et al, 2017). The particle disintegration in MBB occurs due to shear forces, caused 
by vigorous aeration and mixing (Ivanovic & Leiknes, 2008). As all systems were equipped with 
MBBs and the same type of biofilter material, it seems that particles from binder systems were 
more able to resist the particle disintegration taking place in the biofilter. Whether this finding also 
holds true for other types of biofilter needs to be examined in future experiments. However, as 
shear forces were less prominent in fixed bed biofilters (Fernandes et al, 2017), an even clearer 
effect on particle distributions is expected to occur in systems equipped with this type of filters.   
 
Particle dynamics during the day (24 h) were measured once during the experimental period. For 
small particles, TPN and TSA were lower in binder systems compared to the control, whereas the 
opposite was observed for large particles (fig. 3.14 - 3.15). There was a significant difference 
between the two system types regarding large particles, whereas this was not the case for small 
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
particles. A more or less stable level of small particles during the day was observed for both binder 
and control systems. A stable particle level was in accordance with previous findings (Fernandes et 
al, 2014). In comparison, there was a tendency for more large particles being produced in the 
evening. In a commercial flow-through system, Brinker and Rösch (2005) found a higher 
prevalence of large particles during the night, which was assumed to be caused by the reduced 
activity of fish causing reduced shear forces. The similar result obtained in the present experiment 
could fit well with the photoperiod. However, the peak observed for both TPN and TSA in the 
samplings done at 20.00 and 24.00 were most likely related to the fish starting to defaecate after the 
feeding period stopped at 18.00.  
 
As fine particles represent a large surface area to volume ratio (Brinker and Rösch, 2005), a reduced 
accumulation of fine particles will in theory reduce the area available for bacterial colonization. In 
the present study, particles < 20 µm constituted 99 % of all particles present. This was, although 
slightly higher, in accordance with previous findings (Patterson et al, 1999; Fernandes et al, 2017). 
Again, the difference might be caused by a difference in the type of biofilter. 
Previous studies have examined the relation between particle prevalence and bacterial activity for 
particles larger than 5 µm (Brinker and Rösch, 2005; Fernandes et al, 2015; Pedersen et al, 2017), 
but no studies have so far examined particles below this limit, due to technical limitations in the 
measuring instruments. The Beckmann Coulter Counter therefore seems to provide a new tool for 
determining particles less than 5 µm. Based on particle counting, these particles constitute around 
98 % of the total particles present in this experiment. However, this number was most likely 
influenced by the presence of bacteria, as the size of bacteria varies from 0.5 – 5 µm (Brown, 2015). 
Therefore, bacteria were most probably also detected by the Coulter Counter, thus contained in the 
total number of particles present. This would cause interference and result in an overestimation of 
the actual surface area present for bacterial colonization.  
 
Based on the findings in this study, Guar gum evidently seems to reduce the formation of small 
particles. Particles, specifically fine particles (< 30 µm), are unwanted in RAS since they are 
harmful to fish, increase the organic load and cause imbalances in biofilter performance (Chapman 
et al, 1987; Pedersen et al, 2017; Fernandes et al, 2017; Becke et al, 2018). As particles also 
constitute surface area for bacterial colonization, a reduction in particle surface area should lead to 
reduced bacterial activity, as found by Pedersen et al (2017). A reduction in particle load in RAS 
==PAGE:56==
55 
 
would not be restricted to optimization of a single water quality parameter but have multiple 
positive effects on overall system performance. As mechanical filtration is the best way of 
removing particles from a RAS, and since such units are only able to remove large particles, these 
are desirable. In this context it seems that inclusion of Guar gum was able to contribute to a better 
particle removal, by increasing the number of large particles present.  
 
4.3 Suspended solids  
 
Suspended solids were estimated as what was left over when filtering a water sample through a 1.6 
µm filter. TSS level was hypothesized to be lower in systems fed binder due to the increased 
stability of solid particles, and thereby better removal of these. There were no significant 
differences between systems in relation to TSS-level, although binder systems showed slightly 
higher values compared to control systems (fig. 3.10). Therefore, the hypothesis regarding 
suspended solids did not apply at least to the current setup.  
  
Total suspended solids did not accumulate through the experimental period. This was in contrast to 
previous findings (Becke et al, 2018). This difference could be related to the shorter trial period 
applied in the present study (26 days) compared to the mentioned study (126 days). An elevated 
concentration of TSS was observed at the second test sampling, but was presumably related to an 
increase in suspended feed particles due to the fish losing their appetite. Due to the definition, TSS 
covers a broad variety of substances. In itself, TSS is a “lumped” measure that does not contain any 
information about how the solids distribute in relation to size as it does for, e.g., analysis of the 
particle size distribution. As a consequence, only large variations in solid load would be reflected 
by the TSS value. As systems in general showed the same level of suspended solids, no differences 
between the two feed types were observed. The finding in the present experiment was in contrast to 
Brinker et al (2005), where a significant reduction of TSS by 0.3 % Guar gum inclusion was 
observed. This was obtained in a drum filter-equipped flow-through system, thus not directly 
comparable. Due to the significant difference in large particles between binder and control systems, 
application of a drum filter would most likely have shown a difference between the levels of 
suspended solids for the two feed types, as large particles would be continuously removed from 
system water by the drum filter.

4.4 Solid characteristics  
 
Sludge production was measured daily by manually reading the height of the settled solids present 
in the solid collectors. The daily amount of sludge differed significantly between the two feed types, 
with more solids being removed from the systems fed binder (fig. 3.18). As large particles settle out 
faster in the swirl separator (Lekang, 2008), this observation was consistent with the observation 
that more large particles were present in binder systems. In a previous study, Brinker et al (2005) 
found a reduced density of Guar gum faecal solids due to the water binding property of Guar gum, 
but this did not influence the settling rate as the larger size compensated for the density reduction. 
Some uncertainty was connected to the daily measure of solid height, as reading could only be done 
with an accuracy of ± 0.5 cm. Sludge from systems fed the binder diet also had higher ash content 
compared to control systems, although the difference was not significant. This could possibly be 
related to an undetected feed waste, which was not removed. As there were no differences in the 
volume of the solid collectors, and as the DM content was identical for the two feed types, the 
larger sludge production seen in binder system also resulted in a larger removal of dry matter.  
 
There was no significant difference between the ash content in sludge for the two feed types, 
although sludge from binder systems had slightly higher ash content. As the only difference 
between the two feed types was the inclusion of 0.2 % Guar gum, no difference between ash 
content was expected. Although the difference was small and possibly could be caused by random 
biological differences, it could also be related to the higher feed waste observed in systems fed 
binder diet. As ash constitutes inorganic compounds, primarily minerals, any non-removed feed 
waste would increase the ash content in the sludge, as fewer minerals were absorbed by the fish. 
Undetected feed waste would therefore increase the ash content in the sludge.  
 
4.5 Bacterial activity 
 
Bacterial activity was estimated as the elimination rate constant determined by the degradation of 
H2O2 (HP-activity) and using the BactiQuant® method (BQV). HP-activity was determined at all 
sampling dates during the entire experimental period (fig. 3.16), whereas BQV values were only 
determined on the last sampling date (13/03) (fig. 3.17). Bacterial activity was hypothesized to be 
lower in systems fed binder compared to the control systems due to less organic particles being 
present, serving as energy substrate. There was a tendency towards a higher BQV in binder systems 
==PAGE:58==
57 
 
compared to the control systems (fig. 3.17). Elimination rate constants were lower in binder systems 
(fig. 3.16), indicating a lower bacterial activity, but the difference was not significant. Elimination 
rate constants increased throughout the duration of the experiment, indicating an increase in 
bacterial activity/presence (fig. 3.16). This was confirmed by the same findings by Tirado et al 
(2018). In the present experiment, the HP-activity (≈ 2.5 h-1) was higher than the 1.70 h-1 found by 
Tirado et al (2018). This was in spite of a daily feed load of 2 kg/m3 MUW in the present 
experiment, compared to 3.25 kg/m3 MUW in the study by Tirado et al (2018). There was no 
significant positive correlation between HP activity and BOD5part during the trial period, which was 
not expected as particulate organic matter serves as both energy and colonizable area for bacteria, 
as found in other studies (Prest et al, 2016; Pedersen et al, 2017). This could, however, have to do 
with the HP-activity that also assessed the presence of free-living bacteria, where surface areas were 
less important. There was a positive correlation between BOD5part and BQV, which was expected as 
the BactiQuant® method was targeted at measuring surface-colonizing bacteria. The correlation 
was, though, not significant, and was presumably due to a small test size (N = 6).  A negative 
correlation between HP-activity and BQV was observed but was not significant. This difference 
was surprising, as a positive correlation was expected, but could be related to the small test size for 
the correlation test (N = 6) as well. BactiQuant® assesses the bacterial activity by measuring a 
specific hydrolase enzyme in most of the surface colonizing bacteria (Reeslev et al, 2011), whereas 
HP-activity focuses more widely on all bacteria present, including free-living bacteria. In a previous 
study, Pedersen et al (2017) found a linear correlation between particle surface area and BQV, thus 
estimating the presence of surface-colonizing bacteria. As previously mentioned, small particles 
have a larger surface area to volume ratio. As small particles were more common in control 
systems, BQV for these systems was therefore expected to be higher, but was not observed. 
However, as the cumulative TSA for binder systems was higher than for the control systems, the 
higher BQV observed in binder system could simply be due to the presence of more total surface 
area. Formalin treatment had an observable negative effect on bacterial activity, which was not 
surprising due to the bactericidal effect of formaldehyde (Pedersen et al, 2007; Sortkjær et al, 
2008). The effect of formaldehyde could, however, act in two ways, as it might have served as 
energy substrate for some bacterial strains while at the same time exhibiting its bactericidal effect 
on other bacterial strains (Pedersen et al, 2007). Formaldehyde could potentially also have changed 
the bacterial composition in the biofilter, favouring unwanted fast-growing opportunists (r-
strategist) rather than more slow-growing specialists (K-strategists), as proposed by Vadstein et al 
==PAGE:59==
58 
 
(2004). Such a breakdown of the bacterial community would also supply the pool of organic matter, 
as dead microorganism and biofilm from the biofilter would contribute with organic carbon, as 
described by Pedersen et al (2007). Thus, this would provide bacteria with an extra source of 
energy and at the same time increase the presence of organic matter, which would be reflected in 
both BOD5 and COD levels.  
 
4.6 Fish performance 
 
The fish accepted both diets well, despite the period of appetite loss. The feed waste from each 
system was counted as the number of uneaten feed pellets collected in the solid collectors each day. 
There was a small tendency for feed waste to be higher in systems fed binder, but this was not 
significant. SGR was measured as the biomass growth in percentage per day. There was no 
significant difference between the two feed types, although there was a clear difference towards a 
reduced growth in binder systems (table 3.2). FCR did not differ significantly between the two feed 
types, although FCR in binder systems was higher than in the control systems (table 3.3). The 
relation between SGR and FCR was probably related to a larger feed waste rather than reduced 
utilization of the feed. The effect of Guar gum on FCR and SGR has been examined in previous 
studies (Brinker, 2007; Brinker and Reiter, 2011), showing no effect at low inclusion levels (< 0.3 
%). Similarly, the variation between the two feed types in relation to both FCR and SGR in the 
present experiment was not assumed to be related to the feed type. However, it could be related to 
the observation that the appetite loss prior to the start of the experiment was more pronounced in 
binder systems, resulting in a lower weight gain and higher feed waste in those systems.  
 
4.7 Nutrients  
 
Concentration of Total Ammonia-N (TAN) varied during the trial period (fig. 3.21) and exhibited 
very high concentrations (≈ 6 mg/L) compared to recommendations (Dalsgaard et al, 2013). 
Although concentrations were high, fish behaviour did not seem to be influenced. The high 
concentrations were presumably related to the decline in biofilter performance due to the formalin 
treatment. Alternatively, the high concentrations may have been an artifact of the measuring 
method. Formaldehyde had previously shown interference with the reagents in the ammonium test, 
causing artificial high concentrations (Sortkjær et al, 2008). The same pattern as for TAN was 
observed for nitrite, with even more harmful concentrations being observed (fig. 3.22). In theory, 
==PAGE:60==
59 
 
the nitrite concentrations should have had a toxic effect on the fish. However, municipal tap water 
was used in this experiment deriving from a water board located close to the sea (< 5 km). This 
might have resulted in higher, but allowed, chloride concentrations in the water, compared to water 
from far inland drinking wells. As chloride has a reducing effect on the toxicity of nitrite (Francis-
Floyd et al, 2009), this explains why no intoxication of fish was observed in spite of high 
concentrations. Variations in TAN and nitrite concentrations were observed throughout the duration 
of the experiment period and were most likely due to the imbalances that formaldehyde caused in 
the biofilter by suppressing ammonia- and nitrite-oxidizing bacteria (Sortkjær et al, 2008). 
Although low dosages of formaldehyde were applied, the effect on the biofilter was greater than 
anticipated. As biofilter material was obtained from an already-established RAS, equilibrium should 
in theory have been reached, and the biofilter should have been able to withstand the low dosage of 
formaldehyde being applied, compared to another study (Pedersen et al, 2007).  
Nitrate-N was expected to increase over time due to a continuous conversion of nitrite into nitrate. 
Also, the accumulation of nitrate-N was expected to follow an exponential mode as shown by 
Tirado et al (2017), where the increase in nitrate-N would decline over time and stabilize around an 
asymptotic equilibrium determined by the daily replacement of water. The equilibrium in nitrate-N 
concentration was not reached in the present experiment, as concentrations continued to increase 
(fig. 3.23). Also, the concentration of nitrate-N was most likely influenced by both steps in the 
nitrification process were hampered, reducing the input of nitrate. This meant that the daily water 
renewal removed nitrate from the system, reducing concentrations. If biofilters had not been 
impaired, a more stable accumulation of nitrate-N would most likely have been observable, as this 
has been the case in previous studies (Tirado et al, 2017; Tirado et al, 2018). Furthermore, might 
the accumulation have been significant if nitrate-N concentration was measured from the start of 
fish insertion to the systems, as the steepest part of the accumulation then would be included in the 
graph. Total nitrogen was measured at raw, dissolved and particulate level. In general, the 
concentrations were lower in systems fed binder compared to the control systems, but the effect was 
not significant for any of the three fractions (fig. 3.24 - 3.25). This was most likely due to lower 
concentrations of TAN, nitrite and nitrate, causing a lower cumulative sum of nitrogen compounds 
in binder systems, rather than it being a direct effect of the binder. However, if it was a property of 
the binder the causing of larger particles could lead to reducing leaching of nutrients, as the exposed 
water to surface area would be reduced. Reduced leaching has previously been reported as an effect 
of Guar gum (Brinker et al, 2005). In comparison to results obtained by Tirado et al, (2017)

concentrations of total nitrogen in raw samples were similar in the present study, averaging 35 – 45 
mg/L during the trial period compared to approximately 50 mg/L in the mentioned study.

experiment. The high formation was independent of feed type and occurred with varying degrees in 
all six systems. This event was a reminder that the present swirl separators might have been 
underdimensioned.

As solids provide both surface area and energy, a suitable habitat for bacterial growth was provided.

observed. More solids will also result in the presence of more organic material in the system, which

biofilter, as organic matter has been found to cause imbalances in biofilter performance. The 
underdimensioned swirl separator could also cause reduced removal efficiency, thereby leading to 
an increased presence of easily degradable organic matter. The focus in future studies should 
therefore be to examine how Guar gum and the presence of a drum filter influence these parameters.

4.9 Results in relation to commercial facilities  
 
Despite the potential beneficial effect dietary inclusion of Guar gum has shown in previous studies 
(Brinker et al, 2005; Brinker, 2007), no published studies have examined the effect of Guar gum in 
relation to commercial RAS. This experiment was conducted in a pilot scale setup; hence the results 
were not directly comparable to commercial facilities. The main reason for this was an immense 
difference in system dimension between the present pilot scale and a commercial RAS. Pilot scale 
systems are used to evaluate experiments, whereas commercial facilities are designed with the 
overall aim of optimizing economic profit. In spite of these differences, the results presented here 
still give some indications of the potential effect in a large-scale set up.  
In a commercial RAS the stocking densities are typically higher than the 19 kg/m3 applied in the 
present experiment. The higher the stocking density, the more feed needs to be applied, thus the 
higher the load of organic matter and solids (Tirado et al, 2017). As a consequence, the binder 
effect would presumably be even more pronounced in a commercial facility. However, the effect 
might be reduced by the larger hydrodynamical forces taking place on a commercial scale, due to 
the magnification of piping, pumps, etc. resulting in greater hydrodynamical forces (Lekang, 2008). 
In commercial RAS, solid management is considered the most difficult task as it influences all 
water quality parameters in the system (Badiola et al, 2012). In such systems, solid removal is 
performed mechanically, typically by a drum filter which is usually limited to a particle size of 40 
µm. Drum filters have a higher removal efficacy compared to swirl separators, as particles are 
removed based on their size rather than their ability to settle out, which is influenced by their 
density, shape, etc. (Lekang, 2008). Though more efficient, the tendency of particle disintegration is 
larger in drum filters, as particles are removed more harshly. To avoid blockage of the filter, 
backwashing of the drum filter is applied, which tends to wear the filter (Lekang, 2008). If less fine 
particles are present, the need for backwashing is reduced, which in turn prolong the longevity of 
the filter, thus reducing the operational costs.  
As conditions in commercial RAS are well-suited for bacteria growth due to the presence of 
nutrients and surface area, such systems are typically equipped with disinfection installations 
(Lekang, 2008). Mostly, chemical treatment, UV-radiation or Ozone treatments of the water are 
preferred, either individually or in combination. As a reduction in particles would improve 
microbial water quality, the level of application of these treatments could be reduced. Reducing the 
level of particles present would also improve the effect of these treatments. This would lead to 
reduced operating costs and it would create a more stable microflora.  
==PAGE:63==
62 
 
In a RAS, devices such a pumps and drum filters are turned on at all time. Even though Guar gum 
inclusion might only have a small effect on water quality, all these cost savings when added up, 
could result in an improved economy of the RAS.

5 Conclusion 
 
Inclusion of Guar gum as a dietary binder has previously been shown to have a positive effect on 
water quality parameters such as particle level and suspended solids in flow-through systems. 
However, the effect in RAS, where particles are more exposed due to reuse and hydrodynamical 
forces, had not previously been examined. Therefore, the aim of this study was to examine how 
Guar gum, added as a dietary binder to a rainbow trout diet, affected the level of suspended 
particles and overall water quality when fed to simple, pilot scale RAS. The experimental setup 
included 6 identical, independent RAS. In relation to water quality parameters, the primary focus 
was particle load and distributions, organic matter, solid characteristics and bacterial activity. To 
control overall system stability and performance TAN, NO2-, NO3- and TN were also measured. 
Likewise, were fish performance in form of SGR and FCR also measured.  
The fish accepted both types of feed well, and no significant differences were observed regarding 
FCR and SGR, though growth was lower in binder systems. This was, however, not an intended 
feature of the binder. The general load of organic matter was high in the present experiment, in 
comparison to that seen in other studies, and the inclusion of Guar gum did not significantly reduce 
the presence of organic matter. Inclusion of the binder had, however, a significant effect on particle 
load, resulting in fewer small particles (< 30 µm) and more large particles (> 30 µm). There were 
no significant fluctuations during the day in relation to particle load, thus the binder had no effect 
on this. There was a significantly larger solid production in binder systems compared to control 
systems. Also, there was a significantly higher removal of DM from binder systems. Bacterial 
activities were measured using two different methods, HP-activity and BactiQuant®. HP-activity 
was lower in binder systems, whereas BactiQuant® values were higher in binder systems. None of 
the differences were however significant. An unexpected outbreak of white spot disease, resulting 
in appetite loss and increased mortality was treated with formaldehyde twice during the experiment. 
This treatment caused fluctuations of TAN and NO2- due to inhibition of the biofilter and affected 
the load of organic matter acting as a carbon source and by disruption of biofilm. The present 
experiment was conducted in pilot scale RAS, which differs from large commercial RAS due to 
differences in hydrodynamical forces and solid removal technology. It should therefore be possible 
to obtain an even better water quality effect of Guar gum inclusion in commercial RAS. However, 
future studies have to be conducted to examine the exact effect in such commercial facilities.
