Abstract  
It is hard to deny that plenty of countries worldwide have adopted photovoltaic (PV) energy 
generation in their total electrical energy generation policies. Financing the non-polluting renewable sources, 
solar power has become one of the main production solutions for the most developed countries. On the 
contrary the continuously growing numbers of decentralized energy sources result additional consequences 
that are mainly related to the need for energy management to adjust the consumption patterns with the 
injected power from those sources.  
Storage of the produced PV energy at the local decentralized systems offers numerous advantages 
balancing the fluctuating power generation capacity. Those storage systems are the main topic under 
discussion for this thesis report. The inverter companies have turned their interest to combine the DC to AC 
topologies with battery management systems to increase the efficiency of the produced energy in parallel with 
the storage of the generated energy locally. In most of the solar inverters on the market a DC link is included 
where maximum power point tracking (MPPT) performed which is a common technique to optimize the power 
that is delivered to the grid. At this part of the circuit a boost converter is implemented especially in non-
isolated grid tight DC to AC inverters. The total efficiency of such inverters depends on both the inverter leg 
losses but in addition on the losses that occur on the DC link.  
The PV-to-battery storage is the main focus of this study that is under examination where the storage 
topology is combined with the PV circuit and tested under load. The study further analyses the losses that 
occur in these converters and the best possible interconnection between them in order to increase the overall 
efficiency of not only the converters but also the storage strategy. In this study, two topologies are 
implemented in the laboratory where they are tested at full power aiming to derive the efficiency graphs that 
conclude the performance under the multiple working scenarios of depth of discharge (DOD) and solar energy 
conditions to. As a conclusion the study aims to comment on the advantages and the disadvantages of those 
two possible circuit configurations.

Motivation of the project 
 
1.  Motivation of the project 
 
This chapter summarises the global trends for the photovoltaic systems globally that introduce power stability 
issues to the grid. Because of those issues the need for energy improvements becomes significant goal to the 
power electronics engineering and constituted the motivation of the project.  
1.1 
Current global trends  
Photovoltaic market had a record year on the installation of PV systems that resulted the capacity to increase 
by 98% the past two years [1]. Fig. 1 illustrates the rapid increase of the PV capacity for the nine countries that 
embraced the solar energy production strategy.   
 
Fig. 1. Solar PV Capacity and Additions, Top 10 Countries, 2013 [1] 
 
One of the reasons of such a growth is the continually falling of the PV module manufacturing costs due to low 
material costs (especially for Polycrystalline silicon), combined with improvements in manufacturing processes 
that scaled up with top Chinese producers approaching costs of USD 0.12/W in 2013. To support that, over the 
last decade, PV module production share has shifted from the United States to Asia with China dominating 
shipments since 2009. By 2013 Asia accounted for 87% of global production with China only having the 67% of 
the world total production in 2012 [1]. 
 
1.2 
Grid disturbances and stress 
 
As a result of this increasing growth, additional considerations are required in order to successfully integrate 
the electricity generated of the renewable energy sources (RES-E) like PV systems are. These aforementioned 
increasing trends of the PV installations are introducing important issues to the power generation. The reason 
is that because of the fact that the amount of energy injected to the grid is fluctuating - depended on the solar 
irradiation and the weather condition - the solar generation patterns are often unpredictable since they 
depend on the solar irradiation and the weather conditions. Apart from that, the large growth rates of installed 
power lead to situations where the generated power exceeds the demand. Fig. 2 summarizes the capacity in 
some of the European countries. Most of them have a big residential share of the total production that 
strengthens the need for stability management.

reinforcements as long as the majority of the produced power is consumed at the same voltage level. Thus, a 
decentralized power policy (storing of energy close to the production) can be proven beneficial due to the 
short distances and therefore minimize the transition losses and supporting with high feed-in energy when 
needed.

Li-ion Batteries and the impact on the dc-dc converters 
 
2. Li-ion Batteries and the impact on the dc-dc converters 
 
This chapter describes the current state of the art in Li-ion battery technologies that are commonly used for 
battery banks. These batteries are able to combine high power and energy density with a large life span. No 
batteries were used for the project but understanding their operational characteristics is important to size the 
components and the operation limitations. 
 
2.1 Battery Technologies 
 
Li-ion batteries are divided into classifications that are based on the cathode and anode different materials, 
while other divisions are based on the electrolyte (liquid vs. polymer electrolytes) and on the battery casing 
(cylindrical, prismatic and pouch cells). For the purposes of the thesis not further details on those chemical 
characteristics will be analyzed as long as those do not affect the design of the storage systems [4].  
 
Table 1: Different battery technologies and its characteristics [5] 
 
 
Various battery technologies have been used in electrical systems as storage components. They differ from 
each other in terms of chemical and electrical properties. Table 1 summarizes the main electrical properties of 
two battery technologies, namely lead-acid and lithium-ion (Li-Ion). Due to technical and most importantly 
economic reasons, these are the main technologies which are expected to be used as storage elements in PV 
systems. LiFePO4 (LFP- Lithium iron phosphate battery) is the battery technology that is used widely in the 
Electrical vehicle manufacturers due to its low cost materials. Moreover, these cathode materials are very safe 
as they are unreactive with electrolytes up to 350oC and thus they are used by many EV manufacturers due to 
its high safety level, the high power capability and the long cycle life. 
 
Some of the summarized advantages are: 
1. Low self-discharge rate in between 2 to 8 % per month. 
2. Long cycle life of more than 1000 deep-cycles, reaching 3000 cycles at 80 % depth of discharge (DoD) 
when the appropriate voltage limits are respected. 
3. Broad temperature range: charging is allowed between 70 and -45oC, while discharging is allowed 
between -40 and 65oC. Although these temperatures are allowed, in order to prolong the battery life 
time the operating ranges are -20 to 50oC. 
4. High cycle efficiency (Eout/Ein ) of 90% , depending on the charge and discharge rate.  
5. Low maintenance: no memory effect, no scheduled cycling to prolong battery life, no electrolyte refill 
that are usual maintenance tasks that should be performed for other types to ensure the safety of the 
installation.

Some of the drawbacks of the Li-ion batteries are: 
1. Degrade issues when discharged below 2-2.5V and thus the battery charger needs to be able to 
measure the battery voltage and safely stop the discharging below the specified limits 
2. In order to prolong cycle life, cooling is necessary when the battery temperature rises above 30-40oC 
because above the limit temperature the batteries degrade really fast. Furthermore, heating is 
necessary for most cell chemistries below 0oC. 
3. Among the safety concerns the implementation of mechanical safeguards at cell level and a Battery 
Management System at pack level is very important and require extra balancing circuits. These 
systems compare the cell voltage of one battery with the voltage drop of the previous battery in the 
battery bank. Then during discharges the battery with the highest cell voltage start first to discharge 
over a balancing resistor.  
4. Li-ion batteries are expensive, with a price per kWh ranging between 300 and 900 $ per kWh in 2012 
which is three times more expensive than lead-acid batteries with a comparable capacity. But, on the 
other hand, Li-Ion batteries can achieve up to 20 years of operation, in contrast to only five years 
expected for the lead-acid types. An alternative to increase the lifetime of lead-acid batteries is to 
oversize the storage capacity of the system. This would avoid a deep discharge of the batteries; 
however it would increase the cost of the overall installation and the size of the storage room that are 
placed. 
 
Charging times present an additional technical challenge and a commercial barrier that must be addressed and 
is explained later in this chapter. When it comes to using the EV battery, charging by means of a 240-volt out 
let with increased power (40 amps) can take two hours, while charging at a commercial three-phase charging  
station can take as little as 30 minutes [6]. Usually, fast charging methods employ more sophisticated charging 
techniques to reduce this time. More details on the EV charger is explained in appendix A where the existing EV 
car manufacturers follow the market rules aiming to increase the battery capacity following the EPA strategy 
for testing the range of battery on EVs. No further study was performed to investigate the charging techniques 
because these techniques are based on more sophisticated charging circuits that their efficiencies are quite 
lower than the ones the thesis is aiming for.  
 
It is true that most of the battery technologies require the smallest possible current ripple for both charging 
and discharging states. These restrictions, in addition to a balanced design in terms of the physical size of the 
filters, result the ripple requirements and sizes of the components of the filters listed later in Table 5 of 
following chapter. 
2.2 Charging and discharging limitations  
 
Charging time is limited primarily by the capacity of the grid connection. A typical household system delivers 
1.5 kilowatts (in the US, Canada, Japan, and other countries with 110 volt supply) and 3 kilowatts (in countries 
with 240 V supply) [7]. Those voltage levels combined with current limitations of domestic consumers with 
single phase systems of up to 25A and some with 3 phase system fused at 16-25 amp allowing for a theoretical 
capacity of around 5.7kW for the first and 11-17 kW for the least. However, this capacity is high it is not meant 
to be reached practically and it is meant to feed multiple domestic loads of the same location. Even if the 
supply power could be reached, most batteries do not accept charge at greater than their charge rate ("1C"), 
because high charge rate has adverse effect on the discharge capacities of batteries.

resistance of the battery ESR). The effect of high ripple current on the batteries is typically seen as excess of

3.1 State of the art solutions  
 
Several single-phase photovoltaic inverter manufacturers embraced the idea to integrate the battery 
management stage - which is responsible for storing the excess energy to the battery bank - into the existing 
inverter topology. Typically the photovoltaic converter, for residential use of about 5kWatt installed power, is 
composed by a boost converter or a buck converter that is directly connected to the PV strings of panels 
(where the MPP tracking is applied) tied to a DC link capacitor stage (DC link) and then to the DC to AC 
converter as Fig. 6 illustrates. At that part of the circuit configuration only filtering is needed to match with the 
THD regulations and the circuit configuration can be connected to the grid.

4.3 Inductors loss estimations 
 
At this part of the thesis the inductors design is presented followed by measurement results and a discussion of 
the losses and the temperature rise of the final products. For the inductors design several core materials and 
types are considered to be suitable for such application. Selecting a power inductor is a function of several 
trade-offs. A designer aim for the following: 
 
cost of the core material 
 
lowest possible resistance of the winding  
 
highest possible current rating 
 
best saturation characteristics 
 
size and space available on the circuit board 
For the purposes of the thesis the cost the simplicity and the saturation of the core was the main goals.  
4.3.1 Materials and saturation  
Magnetic iron powder core material is the most cost effective type and is made by having iron or iron alloy 
powder mixed or glued with an insulation material and compressed.  
The main characteristics are: 
 
Distributed gap which gives low value of effective relative permeability 
 
Same effects as that of an air gap distributed along the core  
 
High DC current can be tolerated before the iron saturates 
 
The linear dimension of the iron particles is less than the skin depth at the desired operating 
frequency, resulting in low eddy current loss 
 
The resulting inductance values tend to be very stable over a wide temperature range. 
Powder cores are suitable for applications where air gap are desired, such as energy storage inductors of 
switched mode power supplies, filters and chokes. 
 
The inductors are designed in such a way that no second layer is needed to from the required number of turns 
which results no proximity effect issues (refer to Appendix C). Apart from that, the inner cross diameter of the 
inductors is picked to be enough to include the required number of turns and leave enough distance to reduce 
the skin effect phenomena and the parasitic capacitance between the windings with different voltage drop. 
Some design hints have been taken into consideration for optimising the final inductors. One way to reduce the 
total inductor losses (to reduce the core losses) is to increase the effective length 𝑙𝑒 while preserving 𝐴𝑒 (to 
keep copper losses constant). Additionally, by stacking several cores on top of each other it is achieved an

7. Conclusion 
 
To summarise and evaluate the research performed on the project let us have a final overview that will help to 
conclude. The losses investigation that performed revealed some key losses characteristics that affect the 
overall conclusion and comments and created the need for further investigation.  
 
Starting from the switching losses of the semiconductors it was clear that in the Direct Storage topology the 
fact that the PV boost converter does not operate, gives a big advantage to this configuration than the 
conventional one. Despite the fact that the stress on the IGBTs is greater when the Direct Storage operate the 
final measurements with the not optimum Toroid inductors result higher efficiency of the Direct Storage rather 
than the Conventional Storage (operation of the Buck converter) because the streets of the inductors is higher.  
 
To continue, the initially designed inductors proved to be inefficient enough affecting the overall losses of the 
circuits but a clear picture could extracted when it comes to the comparison between the Conventional and the 
Direct circuit configuration. Further measurements were performed in order to investigate how these losses 
affect the total efficiency of the converters and how critical can they be. The results showed significant 
improvements but the overall result, when it comes to the comparison between the two topologies, is the 
same. 
 
The measured and estimated results where enough in order to conclude to the key characteristics of the 
project. Based on the final results, having a first comparison it can be clearly seen from both the measured 
data and the laboratory measurements that the Conventional Storage is less efficient than the Direct Storage 
one. On the other hand, aiming for high efficiency energy conversion the PV boost converter proved to be a 
quite inefficient (<97%). Especially if it is taken into account that, as part of the inverter circuit, it is followed by 
a DC to AC converter that add losses reaching at best the efficiency of 98%.  
 
Evaluating the progress and the final outcome of the thesis, it is clear that the importance of the inductors was 
underestimated from the beginning causing the need for two rounds of measurements. On the other hand, the 
fact that the potential that efficiency can be improved using the Direct Storage is proven and that generates 
the basis for further optimisation on the design.

8. Comments on future work 
 
 To sum up, further investigation need to be performed in order to optimise the efficiency of the boost and the 
buck converters individually. One additional and hopefully not dominant disadvantage of the Direct Storage 
system is the fact that the MPPT algorithm has to be modified in such a way that the PV boost converter

semiconductors. Additionally, the diode reverse-recovery current and the IGBT switching losses can be 
drastically reduced by carefully choosing the diodes. The double pulse measurement circuit should be 
performed in order to determine the switching losses of the switching losses especially if switching frequency 
of above the 20 kHz is chosen there the switching losses become dominant factor.  The potential use of 
MosFets is another field of improvement since using MosFets choosing higher switching frequency is a subject 
under discussion. 
 
In addition to the losses, we should keep in mind that the ripple to the battery has to be kept as low as possible 
as the temperature rise is an important factor of the design. Choosing one stage buck converter and low 
switching frequency will always result high ripple current. The possibility of using interleaved stages and low 
switching frequency seems to be a fair solution that could improve the overall efficiency keeping low ripple.

are usually considered portable equipment. This type provides an economical and versatile EV 
charging solution 
 
Level 2: Permanently wired electric vehicle supply equipment is used only for EV charging and are 
rated from 208 up to 240 volts AC and up to 60 amps or otherwise 14.4 kilowatts charging five times 
faster than the level 1 ones.

greater than 14.4 kilowatts. Fast chargers are rated as Level 3, but not all Level 3 chargers are fast 
chargers. This designation depends on the size of the battery pack to be charged and how much time 
is required to charge the battery pack. A charger can be considered a fast charger if it can charge an 
average electric vehicle battery pack in 30 minutes or less.

 
Both the devices are voltage controlled. Gate-drive requirements for IGBTs and power MOSFETs are 
similar [31]. They both require 12 to 20V for full turn-on the device and need negative or not voltage 
for turn-off.  
 
In a MOSFET, the power losses come mainly from conduction losses, and switching losses are 
negligible at frequencies as high as 20 kHz. In an IGBT, conduction losses are much lower than in a 
MOSFET, but then switching losses become significant at high switching frequencies.  
 
The on state resistance has no theoretical limit, hence ON state losses can be far lower. The MOSFET 
also has a body drain diode, which is particularly useful in dealing with limited free-wheeling currents. 
IGBTs have advantages of high current handling capability but still have the disadvantages of a 
comparatively large current tail and no body drain diode [32] (added nowadays into the package). To 
lower RDS(ON) in MOSFETs manufacturers combine millions of tiny MOSFETs in parallel. The parallel 
connection results in extremely low on-resistance, in some cases, only a few milliohms. 
 
Most of the IGBTs are PT (Punch Through) devices. In general, PT devices have a NTC (Negative 
Temperature Coefficient). This is related to their operation when they are connected in parallel and 
the PT devices do not share current when operated in parallel (packaging them together can improve 
their performance). MOSFETs have a PTC (positive temperature coefficient) that provides good 
current sharing by stopping thermal runaway.  
 
In general, MOSFETs typically excel for lower power and higher frequency applications while IGBTs have distinct 
advantages for lower frequency and higher power designs. To sum up, choosing between IGBTs and MOSFETs is 
deepened mostly on the application specific characteristics since the cost, the size, the switching speed and 
thermal requirements are questioned.

To reach the high efficiency goal, power-supply devises must conclude the new power converter electronic 
components such as high-voltage silicon-carbide (SiC) Schottky rectifiers. These diodes take advantage of

that the cost of such diodes is dramatically higher than the silicone rectifiers. Moreover, according to [38] the 
limited temperature dependence the SiC diodes give more reliable characteristics compared to the Si diodes 
that becomes important factor for high power switching topologies.

F. 
Power stage PCB design 
 
Designing the power stage PCB requires a careful investigation on the circuit. A basic property of a transmission 
line (now the PCB traces) is the characteristic impedance [39]. That impedance changes depending on the 
layout (traces, vias, connectors, ect.) and results reflections and noise problems. To avoid those phenomena 
the designer should: 
 
Avoid the 45corner bends in the traces and try to route them rounded 
 
Separate high-speed signals (switching signals) from low-speed signals (power) 
==PAGE:64==
64 
Appendix 
 
 
Separate the digital (gate driver circuits) from analog parts of the board (high voltage) 
The inductance of the traces is proportional to the length of the trace. For each part of the board that high 
power is transferred, that stray inductance creates voltage drops, radiation and propagation delay for the 
signals. Gate drivers design was made by the PhD student Alex Anthon but the drivers have to be placed as 
close as possible so the inductance has to be kept low. By minimizing the length of the traces and expand their 
width but also considering the clearances the final design is illustrated by Fig. 64. 
 
Power loops 
The high frequency power loops needed to be recognized for the design of the PCB.  The main power loops 
consist of the return path through the load back to the -Bat and/or the –PV connector.  Another loop is the one 
of the supply of the gate drivers Vcc+ to GND. Because of the fact that there are many different loops in every 
circuit the need of a ground plane becomes important. But to avoid noise coupling between the digital part 
and the sensitive analog part the ground planes are split. 
 
Fig. 62: High frequency switching nodes 
 
High frequency switching nodes 
As Fig. 62 indicates with the red dashed lines the traces between the switched are the most critical nodes of 
the power stage PCB. Keeping the traces short and wide enough the parasitic inductance (L trace) is minimum. 
This inductance combined with the parasitic capacitance between the inductor windings and the ones of the 
IGBT and the diode cause unwanted voltage and current oscillations [40]. 
 
Gate driver implementation 
Commonly in high power electronics the Gate drivers are placed right next to the switches. In this project 
because of flexibility it was designed to be placed vertically to the power stage PCB. It was hard to place them 
in another way and keeping at the same time the high switching nodes as short as possible.  
==PAGE:65==
65 
Appendix 
 
 
Fig. 63: Gate driver and power stage diagram including parasitic 
 
Parasitic turn-on of the gate driver is one of the most important considerations for designing the gate driver 
and can be prevented by reducing the off-gate resistor. The smaller off resistor will also reduce switching loss 
but this causes higher over-shoot Vce and oscillation during turn-off due to stray inductances. Those stray 
inductances can be high enough to cause this high over shoot and the this is where the power stage should 
keep them as low as possible as Fig. 64 shows with yellow colour for the IGBT S1 and   
 
Other design hits are taken into account: 
A routed trace over a gaps or around holes creates large loop areas, because the return current cannot flow 
beside the signal, and the signal can induce noise into the nonrelated reference plane. Here in order to place 
the IGBTs on the heat sink holes are needed to have access to the screws of the IGBTs so carefully the holes 
are placed to avoid creation of loop areas.  
 Attention should be played at the return current. It should not be routed signals referenced to digital ground 
over analog ground and vice versa. The return current cannot take the direct way along the signal trace and so 
a loop area occurs. Thus, the position of the +Load point and the –Load, –Bat and -PV points are critical 
keeping the length of the loops as short as possible.

G. 
Heat sink calculations  
 
In this part of the Appendix the suitable heat sink is being determined. The calculations below lead to choose 
the type, the size and the shape of the heat sink. Natural conversion was chosen as long as those are the 
cheapest types of heat sinks can be found. Since the size is quite bigger than the forced convection ones both 
topologies can be mounted on one heat sink but not operate at the same time. 
==PAGE:66==
66 
Appendix 
 
 
Initially the calculations aim that both the IGBTs and the Diodes could me mounted to the same heat sink but 
because of a mistake on the footprints of the Altium designer only the IGBTs are finally mounted on the heat 
sink. Both the IGBT’s and the diodes have maximum operation temperature 175oC which means the used heat 
sinks should ensure this limitation. Moreover, even if the heat sinks will be calculated, an external fan is used in 
case of a miss calculation and the thermal camera is used to capture the heat sink temperature but not the 
junction one. 
 
The construction of the thermal equivalent diagram is given by a proportional dependency between the losses 
and the rising temperature. The proportional factor has the dimension K/W and it is called the thermal 
resistance. For the designed converters we will need to attach to a heat sink the three IGBTs and two additional 
heat sinks are used for the diodes. The values of the thermal resistance are given by the manufacturers of both 
diodes and IGBTs. The following list contains the values extracted from the datasheets of the IGBT and the 
diode. 
 
Fig. 65: A schematic drawing of the modules mounted on a heat sink 
 
In addition thermal grease is used between the TO220 and the isolation pad and also between the pad and the 
heat-sink it is observed see in the Fig. 65. The grease is used to increase the contact surface of the TO220 and

H. 
Control board design 
 
Based on previous experience of controlling other projects during the studies the main control board used by 
the university C2000 Piccolo LaunchPad5 proved to be quite noisy. Namely, the ADC inputs of the DSP seem to 
pick up noise that affects the read signals of the microcontroller causing the control loops to be unstable. Fig. 
68 illustrates the read signal on the ADC6. The values can vary between about 70 to 100 bits which can be 
translated to voltages up to 15V on the DC link. That will affects the robustness of the controller and thus the 
control board was decided to be re-designed. 
 
 
Fig. 68: Signals read for the ADC6 of the TI launchPad (left) and of the designed control board (right) for 
different voltage 0-3.3V (0-610V) or 0 to 4094 bits. 
 
The control board design borrowed a lot of time of the thesis aiming to help perform control on the PCB. The 
board contain 4 main parts: 
 
Power Supply 
At that part three isolated voltage level are supplied that contain ±12𝑉, ±5𝑉, 𝑎𝑛𝑑 3.3𝑉 for the DSP. Carefully 
placed on the PCB using a split on the ground plane as Fig. 70 shows (ground plane – yellow layer), the power 
supply is designed to supply all operational amplifiers and voltage opto-couplers described below. The 
                                                                
5 Texas Instruments Launchpad (http://www.ti.com/tool/launchxl-f28027)

Operational amplifiers 
At first three dual supply single Op Amp (see Voltage measurement Appendix I for details) that operate at ±5𝑉 
with SOT235 package, and two single supply dual Op Amp (see Current measurement Appendix I for details)

used for any other application as filters or signal amplifiers which add flexibility to the control board for other 
projects as they can be part of the feedback loops or combined with other voltage and current measurement 
components. 


Low pass filtering

bits.

The following ‘check list’ summarise some design considerations where taken into account based on [41] and 
[42]  for high speed design of PCBs. 
 
Do not split the ground plane creating narrow paths but use one solid plane under both analog and 
digital sections of the board but separate the analogue from the digital power planes. 
 
Spread the ground plane along the PCB creating a large area for low impedance current return paths 
 
Keep over 75% board area for the ground plane 
 
Locate all analogue and power components and lines together over one part of the ground plane and 
all digital components and traces over the rest of the ground plane. 
 
Do not route traces over the split in the power planes and think about where and how the ground 
return currents are actually flowing 
 
Keep enough clearance between signal traces, short distance between them cause stray capacitance 
that slow down the signals and creates ringing to switching signals  
 
Minimize trace lengths to avoid trace inductance and keep the amplifier connectors (inputs and 
outputs) as close as possible. 
 
Use bypass capacitors but put them on the same layer and use many vias for their ground connection
