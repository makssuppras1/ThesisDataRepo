Abstract
Human labor in today’s production lines is often physically demanding, very repetitive, and
thus unhealthy for the workforce, but remains too complex to replace by current commer­
cial standardized automation solutions. One of the primary roadblocks in fully automating
all production lines is that industrial robots, in general, are unable to adapt to even small
changes in their environment. This can be attributed to two main issues: First, robot ma­
nipulators, as they are implemented in the industry, do not receive sensor inputs about
general changes in their environment. Second, the joint level controllers used by robot
manipulators in the industry are unable to handle large changes in the dynamics of these
robot manipulators.
In this project, state of the art literature within robot manipulator adaptive control, as well
as visual object detection, tracking, and prediction is reviewed and presented. A predictive
and adaptive control framework has been developed and implemented, allowing a robot
manipulator to track an object approaching from an arbitrary direction.
An Asynchronous Predictive Kalman Filter is designed and implemented, allowing for
predictive position and velocity tracking of a target object based on the input from a cam­
era mounted above the robot manipulator. Control logic is designed and implemented,
allowing the robot to interact with a target object when within reach, using the inverse
kinematics solver TRAC­IK.
Two joint space adaptive controllers are implemented and tested on the framework: the
hyperstability based Model Reference Adaptive Controller (MRAC) and the Active Infer­
ence Controller (AIC). The MRAC is used as a baseline for testing the AIC, since the
MRAC is a conventional adaptive controller and the AIC first appears in literature regard­
ing robot manipulators in 2020.
The predictive framework is found to be able to continue tracking the target object, despite
occlusions, as long as the target trajectory predictor has received sufficient measurements
to predict the target trajectory during the time it is occluded.
The AIC was found to outperform the MRAC in task space tracking performance while
using similar amounts of torque.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The march of human progress has seen several large turning points in the last three
centuries. These are often dubbed ”revolutions” and an overview can be seen in figure
1.1. We are currently in the middle of such a turning point, the ”Fifth Industrial Revolution”,
which aims to reintroduce humans into the otherwise fully automated production line of
industry 4.0. The goal for this transition is to enable mass customization. The concept
of mass customization is to ensure the production line is sufficiently adaptable to enable
complete customization of the end product to the end­user’s specifications. This transition
from industry 3.0 and 4.0 to 5.0 is widespread in the electronics and integrated circuit
manufacturing industry. It is now possible to design completely custom electronics or
integrated circuits and have them manufactured and assembled from raw materials within
a single production facility [2]. However, in many areas of manufacturing, this transition to
industry 5.0 has not yet happened in part because, industry 4.0 has not been fully adopted.
In Denmark the transition to 4.0 in manufacturing has only happened in large companies
while many small and medium sized companies generally have not transitioned yet. The
main reason is that a large upfront cost is associated with the implementation of industry
4.0 [1]. One of the reasons this transition is expensive, is that the available standardized
robotic solutions are not sufficiently adaptable to support the framework of industry 4.0 and
5.0. Therefore, the companies generally have to invest in large amounts of customization
for their solutions, which is expensive.
The insufficient adaptability of the robotic solutions is caused by their requirement for re­
programming, whenever their environment changes even just a tiny bit. That is because
at present, the robotic solutions available are in general not aware of their wider surround­
ings. Even collaborative robots are only aware of their surroundings to the extent that they
notice when a sufficiently large external force is applied to them. The only solutions avail­
able that enables robots to partially adapt to changes in their environment involve having
a camera placed on the end­effector of a robot. Visual feedback from such cameras are
then used to conduct online adjustments to the path of the robots, relative to detected
target objects. The problem with this solution, is that it only enables minor adjustments to
the end­effector path and orientation when the end­effector is already close to the target
object. This is due to the cameras being used having a relatively narrow depth of field
and field of view. Therefore, robots are not generally able to adapt to large changes in
their environment.
This project aims to design a controller and framework that can adapt on the fly to drastic
changes in the environment. The framework is supposed to identify similar target objects,
approaching from different directions and adapt its moving pattern accordingly. Thus it
would be able to interact with a target object and adapt to changes in a similar fashion to
that of a human.
A typical production line scenario in manufacturing is that a robot arm is placed near
a conveyor belt. The conveyor belt is transporting an object the robot should interact
with. For example, that interaction could be grasping and moving it to a separate part of
the production line or it could be inserting a bolt or screw in the object on the conveyor
belt. In the scenario that the location of the robot arm, relative to the conveyor belt, is
changed, a conventional robot would not be able to adapt to this change. By placing a

1.2
Project Scope and Research Questions
In the industry, the low level controller used in almost all robot manipulators are Propor­
tional Integral Differentiating (PID) controllers [9]–[11]. However, these controllers do not
handle large changes in system dynamics well. Therefore, adaptive low level control re­
search has been done with robot manipulators such as the Franka Emika Panda. In this
project, a real implementation of two adaptive joint level controllers, the Active Inference
Controller (AIC) [12] and the Model Reference Adaptive Controller (MRAC)[13] will be
done. The references will be computed in task space (carthesian world coordinates) and
then transformed to joint space, such that they can be used for references by the joint
level controllers. The AIC implementation from [12] will be expanded upon, based on
[14], such that instead of using a constant position reference, both a time varying posi­
tion and velocity reference in joint space is used. The MRAC implementation from [12],
[13] is already capable of accepting a time­varying position and velocity reference in joint
space, though the MRAC implementation in [12] was tested only with a constant position
references.
As the controllers (AIC and MRAC) will be tested with a time­varying position and velocity
references in task space, a moving target is used. The target is a box on which an April­
Tag [15]–[17] is mounted. This AprilTag will be detected and localized by a BlueFOX3
camera. The relative pose between the base of the robot arm (Franke Emika Robot) and
the camera is known ahead of runtime. Thereby, through a basis transformation, the loca­
tion of the target objects with respect to the camera can be converted, such that they are
known with respect to the location of the robot arm instead. An inverse kinematics solver
then converts the task space pose into a joint space pose. The joint space pose is used
by a joint space path interpolator to make the step size between the joint position refer­
ences smaller. Lastly, each of these angular position references are used to generate a
corresponding angular velocity reference that can be used by the low level controllers.
Only the pose (position and orientation) of the target object is measured, but because
the target object is expected to move at a constant velocity, the velocity is estimated with
a constant velocity model using a Kalman filter as further described in sections 3.3 and
3.4. As will be explained in section 7.1 Future Work, a predictive trajectory optimizer can
be implemented to potentially increase the performance of the tracking of the robot arm.
To this end an asynchronous predictive Kalman filter has been implemented and tested
using the asynchronous predictive Kalman filter, the BlueFOX3 camera, the conveyor belt,
and an AprilTag. In this implementation, the 20th step ahead prediction of the target pose
and trajectory is used to compute the reference trajectory. As inverse dynamics were
not implemented, the joint space velocity reference was implemented using filtering of
the position reference and not generated from the predicted target velocity. Additionally,
the predicted velocity was intended to be a reference for benchmarking the controller’s

2.1.1
Conventional Controllers for Robot Arms
Using a PID for individual joint control in an inner control loop is the industry standard in
terms of controllers for robot arms [9]–[11]. The references to these PID’s is then typically
either given by an outer loop PID controller or an interpolator which interpolates between
the initial and target position and then schedules when the references is changed to the
joint­level PID controllers [18].
Despite PID based robot controllers being able to attain great performance in certain as­
pects, they do have a couple of drawbacks. The number of tuning parameters for a PID
controller for a robot arm is very high as there will be at least three for each joint if no cross
coupled PID controllers are used. Therefore, designing a robot controller based on PID
controllers can often be a very cumbersome task if the number of degrees of freedom for
the manipulator is high.
The PID controller is fundamentally also only designed to operate on single input single
output (SISO) systems as the PID computes a control action based on a scalar error
signal. Therefore, when multiple PID controllers are used in a multi­input multi output
(MIMO) system context, the controllers are essentially fighting against each other, which
further increases the challenge in tuning the controllers [19].
Problems with integrator windup are also prevalent seen as the PID controllers may in­
directly be actuating each other, thus adding a disturbance that must be rejected by the
other controllers [19], [20]. PID controllers are in general great at rejecting disturbances
but because of the high level of interconnectivity and tuning parameters, it can be very
difficult to tune the individual controllers such that their internal interaction yields desirable
dynamics.
However, the main problem with PID controllers for this project, is that they are only able
to perfectly reject constant disturbances [20]. The couplings between the joints of the
robot arm are nonlinear, as they are rotational and seen as the inertia of the robot arm
A Model­Free Predictive Control Framework for Trajectory Optimisation

can change drastically if it picks up a heavy object. Due to this nonlinearity of the system,
the PID controllers may be unable to reject such a disturbance.
A possible solution, for overcoming the problems of nonlinear disturbance rejection and
the PID controllers for the individual joints fighting against each other, is to use adaptive
controllers. Adaptive controllers, depending on their type, can recreate, and thereby,
reject nonlinear disturbances. Several types of adaptive controllers exist, but in general
there are two categories of adaptive controllers: model­free and model­based adaptive
controllers.
2.1.2
Model­Based and Model­Free Adaptive Controllers
The general concept of adaptive controllers, and the difference between model­based
and model­free adaptive controllers will now be introduced, after which the adaptive con­
trollers specifically relevant for this project will be introduced. As mentioned in chapter 1
Introduction, this report investigates the feasibility of using model­free adaptive control in
a predictive framework, with a traditional model­based adaptive controller as the bench­
mark. For this reason, once the adaptive controllers relevant for this project have been
introduced, the concept of predictive adaptive controllers will be introduced.
The basic concept in adaptive control is to use the measurements of the system, and
the actuations supplied to it, to generate an expected measurement signal. Several ap­
proaches to generating this expected measurement signal exist, both model­based and
model­free. The error between the measurements and the expected measurement sig­
nals are then used to update the feedback gains of a separate controller. The feedback
gains are typically either adapted such that the system dynamics approach those of some
model, or the gains are adapted to optimize an appropriate cost function.
The main problem with model­based adaptive controllers is that they inherently suffer from
disturbances generated by model mismatch. A common type of model­based adaptive
controller, the Model Reference Adaptive Controllers (MRAC) [21] suffers from this. The
MRAC as the name implies, is a type of model­based adaptive controller and is the one
which will be used in this project. The specific MRAC used in this project [13] seeks to
adapt the feedback gains of the controller, such that the system dynamics approach the
dynamics of a model designed offline. If the dynamics of the real system are far away from
those of the desired dynamics it may be difficult for the adaption to successfully suppress
the dynamics of the real system while enforcing the dynamics of the desired system, thus
potentially leading to undesirable behavior.
The problem with model­free adaptive controllers is that they in general don’t use much of
the potentially available knowledge of the system dynamics for state estimation and adap­
tion. They typically rather use a learning based approach [12], such as gradient descent
[22], [23], or quadratic optimization [24]. The type of model­free adaptive controller which
will be used in this project is referred to as an Active Inference Controller (AIC) [12], which
is a variant on the Speed Gradient Descent (SGD) controller [22], [23], [25] combined with
a state estimator, which estimates the states by solving a quadratic optimization problem.

is to design a system model with some desirable dynamics. The error between the real

2.1.5
Predictive Control
Predictive Control has been implemented in several forms both as General Predictive
Control (GPC) [24], [36], [37], different types of Model Predictive Control (MCP) [19], [37]
and Predictive Model Reference Adaptive Control (PMRAC) [38]. GPC is a type of model­
free predictive control, based around optimising a (typically quadratic) cost function, most
often formulated as a weighted cost on the state error, the actuation and their derivatives.
The GPC in its basic form is an unconstrained MPC using a predictive Kalman filter for
predicting the states and a receding horizon LQR for optimizing the actuation based on
the state estimates. In [36] the GPC is formulated in terms of ARMAX but can also be
formulated in terms of state space, as is done in [37].
The PMRAC described in [38] does not generate torque commands, unlike the AIC and
the MRAC originally used by [12] in their original comparison between the AIC and MRAC.
For this reason, the PMRAC will not be used in this project.
Model Predictive Control for planning and trajectory optimization for robot manipulators
has shown great promise [19], [39], though their main challenge in general is maintain­
ing a sufficiently low computation time while still optimising the trajectory and actuation
commands with constraints. One solution to the problem of high computation times is
Multiple Shooting MPC (MSMPC) because the optimization can first be parallelized, and
then distributed to a GPU for fast parallel computation. The solution of using MPC for
optimizing the trajectory is only proposed as a reasonable solution, under the assumption
the environment in which the robot manipulator exists, varies. As ideally the controller for
the robot manipulator should be able to adapt to arbitrary changes in its environment, a
Model Predictive Control approach could have many advantages, as the model over which
optimization is done could be updated with the detected changes in the environment.
As this project has its focus on the comparison between Model Reference Adaptive Con­
trol and Active Inference Control in a predictive framework and the purpose is to develop
a control algorithm capable of adapting to changes in the work environment of the robot

2.2.1
Visual Object Detection
Visual object detection is the act of detecting objects such as barcodes, boxes, or peo­
ple from visual data, typically images obtained from a camera. Several methods exist for
detecting objects through visual sensing. Various types of pattern recognition are very
common, such as bar­, QR­code or AprilTag detection. If tag detection, as it is generally
referred to, is not an option, the target object may alternatively have a very distinct shape
or appearance that remains relatively unchanged when being observed. In such cases,
template matching [40] can be a good option for detecting the object. Template matching
works by having a template image of the target object that is then moved over the image
as a kernel. The part of the image where the difference between the pixel values of the
template and the full image is smallest is the location in the image the object is determined
to be. The downside to this method is that unfortunately it is very sensitive to changes
in the appearance of the target object. Some typical changes are either changes in the
lighting, rotations of the target object or a slight altering of the target objects color or hue
such that the difference between the template and the target object is no longer small.
Such changes in the appearance of the target object generally make it difficult for it to
be detected though template matching. Thereby, if the lighting or the shade of the target
object changes, the target object may no longer be what is most similar to the template
in the image [41]. As such, false negatives and positives are much more likely to occur.
Another common approach, that is also based on pixel­wise comparisons, is color de­
tection [40], which looks for the area of an image least different from a designated color.
This approach suffers from the same problems as template matching, in that if the lighting
changes and the algorithm doesn’t do online color correction to compensate for the color
changes, for which reason incorrect detections are highly likely.
AprilTag, Bar­ and QR­code
In this project, AprilTag 2 [15]–[17] is used for visual detection of the target objects on the
conveyor belt. AprilTag, similarly to bar­ and QR­codes have been designed such that
they are easily detectable. Bar codes are designed to be detectable using various types
of scanners, such as single diode laser scanners, charge coupled device (CCD) readers or
camera­based readers. QR­codes, one of the 2D bar code standards, is typically detected
using vision and a vision detection algorithm. The problem with conventional bar codes,
1­ and 2D, is that they are difficult to detect at large distances and especially if they are
moving. This problem is something the AprilTag is specifically designed to alleviate. The
downside to AprilTags is they contain much less information than bar and QR­codes as
the specific AprilTags have an assigned ID and only contains that ID as its information.
The advantage is as mentioned that AprilTags in general can be detected at distances
beyond one meter, which in general is not the case for bar­ and QR­codes. Proposing
a solution to overcome this problem of the AprilTag containing very little information, the
AprilTag could be used merely for target detection. Then, when the robot arm attaches
itself to the target object with its end­effector, if the robot has a pick­and­place camera,
laser scanner or CCD, it may be able to use such a scanner to identify the target object
further, for example by using a conventional QR­code to store additional information.

Advanced Object Detection Methods
If more complex objects are to be detected or if the environment in which the detection is to

2.2.2
Visual Object Tracking
Visual object tracking is the act of recording the location and identity of all target objects in
an image and to then repeat this process in the following pictures, while also determining
if any target objects have reappeared in the image. Depending on the type of tracking,
the change in position over time can be stored as a velocity, which can then also be used
for calculating an acceleration. A common approach to tracking is to use a graph for
storing the detected target objects, and using a Kalman filter for estimating the next state
(typically position, velocity, and acceleration) of the individual target objects [41]. The ma­
jor challenges of object tracking in computer vision is occlusions, inability to differentiate
between target objects, and inability to estimate their state [41]. The reason for using a
state estimator when tracking the target object is that if the target becomes occluded, the
estimator will still update the belief about the target objects state. Thus, for instance, if
the target object is an object on a conveyor belt, the position and velocity of the object
can still be estimated if the object is occluded by a robot arm that is about to interact with
the object.
Even though there are standard solutions to tracking of objects, several methods exist
for visual object tracking [40], [41] both for single and multiple object tracking, as it is still
an open problem to be solved for hard tracking problems. Most approaches with tagged
objects, such as AprilTags, assume both the present location and identity of the given
object has been determined as described in section 2.2.1. As mentioned, this project
will use AprilTag (see 2.2.1) for detecting the target objects. Thus, neural network based
instance segmentation [40] is not needed as the target objects already are equipped with
identifying tags so they can be distinguished. Additionally, single target tracking will be
used in this project and multiple target tracking will not be investigated beyond this review.
2.2.3
Visual Trajectory Prediction
Prediction is the act of using previous data about a target to inform the future behavior
of a target. This can be done in several ways, but in the context of vision and trajectory
prediction, the typical approach is to first detect and identify the target objects in the image
and then use a predictive Kalman filter [24] to predict the future state of the target object
or objects. Typically the states predicted for the target objects are their positions, veloci­
ties and accelerations in either 1­, 2­ or 3D space depending on the configuration of the
vision sensor or sensors. This approach is very similar to the approach described initially
in section 2.2.2 which was to use a Kalman filter for state estimation of the target object.
But, instead of merely using the Kalman filter to estimate the present state to correct mea­
surements or handle occlusions, the estimator is used to compute the future states of the
target objects as well. A different approach to target trajectory prediction is to predict the
entire image using a predictive neural network [43], [44], after which an object detection
algorithm is run on the predicted images followed by a correlation algorithm to determine
which objects in the predicted images correspond to which objects detected in the origi­
nal image. However, as the computational requirements for running the network used by
[43], [44] in real time are unknown, a predictive Kalman filter is used for visual trajectory

3.2.2
Hyperstability (Passive Systems)
As thoroughly described in [52, App. C], hyperstability of a system is a generalization of
the stability concept passivity for a system. A system is passive, and thereby hyperstable,
if the stored energy in the system within the considered time horizon t = tend but after the
initial time t = 0, is less than or equal to the energy stored in the system at initialization
t = 0, plus the energy added to the system via its inputs within the considered time
0 < t < tend.
Fundamentally, passive, or hyperstable systems, are a class of systems only capable of
conserving energy or dissipating it, but not generating it.

4
Method
In this chapter the structure of the implementation as well as some of the more tech­
nical details will be elaborated upon. This project is implemented using a framework
called Robot Operating System (ROS). ROS is a framework for subdividing a project into
smaller programs called nodes. These nodes can communicate with each other via the
ROS framework. This is done using something referred to as publishers, subscribers and
topics. A topic is an address where data is stored. Nodes can store data in a topic by
publishing the data to the topic using a publisher. While other nodes can retrieve data
from a topic by subscribing to the topic using a subscriber.
Because of the way the different nodes are implemented, the data generated by the nodes
is not always generated in the same coordinate system. For this reason, as some of
the data generated in one coordinate system is needed in another coordinate system,
this data must be transformed to the correct coordinate system. This is also a feature
integrated in ROS referred to as transforms or tf’s. This project uses the second version
of the ROS transforms ”tf2”.

All data published from nodes in this project contains a ROS header [55]. The header
contains a sequence value seq of type uint32, a time stamp of the ROS defined type time
and string, naming the coordinate system to which the data in the publication references.
The important frames for this project are:
• camera
• panda_link0

4.6
Coordinate Transformation using /tf2_ros
The inverse kinematics solver described later in section 4.9 requires the target position
for the end effector is received in the base frame of the robot manipulator, that being
panda_link0. See figure 4.2 for a diagram of the coordinate frames used in this project.
Seeing as the tag detections generated using AprilTag are generated in the camera frame,
the detections must be transformed before they are supplied to the inverse kinematics
solver. Due to uncertainty in how the communication pipeline would be designed, it was
decided to compute the transformation as early in the communication pipeline as possi­
ble. The alternatives would be to compute the transformation after the predictive Kalman
filter or a path planner if such had been implemented. Computing the transformation after
the Kalman filter would result in an unnecessary computational load, as the entire pre­
diction vector would have to be transformed, as opposed to a single pose. At the time of
developing the inverse kinematics, it was unknown if a path planner would also require
the target locations be provided in the robot base frame. After implementation of the in­
verse kinematics, it was determined that a path planner was not required for testing the
prototype of the framework. It was speculated that a path planner may be needed for
developing the prototype, as it was unclear to which extend the inverse kinematics solver
made checks to find joint configuration solutions that avoid self­collisions. It was found
that the inverse kinematics solver used in this project did sufficiently avoid self­collisions,
provided the target position was within the robot’s reachable space. The reason for this
is that the inverse kinematics solver finds several joint configuration solutions and then
chooses the best one based on different conditions, such as if any of the solutions result
in joint violations or self­collisions [62].
The coordinate transformation was implemented in two steps. First a static broadcast
transformation was implemented in a roslaunch file, the procedure for which is explained
in [63]. Now that the static transform between the camera and panda_link0 frames has
been established, the transformation has to be computed. The transformation is com­
puted in the node /target_pose_tf_cam_to_panda which implements a class based on
48
A Model­Free Predictive Control Framework for Trajectory Optimisation
==PAGE:67==
this example [64] using the tf2_ros/transform_broadcaster package. This node sub­
scribes to the topic containing detected AprilTag poses published by /my_target_selector
in the camera frame. The transformation is then computed after which the pose, now
transformed to the panda_link0 frame is published to the predictive Kalman filter.
4.7
Asynchronous Predictive Kalman Filter
The implemented Kalman filter is an Asynchronous Predictive Kalman Filter. As described
before, the system measures position and estimates position and velocity in a 3D carthe­
sian coordinate space. The Kalman filter needs to be asynchronous, as the measure­
ments acquired by the camera and detection algorithm are not supplied at a steady rate.
The Kalman filter is predictive because it would enable a planner to plan an interception
route, rather than chase the target object if the planner was only given the present position
of the target object.
A path planner has not been implemented, but a prediction selector has. The working
principle of the prediction selector is described in section 4.8. The Kalman filter, run­
ning in the node kalman_predictor_3d, operates by accepting a measurement from the
node /target_pose_tf_cam_to_panda if it is available. If a measurement is available the
Kalman filter computes a measurement update, otherwise it only has a state update.
To achieve fast and readable code, the Kalman filter is written in C++, primarily using the
Eigen­package. This package enables writing of complex matrix operation in a much
more readable fashion than would be the case with the std library. This is because Eigen
objects can represent matrices and vectors that can be multiplied and manipulated with
a syntax similar to that of conventional scalar operations, while including functions simi­
lar to those included in numpy for Python for more complicated operations, but retaining
readability and computational speed.
The noise variance of the position measurement was acquired by continuously detecting
the position of the target object in 3D space for 15 minutes while the target object was static
with respect to the camera used for detection. The covariance matrix of the measurements
were then computed, the results of which can be seen in section 5.2.
As the predicted state of the target object is the position being passed onto the target
position and prediction selector, the target position and prediction selector will still receive
the predicted target positions of the target object, as the Kalman filter will simply move
the prediction horizon one time­step forward. This is the case even if the target object is
occluded such that no new measurements are attained, thus handling of target occlusions
has been implemented.
4.8
Target Position and Prediction Selector
The target position and prediction selector serves multiple purposes. Its most important
feature is that it defines the reaching zone of the robot. The reaching zone defines the area
where where the robot can reach. Thereby, if the target pose delivered by the predictive
Kalman filter is outside the reaching zone, the target position and prediction selector tells
the inverse kinematics solver to move to a predefined homing location rather than the
predicted pose from the Kalman filter.
The datasheet of the Franka Emika Panda[65] states that the robot arm has a maximum
reach of 855mm by the end effector’s outer most edge in the base plane of the panda_link0
frame. The panda_link0 frame is illustrated in figure 4.4.
A Model­Free Predictive Control Framework for Trajectory Optimisation
49
==PAGE:68==
For this project, through hand tuning, it was found that the 20th step ahead prediction
yielded the most accurate task space tracking results. For this reason, the target position
and prediction selector determines if the pose of the 20th step ahead prediction lies within
the reaching zone and passes this pose to the inverse kinematics solver if that is the case.
Otherwise, as stated before, it passes a safe homing location instead.
4.9
Inverse Kinematics and Dynamics Solution
The company Franka Emika does not supply an inverse model, neither kinematic nor
dynamic, of the robot manipulator Franka Emika Panda as part of their software packets
franka_ros or libfranka. For this reason an inverse kinematics solver must be obtained
elsewhere. An inverse dynamics solver was not implemented due to time constraints.
They do, however, supply a forward kinematic model from the libfranka API and a ROS
interface with the franka_ros package, which is useful for measuring the end effector
position of the robot.
Several inverse kinematics solvers are available, KDL, IKFast and TRAC­IK were con­
sidered. No specifications of the IKFast solver were found for the Franka Emika Panda,
however the TRAC­IK solver claims to have a much greater solve rate and lower average
solve time than KDL [66]. For this reason the TRAC­IK solver was implemented for this
project. Neither KDL nor TRAC­IK solver uses the available mesh of the robot to deter­
mine if a valid solution would result in self collision. They do, however, check if the joint
limits are violated for a given configuration solution. It was not determined whether IKFast
implements self collision, but the documentation does not advertise that it does [67].
The inverse kinematics solver, reference interpolator, and low level controller are all exe­
cuted as part of the /franka_control node.
4.9.1
TRAC­IK
TRAC­IK is an inverse kinematics solver build on top of KDL. KDL uses inverse Jacobian
and Newton’s method for solving the inverse kinematics. When solving inverse kine­
matics, TRAC­IK runs both a modified version of the KDL solver, as well as their own
implementation of a sequential quadratic programming nonlinear optimizer. The solver
uses a quazi­Newton method, which handles joint limits better than the inverse Jacobian
and Newton’s method approach [66]. Since joints limits are present in the Franka Emika
Panda, the approach used by TRAC­IK was expected to yield the best performance. A
comparative analysis of TRAC­IK and KDL was not made part of this project.
The inverse kinematics solver attempts to acquire a new target pose at each control loop
iteration. If a new measurement is obtained, the TRAC­IK computes a new reference
position in joint space, which will then be used by the reference interpolator.
4.10
Reference Interpolator
When a new joint reference position has been calculated using the inverse kinematics
solver, the path between the present and desired joint positions must be interpolated.
This is done to avoid giving the low level controller unreasonably large reference changes,
which would increase the likelihood of actuating the robot beyond its physical limitations.
Particularly, the risk of setting a torque command beyond the safety thresholds of the
robot increases causing a torque­violation­fault terminating the program. Additionally, the
greater torques induced by the controllers would also likely lead to greater overshoot,
which may move the robot past its joint position limits or faster than the velocity limits.
The reference interpolator uses a trapezoidal velocity profile [68] when interpolating the
position reference. When the velocity interpolator has computed the interpolated position

limits of the robot. This limited angular position reference is then passed through a low
pass filter also generating an angular velocity reference.

As previously described, this project uses two joint level controllers. Those being the

The controllers, like the Kalman filter, are implemented in C++ using the Eigen library to
obtain fast and readable code when executing the matrix computations used by the control

4.13

As described throughout this chapter, the communication between the ROS nodes is done
through the publishing and subscription to topics. This is also the method used for mon­

between the two low level controllers was as fair as possible, the MRAC was first tuned
such that it was able to follow the same paths as the AIC. That is to say, the tuning pa­

the target object was obscured manually at the correct distance from the robot arm. The
recorded paths were then used as references for the low level controllers using rosbag

5.4
Joint Space Measurements of Low Level Controllers
In this section a plots characterizing the low level controllers when working in conjunc­
tion with the inverse kinematics solver are displayed. Characterization of the low level
controllers is first done on the controllers individually, and then plots comparing the two
controllers are displayed. Several measurements have been performed. A description of
each of these measurements can be seen in the corresponding paragraphs. All measure­
ments performed of the low level controllers are conducted in conjunction with the inverse
kinematics. The reference provided to the inverse kinematics is the 20th step ahead pre­
diction computed by the Kalman filter. To ensure the test between the two controllers
while using the inverse kinematics was fair, the references generated by the Kalman filter
were stored using rosbag record. These recordings were then used as references to
the two low level controllers.
Three measurement session were conducted on the AIC and MRAC with the conveyor
belt having the potentiometer set to index 2, 3 and 4 respectively. It will be discussed in
section 6.3.2 why only the first three position references were used for testing the inverse
kinematics with the low level controllers.
Three measurements were performed with a moving target for each of the low level con­
trollers. Those being with the potentiometer on the conveyor belt set to 2, 3, and 4. The
horizontal axis for all the plots is the time axis. To make the plots more easily comparable,
the plots start at time t = 0 which for these plots is the moment when the predicted pose
of the target objects moves within the reaching zone thus changing the target reference
pose from the homing location to the predicted pose.

6
Discussion
In this chapter the results from the measurements and the consequences of some of
the implementation choices will be discussed. First the large conveyor belt characteriza­
tion measurements will be discussed, after which the unexpected cross correlation of the
AprilTag measurements will be discussed. Then, a brief argument for the Kalman filter
working correctly will be made, followed by an explanation of why the low level controllers
were only tested once on the first three and slowest tracking challenges and not tested on
the latter 6 and faster tracking challenges. Finally, the performance and measurements
regarding the low level controllers and their integration with the inverse kinematics solver
is discussed.
6.1
Conveyor Belt
As can be seen the measurements of the velocity of the conveyor belt in table 5.2, the
variance is practically non­existent for measurements at dial­increments 3­High. How­
ever, at dial increment 2, the measurements have a large variance. As the velocity was
measured manually by timing the laps of the conveyor belt with a stop watch, and con­
verting the lap time to m/s using the conveyor belt dimensions, the most likely source of
variance for measurements 3­High is inconsistency in timing the lap time correctly. This,
however, doesn’t fully explain the much higher variance of the conveyor belt at increment
2. When measuring the lap time, over the course of the measurement period, there was
a slow decrease in the lap time of the conveyor belt, see figure D.1. This may be due to
the conveyor belt heating up, thus expanding and reducing the mechanical resistance put
on the motor. Alternatively, if the motor is controlled using an Electronic Speed Controller
(ESC), the potentiometer may be setting a reference to a controller. If that is the case, the
large variance may be due to the controller having a long settling time. The most likely
reason this settling time is not present for the other eight measurements, is that the mea­
surements were conducted consecutively with a few minutes in between without stopping
the conveyor belt, thus the controller is likely to have settled at its new reference before
the next series of measurements were conducted.
6.2
Noise Measurement of Static AprilTag Detection
As the measurement in equation 5.1 shows, there seems to be a high degree of positive
correlation in the measurement of the pose of the AprilTag between the three axes. Due
to the nature of the experiment, the correlation between the axes was expected to be
close to zero. However, as it can be seen, that is not the case. In an attempt to falsify
these measurements, additional measurements were performed, and separate research
investigating the measurement noise of the AprilTag 2 algorithm was sought out, but none
was found suggesting this is a topic for further research.
6.3
Asynchronous Predictive Kalman Filter
6.3.1
Convergence of Covariance
As can be seen in the figures in section D.3, the covariance decreases rapidly when­
ever the Kalman filter updates and a measurement is available. This indicates that the
Kalman filter works as intended. In the experiments performed in this project the target
object moves at constant velocity. As can also be seen in the figures in sectionD.3, the
86
A Model­Free Predictive Control Framework for Trajectory Optimisation
==PAGE:105==
covariance continues to decrease whenever a measurement occurs, even when the tar­
get object is moving. This indicates that the Kalman filter can estimate the position of the
target position successfully. The estimation of the non­zero velocity of the target object
can, of course, only occur if a measurement of the target object occurred when it was
moving.
6.3.2
Inverse Kinematics Solver’s Inability to Track High Velocity
References
As is clear from the results in section 5.3.1, nine trajectories were generated using the pre­
dictive Kalman filter, while only three measurements on the robot arm were conducted, as
is displayed in sections 5.4 and 5.5. The root cause of this is due to motion blur caused
by a long exposure time in the camera due to insufficient lighting. The generated refer­
ences can be separated into two groups. Group 1, which contains measurements for the
potentiometer at positions 2, 3 and 4. And group 2, which contains measurements for
the potentiometer at positions 5 to High. Group 1 are the measurements that were suc­
cessfully used as reference trajectories, as can be seen in figures 5.1, 5.2, and 5.3. The
main difference between measurement group 1 and 2 is that group two did not manage to
acquire a sufficient number of measurements of the position (plotted as dashed
line)
of the target object while it was moving. While there is a lack of measurements while the
target is moving in the measurements in group 2, the state predictions after the position
measurements end still appear to increase or decrease in value similarly to the measure­
ments in group 1. Be aware this is only due to the y­axes having a smaller span in the
latter six measurements, as seen in figures 5.4, 5.5, 5.6, 5.7, 5.8, and 5.9. Thus, the main
reason for why the inverse kinematics solver, path interpolator, and low level controllers
were unable to track the target object, was not because the inverse kinematics solver
was unable to generate a solution for the position of the target objects. Rather the reason
was that the detection algorithm was unable to generate sufficient measurements which
could be used by the Kalman filter for generating an appropriate trajectory in task space.
AprilTag 2 is quite capable of detecting AprilTags in the image, as long as the pixels of
the AprilTag are sufficiently sharp for the algorithm to detect.
To determine if the motion blur prevented the detection of the AprilTags when the conveyor

The Target position and prediction selector receives its input from the predictive Kalman
filter. Because of this, if the target object becomes occluded, which typically happens
when the robot arm reaches for the target object when it gets within the reaching zone,
the robot arm will still track the target object via the state predictions from the Kalman
filter. Thus handling of occlusions for simple tracking challenges has been implemented.

6.4
Performance Evaluation between the AIC and the MRAC
In this section, the results from the measurements of the low level controllers are dis­
cussed, as well as some of the choices made regarding their implementation and the
consequences thereof.
6.4.1
Filtering of Saturated Reference for the Low Level Controllers
Filtering of the reference position and velocity to the MRAC was implemented to alleviate
the problems with the controller activating the robot’s safety shut down features on startup.
The main cause of this problem was found to be the inverse kinematics solver.
The inverse kinematics solver, when repeatedly asked to compute a joint space config­
uration for the same task space position, occasionally returns an entirely different joint
configuration, resulting in the same end effector position. It is strongly suspected that this
occurs because TRAC­IK computes an inverse kinematics solution using two separate
methods in parallel where, by default the solution calculated first is used, which is how it
was implemented in this project. This situation occurs when the target object is outside the
reaching zone, thus causing the target selector to repeatedly send the task space homing
location to the inverse kinematics solver. This large change in target joint configuration
results in a large spike in the joint space position and velocity references. To alleviate this
problem a 2nd order low pass filter was implemented to filter out these spikes in position
references. This filter managed to reject the large spikes in the joint reference, while not
damping the reference such that it impeded its tracking performance to an extend that
was noticeable in the results.
Filtering of the position and velocity reference for the AIC was not implemented, as it
resulted in a violation of the ”maximum power draw threshold” when the controller was
initialized with the filter. The effect of not implementing a low pass filter on the position
and velocity reference in the AIC, unlike in the MRAC, will be discussed later in section
6.4. Under normal circumstances the implementation of a low pass filter on the reference
of a controller should not be able to cause instability, as the low pass filter is not part of
the low level control loop, but merely a step between the reference generated by the path
interpolator and the low level controllers. It is therefore deemed that the most likely cause
for the low pass filter causing instability is an error in the implementation.
An alternative solution to this problem would be to compute a check before a task space
configuration is sent to the inverse kinematics solver, to determine if the new task space
configuration is the same or within the desired tolerances of the previous configuration.
In that case, a new joint space configuration should not be computed, and the inverse
kinematics solver should simply wait until a new task space reference becomes available.

respect to the base frame [69], for which reason only an analysis of position tracking in
task space has been done. As can be seen in table 5.6 the AIC has better tracking in all
three tracking challenges for the y and z axis, but only outperforms the MRAC along the
x­axis in the slowest of the tests, that being at potentiometer index position 2. As can be

6.4.4
Torque Utilization
From inspecting table 5.7 containing the RMS values for the commanded torque by the
two controllers, it can be seen they are quite even when comparing each joint at each
tracking challenge individually between the controllers. This implies that the controllers
are tuned relatively evenly in terms of aggressiveness.
There are a few notable exceptions though. Particularly joint 3 for potentiometer index
position 3 and 4, as well as joints 5 and 6 for potentiometer index position 2, 3, and to
a lesser extend, 4. The most likely cause for the significantly higher RMS value of joint
3 for the MRAC is that when joints 1 and 3 were in gimbal lock, the inverse kinematics
solver would jitter rather severely in a fashion similar to the problem described in 6.3.2.
This jittering is most likely due to the solver being unable to optimize smoothly which of
the joints should be displaced most from its center position.
The MRAC using more torque in joints 5 and 6, at least in terms of RMS, is most likely due
to the slightly more oscillatory behavior of the MRAC compared to the AIC. The decreased
difference in the RMS for the test with potentiometer index 4 is most likely due to the AIC
also being increasingly oscillatory at higher potentiometer values, thus the difference in
the RMS of their torque utilization decreases. Plots showing the torques commanded by
the two controllers can be seen in figures D.40, D.41, and D.42.

7
Conclusion
In this project two types of adaptive controllers, one being model­based and the other
model­free, were implemented for a Franka Emika Panda robot manipulator, in order to
compare their relative performance. The chosen controllers were an Active Inference
Controller (AIC) and a Model Reference Adaptive Controller (MRAC). Instead of the ref­
erences passed to the controllers being constant position references in joint space, as
they were in [12], they are instead time­varying position and velocity references in joint
space. These joint space references are generated by a predictive Kalman filter in task
space, after which they are converted to joint space position references by an inverse
kinematics solver (TRAC­IK). Because the reference for TRAC­IK was generated by a
predictive Kalman filter, the robot end effector was able to track the target, even when the
end effector occluded the target object from the camera.
Under the conditions where the AIC and MRAC were tested, the controllers were found
to have a similar degree of actuator utilization, while the AIC managed to generally out­
perform the MRAC in terms of position reference tracking in task space.
The inverse kinematics solver was found to jitter when it was repeatedly asked to find joint
space solutions to the same task space pose. This jitter causes the MRAC to violate the
power draw threshold or torque command threshold of the robot. To resolve this issue
a low pass filter was implemented to filter these spikes. The cause of the jittering is hy­
pothesized to be that the TRAC­IK solver finds a joint space solution using two separate
methods and uses which ever solution is computed fastest, as explained in its documen­
tation. Most of the time the same solver is fastest, but occasionally the other solver returns
its solution for the task space pose faster, giving a solution far from the current position in
joint space.
Due to the reference passed to the MRAC being low pass filtered, unlike the AIC, enabling
the MRAC to operate without engaging the robots fail­safe systems, it was not possible
to compare the controllers’ joint space position or velocity reference tracking directly.
This research indicates that the AIC has increased position tracking performance in task
space compared to the MRAC, while having similar torque utilization.

– Implement the joint space reference position and velocity low pass filter as a 2nd
order low pass filter in state space, instead of as transfer functions for both the
AIC and MRAC to solve the issue of non­smooth joint space velocity reference
generation.
• Investigate the cause of correlation in the measurement noise in the AprilTag 2 de­

Startup procedure for ROS with Panda
1. Ensure everything is correctly connected
2. Engage Emergiency Stop Button (red button on yellow and black box)
3. Engage E­Stop Button (red button on yellow and black box)
4. Turn off Controller Box
5. Wait 10 seconds
6. Turn on computer
7. Turn on controller box
8. Disengage Emergency Stop Button
9. Open browser and go to website: https://172.16.0.3/desk/
• The light of the robot should now be white. This means that the robot is in ”Free
Moves” mode. You can press the two buttons on the side of the end effector
(the two that are right across from each other) to move the robot arm freely.
• If the lights on the robot turn pink it means the robot has received conflicting
commands. Typically this happens when you try to push the robot while it is
not in ”Free Moves” mode.
• I don’t know yet if ”conflicting commands” causing state pink can also be caused
if the robot arm is blocked by something during a programmed trajectory?
10. Disengage E­Stop Button (Rotate it clockwise and let go)
• The lights on the robot arm should now be blue. The robot can now accept
external commands. For example from ROS nodes.

Pezzarto (Those being the AIC and MRAC controllers) and one from libfranka.
