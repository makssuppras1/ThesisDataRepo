Abstract
5G technology has introduced significant advancements in Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs),
particularly in real-time video communication. This thesis investigates the latency performance
of 5G networks for drone video communications, focusing on key performance indicators (KPIs)
such as round-trip time (RTT), throughput, and video latency. The study involved a series of
experimental flights conducted in March and June 2024, utilizing tools like iPerf3, Ping, and
OpenCV to measure network performance.
The experiments revealed that while 5G networks offer substantial improvements in throughput
and low latency, environmental factors, such as physical interference and network congestion,
still pose challenges. The March test flight indicated significant RTT fluctuations coinciding with
video feed events, with minimum values of 90ms and peaks of over two seconds. In June, tests
at 50m and 120m altitudes showed increased latency and variability with altitude, highlighting
the impact of physical and network factors on performance.
Video latency analysis showed that maintaining low latency is critical for real-time applications.
The video latency can vary from minimum values of 20ms to 70ms at 50m altitude and increases
significantly at higher altitudes, reaching minimums of 40ms at 120 meters. The findings align
with existing literature, confirming that 5G networks can support the high data rates required
for video transmission but are affected by altitude and network infrastructure.
Future work should optimize network infrastructure, improve congestion management, and ex-
plore advanced technologies like network slicing or bean forming to enhance 5G performance
in UAV applications.
Addressing these challenges is crucial for deploying effective real-time
video-based control systems and applications like surveillance.

Introduction
The advent of 5G technology ushered in a new era of possibilities for Unmanned Aerial Vehicles
(UAVs), particularly in the realm of real-time video communication. This thesis explores the
latency performance and key performance indicators (KPIs) of video communication for drones
utilizing 5G networks.
A comprehensive understanding of these parameters is critical for advancing drone applications
in surveillance, disaster management, and remote sensing. The focus has been on evaluating
latency, experimental methodologies, and the implications of factors such as VPN usage, flight
path heights, and video quality on communication performance.
Previous studies highlighted the potential of 5G networks to reduce communication latency for
drones drastically. For instance, Zhang et al. (2020) demonstrated that 5G significantly lowered
latency in real- time video transmission compared to earlier network technologies, making it
a robust solution for UAV applications [Zhang2020]. Similarly, Doe et al. (2020) conducted
performance analyses revealing that 5G networks provided the necessary low latency and high
reliability required for effective drone communication [Doe2020]. In terms of KPIs, Gupta et
al. (2020) identified critical metrics such as latency, throughput, and reliability as essential for
assessing the performance of 5G-enabled drone communication systems. Their findings under-
scored that 5G met the stringent requirements for high-quality video transmission [Gupta2020].
Further experimental studies, such as the one conducted by Johnson et al. (2020), provided em-
pirical data on latency improvements, reinforcing the advantages of 5G in maintaining high
video quality with minimal delays [Johnson2020]. A series of Test flights conducted in March
2024 and June 2024 in collaboration with Sky Watch A/S has been a practical case study within
this thesis. The first experiment utilized a 5G network to evaluate video streaming performance
from a fixed-wing UAV. Two different computer setups have been employed for flight control
and measurements, connected via a Tailscale VPN. Ping and IPerf3 tools are used to measure
the Round-Trip Time (RTT) and throughput, respectively, providing insights into the latency
and network performance during the flight. During the test flight, it was observed that the
start/stop of the video may have had a significant impact on RTT, suggesting that video trans-
mission could induce network congestion and affect latency.
The analysis showed different variations in the latency performance with minimum values of
90ms( in some of the tests) and maximum values over 200 milliseconds for some specific cases.It’s
worth mentioning that the VPN and 5G hotspot setup contributed to these high RTT values
for 5G standards.
Building upon the insights gained from the Sky Watch’s first test flight, this thesis also encom-
passed a test flight conducted in June 2024. This test involved a series of flights conducted at
varying altitudes, including the lowest altitude of 50m and the highest permissible under regula-
tory standards of 120m. The evaluation process mirrored previous methods, utilizing Iperf and
Ping commands to assess latency. Additionally, packet loss was measured as part of this test to
provide a comprehensive network performance analysis. The video was streamed and recorded
in this test to evaluate quality and latency.
OpenCV facilitated the video streaming, with timestamps added to both the transmitted and
received videos to measure fluctuations over time. Measurements were conducted for five min-
utes, with data logging for further analysis. The test also compared the latency introduced by
running all tests in parallel vs. running them separately for 5 minutes.
These metrics were crucial for understanding the quality and performance of the 5G connection
used by the drone
The primary goal of this thesis is to thoroughly evaluate the latency and packet loss in 5G-
enabled drone video communications. By comparing the performance metrics across different
altitudes and connection setups, this research aimed to identify the optimal conditions for min-

imizing latency and packet loss while maintaining high video quality. The expected outcomes
include:
• Detailed performance profiles of 5G networks in drone video communication applications.
• Insights into the impact of VPN usage on latency and packet loss and on how reliable
off-the-shelf tools are for this specific case.
• An understanding of how altitude affects communication performance.
This research is significant, given it addresses the critical need for low-latency, high-quality

and remote sensing, where reliable real-time communication is essential. Moreover, the insights

tion of 5G networks in drone video communication, paving the way for enhanced performance
and reliability in UAV operations involving real-time 5G-controlled drones.

Literature Review
This literature review aims to provide an overview of recent research in these areas, integrating
findings from multiple studies to offer a comprehensive understanding.
In 2023, a comprehensive evaluation was performed on the improvements of 5G over 4G in low-
and mid-bands. This study analyzed throughput and latency improvements of 5G using iPerf
tools in real-world scenarios. The findings indicated that while 5G offers substantial improve-
ments in throughput, latency measurements using iPerf revealed that achieving consistent low
latency remains challenging due to various environmental factors and hardware limitations [1].
The integration of 5G technology with drone communication has opened new possibilities for
applications requiring reliable and low-latency connections. The study ”First Experiments with
a 5G-Connected Drone” investigated the performance of 5G-connected drones in different flight
scenarios. The authors reported that while 5G connectivity provided high downlink throughput,
frequent handovers to 4G were a significant issue, affecting the overall latency and reliability of
the connection [4].
Another study titled ”End-to-End Performance Measurements of Drone Communications in 5G
Cellular Networks” presented results of a measurement campaign for drone communications in
an experimental 5G network. The measurement scenarios covered the capacity and quality of
the link between the drone and the ground station, the robustness of drone communications
with network slicing, and the stability of the link for long-distance flights. The results indicated
that 5G networks could support the reliability and latency requirements for video and command
and control data for long-distance flights, even when the network was optimized for terrestrial
devices [3].
The paper An Overview of Drone Communication, Applications, and Challenges highlights the
critical aspect of latency in drone communication, which is essential for real-time applications.
5G networks offer significantly lower latency compared to previous generations, enabling drones
to perform tasks like remote control, search and rescue, and real-time video streaming effectively.
The latency in 5G networks is crucial for maintaining the quality of service in applications that
require immediate feedback and interaction. For instance, the latency should not exceed 50-
100ms for real-time visual and audio data transfer, and for surveillance tasks, it should be even
lower, around 3ms[2]

Video latency is a critical factor for applications such as aerial surveillance and real-time mon-
itoring. Studies have shown that while 5G networks can support the high data rates required
for video transmission, the latency can vary significantly depending on the drone’s altitude
and the network’s infrastructure. This was highlighted in the paper ”End-to-End Performance
Measurements of Drone Communications in 5G Cellular Networks” [3].
Several other studies contribute to the understanding of 5G performance in various contexts.
For example, the study ”Latency Analysis and Field Trial for 5G NR” examined the latency
requirements for different 5G applications, emphasizing the need for ultra-reliable low latency
communication (URLLC) in mission-critical scenarios [4]. Another relevant study is ”Evalu-
ating 5G Uplink Performance in Low Latency Video Streaming,” which focused on the uplink
performance of 5G networks, crucial for applications where drones need to send high-quality
video back to the control center [5].
The integration of 5G technology with tools like iPerf and ping, as well as its application in
drone communication and video transmission, has demonstrated significant potential for vari-
ous applications. However, challenges such as maintaining low latency and handling frequent
handovers remain.
The literature reviewed here showed a research gap when analyzing video latency in 5G drones
at different height paths and in a fully deployed 5G network.

3
Methodology
This chapter delves into evaluating 5G network performance in drone communications, specifi-
cally focusing on video latency during the Sky Watch Test Flight conducted in March and June
2024. The primary objective of this study is to analyze the latency and throughput characteris-
tics of 5G networks in the context of real-time video streaming from drones, utilizing tools such
as Ping and IPerf3. The findings of these experiments will be analyzed and compared with the
current bibliography in the discussion section.
3.1
Brief Experiment Description
The experimental setup involved a dual-computer configuration in the first flight test performed
in March, where one computer handled flight control and another was tasked with measurement
and acting as an IPerf3 server, and a single computer configuration for the second experimental
test performed in June. In both experimental flights, the drone, a fixed wing UAV equipped
with a live-streaming camera, transmitted video to the base computer. To ensure both the drone
and the ground computer operated under the same IP address, Tailscale VPN was employed,
connecting the setup through a 5G wireless cellular hotspot. Iperf3, Ping, and video data were
recorded with this setup for further analysis. It is worth mentioning that both experimental
flights took place in a suburban area a few kilometers outside of Støvring in the Nordjylland
Region, Denmark. The weather conditions were optimal, and the 5G antennas had a free line of
sight to the drone’s flight path. No visible source of interference was detected in the surrounding
area. Further details of the experimental settings of both dates will be provided in the experiment
subsection.
3.2
Software Description
In this subsection, we will briefly review the different software tools utilized for the experiments
and their posterior analysis.
3.2.1
Iperf3
Iperf3 is the updated version of IPerf and measures the maximum TCP and UDP bandwidth
performance. It is a powerful and versatile tool for network performance testing, offering a range
of features suitable for many scenarios. Its ease of use, flexibility, and comprehensive metrics
are significant advantages. Iperf3 operates on a client-server model. This means that a server
listens for incoming connections from the client. It processes the data and provides performance
metrics. Conversely, the Client initiates the connection with the server and sends data to it.
The client specifies parameters for the test, such as protocol, duration, and bandwidth.
• Metrics
– TCP Metrics
∗Throughput: the amount of data transferred over a given time period, usually
measured in bits per second.
∗Transfer: the total amount of data transferred during the test.
∗Re-transmissions: number of packages re-transmitted.
∗Cwnd Congestion Window: related to TCP congestion control mechanism
– UDP Metrics
∗Throughput: similar to TCP but typically higher since UDP does not require for
acknowledgment of received package.
9
==PAGE:12==
∗Jitter: Variation in package arrival times
∗Package loss: percentage of packages lost during transmission.
• Output
– Interval: The time interval for which the statistics are reported.
– Transfer: The total amount of data transferred during the interval.
– Bandwidth: The average transmission rate during the interval.
– Retr: Number of re-transmitted packets (for TCP).
– Jitter: Variation in packet arrival times (for UDP).
– Lost/Total Datagrams: Number and percentage of lost packets (for UDP).
• Congestion Windows (Cwnd):
Is worth mentioning how Cwnd parameter works for further understanding of the graphs
in this chapter. This TCP variable limits the amount of data the sender can transmit into
the network before receiving an acknowledgment (ACK) from the receiver, changing its
size dynamically. The test begins with a small Cwnd size and increases it exponentially
with each received ACK. This helps discover the network’s available bandwidth. Once a
threshold is reached, cwnd switches to linear growth; this helps to avoid congestion by
carefully probing the network capacity.
Once a package loss is detected ( interpreted as a sign of congestion), TCP reduces cwnd
significantly to reduce the load on the network, typically cutting it in half and adjusting
the low start threshold. After detecting congestion, TCP enters a recovery phase where it
slowly increases cwnd again to probe the available bandwidth.
In summary, the Cwnd parameter will increase as long as the connection is stable and there
are no package losses. It will drop and slowly increase once the network gets congested
and a package is lost.
For the experiments and test in this thesis, we will run Iperf in TCP mode, recording Transfer,
Bandwidth, and Congestion Window values.
3.2.2
Advantages and Disadvantages of Iperf3
Iperf3 is a powerful and versatile tool for network performance testing widely used by engineers
and IT professionals, offering a range of features that make it suitable for many scenarios.
Its advantages are its ease of use, flexibility, and comprehensive metrics. However, its need
for careful synchronization between client and server, the potential resource intensity, and the
synthetic nature of its tests can be perceived as disadvantages of this tool.
3.2.3
Ping
Ping is a fundamental network diagnostic tool that measures the round-trip time (RTT) for
messages sent from the originating host to a destination computer. Ping is highly effective for
basic network troubleshooting and connectivity checks, providing a simple yet powerful means
to gauge the latency and reliability of network connections. Its ease of use and the immediate
feedback it provides are significant advantages.
Ping operates by sending Internet Control
Message Protocol (ICMP) Echo Request messages to the target host and waiting for ICMP
Echo Reply messages. The tool measures the time it takes for the Echo Request to reach the
destination and for the Echo Reply to return, thereby calculating the round-trip time.
• Metrics
10
==PAGE:13==
– RTT (Round-Trip Time): The time it takes for a packet to travel from the source to
the destination and back again. This is typically measured in milliseconds (ms).
– Packet Loss: The percentage of packets that are sent but do not receive a correspond-
ing Echo Reply. This metric helps identify network congestion, faulty hardware, or
configuration problems.
– Jitter: The variation in the time for packets to travel from the source to the destina-
tion. Although not typically provided by basic Ping, advanced implementations can
calculate jitter.
– TTL (Time to Live): The maximum number of hops a packet is allowed to travel
before being discarded. TTL is decremented by each router through which the packet
passes.
• Output
– Bytes: The size of the ICMP Echo Request packets.
– Time: The RTT for each Echo Request and Echo Reply pair.
– TTL: The remaining TTL value when the Echo Reply is received, indicating the
number of hops.
– Statistics: Summary of packets sent, received, and lost, including the minimum,
maximum, and average RTT.
• Latency with Ping:
As measured by Ping, latency is a crucial parameter for assessing network performance
and stability. Ping results provide insights into the Round-Trip Time (RTT), the time
it takes for a packet to travel from the source to the destination and back again. Un-
der normal operation, RTT values should be relatively stable, indicating a responsive and
efficient network. However, variations in RTT can reveal important information about
network performance. A sudden increase in RTT may indicate congestion, routing issues,
or other network problems. Consistent high RTT values suggest persistent latency issues
that can affect the performance of real-time applications such as video conferencing and
online gaming. By regularly monitoring RTT with Ping, network administrators can di-
agnose and address latency-related issues, ensuring a smoother and more reliable network
experience. This makes Ping an invaluable tool for understanding and managing network
latency.
For the experiments and tests in this thesis, we will use Ping to measure RTT, packet loss,
and TTL values. These metrics will help us evaluate the network’s performance and have
a better understanding of the latency in the network.
3.2.4
Advantages and Disadvantages of Ping
Ping is a widely used tool for network performance testing, and it is known for its simplicity
and effectiveness. Its advantages include ease of use, immediate feedback on network connec-
tivity, and the ability to quickly diagnose common network issues such as latency and packet
loss. However, Ping also has its limitations. It relies on ICMP, which some networks may block
or deprioritize, leading to inaccurate results. Additionally, Ping provides limited information
compared to more advanced diagnostic tools, and its simplicity means it cannot perform com-
prehensive network tests. Despite these limitations, Ping remains an invaluable tool for initial
network diagnostics and quick checks on network health.

3.2.5
OpenCV
OpenCV (Open Source Computer Vision Library) is an extensive open-source library that sup-
ports various computer vision and machine learning algorithms. OpenCV is highly versatile and
powerful, offering a range of features that make it suitable for numerous applications, including
image processing, object detection, and real-time video analysis. Its ease of use, comprehen-
sive functionality, and performance efficiency are significant advantages. OpenCV operates by
providing a rich collection of functions and modules that developers can utilize to implement
complex vision algorithms in a simplified manner. The library supports multiple programming
languages, such as C++, Python, Java, and MATLAB, making it accessible to a wide range of
users.
• Modules and Functions
– Core Functionality
∗Basic Data Structures: Provides fundamental structures like Mat (matrix) for
handling images and matrices.
∗Image Processing: Includes functions for filtering, transformation, and analysis
of images.
– Image Processing
∗Filtering: Functions for blurring, sharpening, and edge detection.
∗Geometric Transformations: Tools for scaling, rotating, and warping images.
∗Color Space Conversions: Methods to convert images between different color
spaces (e.g., BGR to Grayscale).
– Video Analysis
∗Motion Detection: Algorithms for detecting and tracking moving objects in video.
∗Background Subtraction: Techniques to separate foreground objects from the
background.
– Object Detection
∗Haar Cascades: Pre-trained models for detecting faces, eyes, and other objects.
∗Deep Learning: Integration with deep learning frameworks for advanced object
detection and recognition.

4
Analysis and Discussion
This section analyses the results of the latency evaluation of 5G video drone communication
experiments conducted in March and June 2024.
The findings are compared with existing
literature to comprehensively understand the 5G network’s performance in UAV applications.
4.1
Analysis of the results
March Test Flight:
During the March test flight, Ping was used to measure the Round-Trip Time (RTT), while
iPerf3 was utilized to evaluate throughput. The results indicated significant RTT fluctuations,
especially coinciding with the start/stop of the video feed. The histogram showed that most
RTT values were below 1000 ms, with 80% of the values below 190 ms and minimum values of
90 ms. The congestion window(Cwnd) showed a congested network with a marked pick when
the video stoped and steep drop when it re-started this being also observed in the RTT graph,
which allows us to better comprehend the implication of video in the bandwidth performance.
The analysis also revealed that network congestion affected RTT, as shown by the correlation
between RTT spikes and video transmission events (see Figures 3 and 4) [1].
June Test Flight:
In June, the tests were conducted at two altitudes, 50m, and 120m, sequentially and in parallel.
At 50m, in the sequential test, the RTT values were clustered around 25-70 ms, with spikes up
to 200 ms. The congestion window (Cwnd) analysis showed significant fluctuations, indicating
periodic network congestion. At 120m, the RTT values were higher on average, with a mini-
mum of 40ms and spikes over 160ms.A greater scattering and fluctuation on the values and a
more reactive congestion window suggest altitude-related network performance degradation (see
Figures 18 and 19) [4]. For the parallel analysis, a greater ill performance of the network can be
noticed with a significant impact of video communication on the performance metrics and vice
versa. Higher congestion in the network was perceived in the cwnd analysis for the four flights
conducted in parallel analysis, with even worse performance (as expected) in tests conducted
with 5G hotspots. Latency values for the 50-meter flight in this modality went from 40ms up to
spikes of 700ms, with 75% of the concentration under 187 ms. This shows a significant increase
in comparison with the values obtained in the sequential flight and pushes us to the conclusion
51
==PAGE:54==
that Ping and Iperf ran separately, can provide a biased understanding of the network, and
that there is room for a comparative experiment focus on the performance of this tool. The
120m flights, on the other hand, showed a similar scattering pattern in the RTT vs time graph
but with relatively higher values. The sequential started off with a 40ms minimum and a 75
percentile concentration under 67ms, and the parallel started off with a 39.9 ms minimum and a
75 percentile concentration over 100ms, with a more significant spike of 350ms. On a final note
of the analysis, we can observe severe ill performance in the 5G parallel test. The main reason
of this 5G hotspot to be taken into consideration was the necessity to replicate the conditions
of the first flight in March. The results of these tests were erratic, with severe package loss,
which can be observed in the Cwnd performance and which led to the transfer and bandwidth
dropping to 0 in many observations. The Rtt test showed values of over 200ms, reaching up
to a second in the spikes. The reasons for this poor performance in the test can vary from the
configuration of the hotspot in the cellphone to the battery considerations and the modulation
of the cellphone to provide a WiFi connection to the base computer. The suggestion for these
values is for them to be considered as a particular case and not the generality of the drone com-
munications with 5G and probably a base for a future study in the implicates of 5G hotspots in
the drone communication and its applications scenarios
These results align with the observations made by Muzaffar et al. in ”First Experiments with a
5G-Connected Drone,” where altitude and network infrastructure significantly impacted latency
and reliability of drone communications [4].
4.2
Video Latency Evaluation
The video latency was assessed using timestamp differences between transmitted and received
videos. The analysis indicated that at 50m, the average latency was around 200 ms, which
increased significantly with altitude. This was corroborated by the dissimilarity score analysis,
showing higher dissimilarity at greater altitudes (see Figures 15 and 16) [5].
These findings are consistent with the study ”Latency Analysis and Field Trial for 5G NR,”
which emphasized the challenges of maintaining low latency and high video quality in real-time
applications, particularly at varying altitudes and distances [3].
4.3
Comparative Analysis with bibliography
Comparing the sequential and parallel tests, it was evident that running iPerf, Ping, and video
transmission simultaneously resulted in higher RTT and more pronounced network congestion.
This suggests that simultaneous operations strain the network more than sequential opera-
tions, highlighting the need for robust network management strategies to handle concurrent
data streams efficiently (see Figures 12 and 21) [4].
The results indicate that while 5G networks substantially improve latency and throughput for
UAV applications, challenges such as network congestion, environmental factors, and altitude-
related performance degradation remain. Future research should focus on optimizing network
infrastructure, improving congestion management, and exploring advanced technologies like net-
work slicing to enhance 5G performance in UAV applications [5].
Comparatively, in the study ”End-to-End Performance Measurements of Drone Communications
in 5G Cellular Networks,” Festag et al.
found that while 5G networks generally supported
low-latency requirements, environmental factors, and network congestion significantly impacted
performance, consistent with our findings of variable RTTs due to video feed impacts on network
load [3].
The experiments conducted in this thesis demonstrate the potential and challenges of using 5G
networks for real-time video communication in drones. The findings align with existing litera-

ture, emphasizing the impact of network congestion, altitude, and simultaneous data streams
on latency and video quality. Addressing these challenges through advanced network manage-
ment and optimization strategies will be crucial for successfully deploying 5G-enabled UAV

5.1
Procedures successfully applied
During the duration of this project, different procedures and tools were considered when per-
forming the test. The ones used are listed, and a brief description of why they were chosen.
1. Iperf and Ping for network performance analysis: This software was chosen for its wide
industry use and proven reliability. The option of proprietary software would still imply a
comparative analysis with this tool assets the precision of the software itself.
2. Video Analysis with OpenCV: The necessity of evaluating video performance with a com-
parative tool in order to determine fluctuations not only in the quality but also in the
transmission time of the video made the use of this tool key. This thesis successfully im-
plemented OpenCV and FFmpeg to extract and analyze the video discrepancies between
the one produced at the drone and the video received in the base computer.
3. Determination of High and Low flight path: for choosing the altitude used in this test, the
maximum legal height was limited to 126 meters the legal maximum flight height for this
test. For the minimum flight height, 50 meters was chosen arbitrarily as a consensus with
the drone pilots to avoid any possible interference that could appear in the line of sight.
4. Tailsacale VPN ensured the connection between the drone and base computer under the
VOXL2 protocols. This is a factory default setting, and the attempts to replace it with
Static IP weren’t successful during June Testing.
5. A better server connection was used in the June Test compared to March. This is due to
the possibility of the 5G hotspot being a funnel in the communication between the drone
as a client and the computer as a server. This assumption proved to be a factor in the
second parallel test.
This thesis successfully explores the different implications that Height has on video Latency
in 5g drones by using well-proved tools like Iperf and Ping and convincing them with Video
comparison. This project used Different Python scripts for video processing and data analy-
sis. Different test modalities were tested to rule out possible factors and maximize the results
representations of reality.

7
Conclusion
The evaluation of latency in 5G drone communication reveals significant advancements and on-
going challenges that impact real-time applications. Through extensive testing and comparison
with existing literature, several critical insights have emerged.
5G technology promises substantial improvements in network performance, particularly in terms
of latency and throughput. Our experimental results demonstrated that 5G can achieve low
latency, essential for real-time video applications, a crucial requirement for applications like
blue light surveillance and video-based control systems. During the March and June test flights,
RTT values were generally low, with most measurements under 250 ms, although environmental
factors and network congestion caused fluctuations[3].
The study ”First Experiments with a 5G-Connected Drone” confirmed that altitude and network
infrastructure significantly impact the latency and reliability of drone communications, with
higher altitudes resulting in increased RTT values due to greater signal propagation delays and
potential obstructions[6]. Similarly, our findings indicated higher latency at 120m compared to
50m, highlighting the need for optimized network infrastructure to support various operational
scenarios.
For video latency, maintaining consistently low latency is critical for applications requiring real-
time video transmission, such as surveillance and monitoring. Our tests showed an average video
latency of around 200 ms at 50m, which increased with altitude. This aligns with Festag et al.’s
findings, where 5G networks supported high data rates but experienced variable latency based
on environmental conditions[3].
Comparing sequential and parallel operations, it was evident that running iPerf, Ping, and
video transmission simultaneously strained the network more than sequential operations, causing
higher RTT and more pronounced network congestion. This suggests that effective network
management strategies are crucial for handling concurrent data streams efficiently.
5G’s evolving technology offers significant potential for enhancing drone communication systems.
Supporting high-throughput, low-latency connections is pivotal for real-time applications, in-
cluding blue light surveillance, where reliability and rapid data transmission are essential. How-
ever, the technology must continue to evolve to address challenges such as network congestion,
environmental impacts, and maintaining low latency across different operational conditions.
Future research should focus on optimizing 5G network infrastructure, improving congestion
management, and exploring advanced technologies like network slicing to enhance the perfor-
mance and reliability of 5G-enabled UAV applications. These improvements will be crucial for
deploying effective real-time video-based control systems and ensuring the robustness of appli-
cations requiring ultra-reliable low-latency communication (URLLC).
In conclusion, while 5G technology significantly advances drone communication capabilities,
ongoing developments, and optimizations are essential to realizing its potential in real-time,
high-stakes applications fully. Addressing the current challenges will ensure that 5G can meet
the stringent requirements of applications such as blue light surveillance, ultimately leading to
more reliable and effective video-based control systems.

A.1
Dissimilarity Score Calculations
A Python script was created for the Dissimilarity Score calculation.
1. The script uses the ”cv2.absdiff” function from OpenCV to compute the absolute difference
between the pixel values of the two frames. This function subtracts the pixel values of one
frame from the corresponding pixel values of the other frame and takes the absolute value
of the result. This operation highlights the differences between the two frames.
2. The resulting difference image is converted to grayscale using cv2.cvtColor.
This step
simplifies the difference image by reducing it to a single channel, making it easier to
threshold and analyze.
3. The grayscale difference image is then thresholded using ”cv2.threshold”. This function
converts the grayscale image to a binary image where pixel values above a certain threshold
(in this case, 30) are set to the maximum value (255), and pixel values below the threshold
are set to 0. This step helps to emphasize significant differences while ignoring minor
variations.
4. Finally, the script counts the number of non-zero pixels in the thresholded image using
np.countnonzero. The number of non-zero pixels represents the regions where significant
differences were detected between the two frames. The more non-zero pixels there are, the
greater the difference between the frames.
Finally The count of non-zero pixels (non zero count) is used as the dissimilarity score. This
score indicates the extent of dissimilarity between the two frames, with higher scores representing
more significant differences.
A.1.1
Interpretation of the Dissimilarity Score
• Low Dissimilarity Score: Indicates that the frames are very similar, with few differences.
• High Dissimilarity Score: Indicates significant differences between the frames, which could
be due to various factors such as frame drops, changes in resolution, or other artifacts.
This quantitative measure helps objectively analyze and compare the two videos frame by frame.
