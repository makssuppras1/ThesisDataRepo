Abstract
There is a growing interest in understanding how the indoor environment impacts stu­
dents’ overall well­being and academic performance. Numerous studies highlighting the
positive effects of natural light on health and learning. Danish elementary schools were
previously assessed for indoor air quality, but there has been limited exploration into the
availability of daylight in these educational institutions. This research explores the rela­
tionship among daylight, building typologies, and students’ academic performance, aiming
to present the existing daylight conditions in specific elementary schools within the Copen­
hagen Metropolitan area. In Copenhagen, there are a total of sixty elementary schools,
each were categorized based on their architectural characteristics and classroom layout.
The final selection accounts for a total of six schools, totaling 11 buildings, and 161 class­
rooms. The buildings were modeled in the software Rhino and simulating with the Lady­
bug/Honeybee tools in Grasshopper. The simulations inputs followed the EN 17037:2018
Standards and the Danish Building Regulations (BR18). The assessment of classrooms
involved considerations of Window­to­Floor Ratio, Daylight Factor, and Spatial Daylight
Availability. Evaluations were conducted both with and without considering the surround­
ings. The findings indicate that 70% of the classrooms meet the minimum illuminance
requirement of 300 lux. Additionally, all buildings exhibit an average Window­to­Floor
Ratio (WFR) exceeding the 10% threshold specified by BR18. The various architectural
typologies demonstrate the ability to achieve the recommended minimum illuminance for
educational settings, however the outcome is significantly influenced by the urban con­
text. Based on the available data and results, establishing a correlation between students’
performance and absenteeism with daylight availability was not feasible. Further research
will be required to investigate this subject more comprehensively.

1
Introduction
Elementary schools in Denmark were previously monitored regarding their indoor air qual­
ity, but little investigation was made regarding the daylight availability in the schools. Day­
light has an important role in human performance and learning processes. Examining the
daylight typology of schools is essential for gaining a comprehensive understanding of
the current learning environment for children. This study will present the current daylight
availability in some selected elementary schools in Copenhagen’s Metropolitan area.
1.1
Context and Motivation
In the past years, there was a growing interest regarding the indoor environment of the
schools and how it affects the overall performance and health of the students. Numerous
studies demonstrate the value of natural light for peoples overall heath. Beyond merely
the visual areas, light exposure plays a crucial role in the hormonal and emotion regu­
lations [1]. According to Küller and Lindsten article (1992) on the correlation between
children’s health and behaviour in classrooms with and without windows, the lack of ex­
posure of daylight has an influence on the children’s chronobiological rhythms and the
basic hormone patterns, affecting their ability to concentrate, cooperate, and eventually
having an impact in the sick leave and annual body growth [2].
1.2
Objectives
This study investigates the different daylight typologies in Copenhagen’s Elementary Schools,
and how they influence the daylight availability in the classrooms. The objectives are:
• Through a systematic review underline the research approach in daylighting and
lighting in schools and the state of knowledge related to daylighting and lighting as
part of IEQ;
• Categorize the schools based on several parameters, i.e., era they were built, rooms
distribution, facade elements, surrounding obstacles, orientation, and window to
floor ratio.
• Select representative schools depending on the aforementioned parameters;
• Compare the daylighting of the schools;
• Assess the daylighting performance based on existing performance criteria;
• Assess the daylighting performance of the schools based on socio­economically
weighted performance indicators in 9th grade;

This chapter establishes the foundational knowledge for the study. It begins by explaining

2.1
Daylighting in built environment: from definition to
design criteria
The properties of light can be explained by both the wave and particle theories, the wave­
particle­dualism of light. Light is described as the segment of the electromagnetic spec­
trum that is perceptible to the human eyes, between the range of 380 to 780 nm [3], see
Figure 2.1.
The eyes are the organs that detect the light through the photoreceptor in the retina:
cones and rods. The cones are responsible for detecting color vision in bright light. There
are three different types of cone photoreceptors: short­, medium­, and long­wavelength
cones. The rods, on the other hand, are responsible for low­light vision. The Bipolar
neurons receive the signals from the photoreceptor cells, and send it to the ganglion cells.
The ganglion cells send the signal to the optic nerve, which carries the visual information
to the brain. This process is referred to as the ”Visual Pathways”. The Visual Pathway is
also responsible for synchronizing the body’s Circadian rhythms and sleep through ”non­
visual pathways”. [4].
Figure 2.1: Electromagnetic spectrum (wavenlength in nm)
Besides the visual pathway, light exposure influences human physiology and behavior,
and it is an important determinant of health, well­being, and performance. The lack of
light exposure can affect negatively circadian rhythms, sleep quality, and alertness. It can
deregulate the neuroendocrine system, affecting the production of hormones crucial for
the proper functioning of the body [5].

The non­visual pathways are received by a specialized type of retinal neurons called intrin­
sically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs). These cells are capable of sensing
light independently of the visual pathway and contribute to regulating various physiologi­
cal functions. The light­sensitive pigment in the ipRGCs is melanopsin, having the peak
sensitivity at a wavelength of around 480 nm. The daytime light recommendation for in­
door environments is a minimum melanopic Equivalent Daylight Illuminance (EDI) of 250
lux at the level of a seated person (approximately 1.2 m height) [5].
Daylight is the sum of the skylight­diffused solar radiation and the direct solar radiation
from the sunlight. Daylight is dynamic and changes across the hours and the weather
conditions, changing colour, direction, and diffuseness. Daylighting is the strategy of the
use of natural daylight to illuminate the interior spaces of a building [6]. Most of daylighting
and lighting design criteria in buildings are now regulated based on users’ needs through
their visual pathways. Hence, this study is focused mainly on metrics that allow for the as­
sessment of daylight performance based on visual pathways as described in the following
sections.

Luminous Flux (lumen) refers to the power of visible light, it quantifies the perceived
brightness of light from a light source in all directions [7] [4]. Luminous intensity is em­
ployed to measure how light is distributed by a luminaire [4].
Luminous Intensity (candela) is the power of a light source beam in a given direction or
a certain angle. One candela (cd) is roughly equivalent to the luminous intensity of one
candle [7].
Illuminace (lux) is the amount of light (luminous flux, lumens) that falls into a surface
divided by the area (in square meters) [8].
Luminance (cd/m2) is the amount of light reflected from a surface reaching the eye [3],
being dependent on the angle of the receptor [7].
Reflectance, Transmittance and Absorption (ρ, τ, α) are the properties of the materi­
als. Reflectance indicates the amount of light hitting the surface that is reflected back;
Transmittance is the amount of light that goes through the material and absorption is the
measure of how much light is absorbed by the object. The units are given in percent­
age, and the sum of reflectance, transmittance and absorption for any material is 100%
[7]. The color and texture of a surface influence the reflectance of the material. A white
surface has typically a reflectance of 85%, whereas a black surface has only 5% [3].
The physical characteristics of the surface determine in what direction and ways the light
will be reflected. A very smooth polished surface (e.g. mirror), has a specular reflection;
the angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection. An opaque matte material dif­
fuses the light, redirecting the light in many different directions. Most surfaces present
both specular and diffuse characteristics [3]. Knowing the materials is important when
performing simulations using a dynamic method for daylight assessment since the char­
acteristics of the materials are taken into account in the calculation. See the Table 3.2in
Section 3.2.1 for the reflectance of some materials defined by the BR18 standards.
Daylight Factor (DF) is a static method for daylight assessment. It is the ratio between
the illuminance on a given indoor surface by the simultaneous illuminance from an out­
door surface under a CIE Standard Overcast Sky, being expressed in percentage. Direct
sunlight is not included in this calculation method [8]. See Equation ?? for the daylight
factor formula.

Daylight Autonomy (DA) is a dynamic method for daylight assessment. The advantage
of a dynamic daylight method is that it takes into consideration the changes in illuminance
levels during the day and the seasonal variations for a given location, building geometry
and the surroundings. The DA uses the work plane illuminance level as an indicator of
the daylight levels in that space, determining the percentage of time during the analysis
period that the target illuminance level is met without additional electrical lighting [10].
View­out is another important function of the windows, influencing the occupants’ well­
being and productivity. Interesting view­out can have therapeutic effects on the users,
especially views of nature. However, any view is better than a windowless interior [8].
Some building codes and certification systems include the quality of the view­out as design
criteria.
Glare could be defined as a non­uniform illumination in the wrong context [4], causing
discomfort or impairing performance [8]. Glare Index is a tool to quantify the level of dis­
comfort caused by glare. Depending on the intensity, glare can cause physical discomfort
such as headaches and fatigue, and reduce the user’s productivity [3].

2.2
Educational buildings’ in Denmark
The architectural typologies of the educational buildings in Denmark have been thoroughly
studied by Lisbet B. Jørgensen, Hakon Lund and Hans Edvard Nørregård­Nielsen. They
analysed the buildings evolution from the end of the 18th century (1800) until the middle of
the 20th century (around 1980). They classified the buildings according to their architec­
tural characteristics: facade aesthetics, building volume, rooms disposition, and architec­
ture trends of the time. The categorisations have been done based on the dominant archi­
tectural characteristics which has resulted in 7 categories, namely, Artfully Clad Facades,
Luxurious Facades, Village Schools, Danish School Houses, Functionalist schools, Hu­
manized Functionalist schools, and Open Architecture in different eras. In the Copen­
hagen area, the Functionalist and Humanized Functionalist, and Danish School Houses
types are seen more dominantly, with construction between 1900 and 1969.
School Act of 1814 and the architectural development in school buildings in
Denmark
In 1814, the School Act (”Skolepligt 1814”) had made education mandatory from the age
of 7 years old until the church Confirmation, which typically took place at the age of 14­
16 years old [14]. There was a gradual increase in the demand and expectations for
both the architectural design of the schools and the educational standards. The schools
should look more than ordinary village houses, instead, it was required to embody and
instil respect for the institution and its educators [15].
Artfully clad facades
During the 1800s, people admired the artfully clad facades. Andreas Bentsen, who ran
the Artisan School in Vallekilde and was in the construction sector, began to develop a
special architecture style with a Nordic accent. His first educational building was built in
1884. The building was a sports hall for Vallekilde High School made of wood, painted in
a vibrant turmeric­yellow, red and green colors (see Figure 2.3). In 1900, Andreas made
several more projects, both in wood and bricks [15].
Figure 2.3: Vallekilde High School Sports Hall, photo by Jens Cederskjold [16]
Luxurious facades (”Corridor Schools”)
In Copenhagen, the public and private sectors contributed significantly to education. From
the 1840s, one large school after another was constructed. The facades followed the me­
dieval and North Italian Renaissance style, creating an atmosphere honesty and moral
correctness. The new constructions prioritized the health and well­being of children, in­
corporating central heating and ventilation systems into the buildings. Constructed in

Landsbyskolerne (”Village Schools”)
In 1856, J. D. Herholdt created a rural school for the Danish Ministry of Culture. Departing
from classical symmetry, the school adopted a picturesque style. Despite its modest size,
the school conveyed an institutional character to its users. The various structures could be
constructed grouped or built together in accordance with the picturesque style, ensuring
a minimum space of 90 cubic feet per child [15].
Around 1900, several improvements were implemented regarding schools’ architectural
requirements. The maximum number of students per class should be 37, and a minimum
of 130 cubic feet per child was established. The floors should be varnished, and the
ground floor should be at least 18 inches above the terrain, and covered by concrete.
Windows were to be situated along one longitudinal wall, preferably allowing light from
the left for the children. The glass area should have a ratio of 1:5 to the floor area, and
all windows had to be operable to ensure appropriate ventilation, and equipped with light
gray roller blinds that could be cleaned. The vestibule should be 3 square feet per child
and covered by tiles [15].

Originally, a rural school building could be any house with room for a classroom and a
house for a teacher. With the new rules from 1900, a guideline for plans for schools of
different sizes was designed by Christian Thuren. At the same year, drawings for gym­
nasiums were also provided, executed by Andreas Bentsen. The interest in school ar­
chitecture was increasing, and in 1908 a Committee for a Better Rural Building from the
Academic Architectural Association reached out to the Ministry of Culture to seek support
for a publication containing proposals for school buildings [15].
The Danish School Houses
Following 1900, there was a general desire for school buildings with a more Danish char­
acter, maintaining the ongoing tradition and refraining from imposing foreign construction
methods on buildings. P.V. Jensen­Klint advocated for Danish craftsmanship, drawing
inspiration from Medieval Danish brick architecture as the foundation for the country’s
unique architectural identity.
One notable example of his design philosophy was the
Gotvedinstituttet in 1898, where the emphasis was on the raw material as the aesthetic
element, foregoing excessive adornments associated with Renaissance styles [15]. In
the subsequent years, there is a shift towards greater flexibility in school construction,
particularly concerning the floor plan. The central corridor is eliminated, and attempts
are made to implement more adaptable solutions that align better with diverse building
plots. At Vigerslev School, Hans Wright adopted a pavilion system, drawing inspiration
from, among other places, at Ny Carlsbergsvejen School (Vesterbro Ny Skole), he tested
a horizontal division, allocating the two lower floors for boys and the two upper floors for
girls. Both schools exhibit simplicity and a distinctly Danish character, evident in their red
brick facades [15]. Designed by N.C. Christensen in 1912, the municipal schools in Sund­
byøster, Sundbyvester (Amager Fælled Skole), and Nyboder from 1918 are characterized
by a three­winged structure with side corridors and a vertical division. The facades, in line
with tradition, reflect influences from studies in older Danish brick architecture. Following
the decline of C.F. Hansen’s ”tedious, authoritarian, and international” classicism, which
is now seen as simple, rational, and national, many embraced the pure monumentality of
these schools. These sentiments weren’t limited to Denmark; they constituted an interna­
tional phenomenon. State schools in Randers and Viborg, designed by Hack Kampmann,
showcase expansive monumental classical complexes featuring columns and symmetry.
The architect aimed to create an atmosphere of high morality and rational thinking. It was
like a new life after a long period of decline [15].

Functionalism
In 1920, Edvard Thomas and G. B. Hagen won the competition for designing the Øregård

by industrial production. According to the movement, a democratic state did not need
monumentally and unnecessary ornaments. It need functional buildings, being the space
and form as simple and appropriate as possible [15] [20]. The buildings had horizontal and
vertical lines and planes (e.g. flat roof), and modern materials (steel, glass and concrete)
[20].

Munkegård School in Gentofte (see Figure 2.9b) achieved a comforting atmosphere for
around 1,000 students through precise structuring of functions, creating villa garden­like
courtyards. The advantages of low schools, including optimal daylighting, were evident
in Munkegård School designed by Arne Jacobsen, and Stengård School in Gladsaxe de­
signed by Vilhelm Lauritzen (See Figure 2.10). Steen Eiler Rasmussen’s design for a
preschool in Rungsted in 1953 emphasized optimal daylighting and outdoor teaching op­
portunities. In Hanssted School in Copenhagen, designed by F.C. Lund and Hans Chris­
tian Hansen, the artistic use of daylight and various materials contributed to a visually
appealing environment. There was no consensus on whether the architectural design of
schools should be impressive or modest, and whether it should resemble a palace or a
more homely environment.[15]
Open Architecture
During the 1970s, there was a recognition of the importance of open architecture capable
of adapting to evolving functional requirements over time. The structure should have a uni­
versal character and feature versatile floor plans. When undertaking extensive projects,
it is crucial to create a framework that aligns with human objectives, facilitates effective
communication, and integrates systematic installations—all constructed from industrially
manufactured units. However, alongside considerations of economy and functionality,
experience holds equal significance. Aesthetic standards for both overall assembly and
intricate details, as well as the necessity for diversity, remain unabated. Even though the
current aim is a ”neutral” architecture lacking of rhetorical expression, the aspiration is not
to revert to the ”honest factory building” of the 1930s.
Some examples of buildings following the Open Architecture guidelines are Odense Uni­
versity (1971), Technological Institute in Høje Tåastrupe (1970) Copenhagen University
on Amager (1972), and Technical University of Denmark (1959), among others. These
buildings are characterized by modular structures and an urban layout along a central
axis, designed to accommodate expansion over an extended period. The open design
incorporates centralized hubs and lacks definitive facades, facilitating easy adaptation for
various purposes. [15]

2.3
A Systematic Literature Review: Daylight and Lighting in
schools
The process was initiated by conducting a systematic literature review with a focus on the
impact of daylighting and lighting on students’ performance. This served as a foundation,
emphasizing the significance of evaluating and ensuring optimal lighting in educational
buildings.
2.3.1
Systematic Literature Review
A systematic literature review was performed to access the available publications and
studies regarding the topic artificial and/or natural lighting and students performance. The
first step was to research in the databases, such as Science Direct, Scopus [34], Sage
[35], Taylor & Francis [36], and identify all the publications available today. The second
step was to go through the abstract and filter the relevant papers; identify the methodology
used, how the experiment was performed, the parameters used, researched group age,
results, geographic location and Köppen Climate Classification [37].
In the last 10 years, the number of publications increased significantly, being most of
these studies carried out in Europe and Asia, with 34 and 18 publication respectively.
The United States of America and United Kingdom are the countries with the highest
number of publication. See Figure 2.12. The total of 70 relevant papers were identified
through the systematic research, and they were published between 1974 and 2023. Of
the 70 papers, 14 studies were about daylight, 33 were about artificial lighting, and 23
were combined (daylight and artificial lighting).
The studies were categorized by type of environment: controlled environments (CE), con­
trolled real environments (CRE), field study (FS), and controlled real environment + field
study (CRE/FS). Other studies features was: the number of environmental factors as­
sessed, the number of lighting parameters assessed, duration of data collection, consis­
tency of the measurements regarding lighting parameters, consistency of the measure­
ments, and sample size.
The predictor variables evaluated for lighting parameters included window view, blind op­
eration, window­to­wall ratio (WWR), daylight factor (DF), illuminance (both horizontal and
vertical), luminance, glare, correlated color temperature (CCT), correlated reproduction
index (CRI), enriched light, light uniformity, and students’ exposure to daylight.

2.3.2
Main findings
Several studies showed that good lighting (both daylight and artificial lighting) is associ­
ated with the overall satisfaction of the students [38][39][40][41][42], their behaviour such
as seating preferences and amount of time spent in the space [39], and have a signifi­
cant impact on learning and academic achievements [43]. Nevertheless, various factors
contribute to overall student satisfaction and performance.
Research that considered a range of factors within Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ)
highlighted Thermal Comfort as the most influential environmental parameter affecting
students’ overall satisfaction and performance. While Visual Comfort is also a signifi­
cant factor, it doesn’t hold the same level of influence as Thermal Comfort [44][2][38][40]
[41][45][46][47][48][49][50][51]. Suggesting a necessity for comprehensive studies involv­
ing multiple modes to enhance the understanding of light in indoor environments.
Daylight and View­out
Daylight alone has not demonstrated an impact on students’ performance [52], and in
certain instances, adverse effects have been documented, particularly in situations char­
acterized by glare [53][44][39][47][54][55]. Having control over shading devices exhibited
a notable positive influence on students’ performance [55], with contrast glare frequently
identified as the primary cause of reported glare [56].

==PAGE:31==
In the absence of daylight, significant disruptions to the chronobiological system were
noted, leading to subsequent decreases in students’ concentration levels [2][57]. The
level of urbanization is connected to daylight exposure, with less urbanized regions ex­
periencing greater exposure to daylight. This is associated with a higher prevalence of
morning types and a reduced incidence of excessive daytime sleepiness [57].
The presence of a view and a natural scene has been linked to increased satisfaction
[58] and pleasantness [39], as well as improved student performance [59][55][60] and
learning outcomes [53][44][61], often resulting in higher grades [58][55][60]and enhanced
attention [52]. Performance was not significantly impacted by the quality of the outdoor
view (whether natural or urban) or the orientation of the window [55].
Artificial Lighting
Students exhibited a preference for illuminance levels within the range of 300 lux to 500
lux [62][63][64], aligning with the recommended lighting guidelines. Perceptions of lighting
and the levels of illumination alone have demonstrated no impact on reading performance
[65][66][67][68]. However, effective lighting control demonstrated favourable impacts on
both students’ cognitive performance and circadian cycles [69]. Additionally, research
indicated improvements in reading speed and comprehension under conditions where in­
dividuals could choose their lighting levels [70] [71]. The combination of light levels [72]
and Correlated Color Temperature (CCT) have demonstrated multiple positive effects on
the directly assessed performance of students [72][73][74], as well as on reading perfor­
mance [70].
Blue­enriched white lights exhibit advantageous effects on students’ performance, leading
to faster cognitive processing speed and improved concentration, as observed in studies
[42][75]. It is noteworthy that monochromatic light could have an almost instantaneous
impact on cognitive functions [75]. Attention and memory tasks require different levels of
illumination, while attention improves with higher light levels, memory improves with lower
light levels [73].
In terms of students’ behavior in the classroom, under Full­Spectrum lighting conditions,
there was a noted decrease in hyperactive behavior [76], along with lower heart rates
and enhanced visual comfort [77]. Furthermore, students exposed to these conditions
demonstrated improved attendance rates, faster academic progress, and greater height
gain [68]. On average, students exhibited more engaged behavior with LED lighting com­
pared to fluorescent lighting, with a particularly significant change observed in students
with developmental disabilities [65], potentially attributable to the flicker effect in fluores­
cent lamps. The lighting system can also contribute to disturbing glare, impacting stu­
dents’ performance [78]. Additionally, the exposure of children to low levels of UV light
has been linked to the prevention or reduction of dental caries [68].

According to the Daylight in Building EN 17037:2018 Standard [9], the simulation refer­

3.3
Daylight parameters
To evaluate the daylight characteristics within schools, a Spatial Daylight Availability (sDA)
simulation was conducted for each selected building. Two simulations were performed for
each building: one considering the surrounding environment and another without it. By
eliminating the influence of external factors, the simulation aimed to provide a clearer un­
derstanding of how different building types contribute to illuminance levels. Furthermore,
a single room with comparable window orientation, and ideally located on the same storey
level, was chosen from each building for the purpose of analyzing and comparing their
respective daylight characteristics.
The target sDA level was set to the minimum and medium requirements according to
the Standard mentioned in the Section 2.1 and the Table 2.2 presented in the Section
2.1.2. To achieve the minimum requirement, the room should have a minimum illuminance
of 300 lux in 50% of the working plane area, during 50% of the occupancy time. For
the medium requirement, the minimum illuminance is 500 lux. As the Danish Building
Regulations (BR18) employ the WFR of 10% as one of the daylight assessment methods,
the calculation was integrated into this study as well.

5
Discussion and Conclusion
After the School Act of 1814, it was possible to identify a clear trend of the schools design.
In Copenhagen, the majority of schools were designed and constructed for educational
purposes, rather than being adaptations of existing structures. However, classifying and
grouping these schools based on the year of construction proved to be a complex task.
As previously mentioned in the Section 3.1.1, architectural design lacks a strict temporal
division. Consequently, after being classified by the architecture period, the buildings were
grouped according to classroom arrangements and other architectural characteristics.
The final selection of schools encompasses all the architectural eras described in this
study: 2 buildings ­ Luxurious Facades (1840­1899), 2 buildings ­ Danish School Houses
(1900­1929), 1 building ­ Functionalism/Humanized Functionalism (1930­1969), 3 build­
ings ­ Open Architecture (1970­1999), and 3 buildings ­ Contemporary Architecture (2000­
2023). And all the different floor plan types: A, B, C, D and E.
Figure 5.1: Dormer window type
in Rådmandsgade Skole B1 B
(Google Street View)
Most schools adhere to the current Danish Building Reg­
ulations (BR18), specifically the 10% rule for Window­
to­Floor Ratio (WFR). However, two buildings, B1 of
Sølvgade Skole (1847) and B1 B of Rådmandsgade
Skole (2011), did not meet these requirements. Both
structures featured rooms with a WFR of 9%. In Sølv­
gade Skole, the lower percentage may be attributed to
the sturdy window frame and grilles dividing the glass
area. Although constructed in 2011, the second floor
of Building B1 B in Rådmandsgade Skole has relatively
small square windows and a type of dormer window in
the roof (see Figure 5.1), it still falls short of achieving
sufficient glazing area.
Two schools achieved excellent results in terms of sDA
300 lux/50%: Skolen på Amagerbro and Ålholm Skole;
with all and classrooms achieving the minimum illumi­
nance target of 300 lux. These schools are located in less dense urban settings, featuring
more significant spacing between buildings and fewer obstructions/shading from adjacent
structures. The good performance of the B4 in Skolen på Amagerbro may be attributed to
its unique geometry: a single­storey block­style structure with a garden situated in front
of the primary windows. The spacing between the blocks minimizes the obstruction of
daylight within the classroom. Despite being one of the deeper classrooms, the room’s
geometry, along with a tilted ceiling, aids in redirecting light from the high window facing
North­West. The favorable illuminance levels in the classrooms at Ålholm Skole B1 may

5.1
Conclusion
The purpose of this study was to explore the relationship between daylight and the typolo­
gies of buildings in elementary schools in Copenhagen. Daylight is a significant factor that
could influence students’ overall performance and satisfaction. Enhancing the academic
environment, especially for children, is of great interest for our society. However the cor­
relation between student performance and daylight, as well as sick­leave and daylight,
could not be identified in this study. Numerous other factors may impact students’ per­
formance, being necessary a more comprehensive approach to evaluating indoor envi­
ronmental conditions in schools. This includes considering the socio­economic context in
which the children are immersed. Most of the schools assessed showed positive results
for sDA 300 lux/50%, especially when evaluated without taking the surrounding context
into account. Among the examined buildings, the architectural styles that yielded the
most favorable outcomes were Functionalism/Humanized Functionalism and Open Ar­
chitecture. Similarly, the floor plan types, Type C and Type D demonstrated the most
positive results.
By observing the facades and windows of the Contemporary Architecture period, it be­
comes apparent that many of these buildings feature bigger glazing areas when com­
paring to other periods. However, a larger glazed area does not necessarily guarantee
optimal illuminance in classrooms. The surrounding environment, as well as the arrange­
ment and geometry of windows, significantly influence the final daylight availability and
distribution. Large windows may also imply issues with glare and/or overheating. Older
buildings from the Luxurious Facades and Danish School Houses era also have the po­
tential to meet the minimum illuminance target, but this is contingent on the urban context
in which they are situated.
In conclusion, it can be deduced that, overall, all architectural typologies have the capabil­
ity to meet the minimum recommended illuminance for school environments. The results
demonstrate the importance of analyzing carefully the context in which the building is sit­
uated, and not just blindly following recommendations for the proportion of windows to
floor area.
5.1.1
Prospective and future steps
Concerning daylight typology, an alternative approach to evaluating the schools could
have involved simulating a single room in each building with identical window orientations,
excluding the surroundings. This would have ensured a more standardized basis for
comparison. A greater number of rooms would have provided more comprehensive data
for comparing and identifying potential trends within the various typologies. Additionally,

==PAGE:79==
it would have allowed testing different settings, such as varying levels of urbanization and
window orientations.
In this study, the school with the smallest window area exhibited the highest average
grades, and schools with a bigger window area had a lower average grade. It would
be interesting to investigate the importance of the view­out in the elementary schools, to
evaluate what are the effects on the students performance. Previous studies, focusing
on kindergartens and universities, have demonstrated that the view­out aspects have a
positive effect on students performance and overall satisfaction. Nevertheless, it would
be interesting to investigate if the outside view might have a more distracting effects and
negative effects on performance depending on the age of the students. In addition, larger
window areas could introduce issues such as disturbing glare and/or overheating in the
classrooms.
There are many factors that could influence the students’ performance and overall well­
being, therefore it would be interesting to explore a more comprehensive approach when
assessing the indoor environment of schools. The schools Sølvgade Skole and Skolen
på Amagerbro could serve as intriguing subjects for research, given the unexpected out­
comes observed in terms of performance, well­being, and daylight.
5.2
Limitations
The examination specifically targeted elementary schools, and the study primarily re­
volves around daylight illuminance. The study relies entirely on simulations and public
available data, without any on­site measurements taken. Building measurements were
extracted from existing blueprints. However, the documentation did not provide clarity on
whether the available drawings were updated or not. The simulations did not take into ac­
count electrical lighting, and surface reflectances adhered to the Danish Building Regula­
tion Standards (BR18) due to insufficient information about finishing surfaces. Information
about the surroundings, including building heights and urban density, was acquired using
the Google Earth tool. Vegetation was not taken into account in this study.
It’s noteworthy that potential considerations for the impact of lighting on users’ well­being
may be explored in future studies. This methodology highlights the dependence on simu­
lation techniques, utilization of available data sources, and the defined scope and limita­
tions of the study, particularly in the investigation of daylight typologies in Danish schools.
• The research is solely reliant on simulations, and there are no on­site measurements
conducted;
• Measurements of the buildings were based on the available blueprints;
• No electrical lighting was not taken into consideration in the simulations;
• There was no information about the finishing surfaces, so the surfaces reflectances
followed the Danish Building Regulation Standards (BR18) [12];
• The information of the surroundings (building heights, urban density, vegetation,
etc.) were taken by Google Earth tool [89].
• Only elementary schools were analysed;
• This study is illuminance based;
• The benefits of lighting in the users’ well­being could be addressed in future studies;
