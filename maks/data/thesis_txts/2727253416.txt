Abstract
As of January 2023, estimations say that 5.44 billion people in the world use the
internet, of which 4.76 billion people use social media. In recent years, online commu-
nities have become popular platforms for people to engage in discussions and share
information with like-minded individuals, changing the Internet from being a network
of documents to becoming a network of people, growing at an unprecedented pace.
The Internet’s transformation has a huge strategic impact for global companies
like Unity Technologies, with 3.9 billion end users influencing the company’s brand.
Experts users, with advanced insights and extensive experience play a crucial role in
understanding a company’s products or services. Engaging with these users brings
valuable feedback, innovative ideas, and potential brand ambassadorship. This en-
gagement allows companies to build relationships with knowledgeable customers, lead-
ing to increased user satisfaction and brand perception, creating a competitive edge in
the market. Consequently, companies like Unity Technologies must devise strategies
to identify, communicate with, and nurture subject matter experts while managing
associated Big Data.
In this thesis, our mission is therefore to uncover subject matter experts within the
dedicated discussion forum hosted by Unity Technologies: Unity Forums. Compared
to already existing studies, we investigate an unprecedented large-scale dataset, mak-
ing us able to capture the entire spectrum of an online community, making our work
highly novel.
We evaluate several techniques for identifying users within expert knowledge do-
mains. We employ seven different clustering algorithms and use common evaluation
measures to choose the best performing method. K-means, one of the best known and
benchmarked clustering algorithms, delivers the best performance, hence we employ
this method to uncover interesting groupings from the data. Our results show that
the user-groups found are highly structured.
With creating a social network of thread participation, we back up our resulting
clusters using prevalent methods for detecting community structures. We find that
communities are hard to detect with standard approaches, and employ an alternative
method for finding structures within the network created. Our results show that the
clusters found exhibit a high level of assortativity for multiple features, that is users
in the clusters tend to connect to other users of similar characteristics.
Finally, we discuss the methodology, limitations, and applications of our process and
the results found, while also providing potential directions for future research.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
As of January 2023 estimations, 5.44 billion people in the world use the internet, of
which 4.76 billion people use social media, accounting for 64.4% and 59.4% of the
world’s population, respectively [2]. In recent years, online communities have become
popular platforms for people to engage in discussions, share information, and connect
with like-minded individuals, changing the Internet from being a network of docu-
ments to becoming a network of people [3,4].
The literature holds various definitions of what defines an online community. Starting
with ”online communities include users who form relationships with other users due
to a mutual interest or goal”, multiple additions of characteristics to this definition
have been proposed [3]: First, an online community needs an adequate number of
members; second, users should share a mutual interest or concern to interact with
each other, that is a common project, goal, profession or likewise; third, rules and
practices should govern the behaviour of community members; fourth, participation is
key for novice and experienced users. Finally, a feeling of belonging to the community
should exist, and members should be willing to participate and exchange information.
Today it is common for end users of products or services, software or otherwise, to
show a significant online presence in various community or social media platforms:
dedicated discussion forums hosted by the service provider, or public platforms such
as Twitter, YouTube, Instagram, Reddit and similar – users expressing all from prod-
uct opinions, satisfaction, paint points, roadmap wishes and brand perception, which
can greatly impact a company brand [1,4,5].
Online forums have become a powerful source for businesses to analyse online users’
behaviour, and with the rapid growth of online communities, a growing need has
emerged to understand user behaviour, community dynamics, and group structures
within these forums. By identifying and characterise users from the groups found,
community managers can formalise assumptions on how users behave and connect
with their environment. This ultimately helps managers develop strategies for mo-
tivation, involvement and communication of community members. Different types
of users exist, and understanding the diverse needs of these types of users, know-
ing how to motivate and keep them engaged, is of great importance to community
managers [3].

1.1
Related Work
Studying how users group together in online forums is far from a novel phenomena [6].
Efforts to group users into roles according to their observed behaviour has been tried
many times, usually by using unsupervised clustering algorithms due to a general lack
of a reliable ground truth [7, 8]. In the era of Big Data and Big Data Analytics [9],
common approaches in data mining for uncovering similar groups of users include
clustering, and for finding trends among people, hot topics include Social Network
Analysis (SNA).
Clustering is one of the most useful techniques in data mining tasks for discovering
patterns in data. [10]. Clustering is the process of organising observations into groups
(”clusters”) such that the collection of observations are similar, and dissimilar to other
collections of observations.
SNA focuses on studying the relationships and interactions between individuals or
entities within a network, and network-based clustering aims to partition the network
into distinct communities or clusters, where users within the same cluster are more
closely connected than to users in other clusters [11–13].
Stephan A. et al. use document clustering and topic modelling in two online social
networks, Twitter and Reddit, to derive groups of interest based on Term Frequency
Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) between users [4, 14]. They use clustering
techniques applied to neural embedding feature representations, train document-level
dense vectors, and interpret the clusters with TF-IDF weights combined with embed-
ding distance measures.
The study finds that representing documents in an em-
bedding space combined with k-means clustering of the vector space gave the best
performance in clustering the users.
Especially Twitter data (Twitter: a tool for social communication [15]) has been
explored for clustering the user base of a company. Friedmann [16] uses k-means to
cluster publicly available Twitter data, and found that the method performed well
on features derived from the scraped Twitter data, and highlights that the k-means
method exhibits potential to further improve given access to more data.
Noufa Alnajran et al. [12] also apply several unsupervised machine learning ap-
proaches, particularly clustering, to unstructured Twitter data. They highlight that
the algorithms perform well in terms of eﬀiciency and accuracy, but stress that the
high performance may be due to the small size of the datasets available, hence scala-
bility has not been evaluated.
Gong, Lim, and Zhu [17] employ SNA on Twitter data to find users that opt to be
silent. They propose that silent users are especially important in targeted advertising.
They define four types of tweet content features to develop their profiling method,
and apply Naïve Bayes and Support Vector Machine (SVM) to train their classifier.
The study shows that by using silent users’ neighbours content, one can profile these

users with accuracy comparable to that of profiling active users.
Ye and Pennisi [18] use SNA to study students’ online discussion interaction pat-
terns and investigate how they are related to students’ learning performance. They
find that significant positive correlations exists between learning performance and
out-degree and closeness, respectively. They further conclude that that knowledge
construction mainly happens in reading and commenting on others’ posts by internal-
ising and integrating external useful information from others, and provide strategies
to encourage students to actively reach out to other students.
We cannot fulfil this review of literature without mentioning Reddit (an American
social news aggregation, content rating, and discussion website). Choi et al. [19] inves-
tigate the characteristics of online threaded conversations and how content properties
and user participation are associated with such characteristics. They structure the
threaded conversations as trees, characterise these trees by volume, responsiveness
and virality, and then perform an analysis based on semantic similarity, that is word
count, document diﬀiculty, and document relevancy (TF-IDF). The results suggest
that large, responsive, and viral conversations have high document relevancy between
comments in threaded conversations, and that these conversations are built by recip-
rocal communications from a relatively small portion of users.
Studying user groups (clusters) in online forums holds significant importance due to
several factors. First, it allows for the ability to uncover patterns that may not be
immediately apparent. Second, it opens up for the possibility of identifying specific
characteristics, preferences, or needs that are common within each cluster, which allow
community managers to develop strategies for targeted interactions, interventions,
knowledge sharing and collaboration [6,7].
1.2
Critical Analysis of and Gaps and Limitations in the Existing
Literature
While acknowledging significant contributions from the literature on clustering users
in online communities, several gaps and limitations remain, indicating areas that
require further exploration. The following list addresses the most prevalent limitations
found in our research, and advances our understanding of clustering users in online
forums.
1. Diversity of approaches: The literature on clustering users on online forums
exhibits a wide range of approaches, including partition-based, network-based
and behaviour-based clustering. This diversity of methods allows researchers to
consider different aspects of user behaviour and interaction. However, the ab-
sence of a standardised approach makes it challenging to compare and replicate
studies, hindering the establishment of a unified methodology. [5,12]
==PAGE:28==

1 Introduction
2. Data availability and quality: Many studies have faced challenges related
to data availability and quality when analysing online forums. First, privacy
concerns (GDPR [20] and CCPA [21]) and limitations in data access restrict the
availability of complete datasets: Most of the datasets available are of relatively
small size, the datasets thus not being indicative of the real patterns in social
behaviour. These points make it diﬀicult to draw comprehensive conclusions,
hence generalised conclusions cannot be drawn. Furthermore, the sheer amount
of data, if obtainable, contains many types of information (text, images, code
pieces etc.) adding to the complexity of the clustering process [4,12,16]. With
having access to an unprecedented large-scale dataset of an online community,
this work has the opportunity of contributing to this limited area of the field.
3. Evaluation metrics and benchmarks: Limited data availability and qual-
ity (from above point) imply that evaluation of clustering results remains a
challenge in the field. While various metrics like within-cluster sum of squares
(WCSS), Silhouette coeﬀicient, or Rand index are employed [22,23], the choice
of appropriate metrics depends on the research objectives and available ground
truth. With the absence of benchmark datasets and standard evaluation pro-
tocols, it is challenging to compare and validate the effectiveness of different
clustering algorithms across studies [5–8]. Alternative approaches have been
suggested To overcome this. Caruana et al. suggest using expert domain knowl-
edge to select clusterings of the data that best fit the researchers’ needs [23],
and further argue that clustering quality depends on how the clusters will be
used.
Overall, clustering methods can provide valuable insights into user behaviour and
community dynamics in online forums. Identifying and characterising users of im-
portance to community structures found, enables community managers to formalise
assumptions on how users behave and connect with their environment [1]. This can
in turn facilitate targeted support and interventions, improving community manage-
ment, user engagement and strategy design for e.g. enhancing satisfaction.
In this work, we will use the approach of clustering in combination with SNA to find
interesting groups of users on Unity Technologies’ online community: Unity Forums.
Hence, we will provide the reader with a short overview of prevalent types of clustering
methods used today (see Section 1.3). The list is not exhaustive.
1.3
Overview of Clustering Methods - Current Trends
This section provides an overview of commonly used clustering methods, and is mainly
inspired by [5,24,25] if not otherwise indicated.
Clustering is the process of organising or partitioning observations into groups (”clus-
ters”) such that the pairwise dissimilarity between observations assigned to the same
==PAGE:29==
1.3 Overview of Clustering Methods - Current Trends
7
cluster tend to be smaller than observations assigned to different clusters. [26, p 459].
Said differently, a cluster is a collection of objects where these objects are similar,
and dissimilar to other clusters.
As we now know, clustering methods play a crucial role in analysing user behaviour
and community dynamics within online forums. Various approaches have been em-
ployed to cluster users based on their interactions, content, or behaviour. The follow-
ing (non-exhaustive) list is intended to give the reader a bit of background knowledge
on some of the most common clustering methods:
Hierarchical clustering. In hierarchical clustering, clusters are formed either by
iteratively dividing patterns using either a top-down or a bottom-up approach, [24].
There exists two types of hierarchical clustering: 1) the agglomerative hierarchical
clustering, which uses the bottom-up approach and builds up clusters starting with
a single object, merging the objects into larger and larger clusters until all objects
are in a single cluster, and 2) the divisive hierarchical clustering, which follows a
top-down approach, breaking up clusters until each object forms a cluster of its own.
The hierarchical nature of this method allows for exploration at different levels of
granularity, facilitating a deeper understanding of the clustering structure within on-
line forums [12].
Partitioning-based. Partitional clustering is opposite to hierarchical clustering. In
partitional clustering, data are assigned into k clusters by optimising some criterion
of similarity – the Euclidean distance being the most common [5,24].
K-means, one of the best known and benchmarked clustering algorithms, is the
most common approach to solving clustering problems. This method iteratively as-
signs samples to the nearest centroid (mean of the data points in each cluster) and
updates the centroids based on the mean of the assigned samples. K-means clustering
is computationally eﬀicient and works well with a large number of samples. However,
it requires a pre-specification of the number of clusters, and its performance can be
sensitive to the initial selection of centroids. Additionally, it assumes clusters with
convex shapes, limiting its applicability to complex structures – often observed in on-
line forums. K-means has been adapted to suit datasets including numerical, binary
and categorical features [12].
Density-based: Here, the object is to separate data based on their regions of den-
sity. For instance, DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with
Noise) [12] group samples based on their density within a specified distance thresh-
old. This approach identifies clusters as regions of high-density separated by regions
of low-density. This kind of method is advantageous when dealing with weird- or
arbitrary-shaped data. The overall density of a point is analysed to determine the
functions of datasets that influence a particular data point, hence this method pro-
vides a natural protection against outliers.
==PAGE:30==
8
1 Introduction
Model-based clustering: Optimises the fit between the given data and some pre-
defined mathematical model.
The assumption here is that data points are gener-
ated from a mixture of (underlying) probability distributions.
Examples include
Expectation-Maximisation (EM), Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) and Latent Dirich-
let Allocation (LDA). These methods estimate the parameters of the underlying dis-
tributions and assign data points to clusters based on their likelihood of belonging
to each distribution.
Hence, these methods lead to a way of automatically deter-
mining the number of clusters based on statistics, accounting for noise – resulting
in a robust way of clustering data. However, these models may be computationally
demanding, and their performance is sensitive to the quality of parameter estimation.
Network-based clustering: Examines the social relationships and interactions be-
tween users, utilising social network analysis techniques to identify communities or
clusters. Detecting communities (or clusters) of networks is an interesting problem
and is applied widely in various fields, especially on online discussion forums, such as
Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn, Google+, MySpace and Reddit [11–13]. This method
can in turn be used to create reports about community health, comparisons of sub-
groups, and identification of important individuals, as well as in applications that
rank, sort, compare, and search for content and experts [27].
Clustering based on content or behaviour have also been applied in addition to the
clustering methods described above. While content-based clustering focuses on the
textual content, that be analysing semantic similarity distance of users’ posts or pro-
files, behaviour-based clustering focuses on users’ engagement and actions, such as
posting frequency. However, when selecting the most appropriate method for cluster-
ing online forum users, it is important to consider the advantages and limitations of
the methods available.

Scope

2.1
Data Collection
Providing the grounds for analytics of users’ posts in social media that relate to a
company, here Unity Technologies, is achieved using the following steps:
• Data Ingestion: custom services that ingest the data from common social plat-
forms are orchestrated by Cloud Composer [30] and run periodically (e.g. daily).
• A number of natural language processing (NLP) models are applied on the
collected texts at post and thread level: sentiment analysis, topic modelling,
semantic similarity etc..
• Results of model inference, original posts, and other structural information

6.1
Introduction & Selecting Clustering Algorithm
6.1.1
Defining Clustering
We wish to find interesting groupings of the active users from our data derived in
Chapter 4. For this part of the analysis we will use the approach of clustering, an
unsupervised machine learning approach [26, p. 2]. In unsupervised machine learning
approaches, there are no predefined classes and no examples that hint to desirable
outcome – We simply observe the features.
Clustering is the process of organising (or partition) observations into groups, that
is clusters, such that observations assigned to a cluster are similar, and dissimilar to
observations assigned to different clusters [26, p. 459]. There are several definitions
of how to categorise different types of clustering methods. For instance [10] uses the
term crisp clustering for clustering algorithms where an observation can only belong
to one cluster, whereas [26] uses the term hard clustering. Conversely, [10] uses the
term fuzzy clustering for clustering methods where an observation can belong to mul-
tiple clusters, whereas [26] uses the term soft clustering. We will use the notation
from [26, p. 415] and refer to clustering methods as being hard or soft.
We naturally wish to evaluate a clustering algorithm after obtaining a partition from
the data. We also wish to compare the partitions across different choices of clustering
==PAGE:58==
36
6 Clustering: Methods & Results
algorithms. Hence a pressing question arises: How can one evaluate different cluster-
ings and determine the best result? The following section provides insights into how
we evaluate clustering techniques.
6.1.2
Evaluating Clustering Techniques
Clustering performance is diﬀicult to evaluate [23]. In supervised learning, model
performance is assessed by comparing model predictions to labelled targets with per-
formance metrics [26, p. 2]. In unsupervised learning, and in this case clustering,
we do not have labels or targets, and we (usually) do not know beforehand what
partitions of the data are best. Thus it is hard to ascertain whether one clustering
approach is better than another. Defining a good clustering criterion requires a good
understanding of the data, but clustering is also one of the principal tools used to
help understand the data [23]. Hence, a considerate amount of data exploration must
be conducted to define a good clustering criterion. Conversely to supervised learning,
where it makes sense to talk about ”the best model”, in clustering it does not make
sense to talk about ”the best clustering”. Recent studies suggest that no ”correct”
clustering exists [34], however, one can set up appropriate clustering criteria to eval-
uate the resulting partitions [23].
Several methods exist for validation or evaluation of clustering techniques, including
within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS), which evaluates the variability within clusters,
and Silhouette Score, which essentially evaluates the distance between clusters.
For the purpose of this work; finding subject matter experts, we naturally want the
groups of users (clusters) to be somewhat distinct from one another.
Ultimately,
we want clusters of similar users that are distinct to users of other clusters. Hence
we choose to evaluate our choice of clustering algorithm up against WCSS (see Sec-
tion 6.1.3) for within-cluster variability, and up against Silhouette score (see Sec-
tion 6.1.4) as a measure of cluster separation.

sum of squares. Clusters that have higher values exhibit greater variability of the

6.1.5
Selecting Clustering Algorithm
Many prevalent clustering algorithms exist, among them we choose just a few to
compare. We implement the clustering algorithms using Scikit-learn’s cluster mod-
ule [37]. The following list presents the clustering algorithms considered in this work:
• K-means: one of the most popular iterative descent clustering methods. Uses
optimisation of an objective function, usually minimising the Euclidean distance
between an observation and cluster centres [10, 26]. Hard, partitioning-based
clustering algorithm.
• Mini-batch k-means: a modified k-means algorithm using mini-batches to
reduce the computation time in large datasets.
In addition, this algorithm
attempts to optimise the result of the clustering.
To achieve this, the mini
batch k-means takes mini-batches as an input, which are subsets of the whole
dataset, randomly. The mini batch k-means is considered faster than k-means
and it is normally used for the large datasets [38, p 195]. Hard partitioning-
based clustering algorithm.
• Agglomerative Clustering: A hierarchical clustering algorithm, producing
clusters that can further be divided into new clusters (much like a dendogram).
Produces a sequence of clustering schemes of decreasing number of clusters at
east step. The clustering done at each step results from the previous one by
merging the two closest clusters into one [10], producing a grouping at the
next higher level with one less cluster at each step (bottom-up approach). The
pair chosen for merging consist of the two groups with the smallest intergroup
dissimilarity [26, p 472]. Hard hierarchical clustering algorithm.
==PAGE:60==
38

• Birch: Uses a hierarchical data structure called CF-tree for partitioning the
incoming data points. A CF-tree is a height-balanced tree, which stores the
clustering features and it is based on two parameters: Branching factor B and
threshold T, referring to a diameter (or radius) each cluster must be less than
[10]. Hard hierarchical clustering algorithm.
• Spectral Clustering: Constructs an aﬀinity matrix indicating the similarity
between two points i and j. It can be seen as a weighted graph representation of
the data. The eigenvectors and -values are then used for partitioning the data
according to a given criterion, the most popular criterion being the Laplacian
matrix [39]. In practice, Spectral Clustering is very useful when the structure
of the individual clusters is highly non-convex, such as when clusters are nested
circles on the 2D plane [22]. Hard network-based clustering algorithm.
• Gaussian Mixture: Representation of a Gaussian mixture model probability
distribution. Allows to estimate the parameters of a Gaussian mixture distribu-
tion. The k-means clustering procedure is closely related to the EM algorithm
for estimating a certain Gaussian mixture model [26, p 463]. Suppose we want
to find k clusters. Then we can specify k mixture components, each with a
Gaussian density with covariance σ2I. Then the relative density under each
component is a function of the Euclidean distance between the center of the
mixture and the data point. This method can be considered a soft version of
k-means clustering. Soft model-based clustering algorithm, probabilistic.
• Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF): A matrix approximation method
which factorizes a non-negative matrix into two non-negative matrices having
lower rank (dimension) than the original, with the goal of reconstructing the
original matrix. Widely used for image reconstruction and signal processing,
and NMF has also been used for clustering in different contexts [40]. Soft clus-
tering algorithm.
We compare different kinds of algorithms using the evaluation criteria WCSS (see
Section 6.1.3) and Silhouette Score (see Section 6.1.4). Figures 6.1 and 6.2 present

It is commonly argued that one should choose the clustering algorithm that best
fit ones needs [23]. Hence, we employ at frequently used evaluation criteria (see Sec-
tions 6.1.3 and 6.1.4) as well as analyse the resulting clusters themselves in search
for reasonable clusterings of the data used in this work. From the evaluation criteria
chosen, we elect to cluster our users with the k-means clustering algorithm.

6.3.1
Feature Selection, Scaling of Features & One-hot-encoding
We use the features derived from Tables 4.1 and 4.2 as input features into the k-means
clustering algorithm (excluding user ID).
As many of the features in the feature set have Poisson- or exponential-like dis-
tributions with heavy tails, to improve performance of the algorithm, we scale the
numerical feature space using Scikit-learn’s QuantileTransformer [37], scaling each
numerical feature to follow a uniform distribution. Quantile-scaling features reduces
the impact of (marginal) outliers, hence it is therefore a robust preprocessing scheme.
The transformation is applied on each feature independently. An example of Quantile-
scaling an exponential-like feature can be found in Figure 6.3.
As some of the features are categorical, we transform these features into numerical
features for the k-means clustering algorithm to work. We find that applying Scikit-
learn’s OneHotEncoder [37] to the most common forum and subforum increase the
clustering performance with regards to figuring out where the active users are creating
threads and posts – implying a user’s area of expertise. This procedure expands the
feature space immensely but also gives us important feedback from the results. Hence,
categorical features are One-Hot-Encoded.
6.3.2
Choosing the Amount of Clusters
We try various methods for choosing the amount of clusters. Table 6.1 presents the
methods used in this work, and the results of said methods. A detailed description
of the methods are provided in Appendix E. The results of these methods, although
being prevalent methods, are ambiguous, that is all methods have different proposals
as to how many clusters we should choose. An accepted practice when using these
methods, and when the method results are ambiguous, is to take the average value
of the results, which leaves us with (9 + 2 + 2 + 27 + 3 + 28)/6 = 11, 8(avg) clusters.
We choose to use this result as a proposal, and choose to derive 10 unique clusters.

7.1
Introduction to Network Analysis
Online discussion fora provide a rich source of user-generated content and interactions,
making them valuable platforms for studying community dynamics and information
exchange [11,13,19]. Analysing these forums can uncover underlying structures and
patterns, such as network communities or clusters of users with similar interests or
patterns. Network-based clustering, utilising social network analysis techniques, of-
fers a powerful approach to identify and understand these communities within the
context of online discussion forums.
Social network analysis focuses on studying the relationships and interactions between
individuals or entities within a network [13]. In this work, the network will consist of
active users who engage in discussions, create threads, reply to posts, and exchange
information on the Unity Forums. By representing these interactions as a network,
with users as nodes and their connections as edges, we can apply network analysis-

60

based methods to identify groups of users sharing common characteristics or exhibit
similar behaviour – often called network clustering or community detection [27,43].
Network-based clustering aims to partition the network into distinct communities
or clusters, where users within the same cluster are more closely connected to each
other than to users in other clusters – similar to the objective of clustering meth-
ods, described in Chapter 6. Various algorithms and techniques can be employed
for this purpose, including, but not limited to, modularity optimisation, hierarchi-
cal clustering, and community detection algorithms such as the Louvain method and
Girvan-Newman algorithm [11,13].
Before employing network-based clustering methods to find interesting communities,
we first define what a network is, and from this definition create the network we are

7.2
Defining the Network
Networks, or graphs, have a solid basis in classical graph theory. A graph G is rep-
resented as G(V, E), where V is a set of vertices (or nodes) and E is a set of edges
(or links) connecting some node pairs in V [13,44]. In this work, we consider a social
network of thread participation – defined by examining the interactions and relation-
ships between individuals based on their engagement in online discussion threads. In
the context of online communities like Unity Forums, we construct a social network
by considering users as nodes and their interactions as links connecting them. We
define and create our social network of thread participation as follows.
1. Identifying participants: We identify participants of the of the network by
considering users who actively engage in threads within Unity Forums (see Chap-
ter 4 and Appendix B.1). Each participant plays a role of a node within the
network.
2. Defining interactions: Participants’ interactions are determined based on
their engagement in threads. When a participant responds to a thread initiated
by another participant, we recognise this as an interaction. These interactions
are represented as links connecting the corresponding nodes.
3. Weighing interactions: We have the option to assign weights to links, repre-
senting the strength or intensity of interactions. In this work, we have chosen
to focus on the simplest version of the network of thread participation, omitting
weighing of interactions. However, future work can consider adding weights to
the interactions.
4. Constructing the network: Once the interactions (and their weights) are
determined, we construct the network by representing participants as nodes

and their interactions as links connecting them. Then various network analysis
techniques can be applied to analyse the structure, properties, and communities

Note that constructing a social network of thread participation may vary in method-

Future studies may apply different criteria to to define interactions and assign weights

7.4
Community Analysis
Community detection, or graph clustering, essentially consists of partitioning nodes
of a network into clusters that are more densely connected [43]. Community analysis
of social networks is widely employed, and can help researchers gain insights into the
social dynamics and user behaviour within a given network – in this case our social
network of thread participation from active users on the Unity Forums. Understand-
ing the underlying patterns of a network can help researchers formalise assumption
on community health, comparisons of subgroups, and identification of important in-
dividuals, as well as in applications that rank, sort, compare, and search for content
and experts [27]. In our case, community analysis can help us identify users in cer-
tain knowledge domains, taking us one step closer towards characterisation of subject
matter experts.
Various methods for uncovering communities exist, among them we apply mod-
ularity optimisation, graph partition, hierarchical clustering and Bayesian methods,
such as Nested Stochastic Block Model Inference [43,48].
7.4.1
Community Analysis Methods Applied
1. Modularity Optimisation: Modularity is a measure of the quality of net-
work division into communities.
Modularity optimisation algorithms aim to
maximise the modularity value by iteratively partitioning the network into sub-
groups that maximise the within-group connectivity and minimize the between-
group connectivity [13, p. 21]. We use greedy_modularity_communities, a
NetworkX implementation [49] that uses Clauset-Newman-Moore greedy mod-
ularity maximization to find the community partition with the largest modu-
larity [50]. Greedy modularity maximization begins with each node in its own
community and repeatedly joins the pair of communities that lead to the largest
modularity until no further increase in modularity is possible (a maximum).
2. Infomap: The Infomap algorithm is a popular method for community detec-
tion in social networks. Infomap is developed by Martin Rosvall and Carl T.
Bergstrom in 2007, and is based on the concept of optimizing the description
length of random walks on a network. The algorithm aims to find the most com-
pact and meaningful representation of a network’s community structure [51].
3. Graph-Tool’s Nested Stochastic Block Model Inference: The Stochastic
block model is a random graph model with planted clusters, and aims to identify
the large-scale structure of networks through statistical inference [43,48].
Essentially, this method defines a model that generates a network based on the
the Bayesian posterior probability of model parameters inferred from the data.
The model parameters control how the node partition affects the structure of
the network.

7.5
Assortativity Analysis of the Network
Given the results obtained from traditional methods of deriving communities, we
now explore an alternative approach to uncovering community structures in networks.
Community structures may also refer to groups of nodes that connect similarly to the
rest of the graph without having a higher inner density, such as disassortative com-
munities that have higher external connectivity, or assortative communities, where
nodes have a tendency to connect with other nodes of similar characteristics [43,53,54].
Assortativity with respect to a specific feature is the Pearson correlation between the
features of connected non-identical node pairs [53]. Per this definition the assortativ-
ity coeﬀicient is a number from -1 to 1. If the assortativity is positive, then connected
nodes tend to have positively correlated features. If assortativity is negative, the con-
nected node pairs tend to have negatively correlated features.
Said differently, if
assortativity is positive, similar nodes tend to interact, and if assortativity is nega-
tive, similar nodes tend to avoid each other.
We will now take a closer look into whether groups of nodes emerge based on as-
sortativity of node features. Hence, we add the user-level features we calculated in
Chapter 4 (see Table 6.2) to each node (user) in the network. Table 7.1 presents the
user-level features evaluated for the assortativity analysis conducted in this chapter.

For good measure, we will re-iterate how we calculate the user-level features in our

• In aggregating the statistics per user, we analyse a user from two perspectives:
From a thread-perspective, and from a post-perspective:
– Thread perspective: We only consider threads a user has created, and
then aggregate thread level statistics for said user. The aggregated thread
statistics are derived from threads a user has created, not threads a user

Hence, statistics from other users’ threads cannot

users’ posts cannot impact the post-level analysis on the user in question.
• Hence, a user’s feature values are already calculated before the network is cre-
ated (except network-specific metrics), and
• users cannot accidentally ”be drawn” into other users’ derived measures by
connecting with new nodes in the network.

pothesis test [56] making use of proof by contradiction [57].
A permutation test
involves two or more samples. The null hypothesis is that all samples come from the
same distribution H0 : F = G. Under the null hypothesis, the distribution of the
test statistic is obtained by calculating all possible values of the test statistic under

form of resampling [58].

CHAPTER 9
Discussion
In this study, we discovered that the most effective approach for identifying users
within expert knowledge domains in Unity Forums involves utilising k-means cluster-
ing in combination with assortativity analysis on a network of thread participation.
Our findings align with existing literature, which points towards k-means being the
preferred method for clustering tasks – a point we can now strengthen due to our
unprecedented access to a large-scale dataset. However, we acknowledge that the
selection process for these methods was not straightforward and thus deserves discus-
sion.
In this chapter, we first present the findings derived from our cluster analysis, which
reveal a distinct structure within the clusters of users. We address the challenges
encountered in applying clustering, discussing the process leading to us using k-means
clustering, clustering methods considered, evaluating clustering results, scaling of data
in preparation to clustering, centroid convergence, feature selection and improving
clustering performance.
Second, we outline our findings from our analysis on the network of thread partic-
ipation, which provides valuable insights into the dynamics of user interactions and
the formation of expert knowledge domains. We then discuss the challenges encoun-
tered in detecting communities and the following assortativity analysis to understand
the network structure.
Finally, we conclude the discussion with final remarks towards implications of this
work.
9.1
Clustering
The clustering analysis successfully partitioned the active users of Unity Forums into
unique groups, each group exhibiting unique characteristics. We observe that the
users are nicely divided based on their area of interest, pointing towards topics and
sub-categories in working with the products of Unity Technologies. For instance, one
cluster focuses on Assets and Asset Store, while other clusters show predominant inter-
est in scripting and working with the Unity Editor. This clear division demonstrates
the existence of different expert knowledge domains within the Unity Forums.
Moreover, our analysis uncovers some variability in the level of activity between
clusters. Some clusters exhibit high levels of engagement, actively contributing to or
initialising threads. Conversely, some clusters exhibit low levels of activity, implying
passive behaviour. The different level of engagement points to varying degrees of
==PAGE:116==
94
9 Discussion
involvement and contribution, which relates to knowledge sharing and collaboration
within certain domains.
We observe that users within specific clusters tend to concentrate their interac-
tions within certain forums or subforums, suggesting a preference for certain areas of
discussion. In addition to this, unique patterns emerge in the content of the discus-
sions, which is evident from frequent usage of certain keywords or tokens in specific
groups. This implies that users not only engage in certain knowledge domains (fo-
rums and subforums), but also engage in specialised conversations relating to those
domains.
With the above points, we acknowledge the limitations of our cluster analysis. The
results are dependent on choice of clustering algorithm, evaluating criteria, scaling of
data, and the choice of features considered. These points deserve discussion.
Discussing Clustering – Outline
In the following, we discuss the process leading to us utilising k-means clustering,
including clustering methods considered, evaluating clustering results, scaling of data
in preparation to clustering, centroid convergence, feature selection and improving
clustering performance.
Choice of Clustering Algorithm
In our quest of finding subject matter experts, clustering successfully partitioned the
active users on the Unity Forums into groups, each group exhibiting unique patterns
and characteristics. Multiple different approaches to clustering the active users were
employed, and k-means clustering was far from our first choice.
Due to NMF’s property of decomposing objects as an additive combination of
(often very meaningful) parts, and because this method has shown promising results
in earlier research on clustering behavioural data [60], we first employed NMF to our
dataset – in this context applied as a soft clustering method. However, we encountered
a lot of noise in the results of the clusters derived, observed through a lot of variability
within clusters as well as overlapping clusters. This became evident from pairwise
visualisations of cluster features and from visualising data in a 2D plane through
dimensionality reduction. Previous studies on clustering for topic classification in
online communities have also observed overlapping clusters with a lot of variability
within [12].
An interesting observation is that NMF not always returns the amount of clusters
specified when initialising the method, whereas k-means in our case always returned
the number of clusters specified. This may be due to NMF returning cluster loads
per user, which can be interpreted as a vector of probabilities of belonging to each
cluster (if normalised). To overcome this challenge, we allowed users to belong to
multiple clusters: A user was assigned the cluster with the highest load, and if loads
towards other clusters laid within a certain threshold of the first cluster assigned, the
user was considered belonging to all clusters within said threshold. We meddled with
==PAGE:117==
9.1 Clustering
95
various different thresholds, only to find that this procedure resulted in even more
overlapping clusters and higher variability within clusters found.
Evaluating Clustering
We evaluated several clustering algorithms up against WCSS and Silhouette Score to
understand why the clustering algorithms applied did not ”behave well”. We derived
the WCSS manually for all other methods than k-means, which uses an in-built
method: kmeans.inertia_. We tested our implementation of WCSS thoroughly up
against the k-means’ in-built function. Our discoveries included that the WCSS for
NMF diverged for an increasing amount of clusters, and that the Gaussian Mixture
Model (GMM) had similar behaviour, albeit not as significant. The reason for these
results also requires discussion.
Scaling of Features
Deriving meaningful clusters from the methods applied was challenging. This was due
to a lot of the input features having a Poisson- or exponential-like distributions with
heavy tails. This can be attributed to the nature of the dataset, thus affecting the
user-level features derived. To surpass this limitation, we explored several methods
for scaling the data, including standard-scaling and min-max-scaling, the results of
which being equivalent with the previous findings.
Log-scaling was applied to the data such that the clustering algorithms would
work in orders of magnitude. Surprisingly, NMF did not benefit from log-scaling the
features, prompting us to explore alternative methods of preprocessing our data in
order to get meaningful results from the clustering methods applied. We considered
removing outliers using the IQR method, which yielded slight improvements to our
previous results. However, we decided to revert, as we wanted to work with all the
available data.
When applying quantile scaling to our data with a uniform output distribution, we
observed significant improvements in the behaviour for all clustering considered. This
was explained by monotonically decreasing within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS) for
increasing amount of clusters. Moreover, a clear distinction in performance between
the methods emerged. These findings stress the importance of properly scaling data
during data preparation for clustering. We found that unsupervised learning were
sensitive to the relative scale of different features. Hence we suggest conducting an
in-depth data exploration, facilitating proper data preparation in terms of scaling for
employing various clustering methods.
Feature Selection
When performing feature selection, considering the relevance of each feature to the
specific clustering task is of great importance. We select features that are likely to
capture meaningful patterns based on our research objective: Finding subject matter

experts in the Unity Forum domain. Through a comprehensive data exploration on
the available data, we get to understand the domain and the characteristics therefrom.
This understanding has helped us guide the selection of features, ensuring that we
passed relevant information to the algorithm.
The features derived in this work are features that can be derived from almost
any online discussion forum. We have also taken into account relevant work on clus-
tering users on Twitter and Reddit – the main sources of data in current research on
clustering users on online forums [4,16].
Many different kinds of features have been applied in the context of clustering users
on online communities. For instance, recent studies have clustered users based on
semantic similarity of tweets. However, for topic classification and target recommen-
dations, late research have used features similar to ours and found that the algorithms
applied performed well in finding clusters with unique characteristics [12].
In this study, some of the features considered are derived from the same metric,
just with other goals in mind. For instance ”average post polarity” and ”max post
polarity” are derived from the same ”post_prediction_output”, however they imply
both average sentiment polarity as well as sentiment polarity volatility of a user. We
know that highly correlated features might skew the clustering process, but at the
same time we want relevant information to not get lost in the process.
Irrelevant features do not change the target concept learnt through machine learning,
and redundant features do not include anything new to the target concept [61]. How-
ever, the inclusion of irrelevant and redundant features may significantly increase the
computational cost of the learning process by increasing the dimensionality of the
feature set.
In classical terms, the dimensionality of a feature set is considered large if it is
in the range of hundreds. However, in recent applications of feature selection, the
dimensionality extend to tens or hundreds of thousands [62]. Such high dimensionality
presents a challenge known as the “curse of dimensionality” [26, p. 22]. To overcome
this issue, feature selection techniques have been developed to select relevant and
non-redundant features.
In this study, we operate within a relatively low-dimensional space compared to
newer applications. Hence, we did not encounter any computational limitations for
the clustering task. Moreover, the feature space was intentionally selected to facil-
itate the emergence of user groups based on these features. Future work may look
into whether feature selection methods can further improve performance.
We compare the clusters using a subset of the available feature space. It is important
to note that our analysis in this study, which focuses on a subset of the available
feature space, may not capture the entire spectrum of user behaviour within the Unity
Forum. Excluding certain features may impose limitations on the depth of insights
gained from the clusters. In order to gain a more comprehensive understanding of
the resulting clusters, future studies should explore including other dimensions.

Limitations of Clustering Method Selected
We choose our clustering algorithm based on the WCSS score and Silhouette Coeﬀi-
cient. Since the k-means algorithm’s optimisation criterion is, in essence, the WCSS
score, we may have introduced an unintentional bias towards the k-means algorithm.
Hence, future studies utilising k-means should exercise caution and consider different
evaluation criteria for comparing clustering algorithms. However, in conducting this
research, we not only evaluate the clustering algorithms based on WCSS, but also
explore the resulting clusters, confirming our selection of k-means as the preferred
method.
Trusting the Clusters
To ensure that we can trust the clusters derived from the k-means method, we im-
plement a strategy to validate centroid convergence. To accomplish this, we observe
100 different runs of k-means, each with a different random initialisation of centroids.
Firstly, because we use a fairly large feature set, we performed dimensionality reduc-
tion (UMAP) of both the feature set and the centroids created, and then updated the
centroid locations in each run of the algorithm (in the same embedding space). We
show, visually represented with a fixed embedding space, that the centroids of the
k-means converge to the same locations. An important note is that UMAP is sensi-
tive to initialisation, hence careful seeding is needed to achieve comparable results in
future studies.
To confirm our findings, we show centroid convergence numerically by running
two iterations of k-means, comparing labels assigned to the users in the first iteration
with labels assigned in the second iteration. The result aligns with our findings from
the UMAP representation, verifying centroid convergence.
Improving Performance
Improving the clustering results can be very useful in both the implications and appli-
cations of the findings. We developed and implemented a custom grid-search approach
to optimise parameters of the models considered in this work, and still found that
k-means performed the best with respect to our evaluation criteria. This brute-force
grid-search approach iterates through discrete ranges of values and hence cannot be
deemed exhaustive in the search of the best parameters, and it is very slow. However,
we narrowed down the range of parameters that lead towards better performance of
the algorithms considered.
Late research stress that is important to not overfit when aiming to improve perfor-
mance of clustering tasks [22]. The research suggest that adjusting parameters, or
selecting features, that leads to increased performance of clustering methods is a sign
of overfitting – leading to poor generalisation on unseen data.

9 Discussion
We use a substantial amount of features derived from users and their activity on
Unity Forums. Henceforth a pressing question arises when discussing ”the best clus-
tering” of a dataset: What composes the optimal outcome or performance? Research
on this particular matter argues that ”the best performance” of the clustering is ”the
best generalisation” of the features and methods selected [22].
Considering having to deliver a product to the Community Team in Unity Tech-
nologies, should our focus not be on achieving the clustering that best aligns with our
use case – the best division and hence most effective grouping of users? Is the best
result a generalised method to derive clusters on any forum of choice, or is the best
result in this case obtaining the best divided groups of users on the online community
at hand, facilitating action from the Community Team?
Existing research point towards selecting features to avoid overfitting on the spe-
cific dataset at hand [22], but other research also suggest obtaining the clustering that
best fits ones’ needs [23]. The features derived in this work are generalisable to other
online social networks (online communities), hence we strongly assume that future
studies can obtain similar results on other datasets. The exercise of generalising the
method derived in this work to other datasets will be left for future work.

Network Analysis
Network analysis provided valuable insights into the dynamics of user interactions
and the formation of expert knowledge domains. In this section, we address the chal-
lenges encountered in community detection, and discuss the following assortativity
analysis to understand the network structure.
Community Detection
Standard approaches to detecting communities, such as modularity optimisation, In-
fomap, and Nested Stochastic Block Model Inference, did not yield any useful results.
These findings suggest that the structure of our network does not agree with the
assumptions of these community detection algorithms. The complex nature of knowl-
edge sharing in online communities may be a reason for this.
In our network of thread participation, it is indeed possible for users (nodes) to
be a member of a dense community whilst connecting to external communities as
well. In this case, we learned that modularity optimisation will already show signs
of weakness. Future studies could look into how networks of different structures, for
instance a network of reciprocal connections, affect these approaches of community
detection.

Assortativity Analysis
To overcome the challenges above, and in effort to still find community structures
within our network, we employed assortativity analysis. This method allowed us to
gain insights into whether nodes have a tendency to connect with other nodes of
similar characteristics.
We observed a pattern of high assortativity across clusters, especially for thread
and post sentiment polarity as well as for common place of engagement. This ob-
servation implies that users tend to share similar sentiment as others when engaging
in discussions.
These results also build upon our observation from the clustering
discussion, emphasising that users tend to engage and contribute to certain knowl-
edge domains. Future studies could look into uncovering which users drive sentiment
and opinions in threads, essentially finding influencers of sentiment spread within the
forums.
The high assortativity on common place of engagement highlights that users
within expert knowledge domains tend to cluster together, aligning to the concept of
communities of practice [7]. Again, these results facilitate development of strategies
for targeted interactions, interventions, knowledge sharing and collaboration.
We recognise that the assortativity analysis conducted in this work has its lim-
itations. Our analysis relies on the assumption that users within the same cluster
are more likely to exhibit similar sentiment polarity and engage in common knowl-
edge domains. However, our analysis does not provide a detailed understanding of
the content and context of the discussions made. We plan to, and future studies
are encouraged to, explore a more granular approach to text analysis, such as topic
modelling or multi-document summarisation.
9.3
Concluding the Discussion & Implications
In conclusion, our network analysis of Unity Forums uncovered challenges in tradi-
tional community detection methods, making us turn towards assortativity analysis.
The high assortativity observed in sentiment polarity and common place of engage-
ment indicates the presence of distinct user groups within expert knowledge domains.
In combining the results from the assortativity analysis with the clustering results, we
gain a deeper and more comprehensive understanding of the user groups and network
structure on the Unity Forums.
The clustering analysis identified users within expert knowledge domains, and the
assortativity analysis revealed patterns of engagement. The structure found from the
cluster and network analysis conducted has several implications for user engagement
and knowledge sharing within Unity Forums – enabling community managers and
moderators to better understand the interests and expertise among active users.
Identifying and characterising users within expert knowledge domains facilitates
the development of strategies for motivating community members and for utilisation

Conclusion and Future Work
In this thesis, we use clustering and network analysis-based methods in search for
subject matter experts on the online discussion forum hosted by Unity Technologies:
Unity Forums.
We evaluate seven different clustering algorithms, and find that k-means performed
the best. Consequently, we use k-means to find users within expert knowledge do-
mains. We support the identified clusters by analysing a network of thread partici-
pation, and study the assortativity within the clusters found, which is the tendency
for nodes in networks to connect to other nodes or similar characteristics.
Our results indicate that a combination of clustering on features derived from an
online discussion forum and assortativity analysis on user interactions may be used
effectively for finding users within expert knowledge domains.
Merely identifying
knowledgeable users is not suﬀicient for finding subject matter experts within these
domains, however, it takes us a long way towards characterising the experts in the
knowledge domains found.
Through applying these methods on the data collected, we outline two key results.
Firstly, our results show that the user groups found from using k-means are highly
structured – divided up nicely based on level of activity, where the users engage, and
what they talk about. Second, in analysing a network of thread participation, we
find that users connect to other users of similar characteristics, especially on common
place of engagement.
We interpret the findings using features representing user behaviour and content
preferences, and create a poster holding all relevant cluster information to the reader
– demonstrating that the methods and procedures from this work can provide a rep-
resentative interpretation of the expert knowledge domain for a cluster.
Finally, we discuss our findings and limitations of the methods we employ, and provide
guidance for future work.
Future work
We plan several extensions to this work. First, we aim to obtain a clearer understand-
ing of the content discussed in the threads and posts collected. In this study, we
analysed the most used tokens and frequent words. While this method in reality is
quite simple, due to the sheer amount of data, we could not perform a more complex

text analysis, mainly attributed to hardware constraints. To overcome this challenge,
one can employ Apache Spark (pyspark) [63] – a unified engine for big data processing
utilising multi-core processing.
Recent developments in Large Language Models (LLMs) allow us to look towards
utilising text summarisation for topic modelling, facilitating even deeper insights into
the clusters derived and the content discussed. Already available LLMs allow for text
summarisation at scale, such as GPT-3 or GPT-4. This aim can also be accomplished
by fine-tuning an LLM using the recent integration to Huggingface Transformers [64].
Second, k-means clustering and network analysis can be applied to any social media.
With the above improved method of topic modelling combined with multi-document
text summarisation, next iterations of this work will look outwards to other online
communities containing threads and posts pertaining to Unity Technologies, that is
Reddit, Twitter, YouTube, and others if relevant.
Finally, the work conducted in this thesis uncovered users within expert knowledge
domains. However, for finding subject matter experts within these domains, merely
identifying knowledgeable users is not suﬀicient. In extension to this work, we plan
to add analytics on product usage and behaviour for the groups of users found. By
combining the results of this work with inferring level of expertise from product
analytics, we may have suﬀicient granularity to nail down subject matter experts
within the expert knowledge domains found.

B.1
Active Users: Definition and filtering
We discussed with the Community Team from Unity Technologies what defines an
active user on the Unity Forum. We, together with the team, looked at which features
from the dataset to constrain, with the goal of 1) ending up with users from the dataset
that would be interesting in this work to analyse, and 2) providing the Community
Team with grounds for action on their behalf (what actions they can take is explained
in the main text)..
Ultimately, we want to analyse users having experience on the Unity Forum, that
today still engage in discussions, that is users that today still create threads and
posts (we compare with the last activity date from the dataset). Figures B.2 and B.3
present histograms of days since last thread creation (per user), before and after
imposing a constraint of users having to have created more than 40 threads in total,
respectively. Figures B.4 and B.5 present the same as above, but for users creating
posts. The figures demonstrate that when a constraint of above 40 threads or posts
created is imposed, the distribution of last activity shifts towards users being more
recently active.
It should be noted that the number of 40 threads and posts is selected from a
background of experience working with this data as well as from expert knowledge
provided by the Community Team. For determining the best possible constraints
to impose, a sensitivity analysis can be applied; determining how target variables
are affected based on changes in input variables. However, on the grounds that this
thesis is not about selecting input variables, a sensitivity analysis is outside the scope
of this work and will be left for future work. Another point that must be made is
that the accumulated amount of users having created above 40 threads or above 40
posts is monotonically increasing, implying a growing userbase. This can be seen in
Figures B.6 and B.7.

E.1
Choosing the amount of clusters
We follow a boilerplate from Towards Data Science to derive the optimal number
of clusters for the applied k-means clustering algorithm [65]. This includes the Gap
Statistic, Elbow Method, Silhouette Score, Calinski-Harabasz Index, Davies-Bouldin
Index, a Dendogram and the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). Further informa-
tion about these methods and research papers backing the methods can be found in
the following sections.
E.1.1
Gap Statistic
The gap statistic was developed by Stanford researchers Robert Tibshirani, Guenther
Walther and Trevor Hastie [66]. The idea behind this approach is to compare com-
pactness of a cluster to a distribution with no obvious clustering – with the farthest
value from this null-distribution being the optimal number of clusters. Figure E.1
presents the Gap Statistic for multiple amount of clusters (K):

E.1.6
Dendogram
This technique is specific to the agglomerative hierarchical method of clustering, also
described in Section 1.3. The agglomerative hierarchical method of clustering starts
by considering each point as a separate cluster and starts joining points to clusters
in a hierarchical fashion based on their distances.
To get the optimal number of
clusters for hierarchical clustering, we can use a dendrogram which is tree-like chart,
showing the sequences of merges or splits of clusters. If two clusters are merged, the
dendrogram will join them in a graph and the height of the join will be the distance
between those clusters. We plot the dendogram using an in-built function from the
Scipy python library. We will plot the graph using the dendogram function from
