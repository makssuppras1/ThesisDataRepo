Abstract
Lead-free piezoelectric ceramics have received a great deal of interest in recent
years due to regulatory pressure from the European Union. Among the materials
currently being explored, K0.5Na0.5NbO3 (KNN) is one of the leading candidates
to replace lead-based devices due to the discovery of high piezoelectric responses
in doped versions of these materials. The aim of this work was to use templated
grain growth techniques to create textured microstructures in robocast ceramic
materials.
A ﬁnite element analysis model was developed to explore the eﬀects of texturing
on piezoelectric behavior. The model predicted an increase in the piezoelectric
response as a result of microstructural orientation in unpoled KNbO3.
The
experimental portion of this work aimed to replicate the microstructure which
was visualized in the model by synthesizing high aspect ratio K2Nb4O11 template
particles and converting them into KNN via molten salt synthesis reactions.
Although KNN was successfully synthesized, attempts to replicate the synthesis
of K2Nb4O11 ultimately failed and instead produced large crystals of KNb3O8
with smaller KNbO3 crystals interspersed.
An ink system was developed for robocasting multi-layered KNN ceramics. Inks
with 30vol.% solid loading and a solvent consisting of sodium carboxymethyl-
cellulose, glycerol, and water were successfully extruded from a 410µm nozzle.
The dispersion of template particles into the ink caused clogging and a wider
nozzle with a 580µm diameter was used to print templated structures. 1.0cm x
0.6cm x 0.25cm rectangular prisms were printed and sintered at either 1100◦C
or 1130◦C.
The piezoelectric properties of KNN samples without templates, with potassium
==PAGE:4==
ii
niobate templates, and with KNN templates were tested both with and without
the presence of an electric ﬁeld. The templates were not able to produce textured
microstructures in any of the samples. Leakage currents were present in all of
the samples but were particularly severe in samples that were sintered at 1130◦C
and those where potassium niobate phases were present.
Based on the results, optimization of the synthesis parameters for producing
K2Nb4O11 and for the sintering of KNN ceramics is recommended.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Piezoelectric materials are critical components in many devices which form an
integral part of our technological environment.
They can be found in such
varied applications as sensors in sonograms and microphones; pickups for record
players and guitars; focusing rings for optical lens stacks in mobile phones;
ignition sources for gas ranges and cigarette lighters; strain gauges; actuators
for speakers and inkjet printers; linear motors for scanning electron microscopes;
and ultrasonic transducers, among many others.
The piezoelectric eﬀect was discovered in 1880, but it was not until World War I
and the development of sonar that piezoelectric materials were used in a major
application. The piezoelectric response is fairly weak in most natural materi-
als however, and it took until the development of synthetic materials with high
piezoelectric responses for the use of piezoelectric materials to become practical.
In the 1950s, it was discovered that solid solutions of lead titanate (PbTiO3)
and lead zirconate (PbZrO3) form materials with excellent piezoelectric proper-
ties due to the ease with which their ferroelectric domains can be oriented [1].
This class of materials is known as PZT and formed the basis for much of the
development of piezoelectric devices.
One of the reasons that PZT has such a high piezoelectric response is that if
the ratio between PbTiO3 and PbZrO3 is close to 1, both rhombohedral and
tetragonal crystals have similar free energies and can exist stably. The bound-

ary between these two composition dependent crystal structures is known as the
morphotropic phase boundary (MPB). Grains with a tetragonal crystal struc-
ture can be oriented at 90◦or 180◦relative to each other, whereas rhombohedral
grains can be oriented at 71◦or 109◦. When both of these phases coexist it per-

eﬃciency of the poling process [2]. Additionally, the grain structure of PZT
becomes extremely ﬁne near the MPB with domain widths on the order of tens
of nanometers. The movement of the ferroelectric domain walls in these grains
is signiﬁcant and may account for up to 70% of the piezoelectric response of
PZT materials [3].
Despite possessing some of the highest known piezoelectric responses, PZT ce-
ramics have come under scrutiny in recent years due to environmental concerns
regarding lead toxicity and pollution. Lead oxide (PbO) is a typical precursor
material used in the fabrication of PZT. PbO has a high vapor pressure at el-
evated temperatures and volatilizes during calcination and sintering processes,
resulting in a signiﬁcant source of lead pollution. Additionally, PZT was found
to be unstable in aqueous environments and can leach into soil and water if it
is not properly disposed of [1].
Due to these ecological concerns the European Union has passed legislation
restricting the use of lead in piezoelectric devices. As of today, no alternative
formulations with random poly-crystalline microstructures have been developed,
however it has been shown that the piezoelectric response can be increased by
orienting grains so that the poling eﬃciency is increased. This process is known

as texturing and is currently being explored as a means to produce viable lead-
free alternatives to PZT [4]. In order to produce texture in a ceramic material a
process known as templated grain growth (TGG) can be used. In TGG large sin-
gle crystal particles can be used to convert a poly-crystalline matrix into a single
crystal with the same orientation. If the template particles are oriented during
processing and prior to sintering, dense ceramics with oriented microstructures
can be produced and in some cases the resulting piezoelectric properties can be
more than doubled [5].

The development of lead-free compositions has mostly focused around replicat-
ing the morphotropic phase boundary conditions found in PZT, as the presence
of diﬀerent crystal structures facilitates the rotation of the polarization vector
during poling. Barium titanate doped with calcium and zirconium is one such
material, as the doping ions create a tricritical point where cubic, orthorhom-
bic, tetragonal, and rhombohedral phases can coexist. Sodium bismuth titante
(NBT) has also been studied due to the similar electronic structure of bismuth
and lead as well as its complex phase diagrams.
Potassium sodium niobate
(KNN) is another lead-free piezoelectric material with polymorphic phase tran-
sitions and good piezoelectric properties [6].

1.1
Motivation and Objectives
This demand for more environmentally friendly piezoelectric ceramics has re-
newed interest in alkali niobates. Much research has been done on improving
the performance of these materials in order to create a viable alternative to
lead-based systems. Doping, domain engineering, and texturing have all shown
the possibility of producing marked increases in the piezoelectric performance
of alkali niobates. This work is focused on the use of a particular alkali niobate:
K0.5Na0.5NbO3 (KNN).
There are several reasons behind the choice of KNN as the target material. KNN
can be produced from a range of diﬀerent potassium niobates, providing a variety
of diﬀerent template morphologies. In particular, K2Nb4O11 forms crystals with
well-deﬁned needle shapes which can be converted to KNN and then easily
oriented during processing due to their high degree of anisotropy. KNN also
has a high piezoelectric coeﬃcients for a lead-free material; d33 = 160pC/N,
and optimized versions can have a d33 value of up to 410 pC/N (compared to
500-600 pC/N for PZT and < 200 pC/N for typical lead-free piezoelectrics) [7].
Its also has a high Curie point (above 400◦C) and electromechanical coupling
coeﬃcient kp = 0.45 [8].
This thesis aims to build upon established work by combining the relatively well
understood process of texturing with the comparatively new technology of robo-
casting in order to produce dense KNN ceramics with oriented microstructures.
The goal is to determine whether an improvement in piezoelectric properties
can be realized and if so measure the magnitude of the improvement. In order
to achieve this goal, the project has been structured in the following manner:
• Synthesize phase pure anisometric K2Nb4O11 templates and convert to
K0.5Na0.5NbO3
• Evaluate robocasting ink systems, select a candidate for printing KNN,
and optimize the selected ink such that stable 3D structures can be printed
• Develop a procedure for sintering oriented KNN samples into dense ce-
ramics
• Print 3D structures and determine the degree of orientation of the template
particles
• Test the piezoelectric properties of robocast oriented and unoriented KNN
ceramics to determine the eﬀect of orientation

Chapter 2
Background
The production of piezoelectric devices involves complex processes, many of
which span multiple disciplines and ﬁelds of study. The same can be said re-
garding the understanding of atomic relationships in these materials which give
rise to phenomena such as piezoelectricity.
In order to eﬀectively create and manipulate piezoelectric ceramics into func-
tional materials, it is important to understand the theories underpinning their
behavior. The following section will present some of the basic theory of piezo-
electric ceramics; from their structures, to behavior, and processing.

6
Background
2.1
Dielectrics, Ferroelectricity, and Piezoelectricty
The piezoelectric eﬀect is a phenomenon that occurs in certain materials in
which a voltage is generated that is proportional to an applied mechanical stress.
The converse also occurs in these materials; when placed in the presence of an
external electrical ﬁeld a mechanical strain is produced. This behavior can be
observed across a wide range of diﬀerent materials, including certain crystals,
ceramics, polymers, and even biological materials such as wood and bone.
Figure 2.1: The permanent strain on a piezoelectric material after poling and
an example of the inverse piezoelectric eﬀect (top). The direct
piezoelectric eﬀect (bottom) [9].
The eﬀect was ﬁrst discovered by Jacques and Pierre Curie in 1880, when the
two brothers reported to the French Society of Mineralogy that an electric polar-
ization was observed upon the compression of certain asymmetric crystals along
their hemihedral axis. The inverse eﬀect was predicted to exist the following
year by Gabriel Lippmann, who used a thermodynamic approach to demon-
strate that in order for conservation of energy and charge to be maintained the
process should be reversible. The Curie brothers were able to experimentally
conﬁrm Lippmann’s hypothesis just one year later [10].
Today, 140 years after the discovery of this phenomenon, piezoelectric devices
are found in a wide array of diﬀerent applications, including sonar, accelerom-
eters, transducers, vibration control, frequency control, sensors, actuators, and
ignition systems [9]. Many of these devices perform critical functions in our
==PAGE:19==
2.1 Dielectrics, Ferroelectricity, and Piezoelectricty
7
technological environment; therefore it is important to understand the underly-
ing causes of the piezoelectric eﬀect in order to improve the performance of these
devices. Oftentimes the functional material is a piezoelectric ceramic, due to
their high piezoelectric response. Historically, the development of piezoelectric
ceramics involved three major steps: the discovery of high dielectric constants,
linking of the dielectric behavior to ferroelectricity, and the discovery of the
poling process [11].
2.1.1
Dielectric Eﬀect
Ceramics are typically non-conducting (with some signiﬁcant exceptions) due
to the lack of loosely bound or free electrons. When insulating materials are
placed in an electrical ﬁeld little to no current can ﬂow, and instead an electrical
polarization occurs as the positive charges within the material are displaced
in the direction of the electric ﬁeld and the negative charges are displaced in
the opposite direction. This phenomenon is called the dielectric eﬀect. The
movement of these centers of charge creates an internal electrical ﬁeld which
reduces the overall ﬁeld strength within the dielectric [12]. There are several
diﬀerent polarization mechanisms:
• Electronic - Results from the displacement of the center of the negatively
charged electron cloud relative to the center of the positively charged nu-
cleus. This occurs to an extent in all atoms.
• Ionic - Occurs only in ionic materials, where an electrical ﬁeld creates a
dipole by displacing cations and anions in opposite directions.
• Orientation - Occurs only in materials with permanent dipole moments
within their structures.
Polarization therefore results from rotation of
these moments in order to align with the applied ﬁeld.
• Interfacial - Results from the polarization of the space charge at heteroge-
nous areas such as grain boundaries. This is the least signiﬁcant source.
Some of these contributions may be absent or negligible depending on the struc-
ture of the material. The total polarization that the material experiences is a
summation of the contribution from all of these eﬀects [9]. How quickly each
of these polarization mechanisms can be activated is given by their relaxation
time. Electronic polarization is extremely fast, ionic polarization is slower, while
orientation and interfacial polarization are relatively slow as they involve coop-
erative movements between charge domains. If a dielectric material is placed in
an alternating ﬁeld, only the polarization mechanisms which have a relaxation
time which is faster than the ﬁeld frequency will be activated [12].

2.1.2
Piezoelectricity
The piezoelectric eﬀect is a behavior which can be observed in certain dielectric
materials that emerges from the arrangement of their crystal structure.
In
crystals which lack a center of symmetry, the application of a stress ﬁeld can
displace the ions from their equilibrium positions [13] as the atoms move in order
to minimize the crystal energy at the given temperature and pressure, resulting
in the formation of an electric dipole [14]. The dipole thus forms as a result of
the diﬀerence in the location of the center of the positive and negative charges
in the crystal’s unit cell, and the strength of the dipole is proportional to the
displacement between the two centers.
Figure 2.3: Arrangement of oxygen and silicon atoms in unstrained α-quartz
[2].
==PAGE:21==
2.1 Dielectrics, Ferroelectricity, and Piezoelectricty

Figure 2.4: Strain in the crystal lattice displaces atoms from their equilibrium
positions resulting in the formation of a dipole in what is known
as the direct piezoelectric eﬀect (left). In the reverse piezoelec-
tric eﬀect an external ﬁeld displaces atoms from their equilibrium
positions resulting in a strain in the material (right) [2].
Asymmetry in the crystal lattice is a necessary prerequisite for the formation of
a stress-induced dipole. Therefore, the piezoelectric eﬀect is limited to 20 of the
32 crystal classes, including all classes which lack a center of symmetry except
for the 432 crystal class where equal and opposite charges are produced along
the [1,1,1] crystallograpic axes that cancel each other out. The magnitude of
the charge that forms on the faces of piezoelectric materials is equal to the net
sum of all the individual dipole moments.
In an ideal material, all dipoles are aligned and so the measured surface charge is
a simple summation of the contributions from each unit cell. However, in most
real materials crystallographic domains possess a random orientation, resulting
in no net charge as the summation of the dipole moments in any direction is ap-
proximately zero. In order for poly-crystalline ceramics to exhibit piezoelectric
behavior at macroscopic scales crystalline domains must be arranged in such
that they possess a net orientation in their dipole moments [2].
2.1.3
Ferroelectricity
Harnessing the piezoelectric eﬀect at bulk scales requires the ability to impart a
net orientation in the dipole moments of a material. Certain materials possess a
permanent electric moment even in the absence of an external electric ﬁeld. In
these materials, the local electric ﬁeld caused by ionic displacement is greater
than the elastic force of the lattice, resulting in an asymmetrical shift in the
positions of the ions which creates a permanent dipole [15]. The ionic shift can
manifest itself in one of two or more energetically degenerate states and it is
==PAGE:22==
10
Background
possible to break this degeneracy and favor one state through the application of
an external electrical ﬁeld. The lower energy state is preferred and the direction
of the ionic displacement will switch to the preferred state if the applied ﬁeld
is strong enough to overcome local restrictions. The ability for a material to
switch the direction of its spontaneous polarization vector and maintain the
changed state after the application of a suitably strong electrical ﬁeld is known
as ferroelectricity [16].
Most ferroelectric materials undergo a temperature induced phase transition
from a low-temperature ferroelectric phase into a high-temperature paraelectric
phase. The symmetry of the ferroelectric phase is always lower than that of
the paraelectric phase as the ferroelectric structure is typically a slightly dis-
torted version of a non-polar structure. The temperature at which this phase
transformation occurs is called the Curie point, TC. Above this temperature,
the crystal unit cell becomes centrosymmetric and the spontaneous polariza-
tion vector disappears, resulting in a loss of piezo- and ferroelectric properties
[12][17]. Ferroelectrics are a sub-set of piezoelectric materials; a material may
be piezoelectric without being ferroelectric. For example, quartz is piezoelectric
but not ferroelectric whereas barium titanate is both [15].
Prior to 1940, only two types of ferroelectric materials had been discovered;
sodium potassium tartrate tetrahydrate (Rochelle salt) and potassium dihydro-
gen phosphate. The next breakthrough occurred when two researchers at the
American Lava Co. were testing barium titanate compositions at the laboratory
of one of their customers. At the time the material with the highest known di-
electric constant was titanium dioxide, with a value of around 100. The barium
titanate samples were found to have dielectric constants as high as 1100, spark-
ing signiﬁcant research eﬀorts in the discovery and development of ferroelectric
ceramic materials [11].
The high dielectric constants found in ferroelectric materials are a direct result
of their spontaneous polarization. By virtue of having a permanent electric mo-
ment that is inherently tied to their crystal structure, their electric susceptibility
is high since these dipoles have a tendency to align themselves in the direction
of the ﬁeld. Electric susceptibility measures the ability for a material to po-
larize in response to an applied ﬁeld. As the dielectric constant of a material
is directly related to its electric susceptibility, materials with highly orientable
charged domains will also display a strong dielectric response.
Ferroelectric ceramics can be divided into two major groups based on the mech-

The second group of ferroelectric materials obtains its polarization through the

the ordering of protons in a hydrogen bond or the ordering of radical species

the formation of dipoles due to the deformation of structural elements in the

2.1.4
Poling
As discussed in the previous section, when a ferroelectric material is cooled
down through the Curie point its crystal structure becomes distorted and non-
centrosymmetric resulting in the formation of electric dipoles. The dipoles are
locally aligned in regions with uniform polarization which are known as Weiss
domains. These domains have boundaries between each other and are the result
of stresses arising from the geometric distortion that accompanies the phase
transition from paraelectric to ferroelectric, uncompensated surface charges, and
crystallographic defects. Crystals that have uniform alignment throughout their
bulk are rare and as such, most ferroelectric materials are initially isotropic and
do not show macroscopic polarization [2].
Figure 2.6: Representation of Weiss domains in an un-poled material (left),
during poling (middle), and after poling (right) [2].
In order to create useful ferroelectric materials a process known as poling is
employed. During poling, the domains in a ferroelectric material can be aligned
in any direction through exposure to a suﬃciently strong electric ﬁeld at a
temperature just below the Curie point. Under these conditions domains which
are aligned with the ﬁeld will grow at the expense of any unaligned domains.
==PAGE:25==

13
When the ﬁeld is removed from the material, some domains will return to their
original orientation due to intergranular stresses. The remaining domains will
maintain the imposed orientation resulting in a net polarization and permanent
deformation of the material.
Figure 2.7: Hysteresis loop for the polarization of a PZT ceramic [2].
Once a material has been poled its properties become anisotropic. Additionally,
if the material is then exposed to a volatage with the same polarity as the pol-
ing voltage, its polarization will increase and it will experience a strain in the
direction of the poling axis and a contraction in the perpendicular directions.
The maximum polarization that the material can sustain is known as the sat-
uration polarization, Ps. When the ﬁeld is removed the material will maintain
some level of polarization known as the remnant polarization Pr. If the material
is placed in a strong enough ﬁeld of the opposite polarity, the direction of the
polarization can be ﬂipped. The ﬁeld strength required to induce a change in
the polarization orientation is known as the coercive ﬁeld, Ec [9]. The strength
of the coercive ﬁeld is inversely proportional to temperature which is why poling
is typically performed at high temperatures which are near the Curie point [2].
This relationship also holds for the direct piezoelectric eﬀect. If a compressive
or tensile force is applied in the poling direction, a voltage will be generated
between the surfaces perpendicular to the poling axis. Changing the force from
tensile to compressive or changing the force vector from parallel to perpendicular
relative to the poling axis will ﬂip the polarity of the generated voltage [9].

2.3
Sodium Potassium Niobate
Among lead-free piezoelectric ceramics, sodium potassium niobates have been
the focus of many research eﬀorts since the late 1990s due to their non-toxic na-
ture and relatively high ferroelectric properties when compared to other lead-free
materials. They were initially studied in the 1950s but interest in these mate-
rials surged after the discovery of an enhanced piezoelectric response in doped
versions of these ceramics. Within this family, K0.5Na0.5NbO3 (KNN) is the
most commonly used formulation. It is a solid solution of ferroelectric KNbO3
and anti-ferroelectric NaNbO3 with a 1:1 stoichiometric ratio between Na and
K [8]. KNN’s utility is due to its relatively high Curie point of around 400◦C
[24], good piezoelectric properties (d33 ≈160 pC/N), a high electromechanical
coupling coeﬃcient k = 0.45 [8], good temperature stability of its piezoelectric
properties, fatigue resistance, biocompaitibility, and compatibility with base
metal electrodes [26].
The performance of KNN can be additionally improved through doping. The
introduction of LiTaO3 and LiSbO3 creates a pseudo-ternary system with d33
values of around 300 pC/N [27], higher than common PZT formulations such as
PZT-1 (200 pC/N) and PZT-4 (225 pC/N); comparable to higher performing
PZT-5A compositions (250 pC/N) [28]. LiTaO3 has a pseudo-ilmenite structure,
and its presence creates lattice distortions in KNN as well as a new morphotropic
phase boundary between the ilmenite and perovksite structures which increases
the poling eﬃciency. Further improvements in the piezoelectric response can
be made through texturing; d33 values as high as 416 pC/N were recorded in
samples with a Lotgering factor of 0.9 [27], short of the performance of the
highest performing PZT-5H (585 pC/N) [28] ceramics but still incredibly high
for a lead-free piezoelectric.
Despite these promising results, KNN is scarcely used in industrial applications
due to the diﬃculty of processing these materials. KNN is notoriosly diﬃcult to

2.3.2
Synthesis
KNN can be directly synthesized from a mixture of sodium carbonate, potas-
sium carbonate, and niobium oxide powders in a solid state reaction. However,
compounds produced through solid state reactions can have low phase purity
due to poor diﬀusivity of the reactants and their volatilization at high temper-
atures. These problems can be overcome through the use of a lliquid ﬂux in a
technique known as molten salt synthesis (MSS).
2.3.2.1
Molten Salt Synthesis
In many high temperature reactive processes small amounts of low melting salts
are added to the powdered reactants in order to increase the reaction rates. For
a molten salt synthesis reaction, the amount of salt is increased such that it
forms a liquid solvent upon heating. This technique is often used to synthesize
complex oxide ceramics.
The use of a molten solvent is beneﬁcial for a number of reasons. The presence
of a liquid phase results in signiﬁcantly higher reaction rates by increasing the
mobility of reactive species and the overall reactive contact area by allowing
for dissolution, mass transport, and the precipitation of the product phase to
occur. For example, in a solid state reaction material transport occurs on the
order of 10−18cm2s−1 compared to rates of between 10−5 and 10−8cm2s−1 in
an MSS reaction. The vapor pressure of the alkali oxides is also signiﬁcantly
reduced, preventing an unwanted mass loss of the reactants and a subsequent
deviation from the desired stoichiometry, and it is easier to control the product
particle size and shape. For these reasons products produced via MSS tend to
have higher homogeneity and phase purity than those produced via solid state
reactions [32].
Salts are chosen such that they have a low melting point and do not react
with either the products or reactants.
Typically the amount of salt used is
between 80-120% of the weight of the reactants, such that there is enough salt
to ﬁll the interstitial spaces between the reactant particles and coat the reactive
surfaces but not enough to create a large enough liquid phase for sedimentation
to occur. The formation of the products occurs in two phases; reaction and
particle growth. A solid state reaction can begin below the melting point of
the salt and produce product or intermediate phases on the reactant particle
surfaces. Once the salt melts however the reaction rates are greatly increased.
The nucleation of product phases is similar to what is observed when growing

single crystals from solution. The crystal structure of the products determines
which facets are stable and thus the growth rate of each of the crystallographic
faces depends on the chemical composition. The morphology of the crystals

salt, and the reaction time and temperature as these will all aﬀect the growth
rates of each of the crystallographic faces and the ﬁnal particle shape.
The
nucleated product particles grow through an Ostwald ripening process until all

2.3.3
Sintering
Sintering is a process in which powder compacts are consolidated into dense
polycrystalline materials through prolonged exposure to elevated temperatures
below their melting point. The surface area of the powder is high relative to
its volume and so the driving force behind densiﬁcation is the reduction of
surface free energy.
Sintering is a mass transport phenomenon with various
diﬀerent components that can be divided into either surface or volume diﬀu-
sion mechanisms. Surface transport mechanisms such as surface diﬀusion and
evaporation-condensation contribute to particle growth without producing an
overall shrinkage, also known as coarsening. Bulk transport mechanisms such as
lattice diﬀusion, grain boundary diﬀusion, and viscous ﬂow reduce intra-particle
porosity and lead to a densiﬁcation of the material.
Coarsening and densiﬁcation mechanisms are in competition with one another
as they both result in a reduction of surface free energy. The total change in
surface free energy is given by the equation:
∆(γA) = (∆γ)A + γ(∆A)
(2.15)
where γ is the interfacial energy density and A is the interfacial area [40]. The
ﬁrst term describes densiﬁcation mechanisms as they promote a reduction in
surface energy without decreasing surface area whereas coarsening mechanisms
have the converse eﬀect (see Figure 2.18).
Figure 2.18: Simpliﬁed diagram of phenomena which can occur during sinter-
ing [40].
==PAGE:42==
30
Background
Material transport through diﬀusion processes occur through defect interactions.
Thus the activation energy is lowest for diﬀusion along the surface of a particle or
across/along grain boundaries as these regions are rife with lattice mismatches
and disorder. Diﬀusion in the bulk of a particle can occur through either vacancy
or interstitial lattice point defects. These require a higher activation energy and
thus will only become signiﬁcant at higher temperatures. The contribution of
each transport mechanism is time dependent however as the fraction of free
surfaces and grain boundaries relative to the bulk volume evolves during the
sintering process [26].
Figure 2.19: Material transport paths during sintering [40].
Diﬀerences in bulk pressure, vacancy concentrations, and vapor pressure induce
material transport between adjacent particles. In the early stages of a sintering
process, diﬀusion occurs across the contact area between adjacent particles cre-
ating a neck which bridges the two particles. Material ﬂows from the surfaces
of the particles only lead to an increase in the neck size and do not decrease the
interparticle distance. Only mass transport mechanisms which result in viscous
ﬂow or material transport along the boundary between the two particles result
in densiﬁcation. [40].
This process becomes further complicated in complex ceramics where diﬀerent
ions can diﬀuse at diﬀerent rates, resulting in local variations in stoichiometry.
Additionally, mass transport can occur via evaporation-condensation where voli-
talized species deposit in the bottom of the neck region due to the lower vapor
pressure in regions of concave curvatures compared to those which are convex
which increases exponentially with increasing particle curvature [26][40].
A sintering process can be divided into three distinct stages that often occur
simultaneously. In the ﬁrst stage diﬀusion occurs between adjacent particles
==PAGE:43==
2.3 Sodium Potassium Niobate
31
and necks form resulting in a porous interconnected particle network with a
low relative density of 60-70%. Densiﬁcation mechanisms are activated in the
intermediate stage, resulting in shrinkage and relative densities above 90% with
closed porosity as the channels between pores are closed oﬀ. The ﬁnal stage is
characterized by grain growth, and the pores can either shrink and disappear
or coalesce and grow.
Once grains are in close contact with one another grain growth occurs through
atomic diﬀusion across grain boundaries, driven by diﬀerences in free energy
across the grain boundary. Grains with more than six sides will have concave
edges, while those with fewer than six sides will have convex edges. Convex edges
have higher surface energy than concave edges, prompting atomic migration
from the former to the latter. This results in the grain boundary edges moving
towards their centers of curvature and the growth of large grains with more than
six sides at the expense of smaller grains.
If the grain growth process results in a continuous increase in the average size
of grains without aﬀecting the grain size distribution, it is described as normal.
However, in some materials a small percentage of grains can grow extremely
quickly relative to the majority of grains resulting in a bimodal grain size dis-
tribution. The fast movement of grain boundaries results in pores becoming
trapped within the grains themselves, impeding the ﬂow of material into the
pores and terminating the densiﬁcation process. This typically occurs if the
particle size distribution is non-normal to begin with, grain boundary ener-
gies are anisotropic, dopants or impurities segregate to the grain boundary, the
formation of second-phase particles occurs, or if a liquid phase forms during
sintering [26].

Sintering of KNN
Producing dense and phase-pure KNN ceramics is particularly diﬃcult due to

The high temperature and low vapor pressure of alkali compounds
results in volitalization of the alkali elements at temperatures above 1100◦C
as well as partial melting and deformation of the cubical grains. Additionally,
the evaporation rates of sodium and potassium can vary signiﬁcantly. Sintering
studies of KNN at 1100◦C for 24 hours showed the formation of niobium rich
secondary phases with ratios of Na and K to Nb of 0.3 and 0.6 respectively,
suggesting that sodium volitalizes faster than potassium [29].

resulting in abnormal grain growth which produces a bimodal grain size distri-

matrix of ﬁner grains [26].

Processing
Materials such as metals and plastics can be formed into desired geometries
through an incredibly wide variety of processes due to their relatively low melt-
ing temperatures, low hardness, and high ductility. These techniques range from
mechanical deformation processes such as drawing or forming, material removal
processes like milling or turning, solidiﬁcation processes including melt extrusion
or casting, among many others. Ceramics, refractory metals, and intermetallic
compounds due not have this luxury due to their high melting points, brittle
nature, and exceptional hardness. Forming of these materials is therefore typi-
cally achieved through the consolidation of ﬁne powders which are subsequently
sintered.
Usually powder forming processes involve the use of a carrier ﬂuid and/or binder
in order to improve plasticity. Solvents and binders can provide a lubricious
eﬀect, which lower inter-particle frictional forces and reduce the force needed
for compaction or ﬂow, as well as improving the yield strength of the mixture,
which allows pre-sintered parts to hold their shape prior to sintering. Powder
mixes can be formed using a variety of diﬀerent methods depending on the
desired geometry of the ﬁnal part and cost-eﬀectiveness of the process. Common
powder forming methods include pressing, tape casting, slip casting, injection
molding, and extrusion [42].

Robocasting
Traditional powder processing techniques such as the ones listed above are well
understood and widely employed but are limited in the types of geometries they
can produce. Creating parts with complex shapes or features can often be costly
or outright impossible. The advent of additive manufacturing processes has pro-
vided a route for producing complex three-dimensional structures out of a broad
range of materials. There are many diﬀerent additive manufacturing methods
which have been used to produce ceramic devices including stereolithography,
two-photon polymerization, inkjet printing, robocasting, selective laser sinter-
ing, laminated object manufacturing, and fused deposition modeling; each with
its own advantages and drawbacks [43].
This work will focus on one particular additive manufacturing process: robo-
casting. The technique is referred to by an array of diﬀerent names including
Direct Ink Writing, Direct Write Assembly, and Robot Assisted Deposition but
regardless of the diversity in nomenclature the processes are identical. Robo-
casting is essentially an extrusion operation where the extruder head has three
axes of freedom for linear motion. During the process, a ceramic paste is contin-
uously extruded through an oriﬁce as the nozzle moves relative to a substrate.
The desired object is built in a layer-by-layer fashion until the part is complete
[24][44].

Robocasting has some key advantages relative to other additive manufacturing

2.4.2.1
Templated Grain Growth
During the sintering process of a polycrystalline material with a distribution of
grain sizes, large grains grow at the expense of small grains due to a diﬀerence in
the surface free energy as was discussed in Section 2.3. This process is known as
Ostwald ripening. Typically the rapid growth of a few large grains is undesirable,
however this eﬀect can be purposely harnessed to create materials with oriented
microstructures through a technique known as templated grain growth (TGG).
In a TGG process, a few large anisometric particles are dispersed in a matrix of
relatively ﬁne equiaxed particles. These larger particles initially have a random
orientation distribution but can be coaxed into aligning with a particular di-
rection during processing through shear forces, electric ﬁelds, or magnetic ﬁelds
depending on the type of process and the physical properties of the template
material.
When the material is later sintered, the large particles act as substrates for the
nucleation and growth of the desired phases. If the template and matrix materi-
als are the same, the larger particles will grow into the matrix until reaching the
expanding grain boundary of another large crystal domain. The ﬁnal grain size
after sintering can be determined by the spacing of the templates, χT , which is
dependent on their frequency fT [5]:

2.5
Environmental Impact of KNN
The primary motivation for the development of lead-free piezoelectric ceramics
has been the threat of a regulatory ban on lead-based piezoelectric ceramics.
EU directives on Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) and Re-
striction of Hazardous Substances (RoHS) have long encouraged or mandated
the phasing out of lead-based materials in electronic applications. PZT ceramics
are currently exempt from these regulations but exemptions must be periodi-
cally renewed and the precariousness of their legality has sparked a race to ﬁnd
alternatives.
The impacts of lead on human health are well documented. Children are par-
ticularly vulnerable to lead poisoning and it can severely impact their mental,
intellectual, and physical development. In adults lead poisoning is associated
with fatigue, high blood pressure, joint and muscle pain, abdominal discom-
fort, vomiting, and can cause miscarriages in pregnant women. Therefore the
concerns about lead toxicity are legitimate and eﬀorts should be undertaken to
reduce lead emissions into the environment [49].
The main source of lead in PZT comes from PbO which accounts for 60% of
the precursor material for PZT synthesis. There is no evidence that suggests
that lead-based piezoelectric ceramics are harmful to human health during the
use phase. However lead pollution can occur during mining for lead ore, evapo-
ration of oxides during calcination and sintering, and via leakage from landﬁlls
or improper waste disposal [50]. Any replacement materials for PZT should
not only have comparable piezoelectric properties but also possess a smaller
environmental and toxicological footprint.
The materials used in the synthesis of KNN are relatively benign and the com-
pound itself shows no negative human health eﬀects. In fact, it is biocompatible
and is being explored for use in implantable devices. However, recent studies
have shown that KNN has signiﬁcantly higher environmental and human health
impacts than PZT across all major impact categories. Whereas many of the
concerns around PZT revolve around the emission of lead into the environment
during processing and disposal, KNN’s environmental impacts are shifted to
earlier points in its life cycle.
The fabrication stages of drying, calcination, and sintering consume more ther-
mal and electrical energy for KNN than the equivalent processes for PZT. This
is due to the notably higher heat capacity of KNN (0.55 J/gK @ 345K [51][52])
as compared to PZT (0.35 J/gK). Since the majority of the energy used in these
processes is derived from fossil fuels, the higher thermal and electrical demands
of KNN also produce higher greenhouse gas emissions, environmental toxicity,

3.1.1
Voronoi Diagrams and Lloyd’s Algorithm
The dispersion of template particles in a matrix is modeled through the cre-

segments.
The line segments represent the positions and orientations of the
template particles after robocasting. The initial positions and orientations of
the lines are random, and the degree of variability can be controlled through a
set of model parameters.

material is created with the use of generalized Voronoi diagrams which are
generated from line segments rather than from points. In a conventional Voronoi
diagram a given space with n nodes is partitioned into n convex polygons. The
partitions are placed such that each region only has one node, and all the points
within a given region are closer to their own node than any other node.

Grain boundaries are known to impose diﬀerent electrical and mechanical bound-
ary conditions which aﬀect the domain conﬁguration of the material.
The
boundaries have a relatively low permittivity which weakens the interaction of
polarization between grains. The result of this dielectric boundary is a shield-
ing eﬀect which allows grains to have some degree of independence from one

4.1
Molten Salt Synthesis
4.1.1
Powder Milling
The ﬁrst step in producing KNN needles is the synthesis of the K2Nb4O11 pre-
cursor via a molten salt synthesis reaction.
In previous experiments it was
discovered that the particle size of the Nb2O5 powder as received from the sup-
plier was too high, resulting in the slow dissolution of Nb2O5 into the molten
salt which allowed niobium deﬁcient phases to form and stabilize before all of
the niobium became available to react. In order to compensate for the slow
dissolution, the powder was milled down to a ﬁner particle size.
Milling of the powder was accomplished by mixing 16.62g of Nb2O5 powder
(Sigma Aldrich 208515, 99.9%) with 26.0g of ethanol and 200g of YSZ milling
media in a 200mL plastic container. The slurry was ball milled for 72 hours at
85 speed. After milling, the slurry was drained into a ﬂat glass dish and heated
to 65◦C while being agitated with a magnetic stirrer. Once the ethanol had
evaporated, the powder was collected and placed in a drying cupboard at 120◦C
for 1 hour.
==PAGE:84==
72
Experimental
4.1.2
Synthesis of K2Nb4O11 Templates
For the production of potassium niobate templates milled Nb2O5 powder, K2CO3
powder (Sigma Aldrich 209619, ≥99.0%), and KCl (Sigma Aldrich P3911, >99.0%)
ﬂux were mixed using one of two methods depending on the sample. Prior to
mixing, the K2CO3 powder was dried overnight at 200◦C to remove any ab-
sorbed moisture.
In the ﬁrst powder preparation method, the powders were ground by hand with
a quartz mortar and pestle until the powder distribution was homogenized. The
second method involved placing the powders in plastic containers with ethanol
and YSZ milling media. The reactants were milled for an hour and then drained
into a glass dish. The dish was placed on a hot plate at 65◦C until the ethanol
had evaporated, after which the powders were scraped oﬀand placed in a drying
cupboard. The mixes were then loaded into an alumina crucible and covered.
The crucible was placed in a furnace and the temperature proﬁle was set to rise
with a 180◦C/hr ramp rate up to a ﬁnal temperature of 1000◦C, which was then
held for 6 hours. The cooling rate was set to match the heating rate and the
sample was removed after reaching room temperature.
In order to remove the KCl salt, warm water was poured into the crucible
while a magnetic stirrer spun inside. The resultant mixture of water, salt, and
potassium niobate was poured out of crucible into a large beaker and the process
was repeated until all of the material in the crucible had been visibly extracted.
A vacuum pump was used to run the mixture through a set of ﬁlter papers to
separate the water and dissolved KCl from the undissolved potassium niobate
powder. The ﬁlter papers were then dried in a drying cupboard overnight at
200◦C, after which the powders were scraped oﬀof the ﬁlter paper and stored
for later analysis and use.

4.2.1
Ink Preparation
Printing of the KNN powders was accomplished with the use of an ink system
developed by Christophe Gadea at DTU Energy. The solvent is a mix of 59.4
wt.% distilled water, 39.6 wt.% glycerol (Sigma Aldrich G7893, ≥99.5%), and
1.0 wt.% of sodium carboxymethyl cellulose (Süd-Chemie CMC-CA600G1). The
cellulose functions as a binder and gelation agent in the system and was dispersed
by mixing with a magnetic stirrer for a minimum of 0.5 hours.
Dispersion of templates was done by combining 10 wt.% of template particles
with 90 wt.% of KNN powder (Ceramic Powder Technology A/S, Heimdal,
Norway) in a plastic container.
Ethanol was added to the powders and the
slurry was placed in a sonicating bath for 10 minutes to break up agglomerates
of template particles. The mixture was then further mixed with a magnetic
stirrer for another 10 minutes and then poured into a glass dish. The glass dish
was placed onto a hot plate at 65◦C until all of the ethanol had evaporated,
after which the powder was scraped oﬀand collected.
After mixing, the desired amount of KNN powder was weighed out in a mea-
suring boat. The amount of solvent corresponding to an ink composition with
30 vol.% solid loading was added carefully to the bottom edge of a plastic sam-
ple bag. The KNN powder was then poured onto the solvent and the bag was
twisted to prevent the mixture from spreading too thinly over the inside of the
bag. The mixture was kneaded until all of the powder was incorporated and
the paste appeared to be homogeneous. The paste was then loaded into a 3mL
syringe via a small hole cut into the bottom corner of the bag and transferred
to the robocasting cell.

Processing of the ceramic inks was done with a BioX printer from Cellink. The
paste was extruded through nozzles with an oriﬁce diameter of either 410µm or

for analysis.

6.1
Finite Element Modeling
A ﬁnite element model was constructed in COMSOL in order to study the eﬀect
of microstructural orientation in piezoelectric materials. The model was able
to generate realistic looking microstructures and replicate expected behavior.
While it is far from being a ﬁnished product, it can serve as a reasonable starting
point for further development.
Simulations showed a net piezoelectric eﬀect in un-poled crystals of KNbO3 with
oriented microstructures. A much weaker eﬀect was seen when the microstruc-
ture was random but this is thought to be an artifact due to the small number
of grains. In a larger sample with more randomly oriented grains present it is
expected that the net piezoelectric eﬀect would be close to zero, similar to what
is observed in real un-poled piezoelectric materials.
There are several limitations to the model however. The full piezoelectric and
elastic tensors of a material need to be known in order for the model to work
and this data is often scarce. The full physical description of a crystal is time
consuming and diﬃcult to obtain; typically only the most relevant properties
are published in literature. In this work the full description of a K0.5Na0.5NbO3
crystal could not be found and KNbO3 had to be used a substitute material.
==PAGE:110==
98
Discussion
Furthermore, only the un-poled properties of KNbO3 could be located. The
piezoelectric response of a material is only relevant after poling as no devices
would be used in the un-poled state. This places serious limits on the applica-
bility of the results which are presented in this report.
The model also assumes perfect growth of the template particles into the matrix
phase and complete densiﬁcation of the material. Figure A.55 demonstrates
that this is often not the case in real systems. The templates may not consume
the entirety of the matrix phase and some degree of porosity will be present
in the sample. It has been shown in literature that pores aﬀect the electrical
ﬁeld conditions within the material and can severely aﬀect the domain structure
when they are present within the crystal [60].
Additionally, the eﬀect of grain boundaries is also not considered by the model.
Grain boundaries impose electrical and mechanical boundary conditions which
aﬀect the domain conﬁguration of the material. The low permittivity of the grain
boundary weakens the interaction of polarization between grains, producing a
shielding eﬀect which allows grains to have some degree of independence from
one another [60]. The lack of grain boundary eﬀects also places a limit on the
accuracy and representativeness of the model.
It is possible to make the model more sophisticated by incorporating these
elements but it would require additional development. The MATLAB script
which generates the microstructure could be adapted to generate realistic pore
structures. A dielectric phase between grains could also be added to the model
to replicate the eﬀect of grain boundaries.
6.2
Template Synthesis and Conversion
Perhaps the most signiﬁcant setback suﬀered over the course of this thesis con-
cerns the inability to repeat the synthesis of K2Nb4O11. While the exact reason
as to why the synthesis reactions produced a broad range of phases remains
unknown, the last set of attempts, SX-8272 and SX-8273, may provide some
insight. These two experiments were the only synthesis reactions that produced
a unique set of products; the 5 previous attempts resulted in virtually identical
particle morphologies and distributions. Varying the selection of of crucible,
furnace, s/o ratio, mixing procedure, and temperature did not produce any no-
table diﬀerences. The one variable that seemed to aﬀect the outcome of the
reaction was the choice of Nb2O5 powder.
The original batch of milled Nb2O5 powder was lost during the relocation of

the laboratories. Once the laboratories in Kongens Lyngby were commissioned,
a new batch of powder was milled with the same procedure that was used to
produce the original batch. Despite care being taken to ensure that the condi-
tions between the batches remained identical, there was one notable diﬀerence
between the two. The milling of the second batch was originally programmed to
last 72 hours but at some point a new container was placed on the ball milling
equipment and the timer was reset, resulting in a high degree of uncertainty in
the total milling time. As a result, the second batch may have been milled for
up to 50% longer than the original. Since the particle size analyzer has not been
recommissioned, the particle size distribution of the second batch could not be
accurately measured.
During the ﬁrst set of experiments at Risø, it was determined that the formation
of niobium-deﬁcient phases during the synthesis reaction was likely due to the
large particle size of the Nb2O5 powder and its slow dissolution into the molten
salt. It was theorized that in the early stages of the reaction relatively little Nb
was available to react and as such the ﬁrst phases to nucleate and grow were
K4Nb6O17. This problem was solved by milling the Nb2O5 powder in order to
increase the dissolution rate and therefore the amount of available Nb during
the early stages of the reaction. The theory seemed to be validated when the
subsequent synthesis reaction produced a nearly pure sample of K2Nb4O11.
Here, the author would like to posit that the reason why K2Nb4O11 could not
be synthesized using the second batch of Nb2O5 powder was precisely for the
opposite reason; that in these experiments the particle size was too ﬁne. The
extra milling time that the second batch experienced most likely signiﬁcantly
reduced the particle size relative to that of the ﬁrst batch. While SEM images
of the ﬁrst batch were not taken, visually comparing the as-received and second
batch of milled powders shows a drastic reduction in the particle size (see images
at 250x magniﬁcation in Figures A.8 and A.9).
The high surface area of the Nb2O5 powder from the second batch allowed for
its fast dissolution into the molten salt when compared to the degradation of
K2CO3 and subsequent dissolution of K2O. This scenario would result in a glut
of excess Nb being present in the molten salt during the early stages of the
reaction. As the stoichiometric ratio between Nb and K that was available to
react during these early stages was higher than the 2:1 ratio required for the
formation of K2Nb4O11, the ﬁrst phases to form were those which were closest to
the stoichiometry of these two elements in solution at this point in the reaction.
In this case the ﬁrst phase that was able to nucleate was likely KNb3O8. The
KNb3O8 crystals which began to form were stable and as the reaction progressed
they continued to grow, consuming dissolved Nb and K in the process.
The nucleation and growth of this niobium-rich phase in a set of reactants that
==PAGE:112==
100
Discussion
did not match its stoichiometry meant that at some point during the reaction,
the ratio of Nb to K in solution went below the 3:1 limit required to form
KNb3O8.
The next phase to form was probably K3Nb7O19.
As this phase
nucleated and grew, it too consumed dissolved Nb and eventually the ratio of
dissolved Nb to K in the molten salt dropped below 2.33:1 and the nucleation
and growth of K2Nb4O11 became favorable. As much of the Nb had already been
consumed in the formation and growth of relatively Nb-rich phases, the growth
of these particles could not continue indeﬁnitely and eventually the ratio went
below 2:1, after which the formation of K4Nb6O17 began. The process again
repeated itself when in the late stages of the reaction there was only suﬃcient
quantities of Nb to form KNbO3.
This would explain why broad area EDS
measurements show an Nb to K ratio of 2:1. The overall stoichiometry of the
products matches that of the reactants, however the individual elements are not
evenly distributed among the products.
Some evidence to support this theory lies in the relative crystal sizes of the
diﬀerent phases. In all of the samples where KNb3O8 was present, the size of
these crystals visually dominated the samples. They often reached lengths of
50-200µm and widths of up to 10µm, much larger than any of the other phases.
In comparison, Nb-deﬁcient phases such as K4Nb6O17 and KNbO3 typically
had crystal sizes measuring around 5x5µm and 1x1µm respectively. It is clear
from experiments SX-8155 and SX-8273 that if K4Nb6O17 is able to nucleate
early on in the reaction process that it has no problem producing platelets with
dimensions as large as 100x100µm. If the Nb-rich phases nucleate and grow
ﬁrst and the Nb-deﬁcient phases form towards the end of the reaction, it could
explain the disparity in crystal sizes.
However, it must be noted that there are many factors that inﬂuence the growth
rate of a crystal. Among these, the energy required for the creation of a new
step edge and the energy required for step growth are particularly important.
These energies are aﬀected by composition, crystal structure, and defect density
and can vary signiﬁcantly from one material to another. Even across the same
material, diﬀerent crystallographic faces can have wildly diﬀerent energetic bar-
riers to growth, leading to preferred growth in the crystallographic direction
perpendicular to the crystal face with the highest energy [62]. Due to the large
diﬀerences in the free energy required for growth between crystals, comparing
the crystal sizes of diﬀerent phases is not a perfect measure of when these crys-
tals formed and should only be used as an indication.

SX-8273 were accidentally switched around at some point during the extraction
process. It was intended for the sample labeled SX-8273 to use milled Nb2O5

similar to what was observed when using the unmilled powder in experiment

6.3
Robocasting and Template Orientation
Multi-layer KNN structures were successfully printed during the course of this
project. It was shown that an ink system comprised of 30vol.% KNN dispersed
in a solvent made of water, glycerol, and sodium carboxymethylcellulose had
rheological properties that were suitable for printing. None of these properties,
neither the yield stress nor the relationship between shear stress and viscosity,
could be measured as the rheometer had not yet been recommissioned. However,
it was shown that in the absence of templates the ink could be easily extruded
through nozzles as small as 410µm without clogging and that layered structures
could be printed without slumping or deformation. The successful production
of robocast structures directly veriﬁes that the rheological properties of the inks
are suitable for printing.
Attempts to print inks with dispersed templates through 410µm nozzles re-
sulted in frequent clogging and no complete structures could be produced. This
is likely due to the formation of large agglomerates of template particles. Tem-
plate particles form dense mats during the ﬁltration process and it is likely that
similar interactions between these elongated structures occur within the ink.
The templates and KNN powders were placed in a sonicating bath to break up
the mats and disperse the particles but it possible that a longer sonicating time
is required. It may also be that additional steps to break up the mats such as
grinding or milling are necessary, although these run the risk of breaking the
templates and reducing their aspect-ratios. The use of dispersants in the ink
system may also prevent the agglomeration of the particles and improve the ﬂow
properties of the ink. In general, more work needs to be done to optimize the
templated ink system for use with ﬁne nozzles.
If clogging is found to be an inherent problem with extruding templates through
ﬁne nozzles regardless of how well dispersed the particles are, it was still shown
that these inks could be easily printed with larger nozzles. 580µm nozzles were
used to print structures with templated inks without clogging. Optimization
of the printing parameters is still needed with the the use of a wider nozzle
however. The mass ﬂow rate at the minimum pressure required for ﬂow was too
high and the ink tended to spread out farther than the intended cross-section of
the print line. This might be easily solved by increasing the translational speed
of the printhead such that the product of the mass ﬂow rate and the printhead
speed is not higher than the intended volume.
While structures with templates could be robocast, no net orientation could be
observed in the template particles. The 580µm nozzle might be too wide and
the resultant shear forces too low to coax templates into alignment with the ﬂow
direction. Even if some alignment was achieved during printing, it may have
==PAGE:115==
6.4 Sintering
103
been disturbed by the spreading that occurred as excess ink ﬂowed into the
already deposited material. The orientation of the template particles should
be reevaluated again after the printing parameters are optimized.
If no net
orientation can be observed then it is imperative that the ink system is adjusted
such that it can be extruded through ﬁne nozzles in order to increase the shear
force.
Decreasing the number of template particles may also ameliorate the
propensity for agglomeration. If smaller nozzles cannot be used for processing
templated inks, the alignment of particles can also be improved by increasing the
viscosity [63]. The extrusion pressures when using the 580µm nozzle (50-85kPa)
were well below the 200kPa maximum pressure that can be supplied by the
printer. This leaves room for thickening the ink while maintaining printability.
The solid loading percentage can be increased or viscosity modiﬁers can be added
to try to increase the shear forces in the ink and improve template alignment.
6.4
Sintering
KNN is a notoriously diﬃcult to sinter material and ideally a sintering study of
the standard powder would be carried out prior to the production of components.
However, due to the time constraints imposed by the COVID-19 shutdown and
the movement of the laboratories no such study could be conducted. Sintering
conditions were chosen based on literature values and previous experience.
The ﬁrst samples sintered at 1100◦C were able to reach relatively high bulk
densities of around 93-95% suggesting that densiﬁcation mechanisms were ca-
pable of being activated at this temperature. Despite this, the microstructure
of the samples showed a strongly bimodal distribution of grain sizes. While the
majority of grains were relatively small, measuring roughly 1-3µm in diameter,
a number of massive grains could be seen embedded throughout the sample.
These grains reached sizes well over 50µm in diameter and were clear evidence
of exaggerated grain growth.
It is unclear which grain structure produces the optimal ferroelectric response
in KNN ceramics.
Larger grains tend to have larger domain structures and
the rotation of larger domains requires higher energies [64][65]. As the grain
size decreases, so does the size of the ferroelectric domains and the activation
energy required for rotation. This is counteracted by the eﬀect of grain bound-
aries which becomes increasingly signiﬁcant with decreasing grain size.
The
rotation of 90◦domains is inhibited due to the presence of lattice defects in
the grain boundary. These defects are able to pin domain walls and prevent
their reorientation during poling [64]. Smaller grains have a higher density of
grain boundaries and so this eﬀect becomes more pronounced at smaller grain
==PAGE:116==
104
Discussion
sizes. Additionally, there is some evidence to suggest that the presence of abnor-
mal grain growth improves the piezoelectric strain in KNN-based materials [65].
This again reinforces the idea that a sintering study should be performed on the
KNN powders used in this experiment to determine the eﬀect of temperature
on both the microstructure and piezoelectric response.
High temperature sintering of SX-8280 and SX-8281 at 1130◦C likely resulted
in the formation of liquid phases. Slumping of the samples during sintering and
their adhesion to the substrate were the main indications that liquid phases were
present. The solidus temperature of strictly stoichiometric K0.5Na0.5NbO3 oc-
curs at 1140◦C, but this temperature decreases with a decreasing ratio of Na/K
[66]. The exact stoichiometry was not measured for either the synthesized or
purchased KNN powders and therefore the exact solidus point is unknown, but it
is possible that the samples were slightly sodium-deﬁcient which could place the
solidus point of the material below the sintering temperature. Even if the KNN
powders were perfectly stoichiometric, the high volatility of alkali elements at
temperatures close to the solidus point can lead to evaporation of these elements
and a loss of stoichiometry. As discussed in Section 2.3.3.1, during sintering of
KNN sodium is able to volatilize faster than potassium. As more sodium atoms
than potassium atoms evaporate, the stoichiometry of KNN becomes K-rich
which can also suppress the solidus temperature (see the phase diagram in Fig-
ure 2.12). Additionally, if the evaporation of alkali elements becomes signiﬁcant,
at a certain point the perovskite lattice is unable to accommodate a signiﬁcant
deﬁciency of these A-site ions which then leads to the formation of secondary
phases, particularly K2Nb4O11 or K4Nb6O17. These secondary phases can have
a lower melting temperature than KNN and form a liquid phase at the sintering
temperature.
Degradation of KNN into secondary phases during high temperature sintering
was conﬁrmed with XRD measurements. Samples sintered at low temperatures
in SX-8279 produced a diﬀraction pattern with narrow peaks in the locations
expected based on literature. The secondary phases appear as minor peaks in
the noise between the main peaks of KNN. These peaks are found at 15◦, 24◦,
28◦, and 48◦in sample SX-8280 and at 18◦, 23◦, 28◦, and 46◦in sample SX-8281
(see Figure 6.1). Peak broadening was seen in these samples which can occur if
samples are not level or have rough surfaces. As the sintered samples were not
polished, the sample surface may also have aﬀected the measurements.
Diﬀerences in the densiﬁcation behavior between samples sintered 1100◦C and
1130◦C are also supported by the density measurements.
SX-8279 samples
sintered at 1100◦C had lower apparent densities than those which were exposed
to higher temperatures. There are a number of possible explanations for this
observation. These samples were printed using a ﬁner nozzle size, which led to
higher control over the shape and size of print lines. The smaller print lines
==PAGE:117==

105

had less contact with adjacent lines than those printed in the following two
experiments with the larger nozzle. The shape of the print lines in the latter
case was harder to control and had a tendency to expand past the desired
diameter due to the mass ﬂow rate being higher than the product of the desired
cross-section of the print line and the transverse speed of the printhead. The
extra mass which ﬂowed in in the latter cases may have helped ﬁll the porous
cavities which inherently form between print lines. Mass may have also ﬂowed
into pores in these samples aided by the formation of liquid phases.
Despite having higher apparent densities, the samples sintered at 1130◦C reached
lower bulk densities than those sintered at 1100◦C. The bulk density measure-
ments of the samples generally match the relationship between sintering tem-
perature and density that has been reported in literature (see Figure 2.21). The
variation in densiﬁcation may be due to diﬀerences in how mass transport oc-
curs at the diﬀerent temperatures. Micrographs of samples sintered at the higher
temperature show grain sizes which are an order of magnitude larger than those
sintered at the lower temperature. The higher sintering temperatures can better
promote mass transfer through surface transport mechanisms, either by provid-
ing additional energy which accelerates these processes or due to the enhanced
material transport rate which occurs in the presence of a liquid phase. The
increase in mass transfer through surface transport mechanisms leads to rapid
grain growth, decreasing the total surface area and by extension, the total sur-
face free energy. The decrease in surface free energy in turn reduces the driving
force for densiﬁcation before slower bulk diﬀusion processes can occur and fully
==PAGE:118==

densify the material resulting in lower overall bulk densities. It should also be
noted that higher grain growth rates did seem to suppress exaggerated grain
growth in the samples sintered at high temperatures.
It was also observed that while the KNN template needles grew during sintering,
they were not able to eﬀectively orient the overall microstructure of the sample.
The growth rates of these needles was low enough that cubic KNN grains were
able to grow to sizes similar to those of the template particles. The driving
force for template growth at this point is extremely low and it is unlikely that
additional sintering time would result in further template growth. This is an
important barrier to overcome as the entire purpose of this project was to use
templates as a way to promote alignment of the microstructure. The growth
of template particles may be improved by milling the matrix powder to ﬁner
particle sizes in order to increase the driving force for coarsening. Using lower
sintering temperatures may also be beneﬁcial. It was observed that exagger-
ated grain growth occurred when sintering at 1100◦C. The growth of templates
into the matrix phase is essentially a controlled exaggerated grain growth pro-
cess. The use of sintering conditions which naturally promote exaggerated grain
growth may therefore be beneﬁcial for the growth of templates into the matrix.
The slower growth of the matrix grains at lower temperatures may also help
maintain the driving force for growth of the templates for longer.
6.5
Piezoelectric Performance
The reference KNN sample which was sintered at 1100◦C produced a respectable
out-of-ﬁeld d33 value of around 100pC/N. This is despite the fact that the nei-
ther the printing parameters nor the sintering conditions were optimized. The
sample contained a total pore volume of 15% and two-thirds of this was open
porosity which likely formed during printing. Porosity generally acts as a defect
in piezoelectric materials. The air which ﬁlls void spaces has a lower dielectric
breakdown strength and permittivity than the surrounding ceramic, increasing
the possibility of dielectric breakdown. Charge also accumulates on the pore
surface, and at high electrical ﬁeld strengths can lead to electrical discharge
and arcing which can produce mechanical failure of the ceramic [67]. Although
the conditions that the reference material was prepared in were suboptimal, the
performance of the material was not wholly inadequate.
The performance of all of the samples could be characterized by the presence
of leakage currents when placed under an electric ﬁeld. These currents were
relatively minor in the reference sample but substantial in the templated sam-
ples. Ideally piezoelectric ceramics function as perfect dielectrics, where little
==PAGE:119==
6.5 Piezoelectric Performance
107
to no current can ﬂow through the material. The presence of conductive paths
through the material, particularly at higher voltages, can severely reduce the
piezoelectric properties. The presence of leakage currents in the reference sam-
ple is not surprising however. KNN is known to often suﬀer from high leakage
currents due to the evaporation of K during sintering. The loss of K atoms
from the lattice creates hole defects and these holes are the primary carriers of
leakage current in KNN crystals [68].
An increase in conductivity for the samples sintered at 1130◦C is almost certainly
due to the presence of potassium niobate phases. These phases may be more
conductive than KNN; for example hydrated versions of K4Nb6O17 were found
to be conductive [69]. The presence of phases with diﬀerent crystal structures
in the structure can also lead to an increase in defects along grain boundaries
which would thereby increase the conductivity of the ceramic. But in any case,
all of these phases exhibit either weak or no ferroelectric behavior and their
formation is expected to decrease the performance of the overall material. This
also explains why the sample SX-8280 experienced the highest leakage currents;
potasium niobate templates were purposefully added and its inclusion could
have further increased the conductivity of the material.
High leakage currents made it diﬃcult to accurately measure the piezoelectric
properties of the materials under the presence of an electric ﬁeld. Local max-
ima in current plots could be seen at ±10kV/cm in SX-8279, ±10kV/cm in
SX-8280, and ±5kV/cm in SX-8281. The coercive ﬁeld strength that produces
a reorientation of domains in all likelihood occurs around this point, yet the
coercive ﬁeld strengths were calculated to be 18kV/cm, 24kV/cm, and 21kV/cm
respectively. These values are much higher than what is reported in literature
where the coercive ﬁeld of KNN is typically given to be around 12kV/cm [68].
This value aligns much more closely with what would be expected based on the
current signal that was measured. Leakage currents also distort the polariza-
tion curves and make it diﬃcult to accurately measure remnant polarization.
Residual strain in the samples was also observed once the electric ﬁeld returned
to zero. This could be the result of domains that do not return to their original
orientations after ﬁeld cycling [70] or due to non-planar surfaces in the sample.
It should be noted that the variable size and unpolished surfaces of these sam-
ples can also aﬀect all of the properties listed above and could explain some of
the variation.
Ultimately it can be said that despite the potential for optimization, the per-
formance of the reference sample reﬂected what can be expected from KNN.
Sintering at high temperatures and the inclusion of conductive phases signiﬁ-
cantly decreased the piezoelectric properties of the ceramics by increasing the
total leakage current through the samples. Improvements in processing and sin-
tering conditions would likely result in an increase in the piezoelectric properties

Chapter 7
Conclusions
In order to meet the demand for high performance lead-free piezoelectric ceram-
ics, this thesis aimed to investigate the use of templated grain growth to pro-
duce structures of K0.5Na0.5NbO3 with oriented microstructures and improved
piezoelectric properties. While the goal of producing a material with superior
performance was not met, the information learned over the course of this project
can serve as a foundation for further work.
The groundwork for a ﬁnite element model was built which can be used to screen
piezoelectric materials to determine optimal microstructures. The model was
able to show the diﬀerences between poled and unpoled KNbO3 but requires full
descriptions of the elastic and piezoelectric tensors in order to produce results.
Additional development is needed to add more sophistication to the model such
that it more accurately reﬂects the actual behavior of materials. If this model
can be elaborated it can serve as a useful tool for designing materials with
engineered microstructures; from grain orientation, to porous piezoelectrics and
more. Producing piezoelectric ceramics is a time consuming process and a robust
model could signiﬁcantly cut down on research time and allow for the testing of
ideas even if the means with which to accomplish that idea are not immediately
evident.
Synthesis of K2Nb4O11 could not be successfully completed, and numerous at-
tempts resulted in the formation of relatively large KNb3O8 crystals interspersed
==PAGE:122==
110
Conclusions
with small KNbO3 crystals. A theory was put forth as to why this behavior was
observed but subsequent experiments are needed to verify whether or not the
suppositions of the author are correct. Experiments which converted potassium
niobate phases to KNN were able to demonstrate that the shape of K2Nb4O11
crystals is preserved during the reaction. Not only was the needle shape of the
template maintained, but the resultant KNN needles appeared to be single crys-
tals. This suggests that if a pure sample of K2Nb4O11 can be obtained it can be
converted into single crystal KNN needles using the same reaction conditions,
providing a viable route for template production.
Robocasting was shown to be a feasible method for producing ceramics with
KNN-based inks. An ink with 30vol.% solid loading and a solvent consisting
of sodium caboxymethylcellulose dispresed in water and glycerol was used to
print freestanding multi-layered structures using nozzles as ﬁne as 410µm. The
incorporation of template particles into the ink increased the propensity for
clogging and required an increase nozzle size to print reliably. Structures with
templates were also successfully printed, however the overall orientation of the
templates was low. Further work is needed to develop the processing parameters
such that the alignment of the template particles is improved. However, the
identiﬁcation of an ink system and the beginning the deﬁnition of an operating
regime provide a starting point for developing the methods required to print
complex structures.
Sintering of printed KNN structures was done at 1100◦C and 1130◦C and the
eﬀect of sintering temperature on microstructure and piezoelectric performance
was studied.
Low temperature sintering was able to produce ceramics with
higher bulk densities but the conditions were also favorable for exaggerated
grain growth. High temperature sintering produced microstructures with larger
grains and a more uniform distribution but there was ample evidence for the
formation of secondary and liquid phases during sintering. Micrographs of the
templated samples also revealed that the template phases did not grow to dom-
inate the microstructure as was expected. The experiments performed clearly
show that sintering at 1130◦C is not ideal for KNN. It is recommended that a
sintering study be carried out on both standard KNN powder and KNN with dis-
peresed templates to understand how sintering temperature aﬀects grain growth,
densiﬁcation, and piezoelectric performance.
Piezoelectric properties were measured for samples sintered at 1100◦C without
templates and 1130◦C with templates. While leakage currents were observed
in all cases they were particularly pronounced in the high temperature sintered
samples. Whereas low temperature sintering yielded samples with out-of-ﬁeld
d33 values of 96pC/N, higher temperatures resulted in values ranging from 28-
36pC/N. The inclusion of template phases and the excessively high sintering
temperatures greatly reduced the overall performance of KNN. This reinforces
==PAGE:123==
111
the need the consider the impact of template phases on the defect structure of
KNN and its relationship to conductivity.
The systems described in this thesis are highly complex and yet sensitive to
minor variations in conditions. An immense amount of work has been done by
researchers dating back to the mid-20th century to understand and manipulate
these materials. Considering all of the intricacies involved in the production and
use of piezoelectric materials, the goals which were laid out at the beginning of
the project were ambitious for the limited time frame available. Despite this,
it is the hope of the author that the work that was performed will serve as a
stepping stone for future development of lead-free piezoelectric ceramics.
