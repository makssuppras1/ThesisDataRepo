Abstract
The constant innovation in portable technologies has made it relatively easy today to
measure our biosignals like Electrocardiogram (ECG) and respiration (RSP). This study
focuses on using ECG and RSP data collected using portable devices to build a machine
learning model to detect anxiety. Heart Rate Variability (HRV) features extracted from
ECG signal and Respiratory Rate Variability (RRV) features extracted from RSP signal
were used to build anxiety models that achieved the best accuracy of 75.07 % when tested
across new participants, new video stimuli and through random splits. We introduced
a transitional period between anxiety and non-anxiety events and vice-versa and found
that 30 seconds transitional period starting from when the new stimuli is presented is the
best time period for emotional transition. In this study, we have shown that automated
evaluation of anxiety is realistic using HRV measures and should be the way forward in
mental evaluation of all sorts of emotional states.

Introduction
Anxiety is a negative emotional state, usually characterized by feeling of unease, appre-
hension and symptoms of tension in which an individual anticipates impending danger,
catastrophe, or misfortune. It might cause you to sweat, feel restless and tense, and
have a rapid heartbeat. Anxiety activates mostly the sympathetic nervous system. It is a
fairly common emotion and is prevalent in different stages of the life cycle, especially in
childhood and adolescence [1].
Figure 1.1: Anxiety (Source: Google Images)
Emotions have an affect on both human physiology and psychology. Positive emotions
are associated with better health and work efficiency, whereas negative emotions are
associated with negative outcomes in life like depression [2]. Before 2020, depression and
anxiety disorders were leading contributors to the global health burden which has been
further exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic [3]. As a result of these circumstances,
the need for interventions to reduce these negative emotional states in humans is very
high.
Along with that, these negative emotions can further hinder treatment for other life threat-
ening emergencies, especially in the case of children. At Herlev Hospital in Denmark, chil-
dren coming into emergencies are triaged in the hospital’s emergency department based
on the urgency of their issue and transferred to a single patient waiting room. The children
have to wait in these waiting room for two to six hours before they receive treatment. The
children who might be already anxious are further triggered by this dreadful waiting for an
often-uncertain treatment. Finally, when the wait is over and the medical professionals
come in, the anxious children are harder to handle and it becomes difficult for the medical
professionals to administer life saving treatments to these children. So there is a need for
an easy to use anxiety assessment solution, that can help these medical professionals.

The tool can evaluate when the children are calm and let the medical professional know
when is the best time to visit them. This can help them conduct their procedures and in
a fast and efficient manner without discomforting the children too much. Further, based
on assessed level of anxiety, different measures of reducing anxiety can be developed
and used to calm the children prior to medical treatment. Eventually this would save the
medical professionals a lot of time and fast track the healthcare delivery to all patients in
the ER.
The first step to making these interventions lies in accurate tracking of these emotional
states to recognise the need of an intervention.The standard approaches to assess anxi-
ety is based on questionnaires like the Beck Anxiety Inventory [4] and the Hamilton Anxiety
Rating Scale [5]. But these questionnaires ask questions about anxiety symptoms that
might have bothered you in the past week or month like tingling, fear of dying, hand trem-
bling etc so they are more targeted towards detecting some sort of anxiety disorder rather
than situational anxiety that you might be feeling in the present moment. Another issue
with these scales is that they are self-reported so users are scoring themselves. This can
lead to highly erroneous results due to participant bias and recall difficulties. Thus these
scales are not practical for an everyday use as they fail to provide a continuous objective
assessment for the patients.
New advances in the applications of human-computer interaction, and the advent of new
age of wearables that combine sensing, processing and communication, has opened
doors for health monitoring and tracking at a very low cost. Today, wearables are used
to track all kinds of physical activity and their combination with artificial intelligence has
enabled monitoring of a wide variety of physical states in humans. Up until now, the
combination of these technologies have been mostly limited to physical tracking but a
combination of both wearables and AI has huge potential to help us analyze our emo-
tional states as well [6] This could be very well used to measure anxiety in individuals and
help humans respond to stressful daily life situation by detecting instantaneous stress and
anxiety.

Biosignals can be collected from the sensors in wearables, communicated over the inter-
net to a server where they can be analyzed and interpreted to asses anxiety in individuals.
This can provide an objective and continuous evaluation of an individual emotional state
for better emotional awareness and regulation and appropriate steps can be taken based
in a timely manner to reduce the negative outcomes or an exacerbation of the negative
emotional state like anxiety. This can also help us test different anxiety reducing mea-
sures, study their effects and come up with an arsenal of anxiety reducing methods in the
future where such interventions are carried based on on patients profile and preferences
and there is less randomness in the anxiety reducing interventions.
The new age wearables with their advanced sensors has made it easy to collect all sorts
of biosignal data like electrocardiogram (ECG) signals , respiration (RSP) rate and skin
conductance(EDA) from an individual. These streams of biosignal data has made it pos-
sible to measure anxiety and make prediction about human emotion. Researches have
been carried out in the past where models built on physiological measurements like heart
rate, skin conductance, skin conductance, brain activity , respiration rate etc have been
able to predict anxiety and other human emotions [7] [8].
The measurements made by these biosignals can be processed by a machine where
you can calculate different characteristics about how these signals and see how these
characteristics are changing over time. If we take an ECG signal as an example, we
can process ECG signal to calculate all the locations of your heartbeats in the ECG signal
recorded over a period of time. Once we have the time location of your heart beats we can
change calculate the time difference between two consecutive beats. Contrary to popular
belief, your heart doesn’t beat at a constant rate but keeps on changing depending on
what you are doing, how you are feeling, etc . This inter-beat interval can be further
processed to find out patterns in in your inter-beat interval or your heart rate over time.
This kind of analysis on the ECG, trying to find patterns in your ever changing heart rate is
popularly known as Heart Rate Variability or HRV in short. In research it has been useful
in predicting stress, depression, anxiety, PTSD along with other physical disorders as well
like inflammation, chronic pain, diabetes, concussion, asthma, insomnia, fatigue etc [9].
Similar to the ECG signal, Respiration sensor signals can be used to calculate respira-
tion rate and changes in it over time which have been shown to be correlated with an
individual’s anxiety in the past [10]
Machine Learning and Artificial Intelligence have been used in so many amazing ways
already to improve our lives from image recognition to speech recognition to the newly
released ChatGPT which is able to interact with us in a conversational way. Machine
learning has changed the way we live and is extensively being used in the field of medicine
as well from diagnosing cancer to rare genetic disorders just by analyzing images. So its
totally possible, that they can detect our emotions like anxiety if we feed them the right
data.
Combining machine learning with the biosignal data collected from sensors in wearables
opens up the possibility of real time analysis of human emotions. Features extracted from
biosignal data like changes in heart rate, changes in respiration rate etc can be used to
build such machine learning models. This technology if built can help us detect common
negative emotional states like anxiety felt by all of us on a daily basis. This would then
give us a chance to intervene in a timely manner and help improve the mental health of
numerous people all around the world.
Hence in this study, we explore the feasibility of building such a machine learning model

Background
This chapter intends to give the reader some basic understanding of terminologies and
theories that would help them on this journey of understanding anxiety and its measure-
ment. The chapter includes the meaning of anxiety, its difference from anxiety disorder,
signs of anxiety in our bodies and its connection with our nervous system, different tradi-
tional methods of anxiety measurements, different biosensors that can be used to mea-
sure anxiety, how these sensors can be leveraged for this purpose and the limitations of
these biosensors with respect to this research.
2.1
Anxiety
Anxiety is a unpleasant emotional state or condition which is characterized by subjec-
tive feelings of tension, apprehension and worry, and by the activation or arousal of the
autonomic nervous system [11]. It is a future-oriented mood state associated with prepa-
ration for possible, upcoming negative events [12]. Anxiety leads to the activation of the
sympathetic nervous system in preparation of imminent and unavoidable threat[11]. It is
important to note that, anxiety usually lasts for a short period of time and can be a normal
reaction to stress which can help you to cope and give you a boost of energy or help you
focus in case of a real threatening situation.
Some of the physical symptoms of anxiety are tremors, muscular tension, sweating, stom-
ach discomfort while mental symptoms include worry, restlessness, inattention, irritability,
high sensitivity and palpitations.

2.2
Anxiety Disorder
Even though we are not trying to measure anxiety disorder but are focused on measuring
general anxiety in individuals, it is important to understand what anxiety disorder means,
as a lot of earlier studies in the field of anxiety have focused on either detecting anxiety

disorder or have used data from participants with certain kind of anxiety disorder. Hence
its important that the reader understands how anxiety and anxiety disorder are related to
each other and what differentiates them.
Anxiety Disorder(AD) is a condition where there is elevated anxiety in anticipation of a
generic threat, a disorder specific threat like fear of spiders, fear of social interaction
etc[12]. People with anxiety disorders frequently have intense, excessive and persistent
worry and fear about everyday situations. AD usually involves repeated episodes of ele-
vated anxiety which is difficult to control and interferes with your daily activities. Examples
of anxiety disorders include generalized anxiety disorder, social anxiety disorder (social
phobia), specific phobias and separation anxiety disorder. Diagnosis of anxiety disorders
needs prolonged screening of individuals over longer period of time and has traditionally
been done with self reported anxiety scales where patients answer questions about anxi-
ety symptoms they might have felt in the last week like constant worry, difficulty to relax,
indigestion, hands trembling etc.
Its important to note that a lot of symptoms of anxiety and anxiety disorder are the same
so our bodies internal mechanism getting activated should be similar in both of them. The
difference they have is mainly in the events causing these symptoms and the intensity of
response. Anxiety is a natural response to threat in stressful situations whereas anxiety
disorder is an out of proportion response to threat in normal situations where healthy
people wont get anxious.

2.3
Autonomic Nervous System and Anxiety
Autonomic nervous system is a network of nerves throughout human body that controls
the unconscious processes. Unconscious processes are the things that happen without
us thinking about them, such as breathing and heart beating. Our autonomic nervous
system is always active, even when we are asleep, and it’s key to your continued survival.
The autonomic nervous system can be broken down in three divisions, each with its own
job:

==PAGE:17==
1. Sympathetic nervous system: This system activates body processes that help us
in times of need, especially times of stress or danger. This system is responsible
for our body’s “fight-or-flight” response.
2. Parasympathetic nervous system: This part of our autonomic nervous system
does the opposite of our sympathetic nervous system. This system is responsible
for the “rest-and-digest” body processes.
3. Enteric nervous system: This part of our autonomic nervous system manages
how your body digests food.
Our sympathetic and parasympathetic systems create a balancing act. Our sympathetic
nervous system activates body processes, while our parasympathetic deactivates or low-
ers them. The sympathetic system makes extra energy available and consumes it while
the parasympathetic system spares central nervous system energy. This balance is key
to our body’s well-being and your ongoing survival.
The sympathetic nervous system prepares our body for emergency and it always functions
when our conscious or even unconscious mind notices a need for defence or to provide
energy[13]. Studies indicate that in anxiety disorders the sympathetic nervous system’s
drive is thought to be the main reason for the physical symptoms [14]. Systems of patients
with anxiety disorders exhibit an increased sympathetic tone, adapt slowly to repeated
stimuli and respond excessively to moderated stimuli [13]. The autonomic dysfunction
and the overactivity of the sympathetic nervous system appear to induce many of the
symptoms of anxiety, such as sweating, trembling and heart racing [13].
2.4
Beck Anxiety Inventory(BAI)
The Beck Anxiety Inventory is a self-report inventory for measuring the severity of anxiety
disorder in psychiatric populations. The scale consists of 21 items, each describing a
common symptom of anxiety. The respondent is asked to rate how much he or she has
been bothered by each symptom in the last week based on a 4-point scale [4]. The total
score is calculated by finding the sum of the 21 items. Score of 0-2 indicates low anxiety,
score of 22-35 indicates moderate anxiety and Score of 36 and above indicates severe
anxiety. Some of the symptoms asked are difficulty in breathing, indigestion, dizziness etc.
Its important to point out that this scale is used for monitoring and assessing the severity
of the anxiety over a long period of time and doesn’t provide tools for measuring anxiety
a person might be feeling right now.
2.5
Hamilton Anxiety Scale(HAM-A)
The HAM-A was one of the first rating scales developed to measure the severity of anx-
iety symptoms in anxiety disorder patients, and is still widely used today in both clinical
and research settings. The scale consists of 14 items, each defined by a series of symp-
toms, and measures both psychic anxiety (mental agitation and psychological distress)
and somatic anxiety (physical complaints related to anxiety) [15]. Some items present in
the Hamilton scale are Insomnia, gastrointestinal symptoms, depressed mood etc.
Each item is scored on a scale of 0 (not present) to 4 (severe), with a total score range
of 0–56, where <17 indicates mild severity, 18–24 mild to moderate severity and 25–30
moderate to severe. Similar to Beck scale, Hamilton scale is used for monitoring and
assessing the severity of the anxiety over a long period of time and doesn’t provide tools
for measuring anxiety a person might be feeling right now.

2.6
Electroencephalography (EEG)
Electroencephalograph is the measurement of the electrical activity of brain neurons in the
cerebral cortex or scalp. Small sensors are attached to the scalp to pick up the electrical
signals produced by the brain. EEG provides an immediate assessment of the under-
lying neural activity with a high temporal resolution of a few milliseconds so is ideal for
researching the electrophysiological and cognitive states of the human brain. It is usually
used in diagnosing brain conditions, especially epilepsy and other seizure disorder but
has huge promise in revealing information about our emotional states as well [16]. One
technical challenge with EEG is its sensitivity to movements which is why when using
EEG for diagnosing brain disorders, a healthcare professional sits with the patient for the
entire recording session to note all the movements made by the patients to let the doc-
tor analyzing the EEG know which spikes are normal as they are caused by some body
movement. Since children are very active usually, it is not the best sensor to be used for
our problem for measuring anxiety in children. Another reason not to use it is that the EEG
sensors need electrodes to be put on your head which seems like a scary way to measure
anxiety in children that are not coming in with physiological symptoms like developmental
disorder, seizures etc. This has a high probability of making the children further anxious
and make the parents anxious as well who might contribute to the children anxiety during
the waiting period.

Electrocardiogram (ECG)
An electrocardiogram (ECG) is the measurement of electrical activity of the heart. Sensors
attached to the skin are used to detect the electrical signals produced by a our heart each
time it beats and the electrical activity of the heart is recorded. ECG machines are stan-
dard equipment in operating rooms and ambulances. Nowadays personal devices, such
as smartwatches, offer ECG monitoring. ECG is usually used to detect heart problems
like arrhythmia’s, blocked arteries etc but physiological variation of the interval between
consecutive heartbeats popularly known Heart-Rate variability can be estimated from a
continuous ECG signal as well which can be used monitor physiological states of human
brain along with diagnosing various diseases and clinical conditions.

2.8
Heart Rate Variability (HRV)
Heart rate variability (HRV) is the temporal variation between sequences of consecutive
heartbeats. A normal, healthy heart does not tick evenly like a metronome, but instead,
when looking at the milliseconds between heartbeats, there is constant variation. In gen-
eral, we are not acutely aware of this variation;it’s not the same as the heart rate (beats per
minute) increasing and decreasing as we go about our daily business. HRV is regulated by
the autonomic nervous system (ANS), and its sympathetic and parasympathetic branches,
and it is commonly accepted as a non-invasive marker of autonomic nervous system ac-
tivity. The sympathetic branch activates stress hormone production and increases the
heart’s contraction rate and force (cardiac output) and decreases HRV, which is needed
during exercise and mentally or physically stressful situations. Conversely, the parasym-
pathetic branch slows the heart rate and increases HRV to restore homeostasis after the
stress passes. This natural interplay between the two systems allows the heart to quickly
respond to different situations and needs.
The fluctuation in the time between our heartbeats can indicate different health problems.
The autonomic nervous system controls heart rate, heart rhythm and heart rate variabil-
ity, and is considered as a measure of cardiac autonomic innervation by the brain. It is
speculated that decreased parasympathetic innervation exposes the heart to unopposed
stimulation by sympathetic nerves [17]. Several health conditions increase sympathetic
output of the nervous system and create a self-perpetuating cycle that produces auto-
nomic imbalance and greater allostatic load (Allostatic load refers to the cumulative bur-
den of chronic stress and life events). Thus, Autonomic Nervous System dysfunction is
a systemic common denominator of poor health [9]. Anxiety disorders have also been
associated with reduced HRV.
HRV analysis is generally performed using ECG recordings that are longer than 24 h
(long-term HRV) or for recording around five minutes (short-term HRV) or for recording
lengths of less than 5 mins (ultra short term HRV). Long term HRV has a higher degree
of accuracy but short-term HRV analysis is more practical and simple to use and can
give us a continuous evaluation of mental state. So short term HRV seems ideal for our

If we talk about the different measures and indices that can be extracted from HRV, they
can be broadly categorized into three categories:
1. Time-domain indices: Time-domain indices measures the total variability in inter-
beat intervals (IBI)—the time intervals between successive heartbeats. This is done
through difference based indices and deviation based indices. Examples of time
domain features would be estimating the R-peaks, R–R intervals, the root mean
square of the successive differences (RMSSD) of the neighboring ECG R-peaks
etc.
2. Frequency- domain indices: Frequency- domain indices reflect the distribution of
power across different frequencies bands which can be useful as different regulatory
systems influence HRV at different frequencies. Examples of frequency indices
would Low Frequency(LF) and the High Frequency(HF). LF and HF can be analyzed
from ECG signal where HF is indicative of the parasympathetic system activation,
whereas LF is sensitive to both the sympathetic and parasympathetic systems.
3. Non-linear indices: Non-linear measures capture the non-linear chaotic fluctua-
tions of HR using analytical approaches based on entropy and fractal dimensions
which help capture random sequence of events which affect the HRV with an under-
lying deterministic structure. Examples of non-linear indices would be Detrended
fluctuation analysis (DFA) features which represent the correlations between suc-
cessive RR intervals over different time scales and Entropy indices which measures
the complexities or unpredictability’s of the signal [9].
2.9
Photoplethysmography (PPG)
Photoplethysmography (PPG) is a simple optical technique used to detect volumetric
changes in blood in peripheral circulation. It is a low cost and non-invasive method that
makes measurements at the surface of the skin. PPG makes uses of low-intensity in-
frared (IR) light. When light travels through biological tissues it is absorbed by bones,
skin pigments and both venous and arterial blood. Since light is more strongly absorbed
by blood than the surrounding tissues, the changes in blood flow can be detected by PPG
sensors as changes in the intensity of light.
PPG sensors integrated in wearable devices can be used to measure HRV as well. But
readings obtained through the ECG method have proven to be more accurate and com-
prehensive than those obtained through PPG. This is mainly because ECG reads the
electrical signals produced by the heart activity directly, while PPG provides a more indi-
rect measurement, using light reflection to detect the pulsating activity of the heart. PPG
measurements are influenced by the number and proximity of the blood vessels present
on the placement spot, which makes the accuracy of PPG change according to the body
position. The accuracy of PPG also depends on external factors, like the effect of ambi-
ence lighting and in some cases even motion factors. This means that PPG could become
less accurate in measuring HRV during exercise and may require additional data process-
ing steps to improve accuracy under challenging conditions.

Respiration(RSP)
Respiration signal is measured using sensors that detect chest or abdominal expansion/-
contraction and outputs the respiration waveform. The respiration signal provides infor-
mation on each breathing cycle. A breathing cycle is the process of inhaling and exhaling
air. Inhalation and exhalation onset points can be detected in the respiration waveform
to find breathing cycles which can be further processed to gain more information on res-
piration patterns. One such basic measurement would be the breathing rate which is the
number of breaths a person takes per minute. The rate is usually measured when a per-
son is at rest and simply involves counting the number of breathing cycles in a minute by
counting how many times the chest rises. Other features can be extracted from breath-
ing cycles like changes in inter breath intervals over time etc. Respiration signal or RSP
which gives insights into breathing patters is a known indicator of psychological stress and
anxiety and the respiration signal can be influenced by emotional events. The activities
of breathing in and breathing outcomes from the unconscious regulation of a metabolic
requirement, and expresses emotion involuntarily. There is a correlation between anxiety
levels and respiratory rate: the increase of minute ventilation involves individual anxiety
[10] Breathing rate rises when tension or worry increases. A lot of different features can be
extracted from respiration signals which are classified under the umbrella of Respiration
Rate Variability(RRV).

Respiration Rate Variability(RRV)
Respiratory rate variability (RRV) or Breathing Rate Variability (BRV) is the temporal vari-
ation within the respiratory rhythm. Respiratory rhythm is characterized by the rate of
breathing along with the tidal volume of breathing and changes in these are termed as
RRV. Respiratory rate variability (RRV) can be used as useful predictor of the deteriora-
tion of a patient’s condition. Some studies have shown its usefulness in the field of anxiety
detection as well[19] [20]

Electrodermal Response (EDA)
Electrodermal Response is a measurement of changes in the skin’s electrical conduc-
tance as a result of sweat generation. It is observed as changes in the resistance of the
skin to a small electrical current, or as differences in the electrical potential between dif-
ferent parts of the skin. It is often referred as galvanic skin response, or GSR as well and
has two parameters- skin conductance level, and skin conductance response. The EDA
signal reflects the action of sympathetic nerve traffic on eccrine sweat gland and is one
of the earliest tools used in psychological research and is extensively utilised as a stress
and anxiety indicator. EDA is sensitive to immediate emotional arousal as well as general
mood or acute stress responses.
Though EDA is a very effective technique for measuring emotion, its not that effective
signal for measuring in children. Children’s sweating rates are lower than those of adults
[21] and hence they dont produce much differences in EDA responses. There has been
studies which show no differences in EDA responses of children with psychological disor-
der like autism and normal children even when the children are exposed to sensory and
repetitive stimuli [22]. This makes the EDA signal not the most useful for our problem
statement of measuring anxiety in children.

Related Works
This chapter includes information on prior research in the field of measuring anxiety us-
ing different kinds of biosignals. Methodology used to find these researches have been
described followed by a summary and comparison of the found literature after they were
analyzed.
3.1
Methodology
Google Scholar and PubMed database were used to search for related literature. The
searches were made using a combination of words where the first word or words indicated
the emotion, the second word indicated some way of detection and third word indicated
some sort of biosignal. The first word used phrases like ”anxiety”, ”stress”, ”emotion”
etc while the second word used phrases like ”machine learning”, ”artificial intelligence”,
”detection”, ”measurement”, while the third word used phrases like ”biosignals”, ”signals”,
”wearables”, ”Skin conductance”, ”ECG”, ”Respiration” etc. Along with these searches,
references of the found articles were analyzed to find more relevant articles on this subject.
After a broad initial search, 215 articles were shortlisted from Google Scholar and 171
articles were shortlisted from the PubMed database. There were 22 duplicate articles
found when we compared the articles from the two databases and these were removed
leaving me with 364 articles. The articles ranged from 1977 to 2022. One article was
found as early as 1977 talking about anxiety detection through ECG but the majority of
articles were from after 2010.
Further I filtered the articles based on titles. Articles were removed if they used complex
technologies like radars, fMRI etc or were just carrying out a review of the existing state-of-
the-art techniques or were measuring something other than anxiety like personality traits,
social media use etc or were not done on humans or were measuring changes in HRV
by exposing anxious individuals to light or were protocols to reduce anxiety by exposure
therapy or predicting therapy outcomes.
After filtering based on titles, I was left with 268 articles. Further since the intended goal
was to detect anxiety, I further filtered the papers related to just negative emotions like
stress or anxiety or fear and filtered out all other papers measuring emotions in general,
leaving me with 167 articles.
After that abstract screening was carried out and, articles that were using machine learning
on questionnaire data, genetic data, speech, images, audio and other social data were
removed and only articles that using some sort of biosignals like ECG, RSP, EEG and skin
conductance along with machine learning were kept. This reduced the number of articles
to 76.
After abstract screening, full text screening was carried out and all articles having an im-
proper study design or unclear experiments like using data despite motion related issues,
lack of clear information on how data was labelled etc were filtered out. Additionally stud-
ies not mentioning anxiety at all were filtered out too along with 3 articles whose full text
couldn’t be retrieved.
In the end I was left with 19 articles that have been used in this literature review.

3.2
Summary and Comparison
The 19 scientific publications found in this literature have used different biomarkers (ECG,
EEG, RSP, and EDA) either individually or in combination of each other. The data ac-
quired from these biosensors was classified into either two classes (no anxiety vs. anxiety)
or multiple classes (low vs. mild vs. severe anxiety). After classification, in some cases
the biomarkers were directly fed into the neural network and in other cases some feature
extraction and selection was performed before feeding the data into some machine learn-
ing model like random forest(RF) or support vector machines(SVM). The models were
eventually evaluated based on one of the performance metric like accuracy, recall, preci-
sion or f1-score. Mainly accuracy has been used as the metric for performance in most
researches found in this literature review.
If we look at the biomarkers used in different researches, EEG and EDA signals have
been used 8 times each while ECG has been used 7 times, RSP has been used 5 times,
PPG has been used 4 times along with some other signals which were sporadically used
like Skin Temperature, Blood Oxygen. Most of the studies using EEG are done alone
except 2([23], [24]) which combine EEG with other biosignals. In case of studies using
signals other than EEG except Perpetuni[25] which uses only PPG; all other studies used
a combination of biosignals to predict anxiety.
Looking at the number of participant in the studies; Out of 18 studies, only 3 studies had 80
participants or more while 4 studies had around 55-57 participants. All the studies using

55-57 participants use the same dataset as well collected from spider fearful individuals.
It should also be noted that most studies do not report the number of male and female
participants and only one study[25] used gender as a feature to improve the performance
of the anxiety detection model.
In terms of what these studies were trying to measure, there were two kind of studies
that were observed among all articles. One kind was focused on trying to detect anxiety
disorder patients from healthy individuals while the other kind tried to separate periods
of anxiety from periods of rest. The anxiety disorder studies were done using a healthy
control group and an AD group and the biosignals of the participants were measured
during stressful and rest periods to make prediction of anxiety disorder. While the 2nd
kind of studies used either healthy participants or participants with a specific phobia and
tried to introduce anxiety in them through some anxiety inducing protocol with periods
of rest in between stressful periods. The biosignals of the participants were measured
during this and the researchers tried to detect the periods of anxiety and periods of rest
in the data.
Methods for inducing anxiety or stress included exposure therapy, social performance
task like giving a presentation or mathematical tasks or watching stressful videos or the
Trier Social Stress Test (TSST) [26]. All these studies had a similar setup where anxiety
inducing tasks and rest period were done cyclically, to record biosignals in anxious and
relaxing situations alike.
The labeling of data or ground truth of when the person is anxious/ not anxious was done
through a variety of ways in the found studies. Most studies did it based on the subject
and used some anxiety questionnaires like State-Trait Anxiety, Hamilton Anxiety rating
scale etc conducted after rest and stress periods. Others used video based labelling
or task based labeling of time series data. One study [24] used unsupervised learning
from EEG and ECG signals to label stress and non-stress periods. In case of Anxiety
Disorder studies, data was labelled on existing diagnosis or Social Interaction Anxiety
Scale Questionnaire [27].
After the data was collected and labelled, a similar pipeline was followed in all the papers.
Preprocessing of signals was done on collected data followed by a feature selection pro-
cess except in some cases of Neural Networks followed by a machine learning model to
make the predictions.
For EEG data, PLI connectivity algorithm, Brain Networks, Prefrontal Lateralization and
Recursive Feature elimination were some of the techniques used for feature selection.
For ECG, EDA and RSP data, sequential feature selection, along with correlation based
feature selection like Student-t-test were some of the popular techniques used for feature
selection.
In the end, the selected features were used as inputs to the machine learning algorithms.
Neural networks especially convolutional neural networks(CNN) were applied to both EEG
and other biosignals. Other popular methods used included Random Forest(RF) and
Support Vector Machines(SVM). Xu [28] used a cluster based approach where K means
was used to divide subjects into clusters and then generalized regression neural network
(GRNN) were used on clusters to make final predictions. In many cases, cross validation
techniques such as leave one out or 10-fold cross validation, were carried out to give out
validated results.

and non-anxiety categories. EEG seemed to be the most accurate unimodal biosignal
with the highest accuracy of 94 percent as seen in the Muhammad study [40]. While
ECG and RSP signal when combined with skin temperature achieved an accuracy of 77
percent in Vulpe study [35].
As explained in the background section 2.6, EEG is not ideal for our study because of
EEG’s sensitivity to motion artefact and high propensity of movement and activity in chil-
dren. Similarly EDA is not usable for our study because of lower sweat generation in
children and hence lower fluctuations in skin conductance as explained in EDA’s back-
ground 2.12.
Hence we can only rely on the accuracy results of the studies not using either of those
two signals as we intend to not use them as well. There were two studies not using either
EEG or EDA which are:
1. Vulpe study [35] which uses ECG, ST and RSP with an accuracy of 77 percent.
2. Perpetuini study [25] using PPG with an unreported accuracy.
Vulpe used extra short term HRV for measuring anxiety. On the other hand, Perpetuini
also uses pre-defined HRV features extracted from PPG mainly LF/HF (Low Frequency to
High Frequency ratio), RMSSD (Root mean square of square of successive differences
between normal heartbeats) along with systolic blood pressure and gender for anxiety
analysis. This indicates that its feasible to use HRV for our analysis of anxiety as well.

Problem Statement
Based on the literature review, it seemed like it was possible to measure anxiety using
machine learning on biosignal data like EEG, ECG, EDA, Respiration etc. But collecting
data using EEG and EDA didn’t suit our application of measuring anxiety in children. EEG
sensitivity to motion artecraft made it unsuable in children which are usually highly active
(Refer: 2.6) while EDA is not usuable as children don’t sweat enough reducing the varia-
tion in skin conductance (Refer: 2.12). Only 2 of the studies didn’t use EEG or EDA with
one of them not even reporting the accuracy of predicting anxiety or how to build such a
model.
Though there was diversity in the approaches that were used previously in the literature
if we look at all kinds of signals, the researches found didn’t really establish the efficacy
of the their approach. The comparisons found were limited to building subject specific
models or general models and using uni-modal or multi-modal signals. The studies didn’t
really analyze the data labelling techniques and whether they were closer to the ground
truth or not. Neither the studies experimented with different ways of feature selection. On
the contrary, in most cases information about the feature selection method used was not
available.
Another missing elements in the researches was that most of the studies divided the data
randomly in train and test sets and didn’t try to experiment with detecting anxiety in a new
participant whose data has not been seen before by the machine learning model. The
real test of an anxiety model is when they try to predict anxiety in a new participant and I
believe it essential to test this aspect of an anxiety model.
The researches also didn’t test the model on new anxiety inducing situation which was not
present in the training data. This is also essential to do to see how robust these models
are in measuring anxiety in all kinds of situation regardless whether the situation has been
fed into the model before or not. In real life anxiety comes in all colors and faces so an
anxiety measuring machine learning model should be able to measure anxiety in a wide
range of stimuli and should be able to detect anxiety even when it has not been exposed
to a certain stimuli.
Another important but easily ignored gap in the literature was that the researches were
mostly using data from anxiety disorder participants with a specific type of anxiety. People
diagnosed with AD might also be suffering from other psychological disorders that occur
concurrently. In findings by WHO, Anxiety and depression have been found to concur-
rently [41]. Most studies didn’t separate anxiety and anxiety disorder which is a serious
limitation for our use case as we want to measure the short term emotion of anxiety and
not anxiety disorder.
Also, the way data has been labelled in most researches doesn’t fit our purpose. A lot
of researches focused on labelling anxiety events from non-anxiety events by using self-
reported questionnaires. This would not be a great fit with children as they might not be
able to report their mental state accurately. So different methods need to investigated
which doesn’t involve self-reporting.
Finally another missing elements of the previous researches was that all of them had
assumed that at the end of the stimuli period, the participant stopped feeling the anxiety
emotion instantly and would be relaxed again. But this might not be the case in reality as
Prediction of anxiety using machine learning

there might be a lingering arousal period after the stimuli ends and the new stimuli might
take some additional few seconds before its able to capture the mind and emotion of the
participant.
So in this research, we have tried to touch upon all the limitations that have been found
in the literature.
The main larger goal is to be find out whether its possible to measure anxiety in healthy
participants using ECG and RSP signals. PPG signals have not been used as they like
ECG provide HRV metrics but are not as accurate as ECG (Refer: 2.9. As explained
before, EEG signals have not been used because of their high sensitivity to motion (Re-
fer:2.6) and EDA signals have not been used because of lower sweat generation in chil-
dren (Refer: 2.9). This makes ECG and RSP the most interesting non-invasive signals
for measuring anxiety in children. The goal further being, to test whether HRV parameters
for ECG signal and RRV parameters for RSP signal are viable for measuring anxiety or
not.
The following list breaks down the larger goal of measuring anxiety into smaller steps so
that we can achieve our larger goal:
1. Is it possible to measure anxiety in healthy individuals using biosignals?
2. What is the best biosignal to measure anxiety in ECG or RSP?
3. What is the best feature selection method for measuring anxiety in ECG and RSP

4. What are the most relevant HRV and RRV features that can be used for measuring

5. Is labelling biosignal data based on stimuli presented in form of videos a viable

3. Model’s performance on randomly split data. This will also aid comparison with
previous studies.
4. Model’s performance when taking into account a transitional period between anxiety
stimuli and non-anxiety stimuli. Different anxiety periods will be tested.

In this dataset, ECG and RSP signals were recorded simultaneously while the participants

scale data, participants age, gender and other demographic information was available as

6.1
Overview
The methods used for the experiments in this research followed a similar pipeline. The
first step was to process the signal. The ECG and RSP signals were first preprocessed
to remove noise and calculate relevant metrics from the signal. This was followed by
analysis of the preprocessed signal to extract Heart Rate Variability (HRV) features from
the ECG signal and Respiration Rate Variability(RRV) features from the RSP signal. The
extracted features were then either merged into a new combined feature set or were used
separately in the subsequent methods.
After extracting the features from the signal, a wide variety of feature selection methods
like Sequential Feature Selection, Pearson Coefficient method etc, were applied on the
extracted features dataset to select the most optimal features.
After selecting the best features, the feature set was divided into a train and test data
using a variety of ways. Either the dataset was divided randomly using Stratified K Fold
or one participant data was entirely taken out as the test set or a video data was entirely
taken out in order to predict the model’s robustness in different conditions.
After that the training set was used to train different machine learning models, mainly
Random Forest(RF), Support Vector Machines(SVM) and Xtreme Gradient Boost(XGB).
This was followed by evaluating the trained model on the test set. The last three steps
i.e data splitting, model training and model evaluation were repeated multiple times to get
cross validated results.

ECG Signal Preprocessing

The general pipeline for this preprocessing can be seen in the 6.4.
Figure 6.4: Pipeline for ECG signal pre processing
ecg_clean
The method was used to clean the ECG signal to remove noise and improve peak-detection
accuracy. 0.5 Hz high-pass butterworth filter (order = 5) was used for filtering, followed
by powerline filtering with powerline = 50.
ecg_peaks
The method was used to find R-peaks in the ECG signal using the neurokit method where
QRS complexes were detected based on the steepness of the absolute gradient of the
ECG signal. Subsequently, R-peaks were detected as local maxima in the QRS. The
method used unfiltered ECG signals as input.
ecg_rate
The method was used to calculate the signal rate (per minute) from a series of peaks.
Signal rate is computed as 60 / period, where the period is the time between the peaks.
ecg_quality
The function assessed the quality of the ECG Signal using the ”averageORS” method.
The ”averageQRS” method computes a continuous index of quality of the ECG signal, by
interpolating the distance of each QRS segment from the average QRS segment present
in the data. This index is therefore relative: 1 corresponds to heartbeats that are the
closest to the average sample and 0 corresponds to the most distant heartbeat from that
average sample. Note that 1 does not necessarily means “good”: if the majority of sam-
ples are bad, then being close to the average will likely mean bad as well.
ecg_dilineate
This method was used to delineate the QRS complex. QRS complex represents the dif-
ferent waves of the cardiac cycles. A typical ECG heartbeat consists of a P wave, a QRS
complex and a T wave. The P wave represents the wave of depolarization that spreads

Interval related analysis was carried out on both ECG and RSP signals. Interval-related
analyses was used to calculate features of signal variability and activation patterns like
average heart rate, breathing rate, heart rate variability (HRV) indices etc by dividing the
signal into a set of intervals and data of each interval was analyzed separately.
The intervals were created by separating the signal based on the video being watched by
the participant. A transitional period was introduced as well where a certain time period at
the beginning and end of a video was not included as part of the interval. This was done to
remove the effects of superimposition of emotions when transitioning between the anxiety
inducing video and the non-anxiety inducing video. It was hypothesized that during the
transition between videos, the participant was feeling a mixture of emotion which would
have made the data for that period unusable as it would not be possible to label its emotion
correctly. In order to test the efficacy of this hypothesis, different transitional periods were
tested in the experiments ranging from 0, 10 seconds at both beginning and end of the
video, 20 seconds at both beginning and end, 30 seconds at both beginning and end, 10
seconds in the beginning only, 20 seconds in beginning only, 30 seconds in beginning
only and 40 seconds in beginning only.

tion of events based on video beginning and ending time and the transitional period being

6.3
Feature Selection Methods
6.3.1
Feature Selection
Feature Selection is the method of reducing the input variable to the machine learning
model by using only relevant data and getting rid of noise in data. It is an important step
to build a robust machine learning model. One can collect a huge number of variables
or features to build a machine learning model but not all variables/features are useful in
making a prediction about the target variable, in our case the presence or absence of
anxiety. Having more variables increases the complexity of the model and acccording to
the principle of Parsimony or ‘Occam’s Razor’, the best explanation of a problem is the
one that is constructed with the smallest possible set of elements.
Furthermore, adding redundant variables reduces the overall accuracy of the classifier.
Poor-quality input will produce Poor-Quality output. Another reason for not using all fea-
tures for training is that this might lead to overfitting where we fit the model perfectly on
the training data but in the process we reduce the generalization capability of the trained
model. Thus, feature selection is an indispensable part of building machine learning mod-
els.
Another important reason why we used feature selection for our problem is because a lot
of extracted ECG features from time-domain, frequency-domain and non-linear domain
were highly correlated. Similarly RRV features had a high degree of collinearity within
them. Correlated features might not make our model necessarily worse but would end up
masking the model. It would become harder to interpret the effects of individual HRV and
RRV features due to the presence of multicolinearity.
The goal of feature selection techniques in machine learning is to find the best set of
features that allows one to build optimized models of studied phenomena.
6.3.2
Types of Feature Selection methods
Feature selection can be done in a supervised or an unsupervised manner.
• Supervised: Supervised feature selection methods uses the target variable data
for feature selection. These methods use the target variables to identify the input
variables which can increase the efficiency of the model.
• Unsupervised: Unsupervised feature selection are methods which don’t use the
target variable class for feature selection. These methods are usually used for un-
labeled data. For eg Factor Analysis based, PCA based etc
Further the supervised feature selection methods can be divided into three major buckets-
:
• Filter methods: Filter based methods use a filter metric with respect to the tar-
get variable and features are filtered based on the chosen metric. The metrics are
usually correlation based where its correlation of the input variables to the output
variable is calculated and features are selected accordingly. For Eg: Information
Gain, Chi-Square Test etc.
• Wrapper methods: Wrapper based methods are dedicated to searching the best
set of features for a problem. The data is broken into subsets and a machine learning
model is trained on that. Based on the test results of the trained model, features
are added or subtracted and the new model is trained. Subsets are formed using
a greedy approach and all possible combinations of features are evaluated in most
cases. This process is continued till we have the desired number of features. These
Prediction of anxiety using machine learning
55
==PAGE:66==
methods would redundant features that do not contribute directly to the performance.
Eg: Forward Selection, Backwards Elimination, etc.
• Embedded or Intrinsic methods: Embedded methods use algorithms that have
built-in feature selection methods. This reduces the computation power needed by
combining the qualities of both the Filter and Wrapper method to create the best
subset. Eg. Lasso and Random Forest have their own feature selection methods.

Mutual Information
Mutual Information is also a supervised filter based method. Mutual information measures
how much information the presence/absence of a feature contributes to making the cor-
rect prediction on the target. It encapsulates the information these two variables share
and how much knowing of one variable reduces uncertainty about the other. In essence
it measures the dependency between the two variables. Hence mutual information can
be used for feature selection. Mutual information of all input variable is calculated against
the target variable and the features with K highest scores are selected.
Recursive Feature Elimination with Logistic Regression
Recursive Feature Elimination is a wrapper type feature selection algorithm. A machine
learning algorithm is given and used in the core of the method, is wrapped by RFE, and
used to help select features. In this case, Recursive Feature Elimination is used by using
the Logistic Regression with ridge regression as the machine learning model. RFE is a
greedy optimization algorithm which aims to find the best performing feature subset. It
repeatedly creates models and keeps aside the best or the worst performing feature at
each iteration. It constructs the next model with the left features until all the features are
exhausted. It then ranks the features based on the order of their elimination.

Recursive Feature Elimination with Random Forest
In this method, Recursive Feature Elimination is used by using the Random Forest Clas-
sifier as the machine learning model. Random Forest Classifier has a in-built feature
importance method. Feature importance is calculated by using node impurities in each
decision tree. The final feature importance is the average of all decision tree feature im-
portance in the random forest. So when using Random Forest with RFE, at each step the
feature with the least feature importance is eliminated and in the end the required number
of features are selected based on the order of elimination.
Forward Sequential Selection
Forward Sequential selection is a supervised wrapper type feature selection. It is an it-
erative process where we begin with an empty feature set. In each iteration, we loop
over all of the remaining features and evaluate the performance of the model to check
if its increasing the performance or not, based on a pre-set evaluation criteria. The best
feature with the most increased performance is added to the feature selection set. This
process continues until the addition of a new feature does not improve the performance of
the model or we have the desired number of features we wanted to select. As mentioned
in our case we have selected wither 5 or 10 features. Random Forest Classifier with 500
trees is used as the machine learning model which is trained and evaluated at each step.
Backward Sequential Selection
Forward Sequential selection is a also a supervised wrapper type feature selection. It is
also an iterative approach like forward selection but it works in reverse by using elimina-
tion. This technique begins the process by considering all the features and removes the
least significant feature. This elimination process continues until removing the features
does not improve the performance of the model or we have the desired number of fea-
tures. Random Forest Classifier with 500 trees is used as the machine learning model
which is trained and evaluated at each step.
Extra Tree Classifier
The Extra Tree Classifier or the Extremely Random Tree Classifier is an ensemble algo-
rithm which aggregates the results of multiple de-correlated decision trees collected in
a “forest” to output it’s classification result. This machine learning model has an inbuilt
feature importance method. Similar to Random forest, node impurities in each decision
tree is calculated and averaged over all decision trees to get feature importance which is
used to select the required number of features.
PCA based
Principal component analysis, or PCA, is a dimensionality-reduction method that trans-
forms a large set of variables into a smaller one that still contains most of the information.
Along with reducing dimensionality, PCA can be used as a tool for feature selection. In
this method, the original dataset was reduced to k component (2* number of desired fea-
tures) using PCA. Since each variable contributes with varying degree to each principle
component, we selected the variable which was the topmost contributor to each princi-
pal component. Only one variable was selected from each principal component as the
input feature set was highly correlated. Also since the K principal components are ranked
by importance through their explained variance, we selected the n desired features from
the first n components. K(2*n) components initially were extracted to reduce any repeat
features. It should be noted that this this is a unsupervised approach where the target

Factor Analysis based
Factor analysis is a statistical method used to describe variability among observed vari-

express the relationship of each variable to the factor. Hence using a set number of

Random Forest
Random forest is a supervised machine learning algorithm that is constructed from de-
cision tree algorithms. It is an ensemble method that combines many decision trees to
predict the outcome. The outcome is based on the predictions of the decision trees by
taking the average of the output from various trees.
Decision trees are the building blocks of a random forest algorithm. A decision tree is
a decision support technique that forms a tree-like structure. A decision tree consists of
three components: decision nodes, leaf nodes, and a root node. A decision tree algorithm
divides a training dataset into branches, which further segregate into other branches. This
sequence continues until a leaf node is attained.
A random forest is created by a large number of decision trees. In every random forest
tree, a subset of features is selected randomly at the node’s splitting point. Increasing
the number of trees increases the precision of the outcome. A random forest eradicates
some of the limitations of a decision tree algorithm. It reduces the overfitting of datasets
and increases precision.

6.6
Model Performance
There are different metrics used for evaluating different models in this study. There are two
cases of classification for us mainly. One is condition positive(anxious) and the other is
condition negative(non-anxious). Both positive and negative is divided into true and false.
True means correct and false means incorrect. Therefore, the results of classification are
classified into four types: true positive (TP), false positive (FP), true negative (TN), and
false negative (FN). Using these four types, there are four popular metrics calculated for
evaluating machine learning models:
6.6.1
Accuracy
Accuracy is the most commonly used metric. Accuracy answers the question “Out of all
the predictions we made, how many were true?”
6.6.2
Precision
Precision is a metric that gives you the proportion of true positives to the amount of total
positives that the model predicts. It answers the question “Out of all the positive predic-
tions we made, how many were true?”
 
6.6.3
Recall
Recall focuses on how good the model is at finding all the positives. Recall is also called
true positive rate and answers the question “Out of all the data points that should be
predicted as true, how many did we correctly predict as true?”
6.6.4
F1 Score
F1 Score is a measure that combines recall and precision. As we have seen there is a
trade-off between precision and recall, F1 can therefore be used to measure how effec-
tively our models make that trade-off.
In our study, we have mainly focused on accuracy to aid comparison between different
experiments as when we use video splits, the test set has only one kind of label, either
anxiety or non-anxiety. So metrics other than anxiety fail to provide a reasonable compar-
ison.

9
Discussion
The results in this study are promising and shed light out on a lot of the goals we intended
to cover in this study. Let’s go through each individual goal as mentioned in chapter 4.
9.1
Measuring anxiety using biosignals
Looking at the results in the study, it does seem like it’s possible to measure anxiety in
healthy individuals using biosignal data. The best model accuracy was 75.11 when using
combined ECG and RSP signals were used with Best All feature selector for selecting
10 features with transitional period of 20 seconds both at the beginning and end of video.
This model accuracy was based on testing on new participants, testing on new videos
and testing randomly with a 33 fold cross validation (19 participant splits, 8 video splits
and 6 random splits). So this shows that if trained well, machine learning can be used on
biosignal data to test anxiety across new participants and new stimuli (videos). This also
shows that extracting HRV and RRV features from ECG and RSP signals respectively is
a viable approach to build such a machine learning model.
We are making this assertion not just based on the best model we were able to train. There
were 135 different kind of cross validated models that performed better than 70 percent
and 1286 cross validated models that performed better than 60 percent as seen in Figure
8.1. The baseline was 50 percent and only 47 models out of 1584 trained model did worse
than baseline. Seeing these results, it can be inferred that using ECG signals and RSP
signals and extracting HRV and RRV features from them, does help the machine learning
methods to estimate the psychological changes happening in our body due to external
stimuli.
9.2
Best biosignal to measure anxiety
There were three types of biosignals used in this study. Only ECG, only RSP and, both of
ECG and RSP. Looking at the results from the summary in section 8.5, it can be seen that
using both RSP and ECG signal does perform better but almost negligibly. ECG and RSP
combined, best accuracies for top 5 models were 75.11, 74.61, 73.50, 73.46 and 73.18
percent. While for ECG signal they were 75.07, 73.87, 73.85, 73.55 and 73.51 percent.
While for RSP signal the top 5 model accuracies were 71.80, 71.76, 71.55, 70.55, and
70.52 percent.
There seems to be negligible difference between the results of ECG and ECG+RSP biosig-
nals while RSP signals does show a slight reduced accuracy. So if we would have an
option to choose only 1 signal, then ECG signal would be the preferred choice. This ob-
servation shows that there is not much advantage gained when adding RSP signals to
the ECG signal. This might be because changes in respiration rate can be seen in the
HR and subsequently in HRV. Researches documenting the effect of respiration rate on
heart rate variability have shown that they are connected and deep breathing produced
faster, more variable HR, while shallow breathing had the opposite effects [62]. Also, Res-
piratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA), can be estimated from ECG signal which is the naturally
occurring variation in heart rate during the breathing cycle [47]. So changes in breathing
rate are probably encapsulated in different HRV measures.
Further if we look at the results based on different splits (Ref: 8.5, we see that ECG only
models have the best accuracy of 83.55 % in participant based splits, vs 82.24 % for

==PAGE:120==
combined signals. The RSP only model for participant based split have the best accuracy
of 76.97 % which is considerably lower than the other two. In random splits as well, the
ECG only models have the best accuracy of 80.87 % vs 78.92 % for combined models and
76.28 % for RSP only models. The difference is insignificant when we take into account
the error metrics but still shows that only ECG signal models are capable of detecting
anxiety.
But when we look at the video split results, the ECG only model had the best accuracy
of only 80.26 % vs 87.50 % in models with both signals and 86.84 % in RSP models.
This shows that RSP signal might be better in recognizing new stimuli which was not
part of the training data. This shows that a combined signal model is more robust in all
kinds of situation including detecting new stimuli of anxiety and detecting anxiety in new
participants.
Discussing the aspect of data collection for a bit, we saw in the preliminary noise test of the
two signals that the respiration signal had more noise than the ECG signal. The presence
of more noise was possibly because of higher impact of slight movement in the case of
respiration sensor. This makes respiration signals less reliable in real life as well. Also
in the results, we see as well that the ECG signals were sufficient for detecting anxiety
in most cases unless it was a totally new stimuli. This gives indication that a well robust
model trained on a lot of data from a a variety of anxiety inducing situations can work with
just ECG signal only and we might not need respiration signals at all. But if we don’t have
a lot of data, then a combination of signals is useful since it helps the model learn better
in case of a new never seen before anxiety situations.
Another important point to consider is that RRV metrics are not so much developed yet.
We were only able to extract 25 features from RSP signal compared to 85 signals from
ECG. Perhaps this makes the ECG signal more robust, as different kind of variations
can be captured through different measures, making them a better candidate for anxiety
research as of now.
9.3
Best feature selection methods for this data
Different feature selection methods were explored in this research. Out of 15 best overall
models regardless of signal used, RFE with Logistic Regression was used 6 times, Se-
quential Backward was used 4 times, Sequential Forward was used 2 times and Extra
Tree, Factor Analysis and Best All Selector all were used 1 time each. This shows a very
high preference for wrapper bases supervised methods.
When looking at just the ECG signal, RFE Logistic Regression was used all the 5 times
which shows that it was the best method for selecting HRV features.
When looking at just the RSP signal, Sequential Forward was used twice, Sequential
Backward was used two times and Factor Analysis was used once. This shows that
Sequential Selection works best in this case. Sequential Selection is a wrapper based
technique which uses a machine learning model (Random forest used in this research)
inside it to measure feature performance while sequentially adding or eliminating them.
Another promising result was the appearance of Factor Analysis in the top 5 models for
RSP signal. This shows that feature selection can be done in unsupervised way for RSP
signal, demonstrating the possibility of using unlabelled RSP data for feature selection in
RSP signal and the existence of natural clusters in RRV features that indicate an anxiety
response of our nervous system.
Finally when looking at combined ECG and RSP feature set, Sequential Backward was

Further if we look at only participant splits, Recursive Feature Elimination with Logistic

Selection was used in RSP model for all of the 5 times. For ECG and RSP models RFE

For Video splits, Sequential Forward was used all 5 times with ECG. For RSP signal,

All these results are highly indicative of the benefits of wrapper based methods over other
methods for HRV and RRV measures. But for video split test, Mutual Info was used
equally many times as wrapper methods but Mutual Info gave us better results. Since in
video split test, we tried to detect anxiety in new videos, this indicates that there is some
correlation in HRV and RRV measures in participants across different anxiety events as
correlation based feature selection method (Mutual Info) was able to detect new anxiety
events for the participants.
9.3.1
Best HRV and RRV Features for measuring anxiety
For HRV , HRV_ApEn, HRV_CSI, HRV_Ca HRV_DFA_alpha2 and HRV_Prc80NN were
the best features present in all of the top 5 ECG only models. Except HRV_Prc80NN
which is the 80th percentile of the RR intervals and a time-domain measure, all of the
other measures are non-linear measures. Also when we looked at ” the top 10 features
selected across all ECG only models”, HRV_ApEn, which is the approximate entropy was
selected almost twice as much as the next best feature. HRV_DFA_alpha2 was also
present in ”the top 10 features selected across all the ECG only models” but the other
three features were missing.
When we used combined HRV an RRV measures in our model, HRV_ApEn, HRV_GI
and HRV_VLF were used as HRV measures in the top 5 models. These three were also
present in the ”top 10 selected features across all models for the combined signal” and
”the top 10 selected features across all models for ECG signal”.
So if we combine both these results it shows that HRV_ApEn is the best feature for anxiety
detection. Other features that are most relevant are HRV_GI and HRV_VLF followed by
HRV_DFA_alpha2. This is followed by HRV_CSI, HRV_Ca and HRV_Prc80NN. All of the
above mentioned features are non-linear measures except HRV_Prc80NN (time domain)
and HRV_VLF (frequency domain).
If we look at features selected across all HRV models, the most selected frequency do-
main features were HRV_VLF, and HRV_LF and most selected time domain feature was
HRV_SDNN1. For anxiety research, time domain features appear relatively indiscriminate
when it comes to precisely quantifying the respective contributions of different underlying
regulatory mechanisms. However, they provide a slight general sensitivity to the analy-
sis. Non-linear features perform best carrying the most information. Frequency domain
features related to low frequency also seem to capture some information related to anxi-
ety. Within non-linear methods, Entropy-based methods perform the best. Entropy based
methods measures the complexity (or “unpredictability”) of a signal. They capture the ran-
domness and unpredictability in the signal, hence are probably able to capture the effects

of external stimuli in the most efficient manner. Since ApEn computes regularity based
on short time sequences, it is best suited to capture short-term HRV changes [47].
For RRV measures, RRV_LFn and RRV_ApEn were the best two measures appearing
in the top models of both RSP only signals and ECG+RSP signal models. Both these
features were also present in the ”top 10 most selected features across all RSP only
models” and ”top 10 features most selected across all RSP+ECG models”. RRV_Hfn
was another measure that appeared in top 5 models for RSP only that was also present
in the ”top 10 most selected features across all RSP only models” and ”top 10 features
most selected across all RSP+ECG models”. RRV_SD2SD1 was another relevant feature
present in top 5 RSP models and was present in ”top 10 most selected features across
all RSP only models”.
RRV_LFn and RRV_Hfn are frequency domain measures measuring the normalized low
frequency and normalized high frequency respectively. RRV_ApEn and RRV_SD2SD1
are non-linear measures. RRV_ApEn measures the sample entropy while RRV_SD2SD1
is the ratio between short and long term fluctuations of the breath-to- breath intervals. A
combination of non-linear and frequency domain measures seemed to be better suited
for RRV.
9.3.2
Best machine learning model for measuring anxiety
Moving on to the machine learning models, looking at adequate testing results over 33
fold, SVM models were chosen 7 out of 15 times for the top 15 models but RF showed
the best accuracy but by a negligible margin of 0.04 percent when compared to SVM.
When looking at participant split results, the results show that SVM model fairs better when
predicting anxiety in new participants. SVM is chosen the 9 out of 15 times for participant
splits and has the best accuracy as well amongst all models when tested on participant
splits which was 83.55 percent.
On the other hand, the XGB model performs better when predicting anxiety in video splits.
XGB is chosen 10 out of 15 times for video splits in the best models and has the highest
accuracy as well of 87.50 percent in case of video split test.
If we look at the signals, ECG signals gives its best performance with SVM models while
Random Forest is more preferred for RSP Models. The combined models of ECG and
RSP don’t show any preference in terms of model.
9.3.3
Importance of transitional period
In this research, we included a transitional period between anxiety and non-anxiety peri-
ods in the signal which was recorded continuously as participant watched anxiety inducing
videos and happy videos. This was done to test the inherent inertia because of which our
nervous system takes time to change from one state to another. Transitional period of 0
seconds was also tested along with 10 seconds, 20 seconds , 30 seconds etc
When we look at the result of our best models, we see that transitional period of 30 sec-
onds at beginning only (trans 30_begin_only) was used most while all of the models pre-
ferred having a transitional period of at least 10 seconds at the beginning of a video. Also,
most models preferred having a transitional period at the beginning only and not the end.
This is in line with our expectations as we thought a transitional period is necessary only
at the beginning of the video when a new stimuli is being introduced into our nervous
system. This also indicates that there is a lingering residual arousal after a stimuli is not
presented anymore.

9.3.4
Do we measure anxiety?
Most interesting results of this study, was when the target labels of anxiety were random-
ized i.e we labelled on random whether the video would introduce anxiety or was non-
anxiety inducing video. The results show that there was no statistical difference when
the anxiety labels of the videos were randomized and the models were still able to guess
the new anxiety labels with fairly good accuracies. This indicated that the model was
just predicting the video that is being watched rather than anxiety label. Further when
we used video number as target label, significant difference was seen in the accuracies
of the prediction. But since video number was a multi-class classification with 8 classes,
the baseline was 12.5 % in this case compared to 50% for the other cases. This meant
the video number label results were still very promising, increasing the accuracy of the
classification by many folds. This means that the HRV and RRV measures and our ma-
chine learning models, indeed are capturing the similarity of human biological reaction to
the videos. This could be anxiety or arousal or any other thing and it cannot be said with
surety what emotion is actually being measured in the models.
So it can be said that the models are able to cluster similarly labelled videos together and is
able to predict similar biological responses invoked in the video. The biological response
could be valence or arousal but we don’t know what it is currently. This finding raises
doubt on just using videos as a method for labelling data in anxiety studies. A better way
needs to be developed for labelling data or unsupervised machine learning models needs
to be used in anxiety studies. Salivary Cortisol was an effective way for data labelling
in one of the studies in the literature review. This could give us real time information on
confirmed presence of stress or anxiety and would be a very useful technique for labelling
data. If the data is labelled correctly, there should be no issue with measuring anxiety as
it is seen in this research that HRV and RRV parameters are effective in quantifying our
psychological state.
9.3.5
Other Observations
Another interesting observation was that the model’s accuracy in predicting anxiety seem
to be independent of the baseline anxiety level measured in the participants using Beck
and Hamilton scales. Our theory initially based on preliminary results was that maybe
some participants are more prone to fluctuations in their ECG and RSP signals which
made it easy for the model to predict their anxiety. But this wasn’t found to be the case
when we kept the feature set consistent across all participants. Also at some point, we
did include the Hamilton scores and beck scores to the feature set but they were never
selected during feature selection making them redundant.
9.3.6
Comparison with previous literature
Our results are in line with what previous studies have reported. Vulpe study [35] which
uses ECG, ST and RSP had an accuracy of 77 percent while using a 1D CNN as the
machine learning model. This was the only study that was really comparable to our case
as all the other studies relied on EEG or EDA data as well. Perpetuini study [25] uses
PPG and calculates HRV features on it but the accuracy of this study was not reported.
Vulpe and Perpetuini both used short term HRV for measuring anxiety. Our results also
show that its possible to use short term HRV to measure anxiety.
Our results are in line in what was observed in the Vulpe study. On random splits we were
able to achieve an accuracy of 80.87 percent compared to 77 percent in Vulpe study. This
is slightly better than the Vulpe study. On new participants our best accuracy results were
83.55 percent which is even higher. It should also be noted that we are using explainable
machine learning methods like RF, SVM with feature selection while Vulpe used a deep

Since we tried testing our models on new videos as well, not present in training data, our

If we don’t exclude the other studies using EEG and EDA, a combination of EDA , PPG

performance.
9.3.7
Limitations of this study
There were a lot of limitations of this study. First of all we just had data for 19 partici-
pants which was minuscule. Large amount of data for over 100 participants need to be
collected for a better study. Another limitation was that the individuals were healthy and
in comfortable environment. The ECG and RSP signals response would be much differ-
ent when they are in a hospital or have some other ailing disease which might have an
affect on HRV as well. So data needs to be collected in a hospital like environment to be
able to build a better model. The quality of the data would eventually decide the quality
of the model. But these findings in this study do show that its possible to build a anxiety
detection model using ECG and RSP signals. The models is able to put similar responses
into categories and is able to detect these categories. The challenging part is labelling
the data correctly where we can be confident that anxiety is invoked or anxiety is absent
during different time windows while collecting data using these biosignals.
In terms of videos as stimuli, it was observed that the anxiety model in case of combined
ECG and RSP signals was doing better for non-anxiety videos than anxiety videos except
for the anxiety inducing car accidents video. After I went through all the videos, it seemed
like among all the anxiety videos, only the car accident video was extreme enough to in-
voke anxiety across all participants. The other anxiety videos of a disabled kid, orphan
and natural disasters possibly didn’t invoke consistent anxiety response across all partic-
ipants. On the other hand, the positive videos, were full of happy words and pictures and
relaxing and happy music all across the board.
A caveat to this accuracy results is that the feature selection methods except the unsu-
pervised methods had a look at the entire dataset for feature selection so there might be
some data leakage. We did start with having feature selection inside the cross validation
fold but since we wanted to compare the individual participants and see how they did when
there data was left out, we wanted a consistent feature set for all settings and not one that
is changing over each iteration. This would give us a better insights on the features being
selected and provide consistency across all results. So in the end we decided to move the
feature selection outside the cross validation fold. We did want to run experiments later
with, keeping the feature selection inside the cross validation fold but due to lack of time
and computational resources needed to train all the models for all the settings and get all
the results again, we report a slightly biased version of accuracy results. Though when we
look at the unsupervised methods, they still show a pretty good accuracy of around 71.55
percent compared to the best accuracy of 75.11 percent so the amount of bias introduced
should be minimal.

Future recommendations
In future, it would be interesting to see that if the results found can be replicated using
other datasets especially a large dataset collected with accurate labels of anxiety inducing
method. One such method would be the use of Salivary Cortisol strips to measure anxiety
or stress levels during the experiment. This could help build more accurate anxiety models
as it can be seen in the research that HRV and RRV features are able to detect underlying
emotions in our nervous system, by grouping the common structures together. It all comes
down to labelling the data accurately.
Some recommendation for collecting data in the future would be as follows:
1. Collect biosignals from a large number (>100) of study subjects) need to be used
for study validation.
2. Have more diversity in the subjects in terms of age and gender.
3. Have a consistent and accurate way of labelling data where we know for sure that
the anxiety is invoked at a certain point. One such effective method would be to use
Salivary Cortisol test
4. Collect data in an actual hospital setting.

Conclusion
Overall, our hypothesis about being able to measure anxiety using biosignals like ECG
and RSP seems plausible. Our best model was able to predict anxiety with an accuracy
of 75.11 percent. HRV and RRV features extracted from ECG and RSP seem to be able
to cluster different psychological responses and classify it in anxiety and non anxiety. Fur-
ther it was seen that only ECG signals was sufficient in most cases for measuring anxiety
though the combined model of ECG and RSP did perform better across all kinds of situa-
tions including new participants and new stimuli. When we looked at the feature selection
methods, we found that wrapper based feature selection techniques were best suited for
selecting features from HRV and RRV. The best feature found for measuring anxiety was
HRV_ApEn which is the approximate Entropy which was best able to predict anxiety. The
labelling of biosignal data using video labels doesn’t seem to be the most effective tech-
nique to label anxiety data. Further in our experiments, we see that stimuli has a delayed
emotional response in our nervous system and use of a transitional period could help
deal with those challenges when classifying anxiety. The main challenge found in mea-
suring anxiety was correct labelling of data. Otherwise it should be very much possible to
measure anxiety in healthy individuals.
