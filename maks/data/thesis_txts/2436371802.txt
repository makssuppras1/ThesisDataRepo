Abstract
The balanced armature loudspeaker is the standard in the hearing aid industry due to
its eﬃciency and small form factor.
Weak points of this type of transducer are the
robustness and the non linearities. The recent advancements in fabrication processes
and the rolling out of the ﬁrsts MEMS loudspeakers for audio application may provide
interesting alternatives in the near future.
This study will provide an eﬃciency-wise comparison between diﬀerent topologies of
miniature loudspeakers for the use in hearing aids with the purpose of identify such
potential alternatives to the balanced armature loudspeaker.
For all the loudspeakers analyzed, a simple linear equivalent circuit will be presented and
will be used to derive equations to describe their eﬃciency. Samples of loudspeakers of
diﬀerent topologies will be measured to ﬁnd the value of the components in the equivalent
circuit.
Space is one critical factor in a hearing aid device. For this reason the loudspeakers
analyzed, except for the reference, i.e.
the balanced armature loudspeaker, will be
scaled so the comparison of the eﬃciency will be performed on devices that occupies
the same volume. To do so is necessary to know how to scale the components of the
equivalent circuit with the dimensions. Analytical expressions and FEM simulations
will be used to describe such relations.
After scaling the components of each topology, the eﬃciency of each one of them will
be calculated on the scaled geometry and compared with the eﬃciency of the balanced
armature loudspeaker.

1
Introduction
In the hearing aid industry, the balanced armature loudspeakers are the standard, mostly
for their eﬃciency and small form factor. The drawback of this topology is the robust-
ness: these devices are sensitive to humidity and do not behave well when dropped.
When this happen, the armature may rest in a position that is close to one of the
magnets which results in an increased distortion.
The volume and electrical power available in a hearing aid device are critical aspects
that must be considered when choosing the transducer. In the small space available
inside a hearing aid, the battery, the electronics, the ampliﬁer, the microphones and the
loudspeaker must be ﬁtted which put heavy constraints on the choice of the transducer.
In recent years, advancements in the fabrication process and the production of the
ﬁrsts MEMS (Micro Electro-Mechanical System) loudspeakers could lead to attractive
alternatives to the balanced armature loudspeaker in the near future. MEMS devices
are really attractive due to their small size and batch production. The ﬁrst piezoelectric
MEMS loudspeaker is now under production [1], the ﬁrsts prototypes of electrostatic
MEMS loudspeaker are available for earphones manufacturers to test, although their
data sheet is still conﬁdential, and the research for a moving coil MEMS loudspeaker
is now at an advanced stage [2]. These loudspeaker are not suitable yet for the use in
hearing aids:
• The piezoelectric transducer needs a power supply providing 30 VDC for proper
working condition, which is not suitable for a hearing aid, and an adequate power
ampliﬁer.
• The electrostatic transducer is provided with a transformer in order to use a normal
power ampliﬁer to drive it. This transformer is much larger than the loudspeaker
itself which makes it too large for a hearing aid device. In addition, this transducer
has been designed to be used as a tweeter in high end earphones so it covers a
smaller frequency range than the one required by a hearing aid.
• The moving coil transducer is still in a developing stage and at the moment its
main issues are the eﬃciency and the distortion.
It is clear that at the moment these topologies are not a real alternative to the balanced
armature but things could change in the near future.
In this thesis some of these
topologies are analyzed and their eﬃciency is compared to the reference that is the
balanced armature. The purpose is to see if one of these alternatives might be worth to
further research to provide an alternative to the actual standard.
1
==PAGE:16==
In this thesis four topologies of loudspeaker will be analyzed: moving coil, piezoelectric,
electrostatic and balanced armature that will be used as reference. This work will ﬁrst
analyze the transduction principle exploited by each of the topologies under study and
a lumped parameter model, i.e. an equivalent circuit, will be proposed. While for most
of the topologies analyzed here there is extensive information in the literature, this is
not the case for the piezoelectric loudspeaker at hand. This topology will require a
longer derivation that has been performed with a strong help of Ole Hansen from DTU
Nanotech.
These equivalent circuits will then be used to derive an expression that will allow to
calculate their eﬃciency. The diﬀerent topologies will be compared eﬃciency-wise be-
cause of the limited electrical power available in a hearing aid. The pressure generated
in a close volume such as the ear canal is of main importance but just calculating the
maximum pressure generated is not the ideal approach because it might be the case that
the power needed to generate such pressure is not available. This is why the eﬃciency
is considered to be a much better criterion in this case. To evaluate the eﬃciency in
generating pressure in a closed volume, the acoustic impedance of a 2cc coupler will
included as the acoustic load in the equivalent circuits.
The eﬃciency of the diﬀerent topologies can not be directly compared. Because also the
space is a critical parameter when choosing a loudspeaker, each topology will be scaled so
to occupy the same volume as the balanced armature loudspeaker used as reference. To
do so, it will be necessary to know how each of the components in the equivalent circuits
scale with the dimensions. In some cases there are analytical expression that can be used.
For some of the components this is not possible either because there is no analytical
expression or because they are not suitable for this task. In such cases FEM (Finite
Element Method) simulations will be performed in COMSOL and their solutions will
be imported in the Curve Fitting Toolbox in MATLAB to ﬁnd an analytical expression
that can be used.
Finally, a miniature moving coil (not the MEMS one mentioned before), a balanced
armature and a MEMS piezoelectric loudspeaker will be measured to identify the value
of the components in their equivalent circuits.
The components will then be scaled
and the resulting eﬃciency compared. For the measurements, the loudspeaker will be
coupled to a 2cc coupler which has a well know acoustic impedance that can be modelled
in the equivalent circuits with relative ease.
A ﬁnal note regarding the piezoelectric and the electrostatic transducers: both of these
loudspeaker are brand new and there is still a lack of detailed information about them.
This lack of information leaded to a limited accuracy of the model of the piezoelectric
loudspeaker. In the case of the electrostatic transducer, due to diﬃculties during the
measurements and limited information, it was not possible to included it in the compar-

2
Theoretical Background
In this section will be presented the theoretical background for each topology. The ﬁrst
step will be to obtain the equations that govern the transduction principle. Then, the
physical devices will be analyzed to provide an equivalent circuit to describe it. Using
such circuit, an expression will be derived for the input electric impedance and the
pressure transfer function. Such expression will then be used to calculate the eﬃciency
for each topology.
2.1
Lumped Parameters Models
A lumped parameter model consists of modelling a system using discrete electrical com-
ponents that are capable to capture its main features. This approach can be used to
model system of diﬀerent nature such as: magnetic, mechanical, acoustical, thermal,
etc. These technique uses the so called conjugate power variable to model the main
variables of the system. These variables are called this way because are chosen in a way
that their product gives as result the power dissipated or stored by the system or one
of its components.
In an electrical system these two variables are voltage and current that are also generi-
cally referred to as across/eﬀort and through/ﬂow variables, respectively. In a mechan-
ical system these two variable are force and velocity. For an acoustic system pressure
and volume velocity are used. In this thesis, force and pressure are the across variables
and velocity and volume velocity are the through variables. This is called an impedance
analogy. Is also possible to deﬁne these variables the other way around leading to an
admittance or mobility analogy.
When an impedance analogy is used, is possible to model compliance and stiﬀness as
capacitor, masses as inductors and damping as resistors. This is true for both mechanical
and acoustic systems.
Through this approach is also possible to couple systems of diﬀerent nature.
This
coupling can be done in two diﬀerent ways: using dependent sources or transformers
and gyrators. In this thesis the dependent sources have been used.
A ﬁnal and important note is that these models have limitations and can only simulate
a system with a ﬁnite accuracy. In an acoustic model, for instance, the frequency range
of interest and the dimensions of the system constitute a limitation to the validity of the
model and are only accurate when there is no propagation, i.e. the smallest wavelength
under study is much larger than the dimension of the system.

2.2
Moving Coil Transducer
The moving coil transducer is probably the most widely spread and well known type of
electro-acoustic transducer. Because of this and the fact that this type of transducer
is now more than a hundred years old, many diﬀerent designs have appeared. In this
work, only miniaturized moving coil loudspeakers will be analyzed. Miniature moving
coil have the same transduction principle and topology as the bigger units but also some
unique features:
• They lack a spider, making this design more prone to rocking motion, i.e. the
moving coil moves out of axis aﬀecting the frequency response and increasing the
risk of damaging the motor system.
• Lack of a separated suspension. Because of the reduced space, is not practical to
attach the diaphragm to a separated rubber or foam suspension. The diaphragm
is shaped in way that allows its outer section, the one attached to the chassis, to
provide the needed compliance.
• They usually come in a package that helps protecting the delicate membrane of
the diaphragm and the rest of the components. This package is designed to be as
acoustically transparent as possible so to not aﬀect the frequency response.
• Some designs may lack a proper magnetic circuit, having only a cylindrical perma-
nent magnet sitting at the center of the coil or with a toroidal shape surrounding
the coil. In some designs, as the one under study, the package is used to properly
direct the magnetic ﬁeld and focus it in the air gap.
2.2.1
Transduction Principle and Governing Equations
This transducer consists of a permanent magnet placed in a magnetic circuit which
generate a static magnetic ﬁeld B. In this magnetic ﬁeld sits a coil that is attached
to a voltage source. When a current i ﬂows through the coil, this will experience a
force F due to the law of Laplace [3]. Polar pieces made of soft magnetic material such
as iron are used to direct the magnetic ﬁeld in a way that, ideally, the line ﬁelds are
perpendicular to the coil at every point. This results in a force normal to the plane
made by the lines of the magnetic ﬁeld and the coil. Because of this conﬁguration, the
vectorial character of the quantities involved can be omitted using simple scalars. The
force acting on the coil can be written as follow:
F = Bli
(2.1)

Where l is the length of the wire. The movement of the coil inside the magnetic ﬁeld in-
duces a voltage in the wire. This voltage, usually referred to as back EMF (electromotive
force), is given by:
E = Blu
(2.2)
These two equations describe the coupling between the electrical and mechanical com-
ponents. The product between the B ﬁeld around the coil and the length of the wire l is
the transduction coeﬃcient in this topology of loudspeaker and is usually referred to as
force factor. It must be said that these equations are just an approximation that is valid
for small displacement of the coil where the B ﬁeld around the coil can be considered
constant. When the displacement gets larger, the B ﬁeld will not be the same on all
the windings of the coil anymore and the transduction coeﬃcient will depends on the
position of the coil. This dependency on the position of the force factor is one of the
source of distortion in this type of loudspeaker.
2.2.2
Equivalent Circuit
The structure of a typical miniature moving coil loudspeaker is represented in Fig.(2.1).
The gray area in the ﬁgure represents the permanent magnet. Above the magnet lay the
diaphragm that in this miniature transducers is usually made out of plastic. The outer
section of the diaphragm is not ﬂat in order to provide some compliance and act as a
suspension. The coil is attached to the bottom surface of the diaphragm. The package
that protects the transducer is also represented with its front and back holes.

section (right).
The moving coil loudspeaker can be represented by the equivalent circuit shown in
Fig.(2.2).
The electrical part (a) includes the resistance Re and the inductance Le
provided by the coil and the back EMF that is modelled with a current dependent
voltage source.

(c) acoustical.
The variable current in the wire will produce a magnetic ﬁeld that induces a current
in the polar pieces and such current sets another variable magnetic ﬁeld that alters the
eﬀective magnetic ﬂux in the air gap. Because of this, at high frequencies, the coil does
not behave as a pure inductor and many models have been proposed to include this
eﬀect in the circuit. One of them make use of fractional derivatives and the impedance
corresponding to the inductance can be written as:
ZLe = (jωn)Le
(2.3)
Where n is a scalar with a value between 0 and 1. When its value is 0, the impedance
correspond to a pure resistor. When its value is 1, the impedance corresponds to a pure
inductor. If the value is in between of those two extreme, the impedance models a lossy
inductor.
The circuit represents the mechanical components (b) with an impedance analogy: the
mass of the diaphragm is represented with the inductor MMD, the compliance of the
suspension with the capacitor CMS and the damping of the suspension with the resistor
RMS. The circuit is driven by a current dependant voltage source which represents the
force due to the law of Laplace which drives the coil and the rest of the mechanical
components.

Considering the frequency range of interest, which is the operating range of a hearing
aid (100 −10kHz), the holes at the front of the package can be considered acoustically
transparent. At the front, a big hole is placed at the center and in front of the diaphragm.
Such hole is almost as big as the diaphragm and placed at a short distance from it and
its eﬀect on the propagation of acoustic waves is negligible. Smaller holes are placed
close to the outer edge of the package, right above the suspension. At the back of the
package, smaller holes can be found below the suspension. The scope of these holes is
to reduce the acoustic stiﬀness provided by the rear cavity that would largely limit the
displacement of the diaphragm, then its sensitivity and eﬃciency. Because of the really
small thickness of such holes, they will hardly provide an acoustic mass to the acoustic
impedance seen by the diaphragm and/or acoustic damping because of the lack of wall
surface where acoustic losses can occur. Similarly to a condenser microphones, some
losses could be found in the back volume due to friction between the air and the walls
of the package. Such losses only aﬀect the back volume due to the small size of both
the volume itself and of the holes. At the front, the holes are much larger and occupy
a larger percentage of the frontal surface of the package reducing the surface available
for the losses to occur. In addition, the bigger volume at the front also result in a lower
particle velocity which also lead to less losses. In this regard the losses at the front have
been ignored. The losses at the back are modelled by the resistor RAB but it will not
be possible to separate them from the mechanical losses and scale them accordingly.

2.3
Balanced Armature Transducer
The balanced armature loudspeaker is the transducer of reference when it comes to
hearing aids.
Thanks to their small size, the high output and diaphragm that are
resonance-free for most of the audio range of interest made them the ﬁrst choice for small
devices such as hearing aids. In Fig.(2.3) is represented the cross section of a balanced
armature loudspeaker. This speaker consist of an armature, that can be modeled as a
clamped-free beam, with its free end placed between two magnets, which sets a static
magnetic ﬂux in the armature. In steady state, the armature is equally attracted by
the two magnets.
Close to the clamped end, where there is small or no movement,
a coil surrounds the armature. When a current ﬂows through the wire, an additional
10
==PAGE:25==
magnetic ﬂux is generated that modify the magnetic force between the magnets and the
armature increasing the pull towards one of the magnets and resulting in a displacement
of the armature. With an alternate current the armature will start vibrating. At the
end of the beam, beyond the magnets, the drive pin connect the tip of the beam to the
diaphragm, that converts the mechanical vibrations to pressure changes. The diaphragm
is placed between a back volume, which contains the armature and the magnets, and a
front volume that is usually much smaller than the back volume and is connected to the
spout, where sound is radiated from.
Figure 2.3: Section of a balanced armature loudspeaker (from [4]).

2.4
Piezoelectric Transducer
This type of transducers are based on the so called inverse piezoelectric eﬀect to trans-
form electrical energy into mechanical energy. Piezoelectric materials present a crystal
lattice structure that, when a load is applied, deforms generating small dipoles [5]. The
macroscopic charge inside the material is neutral because the charges of the diﬀerent
dipoles cancel out. At the surfaces, the material is polarized and present a charge distri-
bution which generates an electric ﬁeld. This eﬀect is called direct piezoelectric eﬀect.
The opposite eﬀect is also possible: if a voltage is applied at the surfaces of the material,
the crystals inside will re-orient themselves producing a mechanical deformation.
Depending on the lattice structure of the material, there are preferential directions
for the electric polarization to occur. If a material is deformed in a certain direction
and the charge distribution appears on surfaces perpendicular to the direction of the
deformation, the eﬀect is called direct longitudinal piezoelectric eﬀect. If the charge
distribution appears on surfaces parallel to the direction of the deformation the eﬀect
is called direct transversal piezoelectric eﬀect [6]. The transducer under study employs
the inverse transversal piezoelectric eﬀect.
Piezoelectric material can have either monocrystalline or polycrystalline structures [7].
The ﬁrsts are highly symmetric while the seconds are made by multiple crystals with
diﬀerent orientations. The material used in the transducer under study is PZT (lead
zirconate titanate) which is a type of piezoelectric ceramic which have a polycrystalline
structure. Piezoelectric ceramics can present diﬀerent non linear eﬀect: dependency on
the dielectric constant on the strength of the applied electric ﬁeld, aging which causes
16
==PAGE:31==
changes in the piezoelectric constants with time, piroelectricity which causes charge
distributions to appear on the ceramic surfaces because of changes in temperature, etc.
[8]. The PZT is one of the piezoelectric ceramic that are less prone to the aging process
and presents smaller non linear eﬀects in general.
In this section, for the mathematical derivation of the equations governing the trans-
duction principle, the nomenclature used is the typical used in solid mechanics.

3
Simulations
As it was stated in section 1, in order to properly compare the eﬃciency of the diﬀerent
topologies under study is necessary to scale their components. This is done so the eﬃ-
ciency is computed for the diﬀerent topologies when they occupy the same volume. The
space occupied by the loudspeaker is critical in a hearing aid, where many components
have to ﬁt in a very reduced space.
To properly scale the eﬃciency of the loudspeakers is necessary to know how each
component in the equivalent circuit scales with the dimensions. For some component
there are analytical expressions that can be used. Nonetheless, the relation with the
scale for most of the elements in the equivalent circuits are not so evident. In order
to investigate these relations, a few simulations have been performed using COMSOL.
Through the simulations, numerical solutions were found to the problem at hand. Such
numerical solutions have then been imported in MATLAB so that, by using the Curve
Fitting Toolbox, has been possible to ﬁnd an analytical expression ﬁtting the numerical
results and that could be used to properly scale the corresponding components.
3.1
Acoustic Impedance of a 2cc Coupler
A 2cc coupler has been used in the pressure measurements and as a common acoustic load
for all the topologies under study. In the equivalent circuits the presence of the coupler
has been accounted for by using a capacitor assuming that the air in the closed volume
can be modelled as a simple acoustic spring. This simulation provides a frequency range
where this assumption is valid for the 2cc coupler and a mean to extend its lumped
parameter model to cover the entire working range of a hearing aid.
The coupler was simulated in COMSOL using a simple 2D axisymmetrical model. The
coupler has a radius of 1.8 cm and a height of 1.96 cm as shown in Fig.(3.1). The
acoustic performance of the coupler was analyzed using two diﬀerent physics: Pressure
Acoustics and Thermoviscous Acoustics. In this way will be possible to compare the
acoustic impedance of the coupler with and without acoustic losses.

The boundary conditions used in the simulation consisted of inﬁnite hard walls for all

Acoustic Losses in a Closed Volume
A balanced armature loudspeaker and a single-end electrostatic loudspeaker under study
have a very small back volume. When a closed volume is so small, acoustic losses can
not be neglected a priori. The thickness of the viscous and thermal boundary layer,
as explained in section 3.1 starts becoming relevant if compared to the volume. This
simulation in COMSOL study how the acoustic losses scales with the dimensions of the
closed volume.
This simulation is very similar to the simulation used to analyze the coupler in section

shown in Fig.(3.19). To study the acoustic losses was necessary to use the Thermoviscous
acoustic physics and the boundary conditions consisted of inﬁnite hard walls on all the
boundaries except for the upper one, where a prescribed velocity of u = 1m/s was
applied simulating the displacement of the diaphragm.

volume is not much bigger than the volume occupied by the boundary layer and most
of the internal surface is parallel to the direction of propagation of the acoustic wave.

3.4
Stiﬀness and Damping of a Circular Suspension
For many simple geometries are available expressions that describe the deﬂection when
a load is applied. Using this expressions or solving the bending equation allow to extract
the stiﬀness of the structure. The geometry of the suspension of a miniature moving coil
loudspeaker is not simple enough and there are no expression in the literature that can
be used to describe how its stiﬀness changes with its scale. For this reason and also to
ﬁnd a relation between the mechanical damping and the scale factor has been necessary
to set another simulation.
There are many deﬁnitions of mechanical damping in the literature [12] and not all of
them are suitable for this study. One interesting way of representing the mechanical
damping is through the loss factor η [13]. This measure has been introduced to describe
the intrinsic damping of viscoelastic materials. The loss factor can be used in COMSOL
by deﬁning a complex Young’s Modulus:
Y = Y ′(1 + jη)
(3.10)
Where Y is the complex Young’s modulus, Y ′ is the storage modulus, i.e. the regular
Young’s modulus, and η = Y ′′/Y ′ is the loss factor with Y ′′ being the loss modulus, as
referred to in the literature.
The main reason behind the decision of using the loss factor is that it is a property
of the material and does not change with its dimensions. The drawback is that it has
to be related with the damping coeﬃcient RMS in the circuit shown in Fig.(2.2). If
this is done analytically would result in a damping coeﬃcient that depends on both
the frequency and the scale factor. This is not really practical for the study at hand
and would require a much deeper study that goes beyond the scope of this project. As
an approximation, the complex Young’s modulus will be used to obtain the mechanical
impedance provided by the suspension as a function of the dimensions of the geometry.
The damping coeﬃcient RMS will be obtained from the value of the real part of the
impedance at the mechanical resonance. At this frequency, the imaginary part of the
impedance is zero and the damping is the only component left meaning that is critical
to have a well deﬁned damping coeﬃcient at this frequency.
For this study, a simple suspension has been simulated in COMSOL using the geometry
represented in Fig.(3.27) in a 2D axisymmetrical model. In this simulation, both the
radius of the suspension and the radius of the diaphragm, i.e. the distance between

miniature moving coil loudspeaker is already quite thin and is hard to expect that it

Eﬃciency
In this section, two ways of evaluating the eﬃciency of these transducers will be intro-
duced: the electro-mechanical coupling coeﬃcient and the CIP pressure response. The
ﬁrst is mostly used to evaluate the eﬀectiveness of the coupling between the electrical
and mechanical domains in piezoelectric material. Here, its use has been extended also
to the other topologies and will allow to evaluate the transduction principle without
considering the acoustic components and load. The second method evaluates the pres-
sure generated, in this case, for a 1 W power input at each frequency. In this case the
eﬀects of the acoustics are included and this method will provide a more exhaustive tool
to compare the diﬀerent topology when used in hearing aids. In this case, because the
acoustic load where the pressure is generated has an important role, the pure compli-
ance that was considered in section 2 to model the 2cc coupler has been replaced by a
generical acoustic impedance ZAL. This acoustic impedance will actually consist of a
mass in series with a compliance, being this the best approach to model the impedance
of the 2cc coupler as the simulation presented in section 3.1 highlighted.

The simulated impedance, shown along with the measured one, does not provide a ﬁt

evident by looking at the real part of the impedance. While the resonance is simulated
quite well, it is not the same with the following peak and the increase of the real part
of the impedance with the frequency. These losses seem to be related to the electro-
magnetic components of this topology, being present also in the real part of the blocked
impedance. To understand the nature of this losses, a second order polynomial has been
ﬁtted to the real part of the blocked impedance. This has been done because the losses

Scaling
In this section the knowledge acquire from the simulations in section 3 and theoretical
expressions from section 2 will be used to scale the components of the moving coil and
piezoelectric loudspeakers which will allow to compare the eﬃciency with the balanced
armature loudspeaker.
6.1
Moving Coil Loudspeaker
The balanced armature loudspeaker has been selected as a reference in this study because
is the topology actually used in hearing aids. The model used here, the 26UA03/7 from
the 2600 family produced by Sonion, is also a quite popular one. For this reason the
other topologies will be scaled to match its volume and then their eﬃciency will be
compared.
The balanced armature analyzed here has a volume of 47 mm3 while the moving coil
loudspeaker occupies 7.6 cm3. The latter, model CDM-10008 from CUI, is one of the
smallest moving coil transducers on the market. A scale factor (r) of 0.1836 has to be
applied in order to scale down the components of the moving coil to match the volume
occupied by the reference. Before doing so, is necessary to separate the acoustic contri-
butions from the mechanical ones in the components CMT , MMT and RMT . This task
was performed and described in section 5 and the results are shown in Table (6). This
has to be done because the acoustic components scales diﬀerently than the mechanical
one and, in addition, are multiplied by the surface of the diaphragm squared which also
need to be scaled accordingly. The mechanical compliance of the suspension and the
mass of the diaphragm have been scaled keeping the thickness constant. This is done
because both the suspension and the diaphragm are already quite thin and is not real-
istic for it to be even thinner without changing the fabrication process. The relations
between the diﬀerent components and the scale factor founded in section 3 have been
used and are resumed here:
• Re(r) = Re/r;
• Le(r) = Le · r2;
• Bl(r) = (Bl) · r;
• SD(r) = SD · r2
• CMT (r) = [1/(CMS · r) + (SD · r2)2(1/CAB + 1/CAC)]−1;
• MMT (r) = MMD · r2 + (SD · r2)2MAC.

In this section the coupling between the electrical and mechanical components of the

Moving Coil vs. Balanced armature
The coupling of the moving coil is shown
in Fig.(7.1) for the original geometry and the diﬀerent scaling strategies described in
section 6. It can be seen how the balanced armature provide a better coupling than
the moving coil even without applying any scaling.
The reason is the much larger
transduction coeﬃcient that also happens to be squared in Eq.(4.7). It can be seen that
at low frequencies the big diﬀerence in the transduction coeﬃcient produce a diﬀerence

transduction coeﬃcient. It can be seen how both curves increases with the frequency
until reaching the resonance, as expected. Also at the resonance, all the components that
matters in this frequency range and that appears in the denominator of Eq.(4.7), are
much larger in the balanced armature loudspeaker. But, as before, the large diﬀerence
in the transduction coeﬃcient make the coupling of this design stronger. A diﬀerence
can be seen at high frequencies where the moving coil has a smaller roll-oﬀthan the
balanced armature. In this frequency range, the mechanical mass and inductance of the
coil dominate the mechanical and electrical impedance, respectively. Both components
are much larger than in the moving coil and make the eﬃciency of the coupling in
the balanced armature decrease faster with the frequency than in the moving coil. As
expected, the eﬃciency of the coupling decreases with the frequency squared above the
mechanical resonance.

transduction coeﬃcient in this case is much smaller than in the balanced armature but
the presence of the capacitance C0 in the denominator makes the total eﬃciency much
larger. It can be seen that the only losses in this loudspeaker are mechanical and acous-

from Table (8). For the same frequency range, the coupling in the balanced armature
increase with the frequency and is determined by the mechanical compliance, which is
much smaller than in the piezoelectric, allowing smaller displacements of the diaphragm
and of the air in front of it. The electrical resistance dissipates electrical energy that
will not be converted in mechanical energy.
At the resonance, the coupling in the two transducers is quite similar. At this frequency,
the mechanical compliance has a smaller eﬀect and the losses have a more important
role. The losses in the piezoelectric are smaller than the losses in the balanced armature
but the diﬀerence is not as large as it was between the mechanical compliances. This,
combined with the larger transduction coeﬃcient in the balanced armature results in a
smaller diﬀerence in the coupling of the two topologies for this frequency range.

armature. This is due to the fact that, in this frequency range, the mechanical mass

Moving Coil vs. Balance Armature
The moving coil did not perform very well
in term of electro-mechanical coupling, but things looks quite diﬀerent when analyzing
the ability of generating pressure in a closed volume. In Fig.(7.3) are presented the
CIP pressure response for the balanced armature and the moving coil loudspeaker when
scaled and with its original form factor. The eﬀect of the acoustic load is pretty clear:
the large dip in the response around 5 kHz is a result of the resonance in the coupler. If
the damping term had to be included in the model of the impedance obtained in section
3.1, this dip would have been smaller. It must be noted that around this dip, with a
span of approximately 70 Hz, the model is not really accurate and the results should
be taken carefully.

pressure than the balanced armature for the same input power. The elements in the
86

denominator in Eq.(4.15) and Eq.(4.29) are not that diﬀerent from the ones present in the
electro-mechanical coupling coeﬃcient (Eq.(4.7) and Eq.(4.21)) but with some signiﬁcant
diﬀerence.
For starters, the inductance of the coil does not play a role in the CIP
pressure response. More important is the eﬀect of losses, either electrical, mechanical
and acoustical. All this losses are much smaller in the moving coil loudspeaker which
gives to this topology a deﬁnitive advantage. In addition, the transduction coeﬃcient
can be found both in the denominator and numerator where is not squared anymore and
can not compensate for the losses. It has to be considered that also the surface of the
diaphragm is larger in the moving coil loudspeaker. However, this is not the main cause
of the diﬀerence in the responses. The only element in the denominator that is larger
in the moving coil is the total mechanical compliance. Looking at the low frequency
approximation, this compliance is squared and is dividing the coil resistance in the
denominator, making it even smaller. So, the moving coil is more eﬃcient in generating
pressure in a closed volume because it has a smaller moving mass, less losses and a
larger compliance which theoretically allows for larger displacement of the diaphragm.
Furthermore, this larger displacement is associated with a larger surface which increases
even more the volume of air moved by the transducer resulting in a larger generated
pressure.

real loudspeaker the displacement would be limited be the possible excursion of the
diaphragm before hitting the back plate. Furthermore, such large excursion would also
results in a large distortion due to the non linear inductance, force factor and of the
compliance itself. Above the resonance frequency it can be seen how the reduced mass
of the diaphragm lead to a larger generated pressure than in the original design.
The case of the reduced back volume has not been included because as it was seen in
section 6 it plays a very small role when the geometry is scaled down.

true for the piezoelectric transducer. As seen in Eq.(4.43), the CIP pressure response of
the piezoelectric loudspeaker only depends on the surface of the diaphragm, the losses,

only resonance that can be seen in the CIP pressure response curve for the piezoelectric
loudspeaker is the resonance of the coupler. The balanced armature loudspeaker has
more losses due to electrical, mechanical and acoustical components. In addition, the
relatively small mechanical compliance also contributes to the smaller eﬃciency.

Future Work
This thesis approached multiple topologies of loudspeakers with the purpose of compar-
ing their eﬃciency to evaluate possible alternatives to the standard balanced armature
transducer for the use in hearing aids. The analysis focused only on the eﬃciency to
generate pressure in a closed volume such as the ear canal. Nonetheless, the subject is
quite broad and it was not possible to further investigate some aspects of the diﬀerent
topologies that emerged during this study. Some of this additional topics that could
and should be researched are listed here:
1. Study of the acoustic losses in the back cavity of a miniature moving coil loud-
speaker and the role played by the holes. Their role is probably to provide a larger
back cavity and increase the acoustic compliance increasing the possible displace-
ment and reduce the mechanical Q-factor QM for a larger bandwidth around the
resonance.
2. Include in the study the physical limitations of each device:
• Moving coil loudspeaker: maximum excursion of the diaphragm and maxi-
mum power that can be dissipated by the coil.
• Balanced armature loudspeaker: saturation of the armature, stability condi-
tion to avoid having the armature stuck against one of the magnets.
• Piezoelectric loudspeaker: maximum excursion of the diaphragm.
• Electrostatic loudspeaker: pull in voltage, i.e. the maximum voltage that can
be applied before the diaphragm gets stuck against the ﬁxed plate.
3. Figure out a way of measuring the mechanical properties of the electrostatic loud-
speaker or obtain its equivalent circuit and the value of its components to include
it in the comparison.
4. Include in the study the ampliﬁcation stage. The piezoelectric and electrostatic
loudspeakers have particular requirements that should be analyzed. In addition,
the balanced armature loudspeaker, in some cases, can be used also as a low pass
ﬁlter for a class D ampliﬁer increasing the eﬃciency of the system.
5. Expand the model of the balanced armature loudspeaker to include the losses due
to saturation of the magnetic circuit.
6. Investigate the source of the large real part of the input electrical impedance of
the piezoelectric loudspeaker. Expand the model to include the viscoelastic eﬀect.
7. Evaluate the distortion produced by each topology for the same output pressure.
90
==PAGE:105==
8. Extend the models to include also the main pressure and impedance peaks due to
modes in addition to the mechanical resonance.
9. Analyze the eﬀect of the reduced dimensions of the moving coil loudspeaker on
the rocking motion.
10. Include the constraints of the fabrication processes in achieving smaller form factor
for the loudspeaker analyzed, mostly for the moving coil transducer.

Conclusions
In this thesis four topologies of loudspeakers for the use in hearing aids have been
analyzed. One of them, the electrostatic one, did not made it to the comparison and the
relatives ﬁndings had to be included in the Appendix B. For the rest of them, analogous
circuits have been provided to model their linear behavior. The physical systems have
been measured to ﬁnd the real values of the components in the circuits and analytical
expressions and numerical solutions from FEM modelling have been used to scale them
in order to compare their eﬃciency when they occupy the same volume.
After the comparison is possible to say that the electro-mechanical coupling of the
moving coil at small scales is pretty weak and even more so if compared with the balanced
armature and the piezoelectric loudspeakers.
The performances can be improved if
some strategies are implemented like an increased compliance. This could be somewhat
achieved by a thinner suspension or by changing its shape. Although this solution is
hard to implement with current fabrication processes, a MEMS moving coil could achieve
such a thin suspension making this transducer somewhat attractive considering that is
already under development. Although the electro-mechanical coupling is worst for the
moving coil than for the balanced armature, when the acoustics are taken into account
and the ability to generate pressure in a close volume is evaluated, the moving coil
has the upper hand. If the moving coil loudspeaker is scaled down without employing
any strategy to improve its performance, it still provides a CIP pressure response quite
close to the reference balanced armature loudspeaker. The frequency response of this
loudspeaker would also need some equalization to be used in a hearing aid, although
this consideration is bounded to the strategy used to scale down this topology.
The piezoelectric loudspeaker has shown a strong electro-mechanical coupling and is
able to generate a high pressure in closed volume for a fairly small amount of electrical
power. In addition, it has pretty ﬂat frequency response that would make it suitable
for the use in a hearing aids. It has to be considered that the CIP pressure response is
obtained for 1 W of electrical input power and given the high impedance of this topology,
the real transducer will not achieve such high pressures. Physical limitations should be
considered to evaluate the real maximum pressure that such device can generate.
Such limitations should also be considered for the moving coil. The good performance
of the scaled moving coil with increases mechanical compliance is a direct result of the
large displacement allowed by the softer suspension. In a real device, the excursion of
the diaphragm is limited by the physical space the coil can move in before hitting the
back plate. In addition, large excursions would also result in more distortion due to the
position dependent non linear eﬀects.
The lack of a complete picture prevents to make a deﬁnitive statement regarding which
92
==PAGE:107==
design might have the largest potential and if any of them could replace the balanced
armature in the future.
Nonetheless, the moving coil has shown that with a clever
design could generate a pressure large enough for a hearing aid, taking into account the
power constraints. The piezoelectric instead, would not require a change in the design
being already small enough and presenting a strong electro-mechanical coupling and the
ability to generate large pressures in closed volumes. The main problem with the latter
is the source needed to drive it. The ampliﬁer used to drive this transducer needs a 30
VDC power supply for proper operation: part of it is used to power up the ampliﬁer and
the rest is used to get the piezoelectric-silicon structure in working position.
It must be noted that the eﬃciency of the balanced armature and piezoelectric trans-
ducer might have been overestimated. As it was seen in section 5, these two topologies
presents losses that the equivalent circuits used here were not able to simulate. This
losses that are shown in the real part of the measured input electric impedance would re-
duce the eﬃciency of both transducers. The piezoelectric would probably be less eﬃcient
at low frequencies due to such losses, while, for the balanced armature, the eﬃciency
might be lower at high frequencies due to the hysteresis of the magnetic materials. Al-
though, these losses depends also on the level of the current ﬂowing through the coil
and are one of the non linear eﬀects associated with this topology.
None of the topologies analyzed in this study provide an alternative to the balanced
armature for the use in the hearing aid at this moment. Nonetheless, the piezoelectric
loudspeaker already provides performances and size adequate for a hearing aid but would
need a more eﬃcient way to drive it. The moving coil is too big but research for a MEMS
version, which occupies a much smaller volume, are under way. Considering the speed
of the advancements in technologies and fabrication processes, it would be wise to keep
an eye on the progress made with these two alternative topologies.

B
Electrostatic Transducer
This type of transducers are mostly employed in microphones and in high end loudspeak-
ers. In this work one of the ﬁrst MEMS electrostatic loudspeaker has been analyzed.
This type of loudspeaker basically consists of a capacitor where one of the plates that
hold the charge is free to move and is used as diaphragm. The diaphragm is made out of
conductive material and is very light which results in a high resonance frequency. This
design is referred to as Single-end. An alternative design make use of three plates: two
are ﬁxed and perforated to let air pass through and the third is placed in the middle
and is used as diaphragm. This design is called Push-pull. The loudspeaker analyzed
here make use of the ﬁrst design.
Being basically a capacitor, this type of loudspeaker needs a DC bias to polarize the
plates. This DC voltage is usually pretty high to reduce the non linear eﬀects. This bias
can be provided by an external source, which is not ideal in a hearing aid considering
the voltages involved, or by a pre-polarized material such as electret. The latter is the
case with the loudspeaker at hand.
This loudspeakers present a really high electrical impedance at low frequencies which
makes them unsuited for normal power ampliﬁer. The solution provided by the manufac-
turer of this loudspeaker is to add a transformer in series. In this way the impedance can
be adapted and a normal ampliﬁer can be used. Although, the size of the transformer
is one of the reasons that make this loudspeaker not particularly suited for hearing aids
at the moment. A second reason is the limited frequency range, being this transducer
developed as a tweeter for high end ear buds. Nonetheless, it might still be interesting
to analyze its performances.

Simulation of the Clamped Rectangular Membrane
This simulation is similar to the one presented in Section 3.4 and has been performed
for the same purposes. In this case a clamped rectangular plate is going to be analyzed.
This simulation is used to model the membrane in an electrostatic loudspeaker. A 2D
model has been used with the geometry presented in Fig.(B.4), which has approximately
the same dimension as in the real transducer.
Figure B.4: Geometry used in COMSOL to study the relation between stiﬀness and mechanical
damping and the scale factor for a rectangular clamped plate.
The material used in this case was aluminum from the database in COMSOL. Instead
of using the physical interface Solid Mechanics again, the Plate interface has been used.
The thickness was deﬁned in such interface to be almost two order of magnitude smaller
than the other dimensions. Two diﬀerent instances of this physical interface were used:
in one the thickness was left constant while in the other was multiplied by the scale
factor. This will allow to study how the damping and the stiﬀness change when all the
dimension are scaled and, the more realistic case, when the thickness is left constant. In
this case the study performed consisted of a ﬁrst stationary step followed by a frequency
domain perturbation study. Another diﬀerence consisted in the boundary conditions: to
the edges was applied a condition of ﬁxed constraint and to the surface of the diaphragm
were applied two diﬀerent face loads, to simulate the electrostatic force between the
plates of the capacitor. The ﬁrst one is used in the stationary step to simulate the
applied DC voltage to polarize the plates of the electrostatic transducer. The second
one is employed only in the frequency domain perturbation study to simulate the AC
signal on top of the DC voltage.
