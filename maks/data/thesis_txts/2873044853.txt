Abstract
This thesis addresses the challenge of autonomous drone racing by developing a compre-
hensive platform including a novel implementation of a multi-task reinforcement learning
(MTRL) framework, a corresponding real-time execution system, and a physical hard-
ware prototype. The research aims to advance the state-of-the-art in high-speed, agile
flight by investigating the effect of MTRL on policy robustness and performance. The core
contribution is a flexible software framework, built on TF-Agents and a custom C++ simu-
lation environment, designed to train a single policy on multiple flight tasks concurrently,
specifically racing and stabilization. This approach is hypothesized to attain a more gen-
eralized understanding of flight dynamics, leading to superior performance compared to
single-task learning.
The developed system was evaluated through various simulation experiments. The MTRL-
trained policy was benchmarked against a single-task racing policy, assessing perfor-
mance based on lap times, success rates, and robustness to environmental and per-
ceptual uncertainties. The results demonstrate that while the single-task policy achieved
smoother control and faster initial learning, the MTRL policy exhibited significantly greater
robustness in some situations, successfully navigating severe track deformations and
state estimation noise. Although the MTRL agent displayed more erratic control signals, it
ultimately achieved faster average lap times in prolonged flight, supporting the hypothesis
that the auxiliary task acts as a regularizer. This work provides a foundational platform
for future research in autonomous drone racing, delivering a functional MTRL pipeline
and demonstrating its potential to produce highly resilient and competitive flight control
policies.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
First-Person View (FPV) drone racing has rapidly evolved from a niche hobby into a pro-
fessional sport and from there into a challenging scientific benchmark for robotics and
Artificial Intelligence (AI) [1, 2]. Human pilots guide quadcopters through complex three-
dimensional circuits at speeds exceeding 100 km/h. To compete, autonomous pilot sys-
tems push the boundaries of perception, planning, and control under severe physical and
computational constraints [3, 1]. This makes autonomous drone racing an ideal testbed
for developing algorithms for high-speed navigation in complex and uncertain environ-
ments [1, 2]. The goal is not just to match, but to surpass human capabilities, automating
the highly complex task of racing to push vehicle performance beyond what humans can
achieve [1].
The significance of this research field extends far beyond the actual competitions. The
skills mastered for autonomous racing like speed, agility, and robust navigation in con-
strained environments are directly applicable to a wide range of real-world scenarios [1].
In many applications speed directly translates to increased mission efficiency and effec-
tiveness: a faster drone covers more ground, increasing its operational radius on a single
battery [1].
Innovation in the field of high-speed agile autonomous flight is amplified by major interna-
tional competitions [1]. High-stakes events like the Lockheed Martin AlphaPilot Challenge
and the Abu Dhabi Autonomous Racing League (A2RL) offer substantial prize pools to
attract top-tier talent and incentivize breakthroughs in the field [4, 5, 1]. These competi-
tions are designed to accelerate and shape the development of autonomous systems by
defining the state-of-the-art through their rules and technical requirements [1]. As an ex-
ample, the A2RL drone championship mandates drones to be equiped with only a single
RGB camera [6, 7].
1.2
Problem Statement
The classical ”perceive-plan-act” pipeline for autonomous systems decomposes the prob-
lem into modular steps [8, 9, 10]. While this simplifies development, errors can propagate,
and the resource intense algorithms for optimal planning and control creates a computa-
tional bottleneck on resource-constrained drones [10]. This has opened an opportunity
to use learning-based approaches where model-based control fails. Deep reinforcement
learning (DRL), can offer superior reactivity while being computationally light-weight and
has achieved super-human performance [11], but requires extensive training and faces
the difficult sim-to-real transfer problem [12]. While there is some consensus on the gen-
eral architecture for competitive autonomous drone pilot software, there are many ap-
proaches to tackle each of these challenges and there is much potential in even just
improving small components of these complex systems.
1.3
Objective
The overarching goal of this thesis is to survey the state-of-the-art in software architecures
for autonomous drone racing, identify promising approaches and potential vectors for
improvement and build a working research platform as a basis for DTU’s own entry into
future editions of the A2RL competition. The primary objectives of this thesis can thus be
broken down further:

Related Work
This chapter provides a brief review of the state-of-the-art in autonomous drone racing.
It can roughly be split into two main paradigms: the traditional, model-based ”perceive-
plan-act” pipeline and emerging learning-based approaches.
2.1
The ”Perceive-Plan-Act” Pipeline
Figure 2.1: Schematic of the classic perceive-plan-act pipeline as it is used in autonomous
drone systems. [1]
The ”Perceive-Plan-Act” paradigm is a foundational, modular approach to robot control
dating back to the 1980s, where a system sequentially perceives its environment, plans
its next action, and then executes it [9, 8]. This architecture was the standard in the
early days of autonomous drone racing, such as at the 2016 IROS competition, which
saw modest speeds far from what human pilots can achieve [1]. As teams pushed for
higher performance, the pipeline’s limitations became apparent. The period of 2018-2019,
marked by competitions like the IROS ADR III and the AlphaPilot Challenge, was when
top teams began augmenting the classical pipeline with learning-based components to
achieve superior speeds [1, 13]. The modularity of the pipeline makes the strategic re-
placement of weaker links with more robust, learned modules possible, leading to the
hybrid systems that define the current state-of-the-art [13, 1].
2.1.1
State Estimation
The first stage, perception, is primarily concerned with state estimation: producing an ac-
curate, continuous estimate of the drone’s pose and velocity [14]. Visual-Inertial Odometry
(VIO) is the standard onboard technology, fusing high-frequency, low accuracy IMU data
with lower-frequency camera observations to track motion without an outside reference
system [15]. However, VIO is a relative positioning system prone to accumulating drift, a
problem severely worsened by the high speeds and visual challenges of racing, such as
motion blur and lighting changes, which can cause the estimation to fail altogether [11,
13]. To counter this, hybrid approaches have been developed that use the race gates
as global landmarks to correct drift. This requires robust convolutional neural networks
(CNNs) that can detect the gate’s features (edges or corners) which can be used to tri-
angulate the camera’s viewpoint [13]. The high-frequency but drift-prone VIO estimate
and the high-accuracy but unreliable gate information are then fused using an extended
kalman filter (EKF) [13]. The EKF is a recursive algorithm that combines these measure-
ments to produce a single, drift-corrected state estimate for the entire system [16].
2.1.2
Time-Optimal Trajectory Planning
The planning module computes a time-optimal, dynamically feasible, and collision-free
trajectory through the gates, balancing optimality against real-time computational cost [17,
18]. The offline gold standard involves solving a large-scale nonlinear program (NLP)

that incorporates the full quadrotor dynamics and actuator limits. Methods like ”Com-
plementary Progress Constraints” (CPC) can find truly time-optimal trajectories that have
beaten human pilots, but the computation takes minutes to hours, making it an offline-only
tool [18, 17]. Other techniques use hierarchical methods based on simplified point-mass
models (PMMs) [17]. These trajectories are computationally cheap but not dynamically
feasible [17]. This infeasible path is then passed as a reference to a sophisticated low-
level controller that tracks its shape as aggressively as the full quadrotor dynamics allow.
Recent advances like the TOGT planner are closing the gap, generating near-optimal
trajectories in seconds, though not yet in a real-time loop [19].
2.1.3
Model Predictive Control
The final stage is the controller, which computes the motor commands to execute the
planned trajectory. The leading strategy is Nonlinear Model Predictive Control (NMPC),
which uses a dynamic model of the drone to predict future states and solve an online
optimization problem at each time step. This finds the optimal control inputs that minimize
tracking error while respecting system constraints like motor thrust limits, making it ideal
for aggressive flight [1, 20]. For drone racing, the state-of-the-art is a specialized variant
called Model Predictive Contouring Control (MPCC) [21, 22]. Unlike standard NMPC
which tracks a path against a specific time schedule, MPCC’s cost function is designed to
simultaneously minimize the geometric distance to a path and maximize progress along
it [21]. This allows it to follow a simple geometric path from the planner as fast as physically
possible, making it the perfect counterpart to the hierarchical planning approach [17, 21].
2.1.4
The Rise of Learning-Based Control
The classical pipeline offers a mature, well-understood, and theoretically grounded frame-
work for autonomous flight [1]. However, the modularity and reliance on explicit models
that make it so effective also constitute its primary weakness, particularly when pushed to
the physical limits demanded by drone racing [1, 13]. The performance of the entire sys-
tem is critically dependent on the seamless integration and successful execution of each
component, creating a chain of dependencies where a failure or degradation in any single
module can lead to a catastrophic failure of the entire system [1, 13]. The extreme dy-
namics of agile flight, often exceeding speeds of 100 km/h and accelerations surpassing
4g can induce severe motion blur and rapid changes in scene appearance, which cause
standard VIO algorithms to accumulate significant drift or fail entirely [1, 13]. Further-
more, the online optimization required for both planning and control imposes a significant
computational burden on the resource-constrained onboard computers of racing drones,
creating a bottleneck that can limit reactivity or be traded off for inaccuracy [1, 13]. This
inherent sensitivity and computational cost have lead to a paradigm shift in the field, mo-
tivating a move towards learning-based approaches that have recently demonstrated not
just competitive, but superhuman performance [1, 11, 23].
The most successful and compelling applications of machine learning in this domain do
not represent a complete departure from the classical pipeline. Rather, they integrate
learning strategically to address the most acute weaknesses of the model-based pipeline.
An analysis of highly performing systems reveals that learning is most effectively em-
ployed as a targeted replacement for the modules that are the most computationally ex-
pensive and most sensitive to model inaccuracies: the planning and control components
[1]. For instance, the ”Swift” system, which defeated human racing champions, is such a
hybrid architecture [11, 2]. It retains a robust, classical perception front-end to produce a
clean state estimate, but replaces the planning and control components with a single, re-
active Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) policy. This allows the system to leverage the
reliability of classical state estimation and the raw performance and reactivity of a learned

2.2
Learning Architectures
The integration of learning-based methods into the autonomous drone racing stack has
given rise to various architectural approaches, from small enhancements to classical mod-
ules to fully end-to-end systems. In their survey[1], hanover et al. show some clear
trade-offs concerning performance, modularity and the challenge of Sim-to-Real transfer:
bridging the data gap between simulation and reality.
2.2.1
Augmenting classical modules with learning
The most conservative integrations of learning use neural networks to solve specific sub-
problems within the traditional pipeline, most notably gate detection [1, 13]. In high-speed
racing, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have long proven effective at detecting
gate corners from raw images, a task where classical computer vision struggles [1]. These
2D detections are then fed into a classical pipeline, using a PnP algorithm to get a 3D pose
that is fused with VIO estimates in an EKF to correct for drift [11, 13]. This approach is a
foundational component in virtually all modern racing systems [1].
2.2.2
End-to-end approaches
Figure 2.2: End-to-end learning approaches might be the future but they are unproven.
[hanover2023survey]
At the opposite end of the spectrum, the end-to-end (E2E) paradigm aims to learn a sin-
gle, monolithic policy that maps raw sensory inputs, such as camera images, directly to
low-level motor commands [1, 24]. While conceptually appealing for its biomimetic ap-
proach [1, 3], E2E architectures have not yet proven practical for achieving competitive
performance in real-world racing [1]. The primary obstacles are an especially challeng-
ing sim-to-real gap, extreme sample inefficiency as well as an enormous hunger for high
fidelity input data due to the high-dimensional input space, and a ”black box” nature that
makes debugging nearly impossible [1, 25, 26, 23]. The policy must simultaneously learn
a complete model of the world’s physics and the characteristics of available sensors, a
task far more complex than the targeted modeling used in other approaches [1].
2.2.3
The current state-of-the-art: Learned planning and control

The hybrid architecture has emerged as the most successful and proveb approach, as
demonstrated by the champion-level ”Swift” system [11, 23, 2]. This paradigm retains
a classical perception system to produce a low-dimensional state estimate (e.g., pose,
velocity, gate locations) [1]. This state vector is then fed as input to a learned policy
that directly outputs low-level control commands like collective thrust and body rates, re-
placing computationally expensive classical planning and control modules [1, 27]. The
fundamental advantage of the hybrid model lies in its intelligent management of complex-
ity. The state vector, produced by the classical perception module, is a powerful form of
”privilege information” [26]. It distills the noisy, high-dimensional sensory stream into a
more compact representation that is directly relevant to the control task. This reduces the
learning problem from a combined ”perception + planning + control” challenge to a much
more focused ”planning + control” problem [1, 27]. The learning agent does not need to
waste capacity and samples learning to infer intermediate states information like velocity
or its position relative to a gate from just a sequence of images as it can be provided to it
in a relatively inexpensive way. The value of this state abstraction is acknowledged even
in recent work on E2E systems. For example, Geles et al. (2024) employ an asymmetric
actor-critic framework where the critic, which guides the learning process, is given ac-
cess to the full, privileged state of the drone, while the actor policy has to learn from only
a visual input [26].
2.3
Improvement through multi-task learning
Figure 2.4: Schematic outline of the MTRL architecture that enables drone control policies
to train on multiple tasks in parallel. [27]
Having established the hybrid state-to-action architecture as the most promising approach
to introducing learning into the pipeline, the next big focus lies on improving the learning
process within that architecture. The MTRL model for quadrotors, as proposed by Xing et
al. (2025), is built upon a simple principle: while the objectives of different flight tasks may
vary wildly, the underlying physic of the quadrotor platform remain invariant [27]. A drone
performing a stable hover, tracking a velocity command, or racing through gates is always
governed by the same equations of motion, mass, and inertia. The MTRL architecture is
explicitly designed to exploit this shared structure to accelerate learning [27]. Counter-
intuitively, policies that are trained to perform multiple tasks out-perform drones that are
purpose-trained to the specific task at hand[27]. The key architectural innovation is a
dual-encoder structure that separates shared knowledge from task-specific information
[27]. A shared encoder processes the subset of observations that are common to all
tasks and relate directly to the drone’s core dynamic state and task-specific encoders
process the observations that are unique to each task.

3.1
Quadrotor Dynamics
To control a quadrotor, we must first understand the physics that govern its movement.
This section outlines the standard dynamic model for a quadrotor, establishing the notation
and equations of motion used throughout this work, based on the model described in [27].
3.1.1
Reference Frames and Notation
We define two primary coordinate frames to describe the drone’s state, as illustrated in
Figure 3.1.
• The World Frame, denoted by W, is a fixed inertial frame of reference. Its z-axis,
zW , is aligned with the direction of gravity.
• The Body Frame, denoted by B, is fixed to the quadrotor’s center of mass. Its axes,
xB, yB, zB, are aligned with the drone’s structure.
Vectors are represented by bold lowercase letters, and matrices by bold uppercase letters.
A vector’s frame of reference is indicated by a subscript. For example, pWB represents
the position of the body frame B relative to the world frame W. The rotation from the body
frame to the world frame is described by the rotation matrix RWB.

3.2
Deep Reinforcement Learning
Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) is the machine learning paradigm used in this thesis
to train the flight control policy. It combines the principles of reinforcement learning with
the representational power of deep neural networks to solve complex decision-making
problems.
3.2.1
Fundamentals of Reinforcement Learning
Reinforcement Learning (RL) is a goal-oriented learning approach. An agent (the drone’s
control policy) learns to make decisions by interacting with an environment (the physics
simulator). The interaction is formalized as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) and unfolds
in a sequence of steps:
1. The agent observes the current state (st) of the environment.
2. Based on the state, the agent selects an action (at).
3. The environment transitions to a new state (st+1) as a result of the action.
4. The environment provides a scalar reward (rt), which is a signal indicating how
good or bad the action was in that state.
The agent’s objective is to learn a policy (π), which is a mapping from states to actions
(π : S →A), that maximizes the cumulative discounted reward over an episode.

3.2.2
Deep Learning and Neural Networks
Deep Learning uses neural networks with multiple layers (hence ”deep”) to learn complex
patterns from data. A neural network is a computational model inspired by the structure
of the human brain. It consists of interconnected nodes called neurons, organized in
layers.
• Input Layer: Receives the initial data (e.g., the drone’s state vector).
• Hidden Layers: Perform a series of computations on the data. Each neuron in a
layer receives inputs from the previous layer, multiplies them by weights, adds a
bias, and then passes the result through a non-linear activation function.
• Output Layer: Produces the final result (e.g., the control action for the drone).
The network ”learns” by adjusting its weights and biases to minimize a loss function,
which measures the difference between the network’s output and the desired output.
3.2.3
Combining RL and Deep Learning
In Deep Reinforcement Learning, deep neural networks are used to approximate the pol-
icy (π) and/or the value function (which estimates the expected future reward from a given
state). This allows the agent to handle high-dimensional state and action spaces, such
as those encountered in robotics. This is the core principle behind the DRL agents used
in this thesis, where a neural network takes the drone’s state as input and outputs a con-
troller signal.

4.1.1
Framework Architecture
The training framework is architecturally inspired by recent work by the team at UZH
that developed Swift [11] that shows how multi-task reinforcement learning (MTRL) can
improve racing performance even further [27]. By leveraging the shared physical dynam-
ics of the platform to improve sample efficiency and overall performance across different
tasks, the resulting policies can out-perform specialized agents. Since there is little re-
search on the efficacy of the used supplementary tasks like stabilization and trajectory
tracking, this area promises significant potential for improvement. Since detailed descrip-
tions of the required software components to realize such a system have not been pub-
lished, much of the focus of this work lies on the development of our own system.
The key components are:
• Multi-Task Environment: A custom, modular environment that can manage multi-
ple flight tasks simultaneously (e.g., racing, stabilization, velocity tracking).
• Policy and Critic Networks: A shared actor (policy) network is trained to be as
effective at possible at solving all tasks, while multiple, task-specific critic networks
are used to evaluate performance for each individual task during training [27].
• Shared and Task-Specific Encoders: The framework employs a dual-encoder
structure to process observations. A shared encoder processes information related
to the quadrotor’s core dynamics (pose, velocity), which is common across all tasks.
Task-specific encoders handle observations unique to each task, such as the loca-
tion of upcoming gates in a race [27]. The outputs of these encoders are concate-
nated and fed into the shared actor policy, as illustrated in Figure 4.3.
Each iteration of the RL training process is divided into 2 phases of roughly equal com-
putational cost:
• In the collection phase, large amounts of training data are obtained from the train-
ing environment. Usually, as is the case in our framework, this environment is con-
nected to a simulator that works in a stepped fashion. A stepped simulator does not
run in continuous time, but rather exposes a step-function that advances the simu-
lation by a single time step. Data points are obtained by stepping the simulation.
– Each simulation step applies an action which consists of a command that the
simulator can apply to a drone. Specifically, our actions consist of a collective
motor thrust (N) and three body rates (rad) that affect pitch, roll and yaw of the
drone.
– After the action is executed, the step returns an observation containing shared
and task-specific state information.
– Additionally, a reward is computed to tell the framework how advantageous
the resulting state is in terms of the task’s objectives.
• In the training phase tf-agents and the underlying TensorFlow frameworks perform
the proximal policy optimization in an easy-to-use black-box fashion. A classical
Adam-optimizer updates the weights of the actor and critic iteratively in response to
the obtained rewards.
4.1.2
Training environment
The training framework performs the collection phase by applying actions and collect-
ing observations from a training environment. Since training processes can take mil-
lions of environment interactions to reach an optimum, computational performance is of

paramount importance. The tf-agents framework expects an environment to expose two
crucial functions to interact with it:
• The step() function handles the data collection. For n simulated drones, it receives n
actions as an input, runs n simulations for one step, applying the respective actions

Simulator
By far the most fundamental consideration regarding training performance is the choice
of simulation. A trade-off has to be decided between the computation time of each step
and the simulator fidelity as well as physical accuracy. For this work, we use the simulator
provided by the Sgilicious framework because it has been tested and proven in conjunc-
tion with very similar RL architectures [11]. However, some adaption had to be made to
make the simulator RL-ready. Since the original simulator was developed for the real-time
demonstration and testing of the classical pilot software, a stepped adapter was needed
to advance it only one step at a time. Furthermore, the original simulator was meant to
run sequentially on a single CPU core. To obtain the large amount of data needed for
reinforcement learning and make more efficient use of our CPU resources, OpenMP was
used to run hundreds of drone simulations in parallel.
Orchestrator Class
The AgiSimBatch2 class serves as the central coordinator of the training process. It is
responsible for managing the hundreds of parallel drone simulation instances and passing
down the information flow these environments and the learning algorithm. During the
collection phase AgiSimBatch2 applies actions generated by the current policy to each
parallel environment. After each simulation step, it gathers the resulting observations,

Gate Pass State Management
A problem that arises when storing previous gate pass
states for the reset logic – especially in the beginning – is that many gate passes are
of questionable quality. Sometimes a drone approaches a gate why too fast and at an
oblique angle but still somehow makes it through. Storing such a low-quality gate pass
state and forcing subsequent drones to start with it can hinder learning progress and get
the process stuck in a local optimum. To combat this, we implemented a limited-size store
of gate pass states and a replacement strategy that only stores states if they are of higher
quality than the worst state that is already stored. This begs the question how to quantify
the quality of a gate pass when it occurs. We came up with a strategy, where each drone
keeps track of it’s gate passes and it’s current cumulative reward. Gate pass states are not
stored immediately but collected when the drone terminates. At this point we can easily
compute how much reward the drone eventually accumulated after each gate pass. We
use this ”reward post-fix sum” as a quality measure and it has greatly improved learning
performance.
4.1.3
Training Procedure
The training script (train_MTRL.py) is responsible for the high-level structure of the prox-
imal policy optimization (PPO) training process. Since tf-agents is made to be easy to
use and understand, many of the technical details are abstracted away behind simple
function calls. Still, some customization was necessary to suit our specific MTRL-needs.
The script manages the environment, the PPO agent (which includes the actor and critic),
and the training and evaluation process. Furthermore, it holds all of our training-related
hyper-parameters.
The script holds two instances of our custom environment.
• py_train_env: This is the training environment where the agent will actively learn.
It’s configured to run a larger number of drones in parallel to make optimal use of
compute resources and gather a diverse set of experiences quickly.
• py_eval_env: This environment is used to periodically evaluate the agent’s perfor-
mance. It typically uses fewer drones and is set to ”evaluation mode” to enforce
deterministic start conditions and thus, ensure consistent performance metrics.
The core of the interaction happens inside the main training loop. For a set number of
steps (collect_steps_per_iteration), the script performs the following actions:

19
==PAGE:28==
1. Action Selection: The agent’s collect_policy (which is primarily driven by the ac-
tor network) observes the current state of the environment and computes an action
to take.
2. Environment Step: The action is sent to the training environment, which simulates
the drone’s movement and returns the next state, the reward for the action, and
whether the episode has ended.
3. Trajectory Storage: This entire sequence containing initial state, action, resulting
state, reward, and other policy information is stored as a ”trajectory”.
This collected data, often referred to as ”experience”, is what the agent will learn from.
After collecting a batch of experience, the script enters the training phase within the same
iteration.
1. Agent Training: The agent.train() method is called. This is where the PPO
algorithm does its work, utilizing both the actor and the critic networks:
• Value Network (4.1.5)): The critic (MultiHeadValueNetwork) examines the
states from the collected experience and estimates the ”value” of being in those
states (i.e. the expected future reward). It also calculates the ”advantage”,
which determines if the action taken was better or worse than the average
action for that state.
• Actor Network (4.1.4): The actor (MTRLActorNetwork) then uses the advan-
tage calculated by the critic to update its own policy. If an action led to a positive
advantage, the actor adjusts its network to make that action more likely in the
future. If the action led to a negative advantage, it becomes less likely.
• Clipping Mechanism: PPO ensures that these policy updates are not too
drastic, which helps to stabilize the learning process. It does this by ”clipping”
the importance ratio, preventing the new policy from deviating too much from
the old one in a single update.
This cycle of collecting data in the environment and then training the actor and critic
with that data is repeated for a fixed number of iterations (num_iterations).
4.1.4
Actor Network
The actor network processes observations and outputs a continuous action distribution.
A key challenge in implementing the multi-task encoder structure detailed in 2.3 was over-
coming the limitations of the tf-agents framework. This framework’s EncodingNetwork
is designed to work with a dictionary of preprocessing layers, where each layer corre-
sponds to a static key in the observation dictionary. It does not natively support dynamic,
conditional routing within the network graph based on a value from the observation tensor
itself, such as the Task ID. This limit is not unique to tf-agents but stems from the fact that
MTRL is not yet widely adopted at the time of writing.
To overcome this, we implemented a custom MTRLActorNetwork that not only consists of
the Actor core architecture, but also a custom encoder module that builds this conditional
logic directly into its call method. By using TensorFlow’s tf.where operation, we can
dynamically select the output from the appropriate task-specific encoder for each item in
a batch. This approach allows us to maintain a single, unified policy network that can still
leverage specialized processing pathways for different tasks.

Critic Network
Another crucial feature that the framework does not support out-of-the-box is using a
separate critic network for evaluating the actor’s performance in each task. To address
this, we implemented a custom MultiHeadValueNetwork that effectively functions as a
task-based switch, directing the input to one of several independent critic networks. This
design aligns with the multi-critic architecture proposed in the paper, where each task
is assigned its own dedicated value function to handle its unique objectives and reward
structure.

The critic network, as depicted in figure 4.5, works as follows:
1. Separate Network Pathways: Inside the MultiHeadValueNetwork class, we de-

Stabilization task. Additional pathways can easily be added if more tasks are to be

2. Direct Observation Input: Unlike the actor, the critic does not use the specialized
shared and task-specific encoders. Instead, it takes the full, concatenated obser-

the actor and critic can improve policy performance [27, 29].
3. Conditional Forward Pass: During the forward pass, the network uses the scalar
Task ID from the input observation. Similarly to the multi task encoder from the actor
network, a tf.where operation acts as a multiplexer, routing the entire observation
vector to the appropriate internal critic network.
This multi-headed architecture is critical for our MTRL framework. Since tasks like high-
speed racing and stabilization have inherently conflicting objectives (e.g., maximizing
speed vs. minimizing velocity), training a single value network would lead to interference.
By maintaining separate critics, the agent can learn accurate and independent value func-
tions for each task, enabling the shared actor to learn a policy that effectively performs all
of them.
4.2
Onboard Software System
The ”Agilicious” framework provides us with powerful core flight library called ”agilib”. It’s
central feature is an autonomous drone pilot that continuously runs a classical ”Perceive-
Plan-Act” pipeline. The pipeline consists of swappable modules that provide multiple al-
ternative approaches and implementations of pipeline components like controllers, esti-

comonent that can run a tried and tested classical pipeline in parallel to our custom solu-
tion as a means to recover the drone if it leaves certain safety thresholds. Finally, it also
provides us with a reliable and configurable physics simulation that has been proven to
be accurate enough for the training of our learning-based controllers.
In order to integrate with other systems such as the physical drones and external simu-
lations, the Agilicous relies on a ROS1-based communication layer named ”agiros” [2].
As a key part of this work, the ”agiros” layer was partially replaced and partially adapted
into a new ROS2-based adapter layer [30]. This modernization provides the benefits of
the ROS2 ecosystem, including improved real-time capabilities, a more robust and se-
cure communication protocol (DDS), and better compatibility with modern simulators and
hardware for future development.

command publishing.

• Shared Observation: This contains the drone’s core dynamic state, including its
position (p ∈R3), a 6D representation of its orientation ( ¯R ∈R6), its linear and
angular velocities (v, ω ∈R3), and the action taken in the previous timestep (aprev).
• Task-Specific Observation: For the racing task, this includes the relative positions
of the corners of the next two gates. The corners of the upcoming gate (δp1) are
transformed from the world frame into the drone’s body frame to provide a local, ego-
centric representation. The corners of the subsequent gate (δp2) are represented
relative to the upcoming gate’s corners in the world frame.
A crucial step in this process is scaling. Each component of the raw observation vector is
normalized to a range (typically [-1, 1]) using pre-defined minimum and maximum values
derived from the training data distribution. This scaling ensures that the input to the neural
network is well-conditioned, which is vital for stable and efficient learning and inference.
3. & 4. Inference and Command Publishing

tionary is fed into the loaded policy network. A forward pass is performed to generate a

Robustness to Static Track Geometry Perturbations
In any real-world competition, the physical positions of the race gates will never perfectly
match the map provided to the autonomous system due to measurement errors or setup
inaccuracies. Furthermore, a drone that can adapt to displacements in gate positions can
similarly be expected to handle its own deviations from the learned path gracefully. This
experiment quantifies the policy’s ability to generalize to these unseen track variations.
To simulate this uncertainty, we evaluate the policy first on the unchanged track. Then
we run subsequent policy rollouts and each time displace the third gate (G2) more and
more along the Y axis, 20cm at a time. This is done until policy rollouts reach a crash
rate of 100%. The results, visualized in a degradation plot, show a graceful decline in
performance. As the geometric uncertainty increases, the lap time and gate-passing error
rise, while the success rate falls. Crucially, the policy does not fail catastrophically at low-
to-moderate displacements, indicating that it has not simply memorized the training track
but has learned a more general strategy for targeting and traversing gates. Each test is
run with 16 drones simultaneously and all reported metrics are averages.

5.4.2
Robustness to State Estimation Perturbations
The hybrid architecture employed in this thesis relies on a classical state estimation mod-
ule to provide the drone’s state as input to the learned policy [11, 2]. While so far our
experiments have been using universal ground truths, in the real world, this estimate is
never perfect, suffering from sensor noise, and more critically, drift accumulation from
Visual-Inertial Odometry (VIO) systems, especially during aggressive maneuvers and di-
rectly after gat passes [11]. This experiment evaluates the policy’s sensitivity to the quality
of its primary input.
We simulate this perceptual uncertainty by adding zero-mean Gaussian noise directly
to the state vector components (position and velocity) at each timestep before they are
passed to the policy. The experiment is repeated for several noise intensities, quantified
by the standard deviation of the added noise. For this experiment we use a standard
deviation of 3cm per time step for the linear drift and 0.8° for the angular drift. Performance
metrics are recorded for each noise level.

6
Discussion & Conclusion
This thesis successfully developed and evaluated a multi-task reinforcement learning
(MTRL) framework for autonomous drone racing, delivering on the primary objectives of
creating a robust training architecture, a corresponding real-time execution system, and
a hardware prototype. The empirical evaluation yielded several key findings that provide
a holistic assessment of the MTRL framework’s strengths and weaknesses.
6.1
Summary of Findings
First, the developed MTRL framework proved capable of training policies that can suc-
cessfully pilot a drone in a simulated racing environment. Even though MTRL policy per-
formance was not as convincing as in the paper by Xing et al. [27], the multi-task policy
out-performs its single-task counterpart in some metrics. This confirms the fundamental
viability of the underlying environment, network architectures, and PPO-based training
procedure.
Second, the results make a compelling case for the MTRL approach. The policy trained
concurrently on racing and stabilization tasks, while exhibiting a less stable learning curve
and more erratic control signals, ultimately outperformed its single-task counterpart when
it came to dealing with perturbations. This inherent robustness can be expected to play
a major role when it comes to bridging the sim-to-real gap. Additionally, while starting
slower, it hinted at its potential by achieving a significantly faster average lap time over
long periods of time. These findings empirically support the central hypothesis, advanced
by works like Xing et al. [27], that auxiliary tasks act as a powerful regularizer, forcing
the policy to learn a more generalized model of the drone’s dynamics rather than simply
memorizing a specific trajectory. The stabilization task, by forcing the agent to recover
from a wide distribution of off-nominal states, effectively gives the shared actor network a
resilience that the specialized racing agent lacks.
Finally, the analysis of the control outputs revealed a notable characteristic: both poli-
cies, particularly the MTRL agent, favored aggressive, often oscillating control actions
over smooth inputs. While effective for achieving high speeds and robust recovery, this
extreme style of control presents a significant trade-off, which will be discussed in the
following section.
6.1.1
Limitations
However, the research is subject to several key limitations. The most significant is the
exclusive reliance on simulation. While the simulator from the Agilicious framework is
proven, the sim-to-real gap remains a big challenge especially in regards of the perception
module. The true performance of the trained policies on the physical hardware platform
remains to be tested. Factors such as unmodeled physical effects and sensor noise could
drastically alter performance.
A second limitation lies in the nature of the learned control strategy. The highly aggressive
and oscillatory actions produced by the policies, while effective in simulation, could be
hard to follow by the physical drone. The current reward function, while promoting speed
and gate passing, does not sufficiently penalize this behavior. This suggests a need for
further reward shaping or architectural changes to encourage smoother, more efficient
control.

Finally, the selection of auxiliary tasks was limited to stabilization, based on prior work.
Yet, this is also where the strengths of this modular framework lie. New tasks can easily
be implemented and added to the curriculum. There is not definitive consensus on what
set of tasks could yield the best results but this platform can provide a strong basis for
future research.
6.2
Synthesis and Final Remarks
The overarching goal of this thesis was to design, implement, and evaluate a state-of-the-
art software and hardware platform for autonomous drone racing to serve as a foundation
for future competitive efforts. We present a complete pipeline, from a custom MTRL train-
ing framework built on TF-Agents to a ROS2 systems with a ready-to-use node for policy
deployment.
The central conclusion drawn from our experiments is that multi-task reinforcement learn-
ing can offer significant advantages in developing robust, high-performance flight poli-
cies. However, the system of tasks, environments and hyper-parameters have to be
tuned carefully and extensively. We showed that a policy can successfully learn from
completely different tasks and achieve at least comparable performance with single-task
trained policies while offering superiour generalization.
In conclusion, this thesis provides a solid baseline for future projects in the domain of
autonomous drone racing. It delivers a functional and extensible software system, vali-
dates its effectiveness in simulation, and produces a policy that demonstrates remarkable
resilience.

7
Future Work
This thesis is meant to build the foundation and set a general direction towards the larger
goal of winning an autonomous drone racing competition. Therefore, it is expected that
many subsequent projects will try to improve on the various components of the system
and fill in the components that were deemed outside the scope of this work. As such, the
following section serves a two-fold purpose of firstly, presenting what needs to be done to
complete the system as a functional autonomous racing drone and secondly sharing some
of the pathways we found in our research that could help improve the system beyond what
current teams show competitions.
7.1
Completing the System for Competition
To evolve the current framework from a functional proof-of-concept into a robust, competition-
ready system, several critical components must be developed and integrated. These next
steps focus on bridging the gap between controlled lab results and the unpredictable dy-
namics of a real-world race.
• Robust Perception and State Estimation A primary challenge in high-speed flight
is maintaining an accurate state estimate under extreme conditions. The perfor-
mance of Visual-Inertial Odometry (VIO) pipelines is known to degrade significantly
due to severe motion blur and rapid illumination changes, leading to catastrophic
drift. The choice and implementation of a robust perception system is critical to the
real-world deployment of our system.
• Bridging the Sim-to-Real Gap A policy trained exclusively in simulation is almost
guaranteed to fail on a physical drone due to the subtle but critical differences be-
tween simulated and real-world physics and sensor behavior. The most successful
teams have developed sophisticated methods to address this. The ”Swift” system,
for instance, used data from real-world flights to build empirical models of perception
noise and unmodeled aerodynamic forces, which were then integrated back into the
simulator for policy fine-tuning.

7.2
Pathways to Outperform the State-of-the-Art
Beyond creating a functional racing drone, the foundational framework developed in this
thesis opens several avenues for novel research that could push beyond the capabilities
of current state-of-the-art systems.
• Direct Motor Actuation The current system’s DRL policy outputs collective thrust
and body rates, which are then translated into motor commands by the onboard
BetaFlight flight controller. This introduces an abstraction layer that can limit the
ultimate agility of the system. A more radical approach is to have the neural network
output commands directly to the motors. This end-to-end architecture gives the AI
full control authority over the drone’s dynamics.
• Novel Tasks This work confirmed that using a stabilization task greatly enhances
the policy’s robustness. However, the selection of supplementary tasks is largely
unexplored. Future research could focus on creating novel tasks specifically de-
signed to promote certain flight characteristics. For instance, one could design a
task that explicitly rewards energy efficiency or control smoothness to counteract
the aggressive tendencies observed in the current policies. Another avenue is to
develop adversarial tasks, such as dodging obstacles or navigating through turbu-
lence, to train policies that are not just fast, but also reactive and adaptive to un-
predictable environments. The modular ”Task” interface makes the framework an
ideal testbed for rapidly prototyping and evaluating the impact of these new tasks
on overall performance.
