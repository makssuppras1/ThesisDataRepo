Abstract
This thesis presents a matheuristic framework for solving the Liner Shipping Network De-
sign Problem (LSNDP), integrating a path finding heuristic with a MIP model for service
scheduling and cargo routing. The framework addresses several operationally relevant el-
ements: It allows for vessel speed optimization, is not limited to a specific type of service,
and respects transit time constraints. The model is constructed on a directed time-space
graph and is evaluated on six benchmark instances from the LINERLIB dataset, ranging
from regional to global scales. Results show that the approach performs competitively
on single-hub regional networks, achieving high demand satisfaction. Scalability limita-
tions emerge as network complexity increases, particularly due to reduced transshipment,
fragmented routing, and slower convergence when solving the MIP model. A comparative
analysis with the state-of-the-art method highlights limitations to the current framework,
mainly the inability to consolidate cargo and the simplification of the time-space graph.
The findings point to three areas for improvement: graph construction, path generation
strategy, and MIP model refinement. Overall, the matheuristic demonstrates promising
results for regional networks and forms a foundation for further research on liner shipping
network design optimization.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The global economy relies on efficient and predictable liner shipping services. To stay competi-
tive, shipping companies must design routes that balance operational cost with service quality.
This problem is known as the Liner Shipping Network Design Problem (LSNDP). The objective
is to maximize profit and ensure high service quality. With fuel prices rising and growing cus-
tomer expectations, designing efficient liner shipping networks is more challenging and relevant
than ever.
Operations research (OR) techniques are widely applied in the transportation sector to im-
prove operational efficiency and reduce costs (Brouer, Alvarez, et al., 2014). In recent years,
the container shipping industry has followed this trend, as the use of OR methods has been
increasingly adopted, especially after the publication of the LINERLIB dataset by Brouer, Al-
varez, et al. (2014). The LINERLIB dataset has provided both the industry and the academic
world with a diverse set of instances for developing LSNDP solution methods, serving as a basis
for comparing different approaches.
Improvements in computational resources, as hardware and software continue to advance, have
greatly expanded the ability to solve complex problems. These advancements make it possible
to improve solution quality in NP-hard problems like the LSNDP (Koza et al., 2020).
1.2
Problem Statement
The LSNDP involves determining a set of sailing routes for a fleet of vessels to satisfy cargo
demand. The objective is to minimize operational costs while maximizing revenue and customer
satisfaction. Designing a liner shipping network involves several constraints, including vessel
availability and capacity, sailing speed and frequency, port accessibility, and fuel consumption.
Additionally, the problem is constrained by the cargo’s maximum allowed transit time. To
address these challenges, integrating service design with scheduling decisions would provide
significant advantages.
In certain studies such as Koza et al. (2020) the integrated liner shipping network design
and scheduling problem is referred to as LSNDSP. To avoid the confusion of using multiple
alternating acronyms, the broader abbreviation LSNDP is used in this report even when the
extension of integrated vessel scheduling is implied.
The LSNDP involves solving two major interconnected problems:
1. Vessel Planning: This process involves selecting which services to operate, scheduling
vessel departures and arrivals, and assigning vessels to the services. It requires consid-
1
==PAGE:13==
eration of vessel availability, capacity, speed options, and cost associated with operating
different vessel classes.
2. Cargo Routing and Demand Fulfillment: This involves determining how container
demand is routed across the selected services to meet customer requirements while mini-
mizing total cost and maximizing revenue.
1.3
Complexity of the Problem
The LSNDP is computationally challenging due to the huge number of possible vessel rotations
and cargo routing combinations. Both Brouer, Alvarez, et al. (2014) and Koza et al. (2020)
have proven the problem to be NP-hard, and even state-of-the-art exact algorithms are limited
to solving only small instances. The inclusion of realistic factors, such as variable sailing speeds,
port handling costs, and service frequency constraints, further increases the model’s complexity.
Since the LSNDP is NP-hard, a matheuristic approach - combining heuristic methods with
exact techniques - is often applied to solve practical instances of the LSNDP at scale.
1.4
Research Objective
The main objective of this thesis is to develop an integrated matheuristic framework for the
LSNDP that simultaneously addresses service design, scheduling, and cargo routing. The objec-
tive is to create a matheuristic that combines heuristic techniques with an exact mathematical
program. The quality of the developed framework will be evaluated using the benchmark data
from LINERLIB.
1.5
Thesis Structure
This thesis consists of 10 sections: The current Section 1: Introduction provides a brief overview
of the background of the LSNDP and the research objective of the thesis. Section 2: Problem
Definition is an overview of the domain of the liner shipping industry and its main elements.
Section 3: Literature Review is a summary of the methods traditionally used to solve the LSNDP
and a review of previous solution approaches. Section 4: Theory introduces the algorithms used
in the thesis. Section 5: Methodology offers a detailed description of the matheuristic framework
proposed in this thesis. Section 6: Experimental Setup: LINERLIB introduces the benchmark
instances used to evaluate the matheuristic. Section 7: Performance Evaluation presents the
KPIs used to evaluate the matheuristic. Section 8: Results presents the computational results
obtained by applying the matheuristic to the LINERLIB dataset. Section 9: Discussion dis-

Problem Definition
2.1
The Liner-Shipping Industry
Liner shipping and the development of the container system have significantly transformed the
cargo shipping industry. The introduction of standardized containers in the 1960s increased
productivity, reduced transportation costs, and transit times. Today, global goods can be trans-
ported by using standard containers and scheduled shipping services that connect nearly all
ports worldwide. As illustrated in Figure 1, container trade continues to grow, with volumes
increasing year over year. The companies operating these services are know as carriers (Stop-
ford, 2009).
Figure 1: Global container volume (Millions) and annual change in Twenty-Foot Equivalent
Unit (TEU) (UNCTAD, 2024)
2.2
Services
A liner shipping service refers to a fleet of vessels that operate on a fixed schedule and follow a
designed route between ports, allowing cargo to be transported along these services (Stopford,
2009). A service therefore follows a fixed frequency and a standard route with ports visited in
a specific sequence. Services are typically of weekly or biweekly frequency depending on the
carrier, and are typically operated by vessels of the same class (Koza et al., 2020). The number
of vessels required for a service is determined by dividing the total duration of the service’s
rotation by its frequency. The total duration of a service includes both the sailing times between
ports and the time spent at each port for loading and unloading operations (Koza et al., 2020).
The duration of a service can range from one week up to 20 weeks, with the average duration
of around 8-9 weeks (Brouer, Alvarez, et al., 2014). Within the shipping industry, services
3
==PAGE:15==
are categorized based on their routing pattern and can be: End-to-End, Trunk services, Line
bundled, Indirect feeder services, Direct feeder services, Round-the-world (RTW), Pendulum,
Butterfly, Conveyor belt according to Notteboom (2004). Figure 2 illustrates a butterfly service
in the Baltic region.
Figure 2: Example of a butterfly service in the Baltic region, where Aarhus is the central node
(Christiansen et al., 2020).
2.3
Sailing legs, Transshipments & Ports
The liner shipping network consists of ports and sailing legs, where each sailing leg represents a
direct connection between two ports. To transport cargo from its origin to destination, it must
be routed through the network, that can involve multiple services. Transferring cargo from one
service to another is referred to as a transshipment. The duration of a transshipment depends on
the schedules of both the loading and unloading vessels. A minimum time is typically required
to account for container unloading, storage, transport between quays, and reloading the cargo
onto the next vessel (Koza et al., 2020).
A port can consist of several terminals.
To minimize internal cargo transfers and ensure
smoother service connections, carriers typically prefer to use the same terminal at each port.
However, both ports and individual terminals has its own physical constraints, such as max-
imum draft and berth length limits. These limitations can lead to incompatibilities between
certain vessel classes and specific ports and terminals (Brouer, Alvarez, et al., 2014).
Ports charge a fee for each incoming vessel, which depends on the vessel class.
The ports
also charge for cargo handling, with separate fees for loading, unloading, and transshipment
(Brouer, Alvarez, et al., 2014).
4
==PAGE:16==
Port stay time refers to the total time a vessel spends at a port, including waiting time for
a berth, loading, unloading, and transshipment of cargo. A vessel’s port stay time therefore
depends on the number of containers that need to be handled. Ports have also optimized their
operations, allocating cranes and personnel based on vessel size. As a result, vessels of different
sizes tend to have similar port stay times. This observation is supported by data from Maersk
Line (Brouer, Alvarez, et al., 2014; Koza et al., 2020), and a constant port stay time across
vessel classes is assumed in this thesis.
2.4
Vessels
Container vessels are separated into different classes based on their specifications, such as
FFE (forty-foot equivalent) capacity, speed, length, beam, and draft. Among these, the most
critical attributes for the LSNDP are the FFE capacity, draft, and speed, as they determine
which services a vessel class can operate (Brouer, Alvarez, et al., 2014).
In this thesis, each vessel class has a minimum speed, a maximum speed, and a design speed
at which fuel consumption is optimized.
The minimum speed is the slow steaming speed
for the specific vessel class. Slow steaming is a common practice among carriers to reduce
fuel consumption and lower operating costs. Furthermore, slow steaming also enables lower
greenhouse gas emissions and improved schedule reliability (Maloni et al., 2013).
2.5
Cost of Operation
Operating liner shipping services involves various costs for the carriers, which can be separated
into two main categories: fleet costs and cargo handling costs (Brouer, Alvarez, et al., 2014).
• Fleet Costs:
– Bunker cost is the cost of fuel consumed by the vessels.
– Capital cost is the cost of acquiring or chartering a vessel.
– Canal cost is the canal fee imposed by authorities for vessels traversing a canal.
– Operational cost is the cost of operating a vessel, including crew, maintenance and
insurance.
• Cargo Handling Costs:
– Loading/Unloading cost is the container handling cost at each port.
– Transshipment cost is the cost of transshipping a container at each port.
– Equipment cost is the cost of owning or leasing a container.
5
==PAGE:17==
Bunker consumption varies across vessel classes depending on sailing speed and load, and is
often approximated using a cubic function of speed (Psaraftis & Kontovas, 2014). The canal
fee is also different for each vessel class, since it depends on the size and the load of the vessel.
The market rate of a container ship is called time charter rate (TC rate) and is the cost of
leasing a vessel (Brouer, Alvarez, et al., 2014).

3
Literature Review
This section provides an overview of the general network design problem and introduces how OR
techniques have been applied to combinatorial optimization problems. It includes a summary
of previous research on the LSNDP along with a classification of the most common solution
methods.
3.1
The Network Design Problem
Network design problems are a common class of optimization problems frequently encountered
in the transportation and telecommunication industries. These problems focus on identify-
ing the most efficient use of resources to transport goods or transmit information. Solving
such problems typically involves selecting which nodes to include (such as ports, hubs, or
warehouses), determining the sequence between them, and allocating resources (like vessels or
vehicles). The objective is to minimize costs, maximize revenue, and service quality, while
respecting constraints related to service requirements and resource availability.
The general network design problem is typically formulated as a Mixed Integer Program (MIP).
It combines binary or integer variables to model decisions, such as selecting routes or opening
facilities, with continuous variables that represent flow quantities like cargo, passengers, or data
(Magnanti & Wong, 1984). As the network grows, these problems become increasingly more
complex due to the increased number of binary and integer variables. As a result, solving them
directly with a MIP solver is not practical and is only manageable for small instances. To
address this, more advanced solution techniques have been developed, from column generation
(L¨ubbecke & Desrosiers, 2005) to heuristics and matheuristic methods (Ngoo et al., 2024).
3.2
OR Methods in Combinatorial Optimization Problems
In many areas of OR, such as logistics, crew scheduling, production planning, and transporta-
tion, optimization problems involve making the best decisions within a combinatorially large
solution space. Many of these problems are known to be NP-hard, meaning that the time re-
quired to solve them to optimality grows exponentially with the size of the problem (L¨ubbecke &
Desrosiers, 2005). To successfully address NP-hard problems several solution techniques have
been proposed, including column generation and heuristic methods (L¨ubbecke & Desrosiers,
2005). However, in capacitated network design problems, linear relaxation often provides a
poor approximation of the MIP solution space. As a result, combining exact and heuristic
methods in a single framework has shown promising results (Crainic, 2000). This hybrid ap-
proach is known as a matheuristic.
7
==PAGE:19==
3.3
Related Efforts in LSNDP
The LSNDP has attracted considerable interest from the maritime OR community due to its
inherent complexity, strategic importance, and potential for reducing operational cost.
As
mentioned before, the LSNDP also includes several well studied maritime subproblems, such as
fleet deployment, ship routing and scheduling, speed optimization, and cargo transport (Koza et
al., 2020). Researchers have addressed many variations and combinations of these subproblems
in different contexts. Several literature reviews on the LSNDP-related problems exist, including
recent ones by Christiansen et al. (2020) and Christiansen et al. (2025), which serve as as the
main references for this review.
3.3.1
Evolution of LSNDP models
The literature on LSNDP solution methods has advanced significantly, evolving from basic
models which ignored interactions between routes to more sophisticated approaches that incor-
porate transshipments, speed optimization, and transit time constraints.
Early research on LSNDP was based on simplified modeling approaches and often required
substantial user interaction. Olson et al. (1969) were among the first to address the scheduling
of cargo ships in liner trade through simulation models. Following this, Boffey et al. (1979)
developed a heuristic model to support the scheduling of container ships on the North Atlantic
route. Later, Lane et al. (1987) developed an interactive model that used cost data and set
partitioning to find the best combinations of shipping routes.
In the early 1990s, linear programming (LP) models were introduced to minimize fleet operation
and chartering costs (Jaramillo & Perakis, 1991; Perakis & Jaramillo, 1991; Rana & Vickson,
1991). In the middle of the 1990s, Cho and Perakis (1996) developed a MIP model to select
optimal routes and service frequencies from a predefined route set. Similarly, Fagerholt (2004)
introduced a regional liner shipping model inspired by the vehicle routing problem. A similar
regional model was build by Karlaftis et al. (2009) for transport in the Aegean Sea. One major
limitation in these early models was that they did not consider transshipments between services.
Major advancements were achieved when researchers began incorporating weekly services and
explicitly allowing transshipments. Agarwal and Ergun (2008) were the first to develop a model
that combined ship scheduling with cargo routing while also incorporating a weekly frequency.
The major drawback was that it did not account for transshipment costs or transit time limits.
´Alvarez (2009) introduced a model that combines routing with fleet deployment.
Later, a
key improvement came from Reinhardt and Pisinger (2012), who were able to incorporate
transshipment costs. Their study significantly contributed to the LSNDP literature, since it
incorporated fleet assignment, speed options, and penalties for unmet demand. However, the
8
==PAGE:20==
model did not account for transshipment for all types of services.
The complexity of the models continued to grow with the integration of transit time limits
and speed optimization, which made them more realistic (Gelareh et al. (2010), Meng and
Wang (2011b), Karsten et al. (2017), Thun et al. (2017)). Koza et al. (2020) introduced one
of the most advanced models to date, combining transshipment, transit time limits, and speed
optimization, while also providing detailed schedules for each service. This model forms the
foundation for the approach used in this thesis. Another model by Galle and Tangl (2024) also
integrates network design, scheduling, and container routing and claims better results across
all LINERLIB instances. However, this model remains unpublished and relies on unspecified
assumptions.
3.3.2
Classification of Solution Methods
Christiansen et al. (2020) classified solution methods for LSNDP into four groups:
• MIP-based algorithms: These models combine service planning and container routing
into a single MIP model.
Examples include work by ´Alvarez (2009), Reinhardt and
Pisinger (2012), Plum, Pisinger, Salazar-Gonzalez, and Sigurd (2014) and Plum, Pisinger,
and Sigurd (2014), and Wang and Meng (2014).
• Two-stage algorithms: This method first defines service routes and then determines
container flows. Examples include Agarwal and Ergun (2008), ´Alvarez (2009), Brouer,
Alvarez, et al. (2014), Mulder and Dekker (2014), Karsten et al. (2017), Thun et al.
(2017), and Gelareh et al. (2010).
• Subset of routes approaches: These approaches select a subset of predefined routes
from a candidate pool, aiming to incorporate existing good routes. Heuristic based ex-
amples include Meng and Wang (2011a) and Balakrishnan and Karsten (2017).
• Backbone flow methods: This method first routes containers through a network and
then selects vessel services to serve those cargo flows. Example can be seen by Krogsgaard
et al. (2018).

3.4
Motivation for the proposed solution method
Despite extensive research into LSNDP, current methods still encounter key limitations, par-
ticularly in balancing realistic constraints with computational efficiency. Exact MIP models
often struggle with scalability, while the simplicity of many heuristic approaches limits their
practical use (Crainic, 2000).
This thesis proposes a new approach that combines heuristic methods with an exact mathe-
matical model, using different path finding algorithms within a matheuristic framework. At the
core of the matheuristic framework is the A* algorithm, selected for its speed and near optimal
performance, which has led to its widespread use in many applications (Konakalla, 2014). This
matheuristic framework will try to address two major gaps: the need for approaches that inte-
grate service design, scheduling and cargo routing, and the limited exploration of graph-based
heuristics in LSNDP.

4.1
Dijkstra’s Algorithm
Dijkstra’s algorithm is an exact method used to compute the shortest path from a source
node to all other nodes in a directed graph with non-negative arc costs. The algorithm was
introduced by E. W. Dijkstra in 1959, and works by gradually expanding the set of nodes with
known shortest distances from the source until all nodes have been evaluated (Dijkstra, 1959).
Each node has a distance label that records the current best known distance from the source to
that node. These labels are updated whenever a shorter path to the node is discovered through
one of its neighboring nodes. The algorithm continues this process until the shortest distances to
all reachable nodes have been computed (Dijkstra, 1959). The following pseudocode illustrates
the logic of the algorithm:

4.2
A* Algorithm
The A* algorithm (pronounced A-star) is a heuristic method that builds on Dijkstra’s algorithm
by guiding the search with a heuristic function. In the A* algorithm the most promising node
will always be expanded next. The most promising node is determined based on a combination
of the distance traveled so far and an estimate of the remaining distance to the target node.
Developed in 1968 by Peter Hart, Nils Nilsson, and Bertram Raphael, A* has become a standard
technique to estimate the shortest path due to its accuracy and ability to reduce the number
of nodes explored (Konakalla, 2014).
The A* algorithm evaluates each node based on a priority score defined as: f(n) = g(n)+h(n).
Here, g(n) is the known distance from the source to the current node n, and h(n) is a heuristic
that estimates the remaining distance from node n, to the target node, t. The algorithm works
by expanding the node with the lowest priority score. The accuracy of the A* algorithm is
therefore heavily influenced by the heuristic method selected. If the heuristic method closely
estimates the remaining distance, the A* algorithm is more likely to find the optimal path
(Konakalla, 2014). The pseudocode below outlines the general structure of the A* algorithm:

Yen’s K-Shortest Paths Algorithm
Yen’s algorithm is a method for finding the K shortest loopless paths from a source node s to
a target node t in a directed graph. The algorithm was introduced by Yen (1971).
The algorithm begins by computing the shortest path from the source node s to the target
node t. To generate the remaining K −1 paths, it systematically introduces deviations from
the previously found paths. In each iteration, a spur node is chosen along the most recently
added path, and a root path is formed from the source to this spur node. To ensure that the
new path is different from earlier ones, the algorithm removes edges or sets their distances
to infinity, preventing the reconstruction of previously found paths. A new spur path is then
computed from the spur node to the target node t. The new shortest path is obtained by
joining the root and spur paths. The resulting path must be loopless and unique from any
previously generated paths. This process continues until K unique loopless paths have been
found (Yen, 1971). The pseudocode below outlines the general structure of Yen’s K-shortest
paths algorithm:

Methodology
5.1
Network Formulation
The graph developed to solve the LSNDP is a directed time-space graph, similar to the one
exemplified in Koza et al. (2020). The graph was developed to reflect physical constraints and
assumptions relevant to real world applications, based on input provided by Maersk.
5.1.1
Time-Space Directed Graph
Graph G = {V, A} consists of time-space vertices V and arcs A. The set of ports is denoted
by P, and the set of feasible sailing legs between ports is denoted by L. The set H represents
time and is discretized into uniform time steps of length h. Since services are weekly, the time
horizon H is set to 168 hours. Each vertex v (used interchangeably with the term node) denotes
a port p at a specific time h. Therefore each vertex v = {i, h} ∈V represents a port i at a
specific time h. Figure 3 illustrates a time-space graph with 4 ports and a time discretization
of h = 24 hours.
Figure 3: Time Space Graph
5.1.2
Sailing and Transshipment Arcs
The arc set A consists of sailing arcs AS and transshipment arcs AT. A sailing arc a ∈AS
represents a connection between two vertices (i1, h1) and (i2, h2), with i1 ̸= i2, if a feasible
sailing leg l exists between port i1 and i2, departing from i1 at time h1 and being ready for
departure from i2 at time h2. Set E represents vessel classes, characterized by varying drafts,
ranges of speeds, capacity and bunker consumption. One sailing arc is created for each sailing
14
==PAGE:26==
speed (min, design, max) of each vessel class departing from each node. A transshipment arc
a ∈AT represents the transshipment of cargo from one service to another at a port i ∈P,
connecting two vertices (i, t1) and (i, t2), with t1 ̸= t2.
5.1.3
Arc Feasibility
After the creation of the sailing and transshipment arcs, they are tested to ensure they comply
with a series of feasibility criteria and they are filtered accordingly. The feasibility of the arcs
is based on the following criteria:
• The duration of a sailing arc cannot exceed four weeks.
• The duration of a transshipment arc cannot exceed one week plus a minimum transship-
ment time.
• Vessel class draft cannot exceed destination port draft.
• Vessel class is able to traverse the Panama canal for sailing arcs that go through the canal.
• If more than one sailing arcs have the same origin, destination, departure time, arrival
time and vessel class, only the arc with the slowest speed (and therefore the minimum
cost) is considered.
The maximum allowable duration of a sailing arc is important for the size of the resulting
graph and therefore for the solution quality. A large or unbounded upper limit on sailing arc
duration might allow the connection of port pairs that would otherwise be infeasible in practice.
However, setting an upper bound leads to a smaller graph which decreases computation times
and increases efficiency. Therefore, the choice of a maximum arc duration is a balance between
a larger solution space and a more resource efficient solution approach. The 4-week limit was
chosen based on an effort to keep a computationally reasonable graph size while maintaining
realistic operational assumptions, based on input provided by Maersk regarding the maximum
sailing leg duration in their current operation network.
The duration of one week plus a
minimum transshipment time for the transshipment arcs comes as an intuitively logical bound
for networks where services occur weekly, enabling the capture of all services calling a port.
5.1.4
Arc Attributes
Each arc in the graph is characterized by a series of attributes.
These attributes are the
departure port and time, the destination port and time, the vessel class, the vessel speed and
the arc cost.
The sailing time of each sailing arc a ∈AS is calculated as the sum of the sailing duration and
the port stay time that is defined in Section 2.3. A port stay time of 24 hours is assumed for

all vessels at all ports as used in previous studies (Karsten et al. (2017), Koza et al. (2020)).
The duration of each transshipment arc is defined as the time interval between the arrival of
the unloading vessel and the departure of the loading vessel, considering a minimum layover
time necessary for transshipment. Similar to Koza et al. (2020), a minimum transshipment
time of 48 hours is used.
The cost of each sailing arc is calculated as the sum of the vessel chartering cost, the sailing
cost due to bunker consumption (including idling consumption during port stay time), the port

calculate the bunker costs, as used in Karsten et al. (2017) and Koza et al. (2020). Since the

5.3
Generating Feasible Cargo Paths
In the theory section 4, three algorithms were introduced: Dijkstra, A*, and Yen’s K-shortest
paths algorithm. In this section, these algorithms are the foundation to generate cargo paths for
each demand stream k ∈K which can then be used in the MIP model presented in the previous
section. Each cargo path must satisfy the maximum transit time defined for its corresponding
demand k ∈K. The cargo paths are generated in two phases. First, an initial phase using a
tailored Dijkstra algorithm. Second, a subproblem phase that combines a tailored version of
the A* algorithm with Yen’s algorithm.

5.3.2
A*-Targeted Path Generation
The A*-Targeted heuristic is a modified version of the normal A* algorithm presented in Sec-
tion 4.2.
The A*-Targeted algorithm is also tailored to the LSNDP. First, it respects the
maximum transit time constraint defined for each demand k ∈K. Second, it introduces a
strategy to guide the search toward diverse paths that align with a specified preferred arrival
time. Rather than simply identifying the shortest path, the algorithm generates paths that
arrive close to a targeted arrival time. Its behavior is therefore flexible and makes it well-suited
for generating structurally diverse cargo paths that vary in time.
The algorithm is initialized with a preferred arrival time at the destination node. During node
expansion, it uses a tailored heuristic to assign each node with a priority. The priority score
is calculated based on two components: (1) the absolute deviation between the current path’s
transit time and the preferred arrival time, and (2) the estimated remaining transit time from
the current node to the destination, precomputed using a reversed Dijkstra algorithm.
By combining these two elements, the algorithm creates a time-aware priority rule that directs
the search toward feasible paths that is close to the preferred arrival time at the target node.

The structure of this algorithm is based on Yen’s K-shortest path algorithm, as introduced in
Section 4.3, but adapted to generate time-feasible paths that align with a preferred arrival time
and obey the transit time limit. The first path is computed using the A*-Targeted heuristic.
The remaining K −1 paths still use the logic from the A*-Targeted heuristic and are generated
by following the framework of the classic Yen’s algorithm from Section 4.3.
To ensure different paths are created, arcs that would recreate previously found paths are
removed before generating new paths. Each candidate path is evaluated based on how close
its arrival time is to the preferred arrival time. This time-targeted variance of Yen’s algorithm
produces structurally different cargo paths that hopefully improves the solution quality of the
master problem.

Experimental Setup: LINERLIB
To evaluate the proposed matheuristic framework, we use the benchmark instances from the
publicly available LINERLIB dataset.
Developed for the LSNDP, the LINERLIB provides
detailed specifications for port locations, vessel classes, and weekly container demand between
ports. The dataset is used across academic literature and the state-of-the-art method from
Koza et al. (2020) has been tested on the dataset.
The LINERLIB instances used in this study vary significantly in terms of network scale, geo-
graphical coverage, and operational complexity. This diversity allows for a robust assessment
of the matheuristic’s performance across different problem dimensions.
Specifically, the following aspects are tested using the LINERLIB dataset:
• Graph Construction: A time-expanded graph is constructed for each instance, where
nodes represent port-time combinations and arcs represent sailing or transshipment. This
representation captures the dynamics required for modeling vessel services and cargo
movements.
• Matheuristic Evaluation: The instances are used to assess the computational perfor-
mance and solution quality of the matheuristic approach proposed in Section 5.5.
6.1
Network Size
The LINERLIB instances vary significantly in network size, spanning from single-hub regional
networks to global multi-hub networks. The smallest instance is the Baltic that consists of 12
ports and 132 sailing legs with an average length of 628 nmi, representing a simple single-hub
regional network with relatively small distances between ports. The next regional single-hub
instance is the WAF, representing the West-African area with 20 ports and 402 sailing legs and
an average leg length of 2,096 nmi.
The Mediterranean instance consists of 39 ports and 1,482 sailing legs with an average length
of 1,242 nmi. It is a multi-hub network, characterized by relatively small distances between
ports. The Pacific instance consists of 45 ports and 2,122 sailing legs. It represents the Asia
and U.S. West Coast trade lane and has an average sailing length of 4,475 nmi.
Larger instances, such as World Small and EuropeAsia, reflect major trade corridors and in-
troduce additional operational constraints, such as long-haul transit and canal restrictions.
WorldSmall is a global multi-hub network consisting of 47 main ports and 3,142 sailing legs
with an average length of 8,042 nmi. EuropeAsia represents the trade lane between Europe,
Middle East and the Far East regions and consists of 114 ports and 19,217 sailing legs with an
average length of 7,316 nmi.

Demand Characteristics
Cargo demand in each instance is represented by weekly container flows between OD pairs. Each

In addition to the variation in the number of demand streams, the size of each stream also
differs significantly. Some OD-pairs involve only a few containers per week, while others consist
of several hundred as shown in Table 2.
The Baltic instance is a single-hub network, with Bremerhaven functioning as the main hub
from where most of the demand is originating. The WAF instance has a similar structure, with
Algeciras being a main hub feeding demand to many ports. In contrast, the Mediterranean
instance represents a multi-hub network with demand streams distributed over a broader set
of ports.
The Pacific and EuropeAsia instances represent trade lane networks, with large
demands between specific pairs of ports and significantly smaller demands between the majority
of the other ports pairs. The WorldSmall instance represents a global network with all kinds
of demand profiles. All demand data is deterministic and static, and the weekly demand is
consistent over the planning horizon. An overview of demand streams, volumes, and OD-pair
density is presented in Table 2.

Performance Evaluation
To evaluate the matheuristic, a set of key performance indicators (KPIs) is defined across four
dimensions: economic performance, demand satisfaction, network size, and fleet utilization.
These KPIs will provide inside into the matheuristic performance across the instances from the
LINERLIB dataset.
7.1
Economic Performance
Objective value: Represents the optimal value (upper bound) of the MIP model when the
solver terminates. The objective function maximizes the total profit while incorporating penal-
ties for unmet demand as described in Section 5.2.
Lower bound: Represents the near potentially best objective value that could be achieved by
the MIP model if the solver did not terminate due to a set time limit.
Economics: Includes total revenue and total cost for the final solution. The resulting profit,
calculated as revenue minus cost, indicates whether the proposed network design is economically
viable.
7.2
Demand Satisfaction
Demand satisfaction: Measured both in absolute volume (FEUs fulfilled) and as a percentage
of total weekly demand. This KPI is essential for evaluating how effective the matheuristic
framework is in solving the LSNDP.
Demand stream classification: Breaks down the total demand streams into fully served,
partially served, or rejected categories. It also reports the average volume transported in each
category, offering insight into which demand streams are satisfied and which are rejected by
the matheuristic framework.
7.3
Network Size
Computational time: Is measured across the three stages of the matheuristic framework: Ini-
tial path generation, the subproblem, and the master problem, together with the total runtime.
This KPI helps evaluate the matheuristic framework’s computational behavior and identify
bottlenecks.
Total paths generated (Ωk): Measures the number of unique feasible cargo paths added to
the master problem across all demands k ∈K. Each path introduces a corresponding decision
variable xk
j, contributing to the MIP model’s complexity.
32
==PAGE:44==
Sailing arc usage (%): Indicates the proportion of sailing arcs used in the selected cargo
paths. Higher usage of sailing arcs indicates more direct routing and less transshipment.
Transshipment arc usage (%): Indicates the proportion of transshipment arcs used in the
selected cargo paths. Higher usage indicates greater cargo consolidation at intermediate port
time nodes.
7.4
Fleet Utilization
Vessel deployment: Reports the number of vessels deployed from each vessel class, showing
how the fleet is utilized.
Capacity utilization (%): Indicates how much of each vessel’s capacity is used on average
across its sailing route. This highlights operational efficiency and indicates if there is a potential
to serve additional demand.
These KPIs provide a comprehensive overview of the matheuristic’s performance on the LINER-
LIB dataset. Furthermore, the KPIs are used to compare the results with the state-of-the-art
method by Koza et al. (2020).

Results
This section presents the computational results of the matheuristic framework applied on the
six instances from the LINERLIB database introduced in Section 6. The KPIs introduced in
Section 7 are used to evaluate the solution quality across instances. Results are presented by
instance size, beginning with the regional networks Baltic and WAF, followed by the interconti-

8.1
Baltic
The Baltic instance from the LINERLIB dataset was selected to evaluate the performance of
the proposed matheuristic on a regional single-hub liner shipping network. As the smallest
instance in the dataset, it consists of a limited number of ports and a relatively small set of
demand streams, as summarized in Table 3.
To account for the stochasticity in the heuristic path generation process, the instance was solved
multiple times. A total of 35 runs were performed, with five runs for each max iterations
setting: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 15, and 20. The best performing configuration was selected based on
the objective value obtained from the MIP model in Section 5.2, as well as the corresponding
runtime.
All averaged results across the tested configurations, along with the corresponding solver set-
tings, are provided in Appendix A. Among the configurations tested, max iterations set to 8
achieved the best objective value with the lowest runtime. The results presented in the follow-
ing sections are based on this configuration and include both the best solution identified among
the five runs and the corresponding average.
Economic Performance
Table 4 summarizes the economic performance of the best solution for the Baltic instance, which
achieved an objective value of −214,446. The objective value reflects a balance between gener-
ated revenue, operational costs, and penalties for unmet demand, as described in Section 5.2.

Discussion
This section discusses the computational results for the six LINERLIB instances, examining
the scalability of the proposed matheuristic framework and evaluates its performance against
the state-of-the-art results from by Koza et al. (2020). The discussion directly addresses the
thesis objective: to develop a matheuristic framework for the LSNDP and evaluate its solution
quality against benchmark results.
Across all instances, the proposed matheuristic framework consistently produced feasible and
often profitable solutions. However, as network size and complexity increased, a shift in the
frameworks performance emerged, that highlighted both structural and computational bottle-
necks. These included limited cargo consolidation and low utilization of vessel capacity in larger
networks, that ultimately resulted in lower overall demand satisfaction.
Performance varied significantly across the instances. Regional single-hub networks such as the
Baltic and WAF achieved high demand satisfaction. In both instances demand satisfaction
was above 90%, supported by full vessel deployment and strong average capacity utilization.
Intercontinental networks, such as the Mediterranean and Pacific showed less promising results,
fulfilling only 34.0% and 61.2% of weekly demand. Global instances, such as the World Small
and Europe Asia, clearly showed limitations of the matheuristic framework, with demand satis-
faction dropping to 36.0% and 20.1%, respectively. Average vessel utilization in these instances
also declined to around 30%, with some vessel classes not deployed at all in the larger networks.
These results indicate that the matheuristic framework struggled to consolidate cargo flows in
larger networks, since insufficient transshipment opportunities were created in the path gener-
ation strategy.
To discuss these results, the section is structured into the following sections. It begins with a
comparison to the state-of-the-art solution approach from Koza et al. (2020). Afterwards, the
core modeling components are discussed: 1) graph construction, 2) path generation strategy,
and 3) MIP model formulation. Each component addresses areas for future research, and the
discussion ends with a reflection on real world implications.

To uncover the root causes of these limitations, the next three sections examine the core mod-
eling components of the matheuristic framework and their impact on performance as network
size increases. These components are: (1) graph construction, (2) path generation strategy,
and (3) MIP model formulation.
9.2
Graph Construction
The performance and scalability of the matheuristic framework are closely linked to the struc-
ture and size of the underlying time-expanded graph. In the time-expanded graph, each node
63
==PAGE:75==
represents a specific port at a discrete time, while arcs denote feasible sailing or transshipment
connections between port time nodes. Because each sailing arc introduces at least one binary
decision variable ye
a in the master problem, controlling the graph size is essential to keep the
MIP model manageable in larger instances.
To manage the size of the graph, two key feasibility criteria were introduced when constructing
the sailing arcs, separating this framework from the approach of Koza et al. (2020). First, only
sailing arcs with a maximum transit duration of four weeks were feasible. This assumption
reflects realistic operations and the reasoning behind it is explained in Section 5.1.3. While
this constraint simplifies the graph, it also excludes long haul connections that could be used in
the benchmark data from Koza et al. (2020). Second, only three vessel speeds were considered
when generating sailing arcs between any port pair: minimum, design, and maximum speed.
This significantly reduces the number of arcs and the graph’s overall size, but also restricts
routing flexibility by eliminating possible sailing arcs.
The state-of-the-art model from Koza et al. (2020) does not apply these two feasibility criteria.
Instead, the time-expanded graph includes all feasible sailing speeds within a continuous range
from minimum to maximum. Furthermore, no strict upper limit is imposed on the duration
of the sailing arcs.
As a result, the graph from Koza et al. (2020) contains substantially
more sailing arcs, leading to a greater number of binary decision variables ye
a, as illustrated in
Table 45. This expanded graph structure provides the model with greater routing flexibility and
improves its ability to support transshipment. These differences become particularly important
in larger networks with long distances between ports.
Table 45 compares the size of the time-expanded graphs and resulting MIP models for the
matheuristic and Koza et al. (2020) across all six LINERLIB instances. The differences be-
come substantial as the instances grow in size. For example, in the Europe Asia instance, the
matheuristic graph contains approximately 976,000 arcs and 1.76 million binary variables in
the MIP model, whereas the graph used by Koza et al. (2020) consists of over 7.3 million arcs
and more than 16 million binary variables in the MIP model. Reducing the graph size was
essential to keeping solution times manageable, both during the path generation phases and
especially during the final MIP solve.

to port connections. A hybrid approach could improve routing flexibility and make transship-

reasonable computational times.

9.3
Path Generation Strategy
The performance of the matheuristic framework is based on the quality and structure of the
cargo paths generated during the initial phase, and especially in the subproblem phase. How
paths are generated and which transshipment arcs are used directly affect the opportunities
for cargo consolidation and ultimately determines the level of demand satisfaction that can
be achieved by the master problem. Across all instances, the same underlying heuristics were
used: Dijkstra’s algorithm in the initial phase and the A*-Target algorithm in the subproblem.
However, as network size increases, these methods struggle to generate paths that support
transshipment, thereby limiting the framework’s ability to consolidate cargo effectively.
In regional single-hub instances such as the Baltic and WAF, the path generation process
performed well. Transshipment rates of 37.5% and 25.4% were observed, supporting high vessel
utilization and enabling consolidation of demand streams. As a result, Baltic achieved a demand
satisfaction of 92.1% and WAF reached 96.2%. The feasibility of consolidating demand streams
in smaller networks comes from the higher likelihood that cargo paths intersect at common port
time nodes, even without specifically tailoring the algorithms to this behavior.
In the intercontinental Mediterranean and Pacific instances, as well as the global World Small
and Europe Asia cases, the network size increases exponentially - even with the simplified graph
design - as shown in Table 44. As a result, transshipment usage drop to between 5% and 20% in
these instances, and vessel utilization declines to around 30%. In contrast, Koza et al. (2020)
achieve vessel utilization rates close to 70% in larger instances by using substantially more
transshipment arcs compared to regional ones. This pattern reinforces a key limitation of the
matheuristic framework: limited use of transshipment leads to less cargo consolidation, reduced
vessel utilization, and ultimately lower demand satisfaction. The limitation of the matheuristic
lies within in the behavior of the path generation algorithm. The matheuristic relies heavily on
the customized A*-Target algorithm, which only focuses on reaching the target node as close as
possible to the preferred arrival time. However, it does not actively try to generate paths from
different demand streams that intersect at common port time nodes or shared transshipment
hubs. In the matheuristic transshipments are therefore based on the paths randomly overlap-
ping. As network size grows exponentially, the probability of cargo paths aligning at the same
port time nodes becomes unlikely unless the algorithm is tailored to facilitate this behavior. A
potential improvement could be to strategically construct transshipment hubs, ensuring that
paths from different demand streams overlap at designated port time nodes. This design would
facilitate consolidation, enabling shared services and ultimately improve both demand satisfac-
tion and overall profitability.
To improve the matheuristic framework, and especially the A*-Target algorithm, future re-
search should focus on promoting transshipment more intentionally. One option is to guide
66
==PAGE:78==
the algorithm to generate multiple feasible paths from different demand streams that share
the same transshipment hubs. More specifically, paths should intersect at the same port time
nodes. For example, certain ports could be designated as consolidation hubs, and the algorithm
could be guided to prioritize paths that intersect at these hubs across several time steps. This
would increase the likelihood of paths overlapping and create more opportunities for cargo con-
solidation and improve overall demand satisfaction.
In summary, the path generation strategy is central to the scalability and performance of
the matheuristic framework. While the current approach performs well in single-hub regional
networks, it does not enable sufficient cargo consolidation in larger and more complex networks.
Improving transshipment usage in path generation represents a key opportunity for future
research to improve cargo consolidation.
9.4
MIP Model
The final phase of the matheuristic framework involves solving the MIP model, where vessel
services are constructed by assigning vessel classes to arcs and routing cargo along selected
paths. Across all instances, the master problem consistently accounts for the majority of the
total runtime, especially from the WAF instance and onward.
In the larger networks, the
number of binary decision variables ye
a - associated with sailing arcs and vessel classes - grows
exponentially, while the number of continuous variables xk
j increases with the number of added
paths. As a result, the solver often hits the time limit without proving optimality, making the
MIP model the computational bottleneck in the framework.
Even when the solver terminates due to the time limit, it still reports a lower bound that
represents the potential best objective value that can be achieved with the current set of cargo
paths. In the World Small instance, which had the worst optimality gap of 28%, the solver
returned a lower bound of 47.6 million, compared to Koza et al. (2020) which had an objective
value of -38 million. This highlights a key insight: allocating additional computational time to
the MIP model will never improve the solution beyond the current lower bound and close the gap
to Koza et al. (2020) unless the quality of the generated paths is improved. The quality of the
solution is fundamentally constrained by the quality of the generated paths. As long as the path
set lacks the ability to consolidate demand streams, the MIP model cannot deliver solutions
comparable to Koza et al. (2020). Consequently, solver optimization has limited impact on
improving solution quality but can still help speed up convergence within the current MIP
model.
A major inefficiency in the current framework is the inclusion of long cargo paths that are never
selected in the final solution. This issue is consistently observed across instances from the Baltic
to Europe Asia, where paths with high transit time are included in the MIP model but remain
67
==PAGE:79==
unused. These paths unnecessarily grows the model size and increases the number of decision
variables the solver must evaluate. Figures 9 and 12 clearly illustrate this pattern. Filtering
out these paths before the final solve would reduce the number of continuous variables xk
j,
reduce the solution space, and improve computational time. Furthermore, Koza et al. (2020)
restrict the final MIP model to include only arcs that were identified as promising during their
iterative process. These arcs are fixed in their final MIP model, which reduces the number
of branches on the binary variables ye
a and significantly reduces the solution space that would
improve computational time.
For future research and further development of the matheuristic framework, several improve-
ments could be made to the MIP model. First, warm starting the solver using a feasible solution
from historical data could speed up convergence. This would reduce the time spent on early
search heuristics and allow the solver to prune weak branches more effectively. Second, a subset
of sailing arcs could be fixed before the final solve. Especially, arcs associated with designated
transshipment hubs could be fixed, thereby reducing the solution space and improving runtime.
Finally, it is important to note that all experiments in this thesis were conducted on a MacBook,
with specifications listed in Section 6.4. To further improve computational performance, future
experiments could be conducted on more powerful hardware.
In summary, the MIP model phase does not influence solution quality to the same extent as
graph construction or path generation. Its primary impact lies in computational time, which
affects general use of the matheuristic framework. To improve performance in this phase, future
research should focus on reducing redundant paths included in the master problem and fixing
promising arcs in the network to reduce the solution space and speed up convergence.
9.5
Real World Implications
The matheuristic framework developed in this thesis shows strong potential for real world ap-
plication in LSNDP. In regional single-hub networks, it consistently generates feasible, high
quality services and achieves performance similar or better than the state-of-the-art method
from Koza et al. (2020). However, its effectiveness diminishes in intercontinental and global net-
works, where consolidation must be structurally coordinated and computational performance
optimized. To make the framework useful for large scale applications, improvements are re-
quired across three core components: (1) graph construction must enhance routing flexibility
without inflating model size, (2) path generation must better support transshipment and con-
solidation of demand streams, and (3) the MIP model can be improved to lower runtime. With
these improvements, the matheuristic could be used as a robust framework to support con-
struction of real world liner shipping networks.

Conclusion
This thesis addressed the Liner Shipping Network Design and Scheduling Problem (LSNDSP)
by developing a matheuristic framework that at its core combines a tailored version of the A*
algorithm with exact optimization. A directed time-space graph was designed as the underlying
structure upon which cargo paths and vessel services were constructed.
The matheuristic framework was evaluated across six benchmark instances from the LINERLIB
dataset, ranging from single-hub regional networks to global multi-hub networks. Through these
computational experiments, the thesis investigated both the solution quality and computational
efficiency of the proposed matheuristic framework. Its performance was then compared against
the state-of-the-art method presented in Koza et al. (2020).
The matheuristic framework consistently produced feasible solutions, where vessel services
where designed and cargo were routed along the constructed services. In regional single-hub
networks as the Baltic and WAF, it achieved high levels of demand satisfaction and objective
values that were consistent with results from the state-of-art method from Koza et al. (2020).
However, as network size and complexity increased, the framework’s performance declined.
Intercontinental networks like Mediterranean and Pacific saw moderate demand satisfaction,
while global networks such as World Small and Europe Asia demonstrated low levels of de-
mand satisfaction. The decline in performance was primarily attributed to three factors: (1)
the inability to generate intersecting paths that enable transshipment, that led to (2) the lack of
consolidation of low volume demand streams, and (3) the computational bottlenecks in solving
the final MIP model.
A comparative analysis with the state-of-the-art method from Koza et al. (2020) highlighted
key structural differences. It was evident that the major limitation for the matheuristic frame-
work was the low transshipment usage, which resulted in reduced vessel capacity utilization,
insufficient cargo consolidation, and ultimately rejected demand streams and a lower demand
satisfaction in larger networks.
Despite its limitations, the framework offers a promising foundation for solving the LSNDP. It
consistently delivers high quality solutions on regional networks. On larger instances, it success-
fully produces feasible solutions, demonstrating that even global networks can be decomposed
and solved through a combination of heuristic path generation and exact optimization.
In conclusion, the matheuristic framework developed in this thesis performs well in regional
networks but lacks solution quality in global networks. The most promising direction for im-
provement is to integrate transshipment hubs into the path generation process, allowing for
more effective cargo consolidation, particularly in larger and more complex networks. Overall,
the matheuristic framework showed strong potential to solve real world LSNDP.
