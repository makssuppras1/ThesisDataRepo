TECHNICAL UNIVERSITY OF DENMARK
Abstract
DTU Mechanical Engineering
MSc in Civil Engineering
Numerical simulation of tsunami induced wave boundary layers
by Athanasios MAKRIS
This research utilizes a numerical model coupling the horizontal component
of the incompressible Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes equations (RANS)
with two-equation k-ω turbulence closure in order to investigate and simu-
late the wave boundary layer generated by the 2010 Chile tsunami, as it was
captured in Monterrey Bay, California, USA. There is undertaken a prelimi-
nary investigation of the tsunami current, as provided by Jessica Lacy, where
the bed roughness and the superimposition of a sinusoidal wave, with the
characteristics of the real surface waves of the day, are tested for their possi-
ble beneﬁts in providing a simpler model or an understanding of the bed con-
ditions. However, with the full analysis 1Hz data provided, more compelling
results emerged. To the extent of the author’s information, it is seemingly the
ﬁrst time, the current-wave interaction is demonstrated for an actual event,
and not only theoretically. Similarly, the ﬁrst results about the signiﬁcance
of the converging-diverging effects in a tsunami wave with a period in the
order of minutes are also demonstrated in this thesis.

Chapter 1
Introduction to the thesis topic
Tsunamis, being the aftermath of a seismic event, are themselves unpre-
dictable, but yet anticipated and of course worrisome after an earthquake.
For that reason capturing them though not hopeless is improbable. There-
fore they have been studied in experimental facilities, but real-life data from
the ﬁeld are rare. In fact, to the author’s knowledge, this was the ﬁrst time
velocities were measured inside the boundary layer from such an event and
are published.
The event in question is the 2010 Chile tsunami, that was caused by the
8.8 Mw (or MMS, Moment magnitude scale) 2010 Chile earthquake on the
27th of February off the coast of Maule. The earthquake had casualties, but
thankfully it seems the tsunami outside of Chile did not cause much damage.
Wave heights of about 3 meters were reported from Chilean Islands, and the
height of the tsunami in the deep were measured to 25 cm, which according
to experts 1 is large enough to issue a warning in the areas around the Paciﬁc
ocean.

WBL is indeed that part of the water column where the mechanisms of sed-
iment transport take place. And as such it would be intriguing to attempt
and model this part of the water column, as surveys of tsunamis are other-

1.1
Motivation
As already introduced, real-life velocity data from the boundary layer in a
tsunami current have not been modeled before, and therefore this in itself is
of great interest. The complexity of the events taking place in the Monterey
Bay on February 28, with the tsunami described above and the strong winds
resulting in waves of a magnitude similar to the tsunami (signiﬁcant wave
height was 1.3 m and the tsunami, upon arrival, from crest to trough had
a height of up to 1.1 m), are to be modelled with a 1D model, which is a
welcomed challenge and an instructive process. It was, also, fortunate for
the 1D modeling that the directions of the tsunami and the wind waves more
or less coincided and is treated like that in the following.
Furthermore, previous work (Williams and Fuhrman, 2016), modeling ex-
perimental data has neglected not only short waves, but also the higher order
convective terms, so in this investigation, the converging - diverging effects
are considered and studied.

2.1.1
Boundary conditions
The initial conditions in the model are motionless and it works in a single
vertical dimension, subject to the following boundary conditions. The bot-
tom wall is considered a friction wall, with a no-slip boundary condition
imposed, meaning that velocity variables are set to zero. Furthermore, at the
bottom boundary, a zero-gradient condition is imposed for k, the turbulent
kinetic energy (per unit mass), dk/dy= 0, which imposes a zero ﬂux of tur-
bulent kinetic energy through the sea bed. This condition justiﬁed based on
experimental measurements for steady ﬂows on rough beds by (Fuhrman,
Dixen, and Jacobsen, 2010; Sumer et al., 2003). In (Fuhrman, Dixen, and
Jacobsen, 2010) it is also demonstrated that the zero-gradient condition al-
lows for a natural development of the viscous sublayer near smooth walls
while avoiding the creation of a ﬁcticious one near rough walls, as it is even
generally consistent with near-wall physics, compared to the more standard-
ized use of k=0. This zero-gradient condition has also previously been suc-
cessfully employed in simulations of oscillatory wave boundary ﬂows by
(Fuhrman, Sumer, and Fredsøe, 2011), on both smooth and rough beds.

4.3.1
Tsunami alone

model is actually capable of capturing this effect.

and Sumer, 2009a; Fuhrman, Fredsøe, and Sumer, 2009b) those parameters
are neatly combined in the non-dimensional parameter on the RHS:

Chapter 5
Conclusion
5.1
Future Work
For future work I would suggest that there is value in investigating the other
second order terms, i.e. streaming in length. As mentioned it is not possi-
ble in the current form of the model to account for various separate celerities
with what that entails. However, the deviation from the ﬁeld data that be-
comes more and more prevalent after the peak event has passed, suggests
that the tsunami wave is quite skewed, which of course relates to the fact
that it approaches shallow water from deep water in the Paciﬁc. These ef-
fects may therefore advantageously be accounted for. At the same time, in
case more information can be gathered about the bed conditions, then that
investigation can be extended in the examination of the raw signal, as focus
in the present research quickly turned towards providing enough insight in
the wave-current interaction result and the converging-diverging effect.
Without a doubt a simulation of the induced sediment transport would
also be of interest.
5.2
Contributions
The research outlined in this thesis began as an effort to model, capture and
hopefully predict the evolution of the wave boundary layer emerging from a
real-life tsunami event. With a few modiﬁcations/generalizations in the pro-
cesses of the existing model, a faithful picture of the reality began to materi-
alize. As more and more elements were added, more and more compelling
results were produced. Finally with this 1D vertical model, it became possi-
ble to demonstrate for probably the ﬁrst time the current-wave interaction in
actual events, as well as the importance of the converging-diverging effects
in events of this scale.
