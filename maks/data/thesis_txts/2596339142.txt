Abstract
With increasing world population, the demand for agricultural goods is rapidly growing. Since
arable land is limited, other approaches have historically been used to increase yield. Pesticides
have previously been utilized to combat pathogens, but with the increasing focus on sustainabil-
ity modernized alternatives are needed. Biocontrol is the use of organisms to suppress pathogens
using a variety of functions. A noticeable one is secondary metabolites metabolites that can,
through a variety of functions, suppress the pathogens and preventing them from killing the
plants. The biocontrol agents can possess other functions which increase the yield. Using a con-
sortia of biocontrol agents could prove an even more effective method of supressing pathogens
than singular biocontrol agents. It would allow one to compile methods of suppression and
therefore achieve a wider effect. In this thesis, a genome analysis for plant growth promoting
traits was performed on four Pseudomonas strains that previously had been tested positive for
antagonistic capabilities. Several non-ribosomal peptide (NRPS) clusters were found where
many were possibly for siderophore. An NRPS for cyclic lipodepspeptides and fragin were also
discovered both hold antibiotic effects. Genes related to phosphate solubilization, hydrogen
cyanide biosynthesis and Indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) biosynthesis were also found. These dis-
coveries could possibly mean that the four strains could be used as biocontrol agents. Whether
they can be used in a consortia demands more experimentaiton.
There is still much research to be done before biocontrol consortia agents can be widely used.
Therefore, a proposal for how to conduct an investigation of biocontrol consortia is provided in
this thesis.

Background
1.1
Introduction
The global population is increasing with each passing year which leads to an increasing demand
for sustainable food[1]. The use of pesticides is a commonly used method to safeguard crops
against pathogens and other microbes that could damage it. However, with an increasing focus
on environmental safety and health, there is a need for an alternative. Biocontrol agents (BCA)
have therefore been of increasing interest for organizations and governments [2], as they are
considered an environmentally sustainable method [3]. They can produce secondary metabo-
lites that have beneﬁcial effects through either competing for nutrients in the soil, producing
growth promoting metabolites or suppressing possible pathogens by antibiotic means[4]. Iden-
tifying possible BCA’s are therefore important for future agricultural processes. This thesis is
divided into two parts as my previous experimental work was cancelled due to the corona pan-
demic. First, a proposed experimental procedure to identify a possible BCA will be explained
in addition to how a singular BCA can be compared to a BCA consortia. Second, a genome
analysis for plant growth promotion traits will be performed on four different strains of Pseu-
domonas which were whole-genome sequenced prior to this study.
The following sections will outline concepts and relevant deﬁnitions to give a background to
this topic. The generel structure of the rhizospere, and the concept of suppressive soil is ex-
plained. The use of pesticides for disease management and its consequences and the European
Unoins’ (EU) interest in new methods of disease management is clariﬁed. Finally, is the use of
BCA, Flourescent Pseudomonads potential as a BCA and the potential use of BCA consortia
described.

1.2
Rhizosphere
The soil that surrounds the plant roots is called the rhizosphere. It ranges from a few hundred
micrometers to more than 5 mm from the surface of the root [KennedyRhizosphere]. It is
a niche of high microbial activities which can either be considered beneﬁcial (mutualistic) or
harmful (pathogenic) between plant and the organisms residing in the rhizoshere. The activity
in the rhizosphere will reﬂect the diversity in the microbiome. The root, soil and microbes are
in a continous interaction and inﬂuence the performance and existance of each other [5].
The microbial diversity of the rhizosphere depends on plant species and lifecycle, as this will
change the exudate composition.
This was previously shown in studies by sampling over
time.[6].
Figure 1: Diagram depicting different groups of microbes, as well as how they interact and affect the

effect.
The rhizoshere microbiome can be affected by both abiotic and biotic factors. The abiotic
factors include the soil quality and if the plant was treated with pesticides or fungicides, while

the biotic factors involve the plant species, development stage and health[7].
As seen in Figure 1 the roots can secrete chemicals into the soil that are broadly reffered to as
root exudates. Through this exudation the soil microbial consortia can be regulated to encour-
age beneﬁcial symbiosis, change the chemical and physical properties of the soil and inhibit
the growth of competing plant species and microbes[8][9]. Through these various approaches,
the plant creates a rhizosphere microbiome which provides it with both beneﬁcial effects and
pathogenic effects. It can stimulate the function of beneﬁcial microbes such as saprophytes
which have shown stimulating effects for plant growth and health. Other such beneﬁcial mi-
crobes include decomposers of organic detritus, plant growth promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR)
as well as fungal and bacterial antagonists[10].
In Figure 1, pathogenic microbes are also seen. They can have antagonistic effects on other mi-
crobes in the rhizosphere or damage the plant through the production of phytotoxic compunds.
In addition to damaging the plant, they will have a suppressive affect on other microbes through
the uptake of nutrients and interactions with other microbes. Beneﬁcial microbes can effect the
plant by promoting growth, inhibiting harmful microbes or by inducing the systemic resistance
(ISR). ISR is a state where the plant’s immune system is primed for an accelerated defense
response[9].
Pseudomonads have in particular been of interest for researchers as they have shown antagonis-
tic effects, speciﬁcally against pathogenic fungi. Several fungi exist which can infect the plant
roots and kill it. Antagonistic microbes are therefore of interest as they have shown themselves
capable of suppressing pathogenic fungi.
Two well-known pathogenic fungi are Fusarium graminearum which cause Fusarium wilt and
Gaeumannomyces graminis var. tritici which causes Take-all disease. Both are severe infections
which can cause massive losses in yield when harvesting. However, there are cases where the
soil has developed a resistance, a condition which is called ”suppressive soil”.

1.3
Suppressive soils
Suppressive soil comes in three levels. A pathogen does not establish nor can it persist in the
soil. A pathogen does establish itself but causes little to no damage. A pathogen establish itself
and cause disease for a period of time but the disease is thereafter not a dangerous factor even-
though the disease may still persist in the soil[11].
Suppressive soil is an example of a natural microbiome-based defense which is nourished and
supported by the plant roots and then used as the ﬁrst line of defense against plant pathogens. In
this case, the microbiome will be measured by the microbe’s gene expression, their transcripts,
metabolites and proteins[12]. The suppression can either be a product of starvation, parasitic
exploitation, production of pathogen inhibiting antibiotics or an induced resistance in the host
plant against the pathogen[13]. The opposite of suppressive soil would be conducive soil which
is susceptible to plant pathogens.
Suppressive soils are further divided into two different forms: “General” and “Speciﬁc” sup-
pression. General suppression refers to the ability of soil to inhibit or suppress the growth
and activity of certain soilborne pathogens. This is achieved through the competitiveness and
antagonistic effect of the existing total microbiome in the soil[11]. General suppression is a
natural characteristic for soil and is a preexisting condition. In its essence, general suppression
need-not be more than the outcome at which nutrients are consumed. Nutrients are exuded by
plants and can be found in the rhizosphere where they are consumed by microorganisms. The
general suppression will increase with greater uptake of nutrients, as the pathogens compete
for nutrients with the plants and environment[13]. Soil which is rich in organic matter due to
input of decomposable biomass are in general more suppressive in comparison to soil which ia
low in organic matter. This can come from crop residue after harvesting, barnyard manure and
compost[13].
Speciﬁc suppression is the suppression of a speciﬁc pathogen. In the case of fungi, a prevalent
pathogenic fungus is Fusarium oxysporum which causes Fusarium wilt. Fusarium wilt has been
reported from all over the world and several cases of soils which are suppressive to Fusarium
wilt have been found [14]. When the soils were analyzed, it was found that antibiotic producing

1.4
Disease management
Both biological control and pesticides have pros and cons that makes the usage of them a point
of discussion regarding which to use and what the consequences of these could be. Pesticides is
a term which covers a wide range of products that includes insecticides, fungicides, herbicides,
rodenticides, plant growth regulators etc. These products are widely used on a global scale
and have had a great effect on the agricultural consortia. As can be seen in Figure 2, the sale
of pesticides in the EU has been fairly stable even in the last decade [17], with fungicide,
bacteriocides, herbicides, haulm destructors and moss killers being the majorities sold.

1.5
European Union and Biocontrol
European Union has shown great interest in alternatives methods of disease management. The
EU helped to stimulate the development by, among other things, donating to the BIOCOMES
project. BIOCOMES was a project that started in 2013 and ended in 2017, which focused
on developing 11 new biological control products to control a number of pests[21]. Of the
11 biological products, 2 were ready for market by 2017, and several of the others showed
promise[22]. Currently, there are several biocontrol products on the market which uses bac-
teria, fungi or vira[22]. Cerall is a biocontrol product, and is composed of only one strain of
Pseudomonas, speciﬁcally Pseudomonas chlororaphis, which is used against fungal diseases
such as Fusarium head blight (FHB)[23].
The use of biocontrol agents (BCA) is deﬁned as a “environmentally sound” strategy when

it comes to reducing pathogens. In addition to suppressing pathogens, these treatments also
increase yield, growth and tolerance against diseases.[3]. Biocontrol’s advantages in diseases
management is that there is little to no side-effect of its usage, it is usable as a form of long-term
control of pests and it is non-toxic to plants and beneﬁcial organisms which may already exist in
the rhizosphere[3]. As BCAs are narrow in their spectrum there is little risk of it affecting non-
targeted organisms, and their efﬁciancy against those within the spectrum has proven high[24].
BCAs are non-pathogenic to the plant and prevent the progression of microbes through different
modes of action which are either direct or indirect[4]. Direct modes of actions used by the BCA
are antibiosis and hyperparasitism. Antibiosis involves the production of secondary metabolites
like antibiotics. These metabolites can be cell-wall degrading enzymes, suppress growth or kill
the pathogens[4][25]. Parasitism is when the BCA is on the pathogen as a way to degrade or
consume the pathogen[4].
Indirect modes of actions are colonization or competition. By colonizing the plant with ben-
eﬁcial microorganisms, they can trigger a local or a systemic defense response which would
enhance the plant’s natural resistance. Competition occurs when two or more organisms are
present. They will compete for the available nutrients in the soil or occupational space [25]
[4][26].
As previously stated, in section 1.5, Flourescent Pseudomonads are of great interest in regards
to biocontrol. Products which use Pseudomonas strain arlready exist, such as Cerall[23].
1.6
Flourescent Pseudomonads
The Flourescent Pseudomonads species are among interest for use as a BCA. They are cabal-
ble of producing different metabolites which have either growth promoting effects for plants,
antibiotic abilities or growth inhibiting effects for pathogens. For instance, one of the character-
istics of ﬂourescent Pseudomonads is their capability to producing iron carriers (siderophores)
like pseudobactin (Pvd). They are produced under low levels of iron and transport Fe3+ to the
cell by chelating. It interacts with a speciﬁc outer-membrane receptor on either its producer or
non-producer cells and when bound it is transported into the cell and the iron is reduced [27].
As it takes the nutrients from the soil inhibiting other microbes growth and starving them for
iron siderophore would be a competitive mechanism. Unless other microbes possess a stronger

Other important metabolic functions for obtaining nutrients would be phosphate solubilization
and nitrogen ﬁxation, which are both vital nutrients for plant life. The majority of phosphate
is bound in insoluble forms. Absolving these bonds is mostly done by way of organic acids
such as gluconic acid (GA). The biosynthesis for GA is carried out by glucose dehydrogenase
(GCD) and pyrroloquinoline (PQQ) [30]. Hydrogen cyanide has also been connected to the
liberation of phosphate from insoluble forms[31]. Nitrogen is considered the most important
nutrient for plant growth. BCA with nitrogen-ﬁxation capabilities are of great interest as they
could increase plant growth[32]. Plant hormones such as auxin and ACC deaminase is impor-
tant for plant growth as they both serve functions which allow for plant roots to spread[33][34].
Antibiosis metabolites such as DAPG and Phenazines are both secondary metabolites that are
of interest for their suppressive activity against pathogens in the soil environment[35][36].
Well-known ﬂourescent Pseudomonads strains are Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Pseudomonas
putida, Pseudomonas ﬂourescens and Pseudomonas syringae. Many of these strains has been
connected to a plant protection and anatoginistic effect on fungi and bacteria [37][38][29].
Due to the promise of BCA, and their beneﬁcial effects in agriculture, the idea of using a con-
sortia of BCA in a single treatment has been gaining interest.
1.7
Consortia of BCA
Inconsistent ﬁeld performances often constrict the development of biocontrol agents. The in-
consistencies which could affect the efﬁcacy of the biocontrol agents can be caused by a variety
of abiotic and biotic factors. Abiotic factors, as mentioned before in section 1.4.1, could be
climate and the physical and chemical composition of the rhizosphere. Biotic factors include
interactions with other nontarget organisms, damage caused by nontarget pathogens and pests,
degree of rhizosphere and soil colonization by BCA, initial population level of target pathogen
or pest, host plant species and host plant cultivar [39]. An approach to overcome this perfor-
mance inconsistency, is the use of multiple BCAs. Using a combination of different BCAs in
treatment could provide multiple advantages including: Multiple modes of action against target
pathogen, increased consistency in performance over a wider range of soil conditions and po-

inﬂuence the usefulness of the BCA. As previously mentioned, plant species exude carbohy-

may not work or only a part of it may.

2
Testing communal Biocontrol agents
As there have been focus on the use of BCA, and the formation of consortias have shown
promise on controlling pathogenic fungi, the proposed project concerns the construction of
three different consortias of BCA. The three consortias will consist of Pseudomonas bacteria
picked from predetermined sample spots. The study would investigate the individual strains’
secondary metabolite production and suppressive abilities against pathogenic fungi. The same
would be investigated but as in a consortia. This would provide a better understanding of how
a consortia of BCA would work as a whole and whether each individual isolated Pseudomonas
strain is more effective in a consortia.
2.1
Picking Pseudomonas from three sites and constructing consortias
At ﬁrst the consortia must be constructed. The sites from which the Pseudomonas have been
isolated have had their rhizosphere consortia analyzed for more than a year. They had a stable
and diverse rhizopheric microbiota and showed little change during the year. The stability made
it an intriguing subject as the combination of certain Pseudomonads strains may contribute to
this condition. Therefore, were ﬂourescent Pseudomonads isolated and a phylogenetic tree was
constructed to identify the closest Pseudomonas sp. relative, though this tree is not added to
this thesis.
2.2
Using assays to characterize and analyze the Pseudomonas strains for
biocontrol use
The capabilities of a consortia will depend on the metabolic proﬁle of the BCA which it consist
of. Uncovering which secondary metabolites they produce and their growth rate is therefore
imperative for constructing a useful BCA consortia.
As they all belong to the Pseudomonas genus, there will be similarities between them. How-
ever, some will possess different phenotypes, which will affect the consortia and their ability
to exist within it. If one possesses a higher growth rate than the others, they have the potential
of overwhelming them and consequently killing them. If one has a higher production of a sec-
ondary metabolite such as a siderophore, then it could possibly outcompete the other making it

A growth assay will showcase the isolates individual growth rate. If one strain shows a higher
growth rate it can potentially outcompete and suppress the other strains in a consortia. This
could be accomplished with a 96 microtiter assay where the Pseudomonas OD have been nor-
malized and are deposited into their individual well and grown over a set period of time.
2.2.2
Siderophore assay
BCAs use siderophore to compete with other microbes in the rhizosphere. The assay will make
it possible to identify them by making them compete for iron in the medium. Using a growth
medium with low iron, it can be measured in clearing zones if they produce siderophore and if
they do, how well by measureing clearing zones.
2.2.3
Antagonism assay
Antangonism between the isolates can be discovered by performing a antagonism assay. After
growing the cultures overnight and normalizing their OD measurements, a strain can be plated
with a glass spatula, and the others can be deposited in marked positions. After letting them
grow, antagonism can be seen whether there is a clearing zone around the marked positions.
This will indicate that the positioned strains produce a metabolite which suppress the growth of
the plated strain.
2.2.4
Speciﬁc metabolite: DAPG
DAPG is a well-known antifungal metabolite and is known to be produced by Flourescent
Pseudomonads. This assay will give a better understanding of the strains individual antibiosis
capabilities. Producers of DAPG could be identiﬁed with a biosensor and measure their output
in diameter on plates.

Testing individual strain’s interaction with barley seeds
Barley has been knwon to be attacked by pathogenic fungus. Testing the Pseudomonas isolates

them measurable. This will be done by coating the seeds in the Pseudomonas isolates, growing

2.3.1
Colonization of barley seed assay
Pseudomonads colonize the seeds in the rhizosphere as they live in a symbiotic relationship
with the plant. Colonization is an important attribute, however, if one strain in a consortia has
a much higher rate of colonization, this could lead to them outcompeting the others. Using a
colonization assay to deduce their rate of colonization will give an idea of the possibility of a
strain taking over one of the biocontrol consortias.
2.3.2
Pathogenic fungi suppressing assay
The purpose of a BCA is to suppress pathogenic fungi. An assay which measures the suppres-
sive abilities of each individual strain is therefore necessary. The result can be compared to the
suppressive ability of the communal BCA.

The purpose of this test is to investigate colonization of barley seeds and its suppressive effect on
pathogenic fungi. By performing the same assays but with the consortias, the metabolic produc-

them provides a better understanding of whether the individual or the consortia of BCA are the
most useful as a biocontrol.

Strains

multiple pathways of IAA biosynthesis, they also have the potential to produce large amounts
of auxin. This could potentially provide a great growth enhancement for plant which have been
treated with a possible product made from these strains.

DISCUSSION
5
Discussion
BCAs have shown an increase in yield and suppression of pathogens which would otherwise
harm the crops. The promise BCAs have shown in plant growth promotion have spurred an
interest in investigating consortias which could possibly prove more effective in promoting
plant growth and suppression of pathogens. Researching consortias however will take time as
there are many factors to consider. Root exudate, soil quality and composition and the existing
diversity in the microbiome are important factors that needs to be considered. Identifying what
each strain in your experimental consortia produces or has the potential of producing is also of
importance as it could affect the performance of the consortia.
A genome analysis was performed on strain 6.1, 6.3, 6.7 and 6.10 using antiSMASH. Clusters
for secondary metabolites were found in the strains depicted in Figure 1. Siderophores were
found in all fours strains. Pyoverdine was found in all and achromabactin was found in 6.10.
The most reliable siderophore that was found was achromabactin by way of using BLAST.
Pyoverdine is a fairly common NRPS[56], but because none of the hits had a high identity,
unless multiple clusters were considered as one, the antiSMASH result cannot be considered
reliable. However, as all the strains were ﬂourescent under UV light when Nina Feldthaus
isolated them[45], it must be assumed that they possess pyoverdine. Metabolites related to
antibiosis were found such as cyclic lipodepsipeptide. In addition, two non-identiﬁed NRPS
and a T1PKS were found. For the BLAST results only three PGP metabolites were found where
only 6.3 had all three as seen in Figure 3. Both phosphate solubilization and hydrogen cyanide
are associated with freeing phosphate from insoluble forms, and IAA is a plant hormone which
has been shown to increase plant growth.
When considering the four strains as BCA, they possess a few secondary metabolites that could
possibly perform. At this point in the analysis, the antibiosis metabolites that were found are
cyclic lipodepsipeptide. Cyclic lipodepsipeptides have been connected to antibiotic activity.
Their presence make it possible for them to perform suppressive effects on pathogenic mi-
crobes. At this point it is, however, still unknown what molecules the cyclic lipodepsipeptides
are and how well they would work as a suppressive agent.
As for competition capabilities, they possibly possess the siderophore achromabactin and most
likely pyoverdine. They could potentially inhibit the growth of pathogens by lowering the iron

FUTURE PROSPECTS
6
Conclusion
There is a need for new methods to increase the yield of agricultural crops, arising from the
increase in population and the environmental impact of current methods. Looking at biocontrol
as an alternative method is promising. The genome analysis of the four Pseudomonas strains
showed that they have potential as BCAs. The strains possibly contained secondary metabolite
clusters for siderophore and cyclic lipodepsipeptides which could suppress pathogens either
through competition or antibiosis, respectively. Though the four Pseudomonas have proven as
a possible BCAs, this thesis was performed in silico based on genome analysis. Experimental
conﬁrmations are needed before further process can be done.
7
Future prospects
To further this research, a larger and more thorough genome analysis could be performed on
these particular strains. The PGP genes which they were BLAST against could be increased as
they could potentially possess more. To test whether they express the genes which have been
found, more in vitro and in vivo studies are needed. Investigating the potential of a BCA consor-
tia could also be attempted using the four Pseudomonas. As their individual metabolic proﬁle is
being uncovered, investigating whether they change in a consortia could prove interesting and
illuminating.
