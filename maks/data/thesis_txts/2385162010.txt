1 Preface 
This thesis is developed as a product of a master thesis project from the Technical Univer-
sity of Denmark’s Industrial and Engineering Management education. The thesis consist 
of a research into how social network analysis can be used to map IT software in the con-
struction industry. The thesis was conducted from the 27rd of January 2017 to the 4th of 
august 2017, and accounts for 30 ECTS points. 
The project was conducted through the Technical University of Denmark’s department of 
Engineering Management. 
The objective of the thesis is to give an insight into using social network analysis as a tool 
for mapping the IT software used in the construction industry, with a focus on IT software

6 Abstract 
The Danish construction industry has been stagnated in performance over the last dec-
ades compared to other industries. The IT software in the construction industry has there-
fore been seen as a solution to this stagnation. The IT software in the construction indus-
try has been under a rapid development making it more complex to get an overview of 
which IT software there is being used and for what. 
The objective of this thesis has been to see if it is possible to map the IT software’ used 
through a construction project via social network analysis (SNA). The thesis gives a brief 
insight into which parts that are essential for a construction project and a literature review 
of previous used SNA’s in the construction industry. The foundation of the network anal-
ysis is made through six semi-structured qualitative interviews with different stakeholders 
in the construction industry. 
From the data collected through the interviews, a network has been visualised that rep-
resents the different interviews knowledge on how the IT software interacts with the 
functions in a construction project and the project actors. The results show that it is pos-
sible to make a more transparent overview of the different IT software’s used in a con-
struction project via SNA, there is however some obstacles in regards to using the infor-
mation from the network since the project actors and IT software’s all have different at-
tributes, which makes the relation between most of them different from each other. The 
complexity of doing a broader SNA with more data can therefore become very difficult to 
analyse, since it is necessary to understand the relation between the project actors, IT-
software and functions before it can be analysed. 
The thesis furthermore contains a framework created for a network analysis of IT software 
in a specific project coalition. This framework has been developed on the background of 
the research and learnings from this thesis. The framework aims towards mapping the IT 
usages in a project coalition through a quantitative measurement on interactions be-
tween project actors and the IT software’s.  
Keywords: Construction Projects, Project coalitions, IT software, project actors, functions, 
Social Network Analysis, Project Management

7 Resume 
Den danske byggebranche har været stagneret i effektivitet over de sidste årtier i forhold 
til andre industrier. IT-programmer i byggebranchen er derfor blevet betragtet som løs-
ningen til dette problem. IT-programmerne i byggebranchen har været i en signifikant ud-
vikling, hvilket har gjort det mere komplekst at få et overblik over, hvilke IT-software pro-
grammer der benyttes og til hvad disse benyttes. 
Formålet med denne afhandling har været at kortlægge IT-programmerne, der anvendes 
gennem et byggeprojekt via social netværksanalyse. Afhandlingen giver et kort indblik i, 
hvilke væsentlige dele et byggeriprojekt indeholder for at kunne udføres. Ydermere gives 
der en litteraturgennemgang af tidligere benyttet social netværks analyser i byggeribran-
chen. Grundlaget for netværksanalysen er lavet gennem seks semistrukturerede kvalita-
tive interviews med forskellige interessenter i byggebranchen.  
Fra data indsamlet fra interviews er et netværk blevet visualiseret, som repræsenterer de 
forskellige interessenters indblik i, hvordan IT-programmerne interagerer med funktio-
nerne og projektaktørerne i et byggeriprojekt. Resultaterne viser, at det gennem net-
værksanalyse er muligt at skabe et mere overskueligt overblik over den forskellige it-soft-
ware (programmer), der anvendes i et byggeprojekt. Der er dog nogle hindringer i forbin-
delse med brugen af informationen fra netværket. De fleste projektaktører og IT-pro-
grammer har forskellige egenskaber, hvilket betyder, at relationerne mellem de fleste 
programmer vil være forskellige. Kompleksiteten ved at lave en bredere netværksanalyse 
af byggeriindustrien med mere data gør, at det kan blive vanskeligt efterfølgende at ana-
lysere data, da der skal bruges en forståelse af relationerne mellem IT-programmerne, 
funktionerne og projektaktørerne før at man kan analysere netværket. 
Afhandlingen indeholder desuden fremgangsmetode for, hvordan en netværksanalyse 
kan skabes af en specifik projektkoalition. Denne fremgangsmetode er udviklet på bag-
rund af undersøgelsen og læringen fra denne afhandling. Fremgangsmetoden sigter mod 
at kortlægge IT-anvendelsen i en projektkoalition gennem kvantitative målinger af inter-
aktioner mellem projektaktører og IT-programmerne. 
 
Nøgleord: Byggeprojekter, projektkoalitioner, IT software, projektaktører, social net-
værksanalyse, projektledelse.

8  Introduction 
The construction industry has been in a rapidly changing environment over the last dec-
ades in regards to IT-usages. With even more emerging technologies an overview of how 
the construction industry is operating in its current state is trying to be mapped, and 
thereby showing where we are now, for comparison in the future.  
The implementation of new IT-software’s has kept a progress going which keep changing 
how we work in a project organisation, where the IT tools have given new possibilities of 
cooperation and ensuring better quality in the project. It is however also creating a more 
complex project organisation where new capabilities in IT usages are needed. 
This thesis will examine which role the IT tools are fulfilling in a project, and visualise its 
connection with the project organisation through SNA. The project is based on research 
from earlier SNA in the construction industry and the general concept of the IT system in 
construction found through literature. Based on the foundation of the literature, qualita-
tive interviews in the Danish construction industry has been conducted for gaining further 
insight in how they are handling the construction projects, and which IT tools they are 
using for this. 
The literature in SNA in the construction industry does not involve the IT usages and func-
tion perspective. This thesis therefore tries to give an updated view with extra dimension 
to the SNA, by creating a framework, which can be used for further research into the field. 
8.1 Initial problem statement 
The Danish Construction industry has rapidly changed over the last few decades with mul-
tiple new technologies emerging, the basis of this thesis is to conduct a survey of which IT 
software’s are being used in the industry for conducting construction projects. Hopefully 
the insight can give some directions of major IT trends and give a better view of the in-
dustry to further help business in the construction industry with making feasible strategic 
decisions regard IT software’s. 
The initial basis for this problem statement has been altered throughout the project, 
mainly due to limited research done before. ‘Byggeri – informationsteknologi – produk-
tivitet – samarbejde – foreningen’ (Bips) has conducted one survey with focus on Building 
information modelling (BIM), but nothing more could be found. There were therefore set 
up interviews for analysing the construction industry and understand which IT software 
that is being used. The complexity of creating this survey raised with the new insight. Fur-
thermore, the lack of alliances with powerful stakeholders in the industry would make it 
very difficult to push the survey through. This therefore created the final problem state-
ment and research question as mentioned in chapter 8.2

8.2 Problem statement and research question 
The IT usage in the construction industry has been an ever-evolving mechanism over the 
last few decades and continues to evolve. The perspective of what the IT software is used 
for in a project may vary from company to company and from project to project. An over-
view of how the IT software is used in the construction industry will thereby give a better 
insight in the IT software’s connection to the projects. This thesis will focus on exploring 
how the IT software interacts in a construction project organisation through qualitative 
interviews with different stakeholders in the industry and map it into a network analysis 
Research question:  Can network analysis create more transparency in understanding how 
a project organisation uses IT software’s? 
Sub questions: 
o 
How do the project actors, processes and IT tools interact with each other through 
construction project organisations? 
o How can a company utilise network analysis to study the IT usages in project or-
ganisation? 
o Is it possible to create a framework for analysing familiarity between project ac-
tors, IT software and processes in a construction project?

9 History of digitalisation in the Danish construction 
industry 
This chapter gives a brief walkthrough of how the digitalisation in the Danish construction 
industry has evolved, and gives an insight into why it can be difficult to assess the IT usages 
in the construction industry. 
The digitalisation of the Danish construction industry in a broad perspective was initially 
started 1987 when different organisations in the construction industry took initiative to a 
development programme called Teknologirådets(TR)-initiativområde (Technology coun-
cils - initiative area) (Erhervsstyrelsen,2000). The TR-initiative was the first to collectively 
combine and collect the different development projects and programs, with their focus 
on: 
• 
CAD-Application 
• 
EDI- Application 
• 
Data 
• 
IT-practice in the construction industry 
• 
Reference architecture 
• 
International relations. 
The PPU-consortium, which was a coalition of the organisations (Rambøll, Højgaard & 
Schultz and Arktitektgruppen Aarhus), worked on with the knowledge from the TR-Initia-
tive and ended up in 2000 with a conclusion that the IT-readiness of the industry is in 
general high, but the coordination between the stakeholders demands a massive coordi-
nated effort in the area of IT-infrastructure to succeed. Mainly summarising the issue of 
compatibility and interoperability in the IT-infrastructure was lacking, and therefore 
heighten the cost and lowered the effectiveness. (Erhervsstyrelsen,2000) 
The Danish Building and Property Agency in 2002 launched a workgroup called “Det Digi-
tale Byggeri”.  The workgroups main focus was, if the public sector had a responsibility in 
regards to overcome the stagnated productivity in the sector. They conclude that the pub-
lic sector had a large part in this, since digitalisation across the different stakeholders will 
develop very slowly, and it will therefore take long time to handle the stagnated produc-
tivity. 
Bips developed together with five consortiums a program for how the industry’s stake-
holders can implement the infrastructure needed to address the new requirements from 
the public sector. This is seen as the first major try of trying to standardise the digitalisa-
tion of the construction industry in Denmark. There have been a lot of split opinions in 
regards to how Bips (interviews with Peter Sørensen and Peter Hauch,2017) have tried to 
dictate the direction, where some stakeholders in the construction industry has devel-
oped its own strategy for ICT-management. On a European perspective many similar ini-
tiatives have been made, but the foundation of how they work are different. The industry 
has therefore also had tendencies to look outside of Denmark on how implement the IT-
infrastructure. 
Since there has not been any clear standardisation on the IT subject, nearly every com-
pany has its own strategy for IT-software’s, and uses different IT-software’s for basically 
the same functions.

10 Literature Review 
The purpose of following review is to clarify what have been researched within Social Net-
work Analysis (SNA) and construction projects, and investigate where this thesis can give 
new insight in the use of the method in regards to construction projects. 
Literature for this thesis have been found through Google Scholar and DTU Findit. 
 ‘The construction industry is currently in transition as a result of innovations in pro-
curement and project management approaches, in particular the utilisation of supply 
chain management, and technology or work clusters in the context of partnering rela-
tionships between project coalition actors and the client organization’ (Pryke, 2004) 
Stephen Pryke states that the industry has made a shift in procurement and project man-
agement approaches, this has been drivers for how the project organisations changes 
with new roles emerging such as cluster leaders. These cross-disciplinary roles create al-
tered relationships in the project coalitions, and the effect of these cannot be analysed 
with existing analytical tools (Pryke, 2004). These new relationships have also created a 
need in new IT software, and vice versa. It is important to understand that Pryke’s work 
is both different in the input and output of his work compared to the focal point of this 
thesis. Pryke looks at how project coalitions changes in roles and interactions through 
changing types of procurement. Where this thesis focus on understanding the roles and 
interactions in the project coalitions in regards to IT software. 
Pryke mentions a few existing forms of analysis and which problems they have with ana-
lysing project coalitions. Task dependency analysis which focuses upon the relationship 
between the tasks needed to be performed, and in which order, such as critical path 
method. Structural analysis, which for instance can be the organisation diagram, which is 
often seen as the line management authority relationships. Process mapping is more of 
an eclectic and conceptual approach with use of flow charts and cognitive mapping (Pryke, 
2004) The process mapping also work is with linear responsibility analysis which comes 
closer to the SNA since it can add some indications of roles and relationships. 
 Pryke summarise some of the problems with the existing analysis as: 
• 
Flow charts and critical path analysis have limitations in dealing with independ-
ence as a concept. These methods do not provide information in regards to the 
interdependence between concurrent and sequential operations. 
• 
The differences in the organisational characteristics cannot be quantified. There 
is no opportunity of quantifying the different strategies and approaches via tradi-
tional analytical methods. 
• 
The non-linear processes in the projects are not well represented by flow charts 
or structural analysis. 
• 
Structural analysis represents organisations via a hierarchal approach and are not 
well suited for representing new cross functional roles. 
• 
Earlier SNA’s have focus on interpersonal relationship where for instance 
Loosemore has worked with interpersonal relationship in crisis management 
(Loosemore,1996), and did not look into the contractual relationships, or the IT 
software relationship.

Based on the research into other analytic methods, Pryke went on to developing an alter-
native analytic model, based on intra coalition relationships, and SNA as the method. 
Pryke proposes data is collected in regards to following set of relationships (Pryke,2004): 
• 
Contractual: A Map of the intra-coalition relationship can be created on the data 
from a representation of each company’s dyadic contractual relationship 
• 
Performance incentive: Is a form of financial relationship that are important for 
explaining the changes in activity of the project actors. 
• 
Information exchange network:  is the collection of data in relation for the infor-
mation exchanges within the project team 
In regards to understanding the IT usages and interaction with organisation the most rel-
evant of these are the information exchange network, but it is important to understand 
that the contractual part of a project coalition is not the same for all the project coalitions 
so understanding the difference when mapping a project coalition is relevant, and it will 
also show different outcomes in the SNA compared to the different contractual condi-
tions, which directly influence the information exchange network. 
Pryke published a case study of four different project coalitions in 2012, which was con-
ducted on the base of his framework from 2004. His generic model of what he is measur-
ing can be seen in Figure 1. The interesting part is that this framework is based on the 
supply chain and does not look at the time frame/phases of a project coalition, and there-
fore solely investigates the interactions between the project actors.

As earlier stated the focal connections in the network are the Contractual/financial rela-
tionship, and the information exchange network. Concept can be used to develop a similar 
concept for the IT usages in the project coalitions. The focal points to investigate would 
then be: 
1. The process connection to IT software 
2. The project actor’s connection to the IT software 
3. The project actor’s connection to the processes 
The IT software sometimes is embodied as a media for Information exchange network. 
With that in mind, there is several degrees of how detailed the SNA of the project coalition 
should be. Dependable the scope other data can be relevant for collection: 
1. The contractual relationship embodied in responsibilities of the IT software. 
2. The Information exchange network between the project actors where the IT soft-
ware further can be linked as node gateways. 
’Any structural analysis is limited in its value unless it is accompanied by a description 
of the characteristics of the organisation and its functions. Social network analysis (SNA) 
is no different’ (Pryke,2012) 
As Pryke states a SNA needs to have a clear description of the organisation or project 
coalitions characteristics. For the case of IT software in the project coalition understand-
ing the characteristics of the functions, IT software and project actors is essential for un-
derstanding their interactions with each other. 
’Achieving true comparability when studying construction projects is a challenge. Some 
might argue that the construction project is a social construct that is not a ‘repeatable 
event’’(Pryke,2012) 
Pryke further states that the comparability in construction projects are a challenge since 
nearly non of the projects are identical. This also applies to analysis of the project coali-
tion. It can therefore be very difficult to assess the difference between two SNA, since one 
coalition can be more beneficial in a given situation than another. 
It is necessary to control or limit the influence of variables in the analysis, such as design 
complexity, function of the construction, geographical location and many other variables 
that can influence the output of the analysis. (Pryke,2012) 
’The value of examining networks of relationships in construction (or any other) projects 
lies in the precise classification of a relationship type at both data gathering and analysis 
stages. Many networks are evolving over time.’ (Pryke,2012) 
As Pryke states above and earlier stated it is essential to precise classify what relationship 
that is being investigated, both in the data gathering stage and analysis stage. As further 
stated the networks often evolve over time. It is important to note here that project coa-
lition often seize to exist after the project is finished, and furthermore hard to transfer 
the data due to the low repeatability in the construction sector. For the case of this thesis 
the foundation of the SNA should be focused on generic data gathering for understanding 
the sector, and thereby not analysis specific project coalition. This should also give the 
possibility of making the model evolve over time with more data collected. 
Pryke did four case studies from which he analysis the network of different project coali-
tions. Two traditional procurement projects, one from public sector and one from private

10.1 Discussion 
Prykes’ framework can give a foundation for creating a further developed framework that 
undertake the aspect of IT software into the analysis.  The scope however will become 
larger and more data would need to be acquired. This would mean a larger portion of time 
is needed from the project actors to fulfil the questionnaires for the empirical data. What 
is worth taking from the literature review is: 
• 
No SNA of the general IT software used in construction has been made in Den-
mark. The closes are the SNA thesis on ‘projektweb’ in a project coalition. 
• 
Existing analysis tools can be insufficient in mapping the new procurement condi-
tions, which has some of the same complexities as the IT software usages. 
• 
Setting up the scope of which exact relationships that needs to be analysed, both 
in regards to the data collected and when analysed. 
• 
That the SNA is limited in its value if the characteristic of the organisation and 
functions are not well described 
• 
The IT usages in a project coalition can be very detailed and complex. Simplifica-
tion can therefore be necessary for completing an analysis which can have an ef-
fect on the results. 
It furthermore gives an insight into what needs to be carried out for conducting the anal-
ysis. 
• 
Scoping the network 
• 
Collect data for understanding the functions, IT software and project actors of a 
project coalition (Qualitative data) 
• 
Collecting data based on the understand qualitative data.

11 Project Management in the construction industry 
This chapter seeks to formulate the phases and functions that a project coalition is going 
through, these phases and functions has been the foundation for the interview process 
when discussion the interviewees roles and understanding of how the project coalition 
operates. 
The construction industry is a differentiated project based industry compared to other 
production industries. Most of the projects differentiate from each other, and a lot of tacit 
knowledge is bound to the individuals involved in a specific project. (Haigh,2008) It is 
therefore harder for the organisations to learn from the projects compared to other in-
dustries with more repetitive processes.  
Each project can therefore differentiate in which processes the project goes through. Un-
derstanding the general concept of a construction project is therefore essential for mak-
ing a framework for interviewing stakeholders in the industry. 
The Technical University of Denmark’s (DTU) Campus Service has developed a booklet 
with the description of the processes through a construction project on DTU. This booklet 
is used at the foundation for the interview framework. They have in the booklet devel-
oped 4 stages through a construction project, as seen in Figure 2(DTU Campus Ser-
vice,2014), 
 
 
Figure 2: The four different phases through a construction project 
 
11.1.1 Conceive 
The conceive phase also known as the programming phase, consist of two main tasks, to 
do the predesign/presentation of ideas and the construction programme. 
The predesign part of the conceive phase is the first initial clarifications of the project, 
which can involve a formulation of needs, the vision and the strategy of the project.

13.1 Archetypes of Stakeholders 
Each construction project will consist of a large variety of stakeholders from the internal 
stakeholders such as the Client, Client advisor, Engineer, Architect, Contractor and the 
Suppliers, and many external stakeholders such as the local community, NGO’s and gov-

the IT usages in the project organisation is what is being researched. There could however

13.1.1 Client 
The Client is the initiator of a construction project, and normally also the one financing 
the project. The client can normally split into two categories, a professional client, and a 
normal client. The difference is that a professional client has background knowledge and 
capabilities in the construction industry, and therefore can take part, or manage some 
part of the project internally. Where a normal client, will hire a client advisor for acquiring 
the capabilities for initiate the project. The clients’ main role is in the early phases of the 
project where the pre-design and criteria’s to the project is developed.

13.1.2 Client advisor 
The client advisor is the hired impartial partner for the client, he consults on how the 
project should be developed, helps with knowledge of needs and requirements for the 
project. The client advisor contributes with experience and capabilities for insuring that 
the client makes the right or best decisions in regard to the project. 
The client advisor normally provides services in areas of tenders, contracting, cost plan-
ning and time scheduling. 
13.1.3 Architect 
The Architect often handles the client advisor role, they furthermore contribute with the

13.1.4 Engineering consultants

phase. In a trade contract the contractor will sit without project management responsi-
bilities, and will only focus on the managing of the single trade.

It is necessary to understand which functions are performed during the four phases. Each

14.1.1 Text Programs 
Text programs involves text editor programs, used to write down information for text

14.1.2 Spreadsheet programs 
Spreadsheet programs can in regards to the projects be used to list materials and require-

Sketch Drawing Programs can be used to visualise the most basic needs for the construc-

2D drawing programs are used to visualise the construction, dependable on the phase in

14.1.5 3D Modelling Programs – BIM Modelling programs 
3D modelling programs are used to visualise the construction, the detailing level scales 
throughout the project from being only a hollow building with rooms till a finished model

Revit, and ArchiCAD. These programs can furthermore store information for the models 
in regards to requirements to the components. Thereby contributing to additional infor-
mation compared to what is offered through 2D drawings.

15 Theory – Social network analysis (SNA) as research 
method 
This Chapter outlines how social network analysis can be used as a research method for 
evaluating the IT usages in the construction industry. SNA is developed through a collab-
oration between mathematicians, anthropologists and sociologists. Even though it is de-
signed for sociology purpose it can be transferred over to a more structured analysis of 
construction projects with the addition of functions and IT tools 
Modern SNA started in the 1920s and 1930s with methods of weaving, fabric, web and 
network being used in study of social relations. Wolfgang Köhler’s ‘gestalt’ theory where 
influencing a group of Germans in USA working on cognitive and social psychology and 
this group worked with webs of relations and led to research on the problems of ‘group 
dynamics’. The group explored group structures and their influence on information flow 
through laboratory methods and studies. At the same time a parallel line of development 
was happening at Manchester University. In the 1960’s the final breakthrough in modern 
SNA came when the Harrison White group at Harvard University began investigation of 
mathematical basis of social structures. This lay the foundation for modern SNA. This lay 
the foundation of modern SNA (Scott, 2017). Multiple actors have done further research 
and development of the SNA into what it is today. 
SNA is a system of nodes and edges that represent an organisational relationship between 
the nodes. The nodes are linked through the edges, which are precisely classified. (Pryke, 
2012) Stephen Pryke states that SNA is proposed as a solution for mapping the relation-
ship in the different types of project coalitions (Winch, 2010), on the basis that it is not 
used for excluding other methods. Qualitative contextual data, is particular important due 
to construction projects limitation in repeatability. (Pryke, 2012). 
15.1.1 Reasons for using social network analysis 
Noharia and Eccles identified five reasons for using SNA in general (Noharia,1992) 
• 
All organisations/coalitions are social networks and should therefore be ad-
dressed and analysed in nodes connected by social relationships. 
• 
The organisation is a part of a surrounding environment with a network of other 
organisations. These other organisations need to be described and/or quantified 
with regard to the organisation. 
• 
Organisation reaches over multiple, complex overlapping webs of relationships. 
It is needed to broaden the aspect of a single organisation to analysis the whole 
interorganisational field. 
• 
Actions by actors can best be explained by the role in connection to the organisa-
tional network. 
• 
Network characteristics such as centrality can be quantified and visualised 
through SNA. 
These are some of the general reasons, not explicit in regards to the construction industry. 
Pryke’s states that it is worth to note that Noharia and Eccles do not distinguish between 
the different actors of individuals and companies. In regards to the construction industry 
the actors can both be single persons such as a subcontractor, but it can also consist of 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
multiple persons e.g. the client can often be regarded as one actor even though multiple 
persons will interact on the part of the client. (Pryke,2012) 
15.1.2 Problems with Social Network Analysis in construction 
Pryke states that there are also some problems associated with the use of SNA in con-
struction 
• 
Conducting a SNA is a quantitative method that needs to be linked to qualitative 
material for in relating to the project or actors for providing research data 
• 
SNA does not always provide causality in the analysis. 
• 
SNA is relatively complex both theoretical and mathematical. The complexity cre-
ates barriers when funds and the assignment time restricts the execution of the 
analysis. 
• 
It is necessary to classify the network precisely which can lead to large volume of 
data. 
• 
Sampling is not effective when it comes to SNA, it is therefore important that 
100% of participant answers otherwise estimations of response are necessary to 
provide accurate mathematical values 
This applies to some degree to the analysis in this thesis. There are however, some clear 
differences since this SNA is made from some qualitative interviews that are not bound 
to a certain project coalition. Furthermore, the data is used to explore the different IT 
software’s interaction in with the project actors and functions in a generic manner. 
15.1.3 Nodes 
The nodes in a social network are the people, groups, functions etc. that is inside of the 
system, which is being analysed. For this thesis, the nodes will consist of 3 different type 
of nodes, the functions, the project actors and the IT software. The nodes can also be 
given a weight, for instance this can be on the background that a special node is very 
important in regards to the network. It is important to make the initial decision whether 
a node is a sole actor in the project coalition or a group of actors. If a node is a group, then 
it will be referred as a one-mode network. The nodes can work as both transmitters and 
receivers. A transmitter is linked with another node in an outbound relationship direction, 
where a receiver is linked with another node in an inbound relationship direction. A car-
rier node has both inbound and outbound relationships. Another important aspect of the 
nodes is the volume of inbound, this is referred to in-degree and the outbound volume is 
referred to out-degree. This is also directly linked to the weight if the nodes have been 
given that. 
15.1.4 Edges 
The edges are also defined as the relation. The edges represent the link and flow between 
two nodes, the edge can also be represented through weighting, the weighting can for 
instance be the number of times information is shared between to nodes, or how often a 
node interact with another node. It is important to understand a flow in one direction 
does not necessarily equals the flow in the other directions, this is of cause the direct 
correlation with the transmitter and receiver. E.g. if a person in a project coalition sends 
daily reports to the manager, but the manager rarely sends any information back to the 
person.

15.1.5 Network modes 
There is in general three types of modes for a network, One-mode, two-mode and multi-
mode networks. One-mode networks consist of one set of nodes and one set events, this 
can for instance be persons in village and their social connection to each other. A two-
mode consist of two set of nodes and one set of event, this could be the social connection 
between old and young people in a village. A multi-mode is classified as multi set of nodes 
and events. This is often a more complex network to deal with, and this is also the type of 
network dealing with in this thesis since it holds multiple different types of nodes and a 
few different type of edges. 
15.1.6 Network Density 
The network density is an expression for the total connection of nodes and edges. It is 
normally defined between 0 and 1, where 1 is that all nodes are connected with each 
other, and 0 is that no nodes are connected at all. It is expected that the network density 
outcome will be relatively low for the networks, and it is also limited how useful the in-
formation is in the given situation. There can however be some relevancy if looking at the 
density of only certain parts such as the connection in certain fragments of the entire 
graph.  
Pryke also states that when density is an expression of the number of links present as a 
proportion of the total number of links possible, then it can be argued that a larger net-
work will have lower density. 
==PAGE:36==
36 
 
15.1.7 Centrality 
Centrality is defined as ‘the degree to which information flows are centred on one or a 
few organisational units’ (Mackenzie,1966). Degree centrality means the more connected 
the node is the more central it is for the network. This gives an indication of it being well 
connected to it’s surroundings.  
Closeness centrality captures how close a node is to any other nodes in the network, 
which give an indication of how quick or easy the node can get in touch with another node 
in the network. 
Betweenness centrality tries to capture the nodes role as a bridge or connector to other 
nodes. This is quantified by how many times a node works as a bridge for the shortest 
path between two nodes. 
The centrality can be used to evaluate the importance of specific nodes in the network in 
correlation to the other nodes. It is still necessary to understand the differences in rela-
tions between nodes, especially when working with a multi-mode network. Which again 
leads back to the fundamental of understanding the network is needed before it truly can 
be analysed. 
15.1.8 Modularity  
Modularity is dividing the network up into smaller modules and analysing these parts in-
dividually. This can especially be relevant if the network is a multi-mode network where 
the are possibilities of creating modules of the network and still maintain the relevant 
information that is being analysed in the given module. This can for instance be interesting 
when analysing why a certain group is well connected with each other. In regards to this 
thesis the difference phases can be analysed individually for getting a better understand-
ing of the network in these particular parts of the network. 
15.1.9 Social Network Analysis Layout 
Visualising the Network can be done through different types of layouts such as Circular 
Layout, Force-Directed layout and Arc diagram. Each creating a different layout of the 
network.  The Force-directed algorithm models the network via a physical system of bod-
ies, with forces between them. The algorithm finds a good placement of the bodies by 
minimizing the energy.  (Yifan Hu, 2005). In This thesis the Yifan Hu force-directed algo-
rithm will be used to evaluate the network, it combines the force-directed algorithm with 
multilevel algorithm. The main difference compared to a regular force-directed algorithm 
is a quicker process time and reduces complexity in the visualisation.

This chapter explains the methodology used for collecting the data and processing it. The 
initial basis for the collected data was to create a questionnaire from the data collected 
that could target a large variety of stakeholders in the construction industry for given a 
statistical insight in the IT software used in the construction industry. 
As mentioned in the literature review, a fundamental understanding of the network itself 
is necessary for conduction a SNA. The data collection in this has therefore had the focal

The Data collected has afterwards been used to create a network model through the

16.1 Interviewing process 
The Data was collected through several qualitative interviews with different stakeholders 
in the construction industry. The data was collected from a constructed framework, where 
it was split up in the four phases, Conceive, Design, Implementation and Operation, each 
of the interviewees has different focus points in a project and therefore different areas of 
knowledge in the phases. Through the phases functions were discussed, and which IT 
tools were used for these functions and lastly whom were involved in these functions. The 
reason for the qualitative interviews were collecting empirical data which can be used to 
answer the research question. 
Six interviews have been conducted throughout this thesis, each interview has been done 
in the same semi structured way of interviews, and it has been based upon the framework 
of trying to map the different functions that is conducted through the different phases. 
Each interview has involved analysing the different functions in all the phases. However, 
the interviewees have mostly only focus on and deep insight in one or two of the phases 
since the person’s interviewees come from different companies and positions and only 
participate in one or two phases. 
The interview was conducted by setting up the 4 different phases, then for each phase 
talk about what functions and processes they went through in that phase, if they used an 
IT application for supporting this function and who would be responsible for it. 
There are 3 archetypes when it comes to interviews: Structured, Semi-structured and un-
structured. Unstructured is without any sort of framework or questions made before-
hand, these are best used when wanting a result that is not influenced by any preparation. 
The Semi-structured interview has some guidelines, but gives the interviewee the option 
of given additional information and also allows the interviewer to ask follow-up questions. 
Lastly there is the structured interview that is a beforehand given structure where only 
the questions asked is answered, thereby only providing the information asked for. For 
this thesis the interviews have been conducted as a semi-structured interview. There 
were no premade questions other than the framework of going through the phases and 
adding functions, IT-tools and project actors to these. It furthermore gave the possibility

hour, but there was of cause a variation in length due to the how broad knowledge the

Based on the knowledge collected through the interviews and the different network anal-
ysis a framework for further analysis of a project coalition has been formed. The following 
chapter will go through the parameters and boundaries for using the framework, how to 
use it and what the output can give. 
This network analysis framework is used for assessing and quantifying the connections in 
the project coalition between the function, IT system and project actors Figure 36 visual-
ises the connection concept between the different nodes types that we want to assess. 
Compared to the analysis made in this thesis the edges should be directed and thereby 
quantify which direction the relationship is going between two nodes. 
 
Figure 36 Concept model for network analysis of project coalitions 
 
18.1.1 Constrains and boundaries 
It is needed to set up constrains and boundaries before collecting the data for the network 
analysis. This is done for limiting the distortion in the model and the time consumption, 
furthermore ensuring that it is completely understood what is being investigated. The 
boundaries and constrains should be set up for the three different nodes, project actors, 
IT Software and functions. For instance, the craftsmen from a subcontractor can have 
minimal influence in the network if he/she only participates in a very little task in the 
entire project, the same can go for some of the project actors in the client’s organisation. 
In that aspect another consideration is if it is going to be conducted throughout the pro-
ject, and developing the model when project actors come and go, or should it be used for 
given a static view of a given time in the project. The other main consideration when set-
ting up the boundaries is how specifically the data is going to be collected, through the

18.1.2 Collecting the data 
When the boundaries have been set up the questionnaire for collecting the information 
can be created. The core questions that needs to be asked is: 
• 
Which functions are you connected to and how well? 
• 
Based on those answers the next question will be which IT software do you use in 
the specific functions and how well are you familiar with them? 
• 
Lastly who of the other colleagues in the project coalition do you know/interact 
with, and how well? 
The framework created in this thesis only gives some generic outlines that need to be 
altered for fitting to the specific project. The questionnaire can scale from being very open 
and generic to very specific depending on how well informed the analyst for the project 
coalition is. The more open the questions are the more of the data needs to be manually 
sorted. One of the main obstacles is knowing which IT software is connected to which 
functions, so the questionnaire can either give people the option of openly select between 
all the IT software, type in their own answer or select based on selected software options 
given for the function. The next aspect to consider is how it is going to be weighted, the 
generic questionnaire made in this thesis for this framework has a max weighting of 5. It 
is suggested to make the max weighting between 3-5 for not simplifying too much and 
not creating too much distortion. One example of how one of the questions could be can 
be seen in

19 Discussion 
Conducting a network analysis of a project organisation can end up being a time consum-
ing activity, assessing its value however can be very complicated. It is clear that it will 
contribute to transparency and knowledge sharing in a project organisation. Due to the 
possibilities of clarifying which project actors are connected to a function or IT tool, this 
can possible help with solving internal problems in the organisation about complication 
rising in certain functions or with some IT tools. An interesting part was as expected that 
multiple different IT-software’s are used for conducting the same type of functions, but 
that they still have some small differences in connections to project actors, and maybe 
additional functions. These differences exists because of the way a project coalition is 
build up. Who is responsible for what can differ, and some of the IT software’s can have 
additional utilities that can be used on other functions, or it can be that some of the in-
terviewees don’t see the IT software that they had explained as being used for these ad-
ditional functions. 
The connection between the original model and the generic model is also interesting, 
Some of the clusters of IT software’s in the original model gets more centred in the generic 
model since some of the distortion as overlapping actors etc. is not influencing the posi-
tion. Furthermore, some of the IT clusters that has been combined in the generic model 
will have additional connections to new project actors, which again makes them more 
centred. The generic model gives a simpler view of the connection between the project 
actors, IT software and functions, but can be used for getting a more general understand-
ing of the connection, and therefore an understanding of why the clusters in the original 
model look like they do. 
The data used for conducting the analysis has been very subjective since it derives from 
only a few qualitative interviews. This means that the Network model also only visualise 
a few perspectives. There is definitely more IT software’s to be discovered and mapped, 
they will however probably fall into categories of IT software’s already mapped. The com-
plex concept of how a project coalition is structured creates the main barrier for creating 
a general view of the IT usages through a network analysis. The contractual relations vary 
from project to project, meaning different project actors take different responsibilities 
and therefore use different IT software’s. The research was done on an iterative process 
of trying to understand the industry and its IT usages before any more systematic mapping 
could be conducted, this was however more complex than first assessed. The complexity 
was both in regards to get relevant personal connections in the industry, but mainly due 
to every company having its own strategy for IT usages. This made it difficult to create a 
questionnaire and framework suitable for assessing the entire industry. The network anal-
ysis was therefore only conducted on the basis of these six interviews. The idea was to 
make a larger data collection over a questionnaire, the obstacle occurs in mapping the 
connection, when every project coalition is different. That is also why the framework for 
assessing a project coalitions IT usages has been created. This framework should help to 
enable a researcher to map the IT usages in a certain project coalition, or mapping multi-
ple project coalitions with different contractual parameters, giving an idea of the influ-
ence on the IT usages in project coalitions in regards to how it is contractual bound. 
The complexity of creating a Multi-mode network is an issue in regards to creating a net-
work model that is easy to analyse, the attributes of each of the nodes are almost all 
different, there are small clusters of IT software, that can be analysed in the current net-
work as having identical attributes. Otherwise, it is needed to understand each project 
==PAGE:75==
75 
 
actor’s role in the project coalition for understand what they are using the IT software for. 
This is a clear correlation with the theory from Pryke, that the network needs to be well 
defined for being able to understand the network. Otherwise the information will loose 
it’s value. It furthermore creates issues in using the analytic tools of centralities since the 
relations between some of the nodes can have be useless for exchanging information or 
other form of relation interactions from other nodes. This multi-mode complexity is very 
important to address in further studies, there is a greater need of understanding and 
quantifying the relations and nodes in a multi-mode network, otherwise the relation be-
tween the nodes looses value and it is harder to assess the model. 
The issue with a project coalition also being a dynamic organ that changes throughout the 
project can make it difficult to assess a project coalition if not continuous collecting infor-
mation during the project, or having a clear way of collecting it and then assessing the 
project after it was finished. 
One interesting aspect in regards to further use of SNA will be when or if the construction 
starts to implement the concept of Internet of Things(IoT), where more data is collected 
in how the project is executed. Some of the data collected could be used for further anal-
ysis in the SNA aspect, where for instance the data will be registered every time somebody 
logs on or uses an IT software. This can help quantifying the IT usages of different IT soft-
ware’s. 
The learning from this thesis can be found in the process of creating it, the complexity of 
collecting data from different organisations and project coalitions makes it very hard to 
structure the data given, it would therefore be interesting to conduct a network analysis 
of one of few specific project coalitions more thoroughly, to see what the outcome is in 
that perspective in regards to the IT software. From the data collected for analysis from a 
few project coalitions, a broader study can be conducted that would give a broader view 
of how the industry is using its IT. Ensuring a clean differentiation in the type of project 
coalition can further give an insight into what the different project coalition structures do 
to the interaction between functions, IT software and project actors. Furthermore, quan-
tifying the relationships will give a better insight in how the software is used, is it a niche 
software used a few times through an entire project or is it one used everyday?  
This thesis can largely be seen as the initial step for trying to map the IT usages in the 
Danish construction industry, with reflections and considerations for further investigation 
in the field of study.

20 Conclusion 
The created network models give an insight into how IT software is used in the construc-
tion industry. The SNA can be a tool used for further investigating into this area, but the 
complexity and understanding of construction project and project coalitions, makes it a 
difficult and time-consuming process. It was initially expected to be easier to get an over-
all assessment of the industry, but the need of connections and alliances with stakehold-
ers in the industry is essential for a broader study into the use of IT. 
A framework for analysis a project coalition has been developed from the research, con-
ducted around the IT usages in the construction industry and SNA as a tool for analysis 
the industry, This framework can hopefully help with further investigation into specific 
project coalitions and lay a more solid foundation for a broader research into the industry. 
There are possibilities of mapping the IT software usages in the construction industry 
through network analysis. Mapping the IT software through network analysis can give 
other visualisations and clear understandings of the connection of IT software in the con-
struction industry, compared to more traditional analytic tools.  
The data collected for this thesis has been the Achilles heel of this thesis, the data has 
been limited and even though kept in a framework to unorganised. Meaning that there is 
still IT software’s to be uncovered and there probably have been created unnecessary 
distortion that could have been limited by using a structured interview instead with more 
limitation to what the interviewee had of answer options. 
The future of SNA in regards to investigating IT software can be found in further imple-
mentation of Internet of things, which emphasize more data collecting. If a further devel-
oped framework of what has initially been proposed here is developed, then a live SNA 
model could be created that changes whenever different project actors uses different IT 
software’s. 
The final statement for this thesis: 
• 
The SNA can be an effective tool to analysing and understanding the IT-software’s 
use and interaction in the construction industry 
• 
The complexity of acquiring good and rich data is the biggest obstacle for con-
ducting a good analysis 
• 
Suggestions towards a more project coalition focused analysis has been made 
• 
A framework for how to conduct a more project coalition oriented SNA has been 
developed 
• 
This thesis is only the tip of the iceberg when it comes to analysing the IT-software 
in the construction industry. Further investigation is needed for creating a better 
picture of its current state.
