Abstract  
The effect of NaCl (0-0.4M) on the oil/water interfacial properties of soluble pea 
protein hydrolysate (SPPH) was investigated. Tryptic hydrolysis promoted lower 
molecular weight protein fractions and a more disordered secondary structure. The 
addition of low NaCl concentration (0.1-0.2 M) provoked salting-in effect, decreasing 
both hydrodynamic parameter and ζ-potential, while higher NaCl concentration (0.4 M) 
caused aggregation with a maximum hydrodynamic diameter of 190 nm. Surface 
activity of SPPH improved by increasing ionic strength, with maximum interfacial 
pressure of 14.28 mN/m at 0.4 M NaCl and higher penetration rate of 15.6 × 104 s-1 at 
0.2 M NaCl. Nevertheless, the addition of NaCl had negative effect on elasticity and 
strength of the interfacial film, where the sample without salt exhibited highest 
dilatational and shear storage modulus of 17.98 and 84 mN/m, respectively.

1 Introduction 
Proteins are macromolecules consisted by amino acids which are linked with 
peptide bonds. The sequence of amino acid determines the structure and the molecular 
conformation of the protein (Belitz, et al., 2004, p. 12). This conformation induces a 
variety of functional properties, such as solubility, gelation, emulsion and foam 
formation, which have been well-studied over the years (Barac et al., 2010; Chen et al., 
2011; Crowley et al., 2002; Giménez et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2021). 
In food industry, the vast majority of proteins, which are utilized as functional 
ingredients, is animal origin and only the 33% is originated by plant (Wouters et al., 
2016). However, the environmental impact of animal proteins forced scientists to 
research for alternative protein sources, resulting a continuously increasing interest for 
plant proteins. Pea protein stands out among other plant proteins as it has low carbon 
footprint, low price and it is allergen- and gluten-free (Lan et al., 2020). 
On the other hand, dairy proteins remain the dominant emulsifier, as plant proteins 
stabilized emulsions are characterized by reduced self-stability (Kim et al., 2020). 
Moreover, under environmental stress conditions, such as high ionic strength or high 
temperature, bovine gelatin stabilized emulsions exhibited better stability than those 
stabilized by soy protein (Taha et al., 2019). 
To overcome this issue, several modification techniques, such as enzymatic 
hydrolysis and ultrasonication, have been tested to improve the functional properties of 
plant proteins (Kamani et al., 2021; Tamm et al., 2016). In case of enzymatic 
hydrolysis, the product is a mixture of lower molecular weight peptides, with less 
organized secondary and tertiary structure, compared to the native protein. This may 
result the exposure of hydrophobic group and the increase of polar parts. Consequently, 
the diffusion rate and the affinity to the interface are enhanced (Tamm et al., 2016).  
Factors that can also affect the structure and therefore the functional properties are 
thermodynamic parameters, such as protein concentration and temperature, or 
parameters related to aqueous electrolyte conditions, such as pH and ionic strength 
(Tian et al., 2021). Ladjal Ettoumi et al. studied the emulsifying properties of legume 
properties (pea, chickpea and lentil) and they found out that the best conditions were at 
==PAGE:6==
6 
 
1.5% of protein and less than 50 mM NaCl. Tian et al. investigated the effect of pH and 
ionic strength on interfacial, and emulsifying properties of β-conglycinin. The authors 
reported that the emulsifying ability was improved at pH 3 and 8, while the increase in 
ionic strength improved the adsorption kinetics but not the viscoelasticity of the 
interfacial layer. 
The aim of this study therefore is to investigate the structural changes of pea protein 
after tryptic hydrolysis and the effect of ionic strength in interfacial properties of the 
hydrolysate. It is expected that the addition of salt will improve the interfacial activity 
of the protein and it will affect the elasticity of the interfacial film. 
2. Literature review 
2.1 Pea protein 
Peas are used as global feed and food ingredient (whole, flour, protein concentrate 
etc). It is considered as a sustainable crop as pea plants have a symbiotic relationship 
with soil microorganisms (Rhizobia) which allows them to absorb indirectly 
atmospheric nitrogen into soil. Due to this ability, peas are used as rotational crops with 
cereals as they increase the organic matter, the nitrogen content and at the same time 
improve the soil aggregation and water conservation. Several pea genotypes are 
cultivated, such as field pea, spring pea and yellow pea, with Canada, Russia, USA and 
France being the major pea-production countries. (Tulbek et al., 2016) 
Pea proteins are obtained from pea seeds through wet or dry fractionation/milling 
processes. Based on the process, the protein content varies from 48% to 85% (Tulbek 
et al., 2016). Pea protein mainly consists of globulins, (60-70%) and albumins (15-25%) 
(Djemaoune et al., 2019). Albumins are water-soluble proteins, with molecular weight 
varying from 6 to 80 kDa, and high in lysine and sulfur amino acid content (Yang et 
al., 2021). 
Pea globulins are composed by two fractions of salt-soluble proteins, fraction 11S 
(legumin) and fraction 7S (vicilin and convicilin). Legumin is a high molecular weight 
protein (350 – 400 kDa) with a hexameric quaternary structure. Each subunit of the six 
contains an acidic α-chain (40 kDa) and a basic β-chain (20 kDa) which are linked by 
disulphide bond (Klost et al., 2020). Vicilin and convicilin are more soluble proteins 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
compared to legumin, with molecular weight 150 – 180 kDa and 210 kDa, respectively. 
Both, vicilin and convicilin, are trimeric proteins, however their subunits are not linked 
by disulphide bond. The molecular weight of convicilin subunits is 70 kDa, while the 
molecular weight of vicilin subunits varies (Yang et al., 2021). 
Nutritional benefits, water-holding capacity, foam expansion, foam stability, 
gelation, emulsion formation and emulsion stability are the major functional properties 
of pea protein (Barac et al., 2010; Ladjal-Ettoumi et al., 2016; Meng & Cloutier, 2014). 
Moreover, the functional properties of each fraction have been studied by Cserhalmi et 
al., on 1998. They tested the emulsifying properties, surface hydrophobicity and 
thermal denaturation of pea protein fractions (albumin, total globulin, vicilin and 
legumin) and they found out that total globulin and vicilin fractions exhibit better 
emulsifying properties. In addition, Djemaoune et al., 2019 studied the foaming 
properties of pea albumin aggregates and showed that the surface properties can be 
improved with surface modification. 
In a recent study, García Arteaga et al. applied enzymatic hydrolysis in pea protein 
with eleven different enzymes considering the effect of this process in functional 
properties and sensory perception of the protein. The findings of the study showed that 
trypsin hydrolysates exhibited the highest foaming (2271%) and emulsifying (719 
mL/g) capacities. In another study, Klost et al. examined the effect of partial tryptic 
hydrolysis in interfacial and functional properties of pea protein in a range of pH values. 
The authors concluded that this modification technique increased the solubility and the 
elasticity of the interfacial film compared to native protein at all examined pH.

even amino acids. The reaction takes place in presence of excess water, catalysed by an 
enzyme and it is described as a nucleophilic attack of a water molecule on the peptide 
bond between two adjacent amino acids. The charge of the free carboxyl and amino 
group that are formed is highly dependent of pH in which the reaction is conducted 
(Wouters et al., 2016). The reaction can be simplified and described by Eq. 1

to control an enzymatic hydrolysis, such as the substrate, the enzyme, the enzyme – 
substrate ratio and the temperature.

2.2.2 Enzyme 
Enzymes are proteins with the ability to accelerate biochemical reactions in living 
organisms, however, it is common to be extracted and utilized for the catalysis of a 
wide range of reactions with commercial interest (Robinson, 2015). In food protein 
hydrolysis, it is usually used endopeptidases, such as serine, cysteine and aspartic 
proteases (Adler-Nissen, 1985).The enzyme concentration can be easily used to alert 
the hydrolysis rate. The relationship between enzyme concentration and reaction rate is 
linear (Robinson, 2015). 
Environmental factors, such as temperature and pH, have also a crucial influence 
on reaction rate. At different pH values, both enzyme and substrate have different 
ionised and non-ionised groups, consequently, the binding rate is significantly affected 
(Robinson, 2015). Each enzyme has an optimum pH value where it has maximum 
activity, in case of the most serine proteases, the optimum activity is reached at alkaline 
pH (Adler-Nissen, 1985).The effect of temperature is more complicate. An increase in 
temperature enhances molecular movement and therefore the reaction rate increases. 
Simultaneously, the exposure of enzyme at high temperature for a certain period of time 
leads to enzyme denaturation (Robinson, 2015). 
An example of enzyme with several industrial applications is trypsin, which is 
widely used in food, pharmaceutical and leather industry. Trypsin classified to serine 
protease family and firstly was extracted from the pancreas of cow. Nowadays, it is 
excreted from the pancreas of various mammalians, including human, however the most 
used type is the bovine trypsin (Gong et al., 2015). The optimum pH range for trypsin 
is 7 to 9 (Adler-Nissen, 1985) and the maximum temperature at 65 oC (Gong et al., 
2015). The specificity is to hydrolyse the peptide bonds between the carboxyl-group of 
lysine and arginine residues and the amino-group of the adjacent amino acid (Gong et 
al., 2015).

2.2.3 Enzyme/Substrate ratio  
Another important parameter is the enzyme – substrate ratio (E/S), as it has an 
influence on the reaction rate. In case where the substrate is soluble, the relationship 
between rate of hydrolysis and E/S is linear, on the other hand, when the substrate is 
insoluble, their relationship is not linear (Adler-Nissen, 1985). Karamać et al., studied 
the effect of E/S on the hydrolysis of pea protein isolates by trypsin and found out that 
an increase at E/S leaded to an increase of reaction rate.

2.3 Interfacial properties of proteins 
The ability of proteins to form foams and emulsions stems from their amphipathic 
nature, as proteins contain a polar and a non-polar part that allow them to adsorb in 
thermodynamically unstable interfaces (Dickinson, 1999). Consequently, they are able 
to decrease the surface tension (SF) and interfacial tension (IFT) in air/water and 
oil/water interfaces, respectively. This means that the mechanical work that is needed 
for the formation of an emulsion or a foam is lower (Bos & Vielt, 2001). Henceforth, 
in this report the term interfacial tension will be referred both to air/water and oil/water 
interfaces. Decrease is IFT implies increase in interfacial pressure (IFP), where the

The change in IFP varies and depends on the amount of protein that adsorbed in 
the interface, the extent of protein conformation, the interactions between the adsorbed 
molecules and the thickness of the layer (Bos & van Vielt, 2001). Highly increasing 
IFP rate means high adsorption rate and therefore the protein particles have less time to

unfold on the interface. Hence, the area occupied per molecule is lower and the amount 
of adsorbed proteins is high (Dickinson, 1999). 
The rearrangement of protein during and after adsorption is highly dependent of its 
structure. Proteins with small or no structural changes are characterized as “hard” 
proteins, such as lysozyme in native state, while proteins with significant structural 
changes are characterized as “soft” proteins, such as casein. “Hard” proteins tent to 
maintain their enzymatic activity even after adsorption. On the contrary, “soft” proteins 
which lose their secondary structure loose significantly their enzymatic activity. 
Moreover, the adsorption of “soft” proteins is poorly reversible, while “hard” proteins 
participate more in loosely held multilayers. (Dickinson, 1999) 
In order to develop products with improved properties and stability, it is important 
to understand in depth the interfacial behavior of proteins. To bear more knowledge in 
that area several techniques have been developed. The study of an interfacial system 
can be either close to equilibrium or far from it and can be applied both dilatation and 
shear rheological techniques. (Bos & van Vielt, 2001)

2.3.2 Interfacial shear rheology 
Interfacial shear rheology provides information regarding the inter and intra-
molecular interactions at interface, the strength of those interactions and predict the 
long-term stability to coalescence. It is an indirect method, where the stress is applied 
through the probe at the interface and the deformation is transferred through adjacent 
molecules. Therefore the geometry and the placement of the probe are highly important 
(Krägel & Derkatch, 2010).

2.4 Effect of salt concentration on proteins  
Salt is widely used as additive in food industry, as it does not only enhance the 
taste but also inhibits the microbial growth by reducing the water activity (Albarracín 
et al., 2011). Besides that, salt has a great effect on protein behavior as it can alert its 
functional properties, such solubility, foaming and emulsifying capacity and stability 
(Li et al., 2018)(Akintayo et al., 1999). Hence, it is important to understand the 
interactions occurring in a protein-water-salt system both in bulk and in an interfacial 
environment. 
The effect of ionic strength in protein behavior is highly dependent on protein net 
charge, surface charge density and hydrophobicity, but also on hydration, size, 
polarizability and valency of salt ions (J. Zhang, 2012). In addition, the concentration 
of salt has a significant role in protein-water-salt interactions. 
At low salt concentration, salts can stabilize proteins through non-specific 
electrostatic interactions, that are depended only on the ionic strength of the solution 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
(Arakawa & Timasheff, 1984). At intermediate salt concentrations, specific ion 
interactions are dominant, as non-specific electrostatic interactions significantly 
reduced due to the screening effect by counter-ions. At very high salt concentrations, 
water molecules are captured in hydration zone of ions, thus, there are less available 
water to interact with the protein molecules and therefore hydrophobic protein-protein 
interactions are increased (Kunz, 2010). 
Specific ion interactions were primarily studied by Franz Hofmeister in the 1880s 
and 1890s, who classified salts based on their ability to precipitate proteins; Na2SO4 
<NaCl <NaBr, NaI <NaClO4 < NaSCN. Those anions can be distinguished in 
chaotropic (SO4-2 <Cl- <Br-) and kosmotropic (I- <ClO4 <SCN-). Similarly, cations can 
be characterized as chaotropic (Li+ <Mg2+< Ca2+) and kosmotropic (NH4+ <K+ <Na+) 
(Kunz et al., 2004) Kosmotropes (salting-out) enhance protein stability, while decrease 
protein solubility. On the contrary, chaotropes (salting-in) destabilize protein structure 
by breaking down hydrogen bonds leading to denaturation and increase in solubility 
(Lawal, 2006). However, parameters such as the hydration properties of ions, and the 
affinity of ions to protein surface and backbone have an significant effect in Hofmeister 
series (Zhou et al., 2021) (Paterová et al., 2013)(Okur et al., 2017). 
Beside the effect of salt in bulk properties of proteins, Davis et al. and Ulaganathan, 
et al. studied the effect of ionic strength in water/air interface. The former investigated 
the water/air interface stabilized by whey protein and the latter by β-lactoglobulin. Both 
agreed that close to isoelectric point (pI) of the protein an increase in ionic strength did 
not affect significantly the surface activity. On the contrary, at pH away from pI, an 
increase in ionic strength provoked an increase in surface pressure. Similar effect was 
reported by Tian et al., in 2021, where they studied the same effect in oil/water 
interface. They found out that the protein adsorption improved by addition of salt, 
nevertheless the elasticity of the interfacial layer decreased.

3.1 Enzymatic hydrolysis 
The enzymatic hydrolysis was performed as described by Tamm et al. with some 
modifications. Brief, 2% PPI suspension was prepared and stirred over night at room 
temperature (~20 oC). Then, prior to enzyme addition, the protein suspension was 
transferred to a 500 mL Erlenmeyer flask, heated at 50 oC and pH adjusted to 8 using 
aliquot amount of NaOH 0.5 M. The enzyme-substrate ratio was 1:50. 
The degree of hydrolysis was estimated with pH-stat method as stated in (Eq. 3). 
htot value was 7.2 meqv/g of protein and determined based on the amino acid analysis 
(Table 1). During the reaction aliquot amount of NaOH 0.5 M was added with an auto-
titrator in order to keep the pH constant at 8. 
When a DH of 4% was reached, the Erlenmeyer flask was placed immediately in

3.8 Interfacial pressure and interfacial dilatational properties  
Interfacial pressure and interfacial dilatational properties of SPPH in water/oil

purpose, 0.5% of SPPH was dissolved in phosphate buffer (pH 7.2) and stirred for 1 
hour, followed by the addition of aliquot amount NaCl and 30 min stirring. Afterwards, 
the solution was injected by a syringe into an optical glass cuvette filled with sunflower 
oil. A droplet with volume of 35 μL was monitored for 1800 s and the interfacial 
pressure was measured every 2 sec. Adsorption kinetics parameters, kp and kr, were 
determined by Eq. 7. 
Interfacial dilatational properties were determined through an oscillatory test after 
the droplet was equilibrated for 1800 sec following the described above protocol. An 
amplitude of 2% was applied in a frequency range from 0.1 to 1 Hz. The test was 
performed in 4 steps (0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 1 Hz), with 10 s waiting between each step. The 
droplet was subjected for four cycles at each frequency. Dilatational storage (E’) and 
loss (E’’) moduli were obtained. All samples measured in triplicates.

and 20 mL of sunflower oil were added on the top. Amplitude sweep measurement was

4.1 Molecular weight distribution  
Molecular weight distribution results obtained by size exclusion chromatography 
(SEC-MALS) and electrophoretic analysis (SDS-PAGE) are shown in Table 2. PPI 
exhibited protein fractions of ~920, ~165, ~68 and ~50 kDa. Protein fractions with Mw 
higher than 920 kDa might be attributed to supramolecular legumin aggregates, as those 
reported by Yang et al. Similar protein fractions were also observed by Shevkani et al., 
for field pea protein with purity over 90%. Fraction with Mw of ~165 kDa probably 
corresponds to vicilin, which molecular weight varies from 150 to 180 kDa (Shevkani 
et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2021). Size of ~68 kDa fraction might represent legumin or/and 
convicilin subunits, as they have Mw of 60 and 70 kDa, respectively (Klost, Giménez-
Ribes, et al., 2020). Lower Mw fraction (~50 kDa) was attributes to a mixture of 
legumin, vicilin and convicilin subunits, but also to albumins, which Mw varies from 6 
to 80 kDa (Yang et al., 2021). 
The hydrolysis of pea protein by trypsin caused a decrease in Mw of all fractions. 
Specifically, trypsin cleaved the quaternary stracture of vicilin in smaller peptides 
(Table 2). Those results are in agreement with the findings of Klost and Drusch, who 
reported that vicilin was cleaved even at low DH as 2%. This might casued due to the 
relativly high content of vicilin in aginine (6%) and lysine (6%) (Jackson et al., 1969; 
Rangel et al., 2003). 
The SDS-PAGE analysis of PPI under reducing conditions showed main bands at 
~90, ~66, ~50, ~40, ~30, ~21 and ~18 kDa. The subunit of ~90 kDa was attributed to 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
lypoxygenase (Shand et al., 2007). In several studies (Klost, Giménez-Ribes, et al., 
2020; Shand et al., 2007; Shevkani et al., 2015), subunits of ~50 and ~30 kDa had been 
ascribed to vicilin monomer, while lower Mw subunits (19-12.5 kDa) to vicilin fraction. 
In the same studies, fractions of ~40 and ~20 kDa corresponded to α-chain and β-chain 
of legumin.  
The electrophoretic profile of SPPH (Table 2) showed ~64, ~55, ~25, ~10 and 
lower than 6 kDa. Similar results were repored by Klost et al., in 2020, where trypsin 
cleaved the α-chain of legumin, while the β-chain remaind almost the same. According 
to the authors, this was attributed to the fact that legumin-α has higher contant of lysine 
and arginine compared to legumin-β.

4.2 Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy 
FTIR analysis is a non-destructive method which identifies the chemical 
composition of the whole sample and provides information about the functional groups 
and the protein structure (Amir et al., 2013). In this report it was applied to examine the 
influence of enzymatic hydrolysis in molecular structure of pea protein. 
Starting from high wavelength to low in Fig. 2, the first region was observed from 
3000 to 3600 cm-1, with maximum absorbance at 3275 cm-1 for both PPI and SPPH. 
According to Acquah et al., this region corresponds to Amide A and it is associated 
with the starching vibrations of hydrogen bonds between O-H and N-H. PPI also 
exhibited a low intensity peak at 3070 cm-1, which is attributed to Amide B band. This 
band has been associated to the starching vibrations of intramolecular hydrogen bond 
between N-H groups.(Barth, 2007) The following region, from 3000 to 2500 cm-1, is 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
linked to C–H stretching and it mainly derives from lipids and carbohydrates (Sadhana, 
2014).  
Amide I and II regions which occur from 1600 to 1700 cm-1 and 1500 to 1600 cm-
1 respectively, provide information regarding the secondary structure of proteins. 
Amide I is governed by the stretching vibrations of C=O (70-85%) and C-N groups and 
is linked to the backbone conformation (Acquah et al., 2020). No difference in 
wavelength was observed between the two samples as both exhibited maximum 
absorbance at 1632 cm-1. Amide II band derives primarily from the N-H bending and 
secondarily from the C-N stretching vibrations (Amir et al., 2013). In that region, PPI 
exhibited a maximum at 1527 cm-1, while SPPH exhibited a maximum at 1577 cm-1. 
Amide III can be found in a wavelength from 1400 to 1200 cm-1 and it is a combination 
of N–H bending and C-N stretching. PPI and SPPH had a peak at 1392 and 1397 cm-1, 
respectively (Q. Zhang et al., 2020). A shift towards higher wavelength is an indication 
of a weaker protein network caused by the disruption of hydrogen bonds (Acquah et 
al., 2020) 
 At wavelength lower than 1200 cm-1, three peaks can be observed at 1234, 1050 
and 923 cm-1 for both samples. The peak at 1234 cm-1 is linked with C-H bond of 
protein, while the last two peaks are associated with the C-O bond of carbohydrates 
(Sadhana, 2014). Generally, in Fig. 2, it showed that the intensity of SPPH is lower 
compared to PPI and this is due to the lower protein content of SPPH sample (Acquah 
et al., 2020), however the enzymatic hydrolysis may contribute to that (López-Barón et 
al., 2018).

4.3 Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) 
Differential scanning calorimetry thermogram provides information about the 
phase transitions, such as melting and glass transition, as well as protein denaturation. 
Melting occurs during the absorption of latent heat during the isothermal change, solid-
liquid, and glass transition defined as the change associated with variations in the 
molecular mobility and relaxation time in amorphous solids. (Leyva-Porras et al., 
2019). 
Glass transition (Tg), protein denaturation (Td) and melting point (Tm) of PPI and 
SPPH are showed in Fig. 3. Tg was not observed for PPI as previously reported by Vall-

found out by Sochava and Smirnova, who studied the denaturation of purified legumin. 
A different Tm (180 oC) was reported by Jia et al., however this might be due to the 
different water content (Sochava & Smirnova, 1993). 
Tg of SPPH was observed at 43.9 oC. Similar value (41 oC) was reported by 
Pelgrom et al. for pea protein isolate with dry matter up to 90%. Td and Tm of SPPH

4.4 ζ-Potential and hydrodynamic diameter 
ζ-potential is an analytical method to determine the surface charge of particles in 
solutions. It is referred to the electrical potential of the double layer which is formed 
between the surface charge, a thin layer of opposite charge around the surface and an 
outer diffuse layer consisted by loosely associated ions (Shnoudeh et al., 2019). It has 
values varying from -100 to +100 mV, however a value greater than ± 30 mV indicates 
a high degree of stability (Kumar & Dixit, 2017). ζ-potential and hydrodynamic 
diameter (Dh) of SPPH particles in solutions with different ionic strength are presented 
in Fig. 4. All samples exhibited good stability as they exhibited ζ-potential ≥ -30 mV. 
A value of -33 mV was observed for SPPH without salt. Klost and Drusch studied the 
==PAGE:27==
27 
 
functional properties of hydrolysed pea protein in different pH. The authors reported ζ-
potential of -30 mV for hydrolysed protein at DH 2% and pH 7. 
The addition of 0.1 and 0.2 M NaCl had as a result a decrease of ζ-potential close 
to -36 mV. This may be attributed to salting in effect, where strongly-hydrated cations 
interact with hydrophilic parts on protein surface (Maurer et al., 2011). Consequently, 
the protein surface charge increases resulting greater protein-protein electrostatic 
repulsions. Salting in effect of globular proteins (chymosin, β-lactoglobulin and 
pumpkin seed globulin) at 0.3 M NaCl was found by Maurer et al. Further increase in 
ionic strength (0.4 M NaCl) caused a decline of ζ-potential close to -30 mV. This may 
be ascribed to screening effect of surface charge by counterions (Dombrowski et al., 
2018). 
An increase in ionic strength from 0 to 0.2 M NaCl reduced Dh from ~177 to ~148 
nm. Nevertheless, further addition of NaCl up to 0.4 M provoked an increase in 
diameter (~190 nm). Similar behaviour was observed by Tan et al. for walnut protein, 
where Dh initially decreased until 0.15 M NaCl and then continiously increased until 
0.5 M. According to the authors those results were attributed to salting-out effect which 
caused molecular aggregation.

4.6 Interfacial pressure and adsorption kinetics  
The effect of NaCl in adsorption of SPPH in oil/ water interface was monitoring 
for 1800 s and the results are presented in Fig. 6, where the interfacial pressure (π) was 
plotted over time. Adsorption kinetics parameters, diffusion rate (kdiff), penetration rate 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
(kp) and the rearrangement rate (kr), were determined by the plots of Eq. 6 and Eq. 7 
(Table 3). 
π values close to zero were not detected, indicating a good surface activity of 
SPPH. The addition of NaCl improved π value in all concentrations and the highest 
π1800 value (14.28 mN/m) was measured at 0.4 M NaCl. Similar findings ware described 
by Tan et al., who investigated the effect of NaCl (0.05-0.5 M) in oil/water interface 
stabilized by walnut protein-xanthan gum. The increase in interfacial pressure was 
ascribed to counter-ion screening effect, which provoked a decrease in surface charge 
and consequently an incase in protein hydrophobicity (Tan et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 
2021). 
The estimation of kdiff during the initial step was limited by the experimental 
method used in this report, however, the initial jump in π (Fig. 6) is a qualitative 
measure of the diffusion of proteins. (Álvarez Gómez & Rodríguez Patino, 2006). 
Considering that, it could be noticed that at 0.4 M NaCl the initial jump was higher 
compared to lower NaCl concentrations. Ruíz-Henestrosa et al. found out that the 
increase in ionic strength from 0.05 M to 0.5 M increased kdiff of soy vicilin at pH 7 by 
4-fold. 
kp and kr, were fitted well to the first-order equation (LR > 0.958). The addition of 
NaCl improved both penetration and arrangement of SPPH. Maximum value of kp was 
15.6 × 104 s-1 for 0.2 M NaCl and of kr was 52.9 × 104 s-1 for 0.1 M NaCl. At 0.4 M 
NaCl, both kp and kr decreased compared to 0.1 M NaCl. At neutral pH and low salt 
concentration (0.1 M NaCl), electrostatic repulsions between protein molecules could 
cause exposure of hydrophobic groups and enable the penetration at the interface (Perez 
et al., 2012). At high salt concentrations (0.4 M NaCl) the reduction of electrostatic 
repulsions promotes a denser packing of protein molecules (Ramamirtham et al., 2021). 
Considering the adsorption kinetics of SPPH in oil/water interface can be conclude 
that diffusion is the main mechanism which controls the protein adsorption (Ruíz-
Henestrosa et al., 2007). Penetration is correlated with NaCl concentration (Table 3), 
however no correlation was found between NaCl concentration and rearrangement rate, 
suggesting that post-adsorption changes are not influenced by ionic strength 
(Ramamirtham et al., 2021).

4.7 Dilatational interfacial properties 
Dilatational storage (E’) and loss (E”) moduli were used to describe the viscoelastic 
properties of the interfacial film in different frequencies (0.1, 0.2, 0.4 and 1 Hz) (Fig. 
7). E’ is an indication of the strength of the interfacial film, formed by protein 
interactions due to conformational rearrangement, and the film rigidity resisting the 
deformation of the interface. On the contrary, E” represents the energy loss through 
relaxation process (Tamm and Drusch, 2017). All samples displayed dominant E’ for 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
all the examined frequencies (Fig. 7), implying the formation of a viscoelastic 
interfacial film. 
For all samples, no frequency dependency was observed at low frequencies (0.1 
and 0.2 Hz) for both E’ and E”, indicating very slight relaxation process (Tamm and 
Drusch, 2017). Similar trend was reported by Won et al., for β-lactoglobulin at 
water/tetradecane interface, at frequencies from 0.001 to 1 Hz and low ionic strength 
(1-100 mM). At 0.4 Hz, the sample without NaCl exhibited the highest E’, while the 
addition of 0.1 and 0.2 M NaCl provoked a declined from 13.72 to 11.06 mN/m. E’ 
improved with further addition of NaCl up to 0.4 M, from 11.06 to 12.45 mN/m. Similar 
trend observed at 1 Hz, where E’ decreased from 17.98 to 14.74 mN/m, when NaCl 
concentration increased from 0 to 0.2 M and increased from 14.74 to 15, 47 mN/m by 
adding 0.4 M NaCl. No significant difference was observed for E” at 0.4 and 1 Hz for 
all the samples. 
The increase of ionic strength had a negative effect on viscoelastic properties of 
interfacial film. This might be the results of less interactions between the adsorbed 
proteins (Tan et al., 2017) and it is in good agreement with the ζ-potential results, which 
showed high electrostatic repulsion between protein molecules at 0.1 and 0.2 M NaCl. 
The improvement of interfacial elasticity after the increase of ionic strength from 0.2 
to 0.4 M might be associated with the reduction of Debye length which entailed an 
increase in protein-protein interactions (Rühs et al., 2012).

4.8 Shear interfacial rheology 
An interfacial time sweep was conducted for 25 h at frequency of 0.2 Hz and 0.1% 
strain amplitude in order to monitor the adsorption of protein to oil/water interface (Fig. 
8). The elastic modulus (G’) was determined at 25 h as an indication of the elastic 
strength of the interfacial film (Ramamirtham et al., 2021). 
Three distinct phases of protein absorption can be found by a time sweep 
measurement. The first phase is the diffusion, which is governed by the bulk 
temperature, the particle size and the net charge. The duration of this phase can be 
established as the time in which the rheometer can detect a reliable elastic modulus 
(G’> 10-4 N/m) (Ramamirtham et al., 2021). In order to overcome this induction period, 
high protein concentration was used. The second phase includes protein rearrangement 
at the interface and the formation of a monolayer. It can be identified as a steep increase 
of G’ (Ramamirtham et al., 2021). The third phase is referred to the steady state, where 
G’ reaches a plateau (Casanova et al., 2020).

All the samples demonstrated predominant elastic behaviour (G’>G”), however 
different crossover points were observed. Particularly, the sample without salt 
displayed crossover point at 4 h, while the addition of 0.1 and 0.2 M NaCl had as a 
result a crossover point at 5.7 and 6 h, respectively. At 0.4 M NaCl, the tine required 
for G’>G” reduced at 5.4 h. After that point, a rapid increase of G’ was found out for 
samples with 0 and 0.4 M NaCl, while the increase was modest for samples with 0.1 
and 0.2 M NaCl. Moreover, samples with 0.1 and 0.2 M NaCl reached plateau after 18 
and 21 h, respectively, however no steady state was reached for 0 and 0.4 M NaCl 
within the monitoring time. 
After 25 h, G’ values of 84, 5, 6 and 25 mN/m was obtained for samples with 0, 
0.1, 0.2 and 0.4 M NaCl, respectively. It can be seen that the presence of NaCl had a 
negative effect on the strength of the interfacial layer. Those results were in agreement 
with the dilatational properties (section 4.7). Nevertheless, among the samples with 
NaCl, the increase in salt concentration provoked an improvement on G’. This may be 
ascribed to screening effect which promotes hydrophobic interactions and the formation 
of aggregates (Tan et al., 2017). This hypothesis is in accordance with the particle size 
of solutions as it was measured by DLS (section 4.5).

5 Conclusion 
Partial tryptic hydrolysis of pea protein significantly reduced the size of 
polypeptide chains and also affected the secondary structure due to the disruption of 
hydrogen bonds. In solution system, the addition of 0.1 and 0.2 M NaCl had as a result 
the decrease of ζ-potential and hydrodynamic diameter, however did not affect the 
turbidity. These changes were attributed to the increase of electrostatic repulsions 
between protein molecules and the salting-in effect. On the contrary, 0.4 M of NaCl 
provoked the formation of agglomerates as hydrodynamic diameter and turbidity 
increased, while ζ-potential decreased. These results may be ascribed to salting-out 
effect. 
In oil/water interfacial environment, the addition of NaCl improved the surface 
activity of SPPH, due to the counter-ion screening effect. Nonetheless, dilatational and 
interfacial shear properties deteriorated by the addition of NaCl, due to poor interactions 
between proteins. However, at high salt concentrations the elasticity and the strength of 
the interfacial layer improved compared to lower salt concentrations. This may be 
attributed to the formation of agglomerates at the interface. 
It can be concluded that changes in ionic strength affect significantly the bulk and 
the interfacial properties of SPPH. Despite that, the employment of ellipsometry could 
provide better understanding of protein-water-salt system at oil-water interface, 
regarding the composition, the thickness and the electrical conductivity of the 
interfacial film. Moreover, further investigation on the emulsifying ability of SPPH in 
a real system could bring more insights regarding the effect of salt concentration.
