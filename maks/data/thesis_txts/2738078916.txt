Abstract 
 
Methanol is considered as a promising candidate for supporting power-to-X systems thanks 
to its ease of transport and storage. The Solid Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC), a high temperature 
technology, has the advantage of being fuel-flexible, allowing the direct use of methanol for 
electricity conversion. This study develops a model of a methanol-fuelled SOFC at the cell 
level to better understand its internal functioning. Key results, such as current-voltage 
curves, are generated and compared to a reference case with hydrogen feeding. Deviations 
in performance are observed, with the methanol-fuelled SOFC displaying, on average, a 
lower cell voltage than the hydrogen-fuelled SOFC. While the performance gap does not 
significantly change with temperature, it increases notably with higher values of the current 
density. Analysis of the fuel composition reveals that methanol is not fully converted in the 
SOFC, leaving some unconverted methanol in the exhaust gases. This incomplete 
decomposition is identified as the main cause of performance deviations, as they are 
eliminated when the SOFC is run with pre-cracked methanol. The model also evaluates 
carbon deposition through the Boudouard reaction, showing significant carbon formation at 
low temperatures and low current densities. The results indicate that operating conditions 
must be carefully selected to prevent the reaction from becoming thermodynamically 
favourable.

1. Introduction 
 
Current environmental challenges incite society to transition towards greener energy 
systems, reducing reliance on fossil fuels and sources with high CO2 emissions. Hence, 
Europe has set the target of reducing carbon emissions by 55% in 2030, compared to 1990 
levels, and achieving carbon-neutrality by 2050 [1]. To meet these objectives, renewable 
energy sources (RES) like solar and wind energy, being carbon-free, are being extensively 
developed across the continent. 
Carbon will also remain vital for various competitive sectors such as buildings, 
chemicals, and fuels, which will make it a scarce resource in the future. Consequently, there 
is a growing interest in developing more carbon-free fuels, pushing the maritime sector to 
investigate alternative options like ammonia [2]. The integration of ammonia brings 
additional challenges and costs due to its high toxicity, posing risks to both humans and 
marine biodiversity [2]. Another approach involves recirculating CO2 through capture, 
storage and reuse processes. By combining power-to-X and CO2 conversion technologies, a 
closed loop could benefit green electricity production and significantly reduce CO2 
emissions [3]. Implementation of large-scale storage can directly support the penetration of 
fluctuating renewable energy sources into the energy grid by stabilizing their production [4].  
 
One strategy for improving the reliability of sustainable energy is to utilize green 
hydrogen produced from water electrolysis. However, hydrogen storage and transportation 
are complex and require additional costly measures. The combination of power-to-X and 
CO2 offers the opportunity to utilize a different energy carrier than hydrogen. Methanol is 
generally considered a good candidate thanks to its ease of storage and transport, balancing 
out the extra costs for its production [5]. While current large-scale production mainly relies 
on steam reforming of natural gas [6], more sustainable synthesis methods, such as CO2 
hydrogenation, have been developed [3]. The latter process can be part of a closed CO2 loop, 
taking advantage of captured CO2 and green hydrogen, to produce methanol. The produced 
alcohol is easily stored and transported for reuse as a chemical, fuel or for electricity 
production. Methanol could be directly fed into fuel cells to generate electricity, thus making 
it a complete energy carrier. High-temperature solid oxide fuel cells (SOFCs), known for their 
==PAGE:6==
Page 5 of 36 
fuel flexibility [7], can utilize this alcohol directly, without pre-reforming, as a fuel to produce 
electricity efficiently. Implementation of methanol in energy system layouts has already 
begun but still leads to uncertainties due to assumptions about the SOFC behaviour. A better 
understanding of its internal functioning would be beneficial for refining these system 
designs into more robust and precise concepts. 
 
The study focuses on the development of a SOFC model fuelled with methanol. It first 
goes through a Literature Review to understand the key concepts and challenges of the 
technology. The Methods section presents the governing equations and the assumptions 
made when developing the model. Lastly, the main Results are presented and compared to 
a reference case, followed by a brief Discussion of the model assumptions and potential 
future work.

2. Literature Review                      
 
2.1. 
Solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) concept and modelling 
 
 
Figure 1- SOFC scheme fuelled with hydrogen 
 
A SOFC is an electrochemical device designed to convert chemical energy into 
electricity. Its primary components include the anode, electrolyte, cathode, and 
interconnects. In the case of SOFCs, the electrodes and the electrolyte are made of 
ceramics capable of withstanding high temperatures, 650-950°C [8]. This is generally 
advantageous as it enhances the kinetics of the electrochemical reactions (1) and (2), the 
key processes of the fuel cell. Figure 1 shows a simple representation of the reference 
system with hydrogen supply on the anode and oxygen on the cathode.  Hydrogen reacts with 
oxide ions coming from the electrolyte to produce water and electrons. These charges are 
then transferred to the interconnect, a bipolar plate located under the anode, thanks to the 
electrode being electrically conductive. On the cathode side, oxygen is reduced to oxide ions 
that travel through the electrolyte to react with hydrogen in the anode and complete the loop.

High temperatures can pose limitations on the maximum operational voltage of the fuel 
cells, making room temperature alternatives preferable depending on the application [10]. 
SOFCs still remain valuable thanks to their fuel flexibility [7], allowing for direct feeding of 
other fuels inside the stack. High temperatures favoured thermal decomposition into 
smaller gas molecules like hydrogen that can trigger the electrochemical reactions of the 
SOFCs. Thus, alternative fuels, such as methane, ammonia or methanol, which can be more 
convenient than hydrogen for some energy system layouts, can be used. 
 Methanol is liquid and non-toxic making it a very good candidate for substituting 
hydrogen and solving its inherent issues related to storage and transport. High temperature 
fuel cells can utilize methanol for electricity production at a large scale. While Direct 
Methanol Fuel Cells (DMFCs) enable this at room temperature, their low power density 
significantly restricts their applications [11]. In contrast, direct methanol SOFCs can achieve 
much higher power output. Q. Xu et al [12] established a model of an SOFC producing a 
power density of 1.2 W.cm-2 at 1073K, superior to typical DMFCs with less than 0.1 W.cm-2 
power output. 
When developing a model of an SOFC, various physics domains and equations should 
be considered including mass, species, momentum, heat, and charge transport equations 
[13]. The flow distribution inside the stack is also defined taking into account the geometry 
of the local domain and the microstructure [12] [13]. Introducing methanol directly into the 
SOFC triggers additional reactions within the active domain, also necessitating the 
development of a chemical model [12].

2.3. 
Degradation and carbon deposition problem 
 
An issue that can stem from the introduction of hydrocarbon fuels inside the SOFC 
stacks is carbon deposition. This is particularly problematic when methane is used as a fuel, 
as it significantly impacts the performance stability of the cell and deactivates the catalytic 
activity of the anode [16]. When developing their model, Q. Xu et al [12] decided to neglect 
this issue, arguing that it is less prevalent with methanol since the oxygen atom present in 
the molecule does not favour carbon deposition on the anode. However, Y. Ru et al. [15] and 
J. Sang et al. [14] conducted experiments to directly address this issue in their designs of 
SOFC stacks, acknowledging that it should also be a significant challenge with methanol. In 
their investigation, the flat-tube SOFC with a double-sided cathode of Y. Ru et al. [15] 
degraded rapidly when solely fuelled by raw methanol, with complete deactivation of the 
anode occurring after 11 minutes of operation.  
Carbon deposition could occur through several pathways, all of which contribute to 
anode degradation. When methane is introduced into the SOFC, it can undergo direct 
==PAGE:10==
Page 9 of 36 
decomposition in the anode (10) due to high temperatures, being the primary cause of 
carbon decomposition in SOFCs fuelled with methane [16]. While this is not a direct reaction 
in methanol-fuelled SOFCs, it can still occur due to the methanation reaction (7) within the 
active regions of the anode. Y. Ru et al. [15] identified this pathway as the primary cause of 
carbon deposition in their experiments, particularly because of low methane content when 
degradation did not occur. Other pathways, as outlined by J. Sang et al. [14] with reactions 
(8) and (9), can also contribute to carbon deposition. The authors further investigated the 
mechanism of methanol reforming on the nickel catalytic surface and showed that a non-
favourable pathway could result in some carbon formation. While most of these pathways 
are thermodynamically unfavourable, they can still occur occasionally, leading to an 
accumulation of deposited carbon and deactivation of the active anode.

Various solutions are currently under investigation to mitigate this carbon deposition 
issue. Most SOFCs have a Ni/YSZ-based anode-supported structure, which is highly 
vulnerable to carbon deposition. One approach is to change the anode material by using 
noble metals, as demonstrated by Jung et al. [17], who inserted Cu particles into the Ni/YSZ 
anode, thereby reducing carbon formation. While many solutions related to anode structure 
have succeeded in limiting carbon deposition effectively, none have become commercially 
viable due to their high costs or low catalytic activity, leaving the basic Ni/YSZ-based anode-
supported structure as the preferred option. 
Other solutions suggested by Y. Ru et al. [15] and J. Sang et al. [14] involve modifying the 
fuel composition. Y. Ru et al. [15] investigated the durability of SOFCs when methanol was 
mixed with steam water. In addition to thermal decomposition, methanol also underwent 
humidified internal reforming (3), producing hydrogen and carbon dioxide. The authors 
conducted experiments with different steam-to-methanol ratios and compared the end-of-
life and performance of the SOFC stacks. They found that while SOFCs fuelled by only 
methanol failed after 11 minutes of operation, those fuelled with methanol mixed with steam 
water could run for longer, up to 36 hours (steam-to-methanol ratio of 1) and 120 hours 
(steam-to-methanol ratio of 2) where they stopped the experiment. In the latter experiment, 
the anode interestingly did not exhibit any carbon deposition when observed by Scanning 
Electron Microscope (SEM).  
The second investigation, conducted by J. Sang et al. [14], focused on the addition of 
carbon dioxide to the methanol fuel. Besides its thermal decomposition, methanol was also 
internally reformed by CO2 (2) producing hydrogen, carbon monoxide, and water. The authors 
made experiments for different CO2/MeOH ratios to observe the impact on the SOFC 
performance and durability. They observed better durability for cells fuelled with CO2/MeOH 
==PAGE:11==
Page 10 of 36 
ratios of 1 and 2 as they did not fail even after 500 hours of operation. However, when fuelled 
with a ratio of 0.5, the same SOFC stack failed rapidly after 20 hours of operation. When 
studying the mechanism of the methanol dehydrogenation on nickel particles, the authors 
noted that the addition of CO2 to the fuel mixture supported pathways that remove carbons 
from the nickel particles, thus directly mitigating the carbon deposition problem. While CO2 
is effective in reducing carbon formation, it also decreases the cell’s operational voltage 
[14]. Other degradation issues were also observed at high CO2/MeOH ratios, such as a loss 
of percolation of nickel particles, known as Ni coarsening [13], leading to the conclusion that 
an optimal fuel composition should be identified [14],[18]. 
 
2.4. 
Objectives of the work 
 
The literature review provided a better understanding of the concept and challenges 
of using methanol as a fuel in an SOFC, highlighting carbon deposition as a significant issue 
associated with hydrocarbon fuels.  
 
The main objective is to study the internal operations of a methanol-fuelled SOFC by 
building a simple model that accounts for relevant physics and chemical reactions. The 
focus will be on the comparison and understanding of the difference in functioning between 
a hydrogen-fuelled SOFC and a methanol-fuelled SOFC. 
 
2.5. 
Novelty of the work 
 
The study proposes a model of a methanol-fuelled SOFC, comparing its results to a 
reference SOFC fed with hydrogen. The performance differences are analysed and described 
to better understand the SOFC’s behaviour when fed with methanol. Carbon deposition is 
also evaluated to assess its significance and influencing factors. 
These findings can help refine assumptions for energy systems implementing such 
technologies in their layouts, ensuring that potential failures, such as carbon deposition, are 
avoided. 
 
3. Methods 
 
To better understand the internal functioning of a methanol-fuelled SOFC, a model is 
built on the software COMSOL Multiphysics considering relevant physics and reasonable 
computing load.  
 
3.1. 
Model description 
 
3.1.1. Cell design 
 
 
The study is based on a 2D model of an anode-supported cell, as shown in Figure 3. 
The design and reference model used in this study are provided by DTU and are detailed in 
==PAGE:12==
Page 11 of 36 
[19]. The fuel - whether hydrogen, methanol or pre-cracked methanol depending on the case 
- enters the cell via the fuel channel. Due to the porous structure of the anode and support, 
the gases diffuse to the fuel electrode, initiating the electrochemical reactions. Since the 
electrolyte is a non-porous material, the products and unreacted fuels exit the cell through 
the fuel channel outlet. A similar process occurs with the air entering the air channel, where 
oxygen diffuses to the cathode and is reduced to oxide ions. 
 
In this model, both the fuel and air channels are considered porous structures made 
of electronically conductive material with high porosity- nickel for the fuel channel and gold 
for the air channel.  
The role of the support is to enhance the mechanical stability of the cell, resulting in 
an increase in the thickness of one component, which in this case is the anode. The support 
has a structure very similar to the anode, being a 300 m thick, porous composite material 
of Ni-3YSZ. In comparison, the anode is a 25 m thick, porous composite material of Ni-
8YSZ.  
The barrier layer is a very thin zone of a dense material with high ionic conductivity 
(CGO). It is used to avoid undesirable reactions between the cathode material and the 
electrolyte.  
All these layers are considered in the COMSOL model, with their microstructure and 
parameters accurately represented.

over time. The fuel composition includes five species: hydrogen (H2), water (H2O), methanol

The decomposition of methanol through internal reforming, dry (4) or humidified (5), 
is also excluded from the study. These reactions would be relevant if a significant quantity of 
CO2 or H2O was added to the fuel mixture. Since this is not the case in the current study, they

the chemical modelling of these reactions over a Ni-YSZ anode, which further justifies their 
exclusion. Therefore, only methanol decomposition (3) is considered among possible 
reactions involving methanol. 
Carbon deposition is not fully implemented in the study. Only the Boudouard reaction

water gas shift reaction (7) is implemented, as it is an important reaction that can produce 
additional hydrogen.

3.2.3. Methanol  
 
Since methanol decomposition produces 2 moles of hydrogen for 1 mole of methanol 
consumed, the SOFC is operated with a standard volume flow rate of 12Nl/h of methanol, 
allowing for a realistic comparison with the hydrogen-fuelled SOFC. Current-voltage and 
power curves are made for the same four temperatures used in the validation step.  
The fuel composition must be adjusted to account for the 4% of H2O in the mixture. 
Without this adjustment, the open circuit voltage (OCV), or the voltage at 0 A/cm2, would be 
excessively high due to the absence of water and carbon dioxide. This effect stems from the 
Nernst potential equation (17). Therefore, a fuel composition of 88% methanol, 8% water 
and 4% carbon monoxide is used to get a similar ratio between oxidant and reducing agent 
partial pressures as in the reference case, assuming complete methanol decomposes inside 
the SOFC. If this assumption is not verified, the fuel composition may need to be adjusted 
for a more accurate comparison. 
 
3.2.4. Pre-cracked methanol 
 
1 Nl/h stands for normal litre per hours, a measure of the flow rate of a gas at Standard Temperature and 
Pressure (STP), specifically 273.15 K and 1 atm. This is useful because it can be treated as a molar flow rate 
that is independent of temperature and pressure.

Pre-cracked methanol refers to the fuel mixture where methanol is fully decomposed 
before entering the SOFC. The objective is to understand the difference between cracking

produces two moles of hydrogen and one mole of carbon monoxide for every mole of 
methanol consumed, resulting in an inlet composition of 66.7% H2 and 33.3% CO and 
standard volume flow rate of 36 Nl/h.

density is observed due to their higher oxidation rates, resulting in the production of more 
H2O and CO2. This behaviour confirms the observation made at 0.5 A/cm2 in section 4.2.2, 
as it is the combination of slower methanol decomposition and a significant oxidation rate 
that stabilises the oxidants' mole fraction. The hydrogen mole fraction even decreases at 1.4 
A/cm2 due to the oxidation rate surpassing the methanol cracking rate. 
Figure 13 illustrates that more methanol is converted with increasing current density. 
However, when compared to the drop in oxidants’ mole fractions (CO and H2) from Figure 12, 
the gain in methanol converted appears negligible. Therefore, at higher current densities, the 
average gas composition in the SOFC contains more H2O and CO2, less H2 and CO, and a 
similar concentration of methanol. This directly leads to a decrease in the Nernst potential, 
as defined in section 3.1.4, equation 17. In the same section, the cell voltage is related to the 
Nernst potential through equation 15. Assuming the activation overvoltage is negligible, 
which is generally true at high current density, it follows that a decrease in the Nernst 
potential results in a drop in the cell voltage, as shown on the right of Figure 13. The 
difference between the two variables is mainly due to the electrolyte potential which 
accounts for the ohmic losses of the cell. The electrolyte potential, however, remains the 
same whether hydrogen or methanol is fed to the SOFC, meaning the increased deviation is 
due to the drop in the Nernst potential. While the Nernst potential also decreases in the 
hydrogen-fuelled SOFC, the drop is less significant than that observed in the methanol-
fuelled SOFC. The unconverted methanol fraction may be the reason for this difference 
because it results in a lower concentration of oxidants within the cell. The next section will 
investigate the performance of the cell when pre-cracked methanol is used as a fuel, 
allowing validation of this hypothesis.

4.3. 
Pre-cracked methanol 
 
The current-voltage and power density curves using pre-cracked methanol as a fuel 
can be observed in Figure 14. It is evident that these curves almost perfectly match those of 
the reference SOFC fuelled with hydrogen. This clearly indicated that internal methanol 
cracking reduces the performance of the SOFC at high current densities. The presence of 
unconverted methanol is therefore the primary cause of performance decrease, as it results 
in a greater drop in the Nernst potential at higher current densities.    
The deviation at very high current density is not because using pre-cracked methanol 
is more efficient than hydrogen, but rather due to the higher volume flow rate. The standard 
volume flow rate was set at 36 Nl/h to match the molar flow rate of hydrogen entering the 
channel in the hydrogen-fed reference case. This higher flow rate increases the gas velocity 
inside the domain, reducing the time the molecules spend in the active areas. Thus, it 
maintains a higher ratio of oxidant to reducing agent mole fractions, minimizing the drop in 
the Nernst potential with increasing current density. Figure 15 confirms this hypothesis, 
showing a very close match with 24Nl/h of fuel.

4.4. 
Boudouard reaction 
 
When producing these results, carbon formation from the Boudouard reaction was 
evaluated. The simulations indicated that this reaction was not negligible, and that carbon 
could form depending on the operating conditions. First, the equilibrium constant defined in 
section 3.4 decreases exponentially with increasing temperature, reaching approximately 1 
atm-1 at 700°C. Therefore, carbon formation is favoured at lower temperatures. Carbon 
deposition is also often observed at very low current densities, due to the low ratio of carbon 
dioxide to carbon monoxide concentrations. The operating conditions that led to carbon 
deposition via the Boudouard reaction are listed Table 2. Carbon formation could occur 
==PAGE:30==
Page 29 of 36 
throughout the entire active area (support and electrode) like at 700°C, or in very specific 
areas, such as the right corner of the active area at 750°C and low current density. 
It is also notable that when using pre-cracked methanol, carbon deposition occurs 
under almost every operating condition, except at 850°C and high current densities. This 
suggests that rapid decomposition of methanol inside the cell promotes the Boudouard 
reaction, leading to carbon formation in the SOFC.  
Finally, fuel composition also plays an important role in this reaction. Adding more 
carbon dioxide to the fuel mixture results in a reduction of the carbon formation. This will be 
further discussed in the next section.

5. Discussion 
 
The Results section concluded on the origin of the deviation observed in the 
performance when methanol was fed instead of hydrogen. It also highlighted the importance 
of carbon deposition and the need for careful selection of operating conditions. These 
conclusions were drawn based on several assumptions outlined in the Methods section, 
introducing uncertainties.  
Validation was conducted using experimental data from a hydrogen-fuelled SOFC. 
Therefore, it cannot fully validate the assumptions regarding the implementation of 
methanol, carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide in the model. This approach was necessary 
due to the lack of available experimental data for methanol-fuelled SOFCs and short time 
constraints preventing the preparation of such experiments. Additionally, the developed 
model is a simplified 2D representation that does not capture all the complexity of a real 
SOFC.  
The results still provided valuable predictions regarding the behaviour and the 
performance of the SOFC fed with methanol instead of hydrogen. Real experimental data 
could help eliminate these uncertainties and significantly enhance the validity of the 
conclusions made. 
 
5.1. 
Thermal impacts 
 
 
==PAGE:31==
Page 30 of 36 
Figure 11 demonstrated that the cell is both internally cooled by methanol 
decomposition and heated by electrochemical oxidations. These concurrent effects not only 
cause temperature variations, but also generate opposing thermal stresses. Contraction 
strains are induced by cooling, which are directly opposed to expansion strains caused by 
heating. Therefore, on the left side of the domain, where methanol decomposition primarily 
occurs, the material contracts, while it simultaneously expands on the right side. Depending 
on the constraints, Young’s modulus and thermal expansion coefficients, this could 
potentially lead to mechanical failures within the cell. Additionally, these internal stresses 
could lead to a shortened SOFC lifetime, due to thermal fatigue. 
 
5.2. 
Carbon dioxide in the fuel mixture 
 
As presented in section 4.4, incorporating carbon dioxide into the fuel composition 
helps avoid carbon formation within the active areas of the cell. This was investigated by J. 
Sang et al. [14] and previously discussed in section 2.3 of the literature review. However, the 
results of this study also emphasize that adding species that are not reacting in the main 
electrochemical reactions could cause a degradation in the performance. It was clearly 
demonstrated that the performance discrepancies between hydrogen-fuelled and 
methanol-fuelled SOFCs are due to the presence of unconverted methanol. Therefore, 
adding CO2 to the fuel mixture could potentially lower the cell voltage by inhibiting CO 
oxidation.  
To accurately model the effects of adding CO2 to the fuel composition, it would be 
necessary to include the internal dry reforming of methanol, (4) which was neglected in this 
study, among the possible cracking reactions. This reaction could enhance methanol 
conversion and, more importantly, prevent the performance degradation predicted by the 
model if CO2 was introduced. 
 
5.3. 
Energy system modelling and recirculation 
 
5.3.1. Energy system modelling 
 
One purpose of this study was to develop a model for a methanol-fuelled SOFC that 
could be used to refine assumptions made in energy system designs involving such 
technology. To fully achieve this goal, further work on modelling a complete SOFC stack is 
still necessary. However, power density curves and methanol conversion ratios developed 
in this study are valuable results that can be used for integrating this technology in energy 
system models.  
It is also important to note that this study was based on experimental data, which 
differs significantly from real-world operations. In the experiments, 24 Nl/h of hydrogen was 
fed to the SOFC, leading to a very low fuel utilisation, approximately 12% at 0.6 A/cm2 and 
700°C. In energy system modelling, the goal is to maximise fuel utilisation to enhance the 
efficiency of the system. Therefore, an investigation on the maximisation of the methanol 
conversion ratio would be necessary to implement the results into energy system designs. 
==PAGE:32==
Page 31 of 36 
To increase this conversion parameter, the volume flow rate would need to be lowered. 
However, such a decrease would result in a degradation of the cell performance at high 
current densities, as shown in section 4.3. An optimal value volume flow rate must be 
determined to maximise the efficiency of the system, depending on the operating 
conditions. 
 
5.3.2. Recirculation 
 
In the context of energy systems and maximising the fuel utilisation, the 
implementation of a recirculation loop could be considered. It is particularly relevant in this 
case as full conversion of methanol inside the SOFC appears difficult, due to the slow 
decomposition observed on the right side of the cell. Such loops would take some of the 
exhaust gases and mix them with the inlet gases. This approach would allow the inlet flow 
rate of new methanol to be reduced by replacing some of it with unconverted methanol from 
the exhaust gases. While this might degrade the performance of the cell due to the addition 
of undesired gases such as CO2 and H2O, it would be highly beneficial for maximising system 
efficiency and fuel conversion. 
 
6. Conclusion 
 
To explore the use methanol as an energy carrier for electricity production, a 
methanol-fuelled SOFC model was proposed. A literature review was first conducted to 
understand and explain the main challenges associated with this study. It emphasised key 
processes such as methanol decomposition, water-gas shift reaction, and electrochemical 
reactions. Additionally, it highlighted that carbon deposition is a significant challenge when 
using hydrocarbon fuel in SOFCs, as it permanently degrades the cell performance and can 
lead to its failure. 
 
A base model of a hydrogen-fuelled SOFC on the COMSOL software, provided by DTU, 
was used as a reference to implement methanol and new relevant processes. The model 
features several physics with the transport of species, charges, mass, heat and flow 
calculations all being implemented. A chemical model for methanol decomposition and the 
water gas shift reaction was developed based on published studies.  
 
The model was validated using data from an experiment of an SOFC fed with 
hydrogen, which served as a reference case for subsequent simulations. The SOFC was then 
simulated with a concentration and flow rate of methanol equivalent to the reference. A 
deviation in the performance of the SOFC was demonstrated by comparing the I-V curves 
with the reference case. 
 
Analysis of the internal functioning of the SOFC showed that methanol 
decomposition primarily occurs within a one-centimetre distance from the inlet. This is 
followed by a slowdown in the cracking rate, leaving a non-negligible fraction of unconverted 
methanol in the exhaust gases. This partial conversion is the main cause of the deviation in 
the performance observed in the I-V curves, which increases with higher current density. This 
==PAGE:33==
Page 32 of 36 
hypothesis was confirmed through simulation of the SOFC with pre-cracked methanol, 
which almost completely eliminated the previously observed deviations.  
 
Carbon deposition was evaluated through the Boudouard reaction, demonstrating 
that carbon could form within the active areas of the SOFC depending on the operating 
conditions. The reaction was thermodynamically favoured at low temperatures and low 
current densities.  It is therefore important to carefully select operating conditions to avoid 
carbon formation.  
Lastly, several relevant limitations and challenges of the study were discussed, 
providing perspective for future work. The validation of the model through experiments using 
a SOFC fuelled with methanol could eliminate the main uncertainties introduced by the 
model, which would significantly benefit the model.
