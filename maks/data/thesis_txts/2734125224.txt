Abstract
Climate change caused and will continue to cause more extreme precipitation in Denmark.
This will, in combination with increasing sea surface levels, increase the likelihood of
floods in Denmark. This work aims to contribute to climate adaptation and mitigation
by developing a model to surveil surface water.
This thesis utilises remote sensing data from the Copernicus Sentinel 1 satellite and deep
learning methods – comprising of a UNet and a probabilistic Bayesian UNet adaptation
– to build and present a model capable of mapping surface water.
With its usage of
high–resolution Denmark–specific ground truth and digital terrain model data, this work
pioneers mapping surface water to as small extent as possible, quantifying a minimum
required surface area, given the current model.
The model is applied to three sites of the recent October 2023 Baltic Sea flood, showing
good results. The minimum surface water size was found to be around 50 pixel translating
to 0.5ha. This study argues, however, that the limit could be improved by alleviating
discussed ground truth shortcomings. Furthermore, this work outlines the need for high–
quality flood and rain pond maps for Denmark specifically.

2.1
Difference between deterministic (”ordinary”) CNNs to probabilistic BCNNs.
CNN weights are fixed whereas BCNN weights are sampled from a normal
distribution (a). Therefore, model weights are different for every inference
pass, (b) visiualised by varying connection thicknesses. Thus, the result is
also normal distributed for BCNNs and not a singular value like for CNNs
(c). Figure from [1].

2.4
Definitions of uncertainty in Bayesian Neural Networks. Definition A (row
2) only used the spread of the raw Sigmoid output. Definition B (row 3)
uses both position and spread.
The pixel is classified to the class with
more samples, delineated by the median. The uncertainty is therefore the
percentage of Sigmoid outputs in the other class. Here the non water pixel
has a uncertainty of 0.018, the uncertain pixel of 0.437 and the water pixel
of 0.009. The uncertainty is ∈[0, 0.5]. Adapted from visualisation of [1]

Introduction
The global emission of greenhouse gasses have been responsible and are prognosed to cause
significant changes to Denmark’s climate. Since the 1870s, the average temperature in
Denmark has increased by 1.5◦C and with it, the annual precipitation by 20% – especially
in winter [6]. Besides, precipitation alongside higher temperatures is forecasted to increase
until 2100. Especially in winters where a 10-25% increase in precipitation is forecasted
compared to the 1980s-2010s average. Precipitation averages in summers will most likely
remain on similar to today’s levels, yet with highly variable patterns making dry periods
and floods more likely [6, 7]. The sea surface level will continue to rise because of melting
glaciers, which, in combination with more frequent storm floods, will cause more damage.
Storm surges, which are statistically likely to occur every 20 years today, are forecasted to
occur every one to two years by the year 2100, if the global mean temperature increases
by 3.4◦C in comparison to the pre–industrial period [7].
Over the past decades, demand for robust and reliable crisis information has increased
substantially [8]. With more crises bound to happen, the demand for more high–quality
information will grow.
Both for mitigation as well as adaptation.
Earth observation
satellites such as Copernicus Sentinel 1 (S1) and Copernicus Sentinel 2 (S2) satellites from
the European Space Agency (ESA) have gained increasing popularity for crisis response
mapping. Earth observation satellites are especially suited for the task with their wide
selection of sensors, large spacial coverage, and high temporal frequency [9]. Over the past
century, about one third of all recorded natural disasters were floods [10]. Mapping floods
manually is a time–consuming process, prone to individual annotator biases [9]. Thus,
researchers focus on machine learning and AI–based approaches, for example compiled in
[11].
With the prospect of the drastically increasing likelihood of more and more destructive
floods in Denmark in the future, this work aims to contribute to both flood mitigation and
adaptation. Already today, heavy precipitation events cause rivers in Denmark to swell
and flood cities downstream, such as in Holstebro in December 2015 [12, 13]. Such floods
could potentially be mitigated with more knowledge of the surrounding rivers’ drainage
systems’ dynamics, how and where rainwater accumulates, and how fast it feeds into the
river system itself.
This work introduces a UNet based convolutional neural network to enable rapid flood
extend mapping for crisis response measures in Denmark. Furthermore, the network can
be used to evaluate a minimum surface water size for mapping small surface waters at
which eg. rain ponds can still reliably be mapped. For training, it uses Denmark-specific
high-resolution ground truth data. As model input, the model uses S1 Synthetic Apper-
ture Radar (SAR) data alongside high-resolution Danish Digital Terrain Model (DTM)
information. Furthermore, besides the traditional convolutional neural network, a proba-
bilistic adaptation inspired by [1] is introduced. Probabilistic models intrinsically quantify
prediction uncertainties which should provide important additional information in highly
volatile crises. Enhanced versions of this work’s developed models can hopefully contribute
to accurate rain pond mapping, enabling engineers and municipalities to take mitigating
and adapting actions. Either by expanding river drainage system capacities or slowing
down flow in key areas, spreading water load to stay under critical levels.

1.1
Problem Questions
By the end of this work, the following problem questions are answered. They are postulated
for the special application field in Denmark.
1. Scale
On a global scale, Denmark is a small country with even smaller waterways and lakes.
While Denmark’s DTM has a very high spatial pixel resolution of 0.4m, S1’s is ”only”
10m. Therefore, what is the smallest surface area of a water pond that the developed
models can still reliably identify as surface water? Is the resolution boundary small
enough to map rainwater ponds using S1 data – or is higher resolution satellite data
needed?
2. Ground Truth Data
Which data source for training ground truth is most suitable with regards to mapping
temporary surface water, and mapping small–scale surface water? Is it suﬀicient to
train on images of ”normal” water conditions to predict temporary surface water, or
is it necessary to have labelled flood training data? Furthermore, do labelled flood
masks and/or labelled rain pond masks exist specifically for Denmark?
3. Scalability & Transferability
Developed models are trained and evaluated on defined regions of interest, not on all
of Denmark. Does the application have the potential to be used nationwide? How
scalable is the final solution, which monetary and computational costs are associated,
and which implementation effort would it take? If it can be scaled to all of Denmark,
could it be used on other countries than Denmark, especially concerning the used
DTM.
4. Inference Speed
How fast is the model able to produce inference results? Would it be fast enough
for a potential crisis?
5. Input Data
With which frequency is the DTM updated and how often are new SAR images
taken? Is the temporal resolution suﬀicient to ensure that most flood and heavy
precipitation events can be monitored?

2.1.2
Sentinel 1 SAR Approaches
Using SAR over optical images in water mapping offers one significant advantage. SAR
is an active sensor, whose electromagnetic spectrum is not blocked or obscured by clouds.
SAR can therefore sense water all day and night, regardless of weather conditions, which
is especially beneficial with regards to floods and heavy precipitation events. Water pro-
duces low back scatter intensity in SAR imagery (usually represented by dark or black
pixel). This can be explained by the acquisition geometry. The vertical horizontal (VH)
polarisation is preferable for flood, shallow water and swamp mapping [21]. With regards
to CNNs VH or VH+vertical vertical (VV) combined are the preferred inputs for flood
mapping [22, 23, 9].
Guo et al. compile the past, current and future trends of SAR based water segmentation
in their review [11]. Traditionally, mostly classic Machine Learning (ML) methods were
applied. Namely edge detection, level set methods, active contour models, clustering, ran-
dom forest, support vector machines, Markov random fields and mathematical statistical
algorithms. Overall, they state: ”ML methods always try to formulate some kind of map-
ping relationship to determine the category of a pixel, which leads to a lack of flexibility and
intelligence. For SAR images with a lot of speckle noise, it is diﬀicult for these mappings
to fit all pixels in the entire SAR image” [11]. Recently, DL methods have overcome afore
mentioned limitations and are presented in section 2.3.
2.2
What is Deep Learning?
Deep learning is a sub set of machine learning, usually a neural network with multiple
layers [24]. Neural networks are networks of artificial neurons that are interconnected
with the aim of emulating the (human) brain and to learn from large data sets. Neurons
are usually arranged in layers that connect to their neighbouring layers.
Neural networks can have a multitude of different architectures, designed to perform well in
different application field. For image classification, one of the most relevant architectures
is the AlexNet architecture. It was introduced by Alex Krizhevsky et al. [25] in 2012 and
is one of the most influential DL architectures overal. It spurred research in the genral
DL field, with over 120000 cites on Google scholar.
In other application fields, other
architectures are used. In segmentation, the application performed in this work, the so
called UNet architecture has proven especially usefull. It was introduced by Ronneberger
et al. [26] in 2015 and gets its name from its architecture’s ”U” shape. It gets it shape
from an encoder stage (contracting the information) and an decoder stage (expanding
the information). Both stages comprise of multiple steps, whereas each encoder step is
connected to their respective decoder step by passing forward its information, skipping
the rest of the network. Both skipped and full network information are combined, before
it is inputted to the decoder step. A diagram of the UNet architecture used in this work
is shown in Figure 3.10.

2.3.1
SAR Specific Limitations
The use of SAR over optical images in DL does not only bring the benefit of being inde-
pendent of cloud cover, but poses several specific challenges. The most prominent ones
are listed below.
• Speckle Noise
SAR imagery inherently contains speckle noise.
Speckle is multiplicative rather
than additive noise. Some studies try to alleviate by logarithmically mapping the
SAR images prior to feeding them into the CNN – making speckle a random additive
variable [28]. Zhu et al. furthermore suggests to experiment with additionally filtered
SAR images as secondary inputs to the model [28].
• Acquisition Geometry
SAR image coordinates range and azimuth are not arbitrary, but dependant on the
image taking process. Hence, rotating a SAR image would produce an unrealistic
image [28]. A deeper discussion on data augmentation is presented in section 3.5.
• Satellite Orbit
The satellite’s orbit inherently changes the acquisition geometry.
Hence, model
performance could deteriorate if it does not get information about ascending vs
descending orbit [29].
• Dynamic Range
The Dynamic range of SAR imagery can be extremely large with a very asymmetric
distribution with most pixel being in the low amplitude range. CNNs are usually
not able to handle such dynamic ranges [28]. Therefore, preprocessing is necessary.
Zhu et al. [28] mention different approaches.
• Data Labelling
The acquisition of large–scale SAR imagery is relatively diﬀicult, given the condition
and constraints present.
As previously described, SAR imagery is vulnerable to
speckle noise and sensitive to the acquisition geometry, which makes it challenging
to accurately label surface water (for training purposes) [11].
• Local Features
Hertel et al. [1] outline sources for over and under estimation of flood extend based
on SAR. Water–look–alikes such as smooth surfaces and radar shadows can cause
an overestimation, while roughened surface water through wind and the interaction
of flood water with vegetation can lead to under estimation of flood surface water.
2.3.2
Supplementing SAR with a DTM
Given the study’s question of detecting small / temporary surface water bodies and the
physical certainty that surface water must collect in local minima of the topography – it
is obvious to supplement the SAR with DTM or Digital Elevation Model (DEM) data.
This approach is not novel. For instance Wieland et al. [30] finds increased accuracy when
adding an independent DEM. In their novel dataset [31], they supplement SAR with both
elevation and slope derived from a DEM. Tavus et al. [27] recommends using a DEM for

5
==PAGE:26==
rugged terrain. Casper Fibaek [29] recommended using normalised slope, aspect cosine
norm, aspect sine norm, and height (normalised to the peak in the area of interest) derived
from DTM.
In some contexts, the terms DEM and DTM terms can be used interchangeably. If differ-
ences exist depends on the data source. This work understand the difference as: a DTM
only shows the terrains elevation, whereas a DEM can include vegetation, buildings, etc.
Global scale DEM/DTM products usually have low spacial resolution (the Copernicus
DEM has a resolution of 30m globally [32]). On such scales, differences are marginal.
This work however, will use the 0.4m resolution DTM of Styrelsen for Dataforsyning og
Infrastruktur (SDFI) [33]. Here substantial differences can exist. The DTM is chosen over
DEM as it represents the bare earth surface where the water will accumulate, and thus,
omitting potentially confusing information.
2.4
Detecting Small Water Bodies
In comparison to the overall research volume on water and flood mapping from S1 and S2
imagery, fewer studies can be found on mapping small scale water features. A selection is
presented below.
Soti et al. [34] study the relation between sensor resolution of different satellites and pond
size mapping in arid areas using NDWI and MNDWI. They find a quasi linear relation,
with the highest resolution sensors of Quickbird (2.45m), Ikonos (4m) and SPOT5 (10m)
probably capable of mapping ponds small as 70m2. The S2 satellites were not yet available
at the time of the study.
Pham–Duc et al. [35] train a neural network using S1 SAR data to map surface water.
The model was trained using Landsat–8 multispectral images (30m resolution). Comparing
inference results to Landsat–8 water masks, they find a ≈90% spacial and ≈99% true
positive correlation.
Chang Liu in his master thesis [36] works on mapping standing water in agricultural fields
in the Netherlands using S1. He uses backscatter changes on field scale in a S1 image
series. He finds his approach unsuitable for mapping small ponds on agricultural fields
but doesn’t quantify performance and furthermore notes that pond sizes found during field
work are very small compared to S1 resolution.

2.5.1
Bayesian Convolutional Neural Networks
Bayesian Convolutional Neural Network (BCNN) learn a distribution of weights instead of
a fixed weight value like ordinary CNNs. Hence, every inference pass will result in different
results, necessitating multiple passes. A simple sketch is provided in Figure 2.1, taken from
[1]. While the weights in the deterministic CNN remain fixed in time, the BCNN’s weights
are resampled every pass. Visualised by the varying connection thicknesses. The final
prediction is equally normal distributed, whereas the CNN’s output is a point value. For
a mathematical background and more descriptive information regarding BCNNs, please
refer to [39], [40], or to [41] with their PyTorch compatible Python package torchbnn1.

Squeeze and Excitation Block
The model will have up to 6 input channels (2 SAR and up to 4 DTM), potentially all

adaptive channel-wise feature response recalibration. They show, that models with SE
blocks generalise effectively and achieve higher test scores while simultaneously only adding
a slight computational cost. A sketch of the SE block is shown in Figure 2.2, containing a
pooling layer, Fully Connected Layer (FC), Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU), and a Sigmoid

the layer’s channels C and ratio r. The ratio is a hyper parameter to vary the complexity.
They find that r = 16 offers a good trade off between complexity and accuracy [2].

Model Stem for Multiple Data Sources
Adding a stem – a small addition to the front of the model – originates from the same
desire, to enable the model to best utilise SAR and DTM information. As suggested by
Casper Fibaek [29], instead of inserting a H × W × 3 or 6 tensor straight to the model, it
is split into H × W × 2 (SAR) and a H × W × 1 or 4 (DTM). Each respective input is fed

both ”roots” are fused together and fed to the ordinary UNet. The concept is visualised

2.6
Loss Functions for Model Training
Loss functions optimise certain model behaviour in the training process. Depending on
use–case and desired model functionality, appropriate loss functions that align with said
use–case and target behaviour have to be chosen. Helleis et al. [9] for instance investigate
using a linearly weighted combination of cross entropy loss and Lovász loss for S1 based
flood mapping. Binary cross entropy measures the dissimilarity between target and the
predicted label’s probabilities. It is widely used for classification problems. However, they
also want to promote a high Intersection over Union (IoU) of targets and predictions.
It in not only important that the same amount of water is predicted but also where
it is predicted. For that, they use a region-based loss functions (Lovász loss), directly
optimising IoU in model training. Their findings support, that ”combining distribution-
based and region-based loss functions is beneficial for many segmentation tasks”. Therefore,
this work will also combine these two types of loss function.
The used distribution based loss function binary cross entropy (with logits) loss is presented
in subsection 2.6.1, followed by the Lovász loss in subsection 2.6.2. Lastly, the BCNN
exclusive Kullback-Leibler divergence loss is presented in subsection 2.6.3. All losses are
linearly combined in subsection 2.6.4.
2.6.1

3.3.1
Sentinel Data from Google Earth Engine
The S1 and S2 data are accessed as is from Google Earth Engine (GEE). Only passes with
100% ROI coverage are taken, therefore a full overlap of S1 and S2 data is guaranteed.
Moreover, it is required for S2 to have a cloud cover of ≤5%.
Besides ensuring spacial overlap, temporal proximity has to be ensured as surface water
features are volatile, especially during or past strong precipitation events. Therefore, a
difference in acquisition time (∆t) of max. ≈1 day is set as a secondary requirement.
Thus, for each S2 image satisfying <5% cloud cover, it must be checked if a S1 image
withing ca. 1 day exists. If so, they are kept for training / testing. Lastly, the SAR image
is truncated with range [−35, 5]dB and then re–scaled to be within [0, 1]. An example of
the truncated and resealed SAR bands is shown in Figure 3.3.
3.3.2
Digital Terrain Model
The DTM is intended to be used as a secondary input to the model besides the S1 images.
It can be accessed freely under [33] and comes in blocks of 10x10km with a pixel resolution
of 0.4x0.4m. Choose for each ROI the correct corresponding DTM blocks, ensure full
coverage. The excess DTM will be removed in processing.
Before the DTM can be used as input, it has to be pre–processed. All individual blocks are
combined to one, re–scaled to S1 resolution of 10x10m/pixel , align with and mask by the
acquired S1 data and lastly saved to file. To do so, use the Geospatial Data Abstraction
Library (gdal) and a Geographic Information System (GIS) program (this work used
QGis). The step by step guide is documented in the appendix in subsubsection A.1.8.1.

Water Masks for Training Ground Truth
Data quality is crucial for final model performance, especially labelled Ground Truth
(GT) training data. This work’s GT data is a binary surface water mask. This section
describes the synthesis of both used mask.
First, using S2 imagery to create a water

3.4.1
Automatic Approach: using thresholded S2 water indices
As discussed in section 2.1, combining different S2 bands to an index can identify surface

and S2 of max. ≈1 days is small enough to not introduce significant errors to the model.

have an effective 20×20 resolution, see Table 2.1. Figure 3.5 shows all three water indices,

3.4.2
Semi-Manual Approach: using available static masks
To be independent of clouds and to benefit from human quality control and high de-
tail resolution, another GT approach is taken. Here, different water masks from Danish
government agencies are combined. For an overview, refer to Table 3.1.
To make them compatible in type, shape, and alignment to the SAR data, they have to be
preprocessed. The basin, lakes and Land Geo Danmark data sources are vectors. Hence,
they are rasterised using QGis, cut to S1 image extend and aligned with the pixel in the
S1 image. The land mask is inverted in Python to mask the ocean. A step by step guide
is presented in the appendix in subsubsection A.1.8.2. They are further postprocessed,
which is described in subsubsection 3.4.2.2.
The river dataset consists of strings, outlining river edges. Therefore, it is converted to
polygons (vector data) and then processed the same as the basin, lakes and land data.
Some rivers contained in the river mask are too small to be represented by any model.
They can have cross–sections << 10m hence are also not visible in S1 imagery. Thus, to
exclude unrealistic river pixel, the mask is post processed with thresholds and Gaussian
kernel convolution throughout different steps. The step by step river processing guide is
shown in subsubsection A.1.8.3.

3.5
Data Augmentation, Patches & Test Split
To archive good results and to not over fit, CNNs require a large training data set. Which,
as established in the previous sections, is a time consuming task to collect and process.
Data augmentation, the aim of increasing the size and quality of the data set, is a ”data
space solution to the problem of limited data” [61]. It can improve the generalization ability
of the model, especially for small data sets [62].
The data in array form is cut into patches of e.g.
64x64pixels.
The used buteo [63]
function has a offset parameter, allowing an increase in patches. An visual example in
given in Figure A.11. While offset 0 is equivalent to a simple sliding window without
overlap, an offset of 1 about doubles the data set. The higher the offset parameter, the
higher the overlap.
Usually a value of 2 is chosen.
The patches are partitioned into
a training (80%) and a validation (20%) set and augmentations as shown in Table 3.3
are performed on the training part. The augmentation does not increase the number of
patches. The augmented patches replace the original patches.
Helleis et al. [9] uses 6 augmentations in their benchmark. Namely: left–right flip, ro-
tation, intensity scale, zoom, and gaussian as well as gamma distribution based speckle
augmentation (based on [64, 65] ideas). They find, that random flipping, rotation and
zooming lead to slightly worse test scores, confirming [28], that rotating a SAR image
causes unrealistic data, as the azimuth and range detection in SAR imagery is not ar-
bitrary. But they also note, that left–right flipping can be seen – to the firs order – as
a change from ascending to descending acquisition orbit. With regards to the speckle
augmentation, they find no significant difference between the gaussian and gamma distri-
bution based approaches, despite speckle being gamma distributed. Both augmentation
have a slight positive effect on test scores. Bai et al. [62] finds a minor positive impact for
left–right flip, rotation and image cropping in their study on a different data set. Further-
more, random flipping is applied in the S1S2 water data set [31] which is subsequently used
in [1] which inspired the chosen model architecture. Considering everything, geometric
data augmentation should be handled with care and their impact monitored.
With respect to more modern augmentation methods, Qosja et al.
[66] find, that for
their use case, Mixup has a large positive impact, Cutmix has no significant impact, while
RandAugment deteriorates the test score.
This work will make use of the afore discussed augmentations if they are implemented in
the python buteo package [63]. An overview is provided in Table 3.3.

4.4
Lake Statistics
Concerning problem question scale, it is important to know, how well models perform
wrt. water bodies surface area [pxl]. Therefore, a new measure will be introduced; the lake
statistic. It consists of 2 measures per model, the Ground Truth vs. Prediction (GTvP)
and Prediction vs. Ground Truth (PvGT).
GTvP clusters each individual lake in GT into bins based on their size in pixel – eg. ∈[4, 6).
Looping through each bin and lake by lake, compare each pixel to the corresponding
inference pixel. Thereby, GTvP forms a mean agreeance for each lake and then for each
bin. PvGT works analogously. It clusters each individual predicted water body into bins,
using the same loops to compare it to GT now. GTvP corresponds to false negatives and
thus to Recall while PvGT corresponds to FP and thus Precision. Both measures are
∈[0, 1] with 1 being the best.
Table 4.10 shows the results for the 3c: (**) and 6c: (*) model with stem and SE block
of section 4.3 on the whole Randers ROI. With visual results in the appendix Figure B.8.
The 6c model performs similarly bad for the smallest surface area bin than the 3c model.
However, overlap improves significantly faster in both scores. For the largest lakes, scores
have almost equalised again.

larger surface water areas improves when omitting lakes smaller than 30 pixel surface area

ure B.9. As expected, the overlap for smaller lakes is worse than for the models trained
on full GT data (Figure B.8). However, even the lakes with a surface area well above the
threshold of 30 pixel perform worse than before. Therefore, going forward, the whole GT
will be used.

4.7
The Final CNN model & Postprocessing
Considering results from all previous sections, this section presents the final ”ordinary”
CNN model. Given, that all 6c models were incapable of predicting temporary water,
3c was chosen for the final model. Furthermore, no combination in model parameters
convincingly outperformed the baseline model (**) in all regards. Thus, (**) was chosen

Post Processing based on SAR VH Quantiles
Most models in general and also (**) in particular produce ”noisy” predictions, containing
many FP, where the human eye does not see any water in the SAR input imagery. This
section describes a routine aimed to remove the most apparent false predictions. It is
based on apriori information, that water bodies have a low back scatter signal in SAR
imagery (subsection 2.1.2).
It works using the same approach as lake statistics, enumerating individual water bodies
in model predictions, and also getting array indices for all water pixel by individual body.

body. If the mean value is ≥than the quantile, the whole water body is discarded from
the prediction. A flow chart of the routine is shown in Figure 4.4a. Figure 4.4b shows the
histogram of VH values for the Randers ROI, while Figure 4.4c to 4.4f shown the before

Discussion of Results
This section will discuss and put perspective to the presented results in chapter 4. First,
section 5.1 focuses solely on the model parameter tests. Followed by a discussion about
common false positives in section 5.2, whereas section 5.3 focuses on data quality and avail-
ability. Section 5.4 dives into the introduced post–processing routines. Lastly, section 5.5
discusses the two final presented models.
5.1
Model and Training Parameter
This section discuses the results from section 4.2, 4.3, and 4.5. It furthermore gives reason
as to why parameters for the final model were chosen like they were.
5.1.1
Data Augmentation
The data augmentation test results do not outline individual data augmentation types
that perform better in all tested ROI in all scores than the baseline of no augmentation.
6c models, regardless of data augmentation, are unable to predict temporary water (floods)
and, thus, are hard to evaluate as R–1 as well as F1 and F2 predominately or exclusively
contain floods. This could be explainable by the model having four DTM data channels
vs. the two SAR channels. This causes the model to put too high emphasis on the DTM
which is static and independent of flood occurrences, thus not able to indicate its presence.
This hypothesis would furthermore explain, why the rotation data augmentation model
performs very well on all R–2 scores, despite its drawbacks on SAR input as discussed in
section 3.5. For this reason, rotation was chosen as a data augmentation going forward.
Secondly, mixup was also kept. It showed the second-best results of all augmentation
types on R–2, convincing by optical inspection. Lastly, adding Gaussian noise to the data
is the last of the kept data augmentation types. While it overall had mixed performances,
it showed a MCC > 0.4 for F2–4 and an inkling of flood in R–1 as the only model. The
mirror and cutmix were discarded due to worse performance vs. baseline in almost all
instances.
3c models, trained with only one DTM channel, are generally able to predict floods re-
gardless of data augmentation. Despite good results of the rotation model on R–1 and
most of F1 and F2, rotation was excluded from use in the following models. This is due
to rotation potentially harming 3c model’s abilities utilising full SAR information, given
raised concerns in section 3.5 and with respect to that the model seemingly relies more on
SAR. Mixup and Gaussian noise showed good results for 3c again and were therefore cho-
sen as the only two data augmentation types for subsequent models. Mirror and cutmix
were deemed too inconsistent in their performance vs. the baseline model.
Using 30% in augmented data for both 6c and 3c was not experimentally validated to be
the ideal value. However, it should be large enough to show a significant impact on model
results while not being too large and introducing confusion.
5.1.2
Modifications to the Standard UNet
This work introduced two additions to the standard UNet. First, a model stem. Hypothe-
sising that the introduction of input data source discrete layers would improve the model’s
capability of learning data source-specific features before combining them. This hypoth-
esis, however, is seemingly not backed by scores presented in section 4.3. Performance
across all ROI’s and scores degrade for the stem model. Especially the 3c channel models.

==PAGE:64==
Upon visual inspection of 3c results for R–1 and especially R–2 (Figure B.7) the degra-
dation in score can predominately be attributed to an increase in FP. Inspecting these
newly occurring FP in detail reveals that many FP appear in lower laying areas according
to the DTM. This hints that the stem promotes better utilisation of the two different data
sources. Yet, some other factor (most likely the (training) GT data) causes the model to
over–focus on DTM.
Not all FP are caused by the DTM. Other predicted surface water that is labelled FP
occurs in areas, where the SAR images show low backscatter. This indicates the presence
of surface water, which is not contained in the static surface water map used for training
and validation.
The SE block, as the second addition to the standard UNet, was added to the model for
its channel–wise feature response calibration [2]. Thus, ideally increasing the usage of
both input data types. The models with added SE blocks do not distinctly perform better
on average across 3&6c and ROIs. It is only the model using both stem and SE block
that clearly outperforms by score. Visually inspecting 3c results in Figure B.7 provides
further insight. Introducing SE to the model generally seems to cause a reduction in FP,
regardless of stem or no stem. It can, however, not alleviate DTM caused FPs further
indicating underlying issues with (training) GT.
All in all, the introduction of the stem to the model seems to yield the desired outcome
of promoting better usage of the individual data sources. The same is true for the SE
block, which especially in combination with the stem produces the highest scores of all
tested combinations. Yet, the combined model produces more FP, many of which can be
explained by over-relying on the DTM or by potential surface water that is present but
not resolved in the static water mask. These two circumstances are further discussed in
section 5.2 and 5.3.
5.1.3
Combinations of BCE- and Lovász Loss
The idea of combining a distribution–based and a region–based loss function was inspired
by Helleis et al. [9]. They found the best performance for a weighting factor α = 0.5.
Benchmark results for this work were presented in subsection 4.5.1.
The scores support, that equal weighing yields the best results. 6c unanimously performs
best with α = 0.5, whereas 3c only significantly under–performs in precision for R–2, where
α = 0 scores best. This is, however, not due to better predictions, but less predicted water
overall, as visual inspection of Figure B.11 confirms. As precision doesn’t penalise false
negatives, α = 0 scores best. The IoU score, which is directly optimised by Lovász loss
confirms the visual findings. The score generally increases up until α = 0.5, then followed
by a small decrease in score for α = 0.75.
Increasing α in 6c models causes model predictions to become gradually more overlap-
ping by visual inspection on R–2, indicating that the implemented Lovász loss works as
intended. For 3c models, the overlap for correctly predicted surface water also increases,
but α = 0.5&0.75 also produces more FP given ground truth data. Besides the known
DTM caused FP, it is hard to confirm if the increase in FP is falsely predicted or just not
resolved in GT. 3c model predictions on R–1 show best flood and river cover for α = 0.5.
Hence, supporting that at least a part of R–2 FP can be attributed to incomplete GT.
All in all, results support Hertel et al.’s findings, and therefore α = 0.5 was used for all
following models.

5.1.4
Patch Size
Patch size benchmark results of subsection 4.5.2 concluded that 64×64 patches on average
performed best. It most likely provides the best trade–off between encapsulating context
information while still being small enough to allow adequate representation of small–scale
surface water features in Denmark. In the global context, surface water in Denmark is
relatively small. There exists no large river nor lake and the used Geo Danmark water
mask contains even the smallest ponds, further promoting small scale. For instance, Hertel
et al. [1] uses a patch size of 256 × 256 operating on a global scale.
5.1.5
Model Starting Feature Channels
The chosen final model (**) (C = 8) with its 0.5 million parameters has comparatively few
learnable parameters. The AlexNet for instance has ca. 60 million learnable parameters.
Even though it could be argued that 3c C = 32 or 64 models perform about equivalent
to (**) the 15x / 30x increase in model size can not be justified by their respective
performance relative to the smaller, chosen model. Even the small improvements of C = 32
over (**) in lake statistics of subsection 4.5.4 are not substantial enough.
Especially
concerning the established doubts in GT quality that also will influence that metric.
Furthermore, worth considering is the relation of patch size vs. kernels in each layer. The
chosen patch size of 64×64 is relatively small compared to other applications. Hence, more
layer kernels could hinder the model’s ability to successfully generalise and not over–fit.

5.4
Postprocessing of Model Predictions
The results chapter introduced two new postprocessing routines. First, filtering by pre-
dicted water VH quantile and second, using the original high resolution DTM to mask
flood extend. These two routines will be discussed in the following subsections.
5.4.1
VH Quantile Noise Filter
The VH quantile based noise filter was implemented specifically with water predictions
only based on DTM input in mind, such as the discussed train tracks in section 5.2. By
utilising the apriori information that surface water in SAR imagery should appear as low
values, false positives can be filtered out.
The most straightforward solution to the problem could appear as simply looking at each
individual water labelled pixel and using a threshold to mask.
This, however, would
not be sensible for speckle noise (subsection 2.3.1) influencing the individual pixel’s value.
Neither would it be wise to use a static threshold, as the backscatter signal can vary greatly
between different acquisitions. Therefore, instead of a static threshold, the routine uses
the 95/99% quantile of all scene’s water pixel’s VH values. To alleviate speckle influence,
it was decided to threshold by water body instead of individual pixel level.

5.5
Discussion of the Final Model
Over all presented model tests in chapter 4, the 3c models considerably outperformed
6c models with regards to their temporary water mapping capabilities.
On the other
hand, 6c models seemingly perform better in accurately mapping small surface water
features. Section 5.3 introduced the hypothesis, that insuﬀicient data quality is the cause
of that behaviour. If GT training data did not contain labelled water ponds not present in
SAR, then 6c models are expected to be capable of mapping floods with an overall better
performance than 3c models.
With conditions as is, the 3c CNN (**) was chosen as the final model, confirming among
others [31] in that models trained on no flood containing data can correctly predict floods.
The impact of parameter tuning on test scores was mostly small, indicating that most
performance gains may be achieved by higher quality GT data. 10 training scenes acquired
throughout one year appear adequate. However, the training data set is relatively small,
and further tests with more acquisitions and a larger or multiple training ROI should
be done. The final model was furthermore not trained with orbital information linked
with S1 inputs. As discussed in subsection 2.3.1, ascending and descending orbits change
acquisition geometry and could confuse the model. While some, not presented models,
showed confusion, (**) looked unaffected.
Yet, a next iteration model should include
orbital information.
The BCNN model was trained with the same model parameters as (**), except LR and LR
decay. BCNN’s with lower learning rates demonstrated problems during training, never
lowering the validation loss. The chosen LR still exhibits said behaviour, but manages to
improve scores for some epochs. The score log of the BCNN model is shown in Figure C.5
with (**) logs as reference in Figure C.4. This points to either a bug in implementation
or bad parameter choices.
Inference results show the BCNN model predicting large scale water bodies well, but
completely missing small scale lakes, ponds, or rivers. The R–1 river for instance is totally
missed, while the attached lake is predicted with high accuracy. R–2 barely shows any
predicted surface water. Flood predictions for all three instances, however, show good
results with larger predicted flood extends than (**). At least for F3, CNN predictions
appear more realistic with respect to the S1–VH image (Figure 4.5d vs. Figure 4.8b).
Whether the general BCNN architecture or the sub–optimal training process is causing
described behaviour is unclear. Hertel et al.’s [1] results, while not operating on the same
small scale as this work, describe their BCNN model as roughly on par with their CNN
model. Yet, their presented visual examples show a courser resolution of the predicted
water bodies for the BCNN model, too.
The presented uncertainty maps show the highest uncertainty in phase changes from water
to no water pixel. This is sensible and backed by [1]’s results. Uncertainty is also present at
patch boundaries, which is an undesired by-product of the patch based inference. PAvPU
scores for F3, R–1, and R–2 are all good (above 0.9), though it was not used for further

BCNN specific evaluations. Future work focusing more on parameter tuning for the BCNN
model should utilise the PAvPU score.
The BCNN specifically introduced KL loss plateaus after only a few training epochs (Fig-

must be noted that BCNN models take considerably more time to run. This is caused by
the model’s distribution based weights, that vary each inference run. Thus, necessitating
multiple runs to generate a sound output and uncertainty map.

In previous sections, it was established, why the 6c model could not be chosen as the
final model. Even though the 6c model is considerably better than 3c in predicting small

6
Conclusion and Future Work
This work successfully introduced UNet based deep learning models for temporary wa-
ter mapping in Denmark using remote sensing data. The Denmark specific data sources
of high–resolution water masks and high–resolution digital terrain models were success-
fully incorporated, alongside Sentinel 1 SAR data. The proposed model additions – stem
& squeeze and excitation blocks – to the standard UNet architecture showed improved
performance, confirming their usefulness for water body mapping.
The high–resolution water masks from Geo Danmark, combined together to form a co-
herent water mask for model training, demonstrated superior performance over Sentinel
2 based NDWI ground truth. However, its own drastic shortcomings were highlighted.
Given its fine resolution, it maps surface water in ground truth, where SAR data is un-
able to resolve it. This work hypothesises, that the GT thereby promotes an inherent
over–reliance on the model’s DTM inputs. Which, in turn, causes false inference results.
Furthermore, the water mask is unable to resolve temporary water, due to its static na-
ture. This does evidently cause false false positive prediction labels. This work reasons
that the water mask’s inability also causes full information models (two SAR and four
DTM channels) to become unable to map temporary surface water, whereas less informa-
tion models (two SAR and one DTM channels) can. Full information models seemingly
possess enough information to satisfy the too fine ground truth during training, losing
their flood mapping capabilities. Full information models are expected to perform better
than less information models if Geo Danmark ground truth could be fixed.
Despite ground truth diﬀiculties, the final model showed good inference results on the
three introduced flood instances of the 20.-21.10.2023 Baltic Sea storm flood. The hand
labelled flood extends based on the VH image proved suﬀicient for preliminary evaluation.
Predicted F1 flood predictions, however, exceeded the labelled extent, which was confirmed
by visual inspection of the 2022 orthophoto to be most likely correct. Thus, future works
should aspire to use higher resolution flood ground truth, which ideally is locally cross–
referenced by personnel on the ground.
Flood pond mapping capabilities could not be tested. Ground truth is missing and it was
deemed too uncertain to use manual mapping with no confirmed location of rainwater
pond occurrences. Calling again for improved and more numerous ground truth data. An
indication for a minimum surface area of potential rainwater ponds as a requirement was
discussed and shown to be around 50 pixel, translating to 0.5ha.
Two introduced post processing routines show promising results; one: eliminating false
predictions solely from DTM inputs and two: mapping floods with higher fidelity by using
full DTM resolution. The second routine’s results were compared to results published by
DHI [69] and showed comparable flood extends.
The probabilistic Bayesian adaptation was found to produce overall sensible results, con-
firming Hertel et al.’s [1] work. Additionally, providing uncertainty quantifications could
prove highly useful in crisis response. Yet, this works implementation of the Bayesian
network demonstrated peculiarities, hinting towards an implementation error, or, bad
parameter choices.
This work furthermore confirmed Helleis et al.’s [9] finding that a combination of distribution–
and region–based linearly weighted loss function achieves the best results. Additionally,

==PAGE:78==
this work support the finding by Wieland et al. [31] that deep learning models can success-
fully map floods even when trained on permanent water only. Lastly, this work confirms
the findings by among others, [30, 27, 31] that adding DTM information yields better
results than relying on SAR only.
The used data sources are all acquired at different points in time with different temporal
resolutions. Furthermore, events of interest must not necessarily occur at the same time
as the data does. This can cause events to be blind to the model, especially with surface
water being highly volatile, meaning that one day’s difference can convey a fundamentally
different state. Usually, the SAR image input will pose the highest constraint, yet, the
Holstebro flood event predates the DTM data source. Overall, every user should be aware
of the challenges that come with different acquisition times.
The choice of SAR as model input over Sentinel 2 optical imagery, despite the undoubtedly
high potential of S2, is justified by increased effective temporal resolution. As SAR is
independent of cloud cover, it will be possible to map many more floods. For instance, S2
was blind to the discussed Baltic Sea flood.
6.1
Answer to Problem Questions
Answers to the formulated problem questions were given throughout the entire report.
This section summarises them for each respective question.
1. Scale
50 pixel in pond surface area in model resolution (10m) were argued to be the
approximate minimum requirement. Ponds down to 30 or even 20 pixel surface area
could potentially be mapped, if full channel model performance could be improved.
However, as is, the current performance is most likely not suﬀicient to map adequate
amounts of rain ponds.
2. Ground Truth Data
It was established that Geo Danmark based ground truth is best suited. Yet, it
showed clear weaknesses that should be alleviated. Models could predict tempo-
rary water, only trained on stationary water. Lastly, to the best of the author’s
knowledge, no flood or rain pond data set exists for Denmark specifically.
3. Scalability & Transferability
The model and potential future derivatives should be relatively easy to apply to
all of Denmark. Cost are limited to using a more potent workstation. Applying
it to other countries than Denmark should work fine, too. As long as high enough
resolution DTM data exists.
4. Inference Speed
The CNN model’s inference speed is very fast. Inference on the whole ROI of Randers
ran in ca. 45s. On the other hand BCNN models are considerably slower. Here, the
same area would take at least 20x the time. Both model’s speed should be enough
for any application, given proper computational resources.
5. Input Data
Most areas in Denmark should be covered by about three S1 passes which should
enable to capture a good amount of flood events. However, some will undoubtedly
be missed. The DTM update frequency is low, but most likely suﬀicient given the
expected frequency of changes in it.

A.1.8
Step by Step Processing Guides
A.1.8.1
DTM
1. With OSGeo4W Shell use: gdalbuildvrt »path to .vrt file« »path to *.tif DTM files«
to create a gdal virtual format (vrt) file for easier handling. Use parenthesis for the
paths.
2. With OSGeo4W Shell use: gdal_merge -o »path to combined.tif« »path to vrt« -ps
N N to combine all blocks into one .tif file with pixel resolution of NxN meter.
3. Load both the combined DTM file and one S1 SAR image of the respective region
into QGis.
4. Use the QGis raster function Align Rasters. Here add both raster layers
• if re–scale in pre–processing: choose the S1 SAR image as reference layer.
Requires in step 2: N=10.
• if re–scale in model: choose the DTM raster as reference layer. Use Clip to
Extend and choose S1 layer. Requires in step 2: N=1 (.
5. Export the newly created aligned DTM raster as a .tif file, which then can be used
in python.
6. For deep learning, not only the absolute height of the DTM is used but the orientation
[29]. It consists of sin– and cos– component, slope and a scaled height. In this case
to 175m - as the heighest point of Denmark.
To do so, the Buteo [63] function
raster_dem_to_orientation is used.
A.1.8.2
Basin-, Lake-, and Ocean-mask
1. Open QGis and import the different water mask products. All products are vectors,
however in order to align them to the S1 image they need to be transformed to
raster. For that, the vector need to be editable - which they are not.
2. Save products as GeoPackage and re-import into QGis.
3. These new layers can be edited.
To do so, navigate via ”open attribute table”,
”toggle editing mode” to ”field calculator”. Create a new column with 1s.
4. Toggle edit mode back to off, so that the changes are saved.
5. Use vector to raster function Rasterize with parameters and repeat for all layers:
• Input layer: the layer with the added column of ones
• Field to use for a burn–in value: the column of ones
• Output raster units: Pixels
• With width & height: 100000 (the higher the number the more detail)
• Output extend: via drop–down, then ”calculate from layer” choose the S1 layer
• Rasterized: via drop–down choose location and file name of new file
6. Use raster function Align Rasters. Add newly created raster layers with ”average”.
For S1 layer keep ”Nearest Neighbour” as resampling method.
7. Choose S1 SAR image as reference layer and click OK to run.
8. Export the newly created aligned rasters as .tif files.

9. Use python to invert the ocean mask.
10. The masks as of here form the basis of the ground truth, they can however, if need

11. Remember, that the rasterized data of step (5) use a lot of storage.

A.1.8.3
River-mask
1. Open QGis and import vandloebskant product. It is a multi–line–string.
2. Use Polygonize tool from Processing Toolbox to convert string to polygon. This
will create errors, but less than Lines to polygons. See an example in Figure A.6.
3. Manually clean errors by toggling edit on the river polygon layer and using Select
Features by Area or Single Click then delete.
4. Follow step 2–8 of previous guide to rasterize and align.
5. The river mask has to be post processed in any case – contrary to the other masks.
This is due to the very thin steams that are resolved. Therefore follow:
(a) Use python to threshold the raster with 0.9 to get a binary mask.
(b) Use scipy with gaussian_filter and σ = 1 on the binary mask. Then thresh-
old again with 0.4 to get rid of singular pixels. The result is binary.
