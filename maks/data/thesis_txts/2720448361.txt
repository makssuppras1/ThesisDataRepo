An assessment of economic, technical and environmental attractiveness of an ammonia based fuel system for
maritime propulsion
Abstract
The shipping industry accounts for around 2.4% of the global CO2 emissions, and it is expected that this will
increase. The International Maritime Organisation (IMO) has set targets of GHG emission reductions of 40%
by 2030, and 50% by 2050, with 2008 as a baseline. To achieve this, there is a need for greener alternative
maritime fuels.
The AEGIR project is a collaboration between DTU, Ballard, Coorstek, SENTEF, VARD, and VTT. The
project aims to develop an ammonia-fuelled propulsion system, using a unique combination of Solid oxide fuel
cells (SOFC), Proton exchange membrane fuel cells (PEMFC), and Proton conducting electrochemical mem-
brane reactor (PCEMR). One of its main objectives is to demonstrate a well-to-wake GHG emission reduction
of 90%.
This thesis has assessed the attractiveness of AEGIRs ammonia based fuel system. Trough a literature study,
the cost estimates related to the CapEx and OpEx were identified. Then a techno-economic comparison was
conducted between the alternative drivetrains: methanol, hydrogen and HFO. Based on the techno-economic
analysis alone HFO appears to be the most attractive alternative, and ammonia the most attractive e-fuel.
A decision support tool was created to assess non-economic factors of the drivetrains. The literature study
identified 8 sub-criteria, which covered the main criteria: environmental, technical, and social-political. Based
on the MCDA analysis alone, ammonia seems to be a less attractive alternative than other e-fuels, yet still more
attractive than HFO.
While ammonia does not score the highest within the MCDA, it does provide the benefit of being the most
cost-effective e-fuel for the private sector, and it is still performing significantly better than HFO within the
environmental and social-political aspects.

1
Introduction
The shipping industry is currently responsible for around 2.4 % of the total global emissions of CO2 (IMO
2014). According to The International Maritime Organisation (IMO), the seaborne trade has grown by 40% in
the period 2008 to 2018 and this growth is expected to continue. The continued growth is estimated to increase
emissions between 90% and 130% from 2008 levels by 2050 (IMO 2020). Additionally, IMO estimated that
around 15% of the global CO2 emissions would be attributed to shipping in 2050 (IMO 2014).
With the rising impact of global warming, it is clear that something must be done to offset this trend. IMO
has set targets to reduce the CO2 intensity by 40% by 2030 and achieve a 50% reduction of total greenhouse
gas (GHG) emissions by 2050 with 2008 as a baseline (IMO 2018).
The shipping industry is already using methods to reduce their emissions, such as route optimization using
operational research methods, reducing speed to save fuel and a number of technical methods to reduce emissions.
However, methods to address emissions such as these will not be enough to reach IMO’s targets with the expected
continued growth of shipping. It is clear that a global introduction of alternative fuels is needed. The current
estimate is that at least 70% of the current marine fuel needs to be changed from conventional fossil based fuels
to a greener option in order to meet IMO’s targets. (Ampah et al. 2021).
There are a range of different alternative fuels that have the potential to be the pathway towards meeting those
targets. Several parts of the industry have in recent years been advocating for ammonia as an alternative fuel
(McKinlay et al. 2021). Also, there is a trend in research showing an uptake in the interest of methanol and
hydrogen, as well as ammonia as possible alternatives to traditional fuels. Ammonia, methanol and hydrogen
theoretically have the possibility of a close to, if not completely, zero-carbon emission well-to-wake (Ampah
et al. 2021).
The industry is also showing a willingness to move towards a zero-emission future, with companies like Mærsk
setting a net-zero emission target for 2040. They are investing in methanol as an alternative fuel, with a total
order of 19 container vessels capable of running on green methanol. They are set to be delivered before 2025,
and the first vessels are already in use (Maersk n.d.).
The first commercially ammonia fuelled vessel is scheduled to be delivered in the second half of 2023. Addition-
ally, the demand for ammonia fuel is expected to increase steeply from 2035 and onward, and could contribute
42% of the global market share by 2050 (Wu et al. 2022)

1.1
AEGIR project
The AEGIR project is a collaboration between DTU, Ballard, Coorstek, SENTEF, VARD, and VTT.
The project looks to assess the prospect for ammonia as a future fuel in the maritime sector. It aims to develop
an ammonia-fuelled propulsion system, using a unique combination of fuel cells and a membrane based system.
The propulsion system will be a combination of the following 3 technologies:
1. Solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC). The SOFC will work as a combined NH3 cracker and a fuel cell. It would
enable efficient cracking of ammonia into hydrogen and nitrogen.
2. Proton exchange membrane fuel cell (PEMFC). The PEMFC utilizes the remaining energy in the formed
H2 as fuel to produce electricity.
3. Proton conducting electrochemical membrane reactor (PCEMR). The PCEMR separates the H2 at high
purity and feeds it to the PEMFC.
The AEGIR project has four main objectives:
1. Establish a concept for a fully electric ammonia-fueled ship without CO2 emissions and having more than
60% tank-to-electricity efficiency.
2. Demonstrate a well-to-wake GHG emission reduction of more than 90% compared to Liquefied Natural
Gas (LNG).
3. Achieving 40,000 hour lifespan for the SOFC and PEMFC. Additionally, reaching the ISO 14687 standard
for hydrogen fuel quality through the PCEMR.
4. Identify potential scale up issues for a 20 MW maritime system in a concept study.

1.2
Problem formulation
This thesis aims to assess the attractiveness of an ammonia based fuel solution. The thesis is undertaken as
part of the AEGIR research project, which will provide data for the analysis. The assessment will include
a comparison of alternative drivetrains (namely methanol, hydrogen, and HFO) to identify the attractiveness
of the developed solution. The economic, technical, social and environmental aspects of these solutions will
be considered using techno-economic analyses and multi-criteria decision analyses (MCDA). The latter will be
based on identified KPIs, including non-economic factors. The thesis is comprised of the following sections:
• A literature study will be conducted to improve the cost estimates related to the CapEx and OpEx of the
proposed solution and alternatives drivetrains.
• A techno-economic comparison will be conducted between the alternative drivetrains: methanol, hydrogen
and HFO.
• Create a decision support tool considering relevant KPIs to assess the non-economic factors for the driv-
etrains.
• Determine the attractiveness of the proposed solution compared to the alternative drivetrains.
1.3
Scope
The purpose of this thesis is to assist the AEGIR project in developing decision support tools to assess the
techno-economic and environmental aspect of the proposed concept.
Throughout the thesis, the ammonia-
fuelled propulsion system proposed by the AEGIR project (referred to as the AEGIR system) will be compared
with alternative drivetrains, namely methanol, hydrogen and heavy fuel oil (HFO).
Each of the alternatives will be compared in a techno-economic analysis. Additionally, a multi-criteria decision
support framework will be built to asses the alternatives on environmental, social and technical aspects through
identified KPI from the literature.
One of the main objective for the AEGIR project is to demonstrate a 90% reduction in GHG emission. As such
ammonia, methanol and hydrogen will only be assessed as e-fuels produced by renewable energy.

Literature study
As part of this thesis, a literature study was conducted to extract the necessary data and knowledge in order
to analyse ammonia, methanol, hydrogen, and HFO as fuels for maritime propulsion. The results of the study
will be presented as two separate aspects; the economic aspect and the remaining aspects for the MCDA.
In the economic aspect, the aim was to find estimates for all cost related to the CapEx and OpEx of the AEGIR

In the MCDA aspect, the aim was to identify previously conducted research and technical reports, which as-

The identified research and technical reports were then used

For the AEGIR system the OpEx consists of:

2.1.1
SOFC - CapEx & OpEx
The application of SOFC technology in ships is relatively new, and both the CapEx and OpEx are therefore
fairly uncertain. All estimates found in the literature study are presented in the following.
The CapEx of SOFC today is high as it is a relatively immature technology. DNV (2019) assessed the price of
the SOFC system to be between 2,200-5,600 $/kW in 2019, with an expected reduction of up to 1000$/kW by
2022.
However, according to Xing, Stuart, Spencer & Chen (2021) today’s price for a SOFC system is around 4,000-
9,000$/kW. They predict that through mass production the stack price will drop to 170$/kW, which constitutes
a significant drop in SOFC system price.
Generally, a large drop in price is expected. However, estimates of how much the future price will decrease vary
in the literature. This is mainly due to the estimated production volume. The estimated production price of
stacks vary from 166$/kW to 5387$/kW depending on the production level (Scataglini et al. 2017).
This is reflected in the high variation in the estimated system price of the SOFC, which depending on production
level vary from 150 to 1500 $/kW (Biert et al. 2016).
The U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) have 5 goals for the future state of SOFCs, which they actively work
towards meeting by 2030. The goals are (Department of Energy 2019):
• Improve efficiency of SOFCs to 60 percent without incorporating carbon capture and sequestration
• Achieve a proven lifetime of 40,000 hours or more
• Achieve less than 0.2 percent per 1,000 hours of degradation rate
• Decrease the stack costs of SOFCs to less than $ 225 per kW
• Decrease system SOFC costs to less than $ 900 / kW
For the DOE’ target to be met, it will require an annual manufacturing volume of 100-250 MWe depending on
the system size and production rate (Scataglini et al. 2017).
Expert assessments from 27 different experts from authorities, academia and the industry found that the DOE
targets can be met in the time-span 2035-2050, but will likely be more expensive in 2030 (Whiston et al. 2019).

3.1
Monte Carlo simulation
Monte Carlo simulation is a stochastic simulation, which is a statistical sampling method that collects random
numbers from a given probability distribution.
Depending on the level of knowledge on the uncertain parameter or variables, either a parametric distribution
or non-parametric distribution will be most suitable.
The parametric distribution is most suitable when the uncertain parameters are specified by the literature or
data. This would be a normal, gamma, and beta distribution.
The non-parametric distribution is applied if the uncertain parameters relies on an expert’s judgement. This
would be a triangular and uniform distribution (Barfod & Leleur 2021).

3.2
Multi Criteria Decision Analysis
MCDA is a tool used to support the decision-making process by evaluating different alternatives against several
requirements with different levels of priority to the stakeholders.
MCDA creates a preference structure on different criteria in order to evaluate different alternatives. The MCDA
can be represented by three pieces:
1. The alternatives; A set of alternatives which should be evaluated. For this thesis this is the fuel types.
2. Their characteristics; These are what the alternatives will be evaluated based on and are referred to as
criteria.
3. The preference structure; This is how much each characteristic should be reflected in the decision. I.e.
This is the weight of the criteria as set by the decision-maker(s).
The alternatives and the criteria are considered the objective part of the analysis. The preference structure
is considered more subjective. As "preference" would suggest, this depends on each decision-maker and their
biases (Barfod & Leleur 2021).
3.2.1
Value measurement
Through the value measurement each alternative is associated with a value V(a). This is done in order to
construct a preference order of all alternatives, such that V(a) is preferred over V(b) if, and only if, V(a)>V(b).
Indifference is implied if V(a)=V(b)
There are two components to the value measurement: The partial value function vi(a), and the weights of the
criteria wi.
The partial value function measures the alternatives score on each criteria, i.
The fundamental property of the partial value function is that vi(a) is preferred over vi(b) if, and only if,
vi(a) > vi(b). Also, indifference if, and only if, vi(a) = vi(b).
From these two components V(a) is calculated. The additive model is the most widely used and simplest method
(Barfod & Leleur 2021):

3.2.2
Eliciting scores
This is the process of assessing a value for the performance of alternatives against each criteria, i.e. this is the
assessment of vi(a). The values are assessed on a interval scale of measurement, so a minimum and maximum
point must be defined as end points. This is done either by a local or global scale.
A local scale is defined by the performance the alternatives. The best performing alternative will be set at
maximum score, and the worst performing alternative will be set at the minimum score. The rest will receive
18
==PAGE:31==
An assessment of economic, technical and environmental attractiveness of an ammonia based fuel system for
maritime propulsion
scores reflecting their performance relative to the endpoints.
A global scale is defined by the best and worst possible performance of an alternative. All alternatives are then
scored based on their performance relative to the end points, i.e. the best and worst possible performance of a
alternative.
It is essential that the same scale is used throughout the analysis.
When the endpoints are determined the alternative can be scored. The following two methods have been used
(Barfod & Leleur 2021):
1. Value function, Relates to the performance for measurable attribute reflecting the criteria.
2. Qualitative value scale, Relates to the performance of alternatives by descriptive pointers or word
models which appropriate values are assigned.
This thesis has applied defined scales to score the alternatives, therefore direct rating was not used.
3.2.3
Value function
The value function can be defined as one of the following:
• Monotonically increasing against the natural scale. The highest value of the attribute is the most preferred,
and the lowest is the least.
• Monotonically decreasing against the natural scale. The lowest value of the attribute is the most preferred,
and the highest is the least.
• Non-monotonic. An intermediate point on the scale is the most or least preferred.
It is the decision maker(s) first assignment to determine which one the above list the value function should be.
A method of creating the value function are the Bisection method. The decision maker should identify the
point on the attribute scale that would constitute % value between the 2 end points. The next step is to identify
midpoints between then end points and the previous found midpoint. Hence, being 25% value and 75% value.
It is generally accepted the 5 points (2 end points and 25, 50, 75% value points) are sufficient to sketch a value
function Barfod & Leleur (2021).
3.2.4
Qualitative value scale
When a criteria do not have measurable attributes a qualitative value scale can be constructed. In a qualita-
tive scale the alternatives performance can be evaluated by descriptive point, which are assigned value. Two
endpoints are the bare minimum that have to be defined, however, intermediate point can also be defined.

3.2.5
Value trees
The value tree structures the problem as a multi-level tree. Weights are assigned at as relative weights and
cumulative weights. The relative are assigned within families of criteria, and must be normalized and summed
to 1 (or 100). A family are defined as sharing the same parent criteria.
The cumulative weights of a criteria is product its relative weight, and its parents relative, and so on. The
cumulative weights of all bottom-level criteria sum to 1 (Barfod & Leleur 2021).

Sensitivity analysis
This section presents the Monte Carlo simulation which is used for the sensitivity analysis. As seen in table 20
fuel cost and SOFC system cost are the most significant costs, making up over 90% of the total cost combined.
This thesis assumes a Beta.Perf distribution for both fuel price and SOFC system price. Based on the available
data, a triangular or Beta.PERF will be most suitable, as they require a minimum, maximum and most likely
estimate.
Beta.PERF is ideal for modeling expert opinions, and it puts more emphasize on the most likely value (Barfod &
Leleur 2021). As the identified cost are from trustworthy sources, they are seen as an expert’s opinion. For this

5
Multi Criteria Decision Analysis
The following sections present the MCDA. This thesis uses the value measurement approach as the MCDA
method. This method was deemed most suitable for this analysis as it offers the opportunity to aggregate
preferences values for each alternative on each criteria (vi(a)) into a comparative score for each alternative
V(a). The value measurement approach was chosen over other methods such as the AHD and REMBRANDT
method. These were disregarded due to the vast amount of pairwise comparisons required. Additionally, some of
the criteria are measurable criteria which pairwise comparisons are not as suitable for as the value measurement
approach (Barfod & Leleur 2021).
This MCDA is comprised of 6 sub-sections making up the analysis:
• Criteria selection
• Criteria scoring
• Criteria weights
• Alternative ranking
• Sensitivity analysis
Finally, a comparison of the MCDA ranking and techno-economic analysis is done in order to enable a selection
of the most suitable maritime fuel.
5.1
Criteria selection
The first step of the criteria selection was carried out in the literature study, section 2.2. Here all relevant
criteria from the available literature were identified. They can be seen in figure 5.
The second step is to evaluate the criteria to determine, which can and should be included in the MCDA model.
Each criteria is examined for what they measure in order to identify if any criteria overlap or measure similar
things, which would led to double counting. Additionally, some criteria may be redundant for this analysis.
36
==PAGE:49==
An assessment of economic, technical and environmental attractiveness of an ammonia based fuel system for
maritime propulsion
Figure 5: First iteration of criteria
"Acidification" is mainly based on the NOx and SO2 emissions. Similarly, "Health impact" mainly measures the
particulate matter. Theses two are similar to the criteria: "Effect on NOx, SOx, and PM emission reduction".
As such the 5 criteria are combined into 1 criteria: "local emissions", with the underlining criteria being: NOx,
SOx, and PM emission.
"Fuel energy density" and "Energy storage efficiency" are both a measurement of the potential added cost of a
fuel. These costs are included when calculating the loss of TEU in the techno-economic analysis. Additionally,
the fuel density overlap with the storage cost in the economic calculations. As such, they are excluded from the
MCDA model.
"Engine space" is a redundant criteria for this MCDA model, as we assume that ammonia, methanol and
hydrogen all run on the AEGIR system. The literature study was not able to identify any data suggesting that
the engine size should differ depending on ammonia, methanol and hydrogen.
Additionally, since the fuels are assumed to run in the AEGIR system, it is also assumed that it will be
implemented in new ships only. Therefore, "Adaptability to existing ships" is not relevant for this analysis, as
all new ship will be designed to run on the preferred fuel be it ammonia, methanol, hydrogen or something else.
"Robustness" is deemed redundant for this analysis as the literature study identified no data that indicated the
performance of ammonia, methanol and hydrogen should vary depending external factors, such as wind and
weather. As such they are assumed to manage external factors equally.
This criteria is mostly suited for comparison of different alternative engine systems against each other, such as
wind power and nuclear power.

"Minimum time at berth" would be relevant if any fuel required more time at berth than normally used, say for
refueling or safety. However, no data indicates that this is the case, and it is therefore assumed that ammonia,
methanol and hydrogen will not need extra time at berth. Hence, the criteria is redundant.
"Expertise" is similar to "Technical maturity". It is safe to assume that a high level of technical maturity would
lead a high level of expertise. Therefore, "expertise" will not be considered further.

"GHG emissions" consist of different emissions. However, the most important of them is CO2. For the sake of
simplicity, this criteria will only measure the CO2 emissions of the fuel.
"Component circularity" is similar to scrap value. As mentioned in the economic analysis, it was not possible
to estimate the scrap value. Additionally, "Component circularity" would only be interesting, if the different

Some criteria, although deemed relevant, do not have sufficient data to score the fuel on them. ”Safety area",

5.2
Criteria scores
This section presents the scoring of each criteria. A local scale has been used for the scoring process. This
means that the worst performing will receive 0 point, and the best performing will receive 100 points (Barfod
& Leleur 2021). The local scale has been chosen over the global since it would not be possible to determine the
absolute best and worst performance for each criteria.
The partial value function has been used when using measurable attributes. The bisection was used to create
the value functions. In all cases were the value function was used it was monotonically decreasing.
If measurable attributes were not possible to obtain, a qualitative ranking scale has been used.
5.2.1
CO2 emissions
The value function is used as this criteria shows a quantitative measurement of the amount of CO2 the fuels
emit well-to-wake. The CO2 emissions from ammonia and hydrogen is estimated at 0 g/MJ. This is due to
both having 0 emission tank-to-wake, so any emission would depend on the production of the fuel. As only
e-ammonia and e-hydrogen are considered, it would be produced green, and under production with renewable
energy they will be CO2-zero (Ampah et al. 2021).
Methanol has been estimated to 9 g/MJ due to its tank-to-wake emissions (DNV 2019). This is under the as-
sumption that CO2 capturing will not be used under the production. Had that been the case, the well-to-wake
emission for methanol would be much closer to zero, if not zero (Lloyd’s 2019b).
From the CO2 emissions a value function is created. Using local scales CO2 emission of 0 g/MJ is set to value
100, and 87.5 g/MJ is set to value 0.
A 50% value of the CO2 emissions is determined by the fuel’s ability to meet the 50% GHG reduction by 2050
based on 2008 as baseline, which is the ambition of IMO. To achieve this a 85% reduction in carbon intensity
is needed (Lloyd’s 2019a). Hence % 50 value for the value function is 85% of the HFO emission.
The 25% value is found by a 75% reduction of the HFO emissions. This is a European commission proposal,
with 2020 emissions as a baseline (Gozillon 2022).
The 75% value of the value function is estimated at a 95% reduction of HFO emissions. The recommended
reduction for 2050 is 100% reduction of GHG (Gozillon 2022)(Lloyd’s 2019a). Therefore, it has been deemed
reasonable that 75% value will be close to a 100% reduction. See table 25.

Criteria weights
The weighting of the criteria is based on the literature review.
This problem have been constructed as a
multi-level value tree.
At the third level of criteria, NOx, SOx, and PM emissions must be ranked. In Hansson et al. (2019), the
stakeholders ranked "Acidification" slightly higher than "Health impact". However there is not clear distinction
between NOx, SOx and PM, in "Acidification" and "Health impact". As such they are all ranked as of equal
importance.
At the second level the sub-criteria of Environmental, Technical and Social-Political must be ranked.
"CO2 emissions" is ranked significantly more important than "Local emissions" and "Aquatic risk". In previous
MCDA studies the decision maker(s) have all scored CO2 higher than "Local emissions" and "Aquatic risk"
(Hansson et al. 2019) & (Ren & Liang 2017). Additionally, in Ampah et al. (2021), CO2 emissions was identified
as a key criteria.
"Local emissions" is slightly more important than "Aquatic risk". This is due to the fact that "Local emission"
has been mentioned in most relevant literature, whereas "aquatic risk" has not been as widely considered.
Furthermore, in Hansson et al. (2019), the equivalent to "Aquatic risk" was not deemed important enough by
the decision maker(s) to include in the MCDA.
Hence, the relative weight was "CO2 emissions" = 0.75, "Local emissions" = 0.15, and "Aquatic risk"
= 0.10.

Fuel availability has been rank significantly higher than "Bunker availability" and "Technical maturity". This
is based on the ranking in Hansson et al. (2019), where fuel availability was given the relative weight of 0.71,

Discussion
This section will present a discussion of assumptions and choices made in the techno-economic analysis and in
the MCDA, and what effects this could have on the results.
6.1
Fuel production
This thesis has only examined e-fuels, and the cost estimate of these have been based on 2030 estimates.
However, it is far from a safe assumption that e-ammonia, e-methanol, or e-hydrogen could become the main
energy source in the shipping industry by 2030. This would require wide-spread availability of the fuels for it
to be a realistic scenario. Only around 4% of hydrogen that is currently being produced now can be consider
green, and less than 1% of methanol (IRENA 2021). This is much the same for green ammonia at around %.
If these e-fuels are produced using fossil natural gas (NG), coal or similar "black" energy sources, the total
CO2e emissions are similar or even higher than conventional fuels, like HFO. This is illustrated in table 56,
where the total CO2e emissions are calculated based on the well-to-tank and tank-to-wake CO2e emitted when
the alternative fuels are produced using "black" energy sources (Xing, Stuart, Spence & Chen 2021). The fuel
consumption is based on the same operational cycle used throughout this thesis, this can be seen in appendix
E.
From the table it is clear that the total CO2e emissions when using ammonia and methanol produced with coal
is over 200% worse than HFO well-to-wake. Ammonia, methanol and hydrogen produced with fossil NG are
equivalent or slightly worse than HFO when it comes to total CO2e emissions.
This point is further underlined by the current production of ammonia. There is currently a high production
of ammonia world wide, as it is used in large parts for fertilizer. This production has a very high energy and
emissions footprint. The production typically relies on fossil fuels and is a very energy intensive process. This
production of ammonia accounts for 2% of the global energy consumption and 1% of global CO2 emissions.
From this data it is clear that it is essential that the fuels can be produced green, otherwise it is likely to would
have a massive negative impact on the environment (McKinlay et al. 2021).

Although all fuel can be produced with renewable energy, there may be technical challenges with large scale pro-

Therefore, the assumption of having a full transition to e-fuels by 2030 is not realistic. This would have to be
a slow process, requiring more research and significant investments into the whole supply chain.
6.2
MCDA - strengths and weaknesses
The MCDA was used to create a decision support tool that would assess the non-economic factors of the
different fuels. Through the MCDA it was possible to measure the different alternatives’ performance against
both qualitative and quantitative KPIs.
The MCDA was based on up-to-date literature and research carried out within in the field of alternative maritime
fuels. From this work a selection of criteria were chosen to ensure that the most important aspects related to
ammonia, methanol, hydrogen and HFO were covered.
Following the selection each of the alternatives were scored on each criteria. Although this could be considered
the "objective" part of the analysis, it should be noted that the scoring of qualitative criteria would be improved
63
==PAGE:76==
An assessment of economic, technical and environmental attractiveness of an ammonia based fuel system for
maritime propulsion
by input and validation from industry experts and other relevant stakeholders, such as legislative bodies.
The criteria were scored based on a local scale. There are advantages with the use of a local scale, such as
the ease of the scale compared to the global scale. However, the limitation is that if a new alternative were
to be added to the MCDA, all criteria scores would need to reevaluated. This is of course a weakness for the
decision support tool as a whole, since that would be a time consuming process, but as a whole the local scale
was evaluated as being the most suitable for this specific MCDA.
The criteria weights was chosen based on previous MCDA studies. This was done in order to have representative
weight values based both on authorities and the private sector (since these two in particular tend to value
criteria differently). Although evaluated as being the most representative criteria weights, there will be a level
of uncertainty with this approach. We know that the weights are very influential to the final value scores and
subsequent ranking. This thesis would therefore benefit from input from stakeholders and decision makers in
the industry and in governing bodies to ensure that the preference of the decision maker(s) is represented in an
accurate manner.
6.3
Techno-economic analysis - strengths and weaknesses
The techno-economic analysis was created to assess the economic attractiveness of ammonia against different
fuel types. The analysis showed that HFO was much cheaper than ammonia, methanol and hydrogen, having
both lower CapEx and OpEx.
Ammonia was shown to be the cheapest e-fuel.
However, it is essential to
remember that the calculations and results are only as certain as the estimates they were based upon.
All the calculations made in the analysis was based on best estimates identified in the literature. Based on
the literature study this thesis has created an up-to-date catalogue of the latest research and cost estimates
available. As the AEGIR project is based on relatively immature technologies, such as the SOFC and ammonia
fuel, the price point for these are not as stable or certain as for the technologies related to HFO. This would
normally "just" be a minor weakness for the techno-economic analysis, but since over 90% of the total cost were
attributed to the SOFC and the fuel, this weakness/uncertainty becomes very critical for the analysis.
In order to examine this uncertainty a Monte Carlo simulation was conducted to evaluate the sensitivity of total
cost. It was possible to evaluate the impact of this uncertainty. The total cost of the AEGIR system had a std.
deviation of 44 million $, and a total variation of around 250 million.
Similarly to the MCDA, the techno-economic analysis could improve by collaboration with manufactures and
industry expects to asses and validate the cost estimates used. This would increase the precision and validity
of the analysis. However, since the AEGIR project is dealing with new technologies, there will always be a
relatively high uncertainty when predicting future costs.

6.4
Further studies
The following sub-sections will identify areas where further studies could be made.
6.4.1
Cost comparison
The techno-economic calculations for methanol and hydrogen are based on the assumption that they can run
on the same engine system as the AEGIR system, which was designed for ammonia. Even though methanol
and hydrogen both would be able to run on a system made up by SOFC, PEMFC, and PCEMR, it is unlikely
that they would run at the exact same efficiency levels, or that the optimal units of SOFC, PEMFC, PCEMR
would be the same. Especially the efficiency of the system would be very consequential for the total cost, as this
impacts the fuel consumption, which was shown to be the largest cost by a large margin. It would be interesting
for future studies to utilise a more in depth calculation for methanol and hydrogen’s efficiency level to test the
assumption that the different alternatives can operate on a equivalent level.
6.4.2
Scrap value
Scrap value was identified as a possible benefit for the AEGIR system, but it had to be excluded as a criteria
due to lack of data. There is a trend with focus on component circularity to reduce waste, and the EU is already
a part of a project that aims to improve the recycling of SOFC and PEMFC (BEST4Hy n.d.). As such, it is
very likely that there would be some scrap value from the SOFC and PEMFC when they were replaced. This
could decrease the overall cost of the AEGIR project, and produce a more accurate economic assessment.
6.4.3
Expand on alternatives evaluated
This thesis only focused on evaluating HFO and e-fuels, namely ammonia, methanol and hydrogen. However,
other fuels and alternative energies could be relevant to include. This could be nuclear power, wind energy and
Liquefied natural gasses (LNG). These have all been included in other studies found in the literature study.
However, if the analysis was expanded to include these, the chosen criteria would also need to be reevaluated.
In particular, it could be interesting to include nuclear power, as this is a relatively mature technology already
in use for military ships and submarines. Generally, nuclear power is an energy source with a lot of positive
trades. The criteria "public opinion" becomes much more relevant if nuclear power was to be included, as this
historically has been a very divisive energy source. Additionally it is easy to image that a decision maker would
value safety higher, if nuclear power was included and thereby changing the weights.
Another alternative energy which could be included is wind energy in the form of sail ships. While this may
sound like an antiquated technology, there is some merit to the alternative. In the case this alternative was to
be included, the criteria; durability, should probably be included in the MCDA, as wind energy is very sensitive
to external sources. Wind energy could also have some problems with consistently producing the needed energy
to sail certain routes. This should also be considered if wind energy were included.

6.4.4
CO2 fines
The "CO2 fines" that are already used by larger governing bodies, such as EU, were not included in this analysis.
While these would not effect the cost of the e-fuels, they would for the HFO have been added as an additional
cost. It was apparent in the techno-economic analysis that HFO was far cheaper than the e-fuels, and a CO2
fine would not be expected to change the ranking as it stands. However, "CO2 fines" is an important tool for
governments to influence the private sector to use more green alternatives. As mentioned, ship owners value
economics as the most important criteria. The same was also true for all other stakeholders from the private
sector (Hansson et al. 2019), (Ren & Liang 2017). Hence, it stands to reason that fining the CO2 emission
would make the e-fuels more attractive for the private sector.
At the same time, the authorities will likely increase the CO2” fines as they value the environmental aspect
higher than the economic.
It would be interesting to investigate what the cut-off point would be for the private sector, which would make
them view e-fuels more favourable to HFO.

7
Conclusion
This thesis has assessed the economic, technical and environmental attractiveness of an ammonia based fuel
solution, running on the AEGIR system. Through a literature study the cost estimates related to the CapEx and
OpEx were identified for a techno-economic analysis. Furthermore relevant non-economic KPIs were identified
for a multi-criteria decision analysis.
Through the techno-economic analysis the CapEx and OpEx were calculated for the AEGIR system running
on ammonia as well as the CapEx and OpEx for the alternative drivetrains methanol, hydrogen and HFO.
This was done in order to make a techno-economic comparison. Through this comparison it was found that
HFO was by far the cheapest alternative with a total cost of 62.9 M$. Ammonia was identified as the second
cheapest, with total cost of 208.1 M$. Hence, ammonia was 330% more expensive than HFO. Hydrogen had
a total cost of 214.2 M$, and the most expensive fuel was methanol with a total cost of 293.5 M$. Based on
the techno-economic analysis alone HFO appears to be the most attractive alternative, and ammonia the most
attractive e-fuel.
A decision support tool was created, which considered identified relevant KPIs, to assess the non-economic factors
of the drivetrains. This was done by using a multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA). The MCDA assesses the
social-political, technical, and environmental aspects of the different drivetrains. The drivetrains have been
assessed using 8 different sub-criteria, that was determined through the literature study to be relevant for the
analysis. These cover the main criteria environmental, technical, and social-political. The relative importance
of the criteria were determined based on previous MCDA studies and stakeholders identified in those studies.
Methanol was identified as the best performing alternative, shortly followed by hydrogen and then ammonia.
HFO was the worst alternative in the MCDA, performing significantly worse than the e-fuels.
Hence, based on the MCDA analysis alone ammonia seems to be a slightly less attractive alternative than the
other e-fuel, while still more attractive than HFO.
The overall assessment of the of the best alternative depend on how the decision maker(s) value cost as compared
to the criteria in the MCDA. From the perspective of the private industry, HFO was deemed as the most
favourable solution, followed by ammonia as the best e-fuel, due to them valuing cost higher than any other
criteria.
For authorities, it was deemed more likely to favour methanol, or hydrogen, as they value environmental and
social-political aspect higher than economic.
In conclusion, while ammonia does not score the highest within the MCDA, it does provide the benefit of being
the most cost-effective e-fuel for the private sector, and still performing significantly better than HFO within
environmental and social-political aspects.
Attributes were identified which could be added in further studies to strength the analysis. One such attribute
was CO2 fines, which are an important tool for governments to push the private industry towards a green future.
Also, additional alternatives could be included, like nuclear power, to assess the AEGIR systems attractiveness
against other alternatives than e-fuels and conventional fossil fuel.
In order to strengthen the techno-economic analysis and the MCDA it recommended that industry experts and
