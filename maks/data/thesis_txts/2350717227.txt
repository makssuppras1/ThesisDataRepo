Abstract
Knowledge on hydrological regimes of river basins is very useful for water managers, however data
requirements often limit the applicability of hydrological models in basins with little in-situ data.
Remote sensing provides a unique possibility to acquire information on hydrological variables
in poorly gauged river basins. This study explores how multi-mission remote sensing data can
inform hydrological models as model input and for parameter estimation. The fourth largest river
basin in Africa, the Ogooué basin in Gabon is used as study area. No previous modelling efforts
have been conducted for the basin and only historical ﬂow and precipitation observations are
available. A lumped conceptual rainfall-runoff model based on the Budyko framework is coupled
to a Muskingum routing scheme. Observations of precipitation, temperature, total water storage
change and water elevation are acquired from publicly available databases and incorporated into
the model setup.
1399 single CryoSat-2 altimetry observations and 48 ICESat observations are acquired using a
detailed Sentinel-1 river mask. Furthermore, water heights have been measured by the repeat-orbit
satellite missions Envisat and Jason-2 at 12 virtual stations along the river. The four missions show
a generally good agreement in terms of annual water height amplitudes. The amplitude throughout
the river varies between 2.8 m ± 0.9 and 3.2 m ± 1.5 based on the CryoSat-2 observations and
between 2.22 m and 4.72 m for the virtual stations. The altimetry observations are used to validate
the hydrological model of the Ogooué River. Furthermore, two satellite-based rainfall estimates
(SRFE) Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission (TRMM) and Famine Early Warning System -
Rainfall Estimate (FEWS-RFE) are compared in order to illustrate the impact of choosing between
different estimates of the same hydrological variable. The comparison shows good seasonal and
spatial agreement between the products, however TRMM consistently predicted signiﬁcantly more
precipitation (1726 mm on average per year against 1556 mm for FEWS-RFE). The products are
more similar in recent years than in the last decade. The products are also compared to each other
by using both to force the same model of the Ogooué.
Model calibration combines historical in-situ ﬂow observations and GRACE total water storage
observations from the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) mascon solution in a multi-objective
approach. The two models are calibrated using ﬂow duration curves and climatology benchmarks
to overcome the lack of simultaneity between the simulated and observed discharge. The multiple
objectives are aggregated into a global objective function, and the models are calibrated using the
Shufﬂed Complex Evolution Algorithm. The best results are obtained with the TRMM-driven
model (ﬁnal objective function value of 1.61 versus 1.65). Based on the calibration, the FEWS-RFE
model struggles to compensate for the increase in the mean annual precipitation over the basin
estimated by the FEWS-RFE product since 2011. The RMSE between the simulated and the
altimetry-based water heights varies between 0.8 and 1.5 for the TRMM model and between 0.84
and 1.64 for the FEWS-RFE model.
The study shows the potential of including remote sensing in poorly gauged basins, using the
observations to parametrise, force, calibrate and validate the model. The model can be used to
derive climate change scenarios or be coupled to other model components to extend its application.
The combination of hydrological modelling and remote sensing produces otherwise inaccessible
information. However, neither can completely replace direct observations and it is strongly
recommended to produce in-situ observations in order to validate and quantify uncertainty of the
remote sensing observations, and to evaluate model performance.

Preface
This Master thesis project was carried out at the Department of Environmental Engineering at the
Technical University of Denmark, DTU Environment, under the supervision of Professor Peter
Bauer-Gottwein. The thesis accounts for 35 ECTS and lasted from June 2016 to December 2016.
The project focuses on using satellite-derived data to force and improve parameter estimation of a
large-scale hydrological model and is carried in extension of DTU’s contribution to the European
Space Agency project GlobalWetlands-Africa (GW-A), using the same river basin as a study case.
GW-A project is a joint initiative between ESA and Ramsar Convention and is carried out by
a consortium led by DHI GRAS (Hørsholm, Denmark), with DTU as one of the partners. The
project is part of the TIGER initiative which aims at assisting African countries in capacity building
surrounding the collection and use of Earth Observations, especially from the Sentinel missions,
as a source of information in water resources management, and focuses speciﬁcally on wetlands.
The strategy of choice is the implementation of a free and open-source toolbox and database, the
WOIS - Water Observation Information System. The WOIS includes a "River Basin Hydrology
Product", developed by DTU, which will carry out simulations using hydrological modelling and
integrating remote sensing observations to improve model performance. This thesis investigates the
potential for modelling hydrological states in the Ogooué River, Gabon, using a lumped conceptual
hydrological model and integrating remotely sensed data at various stages of model setup. Free,
open-source software and data are used exclusively (DHI-GRAS, 2016).
I would like to thank my supervisor, Professor Peter Bauer-Gottwein for his excellent guidance
and availability. I also wish to thank Jonas W. Pedersen and Raphael Schneider for sharing their
experience with routing methods and radar altimetry data.
On a more personal note, I wish to thank the excellent people I have met at DTU, especially Maite
Lovring for being my partner in crime - and engineering - during our years at DTU and beyond and
Felix Onses, for believing in me, lovingly pushing me to do my best - and having my back when
things looked glum.
Finally thank you to my family, for loving and supporting me always, and in particular to my

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The impact of climate change and developing human activities on river regimes observed over the
last decades, poses important challenges for water resources managers globally (Mahe et al., 2013).
While the demand for water resources has increased, in-situ gauging networks have thinned out
(Berry et al., 2012). In poorly gauged areas, hydrological models can increase the understanding
of the hydrological regime and its vulnerability to changes (Awange et al., 2014), or serve as
operational forecasting tools (Bauer-Gottwein et al., 2015). However, computational simulations
cannot substitute observations, and physical observations (precipitation, temperature etc.) are
required to force and calibrate hydrological models and crucial to produce useful simulations. In
this context, remote sensing is a very interesting solution as it provides gridded data in near real
time with extensive geographical coverage for a variety of hydrological variables (Tang et al.,
2009).
In recent years signiﬁcant progress has been made in terms of integrating hydrological modelling
and remote sensing data: almost all components of the water cycle can be inferred from remote
sensing observations (Xu et al., 2014). Whether remote sensing estimates of hydrological variables
are used to force a model or facilitate parameter and state estimation, the data products can
supplement and, to some extent, replace in-situ observations, where these are insufﬁcient or
impossible to acquire (Xie et al., 2012; Knoche et al., 2014). As more remote sensing-based
estimates of hydrological variables become globally and publicly available, the need for sound and
scientiﬁcally founded methods to integrate remote sensing observations with hydrological models
increases (van Griensven et al., 2012).
This study explores how multi-mission remote sensing data can inform hydrological modelling
by investigating several data types and monitoring missions. In the following section, a literature
review presenting examples of how remote sensing has been used in hydrological studies is
presented, followed by the research objectives behind this study.
1.1
Literature Review
Earth observations have been used to monitor large scale water bodies for the past decades (Berry
et al., 2012). The development of advanced instruments has increased the diversity of hydrological
variables observable from space and the quality of the observations (Xu et al., 2014). Most of the
major components of the land-surface water balance can be estimated from satellite remote sensing,
with the exception of river discharge (Tang et al., 2009). State variables, which are difﬁcult or
1
==PAGE:16==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
tedious to measure on ground, such as total water storage or soil moisture, can be sensed from
space for large regions. Furthermore, remote sensing provides access to isolated and inaccessible
regions (Moiwo et al., 2011).
Precipitation, temperature and actual evapotranspiration products are derived by combining records
from several instruments and sensors including thermal imagers, passive microwave sensors and
satellite borne radars (Stisen et al., 2008; Thiemig et al., 2013; Gellens-Meulenberghs et al.,
2012; Huffman et al., 2007). Soil moisture is inferred by rescaling passive and active microwave
measurements of surface soil moisture (Brocca et al., 2010; Meier et al., 2011; Wagner et al., 2007).
Total water storage changes is estimated from gravimetric observations of changes in the Earth’s
gravity ﬁeld by the Gravity Recovery and climate Experiment (GRACE) satellite (Landerer and
Swenson, 2012; Longuevergne et al., 2010; Werth, 2010). Water stage can be derived from radar
altimetry data (Berry et al., 2005). The literature is extensive and the following review covers only
selected studies. Comprehensive literature reviews have been published on the speciﬁc technologies
and extraction algorithms, for instance Tang et al. (2009) and Xu et al. (2014).
The contributions of remote sensing information to hydrological modelling can be grouped in three
general strategies according to Xu et al. (2014): model inputs, parameter estimation and state
estimation. State estimation - or data assimilation, constrains model simulations with observations
to improve the state variable (Xu et al., 2014). Data assimilation has been used in a range of studies,
for instance the studies by Michailovsky et al. (2013); Brocca et al. (2010); Han et al. (2012) and
Laiolo et al. (2015). This study focuses on the use of remote sensing data as model inputs and for
parameter estimation. Data assimilation is outside the scope of this study. The reader is referred to
pages 3-6 in Xu et al. (2014) instead, for a review of the speciﬁc methods and previous studies on
the topic.
The ﬁrst, historically dominant approach is the use of remote sensing products as model inputs.
Earth observations have been used to deduce necessary but often unavailable information required
to delineate the river network or parametrise the model. Bathymetry and river geometry can be
estimated from digital elevation models and satellite imagery (Schneider et al., 2016; Pereira-
Cardenal et al., 2011). Remote sensing precipitation and temperature products have been used to
drive hydrological models of poorly gauged basins, for instance in Bauer-Gottwein et al. (2015);
Michailovsky et al. (2013); Michailovsky and Bauer-Gottwein (2014) and Stisen et al. (2008). The
main challenges in terms of using remotely sensed forcing data are the difﬁculty to characterise
input-data bias and uncertainty and their propagation into the hydrological simulations (Xu et al.,
2014; Awange et al., 2016).
Indeed, although several products are available, the selection is not obvious: Ground truthing and
uncertainty quantiﬁcation is often complicated by the lack of gauging stations. Thiemig et al.
(2013); Stisen and Sandholt (2010); Cohen Liechti et al. (2012) and Awange et al. (2016) amongst
others have evaluated the performance of selected precipitation products over the African continent,
and from their studies, it is evident that the differences between the products often depend on the
study area. Because of the crucial role of precipitation in driving the land-surface water balance,
precipitation datasets in particular are often evaluated through their performance as model input
(Awange et al., 2016). For instance, Milzow et al. (2011) identiﬁed differences between the Famine
Early Warning System - Rainfall Estimate (FEWS-RFE), the Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission
re-analysis daily product 3B42, version 7 (TRMM 3B42 v.7) and ERA-Interim using historical
in-situ data and a Soil and Water Assessment Tool (SWAT) model of the Okavango catchment, and
obtained the best results from a FEWS-RFE driven model. Cohen Liechti et al. (2012), on the other
hand favoured TRMM over FEWS-RFE due to the lower autocorrelation in the dataset over the
Zambezi river basin. Stisen and Sandholt (2010) compared several rainfall products using a MIKE

SHE model of the Senegal River and concluded that models calibrated with in-situ data performed
worse than re-calibrated models. Awange et al. (2016) suggest that the impact of using different
forcing inputs can be buffered by the models. Instead, they recommend evaluating the sensitivity of
multiple outputs (e.g. groundwater recharge and actual evapotranspiration) to assess the effect of
different data sets and uncover any interdependency between model evaluation and data.
The second strategy for using remote sensing in hydrological modelling, is the use of remote sensing
data to calibrate model parameters and validate model performance. Hydrological models often
contain conceptual parameters, which are either impossible or impractical to measure directly (Xu
et al., 2014). In order to estimate the best-ﬁtting parameter values - and subsequently evaluate model
performance - observations of hydrological variables are required. Traditionally, hydrological
models use discharge measurements to calibrate and validate hydrological models (Bauer-Gottwein
et al., 2015; Knoche et al., 2014). However in many river basins, in-situ data is limited or insufﬁcient
(Berry et al., 2012). The use of radar altimetry to infer river levels is a relatively new ﬁeld of
research (Schumann and Domeneghetti, 2016). Indeed, the utility of the observations over narrow
water bodies are limited by the footprint of the altimeter and consequent topographical noise. Since
the 1990s, technological advances and the development of speciﬁc extraction algorithms in research
have enabled the extraction of radar altimetry observations of water heights over inland water
bodies. Thereby, the monitoring of remote or poorly gauged rivers has greatly improved (Getirana,
2010). Birkinshaw et al. (2010) retracked Envisat stage measurements for the Mekong river with
RMSE values against observations from adjacent gauging stations of 0.44 and 0.65. Using a
detailed local water mask Michailovsky et al. (2012) extracted Envisat water height measurements
for the Zambezi river at river widths down to 80m. The coarse spatio-temporal resolution can be
circumvented by using a multi-mission approach in combination with in-situ data, when possible.
Tourian et al. (2016) used this approach to densify water level observations over the Po river
in Italy and transferred their methods to the Congo, Mississippi and Danube with good results.
Although river discharge cannot be directly inferred from remote sensing data, Bjerklie et al. (2003)
and Birkinshaw et al. (2010) developed stage-discharge rating curves using geographically close
gauge-stations to obtain discharge time series from altimetry measurements.
Due to the long return periods, traditional time series at speciﬁc sites are not a suitable approach
when working with drifting orbit satellites such as CryoSat-2 and ICESat. For this reason, few
previous studies have used CryoSat-2 or ICESat data. Research has focused on larger water bodies,
mainly lakes, over which meaningful time series can be extracted from the 30-day subcycles - for
instance Kleinherenbrink et al. (2014); Villadsen et al. (2016). However, Villadsen et al. (2015)
recently extracted water levels over the Brahmaputra and validated the data by linearly interpolating
the observations to the nearest Envisat virtual station due to the lack of in-situ observations. ICESat
has been used primarily as a substitute for in-situ data on lake levels (O’Loughlin et al., 2016),
although O’Loughlin et al. (2013) used ICESat data to estimate water surface slopes of the Congo
River.
Radar altimetry has been used in several studies to inform hydrological models both for calibration
and in data assimilation schemes (Michailovsky et al., 2013; Milzow et al., 2011). For instance
Getirana (2010) and Getirana and Peters-Lidard (2013) both used radar altimetry over parts of the
Amazon for model calibration. The use of radar altimetry in hydrological applications is facilitated
by the development of free and global databases providing processed water elevation observations
for inland water bodies (Schwatke et al., 2015; Berry et al., 2005; Santos da Silva et al., 2010).
Schneider et al. (2016) used a combination of Envisat virtual stations and Villadsen et al. (2015)’s
CryoSat-2 data to calibrate a routing model of the Brahmaputra River. Finally, Domeneghetti et al.
(2014) used in-situ and remotely-sensed water levels and a combination of the two to calibrate
a pseudo two-dimensional hydraulic model of the Po river. The best results were obtained with

Several studies have explored the possibilities of using remote sensing-based observations of other
hydrological variables instead of or as a supplement to discharge measurements. Milzow et al.
(2011) used GRACE total water storage combined with Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) soil
moisture and radar altimetry to calibrate a SWAT model of the Okavango river, using a multi-
objective optimisation approach. Model parametrisation was improved by exploring multiple
hydrological state variables Alvarez-Garreton et al. (2014) used in-situ discharge and soil moisture
in a multi-objective calibration. Xie et al. (2012) circumvented the lack of gauging data in Sub-
Saharan Africa by using GRACE data to evaluate and calibrate a large scale SWAT model of the
region, improving performance in humid areas. Abelen and Seitz (2013) used GRACE data and
correlation analysis to validate global soil moisture products, including soil moisture products from
the active microwave sensor Advanced Scatterometer (ASCAT) and from the global hydrological
model WGHM. They showed a strong correlation between GRACE and soil moisture in humid
and temperate regions. They also showed a stronger correlation between the remote sensing
products than between the soil moisture products in certain regions. The study used remote sensing
products for validation to circumvent data paucity. Mulder et al. (2015) used GRACE data to
validate a TRMM-driven rainfall-runoff model of Northern Iraq. The model was parametrised
using Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) and satellite altimetry data. The
GRACE observations of mass loss to the simulated groundwater depletion, and good agreement
was achieved between the two datasets’ annual cycle and long-term cycle.
The choice of model determines the input requirements as well as the level of parametrisation, as
both increase with model complexity. The study by Stisen and Sandholt (2010) used the fully-
distributed physically based MIKE-SHE model and Schneider et al. (2016) calibrated a MIKE
11 1D hydrodynamic model coupled to a rainfall-runoff model of the Brahmaputra river against
CryoSat-2 observations. Other studies have used the semi-distributed SWAT model, for instance
Milzow et al. (2011) and Xie et al. (2012). Finally, several studies have used simpler, lumped
conceptual rainfall-runoff models, such as the heuristic Budyko-type model used by Finsen et al.
(2014) or the more physically-based NAM model used by Pereira-Cardenal et al. (2011). Pereira-
Cardenal et al. (2011) determined stable and well-deﬁned parameters in spite of limited in-situ data
by using a simpler modelling approach. Whilst gridded remote sensing data offers the possibility
to parametrise and drive fully-distributed model with high spatio-temporal resolution, the choice of
model must reﬂect the user requirements and capacities as well as the availability and uncertainty
of the observations used to deﬁne the model (Johnston and Smakhtin, 2014).
1.2
Objective
While several studies have investigated the beneﬁts of using a single type of remote sensing data
to supplement in-situ data, few studies have combined several remote sensing data types with
available in-situ data to inform hydrological models (Milzow et al., 2011). This study investigates
how multi-mission remote sensing data can be used to inform hydrological models in poorly gauged
areas. A multi-objective calibration and validation approach combining multi-mission remote
sensing data with in-situ data is expected to have a synergistic effect on model performance, as
different data types will constrain different parameters and hydrological processes (Milzow et al.,
2011; Xie et al., 2012). The following research objectives are deﬁned:
• Explore and assess the availability of remote sensing data products and their applicability for
hydrological models as forcing data and for parameter estimation
4
==PAGE:19==
1.2. OBJECTIVE
• Develop a lumped conceptual model framework combined with a routing component and a
complexity in line with data availability
• Develop a basin scale hydrological modelling strategy including a calibration approach
• Apply the above for a study case, assessing the beneﬁts of integrating remote sensing
observations into a hydrological model in a poorly gauged area.
The objectives are tested for the Ogooué River in Gabon. The river basin was selected as pilot site
for the GW-A project River Basin Hydrology product (see Preface). The river is an interesting
study area as it is the fourth largest African river by discharge volume but lacks previous modelling
efforts and is poorly gauged (Mahe et al., 2013). This provides the opportunity to evaluate the
potential of the methods applied in this study for a poorly gauged basin using only free, publicly
available data. However, the methods could in theory be applied to any other river basin. Several of
the products used in this study have not yet been used in the proposed form: e.g. no previous studies
have considered CryoSat-2 and ICESat observations to assess hydrological models of African rivers
and no previous studies have used Sentinel-1 radar imagery to extract altimetry observations over
water bodies.
This study uses a Budyko-type rainfall-runoff model coupled to a Muskingum routing scheme. The
method is speciﬁcally selected and developed to integrate remote sensing data into hydrological
modelling and could be applied in the context of GW-A or similar water resources projects in
poorly gauged regions. The method has the advantage of being parsimoniously parametrised and
can be programmed directly allowing for a larger ﬂexibility than a grey-box model such as SWAT.
However, the model does not offer water quality and transport features - unlike the SWAT model,
which has been proposed for GW-A - and whilst climate change scenarios can be evaluated with
the proposed framework, landuse change scenarios cannot. These features were outside the scope
of the present study.
The case study is introduced in Chapter 2, the remote sensing data and processing methods applied
are described in Chapter 3, along with the selected modelling strategy. Results are presented and
discussed in Chapter 4, and Chapter 5 sums up the main conclusions from this project.

CHAPTER 2
Study Area
The Ogooué river is the fourth largest river in Africa by volume of discharge with a mean annual
ﬂow rate of 4700 m3/s. It is 1200 km long and drains approximately 224 000 km2 of which almost
90% lie within Gabon. Tributaries reach into Equatorial Guinea to the West, Cameroon to the
North and the Republic of Congo to the East and South, however as the majority of the basin lies
within Gabon, it is the most signiﬁcant riparian country. The basin is characterised by plateaus and
hills bordering a narrow coastal plain. Although the hills are not very high (mean elevation in the
catchment is 450 m), the slopes are very steep, with cliffs several hundred meters above the plain
below, creating characteristic chutes and rapids (Figure 2.1).
The climate in the basin is equatorial, with two rain seasons: February to May and October to
December, with close to no precipitation in the austral winter. Mean annual precipitation is 1831
mm and varies between 1400 and 3800 mm with temperatures between 21 and 28°C (Mengue
Medou et al., 2009; Carre, 1978). The Ogooué river network is permanent and none of the
main streams dry out during the dry season. Most of the basin lies on Precambrian sediments,
with a coastal belt of sedimentary rocks rich in resources, including iron, gold, manganese and
silver (Upton and Ò Dochartaigh, 2016; Mezui and Boumono Moukoumi, 2013). The basin is
characterised by a dense vegetative cover. In the lower delta, the Bas-Ogooué consists of a large
ﬂuvial plain with a dense network of lakes and rivers (Mengue Medou et al., 2008b).
Ramsar sites are wetlands of international importance as deﬁned by the Ramsar Convention. The
latter is also known as the Wetlands Convention, an international framework for the conservation
and sustainable use of wetlands resources (Mengue Medou et al., 2008b). In total ﬁve of the
Gabonese Ramsar sites are part of the Ogooué catchment: e.g. the chutes and rapids of the Ivindo,
the Mboungou Badouma and Doumé Rapids in the upper Ogooué (Haut-Ogooué) and the Bas-
Ogooué (lower Ogooué). The sites are classiﬁed under the Ramsar Convention due to their diverse
ecosystems, including thousands of endemic species, and their cultural importance. Marshlands
stretch across the river and lake bank forming the Ogooué Floodplain (Mengue Medou et al., 2009,
2008b,a).
African wetlands are some of the most biologically diverse and productive ecosystems, hosting a
large variety of species. They provide important ecosystem services including water puriﬁcation
and ﬂood control. Human activities, particularly agriculture and new settlements are putting a
strain on wetlands, and sustainable management and sound protection are required (DHI-GRAS,
2016). Excessive pressure on wetlands could have devastating effects for their ecosystems and

2.1
The Ogooué
The Ogooué rises in the Ntalé mountains on the Batéké Plateau in Congo. The plateau is between
550 m and 830 m high and an ancient volcanic area. It is characterised by a constellation of soft
sandstones with intercalated clay layers discontinuously overlying the Precambrian Francevillian
formations. This characteristic layout has helped form important aquifers (Carre, 1978; Upton and
Ò Dochartaigh, 2016).
The river runs Northwest into Gabon with a steep turn at Franceville. Between Lastourville and
Ndjolé, the river is not navigable due to rapids and waterfalls created by the steep slopes and

broken terrain. This part of the basin is dominated by volcano-sedimentary and metamorphic
formations setting the scene for the characteristic rapids and chutes of the region. In some rocky
areas, weathering may create fractures, increasing groundwater potential especially in the limestone
series, while large areas with high clay content in soil are highly impermeable, and prone to
mudslides during the rainy seasons (Mengue Medou et al., 2009; Carre, 1978).
After Ndjolé, the river runs west and reaches the Gulf of Guinea south of the town Port-Gentil in the
Ogooué Delta, which is itself close to 100 km wide and 100 km long. The lower part of the Ogooué
is navigable and gentler than the rest of the river, with relatively low bedslopes (0.13-0.07 m/km
according to Mengue Medou et al. (2008b)). The lower Ogooué and its inner delta are underlain by
impermeable bedrock, however the overlying humus and dense vegetation cover help to balance
this by slowing down surface runoff and facilitating inﬁltration in the root-zone (Mengue Medou
et al., 2008b). The coastal sand belts and ﬂuvial alluvium overlying the bedrock in the lower Delta
are directly recharged by rainfall inﬁltration. The aquifer rests on a salt water ﬁlm and was 25m
thick at Port-Gentil in 1989. (UN, 1989).
The river has numerous tributaries. The largest is the Ivindo, which ﬂows from Northeast to
Southwest Gabon before draining into the Ogooué. The upstream slope of the river is gentle until it
drops off the Eastern Gabon plateau into a series of waterfalls and gorges just below the town of
Makokou. The second largest tributary is the Ngounié, which ﬂows from the Chaillu Mountains
along Gabon’s southern border before draining into the Ogooué upstream of Lambaréné (Figure
2.1). Crystalline and crystallophyllian basement rock form the North-Gabon massif (which contains
important iron resources), the Chaillu Mountains separating the Ngounié and the Ogooué and the
Mayumbe Mountains to the west (UN, 1989). The Ngounié basin, like the central Ogooué basin,
consist of metamorphic schists, sandstone schists and argillite (UN, 1989). The Chaillu Mountains
create a sub-climate around the Ngounié basin, which receives more precipitation on average than
the rest of the country (Mengue Medou et al., 2009).
The dense vegetation cover across the basin attenuates the potential ﬂooding from the heavy rain
in the two rainy seasons, and the basin is not particularly prone to ﬂooding. Large portions of the
river are fed by baseﬂow during the drier austral winter months, when cooler temperatures greatly
reduce evapotranspiration (Mengue Medou et al., 2008a).
2.2
Gabon
Gabon is sparsely populated and with around 80% of the 1.725 million inhabitants distributed in the
three largest urban centres, the capital Libreville, Port-Gentil and the Franceville-Moanda-Mounana
area, Gabon has one of the highest urbanisation rates in Africa. The abundance of petroleum and
other natural resources combined with the low population density, has helped Gabon become one
of the most prosperous sub-Saharan African countries. The oil industry accounts for 20.6% of the
real gross domestic product (GDP) only surpassed by the service sector (57.8%). However, due to
a skewed income distribution and high unemployment, a signiﬁcant proportion of the population
(22.7% in 2014) lives with an income below the guaranteed minimum wage. Furthermore the
agricultural sector remains underdeveloped, accounting for 6.3% of the national GDP (World Bank,
2016).
In recent years, oil production has rapidly declined due to depletion of resources and the decline in
global oil prices, slowing public investments and national growth. The access to social services
including electricity, health and drinking water has decreased, affecting especially the poorest
part of the population (World Bank, 2016). A strategic plan for 2011-2016, the "Plan Stratégique

presented to help transform Gabon into an emerging country by accelerating economic growth

The Ogooué river basin plays a signiﬁcant role in development plans, both as part of the energy

mainly due to the lack of precedent of modelling efforts of the basin.
2.3
Previous Studies
No previous modelling efforts are found as of Fall 2016 in spite of a thorough literature search
throughout databases including Web of Science, SCOPUS and Google Scholar. Published studies
on the basin are limited to larger-scale studies of African rivers, as in Mahe and Olivry (1999)
and Mahe et al. (2013). Public reports indicate that efforts were made in the 1950s to build and
maintain a hydrological monitoring network, which was maintained through the 1960s and 1970s
with annual ﬂow reports. The reports note a poor coverage of the rain gauge network and lack of
knowledge of hydrogeological and topographical gradients (Carre, 1978). With the exception of
data recorded in the 2000s at the Lambaréné drinking water plant obtained by Mahe et al. (2013), no
data recorded after 1984 has been published. Similarly, no recent ofﬁcial reports from the country
concerning water resources were found. The lack of precedent in modelling efforts combined with
the relatively undisturbed nature of the river basin, make for a suitable case study on the potential
of using earth observations to force a hydrological model of a poorly gauged river basin.

Data and Methods
Observation records are required to parametrise and force hydrological models. In sparsely gauged
or remote areas, earth observations or satellite data provide a unique opportunity to monitor
large areas and create continuous, gridded time series. Several components of the water balance,
including precipitation, temperature, soil moisture, water storage variations and water height, are
now observable via remote sensing (Tang et al., 2009).
This section outlines the strategies used in this study to incorporate Earth observations in a
hydrological modelling context. First, the data inputs used in this study are reviewed, then the
modelling frameworks applied in the study are described along with calibration and performance
evaluation methods.
3.1
Observations of Hydrological States and Fluxes
The hydrological model used in this study requires two types of inputs: climate forcing and
parametrisation of the river basin including information on subbasin latitudes and areas, reach
lengths and bed slope. Furthermore, observations of hydrological state variables are needed to
optimise the model parameters. This section describes the datasets used in this study and the
methods employed to process them.
3.1.1
Digital Elevation Model
No in-situ information is available on the bathymetry and water levels of the Ogooué river. There-
fore, the Shuttle Radar Topography Mission v. 4.1 (SRTM) DEM at a 3-sec (∼90 m) resolution is
used to delineate the watershed and derive the Ogooué river network’s course (Jarvis et al., 2008).
The DEM also provides initial guesses of river bed slope and geometrical parameters (Neitsch
et al., 2009). The hydroprocessing routines are part of the QSWAT plugin for QGIS (Srinivasan and
George, 2015). 5° tiles are downloaded, merged and cropped to the area of interest. The resolution
is adjusted to approximately 1 km in order to comply with processing and calculation capabilities
of the river network delineation routines (a maximum 2 million pixels can be processed).

3.1.3
Precipitation
In this report, observation and estimation are both used to qualify the precipitation values obtained
from remote sensing missions. Time series of estimates produced from a given centre or mission
and available from databases are qualiﬁed as products.
Satellite-based Precipitation products
Precipitation is a crucial driver of the land hydrological cycle and accurate observations are required
to produce predictions of surface water ﬂuxes and state variables (Thiemig et al., 2013). As the
coverage of rain-gauge networks has globally declined, Satellite-based Rainfall Estimates (SRFE)
are increasingly used as model forcing (Tang et al., 2009). SRFE products offer spatially distributed
datasets in near real time (with a delay ranging from a few days to two months) even in sparsely
gauged regions, with several free products available. SRFE can be obtained from geostationary
thermal infrared (IR), passive microwave, radar (active microwave) and rain-gauges. The strength
of SRFE products lies in the combination of different data sources, taking advantage of their
respective strengths to compensate for their weaknesses, as summarised in Thiemig et al. (2013)
and (Xu et al., 2014). This study uses two widely tested and used SRFEs: the Famine Early
Warning System - Rainfall Estimate 2.0 (FEWS-RFE) and the Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission
3B42 v.7 (TRMM) as precipitation forcing.
FEWS-RFE is a National Oceanic Atmospheric Agency - Climate Prediction Centre (NOAA-
CPC) product. Version 2.0 is operational since 2001. The product is designed speciﬁcally for the
African continent (40°N - 40°S, 20°W - 55°E) at a 0.1° resolution and 3-hour temporal resolution.
The data is available near real-time with a few days delay. Precipitation is approximated from
several individual satellite sources including Geostationary IR and microwave measurements. The
approximation is adjusted using GTS (Global Telecommunication System) interpolated rainfall
ﬁelds from roughly 1000 gauge-stations USGS (2016); Thiemig et al. (2013).
TRMM is a joint NASA-JAXA (Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency) mission launched in late
1997 and was operational until April 15, 2015. The TRMM satellite collected data using three
different on-board instruments for monitoring rainfall: a Precipitation Radar (PR), a TRMM
Microwave Imager (TMI) and a Visible and InfraRed Scanner (VIRS). The TMI-based estimates
are merged and calibrated, combined with VIRS-estimates and rescaled using monthly Climate
Anomaly Monitoring System (CAMS) and Global Precipitation Climatology Project (GPCP)
data. The 3B42-product has a 3-hour temporal resolution and a 0.25° x 0.25° spatial resolution
covering the globe between 50°N and 50°S with up to two months delay (Thiemig et al., 2013).

Although the TRMM-satellite was decommissioned in 2015, TRMM 3B42 v.7 data will still
be produced until mid-2017 as part of the TRMM to GPM (Global Precipitation Measurement)
transition. The GPM builds on the TRMM heritage but will integrate new instruments to improve
the precipitation estimates. Currently, the TRMM 3B42 v.7 precipitation product is produced
from GPM observations, but a retrospective GPM re-analysis of the TRMM dataset is expected to
eventually replace TRMM in the next years (Huffman, 2015).
Gebregiorgis et al. (2012) showed a very high correlation between runoff error and precipitation
misses (85%), highlighting the importance of accurate precipitation estimates. One signiﬁcant
challenge in terms of using remote sensing-based precipitation estimates is the characterisation
of uncertainties and bias, and how these propagate into the hydrological model. Xu et al. (2014)
conclude that the propagation of precipitation data errors depends on the magnitude and nature of
the error (systematic or random), the season and the characteristics of the basin. Several studies
have evaluated the performance of SRFE products in hydrological modelling contexts. While there
have been no speciﬁc studies of the Ogooué, other African river basins and poorly gauged regions
have been used as study cases. There are generally two validation approaches: ground truthing or
model-based validation, also termed hydrological validation (Thiemig et al., 2013).
Awange et al. (2016) concluded that rain-gauge merged products (such as TRMM 3B42 v.7 or
FEWS-RFE) performed better over the African continent than satellite-only products. TRMM also
performed well compared to the other products evaluated, which was supported by the literature.
Milzow et al. (2011) and Cohen Liechti et al. (2012) compared satellite-derived estimates to ground
data in the Okavango River basin and Zambezi River basin respectively. FEWS-RFE estimates
were closest to monthly average measurements in the Okavango basin, while TRMM and FEWS-
RFE performed similarly in the Zambezi basin. Cohen Liechti et al. (2012) favoured TRMM as
input to their hydrological/hydraulic model based on the longer time period covered and lower
inter-correlation. Satgé et al. (2015) compared nine SRFEs over the South American Andean
plateau and showed TRMM 3B42 v.7 performed the best, especially after bias correction against
in-situ data and Sahoo et al. (2015) evaluated the performance of four different TRMM datasets in
the context of assessing large-scale meteorological droughts. TRMM 3B42 v.7 performed better
than the previous generation product (3B42 v. 6) and the operational 3B42RT v.7 product, when
considering spatial patterns and magnitudes of precipitation.
Studies such as Thiemig et al. (2013) and Stisen and Sandholt (2010) have analysed the performance
of different SRFE (including FEWS-RFE 2.0, TRMM 3B42 v.6) as forcing to hydrological models
of selected African river basins and their capability in reproducing observed streamﬂow. For
lowland regions, Thiemig et al. (2013) found the best results were obtained from FEWS-RFE and
TRMM 3B42, especially during high-ﬂow season, while Stisen and Sandholt (2010) concluded
the performance of regional products, including FEWS-RFE, was generally superior compared to
global products such as TRMM. In both studies, bias-correction and recalibration of the model
improved performance. However, as Knoche et al. (2014) note, although model performance
may improve when considering runoff alone, other water balance components may be negatively
affected in return. Knoche et al. (2014) found semi-distributed models were less sensitive to input
data uncertainties than distributed models, however care should be taken when evaluating model
performance based on remotely sensed input data, as data uncertainties are generally buffered by
the model. This issue can be circumvented by analysing hydrological variables in addition to the
hydrograph, especially in poorly gauged regions.
Ground truthing requires simultaneous on-ground observations and a relatively dense gauge-station
network. However, only decades old historical observations of precipitation are available, covering
a very limited section of the basin. Therefore the model is calibrated using both products to evaluate

Data Comparison and Processing
The hydrological model requires one continuous time series of precipitation and minimum and
maximum temperature for each subbasin. The climate data is therefore aggregated to daily observa-
tions. Two daily temperature observation time series are obtained by selecting the minimum and
maximum temperature from the four daily temperature sets provided by ECMWF. The temperature
is converted to °C. Daily precipitation is obtained by accumulating precipitation over 24 hours. The
gridded data is transformed to point data using zonal statistics with a spatial resolution of 0.005°.
Fictional weather stations are placed at the centroid coordinates of each subbasin. The zonal mean
is calculated for each daily gridded observation ﬁle within each subbasin and used as forcing input
to the hydrological model. As there is no overlap between in-situ data and remote sensing data,
and long-term changes in precipitation patterns have been observed, no bias-correction based on
on-ground measurements can be applied to the SRFE data. Furthermore, as no previous studies
have evaluated the performance of the two products against each other in the Ogooué basin, both
are considered.

to point data using 20km buffer zones around the gauge-stations. The comparison focuses on
differences in terms of seasonal and spatial trends, and interannual variations, to overcome the lack
of simultaneity between observed and simulated data. The datasets are compared visually through
massplots and QQ-plots (Quantile-Quantile plots). Massplots show the accumulated precipitation
of one SRFE over the other, whilst the QQ-plots compare the probability distributions of the two

3.1.4
Total Water Storage
The Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment (GRACE) mission was launched in 2002 as a joint
mission between the USA and German space agencies, NASA and DLR (Deutsches Zentrum für
Luft- und Raumfahrt) respectively. The twin satellites map and monitor the changing gravity ﬁeld
of the Earth providing monthly measurements of change. The data has been used in a wide range of
applications from validating soil moisture measurements (Abelen and Seitz, 2013) and improving
drought indicator estimates in the USA (Houborg et al., 2012), to evaluating groundwater depletion
(Mulder et al., 2015; Forootan et al., 2014), ice cap mass losses and snow and glacier dynamics
(Moiwo et al., 2011; Seo et al., 2010) and total water storage at river basin scale (Xie et al., 2012;
Awange et al., 2014; Eicker et al., 2014).
The twin satellites trail each other at a distance of ∼200 km on the same polar orbit. Changes in
the Earth’s gravity ﬁeld affect the distance and speed between the two satellites, thus by precisely
tracking the positions of the two satellites and measuring the distance between them, changes in
mass distribution down to ∼1 cm equivalent water thickness can be intercepted (equivalent to a 1 cm
thick water layer at the land surface). GRACE is sensitive to near-surface mass redistribution and
cannot discriminate between geophysical mass movements such as earthquakes and hydrological
mass movements. Therefore the data is corrected for inﬂuences including atmospheric mass
redistribution, wind and pressure-driven ocean motions, tides etc., using ocean and climate models
amongst others. As the latter evolve, the full data series is reprocessed, continuously improving the
dataset (Longuevergne et al., 2010). The current release of GRACE land data is the ﬁfth release
and is available at http://grace.jpl.nasa.gov, supported by the NASA Measures Program.
Variations in the Earth’s gravitational ﬁeld can be represented as mass variations using spheri-
cal harmonic ﬁelds. This approach is widely used in satellite geodesy to represent the global
geomagnetic ﬁeld and expresses the gravitational potential of a non-spherical central body, the
Earth, with respect to a body-ﬁxed reference frame in terms of spherical harmonic expansion
(Watkins et al., 2015). The spherical harmonic geopotential coefﬁcients are then processed and
ﬁltered to produce time series of total water storage. Three centres produce spherical harmonic
data using three different solution strategies: the Centre for Space Research (CSR) in Texas, Geo-
ForschungsZentrum, Potsdam (GFZ) and the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL). A scaling-factor is
applied post-processing to compensate for the attenuation of small scale mass variations. Sakumura
et al. (2014) compared the three datasets and concluded that the best results were obtained by taking
the arithmetic mean between the three datasets. For more details on harmonic solutions, the reader
is referred to Watkins et al. (2015) for instance. The main disadvantage of this method is related to
the need for smoothing and or destriping algorithms to compensate for the poor observability of
east-west gradients due to the North-South ﬂight direction (Watkins et al., 2015).
An alternative approach to the harmonic solutions, is the use of mass concentration blocks (mascons)
as the native basis function to solve for gravity variations. Unlike the spherical harmonic ﬁelds,
each mascon has a speciﬁc geophysical location and appropriate constraints can be applied when
deducing the gravity solution (Watkins et al., 2015). Furthermore a better separation of land and
ocean is obtained with this approach. Mascons have shown good results in the context of river
basin studies, both when customised to speciﬁc river basins (e.g. Milzow et al. (2011) in the
Okavango) and when aggregating circular mascons (e.g. Mulder et al. (2015) for Northern Iraq).
There are several mascon solutions available: JPL provides a gridded time series, with a CRI
(Coastline Resolution Improvement) ﬁlter to separate land and ocean signals and the GRGS (Group
de Recherche de Géodésie Spatiale) of the CNES (Centre National d’Études Spatiales) provides
processed solutions over selected African river basins.

3.1.5
Radar Altimetry
Altimetry is a method to measure height from satellite using radar altimeters. The altimeter
transmits high frequency signals to the Earth and receives the echo from the surface. The return
echo is proportional to the altitude of the satellite above ground (range) and can be used to determine
the surface elevation. The travel distance is obtained by scaling the travel time measurement to the
speed of light. This measurement is called a "range R measurement". It is corrected for a range of
geophysical inﬂuences and instrumental errors. With precise knowledge of the satellite position,
the surface elevation relative to a reference surface is calculated as the satellite altitude minus the
range (Rosmorduc et al., 2011).
15

As the pulse leaves the radar and travels towards the ground, the beam widens. The "footprint
size" is the surface illuminated by the beam and can be several kilometres wide. It consists of a
multitude of echos from within this footprint reﬂected off varying heights with varying travelling
times. Classical altimetry is pulse-limited, meaning the satellite only records the signals reﬂected
by the surface between the time when the leading edge of the pulse hits the ground and the arrival
of the trailing edge of the pulse. On smooth surfaces, the strongest echo will come from the point
directly below the radar (also termed "nadir", with the remaining points in the footprint termed
"off-nadir") (Rosmorduc et al., 2011).
The "waveform" is the time evolution of the reﬂected power (Gommenginger et al., 2011). The
return signal is measured within a speciﬁc time-window split into equally sized bins, each of which
contain a measure of the power of the return echo. To ensure that the altimeter samples when the
pulse hits the surface, the analysis window of the instrument must be dynamically adjusted by the
on-board tracker. This process is termed "tracking". The tracker predicts the most likely position of
the next echo based on the most recently recorded echo (closed-loop tracking). For more recent
missions, the DORIS navigator system and a DEM are used to reposition the analysis window
(open-loop tracking). Closed-loop tracking is often problematic in very rough terrain (Rosmorduc
et al., 2011).
The waveform is processed on ground to retrieve the travel time between the satellite and the
ground and back (the return echo time delay), a process known as "retracking". This is done by
ﬁtting the shape of the waveform to a model function. The departure of the waveform’s leading
edge from the altimeter tracking gate (the epoch) is computed against the predicted travel time and
used to correct the satellite range measurement (Rosmorduc et al., 2011). The tracker range is then
combined with the retrieved epoch to obtain the ﬁnal range measurement. The retracking algorithm
can be physically based or purely empirical and there are several algorithms available, depending
on the purpose of study. Waveforms vary depending on the underlying surface: over smooth
surfaces, such as oceans, differentiation between nadir and off-nadir points is relatively simple
as the reﬂection from nadir will be the ﬁrst to travel back to the satellite and the strongest return
signal. Early altimetry missions used ocean waveform retracking algorithms only. However, over
rough surfaces, such as land surfaces, it is more challenging to separate nadir and off-nadir signals
and more complex processing schemes are required. The reader is referred to Gommenginger et al.
(2011) and Rosmorduc et al. (2011). The altimetry data used in this study has been pre-processed
prior to acquisition.
Radar altimetry was originally developed to monitor sea height, however as retracking algorithms
evolve, the raw satellite data recorded by past missions has been reprocessed, improving and
extending the altimetry time series and potential uses. For large water bodies (e.g. lakes or wide
rivers and ﬂoodplains), algorithms similar to those used to process ocean waveforms can be used.
However, for smaller water bodies the footprints will often cover non-water features such as river
banks. Because land waveforms have different shapes and water is usually more reﬂective than
land, the return signal can be speciﬁcally post-processed to extract the part of the footprint within
the water body. Santos da Silva et al. (2010) showed the superiority of new retracking algorithms
in extracting water heights from Envisat compared to classical ocean retrackers. Michailovsky et al.
(2012) showed that by adapting the retracking algorithm rather than using only ocean waveforms
and reﬁning the water mask, much smaller water bodies could be included.

HaiYang-2, Jason-2 and -3, SARAL/AltiKa and Sentinel-3. Historical records are available from
ended missions such as Jason-1 and the Envisat, ERS and ICESat missions. Processed datasets from
several of these missions are available for several rivers and lakes throughout the world in large

Repeat-orbit Satellites - Virtual Station Data
Time series of water level measurements can be extracted at so-called "virtual stations". Virtual
stations are speciﬁc locations along a river, where the satellite ground track and a water body
intersect. The advantage of this approach is that it resembles the use of gauge-stations, and
simpliﬁes data extraction as only speciﬁc locations are monitored by the satellite.
Data from two repeat orbit satellites, Envisat and Jason-2, was used in this study. Envisat was
an ESA satellite launched in 2002 to replace the ERS satellites, and operational until 2012 with
the objective of monitoring the Earth’s atmosphere and surface. The satellite carried a total of ten
instruments, including a radar altimeter and the DORIS location system. The satellite was on a
repeat orbit with an orbital period of 35 days. The SARAL mission replaces Envisat and follows
the Envisat repetitive ground track, the objective being to produce a continuous time series and
ﬁlling the gap between Envisat and the Sentinel-3 mission (launched in February 2016). However,
only raw data is available from the Saral mission, and data extraction and processing was deemed
outside the scope of this study. The newly launched Sentinel-3 mission is expected to increase the
usefulness of radar altimetry due to the high along track sampling rate and shorter return period
(Berry and Benveniste, 2013; Paiva et al., 2013). Sentinel-3 data is not yet publicly available at
the time of redaction, but will be published in December 2016. (Kramer, 2016). Jason-2 and
SARAL are complementary missions (Rosmorduc et al., 2011). Jason-2 replaces Jason-1 and
TOPEX/Poseidon as part of a cooperation between CNES, NASA, Eumetstat and NOAA. The
mission payload includes a Poseidon-3 altimeter to measure range, a radiometer and three location
systems. It is operational since 2008.
The data used in this study is obtained from the River&Lake and Hydroweb project databases and
includes altimetry measurements from the Envisat and Jason-2. In total there are 12 crossing points
(11 with the Envisat track and 1 with the Jason-2), shown in Figure 3.2. The River&Lake project
used a Global Land Cover Characterisation (GLCC) landuse river mask to select observations
over water. The waveforms were then compared to a theoretical set of template waveforms and
the retracking algorithm associated to the best ﬁt template was selected (Berry et al., 2005).
Observations over rivers down to 80m wide have been extracted with this approach (Michailovsky
et al., 2012). Two River&Lake stations lie within the river mask developed for this study. The
data is provided relative to the European Geoid Model, EGM96 geoid and reprojected onto the
EGM2008 geoid (Berry et al., 2005). The data is available on the ESA River&Lake project
homepage (http://tethys.eaprs.cse.dmu.ac.uk/ RiverLake/shared/main).

Drifting-orbit Satellites
Altimetry observations from drifting orbit satellites such as CryoSat-2 have never been speciﬁcally
extracted for the Ogooué basin. CryoSat-2 is a Synthetic Aperture Rader (SAR) satellite launched
in April 2010 by ESA to monitor the cryosphere and more speciﬁcally polar regions. The satellite
is unique for two reasons: its drifting orbit, which enables a high coverage of the polar regions, and
its SAR Interferometric Radar Altimeter (SIRAL) sensor. The SIRAL is the ﬁrst of its kind and
designed speciﬁcally to measure sea ice and icesheet elevations. The sensor can operate in three
modes, depending on the level of interest for the monitored region: low-resolution mode (LRM),
SAR mode and SAR interferometer mode (SIN). In LRM, the satellite operates as a classical radar
altimeter. In SAR mode, it sends out bursts of pulses at very short intervals relative to conventional
altimeters and separates the data into 250 m wide strips. Successive strips are superimposed and
averaged to reduce noise. The footprint in SAR is ∼280 m compared to 1.5 km in LRM mode.
The smaller footprint greatly decreases the risk of contamination from surrounding land when

extracting stage observations. In SIN mode, a second antenna receives the echo simultaneously
and by comparing the path-length of the radar wave, off-nadir positions can be corrected. Orbital
position of the satellite is determined by a radio transmitter and a laser retroﬂector.
Due to its drifting orbit, CryoSat-2 has a very long repeat cycle (369 days), forcing a different
approach from the virtual stations paradigm. However, CryoSat-2 follows a track slightly shifted
from the previous one every 30 days. Thus, a spatially distributed time series of river height
with high spatial coverage can be obtained from the frequent overpasses. By treating the river
as a continuous stretch, these data can be used to provide important information on water level
throughout the river.
CryoSat-2 Level 2 data is obtained from the National Space Institute, Technical University of
Denmark (DTU Space) for the period 17/07/2010 to 21/02/2015. The dataset provided is relative to
the WGS84 ellipsoid and projected onto the EGM2008 geoid. The data has been processed using a
primary peak retracking algorithm (Jain et al., 2015). The Ogooué basin is only covered in LRM,
but as the East to West ﬂow direction is perpendicular to the satellite tracks, CryoSat-2 overpasses
the river basin hundreds of times within this time period. The larger footprint increases the risk
of reﬂections from surrounding land surfaces and of off-ranging. When the altimeter operates in
LRM the pulse-limited footprint is circular, as with a traditional altimeter, and both across-track
(snagging) and along-track (hoiking) off-nadir signals can occur (Pedersen, 2016).
ICESat (Ice Cloud and land Elevation Satellite) was a NASA satellite in orbit from 2003 to
2009. Its main objective was ice sheet monitoring and measuring cloud and aerosol heights as
well as characterising land topography and vegetation characteristics. ICESat has an irregular
repeat cycle. ICESat was the ﬁrst laser-altimeter satellite. Laser altimeters have smaller ground
footprint compared to radar altimeters, with a footprint of ∼70 m compared to Envisat’s ∼400
m and Jason-2’s ∼300 m. O’Loughlin et al. (2016) have created the Inland Water Surface spot
Heights (IWSH) database, available for download on the University of Bristol data webpage
(http://data.bris.ac.uk/data/dataset/15hbqgewcrti51hmzp69bi4gky). The altimetry observations
have been ﬁltered over a global inland water body mask and averaged over transects. The data is
relative to the EGM96 geoid and is therefore reprojected onto the EGM2008 to avoid geoid-related
bias.
River Mask: Combining Radar Altimetry and Imagery
For small inland water bodies no inherent information is available within the altimetry observations
to separate measurements over water from measurements over land. Therefore, water masks
derived from other satellite products are necessary to properly differentiate between water and land
measurements. The River&Lake dataset used a static water mask derived from ERS-2, Envisat and
Jason-2 altimeters (Berry et al., 2005), while Theia HydroWeb uses small rectangular masks around
predeﬁned virtual stations (Santos da Silva et al., 2010). Radar imagery from the Sentinel-1 mission
is used to deﬁne a detailed water mask over the Ogooué river network and extract CryoSat-2
observations.
Sentinel-1 is a two satellite constellation launched by ESA in 2014. The two satellites orbit 180°
apart, at a 700 km altitude, ensuring optimal coverage and a short revisit time of 6 days on average.
The main purpose of the mission is land and sea monitoring. Both Sentinel-1 satellites carry a SAR
instrument working in C-band which penetrates cloud cover. Over land the satellite operates in
Interferometric Wide swath (IW) mode by default with a swath width of 250 km and a 5 x 20 m
ground resolution.

As a radar signal hits a ground target, parts of the energy are reﬂected, scattered or absorbed. The
portion reﬂected directly back to the radar antenna is the backscatter. The backscatter coefﬁcient,
σ0, is the normalised measure of radar return; a measure of the reﬂective strength of the ground
target. σ0 provides useful information to discriminate between land and water points. It is a
function of the radar observation parameters (frequency, polarisation and incidence angle of the
electromagnetic waves) and of the surface (roughness, geometric shape and dielectric properties).
Because inland water bodies tend to be more smooth with a dark tonality, only a very small portion
of the backscatter is reﬂected back to the radar and thus the backscatter coefﬁcients are generally
very low.
Polarisation is the orientation of the satellite transmitter or receiver relative to the Earth’s surface.
The most common polarisations are the linear polarisation conﬁgurations of horizontal (H) and
vertical (V) polarisations: HH, VV, HV and VH. The ﬁrst letter corresponds to the polarisation
of the emitter, the second term to that of the receiver. Sentinel-1 satellites carry dual-polarisation
SAR instruments, which can transmit and receive signals in both polarisations. In IW mode dual
polarisations VV and VH are available over land. VH has a higher water/non water contrast than
VV, which in turn gives better results over windy lakes or rivers with high currents (Fabry and
Bercher, 2015).
Level-1 Ground Range Detected (GRD) IW Sentinel-1 images acquired in May and June 2016

Platform), speciﬁcally tailored to Sentinel data. The timing of the image as basis for a single static
mask, is deemed reasonable. First the pictures are calibrated. This step removes the effect of the
incidence angle and relative level of brightness of the product, and ensures each pixel value truly
represents the radar backscatter of the reﬂecting surface. Calibration is product speciﬁc, and SNAP
detects the origin of the ﬁle being processed. Figure 3.3 shows an image of the Ogooué Delta
before (left) and after calibration (centre). The image is mirrored as it has not been terrain corrected
and reprojected.

3.2. HYDROLOGICAL MODEL
3.2
Hydrological Model
Hydrological models have been widely developed to support decision making. They are useful in
long-term planning, for scenario testing and in real-time management, where they can be used for
forecasting. There are two broad categories of hydrological models: rainfall-runoff (RR) models,
which generate runoff from a rainfall input, and hydrodynamic models (HD), which use a routing
component to simulate streamﬂow from runoff. RR models range from holistic lumped, conceptual
models to fully-distributed, physically-based models, which aim at thoroughly describing the
physical laws governing the simulated hydrological mechanisms, but require a high level of user
expertise (Devi et al., 2015).
Fully-distributed models often have large data requirements, and are more sensitive to input data
uncertainties, making them less suited for ungauged basins (Knoche et al., 2014). Indeed, data
paucity must constrain model complexity: parameter uncertainty can easily exceed uncertainties
related to the model structure if the data availability or quality is insufﬁcient (Johnston and
Smakhtin, 2014). Furthermore, lumped conceptual models have limited data requirements and
model parameters, thus reducing computational demands (Blasone et al., 2007). This study uses a
lumped conceptual RR model based on the Budyko framework. The RR model is coupled to a one-
dimensional routing model based on the Muskingum routing scheme. The model is programmed
using Python 2.7 in Anaconda 2.0 (Appendix A).
3.2.1
Rainfall-Runoff Model: The Budyko Framework
The Budyko type RR model used in this study, is based on the work of Zhang et al. (2008) and
Finsen et al. (2014). Budyko’s curve is a simple water balance model relating the dryness index
(potential evapotranspiration over precipitation) to the evaporative index (actual evapotranspiration
over precipitation). The curve has two asymptotes: the energy limit is the x = y line and the water

a catchment based on observed data. Zhang et al. (2008) developed a holistic approach to simulate
catchment water balance down to a daily time scale, based on the assumption that two parameters
control the equilibrium water balance: water availability and atmospheric demand. The former is
approximated by precipitation, while the latter is represented through potential evapotranspiration.

3.2.2
Routing Model
River routing methods can generally be divided into distributed (hydraulic) and lumped (hydrologi-
cal) approaches. The most complex models solve the full Saint-Venant equation in space and time,
whilst, the hydrograph is solved at a given point as a function of time in lumped approaches. The
Muskingum and Muskingum-Cunge approaches considered for this study are both widely used
methods (Chow et al., 1988a; Todini, 2007), provided as part of the SWAT package for instance
(Neitsch et al., 2009). The ﬁnal version of the model uses the Muskingum routing method.

IN,t+1 = ON−1,t+1 + MN,t+1

The runoff MN is the output of the rainfall-runoff model at the inlet of subbasin N. In natural

conceptual tributary reaches were implemented to route the direct runoff of a given subbasin to

discharge at the outlet of the tributary reach and of the main channel in catchment N −1. The
tributary channels are implemented in the routing scheme as parts of the stream network.
Khan (1993) suggested applying the Muskingum routing to river networks consisting of a network
of channel reaches by summing the concurrent tributary ﬂows to represent a single inﬂow, I,
replacing ON−1,t+1 in Equation 3.51. The method is valid under the assumption that all tributaries
are equally important to determine the storage in the routing reach. According to Khan (1993) the
assumption may be problematic if the point of conﬂuence varies. In this study the inlet/outlet points
deﬁning the reaches are kept constant during routing and the river is assumed to be morphologically
constant.

tributary channels are implemented as linear reservoirs by setting X to 0. The tributary channels
are used only to retain water, therefore they were split into a maximum of ﬁve increments to limit
computational time, while still keeping reach length low.
3.4
Calibration
Model parameters are adjusted to optimise the model performance by evaluating simulated state
variables against the observed. In addition to time efﬁciency, the advantage of automatic calibration
over manual calibration is the automatic and systematic evaluation of the parameter space based only
on user-deﬁned parameter bounds, thus limiting subjectivity in the calibration process. Numerous
local and global search algorithms are available and several calibration and parameter sampling
procedures are available, and extensions are readily available for Python, the programming language
used exclusively in this study. Houska et al. (2015) developed the plugin SPOTPY which contains
a range of optimization algorithms, including stochastic probabilistic methods such as Monte
Carlo (MC) and Latin Hyper Cube Sampler (LHS) methods, and optimisation algorithms such as
the Maximum Likelihood Estimation method (MLE), the Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)
method and the evolutionary global search algorithm, the Shufﬂed Complex Evolution algorithm
- University of Arizona (SCEUA). Unlike stochastic probabilistic methods, which sample the
entire parameter space randomly and can be used to provide an ensemble of behavioural models,
evolutionary algorithms are built on a principle of “information sharing”, and typically provide a
single optimal solution for the user-deﬁned objective. The SCEUA is especially suited for large
parameter space problems (Houska et al., 2015).
The advantage of using a global search algorithm is the broad search of the parameter space
(Blasone et al., 2007). The SCEUA is described in Duan et al. (1992) and has been used in several
hydrological studies. In an n-dimensional problem, the algorithm samples 2 × n + 1 parameter sets
within the user-deﬁned parameter boundaries. Model performance is evaluated for each parameter
set, which are then ranked by performance and partitioned into the chosen number of complexes.
Each complex is evolved separately following a simplex algorithm. The complexes are then
shufﬂed, ensuring information about the parameter space is shared and that the entire population
converges towards the global optimum.
To calibrate the hydrological model, 8 complexes are evolved and convergence is deﬁned as less
than 1% change in the objective function and parameter value change over 100 model runs. The

3.5. ALTIMETRY OBSERVATIONS AND MODEL VALIDATION
3.5
Altimetry Observations and Model Validation
The model is validated against observations from ﬁve in-situ stations across the basin which were
not included in the calibration and through comparison with the radar altimetry observations. A
split-sample validation is used for the GRACE observations, as only the ﬁrst 9 years are used for
calibration, leaving 5 years for validation. Long-term trends are evaluated based on the discharge
observations, and a split-sample approach is not suitable. Instead, the in-situ discharge stations are
split into calibration and validation stations.
Whilst the calibrated model may be able to reproduce satisfactory ﬂow values, reasonable water
level predictions are equally important in many hydrological applications. Altimetry measurements
from repeat-orbit satellites provide a time series of water elevation at virtual stations. Because
the bathymetry in the basin is unknown, water elevation from altimetry measurements cannot be
directly translated to the water heights simulated by the model. Instead the amplitudes are compared
by subtracting the mean height over several complete years of observation and simulation from the
elevation observations and simulations respectively.
These observations are especially useful, as they are the only concurrent observations of the river
storage available. Satellite imagery provides reasonable guess at the river widths and the latter are
manually calibrated to obtain reasonable amplitudes throughout the river network. The simulated
and observed deviations from the mean water height are compared using the RMSE and the

CHAPTER 4
Results and Discussion
The focus of the work conducted for this thesis can be divided into two main objectives: the
evaluation of the available remote sensing observations relevant to hydrological applications and
the development of a modelling approach integrating these observations. Several environmental
monitoring missions have estimated the same hydrological ﬂux and state variables providing several
options for the same purpose. The effect of choosing one product over the other is evaluated through
the choice of remote sensing rainfall estimates; the results of the comparison of the two selected
products is presented in Section 4.1.
Since, the use of remote sensing is often hindered by coarse temporal and spatial resolution, a multi-
mission approach is employed to increase the spatial coverage of altimetry measurements of water
stage throughout the basin. The spatio-temporal variations in water height obtained from remote
sensing observations are presented and discussed in Section 4.2. The remote sensing observations
are used to drive and calibrate the model through a multi-objective optimisation strategy. The
sensitivity analysis, calibrated parameters and model simulations are presented and discussed in
Section 4.3 for the two models driven by TRMM and FEWS-RFE precipitation respectively. Finally
limitations and suggestions for future work are presented in Section 4.4.
4.1
Comparison of Satellite-Based Rainfall Estimates
Precipitation is expected to greatly affect the model output and parameter deﬁnition, therefore both
TRMM 3B42 v7 (TRMM) and FEWS-RFE are considered as model inputs. The two products
are compared in the six calibration zones considered by the model for the overlapping period
of record between the two products (2001-2015). The most signiﬁcant results are shown below,
additional plots and tables are shown in Appendix B. Figures 4.1 and 4.2 show the average yearly
precipitation over this period for the two products. The average yearly precipitation varies between
1600 and 2400 mm in the basin based on TRMM estimates and between 1200 and 2200 mm based
on FEWS-RFE estimates. Although the spatial resolutions are different, the spatial pattern is
similar for the two products, with a general north-east to south-west gradient. As expected, the
average annual precipitation is higher in the Ngounié than in the rest of the basin (Chapter 2).
However, the magnitudes are different: TRMM generally predicts more rain than FEWS-RFE.
This is corroborated by the Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) and Mean Deviation (MD)
between the two products: in all six zones and in the whole basin, TRMM is larger than FEWS-
RFE (Appendix B). The RMSD relative to the mean TRMM precipitation is 162.4% for daily
observations and 17.3% for monthly precipitation. The MD relative to the TRMM mean is 9.9%.

Several altimetry missions have crossed the Ogooué and gathered measurements. The altimetry
observations obtained for this study are presented in the following section. Here, amplitude refers
to the mean annual amplitude in water height over several hydrological years. Water height and
elevation are used as synonyms to qualify the altimetry observations, however the altimetric water
heights are referenced to the geoid, whereas the simulated water heights are referenced to the
unknown river bathymetry.
4.2.1
CryoSat-2 and ICESat
762 CryoSat-2 orbits crossed the Ogooué basin between the 17th of July 2010 and the 21st of
February 2015, resulting in 1521 single observations ﬁltered over the Sentinel-1 water mask.
CryoSat-2 heights are almost consistently smaller than SRTM derived heights. The difference can
be attributed to topographical noise from the steep hillsides bordering the narrow valleys of the
basin and to the density of vegetation in the basin (Carre, 1978). SRTM may be recording the top
of the canopy. Discrepancies are found in the longitudinal cross-section along the river line of the
SRTM DEM. The risk of removing potentially valid CryoSat-2 observations based on erroneous
SRTM heights is reduced by correcting the reference heights to the immediate downstream value,
if they exceed the upstream elevation by more than 1 m. Outliers are deﬁned as observations more
than 20 m lower than the reference height or more than 3 m higher. Most outliers are from single-
observation transects. The 1342 outlier-ﬁltered single observations are clustered into 524 clusters.
The 48 ICESat observations of the Ogooué provided on the IWSH database have been ﬁltered using
a global water mask and transect-averaged (O’Loughlin et al., 2016). The observations are ﬁltered
over the Sentinel-1 water mask and reprojected onto the EGM2008 geoid. Figure 4.8 shows the

There is a clear seasonality in the ICESat sampling over the Ogooué - most observations are from
the high ﬂow seasons in March-May and September-November (33 out of 48 observations in total or
almost 69% are recorded over a period representing 48% of the total year). CryoSat-2 observations
on the other hand are more evenly distributed: 326 out of 524 clustered observations are sampled
in the dry season, or 57% of all observations over a period representing 52% of a year. and 33% of
all observations were recorded in the driest period, between June and September, or 25% of a year.
In the delta alone, the balance is slightly more skewed: 60% of the clusters are observed in the two
dry periods. Thus, there is a risk that the peak ﬂows have not been observed by CryoSat-2.
Indeed, the ICESat observations are larger, however this may be a bias introduced by the CryoSat-
based mean used as reference. The RMSD between ICESat and CryoSat-2 is calculated by linearly
interpolating the CryoSat-2 observations to the chainage of ICESat observations. The RMSD is 3.6
m corresponding to 5% of the mean of the ICESat observations. This bias is negligible in light of
other uncertainties related to the observations (topographical errors, off-nadir errors, large footprint
in LRM-mode, time of sampling etc.). ICESat uses a laser altimeter, which has a higher precision
and smaller footprint, however only 48 observations are available, greatly limiting its application in

A Latin hypercube sampling of the parameter space for one of the upstream catchments (the
Ngounié catchment) shows no obvious parameter correlation (Figure 4.15). The best parameter
sets are represented by red points. The sampling evaluates the average water ﬂow velocity, v thus
aggregating n and the geometric parameters to estimate the routing parameter K (Finsen et al.,
2014). There appears to be a threshold effect between α1 and α2. α1 and α2 govern the distribution
of water between processes: indeed, if the retention efﬁciency, α1, is above a certain threshold
(around 0.2 from Figure 4.15), more water is available to satisfy the evapotranspiration demand
or for groundwater recharge, both determined by α2. Thus, to produce the same recharge and
thereby baseﬂow, α2 must be smaller. Reversely, if α1 is below the threshold, more direct runoff is
generated, baseﬂow no longer needs to compensate for the lack of direct runoff and α2 with values
above 0.2 yield good results.
The sensitivity analysis in this study is limited to a local sensitivity analysis due to the large number
of parameters, large computational requirements and limited time frame of the project. A global
sensitivity analysis using one of the methods from Saltelli et al. (2006) for instance, can provide
additional information on correlations and sensitivity throughout the parameter space. Based on the
analysis above and the relatively simple model structure, the local sensitivity analysis is deemed
sufﬁcient for the purposes of this study.

The Budyko parameter α1 determines the retention efﬁciency of the catchment (Section 3.2). For
large α1 values, less direct runoff is generated from a given rainfall volume. In turn, α2 governs
the distribution between actual evapotranspiration and groundwater recharge. Large values of α2
increase actual evapotranspiration, decreasing groundwater storage. Generally α2 is larger than α1,
except in the Booué catchment in the FEWS-RFE model, where the large α1 value is used to reduce
direct runoff. The Booué catchment consists of steep hills and valleys, with areas of impermeable
ground, however the vegetation cover slows down direct runoff (Carre, 1978). These processes
will be reﬂected in the discharge observations but are not directly simulated by the model, which
instead increases retention and evapotranspiration efﬁciencies.
The large differences in parameters for the Ogooué Delta between the models, may in part be
compensating for the ﬁxed Smax parameters. The latter are estimated from preliminary calibrations
with TRMM and FEWS-RFE respectively and are therefore ﬁxed at different values. Preliminary
assessments of the parameter space suggest no parameter correlation (Figure 4.15), however it
is inevitable that the model must adjust calibration parameters to comply with the imposed ﬁxed
values. When evaluating the parameter values of the initial, shorter calibration (10 000 model runs)
on which the Smax values are based (Appendix D), several parameters converged to similar values
in the ﬁnal calibration. This corroborates that the parameter space is well-deﬁned. The preliminary
calibration is not retained due to poor model performance.
d is the baseﬂow reaction factor (Section 3.2). Large values increase the baseﬂow and decrease
groundwater storage. In the TRMM model, the largest contributions to storage are groundwater
storage in the Batéké Plateau, Eastern Gabon Plateau and Ngounié (98%, 86% and 95% of total
storage respectively). In the remaining catchments, soil storage represents 61% (Booué), 52%
(Ndjolé) and 61% (Delta) of the total storage, which is explained by increased baseﬂow from
larger d and/or increased soil storage from larger Smax values. In the Ngounié catchment, the
low value of d is associated to low values of Smax, yielding proportionally larger groundwater
storage, while in the Batéké Plateau and Eastern Gabon Plateau catchments, both are lower than in

other catchments. A similar pattern is seen in the FEWS-RFE model, although the larger Smax
in the Ndjolé catchment increases the proportion of soil storage to 70%. The proportions reﬂect
the hydrogeology of the catchment quite well: large aquifers are concentrated in the Plateaus and

Manning’s n for the tributaries converged to the upper parameter bound, 0.2 in both models. n
is a roughness coefﬁcient and at larger values, increased friction means a higher water column
must build to generate the same discharge (Section 3.2.2). The tributaries are a conceptual storage
unit, therefore the X parameter is ﬁxed to 0, in order to deﬁne the routing component as a linear
reservoir storage. Their role in the model is to retain direct runoff and simulate other retardation
processes occurring at subbasin-level before the water reaches the main channel. The large value of
n, is in fact a lumped approach to calibrating the tributary channel geometry.
In the main channel, Manning’s n varies between 0.015 and 0.05 in the TRMM model, and between
0.015 and 0.045 in the FEWS-RFE model. The values are quite similar between the models. n
is largest in the middle of the river, where the terrain is very rough with several turns and drops,
and several lakes lie close to the river. A higher Manning’s n may be required to retain the water
sufﬁciently. The values are reasonable for a natural channel, considering reference n values (Chow,
1959), albeit the minimum is quite low. The Delta is simulated as a single main channel with a
ﬁxed maximum width, however in reality, it is a braided channel with several ﬂoodplains. The low
Manning’s n results in a lower water height for a given discharge.
According to Reid (2008), who performed a sensitivity analysis of the Muskingum and Muskingum-
Cunge routing techniques, the sensitivity of the variables in the Manning equation can be directly
related to their exponents - 1 for n, and 0.5 for the bed slope. However, for embedded parameters,
including channel width, depth and slope, the relationship is more complex. For trapezoidal
cross-sections, he found that the bed width and channel slope sensitivities are related to the bed
slope exponent - 0.5. Thus changing n is an efﬁcient way to adjust water heights. Furthermore, one
value of the parameter can be given to large areas, thus calibrating several cross-sections at once,
when a reasonable cross-section geometry has been determined.
The Muskingum parameter X is very close to 0.5 in the Ngounié and Booué catchments in the
TRMM model and in the Ogooué Delta in both models. In the rest of the basin, the parameter
varies between 0.16 and 0.29 in the TRMM model and between 0.16 and 0.35 in the FEWS-RFE
model, as expected in natural channels (Chow et al., 1988a). High values of X indicate a full wedge,
resulting in very little attenuation between the reaches (Section 3.2.2). The model leads all the
water from upstream reaches through the main channel at Lambaréné, while in reality, part of the
discharge may have been lost to lakes or wetlands, or to smaller branches of the river. Leading the
water through the channel faster by limiting attenuation and friction is a way to compensate for
excess water. The very low value of n in the Delta could be adjusted by increasing the channel
width in some reaches.
Although a few of the parameters converge to the maximum upper bound, the latter is deﬁned based
on numerical stability criteria (Chow et al., 1988a) and physical knowledge of the system. The
bounds for Mannning’s n in the main channel are based on reasonable ranges for natural channels
from the literature. In early calibrations of the model, very large values of n enabled the model to
retain the water sufﬁciently. Introducing conceptual tributary processes and restricting n forces the
other parameters to compensate for the limited friction.

Performance Measures
Table 4.5 shows the individual objective functions of the TRMM model post-calibration for the
discharge objectives at the calibration and validation stations. The hydrographs, ﬂow duration curves
and climatology benchmarks at the validation stations are shown in Appendix E. The optimisation
algorithm samples randomly in the parameter space prior to starting complex evolution. The
overall objective function is improved from 3.09 (the best random parameter sample) to 1.61 for
the TRMM model and from 3.02 to 1.67 for the FEWS-RFE model.
Both models perform best at the calibration stations, as expected. At the calibration station, the
RFDC is above 0 for all stations except the furthest downstream for the TRMM model, and all
RMSE values for the climatology are below 1.5. A RMSE of 1 can be interpreted as the average
residuals being equal to the interannual distribution of ﬂow on a given day. This is particularly
important when considering the lack of overlap between the observation and simulation periods.
Therefore, values of 1 are considered satisfactory. For both models, the RMSE values are above
1 in all but one case: the TRMM model at Booué. Similarly the FDC calibration is accepted for
positive RFDC values, where the simulated FDC is within 10% of the observed.
The best results are obtained at the Booué station with a RFDC of 0.59 and a RMSE of 0.89. It
is interesting to note that although the Makokou is well within 10% of the FDC, the climatology
RMSE is the lowest in the calibration zones. Indeed, a good distribution of ﬂow volumes can be
obtained without taking into account seasonality and timing. This example highlights the advantage
of including both measures, when updated discharge measurements are unavailable. However,
neither of the models are validated based on both objectives. For the climatology, values below 1
are accepted, only the objective function calculated at the Belinga and Loa-Loa stations are below
1.5.
The FEWS-RFE model generally performs worse than the TRMM model. The best RFDC is
0.43 at the Makokou station, but the RFDC is below 0 at both Fougamou and Lambaréné and the
climatology RMSE is between 1.45 and 1.76 at the calibration stations. Based on the previous
sections and on the performance statistics, the TRMM model performs better in terms of replicating
long term discharge trends.
The choice of uncertainty when calculating the RFDC (in this case 10%) could be reevaluated
or increased: with a threshold of 15% all calibration stations have RFDC values above 0, except
for the FEWS-RFE model at the Lambaréné station. For the validation stations, Belinga for the
FEWS-RFE model and Leyami for the TRMM model have values above 0.

The large simulated peaks in storage suggest more water is stored during than ﬂood seasons than

constrained by the volume of water required for discharge and generated through direct runoff and
baseﬂow. Because the calibration period only includes simulations until 2011, the model is unable
to replicate similar behaviour if the forcing data changes signiﬁcantly.
The simulated total water storage is estimated as the sum of the soil and groundwater storage.
Xie et al. (2012) and Milzow et al. (2011) include the channel storage, however as the storage is
distributed over the entire subbasin, the channel storage represents at most 2% of the total water
storage. Therefore, excluding the channel storage is not expected to have a large effect on the total
water storage calibration.
The GRACE observations help constrain the water balance, as the model is forced to distribute
water between evapotranspiration requirements, storage compartments and river ﬂow adequately.
However, the choice of weighting parameter is especially important when using storage observa-
tions: increasing the weight of the storage objective functions, improved the storage simulations
but worsened the discharge calibration - in particular the timing of the two ﬂood peaks and the
water height amplitudes.
The GRACE objectives represent only 2 out of 14 objectives, meaning improving discharge at a
single station, will have the same effect as improving the total water storage simulation throughout
the basin. Increasing the weight of GRACE objectives in the aggregated function hindered the
overall calibration of the model, as discharge parameters were no longer well constrained. Based
on the experience from this study, the GRACE objectives should not represent more than 15% of
the aggregated objective function.

Channel Widths
The channel widths are adjusted prior to calibration in order to account for the large spatial
variations in amplitude - for instance an amplitude of 4.72 m is recorded at the Jason-2 virtual
station in the upstream Ivindo, against 2.22 m at the Envisat station 133 km downstream. Even

13 virtual stations included in their calibration. It should be noted that the calibration method by
Schneider et al. (2016) optimises the water heights by calibrating cross-section parameters directly
and not through conceptual routing parameters as in this study. Here, the RMSE is slightly higher,
1.09 m, but is still deemed reasonable.

4.4
Discussion
Due to the limited time frame of this project and the vast possibilities in terms of integrating
remote sensing into hydrological modelling, a number of choices are made regarding model setup
and choice of remote sensing products. In the following section, these choices are discussed and
recommendations for further investigations and future research on the topic are presented.
4.4.1
Remote Sensing Observations of Hydrological State Variables
The differences between remote sensing products are inherent to the observation combinations
used to produce them, and to extraction algorithm uncertainties. For instance, there is not yet an
optimal, unique combination or solution which correctly predicts precipitation from space (Xu et al.,
2014). Therefore, input-data bias should be properly characterised prior to use, and information on
different hydrological state variables (such as storage observations) can help ensure the integrity of
the model structure is maintained.
This study uses free, publicly available remote sensing observations relevant to the proposed model
structure to characterise the basin. Other types of products are available but are not included.
One example is remote sensing estimates of actual evapotranspiration. Along with precipitation,
evapotranspiration is an important factor in catchment-scale hydrological processes. However,
evapotranspiration can only be inferred from other products (Xu et al., 2014), and is therefore
not considered in this particular study. As research progresses, the possibilities expand and more
products may become relevant.
Rainfall Estimates
There are no contemporary observations of precipitation available for the Ogooué river basin,
therefore a site-speciﬁc bias characterisation is not possible. In this case, TRMM 3B42 v.7 is
more similar to the gauging data than FEWS-RFE. The main difference between the two products
lies in the mean annual precipitation: TRMM predicts signiﬁcantly more rain than FEWS-RFE
throughout the basin. However, there is not a constant bias between the products: the double-mass
plots suggest they are more similar in recent years, whilst QQ-plots suggest the extreme events
remain very different.
Choosing the input product based on the comparison to decade-old data assumes that rain patterns
(seasonality, long term averages) are constant in time since the time of observation. Mahe et al.
(2013) analysed the signiﬁcance of climate change in equatorial Africa, including the Ogooué
basin. They noted a seasonal change in rainfall patterns and consequences for the runoff patterns.
The change in precipitation caused a decrease in runoff between the 1980s and 2000s and with the
exception of April-May, the monthly runoff had decreased. Furthermore, the ﬁrst ﬂood peak had
shifted from May to April. Thus, the model is forced by data with changed precipitation patterns,
but calibrated against historical trends. The use of the latter is justiﬁed by the lack of alternative,
and the fact that the rupture in the pure equatorial runoff regime in the Ogooué occurred before
the 1970s, where most of the used data is from. Milzow et al. (2011) also based their choice of
FEWS-RFE on a comparison with historical data and the FEWS-RFE-forced model also performed
best.
In this study, TRMM is closest to the observed precipitation and the TRMM-forced model yields
the best performance on almost all counts. FEWS-RFE model performed the worst, mainly due to
the fast response of the model to precipitation: the discharge ﬂuctuates with very large dispersion in
the average discharge observed on a given day of the year. Furthermore, although the FEWS-RFE

driven model is better at capturing the magnitude of total storage ﬂuctuations in the eastern basin
during the calibration period, the validation period suggests an increase in precipitation which
worsens the performance in the most recent years. The increase is also visible in the precipitation
QQ-plots and in the water height amplitudes. The model parameters may be compensating for a
mismatch between the FEWS-RFE precipitation and the historical discharge trends, hindering the
models ability to respond adequately to more rain in the basin. The fact that TRMM precipitation
is closer to the historical precipitation may provide part of the explanation for the superiority of
the TRMM model in replicating historical discharge trends. However, it is impossible to evaluate
whether the FEWS-RFE model would have the upper hand if calibrated against up-to-date discharge
observations. Based on the results in this study and the data availability, TRMM is more suitable in
the Ogooué basin.
The inclusion of in-situ gauge-data in satellite-based rainfall estimates tends to improve rainfall
estimations and reduce bias (Stisen et al., 2008; Thiemig et al., 2013; Xu et al., 2014), but at the
expense of a lag time between the time of observation and the availability of the estimates: FEWS-
RFE observations are available after a couple of days, TRMM after one to two months and the
ECMWF ERA-reanalysis temperature estimates are delayed by two months. The latency between
observation and release is an important factor for operational hydrological modelling. Knoche
et al. (2014) achieved no improvements in hydrological simulations by using high resolution
temperature estimates, therefore a forecasting temperature product could replace ERA-Interim
(Section 3.1.2). However, while the few days delay of FEWS-RFE could be overcome through
assimilation schemes or calibration of a forecasting model, longer delays make forecasting very
challenging. The TRMM product will be replaced by the Global Precipitation Monitoring (GPM)
project during 2017, which will provide a re-analysis of the TRMM timeseries to ensure continuity
(Section 3.1.3). Recalibration will be necessary if the product differ too much (Huffman, 2015).
The GPM re-analysis products currently have a 4 months latency, but the near-real-time late-run
product is available 18 hours after acquisition. The GPM product is not considered in this study, as
only 15 months of observation were available at the start of the project (Huffman et al., 2015).
Total Water Storage
The impact of selecting one product over another for the same purpose is evaluated only for
precipitation in this study. Precipitation is a crucial driver of the land-surface water balance, and
adequate rainfall estimates are essential to properly represent the hydrological processes in a basin
(Gebregiorgis et al., 2012). However, there are alternative products and extraction methods available
beyond the versions selected for this study for most remote sensing estimates. For instance, this
study selects one post-processed version of the GRACE data (Section 3.1.4). However, there are
several other solutions available, which have been evaluated in the literature including harmonic
oscillation solutions (Sakumura et al., 2014; Landerer and Swenson, 2012) or alternative mascon
solutions (Longuevergne et al., 2010). Additionally, post-processed, ready-made solutions are
available for regions and basins. The latter are not considered because the basin delineation differed
too much from the model developed in the study. Figure 4.23 compares two solutions: the JPL
mascon solution developed in Watkins et al. (2015), and the arithmetic mean of the harmonic
solutions produced by the three main processing centres as suggested by Sakumura et al. (2014).
The RMSD between the solutions is 7.23 cm in the western basin and 3.93 cm in the eastern basin
(right in Figure 4.23), and in both cases the amplitude of change is larger with the mascon solution
used in this study. This is expected: according to Watkins et al. (2015), the mascon solution used in
this study is better at replicating small-scale ﬂuctuations.

relative to the observations or to different calibration parameters. Further investigations are required

of total water storage, ground truthing is not a realistic option. Storage is a large-scale variable
and water storage changes are impossible to quantify on ground over meaningful areas. Thus,
remote sensing offers unique insight and provides otherwise inaccessible data, however the choice
of product is an important factor in the ﬁnal results.
Sentinel-1 River Mask
To the author’s knowledge, this study is the ﬁrst to use SAR imagery from Sentinel-1 to extract
CryoSat-2 observations over an inland water body, and the ﬁrst study to evaluate CryoSat-2
observations over the Ogooué. Most recently, Schneider et al. (2016) used a river mask extracted
from Landsat 7 and 8 NDVI imagery (Normalised Difference Vegetation Index). NDVI values
over water are negative, facilitating a land/water classiﬁcation. However, optical imagery does not
penetrate cloud cover and is thus unsuitable for the equatorial climate of the Ogooué basin. Figure
4.24 compares a Landsat water mask (black pixels) to the Sentinel-1 based water mask used in this
study (blue surface). CryoSat-2 observations within the Sentinel-1 water mask are shown in red.
A detailed classiﬁcation of CryoSat-2 observations is possible with the method used in this study,
opening up for new possibilities in tropical regions. Indeed, the size of the Ogooué (over 1 km at
its widest, and over 100 m through the entire main channel), qualiﬁes it as an interesting study
area for altimetry observations. However, without cloud penetrating technologies, it would be very
difﬁcult to produce a satisfactory water mask of the river.
The Ogooué and its tributaries form of a network of morphologically permanent reaches, therefore
the mask is not expected to change signiﬁcantly over seasons. However, static river masks
introduce a source of uncertainty in morphologically active rivers. Schneider et al. (2016) reduced
this uncertainty by updating their river mask annually in the dry, low ﬂow season. SAR-based
water masks could be obtained even in heavy rain seasons and increase the temporal resolution of
water masks. It would be interesting to evaluate whether the use of a dynamic mask would alter the
classiﬁcation of CryoSat-2 observations over the Ogooué.
The largest hurdle in terms of using satellite imagery is the classiﬁcation of backscatter coefﬁcients.
The choice of threshold is subjective and although similar orders of magnitude are used to produce
the binary map for the Ogooué, the thresholds are image-speciﬁc. An automatic classiﬁcation
process would be very difﬁcult as the algorithm would need to be able to identify the water peak
and land peak, and the optimal minimum between the two. Westerhoff et al. (2013) suggested
a probabilistic classiﬁcation of water from SAR images using training histograms. However the

Domeneghetti et al. (2014) showed that altimetry observations of water level of the Po river
by the ERS-2 and Envisat missions improved the model calibration, compared to using in-situ
observations alone. The study shows the beneﬁts of altimetry observations in studies of rivers.
They also improved the representation of the river regime. In this study, the altimetry missions
generally agree very well and the observations provide valuable information on water heights
throughout the river. Although it would be useful to conﬁrm the remotely-sensed observations with
ground observations, the availability of altimetry observations strengthened the validation of the
hydrological model of the Ogooué. The altimetry observations are not included in the ﬁnal versions
of the model developed in this study, as only lumped parameters are calibrated and additional
objectives did not improve the calibration results.
The availability and cost of different products plays a very large role in which products are used for
hydrological applications. According to O’Loughlin et al. (2016), the choice of satellite missions

used in scientiﬁc studies correlates to availability rather than precision or resolution. Indeed, ICESat
is shown to have the lowest vertical error and smallest footprint due to the higher precision of its

in this study, but the post-processing requirements of raw altimetry data exceeded the scope of this

The remote sensing observations used in this study are selected for the speciﬁc study area. For
instance, no reliable soil moisture estimates can be produced for the basin due to the very large
tropical forest coverage. The dense vegetation inhibits microwave sensors from sensing the
underlying soil (Tang et al., 2009). In other areas, this may be a relevant addition, as the model
includes a soil storage component.
One common application of remote sensing data has not been explored in this study: data assimila-
tion or state estimation. The Muskingum model can be used for data assimilation: Michailovsky
et al. (2013) used the model to assimilate altimetry water heights from the Envisat virtual stations
in a model of the Brahmaputra. In the Ogooué basin, the lack of in-situ observations hinders the
evaluation of an assimilation scheme, and due to its coarse spatial resolution, altimetry observations
would not sufﬁce. Therefore, assimilation is not considered. However, it may be relevant in other
basins: soil moisture or GRACE observations could be assimilated into the rainfall-runoff model,
and altimetry observations could be assimilated into the routing model.
The biggest obstacle in using remote sensing data products in hydrological models, is the difﬁculty
to deﬁne uncertainties in the data (Tang et al., 2009; van Griensven et al., 2012). The latter is
still poorly described at a global scale, and current sensors and extraction algorithms are not
precise enough to close the water balance based on remote sensing data (Tang et al., 2009). Data
uncertainties can be buffered by the hydrological model, and in turn conceal the uncertainties in
model structure (Knoche et al., 2014). If only one hydrological variable is considered, optimisation
can occur at the cost of a coherent model. Knoche et al. (2014) and van Griensven et al. (2012)
amongst others, stipulate that while remote sensing input data has opened for new possibilities in
terms of catchment-scale modelling, calibration focused on discharge observations tends to com-
pensate for input-data errors by compromising the representation of other hydrological processes.
Furthermore, Knoche et al. (2014) showed a correlation between sensitivity to input data errors
and model complexity and lumped conceptual models can provide good results in spite of the
reduced complexity (Xu et al., 2014). The model structure chosen in this study can accommodate
the integration of different types of remote sensing observations.
4.4.2
Model Structure
In poorly gauged basins, the paucity of observations greatly strains the deﬁnition of the model
parameters and consequently model complexity (Johnston and Smakhtin, 2014). Remote sensing
data has been used in several studies to compensate for gaps in in-situ observations and has enabled
the deﬁnition of distributed or semi-distributed models even in poorly gauged basins (van Griensven
et al., 2012). Furthermore, the accessibility of remote sensing observations creates modelling
opportunities in basins, where in-situ data is insufﬁcient on its own (Johnston and Smakhtin, 2014).
This is the case for the Ogooué, which to the author’s knowledge only has historical discharge
observations and precipitation records at a handful of stations. The model used in this study has
a fairly simple and ﬂexible structure with few parameters and limited input data requirements,
77

which can accommodate several basin and river network conﬁgurations. Furthermore, although
the model currently does not support reservoir characterisation or abstraction losses, these can be
implemented within the model structure.
The routing model used for the Ogooué river basin model is the Muskingum hydrological routing
model. A Muskingum-Cunge module has been programmed and can be coupled to the rainfall-
runoff model (Appendix A). The Muskingum-Cunge routing was initially considered as routing
method for this study, due to the lower data requirements (the traditional Muskingum method
requires the full inﬂow and outﬂow hydrograph for calibration) and for future assimilation applica-
tions. For the latter, the time-variable parameters of the Muskingum-Cunge method would enable
an update of the entire system for observations on any point on the river. This would be especially
useful for the assimilation of CryoSat-2 observations.
According to Chow et al. (1988b), the Muskingum-Cunge solution is superior to the kinematic wave
solution as it is more ﬂexible and shows less wave attenuation, while providing similar accuracy
to the diffusion wave model in spite of its simpler structure. However, limitations include the
representation of backwater effects (downstream disturbances affecting the upstream conditions)
and struggles with large variations in the kinematic wave celerity, when large ﬂoodplains are
inundated for instance. Indeed, diffusion approximations become invalid when inertia effects
dominate - for instance for very mild slopes (Georgakakos et al., 1990; Chow et al., 1988b). Finally,
the Muskingum-Cunge method is not mass conservative unlike the Muskingum solution (Ponce
and Yevjevich, 1978). Todini (2007) proposes a correction factor to introduce consistency in the
estimation of storage, combined with a reformulation of the Muskingum C-coefﬁcients as functions
of K and X at the previous and current timestep. The reported water balance errors and their
consequences should be investigated and it would be interesting to assess the advantages of using
the proposed corrections.
The Ogooué model is run with the Muskingum-Cunge routing component post-calibration (Ap-
pendix G). The results show that re-calibration is necessary to apply the model. Furthermore,
computation time is drastically increased when changing to the hydraulic routing model as it is
approximately 60 times slower to run. A calibration of the model similar to the one presented
in this study, would require roughly 28 days. For the Ogooué case study, the Muskingum rout-
ing produced satisfactory water height simulations, while the Muskingum-Cunge models appear
unstable, and require a reﬁnement of the spatio-temporal resolution. The FEWS-RFE model is
more unstable than the TRMM model. The precipitation analysis shows that FEWS-RFE has larger
extreme events, which the hydraulic routing model may be unable to handle at the spatial and
temporal resolution deﬁned for this experiment G. Thus, the gain in physical representation of the
system from hydraulic routing would not compensate for the increased computational requirements,
therefore the Muskingum version of the model is preferred in this study.
4.5
Applications of the Model
A model should always be evaluated in light of its purpose (Johnston and Smakhtin, 2014).
The model developed in this study, is the ﬁrst model of the river basin, and can provide useful
information regarding the river regime. The range of applications of a rainfall-runoff model is
wide, and includes ﬂood forecasting, scenario evaluation and as a tool for proper water resource
management (Devi et al., 2015). The model structure presented in this study can be used for several
of these purposes. Furthermore, it can be implemented as an open-source QGIS workﬂow or Python
package. The Python basis would allow for users to modify the code according to the problem at
hand, and the few parameters and simpler structure make the model potentially more accessible for
78

4.5. APPLICATIONS OF THE MODEL
non-expert users. The model also offers room for extensions. This can be useful in two ways: the
model can be customised to a speciﬁc catchment by adding or removing certain components (e.g.
add reservoirs, remove tributary processes, adapt to complex river networks). Furthermore, the
model can be coupled to other models to produce speciﬁc outputs. For instance, it could be used to
drive a linear programming model, which could simulate optimal water allocations in the basin. In
the speciﬁc case presented in this study, this type of approach would inform decision makers on
the trade-offs between development plans and conservation plans according to the available water
resources.
The purpose of the GW-A River Hydrology Product hydrological model, is scenario testing, for
instance evaluating the impact of landuse change, hydraulic structures or climate change on the river
regime and subsequent consequences for downstream wetlands (DHI-GRAS, 2016). The rainfall-
runoff model developed in this study uses a lumped approach to represent landuses. Therefore,
the model cannot be used to evaluate the impact of increased crop production or forestry in the
catchment. However, the model can be used to simulate predicted impacts of climate change
in the speciﬁc catchment. Global climate change models produce large scale future rainfall and
temperature predictions, which could be scaled to the basin and applied to the forcing data in order
to evaluate impacts of climate change on hydrological state variables in the basin.
Furthermore, the effect of hydraulic infrastructures in the basin can be evaluated by the model
by introducing reservoirs at appropriate locations in the basin. Similarly, water abstractions
can be incorporated as loss factors, and used to simulate increases in irrigation or urban water
use for instance. For the Ogooué basin, such scenarios are relevant, in order to evaluate the
planned development plans and their consequences on the river regime (World Bank, 2016). Water
abstractions are expected to increase to satisfy domestic and agricultural users, but the development
of hydropower production and industries along the river banks may also affect storage and ﬂow
dynamics. In light of the lack of observations, the remote sensing observations used in this study
help characterise the otherwise ungauged basin and the model can produce valuable information for
water managers. Indeed, although the model is not fully validated throughout the basin, it produces
water height amplitudes comparable to satellite altimetry. Thus, without additional observations
or on-ground information on the basin, the presented model is a satisfactory representation of the
Ogooué basin. However, model simulations can never replace observations and remote sensing
observations have never been studied in the basin before. Therefore, ground truthing efforts and
in-situ gauging campaigns would greatly strengthen the conclusions of this study.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis explores how multi-mission remote sensing can be used to inform hydrological models,
by using a poorly gauged river basin as case study. The fourth largest African river by discharge,
the Ogooué in Gabon, is used as study area. A lumped conceptual rainfall-runoff model based on
the Budyko framework is coupled to a Muskingum routing scheme and developed for the study area
using remote sensing observations to parametrise, force, calibrate the model parameters and validate
the model performance. The model is forced using remote sensing precipitation and calibrated using
a combination of historical in-situ discharge observations from the 1960s and 1970s, and total water
storage observations from the GRACE mission. Using a multi-mission approach, the hydrological
conditions in the otherwise poorly gauged Ogooué catchment are characterised. Remote sensing
enables the evaluation of the model against contemporary observations and helps constrain model
parameters by looking beyond optimising discharge measurements.
The signiﬁcance of choosing between several products is investigated using precipitation as example:
the two remote sensing rainfall estimates FEWS-RFE and TRMM are compared to each other and to
historical in-situ observations, and as forcing input to the model. The products are relatively similar,
although TRMM predicts more rainfall over the Ogooué basin than FEWS-RFE and is closer to
the historical observations. In this study, the model forced by TRMM is found to outperform the
FEWS-RFE driven model.
In addition, this study shows the potential of the new ESA Sentinel missions by deriving a detailed
river mask from Sentinel-1 radar imagery, used to extract altimetry observations from CryoSat-
2. With the methods applied in this study, a dynamic river mask can be deﬁned and used to
extract relevant observations over inland water bodies. The multi-mission approach increases
spatial and temporal coverage, and acts as a useful supplement to the observed in-situ discharge in
terms of validation, in the regions were the missions agree. Although the model cannot be fully
validated based on discharge alone, the water height simulations are validated against the altimetry
observations at multiple points in the basin.
Progress in remote sensing technologies, instruments and extraction algorithms now allows for
the observation of most hydrological states and ﬂuxes from space. This offers a unique possibility
to obtain observations in poorly gauged or remote areas and to supplement hydrological mod-
elling applications with the necessary input-data and useful observations for parameter estimation.
The model used in this study can be applied in scenario evaluations and provides an otherwise
unavailable insight into the hydrological regime of the Ogooué at catchment scale.

APPENDIX B
Remote sensing rainfall estimates
B.1
T-test
Statistical methods are used in this study to qualify the datasets evaluated. For the precipitation
analysis, the signiﬁcance of the differences between the two products in the six calibration zones is
evaluated with a so-called "t-test". The t-test evaluates the null-hypothesis that two samples have
the same mean - or in other words, whether they are statistically the same. Typically a critical value
of 0.025 is chosen for two sided tests. The paired t-test is used, as the sample consists of matched
pairs (e.g. the mean precipitation in a given calibration zone for each of the two products).
The R-programming language has a built in t-test functionality. Two paired t-tests are conducted to
compare the number of days with rain and the mean annual precipitation.
The comparison of days with rain yielded a p-value of 0.5, which is well above the critical value: the
samples are not signiﬁcantly different. The comparison of mean annual rainfall however resulted in
a p-value of 0.004, well below 0.025. Thus the mean annual rainfall predicted by the two products
are signiﬁcantly different.

APPENDIX G
Muskingum-Cunge Routing
This appendix explores the effect of switching to the Muskingum-Cunge hydraulic routing. The
model is more than 60 × slower to run, and based on the worsened performance, it is clear that
a recalibration with the speciﬁc routing component would be required. This comparison shows
that the simpler, Muskingum routing with time-constant parameters and using Cunge’s method to
estimate K is suitable for the study area. The results also suggest that the model is more unstable -
either a ﬁner spatial resolution or smaller time step would be required, both of which would further
increase the time step. Another interesting observation, is that the FEWS-RFE model appears to
be more unstable than the TRMM model. The precipitation analysis shows that FEWS-RFE has
larger extreme events, which the hydraulic routing model may be unable to handle at the spatial
and temporal resolution deﬁned for this experiment.
