Abstract
Monopile foundations are widely used for installation of oﬀshore wind farms. On top of the
monopile a transition piece is installed which acts as transition between the monopile and
the wind turbine tower. For optimisation reasons a new design for the connection of the
monopile and transition piece is desired, where a bolted ring ﬂange connection is considered
to provide the needed optimisation.
In this thesis the bolted ﬂange connection is investigated with means to determine the ulti-
mate and fatigue limit state capacity. The investigation is performed for an idealised ﬂange
connection, where all imperfections due to manufacturing are disregarded.
The structural capacity is investigated with utilisation of analytical methods and ﬁnite el-
ement analyses with use of the software tool Abaqus/Standard.
The analytical solution
approach is considered conservative as the method considers an equivalent one bolt segment
instead of the full ﬂange connection. Therefore an numerical model of the full ﬂange has
been established to investigate whether the application of the full ﬂange allows for a higher
capacity.
The conducted investigation revealed that the analytically determinant ultimate and fa-
tigue capacities can be challenged by the established numerical procedure. For both limit
states the numerical analysis yielded a lower utilisation ratio for the load demand. Based
on studies, concerning the ﬂange segment and the full ﬂange model, it was found, that the
full ﬂange model allows for a more accurate prediction of the connection behaviour. It was
further observed that the resulting capacity is a direct result of the nonlinear stress transfer
function in the bolt as dependency of the external load. The simpliﬁed analytical methods
provide for a conservative assessment of the bolt stress, where bending stresses in the bolt is
disregarded. With use of the numerical model it becomes possible to include both axial and
bending stresses which proves to have a positive eﬀect on the bolt stress transfer function,
hence resulting in a higher structural capacity.
From the performed investigation it is concluded that use of ﬁnite element modelling provides
for an enhanced analysis of the ﬂange connection resulting in increased resistance towards
failure in ultimate and fatigue limit state.

Introduction
Chapter
1
The majority of oﬀshore wind turbines are installed with a monopile (MP) and transition
(TP) piece connection as foundation type. Traditionally the monopile and the transition
piece have been connected using a grouted connection, however recent investigations have
shown that settlements of the transition piece relative to the monopile occurred causing
fatigue cracks in the connection [32].
Therefore an alternative solution is sought, where
the monopile and transition piece are connected using a bolted ring ﬂange connection as
illustrated in a simpliﬁed manner in Figure 1.1. This thesis presents methods for investigating
the ultimate and fatigue limit state capacity with use of analytical and numerical methods for
this type of connection. The aim is to establish a numerical procedure which can challenge the
analytical solution hence providing increased capacity. The investigation is an initial study,
where a simpliﬁed connection geometry without imperfections is considered. Furthermore
the report considers what still needs to be included in the study before the solution can be
used in real projects.

1.1
State of the art
Monopile foundations are the most common foundation type for wind turbines in oﬀshore
wind farms, where in the year 2014 almost 91 percent of the wind turbines were installed on
monopiles [3]. The foundation type consists of a circular steel tube which is embedded into
the seabed with use of hammering. The pile is constructed with use of tubular steel sections
==PAGE:22==
State of the art
4
which are welded together. On top of the monopile a transition piece is installed, which acts
as a transition between the tower and the monopile.
In recent years great focus has been put on making larger and more optimized monopile
solutions, which means that the boundaries and limits for the structure is constantly chal-
lenged and expanded. Traditionally the monopile and transition piece are connected with
use of a grouted connection, where the space between the monopile and the transition piece
is ﬁlled with grout. The principle of the grouted connection is adopted from jacket struc-
tures used for oﬀshore drilling platforms. However the jackets are primarily axially loaded,
where the monopile instead experiences large bending moments [54]. The bending moments
introduces tensile stresses in the grout, which can cause cracks in the grout and settlements
of the transition piece relative to the monopile [32]. This has been mended by producing the
monopile as a conical shape, which limits the settlement of the transition piece. However the
issue creates a desire to investigate new solutions for connecting the monopile and transition
piece. One suggestion is to use a bolted ﬂange connection between the transition piece and
the monopile. This solution is currently being employed in a number of new design projects,
however there is still a challenges to be considered. Figure 1.1 shows a monopile with the
ﬂange and the installation of the transition piece onto the monopile.

Currently analytical methods for analysing the ring ﬂange connection exist and are widely
used for design of towers and masts. The procedure for ultimate and fatigue analysis is
described in a number of articles, however for oﬀshore wind turbines the application of ring
ﬂange connections has been limited to the joining of the tower. Hence the literature con-
siders bolts with smaller diameters than the one applied for the monopile transition piece
connection. Additionally the highly corrosive environment, in which the ﬂange connection
is located, must be included in the investigation.
Furthermore there is a desire to develop a numerical procedure for investigating the structural
capacity of the ﬂange connection. At the present state the analytical approach is mainly used
in combination with simple numerical models to validate the analytical solution. In general
the analytical procedures are regarded as conservative and therefore it can be valuable to
establish a numerical procedure which can challenge the analytical solution and lead to an

optimisation of the connection. Numerical analyses of bolted connections have been reported
on in various articles, where the general model geometry consists of a ﬂange segment with
one bolt. The investigations mostly relate to ﬁndings concerning preloading of bolts. For
the monopile and transition piece connection the desire is to determine the ultimate and
fatigue capacity with use of numerical procedures, as this topic has not been widely studied
and documented in the present literature.
1.2
Objective
The objective in this thesis is to perform an initial study of the capacity for a ring ﬂange
connection used for an oﬀshore wind turbine foundation. The ﬂange connection is considered
to be ideal, which means that all imperfections are neglected.
The overall objective is
formulated as:
• Investigate the ultimate and fatigue capacity with use of existing analytical expressions,
which are altered with regard to the monopile and transition piece connection.
• Establish a numerical model of the ﬂange connection from where the ultimate and
fatigue capacity can be determined.
• Investigate whether the numerical procedure allows for an optimisation of the ﬂange
connection by comparing the analytical and numerical solution.
The present thesis is a collaboration between the Technical University of Denmark and COWI
A/S, where COWI has supplied information on the bolted connection along with guidance
during the project period.
1.3
Scope of work
The scope of work in this thesis involves an initial theoretical study of literature within the
relevant topics of monopile foundations, bolted ﬂange connections, analytical bolted ﬂange
approach, fatigue and numerical modelling. Each of these topics will be presented in theo-
retical chapters, which form the basis for the ﬂange capacity investigation.
The work in the thesis is primarily comprised of two parts. First an analytical investigation
of the ﬂange connection, where recognised analytical methods for ring ﬂange connections are
studied and altered for the application in the oﬀshore wind turbine foundation.
In the second part numerical models of the bolted ﬂange connection are established, where
the ﬁnite element software tool Abaqus has been utilised.
Finally a discussion and conclusion on the ﬁndings is oﬀered along with recommendations
for future work.
The investigation is carried out using a reference case provided by COWI which is pre-
sented in Chapter 7. The design of the bolted ﬂange is based on an oﬀshore wind farm that
is currently being constructed.

Bolted connections
Chapter

The ring ﬂange connection employed in the MP/TP connection is installed using bolts.
In general bolted joints are a very common element in steel construction design and are
employed in many sizes and steel grades. The bolted joint can be designed to transfer loads
in tension or shear. Furthermore the bolt can be pretensioned, which increases the resistance
towards fatigue. By applying pretension the stress generated in the bolt from external load
becomes dependent on the stiﬀness ratio between the bolt and clamped parts. The following
chapter gives an introduction to the topic of bolted connections in general and the eﬀect and
advantages of using preloaded bolts.
2.1
Bolts
Bolts are used for many diﬀerent types of structures, hence they are produced in many
sizes and steel grades. The typical bolt assembly consists of diﬀerent parts; bolt, nut and
a number of washers. The diﬀerent parts of the bolt are denoted by diﬀerent names, see
Figure 2.1. Bolts of high steel strength is often used for purposes where preload is applied.
This is because the high strength bolts can be tightened until they have very high tensile
stresses leading to the connected parts being tightly clamped together between the head and
the nut. When using high strength bolts material can be saved and the pretension torque
reduced. This permits the loads to be primarily transferred through friction [44]. The general
designation for bolts is Mxx where the M means that it is a bolt with metric thread, where
the xx is the outer diameter of the thread in mm. Hence the notation M64 applies to a bolt
with a diameter of 64 mm [13].

When using bolted connections in a structure some particular aspects must be considered.
This includes the size of bolt hole, ﬁt of contact surfaces and hydrogen embrittlement.
The bolt hole size inﬂuences how the connection acts. If the bolt hole is too small rigid
alignment conditions may occur between the structural members. This can also cause prob-
lems during erection of the structure as the bolt holes may not line up properly. Hence it
can be valuable to use larger hole sizes. In standards like DS/EN 1993-1-8 tabular values are
provided for bolt hole sizes with respect to the bolt size. The holes can either have the same
clearance in all directions or be slotted. Slotted holes are used in cases where it is needed to
adjust the clearance in one particular direction, either aligned parallel or transverse to the
direction of the applied load. However when using oversized and slotted holes some addi-
tional plate material is removed compared to normal sized bolt holes. This means that there
might be an reduction of clamping force, resulting from the smaller contact area. However
this eﬀect can be reduced by applying hardened washers.
An issue when using high strength bolts in a bolted connection is hydrogen embrittlement.
High strength steel may experience delayed fracture caused by hydrogen. When corrosive
reactions occur hydrogen is produced, which is absorbed by the high strength steel bolts.
A high hydrogen content can lead to cracks due to embrittlement. The cracks originate at
the surface where the corrosion occurs. The cracking due to stress corrosion can be deﬁned
as failure under a combined action of stress and corrosion. This both accounts for external
stresses and internal residual stresses. When determining how susceptible the bolts are to-
wards hydrogen embrittlement a number of things must be considered such as the level of
stress present and the bolt material strength and resistance towards fatigue. Furthermore
the process used to produce the bolts and the corrosion protection coating should be eval-
uated. From tests it is indicated that a steel with high strength is more sensitive towards
stress corrosion and hydrogen embrittlement [28].

2.3
Connections with preloaded bolts
During the ﬁrst years of oﬀshore construction many bolt failures occurred. This has resulted
in more strict requirements for the design of bolts. This is especially due to the corrosive
environment and the dynamic loads [43]. Because of the dynamic loading it is recommended
that bolts are pretensioned. The pretension means that the bolts are more eﬀective with
respect to fatigue capacity, as the bolt will only experience a fraction of the applied external
load. By pretensioning of the bolts a clamping pressure is established between the connected
parts, hence enables the load to be transferred by frictional resistance which enhances the
ability to withstand alternating forces.
When using preloaded bolts in a ﬂanged tension connection, the preload helps compress-
ing the ﬂanges such that the surfaces are in contact [36]. When preloading the bolt the load
is gradually applied causing tension and elongation of the bolt while the ﬂanges experience
compression. The behaviour of the members can be described by a force triangle as illus-
trated in Figure 2.4. In the force diagram the behaviour under pretension is divided into
four stages. The ﬁrst stage is the initial stage before preload force Fp is applied. Between
the ﬁrst and second stage pretension is applied to the bolt, where the bolts elongates while
the ﬂanges shortens. Furthermore the clamping force Fc in the plates and the tensile force
Ft in the bolt are equal. The full pretension is applied at stage 2. After the second stage
an external force Fe is applied, which causes further elongation of the bolt. When the bolt
elongates the plates expands as well, hereby reducing the clamping force. This means that
the external load becomes the sum of ∆Ft and ∆Fc. Usually the plate stiﬀness is higher
than the stiﬀness of the bolt, hence the additional force in the bolt is much smaller when
compared to the external force. At the third stage the force generated in the bolt exceeds
the pretension force. This means that the ﬂanges will separate. When the ﬂanges separate
the bolts will experience fatigue failure and therefore it is desired to avoid separation of the
plates in cases of cyclic loading [36].

Fatigue failure is one of the main concerns in the design of oﬀshore structures.
This is
due to the structure being subjected to cyclic loads from wind and waves thereby causing
alternating stresses in the connection. In order to investigate the fatigue capacity of the
bolted connection it is important to understand what fatigue is and how it inﬂuences the
bolted connection.
3.1
Deﬁnition of fatigue
Fatigue in a structure or connection can occur when it is exposed to cyclic load conditions.
The cyclic loads result in a constantly changing stress state which can lead to development
of cracks in the structure.
When the cracks become suﬃciently large the eﬀective cross
section area will be reduced to an extent where the structure fails due to the load exceeding
the resistance capacity. The fatigue damage usually occurs at vulnerable locations in the
structure, this often being at welds and bolted joints. The common practice is to divide
fatigue life into three stages, crack initiation, crack propagation and ﬁnal fracture [8].

Final fracture is where the structure fails due to fatigue. For the two phases crack initiation
and crack propagation the portion of fatigue life spend in each phase depends on the detail
geometry, material and loading [43]. If looking at the crack development in base material
without signiﬁcant notches the initiation time is long compared to the propagation time.
However for details like weld toes, which experience more severe stress concentrations, cracks
often initiate early in the fatigue life, meaning that the propagation phase constitutes the
majority of the fatigue life. Especially connections like welds and bolted joints are susceptible
towards fatigue failure, where the most common failure mode is where the crack surfaces are
pulled apart due to tensile forces. The behaviour of the cracks can be substituted by three
modes as illustrated in Figure 3.2.

In mode I the crack evolves perpendicular to the principle stress direction, therefore being
the tensile mode. This means that the crack surfaces are being pulled apart. Mode II and
III are shearing modes, the crack surfaces moves either in sliding or tearing relative to each
other. The shearing modes can lead to small crack growth, but the tensile mode is the dom-
inant one causing large crack growth for details subjected to tensile stresses. For structures
subjected to ﬂuctuating stresses fatigue capacity is often design decisive. This is because
the stress needed to cause fatigue damage can be much less than the yield strength of the
material.
For a structure subjected to cyclic loads, where it is predicted that cracks will propagate
beyond the initiation phase, the term fatigue life is used. Fatigue life is a measure for the
predicted life expectancy and is deﬁned as the number of stress cycles, at a certain amount of
stress, that the structure can handle before failure. Four primary parameters are governing
for fatigue life of a structure.
• Stress range
• Geometrical design
• Material properties
• Surrounding environment
The stress range is deﬁned as the stress diﬀerence the investigated detail experiences for each
load cycle. This parameter is very decisive for the fatigue life as this determines whether
fatigue cracks initiates.
3.2
Fatigue in bolted connections
When using bolted connections there is a risk of fatigue damage in the bolts. This is due to
the bolts often being subjected to large tensile stresses, especially for tension connections.
For a bolted ﬂange, as the one designed for the MP/TP connection, there is a risk of large
stress concentrations in the bolt and at the weld neck which can lead to fatigue failure. In the
bolt large stress concentrations are formed due to the discontinuities in the bolt geometry,
an example being the transition from bolt head to shank. In order to fully understand how
==PAGE:32==
Fatigue in bolted connections
14
a bolted joint acts under fatigue, ﬁrst the behaviour of the bolt itself must be studied.
The most common location for bolts to experience crack propagation is at locations with a
sudden change in geometry, meaning that these locations are especially susceptible to high
stress concentrations. The stress concentrations are typically developed in the threaded re-
gion at the nut, at the thread run-out and in the shank region just below the bolt head
[43]. This means that when a bolt is subjected to ﬂuctuating stresses cracks will initiate and
propagate either in the radius just under the bolt head or in the ﬁrst thread above the nut
[29]. The threaded region is sensitive due to its minor diameter compared to the shank area.
Furthermore the thread acts as a notch at the thread root, which causes a local concentra-
tion of stress. Figure 3.3 illustrates the typical locations for local stress concentrations in
the bolt.

Between the bolt and nut a transferring of loads happens. The loads are transferred through
the contact thread surfaces between the nut and bolt, which causes bending stresses in
the thread. The load transfer is usually unevenly distributed between the contact surfaces
where the load transfer in most cases, dependent on geometry and material properties, is
concentrated at the ﬁrst engagement of the thread faces, location c in Figure 3.3. If the nut
experiences plastic deformations, the load transfer can become more uniformly distributed.
This is the desired result which means that the washer should be made of a softer material
than the bolt.
For tension connections the fatigue performance depends on the structural detailing and
the applied bolt preload [29]. When assessing the fatigue behaviour of the bolted ﬂange con-
nection the system can be viewed as a spring system, where the bolt acts a spring and the
clamped parts of the ﬂange act as another spring. When determining the spring stiﬀnesses
it is common to assume that the ﬂange thickness is large such that bending ﬂexibility and
prying forces can be neglected [29].
The forces variate in the bolts when external load is applied. The variation is dependent on
the stiﬀness ratio of the bolts and ﬂanges. Hence the bolts experience less load variation if
the ﬂexibility is high. Thus preloading of the bolts means that fatigue failure of the bolts can
be avoided as long as the preloading force is higher than the external load. When the ﬂanges
are clamped together by the bolt they will have a contact area. The size and placement of
the contact area depend on factors like placement, diameter size and preloading force of the
bolts. The contact force is generated by tightening of the bolts and in order to reduce the
force variation as much as possible the location of the contact area is important.

The weld detail must be considered in the design of the ﬂange connections as welds in general
are sensitive towards fatigue. This is due to numerous reasons. One reason is that at the
weld there will be stress concentrations deﬁned as local increase in stresses, relative to the
nominal stress, caused by geometrical changes. In the design standard GL2012 the stress
concentrations are divided into two categories A and B. For category A applies that the
stress concentrations are due to fabrication such as weld imperfections. This covers under-
cut, concave surface and misalignments due to the plates not being perfectly aligned. Axial
misalignment can increase the risk of fatigue in the weld. The misalignment happens if the
two pieces are not directly aligned. Secondary bending stresses can occur as the welded joint
will try to straighten. This should be considered in the design as the heat from the welding
process creates deformations and misalignment. Category B entails the stress concentrations
caused by the design such as the weld being located to near a chamfer. Typically the stress
concentrations due to fabrication are included in the S-N curves used to estimate fatigue
life. However this only applies when the fabricator follows the speciﬁed tolerances, whereas
the design stress concentration factors need to be considered. Another reason is that the
ﬂange connection is placed in a corrosive environment possible leading to stress corrosion
cracking (SCC). Also welding residual stresses should be considered. The welding process
introduces residual stresses into the connection members. The residual stresses are due to
the thermal process where a diﬀerential expansion of the material happens during heating.
When the material cools down it will try to shorten into the original position but due to the
weld the deformation is constraint, hence leading to the build up of residual stresses [44].
Welds are vulnerable toward fatigue due to the geometry. The triangular weld shape causes
stress concentrations at the weld toe, meaning that crack propagation occurs easier at the
weld than in non-welded members. The smoother the weld detail the lesser the stress con-
centration, which suggests what a larger angle can reduce the size of the stress concentration
whereby increasing the resistance towards fatigue. In the connection of the monopile and
transition piece the desired possible failure point of the connection is at the bolt. In order to
secure that this is true the weld seam must be placed in suﬃcient distance from the ﬂange
surface. Therefore an adequate height of the weld neck is crucial for the capacity of the con-
nection. The height is determinant for the magnitude of the stress occurring near the weld.

Due to the geometry design there is a considerable change in dimension when transitioning
from the ﬂange to the weld neck along with a sharp corner. Due to the geometry of the weld
an already increased level of stress is present, and maybe weakened material due to heat
processing. Then if the weld neck is too short large stresses are forming just above the weld
leading to failure. By increasing the weld neck height the stress concentration at the corner
and at the weld will not directly interfere and the risk of failure in that position is very much
reduced. Moreover when the weld neck is short it becomes very stiﬀand the stresses cannot
spread as strains before reaching the weld. Therefore it is important to ensure a suﬃciently
large weld neck height to move the most stressed area away from the weld. Further if the
height is large enough the weld can be neglected in the investigation of the joint itself.
If the height is not suﬃcient there is a risk that crack propagation will occur at the weld
leading to failure in that point and not in the bolted ﬂange. An example of the inﬂuence
of the weld neck height is given in an article published by the danish newspaper Ingeniøren
[56]. The failure applied to a wind turbine tower, but the same theory should be applicable
for the substructure. A wind turbine tower in Samsoe collapsed after just 11 years due to a
crack just above the weld toe. The design life time was 20-25 years. Here the weld neck was
25 mm high, which proved to be too low for the connection leading to collapse of the tower.
4.2
Installation and manufacturing tolerances
In the production of steel members it is inherent and unavoidable to have diﬀering geometrical
dimensions when considering the nominal value in the design and the actual dimensions in
the ﬁnished member. One reason is due to the fabrication often involving high temperature
processes when the components are formed and joined. When applying a heat process like
welding a certain amount of shrinkage and in some situations deformations will occur due to
residual stresses. Deviations in the geometrical dimensions can have a substantial eﬀect on
the behaviour and capacity of the ﬁnal connection, thus the designers are bound to specify
which tolerance limits apply for various parts in the design. Further it should be planned
how to manage the deviations to ensure that the design meets the performance requirements.
The Eurocode series EN 14399 speciﬁes tolerances for bolted connections used for preloading,
where EN 14399-1 states how the members of the bolt assembly should be veriﬁed along with
the accuracy level [20]. The bolts in the joint are of the system type HV and therefore the
EN 14399-4 can be consulted for rules on the bolt and nut [21]. The part also describes
how the maximum bolt force should be during tightening and at which angle the bolt or nut
should be turned. For plain washers EN 14399-5 applies [22]. Common for the EN 14399
series is that tabular values are only provided for bolts up to size M36. For bolts of larger
diameters expanded German versions exist [51]. The design tolerances can be deﬁned within
various ﬁelds, where ﬁve general deﬁnition groups can be used to categorize the tolerances
[72].
• Mechanical ﬁt, essential for the functioning between bolt and nut.
• Fabricated members ﬁt, involves eﬃcient assembly. For a bolted connection the relative
position of the bolt hole is important for inserting the bolt.
• Flatness and straightness, parameters that aﬀect the stiﬀness and strength of the com-
ponents.

changes in the dimension, there are rules dictating the maximum allowable manufacturing
tolerance for the connection parts. In Appendix A the geometry with speciﬁed tolerance

and positive tolerance value are speciﬁed, meaning that the ﬂange can either be thicker or
thinner than nominal. It is on the safe side to assess the capacity with the lowest possible
thickness as the safety increases with increment of the ﬂange thickness. The tolerance value
for the bolt hole is very small. The small value is due to the eﬀect of the distance from the
bolt hole edge and the bolt itself. If the bolt hole diameter is too small the bolt will not ﬁt
and if it is too large the behaviour of the connection can vary from the intended one.
The ring ﬂange must be measured and validated both before and after it is attached to the
monopile top by welding. It is important that the ﬂatness and the thickness of the ﬂange is
within the speciﬁed limits. Deviations of the bolt circle diameter from the nominal diameter,
deviations of each bolt from nominal position in all directions and deviations of bolt hole di-
ameters must be checked at various locations around the circumference. Also the roundness
of the inner and outer diameter must be checked. If the roundness criteria is not fulﬁlled
issues can arise with the installation of the bolts. For the investigated connection the out of
roundness tolerance is 2 mm both for the outer and inner diameter. The tolerance is very
sensitive when comparing to the pile diameter of 6 m. However the connection contains 140
bolts which are very tight ﬁtted. Few millimetres deviation can cause great issues during
installation and have a great impact on the capacity as the parts may not be ideally aligned
if the out of roundness is too big. Especially the roundness must be measured after the
welding has been performed. During the welding process residual stresses are created which
can lead to further unwanted deformations in the ring ﬂange. Hence the ﬂange can sat-
isfy the roundness criteria before being attached to the pile, but the welding process might
cause deformations. If the roundness criteria is dissatisﬁed a sensitive issue occurs as at this
stage the monopile with the ﬂange attached has already been produced. Therefore a out of
roundness issue can result in extensive numerical analyses where it must be investigated how
this change aﬀects the performance of the substructure. One solution to avoid the out of
roundness issue could be to extend the weld neck, which would mean that the ﬂange is less
aﬀected by the residual stresses and hence deformations introduced by the welding process.

4.3
Bolting technique
When using a bolted connection in an environment with dynamic loading the bolts must
be pretensioned in order to achieve the highest strength capacity against fatigue failure.
Therefore the bolting technique used to install the bolts are of great importance. For the
investigated design case the HV stretch bolt system has been discussed as this system has
a number of advantages for the oﬀshore appliance [51]. To achieve the necessary ductility
in the bolt, washer and nut assembly two general approaches exist, the HR system and the
HV system [5]. The HV system uses thinner bolts and a shorter thread length to obtain the
desired ductility by plastic deformation of the threads within the bolt. During the tightening
process the bolt thread might be subjected to plastic strain, thus it is not allowed to re-use
bolt assemblies that have been fully preloaded.
When installing and preloading the bolts various methods can be used.
One method is
the torque method where the bolts are tightened using a torque wrench. Torque is applied
to the nut where the force magnitude must be designed to overcome the friction between
the nut and the adjacent surface, usually a washer, against which it rotates.
When the
bolt is tightened the shank experiences a direct stress which is due to the elongation strain,
and a torsional stress which origins from the torque acting on the threads [69]. Most bolt
manufacturers provide the preloading force that should be applied, otherwise the magnitude

When applying preload with use of the torque method the Kellermann-Klein formula is used
to describe the relationship between the torque, the clamp load and the friction coeﬃcient,
where the guidelines in ISO 16047 should be followed [2]. The formula is used to determine
the tightening torque based on thread pitch and diameter, the desired clamping force and
friction coeﬃcients [37]. When installing a preloaded connection the bolt tightening should
be performed by an authorized person, who follows the approved procedure provided by the
bolt manufacturer. If the tightening of the bolts is not done properly there is a risk of the
bolts loosening over time which can lead to severe damage. An example is the collapse of an
onshore wind turbine in Sweden in December 2015 [53]. The tower was constructed of six
sections each joined with a bolted ﬂange connection. Failure happened in the ﬁrst section
near the foundation leading to the collapse of the tower. The failure was due to the joint
suﬀering from fatigue causing the bolts to have insuﬃcient resistance towards the operational
loads. The fatigue in the bolts generated from the pretension force being too low compared
to the magnitude speciﬁed by the designers. The reduced pretension force was mainly a re-
sult of inadequate installation and inspection, where the assembler who performed the ﬁnal
torqueing of the bolts had no experience with pretensioning. Therefore the proper torque
force was not applied and the connection was not able to resist the force and consequently
the low pretension force led to a decreased fatigue life time. The failure incident emphasizes
the importance of correct preloading technique as seemingly insigniﬁcant details can have a
great impact on the performance of the structure. Due to the critical eﬀect of the preload
the bolted connection should be maintained during the operation life of the wind turbine.
It should be tested that the deﬁned preload level is maintained and if necessary the bolts
should be retightened to maintain the design capacity.

5.1
Ultimate limit state capacity
When considering the ultimate limit state it is investigated how much force the connection
can withstand while still behaving elastically. This means that the strain developed is still
reversible. When the connection is subjected to a certain load magnitude the strain becomes
irreversible and the material is yielding. In this region the material is behaving plastic. In
ultimate limit state the capacity is derived for a static force meaning that no time dependency
is considered. For the bolted connection the tensile resistance force in the tubular section at
ultimate limit state can be derived by Petersen’s method, where a number of failure modes
are investigated concerning development of plastic yield hinges [52]. The method is applicable
for L shaped bolted ﬂange connections subjected to tensile forces. When determining the

5.1.1
Tensile resistance force
The ultimate limit state capacity can be determined using elasto-plastic theory [52]. An
equivalent tension force is applied to the segment, where the force is derived based on the
normal stresses in the shell. The tensile resistance force is determined as the maximum ten-
sion force the joint can experience before failing. The failure of the joint is due to interactions
between moment, normal and shear forces, where moment and normal force interacts at the
outer edges of the ﬂanges. At the bolt there is a shear and moment interaction while the
tubular shell both experiences normal, shear and moment force interaction.
According to Petersen three possible failure modes must be considered as illustrated in Fig-
ure 5.2. The failure modes consider rupture and development of plastic yield hinges in the
connection. The ﬁrst failure mode A implies that the connection fails due to rupture of
the bolt. This failure mode often results in the highest value of the limit tension force as
a bolted connection as a rule is designed with the bolt being the most resistant part [43].
The second mode B considers the development of a plastic hinge in the shell along with bolt
rupture. In the third mode C plastic hinges are developed in the ﬂange and the shell. The
maximum allowable tension force is determined for each failure mode, where the lowest force
value becomes the governing in the design veriﬁcation.
The third failure mode C can be divided into two cases D and E which must be investi-
gated [34]. The two additional failure modes consider a more enhanced bending resistance
of the ﬂange where the eﬀect of the bolt force transfer through the washer is included. In
failure mode D the development of a plastic hinge occurs at the net section of the bolt line.
The failure mechanism is strengthened by the additional bending moment ∆Mpl,2 generated
due to part of the bolt force being transferred through the washer towards the shell. For
failure mode E a plastic hinge develops at the gross section of the bolt at the inner side of
the washer [73].

5.2

The most accurate fatigue life is obtained by performing an analysis on the most stressed
location, in this case the most stressed bolt segment. Fatigue behaviour depends on the
interaction between the component geometry, the material characteristics and the loading
sequence. The fatigue capacity of a bolted ring ﬂange connection is determined based on the
cyclic loads aﬀecting it and thus a simulated load cycle loop has been generated for wind
and wave loads.

history is very irregular and complex, rainﬂow counting is used to reduce the load history
into a series of constant amplitude events contained in a Markov matrix [55]. With use of the
Markov matrix the stress range history can be assessed and ﬁnally the accumulated damage
can be determined using Palmgren Miner summation.

6.1
Numerical modelling in relation to bolted connections
Some central model parameters must be considered when establishing a numerical model of a
bolted connection [74]. It is possible to model and simulate the behaviour of a bolt with use
of diﬀerent approaches, where the bolt is modelled in varying level of detailing. According
to VDI-2230 part 2 four basic FE model classes can be deﬁned [74].
Model class I: The bolt itself is not modelled, but is interpreted by simulating the internal
loads and stressed generated by the bolt.
Model class II: The bolt is represented as a line element.
==PAGE:58==
Numerical modelling in relation to bolted connections
40
Model class III: The bolt is represented as a solid body where the thread region has not
been modelled. The geometry should be adjusted such that the bolt model is in compliance
with the real bolt.
Model class IV: The bolt are modelled in full detail including thread.
The most accurate interpretation of the bolt behaviour is determined for model class IV.
However this model is also the most expensive in modelling and computation time hence it
is often preferred to use a less detailed model. The bolted ﬂange model is modelled in ac-
cordance with model class III as this allows for inclusion of preload and contact interactions
along with an accurate prediction of stress development in the entire bolt.
When establishing a numerical model of a bolted ﬂange some model properties should be
considered and possibly applied to the model [74],
• Compliances of bolt and clamped parts
• Bolt preload
• External loads
• Contact conditions in the interface
• Contact between bolt shaft and plate hole
• Linear or nonlinear material behaviour
For a realistic and full assessment of the bolt and ﬂange behaviour under separation due to
loading it is essential to include bolt load in the numerical simulation. Furthermore the con-
tact conditions and interactions should be included as these allow for the ﬂange separation
to develop, where also the contact interactions introduce nonlinearities in the analysis [16].
It should be carefully considered for which areas contact should be deﬁned. The contact
area between the two ﬂanges are essential for the behaviour, where the friction should be
chosen based on the quality of the material. When the joint deforms there is a possibility of
contact interfaces between the bolt shank and plate hole, which should be considered when
manufacturing tolerances are included.
Furthermore the material model used to simulate material behaviour should be investigated.
If the numerical model is only used to simulate behaviour within purely elastic loading it
is suﬃcient to include a linear material model. However for limit state investigations where
development of plastic yield hinges are critical the plasticity should be considered be deﬁning
the material as elastic-plastic.
In order to include all these eﬀects in the numerical model a nonlinear analysis must be
performed.

6.2
Finite element analysis and Abaqus
To perform the numerical analysis of the bolted ﬂange connection the software tool Abaqus
has been utilized. Abaqus uses the ﬁnite element method to solve numerical problems. In
a ﬁnite element analysis the structure is divided into a number of ﬁnite elements, which are
combined in a mesh. Each element is represented by a set of simple equilibrium equations.
The element equations are derived based on the geometry of the elements and are determinant
for the element properties. The problem is solved by solving the system of element equations
with respect to the applied boundary conditions. In Abaqus the analysis is performed by
dividing the problem history into a number of steps, where conditions in terms of boundaries,
contact and loads are applied. The division into steps are convenient for analysis procedures
where the conditions changes during the analysis. Abaqus provides the possibility to perform
both linear and nonlinear static analyses along with other analysis types. For a nonlinear
analysis the equation system cannot be solved with use of simple element equations, whereas
a iteration method is used instead. The iteration is performed by specifying the load as
a function of time, where each step is divided into several increments. Most often several
iterations are required for solving each step. The following sections describe some of the
measures that must be included in the numerical model of the bolted ﬂange in relation to
the software tool Abaqus.
6.3
Nonlinear solution method
Nonlinearities can occur when the analysis involves large displacement eﬀects, nonlinearity
in the material or boundary nonlinearities in form of contact or friction [65]. Abaqus uses
Newton’s method to solve the nonlinear equilibrium equations. The solution is most often
obtained as a series of increments, where iterations have been performed to obtain equi-
librium within each increment. Newton’s method has a ﬁnite radius of convergence, if the
increment size is to large it is not possible to obtain a solution as the initial state is too far
from the desired one, meaning that it is outside the radius of convergence [66].
When performing an numerical analysis the approximate solution to the problem is gov-
erned by a set of diﬀerential equations. The diﬀerential equations can be solved with use of
the virtual work principle. When solving a problem, where the response of the object is not
linear (stress or displacement) a nonlinear analysis must be performed. Newton’s method is
usually applied as this method has the best convergence rate [66].

6.4
Elements
When using the ﬁnite element method to approximate a solution the model is divided into
ﬁnite elements referred to as meshing. It is possible to apply diﬀerent types of elements,
where the elements are characterized by their shape and number of nodes. Figure 6.2 shows
the typical elements for modelling of solid models in two and three dimensions. It is possible
either to use linear or quadratic elements. For linear elements nodes are placed in each corner
of the element, where for quadratic elements an additional node is placed in the midpoint at
each element side. The additional node allows the quadratic element to use polynomial shape
functions when simulating the stress variation over the element, while for linear elements the
variation is linear [42].
When performing analyses with large deformation problems it is preferred to use linear
elements as these result in a more robust numerical analysis compared to elements of higher
order [25]. This is due to the more simple formulation of the linear element, as the linear
element is only able to behave plastic or elastic, contrary to the quadratic element that can
be plastic and elastic at the same time.

When using elements of the type hexahedral or quadralaterals two types of integration can
be applied, either full integration or reduced integration. The method used for numerical
integration is called Gaussian Quadrature, where a number of Gauss points are calculated
and used to solve the integral. When applying reduced integration less Gauss points are
used in the calculation. The more Gauss points used for the solving the problem the more
accurate the result, but also a longer computation time. Reduced integration means less
computation time but it can have an eﬀect on the accuracy of the solution. However for
nonlinear problems it is advisable to use reduced integration [7]. This is due to the stiﬀness
matrix being overestimated for displacement-based FE analyses, where fewer elements would
reduce the stiﬀness of the element. When using reduced integration one should be aware and
monitor the hourglass eﬀect. When the elements only have one integration point there is a
risk that they will distort in an hourglass shape and the stiﬀness matrix becomes unstable.
This eﬀect is considered by enabling hourglass control, where the hourglass energy should
be less than 5 % of the internal energy of the system [25].
6.5
Contact formulation
Contact interactions are introduced in the numerical model when contact between elements is
established during analysis. When introducing contact interactions into the numerical model
severe nonlinearities arise due to inequality conditions resulting in discontinuous stiﬀness [50].
Contact interactions are introduced to improve accuracy and performance of the model.
When surfaces interact with one another usually a transmission of shear and normal forces
happens across the interface. A relationship exist between the shear force and normal force,
which is deﬁned as the friction between the contacting bodies [65].
The friction can be
expressed in terms of stresses at the interface of the bodies. The friction is determined using
Coulombs friction model, where the maximum allowable frictional shear stress is related to
the contact pressure between the bodies. In basic form the Coulomb friction model allows
two contacting surfaces to carry shear stress up to a certain magnitude across the interface
before they start sliding relative to one another.
This state is known as sticking.
The
coeﬃcient of friction is deﬁned, this deﬁnes at which critical shear stress as a fraction of
contact pressure sliding occurs.
==PAGE:62==

Contact pairs can be deﬁned as surface to surface contact, where one surface provides the
master surface and another the slave surface. The master surface should generally be chosen
as the stiﬀer body and the active contact region should change most rapidly on the master
surface. To avoid convergence issues it is generally advised to apply a more reﬁned mesh on
the slave than on the master [38]. When using surface to surface contact there is a reduced
likelihood of large localized penetrations compared to node to surface contact. Furthermore a
more accurate result without matching meshes are obtained along with a better distribution
of contact forces among the master nodes. The contact is enforced in an average sense over
a region surrounding each slave node, where each contact constraint is formulated based on
an integral over the region surrounding the slave surface.
Small sliding can be used to deﬁne the interaction between two deformable bodies which
can move relatively little with respect to each other. The small sliding is based on a slave
node always interacting with the same subset of master nodes. There is a risk that the
slave nodes penetrate into the master surface. The accuracy of the contact simulation can
be obtained by adjusting the initial position of the slave surface to ensure that all the slave
nodes initially in contact with the master have no penetration.
When deﬁning the contact behaviour, the behaviour is deﬁned in the normal and tangential
direction. For the normal behaviour hard contact is the default relationship. Hard contact
implies that no contact pressure is transmitted unless the slave nodes are in contact with
the master nodes. And further no penetration is allowed at each constraint location. There
is no limit to the magnitude of contact pressure that can be transmitted when the surfaces
is in contact. As default the tangential behaviour is frictionless, however the sliding can
be enforced by the penalty method which means that friction is included in the contact
determination.
6.6
Material modelling
The materials are introduced in the numerical model by deﬁning the material properties
for the applied materials. For the strength properties it is possible to include both elastic
behaviour and plastic behaviour, which is necessary in analyses where the model is experi-
encing plastic deformations. The material behaviour is described using stress strain curves,
which is usually determined by experiments.
6.6.1
Elastic material
The relation between stress and strain varies lineary for elastic materials. The elastic be-
haviour of steel can be described by Young’s modulus and Poisson’s ratio, where Young’s
modulus is the proportional relation between the strain and stress in the elastic region.
Poisson’s ratio deﬁnes the ratio of transverse strain to axial strain.
6.6.2
Plasticity and hardening
Plasticity is used to describe the behaviour of materials when the strain induced from the
applied load is irreversible. The permanent deformations occur when the elastic yield limit is

The Bauschinger’s eﬀect is incorporated by deﬁning the hardening of the steel material, where

is kept constant. This material model is considered to be the best choice when analysing
problems with cyclic low amplitudes [33].

When the pretension is applied a constant pressure equal to the pretension stress in the bolt
is imposed in the ﬂanges near the bolt, where no contact pressure exist at the ends of the
ﬂange. When the external tensile force increases a redistribution of contact forces happens.
The redistribution can be divided into three successive phases, where ﬁrst the external force
is received through a redistribution of stresses in the ﬂange surface. At this phase the bolt
force remains constant due to the preload applied. At the second phase the bolt force starts
increasing and the stress distribution continues at the ﬂange surface. Finally the ﬂanges has
separated to a degree where the stress no longer is able to redistribute. At this point only
the bolt receives the external load meaning that the axial force increases continuously. This
course is seen in Figure 8.18, where ﬁrst the contact pressure is evenly distributed around
the bolt hole. When the external load is applied the stress redistributes and only the inner
ﬂange is in contact. In Figure 8.18b the contact pressure is zero at the bolt, meaning that
only the bolt receives the external load from this point.
It is evident that the magnitude of the pretensioning force determines when these three
phases of stress redistribution occurs. Therefore a study regarding the magnitude of preload-
ing force has been performed on the bolted ﬂange segment. The study has been performed
for three diﬀerent magnitudes of preloading force applied to the numerical model. The bolt
was preloaded with 500 kN, 1000 kN and 1680 kN respectively. Figure 8.19 shows the re-
sults, where the bolt load as function of the imposed tensile force is presented along with
the contact forces as function of the tensile force.
When studying the evolution of bolt force as a function of external tensile force, it is seen in
Figure 8.19a that for all three cases the bolt load converge toward the same value. However
the diﬀerence is found in the bolt load range. The risk of fatigue in the bolt is larger when
the bolt experiences large stress ranges for cyclic loads. Therefore with a low preloading
force the bolt will experience more alternating stresses causing a lower resistance towards
fatigue. It is also observed that a higher preloading force allows for more tensile stress before
the bolt load increases, thereby conﬁrming the notion that the clamping eﬀect increases with
the magnitude of preloading force.

10.2
Numerical procedure
The ultimate limit state capacity for the bolted ﬂange is furthermore investigated with use
of numerical procedures. The numerical model is utilised to investigate whether the result
based on the analytical method can be optimised. The ultimate capacity can be determined
with use of diﬀerent approaches concerning the numerical model. The analytical solution
considers development of plastic yield hinges, and therefore the numerical investigation is
based on the plastic strain developed in the model at a certain load.
When desiring to determine the ultimate resistance force for the connection a process based
on the analytical solution and numerical modelling can be utilized. The wish is to determine
the ultimate resistance force for the ring ﬂange connection. There is no direct analytical so-
lution for verifying the full ﬂange model, only methods concerning the most critically loaded
bolt segment. This notion forms the basis for the numerical investigation, where a calibra-
tion model in form of an one bolt segment is developed. The analytically derived resistance
force is applied to the ﬂange segment model, which provides the foundation for the analysis
of the full ﬂange model. The plastic strains occuring by the imposed load is determined for

The approach with determining the ultimate capacity based on plastic strain in the ﬂange
segment and in the full ﬂange, has the advantages that it is not dependent on any tabular

as both the analytical method, the ﬂange segment and the full ﬂange model is involved.
Therefore in practise it can be useful to adopt a maximum allowable limit for plastic strain
development based on applicable codes. By employing a limit value for plastic strain ϵp,nom
a few options on how to determine the ultimate capacity becomes available. For use of these

experimentally determinate stress strain curves to obtain a correct result. Furthermore it

tensile force or the design moment and axial force dependent the model used. The plastic

Fatigue capacity by numerical analysis
The fatigue life of the bolted ﬂange connection is investigated with use of the numerical
model of the ﬂange connection.
The fatigue capacity is investigated by applying a load interval covering the full Markov
matrix to the model in Abaqus and extracting the stresses generated for each step in the
load interval. Stress transfer functions are determined within a MATLAB script, where also
the linear damage accumulation process presented in Section 5.2.4 is performed.
The damage accumulation method is the same as for the analytical solution except for a
change in detail category. The analytical method for assessing the stress transfer function
is based on a method which only considers pure axial stiﬀness of the bolt meaning that the
bending stresses are neglected. Therefore the reduced detail category 36* has to be applied
[34]. However for the numerical investigation the bolt stresses are approximated by the ac-
tual bolt stress transfer function, where both bending and axial stresses are included. This
means that the detail category 50 can be used for the fatigue calculation of the bolt for
the numerical analysis [59]. The detail category still needs to be reduced with the factor ks
determined by (5.45) as the bolt is larger than M30 [31]. The detail category 50 applies for
hot dip galvanised bolts [31].

The fatigue assessment based on the numerical analysis is performed in a number of steps
where a numerical analysis is performed in Abaqus to determine the bolt stresses generated
from external load. Based on the bolt stresses a linear damage accumulation is performed
using a MATLAB script which is found in Appendix I. The procedure for performing the
fatigue damage assessment for the numerical model is summarized in the steps:
• Deﬁne full range of loading and apply load range in Abaqus model
• Create ﬁeld output for relevant areas of interest
• Create stress transfer functions
• Treat Markov matrix and perform linear damage accumulation
The load history for fatigue loading is provided in form of Markov data and contains a very
large sum of axial forces.
Therefore the fatigue analysis is performed by deﬁning the full range of loading expected
during the life time in terms of either Fmin and Fmax for the ﬂange segment or the numerical
largest moment plus minus half the moment range for the full ﬂange, which is extracted from
the Markov matrix. The load is applied in Abaqus in a number of steps which cover the full
load range, for the bolted ﬂange 15 steps are used to apply the load. The load history both
contains compressive and tensile loads and are deﬁned in a range between -600 kN and 600

In a bolt the typical area of failure is in the threaded region and not in the region near
the bolt head. The bolt is usually manufactured by one of two processes; machining or form-

at the curvature between the bolt head and shank. The residual stresses and deformation
hardened material will help increase the fatigue strength of the bolt at the bolt head. For the
machining process the bolts are manufactured by turning, where common practise is to make
a larger curvature at the head than for forged bolts, to compensate for the missing eﬀect of
the favourable residual stresses. Due to the manufacturing process of the bolt the bolt head
should not be of concern for fatigue. If the bolt head was considered critical the bolt could
be replaced by a stud with nuts in both ends instead. However the numerical model of the
bolt is idealised and the threaded region is not included in the model. Furthermore the bolt
is modelled with a sharp corner at the bolt head. The sharp corner introduces a local peak
stress in the bolt due to a singularity from the sharp corner.

smaller slope than the curve based on the analytical method. This means that larger stress
ranges are calculated with use of the analytical method. The analytical transfer function
is established by the Schmidt Neuper method, where only axial stresses are considered in
the bolt. This means that stress from bending is disregarded and a reduced fatigue detail
category 36* has to be applied.
By performing a ﬁnite element analysis of the ﬂange connection it is possible to include
both stress caused by axial loading and bending in the bolt [59]. This leads to a more accu-
rate prediction of the bolt stress transfer function, and furthermore allows for usage of detail
category 50 instead, hence increasing the fatigue life estimation.
The diﬀerence in the stress transfer function implies that the Schmidt Neuper method pro-
vides a conservative assessment of bolt force. This notion is conﬁrmed by various experiments
conducted by Marc Seidel and Peter Schaumann.
Experiments were conducted on parts of original ring ﬂanges and on operating wind tur-
bine towers [58] [64]. The purpose was to investigate the bending and normal stresses due
to imposed external load. Here it was found that the bolt force and external load has a
correlation, where the variation in bolt force is much less than for the external load due
to initial pretensioning. The experimental ﬁndings were compared to simpliﬁed analytical
calculation methods. With comparison to the Schmidt Neuper method is was found that
the bolt stresses were overestimated for low tensile force for the analytical approach. This
implies that bending stresses should not be neglected in the fatigue investigation, as these
provides for a signiﬁcantly improved fatigue capacity. Therefore an alternative method for
approximating the stress transfer function has been developed by Seidel, however the overall
accepted approach remains the Schmidt Neuper method, which provides for a safe fatigue life
estimate [62]. The same results are obtained by Jakubowski [35] who performed experiments
on large scale bolted tubular sections.
In Figure 11.6 both the stress transfer function for the numerical model of the ﬂange segment
and the full ﬂange is seen. Within the fatigue load range the functions are coinciding which
indicates that the same fatigue capacity is found with use of the two models.
Finally the inﬂuence of the applied S-N curve can be discussed. The applied S-N curves
are based on experiments performed for test specimens under pure axial loading. The bolt
in the ﬂange connection is loaded by a combined axial force and bending moment due to
the eccentricity of the load application. Experiments on a bolt under fatigue loading have
been conducted by Schaumann and Marten [60], where the bolt stress was investigated for
pure axial loading, pure bending and a combination of bending and axial loading. Here it
was found that pure bending was more favourable for fatigue behaviour of the bolt than
pure axial loading, which suggests that it provides additional safety to use S-N curves based
on pure axial tests. Moreover the design S-N curves are based on experimental validation
of relevant fatigue properties but no experiments have been carried out for large bolt sizes
[59]. This may imply that the design standards are not directly applicable for the ﬂange
connection with large bolts even though being reduced with the factor ks.
Also the eﬀect of the preloading force should be discussed in the evaluation of the result.
The fatigue resistance is based on the joint geometry and the preloading force in the bolts.
==PAGE:111==
93
Fatigue limit state capacity
The magnitude of the preloading force is very determinant for the fatigue safety as a loss
of preload leads to less fatigue resistance. For a reduction of preload by 50 % the damage
sum by analytical calculation becomes 1.14, meaning that the structure is no longer safe.
This implies that it is very important to apply the correct pretension force in the bolts at
installation and furthermore maintain the connection throughout the life time of the wind
turbine. The same are concluded from Figure 8.19a where a numerical investigation of the
stress transfer function was carried out for diﬀerent magnitude of preload. Here it was found
that an increased preloading force leads to a more gentle slope, thus reducing the stress
ranges.

Conclusion
Chapter
12
The monopile solution used for foundation of oﬀshore wind turbines is currently investigated
with means to optimise the instalment. The bolted ring ﬂange connection is considered to
provide the desired optimisation and therefore it is of great emphasis to develop an approach
for determining the structural capacity in ultimate and fatigue limit state. The investigation
has been performed with use of analytical and numerical procedures.
The ultimate limit state capacity determination was carried out analytically with use of
Petersen’s method.
Here plastic hinge theory is applied to evaluate where plastic yield
hinges are most likely to develop in the ﬂange connection. The result is dependent on the
ﬂange geometry and provides an answer for the limit tensile force in the tube. The method
considers a simpliﬁed geometry where only a ﬂange segment containing one bolt is included
in the analysis.
The ultimate capacity was further investigated with use of ﬁnite element modelling. For
this purpose two numerical models were established; an one bolt segment and a model of the
full ﬂange connection. The capacity investigation was performed by a calibration process
with basis in the analytical derived result. The numerical model of the one bolt segment was
loaded with the analytically derived resistance force, from where the response and plastic
strains of the bolt segment was noted and used as failure criteria. The full ﬂange model was
then analysed for increasing load until the plastic strains were equal to the ones determined
by the ﬂange segment model.
By comparing the ultimate capacity for the analytical and numerical approach it was found
that the numerical analysis resulted in an increment of 17 % in capacity. The result im-
plies that the analytical approach is conservative, and that design capacity can be gained
by including the behaviour of the full ﬂange connection. A study was performed on the
development of force in the bolt as function of the external tensile force. The bolt force was
determined both for the bolt segment and for the corresponding bolt in the full ﬂange model.
Here it was found that the stress transfer function for the segment model had a steeper slope
than for the full ﬂange model. This indicates that the stress level increases more rapidly
in the bolt in the segment. This is explained by the stiﬀness of the model introduced by
contact interaction. The opening of the ﬂanges causes a redistribution of stresses, where
contact and stiﬀness rapidly is reduced for the ﬂange segment. For the full ﬂange contact
pressure is maintained for parts of the ﬂange, resulting in higher stiﬀness.
Also the fatigue capacity of the bolted connection was assessed. The fatigue damage ac-
cumulation is based on evaluation of stress ranges in the bolt due to cyclic loads. The stress
==PAGE:114==
96
ranges are determined based on stress transfer functions, where the functions have been
established analytically and numerically. For the analytical solution a method proposed by
Schmidt and Neuper was utilised. The method is used to estimate the bolt stress transfer
function, where a trilinear relation is used. The method only considers axial stiﬀness of the
connection members thus disregarding eﬀects from bending stresses.
The numerical analysis for determination of the fatigue life was conducted using the ﬂange
segment model, as it was found that a high mesh density is needed for an accurate result.
Furthermore it was found that the bolt stress transfer function for the segment and the full
ﬂange were coinciding for the fatigue load range. The numerical analysis allows for a more
accurate establishment of the stress transfer functions as both bending and axial stresses in
the bolt are considered.
It was found that the fatigue life prediction is improved signiﬁcantly with use of the nu-
merical solution in comparison to the analytical solution. The diﬀerence in capacity is a
result of the bolt stress transfer functions. The analytically derived function results in larger
stress ranges than the numerical model, where the stress range is almost constant for the
fatigue loading range. The results imply that, when employing the analytical approach for
fatigue assessment, a conservative result is obtained.
All the ﬁndings in this thesis in based on assumptions and simpliﬁcations concerning the
bolted ﬂange connection. Therefore in order to establish a numerical model which can fully
be utilised in the design of the ﬂange connection, further investigations must be included in
the study.
When determining the ultimate limit state capacity with use of numerical modelling, it
is necessary to include plastic material properties. It is therefore strongly advised to con-
duct experiments for high strength bolts with large diameters to determine the strain stress
relation. Also experiments should be performed for steel plates with large thickness. With-
out the correct material properties it is not possible to predict the true behaviour of the
connection within the plastic regime.
In present investigation the butt weld connecting the forged ﬂange and the tube is not
included in the investigation. However welds are susceptible towards fatigue failure. There-
fore it is of emphasis to investigate the inﬂuence of the butt weld. A sensitivity study can
also provide for optimisation of the weld neck height. It is crucial that the weld neck has
suﬃcient height to avoid failure in the weld. However it is very expensive to produce a forged
ﬂange with long weld neck. Therefore a detailed study on the butt weld inﬂuence in relation
to the bolted connection could provide a valuable result.
For an accurate prediction with use of the numerical model imperfections should be in-
cluded. In the current model the ﬂanges are perfectly aligned which provides the optimal
condition for contact interaction. Furthermore everything is perfectly installed. Some of the
issues described in Chapter 4 should be considered.
When the monopile is installed the hammer might cause damage to the ﬂange connection,
which could signiﬁcantly reduce the capacity of the connection. Therefore the possible in-
ﬂuence of damage from the installation process should be investigated with use of an impact
analysis. The result should be combined with the procedures described in this thesis in order
to estimate whether the installation process inﬂuences the capacity.
==PAGE:115==
97
Conclusion
Finally it was concluded from the present investigation that the fatigue resistance assess-
ment is most dependent on the bolt stress transfer function. Currently experiments have
been performed for bolts of diameters M36 [60]. Valuable knowledge could be obtain by
conducting experiments on a bolted L ﬂange with preloaded bolts. The experiments should
be performed for high strength bolts of large diameters subjected to tensile loads in order to
determine the stress transfer functions. The knowledge obtained could be used as a mean to
verify for the numerical model and possibly lead to further optimisation of the connection.

Appendix
E
When performing a numerical analysis, where the plastic behaviour of the material is of
interest, the material properties are usually applied in terms of stress strain curves. The
yield strength is dependent on the thickness of the plate. In various standards it is possible
to ﬁnd proposed properties for various steel grades related to plate thickness. For this thesis
the material properties suggested by DNVGL-RP-C208 are applied in the numerical model
[26]. However the material properties are only available for a plate thickness up to 100 mm.
This is suﬃcient for the tube, but the ﬂange thickness is 180 mm. It has not been possible
to ﬁnd any information on the plastic behaviour of a plate with this thickness, and therefore
it has been decided to do an extrapolation, based on the data provided for thinner plates,
to get an estimate on the properties. For future use it is strongly recommended to conduct
experiments on the matter, but for the current thesis it is considered accurate enough to use
a theoretically predicted yield strength as the primary objective in the thesis is to establish
methods to predict the capacity of the connection rather than on the actual results. The
deﬁnition of the stress strain curve used by DNVGL-RP-C208 is presented in Figure E.1.

F
Diﬀerent studies have been performed to verify the numerical model of the ﬂange segment.
This includes a study of the tubular height, a convergence analysis and the hourglass eﬀect.
Furthermore additional results and plots are provided for the ﬂange segment.
F.1
Height of tubular section
In the modelling of the ﬂange segment only part of the monopile and transition piece tube
has been modelled. As the boundary conditions and load are applied to the ends of the tube
segment it is necessary to verify that these are in suﬃcient distance from the bolted ﬂange
to avoid stress concentrations.
It is generally accepted that for a bar with constant cross section under axial loading F
the stress σ will be uniformly distributed across cross sectional area A. However this is an
assumption, that is not always directly applicable [77]. When an axial load is applied to a bar
the normal stress close to the location of the applied load will be unevenly distributed due
to deformations from the load. However further away the stress will be evenly distributed
as illustrated in Figure F.1. Therefore it is necessary to apply the load at suﬃcient distance
from the area of interest to ensure that the stress distribution is not aﬀected by local stress
concentrations due to the applied axial load. This is called the Saint Venant’s principle.
Figure F.1: Distribution of stress, from [77].
In order to verify that a suﬃcient height of the tube has been included in the ﬂange segment
model a study of the stress distribution has been conducted. Three models were created,
where the height from the ﬂange top surface to the end of the tubular section was 0.5 meter,
1 meter and 1.5 meter respectively. The three models are presented in Figure F.2. The stress
should be constant and symmetric for the two ﬂanges when the boundary and axial force are
in suﬃcient distance from the ﬂange. By studying the von Mises stress it is seen that above
a certain height the stress level is constant. The model with 0.5 meter of tube does not have
a constant stress level at the top and furthermore the stress is not evenly distributed for
the two ﬂanges. This implies that the tubular height should be larger. Another model with

Elements with reduced integration are used for the model. Hence the eﬀect of hourglass
eﬀect must be checked. Hourglass eﬀect means that ﬁrst order elements can act without
suﬃcient stiﬀness whereby produce zero energy node, deﬁned as hourglassing. For reduced
integration only one integration point is applied for each element, this means that the strain
calculated at the integration points are zero [66]. In order to control the hourglass eﬀect
Abaqus introduces artiﬁcial stiﬀness and damping. Therefore the total artiﬁcial strain energy
ALLAE and the total internal energy ALLIE must be monitored and compared with each
other. The artiﬁcial strain energy should be less than 5 percent of the internal energy to
ensure that no hourglass eﬀect is present in the model [26]. For the numerical model of the
bolted ﬂange segment the ALLAE and ALLIE values are presented in Figure F.9 and F.10.
The artiﬁcial strain energy is less than 1 % of the internal energy, which implies that no

The transfer of load between the ﬂange and bolt happens through compression between the
members. This means that a large compressive force is pressing on the ﬂange, which can

shear. The resistance capacity for a tension connection is most often determined by the ten-
sion resistance of the bolt. However in some cases the resistance is governed by the punching
shear resistance [13].
