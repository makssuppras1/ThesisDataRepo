TECHNICAL UNIVERSITY OF DENMARK
Abstract
Design and Implementation of Virtual Reality Controllers
by
Philip Berman - s123995
This thesis explores the physical traits of user interaction in virtual reality. Where the
current consumer standard of virtual reality products consists of holding a light controller
in each hand, there are potential drawbacks when their physical properties do not coincide
with that of simulated objects. If what the user is holding more closely resembles the
simulated tool they are using, there may be a greater sense of immersion and potentially
stronger handling in terms of the object’s purpose. By developing physical prototypes
of various object types and creating simulations for user testing, the following chapters
delve into what signiﬁcance the controller peripheral can have in a VR environment.

Introduction
In the current virtual reality market, one of the top priorities for developers lies within
immersion. The goal of a VR experience is to make the user feel as though they are
physically present within their virtual environment. It mimics real life in a way that
stimulates at least one of the user’s senses to make them believe that what they see around
them is truly there, at least on a subconscious level. The current state of the art medium
for virtual reality experiences on a consumer level is a virtual reality headset. These
headsets usually include a stereoscopic display (an image for each eye), headphones, and
3D-space tracking sensors. These components allow for the user to move and rotate
their head around while the content on the screens and in the audio adapts to the
user’s movements in real-time. Along with these headsets come controller peripherals.
These controllers are light, comfortable to grip, and can be used for most virtual object
interaction purposes within a simulation.
As the headsets and accompanying hardware are consumer products, they are produced
as one-size-ﬁts-all components.
That is to say the controllers are made to be easily
accessible towards both users and developers. In most applications the controllers are
simulated as a multitude of interactive objects such as guns, swords, or even just hands.
If implemented properly, the mind can be tricked into thinking that the object a user is
holding in a simulation is really in their grasp, allowing for a sense of ease in terms of
interaction. However, when certain properties of the simulated object are not present in
the controller, there could be potential drawbacks with regards to immersion.
If a simulated object does not have the shape, weight, or feel of the controller, it could
possibly hinder how eﬀectively or realistically the user can utilize it. For example, if a
simulation involves using a sword, the grip of a plastic controller does not translate well
to that of a sword’s hilt. Additionally, a sword’s center of gravity commonly lies in the
blade away from the hilt, so the feeling of swinging a heavy object is not present with
a light controller either. Lastly, the user might want to hold the sword with two hands,
but the controllers are not made for it. Essentially, when the main focus of virtual reality

Analysis
This chapter discusses the current standard of object interaction in virtual reality as well
as relevant studies on the subject. For meaningful results it is important to be able to
compare the ﬁndings of this project with that of established data. Knowledge of modern
controllers and their features also helps with concept development in terms of deﬁning
crucial physical characteristics.
2.1
Current State of the Art
On a consumer level, there are established hardware industry standards of interaction
within simulations that almost all VR programs are developed with in mind. At the mo-
ment there are three major names in VR hardware development: HTC, Oculus, and Sony
Playstation. Each of these companies has developed a consumer HMD(Head-mounted
display) and sold millions of units[3] across the globe. For the most part these headsets
are shipped along with controllers for in-game interaction. These controllers all happen
to work in very similar ways. The Vive Wand, Oculus Touch, and Playstation Move
controllers come in pairs, one for each hand, and are tracked in 3D space. Accompanying
sensors connected to a PC or console are used to track the position of both the headset
and the controllers. They can also track in real time the the rotation of the controllers
along with the help of in built gyroscopes and accelerometers. The Playstation VR’s
headset and controllers have LED (light-emitting diode) lights attached to them which
get picked up by a camera in order to track their position [4]. The Oculus and HTC Vive
use sensors that track constellations of IR (infrared) LEDs installed at various points
around the headsets and controllers.
As they are made for consumers to use within their homes, they are developed towards
use within small spaces. They can be used while sitting, standing, and moving around a
small area. Whichever stance is most practical depends on the simulation and what the
developer has in mind for the most comfortable experience.

2.2
Relevant Studies
As this project has a focus on substituting the controllers with those resembling the
simulated interactive object, it is important to know what physical properties impact
the suspension of disbelief. A university study on Substitutional Reality [5] delves into
diﬀerent levels of virtual resemblance to a real life object and the corresponding impact on
a user’s experience. They deﬁne substitutional reality as "a class of Virtual Environments
where every physical object surrounding a user is paired, with some degree of discrepancy,
to a virtual counterpart." As the report was written in 2015, it came before there was a set
hardware standard for object interaction in VR. Essentially the authors had recreated
a virtual environment in a simulation based on an oﬃce.
This included objects like
a desk and mug. They had made a custom 3D tracking method in order to create a
simulated representation of both the objects in the room and the user’s hands. One part
of their experiment involved making slight alterations to the virtual mug and having the
users hold the real mug. They would then answer questions about how well the virtual
object resembled what they were holding in regards to ﬁve diﬀerent physical properties:
material, temperature, size, shape, and weight. They would also be asked about the
ease of object manipulation and suspension of disbelief in terms of ability to actually
manipulate the virtual object. The changed properties that seem to aﬀect the ease and
suspension of disbelief the most according to their experiment were the size and shape
of the virtual object. As one might expect, having the virtual material and perceived
temperature changed might not have a large impact on the usability of an object due
to there still being some form of physical material in the same exact places as the real
object.
They had a second user study which involved hitting targets with a virtual light saber.
The participants were handed a number of objects with various physical properties to
determine what diﬀerences were more preferable, engaging, and exerting, both physically
and mentally. Between an umbrella, a ﬂashlight, and a replica light saber, the users found
the ﬂashlight to be the most engaging and preferable and least exerting overall. The most
stated reasoning from the users was that the other two objects were just too heavy and
less maneuverable.
The author’s concluded that "exact replicas are not necessary to
maintain the suspension of disbelief; a substitutive object can be chosen among those able
to provide a similar interactive experience to the one they portray; however it should not
have undesirable qualities (e.g., being too heavy or fragile) even at the cost of increasing
the mismatch with the actual object." They state that even though it might be practical
to build 1-to-1 realistic objects for handling virtual ones, it might not create a fun
experience.

Analysis
2.3
Technological Problem
As the virtual reality industry is a relatively new one, both for consumers and developers,
there is understandably a lot of undiscovered territory in its potential applications. There
are still unanswered questions about what works best, both in hardware and software
applications. For instance, where locomotion in ﬁrst and third person games is very
familiar and standardized, there is still an ongoing discussion about what works in VR
simulations and developers have designed a large number of potential solutions [6]. The
hardware solution in terms of in-game interaction might seem more standardized but the
goal of this project however is to further analyze the physical properties of standard VR
controllers and their use in terms of various virtual interactive objects. This analysis
will be constructed by comparison to custom made controllers more resembling certain
virtual interactive objects in speciﬁc properties.

Concept Development
This section describes the early development phase and overarching goal in more detail.
3.1
Focus Areas
The concept for this project started with the idea of creating a custom controller for a
VR bow and arrow simulation. The notion was that having a physical sense of tension
while pulling back a bow string could both add to the level of immersion and potentially
the level of accuracy as well. This was, however, a complicated endeavor that would take
time before achieving results and perhaps not an objective attainable with my skillset
as my competences lie mostly in software development. On top of this, the currently
available bow and arrow simulations are fun and engaging enough as they are with the
current VR controller standard.
After these realizations I decided to scale back a bit and focus more on the impact of
some of the physical properties mentioned in the Substitutional Reality [5] report. The
authors saw signiﬁcant changes to ease of use and suspension of disbelief when properties
such as size, shape, and perceived weight were altered on an interactive virtual object.
One could argue that in most virtual reality applications on the market, the controllers
do not accurately represent the virtual item the user is trying to manipulate in regards
to these properties. This would mean that according to the report, there is a potential
lack of ease of use as well as suspension of disbelief in modern VR simulations.
After their second study, they state that it is not necessary to create exact replicas of
virtual objects but that substitutions work just as well even at the "cost of increasing the
mismatch of the object". They state that this is a generalized notion for scenarios other
than their speciﬁc simulation. I believe, however, that there is more to be discovered in
this particular ﬁeld. Although fun and engagement are important factors for VR games
and perhaps the most vital in terms of market success, as far as simulations go there are
other elements to consider, one key component being immersion. Where being able to
move two lightweight controllers freely in space makes movement of most virtual objects

easy, the fact that the user is not manipulating the object in the same way as they
might, were they holding the real thing, could set the suspension of disbelief at risk.
In certain simulations, one might be able to create controllers that more or completely
resemble their virtual counterpart without necessarily sacriﬁcing the engagement quality
of it so much as altering the method of use. To test the eﬀects of altering the shape,
size, and perceived weight, the simulations in this project will involve object positioning
and movement.
Another possible way of making the user alter their object utility methods is to somehow
make the virtual object act more like it would if it were being held in real life. If this
made the user interact in the simulated object in a more realistic fashion, it could help
the suspension of disbelief. In the case of moving a virtual object, if the object were
perceived to be heavy, one would expect the movement to go slowly. However, when
actually holding light controllers, the movement can essentially be done as fast as the
user can move their hands. If the virtual object were to only move at a certain speed
within the simulation based on how it would move in real life, then perhaps the user
would interact with it in a more realistic fashion.
3.2
Hypothesis
The hypothesis of this study is that when altering the physical resemblance of a real
controller towards that of a simulated object, the user should behave more as if they
were using the object than when they are just holding the current VR standard for
controllers. This should in turn allow for a greater sense of immersion and suspense
of disbelief.
Additionally, simulating realistic object behaviour even when just using
controllers should also alter the user’s method of use to more ﬁt the regular utility of the
object.

makeshift tube that could adjust easily adjust in diameter out of an empty Pringles®

easily roll into a thinner tube. I wrapped it around the controller’s grip and found that
it sat snug in a diameter of about 40mm while still having a good grip. Holding it with
two hands with some wiggle room required a length of about 25cm.

5.3
Simulation
Many VR simulations involve use of the buttons and triggers to do things like grabbing
objects and shooting projectiles. Because this prototype simply allows for movement
without button interaction, the associated simulation would have to reﬂect that. Addi-
tionally, rather than just having the user interact with an object, they should have some
form of recognizable task or objective within the simulation to keep them engaged. One
game which involves simply moving the controllers is Fruit Ninja VR [11]. Based on
a hugely popular mobile game, the VR installment tasks the user with slicing through
fruit that appears in the air in front of them for points. In the simulation the controllers
are replaced with two samurai swords, with the position and rotation of the sword’s hilt
matching that of the controller’s grip. These simple mechanics allow for the user to swing
the controller in diﬀerent directions with various levels of force. These features stand as
an inﬂuence towards the gameplay of the ﬁrst simulation.
5.3.1
Game Description
The task of the simulation is straightforward, there are a certain amount of enemy
characters that spawn in front of you one after the other and you have to destroy them
before they defeat you. You have a set amount of health points that depletes by one
every time an enemy touches you. If your health reaches zero, you lose and the game
stops. If all enemies are defeated, you win. You are provided with a sword weapon which
can be used to bash enemies away or even into other enemies, destroying them.

5.4.4
Enemies
As the game’s enemies would have to appear one after the other in certain positions,
there needed to be an object that would serve as generator and manager. This object
has a child object in the center of the arena, Center, which has a child object located
near the back wall (the one under the text display), SpawnPoint. The center’s rotation
randomly alternates between 90 degree segments for the left, back, and right wall with
every enemy spawn. When an enemy object is instantiated, it’s positioned to where the
spawn point is, and then translated a random value along it’s x-axis between -3 and 3
metres. The process waits 2 seconds before running again for the next enemy and keeps
doing so until the maximum number of enemies have spawned or the game stops.
An enemy consists of nothing more than a tall red cube, a script, a rigidbody1, and a
box collider. When it appears, it immediately begins to rotate and translate towards the
player’s position until it is hit or touches the player. It consistently checks whether it
is dead(been hit and not moving) or just been hit, either by the player or another hit
enemy. When it’s dead it cannot collide with anything and countdown begins. When
reaching zero, the enemy object is destroyed and the PlayerStats updates accordingly.
5.4.5
Hitting Enemies
Using OVR Utulities, the sword object updates its position every frame depending on
where the plugin says it is. There is notably no incorporation of Unity’s physics engine
in regards to this movement. This means that simply adding colliders and rigidbodies to
the blade and enemies will not achieve desired results when the user tries bashing them.
Instead, the way the enemies reacted to getting hit was manually scripted. The actual
blade component of the sword has a trigger cylinder collider the same size as the blade
and a script. Every frame, the script keeps track of its position in the previous frame.
This allows it to generate a vector between its previous position and its current one.
This vector is what is used to determine the direction and speed of the blade. When an
enemy’s collider is intersecting with the blade’s, it fetches the values of the vector and
adds a force to its own rigidbody with its values. This ensures that the enemy moves in
the same direction as the blade with varying levels of force depending on the magnitude
of the blade’s movement. The OVR plugin’s controller vibration functions were also
applied here to create haptic feedback while the sword is colliding with an enemy.

5.5
Testing
As this was a very early prototype with the goal of simply bringing up a general dis-
cussion and moving forward, there was no formal testing scenario established. Instead
this simulation was treated more like a demo that anyone could try and describe their
thoughts on. During this project, I had a meeting every week with my supervisors and
a few other students whom were working on similar projects. I was fortunate enough
to be able to set up my demos during these meetings and have them all try them out.
I also had a few friends try it out. For each trial, I had the user ﬁrst play the game a
couple of times with just the Touch controller, then with the custom controller, and then
again with the bare controller. This was done so the user would be able to describe their
experience going from one to the other and thereafter back again.
5.5.1
Subject Feedback & Discussion
When users went from using the controller to the custom one, they did not notice much
of a change. For the most part they held it in a similar way and moved the controller
around in very much the same fashion. It was when going from the custom controller
back to the regular one that the users noticed a diﬀerence. They stated that holding the
Touch controller all of a sudden felt very light. They said they could especially notice a
diﬀerence when moving the controller around. This is possibly the result of the feeling
like "something’s not quite right." When just using the controller at ﬁrst, there is no
immediate reference of any kind. The user puts the headset on, and the ﬁrst interaction
they have with the virtual sword is via the Touch controller, so they might subconsciously
assume that there’s no apparent mismatch between the real object and the virtual one.
When moving on to the custom controller, the users did not state that there was an
immediate sense of change. This could be due to the fact that the controller only more
resembles the virtual object, and if they did not sense an obvious mismatch from before,
then using a more realistic controller would not change that. However, when going back
to the normal control method after a few rounds with the custom one, they generally
noticed a change. They felt that something was missing in terms of grasp and weight,
and could more so feel it when playing the game. This is likely due to humans’ tendencies
to sense when something is "a little oﬀ". They were brieﬂy accustomed to the idea that
the controller shares physical properties to the virtual one and when they got taken away
it resulted in a change of perception towards just using the Touch. In terms of the game
and mechanics, the users described it as very easy but in some way oddly satisfying to
hit the enemies.

Concept
Essentially, the point of this prototype was to bring about more drastic changes to the

A target has a small trigger collider at each end and the rod does as well. The target

This chapter explains the development process of the last custom controller developed.
The tests involved with this prototype serve as the main point of discussion for this

7.1
Concept
The physical properties of this prototype were not to focus so much on observational
characteristics like size and shape as it were with the previous models. Instead, it should
adhere more to the use of it. The results of tests so far have suggested that the swinging
motion of heavy objects as opposed to light ones plays a role in the suspension of disbelief,
so this prototype should be speciﬁcally fabricated to support such movement. One such
real world object that accomplishes this is a baseball bat. With a thin two handed grip
on one end and a thick heavy barrel on the other, it is made for people to quickly build up
a large amount of momentum in an object. Daniel A. Russell discussed in an article the
physics behind baseball bat swings [12]. He found that between two bats that weighed
the exact same, the feeling of swinging them was diﬀerent. This is due to the distribution
of weight as he found that they had distinctively diﬀerent balancing points (centers of
mass). He suggested that "the rotational inertia (opposition to a change in rotational
motion) depends on the location of the pivot point about which the object rotates. It is
this moment-of-inertia of the bat, not just the balance point, that determines bat-swing
speed and the eﬀectiveness of the collision between bat and ball." Between swinging a bat
and a controller, they may have a similar pivot point but weight and center of gravity
are drastically diﬀerent, which could in theory provide conclusive results in regards to
their utility and sense of immersion in a bat swinging simulation.

7.3.3.1
Virtual Object Calibration
The rotation for the bat was calibrated exactly the same way as the rod from the second
prototype. One key diﬀerence however, was that the controller was not centered on any
plane with the object this time. This meant that when rotating the real bat in place
the virtual bat would move in a circle while rotating rather than maintain its position.
Rather than trying to re-position the virtual object in relationship to the controller
manually, I created a speciﬁc calibration script for it. Essentially, the script would store
the virtual object’s position, wait three seconds, note its current position, and then move
to the midpoint between the two, and repeat continuously. While running the script, all
I had to do was rotate the bat while keeping it upright and centered. This would ensure
every time the script’s centering function ran, the virtual bat would get closer to the
bat’s actual position. After running for a while the bat was at the correct position on
the xz-plane. For moving it along the y-axis and scaling it the Rift’s nose gap was used
once again.
As for how the bat is positioned when just using a controller, it was placed such that
the hand was at the bottom of the grip and the orientation roughly matched that of the
Touch controller’s grip.
7.3.3.2
Baseball
When the baseball is instantiated it simply is assigned a speciﬁc preset velocity that
ensures it approaches the strike zone. It has a rigidbody and a sphere collider which
starts as a trigger but after it gets hit by the bat isn’t anymore. This is so it can land
and roll on the ground. Visually it is just a white sphere.
7.3.3.3
Hitting the ball
The bat model has a child object on it located in the middle of the barrel. This is the
component that handles all the interactions between the bat and the ball. Like the blade
in the ﬁrst prototype, between each frame it keeps track of its own velocity in space since
from the previous frame to the current one. It also has a capsule collider used to register
when a ball collides with the barrel of the bat. When this happens, a script attached to
the ball fetches the velocity of the bat and uses it to set its rigidbody’s velocity.
36
==PAGE:47==

When testing this method, the ball would not register as colliding with the bat. One
theory behind this is that the position of the bat’s barrel might be in front of the ball in
one frame and behind it in the next. As the position of the controller is applied to the
bat’s model every frame, when moving very fast its collider might simply skip certain
objects in the simulation. To ﬁx this there were created a number of empty gameobjects
along the barrel of the bat. The bat’s script would take note of each of these gameobjects’
position, and the their positions in the next frame. It would create a linecast between
each gameobject’s previous position and its current one and thereafter determine if any
of the casts intersect with the ball object. If at least one of them does, then the ball
would register as being hit and its velocity would change via the same method previously
mentioned.
The speed of bat is determined by taking the magnitude of the vector between its previous
and current position and dividing by Time.deltaTime4. This gives a value corresponding
to the bat’s speed in metre’s per second.

7.4.3.2
Simulated Lag
To implement this feature the bat model object became separated as a child object of
the right hand. Instead the right hand object just has locator for where the barrel would
be in a normal simulation. The bat model is the child object of a diﬀerent object which
serves as a lagging RightHand object, just placed in the barrel’s center. This object has
a script which handles where the model should be. This script contains two initially
empty lists: one for positions and another for angles. Every frame the lists add the
current position and angle of where the barrel would be if the simulation were one-to-
one. It then checks if moving its own current position to the ﬁrst position in the list with
the time since the last frame is possible given the maximum speed. That is to say it
multiplies the time since the last frame with the maximum speed and then checks if the
distance between the to positions is a lesser value. If it is then the model is moved to that
position and oriented with the ﬁrst angle in the angle list. It then iterates through the
position list and re-positions until there is a position it cannot reach with the speed limit.
This ensures that if the model is catching up while the player is holding the controller
still, then when it reaches the desired point it does not re-position itself for as many
frames as the controller as still.
At the start of a frame if the current position of the model cannot reach the ﬁrst position
in the list with the speed limit then it moves towards that position as fast as it is allowed
to. The amount it moves out of the total distance to reach the next point in the list is
saved as a percentage and the amount the model rotates towards the next rotation in
the list is based oﬀthe percentage.

Most participants claimed using the bat felt more immersive. A couple said it was because
it felt like an actual bat and another stated the weight had an impact. Three users stated
that in this particular simulation even with the bat they were not completely immersed
due to the lack of force feedback when hitting enemies. One said the the experience
would be much more intense if there was a greater feeling of hitting something with
apparent mass.
Is one more fun than the other?
Three users said the bat was more fun and three said the controller was more fun. One
user said that the bat was more engaging as they had to consider their swings more.

Another said that the bat was more fun in their experience but that it could just be

heavier than the one-to-one simulations.

Overall Discussion
Going back the hypotheses mentioned in the Concept Development chapter, it would
seem that in speciﬁc cases, user behaviour does in fact change when certain physical
properties of the controller are altered. From the results of the second prototype it’s
apparent that achieving a diﬀerence in interactivity also depends on the simulation.
Where static and controlled movements are not so much impacted by controller variance
in shape, size, and perceived weight, movements which require certain levels of force and
unique maneuverability are.
The Substitutional Reality article stated that going for exact replicas of virtual objects
rather than substitutions can make games less fun as they require more physical exertion.
Some of the subjects of the last simulation stated however that they enjoyed the one-to-
one replica the most, even at the cost of having to put more eﬀort into using it. It was
very noticeable that some users were making moves with the controller that just would
not be possible with the bat, so their mindset in how to approach the enemies in the
game changed along with going from the controller to the bat.
Not many games on the market (if any) have an implemented simulated lag feature to
encourage more realistic movements of a virtual object. It could be due to the idea that
it takes a certain degree of freedom away from the player, and some of the participants
in the last simulation would believe so as well. Some players did, however, seem to have
a much greater sense of actually wielding the object as they perceived it to be heavier
than the controller actually is just by moving it. It brings about the question of whether
or not having a such threshold in a consumer game could actually make it feel more
immersive without sacriﬁcing the player’s level of engagement.
8.1
Future Work
The results of these tests open up a whole range of possibilities in regards to what real
life objects can be used or replicated for use in VR simulations. People have invented

a vast number of tools throughout the centuries that can be applied to the concept of
this study. Where this study focuses on the movement of heavy objects, there are other
ways people manipulate diﬀerent tools every day that cannot necessarily be replicated
with the controllers. There are tools that involve physical properties and manipulation
methods like tension, recoil, compression, expansion, etc. Although these concepts can
be simulated visually, the user is not getting the natural sense of physical feedback they
would otherwise attain with the real object.
In terms of the prototypes made there are more simulations and tests that could be done.
For instance, the simulated lag test simply limited the speed that the virtual bat could
travel. One alteration could be that instead of limiting speed, the program would limit
the acceleration of the virtual bat. This should in theory achieve movements that more
resemble that of an actual swing. If the acceleration is limited, then the time it takes to
make a good hard swing would take longer. The speed tests showed that the baseball bat
swing does in fact take longer than using the controller, so it could potentially work.

Conclusion
In the beginning of this project there was not a clear plan as to what would be tested in
the end. The natural process of making discoveries and then taking it further throughout
proved to be a legitimate and exciting endeavour. The project only became more intrigu-
ing throughout due to the process of narrowing in on a physical characteristic that made
a diﬀerence. The results of the ﬁrst prototype might not have said much but it paved the
way towards the rest of the project and helped achieve the ﬁnal results. As a programmer
who’s primarily developed games in the past this has been a unique opportunity to delve
into the unknown and learn about new methods of digital interaction.
The Virtual Reality industry is a growing one and it is growing fast. With a seemingly
unlimited number of applications, many yet to be discovered, it is important to take
the small steps necessary to uncover the technology’s potential. I am conﬁdent that the
ﬁndings of this report can contribute to one of these steps.
