Abstract
This thesis was carried out in collaboration with the ReHyb project ­ a project focused
on post­stroke rehabilitation using assistive technologies such as an exoskeleton and VR
headsets. In this thesis, we investigate the possibilities of aggregating data from several
streams and using them towards predicting the states of post­stroke patients. Through
analysis of post­stroke complications, three physical states are selected. For each state,
atleast one experiment is designed to gather data and evaluate the potential of predict­
ing the respective states. This resulted in two pain detection experiments, a physical
progress experiment and a spasticity assessment experiment. The thesis finds that pa­
tient states can be predicted using AI­technology, supported by the literature. This state­
ment is backed by a 75% accuracy for three class labels and 95% accuracy for two classes
in the detection of exercise pain. 89% accuracy was reached in classifying imitated spas­
ticity levels. Additionally, the thesis finds that tendencies can be seen in data gathered
on physical progress, but requires additional data and more controlled environments in
further studies. It concludes that with the sensor data available to the ReHyb project ­
fsEMG, sEMG, elbow angle and exoskeleton current ­ it is feasible to quantify and predict
patient states in the project.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Each year 12.2 million people in the world are hit by a stroke and projections estimate
that one in four over the age of 25 will experience a stroke during their life. Additionally,
approximately 6.5 million people die from stroke every year on a global scale [1]. Out
of the survivors, many have serious long­term disabilities. In the USA alone, stroke is
the leading cause of serious long­term disabilities and stroke has cost nearly 53 billion
dollars between 2017 and 2018 in direct and indirect costs. The effects of stroke lead
to reduced mobility in more than half of patients aged above 65 [2]. Stroke survivors
with impaired movement are often able to see improvements in mobility by undergoing
physical rehabilitation following the stroke [3].
Digital twins are an emerging technology driven by data and modelling. They can be used
for monitoring, predicting, simulation, etc., and digitally represent the physical twin’s state
[4][5]. The digital twin concept holds potential for an adaptation to humans, known as
Human Digital Twins (HDTs). A concept that could be of benefit in the individualisation
of therapy. It is based on data­driven virtual representations of the human body. The EU
Horizon 2020 ReHyb project aims to develop a novel stroke rehabilitation system based
on cutting­edge technologies such as exoskeletons, virtual/augmented reality, and patient
HDTs.
With the advancement of technology and medical sensors increasing in availability, it has
been suggested to use sensor data to quantify the severity of the stroke effects and the
recovery level for the stroke survivors [6]. Currently, the methods for assessing stroke
patients are often time­consuming and require the presence of medical staff. An example
of this is the widely used Modified Ashworth Scale. If gathering and processing sensor
data could replace some of the manual assessment, it would save both time and money
while individualising the rehabilitation.
This thesis has its offset in the ReHyb project [7]. The exoskeletons used in the data
collection for this thesis have been developed by previous and concurrent projects at
ReHyb DTU. Parts of the approach of this thesis have been inspired by another thesis
done immediately before, which examined fatigue detection in robotic rehabilitation. This
also made use of Surface Electromyography (sEMG) sensors and a repetitive movement
experiment.
The HDT is intended to hold key information regarding the patient. With that as a starting
point, four physical states are examined in this project. These are all related to prevalent
consequences of stroke. The examined states are range of motion, pain, spasticity and
strength rehabilitation progress.

2
Theory
This project aims at predicting the human’s condition based on selected physical state
variables, using biomarkers captured by a variety of sensors. This section provides an
overview of the technology and theory used in the further course of this thesis.
2.1
Physiological state variables
In the scope of this thesis, the human’s physical condition is represented through four state
variables, namely Range of Motion, Strength, Spasticity, and Pain (elicited by movement).
2.1.1
Range of Motion
Range of Motion (ROM) is a measure of how far a body part can be moved or stretched.
There are different ROM measures; the most prevalent are Active Range of Motion (AROM)
and Passive Range of Motion (PROM). These are respectively the space of range one
can move with only the assistance of their own muscles (active) and the space one can be
moved, by e.g. a physical therapist (passive). A third type exists; Assisted Active Range
of Motion (AAROM). This is a middle category, in which active movements of the subject
are assisted by external support, as often seen in physical therapy, where flexibility and
strength are built up [8].
The range of motion is not the same for all humans. The joint limitation is the inability
to move in its supposed full space, and can be brought on by external factors [9]. The
difference in ROM comes from a difference in joint flexibility and/or how far the muscles
are able to stretch [8]. Furthermore, it varies with age and sex, albeit to a lesser extent
[10].
While ROM is different from person to person, there are established reference values for
what is considered ”natural” ROM [10]. In this project, the elbow is of particular interest,
as it is the joint supported by the exoskeleton used in this thesis. The focus is on the
angles of flexion and extension of the elbow. Table 2.1 provides information on what has
been found to be the minimum required ROM, to complete a series of Activities of Daily
Living (ADLs), along with the max ROM [11].

Range of Motion in Stroke Patients
Range of motion impairment and damage to the motor cortex is often seen as a result of a
stroke [12]. Hemiparesis, paralysis, weakness and abnormal muscle tone (spasticity) are
some of the most common problems related to an injured motor cortex as with strokes [13].
However, it is possible to restructure the functional circuits of the motor cortex, through
rehabilitation during the chronic phase following ischemic stroke [14]. Studies indicate
that range of motion exercises, such as the ones seen in figure 2.1, generally improve
motor function and result in changes in the motor cortex after stroke [15][16]. It has also
been hypothesised that active and passive ROM exercises can lead to the development

2.1.2
Strength
Muscle weakness is associated with diminished ROM [21] [22]. As mentioned in section
2.1.1, weakness is one of the most commonly seen consequences of an injured motor
cortex ­ which is a typical result of a stroke. While strength training primarily addresses the
condition of muscle weakness, research also suggests that it in various ways can increase
ROM [23]. One of the main examples is strength training focused on concentric and
eccentric movements has been shown to increase fascicle length [24]. Strength training
elicits both functional and structural adaptations within the body and brain, leading to an
increase in muscle strength. However, there can be large differences in how well a person
responds to resistance training [25].
To increase the maximum force possible to be exerted in a given movement (strength),
the primary stimulus is the repetition of movements provided by skeletal muscle activity,
requiring force above what would be seen in ADLs [26]. This is a well­established concept
and is widely applied in relation to strength training in general. An increase in strength is
reliant on overload during training. The progression is proportional to the relative force
developed in the muscles and the number of repetitions [26]. This assumes proper rest
between sessions, to not enter a state of overexertion. Generally, resistance training
using eccentric and concentric muscle activation is perceived as more effective in building
strength, compared to isometric actions [27] [28].
Strength Training in Stroke Patients
In stroke patients, a reduction of muscle strength can be caused by the lesions to the up­
per motor neuron often combined with a mixture of denervation, inactivity and sometimes
the effects of age. It is a topic of discussion and research whether traditional strength
training can affect spasticity. However, more recent evidence has been provided, sug­
gesting that strength directly relates to functional performance. Functional performance
can benefit from strength training while avoiding increased abnormal muscle activity [29].
According to a recent study from 2020, current data indicates that resistance training can
be beneficial in the rehabilitation of stroke patients [30]. Even though frequent training is
known to be an important part of learning motor skills [31] and increasing muscle strength,
stroke patients generally seem to only spend shorter periods of time engaging in physical
activities [32] [33] ­ perhaps due to constraints regarding staff schedules and/or motivation

Spasticity
Spasticity refers to a condition in which an abnormal, velocity­dependent amount of mus­
cle stiffness or tone is present [38] [39]. Spasticity is defined as ”disordered sensory­motor
control, resulting from an upper motor neuron lesion, presenting as intermittent or sus­
tained involuntary activation of muscles” [40]. This means, that movement of joints with
surrounding spastic muscles becomes more difficult than it otherwise would have been
(see figure 2.3). By this definition, spasticity can be said to occur when muscles involun­
tarily contract. That also indicates, as heavily supported by countless real­life examples,
that it can have a disabling effect, by impairing movement and speech while also often
being a source of discomfort and pain [41]. The severity of the condition ranges from
slight muscle stiffness to complete rigidity and uncontrollable and painful muscle spasms.
Spasticity is associated with a number of disorders, such as multiple sclerosis, spinal cord
injury, stroke and brain injury. Usually, it is caused by damage to the pyramidal (conscious
muscle control) and extra­pyramidal (unconscious, reflexive control) pathways, which are
used by the brain in sending motor signals, along the spinal cord, to the muscles [42].

location and extent of lesions result in different symptoms and severity of spasticity. In
stroke patients, the extent and onset of spasticity can vary a lot [44]. Spasticity generally
manifests by making stretching of muscles more difficult. This typically happens as mus­
cles are unable to support a full range of motion. This can lead to surrounding tendons
and soft tissue becoming tight. If spasticity is left untreated, it can in the worst case lead
to joints getting stuck in abnormal and sometimes painful positions. This condition of the
joint is what is referred to as a ”contracture” [45]. Examples of upper limb contractures

Pain can be considered either nociceptive or neuropathic. Nociception is the process of

Post­stroke Pain
Post­stroke pain (PSP) is common and a clinical problem which is often overlooked [58]. A
common source of PSP comes from spasticity and contractures. Around 70% of patients
with spasticity also suffer from central PSP. Spasticity can create sudden, painful spasms

difficulty in diagnosis of PSP, is that no medical scale currently exists, dedicated to this
specifically ­ perhaps due to the heterogeneity in consequences from strokes [67].

delirium, it becomes necessary to use other means of assessing pain [70]. Examples

ventilation compliance (tolerance of ventilation tube) [72].

2.3.1
Supervised vs. Unsupervised Learning
Machine learning is often divided into four subcategories which represent four approaches
and types of data ­ supervised learning, semi­supervised learning, unsupervised learning
and reinforcement learning. The two main fields are supervised and unsupervised learn­
ing [105].
Supervised Learning
Supervised learning relies on having a data set with labelled data to train on, meaning
each data point must have a label describing the type or category it belongs to, in addition
to the rest of the information. Using the labelled data, the machine learning algorithm
attempts to create a model, which can take future unlabelled data and deduce the missing
label from the unknown data [105]. E.g. the input could be a set of data regarding the
weather currently and after training the model on the data, the algorithm could then make
a prediction of the weather within the next hour or the estimated chance of rain.
Unsupervised Learning
Unsupervised learning is opposed to supervised learning and does not use a labelled
data set to train on. Instead, it attempts to create a model by finding patterns in the
data and using those to make an estimation and output. Learning techniques include
clustering, dimensionality reduction and other less frequent techniques or combinations
of these [105]. E.g., giving the algorithm a data set of customers and their shopping history
could be used by the machine to find segments of the customers and use each segment’s
pattern to tailor a store’s shopping experience to the customers in hope that they would
increase their spending in the store.
2.3.2
Machine Learning Techniques
This section covers the concepts of the specific machine learning techniques used in the
project.
Regression is a technique that seeks to uncover the relationship between independent
variables (features) and a dependent variable (outcome). Once a relationship is estab­
lished, outcomes can be predicted. Regression plays a key role in forecasting models for
machine learning.
Regression is one of the main applications of supervised learning, alongside classification.
It is used for predicting continuous values, as opposed to discrete values in classification.
Both solve predictive modelling problems and both rely on labelled data as training data.
Models that forecast or predict trends in data are trained using regression techniques
[106].
One example of regression is Logistic Regression. This is a type of statistical model,
which can also be used for classification and predictive analysis. It works by estimating
the probability of certain events occurring.
In contrast to predicting continuous values as done in regression problems, classification
refers to a problem where a model is used to predict a class label for a given data input.

Ensemble Learning
Ensemble methods have been developed to improve predictive performance. It works by
constructing a set of various models to tackle a shared issue [109]. This type of method
can often perform better than any single classifier and produces better results than the
individual classifiers in the ensemble. To decide on a class for non­labelled data, ensem­
ble methods make use of voting procedures to create output [110]. A simple example of
a voting scheme is the majority vote. In this voting scheme, the individual classifiers all
predict the class label. After which the class that gets the most votes is picked as the
output of the ensemble [111].
Ensemble methods are a specific collection of learning algorithms that employ methods
such as (most prominently) Bagging and Boosting. The main difference between the two
is the way they learn. In Bagging, weak learners are trained in parallel while in Boosting
it is sequential. When a series of models are constructed in Boosting, the weights of in­
correctly classified data, in the previous model, are increased. By tweaking the weights
this way, the algorithm identifies the parameters on which it has to focus to improve per­
formance. In Bagging, the models are equally weighted [112].
Bagging models are typically used on weak learners with high variance and low bias while
Boosting methods are utilised on data sets with low variance and high bias [113].
One example, which falls under the fairly large umbrella of ensemble­based learning
methods, is the Random Forest Classifier (RFC). RFC is an extension of bagging. The
foundation of the RFC is the construction of several simple decision trees in training and
the execution of a majority vote for classification. One of the advantages of this strategy
is that it can correct for decision trees overfitting on the training data [114].

Neural Networks
Artificial Neural Networks, or just Neural Network (NN), are a subset of machine learning
and are the core of what is referred to as deep learning. The name and structure of NN are
inspired by that of the human brain [116]. Deep learning differs from usual machine learn­
ing as it typically eliminates some of the pre­processing needs. This type of algorithm can
process raw unstructured data, like text and images, and automates feature extraction.
In contrast to traditional ML methods, deep learning can determine the most important
features within the input data automatically, allowing it to solve even more complex tasks
[117].
Neural networks consist of an input layer, an output layer, and at least one hidden layer.
The first layer received the input data and values from each node in the layer are passed
on to the ones in the next layer. Eventually, a result is produced by the output layer. Nodes
are connected with weighted edges which each has a threshold. The weights are adjusted
during the training of the network, based on the importance of the variable it maps. If the
weighted value a node receives is above the threshold, the data is propagated [118].
A neural network where nodes always pass data forward to the next layer in the network
is regarded as a feedforward network. A visualisation of a feedforward network can be
seen in figure 2.16.
Neural networks require training data to learn and thereby improve their accuracy over
time. They can be very powerful tools, allowing for classification, clustering and regression
at a very high pace. The biggest time sink for NNs is in the training phase [116].

The time series analysis used in this thesis is a combination of the Ridge Classifier and
Random Convolution Kernel Transformation (ROCKET). Additionally, the Neural Network
also relies on time series analysis and forecasting, which is covered in section 2.3.2.
The MINIROCKET ­ Minimally Random Convolution Kernel Transform ­ is a neural net­
work consisting of only a single layer and no hidden layers. In each node, it uses a
convolution kernel to transform the data into a new format. The MINIROCKET extends
on the original ROCKET which had randomised length, dilation and padding on each ker­
nel. The MINIROCKET has reduced the randomness to the bias values produced in the
algorithm. A kernel is a matrix of numbers which can be in an arbitrary number of di­
mensions. Figure 2.17 displays a 3x3 kernel used on a 7x7 data matrix as an example
of this. It can be used to enhance or extract certain features of the data, such as edges
in an image to enhance the contrast or in our case enhance features in a time series for
machine learning classification. ROCKET has several advantages over other time series
analysis methods. This includes very fast training time, even on large data sets, while not
compromising the classification accuracy [120].
As mentioned above, the MINIROCKET is combined with a Ridge Classifier, for the clas­
sification of each time series. The Ridge Classifier uses Ridge Regression to evaluate
which label the data belongs to. Ridge Regression is a regression model that uses an ”l2”
penalty on the regression slope.

LSTM
The Long Short­Term Memory Network (LSTM) is an adaptation of RNN, which is far
more computationally effective. The main difference between LSTM and RNN is the way
in which they remember or retain memory. LSTM is able to handle information for a longer
period of time. The loop in RNNs means that they retain only sequential previous values,
meaning that long­term memory becomes increasingly problematic2. LSTM uses a set of
gates to control information within memory. The gates control when something should be
remembered, for how long, the amount of information, the threshold and when memory
should be forgotten [123].
The darts implementation of RNN can be used for making multi­step, multivariate pre­
dictions. This type of network is able to predict time series, by regression. In practice,
the training of an LSTM network results in a multivariate, multi­step prediction algorithms,

Outlier Detection
When gathering a data set from somewhere, it can have errors in the data or occurrences
which were affected by outside elements that a data scientist does not want to take into
account. These are called outliers or anomalies and are typically poor for machine learn­
ing algorithms. Outliers can skew the generated models, hinder a good model from being
generated or slow down the generation of a model. As a result, they are often removed
during the data processing section of a machine learning project, before any algorithm
is attempted to fit and explain the trends in the data set. E.g. in a data set of heights
containing 3 people with 190 cm, 180 cm and 370 cm respectively, the model would try to
encompass the 370 cm data point. However, from understanding the datatype one would
realise that a person with 370 cm height is a mistake originating from somewhere during
the data gathering process and remove it from the data set. This allows any future ma­
chine learning algorithm to solely capture the appropriate data instead of trying to explain
anomalies and the model will subsequently better fit a model of people’s heights [125].
Feature Selection
Modern technology develops quickly, and various new computer and internet applications
generate an unprecedentedly large amount of data ­ be it video, photos, text etc. These
data often have characteristics of high dimensionality. As dimensionality increases, so
does the volume of space required. For reliable results, the amount of data required often
grows exponentially [126]. The learning accuracy and training speed deteriorate with
redundant features and can lead to problems of overfitting [127]. For cases like these,
Feature Selection (FS) can be used to make the data sets more manageable. However,
even with lower dimensional data, it can be useful in bringing down computational cost
and removing noisy data [128].
Features in a data set are input variables fed to a machine­learning model. A column in a
data set represents a feature. In order to get the optimal result in training a model, there
might be features in a data set that should not be included. In general, too many features
can produce unimportant and noisy learning patterns, which should be avoided. FS is a
process that selects a subset of features to include in a learning algorithm [129].
Usually, FS consists of 1: A search engine that determines the most promising candidates
for the feature subset and 2: some criteria that decide the best candidate.
In theory, increasing the number of features is supposed to provide more discriminating
power. In reality, it comes at a higher computing cost, and often contains a large amount
of irrelevant information. The main benefits from feature selection are the following [127]:
• Lowering storage requirements
• Reducing training and utilisation time (computing cost)
• Facilitating better data understanding and visualisation
In many real­life data sets, there are features which might not contribute to the model.
E.g., in a model for deciding which cars should be crushed for spare parts, we could have
4 features: Model, year, kilometres and owner. In this situation, knowing the owner most
likely would not help in determining which cars to crush, and should therefore be excluded,
to optimise the model.
There are several techniques to choose from when conducting FS. When choosing a
technique, it is important to know that feature selection also can be divided into two types;
supervised and unsupervised. Depending on the machine learning model, the specific
FS techniques are different. Supervised FS models use the correlation between a feature
and class label as the most fundamental element. The supervised model searches for

the feature subset with the highest classification accuracy. These are used for labelled

criterion. The goal is to find the feature subset which naturally classifies the data. These
are used for unlabelled data [128].
Cross Validation
When using machine learning algorithms to develop models, they are often trained on a
data set, but if the data set isn’t extremely large or sufficient in size, the algorithm can find
a piece of information and think it is a general trait for the data. For instance, in a data set
of 3 animals’ appearance ­ a cat, a dog and a snake ­ where, by accident, the dog had
only 3 legs. The model might incorrectly think that all dogs have 3 legs.
To detect and eventually overcome this issue, a method called cross­validation is often
used. In cross­validation the data set is split into a number of folds; the test data set
contains 1 fold and the training data set consists of the remaining folds. An example
could be that the data is split into 5 folds and as such training data set is 80% of the total
data set and the test data set is 20% (see figure 2.21).
Once divided, the machine learning algorithm learns its parameters only based on data
contained in the training data set. Subsequently, the resulting model is tested on the test
data and a score is calculated to evaluate how well the model performed. This is done for
each fold, as seen in figure 2.21 [130].

data set and loses its ability to generalise [131]. Underfitting is if the model can’t capture
the relationship between the input data and the output data [132].

3.1
Objective Pain Assessment
The sensation of pain has shaped human evolution by letting us identify and avoid haz­
ardous situations. Even so, alleviating pain is at the heart of the field of medicine.
The current gold standard in pain assessment relies on self­reporting. This requires the in­
dividual to be able to process and communicate their subjective experience of pain [134].
There are situations where self­reporting is unfeasible, unreliable or too slow. In these sit­
uations, automatic and objective pain detection is needed to ensure accurate treatment.
A study by A. P. Hansen et al. reports newly developed pain in almost half of 275 patients,
in the 6 months following a stroke [135].
Despite the absence of direct objective biomarkers that indicate pain [136], several as­
sessment tools exist that derive pain from secondary markers. E.g. a study by R. Cowen
et al.[137], suggests combining information about the pain­related variability of heart rate,
blood pressure, skin sweating and pupillary size to predict the severity of pain. The re­
sults show that multi­variate approaches perform far better ­ compared to single marker
approaches ­ at predicting pain severity and intra­operative nociception. However, the
research has been limited to uniform patient populations, with only a few surgical proce­
dures and a single type of anaesthesia. Furthermore, it does not support patients with
any complications in these biomarkers.
In contrast, a 2013 study [138] describes how information about a patient’s pain state can
be derived from MRI and near­infrared spectroscopy. Despite promising results, these
methods are highly intrusive and thus not suitable for at­home rehabilitation. The re­
search was conducted on healthy patients and the authors remark that patients with any
brain injuries ­ such as stroke ­ are outside the research’s scope and difficult to translate
the research toward.
Finally, a study by France et. al [139] proposes detecting pain from biopotentials, mea­
sured by electromyography (EMG) sensors. By using these measurements, pain thresh­
olds for individuals can be found quite precisely, by observing the naturally occurring pro­
tective withdrawal reflex ­ known as the nociceptive flexion reflex (NFR) [140]. The results
show a good correlation between the predicted and self­reported pain [137]. However,
NFR is heavily influenced by external factors ­ just two examples are obesity and sleep
[141] [142]. The main issue for these tools is the lack of systems for commercial use that
can monitor in real­time, thus limiting their applicability in current rehabilitation systems.
Facial Pain Detection
Variations in facial expressions can provide information on the occurrence of pain. The
Facial Action Coding System (FACS), is a framework by Ekman and Friesen dating back
to 1978 [143]. FACS is commonly referenced as a founding block for systems in facial
pain detection. It is used to identify facial expressions by breaking down facial movements
into components called ”action units”. The facial expressions represent emotions, hence
certain emotions elicit certain facial muscle activation [144].
Since 2009, several studies have attempted the classification of video recordings of facial
expressions. These studies apply machine learning models for the prediction of emotional
states. This has achieved accuracies of upwards of 90% [145].
A different approach is utilising facial sEMG. A 2022 study made a real­time classifica­
tion of pain levels using EMG data [146]. Specifically, EMG data from zygomaticus and

Progress Prediction
The most prevalent research in progress prediction is seen in sports, where predictions
are calculated to understand the sport’s peak performance metrics. A majority of predic­
tion models in sports relate to an individual’s potential rather than their course of progres­
sion. Examples are predicting peak age for athletes, and performance based on nutrition
or strength based on some factors [148] [149] [150].

Though the results are very uncertain, predicting progress could bring significant poten­

Thus, there is a significant need to explore progress prediction in rehabilitation applica­

Currently, one of the main uses for the prediction of rehabilitation outcomes is used for
predicting when a patient can be discharged. There is little literature on physical rehabili­
tation for the sake of motivating the patient and/or monitoring the current progress.
The common practice for ”discharge prediction” is, and has traditionally, been something
health care professionals estimate based on prior experience and knowledge ­ a man­
ual assessment [152]. A study from 1994 [153] attempted to build a prediction model for

This study achieved a predicted variance at discharge for ”length of stay” at 20%, with the
motor function being the main predictor. However, this does not directly translate to full
recovery and the ability to perform ADLs.

4.3.2
Data Preprocessing
Though EMG is used extensively in many aspects of the world, it has several issues which
need to be addressed or overcome in one way or another. The most apparent problem
in EMG and sEMG is noise in the signal. Noise can emerge from many sources such as
inherent noise in electronics equipment, ambient noise from electromagnetic radiation,
motion artefacts from other biosignals like ECG, the inherent instability of the signal, other
electrical interference or even something as simple as poor electrode placement. For this
reason, the data has to be mended before use [93]. The other data types also suffer from
this, but to a lesser extent.
Outlier Detection
To detect outlier data points among the data points in the dataset, a method called Isolation
Forest was used to sort them out [163]. Isolation Forest uses an ensemble of binary
decision trees. The algorithm first separates the data into sub­samples of ”iTrees”. Each
iTree is then partitioned into branches by taking a random number between the minimum
and maximum value in the data ­ this creates the branching of the iTree. Once all data
point in the dataset is partitioned, the algorithm assigns each value an anomaly score
derived by the depth of the data point in the iTrees. It builds on the idea that anomalies
are far from the other data points and will be partitioned early in a tree, hence having
low depth in the tree gives a higher anomaly score. Using the contamination value ­ the
percentage of outliers in the data set ­ the algorithm then assigns each data point 1 for
normal or ­1 for outliers.

Bin Smoothing
Due to the high frequency of the data sampling from the sensors, the data sets are poten­
tially very large and with such sizes, it can reduce the processing speed and accuracy of
the selected machine learning models. The largest dataset in our repository was above
433.000 data points. To counteract this, we used bin smoothing of the data and reduced
the number of data points in doing so.
When bin smoothing, the number of data points specified is collected and the mean of
these data points is calculated. This mean becomes a single data point in the dataset,
representing the points in the bin. This is done for every interval in the dataset and at the
end, the dataset has been reduced in size by the chosen scalar.
This benefits the machine learning model as small variations in the dataset are smoothed
out and the models do not have to attempt to describe these small variations as well,

4.4
Exercise Pain
Determining levels of perceived pain in an individual is critical for stopping rehabilitation
exercises that are too painful. This is crucial in situations where the person is equipped
with an exoskeleton that could force them into painful movement. Furthermore, being
able to tell if the person is in pain during certain movements could help identify areas of
interest for diagnosis or further rehabilitation.
This experiment consists of curling a dumbbell until the muscular fatigue in the bicep be­
came a source of pain. The experiment has two overall goals. These are predicting the
pain level and forecasting future pain. Pain level predictions were initially made with 2
pain classes, to determine whether it was feasible at all to make distinctions regarding
pain based on EMG. After that, 3 classes were used to see if pain could be predicted on
a more fine grained scale. This approach resulted in models usable in real­time settings.
In contrast to this, a retrospective approach was also taken using a different type of clas­
sification framework referred to as ROCKET, which classifies entire chunks of time series
data. This was done to see if all the data being available and classified together made for
more accurate predictions.
The forecasting of pain attempts to predict states of pain before they occur, using a neural
network.
4.4.1
Exercise Pain Experiment Protocol
Limitations
The exercise pain experiment was designed around one main limitation, being that we
had no stroke patients available to conduct our experiment on. As a result, we had to
come up with an abstraction of the complications often encountered by stroke patients.
The experiment concentrated on pain felt by movement, as this was achievable within
the constraints of the experiment. Due to ethical reasons, we could not afflict pain on
participants. Thus, we required a setup where participants could inflict controlled pain on
themselves. As a result, we chose to observe the pain experienced from muscular fatigue
while performing movements exhausting the muscles. This would fulfil the requirements
while being close to a real­world scenario in which a stroke patient would feel pain during
movement.
Hypothesis
The different levels of pain an individual experiences during exercise can be deduced from
their physical state. Specifically using data gathered by monitoring the facial muscles of
the individual through fsEMG sensors, combined with movement data represented by joint
angle.

The final part of the pain experiments was attempting to predict future pain states of a
patient. The intention was to predict if the future patient state would be intense pain and
therefore stop before reaching the intense pain levels. This would be based on previous
data, on which a neural network had trained. By inputting a sequence of data for an
ongoing exercising session, a model should be able to output future points. In reality, it
was not possible to get data in real­time. A real­time setup was thus imitated instead, by
feeding controlled amounts of data to the neural network.
For conducting forecasting of the pain data, an LSTM Neural Network model was created
and trained. The LSTM network used in this project is from the python library ”darts”,
which specialises in time series forecasting and manipulation [166]. The training of this
type of model can take a significant amount of time, and the time increases proportionally
with the number of features included. The resulting model is a multivariate multi­step
forecasting model. This means it uses multiple variables to predict its outcome, and that
it in principle can forecast any given amount of future steps. However, lower steps seem
to generally result in better predictions.
A classification algorithm, such as the random forest classifier, can be used to classify
the predicted future data points within the already established pain_severity_class.
This effectively forecasts the pain level of the subject. For this classification, the best­
performing model based on the evaluation results was used (see section 5.1.1). The
features selected in the optimisation process of the classifier were the features included
in the LSTM model.
Generally, bin smoothing with bins of size 500 was a good fit for this task. The bin size
meant decent computation speed while getting high accuracy. Increasing the bin sizes
meant vastly reducing the accuracy of the predictions. Furthermore, the computing time
rose as bin sizes decreased while not benefiting the accuracy of the predictions.
To evaluate the accuracy of the future pain level predictions, the following was done:
The data for one experiment run was held out during training of the LSTM network. Based
on the first 10 data points (corresponding to five seconds of information with bin size 500),
the following 10 data points were predicted. In a moving­window fashion, the next 10
data points were predicted based on all the previous data points. There was no overlap
in the data being predicted within windows ­ the next window started where the previous
stopped. By comparing this prediction with the actual class label, accuracy was calcu­
lated. The accuracy was calculated as the sum of the number of correct predictions,
divided by the number of data points predicted.

4.5.1
Acute Pain Experiment Protocol
Limitations
This experiment intended to subject the participant to pain at an intensity high enough
that it could not be endured. Thus, it was important to find a painful stimulus that could be
used without having any real consequence on the participant, other than the discomfort
from the pain itself.
People’s reaction to sudden, intense pain can be quite different. As this experiment col­
lects fsEMG data, the pain is assumed to be expressed in a facial muscular response.
That means the experiment relies on people to have such a response to pain, which
might not be true in every case. Additionally, outside disturbances can prompt a change
in facial expression.
It could be argued that partial paralysis, as a result of stroke, could have an impact on
facial expressions. Paralysis in stroke is often hemiplegia, which means that one side
of the body still functions normally. Facial expressions are generally expressed on both
sides of the face (not necessarily completely symmetrically [167]), and therefore data from
only one side of the face should in principle be enough.
Hypothesis
It is possible to isolate the sensation of pain and the facial expressions it prompts, by
subjecting a person to painful stimuli. The subject should not be moving or receive any
other stimuli.
Whether the individual experiences no pain or an intense level of pain can be deducted
based on monitoring facial muscle activation, through fsEMG sensors.

Robustness in the Pain Experiments
To check robustness, a model was trained on all the data from the EPE and the ATPE
respectively. Then, the pain severity class was predicted for the laugh data sets by said
models. The correct way to classify this ”laugh” data would be to assign all data points
to class 0 (no pain/bearable pain). This would prove that the model understands that this
different expression should not be handled in the same way as a painful expression.
Afterwards, one of the two laugh data sets was included in the training data. Specifically,
one of the laugh­data sets was used as test data, while the other was included in the
training data also consisting of data generated from the pain experiments. This means
that the model had training data available that could enable it to recognise the laugh data
as something without pain. This was done to see if robustness would increase, based on

4.6
Spasticity
Determining where spasticity occurs and its severity is helpful in considerations regard­
ing physical rehabilitation. This experiment seeks to clarify if it is possible to differentiate
between the scores on the Modified Ashworth Scale based on sensor data collected via
the exoskeleton. We chose the MAS as it is regarded as the standard for clinical assess­
ment of extremity spasticity [49]. Furthermore, it had already been adopted by the ReHyb
project, as the scale to use for spasticity in the Human Digital Twin.
A classification model was trained on the collected data to automatically determine the
severity of spasticity in the extension of the biceps brachii.
The experiment consisted of simulating spasticity in its different manifestations, as de­
scribed in the MAS [49].
Like a clinician conducting MAS, the exoskeleton moves the user’s arm through a full
range of motion at a defined speed, while measuring the amount of torque necessary to
do so. Throughout the movement, stimuli imitating a spasticity level in the MAS will be
present. The intent is to be able to create a model, able to predict the imitated MAS score.
4.6.1
Spasticity Experiment Protocol
Spasticity Limitations
Due to technical constraints, namely the unavailability of an exoskeleton that could provide
the torque needed to move the user’s arm, the setup had to be modified to serve as
a general proof­of­concept. Therefore, instead of applying force to a human arm, we
ended up applying the resistance externally in a manual matter. This gave a large amount
of variance in the data. However, this variance is not necessarily negative. For stroke
patients, the variance in spasticity is also great. A variance that is anchored in muscular
differences and the differences in how the spasticity manifests.
The applied external force could not vary greatly, due to the lack of strength in the ex­
oskeleton. That posed an additional challenge during the experiment, as the MAS spec­
ifies multiple levels of resistance that clearly distinguish regarding the described muscle
tone; a slight increase in muscle tone, a more marked increase in muscle tone and a
considerable increase in muscle tone. Thus, given the limited range of applicable force
that the exoskeleton could handle, defining levels of force before the experiment was not
possible. Instead, corresponding levels needed to be found through trial and error.
Ideally, the experiment would be done with an exoskeleton strong enough to move an
arm of a participant passively. Furthermore, these participants should be actual stroke
patients, with varying levels of spasticity. The experiment has been tailored around this
limitation. Thus, participants had no impact on the experiment and were for that reason
deemed unnecessary.
Hypothesis
It is possible to determine levels of spasticity as described in the MAS based on data
regarding time, angle and torque recorded through the exoskeleton, as it attempts to
complete a movement with full ROM.
Experimental Setup and Procedure
The strongest exoskeleton at our disposal was the LEGO exoskeleton, which was why it
was selected for use in this experiment. This choice was based on more strength, allowing
a larger variety in resistance and therefore possibly generating the clearest results. The

The LEGO exoskeleton measures the angle with an internal encoder, within the motor
itself. The measure of torque provided by the exoskeleton was evaluated as the current
drawn by the entire setup. An increase in voltage reflects an increase in torque exerted by
the motors. The baseline current, drawn from the Raspberry Pi, was subtracted to remove
the overhead. As the current is directly proportional to the energy or power exerted by the
exoskeleton motors, it was sufficient to remain in the current domain. This relationship
can be explained as follows:
Voltage is by Ohm’s law directly proportional to the current: V = I ·R, where V is voltage,
I is current in Ampere and R is the resistance. The resistance of the circuits in the system
is assumed to be constant. As torque is proportional to the current (see torque formula in
theory section 2.2.1), the voltage is thereby proportional to torque.

4.6.4
Model Training and Evaluation
To evaluate the data, three distinct machine learning techniques were chosen as the most
suited methods. The models chosen were the best­performing models in the pain exper­
iment, best suited for the type of data collected. The ROCKET classification was used to
investigate the data in a time series manner.
The first two methods were devised with supervised techniques. In the first method, the
time series are analysed by training models on metadata from the time series. The sec­
ond method carries out a full evaluation of the time series as a whole. This is reasonable
as the spasticity assessment using the MAS is not real­time dependent. If we were to
predict spasticity in real­time, this approach would not be possible.
For the unsupervised learning techniques on time series, more advanced tools need to be
used. The selected methods were TimeSeriesKMeans, KShape and KernalKMeans from
the package TSLearn. These methods belong to a branch of unsupervised learning called
clustering. Clustering is a way of dividing and grouping data points into separate parti­
tions. Each element in the partition shares some trait while data points in other groups
are deemed dissimilar from it. It is suited for looking for tendencies and similarities in a
dataset where the user doesn’t know which traits and types are in the dataset [169].
Supervised Classification
The tools used for supervised learning on time series, are previously explained in rela­
tion to other experiments. These include using ROCKET with the RidgeClassifier, as
explained in section 2.3.2 along with some ”classic” models, which have previously been
presented in section 2.3.2.
The goal was to create a model, that predicts the imitated MAS scores with high accuracy
and F1­score.
For this experiment, a lesser amount of these ”classic” models were tested compared to
the Exercise Pain Experiment. We chose to try out a select few models, based on previ­
ous results. Those being KNN, ETC, DTC, AdaBoost, as they showed the best results in
different aspects.

4.7
Progress
Predicting progress could pose significant benefits for future rehabilitation systems [170].
For instance, it could allow providing decision support about which exercise would bring
the best potential therapeutic effect. Therefore, this section describes an experiment that
explores how specific exercises in combination with a specific training paradigm affects
the user’s progress.
In this experiment, progress was based on progress in strength, measured by an increase
in ROM. The experiment has a duration of five weeks. In these weeks, participants will
train their ability to curl a dumbbell. The training was done according to a set training
scheme, and their progress is tracked. Strength starts to increase within a few training
sessions [171], it should therefore be possible to see results within this time frame.
Progress will be assessed based on strength in regards to three elements: Weekly eval­
uation results of ROM for 1­RM+10% with a dumbbell, ROM with resistance bands, and
characteristics of 3­RM with a dumbbell. The Repetition Maximum concept is explained
in the theory section 2.1.2.
4.7.1
Progress Experiment Protocol
Limitations
This experiment required the use of EMG­sensor equipment and exoskeletons, which was
only available in one pair at the beginning of the experiment. An extra exoskeleton was
being produced in parallel to this experiment and extra EMG equipment was in shipping.
These external factors meant, that it was not possible to monitor each training session of
each individual. It was not feasible to suspend the experiment until those external factors
were resolved. Waiting would push the experiment too far towards the end of the thesis.
The solution was therefore to rely on the data collected during weekly evaluations. The
lack of accessibility of equipment meant that evaluations could realistically not be done on
the same day for all participants. The participants had to be available and the equipment
swapped around, for the evaluations to be possible.
The Progress Experiment runs over several weeks. This means that the participants have
to be committed to the experiment over a longer time period.
Hypothesis
It is possible to predict people’s progress in increased ROM with weight 10% above their
1­RM, based on their training pattern and performance during weekly evaluations.

Synchronisation of fsEMG and Exoskeleton Angle data
The initial format of the data was sets of EMG and Angle data for each evaluation. This

Synchronisation of the fsEMG data with the data from the Exoskeleton turned out to be
quite a large challenge for this experiment. The main issue was, that there was no refer­
ence across the two data sets, which could be used as a point of synchronisation. One
of the sensors has to start recording first, resulting in an offset. Even though a timestamp
was provided by both sensors, these were sometimes faulty and essentially useless. This
meant we had to carry out manual synchronisation. Fortunately, it was quite clear that
the amplitude of the EMG signals starts rising as the angle starts increasing. This meant
we could find a common starting point for each evaluation’s EMG and angle data, and
thereby synchronise the two.
The solution to the synchronisation challenge did, however, contribute to solving another
problem ­ the problem of removing excess data at the start and end of data sets.
The whole process of removing excess data and synchronising was done manually. No
effective method was found to automate this process with a satisfying result. Removal
of excess data was especially necessary as test subjects would often do some sort of
movement, such as adjusting grip, outside the movement prescribed by the experiment.
Further reasoning and examples of isolating the real data can be found in section 4.3.1.

4.7.3

Angle Features
The development in angle peak was the most obvious form of evaluating progress. The
angle peak translates directly to ROM, as the motion was started at a 0°angle. This
evaluation form was relevant for 1RM+10% and the resistance band. An increase in
ROM indicates an increase in strength and thereby positive progress.
The peak angle in itself was not a good measure of progress for 3RM, as each movement
in principle should have full ROM. Instead, the analysis was based on the consistency of
the movements. It is hypothesised that the consistency of repetitions would increase with
strength, and thereby be a measure of progress. One way to analyse consistency is by
the sum of height differences between peaks. The height of the peak is equal to the angle
of the arm. A sum of height differences being 0 shows equal ROM in each repetition.
Another way to capture consistency is by looking at the time between the peaks. The
differences between peaks are found by subtracting the time point of the peaks. A sum
of time differences being zero indicates each repetition being done at the same pace.
EMG Features
The EMG features used to evaluate the EMG signal, as discussed in 2.2.2, were the ARV,
ZCR and MDF. The ARV was calculated using the mean of the absolute values, while the
ZCR and MDF used python packages.
Using the package librosa.feature, the ZCR was found with a signal frame length of
200 ms and a hop length of 40 ms. The average of the ZCR for each signal was used as
the defining feature for that evaluation number and type.
The MDF was found using the package SciPy.fft, in which a Fast Fourier Transformation
algorithm converted the domain from the time to the frequency domain. The mean was
then taken as the defining feature.

4.7.4
Progress Prediction Using LSTM Networks
The overall goal was to predict the peak angles for 1RM+10%, based on features ex­
tracted from the data. This value was chosen as the ground truth and the most direct
measure for progression, regarding strength. This was also the value with the clearest
tendency and progression throughout the course of the experiment as a whole.
Prediction
Progress was predicted in two ways. Each required the training of a separate LSTM
network. The first consisted of holding the last data point (fifth evaluation) out during
training of the LSTM network. Then a prediction was made for this ”known” data. This
was done in order to be able to evaluate the prediction. Mean Absolute Percentage Error
(MAPE) was used as the evaluation metric.
The second way was predicting future, unknown data (a sixth evaluation not done in the
experiment), based on all of the previous evaluations. There is no straightforward way of
evaluating future predictions, as we do not have the actual values to compare. Evaluation
is thus done through visual analysis. This gave an idea regarding whether the approach
suits the problem.
Both models were made by feeding the data regarding each participant’s arm to an LSTM
neural network. At first, the approach was to make a general model, based on all the
data. However, this netted very inaccurate prediction results. The predicted values were
always close in proximity to each other, regardless of previous trajectory ­ which could
perhaps be expected from a general model trained on little data.
The second approach was to create models on an individual basis. That means 10 mod­
els were trained, as the experiment had five participants, using two arms each. These
individualised models were generally far more precise.

Discussion
6.1
Experimental Outcomes
6.1.1
Pain Experiments
In the exercise pain experiment, we tried detecting muscular pain as well as acute pain.
We built three models with varying levels of pain; two models with two classes (no pain,
pain) and one model with three classes (no pain, moderate pain and strong pain).
The results of the models were generally good as we reached high prediction accuracies,
however, they were not overly robust. This shows that predicting pain, in general, is
possible, yet it also displays that there are certain challenges. These challenges need to
be handled for the models to work in a real­life setting.
Two Class Labels
The need for real­time analysis of the data is during the rehabilitation exercises while us­
ing the exoskeleton. If the patient enters a state of intense pain or discomfort in general,
the exoskeleton should not continue to move the patient’s limbs.
To accommodate a setting where the importance lies in the accuracy of distinguishing
when the patient was in high pain, we created a separate model with two labels. This
model reached a very high accuracy of 95%, as the model was simplified and only had
to create one threshold point. The drawback of such a model is the loss of information
before the intense pain. Any system wanting to stop before reaching intense pain would
not have information about when that would be. Using this with a supportive exoskeleton
in rehabilitation exercises is less ideal, as the patient never wants to be close to intense
pain. Furthermore, the exoskeleton would lose information about when to give more sup­
port or less support, while at some point suddenly having to completely stop.
As a result, the two­label model gave us a more robust prediction but has limited informa­
tive value.
Three Class Labels
The results of training a model on this data were heavily impacted by the subjectiveness
of pain not being reflected in the data. This is probably due to a combination of the pain
being so small, that the subject can essentially ignore it, and because it occurs gradually.
These factors influence whether a facial reaction is prompted or not. As seen in figure
6.1, there are examples which have no clear development in the EMG signal, going from
class 0 to 1. This makes it very difficult to distinguish between the no pain and the mod­
erate/bearable pain, and due to the subjectiveness of when the participant felt pain, it is
also hard to predict when that individual pain level is hit.
Without the angle data, (only fsEMG data) the prediction accuracy was ∼50% for the
three class label problem. However, with the angle data added, this rose to ∼75%. This
indicates the angle data is useful in predicting the ”middle” values, where this shift from 0
to 1 happens. The Elbow (angle) Velocity Tendency was a very prominent feature in gen­
eral. In cases where the EMG signal did not change much, even though a shift from 0­1
happened (see figure 6.1), the angle data automatically becomes important for predicting
the class. The speed with which the movements are done is heavily influenced by muscle
fatigue. The importance of angle features indicates that the prediction accuracy is reliant
on muscle fatigue as a symptom of pain.
The selected approach was to categorise the pain data into two or three classes. An
alternative approach could be to model the pain through regression, with pain on a con­
tinuous scale. A good regression model would provide results on a very fine­grained
Sensor Data Fusion and Intelligent State Prediction for Human Digital Twins in Stroke Therapy with
Exoskeletons
79
==PAGE:90==
Figure 6.1: The plot displays the difficulty of differentiating between labels 0 and 1 by
fsEMG data alone.
scale. However, due to the nature of the problem and pain being largely subjective, such
a fine­grained scale would likely not generalise well. In the setting of this project, the extra
informative value gained from regression would not compensate for the resulting larger
inaccuracies in the model. We opted for a classification approach as the goal was to iden­
tify specific states of pain. A continuous pain scale would require thresh holds to indicate
when certain states of pain are reached.
The EPE data has imbalanced classes. Classification models have techniques to handle
this issue, such as using different evaluation metrics or sampling. These techniques are
not directly available in regression.
Bin Size Implications
In addition to the labelling, the bin size also significantly influences the model accuracy.
Reducing the dataset by having larger bin sizes generally showed higher accuracy. But
reducing the data set risks lowering the granularity of the models.
When smoothing the data, the very large bin size means averaging over a large number
of points. This reduces the size of the data set, while still keeping the overall tendencies.
The problem is that for both two and three class labels, the class representing intense pain
does not have a lot of data points. It has 15000 points in the raw data, which corresponds
to 15 seconds. The reduction from a bin size of 1500 leads to just ten points. That means
one point covers an entire 1.5 seconds. With only two class labels, that means a vast
majority of the data is made up of data points belonging to class 0. A model that simply
assigned everything to class 0 would have quite a high accuracy. This is because the last
15000 points make up only around ∼5­10% of the entire data set, depending on the size.
Fortunately, this is not the case. In almost all situations, the majority of points belonging
to class 1 are correctly predicted. Many of the mistakes lie in misclassifying points with
class 0 as 1. Keeping the goal in mind, this is a good tendency ­ stopping the exoskeleton
to force movements more often than not is better.
Either way, the results suggest that a bin­size of 500 is a better choice with two labels.
It results in 30 points where each point corresponds to 0.5 seconds. The predictions are
thus more fine­grained. Less than one percentage point in accuracy is sacrificed, using a
bin size of 500 instead of 1500.

The results, especially when observing pain, needs to be seen critically. This is partic­
ularly true due to the pain being observed stemming from muscle fatigue. The results

An argument can be made, that the lack of robustness towards expressions similar to pain

classification of data, it is not important if pain is the cause of not being able to continue
the movement. The important thing is to detect that a certain level of discomfort, fatigue
or pain is reached. Thereby prompting a termination or pause in the exercise, to avoid
overexertion and/or unnecessary pain. It could therefore make sense to build a model
around multiple parameters and emotions, that dictate the stopping of an exercise.
Pain Prediction with LSTM
An alternative to determining the subject’s state of pain in the present is predicting their
future state. This was in practice done by using an LSTM neural network. This produced
a model that could output predictions of future points. By classifying these future points
with a classification algorithm, future pain states are effectively predicted. One of the
ideas behind this was to have the ability to e.g. react before a painful state is reached.
The results from the pain prediction are incomplete as only a subset of cross­validations
has been conducted. This is due to the time­consuming nature of training LSTM networks
(∼3­4 hours for one fold). Thus, it was not feasible to do cross­validation with every data
set produced by the experiment as test data. This meant that the approach to evaluat­
ing the model was slightly adapted. Evaluations used a moving window as explained in
section 5.1.1. On a random selection of 10 data sets, the future pain prediction accu­
racy reached approximately 80% for three labels. Notably, this result is higher than the

Real­Time vs. Retrospective Classification
The goal of the Exercise Pain Experiment was to develop a method of assessing pain lev­
els during exercise. Detecting if a patient is in pain while performing an exercise is key to
avoiding injuries. Exercising has to stop before the patient becomes indisposed. Though
instantaneous information about when to stop an exercise is important, another aspect
can be the general discomfort during certain exercises. This can be useful information to
know which rehabilitation exercises to avoid, due to pain or discomfort.
With this idea in mind, we applied a retrospective classification model as an alternative
to the real­time classification models we created. This can achieve higher accuracy as
it has access to all the information at the same time. The higher accuracy was evident
in the ROCKET classification, as it reached 90% accuracy on three pain labels. Informa­
tion regarding level of pain during certain movements can also be helpful to the medical
staff. It can help avoid reaching the higher levels of pain in future exercises. Without
reaching the intense pain or perhaps even avoiding moderate pain, this model could be
used between rehabilitation sessions to not only avoid pain but also improve the actual
rehabilitation through greater comfort.
Perhaps a future enhancement of this could be to use the retrospective model on time
segments during the exercises. This would slow down the model speed but may increase
the accuracy of the pain detection.
As the golden standard of pain assessment is on a 10­point scale, the accuracy and ability
to distinguish multiple levels of pain might be more achievable with retrospective analysis.
Though the results only showed promise with fewer levels of pain (three), it could be the
best option to reach the 10­level pain scale. It is not unrealistic to think that given more
data, something like a 5­point scale would be within the boundaries of reason.

Spasticity Experiment
The goal of the spasticity experiment was to be able to create a model that could predict
the spasticity level with a MAS score. Through data collected on elbow angle and torque in
certain movements, we wanted to find the correlation to the MAS. The model was based
on the data collected regarding movement and torque. Further, the whole experiment
was based on an assumption that it is possible to imitate the spasticity corresponding to a
certain score on the MAS. The assumption was that a translation of the MAS scores into

than the MAS definitions and as the experiment data was devised from the MAS scale,

Classification Results
The supervised classification results indicated a very high ability to distinguish the six
levels of the MAS, with an accuracy of 89.2%. This supports the idea of an automated
approach to spasticity measurement. Only 8 of the total 74 points were misclassified
and the misclassifications were somewhat evenly distributed. This makes it harder to
pinpoint an exact reason for the misclassifications. A possible explanation could be that
the movement patterns imitated in the MAS can vary quite a bit within each score. The
data points representing the characteristics within each score might be more like points in
another score, than the ones in its own. With more data, it might be clearer for the model
where the cutoffs lie and separate them. This behaviour could also explain why KNN
performs worse than the other models. KNN relies on the classes of nearby (neighbouring)
data points. If the nearest neighbours belong to other classes, KNN will still believe they
belong together and mistakes arise.
The unsupervised clustering algorithm KernelKMeans achieved accuracy close to the one
found for supervised classification. However, the result should not be understood as a
way of classifying the different levels of spasticity alone. Rather, it is an indication of the
precision of having exactly six spasticity levels in a classification model. As seen in the
simplified plot in figure 5.5, the clusters have different traits for each of them. Though
certain clusters have overlapping traits, an accuracy of nearly 80% is relatively high given
such a small dataset. To support the notion, we found that any lesser number of clusters
would decrease the accuracy and ARI score significantly. A higher amount of clusters
increased values slightly for the TimeSeriesKMeans. However, it should be noted that
simply increasing the number of clusters inflates the scores. This is due to small outliers
being clustered into the extra clusters which remove them from being compared to other
clusters. As such, the unsupervised clustering suggests that the six levels of the MAS are
a good distinction of the levels of spasticity.
Pitfalls of the Modified Ashworth Scale
Largely, the MAS translates to specific movement patterns.
The onset of resistance
throughout the movement dictates almost the entire scale, accompanied to some extent
by the shape of movement patterns. The last element is the amount of resistance.
This experiment has an inherent problem in distinguishing between 2 and 3 on the MAS
based on movement patterns alone. That exposes a limitation in the produced data. This
limitation stems from unrealistic amounts of resistance in the experiment.
On the scale, 2 is a marked increase in muscle tone throughout most of the ROM, while
3 is a considerable increase in muscle tone, making passive movement difficult. By this
definition, 3 could in principle still be a smooth motion. This means that situations can
occur, where there are no differences in the movement pattern itself. Thus, there is no
clear distinction between a score of 2 and 3. In reality, the clinician doing the assessment
has to feel the resistance and base the results on that.
An oddity in the scale itself is the expectancy to have continuous motion, over a dura­
tion of one second. It seems unlikely that a movement of 0 and 3 would take the same
amount of time. One is a movement without resistance and the other has considerable
resistance. Consulting clinicians using the scale, could provide answers to this oddity.

The time window of one second and the ability to consistently fulfil it, is one of the main

angle should be the same throughout the movement. This has not exactly been the case
for the experiment but is also seemingly a problem in the real world. One of the strengths
of automating the MAS with a strong exoskeleton, is that it could almost guarantee the

Limitations
The extent of the spasticity was significantly limited by the technical limitations caused by
the project’s dependency on other student projects. Yet, the role it could play in the future
is not reflected in its extensiveness. Furthermore, without access to actual spasticity
patients, the experiment was limited to imitating the spasticity. In this experiment, the
resistance provided was an arbitrary amount. The resistance does vary, as according to
the scale. Yet, the problem is that the resistance does not reflect realistic amounts. This
is due to the limitations in the strength of the available exoskeleton(s). The models trained
in this experiment will in some situations have to distinguish between a score of 2 and 3
based on the resistance alone. In principle, the same can be true for a score of 0. This
means that it could have trouble classifying real data.
The models from the spasticity experiment can not be directly integrated directly into the
ReHyb project. This is due to the resistance data not reflecting realistic amounts. With a
stronger exoskeleton, new data could be created in a similar fashion. With this project, a
code framework exists, enabling easy creation of future models. Inputting the improved
data into this framework would result in an applicable model.
An alternative approach to assessing muscle spasticity, also in an automated fashion,
is the use of myotonometric measurements [53]. This would eliminate the need for ex­
oskeletons. However, that would thereby pivot away from the tools expected to be avail­
able through the ReHyb project. The myotonometric approach would also require stroke
patients to build a model applicable in real life.

Progress Experiment
1RM+10% and Resistance Band
From the progress results regarding the peak angle for 1RM+10% and resistance band,
it is clear that the progress has been positive. The participants generally end at a higher
”Elbow Angle”/ROM than they began with. This is especially visible in the normalised
graphs in figures 5.8 and 5.10.
When looking at the trajectories of progress, there is a general tendency of a ”dip” or
staleness around evaluations one and two. A couple of possibilities come to mind, that
might help explain this phenomenon. These early evaluations might be at a time, where
the participants have not been able to build up an amount of strength, that can compensate
for the initial fatigue of the muscles. An initial fatigue stemming from a sudden onset of
training. After these evaluations, the body has perhaps had enough time to adapt to the
training frequency and the weight. The muscles and nervous system have had time to
develop, while the level of fatigue has decreased.
An exception to this general picture is seen in subject 4. This subject was significantly
more trained than the other individuals. The progress was smaller, and that this dip is
not present in their case (marked with pink and grey on the plots). This could support the
idea, that the sudden onset of training might be the cause of the dip, in other subjects’
cases.
Part of the reason for the dip in peak ROM could also come from the timing of training and
evaluation. The training paradigm might in some cases have clashed with evaluations.
E.g., if the recovery was slower in the beginning, or the training happened the day before

3RM ­ Progression by Movement Pattern
3RM was introduced in the interest of finding a different measure of progress. One that
did not require the subject to do single, very tough movements. The idea was that consis­
tency across several movements would increase with strength. This could then be used
as a measure of progress in itself. If so, it could allow the gathering of progress data on
stroke patients, by recording their general movements.
The consistency has been examined as the sum of time differences between peaks and
the sum of height (angle) differences between peaks. The peaks are in regards to angle
and lie at ”the top” of the repetitions, where the flexion of the biceps is at the its maximum.
Generally, the results on height difference did not show clear indications of improved con­
sistency. There are hints of the difference generally being slightly lower towards the end
of the experiment. Some potential outliers exist for this evaluation form. This might be
due to struggling with one of the repetitions. The potential outliers belong to subject five
and can be seen in figure 5.15. It can also be seen that this person had some noticeable
dips in progression, regarding the peak angle for both the resistance band and 1RM+10%.
This might imply, that the week two and three evaluations were impacted by perhaps fa­
tigue, or other factors for this person. Ultimately, the data returns to states comparable to
the rest.
The trend is more clear for the time differences between the repetitions. At the first evalu­
ation, the values are more spread and generally higher than at evaluation five. This could
indicate, that consistency in time spent on doing a repetitive movement, is correlated with
an increase in strength. That being said, consistency in movement is an abstract way
to model progress. In practice, it would need some sort of transformation, to be of use
for displaying progress to a patient or clinician. But, the fact that there is a trend can be
useful for the prediction of progress in other aspects. For example, predicting the peak
angle of 1RM+10% progression. This is because the correlation can support a machine
learning model in decision­making. It could have been interesting to do the Exercise Pain
Experiment for subjects before and after the Progression Experiment. Then, carry out the
same analysis of the data as done with the 3RM data. From that, it would be possible to
see if progression patterns in the consistency were reflected in the Exercise Pain data as
well.
EMG Signals
The results surrounding the EMG signal proved very hard to conclude upon. Most of
the data were not statistically different at the end of the experiment. There were many
reasons why, especially the limitations we encountered using the EMG sensor. As was
evident in section 5.3, the EMG signal variance was incredibly high ­ above 3000 µV in
the ARV results alone. With a variance that high, it becomes nearly impossible to detect
changes in the EMG features.
We noticed a 20% increase in the ARV for the resistance band. This is in agreement
with the expected tendency, as other studies have found a positive correlation between
the strength of a muscle and the ARV [172]. A possible reason why only the resistance
band data showed the expected correlation, might be the nature of the exercises. The
resistance band has the unique feature of increasing resistance while stretching. If the
participants were able to lift higher, they exerted more force. This was also expected by
the 1RM+10% exercise. However, the latter was challenged by the fact that three out of
five participants became able to complete a repetition with the weight. Completing the
repetition indicates that they could have exerted more force, which was then not captured
by the exercise. People generally spent longer time attempting to lift the weight, compared

Dominant vs. Non­dominant Progression
In the progress experiment, the participants were asked to train their dominant arm at
twice the frequency of the non­dominant arm. Every 2nd and 4th day respectively. The
expectancy was to see the frequency having a significant impact. Specifically, the pro­
gression was expected to be greater for the dominant arm. As seen in table 5.9, the
DA did in general see the largest absolute increase in ROM, and thereby strength. This
harmonises well with the dominant arm being trained at a higher frequency than the non­
dominant one. Yet, the difference is not as large as initially expected. This might very
well be due to the difference in starting point of strength. It is reasonable to assume that
most individuals are stronger with their DA compared to the NDA. This would be espe­
cially true for persons that are less trained, since most people attempt to train their body
symmetrically. Thus, they would use equal weight in training for both their DA and NDA.
As progress in strength and hypertrophy is faster in less trained individuals [171] [151],
the assumption can be made that this is also true on a muscle­to­muscle basis.
Therefore, the difference in frequency might be balanced out due to the progression being
generally slower for the stronger, dominant arm, compared to the weaker, non­dominant
arm.
Progress Forecasting
Progress forecasting was done with a generic model and individualised models. Both had
considerable amounts of deviation from the actual results. The generic model turned out
to be too simple to make any useful predictions. Yet, even with the sparse data, individ­
ualised models were generally far better. This indicates that individual models regarding
this type of progress might be better suited to predict future progress. Most of the devi­
ation in the progress prediction comes from a minority of the participants. Many of the
predictions are visually close, as can be seen in appendix C, figures B.1 through 10. With
a MAPE score of 23.4% indicates that the model is not very robust. The most obvious
reason for this is the high variability within the data itself, and the very sparse amount of
data points. This results in fluctuations having a large impact.
Even though the EMG data did not show clear signs of correlation with the progression
in ROM, it still turned out to be of value for forecasting progress. Without the extracted
features from EMG data as part of the training dataset, the MAPE score was 30.4%, com­
pared to 23.4% with the EMG data.
With more data, a general tendency might have become clearer. The strength of LSTM
networks is the memory. However, in a setting with only four data points and no clear
trend within these, the LSTM might not receive enough information to make reasonable

Limitations
The results of this experiment rely on consistency in training, but also on the participants
giving their all during evaluations. This would be the case for any experiment of this type.
However, failure to meticulously follow the regimen might occur, which could increase
fluctuations in the results.
A longer time duration of this experiment would likely be beneficial. It would reduce the
significance of eventual fluctuations in the sparse data, and could show more long­term
progression trajectories. Stroke rehabilitation can take up to several years. But getting
an idea of just the initial progression track could be useful for a clinician and motivate a
patient.
A very prominent limiting factor in this experiment is the participants. This experiment
assesses progression in healthy individuals. Additionally, the number of participants is
low (five participants), albeit each participant produces data for each of their arms.
The progress, made in terms of strength in a healthy individual, is unlikely to translate
directly to someone post­stroke, who e.g. suffers from spasticity in the given muscle(s).
Ideally, the participants of this experiment would be people undergoing post­stroke reha­
bilitation. Furthermore, the number of participants could be much larger. The variance in
progression between healthy individuals can be great in itself, due to sex, starting point
in strength and age. For stroke patients, this variance is likely even greater, as a variety
of factors caused by the stroke, has an additional impact on their ability to move. Those
could be factors such as spasticity and limited ROM in general.
Similar projects, e.g., [151], observed progress over a longer period of time (12 weeks).
Observing over a longer period of time gives more data points and thus might deliver
clearer results. However, we had to get participants willing to commit to the experiment.
The chance of finding such participants decreases the longer the experiment lasts, the
more effort they have to put in it and the more intrusive the experiment is. Thus, while
longer observation period might significantly improve the data quality and informative
value, it would not have been possible to collect this data in the scope of this project.
Application
The accuracy of the progress prediction is not high enough for direct integration given the
discussed limitations. However, the model itself is implemented in a way that ­ provided it
had better accuracy ­ could be directly integrated in the digital twin engine. In general, a
working progress prediction could help the system or therapists to select optimal exercise
plans based on the exercises’ expected effect on the patient’s progress. For patients and
caregivers, this could further be a motivating factor, showing them why certain exercises
were selected and what effect they would have when reliably being conducted.
The applied model, the LSTM network, would be stronger with more data. It can capitalise
on tendencies in time series data, which can be very hard to manually extract. This model
can be recommended for future attempts of progress prediction with more data.
From the Progress Experiment, we are able to see developments in ROM, based on
training patterns. Even with a limited time frame and participants, there are some clear
tendencies. The predictions are a bit sporadic, but with a MAPE score of 23.4%, it still
gives a reasonable sign of where the progress is headed in most cases. This indicates
that there is potential for creating progress prediction models.
This experiment was conducted in healthy individuals. It is not reasonable to assume that

Throughout the thesis, several challenges and limitations were encountered and over­

6.2.1
Emteq Limitations
While working on the Exercise Pain Experiment, the Emteq face mask had several lim­
itations which had an impact on the experiments. The movement from the participants
performing the biceps curling exercise gave small variances in the EMG signal. This is
partly due to the Emteq EMG electrodes not being attached to the skin with an adhesive.
Rather, it has a series of metal electrodes tightened to the face using straps. Movement
on the contact surface decreases the accuracy and results in spikes in EMG amplitude.
This was also evident in the last section of the EMG signals, as the facial expression
changed and the electrodes could sometimes have trouble staying in place on the skin.
Additionally, on two occasions experiments had to be excluded. The Emteq was not able
to fit one of the participants. In the other case, the participant reacted to an outside distur­
bance during the experiment, including talking. This prompted a change in facial expres­
sion during recording, thus polluting the data. Both resulted in a large enough disturbance
in the EMG signal to merit discarding of the result. It is recommended to use as controlled
an environment as possible for future studies attempting to use a similar setup.
The Emteq had two facial fitting sizes. Though the majority might fit into these sizes, it
creates variances in the fit for different facial structures. Since muscles in the face can be
very small, the placement of the electrodes can mean a large difference in the recorded
EMG signal.
Furthermore, the Emteq was used on healthy individuals and while it worked on them, it
might not work to the same degree for stroke patients. This could be caused by partial
facial paralysis which stroke patients often suffer from. No evidence or data exists for the
Emteq on patients with stroke and partial facial paralysis.
While the intent in one of the experiments was to be able to classify based on Emteq
data in real­time, this was not an option. We contacted Emteq Labs (the creators of the
product) without being able to get an answer as to how we could extract the data in real­
time. Contact was attempted both by mail and phone. Their software displays the data in
real­time, but this data is not available to the user.

6.3
Implications for the Research Questions
In this thesis, we approached the subject from a standpoint defined by our research ques­
tions. The questions were used to keep us on the right path, and helped structure the goals
of the thesis. These questions also allowed adaptations used to overcome challenges that
required redefining the way we approached our sub­goals.
The first research question is about finding ways to quantify patient states using the avail­
able sensor data streams. This project attempts to quantify range of motion, pain, spastic­
ity and strength progression. These themes were found in collaboration with the ReHyb
project and through research on the most prevalent physical complications present in
stroke patients. In practice, this was explored through literature research and notably
by conducting a series of studies. The goal of these was to find ways of quantifying the
patient’s state. They consisted of gathering sample data to evaluate the reliability and po­
tential of the framework. We conducted three main studies. One for each complex patient
state identified. A supplemental study took place during the Exercise Pain Experiment.
This study revolved around fsEMG data from isolated pain.
Range of motion can be read directly from the angle data recorded, by having a subject
attempt to complete specific movements. Thus, this area did not receive further investi­
gation. We found that using the Emteq and exoskeleton sensor data we could quantify
pain levels to a certain extent. Furthermore, we made use of sensors equipped to the
exoskeleton, measuring voltage and elbow angle. These values were correlated to the
MAS, evaluating the level of spasticity in the patient. Finally, a strength­progress ex­
periment was run over a five­week period. We used exoskeleton angle data and biceps
brachii sEMG data to evaluate and forecast the progress produced by a workout regi­
men. The progress experiment had few participants. As such, it is hard to make concrete
conclusions. Yet, we discovered and explored several tendencies.
The second research question revolves around potential benefits emerging from sensor­
based state assessment for future rehabilitation systems. Detecting pain in a patient
undergoing rehabilitation exercises with an exoskeleton is crucial for being able to stop
forced movements and avoid hurting the patient. In using sensors for monitoring the pa­
tient’s current state, data could potentially also be gathered regarding certain movements
resulting in pain. Knowing the level of pain a patient experiences during certain exercises
even on a coarse scale can help guide rehabilitation.
Automation of spasticity assessment holds potential. For MAS specifically, it can remove
some of the human error, which is a big point of criticism for the scale. This also frees up
the human resources otherwise used in the clinical assessment.

evaluate the patient manually.

7
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to identify ways to quantify patient states through sensor data. Data
aggregation and AI­prediction techniques were used to predict the patient states based
on sensor data, gathered through a series of experiments. Using this data, we developed
classification models and neural network regression models, accompanied by real­time
and retrospective prediction models. These models have been applied to predict pain
level, spasticity score and the physical progress of the workout regimens.
It was not possible to access stroke patients for data gathering in this project. The quan­
tification of state has thus happened in healthy individuals as opposed to in patients.
Through an agile scrum methodological approach, three concrete models were designed.
for executing the best achievable dissection and simplification of the key important fea­
tures ­ patient state ­ found by the ReHyb project’s extent as well as the most prevalent
complications found in stroke patients. The first model was based on the detection of pain.
This model enables monitoring the patient’s level of pain, during exercise. The applica­
tion of this model is to avoid excessive pain in rehabilitation exercises. The second model
was designed to detect the level of spasticity. The model correlates the data to a state
on a medical scale appropriate for stroke patients. The choice of medical scale was the
Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS). The MAS is the gold standard of spasticity assessment.
The third model was designed to monitor, estimate and predict the physical progress of
a stroke patient on several levels. Primarily, the prediction of muscle strength through
range of motion, in movement with resistance. A supplemental model for pain was cre­
ated to test pain in isolation. This model was created with an intention of evaluating the
pain detection.
For each model, an experiment was conducted to gather sample data. The models were
evaluated concerning their respective purposes.
The exercise pain model showed a great deal of potential in determining the pain level at
any given time during repetitive exercise. The 3­level pain model hit an accuracy upwards
of 75% while the 2­level pain model reached 95.7%. The strength progress model was
able to evaluate and detect progress from participants. Primarily, the angle data (ROM)
correlated with the progression of strength. The EMG signal showed positive tendencies
despite the data’s high variance and disturbance. With more precise data, these tenden­
cies could correlate to the progress seen in ROM. Finally, the spasticity model quantified
states by determining a score on the MAS. This reached an accuracy of upwards of 90%.
In short, the three models all reached a relative ability to quantify the patient’s state through
their respective sensor data.
Though several challenges and limitations were encountered, the experiments were tai­
lored to accommodate as many as possible. The more technical equipment challenges
did affect the reliability and accuracy of the data and, as a result, the models.
All three models make use of data from multiple sensors. The data is aggregated in data
fusion and AI techniques are applied. This enables quantification of the patient states as
a result. The three models were evaluated in terms of reliability and potential by the data
gathered in the four experiments, conducted during the thesis. It can thus be argued that
the research questions were analysed and answered.
The work done in this thesis has resulted in concrete, possible approaches to predicting

7.1
Future Work
Possible work following this thesis could revolve around the integration of the presented
approaches, into the Human Digital Twin concept. An important next step is to gather
data from stroke patients. The approach and the prediction power of the models will have
to be re­evaluated to some extent. This is because the values within the data will deviate
from that recorded for healthy individuals. However, the framework now exists for such
an evaluation. The framework consists of the code produced throughout the project.
Pain
The Exercise Pain Experiment can in principle be used for future data collection as­is.
More applicable models can then be trained with the new data.
Part of the future work should involve deciding how fine­grained a pain scale might have
to be, to be of value. This project showed that it is reasonable to classify three levels
of pain, in data for repetitive movement with the elbow flexor. Three levels might be too
coarse for a clinical setting.
It is relevant to find out under which specific circumstances a rehabilitation exercise with
an exoskeleton should be stopped. By combining models that predict pain, fatigue and
mental state, a more precise measure of the need to stop can be found.
Spasticity
With a stronger exoskeleton and stroke patients available, the Spasticity Experiment can
be done as initially intended. In this approach, the exoskeleton extends the biceps brachii
of a patient while measuring the resistance provided by occurring spasticity. This could
validate the approach on stroke patients to which the model would be applied in reality.
With an exoskeleton supporting different joints than the elbow, this approach is applicable
for assessing spasticity in other parts of the body as well.
Progress
To better the progress experiment for stroke patients, data should be collected on stroke
patients instead of healthy individuals. The progress seen in healthy individuals does not
equate to progress in stroke patients. Muscles in patients with restricted movements can
progress differently or the progress could be detected in other areas such as fewer muscle
spasms. Exploring these progressions, could better the HDT’s development.
Despite the results being limited in their validity due to the low amount of data, they show
that individualised models generally perform better than generic models. One way to
continue with the progress concept would be to use transfer learning. Here, the first
general models are trained which are then further trained with individual data throughout
the system’s run time. Dividing patients into groups based on initial state of strength,
could help build better general models for those groups.
