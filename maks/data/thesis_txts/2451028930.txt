Summary (English)
The goal for this thesis is to design, implement and evaluate the Time Slot-
ted Channel Hopping Medium Access Control-protocol in combination with
LoRa radios.
Time Slotted Channel Hopping is designed for high-frequence
radios with short transmission times, and controls access to the radio-layers
of a networking stack, and is used to achieve high reliance and low power-
consumption in multi-hop networks. LoRa is a relatively new technology, that
has a distance/power tradeoﬀ, where higher distances reachable also leads to
higher transmission times, and thereby higher power consumption.
First the two technologies are described along with the LoRaWAN-speciﬁcation,
which is designed for use with LoRa, but however gives completely diﬀerent op-
tions, opportunities and disadvantages compared to TSCH, as it is built around
a Star-topology under the assumption that all units are within range of a gate-
way, which is connected to the outside world and without power constraints.
After this, the design and implementation done to make TSCH and LoRa work
together is described. This is done through making the LoRa radio a part of the
networking stack of the OS Contiki-ng, which has a standard-compliant version
of TSCH embedded. After this, a multi-hop experiment is executed to show the
bandwidth and power consumption of the setup for all nodes, and a jamming
experiment executed to test the reliability of the channel hopping when one
channel is jammed. This reliability is shown to be 98%.
This is followed by a brief discussion and conclusion over the ﬁndings, where it
among others is estimated that the overhead of the setup with TSCH, Contikin-
g and the Contiki-ng networking stack is relatively large, but also that it can
==PAGE:4==
ii
possibly be tweaken in future works. Apart from that, it is conclude that it is
possible to achieve a multi-hop setup and jamming-resitent communication over
LoRa with a relatively okay power consumption. It is furthermore assessed that
further research would be beneﬁcial, in part to test the system over a larger
network than merely 3 nodes, with bigger ranges, more spreading factors and
more hops, as well as further estimate and minimize the overhead of the system,
and tweak it, while also considering guard-times.

This thesis was prepared at DTU Compute in fulﬁlment of the requirements for
acquiring an M.Sc. in Engineering.
The thesis deals with combinig the LoRa radio technology with Time Slotted
Channel Hopping and the full contiki-ng stack, enabling IPv6 and seamless

Chapter 1
Introduction
This thesis concerns the industrial internet of things, and the networks that
connect sensor-nodes in wireless sensor networks. As wireless sensor networks
are widely used in the internet of things, to scan and sense anything from tem-
peratures, humidity and light to e.g. pollution and noise, these networks are
usually ’separate’ networks connecting devices to each other, with a gateway to
the internet.
As this is widely used for measurements, new technologies emerge. In particular,
LoRa has been deemed an ’enabler’ for the IoT, as it has extremely long ranges,
and is widely used together with the LoRaWAN-stack. However, as this mainly
just assumes that the radios are always in range, and very limited data, it is very
lightweight protocol whose basic concept is just to ’transmit and hope’. This is
why, in this thesis, it will be attempted to apply the Contiki-ng stack with LoRa,
gaining access to Time Slotted Channel Hopping, oﬀering a standard compliant
way of sending data through a mesh-network, using multiple hops, instead of
merely assuming that the star-network gateway is always in range.
The overall goal of the thesis is to build and design the tools necessary for
testing TSCH - and the mesh-network structure usually used in wireless sensor
networks - on LoRa radios.
Development of a driver for the LoRa device Semtech SX1272 and integration

conﬁgurations needed to make the full stack work. In this process, timeslots for

LoRa-network.
1.1
Iiot and Wireless Sensor Networks
The IoT, Internet of Things, is a current buzzword of the industry. The gen-
eral concept is connecting a lot of devices to the internet, usually aiding in all
sort of applications where some physical aspect, a ’thing’, is connected to the
net. This ’thing’ can be sensors, robots, cars, identiﬁcation cards, smartwatches,
cellphones - essentially everything connected to the internet while not being a
classical PC. Industrial IoT mainly focuses on connecting things to the inter-
net with an industrial view - cutting out electronic refrigerators, smartwatches,
phones etc. A major part of Iiot is Wireless sensor networks, which is the focus
of this work.
Wireless sensor networks are networks of small devices - nodes - with sensors and
radios, that usually connect to the internet through a gateway, which indirectly
means that all data produced in the network needs to reach said gateway. These
sensors can, among others, be sensing temperature, humidity and light levels,
which can be relevant for smart buildings, agriculture or potential ﬁre detection
[1], soil levels [2], or updates about stadium lightning [3]. The main advantage of
wireless sensor networks is that they are easily deployed, without predetermined
locations, and usually battery driven.
This means that deploying sensors is
possible without having to ensure power or other connections like internet to
every single sensor. All that is needed is merely a wireless link from the sensors
to a gateway to send data out.
However, as setting up multiple gateways in order for every single node to be
able to reach the gateway with signals might be cumbersome, and also, for
faraway nodes to reach the gateway, it is possible that they need to increase the

1.2
LoRa
LoRa is a new radio technology, posing new opportunities, as well as challenges
in usage. LoRa operates in the 863-870 mhz-bands in Europe, and in the 902-
928-band in the US. LoRa was developed originally by Cycleo, who was later
acquired by Semtech. The idea behind LoRa is to use Chirp Spread Spectrum to
increase the range of wireless signals, making for low-power radios with longer
ranges [5]. However, the physical implementation of LoRa - the PHY layer -
is not of interest to this thesis, rather the opportunities and challenges it gives
us. Due to LoRa operating in unlicensed bands, there’s limits to how large a
percentage of the time a radio can be on. For e.g. the EU-868 bands this is
limited to 1%.
LoRa has multiple diﬀerent channels in the bands for it’s respective area - e.g.

1.2.1
LoraWan
LoRaWan is the name of a speciﬁcation outlined by the LoRa alliance, whose
goal it is to be a secure, end-to-end protocol.
According to [11], the LoRa
Alliance describe it as a ’star of stars’ topology network, consisting of several
star-networks connected with end-gateways, the outer stars, connected to a
central server, the central star. This, in practice, is merely several star-networks
who all relay messages to the same central server, and has the properties of a
star-network.
A star-network is a network with a single gateway, and all inhabitants of that
network talk directly to that gateway. LoRaWan deﬁnes three sort of device
classes;
Class A: Sends mainly data upstream, and after sending data upstream it listens
for two timeslots to see if any response has come.
Class B: Sends mainly data upstream, but also has listening windows for down-
stream data. Consumes more power than class A, due to turning on the radio
for listening more often.
Class C: Listens all the time, unless when it’s sending messages.
It is required that all devices implement class A, while classes B/C are optional.
However, being a star-topology, this means that all devices are limited by their
range to the nearest gateway. Also, as most nodes risk being far away from
the gateway, a higher percentage of nodes will utilize higher spreading factors,
leading to increased noise, which could be a problem in areas with many nodes,
as described by [12], who also points out that using higher spreading factors
increases time on air for devices, and, as such, using lower spreading factors
and multiple hops might save energy - as seen in table 1.1, going from SF12
to SF10 reduces the time on air by a factor of 3.5. While building a multi-

1.3
TSCH
Time slotted channel hopping is a MAC layer that’s part of the 6TiSCH-
standard of IEEE 802.15.4-2015. It, as a MAC layer, is in general controlling
usage of the radio, and can as such go in as part of any networking stack. The
general idea is to divide the network into an inﬁnite number of timeslots, where
the nodes change channels between the timeslots, to ensure resilience against
interference. As such, a node is on a given timeslot on channel A, and the follow-
ing timeslot on channel D, and this way the node will jump channels, ensuring
that if one or more channels are ﬁlled with noise, while other channels are avail-
able, the network will be spending time on available channels. The scheduling
should also try to achieve that when diﬀerent nodes are communicating, they
are doing it at diﬀerent channels, to minimize collisions, like e.g. in ﬁgure 1.2.
The IEEE 802.15.4-standard doesn’t require any speciﬁc schedule to be imple-
mented, and this is left up to the individuals developing a TSCH-project [13].

1.3.1
TSCH Timeslots
According to the schedule and initial joining of the TSCH network, it must be
ensured that the nodes are as synchronized as possible. This is due to the need
of having diﬀerent nodes starting the same timeslot as close as possible to each
other, as TSCH works under the assumption that they started at the same,
identical time ± an error that is as small as possible.
While it can be assumed that on a network, transmitting messages is simple,
as it merely requires the radio being set in transmission mode for the period
needed. Listening for messages, however, also consumes power, and as such,
listening time would like to be minimised. The naive approach of being always-
on would destroy battery life. What TSCH does is, it deﬁnes a timeslot schedule,
where the recipient starts listening for messages exactly ’RxOﬀset’ after the
start of the timeslot. Then, it will only listen a certain guard time - a time
that the transmitter has to start sending messages.
If the recipient has not
received any sign of a message in this relative short window, it will turn oﬀ
the radio again, minimizing power consumption, while it will keep on listening
for the full message, if it is about to receive anything. The same is done when
sending/receiving acks, to ensure that as little power as possible is consumed
by the listeners. This is modelled in ﬁgure 1.3.

The main motivation for TSCH over LoRa is in part, to increase range for LoRa
networks through making multi-hop networks, which also might reduce power

used in this thesis. In [18] they design and evaluate timeslots for radios faster
than the default, the opposite case of LoRa, but a similar challenge as the one
encountered in this thesis.

LoRa scales badly on a high amount of end-devices due to collisions. In [19],
ranges is found for diﬀerent spreading factors when there exists a clear line of
sight, as well as an obstructed path.
In [9], a study is made about orthogonality of spreading factors, as well as
a suggested protocol, LoRaBlink, which is aimed to give reliable and eﬃcient
multi-hop networking, and bi-directional communication. It is, like TSCH, using
time slots and beacons for synchronization, however, unlike the TSCH-setup
in this thesis, it is not channel jumping, and with no option for scheduling,
other than transmitting in the next slot. Time synchronization is also never
done on receiving acks, rather, the whole network resynchronizes once every
synchronzation period. However, they also achieve multi-hop networks and a
reliability of 80%.
In [20], and the related paper [21], a group of researchers describe embedding
drivers for another LoRa-device, the SX1276, to the Contiki-os, which Contiki-
ng is a fork of. However, currently, the Kratos-project is limited to only sup-
porting MSP430 and Tmote Sky, and it is only MSP430 that has a LoRa-driver,
which is not built as platform-independent. Apart from this, Contiki-os is not
maintained as much as Contiki-ng. However, this technically brings LoRa to
IPv6, but only for a single chip family, and with no analysis of the requirements
for using TSCH with LoRa, and no evaluation of it either.
In the three papers released related to Kratos, there is also no focus at all on
using TSCH as MAC-layer for LoRa.
2.3
Combining TSCH and LoRa
Lora and standard-compliant TSCH has in general not been combined, however,
in [22], they build a combination of TSCH and LoRa. However, rather than bas-

craft it gruond up. They design their timeslots to contain diﬀerent spreading
factors, allowing communication across these in a single timeslot. They measure
relatively low packet error rates, however, no energy measurements is made.

When it comes to implementing the driver, building it from scratch is a major
work. However, as mentioned previously, the radio itself is already supported
by the LoRaWAN-stack with a semtech driver, and there is also an mbed-driver,
while both are also shipping with the lorawan-stack. However, both are clearly
divided into LoraWAN-stack and radio driver, so it was deemed feasible to
extract the radioparts from either of them, and add it to the Contiki-ng-stack.
While implementing the driver, it is attempted to modify as little as the Contiki-
ng sources as possible, to still have the modularity of the stack, giving the option
to merely replace the radio driver if needed.
As the mbed-driver was written in c++, while the full Contiki-ng system is
written in C, it was chosen to pick the oﬃcial Semtech-driver.
Work progressed from then by ﬁrst substituting the calls to the HAL in the
semtech driver to calls to the Contiki-ng-HAL, ﬁrst completely identifying the
HAL, as well as getting the SPI connection between the radio and the TI
Launchpad-board up and running, as well as replacing all calls to timers in
the semtech-driver to calls to Contikis system timers.
This is in part done,
as it will make sanity-checking further development much easier, but also to
have a base to set out from in terms of initializing channel frequencies, setting
spreading factors and so on.

requirements than LoRaWAN. While LoRaWAN is mainly sending, listening
indeﬁnitely, and listening with a timeout, TSCH ﬁrst and foremost requires

drivers - or, at least, very closely resembling them.

remaining functionality needed in the driver, lean on the existing source-code

whenever any doubts arise.

The driver is also interrupt-based as much as possible. Whenever something
happens, an interrupt ﬁres, and the driver then does the cleaning in the interrupt-
call. During these calls, ’RadioEvents’ are also called appropriately - these are
events that the application/MAC layer can specify to be called whenever some-
thing happens. These are ’TxDone’, ’RxDone’, ’TxTimeout’, ’RxTimeout’ and
’RxError’.
While the requirements set by Contiki-ng TSCH are the following:
• Get- and set RX mode without address ﬁltering and autoacks.
• Set reception mode as poll-mode
• Disable CCA before sending
• Set channel
• Get last packet timestamp
• Optionally read RSSI as well as link quality
• Preparing a transmission, then executing it later
• Poll-mode reception
• Reading an unread packet from the radio FIFO at any time.
Address ﬁltering and Autoacks are either oﬀby default or not possible to turn
on, so these are not a problem. CCA is also oﬀby default, and will only turn
on whenever an appropriate register is written. As RSSI and Link Quality are
optional, these have not been looked at.
Setting channels can be done by merely calling the Semtech-driver, with the
addition that according to the datasheet [7], the device has to be in standby- or
sleepmode to set the channel, which has to be ensured, where the semtech-driver
merely assumes this, however, this is trivial.
Apart from this, TSCH bases itself on being able to read whether the radio
is about to receive, or has a pending packet, in a polling-mode, as described
by ﬁgure 3.3. This is due to TSCH wanting to turn oﬀthe radio as fast as
possible whenever it turns out that it is not receiving any messages in its RX
slot. Instead of waiting for either the full duration of the slot for a message, it
is desired to know exactly what happens when the guard time has passed.
Also, not polling the radio would mean waiting for radio to letting data known
through an interrupt, which is suboptimal as the Contiki-ng implementation

3.3.1
Polling Mode
The main issues resolves to reception, as well as noting package timestamps. As
TSCH will be constantly polling the driver and not yielding the process, it will
not be possible to use the interrupt-based layout of the semtech-driver. The
radio itself doesn’t support time-stamping incoming packets, so this must be
done manually as well.
As such it is needed to ﬁnd a way to fast check whether the radio is about to
receive a package, as well as whether there is package pending, and ﬁgure out
how to get the timestamp as well.
3.3.1.1
Currently Receiving?
Polling the radio for whether it is in the process of receiving is done by constantly
reading the modemstatus-register, and whenever it implies that the radio has
either detected or synchronized to a signal, and/or detected a valid header, the
status returns true.
Alternatively reading the RSSI-register is an option. This is however a risky

might also return false negatives, as LoRa can handle a sound ﬂoor down to -

and the amount of background noise present in the testing environment, which

3.3.2

For detection of whether the radio is done receiving, the datasheets as well as

is done receiving is polling the radios interrupt-register to see what interrupts
have ﬁred. Then, whenever an RxDone-interrupt ﬁres, it is visible to the polling,
and the data will be stored.
There is no built-in timestamping possible in the radio, which means that de-
tection of timestamps has to be done by recording the time whenever entering
any of the two pollingfunctions, and then, if an interrupt has ﬁred, assume the
recorded time minus the expected time on air of a packet of the given length to
be the transmission start time. This is highly needed to synchronize the clocks
in the diﬀerent units, ensuring that the timeslots start as close to each other as
possible.
This, however, also means that if the radio was turned on, listening, and picked
up a message, it would be noted in the interrupt-register of the radio. However,
if the MCU then ﬁrst checked the radio 1/10th of a second later to see if any-

leads to a need for adapting the scanning-algorithm of the Contiki-ng TSCH

4.2.1
Contiki-ng conﬁgurations for testing
For testing it was furthermore decided to use the default Contiki-ng scheduling,
which in practice means that every one in n timeslots are used, and no timeslots
are reserved for anything. This is a simpliﬁcation compared to a real-life setting,
where a more intelligent scheduling-choice would have been chosen.
In this case, a timeslot is used every 1 in 5 slots, leading to a ﬂow of one timeslot
of length 278000us, followed by a waiting period of 4 · 278000us.
Security is turned oﬀ, data is not encrypted.
The RPL routing protocol sends out DIO packets regularly.
These packets
contain information saying that there is a network present, allowing nodes that
are synchronized in TSCH to join the network. It is important to note that
picking up an EB doesn’t join a network, it merely ensured that the node will
listen and send at the exact same time as its time-parent.
DIO’s are being sent out consistently every 30 to 120 seconds, controlled by
min- and max-values, where the default settings is a range of 4 seconds to 17
minutes.
DAO’s is a message sent to join a network. As this message need to go from the
node to the root, and be acknowledged all the way back to the node, through
the multi-hop network, the timeout for these have been increased from 5 to 10
seconds, as a minimum of 4 timeslots is needed from a node to a hop to the root
and back, this taking roughly 5,5 seconds.
Channel hopping is done by jumping consistently between 4 diﬀerent channels
from 1 to 2 to 3 to 4 to 1, etc. The diﬀerent channels are unimportant, what
matters is that they are diﬀerent channels.
The scanning process have also been altered. Where the default scanning process
checks very brieﬂy if a packet is on the air - and if it is, receives it and checks if
it’s an enhanced beacon, and if it is not, it changes channel - this was altered - in

Pending packets that have been received before the scanning process are also

4.3
Jamming experiment
4.3.1
Idea
The idea is to take two nodes, a leaf- and a root-node, where the leaf sends mes-
sages to the root-node over rpl. Meanwhile, a third node is constantly sending
out signals on a channel, eﬀectively jamming it and making it unusable. The
goal is then to be seen whether the full system handles resending the messages
on diﬀerent, non-jammed channels, through the channel jumping. Nodes will
detect when messages weren’t received due to lack of acknowledgements. The
messages are 50-byte packets, sent in an interval of once per minute. It should
be noted that the leaf only started transmitting messages when it was known
that it had a connection to the root-node.
The jammer is created by having a node constantly transmit a message, then
wait 1 Contiki-ng tick, the equivalent of 1/128th of a second. The message is
a short 1-byte message, which is done to ensure that as many preambles as
possible are sent, that the other two nodes might pick up and listen to.
According to equation 1.1, the transmission of a 1-byte packet will take 23.8ms,
and waiting 1/128th of a second will be waiting roughly 8ms.
As such, the
channel can be considered occupied 75% of the time.
This experiment was carried out ﬁrst with channel jumping between 4 diﬀerent
channels, being at the jammed channel 25% of the time, and then a second
experiment was done with no channel jumping, always staying at the jammed
channel.

4.4
Multi-hop experiment
Another highly relevant experiment done is a multi-hop experiment between 3
nodes, with node A being the root node, node B being a ’hop’-node, and node
C being a leaf-node. The experiment is designed to ensure that the only path
from C to A is hopping through B. The experiment will measure data error rates
- how many messages sent through the high-level rpl-layer will arrive - as well
as radio time on air transmitting, listening/receiving, and turned oﬀ, as well
as time spent by the board MCU in low-power mode compared to time spent
running in ordinary mode.
All power measurements will be done through the Contiki-ng power measure-
ment system Energest, which tracks the time spent in the diﬀerent states. For
energest tracking of the SX1272, the radio is marked as transmitting for as long
as it is in the transmission-function, which is blocking, and when the radio is
turned on, it is put in listening/receiving-mode, and when turned oﬀit is put
in oﬄine-mode. All these have been done in the driver-wrapper layer.
As such, the standby-mode of the radio that it is in after preparation of a packet
but before sending it has been simpliﬁed away, as this is only for a fraction of
the time the radio spends in transmission-mode, and it’s power consumption
is roughly 1.5 uA, while transmitting is in the scale of 28 mA for 13 dBm-

4.4.3
Network life
We can use the above measurements to give a crude assesment of the ’life’ of a
battery-powered network. As Wireless Sensor Networks are most commonly de-
ployed using battery-powered chips and radios, the time the network will be able
to read data is dependent on battery lifetime, and there, power-consumption.
The network life, in this case, depends on the limitations on the ﬁrst nodes to
die - while it is ﬁne that some nodes keep on transmitting eternally, having a
hop-node run out of power means that every node dependent on the hop will
also be unreachable.
Network life has been calculated assuming the time spent on the diﬀerent activ-
ities - CPU on instead of Low-power mode, radio listening and radio sending,
and simply see how much power these spend, compare to the power on a battery.
The battery chosen is a generic button-battery, which can deliver 3V and 125mah.
Under the assumption that the conversion from 3v to 3.3v used by the radio
is ﬂawless, and no energy is wasted from the battery, it can deliver a total of
0.125·3·602J = 1350J. A normal AA-battery can deliver e.g. 3000 mah at 1.5V
[27], leading to 16200 J, giving a much larger footprint, but also much longer
battery-life.

Chapter 5
Discussion
The results of the experiments are relatively clear in terms of the stability of
the system - having a 98% packet delivery rate on while being jammed on 1 in 4
channels, as well as being able to send through all packets through the multi-hop
experiment. However, power consumption is deemed relatively high, due to the
limited network life of 8.94 days running on a small button cell battery. This is
especially true compared to a solution like LoRaWAN, where the radio merely
wakes up to transmit, sends data, and then goes back to sleep.
Another issue is that when the multi-hop network is ’inactive’, not producing
any data, it still spends .4% of the time in transmission mode, to keep the time
synchronized and network up to date. This is relatively high, considering that
e.g. the EU enforces a maximum transmission time of 1% in the 868mHz-band,
and as such, 40% of the available bandwidth is already consumed.
This is also concerning seeing as the experiments was run on spreading factor
7, with the lowest possible coding rate, this being the options that consume
the least time on air per byte sent. If the network requirements are unchanged
in terms of sending the same bytes and packets, this can potentially be very
limiting to mutli-hop LoRa networks on higher spreading factors, where the
transmission time doubles every time the SF is increased. So, for furthering
this to all spreading factors, optimizations must be done to reduce overhead.
It must also be checked if the overhead consists solely of network declarations

(DIO’s) and Enhanced beacons, as, if that’s the case, it is easy to reduce the
transmission overhead.
However, it might be possible to severely reduce the overhead by sending out
rarer EB’s and network advertisements. While sending out rarer messages will
also lead to rarer synchronization, leading to worsened guard times due to po-
tential clock drift, an optimal trade-oﬀneeds to be found here. Both in terms of
power consumption with increased guard times countering rarer transmissions,
but also in terms of the 1%-requirement.
However, comparing to a solution like LoRaWAN, there is, using the Contiki-ng
stack, IPv6-options, and multi-hop is an option as well. Multi-hop can ﬁrst and
foremost improve the range of the system, as it simply allows data to hop among
radios, also softening the requirements to positioning - any radio just needs to
be within range of the network, rather than within range of the gateway, as is
required by LoRaWAN. IPv6 is giving actual IP-adresses to devices, making it
possible to ping them if need be, and change settings if desired - e.g. frequency
of measurements - putting the actual things on the internet, rather than merely
having them accessible through a radio signal. It practically puts them very
much online, creating a downlink to all devices. As such, this solution puts
in additional possibilities compared to LoRaWAN, but also induces potentially
more overhead and energy consumption, especially in low traﬃc networks, where
a LoRaWAN-radio could ideally just wake up once an hour and transmit a
message.
As such, TSCH over LoRa and LoRaWAN can be considered two very diﬀerent
solutions, catering two diﬀerent needs, where LoRaWAN is requiring all nodes to
be withing range of the gateway, and has much less overhead, meanwhile TSCH
over LoRa potentially can give stronger guarantees in terms of data arrival
through e.g.
the channel jumping, create wider networks as all nodes don’t
have to be within range, and potentially use several small spreading factor hops
to use less power than a single large-spreading factor hop in LoRaWAN. Also,
in sensing networks with high data creation, the relative overhead of TSCH over
LoRa compared to LoRaWAN is lower than when e.g. only sending one packet
a day.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
A LoRa-radio has been integrated with the Contiki-ng stack, leading to the pos-
sibilities of using TSCH with LoRa, as well as the higher level routing layers and
IPv6 with LoRa devices, which wasn’t possible previously. This gives opportu-
nities, but also has limitations compared to solutions like LoRaWAN in terms
of power-consumption, where TSCH over LoRa is clearly stronger in terms of
either creating a network where all devices are not in range, or in general net-
works with high data throughput, as the overhead will be a relative smaller part
of the setup. The setup was also resistant to a jammed channel.
However, to fully embrace it, further research is needed to reduce the over-
head from 40% of the maximum allowed transmission time of the radio, which
will both enhance the maximum possible bandwidth, increase the number of
spreading factors that can be used, and reduce power consumption, leading to
longer network lifetimes. While this is not usually an issue on 802.15.4-radios,
it becomes an issue with LoRa, as the transmission times are much larger -
see e.g. tables 3.3 and 3.5, with the standard launchpad timeslot and LoRa
timeslot, where it is noted that the max transmission time for a standard radio
is roughly 1/10th of the LoRa radio, and the timeslot length is much, much
shorter. Meanwhile, overhead in terms of data production remains unchanged.
Apart from this, larger-scale experiments than merely single-hop would be useful
to analyse network ﬂow, range and power consumption for networks requiring
