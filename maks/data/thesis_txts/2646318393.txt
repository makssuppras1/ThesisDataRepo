Abstract
For hundreds of years, Lactic Acid Bacteria (LAB) have been used to ferment dairy into products
like yogurt and cheese. LAB from the food production are considered to originate from plant sources,
however, through the historic use, the strains used in milk products have been selected on their ability to
ferment dairy. These LAB, therefore, might not be the optimal LAB strains for fermentation of plant
medium. In this project, samples have been collected from nature in hope of ﬁnding potential LAB for
fermentation of plants in order to make plant-based dairy. A pipeline for screening Whole Genome
Sequencing (WGS) data for potential LAB has been made and is here demonstrated thtough analysis of
the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) LAB strain collection.
This study shows that more diverse sampling could beneﬁt the DTU LAB strain collection since none
of the species in the collection have a closed pan-genome and often a very high Average Nucleotide
Identity (ANI) is present within species. Surprisingly, some of the strains identiﬁed to Leuconostoc
pseudomesenteroides have an ANI of only approximately 92% with the reference genome. They are
possibly a new species within the Leuconostoc genus. Most of the found strains look promising for
plant fermentation as they show a high number of genes within carbohydrate degrading groups needed
for plant degradation. Safety in food applications is ensured since only species which have a Qualiﬁed
Presumption Status (QPS) were selected, and none of the strains had any acquired resistance genes.
In the check of genes for exopolysaccharides (EPS), glutamate and the vitamins B2, B11, and B12,
Lactococcus cremoris, strain DK0315 seems to be the most promising of samples in DTU LAB strain
collection as it shows an EPS cluster and has most of the genes required to produce glutamate and
vitamin B2 and B11.

I hundrede år har mælkesyrebakterier været brugt til at fermentere mælk til mejeriprodukter som f.eks.
ost og yoghurt. Mælkesyrebakterier fra madproduktion regnes for oprindeligt at være isoleret fra planter,
men mælkesyrebakterier er gennem historien blevet selekteret for deres egenskaber til at fermentere
mejeriprodukter. Derfor er de måske ikke de mest optimale stammer til fermentering af plantemedie.
I dette projekt er der derfor indsamlet mælkesyrebakterier fra planter i håb om at ﬁnde potentielle
kandidater til produktion af plantebaserede mejeriprodukter. Her er lavet en pipeline til undersøgelse af
helgenomsekventeringsdata for at ﬁnde potentielle mælkesyrebakterier, og pipelinen er demonstreret
ved en analyse af Danmarks Tekniske Universitets (DTU) mælkesyrebakteriestammesamling.
Analysen viser, at mere diverse lokationer og kilder ved indsamling af prøver kan gavne samlingen,
siden ingen af de fundne arter har et lukket pangenom, og der ofte er en høj gennemsnitlig nukleotid
identitet indenfor arterne. Overraskende nok har nogle af stammerne, som er identiﬁceret til Leuconostoc
pseudomesenteroides, en gennemsnitlig nukleotid identitet på kun 92% mod referencegenomet, og der
er derfor måske tale om en ny art indenfor Leuconostoc-slægten. De ﬂeste stammer ser lovende ud til
plantefermentering, da de viser højt antal af karbohydratnedbrydningsgrupper, og ingen af stammerne ser
ud til at have tilegnede resistensgener. I undersøgelsen af gener for exopolysaccharider (EPS), glutamat
og vitaminerne B2, B11, og B12, viser Lactococcus cremoris stamme DK0315 at være den mest lovende
af stammerne i DTUs mælkesyrebakteriestammesamling, da det tydeligt ses, at den har en EPS gruppe,
og at den har de ﬂeste af de påkrævede gener til produktion af glutamat og vitamin B2 og B11.

1
Introduction
The popularity and need for plant-based foods have been increasing in recent years. Animal agriculture
is a major contributor to climate change and together with the growing world population, the need for
plant-based alternatives is bigger than ever. Livestock are thought to be liable for 18% of greenhouse
gas emissions, and by livestock also having huge land requirements, a change to a more plant-based diet
will reduce the emissions of greenhouse gasses and thereby decrease global warming [1].
Lactic Acid Bacteria (LAB) have a long history of being used to ferment dairy into products such
as yogurt and cheese. LAB from the food production are considered to originate from plant sources,
however, through the historic use, the strains used in milk products have been selected on their ability
to ferment dairy and may, therefore, not be the optimal LAB strains for fermentation of plant medium.
Nature might be an immense unexploited source of LAB with useful properties in food applications for
plant-based foods [2].
1.1
Project Aim
The aim of this project is to process and analyze samples from the DTU LAB strain collection in order
to assess potential strains for fermentation of plant-based dairy products.
The ambition is to make a pipeline that enables rapid screenings of Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS)
data to identify and qualify the DTU LAB strain collection. The strains found should be checked
for contamination and antibiotic resistance as well as compared against reference genomes. Finally,
the strains should be investigated for abilities including texture, vitamins, taste enhancers, and their
degradation of carbohydrates.
The ﬁnal pipeline should be automated and easy to use, to enable future samples to undergo the same
procedure as in this project.
1.2
Lactic Acid Bacteria
LAB are a diverse group of gram-positive, catalase-negative bacteria originally based on their common
carbohydrate metabolism and physiological characteristics. The group contains approximately six
families, 40 genera and a continuously growing number of associated species [3]. Common for all LAB
is the phylum Firmicutes and their low GC%, however, with the presence of 16S data, it was discovered
that the phylogenic clustering of the groups did not correspond to the taxonomy of the time, and the
taxonomic relationships and naming are continuously evaluated [4].
The LAB group includes the industrially relevant genera: Enterococcus, Oenococcus, Lactobacillus,
Lactococcus, Leuconocstoc, Pediococcus, and Streptococcus. These genera come with great diversity in
phenotype, and high ﬂexibility and ease of adaption to new environmental conditions [5]. LAB can be
classiﬁed into specialist or generalist LAB, depending on how diverse environments and conditions the
bacteria can survive and grow in. The typical generalist LAB have a larger and more diverse genome
than the specialist LAB [6, 7]. LAB are commonly found in nutrient-rich niches associated with humans,
animals, plants, and foods, due to their need for complex nutrients [3].

1.3
Plant-based Dairy
Fermented foods have been consumed by humans for millennia, and are still a large part of the human
diet. Two main beneﬁts of fermented foods are the increased shelf-life of otherwise easily spoiled foods
and the diversity of food with different sensory properties [8]. Fermentation of plant material has been
historically important to ensure access to vitamins and ﬁbers during the whole year in climates with
only one growth season, and even with modern food preservation, the popularity of fermented food
remains. New interest in plant-based alternatives is rising due to the awareness of climate beneﬁts,
lactose intolerance, and veganism [9].
LAB have been observed present in almost all fermented plant foods, sometimes in synergy with other
groups of bacteria, yeasts, and fungi. The most important property of LAB are their conservation
properties, made possible by lactic acid, which is one of the main end products of carbohydrate
fermentation. Lactic acid inhibits the growth of other bacteria, while LAB are resistant the low pH
conditions that follows [10]. Furthermore, plant-based food fermented by LAB contributes to nutrients,
vitamins, aroma, taste, and texture in plant-based dairy production [9, 11].
As LAB are naturally found on plants, they are expected to have high potential in the fermentation of
plant-based dairy. When collecting new LAB that are to be used in the fermentation of plant products, it
is beneﬁcial to look in nature, as these species are already adapted to grow well in plant environments
[9]. However, the diversity between LAB species and their genes is still thought to be highly unexploited
and to have great potential for optimization of plant-based dairy production [6].
1.3.1
Raw Plant Material
The fermentation type, and hence carbohydrate utilization, is one of the ﬁrst step to look into when
optimizing the fermentation process of LAB employed in food production. LAB can grow on different
sources of carbohydrates and they can exhibit different pathways of carbohydrate fermentation [12]. In
the search for plant-based alternatives to dairy products, the options for raw plant material are plenty
including a variety of cereals, legumes, seeds, and nuts. The raw material choice depends on multiple
factors such as availability of the material, cost, and how well the aroma, taste, and texture the substrates
are able to create [13].
In the Nordic countries, cereals such as oat and barley are of special interest as a starting raw material be-
cause of the high availability and a low cost [14]. In these cereals oligosaccharides such as maltodextrin
predominate. Maltodextrins are formed during the degradation of starches and can for some bacteria be
used as a carbon and energy source [13]. The catabolism of maltodextrin varies a lot in different bacteria,
but usually includes three different enzymatic processes: a-glucosidases, maltose phosphorylases or
maltodextrin phosphorylases, and in most bacteria, 6-phospho-a-glucosidases [11, 15].
It is therefore of high interest to identify and predict the enzymes that modify and cleave the carbohy-
drates which the raw material contains, since knowing the genes LAB are carrying, is making it possible
to understand which carbohydrates they might be able to degrade [12].
CAZymes is a classiﬁcation of Carbohydrate-Active enzymes and consist of enzymes involved in the
synthesis, metabolism, and recognition of complex carbohydrates including monosaccharides, oligosac-
charides, and polysaccharides. Plant-degrading bacteria are particularly abundant in CAZymes genes,

==PAGE:11==
which can degrade these carbohydrates into nutrients [16]. This biotransformation of carbohydrates by
bacteria can be exploited for the degradation of raw food materials into more desired metabolites [6].
CAZymes consists of the classes Glycoside Hydrolases (GH), Glycosyl Transferases (GT), Polysaccha-
ride Lyases (PL), Carbohydrate Esterases (CE), Auxiliary Activities (AA), and Carbohydrate-Binding
Modules (CBM). High interest is in GHs which modify and cleave carbohydrates and GTs which are
involved in their biosynthesis [12].
1.3.2
Texture
Texture properties in plant-based dairy are crucial for new plant-based dairy products to be an acceptable
alternative to their animal-based counterparts. In general thickness and creaminess is preferred attributes
of yogurt, while undesired properties are thinness and wateriness [17].
Exopolysaccharides (EPS) consists of a large number of monosaccharides bound together and are
characterized by a high molecular weight [18]. EPS are produced by bacteria as an external protective
layer, also known as bioﬁlm, and it is important for the attachment of the bacteria to surfaces. With this
bioﬁlm layer, toxins and antibodies can not reach the bacteria giving the bacteria an advantage.
EPS has been proven to bind water, and high production of EPS is correlated with a slimy texture but is
also dependent on a lot of other factors such as the interaction with milk protein [19]. Secreted EPS can
create desirable texture properties for plant dairy products giving properties such as improving viscosity
and creaminess, and can replace added thickeners in plant-based dairy products [19, 11].
EPS genes in LAB are typically organized in a cluster as found in the well-examined EPS cluster in a
plasmid of a Lactococcus lactis subsp. cremoris [20]. However, the EPS gene cluster and nucleotide
sequences are highly diverse in LAB species and the nomenclature designating the genes in EPS clusters
differs among organisms [18]. Capsular Polysaccharides (CPS) is a subgroup of EPS and differ from
EPS by having a higher degree of attachment to the cell surface. CPS has been found to have higher
water-binding properties than EPS, and is especially found useful in cheese production [18].
1.3.3
Flavor
Creating end-products with desirable taste is challenging, as the perception of ﬂavors is different in
individuals, and especially in different regions and cultures. What is perceived as a quality in one region,
might be perceived as repulsive in another [21]. Fermented dairy products have a unique ﬂavor that is
often desired to be recreated in the plant-based dairy alternatives to ease the transition for consumers by
it being highly familiar products [21, 22].
The main components of milk include lactose, citrate, milk fat, and casein which in the fermentation
process releases esters, amino acids, and lipids that all are a part of the ﬂavor formation in dairy products
[23]. However, the compounds found in dairy fermentation are in general not developed in plant-based
dairy fermentation [22]. The natural ﬂavors in milk are different from those found in raw plant substrates,
which instead can contain possibly unwanted ﬂavors that can be hard to camouﬂage. Cereal-based
dairy from raw materials such as oats often come with a "starchy" off-ﬂavor, however, improvement of
these off-ﬂavors are possible, as seen in experiments on a oat-based milk substitute where higher levels
3
==PAGE:12==
of lactic acid generated a better ﬂavor [24]. Furthermore, bitterness is known to be considered as an
undesirable ﬂavor, and to relieve bitterness from the fermentation, glutamic acid is a widely used agent
[21].
Glutamate
Glutamic acid was discovered in 1866 as an enhancing of the taste of food, and it has been discovered
that it is possible to produce glutamic acid by microbiological methods. Glutamic acid has since been
used as a ﬂavor enhancer and add an umami taste. Glutamic acid is naturally found in a variety of
fermented dairy including cheese [25].
Glutamate is naturally found in all living cells, mostly bound in proteins, and is used by almost all
organisms in the biosynthesis of proteins. However, only the free glutamate not bound to other amino
acids add to the ﬂavor of foods [25]. LAB are in general characterized by their high nutritional
requirements and therefore are usually grown on complex media such as MRS or M17.
LAB requires certain amino acids and most LAB species have a nutritional requirement for glutamic
acid. However, few LAB strains have been found to grow on glutamic acid lacking media by the
single-omission technique. One major enzyme is responsible for the ﬁnal synthesis of glutamate in
bacteria: glutamate synthase (GOGAT), which consists of two subunits coded by the genes gltA and gltB
[26, 27].
1.3.4
B-group Vitamins
Vitamins cannot be synthesized at all or in not sufﬁcient quantities in humans, and therefore must be
obtained through the diet. B-group vitamins are found in highest amount in animal products such as
meat, eggs, and dairy products. Plant-based dairy alternatives generally contain no or low quantity
of vitamins compared to their animal origin counterparts. A risk of deﬁciency of certain vitamins is
therefore present in completely plant-based diets, and a challenge to naturally include these vitamins in
plant-based products is present [28].
LAB are in general auxotrophic for most vitamins, but several studies have shown that some LAB can
produce vitamins from the B-group, comprising of riboﬂavin (B2), folate (B11), and cobalamin (B12)
[29].
Riboﬂavin (B2 vitamin)
B2 vitamin is required by the human body for cellular metabolism and is responsible for normal
development, reproduction, lactation, and general physical well-being [30, 31].
The riboﬂavin biosynthesis in LAB have been found to be species-speciﬁc and in some cases strain-
speciﬁc. The presence of riboﬂavin biosynthesis genes has in earlier studies been found in strains of
Lactococcus lactis subsp. cremoris, Lactococcus lactis subsp. lactis, Leuconostoc mesenteroides subsp.
mesenteroides, Lactobacillus brevis, and Lactobacillus plantarum [30].
The enzymatic pathway are encoded by the four genes ribG, ribB, ribA and ribH, which catalyse the
biosynthesis of riboﬂavin from guanosine triphosphate and ribulose-5-phosphate.

1.4
Safety of LAB
When introducing live bacteria in fermented foods, it is important to ensure safety. LAB have in rare
cases been reported to cause infections such as endocarditis, bacteremia, and urinary tract infections,
however, these cases are mostly strains from the genus Enterococcus, which are known to carry virulence
factors [35, 36, 37, 38].
When infections of non-Enterococcus LAB occur, it is only when LAB act as an opportunistic pathogen
in immunocompromised hosts [35].
Ingestion of LAB through fermented food might even function as probiotics to promote health, as
LAB have been shown to positively inﬂuence the immune system, and their presence in the respiratory,
gastrointestinal, and genital tracts might act as prevention and control of infections [39].
Because of its many beneﬁts of being used in food, LAB have a long history of usage in starter cultures

Antibiotic Resistance
Antibiotic resistance is thought to be one of the greatest health challenges in the future and seems to
only be an increasing issue in the treatment of infectious diseases. Antibiotic resistance is the cause of
increased medical costs, prolonged hospital stays, increased mortality, and giving rise to more and more
infections that are difﬁcult or impossible to treat [41].
Two types of antibiotic resistance exist: intrinsic resistance and acquired resistance genes. Intrinsic
resistance includes modiﬁcations of the target site of the antimicrobial agent and a decrease in antimi-
crobial absorption. This resistance occurs due to point mutations in the chromosome, and has a very
minimum potential to be transferred to other genera, which mean they represent a very low risk within
the non-pathogenic LAB [39].
In contrast, acquired resistance is considered serious a public health problem. If acquired resistance
genes are found in LAB, it may not constitute a safety concern, but LAB might act as reservoirs for these
genes, and through horizontal transfer transmission by e.g. transduction or through plasmid exchange,
resistance from LAB can be spread to pathogens and presents a severe safety issue [39].
LAB in fermented food is a major way for living bacteria to enter the human body and come in close
contact with pathogens. Even though LAB are considered GRAS, it has been shown that some LAB have
acquired antibiotic resistance genes. Therefore it is crucial to check if new LAB used for plant-based
dairy production contains any resistance genes [39, 42].

1.5
Bioinformatics
The properties of new potential LAB starter cultures need to be investigated to ensure the absence of
undesired properties such as antimicrobial resistance and to identify desired properties such as genes
for texture, taste, vitamins, and degradation of speciﬁc carbohydrates. A characterization of this sort is
possible with genome sequencing including an extensive bioinformatic analysis, which could unfold
these properties of LAB, which are to be used in the improvement of plant-based dairy products.
1.5.1
Whole Genome Sequencing
DNA sequencing is a way of determining the DNA sequence of an organism and started in the 1970s
and 1980s with Sanger sequencing. DNA sequencing has rapidly become easier and faster and Whole
Genome Sequencing (WGS) is now possible. WGS is when the entire genetic information of an
isolation is sequenced, which otherwise usually requires multiple and time-consuming experiments in
the laboratory to partially cover. In bacteria the genetic information includes chromosomal and plasmid
DNA, and this information can among other things, be used for species identiﬁcation, identiﬁcation of
relevant genes and pathways, and obtaining phylogenetic relatedness [43].
There has been a history of desire to characterize the genetic features of LAB. It was therefore already in
the initial period of WGS that full LAB genomes were published, with the ﬁrst one being Lactococcus
lactis ssp. lactis strain IL1403 in 2001 with Lactobacillus plantarum strain WCFS1. Lactobacillus
johnsonii strain NCC533, and Lactobacillus acidophilus strain NCFM followed shortly in the years after
[44, 45].
The possibilities of WGS were extended with Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) which is high
throughput at a relatively low cost and allows the full genome of an isolate to be sequenced at once. The
advancement of this rapid sequencing method has accelerated biological research and discovery and
has been crucial in the sequencing of countless complete genomes, of which many are now publicly
available. During the earliest time of NGS between 2006 and 2019, the number of full genomes of LAB
exploded to more than 23,000 publicly available at GenBank [45]. However, one of the disadvantages of
NGS is that this rapid increase of genomes comes with the tendency for released draft genomes of low
quality, making it difﬁcult to do complete analysis [45].
NGS is mainly dominated by Illumina short-read sequencing, but other platforms include long read
formats such as Nanopore and Paciﬁc Biosciences [43].
Illumina obtains the full genome sequencing by fragmenting the genome into small sequences, which
later can be assembled in silico. The Illumina sequencing workﬂow is visualized in Figure 6.

1.5.2
Quality Control
Quality control of the sequencing data is important to ensure the preceding analysis is based on reliable
data. Adaptors added for sequencing need to be removed since these are not part of the original DNA
and do not contain any biological meaning for the sample.
The data in FASTQ format have an encoded quality score for each base sequenced, and this quality is
contained a Phred score. It is important to trim the ends of the reads based on this quality. The quality in
Phred score (Q) is logarithmically related to the base-calling probability of error (P) by the formula:
Q = −10·log10(P)
(1)
A Phred score of 20 is often used, corresponding to a probability of error of a maximum of 1% [47].

the average number of times that each position in the sequenced genome is covered by a base read and
therefore relies on the number of reads. A high amount of reads is desired, to make sure that the genome
in the sample is covered multiple times, which ensure a high accuracy assembly, and that all genome
information is obtained [47].

1.5.3
Sequence Alignment
Sequence alignment is a way of identifying regions of similarity between two sequences and is a funda-
mental tool in bioinformatics. This alignment can reveal functional, structural, and evolutionary traits
between the aligned sequences. Sequence alignment is therefore an optimal method when investigating
the genetic information of LAB for useful properties. Alignment is especially very useful when the
properties you are looking for has a well-known and conserved sequence reference [51].
The sequence alignment approach relies on the alignment of a query and reference sequences such
as a database of sequences of genes of interest against either raw reads or a genome assembly [51].
Alignment tools are numerous but include BLAST and KMA [52].
BLAST ﬁnds regions of local similarity between a query sequence and a reference. BLAST is one of
the oldest and most widely used tools available for sequence alignment, however, due to speed issues
when handling big data sets, BLAST remains too slow to align raw reads against large databases in a
reasonable time frame. BLAST is therefore most useful if an assembly of the reads has been made,
reducing the data size signiﬁcantly. This method is highly dependent on the quality of the assembly [52].
KMA is an alignment method using k-mer alignment. K-mer refers to overlapping subsequences, which
a read can be split into with a length of k. KMA is a useful tool for rapid and high accuracy alignment of
raw sequence reads against large redundant databases. KMA ﬁnds matching k-mers between a query
sequence and templates from a selected database [52].
Other alignment methods include the probabilistic Hidden Markov Model (HMM), which creates a
frequency distribution of each residue in a state, while insertions and deletions are represented by other
states. In this way, HMM proﬁles for biological sequences can be created and includes information about
which residues are most likely to differ and their probability of containing an insertion or a deletion.
This is in contrast to BLAST, which penalizes equally on positions in an alignment. HMMER3 is a tool
that exploits this model, and can be used to ﬁnd homologous protein or nucleotide sequences [53].

1.5.4
Genome Assembly
Raw reads from sequencing can be assembled into long stretches of DNA representing the genome of
the isolated LAB sample. There are two major ways to do assembly of raw reads; either read mapping
based on a reference genome or through de novo assembly. Assembly with read mapping based on a
reference genome requires an appropriate reference genome, and genomic regions not present in the
reference will not be included. This leads to a high chance of missing out on accessory genes such as
antibiotic resistance or other novel genes. Therefore de novo assembly is often preferred [47, 54].
The de novo assembly is based on identifying shared regions of overlapping reads, which are then pieced
together into contiguous sequences of DNA called contigs. Commonly used algorithms for assembly of
short-read sequencing are based on the de Bruijn graph approach, as illustrated in Figure 7. In the de
Bruijn approach, reads are splits into k-mers, and two k-mers are connected in the de Bruijn graph if
they completely overlap except for one nucleotide at each end. By going through the optimal path in the
graph crossing each edge once, the de Bruijn graph is transformed into longer assemblies [55]. The ﬁnal
graph can then be illustrated in programs such as Bandage [54].
(a) K-mers created from a longer sequence
(b) A de Bruijn graph made from k-mers

split into k-mers, here illustrated with k=4. In Figure 7b the de Bruijn graph is illustrated, where all k-mers are
connected if they are overlapping except for one nucleotide. Figure adapted from Sohn et al. [56].
The length of k-mer chosen is critical for the construction of the de Bruijn graph, and k is chosen
depending on the quality and length of the reads. If k is too small, repeats in the genome collapse
creating a more tangled graph, while too high k values will result in more fragmented graphs due to
failure of identifying overlaps in the reads. However, newer assembly tools such as SPAdes, employs
multiple values of k-mers [55].
LAB have, like most bacterial genomes, an abundance of repetitive regions in their DNA, which is a
challenge in the creation of the assembly. So even though theoretically, the de Bruijn could give a perfect
graph resembling the genome, due to these repeats and too short reads to cover the repeat areas, the
result in the ﬁnal graph for LAB that should be expected in short-read sequencing such as Illumina is
with overlaps, "bubbles" and separated contigs. Assembly problems can also happen due to low quality
or quantity of sequencing, or contamination issues [57].

To evaluate the quality of a de novo assembly, the N50 value and number of contigs are often used as
measures. N50 is a weighted median of the contig lengths and is deﬁned as the length of the shortest
contig at 50% of the total genome length [57].
Even though the number of contigs and N50 is used in the quality control of an assembly, they are only
estimators of quality, as neither of the measures takes the correctness of the assembly into account.
Assemblies with incorrect linkage can have a very low number of contigs and a high N50, and thus
might look to be of higher quality than those with more correct, but shorter contigs. However, when the
methods and cut-off values are identical in an analysis, they are good estimators of quality [45].
1.5.5
Species Identiﬁcation
One of the ﬁrst questions arising when having isolated and sequenced an organism is what species it is.
The identiﬁcation of LAB is complicated by it being a broad group, with high degree of heterogeneity
between species, and high species richness [58]. Correct identiﬁcation of LAB is important from a safety
perspective, especially when the strains are expected to be used in food [40].
Traditionally, species identiﬁcation is based on morphological data. However, this requires a laborious
characterization and demands a high level of expertise. Currently, identiﬁcation often depends on
technologies that are based on either proteins or nucleic acids, giving rapid and precise results [59].
MALDI-TOF
Matrix-Assisted Laser Desorption/Ionization-Time Of Flight mass spectrometry (MALDI–TOF) is a
method of species identiﬁcation based on the proteins present in the cell. The proteins are ionized and
then accelerated, which separates the proteins based on their mass-to-charge ratio. This creates a Peptide
Mass Fingerprint (PMF) which then can be compared to a database of PMF. MALDI-TOF is quick and
inexpensive, but the result depends on the database available and the resolution can be limited to genus
level [59].
16S rRNA Gene
The 16S rRNA gene is often used as the standard for the classiﬁcation of bacteria, since it is present in
almost all bacteria, and has the property of containing conserved areas combined with variable regions
which give a unique ﬁngerprint. However, disadvantages with this method include that some species
contain several copies of the 16S rRNA gene, a low divergence between species and that the rRNA
16S gene only represents a very small part of the full genome which is a major concern when handling
incomplete or otherwise low-quality genomes [60].
Whole Genome Identiﬁcation
Instead of using only the small 16S rRNA gene, high-throughput genome sequencing and increasing
computational power are becoming available, and it is now possible to exploit the information of the
whole genome of bacteria. This method enables much higher resolution, which can resolve differences
on species and subspecies level.
KmerFinder is a tool that includes whole-genome information. KmerFinder uses k-mers across the
12
==PAGE:21==
entire genome and aligns it to a database of annotated species with KMA. More speciﬁcally, the query
genome and the database are split into 16-mers, but to reduce the size of the data only k-mers that start
with ATGAC are kept. The predicted species for the query genome is the one with the highest number
of 16-mers in common [41, 61].
Another method for whole-genome identiﬁcation is Average Nucleotide Identity (ANI), which is the
calculated mean nucleotide identity of orthologous gene pairs shared between full genomes. ANI can be
used to compare genetic relatedness among closely related microbial genomes and is frequently used in
the deﬁnition of new species [6]. Typically organisms belong to a species if they have ≥95% ANI value
within the species [62, 63].
As WGS did not become widely available until recent years, the experimental counterpart of ANI is
DNA-DNA hybridization (DDH), which traditionally has been used as a measurement for the relatedness
of two species. In DDH, the DNA of the denatured organism of interest is cut into fragments and
added to a denatured reference genome, and they will bind based on similarity. The relatedness can be
measured by the amount of heat needed to be applied before denaturation occurs. The higher identity,
the higher temperature is needed [64].
DDH is still considered the accepted standard for species deﬁnitions, with a value of 70% DDH as a
recommended standard for delineating species. However, DDH values correlate well with ANI values
and it has been concluded by Goris et al. in 2007 that ANI can accurately replace DDH, where the value
of 70% DDH corresponds to an ANI value of 95%. With the decreasing cost and spread of WGS, it has
been propsed that DDH will likely be replaced by ANI as the recommended standard for delineating
species in the near future [64].
ANI is therefore a great method to conﬁrm species identiﬁcation done by other methods, and identify
outliers in a group that might have been misclassiﬁed or even new species that are not present in other
database-dependent tools used for species identiﬁcation [63].
1.5.6
Gene Annotation
Gene annotation is a process of identifying relevant features on a genome sequence and is a way to
adding meaningful information to an assembly and determine potential functions [65]. Gene annotation
identiﬁes the coding regions in a genome and predicts the function of those genes, furthermore, it can
include non-coding RNAs [66].
Gene annotation in LAB can help identify relevant genes and pathways for the improvement of fermen-
tation of plant dairy. This includes genes involved in the carbohydrates they can use as an energy source,
and the possibility of an increase of vitamins, improved taste, and better texture in the food product.
Gene annotation can be divided into three levels. The ﬁrst level consists of identifying the location of
Coding Sequences (CDS) on the genome. The second level extends the locations and aims to determine
which proteins the nucleotide codes and predicts functions. The third level seeks to explore the interac-
tion of these genes in pathways and processes [65].
It is important to note, that not all genome-based predictions are necessarily correct, and the predicted
functions need to be validated experimentally. In gene annotation, false positives can occur due to
13
==PAGE:22==
problems such as similar genes with different functions and non-expressed genes, and false negatives
can occur due to incomplete genomes or missing gene information in the databases. However, gene
annotation still provides crucial insight to guide which samples are the most interesting and to reduce
the number of experiments necessary when having a large number of samples [57].
Automated whole genome annotation can be done with tools such as Prokka [66]. Prokka uses a suite
of existing tools, to achieve as reliable and rich annotation as possible from assemblies. Prokka uses
Prodigal for initial gene prediction of CDS, which are then annotated based on databases hierarchically,
meaning that it starts with small trustworthy databases, to domain-speciﬁc databases, and then moves on
to less speciﬁc databases such as only assigning to a protein family [57]. One of the main advantages of
Prokka compared to other tools is speed, and Prokka is, therefore, suitable in comparative analyses of
large data sets [45]. However, besides using general annotation tools such as Prokka, sometimes more
specialized and curated databases are needed when investigating speciﬁc properties.
1.5.7
Core- and Pan-Genome
The core-genome consists of all the core genes present in a set of genomes, e.g. samples of strains in a
species. By looking at the core-genome, it is possible to gain insight into the most conserved functions
in the species. The gene pool from all species represents the pan-genome and can be used for looking
into the complete genomic reservoir and the gene diversity of a strain collection in a species [41].
To deﬁne the core- and pan-genome, the presence or absence of a gene needs to be deﬁned, which is a
complex problem. A lot of small gene variations are present in a species, and therefore the core- and
pan-genome are most often based on gene families instead of individual genes. For two genes to be
considered in the same family the 50-50 rule is often used, which dictates that at least 50% of the amino
acid needs to be identical over 50% of the length of the longest gene [41].
In practice, the core- and pan-genome can be calculated from automated gene annotation from tools
such as Prokka which output then are used for additional gene orthology in Roary, where the genes are
clustered [66, 67]. Roary employs the tool CD-HIT to rapidly cluster sequences, then performs a more
detailed alignment with BLAST and uses the Markov Cluster Algorithm (MCL) to perform clustering
using the tool OrthoMCL [67].
The core- and pan-genome is also a way of comparing assemblies without using a reference and can be
used for phylogenetic analysis.
1.5.8
Phylogeny
Phylogeny is a way of grouping organisms based on their evolutionary history. It has a long history of
usage in species classiﬁcation and exploration of the taxonomy of organisms.
Phylogeny is often based on aligned sequences in order to generate a measured distance between the
organisms. Until recently it was usually the 16S rRNA gene or other few genes that were used to create
phylogeny, however, with the presence of NGS, whole-genome data has increasingly been used. Whole
genome-based phylogeny gives a much higher resolution which can be used for separation of subspecies
and strains, potentially down to a few nucleotide differences in the genomes [41].
14
==PAGE:23==
Instead of aligning all data from the genome, which might include highly variable regions not present in
all samples, the sequences of the protein families from the core-genome of e.g. a species can be aligned
for inferring phylogeny [68].
Phylogenetic methods are often based on the assumption of linear evolution, however, natural phenomena
such as homologous recombination, gene transfer, gene duplication, and gene loss, violates this and can
lead to errors in phylogenetic trees [68].
Constructing a phylogenetic tree is a statistical estimation problem, and different measures can be used to
approach the true taxonomy as accurately as possible. Often it is estimated as an optimization problem,
and commonly used criteria are:
• Maximum parsimony
• Maximum likelihood
• Minimum evolution
Minimum evolution methods include neighbor-joining. Neighbor-joining performs a greedy search for
the phylogenetic tree of minimal length, by iteratively improving the tree by joining nodes together
based on the distance between them. Meaning that the tree begins as a star topology, and continuously
joins the minimum distance pair of nodes together, until the tree is resolved [68]. By minimizing the
sum of the branch lengths, the tree uses the minimum evolution principle, as it ﬁnds a topology that
minimizes the amount of evolution. While minimum evolution methods are fast and scalable, they are
not as accurate as maximum likelihood methods [69].
Maximum likelihood explicitly models evolution with a transition rate matrix, and the tree with the
highest likelihood is chosen as the tree that best explains the data. This is a complex problem and no
practical method can guarantee that it will ﬁnd the best solution unless all possible options are tried,
which for larger alignments is an enormous search space [69].
FastTree 2 is a tool for inferring phylogeny, which uses neighbor-joining for its initial tree, and then
combines it with maximum likelihood methods to improve the tree, making it an approximately maximum
likelihood method while still being a fast method [69].

2.2
Preparation and DNA Extraction
All collected samples were lightly spun and 1 ml of each sample was transferred to Eppendorf tubes in
dilutions series of 10−1,10−3,10−5 in 0.9% concentration NaCl in water. 100 µl was saved as stock,
transferred to a 900 µl M17 agar plate with 20% glycerol, and frozen at 80 ◦C. 100 µl from each dilution
were plated on MRS and M17 agar plates. Plates were incubated for 48 hours, and then afterward
checked for contamination. Three colonies from each plated sample were clean streaked, repeated three
times.
The samples then underwent a preliminary species identiﬁcation by MALDI-TOF on a Bruker mi-
croﬂex™LT/SH smart benchtop system to select samples for sequencing.
DNA extraction was performed with a 96 well HT kit and DNeasy kit buffers.
2.3
Sequencing
Whole-genome sequencing was performed by paired-end libraries using Illumina NextSeq 500 at the
biotechnology company Novozymes A/S. Ten samples, where one was a co-culture and split into two,
were resequenced on in-house Illumina Nextseq 500. All reads had automatic adaptor removal and
low-quality bases trimmed by Trimmomatic version 0.33.
After DNA sequencing raw reads were obtained in FASTQ format and were uploaded to the supercom-
puter Computerome 2, where all sequential computational work was carried out [70].
2.4
Pipeline Creation
Currently, standard pipelines for genomic screening of LAB for food production was not found and
there is no universal guideline of which genomic properties are the most important for the selection of
LAB for the production of plant-based dairy.
However, a lot of individual tools have been already developed, and in this project, these tools and
databases have been collected for the construction of such an automated pipeline.
The pipeline is freely available and can be downloaded from GitHub with the following command
executed from a terminal:
$ git clone https://github.com/catrinehom/plant_dairy_pipeline.git
New data containing raw reads can be placed under the data directory with a unique project name.When
the data has been added to the ﬁle structure, the following command can be executed:
$ /src/main.sh -n [name of project]
This will execute the whole pipeline on your data, and all results will be generated. The content of the
analysis steps in the pipeline is described in detail in the sections below.

2.5
Quality Control and Genome Assembly
The raw reads from the sequenced samples were sent through FoodQCPipeline version 1.5. FoodQC-
Pipeline is a pipeline that wraps all necessary steps to do quality control and assembly using the tools:
bbduk2 part of the suite bbtools version 36.49 [71], FastQC version 0.11.5 [50], and SPAdes version
3.11.0 [55].
Trimming was done by bbduk2, where bases at the end of the read with a Phred score below 20 were
removed. Furthermore, short reads with a length below 50 base pairs and adaptors were removed.
FastQC checks the quality of sequencing by using statistics to show if there were any problems and
generates a report. Based on this report, samples with less than 500,000 reads and samples with lower
than 50% of percent quality bases after trimming were discarded for further analysis.
The reads of each sample were de novo assembled by SPAdes using multiple values of k (21, 33, 55, 77,
and 99) which is optimized for Illumina reads, using a careful setting that tries to reduce the number
of mismatches and short indels, and coverage cutoff option of 2.0. Contigs below 500 base pairs were
excluded.
QUAST version 4.5 was used to check assembly metrics [72].
Samples with an N50 less than 50,000 and samples with the number of contigs higher than 150 were
discarded for further analysis.
Bandage version 0.8.1 was used to visualize the assemblies, generating an assembly plot of all samples
[54].
2.6
Species Identiﬁcation
Besides the preliminary species identiﬁcation by MALDI-TOF, the trimmed reads went into the whole-
genome identiﬁcation tool KmerFinder version 3.0.2 with the KmerFinder database version 20200816
[61], and SpeciesFinder version 2.0 for identiﬁcation via SSU (16S/18S) with the SILVA database
version 138 SSU [41, 73].
The top hit for each method was selected, and a manual curation took place to see if lower hits indicated
contamination of a sample.
2.7
Average Nucleotide Identity
Whole-genome Average Nucleotide Identity (ANI) was done with FastANI version 1.1 [63] for the
samples separated on genus level and corresponding references using the “many to many” method. The
“–matrix” option was used, to obtain a phylip-formatted lower triangular matrix. FastANI is limited to
ANI values which are over 80%, and values much lower than this will not be calculated. Some missing
data is therefore expected for inter-species comparisons when high diversity is present within a genus.

2.8
Gene Annotation and Clustering
All samples were gene annotated using Prokka version 1.14.0 including an estimation of the number
of tRNA and rRNA genes [66]. Prokka uses Prodigal version 2.6.3 for identifying potential coding
sequences, and BLAST+ version 2.8.1+ and HMMER3 version 3.2.1 [53] for gene prediction using
several databases [66].
To avoid possible biases in the analysis from the references from NCBI due to the different annotation
pipelines, all of the reference assemblies were re-annotated using Prokka.
Prokka was used for general annotation, while when being in the possession of domain-speciﬁc knowl-
edge, manually curated databases can be used. Various of these kinds of databases were used as well in
this project, as seen below.
2.8.1
Resistance Genes
For identiﬁcation of antimicrobial resistance genes, the tool ResFinder version 4.0 was used [41].
ResFinder uses KMA for alignment of reads against a database of currently known resistance genes [52].
The database in ResFinder includes acquired anti-microbial resistance genes for the drug classes:
fusidicacid, phenicol, glycopeptide, trimethoprim, oxazolidinone, tetracycline, quinolone, nitroimidazole,
fosfomycin, aminoglycoside, macrolide, sulphonamide, rifampicin, colistin, and beta-lactam.
2.8.2
Carbohydrate-Active Enzymes
CAZyme genes were predicted using dbCAN version 2.0.11 [16]. dbCAN uses the CAZy database,

10−102 and Hotpep with the option frequency over 2.6. SignalP version 4.1c is also included to predict
the proteins containing signal sequences.
An enzyme group was considered present in a sample if two of the three above mentioned tools predicted
it.

4
Discussion
4.1
Automated Screening of Samples
The creation of an automated pipeline is a challenging task. The human brain naturally recognizes
patterns and catches abnormalities, while a computer program needs to be told exactly what to look for.
However, a good pipeline ensures reproducibility, a streamlined data analysis for all samples, and does
not require attention while running, and is thus often a help for processing big data sets.
All parameters and the cut-off used in the pipeline were estimated based on only the DTU-LAB data set.
Even though it contained 400 samples with some diversity, the samples might not be representative for
all LAB data sets since data collection, sample preparation, and sequencing is not always done with the
exact same methods. The parameters in this pipeline are therefore likely overﬁtted to the DTU LAB
data set. However, if tested on more diverse data, this could be corrected, and a fully automated analysis
would be possible for analysis of future data sets.
The pipeline created in this project is aimed to be a general pipeline to assess potential strains for
fermentation of plant-based dairy products. Gene databases for investigation of texture, vitamins, taste
enhancers, and their degradation of carbohydrates are included since these were deemed the most
important properties in plant-based dairy fermentation. However, different databases could be included
to investigate other properties, and the databases could be updated when new information and sequences
are obtained. It is a great challenge is to create trustworthy databases that are representative of all LAB
species and include all the different pathways possible for the same end product. In this project, the
pathways for glutamate and the B-group vitamins were each based on a few papers. When diving deep
into the literature other pathways might be found, and modiﬁcations of pathways and shortcuts might be
found in different species.
The pipeline also comes with its limitations. Most contamination issues should be apparent with a
low-quality assembly, and will thus be caught by the thresholds for contigs and N50, but a manual
inspection of the data should also be conducted. An actual contamination step in the pipeline could be
implemented to make it more robust. Another limitation is that LAB are known to contain up to multiple
plasmids [76], however, the plasmids were hard to recover in this project, most likely due to the data
consisting of short reads, and an investigation of plasmids was therefore not included in the pipeline.
Only QPS samples are chosen for further analysis, since these species are already approved for food
production, meaning that the strains found interesting are ready to be used in production. However,
by excluding the rest of the samples, only already well-examined species were included, and a lot of
interesting properties in other more novel species might be lost.
4.2
Assuring Quality of Data
A total of 136 samples were removed in quality control due to sequencing or assembly issues, while ten
samples with low quality were clean-streaked and re-sequenced. Quality issues in sequencing could in a
very few cases be correlated with too low sequencing depth under 1 million reads as seen in Figure 12,
but most seem to deem from contamination issues, which in most cases were caught by the threshold for
contigs and N50 in assemblies.
38
==PAGE:47==
The cut off at 150 contigs and an N50 of 50,000 bases were chosen manually by looking at the data to
see where samples started to look suspicious of contamination or other quality issues. The suspected
contamination was based on investigating on three measures. Firstly, the KmerFinder results were looked
into, and if high hits in genome coverage of other genera were present, it was a major concern. Secondly,
the obtained genome size and GC% were compared with the species of the predicted reference genome,
to see if they were consistent. Lastly, when looking into the gene proﬁles of the species very irregular
patterns within species was looked into.
One sample could not be eliminated by the number of contigs or N50 but was obviously contaminated
when looking at the three above-mentioned measures, and this sample was therefore manually removed.
The quality control of the raw data helped to quickly identify poor-quality samples and identifying data
issues. By executing the quality control ﬁrst, it saved a great amount of time in the later analysis.
4.3
Achieving Reliable Identiﬁcation
Correct species identiﬁcation is especially important when the species is to be used in food production.
KmerFinder and MALDI-TOF seem to have a robust performance since they found close to identical
results even on species level on data in this project while being two completely different technologies.
KmerFinder and MALDI-TOF only disagreed on two samples on species level. In both cases, MALDI-
TOF found yeasts (Apiotrichum loubieri and Rhodotorula mucilaginos) while KmerFinder reported
LAB with the highest k-mer identity score, but also had a hit if these yeasts, however, with a lower score
and with a low coverage. This implies that a possible small contamination of yeasts might be present
in these two samples (DK0576 and DK0098), and results from these two samples should therefore be
taken with caution.
The third method used in this project, SpeciesFinder, disagreed repeatedly with the two other methods.
The low resolution of only looking at the single 16S rRNA gene, hold together with too low quality of
the sequenced data to look at SNPs differences might explain the disunity with the other methods.
Differences in prediction can furthermore be explained by the methods using separate databases each
with its own resolution, quantity, and quality.
By reason of SpeciesFinder having a lower resolution, and the species predicted by KmerFinder was
conﬁrmed by MALDI-TOF, KmerFinder were used as a base for the further analysis in the project. This
is supported by other benchmarking studies that report KmerFinder to have the best overall performance
of genome-based methods [77].
The phylogenic and ANI analysis further support the species predicted by KmerFinder and gives integrity
to the results.
4.4
Safety Aspects of LAB in Food Production
A safety concern for LAB, which are to be used in food, are that some have been shown to carry acquired
resistance genes, and by HTG they can pass on the genes to pathogenic organisms, making the pathogen
more difﬁcult to treat [78, 79]. All samples thus underwent an investigation of acquired resistance
genes, and none of the LAB in this study was found to have any. This result might suggest that acquired
39
==PAGE:48==
resistance is not as often found in nature, but more samples and a larger comparison study is needed to
conclude this.
It has been reported that some LAB genera have a natural resistance to bacitracin, vancomycin,
kanamycin, teicoplanin, and quinolones. This intrinsic resistance is less of a safety concern since
intrinsic resistance is very rarely transferred to other organisms compared to acquired resistance genes
[39]. EFSA states that intrinsic resistance from the species in the QPS list can be tolerated for LAB in
food production [40]. Intrinsic resistance is therefore not studied further in this project.
All LAB in this study are therefore considered safe to be used in food products since they have been
identiﬁed at species level as a species with QPS status and no acquired resistance genes were found.
4.5
Exploring the Diversity of the Species
A pan-genome analysis is a powerful method to effectively represent all genomic features for a group of
samples, while the core-genome reveals the conserved functions. This can reveal the natural diversity
found in nature, which potentially can be exploited in food production. Six of the ten species found
had too few samples and was not meaningful to include these species in a core- and pan-genome
analysis. Even Leuconostoc pseudomesenteroides and Lactococcus lactis, which had 14 and 26 samples
respectively, did not have a closed pan- nor core-genome.
Lactococcus lactis non-closed pan-genome of 7300 is near the closed 7800 pan-genome found in the
extensive study of Wels et al. from 2019. which used 44 samples from very diverse isolation sources
including dairy and non-dairy source [11]. The additional pan genes in their study can therefore be
presumed to be dairy-related. The pan-genome of the L. lactis in this study is even larger than a study of
6900 genes from an essentially closed pan-genone found by Kelleher et al. from 2017 [80]. The lower
number of pan genes by Kelleher et al. can be explained by their exclusion of plasmid genes, which is
incorporated in this study.
Even though the pan-genome is not closed for L. lactis in this study, it is still by far the largest pan-
genome of the ten species investigated and the size of the pan-genome is predicted to continue to expand
substantially if adding more genomes beyond the 25 found in this study.
When looking at the phylogeny of L. lactis in Figure 38, it can be seen that the subspecies of L. lactis
subsp. cremoris, L. lactis subsp. lactis and L. lactis tructae are very distinct compared to inter subspecies
differences, and it can be argued that the L. lactis have a broad species deﬁnition. This is also conﬁrmed
with the ANI results where the proposed L. lactis subsp. cremoris DK0315 have an 87.8560% ANI or
less with the L. lactis subsp. lactis cluster, where 95% is usually the cut-off for strains to belong to the
same species [62]. These observations support a proposed revision of Lactococcus by Cavanagh et al,
who recommend a separation of the subspecies L. lactis subsp. cremoris and L. lactis subsp. lactis into
separate species [81].
Pentococcus pentosaceus stagnated at a core genome around 1390 genes, which is comparable but a bit
higher than found in another P. pentosaceus study by Jiang et al. from 2020, where they found 1240
core genes in a closed core-genome [82]. The samples in this study approximated a closed pan-genome,
which was also expected with the high similarity found in the collected samples from the ANI analysis of
a minimum of 98.1283% ANI. However, Jiang et al. found the much higher number of 7938 genes in a
40
==PAGE:49==
non-closed pan-genome of Pentococcus pentosaceus, compared to the around 3500 found in this project.
This substantial difference can be explained by their higher number of samples (73) and more diverse
isolation sources which besides fermented vegetables also include human feces and dairy products. This
conﬁrms that a closed core- and pan-genome only tells about diversity in the strain collection of the
species, which can be limited in location and source diversity, and does not necessarily tells the full story
of the total diversity for the whole species. The smaller pan-genome in this study compared to Jiang et
al. is suggesting there is much more diversity to be found within the P. pentosaceus species and for full
analysis of the pan-genome of in P. pentosaceus, more diverse isolation sources are therefore needed.
For the most sampled species Leuconostoc mesenteroides, an approximately closed core-genome of
1310 was found with a pan-genome of 5006 genes. In 2017, Chun et al. found 999 genes in a closed
core-genome for this species, and 3185 in an approximately closed pan-genome, while only having 17
samples compared to the 77 samples in this study. This indicates that their samples are more diverse, but
they still have a smaller pan-genome due to fewer samples. The high core-genome in this project, even
with the high number of 77 samples, indicates very similar and redundant strains.
A general observation for all species is, that some samples had very high ANI identity, which for some
cases could be explained by identical isolation sources indicating that the samples might be clonal.
These proposed clonal samples had in most cases also identical gene properties. ANI can therefore be
used as early identiﬁcation of whether an oversampling is present in the data, and to reduce the number
of samples in the analysis while still keeping the diversity.
4.5.1
Proposed Division of the Lactobacillus Genus
Lactobacillus has been found to be the most diverse of the genera in this study. This was initially seen in
Figure 16 with a genome size within the genus ranging from the smallest L. curvatus with 1,808,042
base pairs to the approximately 70% bigger genome of L. plantarum with 3,072,534 base pairs. Similar
results are found in a larger comparative study by Wassenaar et al. which included 17 different species of
Lactobacillus [83]. They found an even smaller Lactobacillus: L. sanfranciscensis with 1,375,770 base
pairs, while their biggest genome size was also an L. plantarum with a genome of 3,348,625 base pairs.
The high variety within Lactobacillus in this study is also found when looking into the genomic diversity
as seen in the ANI analysis of Lactobacillus in Figure 18. Here ANI values of 80% or below are present
in the genus, which is the limit of where ANI can be calculated, therefore the lower limit of interspecies
identities is unknown. However, from the ﬁgure, two groups appear; L. curvatus and L. sakei forming
one with around 82-83% interspecies identity, and L. brevis and L. plantarum forming a second group,
with an interspecies identity of approximately 79%. If the genus was to be split up into several genera,
interspecies relationships are a great underlying basis.
It has been suggested multiple times to reclassify the Lactobacillus genus, because of the high diversity
found in genotypic properties as also found in this study, but also due to the great differences in
phenotypic properties such as optimal temperatures of growth (2-53◦C), pH ranges for growth (3-8), and
optimal substrates to grow [10]. As recent as 2020, 23 novel genera have been proposed by Zheng et al.
as a new classiﬁcation scheme for Lactobacillus, and in this L. curvatus and L. sakei are proposed in a
new genus together called Latilactobacillus which is supported with our ﬁndings of these two species
clustering in the ANI analysis, while L. brevis and L. plantarum are proposed to have each their own
41
==PAGE:50==
genus becoming Lactiplantibacillus plantarum and Levilactobacillus brevis [4, 84].
4.5.2
Potential New Species in Genus Leuconostoc
One surprising ﬁnding was seen in the ANI clustering and phylogenic tree of Leuconostoc pseu-
domeserentoides. Both these analyses agreed that only one sample was closer to the reference of L.
pseudomeserentoides, while the rest of the 13 samples were distinct, with low ANI between 92.3447%
and 92.6894% similarity to the reference. When looking into the KmerFinder results, the template
coverage to the reference is only 69-70%, and query coverage as low as 51-65%. The genome size
of the 13 samples was between 14.45% and up to 28.48% bigger compared to the reference of L.
pseudomeserentoides, and had a 0.47% to 1.82% lower GC content.
KmerFinder and MALDI-TOF both agree on the L. pseudomeserentoides species identiﬁcation an all
the 13 samples, meaning they do not have a better reference in their database, and KmerFinder does not
show any indication of contamination.
All this point in the direction of something so different, it might be classiﬁed as a new species. When
evaluating a new species, it is recommended to examine both phenotypic, genotypic, and ecological
traits of the strain. This should be done in compliance with the recommended minimum standards
suggested by Mattarelli et al. which are endorsed by the members of the Subcommittee of the Taxonomy
Biﬁdobacterium, Lactobacillus and related organisms of the International Committee on the Systematics
of Prokaryotes (ICSP) [85]. The minimum standards recommend DDH, but DDH might not be necessary,
as described in Section 1.5.5, since the relationship between ANI and DDH has been reported to be
accurate enough to replace DDH when WGS data is available [64, 86]. In conclusion, more evaluations
need to be done, but the results are so far promising towards a new species.
4.6
Gene Properties of LAB
LAB is generally found to have small genomes with the number of predicted protein-coding genes
varying from around 1700 to 2800 [87]. This is also found in this strain collection as seen in Figure 22
with genes numbers varying from 1708 to 2882 in the species. Since bacteria mostly consist of coding
genes, the number of predicted proteins strongly correlates with genome size, and this is also observed
in the Figure for the LAB samples, with a linear R2 value of 0.975.
To ensure that all genes in the samples were captured, the number of contigs was plotted against the
number of genes in Figure 23 for two of the most abundant species: Leuconostoc meserentoides and
Lactococcus lactis. From this plot no correlation between the number of predicted genes and the number
of contigs was present, indicating that all assemblies are of high enough quality to capture all genes.
A bacteria that needs to survive in variable conditions in general needs a more diverse set of genes, and it
can be hypothesized that bacteria with larger genomes display a more generalist lifestyle while bacteria
with small genomes display a more specialized lifestyle. Following this it could be hypothesized from
Figure 23 that Lactobacillus plantarum has a higher capacity of living in variable conditions than e.g.
the Pediococcus pentosaceus species found with a genome size almost twice as large, but tests in the
laboratory is necessary to conclude this.
42
==PAGE:51==
These predicted genes results were generated from Prokka, which is a very general annotation tool,
useful for all prokaryotes. However, 190,889 out of the total 428,563 predicted genes were hypothetical
proteins, corresponding to 44.5%, implicating that a major black box of unknown functions is present in
the data from this analysis. The Prokka results were therefore only used for an overview of the genes,
and for the phylogeny analysis, but when looking into speciﬁc properties of the LAB, speciﬁc databases
were used instead.
4.6.1
Prospect of LAB as Plant Carbohydrate Degrader
In this study, all LAB went into an identiﬁcation and characterization of the CAZymes to investigate the
possibilities of plant carbohydrate degradation. A high conservation of the CAZymes is found within
the species, except some variation seen within the Leuconostoc meserentoides species which seem to
have very different CAZyme proﬁles, and DK0135 Lactococcus lactis subsp. cremoris which have a
very different proﬁle from the rest of the Lactococcus lactis subsp. lactis samples.
Lactococcus lactis was found to be the species with the highest and most diverse number of CAZymes.
It has earlier been found by Wels et al. that a gene loss of GH families is present in dairy originated
strains of Lactococcus lactis with numbers as low as 19 GH families, while plant-derived samples have
up to 45 GH families present [11]. This analysis further supports this with an average number of 36 GH
families found from these plant-derived samples, so it could be proposed that these excess GH families
are likely involved in the metabolism of plant carbohydrates. One of these is GH67 which is only
found in Lactococcus lactis (DK0085, DK0128, DK0132, DK0188, DK0201, DK0214, DK0224, and
DK0315). GH67 is a family involved in the breakdown of xylan, which is a group of hemicelluloses and
is important for the integrity of the plant cell wall, and therefore a potential source of carbohydrate for
these 8 Lactococcus lactis samples. All Lactococcus lactis together with one Lactobacillus plantarum
(DK0019) and one Leuconostoc citreum (DK0092) are the only ones containing GT5 and GT35 which
is glycogen synthase and glycogen phosphorylase. Glycogen is one of the main carbohydrate storage
forms for bacteria and it has been proved that bacteria that can use glycogen can survive and grow in
more diverse environments [6].
While Lactococcus lactis was found to be the most diverse in CAZymes, some of the samples of
Lactobacillus curvatis was found promising when looking into degradation maltodextrin, as seen in
Figure 25. It could be interesting to see if these genomic features could be replicated in the laboratory.
It is important to note that some CAZymes contain multiple functions, and prediction, therefore, does
not always correlate with phenotypes found by experiments.
4.6.2
Texture Properties
EPS is a very highly desired property in LAB for fermentation of plant-based yogurt due to its property
of increasing viscosity and general improvement of texture. Some degree of genes for EPS production
was found in all strains in this project. However, the function of these genes was very hard to retrieve
other than a general involvement of EPS production. However, one well described EPS cluster from a
plasmid from a Lactococcus lactis subsp. cremoris strain, was found in DK0315 and indicates that it
might be a Lactococcus lactis subsp. cremoris, as also found in the ANI and phylogenic analysis, and
43
==PAGE:52==
this sample could contain useful EPS properties that could be exploited in plant yogurt production.
All Lactobacillus plantarum samples had the full gene group for cps4 for CPS production, which is also
the cps group most commonly found in Lactobacillus plantarum [88]. All the gene references for the
CPS groups come from Lactobacillus plantarum from the strains WCFS1, ZJ316, ST-III, and NC8. Even
though the reference for the cps2 group comes from Lactobacillus plantarum, only the Pediococcus
pentosaceus species have hits in the cps2 group. Pediococcus pentosaceus have only cps2ABCD and in
three samples cps2BCD of the group which can consists of cps2ABCDEFGHIJK, however, it is not an
unusual ﬁnd for LAB to only have the ﬁrst genes of this group, at least when looking at Lactobacillus
plantarum strains [88].
CPS production is linked to probiotic properties, such as cholesterol removal in Lactobacillus plantarum
by Wang et al. in 2011, however, this is found in strains with all four CPS clusters from Lactobacillus
plantarum (cps1, cps2, cps3, and cps4) [88], but some possibility is that the Lactobacillus plantarum
and Pediococcus pentosaceus samples might contain these probiotic properties.
To make the pipeline as general as possible and to contain EPS genes for all LAB species, the gene search
for the database in this project was conducted by making a general search for "exopolysaccharides" in
Firmicutes on NCBI. A deeper dive into the literature, looking at speciﬁc pathways for each species
could yield more extensive and precise results with more information about gene functions.
The production of EPS is highly linked to environmental parameters such as substrate availability,
temperature, and pH which highly inﬂuence the expression levels of EPS [45]. The genetic prediction
must be viewed with high skepticism and needs to be followed up with experiments to validate the
results.
4.6.3
Taste Enhancers
Taste is an important factor when creating food products. Glutamate is a possible taste enhancer, often
described as relieving bitterness and adding umami-ﬂavor, and can be used to emphasize natural ﬂavors
in foods, and by so can be a desired property in a plant-based dairy product.
Glutamate synthase has earlier been found experimentally in Leuconostoc mesenteroides and Lactococcus
lactis by Holden et al. in 1959 and Lapujade et al. in 1998 respectively. This is consistent with with the
ﬁndings in this study as seen in Figure 39, where all the Lactococcus lactis and 61 out of 77 Leuconostoc
mesenteroides samples have hits for the two subunits for GOGAT, while one Leuconostoc mesenteroides
have only a hit for gltB.
The database created had fewer sequences for the small subunit gltA of GOGAT compared to the larger
subunit, gltB, due to fewer annotations in NCBI. Therefore only one sequence of L. mesenteroides and
six sequences of Lactococcus lactis are to be found in the database. No reference genes were found
for the rest of the species in this study, which might explain why there are very few hits for them,
and the gene hits for these species are low-coverage hits of either gene references from Lactococcus
lactis or Leuconostoc mesenteroides. No publications of glutamate production in Pediococcus was
found, while some species of Lactobacillus have been reported to contain glutamate synthase gene [89].
It is therefore less likely that samples of Pediococcus are able to synthesize glutamate than it is for
Lactobacillus. Experimental veriﬁcation of the ﬁndings will be interesting to see if the identiﬁed samples

are able to grow in a medium lacking glutamate and the amino acids of the glutamate family. Many
other ﬂavor-enhancing compounds are to be investigated, such as diacetyl, acetaldehyde and acetone
that are known to be generated from alanine by LAB, but this has not been a priority in this project [21].
4.6.4
B-group Vitamins
The ability to synthesize B2 showed promising results for many species that contains all genes in this
pathway. This includes samples from Lactobacillus plantarum, Lactococcus lactis, Leuconostoc citreum
Leuconostoc mesenteroides and Pediococcus pentosaceus. This is consistent with the results found by
three other genomic studies conducted by Capozzi et al. in 2012, Lv et al. in 2014, and Valle et al. in
2014, which combined have found similar results for all these LAB species [30, 90]. The phenotypic
experiments are, however, yet to be conducted. However, Capozzi et al. have shown that even though
some LAB contain the genes necessary for the production of B2, some have an incomplete operon,
which should especially be the case when the gene ribG (EC 3.5.4.26 and EC 1.1.1.193) is missing,
which is the case for some of the samples [30].
Lactococcus lactis have a potential of B11 production with 11 out of 12 of the enzyme groups necessary.
This is consistent with results found by numerous experimental studies of Lactococcus lactis [31].
Lactobacillus plantarum is also a promising candidate with 10 out of the 12 enzyme groups, and strains
of Lactobacillus plantarum have been found by Sybesma et al.[31] to experimentally produce B11,
however in low amount.
The missing hit in the few enzymes might be due to database errors since some enzymes had a limited
number of sequences available and only in distant phylogenetic related organisms.
B12 is a complex vitamin and the production is very unlikely in any of the samples due to very few genes
of the pathways present. This was expected, since Lactobacillus reuteri have been the only species
to earlier have been conﬁrmed to produce B12 [31, 34]. If a B12 is desired in plant-based dairy, it is
therefore recommended to include Lactobacillus reuteri.
B-group vitamins are produced by the LAB for their own use, and might not show up in high quantity in
production. By culturing strains, it might be possible to enhance the production of the wanted products
if the pathways are present in them. The selection of vitamin-producing strains would increase the
nutritional value of the plant-based dairy by having B-group vitamins naturally present in the products,
and consumers could increase their vitamin intake as part of their natural diet. This beneﬁt for the
product is without adding artiﬁcial compounds and thus without increasing the production cost.

5
Conclusion
In this project, an automated pipeline has been created which can perform a screening of LAB samples
for properties useful for fermentation of plant-based dairy products. The pipeline allows future samples
to go through the same analysis as the DTU LAB strain collection. Other bioinformaticians will
hopefully be able to continue with the use and development of the pipeline so that LAB can easily be
screened for even more functionality.
Species identiﬁcation was conducted of the DTU LAB strain collection revealing four genera and ten
species of QPS LAB. Furthermore, a potential novel Leuconostoc species was discovered since it had
low similarity to the closest reference. Nonetheless, more experiments needs to be conducted to conﬁrm
the novelty.
Safety of the LAB strains in food production was ensured since only QPS species were selected and none
of the strains showed results for acquired resistance genes for antibiotics. The diversity of the samples
was investigated through analysis of ANI, phylogeny, and the pan- and core-genomes, showing that
some strains were possibly clonal while others had distinct gene properties. The pan- and core-genome
analysis revealed that much more diversity is waiting in nature.
Diverse carbohydrate degradation properties for plant materials were found between species with
Lactococcus lactis having the most diverse CAZyme groups. Samples in Lactobacillus curvatus showed
the highest potential for maltodextrin degradation, and thus might be suitable for degradation of cereals.
Several strains in different species showed a potential of glutamate, B2 and B11 vitamin production. One
sample which showed the possibilities of all three properties was the proposed Lactococcus lactis subsp.
cremoris sample DK0315. An EPS cluster was also identiﬁed in DK0315 and it is, therefore, a good
candidate for creating texture in plant-based dairy products. For this reason it is recommended to continue
with this sample in the laboratory to see if these properties can be reproduced experimentally. Other
candidates include the 7 samples of Lactobacillus plantarum samples which not only had promising
results for B2 and B11 vitamin, but also showed a cluster for CPS production.
Since none of the current strains in DTU LAB strain collection had the ability of B12 vitamin production,
the collection would gain from the addition of a Lactobacillus reuteri, that so far has been the only LAB
shown to be able to produce B12 vitamin.
A lot of unknowns are present when searching in genomics and phenotypes can not always be conﬁrmed.
The computational methods are still a way to reduce the number of test in the laboratory substantially by
limiting the search to promising candidates found in silico, and thereby enable scientists to faster and
more easily explore the potential of LAB in plant-based dairy.
