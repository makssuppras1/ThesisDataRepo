ABSTRACT
The natural health benefits of the probiotic Escherichia coli Nissle 1917 and its ability to colonize the
intestines have proven the bacteria as an optimal drug delivery vehicle, and by introducing synthetic
genetic circuits that enables it to produce several different therapeutic compounds, e.g. human hormones
related to obesity or similar metabolic diseases, it is possible to design a gut-colonizing bacteria for
delivery of therapeutics in situ.
However, the main challenge is to engineer the bacteria to produce complex eukaryotic proteins with
their native post-translational modifications, e.g. C-terminal amidation. Moreover, production yields are
often constricted because of the mass, hydrophobicity and sophisticated structure of the peptides.
Therefore, this thesis set out to improve functional expression of human hormones in E. coli Nissle 1917.
In order to improve expression of the apolar human hormone leptin, randomized mutations were
introduced in the translational initiation region, and by fusing the leptin CDS to the β-lactamase gene
bla, a simple cell-survival assay on ampicillin enabled selection of clones that expressed higher amounts
of the fusion. To verify the selected clones’ ability to grow in high ampicillin concentrations, and thus
express leptin, they were subsequently subjected to further selection rounds. Lastly, the bla was removed
and the leptin yields were quantified for each of the clones.
The TIR randomization had enabled selection of one clone able to produce ∼46% more functional
leptin than control. This increase in production was profound; however, it was only obtained in aerobic
conditions.
In addition, the alternative signal sequences DsbA and EndoX were examined for their ability to increase
expression of leptin. This revealed that strains expressing the DsbA SP had increased survival on higher
ampicillin concentrations; however, the yields obtained did not correlate with this increase.
Finally, in this thesis, the possibility of amidating the C-terminus of the human hormones obestatin, PYY,
NPY, NMU, NMB and PPP was investigated by expressing the hormones in E. coli Nissle and co-expressing
the human enzyme responsible for amidation – peptidylglycine α-amidating monooxygenase. An alterna-
tive digestion enzyme, Glu-C, was chosen for LC-MS/MS analysis; however, the synthetic peptides applied
as standards were designed to mimic trypsin digested versions of the hormones, but it was not possible to
conduct comparison between the biological samples and the synthetic peptides. Hence, it was inconclu-
sive whether the expressed human hormones had been amidated, and further research is thus needed.

INTRODUCTION
Synthetic biology (SynBio) has emerged as a core scientific discipline which aims to develop novel genetic
circuits that can be utilized in a wide range of applications. In particular, it has enabled biological, and thus
sustainable, production of industrially relevant compounds and therapeutics. Recently, a very promising
usage of SynBio has surged in the scientific community – the application of cell-based medicine1. This spe-
cific field relies on cells as therapeutic entities where the most predominant research has been made within
human stem cell therapies and cancer research. However, in situ synthesis and delivery of theraputics via
bacteria has yet to be realized as treatment of human diseases2.
The current therapeutic applications of bacteria includes fecal transplants and intake of probiotics or pre-
biotics3–7. Indeed, oral ingestion of Lactobacillus spp., E. coli and Bifidobacterium spp. has been clinically
shown effective for treatment of a wide variety of diseases8–11, but a more compelling strategy would be
to engineer gut-colonizing bacteria with the ability to produce therapeutics in a regulated manner – also
known as Advanced Microbiome Therapeutics (AMT).
This could be obtained by introducing synthetic genetic circuits to bacteria such as Escherichia coli Nissle
(EcN) 1917, enabling the bacteria to produce several different therapeutic compounds, e.g.
human
hormones related to obesity or similar metabolic diseases. Gut-colonizing EcN producing therapeutics
would be administered orally, and thus several factors related to drug delivery and administration would
be improved; the required dosage of the therapeutic agent would be reduced by several magnitudes,
whilst maintaining the desired effect of the drug. This particularly regards proteinaceous compounds
that are prone to degradation in the acidic stomach environment.
Furthermore, current treatment of many metabolic diseases require intravenous administration of the
therapeutic, but by developing an orally delivered cell-based medicine, a greater patient compliance
would be obtained and possibly induce fewer adverse effects1.
Recently, promising preclinical results in animal disease models have been obtained after administration
of engineered microbes able to produce cytokines, to inhibit pathogenic bacteria, and to secrete therapeu-
tics designed for site-specific delivery12,13. Moreover, Duan and coworkers successfully engineered EcN
to secrete the insulinotropic hormones GLP-1 and PDX-113.
Nonetheless, it still remains a challenge to engineer AMTs that are able to produce complex eukarytic
proteins with their native post-translational modifications (PTMs), e.g. amidation of the C-terminal
part of the peptide. In addition, the yields are often constricted because of the mass, hydrophobicity and
sophisticated structure of the peptides.
1
==PAGE:9==
Therefore, this project aimed to improve functional expression of selected human hormones in EcN.
More specifically, the attention was on the metabolism-related hormones leptin, obestatin, peptide YY
(PYY), neuropeptide Y (NPY), neuromedin U (NMU), neuromedin B (NMB) and pancreatic polypeptide
(PPP). When mentioning ’metabolism’, it naturally guides the thoughts onto diseases such as diabetes or
obesity, which are often chronic and thus reduces the overall quality of life for the patients14.
These hormones are predominantly synthesized in the brain and in the gut15–17, except for the former,
leptin, which is mainly synthesized in adipose tissues18.
One of the challenges of expressing these
hormones in a prokaryote such as EcN, is that they require PTMs in order to become bioactive. Leptin
requires formation of disulphide bonds and the others require C-terminal amidation. E. coli has the
innate ability to produce disulphide bonds; however, when expressing leptin using this host, inclusion
bodies are formed, as shown by Jeong et al.19.
When searching the literature for amidation of peptides using bacterial hosts, no successful attempts
have been made, and it is thus widely accepted in the scientific community that production of proteins
requiring this PTM needs to be executed in eukaryotes20.
Accordingly, the overarching motivation behind this study is to improve functional expression of
leptin and to successfully amidate the hormones requiring C-terminal amidation.
1.1 Project scope
Having the above-mentioned in mind, this study aims to improve expression of leptin and to enable
amidation of peptide hormones in EcN. Initially, a literature study will be conducted (Figure 1) for
obtaining the required theoretical knowledge on the subject. Moreover, prior experiments performed by
supervisor Mareike Bongers will be reviewed.
The experiments regarding leptin will focus on:
• Co-expression of the hormone with the disulphide bond enzymes dsbA and dsbC;
• Change of the signal peptide for secretion;
• Improve translation initiation rate (TIR).
Upon accomplishment of these steps, the anticipation is that leptin will be produced in greater amounts.
The experiments involving amidation will include:
• Co-expression of the human amidating enzyme peptidylglycine α-amidating monooxygenase
(PAM);
2
==PAGE:10==
• Analyzing the secreted products using LC-MS/MS.
• If no amidation can be detected in vivo:
– Test enzyme activity in cell extracts using synthetic peptides.
Upon completion, the expectancy is that a new method for in vivo amidation of peptides has been
developed.
Lastly, data analysis will be carried out and next steps will be discussed including proposals for
future experiments.

BACKGROUND
This project seeks to augment the natural benefits of probiotics through recombinant expression of
therapeutic biomolecules. The progress within SynBio have enabled the development of AMT, and recent
studies specifically focus on delivering therapeutics using EcN due to its natural health benefits and its
gut-colonizing abilities. Isabella et al. published a study in 2018 in which they demonstrated the great
capacities of AMT by employing their engineered EcN to reduce the blood phenylalanine concentration
in mice and in Cynomolgus monkeys21. People suffering from the genetic disease phenylketonuria (PKU)
are unable to metabolize phenylalanine, which can result in neurotoxicity, and thus this specific AMT
could provide an alternative to a protein-restricted diet.
Other intriguing applications of engineered EcN as potential AMT include treatment of hyperammone-
mia12 and treatment of colorectal cancer22.
Because of the promising results of these studies, and due to the gut-colonizing ability of the probi-
otic, EcN was chosen for improving expression of the selected human hormones of this project.
Challenges of obtaining this and strategies to overcome these challenges will be presented in the following
sections.
2.1 Improving protein expression in prokaryotes
The most common expression system for production of recombinant proteins is prokaryotes, with Bacilli
strains and E. coli leading the field. Predominantly, Bacilli are used for extracellular expression of proteins,
but less is known about their disulphide bond machinery compared to that of E. coli23,24. In addition, E.
coli is easy to manipulate, culturing is inexpensive and generation of recombinant proteins is rapid. But
because it is a prokaryotic based system, it is not natively able to generate all post-translational modifica-
tions (PTMs) frequently seen in eukaryotic proteins – PTMs that are often essential for correct protein
folding and conformation and hence biological function25. In fact, the only PTM relevant for production
of human proteins native to E. coli is disulphide bond formation, obtained by the disulphide bond enzymes
(referred to as Dsb) residing in the periplasm. The environment of the cytoplasm is highly reductive, thus
the nascent polypeptide chain needs to be directed to the periplasm in order to obtain its correct tertiary
conformation. In addition, there are less proteases in the periplasm than in the cytoplasm so the possi-
bility of producing recombinant proteins that are more biologically active increases by directing proteins
there26.
In regards to a protein as leptin, it contains one disluphide bond, and thus, in theory, E. coli’s native ma-
chinery should be adequate for production of the hormone. However, formation of native disulphide
bonds in sophisticated eukaryotic proteins expressed in E. coli often results in the formation of insoluble
4
==PAGE:12==
aggregates in the periplasm19. Moreover, leptin is natively expressed in adipose tissues, and its highly
hydrophobic composition may be related to this matter (Figure 2)18. The hydrophobic nature challenges
functional expression even further.
Figure 2: The human hormone leptin.
The color coding indicates level of hydrophobicity; red: hydrophilic to green: hydrophobic. Image
obtained from the RCSB PDB (rcsb.org) of PDB ID 1AX8 (Zhang et. al, 199727).
Several studies28–30 have shown that upon co-expression of one of the Dsb enzymes, more soluble and
correctly folded proteins are produced. More specifically, the studies investigated DsbA – an enzyme
involved in protein disulphide bond formation and the main thiol oxidant of newly secreted proteins31.
DsbC is another periplasmic oxidoreductase, and its major function is to isomerize incorrect disulphide
bonds32. More recent studies have shown that co-expression of both DsbA and DsbC markedly enhance
soluble and functional expression of proteins in E. coli33.
Accordingly, DsbA and DsbC are, in this study, being co-expressed along with leptin – a construct that
has been engineered and tested prior to initiation of this project, and as seen in Figure 3, co-expression of
the enzymes nearly doubled the leptin titer (ng/ml) for strains cultivated in aerobic conditions.
5
==PAGE:13==
Figure 3: Optimization of leptin production by co-expression of dsbA and dsbC.
Upon aerobic cultivation, a nearly 2-fold increase in leptin titer had been obtained when co-expressing
the two enzymes. The same trend was seen upon anaerobic cultivation, although the increase was not
as pronounced. These results are kindly provided by supervisor Mareike Bongers (unpublished data).
Facilitating secretion of the expressed protein in large amounts is another obstacle of using E. coli as ex-
pression system. As mentioned, the disulphide bonds is formed in the periplasm, thus, the protein must
be targeted here in order for it to attain proper native structure. A way to overcome this obstacle is by
fusing a signal peptide (SP) to the recombinant protein that targets the protein to the periplasm.
2.1.1 Signal peptides
OmpA SP
In the current construct, the outer membrane protein A (OmpA) signal peptide is fused to the N-terminus
of leptin. The OmpA SP utilizes the general secretory (Sec) pathway of E. coli which allows for the nascent
peptide to be secreted to the periplasm. In short, this take place in three stages. During the first stage, the
proteins are sorted and targeted to the Sec translocase; i.e. chaperones guide the nascent polypeptide chain
to the transmembrane channel SecYEG, which is responsible for transport to the periplasm. The second
stage consist of translocation across the plasma membrane; and, finally, the signal peptide is cleaved off
and the protein is released34.
6
==PAGE:14==
This SP has been shown to increase production of several other human hormones when tested by super-
visor Mareike Bongers. However, as seen in Figure 4, leptin was produced in very low amounts when
comparing to the other hormones.
Figure 4: Yield of human hormones aided by the OmpA SP.
Obtained yields of the human hormones FGF19, TFF3, oxyntomodulin, neurotensin and leptin pro-
duced by EcN. Darker colours represent yields obtained from clones grown in oxygen, lighter colours
are yields from clones grown without oxygen. All of the hormones are produced in the range of ∼10-40
ng/ml*OD600, except for leptin, which only had a yield of ∼1-2 ng/ml*OD600. These results are kindly
provided by supervisor Mareike Bongers (unpublished data).
Because of that, this construct serves as a baseline for improving expression of leptin.
DsbA SP
Fusing leptin to the DsbA SP serves as an alternative approach for increasing the production of leptin. The
signal sequence of the DsbA oxidoreductase directs the nascent polypeptide chain to the signal recognition
particle (SRP) export pathway. This pathway is also dependent on the transmembrane channel SecYEG;
however, it is largely co-translational, which provides for the recombinant protein to be translocated across
the membrane simultaneously with translation of the protein, and by that, preventing the formation of
aggregates in the cytoplasm35. The SRP itself binds to highly hydrophobic signal sequences, e.g. DsbA SP,
7
==PAGE:15==
and it most often transports inner membrane proteins and targets these proteins to be embedded in the
inner membrane36. The ability of this SP to improve secretion of leptin will thus be investigated in this
project.
EndoX SP
Another signal sequence that potentially could increase production of leptin is the Bacillus sp. endoxy-
lanase (EndoX) SP. As with the OmpA SP, this signal sequence directs the growing peptide chain to the
periplasm using the Sec machinery. In a study carried out by Jeong et al. in 200019, the researchers applied
the EndoX SP for facilitating secretion of leptin in E. coli. They were able to produce soluble, functional
leptin by co-expression of the enzyme DsbA. However, this was done using the expression strain E. coli
BL21(DE3), which is engineered to produce proteins. Moreover, they performed their cultivations under
aerobic conditions, and thus, use of this SP is to be tested for use anaerobically in this project.
2.1.2 Introducing variability in the translation initiation region
Upon expression of proteins in prokaryotes, translation initiation is often the rate-limiting step. How well
a protein is being expressed depends on the nucleotide sequence of the translation initiation region (TIR),
which includes the Shine-Delgarno (SD) sequence, the region upstream the SD containing the transla-
tional standby site, and a region stretching from the SD to the fifth codon of the protein’s coding sequence
(CDS). Therefore, an interesting way to achieve optimal protein yields is to carry out an in vitro random-
ization of the TIR and experimentally select the sequences governing the highest yields, as done by Rennig
et al.37. Nonetheless, it would be time-consuming and difficult to screen all of the sequences of the gener-
ated libraries, but this bottleneck was resolved by Rennig et al. through fusion of an antibiotic resistance
gene to the CDS. In that way, clones could be selected using a simple cell-survival assay on the respective
antibiotic.
The study investigated translational coupling of different antibiotic resistance genes and found that the
β-lactamase (bla) was the most suitable candidate. Accordingly, a similar approach will be carried out in
this project for increasing expression of leptin, i.e. TIR libraries will be generated and leptin will be fused
with bla.
2.1.3 Amidation of peptides using PAM
Other eukaryotic peptides require more sophisticated PTMs in order to obtain their bioactivity, e.g. C-
terminal amidation, which is essential for the selected hormones of this project (obestatin, PYY, NPY,
NMU, NMB and PPP). The C-terminal amidation is natively generated by the bifunctional enzyme PAM
possessing two catalytic domains in a single polypeptide; peptidylglycine α-hydroxylating monooxygenase
(PHM) and peptidylamidoglycolate lyase (PAL) (Figure 5)38. The PHM domain is responsible for catalyz-
ing the oxygen- and ascorbate-dependent hydroxylation of peptidylglycine, forming the α-hydroxyglycine
intermediate. PAL then cleaves the intermediate and releases the amidated peptide and glyoxylate39.
8

Figure 5: Mechanism of action of PAM.

Several attempts have been made in preparing an α-amidating enzyme by recombinant or extraction sys-

been produced mainly as inclusion bodies, and as a result, refolding processes are required to regain the
activity. Moreover, the E. coli derived enzyme had activities much lower than that of the mammalian
host40,41.
In this project, a codon optimized PAM will be expressed in EcN in the attempt of successfully amidating
selected peptide hormones.

RESULTS
Due to the apolar nature of leptin and hence its inability to be secreted in high enough concentrations,
several approaches were taken in order to improve production of the hormone. By using a plasmid in
which the leptin gene was translationally coupled to the β-lactam resistance gene bla, resistance to ampi-
cillin was used as a readout for secretion of functional protein. Through PCRs, variability was introduced
in the TIR (Figure 7) and libraries were created. The most promising sequences were selected in order to
test for leptin yield.
In addition, two other signal peptides were tested for their ability to aid in enhancing leptin expression.
The other sub project aimed to enable EcN to perform amidation of the C-terminus part of peptides.
To achieve this, the eukaryotic enzyme peptidylglycine α-amidating monooxygenase (PAM) was co-
expressed with human hormones for which amidation is essential for their bioactivity.
3.1 Leptin
To examine the possibility of improving expression of leptin, the experimental setup was divided into two
subgroups, with the first revolving around generation of TIR libraries and the second on examining alter-
native signal peptides for improved secretion. In order to generate the libraries and plasmids encoding
alternative signal peptides, several PCRs were performed using the plasmid pVTSR-03_RBS_SS as tem-
plate (Figure 6).
This template, which was also applied as control throughout the study, encoded human leptin (designated
HH03) translationally coupled to bla. In addition, leptin was N-terminally fused to the signal peptide
OmpA, which was needed for directing the peptide to the periplasm. Because leptin is natively expressed
in humans, the CDS had been codon optimized for expression in E. coli.

Generation of libraries improves translational initiation
The two leptin expression libraries were created by introducing variability in the TIR. This variability was
obtained by applying primers having degenerate bases right upstream of the OmpA SP CDS. Because
the very first codons are key in translational initiation37, mutations were also introduced in the third
nucleotide of the first two codons, allowing base mutations of either A or G. This did not alter the specific
amino acid being translated but instead, it has been shown to be essential for increasing expression of
proteins37. Moreover, the primers had been designed with overhangs to improve cloning efficiency, and
can be seen in Table 11. Note that they were designed prior to initiation of this study.

4
DISCUSSION
This project set out to improve functional expression of selected human hormones in E. coli Nissle 1917.
More specifically, the aim was to improve production of the apolar peptide hormone leptin and to success-
fully amidate obestatin, PYY, NPY, NMU, NMB and PPP.
4.1 Leptin
4.1.1 DsbA SP-expressing strain enables greater survival on higher ampicillin
concentrations
A couple of reports on secretion of leptin in E. coli are found in the literature. Guisez et el. applied the
OmpA SP, but the amount of leptin produced and the efficiency of secretion were not satisfactory. In fact,
the leptin remained in the periplasm, meaning that the protein had to be recovered by exposing the cells
to osmotic shock47. This recovery method is common for periplasmic protein expression, and is preferred
over cell lysis, which contaminates the purification process with cytoplasmic proteins48.
Nonetheless, the OmpA SP had previously been effective in aiding production of several other human
hormones, as seen in Figure 4, whereas secretion of leptin was not optimal. Thus, it was expected that the
OmpA was not a preferable SP, and was therefore applied as control.
A phage display study carried out by Steiner et al. showed that direction of proteins to the SRP pathway
using the DsbA SP instead of SPs utilizing the Sec pathway, yielded a >100-fold enrichment per phage dis-
play enrichment round49. In another study, they compared different SPs for recombinant expression of a
protein in E. coli, and again, the DsbA SP induced the highest level of secreted product50. Accordingly, the
obtained increased survival of the DsbA SP-expressing strain was expected, and the results are concurrent
with the literature.
By applying the EndoX SP, Jeong et al. generated an E. coli strain that was able to secrete functional lep-
tin in an amount of up to 26% of total extracellular proteins. However, the remainder of leptin was not
properly folded and formed insoluble aggregates19. The challenge of producing soluble proteins has been
investigated by many researchers, and co-expression of the oxireductase dsbA has been shown to reduce
the amount of insoluble proteins30,51. However, up until the study performed by Jeong et al., the cultiva-
tion needed to be supplemented with e.g. reduced glutathione in order for the DsbA to function properly29.
Jeong and coworkers showed, that upon co-expression of DsbA, leptin could be produced under normal
cultivation conditions (37°C and 1 mM IPTG) without any supplements. They obtained a leptin titer cor-
responding to 150 mg/L.
Those results were very promising, end therefore, it was unexpected that the colony counts of EndoX SP-
expressing strains were not higher than they were. However, the low colony counts could be due to low
25
==PAGE:33==
transformation efficiency or wrongful handling of the bacteria whilst performing the transformation, and
thus the strains were applied for further experiments.
The change of signal peptide from OmpA (control) to DsbA enabled growth on higher ampicillin concen-
trations, indicating that more leptin were produced using the latter SP. At a concentration of 400 μg/ml,
only 7% of the OmpA SP clones survived, but for the clones harbouring the DsbA SP, approximately 36%
were able to grow. No growth for OmpA SP was seen in concentrations higher than 400 μg/ml, but DsbA
SP clones were growing even at the highest of concentrations (1000 μg/ml). The EndoX SP-expressing
clones did not grow on concentrations higher than 100 μg/ml, but this could be a result of low transfor-
mation efficiency as only 20 colonies were obtained on the 100 μg/ml plate.
4.1.2 Successful generation of libraries
The generated libraries showed ability to grow on 400 μg/ml and 600 μg/ml for library 1 and 2, respectively.
An expected decrease in growth was observed for library 2 clones, where only one-third grew on 600 μg/ml,
whilst three times more library 1 clones were observed on the 400 μg/ml plate compared to the 100 μg/ml
plate. The increase in survival for library 1 clones indicated that unequal amounts were plated, e.g. due
to pipetting errors. Nonetheless, the results indicated that the clones from both libraries were expressing
more leptin than the control. Because fewer library 1 clones grew on higher ampicillin concentrations,
36 clones were picked for further research, and 48 library clones were selected from the 600 μg/ml plate.
Sequence-randomization of the TIR for optimizing protein expression was carried out in a study by Rennig
et al.37, and the libraries generated in this study induced the same improvements as the ones obtained by
Rennig and coworkers; they allowed for growth on higher ampicillin concentrations indicating higher
production of leptin.
4.1.3 Growth in liquid media
The selected library clones and the strains expressing the alternative SPs were subsequently cultivated
in liquid media in varying ampicillin concentrations in both aerobic and anaerobic conditions. The
intended use of EcN expressing leptin is for it to produce the hormone in the gut, and because it primarily
is an anaerobic environment, it was of great importance that the clones were able to grow without oxygen.
In addition, none of the reviewed studies has produced leptin in this manner.
The highest ampicillin concentration (2 mg/ml) inhibited growth of all of the strains expressing SPs when
cultivated aerobically. Interestingly, several clones from the two libraries were able to grow, indicating
that the induced mutations in the TIR have had a remarked effect on translation. When looking at the
anaerobically cultivated clones, which were only cultivated in 1 mg/ml ampicillin, the controls were
able to grow, but so was numerous of the library clones - in fact, a great deal of those had a shorter lag
time than the control and reached higher cell densities. Nonetheless, the data points obtained from
the plate reader in the anaerobic chamber were fluctuating, which complicated the selection of which
clones to proceed with. This could be a result of the cells clumping together, so if a clump of cells were
26
==PAGE:34==
situated at the specific place and at the time point of which the plate reader made the OD600 measurement,
the obtained value would be much higher than the actual density. Indeed, when subsequently visually
inspecting the cells, aggregates of cells were detected in all of the wells. To examine whether the bacteria
had actually reached the observed OD600-values, their final OD600 were recorded following thorough
resuspension of the cells. The final values indicated that the cells had reached expected densities (data
not shown), and that the recorded anaerobic data was credible.
Moreover, for all of the cultivations, growth were detected in the very beginning of the recorded data,
which most likely was because the transferred cells arose from a fully saturated preculture. Here, they
had been producing the leptin-bla fusion, and as β-lactamase also diffuses to the medium, the cells
initially had sufficient resistance towards the ampicillin when diluted in the fresh media. However, as the
culture grew denser, the availability of β-lactamase decreased and thus the growth halted until the levels
of β-lactamase again had reached a concentration allowing for growth.
In order to ease the selection, the lag times for all of the cultures were therefore determined by applying
OD600 threshold values. This enabled visualization of how much faster than control that the good per-
forming clones initiated growth, which is indicative for a higher production of leptin. In this report, a
few examples on the lag time calculations have been included (see Figure 10). Nonetheless, the picture
remained the same; it was not possible to draw any conclusions from the anaerobic lag times, but the aer-
obic data sets generated reliable results, meaning that well performing clones could be discerned from the
calculations. Due to the inconclusive results from the anaerobic data, several OD600 threshold values were
tried out, but the highest possible value was found to be 0.162. This was chosen so that the controls were
still included. In addition to the lag time calculations, a set of criteria for manually selecting the clones
from the charts were described, all including a requirement for the clone to grow anaerobically. This re-
sulted in selection of thirteen clones for further experiments (B12, C7, D9, D12, E4, E5, E8, F11, G12, H6,
H8, H9).
4.1.4 Second round of selection: Clones selected and DsbA verified as best signal
peptide
Following cultivation of quintuplicates of the selected clones in liquid media substituted with increasing
concentrations of ampicillin, a new round of selection took place. The strains expressing the SPs were
cultivated in quadruplicates, and the growth data obtained from these strains in both aerobic and anaer-
obic conditions were consistent with the previous results of this report; DsbA SP-expressing strains out-
performed both EndoX and OmpA SPs in both aerobic and anaerobic conditions. Indeed, EndoX and
OmpA SPs did not initiate growth at all when cultivated +ox but one of the OmpA SP-expressing strains
did under anaerobic conditions even though the ampicillin concentration were doubled, i.e. 2 mg/ml con-
trary to the initial selection with only 1 mg/ml. However, as only one of the four OmpA SP strains grew
in -ox, it was not reliable to conclude that the control could grow in this concentration. On the contrary,
27
==PAGE:35==
it is more possible that the growth might be caused by another factor; e.g. maybe that specific well did not
contain the same amount of antibiotics as the others.
The fact that DsbA SP strains are performing that much better than the others is indicating that this SP
is better in directing leptin to the periplasm. The DsbA SP guides the polypeptide chain directly to the
periplasm via the SRP pathway, whereas the two other SPs are utilizing the Sec pathway. One could con-
sider that the SecB chaperones are not sufficiently shielding the nascent leptin-bla-fusion from the reduc-
tive environment of the cytoplasm, and due to that, some of the transcript might form secondary structures
and aggregates in the cytoplasm before being directed to the SecYEG channel.
Due to Jeong and coworkers’ study using the EndoX SP for secretion of leptin19, it was expected that the
EndoX SP-expressing strains of this study had better growth kinetics than the results indicates – at least
for the strains cultivated in oxygen, as that was how they did in the study. However, Jeong and coworkers
used another strain of E. coli, i.e. E. coli BL21(DE3), which is an expression molecular biology strain that
lacks the Lon and OmpT proteases and are thus less likely to degrade the recombinant protein52. In this
report, EcN were used for expression of leptin without mutating any of the two proteases, which could
be one of the reasons that EndoX SP strains had inadequate growth compared to the Jeong et al. study.
Moreover, in that study, they engineered the strain to initiate translation of leptin using the weaker pro-
moter Trc (instead of the T7 promoter native to that strain), and found that expression of soluble leptin
increased. Trc is an inducible promoter, whereas the promoter used in the pVTSR-construct (J23107) is a
constitutive promoter. A constitutive promoter had been chosen because of the indented final application:
to create a strain that produces human hormones in situ. However, in future studies, a biosensor enabling
the strain to sense and respond to environmental input signals could be implemented in the construct,
and thus an inducible promoter could replace the constitutive53. This would enable the cell to selectively
produce the protein only when the input signal is present.
The library clones that were selected from this round of cultivation were B12 generated by library 1, and
E5, E12, F11, G12 and H6 originating from library 2. They were selected based on their ability to grow
consistently, i.e. all of the replicates within a certain clone should initiate growth. Moreover, they had to
grow in -ox and at least the 2 mg/ml aerobic condition. The TIR sequences of the library clones (Table 4)
revealed that the translation rates were in the same range as the control, as the library 1-generating primer
was designed to induce translation rates between 390-86,000 au, and the primer generating library 2 had a
predicted range of 88-30,000 au. The control had a translation rate of 10,000 au, and all of the library clones
had translation rates between ∼3,000–16,500, which indicates that the original translation rate itself was
not the reason for for the low expression of the control, considering the wide range that could possibly be
obtained from the two library-generating primers. However, the induced mutations are likely responsible
for the improved translation initiation and thus increased viability on higher amp concentrations, which
is also seen in Rennig et al.’s study37. Regarding the induced mutations, it was not possible to conclude
which bp conferred better expression.

4.1.5 Leptin yields
In order to quantify the amount of leptin secreted from the selected strains, the supernatants were collected
and subjected to a human ELISA kit. The titers were calculated and by using those values, the individual
yields were determined.
One aerobically grown strain produced more leptin than control, i.e.
E5 had an increased leptin
production of 45.61%. However, this increase was only seen in aerobic conditions. When looking at the
yields obtained from the anaerobic cultured clones, none of the selected library clones produced as much
leptin as the control. Nonetheless, many of them were in the same range.
Looking at yield obtained from the DsbA SP-expressing strain, it decreased by more than 60% when
comparing to control. This result was unexpected, as the growth of the strain appeared very favourable.
When comparing the otherwise promising growth data with the yields, it became clear that better
performing clones are not equal to more functional expression of leptin – at least not in a secreted form.
One reason for the low yields could be that, due to the hydrophobicity of leptin, it forms aggregates in the
periplasm or that it is exported in an insoluble form, as seen earlier in Jeong and coworkers’ study19. The
lack of correlation between survival on higher ampicillin concentrations and yield could also be because
bla exerts its function in the periplasm, and thus, the translational fusion of bla does not demonstrate
how much leptin is exported from the cell, rather than suggesting how much has been secreted to the
periplasm.
Another possible reason for the low yields could be that this study expressed both DsbA and DsbC en-
zymes in order to aid further in disulphide bond formation. It has previously been shown that expression
of both of them are useful when the protein has more than one disulphide bond28,33. However, human
leptin contains only one disulphide bond, and because DsbC has the function of reducing incorrect
disulphide bonds, the increased expression of this isomerase may lead to faulty reduction of otherwise
native disulphide bonds in leptin.
4.2 PAM
It was examined whether amidation of the C-terminus could succeed by co-expressing PAM along with
the selected human hormones obestatin, PYY, NPY, NMU, NMB and PPP. Beacuse the digestion enzyme
trypsin in some cases generated fragments too small for the mass spectrometer to detect, it was investi-
gated if another enzyme were available for digesting the hormones in suitable sizes. Only one reviewed
enzyme met this criteria for all of the peptides, i.e. Glu-C, which digests at the carboxyl side of aspartate
(D) and glutamate (E) residues.
However, the same synthetic peptides were applied as standards, hindering comparison between biolog-
ical samples and the synthetic peptides. Therefore, it was inconclusive whether the hormones had been

amidated. In future studies, the correct synthetic peptides will be applied as standards, thus enabling
comparison and reliable analysis of the data.
5
CONCLUSION
5.1 Leptin
Libraries of leptin-expressing EcN were created by randomizing the TIR followed by experimental selec-
tion of the sequences responsible for the highest production. Fusion of bla to the CDS enabled selection
of clones using a simple cell-survival assay on ampicillin.
Several clones were able to grow in higher ampicillin concentrations, indicating an increase in leptin pro-
duction compared to that of the control. When quantifying the yields, one aerobically cultivated clone
(E5) had an increase in leptin production of ∼46%. Anaerobically grown clones did not produce more
leptin than control.
In addition, alternative signal sequences were tested for their ability to increase expression of leptin. The
strain expressing the signal peptide EndoX did not initiate growth in higher ampicillin concentrations,
but as Jeong et al.19 obtained favourable leptin titers using this SP, ineffective transformation may be the
reason for the strain’s inability to grow in higher concentrations. Hence, further research is needed to
examine this signal peptide’s ability to increase expression of leptin.
On the contrary, the strain expressing the DsbA SP showed encouraging growth kinetics when cultured
in high ampicillin concentrations – in particular, it was favourable in anaerobic conditions. Therefore, it
was unanticipated that the leptin yield obtained from this strain was ∼62% and ∼72% less than produced
by control, when cultivated aerobically and anaerobically, respectively.
In conclusion, the TIR randomization enabled selection for a clone (E5) able to produce ∼46% more func-
tional leptin than control. This increase in production was profound; however, it was only obtained in
aerobic conditions. The other selected clones had yields lower than that of control. The examination of
alternative signal peptides revealed that strains expressing the DsbA SP had increased survival on higher
ampicillin concentrations; however, the yields obtained did not correlate with this increase. Indeed, it is
possible that the leptin-bla fusion is expressed in greater extend as β-lactamase exerts its function in the
periplasm. However, formation of inclusion bodies and insoluble aggregates in the periplasm could be
the reason for the lack of correlation between improved growth kinetics and yield.

6
OUTLOOK
It is challenging to develop synthetic bacterial therapy systems that deliver peptides in vivo, but by creating
a strain able to produce human hormones and subsequently optimizing this construct may pave the road
for future success1.
6.1 Leptin
In regards to EcN producing leptin, several steps can be undertaken for optimizing the expression.
6.1.1 Strictly anaerobic selection
In order to select for library clones and alternative SP-expressing strains surviving on higher ampicillin
concentrations, another approach could be to conduct all experiments in anaerobic conditions, instead of
only when the bacteria are subjected to the growth experiments (as done in this study). When E. coli adapts
to environments with different oxygen concentrations, which is vital for competitiveness and growth, it
starts reprogramming the gene expression and cell metabolism. However, oxygen availability determines
how productive the bacterium is, i.e. how much energy that can be generated, and if even the slightest con-
centration of O2 is present, the cell will perform aerobic respiration, given that it is the most productive
metabolic state. This mode is preferred over anaerobic respiration and even more so over fermentation,
which both are initiated upon depletion of oxygen in the environment54. In addition, presence of O2 may
also affect formation of disulphide bonds directly; oxidation and reduction of disulphide bonds require
electron transfer, which is accomplished by DsbA. DsbA achieves these electrons from different compo-
nents under aerobic and anaerobic conditions, and because the electron transport chain varies between
those, less electrons can be generated for use in the disulphide bond machinery55.
As the anaerobic experiments of this project were not conducted solely anaerobically, there is a chance
that EcN had available oxygen during the anaerobic cultivations, as antibiotics were added to the media
in aerobic conditions. Therefore, future experiments will be conducted by performing all anaerobic ma-
nipulations in the anaerobic chamber, which could increase the possibility of selecting for clones able to
express leptin in higher quantities.
6.1.2 Optimization of the DsbA SP-expressing strain
As far as for the results of this project, it was noted that the DsbA SP-expressing strains had greater sur-
vival than control on higher concentrations of ampicillin. Therefore, it would be interesting to create TIR
libraries from that strain. This could possibly result in higher secretion of functional leptin.
32
==PAGE:40==
6.1.3 Expressing fewer or other Dsb-enzymes
Expressing only DsbA, rather than both DsbA and DsbC as done in this study, would be another way to ex-
amine whether functional expression of the hormone could be increased. DsbC functions by isomerizing
non-native disulphide bonds and acts as a chaperone for formation of the native ones, so it was reasoned
that co-expression of this enzyme would lead to less accumulation of leptin aggregates in the periplasm.
However, Jeong and coworkers achieved relatively high expression of leptin by co-expressing only DsbA19,
and studies have shown that coexpression of DsbC is mostly advantageous when the protein of interest
contains more than one disulphide bond33. Therefore, it would be interesting to see the effect on expres-
sion of leptin if this isomerase was not included in the construct.
Another approach would be to implement one or several other enzymes from the dsb operon in the con-
struct. Besides DsbA and and DsbC, the enzymes DsbB, DsbD, DsbE and DsbG participate in formation
of disulphide bonds. DsbG and DsbE have similar functions to that of DsbC; they detect the formed
disulphide bonds and break the non-native ones. Moreover, they have the same chaperone-function56–58.
Whilst the enzymes DsbA, DsbC, DsbG and DsbE reside in the periplasm, DsbB and DsbD are embed-
ded in the inner membrane. Here, they function by catalyzing periplasmic disulphide reduction (DsbD)
and oxidation (DsbB), allowing for dithiol/disulfide exchange58. Hence, functional leptin production
could possibly be improved by co-expressing one or more of the latter enzymes, enabling more rapid
dithiol/disulfide exchange of the periplasmic residing Dsb-enzymes, enabling a faster disulphide bond
machinery.
6.1.4 Leaky cell wall for increased extracellular expression
It is also possible to engineer the cell to have a more leaky cell wall, and by that, extracellular expres-
sion could be increased. There are several ways of doing this, but in a study performed by Yang et al. they
demonstrated how overexpression of D,D-carboxypeptidase pertubed the cell wall resulting in an increase
in extracellular protein production59. The cell wall of Gram-negative bacteria is constituted of phospho-
lipids and peptidoglycans, and upon synthesis of peptidoglycan, D,D-carboxypeptidase functions by stabi-
lizing the peptidoglycan structure, adjust cross-linking and modify the cell wall60. In Yang and coworkers’
study, they obtained the disruption by overexpressing the genes responsible for D,D-carboxypeptidase,
i.e. DacA and DacB, and were able to achieve an increase in production of recombinant amylase and
α-galactosidase of 11.9- and 2.5-fold, respectively, upon overexpression of dacB59. Both amylase and α-
galactosidase contains disulphide bonds, and as extracellular expression of these proteins was increased,
it indicates that the oxidative environment of the periplasm was not disturbed upon destabilization of the
cell wall. Hence, overexpression of dacB could be introduced in the construct to test for higher functional
leptin production.
33
==PAGE:41==
6.1.5 Improving solubility of leptin
Because of the hydrophobicity of leptin, another really intriguing option for increasing functional expres-
sion would be to enhance solubility by fusing the leptin CDS to an amphipathic protein. An amphipathic
protein is constituted of both hydrophobic and hydrophilic regions, which can be utilized to form micelles
when subjected to water. When mixing the amphipathic protein with lipid molecules, the hydrophobic
ends of the protein bind to the hydrophobic regions of the lipid molecules of interest, thus displacing the
lipid molecule. The hydrophilic part of the protein enable water solubility and brings the lipid into solu-
tion61.
In a study performed by Mizrachi et al., they enabled in vivo production of otherwise hydrophobic integral
membrane proteins using an amphipathic protein fusion strategy62. This allowed for direct expression of
soluble products that were shielded from the water by the amphipathic protein apolipoprotein A-I.
Fusion of such a protein could possible enhance solubility and thus functional expression of leptin.
6.2 PAM
Enabling EcN to express α-amidating enzyme is a great challenge, and none has successfully accomplished
in vivo C-terminal amidation of human hormones using E. coli as host.
Therefore, in future studies, the synthetic peptides will be designed as if they were digested by Glu-C
instead of trypsin, which would enable reliable standards for use in the analysis.

7.2 Cultivation
All anaerobic manipulations were carried out in a Type B Vinyl Anaerobic Chamber (Coy Lab Products)
filled with an atmosphere of 95% nitrogen and 5% hydrogen. Minimum 8 hours prior to conducting

experiments – both aerobic and anaerobic – and prepared as standard (GAM Broth, Modified ”Nissui”,
Nissui Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd.). In addition, cultivation was performed with shaking (250 rpm) at 37 °C.
