Abstract 
 
The aim of this project was investigating the effects of Glycerol-Silicone membrane technology by 
embedding the three different antibiotics Triclosan, Tetracycline and Mupirocin in the membranes. 
These membranes are tested on eleven bacterial strains most commonly found in non-healing chronic 
wounds. This project will be based on results obtained from the strains from specific bacterial species, 
in order to mimic real-life reaction to the tested wound dressing. The purpose of this project was to 
uncover the potential use of this technology as an advance wound care product for commercial use. 
Through research and inspiration from previous studies, it was determined that the most suitable 
method for testing Glycerol-Silicone membranes was the use of agar plates containing Brain Heart 
Infusion and Lysogeny Broth. Throughout the experiments for this project, the Glycerol-Silicone 
membranes embedded with three antibiotics and a combination of the antibiotics in the membranes 
were placed on the agar plates. The agar plates were streaked with chosen cultured bacterial strains, 
subsequently the diameters of the clearing zones were measured after incubation. All membranes had 
a substantial effect on the bacteria selected, but it was found that Glycerol-Silicone membranes 
containing Triclosan were by far the most functional and furthermore, the combinations of antibiotics 
did not have a synergistic effect. When the results were compared to the commercially available 
products Biatain and Mepilex on the agar plates, the Glycerol-Silicone membranes were far superior, 
both in their release of drug but also in their ability to sustain the clearing zones after re-incubation 
for an additional two weeks after initial measurements. For all membranes, the effects and clearing 
zones did not change significantly when re-incubated for an additional two weeks, suggesting that 
regrowth of bacteria in the clearing zones does not occur even in the absence of drug release and 
presence of Glycerol. Bacterial growth is observed for several strains for the control sample 
containing Glycerol and Silicone, therefore experiments were conducted to uncover the potential 
adverse effect of Glycerol on bacterial growth. Different volumes of G20, G40 and G80 were placed 
on discs on the agar plates after incubation. It was found that several bacterial strains did utilize 
Glycerol as a carbon source, but it was concluded that the Glycerol in the membranes did not annul 
or diminish the effects of the antibiotics present. Bacterial growth was not observed in the presence 
of antibiotics in the membranes.   
Through the use of a pilot liquid assay experiment it is found that Mepilex was highly functional, but 
only in a liquidated environment. The Glycerol-Silicone membranes were more stable and adjusted 
to both the environment in the agar plates and liquid assay. Furthermore, a mixed culture experiment 
was conducted to mimic real-life scenarios where multiple bacterial stains are present in a wound.

Project outline 
The Danish Polymer Centre (DPC) in the Departments of Chemical and Biochemical Engineering at 
DTU, have developed Glycerol-Silicone membranes imbedded with three antibiotics: Triclosan, 
Mupirocin and Tetracycline. These membranes are particularly indented to be used as wound dressing 
for non-healing chronic wounds, which are a major health issue. To determine the efficiency of the 
Glycerol-Silicone membranes, a collaboration is made between DPC and The Research Group for 
Genomic Epidemiology at The National Food Institute at DTU, where the aim of this project is to 
develop methods and assays that will be suitable for testing the Glycerol-Silicone membranes 
received from DPC.  
 
The objectives 
1. Development of semi-quantitively microbiological assays to test Glycerol-Silicone membranes. 
2. Testing the function of Glycerol-Silicone membranes embedded with Triclosan, Tetracycline and 
Mupirocin on different pathogens 
3. Examining whether the antibiotics used have synergistic effects when used in combination  
4. Comparing commercially available wound dressing products to the Glycerol-Silicone membranes 
embedded with Triclosan, Tetracycline and Mupirocin in different settings.  
5. Determining the negative and positive impact of Glycerol in the growth and inhibition of the 
selected bacterial strains.

1 Literature review 
For centuries bacterial infections have been the main cause of morbidity and mortality, thereby 
remaining a constant threat to human existence. This changed with the discovery of penicillin by Sir. 
Alexander Fleming. Since the discovery, multiple derivatives of penicillin and other types of 
antibiotics have been an important cornerstone of clinical medicine and have saved a great number 
of people from life-threatening bacterial infections. In the 20th century, numerous antimicrobial 
agents have been discovered, facilitating the accelerated eradication of some of the most common 
bacterial infections whilst keeping the death toll of others down. Although antibiotics are of great 
importance to maintain a healthy population, resistance has emerged as a result of their use and 
misuse. With the rapid growing spread of resistance, scientists are forced to search for new types of 
antibiotics or new methods of utilizing the old ones. Multidrug resistance of the pathogenic 
microorganisms has become a major impediment toward successful diagnosis and management of 
infectious diseases. Antibiotics have a large number of applications. One of those is for the treatment 
of non-healing chronic wounds in vulnerable patients suffering from Diabetes mellitus, HIV, Cancer 
and Cystic fibrosis etc. Chronic wounds arise from acute wounds that have failed to proceed through 
the predictable and expected rate according to the normal wound healing process for otherwise 
healthy individuals. The failure of the healing mechanisms caused by pre-existing condition allows 
for the wound site to be open for a longer time, thus aiding the formation of biofilms that consist of 
polymicrobial consortia, allowing the bacteria to live in a highly organized biofilm. The biofilms 
shield the pathogenic microbes from being susceptible to antibiotic treatment, allowing an acute 
wound to become a non-healing chronic wound that is difficult to treat with conventional treatment. 
Biofilm formation can also directly hinder wound closure or cause defective wound closure where 
the wound site appears closed but the skin lacks barrier function (Zhao et al., 2013). The formation 
of biofilms and bacterial infections are known to occur in chronic wounds. In modern medicine there 
is countless effort in identifying the cause of such infections, the duration of those infection and 
research into available treatment options. Due to resistance and the emergence of non-healing chronic 
wounds that vary greatly between individuals, new and more innovative options are being created by 
researchers in the medical field. Therefore, in the following section the cause and underlying 
mechanisms of chronic non-healing wounds is described to further understand the aspects of this 
subject that poses a problem for even the most experienced healthcare officials.

1.1 
Acute wounds 
 
The term acute wound describes any trauma that has occurred to the skin. This could be a cut, burn 
or puncture but could also be other types of injuries such as contusions. The common description of 
a wound is the damage of the skin’s epidermis by a sharp object. Usually these wounds are repaired 
without much difficulty, but for individuals suffering from diseases that disable the skins repair 
mechanisms, the healing of acute wounds is a lengthy process. In recent years, there has been an 
increase in the discovery of diseases that are caused by a defect in the immune system that delay the 
repair mechanisms, but also there has been an increase in diseases such as Diabetes Mellitus type 2, 
which is commonly related to lifestyle choices and advanced age (Järbrink et al., 2017; Raje et al., 
2015). For the greatest part of the population no medical attention is sought or given in the occurrence 
of a wound. But in cases where acute wounds do not regain complete integrity within a reasonable 
timeframe, they are deemed chronic and medical attention is necessary. Chronic wounds are wounds 
that have failed to progress through the reparative process, thus being referred to as non-healing 
chronic wounds (Iqbal et al., 2017).  
 
1.2 
Chronic wounds 
Non-healing chronic wounds are common in individuals that suffer from diseases such as Diabetes 
Mellitus, which has been rapidly increasing due to an aging world population and the adaptation of 
sedentary lifestyle worldwide, but other diseases that are caused by genetic disorders and 
immunodeficiencies are also responsible for the increase of incidences in non-healing chronic 
wounds. The common consensus for diagnosing a non-healing chronic wound is an acute wound that 
has failed to form integrity and functionality within three months. In modern society, researches 
estimate that between 1-2% of the population will experience a chronic wound during their lifetime 
in developed countries (Sen et al., 2009).  Chronic wounds are the main cause of amputation with 
roughly 70% of all lower limb amputations being due to non-healing diabetic ulcers, leaving patients 
handicapped, changing their lives completely (Järbrink et al., 2017). Non-healing chronic wounds are 
not only a burden to the individuals but have also become a great burden to national healthcare 
systems and pose a financial problem. Patients are more likely to be admitted to hospitals due to 
complication caused by their non-healing chronic wounds. These complications include infections 
and chronic pain. There is not a single specific method of treating these wounds as treatment is 
dependent on the available options, bacterial colonies found on the wound and financial state of the 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
individuals. Therefore, these individuals may experience that their chronic wounds may never heal 
or may take years for complete healing (Olsson et al., 2019) .  
Because of the skins inability to heal at a timely order, these non-healing chronic wounds are exposed 
to bacterial infection and biofilm formation that would otherwise be barricaded by the skin, further 
obstructing correct healing. Infections are the greatest threat to proper treatment as many of the 
bacteria causing these infections have become resistant or have gained resistance mechanisms from 
the biofilms formed, making it challenging for the healthcare professional to give the right treatment. 
The most prevalent non-healing chronic wounds include diabetic foot ulcers, pressure ulcers, venous 
leg ulcers and surgical wounds. The formation of non-healing chronic wounds is associated with the 
four main processes by which wounds heal: Homeostasis, Inflammation, Proliferation and 
Remodelling (Figure 1). If the healing process becomes arrested in the progression through these 
stages, the wound will remain open, becoming a chronic wound (Young, 2019).  
 
1.3 Skin repair mechanism  
 
1.3.1 Homeostasis 
When an injury occurs, there is an outpouring of lymphatic fluid and blood. During this initial phase, 
haemostasis is achieved within the first seconds to hours of the incident. The purpose of a wound to 
achieve haemostasis is to form a plug to discontinue further bleeding and stabilize the surroundings. 
During this initial process, the wound will be filled with blood and a scab is formed on the top, 
creating a clot, discontinuing further bleeding which happens fairly quickly. Subsequently, there is 
an accumulation of platelets to the wound area facilitated by ADP, resulting in the clumping of 
platelets and thrombocytes into a fibrin network, initiating thrombosis, which in turn restores the 
skins function as a protective barrier, retaining the skins integrity. This process is called 
vasoconstriction and is short-lived (Periayah et al., 2017; Wallace & Zito, 2019a).  
 
1.3.2 Inflammation 
The inflammatory phase is characterized by vasodilation that aids the migration of neutrophils from 
the bloodstream to the wound area. The influx of neutrophils enables phagocytosis of cellular debris 
and bacteria, decontaminating the wound.  A quick activation of the immune cells in the tissue may 
also occur by the release of mastocytes, gamma-delta cells and Langerhans cells, which secrete 
chemokines and cytokines, promoting inflammation (Nguyen et al., 2019). All of the processes 
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
described occur in a synchronized fashion. After 24-48 hours, the neutrophils die and are replaced by 
mast cells that mature into macrophages. These not only partake in continuing the cleaning and 
decontamination process, but also release growth factor to stimulate adjacent cells to enter mitosis. 
The release of growth factors further stimulates the immigration and the mitosis of fibroblast 
synthesizing collagen, which is vital for skin formation, thus initiating proliferation. Numerous 
signals are responsible for these important transitions, most notably the apoptosis and phagocytosis 
of wound-bed resident neutrophils. Wounds that fail to transition from the inflammatory phase cause 
pro-inflammatory macrophages to persist, and non-healing chronic wounds develop (Ellis et al., 
2018; Krzyszczyk et al., 2018). The inflammation process should ideally last between 1-6 days but is 
known to persist in case of failed transition (Figure 1) (Than et al., 2017).  
 
1.3.3 Proliferation 
The proliferation phase is stimulated by growth factors during inflammation and is estimated to last 
from days to weeks (Figure 1). During this process the fibroblasts have started to lay down new 
collagen and glycosaminoglycans. These proteoglycans form the core of the wound and help stabilize 
it. Epithelialization starts to occur with the migration of cells from the wound periphery and adjacent 
edges. Initially, only a thin superficial layer of epithelial cells is laid down, but with time, a thicker 
and more durable layer of cells will bridge the wound. Once collagen fibers have been laid down on 
the fibrin framework, the wound starts to mature. The wound also begins to contract and is facilitated 
by continued deposition of fibroblasts and myofibroblasts (Wallace & Zito, 2019b) . 
 
1.3.4 Maturation  
The remodelling phase starts approximately at week 3 after the initial injury and can last for years, 
depending on the health and age of the individual. The excess collagen degrades, and wound 
contraction also begins to peak at around week 3 (Figure 1). The newly formed skin will have about 
70-80% strength of the original skin  (Wallace et al., 2019). 
 
1.3.5 Causes of defective wound healing 
There are many causes of defective wound healing including genetic diseases and other disorders that 
disable the repair mechanisms that drive the healing process. However, studies have showed that 
several lifestyle choices such as cigarette smoking and drinking alcohol have a great impact on the 
healing process. Nicotine found in cigarettes is a known vasoconstrictor, decreasing the oxygen 
==PAGE:19==
 
18 
supply to the area. Oxygen is essential in the healing process as it has multiple function such as 
delivery of nutrients which are crucial in the different phases of healing. Furthermore, oxygen is vital 
in the healing process as formation of new blood vessels and collagen formation depend on it. 
Similarly, alcohol has adverse effect on the healing process by decreasing the macrophage response 
during inflammation, interfering with epidermal production, increasing the possibility of infection as 
healing is slowed down (Hashim et al., 2017).

Wound healing is an intricate and delicate process that is vulnerable to both outside influences but 
also to reactions caused by a defect in the body’s own functionality. Figure 1 depicts the phases and 
shows that there is an interplay between the different stages, making the healing process a continuous 
one with multiple overlap. The processes need to function in a highly synchronised manner as failure 
at any given time results in prolonged opening of the wound, subjecting it to outside influence, 
especially from bacteria that inhabit the skin. Once the wound area is infected and the reparative 
systems have failed an infection will occur. Infections can become life-threatening especially for 
those that have pre-existing conditions, challenging health professional in regard to wound 
management. The main cause of infection is colonization of the wound by commensal bacteria, which 
can have resistant mechanisms making them difficult to treat. If the infection is not treated 
immediately, these bacteria form biofilms that provide the bacteria with protection from outer 
environment and resistance mechanisms.

1.4 Biofilm formation and bacterial resistance  
A biofilm is characterized as a population of bacteria that are organized into an extracellular matrix. 
Biofilm formation is characteristic to bacteria. The purpose of the biofilm formation is to form a 
protection from outer environment. The most relevant attribute is the ability for a biofilm to be 
resistant to antibiotics and other antimicrobials as well as providing protection against host defences. 
These are just a few examples of the benefits biofilm provides for bacterial communities. Studies 
have shown that biofilm formation is attributed to cell-cells communication also called QS. The 
accumulation of signals released by bacteria in close vicinity trigger growth of biofilm. Different 
signalling molecules drive the formation of biofilms for different bacterial species. A biofilm can 
consist of only one species but can also contain a combination of species. Furthermore, biofilms are 
not only a threat in respect to non-healing chronic wounds, but effect medical appliances, the food 
industry and implant devices, proving fatal if not dealt with properly. Most biofilms that cause 
infection in non-healing chronic wounds are constructed of multiple species.  
 
Figure 2: Schematic illustration of the steps of biofilm formation on a surface (Rendueles & Ghigo, 2012) 
 
The reduced susceptibility of biofilms to antibiotics and antimicrobials is not by the regularly known 
mechanism by which resistance occurs in bacteria. Biofilms produce extracellular polymeric 
substances that are made up of water, polysaccharides, DNA and other molecules secreted by the 
bacteria in the matrix, making it harder for antibiotics and antimicrobials to penetrate the biofilm 
surface. Furthermore, not only are biofilms resistant to antibiotics and are hard to treat, they are also 
resistant to the most commonly used disinfectants. Studies have established that the resistance ability 
==PAGE:21==
 
20 
is dependent on the size and thickness of the biofilm in question, as thicker biofilms have increased 
formation of persister cells, which are thought to constitute a multi-layered defence. Though, it does 
not constitute the main mechanism by which biofilms gain resistance, studies have suggested that one 
possible explanation of the resistance is the accelerated transfer of plasmid between bacteria in close 
vicinity (Gebreyohannes et al., 2019; Venkata Mohan et al., 2008). 
 
1.4.1 Biofilms in acute and chronic wounds  
Acute wounds on healthy individuals heal within a shorter time span, making the development of 
biofilms highly unlikely. Even in the occurrence of such event, the body’s own natural defence 
mechanisms are enough to combat the biofilms. However, individuals suffering from diseases that 
compromise the wound healing process are usually monitored and the wound site is cleaned and 
covered to avoid further complications.  There is no treatment option for acute wounds for healthy 
individuals, but for those that have pre-existing conditions, a vigorous cleaning regime is put in place 
coupled with a dose of antibiotics, depending on the severity of the injury (Guo et al., 2010). In most 
developed countries, wound dressing is a staple used to cover the open area to avoid further 
complications and plain gauze is used in the majority of the cases. 
The bacteria that colonize wounds after an injury are typically the patient’s own natural flora but can 
also be microorganisms that are transferred via contact with contaminated surfaces and water or from 
the hands of healthcare workers. Gram-positive bacteria such as Streptococcus pyogenes, 
Staphylococcus epidermidis, Staphylococcus aureus, Enterococcus, Corynebacterium and Gram-
negative bacteria such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Acinetobacter and Escherichia coli and 
Klebsiella pneumoniae are all among a list of bacteria that are known to occupy chronic wounds. 
Decades of research has proven that most of these bacterial species have several strains that are 
resistant to antibiotics (Ashrafi et al., 2017; Frykberg et al., 2015). It has been proven that bacteria 
colonizing non-healing chronic wounds exist as highly persistent biofilm communities.  
One study has characterized the biofilm formation in acute and chronic wounds respectively. 
Specimens were obtained from 93 subjects of which 77 suffered from chronic wounds whereas 16 
had acute wounds. An electron microscopy technique is used to identify the biofilms in the specimens 
collected and the study concluded that the presence of biofilms varied greatly between the two groups. 
After examination of 50 specimens taken from individuals with chronic wounds, 30 of the 50 
specimens contained biofilms, constituting 66%. For the group that had acute wounds only 1 in the 
16 specimens analysed contained biofilms making up only 6%. This shows a clear difference in 
==PAGE:22==
 
21 
biofilm formation in these two different types of wounds, proving the high prevalence of biofilm 
formation in non-healing chronic wounds (James et al., 2008). Another study suggested that the 
prevalence of biofilms was 60% in chronic wounds compared to only 6 % in acute wounds. The 
studies conducted on this particular subject paint a unilateral picture of the prevalence and alarming 
presence of biofilms in chronic wounds (Song et al., 2017). 
 
1.4.2 Bacterial strains occupying chronic wounds 
Through extensive literature search eleven of the most common bacterial species in non-healing 
chronic wounds are identified: Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Acinetobacter baumannii, Escherichia coli, 
staphylococcus aureus, Enterococci, Corynebacterium, Staphylococcus epidermis, Streptococcus 
pyogenes and Klebsiella pneumoniae (Table 1). Though very common in human surrounding these 
pathogenic bacterial species contain strains that have over the last decades been reclassified to MDR 
or XDR. In fact, strains that are either MDR or XDR are becoming more prevalent. Studies have 
shown that isolates of different strains within the same species can vary greatly, a variation that has 
an impact on the susceptibility to a specific drug. Furthermore, research conducted solely for the 
purpose of identifying strain to strain variability in respect to resistance have been conducted. In this 
study 1060 bacterial strains from different species was studied and it was concluded that 393 (37.1%) 
of the bacterial strains were MDR and 146 (13.8%) of the 1060 bacterial strains were XDR (Figure 3 
and 4) (Basak et al., 2016).

1.4.3 Treatment of chronic wounds  
Currently non-healing chronic wounds are treated with antibiotics by administration of oral tablets or 
intravenous injections coupled with a vigorous cleaning regime (Mavrogenis et al., 2018).  For those 
with severe injuries, systemic antibiotics are used in combination, typically cephalosporin and an 
aminoglycoside are given intravenously. Studies have shown that the treatment, choice of agent and 
duration of antibiotic therapy is incorrect in 30% to 50% of cases. Furthermore, these studies show 
that 30% to 60% of the antibiotics prescribed in ICUs have been found to be unnecessary, 
inappropriate or suboptimal (Ventola, 2015). Though the use of antimicrobial agents is good at 
combating further complications, they are given systemically and therefore contribute to one of the 
world’s biggest health crisis namely antibiotic resistance. The use of systemic antibiotics is not the 
first choice of treatment, as topical options are available in creams and wound dressings containing 
smaller more precise doses. Only when the local treatment options are exhausted systemic treatment 
is acceptable. The choice of antibiotics solely depends on the strains that are isolated from the 
wounds. If no isolates are taken, research of decades suggest that the drug choice depends on the 
known bacteria that inhabit these wounds. Staphylococci and Streptococci are the most prevalent in 
early stages of chronic wounds whereas the wound is colonized by different species as time passes, 
therefore the treatment options depend on many factors including time, type of wound and the 
location of the wound (Kadam et al., 2019).  
The use of antibiotics and antimicrobials to treat bacterial infection is universal, but it is not the only 
defence against an infection. A wound, whether acute or chronic, is typically covered with wound 
dressings to stop bacterial infections from forming, with the most commonly used types being plain 
gauze dressing or an adhesive bandage. A wound needs an ideal environment for correct healing, 
which is the perfect blend of moisture and a breathable material, generating optimal conditions for 
wound healing (Sood et al., 2014).  
In recent years, advanced wound care products have become commercially available, and have 
proven beneficial as they are adjusted to provide the perfect environment and designed to deliver 
antibiotics and other antimicrobials to a wound. These innovations prevent the use of wrong drugs 
and the overuse of those. Furthermore, it allows medical professionals to prevent chronic wounds by 
opting for topical products much earlier, thereby possibly saving the lives of the patients, but also 
reducing the costs associated with long term out and in-patient care given to patients with non-healing 
==PAGE:27==
 
26 
chronic wounds. The use of these products is also cheaper than the use of systemic drugs. The most 
common commercially available wound dressing products contain Silver compounds.  
 
2 Wound dressing products  
2.1.1 Silver compounds  
Silver compounds have been used as an antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory agent for centuries. It 
has found a variety of applications because its toxicity to human cells is considerably lower than that 
to bacteria. The most widely documented uses are for treatment of burns. The mechanisms by which 
silver kills bacterial cells is not known. However, one research suggests the silver binds to many 
cellular components, with membrane components probably being more important than nucleic acids. 
It is difficult to know whether strong binding reflects toxicity or detoxification; some sensitive 
bacterial strains have been reported as accumulating more silver than the corresponding resistant 
strain, while in others the reverse apparently occurs. In several cases resistance has been shown to be 
plasmid mediated. The plasmids are reported as difficult to transfer and can also be difficult to 
maintain. Attempts to find biochemical differences between resistant and sensitive strains have been 
met with limited success, as differences are subtle. Silver has been proven to have an inhibitory effect 
on E. coli and S. aureus, which are two common bacteria that are known to be a menace in acute and 
chronic wounds. Utilizing silver compounds in wound dressing products has many benefits including 
reduction in the organism's chances of developing resistance (Clement & Jarrett, 1994; Leaper et al., 
2013). Though the mode of action is unclear, the antibacterial properties of silver has been known 
since ancient times. Therefore, silver ions are used to control bacterial growth in a variety of medical 
applications including dental appliances, catheters and wound dressing products.  
 
 
2.1.2 Traditional wound dressing  
Today the plain gauze is still the most used wound dressing product, but with more knowledge on the 
exact mechanisms influencing the wound healing process, more efficient and advanced products are 
being produced. These products provide the wound site with the ideal moist, warmth and provide 
protection from the outer environment as well as delivery of drugs to combat bacterial growth, 
increasing the ability of the wound to heal within an acceptable timeframe.

2.2 
Modern wound dressing products  
Adhesive wound dressings products such as plasters or bandages are used to cover wounds until the 
healing process can protect the wound. However, traditional wound dressings generally adhere to the 
healing wound causing a new injury upon removal and thereby interrupting the healing process. 
Therefore, modern wound dressing products address this problem by incorporating anti-adhesive 
properties to the dressings by coating the common viscose bandages with silica modified with long 
chain alkyltrialkoxysilanes (Hayes, 2011) while others have safetac technology. An additional 
valuable feature of these innovative wound dressings is their excellent absorption properties of wound 
exudates that facilitate the healing process, especially in bedridden patients with non-healing chronic 
wounds. 
 
2.2.1  Mepilex  
Mepilex is a commercial wound dressing product that is embedded with silver ions. This wound 
dressing is the epiphany of cutting edge modern wound dressing, not only supplying a wound site 
with antimicrobial substance but also incorporating a new technology called safetac, which is a soft 
silicone adhesive that is meant to prevent dressing-related trauma. Many known wound dressing 
employ a harder adhesive that may cause further damage to the surroundings, thus causing further 
complication. Mepilex has dual effect. It facilitates the uptake of exudates from the wound in order 
to release the silver ions, as wound drainage is an important part of creating an ideal environment. 
Smaller clinical and case studies have found that the use of Mepilex has a substantial effect on the 
pathogens associated with delay of wound healing (Davies et al., 2017).

antimicrobial active substance in return. Biatain Ag comes in variety of version: some embedded with 
silver whilst others contain painkillers such as ibuprofen, making this technology multifunctional.

3 Glycerol- Silicone membrane technology  
 
Glycerol-Silicone membranes (GSM) are developed by DPC at DTU chemistry for the purpose of 
being used as wound dressing. One of the main capabilities of modern wound dressing is the ability 
to deliver active substances to a wound. These substances include anti-inflammatory agents and 
antibiotics but is not only limited to these. The purpose of the Glycerol-Silicone membranes is to 
deliver antibiotics to wounds and indented to be used for non-healing wounds, as they are mostly 
colonized by biofilms that are hard to treat otherwise. These membranes are ground-breaking both in 
function and in design, as they can deliver a single drug but also a combination of drugs. The delivery 
of multiple drugs simultaneously is very appealing, as biofilms are formed from a host of different 
bacteria. These bacteria react to different antibiotics, and therefore combining a set of different 
antibiotics with different modes of action is the most effective way to combat biofilm formation on 
chronic non-healing wounds. 
 
3.1 Benefits of GSM technology  
 
The GSM technology has many features in common with commercially available products but differs 
in release optimization capabilities and being cost effective and easy to prepare. Furthermore, the 
membranes are excellent in absorbing exudates from the wound area and are proven to efficiently 
absorb water, sweat and blood serum. The advantage of the GSM technology in comparison to other 
products is the ability to carry and successfully release multiple active substances (Mazurek et al., 
2018).

some bacterial stains can use it as a carbon source for growth. Studies have shown that in the absence 
of more favourable carbon sources such as glucose, some strains e.g. E. coli can utilize Glycerol as

3.4 Antibiotics embedded in the GSM 
 
During the course of this project three antibiotics: Triclosan, Tetracycline and Mupirocin are tested 
and remain the main focus area in regard to the Glycerol-Silicone membranes used. Besides these 
antibiotics, commercial wound dressing products containing silver compounds are investigated for

3.4.1 Triclosan  
Triclosan (TCS) is a synthetic compound primarily used in commercial and healthcare products such 
as toothpaste, deodorants and various cosmetics. This has raised a concern on whether this use of 
Triclosan in common household products is justifiable as bacteria can easily become resistant with 
the widespread use. Though its antimicrobial effects are well documented, it is currently not used for 
the treatment of systemic infection. Multiple animal and human studies focusing on the 
pharmacokinetic properties of Triclosan, have shown that the compound is lipid soluble. The high 
lipid solubility of the substance gives rise to questions regarding distribution properties and 
accumulation (Sandborgh-Englund et al., 2006). However, other studies focusing on the effects 
regarding topical use have documented that the compound is safe to use for topical purposes and 
could serve as a lead compound for a potential systemic antibiotic (Fan et al., 2002). Triclosan has a 
broad-spectrum activity at low concentrations to inhibit both Gram-positive and Gram-negative 
bacteria. The drug is bacteriostatic at low concentrations but is bactericidal at high concentration 
against some types of Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria. The mode of action of Triclosan is 
via inhibition of enoyl-acyl carrier protein ACP reductase (FabI) in organisms such as S. 
aureus and E. coli that rely on this enzyme to perform the ultimate step in the elongation cycle of 
bacterial fatty acid biosynthesis (Alfhili & Lee, 2019; Fan et al., 2002).

3.4.2 Tetracycline  
Tetracyclines (TET) are a family of antibiotics discovered in 1940s. Tetracycline belongs to a class 
of antibiotics called protein synthesis inhibitors. They inhibit protein synthesis in susceptible bacteria 
by preventing the attachment of aminoacyl-tRNA to the ribosomal acceptor (A) site, blocking 
bacterial translation by binding reversibly to the 16S rRNA in the 30S subunit, thus making it 
impossible for the anticodons of the charged tRNAs to align properly with the codons of the mRNA 
(Chukwudi, 2016). 
Tetracyclines are generally broad-spectrum agents that show great activity against a wide range of 
Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, and even atypical organisms such as Chlamydiae, 
Mycoplasmas, Rickettsiae and protozoan parasites have been known to be susceptible to 
Tetracyclines. Due to their desirable antimicrobial properties and the absence of major adverse 
effects, they are extensively used in clinical settings on both humans and animals. Though, it remains 
one of the most used antibiotics, a host of bacteria have become resistant. One study aimed to 
categorize the resistance profile for S. pyogenes strains as S. pyogenes is a bacterium known to 
commonly be found in the throat and other surfaces. This study is conducted in Iran where multiple 
strains were isolated in three different regions. The distribution of Tetracycline resistance among 
isolates in the districts studied did not differ significantly. 625 strains isolated were obtained from the 
northern part of Iran, 500 strains recovered from southern Tehran, and 210 strains isolated in central 
Tehran, the rates of resistance to tetracycline were 43.5, 42.8, and 39.9%, respectively (Jasir et al., 
2000). 
In recent surveillance studies, the prevalence of Tetracycline resistance in selected European 
countries was recorded to be as high as 66.9%, though the global Tetracycline-resistance percentages 
were 8.7% and 24.3% for methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and Streptococcus 
pneumoniae, respectively (Grossman, 2016; Jones et al., 2014). 
 
3.4.3 Mupirocin  
Mupirocin (MUP) is an antibiotic that is used for topical purpose only. Its structure and mode of 
action are unrelated to any of the known antibiotics. It is active against a wide range of Gram-positive 
bacteria, including Staphylococci and Streptococci, and it also has moderate activity against Gram-
negative bacteria. Mupirocin is widely used to treat skin ulcers and is considered a better alternative 
to treatment with systemic antibiotic, as the infection is treated locally, greatly reducing unwanted 
side effects. Mupirocin inhibits RNA and protein synthesis by selectively binding to the bacterial 
==PAGE:36==
 
35 
isoleucylt-RNA synthetase (IleS), preventing the formation of isoleucyl-tRNA, halting the 
incorporation of isoleucine into the growing polypeptide chain. This mechanism of action is unique 
to Mupirocin (Lamb, 1991). In a small clinical study lasting 8 days, topical MUP and Erythromycin 
were given to 62 children with Impetigo and patients were divided into two group: 30 received MUP 
treatment and 32 received Erythromycin. The patient’s lesions were measured before start of therapy 
and remeasured on day 4 or 5 of the therapy. The lesions are measured for the third time 7 days after 
the end of therapy. At the first visit after the end of the therapy, 24 children in the MUP group and 
14 children in the Erythromycin group were cured or had at least a 75% reduction in size of their 
lesions. In a later follow up, 29 of the children in the MUP group compared with 27 in the 
erythromycin group were cured. Side effects were few in the group receiving MUP, whereas 5 
children in the erythromycin group developed mild diarrhoea. This study concluded that MUP 
appeared to be safe and effective in treating impetigo in children, as the data for their study showed 
a trend toward more rapid clinical response with MUP compared to Erythromycin (Goldfarb et al., 
1988). 
A comparative double-blind study on the effects of Mupirocin and Tetracycline ointments for treating 
skin infections, 111 patients are divided in to two group, 53 were treated with mupirocin and 58 
treated with tetracycline. In the study both groups improved, and it was concluded that there was no 
significant difference. But a bacteriological assessment revealed a better response to mupirocin. In 
the S. aureus and S. pyogenes that were isolated from the infection sites, 99% of S. aureus were 
sensitive to mupirocin compared with 61% to tetracycline. For S. pyogenes it was found to be resistant 
to Tetracycline and only 14% were resistant to Mupirocin (Wong et al., 1989), such differences are 
not observed during the course of this project.  
 
 
3.4.4 Combination therapy  
Combination therapy has long been a means to combat a bacterial infection that is caused by several 
bacterial strains ordered into a highly structured biofilms, making them hard to combat. Because 
resistance is the most imminent threat to human and animal health, combination therapy is often the 
only way which an infection may be treated. Usually the combined drugs have different modes of 
action, increasing the possibility of targeting each bacterium, as they may be resistant to some drug 
but not others. Commercially available wound dressing products often only contain one drug. 
Combining different drugs in one wound dressing can decrease the treatment time and can increase 
==PAGE:37==
 
36 
the possibility of eradication of the infection. This is of special importance for people suffering from 
diseases that increase their likelihood of developing non-healing chronic wounds. The GSM 
technology allows for combination of multiple drugs in different combinations and concentrations, 
making it a desirable and convenient product in the treatment of non-healing chronic wounds.  
 
3.5 Economic burden  
Wound care products are a major health expense. Based on an economic evaluation of Medicare 
expenditure in 2014, the annual cost was estimated at 28.1 to 96.8 billion dollars, increasing each 
year since (Sen, 2019). The estimation includes all wound types such as pressure ulcers, diabetes foot 
ulcers, surgical and traumatic wounds and infectious wounds. Several studies and reports suggest that 
in developed countries, the treatment and care for acute and chronic wounds account for 3-6% of the 
total healthcare expenditure (Järbrink et al., 2017). The cost is predicted to rise as the complications 
related to non-healing chronic wounds are on the rise. Several studies have identified key points, 
which are both cost-effective and decrease treatment time, that includes quicker intervention by 
utilizing non-invasive options such as wound dressing products containing smaller doses of 
antibiotics,  antimicrobial or a combination (Frykberg & Banks, 2015). These products have the 
ability to improve life quality, allowing users to have a more social life, by clearing leakage and 
masking malodour, and at the same time providing the ideal environment for their wounds to heal 
quicker, avoiding further complication that might result in hospitalization and in worst case scenario, 
amputation of limbs. The modern wound products also increase patient compliance, as they are less 
invasive that traditional treatment methods (Han & Ceilley, 2017).  
4 Project Limitation  
The project aims to determine the function of GSM by utilizing agar plate and liquid assays. The 
experiments are designed to study the aims and objectives of this project and will not be dealing with 
the minimum inhibitory concentrations (MIC). There is full awareness on the standard procedure to 
conclude and investigate the effects of antibiotics is through determination of MIC.  Different 
concentration of the antibiotics is embedded in the membranes to assess the correct dosage in this 
regard. Clearing zones are used a measure for assessment of bacterial susceptibility.

5.1.1 Sterilization test 
 
When a new batch of membranes is received, it is important to ensure that they are sterile before 
proceeding to the experiments. This is done by preparing LB broth; mixing 8g of LB with 400ml 
deionized water, ensuring gentle mixing before autoclaving the bottles (Figure 6). Autoclaving is a 
sterilization method that uses high-pressure steam. For the autoclaving process to be effective, the 
autoclave must reach and maintain a temperature of 121° C for at least 30 minutes by using saturated 
steam under at least 15 psi of pressure. When the autoclaving process is finalized, the broth is left to 
chill for approximately 30 minutes, subsequently 4 ml of the broth is poured into plastic tubes (Figure 
6). The protective layer on the membranes is then removed with tweezers before they are placed 
inside the tubes, one membrane for each tube. The tubes are then positioned on a rack and put into a 
shaking incubator for 18-20 hours (Figure 6).  
 
 
Figure 6: LB broth and plastic tubes containing 4ml of LB broth and membranes 
 
After an incubation of 18-20 hours in 35oC, the tubes are examined to determine whether the 
membranes are sterile. The tubes are retrieved from the incubator and inspected. If the media in the 
tubes have white cloudy substance in them the sample is not used as it is deemed unsterile and no 
==PAGE:41==
 
40 
further experiments are conducted. If the tubes seem clear after the incubation time, the experiments 
are proceeded.  
 
 
5.2 Agar plate experiments  
 
5.2.1 Culture preparation  
Bacterial cultures are taking from the freezer for each bacterium. Small amount is plated using an 
inoculation loop by streaking gently onto a blood agar plate. The plates are incubated for 18-20 hours 
in 37oC, where individual colonies appear. This is of special importance as it ensures the observer to 
determine whether a contamination has occurred. If the colonies are the same size, no apparent 
contamination has occurred, but if the size differs, the culture is replated (Figure 7). When the plates 
for the eleven bacteria are examined and determined to be viable, media is prepared for the 
experiments. 
 
 
Figure 7: Blood Agar plate with Klebsiella pneumoniae 
 
5.2.2 Media preparation  
Media consisting of LB with agar and BHI with agar is prepared; 6g of agar and 8g of LB is added 
to a 400ml bottle. The bottle is filled with deionized water to the 400ml mark. Similarly, 18.8g of 
BHI is mixed with 6g of agar and the bottle is filled with deionized water to the 400ml mark. The 
bottles are stirred to ensure that the agar and LB or BHI are properly mixed before being autoclaved 
(Figure 8). The ratio of agar to LB or BHI is specified on the LB, BHI and agar packages from the 
producers. LB agar plates are used for 10 of the 11 bacteria; only S. pyogenes is plated on BHI agar 
plates as this particular bacterium is unable to grow on the traditional LB agar plates.

After the autoclaving process is completed, the media is placed in a water bath for 30 minutes to 1 
hour, until the media has reached a temperature of 50 °C. The media is then mixed again by slowly

a measuring cylinder, 65 ml of the media is poured into a petri dish with a diameter of 14 cm. After

5.2.3 McFarland standard 
A saline solution of 0.9% NaCl is prepared by adding 3.6g of NaCl into a 400ml bottle and filling the 
bottle to the 400ml mark. The solution is stirred properly before it is transferred into test tubes 
containing 5ml each. The tubes are then autoclaved before use according to the process described 
above.  The reference sample used is a McFarland standard to standardize to measurable turbidity, 
ensuring uniformity. For the experiment, one colony is taken from the blood agar plate (Figure 7) 
with a cotton swap and placed in a tube with 0.9% saline solution. 
 
5.2.4 Experiment  
The nephelometer is turned on and the McFarland reference sample is used to adjust the turbidity. 
Thereafter, a 5ml test tube of 0.9 % NaCl is opened and a cotton swap is used to take one colony from 
the blood agar plate. The swap is then placed inside the 5ml tube containing 0.9% NaCl. The test tube 
is stirred and placed inside the nephelometer to adjust turbidity. When the turbidity is adjusted, a 
cotton swap is used to transfer the bacterial suspension to the LB or BHI agar plates. The cotton is

6 Results and Discussion  
In this section the results obtained from multiple experiments will be presented. Several different 
experiments are conducted during the course of this project in order to determine the effects and 
susceptibility of the bacterial strains towards antibiotics imbedded in the Glycerol-Silicone membrane 
technology and whether the strains selected grew on the Glycerol in the membranes, irrespective of 
the drug embedded. The tested membranes include Glycerol-Silicone-Triclosan (GSM-TCS), 
Glycerol-Silicone-Tetracycline (GSM-TET), Glycerol-Silicone-Mupirocin (GSM-MUP) and a 
combination thereof. 
The use of regular agar plates allows for visually observing clearing zones that are made by the 
antibiotics embedded into the membranes after incubation. The diameters of the clearing zones are 
measured for data analysis and comparison between the antibiotics and the data is further used in the 
assessment of their function, in comparison to commercially available wound dressing products such 
as Mepilex and Biatain.  
Besides the determination of whether regrowth appears in the clearing zones after the absence of drug 
release and the presence of Glycerol, the re-incubation also serves another purpose related to one of 
the main objectives of this project, determining whether the strains selected grow on Glycerol, which 
is a major component of the Glycerol-Silicone membrane technology, and to determine whether the 
growth is enhanced in the absence of drug release, as the drugs are not continually secreted from the 
membrane in those two weeks.  
The results of the pilot experiments confirmed that the use of 1% in the membranes provided the best 
results, as the tested 0.1% of the drugs gave rise to resistant colonies in S. aureus 8325 (Figure 36) 
and it is observed that the clearing zones were remarkably smaller. Therefore, all following 
experiments entail the use of 1% drug in the Glycerol-Silicone membranes. In the antibiotics 
combinations in the Glycerol-Silicone membranes, the percentage of the specific drug decreases in 
proportion to the number of drugs embedded in that certain membrane, In the membrane where two 
drugs are combined, there is 0.5% of each drug and the percentage further decreases in the 
combination of all three drugs: Glycerol-Silicone-TCS+TET+MUP, as there is only a mere 0.3% of 
each drug.  
In addition to testing the effect of each drug on the bacterial strains selected (Table 1), a mixed culture 
experiment is conducted to determine whether antibiotics in the GSM technology have the capability 
to have a measurable effect on a mixture of six bacterial strains selected in order of their prevalence 
in non-healing chronic wounds. The bacteria are equally divided between Gram-positive and Gram-
==PAGE:50==

negative bacteria; P. aeruginosa, A. baumannii, E. coli 25922, E. faecalis, S. aureus 8325 and S. 
pyogenes.

7 Conclusion  
The aim of this project was to develop suitable assays to determine the function of GSM embedded 
with three different antibiotics on eleven of the most pathogens found in chronic non-healing wounds. 
Furthermore, the positive and negative effect of Glycerol on the pathogens had to be determined. 
Throughout the developed assays and methods for the purposes of this project, the effects of the 
antibiotics in the membranes were determined and with the use of different approaches and assays 
the results were good and consistent.  The GSM technology worked remarkably, with GSM-TCS 
being the most functional. Moreover, it can be concluded that the use of combination of drugs in 
GSM does not provide a substantial effect compared to the single drugs in GSM. It has been proven 
that none of the antibiotics used had synergistic effect. Furthermore, it is observed that the GSM 
worked well under different conditions, proving to be stable and functional regardless of 
environment. In comparison the commercial products tested only had measurable effect in the liquid 
assay, and the downfall of such product is the decreased ability to release their antimicrobial content 
in full potential as massive liquid intake is needed for optimal release.  Thus, it can be concluded that 
commercial products are only suitable for wounds that secrete a substantial amount of exudate.   
The Glycerol in GSM was found to be insignificant in the presence of antibiotics, as the high 
concentration in GSM can have an inhibitory effect on bacterial growth. Bacterial growth was also 
found to be concentration dependent. However, correct diagnosis is essential, as applying GSM 
embedded with drugs that does not inhibit the bacterial consortia in the individual wound, might 
increase bacterial growth. The results obtained during the course of this project closely resemble 
findings in other scientific research. 
8 Future perspectives  
The assays developed and utilized during the course of this project provide sufficient data and 
grounds for further investigation into the effects and function of the GSM technology. The next step 
in such investigation would be designing liquid-based assays where cfu is determined to obtain 
sufficient quantitative data, Subsequently, animal models that are reproducible and closely mimic 
non-healing chronic wounds can be developed. The wounds in such model should include wounds 
that differ both in sizes and structure, as a better understanding can be gained from such study. 
Furthermore, the GSM should be compared to multiple commercial products and such data should be 
compared. Though the results of this experiment do prove the potential of the GSM technology, some 
==PAGE:72==
 
71 
adjustments could be explored more, including lowering the concentration of some drugs such as 
Mupirocin and combining different concentration of the drugs to find the best combination.
