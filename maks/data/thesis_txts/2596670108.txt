Abstract
The highly increasing industry of oﬀshore wind energy in recent years, created the need
of development for oﬀshore wind farm maintenance strategies. Wind power production con-
tributes to the energy mix of many countries, with high density in Denmark, Germany and UK.
Production of electricity from wind farms is highly dependent on weather, thus having accurate
weather forecasts is crucial for both Operations and Maintenance. The scope of this thesis is to
validate such forecasts and show how can they be used for oﬀshore wind farm maintenance.
For the analysis of this thesis the output data from the atmospheric model, Weather
Research and Forecasting model (WRF), coupled online with the wave model, Simulating
Waves Nearshore (SWAN), for spring 2020 is used. The coupled forecasting model issues two
forecasts per day of 72 hours with an output time step of 1 hour, for Denmark.The forecast is
validated against observations in terms of wind speed and signiﬁcant wave heights for the west
coast of Denmark. Error statistics of diﬀerent complexity are applied with the aim to analyse
the accuracy of the weather windows provided by the forecast.
The results showed in fact that the output data from the coupled forecasting model can be
used for oﬀshore wind farm maintenance with suﬃcient accuracy. It is shown that the accuracy
is depended on many factors like, the month, the time horizon of the forecast and the desired
weather window length, for which the forecast was issued.
It is also presented how these
validated results can be used in a tool for oﬀshore wind farm maintenance.

Chapter. 1
1.1. Motivation
1.1
Motivation
The development of renewable energy sources had an immense growth in the last three
decades. Wind power electricity generation accounted for 40.2%of the total consumption in
Denmark for 2018, according to the Danish Energy Agency and described in [1] . These energy
sources are highly dependent of weather, thus they can not generate electricity when the wind
speed is low or cloudy. Due to this fact when they generate they also have priority to the power
grid. Because of this dependency of weather the electricity production is higher when there are
strong winds and less in lower winds.
The target of the European Wind Energy Association is to reach 230 GW of installed wind
power in Europe by the end of 2020, of which 40 GW shall be generated by oﬀshore wind
power plants as stated in [2]. The highest expectation of oﬀshore wind farms being in countries
like Denmark, Netherlands, Germany and the UK, due to the high wind resource, availability
of space, low visual and noise pollution according to [3].
The maintenance of the wind farms is known to be an important part of the cost of energy
[4] from oﬀshore wind farms. All this growth of oﬀshore wind farms lead to great dependency
of weather forecasts for oﬀshore Operations and Maintenance. One of the requirements being
how well these forecast can predict weather windows for the maintenance of wind farms, as
used in this thesis.
This need of having accurate weather forecasts resulted in the intensive use of short term
numerical weather prediction (NWP) forecasts in this new industry. The importance of this
accuracy most obviously can be observed in the operation part.
With accurate prediction
resulting in accurate production of electricity, thus a more proﬁtable power plant.
1.2
Oﬀshore wind farm maintenance
The main subject in this thesis is to describe the analysis of weather windows used in
oﬀshore wind farm maintenance. A series of forecast validation is made in order to quantify
the accuracy and length of these windows, which helps ﬁnding the level of access a wind farm
has for the maintenance tasks.
2
==PAGE:14==
Chapter. 1
1.2. Oﬀshore wind farm maintenance
Once the turbines are deployed at sea, maintenance becomes a diﬃcult process compared
to the case if they were installed onshore. One of the main factors is the limited and constrained
access. In order to engage in a maintenance task the meteorological conditions should consist
of periods were the wind speed and signiﬁcant wave heights are under certain threshold value.
These values should be low and long enough for the maintenance tasks to be successfully
completed.
The access to the turbine is most often done by the boat-to-ladder, thus calm
weather conditions are a necessity, as speciﬁed in [5].
Due to the Oﬀshore wind energy Operations and Maintenance (O&M) being a young
research area few tools exist that are fully matured and reliable, while many of this kind of
tools are currently under development to support activities in this ﬁeld. This in turn causes a
lack of real data, thus limited options for validation and veriﬁcation for this type of models.
These models often include the transportation method from the maintenance port and to the
farm area. Diﬀerent kind of vessels are used e.g heavy-lift vessels and normal maintenance,
sometimes even helicopters when the weather conditions are extremely bad. These vessels often
have transfer of personnel to the turbine platform thus having well predicted weather windows
is crucial in these processes, since they take diﬀerent amount of time to complete.
The maintenance tasks for an oﬀshore wind farm are done when: a failure occurs to one
of the wind turbine parts, following a preventive and yearly maintenance plans well established
before the wind farms was built or emergency failures. The most common ﬁve failure categories
as deﬁned in [6]: i) manual reset, ii) minor repair, iii) medium repair, iv) major repair and v)
major replacement.
In order to decide whether to engage in performing maintenance tasks is fully dependent
on these weather windows. The forecasting of these weather windows is crucial and needs to
be accurate. In the case where these forecasts are completely accurate and a look ahead of time
is of inﬁnite length, vessels would be deployed only if they are certain the maintenance task at
hand can be totally ﬁnished. Because this can only happen theoretically the look ahead of time
is ﬁnite in reality and due to this fact the transportation vessels need to postpone the operation
from time to time with needing to access the respective turbine more than once for each repair,
as stated in [4].

Chapter. 1
1.3. Scope
1.3
Scope
The main purpose of this thesis can be synthesized as:
Can a coupled wind and wave forecasting system accurately supply weather windows used
in oﬀshore wind farm maintenance?
A second objective of this thesis can be outlined as:
Can these weather windows be implemented after validation in a tool for oﬀshore wind
farm maintenance?
In order to answer these questions output data from the coupled atmosphere-wave forecast-
ing system set up at Technical University of Denmark Wind Energy Department was analysed
for three consecutive months. It is important for oﬀshore maintenance to know, how long the
weather windows are, in order to plan a future maintenance task. These weather windows can
be deﬁned as periods during which the wind speed at diﬀerent heights and the signiﬁcant wave
heights on a path from the maintenance port to the oﬀshore wind farm, do not exceed a certain
threshold, so the transportation to the wind farm or repairs at the farm can be performed.
The main goal of this thesis is to illustrate the potential and proof of concept of the coupled
forecasting system and how it could be used in such applications, rather than developing an
exact process for oﬀshore wind farm maintenance.

2.1
Forecasting models
In the following subsections a description of the forecasting models both the atmospheric
and wave models, together with an introduction to numerical weather prediction models (NWP)

2.1.1
Numerical weather prediction NWP
The complexity of the atmosphere makes it complicated to be modeled mathematically,
the method used for this is NWP models, which is a method of forecasting the weather based on
current observations. Any numerical model has as base the governing equations which in this
case are the equations that describe the ﬂuid and the ﬂow of it. These equations are translated
into computer code in combinations with other parametrizations of process and integrated based
on initial and boundary conditions on a computational domain (geographical area).
These numerical weather prediction models can be set up for diﬀerent geographical areas
globally GFS and regionally which can include a few countries or even smaller regions WRF,
depending on the deﬁned computational domain and diﬀerent temporal resolution (weeks to
hours). Other important aspects that can be deﬁned are time horizon (the period of time in the
future for which forecasts are made), computational time and number of runs.
NWP models usually have three main components, the dynamical core, which represents
the adiabatic non-viscous ﬂow, the physical equations describing variability of the meteorolog-
ical processes (e.g turbulence and radiation) and the information gathering software code, as
noted in [7]. The results of the NWP is never only for one variable e.g wind, but rather for a
detailed forecast of the atmospheric state at a given time.
Output accuracy of the NWP model is important for the forecast data used in this thesis,
and it depends on the ability of the model to resolve the atmospheric processes on a range of
spatio-temporal scales, as stated in [8].The NWP model used in this paper is the online coupled
of Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) atmospheric model coupled to the wave model,
Simulating WAves Nearshore (SWAN) model.

2.1.2
Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model
The Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) Model is a mesoscale numerical weather
prediction system designed for both atmospheric research and operational forecasting applica-
tions. It features two dynamical cores, a data assimilation system, and a software architecture
supporting parallel computation and system extensibility. The WRF model is non-hydrostatic
and quasi compressible, developed by the Mesoscale and Microscale Meteorology Laboratory
(MMM) of NCAR. The model includes two dynamical cores, referred to as the ARW (Ad-
vanced Research WRF) core and the NMM (Nonhydrostatic Mesoscale Model) core. For this
project the Advanced Research WRF (ARW) which is a conﬁguration of WRF that includes
ARW dynamics solver together with other compatible components to produce a simulation as
described in [9]. In Figure 2.1 the ARW system components are shown.
Figure 2.1: WRF system components.
2.1.2.1
WRF parameters
WRF uses diﬀerent kinds of Planetary boundary schemes (PBL), which are the parameterization
of the atmospheric boundary layer (such derivation of a scheme can be noted in Appendix A).
WRF calculates the following main atmospheric parameters :
• The compressible non-hydrostatic Euler equations.
• Coriolis and curvature terms.
• The map projection used is Lambert comformal

• Mass-based terrain following coordinate and the vertical grid-spacing is variable with the
height.
• Nesting is achieved for one way, two way with multiple nests and moving nests.
• Arakawa C-grid staggering.
• Lateral boundary conditions for the real and idealized cases.

eterization.
• Advection for moisture, scalar and Turbulent kinetic energy (TKE).
2.1.2.2
Initial conditions and domain
WRF is initialized with data forecasts from the The Global Forecast System (GFS). GFS
is a global weather forecasting model with a Δ푥distance of 28 km between the grid points,
stated in [10]. The GFS is run by National Centers for Environmental Prediction (NCEP)
which is a department of U.S National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) and
delivers 4 forecasts per day, to a future time horizon of 16 days, and forecasts on 64 levels in the
atmosphere. The boundary conditions for the outer domain in WRF are set from the output of
the GFS forecasting system. The outer domain (geographical area) that WRF forecasts for data
used in this thesis is shown in Figure 2.2, with the Δ푥distance between grid points of 18 km.

2.1.3
Simulating WAves Nearshore(SWAN) model
SWAN is a state-of-the-art third-generation wave model which computes random, short-
crested wind-generated waves in coastal regions and inland waters, with fully spectral developed
frequencies and directions. It is developed and maintained by Delft University of Technology,
Netherlands. SWAN accounts for the following physical processes: Wave generation by wind,
Shoaling and refraction due to currents and depth, Three- and four-wave non-linear interactions,
White-capping, Bottom friction, Depth-induced breaking, Transmission through and reﬂection
from obstacles and Diﬀraction.
SWAN computations are made on a curvi-linear co-ordinate system, using the same outer
domain as WRF.
SWAN provides among others the following output: One and two dimensional wave

Motives for forecast validation
When working with forecast data an important step is to be able to quantify the quality
and reliability of this data, in order to make an analysis for this, diﬀerent statistical measures
are used. The range of forecast data is large with a few examples being weather forecasts,
economics and ﬁnance. The reasons for forecasting are many and three main ones are reminded
here as: monitoring the forecast quality- in this category falls the accuracy of the forecasts and
improvement over time. Improving forecast quality- the initial stage of getting better is to reﬂect
on what went wrong. Lastly to compare the quality of diﬀerent forecasting models- this leads
to a better understanding about the diﬀerences between models in the same time giving insight
of which models to choose, according to [15].
Probably the most development this process has seen is in the atmospheric sciences as
stated in [16], while other ﬁelds have also made great progress(e.g ﬁnance, economics and
medical diagnosis).The basis for forecast veriﬁcation contains the investigation of the joint
distribution of forecasts and observation (Murphy and Winkler,1987).
The data validation for this particular case is made for a nonprobalistic forecast for discrete
predicatands in the sections where a threshold analysis is made , meaning that a single outcome
will occur for the forecast, while expressing no uncertainty in comparison with a probabilistic
forecast.

4.1. Oﬀshore wind farm maintenance
4.1
Oﬀshore wind farm maintenance
The maintenance of oﬀshore wind farms is greatly dependant on the accessibility of the
wind farm, thus of suitable weather windows. Up to one third of the total cost of the wind farm
is made up by the operations and maintenance, according to [4], and operators try to minimize
this cost with diﬀerent maintenance strategies.
Usually each oﬀshore wind farm has a maintenance port predeﬁned in the operator’s
maintenance strategy, since most of these tasks are executed by vessels. In order to undertake
an oﬀshore maintenance all the spare parts and personnel need to be transported from the port
to the oﬀshore wind farm location. In order to execute these tasks a weather window is required
of the length that at a minimum covers the travel time and repair time at the wind turbine, on
the path from the port to the wind farm. Successive windows can be used if the task requires
longer time as explained below.
In Table 4.1 the categories of corrective maintenance are shown per task, the logistic times
are the times calculated for the need of postponing and accessing the wind farm area again at a
later time, because of the motives speciﬁed in Section 1.2. The Table is only shown for having
an idea of what the duration of diﬀerent maintenance oﬀshore task are.

5.1. Conclusion and discussion
5.1
Conclusion and discussion
The goal of this thesis was to explore how well a coupled wind and wave forecasting system
could predict weather windows used in oﬀshore wind farm maintenance. It was also shown
how a potential tool for oﬀshore wind farm maintenance using these predicted weather windows
could look like. In order to achieve this the output of the coupled wind-wave forecasting model
routinely run at DTU Wind Energy for 3 months for the year 2020 was validated with the
corresponding observations for the same period. The observations for wind speed at 10 m and
100 m height were used, the observations were taken from the met mast at Høvsøre testing
facility. The observations for signiﬁcant wave heights were extracted from the Fino 3 mooring
platform.
In the process of validation diﬀerent error measurements like Bias, MAE and MSE were
applied. The forecast was also validated for more application oriented cases, with the help of
contingency tables and skill scores. Finally, it was explored how the forecast could be applied
in a tool for oﬀshore wind farm maintenance.
Returning back to the main question that this study tries to answer:
Can a coupled wind and wave forecasting system accurately supply weather windows used
in oﬀshore wind farm maintenance?
In the end the answer to the question is yes, with suﬃcient accuracy based on the statistical
analysis made in Chapter 3, however for the accuracy a number of factors play an important
role. First of all the time horizon that the forecast is issued is very important, together with the
time period of which the maintenance task should be done. It was shown that for March the
performance of the forecasting model was lower than May, noted from the lower skill scores
obtained in March. Lastly the length of the weather windows and especially when the combine
parameters were tested, had a big inﬂuence on the performance of the model, based on the lower
skill scores in this case.
In Chapter 3 a statistical analysis was made starting with very general statistical measures
mentioned above following with a thorough description of the more speciﬁc statistics. In Section
3.2 3 error measures were used, it was seen that the forecast was best for May compared with the
