Abstract 
 
This master thesis aims to assess the level of circular economy of a hospital construction 
project in France. The system is studied thanks to a mass flow analysis and an energy flow 
analysis. The goal is to assess the circularity trough indicators only related to mass or energy.  
As the project in under construction, many data are available; more than if the building has 
been built since years. Another particularity of the project is that it is located on an island, 
which makes the context of construction (transport, material purchase, etc.) a bit different 
from what is done usually.  
The mass flow analysis shows that the main component is concrete; and its recycling is 
determining for the global impact of the project. The analysis revealed that most of the 
circularity is created through open-loops; that is to say that they are not completely part of 
the system. 
The energy flow analysis gives results that are highly depending on the energy supplier and 
thus depending on the energetic policy of the country. Indeed, the main energy used is 
electricity; it implies renewable energies but in a portion that differs from one location to 
another. The only significant closed-loop detected is the one created by the solar heat pump 
installed to supply the building in domestic hot water.   
A life cycle cost analysis has been made to study the financial impact of the circular economy 
implemented in the project. It showed that the recycling of waste is valuable for the 
construction company thanks to lower waste treatment costs for recycled materials.

1 Introduction 
 
1.1 Goals and objectives 
 
The goal of the present master thesis consists in the assessment of the circularity of a 
construction project and the possibility to increase this circularity. The object of the study is 
an existing project taken as a reference to study the opportunities of improvement and 
implementation of a circular process concerning mass and energy flows and life cycle costs. 
The project in question is the reconstruction of a hospital that is being built in France; the 
particularity of this project is that it located on an island called Belle-Île-en-Mer in Britany.  
This master thesis has been made in partnership with the construction company BOUYGUES 
BÂTIMENT GRAND OUEST. The insular context of the construction project led the company 
to evaluate the amount of material that needed to be transported onto the island. Thus it 
enables me to have detailed data in order to evaluate the circularity of the project.  
The Life Cycle Analysis of the project will be made through a Mass Flow Analysis. With the 
software STAN, all the data about products going in and out the system “Hospital” will be 
gathered from material manufacturing to final demolition. The circularity of the project will 
be assessed depending on the flows that are going to be found. The Life Cycle of the Hospital 
will also be studied in terms of energy consumption using an Energy Flow Analysis also 
presented on a STAN diagram. Finally, a Life Cycle Cost analysis will permit to see the 
financial impact of the circularity. 
This report deals about the context of the study in general and then in particular by 
presenting the construction project. Then, the methodology will be detailed to explain the 
rules followed, the source of the data, and the results expected. Finally, the circularity of the 
project will be assessed in terms of mass, energy and life cost according to the indicators 
listed in the methodology paragraph.  
 
1.2 Background 
 
The construction sector is one of the industries that play the biggest role in terms of energy 
use, primary resource consumption and CO2 production, not only in France but in the whole 
Europe and even all the developed countries. Mineral extraction is the most important raw 
material extraction for the construction sector; “in the UK over 200 million tonnes of 
minerals are extracted and consumed by the sector each year, representing 84% of the 
country’s annual mineral extraction” (1). In term of energy, 500 million of MWh/year are 
attributed to the residential housing sector in the UK (1), and the building sector represents 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
40% of the primary energy usage in France (2). In Switzerland, construction and demolition 
(C&D) waste is estimated at over 13 million tonnes (Mt) per year and is then “the most 
important waste stream in terms of mass (58% of total)” (3). Besides, the building sector 
accounts for 33% of the greenhouse gases annual emission in France (2) and 28% in the UK 
(1).  
Nevertheless, according to a study from Switzerland, “most of it [the waste] is recycled, for 
example, as concrete granulate and other mineral filler” (3). Moreover, “according to the 
United Nations Environment Program, buildings also have the greatest potential for 
significantly reducing GHG [greenhouse gases] emissions compared to other major emitting 
sectors.” (2) 
Thus, some ideas and elements need to be implemented to contribute to the evolution of 
the building sector to better environmental impacts. It can first concern the construction 
materials; indeed, nowadays most of the buildings are built in concrete and the demolition 
waste could be used but it is mainly not the case. Now, more and more studies are made to 
build tall buildings with a wood structure; it is a more ecological material for manufacturing, 
construction and demolition. For example, in Bordeaux in 2020, a 50-meters tower made at 
80% in wood will rise (4). Then, another idea is to design the building to make future 
renovations easier. Because the use and the need of the owner move faster, the design of 
the building should include possibilities of changes. And make the renovation waste easily 
sortable and recyclable.  
To decrease the impact on the environment and evolve to another economic model, a model 
is trying to be developed more and more in all the domains: circular economy.  
 
1.3 Circular economy 
 
What is circular economy (CE)? 
According to Ellen MacArthur Foundation (5), a circular economy is “looking beyond the 
current take, make and dispose extractive industrial model, [it] is restorative and 
regenerative by design”. When the Linear Economy extracts resources, consumes them and 
throws them, the Circular Economy creates a loop of resources by reusing, repairing and 
recycling (Figure 1). The value created within the system is not anymore based on the 
consumption of goods but on the use of them (6). The expression “Cradle to cradle” is used 
to show the loop, the circle that rules the use of goods. 
  
 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
 
Figure 1 – Linear VS Circular Economy (7) 
 
CE is following three principles described by Ellen MacArthur Foundation. First, to be 
circular, an economy must “preserve and enhance natural capital by controlling finite stocks 
and balancing renewable resource flows” (5). Then, it has to “optimise resource yields by 
circulating products, components and materials in use at the highest utility at all times in 
both technical and biological cycles“ (5). And finally, the economy needs to “minimise 
systematic leakage and negative externalities” that lower the system effectiveness (5). 
In order to improve the circularity of an economy, the systems taking part of it need to be 
studied and analysed. Such a tool is called Life Cycle Assessment, abbreviated in LCA.  
 
What is LCA?  
“LCA studies the environmental aspects and potential impacts throughout a product’s life 
(i.e. cradle-to-grave) from raw material acquisition through production, use and disposal. 
The general categories of environmental impacts needing consideration include resource 
use, human health and ecological consequences” (8). In other words, Life Cycle Assessment 
is the evaluation of a system from its birth to its death in terms of environmental issues. This 
system can be an object (an aluminium can, a phone or a car), a factory or even a whole city; 
but it can also be a particular sector, from example Food Industry. The study can focus on 
one or several environmental issues assessed with indicators like CO2 emissions.  
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
The present report studies the circularity of a construction project. In the literature, LCA run 
on buildings are existing. Some of them are summed up below, with mainly the ideas linked 
to Mass Flow Analysis and Inventory Analysis: 
 Low Energy Building in Spain (9) 
The report Use of LCA as a tool for building eco-design – A case study of a low energy 
building in Spain by I. Zabalza and al. is presenting a LCA method applied to a residential 
building of 60 homes in Spain. The inventory analysis gives a total weight of 8360 Tonnes for 
a surface of 8067 m².  Then the LCA is run and some ideas are given to simplify LCA on 
buildings; for example, it is said that construction and end-of-life stages are usually 10%-15% 
of the total energy impact, so they could be kept out of the system boundary. However, in 
our case, the assessment of circularity would not be complete. Then they propose to limit 
the production stage to the structural elements of the buildings, and leave the energy 
systems of the building out of the inventory analysis. And finally, they suggest to leave 
maintenance and repair processes out of the use stage. We will see further in our study that 
these proposals make the study wrong since many impacts are neglected.  
 Subsurface Buildings (10) 
This example is a master thesis report written by R.M.C Veugen and entitled Environmental 
Impacts of Subsurface Buildings. A comparative LCA is made between a subsurface and an 
aboveground supermarket. The software SimaPro has been used to evaluate the 
environmental impact of several scenarios.  
 UK residential sector (1) 
The paper Environmental impacts of the UK residential sector: life cycle assessment of houses 
written by R.M. Cuellar-Franca and A. Azapagic is a LCA study of three common types of 
house in the UK (detached, semi-detached and terraced). The conclusions give that the use 
stage of the building is the largest contributor of environmental impacts; and the best way of 
reducing the impact is the recycling of building materials at the end-of-life stage. We will 
assess this idea in our case study.  
 
Nevertheless, the environmental impacts indicators are not part of the present study. 
Indeed, the purpose of the present master thesis is not to make the LCA of the construction 
project. The goal is to assess the circularity of the building life through other types of 
considerations than ecological issues. The assessment of the circularity will be done based 
on Mass Flow Analysis.

1.4 Mass Flow Analysis 
 
What is Mass Flow Analysis (MFA)? 
MFA is the assessment of the flows and stocks of materials going through a system defined 
in space and on a certain period of time. It connects the inputs, the outputs and the stocks of 
materials. MFA is ruled by the law of the conservation of matter. Thus, MFA is controlled by 
a material balance comparing inputs to outputs, while taking account of socks within a 
process. (11) 
Some terms are to be defined in order to clearer the explanations: 
 The term material stands for both substances and goods. A substance is defined as a 
single type of matter, for example iron, glass or oxygen. The word “goods” stands for 
both substances and mixtures of substances. Goods have an economic value which 
can be positive (car, fuel, wood) or negative (waste, sewage sludge) (11).  
 A process is defined as a transport, transformation, or storage of materials. Processes 
are linked by flows (mass per time) or fluxes (mass per time and cross section) of 
materials. 
 Stocks are defined as material reservoirs (mass) within the system. They are part of a 
process. For steady-state conditions, the mass law implies Input = Stock + Output.  
 Flows/fluxes across systems boundaries are called imports or exports. Flows/fluxes 
of materials entering a process are named inputs, while those exiting are called 
outputs. A system comprises a set of material flows, stocks, and processes within a 
defined boundary. The smallest possible system consists of just a single process. 
Examples of common systems for investigations by MFA are: a region, a municipal 
incinerator, a private household, a factory, a farm, etc. (11) 
 The system boundary is defined in space and time. It can consist of geographical 
borders (region) or virtual limits (e.g., private households, including processes serving 
the private household such as transportation, waste collection, and sewer system). 
When the system boundary in time is chosen, criteria such as objectives, data 
availability, appropriate balancing period, residence time of materials within stocks, 
and others have to be taken into account.  
 
The symbols used in MFA diagrams are shown in Figure 2. Diagrams with such symbols will 
be made for the Inventory Analysis for Mass and Energy Flows, thanks to the software STAN.

MFA is usually used for one particular type of material (12), but here the MFA will be run for 
several elements: all the different materials used for the construction of the Hospital. It is a 
multi-flows system. Thus, flows will not represent one element, but several goods gathered 
by their start point and end point.  
 
1.5 MFA for circularity assessment 
 
As mentioned before, the goal of this master thesis is to assess circularity with MFA. Indeed, 
even if an inventory of mass flows is necessary to run a complete LCA, it is quite rare to 
study circularity solely with mass. But a bridge between MFA and CE has been underlined by 
C. Bellstedt in her master thesis Material Flow Analysis for a Circular Economy Development 
in 2015: “After elaborating on the importance of taking stock from a scientific, resource 
management and policy perspective, considerations for the importance of creating a 
relationship between MFA and CE were described extensively. […] the bridge between the 
two fields is formed by a co-dependence: the CE provides MFA with a sense of direction 
from its values and MFA generates transformational knowledge for the advancement of the 
CE. […] The actual power [of MFA] stems from transformational knowledge that is now 
gained from multi- and cross-disciplinary research. Over and above, this study is the first 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
MFA waste-related application study that is connected to the built or urban environment 
and the circular economy. Other such studies and multidisciplinary research will become 
more important, because they allow to better accommodate and mirror the complexity of 
our world, instead of examining issues in an isolated state” (13)(Pages 28-29). To sum up, 
the idea here is to say that any LCA study begins with a Mass Flow Analysis, and MFA is the 
tool that links all the multidisciplinary studies.  
But, to go further, we want MFA to be an indicator of circularity, not only a step before 
circularity assessment. Indicators and assessment methods will be detailed further in the 
report. The values directly extracted from MFA will be detailed in the paragraph 2.3.2.

2 Methodology 
 
LCA and MFA follow methodologies that have been established through years. In the book 
Life Cycle Assessment – Theory and Practice (14), the main methodological phases of LCA 
and the way they are assessed are described. “The LCA framework operates with four 
separate phases, Goal and scope definition, Inventory analysis, Impact assessment and 
Interpretation.” (14) 
 
The first step is to define the goal of the analysis. Why making the study? For who? For 
which application? Then the scope definition is necessary to define the physical and 
temporal boundaries of the study and select the different processes taken into account.  
 
Once the frame is given, the Inventory analysis can begin. This step is the most important 
one since it gathers all the basic data in terms of inputs (for the resources, the construction 
materials), outputs (such as waste) and the stocks (that are, in our case, the materials 
entering in the composition of the Hospital and staying there until the end-of-life of the 
building). The analysis studies all the processes playing a role in the system and the flows 
linking them. The inventory analysis relies on data coming from different sources. They can 
be found on databases for general products, they can come from estimations and 
calculations, and they can also be a result of measures. The outcome of the inventory 
analysis is the Life Cycle Inventory (LCI), a list of quantified physical elementary flows for the 
product system. 
 
Finally, as defined in Life Cycle Assessment – Theory and Practice, “the impact assessment 
translates the physical flows and interventions of the product system into impacts on the 
environment using knowledge and models from environmental science”. Nevertheless, for 
this step, the work will not be the same. Indeed, the goal of the present study is NOT to 
assess the environmental impact of the Hospital but to assess its circularity through a Mass 
Flow Analysis. Thus, this final phase will consist in finding and evaluating some circularity 
indicators.  
 
The general methodology is kept similar and LCA is an iterative process. That means that 
each stage is adapted and adjusted with a constant interpretation of the outputs. As showed 
in Figure 7, LCA involves many feedback loops between the different phases of the LCA. The 
impact assessment is used in refining the inventory analysis and both of these phases can 
help to adjust the scope definition.  
 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
 
Figure 7 - Framework of LCA modified from the ISO 14040 standard (14) 
 
The results have to answer the question(s) raised in the goal definition. The interpretation 
must be done with the goal and scope definitions in mind and respect the restrictions 
previously described, for example in terms of temporal assumptions. The interpretation 
considers both results of the inventory analysis and the impact assessment.  
 
Finally, sensitivity and uncertainty analysis are run to check the entire process in order to 
identify the key figures or key assumptions of the study and the data that are associated 
with the largest uncertainties. Each phase of the methodology provides feedback to the 
previous phases of the study and helps target the next iteration of the LCA. Following the 
impact assessment, the parts of the product system that contribute most strongly to the 
total results can be identified, and the chosen boundaries of the product system can be 
tested.  
 
2.1 Goal and Scope definition 
 
Goal definition 
This study is a Master Thesis project and is overseen by DTU. Like described in the book of 
Michael Z. Hauschild and al. Life Cycle Assessment – Therory and Practice, this type of study 
“is not to be used to support decisions and is of a purely descriptive nature. It is 
documenting what has already happened or what will happen due to a decision that has 
already been taken. Therefore, the presence of the LCA study will not lead to changes on 
other systems.” (14) The main target audience is consequently the teachers; even if the 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
results and the approach can be used with precaution for other research in that field. The 
main actor and the main source of data is Bouygues Bâtiment Grand Ouest, the construction 
company.  
The goal of this study is to assess the circularity of the Hospital project along its entire 
lifetime thought mass and energy flow analysis and life cycle cost. The intended applications 
of the results are multiple. First it is to identify the parts of the system that contribute the 
most to the total mass or to the energy consumption. It is called “Hot spot identification”. 
Thus we know where the changes must be done to be efficient. Then, it will be possible to 
find construction materials that could reduce the overall energy consumption or increase the 
recycling. Another applications of the results is to observe the loops created in the system 
and then work on these loops to improve the circularity of the model, in terms of mass or 
energy. Finally, it would also be interesting to see how the Life Cycle Cost (LCC) can be 
impacted by circularity and then establish some alternative possibilities to lower the costs; it 
is called “What-if scenarios”.  
 
Scope Definition 
The deliverables of this study are first the Life Cycle Inventory, given via a Mass Flow Analysis 
and an Energy Flow Analysis. The Life Cycle Cost of the project will also be established. The 
object of the study is a residential/hospital building of 7700 m² for 30 years in Brittany in 
France. It has been designed to last 30 years. The construction is made for a total budget of 
19 781 720 €. The building needs to fulfil the ongoing building regulations in terms of 
thermal insulation, fire safety, natural area, etc. Note that the particularities of this building 
(function, location) make it delicate to compare with LCAs run on other buildings.  
The boundary of the system is limited to the surface of the hospital; that is to say that the 
garden and the roads around are not taken into account. The temporal boundary is 
delimited by the manufacturing of the construction materials to the demolition of the 
Hospital. Most of the values will be given in Tonnes for the MFA, in kWh for the Energy Flow 
Analysis and in Euros (€) for the Life Cycle Cost. The building model, to quantify mass and 
energy flows, will be established following the standard EN15978. This model, as well as the 
Reference Study Period (RSP) will be detailed in the next paragraph.  
 
2.2 Inventory Analysis 
 
The goal of the inventory analysis is to obtain a Life Cycle Inventory (LCI), that is to say a list 
of elementary flows that link the processes occurring in the system. The first step of the 
inventory analysis is thus to identify these processes. Then the collection of data, from 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
different sources, can be established. With these data, the LCI model is made and finally the 
results are reported.    
 
2.2.1 General methodology 
 
2.2.1.1 Identify processes 
 
As said before, the first step of an inventory analysis is to identify processes for the model of 
the product system (14). The entire life of the building needs to be cut into smaller stages. 
According to the standard EN 15978 – Sustainability of construction works, Assessment of 
environmental performance of buildings and Calculation method (15), the different stages 
for a building assessment are as depicted in Figure 8.  
 
 
Figure 8 – Simplified display of modular information for the different stages (in bold) of the 
building assessment from EN 15978 (15) 
 
The first stage, called PRODUCT stage, concerns, in the case of the present study, all the 
construction materials; from raw materials to the manufacturing of finished products. The 
second stage is the CONSTRUCTION PROCESS stage, including the transport of materials to 
the site. Then comes the USE stage; it gathers all the maintenance and refurbishment 
process needed during the life of the building. Final the END-OF-LIFE stage is the demolition 
of the building and the management of the waste produced. All these stages will be detailed 
in the following paragraphs.  
An additional process is added and concerns the supplementary information; benefits or 
loads beyond the system boundaries, such as energy export for example. In the context of 
the Hospital project, the solar cells will be part of that process since it is energy produced by 
the building and directly consumed by the same building. Thus, it does not enter into 
account for this stage.

The first source of data is the construction company Bouygues Bâtiment Grand Ouest 
(BBGO). They run studies to prepare the construction of the project (all the construction

2.2.1.3 Construction LCI model: Mass 
 
Now the processes have been explained and the data collection is planned, the LCI model 
can be constructed. Each of the stages listed previously is transformed into a block in the LCI 
model. The processes are linked by flows. Thus a process can have one or several flows 
coming in or out of the process: inflows and outflows. When the sum of inflows is different 
from the sum of outflows, the process contains a stock.  
Figure 13 below is the LCI model of the Hospital project for Mass Flow Analysis; it has been 
drawn thanks to the software STAN. The system is delimited by the dotted-line rectangle. 
There are five processes in the system; they correspond to the different stages of a building. 
To simplify the reading of the model, several rules have been applied. The inputs are 
entering the process from the left. The raw materials are entering the system from the 
symbol “I” in a round. The outflows, excluding waste, are going out from the right. The waste 
is leaving the process at the bottom; the yellow flows are disposal waste (symbol “E” in a 
round), while the green flows represent the waste that is going to be recycled. Besides, for a 
better understanding, the process previously called PRODUCT in now called 
MANUFACTURING.  
 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
 
Figure 13 – Frame of the Mass Flows diagram obtained for the hospital project 
 
==PAGE:30==

Among the outflows of the DECONSTRCUTION process, the concrete has been separated 
from the recycled waste and the disposal waste. Indeed, the concrete will certainly be 
around 80% of the total composition of the building, according to the study run by E. Hoxha 
and al. in their article Influence of construction material uncertainties on residential building 
LCA reliability (2). Among all the buildings they studied, a majority of them have a portion of 
concrete (gravel, sand and cement) between 70 and 90%, as show in Figure 14. They found 
that “Greater variability of materials is observed in the single-family houses than in the 
multi-family buildings, where the main material is concrete. The structure of the multi-family 
buildings is made of reinforced concrete, but the projects differ in the types of external and 
internal walls, windows, doors and technical equipment.” (2) 
 
 
Figure 14 – Mass distribution of the components used in building projects. Category “Rest” 
include: PVC, paint, polyethylene, bitumen, porcelain, acrylic, enamelled, stone, aluminium, 
copper, windows, and solar hot water panels. Extract from (2) 
 
About recycling, the model shows a process that is out of the system. It is an imaginary 
process symbolizing the recycling of waste. Recycled waste produced by the system is an 
inflow of this process. The secondary use materials that enter the system are coming from 
this process, but there is no physical link between the inflows and the outflow of the 
RECYCLING process. The waste of the system is not the same matter than the one used to 
manufacture the product. Indeed, there is a temporal impossibility since the products are 
first manufactured before being placed in the construction site. Then, the system is handling 
flows that are multi-elements flows, a sum of wood, metal, glass and many other types of 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
materials. Nevertheless, in order to keep the mass balance, the process has a fictive stock; it 
will then symbolize the default or the gain of matter for a complete loop.  
Note that the RECYCLING process creates the circularity of the system; it is thus an open-
loop and not a closed-loop, since the RECYCLING process is out of the studied system.  
 
2.2.1.4 Construction LCI model: Energy 
 
The construction of the LCI model for Energy Flows is different from the Mass Flows since 
there is no transfer of matter from one stage to the other. The principle of conversation of 
matter within the system does not apply, inputs and outputs are not anymore linked. Inputs 
are consumption of energy and outputs are production of energy.  
Figure 15 below synthetizes the model for Mass Flow Analysis that we have drawn on STAN.  
Greens lines are renewable energy, purple lines are a mix of mainly non-renewable energy, 
yellow lines are energy supplied by oil, and finally blue lines are electricity.  
In the same idea as before, the process ENERGY RECOVERY is a way of visualizing the open-
loop created by the system. We will see later that the inputs of this process come from 
energy export during the manufacturing stage (and maintenance because it is in fact linked 
to the manufacturing of the replacement products).  
The CONSTRUCTION stage contains two sub-processes; the transport on the island by boat 
and the construction on-site. The transport by boat is a particularity of the project and it 
consumes oil. The construction works are made with electric tools (directly plugged-in or 
with a battery) and the crane is supplied by electricity. 
During the USE stage, the Hospital will consume electricity, oil and solar power for the HVAC 
system (Heating Ventilating Air Conditioning), plus lights and Domestic Hot Water (DHW). 
We can see that the use of solar panels creates a closed-loop within the system. All these 
consumptions will be detailed further.  
Finally, the DECONSTRUCTION gathers the energy to demolish the building and the energy to 
send the waste on the continent.  
However, electricity is a mix of different sources of energy: renewable, fossil or nuclear. As 
the project is located in France, the French electricity mix is used. In France, 72.3% of the 
electricity come from nuclear power plants, whereas the renewable energies represents 
19.1% (16). Thus an open-loop is hidden into the model. Nevertheless, to simplify in a first 
approach the model, the electricity flows are not split into renewable and non-renewable. A 
more detailed study will be done during the circularity assessment further on the report.

This stage has been split into two parts; one concerns the transport by boat onto the island,

concerns the consumption linked to the construction site.  
 TRANSPORT: 
The majority of the maritime transport was made by the company TMC (Transport Maritime 
Côtier), which owns three boats that travel between Belle-Île-en-Mer and Vannes to 
transport materials for the industrial sector mainly. The manager of the company kindly gave 
me some information about his boats oil consumption. Two of the boats consume 850L of oil 
during one rotation (Outward and return), whereas the third one consumes 1000L per 
rotation. We consider that there is an equal repartition of the travels between the boats; the 
average consumption is then 900 L per rotation; that is to say 450 L for a one-way.  
Concerning the weight of material transported per boat, it is usually around 140 Tonnes, but 
it can climb to 300 Tonnes if it is sand or gravel. We will consider that the boat can handle 
140 Tonnes of the construction materials, except from the concrete. We will consider that it 
has been fictively transported by boats of 300 Tonnes of capacity. Indeed the concrete was 
made on the island, but the sand and cement composing the concrete has necessarily been 
transported on the island one day.  
Knowing that the heating value of diesel oil (18) is 38 080 kJ/L = 10.58 kWh/L, we have in 
total 174 467 kWh of primary energy for the boat transport of the construction material. The 
transport for waste has also been added in the final Inventory Analysis.  
 CONSTRUCTION: 
The electricity consumption has been estimated thanks to an electricity meter dedicated to 
the construction site. Monthly values have been reported and, since this report is written at 
a moment where the construction is not over, the previous consumptions are extrapolated 
to the total estimated duration of the construction site. However, the value from the 
electricity meter concerns the phase of the construction where the consumption is mainly 
due to the crane; in the coming months the crane will be dismantled but more workers with 
more electrical appliances will come on the construction site. Thus, it is estimated that the 
crane consumption and the arrival of new electric tools are approximately balanced, so the 
electricity consumption is almost constant. The electricity meter also take into account the 
consumption of the sanitary and offices settled for the construction work.  
The 22nd of May, the electricity meter indicated 211 732 kWh, which represents the total 
amount of electricity that has been consumed for the construction at this date, since this 
meter has been installed at the right begin of the construction. The construction teams have 
arrived in April 2017, but the crane only in October 2017. Thus, to obtained an average of 
monthly consumptions, we consider that the beginning of the construction correspond to

the arrival of the crane, that is to say 8 months. As a result, the monthly electricity 
consumption is 26 467 kWh. The estimated consumption during the entire period of the 
construction is 476 397 kWh, knowing that the building has to be functional in March 2019.

2.2.2.4 End of life 
 
The standard 15798 says that “the end-of-life stage of a building starts when the building is 
decommissioned and is not intended to have any further use. At this point, the building’s

source of materials, products and building elements that are to be discarded, recovered, 
recycled or reused” (15). This stage takes into account Deconstruction (C1), that includes all 
the on-site operations (deconstruction, dismantling, and demolition) and temporary off-site 
operations; but also Transportation of discarded material (C2), such as the travel to a 
recycling site or to final disposal. It must consider waste processing of reuse, recovery or 
recycling (C3), and finally the last waste processing which is disposal (C4).

Indicators 
Now the Inventory Analysis is done, the assessment of the circularity of this system can 
begin. First of all, we can see that the loop visible on the graphs comes from the RECYCLING 
process. Thus, the first indicators that can assess circularity are based on recycling 
percentage. There is the Recycling Rate that is the portion of waste that is recycled. The 
recycling rate can be done at each stage and on the total life cycle of the building. As 
==PAGE:50==
50 
 
explained in the article of Haupt et al. (3), “Recycling rates (RRs), an indicator for the 
circulating behaviour of materials, are often used as measure for the degree of circularity of 
an economy […]. Whereas CR [collection rate] refers to collected material that enters the 
recycling process, RRs measure the available secondary resources produced from recycling 
processes.” Nevertheless, in our study, RR and CR are assumed to be identical, or in other 
words, we consider that 100% of the waste entering the recycling process is available to be 
used as a secondary resource.  
Nevertheless, this type of circularity occurs out of the system; it is called an open-loop. On 
the contrary, circularity inside the system would be called a closed-loop. M. Haupt  (3) 
explains in her article the necessity of changing from open-loop to closed-loop recycling 
processes. “Rates for closed-loop recycling, which implies that secondary material is recycled 
back into the same product, and open-loop recycling, where the secondary material is used 
to manufacture something that differs from the preceding product, are often not published 
individually, but communicated as one rate. This is problematic because open-loop recycling 
is commonly found in situations where recovered secondary material is used in product 
systems with lower quality requirements. Improving recycling efficiency for a more circular 
economy hence includes a transition from open- to closed-loop recycling”. However, it 
seems easier to implement closed-loop into an industrial process, within a factory with 
precise and detailed steps; whereas the world of construction sites is undergoing hazards 
liked to external elements that makes difficult the reuse of matter. Besides, the time scale of 
a building life is really long and a closed-loop within the system would require to plan things 
over 30 years. But small closed-loops have been found in the Hospital project system and 
will be detailed in the next paragraph.  
Then, M. Haupt is asking about the real purpose of circular economy. “The question remains 
whether the primary goal of the circular economy is the supply of alternative resources from 
secondary sources or the minimization of environmental impact. In the former case, RR is a 
good performance indicator. […]. If the lowering of environmental impacts is the primary 
goal, detailed assessments of the various recycling processes are needed, including the 
modelling of substitution and the associated displacement effects” (3). In the study of the 
Hospital project, the goal is to assess the circularity of the construction; whenever this 
circularity has better impact on the environment or not. Thus, it seems that Recycling Rates 
are good mean to assess this circularity.  
Then, after focusing on waste production, we can now look on the consumption or not of 
raw materials. Indeed, circularity is not only about outflows but also and firstly about 
inflows. So, another indicator of circularity can be the ratio of use of secondary resources. 
This ratio is relevant for the MANUFACTURING process. This ratio can be evaluated as the 
portion of inflows for the MANUFACTURING process that is coming from secondary 
resource. It can also be expressed as a ratio between secondary resource used from new 
products and the amount of waste recycled. Thus, even if the loop is an open-loop, 
==PAGE:51==
51 
 
happening out of the studied system, this ratio can link the outflows with the inflows that 
creates circularity around the Hospital project.  
Finally, Yuan Hu and al. deal about an indicator called Natural resource utilization efficiency. 
“Natural resource utilization efficiency (also known as the primary resource productivity 
(PRP), which refers to the average contribution of the use of natural resources per unit to 
the national economy and is usually expressed as the ratio of GDP in constant prices to the 
amount of primary resources consumed), represents the economic, social and 
environmental consequences generated by using units of natural resources” (page 3) (24). In 
this article, the goal was to assess the circularity of the economy of China; the value mainly 
used to compare the economy of countries is China. Here, the goal is to assess the circularity 
of a building; the unit used to compare building is the meter square. Thus, the indicator PRP 
will be expressed in tonnes/m². In the same way, we can calculate not a primary but a 
secondary resource productivity value. It would be two tools that enables to visualize easily 
the portion of primary resource and secondary resource weight in one meter square of 
building.

2.3.4 Life Cycle Cost 
 
According to M.Z. Hauschild and al. “LCC is an abbreviation for life cycle costing which aims 
to quantify all costs associated with the life cycle of a product that is directly covered by one 
or more of the actors in that life cycle “ (14). The paper Energy Saving through Promotion of 
Life Cycle Assessment in Buildings lists the most common cost groups: “Despite some 
different approaches all regulations have in common to group the expenses in the life cycle 
of a building into the following cost groups: Cost for investment, construction; Cost for 
annually occurring energy use, operation, maintenance and repair; Cost for non-annually 
occurring refurbishment and replacement; Cost for end of life, demolition and disposal “ (8). 
There are two relevant points of view that can be chosen for the study of life cycle costs. 
First, the one of the owner; who has ordered the reconstruction and will use it until its end 
of life. The building has to meet the owner’s needs. The owner will pay the majority of the 
costs linked to the building all along its life. This is the main actor of the Hospital 
reconstruction project. Another interesting point of view is the one of the construction 
==PAGE:54==

company BBGO. With the budget established by the owner, the company has to construct 
the building, which induced many different types of costs. 
The cost of the public contract for the Hospital reconstruction is 19 781 720 € (26). The 
construction company major costs have been identified below: 
- 
Purchasing of construction materials 
- 
Renting and purchasing of construction tools 
- 
Wages of workers, engineers, secretaries, etc.  
- 
Water and electricity consumption on the construction site 
- 
Costs due to the island

the costs. The idea of this paragraph is to estimate the costs or the savings of the different 
loops detected in the mass flow and energy flow analysis, in order to assess the circularity of 
the project through another unit.  
 
Savings due to loop in the MFA 
The open loops in the MFA are created by the recycling of waste. Apart from an obvious 
improvement of the ecological footprint, the recycling has a direct impact on the costs. 
Indeed, during the CONSTRUCTION phase, the construction company has to evacuate the 
waste produced of the site; and there are different costs for the waste treatment depending 
on the type of waste. So it is possible to calculate the savings made by sorting and recycling 
the waste, knowing the cost of treatment and the mass of the waste. The costs of waste 
treatment can also be known for the other stages, apart from the MANUFACTURING stage 
since the costs depends of the company in charge of the recycling and the location of the 
manufacturer.  
A small closed-loop has been identified in the MFA; the reusable shapes for concrete walls 
and floors. The systems are in metal and are rent. It enables to speed the construction and 
consume less plywood. In the case of this project, it is even more relevant to reduce the 
costs of transport and waste of plywood. 
 
Savings due to loops in the EFA 
The open-loops created by the energy recovery during the MANUFACTURING stage cannot 
be studied since no information is available about the conditions of this energy recovery. 
Nevertheless, there is a closed-loop in the EFA that can be studied: the free solar energy

Insular context 
The particularity of this project is the location of the hospital on an island. Thus, it seems 
interesting to see the impact of the insular context on the circularity and also the costs.  
First, the boat transport has a large impact on the total cost of the building. It can be roughly 
estimated based on the total weight of materials brought on the construction site and on the 
public price of maritime transport which is approximately 50€/T. Some material were 
directly bought on the island, (concrete from example), but the price of purchasing was 
obviously higher than the one normally made. Besides, as most of the workers did not leave 
on the island, they needed to find a second accommodation on the island; which induces 
higher wages, and thus a higher total cost of the construction. 
Then, as there is no treatment plant for waste, all the waste needed to the transport by 
boat. Thus the waste treatment on the island is much more expensive than what is made 
usually. The reduction of waste was something important and some solutions have been 
found. For example, all the TRESPA cladding have been pre-cut before the transport to the 
construction site in order to lower the cost of transport and the amount of waste created by 
cut-offs.

This paragraph is here to check the quality of data and assess the representativeness of the 
results. The quality of data is first due to the source; Table 2 – Data sources, specificity and

cycle of the Hospital. The quality of the data is roughly estimated to be low, medium or high. 
The sensitivity analysis of the study is also run. The book Life Cycle Assessment – Theory and 
practice explains that “sensitivity check has the purpose of identifying the key processes and 
most important elementary flows as those elements that contribute most to the overall

contribution analysis (which activities contribute to which environmental impact scores, by

uncertainty to sensitivity of data to detect the focusing issues; where the search needs to be

3.5 Improvements  
 
There are two ways of improving the circularity of the system; increase the flow of the 
existing loops and creating new loops.  
First, we have seen during the Mass Flow Analysis that there is more recycling than 
secondary use materials. Thus the improvement of the circularity can first come from a 
rethinking of the manufacturing processes. MANUFACTURING is the process that produces 
the biggest amount of waste; and has also the worst recycling rate. Nevertheless, if the 
==PAGE:71==
71 
 
totality of the construction materials come from secondary use materials, the amount of 
recycled waste is too low. Thus, the materials used in the project must be easily recyclable. 
In order to reach a zero-waste system, the design of the building need to be rethought. 
There is a necessity of design for renovation, a design meant for future recycling. The 
construction elements needs to be easy to separate to sort each elements. Besides, each 
component has to be recyclable.  
Wood construction is a promising solution in terms of low carbon emission and zero waste 
construction. Indeed, wood waste is completely recyclable. Wood is also less heavy than 
concrete. It does not need excavation and high energy process of extraction like stones.  
In the Energy Flow Analysis, the improvement of circularity would be to increase the part of 
renewable energies. For the USE stage, it concerns a conception choice; other sources of 
energies could have been chosen. A larger use of solar energy was technically possible. 
Moreover, knowing the waste treatment problems of the island, a system of energy recovery 
for organic waste would have been very interesting. Unfortunately, this kind of system 
would have require a coordination of several stakeholders, and would have also been much 
more expensive. For the other stages, the action need to be taken by governments and 
energy suppliers. The sources of electricity are a choice that is independent of the system.  
Besides, each manufacturer is also responsible for the energy use of its firm, and then the 
amount of renewable energy could increase also in the MANUFACTURING stage, as well as 
energy recovery during the manufacturing of products.  
 
To conclude this improvements reflection, it is important to note that circular economy 
cannot be reached in such a small system like a building. Many stakeholders are influencing 
the circularity of the system. Thus, the decision making tool can identify the sources of 
improvement; but the implementation of solutions can be limited by the interaction with the 
other stakeholders.

4 Discussion - Conclusion 
 
In this master thesis, the circularity of a reconstruction project of the Hospital in Belle-Île-en-
Mer has been assessed. It has consisted in the study of the implementation of circular 
processes in terms of mass and energy flows. Using the software STAN where all the data 
gathered, the life cycle of the building has been studied from material manufacturing to final 
demolition and its circularity has been quantified through different indicators. The 
possibilities to increase the circularity have been evocated. The costs related to the circular 
flows have been calculated in order to evaluate the benefits of Circular Economy. The 
impacts of the particular insular context of the project has also been discussed. 
 
4.1 Limits of the methodology 
 
The methodology used during this study can be discussed; first this study is not a complete 
LCA, the circularity is assessed on non-environmental indicators. The considerable weight 
taking by only concrete can erase the other flows impacts. Nevertheless, it is true that the 
main component of the Hospital is concrete; as it is the case in most of the buildings 
construct nowadays, at least in France.  
The methodological choices induces necessarily limitations. One of the limitations of this 
study is the lack of data, or more precisely the difference of quality of the data. Indeed, 
some of the elements will be quite detailed because the advancement of the project is far 
enough to have good data; but some other elements will be more uncertain due to the 
necessity of estimation. The construction of a Hospital takes time; the first draws have been 
made in 2016, the building construction will end in 2019 and the use of the Hospital will 
certainly last over 30 years. It is then difficult to have a homogeneity in the data. Besides, 
the refurbishment and renovation impacts have been calculated with the data of the 
construction products; but in 30 years, the products will not be the same. 
Then, the fact that the flows are multi-elements flows can decrease the use of the results 
because all the materials data are merged. Nevertheless, the elements composing a building 
are so numerous, it would have been difficult to present the results and have a global vision 
on them if each type of material would have been separated. Note that the elements in large 
quantities have been underlined in the results, such as concrete. 
Also, the sources of data are really fluctuating in terms of quality, because many 
stakeholders are gravitating around the project. And as the Hospital was under construction, 
the different parts of the building was not all as much advanced.  
Moreover, some elements were not taken into account, since they have been considered as 
negligible, like screws for example. Also, the tools used during the construction have not 
==PAGE:73==
73 
 
been counted in the system since they are the propriety of the construction company and 
they are used and reused for many other projects; however, it is still a flow a matter.  
Concerning the results, the Contribution Analysis in Figure 35 and Figure 36 shows that most 
of the flows can be considered as valid, apart from the end-of-life stage that is highly 
uncertain and really sensitive.  
 
4.2 Validity of the methodology 
 
Thus, MFA can be used to optimize systems. It is a decision support tool. Circularity can be 
assessed in terms of mass. We have seen that a study of mass flows, linked to a cost analysis 
could support decision to reach financial goals.  As said in the Life Cycle Cost Analysis, the 
loop created thanks to the recycled materials is a source of economical savings; indeed, the 
policy of waste treatment encourages companies to sort their waste by applying different 
prices on the different waste types.  
Nevertheless for environmental consideration, MFA is not enough. This limit has been seen 
in paragraph 3.2.1 about the transport of concrete waste. Circularity assessed with MFA 
would say that the concrete needs to be recycled; but a complete LCA would say that the 
transport of concrete would create more negative impacts.  
In other words, Mass Flow Analysis can be used to make a map of the system, detect the 
loops and thus visualize the circularity of it. It is a decision support tool very clear for waste 
production and hot spot identification. However, Mass cannot gives a global environmental 
impact of the system. A 100% recycling system would be great viewed for MFA point of view, 
but can be highly energy consuming.  
 
The energy flow analysis, EFA, is also useful to detect the “hot spot”. It also permit to see 
that the insular context do not have such a large impact of energy consumption, at least at a 
complete life cycle scale. Nevertheless, it gives results that can be difficult to compare with 
literature since the conversion to Primary Energy depends of the country.  
 
Finally, we can say that mass and energy are units that can be easily transferred to costs 
considerations. Linking MFA and EFA to financial benefits will give an important arguments 
for the stakeholders. It gives more impact and make MFA and EFA useful and powerful 
decision support tools.  
 
==PAGE:74==
74 
 
4.3 Future research recommendations 
 
In order to go further in that study, iterations would be necessary. Indeed now we know 
which flows are sensitive, data of higher quality are to be found for these flows.  For the 
construction stage, more precise data would be available at the end of the construction, for 
both MFA and EFA.  
It would be also important to take into consideration the tool and machinery impacts in 
MFA. Even if, some are reusable tools, it would be interesting to see the contribution for 
them on the system.  
Also, a big work can be done on the forecasts on the future energy sources, waste 
treatment, recycling, design solutions or energy recovery. Indeed, the life cycle of a building 
is really particular seen the reference study period exceed 30 years. At the speed of 
technologies evolution, the results will be highly influenced by the improvements of the 
construction, demolition, recycling, and material and energy production processes.
