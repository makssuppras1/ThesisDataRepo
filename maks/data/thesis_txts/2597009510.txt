Synopsis:
This project investigates the pos-
sibility of using a stereo cam-
era for assisting the demanding
task of unloading on-the-go dur-
ing the harvesting operation. A
computer vision system, utilis-
ing both 2D (image) and 3D
(point cloud) data has been de-
veloped aiming in classifying, lo-

Abstract
During the last decade, there have been signiﬁcant achievements towards a higher level of
autonomy in various sectors. Given the growing pressure of increasing yield and reducing costs,
such technologies could not be absent from the agricultural sector.
This project presents a
computer vision system for assisting the demanding task of unloading on-the-go during the
harvesting period.
The system can be simpliﬁed in three major parts, (a) detection of the
unloading container, (b) localisation in 3D space, and (c) container unloading. Each part has
been tackled individually, utilising both 2D (single images) and 3D (point cloud generated from a
stereo camera) data. In the detection task, six versions of the YOLO object detection algorithm
were trained on two diﬀerent datasets. For the speciﬁc task, TinyYOLOV3, with an input size of
416x416, showed the best balance of accuracy and speed. Additionally, cost-free augmentation
techniques on the training data set proved to increase the performance of the detector. In the 3D
localisation task, the Coherent Point Drift point set registration algorithm allowed the precise
localisation of the container in the 3D world. For the unloading task, reducing and grouping
the number of points in the point cloud permitted a fast and accurate volume estimation of the
container. The ﬁnal results show promising results for upscaling the solution in a real scenario
of on-the-go unloading. Furthermore, the individual tests of each module could be useful for
applications in diﬀerent ﬁelds applications if further investigated.

Introduction 1
This chapter explains the motivation behind the selection of this project. The problem, goals, and
requirements of the project are presented. This chapter culminates with the workﬂow and the
structure of the report.
1.1
Motivation
Modern agricultural machinery is continuously increasing in performance and size over the past
years. The increasing cost of supplies and shortage of labour, combined with the responsibility of
humanity to supply suﬃcient food to a booming population, demand solutions for achieving more
with less. In the future of agriculture, some of the most signiﬁcant challenges will be solved by
autonomous applications aiming at reducing time and costs [1]. Typically, during the harvesting
period, there are two main actions required, harvesting and transporting goods. Although the
combine is equipped with a tank for grain storage, both operations are rarely performed by
using only the combine unless the ﬁeld is small and the storage area close to the ﬁeld. Usually,
harvesting involves a combine and at least one transport entity, which is a tractor or truck with
a container attached. The safest option is stationary unloading, as the combine operator can
focus just on this task. However, during the harvesting period of high-volume crops, unloading
can consume up to 40% of the total time [2]. Thus, unloading on-the-go is widely used as a
means of minimizing the unloading time and increase harvest eﬃciency [3]. Unloading on-the-go
is the process during which the combine continues its regular operation of harvesting while the
second vehicle follows in close proximity so that the unloading spout of the combine can reach
its container and unload. In a model by Webster in 2011, it was estimated that if 90% of the
unloading process is conducted on-the-go, the harvesting costs can drop by more than 25% [4].
This parallel operation requires skilled drivers that need to be in a highly focused state for as
long as it takes to unload. The combine driver is responsible for (a) driving correctly and safely
on the ﬁeld, (b) ensuring that the unloaded product falls within the container, (c) monitoring
his relative position to the tractor’s position to achieve a uniform distribution of product inside
the container and (d) making sure not to overﬁll the container. The uncertainty, due to the pose
diﬀerence between the two vehicles caused by the environment and the drivers themselves, adds
complexity in completing this task. The problems that arise are mainly (a) loss of product due
to overﬁll or wrong positioning of vehicles during unloading, (b) loss of time to recollect lost
product, and (c) stress of drivers due to continuous multi-tasking.

1.2
Goal
The main goal of this master thesis is to develop a computer vision system to assist unloading
of a combine harvester while operating in the ﬁeld.
1.3
Project Requirements
The system should tackle tasks that are presented below and are normally performed by the
operator:
• Determine presence and type of container
• Assert relative position of combine auger and container
• Estimate fullness of container
1.4
Methodological Approach
To meet the aforementioned requirements, three major areas of interest can be identiﬁed:
• Container classiﬁcation and localisation in 2D
• Container extraction and localisation in 3D
• Container partial and total volume estimation
1.5
Structure of work
The project began with the planning phase. This includes (a) the analysis of the background
of the problem, (b) the main reasons behind the importance of such a system, and (c) previous
academic and industrial implementations. Then followed research for new tools from other ﬁelds
that could enhance previous applications. Finally, the problem was divided into three major
parts that were described in section 1.4.
For the development of the system, it was decided to follow the sequence of events that happen
in the real scenario and solve the tasks one by one. For each task, the existing methods were
analysed, followed by an implementation of one or more relevant ones leading to the selection of
the best performing method. Then, the next task was examined. Finally, the whole system was
ﬁne-tuned as one entity. A visualisation of the main parts for the development of the system is
included in ﬁgure 1.1.
The structure of this report is based on the workﬂow of the implemented solution. After the
introduction, an analysis of existing technology that tried to solve this or similar problems follows.
Then, the experimental setup, sensor conﬁguration, and container modelling, are presented.
Afterwards, each task is analysed as an individual chapter, containing information on why it is
an important task, what is to be achieved, an explanation of the methodology and experimental
results.
It was decided to choose this format for the ease of reading this report.
The ﬁnal
considerations and discussion will conclude the document.

Analysis 2
This chapter contains background information relevant to the problem, an analysis of existing
solutions, and general trends in autonomous agricultural applications.
2.1
Background
Interest in autonomous applications for grain unloading has been active since 1996 with a patent
from Pollklas and CLAAS. The device aimed in providing an automatic, loss-free ﬁlling, to an
as-full-as-possible state container by using one or more optical or acoustic sensors for measuring
the container and ﬁll levels [5].
During the next years, research in CLAAS brought a system called Assistant System for the
Unloading Process (ASUL) [6].
This system was based on relative positioning between the
harvester and the tractor and required seven Global Positioning System (GPS) receivers, four on
the harvester and three on the tractor. For the unloading process, a laser scanner and elementary
mathematic 3D functions such as hyperboloids and cones were used to model the bulk of the
product. In their analysis, computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) and digital elevation model
(DEM) techniques were considered but not applied due to their computational cost. Although
the achieved positioning accuracy was in the 5cm range, the unloading accuracy was around
50cm. Afterwards, the industry focused on reducing sensor costs and improving the accuracy of
the relative positioning of the two vehicles. The Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS)-
based applications require more than one receivers or more expensive entities such as Real-Time
Kinematic (RTK) positioning, which need to be attached on both vehicles.
In 2009, CLAAS launched Autoﬁll which is a system based on a three-sensor camera (two
grayscale and one RGB) image processing. The ﬁrst step is ﬁnding the top boundaries of the
trailer using the disparity image. Then, since the camera is at a ﬁxed position, the trajectory of
the crop can be predicted and the unloading process can start. As the unloading process starts,
the real trajectory is calculated using real-time data and the ﬁll level of the diﬀerent areas of
the trailer is estimated, providing information on full areas and areas where the crop should be
unloaded [7].
Similarly, John Deere Active Fill Control also uses stereo technology for automated cart ﬁlling
on a preset ﬁlling strategy. This system needs to be manually engaged, and once it launches, it
looks for the container in the camera ﬁeld of view and ﬁlls it automatically, once detected. After
headland turns, the spout automatically returns to the last ﬁlling position. Experiments showed

that the operator’s attention on the loading process decreased from 32% to 23% allowing other
tasks such as monitoring quality or maximize throughput [8].
In 2010, CASE IH announced their Vehicle-to-Vehicle Control (V2V) system focused on
synchronizing the combine and the tractor using a master and slave approach respectively through
wireless communication. To achieve vehicle alignment, the travelling speed and steering of the
tractor were adjusted depending on the actions of the combine driver [9].
In 2011, John Deere announced Machine Sync, a system using radio communication to allow
small adjustments of the tractor position from commands of the combine operator [10].
In the history of research on this ﬁeld, the focus was to automate or assist the unloading process.
Solutions vary from smart positioning between the combine and tractor to vision systems for
controlling the spout so that the operator can focus on the ﬁeld. Positioning technologies proved
to increase the quality of the unloading process by improving the accuracy of the calculated pose
diﬀerence between the combine and the container.
2.2
Machine Guidance
The recent improvements in the accuracy level of positioning systems combined with the reduced
costs of acquiring such a system have made machine guidance systems more and more popular.
Machine guidance systems use GNSS data to assist the operator by providing driving directions,
and in their more advanced level, can take complete control of the steering operation through
integrated machine hydraulics [11]. The beneﬁts of using such systems include reduced skipping
or overlapping of agricultural rows, the potential of working in poor visibility conditions and

3.2
Stereo Vision
Stereo vision allows three-dimensional perception, introducing depth information using two
images of the same scene. The term “stereo” comes for the Greek word "στερεό" (stereó) which
means solid and is, therefore, the ability to describe the three-dimensional shape of an object.
The ability of humans to perceive depth is a combination of the human anatomy and a cognitive
process that starts happening in the very early years of life and evolves through observation.
Although the brain learns to use indicators to describe depth and location of an object in the
three-dimensional space such as (a) relative size, (b) motion parallax, (c) interposition, (d)
texture, and (e) lighting and shadows, the fundamental cause is the fact that it processes the
scene from two viewpoints, the eyes, allowing binocular vision. The slight diﬀerence of an object
in the scene as seen by the left versus the right eye is called binocular disparity. In general, the
smaller the diﬀerence (disparity) the further the object is and vice versa.
Based on this principle, in computer stereo vision, two or more camera lenses and sensor pairs
capture a scene simultaneously (see ﬁgure 3.5).
Figure 3.5: Raw images, A: left and B: right
Using the parameters of the camera (see table 3.1) such as the optical centre and the focal
length, the images are then undistorted to compensate for the lens distortion, as visualised in
ﬁgure 3.6. A loss of parts of the image, usually in the corners is often visible due to the process
of undistrortion.
Figure 3.6: Undistorted images, A: left and B: right
The goal is to identify the same part of an object in the scene, in both images, with pixel
accuracy. By identifying the matching pixels between the two images, an estimation of a 3D
model of the scene can be made, a process called stereo matching. Due to the computational
cost of searching the whole space of each image for that same pixel, a fundamental process is
image rectiﬁcation.
In 3.7 (a), c0 and c1 are the left and right lens centers respectively and the line that connects
the two centers is called baseline and therefore describes the two centers. The points e0 and e1
are the points where the left and right image plane are intersected by the baseline. Using the
10
==PAGE:23==
3.2. Stereo Vision
point P in the physical world as an example, P is captured at the point x0 on the left image and
point x1 on the right image. The position of the points x0 and x1 on the image are deﬁned by
the pixel coordinates of the image. If this point moves towards the center of the left image (c0),
it will continue to be captured on x0 of the left image, but will now be captured at a diﬀerent
point on the right image. The line that contains all those potential points (see line between e1
and x1) is called epipolar line. In the same manner, there exists an epipolar line in the left image
that contains all possible candidates that are captured at a speciﬁc point on the right image.
The two lines are visualised as l0 and l1 in ﬁgure 3.7 (b).
Figure 3.7: Epipolar geometry
For all the points that would form an object in the physical world, a set of epipolar lines can be
generated which converge and meet at one point. Image rectiﬁcation is the process of projecting
both images into a common image plane, which results in overlapping parallel epipolar lines.
When rectiﬁed, the corresponding pixel points have identical vertical coordinates in both images,
which allows point-by-point matching by scanning the horizontal lines˚). This reduces the search
space from two dimensions to one which signiﬁcantly improves the search time.
Figure 3.8: Horizontal scan lines
The process of determining which pixel of the left image corresponds to each pixel of the right
image is also called correspondence problem. After matching the pixels, using the diﬀerence in
pixel position in the horizontal axis between the two images, a disparity map can be generated
(see ﬁgure 3.9). The brighter values mean that the object is closer, and the completely black
values are non identiﬁed pixels, therefore, no disparity is assigned.
While observing the disparity map, there are some parts of the image that are incorrectly
estimated with pixels having values that do not represent the reality.
In very controlled
environments the stereo correspondence can be simply applied as explained before, however, in
the real world, this is not the case. Various factors, such as (a) small diﬀerences in illumination,
(b) shadows, and (c) occlusions where one particular point might not be visible in both images,
introduce complications in the matching process. In other cases, two pixels may be matched as
11
==PAGE:24==
3. Preparation
Figure 3.9: Disparity map
they seem identical, while in reality they belong to diﬀerent objects. For these reasons, there is
a need for methods to match and validate more than one points at the same time, given some
speciﬁc conditions. Besides (a) the similarity condition and (b) the epipolar constraint that were
already described, other constraints are used such as that (c) each point should be unique, so it
does not appear on the other image more than once and (d) the points should usually be bound
by the ordering constraint, which means that some consecutive points in one image will remain
in the same order in the other image.
A basic categorisation of stereo correspondence algorithms is based on the resulting output
which can either be sparse or dense.
At the early stages of stereo correspondence, sparse
algorithms were mostly developed due to the limiting factor of computational resources. The
algorithms that generate sparse disparity maps use speciﬁc features from the two images
such as edges and segments, leaving occluded or poorly textures areas unmatched, therefore
achieving fast and accurate results but with limited density [26]. For some applications, sparse
correspondence is very useful, however applications interconnected with the boom in robotics and
autonomous navigation requires more and more dense and accurate stereo correspondence. Dense
correspondence algorithms can be categorised as local or global based on the taxonomy proposed
by Scharstein and Szeliski [27]. The main diﬀerence is that local methods (also called window-
based methods) use a block of pixels (window), whereas global methods (also called energy-
based) use the whole image to ﬁnd the best correspondences. The most common metrics for
local based methods are (a) normalised cross correlation (NCC), (b) sum of absolute diﬀerences
(SAD), (c) sum of square diﬀerences (SSD), and (d) census transform. The most common global
methods usually use (a) graph cuts or (b) dynamic programming.
Although the taxonomy
proposed by Scharstein and Szeliski is a fundamental concept for stereo correspondence, research
has brought signiﬁcant advances in this ﬁeld in the last two decades.
As a result, a lot of
methods combine concepts of both global and local methods. Some of the most commonly used
stereo correspondence algorithms are (a) Semi-Global Mathing (SGM) introduced in 2005 by
Hirschmuller [28], (b) Semi-Global Block Matching (SGBM) [29] which is a modiﬁed version of
SGM.
The metric used in this project is census transform which has been proven useful in various
computer vision applications such as optical ﬂow and stereo vision, and has been assessed as one

Introduction
One of the most important sub-tasks of this project is the ability to detect the presence of a
cart. Object detection is the combined act of localising and classifying an object [33]. Usually,
in the scenario of unloading on-the-go, diﬀerent types of carts will be used, sometimes rented
from local farmers. Knowing the speciﬁc type of cart can provide crucial information such as
exact dimensions and total loading volume. With the object detection module, an estimation of
the location of the object can be made in the form of a bounding box and the equivalent type
of object. As mentioned in chapter 3.1 and is further explained in chapter 6, in the following
parts of the project point cloud processing is required. Since the point cloud, in stereo vision, is
generated from the image, being able to crop the image from the input of the object detector will
result in a smaller point cloud, which improves the execution time of the point cloud algorithms.
Furthermore, by knowing the real size of the object and by comparing it to the predicted size of
the detected object in the image, a collision warning system can be triggered. In addition to the
beneﬁts of using an object detector for localizing and classifying the cart for on-the-go unloading,
by using the same method, with no additional computational cost, various objects of interest
can be detected for diﬀerent tasks (e.g. detect the tractor or truck carrying the cart, detect
humans/animals or obstacles). The main challenge of deep learning-based object detection is
that it requires training data. In some cases, public datasets with such annotated images exist
but are usually used for comparing diﬀerent algorithms. When developing a new application,
new datasets need to be created based on the objects and the environment that they will be used.
For each object (class) that is to be detected, a vast amount of images needs to be collected and
then annotated which is the process of drawing a rectangle (bounding box) with its location on
the image. In addition to the challenge of annotating training data, in order to evaluate the
detector’s performance, a subset of the annotated dataset needs to be kept solely for testing
purposes, resulting in a loss of annotated images for the actual training. The task of annotating
images is an active research topic and methods used for augmenting the quality and minimizing
the preparation time of a dataset are continually introduced. In section 4.2.2 more information
on the dataset preparation and data augmentation techniques will be presented.

4.1
Convolutional Neural Networks
4.1.1
History
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are models inspired by the cat’s visual cortex, as
described in 1962 by Hubel and Wiesel. In their hypothesis, for every instance of detection,
clusters of neurons are activated and detect a set of features [34].
In 1982, Fukushima and
Miyake used this as an inspiration and introduced the neocognitron, which is regarded as the
precedent of CNN [35]. The modern framework of CNN, was developed by LeCun et al. in 1990
[36] and later improved [37]. CNNs received much attention after AlexNet CNN classiﬁer won
the 2012 Imagenet competition [38]. Since then, CNNs have been increasingly applied in various
tasks and ﬁelds, delivering outstanding performance [39].
4.1.2
Architecture
In the area of CNN-based object detection, hundreds of types of detection modules exist, which
diversify from one another either by major diﬀerences in the main architecture or due to small
parts within each module.
However, the concept of a simpliﬁed detector can be used as a
general guideline of this process. Convolutional Neural Networks derive their name from the
“convolution” operations which are part of the feature extraction layers followed by a classiﬁcation
module as seen in ﬁgure 4.1. During feature extraction, three operations follow one another,
convolution, activation function and pooling, and as they repeat, the neural network deepens.
Figure 4.1: General architecture of a CNN [40]

The core of CNNs is the convolutional layer. As visualised in ﬁgure 4.2, there are three elements
that form the convolution operation:
• Input image of speciﬁc dimensions and color channel(s)
• Kernel or “feature detector” or “ﬁlter” of speciﬁc dimensions
• Activation map or “feature map” which is the result of the convolution operation

Pooling layer
The pooling layer, also known as the down-sampling layer, is used to reduce the spatial size of
data representation. Besides speeding up the detection procedure, it also helps control over-

features too speciﬁc to the training data, and fails to predict unseen situations. There are several
pooling functions, including the two most known, average pooling and maximum pooling, that
are visualised in ﬁgure 4.5. The pooling layer works with kernels similarly to the convolution
layer. In the visualised examples, a 2x2 operator runs through the image, with a stride of 2.

last fully connected layer which produces the ﬁnal prediction values for each class of the dataset
using the values that are the outputs of the feature extraction part of the CNN are that produces
the ﬁnal prediction values for each class of the dataset.
4.1.3
Training Process
As mentioned in section 4.1.2, there are two main modules, the feature extraction and the
classiﬁcation. However, the two main components that are unknown before the training are
which features should be extracted (the values of the kernels) and how important is each feature
for classifying an object (weights). In general, four steps can summarise the training process:
20
==PAGE:33==
4.1. Convolutional Neural Networks
• First step: The kernel values and layer connecting weights are randomly initialised.
• Second step: An image is fed as input, going through the operations above (convolution,
activation function, pooling) and ﬁnally, an output of probabilities is generated after the
classiﬁcation.
• Third step: Using the ground truth (annotated images, subset of dataset), the total error
is calculated.
• Fourth step: Using the gradient for all the weights in the network, the weights are corrected
proportionally.
This process runs iteratively for all the training images, and usually, each image is fed more than
once into the networks.
4.1.4
Detector Selection
In the simpliﬁed example of the CNN detector presented above, it was implied that the detector
looks through the whole image for potential objects with a sliding window approach. Although
this technique would ensure that the whole image has been scanned, it would be too slow for
a video feed. Similarly to human vision, state-of-the-art detectors focus on parts of the image
that are most likely to contain an object, in order to be able to process things faster. Based
on the technique used to solve the task of ﬁnding interest areas in the image, detectors fall
in two categories, two-stage detectors and one-stage detectors.
In the ﬁrst category, at the
ﬁrst stage, a region proposal network generates potential bounding boxes, and at the second
stage, a classiﬁer processes the regions, followed by post-processing techniques to tighten the
boundaries and remove duplicates. In one-stage detectors, the region proposal stage is skipped,
and the detection runs directly on possible locations that have been pre-determined. The most
representative two-stage detectors are Faster R-CNN, R-FCN and Mask R-CNN, and one-stage
detectors are SSD, YOLO and RetinaNet. The diﬀerence between the two groups of detectors
is visualised in ﬁgure 4.6.
Figure 4.6: One-stage and two-stage detectors [46]

4.2.2
Dataset
Dataset quality is recognised as being the most essential component in the object detection
framework [52]. The manual factor of the dataset creation, as well as the lack of standardised
amount of training images required for a good model, are the drawbacks of the CNNs and
supervised techniques in general. When presenting the pooling layer in section 4.1.2, a failure of
model generalisation was mentioned, that of over-ﬁtting. When over-ﬁtting, the model learns the
details of the training data to the extent that it impacts the performance negatively on unseen
data. Another case of bad performance is the case of under-ﬁtting. Under-ﬁtting is the condition
when the training data can not be modelled, and as a consequence, the model can not be used
for new data as well. In practice, the goal of dataset preparation is to achieve a performance
precisely at the spot between the two aforementioned conditions. In CNNs, dataset preparation
consists of data acquisition, data labelling and optionally, data augmentation.
Acquisition
At the beginning of the project, a small cuboid box with one open edge was used as an alternative
to an agricultural cart. At a later stage, to validate that the developed system could be used in
a diﬀerent and bigger box and to focus more on the volume estimation task, which was limited
due to its small dimension, it was replaced with a bigger built box. For each box, a recording
of around 5 minutes with three light variations (natural light only, artiﬁcial light only, and a
mixture of light conditions). For both boxes, at ﬁrst, the box remained in a ﬁxed position and
the camera moved around it, and after, the box was rotated on the spot with ﬁxed camera setup.
The recordings resulted in more than 2000 frames, of which 100 were selected randomly to form
the training dataset (see ﬁgure 4.7). Additionally, 100 frames from the remaining data were used
for testing the performance of the detector after it was trained.
Figure 4.7: Sample images from the original 100 dataset

Labelling or annotation in CNNs is the process of loading the images and drawing a rectangular
bounding box that indicates the location and class of the object(s) present in the image.
Data labelling has become a central research topic aiming to accelerate the time-consuming
manual process and eliminate mislabelled data. The Computer Vision Annotation Tool (CVAT)

Augmentation
Data augmentation refers to the improvement of the dataset in quantity and quality without
collecting new data. Some widely used techniques include (a) using parts from other datasets

Speed
The speed of the detector is expressed in two diﬀerent units, depending on the application. In
the early days of object detection the speed was evaluated in seconds as single images were used
as input. With the improvements in the CNN modules, usually the speed on single images is
now evaluated in milliseconds. Most commonly, when using more than one images, the speed is
evaluated in processed frames per second (fps).
Evaluation
In the history of object detection based on deep learning, it has been proven that the two
metrics (accuracy and speed) are connected in an inversely proportional way. Usually, there is
an expected decrease in speed when achieving better accuracy, and in order to speed up the
detector, some accuracy must be sacriﬁced. While the two metrics are independently successful
in quantifying the performance of the detector, the selection of the detector is subjective and
depends on the application.
In some cases, choosing a much slower detector for a slight
improvement in accuracy is more important (e.g. medical applications), whereas in other cases
(e.g.
fast moving autonomous car), being able to detect an obstacle faster, even with less
certainty, is crucial.

Boundary Localisation 5
This chapter contains the theory and practical methods of ﬁnding the core parts of the container in
order to proceed with the process of 3D localisation. The edge detection and line ﬁtting techniques
will be explained, and the relevant results presented.
Introduction
The purpose of this part is to detect the core components of the container, that do not change
during the unloading process and are visible from multiple viewpoints. These parts have been
identiﬁed as the open plane that serves as the unloading boundary (boundary surface), the four
top lines (boundary lines), and the corners where the top lines intersect (boundary corners) (see
ﬁgure 5.1).
Figure 5.1: Container boundaries
For these components, the lines are the common part, and therefore, the next part concerns the
line detection. The most adapted line detection algorithms are RANdom SAmple Consensus
(RANSAC) and Hough transform [56][57]. Both algorithms require binary images as input, and
therefore, the step of converting the two images from colour or gray-scale to binary is required.
Before the line detection algorithms are presented, the edge detector that converts the images
to binary by ﬁnding edges will be presented.
5.1
Edge Detection
5.1.1
Canny Edge Detection
Canny edge detector is an algorithm used to detect edges in an image in the form of a binary
image with some speciﬁc pixels being categorized as edges, and the rest as non edges [58]. The
30
==PAGE:43==
5.1. Edge Detection
basic idea is to ﬁnd potential areas that may be edges, thin the edges to a single pixel in the
direction of the edge, and remove the edges that are not strong enough.
The steps of canny edge algorithm are [59]:
• If image is RGB, convert image to grayscale
• Image smoothing with a gaussian ﬁlter
• Gradient computation in x and y directions
• Non-maximum suppression on gradient
• Weak edge removal using hysteresis thresholding
The concepts for the ﬁrst three steps have already been presented in the previous chapter. For
image smoothing, a kernel based on the 2D gaussian distribution (gaussian ﬁlter) is used and
outputs blurred imaged. Similarly, with one diﬀerent kernel for each direction (x, y), the gradient
can be computed. In the non-maximum suppression step, the edge is thinned by using the higher
value of the edge in the direction of the gradient. The ﬁnal step in the edge detection is the
weak edge removal. A simple approach would be to disregard the pixels that their intensity
gradient value is below some speciﬁc threshold, and keep the rest as strong edges. However,
it has been found that by using only one threshold a classiﬁcation for strong and weak edges
would not be accurate. There is a need for a preliminary step which categorises the edges into
three groups, based on two threshold values. If the intensity gradient value of a pixel exceeds
the upper threshold, it is accepted as an edge, whereas if the value is below a lower threshold, it
is rejected. For the pixels that are between the two thresholds, it is kept as an edge only if it is
adjacent to a pixel that belongs to the ﬁrst group, above the upper threshold.
Although canny edge detection is widely used in various applications, its major drawback is the
fact that it requires manually adjusted parameters to work. In some environments with ﬁxed
conditions such as lighting and background colours, this may not be a problem. However, in
dynamic environments, the best-performing parameters change, so even if found at some point,
they have to be readjusted. In the initial tests of the system, the best-performing thresholds were
manually set through trial-and-error, however, in the later development, some important edges
were not detected. The importance of setting the two thresholds accordingly will be noticeable
in the results section that will follow.
Since 1986 when Canny algorithm was introduced, there have been a lot of attempts into setting
these parameters automatically based on some information of the image. In the next part, two
methods used to automatically set the threshold will be presented.
5.1.2
Modiﬁed Canny Edge Detection
Improvements in Canny edge detector have been constantly introduced, by changing the methods
used in each step and by adding components between those steps. However, the most important
factor in the performance of this algorithm is the selection of the two threshold values. For a
successful edge detection, it is important to set those values correctly. The main problems of
this manual procedure are two: (a) the correct values are subjective and (b) the correct values
31
==PAGE:44==
5. Boundary Localisation
can change drastically as the environment changes. For this reason replacing the manual process
and allowing dynamic adaptations of the upper and lower threshold of the Canny edge detector
has been actively researched. In this project, two methods were used and evaluated.
Otsu’s method
The ﬁrst solution is based on Otsu’s method (also called Otsu’s binarisation) [60]. Otsu’s method
is used to separate data into two categories (e.g. background and foreground). Using information
about the pixel values (in gray-scale) an optimal threshold that maximises the between-class
variance is explored.
Using this value, the grayscale could be binarised preserving the most
important information.
In [61], Fang et al.
propose using the upper and lower hysteresis
thresholds for the Canny edge detection algorithm as presented:
• threshupper = threshotsu
• threshlower = 0.5 * threshotsu
Median method
An alternative method of solving this problem is using the median value of the gray-scale image.
In this case, a parameter still needs to be set that determines the upper and lower thresholds
based on the median, by adding and subtracting a percentage of that value. Therefore, assuming
a parameter sigma, the lower and upper hysteresis thresholds can be calculated as:
• threshupper = (1+sigma) * median
• threshlower = (1-sigma) * median
In various projects that the median method for ﬁnding the canny edge thresholds, the value of
0.33 was used for sigma, and was the value used when testing the setup.
In order to continue to the next step of line detection, an accurate edge detection had to be
achieved. The details behind the selection of the method are presented in section 5.3.
5.2
Line Detection
5.2.1
Hough Transform
As mentioned previously, the most important part of the container is the upper boundary, which
is formed by four speciﬁc lines. By investigating the image with the edges that was the output
of the previous part, some lines can be identiﬁed. Hough transform has proved helpful in many
computer vision applications, including detection in agriculture [62].
This method was named after it’s creator, Paul Hough, who introduced it for detecting lines
in photographs. In 1972, it came closer to it’s current form after Duda and Hart introduced
a diﬀerent parameterisation to help with cases when the lines are close to vertical as the slope
approaches inﬁnity. Hough transform is considered a feature extraction technique and in the
following years, it started being used for other shapes such as circles and ellipses. In a simpliﬁed
scenario of detecting one line it would be suﬃcient to iterate through all the pixels in an image
32
==PAGE:45==

ﬁnd its slope. In the complex real world scenarios, there are various complications to be taken
into consideration. Firstly, a bigger line can be occluded resulting in two smaller lines, secondly,
the simple pixel-to-pixel line estimator would fail to detect a line with a slight curvature, and
thirdly, some parameters such as what is the minimum length of a line need to be predeﬁned.
Hough transform is a method that can deal with the above problems and has been widely used
as a line detection module in many computer vision applications.

chapter is the concept of point cloud registration, which is the required component of localising

Introduction
The next part of the project investigates the methods for localising the box in the three-
dimensional space. Precise localisation is vital for two main reasons. Firstly, the top surface
of the box (see ﬁgure 5.1) can be determined in the three-dimensional space, which will allow the
unloading process. Secondly, ﬁnding which points from the point cloud belong to the container,
will then allow a classiﬁcation of the points in three categories, (a) the container points, (b) the

point set registration) is used and will be described in section 6.2.1. Throughout this report, the
point cloud that describes the box 3D model will be referred to as model point cloud, and the
point cloud generated through the process of stereo vision as scene point cloud.

3D Registration
6.2.1
General info on point cloud registration
Point set registration (also referred to as point cloud registration) is the process of ﬁnding
the spatial transformation that optimally aligns one point cloud to another [64]. In general,
this can be described as a two-step process which includes (a) ﬁnding correspondences and (b)
estimating the transformation between the point clouds. Although the application of the actual
transformation that aligns the point clouds is not considered as a part of the registration task
but is the following action, most algorithms require temporary transformations as part of their
iterative process of gradually improving their estimation of a ﬁnal transformation.
The main challenges of point set registration are noise and deformation. The main components
that can describe noise in a point cloud are missing points and outliers. Missing points usually
occur when the speciﬁc area is not visible due to occlusion. Outliers are points that do not
match points in the other point set. Deformation, especially in point clouds generated through
the process of stereo vision, happens when there are multiple viewpoints and due to motion
blur in case of relative movement of the camera and the scene. Besides the challenges regarding
the shape, it is crucial to consider the computation time, especially when building systems for
on-the-spot applications.
The point set registration methods can be categorised into (a) groupwise and (b) pairwise, based
on the number of point sets that are compared. In pairwise registration, the alignment is between
a source and a target point cloud, whereas in groupwise, more than two point clouds need to be
aligned. In the pairwise registration category, which is the goal of this sub-task of the project,
there are three main groups based on how the corresponding points between the two point clouds
are estimated. These groups are distance-based, ﬁltering-based, and probability-based methods.
Based on the geometric transformation, two main categories of point set registration methods can
38
==PAGE:51==
6.2. 3D Registration
occur, one being rigid and the other non-rigid. Rigid transformations are rotation and translation
as they preserve the distance between each point. The non-rigid transformations include scaling
and shearing resulting in operations that do not preserve the size and shape.
The most popular method for point set registration is the Iterative Closest Point (ICP) algorithm
[65]. ICP is a distance-based method and iteratively computes the sum of squared distances
(SSD) pairs of points in the two-point clouds and applies a transformation to minimize that
sum. The main disadvantage of ICP is that it creates corresponding pairs of points based on the
smaller distance, and, for that reason, ICP is considered as a local alignment method, and in most
cases, it is used as the late component in a two-step process that starts with a global alignment
method, which roughly aligns the point clouds before ICP is used. Usually, global alignment
methods use 3D feature descriptors which are characteristics of points or neighbourhood of
points that diﬀerentiate them from another.
Some popular 3D feature descriptors are spin
images, principal component analysis (PCA), and Point Feature Histograms (PFH) [66]. The
most popular technique is Fast Point Feature Histogram (FPFH) which is an advanced technique
simplifying PFH for faster results [67],[68]. The idea is to look into a spherical area that is a
neighbourhood of points and calculate the angles between the pairs of points in order to build a
multi-dimensional histogram.
Then, when comparing the histograms of the source cloud and the target cloud, some points will
be matching. However, especially in big point clouds, some histograms may be similar whereas
they have not been generated by looking at the correct corresponding point. Then the ransac
is used to ﬁnd the matches. Instead of looking for all potential point matches, ransac randomly
selects some of the points, and then the FPFH is applied. Then, there is an iterative process of
applying the transformation and calculating the distance between all the point pairs until the
sum of distances is minimized. As mentioned before, this is a rough alignment, and still required
another process for better results, usually ICP. A lot of variants of the original ICP algorithm
have been introduced aiming to improve its performance in speed and accuracy [69], however,
ICP still requires an initial rough alignment between the two-point clouds which can lead to local
minima. Finally, the point correspondences are hard assigned, which means that each point in
one point cloud is assigned to one point in the other, and that aﬀects the performance in case of
outliers or missing points.
6.2.2
Coherent Point Drift registration
By researching the ﬁeld of point set registration [64],[70], one of the most mentioned point
set registrations for non-rigid transformations with noise, outliers, and missing points was the
Coherent Point Drift (CPD) algorithm, introduced in 2010 by Myronenko and Song [71]. The
fundamental element that diﬀerentiates CPD from other registration algorithms is the way of
determining the correspondencies between the points of the two diﬀerent point sets. In CPD,
instead of trying to minimize distances between the corresponding points, the alignment is treated
as a probability density estimation problem. Contrarily to ICP, the corresponding points between
the two-point clouds are soft assigned with probabilities. CPD uses the points of the source point
cloud as the Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) centroids and the points of the target point cloud as
39
==PAGE:52==
6. 3D Localisation
the data observations. Given the source point cloud with n points, as an initial step, n Gaussians
are created with those data points as centroids. Then, a random covariance will be assigned to
the Gaussians. For each point of the target point cloud, the probability that it belongs to any
of those Gaussians is assigned. Using the probabilities as weights, each Gaussian’s mean and
covariance is estimated by minimizing the negative log-likelihood function and again, for all the
points of the target point cloud, the probabilities are recalculated. CPD algorithm has gained its
popularity for its performance on applying a non-rigid transformation to align noisy point clouds
with missing points and outliers, but, it can also be successfully used for rigid transformations
and identical point clouds.

This chapter presents the approach of estimating the state of fullness for the container and the

7.1
Volume Estimation
With the previous achievement of localising the container in the 3D space, the observed points in
the 3D world can be classiﬁed in three categories, (a) the container points, (b) the environment
points and, (c) if they exist, the corn bulk points. The corn bulk points are the points that are
inside a cuboid which is the internal part of the container.
The estimation of the load of the container is achieved by cutting the container cuboid into 16
sections, which are smaller cuboids.
For each cuboid, the data points of the observed scene are compared to its location and if they
are found within its boundaries, those points are stored. For all the points inside each cuboid, a
distance to plane calculation is performed, and the median of all the distances for that section
is used as the representative number of how full that section is.

Conclusion 8
This chapter presents the complete system, the reﬂection on the results, the challenges met, and
further considerations of this project.
8.1
Complete system
The developed system can be summarised in the next steps:
• Step 1: Yolov3 416x416 detector reads each frame from the stereo camera
• Step 2: If nothing is detected, it keeps reading frames (step 1). If a container is detected,
the type and location on the 2D image plane is saved
• Step 3: Using the container type, it reads the 3D physical dimensions from a dataset and
saves it. Using the container location, it crops the image
• Step 4: It detects edges with Canny edge detector using Otsu’s method for the hysteresis
thresholds on the cropped images
• Step 5: It runs Hough transform line ﬁtting on the edges to detect lines
• Step 6: Using the physical 3D length and width of the box, it sets a ratio. By grouping
all the detected hough lines into all possible groups of four, it investigates if any group of
lines corresponds to the boundary plane, and if they do, they are saved
• Step 7: For all the points forming the four lines in the image, it ﬁnds the points in the
point cloud (using the pixel positions of the disparity map)
• Step 8: It runs Ransac to ﬁt all lines in one plane, removing outliers and also ultimately
reducing the problem into a pseudo-2D dimension
• Step 9: Loads subset of model point cloud (boundary part)
• Step 10: Applies point set registration
• Step 11:
Using the results of the two subsets, the whole model point cloud can be
transformed to a position in the 3D world. By using 8 points, that form the simplest
cuboid with the same dimensions as the model point cloud, the physical boundaries of the
box are calculated
• Step 12: Knowing the boundaries of the box, its content volume can be estimated by
dividing the area in 16 sections
8.2
Reﬂection on Results
Although the complexity of the real world cannot be imitated in a small-scale experimental
scenario, some preliminary results can be acquired before a system is thoroughly tested in the

8. Conclusion
real scenario. These conclusions and results will be presented below.
The evidence from this study points towards the idea that a computer vision system based on
one stereo camera mounted on a combine harvester auger can be used for assisting the unloading
process.
In the case of new types of containers, two methods for creating a fast 3D model
and training data set to feed an object detector were presented.
For the detector data set,
the ﬁndings support the idea that augmentation strategies can increase the performance of the
detector with no additional cost. Furthermore, a comparison of 6 setups of the two last versions of
YOLO, was conducted, indicating that the fourth version outperforms its predecessor. In general,
the TinyYOLOV3 and TinyYOLOV4 showed satisfactory performance and demonstrated that
they can be used in a conventional system for both training and detecting objects.
In this
study, two diﬀerent automatically adjusted thresholds for the Canny Edge detector weak edge
removal step were tested, both showing signiﬁcant performance compared to the manually set
thresholds, for dynamic environments. Furthermore, the Coherent Point Drift (CPD) algorithm
showed signiﬁcant performance in low quality point set registration, allowing a satisfactory 3D
localisation of the container in the scene. Finally, using a simple method for estimating the
load of the container, suﬃcient results were acquired, concluding the system as a whole with an
acceptable performance in computational time and computational load.
8.3
Challenges
The biggest challenge of this project was the registration method, in order to localise the container
in the scene. At ﬁrst, the two point sets, (a) the 3D model and (b) the captured scene point
cloud were used as inputs for registration in with FPFH and Ransac techniques. However, due
to high levels of noise in the scene point cloud, and the fact that the container could be full,
changing its perceived shape, these methods failed. The main focus of the project then became
the processing techniques of the point cloud in order to get to a state of having two point sets
that could be registered. Using the CPD algorithm which had been evaluated as high-performing
for noisy data set, and various processing techniques in the images and the point cloud, ﬁnally
allowed a succesful 3D localisation.
Another challenge was the COVID-19 pandemic, which limited the whole implementation and
development of the system in an experimental scenario in a home environment.
8.4
Future Work
Further work needs to be done to establish whether applying the speciﬁc augmentation operators
can be considered as a fundamental cost-free method of increasing the performance of the data set.
More speciﬁcally, a detailed investigation on which augmentation operation was more beneﬁcial
(e.g. rotation vs. synthetic shadow) should be performed. Then the object detectors should be
trained on more than one classes to evaluate their performance.
Then, the system should be tested in an outdoor environment, preferably in the real scenario of
on-the-go unloading.

8.4. Future Work
Additionally, further research should be undertaken in order to determine the position of the
stereo camera, and whether adding a second monocular camera, can improve some parts of the
task.
Finally, in the volume estimation part, the processing time as well as the volume estimation
results could be ameliorated with a modiﬁed or diﬀerent approach.
The prospect of being able to accurately detect, localise and estimate the volume of an
agricultural container serves as a continuous incentive for future research towards assisted, and
eventually full, autonomous harvest.
