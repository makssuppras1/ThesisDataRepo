Abstract
Optically transparent conductors are pivotal in various advanced technologies, including
flat panel displays, touch screens, thin-film solar cells, and organic light-emitting diodes
(OLEDs). The predominant material in this domain has been indium tin oxide (ITO).
However, the high deposition temperature of ITO makes it unsuitable for use on flexible
substrates and microelectronics, which are susceptible to heat damage. In addition, ITO
poses other challenges, including a brittle nature, high material cost due to the scarcity
of indium, and complex processing.
This thesis explores an alternative approach by evaluating a hybrid electrode that con-
sists of a silver nanowire (AgNW) network embedded in a zinc oxide (ZnO) matrix. A
thicker ZnO layer, particularly 140 nm, combined with a thinner AgNW diameter (around
25 nm), enhances optical transmittance to 92.8% in the visible region. Only 0.75% re-
duction from pure ZnO. This performance is on par with that of equivalent thicknesses of
aluminium-doped zinc oxide (AZO) and ITO, which exhibit transmittances of 93% and
93.2%, respectively. Additionally, by integration of AgNWs into ZnO, an almost complete
reduction in sheet resistance was observed, bringing it from MΩ/sq down to Ω/sq values.
Interestingly, the lowest sheet resistance (RS) of 2.13 Ω/sq was achieved with a hybrid
electrode comprising AgNWs with a diameter of around 40 nm within a 70 nm thick
ZnO matrix. This highlights the potential of AgNW-ZnO hybrid systems in achieving
low electrical resistance, which is essential for high-performance conductive applications.
However, significant variance in sheet resistance across samples points to the critical role
of post-processing, particularly in the junctions between nanowires.
The thesis concludes with suggestions for future research, emphasising the optimisation of
colloidal solution handling and further reduction of sheet resistance through techniques like
laser plasmonic welding, which could improve junction conductivity between nanowires.
These advances could pave the way for more eﬀicient, cost-effective, and flexible trans-
parent electrodes that can overcome the limitations posed by ITO and other traditional
materials.
Keywords: Transparent Conductive Electrode, TCE, TCO, AgNW, ZnO, hybrid elec-
trode, SEM, AFM, Ellipsometry, transmittance, sheet resistance, colloidal, solar cells,
thin-film photovoltaics, TFPV

1.1
Background
Energy remains a fundamental pillar for human societies, and its importance is increasing
with the advancement of civilisation. The past few decades have seen an exponential in-
crease in energy demands, driven by the growth of the global population and the growing
dependence on technological advances [1]. In particular, renewable energy sources, par-
ticularly green technologies, are crucial to ensure a sustainable energy supply and combat
climate change[1, 2, 3]. With 2023 set to be the warmest year on record, the 28th meeting
of the Conference of the Parties (COP28) to the United Nations Framework Convention
on Climate Change (UNFCCC) underscored this importance by global efforts to triple

The growth and advancement of the PV industry is based on the evolution of PV cells. Es-
sentially, these cells are precisely designed semiconductor diodes, optimised to effectively
transform solar radiation into electrical power [8]. In this diverse landscape, thin-film solar
cells have emerged not as competitors but as complementary options to crystalline silicon
(c-Si) cells. These solar cells are not only lighter and potentially more flexible than their
counterparts in c-Si, but also have the advantage of being produced with less material [9].
With their lighter weight and potential for flexibility, thin-film cells cater to niche appli-
cations where larger and heavier c-Si cells are unsuitable, such as in building-integrated
PV and portable devices. They also facilitate innovative designs and functionalities, par-
ticularly in the dynamic context of urban architecture, due to their reduced thickness and
material use. Although c-Si cells continue to lead the market, primarily due to their cost
effectiveness and module eﬀiciency that affect the levelized cost of solar electricity, the
distinct advantages of thin-film technology highlight its role in diversifying and enriching
the solar energy sector [10]. The ongoing challenge is to improve the eﬀiciency of thin-film
devices to maximise their potential and broaden their application scope [11].
However, like all technologies, thin-film solar cells come with their own set of challenges.
One of the critical components of these cells is the transparent conductive electrode (TCE)
layer. This layer plays a dual role in allowing sunlight to enter and be absorbed, while
also facilitating the transport of electrical charge. The eﬀicient functioning of TCEs is
paramount for the performance of thin-film solar cells [12]. Indium tin oxide (ITO) has
been a traditional favourite as a TCE due to its impressive optical (T>85% in visible
light) and electrical properties (Rs = 40 Ω/sq). However, the scarcity of indium and the
brittleness of ITO films, among other issues, require alternative materials that are more
sustainable and versatile [9].
Silver nanowires (AgNWs) have shown promise as potential replacements for ITO in the
realm of TCEs. With their inherent conductivity, flexibility, and potential cost advantages,
they present an exciting frontier for research [13]. As the world moves toward a sustainable
future, exploring the potential of AgNWs in thin-film solar cells might not only be a
scientific endeavour but also present itself as a versatile and flexible TCE.

2.1
Fundamentals of Photovoltaic Cells
Photovoltaic (PV) cells operate on the principle of converting light into electricity, a
phenomenon in which specific materials, upon exposure to photons, generate an electric
current. Central to this process is the concept that charge carriers, primarily electrons
and holes, are excited by incident light photons [8].
Semiconductors are the cornerstone of PV cells, with electrical properties situated between
those of conductors and insulators. These properties are profoundly influenced by whether
the semiconductor is intrinsic, having equal numbers of electrons and holes, or extrinsic,
where conductivity is enhanced by doping with impurities. The extrinsic materials are dif-
ferentiated into p-type, with a majority of hole carriers, and n-type, with a predominance
of electron carriers. The p-n junction, a boundary between these types of semiconductors,
is where photovoltaic technology occurs [14]. The junction is the site where electrons and
holes recombine, creating a ”depletion region” that is essential for cell operation. The
depletion region forms because of the difference in the bandgap energies of the p-type and
n-type semiconductors in a diode. The wider the bandgap is, the larger the depletion
region. The concentration gradient of charge carriers across the junction establishes an
intrinsic electric field, driving the separation of electron-hole pairs and thus the generation
of an electric current [15].
For the Sun with a surface temperature of 5800 K, and according to Planck’s radiation law,
the surface temperature determines the spectrum of radiation. This leads to the idealised
Black Body Spectrum shown in Figure 2.1. The actual spectrum measured outside the
Earth atmosphere (extraterrestrial sunlight) roughly follows this idealised line. Terrestrial
sunlight is affected by the atmosphere where sunlight is reflected and scattered.
The
dips are due to the absorption of atmospheric molecules [16]. The relationship between
the solar spectrum and the semiconductor bandgap is crucial because the bandgap of a
semiconductor determines the portion of this spectrum that the material can absorb [14].

The Shockley-Queisser limit, also known as the detailed balance limit, is a theoretical limit
to the maximum eﬀiciency of a solar cell’s energy conversion. Consider a cell with a single
p-n junction that accounts for various loss mechanisms, including the thermalisation losses
aforementioned and the voltage drop as a result of recombination. For single-junction
cells, this limit is approximately 33% under standard illumination conditions, although it
varies slightly with the bandgap of the semiconductor material. This limit underscores
the importance of bandgap engineering in the design of semiconductor absorber materials
to be close to theoretical maximum eﬀiciency, or by creating multi-junction cells [14].
2.1.1
Working Principle of Thin-film Solar Cells
Thin-film solar cells are a subset of photovoltaic devices that harness sunlight to generate
electrical energy through layers of semiconductor materials with thicknesses ranging from
a few nanometres to several micrometres. These ultrathin layers offer a contrast to bulkier
c-Si cells, which are typically hundreds of micrometres thick.
The reduced thickness of thin-film solar cells is mainly enabled by the high absorption
coeﬀicients (α), given in cm−1, of the semiconductor materials used in their construction
because they have a direct bandgap [14]. Direct-bandgap semiconductors have electronic
band structures that allow eﬀicient absorption of photons, because the minimum of the
conduction band and the maximum of the valence band have the same crystal momentum.
In contrast, the indirect bandgap has a mismatch with the minimum and maximum of the
conduction and valence bands, and requires thicker layers to absorb the same amount of
solar radiation effectively [16].

In thin-film solar cells the active semiconductor layers are sandwiched between a trans-
parent conductive electrode (TCE) and an electric back-contact. Unlike c-Si cells, where
the p-n junction is formed in a single crystal with dopants, thin-film cells usually have
separate layers for the p-type and n-type semiconductors. In addition, these cells may
include buffer layers that promote better junction formation and window layers that allow
light to pass through while protecting the sensitive semiconductor below [14].
2.2
Composition of Thin-Film Solar Cells
A thin-film solar cell consists of multiple layers, each crafted from a distinct material or
compound, specifically optimised for its function and compatibility. The composition of
layers in thin-film solar cells varies greatly; there is no single standard structure. The
absorber layer, which lends its name to the solar cell, acts as the core of the system, while
the surrounding layers each contribute by performing essential tasks to enhance the cell’s
overall performance. In Figure 2.2 is a copper indium gallium diselenide (CIGS) solar cell,
which consists of multiple other layers together with the CIGS absorber material.

2.2.1
Substrate
The substrate forms the foundational layer of a thin-film solar cell, which provides me-
chanical support primarily. This support is crucial because thin semiconductor layers are
structurally insuﬀicient on their own [14]. During the production process, cell materials
can be exposed to elevated temperatures; therefore, the substrate material is selected for
its thermal stability and a thermal expansion coeﬀicient that closely matches that of the
neighbouring layer [18]. In addition, the substrate serves as a protective barrier, shielding
the cell from environmental elements such as moisture and oxygen, which can degrade the
components of the cell and shorten its operational lifespan [10].
In cells utilising a superstrate configuration, where the substrate also functions as the
initial layer through which light enters, the optical characteristics of the substrate become
critical.
A transparent substrate can contribute to the eﬀiciency of the solar cell by
improving light capture through waveguiding or improving the internal reflectance, thus
increasing the optical path length within the absorber material [10, 14].

2.2.2
Back Contact
On the substrate, the back contact layer is deposited, which establishes the primary elec-
trical pathway for the cell. This layer must have high electrical conductivity to ensure
eﬀicient transfer of electrons from the absorber layer [16]. Metals are commonly used, such
as gold, platinum, silver, nickel, molybdenum, cromium, and copper. Another important
factor in selecting the metal is the chemical and photochemical stability [19]. Molybdenum
is preferred for back contact due to its superior electrical conductivity and strong adhesion
properties for the formation of MoSe2 [15].
2.2.3
Absorber
The absorber layer is the critical component of a thin-film solar cell, responsible for the
photovoltaic conversion that gives the cell its purpose. Typically, this layer ranges from
1500 nm to 2500 nm in thickness, with the understanding that a thicker layer can generate
more current by absorbing more light [14]. The eﬀiciency and stability of a solar cell,
along with its spectral response, are greatly influenced by the properties and quality of
the absorber layer. The bandgap of the absorber material determines the energy range of
photons that can be absorbed, and an ideal bandgap to maximise solar absorption and
conversion lies between 1.1 and 1.7 eV for single-junction solar cells, as it is the highest
spectral irradiance [14].
The typical absorber material for thin-film solar cells is amorphous silicon (a-Si), III-
V gallium arsenide (GaAs) semiconductor and heterojunction chalcogenide solar cells,
such as CIGS, cadmium telluride (CdTe), copper zinc tin sulphide (CZTS) and variants
thereof [14, 20]. The most widely used in PV systems are a-Si, CIGS, and CdTe [20].
CIGS and CdTe solar cells are known for their high conversion eﬀiciencies, as shown in
Figure 2.3. Especially CIGSs have received significant attention as a result of their long-
term stability on both flexible and rigid substrates, making them suitable for a variety
of applications.
CdTe is known for its low-cost production and good eﬀiciency rates.
CdTe and CIGS technologies are believed to hold great promise for the future of thin-film
solar cells. a-Si PV cells are typically less eﬀicient, but offer advantages in terms of lower
manufacturing costs and flexibility, and are often used in applications where high eﬀiciency
is not the primary concern, such as calculators and other small electronics [20].

2.2.4
Buffer
The buffer layer, positioned between the absorber and the transparent conductive oxide
(TCO) layer, plays a nuanced role in the architecture of the solar cell. Its primary function
is to optimise the transfer of electrons from the absorber to the TCO while selectively
blocking holes to maintain separation of charge carriers. This action effectively contributes
to the formation of a p-n junction at the interface [22].
Materials commonly used for the buffer layer, such as cadmium sulphide (CdS), are chosen
because of their ability to form a heterojunction with the absorber material. The success of
this layer hinges on the concept of band alignment. Proper alignment between the band
energies of the buffer and absorber materials is essential to minimise potential barriers
that can impede electron flow. The alignment must be such that it facilitates the forward
movement of electrons towards the TCO while simultaneously acting as a barrier to holes,
effectively contributing to the charge separation process critical to cell eﬀiciency [22]. The
material is of great interest for substitution, even though insoluble cadmium compounds
such as CdTe and CdS are much less toxic than elementary cadmium. It is very important
to prevent cadmium from entering the ecosystem [14].
2.2.5
Transparent Conductive Oxide
Transparent Conductive Oxides (TCOs) have made a special place in the field of TCEs in
the last few decades because of their exceptional properties, such as electrical conductance
and optical transparency. They typically consist of doped binary compounds, which im-
proves the sheet resistance of a compound without decreasing the optical transmittance [9].
In the context of thin-film solar cells, TCOs not only act as electrodes but also play a role
in light management within the cell structure. Because it is part of the front interface,
the TCO can influence the overall light absorption of the cell by enhancing light trapping
through its refractive properties. The choice of TCO material and its deposition technique
can significantly impact cell performance, with common TCOs including materials such
as indium tin oxide (ITO), aluminium-doped zinc oxide (AZO), and zinc oxide (ZnO)
each with its specific optical and electrical characteristics that can be tailored to the
requirements of solar cell design [23].
Research conducted by Gwamuri et al. [24] highlights significant challenges associated
with the use of oxide materials, particularly ITO, in thinner forms used for ultrathin PV

2.3
Transparent Conductive Electrodes
Transparent Conductive Electrodes (TCEs) play a pivotal role in optoelectronics, par-
ticularly influencing the eﬀiciency of PV devices. These electrodes serve multiple func-
tions, including acting as electrode elements, structural templates, and diffusion barriers.
Their work function is especially critical in determining the open-circuit voltage of PV
devices [25]. ITO has traditionally been the preferred choice for TCEs, because of its
exceptional optical and electrical characteristics. However, the reliance on indium as the
primary material for ITO raises concerns about its sustainability and economic viabil-
ity [26].
When selecting TCEs for PV applications, considerations such as work function, band
alignment, compatibility with other materials, processing methods, and overall cost come
into play. ZnO stands out among various metal oxides for its high electron mobility, su-
perior transparency in the visible spectrum, cost effectiveness, and environmental friend-
liness [27].
Although undoped ZnO is inherently resistive due to its ineﬀicient native

substituting elements such as Al, In, or Ga, enhancing its conductivity [25]. Traditional

2.5
Localised Surface Plasmon Resonance
Plasmon describes the collective oscillation of conduction electrons in metals and can be
considered as a quasiparticle since it arises from the quantisation of electron oscillations.
When the electron oscillation is confined to a nanostructure with sizes smaller than the
incoming wavelength of light, it is termed localised surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) [47].
LSPRs in metallic nanoparticles are characterised by specific absorption at certain wave-
lengths, leading to distinctive colours due to resonance with oscillating electrons [48]. With
incoming light irradiation, the electromagnetic field of the light causes the free conduction
electron density in the metal nanoparticle to polarise to one surface, creating a net charge
difference at the nanoparticle surface (electric dipole). This dipole generates an electric
field within the nanoparticle opposite that of light, which in turn acts as a restoring force
to drive the electrons back to the equilibrium position [47]. This phenomenon is highly
dependent on the size, shape, and composition of the nanoparticles [48].
For nonspherical structures, the oscillation can be transversal or longitudinal. Transverse
surface plasmons are excited when incident light induces electron oscillations perpendicular
to the long axis of a nanostructure, and longitudinal surface plasmons involve electron
oscillations that occur along the length of the nanowire [48]. Non-regularity might cause
multiple excitations for the same axis [49].
Transverse surface plasmons can be used to join nanowires to create electrical connec-
tions or to form larger structures. This process of creating stable, conductive junctions
between nanostructures is essential for the development of nanoscale devices. The benefit
of utilising the transversely localised surface plasmons of the nanostructure is that it is a
local effect and the integrity of the nanowire as well as the rest of the substrate remains
unaffected [50].

Welding is an ancient manufacturing method for fusing metals or thermoplastics that tradi-
tionally involves the application of heat, by elevated temperatures, or significant pressure.
As the development of nanodevices advances toward miniaturisation, integration, and en-
hanced functionality, these advances require reduced sizes, more compact integration, and
increasingly intricate circuits for the nanomaterials used in constructing chips and nanode-
vices. In contrast to conventional welding techniques, cold welding represents a solid-state

welding approach without fusion, typically facilitated by high pressure or friction. At the
nanoscale, cold welding is characterised as a process that achieves unification without the
need for solder, heat, or external forces [51].
Light-induced heating of materials at the most basic level depends only on two factors:
material absorption and light intensity [51]. Garnett et al.[52] subjected a nanowire junc-

achieved the welding of the samples.
Non-thermal nanowelding can be accomplished using ultrashort laser pulses, those shorter
than 10 picoseconds. This is because the thermal coupling time that the electron lattice
takes is longer than the duration of the laser pulse, preventing the electrons from having
suﬀicient time to transfer energy to the lattice [54]. In contrast, with nanosecond (ns)
pulses or lasers with longer pulse widths, thermal effects become predominant, causing
the entire nanoparticle to heat up. However, in the case of femtosecond (fs) pulses, only
the surface lattice is subject to heating. This surface melting is a distinct characteristic
of fs laser irradiation, allowing the shape and size of the nanoparticles to remain largely
unchanged due to localised melting, as illustrated in Figure 2.5 [55]. Hence, a successful
nanowelding might not have an enlarged junction or a prominent reduction in height,
because the wires do not melt together.

is used before it needs to be replaced [58].
In magnetron sputtering processes, high substrate temperatures, typically over 200 ◦C,
are common to achieve optimal results in terms of layer transparency and conductivity.
However, certain applications, such as solar cells and devices involving plastics, require
low deposition temperatures. This is due to the potential damage that high temperatures
can cause to the structure of the underlying electronic device or the substrate itself. Con-
sequently, process optimisation for depositions at room temperatures becomes crucial [36].
RF sputtering distinguishes itself from direct-current (DC) sputtering primarily by using
an oscillating voltage at radio frequency (13.56 MHz), in contrast to the steady voltage used
in DC sputtering. This key difference allows RF sputtering to effectively handle insulating
target materials, whereas DC sputtering is more suited for conductive materials. The two
methods also differ in the pressure of the system, the morphology of the deposition, and
the overall complexity of the system. A lower frequency (50-250 kHz) in the oscillating
voltage is termed pulsed DC [56].

2.7
Laser
Lasers produce light through a process known as stimulated emission.
Unlike natural
light sources, which emit a spectrum of various wavelengths, lasers generate light that is
coherent, monochromatic, and highly directional. The wavelength of a laser beam, the
distance between successive peaks, determines its colour and is a critical parameter in its
applications. A basic laser setup includes a gain medium, which amplifies light, and a
pair of mirrors, one fully reflective and the other partially reflective. These mirrors form
an optical cavity, reflecting light back into the gain medium to facilitate its amplification.
A portion of this light escapes through the partially reflective mirror, forming the laser
beam. The design of this setup ensures that suﬀicient light remains within the cavity to
maintain the amplification process while allowing a useful beam to escape [59].
2.7.1
Pulsed and Continuous Wave Lasers
Laser beams can be emitted in different modes: pulsed or continuous wave (CW). CW
lasers emit a steady and uninterrupted beam of light. This consistency is crucial in appli-
cations such as cutting, welding, and communications, where constant energy delivery is
needed. Pulsed lasers, in contrast, emit light in brief, high-intensity bursts, characterised
by their peak power and pulse duration. This mode is particularly beneficial in appli-
cations that require precise energy delivery without extensive thermal effects, such as in
medical procedures or fine material processing [60]. The formula for the fluence (Φ) of a
pulsed laser, which is the energy per unit area, is given by Equation 2.3. Where I0 is peak
intensity and τ is the pulse duration.
Φ = I0 · τ [J/m2]
(2.3)
For Gaussian beams, the spotsize is often related to the beam’s 1/e2 width. A Gaussian
beam is a common model for laser beams, characterised by its bell-shaped intensity profile.
The intensity of a Gaussian beam is highest at its centre and decreases exponentially
towards its edges. The width of a Gaussian beam is often specified by its 1/e2 width,
which is the diameter at which the intensity drops to the width 1/e2 (about 13.5%) of
its maximum value. This width is a critical parameter for understanding how the laser
interacts with materials and for calculating the fluency at different points throughout the
beam [61].
2.7.2
Supercontinuum and Fibre Lasers
Supercontinuum lasers are a type of laser that produce extremely broad-bandwidth light,
spanning a wide range of wavelengths. This is typically achieved through the nonlinear
interaction of light with a medium, often in specially designed optical fibres. As the laser
emits a broad spectrum at once, external devices are used to select the desired wavelength
from this broad spectrum, as seen in Figure 2.7(left). These lasers are useful in a variety
of applications, including spectroscopy and imaging, due to their wide spectral coverage
and high brightness [62].
Fibre lasers use a doped optical fibre as the gain medium and are known for their high
output power, excellent beam quality, and eﬀiciency. Hollow core fibres are, on the other
hand, a type of optical fibre in which light is guided through a hollow core rather than a
solid material. This design reduces the interaction of light with the material, minimising

2.8.1
Spectrophotometry
Spectrophotometry is an analytical technique used to quantitatively assess the absorption
or transmittance of electromagnetic radiation by a substance. Transparency is a measure
of a material’s ability to transmit light through it without significant absorption or scat-
tering. In the context of spectrophotometry, transparency is quantified as the fraction
or percentage of incident light that passes through a sample, called transmittance. In
spectrophotometric analyses, wavelength-dependent transmittance can provide valuable
insight into the optical properties of the material in different regions of the electromag-
netic spectrum, such as ultraviolet (UV), visible, or infrared (IR). For example, a material
may be transparent in the visible spectrum but absorbent in the UV or IR range [65]. This
wavelength-specific transparency information can be vital for applications ranging from
optical devices to environmental sensors and solar energy harvesting systems. A typical
spectrophotometric setup is described in the following and illustrated in Figure 2.8.
1. A light source emits electromagnetic radiation that encompasses a spectrum that
may span ultraviolet (UV) to infrared (IR) regions.
2. A collimator or lens is employed to focus and direct this radiation.
3. Subsequently, a monochromator disperses the radiation into its constituent wave-
lengths, producing a nearly continuous spectrum.
4. A wavelength selector or a slit is then used to isolate a specific wavelength, ensuring
that only monochromatic light interacts with the sample.
5. The intensity of this monochromatic light is gauged both before and after its inter-
action with the sample.

2.8.2
Ellipsometry
Ellipsometry is an indirect optical characterisation method for single- and multilayer thin-
films.
The first ellipsometry measurements go back to Drude in 1887, but saw rapid
evolution due to advances in computer technology in the 1990s [66]. Ellipsometry works
by measuring the change in parallel (p / TM*) and perpendicular (s / TE*) polarisation,
as light reflects or transmits from a material structure as a function of the phase difference
(ψ) and the amplitude ratio (∆). The measured response depends on the optical prop-
erties and thickness of the individual materials [67]. Thus, ellipsometry is primarily used
to determine film thickness and optical constants. However, it is also applied to charac-
terise the composition, crystallinity, roughness, doping concentration, and other material
properties associated with a change in optical response [68]. *TM and TE are the current
terminology [69]
The selection of the appropriate incident angle in ellipsometry is typically based on the
optical constants of the material, particularly its refractive index (n). Near Brewster’s
angle, where the reflectance of TM-polarised light becomes minimal for non-absorbing
materials, is often chosen for sensitive measurements [70]. However, it is not crucial if
measurements are taken over a range of wavelengths. The optimal angle can also depend
on the desired sensitivity and the specific material system being studied [69].
Despite its many advantages, ellipsometry does have limitations. It can be less effective
on surfaces with small roughness or those measured at oblique incidences. Furthermore,
the interpretation of ellipsometric data requires a good understanding of the properties of
the sample and the appropriate modelling to accurately deduce the characteristics of the
material [69].

Atomic Force Microscopy
Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) is a powerful technique for imaging surfaces at micro-
scopic and sub-nanometre scales. AFM operates by scanning a cantilever with a sharp
tip over the sample surface. The tip’s interaction with the surface forces is measured and
used to generate topographical maps with high spatial resolution. AFM operates primar-
ily in three modes: contact, non-contact, and tapping mode. Contact mode maintains

continuous contact between the tip and the surface, yielding high-resolution images but
potentially damaging delicate samples. In contrast, non-contact mode involves the tip os-

to external disturbances. The tapping mode offers a compromise, where the cantilever
oscillates at or near its resonance frequency, allowing the tip to gently tap the surface,

2.8.5
Scanning Electron Microscopy
A scanning electron microscope (SEM) is used to study the microstructure and topography
of a material sample. In an electric field, a thin electron beam (referred to as the primary
electron beam) is focused towards the respective sample surface. The beam creates an
interaction volume below the surface of the sample, where various signals are produced at
different depths [75], as illustrated in Figure 2.12.
Different detectors within the SEM system capture these signals, which originate from
distinct depths within the sample. The primary among these are signals from secondary
electrons (SE), which are dislodged from the atoms’ inner shells by the primary electron
beam. SE signals, emerging from surface depths ranging from 0 - 100 nm, are instru-
mental in providing detailed topographic information about the sample. These SEs are
further classified into four categories: SE1, directly dislodged beneath the primary beam
(indicated by the red arrow in Fig. 2.12); SE2, originating from varying sample depths;
and SE3 and SE4, which are generated within the SEM chamber [76]. From 100 - 1000 nm
depth, back-scattered electrons (BSE) are created. The attraction of the primary electron
to the positive atom core causes part of the primary electrons to deflect and emit from
2.8. CHARACTERISATION TECHNIQUES
19
==PAGE:34==
the sample. The BSE beam provides information on heavy and light phases (known as
atomic-number contrast or Z-contrast). BSE beams may also provide information on the
topography of the sample, but not with the same quality as SE [77].
Electron detectors in SEM quantify the SE and BSE emissions, transforming them into
electrical signals. The signal strength correlates with the electron emission intensity, where
areas with heavier elements, which backscatter more electrons, appear brighter in the
resultant image. The scanning process across the entire sample area produces an image
with varying shades of grey, revealing the microstructure of the sample with striking
contrast [77].
The sample needs to withstand vacuum, be dry, and be electrically conductive. A thin
metallic surface coating (e.g., gold) must be applied to nonconductive materials [75].

electrodes, it is important to measure the transmittance of a double-deposited layer to

ZnO100 and ZnO140. The transmittance of ZnO shows an increased transparency in visi-

93.5%, respectively. This is an expected behaviour as the light waves reflecting off the top
and bottom surfaces of the ZnO layer interfere with each other. The destructive interfer-

bottom surface has to travel much further, reducing its ability to interfere effectively with
the light reflecting from the top.

is crucial to distinguish between electromagnetic field confinement and field enhancement.
Although LSPR effectively confines the electromagnetic field to sub-diffraction limits, sig-
nificant field enhancement, particularly relevant to the welding mechanism, occurs in the
narrow regions, such as the junctions between nanowires. In these areas, the field is not
only confined but also intensified, potentially leading to more eﬀicient energy absorption
during welding.
Further insights into this phenomenon have been provided through finite-difference time-
domain (FDTD) simulations by Ha et al. These simulations for two-dimensional free-
standing AgNWs indicate that the welding of AgNWs is less about the surface plasmons
associated with individual AgNWs and more about their proximity to the junctions be-
tween two AgNWs. It is in these junctions that the combined effects of field confinement

wavelength and CW light sources. The energy per photon varies with wavelength, in-
fluencing the type of material interaction, such as the absorption or excitation of LSPR.
The complexities of laser-material interactions are further exemplified by considering the
impact of delivering a high-energy dose in a single pulse versus multiple lower-energy
pulses. Delivering 5 J/cm2 in one instance is not equivalent to administering 100 mJ/cm2
in 50 sequential pulses. The outcome of the latter can differ dramatically depending on
the temporal separation of the pulses, with intervals of 10 µs or 1 s leading to varied
effects due to factors such as thermal dissipation. This is observed for pulse duration as
ablation mechanism, where the transition from CW to nanosecond to femtosecond laser
pulses represents a shift from heat-based to increasingly precise, minimal-heat interaction
mechanisms.
This distinction becomes particularly evident in processes such as UV-curing adhesives,
where total exposure to UV light dictates the curing outcome. On the contrary, in pro-
cesses such as glass annealing, the power must surpass a certain threshold to adequately
heat the glass. Consequently, in such contexts, it is not only fluence, but also dose, de-
fined as the accumulated energy imparted over the duration of the process, that becomes a
determinant of success. Considering the average power, which integrates both the energy
of pulses and their frequency, could also offer a comprehensive understanding of the effect
of irradiation.
In their respective studies, Lee et al.[53] and Ha et al.[28] explored nanowelding techniques
using wavelengths of 532 nm and 248 nm, respectively, both employing nanosecond pulse
duration.
This approach is categorised as thermal nanowelding primarily because the
duration of the nanosecond pulses is long enough to effectively heat the lattice of the
materials involved.
In a comparative analysis, Ha et al. [28] examined the differences
between nanowelding using femtosecond pulses and nanosecond pulses for AgNWs. They
discussed that femtosecond pulse welding was more advantageous, resulting in an initial
lower sheet resistance after welding. A key benefit of using femtosecond pulses was their
ability to induce highly localised heating, which significantly minimised the thermal impact
on the surrounding substrate. This precision in heating not only improves the welding
eﬀiciency, but also preserves the integrity of the nanowire network and the substrate.

Future Work
In the study conducted by Kim et al. [33], it was discovered that an optimal balance
between transmittance and sheet resistance is achieved in AgNWs with a coverage area of
27%, particularly after UV treatment to remove PVP. In Section 4.1.1 of this thesis, the
highest coverage area achieved was 26%, slightly below the optimal value reported. This
discrepancy may partly arise from the inability to directly correlate the local coverage
area with the overall sample transmittance at a magnification of 5000x. Several factors
contribute to this variance, including the limited statistical measurements available to
substantiate this finding and the inherent density variations within the colloidal solution
of AgNWs in ethanol.
The electrical characterisation discussed in Section 4.2.3 further illustrates the challenges
posed by the colloidal nature of AgNWs in ethanol. The significant variability in the results
emphasises the need to decrease both the sheet resistance and its associated variance.
Exploration into femtosecond pulsed laser enhancement at UV wavelengths has unveiled
promising avenues for advancing these parameters through nanowelding techniques. How-
ever, achieving meaningful and conclusive results requires a substantial reduction in the
spread of sheet resistance. The Gaussian beam profile used in this process results in rapid
intensity reductions over minor distance changes, complicating the determination of spe-
cific intensity levels at which welding occurs from SEM analysis alone. In instances of
femtosecond pulses, successful welding may occur without noticeable changes in SEM im-
agery, suggesting that monitoring sheet resistance could be a more indicative measure of
effective welding.
In the context of the welding process, substantial field enhancement is observed in the
narrow regions at the junctions between nanowires by Ha et al. [28]. This enhancement,
coupled with field confinement, plays a crucial role, emphasising the importance of the
nanowires spatial arrangement. For future experiments, the precision and consistency of
this arrangement could be improved by using a more uniform AgNW solution.
Furthermore, observations from Figure 4.16a indicate that non-welded areas can persist
even at appropriate fluency levels, particularly in the regions between the ablated trenches.
To address this, future investigations might consider employing a flat-top laser beam profile
instead of a Gaussian one.
Using a broader and less focused beam could potentially
enhance the uniformity of the welding process, reducing unwelded gaps, and contributing
to more consistent and reliable enhancements in both transmittance and sheet resistance.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
The first part of this study was focused on individual layers of TCEs, revealing the role of
AgNW diameter, spin-coating speed, and ZnO layer thickness in determining the morphol-
ogy, optical performance, and electrical characteristics of the materials. Using the results
of Section 4.1, it was found that D1 AgNW25 and ZnO140 layers exhibit the highest trans-
mittance of T = 97.7% and T = 93.5%, respectively. A greater coverage area was observed
with a slower spin-coating speed, but this does not translate into a lower transmittance
for the higher coverage area. Both coverage area and transmittance measurements are
local values determined from a very small spot of the sample, and no clear impact of the
spin-coating speeds was observed in the range studied. This decreases the validity of the
results, and no further conclusions were drawn regarding the spin-coating speeds. The
room-temperature-deposited ZnO has a sheet resistance too high to be employed as TCE
on its own but it is homogeneous and smooth and, therefore, is suitable as a component
for spin-coating application of AgNWs.
The second part of the study explored AgNW-based hybrid electrodes, providing a deeper
understanding of how these materials combine to form functional TCEs.
Morphology
analysis highlighted some of the AgNW distribution patterns, confirming that there is still
a network of overlapping wires and that ZnO is gradually creating a smoother surface.
The optical performance evaluations of Section 4.2.2 confirmed that the hybrid electrodes
could achieve high transmittance T ≥90%, with variations depending on the specific
configuration of AgNWs and ZnO layers. Hybrid electrodes with ZnO140_D1-3 achieved
the highest transmittance, T = 92.8%, only a 0.75% reduction in transmittance compared
to what ZnO140 alone achieved, together with an almost complete reduction in sheet
resistance, bringing it from MΩ/sq down to Ω/sq values. The electrical characterisation
(Figure 4.14) demonstrated the potential for low sheet resistance, essential for effective
TCEs, while also indicating the need for careful optimisation of the fabrication process.
A standardised mixing procedure of the colloidal AgNW solutions might promote a more
homogeneous distribution of the AgNW film and more consistent reproducible results.
Based on the comparative studies in Section 4.2.4 in general, an increase in FoM was
observed with an increase in the thickness of ZnO. Functionality-wise, for a solar cell,
we know that the photons must pass through this layer to get to the absorber, and an
infinitely thick layer will reduce the eﬀiciency. Some hybrid electrodes deviate from this
observation, as the mean sheet resistance increased greatly. The same hybrid electrodes
had a variance in sheet resistance, greater than 300 Ω/sq. This underscores the importance
of improving the fabrication process to promote more reproducible results.

==PAGE:64==
Additionally, the study’s exploration of femtosecond pulsed laser enhancement at UV
wavelengths opened up new possibilities for improving the conductivity and durability of
the AgNW network through targeted nanowelding. With a Gaussian beam profile, there
are great reductions in intensity and fluency over the span of small changes in distance
relative to the centre of the ablated trench. It is diﬀicult to determine from SEM at which
fluency welding occurs. Especially since it is aligned using SEM and an estimated intensity
profile. At femtosecond pulses, successful welding is possible without visible difference in
SEM, and sheet resistance results could better indicate welding. However, the spread in
sheet resistance must be considerably reduced to obtain any meaningful and conclusive
results.
In conclusion, the research presented here significantly advances the understanding of the
factors that influence the performance of AgNWs-based TCEs and introduces promising
techniques for their enhancement. While challenges remain in achieving uniformity and
optimal performance, the insights gained provide a solid foundation for future advance-
ments in the design and application of TCEs.

Figure B.1 shows the script used to find the average standard deviations between the
transmittance measurements for the individual recipes. Here is an example for ZnO70
(Fig.4.11, but the same process applies to ZnO100 and ZnO140, as well as pure AgNW on
SLG (Fig.4.3). First, the raw Cary50 csv file is read and given a recognisable electrode
name. Then directly, the hybrid electrodes can be plotted, here grouped by the same
diameter and spin-coating speed. The same group is used when calculating the average
standard deviation, first ensuring the same length of the curves, and then calculated for
each index (every wavelength), and returned as an average value.
Figure B.2: Python script to make a boxplot of the sheet resistance values.
Figure B.3 shows the Python script used to find the desired sheet resistance for the hybrid
electrodes compared to the highest FoM reached by AZO in Section 4.2.4. The first part
is to obtain the average transmittance values in the range of 450 nm to 800 nm, and then
converting from percentage to factors. Then the names are added so that it is possible
to separate them. In addition, the required sheet resistance is calculated by first ensuring
that transmittance is a factor and then rewriting the FoM equation from Haacke [29]. If
the sheet resistance to achieve an FoM of 14 is < 1 Ω/sq can it be deemed ”Not possible”
and further improvement in transmittance must be made.
