Abstract
It is a necessity to mitigate the pressure on the global environment, hence innovative and sustainable
actions are demanded. The growing population results in, among other things, an increased demand
of sustainable fuel for electricity generation and sustainable building materials. By utilizing wood
waste as fuel in power plants and subsequently use the generated wood waste product as partial
replacement of cement in concrete, may solve some of the pressure.
In this thesis, the ﬂy ash fraction generated from combustion of wood pellets was investigated
for its suitability as partial cement replacement in concrete. Furthermore, due to impurities and
hazardous substances normally found in wood waste ash, a preliminary water-washing treatment
was performed on an aliquot of the batch. The inﬂuence of this pre-treatment was also investigated,
including a two parted investigation. The ﬁrst part includes comparison of the characteristics of
untreated and pre-treated wood ﬂy ash, while the second part explores how the aforementioned
ashes as 10% partial cement replacement inﬂuences the setting time, workability, strength devel-
opment and phase development. Strength and phase development were determined by performing
tests after 7, 14, 28, 60 and 90 days of curing. The results obtained in the ﬁrst part were also
compared to relevant requirements speciﬁed by current standards, and the results obtained in the
second part were discussed in respect of the ash characteristics in part one. Results from both
parts were discussed and compared to relevant literature and similar studies conducted by various
researchers.
Based on the results obtained by the ash characteristics, wood ﬂy ash behaved diﬀerently
than traditional coal ﬂy ash, and did not fulﬁll all the requirements speciﬁed by the current stan-
dards. However, the implementation of 10% treated wood ash gave similar results on the compres-
sive strength as the reference samples throughout all the investigated curing periods. Overall, the
treated wood ﬂy ash performed better as a cement replacement than the non-treated wood ash,
indicating a positive outcome of the preliminary washing treatment.

1 | Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
An increased worldwide concern of the environment encourage to innovative thinking including
the three R´s - reduce, reuse and recycle. Reduce the CO2 emission and waste, reuse important
resources and recycle instead of dispose. As concrete is responsible for 8% of the world’s CO2
emission, whereas the cement production is the main concern, incorporation of the three R’s in this
ﬁeld should be a focus area in the ﬁght against climate change (Rodgers, 2018).
The use of woody biomass fueled power plants in place of traditional fuels serve as a conve-
nient way of reducing the greenhouse gas emissions, as biomass is considered being carbon neutral.
Furthermore, it oﬀers an eﬃcient method of disposal of the waste materials from forestry and tim-
ber product manufacturing. In April 2017, 26 of 28 EU nations shared the same agreement in not
to invest in new coal-ﬁred power plants after 2020 and a goal of providing 100 % carbon neutral fuel
by 2050 (Neslen, 2017). Consequently, there will be an increased demand of alternative, sustainable
fuels for energy production.
The reuse and recycle aspects appear in the subsequent use of the waste product generated
by the biomass fueled power plants. Wood waste ash (WWA) is most commonly used as landﬁll,
but restraints are set due to potential leachate of heavy metals and contamination of ground water.
The ﬁne particles of WWA are also known for getting easily air borne by wind and may cause
health problems if the dump site is close to dwelling areas (Chowdhury et al., 2015a). Problems
that arise from WWA increase the demand of a sustainable ash management.
Several researchers have investigated incorporation of WWA in concrete and WWA as po-
tential cement replacement (Elinwa and Mahmood, 2002; Udoeyo et al., 2006; Rajamma et al.,
2009; Berra et al., 2015). Promising results have been obtained, showing that WWA may become
a resource as opposed to a burden. The research revealed that potential reuse is determined by
chemical and physical properties of WWA, as it has great inﬂuence on e.g workability, setting and
mechanical strength. However, the presence of heavy metals and salts are described to likely pose
severe limitations in the reuse of this waste product.
Wood waste ash as a partial replacement material is not allowed by ASTM C618-19 (2019)
or DS/EN 450-1 (2012), which are the current standards regarding implementation of ﬂy ash.
However, these standards preclude any material not derived from coal combustion. As the use of
coal fueled power plants are about to fade out, it is reasonable to assume a future development of
the standards including use of ﬂy ashes derived from any biomass fueled power plants as long as
they meet the physical and chemical requirements speciﬁed in the aforementioned standards.
1.2
Problem statement
Previous studies have revealed that few wood ashes meet the requirements set by ASTM C618-19
(2019) and DS/EN 450-1 (2012) for regular coal ﬂy ash (Udoeyo et al., 2006; Abdullahi, 2006;
Rajamma et al., 2009). However, several studies have shown promising results towards a possible

2.1
Concrete and Mortar
Concrete is known as the most used building material in the world (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006).
The material is cheap and easy accessible which may be the reason for its popularity. Furthermore,
it is resistant to water and capability to be formed into a variety of shapes and sizes.
It is a
composite material that consists of a binding medium and aggregates. Hydraulic-cement concrete
consists of a binder formed by mixing water and hydraulic cement. Concrete without large particles
is called mortar and it is a mixture of sand, cement and water. These mediums will be explained
further in this section. The term concrete refers to Portland-cement concrete in this thesis. Figure
2.1 gives an overview of what components that usually exists within a cement paste, mortar paste
and concrete.
Figure 2.1: Components of concrete. The illustration is inspired by Hansen et al. (2008), page 50
2.1.1
Aggregates
Aggregates typically constitute 75% in concrete by volume, and is the granular material. It typically
consists of rock-like materials such as sand, gravel, crushed stones or recycled materials with a
cementing medium. The grain size varies from 0.1 mm to 50 mm (Sivakugan et al., 2018), where the
term coarse aggregates refers to grain sizes larger than 4.75 mm and ﬁne aggregates refers to grain
sizes smaller than 4.75 mm (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). Aggregates smaller than ﬁne aggregates
are called ﬁllers and will be described in Section 2.1.2. The characteristics of the aggregates used
determine the properties of the material such as porosity, moisture absorption, shape and surface
texture, strength and elastic modulus. (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006).
The size, shape and surface texture of aggregate particles have great inﬂuence on the proper-
ties of concrete. Smooth and round particles require less water to achieve a workable mixture than
rough-textured, angular and elongated particles (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). The particles that
are smaller in thickness relative to height and length are referred to as ﬂat or ﬂaky, and those that
are larger in length than the two other dimensions are referred to as elongated. Elongated particles
should not exceed more than 15% of the mass content of the total aggregate as they produce very
harsh concrete (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006).
Page 3 of 169
==PAGE:18==
2. Theory
There is some evidence that during early age, the mechanical properties of concrete can be
aﬀected by the surface texture of the aggregate. A rough surface tend to help the formation of a
stronger physical bond between the aggregate and cement paste, though at later stage this eﬀect
may not be so important (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). Furthermore, as rough-textured aggregate
requires more water, the small advantage in strength due to a better physical bonding may be lost
with regards to overall strength (Rocco and Elices, 2009).
There are many requirements for specifying size and grading limits of aggregate, and the most
important reason is its inﬂuence on workability and cost (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). Aggregate
grading has a considerable eﬀect on the cement paste requirement of a concrete mixture, and a
diverse size distribution reduce the void content (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006).
2.1.2
Fillers
Aggregate with grain sizes smaller than ﬁne aggregates are called ﬁllers.
This thesis uses the
Herholdt et al. (1985) deﬁnition of ﬁllers with grain size threshold of 250 µm. Fillers can eﬀect
concrete in three diﬀerent ways (Moosberg-Bustnes et al., 2004):
• Physical level - The ﬁller eﬀect where the particles ﬁll empty voids in the cement mix and
thus increase the compactness and strength
• Surface chemical level - The added particles improve the hydration by acting as nucleation
sites
• Chemical level - The particles react with a component in the cement
The particle size distribution strongly inﬂuence the density of a composite material. Bi-
nary packing of spherical particles with various particle sizes can cause a decrease of interparticle
voids, thus make the structure more rigid due to internal friction between the particles (Moosberg-
Bustnes et al., 2004). The small particles will act like a ﬂuid media embracing the larger particles.
Furthermore, the particles will more widely spread the hydration products.
2.1.3
Binders
The mechanical properties and durability of concrete are normally determined by the choice of
binders (Hansen et al., 2008). As seen in Figure 2.1, the binder is a crucial component in concrete,
and as it reacts with water, it develops binding properties. All hydraulic binders can be used to
produce concrete, and the most used binder is Portland cement (Hansen et al., 2008). The term,
hydraulic binder, is used when the hydrated product is stable in an aqueous environment (Mehta
and Monteiro, 2006). Rapid Aalborg Cement (CEM I 52,5 N (MS) (LA)) from Aalborg Portland
is the binder used throughout this pthesis, thus it will be the only cement presented and accounted
for. It will be addressed as Portland cement.
In order to reduce the energy consumption and the price of concrete, some of the cement is
often replaced with alternative binders such as ﬂy ash and micro-silica (Hansen et al., 2008). Fly
ash will be further explained in Section 2.4.1

2.1.4
Admixtures
In the modern concrete mixtures, admixtures are often used as additional mediums to aggregates,
cement and water. Admixtures are applied to the batch before or throughout the mixing process
of concrete, and are applied due to their beneﬁcial properties. For instance, the admixture may be
mineral, chemical or water-reducing - all added for desired properties of fresh or cured concrete.
Properties such as setting and hardening of the cement paste, durability of concrete and risk of
thermal cracking are characteristics that can be improved by the aforementioned admixtures.
No type of admixtures will be used throughout this project, and will not be described nor
discussed any further.
2.2
Portland cement
"Deﬁnition. Portland cement is a hydraulic cement produced by pulverizing clinkers consisting
essentially of crystalline hydraulic calcium silicates, and a small amount of one or more forms of
calcium, sulfate and up to 5% limestone as an interground addition. Clinkers are 5- to
25-mm-diameter nodules of a sintered material that is produced when a suitable composition and
proportions of raw materials is heated to high temperatures." (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006)
Portland cement consist of a homogenized mixture of clay and calcareous materials which
have been exposed to a series of crushing, grinding and blending operations. The raw mix is either
exposed to a wet process or a dry process in the manufacturing of cement. However, modern cement
plants favor the dry process (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). The wet process is less energy eﬃcient
due to the water in the slurry must evaporate before the clinker-process, and requires a fossil-fuel
energy input of 1400 kcal/kg in contrary to 800 kcal/kg for the dry process (Mehta and Monteiro,
2006).
A major step in manufacturing process is the clinkering operation which is carried out
in a rotary kiln.
Temperatures of 1450◦C to 1550◦C are reached, and the chemical reactions
constituting clinker minerals are completed. Clinker minerals consist mainly of complex chemical
compounds made of calcium oxide (CaO), silicon dioxide (SiO2), aluminum oxide (Al2O3) and iron
oxide (Fe2O3), and go by the abbreviations C, S, A and F, respectively (Hansen et al., 1978).
These chemical compounds are forming calcium silicates and calcium aluminates. The four most
commonly found in Portland cement are presented in Table 2.1.
Beside the clinker minerals, that constitute to about 95% of the Portland cement, there are
several secondary components that have signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the properties of the cements, such
as gypsum (CaSO4 · H2O), lime (CaO), magnesium oxide (MgO) and alkali oxides (Hansen et al.,
1978).

2.2.1
Hydration
As the clinker minerals reacts with water, new phases are formed called hydrates which contains
chemical bounded water (Hansen et al., 1978). The four main clinker minerals may be divided in
two, silicates and aluminates, and during the hydration process, hydration products are formed.
The hydration reactions of aluminates and silicates are described in the following sections, divided
in hydration of silicate compounds and hydration of aluminate compounds.
Hydration of Silicate Compounds
By hydration of the impure forms of tricalcium and dicalcium silicate, alite and belite, a series
of calcium silicate hydrates are formed. The two principal solid phases are called calcium silicate
hydrate, abbreviated as C-S-H, and calcium hydroxide, also called portlandite (Ca(OH)2). The
internal crystal structure of C-S-H is yet not resolved, and in older literature the material was
resembled to the natural mineral tobermorite, and is sometimes called tobermorite gel (Mehta and
Monteiro, 2006; Ojovan and Lee, 2014). The C-S-H phase constitute 50 to 60% of the volume
of solids fully hydrated cement paste, which makes it the most important phase determining the
properties of the paste (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). Portlandite makes up 20 to 25% of the volume
of solids in the paste, thus the strength-contributing potential is limited as a result of lower surface
area (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006).

The reaction between the aluminates and water is immediate, and the Portland cement is non-

2.2.3
Setting
The chemical development of hydrated Portland cement has been discussed in the previous sub-
section, but it is also interesting to review the physical aspects of the hydration process, such as
stiﬀening, setting and hardening. As a result of the formation of hydration products, surface ad-
sorption and evaporation, a gradual loss of free water occurs and causes the paste to stiﬀen, and
further on, set and harden (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). The paste will lose its consistency as the
water is the reason for its plasticity. Setting is the solidiﬁcation of the cement paste, and when the
paste has become unworkable the initial set is reached. To fully solidify the paste requires a lot of
time, and the time taken marks the ﬁnal set (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). Until the ﬁnal set, the
paste has little or no strength, and it is ﬁrst during the hardening process the strength develops.
The strength development of hydrated cement paste will be discussed in Section 2.2.5.

2.2.4
Fineness
In addition to the physical aspects discussed in the previous sections, the ﬁneness of the cement
is also an important aspect of hydration process. The ﬁneness aﬀects its reactivity with water,
and the ﬁner the cement, the more rapidly it will react (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). However, the
cost of grinding and the heat evolved in the hydration set some limits on the ﬁneness. The rate of
reactivity inﬂuence the strength development, hence the particle size distribution is important to
consider in regard to the workability of the paste. A major content of smaller particles will result
in quick setting, leaving no time for mixing and placing. Therefore, to increase the setting time, a
wide range of particle size is preferable as the particle size distribution is critical in attaining the
ﬁnal strength of cement in acceptable time (Krishna, 2017).
Normally, 10% of the particles are smaller than 2 µm, 10% are larger than 50 µm and
only a fraction are larger than 90µm (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006; Krishna, 2017). However, it is
generally known that cement particles larger than 45µm are slow to hydrate, and particles larger
than 75µm may never hydrate (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). Cement pastes with a high content of
larger particles make coarse cement paste with higher porosity than those produced by ﬁner cement
(Ojovan and Lee, 2014).

as aggregates (See Section 2.1.1), material properties and curing conditions. However, from the
standpoint of strength, the relationship between water cement ratio (w/c) and porosity is the most
signiﬁcant. As a result of an increasing w/c-ratio, the porosity increases and the strength will
decrease. The tendency of lower compressive strength when increasing the w/c ratio is shown in

2.3
Inﬂuence of Salts
Chemical interactions between the constituents of the cement paste and the aggressive agents in
the environment are often the triggers to deterioration processes in concrete (Mehta and Monteiro,
2006). Corrosion on steel reinforcement is a result of such a chemical interaction. The desired
state of hydrated cement is when the solid phase and the pore ﬂuid are in stable equilibrium. An
environment where the pH-value is below 12.5, may be branded aggressive because a reduction of
alkalinity of the pore ﬂuid would lead to destabilization of the cementitious products of hydration
(Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). The rate of chemical attack is a function of the pH aggressive ﬂuid
and the permeability of the concrete.
There is a protective ﬁlm on the steel reinforcement used in concrete, and in the presence
of chloride ions in solution, this ﬁlm is not stable if the pH stays below 11.5. This could turn
into severely outcome for the structure. However, the protective ﬁlm has been reported destroyed
even at pH levels signiﬁcantly above 11.5. This may be caused by a Cl/OH-molar ratio of above
0.6 where the ﬁlm becomes either permeable or unstable under these circumstances (Mehta and
Monteiro, 2006).
Sulfate (SO3) is also known for deterioration of concrete structures in form of sulfate attack,
hydrolyse or dissolving of calcium-containing products (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). Most soils
contain some sulfate, typically in form of gypsum in an amount which is considered harmless to
concrete. Furthermore, some gypsum are even added to the cement paste to slow the hydration
process as explained in Section 2.2.1. However, degradation of concrete due to chemical reactions
between hydrated Portland cement and sulfate ions occurs if the amount of sulfate ions is consider-
able, and a limit of maximum 3% sulfate content is set by DS/EN 450-1 (2012). Hydrolysis occurs
when water containing sulfate comes in contact with the cement. Calcium hydroxide is one of the
constituents in hydrated cement paste which is most susceptible to hydrolysis. Theoretically, the
hydrolysis will not stop before most of the calcium hydroxide has been leached away or the contact
solution reach chemical equilibrium (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006). Leaching of calcium hydroxide
will lead to loss of strength properties.
Another destructive process familiar within the concrete technology, is alkali-silica reaction
which leads to expansion and cracking of concrete. This phenomenon involves alkali and hydroxyls
ions from Portland cement and certain reactive siliceous minerals. The raw material used for clinker
manufacture is the source of alkalis in cement. All silicate and silica minerals can react with alkaline
solutions. In the presence of hydroxyl and alkali-metal ions, alkali-silicate gels are formed. When
the gel comes in contact with water, it swells by imbibing water through osmosis. The hydraulic
pressure that occurs in the presence of the reaction may cause expansion and cracking of both the
aﬀected aggregate particles and the surrounded cement paste matrix. (Mehta and Monteiro, 2006)

Supplementary Cementitious Materials
Supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs) have been used since the ancient Greeks who created
cementitious mortar by incorporating volcanic ash with hydraulic lime (National Precast Concrete
Association, 2005). Nowadays, strict air pollution regulations have resulted in plenty of industrial

byproduct that can be incorporated as supplementary cementitious materials. They can either
be used in addition to Portland cement, or as a partial replacement, depending on the material
properties and the preferred properties of the concrete mixture. SCMs can be divided in two, based
on their type of reaction: pozzolanic and hydraulic (National Precast Concrete Association, 2005).
Pozzolanic materials react chemically with calcium hydroxide (CH) in presence of moisture and
form cementitious compounds, while hydraulic materials react directly with water.
Pozzolanic reactivity is inﬂuenced by the particle size, temperature and material composi-
tion. A greater total surface area, due to decrease in particle size, will allow the reactions to occur
faster. Pozzolans increase the density and strength of concrete, and decrease the permeability by
consuming CH and producing CSH (National Precast Concrete Association, 2017b). Common used
pozzolanic materials are silica fume, metakaolin and ﬂy ash, and the latter will be described further
in the next section.
2.4.1
Fly Ash
Fly ash is a by-product from combustion, and consist of the lightest particles that move within
the air stream and subsequently are collected through ﬁlters or electrostatic precipitators. The
properties of ﬂy ash depend on the material that are combusted and the combustion method.
Common for ﬂy ash is its content of salts and heavy metal as the materials only burn at very
high temperatures. Most of the ﬂy ash are amorphous and have a spherical shape (Kurdowski,
2014). The most common used ﬂy ash is the ash collected from combustion of coal for electricity
generation and the term will refer to the latter type of ﬂy ash throughout this section.
The three main constituents in ﬂy ash are silicon dioxide, aluminium oxide and iron oxide,
and it is the sum of these three oxides that classify ﬂy ash as a Class F or Class C. In accordance
with ASTM C618-19 (2019), if the sum exceeds 70% percent, the Class F designation is given,
and if it only exceeds 50% the ﬂy ash is classiﬁed as Class C. The most important constituents for
pozzolanic activity are the two ﬁrst mentioned components. With an increasing amount of silica
and alumina, the pozzolanic activity augments (Kurdowski, 2014).
Furthermore, the following
factors are stated to be crucial for the quality of ﬂy ash:
• Low carbon content
• High glass content
• Low alkali content
• High ﬁneness
Class F ﬂy ashes have only pozzolanic properties and are less reactive than Class C ﬂy ashes.
They have lower calcium contents and higher carbon contents. Class C ﬂy ashes, on the other hand,
containing calcium-rich glassy phases, have both pozzolanic and hydraulic properties. They often
exhibit higher reaction rates at early ages, which could be advantageous in manufactured concrete
products where tight time schedules occur. (National Precast Concrete Association, 2010)

3.1
Physical Properties of Wood Ash
Wood waste ash is reported to be a heterogeneous material of variable size particles, generally
angular in shape including unburned or partially burned particles of wood or bark (Ban and Ramli,
2011). Several researchers agrees that the combustion temperature, type of wood and combustion
time plays an important role in the chemical composition of the ash and the quantity of ash
produced (Barathan and Gobinath, 2013; Chowdhury et al., 2015b).
A scanning electron microscopy (SEM) analysis was conducted by Udoeyo et al. (2006) of
WWA incinerated at a temperature of 1000◦C. The analysis indicated through a micrograph that
the ash consists of two dominating phases, a ﬁbre-like continuous layer and a particle like aggregates.
A common ﬁnding was conducted by Rajamma et al. (2009), and their SEM images obtained back-
ing information about the before-mentioned highly angular particles, and also revealed information
about a high extent of surface porosity of the particles.
Ban and Ramli (2011) stated in their article that the high degree in variation of physical
properties of WWA obtained from various sources dictate proper characterization prior to being
implemented as cement replacement material as the physical properties have signiﬁcant eﬀect on
pozzolanic and hydraulic reactivity.
3.2
Chemical Properties
As the wood waste ash can be used as pozzolanic material in blended cement and concrete, the
chemical composition is a critical property to interpret. In order to qualify as a pozzolana, ASTM
C618-19 (2019) states that minimum 70% of the mass must consist of silicon dioxide (SiO2), alu-
minum oxide (Al2O3) and iron oxide (Fe203) for all classes of coal ﬂy ash, hereby referred to as
primary oxides. Elinwa and Mahmood (2002) conducted an X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) on sawdust
collected from some timber milling points in Bauchi, and conﬁrmed such presence as the main
phases of the chemical compound within the ashes.
In addition to the primary oxides, an XRD-analysis conducted by Etiegni and Campbell
(1991) discovered presence of portlandite and lime as additional dominant phases. These results
are in line with the ﬁndings of Doudart de la Grèe et al. (2016). In their investigation of four
biomass ﬂy ashes, they found that quartz was the main constituent of all four, followed by Ca-
containing compounds such as anhydrite (CaSO4), calcite, lime and portlandite. Titania (TiO2)
was also found in various crystalline phases.
Rajamma et al. (2009) performed XRD-analysis on two wood waste ﬂy ash samples. The
results indicated that silicon dioxide and calcite were the two main chemical compounds present
in the ash. An X-ray ﬂuorescence (XRF) was also performed on the two samples, and the analysis
displayed a signiﬁcant amount of essential chemical compounds governing pozzolanic reactivity
such as silicon dioxide, aluminum oxide and iron oxide. The sample with the highest content of the
three chemical compounds showed stronger pozzolanic reactivity. Generally, the analysis revealed
that pozzolanicity of wood waste ﬂy ash varies proportionally with sum of the before-mentioned
chemical content of the ash. Table 3.2 shows a summary of chemical composition and loss of ignition
of WWA found by various researchers.

The incorporation of wood waste ash generates a new type of blended cement, and the physical and
chemical properties alters in comparison to ordinary Portland cement. The new mixture exhibit
great diﬀerence in terms of e.g. workability, structural grade and settlement at various levels of
cement replacement with respect to Portland cement.

Flexural Strength
Even though ﬂexural strength will not be investigated in this thesis, the mechanical strength is
relation to the content of cement replacement is well shown by previous ﬂexural strength tests
conducted by various researchers, thus will be presented in the following section.
Data from various researchers reveals that ﬂexural strength decreases as the level of cement
replacement by WWA increases (Rajamma et al., 2009; Naik et al., 2003; Udoeyo et al., 2006).
Rajamma et al. (2009) studied ﬂexural strength of mortar mixes in a time period of 28 days. The
cement in the mortar mixes were partly replaced using ﬂy ash from two distinct wood biomass
power plants. The mortar bars produced were replaced with 0 (reference mix), 10, 20 and 30% by
total binder weight. From the research they concluded that:
• Wood ﬂy ash can replace cement in mortar up to 20% by total binder weight to maintain
acceptable mechanical strength
• At higher level than 20%, the mechanical strength deteriorated rapidly
Naik et al. (2003) investigated the inﬂuence of WWA on the ﬂexural strength of concrete in
a time period of 365 days. The concrete mixtures were replaced with WWA at 0 (reference mix),
5, 8 and 12% of total binder weight. From the research they concluded that:
• At 7 days, all concrete mixes exhibited higher ﬂexural strength compared to the reference
mix. The mix containing 5% WWA showed highest ﬂexural strength.
• At 28-365 days, the mix containing 8% WWA exhibit optimal ﬂexural strength among all
mixes examined.
• Incorporation of WWA enhanced the ﬂexural strength due to pozzolanic contribution of the
wood ﬂy ash.

3.4.2
Characteristics
Workability: Berra et al. (2015) investigated the workability of blended cement with various levels
of ash replacement. Three ashes were investigated (WBFA1, WBFA2 and WBFA3) and WBFA1
were divided into a treated part with washing and an untreated part. From a mini-slump test
they got diverse results. Both the washed and unwashed WBFA1 increased the water demand,
however, the washed WBFA1 increased the demand to a minor extent relative to the control mix
containing neat Portland cement. On the contrary, the two other unwashed blends resulted in
adverse workability. A decreased demand of water and an increased workability was observed as
the cement replacement increased.
The washed WBFA1 evidenced a beneﬁcial eﬀect of water-washing treatment on mix work-
ability, however, the contradictory results from the unwashed WBFA2 and WBFA3 proved that
the variability within the ashes has great inﬂuence of the workability. Such conﬂicting data are
supported by other researchers, whereas cement replacement has been found to both increase (Kara
et al., 2012; Raheem and Adenuga, 2013)) and reduce the slump of concrete (Elinwa and Mahmood,
2002; Abdullahi, 2006; Udoeyo and Dashibil, 2002), and moreover has no inﬂuence at all of mortar
samples (Ramos et al., 2013).

the unwashed ashes had retarding eﬀect on the ﬁnal setting time, and the blended cement with
replacement of washed ashes had no substantial change. However, they showed delayed setting
time in respect to the reference mix. The results from the test performed by Berra et al. (2015)

3.5
Conclusion
From the ﬁndings associating the state of the art, the following conclusions are made:
• WWA blended cement has higher standard consistency, initial and ﬁnal setting time.
• Higher water demands to achieve a given level of workability, and inclusion of WWA adversely
aﬀects the slump value.
• Generally, incorporation of WWA reduces the mechanical strength, but inclusion at low levels
of cement replacement showed promising results. A replacement levels up to 10% by total
binder weight gave acceptable strength properties and similar to the strength properties of
concrete with neat Portland cement.
• Pre-treatment of ashes reduce the chloride and sulfate content, and treated WWA blended
cement has greater mechanical strength than non-treated blended cement.
Throughout the research for this chapter, several ash contents have been suggested as re-
placement for cement.
Abdullahi (2006) stated in his research that 20% was ideal, while the
majority of researchers stated that no more than 12% should be replaced with Portland cement
as the strength decreases after this point (Udoeyo et al., 2006; Elinwa and Mahmood, 2002; Naik
et al., 2003). Common for all the researchers is their agreement in greater strength is obtained at
greater days.
As for the chemical properties, research has shown that pozzolanicity varies proportionally
with the sum of SiO2, Al2O3 and Fe203 and that WWA show similar properties as neat Portland
cement. However, both the physical and chemical properties are signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by the
source of the WWA and in this investigation, the results conducted by the researchers have shown
Page 26 of 169

great variety. Therefore, proper analysis of the WWA used is important before its application in
concrete.
Considering the great variety in ﬁndings, the test results obtained in this project are expected
to deviate from the discoveries in this chapter. Hence, this literature review will subsequently only
be used for comparison and as a guideline.

The washing process was proceeded as follows:
1. Weigh 800.00 g dry ash on a technical balance into a 5L plastic bottle with a lid. Add 4000

2. Place a ﬁlter paper in a funnel on top of a measuring cylinder. Pour the liquid phase into

IC and ICP.
3. Add new 4000 ml of distilled water to the ash and shake. Leave it standing until there is
clear indication of a separated liquid phase and soil. Pour the liquid phase into the same
ﬁlter paper. Store a sample from the ﬁltrate for content analysis.

5.1.1
Washing of Ashes
As described in Section 3, heavy metals and large salt concentrations make many types of wood
ﬂy ash unsuitable as substitute for cement in concrete structures. Several researchers suggested

The XRF-analysis presented in Section 5.1.9 revealed a lower calcium oxide content in DS
than in the Portland cement, which may constraint the amount of strength contributing compounds
in M-DS, thus lower compressive strength. On the other hand, DS had higher content of calcium
oxide than WS and based on the aforementioned assumption, M-DS should have obtained a higher
mechanical strength than M-WS. These counteracting results emphasize the complexity of concrete
technology, and the importance of awareness in use of cement substitutes.
The same XRF-analysis showed that WS had an higher content of primary oxides than DS,
and a pozzolanic contribution to the strength may be a reason for the higher compressive strength
of M-WS. However, pozzolanic activity has not not been measured and can not be concluded as
the reason.

As it is unknown what contributes or how much the Portland cement versus ashes contributes
to the strength, the ﬁller eﬀect cannot be omitted as a strength contributor. The particles of DS
and WS were not assumed to work as nucleation sites in regard to the setting process, but could
contribute to strength on the physical level. As both ashes are within the limits for ﬁllers, the
particles may decrease the interparticle voids, and thus increase the density and compose a more
rigid structure as explained in Section 2.1.2.
Another reason for the low compressive strength of M-DS may be ascribed to the high content
of sulfate and chloride. Such impurities are reported to eﬀect both setting time and compressive
strength (Kosmatka et al., 2005).
As a result of a prolonged setting time, thus an extended
hydration process, a greater amount of water is required.
If not suﬃcient amount of water is
present, the hydration stops, and important strength contributing compounds remain absent.
Strength contributing compounds may also be aﬀected by sulfate in form of sodium sulfate
(Na2SO4) and magnesium sulfate (MgSO4) (Kosmatka et al., 2005). Present of components in-
cluded in the aforementioned compounds are conﬁrmed by the XRF-analysis, presented in Section
5.1.9. Sodium sulfate reacts with calcium hydroxide and calcium aluminate hydrate forming et-
tringite and gypsum. If the majority of the calcium oxides form gypsum instead of calcium silicate
hydrates, it may eﬀect the strength as calcium silicate hydrates are the main strength contributing
compounds present (Section 2.2.1). However, no supporting literature was found to this assumption
and the actual time frame where the formation of gypsum occurs is not found.
Sulfate in form of magnesium sulfate attacks in manner similar to sulfate, but additionally
forms brucite (Mg(OH)2). Brucite consumes calcium hydroxide, lowers the pH of the pore solution
and then decomposes the calcium silicate hydrates (Kosmatka et al., 2005).
Another assumption made regarding the low compressive strength of the DS specimens, is
the high water solubility, presented in Section 5.1.3. DS has a water solubility of 17%, resulting in
17% less rigid material when mixing with water compared to 3% less rigid material for WS. High
water solubility may therefore counteract the compressive strength in respect to the Reference
specimens and WS specimens.

6 | Conclusion
Throughout this study, the suitability of wood ﬂy ash in concrete has been investigated along with
the inﬂuence of preliminary washing treatment on ash characteristics, strength development and
phase development. Based on the studies conducted, the following can be concluded.
The most prominent eﬀects of the pre-treatment on the ash characteristics were the decrease
in sulfate, nitrate and chloride content, and a signiﬁcantly lower water solubility. The side-eﬀects
were an increased concentration of magnesium, higher loss of ignition and an increased particle
size distribution in respect to the non-treated wood ﬂy ash.
Based on the ash characteristics
obtained by both ashes, none of the ashes were able to meet all the requirements speciﬁed in the
current standards. The pre-treatment lead to fulﬁllment of the requirements regarding anions, but
also lead to an exceeded magnesium content. Despite that, both ashes behaved in a similar way
regarding most of the requirements to varying degrees including loss of ignition, chemical elements
and primary oxides.
Incorporation of both ashes in mortar paste resulted in a delayed setting time and an adverse
workability. The non-treated wood ash inﬂuenced the aforementioned properties to an higher extent
than the pre-treated wood ash.
The diﬀerences between the compressive strength of mortars that contained treated and
non-treated wood ﬂy ash were clearly evident. The mortar specimens containing treated wood ﬂy
ash showed similar mechanical strength properties as the reference specimens throughout all curing
periods investigated. The mechanical strength of the mortar specimens with non-treated wood ash,
performed approximately 70% of the strength of the two former types of specimens. This indicated
three possible scenarios:
1. The treated wood ﬂy ash had strength contributing properties and non-treated wood ash had
no inﬂuence
2. The treated wood ash had no inﬂuence and the non-treated had inhibit eﬀect
3. A combination of scenario 1 and scenario 2
The results conducted by XRD showed presence of the same hydration products in the
reference paste as the the pastes with the wood ﬂy ashes. The main hydration products presented
at 7 days of curing, were portlandite, ettringite, alite, belite and calcite. Presence of quartz was
the only mineral that separated the reference paste with the pastes of wood ﬂy ashes. The main
hydration products were conﬁrmed in the TGA where bound water, ettringite, portlandite and
calcite were investigated.
The highest bound water and portlandite content were displayed by the reference paste
and the paste that contained treated wood ﬂy ash. The bound water indicated a higher extent of
hydration products in the aforementioned pastes in respect to the paste with non-treated wood ﬂy
ash, and higher portlandite indicated higher content of silicate hydrates. This goes well with the
results obtained from the compressive strength tests. The ettringite content of the three pastes
Page 68 of 169
==PAGE:83==

investigated were similar throughout all curing periods, but a clear tendency showed the highest
content occurred in the reference paste followed by the paste with treated wood ﬂy ash. A clearer
diﬀerence between the pastes were obtained by the calcite content, where the paste with non-treated
wood ash had the highest content, followed by the one with treated wood ash.
Overall, the treated wood ﬂy ash performed better than the non-treated wood ash which
indicate a positive outcome of preliminary washing treatment. Despite that the treated wood ﬂy
ash did not meet all the requirements speciﬁed by the current standards, the treated wood ﬂy ash
is considered to be a suitable substitute for cement based on the compressive strength results.

are reasonable and can be accepted as they are, while other results would need further investigation

Digestion of soil or ash – DS259 
 
 
A 
Principle 
 
This digestion method is a Danish Standard (DS 259) for determining the acid-
soluble metals in the soil. Metal concentration is found by this method 
corresponds to an "overall concentration" of the element in the soil.  
 
A total destruction of the soil will give a higher value of the various metals.  
The DS 259 will therefore provide a more determined level of a metal 
concentration of a complete determination of the metal concentration in soil 
 
 
 
B  
Special equipment 
 
Autoclave 
 
For the measurement ICP is used

Reagents

labeled
