Abstract
The following work is based on the Model Predictive Control (MPC) developed by Stentoft
et al. (2021), which controls wastewater aeration under four management objective func­
tions. The goal of this work was to revise said functions and propose new expressions
that consider the different metabolic pathways in which nitrous oxide (N2O) is produced,
thus minimizing the global warming potential (GWP). For this, a revision of relevant opera­
tional parameters that affect N2O production was done, with emphasis in those who could
be monitored online. In this regard, two additional objective functions were proposed and
their performance was evaluated when tested in a digital twin of Kolding WRRF, which
currently has a rule based control (RBC).
The proposed management goals resulted in the MPC obtaining potential reductions for
Effluent Total Nitrogen, Global Warming Potential (GWP) and Total Costs compared to the
RBC. Specifically, the MPC achieved reductions of up to 4.21% in GWP ­when optimizing
for N2O emissions from the Nitrifier Nitrification and Incomplete Denitrification pathways­
and 7.75% in Total Costs –when optimizing for those same pathways with the addition of
operational costs ­. Furthermore, incorporating an objective function for incomplete den­
itrification as part of the soft constraints, improved effluent quality without compromising
Total Costs.

Introduction
According to the latest IPCC report, the largest drive for the increase of nitrous oxide
(N2O) in the atmosphere has been the human perturbation of the natural nitrogen cycle.
From 1980 to 2019, atmospheric N2O increased 31.0 ± 0.5 ppb, mainly because of the
use of synthetic fertilizers and manure, as well as nitrogen deposition resulting from land­
based agriculture and fossil fuel burning [1]. This is of great concern since nitrous oxide
contributes to the anthropogenic radiative forcing and stratospheric ozone loss, having a
global warming potential (GWP100year) of 265 times that of carbon dioxide (CO2)[2].
From non­agricultural anthropogenic sources, the wastewater sector is one of the principal
contributors, accounting for 0.2 – 0.5 TgN(N2O)/year [1]. Water resource recovery facil­
ities (WRRFs) contribute to greenhouse gases (GHG) through direct emissions (Scope
1) of fugitive gases such as CO2 (biogenic carbon), methane (CH4) and N2O, as well as
Scope 2 emissions from its high energy consumption [3].
In the European Union, N2O emissions from domestic wastewater treatment and dis­
charge accounted for 0.19% of total GHG emissions in 2020, mainly due to the contri­
bution of large population countries like Italy, Spain and Poland. Although the general
trend of N2O emissions from this sector decreased moderately between 1990­2020 by
12% because of the development of centralized wastewater treatment plants with nitro­
gen abatement technologies, that has not been the case in all EU countries. Denmark,
for instance, has had an increase of 18% of N2O emissions in the same period [4].
It should be noted, however, that few WRRFs have undertaken direct N2O monitoring
historically. Available data is drawn from emission factors (EF) which vary whether they
are process­type specific, country specific, or based on international best practices [5].
Estimating N2O EFs in WRRF is a challenge, since the amount of quantified emissions is
highly affected by the process type, WRRF characteristics, monitoring strategy, duration
of the monitoring campaign, among others [6]. If WRRFs want to work towards reducing
Scope 1 process emissions in their sector in alignment with the Paris Agreement, it is
critical to have reliable methodologies to allow mitigation interventions to be measured
and monitored [5].
An alternative to the empirical EFs proposed by the IPCC are mathematical models that
employ kinetic and stoichiometric expressions to describe N2O dynamics in WRRFs. For
instance, Domingo­Félez and Smets (2016) developed a mathematical model that de­
scribes N2O production during biological nitrogen removal. The NDHA model, as it was
called, considers three biological N2O production pathways (nitrifier nitrification (NN), nitri­
fier denitrification (ND) and heterotrophic denitrification (HD)) as well as two abiotic path­
ways [7].
The great advantage of using mathematical models is that they can be used to evaluate
control strategies (e.g. N2O emissions mitigation) in simulation environments such as the
Benchmark Simulation Models (BSM). These tools employ a plant layout, a simulation
model, influent loads, test procedures and evaluation criteria that help researchers apply
and evaluate any control and process monitoring strategy [8].
Previous work using plant­wide models to reduce GHG emissions include the use of con­
ventional (cascade) Proportional Integral (PI) controllers to control key process variables
set­points such as DO, NO−
3 , NH+
4 , TSS and carbon addition [9]; using sensors and Fuzzy
2
==PAGE:7==
Logic (FL) rules to control DO concentration [10]; as well as Model Predictive Control
(MPC) for optimizing aeration under multiple control objectives [11]. The three aforemen­
tioned control methods all vary on degree of complexity, expert experience (empirical
knowledge), performance and calculating time consumption. However, for this work only
MPC strategies will be considered, as they have shown to have the greatest potential for
WRRFs with diverse operational management requirements [10].
More specifically, this work will use the MPC developed by Stentoft et al. (2019), which
was build on four management objective functions: optimization of effluent total­N, opti­
mization of electricity consumption, optimization of total operational costs, and the opti­
mization of global warming potential [11]. Of special interest will be the latter, since this
function considers both the indirect GHG emissions of electricity consumption and efflu­
ent total nitrogen (TN), as well as the direct N2O as a function of the ammonium removal
rate. In other words, it considers N2O emissions related to the NN pathway.
This MPC was already implemented in a simulation environment based on the BSM1 –with
some modifications in the layout– to assess how its objective functions would affect the
operation of the Kolding WRRF in terms of N2O emissions compared to the Rule Based
Control (RBC) currently used in the facilities [12]. However, there were some problems
in the simulation model implementation (e.g. some parameters and rates of the NDHA
model were manually changed) and most importantly, the MPC implementation yielded
the highest N2O emissions when optimized for effluent quality, which seems to be counter
intuitive [12].
The aim of this work can be summarized by the research questions:
Q1: Which are the main variables affecting N2O production pathways that can be moni­
tored and therefore controlled?
Q2: How can we formulate expressions that predict N2O from sensor data and that can
be used as objective functions for the MPC?
Q3: Will this new control strategy yield better results than already­in­use RBC?

2
Theory
This chapter will focus on the understanding of the N2O production pathways in biologi­
cal nitrogen removal (BNR) processes, which will serve understand the theoretical back­
ground for Q1 and Q2.
2.1
N2O production pathways
Nitrogen from wastewater, generally present in the form of ammonium NH+
4 [13], is con­
ventionally removed by a two­step process. First, nitrifying bacteria oxidize ammonia
(NH3) to nitrogen oxides (NO−
x) (nitrification), followed by anoxic NO−
x reduction to dinitro­
gen gas (N2) with organic matter (COD) as an electron source by heterotrophic denitrifying
bacteria (HB) (denitrification)[7]. Both processes can lead to the emission of N2O in WR­
RFs, thus a brief explanation of the concrete mechanisms of N2O production and their
mitigation strategies is now given.
2.1.1
Nitrifier Nitrification
Ammonia oxidizing bacteria (AOB) and archaea obtain the energy necessary for their
metabolism from the oxidation of ammonia (NH3) to nitrite (NO−
2) which is conducted in a
two­step process: first NH3 is oxidized to hydroxylamine (NH2OH) by the enzyme ammo­
nia monooxygenase (AMO) and then NH2OH is further oxidized to NO−
2 by the enzyme
hydroxylamine oxidoreductase (HAO)[14]. AOB can produce N2O from the incomplete
oxidation of NH2OH to nitrous acid (HNO2) via nitric oxide (NO) (which is then reduced
by NOR enzyme) or to its reduced form, nitroxyl (HNO) [7].
Figure 2.1: NN and ND production pathways (from Domingo­Félez and Smets, 2016 [7])
N2O emissions from this pathway can be controlled by minimizing the ammonium oxida­
tion rate (AOR), either by: 1) maintaining low ammonium concentrations (SNH+
4 ), which
not only promote a higher AMO gene expression, but also contribute to an accumula­
tion of NH2OH; 2) controlling dissolved oxygen (DO) levels through aeration (SO2); or 3)
controlling the biomass concentration of AOB (XAOB)[15].
4
==PAGE:9==
2.1.2
Nitrifier Denitrification
Under low DO conditions, AOB is also able to reduce NO−
2 to N2O via NO in the presence
of an electron donor such as NH2OH by NO−
2 and NO reductases (NirK and NoR respec­
tively) without the need for organic carbon. This process, known as nitrifier denitrification
(ND) occurs during aerobic conditions together with NH3 oxidation and it is enhanced
under microaerobic conditions [14].
The exact functions of this pathway in AOB remains unclear, but some hypothesis in­
clude: 1) energy conservation and production under low DO concentration or or electron
dissipation when present at high supply rates under high ammonium concentrations; 2)
decrease in competition for oxygen by removing the substrate for NOB; 3) detoxification
mechanism to remove the excess NO−
2 ; or 4) as an electron sink to speed up the oxidation
of NH2OH during aerobic metabolism [14].
The main mechanism to control this N2O generation pathway is to control NO−
2 levels.
This can be achieved either by employing longer SRT to encourage the growth of nitrite­
oxidizing bacteria (NOB) and therefore achieve full nitrification or by changing to intermit­
tent aeration, which would allow heterotrophic bacteria (HB) consume the excess NO−
2
that may have accumulated [15]. DO is also important, since it affects the expression
of the aforementioned enzymes (NO reductases): NO production, regulated by NirK, is
favoured under anoxic conditions; while NorB activity is upregulated under oxic conditions
[7]. A reduction of DO that does not compromise ammonium oxidation extent could likely
result in a reduction of N2O emissions from ND pathway as well as from NH2OH oxidation
pathway [15].
2.1.3
Heterotrophic Denitrification
Denitrification is performed by a very diverse group of microorganisms which couple ox­
idation of organic or inorganic substrates to reduction of NO−
3, NO−
2, NO, N2O and then
to N2 under anoxic conditions. Four different enzymes are involved in the process: ni­
trate reductase (NaR), NiR, NoR and N2O reductase (NoS) [14]. An incomplete reduction
of N2O to N2 in WRRF is mostly due to environmental factors that either impose higher
inhibition effects on NoS than other upstream nitrogen reductases (e.g. NoR and NoS
competition for electrons [7]), or make NoS less competitive for substrates (e.g. at low
COD levels), which results in N2O accumulation [14].
Figure 2.2: HD production pathway (from Domingo­Félez and Smets, 2016 [7])
Mitigation strategies from this pathway thus center on either avoiding the occurrence of
NO reduction or encouraging the N2O reduction.
This include avoiding unnecessary
anoxic periods and addition of external COD to avoid incomplete denitrification. Also,
mantaining NO−
2 levels low (specially as its protonated form FNA) may help avoid inhibi­
tion of NoS [15].
2.1.4
Abiotic Pathways
Nitrous oxide can also be produced abiotically in the environment. The two general routes
for this process are NH2OH decomposition and chemodenitrification [16]. In BNR pro­
5
==PAGE:10==
cesses, reported abiotic reactions for N2O production include the oxidation of NH2OH by
HNO2, O2 and Fe3+, as well as the reduction of HNO2 by Fe2+ [14]. The NDHA model
includes two abiotic reactions: NH2OH decomposition at high pH to N2O and the nitrosa­
tion of NH2OH with HNO2 [7]. N2O emissions from this pathways are usually mitigated by
avoiding NO−
2 accumulation, pH control, and DO control (as DO accelerates the nitration
rate of ammonia) [10]. However, for this work the contribution of abiotic pathways will not
be considered as it is estimated to be very little (less than 3% of the total N2O production)
in pH ranges between 6.5 and 8 [17].
2.2
Operational parameters and N2O production
In this section we will explore how some process variables affect the overall N2O produc­
tion, and which could be a good fit for an objective function that minimize N2O emission
(e.g. if they can be monitored online and/or correlate to N2O emissions). It is worth
mention this distinction between N2O production and emission, since only part of the to­
tal nitrous oxide that is produced in the liquid phase is emitted as a gas, mainly trough
stripping by aeration or mixing in the reactors.
2.2.1
Ammonium
Under high NH+
4 loading, an increased turnover of NH+
4 to NH2OH will cause NH2OH
accumulation, enhancing N2O production via the NH2OH oxidation pathway. Because
NH+
4 can be monitored continuously, e.g. using ion­selective electrodes (ISE) [18], it is
a parameter that can be used for emissions control. Moreover, due to the relation of the
AOR with the NN N2O emissions, NH+
4 has been previously used for the estimation of
N2O in partial nitratation (PN) reactors [19] [20].
In fact, the cost function used by Stentoft [11] to account N2O emissions, which will be used
in this work, follows a similar approach for those estimating N2O from the NN pathway
based on AOR. The comparison of using this approach vs calculating N2O with NDHA is
later presented in Table 4.8.
2.2.2
Nitrite
As discussed earlier, during the nitrification process, nitrite accumulation can enhance the
effect of nitrifier denitrification [21]. Meaning, under high nitrite concentrations AOB will
reduce more NO−
2 to N2O, which in turn will be emitted by the aeration. Moreover, high
nitrite concentrations during denitrification lead to a lower denitrification rate, due to the
competition for electrons between nitrite and NiR, NoR and NoS [22][13]. This can also
contribute to a type of N2O emissions at the start of aeration referred to in this work as
incomplete heterotrophic denitrification.
Nitrite oxidizing bacteria (NOB) play a key role in reducing NO−
2 concentration and achiev­
ing full conversion of ammonia to nitrate. In a monitoring campaign of 14 full­scale wastew­
ater treatment plants in Switzerland, seasonal nitrite accumulation in warmer months
(which subsequently lead to N2O peak emissions) was attributed to low NOB perfor­
mance [23]. This was also found true in a long­term monitoring campaign at a large
Danish WRRF [24]. This is explained because at temperatures higher than 25 °C, the
AOB specific growth rate increases and becomes higher than that of NOB [22].
While nitrite is often measured from grab samples, new sensor technology (e.g. optical
UV­spectral sensors) could facilitate online monitoring of this parameter for N2O emissions
control.

2.2.3
Dissolved Oxygen
In conditions of low NO−
2 accumulation and minimum interference by heterotrophic bac­
teria, DO has been shown to be the main factor governing N2O emission via the NH2OH
oxidation pathway (NN) [25]. Morever, since increasing DO levels require a stronger aer­
ation in the reactors, in turn it stimulates stripping and leads to an increased proportion of
the produced N2O leaving via the gas phase [22].
Low DO in nitrification can lead to more N2O accumulation, mostly by the nitrifier denitri­
fication pathway [13]. As mentioned before, AOB will use NO−
2 instead of O2 as electron
acceptor, and save O2 for the oxidation of nitrate [21]. However, too high aeration rates
during nitrification may increase oxygen introduction to the denitrification process, encour­
aging N2O accumulation through the heterotrophic denitrification pathway.
DO measurement is carried out using probes that use the electrochemical reaction of
oxygen diffusing from the liquid through a gas­permeable membrane [18]. Due to its im­
portance on control of WRRF’s aeration, oxygen sensors are one of the most widespread
instruments in wastewater treatment plants, making online monitoring of DO a good con­
tender for N2O emissions control.
2.2.4
COD/N ratio
During denitrification, organic carbon is required to provide the electron donor for the N
reductases [14]. Both the composition of the organic substrate and its availability are
important factors contributing to N2O emissions. Generally, higher denitrification rates
are achieved by employing easily biodegradable small organic matter as carbon source
[14]. However, under normal operation conditions, it is more difficult to control the carbon
source composition and therefore COD/NOx ratios are used to reflect carbon availability.
Because N2O reductase is less competitive when the availability of biodegradable organic
carbon is limited, N2O emission has been linked to low COD/NO−
3 ratios during denitrifica­
tion [22] [26]. However, finding the right ratio must be done experimentally, as too much
COD may lead to unwanted carbon in the effluent of the denitrification process [27]. More­
over, some authors stress the importance of not only COD availability, but rather the N
species involved, as the simultaneous presence of different nitrogen oxides (NO−
3, NO−
2
and N2O) may have a negative effect on their reduction rates [14]. This effect, known as
electron competition, was found to be the main responsible for N2O accumulation during
denitrification and not the COD/N ratio per se [28].
COD can be monitored online, with some limitations: it takes about half an hour to perform
the digestion, samples must be filtered, and requires chemical reagents [18]. Nitrate, on
the other hand, is measured usually with the same ISE as ammonia and could be used
for N2O emissions control [24].

3
Materials and Methods
3.1
Plant Layout and Simulation Environment
To test the MPC objective functions, this work will use a simulation environment based on
the Kolding WRRF, which has a treatment capacity of 125,000 PE and a load of 75,000
PE [29]. Nitrogen removal is achieved at this plant via the Bio­denitroTM process, de­
veloped by Krüger in the 1970s, consisting of two interconnected oxidation ditches that
can alternate between aerobic and anoxic phases, thus avoiding the need of an internal
recirculation pump [30]. A digital twin of the WRRF has been previously constructed in
MATLAB’s graphical programming environment (Simulink). To get a glimpse of what the
general layout of the plant looks like in this environment, the reader is refered to fig 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Layout of the Kolding WRRF as implemented in Simulink
In broad terms, the incoming wastewater —modeled after average measured dry weather
flow concentrations— enters a primary clarifier whose overflow serves as input to the
reactors. Inside the reactors, the biological processes are simulated by means of calling
an S­Function that uses the NDHA model. The setpoint for aeration is governed by either
a rule based controller, or taken over by the MPC. In the latter, ’sensor’ data (NH4 and
NO2 concentrations from reactor 1) is retrieved from time to time from the workspace so
that the MPC can run a simplified ASM1 model to compute the prediction of outcomes.
Lastly, the treated effluent passes through a settler that recirculates some of the activated
sludge back into the reactors, and exits both the settled effluent and the waste sludge out
of the system.
Said simulation environment will be used to test the performance of the MPC on mini­
mizing the objective functions in different price scenarios. These are presented in the
following sections.

3.3
Performance Indicators
To assess the performance of the different control strategies against the existing control
(e.g. regarding effluent quality, plant operation or GWP), a series of performance indica­
tors were used. A brief explanation on how they were calculated is provided bellow.
3.3.1
Effluent Quality
The main parameter for evaluating effluent quality will be Total Nitrogen. For this work, this
includes the sum of all nitrogen species concentrations considered in the NDHA model:
[NH3, NH2OH, NO2, NO3, NO,N2O, as well as organic nitrogen (soluble and particulate)].
Any control strategy that result in a 7 day average value of TN >8 mg/l will be considered
as a fail, as it would fall outside of the discharge limits.

5
Conclusions
By means of understanding the key variables that govern N2O production, it was possible
to formulate mathematical expressions that would model the NN, ND and ID pathway con­
tributions to the total N2O emissions from online measurements. The proposed objective
functions were tested on a digital twin of Kolding WRRF under different scenarios and its
performance was compared to the Rule Based Control that currently controls the aeration
of said plant.
In conclusion, the Model Predictive Control proposed in this work can achieve reduc­
tions in the Global Warming Potential and Total Costs of up to 4.21% and 7.75%, respec­
tively, when compared to the RBC. Moreover, the objective function proposed here to
account for incomplete denitrification (’N2O_ID’), when added to the soft constraints orig­
inally proposed by Stentoft [11], result in better effluent quality without compromising the
Total Costs. Although no special weights were used to prioritize among objectives, differ­
ent combinations of the proposed cost functions are presented, with their corresponding
percent difference, to facilitate decision making.
While the performance of the objective functions in regards to the specific N2O pathway
contributions remains unclear, a decoupling of the pathways directly from the NDHA is
proposed for future work. A revision of ’N2O_ND’ would be also necessary, in case Kold­
ing WRRF were to implement a NO2 sensor in the reactors (especially since right now
it seems to underestimate the N2O emissions). And last, the code where the MPC was
implemented needs further revision so that it would allow modelling ’negative’ electricity
prices.
