Abstract
In advanced integrated audio ampliÔ¨Åer systems, high-resolution analog-to-digital con-
verters (ADC) are used to gather and process internal signals. This thesis covers the
design and implementation of a low power 14 bit 500 kS
s Successive Approximation Reg-
ister (SAR) ADC using digital calibration as a way to increase resolution in the presence
of errors and device variations.
A design framework is constructed using Python and VerilogAMS modeling to create
an easy transition from system-level design to transistor-level implementation. Utiliz-
ing the Python SAR ADC model, the digital-to-analog converter (DAC) topology and
the unit capacitor value are selected based on capacitor mismatch using Monte Carlo
simulations. The DAC and comparator of the SAR ADC are implemented in a 130 nm
process using 1.5 V devices. The DAC is a binary-weighted charge redistribution topol-
ogy with switches designed for settling time requirements and parasitic minimization.
The comparator is a clocked comparator consisting of a high gain preampliÔ¨Åer and a
low power latch. An analysis of the error mechanisms and their e‚Üµects on the perfor-
mance of the SAR ADC is carried out and ways to improve error tolerance and error
correction using redundancy and digital calibration are investigated.
The full system SAR ADC presented in this project achieves a signal-to-noise-and-
distortion ratio (SNDR) of 72.01 dB which results in an e‚Üµective number of bits (ENOB)
of 11.67 bits. The SAR ADC has a total power consumption of 264.09 ¬µW and takes up
an area of 8346.13 ¬µm2. The SAR ADC achieves a Schreier Ô¨Ågure-of-merit (FOMS) of
161.77 dB with respect to SNDR. Digital calibration shows to be able to account for the
e‚Üµect of capacitor mismatch given that the capacitor weights are known. Future work
should look at a way of combining capacitor weight estimation with digital calibration.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In recent years, streaming services such as Spotify have made music more accessible in
our daily lives. Music streaming is accessible by any device with an internet connection
thereby creating a shift from a single music source such as a CD player, iPod, or
mp3 player to a more distributed number of music sources. Having music available
everywhere has increased the demand for wireless solutions such as Bluetooth speakers
and wireless headphones. One of the main technological challenges regarding wireless
battery powered speakers is the question: how do you provide high audio quality and at
the same time have a long battery life? InÔ¨Åneon Technologies AG answers that question
with their brand of audio chips MERUSTM [1]. The chip uses a highly power-eÔ¨Écient
monolithic class D audio ampliÔ¨Åer which utilizes multilevel switching to achieve great
audio performance and low power consumption. The chip contains multiple integrated
functionalities that enable it to process digital audio signals and output an ampliÔ¨Åed
signal able to drive speakers. To ensure the functionality of all the sub-circuits, the
chip needs to gather information about the signal on the chip itself. The chip gathers
these internal signals to ensure that every element is working as intended.
In order for the main processor of the chip to process the analog signals, the signals
Ô¨Årst need to be converted into digital bits. This task is performed by an analog-to-
digital converter (ADC) which is the subject of this thesis. Having one converter per
signal would take up much space on the chip, instead it is more area eÔ¨Écient to have a
single ADC which converts all the signals. A multiplexer is located in front of the ADC
and selects which of the input signals are to be converted. The challenge arises when
designing the ADC as it needs to be able to convert signals at a conversion rate equal to
2N of the highest input signal where N is the number of input signals. Furthermore, the
ADC needs to achieve a suÔ¨Éciently high resolution to ensure the precision of the signal
measurement and in a system like the MERUSTM chip, minimal power consumption is
essential.

The goal of the project is to design and implement a high-resolution successive approxi-

is chosen for its lower area and power consumption while still achieving high resolution
and conversion speeds. The aim for the ADC is to achieve 14 bit with a sample rate
of 500 kS

error mechanisms of SAR ADCs and how these mechanisms a‚Üµect the performance of
the ADC. Analysis and categorizing of these error mechanisms is to be made. Fur-
thermore, an investigation into error correction and digital calibration techniques is
to be done. These error correction and calibration methods must only have a limited
amount of added analog circuitry because they need to have limited added power and
area consumption. The SAR ADC consists of three main blocks: the controller, the
comparator, and the digital-to-analog converter (DAC). Each of these blocks are to be
modeled, designed, and implemented. The comparator and the DAC are to be imple-
mented using 1.5 V device models from the 130 nm process. The controller is to be
implemented in a hardware description language such as Verilog or Very high speed in-

The Binary Search Algorithm
The binary search algorithm is a well-known algorithm within the Ô¨Åeld of computer
science and is used to Ô¨Ånd a speciÔ¨Åc value in a sorted list. The algorithm locates the
desired value by testing di‚Üµerent values and see whether it is greater or lower than
the desired value. The algorithm can locate the desired value in N number of steps.
The search algorithm starts by testing the middle value of the full search range. If the
desired value is smaller than the middle value the search range is reduced to the lower
50% of the full search range. If the desired value is greater than the middle value, the
search range is reduced to the upper 50% of the full search range. The next test value
is the center of the new smaller search range and this value is compared to the desired
value. In this way, the algorithm reduces the search range by half for each step until

Error Mechanisms in SAR ADC
Like any real circuit implementation, errors in a SAR ADC are prone to happen and
reduce performance. This section aims to present the main error sources present in
SAR ADCs and explain the resulting e‚Üµects on the system.
3.1.1
Conversion Errors
The Achilles‚Äô heel of the SAR is the binary search algorithm itself because an error in
an early conversion step is carried to the output at the EOC. Each bit is only tested
once and a wrong decision is irreversible for the rest of the conversion phase.
A wrong decision in the conversion phase is called a conversion error and the earlier
in the binary search it happens the larger the error is. A conversion error at third LSB
will at minimum produce an error above 0.5 LSB but the error could be as large as 7.5
LSB.
Figure 3.1 visualizes the e‚Üµect of a wrong decision in the conversion steps. The Ô¨Ågure
shows how a decision error at the second conversion causes the output to produce an
error of 3 LSBs.

red curve indicates a wrong decision and the resulting output value. If the 4 bit SAR
ADC has a VLSB of 93.75 mV, the input voltage of 850 mV should produce the digital
code [1 0 0 1]. The conversion error at the second bit-test causes it to be high instead
of low, the output voltage of the DAC would be too high for all subsequent conversion
steps. The SAR ADC has no way of recovering from the conversion error. Even if all
subsequent bits are turned low the resulting output code would equal [1 1 0 0] which
represents an input voltage of 1125 mV. The error voltage of this digital output would

Mismatch Error
A key source of errors in a SAR ADC as stated in the literature [2]‚Äì[5] is the mismatch
of the capacitor array. As the binary scaling of the capacitor array is what enables the
binary search, poor matching severely degrades the performance of the SAR ADC.
Mismatch of capacitors is largely attributed to edge e‚Üµects and area non-uniformity.
Area non-uniformity is the random variation of thickness and permittivity of the insu-
lating material between the two capacitive plates. Edge e‚Üµects are the variation of the
edge of the capacitive plate from a straight edge [14]. Edge e‚Üµects are caused by the
uneven etching of the capacitor material during the fabrication process. It is possible
to reduce edge e‚Üµects by constructing large capacitors from a number of parallel unit
capacitors and by paying special attention to the layout.

Redundancy
In section 3.1.1 it was showed that a normal SAR can not correct for a wrong decision in
the conversion phase and Ô¨Ågure 3.1 visualises how a conversion error a‚Üµect the output
code.
A way to make the data converter more robust towards decision error in the conver-
sion phase is by adding redundancy to the SAR ADC. Figure 3.13 shows a SAR ADC
running with an extra conversion step that results in overlapping search range. The
blue curve shows the ideal conversion decisions for the input and the red curve shows
a conversion with a decision error. It can be seen that even though a decision error
occurs the overlapping search range enables the SAR to correct for the error and arrive
at the correct output value.
Figure 3.13: Redundancy creates overlapping search range which enables robustness
towards decision error in the conversion phase [10].
The overlapping search range means the ADC has multiple ways to arrive at the
same output value. This addition of redundancy results in more conversion steps for the
same number of bits. With redundancy, some amount of settling error can be tolerated
and the ADC with redundancy can thereby achieve a higher frequency of conversion
steps [3] resulting in a similar or even higher conversion rate.
Redundancy can be implemented in various ways. Redundant bit capacitors can be
added to the capacitive array which performs redundant searches for the same value.
This type of redundancy can easily be implemented on a circuit level as the amount of

property of the capacitor array as each new capacitor is made of unit capacitors. The
control logic needs to be changed to accommodate the extra conversion steps [23].
In [24] the decision levels are based on a predeÔ¨Åned error tolerance and amount of
redundancy. The capacitive array constructs several unit-sized capacitors, but unlike

3.2.3

To circumvent the need for extra encoder and decoder circuitry, redundancy can be
implemented directly in the scaling between each capacitor in the capacitive array. The
binary scaling of the capacitors is also called radix-2 scaling. With the scaling of each
capacitor less than two means that the MSB capacitor is no longer larger than the sum
of the LSB capacitors. In other words, by using a sub-radix-2 or sub-binary scaling
of the capacitors in the array, the same output value can be reached by multiple bit
combinations i.e. overlapping search ranges. Figure 3.14 shows the transfer curve of a
radix-2 SAR ADC and a sub-radix-2 SAC ADC. It can be seen that the sub-radix-2

4.1
Design Framework
This project takes a top-down approach in investigating, analyzing and designing the
14b SAR ADC where design Ô¨Çow moves from a functional overview of the SAR to a
detailed implementation. A Python model is developed which describes the function-
ality of the SAR ADC in two orders of complexity. The Ô¨Årst order of complexity is a
zeroth-order model that describes the basic binary search algorithm. The second order
of complexity is a Ô¨Årst-order model which describes the functionality of each block of
the SAR ADC and combines them to perform A/D conversion. The Python model is
expanded by including various error mechanisms. The goal is to give a system-level
perspective of how the SAR ADC operates and how di‚Üµerent error mechanisms a‚Üµect
the performance on a system level. The Python model enables fast comparison between
di‚Üµerent SAR topologies, calibration schemes, and error correction methods.
To ease the transition from the Python model to an actual transistor implementation
a VerilogAMS model is developed. The VerilogAMS model works as a stepping stone
to breach the gap between the Python level and transistor level. Each SAR ADC block
is implemented as a functional VerilogAMS block and the system can be evaluated in
an analog-mixed-signal (AMS) environment with Cadence Virtuoso. The strength of
this approach is that each block in the system can be swapped out for a transistor-level
implementation and the system can be simulated. The VerilogAMS model enables the
test of the individual transistor level blocks and their e‚Üµect on the system without
having to design all blocks at once. The framework o‚Üµers a smooth design Ô¨Çow as
the e‚Üµortless transition from one level to the other creates an easy evaluation of the
mechanisms at each level. The three levels of abstraction create a cyclic design Ô¨Çow
were the Ô¨Åndings in one level feedback into the others. For instance, the size of the unit
capacitor in the DAC is found in the Python level via some assumption about mismatch
but at the transistor level, it is discovered that parasitics degrade performance. This
discovery then feeds back and enables a redesign at the Python and VerilogAMS levels.

canceled out. The charge stored in the channel of the NMOS is oppositely charged

tuned to ensure simultaneous signals.

6.1
Discussion
This section goes over and discusses the main Ô¨Åndings of this thesis. Key results will
be highlighted and compared to the concepts presented in the literature.
The input referred-noise of the comparator shown in Ô¨Ågure 5.12 exceeds 0.5 VLSB
and therefore it can be expected that the comparator would reduce the performance of
the SAR ADC by 1 bit due to input-referred noise. Transient noise during the sine wave
conversion is not evaluated due to very long simulation times. One possible solution to
reduce comparator noise would be oversampling. By oversampling the input it would
be possible to remove some of the e‚Üµects of comparator noise by averaging the over-
sampled values. Using oversampling would either require a reduction of the conversion
rate of the SAR ADC or the DAC should be redesigned to have a faster settling time.
Redesigning the DAC would be a challenge as it would need a settling time X times
faster where X is the amount of oversampling. The DAC is not achieving the desired
performance at the current settling time and increasing it to be X times faster only
increases the design challenge of the DAC. A more suitable solution could be to use
majority voting where the comparator makes several comparisons per conversion and
a counter counts the number of logic highs vs logic lows [38]. Then, based on whether
there are more high outputs or more low outputs the controller sees it as a high or low
comparison. This is a way to implement averaging of the comparator decisions without
having to perform multiple conversions. The propagation delay of the comparator in
this design is so small that it could perform multiple comparisons within one conversion
step. The main trade-o‚Üµhere is an increase in power consumption.
O‚Üµset errors are linear and reduce the full-scale of the signal. The 4 mV input o‚Üµset due
to mismatch of the comparator should, therefore, result in a 12 mV reduction of the full
scale for 3 œÉ. Assuming the SAR ADC application tolerates a 12 mV reduction in the
possible input range of the SAR ADC, comparator o‚Üµset should not reduce performance.

6.1. DISCUSSION
CHAPTER 6
Based on the results presented in Ô¨Ågure 5.17, 5.14e, and 5.13, the conclusion can be
drawn that the main cause of performance limitation is the DAC. Figure 5.17 shows
that the full design implementation achieved 11.67 bits of resolution. By comparing the
performance of the DAC on its own versus the comparator on its own which are shown
in Ô¨Ågures 5.14e and 5.13, it is seen that the comparator can be used to produce an
ENOB of 14 bits whereas the DAC can only produce an ENOB of 11.67 bits. The error
location of the DAC must be due to the switch design as the capacitors have not yet been
implemented. E‚Üµects of charge injection, clock feed through, and parasitic capacitance
have been explained in section 3.1, but the magnitude of these e‚Üµects on the designed
system has not yet been quantiÔ¨Åed. Some e‚Üµort has been put into reducing charge
injection by controlling the complementary signals for the TGs. To locate the source
of the performance degradation more accurately, further investigations should be made
into quantifying the e‚Üµects of the parasitics. Furthermore, the capacitor would need to
be implemented as real circuit implementations before the accurate performance of the
DAC is known. Some e‚Üµort was put into the investigation of the parasitics of the MOM
capacitor structure, but only to a brief extend. As shown in [29], the speciÔ¨Åcs of MOM
capacitor structures and their parasitics are a nontrivial matter. A MOM capacitor
structure with the desired capacitor value would need to be constructed and parasitics
should be extracted to quantify the e‚Üµects of capacitor parasitics more accurately.
A particular Ô¨Ånding in this project is the matching requirement for the split DAC
compared to the binary-weighted DAC shown in section 5.1.3. The split DAC is often
used in the literature, see [39]‚Äì[42] as examples, and the topology is presented in [3] and
[2] as a way to reduce area consumption. From a pure matching perspective, the results
in this project show that the split DAC does not achieve a reduced area as the split
DAC uses a comparable area to achieve matching comparable to the binary-weighted
DAC. The total capacitance of the binary-weighted DAC from Ô¨Ågure 5.4a is 214 ¬∑ 1 fF =
16.384 pF and the split DAC from Ô¨Ågure 5.4c is ‚á°(27 +27 ‚àí1)¬∑68 fF = 17.340 pF. The
greater matching requirement for the split DAC is also highlighted in [19]. The paper
goes on to state that because the binary-weighted DAC has smaller unit capacitor values
which MIM capacitor structures usually do not provide, the binary-weighted DAC relies
on custom MOM capacitor structures. This notion has been presented in this project,
but the paper goes on to state that larger mismatch parameters of MOM structure
cause the binary-weighted DACs to have a greater matching requirement compared to
split DACs as split DACs can use MIM capacitors. A key point of the matching re-
quirement of the split DAC is that when redundancy and calibration are implemented,
which is the case in [19], [39]‚Äì[42], the matching requirements become much less strin-
gent. Redundancy lessens the e‚Üµects of mismatch and calibration aims to account for
the mismatch error that occurs. If redundancy and calibration are implemented, the
split DAC could show a better performance in terms of area and thereby potentially
also power.

6.2. FUTURE WORK
Throughout the work of this project, an important distinction became clear. Weight
calibration and weight estimation, or weight extraction, are two separate concepts. Es-
pecially in terms of the challenge they produce in implementation. This project shows
how weight calibration can be done in the digital domain when the actual weights i.e.
capacitor values are known. The problem many publications try to solve is the question
of how to extract these capacitor values. Publications like [25], [39], [40], [42] all try
to extract information about how the capacitor values have deviated from the nominal
values. Many of these publications only show simulated results or utilize external Field-
Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGA) or computers to implement the weight extraction
and calibration engine. This presents a potential area for research to create a fully
monolithic high-resolution digital calibrated SAR ADC with digital weight extraction.
This project has shown a single design iteration of the full design Ô¨Çow from model-
ing in Python to the transistor level implementation. The knowledge acquired at the
implementational level can be fed back to the computational level to improve the model
further. The framework is set up and future design iterations should be faster to im-
plement and each iteration strengthens the next. For instance, when a MOM capacitor
structure is constructed and parasitics and mismatch are extracted, then these values
can be parameters in the higher-level models. The design framework has also shown its
beneÔ¨Åt in this single iteration, as utilizing the VerilogAMS model enabled the isolation
of the source of the performance degradation of the system to the DAC.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This thesis presents the design and implementation of a 14 bit 500 kHz successive ap-
proximation register (SAR) analog-to-digital converter (ADC) in a 130 ¬µm process.
The SAR ADC was designed using a three-level design framework utilizing system-
level modeling in Python, behavioral modeling in VerilogAMS, and transistor-level
design. The system achieved a signal-to-noise-and-distortion ratio (SNDR) of 72 dB
which corresponds to an e‚Üµective number of bit (ENOB) of 11.67 bits. The total power
consumption equals 264.09 ¬µW and the total area is estimated to be 8346.131 ¬µm2 using
2
fF
¬µm2 metal-insulate-metal capacitors. The system achieved a Schreier Ô¨Ågure of merit
(FOMS) of 161.76 dB. Digital weight calibration was shown to improve the resolution
in the case of mismatch given that the real capacitor weights were known.
Motivation and project description deÔ¨Åning the scope of the project were presented
as well as the design speciÔ¨Åcation and learning objectives.
The fundamental theory and concepts behind data converters were presented which
covers the technical concepts needed to understand the design and performance evalu-
ation of data converters. The basic principles behind SAR ADCs were explained and
the main building blocks of the system were presented.
Errors such as settling error, gain error, o‚Üµset, and conversion error were presented
and the mechanisms which cause these errors were analyzed. Techniques to reduce the
e‚Üµects of these error mechanisms through the use of redundancy and calibration were
shown. A detailed explanation of digital weight calibration were carried out.
A Python model of the SAR ADC was developed which enabled the analysis and
design of the converter on a systemic level. The Python model enabled an investigation
of the e‚Üµects of mismatch by modeling capacitor mismatch in the digital-to-analog
converter (DAC) and by use of the Monte Carlo method. Using mismatch analysis it
was possible to investigate two topologies for the DAC and select the best-suited option.
VerilogAMS was used to implement the controller used to perform the binary search
algorithm which enabled the analog-to-digital conversion of the SAR ADC. VerilogAMS

behavioral models of the comparator and DAC were also implemented to create an easy
distinction between the performance of each block in the SAR ADC.
A binary-weighted charge redistribution DAC was implemented at a transistor level
using transmission gate switches and a bootstrapped NMOS switch. The design criteria
for the DAC were based on the settling requirements as well as the minimization of
parasitic e‚Üµects. The comparator was implemented as a clocked comparator consisting
of a high gain preampliÔ¨Åer and a low power latch. The comparator has an input-referred
o‚Üµset standard deviation of 4.1 mV and input-referred noise of 83.56 ¬µV. Used in a full
SAR ADC with the behavioral DAC in VerilogAMS, the comparator could achieve
an SNDR of 86.822 dB when not accounting for noise. It was shown that the main
performance-limiting factor was the DAC as a similar performance was found in the
full system implementation of the SAR ADC and a system using the ideal VerilogAMS
comparator with the designed DAC.
Future work would need to look into improved switch design as they are believed to
be the main cause of performance limitation. A Metal-Oxide-Metal (MOM) capacitor
structure was not implemented in this project due to time constraints and study into
optimal MOM capacitor structure design would be needed. Furthermore, digital weight
calibration was done under the assumption that the capacitor variations are known.
Future work would look into ways to implement a capacitor weight estimation scheme
and implement it together with the digital weight calibration to achieve a full digital
calibration setup.
