Abstract
With challenges in making industries and supply chains more sustainable and future-
proof, new types of vehicle routing problems need to be investigated.
One of these
problems is the two-echelon location routing problem with split delivery. In this prob-
lem, material is moved through a network using a fleet of vehicles. The material flow
originates at the source, which is in the first echelon and then gets moved to plants,
connecting first and second echelon. In the second echelon, the material is dispatched
from the plants and sent to customers. Hence, there are three different types of nodes
distributed over two echelons. The first echelon contains sources and plants, the second
echelon plants and customers. The overall goal is to fulfill the customers’ demands in
the second echelon with goods from the first echelon. Further, split delivery is enabled
for vehicle routing. Hence, in the first echelon demand of plants and in the second ech-
elon customers’ demand can be fulfilled by multiple vehicles. In addition to the vehicle
routing within each echelon, it is to be determined which of the provided plants are to be
opened. Both, routing decisions and decisions on opening plants need to be coordinated
between the echelons while minimising total costs for vehicle routing and opening plants.
In this thesis, a mathematical model is introduced, which describes this problem.
Further, we propose a memetic algorithm to solve this problem with a heuristic approach.
This heuristic has been studied on different problem types such as two-echelon vehicle
routing problems and location routing problems where it has been proven to be eﬀicient.
Due to its modularity, the memetic algorithm is considered to be adaptable to different
problem types by using and combining already established and well-researched heuristics.
The model and heuristic were tested on a total of 54 benchmarking instances of
different sizes from 20 customers and 5 plants up to 200 customers and 10 plants. All
instances featured only a single source on the first echelon. The model showed to become
intractable at instances with 50 customers and 10 plants, already significantly slowing
down and struggling to find near optimal solutions for instances with less customers
and plants within 30 minutes. The memetic algorithm proved to find better solutions
than the mathematical model within the same time frame, improving solution quality
by 32.4% on average. However, comparing the output to another heuristic developed for
the same problem, it gets outperformed in all instances. The solutions provided by the
memetic algorithm are worse, with an increase in objective value ranging between 24.0%
and 678.7% or 262.6% depending on the time limit. The memetic algorithm especially
struggled with long computation times resulting in less improvement operations with
a growing problem size, indicating that the implementation can be improved in future
research.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In recent years, increasing attention in research was given to reverse logistics and circular
economy among policy makers and in the industry [1]–[3]. According to Kircherr et al.
[3], circular economy is a tool to implement sustainable development for businesses.
According to them, the most common definition for a circular economy’s core values
are ”reduce, reuse, recycle”. In the European Union’s Waste Framework Directive [4],
”recover” is added as a fourth core value [3]. Thus, a circular economy closes currently
linear supply chains reducing and reusing waste by, among other methods, reintroducing
it into the production cycle. This reintroduction is done by reverse logistics, requiring
companies to transition [1].
To enable this transition from a conventional linear supply chain towards a circular
supply chain, different tools are required. In their papers, Mallick et al. [1], Kircherr et
al. [2] and Kircherr et al. [3] looked at circular economy and reverse logistics more from
a managerial perspective, discussing the definition and goals of those concepts (see [2],
[3]) or developing tools for designing a complete concept for reverse logistics (see [1]).
Once a first concept is developed, also operational tools are needed to provide a
more in-depth overview on feasibility and possibly also making the individual steps
in the concept more actionable. One important step is network design, which contains
decisions about waste collection, transport and recovery among others [1]. Especially the
transportation step can be formulated as a mathematical optimisation problem. When
transferring from linearity towards circularity, a transformation on both ends of one or
more supply chains is needed connecting them with each other. Thus, an existing supply
chain needs to be reevaluated and adjusted to make it circular. As already explained,
the basic idea of circular supply chains is to reduce waste by reintroducing it either
into their own or into another product’s supply chain [1]–[3].
Hence, there are two
levels with already existing facilities from one or more supply chains, which need to be
connected. Additionally, before reintroducing them into production, the waste material
might need to be processed, adding one or more new layers between the already existing
ones. Ideally, these processing facilities bundle incoming material streams from different
facilities and also serve demand at different facilities. To help determine a facilities’
optimal position to, for example, minimise costs or maximise material flow, a multi-
echelon vehicle routing and facility location problem can be formulated. This kind of
problem combines both, vehicle routing and decisions on facility location.
In this thesis, we consider the two-echelon location routing problem (LRP2E). This
problem considers two echelons, which are connected on one layer. The first echelon
contains the source of the material flow.
For example, this can be already existing
production facilities producing waste material. To reintroduce this waste into another

supply chain, it needs to be collected and processed at facilities on the middle layer.
However, this layer does not exist yet. Therefore, decisions on facility locations are
required. These facilities are the link between the source and final destination. Hence,
they are both, the destination nodes for the first echelon and the source for the second
echelon. The second echelon also contains the customers, reusing the recycled material
in their production. Further, vehicle routing within the echelons is optimised as well.
This kind of problem is for example considered by Esmailbeigi et al. [5]. They solved
instances for relocation of whey, a byproduct in cheese production, within the dairy in-
dustry, transporting the whey to a facility to processing, before sending it to a refinery.
In their paper, Esmailbeigi et al. [5] solved LRP2E instances with up to 51 cheesemak-
ers and optimality gap by using Benders decomposition. However, when adding more
cheesemakers, they state they were not able to solve the problem within ”reasonable
amount of time” [5]. In industry, also larger instances can occur, especially when con-
sidering a business to customer (B2C) market, making this limitation in instance size a
problem. The exact approach often only solves relatively small instances to optimality
within a certain time frame, whereas metaheuristics are used for larger instances to find
a near optimal solution (e.g. [5], [6], [7]). Some metaheuristics already developed for a
LRP2E are for example an adaptive large neighbourhood search (ALNS) by Ettrich [8]
or a greedy randomized adaptive search procedure by Nguyen et al. [9]. Ettrich’s [8]
metaheuristic hereby also allows split deliveries, which the greedy randomized adaptive
search procedure by Nguyen et al. [9] does not.
In this thesis, we take a different approach by developing and adapting a memetic
algorithm (MA) for the LRP2E with split deliveries. The metaheuristic is implemented
and results are generated using it. The solutions are then compared to the results using
a conventional solver with a time limit. Further, the performance of our MA is compared
to the ALNS by Ettrich [8].
This thesis is structured as follows: In the Chapter 2, a literature review is conducted,
explaining metaheuristics and the LRP2E as well as other types of vehicle routing prob-
lem (VRP) in more detail. Chapter 3 formally defines the LRP2E and gives a short
overview of the verification and the performance of the exact approach. Chapter 4 de-
scribes the specific MA we developed and describes its components in detail. Further,
the process of hyperparameter tuning is conducted. In Chapter 5, we compare our MA’s
performance with the exact approach and the ALNS developed by Ettrich [8]. Further,
we discuss our results and suggest possible changes to the developed MA. Finally, we
conclude this thesis in Chapter 6 summarising our findings and giving a short outlook
on potential future research.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Research
As mentioned in Chapter 1, the problem addressed in this thesis is a LRP2E. Thus, it is a
combination of several VRPs. The hierarchical location routing problem (HLRP), which
is a type of VRP consisting of both, a hierarchical facility location problem (HFLP) and
a split delivery location routing problem (SDLRP). The SDLRP can be further divided
into split delivery vehicle routing problems (SDVRP) and location routing problems
(LRP). Finally, there are two echelons. Thus, it also contains a two-echelon vehicle
routing problem (VRP2E), which is a sub-class of HFLPs. Nguyen et al. [9] define
the overall problem as NP-hard, as it generalizes known NP-hard problems such as the
VRP2E and the capacitated location routing problem (CLRP). Before giving an overview
of heuristics applicable to the LRP2E in Chapter 2.2, in Chapter 2.1 a more detailed
explanation and overview of research on VRPs is given.
2.1
Vehicle Routing Problems
The goal of VRPs is to find an optimal collection of routes which abide an underlying
problem’s constraint [10], [11]. Nearly all variations of VRPs are NP-hard [10]. Thus,
these problems take long to solve, but, ’good enough’ solutions found by heuristics can
be verified quickly [10].
The most studied and basic version of VRP is the capacitated vehicle routing problem
(CVRP) [10], [11]. CVRPs consist of a single depot and a set C = {1, ..., c} of c customers
with demand dc. Further, the problem has a homogenous fleet with k vehicles, which
can travel edges (i, j) with i, j ∈N = {0, 1, 2, ..., c}, where 0 is the depot and nodes
1, ..., c are the customers. Hence, C ⊂N. Traversing (i, j) has cost cij. This original
problem was first introduced as ”Truck Dispatching Problem” for oil trucks by Dantzig
and Ramser [12] and in form of the traveling-salesman problem by Dantzig et al. [13] [11],
[14]. Later, this problem was turned into the more general VRP by Clarke and Wright
[15] [16]. However, this basic model of the (C)VRP mainly is relevant in academia [11].
To be more applicable to the real world, various extensions to the VRP are made, adding
features such as time windows, split delivery or multiples sources [10].

2.1.1
Split Delivery Vehicle Routing Problem
One of these features that can be added to the VRP are split deliveries. Hence, as
opposed to classical VRPs, a customer’s demand can be fulfilled by multiple vehicles
[10], [17]. The constraint that each customer must be visited by only one vehicle is
relaxed.
Thus, the SDVRP is a relaxation of the CVRP [17], [18].
Enabling split
delivery can lead to a significant improvement in the objective value of the optimal
solution compared to its CVRP alternative [19]. However, the optimal solution of the
CVRP with unlimited fleet, therefore not minimizing number of vehicles, can also be
superior over a SDVRP with minimal fleet [17]. Thus, reducing the amount of vehicles
can reduce costs, especially if fixed costs apply per vehicle used. But binding it to the
minimum does not always yield the best results.
As SDVRPs are NP-hard [17], solvability is limited, especially for increasingly large
instances. Therefore, a major research effort is put into designing heuristics for SDVRPs
[17]. Among these methods are also hybrid algorithms which can solve to optimality by
breaking the NP-hard problem down into smaller, solvable ones, intensifying the search
on specific areas within the solution space [17]. An example for such an approach is
shown by Chen et al. [20] who combine an endpoint mixed integer program (EMIP)
with a variable length record-to-record travel algorithm (VRTR). Hereby, the EMIP
looks for a set of reallocation operations for nodes, maximising total savings [20]. The
resulting solution is then improved by the VRTR [17], [20]. Exact approaches are also
tested, solving instances including up to 144 customers to optimality [17].
2.1.2
Location Routing Problem
LRPs combine vehicle routing with decisions about opening or closing a location [10].
Originally, LRPs were uncapacitated. However, now most authors consider capacity
constraints resulting in the CLRP [21]. Within the basic CLRP, all vehicles are shared
between a set P = {1, ..., p} of p depots. Hence, the node set N = P ∪C. Next to
routing, another focus of the LRP is to determine which depots must be opened and
which stay closed [21]. However, this decision is constrained. In routing, a customer
c ∈C is assigned to a route, which picks up material at a opened depot p ∈P. Each
depot has a capacity wp, which must not be exceeded by the demand of all customers
assigned to the depot [21]. Number of routes are limited by vehicles [21]. Vehicles can
only be assigned to one route at most, which starts and ends at the same depot [21].
Finally, all vehicles have a capacity, which must not be exceeded and split delivery is
not considered [21].
The CLRP is also NP-hard, as it consists of two already NP-hard problems - the VRP
and depot location problem [21]. There are exact approaches, like an early branch-and-
bound algorithm by Laporte and Nobert [22] for solving a CLRP where only one depot
should be opened [21]. However, according to Prodhon and Prins [21], exact approaches
become intractable solving LRPs with more than 50 customers. Thus, heuristics and
metaheuristics are needed to solve LRPs, like the memetic algorithm by Prins et al. [23]
[21].

LRPs can also come in different variations and combinations, such as the two-echelon
LRP (LRP2E) [21]. VRP2Es are explained in more detail in Chapter 2.1.4.
2.1.3
Hierarchical Facility Location Problem
Hierarchical facility location problems (HFLP) are a class of problems focusing on select-
ing a certain amount of facilities to fulfill all customer demand [24]. Hereby, the term
’hierarchical’ describes the structure of the facilities or nodes being arranged on different
’levels’ with the customers being on the final level. The facilities can provide different
services fulfilling demand for different customers [24]. Further, according to Sahin and
Süral [25], there are three different types of flow patterns in HFLPs. Single-flow patterns
have a material flow from a start node at the highest level in the hierarchy through facil-
ities at all levels until reaching a customer node the lowest level or vice-versa [24], [25].
In multi-flow patterns sinks are at the same level as in single-flow patterns, whereas the
route can be at any level [24], [25]. However, the material flow only has to go through
a subset of levels, possibly skipping some levels [24], [25]. There is also a third flow
pattern called ’bidirectional flow allowing material flow to go into both directions’ [24].
In this thesis, only single-flow patterns are considered.
For solving the HFLP, there are two different mixed integer programming models [25].
One are flow-based formulations, where demand flows from a facility at one level to a
facility at the next level in the hierarchy [25]. The other, assignment-based, formulation
allocates a specific demand to each facility at each level [25].
The class of HFLPs
can further be divided into four sub-classes [24]: ’Multi-level facility location problems’,
’median and covering hierarchical location problems’, ’hierarchical hub location problems’
and ’multi-echelon location-routing problems’.
2.1.4
Two-Echelon Vehicle Routing Problem
The term VRP2E or more general ’multi-echelon location-routing problem’ describes
types of VRPs consisting of 2 or more pairs of levels called echelons [6], [24], [26], [27].
Here, multiple levels or layers of facilities, interact with eachother. These problems also
fall into the category of VRPs with multiple synchronisation constraints (VRPMSC), as
vehicles and material flow between levels must be coordinated such that deliveries to
touch points from one layer fulfill the demand from the next layer at said touch points
[28]. According to Drexl [28], n-echelon VRPs and LRPs are the most studied VRPMSC,
proofing their relevance, especially in practical applications [6].
Heuristics to solve or optimise VRP2Es include a greedy randomized adaptive search
procedure (GRASP) by Nguyen et al. [9] or MAs like the one by Zhou et al. [29]. Exact
approaches mainly focus on the classical two echelon capacitated vehicle routing problem
(CVRP2E) solving up to 300 customers and 15 satelite depots [30]. In the CVRP2E,
the first echelon encompasses a single source and satelite depots. The second echelon
connects the satelite depots with the customers.

In the problem presented in this thesis, the facilities on at least one layer are not fixed and
therefore require both, vehicle routing between the layers and facility location decisions
on layers without fixed facilities [24]. Hence, resulting in a LRP2E with one or more
sources and depots in the first echelon and depots and customers in the second echelon.
Depots can be opened or closed, which is decided by the heuristic or solver applied on

2.2
Metaheuristics
As exact approaches for most optimisation problems do not scale well, heuristics and
metaheuristics are developed to find a ’good enough’ solution to the problem [31]–[34].
Heuristics thereby describe more problem-dependent solution approaches, whereas meta-
heuristics are more ’generic frameworks’ applicable to multiple optimization problems
[31]–[33]. Hence, metaheuristics are adaptable to solve different types of optimization
problems. More specific, metaheuristics are computational methods trying to find a
better solution to a problem by iteratively trying to improve on or a set of initial solu-
tions with respect to a given quality measure [33]. The term ’metaheuristic’ was first
mentioned by Glover [35] with respect to tabu search. Here, ’metaheuristic’ was used
to highlight the fact that tabu search imposes a tabu on another subordinated heuristic
[35]. Different heurisitcs can be inserted into a metaheurstic, making them usable inde-
pendently from a specific type of problem [31], [32], [35]. Thus, metaheuristics can use
different heuristics, depending on the problem to solve, ’guiding’ the overall search for
a solution to the underlying problem [32], [33]. Further, they can be described as a two-
step approach in generating feasible ’good enough’ solutions. Instead of just plugging in
an initial solution of the instance, a balance between ”diversification and intensification”
is given [32]. The first term, sometimes also referred to as ’exploration’, explores the
solution space looking for new possible solutions [31], [32], [36]. The latter, also called
”exploitation”, tries to improve the current solution [31], [32], [36]. This way, it is easier
to prevent getting stuck in a local minimum.
There are many types of metaheuristics with different approaches. In Chapter 2.2.1,
an overview of several metaheuristics and an approach to classify them is given. In
Chapter 2.2.3, the metaheuristic used in this thesis is explained in more detail.
2.2.1
Common Metaheuristics
There are many different metaheuristics and several approaches to classify them [32].
Common methods for classification are ”trajectory-based and population-based” as well
as ”nature-inspired and non-nature inspired” [31], [32].
Further, Abdel-Basset et al.
[31] suggest a classification by metaphor based versus non-metaphor based heuristics.
Hence, metaheuristics often apply a logic found in the real world, like genetic algo-
rithms [31], [32], [37]. Thereby, most inspiration comes from biology [37]. However,
==PAGE:14==
2.2 Metaheuristics
7
with hybrid algorithms, classification becomes more diﬀicult, as they can fit multiple
categories. This applies especially to a classification by ”nature-inspired and non-nature
inspired” [32]. Ongoing in this thesis, the classification between ”trajectory-based and
population-based” metaheuristics is used.
According to Desale et al. [33], the most widely known meta-heuristics are genetic al-
gorithms (GA), simulated annealing (SA) and tabu search (TS). SA uses a ’temperature’
as a guiding strategy for its search [33]. Its main advantage over other metaheuristics
is its ability to escape local minima [33]. TS is trajectory-based. Thus, it starts with a
single solution and tries to improve it [33], [36]. TS uses ’tabu lists’, which forbid certain
moves trying to prevent revisiting former solutions to avoid getting stuck in local min-
ima [33]. GA is an evolutionary algorithm (EA) managing and improving a population
of multiple solutions to one problem by crossing them [33], [38], [39]. Advantage of
the GA is its global orientation originating from its diverse population [39]. As already
mentioned, GAs are EAs emulating the evolutionary process by mutating and crossing
distinct elements in its population [33], [39], [40]. Further, the GA inspired many other
EAs, like differential evolution [38]. Other well known metaheuristics are ant colony
optimization, scatter search and iterated local search (ILS), among others [41].
Next to classical metaheuristics like the ones mentioned before, there are also hybridi-
sations of multiple metaheuristics. These apply and combine mechanics from multiple
strategies [41]. The next Chapter (2.2.2) goes into more detail on hybrid metaheuristics.
2.2.2
Hybrid Algorithms
Hybridisation of metaheuristics combines the advantages of multiple algorithms by simul-
tainiously reducing disadvantages [42]. The combination of two or more metaheuristics
is called hybrid metaheuristics [41]–[43].
For example, when looking at EAs like GA, two major problems arise: ”premature
convergence” and ”slow convergence” [42]. Both issues are related to the population and
its solutions quality. However, hybrid algorithms also have some disadvantages like high
complexity and a decrease in computational speed [42]. Nonetheless, hybridisation of
metaheuristics improves results in terms of overall convergence speed and accuracy [42].
Further, the ability to combine multiple pre-existing heuristics and their advantages
to provide a higher flexibility in their application are another advantage of hybridised
algorithms [40].
One example for a hybrid algorithm is the MA. MAs are population based meta-
heuristics related to genetic algorithms, which apply multiple improvement strategies
on their population. In literature, MAs and MA-hybrids are a popular metaheuristic
for SDVLRPs and HVRPs, as well as for VRP2Es (e.g. [29], [44]–[51]). The following
Chapter 2.2.3 explains MAs and their application for the problems mentioned in more
detail.

2.2.3
Memetic Algorithms
MAs were first introduced by Pablo Moscato [34] in 1989.
They are based on the
concept of the EA, also applying a local search (LS) heuristic on its population [32],
[34], [52]. Therefore, they are a hybrid algorithm. The original MA is a hybridization
of EAs, simulated annealing and tabu search [40]. However, since then many different
approaches were made. The term ’memetic’ hereby comes from ’meme’, which is the
conversion of a gene into the context of cultural evolution [40]. Other names for MAs
found in literature are ”hybrid EAs” or ”Lamarckian EAs” [40]
Memetic Algorithms are eﬀicient in solving both, large scale optimization problems
[53] and in general problems perceived as hard to solve [40]. The advantage of MAs over
more conventional EAs like GAs is its hybridisation. Hybridisation makes MAs rather
a baseline on which different methods can be added. Therefore, MAs can get adapted
to a specific problem type, by adding already existing, problem specific solutions such
as heuristics, LS methods or even truncated exact methods [40]. Therefore, instead of
finding new approaches, pre-existing problem knowledge is incorporated, possibly even
combining mutliple approaches.
Due to their modularity and adapatbility, each MA might look different in detail.
However, all share the same basic structure. To begin with, an initial population is
created. This population contains a set of initial solutions, which is maintained while
the MA is running [34], [40]. The size of this population is a parameter, which needs
to be defined before initialisation of the population [53]. For the initialisation of the
population, ideally solutions with a good standard are already inserted [53]. However,
a population can contain all types of solutions ranging from any feasible solution over
’proto-solutions’ towards infeasible solutions [40]. Proto-solutions are a type of prelimi-
nary solution structure, which can be extended to produce a feasible solution, whereas
infeasible solutions need to be repaired [40]. These two ’incomplete’ solution types can
be included in the population under the assumption that repairing or extending them
can be done fast [40]. One method to initialise a population is for example by using
both, randomly generated solutions and solutions created by a heuristic like a greedy
heuristic [40], [49]. It is beneficial to use multiple methods in creating the population to
cover different regions within the solution space [49]. To keep the MA from converging
towards a local minimum too fast and maintain a possibility of finding new search areas,
the population’s diversity needs to be maintained [53], [54]. One method to keep a popu-
lation diverse is the introduction of a distance metric to avoid too similar solutions from
being added [44], [45], [53]. Using some kind of distance measurement for population
management, only allowing new solutions in the solution space if a certain distance to
the other population members is kept has proven eﬀicient [23], [44]–[46], [55]. In case a
population has converged too far, a population reset can be added [40].
After creating the population, the main body of the MA starts. Moscato and Cotta
[40] call this part ”generational step process”. Here, each solution is subject to both,
”competition” and ”cooperation” [40]. To begin with, at least two parent solutions are
selected. This selection can be done according to their fitness [40], but in more recent al-
gorithms is done randomly (for example [23], [44]–[46], [48], [55]). One method for parent
==PAGE:16==
2.2 Metaheuristics
9
selection are binary tournaments. which randomly select two members of the population
and pick the member with the better solution [23], [40], [44], [48], [55]. Moscato and
Cotta [40] call these type of selection methods ”non-proportionate” methods. This first
step is part of the ”competition” component. When at least two elements are selected,
the ”cooperative” part follows by combining the selected solutions with each other [40],
[53]. This can either result in multiple new solutions or only one single ’child’, depending
on the MA. This step is already part of the overall improvement process of the popula-
tion. The operators for recombining solutions used here are also called ’n-ary’ operators
with n > 1 [40]. Another type of improvement are unary operators such as mutation
operators like multiple k-Split or LS heuristics [40], [53], [56]. Multiple k-Split removes
k random customers from existing routes and reinserts them into the routeset by Split
Reinsertion, a greedy approach inserting nodes into existing routes [56]. Both processes
are explained in more detail in Chapter 4. These operators can be applied both, on solu-
tions before and after the cooperative step. As already mentioned before, the selection
of operators used can get fitted towards the underlying problem to solve. Both elements,
the improvement and combinatorial part are part of the ’reproduction stage’, where new
individuals are created from the already existing population [40]. After creating and
improving new solutions, it is decided which elements of the newly generated population
(≥1) and the old population are integrated into the new population [53]. For this,
quality and distance measurements are used to both, improve solution quality, but also
keep solution diversity.

2.2.4
Iterated local search
Another well known, more classical metaheuristic is ILS [41]. ILS algorithms can also
be very eﬀicient in improving solutions for VRPs like the ones developed by Silva et
al. [56] and Lin et al. [59]. As opposed to MAs, ILS only uses one single initial so-
lution constructed at each iteration [56], [59], [60]. Thus, ILS is a ’trajectory-based’
metaheuristic [31], [32]. This initial solution is then further improved by applying ran-
domised variable neighbourhood descend (RVND) iteratively within an inner loop [56],
[59], [60]. If an improvement is found within the inner loop, it is decided to replace the
current best solution of the inner loop by the improved solution [56], [59], [60]. After the
inner loop closes, it is decided whether the returned solution replaces the current overall
best solution. The decision criterion can be a simple ’better’ criterion like in the ILS
by Silva et al.[56]. However, these can lead to stagnation [60]. Other criterions like the
late acceptance hill climb by Burke and Bykov [61] try to avoid said stagnation by also
accepting worse solutions based on certain conditions. For example, late acceptance hill
climb, which is also used by Lin et al. [59], compares the current solution to an old ’best’
==PAGE:18==
2.2 Metaheuristics
11
solution from n iterations ago instead of comparing it to the current best solution[61].
Another way of avoiding stagnation are restarts [60].
Within the RVND function, both inter- and intra-route operators are applied. The
prior work by switching nodes between routes, whereas the latter try to improve the
routes themselves by repositioning nodes internally. Examples for inter-route operators
are Switch, Swap, Cross and RouteAddition, as well as modifications of Swap such as
Swap* and SD-Swap* [56], [59], [62]–[64]. Commonly used intra-route operators are
Exchange, Reinsertion, 2-Opt and Or-Opt [56], [59], [62]–[64].
Finally, as ILS like other iterative improvements easily get trapped in local min-
ima, perturbation mechanisms are added [60]. These mechanisms help escaping a local
minimum by modifying the current solution [60]. Examples for these mechanisms are:
• ’Double-Bridge Move’, which cuts four edges and introduces four new ones into
the current solution [60]
• ’Multiple k-Split’, which removes k randomly selected customers from the current
solution and reinserts them by Split Reinsertion [56]
• ’Slack Induction by String Removals (SISR) with SplitReinsertion’, which selects
k customers with SISR and then reinserts them by SplitReinsertion [59]
As opposed to a random selection in ’multiple k-Split’, SISR is a more complex method
selecting customers to be removed based on slack like capacity slack or spatial slack
(distance limitations) within the routes [65]. However, generally saying, edges or nodes
are removed from the existing solution and then modified or reinserted to create a new
solution outside the local minimum. Then, on the newly generated solution, RVND can
get applied again, possibly exploring a different region within the solution space.
To benefit from both metaheuristics, the algorithm developed in this paper is a hybrid
algorithm using both, a MA with ILS. Further the acceptance criterion for new solution
is adapted and reset multiple times, indirectly mimicking a mechanism of SA, however
slightly adapted. The algorithm is explained in Chapter 4. In the next Chapter 3, the
problem is introduced formally. Further, the mathematical model is verified on a small
instance and tests on larger instances are conducted.

CHAPTER 3
Model
As discussed in Chapter 2, the model is a HLRP with two echelons. There is a total of
three different node types. Sources, which are the first layer. Sources deliver goods to
plants, the second node type. All goods transported to plants can then get distributed
to customer nodes. The first echelon is made up of sources and plants. In the second
echelon, plants act as sources for the customers. In common terminology, plants are
also often called satellites, as they serve as satellite depots to a single source (e.g. [9]).
However, as we developed the model to potentially contain multiple sources, we opted
for plants, potentially applying an additional operational step.
For building the model, we made the following assumptions to keep the model as
general as possible:
1. There are two sets of vehicles. One set for echelon 1 for transport from the sources
(layer 1) to the processing plants (layer 2) and one set for echelon 2 for transport
from layer 2 to their customers (layer 3).
2. Routes start and end at the same depot (layer 1 for echelon 1 and layer 2 for
echelon 2) and can only visit one depot on their journey.
3. Vehicles are not bound to specific depots.
4. Only one period is considered.
5. Vehicles can serve multiple customers (layer 2 and layer 3 for echelon 1 and echelon
2 respectively).
6. Vehicles within each echelon are homogeneous.
7. Per vehicle a maximum of one route is possible.
8. Number of vehicles is limited. In case they are not limited by the instance itself,
they are set to the minimum value between minimum amount of vehicles needed
=
total demand
capacity vehicles in echelon and minimum amount of customers, also allowing for a
single route per customer, to prevent restricting number of nodes too much (see
Chapter 2.1.1).
9. Demand of all customers is known and must not exceed total vehicle or depot
capacity.
10. For payloads only weight is limiting, not shape of packaging or the like.

11. Facilities do not have processing time. The only restriction is the facility’s capacity.
12. Facilities are available immediately.

CHAPTER 4
Metaheuristic
As described in Chapter 2.2.3,a memetic algorithm (MA) was implemented using python,
due to its ability to handle large-size optimization problems. As LS structure we used
ILS, resulting in a hybrid algorithm. Due to the structure with the MA as primary
metaheuristic and ILS as subordinate heuristic, the whole metaheuristic is referred to
as MA in this thesis.
In this chapter the general structure of the MA is explained.
Further, the functionality of its components are described in more detail, followed by
hyperparameter tuning. We made the same assumptions as in Chapter 3.
4.1
Structure
The MA is structured as depicted in Figure 4.1. First a population is initialized with a
greedy algorithm and by perturbing the greedy solution. Then, two parents are selected
crossing their chromosomes resulting in a new ’child chromosome’. The child then gets
inserted into an ILS ideally diversifying and improving the solution. If the mean distance
towards the parents is above a fixed threshold, the chromosome with the worst objective
’w’ is compared to the child. If the child’s objective is better, w gets replaced by the
child. In case the threshold is not surpassed, the child gets compared to its closer parent
’p’. If p’s objective is worse than the child’s objective, p gets replaced by the child. In
case the child’s objective is worse than the one it is compared to, the population remains
unchanged. After the comparison, the threshold is divided by 2. Every 15 iterations or
if the current threshold is ≤2, the threshold is renewed. In case the new threshold is
too low, the population gets diversified by perturbing the ’worse’ half. The algorithm
terminates when surpassing either a time limit or its maximum amount of iterations.
After termination, the best feasible chromosome found is returned.
The MA has four underlying structures:
• Instances
• Solutions
• Chromosomes
• Population
Instances provide all necessary input data for the MA to work. The instances were struc-
tured and interpreted in accordance to the mathematical model described in Chapter 3.
Thus, also the instance readers were implemented accordingly.

4.1.1
Population
Each population consists of a list of chromosomes as well as the average distance between
its chromosomes. Similar to Karaoglan and Altiparmak [49], the population size is set

for the chromosome, half of the population is generated with the extended Clarke and
Wright greedy heuristic [15], the other half by perturbing the solutions generated with
the greedy algorithm. If population size is uneven, number of greedy solutions is half of
population size rounded up, whereas the remaining slots are filled by perturbed solutions.

extended ’Clarke and Wright’ greedy algorithm identifies the closest depot with suﬀicient
capacity for all unassigned customers and the penalty for assigning that customer to the
second closest available depot. Depots are available if they still have enough residual

the population. In case population construction interrupts here, we suggest to add more
excess capacity to the depots.

customers within the echelon. In case n < 3, k is set to n −1.

all routes within the echelon. After removal, they are inserted by split reinsertion into
the remaining routes. Split reinsertion inserts a customer c into existing routes with a
knapsack greedy heuristic. Thus, the cheapest location for inserting c in every existing
route with residual capacity in the route and its depot is determined. Then, c is inserted
by iterating over all positions sorted by costs ascending until c’s demand is fulfilled. After
inserting c into existing routes, the material flow is updated accordingly. If the overall
residual capacity of routes or open depots is not suﬀicient, new routes are created until
c’s remaining demand is fulfilled. The newly created routes only consist of a depot i and
c. The depots are selected by availability and ascending distance to c (ˆ= cost cic). The

4.1.3
Chromosome Crossover
After the population is initialized, new chromosomes were created out of two already
existing chromosomes. For that, two chromosomes A and B were selected from the pop-
ulation. The selection process contains two binary tournaments, as suggested by Boudia
et al. [44], Prodhon and Prins [45] and Prins et al. [23] for instance. The first binary
tournament selects two random chromosomes from the whole population. The chromo-
some with the better objective becomes the first parent A. The second tournament works
similar to the first one. However, this time parent A gets excluded from the overall list
to ensure that two different parents are selected. After selecting both parents A and B,
child C is created. For creating C, a list of tuples is created for each parent and echelon.
Hence, with two echelons 1 and 2, four lists are generated.

Figure 4.3 respectively. When both chromosomes are turned into sequences, a cutoff

After selecting ’cut’, the child’s sequences are created top down starting with the
echelon serving the instance’s customers. In the first sequence, A’s sequence is copied
until ’cut’ is reached. After reaching cut the remaining customer demand is filled in by
looping over B’s sequence until all demand is satisfied. In case remaining demand of a
customer is smaller than the demand fulfilled by B, the node is added to the sequence
only with remaining demand.
After ensuring all customer demand is fulfilled, it is checked whether any depots are
over capacity. In case a depot is over capacity, customers in C’s sequence assigned to
an affected depot are moved to another depot with residual capacity. In the first two
iterations, unopened depots are skipped to avoid additional costs. Further, in the first
iteration, nodes are only reassigned if their whole demand can be fulfilled by another
depot. If there is a second iteration, nodes are also split. Hence, if there is a node
n with demand dn, which needs to be split into n1 and n2, n1 remains at n’s orginial
position within C’s sequence, but with updated demand dn1 = dn −dn2. Node n2 gets
appended at the end of the sequence with its newly assigned depot ’depot 2’ with demand
d2 = residual capacity of depot 2.
In lower level chromosomes, due to not all chromosomes always serving the same
customers, before filling in C’s sequence with B, it is checked, whether all nodes with
demand are served. In case any node is missing, it is added to the sequence with the
residual demand. The added node gets assigned to a random depot within the echelon.
In case the depot is over capacity, it gets reassigned with the other nodes.
As customers can be reassigned to different depots, customer demands on the follow-
ing lower level echelon can be impacted. This is due to one echelon’s customers being
another echelon’s depots, except for the highest level echelon serving the final customers.
Thus, sequences are generated by echelons top down.
After creating all sequences, they are turned back into the route structure described
in Chapter 4.1. The routes are created by iterating over each sequence sorted by depots.
If a new route is started, the depot from the current node in the sequence is added at
the first position followed by the node itself with its demand. All following nodes are
added until either the depot of the node changes or the capacity of a route is reached.
In both cases, the current route gets closed by again appending the depot with demand
null at the end of the route. Then, the demands are added up from the end of the route,
resulting in the material flow to each node. After closing a route, a new one is opened
with the depot of the first unassigned node. If the end of the sequence is reached and
all nodes are assigned, the route is closed as described before and the loop breaks.
The routes are then added to a vehicle within their respective echelon. If there are
not enough vehicles for the routes generated, they are appended at the end of C’s list of
routes. After the routes are added, each route is checked for node duplicates. If a route
contains a customer node twice, the total demand is added up at the cheapest existing
position of the respective customer. All other nodes are removed and the material flow
is updated accordingly.

4.1.4
Mutation
To create variance within the population and also from a child’s parents after crossover,
’multiple k-Split’ by [56] is applied to the chromosome. The exact mechanism is described
in Chapter 4.1.1.
4.1.5
Echelon Adaption
Every time after a higher level echelon is modified, ’adaptFirstEchelon’ is called on
the chromosomes. This function checks whether depot demands have changed due to
adjustments in the routing of the next highest echelon. In case changes are detected,
excessive material flow is removed and missing flow is added either to existing routes in
the cheapest position possible or new routes by SplitReinsertion (see Chapter 4.1.1). As
we fitted the metaheuristic to a two-echelon scenario, only the first echelon needs to be
adapted. Hence, the name of the function. However, this function can also be adapted
to apply all following echelons iteratively.
4.1.6
Threshold and Population Diversification
The threshold is used to decide whether a new chromosome qualifies for admission in the
population. The original threshold is set to the average distance within the chromosome.
Per iteration the threshold gets reduced by 30%. Thus, in higher iterations, chromosomes
with lower distances towards their parents are also allowed to replace an unrelated
chromosome within the population. If the threshold falls below 2, meaning that there is
an average of only two differences between chromosomes over all echelons, it gets reset.
The same is done every 15 iterations, to keep the population from converging towards a
local minimum.
When resetting, the threshold gets set to the current average distance (scur) of the
chromosome. Here, the average distance is recalculated as described in Chapter 4.1.2.
In case the population’s average distance gets too low, the population gets diversified.
==PAGE:37==
4.1 Structure
30
Therefore, all chromosomes in the worse half of the population either get perturbed
or replaced by a newly created and once perturbed greedy solution with a randomly
selected feasible combination of opened depots. The selection between perturbation or
replacement is done randomly with a chance of 50% for selecting either scenario. This
process is repeated until the average distance increases above a certain minimum distance
(smin). This value starts off as the average distance of the original population (so) and
decreases with the amount of iterations i of the MA. The current minimum distance
smin = so ∗0.9i, resulting in an exponential decrease. The distance is set to converge
towards 0 over time, as the population’s average diversity always seemed to decrease
faster the higher the amount of iterations was. Thus, diversifying it without loosing
the best solutions took increasingly longer. If scur is below smin, the worse half of the
population gets diversified by applying multiple k-Split until scur is larger or equal smin.
The better half of solutions is kept as is.

• Shift(n,0) removes n adjacent customers from one route and reinserts them into
another route at the cheapest possible position [56], [64]. The selection of routes
and nodes is random with the only criterion being that the second route and its
depot must have enough residual capacity for all n nodes and the first route must

for n = 2 with edge (i, j), this or the inverted edge (j, i) are inserted at the
cheapest location [56]. In case no second route can be found, the original solution
is returned.
• Swap(n,o) swaps n adjacent customers from one route r1 with o adjacent customers
from a second route r2 [56], [64]. We implemented the swap to also insert the
according customers or edges at the cheapest possible position. Again, for n > 1
and o > 1, both directions of the edge to be inserted were considered, selecting the
cheapest option [56]. The selection process this time consisted of two steps. First
r1 and its n customers were selected for the exchange. Then, r2 was selected from
all remaining routes ̸= r1 as well as its o customers. If any route does not have
enough capacity to incorporate the other route’s nodes after removing customers
n and o from r1 and r2 respectively, the selection process restarts. If selection
failed three times, the original solution is returned. The number of iterations was
picked to allow multiple iterations, but prevent the algorithm in being stuck too
long at one operator.

from r2 and introducing edges (i, l) and (k, j) [56], [64]. Selection of both routes
and edges again is random. However, i, j, k and l must not be their respective
route’s depots. Therefore, r1 and r2 must have at least two customers. Further,
the new end of both routes r1 and r2 is modified to end at the respective route’s

found. The functions emptyRoutes and removeNoFlow are executed on g to ensure the

for the ILS are reset. If not all iterations are reached, g gets perturbed again to provide
a new chromosome for the next iteration. Otherwise, it is checked whether g′ is an
improvement over g∗, updating g∗if appropriate and starting the next iteration, until
iterationsmax is reached.
To reduce runtime, some of the originally proposed inter- and intra-route operators
were removed. For testing the effect of each operator on a solution, the metaheuristic
was run on a system with 8GB RAM and an i5-8250U @ 1.60GHz as processor. The
selection was done with the help of an impact analysis. Four instances with 20, 50,
100 and 200 customers were used (Prodhon: coord20-5-2, coord100-5-1, Nguyen: 50-
5MN, 200-10N). For the random selection it was ensured that two instances from both,
Prodhon and Nguyen, were selected. For each instance, a population of five chromosomes
was created with the process described in subsection 4.1.1. The best, worst and middle
(third) chromosome were selected and perturbed twice to create some variance from the
original greedy solution. After perturbing 3 ’childs’ were created from all three possible
combinations of chromosomes. For each child, RVND was run 5 times only allowing
operators of one type.
For testing the inter-route operators, all intra-route operators where deactivated to
get their direct impact on the result. Each iteration used copies of the same chromosomes
to guarantee comparability.

4.1.8
Interplay of Echelons
As already mentioned, the metaheuristic handles all echelons individually when changing
a chromosome. The process for chromosome crossover is already explained in Chapter
4.1.3. Hence, first the echelon on the highest level gets changed. Then, the following
echelons are adapted as described in Chapter 4.1.5. Next, the second highest echelon
gets changed, again adapting all following echelons afterwards and so on. For example,
if there are two echelons, RVND is first executed for the second echelon. If some changes
are made within echelon two, ’adaptFirstEchelon’ is called. Then, RVND is executed
on the adapted first echelon. This top down approach is done to connect all demands
in a hierarchical order with the only known demand in the first position. Further, this
modular approach allows adding multiple echelons, providing more variety in the range
of instance this MA can be applied to.

4.2
Hyperparameter Tuning
Our MA has five parameters allowing for customisation:
• # MA iterations: Sets an upper bound to number of MA iterations. The MA is
stopped as soon the desired number of iterations is reached.
• Time limit: Sets a time limit for the MA. The time is checked after creating
the population and after each MA iteration, terminating the MA if the limit is
exceeded.
• Iterations LS: Sets the number of LS iterations.
• Iterations ILS: Sets the number of ILS iterations per LS iteration.
• popSize: Changes population size. If not specified the default value for a instance’s
population is used (see 4.1.1).
At least one of the first two parameters (’# MA iterations’ and ’time limit’) must be
set. When both are set, the MA stops if either one termination criterions is met. This
chapter focuses on determining a good standard value for both, LS and ILS iterations.

used. Therefore, one core was reserved on the system with RAM of 32GB.

5.1.2
Discussion of Results
Testing on the small instance shows that our MA with LS and ILS parameters set to
values determined during hyperparameter tuning (see Chapter 4.2.1) is able to find the
optimal solution in the same time as the exact approach. This test was meant as a
feasibility study to proof the basic functionality of the MA developed in Chapter 4.
As Table 5.1 shows, in one out of three runs the MA found the global optimum
determined by solver. The routes for both optimal solutions slightly differed from each
other, but overall costs were minimised. The other two runs returned solutions with
slightly higher costs resulting in a small optimality gap. As both non-optimal solutions
again had the same objective value with the same depot opened, we suspect the solutions
to be a local minimum. However, this local minimum was escaped during the run with
optimal objective value. Hence, our MA with population management finds the global
minimum in one out of three cases and also is able to escape local minima.

5.2.2
Discussion of Results
The comparison of the MA’s results with timeout to the results the solver was able to
achieve within the same time frame, shows that our MA also was able to provide solu-
tions with lower objective than the solver. This comparison shows that the mix of cost
minimisation and randomisation within our MA reliably improves given solutions faster
than a commercial solver. However, in case of the MA, optimality is not guaranteed.
We were able to reconfirm the observation already made by Laporte and Nobert [22]
and by Esmaeilbeigi et al. [5]. Both of them struggled solving instances with more than
==PAGE:53==
5.2 Comparison to Exact Approach
46
50 and 51 nodes respectively. For our model, Gurobi even struggled finding any initial
feasible solution for a LRP2E with split delivery instances with more than 56 nodes
within 30 minutes. Interestingly, the solver seems to already struggle with two small
instances from Set I (’25-5N’ and ’25-5Nb’). However, when only instances where an
initial solution was found by the solver are considered, the more nodes an instance has,
the higher the gap between the solver solution and the solution provided by the MA
becomes (GapS) as shown in Table 5.3 and Table 5.4. For both instance sets, the MA
solution were consistently better.
For Set I instances, the largest solution gap was achieved in an instance with 26 nodes,
namely ’25-5MNb’. The same effect is present in Set II and instances ’coord20-5-1b’ and
’coord20-5-2b’, although not as distinct as in Set I. All these instances have increased
vehicle capacity, which seems to make solving the model more complex. This might be
due to the possibility to create longer routes, as more customer demands can be fulfilled
by one vehicle. For instances with 56 nodes, this effect is still present. However, in Set
I this difference is only at 3.7pp comparing the solution gaps of ’50-5MN’ and ’50-5MN’.
In Set II, the difference increases for larger instances, except for the last two instances
presented in Table 5.4.
Overall, the solution gap seems to be dependent on the optimality gap (GapO) of
the solver output. If GapO is big, GapS also has a large value and vice versa. How-
ever, the dependency is not linear, probably due to randomisation factors within the
MA, preventing the solution from getting stuck in a local minimum too early by ap-
plying different route improvements or randomly removing customers from routes and
reinserting them into another one. Thus, the MA seems to find an improved solution
without being influenced too much by a change in vehicle capacity, which increases the
upper bound for material flow through a route. However, after reaching a certain level
of improvement, the solver seems to struggle escaping a certain area within the solution
space with GapS decreasing when GapO decreases as well. This issue might come from
the splitReinsertion operator (see Chapter 4.1.1), which is used both, in perturbation
with multiple k-Split and for adapting lower level echelons to changes in a higher level
echelon and reinserting nodes into existing routes, if a route is removed. This operator
always inserts a customer at the cheapest possible position among all existing routes,
splitting the demand between multiple routes, if necessary. Additionally, during RVND,
both operators, Swap and Switch, were implemented to always insert a new customer
or edge at the best position within the other route. Hence, this might control customer
location within a route too much. An easy way to improve randomisation could be to
adjust Swap to do a ’true’ swap of nodes or edges. Hence, in case of Swap(2, 2), directly
inserting an edge (k, l) from route r2 at the position of removed edge (i, j) in route r1.
Another approach might be to create and maintain a more diverse population. This
could be achieved for example by adding more methods or steps to the population
initialisation process described in Chapter 4.1.1. At the moment, the population only
consists of solutions generated by a greedy approach and the same solutions perturbed
once. This measurement was taken to reduce computation time, which especially was
a limitation at larger instances with n ≥106. However, perturbing an initial solution
multiple times or using ILS directly on some chromosomes within the initial population
==PAGE:54==
5.3 Comparison to ALNS with Split Delivery
47
might increase both, diversity and also solution quality (lower objective value) of the
initial population. Additionally, having a higher quality of initial solutions could boost
search speed by already starting with ’better’ solutions.
To keep the population from converging towards a local minimum within the solution
space too fast, the acceptance criterion for new chromosomes could be adjusted (see
Chapter 4.1.3 and Chapter 4.1.6). Another method could be to change the population
diversification method or let minimum population diversity smin converge towards zero
slower (see Chapter 4.1.6). Also, to measure the distance between solutions, we used
’distance for R-permutations’ by Campos et al. [67], which only compares edges, but not
allocation of these edges to routes with potentially different depots. Another method we
considered for calculating the distance between two solutions was introduced by Prins
et al. [23]. In their approach, distance is evaluated in the context of route assignment,
origin of material flow and neighbouring nodes of an edge (i, j), making it more suitable
for LRPs. However, Prins et al. [23] do not consider split delivery, whereas Campos et
al. [67] do. Therefore, a mix of both approaches might be more appropriate to calculate
solution distance for a LRP2E with split delivery.

5.3.2
Discussion of Results
Although our MA outperformed the exact approach with our mathematical model using
Gurobi as a solver, we were not able to keep up to the ALNS for a LRP2E with split
deliveries from Ettrich [8]. Similar to the observations from Chapter 5.2.2, the solution
gap increases with an increasing number of nodes (see Table 5.7 and Table 5.8).
Further, there is a major increase in the gap when reaching instances with 200 cus-
tomers. This might be due to the fact that we consider population creation as part of
the MA and therefore was included within the 30 minute timeout. During our tests,
we observed an increase in processing time per operation with an increase in instance
size. The perturbation operator for example terminates after 0.79s for a small instance
(’coord20-5-2’), whereas for a large instance it only finishes after 1302.33s (’coord200-10-
3’) when run on a local system with i5-8250U @ 1.60GHz and 8GB RAM. This marks
an increase of 164751.90% in processing time for a single operation. As described in
Chapter 4.1.1, perturbation makes use of splitReinsertion, which iteratively scans the
whole route set of an echelon for the cheapest locations to insert a customer. With an
increase in population size, there is also an increase in possible locations to insert a
customer. Therefore, finding the cheapest insertion location takes longer. This effect
can also be seen to some extend with local search operators and an increasing number
of customers within a route, as these only consider a limited set of routes.
With perturbation being a major part of population initialisation, this increase in
processing time also affected overall time of population initialisation. With large instance
sizes, the MA terminates after 30 minutes during population initialisation returning the
best solution within the population before any further improvements were made by
chromosome crossover and ILS. We were able to confirm this suspicion, as an increase
in time limit enables the MA to at least make one iteration with crossover and ILS
before terminating. The results achieved with extended time limit provided in Table
5.9 show a major improvements over the output for the same instances with a time
limit of 30 minutes. However, as the MA only terminates after the crossover operation,
which also includes the ILS, is completed, we have very long processing times. The
average processing for all ten instances with n = 211 from both Sets I and II was
25572, 79s (426.21min = 7.10h) including initialisation of the population. This marks
an increase of 1320.71% over the prior timeout of 1800.00s. Further, this run time also
exceeds the ALNS’ processing time for these instance. For all ten large instances, the
ALNS terminated on average after 4251.30s (70.86min). Hence, the MA took 21321.49s
(501.53%) longer than the ALNS.
To reduce runtime, we either suggest replacing splitReinsertion by a less time-consuming
reinsertion operation. One option could be to just insert customers in a random position
without comparing all possible insertion locations. Another option is to revisit the im-
plementation, streamlining some processes, as we did not optimise for processing time.
Therefore, leaving space for improvement within the code’s eﬀiciency.
When creating our solutions for the instance set, we initially used a population
size based on the total number of nodes within an instance based on Karaoglan and
Altiparmak [49], as described in Chapter 4.1.1. However, the larger the population gets,

not only initialisation time rises, but also memory usage. While running the MA with the
population size set to #c +#p +#s, we quickly exceeded the limit of 32GB of RAM for
instances with population size 200. For example, when running the instance ’200-10N’,
the MA got interrupted by the server due to peaking in memory usage at 39.6GB with
an average memory usage of 20.1GB. This imposed a large increase in memory usage
compared to instances with c = 100 and p = [5, 10]. For example, average memory
usage for ’coord100-5-1’ with a population of 106 solutions was 5.2GB peaking at 5.4GB.
Memory usage of instance ’100-10MN’ was similar with a population of 111, averaging
5.7GB and peaking at 5.8GB. To avoid this problem, we examined the immediate effect
of different population sizes on solution quality in the short term in Chapter 4.2.2.
Limiting population size to 56 also reduced memory usage for instances with c = 200
to average between 11 and 12 GB, with peaks up to 16GB. However, when not imposing a
time limit on the MA and allowing multiple iterations, a larger, more diverse population
might be preferable. For the use of population sizes ≥200, we suggest to revisit the way
solutions are stored, as the solution construct contains information, which can also be
received if needed or defined as a global variable. One example where a global variable
might be applicable could be the instance itself, which stays unchanged for the whole
MA. Further, constants such as vehicle capacity or flags proving solution validity can
be received from the instance or determined quickly without a need to store them with
the solution class itself. Another issue might be that within the process, every time a
solution is changed, a deepcopy is created, which might keep outdated and therefore
unused solutions stored.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
We successfully created and implemented a model for the two-echelon location routing
problem with split deliveries in this thesis. We verify that our model can solve small
instances to optimality, but also show its limits solving larger instances within a certain
given time limit. To overcome these limitations, we propose a hybrid heuristic. We
design a memetic algorithm with population management and iterated local search to
solve the LRP2E with split delivery, also analysing the effect of different parameter
combinations on solution quality. After verifying basic functionality, our MA is tested
on two common instance sets for the LRP2E. To evaluate the output, we thoroughly
compared our results to the output of our model and another metaheuristic developed
for the same problem. Further, we point out weaknesses and strengths identified in
the evaluation of the output. Then, we discuss the weaknesses and suggest potential
solutions to fix them.
Overall, we contribute to the topic of research on LRP2E with split delivery by
designing a model capable of solving this problem. Further, we show that a heuristic
approach is recommendable for finding ’good enough’ solutions for instances with 20
and more customers. For this, we conceptualised and implemented a new MA with pop-
ulation management using iterated local search as the local search heuristic applicable
to the problem described. To our knowledge, this is the first MA and only the second
metaheuristic developed to solve this specific type of problem. Our MA was also devel-
oped with a degree of modularity in mind, to enable the addition of more echelons or
multiple sources to the instance without much effort. Further, processes like population
management, population initialisation, perturbation and the local search algorithm can
easily be changed.
Our output analysis has shown, that our MA outperforms the solution determined
by the exact approach using a common commercial solver with timeout. However, the
MA in its current state and parameter setting cannot compete with the adaptive large
neighbourhood search, we compared it to. The ALNS is superior in both computation
time and solution quality. Even when increasing the MA’s time limit to the computation
time of the ALNS for large instances, allowing the MA to perform one crossover after
initialising the population, the result of the ALNS is better.
In general, computation time especially with large instances and high memory us-
age with large population sizes seems to be the main issue for the MA. We concluded
that population size does not have a big impact on solution quality in the short run.
Especially, when long computation times limit the amount of crossovers within a given
time frame. Thus, with large size instances, to get ’cheaper’ solutions fast, approaches
only maintaining a very limited to no population like the ALNS might be superior, but
==PAGE:62==
6 Conclusion
55
with a longer time frame, a MA with a suﬀiciently large population might also be able
to compete with the ALNS’ solution quality. This is where we see the modularity of
the MA as an advantage, not only allowing to adjust single parameters, but also to ex-
change different operators such as the local search or population initialisation to better
fit the algorithm to a specific problem set. For example, decreasing complexity of some
operations could reduce computation time allowing for more chromosome crossovers.
With our thesis, we are adding to the work of Ettrich [8], who opened the discussion
about the use of metaheuristics for solving the LRP2E with split deliveries. Further, we
porpose two possible directions for future research. One is to refine the MA developed
in this paper. We suggest to examine effect of different hyperparameter combinations
such as the LS and ILS parameters, number of MA iterations and population size, as
there might be an advantage of larger, more diverse populations the more MA iterations
are executed. Further, adding new operators replacing or complementing current ones
like ’Switch’ and ’Swap’ within the ILS or ’multiple k-Split’ for perturbation. We see
also a possibility to add machine learning components in the MA, for example by ini-
tialising the population with optimal solutions to similar routing problems or detecting
recurring patterns in routes among multiple high quality solutions. Another direction is
to further expand the LRP2E with split delivery by adding multiple sources on the first
echelon or possibly adding one or more echelons. Concluding this thesis, there are many
different ways this discussion can be carried on, hopefully also enabling a sustainable
transformation of complex supply chains towards circular supply chains.
