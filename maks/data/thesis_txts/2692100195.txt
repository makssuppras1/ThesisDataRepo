Abstract
The ﬂow of a Waste to Energy power plant boiler was studied. Large scale experimental
measurements were carried out at Aﬀald+ in Næstved, where particle seeding were re-
leased inside the boiler and the subsequent particle structure movements were captured by
a camera. A data processing method was then developed, where the data was subjected
to contrast stretching and contrast limited adaptive histogram equalization to optimize
the signal to noise ratio in the recorded images. A fast fourier transform algorithm was
then employed to calculate the velocity vectors at various camera positions in the boiler.
A calibration of the lengths inside the boiler was done through geometrical calculations,
where the boiler dimensions and the camera angles were used. Ultimately, it was possible
to generate velocity ﬂow ﬁelds, which were comparable to CFD models. This comparison
was not done due to unknown operating conditions during the measurements, which may
lead to a potentially wrongful comparison of the computational ﬂuid dynamics model and
the experimental measurements.

Introduction
The International Energy Agency forecasts that the global energy demand will grow by
5 to 15 %, depeding on various scenarios, over the next 10 years [1]. In 2019, the annual
global energy demand was 14,416 mega tonne oil equivalent (MTOE). 58 % of this de-
mand was satisﬁed by coal and oil [1], which are non-reusable fuels and large contributors
to the global CO2 emissions [2].
Similar to the global energy demand, the generation of municipal solid waste (MSW)
is expected to increase from 2.01 billion tonnes annually, in 2016, to 2.59 billion tonnes
annually by 2030 [3], corresponding to an increase in MSW of 28 %. Furthermore, The
World Bank estimates that at least 33 % of the MSW is not handled environmentally safe
[3].
Thermal treatment (through combustion, gasiﬁcation or pyrolysis) of MSW has been an
unavoiable part of the treatment process for many years [4]. Historically, thermal treat-
ment of MSW has mainly been done due to two main reasons: 1) to preserve landﬁll space
as thermal treatment of MSW reduces both the MSW by 70-80 % as weel as the volume
by 80-90 %, and 2) to diminish the risk of environmental pollution and sanitary problems
arising from untreated waste [5]. By employing MSW as fuel in a combustion system,
it would be possible to recover otherwise wasted energy [4] which would simultaneously
lend itself as a possibility of reducing the usage of coal and oil by being a viable alternative.
While employing MSW as fuel does provide substantial beneﬁts, as it diminishes envi-
ronmental pollution while recovering previously wasted energy, it also comes with some
challenges. One challenge is, that MSW is classiﬁed as a low-grade fuel, meaning that
MSW is rather heterogeneous fuel with a low heating value, which thereby requires the air
to be preheated [6]. Furthermore, MSW has been shown to have troubling ash behaviour
when used as a fuel [7].
A Waste to Energy (WtE) can replace fossil fuels and re-employ waste, by burning it
under controlled conditions, to create a stable production of heat and power with lower
CO2 emissions. The WtE plant was advanced in 2008 as Madsen et al. [8] proposed a new
furnace design and control system, which allowed for increased electrical eﬃciency. This
superheater (SH) design was leter patented by B&W Vølund and called Steamboost [9].
By utilizing Steamboost, the WtE plants will achieve higher electrical eﬃciency than the
1
==PAGE:10==
previous low eﬃciency WtE plants, which were limited by temperature imposed releases
from the grate combustion process of ash species that form corrosive deposits on the SH
surfaces.
At the WtE power plant, Aﬀal+ in Næstved, this SH technology, Steamboost, was em-
ployed. Later the SH in Aﬀald+ in Næstved was destroyed by the corrosive gasses present
in the WtE furnace. This was not to be expected as it was contradictory to the computa-
tional ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) calculations, which suggested that the corrosive gasses could
be kept away from the SH, while still providing it with ﬂue gas.
1.1
Waste Fuel Types and Compositions
Typically, fuels are evaluated based on values such as the lower heating value (LHV),
moisture content and ash content. Since MSW is a term which covers many diﬀerent
types of waste, the composition varies a lot depending on its cultural and geographical
setting [3]. Furthermore, the composition of MSW has great importance, as features such
as content of inorganic matter and moisture inﬂuences ash formation, caloriﬁc value of
the fuel and the temperature boundaries of the combustion [7].
Due to the variation in composition of MSW many diﬀerent values the lower LHV, mois-
ture and ash content is reported throughout the literature [10].
An overview of MSW ranks as a fuel compared to other solid fuel types has previously been
made by Kleinhans et al. [7] and can be seen in Table 1. Values of the LHV on a dry basis,
ash content on a dry basis, ash fusion temperature and major ash forming components
were reported. From Table 1 it is seen that MSW has a slightly lower LHV than the other
biomass based fuels (with exception of manures and sludges) and approximately a half as
large LHV compared to bituminous coal. As the LHV of a fuel describes the amount of
heat released by combustion of a speciﬁed quantity, the low LHV indicates that MSW is
not as eﬀective as some of the other fuels with regards to generation of heat. Furthermore,
MSW has a lower ash fusion temperature than the all the other fuel types shown in Table
1. Looking at the major ash forming components it is seen that MSW is often Cl rich,
which will result in a higher level of corrosion, as Cl is a large contributor to corrosion
[11]. Which further increases the diﬃculty of using MSW as a fuel.

1.2.1
Steamboost Technology
Bøjer et al. [15] reported that the electrical eﬃciency of waste boilers is 20-25 %. Bøjer
et al. [15] found, that it is possible to contain the most corrosive species such as Cl,
Na, K, Zn, Pb and S at the front end of the boiler. They then showed that it is possi-
ble to provide a ﬂue gas with a high energy content and a relatively low concentration
of corrosive species such that a heat ﬂux could elevate the superheater steam temperature.
Based on these ﬁndings B&W Vølund and DTU invented and patented a superheater
technology called Steamboost [9]. Steamboost aquires a ﬂue gas with high energy content
and low concentration of corrosive species at the back of the grate boiler while keeping the
corrosive species at the front of the boiler. This results in better control of the deposit
formation while reducing the corrosion of heat exchangers in the boiler. Furthermore,
Steamboost allows the superheater to reach a temperature of 480 ◦C as opposed to pre-
vious 380-420 ◦C. This results in an electrical eﬃciency increase to 30 % [15].
The combustion system at Aﬀald+ in Næstved employed Steamboost Technology in the
period of approximately 7 months, but due to high corrosive species, the overhead super-
heater broke down earlier than predicted by CFD model calculations.

1.2.2
Gas Distribution in the Grate Combustion System
Since the bed in the grate boiler is moving, and varying amounts of air are injected
throughout the grate, diﬀerent phenomena with various gas compositions will be present
in the grate. Thus, a stage wise explanation from the fuel entry to the ash exit into the
ash pit will be given [16].
During the ﬁrst part of the grate, a heating of the fuel will take place. This will result in
the moister content of the fuel disappearing through evaporation of water [17]. Once the
moisture has evaporated, the waste will commence pyrolysis and volatile compounds such
as CO, CO2, H2, Cl and S, along side inorganic elements such as Na, K, Pb and Zn, will
be released into the gas phase. Bøjer et al. [17] found that approximately 30-40 % of the
energy contained in the waste is shifted to the gas phase during this part. Furthermore,
Bøjer et al. [17] found that aprroximately 90 % of Cl, 75 % of S, 50 % of Pb and Zn,
10 % of Na and 33 % of K is released into the gas phase in the ﬁrst part of the grate.
As the volatiles has been released from the waste, char (and ash) will be the remaining
part, which will then start to burn. Once the char is burned, only ash remains in the ﬁ-
nal part of the grate. Typically the ash is cooled slightly before moving to the ash pit [17].
The length of these zones, and how much of the various species are removed in each
of them, are highly dependent on the operating conditions of the combustion system.
Speciﬁcally the temperature, the residence time of the fuel and the degree of mixing
between oxygen and fuel. It is desired to have turbulent mixing between the oxygen and
fuel as this ensures adequate mixing [6].
1.3
Ash Properties for Waste as Fuel
1.3.1
Ash Formation and Composition
Ash consists of incombustible parts of the fuel employed in the combustion process [6].
Typically, ash is divided into ﬂy ash (smaller particles around 0.11 µm) or bottom ash
(larger particles of ≥10 mm) [18]. In a moving grate combustion system, bottom ash is
collected in an ash pit at the end of the moving grate [6]. The ﬂy ash is typically either
deposited on surfaces, agglomerated into larger ash particles which then falls into the ash
pit or sent to the ﬂue gas treatment [6].
Ash formation and composition heavily depends on the chosen combustion system and
on the fuel used. As MSW is a highly heterogeneous fuel compared to coal or biomass,
8
==PAGE:17==
it can be problematic predicting the composition. Ash formation from MSW typically
has elevated chlorine levels due to its composition [2]. Furthermore, as mentioned earlier
MSW is classiﬁed as a low rank fuel. Generally low rank fuels also contain large amounts
of alkaline earth metals (Mg and Ca) and alkali metals (Na and K), which are heavy
contributors to slagging, fouling and corrosion, due to their easily release into ﬂue gasses
[18].

1.4.1
Deposition Mechanisms
Inertial impaction
Inertial impaction happens, when the particles have adequate inertia to separate from the
gas ﬂow and collide with a surface as a result of the inertial forces [19]. This phenomena
mostly happens for larger ﬂy ash particles (when the particle diameter, dp, is larger than 10
µm) as they achieve a large enough inertia which makes them unable to follow the gas ﬂow
around a heat exchange surface. Inertial impaction is considered the largest contributor
to the ash deposition build-up process [7, 18]. The inertial impaction of ash particles
can be expressed as a function of the Stokes number. The Stokes number describes the
ratio of a particles momentum response time to the ﬂow-ﬁeld time scale [20]. The Stokes

Condensation
Another phenomena which results in deposition of ash particles is condensation [18].
Condensation happens when inorganic vapours in the ﬂue gas are cooled below its vapor
dew point [7]. Condensation may result in tubes getting a thin sticky ﬁlm, which results in
increased pick-up of other particles [18]. The amount of inorganic vapours highly depend
on the fuel used and operating conditions of the combustion system. This phenomena is
typically more common in lower rank fuels, as they have larger alkaline contents, whereas
for high rank coals this mechanism only has minor eﬀects for the ash deposition rate [18].
In the super heater region, where ﬂue gas temperatures are between 800-1200 ◦C, the
inorganic vapours are primarily: alkali chlorides (KCl, NaCl), alkali sulfates (K2SO4,
Na2SO4), alkali carbonates (K2CO3, Na2CO3), alkali hydroxides (KOH, NaOH) and heavy
metals (Zn, Pb, ZnCl2) [7].

Chemical reaction
Heterogenous chemical reactions between the gas species and deposits on surfaces (and in
rare cases the surface itself) can lead to further surface deposits [21]. The most common
reaction types, resulting in increased deposition are: oxidation, reduction, sulphation,
alkali absorption, carbonation and eutectics [7, 23].
Typically sulfation for an alkali metal atom, M, happens in accordance with eq. (4) and
(5).

are known to attack metal surfaces and lead to corrosion [24].
Turbulent eddy impact
Turbulent eddy impaction are deposits formed by small turbulent eddies near the bound-
ary layer of the surface. This mechanism typically occurs when the particles deposited
are to light or too small to inertially impact the surface [18]. Eddy impaction is typically
the major contributor when inertial impaction and thermophoresis are not present, which
results in eddy impaction often being the main mechanism for depositions formed on the
backside of the heat transfer tubes [18].
1.4.2
Deposit Composition and Consequences
As the deposition mechanisms result in deposits various places on a heating tube, the
chemical composition of the deposits will vary. The inner layers are typically formed by
condensation and thermophoresis and are typically rich in Na, K, Cl, Zn and Pb. Once
a sticky layer is formed by the condensation, a thicker layer composed by heavier atoms
such as Al, Si, Fe and Ca is often formed by inertial impaction [7].
The deposition on the heating tube results in decreased heat transfer coeﬃcients and
may decrease heat transfer to the steam circuit. These deposit layers greatly reduce the
eﬃciency of the boiler. Van Beek et al. [25] found that deposits on the heating tubes
resulted in a 25% decreased heat transfer coeﬃcient [25].
Typically, deposits are classiﬁed as either slagging or fouling. Slagging refers to the deposit
taking place in the high temperature sections of the boiler where the radiant heat transfer
is dominant [26]. Due to the high temperatures, slagging mainly consists of molten ash
13
==PAGE:22==
particles such as silicates and alkaline species. Foiling takes place in the convective heat
transfer zones of the boiler [26] and mainly consists of an alkali-rich inner layer with a
porous shell made from ﬂy ash particles.
1.4.3
Corrosion
Other than causing deposits which reduces the heat transfer coeﬃcients and thereby re-
ducing overall eﬃciency of the combustion system, a large issue is corrosion. Corrosion is
mainly caused by chlorine species such as Cl2 and HCl [11, 24]. As mentioned earlier in
section 1.2.2, these species arise during pyrolysis, when chlorine volatiles are released from
the fuel [11]. When applying MSW as a fuel other chloride species such as KCl, ZnCl2
and PbCl2 will also arise in the boiler. All of these species are of a corrosive character,
which also contribute to corrosion [7].
1.4.4
Deposit Removal
While deposits in the combustion system yield many issues, it is possible to remove the
deposits through various methods and mechanisms. Such methods include mechanically
and thermally induced tensions, which eﬀectively remove deposits from the boiler. De-
posits can also be unwillingly removed through eﬀects such as erosion, gravity shedding
and melting. Each of the methods/mechanisms are brieﬂy described below. [27]
Erosion is a deposit removal mechanism in which unmolten particles collide with non-
sticky areas on deposit surfaces and cause a deformation/cutting action [27].
Gravity shedding is where the deposit is removed by gravitational forces. Gravity shed-
ding occurs when the gravitational forces exceed the bonding forces in the deposit. This
will cause the deposit to fracture and ultimately break oﬀthe surface where it is deposited.
When a break oﬀoccurs, it damage to tubes if the detached deposit is of large enough
dimension. It should be noted that this is mostly relevant for lightly sintered deposits [27].
Melting happens when molten slag is formed on a surface with deposits and causes the
deposits to exceed their melting temperature [27].
Thermally-induced tensions consists of rapidly changing temperature (typically through
cooling). This leads to deposits being broken by the thermal stress. Thermally-induced
14
==PAGE:23==
tensions works well with removing sintered deposits, as they have higher thermal con-
ductivities, a lower porosity and a signiﬁcantly diﬀerent thermal expansion coeﬃcient
compared to that of steel.
Thermally-induced tensions can be achieved through soot
blowing, load changes or through combustion ﬂuctuations [27].
Mechanically-induced tensions, typically consisting of soot blowing or high pressure
washing the heat exchanger surfaces to remove deposits. The eﬃciency of mechanically-
induced tensions are highly dependent on the chemical composition, age and position in
the boiler [27].

This LAP approach where the linking costs are minimized have shown to be ideal when

Kalman ﬁlter based approach
This approach builds on the Kalman ﬁlter, which was ﬁrst presented by Kalman [39] in
year 1960. This approach uses the LAP approach to determine starting points of the par-
ticles. A Kalman ﬁlter is then used on each particle track to predict the most probable
position of the particle. The predicted particles are then linked to actual points using an
LAP framework, where the sqaured distance is used as costs [40].
In some cases, no points will be close to the predicted point at which no points will be
linked to this point. This is not an issue, as the Kalman ﬁlter still allows for predictions
if encountered with missing observations. If a user chosen number of points are missing,
the track will be terminated [40]. Concurrently, if new points arise which is not assigned
to a track, a new track will be created.
A pseudo algorithm for the procedure of the Kalman ﬁlter based algorithm can be found
in the appendix 8.1. In brief, the algorithm consists of an initialization, track elongation,
a track termination and a track initiation phase.
Nearest neighbor approach
The nearest neighbor approach is the simplest algorithmic approaches employed by Track-
Mate [36].
The nearest neighbor approach works by choosing a distance measure which determines
the distance of particles in t and t + 1 [41] (most commonly the euclidean distance is cho-
sen). The chosen distance measure is then calculated for all particles in t and t + 1. The
points with lowest scores on the distance measure is then chosen as the particle position
in period t + 1 [41].

PIVlab also allows for the addition of various ﬁlters, which enhances features of the image
as a part of the pre-processing process before calculation of the correlation matrix. These
pre-processing methods consisted of contrast limited enhanced histogram equalization
(CLAHE), intensity highpass, intensity capping, weiner denoise and contrast stretching.
A very brief description of each of these pre-processing methods will be given below. A
more thorough description of these pre-processing can be found in the references.
The CLAHE was an eﬀective contrast enhancement method ﬁrst developed to increase
the readability of medical images [37, 44]. The CLAHE operates in small image regions,
where the largest occuring frequencies in the image histogram are distributed out over the
full range of the data. This results in both low exposure- and high exposure areas being
optimized independently to achieve the optimal readability of the image [45]. Shavit et
al. [46] have shown that CLAHE signiﬁcantly improves the ability of cross-correlation
PIV methods to detect valid vectors by 4.7 ± 3.2 % [37, 46].
Intensity highpass is an image processing method used when the background information
of the image is of less relevance.
The image is then applied a high-pass ﬁlter, which
conserves high frequency information, while suppressing the low frequency information in
the image background [37, 45].
30
==PAGE:39==
The intensity capping method utilizes an upper limit to the greyscale intensity of the im-
age shown and all pixels above the upper limit will be replaced by the upper limit value.
This method reduced the amount of bright particles and spots in the image. Intensity
capping is typically used, as bright particles or spots will contribute larger to the corre-
lation signal than non-bright spots [37, 46]. This method was also investigated by Shavit
et al. [46] and they found intensity capping improves the ability of cross-correlation PIV
methods to detect valid vectors by 5.2 ± 2.5 % [37, 46].
The Wiener ﬁlter is based on a statistical approach, where the mean squared error be-
tween a stationary random process and the desired process is minimized [47–49]. Thus,
the Wiener ﬁlter works by accepting low noise frequency components and rejecting high
noise frequency components [45]. In practice, the Wiener ﬁlter ”blurs” the image to re-
duce the noises present in the image.
Lastly, the contrast stretching method is brieﬂy explained. This method is also referred
to as normalization [45]. When generating an image, the respective pixel intensities can
be binned in a histogram on the gray-scale. Considering this gray-scale histogram with
an upper value of 1 and lower value of 0 (V ∈[0, 1]), then contrast stretching allows for
stretching of a speciﬁed region of the histogram, such that it occupies the entire gray-scale
from 0 to 1 [45, 50].
The accuracy of PIVlab has recently been evaluated by Thielcke and Sonntag [51]. They
evaluate the accuracy of velocity vector ﬁelds generated by PIVlab. This is done by inves-

noise were added to the data. These types of noise consisted of a checker board, diﬀerence
clouds, gaussian noise and a combination of them all. The various noise types can be seen

error (RMSE) for each of the types. When no noise was present, PIVlab performed best
and achieved a bias error of 0.014% and a RMSE of 0.01%. Also, the highest errors were
achieved for the diﬀerence cloud structures, namely a bias error of 5.6% and a RMSE
of 3.6 [51].

Aim/Project Description
The objective of this project is to gather knowledge about the ﬂow in the boiler of a Waste
to Energy plant. More speciﬁcally knowledge about the ﬂow will be gathered through
measurements carried out at Aﬀald+ power plant in Næstved. Here particles will be
injected and tracked, to allow for ﬂow visualisation of the boiler. The collected data will
then serve to validate CFD models of ash deposits and formation rates. Ultimately the
goal of the project is to generate a method from which large scale operational experimen-
tal measurements can be processed to allow for comparison and validation of CFD models.
The project will consist of 3 main parts.
1. Getting familiar with the theory of: ash formation and deposition in a WtE system
and ﬂow visualization techniques.
2. Carrying out ﬂow visualization experiments on a large scale operational moving grate
waste boiler at Aﬀald+ in Næstved.
3. Process the experimental data to obtain velocity ﬂow ﬁelds of the boiler to validate
computational ﬂuid dynamics models of the ash deposits and formation rates in the boiler.

3
Methods and Materials
In this project, it is attempted to develop a method for measuring and visualising the
naturally occurring ﬂow in large scale boilers. The ﬂow in large scale boilers are measured
by recording the movement of particle structures inside the boiler. The particle structures
are introduced in the boiler through a seeding probe, which disperses the powder inside
the boiler. The subsequent movements are then captured by a camera, which allows for
various ﬂow visualisation techniques. The ﬂame light was suppressed by an optical band
width ﬁlter and the boiler was then illuminated by LEDs, as the light from the LEDs
could pass through the bandwidth ﬁlter. This allowed for the movement of the particle
structures to be seen without interference from the ﬂame light (usually particle distortion
and image blurring).

The experimental setup at Aﬀald+, Næstved, Denmark, employs multiple probes. The

various positions of the probes have been depicted on the schematic of the boiler. The

3.3
Data Processing
While multiple diﬀerent methods of processing the data have been tried, this section
only presents the ﬁnal data processing method. The ﬁnal data processing method can be
divided into three sub-steps: pre-processing, PIV analysis and post-processing. Each of
these steps will be described below.
41
==PAGE:50==
3.3.1
Pre-processing
In the pre-processing part, the data is subjected to constrast stretching and contrast
limited adaptive histogram equalization (CLAHE). Contrast stretching reduced the back-
ground noise in the images and the CLAHE increased the local contrasts in images allow-
ing for easier distinction between particles in the cloud structures. Both of these ﬁlters
resulted in an increased performance when the FFT method was employed to generate
the vector ﬁelds.
Masks were also added to the measurements to increase computational eﬃciencies and
reduce the noise in the images. The masks are areas in which no calculations were made.
The masks were added in the darkened regions, where no structures were detectable, and
on slags that blocked the view of the boiler.
3.3.2
PIV Analysis
The image sequences were pair-wise analysed, such that the cross-correlation was ﬁrst
calculated between images A and B followed by new calculations between images C and
D. This analysis structure was chosen, due to the pulsation pattern of the camera and
LEDs.
For the PIV analysis, a FFT algorithm was employed with 3 passes. The passes were of
size 64x64, 32x32 and 24x24 pixel interrogation areas with an overlap of 50 % in each of
the passes. Additionally, a Gauss 2x3-point sub-pixel estimation method was employed.
3.3.3
Post-processing
After the PIV analysis, the temporal mean of the velocity vectors in each image sequence
was calculated for all images in a given camera position. Afterwards, the vectors were
subjected to a standard deviation ﬁlter and a local median ﬁlter, to further reduce the
noise. The standard deviation ﬁlter removed vectors where values larger/smaller than ±8
standard deviations were removed. The applied local median ﬁlter calculates the median
from the closest 9 points and remove vectors which were 3 times larger than the local
median.
Once the ﬁltering of the meaned velocity vectors were completed, the units were converted
from pixels/frame to m/s, through geometric calculations in the boiler. Finally, the mean
vectors was furhter aggregated in the spacial dimensen, such that a vectors every 100

pixels were present. This was done through a mean ﬁltering and the unﬁltered images
can be seen in the appendix section 8.6.

To determine the accuracy of the data collection and processing method it was decided to
conduct a small scale veriﬁcation experiment to verify the eﬃciency of the method. For
this purpose the ﬂoating tension leg platform wind turbine created by Hansen et al. [52]
is planned to be used. A detailed explanation of this setup can be found in the paper by
Hansen et al. [52] and the thesis by Hansen and Laugsen [53]. In this thesis only a brief
introduction to the wind generator will be presented. A sketch of the wind generator and
its various speciﬁcations and components can be seen in Figure 28.
Figure 28: Sketch of the wind generator in exploded view.
The various constituents of the
wind generator can be seen and the speciﬁcations of the fan, screens and contraction is shown.
Reprinted from [52].
The wind generator utilizes a conventional fan with 3 blades and has a diameter of 0.60
m. Subsequently, the diﬀuser increases the cross sectional area to a factor of 2.5 and
changes the cross sectional shape from circular to rectangular. The diﬀuser includes two
splitter plates, which ensures that the ﬂow at the inclined walls remain unseparated. The
diﬀuser is then followed by a corner section, which turns the air ﬂow from the vertical
to the horizontal plane through a number of guide vanes. This section also increases the
43
==PAGE:52==
ﬂow speed by a factor of 2. The ﬂow is then passed through 2 screens which ﬁlters out
large scale turbulence. Finally, the ﬂow is reduced by a factor of 2.3 in the contraction
area.
The ﬂow would then be measured with both the explained experimental setup employed
at the WtE plant in Næstved and through hot-wire anemometry. Hot-wire anemometry
works by heating a thin electrical wire, which is then cooled by the ﬂow it is exposed to.
The cooling of the wire is then a direct function of the ﬂow velocity and as the temper-
ature and electrical power is easily measurable, data with a high accuracy is generated
[54]. The hot-wire anemometry method allows for inexpensive collection of high resolution
data in both spatial and temporal dimensions, with only few drawbacks such as it being
an intrusive technique with fragile wires that are sensitive to temperature variations [54].

4.3.1
Geometrical Calculations of the Boiler
When converting the units of the generated velocity ﬁelds from pixels to meters, the
geometry of the boiler should be taken into consideration. As the camera is not placed
orthogonally on the xy-plane (which is the plane illuminated by the LEDs), the conversion
factor from pixels to meters will vary throughout the image. Thus, as the camera was
placed such that neither the horizontal and vertical plane was orthogonal to the illumi-
nated plane, both of these dimensions should be subjected to geometric corrections.
As calculating the conversion factors in both the horizontal and vertical are one dimen-
sional problem, only a single one dimensional problem will be solved here. Note that
the solution is implemented in both the horizontal and vertical case, when done in the
Python-script which calculates the conversion factors (and can be seen in the appendix
section 8.3).
First, to solve the one dimensional problem it should be noted that it is assumed that the
light from the LEDs are only present in the xy-plane intersecting the middle of the boiler
orthogonally on the z-axis. It is also assumed that the camera is placed on the boiler wall
during measurements. Thus, from the dimensions displayed in Figure 20(a) it is known
that the shortest distance from the camera to the illuminated plane is 2 m.
Furthermore, from the camera speciﬁcations, it is known that the pixel sizes of the sensor
in the camera are 3.45 µm x 3.45 µm and that the focal point, f, of the camera lens
was 8.5 mm. With these assumptions and the speciﬁcations it is possible to calculate the
magnifying ratio, M, between the camera and the illuminated plane through eq. (13),
where the distance, S1, is equal to the focal point, f, of the camera and S2 is the distance
to the illuminated plane.

5
Discussion
5.1
Comparison of Experimental Measurements and CFD Model
While this project has generated velocity ﬁelds from the experimental data, which would
be comparable to the CFD model, the comparison should be done with the utmost amount
of caution. As the measurements were conducted without knowledge of the operational
conditions of the boiler, it may have resulted in signiﬁcantly diﬀerent operating condi-
tions during measurements than the boundary conditions used for the CFD model. If the
operating conditions and the boundary conditions were far apart, it may result in false
acceptance untrue insights gained from the CFD model and experimental measurements.
Furthermore, the measurements presented in this thesis were conducted without any di-
alog with the control room of the boiler. This may have resulted in varying operating
conditions even during the measurements, making the measurements increasingly diﬃcult
to compare with the CFD model.
In case the operating conditions, such as nozzle air velocity and operating temperature
62
==PAGE:71==
are diﬀerent, the ﬂow of the boiler would be very diﬀerent, making it diﬃcult to compare
the experimental measurements with the CFD model. Depspite of similar operating- and
boundary conditions of the boiler and those used in the CFD model, a larger variation
in the experimental measurements are still expected compared to the CFD model. These
variations would be attributed to the heterogenic nature of using MSW as a fuel as pre-
viously reported by Kleinhans et al. [7], which will result in varying conditions (although
less varied due to similar operating conditions) during the comparison.
Similarly to the CFD model, comparison to the small scale experiments investigated in
the literature review is diﬃcult due to missing operating conditions of the experimental
measurements. It should still be noted that it is possible to see a much more turbulent
structure in the experimental measurements compared to the small scale experiments in-
vestigated in the literature review. This could either be due to a less ideal mixing of the
gasses in the large scale compared to the small scale or due to a large amount of noise in
the measurements.
It is therefore of great importance to know the exact operating conditions and keep them
as constant as possible during future measurements. This could be done by opening up
i dialogue with the control room at Aﬀald+ in Næstved to discuss if their agenda as a
proﬁt maximizing company allowed for keeping the operating conditions as constant as
possible for a select time slot in which measurements could be conducted.
5.2
Assumptions Required to Generate Velocity Flow Fields
While the experimental measurements on a large scale boiler was conducted and it was
possible to generate velocity vector ﬁelds for the illuminated areas, some assumptions
were made to arrive at this point.
To convert the units in the images from pixels per second to meters per second, a few
simplifying assumptions were made. Firstly, it was assumed, that the light from the LEDs
illuminated the xy-plane in the center of the boiler. This assumption implies, that all
particle structures seen in the presented images are on this plane at the center. While
this assumption was needed to determine the conversion factor from pixel to meters in
each pixel throughout the image, the reality is that the light was present in a broader
range in the center of the boiler. This allowed for the camera to capture particle struc-
tures, which were not directly in the center plane, but in a proximity of this plane. This
assumption will thereby induce small errors when determining the velocity magnitude of
63
==PAGE:72==
the particle structures, which were outside of the center xy-plane but assumed to be in it.
Furthermore, the error will be biased such that particle structures closer to the camera
will receive a larger velocity than the actual velocity in the boiler in these areas. Oppos-
ingly, the particle structures behind the center xy-plane will receive a lower velocity than
the actual velocity of the particles.
Secondly, as the insertion length of the camera probe was not measured, it was assumed
that the camera probe was in alignment with the boiler wall when inserted. As this was
not the case, it should be expected that the diﬀerence between the true insertion length
and boiler wall would also aﬀect the velocity magnitude and result in a small error. As
it was attempted to insert the camera probe, such that it was placed close to the boiler
wall, this error is expected to be of small magnitude.
Finally, when estimating the angle in the horizontal plane for the camera probes, this was
done with a large uncertainty, as no direct measuring equipment was used to measure
this angle. For future measurements, this angle should deﬁnitely be estimated with great
precision, as it determines the ”conversion factor” from pixels to meters in the horizontal
angle of the image and may have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the result if not measured
correctly.
5.3
Evaluation of the Data Processing
As it was possible to generate velocity ﬁelds of the boiler, the aim of this thesis, with
regards to the data processing of the measurements, was achieved. With that being said,
this thesis only touches the surface of the topic and there are still several issues to be
addressed and components that should be further optimized. In the pre-processing part
of the data treatment, many ﬁlters were tried to reduce the noise. While it was possible to
reduce the noise of the PIV analysis signiﬁcantly using CLAHE and contrast stretching,
some noise were still present in the ﬁnal processed data.
Potentially, further hyperparameter tuning of the contrast stretch and CLAHE could be
commenced to further reduce the noise. Furthermore, as the true velocity of the boiler
was unknown it was made very diﬃcult to evaluate the choice of hyperparameters used
in these ﬁlters.
64
==PAGE:73==
5.4
Evaluation of Experimental Method
Of course, the experimental method presented in this project comes with both beneﬁts
and limitations. Looking at the beneﬁts, it was possible to generate mean velocity ﬁelds,
which would be directly comparable to the CFD model if the operating conditions were
known. Furthermore, it was possible to achieve the measurements with the illumination
done by LEDs instead of a laser, which is an inexpensive solution.
As addressed in the results section, when looking at all of the mean velocity ﬁelds (dis-
played in Figures 36, 38 and 41) a common characteristic of changes in velocity magnitudes
around the edge of the illuminated light beams and the area in between them, are present.
As this is a common characteristic throughout all velocity ﬁelds generated, it could indi-
cate that the FFT algorithm is struggling with accurate predictions around the edge of
the illuminated area, where the light intensity changes drastically in the measurements.
This was to be expected, as the correlation between two points with signiﬁcantly diﬀer-
ing intensities are not expected to be as great as two point with very similar intensities
[33, 37].
While other measures could be taken to overcome this issues a simple solution could be
to introduce additional LEDs. With more LEDs introduced into the boiler, the images
would contain less areas where a large shift in intensity are present. This allows for less
wrongful cross correlations calculated.
5.5
Project Commissioning
As mentioned earlier in this thesis, due to various project commissioning, it was not
possible to conduct a small scale veriﬁcation experiment within the time frame of this
thesis. This was quite unfortunate, as the small scale veriﬁcation experiment would be
able to provide an estimate of the errors obtained during the data processing method
when determining the velocity vectors from the seeding cloud particle structures.
As
it was not possible to conduct this experiment, the results of Thielicke and Sonntag [51]
presents the best estimation of a potential error for this data processing method. Thielicke
and Sonntag [51] suggest a bias error of 5.6% when PIVlab accuracy was evaluated with
a diﬀerence cloud structures background. Suggesting that an error of similar or slightly
larger magnitude was present during the measurements conducted at Aﬀald+ in Næstved.

6
Conclusion
In this thesis ﬂow visualizing measurements on a WtE plant was conducted. The mea-
surements were generated by capturing images of particle seedings being carried around
in the boiler of the WtE plant. The recorded data was then subjected to multiple pre
processing ﬁlters to reduce the noise while maintaining the signal. It was found that
CLAHE and contrast stretching yielded optimal data pre-processing, before a FFT algo-
rithm was employed to determine the velocity vectors at various camera positions in the
boiler. Through geometrical considerations of the boiler the velocity vectors were then
calibrated, which converted the units from pixel/frame to m/s. Once the calibration was
done, it would in theory be possible to compare the large scale experimental measure-
ments with CFD models. However, it was not possible to directly compare the velocity
ﬁelds generated from the measurements, due to a lack of known operating conditions.
This thesis outlines some initial steps for large scale ﬂow visualisation measurements. The
thesis presents a slightly optimized seeding cloud setup for better ﬂow structure detec-
tion. Where, it was found that a slightly longer pulsed release of the seeding powder was
preferred over a quick release followed by a constant carrier ﬂow.
Furthermore, an experimental method followed by a data-processing method is presented,
which allows for visualization of the ﬂow structures on large scale, to a point where it is
directly comparable to CFD models if the operating conditions of the boiler is known.

Future Perspectives
Of future work, it would be crucial to evaluate the accuracy of the presented ﬂow visualiza-
tion method. As mentioned previously in this thesis, a small scale validation experiment,
which would be able to evaluate the accuracy of the data processing, was scheduled to
take place as a part of this thesis, but due to project commissioning it was rescheduled
to be done after the hand-in of the thesis. When the small scale validation experiment
has been conducted, it would not only be possible to evaluate the data processing part,
but also be possible to further optimize it. There are still many hyper parameters in the
pre-processing methods and the FFT algorithm, that could be ﬁne tuned, when an error
to evaluate the choices were present. Here the small scale validation experiment would be
able to determine the degree of bias introduced from the data processing and determine
an error measure.
Furthermore, as a good ﬂow visualization method has been presented it could also be in-
teresting to look for other methods and software which could generate velocity ﬂow ﬁelds
and evaluate these methods against the presented ﬂow visualization method.
Further improvements could also be made to the experimental measurement setup. More
LEDs, which would allow for an introduction of more light into the boiler would increase
the signal in each of the measurements, by reducing the darkened areas present and al-
lowing for more data generation per measurement.
Finally, a measurement campaign where the operating conditions of the boiler are known
would also be very relevant, as this would allow for a direct comparison of CFD models
and the experimental measurements, which would hopefully result in better tuning of
CFD models in the future.

8.1
Kalman ﬁlter based approach algorithm
The kalman ﬁlter based approach written in pseudo algorithmic language can be seen
below. This algorithm is reprinted from the ImageJ website [40].
• Initialization:
1. Link all the detections of frame 0 to the detections of frame 1, just based on
the square distance costs (for instance).
2. From each of the m links newly created, compute a velocity. This velocity is
enough to initialize m Kalman ﬁlters.
3. Initialize m tracks with the found detections and links, and store the associated
Kalman ﬁlters.
• Track elongation:
1. For each Kalman ﬁlter, run the prediction step. This will generate m predicted
positions.
2. Link the m predicted positions to the n detections in frame 2, based on square
distance.
3. Target detection that have been linked to a predicted position are added to
the corresponding track.
4. The accepted target detection is used to run the update step of the Kalman
ﬁlter.
5. Loop to next frame.
• Track termination:
1. Some of the m predicted position might not ﬁnd an actual detection to link to.
In that case, we have an occlusion. The algorithm must decide whether it has
to terminate the track or to bridge over a gap.
2. If the number of successive occlusions for a Kalman ﬁlter is below a certain
limit (typically 2 to 10), the track is not terminated, and the ﬁlter goes back to
the track elongation step. Hopefully, from the new prediction a target particle
will be found, and the detection in frame t will be linked to a detection in
frame t + 2 (or t + 3 etc).
==PAGE:82==
3. Otherwise, the track is terminated and the Kalman ﬁlter object is dropped.
• Track initiation:
1. Conversely, some detections in frame t + 1 might not be linked to a predicted
position. In this case, these orphan detections are stored to initiate a new
track. But for this, other orphans detections are needed in frame t + 2.
2. This step is identical to the initiation step, but for subsequent frames.
It
requires to store orphan detections in current and previous frames.
3. In frame t + 2, priority must be given to detections linked to the predicted
positions by the Kalman ﬁlters over orphan detections of frame t + 1. So when
you deal with frame t + 2, you perform ﬁrst the track elongation step, get a
list of orphan detections in frame t + 2, and then combine it to the orphan
detections in frame t + 1 to initiate new Kalman ﬁlters.
