Abstract
Many European day-ahead electricity markets (e.g.
Nord Pool) have introduced
power products, called block orders, which refer to tradeable, multi-hour power deliv-
ery agreements. These buy/sell block orders enable market participants to integrate
individual operational constraints into their offers.
The conducted project focuses on the formulation of a power producer’s day-
ahead offering problem, while block orders except of traditional single hourly orders
are possible to be traded. A price-taker power producer is assumed, i.e., the producer
is unaware of his own effect to the market price. The day-ahead price is considered
uncertain and taken into account through a set of discrete scenarios. Therefore, the
problem is formulated as a two-stage stochastic model where producer’s offers are in
the first stage and scheduling for different day-ahead price scenarios is in the second.
The inclusion of block orders and operational constraints results in a mixed-integer
linear problem formulation similar to the initial (single hourly orders).
Furthermore, the developed problem is implemented in an illustrative case for each
of the considered order types and for a comprehensive model accounting for all orders.
By utilizing the results, the distinctive properties of block orders are emphasized and
the initial hypothesis on the potential added value is validated. More precisely, block
orders provide larger profits than single hourly orders, when operationally restricted
power producers (possibly consumers) participate in day-ahead market under price
uncertainty. Additionally, an analysis on the key parameters that affect the bias of
a decision towards block or single hourly orders is conducted. In this analysis, it is
shown that offering block orders creates value for power producers according to the
tightness of their operational constraints. Specifically, block orders have an extensive
advantage over single hourly orders for low ramp and high minimum up-time values.
In principle, the product of this thesis is a formulation of an optimal offering problem
that includes block orders and a description of the conditions that create value for
block rather than single hourly orders.
Keywords: Optimal offering, Day-ahead market, Block orders, Stochastic program-
ming, Price uncertainty, Mixed-integer linear problem, Price-taker, Power producer.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Aim and background
The day-ahead electricity markets, where most of the electric energy is traded nowa-
days, are pool-based markets. As electricity generation is inherently subject to op-
erational constraints (physical limitations), market operators need to include those
restrictions when solving an optimization problem to provide a market clearing which
maximizes social welfare. This requirement is currently addressed by two main differ-
ent methods. On one hand, in USA, power producers share their technical constraints
with the market operator, who solves a unit commitment (UC) problem. Therefore,
the set of all operational constraints is accounted for in the market clearing procedure.
Additionally, non energy product markets (capacity, ramping etc.) are also operating,
ensuring competition in providing all desired reserves ([1],[2]). On the other hand,
market clearing in Europe is in most cases conducted under the economic dispatch
concept. According to this concept, generators’ technical properties are considered
confidential and power producers1 only bid a set of desired offer prices and quantities.
Block orders2 are power products introduced into European markets to allow opera-
tional constraints to be integrated into the problem solving. These orders allow power
producers to bid a block of energy to be delivered throughout a defined time duration,
while the offer can either be fully accepted or not accepted at all (fill-or-kill). Such
blocks might include constant or varying power for each corresponding time step, as
well as other properties (curtailability, conditional linkage etc.) and market clear-
ing algorithms include them as explained in [3], [4] and [5]. Thus, responsibility for
operational reliability is transferred to the producers who need to incorporate their
technical properties in the aforementioned orders. By being allowed to sell block or-
ders, power producers can benefit from integrating internal constraints into the offer
and also inherently hedge the price volatility risk.
The power producer strategy proposed by this thesis, is a profit maximization
problem, faced by a generating agent participating in a day-ahead (DA) market fol-
lowing the European standards. More precisely, the market rules accounted for in
the present project are those of Nord Pool, available in [6] and [7]. Nord Pool is Eu-
rope’s leading power market and offers trading, clearing, settlement and associated

1.2
Market clearing properties in modern markets
Market clearing optimization tools utilized in USA and Europe both include binaries,
as a result of the technical constraints’ and the block orders’ expression respectively.
By solving the initial mixed-integer linear problem (MILP) the optimal values of the
binary variables can be retrieved. Then, by fixing all binary variables to the optimal
value, the problem becomes linear (LP) and solving such a problem allows for the
retrieval of dual variables (prices etc.). However, the prices deduced in this way do
not necessarily cover the generation costs, as start-up, shut-down and fixed costs are
not taken into account in the objective as variable coefficients (due to fixing their
respective variables). Thus, the settled price may be lower than the offer prices of
dispatched offers. Offers that result in negative producer’s welfare, but are accepted
due to the described way of finding prices, are called paradoxically accepted orders
(PAOs). In principle, remuneration of all participants in the day-ahead market is a
basic rule, by means of ensured non-negative profits for all dispatched producers. In
USA, producers who face negative profits in the day-ahead market are compensated
for the losses through uplift payments as explained in [9]. In Europe, a constraint
that restricts producers’ profit to non-negative levels is added to the clearing problem,
resulting in a mixed-integer non-linear problem ([10], [4]). Though, in this case an-
other challenge exists, as maximizing social welfare while none of the producers faces
a negative welfare, creates the possibility that there may be paradoxically rejected or-
ders (PROs). The latter refers to block orders that are in the money3 but do not get
dispatched ([11]), inducing opportunity costs for the producers (bidder unhappiness).
A thorough description of the aforementioned computational challenges in the frame
of Nord Pool is given in section 2.2.

ders. In [4], the authors propose a centralized market splitting algorithm, respecting
the standard market framework of European power exchanges (PXs) and power pools.
According to the proposed method, PXs with diverse designs (including Nord Pool)
are for the first time integrated into such an extensive day-ahead market clearing

1.4
Research questions and contributions
The addition of block orders in the producer’s decision set, is initially modelled and
then studied on the qualitative effect to the strategy and the possible value added.
The objective of this thesis can be illustratively expressed by the following research
questions:
• Which is the mixed-integer linear formulation of the offering problem, that
includes both single hourly and block orders as optimization decisions, when a
price-taker producer’s day-ahead strategy is concerned?
• What is the effect on the value of the offering problem’s solution by incorporat-
ing block orders into a generator’s strategy?
• What is the inherent advantage of block orders when the bidding producer is
limited by operational constraints while facing price uncertainty?
• Which parameters affect the potential excessive yields of block orders as opposed
to single hourly orders?

2.2
Block orders
A block order consists of a specified offered power volume for a certain number of
consecutive hours within the same day and is offered at a single price. The minimum
order duration, according to Nord Pool, is three hours in Nordic and Baltic regions
and two hours in the United Kingdom. A block order’s acceptance by the market is
evaluated upon a fill-or-kill condition, i.e., offered production must either be bought
completely or not bought at all. The acceptance criterion for each block order is given
by the following conditions according to [6]:
• if the order price of a block is lower than the average day-ahead price during the
specified block period, then the block will be fully accepted (unless paradoxically
rejected),
• if the order price of a block is higher than the average day-ahead price during
the specified block period, then the block will be fully rejected,
• if the order price of a block is exactly the average day-ahead price during the
specified block period, then the block can either be fully accepted or fully re-
jected.
In light of the said rules, Fig. 2.4 graphically illustrates the acceptance evaluation
according to the presented conditions for a sample block order.

2.2.3
Curtailable block orders
Curtailable block orders are those that can be partially accepted according to a
producer-defined minimum acceptance ratio (MAR). The motivation behind intro-
ducing such an offering scheme is rooted in the aforementioned market property
concerning paradoxically rejected orders.
To mitigate the effect of the illustrated
fact on a producer participating to a day-ahead market, curtailable block orders are
utilized. A curtailable block order can be potentially dispatched in different output
levels, providing flexibility and enhancing a producer’s ability to sell the maximum
in-the-money quantity possible to be traded. Fig. 2.11 illustrates the sample market
clearing considered earlier, while now one of the block orders is curtailable. It can be
observed that if curtailability is assumed, block orders are more likely to be marginally
dispatched in a day-ahead market, and the social welfare is increased. A block with
MAR= 1 (100%) is a regular all-or-nothing block order, so it is either fully accepted
or fully rejected. A block with MAR= 0.6 may be curtailed up to 40%, while a block
with MAR= 0 is fully curtailable. MAR is equal as a percentage, between all hours
considered as it can be observed in Fig. 2.12, where the pink part is the minimum
and the blue part is the maximum possibly accepted power levels. The magnitude
of the volume weighted average price over the duration of the order is again used for
the acceptance criterion, and the cost of each power unit is equal independently of
the accepted production.

2.2.4
Linked block orders
Linked blocks are allowed to be conditionally connected between them as illustrated
in Fig.
2.13.
The green block is linked to the blue one, i.e., the former can be
bought only if the latter has been bought. In that example, the blue block is the
parent block and the green one is the child. It is implied that, the social welfare
added to the market by a child block being traded, may be enough for parent block
that creates negative social welfare to be saved, i.e., parent block traded as well if
overall social welfare increase is positive. Yet, the aforementioned condition does not
apply in reverse, since if only the parent block provides positive social welfare, then
it can be exclusively accepted to the market, while the child is rejected. A parent-
child-grandchild hierarchy defines three maximum levels of linkage as illustrated in
Fig. 2.14. Equivalently to a child block being able to be accepted (bought) only if
the corresponding parent is accepted, a grandchild block may be accepted only if the
corresponding child as well as the latter’s parent are accepted. In total, three children
and three grandchildren are allowed, accounting for maximum thirteen blocks able
to be included in a single linked block order. The acceptance ratio of a parent block
is greater than or equal to the highest acceptance ratio of its child blocks. All block
order characteristics can be aggregated into a more elaborated linked offer, as in Fig.
2.15.

2.3
Exclusive groups
An exclusive group is a cluster of offered blocks of which only one can be accepted
by the market. If several blocks in an exclusive group are possible to be accepted
according to the producer-defined prices, the block giving the highest social welfare
will be accepted. One portfolio may contain a maximum of three exclusive groups
and each exclusive group can contain a maximum 15 block orders. It is not possible
to offer linked blocks within an exclusive group. Blocks in exclusive groups can be
curtailable, but the Minimum Acceptance Ratio must be higher than 50%. Exclusive
groups are not considered in the producer’s strategy proposed by this thesis.

2.4
Flexi orders
A flexi order is a block order with a maximum duration of 23 consecutive hours. The
activation time for this order is defined by the market operator (maximizing social
welfare) rather than the offering producer (hence the name flexible). The hour in
which the flexi order is accepted, is calculated by the algorithm and determined by
the optimization criterion. Flexi orders are not considered in the producer’s strategy
proposed by this thesis.

day-ahead products, by utilizing an optimization problem. Each section (3.1-3.2.5)

Another

refers to the use of the big-M approach. By expressing conditional constraints through
this method, one may result into a possibly ill-conditioned problem. This fact is true
both for an unintended usage of an M value that is too low and leads to sub-optimality,
as well as of an M value that is too large and distorts the feasible space possibly leading
to a solver failing to reach the optimal solution. Despite the aforementioned facts, the
current thesis utilizes the big-M method to bound variables whose order of magnitude
is well known (production, revenue etc.) thus an ill-conditioned problem is avoided.

Chapter 5 provides answers to the research questions addressed by this thesis and
a discussion concerning its contribution. Section 5.1 includes an investigation into
applications, limitations and future work while section 5.2 presents the conclusion

5.1
Discussion
This section presents an overview of the thesis concerning the possible applications
as well as limitations occurring.
Furthermore, a relevant research question to be
addressed in the future is hereby described.
5.1.1
Application
The current project, provides a practical tool to entities as power producers, aggre-
gators, traders etc.
participating in electricity markets.
From the perspective of
the market operator, block orders are released in order to enhance competition and
ultimately increase social welfare. Yet, each individual producer, participating in a
day-ahead market, has the necessity to measure the value of bidding in that fash-
ion. The developed tool, accurately defines the marginal producer’s welfare achieved
by including block orders except for traditional single hourly orders in a market en-
vironment similar to Nord Pool.
The proposed algorithm can be utilized for the
offering problem of a price-taker power producer under price uncertainty and as it
was explained in section 4.2 the potential yields depend on various parameters (op-
erational restrictions, prices etc.). More precisely, from the sensitivity analysis that
was conducted in section 4.2, it can be deduced that the inclusion of block orders in
a day-ahead offering strategy mostly benefits producers that face tight operational
restrictions.
Having described the main additional profit drivers for block orders as opposed to
single hourly orders, a real-life example that emphasizes the potential uses is hereby
presented. A necessary opportunity for thermal power producers to grab, emerges
==PAGE:80==
60
5 Discussion and conclusion
from the fact that modern electricity markets are transformed under high renewable
energy integration. The latter energy injections, which may also be subsidized, are
characterized by negligible marginal cost of generation but also intermittent power
delivery.
As a result, price variation is a regular event which may be sufficiently
or poorly predicted.
In that frame, base load generators as nuclear, coal, hydro,
biomass etc. plants are facing an unfriendly environment, as far as the remuneration
of their (varying, investment) costs is concerned. Block orders provide a smart offering
method that in such cases can increase the dispatched quantities and subsequently
profits of producers. Towards the decarbonization of the electricity sector, an efficient
operation of the existing assets is not of minor importance.
5.1.2
Limitations
The main limitations that are faced by this project, are deriving from the method that
was used for modelling the potential paradoxical rejection of a block order. As the
current developed offering strategy refers to a price-taker producer, the contingencies
for block orders to be paradoxically rejected where taken into account as exogenous
independent events.
In fact, there is an independent uncertainty stage added to
the problem, for each of the block orders that are optimized in the algorithm. These
stages are characterized by the same uncertain demand scenarios considered, but each
time for a different block order, so that all the possible combinations of acceptance
levels are covered. There are two different types of limitations, as a result of the given
consideration:
• Computational: It is recalled from the modelling of curtailable block orders
(section 3.2.3) that the self-scheduled production for each of the combined
price and paradoxically curtailed demand uncertainty scenarios was given by
pg,h,s,io1,io2,...,ion. It can be observed that the number of these variables is ex-
ponentially dependent on the number of block orders used in the optimization.
More precisely, the number of the production variables (and constraints) is
|G| · |H| · |S| · |D||O| where |D| is the number of demand scenarios for block
orders (e.g. 2 if paradoxical rejection is modelled as all or nothing) and |O| is
the number of block orders optimized. It is deduced, that in a real-life problem
assuming 50 block orders (Nord Pool limit) the number of continuous variables
and constraints becomes more than 1015, i.e., intractable problem. Having pre-
sented the computational burden of modelling PROs and CBOs, depending on
the price-taker producer’s volume in the market, it may be beneficial to neglect
the fact that block orders may be paradoxically rejected into the optimization
problem.
• Conceptual: By modelling the paradoxical rejection of block orders as inde-
pendent events, it is implied that the optimization decision taken is respecting
the operational constraints for each scenario resulting from all the acceptance
level combinations of block orders. Similarly to a N-1 contingency analysis, the
==PAGE:81==
5.2 Conclusion
61
developed problem decides on an offer decision that independently fulfills all
scenarios that refer to a N-1, N-2, ..., N-N acceptance of block orders. The
described fact, implies that the optimal decision may be affected by excessively
tight restrictions. In reality, if some offered quantity gets paradoxically rejected
there are still trading opportunities in other markets (intra-day, balancing etc.)
that can compensate for the loss. Therefore, it would be beneficial to include
paradoxical rejection in an algorithm that co-optimizes more than one markets,
so that no operational feasibility will be needed for the day-ahead level exclu-
sively.
5.1.3
Future work
An interesting and mathematically challenging revision of the developed optimization
problem, is referring to the consideration of a price-maker power producer. In that
case, the producer is operating strategically by taking into account his own effect in
the market clearing when optimizing his individual offer. The strategic producer’s
problem is usually formulated as a bi-level optimization model, with the producer
maximizing his individual profit, at the upper level, and the social welfare maximiza-
tion problem of the market operator, at the lower level. Having presented the recent
literature into methods for implementing a market clearing including block orders in
section 1.3, one may observe a great modelling challenge.
5.2
Conclusion
The main purpose of this thesis was to include block orders as optimization deci-
sions in the classical offering problem of a price-taker power producer, participating
in a day-ahead market.
In order to retain a mixed-integer linear problem status,
various linearization procedures were applied for transforming the initially non-linear
expressions that resulted from modelling block orders. After evaluating two different
modelling approaches, upon their properties in generating binary variables, the one
accounting for the less computational cost was thoroughly described and implemented
in an example case. The results given by implementing the developed strategy in a
small-scale illustrative day-ahead market, where used to support the initial hypothe-
sis concerning the value of block orders for the producer. Then, a sensitivity analysis
was held, where the effect of crucial parameters on the optimization problem was
illustrated. In the following, the research questions that were established in section
1.4 are addressed according to the conclusion deduced.
• Which is the mixed-integer linear formulation of the offering problem, that
includes both single hourly and block orders as optimization decisions, when a
price-taker producer’s day-ahead strategy is concerned?
The main research question of the current thesis, is referring to the provision
of a mixed-integer linear problem that maximizes producer’s profit when block
==PAGE:82==
62
5 Discussion and conclusion
orders can be offered additionally to traditional single hourly orders. As it was
illustrated in section 3.1, the offering problem of a price-taker power producer
is typically formulated as a MILP, since binaries are used for the expression
of the operational constraints. As it is later observed in section 3.2, the initial
formulation of the current problem, including block orders, is a mixed-integer
non-linear problem formulation. By applying methods similar to [17], [18] and
[21], non-linear expressions were transformed into their linear equivalent. The
aforementioned, implies the introduction of a large amount of binary variables.
Therefore, in the same Chapter, there are also described, two different modelling
approaches for block orders that result in different numbers of binary variables
generated. Finally, the more efficient method was chosen and successfully im-
plemented in Chapter 4 for accomplishing the initial objective.
• What is the effect on the value of the offering problem’s solution by incorpo-
rating block orders into a generator’s strategy?
Even though the developed
strategy was implemented in a small-scale example, some general conclusions
can be deduced for the value that block orders’ offering provides to a power
producer. More precisely, in the example of Chapter 4, the solution given by
the optimization problem including only profile block orders (PBOs) was found
to be equal to the perfect information solution (SHOs*), i.e., the solution given
that offered quantity is decided after the realization of the market price for
each scenario considered. In total, for the considered example it stands that
SHOs<PBOs=SHOs*, where SHOs is the value of the solution when only single
hourly orders were offered. Although, the equality PBOs=SHOs* is coincidental
due to the small ratio of scenarios over block orders accounted for, the considered
example is confirming the initial hypothesis, i.e. SHOs≤(SHOs,PBOs)≤SHOs*.
Namely, since the solution considering block orders is at least in one case bet-
ter than the solution of single hourly orders, it is deduced that an offer that
combines both options is in all cases equivalent or better to the latter. Fur-
thermore, if paradoxically rejected orders are taken into account, the value of
block orders over single hourly orders is slightly decreased. However, the latter
fact is highly dependent on the probability of paradoxical rejection, which in a
case of a price-taker producer might be negligible. Finally, the introduction of
linked orders provides a larger amount of scheduling flexibility and therefore an
additional profit margin is captured.
• What is the inherent advantage of block orders when the bidder producer is
limited by operational constraints while facing price uncertainty? By recalling
the initial hypothesis, it can be summarized that block orders were released so
that power producers can integrate operational constraints into their offer. In
that way, producers can achieve larger dispatched quantities for the given prices
and therefore social welfare is improved. As it is explained in Chapter 4, the
fundamental feature that enables the aforementioned mimicking of operational
constraints is that in the case of block orders, multi-hour average rather than
hourly prices are defining the hourly production levels. Therefore, assuming
==PAGE:83==
5.2 Conclusion
63
that power production is limited by inter-temporal ramp constraints, a producer
by utilizing block orders can achieve to integrate his multi-hour ramp schedule
in the offer. In principle, a general condition necessary for a power producer to
be benefited from including block orders in his self-scheduling problem, is the co-
existence of price uncertainty and ramp and/or minimum up time operational
restrictions.
• Which parameters affect the potential excessive yields of block orders as opposed
to single hourly orders?
As it was mentioned above, the necessary parameters for offering block orders to
bring a potential advantage over single hourly orders are price variance, minimum up
time and/or ramp restrictions. As far as the ramp restrictions are concerned, it was
illustrated in 4.2 that offering block rather than single hourly orders is beneficial only
up to a certain threshold of ramp flexibility. In principle, block orders are more useful
when the considered generators’ properties of start-up rate (power output during first
hour of operation), shut-down rate (power output during last hour of operation) and
ramp-up/down rate are inflexible.
Another parameter that was investigated, and
found to be affecting the result is minimum up time of the generator. As expected, a
varying minimum up time value was affecting the profit of SHOs, while in the case of
PBOs the solution was unaffected. In reality, the solution of PBOs is not completely
independent from the considered parameter change, but is indeed by far less affected
than single hourly orders. In principle, larger minimum up time values create a larger
profit opportunity for offering block orders than lower values. Finally, by conducting
an analysis where scenario probabilities where changed, it was illustrated that in the
case of SHOs, the change in probabilities resulted in a change of offered quantities.
On the other hand, the solution concerning PBOs was not affected leading to the
conclusion that the decision considering SHOs is more sensitive to the price variance
than the one concerning PBOs.
