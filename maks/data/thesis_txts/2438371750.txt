Abstract
The fast increase in urban population has led to a growing number of urban residents
living in informal settlements. These areas, characterized by their infrastructure exclu-
sion and associated livelihood challenges, frequently lack adequate access to modern
energy services. The traditional and predominant approach to urban energy access is
grid electriﬁcation. However, this approach has failed to incorporate all the speciﬁc
barriers present in urban settlements, part of the population remains ineligible for
electricity connection or without access to an affordable and reliable source of elec-
tricity.
This study proposes community renewable energy (CRE) projects as alternatives to
the grid electriﬁcation, and investigates their potential to solve the speciﬁc challenges
present in informal settlements in Khayelitsha, South Africa.
This project is based on the analysis of available literature, surveys, and a series of
conducted interviews. It investigates the needs and speciﬁc requirements to imple-
ment electricity services in Khayelitsha, which are subsequently used to assess the ca-
pability of community-based energy projects to improve the access to these services
in the area. This evaluation is based on seven existing community-based projects and
their ability to meet the previously identiﬁed requirements. The process allows to draw
lessons from the analyzed projects and improves the knowledge on existing alterna-
tives to implement electricity services in Khayelitsha.
The results of the study suggest the suitability of community-based renewable en-
ergy projects to improve peri-urban access to electricity, mainly due to the high in-
volvement of the local population in the services, and the ﬂexibility of the model, which
includes different technologies, ownership and ﬁnancial structures and that allows to
adapt the services to the applied context. In addition a series of limitations and key
critical aspects are identiﬁed and translated into a set of recommendations for further
research and political action.

Introduction
Since 2007, more than half of the world’s population lives in urban areas. In 2017 this
number accounted for 55% of the global population (World Bank Group, 2018b), and it
is expected that it will increase to 66% by 2050 (UNDESA, 2014). The current fastest
urban growth is taking place in the global South (Africa, Latin America and South
Asia) (UNDESA, 2014), which is expected to host 90% of the "new" urban population.
(UNFPA, 2007). This ”rapid and unplanned urban growth threatens sustainable devel-
opment”(UNDESA, 2014), specially if political efforts are not made to ensure an equal
share of the beneﬁts of urban life among inhabitants, and the supply of infrastructure
fails to meet the new demand.
More than 900 million of urban residents (one third of the dwellers) live in informal
settlements and slum areas in the global South, 67% of which are just in Africa (Runsten
et al., 2018).
These areas are normally ”spatial, social and economically” excluded from the op-
portunities that arise in urban areas (UN-Habitat et al., 2015). As a result, inhabitants
in informal settlements suffer from ”multiple deprivations” such as insufﬁcient basic
services, bad housing conditions, insecurity of tenure and poverty (UN-Habitat et al.,
2015; Castán Broto et al., 2017). In addition, the placement of these settlements make
them vulnerable and highly exposed to the negative impacts of natural disasters, un-

healthy environments, etc.(UN-Habitat et al., 2015).
The access to electricity and clean fuels commonly constitutes a big challenge in
informal households. It is estimated that around 105 million people residing in ur-
ban areas in sub-Saharan Africa lack access to electricity (Castán Broto et al., 2017).
However, the need of improving access to electricity for the poor population, has been
typically considered a rural issue. Accordingly, rural areas have concentrated the focus
of researchers and policy-makers (Singh et al., 2015), and less attention has been put
to the urban and peri-urban problematic and these areas’ speciﬁc energy challenges
(Castán Broto et al., 2017). Improvements in access to modern energy services (elec-
tricity and clean fuels) are normally interrelated with other challenges such as health
and poverty, and sectors like housing or urban planning (Singh et al., 2015). However,
the coordination among these sectors is particularly challenged in peri-urban areas
which experience speciﬁc governance difﬁculties as they are located outside the formal
or ofﬁcial "city boundaries" (UN-Habitat et al., 2015). The challenge of accessing mod-
ern energy services in urban and peri-urban areas emphasizes the strong relationships
between two of the United Nations’s Sustainable Development Goals, SDG7(Affordable
and Clean Energy) and SDG11 (Sustainable Cities and Communities), and the need of
addressing them jointly by supporting sustainable energy services through inclusive
urban planning (Castán Broto et al., 2017).
1.1
Problem description
Generally, current policies address the access to modern energy services from a tech-
nocentric point of view, and overlooking users’ needs (Castán Broto et al., 2017). This
approach has led to many upgrading and energy delivery services that import external
solutions without adapting them to the speciﬁc context in which they are implemented
(UN-Habitat et al., 2015).
Accordingly, the provision of energy services in the urban context is mainly tackled
through the extension of the electricity grid. This approach relies solely in a speciﬁc
technical solution, in detriment of a deeper understanding of the rest of the energy
needs and sources.
As a result, it fails to adapt to the complexities and constraints associated to the ur-
ban life, and specially to life in low income and unserviced areas such as informal set-
tlements. (Castán Broto et al., 2017).
South Africa has adopted electriﬁcation as the way to improve energy access at a
national level. As a result, since 1994 the government has provided connections to the

1.2
Motivation
Despite the success of the electriﬁcation programme, it is estimated that one third of
the remaining unelectriﬁed households in South Africa reside in urban informal set-
tlements. In many cases, these areas remain ineligible for an electricity connection
or experience particular challenges such as the lack of an affordable or reliable energy
source . These barriers suggest that, despite the proven beneﬁts of electriﬁcation, en-
ergy access improvements based on a unique technology lack capacity to adapt to dif-
ferent contexts. The revision of alternative ways to provide energy services is therefore
necessary (Tait, 2017a).
However, the complexity associated to the deﬁnition of energy access difﬁcult the
evaluation of the different alternatives. Normally, the deﬁnition is based on ”supply
side indicators” such as the energy consumed or energy expenduiture. However, the
impact on users and real experiences of the users should be considered in order to
provide energy services that improve living conditions and development opportunities
(Tait, 2017b).
The motivation behind this project is to improve living conditions in peri-urban in-
formal settlements by facilitating the access to alternative energy services to the usual
grid electriﬁcation.
1.3
Previous studies
The challenge of improving energy access in South Africa has been addressed by mul-
tiple researchers and received signiﬁcant political attention.
Tait (2015) analyzed the electriﬁcation progrmme in South Africa and its integra-
tion in the national policy and ﬁnancial and institutional sustainability . Additionally,
Tait (2017a) studied the main challenges experienced while implementing the electri-
ﬁcation programme in informal settlements with the aim of providing practical rec-
ommendations for the municipal government.A more general exploration of the chal-
lenges to access energy services experienced by poor urban population in South Africa
was conducted by GNESD and Tait (2013), that investigated both the main demand
and supply side barriers to energy access .

Deﬁning and measuring energy access, has also been of great interest among re-
searchers. Bhatia and Angelou (2014) advocated for a multi-dimensional deﬁnition of
energy access, and proposed a global framework to measure access to these resources
"across ﬁve tiers and eight attributes of energy": capacity, duration and availability,
reliability, quality, affordability, legality, convenience, health and safety. Based on this
work, Tait (2017b) developed a series of indicators and metrics to measure energy ac-
cess adapted to the speciﬁc case of South Africa with the aim of including the "contex-
tual speciﬁcities and be useful to in-country stakeholders". The developed framework
focused on the household as a unit and represented four aspects: fuel use, affordability,
safety and reliability.
In addition, several authors have looked into existing alternatives to electriﬁcation.
Runsten et al. (2018) investigated how to provide access to energy services to house-
holds ineligible for grid electriﬁcation, and explored different grid and off-grid alter-
natives and their technical, economic, environmental, social and institutional sustain-
ability. Additionally, Tshabeni and Freere (2018) evaluated three different electriﬁca-
tion alternatives for informal settlements in South Africa, and compared grid connec-
tion with off-grid solar solutions of large and small scale. Cowan and Dieden (2008)
focused on the energy needs and priorities in low-income settlements in South Africa,
and analyzed them with the aim of identifying posible practices and solutions to im-
prove the access to modern energy services in those areas. And Cowan and Dieden
(2008) proposed to engage with the local population to facilitate the identiﬁcation of
these energy needs and the design of adequate energy services.
1.4
Project scope
The speciﬁc case of South Africa and concretely of informal settlements in the town-
ship of Khayelitsha (Cape Town) was analyzed and served as a basis to apply the mehodol-
ogy and ”test” the proposed service delivery methods.
This study applies a people-centered and bottom-up approach to the delivery of en-
ergy services, focused on the local community as a whole and based on the human
development needs and livelihood challenges in the targeted area. Energy services
were considered as enablers to improve living conditions in the settlements, and ac-
cordingly, all aspects of the service, and not only the energy source, are contemplated
in this study. Its relationship with other sectors such as housing, health, etc, are also
included in these report.
The community based renewable energy model was chosen to frame the energy de-

investigation of a wide range of technological and socio-economic aspects inﬂuencing

1.5
Research objectives and questions
In this section, the main objectives of the project, together with their underlaying re-
search questions are detailed. These objectives and questions are deﬁned to guide
the research process and lead the results towards the solving of the previously stated
problems. The presented research questions are aimed to be answered throughout this
report.
The main goal of this project is to evaluate, from a people centered perspective, the
ability of community based renewable energy projects to improve the access to mod-
ern energy services (i.e. electricity) in urban informal settlements. This objective is
summarized and reﬂected in the project objective (P.O) and research question (R.Q)
number zero:
P.O.0-Evaluate community based energy project’s capability to improve energy access
in urban informal settlements in a need-responsive way and considering the contextual
speciﬁc situations
R.Q.0-How can community based renewable energy projects improve the access to
modern energy services in Cape Town’s urban informal settlements?
In order to achieve the previously stated objective, four sub-objectives, led by their
own research questions were setted. Each of these objectives focus on a different step
of the research, and their progresive achievement is expected to lead to a comprehen-
sive understanding of the living conditions and energy challenges in Khayelitsha, and
of the potential of the community-based renewable energy model.

Methodology
The applied methodology and the argumentation of its adequacy to address the prob-
lematics studied in this project, is detailed in the present section.
This study aims to evaluate the suitability of community-based renewable energy
projects to improve the access to modern energy services in peri-urban areas and pro-
poses a people-centered and bottom-up approach to the design and provision of these
services. This approach is based and requires deep understanding of the socio-economic
context in which the service delivery is implemented. Therefore, the presented method-
ology is based on the study of an speciﬁc case of study.
The township of Khayelitsha, situated East to the city of Cape Town in South Africa,
was selected as a demonstrative case to develop a series of context-speciﬁc criteria and
indicators of adequate access to energy sources and services. Additionally, the lessons
learnt and the recommendations developed through the research process were applied
and referenced to this case.
Community-based renewable energy projects were proposed as alternatives to the
existing service delivery initiatives (i.e. grid electriﬁcation). And their capacity to ad-
dress the energy needs in Khayelitsha was evaluated. Seven examples of existing community-
based projects in South Africa were selected and utilized as sources of qualitative data
and information.

This is a desktop research, based mainly on the use of secondary data from arti-
cles, government publications and surveys, that is complemented with primary data
obtained by interviewing experts in South Africa.
Applying the above methods, a comprehensive analysis of the socio-economic con-
text and basic service delivery situation in South Africa was conducted. In addition,
the key barriers to access energy sources and services in Khayelitsha were identiﬁed.
Subsequently, a set of ”energy access indicators” speciﬁc for the case study were de-

2.1
Main case of study
To develop a case-speciﬁc deﬁnition of energy access from a bottom-up perspective, it
was essential to understand the local context. With this aim, a methodology based on
a main individual case of study was applied.
A qualitative case of study is a common tool for researchers that "facilitates the ex-
ploration of a phenomenon within its context using a variety of data sources" (Baxter
and Jack, 2008). And it is commonly considered and utilized, when (Yin, 2005):
• the study aims to answer ”how” and ”why” questions;
• the behaviour of the study participants cannot be manipulated;
• the contextual conditions are considered relevant to the phenomena of study
and therefore want to be covered;
• or the boundaries between the context and the phenomena are not very differ-
enciated .
In this case, the studied phenomena should be investigated withing its context, as
no improvements in the delivery of energy services can be contemplated or designed
without considering the conditions under which challenges and opportunities affect-
ing livelihood arise. A case of study methodology was therefore suitable to include
the geographical, political, social and economic speciﬁc characteristics in the project
analysis.

According to Yin (2005), a frequent concern related to cases of study is that "they
provide little basis for scientiﬁc generalization" as they focus on single speciﬁc cases.
Therefore, conclusions based on cases of study are not generalizable to different pop-
ulations and samples, but "to theoretical propositions" (Yin, 2005).
While leasons and observations from a particular case of study cannot be directly
applied to other cases due to its unique contextual characteristics, it allows for the
elaboration of theories that can be later applied in different contexts to meet their own
speciﬁc characteristics and conditions.
In this project, the main case of study served to develop a case-speciﬁc deﬁnition of
energy access, and to test the community-based energy model.
2.1.1
Why Cape Town, South Africa?
Cape Town in South Africa was the selected area of study. This location was chosen
due to the political, social and economic characteristics of the country, as well as due
to practicalities and the relatively good access to data and information.
South Africa faces some common challenges with other African coutries to deal with
the population increase and rapid urbanization. These problems, enhanced by Apartheid
policies at the time, are leading to a growing number of informal settlements, the in-
crease of urban poor population, and the persistence of inequalities (Runsten et al.,
2018).
In this context, "services such as energy, water and sanitation have become a key fea-
ture of the democratic government’s policy to redress past imbalances, and equality in
energy access, occupies a central role in service delivery" (Tait, 2015). This institutional
focus in the delivery of basic services, as well as the explicit efforts to achieve equality,
constitute a suitable environment for the proposition of new service delivery meth-
ods and to explore the opportunities of renewable energy technologies to improve the
access to energy services.
The city of Cape Town, one of the most unequal cities of the country, was one of the
pioneers that aknowledged the backlog in service delivery in informal settlements and
that started to target these areas with its electriﬁcation initiatives.
In addition, the University of Cape Town has focused a signiﬁcant part of their job
in addressing the basic service delivery situation, specially the electriﬁcation, of these
areas, and investigating different approaches to this delivery. There are also a substan-
tial number of organizations and NGOs operating in the area and running projects to

2.1.2
Khayelitsha
Khayelitsha is one of the biggest townships in South Africa. It hosts 391749 people liv-
ing in 118 809 households, according to SDI&GIS (2013). Additionally, Khayelitsha has
one of the fastest growing population in the country. Housing is very heterogeneous in
the township, and several informal placements are integrated among the formal set-
tlements. It was reported as of 2011, that 54% of Khayelitsha’s population was living in
informal households, of which 8% were backyard shacks and 46% shacks not in back-
yard, and that at least 80% of the total population had access to some source of elec-
tricity. However, there is still a big backlog in terms of delivery of basic services like
water, toilets and sanitation (SDI&GIS, 2013).
Therefore, it constitutes an example of low income and poor, highly populated and
inequal settlement in which different types of housing co-exist and where basic service
delivery fails to support a large portion of the population.
The existance and challenges to livelihood experienced in the informal settlements
in the area are well acknowledged by government institutions and NGOs and, as a con-
sequence, many projects have been developed and tested in the area with the aim of
improving the living conditions and reduce inequealities.As a result, there is a signiﬁ-
cant amount of available information from surveys and interviews performed by orga-
nizations working in the area. As an important phase of most of the projects addressing
informal settlements, the numeration and maping of the households in the area was
performed, and the information is available for speciﬁc areas in the Cape Town town-
ship (Figure 2.1).

2.3
’Community cases demonstration’: a multi-case study
perspective
A series of existing community-based projects and initiatives were selected as exem-
pliﬁying cases to be analyzed. The objective of the analysis was to evaluate their abil-
ity to respond to the identiﬁed needs and barriers in Khayelitsha, by comparing and
ﬁnding relationships between the projects’ results and the results expected to provide
adequate energy access in the area. In addition, the ﬁndings from this analysis were
utilized to build a series of recommendations for the case of Khayelitsha.
This methodology based on the intercomparison of different cases ﬁts into the def-
inition of multiple-case study developend by Yin (2005), in which "several cases are
examined to understand the differences and similarities among them". The applica-
tion of this method serves both to predict similar results and outcomes for different
contexts (literal replication), and different results for different cases due to predictable
reasons (theoretical replication) (Yin, 2005).
The context (location, target group, political situation and actions, etc), motivations,
and focus areas of the various cases were selected from a wide spectrum. Therefore,
as it is explained in following sections of this report, among the chosen cases there
were projects focusing in both urban and rural areas, initiatives addressing housing
and tenure problems, providing access to electricity and clean fuels, targeting informal
settlements, and as well projects in different locations.
The criteria applied to select the case examples were the high degree of engagement
of the local population in the processes and decission making of the project, and the
local sharing of the beneﬁts and outcomes of the project (Walker and Devine-Wright,
2008a). And not their speciﬁc contexts or motivations.
Information regarding the context, motivations, and results of the different projects
was obtained from project reports, news and media, and conducted interviews with
representatives and people involved in some of the projects.

2.4
Expert interviews
Interviews were conducted via phone and video calls with experts and people involved
in basic service delivery projects in South Africa. Experts working in the ﬁeld of renew-
able energy, energy access and informal settlements were interviewed with the aim of
receiving feedback and insights about the development process of the research, as well
as to build on existing literature and comment on their experiences working with the
topics addressed in this study.
In addition, three professionals involved in the community-based projects selected
as exemplifying cases were also interviewed in order to achieve a good understanding
of their projects’ motivations, activities, outcomes and impacts.
Both types of interviews followed the model of semistructured-interviews in which
a number of questions were prepared in advance to lead the conversation and to cover
the most relevant topics for the study but allowing the conversation to take different
directions before covering all the areas of interest (Miles and Gilbert, 2005) .
The interviews were conducted in a period of 4 months from March to June 2018,
and among the participants were researchers of the Energy Research Center (ERC),
of the University of Cape Town and from the Nelson Mandela University, an architect
from Violence Prevention through Urban Upgrading (VPUU), the Solar Utility Manager
of iShack, and the CEO of SolarTurtle.

3.1
Informal settlements and Cape Town
Information regarding the number of households living in informal settlements in the
Cape Town area differ from one source to another. This is mainly due to the intrinsic
difﬁculty of deﬁning "informal settlements" and the diversity of meanings that institu-
tions and organizations associate to the term.
Therefore the deﬁnition of the concept of "informal settlement" is of high relevance
to set the context of the research and to contrast and compare results across studies.
According to Housing Development Agency (2013), despite the existing differences,
all institutions when speaking about informal settlements, refer to their temporary na-
ture, the housing type, ownership, legality and the properties of their located land.

This is the case of Statistics South Africa (SSA) (Statistics South Africa, 2018) and the
Metropolitan Municipality of the City of Cape Town (City of Cape Town, 2018), that de-
ﬁne informal settlements as "unplanned settlements on land which has not been sur-
veyed or proclaimed as residential, consisting mainly of informal dwellings (shacks)".
And refer to informal dwellings as "makeshift structures, not approved by local author-
ities and not intended as a permanent dwelling".
Another important source of inconsistency is the delimitation of the physical bound-
aries of the informal settlement. These boundaries play an important role when de-
signing and applying policies and programmes, and are a key parameter for the under-
standing of the settlements, their magnitude, living conditions, etc. However, the way
these limits are deﬁned also differ substantially from some sources to others, as the
different institutions apply varying methods such as enumerating campaigns or image
and GIS (Geographic Informaton System) based systems to identify the settlements
(Housing Development Agency, 2013).
Furthermore, not all informal households lay within the limits of the informal set-
tlements, and not all households placed in informal settlements are informal. This
usually derives in problems of application of the policies, which are designed to target
collectives within very deﬁned borderlines.
In this report, the deﬁnition of informal settlement utilized by the City of Cape Town
municipality is adopted. These settlements are chosen as the areas of study due to the
higher concentration of informal dwellings , nevertheless, the existence of informality
outside the limits of these settlements and the need to address it are acknowledged.
In the year 2011, the total number of informal households in South Africa recorded in
the Census was 1962733 and corresponded to 13.5% of the total number of households
in the country (SSA, 2012).
When looking at the results of the Western Cape province, and absolute increase in
the number of households is identiﬁed respect to the previous decade (2001). Of these
households, 18% were recorded as informal (1% more than in the previous decade),
and 80% formal. Among the informal households, the shacks in backyards amounted
for 6% of the total, while the proportion of shacks not in backyards remained constant
from 2001 at 12% .The results from the Census 2011 also conﬁrm the existence of a high
proportion of the informal housing located outside the borders of the areas classiﬁed
as informal residential (32% of the shacks not in backyards), and a signiﬁcant share
of formal housing inside these zones, that reﬂect the complexity of these areas and

are 204 recognized informal settlements with 437 individual clusters ranging in size,

In addition to the housing type, the characteristics of the land in which the settle-

tion of establishing settlements in private or state owned land. These characteristics
challenge the stablishment of facilities and the delivery of good services and situate
informal settlements at the backlog of the basic services.
3.2
Khayelitsha’s current status
Khayelitsha is the biggest township in Cape Town, and by 2011 it hosted 391 749 inhab-
itants and 118 809 households. The origin of the township dates from the Apartheid era
(1983) when it was stablished to relocate the black population in the surroundings of
Cape Town. Therefore almost 99% of the population in Khayelitsha is Black African
(SDI&GIS, 2013).
Khayelitsha is not an informal settlement itself yet it hosts numerous informal set-
tlements or clusters of informal households that amount to 64 761 informal dwellings,
corresponding to almost 55% of the households in the area (SDI&GIS, 2013). There-
fore, despite the variety of housing types and tenure status, challenges associated to
informality are highly present in Khayelitsha.

Housing
Of the total 118 809 households of Khayelitsha, only 44.6% were formal dwellings by
2011, with more than half of the households in the township being informal dwellings
(SDI&GIS, 2013). 55 030 (46.3%) of them were shacks not in backyards, while 8.2%

and concentrated in distinguishable hubs or informal settlements (See Figure 3.2).
Regarding tenure, almost 57% of the households in 2011, according to the Census,
were owned dwellings, meaning that they were property of the inhabitants of the house-
hold. In addition, only 12% of households were rented, while 32 816 (28%) were con-
ﬁrmed as occupied dwellings without the need of paying rent.
Due to the high number of dwellings claimed as owned, it is deduced that not all
of them are formal dwellings, which is a sign of the elevated feeling of ownership in
the area despite the high rate of informality. The amount of rental households also
indicates a low dependency of the inhabitants in terms of housing and implies that
regardless of the legal barriers to formalization and the tenure challenges in the ar-
eas, ownership is recognized and felt in the community. This has a big impact in the
willigness of people to invest in their households, the degree of permanency of the set-
tlements, etc.

4.1
South Africa’s actions to improve energy access
The provision of basic services and the energy policy have experienced big changes
in the recent years in South Africa, specially when transitioning from the apartheid
regime to the democratic government in 1994.
Before 1994, the "separate development" principle was guiding all policies in South
Africa, including the energy sector . Due to economic and political reasons, the energy
Page 38
==PAGE:51==

Master’s Thesis
requirements of the industry sector were prioritized, and policies were focused mainly
in electricity and liquid fuels. Regarding the residential sector, the access to modern
energy services was facilitated to the white population, leaving the rest with limited or
no access to these services (Winkler, 2006).
The democratic government focused its efforts on ﬁxing the inequalities from the
previous period and, acting in accordance with the Constitution, addressed the deliv-
ery of basic services from a "rights-based and universal approach" (GNESD and Tait,
2013). Modern energy and electricity were considered important components of these
basic services (Winkler, 2006).
Since then, the delivery of basic services, including electricity, comes from the hand
of national, provincial and local government, that ideally work in a coordinated way
while having differentiated tasks. However, reality shows that there is a strong dis-
connection between the different entities, and communications between the different
levels and sectors constitutes a big challenge that jeopardizes the success of the pro-
grammes. (GNESD and Tait, 2013)
The most relevant policies and programmes addressing energy access are summa-
rized below:
The White Paper on Energy
With the end of apartheid and the election of the new democratic government, a review
of the existing energy policies was required. Accordingly, a new White Paper on Energy
started to be developed in 1994 (DoME, 1998) and was published in 1998 (Winkler,
2006).
The White Paper in Energy is composed of four differentiated parts in which the con-
text and objectives for energy policy, the demand sectors, the supply sectors, and addi-
tional cross-cutting issues are reviewed (Winkler, 2006). It also gathers all the policy ob-
jectives regarding the energy sector, which are summarized in the following points: "in-
creasing the access to affordable energy services, improving energy governance, stim-
ulating economic development, managing energy-related environmental and health
effects, and securing supply through diversity".(Winkler, 2006; DoME, 1998).
South Africa’s Energy Act was established in 2008 with the aim of realizing the objec-
tives from the White Paper on Energy (GNESD and Tait, 2013).
Among the targets stablished in the Energy Act is the need of "facilitating energy
access for the improvement of the quality of life of the people of the Republic", and the
Page 39
==PAGE:52==

Blanca Martinez Nieto
imperative of "contributing to the sustainable development of South Africa’s economy"
(Republic of South Africa, 2008).
Electriﬁcation Programme
The National Electriﬁcation Programme (NEP) was running between 1994 and 1999.
In 1994 a national electriﬁcation meeting took place and started a series events that
led, to the creation of an energy section and an electriﬁcation programme inside the
Reconstruction and Development Programme (RDP) of the elections’ winner African
National Congres (ANC) (Winkler, 2006).
It consisted of a "wide scale electriﬁcation programme targeting poor and previously
un-serviced households", and it was ﬁnanced by Eskom, South Africa’s national utility
(GNESD and Tait, 2013).
During the years that it was implemented, the NEP achieved the target of 2.5 million
households and schools connected to the electricity grid, becoming a great success
(DoME, 2001). This was partly caused by the initial targeting of urban and densely-
populated areas (GNESD and Tait, 2013).
In 2001, the Department of Energy became in charge of the electriﬁcation programme,
that was revised and renamed as the Integrated National Electriﬁcation Programme
(INEP) (GNESD and Tait, 2013).
In the last years, the increase rates of the number of connections has fallen signiﬁ-
cantly, however, a total of 6.7 million households have been connected to the national
electricity grid between 1994 and 2016 (Government of South Africa, 2018).
The Non-Grid Electriﬁcation Programme is part of the INEP and was implemented
to electrify households in areas in which the connections to the electricity grid was not
possible. Far located households or spread villages may be challenging to connect to
the national grid, being in some cases not economically or technically feasible. For
this reason, the programme focuses in the provision of Solar Home Systems (SHS) , in
order to temporarily cover part of the electricity demand of selected households until
the connection to the electricity grid is viable (DoE, 2012). The basic system provided
consists on a photovoltaic panel of 50 Wp, a charge controllers, a battery of 105 Ah, and
four efﬁcient ﬂuorescent lights (DoE, 2012).
In addition, there are a series of requirements that must be met in order to be eligible
for the non-grid electriﬁcation programme. Two of these preconditions are: not having
Page 40
==PAGE:53==
Chapter 4. Energy access, a people centered approach
Master’s Thesis
plans of expanding the grid in the area in the three subsequent years, and being placed
more than two kilometers away from the power lines. Preventing almost all households
placed in urban areas from being eligible (GNESD and Tait, 2013).
Free Basic Energy Policies
South Africa recognizes the right of every citizen to have electricity, and accordingly
includes the provision of this resource in its basic services delivery and assigns a sub-
sidy to provide a basic amount of free electricity to poor households (GNESD and
Tait, 2013). The Free Basic Electricity (FBE) was proposed in 2003 and targets low in-
come households connected to the electricity grid and with electricity consumptions
lower than 450 kWh a month. It is funded by the national government and provides a
monthly amount of 50kWh of free electricity, contributing to a small part of the hose-
hold’s electricity demand (GNESD and Tait, 2013).
The Free Basic Alternative Energy Policy (FBAE) was developed in 2007. It targets
the households that are not connected to the electricity grid, and subsidies a limited
amount of their energy consumption.The subsidy supports the use of other fuels al-
ternative to electricity such as parafﬁn, LPG and biomass, and it is not technology pre-
scriptive. The programme provides a series of source and technology options with their
correspondent assessment, but it is the municipality’s responsibility to select the most
suitable energy solution for every case (DoME).
Due to the high variety of energy options, the logistics and implementation of the
subsidy constitutes an important challenge in most of the cases as it involves many
stakeholders (GNESD and Tait, 2013).
The Integrated Energy Plan
The proposal of the Integrated Energy Plan (IEP) is mandated in the National Energy
Act (2008) and contemplated in the White Paper on Energy from 1998. It presents a
roadmap for the future South African energy system and provides projections of the
needed investments and policy changes. These projections are based on the study of
the present consumption and production patterns, and in the goals to be achieved
regarding energy in every different sector of the economy (DoE, 2017b).

country in 2050, and focuses in three main aspects: economic, social and environmen-

4.2
People-centered deﬁnition of energy access
As it was mentioned in the introduction of this report, signiﬁcant efforts have been put
to understand the meaning and to provide a good deﬁnition of energy access, however,
deﬁning the limits between the ”adequate” and the ”poor” access to energy constitutes
a signiﬁcant challenge. A widely accepted deﬁnition of energy poverty is the one pro-
vided by UNDP (2000) that describes it as "the absence of sufﬁcient choice in accessing
adequate, affordable, reliable, high-quality, safe, and environmentally benign energy
services to support economic and human development".
Measuring the level of access becomes a challenge as great as deﬁning it. According
to the Sustainable Development Knowledge Platform (UNDESA, 2016), the indicators
to be utilized to measure the achievement of the "universal access to energy" target
are the "proportion of population with access to electricity, and the proportion of the
Page 42
==PAGE:55==
Chapter 4. Energy access, a people centered approach
Master’s Thesis
population with primary reliance on clean fuels and technology".
These indicators are based on supply side measurements, which is the most ex-
tended approach to measure energy access (Tait, 2015) due to its simplicity and the
comparability of the results. This methods account for the "quantity aspects" of the
energy access (e.g. number of connections to the electricity grid, number of blackouts
and interruptions, cost of the electricity, etc.), however, they fail to reﬂect all the con-
textual and subjective aspects as well as the "quality" of energy access.
In this study, a people-centered and bottom-up deﬁnition of energy access and ap-
proach to the provision of modern energy services is proposed. This approach is based
on the recognition of energy as a mean and enabler, and not as an ultimate target. Ac-
cording to this belief, the relevance of improving energy access does not lay on the
access to the source itself but in the needs and uses that are covered with the en-
ergy resources.Despite most of the targets being based on delivery rates, the policies
addressing the provision energy are based on the believe that access to this resource
helps the development and improves livelihood, and, that the lack of these services
derive into big inequalities, exclusion, slow development and challenging living con-
ditions. Therefore, the ultimate goal is the improvement of living conditions, devel-
opment, and inequality in the area, and the provision of adequate means to achieve
them. Needs, challenges, lifestyle, working activities, etc. vary signiﬁcantly across the
different geographical areas and are inﬂuenced by multiple social, economic, physical
and environmental factors. These conditions make the access to energy completely
binded to the contextual characteristics and exclusive of each singular case and area.
In accordance with the previous statement, an "adequate energy service" would be
the one able to meet the energy needs in each speciﬁc context. Therefore the method-
ology followed in this chapter aims to identify the characteristics and conditions to
provide adequate energy access in Khayelitsha based on the main challenges encoun-
tered in the area, the services needed and their relationship with energy. There are two
main aspects to be taken into account when studying the energy requirements in the
speciﬁc area : the indispensable resources to cover the basic needs, and the limiting
factors (constraints) preventing solutions to be implemented.

Once the prioritizable challenges are identiﬁed (Chapter 3), their relationship with en-

fuel, delivery method etc. is not able to solve an speciﬁc issue. In this cases, it is im-

Housing and energy
A signiﬁcant part of the daily activities of every inhabitant take place in the household.
House work, cooking, leisure activities, etc. are normally indoor activities that require,
to some extent, the use of energy. Therefore a big part of the energy needs of the pop-
ulation are required in the household.

Housing characteristics have a big inﬂuence in the energy consumption patterns
and preferences, and also act as constraints.
According to the surveys and the problems identiﬁed in Chapter 3, the primary chal-
lenges associated to housing in the area are the size, the structure and the ownership
of the dwellings.
The lack of enough space inside the household is a strong limiting factor for the
household activities due to the lack of privacy, the sharing of spaces, and obvious phys-
ical restrictions. Therefore, the lower the amount of goods and equipment is placed in-
side the house, and the smaller is the size of these facilities, the better is the utilization
of the space.
The size of the household is not dependent on the access to energy resources, how-
ever, it must be taken into consideration when planning and deciding the best way
to provide them. For this reason energy services in Khayelitsha must: occupy as least
space as possible, and be able to be located in non-utilized parts of the houses or out-
side of them (Social Surveys Africa et al., 2017).
Bad housing materials and a poor building structure make the dwelling vulnerable
to weather conditions (rain, snow and heat), involve safety issues, and of course, highly

affects the household’s comfort conditions (temperature, humidity, ventilation and in-
door air quality).
Those situations have a strong and direct impact in the energy consumption pat-
terns and needs of the households. The heating demand represents a good example:
thin walls and ceilings of poor materials lead to very low indoor temperatures during
the winter, increasing the heating demand, while bad ventilation conditions and plas-
tic materials can lead to very high temperatures in the summer.
This sensitivity to outdoor conditions is directly related to the energy efﬁciency and
the capacity of the dwelling structure to keep and transfer heat. The lower the thermal
efﬁciency in the household, the higher the demand of energy for heating or cooling is.
While well isolated and ventilated households of adequate materials normally require
less energy for heating and cooling due to their improved energy efﬁciency.
In the case of Khayelithsa, due to the more or less constant temperatures and mild
weather, heating demand is not signiﬁcantly high, however the poor structure and bad
materials of the households lead to big energy losses. Therefore the implementation
of energy efﬁciency measures in the household is identiﬁed as a relevant factor to im-
prove the energy conditions in the area.
Ownership constitutes the third key concerning aspect regarding housing in infor-
mal settlements in Khayelitsha. The lack of formal status make inhabitants in the area
susceptible to relocation, and therefore, despite the long periods that inhabitants live
in the settlements, they are normally seen as temporary establishments. This has an
extensive effect in inhabitant’s willingness to invest and to upgrade their households
and services, as well as in their priorities and expenditure.
Authorities also do not provide funding for temporary settlements, what supposes a
very big constraint in a low income area like Khayelitsha.
Consequently, it is assumed that big and permanent investments are not likely to
be successful in the area both due to the lack of national or municipal funding and
enough income from inhabitants, and to the temporary nature of the settlements and
lack of securities . And low investment or mobile solutions become more attractive
in these circumstances due to the lower risk of loosing the investment due to possible
relocations or modiﬁcations in the settlement.

can be identiﬁed:
• Small size or possibility to locate. equipment outside the household.

Crime and energy
The relationship between crime and the access to energy is complex. The high crime
rates and unsafe situation require of improvements and measures involving energy
sources and services, but at the same time they act as a limitating factors and con-
straints preventing the success of many of the possible delivery solutions.
According to the surveys (City of Cape Town and Data First, 2006; Social Surveys
Africa et al., 2017), and despite the robberies to households and businesses, the high-
est perceived risk of crime is experienced in the streets and during the night. Darkness
is therefore perceived as one of the most problematic points regarding crime and ac-
cordingly, improvements in street lighting are demanded by the inhabitants (City of
Cape Town and Data First, 2006) and recognized as needed by other entities (?).
In addition, the slow and insufﬁcient response and difﬁculties accessing emergency
services is also expressed by part of the inhabitants (City of Cape Town and Data First,
2006). This could be improved by providing better communication networks, phones,
internet, etc.
The implementation of street lights and communication services both require of the
use of electricity as an energy source, and also of a constant and interruptible source
that is able to provide electricity during the night. In addition, they highlight the need
and importance of providing energy services also outside the household and to address
the energy delivery as a community issue and not as individual household supply.
Crime also constitutes a limiting factor that prevents some solutions from success
due to the high risk and probability of equipment, cables and materials to be stolen or
broken (PVs, solar cookers, cables, stoves,...). This problem is noted by experts working
in informal settlements in South Africa as one of the main reasons of project failure in
these establishments.

Unemployment and energy
Unemployment is one of the biggest areas of concern among the inhabitants in Khayelit-
sha, and despite not being a problem directly related to the use of energy, there are
some aspects in which the access to these services can improve the working condi-
tions, enable the creation of small business or prevent some of the existing ones from
failure. In addition, the introduction of energy services could bring a new opportunity
to create employment and to build capacity in the community by gaining new skills.
According to City of Cape Town and Data First (2006), some of the businesses in
Khayelitsha suffer from problems associated with the availability of energy resources
and interruptions. Examples of these businesses are food shops and phone charging
stations that are affected by the malfunctioning of fridges and electricity means.
In addition, the introduction of new energy services could constitute a good oppor-
tunity to increase the labor and employment in the area, by designating local people
inside the community to work in the design, implementation, operation and mainte-
nance of the equipment and to ensure the correct functioning of the service.
Unemployment rates among young population are a big worry among the surveyed
inhabitants of Khayelitsha, therefore, the training and knowledge transfer of skills such
as project management, installation of equipment, manteinance, ﬁnance, etc. can
serve as a good tool to boost the number of young professionals and job opportuni-
ties in the area.

the energy services in Khayelitsha in order to improve the unemployment conditions
are identiﬁed:
• To training young population in the skills required to design and run the energy
projects.

Basic services and energy
The Constitution of South Africa recognizes the imperative of providing every South
African citizen with electricity and the impossibility to disregard part of the population
when delivering the service.
Normally, the basic energy services in the households are understood as cooking,
heating (space and water heating), and lightning. In the case of Khayelitsha, most of
the inhabitants ﬁll these energy needs by combining multiple fuels without relaying in
a single energy source. The choice of fuels include electricity, parafﬁn, gas (LPG) and
biomass, and is inﬂuenced by many factors including preferences, and availability of
the sources.
Among these basic uses of energy in the household, cooking is the most energy in-
tensive activity and accounts for two thirds of the total energy consumption in a nor-
mal household (Cowan and Dieden, 2008). In order to avoid the need of complemen-
tary fuels and be able to completely cover the basic needs it is therefore essential to
provide access to sufﬁcient energy and at the times needed.
From the provision of other basic services different from energy, some conclusions
can be extrapolated. Looking at the most challenging aspects and points in which the
previous service delivery initiatives have failed to satisfy the requirements of the in-
habitants it is possible to identify some conditions that the new energy service must
comply with in order to avoid the same mistakes.
Among the most problematic points in Khayelitsha are the long distances to services,
the low reliability and frequent interruptions, and the issues related to the operation
and maintenance of the facilities.
Once the services are stablished in the area it is necessary to maintain the infras-
tructure to ensure the good performance and the adequate conditions of the facilities.
However, according to the surveys and interviews, many projects fail due to abandon-
ment of the installations that, in the case of sanitation facilities, derive in very bad
hygiene conditions and drainage, in the case of water to multiple interruptions and
leaking taps, and in the case of electricity facilities to malfunctioning and power out-
ages.
All these conditions can be summarized in the following list of characteristics:

• The energy service must be able to provide enough energy to cover all the basic
needs.
• It should be closely located to the point of use.
• It must be a continuous and reliable energy source without sudden interrup-
tions.
• And it must have easy and few operation and maintenance needs.
Povery and energy
There is a clearly deﬁned relationship between the income poverty and the energy con-
sumption and patterns. This relationship is highly determined by what is referred as
the affordability of energy. Affordability relates to the ability of a household to pay the
required amount of energy needed without interfering or hampering other household
needs.
The symbolic affordability threshold is normally represented by the ’energy burden
level’ or the ’percentage of household income which is used to purchase energy ser-
vices’, that normally focuses on fuel expenditure but it can include appliance costs, and
externalities such as health and safety risks, limitations on productivity, etc. (Cowan
and Dieden, 2008). The deﬁnition of this term, however, reﬂects just the effect of eco-
nomic factors such as household income and the prices of energy, but neglects to rep-
resent the subjective aspects of affordability (Tait, 2015).
Preferences and priorities of the users also have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence the afford-
ability of energy and therefore using purely ﬁnancial terms to evaluate it shows a very
limited picture of reality (Tait, 2015). In the other hand, measuring affordability in a
more comprehensive deﬁnition constitutes a big challenge.
However, without the intention of measuring it, there are some conditions than can
be applied when designing the energy delivery service to ensure that the solution pro-
posed is as affordable as possible: trying to reduce the costs and prices all over the
service chain while ensuring the quality, and covering the basic needs and preferred
uses.
In order to reduce the cost and price of the energy consumed as much as possible
a cheap energy source and generation or distribution technology must be chosen. In
addition, the need and costs of maintenance should be reduced as much as possible
while ensuring the quality and well-functioning of the technology.

==PAGE:63==

Making sure of covering all the basic and preferred needs of the community is also
an important requirements as it increases substantially the level of acceptation of the
service and the probability of becoming a priority for users, avoiding the need of com-
plementary services that increase the costs.
And ﬁnally, the efﬁciency of the energy source, equipment and appliances used has
also a big impact in the affordability of energy. The use of efﬁcient equipment leads to
lower energy consumptions and therefore lower costs.
Therefore, from the previous analysis, measures to be adopted to improve the af-
fordability of energy are identiﬁed:
• The use of a cheap energy source.
• The use of a cheap conversion method and equipment.
• The use if equipment with low an easy maintenance requirements.
• The use of efﬁcient equipment and appliances.
• The provision of adequate energy to cover the basic and preferred needs.
Education and energy
A direct way in which the access to energy contributes to the improvement of educa-
tion is with the provision of adequate conditions for learning in schools. These con-
ditions include appropriate lightning, electricity for appliances, heating and cooling,
etc.
Nevertheless, education also has a big inﬂuence in the energy access and use. Many
of the social barriers preventing energy solutions from being widely utilized or unap-
propriate energy sources from being abandoned are consequence of an insufﬁcient
knowledge of the available resources and their use.
Education support in ﬁelds like energy efﬁciency, electricity risks, safety and the ad-
equate use of the energy sources, is a key component in the introduction of new tech-
nologies or energy services, and therefore it is also an important factor to consider
when designing the energy delivery service in Khayelitsha.

The choice of appropriate energy sources and conversion methods has a big impact in
the health of the users.

• And provide safe equipment and its required maintenance.

The irregular spatial planiﬁcation, lack of urban patterns and the high density of the

They also challenge the energy delivery method, that needs to be customized and

Densely populated areas also entail safety risks due to the proximity of the house-

Technical constraints

service, a ﬂexible and easily upgradable solution is required.
In addition, as it was previously introduced, the maintenance requirements of the
equipment also constitute a signiﬁcant constraint: the high costs and the difﬁculty of
maintaning the facilities could lead to the abandonment, bad performance and de-
crease of the lifetime of the facility.
Economic constraints
Due to the low income conditions, the investment in energy sources or access improve-
ments is an unthinkable possibility for most of the population in Khayelitsha, restricted
just to the few who have access to funding. This funding is normally very limited and
in most of the cases is not enough to pay the upfront costs, hence the high reliance in
credit and the extended use of the ’pay as you go’ system.

is an indispensable condition to develop energy projects. However, the informallity of

Environmental constraints
Environmental problems are not explicitly expressed as a big area of concern by inhab-

Social constraints
Social constraints play an key role in the adoption of new services and technologies. As
new services are introducted, live routines are supposed to vary accordingly, however,

Therefore preferences constitute signiﬁcant social constraints, as the beneﬁcial as-
pects of the new service could be overshadowed if it does not constitute a clear im-

multiple fuels in electriﬁed households, that keep using alternative sources to electric-

Community based energy projects
This chapter presents a simpliﬁed review of the changes in the energy system towards
a more diverse and distrubited generation and management of the electricity. Addi-
tionally, the concept of community renewable energy (CRE) and the opportunities of
this model in South Africa are introduced.
5.1
Towards a more distributed energy system
Traditionally, the generation, transmission and distribution of electricity have been
highly centralized industries, and the approach to power generation and consumption,
has been based on the production of electricity in large power plants at a big scale, and
its supply to consumers through transmission and distribution operators. This sys-
tem has led to a divided power system with two main differentiated sides: supply and
demand.
The supply side encompasses all electricity generators in a deﬁned system, while the
demand side includes all the small and large consumers inside the residential, com-
mercial and industry sectors. The amount of electricity generated is bonded and de-
termined by both sectors, their requirements, and constraints.
Page 63
==PAGE:76==

South Africa constitutes a clear example of a highly centralized power system. By
2016, the national utility Eskom owned and managed 91% of the country’s total gen-
eration capacity, and it was and still is in charge of the transmission and distribution
to consumers of the majority (around 60%)of the electricity generated (Africa-EU Re-
newable Energy Cooperation Programme, 2016). In the last years, South Africa has
experienced signiﬁcant problems with its power supply, and the power consumption
has overcome the power system’s capacity (Independet power Producers South Africa,
2018a). Predicting a continuous increase of the electricity demand in the future years,
the building of new generation capacity has become an urgent issue in the country.
Since 2003, when the participation of private investors in the power sector and the
division of the power capacity between Eskom and independent Producers (IPPs) was
approved (DoE, 2017a), a signiﬁcant amount of newly installed generation capacity
has resulted from the programme and private investment. The Department of Energy
(DoE) was given the responsibility to boost and promote the participation of investors
in IPPs through a bidding process focused on a diversiﬁed list of technologies (DoE,
2017a). As a result of this mechanism, the municipalities and IPPs accounted for the
remaining 9% of the generation capacity if South Africa in 2016.
All the electricity produced by South African IPPs is sold to Eskom, that consequently
sells it to the Regional Electricity Distributors (REDs) at a ﬁxed tariff determined by
the National Energy Regulator of South Africa (NERSA). These REDs distribute approx-
imately 40% of the total generated electricity among the consumers, and include 137
municipalities that participate in the distribution tasks. The rest of the electricity is dis-
tributed by Eskom itself (Africa-EU Renewable Energy Cooperation Programme, 2016).
Eskom’s generation capacity is mainly composed of coal ﬁred power plants (more
than 85%) what derives in a high carbon intense power system. However, in alligne-
ment with the National Development Plan (NDP), integrated energy Plan (IEP), and its
commitment to a low carbon development, a Renewable Independent Power Procure-
ment Programme was implemented (REIPPPP). This programme has achieved suc-
cessful results (Africa-EU Renewable Energy Cooperation Programme, 2016) and it is
expected that 17800 MW of the new capacity target for 2030 come form renewables and
the RIPPPP (Independet power Producers South Africa, 2018b).
Despite the large scale and centralized perspective under which electricity genera-
tion has been typically addressed, the use of smaller and distributed generators for the
residential, commercial or industrial sectors has also being present, specially in off-

==PAGE:77==

grid solutions in places with no electricity connection or to be used with emergency or
back up purposes (small diesel generators, wood stoves, etc.). However, the increas-
ing environmental awareness, the higher penetration of renewable energy technolo-
gies as well as the signiﬁcant drop that their prices have experiences in the last years
are bringing new opportunities to generate electricity at a small scale and in a more
cost-effective way. This has resulted in a signiﬁcant increase in the use of small renew-
able generators for a wider range of purposes, enabling individuals, small companies
or groups of people to invest in producing their own renewable electricity, and diluting
the strongly marked separation between supply and demand.
In South Africa, the distributed approach to electricity generation based on small
scale renewable technologies has been applied in several programmes addressing en-
ergy access. One example is the non-grid electriﬁcation programme included in the
INEP, that provides small photovoltaic solar panels (PVs) to households in which the
connection to the electricity grid is not feasible, typically in rural areas. South Africa’s
regulation, also allows to connect these small generation units to the national grid. The
so called Small-Scale Embedded Generation (SSEG) refers to the small generators con-
nected to the national electricity grid at the point of consumption, that can be utilized
with residential, commercial or industrial purposes (SALGA et al., 2017). Just in the
Western Cape province, 18 out of the 25 municipal electricity distributors allow to con-
nect SSEGs to their grid, 13 of which have an ’ofﬁcial application system’ and tariffs for
SSEGs. The municipality of City of Cape Town is included among these 13 distributors
(SALGA et al., 2017).
Changes in the power system and swifts towards a more distributed electricity gen-
eration are supported and enabled by regulatory changes and the development of new
complementary technologies such as the smart and bidirectional meters, internet, bat-
teries and small storage facilities, smart phones, etc. that improve measurement and
communication capacities and therefore allow a greater and faster demand response,
new ﬁnancing methods, improved payment methods, trading and revenue sharing
methods, and a better management of the ﬂuctuating and variable electricity gener-
ation.

5.2
New models for energy management, energy commu-
nities
As mentioned in the previous section, the appearance of new technologies and the
reduction of the cost in renewable energy generators has boosted the development
of alternative methods and business models for the generation, transmission, distri-
bution, and management of energy and specially electricity. Among these, the terms
Community Energy (CE), and Community Renewable Energy (CRE) are growing in im-
portance and are being largely adopted in different countries and sectors with the aim
of empowering communities to manage their own energy. These term includes energy
cooperatives , and any other schemes in which the members of the local community
are involved in the management and governance, development or planning of an en-
ergy technology (Šahovi´c and Da Silva, 2016).
In opposition to the classical centralized and fossil fuel reliant power system, CRE
proposes a very different model in which the relationships between production and
consumption are not as clearly deﬁned as in the traditional system and that is based
on the use of renewable energy technologies. According to the literature and the avail-
able projects claiming to be inside the CRE classiﬁcation, the term encompasses initia-
tives with very different ownership structures, technologies, legal and ﬁnancial models
(e.g. cooperatives, community charities, development trusts representing communi-
ties’ interests, and shares owned by community based organizations (Šahovi´c and Da
Silva, 2016) ), and stablishes within signiﬁcantly variable contexts.
Therefore, CRE constitutes another challenging term to deﬁne. There is no general
agreement in the speciﬁc deﬁnition among the available literature, research and exist-
ing CRE projects, however, there are some elements that are common to all deﬁnitions
and practices: the involvement of renewable energy technologies; the participation of
the local community; and its non-prescriptive nature in terms of technology, owner-
ship scheme, legal status, etc.
In one hand, the broad deﬁnition of CRE can constitute an opportunity, providing
the required ﬂexibility to develop solutions and projects that are responsive to the local
context, needs and preferences of the community involved (Hicks and Ison, 2018). But
in the other hand, this lack of speciﬁcations can lead to misunderstandings or difﬁcul-
ties in the deﬁnition and application of policies and regulations, and when determin-
ing which projects can be consider CRE and which cannot (Hicks and Ison, 2018).

In light of these non-prescriptive deﬁnition of community energy, the question of
what do all community renewable energy projects have in common that distinguishes
them from the rest of the projects laying out of this category, has guided numerous re-
search projects. From their experience with energy communities in United Kingdom,
Walker and Devine-Wright (2008b) identiﬁed ’two key dimensions that underline the
views of policy makers, administrators, activists, project participants and local resi-
dents’ in every CRE project: the process and the outcomes. Accordingly they propose
three different interpretations of the term CRE that are represented in Figure 5.1.
The ﬁrst interpretation (A) focuses on the need of a ’high degree of involvement’ of
the local population in all the planning, development and running of the project, and
prioritizes the participation and building of capacity inside the communities. The sec-
ond one (B), does not pay as much attention to the process but to the outcomes of
the projects and the distribution of the beneﬁts and positive impacts among the com-
munity. The third approach (C) comprehends a wider area of combinations between
outcomes and processes, as long as they lead to positive impacts in the communities
(Walker and Devine-Wright, 2008b).
Decisions and actions taken in order to engage the community in the process or to
direct the outcomes and impacts of the projects towards the local population are also
analyzed in the literature in order to give a sense to the large range of solutions and

models embraced under the CRE deﬁnition. Hicks and Ison (2018) defends that these
actions are taken to meet and ’suite’ the project’s motivations and context. In accor-
dance with the previous statement, this study evaluates the diverse range of models
that are enacted from the trade-offs among projects’ motivations and contexts, and
the decisions taken (Hicks and Ison, 2018).
The technology to be implemented is one of these deﬁning decisions. Traditionally,
the most applied technologies in CRE projects have been solar PV and wind turbines.
PV panels are characterized by their high ﬂexibility and simplicity, that together with
their low maintenance requirements make them suitable for off-grid solutions, small
scale installations in households, public buildings, community spaces, etc. In the case
of wind energy (on-shore), the reliability of the technology and the simplicity of the
generating process have played a key role in the success of the technology among CRE
projects (Šahovi´c and Da Silva, 2016).
CE and CRE have been highly developed and adopted specially in European coun-
tries such as United Kingdom and Denmark, were wind energy cooperatives, among
other models, contribute to a very signiﬁcant share of the countries’ total renewable
production. In the case of South Africa, as it was previously mentioned, the integra-
tion of renewable energy in the system is signiﬁcantly low, and just recently with the
introduction of the RIPPPP, investment is starting to derive towards the renewable gen-
eration technologies.
The RIPPPP also mandates the creation of positive socio-economic impact in the lo-
cal communities in which the projects are implemented (Independet power Producers
South Africa, 2018b). This is one of the steps that the country is taking towards a greater
inclusion and consideration of the local communities in the energy sector. However,
despite the existence of initiatives claiming to be community projects in South Africa,
the number of these initiatives is still very limited and the concepts of CR and CRE are
yet not very developed, partly due to the centralized approach towards power genera-
tion and service delivery that has traditional led the country’s policies and decisions.
The previously reviewed trends of the energy system, and generation alternatives are
driven mainly by technological and ﬁnancial improvements, however, the adoption of
these new technologies and methods is highly subjected and constrained by the regu-
lation of the country and municipality in which the projects are implemented. A brief
description of the most relevant regulation about individual or shared energy produc-
ers in South Africa can be found in Appendix B.

6.1
A people-centered and ﬂexible solution
In this project, a bottom-up approach to the delivery of energy services is proposed
with the aim of providing alternatives that respond to the local contextual characteris-
tics, targetting the needs and livelihood challenges in the communities.
Therefore, when focusing on a delivery method, it is necessary to ﬁnd a model that
provides the required ﬂexibility and includes a wide range of technologies, ﬁnance
methods, governance structures, etc. to adapt the service to the speciﬁc needs, con-
straints and requirements of each speciﬁc time and area.
Despite the already mentioned drawbacks associated to the broadness of the CRE

model, its "diversity can be seen as an inherent strength" (Hicks and Ison, 2018), that
provides a ﬂexible space in which projects can operate and adapt to the speciﬁc con-
textual characteristics without following a deﬁned structure of how community energy
projects should be implemented (Walker and Devine-Wright, 2008b; Hicks and Ison,
2018).
It is therefore, the ﬂexibility that this term offers, what makes it suitable to be ap-
plied in this study, and the characteristic that could make this delivery method suc-
ceed where other methods have failed due to their top-down, technology centered,
and standardized approach. Especially when the targeted areas struggle to comply
with strict requirements, like in informal settlements.
The focus of CRE in the community also constitutes an advantage of this method
over the traditional services, which are focused on particulars, when addressing the
socio-economic development of a speciﬁc area. As it includes both the shared beneﬁts
and results over the group of inhabitants as a whole, and their associated impacts over
each individual.
CRE’s advocacy for the provision of energy through renewable sources and technolo-
gies also accords with the objective of the energy service proposed in this study to pro-
vide renewable electricity.
According to Walker and Devine-Wright (2008b) and as presented in Chapter 5, the
different degrees of involvement of the local population in the CRE project, and the
way in which its results are directed towards this group, lead to different ”community
energy solutions”. Accordingly, the maximum representative of the CRE model would
be a project in which the ownership and power of decision are shared equally among
the members of the community, and in which they all equitably beneﬁt from all the
impacts of the project.
However, in this study, a wide deﬁnition of CRE is contemplated, and solutions with
lower degrees of community-governance are included. This is due to the unique char-
acteristics and the elevated number of legal and socio-economics constraints that limit
the viable alternatives in peri-urban informal settlements.
As a result, the deﬁnition adopted to frame this study is the one of an energy project
that utilizes renewable sources exclusively, and that involves the community in the de-
cission making, planning, design, implementation and operation of the project, while
focusing on beneﬁting the local population through its outcomes.

Hopeﬁeld wind farm
The Hopeﬁeld wind farm is a large scale 66.6 MW wind farm comprised of 37 Vestas
V100 wind turbines of 1.8 MW . It generates a yearly average 176600 MWh of renew-
able electricity that is fed to the national electricity grid . This amount is estimated to
cover the equivalent demand of approximately 49 000 low-income households (using 3
600kWh/year), or 22 000 medium-income South African homes (using 7 800 kWh/year)
. However, despite the great results in terms of adequacy, the big scale of the wind farm
has great associated land requirements (900 hectares), and restricts the proximity and
accessibility of the wind farm to the point of consumption . These impacts are not only
inﬂuenced by the scale of the project and installed capacity, but also by the utilization
of the wind resource, which requires speciﬁc wind intensive location to maximize its
output and efﬁciency (Umoya Energy, 2018b).
The local community beneﬁts from the performance of the wind farm through Umoya
Energy, the company created to implement and operate the Hopeﬁeld wind farm. Umoya
energy is owned by Old Mutual, various funds managed by AIIM, Tomlo Commodities
Pty Ltd, and the Local Community Company (Umoya Energy, 2018b), being the Local
Community Company a non-proﬁt company (Old Mutual, 2015) stablished to rein-
vest in development programmes for the communities surrounding the wind farm,
and which owns 5% of Umoya Energy’s wind farm (Old Mutual, 2015). The Hope-
ﬁeld wind farm is ﬁnanced by private investment and registered as an IPP, and it has
an agreement on a 20-year power purchase agreement (PPA) with Eskom (The World
Bank, 2018). Through the partial ownership of Umoya Energy, the representative of the
local community has decision making power over the project, and the payment guar-
antees allow ﬁnancing of multiple programmes that have resulted into employment
opportunities, improvements in education and environmental impact, and household
upgrades (Umoya Energy, 2018a).
The Hopeﬁeld Home Improvement Project (HHIP), is one of these implemented
programmes that provides individual Solar Water Heaters (SWH), insulation of the house-
holds’ ceilings, and electrical wiring to the households in the community . As a result
of this programme 590 homes have been provided with the whole equipment and up-
grades (and 360 more are planned) improving the energy efﬁciency of the housheolds.

to 18 unemployed men in the community (Umoya Energy, 2018a).
An education programme was also implemented and has provided funding to pay 3

Kuyasa CDM
Kuyasa CDM’s positive impacts are very closely related to its decisions regarding ﬁ-
nance and technology, and the good management of the available opportunities and
partnerships. Kuyasa is the ﬁrst Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) developed in
Africa (Atmosfair, 2017), which allowed to account for the environmental impact of the
project, and to reduce the costs through the generation of approximately 6580 CERs a
year and the associated revenues (of approximately R1 million or 75,567 US dollars a
year) from their sales (UNFCCC, 2018).
The project was ﬁnanced by a range of different institutions that worked in part-
nership with the NGO South South North: the City of Cape Town, the Department
of Environmental Affairs (DEAT) , the Department of Housing and Local Governance,
the Southern Africa Enterprise Development Fund (SAEDF), and the community of
Kuyasa (Goldman, 2010). The greater part of the investment was provided by the DEAT
by including the project under the Expanded Public Works Programe (EPWP) which
aimed to improve employment opportunities in the sector. According to this pro-
gramme 30% of the budget was allocated to the creation of job opportunities (Gold-
man, 2010). In addition, the SAEDF compromised to provide the rest of the needed
funding, which would be later recovered from the monetary collections from residents
and the sale Carbon Emission Reduction certiﬁcated (CERs) (Goldman, 2010). These
partnerships and ﬁnancial support of the project enabled to provide complete instal-
lation of a rooftop Solar Water Heater (SWH), energy efﬁcient lighting and ceiling in-
sulation to 1800 RDP households in Kuyasa (Goldman, 2010). However the project was
also committed to provide speciﬁc minimum outcomes in terms of local employment
and CO2 emissions reduction.

People’s Housing Process
The (e)PHP is a process approved and implemented by the government of South Africa,
which transfers the responsibility of the support of the programme to the provinces
and municipalities. In these regards, the partnership between communities and gov-
ernmental institutions is strong during the whole process. The province and munic-
ipality provide subsidies related to the housing project and other resources such as
building materials, while the beneﬁciaries, together with a Housing Support Organiza-
tion (HSO), contribute with their efforts to the building process ("sweat equity") (West-
ern Cape Government, 2017; Newton, 2013). As a result the houses built through the
(e)PHP comply with all the legal requirements and are fully formalized, and the beneﬁ-
ciaries of the programme become the owners. However, the requirement of recipients
being the ones building their house make the process signiﬁcantly inefﬁcient and time
Page 90
==PAGE:103==

Master’s Thesis
and money consuming. This is also enhanced by the exceess bureaucratic procedures
and difﬁculty of the process which is strongly standardized and regulated (Landman
and Napier, 2010).
One of the advantages of the (e)PHP is its ﬂexibility, as it is applicable to different
types of buildings and projects, such as hostel redevelopments or township upgrad-
ing (Newton, 2013). In terms of adequacy, the houses built are generally larger than
the RDP houses and designed in greater accordance to the households’s needs and
preferences of its inhabitants . This is found to be strongly associated to beneﬁcia-
ries’ given opportunity to "exercise control over the process" of designing and building
their house (Landman and Napier, 2010). The allowed variability in the use of materi-
als, sizes, locations, etc. also facilitates the personalization of the houses.
However, examples in some communities (including Netreg (Newton, 2013)), show
that in reality, the available options and types of housing that are ﬁnally constructed is
limited. This is specially due to the costs of the process, that constitute a constraining
factor.
Among the support provided by the HSOs and municipalities, the transfer of skills
regarding housing and construction is included (Western Cape Government, 2017).
SolarTurtle
SolarTurtle focuses on the delivery of a technology, which design has been carefully
thought of, to maximize security and adaptability. The choice of a shipping container
as the supporting structure of the SolarTurtle facilitates its transport and makes it an
adaptable solution that can be easily placed close to the point of use. In addition, it
constitutes a simple, secure and also robust solution. The PV panels are located on
the top of the container and can be quickly and automatically folded, ensuring that
the technology is secured inside the contained while it is not being used (SolarTurtle,
2018; Shabbir, 2018; Jackson, 2017).
Inside the container, a battery charging station is built. The available space limits
the power generation and storage capacity of the SolarTurtle, being 4 KW its typicall PV
capacity. This provides enough electricity for activities such as e-learning, lighting and
internet (Nkuna, 2018), but not to cover the whole energy demand of the households
in the community.
The SolarTurtle is built so its interior can be utilized for business purposes, being
able to convert into small clinics, ofﬁces, shops, etc. By providing a safe working space,
it improves the business opportunities in the targeted communities (SolarTurtle, 2018;

==PAGE:104==

Jackson, 2017; Nkuna, 2018). The company also advocates for an active support of
women in rural communities and facilitate them to work in the operation of the charg-
ing stations (Shabbir, 2018), however the number of workers needed per facility is sig-
niﬁcantly low (2-3 people). The design and construction of the SolarTurtle itself does
not provide employment opportunities in the community as the facilities are already
designed and built off-site, and driven in trucks to their ﬁnal location (Shabbir, 2018;
Jackson, 2017).
This sudden introduction of a predesigned and built technology could be connected
to the initial low mobilization experienced in the ﬁrst stages of the project, when it was

diversify and give continuation to its revenue streams (Jackson, 2017). This is possible
due to the adaptability of the charging station, that can be oriented towards different
purposes by installing different appliances inside the container.

Active Boxes (VPUU)
VPUU, advocates for a situational and social approach to crime prevention. Accord-

7.2
Governance, actors and decision making
Tenure and ownership are key issues in Khayelitsha’s informal settlements, in which
the big majority of the households do not comply with the legal requirements, and the
formalization of services, businesses and infrastructure constitutes a challenge.
The chosen ownership structures vary signiﬁcantly accross the seven analyzed projects,
which are generally implemented by non-proﬁt companies created for the project,
public institutions (municipalities, provinces,..), research centers, or private compa-
nies. However, in none of the studied cases, there is a direct ownership of community
members over the projects or implementing institutions.
The only case, trying to give ownership over the project to the community is Hope-
ﬁeld wind farm. Umoya Energy is partly owned for the Local Community Company
(HWFLCC) mentioned in the previous chapter. This HWFLCC is a non-proﬁt company
that aims to represent the local communities and that redirects all its revenues to in-
vest in development programmes for the local population.
Despite this lack of ownership over the projects themselves, some of the services are
based on the provision of equipment or infrastructure. This leads to different own-
ership structures over the equipment, facilities and infrastructure delivered. In some
cases the ownership is achieved by buying the equipment (SolarTurtle), by contribut-
ing to the partial covering of the costs of the equipment (Kuyasa and iShack), or by ac-
tively participating in the implementation, installation and development of the project
(’sweat’ cost in PHP). In the case of Hopeﬁeld Home Improvement Programme (HHIP) ,
the equipment was provided without incurring costs to the beneﬁciaries. However, the
experiences of these projects showed that the monetary contribution of beneﬁciaries

aesthetics in Khayelitsha itself, where the use of the facilities was encouraged and the
feeling of safety was increased by applying a carefull design of the facilities.
Mobility constitutes a challenge in Khayelitsha, not only due to the lack of adequate
access to transport but also due to the violence and crime in the area, long distances
and unsafe spaces. Acknowledging this as a problem, the Active Boxes were carefully
designed to improve the mobility in the area and facilitate the access to the facilities, by
placing them along major pedestrian routes, ﬁlling unsafe empty spaces and providing
them with good lighting. The good results achieved, suggest that measures addressing
good access and mobility worth being considered when designing an energy service in
the area.
Crime does not only affect the mobility and access to the services but also the in-
frastructure and equipment itself. Mentioned in most of the conducted interviews and
studied projects, the high rates of theft of equipment, constitutes one of the ﬁrst causes
of project failure. Addressing this problem, SolarTurtle focuses a lot of attention in the
design and security of the battery charging stations, to avoid crime and prevent the
equipment from being broken or stolen.
These security measures are recommendable for the case of Khayelitha, where crime
and robbery rates are signiﬁcantly higher than in the rest of the country and other rural
areas.
Other concerning aspects about the delivery of equipment are its associated safety
risks (exposure to bad wiring and cable connections,to boiling water from geysers,
etc.). These risks are enhanced in low-income and self-built informal houses in which
there is no supervision of the safety requirements. Low income informal settlements in
Khayelitsha are areas succeptible to suffer these problems identiﬁed in Kuyasa’s RDP
houses. Therefore it is recommended that the provision of appliances, equipment and
services is accompaigned with the compeeting safety measures to avoid newly intro-
duced risks.

7.4
Finance
Financing development projects can constitute a challenge, specially when the aim
is to target the greater number of people possible and to maximize the beneﬁts for
the community. Most of the projects found addressing energy access in South Africa,
are ﬁnanced by external investors outside the community, being any of the projects
analyzed in this study fully ﬁnanced by the local beneﬁciaries.
Therefore the partnerships with external institutions and investors (as mentioned
above) play a key role in the ﬁnancing of these projects. Due to the challenges to gather
capital normallya ssociated to low-income settlements lacking access to electricity, the
ﬁnancing of the projects is ususally done through a non-proﬁt representative company
that gathers the funds around and speciﬁc project (VPUU, iShack, SolarTurtle, Kuyasa
CDM, Hopeﬁeld Wind Farm Local Community Company, etc.)
External investors are usually public institutions such as municipalities, governments
and research centers, or private investors receiving very low proﬁt margings.
In the case of informal settlements in Khayelitsha, communities and their members
are not eligible for subsudies and support schemes, so the presence of intermediary
project companies or NGOs is necessary to receive public funding and more likely
private funding too (due to the high investment risk). An example of the beneﬁts of
intermediary community companies is the ﬁnancing method applied in Enkanini by
iShack that reveices the Free Basic Electricity subsidy of all the community members
to ﬁnance the common project.
iShack advocates for the willingness of population in low-income informal settlemetns
to pay for upgrades. Accordingly it applied a loan based ﬁnancing scheme in Philippi to
prove the ﬁnancial sustainability of the project and demonstrate the municipality the
high value of the project showing great results. SolarTurtle’s installations are ﬁnanced
on a pay-as you go basis with contributions of the users, who pay each time they utilize
the service. The willingness of population in Khayelitsha’s informal settlements to in-
vest in livelihood and household improvements is identiﬁed in the extense presence of
informal business, illegal electricity connections and extended cords, rental of houses,
etc. and their use and success.
However, the low income of most of the population and the high reliance in credit
(e.g. in spaza shops), challenges the collection of the payments and the ﬁnancing sys-
tems, that need to be ﬂexible and strong enough to allow delays in payments and other
issues.

Discussion and Recommendations
In this section, a review and discussion of the research process and its key results is
provided. It is followed by a series of suggestions for further research, and recommen-
dations for political consideration.
8.1
Scope and methods
The scope of the project limited the study to the particular case of informal settle-
ments in Khayelitsha, South Africa. Additionally, it restricted the energy service alter-
natives studied to community-based energy projects, which should focus on the provi-
sion of electricity generated through renewable technologies. These choices provided
a narrowed framework that allowed a detailed investigation of the context in Khayelit-
sha and the key aspects involved in the development of community energy projects.
The scope aligned with the global energy targets in their goal to reduce environmen-
tal impacts by implementing renewable technologies. However, these decisions were
made for practical reasons to focus the study, and not with the aim of prioritizing tech-
nologies nor overlooking the potential of clean fuels and other non-renewable energy
sources to improve energy access in the area.
Page 111
==PAGE:124==

Blanca Martinez Nieto
The methods applied in this project were designed to facilitate a result oriented eval-
uation of the community energy model, based on the human development needs in
Khayelitsha.
The use of a case study enabled to investigate a speciﬁc context. Consequently and
according to Yin (2005), the results of this analysis are only valid for the speciﬁc case of
Khayelitsha, and cannot be directly extrapolated to other areas. However, the adopted
approach and methodology can be applied to obtain comparable results in other con-
texts.
Seven community-based existing projects addressing development in South Africa
were chosen as secondary cases of study to evaluate the potential of community-based
energy projects based on the real experiences of projects and not uniquely on theoreti-
cal assumptions. Projects from different sectors, and implemented in different areas of
South Africa were selected. This added difﬁculty to their comparison and application
of lessons to the case of energy services in Khayelitsha, however, it also enabled the
investigation and learning from other sectors.
These projects were analyzed according to the CRE Development Framework, pro-
posed by Hicks and Ison (2018) (Chapter 5). Accordingly, the interactions between their
project decisions and practices, and their results, were investigated. The complexity of
these interactions was not analyzed in this study, however, the impossibility of assum-
ing a direct causality between actions and results was acknowledged. For this reason,
only the relationships mentioned as such in the reviewed sources of information were
considered, while the rest of the results were assumed to derive from the joint applica-
tion of all the project activities.
The application of these methods led to the learning outcomes discussed in the fol-
lowing section.

8.3.1
Information about informal settlements
The lack of reliable information about informal settlements, their number of house-
holds, spatial limits, and inhabitants, challenge the design and implementation of en-
ergy services in these areas. The uncertainty in the population numbers increase the
risk of leaving part of the population out of the participatory processes of services
themselves. In addition, it difﬁcults the operation of the services. Examples of these
associated difﬁculties are the collection of moneraty contributions to pay the provided
SWHs in Kuyasa CDM (Goldman, 2010), or the loss of SHSs claimed by iShack due to
inhabitants moving of buy their households.
The absence of transparency in the information about informal settlements also af-
fects the research and the investigation conducted in this project. The limited avail-
ability of data and surveys, and its consequent missrepresentation of a big part of the
population in Khayelitsha, may have led to erroneous conclusions and results.

Legal framework
The current energy and housign policies have a signiﬁcant limiting effect on the ap-
plicable solutions and energy services in Khayelitsha, and restrict the technicalgover-
nance, and ﬁnancial structures and characteristics of the service.
As a result, despite the multiple available options to generate and distribute electric-
ity (on-grid, off-grid), the strong land and tenure requirements leave informal settle-

existing programmes.
However, recently more political attention is being placed to informal settlements in
South Africa. The move from a ”relocations” approach to the hpgrading of settlements,
to an in-situ upgrading policy allowed the emergence of new services such as iShack.
In addition, the City if Cape Town’s efforts to include these areas in the urban planing
are taking effect though programmes, subsidies, and partnerships.
Small changes in policies and the legal framework can open or close many opportu-
nities and alternatives to implement energy services to improve energy access. There-
fore, the high link of the conclusions obtained in this project to the temporal context
and current legal situation.

8.4
Recommendations
In this section a series of recommendations for consideration by national or local gov-
ernments are presented. These recommendations are based on the observations gath-
ered through the whole analysis presented in this report, experiences from the studied
projects, and insights from the conducted interviews.
Increase security of tenure
The lack of tenure constraint social and economical activities in informal settlements
by affecting their opportunities to access ﬁnance, basic services, jobs, etc. It is there-
fore needed to reduce the land tenure requirements to allow settlements’ households
to be connected to the electricity grid or to be eligible to other energy services and
subsidies. Experiences in Thailand showed the good results of providing temporary
"quasi-household IDs" that allow inhabitants to apply for grid connections without
legally owning the land (Singh et al., 2015).
The progressive securing of tenure in informal settlements, is also a requirement
for the in-situ upgrading of the settlements. This approach, currently prioritized in
South Africa, is expected to culminate with the acquisition and legal ownership of
land (NUSP, 2015b). However, this is not always a possibility. Accordingly, it is rec-
ommended to integrate this tenure requirements’ "relaxation", not only in the energy
policy but also in housing and development ones.
Provision of interim services
When applying for household electriﬁcation, upgrading, relocation, etc. the excesive
bureaucrazy and governmental inefﬁciencies normally result in long processes and
lead to waiting periods that can last for years (Tait, 2017a). During these periods, set-
tlement’s inhabitats lack access to the awaited services. Consequently, the provision
of interim services is recommended. Experiences of initiatives facilitating interim en-
ergy services in informal settlements while waiting to be connected to the electricity
grid have shown great beneﬁts in the population (e.g. iShack and SolarTurtle). These
services provide a basis to alleviate the major problems associated to the lack of per-
manent services, that can be subsequently upgraded.

Multidimensional approach to energy access
The deﬁnition of energy access as a multidymensional challenge that involves differ-
ent sectors and livelihood aspects should be adopted by governments in order to guide
policies and integrated programmes. The delivery of energy services can serve as an
opportunity to improve additional livelihood aspects by focusing, not only in their
energy-related characteristics, but also in the non-energy related ones, such as safety,
housing conditions, location, easthetics, etc. In accordance with this approach, a joint
action among sectors to improve living conditions in informal settlements is recom-
mended, which requires of a good coordination among institutions and policy makers.
Partnerships and institutional coordination
In relation to the previous statement, the coordination among institutions and the
partnerships between communities, private, and public institutions, are among the
key recommendations. Specially when addressing development and targeting low-
income and high risk areas, the support of governmental institutions is indispensable
to achieve a legal and ﬁnancially sustainable project.
As previously mentioned, the strengthening of the relationships and collaborations
among different sectors (e.g. housing, energy and urban planning), is required. In ad-
dition, coordination between national, provintial and municipal governments, which
is one of the greatest problems, should be strengthen.
Support of alternative energy services
Lastly, the political advocacy and support of a greater variety of services to improve
energy access is recommended. The current focus of South Africa’s policy addressing
energy access, is almost exclusively put in a single technology and delivery method
(grid connection). Despite its overall successful results,this method has failed to in-
clude and address the contextual characteristics and barriers of informal settlements.
Therefore a non-technology prescriptive approach, based on the diversiﬁcation of ser-
vices and opportunities is recommended.

Conclusions
This project investigated the capability of community renewable energy (CRE) projects
to improve the access to electricity in peri-urban informal settlements. The study fo-
cused on informal settlements in Khayelitsha, a township situated outside Cape Town,
in South Africa. Additionally, a "people-centered" deﬁnition of energy access and ap-
proach to the provision of modern energy services was proposed.
The CRE model was tested through the analysis of seven community-based projects
existing in South Africa, which included the evaluation of their results and experiences.
As a result, a series of applicable practices in terms of technology, governance, ﬁnance
and community engagement were identiﬁed for the case of Khayelitsha. This process
gathered knowledge about the available options to design energy services and led to
the proposition of a series of recommendations.
The biggest challenges and areas of concern identiﬁed among the population in
Khayelitsha were housing, crime, unemployment, basic services, transport, education,
health, and poverty. With the aim of solving these speciﬁc livelihood problems, a set
of energy services’ requirements to provide adequate access to electricity in Khayelit-
sha was proposed. These characteristics were utilized to develop a set of indicators,
which included both the energy and non-energy related characteristics that should
be investigated when planing energy services in Khayelitsha: adaptability, cost, ade-
Page 123
==PAGE:136==

quacy, proximity, tenure, community impact, health impact and environmental im-
pact. These indicators did not aim to measure energy access as such, but to highlight
the important aspects to address when planning energy services in Khayelitsha. Ac-
cordingly, they served as a framework to evaluate the results of the seven selected ex-
isting community-based projects.
From the analysis, a series of applicable and recommended practices observed in ex-
isting community-based projects in South Africa, was proposed for the case of Khayelit-
sha. As a result, CRE contemplated a wide range of ﬁanance, governance, commu-
nity engagement and technology opportunities. Accordingly, it proved to be a ﬂexi-
ble framework that allowed to adapt energy services to the contextual characteristics
of the place where they were implemented. In addition, the focus of this model on
the local population and their engagement in the services consituted a key particular
beneﬁt of this model. Limitations of the conducted research and the applicability of
its conclusions were identiﬁed, leading to a series of recommendations for further re-
search and political action. The importance of addressing security of tenure and the
provision of interim services was highlighted, as well as the need of adopting a multi-
dymensional approach to energy access and supporting diverse alternatives of energy
services. Additionally, the partnerships and coordination among governments and in-
stitutions were identiﬁed as key drivers towards the achievement of universal energy
access.
As a conclusion, this project aimed to contribute to the existing research with a new
methodological approach to the deﬁnition of energy access, that sought to include the
speciﬁc contextual characteristics of the areas. This methodology served to evaluate
the CRE model with the aim of building on knowledge about existing electricity ser-
vice alternatives to the grid expansion, and to identify both problematic points and
opportunities to improve energy access in informal settlements in Khayelithsa. The
main value of this study for academics and researchers resides in the applicability of
the proposed methodologies to different contexts, to build knowledge about available
solutions to improve energy access.

This appendix summarizes the key policies and programmes addressing informal set-

Constitution of South Africa (1996)
The Constitution, approved in 1996, is the "supreme law of the Republic" (Constitu-
tional Assembly South Africa, 1996) and therefore it inﬂuences all the national policies.
It recognizes the access to adequate housing as a basic right (Section 26) of all South
Africans, and the responsability of the government to support the realization of this
right through legislative measures (Constitutional Assembly South Africa, 1996). Ac-
cording to these statements, the government is not allowed to ignore the existence of
informal settlements and compromises to provide laws, programmes and resources
to ensure that every inhabitant has access to adequate housing in the shorter time as
possible.

Housing policy
The Constitution is supported by the Housing Act, that was enacted in 1997. It con-
templates the principles to be applied in housing development and acknowledges the
need of prioritizing poor population’s needs among the rest’s, the need of affordable
and sustainable housing, and the need of diverse household ’tenure options’ (NUSP,
2015a) .
The Housing act also recognizes housing as a "product of human endeavor" , and
the government’s duty to support this and "upgrade where possible and build on what
people have already developed"(NUSP, 2015a).
In 1998 the Prevention of Illegal Evictions Act (PIE) was approved. It endorses the
fact that ’any person may be evicted from their home, or have their home demolished,
without and order of court made after considering all the relevant circumstances’ hav-
ing as an ultimate target to protect occupiers, and to avoid all "arbitrary evictions"
(NUSP, 2015a).
The Breaking New Ground Policy (BNG) became in 2004 the ﬁrst policy expressly
recognizing "informal settlements and the need to upgrade them" , contrasting with
the previous policies that focused on the provision of subsidized housing to be formal-
ized and moving from a policy approach based on houses towards the development of
"sustainable neighbourhoods with access to social and economical facilities" (NUSP,
2015a). The policy defends an approach to informal settlement eradication based on
a progressive upgrading of the settlements in situ, combined with the relocation of
households in case of being unable to upgrade or if preferred by the inhabitants.
The National Housing Code (2009) gathers the most relevant aspects, regulations
and rules of the housing policy of the country and the national housing programmes.
In this code, similar objectives to the ones of the BNG are stablished respect to the
informal settlements and their incremental upgrading: to prioritize in situ upgrad-
ing when possible, to recognize dwellers’ rights and ownership, to provide basic engi-
neering services and sustainable and affordable infrastructure, and to empower com-
munties (NUSP, 2015a).
There are several programmes and subsidies supporting these policies. The main
one in charge of meeting these objectives is the Upgrading Informal Settlements Pro-

gramme (UISP).
The UISP was created and presented in 2004 in the BNG with the aim of meeting
the informal settlement upgrading objectives. According to the vision of the upgrad-
ing as a gradual process, the programme is divided in four different phases in which
community participation play an important role and which inlude the provision of
"interim services" in the early stages as a short term solution during the upgrading
process (NUSP, 2015a).
Lastly, the National Development Plan (NDP) was adopted in 2012. Its main objec-
tive is to provide a long-term vision of the development path of the country, the in-
tended results and the steps to follow, identifying the required actions and interactions
of each sector to achieve the goals (South African Government, 2018).
The NDP also expresses the ambitious goal of eliminating income poverty and re-
ducing inequality by 2030 and the objective of upgrading all informal settlements on
suitable, well located land by 2030 (South African Government, 2018). The document,
also recognizes the deﬁcient spatial legacy and inequalities derived from the Apartheid
policies (National Planning Commission, 2010). And accordingly, it recognizes the
transformation of human settlements and the universal access to services as policy
priorities, proposing "a people-centered approach focused on understanding and cre-
ating ingrated neighbourhoods" (NUSP, 2015a).

South Africa’s regulation contemplates different ownership and ﬁnancial schemes,
and different technology approaches to the generation of electricity. Private invest-
ment in generation facilities of all scales (large scale producers or small scale residen-
tial installations) is considered and allowed in the country, however, every different
model and technology is subject to a series of requirements, permits and regulations.
The information presented in this appendix aims to summarize the most relevant
programmes, requirements and conditions to be considered when planning to install
generators of different sizes and characteristics in South Africa and more speciﬁcally
in the municipality of the City of Cape Town.
Generation capacity under the Independent Power Producers Procurement Programme
(IPPPP)
The Independent Power Producers Procurement Programme (IPPPP) was stablished
by the Department of Energy (DoE), National Treasury (NT), and the Development
Page 129
==PAGE:142==

Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA) in order to support and meet the intended IPP ob-
jectives. The IPPPP is in charge of providing advisory, management, monitoring, eval-
uation, and contract management services, and its followed strategies and objectives
are setted according to the generation capacity targets stablished in the Integrated Re-
source Plan (IRP) in 2010. (Independet power Producers South Africa, 2018a).
A series of guidelines to be applied during the IPP bidding programme are developed
and presented in the New Generation Regulations. The need to comply with the IRP,
the requirement of accepting an standardized Power Purchase Agreement (PPA), the
prioritization of plants located in points of the grid in which it minimizes losses and
contributes to grid stabilization, and the preference of plants and technologies that
contribute to the economic development, are some of the most relevant conditions to
consider during the process (Independet power Producers South Africa, 2018a).
The IPPPP includes the Renewable Independent Power Producers Procurement Pro-
gramme (RIPPPP), that aims to boost the investment in renewable energy capacity,
and to meet the objectives stablished in the IRP, while contributing "towards socio-
economic and environmentally sustainable growth"(Independet power Producers South
Africa, 2018b), through a bidding process and in terms of the IPP.
In this case, the potential investors are required to bid on a certain energy tariff ,
and on socio-economic development objectives (Independet power Producers South
Africa, 2018b). The technologies eligible under this branch of the IPPPP are wind (on-
shore and offshore), concentrated solar thermal, solar PV, solid biomass, biogas, land-
ﬁll gas and small hydropower (Independet power Producers South Africa, 2018b).
All the speciﬁc requirements for each bidding process can be found in the Request
for Proposals (RFP) that is released by the Department of Energy (DoE), and contains
all relevant information, dates, and the participation and qualiﬁcation criteria before
the process starts (Independet power Producers South Africa, 2018b). In addition, all
the bids collected are evaluated in two different stages: a ﬁrst one in which the capac-
ity of each bidder to meet the requirements is assessed according to a series of qual-
iﬁcation criteria considering technical, environmental, legal, ﬁnancial and economic
factors; and a second stage in which the different bids are compared per technology
and according to the tariff and the socio-economic development impacts proposed
(Independet power Producers South Africa, 2018b).

Legal requirements of Small-scale Embedded Generation (SSEG)
SSEG refers to the small generating installations of maximum 1 MVA of apparent power,
that are "embedded" or connected to the municipal electricity grid.
While off-grid generators, or generators that are completely disconnected from the
grid, do not need any approval from the Director of the Electricity Generation and Dis-
tribution Department, they are required to submit a "Declaration for Off-grid Embed-
ded Generation form" with the detailed information about the project in order to prove
evidence of the total disconnection of the installation from the grid City of Cape Town
(2017).
However, in case of being connected to the electricity grid, installations need to com-
pile with a series of extra requirements in order to be approved.
The municipality of the City of Cape Town does not consider as an obligation to
purchase a yearly average amount of electricity higher than the amount that it sells to
the same installation, and therefore "net generators" (that generate in excess and feed
to the grid more on average than they consume) are not allowed to be connected in the
City’s distribution grid (City of Cape Town, 2017).
The transmission of electricity to different locations to the one in which it is gener-
ated is not allowed, and therefore the power produced by the SSEG must be consumed
at the point where it is generated or fed into the national grid.
In addition, in order to feed the electricity to the municipal grid, there is a list of in-
verters approved that meet the municipality’s requirements. It is allowed to transfer the
ownership of the SSEG or property in which it is installed, however, it is required that
the new owner sign a supplemental Contract to avoid the SSEG to be decommissioned
City of Cape Town (2017).
Once complying with all the conditions, SSEGs can choose between two different
ways to connect to the grid: reverse power ﬂow, and applying SSEG tariff.
In the case of wanting to be connected to the grid without being compensated for the
electricity produced and withdrawn to the grid, depending on the amount and capacity
of electricity fed into the grid, different metering requirements are be applied. While if
the SSEG’s owner wishes to beneﬁt from the tariff applied to the electricity sold to the
grid, a bi-directional credit meter should be installed. The tariffs are only applicable
for "net consumers", are annually deﬁned by the municipality, and are composed of a
ﬁxed daily service fee, charges from the electricity consumed (double tariff), and the
payment per excess kWh sold to the gird City of Cape Town (2017).

Medium scale off-grid solutions, mini-grids
NERSA requirement of licensing does not currently apply for off-grid solutions and
demonstration projects. However the exceptions and requirements suffer continuous
changes. In the case of mini-grids ("decentralized generation and distribution systems,
that serve the same purpose of the main grid, and are built to supply a group of end-
users in an unserved location" (Foreign Commonwealth Ofﬁce, 2017)), it is necessary
to register with NERSA even though the license is not required.
The municipality has to provide an operation license to the developer of the mini-
grid or other generator independently of the size, mainly due to safety reasons. And
a distribution license is also required for mini-grid operators, which are mandated to
develop an annual cost of the electricity calculation and to prove that they meet all
the grid regulation and technical and safety standards(Foreign Commonwealth Ofﬁce,
2017).
In addition, there are other regulations that apply to mini-grids depending on the
sizes, technologies or purposes. For example, in the case of delivering a service as an
external provider for the municipality (e.g. energy access in rural areas) , mini-grids
need to comply with the municipal Systems Act and Municipal Finance Management
Act and the Energy Regulation Act. Energy projects above 1 MW also need to comply
with the national land development permitting regulation, and in some cases it is also
necessary to perform Environmental Impact Assessment(Foreign Commonwealth Of-
ﬁce, 2017).
