Abstract
ParameterôÙEfficient FineôÙTuning (PEFT) methods for Large Language Models (LLMs)
have shown great promise but face challenges, particularly when dealing with diverse
data sources for multiple tasks. In these scenarios, current methods tend to either overfit
to certain tasks or underperform in others. Efficiently combining multiple PEFT adapters
to address this issue remains a complex and unresolved problem.
This thesis presents côÙMoLoRA, an advanced ParameterôÙEfficient FineôÙTuning method
for Large Language Models. côÙMoLoRA incorporates Unsupervised Domain Discovery to
adapt LLMs to a diverse range of tasks more efficiently. The method is characterized by
its modular design, allowing for independent training of experts and flexible adjustment of
the routing layer, a notable improvement over conventional PEFT methods.
The key innovation of côÙMoLoRA lies in its ability to handle datasets with significant diôÙ
versity. By segmenting datasets into distinct domains, côÙMoLoRA enables specialized
training of experts, leading to improved performance compared to standard MoLoRA and
LoRA methods. The thesis explores various routing strategies, including linear, attentionôÙ
based, and uniform routing, with the latter showing unexpectedly effective results, highôÙ
lighting potential areas for further exploration in routing efficiency.
Experiments conducted in this study involve two main scenarios: training on homogeôÙ
neous datasets to understand the impact of scaling the number of experts and routing
strategies, and training on heterogeneous datasets to evaluate the efficacy of côÙMoLoRA
against challenges like overfitting in diverse data environments. Results demonstrate that
côÙMoLoRA not only competes favorably with existing PEFT methods but also offers enôÙ
hanced modularity, facilitating swappable model components and efficient parallel trainôÙ
ing.
Keywords:
PEFT, LoRA, LLM, MixtureôÙofôÙExperts, Efficient Machine Learning, MoLoRA,

Introduction
Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPTôÙ4 [1] and Gemini
[2], have revolutionized natural language processing, providing unprecedented capabiliôÙ
ties in text generation, comprehension, and various other tasks. The openôÙsource domain
has seen notable contributions with models like Llama 2 [3] and Mixtral 7B [4], narrowôÙ
ing the gap between open and closedôÙsource LLMs. These models have inspired the
development of specialized models for tasks including Q&A, code generation, and sumôÙ
marization. FineôÙtuning, a process of additional training on preôÙtrained LLMs, is essential
for tailoring these models to specific requirements.
However, fineôÙtuning LLMs poses challenges, primarily due to the data and computaôÙ
tional demands of large preôÙtrained models.
ParameterôÙEfficient FineôÙTuning (PEFT)
techniques address these challenges by adapting LLMs to specific tasks with minimal upôÙ
dates to parameters. PEFT involves integrating adapters ãsmall, trainable componentsã
into preôÙtrained models.
PEFT methods vary, each offering distinct approaches to model adaptation. Despite their
potential, questions remain regarding their effectiveness and the balance between paôÙ
rameter efficiency and performance. This thesis evaluates various PEFT methods across
different tasks to understand their efficacy and the factors affecting their performance. It
will focus on ParameterôÙEfficient MixtureôÙofôÙExpert (PEôÙMoE) methods.
MixtureôÙofôÙExperts (MoE) models [5], comprising multiple expert networks and a trainable
gating mechanism, are at the forefront of efficient computation in deep learning. The gate
dynamically assigns weights to experts based on individual inputs, enabling expert speôÙ
cialization over different input segments. This specialization can enhance both predictive
accuracy and model interpretability.
This work utilizes Llama 2 [6] as the foundational preôÙtrained model and introduces côÙ
MoLoRA, a novel PEFT method. côÙMoLoRA, which builds on MoLoRA [7], incorporates
Unsupervised Domain Discovery inspired by côÙBTM [8]. The frameworkãs primary contriôÙ
butions include:
ãÂ Segmentation of datasets using Unsupervised Domain Discovery
ãÂ Parallel training of experts
ãÂ Modular design allowing the integration of various experts and routing strategies
While côÙMoLoRA is based on MoLoRA, the framework accommodates other ParameterôÙ
Efficient MoE methods such as MoV.

how we communicate with computers, search for information, and learn. These models,

Background
This section provides an overview of the historical developments as well as technical
explanations to understand the rest of the document. In it, we have tried to document the
evolution of the field of Natural Language Processing with a focus on Large Language
Models, highlighting significant advancements and innovations that have contributed to
its present state.
2.1
Origins of Natural Language Processing
Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a field that bridges linguistic communication and
computational methods. Its origins can be traced back approximately 70 years. In 1954,
Georgetown University and IBM successfully built a system that could automatically transôÙ
late more than 60 Russian sentences to English [11]. This system relied on six grammar
rules and 250 lexical items to translate over 60 sentences from Russian to English.
In 1966, the landscape of NLP expanded with the introduction of ELIZA by Joseph WeizenôÙ
baum, a program able to simulate conversation through pattern recognition and preôÙ
programmed responses, the most notable being the DOCTOR script [12]. However, the
optimism of these early developments decreased after the 1966 ALPAC report, which
casted doubts on the potential of machine translation to perform on par with human transôÙ
lators [13].
The resurgence of interest in NLP was evident in the late 1980s with the start of the
Machine Learning (ML) era, marked by increased data availability and computational caôÙ
pacity. This period was characterized by a shift towards statistical methods in machine
translation, especially at IBM with the release of the first statistical machine translation
system [14] [15], which offered a way to mitigate the limitations of ruleôÙbased systems.
The limitations in traditional ML approaches, such as data sparsity and high dimensionalôÙ
ity, led to the exploration of alternative methods. This exploration culminated in the early
2000s with the emergence of Deep Learning, which employs neural networks with mulôÙ
tiple layers to achieve significant improvements in language processing tasks. A pivotal
moment happened in 2006, when Geoffrey Hinton and his colleagues proposed a novel
algorithm for effectively training such networks [16]. This advancement laid the groundôÙ
work for the current generation of NLP applications, which leverage Deep Learning for
language understanding and generation.
2.2
Fundamentals of Language Modeling
Language models are probabilistic frameworks designed to predict the next item in a seôÙ
quence, ranging from the next word in a sentence to the next subword, depending on
the modelãs granularity. They play a crucial role in a wide range of applications, from
simple autoôÙsuggestion of relevant search terms to complex machine translation, speech
recognition, and text summarization.
At their core, language models assign probabilities to sequences of words or tokens,
enabling us to assess the likelihood of a text string. The effectiveness of a language
model lies in its ability to accurately predict unseen text, demonstrating its proficiency in
capturing linguistic patterns, grammatical rules, stylistic nuances, and even contextual
information.

2.3.1
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) emerged as a natural extension of feedforward neural
networks for processing sequences. Unlike their feedforward counterparts, RNNs have
recurrent connections that form a directed cycle, allowing them to maintain a form of memôÙ

predicting subsequent elements in a sequence.
RNNs process sequences one element at a time, with the output from one step feeding
into the next, along with the next element of the sequence. This recursive processing
allows them to develop an understanding of the sequential context, which is essential for
tasks such as language modeling, where the meaning of a word can be heavily influenced
by the words that precede it.

The power of RNNs in language modeling[23] lies in their ability to handle variableôÙlength
sequences and their capacity to learn temporal dependencies. For example, when trained

as vanishing and exploding gradients. To combat these issues, various gated architecôÙ

Nevertheless, the challenge of training deep models persisted, hindered by issues like
vanishing and exploding gradients, suboptimal weight initialization, and internal covariôÙ

Layer Normalization [28], training larger and more complex models has become feasible.
These techniques have not only enabled the training of deeper networks but have also
significantly improved the performance and stability of large language models.

2.5.1
LoRA
A notable technique in this context is LoRA[37], which stands for LowôÙRank Adaptation.
LoRA modifies the architecture of preôÙtrained models by introducing lowôÙrank matrices
that are easier to train and update. This method allows for the adaptation of preôÙtrained
weights without the need to retrain the entire model. It operates by decomposing the
weight matrices in the Transformer layers into lowerôÙrank matrices. During fineôÙtuning,
only these lowerôÙrank matrices are updated, while the original preôÙtrained weights remain
frozen.
The core idea behind LoRA is that small, targeted updates to the modelãs weights can
lead to significant changes in the modelãs behavior. By updating only the lowôÙrank maôÙ
trices, the model retains the benefits of the large preôÙtrained model, such as its general
language understanding, while also gaining the ability to adapt to specific tasks. LoRAãs
effectiveness lies in its balance between preserving the original modelãs capabilities and
allowing for sufficient flexibility to adapt to new tasks. This balance is achieved by careôÙ
fully selecting the rank of the matrices to be updated: too low a rank might not capture
enough complexity for the adaptation, while too high a rank could lead to overfitting or
inefficient training.

This section presents some of the latest and most relevant research in ParameterôÙEfficient

LoRA: LowôÙRank Adaptation of Large Language Models [37]
The paper introduces LowôÙRank Adaptation (LoRA), a technique designed to adapt preôÙ
trained large language models (LLMs) with minimal computational overhead. Recognizing
the challenges associated with fineôÙtuning such models due to their enormous size and
parameter count, the authors propose a method that adjusts the preôÙexisting parameters
through lowôÙrank updates, as opposed to full reôÙtraining or adapting all parameters.
The core idea of LoRA is to introduce trainable lowôÙrank matrices that are used to update
the weights of the attention and feedôÙforward layers of transformer models. By only opôÙ
timizing these lowôÙrank matrices, the model can use the representations learned during
preôÙtraining while significantly reducing the number of trainable parameters (less than 1%
in most cases of the original parameters) during the fineôÙtuning phase.
Experimental results demonstrate that LoRA can achieve comparable or even superior
performance to full fineôÙtuning on a range of NLP tasks, including GLUE, a collection
of sentenceôÙlevel and singleôÙsentence classification tasks. This approach enables the
adaptation of LLMs even in resourceôÙconstrained environments.
The authors argue that LoRA not only preserves the generalizability of the original large
model but also offers a more parameterôÙefficient way to specialize the model for particular
tasks. Furthermore, because LoRA only modifies a small fraction of the modelãs weights,
it also presents potential advantages in terms of model robustness and stability.
Switch Transformers: Scaling to Trillion Parameter Models with Simple
and Efficient Sparsity [48]
The paper presents Switch Transformers, a novel architecture that enables the scaling
of transformer models to over a trillion parameters. The authors address the significant
computational and memory challenges associated with training such large models by inôÙ
troducing a sparselyôÙgated mixtureôÙofôÙexperts layer (MoE), which allows for conditional
computation.
Switch Transformers deviate from the standard practice of increasing model width or depth
uniformly. Instead, they integrate the MoE approach, which activates only a subset of
model parameters for each input token. This method provides a pathway to increase
model capacity without a proportional increase in computation during training.
The architecture employs a routing algorithm to determine which expert in the MoE layer
should be engaged for a given input to balance the computational load across different
experts and ensuring the modelãs efficiency. Results show that Switch Transformers can
be trained on various language tasks and achieve improved performance over smaller

routing strategies that may lead to under or overôÙspecialization of certain experts because
previous methods allocate a fixed number of experts to each token using a topôÙk function,
without considering the varying importance of different tokens. This approach can result
in poor training efficiency and an imbalance in the utilization of experts.
To address these challenges, the paper introduces a novel routing method employing a
heterogeneous mixtureôÙofôÙexperts with an ãexpert choiceã approach. This method deviôÙ
ates from traditional models by having experts select the topôÙk tokens, rather than tokens
selecting the topôÙk experts. This shift allows each token to be routed to a variable number
of experts, while each expert maintains a fixed bucket size.
The proposed approach demonstrates over 2x faster training convergence compared to
stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart methods like Switch Transformer [48], using the same computational reôÙ
sources. Additionally, it shows enhanced performance in fineôÙtuning tasks on the GLUE
and SuperGLUE benchmarks.
AdaMix: MixtureôÙofôÙAdaptations for ParameterôÙefficient Model Tuning [59]
The paper introduces AdaMix, a general PEFT method that tunes a mixture of adaptation
modules within each Transformer layer of the model while keeping most of the model
weights frozen. AdaMix can leverage various PEFT techniques, such as a mixture of
adapters like [36] or a mixture of lowôÙrank decomposition matrices like LoRA, to enhance
downstream task performance. The key innovation in AdaMix is its ability to match the
computational cost and the number of tunable parameters of the underlying PEFT method,
while only tuning 0.1 ôÙ 0.2% of the model parameters.
AdaMix outperforms stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart PEFT and full model fineôÙtuning methods for both
natural language understanding and natural language generation tasks, by a considerôÙ
able margin. The method achieves this superior performance with a minimal increase
in the number of tunable parameters, demonstrating a more efficient use of resources.
Furthermore, AdaMixãs flexibility in integrating different types of PEFT methods makes it
a versatile tool for improving task performance across a wide range of applications.
Pushing Mixture of Experts to the Limit: Extremely Parameter Efficient
MoE for Instruction Tuning [7]
The paper presents an innovative parameterôÙefficient mixture of experts (MoE) architecôÙ
ture. This new approach trains a collection of PEFT adapters and a routing layer endôÙtoôÙ
end, and demonstrates its capability to surpass standard PEFT methods.
The authors concentrate on two methods: Mixture of Vectors (MoV) using (IA)3 adapters[40],
and Mixture of LowôÙRank Adaptations (MoLoRA). These methods are specifically deôÙ
signed to tackle scalability issues in instructionôÙtuned LLMs. Empirical results show that
by updating less than 1% of model parameters, the resulting model improves median
accuracy across various unseen tasks.
The paperãs findings include compelling evidence that soft merging is more effective than
discrete routing, particularly in the MoVôÙ10 configuration. Furthermore, it is observed
that experts developed distinct skills, influencing task performance in line with the taskãs
characteristics.

The study concludes that MoV and MoLoRA offer significant advantages for instruction

SiRA: Sparse Mixture of Low Rank Adaptation [60]
This paper introduces SiRA (Sparse Mixture of Low Rank Adaptation). SiRA enhances
the performance of LoRA by leveraging the Sparse Mixture of Experts (SMoE) approach.
It incorporates a method that enforces top k experts routing with a capacity limit, which
restricts the maximum number of tokens that each expert can process. Additionally, SiRA
introduces a simple yet effective expert dropout mechanism on top of the gating network
to mitigate overfitting issues. This approach aims to enhance computation efficiency while
maintaining or improving model performance.
SiRA represents a significant advancement in the field of parameterôÙefficient tuning for
large language models. By integrating sparse computation with lowôÙrank adaptation, SiRA
demonstrates superior performance compared to other PEFT methods. SiRA outperforms
both LoRA and other mixture of expert approaches such as MoLoRA in a variety of singleôÙ
task and multitask settings. This improvement marks a noteworthy contribution to the
development of more efficient and effective methods for adapting large language models
to a wide range of tasks.
Summary
This literature review explored recent advancements in ParameterôÙEfficient FineôÙTuning
and MixtureôÙofôÙExperts approaches for LLMs. A key trend in current research is the emôÙ
phasis on efficiency and scalability.
The methods reviewed prioritize minimizing computational overhead while preserving or
improving model performance. Moreover, the concept of sparsity, exemplified by Switch
Transformers, is gaining importance. Activating only a subset of model parameters for
each input, this approach holds promise for scaling model capacity without a proportional
increase in computational demands. However, it also adds a layer of complexity, necesôÙ
sitating new approaches for efficient routing and token processing.
The application of PEFT methods in MoE architectures is also evolving. MoLoRA and
MoV utilize a dense approach, while SiRA employs a sparse strategy. Current literature
primarily focuses on training MoE systems endôÙtoôÙend. Independent training of experts
and how to integrate them is still an open question.
In our work, we explore various routing mechanisms within the dense PEFT MoE frameôÙ
work, focusing on the independent training of experts. This exploration aims to contribute
to the understanding and enhancement of MoE systems in the context of LLMs.

5.1.1
Linear Layer Routing
Linear layer routing is a basic form of routing in MixtureôÙofôÙExperts models. This routing
layer takes the input dimension of the model and outputs a set of weights corresponding to
each expert. Unlike other routing mechanisms, it does not use nonôÙlinear activation funcôÙ
tions. We also experimented with dropout, although this approach yielded no significant
impact.
5.1.2
TopôÙK and TopôÙP Routing
TopôÙK and TopôÙP routing mechanisms extend upon the linear layer routing approach.
TopôÙK Routing
In TopôÙK routing, the model computes routing scores and selects the top K experts based
on these scores. Scores are normalized using a softmax function, ensuring a probabilistic
interpretation.
TopôÙP Routing
TopôÙP routing, analogous to TopôÙK, selects experts whose cumulative scores remain beôÙ
low a predefined threshold. This approach allows for a dynamic number of experts based
on the distribution of scores.

Conclusion and Future Work
This thesis has introduced côÙMoLoRA, an innovative ParameterôÙEfficient FineôÙTuning (PEFT)
method, based in MoLoRA and inspired by the côÙBTM framework. The côÙMoLoRA methodôÙ
ology comprises four key steps:
1. Perform Unsupervised Domain Discovery on datasets.
2. Train a Base expert on a data subset.
3. Independently train experts within each identified domain.
4. Integrate these experts and fineôÙtuning the routing layer.
côÙMoLoRAãs modular design enables independent expert training and flexible routing layer
adaptation. This facilitates experimentation with various routing mechanisms without the
need to retrain the entire model, a significant advantage over MoLoRA.
In terms of performance, côÙMoLoRA demonstrates improved results compared to MoLoRA
and LoRA, especially with diverse datasets. Experts trained on distinct, wellôÙsegmented
data offer better outcomes.
Our exploration into scaling the number of experts revealed potential benefits and effiôÙ
ciency gains through topôÙK and topôÙP routing strategies. However, alternative routing
strategies like selfôÙattention did not yield superior results compared to linear or uniform
routing.
In the first experiment series, we evaluated PEôÙMoE and other PEFT methods on a
smaller, homogeneous dataset. This exploration revealed that côÙMoLoRA is competitive,
often outperforming other methods like LoRA and MoLoRA.
The second experiment series, involving a more diverse dataset, demonstrated côÙMoLoRAãs
superior performance when compared to other PEFT methods, emphasizing the effecôÙ
tiveness of its Unsupervised Domain Discovery strategy. This approach allows experts
to specialize in different data segments, leading to enhanced performance.
Key findings of this work include:
ãÂ The results highlight the potential of the côÙMoLoRA approach in enhancing the capaôÙ
bilities of ParameterôÙEfficient MixtureôÙofôÙExperts models. Further research should
explore optimizing côÙMoLoRA configurations for even better performance.
ãÂ Investigating how different experts contribute to the overall performance can provide
deeper insights into the functioning of language models.
ãÂ FineôÙtuning across diverse datasets presents challenges, but côÙMoLoRA offers a
promising solution to address overfitting and underfitting in various domains.
ãÂ The modularity of côÙMoLoRA introduces unique benefits, including customizable
model components and efficient parallel training. By training a base expert and the
router in a subset of the data, the bottleneck during training is given by the domain
containing the largest amount of data, assuming full parallelization of experts.
In summary, côÙMoLoRA stands out as a significant advancement in parameterôÙefficient
fineôÙtuning for large language models, offering substantial improvements over traditional
MoLoRA and LoRA approaches.

7.1
Future Work
This thesis has identified several directions for future research in the fields of Large LanôÙ
guage Models (LLMs) and ParameterôÙEfficient FineôÙTuning (PEFT) methods. Our underôÙ
standing of these modelsã capabilities and limitations is still developing, revealing numerôÙ
ous opportunities for exploration. Key directions for future research include:
Explore Other Routing Strategies
Our experimentation with linear, attention, and uniôÙ
form routing strategies have shown promising results, although they donãt take full advanôÙ
tage of the potential of individual experts. Future research should focus on developing
and improving routing strategies that optimize the utilization of expert knowledge in PEFT
methods.
Investe Uniform Routing
The unexpected effectiveness of Uniform Routing, which asôÙ
signs equal weight to each expert without learning a tokenôÙtoôÙexpert mapping, demands
further investigation. Understanding why this approach works well could shed light on the
impact of routing mechanisms on model outputs.
Optimize the Quantity of Router Layers
In the current côÙMoLoRA model, a routing
layer is introduced at each layer, mirroring the MoLoRA approach but with independently
trained experts. Considering the success of Uniform Routing, future work could explore
whether a single routing layer for the entire model could be sufficient, potentially simplifyôÙ
ing the model while maintaining efficiency.
Explore Router Loss Functions ôÙ ZôÙloss
Balancing expert utilization in MoE models
remains an active research area. Different loss functions, like ZôÙloss[90], have been sugôÙ
gested for routing layers. Investigating these and other loss functions could lead to more
effective expert utilization strategies.
Implement Expert Token Capacity
Zhu et al.[60] introduced the concept of Expert ToôÙ
ken Capacity, which limits the number of tokens processed by each expert to ensure
balanced utilization. Applying this approach to our method could yield new insights and
results.
Extend to côÙMoV
Zadouri et al.[7] introduced MixtureôÙofôÙVectors (MoV) alongside MoLoRA,
where MoV showed better results within the same parameter budget. Investigating how
Unsupervised Domain Discovery and our proposed training framework perform when apôÙ
plied to MoV could provide valuable insights.
Evaluate Balanced KôÙMeans in Clustering
Our method uses embedding representaôÙ
tions for clustering, but the potential benefits of a balanced KôÙMeans algorithm, as used by
Li et al.[8], remain untested. Assessing whether this approach could enhance our modelãs
performance is a question that merits further exploration.
