Abstract
Floating offshore wind is an emerging technology with great potential, but further research is required
to enhance its maturity. This master’s thesis focuses on examining the impact of Floating Offshore Wind
Turbines’ (FOWTs) motion on Annual Energy Production (AEP). To achieve this, a quasi-static motion
model, developed by Ju Feng [1], is integrated with PyWake [2], an open-source Python tool developed
by DTU Wind Energy for wind farm simulations.
The analysis reveals that the motion of FOWTs, particularly the increase in pitch angle resulting from
the motion, leads to a reduction in the FOWF AEP due to a decrease in rotor area. Additionally, when
accounting for shear effects, the AEP decline is further influenced by lower wind speeds experienced at
lower hub heights, attributed to both the higher pitch angle and heave motion. Specifically, a -0.435%
AEP decrease is observed compared to a bottom-fixed wind farm with identical configuration, where
62.5% of the overall reduction can be attributed to the reduced rotor area, while the remaining decline is
associated with shear effects.
However, FOWT motion presents an opportunity to strategically reposition the FOWTs to mitigate
wake effects and enhance overall FOWF performance. Consequently, various studies are conducted to
optimize this parameter for each FOWT, employing both SLSQP and Random Search optimization algo-
rithms. The best mooring system configuration from the carried simulations reported a +0.5% increase
in AEP compared to the base case, underscoring the potential for further exploration of this parameter to
yield significant findings. Additionally, sensitivity analyses are performed on mooring line length, sea
depth, and anchoring position, highlighting higher AEP values when the mooring line is less tensed in
an initial scenario without external forces acting on the FOWT. Nonetheless, further investigations are
necessary to draw conclusive insights from this study approach.
Keywords: Floating offshore wind, Floating Offshore Wind Turbine, FOWT, Annual Energy Produc-
tion, AEP, PyWake, quasi-static motion model, motion effects, surge, sway, heave, pitch angle, wake
effects, mooring system, mooring line configuration angle, anchoring position.

Introduction
The importance of increasing the share of Renewable Energy Sources (RES) extends worldwide, as
the growing energy demand and greenhouse gas emissions require a rapid and sustained deployment
of renewable energy sources. According to data, the energy sector is responsible for more than 75%
of the EU’s greenhouse gas emissions, making it a key sector in the fight against climate change [3].
To achieve the EU’s energy and climate objectives and reach net-zero emissions by 2050, the recast
Renewable Energy Directive established a binding renewable energy target of at least 32% by 2030, with
a proposal to increase this to 45% under the REPowerEU plan [4]. These ambitious targets and measures
are significant steps towards achieving a sustainable and decarbonised global energy system.
Global wind power capacity increased by 77.6 GW in 2022, reaching a total of 906 GW and a 9%
growth compared to 2021. Onshore wind accounted for 68.8 GW of the new capacity, resulting in a
cumulative global onshore capacity of 842 GW with 8.8% year-over-year (YoY) growth. On the other
hand, offshore wind added 8.8 GW in 2022, bringing total global capacity to 64.3 GW, which is 58%
lower than the record year of 2021, but still the second highest growth in history [5].
Despite the lower growth compared to the record year of 2021, annual offshore wind installations are
expected to reach 18 GW in 2023, with a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 32% in the next
five years. China and Europe will contribute to near-term growth, while the US and APAC markets will
start gaining market share from 2025. Furthermore, 130 GW of offshore wind is expected to be added
worldwide in 2023-2027 [5].
The International Energy Agency (IEA) and the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA)
have highlighted that to achieve net-zero by 2050, wind and solar PV energy will need to supply around
70% of electricity generation. Offshore wind will be a critical component of decarbonisation, with the
IEA calling for offshore wind to grow as a share of total wind deployment. The report forecasts that more
than 2,000 GW of offshore wind installed capacity will be needed by 2050 [6].
Water depth represents a primary constraint in the development of offshore wind farms, since the
economic feasibility of bottom-fixed foundations for offshore wind turbines diminishes beyond a water
depth of 50 meters [7]. However, the emergence of floating offshore wind turbines (FOWTs) has enabled
the exploitation of vast wind resources located further away from the shore without this limitation. Prime
locations for floating wind turbines are located off many coastlines, and in general, the wind resource
in these deeper water locations is better than for bottom-fixed offshore wind. FOWTs exhibit enhanced
operational efficiency and higher capacity factors, leading to increased power generation [6].
Furthermore, 80% of the world’s offshore wind resource potential lies in waters deeper than 60m, and
for many countries, their potential for fixed offshore wind is limited [6]. Therefore, supporting countries
whose best option is floating offshore wind is essential for true global growth of this technology.
Although floating wind’s current contribution to total wind installations is only 0.1%, the report fore-
casts that annual installations will surpass 1 GW by 2026, contributing to rapid cost reduction, with full
commercialisation expected by the end of the decade. The report also notes that floating wind can bring
socio-economic benefits, particularly in countries with offshore oil and gas activity and expertise, where
the skills developed in these industries can be transferred to floating wind projects [6].

As the world continues efforts to decarbonise, exploring and investing in floating offshore wind tech-
nology is crucial in achieving sustainable and decarbonised global energy systems. However, govern-
ments must ensure the right policies and regulations are in place to support the early stages of com-
mercialisation, which will require defined floating wind targets and measures to mitigate environmental
impacts.
Floating Wind: An Emerging Technology
The development of floating offshore wind technology has garnered significant participation from
several prominent European oil and gas corporations. These entities bring unrivaled expertise in offshore
engineering and financial strength, leveraging their extensive experience in deep-water operations. With
insights from bottom-fixed offshore wind developers, these companies are well-positioned to transition
floating wind technology from the current demonstration phase to complete commercialization by the
middle of the current decade [6].
The limitations of fixed platforms in deep waters due to resonance phenomena drove the development
of floating platforms, including semi-submersible, Tensioned Leg Platforms (TLP), and spar platforms,
which have played a critical role in facilitating offshore wind turbine viability [ [8], [9] ]. However, due to
its nascent stage, there is insufficient research and experimentation on this technology yet, emphasizing
the need for thorough investigation. This foundation system comprises of a floating platform and anchor
system that is fastened to the seabed:
• The semi-submersible type of floating wind turbine platform is designed with a few large column
tubes that are connected by tubular members. These tubes, partially filled with water, provide
the necessary ballast and buoyancy stability to the wind turbine, whereas mooring lines keep the
semi-submersible platform in position. The wind turbine can be positioned either at the geometric
center of the tubes with support from lateral bracing members or on one of the column tubes. In
the latter case, ballast is added to counteract the weight of the turbine [10]. One advantage of
this design is that the relatively shallow draft allows for flexibility in site selection. The semi-
submersible design also allows for onshore construction and subsequent towing to the offshore
location, providing logistical advantages [ [11], [12] ].
• Spar-buoy systems are slender vertical hulls with a relatively small diameter that are used in off-
shore wind turbines. The upper part of the structure features a reduced diameter compared to the
lower part, as a measure to minimize wave effects [8]. Stability is attained by positioning the center
of gravity below the center of buoyancy, with the addition of ballast at the lower end [13]. These
ballast-stabilized platforms are moored to the seabed with mooring lines, and the turbine installa-
tion needs to be done offshore. Thus, the key challenge of this technology during installation is to
transport the deep-draft platform from the shallow assembly harbor.
• The TLP consists of a central body slightly submerged in water, connected to taut mooring lines,
ensuring platform stability and restricting its motion. Compared to other floating wind turbine
types, such as the spar-buoy or pontoon types, it has a relatively less dynamic response to waves
but it is subjected to a phenomenon called ”pull down”, which increases its draft as the platform
moves from its equilibrium position. This stability is advantageous for wind turbine performance
prediction, but the high tension in cables can lead to mooring-related issues during the installation
phase and resonance cases due to high-frequency dynamic loads [13]. However, the wind turbine

Thesis Outline
The structure of this master thesis is detailed in this section. The thesis is divided into five chapters,
each addressing specific aspects of the research.
The first chapter provides an extensive review of the literature, focusing on the significance of system
engineering and optimization in the design process of wind energy systems. It also delves into the motion
behavior of a FOWT and explores how different configurations can enhance its performance.
In the second chapter, the methodology used in this research is presented. The chapter begins with
a description of the FOWT and the specific site selected for the study. It then outlines the working
environment and simulation setup. The static-motion model employed in the research is explained, and
several case studies are presented. Finally, the chapter describes the path taken to achieve optimization
results and provides a detailed account of the parametric analysis conducted in the subsequent chapter.

Literature Review
The growth in the wind energy industry brings the need for effective and efficient system engineering
to ensure the successful development, deployment, and operation of wind energy systems. System en-
gineering in the wind industry involves the integration of various components and subsystems, such as
wind turbines, control systems, electrical systems, and grid connections, to ensure optimal performance

including the design, testing, and optimization of wind energy systems.
1.1
Wind Energy System Engineering: Design and Optimization
Various definitions exist for systems engineering, but two notable ones are from the National Aero-
nautics and Space Administration (NASA) [15] and the International Council on Systems Engineering
(INCOSE) [16]. NASA defines Systems Engineering as ”a methodical, multi-disciplinary approach for
the design, realization, technical management, operations, and retirement of a system. A “system” is
the combination of elements that function together to produce the capability required to meet a need.
The elements include all hardware, software, equipment, facilities, personnel, processes, and procedures
needed for this purpose. Systems engineering is the art and science of developing an operable system
capable of meeting requirements within often opposed constraints.”
Given the challenges faced by the wind energy industry, it is clear that a multidisciplinary and life
cycle-oriented approach to system design is essential. As described earlier, systems engineering offers a
methodical and integrated approach to designing, realizing, managing, operating, and retiring a system.
With the combination of physical design drivers and diverse stakeholder interests involved in wind en-
ergy systems, a systems engineering approach can optimize wind plant systems to achieve the lowest cost
of energy while maintaining or improving annual energy production (AEP). By incorporating techniques
such as multidisciplinary design analysis and optimization (MDAO) and trade studies, research and de-
velopment efforts can be prioritized, and widely different design concepts can be assessed. The resulting
benefits, including significant cost reductions and long-term operation and maintenance improvements,
make a systems engineering approach an ideal fit for the wind energy industry [17].
Optimization serves the purpose of identifying the optimal solution for a given problem by translat-
ing real-world challenges into precise mathematical equations and variables, encompassing three key
components: an objective function, decision variables, and constraints [ [18], [19] ].
• The objective function, often denoted as f or z, encapsulates the specific quantity that needs to be
either maximized or minimized during the optimization process.
• Decision variables, often represented as x (commonly in vector form), encompass the aspects of the
problem that the decision-maker possesses control over. These variables can include both directly
adjustable parameters and indirectly influenced factors determined by the choice of other decision
variables.
• Constraints play a critical role in optimization by defining the limitations and conditions that re-
strict the feasible range of values for decision variables. It is imperative to incorporate all relevant
constraints, regardless of their perceived obviousness (e.g., the impossibility of a negative toll),

to ensure a comprehensive and accurate representation of the problem. Furthermore, the feasible
region can be defined as the collection of points that satisfy all the given constraints.
In the context of wind energy, optimization’s objective function could involve maximizing the AEP,
minimizing the levelized cost of energy (LCOE), or achieving specific performance metrics such as tur-
bine reliability or grid integration, among others. These objective functions provide a quantitative mea-
sure of the system’s performance and guide the optimization process.
The decision variables in wind energy optimization may include turbine location, rotor diameter, hub

variables, it is possible to enhance energy capture, reduce wake effects, and improve overall wind farm

Constraints in wind energy optimization can encompass turbine spacing requirements to mitigate wake

land-use restrictions, noise considerations, etc. By incorporating these constraints, optimization algo-

Moreover, optimization models in wind energy incorporate various parameters, such as wind speed
data, turbine performance curves, cost models, grid characteristics, and site-specific conditions. These
parameters are essential for accurately representing real-world systems and facilitating informed decision-
making throughout the optimization process.
1.1.1
Optimization Methods
Optimization algorithms are essential for finding optimal solutions to complex problems. They utilize
various search strategies to explore the solution space and converge towards the best possible solution.
Two prominent algorithms in this field are Sequential Least Squares Programming (SLSQP) and Random
Search (RS).
SLSQP, introduced by D. Kraft in the early 1980s [20], is a derivative-based method primarily used
for constrained optimization. It is known for its robustness and efficiency in handling problems with
equality and inequality constraints. By combining least squares approximation and quadratic program-
ming, SLSQP iteratively refines an initial feasible point by solving subproblems and utilizing line search
techniques. This allows it to converge towards the optimal solution while respecting the constraints.
In contrast, RS is a derivative-free optimization algorithm that explores the solution space by randomly
sampling points and evaluating the objective function at each point. Despite its simplicity, RS is powerful
and capable of handling both constrained and unconstrained optimization problems. It starts by generating
a set of random solutions and retains the best solution found. Subsequent iterations continue generating
random solutions, updating the best solution if a superior one is discovered [21].
Compared to SLSQP, RS offers benefits such as ease of implementation and the ability to escape local
optima due to its inherent randomness. However, it may require more iterations to converge compared
to gradient-based methods.

1.1.3
Design of Mooring Systems for FOWTs
Designing mooring systems for shallow water depths presents a significant challenge, particularly in
the transition zone between bottom-fixed and floating wind turbines. This specific range, typically span-
ning from 50 m to 80 m, has gained notable attention due to the potential cost-effectiveness of floating
concepts compared to bottom-fixed ones. The complexities associated with mooring design in this shal-
low water regime necessitate extensive investigation and optimization to ensure the reliable and efficient
operation of FOWTs.
Various researchers have focused their efforts on exploring different aspects of mooring system design
within this range of water depths, aiming to address the unique challenges and capitalize on the advantages
offered by floating concepts. Brommundt et al. [24] developed a numerical tool to optimize catenary
mooring systems, conducting a case study using a semi-submersible FOWT with water depths of 75 m and
330 m. The study focused on determining the optimal line length, angle, and horizontal distance between
the anchor and fairlead. Their findings highlight the importance of incorporating spectral wind loads into
the design of mooring systems and the need for more precise simulations that take into account an accurate
representation of viscous damping to obtain precise simulation results. Moreover, incorporating factors
such as wave-drift, aerodynamic effects, mooring line damping, and line friction on the sea floor can
effectively reduce the displacements of the floating platform and the maximum tensions in the mooring
lines.
Additionally, Campanile et al. [25] conducted a study that proposed different catenary mooring de-
signs for the Dutch tri-floater wind turbine. They considered water depths ranging from 50 meters to 80
meters and 200 meters to 350 meters. The study provided recommendations on line quantity, permis-
sible platform offset, minimum spacing between adjacent wind turbines, and conducted a preliminary
cost analysis covering installation and maintenance costs. In this context, the research concludes that
6-line mooring systems present the most optimal configuration for water depths ranging from 50 to 350
meters, outperforming the 9-line configurations commonly used. These 6-line systems ensure redundant

1.1.4
Design of a Floating Offshore Wind Farm
Enhancing the efficiency of wind farms can be accomplished through the implementation of effective
strategies, such as optimizing the layout and integrating real-time control systems. The optimization of
wind farm layout plays a critical role in determining the optimal placement of turbines even before the
construction phase begins. This process involves analyzing site-specific historical wind data to maximize
energy capture [28].
Several approaches have been explored to tackle the challenge of layout optimization. Pioneering
work by Mosetti et al. [29] introduced the concept by utilizing a genetic algorithm to position turbines
within a discrete 10×10 grid. Their objective was to strike a balance between energy production and
cost. Subsequently, Ozturk and Norman [30] advanced the field by incorporating continuous turbine
locations into the optimization process. Since then, researchers have investigated both continuous and
discrete turbine placement methods. However, continuous placement methods have exhibited superior
performance in optimizing wind farm layouts. Criado et al. [31] developed a novel method to incorporate
irregular boundaries into optimization. They used parameterized representations and analytical gradients
to achieve continuous optimization. The authors suggested two strategies, boundary relaxation and the
smart-start algorithm, to improve wind turbine placement within inclusion polygons. Compared to a
gradient-based solver, both strategies led to substantial increases in AEP. The relaxation strategy resulted
in a 10.2% AEP improvement over the baseline, while the smart-start algorithm achieved a 20.5% AEP
increase.
An essential challenge in wind farm operation is the occurrence of wake losses resulting from aerody-
namic interactions when a downwind turbine is situated within the wake of upwind turbines. As a result,
various approaches exist for optimizing the performance of FOWFs. These approaches include power
de-rating of upwind turbines to mitigate the wind velocity deficit created in their wake (also known as
axial induction-based control), yaw-based wake redirection of upwind turbines to redirect the wake away
from downstream turbines, or real-time turbine relocation to minimize the wake effects [ [28], [32] ].
Power de-rating was introduced by Steinbuch et al. in 1988 [33] as a method to improve overall power
production in wind turbines. It involves reducing the axial induction factor of a specific wind turbine,
decreasing its power output and rotor thrust force. This reduction benefits downstream turbines, as they
experience higher wind speeds that compensates for the power loss of upstream wind turbines [32].
On the other hand, yaw-based wake redirection is a strategy where wind turbines are intentionally

Finally, a noteworthy concept that has recently gained attention is the repositioning of FOWTs. This

1.2
Understanding the FOWT Platform Behavior
The design of FOWTs platforms involves choosing the most efficient platform type and optimizing its
shape. Knowledge of global platform motions has been derived from oil-and-gas-related projects, but the
installation of the turbine and tower can significantly change the responses. FOWTs pose unique chal-
lenges due to the interaction between dynamic loads induced by wind and wave forces, which coupling
affects power generation, structural loads, and wind flow around the turbine. Therefore, understand-
ing the motion behavior of FOWTs is essential for designing efficient and reliable systems for power
prediction models [12].
The movement of a floating platform can be described by six degrees of freedom (DoF), which in-
clude surge, sway, heave, pitch, roll, and yaw. These displacements of the platform result in changes
to the position of the turbine rotor, causing gain or losses in the power generation. Figure 1.1 provides
a visual representation of these changes in platform and rotor position. Surge refers to the platform’s
linear displacement along the x-axis, whereas sway refers to the platform’s linear displacement along the
y-axis. Heave is the platform’s linear displacement along the z-axis, representing vertical movement.
When looking at the angles, pitch refers to the angle between the platform’s longitudinal axis and the ZY
plane, indicating forward or backward tilting; whereas roll is the angle between the platform’s longitudi-
nal axis and the XZ plane, representing side-to-side tilting. Furthermore, yaw is the angle between the
rotor center orientation and the inflow wind direction, indicating rotation around the z-axis. These DoF
are critical in understanding how the motion of the platform affects the position of the turbine rotor and
ultimately the power generation [37]. It is important to account for every DoF when designing FOWTs
to ensure efficient and reliable energy production.

1.3
Moveable Floating Offshore Wind Turbines
As previously stated, one crucial challenge in wind farm operation is the occurrence of wake losses
caused by the aerodynamic interactions when a downwind turbine is situated within the wake of upwind
turbines. However, the emergence of FOWTs introduces a promising opportunity to mitigate these wake
losses within a Floating Offshore Wind Farm (FOWF).
Mahfouz and Cheng (2022) [38], addresses the potential of FOWTs to reposition themselves in the
horizontal plane, thus reducing wake losses and increasing the FOWF’s AEP. It explores leveraging the
Mooring System (MS) of FOWTs for enhancing the FOWF’s AEP. This approach introduces a new DoF
in the wind farm layout design by allowing FOWTs to autonomously adjust their positions based on
wind direction and wind speed. The proposed self-adjusting wind farm layout equips each FOWT with
a customized MS design. This allows FOWTs to relocate favorably according to the wind direction,
optimizing overall AEP. The methodology employed in their research involves assigning a random MS
design to each FOWT in the farm from the available MS design space. Subsequently, the AEP of the
corresponding FOWF is calculated. The next step entails iterating over all possible MS designs for a spe-
cific FOWT (FOWTj), selecting the MS design that yields the highest AEP for the FOWF. This iterative
process is repeated for all FOWTs in the FOWF until the energy production of the entire farm converges,
ensuring that the MS attached to each FOWT is saved. By adopting this approach, the computational
time required to identify customized MS designs for each FOWT is significantly reduced compared to an
optimization process that considers all possible combinations of MS designs. In order to facilitate the im-
plementation and further development of their approach, the researchers have developed an open-source
Python tool. This tool promotes collaboration and advancements in the wind energy community. A case
study demonstrates a 1.6% increase in FOWF’s AEP compared to a state-of-the-art optimized layout.
Paul [13] focused on maximizing AEP in FOWFs using a passive turbine relocation technique. This
involved adjusting the yaw angles of the turbines on the water surface to alter the direction of the thrust
force, aiming to optimize the yaw angles and reduce wake losses. The study utilized PyWake, a Python
package for wind farm calculations, and OpenMDAO, an open-source Python package for optimization.
Multiple scenarios were analyzed to compare FOWFs with passive relocation to traditional bottom-fixed
turbines. The results showed that a FOWF employing the passive relocation approach and equipped with
sufficiently long mooring lines achieved a 9.5% higher AEP compared to an equivalent wind farm with
bottom-fixed turbines and no wind farm control strategy. Moreover, this AEP was 0.95% higher than that
of the same wind farm with bottom-fixed turbines using yaw optimization for maximizing power output.
Rodrigues et al. [39] conducted simulations to investigate the automated repositioning of FOWTs
using a three-mooring system. They utilized an active system of winches to adjust the mooring lines,
aiming to find a position with minimal wake loss. Their proposed framework employed an evolutionary
optimization strategy in a nested configuration, simultaneously optimizing the anchoring locations and
turbine positions within the mooring lines for each wind direction. The study revealed that energy gain is
enhanced by optimizing both the layout resulting from the anchoring locations, and the turbine positions
within the mooring lines for specific wind directions.
These findings emphasize the importance of the passive relocation strategy in FOWFs and highlight
the crucial role of mooring systems in enhancing the efficiency of FOWFs.

Working Environment and Site Simulation Setup
The wind farm simulations were performed using Python, an open-source, high-level, general-purpose
programming language. To carry out these simulations, a coupling between Ju Feng’s motion model [1]
(detailed in the following Section 2.3) and PyWake [2] was developed. PyWake is an open-source Python
framework for simulating wind farm performance and optimizing layouts. Its main capabilities include
selecting a wake model, defining wind turbines, computing flow fields, estimating the power output of
individual turbines, determining the AEP of a wind farm, and generating plots of the results [2].
The wind farm site was defined using PyWake’s UniformWeibullSite model, which provides a sector-
dependent Weibull-distributed wind speed based on user-specified parameters. The necessary parameters,
such as sector frequencies, Weibull scale and shape parameters, were obtained from data from the Horns

Wind Farm Motion Model
As explained before, the motion model plays a crucial role in predicting the performance of a wind
farm under various wind conditions. In this study, the motion model is computed individually for each
WT in the wind farm, taking into account the wd and ws. A nested loop is implemented to iterate through

2.4.1
Minimizing the Motion Model Computational Time: Investigated Case Studies
In the context of wind farm simulations, the computational time plays a crucial role as the complex-
ity of the wind farm increases. As the number of turbines and the intricacy of the configuration grow,
computational resources are more heavily utilized, leading to longer processing times. Therefore, it be-
comes essential to investigate the impact of parameter variations on both the computational time and the
accuracy of the simulation results. This investigation can be effectively conducted through a sensitivity
analysis, which allows for a systematic exploration of the relationships between the input parameters and
the termination of iterations within a given flow case.
Considering the scenario at hand, where the wind farm evolves from a simple configuration with three
turbines (3WT) to a more intricate setup with sixteen turbines (16WT), the computational time exhibits
a noticeable escalation. To address this issue, a sensitivity analysis is proposed as a means to manage the
computational burden. The primary objective of this analysis is to examine the parameters that govern
the termination of iterations in a specific flow case. In other words, the focus is on identifying the criteria
or conditions that determine when the simulation can stop iterating.
To accomplish this goal, an initial case, known as the Base Case (BC), is defined as the starting point.
The BC serves as a baseline for subsequent analysis. In this case, specific threshold values are established
for various parameters: the thresholds for the x and y coordinates are set at 0.1 m, while the threshold
for the z coordinate is set at 0.01 m. Additionally, the threshold for the FOWT pitch angle is set at 0.1°.
Furthermore, the analysis encompasses wind directions ranging from 0° to 359°, with a step increment
of 1°, resulting in a total of 360 wind directions. Moreover, wind speeds ranging from 4 m/s to 25 m/s
are considered, with a step increment of 1 m/s, yielding 22 wind speeds. These defined parameters and
their ranges comprise the initial BC for subsequent sensitivity analysis.
By conducting the sensitivity analysis, it becomes possible to comprehend the trade-offs between com-
putational time and accuracy. The analysis provides insights into how variations in parameters impact
the termination of iterations, allowing for the identification of critical factors that influence the simula-
tion’s convergence. Ultimately, this investigation enables the optimization of computational resources
by striking an appropriate trade-off between the desired accuracy and the computational time required
for wind farm simulations.
In this regard, to explore different strategies for reducing the computational time, three additional
cases were derived based on the BC, which requires thirty minutes to compute the entire motion model.
By analyzing these cases, valuable insights can be gained into potential approaches for improving the
efficiency of the wind farm simulations.

Iterative Quasi-Static Motion Model
It is essential to understand the wake effects and how they affect the power output of downstream
wind turbines. Wake effects refer to the reduction in wind speed and increased turbulence caused by
the passage of a WT. These effects can significantly impact the performance of downstream turbines as
they are exposed to slower and more turbulent wind conditions, which can cause a decrease in its power
output. Additionally, the increased turbulence in the wake can cause increased structural loading on the

tions and wake effects are considered, facilitating a more precise prediction of each WT’s performance.

To compute the motion model, a code to merge the quasi-static motion model [1] and PyWake [2] has
been developed. This process is illustrated in Figure A.1 of the Appendix (Section A). In this regard,
PyWake is used to define the site and compute the wind farm simulation results, that is, the effective
wind speed and AEP, whereas the quasi-static motion model [1] computes the wind turbines motion for
every flow case.
The first step in the code involves determining the computational index order for each section within
the wind farm. The specific order depends on the number of wind turbines in the wind farm. In the
current study, since the wind farm consists of 16 FOWTs, the computational order remains as previously
explained (Figure A.2). This order determines the sequence in which the wind turbines are processed
during the calculations.
Subsequently, a nested loop is executed to determine the repositioning of each wind turbine for every
flow case. The loop iterates through each wind turbine, considering its motion and position in relation
to the surrounding flow conditions. By performing this iterative procedure for each flow case, the code
accurately computes the motion of the FOWTs within the FOWF.
In order to compute the FOWTs motion using the quasi-static movement model [1], the thrust force
of the corresponding FOWT under the respective wd and ws must be known. Thus, the first step in
computing the motion model is obtaining the initial effective wind speed (wseff) for the FOWT before
repositioning, which is done using PyWake [2], followed by obtaining the corresponding thrust force (FT )
from the quasi-static movement model [1]. The provisional FOWT rotor center position is then computed
using the quasi-static motion model [1]. The effective wind speed and thrust are then recalculated for the
new FOWT coordinates, and the second approach to obtain the updated FOWT coordinates is computed.
This iterative process continues until the disparity between the computed FOWT movement in the current
and previous iterations falls below a predefined threshold for each computed variable (ϵx = ϵy = 0.5 m,
ϵz = 0.05 m, and ϵγ = 0.5°), after considering the impact of the rotor center position on AEP, as
explained in Section 2.4.1. Furthermore, to prevent excessive computation time, a maximum limit of six
iterations per FOWT is imposed.
It is crucial to acknowledge that the effective wind speed and thrust force of each WT must be in-
dividually computed for every wind flow case, following a predetermined computational order. This
is attributable to the fact that the displacement of an upstream WT influences the effective wind speed
experienced by downstream WTs. Similarly, in each iteration, the effective wind speed and thrust force
must be recalculated until the final WT position is determined for the specific flow case.
Once the updated wind turbine coordinates are determined for the corresponding wd and ws combi-
nation, the next WT in the computation order is processed using the same methodology. This process
continues until the final layout of the wind farm is obtained for every wd and ws combination. By adher-
ing to this methodology, the motion model of the wind farm can be efficiently and accurately computed
for a range of wind directions and wind speeds, thereby facilitating the calculation of the wind farm’s
AEP for the moveable wind farm.

Optimization and Parametric Analysis
In the Literature Review addressed in Section 1, different strategies and techniques for improving
the performance of FOWFs are discussed. It highlights the importance of addressing wake effects and
their impact on the overall efficiency of FOWFs. One of the key approaches identified is the real-time
relocation of FOWTs through the use of non-taught mooring lines. By allowing controlled motion, these
systems aim to optimize the positioning of wind turbines in response to changing wind conditions, thereby
enhancing their energy capture capabilities.
The motion of FOWTs is not solely determined by the configuration angle of the mooring lines but
also influenced by the anchoring position and the length of the mooring lines, which may vary depending
on the sea depth. Therefore, it becomes imperative to optimize these parameters to effectively mitigate
wake effects and achieve the best possible performance of FOWFs.
The primary objective of this chapter is to explore the optimization of mooring systems for FOWFs,
with a focus on reducing wake effects and improving overall performance. To achieve this objective,
the chapter will delve into the various factors influencing the motion of FOWTs, including mooring
line configuration angle, anchoring position, sea depth and mooring line length. It will also discuss the
challenges associated with optimizing these parameters and propose potential solutions. Furthermore,
the chapter will present case studies and simulation results to demonstrate the effectiveness of optimized
mooring systems in enhancing the performance of FOWFs.
It is crucial to recognize that these analyses serve as a foundation for future studies on these parameters,
aiming to achieve a better FOWF performance. They provide an introductory exploration into these
interesting topics. Therefore, further refinement and in-depth investigations are required to draw final
conclusions and insights in this field.

Conclusions and Future Work
In conclusion, this master’s thesis project has contributed to the field of floating wind energy by fo-
cusing on the motion effect of FOWTs on AEP and addressing the associated challenges. The findings
of this study shed light on the significant influence of tilted FOWTs on AEP and emphasize the potential
of optimizing FOWTs’ configuration to enhance the performance of FOWFs.
Under the simulation setup, which considered 12 wind speeds and 30 wind direction sectors, a decrease
of -0.435% in AEP was observed when shear effects were considered compared to bottom-fixed wind
farms. However, without considering shear effects, a higher AEP was achieved, resulting in a change of
-0.278% compared to bottom-fixed wind farms. These results indicate that 62.5% of the total AEP drop is
attributed to the reduced rotor area due to tilted FOWTs, while 37.5% is caused by shear effects resulting
from the lower hub height. Therefore, mitigating the pitch angle induced by the motion of FOWTs is
crucial for maximizing AEP.
Various optimization studies were conducted to determine the optimal mooring line configuration angle
for each FOWT. These studies focused on a single wind speed (11 m/s) to reduce computational time. The
SLSQP method yielded the best result, improving AEP by +0.5%, while the Random Search approach
improved AEP by +0.37% compared to the base FOWF. However, when implementing the optimized
configuration angle obtained from simulations and considering the whole range of wind directions and
speeds, the AEP improvement was found to be +0.11%. This emphasizes the significance of assessing
the actual impact of optimizing the mooring line configuration angle to reduce wake effects under various
wind conditions.
Furthermore, the correct determination of mooring line length and anchoring position is crucial. Dif-
ferent parametric analyses were conducted to examine these parameters, along with sea depth, using 30
wind direction sectors, 12 wind speeds, and a mooring line configuration angle of 0° for every FOWT.
The findings revealed that when the mooring line is less tensed in an initial scenario without external
forces, the maximum allowable horizontal displacement of the FOWT is higher, resulting in a higher
AEP. This is due to the higher wind speeds experienced at the FOWT’s rotor center, given the higher hub
height. However, no significant changes were observed in the pitch angle across the evaluated situations.
On the other hand, considering fixed sea depth for a given water area and limited mooring line manu-
facturers, a final study aimed to find the best anchoring position, given these parameters. By setting the
maximum allowable fairlead point horizontal displacement to half the minimum distance between adja-
cent FOWTs (∆xmax = 5D/2), a modest AEP improvement of +0.03% was achieved, accounting for
all wind direction sectors and wind speeds while maintaining a mooring line configuration angle of 0°.
Although this improvement may seem minimal, it highlights the existing knowledge gap that warrants
further examination for conclusive findings and potential enhancements.
While this study focused on a regular 16-WTs FOWF, it is important to highlight that layout opti-
mization plays a crucial role in reducing wake effects and achieving better AEP performance. Therefore,
future studies should focus on analyzing the behavior of FOWFs with more complex and irregular layouts.
Additionally, optimizing the mooring line configuration angle has shown improvements and a knowledge
gap that should be further investigated to understand its influence on reducing wake effects for a broader

range of flow cases, different FOWTs sizes, and more complex layouts, as previously mentioned. Fur-
thermore, although the SLSQP method yielded the best results in the computed simulations, it is worth
considering that it may encounter challenges in reaching the global optimum and could potentially get
stuck at a local optima. Thus, an additional optimization study could be conducted, incorporating a se-
ries of iterations using the Random Search method, to further refine the results obtained carrying out an
SLSQP optimization problem, initializing the design variables with the previously obtained results.
In addition, the quasi-static motion model used in this study only considers 4 DoFs (surge, sway, heave,
and pitch), wave loads are not taken into account, and both sea depth and level are considered to be
uniform. Therefore, future studies could investigate the impact of including metocean data to understand
its influence on FOWTs’ motion, as well as different bathymetry levels to account for various mooring
system configurations, anchoring positions, and cable lengths.
Moving away from finding the optimal FOWF setup to maximize AEP, an interesting avenue for in-
vestigation would be to conduct an economic analysis, in which the chosen mooring lines would play a
crucial role. Considering factors such as cost, reliability, and maintenance requirements, an economic
analysis could provide valuable insights into the feasibility and long-term viability of different mooring
line configurations in the context of commercial-scale FOWFs.
Overall, this master’s thesis project has laid a solid foundation for further research in the field of
floating wind energy, with potential areas of exploration. These future studies will contribute to advancing
the understanding and practical implementation of floating wind energy systems.
