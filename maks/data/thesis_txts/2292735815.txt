Aim of study 
2.1 Objectives: 
•
To generate ergosterol profiles of an array of clinical C. albicans isolates, including isolates found toharbor
the frameshift mutation F105SfsX23 in the ERG2 gene.
•
To genetically create a frameshift mutation F105SfsX23 in the ERG2 genes of first S.cerevisiae andthen
C.albicans and transforms them in these yeasts.
•
To generate ergosterol profiles for the created transformants as well test them for susceptibility to the
antifungal drug amphotericin B.
•
To establish a link if possible, between the frameshift mutation (F105SfsX23) depleted ergosterollevels
and polyene resistance.
The incentives behind this study are among others,  the fact that polyene resistance has been observed in some clinical 
C. albicans isolates,  found to be harboring (in addition to other mutations), the frameshift mutation F105SfsX23 in 
the ERG2 gene .9  Furthermore, other studies have linked ERG2 mutations to ergosterol depletion and antifungal
resistance thus it is an objective of this study to experimentally establish this.13  In order to investigate whether or not
the specific base pair deletion F105SfsX23 is in fact solely responsible for resistance to polyenes, mutants with the 
specific ERG2* mutation will be genetically generated for this purpose. An ergosterol profile of the mutant strains will 
be generated and compared to WT strains to establish whether the altered sterol-composition phenotype is 
consequential of the mutation. Subsequently a polyene susceptibility test will be carried out to investigate and whether 
or not the mutation mediates resistance.

4.1 The ergosterol biosynthesis pathway 
Ergosterol is a vital membrane lipid that provides rigidity, stability, stress resistance as well as keeps yeast cell non-
permeable. 17The areas of main interest for this study in the ergosterol biosynthesis pathway are depicted in the green 
and red boxes on figure 1. The reaction in the green box is an intermediate step in the pathway, mediated by the sterol 
C8-C7 isomerase encoded by the ERG2 gene. This enzyme is essential for cell proliferation and specifically 
responsible for the catalytic isomerization reaction of fecosterol resulting in unsaturation of the C-7 in the B ring of 
episterol. This enzymatic activity is transcriptionally down-regulated when ergosterol is in excess.18

4.2 Antifungal drugs and their mechanisms of action 
There are three chemical classes of antifungal agents available for the treatment of C. albicans infections. 
Classification of these drugs is dependent upon their cellular targets. They comprise azoles, echinocandins and 
polyenes. Azoles inhibit the activity of E5*11, while echinocandins inhibit the activity of F.61 and F.62 The
polyenes target ergosterol which is synthesized by several enzymes among them, C8-C7 isomerase which is the focus 
of this study. Polyenes mechanism of action involves sequestering of the ergosterol molecules and also specific 
interaction with the ergosterol molecules in fungal membranes which mediates the formation of oligomeric complexes 
which in turn form cation-selective pores in the fungal cell membranes making them permeable and thus destroying 
the cells. Figure 3 shows the cellular targets of antifungal drugs while figure 4 shows the two mechanisms of action of

4.3 Gene targeting techniques 
4.3.1
Adaptamer mediated Fusion PCR 
Adaptamer technology is a technique that can be used to fuse two PCR fragments by an additional round of PCR 
known as fusion PCR. Fusion PCR is a much faster and versatile method of obtaining large amounts of linear DNA 
fragments that can be used for transformation than the conventional cloning procedures such as transformation into 
E.coli. 20 An adaptamer is a short addition to the 5’-OH end of a primer. The additional sequence may or may not be 
homologous to the region to be amplified. However, in order to link two DNA fragments using this technology as is 
the case in this project, the two fragments must have complementary sequences at the adaptamer region thereby acting 
as links or adapters between the two substrates in allowing for fusion in a subsequent round of PCR. 21

4.3.2
Homologous Recombination 
This is a gene targeting technique that employs the DNA Double Strand Break (DSB) repair pathway. This pathway 
can be employed to introduce desired genomic alterations such as targeted gene disruptions, gene fusions and novel 
gene mutations into genes of interest.20 This is achieved by introducing non- endogenous DNA with great similarity 
to the desired target in the form of a double strands break (DSB), where it is then recognized within the target 
organism as an endogenous DSB needing to be repaired. Consequently one of two DSB repair mechanisms comes into 
play, which is either homologous recombination (HR) or non-homologous end joining (NHEJ).The repair mechanism 
essentially integrates the foreign DNA into the organism's genome

4.4 Phenotypic Analyses 
4.4.1
Ultra High Performance Liquid Chromatography (UHPLC) 
In Ultra High Pressure Liquid Chromatography (UHPLC), much like the traditional High Pressure Liquid 
Chromatography (HPLC) method, mixtures of particles dissolved in solvent known as a mobile phase are
passed through a column packed with solid material at very high pressure. As the liquid/mobile phase passes 
through a column packed with a solid material known as the stationery phase, the time taken to travel 
through the column is recorded. This time differs depending on the partitioning behavior of the compounds 
being analyzed.  The retention time as well as UV absorption at a set wavelength for each compound are 
recorded by the detector and sent to a data integration system which interprets the data into spectral peaks. 
UHPLC however, uses much higher pressure but has some advantages over HPLC and other methods of
chromatography some of which are, higher selectivity, sensitivity, increased sample through-put and faster
analysis run time, higher resolution (peak areas (<0.1% RSD), improved precision for retention times (2–3-
fold), less solvent consumption and thereby operation costs as well.22, 23

5.5 Phenotypic analyses 
Several techniques were used to analyze and compare the phenotype of the mutant strains versus WT. Both the 
macromorphology and micromorphology were analyzed using classical mycological methods such as visual 
observation and microscopy. These were important to undertake as ergosterol is makes up a large percentage of the 
yeast cell membrane and might therefore be observable using these simple and effective analyses.  Sterol composition 
profiles were also generated using UHPLC and GC-MS to analyze the phenotypic behavior emanating from the
PXWDQW ERG2* genotype versus WT ERG2.
5.5.1
Sterol composition analysis 
Single colonies of C. albicans were inoculated in 5ml YEPD media and incubated at 30°C on a rotary shaker O/N. The 
following day the 5ml inoculums was transferred into 50ml YEPD media in 0.5L flasks and again incubated in the 
same way O/N. The next day sterols were extracted from the yeast cells using 1ml Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH) per 
sample to hydrolyze it by breaking Ester bonds. To fixates the cells and prevents any further biochemical processes 
4ml methanol (CH3OH) was added to each sample, followed by incubation at 90 ࡈC.  After incubation 6 ml pentane 
was used to dissolve the fatty acid chains including Ergosterol within the sample while the rest of the cellular content 
sedimented creating phase separation. 4 ml of the upper phase (pentane phase) were then carefully pipetted into a new 
set of falcon tubes.  Subsequently the pentane was evaporated under nitrogen flow at 42°C (app. 12 min) and the dry 
sample re-dissolved in 300 μL 2-propanol and centrifuged at highest rpm. Finally 1.5 ml of the re-dissolved ergosterol 
was transferred by pipette filtered into HPLC vials ready for analysis.
5.5.2
Protein structure analysis 
As a means to analyze the differences in phenotypic manifestation of the Wildtype C-8-C7 isomerase and that of the 
altered genotype ERG2*, homology models were generated. Some guidelines for choosing homologs, similarities 
between the S.cerevisiae ERG2 and the possible candidates were taken into consideration. Protein family, protein 
function and structural similarities including secondary features e.g. transmembrane Helixes, an active site comprising 
of a hydrophobic pocket-like cavity which are essential for enzymatic activity were some of the attributes taken into 
consideration. The essential AA needed for Isomerase activity and fact that the residues required for catalytic activity 
can also be involved in inhibitor binding were considered as well. Inhibitor binding is a trait of the protein family, 
ERG2/sigma1 receptor-like (IPR006716), which ERG2 belongs.  It has also been established by another study that the 
residues that were essential for catalytic activity of C-8 isomerase were also essential for inhibiting activity that is 
==PAGE:24==
ϭϳ
emblematic for ERG2/sigma1 receptor-like (IPR006716) protein family.26 As part of comparison and analysis an 
inter-species sequence alignment was made between counterparts of ERG2 using ClustalW software.
A prediction model for C8-C7 isomerase was generated and analyzed using three different software 
programs CLC main workbench, Phyre2 and Pymol. The WT. DNA sequence for S.cerevisiae was acquired from 
Genbank (accession number M74037) The ERG2* sequence was also generated by manually creating the 
F105SfsX23 mutation from the WT sequence in CLC main workbench. The two sequences were then transferred 
to Phyre2 for homology modeling. Phyre2 generates the protein models by matching the given sequence to a set of 
homologues detected by PSI-Blast. The matches are ranked by a raw alignment score and used to create a template.
These are processed by a support vector machine and the output is an image of the model constructed from the 
provided sequence based on that template. The generated model comes with coordinate in PDB format for input to 
other viewing or analysis programs. In this study, Pymol was used as the platform to perform the viewing and
analysis.

Substrate construction 
First the Cen.PK 113-5D yeast cells was grown on a YPD place  at 30ͼC for two days after which genomic DNA was 
extracted from them. Alongside this the plasmid PWJ716 was transformed into E.coli DH5 Į and incubated LB media 
at 37ͼC O/N. The plasmids were retrieved from E.coli the following day and these together with Cen.PK 113-5D were 
used as DNA templates for substrate construction. Secondly an ERG2* PCR fragment harboring the F105SfsX23 
mutation was amplified by adaptamer technology and Fusion PCR using CenPk113-5D as template DNA. The 
mutation was introduced by omitting the base at position 105 as well as its complementary base on the negative strand 
within the design of primers. The primers ERG2 ADP-F and ERG2- R for the upstream fragment where ERG2-R bore 
the omission, while ERG2-F and ERG2 ADP-R  were  used for the downstream fragment with ERG2-F bearing the 
omission.  The genetic marker K.lactis85$3 gene was amplified from the plasmid pWJ716 using primers KLAC-F 
and K1-R for the N terminal and K2-F and KLAC-R for the C terminal fragment.  Subsequently a copy of the 
generated mutant ERG2* was flanked by the 2/3 N-terminal of the genetic marker with partial overlapping sequence 
of the C-terminal. Another copy of ERG2*was similarly flanked by the 2/3 C-terminal of URA3 fragment.  The results 
were two linear fragments each containing a truncated two third fragment of the URA3 gene flanked by a copy of the 
entire ERG2*gene. The two fragments were then transformed directly into the target yeast strains Cen.PK 113-5D. 
The truncated URA3 fragments mimic an intermediate in the homologous recombination pathway, known as a DSB 
that should mediate repair by homologous recombination, hence integrating the fragments into the ERG2locus of the 
Cen.PK 113-5D genome. Figure 3 below depicts the substrate construction process. Further details on the substrate

5.7.2
Susceptibility testing 
Amphotericin B susceptibility test was carried out using a The European Committee on Antimicrobial Susceptibility 
Testing (EUCAST) method. All the yeasts isolates were cultured at 35 ± 2ºC on non-selective nutritive agar O/N.  
About five distinct colonies from each of the SC-Ura selective agar plates were suspended in 3mL sterile distilled 
water and vortexed. The cell density as with the growth kinetics test was adjusted to the density of 0,5 McFarland 
standard a final suspension of 1-5 x 105 CFU/mL.  100 μL RPMI 2% G a non-synthetic antibiotic medium
supplemented to a final concentration of 2% glucose, is added to each of the 96 wells on a microdilution plate with 
flat-bottom wells. The first column contains a concentration of antifungal agent of 4mg/L; column two contains a 2x 
dilution (2mg/L); column 3 contains 1mg/L and so on to column 10.  100μL of each cell suspension as well as a 
reference strain are subsequently inoculated into the wells for each antifungal concentration within 30 min of 
preparing them to maintain viable cell concentration. Once inoculated the microdilution plates are incubated at 35 ± 
2ºC for 48hrs. The results are read after 24hrs, re-incubated and then read again after 48hrs. The microdilution plates 
must be read with a microdilution plate reader. The recommended wavelength for measuring the absorbance of the 
plate is 530 nm, although others can be used e.g. 405 nm or 450 nm. The value of the blank (background)

Discussion and conclusions
•
Microscopy pictures depicted the WT colonies as having a uniform appearance among the cells while the
mutant colonies showed irregularities is size color and shape among the cells.  The mutant cells were also 
visibly smaller in size; this may be a result of the mutation and depletion of ergosterol which makes up a 
significantly large part of the cell membrane. The irregularities are presumably due to lack of rigidity and 
stability provided by the presence of normal amounts of ergosterol.
•
Growth kinetics revealed that the isolate with low ergosterol  content also had a somewhat hamperedgrowth
rate compared to the WT sterol isolates. These results are in concurrence with the fact that ergosterol as 
mentioned earlier makes up a considerably large percentage of the yeast cellularmembrane as well as being 
important for cell proliferation. Thus altered ergosterol contents are highly likely to affect growth rate as 
demonstrated here.  The growth rates of mutant isolates CA3,CA4 were as conjectured remarkably slower that 
WT. The final optical density values of the mutantisolates were also notably lower that all the other isolates. 
CA9 however despite also being anERG2*mutant did not exhibit a particularly slow growth rate.
•

completely accurate they provided an illuminating glimpse some of the attributes of the twoproteins and the 
differences between them.  It was visible to see that the mutant protein was muchsmaller in size, lacked some

9.3.1
Suseptibility test protocol
The test is performed in microdilution plates. The method is based on the preparation of antifungal agent working 
solutions in 100 ȝL volumes per well (with the addition of an inoculum also in a volume of 100 ȝL).
Medium
A completely synthetic medium, RPMI-1640 with glutamine (Table 1) and supplemented with glucose to a final 
concentration of 20 g/L (2%), is used. A pH indicator but without bicarbonate is recommended [(4-6)]. Zwitterion 
buffers are preferred to Tris, which antagonises the activity of flucytosine, and also to phosphate buffer, which may 
give unexpected interactions with antifungal agents. The buffer 3-(N morpholino) propanesulfonic acid (MOPS) at a 
final concentration of 0.165 mol/L, pH 7.0 is satisfactory for RPMI 1640 media. The recommended medium, RPMI 
with 2% glucose (RPMI 2% G), is prepared as follows: 
1. Add the components listed in Table 2 to 900 mL of distilled water.
2. Stir until components are completely dissolved.
3. With stirring, adjust the pH to 7.0 at 25ºC with 1 M sodium hydroxide.
4. Add water to a final volume of 1 L.
5. Filter sterilise using a 0.22 ȝm filter.
6. Store at 4ºC.
7. For quality control purposes, use one aliquot of the sterilised medium for sterility checks,
for retesting the pH (6.9-7.1 is acceptable) and as a growth control with a reference strain. 
Medium for testing amphotericin B
For the time being it is recommended that the RPMI 2% G is used also for amphotericin B. The non-synthetic broth 
Antibiotic Medium 3 (AM3), supplemented to a final concentration of 2% glucose, has been evaluated for detecting 
resistance to amphotericin B [(7-10)]. However, there is batch to batch variation in this medium, and also variation in 
the performance of the medium from different manufacturers. Preliminary results also indicate that an inoculum of 
0.5-2.5 x 105 cfu/mL is too high for testing amphotericin B in AM3 [(7)]. 
ANTIFUNGAL AGENTS
All antifungal drug solutions should be prepared in accordance with GoodManufacturing Practice. 
Antifungal powders must be obtained directly from thedrug manufacturer or from reliable commercial sources. 
Clinical preparations must not be used because they often contain excipients that may interfere with susceptibility
testing. Powders must be supplied with the generic name of the drug, a lot number, potency, expiry date and 
recommended storage conditions. Store powders in sealed containers at -20ºC or below with a desiccant unless 
otherwise recommended by the manufacturers. Ideally, hygroscopic agents should be dispensed into aliquots, one 
of which is used on each occasion. Containers should be allowed to warm to room temperature before opening in
order to avoid condensation of water on the powder.
Preparation of stock solutions Antifungal drug solutions must be prepared after taking the potency of the lot of 
antifungal drug powder into account. The amount of powder or diluent required to prepare a standard solution 
may be calculated as follows:
==PAGE:54==
ϰϳ
Weigh the antifungal drug powder on an analytical balance that has been calibrated to two decimal places when 
weighing 100 mg. It is recommended that at least 25 mg of powder is weighed. Information on the solubility of 
antifungal compounds should be provided by the supplier. Solvents other than water are required to dissolve some 
antifungal drugs (Table 3). Sterilisation of stock solutions is not normally necessary, but membrane filtration should 
be used if required. Other filter materials are best avoided as they may adsorb significant amounts of drug. When 
filtration is used, samples must be obtained before and after filtration and must be assayed to ensure that the drug is 
not adsorbed to the filter. Unless otherwise indicated by the drug manufacturer, store drug solutions in small volumes 
in sterile polypropylene or polyethylene vials at -70ºC or below. Drugs may be stored at -70ºC for at least six months 
without significant loss of activity [(11;12)]. Remove vials from -70ºC and use them the same day that they are 
defrosted. Discard any drug not used on that day. Significant deterioration of an antifungal agent will be reflected in 
the results of testing the susceptibility of quality control strains (Table 6). If necessary, the drug can be assayed to 
determine the potency.

Standardisation of the inoculum is essential for accurate and reproducible antifungal susceptibility tests. The inoculum 
should be prepared by suspending five representative colonies, obtained from an 18-24 h culture on nutritive agar

Colony suspension method
1.

2.

Cryptococcus spp.
Cryptococcus spp. are non-fermentative yeasts. The lack of fermentation compromises growth in microdilution plates 
set up according to the protocols defined by both the CLSI and EUCAST. A comprehensive study recently explored 
the effects of variation in the EUCAST susceptibility testing methodology in comparison with the standard procedure 
for Candida spp. [(15)]. Modifications included 1) growth medium (RPMI medium versus yeast nitrogen base 
[YNB]), 2) glucose concentration (0.2% versus 2%), 3) nitrogen source (ammonium sulfate), 4) temperature (30°C 
versus 35°C), 5) shaking, and 6) inoculum size (10 3 , 10 4 , and 10 5 cells).
Growth rates and MICs were compared. Although the use of YNB medium, reduction of the incubation temperature to 
30°C and shaking of the plates during incubation all increased the growth rate there were no significant differences 
between the MICs obtained with the different methods. In assessing agreement 
==PAGE:56==
ϰϵ
between MIC values, it was considered that MIC values that differed by less than two 2-fold dilutions were not 
significantly different. It is therefore recommended at present that the EUCAST methodology is adopted for the 
testing of Cryptococcus spp. and that the plates are read when the OD value is above 0.2. In cases where there is 
insufficient growth it is suggested that the test is repeated but with incubation of the trays at 30°C.

The microdilution plates should be inoculated within 30 min of preparing the inoculum suspension, in order to 
maintain the viable cell concentration. Inoculate each well of a microdilution tray with 100 ȝL of the 1-5 x 1105 CFU/
mL yeast suspension. This will give the required final drug concentration and inoculum density (final inoculum = 
0.5-2.5 x 105  CFU/mL). Also, inoculate the growth control wells (column 11), containing 100 ȝL of sterile drug-free 
medium, with 100 ȝL of the same inoculum suspension. Fill column 12 of the microdilution plate with 100 ȝL of 
sterile distilled water from the lot used to prepare the inoculum as a sterility control for medium and distilled water 
(drug-free medium only). Test quality control organisms by the same method each time an isolate is tested. 
INCUBATION OF MICRODILUTION PLATES
Incubate microdilution plates without agitation at 35 ± 2ºC in ambient air for 24 ± 2 h. An absorbance of  0.2 
indicates poor growth and is most commonly seen amongst strains of Candida parapsilosis And Candida
guilliermondii. Such plates should be re-incubated a further 12-24 h and then re-read. Failure to reach an 
absorbance of 0.2 after 48 h constitutes a failed test. As described above, an absorbance of  0.2 after 48 h for 
Cryptococcus spp. should prompt a repeat test with incubation at 30°C [(15)]. 
READING RESULTS 
The microdilution plates must be read with a microdilution plate reader. The recommended wavelength for measuring 
the absorbance of the plate is 530 nm, although others can be used e.g. 405 nm or 450 nm. The value of the blank 
(background) should be subtracted from readings for the rest of the wells.

9.5
Streak purification of a strain:
In many cases unwanted living cells may contaminate a colony on a plate. For example, if a ura3 strain is transformed 
with a DNA fragment that contains URA3 gene, then the resulting colonies may contain living untransformed ura3 
cells. This is possible, because the URA3 transformants excrete small, but sufficient amounts of uracil that allows the 
original ura3 cells to survive within the colony. The presence of ura3 cells in the colony constitutes a major problem 
in the next reaction step, pop-out recombination, where ura3 cells are selected. Thus, if the untransformed living ura3 
cells are not removed, then these unwanted cells would dominate the population after growth on 5-FOA. To avoid this 
situation, the URA3 colonies are re-streaked on a new SC-Ura plate to obtain new single colonies. These colonies are 
very likely to arise from a single cell and are therefore free of the original ura3 strain. This is called streak 
purification, see figure 11.

Case study: Background for isogenic and related isolates
Detailed case report
A 61-year old man, antecedently healthy, was diagnosed with angioimmunoblastic T cell lymphoma stadium 4Bm in 
December 2006. In January 2007 CHOEP-14 was initiated. After conclusion of 6 of 8 series and bone marrow 
stimulation with G-CSF, he underwent stem cell harvesting followed by intensive chemotherapy and 
autologous stem cell transplantation in June 2007. Shortly after, he suffered from long-term pancytopenia. In 
December 2007 his T cell Lymphoma relapsed with increased sized glands in the retroperitoneal cavity. With no
involvement of the bone marrow, he began therapy with C-ICE. After only one treatment course he developed 
deep pancytopenia and the chemotherapy was terminated. In 2008, progression of the hematological disease was 
found with new coming soft tissue density of the oropharynx, spleen enlargement and multiple processes in the lever, 
along with the already acknowledged retroperitoneal glands. He began treatment with thalidomide and 
dexamethasone and also received prednisolone and allopurinol for longer periods. The patient was in full 
remission in May 2010.
From 2006 infectious complications involving extended spectrum ȕ-lactamase producing producing Klebsiella
pneumoniae, noro-virus, Staphylococcus aureus and catheter related infections involving coagulase negative 
staphylococci, in addition to bacteraemic episodes involving Stenotrophomonas maltophilia, Proteus mirabilis and 
Enterococcus species for all of which he received relevant antibiotics. Most life quality compromising were numerous 
cases of oropharyngeal and oesophageal candidiasis, which were initially diagnosed a few years before the diagnosis 
of the T-cell lymphoma but became almost chronic with isolation of the C. albicans strain in oesophageal and colon 
biopsies and faeces. This led to long-term antifungal treatment (Fig. APP 4-1), numerous gastroscopies, and almost 
permanent parenteral nutrition because of stenosis of the oesophagus, severe dysphagia and weight loss. Parenteral 
nutrition was complicated by electrolyte derangement, hunger oedema, ascites and hypo-albuminaemia.
The patient deceased May 2011. Autopsy showed bilateral pneumonia and lung in addition to diffuse 
infiltrating signet ring cell carcinoma in the ventricle and relapse of T cell lymphoma, with malignant lymph nodes 
along the trachea, retroperitoneal cavity and the mesentery of the small intestine.
