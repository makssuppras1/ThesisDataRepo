ABSTRACT
Problems in a building can often be traced back to the requirements processing in the design phase, thus
the processing of client requirements is critical to a successful delivery of construction project. The literature
indicates that the current methods for requirements processing are ineffective.
The client is a complex system of stakeholders, users and investors, and therefore more effort should be done
to capture the client requirements. The client requirements are listed into the design brief, which serves as
the origin for the design processes, while new design solutions incrementally are based on previous solutions.
Research suggests that linking the client requirements to the design process while involving the client can
lead to a higher level of client satisfaction.
The aim of this study is to develop a structure for client requirements that supports integration into the early
stages of building design. By using the Design Science Research method combined with case studies in
Agile Building Design and Advanced Building Design, a set of rules for the structuring of client requirements is
designed and developed.
The intention of the study is not to provide a generic solution, that is applicable for all cases, but to examine
how a set of rules can support the integration.

INTRODUCTION
The original intentions of this study was to examine how the requirements for Facilities Management (FM) is
integrated in the early design stages of building design, by examining the current practice of ensuring smooth
transitions from Building Information Modelling (BIM) to FM. However, it rapidly became obvious to the re-
searcher, that in order for this to result in anything use-able, one had to be sure that the industry has adequate
methods for tracing and developing requirements throughout the life cycles of a building. Therefore, the focus
and scope of study for this thesis shifted away from generic processes from BIM to FM and instead it focuses
on the gaps and challenges related to requirements processing in the early stages of building design.
With the development of BIM comes the possibilities to digitally coordinate, cooperate and collaborate to en-
sure the quality of the built (Treldal 2017). This results in numerous possibilities and solution spaces within a
still growing number of software, which supports calculations and simulations within structures, energy per-
formance, indoor climate solar heating, cost, life cycle assessment amongst many other factors (Østergård et
al. 2016). However, the application of the simulation software in early design stages faces several challenges
due to time-consuming modelling, rapid change of design, conﬂicting requirements, input uncertainties and
large design variability (Østergård et al. 2016). Although the practitioners have formal methods within the in-
dividual disciplines, the ﬁeld is struggling to design and execute good multidisciplinary design, due to lack of
formal methodologies to plan, communicate and control multidisciplinary design processes (Haymaker et al.
2005).
Mondrup et al. (2012) ﬁnds that the key challenges of BIM adoption are related to time because it comes with
a learning curve. According to Mondrup et al. (2012) an integrated approach that combines technical struc-
tures and social practices is required to implement BIM. This includes organisational changes by updating
work practices and interpersonal dynamics (Mondrup et al. 2012). This matches the statements from Holzer
(2011), who states that challenges in implementation of BIM are not, or only minimally, rooted in misconcep-
tion on the technological perspective. The technical advances has been a key driver in the uptake of BIM
in the architectural practice, rather than a “discourse about its cultural implications”. Most of the challenges
with the implementation of BIM perspective occurs due to incapacity or unwillingness of practitioners in de-
sign and construction to swiftly adopt the advantages that BIM has to offer due to cultural reasons (Holzer
2011). Research by Holzer (2011) points to cultural and political levels of an organisation, as BIM necessitates
changes in common work processes inter- and intra-organisationally. Thus, when implementing BIM, changes
related to stafﬁng, training and project team conﬁguration needs to happen which inﬂuence the established
processes and business models of practice in the organisation (Holzer 2011). In relation to the BIM implemen-
tation problem areas, the lack of formalised work methods and processes forces architects to use inadequate
methods to involve users and thus, enables designers to interpret user information in their convenience (Kim

et al. 2016). Therefore, designers skills could be the reason for limited recognition and formalisation of the
requirements processing process (Pegoraro et al. 2017b).
According to Parsanezhad et al. (2016) the industry needs become more industrialised and automated, which
calls for more effective methodologies and systems for requirements management and progressive evalu-
ation and validation of design alternatives. Especially since problems in buildings often can be traced back
to the requirement processing in the design phases. This difﬁculty is recurrent due to the complex and iter-
ative nature and to the great number of stakeholders involved in these type of projects (Kamara et al. 2000;
Pegoraro et al. 2017b). According to Pegoraro et al. (2017a) it is widely believed, that the processing of re-
quirements is critical to a successful delivery of construction projects, however it is currently problematic in
its effectiveness (Pegoraro et al. 2017a).
Client requirements are typically recorded in a building program based on the design brief, however, the build-
ing programs format and coverage varies within aspects of overall goals, activities, spatial needs and very
detailed information on material and condition requirements (Kiviniemi 2005b). While the documentation is
used during the initialisation of the design process, it is normally left aside as the design progresses and
changes are made incrementally based on previous design solutions and the implications are never veriﬁed
and updated in relation to the original requirements mapping. This increases the magnitude of the potential
gab between client requirements and the build (Kiviniemi 2005a). The differences between the requirements
documents and the completed building can lead to well-justiﬁed doubts about the quality of the design and
construction process. Clients are both considered the initiative-takes and investors of a building industry, thus
they are the core of construction projects and an adequate capture and encapsulation is central to client satis-
faction (Kamara et al. 2002a, 2000; Kiviniemi 2005b). Therefore the practice needs to improve to ensure that
the client requirements are met and the implication for satisfying the client involves both an understanding

BACKGROUND
The building industry encounters multiple gaps and challenges in relation to the processing of client requi-
rements during the early design stages for a building. The development and implementation of BIM results
in time-consuming modelling, conﬂicting requirements, uncertainties in inputs and large design variability
(Østergård et al. 2016). The industry has established good disciplinary work methods, but BIM encourages and
demands cross-disciplinary collaboration and the ﬁeld currently struggles to execute good multidisciplinary
design (Haymaker et al. 2005). The development of BIM has resulted in great expectations for multidisciplinary
design across software, platforms and organisations. However, the ways of collaborated is misconceived, as
the industry attempts to design through one shared building model instead of making aggregated models
based on disciplinary reference models (Berlo et al. 2015).
According to (Parsanezhad et al. 2016), the lack of formal work methods relates to the BIM method and causes
the architects to use inadequate methods to involve users. This empowers architects to interpret user informa-
tion in their convenience (Kim et al. 2015). As processing of client requirements and requirements in general is
critical to a successful delivery of construction projects, it is problematic that it is currently problematic in both
effectiveness and methods (Pegoraro et al. 2017a). This is stressed by the fact, that problems in building often
can be traced back to the requirements processing in the design stages (Kamara et al. 2000; Pegoraro et al.
2017b). Kiviniemi (2005a) states, that an increasing distance between the client requirements and the build
occurs due to changes being based incrementally on previous design solutions instead of the design brief
(Kiviniemi 2005a). The differences in the requirements documentation and in the completed design causes
well-justiﬁed doubts about the quality of the design, and therefore it is argued that more effort should be made
to understand and satisfy the client (Kamara et al. 2000, 2002b; Latham 1994).
The client in a construction project is complex and can be difﬁcult to model generically. This makes it essential
to ensure that the client is fully understood before the design activities are initiated. The nature of the client is
often misinterpreted to be one entity instead of the dynamic body of stakeholders, each with individual expe-
riences and expectations for the building being commissioned. Adding to this complexity is the complexity of
its organisations and the prior experience with commissioning buildings (Bertelsen et al. 2005; Kamara et al.
2000, 2002b). The expectations, wishes and needs of the client are represented in the client requirements.
These are often translated and captured into the brief, which serves as the foundation of the design process
(Kamara et al. 2000, 2002b).
In the following, a literature review is conducted to identify the challenges in relation to the client and client
requirements during design stages in a building projects. Additionally it is sought to indicate areas for im-
provement to achieve a higher level of compliance between the client requirements and the ﬁnal solution and
thereby achieve a higher level of client satisfaction.

2.1
Interoperability and collaboration
Building Information Modelling (BIM) has become a commonplace in everyday building practices and is used
by a wide range of professionals like architects, engineers and contractors (Holzer 2011). Additionally the use
of BIM has evolved into the ﬁelds of Facility Management (FM) and thereby BIM is a tool that follows a building
from cradle to grave with the possibilities to comprise a large amount of information.
Based in the industry’s transition from CAD to BIM, a fundamental shift in the way building projects are con-
ceived and delivered is experienced (Holzer 2011). In a research performed by Holzer (2011) it is stated that
professional have high expectations toward BIM implementation for efﬁciency gains and more integrated
collaboration (Holzer 2011). The adoption of BIM is not without challenges, as moving to BIM necessitates
profound changes in common processes interorganisationally as well as intraorganisationally. The entire busi-
ness model of the practice can be affected when BIM is implemented as changes related to stafﬁng, training,
project team conﬁguration and project infrastructure impact previously established processes. Therefore im-
plementation of BIM is not about implementing a new software and according to Holzer (2011) this leads to
an fundamental misconception of BIM as a method for conceiving buildings.
Trying to fully understand and comprehend the BIM process is not easy due to the amount of information
individuals need to read, understand and internalise (Ashworth et al. 2019). Though, Holzer (2011) ﬁnds that
the current BIM tools barely supports early design collaboration across various disciplines, and thus design
explorations are often conducted in disciplinary silos. This makes it impossible to encounter decision making
across collaborating disciplines and as a result, the quick interdisciplinary evaluations of design in collabo-
ration becomes troublesome (Holzer 2011). The time delay between proposing changes by architect to the
response from engineer and consultants to run their analysis makes the industry slow.
2.1.1
Computer-Supported Cooperative Work
According to Zhou et al. (2011), Greenberg (1991) states that "CSCW is about how people work together, and how
the computer and related technologies affect group behaviour" (Zhou et al. 2011, p. 7). BIM is a collaborative
tool, that supports multidisciplinary design in a solution space spanning between different disciplines, organ-
isations, digital tools and persons, therefore the concept of BIM can be catagorised as a computer-supported
cooperative work-system (CSCW). To consider BIM as an supportive approach for collaborative work give rise
to issues, as BIM is still short of effective collaborative mechanisms for teamwork due to methodological,
technological and social challenges (Zhou et al. 2011).
Zhou et al. (2011) highlights three level of collaboration in interactive deﬁnition method to deliver collaborative
BIM planning via network. At a low level, plans and simulation data from multiple users can be integrated, while
at middle and high levels multidisciplinary users can use computer systems to communicate and interact
with each other directly and indirectly. Thereby, they can organise and coordinate their individual tasks and
behaviours in the networked environment. This kind of CSCW is essential to motivate effective collaboration
across multidisciplinary boundaries to be interdisciplinary work (Zhou et al. 2011).

2.1.3
Socio-technical implications
Information sharing is often insufﬁcient to ensure project success in design project and therefore, knowledge
creation and integration should be the goal in a collaborative design process. Consequently, if the actors
will not be able to create and integrate knowledge, they will not achieve a common understanding of the
problems at hand and as a result thereof not meet the design results (Treldal 2017). The disciplinary distinction
supports a reduction of product complexity and exploits the opportunities for parallel execution. However,
the information and knowledge gets scattered into the disciplines, making knowledge management more
complex (Treldal 2017).
In order to cope with the amount of scattered information and the complexity of knowledge management
arising from a technological distinction between the disciplines, a multi-disciplinary collaboration becomes
crucial to increase project success in building design projects, i.e. changing the social context of design
teams to become more integrated (Treldal 2017). The socio-technical system presents a perspective on how
social behaviour impacts interaction with technology and how integration of teams and knowledge can be
optimised (Treldal 2017).

Collaboration is the highest level of interaction that a project team can interact on according to Pikas et al.

• Cooperation is the lowest level of interaction, where control is centralised, while interaction and informa-
tion sharing only happens on an ad-hoc basis. In these types of interaction, the authority and resources
normally rest within the individual organisations.
• Coordination refer to an activity, which is more speciﬁcally to manage dependencies among task or

• Collaboration describes activities where common goal and interests are developed and authority, re-
sponsibilities, resources etc. are shared cross-organisationally.
Collaboration is needed to increase project success within building projects (Pikas et al. 2015; Treldal 2017).
Treldal (2017) ﬁnds that, there are two distinct concepts for shaping groups and human activities and inter-
actions. These are respectively Community of Practice and the Network of Practice. While a Community of
Practice is a group of people who have a common practice or interest, a Network of Practice is a group of
people who share a common goal, but does not share the same interdependence of a common practice.
Seen through the eyes of interaction, it is expected that coordination is the form of interaction in a Network of
Practice, while collaboration is more likely to be achieved in communities of practice (Treldal 2017).
Many design teams today only achieve coordination interaction between design disciplines while desiring col-
laboration. Each design discipline naturally belongs in individual communities of practice and for this reason it
is far more difﬁcult to create an intermediate Community of Practice consisting of different disciplines. Unless,
shared goals and values can be embodied in the actions by each team member (Treldal 2017).
2.1.3.1
Boundary and epistemic objects
How different communities interact can be described using the idea of boundaries and objects, which were
introduced by Star (2010) and Star et al. (1989) to determine how a boundary infrastructure can engage in
linking different communities together (Star 2010; Star et al. 1989; Treldal 2017).
Carlile (2004) proposes three categories for sharing and assessing knowledge across boundaries. These
are syntactic, semantic and pragmatic. The syntactic category shares and access knowledge by developing
a common lexicon and thereby ensuring that the differences and dependencies between actors are known.
Meanwhile, the semantic category stresses the need to develop common meanings to establish a shared
understanding and thereby give an adequate resource to share and assess knowledge at boundary. This
is essential as novelty can generate differences and dependencies that are unclear which leads to biased
interpretations. Coordinating interactions is to achieve only the semantic level of knowledge sharing as it
aims to manage dependencies among task and resources. Collaborative interaction is an ability to achieve
pragmatic knowledge sharing, as common interests are developed to create a shared knowledge.
Star (2010) speciﬁes the original framework from 1989 by Star et al. (1989) and discusses some of the misin-
terpretations extracted from it. It is stressed that boundary objects are not useful at any level of scale if the
entire model is not considered (Star 2010). Furthermore, boundary objects are not intended to form consen-
sus, yet in a boundary infrastructure some boundary objects will form more mutually dependent relationship

2.1.3.2
Participatory Design
Participatory Design (PD) is an Scandinavian tradition developed in the early 1970’s, where future users of a
design participate as co-designers (Velden et al. 2015). Velden et al. (2015) maintain the believe that “Partici-
patory design increases the chance that the ﬁnal result of a design process represents the values of the future
users, i.e the values of the client” (Velden et al. 2015, p. 42).
During the design processes in PD values and deﬁnitions of use emerges while the artefact explores the
different deﬁnition of use during the different stages of development (Velden et al. 2015). Unlike current design
processes in the building industry, PD builds on democratic processes where the designers and co-designers
(client) take design decisions that implicitly and explicitly inscribe values in the ﬁnal product. Together they
present the design’s meaning and prescription or script for use (Velden et al. 2015).
PD fosters mutual learning processes where the participants learn from each other, the co-designers (clients)
learn about design processes and get more clever on their own values while contributing to design. The
methods try to encourage participatory dialogues and to integrate ethical values in the entire design process,
Some methods are particularly productive for exploration and engagement in the early stages of the design
process, where the focus is on understanding practices and identifying needs and wishes (Velden et al. 2015).
One of these is Participatory prototyping, also called cooperative/collaborative prototyping, which is one of
the most important methods used in PD. Participatory prototyping is the activity in the design process in
which values are translated into requirements and become materialised in a designed object. Prototyping
creates a shared design space for designers and client in which tension between what is and what could be
can be examined through enacting scenarios like use-cases. Prototyping can provoke hindrances and new
possibilities or create opportunities and dilemmas. Therefore, prototyping can be seen an iterative process,
where the certainty of the prototype often gets more and more speciﬁc during the iterations (Velden et al.
2015).

2.2
The complexity of the client
In order to talk about client requirements, it is crucial to understand the client’s role in a construction project.
According to Boyd et al. (2006), clients see and experience buildings differently from the rest of the indus-
try, thus understanding clients, by understanding what underpins and prompts their attitudes and actions,
becomes critically important to improving the performance of the construction industry (Boyd et al. 2006).
The client is assumed to be a well-deﬁned entity by construction organisations. Thus, the client possesses
well-deﬁned value parameters that can be clearly deﬁned at the outset of the design process. The reality is
however the contrary, where the client is a rather complex phenomenon (Bertelsen et al. 2005). The client can
be deﬁned as the person or organisation commissioning and paying for the design and construction of a facil-
ity. It can also be the user of the proposed facility, or they may be separate entities. However, as the purchaser
of services, the client should represent the interests of users and other identiﬁed persons, groups or organ-
isations who inﬂuence or are affected by outfall of the building project during one or more building phases
such as the acquisition, use, operation, and demolition of the facility. This means that the term client covers a
wide range of stakeholders with different and often conﬂicting values and needs. The client should therefore
be considered as a ’body’ incorporating the interests of the buyer of construction services, prospective users,
and other interests groups (Kamara et al. 2000, 2002b; Thyssen et al. 2010).
The complexity of the client is caused by the different instances of stakeholders within the client body emerg-
ing from the nature of their organisations. Organisations can vary in the size, it’s composition and the nature
of the organisation’s businesses, where the decision to commission a building project is often caused by a
business need in the organisation. Thus the organisational factors creates complexity in determining the re-
quirements of clients, because both the paying client and the user client must be represented within the client,
despite the potential gaps between their needs and desires (Kamara et al. 2000, 2002b).

Lucas (2004) identiﬁes 4 types of complexity. The ﬁrst and simplest form of complexity is static complexity,
which does not change over time. Adding time to the static complexity as a fourth dimension gives the dy-
namic complexity as the second type. The time-perspective allows temporary patterns, but makes it more
difﬁcult to identify the spacial patterns within the system, as these may change over time. The third type of
complexity is related to evolution of systems, where systems become open-ended and unpredictable, as they
may evolve. The fourth and most complex type of complexity combines the internal constraints of closed sys-
tems with the creative evolution of open systems like combining machines and people. Thereby it becomes
impossible to describe this self-organising complexity, without describing how it relates to the wider outside
world (Lucas 2004).
The complexity viewpoint is not only restricted to scientiﬁc areas, but can be applied in many personal and
social situations where complex interactions and difﬁcult decisions need to be evaluated (Lucas 2003). Com-
plexity thinking combines system thinking, organic thinking and connectionist thinking to recognise situations
where one-dimensional representations of the real world does not apply. Systems thinking is used to de-
scribe an interdisciplinary ﬁeld, which identiﬁes common properties across different types of organisations.
Therefore, system thinking investigates generalised or abstract systems, rather than more conventional spe-
ciﬁc forms. Organic systems have a metabolism, which means that they are both self-producing and self-
maintaining. These systems are responsive to their own environment, but unlike the general systems, also
adaptive in behaviour over time, thus evolutionary behaviour and being innovative. Connectionist thinking is
derived from artiﬁcial neural networks in cognitive science, where connections allow information to commu-
nicate across the system causing attractors to form. The style of the system is thus deﬁned by the connec-
tions and not the parts, which allows static, chaotic and organised modes of operation with more dynamical
systems exhibiting mixtures of modes. From the combination of these, Lucas (2003) arrives at a ’type 4 self-
organising complex system’. The main characteristics of a type 4 complex systems are considered to be:
• Responsive - the systems uses selected or relevant environmental data to adapt.
• Edge of chaos state - the systems are neither too responsive (chaotic) nor unresponsive (static).
• Learning - changes in rules or connections are permissible, thus attractor structure can change.
• Multiple equilibria - the history and memory of the system are important to determine current state.
• Path dependence - irreversible choices are allowed be alternative routes, trajectory splitting and merging.
• Self-stability - the system is resistant to perturbation as an overall self-maintaining system.

The complex system is deﬁned based on 18 complex system axioms deﬁned by Lucas (2003). Three of the
axioms describes the general characteristics of a complex systems, while the remaining 15 describes the
systems - the clients - behaviour in terms of general, evolutionary and connective behaviour. The attempt of
the research conducted by Bertelsen et al. (2005) is not to prove that the typical construction client shows
all behaviours expected in a complex system, but to provide an understanding of the often irrational client
behaviour. By applying the perspective of behaviour of complex systems, Bertelsen et al. (2005) argues that
the client in construction should be seen as a complex system, and thus the client’s irrational behaviour can
be expected.

Organisational Semiotics
Tian et al. (2017) deploy semiotics, the theory of signs, to analyse and develop BIM from an information sys-
tems point of view. More speciﬁcally, methods from Organisational semiotics supports BIM in linking building
fabrics to facility service activities (Tian et al. 2017). Organisational semiotics (OS) is a sub-branch of semiotics
and facilitates the understanding of organisations as information systems through using semiotic methods.
26
==PAGE:41==
2 | Background
The focus of the research is to develop BIM for Facilities Management (FM) services with OS and as a result,
the contents of Tian et al. (2017) evolve around this matter. However, the ﬁndings related to OS and how it
provides a structure for services can become a parallel if one thinks about it as requirements for services.
By considering a building as a socio-technical environment and its virtual representations, BIM is a complex
sign system that allows stakeholders to utilise, interpret and interact with. OS provides a framework of six
semiotic levels through which information systems can be interpreted and by acknowledging BIM as an infor-
mation system OS can be applied for interpretation. The six semiotic levels are respectively the Social level,
the Pragmatic level, the Semantic level, the Syntactic level, the Empirical level and the Physical level (Tian
et al. 2017).
Combining OS and the theory of habitat enables an analysis of a building from a semiotic perspective, as
semiotics provides a solid theoretical foundation for stakeholders’ understanding of the characteristics of
sign-based and service oriented building environments according to Tian et al. (2017). Buildings provide func-
tional spaces that enables people to live, work and achieve their goal and thus buildings can be considered
as a habitat (Tian et al. 2017). May et al. (2001) introduces the habitat as a design metaphor to study the
requirements arising from information systems embedded in the physical environment (Tian et al. 2017).
A habitat is described from three different features that are deeply linked together and therefor the impact of all
three must be considered when designing an artefact. The three perspectives are the physical, informational
and pragmatic dimensions (May et al. 2001; Tian et al. 2017). The physical habitat represents the physical
space that addresses how interactions between space and users are dynamic over time, e.g. a partition wall
between two meeting rooms, that can be removed to create one big meeting facility. The informational habitat
supports the inhabitant’s (the user’s) need for information and communication. The pragmatic habitat deﬁnes
the social aspect of a space incl. the potential activities and patterns of behaviour. Thereby it comprises the
expectations and intentions for use of space which might need knowledge and skills to enable use which
would then be governed by organisational norms.

The principles of requirements engineering and management originally stems from Software Engineering,
but it can bring important conceptual contribution to the building industry, and thus it is found applicable
in processing of client requirements (Pegoraro et al. 2017a). Client requirements are normally captured in
the brief, the decisive interactive element that incorporates the client requirements into the building process.
Therefore, if this process can take place in a way that strengthens and develops relationships in a positive way,
there is every chance that the brief will also improve and strengthen the clients business activity (Ryd 2004).
Being the primary source of information for a construction project, an adequate understanding of the client
requirements plays a vital role for the project and the client satisfaction. The complexity of the client makes
processing of client requirements crucial and becomes additionally important, since clients do not necessarily
have a background or previous experience in building design and development and thus their requirements
have a tendency to comprise their objectives, needs, wishes and expectations in non-design terms (Kamara
et al. 2000).
Kamara et al. (2002a, 2000, 2002b) develops the CRPM to support the capture of client requirements in con-
struction projects. By deﬁning levels of requirement as respectively primary, secondary and tertiary require-
ments, a basis for translation of client requirements into solution-neutral design speciﬁcations is suggested.
These speciﬁcations consists of prioritised design attributes and target values, which are required for the de-
sign of the facility in order to satisfy the business need of the client (Kamara et al. 2002a).
Kamara et al. (2002a, 2000, 2002b)’s solution is arbitrary according to Kiviniemi (2005a), as the lower-lovel
requirements are difﬁcult to distinguish from the higher-level. The weighting system suggested in the CRPM
only applies to a small number of requirements, and requirements are seldom determined individually put
based on a combination of them (Kiviniemi 2005a).
As an alternative Kiviniemi (2005b) suggests that a simple active link between client requirement and design
models could minimise the gap between requirements and design models, and to support this the structure of
client requirements is outlined. By distinguishing requirements from the design, production and maintenance
model, the IFC speciﬁcations can be used to link objects together in one instantiated model. The IFC speciﬁca-
tions cannot be applied to link objects together between the Requirements model and the Design-Production-
Maintenance models, because they are different data-sets but by extending the proprietary descriptions this
can be achieved (Kiviniemi 2005a).
OS enables BIM to be analysed and developed from an information systems point of view (Tian et al. 2017).
Taking a FM perspective, service-related information supports the delivery of facility service, and therefore the
BIM-based model should provide information for FM. The habitat provides the context of the services which
leads to the BIM serving as a pre-set service context for FM. The context information featured in BIM can
be classiﬁed into the four dimensions, each a collection of reference information supporting FM activities by
interrelation between service-related dimensions and habitat aspects (Tian et al. 2017).

2.4.2
Compliance checking for client requirements
Pegoraro et al. (2017a) suggest a framework where the requirements processing is spiralling around the life
cycle of the building as the requirements gets more and more speciﬁc. Building on that perspective, Kamara
(2017) questions of how focus on client requirements over the life cycle of a construction project can be
maintained, knowing that it can aid to ensure that the end-result of the project truly reﬂects client wishes.
Kamara (2017) ﬁnds following objectives:
• It is necessary to ensure client requirements do not get confused with contextual factors and that they
are still identiﬁable within the design/project documents in which they are described.
• It is necessary to have a robust system for documenting requirements, tracking and managing change
in requirements and for checking how changes affect design/project decisions and activities.
• The growing use of BIM and other digital project tools suggest that client requirements needs to be
represented in ways that allow interoperability with other design and project applications
Even though literature and research conducted by Kiviniemi (2005a,b) and Kim et al. (2015) present exam-
ples on how requirements management can be applied to construction, Kamara (2017) ﬁnds their research
incomplete, because they present a positivistic view, where it is assumed that requirements are completely
identiﬁable and speciﬁable by disregarding the behavioural dimensions (Kamara 2017). According to Kamara
(2017) this dimension needs to be included in requirements management, where soft facilitation skills are
combined with formal software methods, to be suitable in building projects (Kamara 2017).
Kamara (2017) suggests using a requirements-oriented project process (ROPP) based on the Design Quality
Indicator tool (the DQI) which is an assessment tool, that facilitates the deﬁnition and assessment of DQI’s
and priorities for buildings by a cross-section of project stakeholders. During the research, some shortcom-
ings are found in the use-ability of the assessment tool. First, the tool is missing a contextual link as the
representation of requirements in the brieﬁng record and other outputs focuses solely on the clients needs.
The DQI workshops are time-consuming, and if an insufﬁcient amount of time is used to explore all aspects
of the clients needs, the descriptions of client needs are on only conducted in a superﬁcial level, and does not
contain types and numbers of space and room data sheets.
The organisational factors are only included in the background information by the DQI facilitator and the stake-
holder representatives, and thus it relies on the facilitator to ensure that they are represented in the DQI work-
shops. Therefore Kamara (2017) argues, that while DQI facilitates a representation of aspirations and priorities
of stakeholders, allowing focus on these wished without their contextual focus, the quality of the information
may depend on the quality of the facilitators report (Kamara 2017).
The DQI tool provides a robust mechanism for documentation and tracking of requirements by comparing

of, how changes affect design and project operations on a day to day basis. This can be done by redoing the

to decrease the challenges in the industry governing failure to deliver projects within time and budget, poor
decision management and customer satisfaction.

2.5
Summary
During this chapter, multiple challenges in the industry related to client requirements and design processes
have been identiﬁed. The complex nature of BIM supports multidisciplinary working in theory but in practice,
a proper balance between disciplinary silos and joined models have yet to be found. Berlo et al. (2015) ﬁnds
that the most optimal way to collaborate is by sharing disciplinary models which joined together creates an
aggregated model, containing less data than one shared model would. This means that the tasks of informa-
tion sharing becomes crucial to ensure that all disciplines have the information they acquire available when
they need them (Berlo et al. 2015; Holzer 2011; Treldal 2017).
The complexity of the client adds to this complexity. The client is often misinterpreted to be one entity instead
of the dynamic body of stakeholders that it is in reality. The complexity of the client depends on the complexity
of its organisations and the prior experience with commissioning buildings. These factors makes it difﬁcult to
model the client generically because each project will have an unique client. The client is also assumed to be
stationary and to maintain the same wishes, needs and requirements throughout the design process, which
is non-realistic due to the human evolving nature, where one will learn from experience and conversations.
Therefore, it needs to be considered that clients can and will change their minds during construction projects
(Bertelsen et al. 2005; Kamara 2017; Kamara et al. 2002b).
The client requirements represents the client’s needs, wishes and expectations for a building, and as the client
considered a complex system, their requirements must be assumed affected by this. This means that client
requirements are difﬁcult to capture, identify and specify from the beginning of a design process due to their
evolving nature. The requirements of the client is translated and captured into the brief, which serves as the
foundation of the design process. This means that in order for the design goal to be continuously aligned
with the requirements, the client needs to be able to express their need in a solution-neutral term to avoid a
36
==PAGE:51==
2 | Background
misalignment between the designed and required. According to Kiviniemi (2005a), regardless of the above, a
misalignment between the client requirements and the design solution can occur because the client require-
ments only serves as a starting point of the design process and does not continue into the following design
iterations. These iterations are instead validated based on the previous design solutions, causing the design
to shift away from the required. To solve this matter Kiviniemi (2005a) suggest a simple active link between
requirement and design models, enabling both requirements and design to be changed to reach alignment
between them.
Pegoraro et al. (2017a) suggest that the brief processes should be extended far into the design process, which
allows the client to become a more participating part of the design process. In a similar matter, Kamara
(2017) develops the ROPP framework, where client requirements speciﬁcation, application and documentation
throughout the design process is developed to ensure continuous traceability and compliance between the
required and the designed.
Summarising this shortly following challenges have been identiﬁed:
• The client in a construction project is complex and can be difﬁcult to model generically.
• The client requirements are unique and difﬁcult to capture the essence of within the brief that serves as
’one point of entry’.
• The client requirements are not properly integrated during the design process and thus they are neither
testable nor traceable.
2.6
Solution objectives
Combining these challenges with the context for this research, the solution objectives becomes
An ontology structure for client requirements that supports
• test-ability and traceability of client requirements through the design process
• integration of the client requirements into the design process
To achieve this, the client requirements should be
• listed in separate entities
• easy to ﬁnd and sort through
• adaptable during the design process

• testable during the design process

METHOD
A proper method must be selected to support the exploration of challenges of integration the client and client’s
requirements into the early stages of building design, and to support the development of a method that sup-
ports this integration during design integration. While natural science focuses on why things work the way
they do and ﬁnding new truths, design science investigates how things ought to be in order to attain goals and
function (Geerts 2011). In order to get over the ’stepping stone’ with the research objectives of this project,
one needs to not only understand how things work the way they do, but also to look into how the industry and
work methods ought to develop to improve the processing of requirements.
The way the artefacts are designed and developed depends on the selected design process. Design processes
can vary in terms of what the center of the design are, and thus, which goals or for whom the design should
especially be developed for. As the aim of this thesis is to develop a method supporting computer-human
interactions during design, such design approach should be integrated during the research cycle. Computer-
Supported Cooperative Work (CSCW) is ﬁeld of research, where the individuals roles are played as members
of groups (Shengjun et al. 2008). This is very much like the perception of individuals in this research, where
human beings are not only considered as an individual entity, but also as being embedded into the society in
which they interact.
Therefore, the design processes and interaction between the client entity and project group must be charac-
terised as a group-oriented process with social interactions, where both personal interchange processes and
task-oriented processes are crucial between the client and project group, but also within each of these organ-
isations (Shengjun et al. 2008). The combination of the design science research method (DSRM) and CSCW
ﬁts perfectly for the research activities.
3.1
The Design Science Research Method
Figure 3.1 shows the framework for the DSRM, which comprises 6 activities. The ﬁrst step is problem identiﬁ-
cation and motivation, where the research problem is deﬁned and the value of a solution is justiﬁed. In order
to capture the complexity of the problem it might be helpful to atomise the problem conceptually. By justify-
ing the value of a solution one can accomplish to motivate pursuing a solution, accepting the outcomes and
understand the reasoning associated with researchers understanding of the problem.
Following, the objectives of a solution from the problem solution is inferred by constructing new artefacts. The
new artefact is expected to support solutions to problems which is not addressed to the date. The solution
objectives should be inferred rationally from the problem deﬁnition. In Design and development an artifactual
solution is created by determining the artefact’s desired functionality and it’s architecture, and thereby create
39
==PAGE:54==
3 | Method
the actual artefact. In the fourth step, demonstration, the efﬁcacy of the artefact to solve the problem is
demonstrated. This can be done either by experiments, simulations, case studies and by prooﬁng.
At the evaluation-step, it should be measured and evaluated how well the artefact supports a solution to the
problem. This is done by comparing the solution objectives to actual observed results from the use of the
artefact. At the end of this step, one can either proceed, or return to activity 3 to improve the effectiveness
of the artefact. Lastly, at the step named communication, the problem and it’s importance should be commu-
nicated together with it’s artefact, it’s utility and novelty, the rigour of it’s design and the effectiveness of the
solution (Peffers et al. 2006, 2007).
Figure 3.1: The design science research method developed by Peffers et al. (2006).
Despite the process being structured in a nominally sequential order, it is not expected that research con-
ducted should proceed systematically from step 1 to step 6. On the contrary, almost any step can serve as a
starting point, as the possible entries for research will most likely depend on the approach (Peffers et al. 2006,
2007). Furthermore as the arrows from evaluation and communication indicate, the DSRM allows the process
to iterate back to respectively the step of deﬁning solutions objectives or the design and development-step.
Hevner (2007) illustrates these iterations as three closely related cycles of activities as in Figure 3.2, namely
the Relevance Cycle, the Design Cycle and the Rigour Cycle. The Relevance Cycle bridges the contextual en-
vironment with the design science activities, while the Rigour Cycle bridges the design science activities with
the knowledge base of scientiﬁc foundations, experiences that informs the research project. Lastly, but most
importantly, the Design Cycle iterates between the core activities of building an evaluating the design artefacts
and processes of the research (Hevner 2007).

Figure 3.2: Three cycle view of Design Science Research by Hevner (2007) and Hevner et al. (2004)
The Environment replicates the application context, which does not only provide the requirements for the
research but also generates the acceptance criteria for the ultimate evaluation of the research results, by
asking: “Does the design artefact improve the environment and how can this improvement be measured?”
This provides the framework for pulling back the outputs of the design science research into the contextual
environment for study and evaluation. The outcome of this will determine whether additional iterations of the
relevance cycle are needed in the research project (Hevner 2007).
The Knowledge Base comprises a foundation of knowledge for the research project as it comprises scientiﬁc
theories and engineering methods. Thereby, the Rigour Cycle provides past knowledge to the research project
to ensure its innovation. The rigour cycle provides a theoretical and methodological approach along with
expertise and experience from the research domain, thereby adding new knowledge generated by the research
to the growing knowledge base (Hevner 2007).
The heart of the DSRM is the internal Design Cycle, which iterates between the construction of an artefact,
its evaluation and feedback to reﬁne the design further. Thereby, the design cycle supports a tighter loop
of research activity for the design, development and evaluation of design artefacts and processes (Hevner
2007).

4.1.2.1
Disciplinary systems
The disciplinary subsystems are deﬁned based on traditional disciplines in the industry based on competences

couple of different orders which shows the possibility for ﬂexibility in the future. Additionally descriptions of
the disciplinary systems are found in Appendix B.1
Currently the systems works in following order:
Space 1 →Space 2 →STR1 →STR2 →ME1 →ME2
The disciplinary systems and their functionalities are outlined in the following.
Space
The space system is divided into two subsystems, respectively Space 1 and Space 2. Space 1 deﬁnes the
building envelope based on the number of users and a given ﬂoor to ﬂoor height. It contains different method
to approach the design:
• Envelope from maximum height
• Envelope from number of users
Space 2 deﬁnes the user behaviour and computes room divisions. It is used to see where the users go during
the day, so that the room divisions becomes based on the activity behaviour simulated. Currently this is not
automatic, but based on a slider.
The space-system can superﬁcially take changes into account, but more work should be put into this part of
the system to elaborate on:
• The performance of the rooms and building
• Outline of a few scenarios that can be extracted and compared and provided for the client
Structure
The structural system is divided into two subsystems, respectively STR1 and STR2. STR1 generates non-
structural elements, that are not used directly in the structural computations, but instead generates informa-
tion for the space division. The challenges in the development were evolving around the grid and making it
ﬂexible to a changing space division and how to enable a continuous ﬂexibility in the grid. STR1 also deﬁnes
indirectly that the structural systems must be based on a column-beam system. The subsystem tests for in-
formation from Space 1 and Space 2 in terms of space divisions, and are able to continuous in both presence
and absence of this information.

DISCUSSION
Multiple gaps and challenges related to the integration of the client requirements into the early stages of
building design have been identiﬁed in the industry. The Background identiﬁed the complexity of the client as
one of the main challenges, because it complicates the understanding of the client, the capturing of client
requirements and following, the integration of the client requirements into the design process.
The aim of this study is to develop a schema that contains rules for an ontology for the client requirements
structure. The schema must be able to validate the quality of requirements from a case study. The schema
is developed based on 11080, which serves as the project environment for the artefacts design. The intention
is that the ontology structure can facilitate a smoother transition of the client requirements into the design
process. To do this multiple points of entries have been used.
11080 - Advanced Building Design is considered the project environment, which gives a limited space to per-
form analysis based on. Agile Building Design gave insight in the agile design processes and how requirements
can be linked into programming, in this case by visual programming in Rhino-Grasshopper. The meetings with
CAS gave a hint of reality to the project. The issues, that CAS experienced with, e.g. software being developed
while used, and the less successful interface to the clients, validates the need for supporting the client in the
construction industry.
The intention with the case studies conducted in Agile Building Design was to develop a design system, that
generates design proposals based on as little information and requirements as possible. If the system is
provided information about the use, the budget and the location, the system can generate design proposals
based on theories like e.g. automated space-activity planning and human behavioural pattern. The nature of
the systems enables the clients to make decisions on their requirements closer to the building construction,
while they can also be connected closer to the design processes. This aligns with the suggestions by Pegoraro
et al. (2017a), who suggest that the brief processes should be extended far into the design process.
7.1
Structuring the client requirements
Initially, an attempt to structure the client requirements using organisational semiotics is conducted inspired
by Tian et al. (2017). It is however quickly found not to add any useful dimensions for the purpose of structuring
the requirement.
Therefore, the focus is put on JSON-documents, and the development of rules for requirements by using the
JSON schema. The developed schema provides a set of rules that can be applied to the client requirements
for a case (11080) to assess the quality of the data and information. This ensures the functionality of the

The requirements ontology can make the sometimes complex requirements more readable, however, as it is

7.2
Study limitations
This study has been conducted withing the boundaries of 11080, in terms of requirements. Therefore, the
ﬁndings during the design and development cannot be directly implemented in the real world, but instead
creates a foundation for the future work that may happen in this ﬁeld.
The knowledge base for speciﬁc requirements structures in the building industry is limited in the literature
review, even though a lot of research on requirements engineering in the building industry has been conducted.
The challenge is that these studies remains somewhat conceptual and does not specify the structure of the
requirements in detail or elaborate on how the requirements are processes throughout the project life cycle.
To make the project more realistic, real building projects and their client requirements could have been inte-
grated into the study, while the usability of the schema should be extensively examined.

7.3.1
Linking URI to course home page and generating one point of entry
The combination of URI as ID for the requirements and path to the course home page, where potentially
the project team could ﬁnd more information on that speciﬁc requirements, contains great potentials. First,
the linking to the course home page can be used to develop a method to capture the development of the
requirements and thus ensure the traceability. Secondly, this would mean that all requirements information
are gathered at the same place instead of being scattered in meeting minutes and multiple documents. Thirdly,
a interface towards the client can be developed like the one developed by IM in Agile Building Design 2.0. Here
the client could ﬁnd information governing the project state and development and how the current compliance
with their requirements is based on the design solution. Fourth, the client interface can be extended, to contain
a solution space where the client can enter their requirements during the project. The rules for requirements
can then be used to verify the client requirements, and the design system can work more or less directly from
the requirements.

CONCLUSION
The research objectives of this study was to identify the challenges related to integration of the client require-
ments into early stages of building design. During the literature review in Chapter 2 multiple challenges was
identiﬁed. The client is a complex body consisting of multiple stakeholder, who each has personal opinions,
expectations and wishes for building. However, the client is often misconceived in the industry as a single
entity. Additionally, the evolutionary behaviour of the client is often not considered, and therefore the client
requirements are not expected to change (Bertelsen et al. 2005; Kamara 2017; Kamara et al. 2002b). The
complex nature of the client makes it difﬁcult to capture the client requirements, which is why multiple re-
searches have been developing methods for this during the last twenty years (Kamara et al. 2002a, 2000,
2002b; Pegoraro et al. 2017b). Even if the client did not develop his opinion and needs about the required
building, the compliance between the client requirement and the designed is still not a given. The brief is only
used as an foundation for the design development, while incremental changes are based on previous design
solutions (Kiviniemi 2005a). This can be remedied by a simple active link between the requirements model
according to Kiviniemi (2005a), which then binds the design goal and design solution together. Pegoraro et al.
(2017a) suggests that the brief should be extended into the design development, which will enable the client
to become more clear about the needs and expectations for the building during interaction with the design
team. Kamara (2017) likewise deﬁnes an approach where the client requirements are processed throughout
the project life cycle.
The challenges related to the integration of client requirements into the early stages of building design is thus
related to the complexity of the client, the difﬁculties of capturing and structuring the client requirements
and a misconception of the nature of both client and client requirements. This thesis therefore develops a
set of rules for client requirements with the aim of supporting integration of the client requirements into the
early stages. By deﬁning a clear ontology, the structure of the requirements becomes a ﬁxed measure where
the requirements can be listed into. The rule-set was developed based on the client requirements for 11080
with use of DSRM and JSON and concepts of design systems from Agile Building Design. By combining the
schema with a solution neutral space, where the client and the project team can examine an extraction of
design solutions, the client will achieve a higher feeling of integration. Additionally, the linkage between the
course home page and the client requirement opens new doors for future work.
The integration of the client into early stages of building design cannot be remedied by a simple rule-set, but
requires time and effort in order for it to become successful. Additionally further work is required to develop a
link between the client requirements, as well as interfaces for traceability and testability of the requirements.
