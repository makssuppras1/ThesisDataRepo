ABSTRACT 
In order to improve energy consumption and indoor climate in heritage buildings, internal 
insulation is used in building’s retrofitting. Moisture related problems rise with the increase in 
thermal resistance of old masonry walls. Solutions to avoid moisture problems are subject of 
several studies when external insulation is not an option. Traditional insulation with vapour 
barrier, capillary active systems and facade treatment to avoid driven rain are some of the 
methods used. Recently, a new concept of internal insulation with ventilated air cavity with 
active moisture control due to the need of a safe moisture solution for retrofitting heritage 
buildings in cold climates. The new concept consists of a ventilation performed by a 
dehumidifier unit, which keep the airflow circulating and dry. Nonetheless, noise related issue 
has been reported and previous studies suggest the airflow rate is over dimensioned. In this 
piece of work, analytical investigations in heat, air and moisture transfer were performed to 
find out the minimum requirements for the airflow. Furthermore, analytical and CFD 
simulations, in ANSYS Fluent, were carried out to analyze the airflow distribution, velocities 
and pressure drop. In addition, ducts responsible for air distribution along the wall were 
separately simulated and a method of design it was established. By the end of this project, 
solutions for the lack of airflow under window were developed, simulated and tested. To 
validate the findings in this report, a mock-up test was built in the laboratory of the Technical 
University of Denmark. The final results show that it is possible to have a reduction of 30% in 
the airflow rate and still be moisture safe. A proposal to increase the air distribution under 
window should be implemented to guarantee its drying performance. Additionally, the noise 
related issue in the dehumidifier unit could achieve considerable good sound levels with this 
accomplishment. This method of retrofitting heritage buildings is a promising moisture safe 
solution insulation system. 
 
Key-Words: Retrofitting, internal insulation, moisture, airflow optimization, drying 
performance.

1. INTRODUCTION 
By 1997 in the city of Kyoto, Japan, the world leaders started to be concerned about 
the environment impact of fossil fuel emissions, and, set a goal to reduce the CO2 emissions 
of 1990 by 5%. Due to this agreement many different actions, initiatives and technologies 
were, and are being, developed in order to achieve that goal. Nowadays with the growth of 
renewable energies field, the reduction of CO2 emission could be reduced in a shorter period 
of time. To achieve this not only the use of fossil fuels in industry but also the energy demand 
should be reduced. In Denmark, the goal to be fossil free by 2050, imposed highly restricted 
standards of energy consumption for new buildings, as the housing sector accounts for 40% of 
the total energy consumption of the country [1].  
 
 
Figure 1: Danish building codes from 1961 to present: Maximum allowed energy demand per year per 
m2 to supplied energy for heating, ventilation, cooling and domestic hot water [2]. 
 
In addition, old buildings require high energy demand for heating due to its poor 
level of insulation and airtightness. According to the Danish building regulations, when a 
renovation of an old building is taken place, it must be concerned about thermal and technical 
installations. If the project affects more than 25% of the building surface area, the cost of it is 
over 25% of the latest public property assessment minus land value or the facade or technical 
installations are subject of the refurbishment [3]. Thermal renovation always requires 
additional insulation of walls, that can be done by the exterior or interior of the buildings. 
Many of the buildings from 1850 to 1930 has its heritage value and the facade cannot suffer 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
 
any interference, internal insulation is the only option. Nevertheless, internal insulation can 
develop several issues related to moisture development, as mould growth, wood structure 
decay and health problems for the dwellers.   
Due to this presented problem, a new conceptual model of internal
insulation came up, which is internal insulation with ventilated cavity that it is aimed to solve 
these issues. This new concept still need further investigation and development to be fully 
applied in the housing market. Because of that, a research project of Saint-Gobain ISOVER 
S/A in collaboration with DTU was initiated. This part of the project contains investigations 
of moisture, pressure drop, development and analysis of airflow modelling and experimental 
investigations. 
 
1.1. BACKGROUND 
In this chapter, an overview of the historical buildings and insulation solutions are 
presented. Also, the findings of previous studies about the RetroWall. 
 
 Historical Buildings Construction Aspects 
The buildings that are dated between 1850 and 1930 are the target range for the 
development of new internal insulation methods. Due to the construction aspects, methods 
and materials that were commonly used in these buildings. Furthermore, most of the buildings 
at this time are preserved due to historical reasons. The construction of these dwellings was 
masonry. The last floor would have an outer wall of 1 ½ brick thick and would increase 
downwards ½ brick per floor [27]. A critical area is under window, where the thickness of the 
wall is just one brick. For this reason, the temperature drop in the wall is higher, and the 
indoor climate is poor.  Also, the wood beams are supported in the middle of the outer wall, 
which has low temperatures. Moisture problems can start when insulation is installed on the 
inside. This cold wall is responsible for drafts and radiant temperature asymmetry. Increasing 
the insulation thickness in it, will increases the moisture in the middle of the wall/insulation 
causing several problems. 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
 
 
Figure 2: Beam end in masonry walls 
 
Insulation can be done in several ways. The most common solutions are insulating the 
wall internally and externally. Externally has more advantages as it avoids thermal bridges 
because it covers the whole facade, the wall temperature profile avoids interstitial 
condensation reducing by this the mould growth risk, lower heat loss of the building and the 
renovation process does not interfere in users dwellings. The main disadvantages are change 
of the aesthetic of the facade and the need of public space to mount scaffolding to reach the 
floor. 
 
Internal insulation, on the other hand, preserves buildings’ facade and design. It is 
easier to apply but its construction will disturb dwellers. The disadvantages are creating cold 
bridges in corners, between floors and structure intersections. This method cool down the 
internal wall, and by this moisture related problems can be risen. Finally, it reduces internal 
space. Besides all the disadvantages, internal insulation is the only option for historical 
buildings from 1850 until 1930 with historical value for the society.  
 
 Internal Insulation - Traditional / diffusion closed 
The most common method of applying insulation in buildings, is by adding materials 
with high thermal resistance to the interior wall, such as mineral wool, glass wool and PUR 
that are shown in Figure 3. Internal insulation improves the building thermal envelope, but it 
changes the temperature profile of the wall. The interior wall that used to be warm becomes 
cold, because of the heat gain from the inside, which is blocked by the insulation. This 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
 
method of insulation reduces the internal capacity of drying the wall. Accumulation of 
internal condensation is avoided using vapour barrier, but in the same time, it can create 
interstitial condensation. Moreover, in the case of wind driven rain, the facade gets wet and by 
capillarity water is transported to the middle of the wall, leading to moisture problems. A 
combined solution using hydrophobic treatment could improve this kind of system. 
 
Figure 3: Traditional insulation method 
 
Increasing the thickness of internal insulation improves energy savings, but also 
increase the issues with condensation and moisture related problems due to the drop in the 
temperature profile along the wall [4]. The outer circle in Figure 4, shows that an internal 
insulation with 150 mm has up to five months of risk of condensation. Meanwhile 50 mm has 
no risk, this is explained by the internal drying capacity that declines with the increase in 
insulation thickness. 
 
Figure 4: Condensation and risk of mould growth for 50, 100 and 150mm internal insulation 
[32]. 
 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
 
 Internal Insulation - Capillary active/Diffusion opened 
In this case the internal insulation material used for its construction have high capillarity 
properties. It means that water is driven to the interior of the room, drying it inside or to the 
lowest moisture concentration side. It is a substitution for vapour barriers, but the risk of 
interstitial condensation is high when internal air meets the cold wall [28]. Currently 
discussions are going on regarding whether this solution is more effective than traditional 
vapour barrier or not. Some studies state that internal insulation with high capillarity 
properties works only for thicker masonry walls, while others stated that it is not suitable for 
geographic areas with high precipitation like Denmark. Unless a facade is treated with water 
repellent, to reduce the amount of moisture absorption in it is applied [8]. 
 
 Internal Insulation - RetroWall Concept 
The RetroWall is an internal insulation system, which has an air cavity that is ventilated 
with active moisture control as shown in Figure 5. This technique can be ideal for retrofitting 
historical buildings.  
 
Figure 5: RetroWall System 
 
The system blows the air inside of 25mm air gap from the top. The air is collected on the 
bottom part of the wall and dried out in the dehumidifier. The system is composed by an 
airgap of 25 mm followed by 50 mm of insulation with mineral wool, a 16 mm plywood 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
 
board and finishing with a 13 mm gypsum board, as shown in the Figure 6. The structure of 
the system is supported by horizontal steel brackets (23x100 mm) which have a 40% 
perforated area allowing the air to go through. The air supply and extraction is done on the top 
and bottom parts of the wall by steel ducts (54x110 mm). 
 
 
Figure 6: RetroWall cross-section 
 
The moisture control is performed by a dehumidifier, which is developed by Cotes A/S, 
which keeps the relative humidity about 50%, preventing moisture related problems such as 
mould growth and wood decay. It also heats up the air by 5 to 10 degrees Celsius, before 
returning air into the cavity. The dehumidifier unit is split into two section regarding airflows, 
the process and regeneration. The process air is the airflow within the air cavity, this creates 
an airflow that is responsible for keeping the air cavity dry. The moisture that is removed by 
the processed air is then dried into a desiccant wheel. The regenerating airflow is responsible 
to dry the absorbed moisture in the desiccant wheel, see Figure 7. 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
 
 
Figure 7: Dehumidifier unit design 
 
The dehumidifier is connected to sensors fixed in several parts of the wall, including 
critical places like under window. It runs every two hours for 5 minutes with an airflow of 16 
m3/h to get a sample of the air and measurements of the relative humidity in the cavity. In the 
case the relative humidity is above 65%, it runs until the set point of 55% humidity is reached 
[7]. The RetroWall has been proven to be suitable for retrofitting historical buildings, as has a 
capacity to solve the moisture related problems of internal insulation, but this system requires 
further investigation in the airflow distribution. The system has been studied by other research 
projects, and the airflow rate seems to be over dimensioned. Additionally, it has been facing 
critics regarding noise level of the dehumidifier unit. The first step is to analyze how the 
airflow rate can be reduced without affecting the air distribution, which is the subject of this 
thesis. 
 
1.2. LITERATURE REVIEW 
Previous studies of RetroWall have been done by Saint-Gobain ISOVER A/S in 
collaboration with DTU. Kjersti Fosso master thesis [6] studied the challenges regarding dry 
air distribution under and around window. Also, it focused on moisture related effect on 
wooden floor construction and in separating t-walls. The conclusions were: 
Part 1: Moisture development in Delphin 
• Airflow of 1.2 m3/(m2.h) was more than enough to remove the required moisture 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
 
• Average diffusion into the airflow in the gap: Winter: 0.22 g/(m.h) / Summer: 
0.58 g/(m.h) 
• Average required drying of airflow in the gap: Winter: 0.15 g/kg / Summer: 0.40 
g/kg  
Part 2: Airflow 
• Vapour diffusion in still air is not sufficient enough to keep RH 70% underneath of 
window and its airflow is approximately 20% of the main airflow. 
• Initial air velocity beneath the window: 0.007 m/s. Air velocity with sealed 
perforations: 0.016 m/s >> required velocity to keep RH below 70% 
• Pressure drop in the system is calculated and measured to be approximately around 6 
Pascal while if the system is installed in the middle of the wall it will give about 1 Pa 
less pressure drop to the system. 
Part 3: Dehumidifier 
• Drying capacity of dehumidifier is nearly 50% greater than the drying demand when 
desired RH is 50%.  
• Winter: 0.32 g/kg > 0.15 g/kg  
• Summer: 0.68 g/kg > 0.40 g/kg 
• The airflow of 1.2 m3/(m2.h) is large compared to the necessary demand, which means 
the decrease in airflow due to the external pressure exceeding the fan capacity with 1 
Pa will only result in a little higher increase in RH along the wall.  
The research of Anton Øbærk [7] studied the cases where the RetroWall system was 
mounted in apartments and offices with sensor in order to analyse and obtain necessary data, 
as well as see overall performance of technology. In the case of Varde Office system V1 
(East-facing with window), obtained measurements showed that temperature, relative 
humidity and moisture content did not reach the levels needed to develop mold growth. Some 
results demonstrated the RH level over 80%, but this abnormal result could be due to fact that 
measurements were obtained on a cold day and not for long period of time. By initial 
calculations it was found that in order to develop mold high levels of humidity should be 
present for at least 32 day period. By analyzing the moisture content graph it was stated that 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
 
measurements were distributed evenly, showing that all the places where the sensors were 
placed had similar measurements. Moreover, the dehumidifier successfully removed between 
0.2 g/kg and 2 g/kg of moisture and the overall RH was below 75%. 
Some adjustments were made, for example humidity levels that initiated the activation of 
dehumidifier were changed from 65% to 55%, this adjustment was made as the equipment 
had a 10% difference between the sensor and the data. Also humidity levels that resulted in 
dehumidifier turning off were changed from 55% to 45%. Exchange of the desiccant wheel of 
the dehumidifier had a positive effect on overall measurements the RH.  Anton Øbærk [7] 
concluded that the system is able to maintain the RH below 80% for a full thickness wall and 
also supplied air through 20mm holes had enough capacity for wood beams. The zone under 
the window with just a brick thick required the biggest moisture removal. The dehumidifier

 Moisture related problems 
Insulating internally could lead to mould growth and moisture problems in the structure

can be faced. If relative humidity is kept under 80% mould growth is assumed not to arise [9]. 
One method for evaluation of mould growth is by comparing the number of days that

Celsius, an average of 30 days running should be overcome.

 Thermal bridges  
Thermal bridges are known as the weakest point in a building envelope regarding thermal 
aspects. It can lead to leakage of heat, air and moisture. The heat loss not only affects the 
building energy consumption but also the temperature profile of the walls, which may develop

walls, wood beams structure laying in the wall, wall to ceiling or floor and windows to wall, 
as shown in Figure 10. According to the passive house institute [12] when thermal bridge 
losses are smaller than a limit value, set at 0.01 W/m.K, it is defined as thermal bridge free 
design. One way to avoid or reduce thermal bridges in T-Walls is by insulating the corner at

2.2. FLUID DYNAMICS  
A basic understanding of fluid dynamics is needed for the investigation, simulation and 
analyzes of the airflow within the cavity. 
 
 Static, Dynamic and Total Pressure 
Static pressure is the fluid’s pressure at rest. For ventilation systems, it is the resistance 
that the airflow needs to be overcome in order to be in movement, hence used for fan design. 
While dynamic pressure is the pressure created by fluid velocity and density. Figure 11 shows 
how these pressures are measured in a pipe [24].  For incompressible fluids the sum of static 
and dynamic pressure is the total pressure or stagnation pressure. 
 
Figure 11: Static, Dynamic and Total Pressure 
 
 Pressure drop – Major and minor losses 
The difference in total pressure in two fluid’s point is the pressure drop [24]. Pressure 
drop can occur due to friction, fluid obstruction, or fittings in ventilation system. Major loss, 
is caused by the interaction of fluid and a surface, i.e. friction loss [25]. The sliding resistance 
result losses and it is related to fluid flow, velocity, roughness, duct geometry, length and 
density. 
Minor losses are the ones related to components in the piping system [25]. Duct 
connection, diffusers, elbow, flow through hole, each of these fittings have a minor loss 
coefficient which is applied to the fluid’s velocity. 
 
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
 
 Types of flow 
Flow regime can be classified as laminar, transitional or turbulent according to its 
Reynols number, which is the relation between inertial to viscous forces, Figure 12. Whether 
if a flow is laminar or turbulent, the Reynolds number is the relation between inertial to 
viscous forces. For values lower than 2300 it is stated as laminar, over 4000 turbulent and 
between these limits it is transitional [15]. Sometimes due to high roughness, obstructions and 
cross section thickness, turbulence can be developed earlier than the stated limits [14]. 
Differently from laminar flow, whereas the path lines are parallels and following a pattern, a 
turbulent flow has fluctuations and disorder in it. The disorder in the fluctuations is the 
turbulent intensity. Turbulent intensity is the rate between the standard deviation of the 
turbulent velocity fluctuations by the average velocity at the same place and period of time 
[21]. Meaning that an ideal airflow without fluctuations has 0% of turbulence intensity. 
Moreover, there is no top limit as the average airflow velocity can be very small and have 
large fluctuations. Furthermore, turbulent kinetic energy is related to the turbulent intensity 
and average airflow velocity.

 What is CFD? 
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is a method in software used to analyze fluid 
flows or heat transfer by means of mathematical modelling, numerical methods and software 
tools. It enables scientist and engineers to perform computer simulations, analysis and 
interpretations of different variables for an investigation as boundaries conditions, inputs, 
geometries with less labor work and time wise. There are many commercially available CFD 
codes as ANSYS Fluent, COMSOL and PHOENICS that are based on using finite volume 
approach. The objective is solve the problems by using the Navier-Strokes equations 
governing fluid flow and heat transfer problems [14].  
 
 Solver models in CFD 
CFD has been gaining popularity in buildings simulation, to analyse ventilated cavity, 
double skin facade and chimneys. To solve and simulate these elements, common turbulence 
models need to be implemented. K-Epsilon, RNG k-epsilon, k-omega and SST based models 
offer a good balance between accuracy and computational costs [18]. RNG k-epsilon is 
considered to be more accurate than k-epsilon for low turbulence models [16]. K-omega 
solver is stated as a good choice to capture small swirls, and it is appropriate for laminar, 
transitional and turbulent models. The SST option is able to capture the transitional regime 
flow [17].

5. CFD MODELLING AND INVESTIGATION 
The use of computational fluid dynamics software, ANSYS FLUENT, was implemented 
in order to validate the assumptions which were described previously. The analysis of the 
parameters in a CFD tool gives a better understands of the airflow. This chapter has five 
models. Model 1 investigates a section of the air cavity where a brackets obstruction is 
presented. The supply air duct is subject in model 2, which will have as parameters duct 
section, hole size, and whether the duct is acting as a pressure chamber or not. This concept is 
crucial for giving an equal air distribution through the wall length. Third model is a duct to be 
placed under window, in this case a lower pressure drop is desired. Model 4 is the analysis of 
the air distribution under window, using duct of model 3 and other solutions. And finally 
model 5 is the whole wall airflow analysis, in which the airflow distribution can be analyzed. 
All of these models might open suggestion for improvements, which can lead to a reduction 
of airflow rate, improvements in air distribution and mounting aspects. 
 
Before creating a CFD model the mounting of the RetroWall system needs to be 
explained in a deeper detail. To create the airgap, horizontal steel brackets are attached into 
the brick wall, 40% perforated. Metal ducts, model 2, are fitted on the top and on the bottom 
of the wall, to supply and exhaust the air from the dehumidifier. Figure 18 illustrates a section 
of the wall, the red highlighted section is the subject of model 1.

Model 2 – Supply air ducting system 
This component of the RetroWall have been tested and it satisfies the requirements for 
the system.  Its cross-section and geometry were developed first by Maria Roca [31] and 
second by Anton Ørbæk [7]. Roca held some calculations for the duct design and hole sizes.

calculations were taken in place. It was analyzed if and which size of duct cross section, 
would guarantee it to act as a pressure chamber. The dimension of 54x110 mm was the final 
design and several experiments confirmed its performance for the system.  
The modelling of the duct in CFD facilitates the simulation of diverse scenarios of 
airflow, holes size, airflow velocities, distribution and pressure drop. The reduction in the 
width would be a good achievement as nowadays insulation layer around the duct is very 
poor. Due to this reason, the goal was to design a duct with the same thickness as the airgap. 
This not only gives the opportunity to increase the insulation thickness, but also makes the 
buildability faster and simpler. It was explained in the previous chapter that the smaller the 
holes, the more pressurized the duct becomes. As consequence of that a higher pressure drop.

7. DISCUSSION OF THE INVESTIGATION 
The RetroWall system has being proven to be a safe solution for internal insulation 
regarding moisture related problems. The main focus of this thesis was the investigation of 
the airflow distribution and optimization. Previous studies found that the minimum airflow 
velocity under window should be 0.016 m/s and that the airflow rate of 1.2 m3/(m2.h) exceeds 
the demand. 
It was found out in chapter 3, regarding moisture balance, that the airflow rate could be 
theoretically reduced from 1.2 m3/(m2.h) to 0.2 m3/(m2.h), which, would give an equivalent 
airflow rate of 3.2 m3/h instead of 16 m3/h. This significant difference suggest that it requires 
a deeper investigation in the coupled air and moisture simulations and experiments. The fact 
that the dehumidifier runs for just 5 minutes every 2 hours strengthens the idea of reducing 
the airflow rate. In model 5 of chapter 5, it shows that an airflow rate of 3.2 m3/h would not 
give enough air distribution and minimum velocities under window, where is the critical area 
it is. Therefore, a new airflow of 11.2 m3/h was found and simulations were performed. 
The starting point of the investigation was the duct cross section, which should be 
evaluated regarding its air distribution and pressure drop. A method of designing has been 
developed. The width of it could be reduced for the same size of the air cavity (25 mm). This 
achievement not only facilitates the mounting but also reduce the amount of material in its 
production. Several hole sizing and airflow rates were performed so the system could be 
dimensioned for different moisture drying requirements. The method proved to be efficient 
and returned a well distributed airflow along the duct and the holes. The dimensioned ducts 
and holes are design in a way the total pressure drop of ducts, brackets, friction and fittings 
are not over 4 Pa besides the dehumidifier unit. 
 The brackets were also investigated in terms of pressure drop and mounting directions. It 
was found that the change from horizontal to vertical could avoid the pressure drop, but the 
mounting procedure was not satisfactory when tried out in the mock-up test. Therefore, no 
suggestion or improvement has been achieved regarding it.  
Solutions to improve the airflow distribution under window were subject of investigation 
in CFD. The inclusion of an extra duct under the window, and crossing the wall length was 
made it. This duct would not have any supply source, but just redirected the airflow to the 
desired part. Different outlets design were simulated, and two of them were tested in the 
mock-up build in the laboratory of DTU. First the measurement of the standard wall, was in 
==PAGE:85==
85 
 
 
evidenced that there is a lack of airflow under it. The airflow velocities could not be measured 
due to the limitations of the anemometer, in other words, the airflow velocity was smaller 
than the minimum required. The results for the proposed solutions were very promising and 
even though some of the results could not be measured, the overall experiment was 
satisfactory. The solution with the best performance was model 4.2 and 4.5 called jet flow. It 
had a significant increase in airflow velocities under window, and comparing to the standard 
configuration of the wall, velocities could be measured in any location. Furthermore, the 
model 4.3 and 4.6 had the best balance and efficiency regarding airflow distribution and 
pressure drop. The drying performance is strongly related to the airflow velocities, the higher 
the velocities the higher the drying capacity it is. Even though, the airflow rate was reduced, 
the solution to increment the airflow distribution under window stated to increase its 
velocities. Hence the drying performance was increased. 
In the last chapter, it was presented the findings in a new dehumidifier design by Dessy 
Harjani. The promising results depends on the airflow optimization and pressure drop of the 
system. The airflow of 11.2 m3/h, with the extra supply air duct under window not only meets 
the requirements regarding moisture and air distribution, but also achieved a desired noise 
reduction. It went down from 39 dB to 25 dB.

8. FUTURE WORK 
The report had a combined theory, software modelling, simulation and experiment 
related to the airflow distribution. ISOVER has some concept test apartments which are 
running relative humidity measurements monthly, therefore the findings in this report could 
be implemented and evaluated. The main suggestions are to reduce the airflow rate to 11.2 
m3/h and the implementation of an extra duct under window. The model with combined jet 
flow and perforations is more desired. Focusing in the airflow of 11.2 m3/h, it could also be 
subject of coupled heat, air and moisture investigations and simulations with the state of the 
art software. The dehumidifier unit operations time should be considered when changing the 
airflow rate. The duct cross section and fittings could be optimized in order to reduce the 
pressure drop, instead of sharp turns, it could be done smoothly. And the duct cross section 
width for 300 x 25 mm. Laboratory experiments could be performed to measure the pressure 
drop in the system with the modifications.

Appendix C – CFD Inputs Calculations 
 
This appendix will describe the model validation for turbulence, and furthermore the 
parameters used to simulate the five models of this thesis. 
 
Geometry 
The models were modelled in Design Modeller of ANSYS Workbench. 
 
Boundary Conditions 
In order to generate good results, appropriate boundary conditions must be applied. 
Each model has its own specifications, but the most important are inlet velocity, 
symmetry/wall and outlet. 
 
 
Turbulent models Validation

geometry was decomposed. This is done, in order to insert sweepable method, hexahedral 
elements, and automatic method, tetrahedral elements. Table 1, shown the mesh data.

To validate the model mesh, different sizes of mesh elements were compared. It is 
noted that for models with elements reduced by 10 times, the maximum turbulence intensity

Figure 54: Mesh 
 
The present model with RetroWall system had an increase in the inlet velocity in order 
to test the turbulent models. Even though up to 2300 Reynolds the flow is considered laminar, 
[22] presented studies where turbulence or transitional regime can be observed in lower 
Reynolds number depending on the section and the flow distribution. Since dimensions of the 
airgap thickness is significantly smaller than the other two dimensions, and the flow 
obstruction due to the brackets, the model is considered turbulent. To guarantee the condition, 
a laminar model was executed and no convergence of results was reached.  
A next step in model validation before running all the scenarios, was the turbulence 
model to be used. Among the options in ANSYS Fluent, the following were compared, RNG 
k-epsilon, k-omega-SST or transition SST. The k-omega was used with the option of low 
Reynolds flow and which suits the model. The k-epsilon is commonly used for fully 
developed turbulent flow which is not the case. A comparison of these three has shown that k-
omega-sst and transition SST had similar results, therefore k-omega-sst was preferably. 
 
The comparison of the turbulence intensity streamlines in the middle plane through a 
hole in the brackets is shown in Figure 55. It can be seen that the model k-epsilon has the 
higher value and the most turbulent flow, and the other two are more equivalent.   
 
==PAGE:101==

Figure 55: Streamlines turbulence intensity for different models 
 
The output of these simulations with different models gave the understanding that k-
omega-sst and transitional SST are more sensitive to roughness and airflow velocity changes, 
regarding turbulence intensity. And for lower airflow velocities the model k-omega-sst 
reacted better through different roughness levels. Generally, the k-epsilon seemed to suffer 
only with air velocity changes, not being suitable for the model in which roughness is desired 
to be accounted. Figure 56 shows the turbulence intensity, energy and total pressure in each of 
the models described.
