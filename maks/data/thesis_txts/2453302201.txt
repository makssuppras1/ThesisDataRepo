Abstract
In this project an absorber unit was designed to be operated with a pre-existing
desorber unit to construct a mini pilot for CO2 capture using amine scrubbing. The
absorber was constructed to operate at atmospheric pressure with an adjustable
height for data gathering with varying parameters.
An experimental campaign was conducted using three diﬀerent monoethanolamine
(MEA) solvents, two of them having a Vapour Reducing Additive (VRA) to inves-
tigate the eﬀect of VRAs on the eﬃciency of the desorption process. The solvents
consisted of 30%wt MEA in water which was used as a benchmark with the VRA
containing solvents having 15%wt of the chosen VRA as well as 30%wt MEA in
water. The chosen VRAs were urea and ethylene glycol. All solvents were loaded
with CO2 to have a minimum loading of 0.5 molCO2/molMEA.
Experimental results show the MEA solvent containing ethylene glycol to be very
similar to an MEA solvent without VRAs in terms of energy consumption while
experiencing less heat loss through the desorption column. The MEA solvent con-
taining urea proved to be less attractive, as a much higher energy consumption was
observed than for the other two solvents with no gains in terms of CO2 release.
This solvent also caused problems by forming a clot in the condenser, where it was
not expected to be present at all.

Introduction
The Earth is getting warmer every year and it is generally accepted that the main cause
of this rapid change is caused by humans in what is called the greenhouse eﬀect. Indus-
trial plants, transport, and deforestation, among other things, all contribute towards an
increased amount of greenhouse gasses in the atmosphere. These greenhouse gasses then
retain the heat radiated from the sun and the Earth in the atmosphere, causing it to
get warmer. The main greenhouse gasses in the atmosphere are water vapour, methane
(CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2) as well as nitrous oxide (N2O) while other gasses are
present in much smaller amounts[1]. The amount of water vapour in the air is the most
abundant of the greenhouse gasses, but it has a low residence time in the atmosphere so
it is not assumed to be a main cause for global warming[2]. The increased amount of
CO2 and CH4 in the atmosphere is thought to be the main contributing factor to global
warming, and most measures taken to combat this trend is limiting the emission of those
gasses into the atmosphere[1].
The concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere is thought to have increased by a third
since the beginning of the industrial revolution due to the burning of fossil fuels and
deforestation[1]. Among the measures taken to combat the emission of CO2 into the
atmosphere is to create engines that more eﬃciently consume the fossil fuels and thus
emitting less CO2 for each unit of fuel consumed, such as in industry and transport.
Another measure is changing the source of electricity and power from a fossil fuel based
source and to a renewable source, such as electric powered cars. These green power sources
include solar power, wind power and hydro power but many others are available[3].
Although it is much less abundant, CH4 is thought to be an even more active greenhouse
gas than CO2. It is emitted by livestock, waste landﬁlls and agriculture and as a result
the amount of CH4 emitted will increase as the population increases due to the nature
of these sources[1]. Natural gas can be used as a power source, as natural gas consists
mostly of CH4, and so this more active greenhouse gas can be burned to release CO2
instead[4].
By turning potentially more active greenhouse gasses into fuel for burning and release the
less active CO2 instead, the rate of global warming can be reduced. This can be done by
producing biofuels from biomass[3]. Biofuels are produced from contemporary sources,

such as manure and plants, and can be considered a renewable source, unlike fossil fuels
which come from a ﬁnite source. Another beneﬁt from producing biogas from sources
such as manure is that it can then be used as fertilizer afterwards. This fertilizer is more
beneﬁcial as it removes the methane emissions which is emitted from unprocessed manure,
and reduces the nutrient runoﬀfrom the fertilizer so less chemical fertilizer is needed to
compensate.[3] As the amount of fossil fuels available is ﬁnite, the production of biofuels
has increased to meet the demand for fuel while providing a more green option at the
same time. There are various types of biofuels available, such as biogas, biodiesel, syngas
and bioethanol which diﬀer in composition and method by which they are produced[5].
Biogas is produced by anaerobic digestion, in which waste such as sludge or agro-industrial
waste are converted to a gas containing mostly CH4 and carbon dioxide CO2. The ra-
tio of these two components depends on how it is produced, such as the feedstock used
and the pH inside the reactor. Typical concentrations vary between 50-70% CH4 and
30-50% CO2 with trace amounts of other compounds[5]. These other compounds include
nitrogen (N2) at concentrations of 0-3% most likely originating from air saturated in the
inﬂuent, oxygen (O2) at 0-1 % concentration from inﬂuent substrates or due to leakages,
water (H2O) at concentrations of 5-10% resulting from the evaporation of the medium
with even higher concentrations at thermophilic temperatures[4]. Other compounds that
could be present in the biogas are hydrogen sulfate (H2S) at 0-10000 ppmv concentra-
tions, ammonia (NH3), hydrocarbons at 0-200 mg/m3 and siloxanes at 0-41 mg/m3[4].
Of these components only CH4 is desired in the biogas, with all the other gasses being
considered pollutants and should be removed or reduced as much as possible. Both NH3
and H2S are toxic and corrosive, causing damage to metal parts and heat and power
units due to emission of SO2 produced by combustion[4]. Siloxanes can transform into
silicones or silicone oxides inside the machinery, reducing the eﬀectiveness and eventually
causing breakdowns due to increased temperature and friction, by forming a paste on the
walls of the machinery or by precipitating inside[6]. Other compounds, such as CO2 and
N2, reduce the Lower Caloriﬁc Value (LCV) of CH4. At STP conditions the LCV of CH4
is 36 MJ/m3 but is only around 20-25 MJ/m3 at CH4 concentrations of 60-65%[7].
The undesired compounds can be removed by diﬀerent treatments, with each treatment
targeting certain compounds for removal.
One treatment is commonly referred to as

biogas cleaning and is intended for the removal of toxic or otherwise harmful compounds
such as NH3, H2S, and siloxanes. Biogas cleaning mainly targets H2S, with many biogas
plants employing H2S removal units. These units commonly use aerobic sulfate oxidizing
bacteria but other methods can be used as well, such as physical removal by absorption
and chemical removal using iron to form solid precipitates[4]. Another treatment is called
biogas upgrading which is aimed to increase the caloriﬁc value of the biogas.
1.1
Biogas Upgrading
If the biogas is intended as a replacement for natural gas it is necessary to convert it to
a higher fuel standard[7]. This is achieved by using a biogas upgrading treatment on the
biogas, either by removing the CO2 present or by reacting it with H2 to convert it to CH4.
When the biogas has been puriﬁed to the speciﬁcations of natural gas it is referred to as
biomethane[3]. The speciﬁcation for natural gas composition can vary between countries
and is typically 88-90% on a yearly average in Denmark[8]. In 2015 there were more than
17,400 biogas plants in Europe in 2015, with Germany being the largest producer [3].The
types of biogas upgrading treatments are either physical or chemical, with the former
removing the CO2 while the latter converts it to CH4 by hydrogenation. In 2015 there
were 459 biomethane plants, with Germany again being the largest producer[3].
There are many diﬀerent methods employed for biogas upgrading, some of the more
commonly used methods will be described below.
1.1.1
Absorption using water scrubbing
The most common method for biogas cleaning and upgrading is water scrubbing. As
the solubility of CO2 in water is approximately 25-26 times higher than CH4, it can
be absorbed at a much higher rate to remove it from the biogas without sacriﬁcing a
large amount of CH4. This method uses an absorption and a desorption column with a
ﬂash unit in between. The biogas enters the packed absorption column at the bottom
at 6-10 bar pressure and a temperature of 40°C, with water being injected at the top
of the column.
By running the biogas and water in counter-current it is possible to
achieve a higher removal rate of CO2 while keeping the energy consumption and CH4
3
==PAGE:13==
August 1, 2019
absorption as low as possible. The water is then lead to the ﬂash unit, where the pressure
is decreased to 2.5-3.5 bar to recover any traces of CH4 that were absorbed as well as
a small amount of CO2. The gas is recirculated, compressed and mixed with the biogas
entering the absorption column. The water is sent to the top of the desorption column
with air coming in from the bottom. By operating the column at atmospheric pressure,
the CO2 is removed by decompression. The amount of water ﬂow needed to upgrade 1000
Nm3/h of biogas is in the range of 180-200 Nm3/h depending on the water temperature
and pressure. After a drying step it is possible to achieve up to 99% purity of CH4[5].
If H2S is present in the biogas when it enters the absorption column it will be absorbed
into the water as well. Depending on its concentration, it may be necessary to use steam
or idle gas in the desorption to prevent the formation of elemental sulphur[5].
1.1.2
Absorption using organic solvents
This method is run using the same principle as water scrubbing, except using an or-
ganic solvent instead of water. The most commonly used solvents include methanol and
dimethyl ethers of polyethylene glycols but other solvents can be used, such as N-methyl-
pyrrolidone. The advantage an organic solvent oﬀers over water is that the CO2 solubility
is about 3-5 times higher in the organic solvent than in water. This means that less liquid
can be used for the process and the size of the units can be reduced[5].
The solvent enters the absorption column at 20°C and 6-8 bar pressure at the top. The
solvent exiting at the bottom of the column is used to heat the solvent coming in at the
top to keep the absorption column at 20°C. The CO2 containing solvent then enters the
ﬂash unit and its pressure is decreased to release the CH4 contained within. Prior to
entering the desorption column the solvent is heated to temperatures up to 80°C. This
is due to the high solubility of CO2, which makes it more diﬃcult to regenerate the sol-
vent unless the temperature is raised. If H2S is present the temperature may need to
be raised even higher, as the solubility of H2S may be signiﬁcantly higher than CO2 in
some organic solvents. The solvent is then put in at the top of the desorption column at
atmospheric pressure. This method returns a biogas containing up to 98% CH4[5].

1.1.3
Absorption using amine solvents
Also called amine scrubbing, it is a chemical absorption method to remove CO2 from a bio-
gas using aqueous amine solvents. Commonly used solvents include methyldiethanolamine
(MDEA), diethanolamine (DEA) and monoethanolamine (MEA). A common amine sol-
vent used is a mixture of MDEA and piperazine (PZ). The addition of PZ increases
absorption capacity of the MDEA, as it has a high reaction rate to CO2 and can react
it even further with the MDEA. The solvent has a low heat of reaction and thus makes
the regeneration more energy eﬃcient[5].
The system consists of an absorber and a desorption column equipped with a boiler. The
solvent enters at the top of the absorber at 1-2 bar pressure and temperatures of 20-40°C
and removes the CO2 from the biogas coming in from the bottom in an exothermic re-
action while completely absorbing any H2S as well[5]. The reaction mechanism is still
unclear, although there have been made several studies to attempt to reveal it[9]. The
solvent leaving the absorber has therefore been heated to 45-65°C and is sent to the top
of the desorption column.
The reboiler in the desorption column is operated at 120-160°C, it both provides the
required heat of reaction for CO2 realease from the amine solvent and it lowers the par-
tial pressure of CO2 by producing steam. The lower partial pressure of CO2 improves
the kinetics of the desorption. The desorption column is operated at a slightly higher
pressure than the absorber, usually 1.5-3 bar[5].
The solvent leaving the desorption column needs to be cooled down before entering the
absorption column. This is done partly by using heat exchange with the solvent leaving
the absorption column, but the cooling is not suﬃcient and as a result an additional
cooling unit is required. This method returns a biogas containing concentrations of up
to 99% CH4. The amine solvents have the downside of being toxic, potentially expensive
and some loss can be expected due to evaporation[5]. Some solvents, such as MEA, are
corrosive at high temperatures and can therefore cause a maximum of 1 mm corrosion
each year to carbon steel in a plant removing not less than 1 ton of CO2 in an hour[7].
The advantage they provide is that the reaction is very selective and as a result the loss
of CH4 can be less than 0.1%[5].

1.1.4
Pressure swing adsorption
Pressure swing adsorption (PSA) separates gasses by their physical properties.
The
gasses are separated due to their molecular characteristics and the aﬃnity of the chosen
adsorbent material.
Commonly chosen adsorbents have a large surface area such as
zeolites and carbon molecular sieves. Under high pressure, gases will be attracted to the
solid surfaces and thus be adsorbed on the surface. When the pressure is decreased, these
gases will again be released[10].
The PSA process is thus split into four steps. In the ﬁrst step, the biogas is compressed
to 4-10 bar pressure and fed into the adsorption column as the second step. When inside,
the adsorbent material will retain the unwanted components of the biogas (CO2, N2, etc.)
while CH4 passes through and is collected at the top of the column. When the adsorbent
is saturated the pressure is decreased to release the entrapped gasses, this third step is
called blow-down and is a desorption step. The CO2 rich gas is recycled back to the PSA
inlet due to CH4 being adsorbed in the process in some amounts as well. To prevent too
much gas buildup in the system, a ﬁnal purge step is added to the process. Typically,
more than one column is employed to ensure a continuous process is employed while one
column is being regenerated. [10] This process can achieve an upgraded biogas with up
to 98% purity of CH4, but the loss of CH4 can be up to 4%[5].
This method is simple and safe, with regards to both energy cost and capital investment
being low as well as the equipment used can be made compact. On the other hand,
the previously mentioned CH4 loss can be disadvantageous as well as its sensitivity to
H2S. If H2S is present in the biogas when it enters the adsorption column it will bind
irreversibly to the adsorbent material[5].
1.1.5
Membrane separation
By employing a membrane with selective permeability to retain CH4 while other unde-
sired gasses are permeated through, it is possible to upgrade biogas without the use of
chemicals or water. The biogas is cleaned and then compressed to 5-20 bar pressure and
injected into the membrane unit. Inside, the CO2 will diﬀuse to the side of the membrane
with lower pressure while the CH4 will stay on the higher pressure side of the membrane
unit. The biogas needs to be cleaned to remove undesired components, such as H2S or
6
==PAGE:16==
August 1, 2019
water, as they can corrode the membrane[10].
The diﬀusion of CH4 through the membrane is quite high, up to 15% depending on the
type of membrane used. Because of this, more designs for membrane separation have
been made. Instead of a single step separation it is more common to employ a two step
separation, either with a recirculation loop or a swing, or even a three step separation. In
a two step sweep setup, the stream exiting from the second membrane is recirculated and
mixed with the inﬂuent gas and fed into the ﬁrst membrane as a sweep steam. Typical
concentrations of CH4 in the biogas after treatment in membrane separation are around
95%, but can go as high as 98%[5].
All the methods described above are of the CO2 removal type. This is due to them
being more developed and thus more readily available and cheaper as a result.
The
methods which convert CO2 to CH4 are still in development and not as readily available
as the previously mentioned methods. These methods are of great interest however, as
CO2 would be converted to an energy containing compound at atmospheric pressure and
moderate temperature levels. If these methods become widely used they could greatly
contribute to a sustainable bio-based economy, as much less waste is generated if CO2 is
converted to CH4 rather than being separated to be used for other applications.

Methane recovery in a combined amine absorption and gas steam boiler
as a self-provided system for biogas upgrading
This research was submitted from the University of Nanjing in 2018 by H. Gong, Z.
Chen, et. al.[12] By operating a pilot plant with an amine absorption unit and a gas
steam boiler, they sought to determine the energy consumption of amine absorption as a
biogas upgrading method. The need to do such a research was due to amine absorption
often being considered an energy intensive method due to additional heat being required
to strip CO2 from the amine solution, but the data to back up the assumption was scarce.
The goal was thus to compare the energy consumption in operating an amine absorp-
tion plant and compare the results to other biogas upgrading plants using other methods.
The amine solution used consisted mostly of MDEA (40 wt%) but also contained smaller
amounts of DEA (3 wt%), PZ (7 wt%) and additives such as erosion inhibitors and
antioxidants. The biogas was acquired from an on site fermentation plant using mostly
food waste and municipal sludge. The content of the biogas consisted of 62% CH4 and
37% CO2, with H2S being removed to less than 15 mg/m3 via alkali absorption before
being fed into the amine absorption plant.
Figure 2: Schematic diagram of the setup used for amine absorption by H. Gong, et. al
As displayed on ﬁgure 2, the feed gas enters the absorption column at the bottom while
the amine solution enters from the top through spray nozzles. The decarbonized gas

carbon rich amine solution enters the desorption column at the top and trickles down to

Problem Statement
This project aims to develop a technology which can upgrade biogas to biomethane while
also producing high purity CO2 (bioCO2). The chosen method is amine absorption, con-
sisting of an absorption and desorption column using an amine solvent. As amines are
liquid base components which are miscible in water, it is possible to have them react
with the acidic CO2 gas to remove it from the biogas. The amine absorption method is
feasible due to its eﬃciency as it requires much smaller machinery as compared to less
eﬃcient methods, such as the much less eﬃcient water scrubbing method, although an
amine solvent has the disadvantage of being toxic to humans.
The amine scrubbing setup will be as described in section 1.1.3, a mini pilot consisting
of an absorption and a desorption column. The desorption column as well as the reboiler
constructed for the project conducted by K. H. Lejre and K. Fjellerup will be used in this
project as well as some measuring equipment available on site. Other parts will have to
be constructed or purchased speciﬁcally for this project.
The amine most commonly used as a base case for the research of CO2 capture and
storage is MEA, making it typically chosen for research of this kind. MEA has a typical
energy consumption of 4 GJ/ton CO2 removed when used as a solvent. Newly developed
amines have shown the potential to reduce the energy consumption by up to 30% less
than MEA, 2.7 GJ/ton CO2 removed, making it desirable to develop an amine solvent
that consumes less energy than MEA.
This project aims to test and validate new additives in the constructed mini pilot plant.
The new additives are called Vapour Reduction Additives (VRA) and carry the potential
of reducing the energy consumption in biogas upgrading as they are designed to decrease
water vapour pressure. For this reason they are expected to decrease the vapour pressure
of other solvents, such as amines. By introducing VRAs to the amine process it can
have many beneﬁts as the amine process will no longer have the same amount of water
vaporization during the amine recovery, the requirements for condensation will not be
the same and the reduced water content will reduce the amount of drying needed for the

this, the equivalent length method is employed. This method allows the estimation of
pressure loss through pipe ﬁttings such as elbows and tees as a length of the pipe. Al-
though it may not be as accurate as other methods, it is very simple to calculate pressure
drop using this method[15].

Results and Discussion
This section will present the results of the experiments carried out with a discussion of
the results for each experiment set done individually. The results will then be compared
to each other with a discussion on the result of the comparison.
4.1
Experimental Plan
Before experiments could start, the whole setup was investigated by running water
through it.
This was done to check for leakage and to verify that all equipment is
operating correctly before MEA is introduced to the system. This initial check was im-
portant due to the setup not having been used for some time prior to this project. This
was followed by running a solvent containing 30% MEA in water through the system in,
to verify if the condenser and relief valve were running correctly and to decide on the
boiler temperature to be applied to the system as well as the desired pressure.
The experiments were run using three diﬀerent solvents: 30% MEA in water, 30% MEA
and 15% urea in water, and 30% MEA and 15% ethylene glycol in water, all percentages
are a weight percentage. Each solvent was doped with CO2 to achieve a loading of 0.5
molCO2/molMEA. Each solvent was fed into the system, starting with the 30% MEA
in water solvent, and the setup allowed to operate to achieve a steady state while having
the same conditions applied to them, to have comparable results. During the steady state
the temperature proﬁle of the column was measured as well as the outlet ﬂow of gas and
the lean solvent ﬂow and its loading. To investigate the energy released in the system
the ﬂow and temperature change of the cooling water is measured and compared to the
energy consumed by the boiler, as measured by the energy meter.
All experiments were carried out following the procedures proposed by K. H. Lejre and
K. Fjellerup [11] excluding steps for components which were not present during the ex-
periments. The full list of steps is shown in Appendix B.

4.2
Experimental Results
4.2.1
Pre-experimental runs
Running water through the setup revealed some leaks in various parts of the system,
most of which could be ﬁxed by the tightening of bolts. A leak was also discovered in two
hoses, the hose ﬂowing solvent through the hose pump and the inlet hose from the water
bath to the heat exchanger. Both hoses needed to be replaced before experiments could
start. It was also discovered that the outlet hose for the cooling water from the condenser
was too short to reach the sink and was thus replaced with a longer one. The ﬂow in-
dicators were observed to not be functioning correctly, as the ball inside to indicate the
ﬂow would frequently get stuck. It was however determined that neither ﬂow indicator
was essential to the intended measurements, as the ﬂow of condensate was very small and
had negligible eﬀects on the liquid level in the boiler compared to the rich solvent ﬂow
and thus the rich solvent ﬂow could be measured manually from the lean outlet ﬂow.
It was also noted that the outlet ﬂow of water from the boiler had a green colour. This
was assumed to be either due to corrosion or solvent that had remained in the setup after
a previous experiment. By measuring the pH of the green water it could be determined
if the colour was due to corrosion or solvent, as the liquid would be basic if solvent was
present. A litmus test revealed the liquid to be acidic, making corrosion the likely culprit
for the colouring of the water. This resulted in the need to verify if the heat exchanger
had been corroded to a point where it was not usable due to corrosion. As the boiler
had already been conﬁrmed to be working it was not needed to verify if corrosion had
compromised it. By injecting compressed air into an inlet to the heat exchanger while
blocking the corresponding outlet it was possible to determine if corrosion had damaged
it too much, as air would then ﬂow from the opposite side of the heat exchanger and
would result in hot water mixing into the solvent during the experiments and thus com-
promise the experiments. This did not happen and so the heat exchanger could be used
for experiments without worry. Water was allowed to ﬂow through the setup until the
green colour had disappeared.
The batch run with 30% MEA in water showed everything to be running correctly in
the system, so a continuous run immediately followed. The pressure and temperature

4.2.4
Runs with 30% MEA and 15% ethylene glycol in water
A solvent containing 30%wt MEA and 15%wt ethylene glycol in water was prepared and
loaded with CO2. The resulting solvent had a density of 1.1325 g/ml, corresponding to
a loading of 0.51 molCO2/molMEA. The density/loading correlation can be seen in
Appendix C. The previous solvent, the 30% MEA and 15% urea in water, was drained
from the setup after cooling down to room temperature in the same manner as was done
previously.
The same preparations were done for carrying out experiments as had been previously
been done with the other solvents, the boiler at 120°C and the water bath at 80°C. During
the ﬁrst run it was observed that the pressure on the system was rising higher than in
the two previous cases, with the gauge pressure rising above 1.5 kp/cm2, while keeping
the same temperature. It was initially thought to be due to the properties of the solvent
and thus a hose pump able to operate at higher temperatures was borrowed from DTU
building 228 to run the experiments for this solvent.
This was not the case, as the gauge pressure rose to 3 kp/cm2 and the borrowed pump
could not operate at such a high pressure. There had been some boiling of liquid initially
but it stopped as the pressure rose higher, due to its boiling point now being above
the boiler temperature.
It was now observed that, although liquid should have been
present, there was no liquid being pumped through the condensate pump at all and thus
suggesting a clot somewhere in the system. The safety valve was opened to attempt to
relieve the pressure on the system, but no gas ﬂow was detected even if the valve was
opened fully. These two problems suggested something was very wrong in the condenser
and thus it was disconnected from the system to allow for a closer look to identify the
problem. Upon looking into the condenser it was immediately obvious that urea had
found its way into the condenser.
Urea was not expected to be found in the condenser, as it was not expected to evaporate
with the rest of the solvent. The possible explanations for the presence of urea in the
condenser are either that the urea was being evaporated along with the rest of the solvent
48
==PAGE:58==
August 1, 2019
or that it was somehow being carried into the condenser as it was being fed at the top
inlet of the desorber. As the system was allowed to cool down before being drained the
urea had returned to its solid state in the condenser and formed a clot in the narrow
tube leading from the condenser to the condensate pump. As no problems had been
encountered while running experiments the day before, the clot likely formed between
the last run with the urea containing solvent and the ﬁrst run with the ethylene glycol
containing solvent. When the new solvent was introduced it likely ﬁlled the condenser
with liquid and carrying the urea from its bottom into the safety valve as well, as urea
was found to be blocking the safety valve as well.
After cleaning the condenser and the condensate outlet from it the condensate pump and
relief valve were tested and veriﬁed to be back to working shape, as pressure could now be
controlled and the condensate ﬂowed easily back to the desorber. Proper experiment sets
could now be carried out in the system but due to time constraints it was only possible

5
Conclusion and Future Work
The main objective of this work has been to design and construct a laboratory scale
absorber unit to be operated with an already existing desorber unit. The combined mini
pilot was to be run ﬁrst with an MEA solvent to optimize the conﬁgurations to the mini
pilot before MEA solvents containing diﬀerent VRAs are employed to investigate their
eﬀect on the eﬃcency of the CO2 absorption process. Although the construction of the
absorber unit could not be ﬁnished in time, the diﬀerent MEA solvents containing VRAs
could be investigated using the desorber unit.
5.1
Conclusion
A laboratory scale absorber unit was designed at the AT-CERE Research Centre, a part
of DTU’s Department of Chemical and Biochemical Engineering. The absorbtion col-
umn was designed to have an adjustable height, consisting of seven elements that can be
removed or added as desired. Solvent is able to be fed into the column from diﬀerent
heights and samples can be taken from diﬀerent heights as well.
Due to problems with choosing the right parts needed to operate the unit as desired,
combined with a long delivery time of some of these parts, it was not possible to fully
construct the absorber unit in time for experimental runs to be carried out in the time
allocated for this project. The absorbtion column itself, along with the tank and piping,
was fully constructed in cooperation with the workshop located in DTU building 228.
The remaining construction required before the absorption unit can be operated consists
of connecting the experimental equipment to the piping and make adjustments to the
desorber unit so that it can be connected to the absorber unit.
Experiments were carried out using the already existing desorber unit, investigating the
eﬀect of the eﬀect of the addition of VRAs to an MEA solvent. Due to optimism in being
able to fully construct and operate the absorber unit the experimental campaign started
late, with only 5 complete measurements after it had been veriﬁed that all parts to be
used with the desorber unit were working as intended.
56
==PAGE:66==
August 1, 2019
Three diﬀerent solvents, with a CO2 loading of at least 50molCO2/molMEA, were used
for experiments. The ﬁrst solvent investigated was a 30%wt MEA in water solvent. This
solvent contains no VRAs and served as a benchmark for the other two solvents, which
both contained a VRA. The second solvent investigated consisted of 15%wt urea and
30%wt MEA in water while the third solvent consisted of 15%wt ethylene glycol and
30%wt MEA in water. These three solvents were compared by observing their temper-
ature proﬁle, CO2 release and energy consumed/released to determine if there was a
signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the eﬃciency between the solvents.
The temperature proﬁle of both the VRA containing solvents showed much more promise
than the temperature proﬁle of the MEA in water solvent, having a much smoother curve
and less decrease in temperature.
The change in loading was very similar for the MEA and MEA/urea solvents, while the
MEA/ethylene glycol solvent could not be compared with the other two as easily due to
a diﬀerent solvent ﬂow rate. The results nonetheless suggest that a very similar change
in loading is expected of the three solvents at the same conditions.
The urea containing solvent both consumed and released more energy than the other
two solvents, with the energy released being close to ten times more than the energy
released by the other solvents. The energy released by the MEA and MEA/ethylene gly-
col solvents were very similar, with the former releasing around 20J/s less than the latter.
From these results it seems that the MEA/ethylene glycol solvent is more appealing than
the MEA/urea solvent, due to its lower energy consumption/release, but more tests should
be carried out to conﬁrm this. The MEA/ethylene glycol and MEA only solvents seem
to closely resemble each other but more tests should be carried out before determining
if one is better than the other, as the temperature proﬁle of the former is better, mainly
due to the similarities in their energy consumption/release. The problems encountered
using the MEA/urea solvent, in which the urea found its way into the condenser and
clogged the system after cooling down, may not be encountered in a larger scale plant
if the dimensions of the tubes are wider but its energy eﬃciency compared to the other
two solvents does not make it look like a very appealing option for use in a process where
energy consumption is desired to be kept low.

The results can be considered somewhat inconclusive due to the low number of exper-
iments taken. Although the results may give some idea as to what to expect from the
solvents, both VRA containing solvents should be tested further to conﬁrm these results.
5.2
Future Work
The construction of the absorber unit can be ﬁnished and made ready for experiments.
After a veriﬁcation of all equipment being in working order it should be possible to run a
campaign in which the parameters of the mini pilot are optimized for an MEA solvent to
reach a balance in which energy costs are to be kept low and CO2 absorption eﬃciency
is kept high.
When the optimal parameters have been determined for the mini pilot it will be in-
teresting to investigate diﬀerent VRAs under the same conditions. This will allow the
absorption potential of each solvent to be observed with the desorbed amount of CO2,
as a higher absorption of CO2 is desirable to increase the eﬃciency of the process. A
desirable solvent containing VRAs would have a lower energy cost and/or higher CO2
absorption than a comparable solvent without VRAs.
If the results from the mini pilot show enough promise,it can be scaled up to pilot scale
and operated using a solvent observed to have the potential of a higher CO2 absorption
eﬃciency and/or lower energy costs, with a long term goal of having a full scale plant
able to eﬃciently reduce the carbon footprint of humans.
The CO2 gas stream from the condenser is not being harvested in the current design but
arrangements could potentially be made to harvest it into a container. This would result
in the biogas not only being converted to biomethane but also the removed CO2 could
be prevented from being released to the atmosphere and be used as a raw material for
a diﬀerent factory process. Although such an adjustment may not be needed on a mini
pilot scale, it might be desirable to investigate if such an adjustment should be made if
a larger scale plant is constructed.

B
Procedure for running the desorber unit
Below is displayed the full list of procedures for running the desorber unit designed by
K.H. Lejre and K. Fjellerup [11]
Starting the unit
When starting up the system, the following procedures need to be followed to ensure that
the system is started properly.
Fill the constant temperature water bath with demineralized water and turn it on.
Check that at least one of the following valves are open: V3, V4, V5 or V6
Check that V1, V2, V13, V14 and V15 are closed.
Fill the feed tank with rich loading.
Empty the lean loading tank.
When the constant temperature water bath reaches the desired temperature, turn on the
hose pump circulating the hot water.
Turn on the feed pump, to pump MEA-solution into the system.
When the heating unit in the boiler is covered with liquid, turn it on. Start with a tem-
perature under 100°C to slowly build up the pressure and the temperature.
Turn on the water to the condenser.
Check that at least one of the following valves are open: V7, V8, V9, V10, V11.
Check that V12 is open.
Turn on the condensate pump.
Open V14 to let the lean loading out from the boiler.
Turn on the CO2 probe.
Check the ﬂow of the lean loading by collecting the stream for 10 seconds. This ﬂow
should be equal to the rich loading feed ﬂow. Also check through the sight glass, that
the liquid level is constant.
Running experiments
When the system is running, it is ready for sampling. During experiments the following
procedure should be followed:
Check that the boiler temperature is of the desired size and stable. This can be corrected
66
==PAGE:76==
August 1, 2019
by turning the heating element up/down.
Check that the feed temperature is of the desired size and stable. This can be corrected
by changing the ﬂow rate on the hose pump circulating the hot water.
Check that the pressure is of the desired size and stable. This can be changed on the
pressure relief valve.
Check that the CO2 concentration in the exit gas stream is stable.
Check that the temperatures in the column are stable.
Take samples from feed and the outgoing stream, and analyse these with the barium
chloride method.
Note down the temperatures, the pressure, the ﬂows and the concentration.
Change the boiler temperature, the feed temperature or the pressure, according to the
desired level for the next run. Alternatively the feed or the condensate location can be
changed.
Start again from the top to run a new experiment.
While running the experiments, the following things needs to be checked regularly in
order to ensure that everything is running smoothly:
