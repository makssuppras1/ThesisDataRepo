Abstract
This thesis investigates how to implement a coupled Finite ElementВӯBoundary Element
algorithm for electromagnetic scattering problems. To this end the basic steps of the FEM
are derived and presented alongside the chosen FEM framework of FEniCS, which is an
open software suite to solve PDEs with the FEM. FEniCS is tested on a simple problem
for familiarization and the results here of are presented.
Furthermore, the MoM is derived for an 2D object, namely the case of TMz scattering of
a dielectric cylinder. This is done with both piecewiseВӯlinear and constant basis function
as well as different schemes of enforcing boundary conditions. The boundary integral
equation is derived and its components are characterized in terms of the arising weakly
and strongly singular integrals.
The combined FEMВӯBEM system is then presented and formulated and characterized by
their contribution to the solution of the coupled system. They are then implemented using
the methods derived in earlier parts of the thesis, and a model problem is solved and
analysed. The solver shows some potential for the interior solution of the total electric
field, while the total electric field outside the chosen scatterer requires more work.

Introduction
Electromagnetic scattering problems arise in many different areas of electromagnetic field
modeling, such as in remote sensingНҫ the design of satellite antennas and their base staВӯ
tions requires fast and efficient simulations to reduce the overhead spent on measureВӯ
ments. Two of the most commonly used methods in computational electromagnetics to
numerically solve Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) are the Finite Element Method
(FEM) and Boundary Element Method (BEM). While each can be applied to open and
closed Boundary Value Problems (BVPs), they have their advantages and disadvantages.
Both methods are gridВӯtype methods, representing the problem domain by a mesh over
which basis functions are defined to fulfill certain properties.
The FEM directly transforms the underlying differential equation into a set of linear equaВӯ
tions, the BEM takes the intermediate step of first transforming the differential equation
into a integral equation over the boundary of the domain. This is done by means of an
analytically known fundamental solution, which usually is the GreenвҖҷs function of the corВӯ
responding problem. The boundary is thus discretized. The unknown on the boundary is
represented by its own mesh, transforming the integral equation into a system of linear
equations.
Utilization of the BEM thus results in a problem where the dimension is reduced by one,
resulting in fewer unknowns. The BEM is also highly suitable to model open boundary
problems by implicitly satisfying the radiation conditions at infinity. However, it suffers
from creating full, dense matrices requiring more storage and computational effort. FurВӯ
ther, it suffers from difficulties in modeling problems with inhomogeneities and nonlinear
differential equations, as the fundamental solutions are only available for homogeneous
and linear problems. Volume Integral Equations can overcome this but tend to even larger
systems with dense matrices.
On the other side, the FEM produces sparse matrices. It is well suited for closed boundary
problems involving inhomogeneous media. Still, it suffers from the need to discretize the
full problem, which is unsuitable for problems with large regions of free space. Methods
exist to overcome this shortage, such as absorbing boundary conditions and perfectly
matched layers, but increase the mathematical complexity of the problem.
Thus, large electromagnetic scattering problems where structures of many different maВӯ
terials and scales provide problems involving both inhomogeneities and the scattering or
radiation into an unbounded domain suggest a combination of the two methods. PreviВӯ
ous work in studying this coupling has been and still is an area of research laying the
foundation for the work in this Thesis.
First, the model problem of the thesis is derived in chapter 2 with relevant field equations
for both background, scattered, and total fields as well as the subsequent boundary conВӯ
ditions. In chapter 3, the mathematical foundation for the FEM is provided by overviewing
the basic steps taken in deriving a FEM formulation [1], [2], [3]. This involves the familiarВӯ
ization of the FEM in a 2D framework, introducing both triangular and quadrilateral mesh
cells as well as nodal and edgeВӯbased finite element formulations. Furthermore, the FenВӯ
ics project is introduced as the chosen FEM framework of the thesis [4], [5]. The Fenics
project is an openВӯsource python/C++ suite for solving PDEs with the FEM, providing an
efficient assembly of FEM system matrices for the coupling algorithm. An example of the
utilization and syntax of Fenics is given in the case of a generalized eigenvalue problem.

The Finite Element Method
The objective of this chapter is to introduce the Finite Element Method. These basic conВӯ
cepts allow us to solve electromagnetic problems using the FEM and derive the necessary
FE system equations for the larger coupled FEMВӯBEM framework we desire to implement.
Furthermore, this chapter introduces the Fenics project, which is an openВӯsource computВӯ
ing platform utilizing the FEM for solving PDEs with highВӯlevel programming and matheВӯ
matical abstraction.
In section 3.1, an introduction to the basic steps of the FEM is given, where the procedure
of solving a BVP from discretizing the computation domain to solving the final system of
equations is derived. This further includes an introduction to different types of FE formuВӯ
lations as well as their basis functions.
In section 3.2, the FEniCS Project is presented. The use of FEniCS is then established
by working through an example of its utilization on the generalized eigenvalue problem
found in waveguide cutВӯoff analysis.
3.1
Basic Concepts of the FEM
The FEM seeks to solve boundaryВӯvalue problems (BVPs) which arise in the mathematical
modeling of physical systems. A BVP is defined by a governing differential equation in a
domain в„Ұas
LП• = f
(3.1)
together with the associated boundary conditions on the boundary О“, where L is a differВӯ
ential operator, f is a known forcing or excitation function, and П• is the unknown function
to be solved for. The principle of the FEM is to replace a continuous domain with a number
of subdomains, referred to as elements, whose behavior is modeled by interpolating basis
functions containing a finite number of unknown values at the nodes of the element. The
original BVP, a problem with infinite degrees of freedom, is thus converted into a problem
with a finite number of degrees of freedom. A set of equations or a system of equations
is obtained by assembling all domain elements, and the solution to the BVP is obtained
by solving this system. Thus, the overall steps of the FEM can be summarized as:
1. Discretizing the domain.
2. Choice of interpolation functions.
3. Formulation of the system of equations.
4. Solution of the system.
In the following sections, we will describe these steps in additional detail.

3.1.3
Formulating a System of Equations
The third step in the FEM involves formulating the system of equations to be solved. The
two predominant methods for accomplishing this are the Ritz variational and Galerkin
methods. In Sec. 4.1, we will derive the MoM system of equations using the Galerkin
method. Therefore, we will proceed to derive the systems of equations in FEM in a generВӯ
alized manner using RitzвҖҷs principle. The overall procedure transforms the original differВӯ
ential equation into a variational, or weak, formulation, which can then be approximated
by FEM. This involves multiplying the differential equation by a trial function, integrating
over the domain, and applying the boundary conditions. This overarching approach is
general and applies whether discussing the Ritz or Galerkin method.
RitzвҖҷ Variational Principle
The Ritz variational method is for discretely solving partial differential equations based
on the calculus of variation, meaning that instead of working with functions, it works with
functionals. A functional map functions to a scalar, whereas a standard function maps
a scalar to a different scalar. Similarly to functions, these can be used to find stationary
points, i.e., points that describe function spacesвҖҷ minima, maxima, and saddle points. InВӯ
stead of finding the number that, for example, minimizes a function, it finds a function that
minimizes the error of the approximation to the real solution. We illustrate the procedure

3.1.4
Solution to Systems of Equations
Solving the system of equations to obtain the solution set is the final step of the FEM.
Typically, the systems of equations can be categorized into two forms:
KП• = b
or
AП• = О»BП•.

In the context of electromagnetics, the first, a deterministic type of system, arises from
scattering or radiation problems, generally involving a source or excitation. The second

vanishes, and K is expressed as AвҲ’О»B, where О» represents an unknown eigenvalue to
be determined. Once the solution П• is obtained, we can compute the desired parameters
and suitably visualize these. Many methods are available to solve the system, falling into
categories of being either direct or iterative. As with many other aspects of the FEM, the
choice of a proper solution algorithm is problem dependent.

The Boundary Element Method
This chapter aims to introduce the chosen implementation of the BEM with relevance to
electromagnetics, namely, the MoM. The BEM is said to provide the overarching frameВӯ
work for dealing with problems defined by solutions over the boundary, but its specific
implementation can vary based on the requirements at hand. One such implementation
is the MoM.
In section 4.1, an introduction to the derivation of the MoM is presented, detailing the
procedure of solving an unknown function due to a known excitation function. This is acВӯ
complished using the Galerkin method, which contrasts with the Ritz formulation used
in the previous chapter.
Furthermore, it introduces concepts of numerical integration
and schemes for formulating the system of equations, either through point matching or
Galerkin testing. It also introduces piecewise constant and linear basis functions within
the 1D framework, which is applicable to the boundary.
In section 4.2, we derive the boundary integral equation for the model problem of TMz
scattering from a dielectric cylinder. This derivation employs the equivalence theorem to
model the currents on the cylinder. The integral equation is then examined and characterВӯ
ized by its two terms, reflecting the weak and strong singularities arising from the scalar
GreenвҖҷs function.

6
Conclusion
The objective of this Thesis was to study the implementation of the coupled FEMВӯBEM
system and the solutions provided by the system for electromagnetic scattering problems.
To this end, development of a coupled system using the openВӯsource software suite of
FEniCS for the FEM part and an selfimplemented MoM solver was conducted.
First, a model problem for the case of TMz scattering for a dielectric cylinder was studied
in chapter 2. The derivation here laid the ground work for the implementation of the MoM
solver and was also used as a model for developing meshing algorithms related to the
FEM. In chapter 3 the FEM in 2D was studied with an emphasis on triangular mesh cells
with both nodal and edge based basis functions. The FEM was implemented for the case
of a cutoff frequency analysis problem for a metallic hollow waveguide using FEniCS as
a FEM solver. This allowed for the familiarization with the use of FEniCS for different
functions spaces and types of elements to achieve a better and more complete overall
solution.
After this the theory of the MoM algorithm was derived and presented in chapter 4 as a
mean to obtain a BEM for the coupled system. The MoM equations were established
with both piecewise linear and constant basis functions on its boundary. Furthermore, the
boundary integral equation for the model problem was derived, and its contents in form
the of the arising strongly and weakly integrals were characterized in order to deal with
them in the numerical implementation.
Finally, in chapter 5 the coupled system were assembled based on the formulation given
by [1]. Several mapping algorithms were developed along side the assembly of the FEM
and BEM matrices respectively. Solutions were achieved of varying quality compared to
an analytical solution from a cylindrical wave expansion. For the case of a cylinder with
Пөr = 2 the field pattern inside the cylinder were similar to that of the analytical solution,
although there were varying magnitudes. Also the only comparing the solution on the
boundary revealed a case where while some recognition in the pattern, the frequency beВӯ
tween the two solutions were different. The imaginary part was deviant, more likely due
to an implementation error. The scattered field did not show similarities to the analytical
solution, epspecially due to a missing scattering at the front of the cylinder. The deviatВӯ
ing solution to the normal derivative of the field on the boundary might suggest that an
extra look is taken on the assembly of the MoM matrices, especially the Q which directly
affects this solution the most. For the case of the setting the dielectric cylinder and the
background medium almost equal, the plane wave excitation of the background medium
were recovered with slight deviations.
The thesis set out with a goal of using the implemented FEMВӯBEM solver to simulate
different scattering scenarios involving inhomogeneous objects and possibly other strucВӯ
tures and characterize the use of the solver in a more detailed sense than what has been
achieved. The solution presented in thesis is the most promising achieved yet, but was
also achieved late in the process. Should the project continue the focus should be on
working on the scattered field outside the medium and possibly fix this problem, before
more in depth convergence studies and error analyses should be conducted. Then finally,
the work could go on to studying various geometries and scenarios of interest, also with
a focus of trying to speed up the solver as a computational aspect.
