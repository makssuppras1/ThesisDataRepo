Abstract
The Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducer (CMUT) has shown to be a promising
technology to improve the existing ultrasonic technology. It has superior properties compared
to piezoelectric transducers, including higher bandwidth, better design ﬂexibility, lead-free and,
more eﬀective energy coupling to and from ﬂuids. Especially time-resolved volumetric imaging
(4-D imaging) is of considerable interest in combination with the CMUT technology, since the
2-D arrays for 4-D imaging is cumbersome to fabricate in piezoelectric materials.
This thesis presents a novel process for fabrication of 2-D CMUTs based on a local oxidation
of silicon (LOCOS). A 62 × 62 2-D array was successfully fabricated utilizing two SOI wafers,
wafer bonding, and only 4 mask steps. Too reduce the amount of electrical connections needed
to contact the device, a row-column addressing scheme was used. This reduces the amount
of connections signiﬁcantly without reducing the imaging capabilities. The CMUT array is
designed to use the built-in 190 V DC power supply and ±75 V AC excitation voltage of a
bk3000 scanner from BK Medical, enabling the scanner to produce real-time volumetric imaging
using the row-column probe.
Characterization results from the fabricated devices demonstrated the advantages of imple-
menting the LOCOS process such as: few lithography step (4), completely insulated electrodes,
reduced parasitic capacitance, high oxide breakdown voltage (900 V), and oxide-free top plates.
Impedance measurements were used to characterize the electrical performance. The coupling
coeﬃcient was seen to have increased by 28% by utilizing the LOCOS process from the previ-
ous design. No mobile charges were observed in the device as no decrease of performance was
seen over 10 hours in both positive and negative polarity. An oﬀset between the two polarities
was, however, observed. The oﬀset was only 3 V and was suspected to originate from positive
trapped charges in the cavity due to dangling bonds.
The successful ﬁndings of this work lead to an abstract submission to the 14th International
Workshop on Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducers, Dresden, Germany.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Ultrasound for medical imaging was ﬁrst used in the middle of the 20th century, and now is a
common technique for imaging of fetuses, internal organs, vessels etc. Ultrasound imaging has
traditionally been performed with piezoelectric transducers, but these have some limitations.
Impedance mismatch between the piezoelectric transducer and the medium limits the energy
transfer, causing a problem for ﬂuids, thus making this type of transducer ineﬀective for medical
imaging. To improve the eﬃciency a matching layer is placed between the transducer and the
medium, but this improved energy coupling reduces the bandwidth [1].
Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducers (CMUT) has shown to be a promising tech-
nology to improve the existing ultrasonic technology. It has superior properties compared to
piezoelectric transducers, with higher bandwidth, better design ﬂexibility, lead-free and, be-
cause of the low impedance of the CMUT membrane, sound waves can eﬀectively be coupled
to and from the ﬂuid [2].
Especially time-resolved volumetric imaging (4-D imaging) is of
considerable interest in combination with the CMUT technology, since the 2-D arrays for 4-D
imaging is cumbersome to fabricate in piezoelectric materials.
1.1
Ultrasound
Ultrasound is sound with a frequency above the upper limit of human hearing, i.e. above
approximately 20 kHz.
Sound is mechanical energy that needs a medium to propagate in.
In contrast to electromagnetic waves, sound cannot travel in vacuum. The frequency range
for medical imaging lies between 1 MHz and 20 MHz. The two simplest forms of waves that
can run in solids are longitudinal waves, where the movement is in the same direction as the
propagation, and transversal waves, where the movement is in a plane perpendicular to the
propagation. In water and soft tissue, the waves are mainly longitudinal. The frequency, f, of
the wave propagating through the medium is related to the wavelength, λ, and the velocity in
the medium, c
λf = c.
(1.1)
Ultrasound is generated by a transducer ﬁrst sending an acoustic pulse into the medium in a
given direction, and thereafter detecting the acoustic echo generated by the tissue. The echoes
carry information about the acoustic properties of the medium. The echoes that travel back
to the transducer arise from two phenomena, reﬂection and scattering. Both of these phenom-
ena take place when the emitted pulse reaches an interface between two media with diﬀerent

acoustic properties. In general, reﬂections arise when the interface is relatively large compared
to the wavelength, and scattering when the interface is small relative to the wavelength. When
the acoustic pulse is reﬂected, typically only a small part of the wave is reﬂected, the remaining
part being transmitted through the interface, and is nearly always refracted. When the emitted
pulse travels through the medium, some of the energy will be absorbed, hence transformed into
heat. The loss of intensity from the forward propagating acoustic wave due to reﬂection, scat-
tering and absorption together is called attenuation. The attenuation of the acoustic pressure
wave along the propagation direction can be written as
I(x) = I0e−αx
(1.2)
where I(x) is the intensity of the pressure wave at a point x along the propagation direction,
I0 is the intensity at a reference point in space and α is the attenuation coeﬃcient. α depends
on the type of tissue and is approximately proportional to the frequency of the wave. Another
important characteristic of ultrasound for imaging purposes is the resolution, which can be
understood as how small structures can be visualized while still being able to distinguish them
from each other. This is commonly referred to as spatial resolution telling how closely two
diﬀerent types of tissue can be located and still being able to distinguish the signals. Resolution
depends on the scanning frequency, i.e. when increasing the frequency the resolution is also
increased. An increase in the frequency increases the attenuation coeﬃcient as well, decreasing
the penetration depth of the ultrasound. Thus, choice of frequency is a compromise between
resolution and penetration depth.
1.1.1
Medical Imaging
Ultrasonic imaging is a non-invasive imaging method used for visualizing internal body struc-
tures, and is harmless, as opposed to X-ray and CT scanning. Other advantages include that
ultrasonic imaging provides images in real time, is portable in the sense that it can be brought
to the bedside of the patients, and is relatively inexpensive compared to other medical imaging
methods. Drawbacks are that it is diﬃcult to create images behind bones and air due to the
big impedance mismatch to tissue, that it is very dependent on the skill level of the operator,
and the resolution is not as good as CT, X-ray and MRI.
The most well known type of ultrasound images is the B-mode (brightness-mode) image. This
imaging method creates a two-dimensional cross-sectional view of the tissue being imaged, and
is obtained with a one-dimensional transducer array. A sketch of a 1-D transducer is shown
in Figure 1.1(a). The elements are most often rectangular shaped and placed side-by-side in
the scanning direction. The height of the elements is called the elevation. A B-mode image
of my kidney, acquired with a 1-D piezoelectric transducer array connected to a BK medical
3200 commercial scanner is shown in Figure 1.1(b).
The actual creation of an ultrasound image can be divided into the following three steps:
Sound wave creation
A short strong electrical pulse is fed into the transducer to create an ultrasonic pressure wave.
The beam is then focused into the desired depth of the medium. In older equipment, azimuthal
focusing was done using physical lenses, but today signal processing is mainly used to control
the beam, called beamforming.
Electronic focusing enables the possibility of dynamically
focusing of the beam, which is not possible with physical lenses. To ensure a good transmission
eﬃciency of the waves that travels into the body, a coating on top of the transducer is usually

Receiving echoes
When the reﬂected signals returns to the transducer, the result is the reverse process of creating
the sound wave. The sound wave makes the transducer vibrate, and the vibrations are turned
into electrical pulses. These pulses travel to the ultrasonic scanner where they are processed
and transformed into an image.
Signal processing
To create the image, two characteristics have to be determined from each received pulse; the
time it took from the pulse were emitted until is was received, and how strong the received
echo was. By processing these two informations, a picture can be created. Using the time, the
depth of the scatter point can be determined, and the signal strength determines how much
it scatters. If this is converted into an image, the time determines which pixel, and the signal
strength the intensity of that pixel. This is the basics of ultrasound beamforming.
1.1.2
3-D imaging
In recent years, time resolved 3-D (4-D) imaging has received considerable interest, since it
has several advantages over conventional 2-D imaging. Images acquired using a 2-D scanner
is dependent on the location and the angle of the probe, making some areas inaccessible due
to the anatomy of the human body. 3-D imaging does not have the same drawback, since any
view angle is possible from the volume data. It also makes it more accurate to estimate size
of organs, cysts or tumors for diagnostics without relying on the assumptions and the needed
operator skills for 2-D imaging estimations.
There are three main ways of making volume acquisitions using hand held probes. These three
methods are: freehand scanning, mechanical 3-D probes and two-dimensional matrix array
transducers [4], as shown in Figure 1.2 in (a), (b) and (c), respectively. The ﬁrst two methods

These types of probes are therefore limited in frame rates when making time resolved imaging.
To get real time resolved volumetric imaging, two-dimensional transducer arrays are necessary.
Such transducers were ﬁrst seen in the early 1990s [5]. Placing the elements in a rectangular

frame rates, since there is no moving mechanical parts. To obtain similar image quality as
from a 1-D transducer, the same number of elements in both lateral dimensions as the 1-D
transducer is needed. A 1-D array of 192 elements would translate into 192 × 192 = 36864
elements in a 2-D matrix array. From a transducer fabrication perspective, this poses a great
challenge for providing electrical connections to all the elements. The interconnecting wires
between the 36864 elements and the ultrasonic system result in a large and heavy cable, which
is impractical and unsuitable for medical use.
A method to avoid the high amount of electrical connections is by using a row-column address-
ing scheme, which is introduced below.
Row-column addressing
Row-column addressing of 2-D arrays is an addressing scheme that seeks to reduce the number
of active channels needed for contacting the elements in a 2-D array. The principle of row-
column addressing is illustrated in Figure 1.3. The idea is to contact the elements in the 2-D
array either by the row or column index. Each row or column thereby acts as one large element.

1.2
The CMUT Device
In its most simple form a CMUT can be seen as a plate capacitor, where the bottom electrode is
ﬁxed and the top electrode, called the plate, is free to move, see Figure 1.4. The two electrodes
are separated by an electrically insulating material which clamps the plate at the edges making
it otherwise free to move. Between the electrodes and the clamping posts is found an insulating
gap often composed of vacuum.
5
==PAGE:14==

To describe the diﬀerent parts of a CMUT device, let us again consider the outline of the
CMUT, Figure 1.4. The plate can have all diﬀerent kind of shapes, but usually circular with
radius a, or squares with side length 2L is used. The deﬂection at the center of the plate, w0, is
called the center deﬂection. Plate thickness is h, height of the vacuum gap is gv and thickness
of the electrical insulating layer gi. Changing the dimensions of the plate makes it possible to
tune the resonance frequency to match the desired operation frequency.
If a DC bias voltage (VDC) is applied between the electrodes, an attractive electrostatic force
will cause the plate to be attracted to the bottom electrode.
Mechanical restoring forces
resulting from the stiﬀness of the plate will counteract the attraction until an equilibrium is
retained. When the applied voltage becomes larger than a speciﬁc voltage, the restoring forces
from the stiﬀness of the plate cannot counteract the attractive electrostatic force, causing the
plate to snap down to the bottom of the cavity. This is called the pull-in voltage, Vpi. For a
given plate dimension, the pull-in voltage is tuned by changing the insulating gap between the
top and bottom electrode. When the bias voltage is increased the electromechanical coupling
eﬃciency is also increased and will approach unity at the pull-in voltage. Due to this, the
bias voltage is maintained during operation, often at 80% of pull-in voltage to ensure higher
sensitivity and also linear behavior of the device.
1.2.1
Operation of CMUTs
CMUT devices do not necessary consist of one CMUT cell only, but can be multiple cells
connected in parallel and placed in an element. This is necessary to be able to control the
parameters of the device and still keep a good performance transmit and receive mode. Several
elements are placed side by side in an array, called the CMUT transducer. An illustration of
a 1-D CMUT device showing telements placed in an array is shown in Figure 1.5. Zooming in
on one end of two elements shows the closely packed cells as well as the deﬁnition of kerf and
pitch. Each element is contacted separately using the contacts pads, but all share a common
ground connected from the periphery of the device.
Each element can then be controlled
independently, giving the possibility for steering the beam.
The CMUT device can work either in transmit mode or in receive mode. In transmit mode,
an AC voltage is superimposed on the bias voltage and the plate will start to vibrate at a
certain frequency, hence creating pressure waves with that frequency.
The pressure waves
propagate into the medium of interest and will be reﬂected and scattered. In receive mode, the
echoed pressure waves will mechanically deﬂect the plate which can be measured as a change in
capacitance. From this change in capacitance and the time from the emitted wave to detection
of the echoed wave, ultrasonic images can be generated. The two modes of operation for one
CMUT cell including a simpliﬁed version of the electronic circuit is illustrated in Figure 1.6.
1.3
Historical Overview of CMUTs
Capacitive micro-machined ultrasonic transducers (CMUTs) were invented in the mid 1990s
[7] by Khuri-Yakub’s ultrasonics group from Stanford. They developed a non-sealed CMUT
made by the sacriﬁcial release method, illustrated in Figure 1.7(a). This method utilizes surface
micro-machining where a sacriﬁcial layer separates the top and bottom electrode. The sacriﬁcial
layer is later removed by a selective etching, leaving the top electrode hanging free. Silicon
oxide was used as the sacriﬁcial material, and a timed etch to control the lateral dimension. In
1998 Jin et al.[8] changed the fabrication process to include an amorphous silicon as sacriﬁcial
layer to obtain better control of the lateral dimension, and vacuum sealing the cavity after the
sacriﬁcial etch.

In the early years of CMUT research, the main focus was device fabrication and under-
standing of the device operation.
Several diﬀerent fabrication processes based on the sur-
face micro-machining, ﬁrst proposed by Haller et al. [7] in 1994, have since been developed
[8, 10, 11, 12, 13]. In 2003, Huang et al. [14] developed an alternative method to the surface
micro-machined CMUT based on the wafer bonding method, illustrated in Figure 1.7(b). Here
an Silicon-On-Insulator (SOI) wafer is utilized. A SOI wafer is a thin crystalline silicon layer
(device layer) on top of a insulator, usually oxide, called the BOX (buried oxide). The oxide
is buried between the device layer and the handle. The handle is usually made from silicon.
This method allows for better control of the uniformity and the gap height since fewer thin
ﬁlm processes are used.
In 2011, Park et al. [15], proposed an alternative method to construct the cavity. They utilized
a LOCOS process. LOCOS is short for LOCal Oxidation of Silicon, and is a micro-fabrication
process where silicon is selectively oxidized in selected areas. LOCOS was originally created
for CMOS technology in order to electrically insulate the billion of devices which are placed
side-by-side on the silicon substrate. To perform the local oxidation of silicon, areas not meant
to be oxidized has to be covered by a material that blocks the diﬀusion of oxygen. The by far
most common material used in the diﬀusion mask is silicon nitride, Si3N4, which is capable of
tolerating the high temperature oxidizing environment for a reasonably long time. The Si3N4
layer is deposited in an LPCVD furnace, but this type of deposition creates a high tensile
stress in the nitride layer. The result is a large compressive stress in the underlying silicon
substrate, which can lead to defect generation. To relieve the stress, an oxide, called the buﬀer
oxide, is usually grown below the nitride, with a compressive stress, compensating each other
and thus lowering the stress in the silicon. During the oxidation, the oxidizer not only diﬀuses
in a vertical direction but also horizontally, resulting in an oxidation below the edge of the
Si3N4 mask, pushing the nitride layer upwards as shown in Figure 1.8. The transition from
the oxidized area to the fully diﬀusion blocked area below the nitride is called the birds beak,
due the resemblance with the beak of a bird.
Park et al. [15] utilized a double LOCOS process, where the ﬁrst processes uses an oxide as
a partially oxygen blocking layer to create silicon bumps where the cavities should be located.
The second LOCOS process is a classical LOCOS, which utilizes silicon nitride as the oxygen
blocking layer. This second LOCOS deﬁnes the cavities and the post structures. Hereafter
the top plate is fusion bonded onto the wafer, and the elements and contact pads are deﬁned.
Using this method, it is possible to secure a large ratio of gap height to oxide post thickness,
which is desirable to decrease the parasitic capacitances. A cross sectional view of the ﬁnished

1.4
2-D CMUTs at DTU
Prior to this work, two generations of the 2-D CMUT arrays have been developed. Both of the
designs rely on the row-column addressing scheme.

1.4.2
Second generation
The second generation 2-D row-column CMUT arrays was designed by Thomas Lehrmann
Christiansen and a cross sectional view of the device of a single element is shown in Figure
1.13. It was now possible to fabricate a high oxide quality, hence the design could be simpliﬁed.
The design incorporates 4/5 mask steps. The overall device design is the same as the design
fabricated in this thesis, but the fabrication is very diﬀerent. In contrast to the ﬁrst generation
2-D CMUTs, every sub-element consist of many closely packed cells. The bottom electrode and
the cavities are deﬁned by dry etching in the bottom SOI wafer, and hereafter fusion bonded
to a oxidized SOI wafer, where the oxide acts as the insulation oxide. The bottom electrode
openings and the top electrode are constructed by etching processes.
This design has some problems from a device and fabrication perspective. Due to the insulation
oxide grown on the top wafer as shown in Figure 1.14(a), thin fragile oxide membranes are
located where the bottom and top kerfs cross, as seen to the right of the cross sectional view.
If these thin fragile membranes break, there is a risk of short circuiting the top and bottom
electrode since the bottom electrode is not insulated, as shown in Figure 1.14(b), and the device
will malfunction since it is not possible to maintain the bias voltage across the CMUT cell. A

Chapter 2
Analytic modeling
This chapter will introduce the behavior of CMUTs through analytical modeling. The ability to
accurately predict and describe the behavior of CMUTs is essential in the process of developing
and evaluating diﬀerent CMUT designs. First, an introduction to the modeling of plates will
be given with special focus on a multilayered model I have developed prior to and during
this thesis. The governing equations describing the more complex behavior of the CMUT are
then introduced. Using these equations, the electrostatic behavior is analyzed followed by a
description of the electrodynamics using a lumped element method.
2.1
Plate modeling
First, an introduction to the basic plate theory will be given in order to describe the de-
ﬂection/displacement of the CMUT plate. Followed by a newly developed model describing
multilayered plates, taking an arbitrary numbers of layers and residual stress into account.
2.1.1
Materials and method
The analytical calculation is carried out in Mathematica 9, and is compared to a ﬁnite element
method (FEM) calculation performed in COMSOL 4.3b with a full stiﬀness tensor describing
each material. A circular or square plate of (100) silicon and with isotropic aluminum on top
was modeled. The stress in the layers are modeled in COMSOL as a bi-axial stress. The
multilayered plate is meshed in FEM with a triangular mesh on the top surface and the mesh
was repeated seven times through the plate, since the simulations were found to converge at
this point. The mesh through the plate was set to adapt to the thickness of the diﬀerent layers,
so the mesh height was the same for each mesh layer, and independent of the material layer
thickness.

2.1.3
Multilayered plates
In general, two diﬀerent approaches have been used to study multilayered plates, equivalent
layer theory and discrete layer theory [23]. In equivalent layer theory, every layer are assumed
to behave as one equivalent layer, whereas in the discrete layer theory, every layer are consid-
ered individually. The equivalent layer theory can be divided into two subcategories, classical
laminated plate theory (CLPT) and shear deformation theory (SDT) [23].
The most simple form of equivalent layer theory is the CLPT [24, 25, 26, 27, 28]. The theory
is based on the work by Kirchoﬀ[29], Love [30] and Timoshenko [31]. The theory is build
on the assumption that the lines normal to the mid-surface remain normal and straight to
the mid-surface after deformation [32], leading to that the eﬀect of transverse shear stress are
ignored. This theory is therefore only valid for thin plates, but is less computational time
consuming compared to other theories.
To analyze thicker plates, SDT are used [33, 34, 35, 36]. These are often based on the theories
developed by Reissner [37] and Mindlin [38], which are an extension to the Kirchoﬀ-Love theory,
taking shear deformation through the thickness of the plate into account.
Discrete layer theories have been presented to achieve even more accurate results [39, 40, 41,
42]. These theories has many unknowns for multilayered plates, and the number of unknown
increases with the number of layers. This makes the theory computational time consuming to
obtain accurate results.
Pister et al. [24] formulated a system of equations governing the elastic bending of a plate
consisting of two or more thin layers of isotropic material.
The system was formulated to
resemble the isotropic plate equation (2.7), and they showed that it reduced to the same
equation for a single layer.
Thomsen et Al. [21] formulated an analytic description of anisotropic single-layered plates
made of silicon, and showed that errors of up to 10 −25% in the center deﬂection was the
result of using the isotropic description instead of the anisotropic description.
Dong et Al.
[26] developed a small-deﬂection theory governing the elastic bending of thin
laminated anisotropic shells and plates composed of an arbitrary number of bonded layers.
The bending moments and stress resultants are calculated from the stress-strain relation for
each layer, and from these they formulate a system of equations governing the behavior of
laminated shells.
The following section will build on the classical laminated plate theory, to develop a description
of thin multilayered plates. The derivation of the bending moments and the stress resultant
will follow the derivations from Dong et Al. [26], and being a further development of their
theory.

2.1.4
Summary
This section presented a model for modeling of plates with an arbitrary number of anisotropic
layers with incorporated stress. The multilayered plate equation has been developed from the
bending moments and the stress resultants and by solving the plate equation the exact deﬂec-
tion proﬁle has been calculated for circular plates. By using the Galerkin approximation the
deﬂection proﬁle of rectangular plates can be calculated, and it can be simpliﬁed for square
plates. From the deﬂection proﬁles the buckling limits can be established. The natural fre-
quency has been calculated using Rayleigh-Ritz method using the multilayered plate equation
and an approximate deﬂection proﬁle. To support the results, the model has been compared
to a FEM model, and an excellent agreement between the two models are seen with a relative
diﬀerence of less than 2% for all calculations.

Electrodynamics
The previous section introduced the static behavior of the CMUT. In this section the dynamic
behavior will be analyzed through a lumped element model. Lumped element modeling can
be used to describe the behavior of a spatially distributed system, by mapping the diﬀerent
elements into an approximate system of discrete objects. If the elements are mapped onto an
electrical equivalent, the system can be analyzed using standard methods for circuit analysis.
Besides being able to analyze diﬀerent energy domains, it also gives an eﬃcient way of modeling
the coupling between them.
A lumped element is a discrete object capable of exchanging energy with other discrete objects.
Dealing with two components that are able to exchange energy, a power ﬂow can be deﬁned
as a set of variables whose product is the net power ﬂow between two objects. These variables
are called the conjugate power variables. These variables are usually deﬁned as a ﬂow and
an eﬀort variable, depending on which energy domain is considered. The diﬀerent conjugate
power variables for the electrical, the mechanical and the acoustical domain are reported in

for an electrical circuit is used, called the e →V convention, adapted from [48]. This means
that objects sharing a common ﬂow are connected in series in the equivalent circuit, and objects
sharing a common eﬀort are connected in parallel.

The transduction mechanism of a CMUT can be described by three energy domains: the
mechanical, the electrical and the acoustical.
The model is an equivalent two-port linear
element. It converts an input voltage, V , into a pressure volume, p, or vice versa. A schematic

mechanical domain, and from the acoustical to the mechanical domain, respectively.

3.2
Process overview
An introduction to the proposed process for fabricating 2-D CMUT devices based on the
LOCOS process is given. It is a nine step process, where each step is presented in Table 3.2,
with the corresponding illustrations shown in Figure 3.3.
The starting point is a silicon-on-insulator (SOI) wafer with a thick device layer for the bottom
electrode. First, a silicon dioxide layer is thermally grown using a dry oxidation upon which
a silicon nitride is deposited followed by a poly-silicon layer (step 1). Using a conventional
photolithographic process and a wet etch, the poly-silicon is structured to deﬁne the cavity
pattern. The poly-silicon pattern is then transferred to the silicon nitride using a hot phosphoric
acid etch.
The poly-silicon is stripped using a reactive ion etch (RIE) (step 3).
Next the
insulating trenches are deﬁned. This is done using an RIE process to etch the oxide, followed
by a deep reactive ion etch (DRIE) to etch the silicon (step 4). The high selectivity between
the silicon and the oxide will stop the etch at the BOX. The cavities are now deﬁned using
a thermal oxidation process (LOCOS oxidation) in a wet atmosphere. The bottom electrode
wafer is now ﬁnished (step 4).
A new SOI wafer with a device layer corresponding to the desired plate thickness is bonded to
the bottom electrode wafer (step 5). After annealing, the handle layer (silicon) of the top SOI
wafer is removed in an RIE process and the BOX is removed in a wet etch (BHF) (step 6).
Hereafter, the openings to the bottom electrodes are etched using DRIE etch (silicon) followed
by an ICP etch (oxide) (step 7). Metal (titanium and aluminum) for contacts is then deposited
using an e-beam evaporation (step 8). The metal and the underlying silicon plate is structured
to deﬁne the top elements, using an RIE and DRIE etch, respectively (step 9). Finally, the
wafer is ready to be diced out.

4.1.1
Step 1: thin ﬁlm creation
The starting point of the process is an SOI wafer with a 20 µm device layer and 1 µm BOX
layer. This SOI wafer will be the bottom electrode of the device. The ﬁrst step includes two
or three thermal processes depending on the batch.
Cleaning
Before any thermal process the wafers need to be cleaned to avoid contamination of the furnaces
and the wafers. For a CMUT device, a high quality of the oxide is required to withstand the
high voltages during operation, hence the cleanliness of the furnaces is very important. The
most common cleaning procedure is the RCA (Radio Corporation of America) cleaning. It is
composed of two steps, RCA1 and RCA2, with a HF (hydroﬂouric) dip after each step. RCA1
is a mixture of H2O, NH4OH and H2O2 heated to 70 ◦C, and serves to remove light organics,
particles and desorption of trace metals. The top silicon surface will be oxidized by the H2O2,
and then removed in the following HF dip (30 seconds). RCA2 is a mixture of H2O, HCl
and H2O2 also heated to 70 ◦C, serving to remove alkali ions, metal hydroxides and residual
trace metals. A thin oxide layer is also created in this process, so another HF dip is required
afterwards, also for 30 seconds.
Usually one would spin dry the wafers after the RCA clean before loading into the furnace.
At the time the oxidation was performed, a particle problem was observed in the spin dryer.
To avoid these particles, the wafers were instead left in the boat in front of the furnace for
ﬁve minutes before starting the loading procedure into the furnace. This would evaporate the
water using the radiation heat from the furnace. Due to the discovery of the particle problem,
a new spin dryer was installed within a few weeks.
Insulation Oxidation
The ﬁrst thermal process is an oxidation of the bottom element wafer. This oxide will act
as the insulation oxide in the ﬁnal device, hence the highest oxide quality is necessary. A
dry oxidation at 1100 ◦C is therefore utilized. The oxide grows from the silicon interface by
in-diﬀusion of oxygen, where the following reaction takes place:
Si + O2 →SiO2.
(4.1)
Using the Deal-Grove model [53], the necessary time required to grow an oxide of 400 nm is
calculated. This results in an oxidation time of 8 hours and 45 minutes. The wafers are oxidized
in the phosphorous drive-in furnace since this is considered the most clean furnace. After the
oxidation, the wafers are annealed for 20 minutes in a nitrogen atmosphere to minimize the
ﬁxed surface charges [51, p. 355].
A test wafer is used to verify the thickness of the grown oxide. Using the Filmtek instrument,
the thickness and the refractive index of the oxide can be estimated. The instrument measures
the reﬂectance spectrum at zero degrees and 70 degrees angle of incident. By ﬁtting the right
model to the data the thickness can be extracted. A standard silicon wafer is used as test wafer
to simplify the model. The measured thickness at nine points on the test wafer is shown in
Figure 4.3. The mean value of measured thickness deviates 2.5% from the designed thickness,
which is within the uncertainty one could expect.

Nitride Deposition
After oxidation, it is important that the wafers are transferred directly from the oxidation
furnace to the nitride furnace, otherwise the wafers has to be RCA cleaned again. Diﬀerent
types of nitride can be deposited. A stoichiometric LPCVD silicon nitride is preferred because
it is a better diﬀusion mask compared to PECVD and a silicon-rich LPCVD nitride. Both
PECVD and silicon-rich LPCVD nitride results in a lower tensile stress, thus the possibility
for making a thicker diﬀusion mask.
The stoichiometric silicon nitride, referred to as the
nitride in the rest of this thesis, is deposited in the Furnace: LPCVD Nitride (6”). LPCVD
is short for Low Pressure Chemical Vapor Deposition, and is a deposition process where the
wafer is exposed to a volatile precursor at low pressure which reacts/decomposes on the surface
producing the desired deposit. Common source gases are ammonia (NH3) and dichlorsilane
(3SiH2Cl2), and the following reaction at 780 −790 ◦C creates the nitride ﬁlm
3SiH2Cl2 + 4NH3 →Si3N4 + 6HCl + 6H2.
(4.2)
In contrast to the well described oxidation process, the deposition of nitride is very dependent
on the actual system, the gas ﬂows and the number of wafers. To obtain the right nitride
thickness it is therefor necessary to rely on experiences from other users, the quality control
data and, to run the process under the same conditions every time. To obtain the desired nitride
thickness, it is therefor necessary to check the logbook before doing the actual deposition. The
4NITDAN recipe is used and at the time of deposition, the deposition rate was 2.5 nm/min.
Two thicknesses of nitride was deposited, 56 nm and 100 nm. Since the nitride of the noPolySi
etch is thinned down in the wet nitride etch, we need a thicker starting thickness. Two diﬀerent
thicknesses are investigated using the PolySi process, and only one using the noPolySi process
(100 nm).

Photolithography
First part is the photolithographic process that is identical for both processes. The wafers are

minute. The resist is then developed in a diluted solution of TMAH (TetraMethylAmmonium

be extremely toxic, it is preferred over a NaOH (sodium hydroxide) based developer, since the
wafer is not exposed to alkali ions which could contaminate the oxide.

Next is the deﬁnition of the bottom elements. This will electrically insulate each of the elements

Photolithography
The bottom element deﬁnition starts with a standard lithography. This time, the new structure

Oxide etch
The ﬁrst etch is a dry oxide etch using the AOE (advanced oxide etch). The process relies
on a combination of physical etching using bombardment of ions from a plasma, and chemical
etching using radicals. The etching chemistry of silicon oxide is usually ﬂuorine-based, using
C4F8 in this case.
The SiO2 res recipe is used with the platen chiller temperature set at
0 ◦C. The etch rate is around 230 nm/min. During etching, the wafer will heat up due to the
intense ion bombardment, so before starting the etch the platen chiller temperature should be
stabilized at 0 ◦C, this will take roughly 20-30 minutes. To avoid burning the resist, due to the
heating, a wafer should not be continuously etched for more than two minutes.
Even though only 410 nm oxide should be etched it was decided to etch the wafer for 2 × 1.5
minutes. An over etch would not be problematic, since the selectivity to silicon is high and the
underlying silicon will be etched anyway. Due to the big color diﬀerence between oxide and
silicon it can easily be veriﬁed that all of the oxide has been etched.
Silicon etch
Using the same mask as the oxide etch the silicon is etched using a DRIE (Deep reactive
ion etch) process. This system utilizes a Bosch process alternating between a deposition of a
passivation layer, and a physical ion bombardment and chemical etching using ﬂuorine radicals.
The silicon is etched all the way down to the BOX, a total of 20 µm. To avoid notching, a low
frequency recipe is used: the standard SOI etch recipe. Each wafer is etched for four minutes.
To ﬁnalize the step the resist mask is stripped in the plasma asher.

Before the actual oxidation the wafers had to be RCA clean, as described earlier. Because
we want to minimize the etching of the oxide the ﬁrst HF dip is reduced to 10 seconds and
the second is skipped. After the LOCOS oxidation the oxide thickness was measured using

were characterized. The expected and the measured thicknesses are shown in Table 4.1. The
oxide thickness of the clean wafer is measured within 0.5% of the expected value. However, the

critical since the vacuum gap will be reduced, hence lowering the pull-in voltage. To verify that
the same is the case for the device wafers the cavity heights is measured with the DEKTAK

has to be investigated, if we want to be able to fabricate gaps within a few nanometers of the
desired gap dimension.
A solution to this problem is to oxidize the wafer again, but due to a suspected contamination

4.1.6
Conclusion
Both the PolySi and the noPolySi process showed to be viable for fabrication of the bottom
element wafer.
The surface roughness was seen to be roughly the same after the LOCOS
process, and low enough for a good wafer bonding. Surface roughness wise, one process is not
preferred above the other.
The ﬁnal structure from the two processes will be diﬀerent. Due to the partially dry etch of
nitride on the front of the noPolySi wafer, a nitride layer will be present on the backside of the
wafer with the same thickness as the nitride pads on the front side. This is an undesirable eﬀect
of this fabrication process. Because the nitride has a high tensile stress and is only a continuous
layer on the backside the wafer will bend. Together with the oxygen diﬀusion blocking property
no oxide will be grown on the backside. This will further increase the curvature of the wafer,
since a thick oxide is grown on the front side and the oxide has a compressive stress.
Another downside of the noPolySi process is that the thickness of the nitride layer is diﬃcult to
control. The thickness after the dry etch needs to be measured to be able to know the required
mask-less etch time because a too long over-etch would thin the nitride too much, hence it will
not sustain its diﬀusion blocking ability during the whole LOCOS oxidation. In contrast to
the noPolySi process, the PolySi process is not critically sensitive to over etches, since an over
etch would only result in a horizontal etch below the mask. The ratio of ﬁnal lateral dimension
of the nitride, compared to the horizontal, is larger than 500, hence the under-etch is not that

Bonding
The steps concerning bonding will be handled in a separate section, since it is the most crit-
ical part of the fabrication. First, an explanation of the process will be given followed by a
description of the problems encountered.
4.2.1
Step 5: Bonding
Next step is fusion bonding of the bottom element wafer to the top element wafer. The top
elements are fabricated from an SOI wafer with a device layer in the desired thickness of the
silicon plate, in this case 2 µm. The fusion bonding process begins with a cleaning step. Usually
an RCA clean is used whose primary purpose is removal of particles [54].
Fusion bonding is a process where two silicon substrates are fused together forming a very
strong connection similar to a single crystalline silicon.
One can distinguish between two
diﬀerent types of bonding, hydrophobic and hydrophilic. Hydrophobic bonding corresponds to
having two hydrophobic surfaces bonding to each other, where the hydrophobic nature arises
from a clean silicon surface or a hydrogen termination of the surface. Hydrophilic bonding is
the joining of two oxidized surfaces, here the hydrophilic nature arises from the silanol group
(Si-OH) terminating the oxide onto where water can be chemisorbed [54]. Even though the
surfaces bonded in this thesis is a clean silicon surface and an oxide surface, the bonding is
a hydrophilic bonding. This is due to the native oxidation of the clean silicon surface. At
room temperature, when the two surfaces are put in close proximity, hydrogen bonds will
spontaneously form between the chemisorbed water molecules, see Figure 4.11. The wafers are
then annealed at elevated temperatures to create covalent bonds which increases the bonding
strength. The water molecules will either diﬀuse through the oxide and react with the silicon
to form oxide and hydrogen, or it will diﬀuse along the interface to the outside.

ﬁve minutes. The wafer stack is then transported to the anneal furnace where it is annealed
for 70 minutes at 1100 ◦C in a nitrogen atmosphere.
4.2.2
Step 6: Handle etch
Only the device layer of the top SOI is used for the ﬁnal device, hence the handle layer and
the BOX have to be removed. The handle layer is removed using the advanced silicon etcher
(ASE). The process relies on a combination of physical etching using bombardment of ions from
a plasma and chemical etching using radicals. The etching chemistry is ﬂuorine based. The
etch rate is approximately 6 µm/min. The process is monitored visually and aborted when the
handle layer is completely removed. The etch has a high degree of non-uniformity, hence the
silicon is etched faster near the circumference of the wafer than at its center. This is however
not critical since the selectivity of the etch to oxide is above 200. The next step is to remove
the BOX layer in BHF. Blue ﬁlm is used to protect the backside if we want to keep the oxide on
the back of the wafer. The etch time is not critical, since the BHF will not etch the underlying
silicon. This process presented is the standard bonding and handle etch procedure developed
for the CMUT fabrication.
The main problem of the Dry-etch process was the low fabrication yield. This was due to
the plate rupturing during the handle etch. As showed in the introduction, section 1.4, the
plate broke in a circular manner following the shape of the wafer. This step showed that this
new design did not solve the buckling problem. A similar problem was observed during this
thesis, but the plate did not always break in a way following the shape of the wafer as shown
in Figure 4.12(a). Instead the plate broke or buckled along the bottom elements and stopped
at the insulating trenches, see Figure 4.12(a) & (b).

Anneal

4.3
Top electrode
The top electrodes are trivial fabrication wise since it is a well tested process. This section
will only give an overview of the fabrication of the top elements. Before continuation of the
process, another RCA clean has to be performed. This is because the NIL EVG bonder is not
considered clean.
4.3.1
Step 7: Bottom electrode opening
To be able to contact the bottom elements the silicon plate and the oxide has to be removed
above the contact pads. A standard lithography step is performed to create the etching mask.
The plate is bonded on top of the alignment marks, but because it is very thin, the alignment
marks from the deep insulation trench etch is visible through the plate. The accuracy of the
alignment is not that critical since the openings are big structures. This makes it possible to
perform the alignment without removal of the plate above the alignment marks.
The silicon is etched using the DRIE-Pegasus machine applying the same recipe as the bottom
element insulation etch and the stripping of the poly-Si. The silicon is etched for 40 seconds.
The etch will stop at the oxide, hence it is easy to verify visually that all of the silicon is
etched away. Using the same mask, the oxide is etched in the AOE using the SiO2 res recipe.
As discussed earlier, due to the intensive ion bombardment the resist will heat up, hence an
continuous etch time of much more than two minutes is not possible. All of the post oxide
needs to be removed to be able to contact the bottom electrode silicon, hence approximately
1350 nm of oxide needs to be etched. The etch rate is around 200 nm/min resulting in an etch
time of 6.75 minutes. The actual etch time was 3 × 2.5 minutes, even though this was more
than two minutes of continuous etch it showed not to be a problem.
An advantage of the LOCOS process is that the alignment marks are formed both in the oxide
and silicon layer. This makes it almost impossible to remove the alignment marks, and gives
the possibility for opening of the alignment marks at the same time as opening for bottom
contacts. The alignment mark after the oxide and silicon is removed is shown in Figure 4.17.
The previous design had the alignment marks formed only in the oxide, hence it would be
removed in the bottom opening etch unless it was masked before the oxide etch. Another
solution is incorporating an extra step for opening of the alignment marks, using a dedicated
mask. Using the LOCOS process, this is not necessary.
4.3.2
Step 8: Metalization
Metal for contact pads and for lowering of the parasitic resistance is deposited using a physical
vapor deposition (PVD) process. The metal is evaporated by an e-beam heater in which a
high-energy electron beam is focused onto the source material using magnetic ﬁelds.
The
deposition takes place at very low pressure to allow passage of the electron beam and to allow
the metal particles to travel further. The evaporated atoms condense on the wafer surface
to form a thin ﬁlm of high uniformity.
The metal deposited is aluminum but to increase
the adhesion a thin layer of titanium is deposited before the aluminum. Both materials are
deposited at a rate of 1 nm/s, and 20 nm titanium is deposited and 400 nm aluminum. The
rate is monitored by a quartz crystal oscillator. The metal thickness can be controlled very
precisely.

4.3.3
Step 9: Top element deﬁnition
The last step is the deﬁnition of the top elements. This is done by etching of the metal and the
silicon plate serving to electrically insulate the top elements from each other. The ﬁrst part is
a standard lithography step except that HDMS as adhesion promoter is not used, since it has
no eﬀect on metals. The transfer of mask pattern to the resist is critical, since defects in the
pattern transfer could result in short circuiting of the elements.
The metal stack is etched using the ICP metal etcher with the standard aluminum recipe. The
ICP metal works in the same way as the ASE and the AOE, explained earlier, but the etch
chemistry is diﬀerent. The etch chemistry is based on chlorine (Cl2) and hydrogen bromide
(HBr). To etch 400 nm aluminum with the 20 nm titanium as adhesion, an etch time of approx-
imately 80 seconds is necessary. The etch can be visually monitored using the CCD camera
connected to the system. Since the ICP metal is setup for 6 inch wafers, the wafer needs to
be bonded to a 6 inch carrier wafer. The wafer is bonded using crystalbond to secure a good
cooling necessary to obtain a successful etch. After the etch the wafers are debonded and the
remaining crystalbond on the backside of the wafer is removed using a water gun.
The silicon is then etched using the DRIE pegasus with the SOI recipe for 40 seconds. This is
the same recipe as introduced in step 3. After the silicon is etched, the resist mask is removed
in the plasma asher and the wafer is ready to be diced into devices. Figure 4.18 shows an array
diced and mounted on a ﬂexible PCB for connecting to the probe handle PCB.
4.4
Chapter summary
The LOCOS process showed to be a viable process to fabricate CMUT devices. No protrusions
would prevent a good wafer bonding and the surface roughness was below the critical surface

Characterization
The chapters so far have been dealing with the design and fabrication of the 2-D CMUT arrays.

will be compared to devices made with the Dry-etch process and the gain will be evaluated.
The advantages of utilizing the LOCOS process were introduced in the introductory chapter.
They are summarized below, and the LOCOS process will be evaluated according to these
expected advantages
• insulation oxide on bottom electrode ensuring
– no thin oxide membranes
– less fragile design
– bottom electrodes electrically insulated
– no thinning of plate due to oxidation
• thicker post oxide giving
– decreased parasitic capacitance
– increased electromechanical coupling factor
– it can sustain higher operation voltages
• nitride in cavity
– a hypothesis is that the nitride blocks the injection of mobile charges in the insulation
oxide during fabrication

During the fabrication two test wafers were fabricated to examine the breakdown voltage.

of the metal contacts is probed.
The breakdown voltage of the oxide can be measured by applying a voltage to the aluminum
contact pad with the backside of the wafer as reference. The voltage is increased in steps by 1 V
and the resulting current is measured. When the oxide will experience breakdown, a current
will run between the two electrodes. In this case, it would be when the compliance current is
reached (Acompliance = 1 mA). Figure 5.6 shows a color-map of the absolute breakdown voltage
of the insulation oxide wafer (a) and the post oxide wafer (b).
The devices are designed
with the criteria that the breakdown voltage is above 0.5 V/nm, hence the breakdown voltage
of insulation oxide should be above this value. The oxide thickness is measured during the
fabrication, tox = 404 nm, corresponding to a relative breakdown voltage of 0.72 ± 0.07 V/nm.
Using these measurements, a statistical analysis is performed and it is estimated that the
probability of the breakdown voltage is above 0.5 V/nm is 99%.
The thicker post oxide leads to a higher breakdown voltage at the posts. The T4 wafer has
the same thickness as the post oxide, since it has be oxidized in both oxidations. The wafer
has also been exposed to the wet nitride etch, since this etch is used for stripping nitride
directly after KOH etches and this could lead to potassium ion contamination of the oxide.
The measured breakdown voltage is 883 ± 99 V. This is more than four times higher than the
operation voltage, hence there should be no risk of short circuiting the device through the post
oxide. From the breakdown voltage map of the wafer it is seen that the breakdown voltage is
somewhat lower in some places, which could be due to pinholes is the oxide.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This thesis has successfully developed a local oxidation of silicon (LOCOS) process for fabri-
cation of CMUTs. A 62 × 62 2-D row-column array was fabricated utilizing two SOI wafers,
wafer bonding, and only 4 mask steps. The cavities are deﬁned using a LOCOS process with
nitride as the diﬀusion blocking layer. The fabricated devices were characterized to verify the
advantages of the LOCOS process and they fulﬁlled all expectations.
The working principle of CMUTs was presented through analytical models. A multilayered
plate model was developed to understand the eﬀect of multiple layers and residual stress. The
model was compared to a FEM model and an excellent agreement between the two models were
seen with a relative diﬀerence of less than 2%. From the governing equations the electrostatic
behavior was analyzed followed by a description of the electrodynamics using a lumped element
method. This analysis gave an understanding of how a CMUT should be designed. A bias
voltage as close to the pull-in voltage should be used to increase the coupling coeﬃcient. Also,
parasitic capacitances should be minimized and reducing the active top or bottom electrode
area could further increase the coupling coeﬃcient at the expense of a higher pull-in voltage.
Lastly, the CMUT should be designed to operate at its resonance frequency to obtain maximal
coupling to the mechanical domain.
The process was analytically designed using the Deal-Grove oxidation model.
Simulations
showed that the fabrication based on the LOCOS process was viable for CMUTs. No pro-
trusions in the cavities or at the insulation edges are expected to prevent a successful wafer
bonding. Due to the nature of the LOCOS process, the bonding point will not be directly
at the nitride edge but 2 µm further away from the cavity. The distance from the insulation
trench to the bonding point was shown to be at least 6 µm, hence the cavities should be located
even further from the edge to ensure enough bonding area.
The LOCOS process was shown to be viable for fabrication of CMUT devices. It was veriﬁed
that no protrusions were created that would prevent a good wafer bonding and the surface
roughness was below 0.2 nm.
This is below the critical surface roughness, giving the ideal
conditions for a successful wafer bonding. The most controllable way of structuring the nitride
layer was found to be using a wet nitride etch with a poly-si mask. The transfer of the etch
mask from the resist to the poly-si was shown to give problems with the resist adhesion when
using a wet etch, hence a dry etch is preferred. A low yield of the Dry-etch process was observed
because of the plate ruptured during the fabrication. The LOCOS process did not solve this
problem, hence further work have to be made to overcome this. From the investigations made
during this thesis three improvements are proposed. The curvature of the wafer is expected to

6.1
Outlook
For the LOCOS process to be successful for the fabrication of 2-D row-column CMUT arrays
the buckling problem needs to be solved completely. A new batch where half of the devices are
rotated 90◦are currently under fabrication and this will determine if this solves the problem.
The ﬁnished devices should be mounted in a probe handle, and acoustical characterization
should be conducted.
The CMUT arrays could be improved even further by incorporating the double LOCOS process,
as proposed by Park et al. [15]. A sketch of a CMUT cell using the double LOCOS process
is shown in Figure 6.1. The silicon bump obtained through the double LOCOS will not only
contribute to a thicker post oxide, but also lower the parasitic capacitance originating from the
cavity. The capacitance contributing from the edge of the cavity to the silicon bump will be
reduced, since the vacuum gap in this region will increase. In this way the coupling coeﬃcient’s
theoretical maximum can be increased to 45% from 16%, if it is assumed that the silicon bump
covers a quarter of the cavity and only the part above the silicon bump contributes to the
electrical capacitance.

Row-Column Addressed 2-D CMUT Arrays using LOCOS Fabrication:
From Silicon Chip to Transducer Probe
Mathias Engholm1, Thomas Lehrmann Christiansen1, Christopher Beers2, Jan Peter
Bagge3, Lars Nordahl Moesner 3, Anders Lei1, Matthias Bo Stuart1, Jørgen Arendt
Jensen1, and Erik Vilain Thomsen1
1Technical University of Denmark, 2800 Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark
2Sound technology, State College, PA 16803, USA
3BK Medical, 2730 Herlev, Denmark
We here present results from the development and fabrication of a 3 MHz, λ/2-pitch 62+62
channel row-column addressed 2-D capacitive micromachined ultrasonic transducer (CMUT)
array designed to be mounted in a probe handle and connected to a commercial BK Medical
scanner for real-time volumetric imaging.
Results are included from the cleanroom fabrication of the array utilizing a new process that
combines the fabrication process presented by Christiansen et al. at the MUT2014 conference
in Copenhagen, Denmark, with a LOCOS (LOCal Oxidation of Silicon) process proposed by
Park et al. (J. Microelectromech. Syst., vol. 20, pp. 95-103, 2011). Characterization results
from fabricated arrays are presented, demonstrating the advantages of the row-column LOCOS
process such as: few lithography steps (4), fast production time (appr. one week), completely
electrically insulated electrodes, reduced parasitic capacitance, high oxide breakdown voltage
(900 V), and oxide-free top plates.
A SEM micrograph showing a cross-section detail of a
fabricated array is seen in the left ﬁgure.
The right ﬁgure shows an illustration of the probe handle, which is scheduled to be com-
pleted in the beginning of May 2015. The fabricated array is mounted on a ﬂexible printed
circuit board using wire-bonding. This is then interfaced to a 192-channel ultrasound scanner
cable from BK Medical (Herlev, Denmark) using an electronics PCB with pre-ampliﬁers in order
to drive the cable to the scanner. The CMUT array is designed to use the built-in 190 V DC
power supply and ±75 V AC excitation voltage of a 3000 scanner from BK medical, enabling
the scanner to produce real-time volumetric imaging with the row-column probe.
