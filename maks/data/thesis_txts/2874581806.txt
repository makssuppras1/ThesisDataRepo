1. Introduction
The wind energy industry has been developed for many years all over the world, while due to the 
public against and the increase of wind turbines’ size, and the demand for clean and sustainable 
energy continues to rise, offshore wind energy has emerged as a promising solution due to its 
abundant resource potential and reduced environmental impact.
Designing large Floating Offshore Wind Turbines (FOWTs) comes with several challenges. Large 
FOWTs are subject to complex hydrodynamic interactions with waves, currents, and wind. These 
interactions can cause dynamic responses and structural loads that must be carefully analyzed 
and accounted for during the design process.
Another challenge is the stability and motion control. Keeping the platform stable in various sea 
conditions is critical. Effective motion control systems must be implemented to minimize platform 
movements and maintain the turbine's operational efficiency and structural integrity.
This will lead a new challenge to the mooring system of FOWT. Designing a robust mooring and 
anchoring system is essential to keep the FOWT in place and prevent excessive movement or 
detachment from its location, especially during extreme weather events.
The state-of-the-art procedure for the design and analysis of mooring system for FOWTs ignores 
the tower flexibility, while when the scale of wind turbine moves to 15MW or larger, the tower 
flexibility may become more and more important. This project takes Offshore Code Comparison 
Collaboration Continuation (OC4) project as the study object to investigate the effect of the tower 
flexibility on the ultimate loads and fatigue damage of the mooring system.

2. Background
The demand of green energy in the world pushes the rapid development of wind energy in these 
years globally, many different types of wind turbine’s structure has been proposed and developed 
during these years. Among all these types, FOWTs offer numerous advantages as a clean and 
sustainable energy solution. These innovative structures have gained significant attention in 
recent years due to their unique characteristics and potential benefits.
•
Figure 1 Different types of wind turbine’s structure (From DNV seminar)
First and foremost, FOWTs have access to stronger and more consistent wind resources 
compared to onshore and near shore wind turbines. By harnessing wind energy in offshore 
locations, these turbines can operate at higher wind speeds, resulting in increased power 
generation. The uninterrupted wind flow over the open sea allows FOWTs to produce electricity 
more efficiently and reliably, making them an attractive option for renewable energy generation.
Another reason to choose FOWTs is that they have the advantage of being situated away from 
densely populated areas. This reduces potential conflicts over land use and mitigates concerns 
regarding noise pollution and visual impact (Stewart and Muskulus 2016). By harnessing wind 
energy offshore, FOWTs have the potential to minimize disturbances to coastal ecosystems and 
preserve the natural beauty of coastal landscapes (Haberlin et al. 2022).
From commercial perspective, the levelized cost of energy (LCOE) is a key benchmark for the 
wind energy industry. Several study (Myhr et al. 2014, Lerch et al. 2018) investigate different 
types of wind turbine concepts, including both bottom-fixed and floating offshore wind turbines 
(FOWTs). Both studies indicate that the floating wind turbine has a potential to produce equal or 
lower LCOE compared to bottom-fixed concepts.
==PAGE:6==
6
To further reduce the LCOE, wind turbines must be built larger and larger, and the size of wind 
farm shall be larger and larger. FOWTs is relatively easier to increase the scale. Offshore wind 
farms can accommodate a larger number of turbines compared to onshore installations.
While this brings new challenges. From the course we have learnt that the taller a tower structure 
is, the lower nature frequency it has. This could make the sub-structure’s nature frequency closer
to the wave frequency, which will lead to severe consequences. Design the structure more stiffen
will not only increase the cost of the project, but it may also have impact on other system such as 
mooring system. To find out in what extend the affect will be, this project will take a semi-
submersible FOWT as study object, and by changing the stiffness of tower structure, to find the 
numerical impact on the mooring extreme load and fatigue damage.

3. Methodology and tools
This project will take a semi-submersible offshore wind turbine as the study object, and by 
changing the flexibility of the tower structure to find out how the flexibility of the tower affects the 
load and fatigue damage on mooring system.
Many software have been developed and used in wind energy industry, such as HAWC2 from 
DTU, SIMA from DNV and OrcaFlex from Orcina. These software have their own advantages and 
limitations. This project will use SIMA as main calculation tool for analysis to obtain the time series 
of load on the mooring lines.
The SIMA workbench is originally developed by SINTEF Ocean and now integrated into SESAM 
package. It is for efficient analysis of complex multi-body systems, including mooring analysis and 
marine operations. The SESAM package provides an integrated platform to build model for both 
wind turbine and sub-structure and it can perform coupled analysis for mooring system (DNV 
2023). 
Regarding the FOWTs, SIMA has the following specific features.
•
Modelling of offshore wind turbines blades
•
Wind load models
•
Wind turbine modelling, including control system
•
The wind turbine blades and the tower can be flexible
For this project, we will mainly use SIMA to perform time-domain calculation. The result from 
SIMA is time series of load on the mooring lines, the extreme value can be directly read from it, 
while for floating offshore wind turbine, the fatigue damage on the system should also be 
evaluated. Fatigue damage is caused and accumulated in materials by the cyclic application of 
loads. In real world, the load applied on the structure is not constant load, therefore the cycle is 
not easy to define.
There are many methods have been proposed and used for the cycle counting in fatigue damage 
evaluation, such as the rainflow method, the peak method and the range method. As the state of 
the art, the rainflow method that proposed by Endo et al. (1974) has been commonly used and 
probably is the best method since it is closely related to the fatigue mechanism (Kondo et al. 
2003).
The rainflow methon has been integrated in many coding tools such as Python and MATLAB. In 
this project, MATLAB is used to perform the fatigue damage by calculating the damage equivalent 
load for each stiffness setting in all of load cases.

4. State of the art and study object
Ramachandran et al. (2013) studied OC3-Hywind spar buoy and NREL 5-MW wind turbine by 
FAST and WAMIT. This paper uses these two approaches to investigate the influence of structural 
flexibility on the system responses and the results show that the high-frequency excitations 
observed for the flexible turbine are larger than that of the rigid turbine.
Several other papers (Quallen and Xing 2016, Tran and Kim 2016, Leble and Barakos 2016)
studied the coupled FOWT simulations, while they mainly focused on the modelling of 
aerodynamics and hydrodynamics and kept the structural flexibility unchanged. 
Borg et al. (2016) investigated a spar-type floating substructure with the DTU 10MWW reference 
wind turbine and described a method for the inclusion of substructural flexibility in aero-hydro-
servo-elastic dynamic simulations.
While there are few studies focus on the effect of tower structure flexibility on mooring systems, 
this project will take the opportunity to analysis the flexibility of tower structure effects on the 
mooring loads for semi-submersible floating offshore wind turbines, especially for large wind 
turbines. Due to the accessibility of commercial wind turbines data and limitation on time, the OC4 
Wind Turbine is chosen as the study object for this project.
The OC4 wind turbine, also known as the Offshore Code Comparison Collaborative, is a 
continuation of the OC3 (Offshore Code Comparison Collaboration) project, and it’s a significant 
milestone in the field of offshore wind energy research and development. According to the official 
introduction of OC4 wind turbine (Robertson et al. 2014), the turbine used in OC4 is a 5MW wind 
turbine same as OC3. Compare to modern commercial turbines that can up to 15MW, it is 
relatively small. Considering that the completion of the OC4 wind turbine, this analysis may also 
produce useful results for further analysis.
The figure below from National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NERL) shows the basic 
dimensions of OC4 wind turbine. The base of the tower is installed at the top of the main column 
of the semisubmersible and is located at an elevation of 10 m above the still water level (SWL). 
The total length of the tower is about 87.6 m. 
The OC4 platform is moored with three catenary lines spread symmetrically with the angle 
between adjacent lines is 120 degrees. Each line has an unstretched length of 835.5 m with 
diameter of 0.0766 m. The equivalent mass per unit length of 113.35 kg/m, an equivalent apparent 
mass in fluid per unit length of 108.63 kg/m, and an equivalent extensional stiffness of 753.6 MN. 
Three anchors are located at a water depth of 200 m below the SWL and at a radius of 837.6 m 
from the platform centerline (Robertson et al. 2014).

6. Conclusion
The purpose of this project is to find out the possible effects of tower structure flexibility on mooring 
system for FOWTs. SIMA is used to perform the time-domain numerical simulation and MATLAB 
is then used to calculate the damage equivalent load to evaluate the fatigue damage.
Result shows that changing the flexibility of tower structure has only minor effect on the extreme 
load on the mooring lines. A possible explanation is that the changing the flexibility of tower is by 
changing the stiffness of tower cross sections directly, this ensures the mass of tower is kept the 
same. Advantage is to control the variates to analyze the effect of tower flexibility only. While in 
real world, changing the flexibility of structure will normally change the system mass, and the 
change of mass will lead the hydrodynamic response changes.
On the other hand, there are noticeable changes on the damage equivalent load, which is 
calculated to evaluate the fatigue damage. In most operational load cases, there is positive 
correlation between damage equivalent load and tower stiffness. This indicates that increase the 
stiffness of tower structure will have negative effect on the mooring system.
However, when wind speed gets higher and sea state get worse as load case 5-2 and load case 
6, the results become different. When wind turbines are in parked condition due to high wind 
speed and sea states, the results are varied. The minimum axial force on the mooring line 1 was 
negative which is abnormal. After investigation, this could be results of coarse mesh when 
modelling the mooring line in SIMA. 
Among these load cases with harsh sea states, load case 5-2, 6 and 7-2 (load case 7 with finer 
mesh) have the same pattern, which the highest damage equivalent load appears when stiffness 
is 10 times as original. When stiffness is 100 times as original, the damage equivalent load 
decreases. In the real projects, the stiffen the tower structure 100 times is not feasible, so the 
positive correlation within 10 times stiffness could be still seen as a guidance for design of mooring 
system.
Reducing the LCOE of FOWTs is a multidimensional approach that involves technological, 
financial, and operational optimizations. This study focus on mooring system from the perspective 
of tower structure flexibility only, to adapt it for projects, a deeper and overall analysis is required.
As mentioned in the beginning of this thesis, there are a few limitations on this analysis, which 
can be improved in further study. First is that the OC4 wind turbine is relatively small for the study, 
the results especially the damage equivalent loads are expected to have larger differences 
regarding the changes of stiffness.
Secondly, the OC4 is moored by catenary mooring system. Compared to other mooring systems 
like tension leg platforms (TLPs), catenary mooring systems result in higher platform motion due 
to the flexible nature of the anchor chains/cables. The same FOWT with TLPs is expected to have 
larger difference both on extreme load and damage equivalent load on mooring lines. Catenary 
mooring system has advantages in many perspectives, but it has limitations in harsh conditions.
Catenary mooring may not be the ideal choice for areas with extreme weather conditions, such 
==PAGE:30==
30
as hurricanes or cyclones, as it may not provide the same level of stability as other mooring 
systems. Also, catenary mooring is not well-suited for deepwater projects as the required length 
of anchor chains or cables becomes impractical and can lead to excessive costs. According to 
these factors, future study can be focused on a larger FOWT and/or with TLP mooring system.

Appendix B Matlab code for fatigue damage 
calculation
The following MATLAB code is used to calculate the damage equivalent load. First, it will load the 
pre-saved time series of load, then use the built in function “rainflow()” to calculate damage 
equivalent load.
%%
clear all
close all
NEq=1e7;             % for fatigue
mWoehler=3.0;          % may need to change according to the material
A = importdata('AxialForce_x100.txt');
B = getfield(A,"data");
TotalLoad = B(:,2);
C = rainflow(TotalLoad);
n=C(:,1);
MRange=C(:,2);
MRangeDEL = (sum(n/NEq.*MRange.^mWoehler))^(1/mWoehler);
MDEL = MRangeDEL/2;
nSum = sum( n , 'all' );
