Abstract
This thesis analyzes a hollow-core slab-to-stabilizing wall connection to be disassembled
only by breaking the low-strength concrete used as binding material.
This was done by analyzing a low-rise building representing most of the residential build-
ings in Copenhagen, Denmark in the software Robot Structural Analysis and using the
obtained stresses to analyze the connection in Abaqus and Autodesk Inventor to obtain
the weakest binding material that was able to transfer the stresses from the slab to the
wall. Moreover, the disassembly was considered by conducting a preliminary study on
the existing lifting methods of hollow-core slabs and calculating the shear resistance at
the connection and the uplifting force needed to overcome it.
The results showed that the weakest concrete to be used as a binding material was 12
MPa and that the connection would fail by shear failure in the interface between the bind-
ing material and the hollow-core slab, making disassembly of the joint a possibility. The
results also showed that there is a linear correlation between the depth of the hollow-
core slab and the uplifting force making it possible to disassemble hollow-core slabs of
thickness 180 mm to 320 mm with the cranes available in Copenhagen, Denmark which
have a lifting capacity of 60 tons. However, during uplifting a large negative moment and
a high shear force will occur in the slab and therefore, hollow-core slabs that should be
disassembled using the method described in this thesis should have reinforcement at the
top and bottom.
By disassembling the structure and reusing the elements, CO2 emissions for the building
will be reduced by 76.93% and 56.26% of the total overall cost will be saved compared to
if the building was demolished and a new one was built from scratch.

Introduction
1.1
Introduction to Design for Disassembly (DfD)
In the last few decades, the reasons behind climate change have been discussed tire-
lessly and it was found that one of the most affecting factors is excessive use of natural
resources and the emission of CO2 and waste of materials associated with it [1]. The
highest contribution comes from the construction industry as the common practice when
structures are no longer needed for locational, functional, technical, or physical reasons
is to demolish and dump the resulting waste in landfills. Additionally, further pollution is
emitted when a new structure is to be constructed, and thus, a cycle of constructing and
demolishing is created.
Consequently, the construction industry was under pressure to develop a technology to
recycle its products to reduce its contribution to climate change, and the concept of de-
signing structures with the purpose of demounting structural members and reusing them
in future projects was developed. By replacing demolition with disassembly, Design for
Disassembly (DfD) reduces pollution significantly as the life span of certain structures
could be prolonged by replacing structural elements that are no longer able to perform
effectively.
1.1.1
Problem Statement
The construction industry is responsible for 38% of carbon emissions globally [2]. To re-
duce that, structures are being designed for disassembly such that the structural members
can be reused in other projects. However, although several researchers have studied de-
mountable joints, there is no efficient method to safely disassemble structures that does
not require any modification of the elements during manufacturing.
1.1.2
Scope and Limitations
This research will focus on designing a joint between a stabilizing wall and a hollow core
slab to allow a structure to be disassembled such that building elements can be reused
in new structures. The joint will be designed for low-rise buildings with a story height of
3.5 m and 5 m with the stabilizing walls either placed in the center of the structure or all
separated and placed at the edges. The joint will be modeled using the software Abaqus
to study the structural behavior of the joint with varying grades of low-strength concrete
. The results will be verified with analytical calculations and an investigation of how to
disassemble the joint will be conducted.
Due to time constraints, laboratory experiments to verify the obtained results are not within
the scope of this research. Moreover, the joint will not be designed for high-rise buildings
or buildings with story heights above 5 m.

1.2
Background
The development of precast concrete structures is growing very rapidly. After the second
world war, every nation started building new infrastructure to replace damaged structures.
After a few decades, the development of precast concrete structures was found to be very
efficient [3]. It was recognized that using precast members had major advantages such
as quality control, timely availability, economy, applicable for composite construction, and
reduced maintenance.
However, precast members do not come without challenges. Although they are designed
and cast in a controlled environment, when assembling on-site, extra attention should be
paid to the joints in terms of their strength, ductility, volume change, durability, and fire-
resisting capacities because the joints are subjected to different sectional forces such as
compression, tension, shear, flexure and torsion [3].
Figure 1.1: Precast concrete joints [3]
Since then, the use of precast concrete members continues to grow rapidly. The com-
mon practice for the connection of precast members is the use of a high strength concrete
binding material or high strength mortar grouting connecting the reinforcement from each
member. These wet joints are strong, economic, and easy and fast to construct. How-
ever, these joints do not allow the members to be disassembled. Hence, if the structure
needs to be removed or modified, the whole structure would need to be demolished. A
study made by a group of students from Queen’s University, UK [1] stated that about 60%
of natural resources are used by the construction industry along with high CO2 emissions
and billions of tons of waste. Subsequently, the same resources are consumed and the
same amount of pollution is emitted to construct a new structure.
To overcome these problems, the concept of the demountable precast concrete struc-
ture was developed, where the structural members could be disassembled and again
reassembled in other places. The study [1] shows the oldest officially recorded case
was the demounting of an RC garage building which was recorded as a US patent in
1973. Design for Disassembly (DfD) is essential to close the loop of materials, similar to
a cradle-to-cradle model where “waste” is turned into “feed” in comparison with cradle-to-
grave thinking. For the systems to be demounted, dry connections should be used when
possible [1].

Joint A:
[5]For wet joints of demountable precast buildings, more time is needed for placing the
connection, preparing necessary formwork, and curing to erect other precast elements.
Additionally, there is a risk of hampering the structural member when disassembling wet
joints as most wet joints contribute to semi-monolithic joint behavior. Hence dry joints are
popular nowadays. Research done by a Ph.D. student at the University of Canterbury,
New Zealand [5] presented a specific type of dry joint based on the test results with dif-
ferent steel angle beam-column connection configurations, which includes different beam
edge distance, use of different fill materials, duct condition inside the beam (i.e., ungrouted
or grouted) and the number and its arrangement of bolts on the column side which were

[6] Precast concrete structures are highly vulnerable in the seismic zone because the

seemed to be increased in this joint by using embedded rod.

Joint D:
[8]An end-plate beam-column connection with steel plates embedded in a precast con-
crete beam with a gusset plate welded to an endplate seen in Figure 1.7 was proposed
by researchers at the University of Canterbury, New Zealand.
Figure 1.7: a) Layout of connection and b) Assembly of the end plate connection
A precast concrete frame and a monolithic concrete frame were tested under quasi-static
cyclic loading and from the results, it was found that the location of the maximum damage
occurred in the beam away from the face of the column as the capacity of the connection
was higher than that of the beam.
The following cracks were observed in the precast beam:
1. Flexural shear cracks caused by shear and bending of the precast beam
2. Horizontal cracks in front of the gusset plate caused by buckling of the reinforcement
3. Horizontal and inclined cracks where the beam and the column connect caused by
the push/pull of the gusset plate
From these cracks, it was concluded that the damage that occurred is similar to what
would occur in a monolithic concrete frame system. Finally, the connection can be disas-
sembled by simply unbolting it; however, in the tests conducted on the connection in the
lab, the beam was damaged.

From the implemented test, it was concluded that dismounting and reusing precast con-
crete element frames is possible using this connection technology, without damaging
structural elements in the process. The time frame between the assembly and disas-
sembly was done within only two months and therefore, the bond between the previously
cast and the new concrete was easy to dismount as the time frame between the assembly
and disassembly process in practice could be longer and the concrete could be harder,
thus making disassembly more challenging. Furthermore, the openings in the hollow core
slab units can be cleaned easily while the sides require additional time and therefore the
disassembly was considered partially successful.
From the economic and environmental point of view, three concepts were compared:
• Conventional frame
• Demountable wet joint frame
• Demountable dry joint frame
The demountable wet and dry joint frames gave 35 % economical savings compared to
conventional construction and 50 % less CO2 emissions. Appendix A.3 presents the
calculations and overall impact cost and environmental for the three concepts. Further
studies need to be conducted in regards to the dry joints.

1.2.3.1
Circle House
An investigation on applied demounting technologies was done at a site visit to a mockup
called “Circle House” situated in Valby, Copenhagen seen in Figure 1.22. Lejerbo as a de-

Discussion
The connection between structural elements is crucial to ensure the robustness of the
building and this is especially important in the design for disassembly. Based on the
stresses obtained in Robot Structural Analysis, the stabilizing walls should have to be
separated and placed at the edge of the building to achieve the worst-case scenario as
placing the walls in the center will support the structure further since the center of gravity
would be close to the shear center of the building, leading to higher stiffness and a more
robust structure to resist the applied lateral loads. Additionally, the stresses on the struc-
ture due to lateral loads increased with the height of the structure.
Previously done studies have already covered DfD systems with mechanical connections
to transfer the loads where mortar or low-strength concrete was to cover the connection
whereas the joint design in this thesis differs as the low-strength concrete was used as a
binding material to transfer the loads between two precast elements.
To transfer the stresses at the joints, the following low-strength concrete materials were
analyzed: 8, 10, 12, and 16 MPa. The detailed analysis showed that the 8 and 10 MPa
binding materials were unable to transfer the loads as the applied stresses were higher
than the strength of the binding material fck at the time of service of the building. From
the analysis, it was found that the low-strength concrete to be used as a binding material
needs to be 12 MPa to successfully transfer loads from the hollow-core slab to the stabi-
lizing wall.
Moreover, from the results, the shear resistance at the interface between the structural el-
ements and the low-strength concrete binding material was found to be lower than that of
the low-strength concrete itself. This indicates that the joint will fail by shear failure at the
interface between the binding material and the adjacent structural member which makes
it possible to disassemble the structure without damaging any of the structural members.
Note that the calculation is based on the assumption that the facades have been removed
from the structure prior to disassembly. Moreover, since the analyzed structure does not
have any edge beams or tie bars, these have not been considered in the calculation.
Therefore, if a building has edge beams, it is recommended to cut the binding material
between the beam and the adjacent slab using a diamond saw, and in the case of tie
bars in the joint, it is recommended to cut them before uplifting. After the binding material
between the slab and the edge beam has been cut, the side where the beam used to be,
will be free and consequently, the next slab to be removed will only be attached by three
sides as seen in Figure 4.7
By only having three sides that need to be broken during uplifting, it was found that 29
tons are needed to overcome the shear resistance of the 180 mm HCS chosen. By using
the same calculation process, it was found that there is a linear correlation between the
depth of the hollow-core slab and the uplifting force and thus, the uplifting force needed to
lift several sizes of hollow-core slabs was deduced as seen in Figure 6.1. Further details

Conclusion
The study aimed to investigate the possibility of designing a binding material that can
transfer the stresses of the building to be able to reuse the HCS. Results from Robot
Structural Analysis showed a percentage difference of less than 5 % when compared to
analytical calculations which verified that the models were giving satisfying results. More-
over the analysis was done on different models having variable heights with different wall
locations to get the maximum stresses applied on the joint.
From the analysis in Autodesk Robot, the stresses were obtained and applied on the
joint to calculate the required material properties for concrete to be able to transfer the
stresses using Autodesk Inventor. The results showed that concrete strength of 12 MPa
is able to transfer the stresses from the slab to the walls.
Furthermore, after conducting an investigation of the material properties of the low-strength
concrete, the force required to have shear failure at the interface between the slab and
binding material was calculated as 29 tons. The force was based on the HCS of thickness
180 mm and it was found that the method of disassembly in this thesis can be used for
HCS of thickness ranging from 180 mm to 320 mm.
From this applied force, an analysis was done on the slab to check the capacity at the
time of disassembly where a negative moment is developed due to the applied load. Sub-
sequently, the HCS was modified to add top reinforcement to cover the negative moment.
Moreover, the slab was verified by checking the shear capacity of the modified HCS.
Finally, a 3D frame of a single bay containing the analyzed slab-to-wall joint was mod-
eled in Autodesk Inventor to illustrate how the stresses are being transferred during dis-
mounting and the effect on structural elements. The results showed that the structural
members were not significantly affected by the uplifting force. In summary, it was found
that the slab can be dismounted by breaking only the binding material during uplifting, and
by implementing this method, 76.93% and 56.26% of CO2 emission and the overall cost
of the building could be reduced, respectively.

Recommendations
This report allows for further research on the binding material and potentially replacing
the low-strength concrete with lime mortar to easier and faster disassemble structures. In
a study conducted by students at the Technical University of Denmark [30], concrete and
several types of mortars were tested to obtain their compressive strength, tensile strength,
shear strength, and shear resistance. From the experimental test conducted, they found
that the shear strength of the mortar was reduced remarkably when it contained lime. The
test also showed that the shear resistance and the material strength are directly propor-
tional and thus, lower strength materials have a lower shear resistance.
It was found that the interface between the concrete and the mortar was weaker than
the mortar itself, and that the failure between cement mortar and lime mortar either oc-
curred in the interface between the materials or in the mortar which indicates that lime
mortar is weaker than cement mortar and therefore lime mortar could potentially be used
as a binding material in design for disassembly.
Finally, they proposed a polynomial correlation between the shear resistance and the
cement part in the binding material and a similar correlation for the shear strength, mak-
ing it possible to estimate the strength of any lime mortar.
Further research could be done by using the deduced polynomial correlations to obtain
the shear resistance of a HCS-to-stabilizing wall joint and to possibly obtain a lower up-
lifting to disassemble the slab.
It could also be beneficial to investigate the proposed method for disassembly to dismount
taller structures as this study was limited to low-rise residential buildings.

The behavior of all three proposed connections and a monolithic connection was analyzed 
by subjecting the connections to seismic loading. They also tested the connections with 
several types of fill material: Epoxy resin, grout, natural rubber sheets, and dental plaster. 
The end-plate connection performed similarly to the monolithic connection where the failure 
occurred near the end-plate, proving that the connection was stronger than the beam. 
 
More recently, a dry precast beam-column connection using embedded steel plates and 
threaded bolt was studied by Pul and Sentürk. The end-plate in the beam was welded to the 
main reinforcement in the beam while the end plate in the column was welded to the stirrups 
in the column. Moreover, high-strength nuts were welded to the plate embedded in the 
column to act as threaded ducts to hold the bolts as seen in Figure 10. Experimental test 
were conducted to investigate its behavior when subjected to flexural and shear stresses

Wall/wall and wall/foundation connections: 
Wall/Wall joints mainly compression, friction, and shear force in the connection. The 
compressive stresses can be transferred without physically connecting the elements. The 
friction and the shear force are created as a consequence of the compressive stresses; 
however, due to the flexural stress that the connection will be subjected to, there is still a 
need for mechanical joinery. 
Therefore, an H-shaped steel connection was proposed by Sun et al. in 2016. The H-shape 
embraced the wall at both sides and thus, making it resistant to buckling. High-strength bolt 
pretensioned bolts are used to connect the steel connector to the wall. During testing, a 
monotonic horizontal load was applied to the connection and the results showed that the 
failure happened in the wall but not in the connection. The connection also proved to perform 
well in terms of ductility. Based on FEM modeling, it was found that the connection can be 
further optimized by changing the dimensions of the H-shape. 
Finally, this report noted that the authors were unable to find research on beam-beam, 
column-column, beam-slab, slab-slab and column-foundation connections.

A.4
Site Visit to Circle House
Introduction  
Circle House a project developed by Lejerbo. They are constructing the world's first public housing 
building built according to circular principles. The company claims that all the structural and non-
structural members of the building can be separated and used again i.e. all the materials can be 
recycled. The goal is that 90% of the building's materials can be recycled without losing their 
value. This project consists of 60 public housing units in Lisbjerg outside Aarhus.  
 
Background 
When the building fails to fulfill the owner’s demand or its life span is finished, the current practice 
is to demolish it and dispose of it in a landfill site. By building a new structure with new construction 
materials, it will directly affect the decrement in natural resources, environmental degradation, 
economy, and time consumption.  
 
    
 
Figure 1: a) Demolition of precast structures (left) and b) Landfilling from demolished part 
       
To overcome this, the construction industries are heavily pressured to come up with new 
technologies, and researchers and engineers are working to continuously develop new ideas. 
Circle house has an experienced design team that is continuously modifying its ideas. They have 
developed many useful techniques, where they reuse all the construction materials such as 
structural elements and finishing materials and apply them successfully in their public housing 
buildings.  
 
Design Methodology  
The major cost of the building is mainly governed by the cost of structural members, where the 
main challenge in making the building in circular principle is to make the structural joints 
demountable. In this brief report, the different types of construction joints and their corresponding 
binding material used in Circle House are studied. The typical overview of joints can be seen from 
Figure 2 below.

to be protected against fire and corrosion.

Conclusion 
The Circle House project has managed to be built using only recyclable materials as all the 
structural members are demountable and can be used in a different project in the future. 
Furthermore, the erection time of the structure has been cut down as all the elements are precast. 
Moreover, by using lime mortar as a binding material instead of the traditionally used cement 
mortar, CO2 emission were cut down by up to 30%. Additionally, as the lime mortar was found to 
be flushed in a short amount of time, disassembly can be done quickly. Finally, the developed 
joints for Circle House are safe and the real projects developed by using these technologies are 
concrete example of success.

1.1.2.1
Methodology
A dynamic explicit analysis was conducted as the aim is to model a material where the stress-
strain curve continues after reaching its peak. A dynamic explicit analysis does not have con-
vergence criteria and keeps going in small increments and therefore, it is optimal for damage
modeling and material failure. The material properties for the concrete are shown in details in
Appendix A.1 where the concrete damage plasticity is explained. The rigid bodies are modeled
to illustrate how the applied force will compress the cylinder. No material properties are defined
for the rigid bodies.
To simulate a compression test, the cylinder was fixed at the base and compressed at the
top by a downward displacement. The contact between the cylinder and the rigid bodies was
modeled with general contact for all exterior faces.

Conclusion
The accuracy of the analysis results obtained from Abaqus was verified using several ap-
proaches. Firstly, the results of the ruler example were compared to analytical calculations
and a percentage difference of only 1.90% was obtained. Moreover, the results from the anal-
ysis of the concrete cylinder were compared with results from an actual cylinder compression
test conducted in a laboratory and the results differed only by 0.72%. Furthermore, after an-
alyzing the slab-to-wall joint, the stresses obtained in the 12 MPa low-strength concrete were
only 0.005% different from the calculation of the material properties of the binding material as
per EN 1992-1-1:2004.
Finally, a small study was done on the effect of the mesh size on the accuracy of the results, and
it was found that the results can differ drastically if the wrong mesh size is used and therefore,
a smaller mesh size should be chosen while still considering the time for analysis.
