Abstract 
The aim of this project is to conduct a comparative study of policies controlling the transport sector 
(excluding road freight, shipping, and aviation) in the European Union, focusing on 3 pairwise 
European countries selected according to different criteria to encourage sustainable car use and 
reduce GHG emissions. These was done by reviewing different policies related to transport sector 
(excluding road freight, shipping, and aviation), focusing on both supply and demand-side, applied in 
European Union.  
Before starting with the comparative study and after defining the greenhouse gases, various concepts 
such as sustainable development and its three pillars, the sustainability development goals (SDG's), 
their targets and indicators, the relationship between SDG's and sustainable development, and the 
relation between SDG and sustainable development, were defined. 
Second, the Driver-Pressure-State-Impact-Response (DPSIR) methodology was presented and linked 
to the Transport and Environment Report Mechanism (TERM) set, which is composed of European 
Environment Agency (EEA) indicators, allowing us to provide an overview of GHG emissions emitted 
from the transportation sector. 
Third, a case study was performed, beginning with identifying the numerous indicators present on the 
various methodology components, and then presenting the relationship between the five elements 
of the DPSIR approach. After selecting the appropriate indicators based on several criteria, the efficacy 
of the responses was evaluated, and recommendations were made to assist the governments in 
decreasing GHG emissions engendered by passenger car use. Moreover, before concluding the 
analysis, and as a tool of verification of our recommendations and estimations, a cost comparison of 
EVs in two distinct countries was performed. 
Finally, a conclusion was reached followed by suggestions and possibility to improve the actual project.

1. Introduction 
 Background 
Transport is responsible for around 20% of greenhouse gas emissions within the European Union, 
rendering it the second largest emitting sector after the energy industry. Moreover, greenhouse gas 
emissions (GHG) generated from transport are among the fastest-growing in Europe, with 19% growth 
from 1990 to 2017, posing a challenge in creating a low-carbon future [1] (See Figure 1). 
 
Figure 1 Greenhouse gas emissions growth by aggregated sector [2] 
The EU has designed and implemented a series of measures and regulations for the transport sector 
to turn this trend around and accomplish the corresponding emission targets of net-zero emissions by 
2050. In 2017, the EU Commission presented its proposals for how the reduction target for the non-
ETS sector of the economy should be distributed among member states. It also has fixed a climate 
target for 2030 to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, from parts of the economy not included 
in the Emissions Trading Scheme (Non-ETS), by 30 percent compared to 2005. The non-ETS part of the 
economy consists mainly of agriculture, transport, and individual heating for residential and 
commercial buildings. 
To review the present and planned policies, focusing on both supply and demand-side, undertaken by 
EU member states and the G20 to reduce transport emissions, a comparative analysis of different 
policies regulating the road transport sector will be carried out.

 Sustainable development 
Sustainable development can be defined as a way for people to use resources without the exhaustion 
of those resources. This involves developing without impacting the environment or damaging it. The 
expression ‘’Sustainability development’’ was used by the Brundtland Commission2 and defined as a 
development that "meets the needs of the present and also compromising the ability of future 
generations to meet their own needs." 
2.2.1. 
The three pillars of sustainable development 
Whatever the problems in any community, country, or region, they can usually be grouped into three 
issues based on people needs [4]: 
• 
A better environment – that means greener places, good garden areas, play areas, no 
garbage, minimized noise and traffic – a healthier environment. 
• 
A better economy means jobs, reasonable prices, cheaper heat, light and water, and no loan 
sharks. 
• 
A better social condition means good leisure facilities, many community groups offering 
sports and arts, and friendly neighbors.  
These 3 issues represent the pillars of the approach ‘‘sustainable development’’.

2.2.2. 
Sustainability Development Goals (SDGs) 
The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) or Global Goals are a series of 17 interconnected global 
goals created to be a "blueprint for achieving a better and more sustainable future for all" [6]. It applies 
to all nations and means making sure no one is left behind. Simply, SDGs define the world we want. 
The SDGs were established by the General Assembly of the United Nations in 2015 and are expected 
to be completed by 2030. They are a part of a UN Resolution called the 2030 Agenda for sustainability 
or what is known as Agenda 2030 [7]. 
The 17 SDGs are: (1) No Poverty, (2) Zero Hunger, (3) Good Health and Well-being, (4) Quality 
Education, (5) Gender Equality, (6) Clean Water and Sanitation, (7) Affordable and Clean Energy, (8) 
Decent Work and Economic Growth, (9) Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure, (10) Reducing 
Inequality, (11) Sustainable Cities and Communities, (12) Responsible Consumption and Production, 
(13) Climate Action, (14) Life Below Water, (15) Life On Land, (16) Peace, Justice, and Strong 
Institutions, (17) Partnerships for the Goals.

2.2.5. 
Sustainable car use 
To reach sustainability of car use, the vehicle has to meet such requirements ranging from engine 
function, fuel use, emissions of carbon, frequency of maintenance, type of equipment, road impacts, 
vehicle regenerative energy systems, recyclability of worn parts to readiness for manufacturers to 
recycle unusable vehicles, sustainable training for driving technology. 
In practice, sustainability always refers to cost, which includes owning and operating a car. This means, 
for now, a sustainable car might just refer to three things: (i) high energy efficiency for whatever 
fuel/energy source type, (ii) green and clean as possible, and (iii) affordable for the mass market. 
 Contribution of the transport sector and car use to GHG 
emissions 
As considered in previous sections, transport is a major contributor to GHG emissions, one-third of all 
produced gasses are due to the transport sector [14]. In motorized transportation, exhaust fumes 
containing particulates that affect human health and contribute to climate change are also released 
[15].  
Transport systems are responsible for 23% of the global energy-related GHG emissions in 2004, and 
about three-quarters of global GHG emissions are coming from road vehicles. According to The 
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), 95% of transport energy comes from petroleum. 
Energy is consumed in the manufacture and the use of vehicles and is embodied in transport 
infrastructure, including roads and bridges.

Since many EU countries are highly automobile-oriented and the main transit mode that people use 
is personal vehicles. It was valued that about 80% of EU citizens travel, involves cars [14]. These 
reasons let one believe that car use is a high and main contributor to GHG emissions. 
 Can we (How to) reduce greenhouse gas emissions from the 
transportation sector in a sustainable way? 
Some EU countries are making transportation more sustainable in both long and short-term 
implementations [16]. An example is a transition in transport system available, in Germany, in 
Freiburg, where the city has adopted extensive public transport, cycling, and walking methods, and 
large areas in which cars are not permitted. [14].  
The policymakers have implemented both long-term and short-term strategies to achieve their 
objectives by applying sustainable transport policies. Some of the EU's largest towns have relatively 
sustainable transport; Examples include Amsterdam, where 28% of the journeys are made by walking, 
31% by bike, 18% by public transit. In Paris, 23% of trips are done by an automobile while 53% are by 
foot, 3% use bicycles and 34% by public transit. Walking is also the most common transport route in 
the entire area of Ile-de-France [17]. In addition, 62% of people travel to school or jobs by bicycle in 
Copenhagen [18]. 
Some of the proposals, policies, or regulations to minimize the total number of vehicle trips and 
reducing greenhouse gases emissions are related to: 
• 
Increase the cost of internal combustion vehicles by increasing different acquisition and use 
taxes, encouraging people to drive more fuel-efficient vehicles. However, these policies can 
create a social equity problem since lower-income people usually drive older vehicles with 
lower fuel efficiency, which was applied in many EU countries). 
• 
Encourage walking and biking by providing more comprehensive pedestrian pathways, bike-
sharing stations in downtowns, locating parking lots far from the shopping center, limiting 
street parking, and slower traffic paths in the center of the cities. 
• 
Improve public transport by providing a larger coverage area, to provide more mobility and 
accessibility, new technology to provide a more efficient and flexible public transportation 
network. In addition, to boost public transport, the government can use the extra revenue 
from taxes and tolls to help poorer communities.

3. Sustainability assessment methodology 
To get a comprehensive overview of the environmental problems related to the transport sector and 
the progress of sustainable transport goals, a set of indicators will be used to assess the policies from 
the 3 different pillars of sustainability, namely: the economy, society, and the environment. 
 From P-S-R to D-P-S-I-R 
The P-S-R (Pressure, State, and Response) system is the original form of a ‘’linkages-based’’ 
framework. 
The D-P-S-I-R, or the ‘’linkage-based’’ framework, was adopted by the European Environmental 
Agency (EEA) and was defined as: ‘’The causal framework for describing the interactions between 
society and the environment: Human impact on the environment and vice versa’’ [19]. It is 
characterized by social and economic developments drivers (D) leading to impacts (I). Social and 
economic developments drivers (D) exert pressure (P), generating a change on the state (S), leading 
to Impacts (I) on the environment and finally, societal, and political responses (R) affecting previous 
elements of the framework, directly or indirectly [20].

 EEA Indicators and TERM set 
To provide an overview of the GHG emissions from the transport sector (what is happening and why), 
the use of Transport and Environment Report Mechanism (TERM) set, composed of European 
Environment Agency indicators (EEA indicators), allows D-P-S-I-R frameworks to help on problems 
management during various elements of the methodology chain, and eventually learn how a change 
in response (lowering speed limits or use of cleaner and alternative fuels) reduces a certain pressure 
(noise and GHG emissions from traffic) and eventually improves the state of the environment (ambient 
noise level and GHG emission level in norms). 
3.2.1. 
European Environmental Agency Indicators 
The European Environment Agency (EEA indicators) indicators will be used to analyze and evaluate 
the environmental policy related to the road transport sector in the EU. The EEA currently maintains 
122 indicators covering 13 environmental topics and can be divided into different sets. Furthermore, 
indicators can be classified into different types [21]. Criteria for choosing the indicator and defining 
the characteristics of an excellent environmental measure are presented in Appendices 2: Criteria for 
selecting EAA indicators 
3.2.2. 
Typology of EEA indicators 
The EEA typology was developed in response to an indicator classification that allows a more detailed 
analysis of environment-society interactions. Therefore, the different types of EEA indicators were 
created to help in a more detailed way the analysis of environment-society interactions and were 
classified as descriptive (Type A), performance (Type B), efficiency (Type C), policy effectiveness (Type 
D) or total welfare (Type E), depending on which environmental challenge they address and which 
stage of the policy cycle they aim to inform [21]. 
▪ 
Descriptive indicators (A): can be used for all elements of DPSIR. First, they respond to the 
question, what is happening? Then, they describe the actual situation as concerns 
environmental issues. 
▪ 
Performance indicators (B): may use the same variables as descriptive indicators. They 
answer the question, does it matter? And are we reaching targets? In other words, these 
indicators measure progress and are linked to policy responses. 
==PAGE:26==
GHG reductions in European car use – A comparative transport policy study 
10 
▪ 
Efficiency indicators (C): They answers the question: Are we improving? They express the 
relationship between drivers and pressures (in the DPSIR framework) and are commonly 
expressed per capita or unit of GDP. 
▪ 
Policy effectiveness indicators (D): the answer to the question Are the measure working? 
They connect the change in environmental variables to policy measures, linking responses to 
other elements of the DPSIR framework. 
▪ 
Total welfare indicators (E): they respond to the question: Are we, overall, better off? They 
provide a general measure of sustainability, integrating economic, environmental, and social 
considerations. Such EEA indicators do not exist.

4. Case analysis 
 introduction 
Economic development and industrialization have led to population growth and rapid urbanization in 
Europe. This economic growth enabled cities to be hubs of manufacturing, trading, and governance. 
On the other hand, it also generated environmental and health problems due to weakening 
environmental conditions caused mainly by industrial and anthropic activities. Due to the economic 
and population growth the car use has increased during the last decades, this fact has led to high GHG 
emissions to the air. According to the last statistics within the EU-28, transport is responsible for 
around 20% of greenhouse gas emissions, rendering it the second largest emitting sector after the 
energy industry. The EU has fixed a climate target for 2030 to reduce GHG emissions from non-ETS by 
30 percent compared to 2005. 
 Objectives and goals 
To review the present and planned policies, focusing on both supply and demand-side, accepted by 
EU member states to reduce transport emissions, a comparative analysis of different policies 
regulating the private road transport sector will be carried out. 
 DPSIR Framework 
Modern problems, like air pollution and urban growth, are difficult to resolve and often surpass spatial 
and temporal scales, that is why the DPSIR framework is used to analysis the actual situation of the 
transport sector and its impact on the environment and GHG emissions. To reach the aim of the study, 
we start first with the alignment of the data and details on all 5 elements of the DPSIR model. Then 
potential relations between the various elements of the model chain will be described. Finally, the 
effectiveness of the responses can be measured applying the DPSIR modeling framework by using the 
set related to transport topic, Transport and Environment Report Mechanism (TERM), a set of 
indicators provided by the European Environment Agency (EEA indicators).

4.4.1. 
Indicator’s data 
Environmental indicators summarize, simplify, and communicate more complicated data sets: ‘’an 
environmental indicator is a measure, generally quantitative, that can be used to illustrate and 
communicate complex environmental phenomena simply, including trends and progress over time’’ 
[23] and helping to offer an overview into the state of the environment. 
4.4.2. 
Driving forces 
Driving forces are the drivers which motivate human activity and meet fundamental human needs. 
Socioeconomic trends or conditions leading to pressure are also present. They are a fundamental 
reason for the environmental concern. Following drivers can be observed for the case: 
• 
Transport of passengers: It can be considered as a need for people living in a region. As an 
economic driving force, the transportation sector, during the last decades, is living a fast 
development rise. 
• 
Car ownership growth: As a result of the European, socio and economic development, 
population, and economic growth, the number of vehicles in the EU is quickly increasing. 
• 
Energy use: As a result of the car ownership growth, an increase in energy consummation is 
observed in Europe. 
4.4.3. 
Pressures 
Driving forces lead to human activities. As consequences of production (car production) or 
consumption (car use) processes, human activities, transportation apply pressure on the environment, 
contributing to a change in the state. Flowing pressures were found and can be used for the case: 
• 
Use of resources: As a result of the driving forces, the consumption and production of 
resources processes (such as primary energy sources) are submitting a pressure. 
• 
Emission to air: As a result of the rapid growth of car ownership, air emissions exert pressure 
on the environment (for example, GHG emissions). 
==PAGE:30==
GHG reductions in European car use – A comparative transport policy study 
14 
• 
Production of noise: As a result of the growth of car ownership, the noise effect is also 
submitting a pressure. 
4.4.4. 
States 
The state of the environment is affected by the pressure. The state can be a condition of the 
atmosphere, biosphere, lithosphere, or aqua sphere (environmental quality). Flowing states were 
observed for the analysis of the case: 
• 
Air quality: Is an environmental influence and it can be regional and/or national. Many studies 
show that due to the rapid growth of car ownership and car market share, air emissions were 
boosted in the last decades, reducing air quality. 
• 
Health and physical well-being: Are societal effects of the study issue. It is a well-being 
impact on people living in the same region and a health effect due to the bad air quality 
caused by car emissions. 
• 
Environmental damage: This is an environmental influence resulting from decreased air 
quality due to car use and GHG emissions. 
• 
Ecosystem: The ecosystem's state can be influenced by the emissions to air from car use. 
4.4.5. 
Impacts 
This is the economic, environmental, and societal impact of a change in the States. It determines the 
quality of ecosystems and the welfare of human beings. Flowing impacts were noticed for the case: 
• 
Air pollution: Cars GHG emissions create a bad impact on the air quality. It depletes the quality 
of the air very badly.  
• 
Affecting human health: Human health is also severely affected by GHG emissions. The 
engines are responsible for emitting a high level of pollutants which act as airborne particles 
like soot and metals. These toxins cause irritation and allergies to the skin and eyes. In 
addition, even fine particles are often inhaled by people, which leads to respiratory problems 
affecting the lungs. For example, ozone is beneficial for the upper layer of the atmosphere, 
but inhaling it causes chest pain and cough and is tough to breathe in. 
• 
Environmental and ecosystem damages:  Engine oils, brake oils, and other such lubricants are 
thrown on the soil or in water resources like rivers, sea, lakes, which contaminates the water 
(environmental damage) and leads to the death of the water animals (ecosystems damage). 
Other pollutants like sulphur dioxide and nitrogen oxide, when mixed with rainwater, leads to 
==PAGE:31==
GHG reductions in European car use – A comparative transport policy study 
15 
acid rains. Acid rains badly damage the crop quality, forests, plantations, other vegetation, 
and buildings. 
• 
Noise pollution: The noise produced by cars causes noise pollution, which is bad for the ears 
and often leads to physiological problems. 
• 
Destruction of natural resources: Producing cars use a large amount of steel, iron, plastics, 
rubber, and other materials. Also, the production processes consume a significant portion of 
energy, directly or indirectly affecting natural resources. 
• 
Global warming: Cars run by consuming fuel and energy. Carbon dioxide and carbon 
monoxide are the major constituents in the exhausts released by cars. The engines get worn 
up with time and cannot burn the fuel in the required ratio. This inability leads to carbon 
monoxide yield, which harms the ozone layer leading to global warming. 
4.4.6. 
Responses 
It is a response done by the society or policies makers because of an undesired impact. It usually offers 
some possible solutions to the problem. It can affect any components of the DPSIR chain from 'D' to 
'R' components, which means that a response can be feedback on the Driving force, the Pressures, the 
State, or the Impact component. Flowing responses were considered for the case: 
• 
Prioritizing: This is the first step of the response element and is the step where the impacts 
will be prioritized following the different stakeholders' criteria. 
• 
The setting of targets: it is the second process, in the response element of the DPSIR, which 
came after the prioritizing; it is the process to establish performance goals for the sector. 
• 
Policies: It can be environmental, transport, and macroeconomic policies. 
o Taxes on vehicles and their use: Are economic responses related to reducing cars 
emissions, mainly the GHG reduction. (Taxes on cars using nonrenewable energy, zero 
taxes on electric vehicles, parking taxation.) 
o Road fuel price: Are economic response. (Higher fuel price can solve high resource 
consumption, even if it disturbs the equity goals). 
o Subsidies to transport: Are economic response. They are related to car emission 
reduction (Using the subsidies to promote public transport or another mode shifting 
can help reduce GHG emissions, limiting speed in different zones of cities).

Fragmentation of land and ecosystems 
The fragmentation of land and ecosystem is an indicator describing the appearance or development 
of discontinuities (fragmentation) in an organism (environment), causing population fragmentation 
and ecosystem decay. Causes of land and ecosystem fragmentation include geological processes that 
slowly alter the layout of the physical environment and human activity, such as land conversion, which 
can alter the environment much faster and causes the extinction of many species. More specifically, 
fragmentation is a process by which large and contiguous habitats get divided into smaller, isolated 
patches of habitats. 
According to the data from the EEA [36], in 2015, 1.13 million km2, or over 28% of the EU's total area, 
was highly fragmented, registering an increase of 0.7 percent from 2009. The most affected by strong 
fragmentation pressure in 2015 in the EU were arable lands and permanent croplands (around 42.6%) 
and pastures and farmland mosaics (around 40.2%).

Registration tax 
All the concerned countries have an applicable registration tax for all vehicles except Luxembourg and 
Germany. In Luxembourg, the owners do not have to pay a registration tax. Still, when a new or a used 
car is registered in France, the purchaser must pay some taxes. There are two types of taxes applied 
to a vehicle registration in France, (1) the ‘’taxe Regional’’ and (2) the ‘’taxe sur les véhicule polluants’’. 
(1) The regional tax is fixed by the municipality and does not vary considerably across the country. It 
is calculated depending on the emission of the car to the air, following the formula: PF = (CO₂/45) + 
(P/40) ^1.6 Where: PF = Puissance Fiscale, CO₂ = Carbon Emission (gr/km) and P = Horsepower (1 hp = 
0,736 kW). The tax is reduced by half for automobiles that are more than ten years old. The 
municipalities additionally provide a 100% or 50% exemption for cars that operate on clean energy,

are free from levy tax as of January 1, 2020. (2) The ‘’taxe sur les véhicule polluants’’ tax is payable for 
both new and second use cars. Additional ecotax is charged on the purchase of a vehicle and is 
determined by the level of CO2 emissions. The levy for 2020 varies between €0 (vehicle with emissions

In Germany, the registration of a new or used car is exempt from a registration tax. Still, in Poland,

Car use: Use of transport infrastructure and tolls: ROAD CHARGES 
The road charge is a car use tax charged on the passenger cars during the uses of different road 
infrastructure as public or private roads, bridges, tunnels. It also includes road tolls, fees, or congestion 
charges. These charges are collected by the government, municipality, or private organizations. Their 
amount can be fixed for the whole infrastructure length or calculated depending on the distance 
traveled on the infrastructure. 
• 
Road charges: 
Road charges on national motorways in more than half of the concerned European countries are 
applied. The remaining countries either do not generally charge for road infrastructure, such as 
Luxembourg, or impose charges only on certain roads, tunnels, or bridges, such as Germany. 
Moreover, no country offers exemptions or reduced road charges for low-emission vehicles. 
Road charges in Luxembourg are not applicable, so car users don't have to pay any tax for the road 
infrastructure using but in France, car users have an applicable tax in the form of tolls that are payable 
by passenger cars on most motorways; some bridges and tunnels also collect tolls. The French Road 
Charging Network is the largest in Europe, with an overall length of more than 9,000 km. Tolls are 
payable for each use and are usually due when exiting a motorway. Most of these roads are managed 
by operating companies based on concessions granted by the government. There are no reduced rates 
for low-emission vehicles (DGCCRF, 2013; APRR, 2018; ASECAP, 2018). 
Passenger car owners currently pay no road charges in Germany except for selected private roads. But 
Motorways in Poland are charged through the toll gates system for vehicles weighing up to 3.5 t. Toll 
motorways for vehicles weighing up to 3.5 t include the three main motorways A1, A2 and A4. Several 
companies provide the collection of tolls in Poland. The main difference is the possibility of payment 
for passenger cars with a trailer and vehicles weighing over 3.5 t. 
Motorway charges for motorcycles and vehicles up to 3.5 t are collected in Slovenia with the help of 
vignettes, which are on sale with a validity of a week, month, and year. In the western part of the 
country, at the border crossing with Austria, a toll is collected for the passage of the Karavanke tunnel.

Car use: Private use of company car: TAX ON PRIVAT USE OF COMPANY 
CAR 
Tax on private use of company cars is levied periodically to car users who use these cards for a private 
reason (a trip to the workplace is considered private use). The governments or institutions collect 
these taxes. Their amount is calculated depending on the vehicle price, the type of the engine and the 
traveled kilometers, type of acquisition: purchased or leased, and in the case of a purchase, the car's 
age, or a combination of this criterion. 
Namely, a corporation's car is owned or rented by a firm but might be offered for private usage by 
personnel. The usage of a corporate automobile is common in Europe, thanks partly to tax benefits 
for businesses. For example, corporate automobiles accounted for 64% of new car registrations in 
Germany in 2018 [44]. In most European nations, private use of a corporate automobile is considered 
a benefit in kind for income tax purposes. In addition, companies in Germany receive a particular 
discount on the car's list price dependent on battery capacity. 
• 
Tax on private use of company car: 
The provision of a company car (purchased or leased by the employer) is a common practice in 
Luxembourg and is part of the compensation policy of many employers. There are 2 methods for 
evaluating the benefit in kind. (1) The flat-rate method: The benefit in kind is assessed at a monthly 
flat rate of the value of the vehicle when new (including options and VAT) less any discount given by 
the seller with a rate varying between 0,5% and 1,8%, respectively for an electric vehicle with zero-
emission and diesel vehicle with CO2 emissions higher than 150g/km. The evaluation of the benefit in 
==PAGE:64==
GHG reductions in European car use – A comparative transport policy study 
48 
kind is identical for used cars too. (2) The logbook method: The employee keeps a logbook and records 
the number of kilometers traveled when the vehicle is used in a professional capacity. The number of 
kilometers traveled when the vehicle is used in a private capacity equals the difference between the 
total number of kilometers traveled and the number traveled when used in a professional capacity. 
Thus, the taxable benefit amount corresponds to the ratio of the cost of the vehicle and the kilometers 
traveled, multiplied by the private kilometers. 
In France, an employee's private use of a company car is subject to social contributions and income 
tax. There are two methods to calculate the benefit in kind of a company car, both depending on 
whether the car has been purchased or leased and the car's age in the case of a purchase. The first 
method is based on a fixed percentage of the car’s list price, defined as the price provided by car 
manufacturers, including VAT and registration tax. Thus, for example, if the employer pays for fuel for 
private use, the taxable benefit for the employee is 12% of the car's list price. On the other hand, if 
the employee pays for the fuel, the taxable amount declines to 9%. The second calculation method is 
based on real expenses, including annual depreciation from the vehicle's purchase price, including 
VAT, insurance and maintenance costs, and fuel costs for private use. 
In Germany, it is used two methods for taxing the private use of a company car. The first is the 1% 
method, in which the monthly benefit in kind is set at 1% of the new car’s list price, totaling 12% per 
year. A monthly surcharge of 0.03%, based on the vehicle’s list price times the one-way distance 
between home and workplace, is added if the company car is also used for trips between home and 
workplace. The result is the taxable amount to be declared by the employee as part of income tax. In 
addition, electric company cars benefit from a special bonus plan introduced by the German 
government in 2013. The bonus amount is calculated per kWh of the battery capacity. For example, 
electric company cars bought in 2018 receive a flat-rate reduction of €250/kWh of battery capacity 
with a maximum of €7,500. As a result of the bonus, the new car's list price is reduced, making the 
amount payable through income tax. An alternative to the 1% method is the logbook method, in which 
the tax amount is calculated based on total actual vehicle costs, including depreciation, fuel, 
insurance, and maintenance. In Poland, the value of such benefits is fixed at PLN 250 per month for 
cars with engine capacity of up to 1600 cm3 or PLN 400 per month for cars with engine capacity of 
over 1600 cm3. These amounts are properly credited to the employee's income under his employment 
relationship. 
In Slovenia and according to the new amendment, an electric motor vehicle provided to an employee 
by the company and used for private purposes will result in taxable income.  This inclusion is required

Subsidies on car acquisition 
Subsidies to car use are economic responses. Therefore, they are related to car emission reduction. In

Luxembourg is applying for a subsidies program, “Clever driving”, for electric vehicles with a plan of

the plug-in hybrid support will continue until the actual year. In May 2020, the government raised the 
purchase premium for 100% electric vehicles from €5,000 to €8,000, but only for models with 
electricity consumption of less than 18 kWh per 100 kilometers. For electric cars that consume more, 
the amount of the bonus is restricted to €3,000. But electric cars with seven or more seats are still to

Discussion 
To illustrate the effect of policies on GHG emissions, let's look at how much a same car model cost in 
the Netherlands and France. The results of the ownership costs analysis done in the precedent section 
for both selected markets, show the overall calculation cost in Netherlands and France of 4 VW Golf 
variants. The resale value of a vehicle was not considered when calculating the four-year expenses, 
which would effectively reduce the acquisition cost and consequently, the overall four-year ownership 
costs. 
The comparison shows that the Golf BEV variant is the most-affordable choice across both markets 
based on ownership costs over four years. The cost-benefit of the BEV model compared with the 
gasoline version is most pronounced in France, at almost €2,500 excluding road charges (€80), and 
least distinct in the Netherlands at just €400. But this picture is not reflected on the actual market 
share as in France there are 2.1% of EV from the total fleet and 6.69% in Netherlands during 2018. 
This statistic is different today as the EV market share in Netherlands during 2020 is leading the French 
market share with respectively 24.6% vs 11.2% and moreover the Netherlands market share of EV is 
considered as one of the best in the EU after Sweden. This let us to believe that when the Netherlands 
had imposed less levies on EVs, had taken a good choice, and the results are now in favor of the 
government's objective to reduce GHG emissions from car sector.

The diesel variant incurs the highest costs in the Netherlands, despite a lower base price, this is 
attributable to significantly higher taxes. This proves the importance to have high taxes for high

choice if he planned to buy a diesel car due to the high cost in the period of 4 years use.

 Policies recommendations to reduce GHG emissions from private 
and/or company car use: 
To help the decision-makers, and in accordance with the analysis and data reviewed in the different 
sections of this report, the following recommendation can be applied to reach the individual goals of 
each country on GHG reduction: 
• 
Increase of VAT for internal combustion vehicles and a decrease or abandons it for EVs: 
==PAGE:84==
GHG reductions in European car use – A comparative transport policy study 
68 
As previously stated, the amount of VAT has a direct impact on national GHG emissions due to vehicle 
prices (including VAT). When prices are low, people will buy more vehicles, causing an increase in 
emissions. As a tool for GHG emissions reduction, an increase of VAT on internal combustion vehicles 
and a decrease or a waives of VAT imposed to EVs can be a tool to minimize pollution. Contrary to 
SDGs, if VAT is raised, internal combination vehicle market share will drop, and wealthy individuals 
will be privileged to use IC cars, resulting in inequality between classes. But this problem can be 
avoided by the eventual waives of VAT imposed on EVs. A decrease in the VAT can be recommended 
for PHEV but can’t be supposed as an effective solution as that kind of cars still pollutant but with less 
proportion. Both approaches increase the cost difference and reduce the initial cost of vehicles with 
low CO2 emissions. 
The same effect can be achieved by exempting vehicles with low emissions from registration tax. 
• 
Registration tax: Increase for internal combustion vehicles and a decrease for EVs: 
The registration tax policy is playing a key role in the emissions reduction. As observed, countries with 
fewer emissions per capita are the countries considering the emission of cars during the calculation 
of the registration tax. This is by making the owner of pollutants cars pay more than the owner of the 
eco-friendly vehicles. After the base price including VAT is come the registration tax with strong 
influence on consumer choice. This plays an important role in influencing consumer behavior. That's 
why an update of the registration tax is required to reduce the GHG emission provided by transport. 
• 
Increase of Motor Vehicles tax for internal combustion vehicles and a decrease it for EVs: 
A decrease in Motor vehicle tax for cars with low emissions can be considered a solution tool to be 
used by the governments to reduce GHG emissions. It is recommended to impose a high tax on high 
emitters cars (ICV) and decrease the tax for eco-friendly vehicles. As the low emitter vehicles are still 
more expensive than the high emitter cars, the question of sustainability can be raised again here. 
• 
Fuel and electricity taxes and prices: 
It can be recommended that the government use higher fuel prices to limit the high resources 
consumption influencing the emissions level, even if it disturbs the equity goals. However, it was 
observed that countries with high emissions have a high tax share of electricity prices when it comes 
to electricity. This fact makes the increase of electricity's tax a non-reasonable recommendation as a 
tool for GHG reduction. That's mean a fuel price/tax policy can be increased but not for electricity.

5. Conclusions 
Car use has grown in recent decades because of economic and demographic expansion, resulting in 
significant GHG emissions into the atmosphere. According to the last statistics within the EU-28, 
transport is responsible for around 20% of greenhouse gas emissions, rendering it the second largest 
emitting sector after the energy industry. The EU has fixed a climate target for 2030 to reduce GHG 
emissions from non-ETS by 30 percent compared to 2005. 
Many policies have been adopted in the EU with the goal of controlling and reducing GHG emissions. 
This master’s thesis aims to conduct a comparative study of policies controlling the transport sector 
(excluding road freight, shipping, and aviation) in the European Union, focusing on 3 pairwise 
European countries selected according to different criteria to encourage sustainable car use and 
reduce GHG emissions. 
In the perspective of sustainable development, the transportation system, like most other systems, 
offers a complicated challenge to address. It is characterized by intense interconnections across the 
social, technological, economic, and environmental spheres. DPSIR modeling was used to analyze the 
existing status of the transportation sector, as well as its influence on the environment and GHG 
emissions. The efficacy of the responses was then assessed by the same technique (DPSIR) using the 
set related to transport topic, Transport and Environment Report Mechanism (TERM) provided by the 
European Environment Agency (EEA indicators).

6. Future work 
Up to two-thirds of emissions could be slashed by 2030 if countries endorse more ambitious 
‘sustainable development’ targets. While progress is being made, electric cars currently still negligible 
in the European market share. Even though numerous policies and regulations in the transport sector 
have been implemented in the EU, some of them have not been evaluated in this study and can be 
used as GHG reduction measures to help the EU meet its goals. Some of these instruments may be 
recognized, such as speed limits in various city zones, parking fees and taxes, and so on. Reuse of 
resources: fuel and electricity utilized during the manufacturing and usage processes, access to bus 
lines for EVs, construction of appropriate charging infrastructure powered by renewable energy, and 
so on. 
Also following topics concerning the significant barriers to the general adoption of EVs can be added 
to improve the actual report. Insufficient charging infrastructure continues to prevent wider use. 
Despite falling battery costs, rising vehicle production to meet demand, and the promise of savings 
over the lifetime of an EV from lower fuel and maintenance costs, upfront prices remain prohibitive 
for some. On the supply side, there are also challenges related to the poor sustainability levels 
associated with EV batteries: the sourcing of raw materials is frequently concentrated in a few 
developing countries that are often politically volatile and economically fragile. A related concern is 
around recyclability. EV batteries consist of multiple Lithium-ion cells that are largely difficult to 
dismantle, and which contain hazardous materials. But there are some recent examples of the industry 
responding to this challenge (Nissan and Volkswagen are reusing recycling old batteries for production 
of new EV).

Appendices 2: Criteria for selecting EAA indicators 
In the early 1990s, the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Cooperation (OECD) became the 
leader in developing climate metrics at the international level. This work included the setup of 
parameters for the indicator's choice. Also, the definition of the characteristics of a good 
environmental measure [22]. 
Table 10 OCED criteria for selecting environmental indicators [22] 
Policy relevance and 
utility for users 
An environmental indicator should: 
▪ 
provide a representative picture of environmental conditions, 
pressures on the environment or society's responses. 
▪ 
be simple, easy to interpret and able to show trends over time.  
▪ 
be responsive to changes in the environment and related human 
activities. 
▪ 
provide a basis for international comparisons. 
▪ 
be either national in scope or applicable to regional environmental 
issues of national significance. 
▪ 
Have a threshold or reference value against which it can be compared 
so that users can assess the significance of the values associated with 
it. 
Analytical soundness 
An environmental indicator should: 
▪ 
be theoretically well-founded in technical and scientific terms. 
▪ 
Be based on international standards and international consensus 
concerning its validity. 
▪ 
Lend itself to being linked to economic models, forecasting and 
information systems. 
Measurability 
The data required to support the indicator should be: 
▪ 
readily available or made available at a reasonable cost/benefit ratio. 
▪ 
Adequately documented and of known quality. 
▪ 
updated at regular intervals by reliable procedures
