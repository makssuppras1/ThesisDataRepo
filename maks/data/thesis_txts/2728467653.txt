Abstract  
 
The advancements in non-viral gene editing in recent years have facilitated the optimization of 
cell engineering leading to the development of highly personalised therapies for patients. In the 
field of cancer immunotherapy, where the use of chimeric antigen receptor T cells (CAR-T) 
and TCR knock in (KI) cells keep growing, non-viral gene editing strategies could relieve some 
of the concerns attributed to the use of its viral counterpart which implies random integration 
and strict GMP requirements.  
 
The project covered in this master thesis has focused on the generation of cancer specific CD8+ 
T cells towards a known cancer testis antigen NY-ESO-1 by the electroporation of a 
ribonucleoprotein complex (RNP) together with a double stranded DNA (dsDNA) as 
Homology directed repair template (HDRT). The KI strategy chosen in this project does not 
rely on achieving the highest KI efficacy right after transfection but rather on expanding the 
KI T cell populations using the pMHC-costimulatory scaffold technology. The TCR-T cells 
has been expanded by the scaffold technology for 10 days alongside cells expanded with 
peptide and cytokine. The stimulated cells have been compared for expansion capability and 
functionality. The results point to the scaffold technology, being an effective method to 
increase the number of specific cells when compared to the peptide and cytokine stimulation 
control. Moreover, the functional integrity of the scaffold expanded cells seems to show a more 
memory-like phenotype as well as greater killing ability as compared to the peptide- and 
cytokine control. 
 
These findings highlight the huge potential that non-viral gene editing possesses in cancer T 
cell immunotherapy, and their potential use in cases associated with developing precise and 
effective therapies for diseases such as cancer.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1 Basic immunobiology 
 
The human body has an incredible ability to adapt foreign substances and pathogens. This is 
possible due to the immune system, composed of highly specialized organs and cell types able 
to distinguish self from non-self. The cells of the immune system originate from pluripotent 
hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) in the bone marrow which are differentiated into an innate and 
an adaptive compartment. The innate immune system is the first line of defense against 
pathogens and is generally composed of macrophages, monocytes, dendritic cells, 
granulocytes, NK-cells, and mast cells, whereas the adaptive immune system is engaged days 
to weeks after the initial infection with the act of lymphocytes, B and T cells. The innate 
immune cells are equipped with pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) that enable the 
recognition of a limited set of conserved pathogenic motifs referred to as pathogen associated 
molecular patterns (PAMPs) such as the bacterial lipopolysaccharide (LPS) or viral nucleic 
acids. In contrast, T and B cells possess highly specialized receptors, the T cell receptor (TCR) 
and B-cell receptor (BCR), that recognize a vast array of antigens. The BCR on B cells can 
recognize free antigens, proteins, lipids, and carbohydrates whereas the TCR is restricted to 
peptides presented on the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) on the surface of antigen 
presenting cells (Marshall et al., 2018; Vivier & Malissen, 2005).  
 
Upon an infection, the first line of defense will comprise the physical barrier, of the skin and 
the mucosal surface in the airway and gut. In the mucosal surfaces mucus will trap pathogens 
and cilia in the airway will try to push the mucus out of the airway and into the highly acidic 
stomach. If the pathogen breaches the physical barriers, it will encounter the second line of 
defense, represented by the innate immune system that will sense and react in the presence of 
the pathogen binding of the pathogenic components such as the double stranded RNA (dsRNA) 
to the designated PRR e.g., Toll-Like receptor 3 (TLR-3) initiating intracellular events inducing 
the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines and type I interferons shaping the immune 
response towards that particular pathogen and the recruitment of further immune cells to the 
site of infection (Kawai & Akira, 2009). The third line of defense will be engaged as the 
infection continues since antigens derived from the pathogen, as well as activated DCs, will 
migrate to nearby draining lymph nodes (LNs) where naïve T cells will be activated and 
differentiate into effector cells e.g., cytotoxic T cells (CTLs) capable of inducing cellular 
killing towards the infected cells (Worbs et al., 2017).

1.2 T cells  
 
1.2.1 T cell Development  
 
In the bone marrow HSCs are differentiated into an innate progenitor and a common lymphoid 
progenitor (CLP). The CLP that remains in the bone marrow, differentiates into B-cells through 
interactions with bone marrow stromal cells. In contrast to B cells that develop in the bone 
marrow, T cells develop in the thymus situated just above the heart. The progenitor cells that 
enter the thymus through high endothelium venules (HEV) are triggered to proliferate and 
become thymocytes committed to the T cell lineage by the interaction between Notch1, a 
receptor found on the precursor cells, with its ligand expressed on the thymic stroma. This 
induces the expression of two transcription factors, T cell factor-1 (TCF1) and GATA binding 
protein 3 (GATA3) which initiate the expression of several T-lineage specific genes, such as 
those encoding components of the CD3 complex, as well as Rag1 and Bcl11b (Germain, 2002; 
Naito et al., 2011). At this stage these precursor T cells are referred to as double-negative 
thymocytes (DN) because they lack CD4 and CD8. There are four stages of double-negative 
thymocytes (DN1, DN2, DN3, and DN4) depending on the differentiation state. DN1 resides 
at the cortico-medullary junction of the thymus and begins to express receptors such as CD117 
that is associated with cellular growth, proliferation, and survival of these precursor cells (Wei 
et al., 2017). The DN1 will then differentiate into a DN2 cell and migrate to the outer edge of 
the cortex where it expresses the α chain of the IL-2 receptor (CD25) and begins to rearrange 
the β-chain of the TCR (Dβ-Jβ rearrangement). The DN2 cells are irreversibly committed to 
the T cell lineage. The DN3 state is achieved when the rearrangement of the β-chain is 
completed and tested by associating it with a surrogate pre-TCRα chain, which allows the 
assembly of a complete pre-TCR. The pre-TCR is expressed on the cell surface in a complex 
with the CD3 molecule providing the signalling components of TCR. The pre-TCR signalling 
pathway causes the differentiation and proliferation of the DN3 into DN4. At the end of the 
proliferation phase of the DN4 stage, recombination activating gene 1 and 2 (RAG-1 and RAG-
2) genes are transcribed again, resulting in the rearrangement of the α-chain of the TCR. 
Eventually, the co-receptor proteins CD4 and CD8 are expressed, inducing the differentiation 
into double-positive (DP) thymocytes and migration to the cortico-medullary junction. At this 
stage, positive selection will occur where the TCR of these DP thymocytes are tested for their 
capability to recognize the major histocompatibility complex I or II (MHC-I/II) on the thymic 
stromal cell. The DP thymocytes will undergo apoptosis if the TCR:MHC interaction is too 
strong or too weak, whereas if the binding is moderate, those DP cells will undergo lineage 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
commitment where they mature into single positive (SP) CD8+ (recognising MHC class I) or 
CD4+ (recognising MHC class II) thymocytes. After positive selection has selected the DP 
thymocytes according to the strength and ability to bind the MHC I or II molecule, negative 
selection removes potential autoreactive T cells that may exist (Germain, 2002; Naito et al., 
2011).  
 
The thymic stromal cells in the medulla express transcription factors, and autoimmune 
regulator (AIRE), allowing to drive the expression of multiple peripheral antigens, such as 
insulin derived ones. This allows SP thymocytes to be exposed to a more complete set of self-
antigens than those present in the cortex, and therefore more efficiently deletes those T cells 
that are autoreactive. The single positive thymocytes that exhibit strong binding to a self-
peptide:MHC complex will either undergo apoptosis or differentiate into natural T-regulatory 
T cells (nTreg), whereas moderate and weak binders will fully differentiate into naïve T cells 
and migrate to peripheral lymphoid organs such as LNs (Germain, 2002; Naito et al., 2011). 
  
1.2.2 T cell receptor complex 
 
Most T cells in the human body are equipped with a TCR comprising an α- and β-chain on the 
cell surface connected by a disulfide bond. The α- and β -chains both consist of variable- (Vα 
and Vβ) and constant regions (Cα, Cβ). The variable region contains the antigen binding 
domain represented by β-loops referred to as complementary determining regions (CDRs). 
Each chain has three CDRs, CDR1α/CDR1β, CDR2α/CDR2β, and CDR3α/CDR3β, with a 
total of six CDRs that are responsible for binding to the pMHC-complex (Mariuzza et al., 2020; 
Wong et al., 2019). The CDR1α and CDR2α interact approximately at the amino terminal of 
the peptide:MHC complex the CDR1β and CDR2β bind at the carboxyl terminal of the 
peptide:MHC complex, and the CDR3α and CDR3β engage the central amino acids of the 
peptide:MHC complex. The TCR has a transmembrane domain in the constant regions 
containing hydrophobic amino acid residues that interact with the phospholipid tails in the cell 
membrane enabling anchoring of the receptor to the membrane. The TCR is not capable by 
itself of transducing the signaling pathways when antigen receptor engagement has occurred. 
The proteins supporting the TCR signaling pathway, include the CD3 complex (CD3γ, CD3δ, 
and CD3ε chains) and the zeta chain (CD247) (Mariuzza et al., 2020; Wong et al., 2019).  Two 
CD3 dimers interact with one TCR through electrostatic interactions between amino acid 
residues in the transmembrane domain of the proteins to form the final TCR complex (Figure

1). The supporting proteins in the TCR complex contain, on the cytoplasmatic side, 
immunoreceptor tyrosine-based activation motifs (ITAMs) containing two tyrosine residues 
for each ITAM that can be phosphorylated to facilitate the recruitment of intracellular 
signalling molecules needed to propagate TCR signalling (Hwang et al., 2020; Johansen et al., 
2021; Shah et al., 2021).

1.2.3 TCR rearrangement  
The TCR is capable of recognizing a vast set of different antigens ranging from pathogenic to 
self. The theoretical TCR diversity is estimated to generate 10
18 different TCRs in humans 
(Attaf et al., 2015), but in reality “the actual size ”of the CD8+ TCR repertoire in human adults 
is in the range of 10
5– 10
8 (Chen et al., 2017). The generation of such a large repertoire of 
TCRs can be achieved through a process referred to as “TCR rearrangement” occurring under 
T cell development (Attaf et al., 2015). The α- and β-chain of the TCR are encoded by different 
gene segments located on chromosome 14 and 7 respectively. At the genetic level, the α-chain 
is composed of three different gene segments, two for the variable region referred to as Vα 
(TRAV), Ja (TRAJ), and one for the constant region, Cα (TRAC). The β-chain in contrast is 
encoded by four, with three in the variable domain, Vβ (TRBV), Dβ (TRBD), and Jβ (TRBJ) 
and, one constant gene segment, Cβ (TRBC). In the germline DNA, there are approximately 
==PAGE:13==
 
12 
70-80 Vα-, 61 Jα-, 1 Cα-, 52 Vβ-, 2 Dβ-, 13 Jβ- and 2 Cβ gene segments that under T cell 
development get rearranged and tested for the correct structure and binding ability to create a 
functional TCR (Pylayeva-Gupta, 2009).  
The processes involving the rearrangement of the α-and β-chain are controlled and initiated by 
RAG1 and RAG2. These enzymes can, through the binding of recombination signal sequences 
(RSS’s) present on each VDJ-gene sequence, precisely cut the DNA between each coding 
region in the VDJ segments and the respective RSS’s. The RAG1:RAG2 complex binds two 
gene segments simultaneously following a 12/23 rule that implies that the segments destined 
to be spliced together need to have a 12 and 23 base pair RSS respectively for the recombination 
to occur. Once the recombination complex has brought the appropriate VDJ segment together, 
the DNA will be cut, and a hairpin will be formed. The hairpins are then opened by the action 
of the enzyme Artemis complex and random nucleotides are added by an additional enzyme, 
terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT) only expressed in immature lymphocytes (Hassan 
et al., 2018; Peralta-Zaragoza et al., 2004). The opening of the hairpins creates a palindromic 
nucleotide sequence (P-nucleotides) which combined with the addition of random nucleotides 
(N-nucleotides) in the joining regions (V-J and V-D-J) generates a hypervariable region that is 
grouped in the CDR3α and CDR3β of the TCR attributing further TCR diversity (Zarnitsyna 
et al., 2013). Finally, the elongation and ligation processes follow the conventional double 
stranded DNA repair mechanism referred as non-homology end joining (NHEJ) a non-template 
dependent DNA repair mechanism (Section 1.5.1 NHEJ). The result will be a rearranged DNA 
where a Vα and Jα segment is spliced together on chromosome 14 and a Vβ, Dβ, and Jβ are 
spliced on chromosome 7.

1.2.4 T cell activation  
 
Following T cell development, naïve T cells will circulate from the blood to secondary 
lymphoid organs (SLO’s) scouting for their specific peptide-MHC-complex (pMHC) on DCs. 
The migration and accumulation of naïve T cells into SLO’s involves chemokine gradients 
(CCL21:CCR7) and specific cell-adhesion molecules that attract and anchor the naïve T cells 
to HEV and into a T cells zone giving them the highest probability for antigen recognition 
(Krummel et al., 2016). Naïve T cells inside the T cell zone will start scanning the surfaces of 
DCs by transient interactions between cell-adhesion molecules. If the TCR of the naïve T cell 
recognizes its pMHC ligand, signalling through the TCR will induce a conformational change 
in the adhesion-molecules greatly increasing their affinity, resulting in a greater association 
between the antigen specific T cell and the DC (Krummel et al., 2016). The engagement of the 
TCR with its specific antigen and the interaction between the co-receptor, CD4 or CD8, and 
the MHC-molecule is essential for the initial activation of the naïve T cell (Signal 1), however 
this does not, on its own, stimulate the T cell to fully proliferate and differentiate into an 
effector cell (Hwang et al., 2020). Signalling through the CD28 receptor on the naïve T cell 
and the B7 receptor of the DC (Signal 2) is the second crucial interaction required to promote 
the survival and expansion of the T cells (Beyersdorf et al., 2015). Lastly, signalling through 
cytokines (Signal 3) is needed to differentiate T cells into the different subsets of effector T 
==PAGE:15==
 
14 
cells such as TH1 cells for CD4+ T cells and cytotoxic T cells for CD8+ cells (Greene & Pisano, 
2012). Once activated and differentiated into effector T cells, the need for co-stimulation 
(CD28:B7) ceases making them able to mount an immune response directly when recognizing 
its specific antigen.

1.2.5 Effector CD8+ T cells  
 
The migration of effector T cells from the SLO’s and into the effector site is highly regulated 
by the induction and expression of site-specific chemokine receptors such as CCR10 which 
ligand, CCL27:CCL28 is expressed on epithelial cells (Karnezis et al., 2019). This attraction 
mechanism ensures the correct accumulation of effector cells to for instance the skin. In a type 
1 immune response (intracellular pathogens) or malignancy, cytotoxic T cells will represent 
one of the most important cell types due to their direct cell-mediated killing mechanism 
(Greene & Pisano, 2012). The recognition of the specific pMHC complex by the TCR, induces 
the release of pre-formed granules containing perforins and granzymes (degrading enzymes) 
as well as proinflammatory cytokines (IFN-gamma, TNF) that damage the cell membrane of 
the targeted cell and recruits more immune cells to the area respectively leading to apoptosis 
of the targeted cell (Kabanova et al., 2018).

1.2.6 CD8+ T cell phenotype 
 
The phenotypical landscape of cytotoxic T cells is an ongoing topic, with new findings and 
subpopulations being described. Understanding the phenotypical characteristics of these cells 
provides valuable insights into their adaptation to the environment and awareness of their 
effectiveness in carrying out their effector role. 
 
In general, CD8+ T cells can be divided into naïve/naïve-like and effector cells through the 
presence of two extracellular receptors, CD45RA and CCR7. These two receptors are well 
known markers which show an initial overview over the phenotype of the T cells in question. 
CCR7 is expressed by naïve T cells as a migration mechanism attracting them to the T cell 
zone of the SLO where the ligand, CCL19:CCL21 is present and produced by the underlying 
DCs. CD45RA comprises one of the two main isoforms of the tyrosine phosphatases membrane 
family CD45 (CD45RA and CD45RO). CD45RA isoform is present on naïve T cells and is 
crucial for the signalling transduction following TCR ligation/activation. From these two 
markers, four district CD8+ T cells can be extrapolated consisting of naïve/naïve-like (TN, 
CD45RA+, CCR7+) central memory (TCM, CD45RA-, CCR7+), effector memory (TEM, 
CD45RA-, CCR7-) and terminally differentiated effector memory (TEMRA, CD4RA+, CCR7-)  
(Larbi & Fulop, 2014). Additional markers such as CD27, CD28, CD95, and CD127 (IL-7Rα) 
have been identified to further divide CD8+ T cell subsets into memory- and short-lived 
effector T cells. The preferred phenotype for T cell therapies is the stem-like memory T cell 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
(TSCM) due to their capacity for self-renewal, capability to generate various T cell subsets, 
and heightened engrafting ability. The phenotype of TSCM and TN share the markers, 
CD45RA, CCR7, CD27, CD28, and CD127 with the addition of CD95, a receptor expressed 
on antigen experienced memory T cell (Gattinoni et al., 2017; Lu et al., 2023; Tantalo et al., 
2021). Exhaustion markers such as  PD-1, TIM-3, LAG-3, CD57 and partially CD39 (Canale 
et al., 2018; Timperi & Barnaba, 2021) can further describe the functionality of T cells, being 
some markers, such as PD-1 in particular, being highly targeted in therapies against cancer.

1.3 Cancer immunotherapy  
 
Cancer is the second leading cause of death in the world and is projected to become the first 
leading cause in the near future (Heron, 2021; ReFaey et al., 2021). Various cancer 
immunotherapies have in recent years been approved by regulatory agencies such as the FDA 
in the USA and the EMA in Europe promising a more specific and efficient way to treat this 
highly malignant and heterogeneous disease.  The five main cancer immunotherapies currently 
approved are immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI), adaptive cell therapy (ACT), monoclonal 
antibodies, vaccines, and immune modulators (national cancer institute, n.d.). This master 
thesis focuses on the development of a type of ACT by using CRISPR editing to generate 
cancer specific T cells as described in the following sections.  
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
 
1.3.1 Adaptive cell therapy  
 
ACT relies on the cellular infusion of mainly tumor infiltrated lymphocytes (TIL’s) or gene 
modified lymphocytes where most therapies utilize cytotoxic T cells to kill the cancer cells in-
vivo. In recent years, the number of clinical trials surrounding ACTs has increased with the 
main focus being chimeric antigen receptor T cells (CAR-T cell) and TCR engineered T cells  
(TCR T cell) (Albinger et al., 2021). TIL based therapies utilize the patient’s own (autologous) 
cancer reactive T cells isolated from cancer biopsies, expanded in-vitro, and infused back into 
the patient (Kazemi et al., 2022). This strategy relies on the presence of TIL’s and the functional 
phenotype of such. CAR-T cells are T cells expressing a Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) 
introduced commonly through lentivirus transduction that is not pMHC restricted but can be 
designed to recognize free or membrane bound proteins, carbohydrates, or lipids. The CAR 
comprises of single-chain variable fragment with an intracellular domain connecting the 
antigen binding domain to a single, double, or triple signaling domain depending on the type 
of CAR, first-, second-, or third generation respectively (C. Zhang et al., 2017). In contrast to 
CAR-T cells, TCR-T cells are produced to express a novel TCR that as the endogenous receptor 
interacts with the pMHC complex presented by the patient’s cells.  The novel TCR could be 
introduced like CARs through lentiviruses but in this master thesis a non-viral approach has 
been investigated by using the CRISPR cas-9 based knock-in (KI) to perform precise gene 
editing.  
 
1.3.2 T cell expansion in-vitro 
ACT relies on many cells per infusion product, ≈ 109 cells (Brown et al., 2021), therefore there 
is a need for an expansion protocol that ensures clonal expansion with an optimal phenotype 
and killing ability. Various protocols have been established to expand T cell in-vitro, of which 
some exploit the ability of DCs to prime and activate T cells by isolating the patient’s own DCs 
and using them to expand T cell cultures in-vitro. Other protocols have been developed utilizing 
artificial antigen presentation cell lines e.g., K562 that have been engineered to activate T cells 
and act as artificial antigen presenting cell (aAPC) (Woo, 2017). Beads and plate-bound 
antibodies (αCD3 and αCD28) are a non-cellular methodology to activate T cells, but their sole 
usage could result in a suboptimal T cell phenotype.

A novel approach utilizing biotinylated pMHC, IL-2, and IL-21 in a complex with streptavidin 
conjugated dextran backbone that will be referred as “scaffolds” and have shown to expand, in 
an antigen specific manner, T cells in heterogeneous cell cultures. Various scaffold designs 
have been investigated by Post Doc “Siri Amanda Tvingholm” at the XTI research group of 
Professor Sine Reker Hadrup at DTU where the stoichiometry and the selection of the given 
molecules have been tested to achieve an optimal cell expansion and phenotype both in cancer- 
and virus specific CD8+ T cells (Tvingsholm et al., 2023). 
1.4 Gene editing  
 
Genome editing is a term describing the process of altering the DNA of a given organism. 
Some of the most known editing tools include zinc-finger nucleases (ZNF), transcription 
activator-like effector nucleases (TALEN), and Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short 
Palindromic Repeats-CRISPR associated protein 9 (CRISPR/Cas-9) (Porteus, 2019). ZNF and 
TALEN utilize a specific DNA binding domain to directly bind to the DNA and possess 
endonuclease activity towards a conserved DNA spacer sequence. In contrast, CRISPR-Cas9 
relies on single stranded guide RNA (sgRNA) to guide the complex to the desired sequence. 
This key distinction allows CRISPR-Cas9 to offer a more streamlined, high-throughput, and 
cost-effective approach compared to the need to redesign and resynthesize new ZNF and 
TALEN for each new DNA binding target (Gupta & Musunuru, 2014).

protein complexes. The first breakthrough in the field of nucleases was the Zinc-finger nuclease 
where protein engineering of the nuclease’s targeting site was performed to enable them to 
recognize endogenous genes which was the limitation of earlier discoveries. The current state 
in the art gene tool includes the use of an RNA and protein (Cas) for a more high throughput 
approach. (Porteus, 2019).

1.4.1 CRISPR-Cas9 
 
The CRISPR-Cas9 system is a natural defence mechanism utilized by bacteria as an adaptive 
immune response against bacteriophage infections and initially discovered in “Streptococcus 
pyogenes”. The defence mechanism comprises two primary components: CRISPR arrays and 
the Cas-9 protein. Within the CRISPR arrays, there are repetitive DNA sequences interspersed 
with unique DNA segments called spacers. These spacers originate from viral, or plasmid DNA 
encountered by the organism in the past. When the organism gets infected by a previously 
encountered virus, the CRISPR-Cas system is activated. It transcribes the CRISPR array into 
a pre CRISPR RNA (Pre-crRNA). This pre-crRNA is subsequently cleaved by Ribonuclease 
III (RNase III) to multiple crRNA that combined with Cas-9 and trans-activating crRNA 
(tracrRNA) form a complex capable of recognizing and binding to complementary DNA 
sequences (Le Rhun et al., 2019). The Cas9 protein acts as molecular scissors guided by the 
crRNA, which directs it to the target DNA sequence based on complementary sequences with 
the viral DNA. The target DNA segment must also contain a Protospacer Adjacent Motif 
(PAM), a short DNA sequence necessary for Cas9 recognition and binding (Le Rhun et al., 
2019). 
 
Once Cas9, guided by the crRNA recognizes the PAM sequence (NGG), it induces a double-
strand break (DSB) in the DNA approximately 3 base pairs upstream from the PAM sequence 
(Wu et al., 2014). This DSB effectively disables the viral DNA and prevents viral replication.

1.5 DNA repair mechanisms  
 
The human body is equipped with two mechanisms to repair DNA DSBs caused by errors in 
DNA replication or by ionizable radiations from the sun or medical device (CT-scans etc.) 
exposure (Wendy & Pederson, 2017). The two mechanisms comprise a non-template derived 
repair mechanism, NHEJ, and a template-dependent, homology-directed repair (HDR).  
 
 
Figure 8: Upon DSB, two different repair mechanisms can be used by the cell, NHEJ, and 
HDR. Non-homologous end joining is a no template dependent repair system where the 
breakage is repaired with the possibility of Indels in the form of nucleotide deletion or addition 
which could be futile or detrimental for the cell. Homology directed repair is a less error prone 
repair mechanism and requires a template in contrast to NHEJ. Adapted from Biorender®. 
 
 
1.5.1 NHEJ  
 
The predominant DNA repair pathway for DSBs is NHEJ, an error prone mechanism where 
the DNA is essentially reattached with eventual nucleotide insertions or deletions (indels) that 
can give rise to gene inactivation if a stop codon is created prematurely.  
 
NHEJ is initiated by the interaction of the heterodimer Ku70/80 with the blunted ends of the 
DNA. Ku70/80 creates an anchoring point for the DNA-depended protein kinase (DNA-PKcs) 
that through phosphorylation of DNA damage response proteins facilitates the recruitment of 
Artemis, DNA ligase 4, X-ray repair cross-complementing protein 4 (XRCC4) and XRCC4-
like factor (XLF) together will repair the DSB with in some cases with some indels (Chang et 
al., 2017).

by Ku70/80 followed by DNA-PKcs and Artemis. DNA ligase 4 together with XRCC4 and 
XLF repairs the DSB with potential Indels. Adapted from Biorender®. 
 
 
1.5.2 HDR  
 
The second DNA repair mechanism when dealing with DSB is HDR a template dependent 
process with a much lower error rate compared with NHEJ. In humans among other organisms, 
HDR is facilitated by the presence of a pair of homologous chromosomes that allow this 
mechanism to exist and function since the repair mechanism either can use the sister chromatid 
or homologous chromosome depending on the widespread damage. Since this mechanism 
relies on the chromosomal formation to function, the cells need to be in their dividing state, 
between the S- and G2 phase of the cell cycle (Ishizu et al., 2017).  
 
The first step following the DSB in HDR is the interaction with the MRN complex, consisting 
of three proteins MRE-11-RAD50-NBS. The MRN complex creates a 15-20 base pair (bp) 
nick on the blunted 5´end of the DNA, where further resection of the DNA is performed by 
additional exonucleases (SGS1-DNA2 and EXO1). Following the resection of the double 
stranded DNA (dsDNA), ATM kinase, CtIP, and RAD50 flank the blunted ends. ATM 
phosphorylates Histone variant H2AX, becoming γH2AX resulting in a less packed DNA 
around the histones and thereby enabling the recruitment of further proteins such as BRCA1, 
53BP1, and MDC1. The single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) is then coated by RPA acting as a 
docking site for DNA damage checkpoint kinases and eventually exchanged with BRCA1 and 
==PAGE:23==
 
22 
RAD51. RAD51 is crucial for the search of the template needed to perform HDR. Upon the 
finding of an homologous template, a D-loop formation can occur followed by elongation and 
ligation of the DNA (Liu et al., 2019).  
 
 
Figure 10: Overview of the cascade of events in HDR upon DSB. The opened DNA is

and the ssDNA coated by RPA and replaced by RAD51 which scouts for a homologous DNA

1.6 Template delivery  
 
When designing a gene therapy strategy, a critical factor to consider is whether to use viral or 
non-viral delivery systems. Viral-based templates have demonstrated remarkable delivery 
efficacy compared to non-viral methods, mainly due to the inherent properties of viral particles. 
However, non-viral approaches offer the advantage of having less stringent Good 
Manufacturing Practice (GMP) regulations (Lukjanov et al., 2021). This can be particularly 
beneficial for smaller companies aiming to enter the market quickly and to keep manufacturing 
costs down (Ramamoorth & Narvekar, 2015; Sainz‐ramos et al., 2021). 
 
 
 
==PAGE:24==
 
23 
1.6.1 Viral delivery systems  
 
The three primary classes of viral delivery systems are lentiviral (LV), adenoviral (AdV), and 
adeno-associated virus (AAV) vectors. Among these, AAV is a ssDNA virus with a capacity 
of 4.7 kilobases (Kb) and is widely utilized in combination with the CRISPR system. 
Recombinant AAV (rAAV) offers several advantages, including low immunogenicity and 
minimal site-specific genome integration, particularly in the AAVS1 gene. In contrast, 
conventional LV allows for a higher packaging capacity of 8 kb ssRNA, which is eventually 
converted into a DNA template used for non-specific gene integration, potentially leading to 
tumorigenesis. AdV, on the other hand, has the highest packaging capacity of 36 Kb dsDNA, 
allowing for a remarkable cargo size compared to the viral vectors. However, AdV also exhibits 
a higher immunogenicity profile (Ahmadi et al., 2023; Shakirova et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2019). 
 
1.6.2 Non-viral delivery systems  
 
The alternative to viral vectors for the delivery of naked genomic DNA relies on the use of 
physical- or chemical transduction. The main physical transfection methods used are needle 
based, laser-mediated, particle delivery, or through electroporation. The needle-based approach 
relies on the perforation of the cell membrane and subsequent injection of the DNA template 
inside the cell of interest. Laser-mediated transfection and electroporation (section 1.6.3 
electroporation) utilize laser waves and electrical current respectively to transiently 
permeabilize cells to deliver the intended cargo (DNA/proteins).  The last main physical 
transduction method utilizes gold particles coated with the DNA template that can be delivered 
to cells using high-velocity gene guns (Ramamoorth & Narvekar, 2015; Sheikh et al., 2017).  
Chemical transduction is the principle where polymer- or lipid-based nanoparticles are used to 
deliver DNA or RNA by stabilizing their negative charges and ensuring the cellular engulfment 
through phagocytosis or receptor-mediated endocytosis if targeting a specific cell type  
(Ramamoorth & Narvekar, 2015; Sheikh et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2023).  
 
1.6.3 Electroporation 
 
Electroporation is the most used non-viral delivery system and has been used for the first time 
by Wong and Neumann in fibroblast in 1982 and later generalized to all cell types two years 
later (H. Potter et al., 1984; Huntington Potter & Heller, 2018; Wong, Eberhard. Neumann, 
1982). Electroporation involves the application of a brief and powerful electrical current to the 
cells, creating pores in their membrane. This process facilitates the entry of molecules, 
==PAGE:25==
 
24 
including nucleic acids and proteins. The advantage of electroporation as a non-viral delivery 
method is its versatility of usage across different cell types, including plants and mammalian 
cells. It offers the flexibility to scale up or down the process by increasing or decreasing the 
number of cells being treated and the machine used. Additionally, electroporation allows for 
more precise control over the various parameters such as pulse intensity and duration, enabling 
optimization based on specific cell types. Moreover, electroporation is cost-effective when 
compared to viral delivery systems (Z. Zhang et al., 2018). 
 
1.6.4 Template types  
 
 
Various HDR template types can be used in combination with electroporation ranging from 
plasmid DNA to ssDNA. The size of the template is correlated with a high or low expected KI 
efficacy, ranging from high KI efficacy with smaller templates and lower KI efficiency with 
bigger constructs. Besides, obtaining a lower KI efficacy with larger DNA fragments, toxicity 
plays a big role as well, the intracellular machinery of mammalian cells is equipped with DNA 
sensors that can detect exogenous DNA, and the bigger the fragments the more toxic for the 
cells. Plasmid DNAs are widely used for KI experiments but have shown a lower gene editing 
capacity when compared to dsDNA and ssDNA. Moreover, the preparation of plasmid DNA 
requires various rounds of cloning steps which adds time to the desired experimental workflow 
(Denes et al., 2021; Mabuchi et al., 2023; Shakirova et al., 2023). 
 
Regarding ssDNA and dsDNA, ssDNA has shown to be efficient for smaller sequence changes 
whereas dsDNA is a better candidate for longer templates that in this project is 2.2 Kb (figure 
12) comprising of the full length TCR β-chain (VDJ) and the VJ fragment of the TCR α-chain. 
dsDNA are simpler and require fewer steps to be produced compared to ssDNA that relies on 
additional processes that add complexity and time to the editing pipeline (Denes et al., 2021; 
Mabuchi et al., 2023).

3.6 CRISPR/Cas-9 template design.  
The homology directed repair template (HDRT) utilized in this master thesis encodes the TCR 
specific for the peptide New York esophageal squamous cell carcinoma 1 (NY-ESO-1) a well-
known testis antigen with the given sequence: SLLMWITQC. The HDRT was designed 
through inspiration from Alexander Marson’s laboratory (Roth et al., 2019) with some minor 
changes by the Post Doc. Kristoffer Haurum Johansen. The 2.2 Kb HDRT (Figure 12) 
comprises of two homology arms (HAs) a left HA (LHA) and a right HA (RHA) both being 
300 bp for optimal homology between the HDRT and the endogenous TRAC locus. The HDRT 
further encodes for self-cleavable peptides, T2A and P2A that serve to separate the complete 
β-chain (Full length) and α-chain resulting in individual chain expression. The HDRT contains 
only the VJ segment and a small part of the first exon of the constant region of the α-chain 
since the rest of the constant region of the NY-ESO-1 TCR utilized the endogenous TCR this 
==PAGE:31==
 
30 
results in a smaller HDRT which correlates to a potentially higher KI efficacy. This TCR 
mediated gene editing strategy aims to integrate HDRT within the TRAC locus, disrupting the 
endogenous α-chain and enabling the expression of the NY-ESO-1 TCR with the same 
expression level as an endogenous TCR. Furthermore, this strategy limits potential T cell 
exhaustion that otherwise could arise with a non-physiological expression level of TCR.

3.12 General gating strategy  
For this project, two flow cytometers were employed for different purposes. The BD FACS 
Canto and BD LSR Fortessa, both manufactured by Becton Dickinson & Company ®, were 
utilized. The BD FACS Canto was used specifically for analysing the efficiency of KI, while 
the BD LSR Fortessa was employed for conducting the phenotypical analysis.  
To assess the NY-ESO-1 TCR-T cells, specific gates were utilized. These gates were employed 
to select the appropriate cell population for analysis while excluding unwanted components 
such as debris or other surviving cell types from the cell culture. Initially, a lymphocyte gate 
was created based on the Forward Scatter (FCS) and Side Scatter (SCC) parameters. The 
lymphocyte gate served to exclude non-lymphocyte cells and debris. From the lymphocyte 
population, our focus was on individual lymphocytes that have passed through the flow cell 
(single cells). These single lymphocytes were identified by their FSC-Area (FSC-A) and FSC-
Height (FSC-H) values. 
 
Further refinement was performed using a live-dead marker Near-Infrared (NIR) to gate the 
live single lymphocytes. This gating was based on the Side Scatter Area (SSC-A) and the NIR 
signal. Finally, the CD3 marker and APC signal were utilized to identify cells that are both 
positive for CD3 (CD3+) and positive for APC (APC+). This selection was made because the 
translocation of CD3 to the cell membrane is co-regulated with the presence of the TCR.

lymphocyte gate, lymphocyte singlets are further selected using a diagonal correlation between 
FSC-A and FSC-H. C) The live single lymphocyte population was then determined by gating

The amplified and purified HDRT samples were tested to establish the optimal concentration 
required to achieve the desired KI efficacy while maintaining minimal cellular toxicity. 
Furthermore, the objective was to identify the least amount of concentration necessary to attain 
effective gene editing, thereby avoiding unnecessary expenditure on reagents and time 
associated with HDRT production.

containing ≈ 10 mio cells each were used to perform the HDRT titration with the respective

4.3 NY-ESO-1 specific T cell expansion  
 
A significant challenge in using a cell product for therapeutic purposes involves the 
requirement to produce an ample quantity of specific cells appropriate for the intended 
therapeutic application. As a result, it is crucial to establish an effective expansion protocol that 
can elevate the initial small population resulting from CRISPR gene editing to a higher 
prevalence within the eventual infusion product. Three different expansion protocols have been 
tested utilizing the scaffold technology, conventional cytokines for broad T cell expansion, and 
the individual soluble constituents of the scaffolds referred to as soluble antigen in the 
following sections and as described in section 1.3.2 T cell expansion in-vitro.

4.5 killing assay 
Following the phenotypic analysis, a killing assay has been performed to test the cytotoxicity 
of the CRISPR edited NY-ESO-1 specific T cells. The experiment setup in section 3.13 
Incucyte killing assay describes the methodology of this killing assay utilizing the same cell 
cultures of section 4.3 NY-ESO-1 specific T cell expansion and section 4.4 Phenotypic 
analysis from three BCs. The expanded T cell cultures have been titrated in relation to the NY-
ESO-1 pulsed GFP expressing T2-cells that have been held constant (30.000 cells), testing the 
correct relationship between effector- to target cell (Effector: Target) ratio needed to promote 
specific T cell cytotoxicity. The killing assay ran for 48h with a scheduled 3h GFP snapshot 
intervals for the entirety of the experiment. Furthermore, two controls consisting of target cells 
alone and a negative control have been tested to assess a baseline growth rate of the T2 cells 
without effector cells and when in solution with non-NY-ESO-1 specific cells (NC) to compare 
with the soluble antigen-, cytokine- and scaffold expanded cells.

The results from the killing assay, presented in Figure 21, illustrate the different effector:target 
conditions along with their respective effector cells and killing abilities. The scaffold expanded 
cells demonstrate the most effective killing in all four titrations, as shown in figure 21A, B, C, 
and D followed closely by the cytokine control, and lastly, the soluble antigen-stimulated 
cultures which exhibited the lowest killing rates. Regarding the preferred effector:target (E:T)

expanded cells, resulting in approximately 75% T2 cell killing. Moreover, the T2 cells and the

differences between these conditions, which emphasized the specific way the T2 cells have 
been killed by the expanded cells. Additionally, even at the lowest E:T ratio, the scaffold-
expanded cells showed a higher killing ability compared to the other conditions, highlighting 
the statistical significance between all samples. These findings underscore the potency of the 
scaffold-expanded cells in efficiently eliminating the target cells in various E:T conditions. 
5. Discussion 
 
This project has focused on the use of non-viral gene editing for the KI of a specific TCR 
targeting the cancer testis antigen, NY-ESO-1. This approach was based on the CRISPR-Cas9 
system with the utilization of a donor dsDNA as HDRT. The rationale behind choosing 
CRISPR/Cas9 over lentiviral transduction lies in the behaviour of lentiviruses, which integrate 
the gene of interest in a non-specific manner. The uncontrolled gene integration lacks control 
of where or in how many loci the gene has been introduced and which genes will be disrupted 
without further investigation, e.g., genome sequencing and alignment with reference genome, 
to verify the exact loci of the new gene integration. Furthermore, the strategy chosen enabled 
the disruption of the endogenous TCR and introduction of the NY-ESO-1 TCR in the TRAC 
locus even though more optimization from this parameter will be needed in the future. For 
instance, the KO of the endogenous TCR β-chain will eliminate the possibility of TCR 
mispairing that has been linked to autoreactivity and graft versus host disease (Bendle et al., 
2010; Schober et al., 2020). Introducing the NY-ESO-1 specific TCR under the same promoter 
as the endogenous TCR instead of under a constitutive promoter ensures the same expression 
pattern and receptor turnover upon antigen recognition as the endogenous TCR. This has been 
showed to limit T cell exhaustion and increase the therapeutic potency of the engineered T cells 
==PAGE:51==
 
50 
in vivo in mice (Albers et al., 2019; Eyquem et al., 2017).  Moreover, this approach extends 
beyond TCR engineering, as similar outcomes have been demonstrated with CARs. CRISPR 
editing of the TRAC locus exhibited superior performance as compared to lentiviral 
transduction, resulting in consistent CAR expression among human peripheral blood T cells. 
Furthermore, this editing strategy boosted T cell effectiveness, whereas the edited cells 
significantly surpassed conventionally generated CAR T cells in terms of efficacy in a mouse 
model of acute lymphoblastic leukaemia ( Eyquem et al., 2017).  
 
5.1 HDRT production  
 
The production of the dsDNA gave a rapid and easy way to generate large amounts of HDRT 
needed for the KI experiments. The template production pipeline enabled the storage of the 
HDRT at -20 °C for long-term storage due to the high stability of dsDNA compared to e.g., 
ssDNA (Mabuchi et al., 2023). Other template types, ssDNA, and close circular DNA (ccDNA) 
were explored and tested before the work of this master's thesis. These alternative templates 
exhibited higher KI efficacy compared to dsDNA. However, they were not pursued further due 
to the intricate and costly downstream processing associated with them (Denes et al., 2021), 
alongside the effectiveness of the scaffold technology in expanding low-frequency populations 
to significant sizes (section 4.3 NY-ESO-1 specific T cell expansion). Consequently, in the 
template selection process, the actual properties and manufacturing considerations of the 
template hold more significance in achieving a higher KI percentage before cell expansion, 
particularly when utilizing a specific expansion technology. Using ssDNA comes with the 
drawback of requiring additional processing after PCR amplification, while ccDNAs 
complexity makes it reliant on external companies with high pricing and potentially slow 
delivery times. This situation results in increased time and budget requirements, respectively. 
 
5.2 HDRT titration 
 
To have a strong statistical significance, a minimum to three biological or technical replicates 
depending on the research question are needed. The experiment in section 4.2.1 HDRT 
titration covered two biological replicates, attributed to the third BC being contaminated and 
therefore discarded. Although the statistical power of this experiment was reduced, having two 
biological replicates can still provide some insights and preliminary information. The standard 
deviation (SD) between samples was low (Figure 15) both when addressing the KI efficacy 
and the number of specific cells generated with the various template concentrations indicating 
==PAGE:52==
 
51 
a correlation that is replicable among a bigger sample size. Furthermore, the results of this 
experiment indicate that even at the highest tested template concentration of 80 nM, there was 
no noteworthy rise in cellular toxicity observed, given the HDRT size of 2.2 kb. This 
achievement stands as a notable outcome in comparison to earlier research findings showing a 
steep decline in viability with increasing HDRT concentration. (Shyet al., 2017; Roth et al., 
2019)  
 
5.3 NY-ESO-1 specific T cell expansion  
This part of the project showed the efficacy of the scaffold technology in comparison to the 
soluble-antigen and cytokine controls in generating higher numbers of NY-ESO-1 specific T 
cells following CRISPR editing. The cancer specific population that after electroporation was 
under 10% was able to be expanded up to ≈ 98 % in the scaffold expanded cells and with a 
higher number of cells in contrast to the other expansion methods used. The ability to 
generating predominantly a cell population in a heterogeneous cell culture is crucial to limit 
off-target effects that could arise from the presence of non-specific T cells (Britten et al., 2023).  
5.4 Comparison of in vitro expansions 
 
The expansion period following electroporation was 10 days, with three feeding rounds (Figure 
11) enabling the increase in cancer specific T cells. Under the 10 days expansion, different 
proliferation patterns were observed between conditions. The soluble antigen cultures followed 
the scaffold - and cytokine expanded cells on day 7 (Figure 18) following the first feeding (day 
4) in both cell number and percentages of NY-ESO-1 specific cells (figure 18B, C, and D). On 
the following days, the degree of cancer specific expansion varied between samples with the 
scaffolds, which performed best by generating the highest number and percentage of NY-ESO-
1 specific T cells. A plausible explanation for the initial similarity in behaviour between the 
soluble antigen conditions and the scaffold-expanded cultures on day 7, followed by a 
subsequent notable divergence, could be attributed to the possible presence and subsequent 
decline of APCs within the culture. While the predominant composition of the cell cultures is 
expected to be T cells due to T cell enrichment, it is feasible that a small number of APCs may 
have persisted and contributed to supporting the NY-ESO-1 T cells. To investigate this 
hypothesis, it would have been valuable to include markers for dendritic cells (DCs), 
macrophages (MØ), and B cells in the flow panel used to monitor the growth pattern of NY-
==PAGE:53==
 
52 
ESO-1 specific T cells. Such approaches could provide a control to explore the presence and 
dynamics of these potential APCs and their potential impact on the observed outcomes. 
 
Another observation within the expansion involved the presence of two NY-ESO-1 specific 
populations in the cytokine and soluble-antigen stimulated culture for the 10-day expansion 
period, whereas in the scaffold expansion, they were only present after the initial stimulation 
(day 7) as depicted in Figure 18A. The existence of multiple positive populations may be 
attributed to the endogenous β-chain of the TCR still being expressed, potentially leading to 
the mispairing of the NY-ESO-1 α-chain with the endogenous β-chain. This hypothesis could 
be tested by knocking out the endogenous β-chain, thereby eliminating the possibility of 
mispairing. The disappearance of the lower APC+ population after day 7 (shown in figure 18) 
in the scaffold expanded culture, could be likely due to the enhanced TCR cross-linkage 
between the scaffold and the given specific T cell. This allows the formation of an 
immunological synapse between the specific T cell and the scaffold, leading to the expansion 
of only the population with the highest avidity, i.e., the T cells with the least mispairing. 
 
5.5 Phenotypic analysis   
The samples analysed for the phenotypic analysis were processed without including non-
expanded cells from the same donor. Consequently, the absence of a baseline expression level 
for various markers hinders the assessment of potential phenotypic changes attributed to the 
expansion protocols. Despite this limitation, the phenotypic data still provides valuable insights 
into the cell phenotype. The primary focus of the analysis is not to observe a phenotype change 
from baseline but rather to compare the phenotypes between different expansion techniques. 
Moreover, the cells investigated have been the NY-ESO-1 specific T cells and not bulk CD8+ 
cells to focus the analysis on the gene-edited cells.  
The cells were stained with a CD95 antibody to depict the CD95 expressing cells. This marker 
is expressed on antigen experience T cells and is used to distinguish between true naïve-, 
memory- and effector T cells  (Tantalo et al., 2021). Unfortunately, the CD95 expression level 
has not been used to draw any conclusion because the all cells were highly CD95+ with no 
slight CD95- population across all samples thereby indicating that some staining problem 
might have occurred. 
==PAGE:54==
 
53 
The overall results suggested that the cytokine expansion protocol generated the least of naïve-
like memory phenotype since most cells were CD27-, CD28-, and CD127- even with the 
presence of CCR7+ and CD45+ cells. T cells deficient in CD27 and CD28 have been showed 
to have shorter telomeres and eventually linked to senescence (Lu et al., 2023; Plunkett et al., 
2007). The cells expanded with cytokines displaying elevated levels of CD57 and the highest 
TIM-3 expression compared to the other samples. This, coupled with the presence of a low 
frequency of naïve-like and memory cells, indicates a less favourable T cell subset profile from 
a phenotypic perspective. The cells stimulated with the soluble antigen produced a slightly 
greater proportion of phenotypically functional cells in comparison to the cytokine control. 
However, due to the presence of established exhaustion markers, CD57, TIM-3, and CD39 
(Figure 20E) and a constrained capacity for expansion (Figure 18B, C, and D), the existing 
soluble antigen stimulation protocol looks like not optimal in its present state. The scaffold 
expanded cells achieved a more favourable phenotype with higher CD127, CD28, and CD27 
co-expression in contrast to the soluble-antigen- and cytokine stimulated cells. As stated in 
section 4.4 Phenotypic analysis most of the NY-ESO-1 specific T cells would be characterized 
as TEMRA cells since that population lacks CCR7 expression. Potentially the apparent loss of 
CCR7 expression could be transitory and could be challenged by letting the cells rest in low 
concentration of cytokine e.g., IL-15 for a couple of days following day 14 and observing 
whether CCR7 is recirculated or not. Furthermore, the combination of co-stimulatory 
molecules, CD27 and CD28, along with the memory marker CD127, indicates that those T cell 
subpopulations have been associated with sustained presence in patients who underwent ACT 
and received an infusion product with this phenotype, as observed in previous studies 
(Rosenberg et al., 2012; Zhou et al., 2005). Moreover, the reduction in CD127 expression on 
primed T cells has previously been linked to chronic stimulation in cases of acute HBV 
infection, and with T cell exhaustion (Lv et al., 2010). Additionally, the scaffold expanded cells 
expresses high level of TIM-3 and CD39 which is suboptimal. The exhaustion markers and T 
cell subset markers were stained in separate panels so no insight into whether the CD127+ 
population expresses TIM-3 or CD39 can be drawn from this analysis.  
The next phase in the characterization of the expanded cells, should include the analysis of 
well-known effector molecules, and transcription factors e.g., interferons, granzymes, TOX 
and Ki67 (Blessin et al., 2021)(Theos et al., 2005) that could either be done in a flow based 
analysis to keep the cost down or through a single cell analysis.

5.6 Killing assay 
 
The results from the killing assay, demonstrated the successful generation of functionally 
CRISPR-edited T cells. This represents a significant finding in the context of producing cancer-
specific cytotoxic T cells. Among the different expansion protocols, the scaffold-based 
approach proved to be the most effective in generating T cells capable of efficiently killing the 
target cells, even at the lowest E:T ratio. This success can be attributed to the scaffold-expanded 
T cells having a more favourable phenotype. On the other hand, the soluble-antigen and 
cytokine expansion protocol resulted in a lower number of cancer-specific T cells, especially 
evident at the lower E:T ratios. These conditions showed very few NY-ESO-1 specific T cells 
compared to the scaffold-expanded cells, as suggested by the killing curve (Figure 21) for the 
soluble-antigen and cytokine expansion, which follows the growth pattern of the negative 
control (NC) and T2 cells alone. 
 
The Incucyte-based killing assay utilized the GFP signal from the T2 cells as the sole 
parameter, allowing for the tracking of T2 cells growth rate and, consequently, assessing the 
killing efficacy of the effector cells. However, this approach lacks information on the T cell 
proliferation or decline. To gain insights into T cell proliferation or cellular death over time, 
the assay could have incorporated a cellular trace dye to label the effector cells, enabling an 
overview of their dynamics. During the killing assay, approximately 75% of T2 cells were 
killed in the 2:1 and 1:1 effector-to-target ratios over the 48-hour incubation period. To further 
explore the killing efficacy, the next step could involve extending the incubation time to 
observe potential incremental killing or any signs of T2 cell regrowth. In addition, a rechallenge 
experiment could be conducted. This involves the introduction of an additional dose of T2 cells 
after the initial 48-hour incubation in each condition for another 48 hours. This setup can 
provide insights into a potential second round of T cell killing or cancer growth, indicating the 
generation of either functional but short-lived effector T cells or T cells with a memory 
phenotype. In the case of the latter, re-exposure to their specific antigen would trigger their 
expansion and subsequent elimination of the target cells. Moreover, a potential implementation 
in the killing assay to test the influence of the high TIM-3 expression found in the expanded 
cells could be through the use of a cell line expressing one of the four known TIM-3 ligands, 
galactin-9, phosphatidylserine, high-mobility group protein B1 and CEACAM-1 (Acharya et 
al., 2020), thereby observing the potential decrease or same killing ability compared to controls 
without TIM-3 ligands. 
==PAGE:56==
 
55 
Once incorporated these additional steps the killing assay could provide a more comprehensive 
understanding of T cell behaviour, proliferation, and long-term efficacy. This will facilitate a 
deeper insight into the dynamics of effector T cells in response to cancer-specific targets, 
contributing significantly to the overall effectiveness of the immune response against cancer 
cells. 
In conclusion, the scaffold-based expansion protocol from this preliminary killing assay 
demonstrated a superior potential in generating cancer-specific T cells capable of efficiently 
targeting and killing the designated tumour cells, making it a promising approach for cancer 
immunotherapy. 
6. Future Perspective  
In summary, this master thesis has shown a preliminary overview of the use of the scaffold 
technology to successfully expand TCR-T cells in an antigen-specific manner and the effect on 
the phenotype and killing ability that it gives. The results showed the ability of this technology 
to expand a small population, following CRISPR KI editing of the TRAC locus, into a 
remarkable size being cancer specific and with a higher number of T cells having the desired 
phenotype and killing ability. 
The scaffold technology has found extensive application within the XTI research group, 
particularly under the guidance of Post Doc Siri Amanda Tvingholm. In her work, she 
successfully expanded virus-specific T cells sourced from BCs alongside TILs obtained from 
tumour biopsies (Tvingsholm et al., 2023). The outcomes of her research mirror the phenotype 
observed in this project, which primarily involves the generation of CD27+, CD28+, and 
CD127+ T cells (Tvingsholm et al., 2023).  However, there are challenges associated with 
expanding TILs or immune responses. One major issue is the limited population size, which 
might hinder significant expansion or the possibility that the cells are already experiencing 
chronic antigen stimulation. Such chronic stimulation could potentially lead to T cell 
exhaustion upon strong in-vitro stimulation using the scaffold technology. In contrast, the 
approach employed in this master’s thesis addresses this issue differently. The processes are 
scalable, allowing for the utilization of larger cell populations sourced from healthy donors, 
which can be expanded, as shown here, to yield sufficient cells. Moreover, the strategy involves 
heterogeneous T cells isolated from BCs rather than pre-primed and potentially chronically 
exhausted specific T cells. This distinction contributes to a more favourable scenario, reducing 
==PAGE:57==
 
56 
the likelihood of T cell exhaustion and enhancing the potential success of the expansion 
process.  
Even though the results shown here seem promising, the path to the clinic is still far into the 
future. In this thesis, I have only induced the expression of one TCR and therefore the effect 
seen could differ with different TCRs and should be further investigated. Moreover, the 
selection of the given TCR can be challenging since there is the potential for cross recognition 
between the intended antigen and a self-antigen. The most famous study with engineered T 
cells associated with in-vivo toxicity surrounded the targeting of the cancer testis antigen 
MAGE A3 (EVDPIGHLY) with a high affinity engineered TCR. The preclinical phase showed 
promising results but did not translate into the clinic where the infusion product caused multiple 
deaths attributed to cardiotoxicity (Cai et al., 2020; Cameron, 2013; Linette et al., 2013; Tcr et 
al., 2014). The death of the participant in the clinical phase, where caused by the cross 
recognition by the  MAGE A3 specific T cells of the muscle protein, Titin (ESDPIVAQY) only 
expressed in beating cardiac tissue (Cai et al., 2020; Cameron, 2013; Linette et al., 2013; Tcr 
et al., 2014). A potential method that is utilized by PACT Pharma, Inc. and Neogene 
therapeutics, Inc. now acquired by AstraZeneca, Inc. to circumvent the potential creation of 
auto reactive T cells is attributed to sequencing the tumour biopsies identifying potential cancer 
antigens, screening for TCR sequences in the tumour microenvironment that recognize the 
given epitope and create autologous CRISPR edited T cells expressing the neoantigen-specific 
TCR (neoTCR) as depicted in Figure 22 (Foy et al., 2023). This strategy ensures that the “TCR 
hit” has undergone positive and negative selection in the thymus and therefore should decrease 
the chance of it being cross reactive. The results from the phase I clinical trial (NCT03970382) 
of PACT Pharma Inc involving 16 patients with different refractory solid cancers showed that 
5 patients experienced no further disease progression whereas the remaining 11 patients 
showed disease progression when infused with up to three distinct neoTCR transgenic cell 
products (Foy et al., 2023). The phase I clinical trial (NCT03970382) has been suspended 
according to PACT Pharma Inc. because of a “business decision”, nevertheless the study 
demonstrated the feasibility of isolating and cloning multiple TCRs, simultaneous knockout of 
the endogenous TCR and knock-in of neoTCRs using, non-viral precise genome-editing and 
achieving the manufacturing of clinical grade neoTCR engineered T cells (Foy et al., 2023).

biopsies to infusion products. The procedure starts by identifying patient-specific tumour 
mutations through sequencing data and bioinformatics predictions of mutated neoantigen 
peptides. From the blood, native CD8 T cells that bind these neoantigen targets are isolated 
using barcoded and fluorescently labelled peptide–HLA multimers. The neoTCR sequences

7. Conclusion   
The overall aim of this project was to generate CRISPR edited cancer specific CD8+ T cells, 
assessing their functional and phenotypical landscape by comparing the scaffold technology to 
soluble-antigen and cytokines. I have successfully edited non-cancer specific T cells derived 
from healthy individuals to recognize the testis antigen NY-ESO-1 through non-viral gene 
editing and expanding the initial small population ≈ 4-9 to culture ≈ 98 %. Furthermore, it has 
been clear that the scaffold technology was superior in generating a larger number of cancer 
specific T cells from a rather small cell population compared with the soluble-antigen and 
cytokine approach. The phenotype of the scaffold expanded culture generated demonstrated a 
higher proportion of memory phenotype and with a greater killing ability even with a very low 
effector to target ratio compared to the previously mentioned in-vitro expansion methods.  
Further development in the expansion protocol will be needed to increase the validity of this 
technology. Some of the future steps comprise, knocking in other known cancer specific TCRs 
beside the NY-ESO-1 TCR to test if the same results will be observed. A mixture of two 
different KI populations could be set up to try to understand the expansion dynamics when 
utilizing the scaffolds where the TCRs either are restricted to the same HLA or not. This 
mixture could serve to potentially observe a normalized distribution or prevalence of one 
response either peptide- or HLA affinity driven. Moreover, additional phenotypic investigation 
might be needed either flow based or through single cell analysis to fully understand the effect 
of the scaffolds on the transcriptomic landscape of the T cells. Killing assays including multiple 
peptide pulsed T2 cells could be used to observe potentially non-specific killing events before 
proceeding to animal models where a tumor is introduced and the in-vivo killing dynamic could 
be analyzed including parameters such as the degree of tumor penetration and killing ability.
