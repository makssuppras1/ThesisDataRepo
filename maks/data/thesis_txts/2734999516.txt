Abstract
Early detection of cardiovascular diseases can reduce the mortality and morbidity rate.
A main underlying cause of cardiovascular diseases is atherosclerosis, which affects the
hemodynamic flow patterns. Pressure gradients has been used for detecting and assessing
the severity of stenosis. Current methods for assessing pressure gradients have different
drawbacks. The current gold standard is invasive catheter measurements, limiting the pos-
sibility of routine diagnostic assessment, due to the related risk. Non-invasive techniques
have been investigated, based on the Bernoulli and Navier-Stokes equations. However,
most of these methods oversimplify the complexity of the hemodynamic flow patterns.
One of these methods is a 2D streamline method, in which viscosity and out-of-plane
velocities are neglected.
This project therefore investigates if a 3D work-energy relative pressure (WERP)
method for estimating pressure gradients is more precise and accurate than the stream-
line approach. The WERP method is based on the Navier-Stokes equations, and includes
both an advective energy term, a kinetic term and a viscous term contributing to the
total pressure gradient. The method is tested on simulated data (CFD and FIELD II
simulations) of a straight vessel with a 70% constriction, and a measured dataset from a
phantom model of a carotid bifurcation.
The results of applying the WERP method to simulated data showed high precision
(CV = 3.33-6.87%), and accuracy (R2 = 0.907 −0.966) when compared to the actual
pressure gradients. However, the results showed a general underestimation of the pressure
gradients with the WERP method. Results on one phantom dataset shows lower precision
and accuracy of the WERP method, compared to results of the streamline method on
two phantom datasets. The viscous contribution that is neglected from the streamline
method, shows potential significance on simulated data. Based on the limited amount of
data, it can not be concluded if the WERP method is more precise and accurate than the
streamline method.

1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the background for this thesis. It describes the reasoning for
assessing pressure gradients, and introduces the current methods for doing so. At the end
of the chapter, the hypothesis of the project is presented.
1.1
Background and motivation
Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) are the most common cause of death, with 690 882
deaths in the US in 2020 [1]. A main underlying cause of CVDs is atherosclerosis [2].
Atherosclerosis develops over a period of around 50 years, with several risk factors increas-
ing the possibility of occurrence [2,3]. These risk factors include smoking, hypertension,
diabetes mellitus, being overweight and genetic predisposition. Early diagnosis of CVDs
can reduce the mortality and morbidity rate [4]. Therefore, methods for early detection are
important. Hemodynamic flow patterns can be analysed to detect abnormalities related
to possible CVDs [4]. Intravascular pressure gradients can give an indication of a stenosis
and its severity, or other CVDs [5]. Therefore, the evaluation of pressure gradients could
be a tool for early detection of atherosclerosis and CVDs.
Current methods for assessing pressure gradients include both invasive and non-invasive
methods [6–8]. The gold standard for measuring pressure gradients is invasive catheter-
ization, using fluoroscopy for guidance [6].
Due to the invasiveness of this method, it
includes risks like blood stream infections, risk of inducing stroke, and hematomas [6,9].
Another drawback of the catheter measurements is, that the blood flow is disturbed by
the insertion of a catheter into the vessel. This means that the measured pressure will not
fully reflect the actual pressure in the vessel. Due to the invasiveness of the procedure, it
is not suitable for frequent repetitions, or routine diagnostic pressure assessment [6,10].
These drawbacks has given a basis for investigating different non-invasive techniques for
pressure gradient estimation [11–13]. These methods include Medical Magnetic Resonance
(MRI) and ultrasound imaging techniques. Some of these techniques use variations of the
Bernoulli or Navier-Stokes equations, to estimate pressure gradients based on estimated
velocities in the vessel [6]. The simplified Bernoulli equation has been widely used [6,10].
However, the application is based on several assumptions, oversimplifying the complex
hemodynamics in the human body.
Common to the use of both the Bernoulli and the Navier-Stokes equations, for estimat-
ing pressure gradients, is that they rely on measurements of flow velocities [6]. Both MRI
and ultrasound can be used for obtaining these velocities non-invasively. Even though
MRI is accurate in estimating flow velocity and volume flow, it has limitations for clinical
use [6]. Performing an MRI can be both expensive and time consuming. Furthermore,
some patients are not suitable for MRI scans, due to magnetic implants or claustropho-
bia. Ultrasound has also been used in various ways to obtain velocity estimates in blood
vessels [14–17]. Doppler ultrasound has been used for long, to provide velocities, and flow
volume data [17]. However, it comes with different limitations. One limitation is the fact
that it is angle dependent, and therefore also limited when the imaged flow is complex.
The Doppler imaging requires a flow angle ≤60◦between beam and flow, which can be
diﬀicult to achieve since many blood vessels are parallel to the surface of the skin [15].
A method for estimating pressure gradients based on ultrasound data has been pre-
sented in Haslund et al. [18]. The method uses the unsteady Bernoulli equation for a

streamline approach. This method is based on 2D Vector Flow Imaging (VFI), and ne-
cessitates laminar in-plane flow. Based on the streamline approach, pressure gradients
were estimated with an R-squared value of 0.985. The method has limitations, such as
neglecting viscosity, and only capturing in-plane velocities. Therefore, it is restricted to
straight vessels where the transducer can be placed parallel to the blood vessel.
Donati et al. [10] presents a method for non-invasive assessment of pressure differences,
using 4D PC-MRI. The pressure gradients are estimated based on the Work Energy Rela-
tive Pressure (WERP) method. The 3D WERP method is derived from the Navier-Stokes
equations, and includes more aspects of the hemodynamics than the streamline-method
based on the Unsteady Bernoulli equation, like viscous contributions and velocities in 3
directions. However, as described, MRI scans are more expensive and time consuming
than ultrasound.
Based on the limitations of the current methods, this project looks into the possibility
of estimating pressure gradients based on 3D ultrasound imaging, and the WERP method
presented by Donati et al. [10,19].
1.2
Objective/hypothesis
The hypothesis investigated in the project is, that the 3D Work-Energy Relative Pres-
sure (WERP) method is more precise and accurate than the 2D streamline method.

Theoretical background
The following chapter describes different theoretical knowledge, that will be needed
to understand the methods used in the project. It describes the anatomy of the carotid
arteries and the patophysiology behind atherosclerotic plaque. Furthermore, the blood flow
in human vessels is described, in order to understand the changes that occur when a
vessel is partially obstructed. This includes descriptions of the Bernoulli principle and the
Navier-Stokes equations. The WERP method, derived from the Navier-Stokes equations is
then presented. Lastly, knowledge on ultrasound, including Row-Column Arrrays (RCAs)
and Tensor Velocity Imaging (TVI), is included, to explain the methods behind acquiring
the 3D velocity data needed for the WERP pressure gradient estimations.
2.1
Anatomy and physiology of the carotid arteries
The circulatory system is responsible for the exchange of gasses in the human body
[20, 21]. Blood carries oxygen and nutrition to the organs, and disposes waste products.
The flow is initiated by the pumping of the heart, leading the blood through the aorta,
which branches out in different arteries. These arteries branch into smaller arterioles, and
then into capillaries, where the exchange takes place. The blood is carried back to the
heart through venulae, that piece together into veins. In the pulmonary circulation, the
blood passes through the lungs, where it is re-oxygenated, and carbon dioxide is released.

The head and the neck receives it’s blood supply through the common carotid arteries
and the vertebral arteries [21, 22]. At the top of the neck, each common carotid artery
bifurcate (branches) into the internal carotid artery and the external carotid artery, as
shown on Fig. 2.1. A common site for the formation of atherosclerotic plaque is where

walls, can calcify and become fibrotic, leading to atherosclerosis. Atherosclerosis affects
the blood flow in various ways, since it narrows the vessel lumen, and also influences the
vascular compliance (the elasticity of the vessel wall).
2.2
Blood flow in the human circulatory system
Blood flow in humans is complex, with many factors influencing the flow [20]. This
includes for example the length and cross-sectional area of the vessels and the viscosity
of the blood. In the arteries, the pressure varies in a cycle, following the contractions of
the heart [21]. When branching on to the smaller vessel like arterioles and capillaries, the
cyclic variation lowers, and in the capillaries the pressure is at a steady level.

2.4
Ultrasound
Ultrasound has been used in medical applications since the early 1950s [20].
It is
based on mechanical vibrations above the audible range (20 kHz), being emitted from
an ultrasound transducer. The transducer is made with piezoelectric crystals, converting
electrical energy into the mechanical vibrations. The waves propagate through the tissue,
where scattering, reflection and absorption occurs, when the waves meet tissue boundaries.
This creates echoes, that return to the ultrasound transducer. In the transducer, the echoes
are converted back to electrical energy, that is used for creating the ultrasound images

Interleaved synthetic aperture imaging sequences
An interleaved SAI sequence works by repeating each emission twice, as illustrated on

the emission sequences needed for RCA imaging are generally longer than others typically
used for velocity estimation [30].
2.4.2
Row-column arrays and Tensor Velocity Imaging
Row-column arrays (RCAs) are a type of 2D arrays, that enables 3D imaging [29–31].
They offer the possibility of obtaining a similar image quality with an array of N + N
channels as with a N2 channel array.

Effectively, RCAs function as two 1D arrays, one with rows and one with columns,
where each elements is elongated, as illustrated on Fig. 2.6. Thereby, they address an
entire row or column using a single channel. When performing volumetric imaging with
RCAs, one of the 1D arrays functions as transmit aperture, while the other functions as
receiving aperture.
RCAs can be used for Vector Flow Imaging (VFI). 3D VFI can be obtained through
different techniques, including a plane-by-plane technique or SAI, where the SAI technique
shows better volumetric coverage [29]. 3D VFI in 3D space and time can be referred to
as Tensor Velocity Imaging (TVI) [29]. Combining RCAs with SAI sequences makes it
possible to do TVI at high frame rates, with a reduction of channels compared to other
volumetric imaging methods [32,33].

Data processing
Velocity data was obtained from the simulations and the phantom setup. Furthermore,
the CFD simulation data included a pressure field, with pressure values for each position
in the vessel. The format of the CFD simulation and the FIELD II simulation was not the
same. Therefore the CFD simulated data was mapped to the coordinates of the FIELD

fields of velocities in the x, y and z-direction of 20x20x20 samples (vy, vy, vz) for 691
time frames, corresponding to 3.5 seconds of data. Moreover, the data included position
matrices (X, Y andZ) with the x, y and z-positions of the velocity vectors. The velocity
fields were mapped through the arrow3fast-function, presented in appendix A.10. The
CFD data also included a matrix with the actual boundaries of the vessel from the CFD
simulation. Using the function isosurface in MATLAB, these boundaries were visualized on
the velocity fields. This revealed that velocity values occurred outside the vessel boundary
for the FIELD II simulation.
Therefore, two different types of masks were applied to
the simulated data, in order to test their influence on the accuracy and precision of the
results. The first mask was an ideal mask, based on the matrix with the actual boundaries

of the vessel from the CFD simulation, The second mask was an angle-discriminator mask,

3.2.1
Angle-discriminator
For the phantom data of the bifurcation, a mask was applied before the data was
provided to this project, filtering out velocity data outside the vessel lumen. The same
type of mask was applied to the FIELD II simulated data. This was done by calculating
the average change in angle over time, for each velocity vector in the dataset. The angle-
discriminator mask was based on an assumption that the velocities within the vessel lumen,
would not have a significant change in angle from frame to frame, while noise coming from
surrounding tissue could change angles more randomly between frames. For each velocity
vector, the angle between each frame was calculated as:
cos−1(θ) = |⃗a ·⃗b|
|⃗a||⃗b|
,
(3.1)
where ⃗a ·⃗b is the dot product of the two vectors ⃗a and ⃗b. These vectors are vectors at
the same position, at two consecutive time points (⃗b = ⃗a(t+1)). The mean change in angle
for each velocity vector position was calculated, and used for creating the mask. For each
position with an angle above a given threshold, the value of the mask was set to zero. For
the remaining positions, the mask consisted of ones. When multiplying this mask with the
original data, the velocity vectors in the positions with angles above the threshold value
would be zeros, while the velocities in the positions with lower changes in angle would
keep their original values. The chosen threshold angle was based on visual inspection of
the velocity field plots, with the vessel surface included in the plot. The choice of angle
had to exclude as many of the vectors outside of the vessel, without removing valuable
information from within the vessel. For the phantom data, the mask was already applied,
with a threshold angle of 13◦[30]. On the simulated data, an angle of 2◦was used as
threshold, since higher angles removed to much of the velocity vectors.

5
Discussion
5.1
Simulation results
When looking at the velocity fields of the simulations, it is evident that the FIELD II
simulated velocities is not limited to be within the boundaries of the vessel. Applying the
ideal mask removes all velocities outside the vessel boundary, but the estimated velocities
within the vessel differ from the actual CFD velocities. The angle mask does not remove
all velocities outside the boundary of the vessel, but it removes the largest ones, that would
influence the results the most. When looking at the average flow rates from the different
simulations, the FIELD II simulation shows higher flow rates than the CFD simulation,
indicating that the velocities in the FIELD II simulation are either overestimated, or that
more velocity vectors are included in the flow rate estimate. Applying the ideal and the
angle discriminator mask to the FIELD II velocity data brings the average flow rates closer
to the flow rates based on the CFD velocities. However, this does not necessarily mean
that the distribution of the velocities is correct. If the FIELD II estimated velocities does
not follow the same flow pattern as the CFD simulation, this would influence the different
contributing elements in the total pressure gradient, like the viscous contribution. Since
the viscous contribution includes spatial derivatives, it would be very different for a flow
where the velocities were similar across the cross-section of the vessel, than when they
follow a parabolic profile with larger velocities in the center of the vessel. Another thing
to consider regarding flow-rates is that the estimation of the WERP gradients includes
division by the flow-rate. An issue therefore arises at time points where the flow-rate is
close to zero. If the flow-rate approaches zero, the pressure gradients will approach infinity,
leading to possible over-estimations of the pressure gradients at very low flow rates.
Applying the WERP method to the velocities from CFD simulations resulted in pres-
sure gradients approximately following the same course as the actual CFD pressure gra-
dients. The pressure gradient ranges are similar when based on CFD velocities, FIELD II
velocities and masked FIELD II velocities (ideal mask). However, the FIELD II gradients
did not follow the patterns of the actual pressure gradients as much as the CFD velocity
gradients. The angle masked FIELD II gradients varied in a range closer to the actual
CFD gradients, but that does not necessarily mean that this is more precise and accurate
than the other gradients.
Precision
Evaluation of the precision of the WERP method is based on the coeﬀicient of variation.
The lowest precision based on the simulation data is found for the FIELD II simulation
without masks applied (CV = 6.87%). The higher variation between cycles indicate that
the velocities estimated through the FIELD II simulation are not following the exact same
pattern as the CFD simulation they are based on. However, the range of the averaged
pressure gradients was similar to the ones based on the CFD velocities, as presented in
table 4.1. Applying the ideal mask improved the CV to 4.1%, by removing velocities from
outside of the vessel.

Accuracy
The results of applying the WERP method to the simulated data (both CFD and
FIELD II simulations) generally shows good correlation with the actual CFD pressure
values (R2 = 0.908 −0.966 for the FIELD II velocities (unmasked and with ideal mask)
and R2 = 0.997 for the CFD velocities). This is also evident when looking at the plots of
the pressure gradients, were the courses of the WERP estimated pressure gradients follows
the course of the actual CFD pressure gradients. The results of the linear regression on the
pressure gradients based on CFD velocities result in values that lie close to the regression
line, but does not exactly follow the actual CFD pressure. This indicates that the WERP
method results in an accurate course of the pressure gradients as a function of time, but
there is an underestimation of around 24% for the WERP gradients compared to the
actual CFD gradients. The reason for this could be inaccurate velocity estimates.
5.2
Phantom results
When looking at the results on the phantom data, it is evident that the precision of
the pressure gradients is lower than for the simulated data (CV = 14.25% vs.
CV =
1.16-6.87%). Since the WERP method shows higher precision on the simulated data, this
is most likely due to the quality of the estimated velocity data.
The contributions from the viscous term on the phantom data appears to be higher
than in the simulations. The phantom is a model of a bifurcated carotid artery, meaning
that the vessel splits into to smaller vessels between the two cross-sections used for the
pressure gradient estimation. From theory we now that the viscous contribution is related
to the diameter of the vessels. Therefore, the two smaller vessels would each contribute
more to viscous losses than the one larger vessel.
The accuracy of the phantom results was not evaluated, since there was no ground
truth to compare to. To evaluate this, a catheter measurement of the gradients would be
feasible.
5.3
WERP vs. streamline
In terms of precision, the 3D WERP method results in CV values of 3.44 to 6.87%
for the FIELD II simulated data. For the measured phantom data, the CV value was
14.25%, which was higher than for the streamline approach on different phantoms (3.3-
5.44%) presented in Haslund et al. [18]. These phantoms were however a common carotid
phantom, and a phantom of a stenoic vessel, so the results are not directly comparable. The
accuracy of the streamline method was presented by Haslund et al. as a R-squared value
of 0.985 when comparing the estimated gradients to the actual CFD pressure gradients.
For the WERP method, the R-squared value varies from 0.907-0.966 for the FIELD II
estimated velocities with and without masks applied. Based on this, the streamline method
appears to be more precise and accurate. However, when comparing the 3D WERP method
to the 2D streamline method, different things have to be considered in addition to the
accuracy and precision. One of the differences between the two methods is the dimensions
of the data, and the fact that the 2D streamline method uses one single streamline in the
middle of the vessel, where the 3D WERP method used full cross-sections of the vessel.
This means that the streamline method mostly rely on the velocities in the middle of the
vessel to be estimated correctly, while the full velocity field needs to be accurate for the
WERP method to estimate the true pressure gradients. Since the possibilities of obtaining
3D data is more limited than for 2D, and requires more complex techniques, the streamline
method could currently be more easily implemented in clinical practice.

Looking at the different contributions in the WERP equation, it shows some similarities
to the streamline method, as the advective term of the WERP method and the spatial
term of the streamline method are comparable. Furthermore, the kinetic term (WERP)
can be compared to the temporal term in the streamline method.
Therefore another
main difference between the two methods, besides the dimensions, is that the streamline
method neglects the viscosity. Therefore, the influence of the viscous term is of interest,
when deciding which method is more precise and accurate.
5.3.1
Viscous contribution
The influence of the viscous term was evaluated by plotting the pressure gradients with
and without the viscous term against each other. This was done for both the gradients
based on the CFD velocities, and the FIELD II velocities (without mask applied). For
the CFD velocities, a clear distinction of the two lines was evident in a segment of the
data where the corresponding flow-rates were at the highest levels during a flow cycle.
When looking at (2.7), this corresponds to the theory, if the viscous resistance is the
same between the to measuring points at all times. In that case Rf would be constant,
and increasing the flow-rate would also increase the viscous contribution to the pressure
gradient. However, this clear distinction is not visible on the results for the FIELD II
simulation, indicating that the viscous contribution does not necessarily contribute much
to the total pressure gradient when applied to data closer to actual measuring situations.
The MAEs on the gradients based on the CFD velocities are 5.29 Pa (without viscosity)
and 3.24 Pa (with viscosity). This difference, along with the clear distinction between
standard deviation areas, suggest that if the velocity estimates were as precise as the CFD
simulation, the velocity term would significantly influence the results.
The viscous term of the equation is calculated based on partial derivatives of the
velocities in space.
The viscous term will therefore have a higher influence if there is
larger variations in the velocities when moving around the vessel. Therefore, it would also
influence the resulting pressure gradients if the accuracy of the velocity estimates differed
between high and low velocity. If low velocities e.g. is generally overestimated, while
the high velocities are close to the actual values, it would especially affect the viscous
contribution, since the spatial derivatives would not be as high.
5.4
Clinical applications
As described in chapter 1, the current methods for pressure gradient estimation has
different drawbacks. The gold standard is invasive catheter measurements, which are not
applicable for routine diagnostic procedures. However, the importance of early diagnosis of
CVDs indicate a need for a non-invasive method, that is possible to use on more patients.
The ultrasound WERP method is non-invasive, which allows for more possibilities in
clinical settings. There is still need for improvement and testing of the method, to ensure
accurate and precise estimations of the pressure gradients, but it could potentially be
applied for screening purposes for people at higher risk of atherosclerosis.
5.5
Future work
For this project, the 3D WERP method was only tested on limited amounts of datasets.
The method needs to be tested on more data-sets, in order to conclude whether it is more
precise and accurate than the 2D streamline method, or other methods. It was based
on one CFD simulation, and the corresponding FIELD II simulation, and one measured
phantom dataset. More datasets would give a better basis for concluding the precision
and accuracy of the method. The simulations were based on only four cycles. More cycles

would make a greater basis for concluding on the precision of the method. Furthermore,
it would be interesting to test the method on in-vivo data, in order to make possible
adjustments for the WERP method, making it applicable to actual data from human
vessels. In addition to this, the viscous term showed different contribution between the
simulations and the measured phantom data. Therefore, it would be interesting to test
this on more datasets, and possibly try other approaches than the in-build diff function
for the calculations of the spatial derivatives.

Conclusion
Early detection of cardiovascular diseases can be an important measure in reducing
mortality and morbidity. A way of early detection is analysing flow patterns to catch
abnormalities, like pressure gradients occurring across stenoic sections in a vessel. The
current gold standard for pressure measurements is invasive catheterization, limiting the
possibility of routine pressure assessments. Current methods for non-invasive assessment
includes a 2D streamline approach, neglecting viscosity and out-of-plane velocities. The
purpose of this project was to investigate whether a 3D Work Energy Relative Pressure
(WERP) method was more precise and accurate than a 2D streamline approach. The
results of the implementation of the WERP method shows high precision when applied to
simulated data (CV = 3.44 to 6.87%). However, it is less precise when applied to a phan-
tom dataset (CV= 14.25%), compared to the streamline method (3.3-5.44%). In terms
of accuracy, the WERP method resulted in R-squared values lower than the streamline
method (0.907-0.966 for WERP based on FIELD II, compared to 0.985 for the streamline
method). Moreover,the results showed a general underestimation of the pressure gradi-
ents with the WERP method, when compared to the actual CFD pressure gradients. The
viscous contribution, that is neglected from the streamline method showed a possible sig-
nificance when applied to CFD velocity data, but not when applied to unmasked FIELD II
velocity data. However, the results are based on a limited amount of datasets. Therefore,
further investigation of the method, and possibly improvement of the 3D tensor velocity
data is needed, in order to conclude if the 3D WERP method is more precise and accurate
than the 2D streamline method.

Project plan
This appendix includes the initial project plan, and an updated project plan. Fur-
thermore, it includes reflections on the project process. The thesis was conducted at the
department of Health Technology at DTU, from 21st of August 2023 to the 21st of Febru-
ary 2024. The project accounts for 35 ECTS points. The supervisors of the project was
Jørgen Arendt Jensen, Lars Emil Haslund and Lasse Thurmann Jørgensen
B.1
Project description
From the project description: ”The project uses data from row-column ultrasound
transducers for estimating the tensor velocity for blood. From this the pressure gradients
are derived in the volume.”
This project is going to investigate the possibilities of developing a method for pressure
gradient estimation on 3D data obtained by a row-column array. This is done through
implementation of the Work Energy Relative Pressure gradient (WERP) method, which
will be implemented in MATLAB.
B.2
Objective
During this project period, it is going to be investigated whether a WERP method
can be developed for 3D data obtained through a setup with a heart phantom model and
measured with a row-column array. The results will be compared to an already existing
streamline method for pressure gradient estimation in 2D.
