ABSTRACT
This report studies the modeling of sound absorption and surface impedance properties. This was
conducted by impedance tube measurements, ISO 10534, theoretical impedance calculations, and
simulations from the general impedance model, RMK+1. The study was performed on a porous
absorber and resonant absorbers. Investigation of the impact of applying an additional porous
layer in a Helmholtz absorber was conducted, which led to a comparison of theoretical calculations
and practical measurements of one and two porous layers, respectively. Hence the study aims to
evaluate the comparability between surface impedances.
Modifications were made to the radiation impedance to increase the correlation of the RMK+1
mapped surface impedance to the measured. Moreover, the input Sabine absorption coefficients
to the impedance model, belonging to the resonant absorbers, were offset to minimize the uncer-
tainties about diffusivity and the fact that the absorbers were measured once by the reverberation
chamber method. The porous absorber studied was a 100 mm Ecophon Industry Modus S. The
RMK+1 mapped surface impedance indicates a constant resistance. The correlation coefficients
confirm a significant correlation at low frequencies towards the measured surface impedance for
both the resistance and the reactance. The theoretically calculated impedance, found by Miki’s
model, increased the resistance’s correlation. The measured Helmholtz absorbers with the perfo-
rated panel PH5 and PH10 from Gustafs, with one layer of porous material, indicate significant
oscillations at the surface impedance independently of the cavity depth. Generally, the RMK+1
mapped surface impedance overestimated the resistance at low frequencies with the applied con-
straints, whereas the theoretically calculated impedance underestimated the resistance. Although
the theoretical methods could not reproduce the oscillations shown at the measured impedance,
the correlation coefficients did indicate a significant correlation. The studied multilayer absorber
with the microperforated panel NANO and a 30 mm air cavity did show the most significant cor-
relation to the RMK+1 mapped resistance. Yet, this construction’s measured absorption differed
the most from the Sabine absorption coefficients. There were difficulties in identifying the actual
resistance level using the methods. The RMK+1 model was the best at determining the surface
impedance of the porous absorber compared to resonant absorbers. The results demonstrate a
proportionality between the perforation rate and the impact of the porous layer. Furthermore,
the measurements and theoretical calculations of the resonant absorbers with two porous layers
indicate similar absorption coefficients at high frequencies as with one layer. Thus the additional
porous layers mainly impact low-frequency absorption.

INTRODUCTION
Simulation of room acoustics is a fundamental tool when designing an architectural space. Nowa-
days, room acoustical software is based on geometrical acoustics, a high-frequency approximation
accommodating accurate results above the Schroeder frequency, and has relatively short compu-
tation times. The Schroeder frequency depends on the room volume and the reverberation time.
As a result, a low-frequency problem or a problem with small rooms arises due to the low modal
density. When performing simulations with geometrical acoustics, absorption and scattering co-
efficients represent the boundaries. However, these parameters do not have phase information,
which is essential for accurate predictions at low frequencies. Alternative methods to simulate
room acoustics are methods such as the finite element method (FEM), boundary element method
(BEM), or finite-difference time-domain (FDTD), which are wave-based methods that solve the
wave equation with numerical discretization and therefore contain phase information at the bound-
aries. Yet, these methods are much more time-consuming. A phased parameter is necessary to
make accurate boundary predictions, for instance, the surface impedance. An issue occurs in the
accessibility of this quantity, as the manufacturers of sound absorbers only provide data on the
absorption coefficients, which are measured in a reverberation chamber according to ISO 354, as
this is the industry standard. However, identifying the complex-valued surface impedance can
be conducted by ISO 10534, which is performed for this report, although the quantities for this
method are only for normal incidence. The surface impedance can be established from the statis-
tical absorption coefficient to compensate for the normal incidence. This relationship between the
surface impedance and the statistical absorption coefficient has been investigated by B. Mondet,
which led to six different general impedance models [2].
This report, therefore, studies the general impedance model RMK+1 and the process of mate-
rial fitting, where the outcome from the impedance model was compared to practical impedance
tube measurements and theoretical impedance calculations to understand the correlation and to
investigate if it is possible to reproduce the measured complex surface impedance with theoretical
methods. The study was performed on two types of absorbers: porous and resonant. The resonant
absorbers were constructed as Helmholtz and multilayer absorbers, consisting of three perforated

Types of absorbents
Absorptive materials are divided into two broad categories: porous absorbents and resonant devices
[8]. The different types of absorbers have the maximum absorption at a different frequency, which
is shown in figure 2, and absorbers performances are most commonly evaluated using absorption
coefficients.
Figure 2: Random incidence absorption coefficients of different absorbers.
Getting broadband absorption in one absorber involves taking advantage of significant resonance
and porous absorption, thus creating a multilayer absorber. For porous absorbers applies that, at
high frequencies, the absorption is significant, and the opposite at low frequencies, the absorption
is small, as seen in Figure 2.
While porous absorbers are high-frequency absorbers, resonant
absorbers have the highest performance at low frequencies. Therefore, the most efficient way of
gaining more low-frequency absorption is to exploit resonance, which can be done by placing a
perforated sheet in front of the porous absorbent, thereby a Helmholtz absorber is created [8].

3.1.1
Impedance tube method
The impedance tube method complies with ISO 10534-2 [11]. The method consists of placing a
specimen at the end of the tube while the other end was driven by a sound source that generates a
random signal. Ideally, the tube was completely rigid and airtight, thus not allowing any leakage.
The measurements were performed using the two-microphone test procedure. The two microphones
were placed in the tube. Therefore they do not perforate into the tube and change the inner volume
of the tube. The two microphones were 1/4 inch microphones of type MV301 from Microtech Gefell.
The sound pressures at the two microphone locations were measured with the software ’PULSE
Labshop’ (Version 18.1.1.9) from Brüel & Kjær, where the complex acoustic transfer functions of
the two microphones were determined. Afterward, the transfer function was used to calculate the
normal incidence complex reflection factor, the normal incidence absorption coefficient, and the
surface impedance of the test specimen, based on the equations (11)-(19) from reference [11].
According to the standard ISO 10534-2 [11] and the AcoustiTube Manual [12] the method has
an operating frequency range of the measurements, which were limited by the geometry of the
impedance tube. These frequency limits were defined as fl < f < fu, where fl was the lower
frequency limit of the tube, f was the operating frequency, and fu was the upper-frequency limit
of the tube. fl was limited by the accuracy of the signal processing equipment, and the fu was
determined to avoid non-plane wave mode propagation. The condition for fu in a circular tube
was found by:

3.3
General impedance model, RMK+1
Several impedance models have been produced with different levels of complexity to represent
different absorbents. Based on B. Mondet’s study, Retrieving complex surface impedances from
statistical absorption coefficients [2], six general impedance models were developed with three,
five, or seven different parameters, respectively.
It was these parameters that determined the
complexity, and the models were developed based on, among other things, fractional calculus. The
study showed that the models ’RMK+1’ and ’R+2’ were the most efficient, thus, models expressed
with five different parameters [2]. These impedance models generate random incidence absorption
coefficients and surface impedance from an input being Sabine absorption coefficients.
Throughout this study, the RMK+1 model was used as the retrieval method, which was expressed
as [5]
Zs(ω) = K · (jω)−1 + R + M · (jω)1 + G · (jω)γ,
(23)
where Zs was the surface impedance and R, M and K illustrate the classic damped oscildeter-
minedel [5], and represent the resistance, the mass, and the stiffness, respectively. Furthermore,
it applies that R, M, K, G ≥0, and the fractional exponent. Thus the lower and upper bound as
−1 ≤γ ≤1 [5].

The input absorption coefficients were a real-valued function, and from the impedance model, it was
transformed into a complex-valued surface impedance. This means that an optimization problem
occurs, as the transformation was ill-posed. The ill-posed transformation occurs as there were
infinite numbers of surface impedances that match a given absorption coefficient. The parameters in
Equation 23 were determined by solving a constrained optimization in MATLAB, which identified
a feasible solution for an extensive set of options concerning certain constraints. The optimized
function was, in this case, a cost function, F, which was to be minimized. The optimization was
performed to find the surface impedance, Zs, which minimizes the difference between the input
absorption coefficient, αin, and the mapped statistical absorption coefficient, αs. The cost function
can therefore be expressed as [5]
F(Zs) = ||αin −αs(Zs)||2,
(24)
where the statistical absorption coefficient was calculated by Equation 14. Equation 14 consists of
the surface impedance, and the radiation impedance, where the radiation impedance was calculated
based on equations (53)-(63) from reference [16].
This radiation impedance was based on S.I.
Thomasson’s radiation impedance [6]. The value of the surface impedance, Zs, was thereby found
based on a given constraint, the input absorption coefficients, the radiation impedance, and the
model’s limitations. The constraint applied to the model and the optimization depends on the
type of absorber that was investigated.
The RMK+1 model was performed in MATLAB, where the ocost function’s optimization occurs,
hence calculating the mapped surface impedance and the mapped random incidence absorption
coefficients. In the MATLAB script, the constrained optimization was accomplished by the inte-
grated MATLAB function ’fmincon’, a nonlinear optimization method. By doing the MATLAB
optimization, five different values were found, and these were solutions to the five parameters;
K, R, M, G and γ from Equation 23, thus the surface impedance was calculated. Furthermore, to
solve the optimization, a loop was performed if the cost function was above the validity condition,
with random starting points and running the optimization as a stability analysis, to ensure the
optimization is set correctly [5]. However, if the cost function was below the validity condition, the
loop was not performed, and the solution to the five parameters was released. If running the model
several times with the same input and constraint, the same outcome did appear. Nevertheless, the
RMK+1 model has some limitations, as the method relies on equations assuming local reaction
[5].
For each absorbent mapped with the RMK+1 model, the absorption coefficient and the surface
impedance were repeatedly compared towards the measured results and theoretical calculations to
evaluate the comparability between the results. Hence the fundamental idea behind the optimiza-
tion was to optimize the mapped surface impedance that produces the best match between the
generated and measured absorption coefficients.

to the model depending on the absorber type.

3.3.2
Offset input absorption coefficients
The input absorption coefficients, illustrated at subsection 3.2, were Sabine absorption coefficients,
which were statistical absorption coefficients based on reverberation time measurements according
to ISO 354. However, this method has two critical issues. First of all, the absorption coefficients
can sometimes exceed unity due to the finite size of the specimen and therefore be greater than
the random incidence absorption coefficients. Second, a poor reproducibility between chambers of
the Sabine absorption coefficients because the measurements were performed with the assumption
of a diffuse field, yet the chambers were non-diffuse in many different ways. The chambers were
different in dimensions, geometrical shape and in, shape, quantity, and type of diffusers, which
were factors that were frequency dependent and impacted the diffuseness.
These uncertainties mean the Sabine absorption coefficients will be inaccurate and should therefore
be converted into random absorption coefficients. This way, the absorption coefficients will never
exceed unity.
Several measurements in different chambers should be performed to ensure the
measured absorber was given proper absorption coefficients. However, for commercial absorber
products, only one chamber measurement was allowed, and the commercial method to measure
absorbers products was the standard ISO 354 - the reverberation chamber method.
The RMK+1 model generates random absorption coefficients from Sabine absorption coefficients.
Yet, the Sabine absorption coefficients might be incorrectly estimated due to the chamber method.
The measurements performed during this study illustrate a tendency to produce a lower surface
impedance than the RMK+1 mapped, meaning the mapped surface impedance was overestimated.
The overestimation in surface impedance was because of the input absorption coefficient. From sub-
section 2.3, it was clear that the absorption coefficient must be increased to reduce the impedance.
The input absorption coefficients were, therefore, offset to observe the impact, and a reproduction
of the input Sabine absorption coefficients was performed by the offset. The interpretation of the
offset must consequently be that by offsetting, the difference in diffuseness was considered, thereby
minimising the uncertainties that existed during the reverberation chamber measurements, and
that the absorbent only has been measured once.
The reproducibility of the random incidence absorption coefficients converted from the Sabine ab-
sorption coefficients was studied by C.H. Jeong and J.H. Chang, and according to C.H. Jeong and
J.H. Chang, the standard deviation of the reproduced random incidence absorption coefficients
was significantly decreased compared to the Sabine absorption coefficients [19]. A decrease in the
standard deviation means an increase in precision, hence an optimization conversion was illus-
trated by a frequency-independent method. The method was applying a frequency-independent
room factor for the test chamber, ∆αroom, to the absorption coefficients as an optimization as;
αsize&room(f) = αsize(f) + ∆αroom [19]. The frequency-independent room factor, ∆αroom, com-
pensates for the overestimation or underestimation which might occur from the Sabine absorption
coefficients. Therefore, such frequency-independent room factor was applied to the input Sabine
absorption coefficients in the RMK+1 model in MATLAB to offset the absorption coefficients and
decrease the surface impedance.
This test was conducted on all the investigated absorbers. The offset result at the perforated panel

3.4.2
Surface impedance of perforated panels
A perforated panel is a resonant absorber, meaning that the absorption occurs in low to mid
frequencies. There are two main resonant absorbers; a membrane and a Helmholtz absorber. The
mechanism behind a resonant absorber implies a mass vibrating against a spring. The idea of a
Helmholtz absorber is that the mass is an obstruction of air in the opening of a perforated plate,
and the spring is obtained by air enclosed in the cavity [8].
The resonance frequency of such
construction is where the maximum absorption occurs.
The panel system investigated throughout this study was a typical construction of a Helmholtz
absorber, and such an absorber was illustrated in Figure 9.
Figure 9: A typical construction of a Helmholtz absorber.
As described in subsection 3.2, the perforated panels were combined with a complete panel system
with a perforated panel, a porous layer, and an air cavity. The porous layer was added; thus,
damping was provided by damping to remove sound energy, and damping was required to achieve
losses. Yet, microperforated absorbers were excepted, as the damping was provided by viscous
losses within the small perforations [8]. The construction with a microperforated panel was, there-
fore, a multilayer absorber, as the microperforated panel exploits the resonance of a Helmholtz
absorber by its many tiny holes to aid absorption, along with a backing condition of a porous layer
to further increase the absorption.

5
DISCUSSION
As described in subsubsection 3.1.1, the measurements were performed at two impedance tubes
with different geometry. The materials investigated throughout this study consisted of a porous
absorber and three perforated panels with different backing conditions.
The materials for the
measurements were therefore carved to fit the tubes, which created various uncertainties. At first,
the perforated panels had a pattern that involved the carved material having a perforation at the
edge (as shown at Figure 4). This could not be avoided due to the pattern of the perforation, and
the perforation rate would change. Based on the panels used for the measurements, the actual
perforation rate was calculated to compare if it matched the rate from the manufacturer. The
perforation rate was calculated as; rate = S1/S2, where S1 was the total area of the perforation,
and S2 was the total area of the panel. For the large tube, the panel, Gustafs PH5, has a perforation
rate of 5.13%, which corresponds with the rate of 5% given by the manufacturer. However, the
issue arose for the panel used for the small tube as it has a perforation rate of 2.78%, which was a
much lower perforation rate than for the large tube. This means there will be a mismatch between
the low and high-frequency measurements, which impacts the surface impedance and absorption,
especially at high frequencies due to the smaller wavelength. The measures would therefore be
less accurate for high frequencies. The perforated panel, Gustafs PH10, for the large tube has a
perforation rate of 19.7%, which was a bit larger than the 18% given by the manufacturer, and
the match between the measured absorption and the Sabine absorption coefficients determined
by the manufacturer would thereby differ.
Secondly, due to the panels being segments of the
original material, few perforations would be placed at the edge, which induces an inaccuracy. This
could cause leakage as the panel was not completely airtight, and the incoming sound waves would
therefore reflect differently than the actual case, changing the surface impedance. A similar issue
occurs for the microperforated panel, Gustafs NANO, as the panel consists of a thin wooden plate
that has the microperforation, a core of fiber gypsum with air pockets, and a thin membrane at
the end. As the material was carved, the air pockets would change in size, which could lead to the
material being less airtight.
Furthermore, the porous materials, both the porous absorber Ecophon Industry Modus S and the
Rockwool Flexibatt 34, were soft and could easily be squeezed to fit the material in the impedance
tubes, based on the materials should be airtight. Yet, the fiber structure and characteristics would
change by squeezing the porous specimens. Additionally, measuring with two porous layers adds
another uncertainty, as the two porous layers needed to be placed close enough. Thus no air gap
between the layers would appear. Yet, it was impossible to know when the material was squeezed
towards each other or not airtight enough, making the measurements difficult. However, based
on the measurements with two porous layers, shown at subsection 4.5, indicate similar absorption
coefficients at high frequencies as with one porous layer. This means the results were assumed
to be conducted properly. Yet, the results with the perforated panel PH5 and two porous layers
differ from the panel with one porous layer. As the same carved panels were used to conduct
the measurements with one and two porous layers, another affair could have influenced that exact
measurement.

DISCUSSION
It was described in subsection 3.2 that the multilayer absorbers use the Rockwool material ’Flex-
ibatt 34’ as the porous layer. This material was chosen based on the knowledge from a report
describing that Gustafs perforated panel systems used stone wool with a density of 28 kg/m3 and
a thickness of 45 mm. Yet, when carving the material, the center of the product should be used
to ensure a similar density. Even so, it was difficult to know if the carved material had identical
characteristics as the one described in the report. Furthermore, as the Rockwool porous material
was carved, it became even more sensitive, and several pieces were therefore made to perform
all the measurements. This would impact the comparison of the measurements of the panel sys-
tem with one and two porous layers, respectively, as it was not the same carved material used
to perform the measurements. This means the two specimens of the same material might not be
identical, and the material characteristic would, therefore, neither be. This was important to have
in mind when evaluating the measurements. This measurement uncertainty may be the reason for
the significant variation in absorption between the Helmholtz absorber with the perforated panel
PH5 and one and two porous layers, respectively. Moreover, not knowing the same porous material
used for determining the Sabine absorption coefficients by the manufacturer Gustafs would always
be an uncertainty that highly impacts the comparability. Even though a specimen with the same
characteristics, based on the knowledge that has been acquired, has been used, without knowing
the exact material, the measurements could not have been carried out with different precision.
The determination of absorption coefficients from manufacturers for commercial absorber products
was obtained by the reverberation chamber method, ISO 354, as it was the commercial method,
as described in subsubsection 3.3.2.
However, this method often overestimates the absorption
coefficients and could exceed unity due to the finite size of the specimen.
Applying an offset
of the input absorption coefficients was thereby a way to take into account the uncertainties of
the reverberation chamber method, even though the offset did increase the coefficients without
knowing if the absorption coefficients already were overestimated. The coefficients could thereby
be overestimated even more. As described in Chapter 3.4 in Acoustical Absorbers and Diffusers
the absorption coefficients could vary as much as 0.4 depending on the laboratory the chamber
method was performed in [8]. The implementation of the offset should minimize the difference in
the surface impedance. Nevertheless, the offset was performed as it was seen how it improved the
surface impedance for the resonant absorbers. Yet, the offset did not improve the surface impedance
of the porous absorber, thus, the difference was dependent on the absorber type, although the main
uncertainties about the chamber method consist of factors relating to the chamber and not the
absorber.
Manufacturers do not provide absorption coefficients below 125 Hz or other material character-
istics. The RMK+1 model and this study did investigate down to the 63 Hz octave band, and
it was, therefore, necessary to assume the input values. Throughout the study, the assumption
for the 63 Hz absorption coefficient was based on the tendency observed from the measurements.
Based on the measurements, which were the only situation that provided knowledge below 125 Hz,
an approximated absorption coefficient was chosen. Thus the RMK+1 model and analysis at the
low frequencies could be performed. Yet, as the input absorption coefficient at 63 Hz was based
on assumptions, the comparability between the mapped and the measured should be evaluated

having that in mind. Due to the fact that the actual Sabine absorption coefficient was not known,
uncertainties arise for the correlation and the performance of the RMK+1 model. The numeri-

frequencies due to the computational time [5]. This leads to a greater necessity for commercial ab-

yet, only knowing the density of the porous material created an approximation of the material’s

theoretically calculated would be less consistent due to the lack of data information. As B. Mondet
explained, "Consequently, better input data to the numerical models will lead to better simulation
results" [5].
At subsection 3.3, it was explained that the RMK+1 model uses a cost function, F, to find the
mapped surface impedance based on the difference in absorption coefficients. With the constrained
optimization performed in MATLAB, it was crucial to identify the proper constraints to the model,
therefore, the type of absorber which was investigated, as the impedance outcome relies especially
on this information. However, a restriction occurred as the cost function associated with the opti-
mization was not guaranteed to have a unique minimum regardless of the applied constraints [5].
This means even if a stability analysis was performed, that there might be another cost function
that matches the impedance measurements. Yet, by testing the other possible impedances from the
MATLAB loop, a better match was not found, and running the model with the same constraints
gave the same outcome, as explained in subsection 3.3. A more considerable cost function could
retrieve a surface impedance with a more significant correlation towards the measured, as several
impedance values correspond to the same absorption coefficient as illustrated at subsection 2.3.
However, other outcomes were not appearing due to the constrained optimization, and the ab-
sorbers depended highly on the applied constraints. Based on the results, shown at section 4,
the solutions to the constrained optimization indicate difficulties in identifying the proper resis-
tance level, and the issue could be referred back to the applied constraints. The RMK+1 model,
therefore, requires very specific constraints, depending on the type of absorber, as it was seen the
resistance level highly depends on which type of constraint was applied. Several constraints were
tested to retrieve these results, yet it was unknown whether the correct constraints were found in
this study.

assess the nonlinear association. Yet, the coefficients were used to understand the trend in col-

CONCLUSION
6
CONCLUSION
This study has conducted measurements on different types of absorbers, resonant and porous
absorbers. The measurements were conducted with the impedance tube method, ISO 10534, to
compare the measured surface impedance with the mapped surface impedance from the general
impedance model, RMK+1, and theoretical impedance calculations. The resonant absorbers were
constructed as Helmholtz absorbers with two different types of perforated panels with different
perforation rates and multilayer absorbers with a microperforated panel. Furthermore, the resonant
absorbers were measured with one and two porous layers to investigate the impact. The RMK+1
model was modified, demonstrating an increase in the correlation towards the measurements.
The modifications consist of changing the radiation impedance. Thus the porous absorber uses a
complex conjugated radiation impedance, and the resonant absorbers use the absolute value of the
radiation impedance. Secondly, the input absorption values were offset for resonant absorbers due
to an increased correlation between the surface impedances.
The porous absorber studied was a 100 mm Ecophon Industry Modus S. The measurements in-
dicate similar absorption coefficients as determined by the manufacturer, high absorption at high
frequencies, which characterize porous absorbers. The RMK+1 mapped absorption coefficients
did show a strong relationship towards the corrected input, being the offset Sabine absorption
coefficients, by having a significant coefficient of determination. Yet, it was shown how adjusting
the absorption coefficients did increase the relation. However, as the adjustments were based on
the absorption coefficients, the adjusted mapped surface impedance did worsen the correlation to
the measured impedance. The RMK+1 mapped surface impedance generally indicates a constant
resistance. The theoretically calculated surface impedance based on Miki’s model increased the
resistance’s correlation by illustrating the inversely proportional tendency. The correlation coef-
ficients confirm a significant correlation at low frequencies towards the measured impedance for
both the resistance and the reactance.
The measured Helmholtz absorbers with the perforated panels PH5 and PH10, with one layer of
porous material, indicate significant oscillations at the surface impedance independently of the
cavity size. The measured normal incidence absorption coefficients illustrate coefficients similar to
the manufacturer. Yet, the maximum absorption for the resonant absorbers was expected to differ
from the manufacturer, as the measurements were performed with the impedance tube method,
and the manufacturer determined the absorption coefficients based on the reverberation chamber
method, which the comparison of coefficients illustrates. The measured absorption of the multilayer
absorber with the microperforated panel NANO indicates much lower absorption coefficients than
the manufacturer. However, several uncertainties arose as the perforated panels were carved to
fit the impedance tubes, and the accuracy of placing the porous layer into the tube impacted the
absorption.
Both methods demonstrate reactance with excellent correlation towards the measured at low fre-
quencies, and the correlation coefficients did indicate a significant correlation. Yet, the problem
arose for the methods as none obtains the actual resistance level, even though the coefficient
shows a great correlation. The theoretical calculation and the RMK+1 model main uncertain-

ties consist of information. However, the RMK+1 model proves to be the best for determining
the surface impedance of the porous absorber compared to resonant absorbers. Generally, the
RMK+1 mapped surface impedance overestimates the resistance at low frequencies with the ap-
plied constraints, whereas the theoretically calculated impedance underestimates the resistance.
The manufacturers only provide absorption coefficients and only down to the 125 Hz octave band.
Therefore, accuracy would be increased by having the correct information about the absorbers.
The measurements and theoretical calculations of the resonant absorbers with two porous layers
indicate similar absorption coefficients at high frequencies, meaning the additional porous layers
mainly impact low-frequency absorption. However, the construction of the Helmholtz absorber with
the perforated panel PH5 shows the most significant reduction in absorption from one to two porous
layers. The results demonstrate an increase in impedance, where the most significant increase was
seen for the perforated panel with the most excellent perforation rate. Thus a relationship between
the impact of the porous layer and the perforation rate was proportional.
As only a few absorbers have been studied, further investigations would be applicable, thus, fur-
ther material investigation of resonant absorbers, multilayer absorbers, and the associated surface
impedance properties to use the impedance as an input to room acoustic simulations. To increase
the correlation to the measurements and accuracy of the calculations, as well as the simulations,
increase the demand for additional information about the absorbers from the manufacturers.
