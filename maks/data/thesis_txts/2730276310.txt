Abstract
Non­destructive testing is an important tool to test the mechanical properties of construc­
tion materials, with respect to their service­life assessment or reuse. The reuse of building
components is almost non­existent in the building industry today. The well known non­
destructive method of ultrasonic testing, was used in the experimental part of the project.
The investigation was based on an extensive literature review, used to identify the influ­
encing parameters on ultrasonic testing, and the’ applicability in detecting damage as well
as the quality and durability parameters in cylindrical concrete specimens.
Therefore, this project aims at using available NDT results from laboratory samples with
different densities that can be used to develop a model for how to estimate the ultra­
pulse velocity of layered composites with known physical properties and different levels
of pretension.
Furthermore, a significant number of specimens, made by different material than con­
crete, and drilled in the way that could match the diameter of the concrete specimens,
were used during the project. The developed model would help to assess materials that
develop mineralogical zones due to their exposure conditions such as concrete exposure
to seawater. So, naturally a question was raised through these several experiments:
­ How these concrete elements or at least these materials would improve and be imple­
mented into the marine environment, so that the influence would have been the lowest
into the ecosystem?
Additionally, complex surfaces pose challenges due to geometric limitations for the place­
ment of the measurement heads (sender / receiver) and rough surfaces. Experimental
tests were performed on cast concrete specimens and the ultrasonic pulse amplitude
(UPA) was used to investigate the detection of artificial location in cast concrete speci­
mens.
Moreover, the ultrasonic pulse amplitude of concrete specimens containing voids showed
significantly lower amplitude than a homogeneous reference specimen. In addition, the
ultrasonic pulse amplitude is an applicable method for detecting artificially created voids
in cast concrete specimens.
Lastly, a specific set­up was build in DTU Laboratory, to help keeping the different speci­
mens connected, during the testing with the Pundit device. On this set­up, three different
moments were applied by a mechanical hand torque, so that three different compressive
strengths, were applied in the samples to keep the specimens connected. That way, was
thought a proper way that would guarantee the sample’s continuity and the information
coming from the sender to the receiver, would have been much clear, than compared
to the case without the application of a compressive strength, or at least the information
wouldn’t had been lost into the specimens voids.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The quality of concrete strength assessment by the measurement of NDT physical pa­
rameters (as velocity of ultrasonic waves), is affected by various sources of uncertainty
due to the quality of the measurements and to the variability of the concrete.
The idea of combining two or several non­destructive techniques to overcome the limits of
a single NDT method and improve the assessment of the concrete compressive strength,
has been matter of research in the last decades [1]. Many case studies exist in which
different techniques have been combined, but real added value can only be obtained if
the issue of combination is correctly analysed [2].
Environmental investments are often downgraded in today’s society, because the effects
are only visible far into the future. The construction industry, which is a significant con­
sumer of resources and energy, plays a vital role in creating change.
Today, the building industry is responsible for approximately 40% of all the materials used
on a global scale, while it produces around 35% of all waste in the world. Therefore, it is
essential to reduce material consumption and generated waste in this industry to aid in
contributing to a more sustainable world.
Through reports, it is known that cement is the most important contributing factor to the
environmental impacts and that no evidence recycling concrete has a substantial reduc­
tion in emissions. On the other side, cement is also one of the most important materials
on producing concrete. Meaning that the usage of it cannot be eliminated, but it has to be
reduced as much as possible, or companies should be producing other relevant types of
cement that affect the least the environment, which is something that wasn’t elaborated
in this project.
A large part of the circular economy model is developing systems and models that incen­
tives businesses, to adopt a more circular approach to the life cycle of building materials.
Many recommendations have been made on how to operate more circularly.
To document and evaluate the physical properties in a material passport, it is necessary
to investigate the current state of structural elements through field testing. It is, therefore,
necessary to evaluate components in a non­destructive manner. The challenge then be­
comes to evaluate the properties using non­destructive tests as accurately as when using
destructive tests.
One of the strengths of non­destructive testing, is its ability to be used to evaluate the
quality and durability without compromising the integrity of the element. Ultrasonic testing
is a recognized non­destructive test method to evaluate these parameters.
The challenge was to integrate this method into developing a model, that would optimize
and analyze the NDT results, not only for the samples made by same concrete material
specimens, but also by combining them with different specimens made by other mate­
rial, different from concrete, or maybe even made by concrete, but that would have had
different material properties, such as different densities.
==PAGE:8==
1.2
Scope and problem statement
Before starting the project, a fundamental part was the initial literature review of this study,
as it helped to define the extent of the scope and thereby shaped the problem statement.
The initial literature review, was focused mostly on the initial research theory on different
non­destructive test methods, and their applicability on evaluating quality and durability
parameters in concrete.
Moreover, The Pundit Lab/ Pundit Lab+ ultrasonic instrument literature was reviewed,
in order to have a more thorough information on the instructions about the limitations and
the usage of the device.
The last topic covered in the initial research, was on understanding the research data
for the influence and how to implement the required model into the marine environment,
without or the least damage caused into the sea ecosystem.
The aims of this project were as below:
­ Determining and developing a composite material model based on the limits of the NDT
methods, when applying them into concrete elements.
­ The limitations of the methods for lab analysis in combination with optimization of con­
crete recipes towards more efficient use of cement, that may favor the growth of biogenic
layers in marine environments. Moreover, determining the influence of the concrete ele­
ments in the marine environment.
­ The influence of different materials layering on the UPV value, and if possible developing
a model made by different material and different density layers and how the results will
compare to the model developed by different concrete layers.
It is virtually implausible to evaluate the properties of the interface between layers using
visual inspection. Thus, performing an appropriate non­destructive test (NDT) could be
advantageous in damage detection.
The non­destructive test includes many techniques; however, ultrasonic testing (UT) has
been widely employed due to its low cost, flexibility, and ability to detect damage in struc­
tural elements [3]. Consequently, ultrasonic testing is an appropriate method to identify
damage in a composite material such as concrete [4].
Linear ultrasonic testing relies on the gradual changes of ultrasonic measurements, such
as ultrasonic pulse velocity (UPV). Ultrasonic pulse velocity has been widely used due
to its simplicity, versatility, and repeatability. Various researchers studied the UPV mea­
surements to determine the best correlation between compressive strength and UPV.
However, the UPV test could not accurately identify the damage caused by an interlayer
in the concrete specimens, but was seen that the damages did affected and lowered the
intensity of the information coming from the sender to the receiver, regardless if a whole
sample was tested or a sample made by different layered specimens.
Based on the above parentheses and the background of the project, the main question
was obvious:
­ Is it possible to determine and develop a model, that would present the same, or almost
the same results, when comparing the whole sample, or by having different single layered
specimens, with the case when connected together by a set­up and by applying a specific
compressive strength?”

2
Theory
2.1
Ultrasonic testing
The Ultrasonic test is a well recognized non­destructive test method used to evaluate the
quality of concrete for more than 60 years [5]. Ultrasonic testing is becoming prevalent in
the field of civil engineering, and its applications mostly include the evaluation of concrete
structures and pavements [6].
Ultrasonic pulses are mechanical waves with a frequency above 20kHz, which can, in
essence, behave similarly to audible sound waves. However, ultrasonic waves do not
transmit through the air or a vacuum. Because of this, electronic coupling in the form of
aqueous transmission gel, need to be applied between transducers and the surface of the
concrete, which is done to ensure full transmission.
When the ultrasonic waves enter the concrete, they will scatter. This is due to in­homogeneity
in the concrete caused by the structure of the material being made up of different con­
stituents. The interfacial transition zone (ITZ), which is the region of the cement paste
around the aggregate particles, are shown to reflect the seismic waves out of the main
beam, which is the direct path between the transducers.
2.2
Wave types
Ultrasonic waves include longitudinal waves, shear waves, plate waves and surface waves.
Within the field of ultrasonic testing, the two types of waves that are most commonly used
are longitudinal waves (P­waves) and shear waves (S­waves or transverse waves), which
are illustrated in the picture below:
Figure 2.1: Longitudinal and share waves
For the longitudinal waves (P­waves), oscillations happen in a longitudinal direction, which
is where the name comes from. These waves are also known as pressure or compression
waves due to the active forces in the waves being expansion and compression.
==PAGE:10==
Longitudinal waves are able to be generated in solids, liquids and gasses due to wave en­
ergy being able to travel through atomic structures. For shear waves, oscillations happen
at a right angle or in the crossing of the direction of propagation. Unlike longitudinal waves,
shear waves are not effective at travelling in liquids or gasses, and they only propagate
effectively in materials that are considered acoustically solid.
From seismic theory, it is known that longitudinal waves propagate through a solid with
the highest velocities and that the surface waves travel with the lowest velocities [7].
The velocity of a longitudinal wave, mostly depends on the elastic properties and density
of the concrete, and is given by the following expression:

2.3
Transducers
The transducers, while taking ultrasonic tests, can be arranged in three different trans­
mission configurations: a) direct transmission, b) semi­direct transmission, and c) indirect
transmission.
The direct transmission configuration is the most preferred of the three, because it allows
for the maximum amount of energy to be received. The semi­direct transmission config­
uration is also widespread, as it is advantageous in situations where it is necessary to
avoid reinforcement.
Moreover, if the transducers are too far from one another, the ultrasonic wave will atten­
uate to a level not detected by the receiver and the transmission intensity will be very low
1%.
Furthermore, the indirect method of arranging the transducers, has a higher probability
of error, and it is, therefore, necessary to follow a different, and longer procedure, as the
signal received is greatly weakened compared to the direct method. The indirect method
is particularly useful for determining crack depth, surface quality or in the case when only
one surface is accessible.

Methodology and materials

left into the curing chambers inside deionised water for some time, were taken into ac­
count. Those concrete specimens were obtained from a specific mix design as seen on
Figure 3.1 and Figure 3.2.
It has to be stated, that all the prepared specimens and the data, were the ones pro­

3.2
Development of set­up and test equipment
After all the necessary samples were stored, the first connecting experiments started. In
the beginning clamps were used, but it was seen that the set up wasn’t the right one and
the information between the transducers was lost or it was very low sometimes, as seen
in Figure 3.5.
Another issue when using the clamps, was that the applied compressive strength on the
samples couldn’t be calculated, and it would have been difficult to understand or define
the applied pressure for the continuity of the experiments.
==PAGE:15==
Figure 3.5: Initial tests with clamps for the connection of the samples
Based on the first attempts, it was important to find or even build a set­up that would
have been more helpful and would be idealistic for the conduction of the experiments.
Therefore, some simple sketches were made and it was discussed with the craftsman of
DTU Laboratory if they could help or think of something to come in help. Finally, it was
agreed and it was built a unique for this project set­up, that would help in conducting all
the necessary experiments.
It had to be stated, that the set up was built specifically for this project, and it was requested
to be used only for this project, as a helpful tool for the experiments.
The set­up was built with two circle sides, made of hollow steel tubes to match the samples
diameter D =≃100mm , with an opening in the center to match the transducers diameter
d1 ≃5 −6mm, and with four nodes welded in the outside part of the hollow tubes. In
order to conduct the experiment, four steel throd­bolts with a diameter d2 ≃8mm and a
length L = 300mm, were used to connect both sides of the set­up as seen in Figure 3.6
and Figure 3.7. This set­up not only allowed the conduction of the experiments, but could
help into calculating the compressive strength applied in the throd­bolts and could keep
the samples connected and compressed during the experiments as seen in Figure 3.8.

5
Conclusion
After numerous experiments and data analysis the conclusions were almost visible and
as below: ­ First, when compared two different concrete samples it was detected that
the more cuts were made into the samples, the lower the transmission intensity and the
information taken from the device was.
All these information was shown through tables and graphs.
The same outcome was even when was taken into account a simple concrete sample,
but cut in several progressive specimens.
­ To continue, the application of gel between layers and the mechanical torque, as a com­
pressive tools for the specimens in the set­up, were two methods that improved signifi­
cantly the initial results for the concrete specimens, and for the other material specimens,
of which the material properties differ from concrete.
­ Moreover, when compared concrete specimens with gypsum and LECA block speci­
mens, it was seen that concrete had higher resisting properties and could bear nigher
values of applied torsional moment, as part of its homogeneity and solidity as material.
­Lastly, it was developed a model that had better and improved properties than all the other
combinations experimented, and it was nearly achieved the main scope of the project, by
developing a model that would be set­up from small specimens, but will behave or have
the same material properties as compared to a whole concrete sample.
