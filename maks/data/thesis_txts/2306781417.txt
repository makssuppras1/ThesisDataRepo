Preface
This report represents the final thesis of the master’s program at the Technical University 
of Denmark - DTU, giving the degree of Master of Science in Architectural Engineering. 
The work and report represents 30 ECTS points. 
The project has been conducted, partly at International Centre for Indoor Environment 
and Energy (ICIEE) at DTU, Kgs. Lyngby and partly at Ørtoft A/S, Kgs. Lyngby in the time pe-
riod 25-01-2016 to 25-06-2016. Measurements were performed at the building in focus, 
located in Nærum, Denmark - 20km north of Copenhagen. 
Technical University of Denmark - Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark
24th of June, 2016

Abstract
A European Commission did in 2011 set a common goal for the European members to 
reduce the total energy use by at least 20% at 2020 compared to the energy use in 2011. 
With almost 40% of this total energy use originating from the building mass and 80% of 
this being used for operation and maintenance is reducing initiatives within the building 
sector much needed. The use of sustainable solutions, such as TABS (Thermally Activated 
Building System), which can be combined with renewable energy sources, is increasing in 
the new-built segment. However, the saving potential in renovating the existing building 
mass is, by the Danish building research Institute SBi estimated to be 30-35% [1]. 
In a case building located in Nærum, Denmark has a new radiant cooling system been 
installed. The building, being an office building, has a high internal heat load and requires 
an effective HVAC system accompanied by an intelligent control. The system consists of 
pipes mounted to the lower surface of the floor separation slabs with a covering alumini-
um profile, intended to enhance the cooling output. With this design is the system work-
ing somewhere between a radiant panel and a TABS, due to the direct contact with the 
concrete slab. 
The performance of the system and the resulting thermal indoor climate has been inves-
tigated by calculations based on a set of standards, heat transfer simulations in HEAT2, 
dynamic indoor climate and energy simulations in IDA ICE, short- and long-term meas-
urements and via questionnaires send out to the occupants in the building. The basis for 
these investigations has been a thorough study of the buildings HVAC system and how it is 
operated.
The results show that the thermal indoor climate of the building, in the measuring period 
in general are satisfying and within DS/EN 15251 Category II backed up by both measure-
ments and questionnaires. However, there were indications of risk for high temperatures 
at warm weather. The radiant cooling systems cooling performance was via simulations, 
calibrated with measured temperatures, estimated to be 11,3W/m2, which is only around 
50% of the, by the designing engineer, assumed output. The reason for this is mostly the 
influence of the suspended ceiling and the air layer resistance it provides.

Background
2.	Background
2.1.	
Low-energy Heating and Cooling Systems 
The required reduction in the primary energy need for buildings required by government, 
especially when used commercially, has increased the need for well-insulated building 
envelopes, which has led the way for low-energy systems, using renewable energy sources 
such as solar energy and geothermal energy to name a few. These low transmission losses 
make it possible for the heating and cooling systems, by exploiting e.g. the ceiling or floor 
as a radiant surface, to operate at temperatures close to the desired indoor temperature, 
which improves the efficiency of e.g. heat exchangers, pumps etc. [3]. For office buildings 
with a high internal heat gain and a low transmission loss is cooling required almost all 
year around [4], but with high temperature cooling is this done by supply water tempera-
tures in the range of 16-20 °C [3]. 
Different radiant system principles exists, which is further described in the Rehva Guide-
book no. 7 [5]. These principles can be sorted into three main categories: 
•	 Radiant heating and cooling panels
•	 Pipes isolated from the main building structure 
•	 Thermally Activated Building Systems - TABS
Embedded systems, also known as TABS, is usually pipes embedded in a building structure 
of high thermal mass, without being isolated from the structure. This way is it possible via 
a heat carrying medium (water at the most) to heat up or cool down the  construction, 
resulting in a changed indoor air temperature as well. Utilizing the high level of thermal 
inertia of the structure is a slow process and results in a long response time compared to 
traditional HVAC systems.

Background
Panel systems work independent from the construction. This is in practice typically done 
in wooden floor constructions or suspended ceilings, where a pipe typically via an enhanc-
ing plate of a high conductive material transfers the heat to the surrounding air. Independ-
ent systems’ response time are very short, compared to embedded systems. 
Common for both systems is that they can only add or remove sensible heat from a room. 
In order to manage latent heat is ventilation systems necessary. However, compared with 
fully air-conditioned spaces can this system be down-sized, potentially improving the com-
fort (draught and noise) significantly. 
2.1.1.	 Thermally Activated Building Systems (TABS) 
Thermally Activated Building Systems (TABS) have via research and monitoring the last 20 
years proved that it can meet the requirements, which today’s building code regulations 
dictates. A study made by Rijksen et. al. [8] showed that with a given TABS installed in a 
building with 30% window area compared to floor area and an internal heat gain of 40W/
m2 was the required cooling capacity 30W/m2, whereas the same building with a system 
without thermal storage required 60W/m2. This is further supported by Behrendt et. al., 
who concludes that long operation times and high supply temperatures can cause reduc-
tion in the required cooling power for the chiller up to 50% [9]. One reason for this is that 
the utilization of the thermal mass of the building “serves to flatten out peaks in energy 
demand”[4]. However in order to ensure such reductions, is a certain level of automated 
control of the HVAC systems required due to the vulnerability of such system - the long re-
sponse time, which makes manual control nearly impossible and should be avoided [10]. 
2.1.2.	 Principle of TABS 
The principle of TABS is to utilize the high thermal inertia of the building structure (con-
crete) as a buffer. Pipes are embedded in the structure, typically in the either lower or 
upper part of the floor deck, where it provides either cooling or heating. 
This system generates a diurnal cycle of charging and discharging of the construction 
alternately, as illustrated in Figure 3. In the figure is the cycle beginning at a temperature 
of 21°C (1). The introduction to internal heat gains from occupants and solar heat gain 
causes a rather rapid increase in air and room temperature. The difference in air and room 
temperature ((2)-(2’)) can be explained by the fact that the convective fraction of the 
heat sources is greater than the radiant. After a momentary drop in temperature during 
lunch it continues to increase until the end of the day (3) where the temperature drops.

causes a gradually rise in surface temperature ((1)-(4)). The discharge of the construction 
((4)-(5)) done by the removed heat via the embedded pipes ensures that the room tem-

prices in some countries are lower during night, which reduces the overall operating costs.

Background
2.1.3.	 Advantages and Disadvantages of TABS
As implied earlier has TABS clear advantages, which appeals to implement it in the build-
ing. 
The main advantages of TABS are:
•	 It takes advantage of the building structure’s thermal storage capacity, reducing peak 
loads through longer cooling periods 
•	 Large heat exchange surfaces and the storage capacity makes low supply temperatures 
close to the operative temperature possible which opens for renewable energy sourc-
es
•	 Floor height can be reduced due to less need for ventilation and thereby reduced duct 
sizes. In multi-storey buildings can this potentially make room for another floor
•	 Possibility of low maintenance-, installation- and operation costs 
•	 No pipes above the suspended ceiling (compared to e.g. radiant panels) - higher level 
of flexibility
However, there is certain critical aspects, which requires attention in order to achieve a 
good design: 
•	 TABS requires a relatively clear, unobstructed view to the room, which when used as 
a ceiling system somewhat eliminates the use of suspended ceilings, that normally 
serves as an acoustical absorber
•	 A slow response time requires a good building design with proper prevention of over-
heating (solar shading etc.)
•	 In refurbishing situations is a great level of cautiousness required if changes to the 
structural system is made

Heat is mainly transmitted via three different principles, where their individual interaction

2.2.3.	 Convection
By convection is energy transferred through movement of a fluid between a cold and hot 
body - a mass transfer of either liquid or gas, between e.g. a surface and air. Convection 
can be split into two categories - natural or forced. Natural convection is driven by a buoy-
ancy effect, where a density change caused by temperature differences causes the con-
vection. Forced convection on the other hand is a fluid motion generated by an external 
source such as a fan, pump etc. Regardless of principle can the heat transfer be described

theoretically, due to the complexity of convective flows and the fact that hk depends on 
the geometry and the specific fluid. Instead is experimental found values used.

water have a 4 times higher heat capacity. When combined with a construction material

energy use of up to 50% compared with cooling by ventilation [8].  It also provides an 
opportunity for ducting to be reduced significantly and thereby keeping the room volume 
but decreasing the building height.

4.	 Theoretical Investigations, HEAT2
Several investigations of the cooling system as well as the indoor climate it contributes to 
has been performed in order - among others, to assess the functionality of the system as 
well as to find out whether the system performs as originally intended. 
These investigations are spread over different areas but are generally divided into two 
main categories - theoretical investigations and practical/measuring investigations. This 
chapter addresses the theoretical investigation of the system. 
4.1.	
Performance Investigation of the Cooling System’s Cooling Output
The cooling system is built with the intention that it will somewhat work as a TABS. How-
ever, it is interesting to see whether it acts as intended or if it behaves differently. A quite 
possible solution is that the system acts more as a radiant panel and less as a TABS system, 
due to the nature of the system.  
 
The cooling system has been investigated with three methods; simple calculations by hand 
based on calculations methods given in ISO 18566-3 and DS/EN 15377-1, numerical heat 
transfer simulations in the software HEAT2 and on-site measurements. The first two meth-
ods are included in this chapter.

4.1.3.	 Discussion & Conclusion
After the investigations in HEAT2 it is clear that the resistances have a big influence on the 
thermal performance of the system. A deep knowledge of the system design is required 
in order to achieve valid results. The installed system is unique in a sense that it has not 
been applied before or at least never been thoroughly investigated before. For the same 
reason have it been hard to determine exactly, which resistance values and calculation 
methods are most suitable for the system. In order to determine the correct resistances is 
detailed measurements required, which have not been a possibility for this thesis. 
In general is it seen that the total heat output is higher for the embedded system com-
pared to the panel system. This can be explained by the utilization of the thermal mass of 
the concrete along with the bigger distribution surface. 
Depending on the chosen air layer resistance for the plenum does the system perform 
more as a floor cooling for the room above, than as a ceiling cooling for the room be-
low. This is clearly seen in Figure 24. Stagnant air works successfully as an isolator and 
will reduce the heat extraction from the room below, causing a lowering of the overall 
slab temperature and thereby increasing the heat extraction from the overlaying office. 
Depending on the magnitude of the air layer resistance, does the downwards heat flux  
consists of 20-75% of the total heat flux. An air layer resistance based on a somehow 
ventilated plenum (Dom. M.’s findings), will give an increased total heat flow - in this case 
almost 20%, and at the same time a more equal division of the heat flow. This is also true 
for the embedded system.

5.1.1.	 Introduction 
The energy renovation of the building has been made, partly in order to reduce the 
energy use of the building but also to improve the indoor environment. An analysis of 
the energy use as well as the indoor environment has been performed, together with a 
parametric study of, which measures could improve both energy performance and indoor 
environment. 
The analysis has been performed in IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE) version 4,7. 
IDA ICE is a dynamic multi-zone simulation program used to make detailed studies of in-
door environment of individual zones as well as the energy use of the entire building [16].  
The software takes into account the buildings physical parameters of the building but also 
shading from eventual adjacent buildings and structures, the winds influence on the in-
door temperature with air flow models defined from the location type (urban, sub-urban, 
open country) are considered as well as the outdoor climate of the location. It is possible 
to model detailed HVAC systems and create specific control macros.

5.1.5.	 Discussion & Conclusion
The calibration of the model have been rather difficult due to multiple reasons. The heat-
ing system is manually controlled by the occupants and is thereby difficult to estimate. 
The supply air temperature for both ventilation and radiant cooling systems are manually 
controlled, which normally does not take place before the occupants complain about high 
temperatures in the offices. The blinds are controlled manually by the occupants, which 
can be assumed only to being in use when glare is occurring. The energy consumption is 
metered totally for the entire building and not by systems, functions etc., and the distribu-
tion was therefore estimated. 
Radiant systems, both panels and embedded pipes are simulated with standard given 
templates for the systems in IDA ICE. The details of how each template works - such as 
resistances, prevention of upwards heat flow etc. is unknown. What is possible to define 
is the cooling/heating output of the system, design temperature as well as the embedded 
depth of the pipes (for embedded system). With this in mind, is the results regarding radi-
ant panels an approximation and not necessarily fully similar to the actual system. It can 
however be commented, that the radiant panels affect the thermal indoor climate signif-
icantly with only a small increase in energy use. Combined with ventilation and following 
==PAGE:74==
DTU  -  Rasmus von Wurstemberger Nielsen
Page 74  of 108
Theoretical Investigations, IDA ICE
the general TABS principle with radiant cooling during night is the increase negligible but 
the improvement of the thermal environment is significant. Further more can it be said, 
that with the combination of ventilation and radiant cooling lies a saving potential, due to 
the fact that electricity is cheaper to buy during night hours than day hours. 
When comparing cooling by radiant panel and embedded system (no. 1 and 3) is it seen 
that the energy use with an embedded system is reduced as well as the amount of over-
heating hours. This may be due to the fact that the embedded system takes advantage of 
the thermal inertia of the concrete, which increases the effective surface area as well as 
potentially benefiting more of the thermal buffer that it provides. 
The investigation of reduction of energy use with heat loss reductions is on a hypotheti-
cal level since neither the heat losses from heating and DHW strings, nor the specific use 
from local zone cooling coils for ventilation distribution ducts is known. The reduction is 
therefore somewhat relative, but gives a sense of the saving potential. 
The increased PPD and thereby worsened thermal comfort for the model with combined 
parameters, is  mostly caused from the changed heating schedule. However, the PPD 
is still low and in fact within category I. The amount of overheating hours are however 
decreased along with the energy use. The yearly saving potential for the building with 
this system implementation is ideally 25%, corresponding to 156MWh. It should again be 
mentioned that these numbers are based on simulations with models, where assumptions 
have been made in order to achieve a performance close to the actual buildings and are 
therefore guiding but not completely accurate.

6.3.2.	 Questionnaires 
One way to assess the indoor environment is via standards. However, these are based on 
a generalization of the majority of buildings and its occupants, where the specific building 
may differ. A way to achieve quantitative data is via questionnaires, where the targeting 
group have the possibilities to address how their personal assessment of the indoor envi-
ronment, whether they are satisfied or not. 
A questionnaire has been conducted in collaboration with ICIEE - International Centre for 
Indoor Environment and Energy, DTU and focuses on following aspects: 
•	 Background (sex, work type, average work day, location in building) 
•	 Overall assessment of the indoor climate (very well vs. very bad)
•	 Thermal comfort (satisfied vs. dissatisfied and its impact on the work performance)
•	 Addressing of when and where the dissatisfaction derived from
•	 Air quality (satisfied vs. dissatisfied and its impact on the work performance)
•	 Health symptoms (fatigue, headache, sleepy; relation to the building or a general 
issue)
•	 Building features (satisfied with eventual user control of e.g. heating system, light, 
blinds etc.)
The full questionnaire is found in Appendix E.4. When answering, were the occupants 
asked to focus on the last month.

6.5.6.	 Discussion & Conclusion
In general is it from both measuring results as well as questionnaires seen that the indoor 
environment is satisfactory in the building. However, especially the north-oriented office 
experiences a high level of dissatisfied occupants. Only 50% is satisfied, where 15% is 
more than "just dissatisfied" hinting that the lack of indoor quality, in terms of both ther-
mal comfort and a good indoor air quality, is a general problem. 
If focusing specifically on the thermal environment however, are the occupants from the 
north-oriented office most satisfied, when looking at the questionnaires. 83% declare 
themselves satisfied with the thermal environment, whereas the other offices are be-
tween 61-64%. Here is the general perception, that the temperature is too high in the 
offices - 70% in weather which is neither too cold nor too warm. However, if looking on 
the corresponding PPD for the given period (May) is the environment in the north-orient-
ed office within Category II 81% of the time. For the south- and west-oriented offices is 
the time in Category II 95% and 85% respectively. With this it can be concluded that for 
the building is the DS/EN 15251 optimistic compared to the actual perception. The rela-
tively low level of satisfaction of the east-, south- and west-oriented offices compared to

the north-oriented office when looking at the questionnaires, corresponds well with the 
assumption, that the solar heat gain has a big influence of the thermal environment. This 
is further underlined by the calculated PPD throughout the orientations, where the meas-
ured time in Category II decreases for the east-, south-, and west-oriented offices.
 
The occupants perceptions of the thermal environment is not corresponding with the cal-
culated, when comparing the different orientations. An explanation for this may lie in the 
human factor in the questionnaire, meaning that not all occupants perceive the thermal 
environment the same. Figure 46 indicates that there are more females in the offices in 
general and that the overweight is highest in the south- and west-oriented offices. 
In general can it be argued, that with only a month (roughly) with high temperatures dur-
ing the measurements, is there a risk that the amount of overheating hours will increase 
- especially for the east-, south- west-oriented offices. This is also indicated by the results 
from the indoor climate simulation seen in chapter 5.
If focusing on the air quality of the offices does almost 75% find the level of stuffiness a 
substantial problem, whereas 95% finds it a problem. Furthermore does 20% believe that 
the ventilation have an impact on the level of health problems (fatigue, headache etc.). 
66% believes that their fatigue etc. is directly related to working in the building. These 
factors indicate that the air change, recommended by DS 3033 is too low. A suggestion for 
improvement could be to install a CO2-sensor, that indicates the CO2-level for the occu-
pants, along with a local control unit that makes it possible to overwrite the system and 
thereby for a period increase the flow rate. An increase in the supply air flow may howev-
er affect the thermal comfort in terms of draught and should be done with caution. This 
suggestion means substantial changes of the ventilation system, which most likely is not 
profitable. 
From the results is it seen, that the radiant cooling system does not perform as intended. 
With an expected total effective cooling output of 18,6W/m2 is the more realistic, found 
by simulation, output 11,3W/m2 a rather significant reduction, which in the overall energy 
balance results in periods with a insufficient cooling capacity. The reason for this differ-
ence, which were also commented on in chapter 4, is that the resistance of the air layer 
between the slab and the suspended ceiling has been underestimated.

The effect of this resistance was already in by Ørtoft A/S' simulations indicating that the 
system operated more as floor cooling than ceiling cooling. However, with the results from 
chapter 4 was it seen, that the distribution was less equal - instead of a 63/37% distri-
bution upwards/downwards respectively, is it more realistic with a distribution around 
80/20%. From the measurements is it seen that the downwards heat flux from the slab 
to the plenum by a weighted average is 7,3W/m2, whereas it from the suspended ceiling 
is only 1,9W/m2. From this can it be discussed whether the remaining heat flux is simply 
extracted by the ventilation outlet, which is positioned in the immediate same height as 
the suspended ceiling. This is however not documented. 
The main reason for the calculated heat flux found by the formula (28) given by Rehva 
Guidebook no. 07 (based on the measured surface temperatures) is higher than the meas-
ured heat flux with the hukseflux sensor, can be found in the fact that the formula is not 
directly intended for this particular radiant system and the nature of it. 
Regarding the suspended ceilings effect on the downwards cooling output could a way to 
decrease the influence of the air layer resistance be to replace the horizontal suspended 
ceiling plates with vertical absorber baffles. This way is the air layer resistance more or 
less eliminated with a maintained proper acoustical absorption of the room. 
The energy balance indicates that during days with a high outdoor temperature and a 
clear sky does the building have an insufficient cooling capacity. The calculated heat load 
is a realistic worst case scenario taking into consideration that not all occupants are at 
work. The biggest contributor is the solar heat gain, which is only controlled via the ex-
ternal blinds. This is however controlled manually and therefore assumed to be used only 
due to issues with glare. If it was controlled by temperature instead, assuming a shading 
factor of 66% instead of 33% (used value for solar gain calculations) would sum of loads 
be 0 for all orientations. The heat load from lighting, which is assumed on all the time, 
could be decreased by considering that daylight sensors are regulating the lighting. How-
ever this does not affect the result much. In general can it be said that the energy balance 
is based on a worst case situation and is therefore a picture of a critical situation and not 
of the average situation.

Discussion
7.	 Discussion
Renovation of the existing building mass in Denmark possess a big potential, and with an 
increase in the desire to operate the building sustainable is new ideas needed. The princi-
ple of the installed radiant cooling system in the case building is well known but is how-
ever unique in a sense that the nature of the system is rarely seen, if at all. For the same 
reason have thorough investigations never been performed before, in order to determine 
the actual performance of the system as well as assessing whether it performs better than 
conventional cooling systems or not. 
It can be concluded that the construction of the system affects the performance signifi-
cantly. Especially should the choice of absorption regarding the acoustical environment be 
considered carefully. A horizontal suspended ceiling covering the majority of the ceiling 
surface should be avoided if the covering material is not perforated, since the convective 
air flow in the perimeter-openings caused by buoyancy effect is not strong enough. An 
ideal alternative would be to use vertical baffles as absorber plates instead. Another possi-
ble improvement could be to combine the radiant cooling system with diffuse ventilation, 
but this is base for another study and outside the scope of this thesis. 
With a smaller or no resistance, it might possibly work less as a TABS and more as a ra-
diant panel, as seen in Figure 24 where the majority of the heat flux is downwards with 
no suspended ceiling. The fact that the ventilation exhaust diffuser is located in the same 
immediate plane as the suspended ceiling also suggests that part of the provided cool-
ing, which may be trapped in the plenum is extracted directly into the ventilation exhaust 
causing a short circuit and thereby not provided to the room. However, this is not investi-
gated. 
Measurements show that there in the measuring period is no immediate critical situa-
tions and that the occupants in general are satisfied. However, with relatively high level of 
overheating already, especially in the west-oriented office and with the knowledge that 
the summer has only just begun could one worry that the cooling system - or the current 
operation is inadequate.

Discussion
In general is the fact that the system 	is manually control a problem. Sealens et al. could in 
an analyse of energy and comfort performance for TABS [25] conclude that manual control 
of TABS in general should be avoided due to the fact that immediate control is impossible 
with such system. Manual control means that changes does most likely not occur before 
people becomes dissatisfied, which most likely is too late. 
The following are suggestions to improve the system performance and the indoor environ-
ment: 
•	 Supply air temperature is controlled automatically by a room sensor for each orienta-
tion. This way it is possible to accommodate the changing need for cooling throughout 
the day.
•	 The radiant cooling system should be operated as the principle in chapter 2.1.2 during 
night hours, combined with night-ventilation when necessary. If the desired tempera-
ture is not reached the next morning, should the system operate until reached. 
•	 Supply water temperature for the radiant cooling system should be controlled auto-
matically by a room sensor combined with a tendency-control, which at several days 
with high outdoor temperatures will start the system. 
•	 External shading should be controlled automatically, by both glare in terms of solar 
intensity and room temperature. 
•	 Alternatively should booklets with information about the external shadings impact 
on the indoor temperature be handed out in order to educate people to take actions 
themselves. 
•	 Overwriting of the system / changing the set points should not occur, every time one 
person addresses his/hers dissatisfaction. People complain when dissatisfied, but does 
not necessarily represent the general opinion.

Conclusion
8.	 Conclusion
The system can at optimal conditions provide a total effective cooling of 11,3W/m2, found 
by simulation with measured inputs. For this cooling capacity is the majority - 77% of the 
heat flux upwards, which makes the system more a floor cooling system for the room 
above than a ceiling cooling system for the room below. The fact that the heat extraction 
is biggest from the upper side of the pipe and thereby from the construction/slab suggests 
that the system in principle may work as a TABS, understood in the way that the accu-
mulated heat in the concrete is extracted by the system discharges the construction, as 
explained by Figure 3. However it should be mentioned that this is only the case due to 
the suspended ceiling below the pipes and its resulting high downwards resistance. 
The indoor environment of the landscape offices is in general satisfactory but could be im-
proved by a higher air change, indicated by the fact that 75% of the answering occupants 
address the indoor air quality and the level of stuffiness as being a substantial problem. 
Assuming that the provided air rates correspond to the intentions of fulfilling the recom-
mendations from DS 3033, can it be concluded that the recommendations for this building 
should be increased.
With intelligent control of the building systems, including the radiant cooling system, the 
heating system, external blinds etc. is a 25% energy use reduction potentially possible, 
combined with an improvement of the indoor environment in terms of a reduced over-
heating. The PPD is slightly increased but is still well within thermal environment category 
II according to DS/EN 15251.
