Abstract
This study investigates the possibility to revise recommendations on the ventilation prin­
ciples in operating theatres in hospitals, set by different standards. Ventilation systems in
operating theatres use energy, hence the ventilation systems ensure cleanliness to avoid
surgical site infections to the patients. In the last 1,5 years, energy prices have risen, and
the awareness of being more sustainable is significant. Though, old recommendations re­
strict the possibility to lower energy use for ventilation systems in operating theatres, as
these have not been updated to complement these new issues.
The study is based on a model of an operating theater with dimensions 5,3 m (length) x 4,8
m (width) x 2,6 m (height), used in CFD simulation, full­scale experiments, and energy
simulations. The air temperature in the operating theatre was set to be within 20 ­ 24 °C, to
complement requirements from ASHREA standard 170 [1]. Three surgical staff members
surrounded the laying patient on an operating table in the middle of the room, with surgical
equipment placed near the patient’s head.
The effect of 3 different air changes rates (20 h−1, 15 h−1, and 10 h−1), placement of
exhausts, different supply diffusers (swirl, lobbed, and laminar), and the use of laminar
and turbulent airflow has been investigated through the use of CFD simulation. In the
simulation, the air temperature, air speed, and bacteria concentration were monitored, and
contour plots were used to evaluate the results.
Full­scale experiments were used to investigate the placement of exhausts in combination
with two different supply diffusers (semi­laminar box and honeycomb box), which were
costume made to provide a laminar airflow with an air change rate of 10 h−1. A thermal
manikin was used to simulate the patient. The air temperature, tracer gas concentration,
and manikin comfort were measured in all cases, hence the air velocity, draught rate, and
turbulence intensity were measured in some, with smoke visualizations. A heated blanket
in the operating theatre was also investigated in the full­scale experiment in combination
with the honeycomb box and low­wall exhausts to evaluate the comfort of the patient and
prevent hypothermia.
By the use of CFD simulation and full­scale experiments, results showed that a laminar/semi­
laminar airflow would provide a high ventilation effectiveness in the critical zone above
the patient, and hence minimize the risk of surgical site infections. In combination with
a laminar supply diffuser, the exhausts should be placed at a low­wall level, at opposite
walls, to remove contamination. The low­wall exhausts provides the best conditions in
combination with a laminar airflow.

The use of the heated blanket in combination with the honeycomb box diffuser showed
insignificant changes in the air movement in the critical zone, though it provided comfort­
able conditions to the patient. It is therefore recommended to implement a heated blanket
for the patient, as results without the heated blanket showed an unrealistic use of heat from
the thermal manikin.
The energy use of the three different air change rates in the operating theatre was investi­
gated through a yearly energy simulation. Hence, it was not possible to make changes to
the type of airflow, supply diffuser, and placement of exhausts. The results showed that
by using an air change rate of 10 h−1, there are 111 % energy savings compared to an air
change rate of 20 h−1.
Based on findings of this study it is recommended to revise the requirements set by stan­
dards on the ventilation principles in operating theatres, as changes could provide lower
energy use and therefore be more sustainable.

Introduction
Introduction to the relevant background information and theory are presented in this chap­
ter. First, the term surgical site infection is described, with some statistical information
on the occurrence. Then operating theatres are described, where relevant requirements are
presented, which are used to design a ventilation system for operating theatres. Hence, the
most common ventilation systems used in operating theatres are presented. Results about
energy use for ventilation systems are presented, based on earlier studies. And lastly, the
justification and objectives of the study are described.
1.1
Surgical site infection
When patients go to hospitals for an operation, the surgery will take place in an operating
theatre (OT). When entering a hospital as a patient there is a risk of getting a healthcare­
associated infection (HAI), which can occur during surgery or during recovery at the hos­
pital. Surgical site infections (SSIs) are the second most common cause of HAIs in Europe
and the United States [2]. During and after surgery, there is a 7% risk that bacteria can
contaminate the open wound, known as surgical site infection [3]. Surgical site infec­
tions are defined as infections occurring within 30 days of the procedure, and 1 year in
alloplastic surgeries [4].
SSI can threaten the lives of patients and lead to increased consumption of resources in the
healthcare system [5]. On average, SSI increases the hospital stay by 9,7 days, and mor­
tality associated with SSI can reach 3% [2]. 20 ­ 30% of SSI is caused by staphylococcus
aureus bacteria [6], and are commonly found on the human skin and in the upper respi­
ratory system. During surgery, the surgical team has been found to be the main source
of microbial contaminants [7].Hence, the surgical staff is wearing sterilized protection to
minimize contamination but some bacteria may still escape from gaps and exposed skin
e.g in the face.
1.2
Operating Theatres
Operating theatres also known as operating rooms in hospitals are used for operating pa­
tients every day. An operation requires surgeons and nurses with specific skills according
to the type of operation and the use of different tools. The OT also requires high cleanliness
to avoid the risk of SSI, hence the room has an aseptic environment, and strict protocols
and procedures to be followed thoroughly.

Layout of a typical operating theatre
When designing an operating theatre many requirements need to be fulfilled, which are set
by standards and the hospital. All surgeries have different needs for equipment and staff
skills, but some needs are more common. In Figure 1.1 a rendering of a new operating
theatre at Aarhus hospital in Denmark is shown, which gives a good example of the interior
in an OT. The OT requires a well­lit environment provided by surgical lamps and normally
there are no windows in the room.
Figure 1.1: Example of an OT layout at the new university­hospital at Aarhus [8]
In the Danish health institution guideline recommendations for the layout of the OT are
set. All surfaces and equipment need to be easy to clean and should tolerate disinfection.
The placement of non­moveable equipment on the floor might need special cleaning. The
lighting above the surgical area should interrupt the airflow as little as possible [5].
The operating table (size 2 m x 0,6 m) for the patient should be placed in the middle of
the room at a height of 0,7 m, but it should be possible to lower and raise the table, to
give easy access and comfortable working positions for the surgical staff. A table or more
for instruments is placed around the operating table. All surgeries are different and have
different needs, and the monitoring of the patient depends on the patient’s condition, size
and duration of operation, availability of instruments, and experience. In surgeries such as
the alloplastic implant, the patient needs to go under anesthesia, which requires monitoring
the heart function and circulation, as well as respiration and temperature. All equipment
is usually placed in a so­called tower, located near the patient’s head to not interrupt the
surgery.

1.2.4
Ventilation principles
The ventilation system in an operating theatre must ensure that the pre­defined air clean­
liness level can be achieved. This can be done by using either a Unidirectional Airflow
(UDAF) also known as laminar airflow (LAF), or by using a non­unidirectional (non­
UDAF) airflow also known as turbulent airflow (TAF). No standard defines which should
be used, hence recommendations from e.g the Danish national health organization suggest
using LAF, and if using TAF the number of people in the OT should be controlled and the
air change rate increased [5]. Both ventilation systems are used in operating theatres, as
studies have shown contradicting results as to which is best [18]. In Denmark, LAF was
used in 90 % of alloplastic operations from 1998 ­ 2008 [19].
As shown in Table 1.1 the OT is kept at a slightly higher air pressure of 10 ­ 15 Pa compared
with the surrounding rooms. As a result of this positive pressure, whenever a door opens
or if there is a leak in the seal of the room, clean air will flow out, and prevent unfiltered
air from entering the OT.
Figure 1.3: Turbulent airflow patter
Figure 1.4: Laminar airflow pattern
Laminar airflow is defined as an airflow pattern within a designated space that is uniform in
both velocity and direction and the air streams do not cross each other. When the Reynolds
number is in the range of 0­2000 then the airflow is laminar, which occurs at low air
velocities. With a laminar airflow, the air is normally supplied from a LAF diffuser at the
ceiling above the operating table and exhausted from grills at a low­wall level. The LAF
diffuser in an operating room should have a size of 3,2 m x 3,2 m according to the German
6
Optimization of Ventilation Principle And Use Of Energy In Operating Theatres
==PAGE:22==
standard DIN 1946­4 [11].
When designing a LAF system the following should be taken into consideration: required
air velocity, staff movement, heat sources, and airborne contamination removal efficiency
[20]. When using laminar airflow, movement and obstacles in the LAF zone can have a
big influence on the airflow and air cleanliness. Studies have shown that the laminar air
from the inlet to the outlet should not be interrupted by obstacles, to keep clean air [10].
When a limited space is available the obstacle should be placed to prevent dirty air from
coming into the clean air zone [21].
Turbulent airflow is the opposite of laminar airflow, where the air moves in an unpre­
dictable pattern with higher air velocities and mixes the air molecules around the room.
The airflow is turbulent when the Reynolds number is higher than 4000. With a TAF the
air is normally supplied from the ceiling in one or multiple smaller areas and exhausted
from the ceiling or on the wall at different heights. For TAF, many different supply dif­
fuser designs have been made, such as circular, squared, 2/3/4­way, perforated, and swirl
diffusers, which provide different air patterns in the room.
A study comparing LAF and TAF in an experimental mock­up showed that the LAF per­
formed better than the TAF on a protection factor weighted on the particle concentration.
The temperature was also measured and showed that the temperature distribution in the
room plays a significant role in the performance of the system [22]. However, a study
comparing LAF and TAF on the incident of prosthetic joint infection of real surgeries
over a time period of 10 years, showed no statistically significant difference [23]. WHO
Guideline on the prevention of surgical site infections does not suggest using LAF in
OT when performing total arthroplasty surgery, based on 7 studies conducted in different
countries [21]. When taking recommendations on the use of TAF vs LAF the information
and research behind the argument should be considered and weighted, as many studies
have shown contradictory results.
1.3
Energy use for ventilation system
From the first half of 2021 to the first half of 2022 energy prices in Denmark rose 57 %, and
energy prices in the rest of the EU also rose [24]. In Denmark, hospitals use 90 % of the
government’s energy expenses, as hospitals use energy for big machines and ventilation
which is running at all times a day [25]. The ventilation system in operating theatres uses
a high flow rate creating an air change rate of 20 h−1 which is a design recommendation
by the ASHRAE standard 170 [1].
Optimization of Ventilation Principle And Use Of Energy In Operating Theatres
7
==PAGE:23==
In hospitals, spaces for operating theatres use three to six times more energy than other
areas in the hospital. The carbon footprint related to the energy use of OTs is reported to
be 200 kt CO2e, which is equivalent to 22 % of the total National Health Service retained
estate­related carbon emissions. 58% of the carbon emissions from OTs are caused by the
directly consumed energy. This adds up to 473 kt CO2e total annual emissions associated
with operating theatres [26].
Two studies have investigated the energy use of ventilation systems in operating theatres
in Denmark. The first project studied if the use of Laminar Air Flow (LAF) in opera­
tion theatres used for alloplastic implant surgery reduces the infection risk as compared
with conventional Turbulent Air Flow (TAF), and the potential for optimizing energy use
based on the performance of one LAF and one TAF OT [27]. The study was conducted at
Gentofte Hospital, with mock­up operations on a patient dummy with full staff set up in
the two OTs. Tracer gas measurements were used to evaluate the air distribution. Experi­
ments showed that with LAF, air quality recommendations including bacterial counts were
met, even at 50 % outdoor air supply. In the TAF OT, the bacterial count exceeded the
guideline value of 10 CFU/m3 for ultraclean air in 4 out of 16 simulated surgeries. The
electricity use of the LAF was reduced by 41% without increasing the bacterial count.
This electricity use was below the electricity use in TAF OT running at 100% outdoor air
supply.
The second study done in 2015 made measurements of air hygiene in 17 OTs, ventilated
either by LAF or TAF during 51 live (not simulated) surgeries [28]. Estimated energy use
for transport and heating and cooling of the ventilation air under three operation condi­
tions: 1) typical operating condition (”As is”), 2) reduction of time when OT ventilation
runs in active mode (Level 1), and 3) reduction of outdoor air supply and total delivered air
supply when OT is not active (set back period) (Level 2). Measurements found that LAF
OT reduced the cfu counts compared with TAF OT during live joint arthroplasty surgeries.
All LAF OT provided ultraclean air with an active cfu count below 10 CFU/m3, whereas
TAF had nine surgeries exceeding this threshold. Results of the energy use of the three
different conditions showed that there was a saving on energy consumption and running
cost of approximately 15 % from ”As is” to Level 1 and 45 % from ”As is” to Level 2 in
the LAF OT, and in the TAF OT there was a 2 % saving from ”As is” to Level 1 and 32
% saving from ”As is” to Level 2.
Results from the project have raised interest in investigating the ventilation system in
more detail under controlled conditions in a climate chamber, as different variations can
be compared and evaluated on energy use.

1.4
Justification
A full­scale experiment measured the impact of different air change rates on the air qual­
ity of the surgical microenvironment in an operating room with mixing ventilation [29].
Investigation of ACH of 10 h−1, 15 h−1, 20 h−1, and 26 h−1 showed that the air change
rate should be higher than the recommended 20 h−1 to reduce the simulated air pollutants
concentration. The study used N2O tracer gas to simulate pollutants and to evaluate the
relative difference in the N2O concentrations obtained for the different cases. They con­
cluded that the air change rate should be higher than 20 h−1 since the increase of ACH
from 20 h−1 to 26 h−1 showed only a small difference in the N2O concentration. Based
on these results a question arises: is it beneficial to increase the ACH for a small decrease
in contamination, which would increase the energy use?
Another study showed that it is possible to have a lowered ACH of 18 h−1 with a high con­
tamination removal efficiency [30]. The study investigated the exhaust placement, ACH,
and the resulting airflow patterns in an OT with mixing ventilation in full­scale conditions.
Measurements of N2O tracer gas concentration and air velocities were conducted. Mixing
ventilation was achieved by 4 supply diffusers with 81 rotat­able nozzles installed in the
ceiling which delivered air in swirl­air distribution pattern is achieved, at ACH of 20 h−1
or 18 h−1 and with 4, 6, or 8 exhausts placed on the walls. An ACH of 18 h−1, needed
6 exhausts to achieve the highest contamination removal efficiency of 38 % higher than
with 8 exhausts. The study shows that it is possible to achieve good contamination re­
moval with a lowered ACH, but what are the energy cost benefits of this? In both studies,
[30] [29] no target air cleanliness is stated, therefore it is unsure if the conclusions in the
studies fulfill the requirements of a high­risk operation.
An investigation on airflow and contamination distribution in operating theatres measured
the quality of an OT with mixing airflow created by 15 supply units placed in the center of
the ceiling, during an unoccupied period (at­rest) [31]. The results showed that the OT was
within ISO class 7 with an ACH of 22 h−1, based on airflow rate, pressurization, particle
counts, temperature, and relative humidity measurements. The result of the measurement
was used to model a CFD simulation and thereby ensuring that the model is representable
of the measured OT and in accordance with the desired design. The CFD then looked into 3
different stages, at­rest, normal operating conditions, and actual condition with personnel
inside and some medical equipment blocking the return air. The CFD model evaluated
the ventilation effectiveness of air change rates at 15 h−1, 22 h−1, and 29 h−1. The results
showed that the ventilation effectiveness was the highest at the highest ACH. By using an
ACH of 29 h−1 the use of energy is increased, hence the study recommends using an ACH
Optimization of Ventilation Principle And Use Of Energy In Operating Theatres
9
==PAGE:25==
of 15 h−1 with at­rest conditions to save energy.
Both laminar and turbulent airflow is used in OTs, and a study has investigated which of
the two is the most efficient when evaluating CFU count [18]. This was done in 17 real
OTs, 12 with LAF and 5 with TAF, with an ACH median of 87 h−1 and 16 h−1 respectively,
during 51 live surgeries. All OTs fulfilled ISO class 5 by not exceeding 10 CFU/m3, by
measuring active and passive air sampling of the cfu count. Results showed that the LAF
in all cases provided ultraclean air, whereas 9 out of 16 procedures with TAF exceeded the
CFU threshold. The effectiveness of LAF and TAF was studied in many different OTs,
indicating that this has not been compared in a full­scale model with the same physical
conditions, e.g the impact of different exhaust locations has not been compared.
The laminar airflow was investigated in an experimental study conducted in a full­scale
room [32]. The room was set­up as an OT with downward laminar airflow created from
a UCV (ultra­clean ventilation) system module in the ceiling to investigate the placement
of exhausts in the ceiling, the length of the skirt, and percent ceiling­return­to­supply flow
rate to prevent recirculation of the air in the room. Two cases with different ACH were
investigated with ACH of 56 h−1 and 88 h−1, based on recommendations from the Hospital

There is a missing investigation on the energy use of highly demanding ventilation in
operating theatres in combination with an improved ventilation system with a focus on

studies are based on measurements in real OT and CFD simulation, hence there is a need

1.5
Objectives
The current project developed new or modifications to existing ventilation principles in
operating theatres to deliver the required hygiene at lower energy use. The research was
based on numerical modeling, energy simulation, and experimental work. Full­scale ex­
periments investigated different types of supply diffusers providing a laminar airflow,
placement of exhaust, and the use of a heated blanket. The numerical modeling was used
to further investigate the air distribution in the operating theater with different air change
rates, turbulent airflow with different supply diffusers, laminar airflow, and placement of
exhausts. Energy performance simulation was used to assess the energy demand of the
different ventilation methods. The project outlines recommendations for ventilation de­
signs that will provide lower energy use of operating theatres and be more sustainable.
The results validate the robustness of the standard methods to ventilate operating theatres
and what it means for the infection risk.

2.1.3
Converging
The program solves the algebraic equations, using iterative procedures to reach a con­
verged solution after a finite number of successive iterations. Convergence is achieved
when the solution no longer changes significantly with further iterations. It indicates that
the numerical solution has reached a steady state or a stable solution. The heat and mass
balance was considered, and the residuals of the Navier­Stokes equation. The solver takes
changes in the following monitor variable into consideration; pressure, air velocity, air
temperature, bacteria concentration, the kinetic energy of turbulence, dissipation of tur­
bulence, turbulence viscosity field, and density field.
The monitor points were used to determine if the model reached a steady state. When the
variation in the variables was small or changing by a constant amount, then the solution
was considered effectively converged. In order to use monitor point convergence as a
decisive factor, the monitor points should be placed in all locations of the model where
the values are required to converge. Hence, the monitor points were placed above the
patient, and around the occupants.

4
Discussion
Constructing hospitals and operating theatres can be expensive and use plenty of space.
The space should be designed and optimized to hold all necessary needs and functions set
by standards and requirements. The ventilation system is key when designing an operating
theatre, but many different ventilation designs and principles can be constructed. Based
on the result of the study some recommendations on the ventilation design of an operating
theatre can be discussed.
4.1
Lowered air change rate
One parameter investigated in the study was the possibility to lower the air change rate
in the operating theatre, hence lowering the energy use of the ventilation system, and
therefore being more sustainable.
Lowering the air change rate from 20 h−1 to 15 h−1, and 10 h−1 showed changes in the
energy use, ventilation effectiveness, and the air speed measured in the critical zone above
the patient. Changes in the ACH were investigated in the CFD simulations and the energy
simulation. By lowering the air change rate from 20 h−1 to 10 h−1 the air movement
from the different diffusers (swirl, lobbed, and laminar) showed variations in the air speed
patterns. With the lowered ACH of 10 h−1, the air pattern had a tendency to be disturbed by
heated objects, which changed the air jets direction from the diffuser, compared to an ACH
of 20 h−1. This is because the magnitude of the air jets were lowered and can therefore
easier be interrupted by heat sources. Though, with the swirl diffuser in combination with
the low­wall exhausts, this created air jets pointing downward to the critical zone, and
increasing the ventilation effectiveness in this area. Compared to the ceiling exhausts, the
air movements from 20 h−1 to 15 h−1 and 10 h−1 showed the same air pattern, though
the ventilation effectiveness was increased when lowering the ACH. The same tendency
was also discovered in the lobbed jet diffuser with both the ceiling exhausts and low­wall
exhausts.
These observations provides the following recommendations to when designing the ven­
tilation system. Choosing a turbulent airflow it is suggested to perform CFD simulations
to analyze the air movement on the desired air change rate, because the air movement and
hence the resulting ventilation effectiveness can be difficult to predict when lowering the
air change rates, without the CFD simulations.
70
Optimization of Ventilation Principle And Use Of Energy In Operating Theatres
==PAGE:86==
The laminar diffuser results from the CFD simulation showed that the ventilation effective­
ness in the critical zone was lowered when the air change rate was lowered. The laminar
diffuser had one air stream pointing directly down into the critical zone, and when lower­
ing the ACH, the air stream pattern had the same air pattern direction. These observations
on the laminar diffuser make it possible to predict air movement depending on a differing
air change rate.
The energy performance simulations showed lowered energy use per year when lowering
the air change rate. Results showed a linear decrease in the energy use with the lowered
air change rates. When lowering the ACH from 20 h−1 to 15 h−1, the yearly energy use
was lowered by 55%, and from 20 h−1 to 10 h−1, it was lowered by 111%, in an operating
theatre. The linear decrease in energy consumption, was due to the minimized possibilities
to modify the ventilation system in the energy simulation. Hence, the results showed a big
impact on the energy used in an operating theatre, by lowering the air change rate, in which
this should be a factor to consider when designing the ventilation system.
The schedule on the occupant and the equipment in the energy performance simulation
was investigated, because it might not represent reality. Therefore, the it waschanged
from being always present to present from hours 6 ­ 18 all days, which showed that the
energy use for the ventilation system increased. The increased energy use is more realistic,
as an operating theatre at a hospital would not be in use all the time. Hence, the schedule
applied in the simulation might also not be realistic, as the operating theatre is available at
all times. The schedule was set to be present from hours 6 ­ 18 every day, meaning it was
turned off at night time. The possibility that the OT is in use at nighttime is present, but it
might also not be occupied 100% from 6 ­ 18. Though, a schedule that would represent a
fully realistic operating theatre use is not possible to create.
When designing the ventilation system on the use of energy it is sufficient to take the use of
the operating theatre into consideration, as the results showed increased energy use when
the use of the OT was changed.
In the full­scale experiments the air change rates were set to 10 h−1, and results showed
that the thermal manikin, simulated as the patient had a heat flux of 118 W/m2. This heat
flux was high compared to a resting person without clothing. As the lowered air change
rate provided unrealistic conditions for the patient, a heated blanket for the patient was
investigated, which showed that the heat flux was lowered to 54 W/m2. When lowering
the air change rate in the operating theatre, the patient still need a heat source to prevent
hypothermia, which should be taken into consideration when designing operating theatres.

4.2
Placement of the exhausts
Another parameter investigated in the study was the placement of the exhausts, where
ceiling and low­wall exhausts were used. The placement of the exhausts can have an in­
fluence on multiple factors, such as the ventilation effectiveness and air movement, which
were investigated, through CFD simulations and full­scale experiments.
In the CFD simulation the placement of exhausts were studied on all three diffusers (swirl,
lobbed, and laminar). The results showed varied results in which the placement of exhausts
provided the best conditions in the operating theatre. Choosing a turbulent airflow, the type
of supply diffuser is dependent on obtaining the optimal placement of exhausts. As, the
swirl diffuser, provides the best conditions with ceiling exhausts, the lobbed jet diffuser
provides the best conditions with low­wall exhausts. Thus the air temperatures with the
low­wall exhausts and the lobbed jet diffuser were too low, but this could easily be adjusted
by raising the supply air temperature. Therefore, the type of supply diffuser should be
investigated through CFD simulations before choosing the placement of the exhausts in
the OT.
In the full­scale experiments the placement of the exhausts were testes with the semi­
laminar diffuser. Which showed improved conditions when the exhausts were placed at
a low­wall level, as it provided the higher ventilation effectivenesses in the critical zone
above the patient, but the ceiling exhausts gave higher ventilation effectiveness in the
room, thus this area is less important.
When choosing to have low­wall exhausts in operating theatres, the ventilation system
needs space around the walls for installation. When installing ceiling exhausts, the extra
space around the walls is not needed, as the ventilation system is normally placed in the
ceiling. The extra space for the wall exhausts costs non­usable floor area and more money
which can be an important factor. Though, the low­wall exhausts have been shown to
provide better ventilation design which can provide a lower risk of surgical site infections.
And as SSI cost money due to the patient’s stay is longer at the hospital, therefore investing
in a ventilation design with low­wall exhausts are beneficial.
4.3
Type of airflow
The type of airflow in the operating theatre can provide different environmental condi­
tions, hence the airflow should provide an environment that complements the require­
ments and criteria of the operating theatre. The turbulent airflow was investigated in the
CFD simulation and the laminar airflow was investigated in the CFD simulation and the
72
Optimization of Ventilation Principle And Use Of Energy In Operating Theatres
==PAGE:88==
full­scale experiments.
The comparison of the CFD simulation and the full­scale experiments showed differences
in the results. The airflow in the CFD simulation was fully laminar, and the semi­laminar
supply diffuser in the full­scale experiment did not provide fully laminar airflow, it is
therefore expected that the results differ. The comparison of the results from the CFD
simulation and full­scale results is therefore in question due to the difference in the airflow.
Hence, it was not possible to install a fully laminar diffuser in the climate chamber, though
it was intended to simulate a laminar airflow in the climate chamber with the semi­laminar
diffuser.
As the semi­laminar diffuser in the full­scale experiment did not provide fully laminar
airflow, it would be proper to investigate a fully laminar supply diffuser in a full­scale
experiment in order to make a precise comparison of the CFD simulation and the full­
scale experiments.
The difference in results from the CFD simulation and full­scale experiments makes it
difficult to argue on the use of primarily CFD simulations. Full­scale experiments pro­
vides the most accurate results, but this method is time consuming and cost money for
equipment and facilities. The CFD simulation model in this study did show limitations.
When using CFD simulation it is important to chose a software that provides the necessary
modifications, hence validation of the model by using grid independence tests, monitor­
ing the results on defined convergence criteria, uncertainty, and a sensitivity analysis are
important factors to obtain results that can imitate real case results.
4.4
Contamination simulation
In the CFD simulation, the contamination source was placed on all three staff members,
whereas in the full­scale experiment, only two of the members produced contamination.
In the full­scale experiment, it was not possible to have all three staff members produce
contamination, hence the CFD contamination simulation is the most realistic, as all three
staff members produced bacteria. In a real operating theatre, all staff members would
generate contamination, though the contamination production can differ from person to
person.
The contamination simulation in the CFD resulted in a high number (>8000) of particles in
all cases except case 13, as shown in appendix Appendix C Table C.4 and Table C.5. The
high number of particles is unrealistic, as an air change rate of 20 h1 should be sufficient

4.5
Limitations
The conducted study has raised some interesting knowledge, thus the study does contain
some limitations.
• The software Flovent could only operate in squared geometries, which made the
CFD simulation a simplification of a real case. The squared geometry can give
incorrect results on airspeed, as harsh lines and edges can interrupt the airflow in an
unrealistic way. In a realistic case of an operating room, the surgical staff would be
in a bending position. Hence, this was not possible to model in the CFD simulation.
• An uncertainty and sensitivity analysis should have been carried out on the CFD
simulation, as results showed unrealistic results on the bacteria concentrations.
• The standing dummies that simulated the surgical staff in the full­scale experiment
may not fully represent a realistic person, regarding movement, surface area, and
surface temperature. Also, the posture of the thermal manikin simulated by the
patient may not be realistic as the legs had an angle at which the feet could not be
placed on the table. However, the dummies and manikin provided ergonomic and
thermal obstacles for observing and analyzing the formation of thermal plumes.
• Generally, the setup in the climate chamber for the full­scale experiment was a sim­
plification of a real operating theatre. HEPA filters were not installed in the venti­
lation system, pressurization was not obtained, and surfaces were not easy to clean
with disinfection.

• Contamination simulation in the full­scale experiment is limited to the use of tracer
gas. Therefore, a specific particle count could not be obtained in the experiment,

• The energy simulation did not vary on the ventilation principle regarding supply
diffuser type, LAF/TAF, and placement of exhaust, as the software did not provide

4.6
Further research
Based on findings from this study it would be interesting and relevant for further research.
The laminar and semi­laminar airflow from the CFD simulation and full­scale experiments
showed improved conditions in the operating theatre, hence the lowered air change rate
did show a decrease of ventilation effectiveness in the critical zone. By lowering the
supply diffuser closer to the patient, the ventilation effectiveness could increase, hence
this configuration should be investigated. When lowering the supply diffuser, it might
also be possible to lower the air change rate further and save more energy.

5
Conclusion
Air quality in an operating theatre is critical to prevent the formation of surgical site infec­
tions in patients undergoing surgeries such as alloplastic implant. The ventilation system
in the operating theatre is therefore a significant factor, to prevent this.
This study investigated the possibility to change the design of ventilation principles and
lower the energy use in operating theatres hence still provide a clean environment fulfilling
the requirements. The air change rate, placement of exhausts, type of diffuser, and airflow
type was investigated through the use of CFD simulations, full­scale experiments, and
energy performance simulations.
By the use of CFD simulation and full­scale experiments, results showed that a laminar/semi­
laminar airflow would provide a high ventilation effectiveness in the critical zone above
the patient, and hence minimize the risk of surgical site infections. In combination with
a laminar supply diffuser, the exhausts should be placed at a low­wall level, at opposite
walls, to remove contamination. The low­wall exhausts provides the best conditions in
combination with a laminar airflow.
The use of laminar airflow in combination with a lowered air change rate of 10 h−1, com­
pared to the recommended 20 h−1 set by standard ASHRAE 170 [1], would provide a
sufficient contamination removal evaluated on the ventilation effectiveness and use less
energy. By lowering the air change rate from 20 h−1 to 10 h−1, the energy consump­
tion can be reduced by 111 %, based on the yearly energy consumption of the ventilation
system in an operating theatre.
If using a turbulent airflow in the operating theatre, the type of supply diffuser affects the
optimal placement of the exhausts. The pattern of the air movement has an influence on
the contamination removal, and hence the placement of the exhausts should be placed to
complement the air movement.
The findings of this study will help provide new recommendations on the ventilation prin­
ciple in operating theatres, hence lowering energy use and being more sustainable. There­
fore it is suggested to revise the ventilation principle recommendations set by the stan­
dards, hence further investigations might be needed.
