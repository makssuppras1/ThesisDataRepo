Abstract
Bristlebots are a type of vibration-driven robot propelled by uneven frictional conditions
caused by being supported by slightly inclined bristles. While it is possible to create com-
plex bristlebots that are controllable, a simple bristlebot is typically considered to behave
unpredictably when allowed free movement in all directions. In this thesis, the behaviour
of a bristlebot allowed free movement while subjected to base excitation through a time-
varying supporting surface in the form of a transversally vibrating plate is analysed exper-
imentally. Additionally, the behaviour of the bristlebot is analysed experimentally when
constrained to uni-directional movement along a transversally vibrating beam. From the
experiments, two models, and an analytical solution to one, are proposed to explain the
movements and behaviour and to analyse general bristlebot performance. The analytical
solution is inaccurate for most cases but may be used in parameter studies. Further-
more, if the models are numerically simulated, they are deemed qualitatively functional
in describing the movements.

Chapter 1
Introduction
A bristlebot is a type of vibration-driven robot, a simple example of which is seen in
Figure 1.1. They are driven to horizontal movement by being excited vertically. This
thesis aims to further the understanding of bristlebot movement, and how such a robot
responds to vibrations of the surface they are placed upon, called base excitation, including
how it responds to time-varying geometrical changes in the surface.
Bristlebots, also called hexbugs, are simple, robust, and inexpensive to manufacture. The
simplest bristlebots can be made by taping a vibration motor to the head of a toothbrush,
though more complex variations are used in specific use cases. These include urban search-
and-rescue [1], and inspection technology for narrow, snaking environments [2], for which
the bristlebot has the form of a long snake with bristles on all sides and a camera in
the tip. Bristlebots are also used in research of robotic swarm behaviour [3]. A greater
understanding of the movements of bristlebots may lead to even wider use cases, and to
the ability to control the robot in a plane with only a single motor.
Vibration-driven locomotion is a well-known field of study and has been for many years,
as exemplified by [4], which concerns vibrational conveyors, also called vibro-transport.
Figure 1.1: A simple example of a bristlebot made from the head of a toothbrush and a
vibration motor.

In most cases of vibro-transport, the objects are transported on a vibrating plate using
an alternating stick-slip movement. This pushes the object forward in the stick phase,
it then uses the trend that static friction is higher than dynamic friction to retract the
surface below the objects in the slip phase. Repeating these phases allows objects to be
transported by longitudinal vibrations in a plate. In some cases, transverse vibrations,
anisotropic frictional surfaces, or both, are used to improve the performance of these
transport methods. Bristlebots, and some other vibro-crawlers, however, only require
transverse vibrations to be excited into longitudinal motion. As transverse vibrations are
significantly easier to excite, this provides some advantages. Bristlebots are a relatively
new addition to the field of vibration-driven locomotion, as they were, to the authorвҖҷs
knowledge, first proposed 24 years ago in [5]. More recently studies have been made to
further study their capabilities, [2, 3, 6, 7]. Multiple methods of controlling bristlebots
have been developed; in [8] the robot is controlled by the bot having multiple sets of
bristles with different properties which may be excited differently by the same frequency.
In [9] the robot is controlled by having rotatable bristles.
[6] proposes a method to
control the forward and backward movement of a bristlebot in one direction by varying
the excitation frequency, with the excitation coming from the bot itself. [7] builds on
the same idea and validates it experimentally, with the difference that the excitation here
comes from a vibrating plate to make controlling the frequency easier. [10] proposes how
the shape of the supporting surface might change the interaction with the bristles.
To contribute to the understanding of the movements of a bristlebot the analysis is split
into three distinct goals.
The first of which is to conduct experiments to determine
patterns in the movements of a bristlebot. The experiments will be made on a vertically
oscillating flexible plate. Second an analytical model for the position of the robot in one
direction when excited by a rigid vertically vibrating plate is made. This will be done by
determining the equations of motion and finding an analytical solution using perturbation
theory. This will be very similar to the results in [7] as the system is the same, except
for the desired model describing the position of the robot and not just the velocity. A
numerical solution will also be found, and the analytical and numerical results compared.
Third, a numerical model of the position of the robot on a vertically oscillating flexible
beam will be found, by determining the equations of motion and using a numerical solver
for ordinary first order differential equations.
The analyses will contribute to understanding how a bristlebot will move excited by a time-
varying supporting surface. Especially interesting will be what impact the deformation
angle of the supporting surface has on the locomotion and orientation of the robot; and
if it will lead to the possibility of a form of open-loop control of the system, using stable
solutions that may change when the plate is excited in new mode shapes.
The thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 describes and discusses the experiments.
Chapters 3 to 4 concern the analytical and numerical models of a bristlebot excited by

2.3
Summary of experimental results
Experiment 1 shows that if placed freely on a vibrating plate, the bristlebot will move
away from anti-nodes and toward a nodal line with which it will align.
Experiment 2 shows that, if constrained to moving along a line, the bristlebot will move
toward an anti-node. If it manages to cross the anti-node, it will move on toward the
following node and stop there, likely due to a lack of excitation. When moving toward an
anti-node, the bristlebot will attempt to rotate away from it, but strings constrain it to
either side.
The behaviour observed on the plate and in the beam approximation suggests that the
bristlebot will be propelled by the excitation in a direction away from that in which the
bristles are bent, as is expected of a bristlebot.
If the bristlebot is allowed to rotate
while moving, it will move away from excitation peaks and toward the nodal lines of the
supporting structure. If the bristlebot is not allowed to rotate, the bot will move toward
the anti-nodes while attempting to rotate until it reaches a form of equilibrium or it
overcomes the peak and continues onward.
Two models are proposed to attempt to explain the behaviour witnessed in the experi-
ments. The first seeks to explain the behaviour of a bristlebot under basis excitation from
a rigid plate; this is to gain an understanding of how physical parameters may change the
system. The second seeks to explain the bristlebots behaviour when excited by a flexible
beam, as is approximated in the second experiment.

Defining the system
A bristlebot is propelled by the shear force created through the vertical movement of the
robot or surface, the horizontal movement, or both. There are several ways to model the
shear force. In [12], a model, T = вҲ’ВөN Лҷu(t), for the shear force, which is proportional to

acting on the tip of the bristles.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
6.1
Summary and discussion of results
From the first model (3.5), the system can be seen to be Taylor-expandable for small
but finite |Оё| if the static loading Оі is low. This requirement is weaker at higher values
of the bristle angle Оұ. The multiple scales solution to near-resonant excitation is seen
to be inaccurate for most combinations of the system parameters. The analytical sys-
tem can, however, be used to gain a qualitative understanding of how the dimensionless
parameters change the characteristics of the system. The changes are of the same type,
if not magnitude, as in the numerically simulated system. From the examination of the
effects of high-frequency excitation of the numerical system, the excitation amplitude О·
is seen to increase the movement velocity of the system quadratically. In contrast, the
non-linear damping parameter О» increases it linearly. The velocity is also seen to increase
quadratically with the excitation frequency Пү.
The second model, (4.11), describes a system excited by a beam excited into a mode
shape. From this, it is seen that it will gravitate toward nodal points when excited at
a frequency below the resonance frequency of the bristlebot. If, on the other hand, it is
excited above its resonance frequency, it will gravitate toward anti-nodes. A change in
the phase of the bristle movement may be the cause of this behaviour.
The experiments show that a freely moving bristlebot on a plate that vibrates in a mode
shape, as seen in Figure 2.3, will move away from anti-nodes. This behaviour corresponds
to the behaviour of the second model when subjected to excitation below the resonance
frequency of the bristlebot.
The movement on a beam, seen in Figure 2.4, is almost
stationary for tests 1 and 3, and test 2 exhibits a pause before continuing its movement
across an anti-node. The second model may explain this if the excitation frequency is
higher than the resonance frequency of the bristlebot. The distance from the anti-node can
be seen to increase with the excitation frequency and, thus, the incited velocity. However,
both explanations of the behaviour cannot be true simultaneously. The first explanation
requires that the excitation frequency is below resonance, and the other is above.
43
==PAGE:52==
44
6.2. Conclusion
The most likely option is that the plate is excited at a frequency above the resonance
frequency of the bristlebot, as the bristle stiffness is likely relatively low. This explains
that the bristlebot moves toward the anti-nodes while attempting to rotate away from
them in the beam approximation. As the model cannot rotate, it can only move toward
the anti-node.
It should also be noted that the bristles were often not in contact with the supporting
surface during the experiments.
As constant surface contact is an assumption in the
models, it may lead to issues at larger excitation amplitudes as the lack of contact will
be more pronounced.
6.2
Conclusion
The analytical model is not suited to describing the system quantitatively. It may, how-
ever, be used qualitatively to study how changing parameters in the system might change
its behaviour. The static loading, proportional to gravity and the mass of the bristle-
bot, and the bristle angle offset are of particular importance in the performance of the
system. Both parameters change the system significantly. The numerical model is likely
more reliable and quantitatively more accurate, though this claim requires quantitative
experiments to verify.
The model describing the bristlebotвҖҷs movement on a flexible beam yields results that fit
the experimental results found in Section 2.2. The model predicts that if a beam is excited
into a mode shape at a frequency above the resonance frequency of the bristlebot, the
bristlebot will move toward anti-nodes. Experimentally the bristlebot may overcome the
anti-node. It will then move toward a node and halt due to the low excitation amplitude.
If the bristlebot is allowed free movement on a plate, it will rotate away from the anti-
nodes and move toward a nodal line with which it will align.
6.3
Further work
A number of avenues for further work are available.
The frictional modal used in this analysis and in [7] seems to yield results that qualitatively
concur with experimental results. It may be beneficial to also create a model of the system
using different friction models, such as Coulomb friction, and determine differences.
It may also be interesting to see if the models are functional quantitatively as well as
qualitatively, to do this more experiments would be required.
Creating a model for the bristlebot movement when on a flexible plate is relevant for
further understanding of bristlebot movement, and may allow a form of open loop control
of the movements.
