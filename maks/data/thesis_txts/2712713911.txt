Abstract
Entity linking (EL) is a subfield of Natural Language Processing that enables machines to
provide a unique identification of real-world entities (e.g. person and objects) mentioned
in a text. Consequently, EL can be used to automatically process and link articles, press
releases, and other written materials with meaningful contextual information. Because of
such capabilities, the field of EL has increasingly gained interest over the years not only
within research but also in the industry.
Due to a common interest in the applicative aspects of EL today, this thesis is the product
of a collaboration between DTU and Infomedia. For this reason, its aim is two-fold. From a
scientific perspective, we seek to investigate, evaluate, and communicate central methods
within EL, while from the perspective of Infomedia, the thesis serves as a study that
provides insight into the potential of using established English EL methods to eventually
create a Danish EL model for commercial use.
In this thesis we have chosen to present a reproduction of the EL model, Wikipedia2vec for
EL, proposed by Yamada et al.[Yam+16] that in 2016 achieved state of the art performance
on the AIDA-CoNLL data set. The purpose of this is to test today’s predictive capabilities
of the model by training it using the latest Wikipedia data.
Moreover, we choose to
study the effect of using other embeddings methods than the one original used for the
Wikipedia2vec for EL model, and thus train the EL model with embeddings obtained
using Explicit Semantic Analysis, a pre-trained BERT model, and a fine-tuned SBERT
model. To facilitate comparison with today’s SOTA performing model (LUKE for EL)
[Yam+22] we reproduce the LUKE for EL model result on the AIDA-CoNLL data set and
train all other considered models in the thesis using the same data set.
Based on our results, we do not obtain any performance increase with the use of the
alternative embedding approaches. However, we find a significant performance increase
for the Wikipedia2vec for EL model trained on the latest Wikipedia compared to the
performance stated in 2016.
As a result, the Wikipedia2vec for EL model is in fact
among top performing models on the AIDA-CoNLL data set – although, still the LUKE
for EL performs significantly better. Due to the high performance results and the ease
of implementation of the Wikipedia2vec for EL model, we recommend that Infomedia
initially develop a Danish version of the Wikipedia2vec for EL model.

Resumé
Entity linking (EL) er et underområde af Natural Language Processing, der gør det muligt
for maskiner at på unikt vis at identificere virkelige entiteter (f.eks. personer og objekter),
der er nævnt i en tekst. Som følge heraf kan EL bruges til automatisk at processere og
linke artikler, pressemeddelelser og andet skriftligt materiale med meningsfuld kontekstuel
information. På grund af sådanne kapaciteter har EL-området i stigende grad vundet
interesse gennem årene, ikke kun inden for forskning, men også indenfor industrien.
Grundet en fælles interesse for de anvendelige aspekter af EL i dag, er dette speciale
et produkt af et samarbejde mellem DTU og Infomedia.
Af denne årsag er formålet
med specialet todelt. Fra et videnskabeligt perspektiv søger vi at undersøge, evaluere
og formidle centrale metoder indenfor EL, mens specialet set fra Infomedias perspektiv
fungerer som et studie, der giver indsigt i potentialet i at bruge etablerede engelske EL
metoder til på sigt at skabe en dansk EL model til kommerciel brug.
I denne afhandling har vi valgt at præsentere en reproduktion af EL-modellen, Wikipedia2-
vec for EL, foreslået af Yamada et al.[Yam+16], der i 2016 opnåede state of the art resul-
tater på AIDA-CoNLL-datasættet. Formålet med dette er at teste modellens prædiktive
evner i dag ved at træne den ved brug af nyeste Wikipedia-data. Desuden undersøger vi
effekten af at bruge andre embedding-metoder end den originale brugt til Wikipedia2vec
for EL-modellen, og træner derfor EL-modellen med embeddings opnået ud fra Explicit
Semantic Analysis, en præ-trænet BERT-model og en finjusteret SBERT model. For at
facilitere en sammenligningen med en af nutidens bedste EL modeller (LUKE for EL)
[Yam+22] reproducerer vi resultatet fra denne på AIDA-CoNLL-datasæt og træner alle
andre betragtede modeller for dette speciale på samme datasæt.
Ud fra vores resultater, opnås ingen præstationsforøgelse ved brug af de alternative embedding-
metoder. Tilgen-gæld, ser vi en betydelig forøgelse af ydeevne for Wikipedia2vec for EL
modellen trænet på det seneste Wikipedia sammenlignet med ydeevnen angivet i 2016.
Som resultat heraf er Wikipedia2vec for EL-modellen faktisk blandt de bedste modeller
tested på AIDA-CoNLL-datasættet – selvom LUKE for EL stadig klarer sig signifikant
bedre. På grund af den høje præstation og nemme implementering af Wikipedia2vec for
EL-modellen, anbefaler vi, at Infomedia i første omgang udvikler en dansk version af
Wikipedia2vec for EL modellen.

1
Introduction
Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a field of Artificial Intelligence (AI) that aims to
make human language intelligible to machines. Combining the power of linguistics and
computer science, NLP is used to study the structure of language and create intelligent
systems that are capable of understanding, analyzing, and extracting meaning from text
and speech. Such systems have proven to have a successful societal impact and constitute
the core of tools that many people use daily; from translation software, chatbots, spam
filters, and search engines to grammar correction software, voice assistants, and social
media monitoring tools. As an example, this thesis is written with the use of an AI tool
for correcting grammar – hopefully with increased readability as a result.
Intelligent systems that can perform the above-mentioned tasks are based on machine
learning (ML) algorithms that enable an automatic way to model language. Consequently,
the systems do not need to be explicitly programmed to deal with rules of language such
as syntax (the arrangement of words) and semantics (the meaning of words). A subfield of
algorithms that has proven to be particularly useful for modeling such structures in text
is deep learning.
The neural network architecture, which forms the basis of deep learning, date back to the
1940s [MP43]. Nevertheless, it is first during the 2010s that the field of deep learning
has experienced a renaissance, which has led to sizable strides towards complex language
models being taken. The reason for this is mainly due to three linked factors, the first of
which is of the greatest importance for this thesis:
1. Innovation of model architectures
2. Increased amount of data
3. More compute power
Since 2013, neural networks have proven particularly effective in learning enhanced text
representations, also called embeddings [[Mik+13a],[PSM14]]. In conjunction with deep
learning models these embeddings have been widely applied within NLP and paving the
way for new state of the art (SOTA) performance within various NLP tasks. However, with
the advent of the neural network model architecture Transformer [Vas+17] in 2017 a new
era of NLP began. The Transformer architecture allowed for an effective way of modeling
long-range dependencies among words, offering enhanced parallelization, and moreover,
turned out to be effective for transfer learning.
These characteristics have proven to
be highly effective when trying to solve various NLP tasks, and many variations of the
transformer architecture have since been successfully applied for models such as BERT
[Dev+18], RoBERTa [Liu+19], XLNet [Yan+19b], T5 [Raf+19], and GPT-3 [Bro+20].
Furthermore, as larger models have appeared to capture more and more complex language
patterns, these models have over the years grown exponentially, and in April this year the
model PaLM [Cho+22] by Google became the largest transformer model so far with 540
billion parameters.

While larger and larger models seem to continue to get closer to human performance within
certain NLP tasks [Cho+22] their need for large-scale resources still hinders them from
being generally applied in academia and industry. As a consequence, many practitioners
still have to rely on simpler NLP models.
Fortunately, such models have been shown
to be effective within many domains, especially when influenced by explicit modeling of
knowledge [Yam+20]. A particular example of this is the domain of interest for this thesis;
entity linking.
1.1
Entity linking
A major diﬀiculty in understanding natural language is that words and expressions, es-
pecially names, can be interpreted in multiple ways, a quality termed ambiguity. When
trying to understand the meaning of a word or a phrase, we humans therefore often have
to consider the whole context in which it is used. Take, for instance, the sentence:
”Michael returned to Chicago in March 1995.”
To actually capture the meaning of this sentence and to whom Michael and Chicago refer
requires reasoning at a deeper contextual level than merely at the level of words. One must
have acquired a certain basketball knowledge prior to be able to identify that Michael in
this case refers to the famous basketball player Michael Jordan, while Chicago refers to the
basketball team Chicago Bulls. Being able to identify what or whom a specific instance in
a text refers to is within NLP called entity linking (EL) or named entity disambiguation
(NED).
More formally, entity linking can be described as the task of assigning a unique identity to
entities mentioned in a text. In this context, an entity is referring to words of interest as,
for example, companies, locations, or famous individuals such as Michael Jordan in the
above example. The unique identification of entities is accomplished using a knowledge
base (KB), containing several unique entities, which serves as a register, that can be used
to match an entity appearing in a text with its KB counterpart. Within the field of EL, a
widely used KB is the online encyclopedia Wikipedia, which basic entry is an article that
defines or describes an entity or a topic. Each article is uniquely identified by its title, and
therefore linking entities in a given document with their corresponding Wikipedia articles
allows for a unique distinction between different entities. The following example shows
how Wikipedia can be applied to link the entities in the above given text example.
1.2
Motivation
The significance of EL lies in its importance as a preprocessing step for many downstream
NLP tasks, such as relation extraction [Lin+16], knowledge base population [JG11], con-
tent analysis [MM10], and information retrieval [BML13]. As a result, EL can be used
to automatically process and link documents, reports, press releases, and other written
materials with meaningful contextual information. Due to such capabilities, the field of

EL has increasingly gained interest not only within research, but also in the industry, for
example, by the Danish company Infomedia.
Infomedia is a media intelligence company that, among other things, collects and monitors
news material from various textual media with the aim of providing deep insight through
advanced analytics.
As part of facilitating such services, Infomedia already applies a
broad palette of SOTA NLP tools and is, for instance, able to identify entities in texts
using named entity recognition (NER) with high precision. A natural extension of these
capabilities is to apply entity linking to improve understanding of entities. Due to common

1.3
State-of-the-art
In recent years, several deep learning-based EL methods have been proposed with the use
of various types of networks ([Sil+17], [Sun+15], [Fan+19]). However, with the advent
of transformers, the ability to fine-tune large pre-trained language models on specific EL
tasks has brought even more promising results [Yin+19], and today many researchers
take advantage of BERT and BERT-type models. For example, [Bro19] has trained an
end-to-end entity linking system based on BERT, [Fév+20] addressed EL using BERT
by classifying mentions spans to correct corresponding entities, and [Cao+20] addressed
EL by training BART [Lew+19] to generate referent entity titles of target mentions in an
autoregressive manner.
Recently, [Yam+22] proposed a new SOTA model for entity linking, based on the LUKE
model [Yam+20], which in 2020 presented a novel way of modeling textual context. The
architecture of LUKE is based on BERT, but it differs, as it is designed to be specifically
useful for entity-related tasks by treating not only words but also entities as independent
inputs. As a result of using this design, along with a method that sequentially resolves
entities, [Yam+22] has succeeded in obtaining SOTA performance on multiple EL data
sets.

textual embeddings are obtained and applied [She+21]. As mentioned in the previous

many other types of model within EL have shown great results. This includes the model
”Wikipedia2vec for EL”1 that pushed SOTA in 2016 using embeddings obtained from a
skipgram-type model based on a Wikipedia corpus (articles) and a supervised ranking
model [Yam+16]. Naturally, this raises the question: Is the Wikipedia2vec for EL model

2.1
Entity Linking
The entity linking (EL) task was briefly introduced in the introduction chapter. In this
section, we want to expand the understanding of the task and provide a more comprehen-
sive overview of key EL approaches and concepts. We will, among others, get acquainted
with terms such as entity mentions, entity popularity, and coherence, which will be relevant
to understand prior to the models presented in the following sections of this chapter.
As stated in section 1.1, the task of entity linking is to assign a unique identity to entities
mentioned in a text, called entity mentions. These entity mentions could, for example, be
organizations or individuals that are of special interest. The identification is carried out
using a knowledge base (KB) that contains several unique entities, and the task is then
to find matches between entity mentions in a text and their KB entity counterpart. To
illustrate the task, a small EL example is depicted in figure 2.1.

In general, the EL task is carried out in three steps:
1. Mention detection/entity recognition: Identify text spans of potential entity
mentions in the input text.
2. Candidate generation: For each entity mentioned, retrieve potential entity can-
didates from the KB.
3. Entity linking/disambiguation: Among the candidate entities, uniquely identify
the correct candidate associated with the respective entity mentioned in the input
text.
EL can be viewed as an end-to-end process or pipeline that involves all three steps. How-
ever, each step is considered mainly separately, and many EL models presuppose that the
preceding entity recognition is carried out in advance [SWH15]. A reason for this rationale
is that much research is being done within the NLP subfield of named entity recognition
(NER), and sophisticated models with high precision for entity recognition already exist
today [Pap22]. This entails that the EL task can be formally stated as follows.
Definition (Entity Linking): Given a document D containing a set of
recognized entity mentions M = {m1, m2, ...m|M|} and a target KB containing
a set of named entities E = {e1, e2, ..., e|E|}, the goal is to map each entity
mention mi ∈M to its corresponding entity ei ∈E in the KB.
2.1.1
Knowledge Base
A KB is a fundamental component for any EL system.
It encompasses central prior
knowledge for the linking task and, furthermore, constitutes a bound on the achievable
entity links that can be obtained. Depending on the intended application, the structure
of a KB might differ, however, in general the purpose of any KB is to provide various key
information about entities and their relations.
It is natural to believe that the ideal KB would contain any entity existing in the world.
However, such a vast KB would be almost impossible to obtain, and even if one could, EL

would nonetheless still be challenging. This is in particular due to the many possible name
variations, also called surface forms, that an entity mention can have. For instance, the
city of New York could be mentioned using the full name New York City, the partial name
New York, the alias Big apple, or the abbreviation NYC. Consequently, EL is a non-trivial
task, however, fortunately the use of the online encyclopedia Wikipedia as a KB turns out
to be particularly useful in this regard.
2.1.1.1
Wikipedia
As mentioned briefly in section 1.1 Wikipedia consists of multiple linked articles and
can be viewed as a KB of connected entities that are each defined or described by the
article text. The high coverage of unique entities and the link structure is what makes
Wikipedia so well-suited for the EL task. Combining these characteristics improves the
chances of differentiating between entities and finding matches between entity mentions
and Wikipedia entities. However, in addition to the general structure, Wikipedia also
provides a set of highly useful features for entity linking in the form of redirect pages,
disambiguation pages, and anchor texts.
• Redirect pages.
A redirect page is a page that refers to an existing entity in
Wikipedia but has an alternative name. For example, the article titled Microsoft
Corporation which is the full name of Microsoft contains a pointer to the article of
the entity Microsoft. Therefore, searching for Microsoft Corporation on Wikipedia
would bring you to the Microsoft article page.
• Disambiguation pages. When multiple entities in Wikipedia could be given the
same name, a disambiguation page is created to separate them. The disambiguation
page contains a list of references to those entities. For example, the disambiguation
page for Michael Jordan at the time of writing lists 13 associated entities that have
the same name as Michael Jordan.
• Anchor texts. An article on Wikipedia often contains hyperlinks in the text that
link to other Wikipedia articles. These hyperlinks constitute the connections between
Wikipedia entities. For example, the Wikipedia article for Steve Jobs is connected
with the articles Chief executive oﬀicer and Apple Inc. through the hyperlinks in
the article text of Steve Jobs:
He was the cofounder, chairman, and CEO of Apple [...]
The names of the hyperlinks (colored blue) are called anchor texts, and one should
note that the anchor text does not need to have the same as the Wikipedia article
it refers to. Hence, an article such as Chief executive oﬀicer can be linked to using
various aliases or abbreviations like CEO. Moreover, it is worth noting that similar
anchor texts can refer to different articles. For example, a hyperlink with the anchor
text Apple can in some context refer to the tech company Apple and in another
context to the fruit.
Together, the above pages and the anchor texts can be used to construct a dictionary of
alternative names, e.g., abbreviations or aliases, by which each entity might be charac-
terized. Such a dictionary comes in handy for the EL task, especially in the candidate
generation step where it can enhance the chance of having the correct KB entity included

2.1.2
Entity linking taxonomy
Entity linking is typically regarded a ranking task, and although the implementation
details may vary considerably, the general steps that EL methods apply to rank entities
are very similar. In the following, we present a general taxonomy for the candidate entity
ranking task, which can be divided into the following steps.
1. Text embeddings: Given an entity mention in a document and a set of candidate
entities with descriptive texts, various text embeddings are generated.
2. Features: Using the embeddings, key EL features can be obtained.
3. Algorithm: The features are fed into an algorithm to rank the candidate entities
with the top-ranking results chosen for the final linking results.
2.1.2.1
Text embeddings
A key to improving the performance of the entity linking task is to effectively model text
[Yam+16] and in this regard text embeddings play a vital role. The main idea behind text
embeddings is to represent the properties of natural language using dense low-dimensional
vectors.
As a result, the notions of similarity and difference between texts would be
easy to capture using mathematical vector operations. For example, applying the cosine
distance measure to the embeddings of two texts could provide a numerical score of textual
similarity.
There exist various ways to achieve text embeddings, ranging from simple count-based
methods to sophisticated probabilistic (deep learning-based) methods, and in later sections
we shall examine some of these methods in more detail.
In this section, however, we
will focus on introducing three categories of text embeddings that are central to most EL
models, including those considered in this thesis: Word, entity, and alignment embeddings.
Word embeddings
A word embedding is defined as a mapping f →Rd, that maps a word w from a vocab-
ulary V to a vector θ in a d-dimensional embedding space. Word embeddings are based
on the idea that contextual information constitutes a viable representation of linguistic
characteristics. Consequently, word embeddings that capture semantic similarity would
be learnable solely on the basis of word or document cooccurrences in large, unstruc-
tured text corpora. Interestingly, this idea has been proven to hold[Mik+13a], and today
there are various models capable of producing word embeddings whose relative similarities
correlate with semantic similarity.
For EL, word embeddings play a key role in modeling contextual information.
As an
example, the context of a mention m can be modeled using a context embedding vector,
which is obtained by averaging the word embeddings of surrounding words. Obviously,
such context vectors are highly dependent on the ability to obtain high-quality word
embeddings, however, this is a nontrivial task.
First, it requires large amounts of text to train word embeddings. Fortunately, many EL
models in recent years have been able to exploit pre-trained word embeddings that are
8

learned in advance by some language model using large text corpora. Such pre-trained
embeddings have successfully proven to capture the semantics of words and have been
widely used; however, the inherent generality of these word embeddings might not always
be suitable for specific domains. As a result, some EL models seek to either further adjust
the pre-trained embeddings by some fine-tuning or even train them from scratch to learn
domain-specific word embeddings. In this thesis, we shall encounter both models using
pre-trained and fine-tuned embeddings, as well as embeddings trained from scratch.
Moreover, it is common to work with models that produce embeddings of words that
are static, which means that each word has a single vector representation, regardless of
context. An example of such is the Wikipedia2vec for EL model presented later in section
2.2. However, static embeddings can potentially pose problems for polysemous words1,
since all senses of such words have to share the same representation. To a great extent,
this issue has been addressed with the advent of deep neural language models that can
produce contextualized word representations (CWR) – word vectors that are sensitive
to the context in which they appear. Such embeddings are also referred to as dynamic
embeddings. Later in section 2.5 we shall present how the BERT model can be used to
obtain CWRs.
Entity embeddings
The purpose of entity embeddings is to represent each entity in a KB in a continuous
vector space. Similar to word embeddings, entity embeddings are sought to map similar
entities close to each other in the same space. This mapping proves to be very useful
in being able to capture two important features regarding entity linking; coherence and
contextual similarity. These features are described in more details in section 2.1.2.2 but in
short the coherence feature seek to measure the interrelation between entities mentioned
in the same document, while the context similarity feature seeks to capture the relation
between context of the mention and the entity.
There exist different ways of deriving entity embeddings, but the most common way is to
use entity descriptions. Such entity descriptions are typically provided for each entity in a
KB and mostly consist of a concise summary of the salient properties of the entity. In the
case of using Wikipedia as KB, the entity descriptions would naturally be the text of the
article. These articles are often rich in descriptions of each entity and are even so detailed
in some cases that it is conceivable in practice to use only a fraction of the text.
Alignment embeddings
In many cases word and entities embeddings are learned separately. Consequently, it is
not possible to effectively calculate the similarity between words and entities.
In this
regard, the alignment of word and entity embeddings into the same space, so that similar
words and entities are placed close to each other, is important. Anchor texts, mentioned
previously, cf. section 2.1.1.1, are a key resource for aligning embeddings of words and
entities. The window surrounding the anchor text can be regarded as a context of words
of its referring entity, and could provide word-entity cooccurrences examples that could
be leveraged to align embeddings. For the models Wikipedia2vec for EL [Yam+16] and
LUKE for EL [Yam+22] such alignment embeddings play a vital role.

2.1.2.2
Features
Traditionally, features for EL models have been designed manually [SWH15] with the
aim of capturing the similarity between the entity mention and the candidate entity in
various aspects. However, today, the field of EL is dominated by deep learning models
that utilize embeddings directly as input. Thus, the models do not require features to
be explicitly created in advance and, thereby, be affected by human intuition. Despite
these apparent benefits, still manually obtained features are applied in recent DL-based
EL methods [She+21]. This is also the case for the Wikipedia2vec for EL model, which
constitutes the central model presented in this thesis. For this reason, in this section, we
will introduce some of the key features found to be useful for the candidate ranking that
are also aplied for the Wikipedia2vec for EL model.
Surface form similarity
Intuitively an entity mention and an entity candidate with the same or similar surface
form are likely a match. For this reason, the comparison of name strings has been one
of the most direct features that has been applied for EL models [SWH15]. Many string
similarity measures have been used as features; however, here we will only mention a few
features that have been particularly used and that are also used by Wikipedia for the EL
model.
• Edit ditance: Quantifies how dissimilar two strings are to one another by counting
the minimum number of operations required to transform one string into another.
• Equals/contain: Binary variable that signifies whether the title of an entity e exactly
equals or contains the surface of the mention m.
• Start/end: Binary variable that signifies whether the title of entity e starts or ends
with the surface of mention m.
Entity popularity
Two measures that have been frequently observed in past EL studies are entity prior P(e)
and prior probability P(e|m) [Yam+16]. They both seek to express the probability of the
appearance of a candidate entity without taking the context into consideration; thus, they
are context independent.
The entity prior is solely a measure of the popularity of a candidate entity. If considering
Wikipedia as KB the feature can be calculated simply by using the anchors (links) of
Wikipedia. We then define the entity prior by
P(e) = |Ae,∗|/|A∗,∗|
(2.1)
where A∗,∗denotes all the anchors in the KB and Ae,∗is the set of anchors that point to
the entity e.
The prior probability is a conditional probability that measures the popularity of a can-
didate entity given a certain mention. To exemplify its usage, we can consider the former
president Barack Obama and his wife Michelle Obama as candidate entities e. Since the
US president is more popular than his wife Michelle Obama, and both can be referred to
by their lastname, the mention m = Obama in a text would in most cases refer to the

former president rather than his wife. Based on the usage of Wikipedia as KB, the prior
probability can be defined as
P(e|m) = |Ae,m|/|A∗,m|
(2.2)
where A∗,m represent all anchors with the same surface as mention m in the Wikipedia
KB and Ae,m is a subset of A∗,m that points to entity e.
The prior probability feature has within EL research proved to be a significantly good
feature [She+21]. In many common enity linking datasets, the accuracy of using then
prior popularity feature even alone can reach 70%-85% [GH17]. Possibly, this is the reason
why Yamada et al. for the Wikipedia2vec for EL model consider a feature whose purpose
is to seek improving the reliability of the prior probability feature. This feature represents
the maximum prior probability, max P(e|m) , of the candidate entity e of all mentions in
the document.
To illustrate the incentive of using max P(e|m), we here provide a small example:
”They reject German call [...] and Germany’s representative [...]”
Provided this text, the goal is to link the entity mention German with one of two entity
candidates from a Wikipedia KB: German language and Germany. Using the anchor texts
from Wikipedia we might calculate the prior probabilities P(e|m) to have the following
values:
P(e = ”Germany” | m = ”German”) = 0.32
P(e = ”German language” | m = ”German”) = 0.37
This would lead us to slightly favor the candidate entity German language, which in
this case would be wrong. However, in the above quote another mention Germany ap-
pears later. Using the two mentions for the calculation of the maximum prior probability
max P(e|m) yields the following.
max P(e = ”Germany” | m ∈{”German”, ”Germany}) = 0.79
max P(e = ”German language”| m ∈{”German”, ”Germany}) = 0.37
As a consequence of the strong relation between the entity mention Germany and the
entity candidate Germany, which can be calculated using the prior probability P(e =
”Germany” | m = ”German”) = 0.79, we now would seem to prefer the entity candidate
Germany over German language.
Context similarity
The context similarity is a feature that measures the similarity between a representation
derived from the context around the entity mention and a representation associated with
the candidate entity. As was very briefly touched upon in section 2.1.2.1 the surrounding
context of a mention m can be encoded in a context embedding, which for a large number
Entity linking using knowledge-enhanced embeddings
11
==PAGE:22==
of EL methods, including the ones presented in this thesis, is obtained by leveraging word
embeddings.
In the simplest case, the context embeddings are computed by averaging the word embed-
dings of the context words that appear within a certain fixed window size of the mention
m. Subsequently, to obtain the final context similarity feature, a similarity score is calcu-
lated between the generated context embedding and the entity embedding mentioned in
section 2.1.2.1 is calculated. In this regard, various computing methods can be utilized;
however, cosine similarity is the most widely used scoring method.
Coherence
The aforementioned features mainly concern the similarity between the entity mention
and one of its candidate entities. Such features are within EL research referred to as local
features, and the sole use of these, could result in each entity mention in the document
being linked independently. However, an imminent assumption is that cooccuring entity
mentions in the same document often refer to entities that are topically coherent – there is
a interdependence among entity mentions. As a result many models take into consideration
the global topical coherence among referring entities within the same document, which can
be expressed with a coherence feature.
Although the modeling of coherence is shown to be key in improving the performance
of EL ([GH17]+[LT18]) it is not trivial to achieve. This is due to that the assignment
of entities to mentions, which is required to measure coherence, is not possible prior to
performing EL. To resolve this chicken-and-egg problem, which is shown to be a NP-hard
problem, a great number of methods, including the ones considered in this thesis, link
entity mentions in a document in a sequential or partially sequential manner. Hereby,
they utilize already disambiguated entities to model topical coherence for the subsequent
candidate entities [She+21]
2.1.2.3
Algorithms
The final step of the entity ranking task is carried out using one of many possible algo-
rithms. The algorithm takes features and/or embeddings described in the above sections
as input as well as an annotated data set, and outputs the final entity-linking result. More
specifically, the algorithm learns how to score the relation between each candidate en-
tity and the respective mention, and subsequently the candidate entity with the highest
ranking score is chosen as the output linking entity. Several algorithms can be utilized to
perform this ranking task; however, in this thesis the focus is on algorithms that formulate
the entity ranking problem using a classification mindset. More specifically, the focus is
on two models, Wikipedia2vec for EL and LUKE, that respectively formulate the ranking
problem in a similar way to a binary and a multiclass classification model.

2.2
Wikipedia2vec for EL
In 2016 Yamada et al. proposed a special embedding method designed for entity linking
[Yam+16]. The method, named Wikipedia2vec, is capable of jointly mapping words and
entities into the same continuous vector space and place similar words and entities close
to each other in the space. This is obtained by extending the skip-gram model[Mik+13a]
with two additional skip-gram-based models that are jointly trained using Wikipedia.
1. The conventional skip-gram model that learns static word embeddings.
2. A KB graph model that learns static entity embeddings.
3. An anchor context model that learns to join the word and entity embeddings into
the same vector space.
As a result of the embedding method, the similarity between any pair of items (words,
entities, and a word and an entity) can be measured simply by calculating the cosine
similarity. The method thus enables an easy way to measure contextual information for EL,
such as the similarity between a context word and a candidate entity and the relatedness
of entities required to model coherence among candidate entities. Consequently, Yamada
et al. as an extension of the Wikipedia2vec embedding method in the paper [Yam+16]
also proposed a way of using the embeddings for performing EL, based on a Gradient Tree
Boosting (GTB) machine learning model. This method led to SOTA performance on two
major EL data sets in 2016.
In the following sections, we shall first examine the three different models that together
make up the Wikipedia2vec embedding. Subsequently, we shall see how the embeddings
obtained from the Wikipedia2vec method can be applied to create two key contextual
features as proposed by Yamada et al. In the last section we present the GTB model
which Yamada et al. applied for the final EL task.
2.2.1
Skip-gram model
The skip-gram model was originally proposed by Mikolov et al. in 2013 [Mik+13a] with
the aim of learning continuous vector representations (embeddings) of words using large,
unstructured text corpora. As a result, the embedding of a word would encode its meaning
and the similarity between words would be quantifiable using simply the dot product
between word embeddings. The idea of how to learn such embeddings was based on the
notion of distributional similarity by which it is hypothesized that learning a representation
of the meaning of a word can be carried out using solely the context in which the word
appears.
In practice, the skip-gram model implements the idea by iterating word by word through
a large text corpus of size T. For each word wt, denoted as center word, the model seeks to
predict words in its context within some window size j. This is accomplished by defining
a probability distribution that states the probability that a word appears in the context
given the center word. In figure 2.2 a small example is illustrated of how the center word
banking is used to predict the context words within a size of two.

Explicit Semantic Analysis
In 2007 Evgeniy Gabrilovich and Shaul Markovitch proposed a method for computing
semantic relatedness of natural language texts named Explicit Semantic Analysis (ESA)
[GM+07]. The method represents the semantic meaning of text with vectors (embeddings)
in a high-dimensional space; however, in contrast to purely statistical methods, the em-
beddings are obtained explicitly from natural concepts (articles) derived from Wikipedia.
Consequently, any text is represented as an embedding consisting of a weighted mixture
of Wikipedia concept.
To obtain the Wikipedia concept embeddings a tf-idf weighing scheme is applied [SM83].
Tf-idf is a statistical measure for evaluating the relevance of a word to a document in a
collection of documents, and is calculated by multiplying two metrics: The term frequency
(tf) and the inverse document frequency (idf). The term frequency is the raw count of
instances a word appears in a document, whereas the idf is a way of reweighing the count
by reducing the importance of frequently occurring words across documents and increasing
the importance of rare words. Various formulas for the tf-idf score, however, in this thesis
the tf-idf is calculated as
tf −idf(t, d) = tf(t, d) × idf(t)
(2.16)
where tf(t, d) is the frequency count for each word (term, t) in a given document d and
idf(t) = log

, where df(t) is the document frequency for the word t and N is the
total number of docuents.
Using the tf-idf scheme each Wikipedia concept can be represented as an attribute vector of
words that occur in the corresponding article, where the word entries are assigned weights
according to their corresponding tf-idf score. Hence, the weights quantify the association
of strength between words and concepts.
Following the terminology of Gabrilovich and Markovitch, the above tf-idf matrix consti-
tute a central component of a semantic interpreter, which can map fragments of natural
language text into a weighted vector of Wikipedia concepts. We shall refer to such weighted
vector as an ESA embedding. Given the pre-calculated tf-idf matrix, the process of the
semantic interpreter can be illustrated as in figure 2.8 and described in the following steps:
1. Split the input text into separate words.
2. Obtain the raw count, term frequency (tf), for the input words and subsequently use
the inverse document frequency used for obtaining the tf-idf matrix to obtain the
tf-idf score for each input word. Combine the tf-idf scores into a vector called the
query vector.
3. From the tf-idf matrix retrieve the concept vectors for each word appearing in the
input text.
4. Use the dot product between the transposed query vector and the retrieved concept
vectors to obtain a weighted vector of Wikipedia concepts; the ESA embedding,
which additionally can be normalized.

2.4
Transformer
In 2017 a new modeling paradigm was introduced as Google researchers published a paper
that proposed a novel neural network architecture, dubbed transformer, for sequence mod-
eling [Vas+17]. Prior to the transformer, recurrent architectures had been dominating the
field of sequence modeling, and architectures such as LSTMs [ref], that allow information
to propagate from one step to another, were considered ideal for modeling sequential data
like text. However, with the advent of the transformer the idea of modeling text based on
recurrence was dropped altogether. Instead a special form of attention called self-attention
was entirely relied on.
We shall cover self-attention in more details in section 2.4.3, but the basic idea is that
it allows the transformer to operate on all tokens in a sequence simultaneously rather
than sequentially. As a result, the architecture can be trained much faster than recurrent
models while also allowing for a more effective way of modeling long-range dependencies.
Not only that, by combining the transformer architecture with unsupervised learning, the
need to train task-specific architectures from scratch is removed. These advantages have
contributed to transformer models being particularly prevalent in the field of NLP, where
they have paved the way for many recent breakthroughs.
The transformer architecture forms the backbone of the BERT, SBERT and LUKE models,
which will be presented in later sections.
Thus, in order to establish a fundamental
understanding and appreciation of the capabilities of these models, we will in the following
sections examine some of the key elements that make up the transformer architecture.
2.4.1
The encoder
The transformer was originally designed for sequence-to-sequence tasks such as machine
translation where one sequence of words is translated into another.
Based on preced-
ing sequence models the transformer also adopted an architecture consisting of two main
components; an encoder and a decoder, which are sketched in figure 2.9. The task of
the encoder is to encode the information from an input sequence of tokens into numer-
ical representations (embeddings) often referred to as hidden states or contextual word
representations (CWRs). Subsequently, the decoder uses the encoder’s hidden states to
iteratively generate an output sequence of tokens, one at a time.
Though the transformer architecture was originally designed for sequence-to-sequence
tasks both the encoder and decoder have later been adapted as standalone models. As
a result, transformer models are nowadays roughly divided into three different classes
of models; encoder-decoder, decoder-only and encoder-only. The transformer models pre-
sented in this thesis belong to the third type, which have the ability to convert an input
sequence of text into rich contextual word embeddings well-suited for various NLP tasks
including entity linking. For this reason we shall in the following only focus on the encoder-
only model architecture.

2.4.2
Token embeddings
Transformers cannot receive raw text as input, but rely on the text being tokenized and
encoded into numerical vectors also referred to as token embeddings, as depicted in figure

The most adopted tokenization strategy is subword tokenization that works by
splitting rare words into smaller subwords based on an approach reminiscent to the use
of prefixes and suﬀixes. As a result, the word ”tokenizing” would be splitted into the two
subwords ”token” and ”##izing”, where ”##” indicates that the subword should be put
in conjunction with the preceding subword.
Following the tokenization, each token is mapped into some token embedding of dimension
D, in figure 2.10 D is sketched with 4 boxes. These token embeddings are at first all
randomly initialized, however, as the model is trained the embeddings are updated and
eventually end up obtaining characteristics in much the same way as the word embeddings
based on the skip-gram model does, cf. 2.2.1. While the token embeddings are so-called
static embeddings, we shall shortly see how they can be modified by the self-attention
mechanism in order to obtain ”dynamic” hidden states vector representations.
2.4.3

Originally, the attention mechanism was developed for recurrent sequence models. The
purpose was to allow each output of a model to be generated based on a different amount
of weight or attention being assigned to each element in the input sequence. For text
sequences, the elements are token embeddings like the ones encountered in the previous
section. Due to its success, the attention mechanism was also adopted for the transformer,
however, in contrast to the recurrent attention mechanism, the transformer applied so-
called self-attention [Lew22].

2.4.5
Feed forward, skip connection and normalization
In the previous sections it was presented how a multi-head self-attention layer can be used
for reweighing the token embeddings in a sequence to include more contextual relevance.
In this section, we shall see that such attention layer constitute one of the processing steps
that, combined with several others, make up a single encoding layer as illustrated in figure
2.14, which further can be combined with multiple consecutive encoding layers of the same
structure to form the total transformer model.
Aside from the multi-head self-attention layer a feed forward layer constitutes a key part of
the encoder layer. It consists of a two-layer fully connected neural network, however, with
a slight twist. Instead of processing the entire sequence of embeddings as a single vector,
it processes each embedding independently and identically. Therefore, this layer is often
referred to as position-wise feed-forward layer. Notably, a rule of thumb from literature
is for the hidden size of the first layer in the feed forward network to be four times the
size of the embeddings. Consequently, most of the weights in the encoding layer are in
the feed forward network and it is hypothesized that this is where the most capacity and
memorization happens [Lew22].
Besides the multihead self-attention and feed foward sub-layers, some techniques to im-
prove training are also contained within the encoding layer: Skip connections and layer
normalization. The skip connections pass the input around each sub-layer to the next
layer of the model without processing and add it to the processed vectors [He+15]. These
skip connections enable training of very deep neural networks, by ensuring that each pro-
cessing layer at least learns the identity mapping, and hence minimizing the degradation

were based on, which takes the order of word into account. As a result, the transformer
does not have any sense about position of each word. Consequently, there is a need for a
way to incorporate the order of the words into the transformer model.

vector as depicted in figure 2.10. Given that the pattern is characteristic for every position,
it is hypothesized that the attention heads and feed-forward layers in each encoder can
learn to incorporate positional information into their transformations.

sinusoidal functions of different frequencies:

2.5
BERT
In 2018, Google introduced and open-sourced the model BERT (”Bidirectional Encoder
Representations from Transformers”) [Dev+18], which caused a stir within the NLP com-
munity. The reason was that the model obtained state-of-the-art results in a wide variety
of NLP tasks including sentiment analysis, question answering, sentence classification and
the disambiguation of polysemous words. What further intrigued many was that this was
obtained by a framework consisting of two steps: pre-training and fine-tuning. During
pretraining the parameters of the BERT model were trained in an unsupervised fashion
on a vast amount of unlabeled text. Subsequently, in the fine-tuning step these parameters
could be combined with just one additional output layer (also referred to as a classification
head) and further fine-tuned using labeled data from a downstream NLP task.
As the name suggests BERT is an example of transformer model which is only based on
the encoder part of the original transformer model. Hence, its architecture resembles the
architecture presented in the previous section 2.4 where an input text sequence is trans-
formed into token embeddings, augmented with positional embeddings, and subsequently
fed through multiple encoder layers to produce the final contextual word representations
(CWRs) or hidden states, as depicted in figure 2.16. In relation to figure 2.16, we shall
later present why the BERT model has more processing steps regarding the input and
what the classifiers are meant for.
Consequently, the key capability of BERT is that
it can produce CWRs that encode some contextual information in the sequence.
The
”bidirectional” in the name comes from the fact that in contrast to previous models, that
processed text sequentially either from left to right or combined, BERT processes text by
jointly conditioning on both the entire left and right context. This capability, enabled by
the transformer architecture, allows the model to learn the context of a word based on all
of its surroundings (with no locality bias) and better capture long-range dependencies.
2.5.1
Pre-training of BERT
Using the bidirectional capability, the pre-training of BERT, in contrast to traditional
language models, is carried out using two tasks: Masked Language Modeling (MLM) and
Next Sentence Prediction (NSP). The idea of MLM is to randomly mask some of the
tokens and make the model learn to predict the original tokens based only on the context
tokens. In addition, the objective of Next Sentence Prediction training is to have the
model predict whether two given sentences have a logical, sequential connection. These
training tasks are trained at the same time and can be illustrated for a single training
example as shown in figure 2.16. In the following we shall elaborate on these pre-training
tasks and explain why BERT has multiple processing steps regarding the input compared
to the earlier presented transformer, cf. section 2.4.

As input the BERT model takes two sentences at a time, sentence A and B. For the
given example in figure 2.16 these are (A) My dog is cute. and (B) He likes playing. These
sentences are at first split into tokens and combined with two special tokens; a classification
token [CLS] added at the beginning of the sequence and a separation token [SEP] between
each sentence.
Subsequently, the tokens are embedded with a 768-dimensional vector
represented by a square in figure 2.16. Next for the MLM task, 15% of the words are
masked or flagged for later predictions as follows. 12% are replaced by a [MASK] token
(yellow), 1.5% are flagged as swapped without being swapped (green), and 1.5% are flagged
as swapped with another token (red). Following the masking, positional embeddings are
added, and subsequently a sentence embedding, used for the NSP task, is applied to mark
what tokens belong to sentence A and B.
Once the input processing steps have been carried out, the input embeddings are send
through BERT’s encoder layers. At the output, the CWR for the first token ([CLS]) is
given as input to a NSP classifier. This classifier uses a small neural network to predict
whether sentence B is a direct extension of sentence A. Moreover, the output positions of
the masked and flagged tokens, the respective CWRs are each send through a different
MLM classifier (also neural network). These classifiers have no knowledge about what
tokens that have been masked, swapped or kept the same and their job is then to predict
the original tokens. As a result, by jointly using the loss of the NSP and MLM classifiers
to update the weights of the encoder layers BERT eventually learns to process an input
sequence and represent each token by a CWR that encodes the context.

2.5.2
Using BERT for sentence embedding
What is interesting about BERT in the context of this thesis is that the pre-trained BERT
can be used to represent a text sequence in a single vector (sentence embedding). Thus,
BERT can be used to process documents and entity text descriptions (e.g. Wikipedia
articles) to create context and entity embeddings in a vector space where semantically
similar sentences are placed close together. As a result, these BERT sentence embeddings
can be used as a replacement for the embeddings originally used for the Wikipedia2vec
for EL model to produce context similarity and coherence scores in similar way to the
approach presented in section 2.14.
There are several ways to create such sentence embeddings using the CWRs from the
BERT model’s output. One way is to use the CWR of the [CLS]-token from the last BERT
encoder layer, which in the context of EL has been applied by [Wu+20] and [ZLY21]. The
reason for the use of just the [CLS]-token CWR is that it is hypothesized that due to
the NSP pre-training task, this token learns to encode a sentence-level understanding. An
alternative approach to obtain sentence embeddings using BERT is to apply mean-pooling
that averages the CWR’s of all tokens in the last layer. This approach has in many cases
been shown to provide better sentence representations, and has among others been applied
for EL by [Li+22]. Furthermore, the mean-pooling approach can also be applied to obtain
a sentence embedding for only specific words in the input text. For instance, the context
embedding for the mention Nordic in the example provide in figure 2.17 can be obtained
by simply averaging the output CWR’s for all words except the word Nordic. Due to
these abilities of the mean-pooling method, this is the approach that is used for all our
experiments regarding the BERT model.

2.6
SBERT
In the previous section it was presented how the CWR outputs from a pre-trained BERT
model can be used to create context and entity embeddings.
Subsequently these can
be used to obtain context similarity and coherence scores. However, BERT is actually by
design tailored to perform sentence similarity tasks. This is due to BERT’s so-called cross-
encoder structure that allows BERT to process sentences simultaneously. By augmenting
the BERT model with a preceding feed-forward neural network (FFNN) classification
layer BERT can be fine-tuned using labeled data to perform various sentence regression
or classification tasks directly as shown in figure 2.18.
Figure 2.18: Example of how BERT, due to its cross-encoder structure, in combination with a
FFNN can be used to train to predict sentence similarities.
It is obvious that the cross-encoder structure with respect to EL could be used to obtain
context similarity and coherence scores. However, the structure is actually not so eﬀi-
cient and causes a massive computational overhead. To illustrate this, one could consider
the task of finding the pair in a collection of 10.000 sentences with the highest simi-
larity. This would in the case of the cross-encoder structure require BERT to perform
n˙(n −1)/2 = 49, 995, 000 inference computations, which takes about 65 hours on a V100
GPU. Conveniently, in 2019 the Sentence-BERT (SBERT) architecture was introduced to
tackle this problem [RG19]. SBERT is based on a modification of the pre-trained BERT
network and uses a siamese network structure to derive semantically meaningful sentence
embeddings that can be compared using cosine-similarity. Due to this architecture, the
effort for finding the most similar pair is reduced from 65 hours to 5 seconds while the
accuracy of BERT is maintained.
The original approach to training the SBERT model is depicted in figure 2.19. It shows
that the model first takes two sentences as input that are each fed to a pre-trained BERT
model. These BERT models share the same network weights and thus in practice it is
the same model that is applied for processing both sentences. After the BERT processing
of each sentence mean pooling is applied on the final output layer to produce a sentence
embedding for each sentence, u and v.
These are then concatenated together with a

vector of their absolute difference (|u −v|) and fed through a feed forward neural network
(softmax classifier) that scores the similarity of the input sentences on a scale from 0 to 1.
By using this training approach, the pre-trained BERT model can be fine-tuned to learn
to produce sentence embeddings that are better at capturing textual similarities between
any two sentences [RG19]. Following the training, a sentence embedding from the SBERT

2.7
LUKE
Contextualized word representations (CWRs) based on the transformer [Vas+17] such as
BERT [Dev+18] provide effective general-purpose word representations. However, many
natural language tasks involve entities, e.g. question answering, named entity recogni-
tion and not least entity linking (EL). A key to solving such tasks is to effectively learn
representations of entities. Although, it is possible to use the architecture of CWRs to
represent entities, there are several reasons why CWR’s are not well-suited:
• Since CWR models do not output span-level representations of entities, they typi-
cally need to learn to compute such representation based on a downstream dataset.
• Many entity tasks, such as EL, involve reasoning about entity interrelationships.
Although, the transformer can capture token relations, it is diﬀicult to perform such
reasoning between entities as these can be split into multiple tokens.
• The word-based masked language modeling pre-training task of CWR’s is not suit-
able for learning entity representations. This is due to that predicting a masked
word is much easier than predicting the entire entity (which can be made up of
multiple words), e.g, predicting ”Rings” given ”The Lord of the [MASK]”, is clearly
easier than predicting the entire entity.

To address the issues of CWR’s Yamada et al. in 2020 proposed a novel method for creat-

Understanding with Knowledge-based Embeddings), which achieved SOTA performance
in several entity related tasks. Based on the bidrectional transformer RoBERTa [Liu+19],
the LUKE model besides words treats entities in a given text as independent tokens,
which enables it to model the interrelationships between entities and words and output
contextualized representations for both, as shown in figure 2.21. Thus, LUKE and the
Wikipedia2vec embedding method described in section [WIKIPEDIA], in some sense share

3.1
Wikipedia2vec
In 2018, the code for the Wikipedia2vec embedding method, described in section 2.2,
was published by Studio Ousia [Yam+18] on Github with documentation available at
http://wikipedia2vec.github.io/. As a result, it is possible to freely train Wikipedia2vec
embeddings such as those proposed by Yamada et al. in 2016 [Yam+16]. The training
consists of a series of commands, available on the Github page, which, in addition to
providing a way to achieve word and entity embeddings, also provides a KB dictionary
and access to a number of functionalities that conveniently facilitate preprocessing of
Wikipedia data.
In the following, we will present step by step how we have run these commands (what
settings we have used) to train the Wikipedia2vec embeddings in a way that as much as
possible is similar to how Yamada et al. did it for [Yam+16]. In addition, we will also
present some of the key functionalities which we have found particularly useful for our
data processing. On the Github page, certain parameter values are set as default for each
command, which might lead one to believe that these were the values used in [Yam+16].
However, we find that the values sometimes do not correspond to the ones provided in
the article, and therefore in the following we present the default values that have been
overwritten. The following results are obtained using Wikipedia2vec version 1.0.5 and
Python 3.7.11 – Wikipedia2vec is at the moment of writing not compatible with Python
versions after 3.7.

Wikipedia2vec training commands

downloaded on March 26th. Following this download, the subsequent commands are given
by:
1. build-dump-db: Takes the Wikipedia dump file as input and creates a database
that contains Wikipedia pages, each of which consists of texts and anchor links.

It turns out that with Wikipedia2vec version 1.0.5, running the command build-
dictionary results in a remarkably large number of entities. Upon closer inspection,
we found that this is due to Wikipedia having a number of empty links; hyperlinks
that link to non-existent Wikipedia articles.
On Wikipedia pages on-line, these
hyperlinks are marked in red, which indicates that a page will be created soon or
that an article should be created. Without correcting the code, the Wikipedia2vec
embedding method will try to train embeddings for these blank articles, which for
obvious reasons leads to poor embeddings and unnecessary processing. To hinder
this, we have edited the code, which is shown in the appendix A. As a result, the
command build-dictionary creates a dictionary with 5,525,233 entities and 2,196,922
words.
3. build-mention-db: Builds a database that contains the mappings of anchor texts and
their referent entities. Consequently, this database can, e.g. be used to find out which

To include all referent entities (min-prior-prob=0), 2) not to ignore entities because
the probability of their name appearing as a link is less than a certain threshold
(min-link-prob = 0), and 3) set the maximum number of characters in an entity
name to 1000 to allow any entity name length.
4. train-embedding: This is the last command which runs the training of the embed-
dings. Following the paper [Yam+16], we have set the number of dimensions for
the word and entity embeddings to 500. The learning rate was set to 0.025, which
decreases linearly with the iterations of the Wikipedia dump. The context window
was set to 10 and the number of negative samples to 30. Training was carried out by
iterating over pages in the dump database 10 times and was carried out on an AMD

3.3
ESA – Semantic interpreter
In order to create ESA embeddings for context and entities, we have implemented the
semantic interpreter, described in section 2.3. This is carried out by first extracting entities
from our KB using the Wikipedia2vec dictionary and subsequently finding their respective
article texts in the dump database. The texts are then run through a preprocessing step,
where special tokens are removed, and stemming is applied to every word. Subsequently,
the tf-idf table is built using the TfidfVectorizer implementation in scikit-learn version
1.0.2. The only parameters that we have changed from default are respectively stopwords
which is set to english and max_df which is set to 0.7 (ignoring words that have a
document frequency strictly higher than this value). The final tf-idf table is a 5, 525, 233×
16, 497, 317 sparse matrix, where the very high number of terms (dimensions) is caused by
the special characters from different languages, e.g. Chinese. Subsequently, context and
entity coherence embeddings have been obtained as described in section 2.3.
3.4
Transformer models
For both BERT and SBERT, we have used the BERT model bert-base-cased, which can
be accessed via HuggingFace’s library transformer [Wol+19]. The model is an implemen-
tation of the original BERT transformer model, and its pre-training specifications can be
found in [Dev+18]. We use the cased version of the BERT model, which distinguishes be-
Entity linking using knowledge-enhanced embeddings
47
==PAGE:58==
tween upper and lower case words such as english and English, as the information learned
in this model might be helpful in relation to the entity linking task, where entities are
mostly cased. The base in the model name refers to that the model is implemented with
12 encoders and 12 bidirectional self-attention heads, in contrast to the large model ver-
sion consisting of 24 encoders with 16 bidirectional self-attention heads. The reason for
choosing the base model is computational eﬀiciency.
BERT base model can process up to a maximum of 512 tokens, however, most AIDA-
CoNLL documents and Wikipedia articles do not fit the limit of 512 tokens. Moreover,
the memory complexity of the self-attention model in the BERT model is quadratic in
length of the input sequence.
For this reason, we have chosen to process only texts
with a maximum length of 128 tokens, which yields both for the document texts and the
Wikipedia articles. For the document texts, we use a context window of tokens surrounding
the mention, while for the Wikipedia articles, we use the first 128 tokens of the article,
which we hypothesize capture the most important aspects of the article. For consistency,
the maximum of 128 tokens is selected for both BERT and SBERT. To obtain the context
and entity embeddings for respectively BERT and SBERT, the mean-pooling method
described in section 2.5 is applied for both. Lastly, it should be noted that all processing
of transformers is carried out using an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3080 Ti GPU with CUDA
Version 11.4.
3.5
SBERT embeddings
To train SBERT embeddings, we have chosen to use the architecture with MNR loss
described in section 2.6. Training is performed on the train data, and the development
set is used for validation. As sentence (text) pairs, we use only the document texts and
Wikipedia articles for the matching mentions and candidate entities.
As a result, the
training data consists of 18,440 text pairs while the validation data consists of 4,789 text
pairs.
Furthermore, we have chosen to consider two different training strategies. The first strat-
egy considers the entire text of the documents as input, shown in figure 3.1a, where texts
are processed by BERT, mean-pooling is applied to obtain sentence embeddings, and the
embeddings are fed to the MNR loss. In addition to this strategy, we hypothesize that by
forcing the BERT model to use only the context words in the document text surrounding
the mention, better contextual representations can be obtained. For this reason, we also
propose a masking strategy in which we mask the mention, which is shown in figure 4.8b.

The assessment of entity linking systems is usually performed in terms of evaluation mea-

that should be linked, and thus precision=recall=F1 score=accuracy. For this reason, the
accuracy reported for our models is simply calculated as the number of correctly linked
entity mentions divided by the total number of all entity mentions. When comparisons
are made between the models, an approximate 95% confidence interval is reported. This
is calculated using the normality approximation for binomial observations [LLC]:

Fine-tuning of SBERT
For the training of sentence embeddings we have experimented with various learning rates,
that are tested for both the total text and the masked SBERT models, presented in sec-

respective learning rates are shown, and for both model types, the best performing models

we note that the learning rates do not significantly affect the training. This might be

It is notable that there is a significant gap between the train and validation for the masked

immediate thought is that this might indicate an overfit, and that the masked models

learn patterns in the training data that do not generalize to the validation data. However,
one must be careful about directly comparing the learning curves for the total text and
masked models, due to the fact that they, after all, are trained using different tasks.
Furthermore, we are not witnessing a definite overfit, as the validation error does not
increase, but in fact seems to keep decreasing very slowly. Thus, it can not be ruled out
that the gap potentially is due to the model training slower, and that a higher number
of epochs should have been chosen.
Due to this uncertainty of whether the model is
overfitting, we investigate the matter further in section 4.1.1.
From the plots of the losses it seems that the total text models are better at learning
similarity patterns between the document texts and the Wikipedia articles, and thereby
should produce better embeddings. However, we actually hypothesize that the loss curves
give false expectations. This is due to that the total text model has access to the en-
tire text (the 128 tokens) for respectively documents and Wikipedia articles. As many
mentions have a surface name that appears exactly the same way (or similarly) in both
the document it appears in and its associated Wikipedia article, the model would quite
effortlessly learn to score such text pairs as similar. To illustrate this, a simple training
example is provided in figure 4.2 with a batch size of two for the mentions China and
Czech Civic Democratic Party. From this example it is evident that the document and
entity (Wikipedia article) SBERT embeddings prior to training most likely already would
have quite similar embeddings, especially due to the name similarities between the texts
marked in red. As a result, the task of learning to detect the matching document-article
pairs would be rather trivial, and one might question how much is learned based on sen-
tence information. Conversely, as soon as the mention is masked (removed), the model
is forced to learn from the context, which is much more diﬀicult – especially if to find
patterns that can be generalized.

Error analysis
A single-score evaluation like accuracy may hide important conditions of inaccuracies. For
this reason, we present in this section an analysis of errors made by the models in table

to seek identification of general patterns of the errors and possibly enable a diagnose of
the root causes behind these errors. Consequently, this would facilitate the possibility of
making adjustments that potentially can increase the model’s performance.
Initially, we have examined the correlation between errors made by the models. To find
these correlations we have first created a 9 × 4, 483 error table for the 9 models, consisting
of zeros and ones for respectively correct and incorrect predictions for the 4, 483 mentions
in the test data. Subsequently, we have used this table to find the mutual correlations
between the model predictions. The result is shown in figure 4.5, where we have sorted the
models according to accuracy - note the Wikipedia2vec for EL model using Wikipedia2vec
embeddings is abbreviated w2v. From the figure, it appears first and foremost that there
seems to be a quite clear split between low performance models (in the top left corner)
and high performance models (bottom right). It is particularly noteworthy in this regard
that while the low performance models have relatively high mutual correlations, the high
performance models do not correlate as much as one might expect. This indicates, that
despite their higher accuracies these models make quite different mistakes.

4.4
Chapter summary
We began the chapter by presenting the results from training the SBERT model with two
different training strategies. These results revealed that the masked SBERT model was
potentially prone to overfitting. Thus, to investigate this problem further, we examined
the the train and validation distribution for the SBERT models output, however, we found
no immediate cause for concern. We were instead able to show that especially the SBERT
masked model in fact is able to learn embeddings that capture much information in relation
to the considered EL task.
We then continued with a presentation of the results obtained for each of the models
considered in the thesis on the AIDA-CoNLL data set. In this regard, the key findings
were found to be the following:
• We were able to reproduce the results of the SOTA LUKE for EL model, which
enables a comparison between all models and the SOTA performance.
• It was discovered that we were not only able to reproduce the findings of Yamada et
al. 2016 [Yam+16], but that the Wikipedia2vec for EL model performs significantly
better today than it did in 2016. We believe this is due to the constant changes of
Wikipedia, and the fact that we, in the cases of missing descriptions in the original
article [Yam+16] have had to make a number of modeling decisions ourselves in our
reproduction.
• No gain in performances for the Wikipedia2vec for EL model was shown by applying
embeddings obtained using ESA, BERT or SBERT. However, we found that by fine-
tuning the SBERT model we could obtain a performance close the one obtained
using Wikipedia2vec embeddings.
• The performance of the SBERT model depends on the strategy chosen for its fine-
tuning. The total text training strategy did not lead to better embeddings than the
original pre-trained BERT embeddings, however, using the masked training strategy,
the SBERT model seems to be able to learn embeddings that capture key contextual
information in relation to the considered EL task.
To gain further insights into the predictive capabilities of the models, we lastly carried out
an error analysis. A notable observation in this regard was that while the lower performing

5
Conclusion
In this thesis, we set out to explore EL from both a scientific perspective (DTU) and a
company perspective (Infomedia). The aim has thus partly been to investigate, evaluate
and communicate central methods within EL, and partly to provide insight into the po-
tential of using established English EL methods to eventually create a Danish EL model
for commercial use at Infomedia. As a result, we chose to focus on the following three
main studies:
1. Reproduce the findings of the Wikipedia2vec for EL model proposed by Yamada et
al. [Yam+22]
2. Test the ESA, BERT, and SBERT models as alternative embedding approaches to
the Wikipedia2vec method applied for the Wikipedia2vec for EL model.
3. Reproduce the result obtained by [Yam+22] on the AIDA-CoNLL EL data set to
facilitate a comparison between the SOTA performing model and the other methods
considered in the thesis.
Using 1) the open-source embedding tool Wikipedia2vec, 2) features that we have obtained
by trying to comply with the descriptions in the article [Yam+16], and 3) the Gradient Tree
Boosting (GTB) model implementation from scikit-learn, we have been able to reproduce
the findings of the Wikipedia2vec for EL model. Not only that, we have found that the
model even performs significantly better today than it did in 2016 – an increase in accuracy
from 91.5 to 93.6. Consequently, the model is among some of the best performing models
on the AIDA-CoNLL EL data set.
We believe that part of the explanation is caused by two key factors. First, not all details
on how Yamada et al. implemented their model in 2016 have been available. As a result,
we have had to make a number of model design choices on our ow. Among other things, we
have had to decide on the number of entities to include in our Wikipedia KB, how many
of these entities to consider for the calculations of the important P(e|m) feature, and
the decision to apply oversampling on the training data for the GTB model to approach
the issues of imbalanced data. Second, we believe that part of the explanation for the
improved result may be due to the fact that Wikipedia is a resource that is constantly
changing. From 2016 to 2022 alone, the number of Wikipedia articles has grown from
approximately 5 million to 6.5 million.
In our study of using alternative embedding methods for the Wikipedia2vec method, we
have not been able to obtain results that outperform the original Wikipedia2vec for EL
model. However, with the use of embeddings obtained from the fine-tuned SBERT model,
it has been possible to achieve an accuracy of 93.0, which is close to the accuracy of the
Wikipedia2vec for EL model. Consequently, the idea of using an SBERT model to fine-
tune BERT to achieve embeddings that to a greater extent capture relevant contextual
information in regards to EL has proven successful. In fact, we have even been able to
show that the knowledge contained in the SBERT embeddings alone can solve the EL task
on the AIDA-CoNLL data set in more than 71 % of the cases. However, it has also proved
Entity linking using knowledge-enhanced embeddings
69
==PAGE:80==
to be essential how the fine-tuning of SBERT is performed. We have in this regard tested
two training strategies and found that the great results obtained using the SBERT model
were only found in the case of using our proposed masked modeling strategy, which forces
the model to be more contextually focused.
To reproduce the result for the SOTA LUKE for EL model, we have used the code and
the AIDA-CoNLL data set, both of which have been made freely available by the authors.
We have successfully obtained the exact same result (accuracy of 95.0), as presented in
the article [Yam+22], which compared to the results of the other models considered in this
thesis, is significantly better. Through an analysis of model predictions, we have been able
to show that LUKE for EL differs from the other models in that it is eminently able to
capture global contextual information. Where the various versions of the Wikipedia2vec
for EL model to a greater extent, due to the popularity features, can lead to predictions
that are too generalizing, e.g. predicting the more popular entity ”Denmark” rather than
the ”Danish football national team”, LUKE for EL is conversely able to capture and
use topical information to correctly identify very specific entities. However, we have also
been able to show that this property in some cases can lead LUKE for EL to become
”overconfident”.
Through an analysis of the sequential confidence order method that
LUKE for EL uses to resolve entity links, we have been able to detect that LUKE for EL
more often fails for entities resolved at the end of the process. We hypothesize that this
is because the ”topical belief” that the model captures through finding entity links in the
early part of the process may not comply with the later link predictions.
5.1
Recommendations
From the studies in this thesis we have been able to show that the Wikipedia2vec for EL
model, as Yamada et al. presented in 2016, still performs among some of the best EL
models. Consequently, we find that there is potential in using the model in relation to
the development of an EL system for commercial use. What makes the model particularly
attractive in this respect is not only its high performance, which is not far from SOTA, but
also the fact that it would be quite straightforward to create a Danish version. The reason
for this is that the code to train Wikipedia2vec and produce embeddings is made freely
available under Apache license 2.0, and only a few modifications are needed in relation to
the original code to make it work with the Danish language.
In addition, it has recently been made possible to work with a Danish version of the
LUKE model named DaLUKE, which has been developed through a bachelor project at
DTU [SW21]. Consequently, the basis for training a DaLUKE for EL model is established.
However, despite the potential shown for the LUKE-based model in this thesis, the creation
of the DaLUKE for EL model will require significantly more work than the Wikipedia2vec
for EL model. Consequently, on the basis of the work carried out for this thesis project,
our final recommendation is that Infomedia should initially develop a Danish version of
the Wikipedia2vec for EL model.
