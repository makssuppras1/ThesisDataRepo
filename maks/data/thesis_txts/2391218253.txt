Abstract 
Additives such as nitrite and ascorbic acid are often used when preparing cured meats, 
due to their bacterial inhibiting properties, and antioxidant effect. Previous studies show 
that storage time has a decreasing effect of the nitrite content.  
Therefore, the aim of this thesis was to investigate the effect of storage time on 
antioxidant activity in cured meat, with different amounts of added nitrite and ascorbic 
acid. It was investigated how different amounts of added nitrite influenced the protein 
level and antioxidant activity, and if these changed with the storage time. Furthermore, 
it was assessed if the storage time had an impact on the residual nitrite and ascorbic 
acid in ham. 
Hams with six different amounts of additives were used for this experiment (nitrite, 
ascorbic acid in ppm, 1)0-0, 2) 60-240, 3) 150-600, 4) 250-1000, 5) 500-2000, 6) 1000-
4000). These were stored at 5 ºC from day 0 to day 30 with an outtake every third day.  
All six hams were produced in the same way from the same meat, only differing in the 
added nitrite and ascorbic acid level. Therefore, it was expected that all the samples 
would have the same protein content.  
From the two protein assays performed, there was a large difference in the protein 
content depending on in which assay was used. One assay showed a slight variation in 
protein content, but with no explicit pattern, where the other assay showed a correlation 
of protein level and amounts of additives and storage lengths. 
When looking at the antioxidant activity from the different in vitro antioxidant assays, 
there was no correlation between storage time and lowering of the antioxidant activity.  
From this, it can be concluded that the storage time does not seem to influence the 
antioxidant effect of for instance nitrite and ascorbic acid within the tested timeframe.

1 Introduction 
When producing cured meat products, nitrite is a common multipurpose additive to 
preserve and increase the shelf life of the meat.  
Nitrite is added due to its properties as a bacterial inhibiter, for especially Clostridium 
botulinum. 
Other reasons for adding nitrite than the benefits of bacterial inhibition, is the fact that 
the nitrite reacts with the meat’s myoglobin. This reaction causes the red color known 
in cured meats, as it works as an antioxidant, and can inhibit lipid oxidation.  
Nitrite has many benefits, however, nitrite can be converted into both nitrate and 
nitrosamines that can carcinogenic, and therefore certain limits have been set by the 
Danish Food Authorities, which are 60 ppm for ham. 
These are accessed to be the amount of nitrite that can be added, without causing harm, 
when looking at the amount of cured meat a person would eat. 
Honikell had made a large review of studies about the effect of storage time and heating 
on residual nitrite, where results from Kudryashow(2003) and Dordevic et al (1980), 
show a correlation between heating and storage time on the decreasing amount of nitrite 
[1]. 
Even tough nitrite works as an antioxidant, it would be dangerous to add the amount 
needed to work as an antioxidant by itself, therefore ascorbic acid is often added to 
cured meat. The amount that is most often added is four times the amount of nitrite

2.1 Aim 
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the effect of storage time on antioxidant activity 
in cured meat, with different amounts of added nitrite and ascorbic acid. It will be 
investigated how different amounts of added nitrite will influence the protein level, and 
antioxidant activity, and if these changes with the storage time. Furthermore, it will be 
assessed if the storage time has an impact on the nitrite and ascorbic acid in ham. 
2.2 Hypothesis 
The hypothesis behind this project is: 
Storage time will cause a decreasing effect on antioxidants ability to prevent lipid 
oxidation.

3.1 Lipid oxidation 
Lipid oxidation occurs in fats and oils and 
is a process that occurs in presence 
of oxygen via a self-sustaining free radical 
mechanism that produces peroxides and 
hydroperoxides (see Figure 2). Sometimes 
lipid oxidation can be useful, for instance 
by creating wanted flavors in cheese, 
however, lipid oxidation is often something 
manufacturers wants to avoid as lipid 
oxidation often decreases the quality of 
meat, as it causes unwanted flavoring, such as rancidity, changes in texture and color, 
and decreases shelf life. Sometimes even carcinogenic compounds can be produced by 
lipid oxidation [2]. The oxidation process of lipids can be broken down by for instance 
adding nitrite or antioxidants. Lipid oxidation is most initiated by pro-oxidants such as 
salt and metal. These causes the formation of free radicals.  
3.2 Free radicals  
Free radicals and reactive oxygen species (ROS) are highly reactive molecules that are 
generated by for instance cellular processes, environmental stress, and UV irradiation. 
ROS reacts with cellular components, damaging DNA, carbohydrates, proteins, and 
lipids, causing cellular and tissue injury. Excess production of reactive oxygen species 
can lead to several illnesses including cancer. Organisms have developed complex 
antioxidant systems to protect themselves from oxidative stress, however, excess ROS 
can overwhelm the systems and cause severe damage. The same reaction can occur in 
meat, where free radicals can be formed, and this can lead to lipid oxidation and fat 
rancidity [3].

3.3 Nitrite, nitrate and nitrosamines 
Nitrate (NO3- ) has been used for curing of 
meat for many years. Nitrate is easily 
converted into nitrite (NO2-). As a result of 
this nitrate is not commonly used and often 
replaced by nitrite[4]. 
In most countries, the use of nitrites, 
commonly added as sodium or potassium 
salts is regulated. In Europe, the number is 
150 ppm and in Denmark, it is limited to 60 
ppm. 
Nitrite works as an inhibitor for unwanted 
bacteria’s in meat products, especially Clostridium botulinum. Another reason for 
adding nitrite is to counteract the effect of NaCl, which increases the oxidation of fat 
by inhibiting lipid oxidations. Nitrite has an antioxidant effect. This, for instance, have 
an effect of the coloring of the meat.  
Even though nitrite is an antioxidant, it has different properties compared to ascorbic 
acid, as if there is a higher amount of nitrite, there is a larger possibility of forming 
nitrate and nitrosamines, and here ascorbic acid will help counteract this development.  
The lethal oral doses for humans have been established to be 80–800mg 
nitrate/kg body weight and 33–250 mg nitrite/kg body weight [5]. 
Figure 3 indicates the nitrogen numerous oxidation and thus also provides a 
picture of the many forms it may take. This underpins the complexity of 
nitrogen's chemistry. From nitrite and nitrate, many by products can be produced, 
one of them is as mentioned nitrosamines. They are formed when a nitrous acid 
reacts with secondary amines. As the most nitrosamines are carcinogenic, this 
reaction is unwanted in all meat products.

3.4 Ascorbic acid 
Ascorbic acid, also known as vitamin C, is an essential nutrient naturally found in citrus 
fruits and some vegetables.  
It is highly water soluble and works as an antioxidant/oxygen scavenger/reducing agent, 
which means that it will not have full potential in food with high amounts of fat or oil 
as ascorbic acid is insoluble [6]. 
It is often used as an antioxidant, as it has status as generally recognized as safe (GRAS) 
with no usage limits. When ascorbic acid acts as a hydrogen donor, ascorbyl radicals 
produced may reduce or terminate radical reactions; hydroperoxides may then be 
converted into stable products. Ascorbic acid also has the ability to reduce primary 
antioxidant radicals by acting as a synergist. Besides that, ascorbic acid can shift the 
redox potential of food matrixes to the reducing range and can act synergistically with 
chelators and regenerate primary antioxidants other than tocopherols [6].  Ascorbic acid 
counteracts the oxidative rancidity that happens when lipids are exposed to sunlight, 
heat, oxygen, enzymes or metal ions, from for instance packaging or process equipment 
[4].  
Ascorbic acid is also used as a flavoring. However, heat treatment makes ascorbic acid 
unstable, and it participates in nonenzymic browning reactions and degrades through 
reductone formation.  
It is common to use ascorbic acid in meat products where nitrite is added. The three 
most important properties of ascorbic acid are  
o Accelerate the salting process 
o Stabilizes the color 
o Reduces nitrosamines, by inhibiting the formation of these [7]

3.6 Protein detection 
In this thesis two different form of protein detection kits were used, both from Thermo 
Fisher. The two kits use two different approaches to find the amount of protein but are 
both based on measuring absorbance.  
 
3.6.1 BCA 
BCA uses the well-known reduction of Cu2+ to Cu1+ by protein in an alkaline medium. 
Where the violet/purple color is formed when cupric ions are complexed with peptide 
bond in an alkaline medium.  
There are multiple steps in the BCA procedure.  
The first step in the chelation of copper with the protein. This reaction is known as the 
biuret action where the peptides contain over three amino acids residues forms a colored 
chelate complex, with cupric ions in an alkaline environment containing sodium 
potassium 
tartrate. 
The next step of the development of the color, BCA and the reduced cation from step 
one reacts. Where the purple color comes from the chelation of one cuprous ion with 
two molecules of BCA. The reaction that leads to BCA color formation is strongly 
Figure 4 – Genreal illustration of dialysis[18] 
==PAGE:16==
 15
influenced by four amino acid residues (cysteine or cystine, tyrosine, and tryptophan) 
in the amino acid sequence of the protein. The absorbance is proportional to the content 
of protein in the sample [9].   
 
3.6.2 Lowry  
The Lowry method combines the biuret reaction with the reduction of Folin-Ciocalteau 
phenol reagent. The procedure involves reaction of protein with cupric sulfate and 
tartrate in an alkaline solution, resulting in formation of tetradentate copper-protein 
complexes. When Folin-Ciocalteu Reagent is added, it is reduced in correlation to the 
chelated copper complexes, producing a water-soluble product where the blue color can 
be measured at 750nm [10] 
 
3.7 Antioxidant assays 
Three different antioxidant assays were performed on the dialysate, to access how much 
antioxidant effect the different amounts of nitrite and ascorbic acid caused, and if 
storage time influence these factors. 
The assays are 
• Iron chelating 
• Reducing power 
• ABTS radical scavenging  
 
3.7.1 Iron Chelating 
Ferrozin (Figure 5) is a molecule that can bind iron. Its molecular 
structure is shown below. Iron ions (Fe2+) make a pink 
complex with Ferrozin, which has an absorbance maximum at a 
wavelength at 562 nm. 
When antioxidants chelate Fe2+ the absorbance measured at 562 nm 
will be lower since there will be less iron to form the colored complex 
with Ferrozin.

3.7.2 Reducing power 
The presence of reductants which have reduction potential such as 
antioxidant substances in the antioxidant samples causes  
A reaction with potassium ferricyanide (Fe3+) to form potassium 
ferrocyanide (Fe2+) which then react with ferric chloride to form a ferric 
ferrous complex (Prussian blue).  
The reducing capacity of a compound may serve as a significant indicator 
of its potential antioxidant activity. If there is not an excess of Fe3+, the 
blue color will not be formed. 
An increased absorbance indicates increased reducing power, if there is 
no antioxidant presence no blue color will be formed (see Figure 6).

3.7.3 ABTS radical scavenging  
2,2'-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulphonic acid (ABTS) radical scavenging 
assay is used to determine the antioxidant capacity in the sample. The technique 
involves the direct production of the blue/green ABTS chromophore by the reaction 
between potassium persulfate and ABTS [11].  
An addition of antioxidants to the pre-formed radical cation reduces ABTS and thereby 
measures the radical scavenging activity by investigating if the antioxidants present, as 
these have the ability to scavenge the ABTS radical formed.  
Trolox, a water soluble vitamin E, is used as a positive control inhibiting the formation 
of the radical cation[12].

5.5 Summarizing discussion 
As the results from protein detection were not comparable and showed a large 
differentiation, it would be advisable to research different protein methods. Jeffrey 
Moore e. al. have made a large comparison of the different protein concentration assays 
that is invented. As Kjeldahl equipment is available at DTU, this could be 
recommended, as it is both affordable and the assay has been used and re-valuated in 
many years [15] 
When accessing the antioxidant activity overall, the storage time do not decrease the 
antioxidant activity, and the results from iron chelating does not show an impact when 
looking at the amounts of additives. From a study made by D.U Ahn et al. the opposite 
conclusion was made. As it was shown that there was a direct correlation between the 
amount of antioxidant added, and the formation of lipid oxidation, under a long storage 
period, lipid oxidation would cause rancidity if additives were not added [16].  
Residual nitrite lowers through the storage period, why this cannot be the cause of 
maintaining the antioxidant activity. This could be caused by the residual nitrite forms 
into other compounds that work as an antioxidant, and there by maintaining the 
antioxidant activity. This could for instance be NO, as mentioned by Honikel [1] 
It could be considered to perform experiments where lesser nitrite where added, and 
where the amount of ascorbic acid was increased, so it was more than four times the 
amount. Studies from Mulvey et al. have investigated that ascorbic acid could increase 
the conversion of nitrite to nitric oxide, and thereby speed up the reaction of nitric oxide 
with myoglobin, where the added nitrite could be lowered by approximately one-third, 
and still sustaining the antioxidant activity. [7] 
From the residual nitrite content, it could be seen that there was a direct correlation 
between the added amount of nitrite and the percentage of decrease, as mentioned in 
Table 11. From that it can be deduced that higher amount of nitrite, the more likely it 
is to form into other compounds.  Therefore, it is not preferable to add higher amounts 
of nitrite in that context, as nitrite is not converted to other compounds one-to-one. 
Seeing from an antioxidant activity perspective, it is not profitable to add more nitrite, 
and as nitrite can form into carcinogenic compounds. And it will be most responsible 
to keep nitrite level as low as possible, however lowering the nitrite content can lead to 
==PAGE:48==
 47
a bacterial formation in the product. There is no direct correlation between the 
antioxidant activity, as nitrite lowers, and it is expected that ascorbic acid also lowers, 
even though this is not seen, this could be due to other compounds, such as natural 
occurring antioxidant peptides. 
 
6 Conclusion 
The study aimed to investigate if there was a direct correlation of the storage time and 
the lowering effect of antioxidant activity. 
From the results in the protein detection assays, it can be concluded that the two 
methods show a large variation between the protein content, when comparing the 
samples with higher amounts of additives. From this result, it would be necessary to 
assess different analysis types, to see if these results show more similarity within the 
protein content. This is because it was to be expected that the protein content would be 
at the same level throughout the whole experiment regardless of the added nitrite and 
ascorbic acid content. From the antioxidant assays, there was no large difference 
throughout the storage time. ABTS radical scavenging, as well as iron chelating, both 
had quite high antioxidant activity, which was more than a 100%, this was not expected, 
and this means that more than 100% of the color formation was decreased. When 
comparing the separate assays, there was in most cases not a significant (p<0.05) 
difference in the results over storage time, which means that the expected falling effect 
of antioxidant activity is not occurring. From this it can be concluded that the storage 
times does not influence the antioxidant activity in any direction, and therefore the 
ability to increase or decrease lipid oxidation, this is even though the residual nitrite 
lowers. The results show that there was an increase in residual ascorbic acid over 
storage time, but as ascorbic acid is normally easily destroyed, it is not safe to conclude, 
that the results are accurate. 
As mentioned the residual nitrite lowers during the storage period, but it can be seen 
that there was no direct correlation to the transformation of nitrate, concluding that 
other compounds are formed from nitrite, such as NO. 
From these results, we cannot deduce a clear conclusion on the processed data. As the 
results do not fit the hypotheses, as there is no direct effect of neither storage time, 
added nitrite, or added ascorbic acid in any amount of the antioxidant activity.

7 Further perspectives 
In this report, the results are treated with the assumptions that the dialysates are 
comparable. 
It is known that the dialysates are prepared with slightly different meat dilution ratios, 
and therefore it would be optimal in the future to see if correcting or normalizing the 
data to the actual meat dilution ratios applied would result in a different conclusion. As 
mentioned in the discussion the results could also benefit from a new protein detection 
method, such as Kjeldahl, as Lowry did not give reliable and unambiguous results, and 
BCA seemed to have interference with the additives.  
As the HPLC analysis of ascorbic acid showed increasing amount of residual ascorbic 
acid over storage time, it would be possible to test how ascorbic acid and nitrite reagents 
in water, as a null sample, by mixing 1:1 with dH2O.  
In the future it could be investigated to see what kind of influence it would have to 
replace ascorbic acid with added antioxidant peptides [17].

Appendix VI – Reducing Power Protocol 
Antioxidant activity: Reducing power 
  Method – DBL 2015, NGRI 2013, ADMS 2012 
 
This version contains updates on ADMS’ version from 2012. Time and use of plastics 
have been reduced. 
 
Antioxidants with the ability to reduce Fe3+ to Fe2+ in a calorimetric redox reaction can 
be determined by this assay. The reaction is measured spectrophotometric at 700 nm. 
An increased absorbance indicates increased reducing power, if there is no antioxidant

Sample preparation: 
Sample preparation was conducted according to the procedure described in Valls et 
al., 2002 with some modifications: 
 
• 
2.5g of finely minced ham is transferred to 50mL centrifuge tubes in duplicate 
 
• 
10mL of freshly prepared 5% W/V meta-phosphoric acid and 0.5mL of EDTA solution

minutes

• 
The upper solution is filtered through a Munktell quantitative filter paper grade 00H 
Ø125 
 
• 
Finally the samples are refiltered through Millipore filters (0.20µm) using a syringe into 
amber vials for the HPLC analysis
