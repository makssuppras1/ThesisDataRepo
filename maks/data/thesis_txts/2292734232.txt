Abstract
Diatoms is a diverse group of micro algae characterized by having a protective silica
shell, the frustule. The frustule contains a nanostructured pore pattern unique to the
speciﬁc species of diatom. The aim of this thesis is to identify optical properties of the
frustule of the diatom species Coscinodiscus granii. This is achieved through a thorough
experimental study of frustule samples illuminated by light at wavelengths ranging from
UV-A to NIR.
Photoluminescence in the frustule excited by UV light has been examined, and it was
found that the frustule did not exhibit intrinsic photoluminescence.
Interference of
the transmitted light was tested for with respectively broad band light and monochro-
matic light. An interference pattern along the optical axis of the transmitted light was
identiﬁed for both sources of light in the full wavelength range tested. The minimum
distance from the frustule, periodicity and width of the peaks of constructive interfer-
ence were observed to be dependent on wavelength. Finally the eﬀect of transmitting
light with oblique incident angles was examined. It was found that the incidence light
was redirected towards the center of the frustule upon transmission, most eﬀectively
for wavelengths below 600 nm. Interference points like those investigated at normal
incidence were observed at incident angles up to 10◦. At incident angles between 10◦
and 30◦a drop in transmission compared to normal incidence was seen around 390
nm. The eﬀect of using polarized light was examined, but no signiﬁcant change in the
transmission compared to unpolarized light was seen.

Resume
Kiselalger er en mangfoldig gruppe af mikroalger kendetegnet ved at de har en beskyt-
tende silica skal, en frustule.
Frustulen indeholder et mønster af nanostrukturerede
huller som er unikt for hver speciﬁk art af kiselalge. Målet med dette speciale er at
identiﬁcere optiske egenskaber af frustulen fra arten Coscinodiscus granii.
Dette er
gjort gennem en grundig eksperimentel undersøgelse af frustuler belyst med lys ved bøl-
gelængder fra UV-A til NIR.
Ved undersøgelse af fotoluminescens i frustulen exciteret med UV lys sås det, at frustulen
ikke udsendte fotoluminescens. Interferens langs den optiske akse blev undersøgt i både
monokromatisk og bredspektret transmitteret lys. I begge tilfælde blev der identiﬁceret
interferens punkter langs den optiske akse ved alle de undersøgte bølgelængder. Det blev
observeret at minimumsafstanden fra frustulen, periodiciteten samt bredden af interfer-
enspunkterne var afhængig af bølgelængden. Endeligt blev eﬀekten af at transmittere
lys ved ikke vinkelrette vinkler undersøgt. Retningen af det indkomne lys ændredes ved
transmission ind mod midten af frustulen, mest eﬀektivt for bølgelængder under 600
nm. Ligesom for lys med vinkelret indfaldsvinkel blev der observeret interferenspunkter
for indfaldsvinkler op til 10◦. Ved indfaldsvinkler mellem 10◦og 30◦sås det at trans-
missionen i forhold til lys ved vinkelret indfaldsvinkel faldt ved omkring 390 nm. Det
blev undersøgt om polarisering af lyset havde en eﬀekt, men ingen væsentlig ændring af
transmissionen blev fundet i forhold til upolariseret lys.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Diatoms is a big and diverse group of unicellular microalgae living in lake and marine
environments. They are characterized by having a silica shell, the so-called frustule,
surrounding the organic cell. The frustule has a nanostructured pore pattern, uniquely
determined by the species of diatom.
In current times, big eﬀort is put into ﬁnding new and green technologies and sources
of energy. For this reason projects for cultivation of microalgae for uses such as e.g.
biofuel and animal food stock are seen world wide [1],[2],[3], since the main requirement
for growing algae is sunlight. With the purpose of algae cultivation being harvesting the
organic biomass, the inorganic frustules in such project would be a waste product. As
such, in recent years great interest has been taken in identifying potential uses for these
silica frustules.
Many applications of frustules of diﬀerent diatom species have been investigated: The
pores of the frustules give rise to a high surface area compared to the size of the frus-
tule. This quality has been exploited for applications within drug delivery, along with
the mechanical stability and biodegradability of the frustule, where regression in cancer
growth in mice has been shown [4]. A larger surface area is also of use in solar cells where
it leads to higher area of interaction with the sun’s rays. Incorporating the diatoms in
a dye sensitized solar cell by coating it with titania nanoparticles, has been shown to
increase the conversion eﬃciency signiﬁcantly compared to standard dye sensitized solar
cell [5].
Another big area of interest for the diatom frustules is for use as sensors. By exploitation
of intrinsic photoluminescence in the frustule of some species of diatoms, detection of
diﬀerent gasses has been shown [6]. Further, by coating diatoms with antigens, prelim-
inary results of detection of DNA has been shown [7].
The periodicity of the pore strucutre of the frustules resembles photonic crystals. As
such it has been proposed that bandgaps might occur in the diatom frustule [8]. Further,
the periodic structure has been used to induce surface enhanced Raman scattering, by
using the diatom to template arrays of nanoplasmonic particles [9].
Though the optical properties of especially radially symmetric diatom frustules have
been examined extensively, there are still many unanswered questions. So far, frustules

have almost solely been examined with light at normal incidence. Due to the periodicity
in the frustule, changing the angle of incidence of the light might aﬀect the interaction

• Intrinsic photoluminescence in the frustule

Chapter 2
Diatoms
Diatoms are microscopic, unicellular algae, of the class Bacillariophyceae. The diatoms
are a diverse group of algae that covers up to 200.000 diﬀerent species [10]. They are
found in marine as well as freshwater habitats, and the living conditions of diﬀerent
species can span from cold to warm temperature, diﬀerent pH values and living in the
ﬂoor of lakes as well as ﬂoating in the water column. It is estimated that they contribute
to between 20% and 25% of the world’s total primary production [12]. Common for the
group is the fact that the organic part of the cell, the protoplast, is surrounded by a
silica shell called the frustule. The frustule of the diatoms contains a nanostructured
pore pattern that is unique to the individual species. Though the purpose of the pore
structure of the frustules is not yet fully understood, its potential optical properties are
of great interest. In the following sections the structure of a diatom as well as their
growth is discussed, and ﬁnally the speciﬁc species of diatom used in the experiments is
presented.
2.1
Structure of a diatom
Figure 2.1: Schematic structure of a diatom. The organic protoplast is protected by the
silica shell, the frustule. The frustule contains the two valves as well as associated girdle
bands.
Since diatoms are so diverse, the structure of their frustules as well as protoplast may
vary from species to species. However, the majority of diatom species have similarities
in their frustule structure. A schematic diagram of the diatom structure in so-called
girdle view is shown in ﬁgure 2.1. The silica frustule resembles a pill-box, with an upper
and a lower part called valves, one being slightly larger than the other. Each has a
3
==PAGE:11==

series of associated bands, girdle bands. The bigger and older valve with its associated
girdle bands is called the epitheca, whilst the smaller valve and girdle band is called
the hypotheca [16]. The number of girdle bands will vary between species, as will their
structure.
Some species have bands that consist of connected circular bands, while
others have bands consisting of a number of silica plates. Like the valves have nano
structured pore patterns, so can the girdle bands have them. From the top view, or
valve view, the similarities stop, as the shape of the valves can vary strongly as seen in
ﬁgure 2.2. Diatoms are typically divided into two groups, namely the centric and the
pennate diatoms, based on the shape of their valves. Centric diatoms have valves that
are radially symmetric, where the pennate diatom valves are bilaterally symmetric.
Figure 2.2: Frustules from diﬀerent species of diatoms. The size as well as the structure
vary greatly depending on the species [11]
2.2
Growth of diatoms
Diatoms reproduce asexually through mitosis [12]. When that happens, the cell divides
in two, each daughter cell keeping one of the original valves. This becomes the epitheca
in the new diatom, and a new hypotheca is grown. This in general means that one of
the daughter cells will shrink in frustule size. As such, within a given species of diatoms
there can be considerable variance in the frustule size depending on the age of the diatom.
When cultivating diatoms the growth conditions are controlled. To imitate the light
available to the diatom in the nature, the light is set to have a daily cycle of light and
darkness. It has been shown that the spectral composition of the light may aﬀect not
only growth rate but also the size and pitch of the frustule hole structure [13]. Other

factors like pH value and salinity of the water environment the diatoms are grown in
can also aﬀect the frustule structure [14],[15].
2.3
Sample used in experiments
Figure 2.3: SEM image of a frustule valve of the species C. granii
The diatom frustule used in experiments throughout this report is of the species Cos-
cinodiscus granii (C. granii), strain K-1048 from the Scandinavian Culture Collection
of Algae and Protozoa. It is a centric diatom, with a circular valve and pore pattern
resembling the cross section of a photonic crystal ﬁbre. A SEM image of a frustule valve
of the species is shown in ﬁgure 2.3, seen from the inside, the side turned towards the
diatom protoplast. The diatoms were grown under cool white light conditions at 15 ◦C
16/8 h dark/light cycle. The process used to harvest and clean the frustules to ready
them for experimental use is described in [17].
Figure 2.4: Schematic drawing of the layers in a C. granii valve. The cut-out shows
an SEM image of the layer structure. SEM image made by M. Ellegaard (Ellegaard,
Gössling and Su), reprinted with permission
The frustules of C. granii have valves up to approximately 100 µm in diameter, though
the size varies. The valves used for the experiments are all in that order of magnitude.

The valve consists of three layers with diﬀerent structures, as illustrated in ﬁgure 2.4. On
the inside is a layer with a radially symmetric hole pattern. The holes are situated in a
hexagonal pattern and are in the order of 0.5 µm in diameter, with the distance between
them, the pitch, being approximately 1.4 µm. It is worth noting that the inside hole
structure is not completely regular, and the pitch can therefore vary slightly depending
on the location of measuring. In the middle is a layer with a hexagonal honeycomb
structure. On the outside, there is a layer with a pattern of smaller holes, in the order
of 100 nm. The small holes on the outside as well as the bigger holes on the inside are
situated over the open spaces in the honeycomb structure.

Chapter 3
Setup and equipment
This chapter is dedicated to a concise survey of the setup that forms the basis of the
experiments, as well as the equipment used throughout the experiments.
In the ﬁrst section the basis setup is introduced. In the second section, the diﬀerent
components will be listed and a brief characterization of each is carried out.
3.1
Setup
The setup used for the experiments varies for each individual experiment. However,
the core of the setup shown in ﬁgure 3.1 remains the same. It consists of a continuous
wave light source, which is shone through a sample containing one or more frustules
mounted on a sample holder. The output is directed and focussed using a microscope
objective and a tube lens, referred to as the imaging system. It is then respectively col-
lected and inspected visually with a CCD camera, or quantitatively with a spectrometer.
Figure 3.1: General setup used for the experiments. Details such as speciﬁc light source,
sample holder and mounts on translation or rotation stages vary from experiment to
experiment and are not shown in the general setup.
When investigating the transmitted light with the spectrometer, there are a few im-
portant things to note. First oﬀ, the light collected by the spectrometer comes from
a limited area. When doing measurements on the frustule, the CCD camera is always
used initially to focus and center the frustule in the ﬁeld of view. This way, it is easy
to center the ﬁbre connected to the spectrometer and be certain the measured light is
transmitted through the frustule. Secondly, since a broad band light source is used,
the transmission at each wavelength is found under the assumption that no frequency
conversion happens during the experiment. This assumption is very fundamental to the
7
==PAGE:15==
CHAPTER 3. SETUP AND EQUIPMENT
setup, since the transmission at each wavelength can then be measured directly as the
spectrum of the transmitted light. However, there are several optical eﬀects that could
cause frequency conversion of the source light. A big category are non-linear processes
like e.g. two-wave mixing and two-photon absorption. Common to all non-linear pro-
cesses is that they require high intensities of light to happen, as they depend on the ﬁeld
strength squared or higher. Since all experiments use a continuous wave source, the
average and peak intensity are the same, in the order of 1 mW
mm2 . As such, it is a good
assumption that non-linear processes does not occur in the system, as the intensities are
simply not high enough. Another possibility for frequency conversion is photolumines-
cence in the sample or the equipment. This is examined and discussed further in the
chapter 4.
3.2
Components
In this section the components used for the experiments are described.
3.2.1
Light sources
In the experiments a number of diﬀerent light sources are used:
For the investigation of photoluminescence in the frustules a HeCd laser of 325 nm wave-
length is used. It has a power of approximately 20 mW with a spot size of 1 mm. The
light from this source is collimated and linearly polarized.
A green laser diode from Thorlabs is used for aligning of the primary setup, as well as
initial measurements of the transmitted light depending on respectively the angle of the
incident light and z-distance of the objective. The laser emits light at 532 nm and has
a power of 4.5 mW, and spot size of 3.5 mm
Finally a sun simulator is used as the main light source in the angle dependency exper-
iments, as well as to examine the interference points along the optical axis. This light
source is a ﬁbre coupled LOT sun simulator, with a spectrum spanning from 200 nm to
2500 nm. The light source is designed to simulate the spectrum of the sun within the
atmosphere of Earth. The spectrum of the sun simulator between 200 nm and 950 nm
(range of the spectrometer used) is shown in ﬁgure 3.2. The light source has an average
power of 177 mW, with a spot size of 15 mm and emits unpolarized light.
The intensity of the sun simulator ﬂuctuates over time, as it is a thermal light source.
The light stability is examined by measuring the sun spectrum with the setup in ﬁgure
3.1 without a frustule sample. Measuring over a period of 30 seconds it is found that the
standard deviation is approximately ±200-300 counts with acquisition time of 300 ms,
in the observed spectral range between 400 nm and 700 nm. The deviation is measured
in counts, and varies with the acquisition time as described in section 3.2.5.

3.2.2
Half-ball lens
When executing experiments with incident light at oblique angles, an Edmund Optics
half-ball lens is used as sample holder. The sample is placed on the ﬂat side of the lens,
and the curved side is turned towards the light source. This is done so that the incident
light will always be normal to the lens, independent on the angle between the light and
the frustule. The half-ball lens used is 5 mm in diameter, and made of uncoated fused
silica with refractive index n = 1.458. The lens transmits light both in the visible but
also in the UV spectrum. The transmission spectrum of the lens is shown in ﬁgure 3.3.

3.2.3
Microscope objective and tube lens
To be able to focus and image the light transmitted through a frustule, an imaging
system is needed. This consists of a x40 Nikon Plan Fluorite inﬁnity imaging objective

Photoluminescence
One of the optical properties we wish to examine in the diatom frustules is photolumi-
nescence. Certain species of diatom frustules have proven to exhibit photoluminescence
induced by irradiantion with UV light [21],[22], and as such it is interesting to examine
this property in the C. granii ditom species. Photoluminescence has a variety of uses,
for example in diﬀerent types of sensors. It has been shown that biochemical sensing
using photoluminescence in diatoms has a potential [22], [23]. Further, it is important
to examine this property in the frustule as well as the experimental equipment, since
the measurement of transmission through the frutule in later experiments rely on no
signiﬁcant frequency conversion of the source light.
In this chapter the theory behind photoluminescence is explained. Further, a series of
experiments conducted to test the experiment components and frustule for photolumi-
nescence are described and the results presented.
4.1
Theory of photoluminescence
Photoluminescence is a process in which a photon excites a system by absorption, and is
subsequently re-emitted at the same or lower energy. The relaxation of the system might
happen in several diﬀerent ways. Figure 4.1 (a) shows a transition without energy loss,
where the emitted photon will have the same wavelength as the original. Figure 4.1 (b)
and (c) shows transitions partially through non-radiative, phonon transmissions, where
energy is lost as heat in the system. Here the emitted photon will have lower energy
and longer wavelength than the original one. The illustration in ﬁgure 4.1 is simpliﬁed,
since rather than discrete energy levels, it will be a close band of available energy states.
The photoluminescence will thus have a range of possible emission wavelengths, which
forms an emission spectrum. A schematic illustration of a possible emission spectrum
is shown in ﬁgure 4.2. In a similar way, there might be more than one photon energy
that can excite the system to create the photoluminescence, leading to an absorption
spectrum.
If the photon is emitted due to relaxation between spin allowed states (i.e. from singlet
to singlet, or triplet to triplet states), the process is called ﬂuorescence. If it happens

from spin forbidden transitions (singlet to triplet or vice versa), the process is called
phosphorescence. The main diﬀerence between the two processes is the timescale at

Interference along the optical axis
An optical eﬀect that has been conﬁrmed in frustules from several species of diatoms,
is the formation of high intensity points along the optical axis [27],[28]. These spots of
higher intensity is formed by constructive interference of the transmitted light, referred
to as interference points. The phenomena has been examined primarily using monochro-
matic light, and it has been shown that the distance of formation will depend on the
wavelength of the light [29],[30]. Because of high diﬀerence in transmission of light be-
tween the constructive and destructive interference points, it has been suggested that
one application of frustule is as an optical switch. Due to the wavelength dependence,
it might be possible to tune the wavelength of the switch by varying its position.
In this chapter the interference property of the C. granii frustule is tested. Initially the
frustule is investigated with two monochromatic light sources, namely a red and a green
laser, in an eﬀort to recreate the results of previous studies. Subsequently, the broad
band sun simulator is used to test whether interference points are formed for a broad
range of wavelengths.
The presented results in this section are from our previous experimental work. However
new evaluation of the data brings new information to the results of our experiments
5.1
Interference and diﬀraction
Interference is a phenomena caused by the fact that light is electromagnetic waves.
When two electric waves illuminate the same area, the intensity in that area is not just
the sum of each wave’s intensity. Rather, depending on their relative phase, the waves
may interfere constructively to get a higher intensity than the sum of the two, or de-
structively to get a lower intensity than their sum. For waves of the same individual
intensity and with same frequency, they can have zero intensity at destructive interfer-
ence, and double the sum of their intensities at constructive interference [31].
When light is obstructed by an object, for example when passing a hole in an opaque
plate, the light gets diﬀracted. This means that the light will interact with itself, and
22
==PAGE:30==
CHAPTER 5. INTERFERENCE ALONG THE OPTICAL AXIS
interference patterns can be observed. This phenomena is most pronounced when the
obstruction of the wave has dimensions on the order of the wavelength of the light. As
an example, when light passes through a circular hole in an opaque plate, the light will
create an interference pattern that consists of concentric circles of constructive interfer-
ence with a dot in the center with highest intensity, a so-called jinc function.
The inner layer of the frustule can be considered as a periodic structure of such diﬀraction
holes. The interference seen when illuminating the frustule could then be expected to
be an interference of the jinc functions from all the pores in the frustule. However, the
silica walls of the frustule are not opaque, so this will not fully hold. Further, the curved
edges of the frustule might also have an eﬀect on the transmitted light.

Chapter 6
Transmission at oblique incidence
In this chapter, the eﬀect of transmitting light at oblique incidence through the diatom
frustule is shown. For the ﬁrst time, angles as high as 60◦in relation to normal incidence
are used. Previously, diatom frustules have mainly been investigated with light at nor-
mal incidence. A single study has used incident angles up to 10◦, showing interference
like that seen in chapter 5 as well as redirection of light towards the center of the frustule
[32].
There are two main things that motivate examination of transmitted light at oblique in-
cidence. Nanostructured surfaces can contain many diﬀerent properties like wavelength
dependent reﬂection as seen in e.g. butterﬂy wings [33], broad-band anti-reﬂection from
the so-called motheye eﬀect [34], and frequency bandgaps as seen in photonics crystals
[35]. However, since the periodicity of the nanostructures in the frustule is situated
parallel to the surface of the frustule, it is expected potential optical phenomena like the
ones mentioned above will not occur at normal incidence. As such, studying the frustule
with insident light at an oblique angle is of interest. Secondly, diatoms are non-motile
and as such cannot adjust their orientation towards the sunlight [36]. As they are pho-
tosynthetic, light harvesting is essential for their growth. This leads to the proposition
that the frustules potentially can redirect the incoming sunlight by their pore structure
and shape, towards the diatom protoplast where the chlorophylls responsible for the
photosynthesis are situated.
In this chapter, the theory behind photonic crystals is presented. The setup for the an-
gled experiments is described, as well as the eﬀect of using an imaging system with light
of oblique incidence. Finally results of the experiments using angled incident light are
presented. This includes transmission spectra depending on angle, transmission below
the critical angle and polarization dependence.
6.1
Photonic Crystals
A photonic crystal is a material that has a structure with a periodically varying refrac-
tive index, in one, two or three dimensions (1D, 2D and 3D). Figure 6.1 shows examples
of photonic crystal structures for one and two dimensions.
28
==PAGE:36==
CHAPTER 6. TRANSMISSION AT OBLIQUE INCIDENCE
Figure 6.1: Schematic drawing of a 1D photonic crystal (left) and a hexagonal 2D.
Considering the 1D multilayer ﬁlm, it is seen that the structure is homogeneous in the
xy-direction.
This means that there are no restrictions in the electric ﬁeld, and all
wavelengths can propagate. However, in the z-direction the interfaces between the dif-
ferent refractive index regions introduce restrictions on the possible ﬁelds. At incidence
normal to the interfaces, a range of frequencies will appear that cannot propagate in
the structure, a so-called band gap. The size of the bandgap will depend both on the
thickness of the diﬀerent layers and their respective refractive indices. Since there are
no diﬀerence in the structure between the x- and y-direction the allowed frequencies are
independent of the polarization. At oblique incidence the bandgap disappears for angles
that are not very close to normal. Considering a photonic crystal in 2 dimensions, the
periodicity is along the xy-plane, while the z-direction is homogeneous. As such, the
allowed frequencies depend on the direction of propagation along the xy-plane. Further,
the polarization will aﬀect the allowed bands, since there is a big diﬀerence in whether
the electric ﬁeld is oriented in the homogeneous direction z, or parallel to the xy-plane
where the structure will inﬂuence it. It is possible to design 2D photonic crystals that
has a bandgap only in one polarization directions. However, the 2D structure shown
in ﬁgure 6.1 is designed to have a bandgap in both polarization directions. It is seen
that the displayed 2D photonic crystal has a pore pattern very close to that of the
inner layer of the C. granii frustule. However, where the photonic crystal is homoge-
neous in the z-direction, the limited width of the frustule layer will aﬀect the potential
bandgaps in the structure. The frustule layer resembles the so-called hole-slab, similar
to the displayed 2D photonic crystal but with a ﬁnite height in the z-direction, which
only contains bandgaps in the polarization direction that has its electric ﬁeld parallel
to the xy-plane. As such, in the physical structure of the frustule it is expected that
the polarisation has a big inﬂuence on potential bandgaps. However, like with the 1D
photonic crystal it is important to note that bandgaps occur for in-plane propagation of
light. At oblique angles, unless they are very close to normal, the bandgaps disappear.
Bandgap calculations of diatom frustules has been carried out in earlier studies [37],[8],
for incidence along the plane of the frustule. It is expected that incident angles close to
90◦is needed to observe potential badgaps in the structure.

Chapter 7
Discussion
In this chapter the results obtains from the experiments are discussed in further detail.
When comparing the observed interference points using monochromatic light with those
using broad band light, the experiments were not executed on the same frustules. Since
the diﬀraction from the pores and the subsequent interference is expected to be de-
pendent on the dimension of the pitch and pores of the frustule, it makes it diﬃcult
to determine whether changes in periodicity and minimum distance the interference is
observed at are due to the broad band light source or diﬀerent structure dimensions.
Making SEM images of the studied frustules would give the dimensions of the frustule
structures which is not clearly measurable on the CCD images, to make it easier to
determine the cause for changed periodicity and minimum distance from the frustule.
Still, it is expected that the smaller peaks and shorter distances at where the interfer-
ence points are distinguishable is due to use of incoherent light, as this should not be
aﬀected much from diﬀerent dimensions.
During the angled experiments, several challenges were observed. The setup was highly
sensitive and easily misaligned when components were adjusted. A big step towards a
more stable setup would be a bigger half-ball lens, since the setup would be less sensi-
tive to small changes, and for example the slight oﬀ-center placement of the frustules
would have less inﬂuence. Further, the fact that the inﬁnity corrected tube lens did not
transmit below 380-400 nm, made it next to impossible to examine the UV properties
of the frustules.
When discussing the reasons for transmission drop in the 380-500 nm and 600-900 nm
ranges, it would be desirable to be able to measure the reﬂected light as well as the
transmitted light.
This would give a better idea of the causes for the eﬀects seen.
Another possibility is illumination of smaller areas combined with more area speciﬁc
collection of light, to be able to judge how the light is distributed in the frustule. Finally,
for investigation of polarization dependence of the transmitted light, it became clear
that it is crucial to illuminate a smaller area. This way, it is easier to determine the
polarization direction in relation to the structure of the frustule.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
In this thesis the optical properties of diatom frustules from the species Coscinodiscus
granii were examined. Three overarching properties were tested, namely photolumines-
cence, interference and transmission at an angle.
Photoluminescence excited at 325 nm, with emission peaks at 455 nm and 561 nm were
observed from speciﬁc areas of a frustule. However, no other frustule examined exhib-
ited photoluminescence. Since the photoluminescence was visually seen to come from
areas within the frustule, with lower intensity at the silica walls, it was concluded that
the photoluminescence stems from residue organic material rather than from the silica
walls of the frustule. That is, diatom frustules of the species Coscinodiscus granii does
not contain intrinsic photoluminescence.
Points of constructive and destructive interference along the optical axis were observed
with both monochromatic light and broad spectrum light. Using lasers with light at
532 nm and 632 nm, strong interference points were observed from 120 to 290 µm from
the focal point. The interference points broadened with increasing distance along the
optical axis. Further the 532 nm laser created narrower peaks with higher periodic-
ity compared to the 632 nm laser. An extinction ratio of 11.6 dB and 23.3 dB were
measured for respectively the 532 nm laser and 632 nm laser. With the broad band
light source, interference points were observed for all wavelengths in the range between
380 nm and 950 nm. The bands were created closer to the focal point for increasing
wavelengths. At 532 nm and 632 it was found that fewer peaks were distinguishable
with the broad band light source than the monochromatic light sources. The peaks
also had lower extinction ratios of respectively 2.0 dB and 2.9 dB. This is contributed
to the fact that the sun simulator emits incoherent light which result in less interference.
The transmission through the frustule of light at oblique incident angles were shown.
From the maximum collection angle of the objective, determined by its numerical aper-
ture NA, it was observed that the frustules redirect the incident light towards the center
of the frustule. This happens less strongly at wavelengths above 600 nm than below.
Interference points like those investigated at normal incidence were observed visually
at incident angles up to 10◦. The transmission at incident angles between 10◦and 30◦

Chapter 9
Outlook
Though many optical eﬀects has been studied and identiﬁed in the Cosinodiscus granii
frustule, these are still only initial steps to understanding the interaction between light
and frustule. Studying diatom frustules in light at oblique incidence angles is, besides
this study, a virtually unexplored ﬁeld. As such there are some things that would be of
interest to study further.
To understand the interference points created by an incoherent light source better, it
would be interesting to use numerical simulations to predict the placement of interference
points with a coherent light source. This might give an insight in whether using an
incoherent light source aﬀects the position and periodicity of the interference peaks,
an important step towards potentially using diatoms as optical switches. Further, a
quantitative study of the interference points created by oblique angled illumination
would be interesting. Not only would it give more understanding of the interference
patterns, but it would provide an insight in the redirection of the light towards the
center of the frustule.
Another interesting area to study further is in-plane illumination of and collection of
light from the frustule. Potential bandgaps in the photonic crystal-like frustule layer
would be seen primarily with incidence light parallel to the frustule layers. Further, to
conﬁrm potential waveguiding in the frustule, collection of light at the edges or in the
plane of the frustule would be a good next step.
A proposed property in diatom frustules is the potential for UV shielding. As many
applications are suggested in the way of UV protection with diatom frustules, this would
be interesting to investigate further, with a setup optimized to illumination in the UV
region.
Finally, it is worth noting that all experiments in this thesis has been done with the
frustule in air. While this is interesting from a materials point of view, studying the
frustule suspended in water would be a big ﬁeld worth studying to get a better insight
in the biology of the frustule.
