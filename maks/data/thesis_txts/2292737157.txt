Chapter 3
Abstract
The demand for high eﬃciency converters is still increasing. The LLC resonant con-
verter can achieve both high eﬃciency and low noise at the same time because it has zero
voltage switching. But in order to achieve even higher eﬃciency, synchronous rectiﬁca-
tion must be applied. The implementation and control of the synchronous rectiﬁer are
discussed in this thesis. Analogue drivers for synchronous rectiﬁcation are controlling the
synchronous rectiﬁer by measuring a voltage. But higher eﬃciency can be achieved by
using digital control of the synchronous rectiﬁer. A digital control have been made with
the switching frequency as a feedback, and the operation have shown decent performance.

Chapter 4
Introduction
Normally you will use a laptop in a house or a building close to a power socket. But
what if you wish to use your laptop in the car which does not always come with a power
socket. Either you could buy a 12 VDC to 230 VAC inverter and use the charger that
came with the laptop. But then you have two energy conversions between the car battery
and the laptop. So a better solution will be converting the 12 VDC directly to the voltage
needed for the laptop, typically 19 VDC. The converter in this project is intended to use
the cigarette lighter plug that is to be found in every car nowadays.
This thesis will cover the use of synchronous rectiﬁcation in a LLC resonant converter.
Normally rectiﬁer diodes are used for rectiﬁcation on the secondary side. Since a diode has
a relative high forward voltage, the main contribution to conduction losses comes from
the rectiﬁer diodes. By replacing the diodes with MOSFETs the conduction losses are
tremendous reduced due to the low on resistance in the MOSFET. A typical schematic
with synchronous rectiﬁers can be seen in ﬁgure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Typical schematic of the half bridge LLC converter with synchronous rectiﬁers
[1]
In this project commercial drivers for synchronous rectiﬁcation will be tested in a LLC
converter and the results will be compared with use of Schottky diodes. The synchronous
rectiﬁer can be controlled by sensing the drain source voltage across the synchronous rec-
tiﬁer MOSFET. Commercial drivers for synchronous rectiﬁcation builds on this topology.
But the body diode in the MOSFET is conducting some current before and after the MOS-
FET have been on. Therefore a digital control where the synchronous rectiﬁer MOSFET
is controlled by the switching frequency will be designed. If the load and input voltage are
7
==PAGE:8==
constant, then the optimal ON time for the MOSFET will result in the highest switching
frequency and more important, highest attainable eﬃciency for that input voltage and
load. In this digital control only the turn oﬀtime is regulated according to the switching
frequency. The results will be compared with the commercial drivers available on the
market.
Since the digital control consist of an Arduino microcontroller (16 MHz) the character-
istics of the resonant converter will be accommodated for this. The magnetic components
will be designed in such a way that the losses are minimised.

Chapter 6
LLC Resonant Converter
The LLC converter got increasing popularity because of high eﬃciency, high power
density and low switching noise. A LLC converter is a resonant converter and consists of
a series inductor Lr, magnetising inductance of the transformer Lm and a capacitor Cr
hence the abbreviation LLC. These three components represent the resonant circuit of the
converter as illustrated in ﬁgure 6.1.
Figure 6.1: Typical power stage of a half bridge LLC converter [2]
The square wave generator can be either a half bridge or a full bridge. The current in
the half bridge is twice than the current in a full bridge, and the squared RMS current is
four times larger. But a half bridge only have two MOSFETs contrary to the full bridge
which have four, hence the MOSFET conduction losses in a half bridge is twice compared
to a full bridge. The diﬀerence between a full and a half bridge is shown in ﬁgure 6.2.
10
==PAGE:11==
Figure 6.2: Full bridge square wave generator to the Right. Half bridge to the Left [3]
In order to achieve the same voltage gain, the half bridge primary windings are half
compared to a full bridge. Although the half bridge primary winding resistance is the half,
the primary copper losses is twice compared to the full bridge due to the squared RMS
current in the half bridge is four times. For converters with high primary currents a full
bridge is recommended in order to reduce primary conduction losses.
On the secondary side the rectiﬁer can consist of either two or four diodes as can be
seen in ﬁgure 6.3.
Figure 6.3: To the left a Full bridge rectiﬁer. To the right a Full wave rectiﬁer [3]
The diodes in the full wave rectiﬁer have to handle twice the voltage compared to a
full bridge rectiﬁer, but it only needs two diodes instead of four. Since the average current
is the same for the two rectiﬁer circuits, the conducting losses for the full bridge rectiﬁer
is twice compared to the full wave rectiﬁer. The full wave rectiﬁer has two secondary
windings that are center tapped. This mean a double resistance for the same winding area
and therefore the copper losses is twice compared to a full bridge rectiﬁer. For high output
voltages the full bridge rectiﬁer is preferred since the diodes only handles the half voltage
compared to a full wave rectiﬁer. For low output voltages and high currents the full wave
rectiﬁer is preferred due to lower conduction losses and fewer diodes. The conduction losses
in diodes are high compared to the losses in the secondary windings. This is the reason
why synchronous rectiﬁcation will be investigated.
Due to this explanation about the
currents, it is decided to use a half bridge on the primary side and a full wave rectiﬁer on
the secondary side, since the output voltage is low and the currents in the converter is low.
The square wave generator operates with a constant duty cycle of 50 %. A small dead time

is inserted between the duty cycle to avoid shoot-through in the MOSFETs. The output
voltage is regulated by changing the switching frequency, whereas ﬂyback, forward con-

frequency.

6.1
First Harmonic Assumption
The ﬁrst harmonic assumption is a guideline when designing a resonant converter and
to ﬁnd its voltage gain function. The current in the resonant circuit is considered as a pure
sinusoidal. In practice this is almost true when the converter is operating at or close to the
resonant frequency. The ﬁrst harmonic assumption only use the fundamental harmonic of
the square wave, which mean all higher order harmonics are ignored. Further it is assumed
that the square wave duty cycle is 50%.
On the secondary side of the converter, the leakage inductance and the eﬀect of an
output capacitor must be ignored. The obtained secondary side variables must be referred
to the primary side. The referred secondary voltage ( Vso) and its current is referred with
the fundamental components only. Again no higher order harmonics.

Previous designed LLC converter
A LLC resonant converter has been made in a previous course and documented in a
technical report [4]. The application is a laptop charger with the same voltage and power
ratings as in Chapter 5.
This converter is based on a controller UCC25600 made by Texas Instruments. The
output voltage is controlled by the switching frequency and with a voltage feedback. The
voltage feedback consists of a voltage reference TL431 and an optocoupler which mean
the converter has galvanic isolation. An overcurrent protection is implemented as well
(OC). The converter is a half bridge and have normal Schottky diodes as rectiﬁers on the
secondary side, and the schematic can be seen in ﬁgure 7.1.
Some smart features are for example the adjustable dead time, which mean a lot of
diﬀerent MOSFETs can be used and the dead time can be set accordingly to the capaci-
tances and gate charges in a speciﬁc MOSFET. This design has a dead time of 200 ns. The
gate driver is connected to a transformer directly. The gate drive transformer has a ratio
of 1:1:1. The minimum switching frequency can be set as well. This feature can be used
to ensure that the converter is only operating in the inductive region, as shown in ﬁgure 6.6.

Chapter 8
Synchronous Rectiﬁcation and the
Objective
The beneﬁt of using synchronous rectiﬁer (SR) instead of diodes is the reduced con-
duction losses on the secondary side of the LLC converter. The conduction losses by using
diodes is proportional to the conducted forward current and the forward voltage drop.
By using synchronous rectiﬁer the voltage drop is much smaller due to the low RON in
MOSFETs. This on resistance is in the range of milliohms.
On ﬁgure 8.1 the loss breakdown of a LLC converter is shown. The converter is made
on Institute of Power Electronics at Zhejiang University [5].
Figure 8.1: Loss breakdown of a 4.8 W LLC converter [5]
It is very clear that the main contribution to the losses, comes from the rectiﬁer diodes
on the secondary side. By implementing the synchronous rectiﬁcation the secondary side
conduction losses can be tremendously reduced.
8.1
Timing the synchronous rectiﬁer
The main issue with the control of synchronous rectiﬁcation, is to set the optimal turn
oﬀtiming for the synchronous rectiﬁers. The duty cycle for the synchronous rectiﬁers
18
==PAGE:19==
depends on the switching frequency. Below resonant frequency the synchronous rectiﬁer
stops conducting before the MOSFET on the primary side is turned oﬀas can be seen in
ﬁgure 8.2 at time t1 and t4.
Figure 8.2: LLC converter operating below f0. Is stops conducting before t2 and t5
This mean the gate signal Vg Q1,2 cannot be used directly to control the synchronous
rectiﬁers. The Vg Q1,2 might be useful to set or sync the turn on time for the synchronous
rectiﬁer but not the turn oﬀtime.
In this thesis commercial drivers for synchronous rectiﬁer have been investigated and
tested in a LLC converter that was explained in Chapter 7.
A digital control for the synchronous rectiﬁcation have been designed in a way where
the turn oﬀtime will be adjusted according to the measured switching frequency. The
idea was to keep the input voltage and load constant. Then ﬁnd the ideal turn oﬀtime
which will require the smallest voltage gain. When the ideal turn oﬀtime is found, the
converter will operate at the highest switching frequency for that speciﬁc input voltage
and load. But more important, it does operate with highest eﬃciency.
With other words, the switching frequency was used as a feedback in order to ﬁnd the
optimal turn oﬀtime for the synchronous rectiﬁer. In that way the highest attainable
eﬃciency was achieved.

Chapter 9
FAN6208 Driver for Synchronous
Rectiﬁcation
The FAN6208 is a gate driver from Fairchild semiconductor. The driver is used to
control synchronous rectiﬁcation in a LLC converter driving two individual MOSFETs.
The SR is controlled by sensing the drain source voltage ( VDS) across both MOSFETs.
When the body diode in the MOSFET starts conducting the drain source voltage drops
to zero. The typical operation with FAN6208 can be seen in ﬁgure 9.1.
Figure 9.1: Typical operation with the FAN6208 SR driver [6]
DETL is the input pin and the drain source voltage is compared to a 2 V reference.
350 ns after the drain source voltage drops below 2 V, the MOSFET is turned on. This
delay is a sum of a debounce time (150 ns) and a propagation delay (200 ns). The con-
ducting duration is then measured ( VDS below 2V). The turn oﬀtime is then set by the
conduction duration of the previous swicting cycle, and a dead time is inserted. The dead
time is set by an external resistor and will be described later in this chapter. Since the
turn oﬀtiming is calculated from the previous cycle, there is a potential risk of shoot
through in the synchronous rectiﬁer MOSFETs if the switching frequency will increase
fast. Therefore a gate shrink function is included in the chip. The gate shrink is enabled
if the dead time is less than 125 ns, as can be seen in ﬁgure 9.2.
20
==PAGE:21==
Figure 9.2: 125 ns detection window for gate shrink function [6]
If an insuﬃcient dead time is detected the next cycle is shrunk by 1.25 µs. Another
shrink function occurs if the drain source voltage has ringing around zero. The synchronous
rectiﬁer duty cycle is shrunk by 1.2 µs if the ringing rises above 2 V within 350 ns after
VDS drops to zero, and the ringing stays above 2 V for more than 150 ns, as can be seen
in ﬁgure 9.3.
Figure 9.3: Gate shrinking activated because of voltage ringing [6]
RP Pin
The RP pin is connected to an external resistor. This pin serves two functions. First,
the resistance determines the dead time for the synchronous rectiﬁers and hence the turn
oﬀtime. Second, the driver can operate in two modes, a high and a low frequency mode.
If the external resistor connected to this pin is less than 36 kΩ, the driver operates in
the low frequency mode between 70 kHz and 140 kHz. The high frequency mode operates
between 160 kHz to 240 kHz.

frequency is above the capability of the driver, and the synchronous rectiﬁers are com-
pletely shut oﬀand the current is only running in the body diodes. This results in lower
eﬃciency than by use of Schottky diodes, which indicates that the synchronous rectiﬁer
body diodes have higher forward voltage than the Schottky diodes, leading to higher losses
in the rectiﬁers when the MOSFET is oﬀ.

Chapter 10
SRK2000 Driver for Synchronous
rectiﬁcation
The SRK2000 is a driver for synchronous rectiﬁcation produced by ST. The purpose
of the driver is to turn on the synchronous rectiﬁer MOSFET as soon as current starts
ﬂowing in the half winding, and turn it oﬀagain as the current approaches zero. In order
to accurately time the synchronous rectiﬁer MOSFETs, the Drain Voltage Sensing Pins
(DVS) are introduced. The gate drivers have an interlocking function to avoid both gate
drivers are on at a time, and the risk of shoot through in the MOSFETs are reduced. The
typical operation with the SRK2000 can be seen in ﬁgure 10.1.
Figure 10.1: Typical operation with the SRK2000 SR driver [7]
The turn oﬀtime can be slightly delayed by adding a capacitor to the input pin DVS
(Drain Voltage Sensing). But care must be taken because if the MOSFET is turned oﬀ
after the drain source voltage becomes positive, the current will ﬂow from drain to source
and there will be a potential risk of shoot through, since the body diode in the opposite
MOSFET has started conducting. The eﬀect of adding a capacitor to the DVS pin is
explained in section 10.2.

IDVS1,2On is activated as it exceeds 50 µA.
The turn on threshold voltage is:
−VTH−ON = −(RD · IDV S1,2On) −VDV S1,2−Th = −(2kΩ· 50µA) −0.2V = −0.3V (10.3)
A debounce delay of 250 ns is inserted before turning on the MOSFET ( TPD On), which
means that IDVS1,2On must exceed 50 µA for 250 ns. Since the MOSFET body diode is
conducting before turning on the MOSFET, zero voltage switching for the synchronous
rectiﬁer is achieved.
The turn oﬀtiming is controlled by the Enable pin which has a comparator that sets
the turn oﬀvoltage at either -12.5 mV or -25 mV. A simple pull-up resistor determines
the turn oﬀthreshold voltage. If the pull-up resistor has a value larger than 633 kΩthe
threshold voltage is set at -25 mV. A pull-up resistor smaller than 296 kΩwill set the
threshold voltage at -12 mV.

But the Enable pin could serve as an undervoltage detection as well. This requires a
voltage divider consisting of two resistors. If the undervoltage threshold is triggered then

As the drain source voltage reaches above 1.4 V the opposite gate driver is armed. This

Custom Gate Driver
In this chapter a custom made gate driver will be designed and tested. The driver
UCC25600 on the primary side controls the half bridge square wave generator as can be
seen in ﬁgure 6.1.
The idea is to use this gate signal to synchronise the synchronous
rectiﬁers.
The control will consist of logic gates and comparators for the synchronous rectiﬁer
gate driver. A comparator will set the turn on time for the synchronous rectiﬁers, and
another comparator sets the turn oﬀtime. Both signals are feed into an AND gate and
the output goes into a gate driver which drives the synchronous rectiﬁers. The schematic
can be seen in ﬁgure 13.1
Figure 13.1: LTspice schematic of the custom made gate driver for simulation
The ﬁrst part consists of a series resistor R2. The signal is feed into a comparator and
compared with a variable DC voltage. The square wave will have some rise time and by
setting the DC reference a delay can be inserted. By adjusting Vt on, the turn on time can
be adjusted. The output signal from U3 is feed into R1 and C1 which acts as an integrator
and the output is a simple triangle wave. But this time the output of the comparator is
inverted. The turn oﬀtime is set by Vt oﬀand by adjusting this voltage the turn oﬀtime
can be changed. Finally the turn on and oﬀsignals are feed into an AND gate and the
output is sent directly into a gate driver for the synchronous rectiﬁer (not illustrated).

Chapter 14
Digital control of the synchronous
rectiﬁers
Since I have gathered some experience with the Arduino and C++ language the Ar-
duino Uno is chosen for the digital control of the synchronous rectiﬁer. Because of the
relative low clock of only 16 MHz the control of synchronous rectiﬁer will be both analogue
and digital combined. The schematic can be seen in ﬁgure 14.1.
The analogue part will take care of the synchronisation and ensures that none oﬀthe
synchronous rectiﬁers are on at the same time. Actually it is the same gate driver as in
Chapter 13 but the turn oﬀtiming is now controlled by the Arduino microcontroller with
a PWM output.
The software architecture can be seen in ﬁgure 14.2 as a ﬂowchart. The ﬁrst thing the
microcontroller does is an initialization of the PWM output with a 63% duty cycle (2).
All steps are numbered in the upper right corner. This duty cycle has been chosen because
it will create a safe condition at start-up as can be seen in ﬁgure 14.4. The PWM output
serves as a voltage reference for the U3 and U6 comparators shown in ﬁgure 14.1, and will
set the turn oﬀtiming.
The Arduino Uno has a 8-bit timer for the PWM output which mean a counter reso-
lution of 28 = 256. The voltage reference for the microcontroller is 5 V so each voltage
step is 5V/256 = 19.5 mV. This has shown to be a too large voltage step for the system
and the current on the secondary side is oscillating up and down in magnitude and a
higher counter resolution is needed for the PWM output. Therefore the Arduino Mega is
considered because it has 16-bit timers, able to generate fast PWM. In this case i have
chosen to conﬁgure a 11-bit PWM output with a counter resolution of 2048. The voltage
step size is then 2.4 mV. The PWM signal is smoothed out into a DC voltage reference
for the comparators U3 and U6.
Once the PWM is initialized the microcontroller will measure the switching frequency
(4). The microcontroller has a function called input capture. The input capture is trig-
gered on an external rising edge. The timer/counter will set a time stamp when an rising
edge is captured. This time stamp can then be used to calculate frequency or duty cycle.
56
==PAGE:57==
Figure 14.1: Combined digital and analogue control of the gate driver
57
==PAGE:58==
Figure 14.2: Software ﬂowchart of the digital control
The calculated frequency can reach higher precision if more rising edges are captured,
and the average time of a single period can be found. Observations have shown that 30
rising edges will create a quite stable calculation of the switching frequency. Figure 14.3
shows a screenshot of the terminal window with the calculated frequency, and it can be
seen that the calculated frequency is quite constant.
A variable contains the previous calculated frequency and the new calculated frequency
is compared with this. If the calculated frequency is higher than the previously calculated
frequency, then the new frequency is loaded into another variable that contains the highest
frequency (5). After that the PWM output will be incremented by 1 step (6).
At some point the optimal turn oﬀpoint is found, and the calculated frequency is
equal to the variable that determines the highest frequency, and the PWM output is not
incremented any further. The code then enters a new loop that compares the calculated
frequency with the stored value for the highest obtained frequency (7). If the input volt-
age and load is constant and the optimal turn oﬀpoint is found, then the software only

switches between case 7 and 8. But if the calculated frequency diﬀers with more than ±
40 Hz, then the PWM duty cycle is decreased (9), and the loop starts all over again at

by using frequency as feedback is that the SR driver basically is self learning, in order to

Improvements to the converter
In this chapter some changes made on the converter will be explained. These changes
have risen the overall converter eﬃciency. The ﬁrst thing is the zener diode for the op-
tocoupler in the voltage feedback. Its purpose is to supply the LED in the optocoupler
with a clean DC voltage. A series resistor is mounted in front of the zener diode and the
diode will sink some current in order to keep the output voltage constant (reverse voltage).
The converter has shown that the output voltage is stable enough in order to supply the
optocoupler. In this way a resistor and zener diodes are saved, and same goes for some
current which will lead into increase of eﬃciency.
The LLC converter from the previous project where mounted with 150 Ωgate resistors
at the square-wave generator. The self heat generation was quite big and the gate resistors
where exchanged with 10 Ωresistors. At the same time a capacitor is mounted in series
with the primary side of the gate drive transformer.
With regard to the magnetic components an interleaved transformer have been designed
and implemented. The interleaving was made in a way that the magneto-motive force is
smallest, resulting in less AC copper losses in the windings.

better than the FAN6208. The last mentioned driver has a limited frequency band of

Chapter 18
Conclusion
A LLC resonant converter have been made with synchronous rectiﬁcation. It has shown
that the overall eﬃciency have been increased with synchronous rectiﬁcation compared
to operation with Schottky diodes.
With the SRK2000 SR driver the eﬃciency have
increased by whole 3.8%. Eﬃciency went from 83.8% to 87.6%. The SR driver FAN6208
have increased the eﬃciency as well, but the driver has a limited frequency bandwidth
which mean the synchronous rectiﬁers are deactivated at low load conditions. The highest
eﬃciency with the FAN6208 is 87.1% at full load.
The resonant circuit was redesigned in such a way that the voltage gain function
became more ﬂat. This was necessary since the microcontroller has a relatively low clock
frequency of 16MHz. The transformer have been made with interleaving layers which
keeps the losses to a minimum. The switching frequency have been moved down from
190 kHz to 90 kHz and the inductor ratio was increased from 3 to 9.
The digital control of the synchronous rectiﬁer was made and the performance did
show excellent results. The digital control was able to produce even higher eﬃciency than
the commercial SR drivers. The eﬃciency with the digital control did reach 93.6% for the
power stage. The power consumption for the combined analogue and digital part is not
included. If this power is included then the eﬃciency of the digital control is lower than
the commercial drivers. With the SRK2000 the highest eﬃciency is 92.6% and 92.7% for
the FAN6208. Both at 13 V input and with the new resonant circuit.
With a new resonant circuit and the digital SR control the eﬃciency is increased by
nearly 10% compared to the LLC converter from a previous project with Schottky diodes.
And by adjusting the dead time 94% eﬃciency is attainable. But this does not include
the SR driver power consumption.

Future work
The custom made driver for the synchronous rectiﬁer have been made with analogue
components. The idea of operation is simple but the actual circuit counts a lot of compo-
nents as can be seen in ﬁgure 19.1. All the analogue ICs will draw current and reduce the
overall eﬃciency.
Figure 19.1: Combined analogue and digital gate driver for synchronous rectiﬁer
If the driver in the future is going to be made, then it is obvious to design it as a
digital SR driver. The digital control can be seen in ﬁgure 19.2.
Figure 19.2: Digital gate driver for synchronous rectiﬁer
If the CPU clock frequency of the microcontroller is high enough, then a PWM output
can drive the discrete gate driver directly. Lets for example say that the driver is capable of
switching frequencies up to 200 kHz. The rectiﬁed current has the double frequency than
the switching frequency, so it is maximum 400 kHz, and the minimum PWM resolution is
256 steps (8-bit). The minimum clock frequency is then:

The AND gate serves as a protecting against both synchronous rectiﬁer MOSFETs are
on at the same time. In order to achieve high eﬃciency with low density the switching
frequency must be high. This will require a high clock for the microcontroller and will
increase the overall cost.
Therefore the third design takes origin in the combined analogue and digital driver. But
the comparators will be replaced with low power, rail to rail comparators that do not need
pull up resistors. The AND gate may be a low power as well.

This design does not require a high clock frequency for the microcontroller, because the
PWM output is smoothen into a DC voltage anyway. The microcontroller will only need
two timers. 16-bit preferably. One timer for the input capture to measure the frequency. A
second timer is used for the PWM output that sets the turn oﬀtiming for the synchronous
rectiﬁer.
But since the input comes directly from the primary side driver, the galvanic isolation
is no longer achieved.
This can be solved with two extra windings in the gate drive
transformer. The beneﬁt is the voltage can be converted according to the ICs used for the
SR driver. The solution with galvanic isolation can be seen in Appendix C. Finally the
software for the microcontroller will need some ﬁne-tuning.
