Summary (English)
This thesis shall explore the concept of α-β privacy for deﬁning privacy goals
and verifying privacy properties of security protocols. In a nutshell, the idea is
to specify for each state of the protocol two formulae α and β in ﬁrst-order logic
with Herbrand universes, where α contains the information that intentionally
have been released, and β contains the cryptographic messages that an intruder
can obtain.
There are three aspects of the thesis to explore: ﬁrst to model
and manually verify one or more protocols that are concerned with privacy.
Second is identifying a fragment of the used logic for which we can automate
the analysis, and make if possible a user-friendly connection to the popular Alice
and Bob notation. Third is then a prototype of an automated procedure for the
identiﬁed fragment, possibly further restricting the class of expressions that can
be handled.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The problem of formally describing privacy goals is no trivial matter. To address
the problem the concept of α-β privacy was introduced in [MGV13]. This thesis
will utilize α-β privacy, in an attempt to make an automated procedure, which
can reason about privacy goals of a protocol, that has been speciﬁed using α-β
privacy. The ﬁrst step towards achieving this, is to get a proper understanding
of the Herbrand logic [HG06], which is used to express the formulas α and β that
is the essence of α-β privacy. In short the idea is, that α formulates information
which is intentionally released when a user uses the formulated protocol, and β
formulates technical data an intruder can obtain from the use of the protocol.
Privacy is then respected if the intruder cannot derive any further information,
than that given in α, from β.
The thesis focuses on protocols which can be formulate as a message analy-
sis problem. The Second step, is then to identify the essential aspects of an
evaluation on β in a message analysis, in order to uncover data structures and
functions, which is key to automating the evaluation. This is done by evaluating
example models by hand, and describing how the uncovered functions should in-
tentionally work. The uncovered data structures and functions will then have to
be formally speciﬁed, such that it is possible to reason whether or not an imple-
mentation follows the theory. The implementation of the automated procedure
will be preformed in the functional programming language Haskell.

Chapter 2
Theory
The automated procedure, which will be speciﬁed and implemented through the
course of the thesis, will aim to argue whether or not privacy goals, formulated
in α-β privacy, is violated. α-β privacy is introduced in [MGV13] and this theory
chapter will provide a summery of the content in [MGV13]. The content of the
chapter should therefor be regarded as background knowledge for the thesis, and
not part of its contribution. All credit for the content of the theory chapter,
goes to the authors of [MGV13].
The main idea of α-β privacy, is to describe privacy goals by specifying two
formulas α and β. α formalizes intentionally released information i.e. informa-
tion that can legitimately be given to the intruder, which will be referred to
as payload. As an example consider a voting scheme. A user may choose from
a well known pool of possible votes, without releasing any information of the
actual vote he chose. β then formalizes the technical information which is the
"actual knowledge" that the intruder has. This includes names, keys etc. that
the intruder initially has, the cryptographic messages he has observed and what
he knows about these messages. He might be unable to decrypt a message, but
will anyway know that it has a certain format and contains certain (protected)
information, like a vote. α-β privacy then means that the intruder cannot derive
any interesting "non-technical" statement from β that he cannot derive from α
already.

Herbrand Logic
To formalize the approach, an appropriate logic had to be
chosen. Using standard ﬁrst-order logic (FOL) would result in problems when
one would have the interpret constants and cryptographic operators. Instead
the choice fell upon [HG06] FOL with Herbrand Universes which can be seen
as a logic in its own right. This is justiﬁed both in [HG06] and in example 2 of
[MGV13]. Deﬁnition 2.1 formulates the syntax of Hebrand Logic, deﬁnition 2.2
formulates the Herbrand Universe and Algebra, and deﬁnition 2.3 formulates
the Semantics of Herbrand Logic which are all given in [MGV13]. There are
however a few diﬀerences between the deﬁnition of Herbrand Logic in [MGV13]
and [HG06], a discussion of thees diﬀerences are given in [MGV13].
Definition 2.1 ([MGV13, page 4]) (Syntax of Herbrand Logic.) Let
Σf ⊎Σi ⊎Σr be an alphabet that consists of a set Σf of free function symbols,
a set Σi of interpreted function symbols and a set Σr of relation symbols, all
with their arities. To distinguish we denote the set of considered cryptographic
operators by the subset Σop ⊆Σf. Constants are the special case of free func-
tion symbols with arity 0.
Let V be a countable set of variable symbols, disjoint from Σ. We denote with
TΣ(V) the set of all terms that can be built from the function symbols in Σ
and the variables in V. We simply write TΣ when V = ∅, and call its elements
ground terms (over signature Σ).
We deﬁne the set LΣ(V) of formulae over the alphabet Σ and the variables V as
usual: relations and equality of terms are atomic formulae, and composed for-
mulae are built using conjunction ∧, negation ¬ and existential quantiﬁcation
∃.
As an inclusion to deﬁnition 2.1, the standard syntactical sugar are included,
for example ∀x.φ for ¬∃x.¬φ.
We also write x ∈{t1, . . . , tn} to abbreviate
x = t1 ∨. . . ∨x = tn. The function fv returns the set of free variables of a
formula.
Definition 2.2 ([MGV13, page 5]) (Herbrand Universe and Alge-
bra). Formulae in Herbrand logic are always interpreted with respect to a given
ﬁxed set Σf of free symbols (since this set may contain symbols that do not oc-
cur in the formulae) and a congruence relation ≈on TΣf . we may annotate all
notions of the semantics with Σf and ≈when it is not clear from the context.
we write [t]≈= {t′ ∈TΣf |t ≈t′} to denote the equivalence class of a term
t ∈TΣf with respect to ≈. Further, let U = {[t]≈|t ∈TΣf } be the set of all
equivalence classes. We call U the Herbrand universe (since it is freely generated
by the function symbols of Σf modulo ≈). Based on U, we deﬁne a Σf-algebra A
that interprets every n-ary function symbol f ∈Σf as a function f A : U n →U
in the following standard way. f A([t1]≈, . . . , [tn]≈) = [f(t1, . . . , tn)]≈where the
choice of the representatives t1, . . . , tn of the equivalence classes is irrelevant

Definition 2.3 ([MGV13, page 5]) (Semantics of Herbrand Logic).
An interpretation I maps every interpreted function symbol f ∈Σi of arity n

We extend I to a function on TΣ(V) as expected: I(f(t1, . . . , tn)) = f A(I(t1), . . . , I(tn))
for f ∈Σf and I(f[t1, . . . , tn]) = I(f)(I(t1), . . . , I(tn))

Payload and Technical Information
Recall that α will formulate the in-
tentionally released information known as the payload, and β will formulate
the technical information in the intruders knowledge. α-β privacy then means
==PAGE:18==
8
Theory
that the intruder cannot derive any interesting "non-technical" statement from
β that he cannot derive from α already. This seems as a legit statement, ex-
cept for the use of the word "interesting". [MGV13] are able to deﬁne the term
"interesting" on a logical basis, in deﬁnition 2.5 by utilizing that the payload
α may be formulate over a restricted alphabet Σ0 ⊊Σ, whereas β may use the
full alphabet Σ.
Definition 2.5 ([MGV13, page 9]) Let Σ0 ⊊Σ. given a payload for-
mula α ∈LΣ0(V) and a technical formula β ∈LΣ(V), where β |= α and
fv(α) = fv(β) and both α and β are consistent, we say that a statement
α′ ∈LΣ0(fv(α)) is an interesting deviation from β (with respect to α) if β |= α′
but α ̸|= α′. We say that β respects the privacy of α if the intruder cannot
derive any interesting statements from β, and that β violates the privacy of α
otherwise.
Deﬁnition 2.5 introduces α′ as an interesting derivation, which an intruder can
derive from his observations on β as long as it is a non-technical statement of
LΣ0 and it does not follow from α alone. This allows a model to capture that
an intruder may gain knowledge of some technical details, but as long as thees
details cannot be tied to a relevant information α′, then it does not matter.
Privacy on Message
Now that the logic have been formulated, lets see how
it can be applied, to ask whether an intruder can violate privacy in a ﬁxed
state of a complex system. Consider example 2.1 from [MGV13], note that that
the cryptographic operators have been changed to the ones used in the thesis
instead of those from [MGV13].
Example 2.1 ([MGV13, page 10]) Let the payload alphabet be Σ0 = {a, b, c}
and let us model that users choose values x from Σ0. This is the only informa-
tion we want to give the intruder. Suppose there is a protocol in place where
each user sends out a message oneWay(pair(n,x)) that the intruder can observe,
that is, a hash of the choice x and a ﬁxed number n (that is a secret from Σ\Σ0).
Obvioulsy, using such a ﬁxed number, even though secret from the intruder, is
a risk for "guessing attacks". Suppose further that the intruder has previously
observed the message oneWay(pair(n,a)) and thus that he knows that the choice
in this case was a. Let us ﬁnally assume that a user has chosen x =b and thus
sent out oneWay(pair(n,b))
Example 2.1 refers to both concrete messages like oneWay(pair(n,a)) and struc-
tural messages like oneWay(pair(n,x)) where x is the choice that is interesting
to investigate. As deﬁned, use the function cocnr to map concrete messages to

Before any eveluation is preformed on example 3.1, some basic data structures
for representing α and β will clearly be needed.
A Message data structure
for representing constants, free variables and compositions expressed by the
cryptographic operators speciﬁed in table 2.1 is essential to build terms. The
data structure for β, would need to hold both the structural messages and its
corresponding concrete messages in the same memory locations.

and a Substitution data structure, which represents an interpretation, with
respect to α, of the free variables as it is deﬁned in deﬁnition 2.3. Applying the

Message:
The Message data structure must implement the cryptographic
functions deﬁned in table 2.1 by implementing the constructors from the ta-
ble. The deconstructors and veriﬁers will not be needed here, as decomposition
of messages can be formulated by the decomposition rules in table 2.2, exactly
how decomposition of messages is achieved will be explained in the implementa-
tion of the function analyze in chapter 4. Because the cryptographic operators
are formulated in a recursive fashion, Message will be implemented as a tree
structure, with constants and variables in the leaf nodes, and the constructors
of the cryptographic operators as the internal notes.
Message = Constant || Variable || Function
The Constant and Variable are of the type String and Function have the
following structure, where [] denotes a List.
Function = TransparentFunction([Message]) || OneWayFunction(Message) ||
PublicKey(Message) || PrivatKey(Message) ||
Crypt(Message,Message) || Scrypt(Message,Message) ||
Sign(Message,Message)
Beta:
Beta will represent the formula β of a model in Hebran logic. This is
represented by a List of Messages, referring to the structural messages in β.
The index of a Message in the List, will denote the memory location of the
message in β. It is therefor extremely important that elements of the List will
not be shuﬄed around at any point. Alterations of the list, may only occur
in the form of concatenations to end of the List, since this will not aﬀect the
indexing of the original, but simply extend it with additional elements. It is
acknowledge, that Beta does not directly represent the axioms of the Hebrand
logic in β, but will instead be reﬂected in the functions from 3.4 which operate
on Beta. Also note that Beta does not hold the concrete messages directly,
but thees can be obtained by applying a Substitution to Beta, changing the
messages from structural messages to concrete. See Substitution for a further
explanation.

In order to easily use and test the implemented functions, and check that the
outputs in fact are as expected, there need to be an easy way to formulate an
α-β model of a protocol, as it is deﬁned in deﬁnition 2.6. For that purpose the
popular Alice and Bob notation for describing security protocols, by formulating
messages sent from one user to another, pops into mind. However, the Alice
and Bob notation is not directly applicable to describe an α-β model.
The notion of Alice A, is sending a message M, to Bob B, such that A →B : M
is not really used by the α-β model, formulated in deﬁnition 2.6. Since the
α-β privacy veriﬁcation module, does not deal with authentication of users, and
there is no notion of how messages was obtained, i.e. only by a passive intruder
or by being active interfering with the communication between users. The only
interesting fact from A →B : M regarding the α-β model, is that an intruder is
able to obtain M. M will be some concrete message that was sent from Alice to
Bob, but the α-β model will also need to know the structure of M, as the intruder
knows the structure of all concrete messages by assumption. Furthermore, a way
of formulating the possible choices of the free variables, which is deﬁned in α,
is not standard in Alice and Bob notation, but will be needed.
As a consequence of thees diﬀerences, the classical Alice and Bob notation will
not be used for the formulation of protocols, and a notation with the following
structure is used instead. A protocol description will have a name Protocol

Conclusion
In this thesis, it was attempted to make an automated procedure, for verifying
privacy goals of protocols, formulated with concepts of α-β privacy. The attempt
was successful and the procedure alphaBetaMain is able to ﬁnd violations of
privacy, by comparison and composition of messages formulate in the formula
β, which represents an intruders knowledge.
Through evaluation of example models by hand, it was investigated what func-
tions and data structures that would be essential, to implement an automated
procedure of the evaluation. The data structures was compared to and formu-
lated from the models of α-β privacy, such that they properly reﬂect the theory.
In this process, the modeling of α was restricted to a mapping from free vari-
ables to the possible interpretations of thees variables and it was mentioned that
a more complex modeling of alpha could be preformed as further work, to get
a more sophisticated automated procedure. Furthermore, it was assumed that
encryption of messages can not happen with keys that contains free variables
and that composition of public private key pairs can not be preformed by an
intruder. Both of thees assumptions was also suggested as a possible candi-
dates for further work. The functions discovered through the investigation, was
speciﬁed formally, in order to reason that the implementation behaves properly
compared to the formulation of α-β privacy, and that violations of privacy was
in fact found in a correct way, by comparison of messages in β.
