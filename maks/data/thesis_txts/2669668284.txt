Abstract
In recent years the application of Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI) techniques to various types
of data has increased dramatically. Many projects have indicated that speciﬁc AI tools
show promising results on satellite data of several kinds. Sea ice monitoring in the Arctic
region is particular ﬁeld of applied science which has been investigating the performance
of such tools on satellite data, enhanced by the increasing amount of freely available
Synthetic Aperture Radar(SAR) data. Currently, satellite data for the Arctic region,
and importantly in the present context for Greenland, undergo a manual interpretation
and segmentation of SAR images by expert ice analysts, dividing the images into regions
of Sea Ice Concentration (SIC).
Manual sea ice charting is a highly time-consuming process, which makes it subopti-
mal for operational use in dynamic sea ice regions. This motivates the creation of a
robust AI classiﬁcation tool that can provide fast and reliable segmentation maps, as-
sisting the ice analyst, and which eventually may provide unassisted and automated
sea ice classiﬁcation. Recent studies have shown that Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNNs), applied to the ﬁeld of Semantic Segmentation, can provide promising results in
the classiﬁcation of Sentinel-1 SAR data.
The AI4Arctic/ASIP Sea Ice Dataset - version 2 (ASID-v2) contains SAR images and
corresponding sea ice charts established by the Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI),
and it is used to train and to evaluate severald AI architectures. This dataset along with
its predecessor have previously been used for similar investigations, the present project
di↵ers from previous studies by exclusively utilizing Sentinel-1 SAR data form ASID-v2,
and not the temperature brightness data from the instrument AMSR2, which are also
included in the dataset. Instead, it investigates several Semantic Segmentation archi-
tectures, including variations of the wellknown U-Net, the Residual U-Net(ResUNet)
proposed by Zhengxin Zhang (2017) and colleagues (2017), and the ResUNet++ intro-
duced by Debesh Jha and colleagues (2019).
The primary objective of this thesis has been to produce an AI model which provides
a segmented sea ice map when given a SAR scene. Although the sea ice charts in the
dataset have an inherent uncertainty particularly in the intermediate SIC, the aim is to
reproduce these ice charts largest extent possible.
In this thesis, ﬁve di↵erent CNN architectures are introduced and applied to the problem
of creating sea ice charts, and their performance is assessed using the several current
measures of accuracy. Each of these architectures are seen to have their pros and cons,
and their performance are not strikingly di↵erent, However, it turns out that a partic-
ular network architecture, which is variation of the U-Net, denoted UNet 2, achieved
the highest precision, so that this particular model is subjected to further tests and
assessments.
As an overall result of the project, it may be concluded that the use of AI techniques
in the context of sea ice monitoring seems to be coming closer as the development of
these techniques progress, and the automatization of sea ice chart production may be
achieved in the not so distant future.

Chapter 1
Introduction
For decades, operational sea ice monitoring in the Arctic has been performed by ex-
pert ice analysts through manual interpretation and segmentation of Synthetic Aper-
ture Radar (SAR) images. This procedure, referred to as sea ice charting, is highly
time-consuming and not optimal for operational use in dynamic sea ice regions. The
increasing amount of freely available SAR data covering the Arctic Ocean o↵ers the
possibility of delivering ice chart products faster and more frequently, covering larger
geographical areas. Recent studies show that Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)
might o↵er a promising way forward in the design of an automatic and robust sea ice
classiﬁcation scheme.
The present project aims at investigating further the potential of CNN techniques to
perform open water discrimination and sea ice classiﬁcation from satellite SAR images
in the Arctic Ocean. It follows the line of similar conducted investigations and ongoing
examinations carried out by e.g. Automated Sea Ice Products (ASIP) and the AI4Arctic
project. It makes use of previous experience and provides new information within the
usage of Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI) to the analysis of sea ice satellite data. The anno-
tated AI4Arctic/ASIP Sea Ice Dataset - version 2 (ASID-v2) containing 461 SAR scenes
and corresponding sea ice charts, will be the foundation for all Machine Learning(ML)
training and testing procedures.
Manually produced sea ice charts are at the current stage comprised of multiple exten-
sive polygons which portray the sea ice concentration in the region they represent. The
main objective of this thesis is to develop one or several robust Neural Networks (NN)
that resemble the existing polygons to the greatest extent possible. More speciﬁcally,
these networks will utilize the concept of semantic segmentation to provide a per-pixel
classiﬁcation of a SAR data input, using the Danish Meteorological Institute(DMI) sea
ice concentration label scheme. Such automatized sea ice charting would be a consider-
able step forward in the ﬁeld of sea ice monotoring: Where an ice analysts would use
hours dividing a SAR data into di↵erent sea ice concentrations, the NN would do it in
few minutes. Existing similar projects have been conducted in recent years. The present
project, however, di↵ers from previous studies in examining beneﬁts of several di↵erent
NN architectures as well as by investigating their performance on the ASID-v2.
Previous studies have used Advanced Microwave Scanning Radiometer 2(AMSR2) bright-
ness temperature data as a complement to the SAR data. This project aims at inves-
tigating how well a NN can be trained without using brightness temperature data, and
to which extent di↵erent NN architectures can be optimized for SAR data only. The
aspiration is to detach a model’s need for AMSR2 data, so that only SAR images will be
required for the automated sea ice chart production, something which will be a major
advantage as co-located data will not be necessary.

1.1
Structure
In Chapter 2, the physical background knowledge required to interpret the ASID-v2 is
outlined. The chapter will initially introduce sea ice and the di↵erent types of sea ice.
Thereafter the satellite imagery instrument of SAR will be explained, along with di↵erent
scattering and backscattering mechanisms which are crucial for the SAR system. Finally,
the signatures of di↵erent sea ice types in SAR images will be pointed out and discussed.
The following Chapter 3 introduces the concepts of DL and NNs. Initially, the prin-
ciples of predicting and learning of simple networks will be explained, followed by an
introduction of more sophisticated networks such as CNN, which are particularly suited
used for image analysis. Thereafter, the concept of semantic segmentation for pixel-wise
image classiﬁcation will be presented together with several approaches for solving such
image classiﬁcation problems. The methods include newer and older CNN architectures,
namely the UNet from 2015, the ResUNet from 2017, and the ResUNet++ from 2019.
The next chapter, Chapter 4, introduces ASID-v2 and explain the parts of the dataset
which will be utilized for the training and testing of the network architectures to be
developed. The type of SAR data, the time and location of the acquisition, and the
temporal and spatial coverage will be clariﬁed. Each data ﬁle of the dataset contains a
SAR scene and a corresponding DMI sea ice concentration chart with per-pixel labeling
which will be introduced as well.
The chapter will also include a detailed explanation of the pre-processing steps carried
out on the data to prepare the data and modify it to a format appropriate for a neural
network, along with a discussion of certain design choices.
Chapter 5 explains the implementation in the Python framework. Initially, the soft-
ware and hardware will be introduced brieﬂy. Thereafter, each of the 5 architectures
chosen for investigation in the thesis and their merits will be explained along with the
way that they are implemented in Python.
The next chapter, Chapter 6, displays the results of models of the di↵erent architec-
tures after they have been fully trained. First, the models of each architecture will be
examined individually by their performance on a test set. Then, their performance will
be visualized on 6 selected regions of di↵erent coastal parts of Greenland.
1.2
Objectives
The primary objective of this thesis is to establish and implement several NN archi-
tectures, which can utilize Sentinel-1 SAR scenes as input and output fully segmented
maps of of sea ice concentrations (SIC)s. To achieve this goal several sub-objectives are
deﬁned, which in turn gradually approach the main objective.
Step 1: To investigate and select several NN architectures appropriate for sea ice
classiﬁcation
Step 2: To implement and train the relevant architectures using the ASID-v2
Step 3: To intercompare the established architectures and evaluate their performances
Step 4: To asses which architecture provided the best results

1.3
Scope
The new dataset ASID-v2 was released in Fall 2020 by a collaboration of the Danish
Metrology Institute and The Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The dataset con-
tains 461 Sentinel-1 SAR images with corresponding sea ice chart labels.
Prior to initiating this project, the author had no knowledge of DL and only little ex-
perience in the subject of Remote Sensing. Therefore, much time was devoted in the
early stages of the project to get acquainted with both subjects to a reasonable extent.
Similar projects using Sentinel-1 SAR data and AMSR2 brightness temperature from
the ASID-v2 and ASID-v1 have been conducted in previous years. This thesis will inves-
tigate how well one or several NN can be optimized only using ASID-v2 SAR data, and
thereby investigate whether use of AMSR2 brightness temperature data can be omitted.

Chapter 2
Physical background
2.1
Sea Ice
Sea ice, as opposed to icebergs, glaciers, and ice sheets, originates from regions at sea,
not on land. It forms, grows and melts in the ocean in the polar regions, see [64]. It
forms during the winter of the hemisphere and partly melts during the summer periods,
and plays a major role in the polar ecosystem. Signiﬁcantly, it plays an important role
in the albedo, which is a measure of surface reﬂectance, of the polar regions, which not
only a↵ects the polar climate but also global climate. Moreover, it releases nutrients
into the water when melting, and helps the circulation and mixture of the water, see
[29].
Since sea ice is produced in the ocean, it is formed from salty sea water, which also is a
main di↵erence from land ice. The freezing point of sea water is lower as a consequence
of the concentration of salinity, also known as freezing point depression. Consequently,
the ambient air temperature must reach a lower point in order to freeze the ocean water.
In polar regions the ocean salinity is roughly about 32 grams per kilogram sea water, so
that the surface temperature must be cooled to about −1.8◦C [45] before ice formation
can start. Secondly, as the temperature of the salty ocean water decreases, the density
increases, unlike the density of freshwater which increases. Consequently, much ocean
water sinks before being cold enough to freeze to solid ice. Nevertheless, a very large area
is covered by sea ice every winter in the polar seas and neighboring oceans. In Figure
2.1 the Arctic region both at winter’s end in March and at summer’s end in September
is displayed, both from 2013.
From the ﬁgure it is apparent that the extent of the sea ice varies signiﬁcantly through-
out the seasons of the year. It can be seen that during the winter much of the coastline

2.1.1
First-year ice (FYI)
Sea ice forms under several di↵erent conditions. In calm water conditions long ice crystals
start forming on the ocean surface, and they have a very thin and smooth surface. As
these crystals grow they become unstable, and at some point they break up and form
smaller pieces, creating a soupy suspension, an ice type called frazil or grease ice [44].
For calm conditions these frazil crystals start fusing together to form thin ice sheets,
called a nilas. At some point a process called congelation growth will start taking place,
where water molecules freeze on to the bottom the newly formed ice sheet. At this
point the sea ice reaches the category of ﬁrst-year ice, which in a single season can reach
thicknesses of up to 2 m[12]. Sea ice growth is highest at the early thin stages, and slows
down as the ice grows thicker.
In rough waters the new ice maintains the frazil crystal state, rather than evolving into
nilas.
However, due to the particle orbits in the wave ﬁelds, the crystals eventually
freeze together to form small chunks of slush, which by accreting more crystals progress
to so-called pancake ice. These pancakes are very thin near the edges, and can reach up
to 70 cm near the center[12].
2.1.2
Multi-year ice (MYI)
If sea ice survives one or more summers, the ice is called second-year ice and eventually
multi-year ice (MYI)[45]. More than half of the ice in the Arctic is MYI [64]. Multi-year
ice continues to grow winter after winter until a thickness of about 3 meters is reach,
at this point the volume increase in the winter corresponds to the melting loss in the
summer, see [64].
As MYI survives for many years, a process called brine rejection occurs. The crystal
structure of water ice rejects salts, so that over time salt will be expelled from the sea
ice. Usually, the rejection of salt is about 3-4 parts per thousand in the Arctic. After
several years, it has become drinkable[44].
The reduction of the salt changes the properties of the sea ice. It both becomes much

2.2
Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR)
Spaceborne and airborne remote sensing sensors are frequently used in the o↵shore do-
main for monitoring a broad range of natural and anthropogenic geophysical research
ﬁelds.
Since the launch of the ﬁrst satellite more than 60 years ago, a wide variety
instruments and technologies have been developed and adapted to serve di↵erent data
acquisition objectives.
Some investigations include general space exploration, others
study the Earth itself.
Images of the Earth’s surface have always been of interest both from a scientiﬁc and a
national security perspective, and this has pushed the quality and resolution of especially
optical and Multi Spectral Imagery (MSI) to advance relatively quickly, see [57].
A considerable challenge for satellites recording wavelengths in the optical spectrum or
shorter is that these are highly dependent of weather condition such as cloud coverage
and solar illumination. The concern regarding cloud coverage is much more profound
in some regions compared to others, especially in the subtropics and the polar regions.
Long periods of cloud coverage make optical images much less reliable for capturing
short-term changes. Another crucial disadvantage of instruments operating in the op-
tical spectrum is that they are signiﬁcantly impeded by lack of illumination, which in
particular is an issue for polar regions as well. Due to the axial tilt the Arctic and
Antarctica experience entire days of sun in the summer, and enter a period of complete
absence of sunlight, the so-called polar darkness in winter, see [3].
Consequently, optical data acquisitions at the poles are highly inconsistent throughout
the year, something which is very problematic for inspecting and monitoring short-term
changes.
Other technologies however have been developed which are much less a↵ected by clouds
and additional atmospheric disturbances. A SAR is an either air-born or space-born
radar which records the structure of some surface area by transmitting pulses of radio
waves and registering the amount of backscatter received after the pulses have trav-
eled to the surface and back.[42] Since the launch of the ﬁrst spaceborne SAR on-board

2.2.3
Speckle
Speckle is a very pronounced granular interference inherent in SAR images in particular
in homogeneous regions. The speckle noise gives SAR images a rather noisy, grainy
texture appearance, and it is not so much a noise as an unwanted modiﬁcation to a
desired signal. It is caused by the presence of multiple elemental scatter sources within
a resolution cell. The coherent sum of their phases and amplitudes cause ﬂuctuations
in the backscatter values [43]. In oceanography, the main elementary scatterers are the
gravity–capillary ripples on the open water surface, which manifest a background of
speckle noise in the image spectrum [31].

Deep Learning (DL) is a subﬁeld of Machine Learning (ML), which deals with comput-

3.1
Neural Network (NN)
A Neural Network (NN) is a structure composed of individual neurons connected in a
suitable way, and the construction of NNs is inspired by our knowledge of the workings
of the human brain, hence the terminology. A neuron is a device which takes one or
several inputs, given as numbers in either [0; 1] or [−1; 1], and take them to outputs in

3.1.1
Backpropagation
For a fully trained network, the weights and biases have been determined and remain
unchanged, providing the designated output each time. But in order to determine the
weights and biases, the network must undergo a training process, see [62]. The principle
of this training is simple enough: weights and biases should be modiﬁed so that the
18

Figure 3.2: Two illustrations showing the same NN, but exposed to di↵erent inputs.

output is satisfactory in all the examples on which the network is being trained. Prin-
cipally, all these values could be modiﬁed manually to get a desired output, but if the
network contains thousands or millions of parameters, this is not feasible. An additional
complication arises since change in one layer e↵ects the output of all following layers,
making it diﬃcult to assess its impact. To adjust all parameters according to the train-
ing data, on uses an algorithm based on the principle of backpropagation, which allows
for an assessment of parameter changes in one neuron on all the neurons in higher layers
and thereby to the ﬁnal output. This method is outlined below:
We assume given a large training dataset for the NN. This dataset contains of inputs as
well as the outputs that we would like the network to obtain, also known as targets or
the ground truths. Initially all weights and biases are given random values, presumably
leading to wrong and unresolved classiﬁcations of the input (except in cases of extreme
luck). To improve the assignment of weights and biases, a cost function is introduced,
measuring how far the computed output is from the ground truth, see [46], the most
common one being the sum of squared di↵erences between predicted desired output val-
ues.
The gradient of this cost function indicates the direction steepest ascent, and changing
sign we obtain the direction of steepest descent, see [46]. The step length in the direction
of steepest descent is called the learning rate and should be chosen with some care, since
too small a learning rate can result in slow progress, while with too a large learning rate
one may overshoot the local minimum.
The backpropagation algorithm computes this highly complex gradient and e↵ectuates
the desirable changes in weights and biases. The main objective using backpropagation
is not only to change the weights and biases so that the activation of the desired class
is high, but also so that the activations of neurons representing other classes are low to
absent. As can be seen from Equation 3.4, changing a weight by a large magnitude does
not necessarily correspond to a large change in output. Therefore, it is important also
to change activations of neurons in previous layers. The algorithm continues backwards
in the network ﬁnding parameter changes giving a rapid decrease in cost, which is where
the term backpropagation originates from, see [46].
The training process is carried out for each training element, in our case, each image.
Usually, the gradient descent steps are not computed from the entire training dataset at
once, instead smaller batches of the dataset are drawn randomly. The method is called
Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), it does not provide the most eﬃcient step towards
local minima, but will signiﬁcantly speed up the training process, see [62]. If the amount

of training data is large enough, the NN will become increasingly conﬁdent on predicting
the desired outputs.

3.3.1
U-Net
There are several methods for obtaining a segmentation map, but the method called
U-Net has been one of the leading approaches for Semantic Segmentation in later years.
Since the use of low level details of the input is important in semantic segmentation, but

information about high level details must be kept as well, a large amount of training
data is needed, see [34] or [32]. The U-Net meets this challenge by employing extensive
data augmentation. The idea behind the U-Net is to extract lower and lower levels of
details using convolution and pooling, while saving each previous result. By combining
the results of low level features with the corresponding high level, both serve as a type
of data augmentation, and this is thought to mitigate the training problem.
A U-Net uses the operations and layers as a regular CNN, but it does not ﬁnish at a
fully connected layer, see [34]. Instead, it expands again, heading towards a full picture
with the same height/width dimensions as the input picture. The third dimension of the
output layer, however, is determined by the number of classes informed by the designer
to be in the image, and it contains activation values for each pixel mapped to each
class. The common approach to go from this 3-dimensional output to the 2-dimensional
masked segmentation map is by choosing the class of highest probability, thereby re-
moving the third dimension.

3.4
Training Neural Networks
The training of an NN is a crucial part of obtaining a functioning model for classifying
data in a satisfactory manner. In this thesis we use supervised learning, meaning that
a dataset of both inputs and corresponding outputs is required. A model takes inputs,
interpret them, and provide an output, and using data the network learns to mimic how
the inputs give rise to the corresponding output, using the process of backpropagation
introduced earlier. During the training the network model will iteratively update the
model parameters to reduce the discrepancy between prediction and truth, thus improv-
ing the accuracy, deﬁned as the number of correctly labeled inputs compared to the total
number of predictions.
27
==PAGE:38==
Philip Smith, s154668
February 16, 2021
During the training process the model will continue improving the predictions to re-
duce the loss. However, at some point the model increasingly remembers the training
set rather than ﬁnding general characteristics and features of the set. This means that
from some point the model will perform increasing well on the training set, but decreas-
ingly well on new data. To mitigate this, a validation set is introduced. The validation
set is used in the training phase, but it is not used for training. Instead, at each epoch
the model uses the updated model parameters to predict and classify the validation
data, and a validation loss is obtained, cf. [38]. The validation set will therefore only
be used to determine the performance of the updated model. Usually, the training ac-
curacy will continue to improve until an accuracy of almost 100% is obtained.
The
validation loss and accuracy will however not continue improving. Most commonly, the
best model is selected when the lowest validation loss is obtained or a brieﬂy before that.
Since the validation data are used to asses whether or not a model is improving, the
ﬁnal model will have a slight bias towards these data as well, and for this reason, a third
dataset, called the testing set, is used to evaluate the performance of a fully trained
model.
3.4.1
Ensemble approximations
In supervised DL a model is trained on a dataset, whereafter it can be used to label or
categorize new unseen data. However, it may happen that di↵erent network architectures
perform well on di↵erent aspects of the training data. One architecture might predict one
label better than another one and conversely. Even if two architectures perform equally
well, combining them may reduce the variance on parts of the data.
As previously
mentioned, most networks are trained using a stochastic training algorithm, and this
makes them sensitive to the training data and can cause di↵erent results, and di↵erent
model weights, even if trained with the same dataset and containing the same trainable
parameters.
An increasingly used approach to mitigate the high variance is to train several models,
using identical or di↵erent architectures, and then combining the predictions of the
models in some appropriate way using e.g. an average of the predictions. This approach
is called ensemble learning, see [7]. Even if the combination of two models trained on
the same data and using the same architecture can provide better results, usually some
aspects are changed in order to improve performance, such as (1) changing training
data, e.g. by including di↵erent augmentations or switching parts of the validation and
training data, (2) changing the architecture of the model, or (3) changing the method
by which the models are combined. For further details, see [7].

A precondition for a well-operating DL algorithm is a well prepared dataset to be used
for the training and testing of any suggested NN. Throughout this thesis, the dataset
used was assembled and processed in a collaboration between the Danish Meteorological
Institute (DMI), and the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), and Harnvig Arctic &
Maritime as a part of the ASIP (Automated downstream Sea Ice Products) project. More
speciﬁcally, the AI4Arctic / ASIP Sea Ice Dataset - version 2 (ASID-v2) is produced with
and to be used in the ESA AI4Arctic project and is a further development of the earlier
version ASID-v1, and can be found at [1]. The previous version ASID-v1 contains data
with recordings dating 2014-2017, while this newly released dataset ASID-v2 covers data
spanning 2018-2019. The ASID-v2 contains 461 Sentinel-1 Synthetic Aperture Radar
(SAR) scenes matched with sea ice charts produced by the (DMI. Each Sentinel-1 SAR
scene is comprised of two images, one for each polarization: HH, HV. As previously
mentioned, these facilitate a better di↵erentiation of the surface structures of the Earth.
4.1
Sentinel-1 SAR data
Sentinel-1 is a radar mission of EU’s Copernicus Earth Observation programme. It is a
combination of two polar-orbiting satellites, namely Sentinel-1A and Sentinel-1B, both
satellites equipped with the same SAR system, see [19]. The on-board SAR instrument
consists of a right-looking active phased array antenna and has a C-band designation
operating at a center frequency of 5.405 GHz [19]. It supports operation in dual polar-
isation, in the combination HH, HV or VV, VH, and it inherently also supports single
polarization HH or VV.
The Sentinel-1 satellites are capable of utilizing 4 di↵erent acquisition modes: Stripmap
(SM), Interferometric Wide swath (IW), Extra-Wide swath (EW), and Wave mode
(WV), see [19].
The signatures of the di↵erent modes can be seen in Figure 4.1. These di↵erent modes
have a large impact on the recorded SAR scenes. The SM mode produces images of high
resolution but of swath up to 80km with an approximately constant incidence angle, one
of six predeﬁned elevation beams. The IW mode covers a larger area with a swath of
250 km consisting of 3 sub-swath, and with a moderate geometric resolution (5 m by 20
m). The EW mode covers the largest area up to 410km comprised of 5 sub-swaths but
with a lower resolution (20 m by 40 m). This acquisition mode is particularly useful in
ice and polar zones where wide coverage and short revisit times are desirable.

4.2
DMI Sea Ice Charts
The ASID-v2 contains ice charts of each SAR-scene produced by the Greenland Ice
Service at the DMI. The SAR satellite images have been inspected by a group of experts,
who by manual interpretation have categorized areas within the images as containing
di↵erent sea ice concentrations. The ice charts are comprised of a number of separate
polygons containing di↵erent sea-ice concentration which collectively represent a whole
SAR-scene. Each polygon in a given scene is assigned a distinct ID number (1,2,3,..)
which can be translated into a sea ice concentration code using so-called SIGRID3 codes.
Each data ﬁle includes a separate translation scheme. Several numbers can be translated
into the same sea ice concentration code, but the polygons are not spatially connected.
As shown in the previous section the SAR-scenes are oriented the ground range direction
with increasing incidence angle from left to right. As a result many SAR-scene borders
are included in the images, and the pixel values beyond the borders are assigned NaN-
values. These areas along with the land areas are assigned the polygon number 0, as it
is not meaningful to discuss sea ice concentration.

Since the polygons are drawn and produced by DMI experts by hand based on
the subjective judgement, they may contain some amount of uncertainty. No explicit
associated uncertainty can be expressed [55], but in some cases the uncertainty can be
up to 20%1. It is in particular the intermediate sea ice concentrations which have high
uncertainties, as it can be diﬃcult to distinguish between sea ice concentration of e.g. 50%
and 60%. Moreover, the polygons are drawn following structures and curvatures visible
in the SAR images, whereafter a smoothing operation are performed. As a result the
polygon edges can sometimes be several hundreds of meters from the actual curvatures
in the image. The high and low sea ice concentrations contain less uncertainties, and in
particular open water areas are required to be quite precise as these maps are used for
ship navigation.
In supervised DL the results used for training an NN are called ”ground truths”, but
in this thesis they will be called ”sea ice concentration reference” as they cannot be
considered as true labels.

4.3.1
Loading data ﬁles
The data ﬁles of the dataset are provided in NetCDF2 (*.nc) ﬁle-format, which contain
all available data of each product. Initially, the three data collections: ”sar primary,
sar secondary, and sar icechart were extracted from the dataset along with a latitude/-
longitude grid, and converted into a GeoTIFF3 (*.tif) image-format.
The land areas and the parts enclosing the SAR image are as previously mentioned
masked out as NaN. They are unsuitable for training an NN. One way to accommodate
this problem is to cut out the parts containing NaN-values. Later the images will be
split into several smaller patches, and the patches including NaN-values could then be
removed. This is however not an ideal approach, as a large part of the data will be
excluded. Moreover, it can be important to obtain information about areas close to
land, but then NN would not have learned anything about them. Consequently, another
method has been used for this thesis. Instead of removing the pixels with NaN values,
they are all assigned a speciﬁc value and given a label, so that the network can learn
them and predict them.
The pixel values of SAR images are packed in the interval [-1,1], which makes the train-
ing of an NN easier. The values given to the pixels with NaN values should also be in
this range, and the speciﬁc value is chosen to be 0.0. In Table 4.2 it can be seen that
this value actually is translated into a real dB value with a physical meaning, something
which is highly undesirable. However, it turns out that as all pixels are ﬂoat values with
a large number of decimals, none of the pixels has the exact value 0.0. Later we shall
see that all the NN architectures introduced in this thesis comfortably distinguish 0.0
from values very close to it. The motivation for not using a very large or very small
value is that it will be computationally expensive and can cause additional problems in
the training phase.
Each NetCDF ﬁle also contains SIGRID3 codes corresponding to the polygons which
in this loading phase are used to assign new values to the pixels in the polygons. To
facilitate the training of an NN, the pixels are not assigned the sea ice concentration
in % or SIGRID3 codes as displayed in Table 4.3, but a new arrangement of labels is
introduced. These new labels can be seen in the updated Table 4.4. The updated scheme
consists of labels from 0 to 12. As the previously masked out areas in the SAR scenes
are now used, they are given the label 0 in the ice charts. The categories ”Ice Free”,
”Less than 1/10 (open water)”, and ”Bergy water” have been merged into one label, 1,
which will be considered open water.
Contrary to previous similar classiﬁcation approaches for sea ice charting, the categories
95% and 100% are not concatenated into one class, as it is expected that these have
considerably di↵erent backscatter signatures. The reason for this assumption is that a
sea ice concentration is only labelled 100% if its land fast ice, which means that it is not
moving and has signiﬁcantly di↵erent scattering mechanisms. The landscape, islands,

Incidence angles
In Section 2.2, it was mentioned that the images produced by a SAR instrument (at least
initially) will have larger backscatter closer to the satellite, where the angle of incidence
is smaller. This is also the case for the DMI and DTU dataset used in this thesis, as
can be observed in Figure 4.2 of Section 4.1. The dataset by the Nansen center has at-
tempted to correct for this phenomenon, but this can be a challenging task, as di↵erent
targets may vary substantially in their proportionality factors of incidence angle and
backscatter coeﬃcients as explained in Section 2.2.5.
Therefore it would be advantageous if an NN could learn these trends and patterns by
itself. Solely by exposing a suitable network to the images of the dataset, it is very likely
that a model would learn rather quickly that the images have a tendency to be brighter
to the left in the images (as they are all oriented identically) even though the resulting
label is the same.
But the model could also enforce such a regulation if the NN was provided with in-
formation about the incidence angle. In the dataset the information of the incidence
angle as a function of the ground range is available along with a grid for the incidence
angle. In Figure 4.8 this incidence angle/ground range correlation can be seen to the
left as a function. In the middle ﬁgure the incidence angle has been converted into an
image with a grid size identical to the SAR image in 4.2, where each pixel is assigned
a incidence angle value and the blue to red colors indicate the incidence angle. The
values have been rounded to 2 decimals to not put an excessive weight on the inﬂuence
of the incidence angle. Finally, to the right in the ﬁgure the land and the parts outside
of the SAR image boundaries have been masked out. When corresponding incidence

4.3.2
Pre-processing steps
After loading the information from the dataset in a desirable image format, a number of
operations will be carried out on the data in preparation for the DL process. In many
cases, the sea ice chart corresponding to a SAR image continues beyond the SAR image
borders, so that it reﬂects an area not included in the SAR scene. This can lead to a
systematic misclassiﬁcation for an NN, as it gains information and knowledge from areas
which, as mentioned above, have been given the value 0.0.
Consequently, these areas are masked out of the ice-charts as well.
Correction of data
The data ﬁles are already corrected according to the ESA guidelines, see [40]. These
corrections include some average noise correction, which can result in non-meaningful
negative backscatter anomalies. The pixels of negative backscatter coeﬃcients are set
to -100 when converted to dB corresponding to -4.5 in the scaled values.
Since it is not desirable to provide an NN with these error pixels, some mitigation
would be desired, but not always appropriate. Nevertheless, as the ﬁnal objective is to
automatically classify SAR scenes into di↵erent sea ice concentrations and ultimately
to sea ice charts, and as the polygons in the ice charts are spatially quite extensive,
a reasonable way of accommodating this problem is by checking the neighboring non-
error pixels and giving the error pixel a median value of these. Therefore, two median
functions were created, the ﬁrst one only checking the 4 directions up, down, left, and
right, ”Error correction 4” and the second checking in all 8 directions of the pixel, ”Er-
ror correction 8”. Both functions will continue in each direction until a valid value (by
a threshold chosen by the user) is reached, or a chosen max distance from the error pixel
is reached. The threshold of acceptable values is chosen as [-1.25;1.25]. Afterwards a
weighted median operation is performed and the pixel is changed.
If the number of pixels with error in an image exceeds 5% of the full number of pixels,
the corresponding data ﬁle will be excluded from further usage. Out of the 461 data
ﬁles in the dataset 25 were removed due to an intolerable quantity of noise, all of which
Sentinel-1A images. The total number of distinct images was therefore reduced to 436.
Down-sizing of data
A crucial step in the pre-processing is deciding whether the SAR scenes should be com-
pacted into images of smaller sizes, and if so, by which factor. As previously mentioned,
some Sentinel-1 scenes extend over 10000 ⇥10000 pixel (400 ⇥400 square kilometers),
and the ice chart polygons cover large areas with the same label, so that it is reasonable
to perform some down-sizing operation. Another motivation for down-sizing the SAR
scenes and ice charts is that they will split into a number of smaller patches and enable
an NN to take them as input. However, it is very important to consider the loss of infor-
mation and texture in the SAR images, which occur when down-sizing or downsampling
the data. For some applications, this loss in resolution would not be acceptable e.g. ship
detection or oil spill detection see [22], as crucial features would be removed. However,
for extensive sea ice charting an equally high resolution is not necessary. Consequently,

Patchiﬁcation of data

5.1.1
Hyperparameters
Hyperparameters are a important in DL for achieving high performing networks. The
hyperparameters include most adjustable parameters in an NN. They are chosen before
the training process and are not learned.
Hyperparameters include:
• Batch size:
The batch size determines how many training examples the NN will be exposed to
before updating the trainable parameters in the network. The smaller the batch
size the less accurate the estimation of the gradient will be per epoch, giving slower
convergence. A large batch size allocates a large amount of memory in the GPU,
and this may not be available for many GPUs.
• Epoch:
The number of epochs determines the length of the training period for the network.
At each epoch, the batches of training examples will be used to estimate better
parameters for the model. In this thesis the Keras function ”EarlyStopping” will be
used to stop the training if the estimates of the validation data have not improved
for a preassigned number of epochs.
• Learning rate:
The learning rate matters much for the training of the network.
If too small,
the NN will converge very slowly, and if too large it may overshoot local minima.
During the training of the NNs in this thesis, the Keras function ”ReduceLROn-
Plateau” was used, so that if the results of the training have not improved for a
selected number of epochs, the learning rate will be reduced by a chosen factor.
• Number of ﬁlters for the convolutional layers:
Traditionally the number of ﬁlters for each deeper layer are doubled. Therefore,
the number of ﬁlters are usually given for the ﬁrst layer and then doubled in each
following layer.
5.2
Implementation choice of di↵erent architectures
In DL it is very diﬃcult to know beforehand which architectures will provide the best
results for a given dataset. Much of the knowledge in the DL research ﬁeld originates
from trial and error experiments of di↵erent structures of operations, searching for archi-
tectures which perform better and more robustly. Furthermore, some architectures are
well-functioning on some dataset and work less well on other sets. Thus, an investigation
of the performances of di↵erent architectures trained on the AI4Arctic / ASIP Dataset-
v2 can improve our knowledge of the amount of information that can be extracted from
the dataset.
In this chapter, 5 separate DL architectures will be introduced. All architectures will
be trained on the same dataset and provide fully segmented maps as output. The input
images have as mentioned above a size of (256 ⇥256). The initial three architectures
will be variations of the UNet, while the last two architectures will be variations of the
ResUNet.

5.2.2
Deep Residual U-Net (ResUNet)
Intuitively, a deeper U-Net should improve the performance or at least perform as well
as a less deep version: Giving a model more parameters and additional information, the
network should be able to ﬁt the training data at least as well as. This is also the case
sometimes, but often a deeper U-Net can result in inferior training accuracy due to a
degeneration problem.
One such problem is the increasing probability of either vanishing or exploding gradi-
ents, something which signiﬁcantly restricts the convergence. However, this problem
49
==PAGE:60==
Philip Smith, s154668
February 16, 2021
Figure 5.3: The architecture and corresponding operations for (UNet 3)
has been reduced considerably by the use of batch normalization. Another persistent
problem occurs, as the depth of the network increases, namely that the accuracy gets
saturated and degrades. This problem is thought to arise because a deeper network is
more diﬃcult to optimize. The conjecture is that an increasingly number of weights in
the network becomes zero, and then they are not further updated.
A way of mitigating this degeneration problem is proposed in [68], where a deep residual
learning framework is introduced. The authors improved their results as compared to a
normal U-Net, and this has motivated an implementation of the ResUNet in this thesis.
In this framework, the degeneration problem is managed by ﬁtting a residual mapping
between the layers instead of directly mapping underlying layers, shown as the addition
layer in Figure 5.4. The residual mapping takes the form
yl = h(xl) + F(xl, Wl)
xl+1 = f(yl),
(5.1)
where xl is the input from the previous layer and xl+1 is the output. The function f is
as previously the activation function and the F is the residual function. The function
h commonly takes the form h(xl) = xl, and Wl denotes the weights and biases related
to the residual mapping. Figure 5.4 shows a ”normal” U-Net to the left together with
a residual unit to the right. The input for Equation (5.1) is indicated at the top of the
residual unit. This input will subjected to the layers F as in the normal U-Net but will
also undergo be transported by an identity mapping as seen in the ﬁgure. The outputs of
the layers and the output of the identity mapping are then added together. The identity
mapping function, also called the ”shortcut”, can be chosen among several appropriate
options, the simplest being to use the input itself without modiﬁcations and add it to
the output of the layers. In this thesis this mapping is a simple convolutional layer with
kernel size (1 ⇥1) and with the number of ﬁlters corresponding to convolutional layers
of F. The outputs of the layer F(Xl) and the mapping Xl are then added and used as
input for the following layers.

The Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) block
As previously seen, the di↵erent convolutional layers will give rise to several di↵erent
feature maps, of which some may be very important and some less important.
The
Squeeze-and-Excitation block examines the channels and assign to them a weight or at-
tention based on their importance, see [41]. The SE-block consists of three components:
A squeeze module, a excitation module, and a scale module.
Using each full size feature map to rate its importance is a computationally very ex-
pensive method, and this motivates the use of an alternative assessment method. The
squeeze module uses a feature descriptor to decompose or to ”squeeze” the informa-
tion of each channel into singular values. This entails that an input of size W⇥H⇥C
(width⇥height⇥channels) gets reduced to 1⇥1⇥C by a feature descriptor, so that each
channel is represented by only one number.
The feature descriptor can be chosen so as to ﬁt the purpose. Most commonly, either
Max-pooling or average pooling is used to reduce the spatial dimension. As previously
discussed, Max-pooling is a good method to preserving very active pixels, but in the
present context it can make the output very noisy, taking into account only the brightest
pixels, not their neighbors. For that reason a global average pooling operation is com-
monly used in the feature descriptor for the SE-block. More sophisticated approaches
have also been suggested after the SE-block was released, e.g. analyzing only parts of
the map and taking a maximum or average value of that area.

The ASPP-block is a method to extract information from an input tensor in di↵erent
scales, commonly by using several convolutional operations with di↵erent parameters
to extract features at multiple scales. The dilation rate, also called the atrous rate, of
the convolutional kernel is often used to extract these di↵erent maps. The dilation rate
determines the e↵ective ﬁeld of view in which the convolutional operation analyzes the
input. An example can be seen in Figure 5.9. In the ﬁgure four convolutions are applied

one another, and so on.

the output from the ASPP-block.

Custom loss function/weighted loss
As previously mentioned in Section 4.3.2, the distribution of classes is heavily imbal-
anced with some sea ice concentration labels occurring much more often than others. To
mitigate that, a large amount of patches were removed. However, as the dataset is still
signiﬁcantly imbalanced a custom loss function is introduced as well. The fundamental
concept is to penalize the networks much more to fail to predict under-represented labels
compared to over-represented ones.
This custom loss will be a modiﬁcation of the Sparse Multi-class Cross-Entropy Loss
from Equation (3.5) but applied a penalization weight for each label prediction. This
implies giving a larger weight or a large importance to some labels and less to others.
The modiﬁed loss equation is then given by

Chapter 7
Discussion
In the previous chapter the performances of each model on the test set and the test scene
were demonstrated. On the test set the fully trained models of di↵erent architectures
performed fairly well. On the test scenes the UNet 2 performed noticeably better than
the other models.
In this chapter, several points will be raised in order to highlight and emphasize dif-
ferent aspects of the model performances.
7.1
Method for splitting training/valid/testing sets
In Section 4.3.4 we have described the decision of splitting the original patch dataset
using a random generator. This is a very common approach to splitting datasets in DL
in such a way that the training data are the as representative for the whole set as possi-
ble. However, as also mentioned in the section, other approaches could have been used.
When splitting the dataset randomly, many neighboring patches will be in the split into
training/validation/testing, meaning that the patches are not uncorrelated. This is not
necessarily a problem, as training on one patch is not equivalent to easily estimating
neighboring patches, but some features might cross over the patches and might help the
trained network to evaluate the patch.
Furthermore, it should be recalled that when splitting the dataset, the overlapping
patches of the testing patches were included in the validation set. This arguably is not a
satisfactory approach, since the model will be slightly biased towards the validation set
as well. However, it is also important to state that the networks has not been trained
on any of the overlapping patches.
In Section 6.2 it could be seen that all models of all architectures performed well on
the test set, and some performed very well. However, when the di↵erent models were
used to predict the testing scenes in Section 6.3, the results were considerably less ac-
curate. This indicates that training on patches and testing on some of the neighbors
will have a substantial impact on the model performance. An explanation could be that
since the intermediate SIC polygons are more variable from ice chart to ice chart, if one
of the neighboring patches of the test patch was used for training, the NN would be
better at estimating the SIC corresponding to the DMI reference in that particular area
and on that particular day.
Nonetheless, it should be emphasized that the test scenes were completely excluded from
the training, validation, and testing sets, and the UNet 2 model obtained a balanced ac-
curacy score of 42.33 on the test scenes. This is quite a high score for a 13 class semantic
80
==PAGE:91==
Philip Smith, s154668
February 16, 2021
segmentation network, see other high performing semantic segmentation network [69],
and especially with training data having signiﬁcant uncertainty in some classes.
The test scenes represent 5% of the total dataset, which is a small, but not insigniﬁcant,
amount, For that reason an assessment of the model performance on the test scenes
alone can also provide worthwhile information.
In future similar projects a random selection of full SAR scenes for the test set may
provide more reliable and robust predictions.
7.2
Misclassiﬁed open water
In Section 6 the performances of fully trained models of each architecture were investi-
gated on the test set and the test scenes. Their performance evaluations were predomi-
nately concentrated around the pixel-wise accuracy of classiﬁcation and the models were
ranked based on this score.
However, for some applications, other properties and qualities of the model performance
are more important than estimating all classes correctly to a large extent. In the mar-
itime sectors and industries, ﬂawless estimations of intermediate sea ice concentrations
are of less importance, while the di↵erentiation between open water and 95% sea ice is
more useful and valuable. Moreover, ships will to a large extent avoid sea ice concentra-
tions altogether and stay in completely open water regions if possible.
This motivates an evaluation and ranking of the ability of the models to classify open
water, and more importantly a way of measuring how often the models predict open
water when sea ice is present. In earlier sections overprediction and overrepresentation
of the open water class in predictions of several models were brieﬂy pointed out as a
problem. To understand where such misclassiﬁcations occur, an example from Scene 3
used again. Figure 7.1 to the right displays the magnitude of errors and the locations
as shown in Section 6.14. However, in this illustration only situations where the model
predicts open water, but the DMI ice chart indicates sea ice presence, are displayed. To
the left the corresponding sea ice chart is displayed.
In the ﬁgure it can be seen that the misclassiﬁcations of SICs of various levels as open
water typically occur near the boundary between regions containing sea ice and regions
of open water. In the regions close to the boundary, the error is commonly 2 or 3 classes
from the correct label. The larger and more problematic errors occur when narrow tex-
tures of SIC are located in open water. The model has diﬃculties with these changes,
as it more often classiﬁes larger polygons and attempts to ﬁnd an average SIC over an
extensive area.
If the main objective was to prevent misclassiﬁcation of di↵erent SICs as open water,
an approach could be to modify all or most labels SICs not pertaining to open water
so as to have the same label value, retaining the labels of importants to the users. In
that way the number of classes would be reduced considerably, and the models would
perform better with the smaller set of classes.

7.4
Small scale labelling
As mentioned several times throughout this thesis, the primary objective has been to
resemble the DMI sea ice charts to a large degree. In supervised learning it is not pos-
sible to get better results than the data provided for the training. However, the large
scale polygons in the traditional DMI ice chart might not be what is desired, rather
this has been the most eﬃcient way to classify large amounts of SAR images by hand.
When analyzing the structures within many of these polygons, in most cases they are
not homogeneous. There will be parts with high SIC and parts with open water, but
the polygon as a whole will be assigned an estimate of the overall average.
Since these smaller structures are visible in the SAR images, future sea ice charts might
reﬂect them better that what is obtained using extensive polygons. In principle this can
only be achieved using some AI method, as manual analysis of SAR images to such an
extent would be impossible. However, it is very unlikely that a robust AI system would
be able to do this using only the AISD-v2 dataset. When a supervised NN is trained, it
is penalized each time it deviates from the ground truth, in this case the DMI ice charts.
For that reason the network will learn to estimate the average concentration of sea ice
over a region, as in the DMI ice charts, instead pointing out the smaller features.
If ice charts of higher resolution were the primary objective, some unsupervised method
should be used, at least as a complement to the supervised system.

7.5
Patch size
In this thesis the dataset the Sentinel-1 SAR images and corresponding DMI ice charts
were split into patches as mentioned repeatedly. The choice of a patch size of (256⇥256)
was mainly due to the large increase in computational resources which would follow from
choosing a larger input image size. Since the aim of this project was to investigate sev-
eral architectures, some already computationally expensive, and each architecture was
trained several times to obtained better results, a patch size of (256 ⇥256) was selected
as the best trade-o↵.
However, as seen in multiple examples, some patches are diﬃcult to estimate due to
the limited amount of information within the patches and lack of knowledge of the sur-
rounding patches. This is in particular a problem when the objective is to resemble very
large average structures such as ice chart polygons.
For that reason a test was carried out, whereby one of the architectures were modiﬁed
slightly so as to use patches of size (512 ⇥512) as input. As the model of UNet 2 archi-
tecture has overall best performance, this architecture was used to train the new model

model (256 ⇥256) performs better in other labels. Thus, the inﬂuence of the patch
size on the results seems not to be quite straightforward. However, it should be noted
that models of the UNet 2 architecture and using patches of size (256 ⇥256) have been
trained many times, so that the hyperparameters to some degree have been optimized
for this situation. The training of the UNet 2 with the patches of size (512 ⇥512), has

Chapter 9
Further work
In the previous sections several architectures and models have been introduced and
tested. Although some models performed fairly well, the results on the test scenes indi-
cates that signiﬁcant improvements can still be made.
One type of improvement could be to enlarge the training dataset. Obtaining additional
annotated data may very likely improve the performances. The predecessor of the em-
ployed dataset, ASID-v1, may provide additional information and training examples,
although some processing steps of this dataset are di↵erent from the ASID-v2. Some
mitigation of this discrepancy is therefore necessary.
Including other variations of augmented data would presumably increase the perfor-
mance as well. In this thesis only rotated augmented data were incorporated, which
appeared to increase the performance of the models. A continued investigation could
consequently also examine the e↵ects of including ﬂipped or scaled variations of the data.
A di↵erent approach for the selection of the test set would very likely provide better
performance assessments and more reliable evaluations. A random selection of full SAR
scenes could be a promising approach.
Other improvements regarding the architectures and hyperparameters of the di↵erent
Neural Networks introduced in this thesis could be carried out as well. Most of the
parameters for the networks were chosen and tested by the method of trial and error.
This was natural given the limitations of time and magnitude of the di↵erent other in-
vestigations of the project. Future projects may attempt to quantify better parameters
in a scientiﬁcally more satisfactory manner.
Moreover, the custom weights for the loss function should be experimented with. Instead
of choosing the inverse frequency as the weight, a more qualitative assessment of these
weights could plausibly provide better model performances. In particular the situations
and regions where a model predicts open water in areas containing sea ice should be
penalized mopre heavily.
Also the two custom noise correction median ﬁlters ”Error correction4” and ”Error correction8”
may not provide the best mitigation of noise. Other methods, which investigate whether
the surrounding region is homogeneous, and correct with respect to that, might be de-
veloped, or alternatively one should discard patches containing anomalous values.
The incentive to implement wind data was considered, since and in rough water, wind
speed can have a very large e↵ect on the SAR measurements. Wind data can be obtained
freely from [18] and is described in [27]. It can be seen that the spatial resolution of the
88

wind data is signiﬁcantly lower than the SAR images, covering several square kilometers
per pixel cell. Including wind data as input for the algorithms would likely improve the
results, but due to the time limitations, other investigations were prioritized higher.
Lastly, due to the author’s limited knowledge of DL, other promising architectures were
not studied or considered in the early process of the project. Although several of the
architectures studied in this thesis were quite well-functioning, other architectures such
as the Fully Convolutional Network (FCN) may provide a useful supplement. The FCN
is interesting as it is the ﬁrst CNN-based approach, which can take inputs of arbitrary
size and generate a correspondingly-sized output, see [Semantic˙multi˙compar˙bib].

Chapter 10
Conclusion
This project set out to establish and implement several NN architectures for sea ice
classiﬁcation using semantic segmentation.
With this purpose in mind, the required
background knowledge of DL and Remote Sensing was introduced, whereafter a detailed
description of the ASID-v2 Sentinel-1 SAR images and corresponding sea ice charts was
carried out, followed by an explaination of the necessary and chosen data pre-processing
steps, including down-sizing of the SAR scenes by a factor of three and dividing each
scene into patches of size (256 ⇥256). The AMSR2 brightness temperature data also
included in the ASID-v2 and used in previous similar studies of the ASID-v2 were not
utilized in this thesis. Instead an investigation was carried out with the aim of assessing
how well di↵erent NNs could be trained with only Sentinel-1 SAR data.
Five di↵erent NN architectures were selected, described, and implemented in Pyhton
using Tensorﬂow’s DL API Keras. The networks included three variations of a well-
known U-Net [32], denoted UNet 1, 2, and 3, and two variations of a deep residual
network, namely the ResUNet proposed in [68] and the ResUNet++ proposed in [30].
After having fully trained a model of each architecture, these models were investigated
one by one. Firstly, the model performance on the test set was examined, and secondly
this was followed by an investigation of the models’ ability to predict 6 full SAR scenes.
The model that overall provided the best results was trained using the UNet 2 architec-
ture. It performed well on both the testing set and scenes. The performance scores on
the testing scenes are the most reliable, as these scenes were excluded from train, the val-
idation, and the testing set. On these scenes, the UNet 2 model got an F1 score of 0.754.
The overall conclusion to be obtained from this project is that it is possible to set
up an automatic classiﬁcation tool which can di↵erentiate between di↵erent sea ice con-
centrations in the Arctic region. This tool can take the form of an NN, and even with
the technology developed at the present stage, it can deliver sea ice charts of a quality
which is not perfect but on the other hand reasonably close to what is being produced
manually. The project also provides some more detailed knowledge and insights into
the pros and cons of di↵erent network architectures, and the fact that the best per-
forming NN in the present situation was not the most recently developed architectures
but a comparatively simple one was in no way to be expected beforehand, showing that
the ﬁeld of machine learning and neural networks may still hide many surprises for the
researchers.
