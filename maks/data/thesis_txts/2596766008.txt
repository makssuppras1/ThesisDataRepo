Abstract
In the last hundred years, Earth has experienced an unprecedented increase in the
level of harmful emissions instigated by human activity and development. In 2015,
the United Nation’s (UN) “Sustainable Development Goals” (SDGs) were finally real-
ized, around 25 years after sustainable development was first formally discussed. The
demand for perishable commodities is at an all-time high. The integration of sustain-
ability in social, environmental, and economic development presents a challenge of
paramount importance, especially in the sector of cold supply chain management and
logistics. This thesis confronts that challenge by presenting a taxonomy consisting of
a set of 38 papers, all relating to cold chain logistics and sustainability. Three pa-
pers are then selected and discussed in detail based on their originality and relevance.
Findings from the taxonomy reveal that a majority of studies focus on cost-efficiency
or less often minimization of energy or emissions, while accounting for emissions re-
duction policies, such as the carbon trading and carbon tax. Findings also infer a lack
of integration regarding alternative energy sources within cold chain logistics research,
leaving a crater of unused potential. The observed tendency of the modelling trends
reveals the need to examine the social scope of sustainability and renewable energy
from a deeper perspective. Research covering the impact of governmental decisions
and incentives on sustainable adoption as well as the sustainability of the cold-chain
market of the fast-developing giants, like India, will enable the sector to follow-up
and make a green transition into the future. Lastly, further research on potential
emission reductions from transportation and various other processes across the entire
value chain of cold chain logistics will expose and optimize many potential solutions.

Preface
This thesis is undertaken in collaboration with the Department of Technology, Man-
agement, and Economics within the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). It serves
as the last project for the finalization of a Master’s of Science in Engineering (M.Eng.)
in Transportation and Logistics, under the study line of Logistics and Transport Op-
timization.
The two-year period as a student at DTU inspired and fueled this research, as sus-
tainability is embraced and promoted as the backbone of future development in the
sector. The efficient flow of goods, services, and information from the beginning until
the end of the value chain, namely logistics, comprises one of the most vital sectors
of our times and a key challenge for the green transition. Even more so, the cold sup-
ply chains have an unexplored potential towards environmentally-friendly practices,
therefore this thesis aims to focus on the intersection between sustainability and cold
chain logistics.
Kongens Lyngby, 15th July 2020

Introduction
The world population is projected to grow from the current 7.7 billion to 9.8 billion
by 2050. China and India constitute the two most populous countries of the world,
comprising 19% and 18% of the total world population respectively [10]. India’s pop-
ulation is expected to surpass China’s by roughly 2024. Besides population growth,
population aging is also an insightful global demographic ”megatrend” raising red
flags in the coming decades. An increase in the rate of longevity across countries is
also expected, considering the easier access to medication and amelioration of living
standards.
During the last century, on the other hand, the world has seen an unprecedented
increase in the level of harmful emissions caused by human activity and the rapid de-
velopment humanity instigated. Sustainable development was formally discussed for
the first time in the late ’80s, but it was only after 2010 that Sustainable Development
Goals (SDGs) were addressed by the United Nations. In 2015 they finally came into
effect, bringing sustainability and its imperative substance into the spotlight of the
social and private sphere. The 17 goals of this blueprint aim to achieve a better and
more viable future for all by the year 2030 through the integration of the overarching
dimensions of sustainability; social, environmental, and economic.
The global population increase, the change in nutritional needs as economies grow, as
well as prolonging of life expectancy all lead to a significant increase in demand for
perishable commodities, mainly food and pharmaceuticals. This augmenting pattern
coupled with the all-embracing effort to incorporate sustainability in social, environ-
mental, and economic development poses a phenomenal challenge in the sector of
cold supply chain management and logistics, calling for green transition in produc-
tion, processing, and distribution of temperature-sensitive products. This challenge
serves as the motivation for research within this area of study.
Research in the field of supply chain management and logistics has put incremental
attention on the topic of sustainability during the past 30 years, however literature
that focuses on refrigerated chains has only recently bloomed, with a variety of ways
in addressing the problem. Therefore, the purpose of this thesis is to expose and
taxonomize the knowledge and trends around sustainable cold supply chain logistics,
mainly from an environmental point of view, but also to compare and contrast all

aspects of sustainability within its scope. A taxonomy around the concepts and mod-
elling of sustainability in cold supply chain logistics is developed, based on relevant
criteria, followed by a review of the interesting approaches within the field and a
critical discussion on the findings.
The economic and environmental sustainability are discussed along the lines of this
thesis’ analysis and arising recommendations, but the selection and evaluation of
papers for the taxonomy are limited to environmental criteria, specifically towards
carbon emissions within the field of cold chain logistics.
The rest of the thesis is organized as follows:
• Chapter 1 presents a retrospect on the existing literature base around sustain-
ability and the status quo, supply chain management, and cold-chain logistics,
with a view to set the boundaries of the thesis’s problem.
• Chapter 2 starts off with a chronological review of the green literature within
the supply chain management field over the last 20 years and moves on to the
particulars of the taxonomy development; the retrieval and selection of papers
as well as the criteria utilized. The chapter ends with the taxonomy and the

CHAPTER 1
Literature review
A review of the existing literature in topics relevant to this study’s scope is attempted
in this chapter. The notion of sustainability is introduced, setting the scene for the
burning issue of carbon emissions and the great accountability of transportation on
that phenomenon. A foreword to green supply chain management and a closer look
at cold supply chain management will establish the boundaries of the research area.
The introduction of carbon incentives and policies for the reduction and control of
carbon emissions will outline the importance of regulatory action towards sustainable
development. Renewable energy will play a major role in that transition. The aim of
this chapter’s review is to summarize the necessary knowledge and promote dialogue
in the coming chapters.
1.1
Sustainability
Environmental consciousness has increased dramatically over the last decades. Reg-
ulatory bodies have started focusing on the effect human activities have on the en-
vironment, spotlighting issues of global warming, scarcity of resources, and carbon
emissions [11]. Customers and citizens are pushing the public and private sectors
toward the adoption of sustainable practices, leading to increased interest in eco-
efficiency from managers and decision-makers. The Brundtland report in 1987 [12]
defines sustainable development as meeting the needs and aspirations of the present
without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. Part-
nerships of governments, the private sector, civil society, and academia are required in
order to achieve this ambitious development, hence the growing attention it has been
receiving [13]. The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) adopted by all United Na-
tions Member States at the COP21 Paris Climate Conference in 2015 comprise the
most widely-accepted call to holistic action, addressing the end of poverty, protection
of the planet, and universal assurance of peace and prosperity by 2030. Moreover, the
Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Protocol [14] for greenhouse gas accounting and the Triple
Bottom Line conceptual framework [15] are paradigms of how sustainability can get
actively integrated into corporate practice and target setting.

1.3
Transportation
Emission rates from transportation constitute the second biggest contributor to global
carbon emissions. Road transport plays the most significant role in transportation
quota for all regions in the world. Interestingly, Asia almost tripled the amount of
road transport from 1990 to 2010. Overall, 7.0 gigatons of carbon dioxide equiva-
lent (GtCO2eq) comprising of direct GHG emissions (including non-CO2 gases) were
produced in 2010, accounting for approximately 23% of total energy-related CO2
emissions [4]. The rise of global trade led to an increased amount of containers and
consequently to the emerge of intermodal transportation [20]. Intermodal transporta-
tion is defined by the International Transport Forum as; multimodal transport of
goods in one and the same intermodal transport unit by successive modes of trans-
port without the handling of goods themselves during intervals of mode change [21].
Intermodal transportation can significantly contribute to the reduction of logistical
cost and emissions but is less agile in cases of disturbances [22]. Competition among
shippers is a driver for cost reduction while high utilization of barge and train con-
tainer capacity can impact emission reduction.
Transport demand per capita differs per region; it is much lower, on average, in de-
veloping and emerging economies (Economies in transition/EIT) than in countries
belonging to the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD).
This gap is expected to narrow and demand in EIT is projected to increase at a much
faster rate in the upcoming decades, as income and infrastructure development contin-
ues to rise. Naturally, the concern of the environmental impact occurs. Decoupling
transport GHG emissions from the gross domestic product (GDP) is a possibility if a
set of strong and mutually-supportive policies are implemented in all regions. Other
parameters also contribute to the mitigation of emissions. The decline in voyages
and modal shifts attributed to behavioral change, low-carbon fuels, improved vehicle,
and engine performance technologies, investments in transport infrastructure, and
changes in the built environment constitute promising initiatives for decarbonization
of the transport sector in the future.

incorporate all possible actors; suppliers, manufacturers, transporters, warehouses,
retailers, and end-users [23]. Marketing and regulatory compliance incentivize sus-

1.5
Cold supply chain management
Cold chain logistics encompasses the handling, storage, and transportation of temper-
ature-sensitive products needing refrigeration throughout all stages of transportation,
in order to maintain their freshness and usability before reaching the end-customer.
Even though refrigeration is essential in preserving the safety and quality of perish-
able products, it consumes a great amount of energy. In fact, refrigeration accounts
for 30% of worldwide energy consumption [26] and 15% of the worldwide electric-
ity consumption [27] and contributes to ozone depletion and global warming, due
to direct emissions from leakage and indirect emissions from fossil fuels used in the
electricity needed to power the refrigerating equipment.
James and James (2010) [28] outlined the emissions of cold chain logistics accounting
for almost 1% of global CO2 emissions. This contribution to carbon emissions varies
depending on the region where, in the United Kingdom, for instance, cold chains
account for 3.5% of the total regional emissions [29]. Such concerns bring out the
need to consider environmental aspects and explicitly account for carbon emissions
in integrated inventory models seeking to optimize the performance of cold supply
chains.
As discussed in Allied Market Research (2019) [30], the value of worldwide cold chain
logistics market accounted for 160,000 million dollars in 2018 and is projected to rise
to 585,000 million dollars by 2026, with a growth rate estimated at 17.9% per year.
The market is split in small yet numerous shares as opposed to large corporations
owning a big market share [31]. The market segmentation consists of six commodity
categories; fruits and vegetables, bakery and confectionery, dairy products and frozen
desserts, meat, fish and seafood, drugs and pharmaceuticals, and others [30].

1.7
Carbon incentives and policies (Carbon Tax
regulation & Cap and trade)
On the operational front of enterprise, carbon emissions mainly derive from pro-
duction and transportation activities [41]. In supply chain operations, high carbon
emissions are released throughout the supply chain in transportation, storage, pack-
aging, loading and unloading, handling, circulation, processing, and distribution [42].
In reference to cold supply chains where refrigeration is entailed at all stages, the
energy costs, the negative environmental impact, and pressure from the different
stakeholders coupled with regulations are even higher [43]. The implementation of
carbon incentives will unavoidably increase the cost and decrease the profit within
the value chain. The challenge of balancing the environmental versus the economical
aspect while vigorously evaluating their carbon footprint is critical for corporations
to consider and has thus received close attention from researchers.
The substantiation of sustainability within the supply chain requires relevant incen-
tives and policies for the public and private sectors. Different measures that target the
reduction of carbon and greenhouse gas emissions have taken place in many countries.
Τo name a few; mandatory carbon emission capacity, carbon cap, carbon emission
tax, cap-and-trade, clean development mechanism, and joint implementation [44].
The carbon cap regulation was originally proposed by the Congress of the United
States.
The carbon cap offset regulations are oftentimes applied when the entity
voluntarily wants to decrease its carbon emissions through carbon offset projects or
activities [45]. Mainly two market-based instruments targeting CO2 emissions have
drawn the highest attention throughout the last decade; carbon tax and cap-and-
trade [46].
The Kyoto Protocol, which came into effect in 2005 [47] is considered by many as the

first and strongest agreement of international interest on the agenda of climate change.
The Protocol was followed by reduction emission obligations and limits for economic
entities and countries, also named “flexible mechanisms”. One of them is the widely
known cap-and-trade mechanism “Carbon emission trading”. It constitutes a flexible
framework for the reduction of emissions by exhaustively combining regulatory and
market measures to achieve emission reduction targets. More specifically, within this
regime, all enterprises receive a corresponding emission cap by the environmental
authorities first. Afterward, an organization is allowed to buy emission permits if it
becomes difficult to achieve the emission reduction targets from other organizations
who have excess [47] and are willing to trade it or save it for their own activities.
In this way, the emission permits are deployed as an operational resource that can
be traded through a system, such as the European Union Emissions Trading System
(EU-ETS) but most importantly impacting enterprise strategy and operations within
its scope.
On the other end, by levying a tax rate per unit of carbon emitted from enterprises,
the carbon tax is a cost-effective policy to curb carbon emissions [48]. A lot of dis-
cussion between proponents and opponents surrounding these two regulations has oc-
curred, with the carbon tax policy having a higher representation (credibility) among
researchers and economists [49], [50]. By some, the carbon tax mechanism allows
for greater flexibility under uncertainty [51] or latter modifications and updates [52].
It also has a high ease of implementation and a more positive impact on economic
growth [53] compared to other reduction emission mechanisms while requiring less
control over implementation boundaries [54]. The supporters of the cap-and-trade
approach argue that it can be cost-effective and compensatory given the great emis-
sion inequalities that exist while also easily harmonizing with other countries’ carbon
policies [46]. In addition, it is believed that the targets will be less intense if carbon
taxes are employed, rather than a cap-and-trade system, since there is no emission
limitation or sanction, besides the monetary value of emissions.
1.8
Renewable energy
Renewable energy comprises the energy produced by renewable resources such as wind,
sun, water (rain, waves, and tides) and geothermal heat. The term renewable is used
to express these sources as naturally replenished over a short period of time. This
energy is oftentimes referred to as clean energy since it does not make use of fossil fuels.
Fossil fuels, such as petroleum (oil), coal, and natural gas are continuously formed
by natural processes but characterized as non-renewable resources, due to the fact
that it takes millions of years for their restoration and the known reserves are being
drained much faster than they can regenerate. Meanwhile, the use of fossil fuels is
largely responsible for the environmental deterioration of the atmosphere throughout
the past century, since only a small amount of these harmful emissions released can

The most prevailing applications of renewable energy today are wind power enabled
by wind turbines, solar photovoltaic panels, and hydropower, presently representing
approximately 15% of domestic electrical generation [55]. Over the past 50 years,
renewables have seen a great reduction in cost and they are becoming all the more
cost-efficient. The main applications of renewable energy nowadays are on and off-grid
electrical power generation, fluid (water and air) heating/cooling, and transportation.
The scalability of renewables in domestic, industrial, or even urban and regional
scale applications signalizes a great challenge and opportunity for the decarbonization
of future development. Research within the industrial incorporation of renewables
regarding the field of supply chain and logistics is still scarce, though it is expected
to play a major role in the sustainable transformation of the sector in future years.

2.1
A chronological retrospect to green supply chain
management literature review of the past two
decades
The topic of sustainability within supply chain management is not quite new since
it has been researched for over 30 years now. It is within the last ten years however
that the number of papers published within this field has picked up, parallel to the
mobilization towards greener enterprises. Likewise, interest in sorting and reviewing
the currently available and older literature also rose.
As has been seen already in 2003, Murphy and Poist [56] implemented a comparative
study for the green perspectives and practices in logistics between companies based
in the U.S. and outside the U.S. The study found that both have akin environmental
perspectives and practices in terms of logistics and highlighted the need of broader
research and higher adoption rate of sustainability practices by companies. Vachon
and Klassen in 2007 [57] investigated the role that integration of environmental tech-
nologies plays in supply chain management, with a large focus on the intersection
between buyer–supplier. Within these boundaries, two technological investment ar-
eas were examined; those pertaining to the logistical liaison between buyers and
suppliers, and those linked to strategic initiatives such as product development. This
research suggested that control over sustainability should be more evenly distributed
throughout the supply chain, by shifting the excessive weight from upstream actors
to downstream actors.

A great part of the examined literature sheds light on various niche supply chains,
including but not limited to, maritime shipping [58], the petrochemical and coal in-
dustry respectively [59] [60], the automobile industry [61], [62], [63] or the field of
constructions [64]. A great emphasis has been put into the extrinsic drivers towards
sustainability within supply chain management in these later years, as the literature
shows. In 2015, Zhang et al. [65] published their paper on the relationship between
external pressures (environmental regulation, community, competition, and customer
demand), divided into mimetic, normative, and corporate energy conservation prac-
tices, considering the decision-making impact. The research indicated that the man-
agerial environmental concerns and the corporate energy-saving practices are directly
correlated.
Two years later, a like-minded study gets implemented by Chu et al. [66], in which the
influencing drives are conveniently sorted into three categories; the coercive pressure,
the normative pressure, and the mimetic pressure. The first arises in organizations by
the cultural and societal expectations in the form of formal and informal conformity
standards. These standards typically come from governmental agencies. The second
category refers to professional codes, by which professionals will abide by since they
are aligned with conventions of formal education and the respectful professional circle.
Lastly, the mimetic pressure describes an organization adopting views and practices
from successful competitors who push the industry further through the implementa-
tion of sustainability. From the study followed that the coercive pressure had the
smallest impact in managerial action while normative and mimetic pressure had a
more significantly positive influence.
Interestingly, an increasing number of researchers have been stretching the impor-
tance of external forces [67] towards greener practices. In 2016, Xu et al. [68] presented
their study on how supply chain performance can be improved through industry stan-
dards in collaboration with community socialization, doing so by highlighting the
essential role of industry standardization in the optimization of an environmentally
performing supply chain. Attention to the environmental impact in reverse logistics
has also increased [69], as the logistics industry becomes one of the major contributors
in the carbon emissions and climate action front, with its activities seeing considerable
growth not only in forward-flow but also in reverse-flow. Unavoidably, technological
advancements also act as an accelerator of sustainability across supply chains. They
better serve and enable clearer communication and collaboration across partners, as
well as allowing for higher control throughout the entire supply chain spectrum. In-
formation technology is recognized as a key enabler for supply chain efficiency and
transition towards a greener future in the literature survey presented by Thöni and
Tjoa [70] in 2017. Lastly, the role of supply chain collaboration in the adoption of
sustainable practices is also regarded as critical, according to the study by Irani et al.
in 2017 [71].

formance has little to no influence on both sustainable practices and environmental
and/or economic performances.

2.2
Literature Retrieval and Selection
This study duly classifies the pre-existing literature related to cold chain logistics in
order to examine it based on various different parameters, all associated with sus-
tainability in some way, shape, or form. In particular, the different criteria examine
the modelling of sustainability and other supply chain logistics specifications. The
method followed for building the taxonomy in this paper is based on the taxonomy
framework accounting for the effect of speed in energy-efficient maritime transporta-
tion, as proposed by Psaraftis and Kontovas in 2013 [85].
The online library used for part of the research is DTU Inside; this online library ser-
vice provides access to different online library platforms such as ”Scopus” and ”Web
of science” and covers a wide variety of articles and books from the field of supply
chain management and logistics, in addition to all research areas pertaining to DTU.
With sustainability at the fore-front, maintaining a vital role in this study, various
keywords were used in the different phases of research; “Cold chain logistics sus-
tainability”, “Cold chain logistics footprint”, “Environmental sustainability cold food
transportation” and “perishable logistics sustainability”. The applied filters restricted
the findings to the last three years and the current (2020), while only journal articles
in English were selected for this study, summing up to a total of 135 papers. The

When it comes to sustainability in cold supply chains, carbon emissions and other

specifically, the measurement of emissions generated through the various processes
along the stages of the cold supply chain is one of the fundamental points of the

are usually translated into cost, performance, or environmental impact.
There is not a single particular way to calculate carbon emissions in the cold supply

The sources of emissions in this field vary. There is also a variety of frameworks
or methods for calculating each category’s emissions, but none of them comprises
the norm. Emissions deriving from transportation, electricity, and refrigeration are
usually estimated with different methods from the relevant literature. However, the
measurement of emissions from incineration or the storing process is not nearly as
regular.
Different parameters can affect the emission rates in the different methods seen in
the literature. The freight load or vehicle utilization, the speed, the mechanical data
of vehicles such as combustion and ignition for the transportation emissions, or the
ambient temperature for refrigeration emission all influence the carbon emission rate.

2.5
The taxonomy fields
The criteria used in the taxonomy are explained in this section. These different fields
collect scientific information from each paper, such as the methodology used, or other
information relevant to the paper, such as the goal of study and mode of transport.
Most of the parameters collect information with consideration to carbon emissions,
also referred to as sustainability parameters.
2.5.1
Goal of study
This field examines the objective of the study. Naturally, not all problems are opti-
mization models, therefore in spite of the maximization or minimization of a param-
eter, other variants might be incorporated under the label “Other”, followed by an
explanation of the study’s goal. Most often a model tries to minimize the cost, or
==PAGE:48==
30
2 Cold chain taxonomy
the carbon emissions, or quality deterioration while contrarily a maximization func-
tion focuses on profit or customer satisfaction. These types of problems belong to
linear programming with a single objective function, which minimizes or maximizes
a parameter. It is common in optimization problems that more than one parameter
is being optimized; this is called a multi-objective function. Models that simulate
a problem or create a conceptual framework for decision-making have a more of an
exploratory perspective in the problem, and thus they are descriptively listed in the
taxonomy.
2.5.2
Methodology
There are four different variants in the field of methodology. Various optimization
models make use of a mathematical modelling method. Among various ways of cre-
ating and solving mathematical models in this taxonomy, four alternatives are seen;
linear programming, constraint programming, system dynamics, and analytic mod-
elling. Linear programming optimization is a commonly used technique that finds
the optimal (minimum or maximum) outcome in a mathematical model whose con-
straints are represented by linear relationships. In this method, the objective function
returns the best feasible (maximum or minimum) solution for a specific parameter
and the corresponding values to the problem’s decision variables, all the while other
parameters of the problem remain known. A constraint programming optimization
model has the same structure as a linear programming model, namely a set of decision
variables, an objective function to maximize or minimize, and a set of constraints, but
the relationship of constraints is not limited to linearity. These types of problems
only support discrete decision variables (integer or binary) and activity or time-based
variables, while a linear programming model enables the use of both discrete and con-
tinuous decision variables. These two techniques have their strong and weak points
but can be used to solve problems to optimality - it is up to the model creator to de-
cide which one to use or combine them in a way that fits best to their study. System
dynamics comprise an approach for representing and simulating the dynamic behav-
ior of complex non-linear systems over time. This method pertains to computer-aided
mathematical modelling and it can be applicable to all types and sizes of systems, sup-
porting the visualization of the interaction and changes of different system elements
periodically [116]. Analytic models are mathematical models with a closed-form solu-
tion, meaning that they describe a system with the use of equations and present the
solution as a mathematical analytic function. There is no requirement for linearity,
thus this method enables the understanding of the model’s behavior only by looking
at the model’s description before having the numerical solution available. Other mod-
els aim at creating a conceptual framework to enable understanding and successful
decision-making for policymakers and managers. The simulation is typically a system
modelled in software and run under specific variables, with known or unknown values,
followed by an analysis of the study after the simulation runs its course. A popular
software solution for life cycle assessment in cold chain logistics is SimaPro. Compa-

nies are likely to use some sort of simulation tool or software since this enables them
to collect, analyze, and monitor their data over time. Lastly, an experimental study
investigates the effect of any given phenomenon under specific conditions and time-
frame in order for the examined results to be used in further research and knowledge
reference.
2.5.3
Industry
As has been discussed, there are multiple different industries continually associated
with cold chain logistics, due to the increasing number of perishable commodities
getting transported for long distances. Food and agricultural products comprise the
highest represented market segments within cold chain logistics. Often times a model
deals with a specific commodity already located within the food industry, for example,
dairy products. In that case, the research is often sorted under the variant Perishable
food supply chain (PFSC). It is not particularly unusual that research is found not
looking at a specific commodity, but considers temperature-sensitive products on a
single or various stages of the cold chain. Therefore, the industry is classified as Cold
chain supply management (CSCM), and this might entail transportation, inventory,
or the like. It is however not unlikely that a research study examines a very specific
niche of the market (Other), usually focusing on a specific stage or particular piece of
equipment or technology within the cold value chain. An example of this is the reefer
container market in ports or alternative sources of energy in cold chain logistics.
2.5.4
Mode of transport
The dominant mode of transport in the examined field is road transport enabled
by trucks, hence it is the most commonly modelled in optimization problems. In
some other literature samples, the term is more generic and it is the mode of trans-
port referred to as refrigerated vehicle. More extended approaches adopt intermodal
transportation, using a combination of railway, maritime, and road transportation.
Problems that pertain to inventory management introduce the term warehouse vehi-
cles, referring to the industrial (sometimes automated) electrical vehicles used in the
warehouse processes.
2.5.5
Carbon tax
The carbon tax is typically modeled as carbon emission cost added to the overall
expenses of the enterprise. The researcher in that case assumes a cost per unit of
CO2 emitted from the operations they take into account. Carbon tax comprises the
most common way of incorporating emission regulations into a study.

2.5.6
Carbon trading
Carbon trading is the second most popular method used to account for carbon emis-
sions from a regulatory point of view. In this way, the researchers model the emissions
quota that the enterprise is allowed to emit without getting charged. Most frequently,
the models consider the extra cost (or tax) arising when the enterprise exceeds the
carbon quota, but it can also be maintained that the company cannot exceed this
quota for environmental purposes. This is modeled by imposing a constraint on the
overall amount of emissions.
2.5.7
Other parameter tied to carbon emissions
This parameter is introduced by the author to explain in what way each study is view-
ing or modelling the carbon emissions when there is no regulatory measure considered.
In other words, when there is no reference to carbon tax or carbon trade, which are
externally imposed measures for carbon emission reduction, then the study aims to
optimize or contribute to carbon emission control using other measures, commonly in-
ternal, solely for the enterprise. This can, for instance, be the cost of electricity - this
is a unit that can directly be translated to carbon emissions. It is not particularly rare
for a model to have the objective of minimizing the overall carbon emissions without
regarding regulation thereof. Other examples of such could be the environmental im-
pact or global warming potential measured in weight of CO2 emissions. Emissions are
sometimes also translated to a carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2-eq). This is a metric
unit of measurement exploited when comparing the emissions from other greenhouse
gases, such as methane or nitrous oxide, on the basis of their global-warming potential
(GWP), by converting amounts of other gases to the equivalent amount of carbon
dioxide with the same global warming potential [117].
2.5.8
CO2 from transport
This field is valued as YES when the carbon emissions from transportation operations
are regarded in the study. Emissions arising from transport are almost always consid-
ered since transport comprises the activity with the highest carbon emissions among
companies’ operations overall. Excepted are the models which focus particularly on
a specific stage of the supply chain, such as inventory management.
2.5.9
Distance considered
The criterion of distance is introduced in relation to emissions arising from trans-
port operations.
The distance traversed plays a great role in a study accounting
for emissions from transport, principally associated with the consumption of fuel per
kilometer or more importantly, carbon emissions per kilometer.

2.5.10
Freight considered
The load is also used as a form of measurement with regards to emissions since on
one side, a higher load affects the mechanical performance of a truck negatively in
terms of carbon emissions while on the other side it increases the utilization of the
vehicle, meaning that net payload of a truck is low during transport. The load-based
emission percentage has been examined by Turkensen in 2016 [118], and it is a field
of high interest within logistics, since decision-making based on load can significantly
affect not only the cost, but also the environmental performance of a supply chain.
2.5.11
Vehicle specs considered
Various vehicle particulars play a significant role in energy consumption and conse-
quentially the corresponding emissions during transport too. Vehicle technical specifi-
cations such as combustion and ignition can affect fuel consumption during transport,
thus they are modeled along with transportation emissions.
2.5.12
Speed considered
Speed is oftentimes related to energy efficiency models, as it is a factor directly affect-
ing the fuel consumption during transport. Road and rail transport are not concerned
as much with respect to speed limitations, but in maritime transportation, optimiza-
tion of ship speed has been extensively discussed for environmental and economic
purposes [85].
2.5.13
CO2 from refrigeration
This criterion is deemed necessary considering the significant role refrigeration plays
in cold chain logistics.
Cost related to refrigeration is typically always accounted
for in this research area. It is not however always the case that researchers consider
emissions from refrigeration in their studies of cold chain logistics. The most common
way to compute emissions from refrigeration in warehouse or transportation is through
electricity consumption but it can also associate with fuel consumption from the
various vehicle parameters accounted for during transportation.
2.5.14
Ambient temperature considered
Ambient temperature greatly affects the rate of refrigeration and therefore the elec-
trical power consumed for that refrigeration. During transport or storage, the rate of
heat transfer between the outdoor air and the refrigerated air inside is highly depen-
dent on the ambient temperature. This is most commonly due to door openings when
products are removed, loaded or unloaded, or from conduction through the surfaces
==PAGE:52==
34
2 Cold chain taxonomy
(walls, roofs, and floors) of the refrigerated warehouses and/or vehicles, including the
length of the journey [110]. If the ambient temperature is higher, then refrigeration
need is also increased, and the emissions related to it rise accordingly.
2.5.15
CO2 from storage
The process of storage operations, mainly loading and unloading, is not often modeled
with respect to emissions, because most of the relevant research focuses on transport
emissions since it tends to be the most dominant figure. Carbon contribution from
storage is typically measured through electricity consumed from the warehouse vehi-
cles.
2.5.16
CO2 from degradation
Degradation refers to quality deterioration and naturally implies wasted commodities.
Wasted commodities have a negative correlation to customer satisfaction and profit
while at the same time waste of products means waste of resources employed during
production, such as water or electricity.
Waste of resources is directly associated
with carbon emissions. This parameter is modelled much more easily when a specific
commodity is examined. This process relates to the commodity specifications mea-
suring the freshness and survival rate of any given product. As discussed by Ma et
al. [94], the deterioration rate can be a constant value, investment-level dependent,
or time-sensitive. Degradation is oftentimes modeled together with waste.
2.5.17
CO2 from packaging
Packaging processes require energy and resources, which can directly be translated to
CO2 emissions. The modelling of carbon emissions from this area is usually enabled
through the amount of electricity consumed during the packaging process, but it is
not as common within the studies of cold chain logistics.
2.5.18
CO2 from electricity
Electricity is one of the most prevalent ways to account for carbon emissions, as the
generation of electricity is directly associated with non-environmental-friendly carbon
emissions. In the following Figure 2.3 [6], it can be seen that only 25% of electricity
comes from renewable energy sources, such as the wind power and solar power, while
38% comes from coal, 23% from natural gas, 10% is nuclear and 4% comes from oil.
As discussed before, the required electrical energy for different operations is used in
many studies to express carbon emissions generated by each of those operations.

2.5.19
CO2 from incineration
Along the previous line of discussion, incineration is translated to CO2 emissions or
CO2-eq, due to the transportation towards the landfill required, but more impor-

in the IPCC emission guidelines [119], the process of waste incineration involves the
generation of GHG emissions, with the highest percentage of gases to be CO2 (carbon
dioxide) as well as N2O (nitrous oxide), NOx ( oxides of nitrogen) NH3 (ammonia)
and organic C – all measured as total carbon, with CO2 being the chief pollutant.
2.5.20
Miscellaneous
In this section, some model functions that are not relevant to carbon emissions are
collected. In logistics optimization models, time windows are commonly used. The
recipient of the products flowing in the supply chain, i.e. the customer, expects the
delivery to be performed within a specified time slot. If the time frame is not re-
spected, additional costs (punishment) arise for the logistical firm. Moreover, a fleet
of vehicles belonging to a company is typically heterogeneous. Extending this to a
model, it is found that vehicles of different capacities and characteristics will be avail-
able for allocation and use. Lastly, demand uncertainty is a rather interesting topic
in optimization modelling. Usually, demand is known in the model, also known as
deterministic. But dynamic modelling of the demand using a mathematical distribu-
tion allows for a stochastic approach - naturally, this approach represents actual life
problems more realistically.

2.7.2
Literature gaps
The majority of studies focus on operational and managerial optimization and decision-
making, while only a few of them focus on governmental responsibility for the com-
pliance and adoption rate of the private sector. Another observation shows that only
a handful of the taxonomy inputs examine or model the social dimension of sustain-
ability, which is exemplarily designed by Zhang et al. (2019) [93] on their bi-level
programming model, minimizing total social cost and the total cost of the enterprise.
Unexpectedly, the alternative resources of energy are not adequately discussed, since
only Wu et al. (2019) [100] and Meneghetti et al. (2018) [7] examine the topic. The
field of cold chain logistics has a vast and yet unused potential of emissions mitigation
by the introduction of renewables, especially solar power.
A great part of the taxonomy literature looks into the sustainability of cold supply
chains within the context of China, yet India’s developing context needs to be fur-
ther represented with sustainability-oriented research. This is especially important
because the population of these two countries constitutes almost 40% (19% and 18%
respectively) of the overall world population and their economies are extremely com-
petitive, with China being the second-largest economy and India being the fifth. The
interest in the adoption of sustainable policies is of high importance, especially for
India, because it is the fastest-growing trillion-dollar economy in the world.
The observed focus on road transport and the lack of research within intermodal
means show that sustainable transition regarding transport has not yet progressed.
This is of special significance, given the emissions of this sector in and outside the
context of cold chain logistics. Finally, it is seen that emissions from the different
supply chain and logistics operations are not uniformly considered throughout the

2.7.3
Recommendations for the future
Above all, future researchers are encouraged to focus on this intersection between gov-
ernmental decision-making and sustainability, examining its impact on the adoption
rate and emissions reduction. Further research on the social dimension of sustain-
able development and its modelling will give new insights on the relationship of cold
supply chains with the social environment, as well as elevating the research’s pur-
pose in meeting the outlined Sustainable Development Goals by 2030. Additionally,
the paramount importance of perishable products in human life, mainly food and
pharmaceuticals, makes the social character of sustainability almost inherent to cold
chains, therefore necessitating further examination. Scholars are urged to investigate
the integration of renewable resources of energy into cold chain logistics since energy
requirements in the form of fuel or electricity comprise the biggest determinants of car-
bon emissions within the industry. The cold-chain logistics of countries with very high
emissions and rapidly growing economies, like India, need to bridge the gap between
industry and academia covering all dimensions of sustainability. In addition, research
on the aspect of transportation from different perspectives, such as electrical vehicles
and intermodal patterns, will motivate the adoption of environmentally-friendly dis-
tribution practices and facilitate lower emissions. Lastly, research on emission gen-
eration throughout the various processes (e.g. storage) of the supply chain needs to
intensify in order for the industry to comprehend and transition to greener practices
concerning the entire value chain.

The most important relationship positively affecting operational performance was
found in collaborative green transportation and cold storage. More specifically, through
optimization of package size, multimodal transportation, effective capacity utilization,
reduction of transportation and material handling, and reduction of waste and pack-

Next to this, green manufacturing comprises a great drive towards increasing op-
erational performance. Green manufacturing implies that the company’s suppliers
comply with eco-friendly practices and guidelines for product design, recycling, and
branding. Additionally, green manufacturing necessitates energy efficiency and the
decrease of non-environmentally-friendly materials and gas emissions through the

Vertical space becomes very useful for the operations inside the warehouse with the

Photovoltaic rooftop power generation has been largely studied for residential con-
sumption but very few studies focus on industrial application, especially considering
that the performance of PVs peaks in the summer; this time of the year the refrigera-
tion requirement is also at its highest level. This model examines an R-AS/RS with
rooftop PVs for frozen products without the use of battery energy storage systems
(BESS) and without the consideration of selling electricity to the grid. It is worth
mentioning that such low temperatures of refrigeration require big portions of energy,
therefore the PVs are not expected to cover the full requirement.
The model exploits the Constraint Programming method, taking advantage of the
unlimited types of relations that can be developed among variables. This method
also offers flexibility in modifying the model compared to other methods because the
modelling and solving phases are separated. In that way, latter requirements can be
incorporated into the model using constraints while still preserving the model. The
model was developed using the Comet package which is highly recognized in the Con-
straint Programming community.
All domains of the decision variables are properly fixed to simulate the finite nature
of the problem. For the model to be as close to reality as possible, real catalogues and
design processes are followed for the racks, while the selected energy models define the
demand realistically. The objective function minimizes total yearly cost, including
investment (land and building, rack, cranes, satellites, and refrigeration equipment)
and operational (energy supply and maintenance) costs. In order to allow for compar-
ison between solutions that consider sustainability and those that don’t, the model
permits the user to select a minimum net energy consumption approach (see green
arrow in The study’s framework for integrating cold chain, PVs, and R-AS/RS design
models [7].). This is when energy withdrawal from the grid is required after using all
PV generated energy.
A multi-period approach that considers the average hour temperature per month is
adopted to realistically address the interaction between refrigeration requirements
and PV power generation, leading to 288 periods as a time reference. The refrigera-
tion loads for the different time windows of the year are calculated based on ASHRAE
(American Society of Heating, Refrigerating, and Air-Conditioning Engineers) guide-

lines. With respect to crane and remote-controlled battery charged satellite energy
consumption, the first depends on the horizontal and vertical rack configuration while
the latter is less complex, with different speeds considered based on load. The energy

and follows the trapezoidal speed profile in the acceleration/deceleration phases of
the AS/RS machine. The investment cost is estimated to be 1658 €/kW while yearly

Conclusion by authors
Industrial refrigeration, particularly the process of cold storage, has great unused
potential for energy savings, motivating the authors to use Constraint Programming
in analyzing the impact of the integration of rooftop photovoltaics in the designing
and performance of an R-AS/RS. The reference case reveals that both environmental
(reduced energy withdrawal from the grid by 16.4%) and economic (reduced yearly
total cost by 1.3%) profits can be sustained from the installation of a PV field when
the cost is minimized in the objective. Further energy savings (2.6%) can be achieved
when the energy withdrawal from the national grid is minimized in the objective,
however the total yearly cost increases by 15.1%. Supply chain variables, namely
incoming food and storage temperature, throughput, and facility location can greatly
affect the design and performance of the warehouse. An increase in incoming food
temperature and a decrease in storage temperature can be allowed by the integration
of photovoltaics, with beneficial results both environmentally and economically.
With respect to throughput, attention should be paid in the configuration of the
warehouse for the benefits of PVs to be fully exploited. Climate conditions related
to the facility location can highly affect the PV performance in terms of refrigeration
requirement and PV energy production - warmer sites have higher benefits. It is how-

5.3
Discussion
This paper scrutinizes the impact on the design and performance of an automated
refrigerating warehouse when a photovoltaic plant is installed on the roof. It also
analyzes the effect other important supply chain parameters have on its design and
performance.
The authors exert a very professional use of language and structure on the paper.
They thoroughly examine the related literature, starting with sustainability and pro-
gressively moving to cold chains, automated warehouses, and renewable energy.
The topic of research is quite intriguing, combining cold chain logistics automation
with solar power. The field of renewables in supply chain management has not been
extensively explored and this paper contributes substantially to bridging this gap
and further encouraging future studies in relevant domains. With renewable energy
at the forefront of the study, the authors challenge and motivate the area of cold
supply chain logistics, a field where economic savings and reduction of emissions from
electricity have been disregarded and more emphasis has been given to transport op-
timization.
The content is very knowledgeable and adds value to the area of research by investigat-
ing the problem from different viewpoints and for various values of the defining supply
chain variables, while the reported results are comprehensively explained. This study
can easily be read by a user without scientific background and still be understood,
due to the extra effort the authors took in interpreting their results both illustratively
with the use of charts and tables, and descriptively with words. However, for an ex-
perienced user, the explanation of results may appear overly thorough and repetitive
at times.
The results focus on economic and energy savings indicators, while interestingly GHG
emissions appear toward the end when the impact of the facility’s location on the de-
sign and performance of the warehouse is assessed. It would be very interesting to see
the avoided GHG emissions in the rest of the results, since this is a direct language
for environmental sustainability, and electricity can easily be translated to carbon or

6.1
Further discussion on findings from the three
papers reviewed on chapters 3,4, and 5
This section will serve as further discussion regarding the inter-relation of the three
papers reviewed in the previous chapters with a comparison of the taxonomy trends
developed in the second chapter.
The first paper by Raut et al. (2019) [110] focuses on contemporary Indian business
practices that promote sustainability in cold supply chains. By doing so, it highlights
the underdeveloped reverse logistics practices in the idiosyncrasy of Indian cold chain
systems, revealing that regulatory pressure is not strong enough to drive change in
the sector. Instead, companies adopt GSCM for financial purposes. There is poten-
tial for more regulatory initiatives in developing countries and regional constraints
are proven to shape those trends and impediments of sustainable adoption in the
industry. Conceptual modelling that links supply chain management performance
with sustainability is not uncommon, yet this paper interestingly connects directly
with the industry and derives information by 525 professionals within the Indian cold
chain spectrum, thereby informing their study with up-to-date and high-value data.
The second paper by Qin et al. (2019) [92] presents a model that minimizes the ratio
of cost to customer satisfaction, namely the cost of unit average customer satisfac-
tion, by taking carbon emissions into account. This study is very insightful in regard
to carbon policy impact, showcasing the price of carbon intensity as only effective
within a value interval. If the price is not within the effective range, the mitigation
of carbon emissions will not promote sustainable development holistically - if too low,
the incentive is not strong enough to drive change, whereas too high will compromise
competitiveness and economic viability. This study is prominent because it exhibits
sustainability, not only measured in emissions but within a broader spectrum of har-
monization of the economic, social, and environmental dimensions. Additionally, it
==PAGE:96==
78
Conclusion
sheds light on the shared responsibility of the private and public sectors, i.e. compa-
nies and governments towards the green transition.
The last paper by Meneghetti et al. (2019) [7] comprises research regarding the inte-
gration of renewable energy, specifically solar panels, and their effect on the economic
and environmental performance of refrigerated warehouses. The introduction of al-
ternative energy resources within cold supply chain management and logistics is very
promising, as the research in that field is poorly represented. This paper also succeeds
in accentuating the multi-faceted nature of sustainability, exploring synchronous envi-
ronmental and economic sustainability. The region can greatly affect the application
of sustainability practices, both in terms of renewable energy applicability and with
respect to regional policies and incentives. The performance of renewables is highly
dependent on climate conditions over the year, but the payback will also be relevant
to the carbon intensity adopted by the government. In that regard, this paper places
private organizations, public institutions, and governments responsible for putting
sustainability into realization.
The joint distribution is remarked in both the first and second papers, where its great
importance in attaining sustainability in cold chain logistics is pointed out. The first
and third papers stress the effect of regional impact; both discuss the topic from the
standpoint of governmental stimuli and policies, while Meneghetti et al. (2019) [7]
emphasize the climatic impact on the solar panel performance. The second and third
papers implement a sensitivity analysis, evaluating the impact of various parameters
such as carbon price under the carbon trading scheme or the carbon tax scheme sub-
sequently. Storage and ambient temperature parameters are also considered. This
multi-level analysis shows that the parameters affecting sustainable performance are
endogenous as well as exogenous, as indicated by the taxonomy criteria.
Lastly, the same two papers discuss the economic and environmental sides of sustain-
ability, with the second paper incorporating customer satisfaction on their model’s
objective. It can be inferred that customer satisfaction extends to the competitiveness
of the enterprise and the underlying economic viability. A social character can also
be attached to this parameter. This is primarily because economic sustainability safe-
guards the healthy development of companies and economies, subsequently enabling a
higher quality of life for the people who live and work in these communities. Secondly,
because customer satisfaction elevates the quality of products and services that peo-
ple receive. However, a direct approach to the modelling of social sustainability was
not attempted by any of the three papers. It is therefore crucial for future researchers
to touch upon this topic and efficiently incorporate social performance criteria into
cold chain management and logistics research revolving around sustainability.

6.2
Conclusion
As the trajectory of our world’s population exponentially increases, parallel with the
prolongation of a human’s lifespan and the growth of developing economies, harmful
emissions on the planet have reached an unprecedented level.
It is of paramount
importance that the increasing need of perishable goods is met by sustainable supply
chains. As Ban Ki-moon, the United Nations Secretary-General from 2007 to 2016,
infamously stated in November 2016, ”We don’t have plan B because there is no
planet B.”
Research on sustainability within the area of cold supply chain logistics has intensi-
fied during recent years, following the emergence of internationally traded perishable
commodities. This thesis develops and presents a taxonomy consisting of a selected
38 papers that all relate to cold chain logistics and sustainability based on various
criteria. The modelling of sustainability, as well as the endogenous (e.g. transporta-
tion) and exogenous parameters (ambient temperature, carbon emission reduction
regulations) which affect it, became the main fields of examination.
There are multiple ways of conceptually or mathematically modelling sustainability
within the context of cold supply chain logistics. Findings from the taxonomy show
that a majority of studies develop optimization models focusing mainly on minimiza-
tion of cost, with sustainable options such as minimization of energy consumption or
carbon emissions being less popular alternatives. The governing mode of transport
in most models is the trucks while almost two-thirds of the literature base account
for emission reduction measures, with carbon tax being slightly more popular.
Three influential papers from the taxonomy were selected based on the originality
of their topic and discussed in the previous chapters. The first paper by Raut et al.
(2019) [110] develops a conceptual model revealing that ”collaborative green trans-
portation & cold storage” and ”green manufacturing” comprise two central sustain-
able practices towards increased operational performance. Regulatory and competi-
tive pressure also has a smaller yet positive impact on the Indian cold chain market.
This area of research is interesting for other developing contexts and supply chain
functions because it allows the testing of formulated hypotheses with the aid of statis-
tical tools. The pursuit of employee training and collaboration with academia within
”green transportation & cold storages collaboration” is strongly suggested to man-
agers and decision-makers.
In their mathematical model minimizing the cost of unit satisfied customer by con-
sidering emissions, Qin et al. (2019) [92] find out that there is a specific range for the
carbon trading price, under which emissions effectively diminish. Governments should
then cautiously select the price of carbon trading within the effective interval, keeping
in mind that very low or very high prices will not be beneficial for sustainable develop-

carbon emissions that are dependent on market competition. New technologies and
practices in terms of transportation, such as low-cost and environmentally-friendly
vehicles and joint distribution transportation modes, can have a positive impact on
both emission reduction and customer satisfaction. Researchers are encouraged to
further investigate the relation of customer satisfaction with other cold supply chain
parameters and stress out the utmost necessity of developing a greener mindset in
the cold chain industry.
The impact of an integrated photovoltaic field in industrial automated and refriger-
ated warehouses is examined by Meneghetti et al. (2019) through their Constraint
Programming model [7]. When the cost is minimized with the use of PVs, economic

achievable even if the temperature of incoming food increases or storage temperatures

highly dependent on regional inputs, warmer regions favor it more. Lastly, national
carbon intensity and the price of electric energy regionally impact the return of in-

energy systems will help detect more energy savings.
Location can impact the adoption of sustainability from a perspective of regulatory

A further influence on sustainable performance is accounted for in the

sis, as highlighted by the taxonomy criteria. Joint distribution practices enable the
collaborative action of supply chain actors towards more efficient and sustainable dis-
tribution methods, presenting a challenge for future research.
Entrepreneurial responsibility is majorly represented through operational optimiza-
tion and managerial decision-making. More research on the governmental role may
expose new approaches toward the achievement of sustainable development, or op-
timize the existing ones. What is more, the social scope of sustainability is yet to
be examined from a deeper perspective, as this dimension of sustainability is greatly
underrepresented in cold chain logistics sustainability-oriented research. The intro-
duction of sustainability within the Indian cold chain context is considered essential
given the rapidly changing pattern of its economy and demand.
Alternative resources are not yet integrated into cold chain logistics research, making
this the biggest potential gap to bridge in this particular area of research. Alterna-
tive energy will be the backbone of future sustainable development, research cover

supply chain under carbon tax regulation
Journal: Journal of Cleaner Production — 2017, Volume 166
The elevated level of public and organizations awareness concerning sustainability has
necessitated the adoption of environmentally conscious practices in the management
of supply chains. As such, classical inventory models wherein the sole measure of
performance is cost minimization are no longer appropriate to handle such emerging
and pressing issue. This paper assesses the impact of accounting for carbon emissions,
resulting from transportation and storage activities of a cold item, in the context of a
multi stage supply chain comprised of a plant/warehouse, a distribution center (DC)
and a retailer. We present an operational cost minimization model, a carbon footprint
minimization model and a hybrid economic and environmental minimization model
where all models seek to determine the optimal lot sizing and shipping quantities,
alongside the number of trucks to be used in the upstream and downstream direc-
tions of the supply chain as well as the number of freezer units utilized at the DC
which is owned by a third party logistics provider. The structural properties for the
optimal solution of the three models are identified and solution algorithms are also
proposed. Upon conducting computational experiments, it turns out that the incorpo-
ration of carbon related costs, through carbon tax regulation, may call for adjusting
the adopted operational policy which results in a minor increase in the operational
cost. However, this increase is outweighed by the savings resulting from the carbon
related costs. Numerical results of the sensitivity analysis on the carbon tax policy
indicate that as the tax rate increases, substantial reductions in the amount of carbon
footprint emitted are realized and cost savings are also achieved when adopting the
lot sizing and shipping policy of the third/integrated model over the operational cost
minimization model.

Meneghetti et al. (2019) [108]
Title: Energy-efficient frozen food transports: the Refrigerated Routing Prob-
lem
Journal: International Journal of Production Research — 2019
Given the growing importance of cold chains and the need to promote sustainable
processes, energy efficiency in refrigerated transports is investigated at operational
level. The Refrigerated Routing Problem is defined, involving multi-drop deliveries
of palletised unit loads of frozen food from a central depot to clients. The objective
is to select the route with minimum fuel consumption for both traction and refriger-
ation. The problem formulation considers speed variation due to traffic congestion
phenomena, as well as decreasing load on board along the route as successive clients
are visited. Transmission load for exposure of the vehicle to outdoor temperatures
and infiltration load at door opening are modelled, taking into account outdoor con-
ditions varying along the day and the year. The resulting multi-period problem is
modelled and solved by means of Constraint Programming. Test scenarios come from
a real local network for frozen bread dough distributed to supermarkets.
Results
show how fuel minimisation leads to the selection of different routes in comparison to
the traditional total travel distance or time objectives. Energy savings are affected
by demand distribution among the clients, departure time, number of visits per tour,
seasonality and location of the delivery network.

Description of the PV plant and power generation
model
Input data
The data for the calculation of photovoltaic field production are found from the
CMSAF database of the Photovoltaic Geographical Information System (PVGIS),
developed by the Joint Research Centre of the European Commission.
The four
inputs presented below are required:
• Optimal tilt angle bo
• In-plane irradiance G on a surface facing south (i.e., azimuth angle a = 0◦)
• Ambient Temperature Tamb
• Wind speed v
The last three inputs have been assessed for the time-periods of the model through
the average for each hour of each day per month, on a multi-year horizon considered
by the PVGIS database, as seen in Figure 8.
Model formulation
The warehouse rooftop where the PV field is installed is horizontal, has nrow number
of rows, and each of them has nmodr numnber of PV modules, see Figure 8 (a).
Between two rows, distance D is kept, so that the modules are not covered by shadow,
while Lmod is the module length, d is the declination during the winter solstice (i.e.,
23.4◦5), F is the latitude of the location, Wmod is the width of the PV module, Wroof

is the width of the warehouse roof, and We is the space that needs to be left at
the edges of the roof for installation issues. For the calculations, please refer to the
equations (1)-(4). PST C represents the nominal power of the PV plant at standard test
condition, while PSTCmod is the nominal power of each PV module. The module’s
efficiency of energy conversion depends on the module’s temperature Tmod (depends
on ambient temperature Tamb, on the solar irradiance G and on the local wind speed
v) and the solar irradiance, with the coefficient U0 describing the effect of the radiation

coefficients are defined by the module’s technology, for crystalline silicon the values
