Abstract
The following thesis examines the structural behaviour of pre-fabricated Timber-Concrete Composite
beams (TCC) with the novel use of notches and mechanical fasteners. The study contributes to a list
of experimental work, focusing on recent experiments including a full-scale four-point bending test of
fourteen TCC-beams spanning six meters. The test subjects had multiple designs with variations in
notch width and artificial gap at notched connection. First key observation was found in the bending
stiffness in the serviceability limit state.
The findings states that notch width and fastener have no
influence on the bending stiffness in the serviceability limit state and the degree of composite action.
With the use of Digital Image Correlation, a time series was made, linking the initiation of cracks to the
load instances, where the stiffness was found reduced. Additionally, it was found that the mechanical
fastener maintained composite action in the case of local failure at the notches. A direct correlation was
found between relative displacements between concrete and timber and activity in the fastener. Lastly,
it was observed that concrete had zero contribution to the total bending stiffness in the Ultimate Limit
state.

Introduction
As of now, the building industry is one of the main contributors to CO2 emissions and hereby global warm-
ing, which has kick-started a wave of research projects with focus on sustainable solutions within the field
of structural engineering. Timber has long been popular for its aesthetic appeal and sustainability, but has
yet to be used as slabs for commercial buildings. This is due to the modern requirements in e.g. structural
performance, fire safety and acoustics. A solution to these problems can be found by combining timber
with concrete, as the weaknesses found in the timber is counteracted by the strengths of the concrete. For
decades, research has been made on the structural performance of timber-concrete composite decks in order
to see commercial use. This thesis aims to further drive the research in timber-concrete based sustainable
solutions within the field of structural engineering.
This thesis is a component of an experimental study on Timber-Concrete Composite Slabs (TCC) with
specially designed notched connections conducted at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). In the
spring of 2022, a small scale push-off test was conducted on a TCC configuration with a prime focus on
local behaviour of the notches. In this study, full-scale tests of TCC beams with notched connections are
conducted with focus on the local response in the notches, as well as an examination of global response.

2.1
Specimen Components and Characteristics
The Timber Concrete Composite beam (TCC) is composed of a timber element at the bottom and two
concrete elements on top. The connection between the timber and concrete elements are made with six
notched connections with three notches at the end of each element finalised with a fastener at the notch
location, which ensures a mechanically robust connection. Crafting these elements demand high precision in
execution and solid coordination between the timber and concrete manufacturer. The timber used for the
composite beam is glued and laminated timber class GL30h, and is delivered by DLT Group A/S. The notches
in the timber are crafted using a CNC machine (Computer Numerical Control) ensuring precise execution
of the desired design. A corresponding casting mould was delivered to Contiga A/S, who manufactured
the concrete elements using the mould ensuring a guaranteed fit with the timber element. The concrete
elements were manufactured with a desired compressive strength ranging between C20/25. The concrete
and timber were delivered to DTU over several batches and assembled in the laboratory under controlled
climate conditions. The last step in the assembly process was to join the two concrete elements using a
mortar joint. The process from manufacturing the elements to assembly at DTU is shown in fig. 1. In
addition to the timber and concrete elements, a large piece of timber and a significant amount of concrete
cylinders were delivered to DTU, which allowed for supplementary classification of the material properties.
Figure 1: Time series from manufacturing to assembly
In the experiment, fourteen specimen were assembled and divided into seven test series with three different
key designs. This includes a pre-stress force of 30 kN, variation in notch width and a 1 mm artificial gap at
the notched connection. The indexing of test series and corresponding designs are shown in table 1.

2.2
Test Setup and Data Acquisition
The specimen were tested under a four-point bending configuration. The test setup was designed by a project
manager and executed by laboratory employees at DTU. The distance between the supports were six meters
and the load was applied at 1/3 and 2/3 of the span. The design of the supports allowed for horizontal
movement and rotation of the beam and the cylinders bearing the actuator/piston were fixated laterally,
ensuring no undesired lateral forces. Lastly, a steel beam was used as a load distributor with steel plates
located at the designated load location. Teflon was laminated between the steel plates at the interfacing steel
components, ensuring no undesired lateral forces. A 3D-model is illustrated in fig. 2 with supplementary
images of the test setup shown in fig. 3.
Figure 2: 3-D model of the four-point bending test setup

A significant data quantity was collected during each test, which required a variety of data acquisition tools.
At the front side of the beam (fig. 3a), the optical method in digital image correlation (DIC) is used to
measure the deflection and local behaviour of the notches. On the backside (fig. 3b), vertical and horizontal
Linear Variable Differential Transformers (LVDTs) were mounted at notch locations in order to capture the
relative movement between the concrete and timber. Additionally, two LVDTs were mounted at the ends
of the specimen. In order to measure the force in the fastener, donut load cells were installed between the
screw nut and the specimen. Lastly, a special configuration was made in order to capture the time instance
of the establishment of contact for the series with notch imperfections. Cobber tape was installed at each
notch constructing an electrical circuit. An electric signal was transmitted through the circuit and in the
case, where the data acquisition receives the signal, contact is established. If no signal is received, contact
is not established. Lastly, the entire test setup as described in this section was connected to a wireless data
logger. Three Lord Microstrain model V-Link-200 were used to log all the data including the actuator load.
The positions of the data acquisition components and corresponding indexing are illustrated in fig. 4.

2.3
Test Programme
The main test was performed in accordance to DS/EN 26891 [1] and has three characteristic load phases.
The first load phase was load-determined, which lasted until reaching 40% of the estimated load. This was
followed by an unloading phase until 10% of estimated load was reached. Finally, the specimen was loaded
until failure and was deformation-controlled. The project manager defined the estimated load for each series
based on simulations. The sketch from the test programme is illustrated in fig. 5, which is taken directly
from the standard.

4
Investigation of the Elastic-Plastic Transition
In the previous section, the behaviour of the TCC-beam was examined in the first loading phase, describing
how the beams behaves in the serviceability limit state. In this section, the transition from elastic to plas-
tic behaviour is examined with the use of DIC. This analysis is divided into three parts each with specific
objectives. The first analysis presents the failure mechanism in the concrete at the notched connection,
highlighting the final crack propagation with the use of DIC. The second analysis focuses on the crack devel-
opment with respect to the time-load curve. The main goal is to establish a direct correlation between the
crack propagation and the change in global response. The last analysis focuses on the horizontal slip and
vertical gap between the concrete and timber at the notches. Ultimately, it is desired to find a correlation
between the screw force and the relative displacement between the elements at the notched connection. The
two specimens 1(0-0-0)200-2 and 1(0-0-0)300-2 have been chosen for the analyses. Notch 4-6 are examined
in both specimens.
Before presenting the DIC-analysis, general comments will be made on the global response and the ob-
served failure mechanisms at the notched connection.
4.1
Global Response
With the exclusion of specimen 0(0-0-0)400+nf, the same characteristics in the response were observed for all
specimens. The response curves can be divided into three segments, where the specimens show differentiated
characteristics. This is illustrated through the load-displacement curve for specimen 1(0-0-0)200-2 in fig. 11.
Figure 11: Segmentation of the load-deflection curve for specimen 1(0-0-0)300-2
The first segment I is the linear elastic behaviour, which starts ceasing as the curve drops and becomes
non-linear presumably due to crack initiation at the notched connection. The next segment II is denoted
as the transition phase, which is characterised by a highly non-linear behaviour. A special instance occurs
in this phase and is located at the load 55kN, where the load-displacement curve flattens. At this instance,
ideal plastic behaviour is observed. This instance will be referred to as the yielding load and is presumably
due to plastification of the notched connections. For a small segment, ideal plastic behaviour is observed
and as the load-deflection curves start developing, the last segment III is initiated, denoted as the plastic
regime. The specimen 0(0-0-0)400+nf, containing no fastener, saw a complete loss of composite action at
the instance of notch failure. For the remaining specimen with fastener, it is observed that composite action
is preserved highlighting the influence a fastener brings to the global stability. In the plastic regime and
for the majority of the specimen, a logarithmic growth is observed in the load-deflection curve indicating a
reduction in stiffness to the development of cracks in the concrete.

Crack Development in Relation to the Time-Load Curve
The analysis conducted on the crack development contains a large quantity of results from GOM-correlate.

full time series are found in Appendix F.
The development in cracks will be described through three load instances. The first load instance is around
the formation of the first crack, the second is prior to the ideal-plastic regime and the last load instance
is found in the plastic regime. The GOM results for notch 6 is selected for specimen 1(0-0-0)200-2 and
notch 5 is selected for 1(0-0-0)300-2. The same characteristics can be found in the remaining notches in the
respective specimen.
4.5.1
Initial Crack - figs. 25 and 26
The initial crack starts developing around the load instance P = 47kN and P = 54kN for specimen 1(0-0-
0)200-2 and 1(0-0-0)300-2 respectively. Although subtle around this load instance, the slope in the time-load
curve shows a decrease in steepness due to the formation of the first crack. The main difference between
the crack formation in the specimen is found in the direction of the propagation. The cracks in specimen
1(0-0-0)200-2 had a tendency to propagate vertically, whereas the cracks in 1(0-0-0)300-2 had a horizontal
inclination. The initial cracks in both specimens are induced by a rotational failure mechanism sketched in
fig. 25, where an enclosed volume of concrete rotates around the contact point. This failure mechanism was
not finalised due to compressive failure being reached first.
4.5.2

The specimen show ideal plastic behaviour at the load instances P = 55kN and P = 70kN for specimen

At these
load instances, the crack formation from the DIC-results shows clear signs of compressive failure in the
concrete. The main observation show that the transition loads from ’elastic-esque’ to plastic behaviour is
highly influenced by the notch width given that the compressive strength in the concrete is roughly the
same for both specimens. Based on the cylinder test [3], the average compressive strength for specimen
1(0-0-0)200-2 was found as fc,200 = 25MPa and fc,300 = 30.8MPa for 1(0-0-0)300-2, which may indicate that
the influence of notch width is drastically underestimated solely based on the transition loads illustrated in
figs. 27 and 29.

Firstly, some generals remarks will be made regarding the use of GOM-correlate. This is targeted directly
towards the analysis made in section 4.6. It is acknowledged that the usage of GOM-correlate is sub-optimal,
and can be optimized. The use of two reference points allowed for unnecessary potential errors, which might
invalidate the results. The selected reference points were manually executed for all notches and therefore
not optimally aligned in the vertical axis leading to skewed results. Another important factor is the topic
of precision. Since this analysis deals with displacements in a micro scale, the potential of gaining invalid
results is high, due to the lack of acknowledgement and control of potential sources of errors.
Assuming the technical aspect with GOM is sufficient, a few topics will be mentioned that might have
affected the results in section 4.6. Direct comparison of results between specimens should be carefully con-
sidered, as the chosen reference points between the series are different. As previously stated, the reference
points are significantly closer for the 1(0-0-0)200-series compared to the 1(0-0-0)300-series due to the large
rupture zone around the contact area. This might also explain why negative displacements were measured,
as undesired elastic deformations in the elements were included. Another factor that might have influenced
the results is the occurrence of curvature. At the beginning of the test, the reference points remain plane
and as the specimen curves, the horizontal and vertical displacement are measured in an axis that are not
plane with the cross-section.
It was stated that the results shown in table 8 indicated that the horizontal displacement was more in-
fluential to the development of the screw force compared to the vertical displacement. Needless to say, in
order to support the statement the analysis should be done on the remaining specimens. Lastly, some gen-
eral aspects of the fastener has not been touched upon. For this study, the influence of pre-tensioning force
has been neglected, and so was the lateral displacements of the fastener which was observed for specimen
1(0-0-0)200-2.

through compression, illustrated with red arrows in fig. 41. The composite action introduces normal forces

CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
The main goal of this thesis was to achieve greater understandings on the structural behaviour of Timber-
Concrete Composite beams TCC. The research was based on measured global response of the TCC experi-
ments and was subsequently divided into three major analysis.
The first analysis focuses on the structural behaviour of the TCC beam in the serviceability limit state.
The bending stiffness was extracted from the load-deflection curves for specimens with various notch widths
and for a specimen with no fastener. Based on a sample size of 7 specimens, the study concludes that the
notch width and the inclusion of a fastener has no effect on the bending stiffness in the serviceability limit
state. Additionally, the experimental bending stiffness was compared to the cases with no- and full composite
action. The degree of composite action was found ranging from 64% to 69%.
The second analysis focuses on the local behaviour of the notches with respect to the global response,
using Digital Image Correlation. It was observed that the first crack propagation in the concrete resulted in
a transition from a linear to non-linear behaviour in the response curve. Compressive failure in the concrete
at the notched connection was observed for all notches and at this load instance, the notched connection show
plastic behaviour. The crack development stagnates during the transition to the plastic regime, all-while
rapid development was observed in screw force.
Different geometry in failure mechanisms was observed in the concrete. At the instance of failure at the
notched connection, a small rupture zone was observed in the concrete in specimen 1(0-0-0)200, compared
to specimen 1(0-0-0)300. Additionally, it was observed that the load instance in which compressive failure
occurred was significantly lower for 1(0-0-0)200 compared to 1(0-0-0)300, indicating that the notch width
has high influence on the yield load. This statement is furtherly supported by inspection of the compressive
strength, as the compressive strength is greater for 1(0-0-0)200 compared to the compressive strength found
in 1(0-0-0)300.
To round of the second analysis, an investigation of local displacement at the notched connection was
conducted with the use of DIC. Relative horizontal and vertical displacements were measured at the contact
points and compared to the measured screw forces. The results of the investigation show that the relative
displacements between concrete and timber start developing at the instance of notch failure. The same
rapid development is observed in the screw force, indicating a clear correlation between displacements at
the notches and activity in screw forces. Lastly, it was observed that the relative horizontal displacements
showed more development compared to the relative vertical displacements.
The third and final analysis consist of an estimation of the ultimate load. The calculation was done for
specimen 1(0-0-0)200 and 1(0-0-0)300. The ultimate capacity for timber was based on experiment measure-
ments. A simplified model was considered, from which the load share was defined by the ratio in bending
stiffness. Three calculation were made: 1) A case, where the bending stiffness was extracted directly from
the load-deflection curve and from the ULS-region; 2) Analytical calculation of the bending stiffness for an
uncracked cross-section; and 3) Same calculation as in 2), but with a cracked cross-section. The first method
showed a large underestimation of the ultimate capacity with a percentage deviation of -31.8% and -18.3% for
1(0-0-0)200 and 1(0-0-0)300 respectively. The second method showed a large overestimation of the ultimate
capacity with a percentage deviation of 22.7% and 33% for 1(0-0-0)200 and 1(0-0-0)300 respectively. The
third method had results in closer agreement with the experiment with a percentage deviation of -20.1& and
-11% for 1(0-0-0)200 and 1(0-0-0)300 respectively
Lastly, the global bending stiffness was extracted from the ULS-region and compared to the bending stiff-
ness of the timber. It was found that the bending stiffness was identical, indicating that the timber alone
contributes to the global stiffness.
