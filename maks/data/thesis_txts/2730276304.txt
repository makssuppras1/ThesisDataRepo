Abstract
The escalating demand for shipping in recent decades, accompanied by the industry’s growing
environmental footprint, has compelled numerous organizations and companies to explore effective
decarbonization strategies. To improve the efficiency of ships, this project employs a comprehensive
analysis of noon report data, complemented by AIS and weather data, across a fleet of seven
vessels. This meticulous approach aims to monitor ship performance, identify one suitable key
performance indicator for monitoring hull fouling, and ascertain precise timings for hull cleanings
and associated maintenance activities. For this aim, the added resistance due to fouling of the vessel
is calculated based on the added fuel consumption due to fouling. The main engine consumption
of each ship is reported in the noon reports and is assumed to be responsible for all the resistance
components, which here are only considered to be calm water resistance, wind resistance, added
wave resistance, and fouling resistance.
The first three resistance components are determined
through empirical formulas and converted to power and consumption. The difference between this
consumption and the reported consumption is assumed to be the added consumption due to fouling.
Further, the fouling resistance and its coefficient over time are calculated. Furthermore, the fouling
growth is predicted, and a comparative analysis of fouling growth rates among different coatings
is conducted. This project introduces an innovative approach to illustrating the progression of
fouling by employing ”ship static time” as a metric, in contrast to the traditional measure of ”days
since last cleaning”. ”Ship static time” is defined as periods when the ship is either idling or
operating at a speed of less than 3 knots, highlighting instances where the vessel is not in motion.
This innovative approach is poised to captivate the interest of the shipping industry and affiliated
companies, offering nuanced insights and augmenting decision-making processes for maintenance
and environmental conservation.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The maritime industry is responsible for approximately 3% of global carbon emissions. Over the
past few decades, there has been a need for the reduction of carbon emissions, also in the maritime
industry.
The need for carbon emission reduction primarily arises because of the detrimental
effect on our planet’s climate, which is significantly imposed on humans and ecological systems.
Carbon dioxide is one of the main greenhouse gases which contributes to global warming and turns
into climate change. Climate change effects include more frequent severe events, like hurricanes,
droughts, and heatwaves, sea-level rise, and shifting wildlife populations and habits. The CO2
emissions that end up in the oceans lead to ocean acidification that harms marine life. Carbon
emissions in the form of air causes air pollution and is a public health risk. The consequences of
climate change can lead to severe economic damages, such as damages to property or infrastructure.
Reducing carbon emissions is key to mitigating these impacts.
Moreover, due to an increase in the level of demand for shipping, maritime transportation carbon
emissions have increased as well. Thus, it is vital to use innovative methods to make the maritime
industry more aligned with the decarbonization plans. This can be done by using less polluting
fuels, such as green fuels, such as blue ammonia and second-generation biofuels, optimizing fuel
consumption and preventing fuel overconsumption.
One of the main reasons for the fuel overconsumption of ships is the fouling of the hull and
propeller of the vessels. There are many microorganisms living in the oceans that tend to attach
to the surfaces.
When a vessel is sailing, these species form a layer of biofouling on the hull
and propeller and niche areas of the vessel and increase the surface roughness, which leads to
higher frictional resistance of the ship and more fuel consumption. Brynolf et al. (2016) claims
that for each 0.1mm increase in the thickness of the hull fouling, the fuel consumption increases
by 6%.
Fouling of the propeller leads to poor performance of the vessel.
In order to reduce
overconsumption due to fouling, vessels go through cleaning events, including a) underwater hull
cleaning, b) propeller polishing, and c) dry-docking (cleaning, re-painting, and re-applying coating
in a dry dock). While cleaning the vessel is helpful, it is very expensive and time-consuming,
which leads to an optimization problem between sailing with a fouled hull with overconsumption
and delivering the goods faster (Thus faster earnings but more fuel cost) versus cleaning the vessel
(spending money on cleaning and spending time and have delayed or slower delivery of goods).
Furthermore, IMO has developed guidelines for controlling and managing ships’ biofouling and re-
quires vessels to conduct regular hull inspections and cleaning and use effective and environmentally
friendly anti-fouling systems.[IMEPC.378(80) (2023)] Dry-docking is part of a larger scheme of reg-
ular surveys, inspections, and maintenance required by various maritime regulations and standards,
all intended to maintain high levels of safety and performance in the global shipping industry. By
performing periodic hull cleaning and propeller polishing, vessels would have less fuel consumption
and more profitability.
While the utilization of innovative types of anti-fouling paints can mitigate the rate of fouling
formation and offer protection to the hull and propeller, the necessity for cleaning events persists.
The capacity to determine the optimal timing for these cleanings and predict them underscores
the essentiality of conducting a more nuanced and in-depth analysis of fleet performance
Consequently, conducting ship performance analysis utilizing vessel operational data proves to be
advantageous. This process enables the quantification of excessive fuel consumption attributable
to fouling, ascertained by the increased frictional resistance and surface roughness of the vessel. A
meticulous analysis of fuel overconsumption facilitates a holistic understanding, aiding in the se-
lection of appropriate key performance indicators for monitoring fleet performance. Moreover, this
insight proves instrumental in informed decision-making regarding the timing and necessity of hull
and propeller cleaning events to optimize operational efficiency and environmental sustainability.

1.2
Objectives
The main aim of this thesis is to identify, develop, and validate a model for predicting the increase
in fuel consumption attributable to the fouling of the hull and propeller of vessels. This model
employs three main datasets: AIS data, noon report data, and weather data from both CMEMS
Forecast and ERA5. These datasets pertain to a fleet of seven bulk carrier ships provided by a
Norwegian company. A specialized method utilizing empirical equations is employed to calculate
both the fouling resistance and the associated increase in fuel consumption.
In addition to the core objective of developing and validating the predictive model, this thesis
also explores the impact of cleaning events on mitigating fuel consumption.
Insights into the
effectiveness of various anti-fouling coatings and the influence of vessel ageing on fouling are gleaned
from the analysis results and discussed in detail.

2.1
Background and Motivation
Over the past few decades, the demand for maritime transportation has undergone a significant
surge, driven by the necessity to transport diverse commodities such as grains, foodstuff, industrial
equipment, and raw materials, including oil, iron ore and coal, among others. The lack of vicinity
of all the supplies and equipment for industries and companies led to a need to transport from
far away suppliers, which meant longer shipping, hence more greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions.
For example, the iron ore required by Norwegian companies is imported from Brazil, which can
take up to 21 days to sail with an average speed of 12 knots.
In the context of such lengthy
voyages, it is imperative to acknowledge the environmental implications, particularly concerning
fuel consumption and carbon emissions. A general cargo vessel consumes approximately 16 metric
tons per day of fossil fuels, contributing to an equivalent carbon emission of 50 metric tons per
day. This carbon footprint for the 21 sailing days would be 1050 metric tons, equating to the
carbon footprint of energy consumption of 132 households in Denmark over a year or the annual
carbon emissions from 228 cars. These statistics become even more astonishing when extrapol-
ated over extended periods of maritime activity. Considering the shipping industry’s significant
environmental footprint, optimising voyages for fuel efficiency while minimizing fuel consumption
becomes a paramount concern.
Due to the adverse effects of GHG emissions on the environment and society and the resulting
global warming, there is a strong need to reduce these emissions. The complexity of this endeav-
our necessitates a gradual and strategic approach towards achieving carbon neutrality. To this
end, regulatory frameworks have been instituted across various sectors to facilitate the progress-
ive reduction of net carbon emissions. The Paris Agreement constitutes a pivotal international
initiative to address the global climate change issue through collaborative commitments and co-
operation.[Climate Change UN (2019)]
International Maritime Organization (IMO) [IMO (2018)] has introduced regulations to limit car-
bon emissions from ships. IMO initially stipulated a reduction target of at least 50% in carbon
emissions compared to the levels recorded in 2008 for the maritime industry by the year 2050.
However, a significant policy shift occurred in August 2023, wherein the IMO revised its directive
to a far more ambitious goal. It now endeavours to accomplish complete carbon neutrality by 2050,
signifying the necessity for achieving a net carbon emission of zero within the maritime sector by
the year 2050.[IMO (2023a)]
Furthermore, IMO introduced factors such as EEXI (Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index) and
CII (Carbon Intensity Indicator) to enable shipping companies to track their carbon footprint,
and they came into force starting in 2023. The EEXI is a measure of a ship’s energy efficiency,
calculated based on its design and operational parameters, such as engine power, fuel consumption,
and cargo-carrying capacity. The regulation requires ships to meet a minimum EEXI value, which
is determined based on the ship’s size and type, as well as the latest technology and operational
practices available. The CII, on the other hand, is a new regulation that will require ships to meet
a particular carbon intensity target, calculated as the ratio of emissions to distance travelled over
a three-year rolling average period. The target will be based on the ship’s size and type, as well
as the average carbon intensity of the global fleet in 2019 [IMO (2021)].
However, the CII has been criticized for potentially favouring non-stop voyages, which may distort
its effectiveness in assessing environmental friendliness. As an alternative, the Energy Efficiency
Operational Indicator (EEOI) has emerged as a more comprehensive metric, focusing on a ship’s
energy efficiency during its operational phases. This metric accommodates technological advance-
ments and improvements in existing ships’ energy efficiency. The practical implementation of the
Energy Efficiency Operational Indicator is channelled through the Ship Energy Efficiency Manage-
ment Plan (SEEMP), which enables shipping entities to oversee the performance of their vessels and
fleets over extended periods. In essence, the Energy Efficiency Operational Indicator encapsulates
the concept that larger vessels tend to be more efficient in their operations, thereby contributing to
the overarching endeavour of curbing global warming. Currently, 36 shipping enterprises and their

Hence, the vessels need to sail more efficiently to minimise these footprints. Based on ship hydro-
dynamics, this can be done by having either more efficient machinery and propeller performance
or a more efficient hull. Generally, the consumption is for overcoming the resistance forces acting
on the ship. Apart from the weather effect, the resistance mainly consists of frictional and pressure
resistance. Pressure resistance is also known as wave-making resistance for ships and is mainly op-
timized by making the hull form streamlined and adopting an efficient propulsion system. On the
other hand, the frictional resistance is mainly viscous resistance and can be optimized by having
a smoother hull surface.
A ship’s hull and propeller invariably experience biofouling, a phenomenon characterized by the
accumulation of marine organisms. Biofouling is stratified into two primary categories: microfoul-
ing and macrofouling[Weber and Esmaeili (2023)]. Microfouling pertains to the establishment of
a biofilm layer on underwater surfaces, while macrofouling encompasses the adherence of larger
marine entities such as barnacles, soft corals, and marine macroalgae, culminating in the form
of a complex fouling community. This accrued biogenic matter increases the surface roughness
of maritime structures, thereby exacerbating frictional resistance. Moreover, propeller efficacy is
compromised as a consequence of this biological encumbrance.
Recent studies show that biofouling can increase fuel consumption by up to 40% if no preventative
action is taken[Milne (1990) ]. Schultz (2007) states that only the soft layer of biofouling in the hull
surface causes a 21% increase in the shaft power, while for the heavy fouling layers, this number can
reach up to 86%. Further supporting these findings, Munk et al. (2009) shows the added resistance
diagram showing increased frictional resistance during the vessel voyages over time. According to
this research, light fouling can result in a fuel consumption increase of approximately 10%, while
for heavy fouling, the fuel consumption can rise by 35%. As a result, biofouling control decisions
should be made, including using coatings and mechanical cleanings.
IMO mandates that all vessels undergo dry-docking for comprehensive structural assessments every
five years. This process entails the full removal of a vessel from water, facilitating extensive repair
work and repainting. Ship proprietors can capitalize on this occasion to eradicate as much biofoul-
ing as possible. While there are undeniable benefits in hull cleaning and propeller polishing during

sels should be subjected to periodic hull cleanings and propeller polishings. These maintenance
activities encompass procedures like biofouling eradication from the hull and the application of a

cesses and posits that with increasing vessel age, the inspection frequency might need to intensify,
potentially shortening to less than the standard five years.
Ideally, vessels would undergo hull cleaning prior to each voyage. However, the financial implica-

it challenging. Furthermore, the feasibility of such frequent cleanings varies between vessel cat-

reduce the risk of damage to the divers but are currently expensive), it can be done at the times
that the vessel is during the loading and offloading period.
Since cleaning, polishing, and painting are expensive activities, the shareholders should find proper
time for these activities to optimize their profit. Shareholders include shipowners, charterers and
operators. While the ship owner’s profit might not be affected by the amount of fuel consumption
as it is the charterer and operator’s concern, since not caring about fouling cleaning or delayed
cleanings can lead to permanent damages to the hull, and it is the ship owner responsibility for
repairing the permanent damages of the ship, it proves that the ship owner is also indirectly
involved in making or supporting these decisions regarding, for example, more frequent cleaning
events, or paying for a cleaning with higher quality. To be more specific, they shall use the most
suitable coatings for the hull surface so the fouling protection lasts longer and does not harm
the environment. The effectiveness of different coatings and the adverse effects on the marine
ecosystem is discussed in detail in the following section. While high-quality cleaning and painting
services may not be available at every port, a vessel might face a trade-off between enduring fuel
overconsumption due to a fouled hull while navigating to a port with superior cleaning services or
settling for subpar cleaning at the current location.
The vessel’s added power due to fouling should be calculated for hydrodynamic performance ana-
lysis. For this aim, the added resistance due to fouling is required. This can be calculated by
a forward or backward solution. The forward solution is to compute each resistance component
and subtract them from the total resistance. In the backward model, the fouling resistance is
assumed to be a function of the time of idling and weather variables and can be estimated using a
fouling growth model. The details of computation are provided in the theory section. The details
of biofouling, biofouling control methods, ship performance monitoring and resistance calculation
methods are discussed further in this chapter.
2.2
Biofouling
The marine environment is filled with biodiversity, specifically near the shores where the depth is
low and is considered to be shallow waters. These living organisms tend to colonize on any surface
underwater. Thus, any vessel gets biofouling on its surface, hull, and propeller, mainly when it
is near the coastlines. Fouling further both harms the functioning of mechanical equipment and
increases surface roughness and causes the vessel to have more resistance. Hence, it causes the
ship to have a lower propulsive efficiency. In order to keep the same speed for a vessel, it needs to
increase the power consumption, which means using more fuel, leading to more carbon emissions,
which adversely affect the environment. In addition to the effect of biofouling on performance
efficiency and global warming, Duarte et al. (2013) claims that biofouling also has a bio-security
risk, meaning that the vessels moving across various ports can transfer unsuitable organisms into
new locations and cause the spread of non-indigenous species, which can drastically damage the
ecosystem. This phenomena is known as the ”spread of invasive aquatic species (IAS)”, which
endanger biodiversity and cause secondary biofouling problems. [Williams et al. (2013), Havel
et al. (2015)]. Figure 1 shows the effects of bioufouling.

Various elements affect marine biofouling, such as geographical location, water temperature, sa-
linity, nutrient abundance, the velocity of water flow, water depth, light density, acidity level of
water [McDonald et al. (2009), Bixler and Bhushan (2012), and Antunes et al. (2018)]. Moreover,

important role in the amount and rate of forming the biofouling. Physical factors mainly depend
on surface properties, including micro-texture, surface charge, wettability, roughness, colours, and
contours.[Bixler and Bhushan (2012)]
McDonald et al. (2009) suggests that the acidity of the water is also one of the effective parameters
on the rate of biofouling growth. The study found that exposure to elevated carbon dioxide (CO2)
levels and reduced pH (i.e., ocean acidification conditions) harmed the barnacles’ survival, growth,
and development. Specifically, the researchers observed a decrease in juvenile barnacles’ survival
and growth rates and a delay in the onset of metamorphosis from the juvenile to the adult stage.
The researchers also observed changes in the structural properties of the barnacle shells, which
became thinner and more brittle under acidified conditions. This could increase the susceptibility
of barnacles to predating and physical damage and reduce their ability to attach to surfaces.
Overall, the study suggests that ocean acidification could significantly impact the growth and
development of barnacles and other fouling organisms, affecting the composition and productivity
of marine ecosystems.
The findings also highlight the importance of considering the potential
effects of ocean acidification on marine organisms when developing strategies for mitigating the
impacts of marine biofouling.
Marine Biofouling initiates with exposure of marine species to surfaces underwater and progresses
into soft or hard fouling, where soft fouling is the initial biofilm layer consisting of biomacro-
molecules, bacteria, diatoms, microalgae and other Protista. [Antunes et al. (2018)]. Diatoms
are the most common type of phytoplankton, and a major group of algae. They have a unique
characteristic which is a hard cell wall made of silica (SiO2) called frustle. Their reproduction
system is first size-diminishing, and at a certain point, it stops and follows by the formation of an
auxospore. This auxospore expands in size to give rise to a much larger cell, which then returns
to size-diminishing divisions. Diatoms express an adhesive glue that mainly consists of polysac-
charides. [Dugdale et al. (2006), Lachnit et al. (2019)]. While the surface properties do not affect
the initial settlement of diatoms, the adhesion strength of diatoms peaks on hydrophobic surfaces
according to the study done by [Thompson and Coates (2017)]. Microalgae is also a phytoplankton
found in freshwater and marine systems. In marine biofilms, alpha-proteobacteria are predominant
species, with C.crescentus serving as a representative model organism. This bacterium exhibits
a distinctive holdfast adhesion comprised of glucose, mannose, xylose, and N-acetylglucosamine.
Notably, this adhesion mechanism demonstrates significant affinity for hydrophobic surfaces akin
to diatoms.
There is a vast variety of species in marine biofilm, and it has negative and positive consequences

They are invisible to the naked eye, but the accumulation of this microfouling paves the way
to transition to macrofouling as they provide a more suitable environment for bigger species to
find the surface to attach to by providing cues for larval settlement of higher organisms in the
first phase of behavioural searching. Macrofouling organisms tend to search for surfaces that are

Biofouling Control Methods
The primary strategies for managing biofouling include the application of specialized coatings and
direct mechanical removal. Antifouling coatings release biocides to deter or kill marine organisms,
ensuring the vessel remains free from fouling. In contrast, fouling release coatings make surfaces
inhospitable for attachment, allowing organisms to be easily dislodged during vessel movement.
Mechanical cleaning offers a direct approach, physically removing organisms from surfaces. The
choice of method often depends on the vessel’s operational profile, the environmental conditions it
encounters, and economic considerations.
According to [Weber and Esmaeili (2023)] There are three categories among the coatings systems
which they discuss the first two in details.
1. Biocidal anifouling (AF) coatings
2. Fouling release (FR) coatings
3. Hard coatings

2.3.1
Biocidal Antifouling Coatings
Antifouling coatings are designed to prevent the attachment and growth of marine organisms on
submerged surfaces, primarily ship hulls. They function by releasing biocidal agents that deter or
kill marine organisms, preventing them from colonizing the surface. Over time, the biocidal agents
are depleted, which means the coatings have a limited effective lifespan.
Transcriptoms, which are the sum of all the messenger RNA molecules expressed from the genes of
an organism, show that the impact of biocides on the natural diversity of biofilms, which a change
of lowering the diversity was observed in [H.-J. Kim et al. (2021), Winfield et al. (2018)]. When the
biofilm structure alters in such a way, it directly affects the structure of subsequent macrofouling.
Papadatou et al. (2021) and Bressy et al. (2022) demonstrate that a non-biocidal coating leads to
high biodiversity and more coverage during static field immersion tests. Swain et al. (2000) shows
a similar conclusion that the geographical location alters the initial composition of the biofilm and
subsequent macrofouling composition.
2.3.2
Fouling Release coatings
Fouling release coatings (FRCs) are a type of marine coating designed to reduce the adhesion
strength of organisms to the ship’s hull. Instead of killing the organisms (as biocidal antifouling
coatings do), FRCs make it difficult for organisms to attach firmly. These coatings are typically
smooth and contain silicone or other slippery materials. When a ship moves, the water flow can
easily dislodge any loosely attached organisms.
2.3.3
Mechanical Cleaning
The mechanical cleaning process, which encompasses methods such as scraping, brushing, or utiliz-
ing water jets, is dedicated to physically eradicating fouling organisms from various surfaces. While
this approach proves effective, it necessitates precision to avoid inflicting damage to the underlying
surface. Adhering to established standards is essential to optimize the efficiency and timing of these
cleaning events. The ISO 19030 standard serves as a pivotal reference in this context, offering a
meticulously crafted procedure. It is instrumental in monitoring the speed loss and deterioration
of vessels’ overall performance due to fouling. This standard is not just a procedural guide but
a comprehensive resource that outlines fundamental principles for evaluating changes in the hull
and propeller performance. Additionally, it introduces a set of performance indicators tailored for
maintenance, repair, and retrofit activities, ensuring that the cleaning and maintenance processes
are as efficient and damage-free as possible. [ISO19030 (2016)]

2.4
Ship Performance Monitoring using Ship Operational Data
In order to increase the fuel or energy efficiency of ships, their hydrodynamic performance can be
monitored using different types of data. It depends on the method of data collection that each
vessel chooses to have. Examples of these would be In service data, noon report data, and AIS
data. Here, some literature that used different type of data to analyze the hull performance and
resistance calculations are provided.
2.4.1
In-service data
In-service data for ships refers to the data collected during the operational lifetime of a vessel,
including information on the ship’s performance, maintenance, and repair history.
This data
is typically recorded by the sensors installed onboard the vessel and can include a wide range of
metrics and measurements, such as fuel consumption, speed, cargo capacity, delivered power, draft,
and some environmental conditions. The sampling frequency can be adjusted as preferred, and a
data acquisition system (DAQ) will collect and restore data while it is connected to an onshore
central computer. The central computer or data logger collects the data from the sensors and
instruments and stores it in a database for later analysis. The data can be displayed in real-time
on a dashboard or other interface, allowing the crew to monitor the ship’s performance and make
informed decisions about optimizing their operation. Not only can DAQ be used for performance
analysis, but it can also be a valuable help for detecting sensors’ failures.
Gupta et al. (2022a) introduces two main features of in-service data as sampling frequency and
list of recorded variables. Sampling frequency can be uniform as in Gupta et al. (2022a) or non-
uniform as in Dalheim and Steen (2020), where in case of non-uniform, more pre-processing such as
removing jumps in the time series data, is required. Furthermore, a study by Virtanen et al. (2020)
shows that the collected data can be down-sampled or up-sampled to obtain a uniform sampled
dataset. Generally, in-service data outliers detection is a challenge, and methods for detecting and
eliminating them are provided by Dalheim and Steen (2020).
Mittendorf et al. (2023) uses a combination of in-service data of a fleet of 208 container vessels
with ocean reanalysis data from ERA5 to assess the added resistance estimation. This study used
Hollenbach (1998) method for calm water resistance and ISO 15016 for wind resistance. The mean
added wind resistance is calculated using an empirical formula from Nielsen et al. (2021). This
method, however, disregards added resistance due to the fouling of the hull and propeller.
2.4.2
Noon Report Data
A noon report (NR) is sent by the ship every 24 hours. It contains ship performance data, including
Shaft rotational speed rpm, main engine fuel consumption, speed over ground, and weather data.
It has been used for vessel performance analysis to optimize fuel consumption and reduce CO2
emissions. However, since this dataset has a very low sampling frequency (approximately every 24
hours), it is considered a low-resolution dataset.
A low sampling frequency can pose significant challenges in accurately assessing and correcting
ship performance parameters. Such a limitation may not capture the rapid variations in speed,
weather conditions, and steering adjustments. With insufficient data points, it becomes difficult
to generate a comprehensive and accurate analysis of the vessel’s performance.
Consequently,
potential issues and inefficiencies may remain undetected and unaddressed, leading to suboptimal
performance and increased operational costs. In essence, a higher sampling frequency is critical
for a more refined and precise evaluation, ensuring that corrective actions are timely and effective.
Since decisions for reducing fuel consumption and optimization, both for environmental purposes
and profitability, are made based on the results from noon report data analysis, the data should have
an acceptable level of accuracy. Accuracy assessment of the models made using noon report data
is essential. Smith et al. (2013) presents a method for quantifying the uncertainty in reported fuel
consumption from NR data, compares the accuracy of data from NR and a continuous monitoring
9
==PAGE:21==
(CM) system, and claims a significant reduction in uncertainty when CM data is used.
Adland et al. (2018) investigates the performance of a fleet of eight identical Aframax-size crude
oil tankers using their noon reports and predicts the effect of periodic hull cleaning events and
dry-docking on fuel consumption. It claims that dry-docking decreases fuel consumption by 17%,
while hull cleaning reduces it to 9%. Moreover, it states that the hull cleaning energy efficiency
is more significant for the laden conditions than the ballast condition. In this paper, the weather

Uyanık et al. (2020) estimates ship fuel oil

2.4.3
Automatic Identification System(AIS) Data
AIS transponders are designed to automatically provide identification and other information about
the ship to other ships and coastal authorities. IMO regulations require all vessels over 300 gross
tonnages engaged on international voyages, cargo ships over 500 gross tonnages not engaged on
international voyages, and all passenger ships to be equipped with AIS. [MEPC (2017)]According to
Harati-Mokhtari et al. (2007), the AIS data include the vessel sailing information such as position,
speed, and loading status and can be received by Vessel Traffic Service centres (VTSC). They
reported flawed data from 2% of the maritime mobile service identity information, 30% of ships’
navigation status, and about 18% ship dimensions information.
Lapinski and Isenor (2011) claims that the quality of this data depends on the AIS equipment,
the vessel navigational condition, and the AIS message receiver base station. The raw AIS data
contains noise, which leads to wrong conclusions. Zhang et al. (2018) explains the effect of noise
in an example of a vessel sailing in Singapore port, which shows that the ship’s trajectory from
AIS data is zigzag while, in reality, it is a smooth curve. It also provides a novel filtering model to
remove the noise from AIS data. Gupta et al. (2022a) suggests that the key parameters from AIS
data, including GPS position, speed, and course, should be checked, and the incorrect data should
be removed. For instance, the GPS speed gives speed over ground of the vessel and might have an
abrupt jump from the adjacent data sample.
Using AIS data for calculating ship emissions first used by Jalkanen et al. (2009) where the con-
sumption is predicted by the developed STEAM (Ship Traffic Emission Assessment Model) and
validated by comparing with the actual consumptions from the shipowners within an accuracy of
6%. The primary source for the STEAM is the internal database of more than 20000 vessels’ tech-
nical information. The developed program recodes the received AIS transmissions and is used to
analyze emissions in the North Sea and the Baltic Sea. In another study by Olesen et al. (2010), a
similar analysis is done for Danish waters to investigate the effect of ship emissions on air pollution.
Moreover, a real-time AIS-based emission monitoring system was developed by Astito, Hamdoune
et al. (2014) to determine the carbon emissions in the Strait of Gibraltar. The system was installed
and tested for 28 days and received the AIS data from the ships within the region. However, the
abovementioned studies only used the weather influence across the board, which involves high
inaccuracy. Correspondingly, they required a high level of detail from the data and very high
computing power. Thus, they are not feasible to apply for worldwide applications. Hensel et al.
(2020) presents a model capable of being used worldwide and includes weather and environmental
factors. This model used the length of overall, breadth, latitude and longitude, speed over ground,
course over ground, and true heading of the vessel from AIS data.
It uses wind speed, wind
direction, primary wave direction, and significant wave height from weather and environmental
databases. Then, it calculates speed through water from speed over ground and current speed
and direction and uses this value to obtain the additional resistance due to surface roughness
and fouling.
Finally, the added fouling resistance and extra emissions are obtained using an
engine model. However, estimating speed through water involves inaccuracy in the model since
the weather data source is not adequately accurate, as claimed by Antola et al. (2017).

Additionally, Gupta et al. (2022a) indicates that since the speed of currents changes at different sea
depths, the incident longitudinal water current speed has to be obtained as an integral of the water

ships and two bulk carriers. The VERDE has a three-layer structure, including providing data
sources, ship resistance modelling, pre-processing data, and evaluating an engine model’s main
engine fuel consumption. This model seems inaccurate since it only considers statistical analysis
and is not a physical base, and it uses the regression line without discussing the scientific feasibility.
2.5
Fouling Prediction
To understand the impact of fouling on fuel consumption, one must first calculate the added
resistance from fouling, which then allows for estimating the additional power needed. Two primary
methods, the forward and backward approaches, facilitate this. The forward method computes
resistance components to estimate the associated power, while the backward method determines
the fouling growth rate during idle periods. This rate then helps deduce the change in surface
roughness and the resultant power increase. These techniques are detailed in various studies, some
of which are discussed in the following. In the Forward method, as used by Mittendorf et al. (2023),
Adland et al. (2018), [Guo et al. (2022)], the resistance components are calculated. For finding the
fouling resistance, the difference in the reported and calculated consumption is assumed to be the
fouling resistance, as per Foteinos et al. (2017).
In the backward method, various studies evaluate ship resistance affected by fouling growths; here,
two models are reviewed; one investigates the effect of sea temperature, and another reviews the
effect of water salinity on fowling growth rate.
1. Temperature
Uzun et al. (2019) presents a method for predicting the effects of biofouling on ship resistance
and powering over time.
This model is based on the time-dependent biofouling growth
formula, which considers the influence of seawater temperature on the rate of fouling growth.
This model considers three main types of biofouling, namely, slime, non-shell organisms,
and calcareous, and analyzes the effect of their growth on hull roughness for each of them.
The paper describes extensive field tests conducted by a paint company to evaluate the
performance of a Self-Polishing Copolymer (SPC) type anti-fouling coating on ship hulls.
The tests were conducted for one to three years in the Mediterranean and Equatorial regions,
which are environmentally diverse. The method is validated using experimental data from a
full-scale ship and is shown to provide predictions of the effects of biofouling on ship resistance
and powering over time. The method can be used to optimize ship maintenance schedules
and assess the potential fuel savings achieved by cleaning or anti-fouling treatments.

Resistance components

For each component, there are various studies and methods. Some are explained in the following.
2.6.1
Calm Water Resistance
Calm water resistance refers to the resistance a ship encounters when navigating through tranquil
waters. Several methods exist to quantify calm water resistance, each with its strengths and limit-
ations. Generally, there are three primary methods to evaluate calm water resistance: Empirical,
numerical, and experimental.
Numerical methods rely on computational models to predict resistance. Common numerical tech-
niques encompass: Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), and Panel methods. Computational
methods like CFD are certainly realistic for calculating the calm water resistance of a ship. The
computational expense is not excessive today. However, detailed hull geometry is required, which
is not available in this project. Hence, according to this project’s scope, numerical methods are
not explored.
Experimental methods involve physical tests to determine resistance. One notable experimental
method is the Model Test, which is elaborated upon in the appendix. The model test is a rigorous
experimental method. The experimental method to obtain ship resistance from a model involves
conducting model tests in a towing tank. In these tests, a scaled model of the ship is towed through
calm water or waves at various speeds, and the force required to tow the model is measured using
a force balance. This force is then used to calculate the resistance of the model, which can be
scaled up to estimate the resistance of the full-scaled ship. Model test outcomes are proprietary,
and shipyards tend to withhold this data to secure their designs and maintain a competitive edge.
However, model test results were not available for the ships studied here.
Empirical methods are used in this project since they do not need vessel details and are applicable
to a wide variety of vessel types. Empirical methods harness established formulas derived from
previous experimental and observational data.
Two of the most renowned empirical methods
include:
• Hollenbach’s Method: Detailed information regarding Hollenbach’s approach can be found
in Hollenbach (1998).
• Holtrop’s Method: This method is extensively discussed in a later section as it forms the
foundation for this project’s approach to gauging calm water resistance.
Holtop’s method is chosen for this project due to its applicability to a broad spectrum of vessel
types without requiring intricate vessel details. This method and its specifics are elaborated upon
in the Theory section.

2.6.2
Added Wind Resistance
The J. ITTC (2017) recommends three methods for estimating the added wave resistance: wind
tunnel model tests, STA-JIP, and Fujiwara’s method, as discussed by [Gupta et al. (2022b)].
Wind tunnel model tests, if available for the vessel, are considered the most accurate means for
estimating added wind resistance. This method employs scaled models of ships in a controlled
environment to directly measure the impact of wind loads, offering precise and reliable data.
The STA-JIP database, established by Boom et al. (2013), provides experimental values of wind
resistance coefficients according to specific ship types. It is a comprehensive resource for practition-
ers lacking the means to conduct wind tunnel tests, offering empirical data grounded in extensive
experimentation.
Fujiwara’s method, proposed by Fujiwara et al. (2005), is based on the regression analysis of data
obtained from several wind tunnel model tests for different ship types. It offers a mathematical
approach to estimating wind resistance, balancing accuracy and convenience.
Regardless of the chosen method, two main sets of parameters are essential: incident wind para-
meters and the information regarding the area exposed to the wind. Incident wind parameters can
be derived from onboard measurements or weather hindcast data, while the exposed area inform-
ation can be estimated using the general arrangement drawing of the ship or a regression formula
presented by Kitamura et al. (2017).
In practice, Fujiwara’s method is often favored for its balance between accuracy and accessibility.
It utilizes regression analysis, making it a versatile tool that does not require specific model tests
for each vessel, thereby offering a practical approach for a wide range of ship types and operational
scenarios. Thus, it is used for this project. The details of calculations are discussed in the theory
section.
2.6.3
Added Wave Resistance
The added wave resistance is one of the most important components affecting both ship design and
operation regarding safety and energy efficiency. In moderate sea states, the decrease in service
speed of merchant ships (slow steaming) leads to relatively larger added wave resistance. In severe
sea states, the concern is the risk of losing manoeuvrability, especially for blunt-type ships such
as tankers and bulk carriers.
This safety concern is the main motivation of the legislation of
Minimum propulsion power (MPP) regulations by IMO, in which various semi-empirical formulas
for the practical determination of the added wave resistance and the associated speed loss in a
seaway are recommended.[IMO (2013), MEPC (2017)]
Various empirical methods have been developed and refined over the years to assess this added
resistance, aiming to enhance the accuracy and reliability of predictions, each offering unique
advantages and challenges.
ISO 15016 recommends STA-1 and STA-2 for all types of ships ISO (2015). The STA-1 is restricted
to moderate sea conditions. As a result, STA-2 has been developed to enhance its applicability
in higher sea states. Both STA-1 and STA-2 are only valid for head sea (+45) degrees off bow,
implicitly assuming zero added resistance for other headings.
The CTH method, proposed by Lang and Mao in 2021 Lang and Mao 2021, offers a more versatile
approach in estimating added wave resistance. It is capable of providing accurate estimations for
various wave headings, overcoming the limitations of STA-1 and STA-2.
Liu and Papanikolaou introduced the SNNM method in 2020 Liu and Papanikolaou 2020. This
approach also supports arbitrary wave headings and has shown significant improvements in accur-
acy and reliability compared to traditional methods, especially when detailed hull forms are not
available.
The Combined method, developed by Kim et al. in 2022 Y.-R. Kim et al. 2022, integrates various
13
==PAGE:25==
techniques to offer enhanced performance across different sea states and wave headings. It is known
for its comprehensive and robust estimations.
A comparative analysis of CTH, SNNM, and Combined methods by Esmailian et al. (2022) reveals
distinct advantages each methodology brings to wave resistance estimation. All three methods have
shown to outperform STA-2 and are not constrained by wave headings, offering more comprehensive
assessments.
Among these advanced methodologies, SNNM stands out due to its applicability even when the
precise geometry of the vessel is unavailable. This characteristic makes SNNM a highly valuable
tool for assessments where detailed hull geometries are not available, facilitating more informed
and flexible decision-making in ship design and operation. Thus, this methodology is used for this
project mainly because it is more applicable and considers both head and following waves.

3.2.4
Fouling resistance
Fouling resistance refers to the resistance added to a ship’s hull due to marine fouling. Fouling
is the accumulation of microorganisms, plants, and animals on a ship’s hull in the water,
which can increase the roughness of the hull surface and create a layer of biofilm, reducing
the ship’s speed and increasing fuel consumption. Fouling resistance is an essential factor to
consider when designing and operating ships, as it can significantly impact their performance
and efficiency. To mitigate the effects of fouling, various antifouling coatings and treatments
can be applied to the hull to prevent or reduce the buildup of fouling organisms. These
coatings can also reduce the need for cleaning and maintenance, which can lower operating
costs and reduce the environmental impact of shipping.
Generally, there are two approaches of the forward and the backward model to finding the
added resistance due to fouling. The first method (forward model) is finding the fouling res-
istance by subtracting resistance components from the total observed resistance(estimated
from fuel consumption). The second method (backward model) uses fouling models that
predict the fouling resistance based on variables other than resistance components, i.e. en-
vironmental conditions like sea water temperature, salinity, etc. The theory of the backward
model is provided in the appendix.
3.2.4.1

In the forward model, the Fouling resistance can be obtained using equation 65 presented
by Fonteinos et al. (2017). This method calculates the total resistance by having the speed
through water and effective horsepower. Effective horsepower includes total propulsive effi-

Methods
In this section, first, the data pre-processing steps are explained. Second, The main calcula-
tions are explained in detail.
4.1
Data Description
n the present study, a fleet of seven vessels is analysed. Three main datasets are utilized
for each vessel in this project. The Automatic Identification System (AIS) data, weather
data provided by Copernicus Marine Environment Monitoring Service (CMEMS) [Monitoring
and Center (2018)], and the Noon Report (NR) dataset provided by a Norwegian shipping
company.
The AIS dataset, characterized by its multiple daily entries denoting the vessel’s status at
varying intervals, was coalesced with the NR dataset. The data is merged since having more
sampling frequency gives more accuracy in the analysis, and adding supplementary informa-
tion from NR into AIS data makes it usable for ship performance analysis, as demonstrated
here.
Noon reports are typically recorded once a day, ideally at noon. However, in real-life sailing
scenarios, a vessel’s speed and fuel consumption can vary due to fluctuating weather condi-
tions. Factors such as wind and waves can change rapidly, even over short periods like an
hour, potentially causing the Noon Report (NR) values to lack precision. As vessels experi-
ence varying forces throughout the day, the recorded NR values might not consistently reflect
the actual conditions and performance of the vessel for the whole 24 hours of a day.
The AIS data includes the location, heading and speed of GPS of the ships multiple times
per 24 hours. This indicates that there is more information about what the vessel has been
experiencing during 24 hours and can give a more accurate result when it comes to fuel
consumption prediction. The location, GPS speed(speed over ground), and heading of the
vessel are used from AIS data. Displacement and drafts, and main engine consumption are
taken from NR data.
4.2
Data Pre-processing
The pre-processing steps on the datasets are explained in this section, including data merging,
cleaning, and filtering. Finally, the columns that are used in the calculations are reviewed.
4.2.1
Data Merging
The weather data is combined and interpolated for each data point of the AIS data. Then, the
AIS data with interpolated weather data is merged with the NR data to form an integrated
dataset for each vessel.
Displacement and drafts that are used from NR data are the same for all the data in one
day. Generally, when the vessel is sailing loaded or ballast, the displacement does not change
significantly when the cargo is not changing. Hence, using the same displacement and draft
values for all the AIS data in one day in reasonable.
Prior to the integration of the AIS and NR datasets, a crucial preprocessing step was necessit-
ated due to the disparate datetime formats inherent to the two datasets. The AIS timestamps
adhered to the format DD/MM/Y Y Y Y HH : MM, whilst the NR dataset employed the
ISO 8601 standard, represented as Y Y Y Y −MM −DDTHH : MM : SS. To reconcile these
discrepancies, a uniform datetime format was instituted using Python’s pandas library. For
the AIS dataset, datetime strings were meticulously parsed in accordance with their intrinsic
format. Conversely, for the NR dataset, the pd.to datetime function intrinsically managed
the ISO 8601 format.
For each day, one reported NR data is considered. Mostly the vessel reports one data at
Noon. However, in the dataset, it was observed that they reported more than one NR data
26
==PAGE:38==
for some days, which is discussed further. Having one NR data per day, the reported values
of this data in the NR data are used for all the AIS data of that day. This is done for
displacement and drafts, that are used from NR data. For the consumption, it is explained
in the next chapter.
4.2.2
Data cleaning and filtering
The chance of having duplicates in the reported values is checked. Figure 5 shows the number
of noon report data before and after removing some of the duplicates. A sample is considered
a duplicate if the values of location, time, and other relevant variables are exactly the same.
Figure 5 shows that before cleaning the duplicates, most of the vessels have 2 and 3 reported
data per day. For example, vessel 5 has more than 700 days during which they sent three
noon reports. After cleaning and removing the duplicates, for the same vessel the number of
days that the vessel sent 3 noon reports is reduced to less than 100.
Figure 5: Histogram of noon report data for all the vessels before and after removing the duplicates
It can be seen that even after cleaning, some of the days have more than one reported
observation, and it is up to 12 observations per day.
While it happens rarely, it can be
beneficial to use all the data reported in one day on the noon report.
The higher the
frequency of the reported data, the better the accuracy of the calculations. However, in
this report, it is decided to use only one of the values per day to keep the consistency of
timeshare calculations, which is explained further. The calculation based on more than one
Noon report data per day is out of the scope of this project. The data selection criteria are
explained in the following.
For the noon reports data, when multiple entries exist for the same date, a selection criterion
is employed to determine which entry to retain for further analyses. The rationale behind
each step and the corresponding methodology adopted are elaborated further.
(a) Data Completeness: If only one of the entries on a particular day contains non-
null values in the ‘displacement‘, ‘draft‘, and ‘main engine power‘ columns, that entry
is prioritized. This is premised on the idea that although interpolation or regression
methods could be employed to fill missing data, utilizing reported values invariably
offers more reliability. Data imputation introduces an inherent level of uncertainty that
is best avoided when direct measurements are available.
(b) Weather Conditions: The ‘good weather‘ column, annotated by the ship’s crew,
indicates the data’s reliability. A ”Y” implies favourable weather conditions, making
the data more credible. Given the many external forces acting on a vessel in adverse
weather conditions, especially due to high winds and waves, measurements taken during
such times may not account for all variables, thereby reducing the reliability of the
calculated results.
(c) Proximity to Noon: In scenarios where the above two criteria do not clearly distin-
guish, the report closest to noon is retained. This is to ensure consistency in the data
selection process. If two times equidistant from noon are present, the earlier time is
chosen, ensuring data continuity.
27
==PAGE:39==
While the aforementioned methodology has been chosen for this study, it is worth noting that
it might not be universally optimal. A more comprehensive approach would involve using all
available data entries to ascertain which one yields the most accurate resistance calculations.
Variations in resistance values could hint at outlier data, which can then be attributed to
suboptimal weather conditions during data recording. However, this would require a more
detailed exploration, which lies outside the purview of this thesis.
A cursory analysis of
resistance graphs, derived using all data points, did not reveal significant variations, which
could suggest that the discrepancies between different data entries for the same day might
be minimal. However, the underlying reason for multiple reports on the same day remains
an avenue for future investigation, potentially involving consultations with bulk carrier crew
members or experts in the domain.
4.2.3
Review the used columns
This section reviews the columns used for resistance calculations for outliers and NaN values.
(a) Displacement and draft
In the analysis of the Noon report data, it was observed that the displacement column
contains missing entries. These omissions can be attributed to various scenarios: when
the vessel is in port for loading or unloading and during its voyages but missed from the
crew to report. When the draft during a voyage is reported, the displacement can be
interpolated from other available data points. This interpolation introduces potential
uncertainties, therefore it is decided to exclude data with missing displacement values
from the analysis.
Upon further investigation, it became evident that some displacement values were erro-
neously reported with an additional zero, artificially inflating their magnitude. These
anomalies are corrected here such that the revised values align with the acceptable
range.
(b) Weather Data
Weather data corresponding to each resistance component is utilized in the analysis. For
calm water resistance, the longitudinal current speed is used; for added wind resistance,
parameters including wind speed, wind direction, and heading are utilized; for added
wave resistance, the relative mean wave direction, significant wave height, and mean
wave period are employed.
The integrity and accuracy of the weather data are pivotal in ensuring the reliabil-

(c) Temperature
In order to ensure the accuracy and reliability of the data, a filtering step was performed

are typically measured in Kelvin, any temperature below 275 Kelvin was considered
erroneous and deemed inappropriate for further analysis.
A Python code snippet was written to filter the dataset. By applying this condition,
only the rows with sea surface temperatures equal to or above 275 Kelvin were retained
in the dataset. This process effectively eliminated any potentially incorrect or unrealistic
temperature values.

4.3.1
Ship dimensions and areas
The fleet of vessels investigated in this thesis are in three groups of sister ships, each char-

used to calculate dimensions and areas. The waterline length for various drafts is calculated
and then interpolated for each data point to get the waterline length as per the vessel draft.
In this analysis, the waterplane area for each vessel is retained as a constant equal to the
design value, as detailed draft-dependent drawings are unavailable. Contrarily, the trans-
verse and lateral areas exposed to the wind are calculated as draft-dependent, owing to the
accessibility of relevant drawings that facilitate precise computations at varying drafts. The
superstructure area exposed to the wind, a constant metric, is derived from the general
arrangement drawings.
4.3.2
Ship body coefficients
The required coefficients for resistance calculations are explained in the theory section. In
all of them, the values of length, breadth, draft, and volume displacement are the same.
The midwater area is the area of the midsection, which can be approximated as a rectangular
with the length of breadth and width of the draft minus the area outside the bilge radius
from both bottom corners. The area of the bottom corners is estimated as triangles, and the
dimensions are used from the general plan of each vessel. The draft is used from the reported
value from the noon reports, and thus, the draft dependency is considered.
The waterline area is calculated from the drawings of the general plan for each vessel. The
draft dependency is not considered since the drawings for each draft are not provided.
The water line length is changing with the change of draft. So, for each vessel, the length of
the ship for different drafts is calculated, and then an interpolation is assumed to find the
length for all the drafts. For the drafts lower than the transom stern, since the length of the
vessel changes with a curve based on the vessel’s stern type, more data points are considered
and the interpolation is not linear.
The length between perpendicular is constant and based on the designed LP P reported in
the dataset. For all practical values of draft, the breadth would be the same. T is moulded
draft, and the reported value from the noon reports is used.

Reynolds number and Froude number
The Reynolds and Froude numbers are essential non-dimensional parameters in fluid dynam-
ics, often used to predict the flow regimes and the resulting forces on bodies submerged in
fluids. In this study, both these numbers are computed for various vessels using their re-
spective speeds through water, which has been converted to meters per second to maintain
consistency with the metric system.
The resulting Reynolds numbers for the vessels under investigation fall within a specific
range, indicative of the flow characteristics around the vessels. It provides insights into the
transition between laminar and turbulent flow regimes, influencing the drag coefficients and
the overall hydrodynamic performance of the vessels.
On the other hand, the Froude number is a significant parameter in the study of wave
resistance experienced by a ship or a similar structure. The calculated Froude numbers align
well with typical values for vessels of similar sizes and speeds, offering insights into the wave-
making characteristics and the associated resistance. A lower Froude number indicates that
the wave-making resistance is minimal, while a higher value signifies notable wave resistance,
impacting the vessel’s speed and fuel efficiency.

Vessels 4 and 5, being sister ships with the same coating, showed similar performance trends
pre-cleaning. A more pronounced initial increase in the fouling coefficient in Vessel 4 could
potentially be attributed to longer port stays or operations in regions conducive to rapid
fouling.
Post-cleaning, both vessels exhibited an increase in the fouling coefficient slope,
indicating accelerated coating deterioration.
Vessel 6, despite being the oldest, maintained a consistent fouling coefficient rate post-
cleaning, even showcasing a decline, evident from a negative slope. Vessel 7 also depicted a
negative slope, though the data for these two vessels is not robust enough to draw concrete
conclusions. There’s a hypothesis that the type of coating on these vessels may include pro-
tective layers that counteract fouling over time, but this requires further investigation with
more precise data.
In summary, Figure 45 serves as a valuable resource for comparing coating performances
across different vessels, although caution must be exercised due to the relatively new state
of these vessels and the potential impact of operational regions on fouling rates.

5.4
Uncertainties
In every project that involves the process of data collection and analysis, a level of uncertainty
is always present. This project is not exempt from such a challenge. These uncertainties can
arise from a variety of sources, including the inherent imperfections of measuring tools and
human errors. For instance, sensors employed for gauging temperature and speed might not
always provide accurate readings due to calibration errors, environmental fluctuations, or
other unpredictable factors. Human errors, such as mistakes made during data recording
or system calibration, can further amplify these uncertainties, leading to data that is not
entirely reliable.
In the context of this project, even the physical environment, like temperature and draft,
can notably influence the data and its subsequent analysis. For example, a slight variation in
temperature can affect the sensors’ readings, leading to data that may not precisely reflect
the actual conditions. Similarly, the draft can vary due to several factors like load, water
density, and others. These variations can lead to significant changes in the block coefficient
and area interpolation, directly impacting the model’s accuracy and the overall uncertainty
of the results. While the draft dependency has interpolated for lateral and transverse areas,
it is not considered for the waterplane area since the relevant drawings were not available.
61
==PAGE:73==
The waterline length is also interpolated for different drafts, and the interpolation reduces
the accuracy and causes uncertainty.
Furthermore, assumptions made during the modelling process contribute additional layers of
uncertainty. The physics-based model in this project, for example, averaging the perform-
ance and weather data from AIS data over an an hour and downsampling the data adds
uncertainty, though it is insignificant. Also, in every averaging during the modeling pro-
cess, the uncertainty level increases. Additionally, the empirical formulas for determining
the longitudinal centre of buoyancy and appendages area are based on estimations, which
might not capture the intricate real-world variations and interactions. Empirical methods
for calculating resistance components also bring uncertainty to some levels.
In conclusion, these inherent uncertainties are critical to consider in interpreting and applying
the project’s results. A nuanced approach that considers these uncertainties can lead to more
informed decisions, enhancing the project’s reliability and applicability. While the model and
data provide valuable insights, acknowledging and understanding their limitations ensures
that conclusions drawn are both robust and adaptable to complex and dynamic real-world
scenarios.

Conclusion and Future work
This project used a combination of AIS data, noon report data and weather data for a fleet
of seven vessels.
The resistance components for calm water, wind and added wave were
calculated using the chosen empirical formulas. The corresponding power for the sum of
these three terms is found to represent the total power of a vessel without fouling. The fuel
consumption for this power is found using the timeshare of each data and SFOC values of
the engine from the engine manufacturer documents. The difference between the reported
main engine consumption from noon reports and the calculated consumption values is then
defined as the fouling consumption, representing the part of the consumption that is due to
the fouling of the vessel. The fouling resistance and the coefficient are found and represented
in an innovative way: the fouling coefficient versus the ship static time. Ship static time is
defined as the number of days that the vessel has been idling or had a speed of lower than 3
knots.
The discussion of the assessment of the final plots for the fouling coefficient concludes that
while the literature suggests the fouling increases with a logarithmic trend over time and
then drops after cleaning, this is not shown in all of the cases. In the case of unexpected
results, the reason can be due to the uncertainties and gaps in data. It is also important that
the only cleaning events that are known are the latest cleaning and the build time. Hence,
many cleaning events are not available, and the trend lines can change. To be more specific,
in the case of an unreported cleaning, the fouling decreases, and the reported consumption
is lower than a fouled vessel, so the fouling coefficient decreases. However, since no cleaning
line is assumed, the trend will consider the lower point in the average calculation, and the
whole trend comes lower than what it should be.
Further, the prediction of the trend line for the fouling coefficient is made, and the cleaning
time is suggested. This can provide insightful results for the shipping companies to be able
to plan the cleaning event in advance. Finally, the performance of different coatings and the
effect of the age of the vessel are discussed. Since the vessels are fairly new, the ageing of the
vessel and the coating performance can not be reviewed fairly as more data and more years
are required. This methodology, however, remains valid and can be used for older vessels
with more data, for example, more than five years, to see the effects of age. Also, the coating
performance for different coating can be done with more certainty if there is more than one
vessel with the same coating.
In this study, a detailed analysis of fouling trends for seven vessels, evaluated against ship
static time, unveiled distinct fouling patterns, emphasizing the need for individualized main-
tenance strategies. Machine learning, utilizing sklearn, has proven vital in forecasting fouling
trends, enhancing preventive maintenance, and optimizing cleaning schedules to counteract
increased fuel consumption. A comparative study of different coatings revealed variations in
performance, influenced by external factors such as the vessels’ operational regions. Cleaning
events have a notable impact on fouling trends, with some vessels experiencing an increased
fouling coefficient post-cleaning. Given the relatively new age of the vessels in the study, a
comprehensive evaluation of the impact of ageing on fouling and coating performance neces-
sitates a more extended study, incorporating older vessels for a well-rounded analysis.
Future work can use the backward method and compare the fouling growth rate and fouling
consumption with the forward method. Moreover, using all the reported noon reports per
day instead of choosing one is going to give better accuracy.
Another future work can
be to calculate resistance components based on different empirical methods and compare
them. One important future work will be the uncertainty analysis of the calculations. The
optimization of cleaning and overconsumption can be done by having prices that change over
time (As the fuel price and cleaning prices are not fixed and are changing rapidly. They can
change dramatically in case of big events such as sudden war or pandemics.)
In the retrospective analysis of this study, it’s observed that the synergy between AIS, noon
report, and weather data profoundly enhances the precision of assessing fouling impact on
vessels.
The incorporation of ship static time as a metric offers a nuanced perspective,
embedding operational dynamics into the evaluation of fouling coefficients. However, the
63
==PAGE:75==
complexity of variables and the evolving nature of operational conditions necessitate a more
granular approach to data integration and analysis.
Looking ahead, the extension of this methodology to a broader fleet, including older vessels
and diverse operational contexts, is a prospective avenue to refine the accuracy and applicab-
ility of the findings. Enhancement in data granularity, particularly concerning cleaning events
and operational variables, can further nuance the insights, offering a more tailored strategy
for maintenance and operational efficiency. Additionally, the exploration of machine learning
and artificial intelligence tools can augment predictive accuracy, embedding adaptability to
the dynamic maritime environment. The objective remains to converge towards a model
that is both predictive and adaptive, offering real-time insights and prescriptive strategies
for vessel maintenance, fuel efficiency, and operational optimization amidst the oscillating
operational and environmental conditions.

Appendix B: Additional Explanations
Here, the extra explanation required for more understanding of the topic is provided.
Sea trial data
Sea trials are designed to test the performance and functionality of a vessel under various
operating conditions. The specific tests performed during sea trials can vary depending on
the type of vessel but generally include the following:
(a) Speed Trials: This test measures the vessel’s maximum speed and speed at different
power settings.
(b) Maneuverability Tests: This includes tests to assess the vessel’s ability to turn, stop,
and navigate in tight spaces.
(c) Seakeeping Trials: These tests evaluate the vessel’s ability to handle different sea con-
ditions, including waves, currents, and wind.
(d) Stability Tests: These tests evaluate the vessel’s stability in various conditions and
under different loading configurations.
(e) Equipment and Systems Tests: This includes tests to verify that all systems and equip-
ment on board are working correctly, including navigation equipment, communication
systems, and safety equipment.
Sea trial data is typically collected using a variety of sensors and instruments on board the
vessel and is analyzed to ensure that the vessel meets the performance requirements specified
by the customer and regulatory authorities. Any issues identified during the sea trials can
be addressed before the vessel is delivered to the customer.
Transom Stern Resistance
Transom stern resistance occurs due to the flow separation at the stern of the ship. In a
transom stern design, the flat surface of the stern causes the flow to separate from the hull,
resulting in a large re-circulation zone or eddy behind the ship. This eddy creates additional
drag or resistance that opposes the forward motion of the ship. The resistance caused by the
transom stern can be significant, especially at higher speeds, and can lead to increased fuel
consumption and reduced speed. To minimize the transom stern resistance, designers often
use various methods such as optimizing the shape of the stern or adding appendages like a
stern bulb or spoiler to improve the flow pattern around the hull.
The separated flow creates a drag force, called base drag, and is calculated based on an
empirical formula shown in equation 92 based on experiments with projectiles in air.

Power Prediction
The power exerted by the propeller into the water, known as the thrust power PT , is integral
in understanding a ship’s propulsion dynamics. It is calculated using a given speed of advance
VA and the thrust T and is influenced by the hull efficiency ηH, as shown in equation 141.
PT = PE/ηH = VA × T
(141)
Open water tests, which measure thrust, torque, and the speed of advance at a specified
