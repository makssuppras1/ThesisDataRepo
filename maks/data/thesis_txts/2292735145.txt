Abstract
Large nightclub ﬁres involving people with alcohol related impairments and causing many fatalities, have
occurred in the past decades. Information concerning the evacuation of alcohol-aﬀected people is rele-
vant for ﬁre safety design and for assuring the safety level in the nightclubs. People aﬀected by alcohol
or drugs can be considered to be temporarily impaired, with respect to mobility, reaction time, sight,
awareness and cognition. Literature gives only limited information on the evacuation behaviour of people
with temporary disabilities and even less information is found on alcohol related impairments.
The aim of the current study is to determine the eﬀect of the impairment of alcohol on the evacuation of
people. The study is divided into two large-scale evacuation experiments. The ﬁrst experiment is carried
out using equipment inducing solely the visual impairment of alcohol. In order to simulate that the
participants are aﬀected by alcohol, the participants are equipped with special Fatal Vision® Impairment
Goggles provided by Fatal Vision®. In the second experiment the participants consume alcohol, which
is Carlsberg Pilsner sponsored by Carlsberg. During the two experiments, several evacuation tests are
carried out and the data is recorded using video footage. The recorded data includes: free walking speed,
relation between walking speed and person density, reaction time and balance, observation of the human
behaviour and the social interaction between the participants.
Based on the results in this study it can be concluded that the consumption of alcohol aﬀects evacuation
characteristics. Therefore it is necessary to take this fact into account when designing buildings such as
nightclubs where people are often strongly aﬀected by alcohol.
The results regarding the free walking speed (meaning being able to walk without inﬂuence from other
people) in both of the experiments are that the walking speed is reduced. When wearing the goggles the
free walking speed is reduced, the speed reduction is the same no matter the BAC level of the goggles.
But in the experiments where people have consumed alcohol the free walking speed decreases when in-
creasing the BAC level.
The study shows that the relation between walking speed and person density changes due to the inﬂuence
of both the goggles and of alcohol. But the conclusions from the two experiments are very diﬀerent: In
the experiment with goggles the walking speed is reduced compared with the reference sample. It is
found that alcohol has the exact opposite eﬀect on the pace of the participants. Their walking speed is
increased compared to the reference and the participants tend to be more daring.
From the results in both experiments it is seen that the walking speed is inversely proportional to person
density. In the results there is a tendency, which indicates that the more inﬂuenced people are the less
eﬀect does person density have on the walking speed. Furthermore, in the goggle experiment a walking
speed higher than 0 m/s was seen for person density higher than 3.75 pers/m2.
The diﬀerences in how the goggles and alcohol aﬀect the human body’s reaction patterns are obvious.
The goggles induce a vision impairment while alcohol induces a cognitive impairment. However, this
does not mean that the goggles are useless in experiments where a simulated alcohol inﬂuence on people’s
movement pattern is requested. It is nevertheless necessary to take these diﬀerences into account when
applying the goggles in order to achieve the most valid results.

Resumé
Gennem de seneste årtier er der sket ﬂere store brande i natklubber og barer, som har involveret
alkoholpåvirkede mennesker og kostet mange menneskeliv. Information vedrørende evakuering af alko-
holpåvirkede mennesker er relevant i forhold til, at skabe et brandsikkert bygningsdesign og for, at opnå
et tilstrækkeligt sikkerhedsniveau på natklubber og barer.
Hvad angår mobilitet, reaktions tid, syn,
bevidsthed og kognition kan mennesker som er påvirket af alkohol eller stoﬀer, anses for at være midler-
tidig handicappet. Litteraturen giver kun begrænset information om evakuerings adfærd, for midlertidig
handicappede mennesker. Endnu mindre information er fundet, omkring evakuerings adfærd for alko-
holpåvirkede mennesker.
Formålet med dette studie er, at bestemme hvilken eﬀekt alkohol har, på menneskers evakuerings adfærd.
Studiet er opdelt i to omfattende evakuerings forsøg. Det første forsøg er udført med brug af udstyr,
som kun påvirker synet. For at simulere en alkoholpåvirkning, er forsøgsdeltagerne udstyret med specielle
Fatal Vision® Impairment Goggles, produceret af Fatal Vision®. I det andet forsøg er deltagerne påvirket
af alkohol, hvilket er i form af Carlsberg Pilsner, som er sponsoreret af Carlsberg. Gennem de to forsøg
er der udført ﬂere evakuerings tests, som alle er optaget på video. De indsamlede data indeholder: fri
ganghastighed, reaktions tid, balanceevne, observation af menneskelig adfærd, social interaktion mellem
forsøgsdeltagerne, sammenhængen mellem ganghastighed og person densitet.
På baggrund af resultaterne i dette studie, kan det konkluderes, at indtagelsen af alkohol har en eﬀekt
på evakuerings karakteristika. Det er derfor vigtig, at tage højde for netop dette når bygninger såsom
natklubber og barer skal konstrueres og udformes.
Resultaterne vedrørende den frie ganghastighed (med dette menes at man er i stand til, at gå uden
indﬂydelse fra andre mennesker) viser i begge forsøg, at ganghastigheden er reduceret. Når deltagerne
er udstyret med briller bliver den frie ganghastighed reduceret, reduktionen er den samme uafhængig
af brillernes angivne promille. Men når deltagerne er påvirket af alkohol ses det, at jo højere promille
deltagerne har, jo langsommere bliver deres ganghastighed. Studiet viser, at sammenhængen mellem
ganghastighed og person densitet ændre sig, både når deltagerne er påvirket af briller og alkohol. Men
konklusionen fra de to forsøg er meget forskellige. I forsøget med brillerne ses det, at ganghastigheden
bliver reduceret i forhold til referencen, når deltagerne er påvirket af brillerne. Det ses at alkoholen har
den præcis modsatte eﬀekt på deltagernes hastighed. Deres ganghastighed stiger i forhold til referencen
og deltagerne bliver mere overmodige efter at have indtaget alkohol. Fra resultaterne i begge forsøg kan
det ses, at ganghastigheden er omvendt proportional med person densiteten. I resultaterne ses en tendens
som tyder på, at jo mere påvirket deltagerne er, jo mindre indﬂydelse har person densiteten på deres
ganghastighed. Derudover er der i brille forsøget, fundet ganghastigheder på over 0 m/s, for en person
densitet på mere end 3.75 pers/m2.
Det er tydeligt, at brillerne og alkoholen har forskellig eﬀekt, på menneskets reaktions mønster. Brillerne
forårsager et synshandicap, mens alkoholen forårsager et kognitivt handicap. Dette betyder imidlertid
ikke, at brillerne er ubrugelige i forsøg, hvor det ønskes at simulere en alkohol eﬀekt, men for at opnå de
mest valide resultater er det derfor nødvendigt, at tage højde for disse forskelle, når brillerne anvendes.

Chapter 1
Introduction
There have been nightclub ﬁres involving people with alcohol related impairments and causing many
fatalities in the past decades. Only a relatively small amount of scientiﬁc studies have been made on
evacuation and evacuation characteristics for vulnerable populations [Shields (1999)]. These populations
cover people with disabilities, children, elderly people and people impaired by alcohol or drugs.
To avoid and control ﬁres in nightclubs it is important to consider ﬁre safety in new and already existing
buildings. When planning or redesigning the safety measures of a building, the purpose of the building
and the characteristics of the occupants and users must be taken into consideration.
Traditional ﬁre safety codes, the so-called prescriptive codes are used to design the ﬁre safety for tra-
ditional buildings. The application of these codes often implies categorization of buildings according
to types of occupancy [BR10 (2010)]. There is a number of user-categories for able-bodied people and
only one category for people with impairments. The aim of this one category is to ensure the safety
of vulnerable people for a certain type of building. However, able-bodied occupants and visitors being
temporarily impaired due to the consumption of alcohol or drugs seem to fall outside these categories.
Performance based ﬁre codes allow ﬁre engineers to apply numerical tools when designing buildings with
suﬃcient safety standards. In this process, the available safe egress time is determined as well as the
time required to evacuate people to a safe place. The buildings should be designed and constructed so no
occupant may be exposed to critical conditions during the evacuation. The applied software is based on
studies on movement of people. When calculating the evacuation times the applied theory is often based
on homogeneous groups involving people with normal mobility, who are able to evacuate by themselves
[Over (2006)]. These groups are rarely representative for the population in a building, especially not in
nightclubs.
In connection with the ﬁre safety design of nightclubs it must be taken into consideration that a majority
of the population present in the nightclub is impaired by alcohol. This means that reaction time, walking
speed and social behaviour of the occupants most likely are aﬀected, creating new demands for ﬁre safety
and evacuation [Christoforou (2013)], [Prouls (2008)], [Wood (1972)].
There are few studies on the evacuation of people who are inﬂuenced by alcohol. Limited investigations
exist on the evacuation ability of people with visual impairments [Sorensen (2013a)]. The study Opti-
mizing Fire Alarms Notiﬁcation for High Risk Groups shows that alcohol intoxication reduces the ability
to respond to an auditory alarm signiﬁcantly [Gwynne (2007)]. Most of the literature regarding alcohol-
related impaired people is limited to guidelines describing how to manage drunken persons [FIFA (2014)].
The aim of the current study is to increase knowledge on the evacuation behaviour and reaction modes
of people with alcohol-related impairments. There are many ways to study evacuation behaviour and
reaction modes of intoxicated people, but in order to obtain veriﬁable results the focus of this study has
1
==PAGE:9==
been narrowed down to an investigation of walking speed, person density, reaction rate, human behaviour
and social interaction between the participants.
It is extremely diﬃcult to establish experimental models of ﬁres in crowded nightclubs and to simulate
the behaviour of panicking crowds in smoke ﬁlled, darkened rooms, but by establishing simple and frac-
tioned situations the ambition of this study is also to discuss whether vision impairment equipment can
be used to simulate how alcohol inﬂuences the above mentioned walking speed, person density, reaction
rate, human behaviour and social interaction.
The focus of this study is on evacuation of a ﬁre before possible panic arises because panic changes peo-
ples’ patterns of movement and behaviour in ways that are diﬃcult to study in this model.
The current project consist of two large-scale evacuation experiments involving participants aﬀected by
impairment-induced equipment or alcohol.
The ﬁrst experiment is carried out using equipment inducing solely the visual impairment of alcohol.
The second experiment is conducted were the participants is consuming alcohol. To validate the eﬀect
of the visual impairment equipment on the human body a comparison of the two experiments is conducted.
1.1
Background
In the past decades there have been ﬁres with drunken and otherwise aﬀected casualties. Many of the
larger ﬁres which involve people with alcohol related impairments occur in nightclubs, the so called night-
club ﬁres. Two examples of nightclub ﬁres are the Cocoanut Grove nightclub in Boston US, where 492
people died in a ﬁre in 1942 [www.edition.cnn.com] and the Gothenburg dance hall in Sweden, where a
ﬁre occurred in a second stairway leading to 63 fatalities in 1998 [Comeau (2000)].
In this section a list of the 15 deadliest nightclub ﬁres in the world is presented, table 1.1. In the presen-
tation of these ﬁres, information regarding the cause and origin of the ﬁres is given as well as the number
of casualties and the year of occurrence.
The list is created to give an overview of how deadly these kind of ﬁres are and to justify why it is
necessary to investigate the conditions around these ﬁres. For each of the 15 nightclub ﬁres information
about the ﬁre safety installations or the lack of it, can be seen in appendix E.1.
Table 1.1 provides a historical list showing deadly nightclub ﬁres. The list covers the years from 1940
until today. The average number of casualties in the ﬁres is 210 people. The ﬁres were mainly caused
by events belonging to operations and maintenance of the building. 4 of the ﬁres were induced due to
indoor ﬁreworks, 11 ﬁres were located in a part of the construction, especially the ceiling.

Two consistent problems seen in many of the 15 ﬁres is overcrowding and blocked/hidden exits.
The number of people that have to leave the building is an important factor for the evacuation speed.
In 7 out of 15 ﬁres the premises were overcrowded. Two examples are the Ozone Disco Club in the
Philippines and the Gothenburg dance hall in Sweden. The Ozone nightclub had a license to room 35
people but during the ﬁre there was approximately 350 people present. The nightclub in Gothenburg
had at least 243 people present during the ﬁre but the club was only permitted to room 150 people.
It can be observed how all of the nightclubs had exits, but it can unfortunately also be observed how
almost all of the nightclubs had problems with blocked or hidden emergency exits. The blocked exits are
in some cases blocked by the ﬁre, but often they are locked or blocked by things or furniture before the
ﬁre starts. The hidden doors are often unlit because of missing exit signs or they are hidden behind a
bar or similar.
It is diﬃcult to categorize the users of a nightclub. They are of mixed sex and age, some are sober but
often the main part are inﬂuenced by alcohol or other drugs.
People aﬀected by alcohol or drugs can be considered to be temporarily impaired. The impairment symp-
toms range from slight euphoria and loss of shyness to signiﬁcant impairment of coordination and loss
of good judgement. Balance- and vision impairment are also common symptoms from consumption of
alcohol or other drugs. In worst case it can lead to mental confusion and ﬁnally onset of coma, and in
worst case scenario even death [www.sikkertraﬁk.dk]. Hence, the impairment covers mobility, sight and
cognition. Temporarily disabled people need to be considered in a ﬁre safety design in order to ensure
corresponding egress.
It is estimated that alcohol is involved in more than 40 percent of all residential ﬁre deaths in the United
States of America [FEMA (1999)]. As mentioned earlier it must be assumed that many occupants of a
nightclub are under the inﬂuence of alcohol or other drugs. It is therefore very important to consider the
design of the building due to the impairment of the occupants.

Structure of the report
In this section a short description of the report is made. This description is divided into the chapters
below. Furthermore, there is a description of the appendix. Apart from the questionnaires the material
for the two experiments are identical and are therefore only listed once. It has therefore been decided
that only the material from the experiment concerning alcohol are listed in the appendix.
Tables, ﬁgures and pictures are labelled with references. If there is not a reference displayed the tables
or ﬁgures are produced by the authors of this report.
Chapter 1. Introduction
In this chapter an introduction to the aim of the current study can be found. The background and the
structure of the report is also found in this chapter. The section regarding the background concerns prior
ﬁres and a more profound description of why this area is so important to investigate.
Chapter 2. Theory
This chapter is a general description of the theory regarding evacuation. The chapter is further divided
into seven sections which describe warning time, reaction and decision time, travel time and queue/ﬂow
time. Furthermore, there is a description of several ways to determine person densities. The focus of this
report is on alcohol and crowed behaviour. Therefore, the theory also includes a section regarding the
eﬀect of alcohol on the human body and crowd behaviour.
Chapter 3. Method and Experiments
The chapter is a description of the experiments, the preparations which have been done before the exper-
iment, and the execution of the experiments. This includes the set-up of the experiments, a description
of the equipment used in the experiment, a view of the participants and how the data of the experiments
is collected. Furthermore, the chapter includes the errors and limitations in the experiments and the
ethical issues which must be considered before and during experiments with humans.
Chapter 4. Results and discussion
In this chapter the results for the two experiments are displayed, described and discussed. This is in-
cluding the observations from the investigators’ point of view, as well as from the participants’ point of
view. In the end of this section there is a validation of the vision impairment equipment.
Chapter 5. Conclusion
A sum-up of results and the conclusion of the current thesis are found in this chapter.
Chapter 6. Future work
This chapter considers the future work that can be performed including questions raised from the results
of the study.

A list over the literature used in this report is made in this chapter.

Chapter 2
Theory
This section treats the basic theory of evacuation, such as person density, walking speed, reaction time,
the eﬀect of alcohol and crowd behaviour.
In literature concerning evacuation [Nelson(2008)] two types of time frames are used, the available safe
egress time (ASET) and the required safe egress time (RSET). The ASET is the time from ignition until
the ﬁre becomes critical for the occupants (when the building/room becomes untenable). The RSET is
the time needed to evacuate the occupants. To make sure the occupants are safe the relation between
ASET and RSET should be:
ASET > RSET
The ASET is based on threatening conditions such as, toxic gases, temperature, radiation from the ﬁre
and the smoke layer height. The RSET is based on the detection and alarm time, the pre-movement time
and the travel time. This can be calculated using equation 2.1
RSET = tevac = ta + tr+d + tm
(2.1)
• ta is the warning time.
• tr+d is the reaction and decision time.
• tm is the movement time.

2.1
Warning time
Warning time can be described as the time from ignition until the ﬁre alarm is activated and the occu-
pants are notiﬁed by the ﬁre alarm. The shorter this time is, the better people’s chances are to evacuate
safely. Detecting a ﬁre can take from few seconds to hours depending on the type of ﬁre and the detection
devices in the building [Prouls (2008)].
Also the time for alarm depends on the ﬁre alarm equipment used in the building, and can therefore also
vary. Detection and alarm are two events which in some cases can occur simultaneously. Delays between
the two events can occur - this may be caused by manual activation of the systems.
Warning time, which consists of detection time and alarm time, depends on the type of system installed
in the building - is it an automatic ﬁre alarm or is it a ﬁre alarm which should be activated manually by
the occupants, thus depending on human response to the ﬁre?
For example in places where detection and alarm systems depend on the occupants’ response, a small and
slow ﬁre can start or the ignition site can be separated from the place where the occupants are staying,
which may lead to it taking hours before the ﬁre is detected allowing the ﬁre to grow in size before being
detected.
If the detection and/or the alarm systems are not working properly, warning time will be further extended.
2.2
Reaction and decision time
Reaction and decision time is the time between a ﬁre alarm is activated and people start evacuating.
Reaction time is the time it takes before people react to the ﬁre alarm. Decision time is the time used to
decide what to do about the alarm.
Reaction and decision time is a time frame which contains many uncertainties due to the fact that it
depends on human behaviour.
A proposed approach to decision time is to divide the occupants of the building into four categories. The
categories are based on the interpretation of the ﬁre seen from each individual’s point of view [Levin
(1985)].
1
The occupant believes that there may be a ﬁre, but it is not certain.
2
The occupant believes that it is likely there is a ﬁre.
3
The occupant is sure that there is a ﬁre and has seen suﬃcient smoke
to believe that it is a dangerous ﬁre.
4
The occupant has seen ﬂames.
The people in the ﬁrst category are the ones who use most time deciding to evacuate, because they spend
time seeking other information. The last category is the fastest to react to the ﬁre.
The division into these categories is only based on a tendency and it is not certain that the occupants’
reaction time can be determined from these observations.
Studies in Great Britain and the United States of America have shown that people tend to do many other
things after they have been warned of a ﬁre, than to evacuate the building [Wood (1072)].
Furthermore, the crowd behaviour described in section 2.7 has a great impact on the reaction and decision
time. The relation between the occupants aﬀects the reaction and decision time. A close relationship will
7
==PAGE:15==
lead to a longer reaction and decision time because the persons tend to use more time to interact with
each other. Thus a family would want to make sure that everyone is ready before they start moving,
whereas strangers would tend to be less interested in putting themselves in danger to help each other
[Hagen (2005)].
Another type of response connected with crowd behaviour is that many people tend to believe it is a fake
alarm when they ﬁrst hear a ﬁre alarm, especially if they do not see any ﬁre or smoke. Most people will
not start evacuating before they see other people doing so, they would rather wait for a leader to take
action and exposing certainty that it is not a fake alarm.
A study has also shown that if evacuation tests or warning tests are often performed, the users of the
building tend to take them less seriously, and sometimes do not react on the alarms. This can lead to a
long reaction and decision time in case of a real ﬁre, if people ﬁrst believe that it is a test. To avoid this,
the warning system could contain a live speaker alarm, which informs the users that the ﬁre is real and
that the threat must be taken seriously [Bengton (2005)], [Hagen (2005)].
It must be mentioned that another study has shown that rehearsing evacuation shortens the evacuation
time, and also the reaction and decision time. If an evacuation has been practised people know how the
warning and alarm system sound and they know what they should do [Hagen (2005)].
In Denmark this training eﬀect from the evacuation exercises is obtained in for example elementary
schools where they have annual ﬁre drills.
It is not possible to train an evacuation in all types of buildings, since many buildings house institutions
or oﬃces with visitors who do not visit the buildings regularly and therefore they may not know their
way around. A positive inﬂuence on the reaction and decision time can be to train the personnel or staﬀ
of the building, to make sure there are some people, who are familiar with the building and therefore can
help and assist the other users of the building.
Reaction and decision time is also inﬂuenced by the design of the building. If a building is transparent,
so the users can see the other people in the building, they are able to react to the movement and reaction
of other people in the building. If the building contains many small rooms without a view to the other
users of the building, a person can be unaware of an ongoing evacuation because she or he can not see
the threat and is not aﬀected by the other users [Prouls (2008)]. The transparency of a building is also
aﬀected by the light inside and outside the building. If it is dark outside and there is not much light in
the building, it is harder to see other people in the building and harder to interpret their reaction.

react and decide. The estimated reaction and decision time depends on the user category of the building

The eﬀects of alcohol
It is commonly known that the consumption of alcohol has an eﬀect on the brain. The parts of the brain
that are most vulnerable to alcohol are listed bellow.
• CEREBELLUM - This part controls coordination,
damage to the cerebellum results in stumbling and loss of balance.
• LIMBIC SYSTEM – This part controls memory and emotions.
• CEREBRAL CORTEX - This part makes us able to think,
plan and act socially with other people.
This part also connects the brain to the rest of the nervous system.
The brain systems communicate with each other, and the rest of the body through nerve cells called
neurons. The messages from one neuron are carried to another neuron by chemicals called neurotrans-
mitters. One of the neurotransmitters is called glutamate, among other things the glutamate aﬀects our
memory. It is believed that the reason why some people experience a so called "black out" when drinking
large amounts of alcohol is that the alcohol interferes with glutamate action [National Institute (2010)],
[www.omalkohol.dk].

2.7
Crowd Behaviour
During the evacuation of a nightclub or of similar buildings, the people present are often acting in one or
more groups. But how does the group or the crowd inﬂuence the evacuation patterns when evacuating a
building in case of an emergency?
When talking about groups in this section, groups are deﬁned as people who know each other, there is
a well deﬁned hierarchy, and the group members are satisﬁed with their role in the group, and that the
position of the leader is clearly established [Agurre (2011)].
A crowd is deﬁned as individuals who are gathered in the same place, in an unfamiliar situation, where
they behave in a socially coherent manner. There have not been a prior awareness or communication
between the individuals regarding the norm and values of the group to guide their behaviour [Challenger
(2009)]. A crowd can also consist of smaller groups.
There can be many types of crowds and each kind of crowd is diﬀerent and reacts diﬀerently. One deﬁ-
nition of crowds divides them into four categories described in table 2.3.

A big issue when evacuating a crowd in a public place is that none of the visitors are familiar with the
place and all its exits. When people enter a nightclub or a similar place, they do not automatically look
for exit signs. If or when they get a couple of drinks, they can not remember where the exits are or they
only have a vague idea of how they got in. Because the visitors are not familiar with the place, it has
been seen that people during evacuation or in similar situations tend to follow the crowd, which means
that they do not use the closest exit. Choosing the closest exit might have saved their lives.
On the other hand it has been observed in several ﬁres e.g. Beverly Hills Supper Club, that people tend
to put their own life in danger to help, rescue and save others [Agurre (2011), p. 2]. This applies to some
of the visitors and many of the staﬀmembers.

The reason that there are good chances of surviving a building ﬁre, when people are in a group in a
simple building, is that people in a known group are good at helping each other and are good at following
a leader. There is a greater chance that the leader of the group ﬁnds a secure way out in time and the
leader will help everybody in the group or get others to help so that all members of the group will survive.
In a simple building if a person is alone during a ﬁre, there is no one who is aware that the person is
missing or has an obligation to help that unknown person.
The picture is diﬀerent if the building is diﬃcult to escape from, because there is a larger chance that
the leader stops, or holds on to the main group, until all the members are gathered and ready to leave
the building. Another reason why the chances for surviving a ﬁre in a complex building are smaller, can
be that the leader does not know the way out of the building and therefore misleads the group or spends
too much time to ﬁnd the exit.
In a diﬃcult building, a single person who is alone and with no strings to the surrounding persons is not
inﬂuenced by a leader or the need to follow a group, but can make his or her own decision how to survive
and ﬁnd a way out [Agurre (2011)].
A theory in decision making is that a few informed individuals are able to reach a consensus decision
without knowing whether they are in the majority or minority or whether their information conﬂicts with
other informed members of the group [Challenger (2009)]. Since some speciﬁc persons can lead a crowd
it would be a good idea to guide a crowd in an evacuation situation, with some key ﬁgures who would
have the necessary information to guide the crowd out. These key persons could be staﬀof the building
or volunteers.
In this part three main perspectives regarding crowd behaviour in an emergency situation will shortly
be mentioned. The three main perspectives are mass panic, aﬄiction and normative approach and social
identity approach.
A crowd is in the mass panic perspective thought of as more emotional and less intelligent than the indi-
viduals alone, which result in a mindless, instinctive, irrational and self-centred behaviour of the members
of the crowd when they feel trapped. This results in the individuals moving or attempting to move a lot
faster than they normally would do. The interaction between the members becomes more physical i.e.
they start to push each other. The movement in the crowd becomes uncoordinated especially when they
must move through bottlenecks, such as a slim corridor or a door. As a result the escape is slowed by
fallen or injured individuals who are in the way. In all this panic, alternative routes and other exits tend
to be overlooked or ineﬃciently used [Challenger (2009)].
18
==PAGE:26==
The aﬄiction model suggests that people tend to seek familiar places and move toward familiar people
in an emergency situation and help others from the family group. According to the normative model,
the behaviour in emergency situations is still governed by the same social rules as if the situation were
normal. Thus it was reported that in the Beverly Hills Supper Club ﬁre in 1977, women received more
help than men and that it was the men who helped. The women on the other hand, were the ones who
typically oﬀered emotional support to the injured [Challenger (2009)].
The last model suggests that the crowd changes from a physical crowd to a psychological crowd with a
shared social identity. This gives the members a feeling of unity and an expectation of mutual support.
This results in a crowd who acts as a source of strength for the individual crowd members [Challenger
(2009)].
There are many theories concerning crowd behaviour and when combining knowledge on the basis of
these theories with information from ﬁres that have already occurred, it is possible to learn more about
crowd behaviour in emergencies and how the building and the evacuation must be designed to help in
future emergencies.
In this section diﬀerent theories concerning crowd behaviour and evacuation have been studied and
discussed. Some of the theories contradict each other which may be due to the many uncertainties and
variables in connection with the subject, human behaviour and human actions. Even with the scientiﬁc
work which has been conducted until now, this ﬁeld of study is still unexplored in many areas and there
are as just mentioned many uncertainties regarding crowd behaviour and evacuation due to the big role
human behaviour plays in connection with evacuation.

Method and Experiments
This section concerns the methods used in the experiments and the conduction of the two experiments.
The section is divided into subsections which give a description of the experimental set-up and the equip-
ment, as well as a description of the participants. The preparations and the execution of the experiments

Flamingo test
The Flamingo test is a test of the participant’s balance. The purpose of the test is that the person is
balancing on the right leg for 60 seconds. The left foot is lifted and must not touch the ground. Every
time the person loses his balance and needs to touch the ground to restore the balance the time is stopped
until the person has restored the balance on one leg. The result is the number of times the test person
touches the ground and this is counted during the test. The experiment is video recorded.
To conduct the experiment, the following equipment is used: A timer and a bar. The bar must have
the dimensions: 5 · 3 · 50cm. The bar can be seen in ﬁgure 3.8. Furthermore, two persons are needed to
execute the test. The test person is balancing on the beam and the other person is managing the time
and counting the number of times the test person touches the ground. [www.topendsports.com].
Figure 3.8: The equipment used in the ﬂamingo test.

Up&Go test
The Up&Go test is a test of both reaction time and balance. In this test, the part regarding the reaction
time shows the time from the hearing the start signal to initiating movement and the balance part shows
the persons’ general balance in movement.
Before the test is started two chairs are placed with a 2.45 m distance between them, the chairs are facing
towards each other, see ﬁgure 3.9. The test person is sitting on one of the chairs and should get up as
fast as possible, run around the other chair and back to start and sit down on the ﬁrst chair. Another
person starts the test person with a "GO" and then records the time from the "GO" until the test person
is sitting on the chair again and says "STOP". The experiment is video recorded.
The equipment used for this test is a timer, two chairs and two persons. The test person does the running
and the other person manages the time [www.fysio.dk].

3.8
Errors and Limitations
In this section the probable errors and limitations of this study are described. In the ﬁrst part of the sec-
tion the errors are described. They fall into three categories: a) errors in connection with the equipment,
b) errors in connection with the fact that both procedure and data collection depend on human behaviour
and c) errors stemming from the interpretation of the information gathered during the experiments. In
the second part of this section the limitations of the experiment are explained.
As already mentioned the two experiments are carried out on two diﬀerent days. The locations of the
experiments are not the same. The ﬁrst one is carried out in a wide corridor where there is approximately
3.5 meters to the ceiling. The second experiment is carried in a hall where there is approximately 12
meters to the ceiling. The two experiments are carried out at diﬀerent times of the day and year which
gives diﬀerent lighting eﬀects on the lane and on the three tests. All of these variations in time and
location could have an eﬀect on the participants’ performance but this has not been investigated.
The errors regarding the equipment can be connected to the position of the cameras. Before the ex-
periment a draft for the positioning of the cameras was developed. But it turned out that the cameras
could not be placed as envisioned but at diﬀerent angles from what had been planned which might have
inﬂuenced our measurements a little because it was very diﬃcult to get a good view of the small screens
on the cameras. Because of the blind spots, it would have been better if there had been one more camera.
Also it was diﬃcult to ﬁx the cameras solidly which caused some problems and we had to replace one
camera with a spare camera.
Choosing to run the experiments with test persons gives another set of possible errors, because it is an
experiment with real people and you never know how they will react. This can give unexpected errors
due to their behaviour. The behaviour of the participants might also be aﬀected by the amount of sleep
they had, if they are in a period which contains a lot of stress or other similar impacts which can not be
controlled by the investigators in the experiment.
Some of the errors regarding the human interactions in the experiments stem from communication be-
tween:
• the investigators.
• the investigators and the participants.
• the investigators and the assistants.
• the assistants.
• the assistants and the participants.
• the participants.
In the experiments the test subject gets his information regarding the test from the information paper.
If there is a ﬂaw in this paper, all of the participants will misunderstand that part of the test. Therefore
all information is both given in writing and orally to minimize mistakes and so that potential errors can
be found and corrected.
Since there is up to 90 participants and only 10 assistants and investigators, each participant can not
be helped at the same time and errors can occur even though the participants are good at helping each
other. In all of the tests the participants must gather their own results. It is therefore not possible to
monitor if any of the test persons cheat with their results.
In the experiment based on alcohol consumption a few of the participants had taken part in the experi-
ment with the vision impairment goggles and therefore had performed all of the tests once before. They
34
==PAGE:42==
may therefore be aﬀected by the training eﬀect which can be developed if you have done an exercise once
before. This training eﬀect is presumably so small that it only interferes with the results to so small an
extent that it is hardly measurable.
The analysis of the videos opens up for some error possibilities. One of them is interpretation errors in
determining the exact position of the participants. This can be caused by reduced quality of the videos
and the reduced amount of light in the exercise area. The problem can also occur due to the camera
lens which has a ﬁsh-eye perspective, even though the chess mats minimize this problem. Another issue
regarding the accurate position of the participants is when they stand in front of each other on the videos,
especially during the experiments of density and the impact of density on walking speed.
When conducting experiments which should imitate real situations and at the same time must give valid
results, there are limitations. In the two experiments conducted for this thesis, two areas of limitations
concern the limited number of participants and the trainings eﬀect.
Regarding the number of participants there are two limiting issues: The ﬁrst limitation concerns the
limited number of participants in the experiments. In the free walking speed and the three tests, there
is a maximum of 30 participants in each group when the participants are aﬀected. To make the results
more valid the number of participants and amount of data should be higher. The second issue regarding
the number of participants occurs during the experiment where the impact of the density on the walking
speed is investigated. In this experiment all of the participants walk inside the lane several times. If
the experiment and the results should be optimal the participants should only walk inside the lane once
and the lane should be longer. To solve this issue, a larger number of participants should participate in
the experiment. To gather such a large number of participants is diﬃcult and to conduct such a large
experiment would also be hard.
Some of the participants participate in both experiments.
This could lead to the participants being
aﬀected by a trainings eﬀect which in turn could aﬀect the results from the tests involving these partici-
pants. The ﬂamingo test and the Up&Go test are produced in such a way that they are not aﬀected by
any trainings eﬀect which might occur in some of the other tests. In the lane test with both the horizontal
free walking speed and the impact of the density on the walking speed, it has not been investigated if the
participants can be aﬀected by the trainings eﬀect.

A comparison
This section compares the measured results from the goggle experiment and the alcohol experiment. Be-

from the two experiment is displayed and discussed.
Figure 4.21 shows a comparison of the reference values from the two experiments. The reference from

the alcohol experiment is displayed as a light grey dot with a matching trend line. The dark dotted line
represents the theoretical speed of travel compared to the person density, explained in section 2.3.
The reference from the two experiments was expected to be similar, but as it can be seen in ﬁgure 4.21
the two references are very diﬀerent. The walking speed is in general higher in the alcohol experiment
compared to the values in the goggle experiment.
On the other hand, the obtained densities in the
goggle experiment become higher than in the alcohol experiment. Both of these observations could be
caused by the diﬀerent numbers of participants in the two experiments. It is clear that a higher number
of participants in the goggle experiment makes it possible to obtain a higher density when the size of
the lane is the same as in the alcohol experiment. The reduced number of participants in the alcohol
experiment could also have had an inﬂuence on the walking speed due to the fact that the participants
walk through the lane multiple times. To be able to keep a steady ﬂow inside the lane the participants
walk a bit faster outside the lane. This could be the reason for a higher walking speed inside the lane
as well. Furthermore, the two references are compared to the theoretical speed of travel. Here it can be
seen that none of the results from the experiments are consistent with the theory. Both trend lines have
a smaller slope angle than the theory, which could indicate that the person density does not inﬂuence the
walking speed to such as much as the theory describes. The trend line from the goggle experiment shows
that it has not been possible to create a high enough density in order to obtain a walking speed equal
to zero. This indicates that higher densities are necessary in order to obtain a walking speed equal to zero.
When comparing the walking speed for the two experiments with the theoretical trend line it can be seen
that they are very scattered at low densities. In accordance with the trend lines for the two experiments,
a lot of the participants have a low walking speed at low densities compared to the theory. This could
indicate that the participants are not only aﬀected by the people close to them, but also by the fact
that they can see a lot of people ahead of them near the exit. Besides the participants walking at a
low walking speed the ﬁgure also shows that some of the participants are walking faster than the theory
suggests. This could be explained by observations on the video footage where some of the participants
are passing the line of participants by walking faster near the walls.
As mentioned above and as shown in ﬁgure 4.21 the trend lines for the two experiments are very diﬀerent.
This diﬀerence could be caused by the diﬀerent amount of participants and the fact that it is experiments
with humans which cause an uncertainty. Due to this human factor it is not possible to compare the
measured values directly between the two experiments. Instead the diﬀerences and the tendencies from
the two experiments are compared.

Crowd behaviour and observations
This section is divided into two parts, one concerning the investigators’ observations of crowd behaviour

Observations from the experiments
In this subsection the crowd behaviour and other important observations during the experiments are
described. In the ﬁrst part the behaviour observed during the goggle experiment is described and in the
second part the behaviour observed in the alcohol experiment is described.
During the experiment with vision impairment equipment, the behaviour of the participants changes. In
the beginning, the participants form aﬃliated groups. Hence, they only talk to the persons they already
know and are familiar with. After wearing the impairment goggles for a while, the aﬃliation weakens
and the participants start to talk to the strangers next to them. The aﬃliated groups tend to dissolve
during the experiment, but many of them are re-established when they have ﬁnished the experiment and
sit down for supper.
As mentioned in the previous part regarding free walking speed, the participants tend to walk further to
the left when wearing the goggles than without the goggles. This tendency is caused by the goggles, which
move the participants focus to the left. It is also observed how the participants start to use their hands
to orientate themselves, when they are wearing the goggles. They need to feel their way around, just like
people do when they are blindfolded. They touch the walls to make sure they are not walking into them
and thus they have something to help them walk straight as seen in literature [Fridolf (2013 b)], [Sorensen
(2013c)]. Some of the participants start to help each other inside the lane, so they have something to
hold on to. At the end of the lane, the assistants of the experiment need to help the participants get
out of the corridors because they keep walking into the walls and concrete columns. This observation is
consistent with the results found in the balance tests. In the balance tests it is found that the goggles
have a major eﬀect on people’s balance, which could be the reason why people inside the lane have to
use the walls for support and to be able to walk straight.
During the exercise regarding people density, it is observed that many of the participants’ bodies begin
to tilt and sway when the density is high. When the density is low, there is no body sway to detect.
Therefore, it can be argued that when people walk closely together, they have a tendency to aﬀect the
walking pattern of each others. The body sway is both observed when the participants are wearing gog-
gles and when they are not.
During the experiment and afterwards many of the participants tend to experience a headache and some
of them also experience nausea. These side eﬀects go on for a while after they have removed their goggles.
This is also consistent with the result that people’s balance is very aﬀected by the goggles.
During the experiment regarding alcohol, there is a change in the behaviour of the participants. After
the participants have consumed the beers they are very hard to control and organize. It is hard to make
them understand which kind of test they should perform and the communication in general are weak-
ened. The communication with the participants both when talking to them face to face and when giving
them information as a group is hard. They have a hard time standing still and concentrating when the
information is given. When the information is given, they act like they have not heard it.
The participants are in general having a hard time taking the experiment and the test seriously.
They become restive and start to do other things than participating in the experiment. An example can
be that they go for a smoke or do other similar things.
The participants tend to arouse each other in diﬀerent ways. For example during the three tests they
59
==PAGE:67==
challenge each other and themselves to be better than they were before and better than each other.
Another example is that during the lane test, they almost run around instead of walking and some of
them raise their hands and shout "Panic, panic, it’s burning!". Furthermore, one of the participants tries
to roll under the gap in the walls maybe to get through the lane as fast as possible.
During especially the Up&Go test it is observed how the participants get more daring. This is observed
by the way they place themselves on the chairs at the end of the test. When they are sober, they sit
down on the chair with care, so the chair does not get knocked over. When they are aﬀected by alcohol
many of them almost jump and throw themselves onto the chairs, knocking them over.
When the participants have consumed the alcohol the atmosphere in the room is very relaxed and casual.
The participants walk around hugging each other and walk arm in arm. Many of the participants tend to
show their happiness a lot more after the consumption of alcohol. For example one of them is so grateful
to be invited to attend the experiment that he thanks the investigators and the assistants more than
once.
It is also observed how many of the participants get much more social after consuming alcohol. For
example one of the participants is much alone during the ﬁrst part of the experiment and does not like
to talk much to neither the other participants nor the assistants. After only consuming his ﬁrst beer, he
is very eager to participate in the social interaction with the other participants.
Another observation regarding the atmosphere in the hall is the sound level. The participants start to
yell and talk very loudly during and after consuming the beers. Even though they are standing close to
each other they talk much louder than before the beers.
During the consumption of alcohol a certain amount of group pressure is detected. In the beginning this
pressure means that many of the participants pressure each other to drink as quickly as possible. The
group pressure continues during the three tests, where they challenge each other to perform better.
Regarding group deformation and group behaviour, the diﬀerent roles in the groups get more clear when
the participants have consumed alcohol. It is easy to see who of the participants are the leaders and who
are the underdogs. For example when the participants start to do the lane test, one person starts to run
with his hands over his head. As soon as his friend notices this, he also begins running like this.
During the alcohol consumption the groups’ size and shape changes. The original groups merge into
larger groups. Maybe because they have a common goal in consuming the same amount of alcohol and
in participating in the same experiment. An example of this is during the consumption of alcohol. Some
of the participants start to make a drinking game. After a few minutes several other participants join
the game, even though they do not know each other in advance.
A big experience from this experiment regarding time is that the part where the participants are aﬀected
by alcohol, takes much longer time to perform than when they are unaﬀected.
In both of the tests the participants tend to walk in the middle of the lane, when the tables are creating a
doorway. This is forcing the participants inside in the middle of the lane. When the tables are removed,
so the participants would not have to start in the middle of the lane, they use all the space inside the lane.
This could indicate that people and there walking pattern are aﬀected by the design of the lane/room.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
On the basis of the results in this study it can be concluded that alcohol aﬀects evacuation characteristics.
Therefore it is necessary to take this fact into account when designing buildings such as nightclubs where
people are often strongly aﬀected by alcohol. But it is also important to notice that this study initially
raised the question whether visual impairment goggles could be used to simulate the eﬀect of alcohol
intoxication’s inﬂuence on human behaviour and movement characteristics in case of a ﬁre in a large
public room such as a nightclub.
The study indicates strongly that the goggles only to a limited extent simulate a drunken behaviour be-
cause they only resemble intoxication’s inﬂuence on balance and free walking speed and signiﬁcantly diﬀer
when it comes to the impact of person density on the walking speed, human behaviour and cognition:
Thus the balance and reaction tests show that balance is highly aﬀected by the goggles, but surprisingly
it can also be seen that the reaction time is hardly aﬀected. This is not consistent with the statement
from the producer of the Fatal Vision Goggles®. The reason for this result is that the goggles only have
an inﬂuence on the vision and do not induce a cognitive impairment.
Also the lane test makes it evident that the goggles aﬀect the balance of the participants and that this
has an inﬂuence on the free walking speed and the walking pattern of the participants. The free walking
speed is reduced because of the poor balance and the participants have to use the walls and each other
as a support to be able to walk straight. Another result from the lane test is that the relation between
walking speed and person density changes due to the inﬂuence of the goggles. Again the walking speed
is reduced compared to the reference, which means that the slope of the trend line is decreased. From
these results it could also be indicated that the more powerful the goggles become the less eﬀect does the
person density have on the walking speed. Furthermore, this experiment has shown that it is possible to
reach a walking speed higher than zero even when the person density exceeds 3.75 pers/m2.
From the lane test the importance of a building design can also be seen. It is evident that the width of
the entrance has a big inﬂuence on the participants’ walking pattern. The walking speed is slowed down
and becomes almost constant in proportion to the person density when the entrance is wide. When the
entrance is narrow the participants do not use the entire width of the lane but the walking speed is higher.
The current study indicates strongly that evacuation characteristics are changed when people are aﬀected
by alcohol, as alcohol induces a cognitive impairment.
From the balance and reaction tests it can be concluded that balance is aﬀected by the alcohol. It is also
seen that the reaction time is slightly aﬀected by the alcohol. The diﬀerence in reaction time between
being sober and being intoxicated though, is not as large as expected, even though the alcohol induce a
cognitive impairment. The cognitive impairment, however, is especially obvious in the behaviour of the
participants during the tests which means that the more intoxicated the participants are the more they
seem to disregard the safety of themselves and people around them.
The results from the lane test are conﬂicting. From the free walking speed results it can be seen that
66
==PAGE:74==
the alcohol aﬀected balance has an inﬂuence on the pace of the participants. The free walking speed
is reduced after the participants have consumed alcohol. Another outcome of the lane test is that the
relation between walking speed and person density changes due to the inﬂuence of alcohol. But here the
alcohol is found to have the exact opposite eﬀect on the pace of the participants. The walking speed
is increased and the participants tend to be more daring. An explanation of this increase can be the
fact that the participants tend to arouse each other in diﬀerent ways. Another explanation can be the
reduced number of participants. This forced the participants to walk faster to keep a steady ﬂow inside
the lane. Again it is evident that the alcohol induce a cognitive impairment in the participants. When
comparing the three groups after consuming alcohol, it is indicated that the more inﬂuenced people are
the less aﬀect does the density have on the walking speed. This is clearly shown for group C where the
trend line is almost constant.
The diﬀerences in how the goggles and the alcohol aﬀect the human body reaction patterns are obvious.
The goggles induce a vision impairment while the alcohol induce a cognitive impairment. This is veriﬁed
by the statements from the participants. The participants state that when they are aﬀected by alcohol
they are not induced with the same heavy vision impairment as the goggles induce them with.
However, this does not mean that the goggles are useless in experiments where a simulated alcohol aﬀect
is requested. It is nevertheless necessary to take these diﬀerences into account when applying the goggles
to achieve the most valid results.

Chapter 6
Future work
During the current study several tendencies regarding evacuation of alcohol impaired people have been
found. Additionally new and important knowledge has been established. On the basis of the obtained
results new questions are formed and create a basis for future studies.
In general the tests and experiments regarding the behaviour of alcohol-aﬀected people when exposed to
threatening situations such as building ﬁres are limited. In order to ensure statical validity of the results
found in this study the data needs to be extended. In a future study it is therefore necessary to increase
the population size. The number of participants in the goggle experiment and especially in the alcohol
experiment must therefore be increased.
Some measurements in this study show that it is possible to obtain a walking speed even though the
person density is above 3.75 pers/m2. It has not been possible to obtain a person density high enough to
get a walking speed equal to zero. Accordingly it could be interesting to investigate how high a density
it is able to obtain before the walking speed becomes zero.
During this study of vision-aﬀected and alcohol-aﬀected persons, several tests have been conducted, but
not all of tests have yet been analysed. The topics that have not yet been analysed focus on:
• The ﬂow through a door opening at the end of the lane.
• The movement pattern of the participants inside the corridor.
• How fast the participants move inside the corridor.
• Does the music played during the test have an eﬀect on the participants’ walking speed and pace?
The data and collected material to investigate these questions can be found in the ﬁlms which have been
produced during the two experiments.
During the alcohol experiment all of the participants completed a survey before and after consuming the
beers. The survey treats the issue of social and individual behaviour and dominance and how individuals’
ethics and behavioural patterns are inﬂuenced by the consumption of alcohol. These questions have not
been analysed in this thesis but might state if alcohol has an impact on the persons’ behaviour regarding
aggressiveness, aﬀection for other persons and other observations consistent with the crowd behaviour
already observed in this study, and may consequently provide useful information in the process of under-
standing how alcohol changes evacuation patterns in connection with ﬁres in nightclubs, restaurants, etc.
68
==PAGE:76==
From the alcohol experiment it was experienced that the participants had a hard time receiving informa-
tion. It could therefore be interesting to investigate how a mixture of sober and intoxicated people would
aﬀect a possible evacuation of nightclubs. A hypothesis could be that when there is a relatively large
number of sober people, reaction time and walking speeds would improve thus facilitating evacuation.
Likewise it would be interesting to study whether it would become easier to communicate with and warn
partying groups of emergency situations if there are many sober guests present.
It is noticed that both experiments with visual impairment and experiments with alcohol related impair-
ments have their disadvantages, but could there be other ways of simulating the stressful atmosphere of
a ﬁre in a public area? Maybe by inducing people to sleep deprivation which according to studies have
some of the same cognitive consequences as the intake of alcohol has as described previously in this study
[Thomas (2000)], [Killgore (2014)].

Etisk kodeks for evakueringsforsøg afholdt af DTU BYG, hvor mennesker 
indgår som test personer 
 
Byggeri overalt i verden bliver i stadig større grad mere og mere spektakulært og komplekst. Dette 
skyldes bl.a. et behov for blandt bygherre og arkitekter at skille sig ud fra mængde. Den øgede 
kompleksitet stiller høje krav til bl.a. sikkerhedsniveauet i sådanne bygninger. Dette giver således 
også øgede udfordringer for brandingeniøren. Der findes forskellige værktøjer til at løse disse 
udfordringer såsom forskellig software til bl.a. at estimere totale evakueringstider. Disse værktøjer har 
imidlertid brug for en række input. Input til værktøjerne kommer fra forskellige videnskabelige 
evakueringsforsøg. En stor del af disse data er mere end 30 år gammels og der er sket rigtig meget 
med måden hvorpå bygninger bygges og designes på siden da. Det er derfor vigtigt at data der 
anvendes som input afspejler den virkelighed hvori de bruges. Det kan dog være svært at få data fra 
virkelige hændelser fordi man ikke ved hvor og hvornår sådanne hændelser forekommer og 
indsamling af data derfor er begrænsede. Dette kan dog imødekommes ved at lave 
evakueringsøvelser, som er så tæt på virkeligheden som muligt. Det er dog væsentligt i den 
forbindelse at udføre forsøgene etisk forsvarligt, da mennesker indgår som test personer i sådanne 
evakueringsøvelser. 
 
Formål 
 
Formålet med dette kodeks er at skabe ensartede retningslinjer for evakueringsforsøg udført med 
DTU BYG som ansvarlig. Kodekset skal sikre forsøgspersonerne rettigheder i forbindelse med 
videnskabelige forsøg. Derudover skal kodekset sikre at information til forsøgsdeltagere er ensartet og 
indeholder de nødvendige oplysninger.  
 
Kodeks for evakueringsforsøg 
 
1. Evakueringsforsøg og projekter med DTU BYG som forsøgsansvarlig skal i udgangspunktet altid 
anmeldes til Videnskabsetisk komite og Datatilsynet. 
2. Deltagere i evakueringsforsøg skal altid modtage både skriftelig og mundtlig information omkring 
projektets formål og metode. Den skriftelige information skal indeholde oplysninger om baggrund 
og formål for forsøget, plan for forsøget, bivirkning og risici, økonomiske forhold og adgang til 
forsøgsresultater, jf. skabelon tilgængelig via Campusnet eller vejleder.   
3. Forsøgsdeltagere skal udfylde en samtykkeerklæring inden de kan deltage i forsøg. 
4. Forsøgsdeltagere kan til enhver tid trække deres samtykke tilbage uden nogen begrundelse. 
5. Forsøgsdeltagere skal sikres fuld anonymitet. 
6. Forsøgsdeltagere må ikke kunne drage økonomisk fordel af at deltage i forsøget, dog kan der 
udbetales kørselsgodtgørelse og være forplejning i forbindelse med forsøget.  
7. Hvis en forsøgsdeltager føler sig dårligt tilpas under forsøgene kan denne straks trække sig fra 
forsøget.  
8. Ved ikke-annoncerede evakueringsforsøg skal alle deltagere straks efter forsøget informeres om 
hvad de har deltaget i og give deres samtykke til brug af data til forskning.

Experimental Setup 
The evacuation experiment is carried out in a test lane build for the experiment. The 
lane is placed in the Oticon building 107 at DTU Lyngby. The lane consists of walls 
that are 2.25 meters high and placed as shown on figure 1. The lane is formed as a 
squared “room” with the dimension 6*3 m. The room has one entrée, where two 
tables are placed, forcing the participants to start from the middle of the entrée, and 
one exit, the exit is through a door opening with a width of 1 meter. Outside the door 
a “corridor” is placed where the participants can choose to go left or right to exit the 
lane. The participants enter the lane from the left, move through the lane and out on 
the right side, see figure 1. The experiment is done with 90 participants. 
 
 
The experiment is divided into 2 different setups. The first setup is a reference and is 
therefore performed without any influence from alcohol.  
Afterwards it is performed again where the participants are affected by alcohol. The 
alcohol that is consumed is beer and these are handed out after the first setup has been 
conducted.  
Furthermore each setup is divided into two parts, one regarding the reaction time and 
the balance of the participants, where the test subject are conducting all of the three 
exercises. While waiting the participants are asked to answer 37 question regarding 
their group behaviour. The other part of the setup is regarding the person density, the 
walking speed and the crowd behaviour, where the subjects are walking in the test 
lane. See table 1 for the setups and parts.

Participants

segment. The participants in this experiment are therefore all in the age group 
between 18-35 years. This ensures that the population used in the experiments reflects 
the reality. Most of the people are recruited at DTU or by using Facebook.

with a colored cap representing which amount of alcohol they should consume in

Information to the participants regarding the experiment 
This information must be read and understood before you sign the statement of consent. If you have 
any question towards the information you are given or during the experiment, you are welcome to 
ask Marlene Westmose or Anne Madsen.  
The purpose of the experiment 
The purpose and aim of the experiment is to investigate how an evacuation is carried out when 
people are under the influence of alcohol. 
During the experiment different aspects are investigated. It is investigated how alcohol influences 
the balance and reaction time of the test subject and how the subject reacts during an evacuation. 
During the evacuation experiment the walking speed, person density and the human behaviour, 
including the social and physical interaction between the participants are investigated.    
Method used in the experiment 
The experiment is divided into two parts, one regarding the reaction time and the balance of the 
participants and the other regarding the density and walking speed.  
 
In the first part of the experiment, three tests are conducted. These are the flamingo test, the Up & 
Go test and a reaction test.  
The flamingo test investigates your balance when you are standing on one leg. The test must be 
performed on the right foot.  
The Up & Go test investigates your reaction time combined with your balance. To do the test, you 
must from a seated position, get up as fast as you can, run around an obstacle and sit down on the 
chair again.  
The reaction test measures the time from your eye observes a change, to you react on the change. 
This is done by using a computer.  
 
In the second part of the experiment, you must walk in a simple lane.  
 
Both parts of the experiments are first performed without being affected by any kind of alcohol. 
Then the two parts are performed again, this time the participants are affected by different amount 
of alcohol.  
The free alcohol is handed out after the first part of the experiment has been conducted. It is 
important that the participants only consume the given amount of alcohol and within the given time 
range. 
To control the experiment the participants are asked not to leave the area during the experiment.   
Consequences, risks and side effects due to the experiment  
During the experiment there are different risks and side effects. 
 
In the balance and reaction tests, there is a risk of losing your balance and of falling.  
 
Under the influence of alcohol you will experience loss of balance, change of your eyesight and 
lack of concentration, which may cause discomfort for some people. Consuming alcohol might 
cause hangovers. You are asked to give information about your experience during the experiments.   
 
If a person is injured the experiment will be stopped or interrupted.

Exclusion from the experiment 
If an unwanted behaviour is observed, the investigators may exclude a participant from the 
experiment. A reason to exclude a participant could for example be inappropriate behaviour 
towards the other participants or a desire to destroy or prevent the execution of the experiment. 
If the participant is excluded from the experiment, this person must leave the test area. 
Anonymity of the participants 
All of the tests and experiments will be filmed. Only the investigators will view these videos and 
have access to the test results measured during the experiment. No one will be used as a single 
example, but all the test results will be used as a mass result.  
Insurance for the participants 
The experiment is at your own risk! 
The Technical University of Denmark has no insurance to cover any injuries that may follow from 
taking part in the experiments.
