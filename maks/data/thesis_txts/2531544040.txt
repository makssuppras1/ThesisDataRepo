Abstract
Vision-based methods show great advantages for UAVs navigation. The goal for this project is to
have a drone following the center line of a waterway and it presents the possibilities and limitations
of using a visual and thermal camera.
It focuses on the analysis and segmentation of images
acquired using Pi Camera Module v2 and Flir Lepton 3.5 plugged into Raspberry Pi 4. Three
diﬀerent segmentation methods for visual images were developed and tested, ﬁrst and second based
on K-means on diﬀerent features and third on thresholding variance of intensity. Although method
2 has been discarded due to a long computational time, the others provided satisfactory results.
The ﬁnal shape of a waterway was extracted as the biggest connected region in the ’water’ cluster
and the central point of the ﬁgure was calculated. Furthermore, by segmenting the pre-treated
thermal images with the Otsu algorithm, we can get additional information to the segmentation
of visual images, especially in the presence of vegetation or ﬂooded areas.

1.1. MOTIVATION FOR THE PROJECT
1.1
1.1
Motivation for the Project
This project has been developed for an application for hydrological measurements, which requires
an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) to follow the central line of a waterway.
UAVs have been considered a good solution to ﬁll the gap between the ground-based measurement
stations and spaceborne observations. The latter, due to its coarse spatial and temporal resolution
are not well suited for assessing the water level of terrestrial surface water bodies and determining
complex water dynamics [1]. UAVs proved to be useful in hydrological surveys thanks to their small
size, low cost, ﬂexible payloads and capability to access even more hostile environments, such as
ﬂood areas. DTU Environment and DTU Space have developed a method to exploit mini-UAVs
(less than 10 kg) for water level measurements ranging system that includes a ranging sensor (radar
or sonar) and a high precision GNSS receiver. It provides much better accuracy than spaceborne
or airborne altimeter measurements as well as spatial resolution allowing for measuring the two-
dimensional spatial variability of small rivers and their interaction with ﬂoodplains. Figure 1.1
explains the measurement principle for retrieving the water level. More details about the method
and its application can be found in Bandini et al., 2017 [1].
Figure 1.1: Measurement principle for retrieving the water level in DTU Environment application ([1])
A challenging perspective for this application is a navigation system, which requires that the UAV
can follow the centerline of the waterway very accurately to get reliable measurements. Currently
implemented methods use pre-programmed ﬂight paths based on the existing maps and satellite

Problem Statement
This project is a feasibility study exploring whether and when it is possible to use vision-based
navigation along the waterway and whether thermal imagery might add further information to the
visual alone.
Due to the lack of a suitable data set consisting of both visual and thermal images captured at
the same instant of time from the same platform, it was found necessary to acquire new data. A
major part of work has been an analysis of the data sets in an attempt to deﬁne possibilities and
limitations of detecting a waterway in the images. The main challenges for segmentation were
surrounding vegetation, man-made constructions and light reﬂections. Also, the optimal ﬂight
altitude had to be estimated. Three diﬀerent methods for the segmentation of visual images were
developed and tested. Results accuracy for various situations and types of waterways was evalu-
ated, trying to include information from segmented thermal images. Finally, new central points of
a waterway were calculated.
It was deemed important to mention at this point that deﬁning what should be a riverbank proved
to be challenging, especially in case of ﬂood or existence of vegetation. Figure 1.3 presents two
diﬀerent ways of choosing riversides in the same image. The problem is described in more detail

2.2
Color Spaces
Every pixel at location (x,y) has speciﬁc values depending on the color space in which the image
is represented. Various color spaces are used in digital image processing and a choice of suitable
space should be based on the nature of the problem and the characteristics of the feature that
should be segmented. The most popular color space is RGB, where every pixel is deﬁned by the
value of the intensity of red, green and blue [8]. People tend to perceive water as "more blue" than
surrounding vegetation and this fact was used in this project. The intensity of blue was chosen as
a possible feature which could help distinguish water in the images (see section 4.2).
Another color space used in the project, YCbCr, is deﬁned by a mathematical coordinate trans-
formation from an associated RGB. Y stands for luminance, depicting brightness in the image,
and CB and Cr are blue-diﬀerence and red-diﬀerence chrominance values [6]. It is widely used in
digital video and photography systems.
Finally, also CIELAB color space needs to be shortly mentioned as it is a basic color space for
superpixels clustering described in section 2.5.3. It is a device-independent 3D color space, deﬁned
according to the typical sensitivity of the three types of cone cells in the human eye [6]. L stands
for luminance or brightness in the image, a is the amount of red or green tones and b is the amount
of yellow or blue tones.

2.4
Thermal Imagery
Radiation is emitted by all materials with a temperature above 0 K and the amount of radiated
energy increases with the increase in their temperature. According to Wien’s Displacement Law
the hotter the object, the shorter wavelength of its emitted radiation. A thermal sensor detects
in the thermal infrared region (TIR) of the electromagnetic spectrum, usually only in the long-
infrared region (8 −14µm). This radiation is not visible for the human eye or the visual cameras,
bringing additional information about the observed environment. The thermal characteristics are
not dependent on color, texture or lighting condition under which the image has been captured
and may also detect an object which is partly covered. Thermal imaging has been used for both
military and civilian purposes, such as monitoring geothermal exploitation areas [10] or agricul-
ture [11]. It has also been proven useful in monitoring temperatures of rivers or streams. [12] [13]
Temperature of a water-body should be homogeneous compared to surrounding vegetation and
structures. This characteristic has been tested in the project as a possible way for extracting a
waterway in the images.
IR sensors have low contrast in comparison to visual cameras and it is challenging to clearly detect
foreground from the background, hence contrast enhancement techniques need to be introduced.
Two diﬀerent techniques have been tested in the project: histogram equalization (HE) and contrast
stretching.
HE modiﬁes the dynamic range of the image by transforming the intensity values
in the input image in such a way that the resulting intensity histogram is nearly ﬂat.
This
mapping procedure is non-linear. HE is computationally easy to perform, although as IR histogram
distribution is diﬀerent from the one of the visual images and concentrated mostly in certain parts,
using HE might lead to over-enhancement.
The other technique, contrast stretching, is more
promising as it can preserve the global contrast and produce a more natural image. It diﬀers from
HE as the stretching of intensity values is linear. Results of both methods are shown in Section
4.2.

2.5. IMAGE SEGMENTATION
2.5
2.5
Image Segmentation
Image segmentation can be deﬁned as dividing an image into similar regions. In other words, it
is a process of assigning every pixel with a label in a way that pixels with the same labels share
similar characteristics. Finally, it might also be described as diﬀerentiating the foreground, in this
project a waterway, from the background. The objective of segmentation is to simplify an image
into something more meaningful and easier to analyze.
The easiest segmentation method is creating a binary image through thresholding. For example
for an intensity image, each pixel is replaced with 0 when the intensity value is smaller than
a certain threshold I(x, y) < T and 255 when the intensity value is bigger than the threshold
I(x, y) > T. Choosing a suitable threshold poses a great challenge and can be done manually,
after a previous analysis of the data or by adaptive algorithms. Further segmentation methods
described in this chapter focus on the adaptive ways of grouping the pixels in the images. However,
in 2.7 a subjective method of choosing a variance threshold will also be introduced.

K-means clustering in segmentation
K-means is an unsupervised machine learning algorithm, that tries to infer an underlying structure
of data matrix X having just the input data and no information about the expected output y. It
aims to divide the data set into non-overlapping sets - "clusters", therefore it is referred to as a
clustering technique. The most basic way of deﬁning a cluster is based on distance measures. In
the K-means algorithm, a cluster is a set of observations where the distance to any point within the
group is smaller than the distance between the observations outside of the group. Therefore, the
goal of k-means is to minimize the sum of squared distances (usually Euclidean distances) between
all points and the cluster center. If K is the number of clusters, µ1, ..., µk are the cluster’s centers
and zik is a binary variable describing which cluster i belongs to, then the goal of the K-means
clustering is to ﬁnd µk and zik which minimizes E in equation 2.5. [15].

In practice, K-means is a simple iterative algorithm, initialized with the cluster centers at random
locations. The second step assigns all the points to the nearest µk and then updates the location
of each µk to be the mean of all the points assigned to it. The step is repeated until the location
of the centers does not change anymore. The algorithm always converges, however, the result
depends strongly on initialization, therefore it is recommended to initialize several times with dif-
ferent center locations.
There are two main drawbacks of the K-means algorithm: the number of clusters needs to be
known beforehand and it prefers "round" clusters with roughly equal size, which means that clus-
tering can be driven by outliers in the data or diﬀerent scales of variables. The second problem
can be ﬁxed by standardization of the input data. The ﬁrst one does not apply to the project as
only two groups of pixels should be found in the images: ’water’ and ’land’.
The K-means algorithm has been chosen as a suitable method for the project as it is a compara-
tively easy and fast to implement, particularly when the number of clusters is known a priori. Also,
it can work for many diﬀerent variables that allow diﬀerent features as input information as well
as spatial location to enforce neighboring pixels being clustered in the same group. Every input
pixel p is described by N values, therefore represented as a vector [pi, ..., pN]. However, as the
K-means algorithm might be memory extensive [8], the number of input information was though
aimed to be minimized as much as possible, which will be described in more detail in 4.2.

Superpixels
Superpixels represent a slightly diﬀerent approach to image segmentation as it groups pixels shar-
ing similar characteristics into structurally meaningful regions to provide a convenient and compact
representation of an image for further data clustering or classiﬁcation tasks. [6] Further segmenta-
tion can be performed over regions rather than for a full image grid, which proves to be especially
useful when working with big images, decreasing computational time. The algorithm used for
computing superpixels in Matlab, and therefore also in that project, is Simple Linear Iterative

PCA
Working with high-dimensional data sets is computationally heavy and urges us to look for the
lower-dimensional representation of the data. Principal component analysis (PCA) is a widely used
technique of reducing dimensionality through feature extraction where new variables are created
as a combination of the original ones, which has an advantage over feature elimination as all the
important information is taken into account. PCA reveals simple underlying structures in com-
plex data sets using analytical solutions from linear algebra such as Singular Value Decomposition
(SVD). Reducing the dimensions of the feature space has also an advantage of facilitating the
understanding of the relationship between the variables and decrease the likelihood of over-ﬁtting
the model.

v1, ..., vN of V and choosing the n-dimensional representation b1, ..., bN ∈Rn where bi is deﬁned as

2.7
Methodology
This project is focused on extracting the waterway from the background in three diﬀerent data sets
(described in more detail in 4.1), trying to detect riverbanks as precise as possible. Another task
was testing whether a thermal camera can add valuable information to segmentation, therefore
self-acquired data set (Bavelse Sø) contains images from two sensors: thermal and visual, captured
at the same time and from the same platform.
Combining information from two sensors can be done at the beginning, as a fusion of raw images
or at the end of the process, as a fusion of already segmented images. Both ways have pros and
cons. The former requires all the pre-processing steps to be done in the same way for both types of
data, but it allows the thermal image to be a channel in the segmentation algorithm. On the other
hand, while combining already segmented images, two sensors are considered as equally important
and the ﬁnal location of the riverbanks becomes an average.
Both of the methods described above need pixel-to-pixel correspondence between thermal and
visual images. Due to the diﬀerent properties of both modalities, performing an accurate stereo
calibration was found to be a diﬃcult task (see Appendix A). As a result, images were processed
independently and further on it was analyzed whether information from a segmented thermal
image can add to the information from RGB image. For this reason, also this section has been
divided into a visual and a thermal part for the clarity of explained methods.

2.7. METHODOLOGY
2.7
Three diﬀerent methods for image segmentation have been tested as summarized in ﬁgure 2.2.
They are further referred to in the text as method 1, 2 and 3 (as in the ﬁgure).
Method 3 is computationally fast and simple as it is based on the fact that intensities of water
are constant and strive for zero, in contrast to the oscillating intensities of vegetation (plots of the
variance are presented in chapter 4). Therefore calculating local variance in a small window and
choosing a threshold below which pixels can be labeled as water, might be a fast way of extracting
the river. In the project, window size has been set to 4x3 pixels. For 4 out of 5 analyzed data
sets, a threshold equal to 0.1 ∗µ, where µ denotes the mean variance in the image, has proved to
successfully segment the image. The method is undoubtedly based on the user’s experience and
comparison of variance in diﬀerent images. It has been realized in the course of the project that
threshold needs to be adjusted for smaller and fast-ﬂowing streams and will probably need to be
adjusted for diﬀerent types of vegetation.
Both methods 1 and 2 are based on the K-means clustering algorithm described in section 2.5.2.
The diﬀerence is the input information that segmentation is based on. Method 2 uses diﬀerent
color spaces together with variance (choice of suitable input information has been discussed in
section 4.2). It is supported by previous superpixels clustering in order to obtain spatial infor-
mation and decrease computational time. Method 2 takes as input information visible diﬀerences
in texture between water and surrounding vegetation. It uses Gabor ﬁlters, described in 2.3 and
additional spatial information.
In most of the cases, it has not been possible to perfectly label the images e.g. due to the presence
of water pools, also clustered as water or smooth ﬁelds nearby which show more similarities with
water than vegetation. Therefore a waterway has been deﬁned as the biggest part of the cluster
with connected pixels, so-called ’blob’. The centroid of the ﬁgure becomes an adjusted central
point of the waterway. Due to the complicated shape of the waterway in some images, the center
of the ﬁgure does not necessarily end up being in between two river banks, therefore results are
better when the ﬁgure is divided into smaller parts.
Table 2.1 shows mean CPU time calculated for every method, based on six diﬀerent images (two
from Aamose, two from Skensved and two from Bavelse Sø). It is clear that method 3 has a great
advantage over the other two for real-time navigation. Moreover, method 2 is around 6 times
slower than method 1 and 22 times slower than method 3, therefore has been discarded from being
suitable for navigation. However, pre-processing and results of segmentation for method 2 are still
presented in chapter 4 to visualize when texture information might improve segmentation results.

3.3
Camera Synchronization and Calibration
Both cameras were plugged into the Raspberry Pi and images were captured using OpenCV as a
framework for the application. Pi Camera was controlled with picamera library commands and the
thermal camera was treated as a USB camera. Images were captured in a loop with a frequency
of 1 frame per second and saved into separate folders together with their timestamps to ensure
that compared images are taken at the same time. Pi Camera resolution has been set to 640 x 480
pixels and thermal images have been resized to 640 x 480 pixels and changed from raw to 8-bit
before being saved. For the purpose of this project, which was a proof of concept rather than a
fully operational navigation system, such synchronization was thought to be suﬃcient. However,
it would be advised to acquire more precisely synchronized data sets with more frames per second
for further tests.
Calibration of the cameras is needed to remove image distortion and provide pixel-to-pixel cor-
respondence between visual and thermal cameras for the segmentation algorithm. The theory
behind camera calibration has been mentioned in Section 2.1. In practice, the most commonly
used method for camera calibration is showing it a chessboard pattern. It works perfectly for the
visible-spectrum cameras as corners in the pattern are easily detected to the high contrast. In
the case of a thermal sensor, a chessboard pattern was laser cut in a wooden plate and attached
to a plate with high thermal conductivity. Another approach tested in this project was to attach
a wooden pattern to an LCD monitor based on the approach presented by Vidas et al. [5] In-
trinsic and extrinsic camera parameters were easily computed using Matlab Camera Calibration
App based on the approach developed by Bouguet, [7]. It yields satisfactory results as the mean
reprojection error was estimated to be 0.11 pixels for RGB images and 0.51 pixels for thermal
images. Graphs and Results are shown in Appendix A.

4.3. SEGMENTATION
4.3
4.3
Segmentation
Methods used for segmentation have been described in section 2.7.
Visual images have been
processed using three diﬀerent methods (referred to later in the text as method 1, method 2 and
method 3):
• K-means segmentation using four channels: variance, blue, chrominance and luminance
• K-means segmentation with additional texture information
• thresholding variance of intensity
For method 1, superpixels were calculated using the Matlab function superpixels and their number
was set to 2000. K-means segmentation was performed using the Matlab function kmeans with a
number of times to repeat clustering using new initial cluster centroid positions set to 5 and the
maximum number of iterations to 1000. For most of the threshold of the case for variance was set
to be 10% of the mean value of the variance and is speciﬁed in the text if chosen diﬀerently. River
as an object has been extracted using the Matlab function bwareaﬁlt, which ﬁnds the biggest blob
in a segmented image. Blobs were searched for only in data segmented by method 1 and method
3 as method 2 is too computationally heavy and has been excluded from further calculations as
mentioned in Section 2.7, Table 2.1.
Thermal images have been segmented using the Otsu method described in Section 2.5.1. In this
section, visual and thermal images are shown in the same ﬁgures for interpretation purposes. How-
ever, due to the calibration problem described in Appendix A it is hard to make an exact estimate
on how much they would improve the segmentation.

4.4. CENTER OF THE RIVER
4.4
4.4
Center of the river
Calculations of the center point of a waterway are based on the previous segmentation, therefore
only a precisely extracted waterway can give a satisfactory result. The central point has been
calculated as a center of mass of the ’blob’, using the Matlab function regionprops. Due to the
complicated shape of the river in some images, better results are obtained after dividing the blob
in two or three separate shapes.
Figures 4.67 and 4.68 show examples of central points calculated in well-segmented images where
the shape of the waterway can be approximated as a rectangle. The blue line and blue crosses are
based on segmentation by method 1 and the red line with red crosses by method 3. In ﬁgure 4.67
the blue central points are closer to the right river bank, which is clear after comparing ’blobs’ in
(b) and (c) as method 1 recognized the waterway also behind a stripe of vegetation.

5.1. COMPARISON OF METHODS FOR VISUAL IMAGES SEGMENTATION
5.1
This chapter explains the performance of the developed algorithm with an emphasis on its lim-
itations. It is divided into four sections, presenting the impact of the surrounding environment
and ﬂight altitude as well as summarizing the pros and cons of diﬀerent segmentation methods
of visual images introduced in Section 2.7. It aims to sum up all the comments from Chapter 4.
Types of waterways were roughly described in Table 4.2.
5.1
Comparison of methods for visual images segmenta-
tion
Methods have been roughly compared while presenting the Results throughout the Chapter 4, but
more structured summary will be presented in this section.
To begin with, method 1 and 2 are based on the K-means algorithm which measures similarities as
the Euclidean distance between values of pixels. Thus results will be driven by an abrupt decrease
in value of, e.g. variance, as the pixel becomes more similar to ’water’ than ’land’. Looking again
at the plots 4.7, it can be realized that even though ﬂattening of the curve is visible, values of the
single pixels can be ’closer’ to the values of water.
In order to minimize the inﬂuence of single pixels’ values, method 1 and 2 takes into consideration
the spatial information, trying to group pixels from the same area in the same cluster. It enforces
clusters to be connected regions and in consequence, if a ’smoother’ patch has been found in nearby
ﬁeld, it will be included in the extracted ’blob’ and the resulting central point of the waterway
will not reﬂect the actual location of water as shown in Figure 4.69. Method 3 is based only on
the value of intensity of gray, therefore ﬁnal ﬁgure does not contain smaller patches of low variance.
Method 1 can detect abrupt changes in the values describing the pixels much better than method
2 and small patches of ’water’ between ’land’ are common in the segmented images. Also, an
extracted shape of a waterway usually contains parts of riverbanks with weaker vegetation and is
wider than the one extracted by method 3 as shown in Figure 4.24. Method 1 detects well all the
ﬂooded regions and connects them with the main riverbed as in image 4.47. It is also sensitive
to luminance values, therefore it might cause problems on a sunny day when the light is reﬂected
towards the camera.
Method 2 proves to be eﬀective in lower altitudes when only a small part of vegetation and a
waterway is visible. It can deﬁne very precise and well-shaped borders as in Figure 4.24. In the
presence of an obstacle, such as stripe of vegetation in Figure 4.48, ’water’ behind the reeds, not

5.2
connected to the main waterbed is considered ’land’. Method 2 fails to recognize waterway in Fig-
ure 4.25 when the vertically aligned river and nearby ﬁelds are cut by a horizontally aligned bridge.
The biggest disadvantage of segmentation based on the texture analysis is its long computational
time, which was already presented in Table 2.1, caused by a big number of dimensions it needs to
work with.
Method 3 is the most simple in its assumptions as it takes into consideration only one characteristic
of the river: its homogeneous variance of intensity. It correctly clusters water pixels in almost every
situation, apart from a fast-ﬂowing stream from the Skensved data set. When the surrounding veg-
etation is smooth, method 3 might cluster it as ’water’, e.g. as in Figure 4.65 (h). Also, the stripe
of reeds 4.43 is not included in the main waterway shape, even though water pixels are detected
on both sides of the obstacle. The shape of the ’blob’ extracted from image segmented by method
3 is usually more narrow than expected as even isolated blades of grass increase the variance values.
It is very hard to compare diﬀerent segmentation methods as they all have advantages but also
face certain diﬃculties. Method 2 can be excluded as the results do not add enough improvement
to other methods to balance for a long computational time. The choice between method 1 and 3
should be based on whether it is more reasonable to have a shape which is too wide or too narrow.
It is also a choice between an adaptive algorithm and a more simple approach that requires user
interaction. Diﬀerence between method 1 and method 3 is well visualized by the diﬀerences in
shape of the extracted ’blobs’ in Figures 4.46, 4.53 or 4.60 as well as in the center points of the
waterway in Section 4.4.
5.2
Types of waterways
The best segmentation results were obtained for a wide river with stagnant water and moderate
vegetation on the sides, such as Site 2 in Bavelse Sø data set. The most diﬃcult waterway to
segment was a fast-ﬂowing stream with visible waterbed from Skensved data set. The quality
of segmentation is also strongly inﬂuenced by the surrounding environment.
Generally, when
the riverbanks have diﬀerent characteristics than the water and there are no abrupt changes in
vegetation, the waterway can be precisely segmented. Features that were found to decrease the
quality of results are:
• trees and bushes
• man-made construction
• shadows

5.3
• reﬂections
• ﬂooded areas
• fast-ﬂowing water
Extensive vegetation might completely or partially cover the water surface and prevent the camera
from ’seeing’ the water as in Site 3 of Bavelse Sø data set, visualized, e.g. in Figure 4.56. Partial
coverage by branches and bushes does not allow to extract the correct shape of the waterway as in
Figures 4.27 or 4.34. Also when the type of vegetation is diﬀerent on the left and right riverbank,
as in Figure 4.40, segmentation will not work correctly, especially the performance of method 2
decreases as the texture of green grass is more similar to the texture of water than taller grass
on the other side. Another challenge is water vegetation, e.g. stripe of reeds in the middle of the
waterway as in Figure 4.44 or water plants as in Figure 4.33. All methods usually cluster water
vegetation as ’land’, which correctly captures its characteristics, however, might lead to narrowing
the ﬁnal shape of the waterway. Using thermal imagery might help to avoid the impact of vegeta-
tion on segmentation results as in Figure 4.38 from Skensved data set. Even though small branches
are still visible above the water surface, segmentation results are correct. However, bigger bushes
as in Figure 4.36 are still clustered as ’water’ and the water plants fall into the ’land’ cluster. The
thermal camera used to acquire data in Bavelse Sø does not capture the vegetation on the sides of
the stream at Site 3, e.g. in Figure 4.57, which indicates a possibility to include thermal images
in the future navigation algorithm.
Man-made constructions, such as a bridge or a road, as in Figures 4.47 and 4.29 are often made of
concrete and therefore they have a smooth texture and homogeneous variance of intensity showing
more similarities with pixels representing ’water’. Moreover, a bridge might also cast a shadow on
the water surface or surrounding vegetation as in Figure 4.41, which decreases the visibility and
lead to incorrect segmentation in both RGB and thermal images.
Reﬂections of surrounding objects in the water surface as in Figure 4.28 lead to sudden changes
in variance, the intensity of blue and luminance, which impede segmentation for methods 1 and
3. Texture segmentation can achieve better results. Reﬂection of light from the water surface is
an additional diﬃculty as shown in 4.50. Method 3, taking into consideration only the variance,
is less prone to mistakes connected to higher values of luminance. It was however surprising that
method 2 does segment the image correctly.
Flooded and muddy areas are challenging and can confuse the visual segmentation methods. Ther-
mal images can clearly distinguish between ’wet’ and ’dry’ areas, which can be seen, e.g. in Figure
4.47. Also, in the results from Skensved data set, thermal images do not capture the irregularities
in a fast-ﬂowing stream and visible waterbed as it does not change the temperature.

5.3. FLIGHT ALTITUDE
5.4
5.3
Flight altitude
In this section, segmentation results for images captured at diﬀerent ﬂight altitudes will be com-
pared. Some initial thoughts were already described in Subsection 4.3.3.1.
To begin with, the choice of the ﬂight altitude should be a trade-oﬀbetween the height at which
both sides of the waterway are visible and the height at which the shape of the water becomes
too complex. Central points can be calculated correctly only if the extracted ﬁgure represents
the waterway and is as close to the rectangle as possible. Moreover, with the increase of height,
details are lost, therefore segmentation based on texture or variance might fail. Images captured
at a clearly too high altitude are shown in the third column of Figures 4.63 and 4.65.
Also, if more of the environment is included in the image, the more diﬃcult it becomes for the
segmentation algorithm to cluster the pixels into just two categories. There is a high probabil-
ity that nearby ﬁelds are smooth and their variance and texture will be closer to values of water
than trees or bushes in the close area. Comparison of Figures 4.24 and 4.26 visualizes the diﬀerence.
Flight height was expected to play a bigger role for thermal images however, it cannot be concluded
from the analyzed data. Though, minimum ﬂight altitude should be adjusted according to the
smaller ﬁeld of view of the thermal sensor.
Generally, it is not possible to recommend a ﬁxed height at which a drone should ﬂy to achieve
the best results. Flight altitude needs to be chosen based on the width of a waterway as well as
ﬂight velocity and surrounding vegetation.
5.4
Algorithm limitations
The main and most important limitation of the vision-based navigation is the amount of informa-
tion visible for the sensors. Thus, whenever the water surface is covered by vegetation, the shape
of the waterway cannot be identiﬁed and central points will not be adjusted correctly. Thermal
images help identifying water borders in case of Bavelse Sø, however a sensor with a better res-
olution used for acquiring images in Skensved data set captures water plants and nearby bushes,
therefore the segmentation is biased in the same way as the visual one.

6.1. CONCLUSION
6.2
6.1
Conclusion
This project was developed to test the possibility of using visual and thermal sensors for vision-
based navigation of UAVs. To achieve useful results of a hydrological survey, a UAV should follow
the center line of a waterway. Pre-programmed paths using GNSS do not always work due to
changes in riverbed locations, therefore central points of the waterway might need to be continu-
ously adjusted.
First, the available data from Aamose and Skensved were used for initial analysis. A new data set
where both visual and thermal images were captured from the same platform at the same instant
of time was collected. The payload conﬁguration was designed to meet all the requirements. It was
chosen to use the PiCamera Module v.2 together with the thermal Flir Lepton 3.5 plugged into the
Raspberry Pi 4. The data set was collected near Bavelse Sø at various ﬂight altitudes and at three
diﬀerent sites, capturing slightly diﬀerent types of waterways. The attempt to perform multimodal
camera calibration using Matlab stereoCameraCalibrator application was not successful, therefore
visual and thermal images were processed separately.
Three diﬀerent segmentation methods for visual images were developed and tested: method 1 based
on K-means segmentation using a variance, blue, chrominance, and luminance, method 2 based on
K-means segmentation with additional texture information and method 3 based on thresholding
variance of intensity. Method 2, even though it is able to eﬀectively segment water and vegetation
and detect a well-shaped riverbank, was discarded as it is computationally too heavy for real-time
computing. Both methods 1 and 2 can correctly segment images in most of the cases. They are
sensitive to abrupt changes in values, therefore can detect pools in nearby ﬁelds. A partially cov-
ered water surface, reﬂections, man-made constructions, and extensive vegetation might decrease
the results of segmentation. The ﬁnal shape of the river was extracted as the biggest ’blob’ of a
’water’ cluster. Shapes detected by method 3 are generally more narrow than the ones detected
by method 1, which inﬂuences the location of central points. Moreover, the ﬂight altitude needs
to be chosen carefully as both riverbanks should be visible, but a too big ﬁeld of view might result
in a too complex shape where ﬁnding central points becomes more complicated.
Thermal images have been ﬁltered by a median ﬁlter with a window size of 16 x 16 pixels to reduce
noise and the contrast was enhanced by contrast stretching. They were segmented with the Otsu
algorithm and proved to be useful in the presence of extensive vegetation and ﬂooded areas.
To summarize, the obtained segmentation results are satisfactory and support the idea that visual
and thermal images can be used to improve UAV navigation along a waterway. The next sections
will give a short overview of points for future work identiﬁed during this project.

6.2. FURTHER WORK ON SEGMENTATION ALGORITHM
6.3
6.2
Further work on segmentation algorithm
Some additional thoughts on the improvement of the segmentation algorithm are presented in this
section.
To begin with, this project was focused mostly on the segmentation of the visual images and the
thermal images were considered only as a possible source of additional information. However, ther-
mal imagery has a lot of potential to solve the problems of extensive vegetation on the riverbanks
and detecting ﬂooded regions. It is thus recommended to give more attention to thermal images in
the future work on UAV navigation. It might be useful to consider other algorithms for the contrast
enhancement, such as a technique proposed by Kim et al., 2011 [18], where contrast stretching is
combined with gradient-domain processing. Moreover, the Otsu algorithm did not work correctly
in the presence of trees and bushes on the riverbanks as they were clustered as ’water’. It is thus
considered a good idea to test algorithms that take into consideration the shape context, similarly
to approaches developed for the segmentation of the medical images. [19] The prior shape of a
waterway is roughly known, especially when images are captured from shorter distances. Thermal
images show fewer details than visual ones, therefore it is easier to ﬁnd a well-deﬁned shape. Also,
as a UAV follows the waterway, it needs to be a continuous object spanning between one side of the
image to the other. Voxel-based segmentation using Markov random ﬁeld models can be identiﬁed
as a possible approach.
A diﬃculty that needs to be overcome to include thermal images in the ﬁnal algorithm is a problem
with multimodal camera calibration that should be solved in future work on UAV navigation. A
point to consider is that the calibration should be done at a distance comparable to the ﬂight
altitude. This should be taken into account when designing the calibration procedure.
Additional work on pre-processing of visual data can improve algorithm performance. As an ex-
ample, limitations due to shadowing might be minimized using algorithms removing shading and
reﬂection, e.g. Retinex algorithm.[20]
The survey might need to be repeated due to the problems with cameras at low temperatures. It
is recommended that thermal data should be saved as raw images rather than changing them to
8-bit as some details might be lost. Besides, it is clear that for the comprehensive overview, im-
ages need to be captured in a diﬀerent season when vegetation is green and the water level is lower.

6.3. NAVIGATION ALGORITHM OVERVIEW
6.3
6.3
Navigation algorithm overview
This Section will present additional problems that need to be solved in the ﬁnal computer-vision
based navigation algorithm. The correct segmentation is an important part of the algorithm but
needs to be looked at in the context of the ﬁnal application.
The purpose of this project was to adjust the pre-programmed central points in case of small
variations in the riverbanks location. It is assumed that the drone ﬂies above the waterway and
the images show water. A diﬀerent approach will need to be introduced if the algorithm needs
to decide whether the water is present in the image. Proposed methods assume that an image
has to be divided into two separate clusters, therefore ’water’ will always be found. In the ﬁnal
navigation algorithm, especially when working with smaller streams, a case when a waterway is
outside of the drone’s ﬁeld of view should also be considered.
Moreover, as mentioned in Section 5.4, only what is visible for cameras can be correctly segmented,
thus a solution for a case when an obstacle (e.g. a tree) partially covers the water surface needs to
be introduced. It is proposed that the algorithm keeps in memory central points and width of the
river from several steps back and that abrupt changes of these values are ignored. The number of
steps needs to be adjusted to the speed of a drone.
Another problem that needs to be solved is the number of frames per second that should be ana-
lyzed to achieve reasonable results. Taking into consideration that the acceptable speed of UAV in
water level application is 2 m/s, processing 2 frames per second will allow adjusting central points
every 0.5 meters. It seems reasonable for wide rivers with a constant shape. However, for more
narrow streams and sharp bends, it might be too sparse and the problem should be analyzed more
carefully.
Finally, the direction in which the cameras should point needs to be decided. In the Bavelse Sø
data set, cameras are facing straight downwards, which helps to avoid reﬂections, but it is not an
optimal approach for the navigation algorithm. The cameras should be slightly tilted, pointing
forwards in the direction of the ﬂight, to be able to predict where the drone is going rather than
where it is right now.
