Abstract
In recent years, the focus on endocrine disrupting chemicals and their potential to disrupt
the endocrine system in humans has increased due to rising incidence and prevalence of many
endocrine disorders such as genital malformation and low semen quality.
Chlorinated paraffins are a group of chemicals widely used for different industrial purposes
suspected to have endocrine disrupting potential. Short-chained chlorinated paraffins (SCCPs)
are classified as Persistent Organic Pollutants, hence their use is regulated in most countries.
The effects of exposure to medium-chained (MCCPs) and long-chained chlorinated paraffins
(LCCPs), are less investigated compared to the SCCPs, and their use is currently unregulated.
In this thesis, nine different CPs, consisting of SCCPs, MCCPs and LCCPs were tested for
their potential to agonize or antagonize the androgen receptor activity and their potential to
interfere with the production of testosterone and estradiol, with the androgen reporter gene
assay and the H295R steroidogenesis assay, respectively.
The results presented in this thesis revealed that the SCCP 1,2,9,10-tetrachlorodecane (CP2)
was able to antagonize the androgen receptor activity and induced the production of estradiol
through the steroidogenic pathway.
None of the other CPs tested were able to agonize or
antagonize the androgen receptor activity nor interfere with the production of testosterone or
estradiol. Furthermore, the results suggest that the chain length is a structural trait with a
determining factor, while the chlorination degree is not.
Based on these in vitro results it is suggested that 1,2,9,10-tetrachlorodecane (CP2), through
its mechanisms of actions, can contribute to the development of male reproductive disorders.

Introduction
In recent years, there has been a growing concern that ”everyday” chemicals have the potential
to disrupt the endocrine system in both humans and wildlife (WHO, 2012). Research has shown
that fetal and early postnatal development in humans is highly susceptible to external factors,
such as exposure to chemicals.
Chemicals with the ability to interfere with the endocrine
system have been found to have significant and often irreversible impacts on the developing
organs during this sensitive period (WHO, 2012).
The human population is on a daily basis exposed to chlorinated paraffins (CPs) through their
diet and through the inhalation of air (Tomasko, 2021; Yuan, 2021). CPs have been measured
in human matrices such as human blood serum at concentrations of 22-35,000 ng/g lipid weight,
but with concentrations differing between countries. The general Chinese population has been
measured to have the highest concentration of CPs in their blood (Tomasko, 2021; Darnerud,
2022). Short-chained chlorinated paraffins (SCCPs) and medium-chained chlorinated paraffins
(MCCPs) have been measured in umbilical cord blood at concentration of 1.33-107 ng/g wet
weight (ww), indicating that fetuses might be exposed to SCCPs and MCCPs during pregnancy
(Qiao, 2018). Furthermore, CPs have been measured in breast milk suggesting that newborns
are exposed to CPs through breastfeeding (Liu, 2020).
CPs are used for many different industrial applications such as flame retardants, plasticizers,
and extreme-pressure additives (Gl¨uge, 2018). They are persistent in the environment and
bioaccumulate in animals and humans where the concentration of CPs has increased over time
(Mourik, 2020; Tomasko, 2021).
CPs consists of polychlorinated n-alkanes with varying chain lengths and chlorination degree
(see Fig. 1). CPs are generally subdivided into three groups based on their chain lengths:
• C10-13: SCCPs
• C14-17: MCCPs
• C≥18: LCCPs
In an industrial context, CPs are characterized based on their chlorination degree rather than
chain length, and therefore CPs are often found as mixtures of either S-, M- and LCCPs (Chen,
1
==PAGE:10==
2022). As CPs are extensively used by manufacturers, more than two million tons of CPs are
produced every year (Darnerud, 2022). Because of their persistency and bioaccumulative and
toxic potentials, SCCPs were categorized as Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs) in Annex A
in the Stockholm Convention of 2017, banning their production and use in Europe and North
America (Gl¨uge, 2018). Nevertheless, SCCPs continues to be manufactured and utilized in
developing countries such as China, Brazil, and India (Mu, 2023). With time, manufacturers
have replaced the use of SCCPs with MCCPs. The increased use of MCCPs may be the reason
why a higher concentration of MCCPs compared to SCCPs has been measured in humans and
environmental matrices since SCCPs were included in the Stockholm convention (Gl¨uge, 2018;
Mourik, 2020).
Figure 1: Example of chemical structures of CPs. Three examples of chemical struc-
tures representing the three subgroups of CPs A) 1,2,9,10-tetrachlorodecane (SCCP) B) 1,2-
dichlorotetradecane (MCCP) C) 1,1,1,3,18,20,20,20-octachloroicosane (LCCP)
Large knowledge gaps related to the endocrine disruptive mechanisms of CPs exist, but litera-
ture suggests that CPs disrupts the transportation of thyroid hormones (Sprengel, 2021), and
that SCCPs induce the production of estradiol in the steroidogenic pathway and exert both
estrogenic and anti-estrogenic activities (Zhang, 2016). Given the potential health risks asso-
ciated with CPs exposure, it is essential to gain novel information on the endocrine disrupting
potential of CPs to prioritize further scientific testing efforts.

1.1
Aims
The aim of this thesis was to examine whether CPs have the potential to interfere with the
androgen signaling pathway, information that can be used for future prioritization of testing
efforts for this group of substances. Further, it was investigated whether specific structural traits
of CPs were driving the endocrine activity. This was achieved by investigating the following
statements:
• CPs can inhibit the androgen receptor
• CPs can interfere with androgen synthesis
• Chain length and chlorination degree of CPs are relevant for the endocrine activity
1.2
Methodologies
The nine CPs selected for this experimental setup represents a variety of SCCPs, MCCPS and
LCCPs. The CPs were tested in vitro for their ability to affect the androgen receptor activity,
according to the OECD Test Guideline no. 458 called ”Androgen Receptor TransActivation
Assay for Detection of Androgenic Agonist and Antagonist Activity of Chemicals using the
stably transfected human AR-EcoScreenTM cell line” and their ability to interfere the androgen
synthesis following the Test Guideline no. 456 called ”H295R Steroidogenesis Assay” (OECD,
2022; OECD, 2020).

2
Background
The endocrine system consists of ductless glands, which can produce and secrete hormones into
the bloodstream, and thereby regulate various functions of the body. Several endocrine glands
are present in the human body, such as the pituitary gland, the adrenal gland, the thyroid gland,
and the gonads (WHO, 2012). The hormones produced by these endocrine glands play key roles
during development and adult life. The focus of this thesis project was the androgen signaling
pathway, how this plays a role in male development, and what happens when an interference
with the pathway occurs. The following chapter is an introduction to the developing male and
the androgen signaling pathway, when going according to plan and when disrupted.
2.1
Male reproductive development
Our biological sex is commonly known to be determined by our chromosomes. If an embryo has
XX chromosomes it develops into a girl and if it has XY sex chromosomes it develops into a boy.
In reality, sex determination and differentiation are much more complicated and are dependent
on more than just our chromosomes. Initially, male and female embryos are indistinguishable,
but will differentiate down two different trajectories after gonadal sex determination (Svingen,
2013). XY gonads differentiate into testes from the expression of the Sry gene (on the Y chro-
mosome), whereas XX gonads differentiate into ovaries (Fig. 2). After testes formation, they
start producing androgens that ultimately control masculinization of the male fetus. The an-
drogen, testosterone, drives the masculinization of the Wolffian duct resulting in the formation
of seminal vesicles and ejaculatory ducts (O’Donnell, 2022). In target tissue, 5α-reductase con-
verts testosterone to the more potent dihydrotestosterone (DHT), which is responsible for the
formation of the external genitalia (O’Donnell, 2022). Lack of androgens allow for feminization
of the female fetus, emphasizing the importance of androgens for the male phenotype, especially
during the masculinization programming window, where the masculinization of the fetus takes
place (Sharpe, 2020). The reproductive development is a tightly regulated process, and hence
a small disruption of, for instance the androgen signaling pathway, can lead to a disorder at
birth or later in life (Svingen, 2013).

2.2
Androgen synthesis and metabolism
The production of testosterone must be sufficient to ensure proper masculinization of the fe-
tus. Fetal Leydig cells (FLCs) found in the interstitial space of the testis are responsible for
producing testosterone (M¨akel¨a, 2019).
During pregnancy, synthesis of androgens happens
through specific steroidogenic pathways that are dominant at different time points. The so-
called ∆4 steroidogenic pathway is responsible for producing androgen during the first semester,
whereafter the ∆5 steroidogenic pathway takes over from the second trimester and onwards
5
==PAGE:14==
(O’Donnell, 2022).
The common precursor for all steroid hormones, including androgens, is cholesterol. In the
Leydig cells, free cholesterol is transported from the outer to the inner mitochondrial mem-
brane, a process facilitated by the steroidogenic acute regulatory protein (StAR)(Scott, 2009).
Cholesterol is converted into pregnenolone through the action of CYP11A, and is subsequently
transported to the smooth endoplasmic reticulum, where the remaining enzymatic reaction
takes place (Fig.
3).
From here, the steroidogenic pathway splits into the ∆4 or the ∆5
steroidogenic pathway. In the ∆5 steroidogenic pathway, pregnenolone is converted to 17β-
hydroxy-pregnenolone and then DHEA, where both reactions are mediated by CYP17. DHEA
is then converted either to androstenediol or androstenedione catalyzed by 17β-HSD or 3β-HSD,
respectively. Finally, androstenedione is converted to testosterone by 17β-HSD and androstene-
diol is converted to testosterone by 3β-HSD. In the ∆4 steroidogenic pathway, pregnenolone
is converted to progesterone by 3β-HSD, which is then converted to 17β-hydroxy-progesterone
and androstenedione by CYP17 and finally testosterone by 17β-HSD (O’Donnell, 2022; Scott,
2009)
6
==PAGE:15==
Figure 3: An overview of ∆4 and ∆5 pathways. Cholesterol is converted to testosterone
by enzymatic reactions carried out by CYP11A, CYP17, 3β-HSD, and 17β-HSD. Testosterone
is then transported through the bloodstream by binding to transporter proteins, whereafter it
reaches target tissues or undergoes renal excretion. The figure is borrowed from (Bech, 2020).
Testosterone enters the bloodstream by passive diffusion and is subsequently transported
throughout the system by binding to specific transporter proteins such as sex hormone binding
globulin (SHBG) and albumin (Fig. 4) (David J Handelsman, 2020). Testosterone is con-
verted to DHT in the target tissues by 5α-reductase (M¨akel¨a, 2019). Besides being converted
into DHT, a small fraction of the testosterone is enzymatically converted to estradiol by aro-
matase, CYP19, in male reproductive tissues as well as in the brain (David J Handelsman,
2020; Schulster, 2016). Most of the synthesized testosterone is metabolized in various parts of
the body such as the liver and kidneys. The inactivation of testosterone primarily happens in
the liver, where testosterone is converted to inactive metabolites by phase I oxidation and phase
II conjugation. Finally, the inactive metabolites are cleared through renal excretion (David J
Handelsman, 2020).

2.3
Androgen receptor signaling
The action of androgens such as testosterone and its more potent metabolite, DHT, in the
target cell is mediated by the androgen receptor (AR). The AR gene is located on the X-
chromosome and is a member of the steroid hormone receptor family, which includes the two
estrogen receptors and the progesterone receptor amongst others. AR is highly expressed in
the male reproductive tissues, but AR is also expressed in many other tissues, although at
lower levels, where it plays a role on several systems such as the cardiovascular, hematopoietic,
and immune systems. The expression of AR is regulated by androgens, but the extent of the
response to the androgens depends on the type of tissue and the type of cell (Sutinen, 2017).
In the cytosol of the cell, androgens bind to the AR inducing a conformational change. The
androgen-AR complex undergoes homodimerization and is very quickly translocated to the
nucleus. In the nucleus the receptor binds to the androgen response element (ARE) (Suti-
nen, 2017). The binding of the receptor to the ARE is guided by pioneer factors, whereafter
the receptor binds to coregulators and mediator complex, thereby inducing or repressing the
expression of target genes (David J Handelsman, 2020). The androgen signaling pathway is
delicate and highly susceptible to disruptions from for instance endocrine disrupting chemicals,
which is covered in the next section.

2.4
Endocrine disruption
Environmental chemicals and their potential to be endocrine disruptors has been of rising
concern as the incidence and prevalence of many endocrine disorders have increased over the
past decades. This includes diseases such as endocrine related cancers, genital malformations at
birth, low semen quality, and early breast development in young girls. The occurrence cannot
be attributed to genetic factors alone since the increase has been relatively rapid, which has
led to the hypothesis that environmental factors, such as endocrine disrupting chemicals, may
contribute (WHO, 2012).
2.5
Endocrine disrupting chemicals
The World Health Organization (WHO) defines an endocrine disruptor as:
“... an exogenous substance or mixture that alters function(s) of the endocrine system and
consequently causes adverse health effects in an intact organism, or its progeny, or (sub) popu-
lations” (WHO, 2012).
Exposure to endocrine disrupting chemicals (EDCs) during fetal life is especially concerning,
as many tissues, including the reproductive tissues, develop during this stage and are largely
hormone dependent. Much work has been done to show that chemicals can in fact affect the
developing fetus by interfering with the endocrine systems.
Mechanism of endocrine disruptors
EDCs classically exert their action through disrupting hormone synthesis, metabolism, trans-
port across membranes or in serum, or their degradation (Luccio-Camelo, 2011). Endocrine dis-
rupting mechanisms can be broadly categorized into the following two groups (Luccio-Camelo,
2011; WHO, 2012):
(i) Receptor mediated disruptors, which act directly on, for example, the androgen receptor
affecting the transcription of target genes.
(ii) Non-receptor mediated disruptors, where the disruptor acts on a specific protein, which
could be involved in the synthesis or metabolism of the hormone, thereby indirectly
modulating, for example, the androgen function.
9
==PAGE:18==
Receptor-mediated activity can be further divided into agonism and antagonism. An agonist
is a molecule that can bind to a receptor instead of its natural ligand, thereby functionally
activating the receptor. An antagonist is a molecule that can bind to a target and inhibit
the binding of a natural ligand or agonist, preventing the activation of the receptor (Grandy,
2016). An example of an anti-androgenic substance is enzalutamide, which is known as second-
generation AR antagonist, used to treat metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer patients
(Kokal, 2020). Enzalutamide affects multiple steps of the androgen signaling pathway such as
the androgen-binding to the AR, the translocation of AR to the nucleus and the binding to
DNA (Rodriguez-Vida, 2015).
2.6
Adverse effects caused by anti-androgenic substances
The term testicular dysgenesis syndrome (TDS) hypothesizes that a spectrum of disorders,
such as hypospadias and cryptorchidism, is a result of a disruption in fetal testis development
(Skakkebaek, 2001). Hypospadias is a condition characterized by an abnormal positioning of the
urethral opening of the penis and cryptorchidism is a condition where one or both testes have not
descended to the scrotum. Both hypospadias and cryptorchidism are congenital conditions and
can be discovered at birth (WHO, 2012). The prevalence of hypospadias and cryptorchidism
has increased over the past decades along with low testosterone levels, poor semen quality and
infertility (Mattiske, 2021). It is believed that TDS is caused by exposure to environmental
chemicals such as EDCs, but can also be caused by genetic factors. The exposure to particularly
the EDCs which affect the estrogen and androgen signaling, is believed to contribute to the
increased prevalence of hypospadias and cryptorchidism (Mattiske, 2021).
2.7
Assessment of male reproductive toxicity
Governments and organizations have created several validated screening and testing systems
including several in vivo and in vitro assays (WHO, 2012). The Organization for Economic
Cooperation and Development (OECD) has established Test Guidelines (TG) that are validated
and, whose purpose is to evaluate the possible effects of chemicals on human health and the
environment.
Anogenital distance (AGD) has been established as a broad biomarker for disrupted androgen
action during fetal life. It is used in rodent in vivo studies to assess early endocrine disruption
10
==PAGE:19==
as well as predicting reproductive disorders later in life, thus AGD has been included in multiple
OECD TGs such as TG. 443, 421/422 and 414 (OECD, 2001; OECD, 2018a; OECD, 2018b;
Schwartz, 2019). AGD is the distance between the anus and external genitalia and is approx-
imately twice the length in males than in females, both in rodents and humans. Disruption
in the androgen action during fetal exposure to EDCs is associated with a shorter male AGD.
Research indicates that a more significant impact on the AGD is likely to be associated with
additional reproductive abnormalities, such as genital malformations (Schwartz, 2019).
Exposure to a group of substances called azoles during fetal life has shown to have an impact
on AGD in rats. Azoles are examples of compounds which are known to inhibit CYP enzymes
in the steroidogenic pathway resulting in reduced androgen synthesis. Some azole fungicides
are also known to antagonize the AR, which interferes with the gene regulation in target cells
(Draskau, 2022).
More specifically, triticonazole and flusilazole are examples known to be
AR antagonist and can disrupt the steroid biosynthesis. Exposing male Sprague Dawley rats
to triticonazole resulted in shorter AGD, whereas flusilazole only resulted an altered steroid
hormone profile (Draskau, 2019).
Nipple retention (NR) is another established biomarker to assess endocrine disruption, where
male pups are observed to have relatively small dark spots in the positions where nipples would
have been located on female rats. Insufficient androgen signaling during the masculinization
program window, permits the development of nipples in male rat offspring (Schwartz, 2021). In
a study conducted by Hass et. al, a mixture of pesticides, epoxiconazole, mancozeb, prochloraz,
tebuconazole and procymidone was given to rats, resulted in increased nipple retention in male
offspring and increased incidents and severity of genitals malformations (Hass, 2012).
The main obstacle of using data from in vivo studies is the translation of effects in animals to
effects in humans, and the use of animals for in vivo studies gives rise to ethical and economic
concerns. Over time, an interest in focusing on non-animal-based test methods has increased,
which includes the development of high throughput screening assays and the use of new ap-
proach methodologies (Parish, 2020; Patisaul, 2018). However, rodents and other animals are
still necessary when testing effects of exposure to EDCs, as more complex outcomes, for now,
cannot be measured in the simpler in vitro cell-based test (Patisaul, 2018).
Cell-based in vitro tests can be useful for providing mechanistic information on the mode of
11
==PAGE:20==
action in certain pathways at a molecular level (Patisaul, 2018). Some examples of OECD TG
for disruption to the androgen signaling pathway is TG no. 458 that assess a chemical’s ability
agonize or antagonize AR receptor activity and TG no. 456, which utilizes cells called the
H295R cells to screen chemicals for their effect on steroidogenesis. The experimental part of
this thesis is based on these two OECD TGs and the principles the guidelines are described in
the following section.
2.8
Methodologies applied in this thesis project
As presented above, the androgen signaling pathway is complex and consists of many different
compartments. The methodologies used in this project address only in part this pathway shown
in Figure 4, namely AR activity and androgen synthesis. Hence no information on the ability
of CPs to affect e.g., transport or elimination of steroid hormones is gained from the assays
used.
2.8.1
Principle of the AR reporter gene assay
The AR reporter gene assay can be used to test the agonistic and the antagonistic effect of
chemicals on the human AR. The assay uses a stably transfected Chinese hamster ovary cell
line called AR EcoScreen.
The cells have three inserted constructs, coding for the human
AR, renilla luciferase and an androgen response element (ARE) together with firefly luciferase.
Human AR and renilla luciferase are produced constitutively. Therefore, the amount of renilla
luciferase present is a measure of the cell viability, whereas the production of firefly luciferase
is induced when AR is activated through binding to the ARE. The amount of firefly luciferase
present is hence a measure AR activity (Bagchi Bhattacharjee, 2014).
Agonists of the human AR activates the AR by binding to AR located in the cytosol (Fig.
5). The AR-ligand undergoes homodimerization and form a receptor-ligand-complex, which
translocate to the nucleus, as described previously. In the nucleus, the receptor-ligand-complex
binds to the ARE, resulting in the transcription of firefly luciferase. Antagonism can take place
in multiple ways, one of which is by the antagonistic binding of the chemical to AR, which
limits the binding of an agonist, resulting in a non-activated AR. Subsequently the AR will not
undergo homodimerization and firefly luciferase is not produced (Bagchi Bhattacharjee, 2014).
12
==PAGE:21==
Figure 5: Principle of the AR reporter gene assay. Agonists binds to the receptor in the
cytosol, followed by homodimerization and translocation to the nucleus. The ligand-receptor-
complex binds to ARE, initiating the transcription of firefly luciferase.
If an antagonist is
present, it will bind to the receptor, which prohibits the binding of the androgen, resulting in
no transcription of firefly luciferase. Renilla luciferase is produced constitutively. By adding the
substrate for firefly luciferase and subsequently for renilla luciferase, a measurable luminescence
signal is produced.
The Dual-Glo luciferase kit obtained from Promega (USA) can be used to measure the AR
activity and the cell viability. The kit consists of two main reagents to measure for luciferase
activity, where one is used to measure the firefly luciferase activity and the other is used
to measure the renilla luciferase activity. The reagents for measuring the amount of firefly

luciferase contains beetle luciferin, a substrate to firefly luciferase, and will cause an enzymatic
reaction when the reagent is added to the well. The enzymatic reaction results in the formation
of oxyluciferin and a detectable signal in the form of emitted light, which can be measured on
a luminometer. The reagent for measuring the renilla activity, contains coelenterate luciferin,
a substrate to renilla luciferin, and reactions such as the firefly reaction produces measurable
light.
2.8.2
The principle of H295R steroidogenesis assay
The H295R steroidogenesis assay is an in vitro model, which investigates substances for their
potential to affect steroidogenesis by measuring the levels of estradiol and testosterone. The
assay utilizes the human H295R adreno-carcinoma cell line, which expresses all enzymes and
transporters needed for steroid hormone synthesis, and produces measurable levels of andro-
gens, progestagens, corticoids, and estrogens. When culturing the cells, steroid hormones are
secreted into the culture medium, where hormones can be measured by e.g., Enzyme-linked
immunosorbent assay (ELISA), and the cells still attached to the bottom of the well can be
used to assess cell viability (OECD, 2022).
Principle of ATP cell viability assay
To assess the cell viability of the H295R cells, CellTiter-Glo® 3D Cell Viability Assay from
Promega (USA) was used. CellTiter-Glo is a bioluminescent ATP assay, and its reagent contains
firefly luciferase, luciferase substrate and a cell lysing agent as some of its components. ATP is
a measure of cell viability, as it is an energy source used and produced by live cells and the ATP
is degraded when the cells die (Riss, 2013). When CellTiter-Glo is added to the cells, the cells
are lysed, and ATP is released into the solution. The firefly luciferase from CellTiter-Glo reacts
with the free ATP because it is dependent on ATP as an energy source to convert luciferin into
oxyluciferin. A detectable luminescence light is emitted when the excited oxyluciferin returns
to ground state, and this light can be measured on a luminometer (Promega, 2023).
Principle of the competitive ELISA method
The concentration of testosterone and estradiol can be measured in media harvested from the
H295R assay by using ELISA. The ELISA kits used in this thesis are based on the principle
of competitive ELISA (Fig. 6). In this experimental setup, our hormone of interest competes
14
==PAGE:23==
with a tracer on the binding to antiserum, which enables the binding of the hormone/tracer to
the antibody on the plate. The addition of Ellman’s reagent to the wells causes an enzymatic
reaction, as the tracer is a substrate to Ellman’s reagent, and yellow color develops in the well.
The intensity of color can be measured on a spectrophotometer at 415 nm. The amount of
antiserum and tracer is constant, whereas the concentration of the hormone of interest varies,
and therefore the intensity of the color in the well is proportional with the amount of tracer
bound to the plate. The amount of hormone is inversely proportional to the intensity of the
color in the well. (Cayman Chemical Company, 2019)

Sample, containing hormone, is added to the well together with antiserum and tracer. During
incubation, the hormone competes with the tracer on the binding to the antiserum, which binds
to the antibody. C) After incubation and subsequent wash, Ellman’s reagent is added to the
well, and colour develops (Cayman Chemical Company, 2019).

AR reporter gene assay
The AR EcoScreen cells were purchased from American Type Culture Collection (USA). The

3.4
ELISA
ELISA was performed according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Samples were diluted 1:16 for
the testosterone kit. Standards and ELISA buffer was prepared and then added to a 96-well
plate pre-coated with mouse anti rabbit IgG or mouse monoclonal anti rabbit IgG as suggested
in the protocol. 50 µL of samples were added in duplicates to the plate. Hereafter 50 µL of
AChE tracer was added to every well except for the blank wells and the wells used to measure
total activity (TA). 50 µL of ELISA antiserum was added to each well except blank, TA and the
wells used to measure non-specific binding and then the plate incubated for 2 hours on a plate
shaker. Wash buffer was prepared, and the wells were washed 5 times with the wash buffer.
Ellman’s reagent was prepared and 200 µL of Ellman’s reagent was added to every well and
5 µL of AChE tracer was added to the TA well. Depended on the assay, the plate incubated
for 60-90 minutes on a plate shaker followed by measurements of the absorbance on EnSpire
(PerkinElmer, USA) at 415 nm.

3.5
Data processing
All data was normalized to the vehicle controls from the same plate and within the indepen-
dent experiment. Subsequently, data was pooled as means of means in the cases where more
independent experiments were performed (AR reporter gene activity, AR cell viability, H295R
cell viability).
The initial data analysis included evaluation of compromised cell viability. A decrease in cell
viability of more than 20% was considered to be a cytotoxic concentration. These concentrations
were excluded from the data set before further statistical analysis of effects on receptor activity
or hormone levels.
The receptor activity and hormone data were evaluated for normal distribution by D’agostino-
Pearson test and variance homogenicity by Bartlett’s test. In cases where criteria for a para-
metric test were met, a one-way ANOVA with a post hoc Dunnet’s multiple comparisons test
was performed, whereas a non-parametric test was performed in other cases, Kruskal Wallis
post hoc test with Dunn’s multiple comparisons test.
The positive controls in the AR reporter gene assay were modelled by a four-parameter curve
fit to derive effective concentration (EC) and Inhibitory concentration (IC) values.
All graphs and statistics were performed in GraphPad Prism.

5
Discussion
5.1
AR activity of CPs
Nine different CPs were screened for their potential to interfere with the AR activity in vitro
using the AR reporter gene assay. The screening showed that CP2, a SCCPs, was able to
antagonize the AR and thereby decrease AR activity. Of the remaining substances some led
to decreasing trends or significant findings, but were deemed inactive, as they were an indirect
effect of compromised cell viability or were not concentration dependent. The results from the
AR reporter gene assay were consistent, and with low standard deviations. The responsiveness
of the AR reporter assay was verified through exposure to positive controls, resulting in signif-
icant changes in AR activity. Subsequently, IC50 and EC50 values were determined to be 0.11
nM and 161.5 nM, respectively, which are comparable to values from previous in-house data
(data not shown). One of the aims for this thesis was to screen CPs for their ability to inhibit
the androgen receptor, and it was observed that only CP2 antagonized the AR with a LOEC
of 6.25 µM. This is to the best of my knowledge the first report of both effect and lack thereof
on AR activity for this substance group.
Another aim of this thesis was to investigate whether chain length or chlorination degree are
contributing factors to the observed effects. As only a SCCP showed to induced an effect on
the AR activity, and no effects were observed for MCCPs and LCCPs, data indicates that the
chain length is a determining factor. The chlorination degree varies within the CPs groups, but
as the different chlorination degrees, especially for MCCPs and LCCPs, did not lead to affect
the AR activity, the data suggests that the chlorination degree is not a determining factor.
5.2
H295R data
It is paramount to emphasize that the H295R hormone data is preliminary. Most experiments
are banked, but there is an ongoing process related to the ELISA measurements and hence firm
conclusions cannot be drawn yet. Large CV values were a deciding factor for data omission
in some cases. For the competitive ELISA experiments, every sample were made in technical
duplicates, a minimum that was suggested by the manufacturers, but technical triplicates were
also suggested, and could have reduced the standard deviations and may be a way forward. The
cells worked as expected, as the responses for the positive controls, prochloraz and forskolin
29
==PAGE:38==
gave responses within the limits given in the OECD guideline test guideline no. 456 (data not
shown).
Based on the data collected so far, these suggest that most of the CPs tested did not affect
the testosterone levels.
A significant change in testosterone levels were measured for CP8,
but this is likely to be due to the cytotoxicity, which was significant at 100 µM, but already
show a decreasing trend at 50 µM, where the decreased testosterone is observed.
Most of
the CPs did not affect estradiol levels in the H295R cells, except for CP2, which was able
to induce the estradiol levels in a concentration dependent manner, leading to a significant
effect at 100 µM. An increasing trend was also observed for CP3, which was not significant.
An increase in estradiol levels have previously been reported for three SCCPs in the H295R
steroidogenesis assay. SCCPs were characterized by chain length of 10-11, and chlorination
degree of approximately 40-66%, which is comparable to CP2 with chain length of 10-11 and
chlorination degree of 40-60% (Zhang, 2016). To the best of my knowledge this is the first time
that MCCPs and LCCPs have been tested in the H295R steroidogenesis assay.
Based on this preliminary data, none of the tested CPs were able to affect the androgen syn-
thesis, which was one of the aims to be investigated in this thesis. Only CP2 was able to induce
the production of estradiol significantly. But as the hormonal levels for CP2 were only tested
twice by ELISA, at least one additional experiment must be carried out to give a conclusion
based on statistical analysis. Collectively, the data suggests that the chain length may be a
contributing factor for the observed changes, as CP2 showed effects, whereas neither MCCP
nor LCCP did.
To broaden the perspective of the H295R assay and CPs ability to interfere the steroidogenic
pathway, the production and activity of other hormones of the steroidogenic pathway could
be investigated, and the lack of effect in the H295R steroidogenesis assay does not mean that
no effects can be expected on androgen levels, necessarily. According to the OECD TG no.
456, the H295R assay cannot be used to measure the levels of the very potent androgen, DHT,
as the 5α-reductase is not expressed in the H295R cell line, and this enzyme is needed for
the conversion of testosterone to DHT (OECD, 2022). Likewise, the H295R steroidogenesis
assay can also not be used to test the synthesis of androgens through the backdoor pathway,
an alternative pathway for DHT formation, as 5α-reductase is also required for this pathway
30
==PAGE:39==
(Miller, 2019).
5.3
Biological relevance of test concentrations
The fact that CPs have been detected in human matrices is in itself proof for human absorption
of CPs. More specifically, SCCPs, MCCPs and LCCPs have been measured in whole blood
samples from China where SCCPs was in a range of 375-35,000 ng/g lipid weight (Darnerud,
2022). The LOEC for CP2 was 6.25 µM corresponding to a concentration of 328,058.6 ng/g lipid
weight, which is more than 10 times higher than the highest concentration measured in human
blood. There are multiple factors that still needs to be accounted for before discarding these re-
sults as irrelevant. There may be marked differences between the nominal concentrations added
in vitro and the biologically effective concentration at the target site, as compounds distribute
differently between the multiple compartments of the in vitro assay. Hence, substances may
exhibit differential distribution between e.g., the medium, plastics and extracellular matrices
of the cells, resulting in a reduced concentration at the actual target (Proen¸ca, 2021).
The CPs analyzed in this thesis were exclusively tested as single compounds, apart from CP8
and CP9 which are inherently mixtures. It is probable that both humans and animals are
exposed to multiple different compounds concurrently, some of which could be EDCs (Evans,
2022). Previous studies have demonstrated that humans are exposed to CPs, hence it can be
assumed that humans are likely exposed to CPs along with other various substances, potentially
EDCs, at the same time. The mixing effect of compounds can, depending on the activities of the
individual compound, be additive, subtractive, or potentially synergistic. Therefore, evaluating
the potential risks posed to human health due to exposure to EDCs and mixtures is an essential
concern, and should be taken into consideration to ensure human health (Evans, 2022).
5.4
Assessment of other endocrine endpoints
The androgen signaling pathway includes multiple processes such as the synthesis of androgens,
regulation, secretion, metabolism, transport, action, and elimination, which are all important
for male reproductive health, and these processes are all susceptible to disruption by EDCs.
This thesis investigated nine CPs’ potential to inhibit the AR activity and interfere the androgen
synthesis, which is only a few of the mentioned processes. CPs’ ability to interfere with any of
the other processes in the androgen signaling pathway still needs to be investigated in order to
31
==PAGE:40==
conclude on the disruptive potential of CPs from the in vitro perspective.
Other perspectives than the androgen signaling pathway should also be investigated to get the
full picture of the endocrine disruptive potential of CPs, including disruption of the thyroid
and estrogenic signaling pathway. In fact, there are some indications that CPs may affect the
thyroid signaling pathway (Gong, 2018; Sprengel, 2021). The estrogen signaling pathway is
especially interesting as the results from the H295R assay showed to induce the production
of estradiol when the cells are exposed to CP2. Besides showing that three SCCPs induced
the synthesis of estradiol in H295R assay, Zhang et. al used a reporter gene assay mediated
by the estrogen receptor α, which showed that the SCCPs induced estrogenic activities, but
also demonstrated anti-estrogenic activities (Zhang, 2016). In fact, these estrogenic modalities
may also play a role in male reproductive development, since estrogens do play a role in the
maturation of the male reproductive system and the development of some areas of the brain
(Guercio, 2020; Rochira, 2023).
5.5
In vivo vs. in vitro outcomes
The general purpose of using the AR reporter gene assay and the H295R steroidogenesis assay is
to gain mechanistic knowledge on the endocrine mode of action of CPs in vitro. In this regard,
it is important to note that the findings of the in vitro assays cannot be directly generalized to
indicate that a similar result would be observed in an in vivo situation in humans and animals.
This is primarily due to the lack of the assays’ ability to account for the complex physiology
in living organisms, such as toxicokinetic parameters, absorption, distribution, metabolism,
and excretion. These are all mechanisms that might affect the biochemistry at the target site,
potentially altering the CPs’ impact in a more complex, live system, compared to the relatively
simplified conditions of in vitro experiments. Secondly, the in vitro assays operate with a fixed
exposure duration, where the corresponding exposure observed in an in vivo environment would
likely be continuous or intermittent over varying periods. This may be particularly important,
since the subtle effects during fetal development may only manifest much later in adulthood.
To predict in vivo outcomes, a comprehensive understanding of the mechanistic properties of
chemicals with endocrine disrupting potential is necessary both for the general understanding
but also in cases of planning experimental setups in vivo, where the use of animals should be
32
==PAGE:41==
reduced.
The fact that the use of SCCPs in the industry is regulated because of their ED potential is
in accordance with the results obtained in this thesis project, as only a SCCP was observed to
disrupt endocrine mechanisms. More knowledge the mechanisms of MCCPs and LCCPs is still
to be discovered. A study with liver microsomes conducted by He et. al, indicates that MCCPs
and LCCPs are transformed in vivo to SCCPs (He, 2021). It is likely that the metabolism
of MCCPs and LCCPs have not taken place in either of the two in vitro assays used in this
thesis project, because of their minimal metabolic activity (Ooka, 2020). It can therefore not
be excluded that MCCPs and LCCPs would have caused effects in the given assays through
their metabolites. If MCCPs and LCCPs are in fact converted to SCCPs, then it would be
expected that they are AR antagonists, and that they increase the production of estradiol in
systems with active metabolism.

6
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to examine whether CPs have the potential to interfere with the
androgen signaling pathway through either antagonizing the AR or inhibiting the synthesis of
androgens. Further, an aim was to characterize whether any in vitro activity was dependent on
specific structural traits of CPs. Nine different CPs were screened for their potential to disrupt
the androgen receptor activity and their ability to interfere with the androgen synthesis.
The results obtained in this thesis project showed that CP2 had the ability to antagonize the AR
activity, and the ability to increase the synthesis of estradiol through the steroidogenic pathway.
The fact that neither MCCPs nor LCCPs were able to agonize or antagonize the AR activity
or interfere with the production of testosterone or estradiol, although SCCPs were partly able
to do this, suggests that the chain length of CPs is a structural trait which influences the AR
activity and synthesis of steroidogenic hormones. CP1 and CP2 have the same chain length,
but different chlorination degrees of 20.1% and 50.6% respectively. As CP1 did not cause any
effects, the chlorination degree does not seem to be a structural trait of determining factor.
The MCCPs and LCCPs tested, also had different chlorination degrees within the groups, but
again, no effects were observed, supporting the assumption that the chlorination degree is not
a key factor. Based on these in vitro data it is suggested that the exposure to CP2 during fetal
stages could contribute to the development of male reproductive disorders, but this should be
further clarified through in vivo studies. It is still to be investigated whether these CPs may
affect other endocrine in vitro endpoints, which could contribute to any reproductive toxicity.
The knowledge obtained in this thesis project will together with other in vitro based assays
contribute to the prioritization of testing efforts of this group of substances.
7
Future perspectives
The use of SCCPs is being substituted with the use of MCCPs and LCCPs and hence humans
are increasingly exposed to these compounds. Literature and studies on MCCPs and LCCPs
are scarce compared to SCCPs, which is more explored due to its status as POPs. It is therefore
more relevant than ever to investigate MCCPs, LCCPs as well as SCCPs for their potential to
disrupt the endocrine system, as well as other toxicological effects.
34
==PAGE:43==
This thesis project provided knowledge on two endocrine endpoints, but other modalities could
be interesting to investigate to gain a fuller picture of CPs’ endocrine mode of action, especially
as CP2 showed to affect both endpoints tested. The combined knowledge gained could be used
for future prioritization of testing efforts such as experiments with animals, which will show
whether the endocrine disrupting potential shown in vitro assays will manifest in the animals
as male reproductive disorders or not.
The need for animal experiments is still required as it is difficult to extrapolate to in vivo
outcomes from in vitro data. The need for animal experiments was shown by Draskau et. al,
who tested triticonazole and flusilazole in vitro and in vivo. Both azoles showed to be strong in
vitro AR antagonist and disrupted steroid biosynthesis, but only triticonazole induced a shorter
AGD in vivo (Draskau, 2019).
More complex in vitro assays are needed to fill the knowledge gaps to allow for extrapolation,
which the existing assays currently do not provide. The combined knowledge obtained from
multiple assays can enhance predictive approaches, such as quantitative in vitro to in vivo
extrapolation methods. With further advancements, it is hoped that these predictions will
become highly reliable, potentially reducing the necessity of animal testing in the future (Fabian,
2019; Scholze, 2020).
