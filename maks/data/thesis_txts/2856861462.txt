Abstract 
Solar panels are vital to the green energy transition but are suspected of being a source of 
environmental PFAS contamination. This is especially concerning, since solar panel parks are often 
constructed in groundwater protected areas and near surface water. This project aims to investigate 
the potential PFAS release from the glass frontsheet of bifacial solar panels due to use phase 
weathering as well as the resulting worst-case environmental concentrations in relation to the 
environmental quality standards (EQSs) of 100 ng/L for PFAS 22 in groundwater and drinking 
water and 4.4 ng PFOA-eq./L for PFAS 24 in surface water. Potential PFAS release is investigated 
with a wipe method and a simulated rainwater method, the latter of which is assumed closer to 
natural in situ release conditions. Thus, the simulated rainwater method is used to quantify the 
comparability of the wipe method to actual PFAS release. The wipe test consists of wiping an area 
on a solar panel frontsheet with methanol a total of 60 times. A total potential PFAS release of 
54±18 ng/m2 PFHxA and 9.0±3.7 ng/m2 PFPeA is found. The simulated rainwater test consists of 
circulating water across the frontsheet of solar panels and measuring the resulting PFAS 
concentration in the water. The test is conducted on two different solar panel types with an average 
total potential PFAS 22 release of 0.28 ng/yr/m2 or PFAS 24 release of 0.20 ng PFOA-eq./yr/m2 
from 10 contributing substances. The most prevalent PFAS found in the simulated rainwater tests 
were PFHxA and PFOA. By comparing the findings of the wipe test and simulated rainwater test, it 
is estimated that the total release in the wipe test equates to 46 and 210 years of rainfall for the two 
panel types, respectively. 
The potential worst-case PFAS contamination of abstracted groundwater and surface water streams 
is investigated with contaminant mass discharge calculations for a case solar park. For groundwater 
a potential worst-case concentration of 45 ng/L (45 % of the 100 ng/L EQS) is determined. The 
potential worst-case concentration in nearby surface streams is estimated at 0.0058 – 3.4 ng PFOA-
eq./L (0.13 – 76 % of the 4.4 ng PFOA-eq./L EQS) depending on steam size. A resulting 
environmental hazard quotient is estimated at ≤ 0.0007. Furthermore, the potential PFAS release 
from the panels in a solar park is found to be 11 - 36 times lower than the PFAS contribution from 
rainfall on the park area for worst-case release conditions. Thus, the potential use phase PFAS 
release from the frontsheet of bifacial solar panels is found to not pose an environmental risk.

1. Background and Aim 
The green energy transition is essential to mitigate use of fossil fuel and reduce global warming. 
Therefore, renewable energy technologies are a thriving area of innovation and commercial 
interests. In 2022, 18.2 % of EU renewable energy consumption and 7.6 % of EU total energy 
consumption came from solar energy, and the trend is steadily increasing(Brown & Jones, 2024; 
Widuto & Pradier, 2022). However, solar technologies rely on complex chemistry and are suspected 
of being a potential source of PFAS pollution. PFAS are a large group of synthetic substances, 
which are of environmental concern due to their high persistency, mobility, and toxicity 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2023). Release of PFAS from solar panels into the environment might result in 
leaching into the groundwater. This hazard is especially relevant in Denmark, where groundwater is 
the main source of drinking water, and the limited available land area makes solar parks a 
potentially attractive land-use for groundwater protected zones, where e.g. agriculture is prohibited 
(dom.dk ApS, 2023).  
In Denmark, environmental PFAS concentrations are regulated by environmental quality standards 
(EQSs). For groundwater and drinking water, the EQS is defined for a sum of 22 PFAS 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2021, 2023, 2024). The PFAS 22 EQS in groundwater is 100 ng/L (Miljøstyrelsen, 
2021). For inland surface waters, an EQS of 4.4 ng PFOA-eq./L for a sum of 24 PFAS has been 
proposed (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023). PFAS have a wide range of industrially useful properties such as 
being efficient conductors of heat and electricity, decreasing aqueous surface tension, being non-
flammable, and being highly stable across a wide temperature range (Glüge et al., 2020). In solar 
panels, PFAS might be incorporated for thermal stability of the photovoltaic cells, electrical 
insulation, protection of the modules against moisture and UV degradation, or in the lubricants and 
cleaning agents used in the panel production (Krystek, 2023; OECD, 2022).  
As of writing, few studies have been conducted investigating the release of PFAS from solar panels. 
The aim of this project is to address the knowledge gap concerning potential release of PFAS from 
solar panels in the use phase. More specifically, this project aims to: 
- 
quantify release of PFAS from the glass frontsheet of solar panels using a wipe method and 
a simulated rainwater method. 
- 
estimate the comparability of the wipe method with in situ use phase release by comparing 
release from the wipe method and the simulated rainwater method.

- 
evaluate the potential groundwater and surface water contamination with PFAS released 
from a solar park and compare with EQS. 
To meet these aims, the project will employ laboratory experiments to measure the potential release 
of 20 PFAS from a bifacial solar panel using both a wipe test method and a simulated rainwater test 
method.  A wipe test is a relatively fast and cheap method of assessing the total potential release of 
an organic contaminant from a smooth surface based on ASTM D6661-17 (ASTM, 2017) and 
Poothong et al. (2019). A simulated rainwater test is a more extensive and time-consuming test 
aimed at quantifying PFAS release facilitated by water. 
The project will include measurements and a literature study of environmental PFAS background

Finally, the project will incorporate theoretical worst-case contaminated mass discharge (CMD)

2. Introduction 
2.1. 
What are PFAS? 
2.1.1. 
Chemical Definition of PFAS 
Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances (PFAS) are a vast and diverse group of synthetic chemicals, 
which are characterized by containing fluorinated carbon atoms. The exact definition of PFAS has 
long been a topic of debate, however in 2021 OECD published a comprehensive report on PFAS 
terminology presenting the following definition: “PFAS are defined as fluorinated substances that 
contain at least one fully fluorinated methyl or methylene carbon atom (without any H/Cl/Br/I atom 
attached to it), i.e. with a few noted exceptions, any chemical with at least a perfluorinated methyl 
group (–CF3) or a perfluorinated methylene group (–CF2–) is a PFAS” (OECD, 2021). 
This definition allows for a multitude of PFAS chemical structures resulting in a wide variety of 
physical and chemical properties within the PFAS group. PFAS can be either polymers or non-
polymers, per- or polyfluorinated, aliphatic or aromatic, saturated or unsaturated, long- or short-
chained, and containing diverse functional groups. An overview of some relevant subgroups and 
substances within the PFAS chemical group is shown in Figure 1.

to this report included (Brennan et al., 2021; OECD, 2021).

Prior to the 2021 OECD report on PFAS terminology, PFAS were defined by an article by Buck et 
al. (2011), which also provides the following definitions of polymeric and non-polymeric 
substances, and per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances as subclasses of nonpolymeric PFAS:  
• Polymeric PFAS have very long alkyl chains with up to 180,000 C-atoms. 
• Non-polymeric PFAS have shorter alkyl chains with less than 14 C-atoms. 
• Perfluoroalkyl PFAS are defined as “aliphatic substances for which all of the H atoms 
attached to C atoms in the nonfluorinated substance from which they are notionally derived 
have been replaced by F atoms, except those H atoms whose substitution would modify the 
nature of any functional groups present”.  
• Polyfluoroalkyl PFAS are defined as “aliphatic substances for which all H atoms attached to 
at least one but not all C atoms have been replaced by F atoms” (Buck et al., 2011). 
This report will focus on a subgroup of PFAS consisting of perfluoroalkyl acids (PFAAs) with a 
single, linear alkyl group of at least three fully fluorinated carbon atoms. This is the PFAS group 
most commonly detected in the environment, most studied, and the group for which regulation has 
traditionally been designed (Brunn et al., 2023; ITRC, 2023). PFAAs are generally considered long-
chained, if the C-F-chain contains six or more fluorinated carbon atoms. Reversely, short-chained 
PFAS have a C-F-chain with five or less fluorinated carbon atoms (Baun et al., 2017; OECD, 2022). 
The two subgroups of PFAAs, which are the primary focus of the Danish EQSs and therefore most 
relevant to this project, are Perfluorinated carboxylic acids (PFCAs) and Perfluorosulfonic acids 
(PFSAs) (see Figure 1). 
2.1.2. 
Properties and Environmental Fate of PFAS 
The environmental distribution and fate of PFAS is governed by the physical-chemical properties of 
the substances as well as the local environmental conditions such as temperature, pH, soil carbon 
content etc. Therefore, the environmental fate is difficult to describe in general terms, as it depends 
on the specific PFAS and site conditions in questions. Since PFAS is a group over thousands of 
chemicals with vastly different structures, the physical and chemical properties of PFAS are 
likewise varying (Glüge et al., 2020; OECD, 2021). However, one characteristic that all PFAS share 
is the C–F bond. The C–F bond has an average bond enthalpy of about 485 kJ/mol at 25 ◦C, making 
it one of the strongest bonds in organic chemistry (owl.it, 2024; Smith et al., 2016). The strength of 
the C–F bond gives PFAS a uniquely stable chemical structure, which has desirable industrial 
applications (section 2.4), but also causes high persistency in the environment (section 2.3) (Brunn 
==PAGE:21==
DTU                                                                                                                           Oline Mannstaedt 
10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091 
 
Page 5 of 82 
 
et al., 2023; Glüge et al., 2020). In contrast to many other environmental contaminants, such as oils 
and chlorinated solvents, PFAS do not form free phases, but instead typically dissociate fully in the 
water phase (VMR, 2022). At high concentrations, PFAS can form micelles, which are layers or 
droplets of PFAS oriented such that the hydrophobic tails are away from the water phase (VMR, 
2022). 
PFAAs have a ‘head and tail’-structure consisting of a hydrophilic functional group with a 
perfluoroalkyl tail, which is both hydrophobic and lipophobic (Gaines, 2023; Glüge et al., 2020; 
ITRC, 2023). Due to this structure, PFAAs accumulate at the interphase between water and other 
media such as soil or air. Therefore, PFAAs can often be found in sea foam, aerosols, rain drops, 
and the capillary soil zone (WSP, 2023). This property likewise makes PFAAs good surfactants.  
PFAAs are ionic and have a negative charge in their dissociated form, which is the most common 
form at typical environmental pH values (pH 5 – 9) (ITRC, 2023). Due to their anionic charge, ionic 
PFAS are less volatile and more water soluble than neutral PFAS. Soil particles are also typically 
negatively charged. Therefore, PFAAs are generally more hydrophilic, less prone to binding to soil, 
and thus more mobile than suggested by properties determined for the non-dissociated compounds 
(Table 1) (Baun et al., 2017; WSP, 2023). In the atmosphere, ionic PFAS are primarily bound to 
particles such as aerosols or raindrops (Bossi, 2024). In the Danish drinking water regulation, the 
environmental quality standard for PFAS has been defined for a subgroup of PFAAs and PFAA 
precursors consisting of 22 substances referred to as the PFAS 22 (see section 2.3.1). 
An overview of the property intervals of the PFAS 22 are shown in Table 1. The properties for the 
individual 22 PFAS can be found in Table 22 in Appendix A (section 8). The values in Table 1 are 
not only approximations due to being intervals over a group of substances with vastly different 
properties, but also because the properties of PFAS are notoriously difficult to measure, which will 
be described further below (see also section 5.1). The intervals in the table cover values found 
through different experimental and theoretical methods from an array of literature collected by WSP 
(2023) and VMR (2022). Generally, PFAS with longer C-F chains are more hydrophobic, have 
lower water solubility, have lower vapour pressure, higher Kow, higher Koc, and higher Kd. Long-
chained PFAS are therefore less mobile in the water phase, more prone to adsorption to organic 
carbon e.g. in the soil, and more bioaccumulative in biota than short-chained PFAS (Table 2) 
(Brunn et al., 2023; VMR, 2022; WSP, 2023). Furthermore, PFSAs sorb more strongly than PFCAs 
and linear PFAS sorb more strongly than branched PFAS (ITRC, 2023).

Kow is the octanol/water partitioning coefficient, which describes the lipophilicity and 
hydrophilicity of the substance (Baun et al., 2017).  Chemicals with log Kow > 4 are considered 
potentially bioaccumulative (Baun et al., 2017; European Commission, 2011), which is the case for 
18 out of the 22 PFAS (WSP, 2023). However, this classification is misleading, as will be described 
below. Kd is the soil/water partitioning coefficient, which describes the proclivity of the substance 
to bind to soil particles and depends on the fraction of organic carbon, foc, in the specific soil. For 
instance, clay has a much larger content of organic carbon than sand (see Table 1). Koc is the organic 
carbon/water partitioning coefficient, which is related to both Kow and Kd and can be used to 
describe the mobility of the substance in the soil phase. A substance with a large Koc is less mobile 
in the soil. Koc < 50 corresponds to a very large mobility. Kaw is the air/water partitioning 
coefficient, which describes the volatility of the substance. pKa is the acid dissociation constant, 
which specifies the pH value where exactly half of a given amount of the substance is dissociated. 
The pKa of the 22 PFAS vary but is below 7 for the entire interval. Therefor all 22 PFAS will be 
mostly in their dissociated forms at neutral pH. The Henry’s Law constant, KH, describes the 
relationship between the amount of substance in the air and the water phase, which reflects 
volatility. A substance with KH > 10-5 is considered as having a significant rate of volatilization 
(Baun et al., 2017). Table 1 summarises the environmental substance behaviours, which can be 
expected based on the given properties according to conventional substance behaviour classification 
(Baun et al., 2017; VMR, 2022).  
However, due to the unique structural properties PFAS, Kow is very difficult to determine 
experimentally, which in turn increases the uncertainty of Koc, and other properties estimated based 
on Kow (see also section 5.1) (VMR, 2022). Thus, the actual mobility of PFAS in a soil medium is 
not governed by the above-described properties, as PFAS does not so much sorb to the soil particles 
as accumulate at the interfaces between soil, air and water. Similarly, since PFAS bind to protein, 
not lipids, the traditional means of determining bioaccumulation from Kow also does not apply to 
PFAS. Especially short-chained PFAS are often more mobile and more bioaccumulative than 
indicated by Kow and its derived properties. Thus, the traditional equations for calculation of 
transport in the environment, such as Darcy’s and Fick’s Laws, also do not quite apply to PFAS. In 
Table 1 there is a clear difference in the mobility-intervals indicated by Kow and Koc versus Kd, with 
Kow and Koc predicting immobile to high mobility substance behaviour, while Kd predicts medium 
to very high mobility. Since the Kow and Koc values presented in the table are estimated from

DTU                                                                                                                           Oline Mannstaedt 
10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091 
 
Page 7 of 82 
 
theoretical calculations not applicable to PFAS, while Kd has been determined experimentally, the 
higher mobility predicted by Kd is the more accurate property in this case. 
PFAS in the water phase or bound to particles can be taken up by plants and biota and most PFAS 
bioaccumulate through the food web. Short chained PFAS are generally more prone to 
bioconcentrate in plants, whereas long-chained PFAS are more bioaccumulating in animals. 
Furthermore, PFCAs are more likely to bioaccumulate in plants than PFSAs. In general, discharge 
from organisms is slower for PFSAs than for PFCAs, for single-chained PFAS than for branched 
PFAS, and for long-chained PFAS than for short-chained PFAS (Table 2). 
Few PFAS have been thoroughly tested for ecotoxicity. In general, the acute toxicity of PFAS is 
relatively low, however PFAS have been found to cause a wide range of long-term toxic effects 
(VMR, 2022). Some long-chained PFAAs, including PFOA and PFOS, are very bioaccumulative in 
biota due to their ability to bind to proteins (Brunn et al., 2023). As these two substances, like many 
PFAS, are persistent and toxic, the bioaccumulative property of PFOA and PFOS designates them 
as PBT in accordance with ECHAs REACH guidelines (Brunn et al., 2023; European Council, 
2006). Furthermore, some PFAS qualify as CMR (Carcinogenic, mutagenic or reprotoxic), and 
these substances are therefore classified as very toxic and hazardous even if they are not PBT 
(OSHA, 2022). In humans, PFAS are suspected of affecting the immune system by inhibiting 
formation of antibodies, promoting asthma and ADHD, increasing cholesterol in the blood, 
heightening the risk of miscarriage and cardiovascular issues, harming the endocrine system 
etc.(Brunn et al., 2023; VMR, 2022). For humans, uptake of PFAS from food, e.g. fish, is a major 
potential exposure source (WSP, 2023). As most PFAS are either PBT or CMR (or both), they 
generally warrant environmental concern and are restricted by regulation cf. REACH Article 57 
(European Council, 2006).

VMR (2022) has modelled the phase distribution of selected PFAS in an unsaturated aquifer using 
the JAGG-model, which takes Koc and foc as inputs. Thus, the model should be considered rather 
uncertain, since it depends on PFAS behaving in accordance with conventional property 
classification. Still, the model can be used to give an indication of likely substance distribution. 
VMR (2022) found significant differences in distribution between substances, but also a general 
trend that a higher fraction of PFAS occurs in the soil phase at higher organic carbon content (foc of 
2.0 % vs 0.1 %). Furthermore, most PFAS was found to only occur in the water and soil phases, not 
in the air phase. Moreover, the model showed that the fraction of PFAS in the water phase decreases 
with increasing molecular size. Thus, retardation increases with increased organic carbon content of 
the soil and increased length of the fluorinated carbon-chain of the PFAS. 
As described above, PFAS are very persistent. In fact, fluorinated carbon-chains are recalcitrant to 
degradation in soil and groundwater under both aerobic and anaerobic conditions (ITRC, 2023; 
VMR, 2022). However, transformation and degradation of precursor PFAS to daughter compounds 
through reactions involving the functional groups can still take place through both abiotic oxidation, 
hydrolysis, photolysis, and biotic transformation. Yet, the end-products of such transformations are 
still PFAS, only smaller substances. Actual degradation of PFAS to non-PFAS components requires 
very high temperatures and environmental PFAS degradation is therefore negligible (Gaines, 2023; 
ITRC, 2023; VMR, 2022). Of the PFAS 22, all except 6:2 FTS and PFOSA are end-products. 
PFOSA is a precursor to PFOS, while 6:2 FTS is a possible precursor to PFPeA and PFHxA. When 
transforming to end-products, PFAS generally become more persistent, hydrophilic, and mobile 
(VMR, 2022; WSP, 2023).  
It is important to note that due to the high persistency of PFAS, even compounds with low mobility 
will most likely eventually leach from surface contaminations to the groundwater (VMR, 2022). 
However, the velocity of this leaching and the path of the contamination plume through the soil 
layers depend on the hydrogeology of the area as well as the properties of the PFAS contaminants in 
question and the climatic conditions of the area, especially the precipitation. When a PFAS 
contamination leaches to the groundwater, it must pass through both the unsaturated and the 
saturated zone. Since PFAS amass at water-air interphases, the transport of the contamination 
through the unsaturated zone of the underground takes longer for geologies with higher porosity and 
lower saturation resulting in a larger air-water interfacial area (Morsing et al., 2024).

2.2. 
Chemical Safety Assessment Procedure 
Assessing the potential environmental hazard of PFAS contamination is done with a Chemical 
Safety Assessment (CFA) as defined by ECHA (2011). The safety assessment consists of four steps: 
1. Information gathering 
2. Hazard assessment 
3. Exposure assessment 
4. Risk characterization 
Information gathering entails investigating the inherent properties of the substance, which have the 
capacity to potentially cause adverse environmental effects as well as identifying potential 
information gabs. Such properties include solubility, partitioning, degradation, mobility, and 
toxicity. The PFAS properties presented in section 2.1.2 represent the information gathering step in 
this project. 
A hazard assessment builds on the property information collected in the previous step to classify the 
substance in accordance with CLP Regulation (Classification, Labelling and Packaging) (ECHA, 
2011, 2019). The CLP Regulation aim to determine the intrinsic hazard of a substance and 
consequently how it should be labelled and packaged for safe distribution and use. Furthermore, the 
hazard assessment evaluates the relationship between the exposure dose of the substance and the 
response of an organism. Toxicity classification is based on ecotoxicological tests for aquatic 
species from three trophic levels: algae, daphnia and fish (Baun et al., 2017), as is the Predicted No-
Effect Concentration (PNEC), which is also determined as part of the hazard assessment. PNEC is 
an expression of the environmental toxicity of a substance. A high PNEC means that the 
environment can accommodate high concentrations without experiencing toxic effects. PNEC is 
determined as the lowest available LC50, EC50 or NOEC from a standardized ecotoxicity test and 
divided by an assessment factor, which describes the strength of the data foundation of the PNEC 
estimation, or PNEC can be determined by applying a species sensitivity distribution (Baun et al., 
2017; European Commission, 2018). Many PFAS have been found to exhibit toxic effect to aquatic 
biota as well as to humans (section 2.1.2). A review of ecotoxicological data for PFAS from 91 
peer-reviewed studies have found that PFSAs have PNEC for freshwater in the range 0.4 − 8.9 
μg/L, while the freshwater PNEC for PFCAs are in the range 4.8 − 2000 μg/L (Wang et al., 2024). 
The study also found increased sensitivity of freshwater species to PFCAs with carbon chain length 
of 8 or more. Thus, PNEC appears to decrease with increasing substance chain length. Finally, the 
==PAGE:27==

hazard assessment also includes an evaluation of whether the substance qualifies as PBT or vPvB 
(ECHA, 2011). 
As described in section 2.1.2, PFAS are often PBT, however the PBT classification scheme does not 
quite fit with the specific concerns associated with PFAS. Since PFAS are especially problematic 
due to their high persistency and mobility, a classification based on persistency and mobility would 
be more appropriate. In fact, in 2022 the European Commission published new regulation amending 
the hazard assessment of the CLP Regulation (European Commission, 2022). This amendment 
includes the new hazard classes of PMT (Persistent, Mobile, and Toxic) and vPvM (very Persistent 
and very Mobile), which will apply for all substances from November 2026 and all mixtures from 
May 2028. The persistency criteria are based on degradation half-lives (Table 3). A substance is 
classified as mobile, if the lowest log Koc < 3 for pH 4 – 9, and as very mobile for log Koc < 2. A 
substance can be classified as toxic due to a number of circumstances including, but not limited to, 
a long-term NOEC or ECx below 0.01 mg/L for marine or freshwater organisms, carcinogenic 
effects, reprotoxicity, and mutagenic effects (European Commission, 2022). As shown in Table 1,

2.3.1. 
Environmental Quality Standards for PFAS 
The Danish regulation for environmental PFAS concentrations is quite complex, since there exist 
standards for a series of different environmental compartments, general and maximum standards, as 
well as standards for both individual substances and sums of substances. Furthermore, the existing 
regulation is supplemented by proposed standards expected to come into effect in the next few years 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2024). However, all EQSs are determined based on a three-step methodology 
assessing the concentration limit needed to protect the environment and humans from three types of 
exposure (Baun, 2023): 
1. Aquatic life from direct toxic effects 
2. Aquatic life from secondary toxic effects 
3. Humans consuming fish and shellfish 
The first step is to determine EQS as PNEC cf. section 2.2, and this step must be applied to all 
substances. The second step furthermore considers bioconcentration and bioaccumulation through 
the food web and must be applied to substances with suspected bioaccumulative properties or high 
toxicity to mammals or bird. The third step encompasses an evaluation of the toxicity of the 
substance to humans as well as a calculation of the resulting allowable concentration in the aquatic 
environment given the exposure of humans to the substance through consumption. This step must 
be applied to substances suspected of being CMR, having irreversible effects, or being both 
bioaccumulative and having serious toxic effects following chronic or oral exposure (Baun, 2023). 
For a given substance, the lowest EQS found through this methodology is applied. As many PFAS 
are bioaccumulative, CMR, and suspected of a wide range of toxic effects, their EQSs are 
determined by the third step of the methodology. Thus, the EQSs presented in Table 4 have been 
designed to protect down-stream human consumption. 
The Danish environmental quality standards (EQS) for environmentally hazardous chemicals in 
surface waters are defined by “Bekendtgørelse nr. 796 af 13/06/2023 om fastlæggelse af miljømål 
for vandløb, søer, overgangsvande, kystvande og grundvand”, which includes both national EQSs 
and EQSs defined at EU level (Miljøministeriet, 2023). The only EQS for any PFAS included in the 
declaration is the EU EQS for PFOS (Table 4). For groundwater with potential as drinking water 
reserves and for drinking water, Miljøministeriet (2024) has set an EQS of 100 ng/L for the sum of 
the PFAS 22 (Table 4). Furthermore, there is a lower EQS of 2 ng/L for the sum of 4 of these 22 
substances, namely PFOS, PFOA, PFNA, and PFHxS (PFAS 4) (Miljøministeriet, 2024;

Environmental Background Concentrations of PFAS 
In the late 1900s, the chemicals PFOS and PFOA began to pop up as widespread environmental 
pollutants (OECD, 2021). Today, a wide range of PFAS, especially PFAAs, can be found in the 
environment across the globe even in remote areas far from human activities (Nguyen et al., 2020). 
Furthermore, PFAS occurs in all environmental compartments: streams, lakes, marine waters, soil, 
groundwater, air, rain, biota etc. This section will investigate measurements of PFAS concentrations 
in inland surface waters, groundwater, rain, soil, air, and biota, as these compartments are deemed 
most relevant to potential contamination from solar panels. A summary of the found measured 
concentration intervals of PFAS in these environmental compartments is given in Figure 3, while a 
more detailed overview of the available literature on environmental concentrations is presented in 
the following sections. Background concentrations in rain are especially important to this study, as 
the PFAS release from solar panels is likely to occur due to rainfall and thus be introduced into the 
environment alongside run-off or percolating rainwater.

2.4. 
PFAS in Solar Panels 
PFAS are a synthetic substance, which means that all PFAS in the environment originates from 
human activities. The first PFAS, PTFE, was invented in 1938. PFAS were first industrially 
produced in the 1940s (VMR, 2022). In the following decades PFAS were further developed and 
put to a broad range of uses across many industrial sectors (Brennan et al., 2021; Brunn et al., 2023; 
Ding & Peijnenburg, 2013; Gaines, 2023). From the 2000s, the global PFAS production began to 
decline due to environmental concerns and resulting regulation (VMR, 2022). PFAS are used to 
increase hydrophobicity of water-resistant coatings of textiles, paints, and sunscreen; to lower 
aqueous surface tension of cleaning products, solvents, and personal care products; to stabilize 
foams; to increase oil-resistance of food-packaging and cookware; in lubricants for manufacturing 
machinery in the metal and plastics industries; to increase heat conductivity of semiconductors; and 
a plethora of other uses. Beside their useful effects, PFAS are also attractive substances to industrial 
production due to their high stability (Glüge et al., 2020; OECD, 2022). 
Solar panels function by receiving solar radiation and converting it into electrical energy using the 
photovoltaic effect, where electrons are excited by the absorption of light (Pastuszak & Węgierek, 
2022). The efficiency of a solar panel therefore depends on its ability to absorb radiation into the 
photovoltaic cells (OECD, 2022). Furthermore, solar panels must be built robustly to withstand 
exposure to diverse weather and outdoor condition at all seasons and, preferably, over many years 
(Krystek, 2023). These are requirements that the properties of PFAS can be used to meet. 
A solar panel is comprised of a pane of photovoltaic cells consisting of a mesh of silicone cathode 
modules sandwiched between encapsulants and a front- and backsheet (Figure 5). In the 
photovoltaic cell layer of a solar panel, PFAS can be used in the adhesives holding mesh cathodes in 
place in the photovoltaic cells, since PFAS lowers the surface tension of the adhesive (Glüge et al., 
2020). Furthermore, PFAS including PFOS, might be used in chemical etching and soldering during 
the manufacturing of the photovoltaic cells (Tang et al., 2023). However, these PFAS are not 
expected to be present in the final panels and are therefore unlikely to contribute to any possible use 
phase release (Kemikalieinspektionen, 2015; Ravn & Tang, 2022). Other components of a solar 
panel, such as encapsulants, sealants, aluminium frame, junction box, and cables, can also 
incorporate PFAS in the plastic components or as traces from solvents, surfactants, or lubricants in 
the metal manufacturing (Gaines, 2023; Ravn & Tang, 2022). Moreover, PFAS can increase 
absorption of sunlight and act as an electrical insulator (OECD, 2022). PFAS interlayers have been 
==PAGE:41==
DTU                                                                                                                           Oline Mannstaedt 
10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091 
 
Page 25 of 82 
 
found to improve electrical conductivity, and thus efficiency, of some solar panel types (Han et al., 
2012). Such hydraulic PFAS elements are not yet used in commercial solar panels but are a subject 
of research and might become more prevalent in future solar panel generations (see section 2.4.1). 
However, the PFAS use in solar panels, which is of primary interest for use phase release, is in 
panel surface coating. For the surface elements of a solar panel, which include the glass, frame, 
sealants, junction box, and cables, PFAS coating might be applied as protection against weathering, 
moisture, and dirt. The frontsheet of a solar panel must be transparent to allow sunlight to reach the 
photovoltaic cells. Therefore, coating with PFAS, which resists dirt and moisture due to the 
hydrophobic and lipophobic properties of the substances, are desirable. PFAS coating can also 
protect the panel from UV light (OECD, 2022). Only PFAS present in these outermost components 
are reasonably available for release into the environment under normal use conditions (Ravn & 
Tang, 2022). However, in relation to a solar park project on Bornholm, IPU found that at least the 
cables used for solar panels are unlikely to contain PFAS (Ravn & Tang, 2023).  
Some solar panels have fluoropolymer backsheets, which consist of PFAS. Such a backsheet 
protects the panel from moisture from below. However, the panel types investigated in this report 
are so called bifacial panels, which have a glass backsheet as well as frontsheet (Figure 5). The 
bifacial solar panel models are of an older design and have, in later panel generations, been replaced 
with polymer backsheets, since the latter are lighter than glass sheets (Dunmore, 2024). However, 
the bifacial panels have the benefit of avoiding the potential PFAS pollution associated with a PFAS 
polymer backsheet. Yet, coating of the glass surfaces with PFAS might still occur. Most PFAS 
reportedly used in solar panels are polymers such as FEP, ETFE, FEVE, ECTFE, PVDF, and PFPE 
(Glüge et al., 2020; Lassen et al., 2024; Nain & Anctil, 2023; OECD, 2022), however the release 
experiments presented in this report will focus on the PFAAs included in the Danish groundwater 
EQS. Still, according to the manufacturers, many solar panels do not incorporate PFAS coatings at 
all (Ravn & Tang, 2022).

2.4.1. 
Solar Panel Types 
The technology of solar panels is in constant development due to the increasing demand for high 
efficiency, high production capacity, low cost, and, perhaps, sustainability (Pastuszak & Węgierek, 
2022). Pastuszak and Węgierek (2022) divides the current photovoltaic cell technologies onto four 
generations with the following characteristics: 
1. Thick crystalline films of silicone and gallium arsenide 
2. Thin-film solar cells of microcrystalline silicon, amorphous silicon (a-Si), copper indium 
gallium selenide, and cadmium telluride/cadmium 
3. Emerging technologies such as nanocrystalline films, perovskite, quantum dots, dye-
sensitized solar cells, solar cells based on organic polymers, and novel synthetic chemistry 
4. Hybrid technologies combining the thin films of the third generation with even more novel 
inorganic nanomaterials 
All four generations might incorporate PFAS coating on the outer sheets, however the third 
generation furthermore introduces ionic liquid components, which incorporate PFAS in solution for 
increased electrical conductivity (Niu et al., 2021; Pastuszak & Węgierek, 2022). For instance, 
fluorinated buffer layers have been found to increase efficiency in organic solar cells (Han et al., 
2012). The liquid PFAS poses a new potential hazard, as leaks from the panel interior might result 
==PAGE:43==
DTU                                                                                                                           Oline Mannstaedt 
10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091 
 
Page 27 of 82 
 
in much larger contaminations than previously presented by the surface release alone. Currently, 
only the first generation is commercially viable and in wide-scale manufacturing, while the later 
generations are still in development.  
Furthermore, solar panels can also be categorized as either N-type or P-type. The photovoltaic cells 
of an N-type panel have a negatively charged bulk layer containing phosphorous and a positively 
charged emitter layer. P-type panels have a positive bulk layer containing boron and a negative 
emitter layer in the photovoltaic cells. The N-type panels are becoming increasingly common due to 
having higher efficiency and longer warranties than the older P-type panels (Solar Magazine, 2022). 
The solar panels investigated in this report are therefore all first-generation N-type bifacial panels, 
and the only PFAS release pathway investigated in the experiments presented here is from 
weathering of the glass frontsheet.

3. Methodology 
In this report, the investigated solar panel types have been anonymized. The individual panel types 
characterized by manufacturer and wattage will be named as “panel A”, “panel B” etc. The author 
and supervisors know the manufacturer and product specifics of the tested panels. The laboratory 
test procedures described below are given as step-by-step guides in Appendix I (section 11.6). 
3.1. 
Literature Study 
The background information on PFAS presented in section 2 is based on a literature study of 
scientific articles, chemical databases, chemical legislative and regulative documents, public 
information brochures etc. This report primarily uses information from official governmental 
sources and peer-reviewed articles, however when necessary other references are included. 
Scientific sources were found on DTU FindIt, ScienceDirect and Web of Science using search 
words such as [“PFAS” AND “photovoltaics”] or [“fluorinat*” AND “solar” AND (“release” OR 
“contamina*” OR “pollut*”)]. Several similar variations of search words were used. Similarly, a 
comprehensive search for articles presenting measured environmental PFAS concentrations was 
performed with search words such as [“PFAS” AND “groundwater”] or [“PFAS” AND “deposit*” 
AND “measur* AND “atmospher*”]. 
3.2. 
Wipe Test 
The developed wipe method is based on ASTM D6661-17 (ASTM, 2017) and Poothang et al. 
(2019). For each of three solar panel modules of panel type A, a 39x55 cm area in the corner of the 
front glass pane (see Figure 6a) was wiped with a 10x10 cm sterile non-woven gauze pad (Nexcare 
3M Sterimed Soft, US) soaked in 5 mL isopropanol (HPLC grade, LiChrosolv Supelco, Merck, 
US). Each panel area was wiped a total of 15 times with a sample frequency of about 3 minutes to 
allow the surface to dry between wipe events. One of the panels was wiped an additional 45 times 6 
weeks later with the same wipe frequency, method, and equipment. The wipings were performed in 
an S-pattern aiming to cover the entire area exactly once and using a pair of tongs to limit sample 
contamination (see Figure 6b). The tongs were cleaned in methanol (HPLC grade, LiChrosolv 
Supelco, Merck, US) between wipe events. The used gauze pads were each placed in a 50 mL 
polypropylene tube (DigiTube, SCP Science, Canada). Background concentrations were measured 
by wiping a 39x55 cm area of the outside and inside of a window in the lab building 5 times using 
==PAGE:45==
DTU                                                                                                                           Oline Mannstaedt 
10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091 
 
Page 29 of 82 
 
the same procedure as described above (Figure 10 and Figure 11 in Appendix B section 9.1). To test 
the potential contamination from the gauze pad or polypropylene tube itself, three blank samples 
were made. To test standard recovery, three samples spiked with 10 ng of each of the PFAS 22 were 
included. Of the first batch of samples all were processed for analysis, however only every fifth 
sample of the second batch was included in the following recovery procedure: To extract the PFAS

proceedings are shown in Appendix B (section 9.1).

3.3. 
Simulated Rainwater Test 
The simulated rainwater test requires upconcentration of a large water sample prior to analysis for 
PFAS. The upconcentration of PFAS in the water solution is performed by sorbing the PFAS to a 
weak anion exchanger (WAX) resin sorbent. In this study, three different WAX resins were tested 
for their ability to sorb and rerelease PFAS in a recovery test (Figure 14 in Appendix B section 9.2): 
• Lewatit® VP OC 1065 Ion Exchange Resin, Supelco, Merck (Lewatit) (Merck, 2024d) 
• C18 Silica Gel Inorganic Adsorbent, Supelco, Merck (Silica Gel Spherical) (Merck, 2024b) 
• Amberlite™ HPR4100 Ion Exchange Resin, Supelco, Merck (AmberLite) (Merck, 2024a) 
Firstly, preliminary experiments for the recovery tests of WAX sorbents were performed. Secondly, 
the sorption and recovery procedure were upscaled and applied to a simulated rainwater setup with 
the solar panel type as the one tested in the wipe test (panel A) as well as a different panel type (B) 
for which a previous study by Özkan (2024) had performed a wipe test. 
3.3.1. 
Recovery Test 
As a preliminary test of the experimental setup and the potential of the three WAX resin sorbents 
Lewatit, Silica Gel Spherical, and AmberLite, a recovery experiment with a PFAS 22 mix was 
performed. For each of the three sorbents the following procedure was followed as inspired by Yang 
et al.(2018): 
Approximately 1 g (Bie & Berntsen A/S, Denmark) of the sorbent was placed on a 0.6 µm glass 
fibre filter (GA55, Advantec Toyo, Japan) in a glass funnel above a 500 mL bluecap bottle (Figure 
15 in Appendix B section 9.2). For conditioning of the sorbent, 20 mL 0.5 % ammonia methanol 
(HPLC grade, LiChrosolv Supelco, Merck, US) was poured over the sorbent twice, and a 10 mL 
sample of the solution was extracted for analysis. Next, 60 mL MilliQ water was poured over the 
sorbent for equilibration, and a 50 mL sample was extracted for analysis. A MilliQ solution with 
100 ng/L (10 ng in 100 mL) of each of the PFAS 22 was poured over the sorbent and the filtered 
solution was set aside for analysis. The filter and sorbent were extracted from the funnel and placed 
in a 50 mL polypropylene tube (DigiTube, SCP Science, Canada) with 30 mL methanol (Figure 16 
in Appendix B section 9.2). The bottle was heated at 60 ◦C for 1 hour in a heater (SPB 50-48, 
Perkinelmer, US). Next, the methanol solution containing filter and sorbent beads was filtered 
through a 0.6 µm glass fibre filter using a vacuum pump (Laboport N 820 g, KNF, US) and the 
filtered sample was set aside for analysis. The procedure was repeated and samples saved for

10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091

analysis. As two of the five samples collected were in a water solution, these were upconcentrated 
and transferred to a methanol solution using WAX solid phase extraction (Chromabond SPE 
vacuum manifold, Macherey-Nagel, Germany). The SPE cartridges were first conditioned with 4

3.3.2. 
Simulated Rainwater Test 
In the simulated rainwater test, 500 L tap water was run across the surface of a solar panel, as well 
as a plain glass pane for reference in a circulated flow: The water is pumped from a container to the 
top of the panels and released from 19 nozzles distributed across the panel top. From there, the 
water flows down the panel front as rain would and is then once again collected in the container 
(Figure 7 and Appendix B section 9.3 Figure 18). The test was performed twice: The first panel 
(panel type B) was subjected to the simulated rainfall for about eight months at a flow rate of about 
0.5 L/min (Figure 7). The second panel (panel type A) received 18 days of simulated rain with a 
flow rate of about 6 L/min. The 500 L corresponds to the approximate annual Danish precipitation 
on a surface area equivalent to that of a solar panel. The resulting 500 L solution was 
upconcentrated using the sorbent Lewatit. The water was solid phase extracted with 40 g sorbent 
(Figure 19 in Appendix B section 9.3) conditioned by pouring 2 L 0.5 % ammonium methanol 
(HPLC grade, LiChrosolv Supelco, Merck, US) over the sorbent followed by 3 L tap water. For the 
first test run, the 500 L water was circulated through the sorbent at a flow rate of about 0.2 L/min 
for 6 days. For the second test run, the 500 L water was circulated across the sorbent at a flow rate 
of about 0.6 L/min for 4 days. Flow rate was measured by manually recording the time it took for 1 
L of water to flow from the tap below the pane frame in each setup (Figure 7). For both test runs, 
the sorbed PFAS was then extracted from the sorbent with two consecutive 0.3 L methanol 
solutions (HPLC grade, LiChrosolv Supelco, Merck, US). Firstly, each of the two sorbent columns 
were washed into a 0.5 L bluecap glass bottle with 0.3 L methanol. Secondly, both solutions were

3.4. 
Contaminant Mass Discharge Calculations for Assessing PFAS 
Release in the Environment 
To assess the potential environmental concentrations of PFAS released from solar panels and 
compare with Danish EQSs for surface water and groundwater, contaminant mass discharge 
calculations were performed for a theoretical solar park based on the Viuf Solar Park project near 
Kolding, Denmark. Viuf was chosen as inspiration for the calculation case study, since it has good 
data availability and was deemed representable for a typical large Danish solar park project. 
Furthermore, the Viuf Solar Park is located close to both a groundwater protected area with drinking 
water abstraction and a stream, which are both possible PFAS recipients of interest to the 
assessment of environmental impact (Figure 2). The specifics of the solar park and environmental 
conditions used in the calculations are given in Table 12. In the calculations are investigated two 
worst-case scenarios: 1) the total released PFAS contamination plume from the solar park flows to 
the abstracted groundwater or 2) the total released PFAS contamination is discharged into the 
stream. Scenario 2 is estimated for two sizes of streams with different flow rates. The minimum 
flow is calculated as the total precipitation on the solar park given the park area and precipitation in 
Table 12. The maximum flow rate is estimated from typical flows of large, Danish streams (Ovesen 
et al., 2000). 
The PFAS concentration in rain is based on measurements by Bossi (2024) at Risø (see section 
2.3.2). For PFAS 22, Bossi (2024) found a concentration in rain of 3.0 ng/L, while the concentration 
of PFAS 24, accounting for RPF, was 3.3 ng PFOA-eq./L (Table 12). Calculations were conducted 
for two net precipitations: 500 mm/yr and 150 mm/yr, which are the approximate average net 
precipitation 1989 – 2019 in West Jutland and Zealand, respectively (GEUS, 2021). Thus, mass 
discharge calculations with these two precipitation values will be used to estimate the national 
variation in PFAS input from precipitation into the case park area. The potentially released masses 
of PFAS used in the calculations are based in the wipe test performed in this project.

4.2.1. 
Recovery Test 
In the preliminary recovery test of the three sorbents Lewatit, Silica Gel Spherical, and AmberLite, 
were tested for 20 PFAS. The test found total recoveries for the sum of unsorbed and sorbed 
fractions of the 20 PFAS of 31 – 111 %. The highest recovery was found for Silica Gel Spherical, 
with an average substance recovery of 94 % and single substance recovery in the interval 73 – 111 
%. The lowest recovery was found for AmberLite, which had an average substance recovery of 57 
% and individual substance recoveries in the interval 31 – 105 %. However, total recovery is not a 
measure of the quality of the sorbents but of the recovery test procedure, since unsorbed substance 
is also recovered from the initial solution. The quality criteria for the sorbents are sorption and 
release from sorbent. These characteristics are shown in Table 15 as average values across the 20 
tested PFAS. The substances have systematic differences in both sorption and recovery, which are 
illustrated in Figure 9. All three sorbents have approximately the same average sorption rate of 50 – 
53 %, while the sorption rates for the individual sorbents range from 0.6 % to 99 % (Table 15 and 
Figure 9a). Figure 9a shows the sorption rates of PFAS to the three sorbents as functions of PFAS 
molecular weight, which reveals a trend of increased sorption for larger PFAS molecules. In 
particular, Silica Gel Spherical was found to have a steep increase in sorption for larger PFAS, with 
the sorption of the five smallest tested PFAS (molecular weight below 380 g/mol and chain length 
below 7) being less than 10 % (Figure 9a). Lewatit and AmberLite have sorption rates for the five 
smallest tested PFAS of 37 – 40 % and 44 – 49 %, respectively. All three sorbents reach sorption 
rated above 90 % for the largest tested PFAS (Figure 9a). The average release rates of sorbed 
substance are 73 % and 94 % for Lewatit and Silica Gel Spherical, respectively, while AmberLite 
only has an average release rate of 20 % (Table 15). For Silica Gel Spherical, there is a clear trend 
of decreasing release rate for larger PFAS substances, while the trend is less apparent for Lewatit 
and AmberLite (Figure 9b). Since Lewatit has higher sorption for short-chained PFAS than Silica 
Gel Spherical and has an average total recovery rate only 11 percentage points lower than that of 
Silica Gel Spherical, Lewatit is deemed to have the best performance of the three sorbents and is 
therefore chosen as the sorbent for the upconcentration of PFAS in the simulated rainwater 
experiment. While Silica Gel Spherical appears to have a high release rate for small PFAS (105 - 
125 % for the five smallest substances), this result only reflects that less than 10 % of the initial 
substances was sorbed and thus available for release. Therefore, the release rates of small PFAS for

5. Discussion 
5.1. 
Evaluating the Wipe Test 
One challenge of doing laboratory work with PFAS is the choice of container materials. PFAS 
adsorbs strongly to glass, so using glass vials might result in a loss of PFAS in the analysis. 
Reversely, Teflon-containing materials might release PFAS into the sample thus increasing the 
concentration found in analysis. The least problematic materials for PFAS containers are considered 
to be polypropylene and polyethylene (Smith et al., 2016). In the experiments conducted in this 
project, tubes of polypropylene with polyethylene caps were used for sampling (SCP Science, 
2024). However, due to practical necessities, glass vials and flask were also be used during the 
experimental procedure. Still, since the spiked samples in the wipe test had a recovery of 77 % for 
PFHxA and 80 % for PFPeA, adsorption to the container does not appear to have been a 
considerable issue. However, 20 – 23 % of the substance was lost during analysis, and thus the 
found PFAs releases might be about 20 % higher than the results suggest. Still, this would not 
change the findings to any considerable extent, and it was thus decided to present the test findings 
without accounting for recovery rate for the sake of data transparency. 
In the wipe test, PFHxA and PFPeA were the only PFAS 22 consistently measured above the 
quantification limit of 0.02 ng per sample. Other PFAS were observed in 2 – 62 % of the samples 
and in lower contents than PFPeA, which itself was 5 times lower than the PFHxA content. The 
most prevalent PFAS besides PFHxA and PFPeA was PFOA with masses up to 0.69 ng/wipe and 
2.7 ng total in the analysed samples. Consequently, the primary focus is on PFHxA and PFPeA. The 
wipe test performed in this project resulted in an estimated potential PFAS release from panel A of 
160±19 ng/m2 for the sum of PFHxA and PFPeA (section 4.1). Özkan (2024) performed a wipe test 
on similar panel types (bifacial) referred to here as panel B, C and D using similar wipe procedure 
as described in section 3.2. He found the released sum of PFHxA and PFPeA to be 32 ng/m2 for 
panel B, 88 ng/m2 for panel C and 220 ng/m2 for panel D. Thus, the estimated potential release for 
panel A is within the same range as the findings by Özkan (2024). 
The follow-up test showed the PFAS released per wipe approximately following the linear trend 
line. However, for later wipes than 35, the release per wipe decreases slower than indicated by the 
trend (Figure 8a). Since the calculation of total potentially released PFAS assumes linear release, 
further investigation of the release trend might be prudent. It can be speculated that as the panel 
==PAGE:61==
DTU                                                                                                                           Oline Mannstaedt 
10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091 
 
Page 45 of 82 
 
surface is depleted of PFAS, the concentration difference between the panel surface and the wipe 
decreases, which causes the release rate to decrease in turn. Thus, release occurs more slowly than 
predicted. The follow-up test also investigated the possibility of slow release of PFAS from the 
solid material as suggested for e.g. PCB. A continuous trend of decreasing PFAS masses released as 
a function of wipe events was observed. However, the first wipe event of the follow-up test does 
appear to have masses of PFHxA and PFPeA higher than expected from the decreasing trend. This 
peak in released PFAS right after 6 weeks break in sampling could indicate that PFHxA and PFPeA 
might occur continually due to release from the solid material, or it could originate from 
contamination in the laboratory facility during storage.  
In the background samples from the outside of a window in the building, where the panels were 
stored during the 6 weeks, the most prevalent PFAS measured was PFOA with masses about 8 times 
higher than the found background masses of PFHxA and PFPeA (see Table 13 in section 4.1). 
Furthermore, the background samples from the inside of the window, which were taken 
simultaneously with the follow-up wipe test of the solar panel, did not find any PFAS above the 
quantification limit in any of the five wipe samples. Thus, assuming that the increased masses in 
wipe 16 are not simply due to the inherent uncertainties of the sampling and analysis, chemical 
processes in or on the panel itself might be a probable source of the occurring PFHxA and PFPeA, 
and these processes would be an interesting topic for further studies. For instance, one could 
measure the potential release of PFHxA and PFPeA precursors not encompassed by the PFAS 22 or 
the release of polymeric PFAS, which could be a source of PFAAs (VMR, 2022). Yet, since the 
measured PFAS decreased to the levels expected by the trend in the very next analysed sample 
(0.37 ng PFHxA and 0.051 ng PFPeA in wipe event 21), the most likely source of the peak in wipe 
event 16 is arguably still external contamination during the 6 weeks break in the wipe test. 
While many manufacturers of solar panels claim that their panels are “PFAS-free”, this study shows 
that the claim largely depends on the limit of quantification (Pers. comm. Skjolding, 2024). Since 
the potential release concentrations of PFPeA and PFHxA found throughout the wipe test are well 
above the reference background concentrations, the observed PFAS does most likely originate from 
the panels themselves and not from external sources such as dry deposition on the panel surface. 
While the solar panels reportedly do not incorporate PFAS coatings or other PFAS components in 
the panel construction, PFAS might still occur as traces from the production process. PFAS are 
prevalent in several aspects of maintenance of industrial machinery as lubricants, greases, and 
cleaning agents (Gaines, 2023; Glüge et al., 2020). An example could be release from the 
==PAGE:62==

lamination process utilizing a PTFE membrane which could lead to release of e.g. PFHxA and 
PFPeA as measured here. A more systematic investigation of the production sites could quantify this 
possible source. However, due to the relatively high pressure and temperature of the lamination 
process it has not yet been possible to find alternatives to PTFE.  
5.2. 
Choice of Sorbents 
Sorbent methods for remediation, such as activated carbon and ion exchange, are already well 
known, however in the current study release of the sorbed PFAS from the sorbent was needed, 
which is a feature not accounted for by the established remediation sorbents (Hjørringgaard, 2023). 
One commonly used setup for sorption is the solid phase extraction (SPE) column, where a PFAS 
solution is poured across a sorbent matrix thus facilitating sorption. The three different solid 
sorbents tested here are so called weak anion exchange (WAX) resin sorbents, which bind 
hydrophobic substances through van der Waals forces (Merck, 2024d, 2024b, 2024a). In the 
recovery test, a PFAS recovery of 40 % – 100 % was achieved. Discounting spillage and 
uncertainties, the fraction of PFAS not recovered likely remained sorbed past the release step 
described in section 3.3.1. This is also supported by the sorption rate and release rate showing 
increased sorption as well as a decrease in release of sorbed substance for longer-chained PFAS 
(Table 15 and Figure 14). Across the three sorbents, Lewatit was chosen due to its relatively high 
sorption capacity for PFAS with chain lengths less than 7. Since the two substances most prominent 
in the wipe-test, PFHxA and PFPeA, have chain lengths of 5 and 4, respectively, this turned out to 
be a good decision. However, while Lewatit had better sorption for short-chained PFAS than the 
other tested sorbents, it still had a rate for sorption and subsequent release skewered towards long-
chained substances. Thus, the found releases of short-chained PFAS are underestimations compared 
to the measured release of long-chained PFAS (Figure 9), which only substantiates the choice to 
focus of PFHxA and PFPeA in the data analysis, as these substances are even more prevalent than 
the test results suggest. 
The effect of e.g. manipulation of pH or longer retention time could be tested as possible paths to 
increased substance recovery. For WAX sorbents, a flow rate of about 1 drop/second, or 3 ml/min, 
is recommended (Merck, 2024e, 2024c; Yang et al., 2018). In this study, the flow rate over the 
sorbents was determined by the practical limitations of the experimental setup. In the simulated 
rainwater test this resulted in a flow rate through the sorbent column of 200 and 600 mL/min for the 
first and second test run, respectively. This could be an area of optimization in further studies. 
==PAGE:63==
DTU                                                                                                                           Oline Mannstaedt 
10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091 
 
Page 47 of 82 
 
However, assuming complete mixture of the water volume, the given flow rates correspond to the 
entire 500 L water in the system passing the sorbent about 3.5 and 7.2 times for the panel B and 
panel A test runs, respectively. Since the preliminary recovery tests showed 53 % (21 – 95 %) 
sorption of PFAS to Lewatit, when the solution passed over the sorbent only once, this flow rate is 
judged sufficient for extracting the released PFAS from the water phase. 
5.3. 
Evaluating the Simulated Rainwater Setup and Comparing with 
the Wipe Test 
The chosen water volume in the simulated rainwater test of 500 L corresponds to the annual Danish 
precipitation on the area of a solar module. However, to test the impact on the release rate two 
different flow rates were applied: 0.5 L/min for about 8 months, which corresponds to about 345 
years of rainfall, and 6 L/min for 18 days corresponding to about 250 years of rainfall. While 
conditions such as solar radiation, wind, and temperature oscillations are all variables which could 
influence on PFAS release but were not included in the current setup, both flow rates estimated 
releases exceeding the guaranteed lifetime of a solar panel (25 years) at least ten times. Thus, the 
total releases found in the simulated rainwater test are most likely higher than the actual releases 
during panel lifetime, but the estimated yearly release might be an underestimation, that could be 
investigated further. In the simulated rainwater setup, the water was supposed to run across the 
entire surface of the panel front glass pane. However, during the test run the water began to collect 
in narrow lanes down the panel and thus only came into contact with about one fourth of the glass 
pane area (see the lines of calcium precipitation on the panel from on Figure 7 (right) and Figure 18 
in Appendix B section 9.3). Thus, the actual potential PFAS release could be about 4 times as high, 
as determined in section 4.2.2. 
When accounting for the differences in flow rate and test duration in the two simulated rainwater 
tests, the two panels showed quite similar PFAS 22 releases per year per panel, namely 0.76 
ng/yr/panel for panel type A and 0.69 ng/yr/panel for panel type B (Table 20). This finding 
substantiates the assumption of constant release, as the yearly PFAS release appears to be 
independent of test run duration in both real time and years of rain simulated. 
One of the main aims of this project is to evaluate the comparability of the wipe test method with in 
situ release from solar panels. This is done by comparing the wipe test with the simulated rainwater 
test, which is assumed to correspond to potential release caused by rainwater in the environment. 
The test methods are compared for two solar panel types: A and B. In this project, the wipe test was

approximately one order of magnitude: 32 ng/panel was found in the wipe test performed by Özkan

Furthermore, while all measured PFAS in the panel B wipe test was PFHxA, the simulated 
rainwater test had a 75 % contribution from PFOA and only 6.4 % from PFHxA.  
It is to be expected that the two test methods have differing results, since the wipe test and the 
simulated rainwater test naturally follow different procedures, which introduce the PFAS to 
differing conditions. For instance, the wipe test might be more likely to precipitate precursor 
transformation during sampling or analysis than the simulated rainwater test, since the extraction is 
performed with methanol and not water. Therefore, it should be noted that PFOSA and 6:2 FTS are 
precursors and that the measured masses of these substances therefore could be included in the 
masses of their respective dead-end metabolites, which are PFOS for PFOSA and PFPeA/PFHxA 
for 6:2 FTS. However, in this project precursors have been treated as individual substances.

correspond to the release from 210 and 46 years of weathering for panel A and B, respectively

phase for panels with an expected use phase of 20 – 30 years. This finding is further substantiated 
by Özkan (2024), who compared PFAS release with the wipe test for solar panels with and without 
previous weathering and found releases of the same magnitude. Thus, the surface of the weathered 
panels had not been depleted from PFAS and it therefore seems likely that natural weathering 
during the use phase of a panel will not result in as high PFAS release as the wipe test conditions. 
There appear to be a substantial difference in release between panel A and B indicated by the total 
release found in the wipe test and the corresponding years of rainfall, for both of which panel A has 
values about five times higher than panel B (Table 20). However, for panel A reaching total PFAS 
release in the wipe test required about 60 wipe events, while the total release for panel B was 
achieved with only 15 wipes. Thus, the number of years of rain-induced PFAS release per wipe 
event is approximately 3 – 3.5 for both panel types. Thus, the two panel types have the same 
approximate release rate, but panel A contains about five times as much PFAS as panel B.

5.4. 
Contaminant Mass Discharge and Assessment of Environmental 
Hazard 
Often, hydrogeological modelling will be applied when assessing the environmental fate and hazard 
of a contamination plume. However, hydrogeological modelling is very complex for PFAS, since 
the underlying assumptions are different for PFAS than for e.g. organic solvents, due to the 
surfactant-properties of PFAS introduced in section 2.1.2. 
The contaminant mass discharge (CMD) calculations of this project assumes that all released PFAS 
will reach the investigated recipient (water works or stream). Thus, the found recipient 
concentrations represent a worst-case scenario where no substance has degraded or been 
immobilized. Factors such as retardation through sorption to the soil will lessen the actual 
environmental impact, at least to the water phase. However, independent of substance mobility, this 
assumption of total transport to recipient might still be prudent, since PFAS are persistent and can 
therefore arguably be assumed to reach any given recipient eventually. The general nature of the 
CMD calculations also means that the results of this project are applicable across different solar 
park projects, since the only inputs are the total possible PFAS release and the precipitation. The 
CMD calculations can be considered as an initial risk screening. If the calculations had resulted in a 
possible environmental risk further modelling might be used to evaluate the risk under more 
realistic and site-specific conditions. 
For all recipient scenarios in the CMD calculation, the environmental PFAS concentration from the 
sum of rain and solar panels is lower than the EQS (Table 19 section 4.3). However, while the PFAS 
==PAGE:67==
DTU                                                                                                                           Oline Mannstaedt 
10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091 
 
Page 51 of 82 
 
EQSs are very low compared to most other substances and analytical detection limits, there are 
some uncertainties about how EQS should be defined to best target the potential environmental risk 
associated with PFAS and different EQS might not be directly comparable (also see section 5.5). At 
face value, the current groundwater EQS of 100 ng/L is much higher than the proposed future 
surface water EQS of 4.4 ng/L. However, the two standards are rather difficult to compare in 
general terms due to not only the difference in included PFAS and environmental compartments, but 
also due the difference in calculation method imposed by the inclusion of relative potency factor 
(RPF) in the 4.4 ng PFOA-eq./L EQS. The RPF is supposed to weigh the PFAS 24 sum towards 
substances with higher toxicity. However, RPF is based specifically on studies reporting the effect 
of oral PFAS intake on liver weight in male rats. Thus, RPF has a rather limited scientific basis for 
predicting general environmental toxicity, while it might be a better indication of substance toxicity 
to humans and other mammals. 
For the two PFAS found to be released from solar panels in this study (PFHxA and PFPeA), the 
RPFs are 0.01 and 0.01 – 0.05, respectively. If a conservative PFPeA RPF of 0.05 is applied, a 100 
ng/L PFAS 22 concentration of 50 ng/L PFHxA and 50 ng/L PFPeA would correspond to 3 ng 
PFOA-eq./L PFAS 24. Thus, a given contamination could very well be close to both EQSs. But 
under conditions with a different substance distribution, the difference in evaluation between use of 
the two EQSs might be more prominent. Thus, it could be relevant to compare the found potential 
environmental PFAS concentrations with something other than EQSs such as PNEC or existing 
environmental concentrations. 
As mentioned in section 2.1.2, when PFAS is taken up by organisms, the substances accumulate in 
protein. As conventional ecotoxicity testing for bioaccumulativity focuses on uptake in lipids, 
traditional testing methodology might have a blind spot for the potential bioaccumulation of PFAS 
in proteins (Baun et al., 2017). Furthermore, since PFAS are both hydrophobic and lipophobic, they 
tend to accumulate at the interphases between water, air, particles, and, in laboratory experiments, 
octanol. Therefore, determining properties such as the Kow, which require measuring the 
concentration in the water phase and the octanol phase, is close to impossible (VMR, 2022). The 
uncertainty of PFAS Kow’s makes it very difficult to assess the environmental distribution and fate 
of PFAS, since Kow is a key property from which many other characteristics such as Koc and Kd can 
be estimated. This is one of the reasons why environmental knowledge of PFAS is sparse. Still, 
environmental PFAS toxicity can be ascertained through experimentally (section 2.2). In section 2.2 
was mentioned that Wang et al. (2024) has found predicted no effect concentrations (PNEC) for 
==PAGE:68==
DTU                                                                                                                           Oline Mannstaedt 
10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091 
 
Page 52 of 82 
 
PFCAs in freshwater to be in the interval 4.8 − 2000 μg/L. For all CMD scenarios, the predicted 
environmental concentration (PEC) is far lower than this PNEC interval. The highest PEC was 
calculated for a small stream and found to be 3.4 ng/l. In a worst-case calculation, where the highest 
found PEC and the lowest PNEC are compared, the hazard quotient becomes 0.0007 (see appendix 
C section 10.2).  
Since the hazard quotient is far smaller than 1, there is no environmental risk. Moreover, the CMD 
calculation was based on the release quantities found in the wipe test and it was assumed as a worst-
case scenario that all measured substance would be released and reach the recipient within a single 
year. However, the comparison of wipe test and simulated rainwater test suggested that the masses 
measured in the wipe test correspond to substance release from around 40 – 200 years. Thus, the 
recipient concentrations from the solar park and the resulting hazard quotient would both likely be 
smaller than suggested by the calculation.  
The contaminant mass discharge calculations for the case solar park found that solar panel surfaces 
contribute 11 – 36 times less PFAS 22 than the rainfall on the park area assuming total release of 
measured PFAS within one year (Table 18 in section 4.3 and Table 20). If the wipe test correspond 
to release over 40 – 200 years rather than one year, this also further decreases the PFAS contribution 
from solar panels compared to that of rain (Table 20 in section 5.4). Given the findings of the wipe 
test, if the PFAS emissions from solar panels surfaces alone were to contaminate a small stream to 
concentrations above the 4.4 ng PFOA-eq./L EQS, this would require PFAS release from about 20 
million solar panels, which corresponds to 76 times as many panels as the Viuf solar park. However, 
it should be noted that, since PFAS is persistent, any release will contribute to the total accumulated 
environmental levels. 
The found potential PFAS release from the solar park could also be contextualised through

5.5. 
On the Challenges of PFAS Regulation 
Since PFAS is such a large and diverse group of compounds, there is a challenge in determining 
which parameters are best used to measure PFAS analytically (Brunn et al., 2023). Single substance 
analysis is a poor proxy for the wider spectrum of substances, which is why EQSs are increasingly 
being defined for sums of many PFAS (see section 2.3.1). However, both individual and sum EQSs 
are based on an analyte-specific approach, which is currently the most common regulative approach 
for EU countries. Reversely, standards can be set for PFAS Total as proposed by Directive (EU) 
2020/2184 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 16 December 2020 (European 
Council, 2020; Thomas et al., 2023). However, the EU has yet to publicise standardised guidelines 
on how to measure PFAS Total. Currently, several methods for measuring the PFAS Total content of 
a sample are being developed (European Commission, 2024). These methods include total organic 
fluor, extractable fluor, and adsorbable fluor, where the unit being measured is the fluor content 
itself, not the substance in which it occurs. With these approaches, all PFAS and precursors, not 
only the ones known and specifically targeted, are included in the analysis. Another relevant method 
is TOP (Total Oxidation of Precursors), where all precursors are oxidized to PFAAs and analysed 
with LC-MS/MS (VMR, 2022). Measuring PFAS Total release from solar panels with one or more 
of these methods could be an interesting topic for further studies. 
The potential of PFAS Total analysis and legislation is especially relevant when considering 
regrettable substitution. (OSHA, 2024). The first PFAAs to become subject to legislative pressure 
were PFOS and PFOA followed by groups of selected long-chained PFAS (see section 2.3.1 and 
2.4). Due to this legislative pressure, the industry is in an ongoing process of finding alternative 
substances among the short-chained PFAS. Since the early 2000s, industrial substitution of long-
==PAGE:70==
DTU                                                                                                                           Oline Mannstaedt 
10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091 
 
Page 54 of 82 
 
chained PFAS with short-chained PFAS has been evident (Cousins et al., 2019). This substitution 
might be deemed beneficial, since short-chained PFAS are less bioaccumulative than long-chained 
PFAS. However, the shorter C–F-chain also causes increased mobility, which might in turn increase 
leaching and substance distribution in the environment (Bossi, 2024; Cousins et al., 2019). 
Furthermore, short-chained PFAS are less well studied than long-chained PFAS. Similarly, there is a 
trend of alkyl carboxyl PFAS being substituted with oxo carboxyl PFAS such as ADONA and C6O4 
(Brunn et al., 2023). Substitution of known PFAS with other, less well known PFAS only increases 
the potential hazard to environment and human health (Brunn et al., 2023). One possible solution to 
this dilemma is to change regulation procedures from EQSs defined for specific substance sums to a 
general EQS for total PFAS defined as total organic fluor or another analytical method (ECHA, 
2023a). 
Furthermore, as described in section 2.4 and 2.3.1, different PFAS subgroups have different 
histories of development, use and regulation. Today, long-chained PFAS are mostly phased out, 
medium length PFAS are in use and subject to increasing regulation, while short-chained PFAS are 
emerging as substitutes for their longer-chained cousins. Therefore, medium length PFAS, with C–
F-chains lengths of about 6 – 10 carbon atoms, are the PFAS most studied (Brunn et al., 2023) and 
the PFAS for which analytical methods and QSAR methods are most effective. Moreover, the 
sorbents used in the experimental aspects of this project, are also likely to work best for medium 
length PFAS: PFAS with short chains will be more disposed to pass through the sorbent medium 
quickly, thus decreasing the chance of sorption. And long-chained PFAS are prone to sorbing to 
strongly and thus failing to release from the sorbent (section 4.2.1) (Pers. comm. Skjolding, 2024). 
In the DTU analysis used in this project was tested for 20 PFAS of which 19 are included in the 
PFAS 22 (see Table 5). The one PFAS included in the analysis, which does not have an EQS as part 
of PFAS 22, is PFTeDA. Since PFTeDA was not observed in any of the test results, it did not 
become relevant weather or not to include it in the release estimations. The three PFAS in PFAS 22, 
which were not tested in this project, are PFBA, PFTrS, and PFUnS. This discrepancy between 
PFAS 22 and tested substances was due to the practical limitation of acquiring the necessary 
standards for the analysis. 
Of the three untested substances in the PFAS 22, one is a short-chained PFCA and two are long-
chained PFSAs. Seeing as the two PFAS, which were consistently found in concentrations above 
the quantification limit in the wipe test, PFPeA and PFHxA, are both short-chained PFCAs, it seems

5.6. 
Comparing PFAS Release with total Danish PFAS Emissions 
So far, the found potential PFAS release form solar panels have been compared to the contaminant 
input from rain, to EQSs, to PNEC values, and to existing environmental PFAs concentrations. 
However, how does the potential contamination from solar parks hold up in comparison to the total 
Danish emissions? A study on Danish PFAS use by Lassen et al. (2024) found an “intentional input 
to society” of PFAAs in 2021 of about 300 tons across 18 application areas, where the energy sector 
incl. solar panels contributed with 4 t/yr. However, the vast majority of this PFAA input is expected 
to be destroyed in waste incineration and thus not be emitted into the environment. The Danish 
emissions of PFAAs to air, soil and surface water are estimated at 2.2, 4.3, and 1.7 t/yr, respectively. 
The total environmental emissions of both PFAAs, TFA related PFAS, and polymeric PFAS in 
Denmark was estimated at about 440 t/yr (Lassen et al., 2024). In 2020 the total emissions of PFAS 
to the environment in the EU were estimated at about 75,000 tonnes (ECHA, 2023b). 
In the wipe test of panel type A was found a total release of PFPeA and PFHxA of about 63 ng/m2 
solar panel. In a study by Dansk Solkraft in 2021, it was estimated that the total solar park area in 
Denmark in 2030 will be 12,500 ha (Dansk Solkraft, 2021). Assuming all solar parks have a similar 
ratio of solar panels per park area to that of the Viuf solar park, this prognosis corresponds to a total 
solar panel area in 2030 of approximately 75 km2. Using the findings of the wipe test, this panel 
area would in turn result in a total release of 4.7 g PFAS 22 from the panel surfaces throughout the 
use phase of the panels. Assuming constant release throughout a 20-year panel use phase, this 
corresponds to solar panel PFAS release contributing only 3⸱10-4 % of the 1.7 t/yr Danish PFAA 
emissions to surface waters. Thus, the PFAS emissions from the frontsheet of solar panels is very 
small compared to the total Danish PFAS emissions across industries.

5.7. 
Comparing PFAS to other Contaminants potentially released 
from Solar Panels 
PFAS is not the only contaminant potentially released from solar panels. To put PFAS 
contamination into perspective, it might be worthwhile to consider the magnitude of the potential 
release of other substances from solar panels. While solar panel components such as the aluminium 
frame, junction box and cables are deemed less relevant for the release of PFAS, they are likely the 
primary sources of heavy metal contamination in the panel use phase. Heavy metals have 
historically been subjects of concern and are well established in terms of fate and effects in the 
literature. However, the concern of solar panel metal pollution appears to mainly be centred around 
leaching in the end-of-life phase due to improper disposal of photovoltaics, while release in the use 
phase is not very well documented, which makes direct comparison between PFAS and heavy metal 
release difficult (Lin et al., 2022; Motta et al., 2016; Nain & Kumar, 2020; Su et al., 2019; Tammaro 
et al., 2016).  
For some emerging solar panel technologies, e.g. perovskite, metal contaminants are of especially 
high concern, since the panel technology directly employs heavy metals, such as lead and cadmium 
(Pastuszak & Węgierek, 2022). Panthi, et al. (2021) found release of silicon, lead, aluminium, 
arsenic and nickel from broken perovskite solar panels and release of aluminium, nickel, and copper 
from broken monocrystalline solar cells. Panthi, et al. (2021) further notes that lead was released 
from both broken and unbroken perovskite panels at about 1.0 mg/L at a pH of 4.93, which suggests 
that release in the use phase is likely. In general, potential end-of-life leaching from solar panels 
have been found to include heavy metals such as zinc, copper, nickel, gallium, lead, indium, 
chromium antimony, mercury, manganese, silver, aluminium, and cadmium as well as ion such as 
chloride, natrium, calcium, and magnesium (Brun et al., 2016; M. W. Johnson, 2010; Motta et al., 
2016; Sharma et al., 2021; Su et al., 2019). 
In relation to use phase contamination, the findings of these studies underline the importance of 
robust module design and use of proper sealants to avoid contaminant release from the interior 
panel components during the use phase. However, since this project focuses on use as intended of 
non-damaged solar modules, release from the outer surface of the panel is the most relevant release 
pathway. Still, comparing the potential metal contaminant releases found in the above-mentioned 
literature with the potential PFAS release could provide an estimate for comparison. Potential 
leaching concentrations of metals found in end-of-life experiments are presented in Table 21 and

5.8. 
Outlook 
Currently, ECHA is processing a proposed restriction of about 10,000 PFAS substances, which has 
been dubbed a “universal PFAS ban” due to its cross-substance and cross-sector restriction 
ambitions. The proposed restriction will place a ban on manufacture, use, and placing on the market 
of PFAS as constituents, in mixtures, and in articles including solar panels (Baas, 2023; ECHA, 
2023a, 2023b). The ban is expected to enter into force from 2025, however the proposed restrictions 
will be subject to derogation periods between 18 months and 12 years depending on the feasibility 
of alternatives for the individual PFAS uses (ECHA, 2023b, 2023a). This ban will be an important 
step towards limiting regretful substitution, as the universal nature of the ban will make it 
impossible to simply exchange one PFAS with another. Furthermore, the ban will require improved 
PFAS Total analysis methods and will therefore most likely cause development of stronger and 
more standardized procedures for total organic flour, total oxidation of precursors, and other PFAS 
Total measuring methods. 
While the release of PFAS from solar panels found in this project is not an environmental risk, there 
are other potential issues to consider for solar parks. The potential release of other contaminants 
such as heavy metals have been discussed in section 5.7. Furthermore, solar parks take up space and 
are noticeable changes in the character of a previously agricultural landscape, which is a source of 
some local opposition. In the Netherlands, the construction of solar parks on agricultural land 
became restricted by law from January 2024 due to the extensive use of scarce space (Hallema, 
2023).  
Yet, solar panels are a key component of the green energy transition, and it is paramount to establish 
a scientifically founded basis for quantifying potential environmental effects of solar parks. In 
relation to PFAS release from panel frontsheets, the following topics could be investigated further 
for a more in-depth understanding of the release processes: 
• Measuring PFAS Total release from solar panels with either Total Organic Fluor, Total 
Oxidation of Precursors, or other methods. 
• Performing simulated rainwater test with replicates to get a better understanding of the 
statistical uncertainties of the method. 
• Performing simulated rainwater tests with flow corresponding to only 1 - 30 years of rainfall 
to assess the potential short term PFAS release.

• Investigating the possible precursor transformation or substance transport within or on the 
glass frontsheet of solar panels, which might be the cause of the initial mass increase in the 
follow-up wipe test performed in this project. 
• Investigating more solar panel types to determine product differences in potential release. 
• Investigating the release rate trend of the wipe test method to determine if the assumption of 
linear release is plausible. 
• Modelling PFAS retention in the soil to better assess recipient contamination.

6. Conclusion 
The wipe test found PFHxA and PFPeA to be the only two PFAS of the tested 20 substances, which 
were consistently measured above the quantification limit and background concentrations in wipe 
samples from solar panel type A. The total potential release from the panel glass frontsheet was 
estimated at 54±18 ng/m2 PFHxA and 9.0±3.7 ng/m2 PFPeA. Thus, the potential release of PFAS 22 
was found to be 63±21 ng/m2 or 160±19 ng/panel and the potential release of PFAS 24, accounting 
for RPF, was found to be 1.0±0.34 ng PFOA-eq./m2 or 2.5±0.59 ng PFOA-eq./panel.  
In the simulated rainwater test for the two panel types A and B, the following PFAS were measured 
in concentrations at least twice as high as in the reference setup and were therefore concluded likely 
to be substances potentially released from solar panel frontsheets in the use phase: 6:2 FTS, PFBS, 
PFDA, PFHpA, PFHpS, PFHxA, PFNA, PFNS, PFOA, and PFPeA. For panel A the dominating 
PFAS was PFHxA (77%), while the PFAS most prevalent in the samples from panel B was PFOA 
(75%). From the simulated rainwater test was estimated a potential PFAS 22 release of 0.76 
ng/yr/panel for panel A and 0.69 ng/yr/panel for panel B corresponding to a potential PFAS 24 
release of 0.25 ng PFOA-eq./panel for panel A and 0.77 ng PFOA-eq./yr/panel for panel B. By 
comparing the potential PFAS releases from the wipe test and the simulated rainwater test was 
found that the total release estimated in the wipe test correspond to 210 and 46 years of release for 
panel A and B, respectively. Thus, the total potential PFAS release from panel A was found to be 
about five times as high as for panel B. However, within a panel lifetime of less than 50 years, the 
panels would release the same amount of PFAS. A follow-up wipe test was conducted 6 weeks after 
the initial wipe test and indicated that the release does approximately follow a linear rate. However, 
the follow-up wipe test found an initial increase in released PFAS mass following the 6 weeks break 
in wiping, which is likely a result of external contamination, but could be caused by processes in the 
panel frontsheet. 
The contaminant mass discharge calculation, based on the potential PFAS release from wipe test 
and the PFAS content of rain, found worst-case PFAS contaminations from a case solar park of up 
to 45 % of the 100 ng/L EQS in abstracted groundwater and up to 76 % of the 4.4 ng PFOA-eq./L 
EQS in small surface water streams. Thus, the potential contaminations remained below the EQSs. 
Moreover, the estimated potential PFAS release from the panels in the case solar park was found to 
contribute 11 – 36 times less PFAS than rainfall on the park area, even when assuming total release 
from the panels over only one year. Furthermore, the environmental hazard quotient for the worst-
==PAGE:77==
DTU                                                                                                                           Oline Mannstaedt 
10/12/2024                                                                                                                                s186091 
 
Page 61 of 82 
 
case potential PFAS release from the case solar park was found to be 0.0007, which is much lower 
than 1 and thusly indicates no environmental risk. 
Overall, the project finds PFAS release form solar panels to be very small when compared to 
environmental quality standards (EQSs), PNEC-values, and existing environmental background 
concentrations. However, as PFAS are persistent, toxic and defy conventional assessment of 
mobility and bioaccumulation, any emission should be monitored and reduced. Furthermore, the 
emergence of novel solar panel technologies incorporating PFAS as well as the development of new 
PFAS regulations and analytical methodologies should be considered when assessing the potential 
PFAS released from solar panels in the future. Still, in conclusion, this study finds that PFAS release 
from solar panels does not pose an environmental risk.
