Abstract 
In waste incineration plants, it is necessary to heat the flue gas to 220 °C from roughly 150 °C to 
guarantee enough NO conversion. Developing catalysts that provide a sufficient and stable SCR 
activity at 150 °C, in a realistic flue gas with 20 ppm SO2 and 15 vol.% water, was the motivation 
behind this thesis. Potassium deactivation in biomass plants, can also be alleviated by using such a 
technology. A total of 33 catalysts on TiO2 have been synthesized using four different synthesis 
methods: DP, WET, IW, or SG and characterized using TPR, TPD, IR, XPS, XRF, TGA, XRD and 
N2-physisorption. A further sulfuration treatment was performed on 6 of the catalysts.  
The TPR peak positions for the V-group catalysts were quite high temperature at roughly 450 
°C, with the MnLa/TiO2 and CuCe/TiO2 catalysts having more reasonable reduction temperatures of 
280 – 380 °C and 180 °C – 247 °C, respectively. No clear trend was found regarding acidity as 
measured with TPD, because the catalysts displayed quite varying results, with only small changes 
in composition. The TGA weight losses at 300 – 600 °C were highest for MnLa/TiO2 catalysts, which 
indicate a superior concentration of surface oxygen. The results showed that the MnLa/TiO2 catalysts 
yielded the highest activity, followed by CuCe/TiO2 and VCe/TiO2, with VLa/TiO2, NbLa/TiO2, 
TaLa/TiO2, and CuLa/TiO2 having little to no activity. 
XRF and XPS analysis, following sulfuration, found a more significant increase in sulfates for 
MnLa/TiO2 and CuCe/TiO2 than for the VCe/TiO2 catalysts. Further IR and the S/N relation from 
XPS, indicated the presence of metal sulfates such as MnSO4 and CuSO4. The BET surface area 
decreased roughly two times more following sulfuration, for the CuCe/TiO2 catalysts than for the 
VCe/TiO2 catalysts. The TPR profiles indicated that sulfates, and especially meatal sulfates, hinders 
the reduction process, particularly for the MnLa/TiO2 and CuCe/TiO2 catalysts. There was a 
significant reduction in SCR activity from both sulfuration and 15 vol.% water, where the 
combination of the two rendered the different catalysts near inactive.

Resumé 
I affaldsforbrændingsanlæg er det nødvendigt at varme røggassen op til 220 °C fra ca. 150 °C for at 
garantere den ønskede grad af NO omdannelse. Udviklingen af katalysatorer der leverer en 
tilstrækkelig og stabil SCR aktivitet ved 150 °C, i en realistisk røggas med 20 ppm SO2 og 15 vol.% 
vand, var motivationen bag dette speciale. Kalium forgiftning af SCR katalysatoren, kunne også 
begrænses ved at bruge denne teknologi. I alt 33 forskellige katalysatorer på TiO2 er blevet 
syntetiseret med fire forskellige syntese metoder: DP, WET, IW, eller SG og karakteriseret med TPR, 
TPD, IR, XPS, XRF, TGA, XRD og N2-fysisorption. Yderligere behandling med SO2 blev foretaget 
på 6 af katalysatorerne.  
TPR viste høje reduktionssignaler for V-gruppe katalysatorerne ved ca. 450 °C, imens 
MnLa/TiO2 og CuCe/TiO2 katalysatorerne demonstrerede mere passende profiler ved 280 °C – 380 
°C og 180 °C – 247 °C, henholdsvist. Ingen klar trend blev fundet i forhold til TPD surhedsgraden, 
fordi resultaterne varierede en del for katalysatorerne, på trods af kun mindre ændringer i 
sammensætning. TGA vægttab resultaterne ved 300 – 600 °C for højest for MnLa/TiO2 
katalysatorerne, som indikerer en højere koncentration overfladeoxygen. Resultaterne viste at 
MnLa/TiO2 gav den højeste aktivitet, efterfulgt af CuCe/TiO2 og VCe/TiO2, imens VLa/TiO2, 
NbLa/TiO2, TaLa/TiO2, og CuLa/TiO2 leverede den lavest aktivitet.  
XRF og XPS analyser, efter SO2 behandlingen, viste en mere signifikant stigning af sulfater for 
MnLa/TiO2 og CuCe/TiO2 end for VCe/TiO2 katalysatorerne. Videre IR og S/N forholdet fra XPS, 
indikerede tilstedeværelsen af metal sulfater som MnSO4 og CuSO4. BET overfladearealet faldt 
omtrent to gange mere, efter SO2 behandlingen, for CuCe/TiO2 end for VCe/TiO2. TPR profilerne 
indikerede at sulfater, og specielt metal sulfater, forhindrede reduktionens processen, der var specielt 
udtalt for MnLa/TiO2 and CuCe/TiO2. Der var en signifikant reduktion i SCR aktivitet fra både SO2 
behandlingen og 15 vol.% vand, imens kombinationen af de to resulterede i en næsten fuldstændig 
deaktivering af katalysatorerne.

 Introduction 
 
 NOx Formation and the Environmental Effect of NOx Emissions 
 
Rapid urbanization, population growth, and increasing development of industry, has led to serious air 
pollution which has given rise to a growing environmental awareness, and subsequent stringent 
regulations that prompts the development of improved pollution abatement technologies [1]. A very 
notable air contaminant is nitrogen oxides, NOx, which has a known role in photochemical smog, 
haze, greenhouse effect, acid rain, and ozone depletion [2]. The reactions related to NOx pollution are 
illustrated in Figure 1.1, the formation of nitric acid is among the most notable. 
 
Figure 1.1: Chemical transformations of NOx relevant for air pollution. Adapted from [3]. 
 
The emissions of NOx mostly consist of NO; however, NO will react with O2 forming NO2, thus the 
emphasis of this work will be on these two gases. Some NOx emissions are a natural occurrence 
through volcanic activity, lightning, and oxidation phenomena, though they only account for one 
fourth of total emissions [2]. Figure 1.2 shows the distribution of man-made NOx in Denmark in 2013, 
where there was a total emission of roughly 124,000 tons down from  an estimated 226,000 tons in 
2003 [4]. The transport sector constitutes the different mobile sources such as ships, trains, airplanes, 
and cars, and contribute a significant amount of NOx. Stationary sources are defined as power plants 
burning coal, waste, biomass or natural gas, and some industrial processes such as cement and nitric 
acid production. The duty on NOx emissions pr. kg was 5.1 DKK in 2017 according to the Danish

200 mg/Nm3 when firing coal according to EU [6].

Heterocyclic organic compounds which could be based on pyridine or pyrrole has nitrogen bound 
into the structure, and can be found in shale oil, heavy oil and coal [8]. Natural gas also contains 
varying, and sometimes significant amounts of N2, due to mixing of the atmospheric nitrogen with 
the natural gas. As can be seen by the large variation in nitrogen content in biomass, it depends on 
the type of fuel, with straw containing more nitrogen than wood and bark. Leather waste have a 
nitrogen %-wt. of 12, due to the high concentration of structural protein. 
Prompt NOx is based on the reaction of hydrocarbon radicals with atmospheric nitrogen resulting 
in the production of HCN, which then reacts to NOx through a series of gas phase reactions. The 
contribution is generally insignificant, as the formation mostly happens at fuel-rich, low temperature 
zones with short residence time, and can thus be abated through appropriate combustion control.

 NOx Formation Limiting and Non-Catalytic Removal 
 
 Primary Measures in Stationary Sources 
 
Primary measures or combustion control are technologies that try to limit the formation of NOx in 
the combustion process. Either through the removal of nitrogen from liquid fuels such as heavy oils, 
or through the control of the combustion process. The latter works by controlling, and slowing the 
rate of which air and fuel is mixed and burned, by burner optimization. The reason is to make sure 
that the combustion happens under reducing conditions, forming N2, instead of oxidizing conditions, 
where excess oxygen facilitates the formation of NOx [3]. It can be done by changing the shape and 
formation of the flame, using plates to control the airflow, allowing a more elongated flame, which 
result in a larger surface area and decreased temperatures, illustrated in Figure 2.1. 
 
Figure 2.1: An illustration of the General Electrics combustion control technology. Taken from 
[11]. 
 
It is possible to retrofit the burners in existing plants and the technology can accomplish a NOx 
reduction of 40 % [3]. There can however be problems with unburnt carbon in the ash, resulting in a 
product that can be unsuited for cement production and poorer fuel utilization. Steps should also be 
taken to control the CO levels. Limiting of peak temperatures, and residence time are also tools that 
can help abate NOx, which can be done through the recirculation of the flue gas or by injecting water 
or steam. There are however also problems with efficiency loss, CO and smoke formation [10].

Primary measures should be used in combination with other NOx abatement methods to reach the

 Selective Non-Catalytic Reduction 
 
A simple post-combustion method is the selective non-catalytic reduction (SNCR), based on a 
nitrogen based reduction agent, such as urea or ammonia, to reduce the NOx to N2. The agent is 
injected into the upper part of the boiler, meeting the flue gas at a temperature between 800 and 1200 
°C. The reduction levels can become upwards of 50 %, without the use of catalyst, with an easy 
installation and a comparatively low price [12]. The temperature interval is important to make sure 
that the reaction is fast enough to prevent ammonia slip, and low enough to prevent the oxidation of 
ammonia to NO, as illustrated in Figure 2.2. Ammonia slip leads to increased pollution, and can cause 
the formation of ammonia salts in the system. It should be noted however that the precise interval is 
based on the system geometry, flue gas composition and velocity gradient [12]. 
 
 
Figure 2.2: Effect of temperature on NOx reduction and ammonia slip. Data from [13], no right side 
y-axis scale was present in the original plot.

The reduction is premised on the creation of the amidogen NH2 radical, which is highly reactive 
towards NO. It is nonetheless necessary to control the temperature interval, to make sure that 
unwanted side reactions of NH2 such as the formation of NO, is suppressed [12]. Additives such as 
CH4, H2 and H2O2 can increase the temperature interval, mostly by creating new paths for the 
formation of reactive radicals, such as NH2.  
The foremost disadvantage of the SNCR technology is the limited removal level, especially 
considering progressively severe demands for NOx removal. SNCR can however be used in 
combination with other NOx removal technologies to achieve the proper removal.  
 
 Selective Catalytic Reduction 
 
 Introduction 
 
Selective catalytic reduction (SCR) is a post-flue gas treatment method that is widely used in mobile 
and stationary applications, based on the reduction of NOx with ammonia or urea over a catalyst at 
temperatures between 150 to 450 °C. The SCR technology can reach the desired conversion, 
especially coupled with primary measures. The reason is that with SCR it is possible to control the 
degree of conversion through the tweaking of reaction temperature, gas flowrate and catalyst loading. 
The foremost desired reactions are 3.1, 3.2 and 3.3, where NO or NO2 is reduced to N2 [14]. The 
reaction scheme is theorized to propagate through reaction 3.3 that is commonly known as the fast 
SCR reaction, because of the high reaction rate at even low temperature, which is one of the reasons 
why preoxidation of 50 % of the NO to NO2 in a ‘CombiCat’ is highly interesting [15]. The reduction 
can happen through a Langmuir Hinshelwood (LH) mechanism, where the reaction between 
NH3/NH4+ and NO happens as two adsorbed species, which Liu et al conclude is the predominant 
mechanism at low temperature (T < 200 °C) on an iron/ titania catalyst [16]. They concluded that for 
the high temperature SCR reaction (T > 200 °C), that it is the Eley-Rideal (ER) mechanism that 
governs, which is the reaction of gas phase NO with adsorbed NH3/NH4+.

 Location of the SCR Unit 
 
When using the SCR technology for stationary applications, it is paramount to optimize the 
positioning of the catalyst to make sure that catalyst deactivation, and pressure drops are minimal. 
Figure 3.1 illustrate the different positions of the SCR unit, which can be either high-dust, low-dust, 
or tail-end. The electrostatic precipitator (ESP) filters out the dust particles, while the flue gas 
desulphurization (FGD) unit removes sulphur down to a concentration of approximately 20 ppm of 
SO2 [10].  
 
==PAGE:15==
Selective Catalytic Reduction 
8 
 
 
Figure 3.1: The process layout for the conventional placement of the SCR unit in boiler-based power 
plants. Taken from  [17]. 
 
The high dust position is commonly used in coal, gas and biomass based power plants, because of a 
low concentration of any alkaline metals such as potassium, and other catalyst poisons such as arsenic 
and hydrogen chloride [10]. The catalyst used is typically V2O5/WO3 on anatase TiO2, which is a 
catalyst with good SO2 resistance. The high temperature of approx. 350 °C benefits the catalyst 
activity, and a low active metal loading of around 1 wt.% V2O5, helps prevent unwanted side 
reactions, such as N2O formation, unselective oxidation of NH3 and oxidation of SO2. Steps should 
be taken to prevent clogging of the catalyst, by using steam soot blowers or sonic horns to blow away 
dust particles, while also keeping the cell density low to decrease the clogging frequency [17].   
In biomass based power plants there are problems with the high concentration of potassium and 
sodium in the biomass, especially in straw. In waste incineration, the story is similar, because of the 
random composition of the waste, which increases the possibility of catalyst poisons such as metals 
from batteries. There are two possibilities to prevent fast catalyst deactivation. The first is the co-
combustion with coal or coal ash to lower the concentration of catalyst poisons in the fuel mixture 
[18]. The addition of coal ash adorbs much of the alkaline metals, making the high-dust position 
feasible. The second method is the tail-end position, where SCR is positioned after the ESP and the 
FGD, making the flue gas practically free of poisons [10]. There are however economic problems

with this position, because of the need to reheat the flue gas with a steam reheater or gas burner to 
facilitate the necessary catalytic activity. The flue gas temperature is only around 150 °C when it 
leaves the dry FGD unit, and needs to be reheated to 200 – 250 °C, continually, and heating large volumes 
of flue gas is expensive [14]. This is the reason for the increasing focus on low temperature SCR (LT 
SCR) catalysts, which can operate sufficiently at 150 °C, while being resistant to SO2 and water. 
 
 Designing a LT SCR Catalyst 
 
A catalytic cycle in heterogeneous catalysis can be divided in five consecutive steps; diffusion of 
reactants to surface, adsorption of reactants, surface reaction processes, desorption of products and 
diffusion of reactants from the surface. The reduction of NO at 150 °C is governed by the surface 
reaction processes, mainly because the kinetics are lower at this temperature than at 300 °C, however 
effects from adsorption and desorption occur. Thus, the overall activity at 150 °C is governed by the 
intrinsic activity and to some extend pore diffusion, which can be optimized through choice of active 
metal, oxidation state, synthesis, support, and co-dopants. The activity also needs to high enough 
throughout the entire operational period. 
 
 Some Important LT SCR Parameters 
 
A superior LT SCR catalyst ought to have the following characteristics based on the recent review 
paper by Lee & Bai [14]: 
1. High BET surface area, with favorable pore size distribution. This parameter is affected by 
mostly by the support, however metal concentration, metal precursors, synthesis and 
calcination temperature should also be considered.  
2. The metal oxides should be in their most active state, often non-crystalline. Good distribution 
of the most active metal oxide oxidation states, which depends on the metal oxide in question. 
A further discussion on this can be found in sections 4.2 and 4.3.  
3. High surface acidity to allow the binding of NH3 with contributions both from the metal oxides 
and from the support.  
4. A high concentration of surface adsorbed oxygen, which is oxygen that is bound to the surface 
either in/on the support or the metal oxide, partaking in the oxidation of ammonia and 
hydrogen abstraction, among other things [19]. The fast replenishment of the surface oxygen 
with gas phase oxygen, is also important. 
==PAGE:17==
Designing a LT SCR Catalyst 
10 
 
5. Low metal oxide reduction temperature, that facilitates the redox cycle at low temperature, 
i.e. that a metal oxide with a reduction temperature at 800 °C, is unlikely to have noteworthy 
activity at 150 °C. There are indications that doping with other metal oxides such as Fe2O3, 
can lower the reduction temperature. It was reported by Putluru et al [20] that Fe lowered the 
peak position on a MnTi catalyst, hinting at a clear improvement in the dispersion of 
manganese oxides together with a higher concentration of MnO2. 
6. Superior resistance towards SO2 adsorption and SO2 based reactions that can lead to 
deactivation and blocking of active sites. Alternatively, easy decomposition of metal oxide 
sulfates and ammonium sulfates is another possible route for catalyst regeneration.  
7. Long term mechanical, chemical and thermal stability in the reaction setup, to prevent the 
need for frequent exchange of the catalyst. Further remarks can be found in section 4.4. 
 
 The Oxidation States of Manganese 
 
Manganese based LT SCR catalysts have been among the most successful, because of the superior 
intrinsic activity at 150 °C under SO2 free conditions [14]. The metal is also interesting because of its 
high abundance and the fact that it is environmentally benign. A reason for the activity of Mn can be 
found in its multiple relevant oxidation states: Mn2+, Mn3+ and Mn4+. There is a discussion on which 
of them is the most active. Fang et al tested unsupported MnxOx and found that Mn2O3 and Mn3O4 
was the most active at 160 °C, with conversion of 44.44 % and 56.43 respectively, while MnO2 had 
a conversion of 20.91% [21]. Thirupati et al also tested pure Mn oxides and found that MnO2 reached 
100 % conversion at 220 °C while Mn2O3 and Mn3O4 reached 60 % and 25 % respectively [22].  
Interestingly, there was a strong effect of temperature on which oxidation state is the most 
active, because they also found that Mn3O4 was the most active at 160 °C with a conversion of about 
72 %, while MnO2 and Mn2O3 had conversions of 60 % and 52 % respectively [22]. It should be 
noted however that the gas hourly space velocity was only 8000 h-1, while their other tests was 
performed at 50.000 h-1, indicating low overall catalyst activity. 
 
 Effect of Crystalline Species of V2O5 and MnxOx 
 
For V2O5 based catalyst there should be a monolayer of polymeric and monomeric species, with a 
high dispersion to prevent the formation of crystallites of V2O5. The polymeric species is 10 times 
more active than the monomeric species, while crystallites of V2O5 decreases the selectivity of NO 
==PAGE:18==
Frederik Bojsen Kværndrup 
 
11 
 
oxidation dramatically [23]. A low vanadium loading is thus required to stay at monolayer coverage 
or below, which also helps prevent unwanted oxidation of SO2. 
For Mn based catalyst there are indications that the presence of crystalline MnxOy, has a 
negative impact on the intrinsic activity of the catalyst [24]. Crystalline MnxOy was found when the 
MnFe/TiO2 was prepared with the impregnation (IMP) synthesis method, however not with the 
deposition precipitation (DP) with a conversion of about 38 % for the former and about 75 % for the 
latter both at 150 °C [24]. A too high calcination temperature seems to promote a lower surface area 
and increased crystalline MnxOy.  
 
 Mechanical and Thermal Stress  
 
Vibrations can lead to fracture, while abrasion from pressure surges, loosening of the catalyst bed, 
and abrasive dust can damage the catalyst surface [25]. Thermal stress from rapid temperature 
changes and problems with thermal expansions, can also lower the catalyst lifetime significantly [25].  
A honeycomb monolith or plate setup is conventionally used, due to the small pressure loss compared 
to when pellets are used in a packed bed reactor. The catalyst lifetime depends on the specific 
environment, however an operation period of around 24.000 hours or about 3 years is common [26].  
 
 Plausible Mechanisms for MnxOx/TiO2 and VxOx-TiO2  
 
The focus will now solely be on LT SCR catalysts, from this point on. To better understand the 
complex nature of the SCR reaction, it is relevant to analyze two of the proposed reaction mechanisms 
for the MnxOx/TiO2 and VxOx-TiO2 catalysts.   
 
 Investigation Into the MnxOx/TiO2 SCR Mechanism 
 
Ettireddy et al made an investigation into the mechanism behind the reduction of nitric oxide, over a 
MnxOx/TiO2 catalyst tested at 175 °C, analyzed with transient isotopic labeling and in situ FT-IR 
[19]. The species changed was 16O2 to 18O2, 14NO to 15NO and 14NH3 to 15NH3, and made it possible 
to investigate the intermediate species that emerged from the catalyst bed. A quadrupole mass 
analyzer was used to scan specific mass to charge ratios e.g. 16O2 (m/e = 32), 16O18O (m/e = 34) and 
18O2 (m/e = 36), with similar products and reactants for nitrogen such as N16O (m/e = 30) and 14N15N 
(m/e = 29). Interferences for ammonia, water and nitric oxide was corrected for by calibration 
experiments.

The product N2 and active intermediates are made from the combination of ammonia and nitric 
oxide, which was shown by using labeled 15NO and unlabeled 14NH3 giving 15N-14N. This means that 
NO and NH3 adsorbs to the surface, either through the LH or the ER mechanisms. Ettireddy et al

significantly different results, and it is therefore difficult to say whether Ettireddy et al are mistaken. 
 
 Fast SCR and Standard SCR Mechanism for VOx-TiO2 
 
A proposal for the complete reaction mechanism for standard and fast SCR on a VOx/TiO2 was done 
by Arnarson et al [29]. The work was done on the basis of earlier proposed reaction mechanisms [30], 
[31] and tried to illuminate the underlying reduction and re-oxidation mechanisms in the redox 
process. Low coverage, monomeric VO3H species on a (001) single crystal of anatase was the basis 
for the density functional theory calculations (DFT). A real multifaceted catalyst, with defects and 
possible polymeric structures, would complicate the calculations. It was possible to calculate the 
enthalpy and Gibbs free energy change for the different adsorptions, desorptions and reactions, as a 
function of temperature.

 Water Deactivation 
 
In any waste incineration, plant or biomass fueled power plant, there is a significant concentration of 
water in the flue gas, about 10 – 20 % [32]. This water will adsorb on the acid sites on the catalyst 
and block the adsorption of NH3, thereby preventing the SCR reaction on that specific acid site, until 
the water desorbs again. The severity of the competitive adsorption is a function of temperature, 
because the loss of activity is more significant at 140 °C than at 200 °C [32] as illustrated in Figure 
6.1.  
 
Figure 6.1: Summary of the effect of water on the SCR activity on MnFeTi and HMO-MnO2 catalysts, 
compared with a commercial VWT. reference at 220 °C (horizontal line). Adapted from [32]. 
 
Similar results by Lian et al [33], show their Mn2Nb1Ox catalyst decreasing in conversion from 100 
to 50 % at 150 °C at only 5 vol. % water vapour, while at 200 °C and 5 vol. % water vapour the 
conversion was largely unaffected. However, because the catalyst was not sufficiently stressed at 
either temperature i.e. well below 100 % conversion, it is not possible to isolate the effect of water 
adsorption.  
The adsorption of water is generally perceived as an associative adsorption, meaning that the gas 
molecule adsorbs without fragmentation, which results in a reversible inhibition and results generally

Frederik Bojsen Kværndrup

show a reversible, though significant, inhibition [34], [14]. A study indicate however, that a 
dissociative adsorption of water that can result in the deposition of hydroxyl groups that block acid 
sites up to 250 °C [35]. In wet conditions for the V2O5/TiO2 catalyst, there is a possibility of changing 
some Lewis acid sites to Brønsted acid sites, through the adsorption of water [36]. Figure 6.2 
illustrates this phenomenon. It is difficult to know the precise effect of this change, because ammonia 
activation can occur on Lewis sites with coordinated ammonia and/or amide species, and on Brønsted 
sites with ammonium ions or NH3+ cations [36].  
 
Figure 6.2: Creation of Brønsted acid sites from surface vanadyl centers/Lewis acid sites by water

The adsorption of a gas is an exothermic reaction, because of the negative entropy from the removal 
of a degree of freedom of movement. Which means that the desorption of water is endothermic. The 
well-known Gibbs free energy equation (∆𝐺= ∆𝐻−𝑇∆𝑆), gives a good indication of why the 
adsorption of water is a bigger problem at temperatures below 200 °C, than at temperatures higher 
than 200 °C. The adsorption of water vapor and any gas typically, becomes negligible at elevated 
temperatures, because the ∆𝐺 of adsorption becomes less negative and because the rate of desorption 
increases exponentially with increasing temperature.  
Preventing the adsorption of water on acid sites at 150 °C is difficult to say the least, suggesting 
that the best way to maintain proper SCR activity at high water vapor concentration, is to make the 
catalyst even more active with a high concentration of acid sites. This is assuming that the competitive 
adsorption is the only cause of inhibition. For the VOx/TiO2 based catalyst there is a crucial step, a 
‘redox effect’ where water desorbs from the structure as described in Section 5.2, which limits the 
effectiveness of just increasing the number of acid sites. The good question is whether there are 
similar ‘redox effects’ for other types of catalyst.

 SO2 Deactivation  
 
The concentration of SO2 in the flue gas, after the FGD unit in the tail end position, is typically around 
20 ppm. This does not sound like a significant amount, there are however substantial problems with 
deactivation of the SCR catalyst at low temperature. The reason can be found in the blocking of active 
sites by ammonium sulfates such as NH4HSO4 and (NH4)2SO4 and metal sulfates such as 
MnSO4∙H2O. The deposition also reduces the active surface area through the blocking of pores. The 
deactivation is often irreversible, with a significant lowering of activity of about 50 % which stays 
even after removal of the SO2 [20], [24], [32], [37]–[41]. The competitive adsorption between SO2 
and NO also inhibits the SCR reaction, with at least 20 % based on the work by Jiang et al on their 
Fe-Mn/TiO2 catalyst, where they saw a reduction in NO conversion from 100 % to 80 % at 150 °C in 
only 33 min. They also state that the NO adsorption was inhibited by SO2, while NH4+ formation was 
promoted by an increased concentration of Brønsted acid sites. More significant reduction from the 
competitive adsorption, could have been seen if the catalyst was properly stressed, i.e. below 100 % 
conversion.  
Temperature programmed reduction (TPR) reveal a significant change in the profile of an SO2 
treated MnFe/TiO2 catalyst with a change in peaks positions from 250 – 350 °C to 400 – 550 °C in 
[32]. This indicates that the thermal decomposition of MnSO4 and (NH4)2Mn2(SO4)3 that cover the 
surface, is necessary to allow the reduction of the MnOx [32]. The number of acid sites is lowered 
because of the blocking effect, however there is a slight increase in acid sites after 250 °C because 
the SO42- facilitate the binding of NH3 [14].  
Wang et al made an investigation into the effect of sulfurization on a Fe-W catalyst [39]. They 
concluded that the addition of SO2 inhibits the formation of NO2, limiting the fast SCR pathway and 
LT SCR activity. The reason for the inhibition can be found in the formation of ammonium nitrate 
which is promoted by the sulfate. Their proposed scheme is illustrated in Figure 6.3.

 Regeneration of SO2 Deactivated SCR Catalyst 
 
Long term stability is not always an option, especially because of SO2 oxidation, which prompts the 
development of efficient regeneration procedures.   
 
 Stability of Metal Sulfates 
 
The combination of SO2 and water can evidently have a substantial inhibiting effect on the LT SCR 
catalyst. Water inhibition is reversible and there should not be any increased effect over time from 
water. The opposite holds for sulfate deposition and a regeneration scheme for the catalyst should be 
developed. The main reason for the use of LT SCR catalyst is the lack of any flue gas reheating, 
which means that it would be favorable for economic and environmental reasons. However, current 
available LT SCR suffers from activity inhibiting poisons, and cheap, fast, and efficient regeneration 
methods are desirable. 
It is industrial practice for V2O5 based catalysts to periodically heat to 350 °C, which can help 
remove deposited NH4HSO4 and recover most of the activity. With ammonia sulfates removed that 
still leaves the metal sulfates. Metal sulfates can be quite stable depending on the catalyst used, with 
decomposition temperatures that are much higher; Ce(SO4)2 ≈ 380 °C, FeSO4 ≈ 530 °C, CuSO4 ≈ 600 
°C, Ce2(SO4)3 ≈ 650 °C, MnSO4 ≈ 700 °C and La2SO4 ≈ 800 °C [43]. Even if it would be economically 
reasonable to heat a Mn based catalyst to 700 °C, it would still present problems with catalyst 
sintering and catalyst degradation. The temperature treatment should also be below the original 
calcination temperature as a minimum.  
 
 Reconstructive Transformation of Anatase to Rutile 
 
Another significant challenge for the possible regeneration, is the reconstructive transformation of 
anatase to the thermodynamically stable rutile. The transformation involves the breaking of bonds 
and reforming of bonds, which result in an overall volume contraction of 8 % and a reduction in 
surface area [44]. The reported transition onset temperature at ambient pressure is roughly 600 °C, 
with the phase diagram for anatase to rutile presented in Appendix A. The kinetics promoted by the 
increased presence and amount of oxygen vacancies. The transition can thus be promoted to lower 
temperatures by the solid solution of small cations with low valence (<4), such as CuO and VO2, 
because of the stabilization of the charge balance and decreased lattice stress [44]. Inhibition occurs 
with large cations with high valence (>4) such as CeO2 and WO3, that increases the lattice stress [44].

Interstitial solid solubility might occur on the other hand, which generally would increase lattice 
stress.   
 
 Possible Regeneration Methods  
 
Putluru et al investigated the possible regeneration of a deposition precipitated MnFe/TiO2 that had 
undergone sulfurization [32]. They found that heating to 400 °C or heating to 350 °C in 1 vol.% H2 
did not regenerate the catalyst. There were some effect of water washing and 1 molar ammonium 
carbamate washing, especially with subsequent heating, which makes sense since MnSO4 and 
ammonium sulfates are water soluble. Water washing resulted in significant Mn leaching because of 
the solubility of the Mn-sulfates, while MnxOx are insoluble. The problem could be alleviated 
somewhat by increasing the pH.  However, it is still difficult to implement the base washing on an 
industrial scale. Figure 7.1 shows the effect of the regeneration methods.

ultrasonic vibration showed good performance [45]. Ten deactivation/regeneration cycles were

indicating that some MnSO4 and Ce(SO4)2 must have been dissolved.   
A regeneration scheme by Shi et al, on a CeO2 catalyst which was calcined at 550 °C, proposed 
a thermal regeneration at 850 °C [46], after sulfuration in 500 ppm at 500 °C for 12 hours. They found 
the same 100 % NOx conversion at 350 °C after regeneration from 70 % NOx conversion at 350 °C 
for the sulfated sample, and concluded that the method was sound. There are however evident 
problems with this statement, because the conversion is so high at this temperature, it is not possible 
to isolate the effect of sintering from the 850 °C treatment. At 200 °C however it is evident that the 
method is poor, with conversions of the following: Fresh catalyst = 58 %, SO2 poisoned = 8 % and 
regenerated catalyst = 18 %. The surface area showed a similar change, with 72.1 m2/g for the fresh, 
1.6 m2/g for the poisoned and 11.8 m2/g for the regenerated. The combination of sintering and pore 
blockage from sulfates, leads to a sharp decrease in surface area. The poisoned catalyst is stated as 
being a poreless material, while there is only a meager improvement after the regeneration. Assuming 
that the method regenerated the sample, heating a big volume of catalyst pellets to 850 °C is also 
extremely expensive and impractical.  
Kumar et al investigated the use of different chemical reductants to regenerate a Cu-zeolite 
catalyst, in a heavy-duty diesel vehicle context [47]. They found that thermal regeneration needed 
650 °C to reach a similar NO conversion as the fresh catalyst, which they deemed as too high for 
practical use. To induce a local reducing environment, they added 1000 ppm short chain alkenes C3H6 
that lowered the needed temperature to 550 °C. The thermal treatment at 550 °C without any C3H6 
gave a conversion of 82.7 % at 260 °C, while the conversion after C3H6 treatment at 550 °C was 88.6 
%, giving a 7 % increase in conversion. They also made an improvement through a 4-step protocol, 
however it is unlikely that a similar system is feasible in a power plant. It would be relevant to test 
the use of C3H6 on other types of catalysts, to investigate if there is a similar effect. The method might 
not be economically reasonable when used in a power plant and would have to be verified.

 X-Ray Diffraction 
 
X-ray diffraction (XRD) uses monochromatic x-rays to make constructive interference with a 
crystalline sample, when Bragg’s Law is satisfied given in equation 10.1. The analysis can determine 
the bulk composition of any crystalline species in a sample. The detection limit is 5 nm in diameter, 
which means that very small crystalline particles won’t be detected. XRD is a fast and powerful 
method to control whether, the synthesis, catalyst loading or calcination treatment, resulted in any 
unwanted crystalline metal oxide phases. In a word, it is generally only the support that should show 
an XRD signal, anything otherwise would indicate poor dispersion of metal oxides and possible low 
surface area. An example of low activity due to poor dispersion can be found in previous work, where 
a MnLaOx on anatase catalyst in the preliminary project, displayed a measurable crystalline LaOx 
phase at 30 mol% La, with subsequent lowering of activity [34].

 X-Ray Fluorescence 
 
X-ray fluorescence (XRF) uses the characteristic X-ray following ionization with a high-energy beam 
to calculate the concentration in arbitrary units ‘counts pr. second’ of the different elements in the 
sample. The inner electrons in shells M, L or K in each element can be excited to a higher energy 
state using a high-energy X-ray source. An electron of higher energy will fill the vacancy and emit 
the excess energy as fluorescent radiation, and it is radiation that is characteristic for each element. 
The wavelength can be calculated from Planck’s law because the energy difference is known. An

 Thermogravimetric Analysis 
 
Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) is a thermal analysis method that can give information about 
physical and chemical phenomena of the sample. It is done by heating the sample in a crucible with 
a fixed temperature ramp to a set temperature upwards of 1000 - 2000 °C, depending on the crucible 
used, while giving the weight loss. This is done in a controlled atmosphere, most often in nitrogen to 
prevent oxidation of the sample. The relevant TGA plots for the purpose of heterogeneous catalyst 
development will show the weight divided by the starting weight after removal of trapped water 
against the temperature. The surface oxygen will desorb at the temperature interval of 300 – 650 °C, 
which could mean that a high weight loss in that interval indicate a superior SCR catalyst [24]. 
 
 Temperature Programmed Desorption 
 
The temperature programmed desorption (TPD) of probe molecules, can be used to measure the basic 
or acid properties of different catalyst surfaces, among other things. The sample is at first pretreated 
in helium with a ramp to the chosen set point, which could be 450 °C, to desorb possible pre-adsorbed 
gas and remove water. What follows is cooling to 100 °C, which is the temperature where the rate of 
NH3 adsorption has its maximum. The adsorbent gas is then added, which could be NH3 in He, to 
==PAGE:39==
Characterization Methods 
32 
 
facilitate the adsorption on acid sites on the catalyst surface. Flushing with He is next, which clears 
the instrument of any remaining non-adsorbed gas in the instrument and removal of any physisorbed 
gas, meaning that it is only the chemisorbed NH3 that is measured. The final step is the desorption of 
the probe molecule, which is done by heating the sample in He to the set point at a certain temperature 
ramp, while measuring the amount of evolved gas. The rate of desorption increases with temperature 
and goes through a maximum and ending a zero when the surface is cleared of adsorbate [59].  
The detector could be a thermal conductivity detector (TCD), that can measure the change in 
gas composition using the changing thermal conductivity (TC) of the gas, which results in a resistance 
change in an electrically heated filament that is translated to a voltage change giving a TCD signal. 
H2 and He has a higher TC on a mass basis, than nearly anything else, which means that the resistance  
in the filament will rise when their concentrations lowers, making the TCD detector a universal 
detector [59]. Quantitative measurements and acid site concentration determinations, are performed 
on the basis of a properly calibrated detector. The area under the TPD curve is proportional to the 
moles of NH3, while the position of the peak maximum indicates the activation energy of desorption 
and the strength of adsorbate binding to the surface.  
 
 Temperature Programmed Reduction 
 
Transition metal oxides can possess multiple stable oxidation states, and this feature facilitate electron 
exchanges between reactants and surface-active sites. The metal oxides on the surface is reduced and 
subsequently oxidized by surface oxygen or lattice oxygen, continually. The reduction should occur 
close to the reaction temperature interval to enable catalytic activity. Knowing the reduction 
temperature of the constituents in the catalyst is relevant, because a low reduction temperature hints 
at good activity at low temperature [20]. The temperature programmed reduction (TPR) method, 
which uses a similar setup as TPD, employ H2 and a linear temperature ramp to analyze the 
reducibility of the metal oxides in the sample. The set point should be high enough to facilitate the 
reduction of the relevant metal oxide species, and an educated guess can be made from literature 
values, however the method is normally limited to 1100 °C.  
The TCD detector can be calibrated to enable the calculation of the total hydrogen consumption, 
which can indicate the availability of the metal oxides. Because H2 is used, there should generally be 
no diffusion limitations, signifying that the effect of pore blockage is often negligible, however it 
depends on the temperature ramp. When a metal oxide is reduced through multiple oxidation states, 
==PAGE:40==

multiple peaks will form, signifying that the reduction of Mn2O3 to MnO to Mn will give a bimodal 
hydrogen reduction consumption pattern.  
 
 X-Ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy 
 
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is a powerful surface-sensitive quantitative spectroscopic 
technique, capable of measuring the elemental composition at the parts pr. million range in a depth 
of 0 – 10 nm. XPS can also be used to compare the relative amount of labile surface oxygen, even 
different oxidation states of metals and sulfur compounds can be quantized [60]. The sample is first 
subjected to high vacuum (P ~ 10-7 mbar) and then irradiated by a focused high energy x-ray beam of 
1486 eV from an Al Kα x-ray [60]. Core electrons will then be photo emitted, called photoelectrons, 
and move through the sample and from the surface at an angle reaching a collection lens. What 
follows could be a time of flight kinetic energy analyzer, culminating in an electron detector that 
counts the electrons, giving an XPS signal. The energy of the incident x-ray is known and the kinetic 
energy of the photoelectron is measured, and it from this that is possible to calculate the binding 
energy of the emitted electron, given by equation 10.5.

Here Ebinding is the binding energy of the electron, Ephoton is the energy of X-ray photons, Ekinetic is the 
measured kinetic energy and Φ is the work function which depends on the detector [60]. The equation 
is thus based on the conservation of energy. Each element gives a characteristic set of binding energy 
signals or XPS peaks and it is from that information that the relative surface composition can be

materials, as a function of energy, often referred to as the universal curve.   
 
 Infrared Spectroscopy 
 
The SO2 treated catalysts was analyzed with Fourier transformed infrared spectrometry (FT-IR), that 
allows for the recording of the whole infrared spectrum. The technique is based on the interaction of 
infrared radiation with matter, through the adsorption of the radiation into the bonds of the molecular 
structure. The adsorption happens at specific resonant frequencies, which is a frequency that matches 
==PAGE:41==

the vibrational frequency. The position and intensity of the vibrational band, makes it possible to 
identify the functional groups in the sample. There can, however be problems with overlap in signal, 
especially from water. The method can consequently then be used to determine what kind of sulfates, 
that has been formed in the catalyst. Nonlinear molecules can vibrate in 3N – 6 degrees of vibrational 
modes, while linear molecules can vibrate in 3N – 5 degrees of vibrational modes. Which means that 
sulfate has 9 different modes. Examples of vibrational modes is the stretching of the bond, such as 
the S-O stretch or the bending of the bond, being the S-O bend.   
 
 Characterization Methods and Parameters 
 
The NH3-TPD analysis used a Micromeritics Autochem-II instrument, where 100 mg of sample was 
placed in a u-shaped quartz tube on top of a layer of glass wool covered with glass wool. Pretreatment 
in flowing 25 mL/min He at 450 °C for one hour, followed by cooling to 100 °C and treatment with 
15 mL/min 1 vol.% NH3 in He mixed with 25 mL/min He at 100 °C. This is the step where the NH3 
adsorbs to the acid sites on the catalyst. The next step involves flushing with mL/min He for one hour 
at 100 °C. The desorption and final step in the experiment was done through heating of the sample 
from 100 °C to 450 °C with a temperature ramp of 10 °C min-1 under a flow of 25 mL/min He.   
H2-TPR were conducted on the same Micromeritics Autochem-II, where 100 mg of sample was 
placed in a u-shaped quartz tube on top of a layer of glass wool covered with glass wool. The 
pretreatment was done by flushing 50 mL/min 5 % H2 in Ar at 50 °C for 5 min. The reduction of the 
sample was done in a 50 mL/min 5 % H2 in Ar, with a temperature ramp of 10 °C min-1 from 50 to 
650 °C or 50 to 850 - 950 °C for some of the Nb and Ta based catalysts. To monitor the change in 
hydrogen concentration in the exit stream a thermal conductivity detector (TCD) was used. Because 
the equipment was not calibrated it was not possible to calculate the H2 consumption, however it was 
possible to get the signal vs temperature which gives important information about change in reduction 
temperature following doping.  
The TGA were conducted on a Mettler Toledo TGA/DSC 1 SF instrument, where 25 mg of the 
sample was placed in a 70 µL alumina crucible. The sample was heated at 10 °C min-1 to 100 °C and 
kept there for 30 min to remove excess water, followed by heating to 600 °C with a rate of 10 °C min-
1 under a N2 flow of 20 mL min-1.  
XRD were performed on a Huber 670 powder diffractometer utilizing Cu Ka radiation in a 2°- 
80° in steps of 0.02°. Peak analysis was performed with Search Match.

 Dry Activity Test 
 
The activity tests were performed in a fixed bed reactor at atmospheric pressure, where the sample 
was kept at 150 °C for the entire experiment. Tests without water vapour or SO2 was performed on 
50 mg of 180 – 300 µm catalyst particles, with a total gas flow of roughly 320 cm3 min-1. The inlet 
concentrations for these tests were: NO = 500 ppm, NH3 = 500 ppm, O2 = 4.5 vol.% and He as balance 
gas.

Activity Test

 The NH3-NOx Gas Analyzer 
 
To monitor the NO concentration continually a Thermo Electron Model 17C chemiluminescent NH3-
NOx gas analyzer. The analyzer works by oxidizing the NO to NO2 in the presence of O3, which 
produces a quantity of light this is proportional to the amounts of NO molecules which has reacted. 
The data was collected continually for the whole experiment; the relevant data was taken from at least 
25 min of steady state NO concentration at 150 °C for each catalyst. The NO concentration at the 
start was used as baseline for the further calculation of the conversion and the first order rate constant. 
 
 Wet and SO2 Activity Test 
 
The study of the effect of water and/or SO2 inhibition was done on different amounts of 180 – 300 
µm catalyst particles, depending on the activity when using dry gas. Approximately 200 mg was used 
for the Mn0.75La0.25/TiO2 catalyst, while roughly 700 mg was tested for the 31.5 wt.% 
Cu0.445Ce0.445Nb0.11/TiO2, 34.7 wt.% Cu0.4Ce0.4Nb0.2/TiO2 and 35.6 wt.% Cu0.365Ce0.365V0.27/TiO2 
catalysts. Roughly 15 vol.% water vapour was present when subjecting the samples to SO2 and a cold 
trap was used to prevent damaging the gas analyzer. The use of a cold trap presented some challenges 
with control of the inlet concentrations, because of the stickiness of the NH3 molecules in the cold 
trap and the oxidation of NO to NO2 also in the cold trap. The result was a measured signal of roughly 
NO = 130 ppm, NH3 = 8 ppm, and NO2 = 80 ppm without any catalyst present. While a test without 
any catalyst present, lacking the cold trap, would roughly give NO = 500 ppm, NH3 = 500 ppm, and 
NO2 = 30 ppm. The activity measurements, will in this way have a lower NO signal, however the 
baseline will also be lower, which makes the first order rate constants realistic. The raw analysis data 
is shown in the Supplementary File 1. The gas flow was 460 ml/min with another 86 ml/min water 
vapour. Steps was also taken through the experiments to stabilize the overall flow, which could be 
affected by flow reduction, by raising the flow of air.  
The Mn0.75La0.25/TiO2 catalyst was exposed to 15.4 ppm SO2 for about 8 hours to investigate the 
time dependent decrease in NO conversion with only 4 vol.% water present, while the other catalyst 
was subjected to roughly 20 hours of 8 - 9 ppm SO2 without data collection.   
Tests of the catalysts gave dry conversion, wet conversion, conversion after sulfuration with and 
without water vapour, giving four distinct conversions and subsequent first order rate constants. The 
outcome is that the effect on the activity of water, sulfuration and a combination of the two is isolated.

is probably due to sulfate, most likely metal sulfates, hindering the reduction process. The thermal 
removal of NH4HSO4 also roughly happens at 350 °C, which fits well with the 346 °C peak. The 34.7 
wt.% Cu0.4Ce0.4Nb0.2/TiO2-SO2 show a similar trend with a peak at 305 °C and one at 370 °C, which 
also suggest that sulfate hinders the reduction process. Interestingly, no peak was found at 600 °C for 
both CuxCey/TiO2 catalysts, which is TGA decomposition temperature for CuSO4 as stated in 
Section 7.1. However, the effect of H2 will probably lower the decomposition to a lower temperature, 
which perhaps could mean that the peak at 392 °C, could be the temperature where Cu from CuSO4 
is reduced.  The 25 wt.% Mn0.75La0.25/TiO2 retains a peak at 284 °C that could mean easily reducible 
MnxOy are present. The reduction of sulfate blocked MnxOy happens at 502 °C, which is a significant 
increase in temperature, from that of the two CuxCey/TiO2 catalysts. This indicate that a combination 
of MnSO4 and NH4HSO4 blocks the catalyst, which is also supported by XPS and IR, however the 
presence of MnSO4 does not explain the near absence of a peak at roughly 350 °C, i.e. decomposition 
of NH4HSO4, as seen for the two CuxCey/TiO2 catalysts.

 Interpretation of Activity Results  
 
The reduction in activity from 15 vol.% water is between 60 % and 90 %, illustrating the significant 
impact of water adsorption on the LT SCR activity, through the inhibition of NH3-adsorption as 
discussed in Section 6.1. The 12 wt.% V0.7Ce0.15Nb0.15/TiO2 and 15 wt.% V0.9Ce0.1/TiO2 displayed 
the most significant reduction of 93.6 % and 90.0 %, respectively, which could be due to problems 
with vanadium or with the synthesis method compared to the SG synthesis. As discussed in Section 
8, there should roughly be a kwet value of 48 cm3g-1s-1 for the catalyst to work sufficiently, meaning 
that a reduction of kdry to 2 – 6 cm3g-1s-1 for the different catalysts, is simply too substantial.  
The activity of the catalysts following sulfuration was also significantly reduced. The CuCeNb/TiO2 
was severely reduced to 3.7 and 0.3 cm3g-1s-1 from 20.2 and 15.4 cm3g-1s-1, respectively, suggesting 
that the catalysts have been completely blocked by sulfates. This is supported by the relatively large 
increase in S from XRF, XPS and TGA. Which might mean that Cu-based catalysts is not a good 
alternative to the Mn-based catalysts. The 12 wt.% V0.7Ce0.15Nb0.15/TiO2 was similarly reduced to 
only 1.7 cm3g-1s-1 from 30.0 cm3g-1s-1, while the 15 wt.% V0.9Ce0.1/TiO2 had a 62.2 % reduction. The 
combination of SO2 and water, results in even poorer results for the catalysts of between 5.33 to 0 
cm3g-1s-1, or roughly 90 % to 100 % reduction from kdry. The commercial catalyst 3 wt.% V2O5-7 
wt.% WO3/TiO2, display a lower decrease from sulfuration, however this is due to a low starting 
activity, and perhaps due to the presence of SO4 before the SO2-treatment, though an activity test of 
a freshly calcined sample was not performed to verify this claim.  
 
 Regeneration of Selected Catalysts at 350 °C 
 
Since the characterizations of 15 wt.%V0.9Ce0.1/TiO2 and 3 wt.% V2O5-7 wt.% WO3/TiO2 mostly 
indicated the presence of NH4HSO4 and Ce(SO4)2∙4H2O, a regeneration attempt was made by heating 
the catalysts to 350 °C for two hours, with the results listed in Table 27. The commercial catalyst was 
completely regenerated, illustrating that the NH4HSO4 is comparatively easy to decompose. The dry 
activity for 15 wt.%V0.9Ce0.1/TiO2 reached an 83 % regeneration, which could be due to the stability 
of Ce(SO4)2∙4H2O, which could mean that the catalyst loses some of the synergistic effects from ceria. 
The wet activity was improved by 37 %, which can be explained be an increase in acid site 
concentration from the sulfate groups.

  Conclusion 
 
The aim of the project was to develop, characterize and test a multitude of different TiO2 supported 
SCR catalysts, that could work at 150 °C with 15 vol.% water and 20 ppm SO2. The reason for this 
goal, was the utilization of the tail-end position in either biomass or in waste incineration plants, 
without the need for continuous heating of the flue gas to roughly 220 °C.   
A total of 33 catalysts have been synthesized using four different synthesis methods: DP, WET, 
IW, or SG. Focus was on lanthanum at first as co-doping metal, followed by further work with ceria. 
Six of the catalysts were sulfurated by exposure to roughly 8.5 ppm SO2 for 20 hours. Meaning a total 
of 39 different catalysts had to be characterized, using all or some of the eight different methods: 
TPR, TPD, IR, XPS, XRF, TGA, XRD and N2-physisorption. Activity tests in dry flue gas were 
performed on all catalysts, while six of the catalysts were tests in 15 vol.% water, followed by 
sulfuration, isolating the effect of sulfate formation and water adsorption on the SCR activity. 
The series of VLa/TiO2 catalysts were limited by an average TPD acidity of roughly 300 μmol/g, 
low TGA weight loss of about 0.5 % between 300 – 600 °C. TPR profiles were elevated slightly in 
temperature by La doping, however the overall peak positions of roughly 450 °C, indicate low LT 
SCR activity. The resulting kdry values of between 10 and 2 cm3 g-1 s-1, is not sufficiently high for LT 
SCR.  
The NbLa/TiO2 and TaLa/TiO2 catalysts were made to investigate a possible LT SCR activity in 
the V-group, however no activity was found at 150 °C, which were further supported by high 
temperature TPR profiles of 450 – 900 °C. The CuLa/TiO2 showed no dry activity at 150 °C, due to 
significant crystalline phases, because of a too high Cu loading. 
A series of MnLa/TiO2 catalysts were made, considering the excellent results for Mn from the 
literature. The TGA weight loss of 1.87 % to 3.7 % between 300 – 600 °C, was the best results of the 
tested catalysts, indicating good levels of surface oxygen. The TPD acidity was between 269 – 362 
μmol/g, and was somewhat lowered at higher La mol%, because the Mn0.9La0.1/TiO2 had the highest 
value. The TPR profiles were largely unaffected by the addition of La, with no significant shifts in 
peak positions, from the bimodal peaks at roughly 280 °C and 380 °C.  
The dry activity illustrated no clear improvement by doping with La, because the kdry was only 
between 105 and 30 cm3 g-1 s-1, compared to 145 cm3 g-1 s-1 for a 25 wt.% Mn0.75Fe0.25/TiO2-DP 
catalyst from literature. The results also indicated that above 15 mol% La there is a notable drop in 
==PAGE:96==
Frederik Bojsen Kværndrup 
 
89 
 
activity, which was most severe at 30 mol%, where the presence of crystalline LaCO3OH from XRD 
also was found.  
Work commenced with ceria on copper and vanadium, because no improvement was found with 
lanthanum doping, synthesized with different methods from the DP method. The CuCe/TiO2 catalysts 
was made with the SG method and with titanium(IV) butoxide, because of problems with ammonia 
complexes.  
The CuCe/TiO2 illustrated little to no crystallinity for TiO2, which was different from the other 
catalysts based on anatase TiO2. The TiO2 and CuO particle size from XRD, was also lower than for 
the anatase TiO2 based catalysts, roughly 15 – 21 nm vs 20 – 28 nm. The BET surface area result of 
114 m2/g for 28.3 wt.% Cu0.5Ce0.5/TiO2, was the highest measured value, and suggests that the SG 
synthesis method creates a more microporous structure, or more mesoporous resulting in a higher 
porosity and lower density. The TPD acidity was found to be in a varied interval between 235 μmol/g 
and 362 μmol/g, however the composition and wt.% metal loading of the catalysts were diverse. 
Similarly, to the TaLa, NbLa and VLa catalysts, there was a somewhat low TGA weight loss of 
between 0.34 % – 1.1 % at 300 – 600 °C, indicating a low concentration of surface oxygen. The peak 
positions in the TPR profiles were generally bimodal, with a peak at roughly 180 °C and a peak at 
220 – 247 °C. There was an indication that ceria lowered the peak position of the high temperature 
peak from roughly 250 °C to 220 °C, which fit well with the findings from literature. The dry activity 
was between 6 and 46.9 cm3 g-1 s-1, which was significantly lower than for the MnLa/TiO2 catalysts.    
The 9, 12 and 15 wt.% V0.9Ce0.1/TiO2-IW illustrated a wide interval in TPD acidity from 218 – 423 
μmol/g, however only based on a single TPD measurement, there are indications because of this 
difference, that problems can occur with the IW synthesis. The 15 wt.% V0.9Ce0.1/TiO2-IW had the 
lowest measured BET surface area of 61 m2/g, also indicating problems with the IW synthesis 
method. The TPR unimodal peak was positioned at 390 °C to 470 °C, with the lowest value for the 
12 wt.% V0.7Ce0.15Nb0.15/TiO2-DP catalyst. The peak position is like that of the VLa catalysts, and 
indicate that vanadium is reduced in a comparatively high temperature interval, regardless of ceria 
doping. The dry activity was between 31.1 and 38.1 cm3 g-1 s-1, which is relatively low, though 
improved notably from the VLa catalyts. 
The characterization after sulfuration, indicated that the CuCe and MnLa-based catalysts had an 
increased tendency to form sulfates, than the VCe-based catalysts. This was indicated by a relatively 
high increase in XPS and XRF signals for S. The XPS S/N relation roughly being 1 for the V-based 
catalysts, indicating that only NH4HSO4 is formed, and 1.4 – 2.1 for the CuCe and MnLa-based 
==PAGE:97==

90 
 
catalysts, hinting at the formation of Cu, Mn and Ce sulfates. The reduction in BET surface area was 
also roughly two times higher for the CuCe catalysts vs. the VCe catalysts. The IR profiles also 
supported this tendency, indicating the presence of Cu, Mn and Ce sulfates, apart from NH4HSO4. 
The TPR profiles illustrated a significant increase in peak position for the CuCe-based catalysts from 
roughly 245 °C to 305 °C – 392 °C, indicating that NH4HSO4 and metal sulfates hinders the reduction 
process. The TPR peak positions for the MnLa/TiO2 catalyst, were similarly increased from 284 °C 
to 502 °C. Sulfuration had little effect on the peak positions for the VCe-based catalysts, because the 
reduction temperature was already significant.  
Sulfuration tests of the MnLa catalyst indicated a time dependent deactivation of the catalyst, 
with a final reduction of 72 %, which could be due to a buildup of sulfates on the surface. The 
deactivation remained after removal of SO2, indicating a deactivation and not just an inhibition. The 
activity of the two CuCe catalysts were reduced by 81.7 % and 98,1 %, respectively, while the two 
VCe catalysts were reduced by 62.2 % and 94.8 %, correspondingly. The results indicate that the 
buildup of sulfates inhibits the different catalysts significantly. Regeneration of a VCe catalyst at 350 
°C, lead to an 83 % regeneration of the catalyst, while a complete regeneration perhaps was prevented 
by stable Ce(SO4)2.   
The reversibility of water vapour deactivation was tested on the sulfurated MnLa catalyst, with 
a decrease from 21 cm3 g-1 s-1 to 3.5 cm3 g-1 s-1 with water vapour present, which returned to 15 cm3 
g-1 s-1 following removal of water, indicating a degree reversible deactivation. The non-sulfurated 
CuCe, MnLa and VCe catalysts saw between 59.1 % and 93.6 % reduction in activity, when subjected 
to 15 vol.% water vapour, illustrating the significant inhibition from water adsorption, and the 
resulting reduction in NH3 adsorption at 150 °C. 
The combination of 15 vol.% water and SO2 exposure, reduced the activity by 86 % to 100 % 
for the different catalysts, rendering them nearly inactive. This indicate that there are significant 
challenges with low temperature SCR. A possible solution, could be a slight increase in reaction 
temperature to combat water adsorption, and the use of a preoxidation catalyst to increase the 
concentration of NO2, to promote fast SCR. This would be coupled with a SO2 resistant vanadium 
based catalyst. There are indications that Co-based heteropoly acids, nonetheless more expensive 
catalysts, could be used as a possible NO preoxidation catalyst. The formation of CoSO4, with a 
decomposition temperature of roughly 650 °C, might inhibit the activity and prevent regeneration, 
limits the usefulness.
