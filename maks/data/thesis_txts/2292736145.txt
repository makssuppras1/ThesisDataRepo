ABSTRACT 
This study points at a perceived need of increased focus on the organizational culture within 
the strategic perspective of organizational managers. By approaching the subject using the 
overall gap analysis methodology, additional models are presented to, in combination, map 
the organizational cultural layout.  
Using an extended version of The Star Model™, the Organizational Culture Analysis 
Instrument (OCAI), the 8 dimensions of culture, the methodology presented by Schein and the 
benefit realization management framework, it develops the Tomato Approach.  
This approach intends to equip the organizational managers with the means to assist 
strategies by guiding the current organizational cultural layout through the transition. 
As The Tomato Approach is developed from a case study of an organization with several 
different business units focused on different market segments and external environments, it is 
intended for organizations with a wide variety of internal segments. 
 
Keywords: Organizational culture; Organizational change; Organizational strategy; The 
Tomato Approach; Competing values framework; Values, Culture profiles; Social Network 
Analysis; The Star Model™; Benefit realization management

1 INTRODUCTION 
Change isn’t always easy to handle. When uncontrolled, it seldom appears when we expect it 
or as we expect it. It is very individual how we react to change, and this versatility makes 
change a very interesting subject of study. It also is one of the areas where it is possible to 
actively choose to have a positive impact on the world we live in. 
Change comes about in many shapes and sizes and has been researched from several 
perspectives. Through this research, change has been divided into two general types; 
incremental or transformational and the action upon it to be either pro-active or re-active 
(Jacobsen & Thorsvik, 2008). The reasons for change can be internal or external sources and 
the process of dealing with change is often derived from the present situation.  
Management of change as a field, views change as a process which can be predicted and 
controlled to some degree (Hayes, 2014), and this control is done by utilizing the mechanics of 
the process and the reasons behind it. By using this understanding it is mostly possible to 
mitigate obstacles and smoothen out the influencing factors which hinder the implementation 
and execution of this process. 
One aspect which is influencing change, in particular the aversion of change is the cultural 
aspect (Kitchin, 2010). Some cultures or personalities embrace change while others reject it 
categorically. Most organizations consist of both personality types which create diversity but 
can also be the cause of conflict. Within organizations these cultural diversities are sometimes 
neglected in the strategic process aiming to bring people closer together in order to facilitate 
growth and efficient workflow, but is it the correct approach?  
This thesis will look deeper into the aspect of culture while focusing on the strategic vision 
within an organization. The institute chosen as case company for this thesis has several 
departments with different service areas and market segments. The strategic goal of the lead 
management aims towards one united customer oriented company which makes it ideal to 
investigate in order to answer the following research question: 
Which models can be used to understand organizational culture well enough, to turn it 
into a tool that facilitates the strategic implementation?

2.2 QUESTIONNAIRE 
The use of a questionnaire was chosen as a quantitative investigation able to reach as broad

One goal of the questionnaire was to build up a baseline “As-Is” state by using a social 
network analysis of the internal communications, while also mapping aspects affecting the 
relationships within the case company. 
Due to the need for building a directional network it was not conducted as an anonymous 
inquiry. Information has however been censored a bit prior to public distribution. 
Another aim was to use the questionnaire to investigate aspects from the competing values 
framework used by (Cameron & Quinn, 2011), see chapter 3.2.2 for further information. 
The questionnaire was divided into four parts: 
1. Information regarding the respondent. 
2. Information concerning relations. 
3. Information which could affect the answers previously given. 
4. Questions from the Organizational Culture Assessment Instrument (OCAI) 
described in chapter 3.2.2.1 
The questionnaire service Qualtrics.com was used in this project to facilitate the process.

3 THEORY/LITERATURE REVIEW 
Numerous authors have researched and invested time in understanding the underlying 
mechanics and flows of culture within organizations. Since the early eighties there has been 
an increased focus on the effects of company culture, in order to comprehend how it affects 
the effectiveness and efficiency of the organization (Jacobsen & Thorsvik, 2008, p. 111). The 
ability culture has when it comes to create barriers or facilitate changes in organizations has 
also been in the focus of researchers. It is some of these studies that this thesis uses as a basis 
for the study of company culture within the case company.

3.1 PRINCIPLES 
Managing change from a process perspective is explained thoroughly by John Hayes in his 
book on change management (Hayes, 2014). Any process can be considered a change process; 
the establishment of something that does not yet exist, the alteration of something existing, 
and even the removal or closure can be considered a change process.  
It all starts with the recognition of a need, a need for improvement, increased efficiency, or 
just a need to keep up with the changes in the environment surrounding the organization.  
After seeing the need, the methodical next step is to confirm the need, since this need 
represents a desired future, and by defining it in details the state of where the organization 
wants “TO-BE” in the future, is established. 
Before setting off towards the desired future, a plan detailing what needs to change has to be 
established, but in order to determine what has to change it is important to establish where 
we are now; the “AS-IS” state. Once “AS-IS” and “TO-BE” is established it becomes easier to 
communicate and plan the needed change, as well as manage the roadmap for change. 
Change processes within organizations are inflicted by several aspects, and changing anything 
which involves people will be affected by the aspect of established organizational culture. 
There exist methods and tools that can be used to visualize and understand aspects within 
organizations, culture included, and this thesis will look at some of them; 8 dimensions of 
culture, The Star Model™, and the Competing Values Framework. But first a look at what it’s 
all about. 
3.1.1 WHAT IS ORGANIZATIONAL CULTURE? 
Culture is usually referred to as “habits, faith, art and all other results of human activity in a 
certain group of people during a certain time period” (Politikens Forlag, 1996, p. 330) and 
when looking at organizational culture, Jacobsen & Thorsvik states it as being the same 
principles, only with the focus of how culture is developed within an organizational context. 
The key element in culture is people, and the interactions amongst them are what creates, 
establish and maintain culture. There are many different interpretations of how to 
characterize and determine organizational culture; (Jacobsen & Thorsvik, 2008, p. 118) states 
==PAGE:19==
 
9 
that organizational culture can be described as; a “pattern of basic assumptions that has 
worked sufficiently well to be considered truths and is being taught to newcomers”. This can be 
interpreted as implicating that any changes in culture starts by questioning the assumptions 
made and establishing new assumptions, that can be proven as valid and working within the 
organization. According to (Johnson, et al., 2012, pp. 95-96), Schein suggests that culture can 
be perceived as four different layers; Values, Beliefs, Behaviors, and Taken-for-granted 
assumptions. Values are often reflected in the formerly stated strategy of an organization, but 
can also be hidden and contradicting the actual stated values. Beliefs are mostly brought to 
light when reviewing the communications within an organization regarding current issues 
faced, it is reflected in the opinions of groups of employees. Behaviors reflect the day-to-day 
actions of the employees; work routines, organizational structure and Hierarchy. They may 
turn into taken-for-granted “ways we do things around here” which can either generate 
inimitable strategic capabilities, or inhibit achieving strategic change if necessary. Taken-for-
granted assumptions are the Implicit Assumptions which weaves its web underneath the 
surface of the organization where they represent the collective experience of the organization. 
Like the Behaviors they can both support strategies as well as inhibit major strategic changes 
or organizational mergers.

Characterizing the organizational culture is the first step to understanding what underlying 
forces are doing to empower or hinder the desired solutions. 
There exist several ways of characterizing and describing culture within organizations. 
Hofstede (1980) initially developed four national culture aspects; Masculinity/femininity, 
Uncertainty avoidance, Individualism/collectivism, and power distance. After working with 
Bond in 1988, Hofstede found that a fifth dimension of Long-term orientation was needed due 
to differences between the Eastern and Western cultures (Kitchin, 2010, pp. 50-53). Some of 
these dimensions are also present in the 8 dimensions of culture by (Jones, et al., 2006). 
Schein (1985) also delivered a substantial advancement in describing organizational culture 
which is described in the next section. 
3.1.2 WHAT INFLUENCES ORGANIZATIONAL CULTURE? 
In 1985, Schein described organizational culture as an iceberg where the largest part of 
culture was unseen, and yet have a large effect when you encounter it (Kitchin, 2010, p. 29). 
He layered the iceberg in 3 layers where the only visible layer above water was the Artefacts 
and the two underwater layers, were characterized as Values and Basic Assumptions of the 
organization. It can be argued that values are something related to the people of the 
organization, and basic assumptions are reflected in the methods of the organization. 
 
3.1.2.1 ARTEFACTS 
Artefacts are observable things, like objects, technology, behavior, stories, structure and 
hierarchy amongst other things within an organization, everything that can somehow have an 
influence on the people who observe these things (Kitchin, 2010, p. 29) (Jacobsen & Thorsvik, 
2008, pp. 123-126). For example, the décor and furniture in a private household along with 
the tidiness and layout of rooms can provide hints as to the family culture of its residents.  
All these artefacts have their own meaning and symbolism to their surroundings, which might 
not be immediately distinguishable but shows over time. 
==PAGE:21==
 
11 
Artefacts are the visible things which affect the people of the organization to behave in a 
certain way, like the colors of the traffic light at the intersection, which has become an 
integrated control aspect for all members of the community. Thus the interpretation or 
conditioned understanding of artefacts can influence organizational culture heavily. 
Introducing a toy frog on your desk as a means to show that you do not wish to be disturbed 
can only be interpreted as such once the entire organization has agreed to the rule. (Kring, 
2008) 
 
3.1.2.2 PEOPLE AND VALUES  
As mentioned earlier, the main element of culture is people; without people there would not 
be any organization and no culture would grow within it. The diversity of people is vast and 
the multitude of variations in their beliefs and opinions are almost incomprehensible, but 
researchers continue to try to generalize and categorize groups of people in order to develop 
some comprehensible archetype. In the 1980s, Myers-Briggs presented their framework 
(Ellis, 1990) and in 1991 the PACE® Palette was introduced (The PACE® Organization Inc., 
2014). In 1993 Belbin developed his 9 team roles (Kitchin, 2010, p. 5), all intended to 
understand the different personalities existing within a team. But their frameworks can be 
used on a larger scale within the entire organization to ensure teams are put together in a way 
which is optimal.  
In some organizations the understanding of the personality of the employee starts prior to 
employment in order to see if he/she fits not only the skills needed for the job but also the 
values and mindset enabling the newcomer to adapt and continue building on the values of 
the company. 
Values within organizational culture are commonly initially dictated by the upper 
management, but as the organization develops over time, new values can grow and challenge 
the existing. Hyde and Williams (2000) (Kitchin, 2010, p. 31) define personal values as: 
“Underlying and relatively stable dispositions which people use to guide their actions and 
decisions and to help them make judgements about what is right and wrong”

3.1.2.3 BASIC ASSUMPTIONS 
Basic assumptions are the things that influence the values of people and the organization. 
They are sometimes referred to as the things that are taken for granted, the implicit 
perceptions and opinions that need not be voiced to be enforced. In national cultures it would 
be called “norms”, like locking the door when you go to the toilet, or that everyone should 
have equal rights. The last mentioned would reflect the national culture of many but not all 
countries, which leads to mention that it is not only the assumptions of the organization that 
influences the culture but very much also the national cultures of the employees, which many 
multinational co-operations experience. 
In order to cope and understand the differences in organizational culture, within smaller 
companies, and even within the same global company, several models exist which assist the 
managers. These models aim at bringing the different perspectives to light in order to see if 
the organization is optimized to reach the desired destination. 
 
3.2 MODELS 
Models assist with analyzing the current state of an organization in order to break down large 
and intangible scenarios into smaller, more comprehensible parts that can be managed by the 
leaders and managers of the organization. The overall change process can be compared to the 
mathematics of a vector. In order to accurately plot a vector, it is required to know 3 things; 
its origin, its direction, and its magnitude. In the change process the origin is the “AS-IS” state 
and the “TO-BE” state is the direction, leaving the magnitude to be the complexity of the 
change. The frameworks and models in this chapter are all tools for analyzing and describing 
this “vector”.

The culture of adhocracy is defined by Cameron & Quinn as “Characterized by a dynamic,

leadership is visionary, innovative and risk oriented. The glue that holds the organization

The second quadrant also has an emphasis on flexibility but an internal focus instead. It is 
named “Clan” because this type of culture makes the organization resemble an extended 
family. The organizational characteristics are focused around “teamwork, employee 
involvement programs, and corporate commitment to employees” (Cameron & Quinn, 2011, p. 
Loc857) and the management focus is “to empower employees and facilitate their 
participation, commitment, and loyalty” (Cameron & Quinn, 2011, p. Loc875).  
The Clan culture is characterized by Cameron & Quinn as “a friendly place to work where 
people share a lot of themselves. It is like an extended family. Leaders are thought of as mentors 
and perhaps even as parent figures. The organization is held together by loyalty and tradition. 
Commitment is high. The organization emphasizes the long-term benefit of individual 
development, with high cohesion and morale being important. Success is defined in terms of 
internal climate and concern for people. The organization places a premium on teamwork, 
participation, and consensus.” (Cameron & Quinn, 2011)Ch.3.3.3 
In the third quadrant, the Hierarchy is represented as having emphasis on stability with an 
internal focus. It is the traditional organization which Max Weber in 1947proposed the 
following seven characteristics as “the classical attributes of bureaucracy: rules, specialization, 
meritocracy, hierarchy, separate ownership, impersonality, and accountability” (Cameron & 
Quinn, 2011). The hierarchy requires a certain degree of stability in the environment of the 
organization and is defined by Cameron & Quinn as “a formalized and structured place to work. 
Procedures govern what people do. Effective leaders are good coordinators and organizers. 
Maintaining a smoothly running organization is important. The long-term concerns of the

The fourth and final quadrant contains the Market culture where emphasis is on external 
positioning and control. The term “Market” refers to the situation where the organization 
functions as a market itself. It focuses on the external environment and transactions with 
external suppliers, customers and other stakeholders while operating through economic 
market mechanisms and monetary exchange. The Market organization creates competitive 
advantage by conducting transactions (exchanges, sales, contracts) with other constituencies, 
and focuses on core values of competitiveness and productivity. In a Market culture it is 
assumed that the organization exist in an hostile external environment where the main focus 
is to increase the competitive position using productivity, results, and profits as the main 
direction. By using a clear purpose along with an aggressive strategy it is assumed to lead to 
productivity and profitability. The Market culture is defined by Cameron & Quinn as “a results-
oriented workplace. Leaders are hard-driving producers and competitors who are tough and 
demanding. The glue that holds the organization together is an emphasis on winning. The long-
term concern is on competitive actions and achieving stretch goals and targets. Success is 
defined in terms of market share and penetration. Outpacing the competition and market 
leadership are important.” (Cameron & Quinn, 2011)Ch.3.3.2

The Star Model™ (Galbraith, 2008) was developed in the 1960s by Jay Galbraith as a 
framework used for analyzing organizations. By allowing management to control the various 
design policies it becomes possible to influence the behavior of the employees in the 
organization and thus adjust both performance and culture aspects.

The strategy at the top is influenced by both the external environment and the internal

Structure relates to the structure of the organization, which in turn represents a layout of the

Departmentalization. Specialization refers to the different skill-sets needed within the 
organization. Shape relates to the hierarchical layout of the organization. Distribution of power 
is not only related to the vertical difference in power between each hierarchical layer, but also 
how it is distributed within each layer. And lastly Departmentalization is the standard 
dimensions determining how the departments are formed, dimensions like functions, market, 
processes and customers.

planes within the hierarchical organization. The vertical processes can be distributions of

Rewards are to some the driver of the organization, the system that aligns employee goals 
with those of the organization. Examples of the monetary policies that can be tweaked are 
salaries, promotions, bonuses and profit sharing, but also non-monetary rewards like 
challenging assignments or recognition of efforts and victories are important. It is imperative 
that the reward system is congruent with the previous mentioned policies if it is to be efficient 
towards the strategic goal set by the leaders of the organization.  
People policies are within the area of human relations (HR). Everything relating to the 
employee recruitment, job rotation, team selection, training and development are within this 
area. This area of The Star Model™ is where you ensure the skill of people within the 
organization match the needed capabilities for executing the strategic direction. 
The Star Model™ is used as a framework where aligning the different policy-elements it 
contains ensures “successful responses to the external business environment” (Jensen & Kreye, 
2014, p. 3). It is introduced by Galbraith as “the foundation on which a company bases its 
design choices” (Galbraith, 2008, p. 1) where design is referring to the structure of the 
organization controlled though the tools that the policies represent. Using The Star Model™ 
enable the management to see, analyze and understand the current “AS-IS” state of the 
organization. When this knowledge is gathered it becomes possible to “overcome the negatives 
of any structural design” which Galbraith argues is present in every organizational structure 
option, and by adapting the surrounding policies to mitigate the negatives while achieving the 
positives an optimal structure is developed. The circumstances of this thesis have uncovered 
the possible extension of The Star Model™ with a central policy named Identity. The Identity 
policy is to some degree influenced by all five parent policies but contains the cultural aspects 
defined by Schein. Further explanation of the extended star model can be found in chapter 4.6.

3.2.4.1 SIX SIGMA 
When Motorola developed the Six Sigma concept they managed to establish an ideology which 
“leads to a corporate cultural change, a paradigm shift toward an expectation of the highest 
quality, which then drives a passion for continuous improvement by all players involved” (Erwin 
& Douglas, 2000, p. 6). Several companies have adapted the Six Sigma and met their goals 
within the set timeframe saving billions of dollars worldwide. 
The principle of Six Sigma is pretty straight forward; six focal points of continuous 
improvements that must be implemented to such a degree that every employee in the 
organization strives to uphold them (see Table 2). (Erwin & Douglas, 2000)  
“There is no one set procedure” (Erwin & Douglas, 2000, p. 10) for implementation. But 
starting with the existing organization, instead of starting over, building on current successes, 
instead of having to establish new ones, and modifying current processes instead of building 
new ones.

The main theme of benefit realization is that the benefits of a project often don’t arrive until 
after it has been completed and implemented and by then the project is often no longer the 
focus of the Base organization (see Figure 13). When using benefit realization management an 
extra step is implemented within the base organization to ensure the original target benefits 
of a project is met. 
According to (Krarup, 2015); (Jenner, 2012) argues that benefit realization management 
increases the rate of success by giving a realistic and thoroughly analyzed overview of the 
benefits. This makes the portfolio management of projects capable of starting the projects 
with the highest probability of achieving the target benefits, and leaving the ones which does 
not have much potential. 
The increased success ratio creates more value for changes and projects, and ensures a 
business focus when conducting change management, while it answers the questions of why 
do wo do it? And what do we achieve doing it? 
It also operationalize the case and provides methods, techniques and tools to identify, plan 
and realize benefits also tracking through several organizational changes.

Benefits are defined as the positive result of a change; they tell us why we want that change, 
and are often described with adjectives like; higher, more, better, cheaper, faster, lower, less…

planned (see Figure 14).

In Testing they are equipped with facilities, big furnaces and setups which would be costly for 
the individual SME to invest in, but is required to test the properties of the materials and 
design with regard to fire safety. In short, they burn all kinds of things using different 
propellants in a controlled environment while monitoring the temperatures and time exposed 
to the fire. They follow both international and national test standards and are accredited by 
the national accreditation body in Denmark, DANAK.  
It is little known to the public, but almost everything around us which is stationary has at 
some point been tested to prove that it is within a set of standards in order to ensure that in 
case of a fire, lives have a high probability of surviving. Materials, components and systems for 
buildings and ships along with materials for furniture, textiles, upholstery, life jackets and 
toys are all tested to ensure the safety of the public. 
In Figure 23 the test of a couple of doors with double glass panes installed are tested in the 
large furnace. On both sides are located several burners, some can be seen in the picture 
which alternate in order to sustain an even temperature inside the furnace. The temperature 
is then monitored on both sides of the wall and doors and the time from ignition until burn-

also handles certification of products at the in-house subsidiary called DBI Certification A/S. 
Courses are also given in security, fire prevention at workplaces with increased risk, 
education of fitters of fire alarm systems as well as theft prevention solutions.

4.2.1.3 FIRE INVESTIGATION 
Fire Investigation is one of Scandinavia’s only certified companies to conduct fire 
investigations. They are hired to investigate the site of the fire after it has been extinguished 
in order to answer the obvious questions; how did the fire start? Did the preventive measures 
function correctly? How did the fire develop? And can something be done to prevent it from 
ever happening again? They have around 700 cases each year. 
 
4.2.1.4 SAFETY AND SECURITY CONSULTING 
The Safety and security consulting department works with the protection of property and the 
prevention of theft. SME’s are in charge of their individual security and have to uphold certain 
standards in accordance with their insurance company. When purchasing security systems, it 
is often a liaison of the manufacturer consulting the SME, who might convince the SME to add 
additional products to the sale which there is no real need for. DBI provides an impartial body 
of knowledge ensuring the tender and implementation is fair and correctly meets the real 
demands of the SME. Because this department resides within DBI it also has access to 
knowledge regarding the interfaces between theft prevention and fire protection systems, 
which if done incorrectly can work against each other. The department also assists SME’s with 
insights to risk management. 
 
4.2.1.5 FIRE SAFETY TECHNOLOGY CONSULTING 
DBI’s fire advisory division offers consulting services in all facets of fire safety technology. 
They assist architects, entrepreneurs, housing cooperatives and building owners ensure that 
their property is living up to the functional requirements for Danish buildings. This happens 
through all phases of the lifespan of a building, from projecting new buildings to retrofitting 
old ones to live up to new standards. Evacuation plans, technical drawings, advice on risks 
and potential fire conditions along with inspections are some of the services provided here. 
The department also updates and maintains the simulation software Argos® which evaluate 
fire risk and simulate smoke spread, temperature, radiation and other aspects of the spread of 
a fire. The department handles around 500 cases per year. 
==PAGE:60==
 
50 
 
4.2.1.6 CORPORATE INVESTIGATION 
Corporate investigation handles cases involving the prevention of fraud, misuse of brands and 
trademarks, and cases clarifying breaches of trademark or leaks of information. Most 
investigations are conducted electronically with analytical tools as well as the internet. They 
work like security detectives, assisting the SME’s with issues they themselves do not have the 
competencies to handle. 
 
4.2.1.7 INSPECTION 
Besides the above areas DBI has a department that specifically handles inspections of fire and 
security installations across Denmark as well. All installations are required to go through an 
annual inspection, in order to be approved for use and compliance with the rules and 
regulations. These are also done in conjunction with the installation of a new system.

5.1 THE SQUARE TOMATO 
 
FIGURE 40 - THE SQUARE TOMATO (INTERPLAYUK.COM, 2013) 
This project started out with the thought of somehow, figuratively, creating an adjustable 
mold in order to supply the structural enablers, that can slowly redirect organizational 
culture as it develops, like a mold for tomatoes. Now, culture does not fit in a box, but it is still 
guided by various organizational aspects which can be modified to facilitate the cultural 
change. By modifying cultural aspects within the organization, it becomes possible to ensure 
that the organizational culture does not pose as a barrier to the implementation of a strategy 
but instead becomes a carrier and facilitator of it. 
With organizational culture being a field with many aspects of influence, there are numerous 
approaches to it, and the ones reviewed during the literature study all showed promise in 
their individual fields. It was surprising to see how much overlap there was between the 
different articles, even though they had different approaches, some principles were present 
across the differences. By utilizing these principles it was determined how to piece together 
an approach towards working constructively with organizational culture and change 
management.

5.2 THE TOMATO APPROACH

The questionnaire used in this investigation (Appendix A) was primarily developed for 
gathering information for the SNA and conducting the OCAI. 
By asking the respondent to indicate whom they communicate with and how often it was 
possible to gather the required information for the SNA. In order to differentiate between 
internal and external communications, the respondent was first limited to the colleagues 
within the department as the only possible answers and subsequently presented with a list of 
all employees of the organization.  
The question of length of employment at the company was done to gain an understanding of 
the maturity of the organization as well as insights on the level of implementation difficulty. A 
traditional organization with a large portion of employees who has been employed for a long 
time will have a higher level of resistance to change due to the highly developed identity and 
traditional group think.  
Following the SNA questions was two segments of qualitative questions which were posed to 
gain an understanding of the work ethics and workload. The initial thought was to see if 
correlations existed between the answers to the OCAI, but as the data-analysis of the 
questionnaire proved quite extensive, the idea was dropped. 
Finally the last segment of the questionnaire was the OCAI presented in Table 1 (p.19), with 
one modification; the word “organization” was replaced throughout the OCAI with 
“department”. This was done to trigger the respondent to base their answer on the internal 
organizational culture of the department. Had the OCAI kept its original phrasing, it is possible 
that the differences in culture presented in this report would be much smaller since all would 
answer based on the collective understanding of the company. This would not enable the 
investigation into the differences of the departments which is a key element of this report. 
==PAGE:83==
 
73 
The idea of turning the last question into a “thank you” note proved to be a bad move, due to 
respondents then signing off before the questionnaire had registered the survey as completed. 
The actual setup of the questionnaire proved to be more difficult than anticipated due to quite 
some time spent accessing and assessing software solutions online to locate a tool which 
could accommodate the specific layout of a network analysis. Initially the website 
“Surveymonkey.com” was evaluated, but found lacking in options. So after consulting the 
project supervisor a new web-service was tested; Qualtrics.com. This site showed promise 
and was chosen for the distribution of the questionnaire.  
Some words of caution though. When preparing a questionnaire for the SNA: pay extra 
attention to the display logic you use, it only takes one mistake to ensure that the question is 
skipped altogether. Also, the use of “timer metrics” is not necessary for this type of 
questionnaire; it only clogs up the extraction of data. 
5.4 REFLECTIONS OF THE OCAI AND SNA 
In the OCAI, Cameron & Quinn uses the average score in their data analysis as to generate a 
collated singular representation of a large group of respondents.  Doing this will adhere to the 
method described, but loose the diversity of the answers given. Depending on the quantity of 
answers gathered, using an individual representation can be very messy to look at, and the 
overview can be lost in the process. It is therefore advised to keep the number of unique 
simultaneous graphs below 20.  
Working with Social network analysis (SNA) it quickly becomes obvious that it is a dynamic 
tool which has numerous possible distinct representations. This thesis has referenced two 
electronic tools which was used to process the data gathered from the questionnaire; Gephi 
(Gephi consortium, 2010) and Net-Sights (DTU Management Engineering, 2014). When first 
extracting the data from Qualtrics the excel-sheet contains data which can be turned into a 
matrix representing the connections between the employees. The main program (Gephi) 
however is not able to import a matrix, which is why Net-sights were used to convert the 
data-matrix into an “edge list”, a list of the connections between nodes. This list could then be 
imported in Gephi. This method saves a substantial amount of time. 
==PAGE:84==
 
74 
The list of possible visual representations that Gephi can provide of the data is extensive and 
the analytical capabilities are equally vast. This report has however primarily used it as a tool 
to provide a visual representation of the informal network within the case company. This has 
been done to identify the level of communication amongst the departments as well as the 
opinion leaders of the departments. 
5.5 FINDINGS 
By establishing a detailed image of DBI as an organization, not only with the usual strategic 
framework but also mapping out the organizational culture, it becomes more explicit where 
the gaps are located.  
DBI has for the last 6 years had a vision of becoming customer-oriented and presenting the 
company as one unit, but the road towards that goal has been longer than expected. By fully 
understanding the inner workings and relations of the organization it becomes easier to 
identify and facilitate the right initiatives to speed up this process. 
Adapting the concepts of Six Sigma and Operationalizing value to the organization as core 
competencies after having confirmed the strategic roadmap using the benefit realization 
management framework will provide the management with the means to successfully 
implement the change. 
The outcome of the SNA has confirmed the impression of some of the organizational 
managers, especially by demonstrating the heightened level of communications between 
some of the departments within the center for knowledge. This has been a focus up until now, 
and it was nice to see it had had an effect. 
The cultural map of the organization developed using the OCAI has, besides from showing the 
diversity of opinions within the organization, enabled DBI to identify the gaps where the 
desired vision of the leaders does not correspond with the current reality. Again it must be 
noted that the responses to the OCAI rely heavily on the interpretation of the statements done 
by the respondent. This may count for a portion of the differences visualized in the results.

6 CONCLUSION 
By using the principles of the gap analysis it was possible to uncover some of the tools and 
models required to analyze and establish the current organizational circumstances, including 
the organizational culture. 8 dimensions of culture (3.2.1) and the competing values 
framework (3.2.2) both helps establish the cultural layout of an organization. Conducting a 
social network analysis (3.2.5) may unearth the hidden power grid and visualize the key 
players who can assist with ensuring the smooth implementation of the strategic process.  
While the aspects defined by Schein (3.1.1 & 3.1.2) describe the organizational culture and 
determines some of the variables that influences and shapes it, the extended version of  
The Star Model™ (3.2.3) describes the layout of the organization which creates and supports 
these aspects. 
In order to ensure that the strategy will be a continued success, some drivers of positive 
cultural aspects was also identified. Six Sigma (3.2.4.1) and operationalizing value (3.2.4.2) 
both provide a continuous improvement perspective which is connected to, not only the 
processes of an organization but also the values and cultural aspects.  
Lastly, due to the core operational foundation of the case company being knowledge, the 
models of post-project reviews (3.2.4.3) has been touched upon, and to ensure that the 
perceived benefit of future projects are realized, the benefit realization management 
framework (3.2.4.4) has been explained. 
Organizational culture is a field with many aspects of influence where one single answer will 
never exist, but a range of options are open to the manager. In order to answer the research 
question: 
Which models can be used to understand organizational culture well enough, to turn it 
into a tool that facilitates the strategic implementation? 
This thesis has presented a combination of models and frameworks which can be used to map 
out the current scenario, the future desired state, and some of the possible aspects requiring 
definition and control, in order to facilitate the change by guiding the organizational culture.

This thesis has reviewed some of the theory on organizational culture mapping and change, 
suggested an extended version of The Star Model™ which gathers parts of the policies at the 
core, and introduced the tomato approach. It is believed that comparing and enhancing the 
approach by reviewing the cultural web (Johnson, et al., 2012, p. 98) may develop a more 
comprehensive picture of the organizational culture. 
Additional studies of the applicability of The Tomato Approach in other settings are also 
recommended.
