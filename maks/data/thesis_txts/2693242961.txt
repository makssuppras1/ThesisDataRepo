Abstract
RANS-based simulations, and especially with a k −ω closure model, are one of the most
widely-used ways to solve numerically a turbulent ﬂow. However, the Boussinesq approx-
imation is known to yield speciﬁc problems that need to be overcome. In this thesis the
innovative Tensor Basis Neural Network (TBNN) introduced by Ling et al. [1] is used, to
improve the anisotropy part of the Reynolds stresses. Modiﬁcations in the input features
are also examined, based on proposals by Wu et al. [2] and Wang et al. [3]. The ﬂows
examined are different types of breaking waves, namely spilling and plunging breakers,
simulated as two-phase simulations in OpenFOAM and utilizing the waves2FOAM tool-
box. The simulations are RANS-based, with the closure models used being the stabilized
k −ω [4] and the Stress−ω [5], implemented lately by Li et al. [6]. It is the ﬁrst time
in the author’s knowledge, that a machine learning algorithm, such as the TBNN, is used
in a time-dependent ﬂow. The nature of such a ﬂow, in conjunction with the two-phase
simulation give rise to particular complications that need to be dealt with. In the present
thesis, the optimal TBNN is identiﬁed based on Bayesian optimization. Consequently,
it is successfully implemented, improving the anisotropy and highlighting in the process
fundamental differences regarding the anisotropy between the two closure models. The
TBNN predictions show improvement both in instantaneous anisotropy results and in time-
averaged ones. However, the direct use of the predictions in the Reynolds stresses is found
to be ineffective in improving them.

1.
Introduction
Nowadays, turbulence modelling is gaining ground and becomes an important tool in the
industry, providing a more thorough and accurate solution to a wide variety of problems.
However, there are still limitations in turbulence modelling, due to the existing compu-
tational power. For this reason RANS-based models have been devised, in an effort to
simulate numerically turbulent ﬂows. RANS-based models are heavily preferred for their
low-cost computational effort. However, most widely-used RANS-based models, such as
the k−ω, rely on the Boussinesq approximation, resulting in poor accuracy in certain ﬂows
with speciﬁc ﬂow features. Such a problem arises in breaking waves at a sloping beach.
Breaking waves create strong undertow velocities with an offshore direction, in an effort
to compensate for the average onshore ﬂux of water. The undertow heavily impacts the
transport of sediments, making the prediction of it of utmost importance, especially in-
side the surf zone. Researches have recently improved existing RANS-based models, such
as the stabilized k −ω model, or implemented RANS-based models that do not rely on
the Boussinesq approximation, such as the Stress−ω model. Although the latter showed
promising results, the use of a k −ω model would be ideal, since it is widely-used in most
applications. However, the accuracy of the stabilized k −ω model is not adequate inside
the surf zone, requiring a solution. Hence, a new alternative, which have been on the rise
recently, is implemented in this thesis, namely the use of machine learning algorithms to
bypass the problems stemming from the Boussinesq approximation.
The use of machine learning algorithms is increasing rapidly in recent years, regardless
the ﬁeld of interest, and the application of them in turbulence modelling is no exception.
An innovative machine learning algorithm has been devised, in an effort to improve the
anisotropy of the Reynolds stresses in RANS-based models using the Boussinesq approx-
imation and ultimately improve the ﬂow ﬁeld. This algorithm embeds Galilean invariance
in the predicted anisotropy tensor and is based on a number of isotropic basis tensors. The
machine learning algorithms already used are either random forests (TBRF) or neural net-
works (TBNN). Widely-used RANS-based models perform poorly regarding speciﬁc ﬂow
features, such as secondary ﬂow in square duct ﬂows or ﬂow separation in ﬂows over a
wavy wall. The use of machine learning have shown promising results in improving the
ﬂow ﬁeld and introducing such features in RANS-based simulations.
Although the TBNN is previously tested on steady ﬂows, it has not been used in time
dependant ﬂows, such as wave breaking. In this thesis, the TBNN is used to improve
the anisotropy predicted by the stabilized k −ω model in both a spilling and a plunging
breaker case. The RANS-based Stress−ω model is used for providing the accurate values
of the anisotropy tensor. The aim of this thesis is to set-up a TBNN, that can be used in
conjunction with the stabilized k −ω model and produce accurate anisotropy predictions
in the absence of high-ﬁdelity data for any wave breaking case.
The simulations of the breaking wave cases are based on experiments by Ting & Kirby
and are conducted as two-phase simulations in the open-source CFD software OpenFOAM
utilizing the waves2FOAM toolbox. The TBNN is set up in Python using the TensorFlow
package and the optimal TBNN conﬁguration is found through Bayesian optimization uti-
lizing the homonymous package. After setting-up the TBNN the predicted anisotropy val-
ues are used to examine whether they can be directly used to improve the Reynolds stresses.
Due to time limitation, the propagation of the anisotropy tensor in OpenFOAM and there-
fore the undertow improvement is not examined in this thesis.

some essential background knowledge on two-phase simulation of surface waves and the
numerical modelling of them is presented.

2.1.4
Closure problem of turbulence
Regarding the modelling of a turbulent ﬂow with the RANS method, the available equations
for the mean ﬂow are 4, namely the RANS equation for each direction (Equation (13)) and
the continuity equation for the mean ﬂow (Equation (7)). However, there are 10 unknowns,
namely the three components of the mean ﬂow velocity, ui, the mean pressure, p and the
six unique components of the Reynolds stress tensor, τi j. This leads to a closure problem,
also known as the closure problem of turbulence. Hence, additional equations are needed,
in order to solve the existing problem.
Numerous methods exist to close the problem of turbulence, each with different as-
sumptions and equations, yielding a variety of models. However in this thesis only two
models are examined, the k −ω and Stress−ω models, proposed by Wilcox [5].

2.2
Two-phase simulation of surface waves
A multi-phase simulation is used to solve ﬂow problems comprising of different phases
(solids or ﬂuids), and take also into account the interaction between them. Surface waves
require a two-phase approach, due to the existence of both air and water, especially in the
surf zone, where the interaction between them is of utmost importance. A two-phase simu-
lation in this thesis is conducted with the open source CFD software OpenFOAM, utilizing
the waves2FOAM toolbox, which is based on the interFOAM solver and the volume of
ﬂuid method (VOF). It should be mentioned that with the VOF method, a variable called
alpha.water deﬁnes the percentage of a ﬂuid in respect to a second one, within a computa-
tional cell. This means, that for this case an alpha.water value of 0 deﬁnes a cell comprised
purely of air, while a value of 1 means that the cell contains only water. Any other value
between 0 and 1 deﬁnes a mixture of air-water.
The turbulence closure models used are the ones mentioned above, with speciﬁc mod-
iﬁcations that are described brieﬂy below. At ﬁrst, both models are modiﬁed to include
an additional buoyancy term in the k and Reynolds stress equations, for the k −ω and
Stress−ω models respectively. These modiﬁcations allow for two-phase (air-water) simu-
lations, which is a common practice for similar RANS-based models. In addition to that,
some additional modiﬁcations are proposed by Larsen et al. [4] to combat the overproduc-
tion of turbulence in free surface waves. Previously deﬁned RANS-based models tend to
overproduce turbulence both prior- and post-breaking, accumulating unreasonable T.K.E.
and resulting in possibly unreliable results. Larsen et al. introduced elegantly an additional
stress-limiter function via ˜ω (see Equation (31)), dissipating any excess turbulence pro-
duced in near potential ﬂow regions, yet collapsing to the original model otherwise. This
makes the modiﬁed k −ω model by Larsen et al., the ﬁrst RANS stabilized model to sim-
ulate surface waves, without interacting in an intrusive way in regions of interest, such as
the surf zone. It should be mentioned, that the Stress−ω model, later implemented by Li et

al. [6], is found to be neutrally stable, meaning that it is an additional RANS-based stable
model for such simulations. More details about the two models can be found in the original
papers by Larsen et al. [4] and Li et al. [6].

3.

Machine Learning
Machine learning algorithms have already been utilized in numerous ﬁelds, becoming an

4.2
Fitting a neural network
Training a neural network, also called ﬁtting, is a complicated process. Although there
are many ways to train a neural network, in this thesis only supervised learning will be
examined. Key information regarding the ﬁtting of neural networks is mentioned below.
4.2.1
Supervised learning
The ﬁtting a model via supervised learning is an iterative process requiring a data set.
The data set is divided into input values and target output values. In each iteration, also
called epoch, a complete use of the input data set occurs, obtaining in the end the corre-
sponding output. Subsequently, the error between the output values and the target output
ones is calculated. This error is used by the model to update the weights and biases, in
an effort to minimize the loss. The error is calculated using the so-called cost function.
Some widely used cost functions are the Mean Squared Error (MSE), Mean Absolute Error
(MAE) and Mean Squared Logarithmic Error (MSLE). For this thesis the MSE, shown in
Equation (36), will be utilized, since it is widely used for regression problems and data sets
with outlier values.

4.2.2
Backpropagation
As it has already been mentioned, the model is penalized based on the loss calculated,
updating the weights and biases of the model. This is achieved via an algorithm called
backpropagation. With this algorithm the gradient of the cost function is calculated with
respect to the weights and biases. The aim is to obtain the smallest value of the cost
function possible, through the update of weights and biases. This method is also known
as gradient descent, due to the effort of ﬁnding the global minimum of the loss function
via its gradient. At this point, the importance of the activation function can be seen. The
mathematical properties of the activation function are of essence in the backpropagation
method. In particular, the derivative, along with its properties, and the non-linearity of
the activation function results in a different learning process for the model via the gradient
descent algorithm. Gradient descent utilizes a hyperparameter called learning rate, which
deﬁnes the rate the optimizer searches for the global minimum of the cost function. A
representation of a cost function and the gradient descent process is depicted in Figure 3.

4.2.3
Overﬁtting and unerﬁtting
The aim of the ﬁtting process is to create a model generalized enough to be able to predict
correct values for a data set never used in its training process. Therefore, certain compli-
cations arise based on this. The ﬁrst common problem encountered when creating a model
is overﬁtting, where the model learns the training data set in such depth, that it is unable to
produce accurate enough results with a data set that has never seen before. The other one is
underﬁtting, where the problem is complicated, yet the model is too simple to map all the
possible nonlinearities.
To avoid these problems, during the ﬁtting process data are usually separated in a train-
ing and a validation data set. The training data set is used to train the model and update the
weights and biases, based on the cost function mentioned above. On the other hand. the use
of the validation data set acts as a safety trigger to stop the training process, when signs of
overﬁtting occur. It should be mentioned, that the validation data set is shown to the model
after each epoch, meaning that the ﬁtted model is biased towards this data set. The training
of the model is carried out as long as the validation data set error keeps decreasing.
Another common practice is to add a tolerance and a checkpoint to the ﬁtting process.
The tolerance is added, due to the fact that the training/validation errors are not decreasing
smoothly. Therefore, the model is allowed to show increased errors in speciﬁc epochs,
as long as the error has decreased to a global minimum at least once within the tolerance
range. When tolerance is added, a checkpoint method is also necessary to be utilized. Since
during the ﬁtting the error is allowed also to increase, the epoch where the minimum error
was recorded is needed. This is feasible by saving the weights and biases of the model,
each time a global minimum is found.

the network need to be carefully chosen. In addition, the regularization and randomization
of the data prior to ﬁtting has been found to improve the results. The ﬁtting process and the

4.3
Bayesian optimization
The Bayesian optimization is an algorithm used to ﬁnd global maxima or minima of a
certain black-box function. The process requires the user to choose the variables of the
function, along with their range of search. The Bayesian optimization works by construct-
ing a posterior distribution of functions (Gaussian process) that best describes the function
requiring optimization. As the number of observations grows, the posterior distribution
improves, and the algorithm becomes more certain of which regions in parameter space are
worth exploring and which are not, as seen in Figure 5 [11].
14
==PAGE:20==
Fig. 5. Example of the Bayesian optimization.[11]
Thus, the Bayesian optimization is an iterative process. More speciﬁcally a number
of random and search iterations need to be deﬁned. The number of random iterations
must be adequate to map the function in the ﬁrst stage of the optimization. Subsequently.
the number of search iterations must be enough to locate the global minimum. For the
application of the Bayesian optimization on neural networks the global minima of the cost
function needs to be found. Therefore, an evaluation data set is needed, based on which the
error is calculated. Consequently, all the previous calculated errors are used to update the
variables. The evaluation data set needs to be independent of the training and the validation
data sets deﬁned previously, to ensure a generalized model. The optimization is conducted
through the Bayesian Optimization package, available on GitHub [11].
4.4
TBNN
In RANS turbulence modelling, the most widely-used closure models (e.g. k −ω), utilize
the Boussinesq approximation, resulting in a linear relation between the Reynolds stresses,
τi j and the mean strain rate tensor, Si j (Equation (24)). This results in low accuracy of the
anisotropic part of turbulence, aij, which is responsible for poor performance in speciﬁc
ﬂow features, such as ﬂow separation as seen in Ling et al.[1]. To combat that, two pos-
sible options are: a) create a model avoiding the Boussinesq approximation or b) improve
the anisotropic part of turbulence by applying machine learning methods. The ﬁrst method
has been taking place for many years, with the models being computationally too expensive
or not being preferred for other reasons. On the other hand, machine learning algorithms
have been recently applied showcasing promising results in steady ﬂows. Ling et al. came
up with a machine learning algorithm, which is used to improve the ﬂow ﬁeld of speciﬁc
steady ﬂows. In this approach, an innovative architecture of a deep neural network has
been devised, in order to embed Galilean invariance on the predicted anisotropy tensor, bi j.
Consequently, the improved anisotropy tensor is linked with the RANS-based simulation,
propagating the corrected anisotropy tensor until a new steady-state is achieved. Propagat-
ing the anisotropy tensor is the ultimate goal, showcasing impressive results in steady ﬂows
both in Ling et al. and Kaandrop et al. [1] [7].

5.
TBNN applied on breaking waves
In contrast to the steady ﬂows previously examined by Ling et al. and Kaandrop et al., the
breaking waves case is a time-dependant and periodic problem. This means, that a model
used in such a case needs to be applicable at any time instant within a period. Hence,
the ﬁtting process must consist of data from several time instants. During the initial stage
of conﬁguring the TBNN, it was found that the required time instants can stem from a
single period, which can be explained by the periodicity of the waves. The number of time
instants needed is a subject of examination in the forthcoming section. Finally, at this point
it becomes clear why the number of periods simulated was chosen to be 41, with the ﬁrst
period used in the ﬁtting process of the TBNN. The rest of the periods are utilized as test
data sets for both instantaneous and time-averaged results.
5.1
Training and test data sets
The process of ﬁtting the TBNN requires a low-ﬁdelity data set, such as the k −ω model
and a high-ﬁdelity one, such as DNS or LES simulations. The aim is for the model to be
able to improve anisotropy predictions, based on solely low-ﬁdelity data, as seen in Ling
et al. [1]. In this work RANS-based simulations, with a k −ω closure model, along with
DNS/LES simulations were used to ﬁt the TBNN. For the breaking wave case, the low-
ﬁdelity data are extracted from RANS simulations, using the stabilized k −ω model. The
high-ﬁdelity data are extracted from RANS simulations, using the Stress−ω model, since
it has been shown to yield accurate results for both the spilling and plunging breaker cases
of the Ting & Kirby experiments [6].
In addition, the essence of the TBNN is its applicability on ﬂow cases it has not been
trained on. Thus, only one of the wave breaking cases can be used in the training process,
while the other one should be used only for the ﬁnal results. In this case, the spilling breaker
was chosen to be the training data set, while the plunging breaker was selected to be the
test one. This means, that the data from the plunging breaker are not used in any way in the
training process of the TBNNs examined. The aim of the model is not only to have high
accuracy in the 40 periods of the spilling breaker case (not directly used in training), but
also in the plunging breaker case. Having said that, the validation and evaluation data sets
stem from the spilling breaker case as well, in order to keep the model unbiased towards
the plunging breaker case. The ﬂow chart of the training and optimization processes is
depicted in Figure 7.

5.2
Data pre-processing
The complications arising from a time-dependant problem is the number of data needed
to ﬁt the model. The domain of the numerical simulation, shown in Figure 1, has a high
discretization in space, with the total number of cells ranging from approximately 200,000
to 350,000 between the two cases. It becomes apparent that the amount of data for multiple
time instants is immense, resulting in a difﬁculty in ﬁtting the model.
However, it is also evident, that not the complete domain is needed, since speciﬁc areas
are of greater interest than others. In particular, areas with no turbulence do not need to
be mapped in terms of the anisotropy tensor, since bi j is bound to the turbulent kinetic
energy. Hence, parts of the domain with low levels of turbulence can be omitted, such as
the ﬂat bottom region (including the relaxation zone). The quantity selected to specify the
turbulence importance is k/(ων) = νt/ν, with a threshold value set to unity. A smaller
value of this variable means that either the turbulent kinetic energy is small and dissipated
solely due to the ﬂuid’s kinematic viscosity or any T.K.E. produced is dissipated by the
speciﬁc dissipation rate, ω.
Furthermore, only grid points containing water are needed, meaning that a threshold
value of the alpha.water variable must be chosen. An alpha.water value of 0 means
that the cell contains only air, while a value of 1 indicates that it contains only water. A
threshold value of 0.5 is chosen, due to the fact that cells with a mixture of water-air might
be important both in the free surface and inside the surf zone.
Finally, the Stress−ω model does not ensure realizability, as mentioned by Wilcox [5].
Realizabilty is an aspect of turbulence modelling that although supported it is difﬁcult to
be applied within a closure model. However, in this case it is necessary to include only grid
points with realistic values for the turbulent quantities, since the quality of the data used is
of essence. Although not many such points exist, any grid point violating Equation (20) and
Equation (21) is omitted in purpose. It should be noted, that most of these points are located
in the air-water interface. This means that these unrealistic values of the anisotropy tensor
might be attributed to the nature of the two-phase simulation and the buoyancy production
term.

Differences between the two RANS-based models at this stage become apparent. In
particular, components b11 and b22 are predicted differently, with k −ω model displaying
an alternating pattern mainly below wave crest positions. This can be explained by the fact
that the k−ω model performs relatively poor at wave deceleration stages [16]. In particular,
it has been found that the k −ω model under-predicts both T.K.E. and R12 during adverse
pressure gradients, when the model was compared with experimental data by Jensen et
al.[17]. In the same experimental data set, the Stress−ω model has been found to yield
better results during the deceleration stage [6]. By examining the anisotropy tensor from
both models, values of opposite sign are predicted at this stage.
What is more, a signiﬁcant difference between the two models can be seen in the b33
component. The k −ω model predicts an anisotropy two order of magnitudes lower than
the Stress−ω one, meaning that the anisotropy is practically zero (b33 < 10−2 ≈0). The
Stress−ω model predicts an anisotropic part of R33 at two locations, one being close to
the free surface and the other inside the surf zone. The anisotropy values close to the free
surface can be considered with extreme caution, due to the buoyancy term added to the
initial model equations. However, the anisotropy values inside the surf zone show a more
realistic view of a breaking wave, in which the violence of the breaking induces anisotropy
in all three normal components of the Reynolds stresses tensor (diagonal elements). The
importance of the non-zero b33 value is highlighted in the ﬂows examined by Ling et al.
and Kaandrop et al. (e.g. Periodic Hills case) [1][7].
Moreover, the Stress−ω model show some grid points with unrealizable values of the
anisotropy component b12, especially close to the free surface. This highlights the need of
the realizability ﬁlter mentioned in Section 5.2.

8.
Conclusion
At ﬁrst, the importance of the RANS-based simulations has been highlighted, since they
provide a reliant and computationally cheap alternative to simulate turbulent ﬂows. The
models used in this thesis are the k −ω and Stress−ω models introduced by Wilcox, but
modiﬁed to be applicable in multi-phase simulations. The modiﬁcations include a buoy-
ancy production term and an additional stress-limiter function to avoid the problem of the
over-production of turbulent kinetic energy. The aforementioned models are used in the
open-source CFD software OpenFOAM utilizing the waves2FOAM toolbox, for simulat-
ing wave breaking cases. The wave breaking cases examined are spilling and plunging
breakers, following experimental data sets, which are ultimately used for comparing the
results. In addition, the realizability constraints of turbulent quantities have been stated,
although the aforementioned models do not incorporate such a feature.
Moreover, the mechanism of machine learning algorithms and more speciﬁcally that of
neural networks is introduced. The complexity of ﬁtting a model is made clear, along with
measures to alleviate arising problems. The importance of machine learning algorithms ap-
plied in turbulence modelling is also mentioned. In particular, such algorithms are utilized
to bypass the Boussinesq approximation used in most RANS-based models and improve
the anisotropy part of turbulence. The machine learning algorithm used in this thesis is the
Tensor Basis Neural Network (TBNN), which embeds Galilean invariance in the predicted
anisotropy values. Although the original TBNN utilizes 5 invariants as input features, 23
additional features have been introduced recently by researchers. Thus, different TBNN
conﬁgurations are examined based on the number of features given.
Furthermore, machine learning has only been applied in steady ﬂows. Breaking waves
is a time-dependant problem, meaning that the predicted values must be accurate for any
time instant within a wave period. Therefore, the training of the TBNN must be based on a
variety of time instants within a period, with the number of instants being a subject of ex-
amination in the present work. However, a time-dependant ﬂow in combination with a large
and highly-discretized domain give rise to problems regarding the amount of data needed
for the TBNN. The increased amount of data required in this case renders the application of
the TBNN prohibitive, due to the excess computational effort needed for data manipulation
and training of the model. For this reason, certain ﬁlters have been introduced, based on
the importance and quality of the data. More speciﬁcally, only grid points with signiﬁcant
turbulence (k/(ων) = νt/ν ≥1) are included, meaning that the relaxation zone is omit-
ted. Moreover, cells containing water are being taken into account (alpha.water ≥0.5),
in addition to applying a realizability ﬁlter. Having said that, the anisotropy components
for the two RANS-based models are illustrated highlighting their fundamental differences.
Under wave crests, different anisotropy values can be seen between the two models and
more speciﬁcally in the deceleration stage. In particular, the inability of the k −ω model
to perform accurately when an adverse pressure gradient is present shows in the anisotropy
tensor as well.
After reducing reasonably the computational effort needed, the different TBNN con-
ﬁgurations examined are optimized. The optimization process is conducted based on a
Bayesian optimization, identifying the hyperparameters of the optimal TBNN for each
case. The hyperparameters examined are the number of hidden layers, the number of
nodes and the learning rate. Subsequently, the optimized TBNNs are tested on the test
cases, namely the second period simulated of the spilling breaker and the ﬁrst one of the
39
==PAGE:45==
plunging breaker. Based on the results, the 5-feature 24-instant TBNN is selected, without
disregarding the possibility using a 28-feature model in the future.
The selected TBNN is used to produce instantaneous, time- and phase-averaged results.
The instantaneous results show a signiﬁcant improvement of the anisotropy, mainly for the
b11 and b22 components. Consequently, anisotropy predictions are used to calculate speciﬁc
Reynolds stresses and examine whether these can be improved directly without propagating
the anisotropy tensor in the RANS-based simulation. Time-averaged proﬁles of speciﬁc
Reynolds stresses and phase-averaged plots of R12 are presented, proving that such an
attempt is not successful. However, time-averaged proﬁles of the anisotropy components
highlight differences between the two models, potentially indicating the underlying issues
behind the differences in the prediction of the undertow velocities. The TBNN results
show adequate agreement in the tendency of the time-averaged b proﬁles, indicating that it
certainly improves the anisotropy tensor.
40
==PAGE:46==
9.
Future work
The ultimate goal of using such a machine learning algorithm is propagating the anisotropy
tensor in the RANS-based simulation. This was straightforward in steady ﬂows, since the
anisotropy predictions were saved, before being used in the RANS-based simulations. In
this case, the problem is time-dependant, meaning that the TBNN must be compiled with
OpenFOAM. Subsequently, in every time step OpenFOAM should call the TBNN to predict
the anisotropy tensor and use it in the calculations. In addition, a steady-state needs to be
reached once again, after propagating the anisotropy tensor, before data can be extracted.
This means, that the overall process might be proved cost ineffective. However, should
such a process produce improved results, it could highlight the underlying issues behind
the k −ω model and its inaccuracy of undertow velocities inside the surf zone.
Moreover, the realizability ﬁlter is applied in the TBNN after the predictions. This
means, that it is not actively part of the training process, but only part of the optimization
process. Ideally, the realizability ﬁlter should be introduced into the TBNN architecture,
since it acts as safety net for over-predicting absolute values of the anisotropy tensor. This
way, the weights and biases are updated based on the realizable values predicted at previous
epochs, leading in a better performance.
Furthermore, the effect of each input feature can be examined, since 28 features could
be regarded as too many. By identifying the importance of each feature on the present case,
some of them can be dropped, potentially improving the TBNN’s accuracy and remove
the signs of overﬁtting. Finally, the possibilities in optimizing a neural network are end-
less. The effect of a variety of hyperparameters has not been examined, meaning that the
TBNN’s accuracy can be further increased in the future.
