Abstract
The Amazon basin is the worlds largest watershed containing the riches biodiversity on the planet. It
is also home to a growing population, many indigenous peoples, increasing human activity in the form
of hydropower development, agriculture expansion and extracting industry, all of which entails com-
petition for, and degradation of, the otherwise plentiful water resources. The countries in the Amazon
created in 2018 the Strategic Action Program (SAP) in order to respond to the environmental and social
pressures in the basin, with a focus on the management of its water resources. This paper aimed to
provide the countries of the Amazon basin with a scientiﬁc basis to help address the issues identiﬁed in
the SAP. In order to do so the paper present a holistic background study on the Amazon basin. This was
used in combination with the WHAT-IF hydro-economic optimisation model to evaluate the state and
sustainability of hydropower and agriculture production in the basin. The evaluation was done on the
current situation and under diﬀerent scenarios of hydropower and irrigation development and climate
change. The paper ﬁnds that some catchments might experience over capacity in their hydropower
plants, due to large seasonal and annual variations in precipitation, which is aggravated under the cli-
mate scenarios. The agriculture production is in many catchment substantially lower than under ideal
conditions of full water demand satisfaction, but this can be greatly improved by irrigating targeted
crops in vulnerable areas. The paper indicates that this can be achieved without consequences to the
production of hydropower and the other water users in the basin, as the total amount of water used and
lost in the basin comprises a negligible amount of the water available. Both hydropower and agriculture
was found to be connected to all three of the top priority issued identiﬁed in the SAP, namely water
pollution, deforestation and biodiversity loss. The paper concludes that a proper assessment of poten-
tial hydropower over capacity should be conducted when planning new hydropower in combination
with an assessment of the river system vulnerability, and that irrigation development could present a
way for the countries to halt agriculture expansion while maintaining or even increasing agricultural
production. The paper further provides a set of speciﬁc recommendations to the countries involved
based on the paper ﬁndings, and a list of recommendations for future work, which would increase the
precision and accuracy of the results and improve the robustness of the ﬁndings.

Introduction
Water is the cornerstone for all life on the planet and is here in abundance. Sadly less than 3% of the
water we have is freshwater and less than 1% is ﬁt for human consumption and readily available to us
(World Bank, 2020). The water we have is shared between a myriad of lifeforms and when we as hu-
mans take actions to meet our needs, it is important that it is in balance with the natural world around
us. The freshwater found in rivers is even smaller, only 0.006% of the total freshwater volume is found
here. This water is not evenly distributed, and although freshwater is scarce in many regions around
the planet, it is not the case for the Amazon. The Amazon river discharges more water than the next 9
largest rivers combined and constitutes 15% of the total discharge from rivers around the world (FAO,
2015a).
This establishes that useful water is not as plentiful as it may appear, but relative to most places in the
world, it is in the Amazon.
This abundance of freshwater is matched by an equal abundance of natural water users. The Amazon
basin is home to 10% of all global species, and has the most diverse ﬂora and fauna found on the planet
(Blanco-Gutiérrez et al., 2020; European Parliament, 2020; FAO, 2015a). It is the most well preserved
rainforest we have and constitutes around half of what remains in the world. The vast forest and river
system sustains the life of animals, plants and insects but also humans both within and outside its bor-
ders.
The basin contains a sizeable population, large agricultural industry and numerous hydropower plants,
from small generators to some of the largest plants in the world. How the population lives spans from
major city centres to small indigenous communities, and the issues they face and their resilience to
them varies greatly (FAO, 2015a; Barthem et al., 2004).
The agriculture industry in the form of crop cultivation and cattle ranching is and has been expanding
within the basin both in order to feed the local population but mainly to meet global demand (Antonelli
et al., 2018; Davidson et al., 2012). This expansion is driving the large scale deforestation seen in the
basin, with consequences to ecosystems and with impacts to the hydrologic cycle, on a local and re-
gional scale.
The hydropower capacity within the basin is also increasing and so is the size of the individual projects.
The creation of reservoirs in order to hydropower production can ﬂood enormous swaths of land with
detrimental eﬀects on ecosystems and indigenous communities. The dams also create barriers for mi-
gratory ﬁsh spices and can alter river morphology.
There are 8 countries represented in the basin: Bolivia, Brazil, Colombia, Ecuador, Guyana, Peru, Suri-

needs. As a result of this collaboration the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB) has in 2018 initi-

aim of this report is to act as a scientiﬁc basis to help address these issues. In order to do so the report

• Create a holistic background analysis of the study area, the Amazon basin.
• Assess the current state of hydropower and agriculture production, and the vulnerability to cli-

• Evaluate the eﬀect of developing hydropower and irrigation in the basin, also in relation to climate
change
• Determine the implications of hydropower and agriculture on water pollution, deforestation and

To do this the paper will ﬁrst present the holistic background analysis of the basin, including human
activities, land use and environmental conditions, which will then be used together with the WHAT-IF
model to assess the interrelations of the Water Energy Food (WEF) Nexus under diﬀerent scenarios
with a focus on hydropower and agriculture, which will all be related back to the three priority issues
in the discussion. The scenarios include the current situation, hydropower and irrigation development
and climate change. The WHAT-IF is a hydro-economic optimisation model developed in collabora-
tion between COWI, MIT and DTU, which deals with the interplay of the WEF Nexus (Payet-Burin
et al., 2019). The model allows for estimating the value and sustainability of new and existing water
infrastructure projects such as hydropower plants and irrigation systems, and to evaluate the eﬀect of
projected scenarios. The paper aims to give a better understanding of the activities and conﬂicts within
the basin and to act as a ﬁrst iteration of the model implementation in the study area. The paper draws
conclusions with respect to uncertainties and identiﬁes areas where the model setup and data could be
improved in order to increase the robustness of the ﬁndings.

The Amazon basin
The basin contains complex ecosystems and provides various services in the form of crops, electricity,
livestock, lumber, minerals, oil, gas, coal. The study area and the issues it faces will be investigated in
this section using a both quantitative and qualitative approach.
2.1
Population
The Amazon basin is characterised by low population density (Barthem et al., 2004; FAO, 2015a). This
is positive for the preservation of the forest and ecosystems, but it has the indirect consequence that
data collection is not prioritised and solid recent data can be scarce for certain parameters. The popu-
lation today is estimated based on geospatial Worldpop data to be around 36 million, which is mainly
concentrated in urban areas along the Amazon river and its tributaries (WorldPop, 2020; FAO, 2015a).
Around 30% of the population is living in the Peruvian Amazon, and the same in the Brazilian, around
20% live in the Bolivian Amazon, 10% in the Ecuadorian, 5% in the Colombian and less than 0.5% in
Guyana, Suriname and Venezuela.
The 8 largest cities in the basin are Manaus (2.3 mil.), La Paz (1.9 mil.), La Santa Cruz de la Sierra (1.7
mil.), Natal (1.5 mil.), Cochabamba (1.3 mil.), Iquitos (0.5 mil.), Cusco (0.5 mil.) and Porto Velho (0.5
mil.), these can be seen in Figure 1 along 137 cities of various sizes also located within the basin (Natu-
ral Earth, nd; PopulationStat, 2020).

Besides urban dwellers the basin is also home to rural communities and many indigenous peoples
(FAO, 2015a; Barthem et al., 2004). There is an estimated 433,000 indigenous people in the Amazon
and 75% lives in protected reserves which covers between 15-27% of the basin, depending on source
(Le Tourneau, 2015; FAO, 2015a; Codato et al., 2019). These areas have generally experienced a lower
degree of deforestation due to the presence of protective legislation and the resistance from its inhabi-
tants (Adeney et al., 2009). The rural and indigenous population in the basin is assumed to be vulnerable
to external stresses as they lack the capacity to treat water if it gets polluted or get water from alterna-
tive sources, or acquire alternative food sources if needed.
Indigenous people live of a diet consisting of both horticulture produce, gathered fruits and vegeta-
bles, hunting and ﬁshing. The importance of each food source varies from area to area, but in riverine
areas ﬁshing may be the most important animal component of the diet, with people consuming wild ﬁsh
at least once a day (Sierra, 2018). However, protective legislation on indigenous territories have been
under constant debate, as it have been seen as a hindrance to development (Bebbington et al., 2018).
Thus legislation on this area has seen both pressure and major roll backs. This have aﬀected both the
livelihood of indigenous peoples and the ecosystem in general (Cecilia Burgos González, 2010).

The economy in the Amazon has historically been of an extracting nature, and based on minerals, fossil
fuels, rubber and lumber (FAO, 2007; UCLG, 2018; ACTO, 2018). This type of economy have not managed
to create prosperity or equality in the region and it is estimated that around 35-45% of the population in
the Brazilian Amazon lives below the poverty line and around 20% live in indigence (FAO, 2007; ACTO,
2018).
Human capital in the Amazon region is seen as a hindrance towards moving away from agriculture
and industry and into a knowledge based economy (ACTO, 2018). The number of trained Ph.Ds in the
Brazilian Amazon is only around 300 a year. To put it in perspective, Denmark produced around 2000
Ph.Ds in 2017 (Danske Universiteter, 2018). Relative to population size that is 41 times more Ph.D.s
educated in Denmark compared to the Amazon.
2.2
The watershed
The amazon basin spans over 6 million square kilometres covering one third of South America, and is
the biggest watershed in the world (FAO, 2015a). It lies within the borders of Brazil(63.9%), Peru(15.6%),
Bolivia(11.7%), Columbia(5.6%), Ecuador(2.1%), Venezuela(0.9%) and Guyana(0.2%). Although the main
part of the basin lies within Brazil, the headwaters are located in the Andean regions namely Bolivia,
Peru, Ecuador and Colombia (Barthem et al., 2004). The Amazon river has many sources but originates
primarily from the two rivers the Ucayali and the Marañon located in the Peruvian Andes (FAO, 2015a).
The Ucayali and Marañon as well as the largest tributaries in the basin and their catchment areas are

2.3
Ecosystems
The Amazon basin contains a myriad of animals and plants, with 14,000 species of trees, 26,000 species
of plants, 2.5 million species of insects, and 2,000 species of mammals and birds constituting 10% of
all global species (Blanco-Gutiérrez et al., 2020; European Parliament, 2020; FAO, 2015a; Gomes et al.,
2019). It is the most well preserved rainforest on the planet but is the subject of constant stresses
from deforestation and climate change. Deforestation is projected could cause a 19-36% and climate
change a 31-37% decline in tree species by 2050 (Gomes et al., 2019). This would result in terrestrial
fauna loosing an averaged 65% of their original habitat and threaten 53% with extinction, according
to the International Union for Conservation of Nature’s (IUCN) Red List Criteria (Gomes et al., 2019;
IUCN, 2020). The basin also contains an abundance of fresh water ﬁsh species. Based on a database of
spacial data made available by the the Red List, 3,366 ﬁsh species were identiﬁed in the basin based on
232,936 records (Red List, 2020). Other estimates suggests a range between 1,300-3,500 species and a
comprehensive study based on a combination of published data and ichthyological collections arrives
at an estimate of 2,716 species (Dagosta and Pinna, 2019). This makes the Amazon the most ﬁsh species

rich region in the world, with more species than North America, Europe and Australia combined and
twice as many as the second most diverse basin in the wold, Congo. The ecosystem of the Amazon
is an interplay between species, most of which occupy unique ecological niches, and although species
richness is viewed as creating robustness in an ecological system it also makes it impossible to foresee
the large scale eﬀects of local changes to the system (S. Albert et al., 2015).
One of the initiatives proposed to mitigate the decline in species richness is to regenerate rainforest from
areas that have seen signiﬁcant human impacts (Whitworth et al., 2016). It is estimated that regenerated
forest can reestablish between 57-87% of previous species richness, and it could thus be seen as a good
counter measure, but still not as eﬀect full as preventing primary forest degradation from occurring.
2.4
Land use
The basin contains diﬀerent human activities such as agriculture, power plants and natural resource
extraction which takes up land. These activities and their impacts will be presented in this section.
2.4.1
Agriculture
Based on geospatial data from the International Food Policy Research Institute’s (IFPRI) Spatial Pro-
duction Allocation Model (SPAM) there is an estimated 12.5 mil. ha of harvest cropland within the
Amazon basin (IFPRI, 2019). Of this, 63% is in Brazil, 20% in Bolivia, 15% in Peru, 2% in Ecuador and
1% in Columbia. Venezuela accounts for less than 0.1%. The most important crops in the basin are
soybean and maize which account for 45% and 16% of the total harvested area respectively. A list of
all crops grown in the basin can be found in Appendix E.1. Especially Brazil and Bolivia are major
producers of these two crops, and are among the largest exporters in the world (Garrett et al., 2018).
Soybean and Maize are the largest crops in Brazil (59% and 19%) and Bolivia (40% and 11%), but in Peru,
Ecuador and Columbia the crops are more diversiﬁed and here there is almost no soybean production.
A list containing the quantity of the crops included in the study grown in each country can be found
in Appendix A.1. Irrigation is only practised to a small degree and is done on roughly 4% of the total
harvested area and mainly in Peru (60%) and Bolivia (34%). Still South-eastern Amazonia has high crop
yields, comparable to those in the United States (Lathuillière et al., 2016). Figure 3 shows the cultivated
area of the crops included in the model. The ﬁgure is made based on global spatially-disaggregated crop

2.5.2
Hydropower
Hydropower provides a way for the countries connected to the great hydropower potential of the Ama-
zon basin to reduce reliance on fossil fuels and to reach climate targets. Studies have, however, shown
that this green technology does come with a set of drawbacks, which can be especially detrimental
in the context of the Amazon (Berchin et al., 2015).The construction of large hydropower plants in the
Amazon can entail ﬂooding of vast forest areas, causing damage to local ﬂora and fauna, displacement of
native people and riverside settlers and changes in both upstream and downstream hydrology (Berchin
et al., 2015; UCLG, 2018). Furthermore, the construction requires infrastructure in the form of roads
and connections to the grid, and the power production allows for increased industrial activity, all with
a negative eﬀect on the ecosystem. The impacts of hydropower will be further addressed at the end of
the section.

Impacts
• Loss of biodiversity and ecosystems:
The construction of dams can alter the ﬂow patterns of a river, destroying the habitats of highly
specialised niche occupying species (Lees et al., 2016). The dams also obstructs the ﬂow of sed-
iments and can block the natural migration of ﬁsh species, blocking their passage to breeding
spots and breaking up populations. They can also result in the direct killing of ﬁsh in two ways
1) injure or kill migratory ﬁsh which concentrate immediately downstream of the plant, when
water is released at plant start-up, and 2) dragging ﬁsh into the turbines during water release
(CEMIG, 2017). It is estimated by Brazil’s Ministry of Environment that 71 of the ﬁsh species in
the Amazon basin are threatened and close to 70% is due to hydropower plants (ICMBIO, 2014).
Especially dams with reservoirs such as the Belo Monte, Jirau and Santo Antonio have large im-
pacts on ecosystems as they create permanent ﬂooding of massive areas (Fearnside, 2014b). Belo
Monte have ﬂood an estimated area 6589 km2 and Jirau and Santo Antonio a combined area of
529 km2 (Fearnside, 2006, 2014b).
• Displacement of people:
The ﬂooding of forest related to the ﬁlling of reservoirs can also lead to the displacement of people
living in these areas and the obstruction of ﬁsh migration can impact the livelihood of riverine
inhabitants who depend on the ﬁsh as a vital source of food and income (Fearnside, 2014b, 2016,
2017). An example of the latter is the giant catﬁsh on the Madeira river, which has been an
essential food source for the people living along the river. The construction of the Jirau and Santo
Antonio dams have obstructed its migratory routs, essential for its breeding cycles, and greatly

• Deforestation:
The improved infrastructure, such as roads and power, following the construction of hydropower

been expected.
• Greenhouse gas emissions:
The large forest areas ﬂooded to create reservoirs can results in organic matter decomposing
and emission of greenhouse gasses in the form of methane and CO2 (Demarty and Bastien, 2011;

in tropical regions tend to see large emissions of methane due to the creation of anoxic bottom
layers. The emissions observed from the Balbina dam has been estimated at around 3 million tons
CO2-eqs per year, equivalent to twice the yearly emissions of Copenhagen municipality (Kemenes
et al., 2011; Copenhagen Municipality, 2015). Similarly over a 40 year period the Belo Monte dam
is expected to have similar GHG emissions as a natural gas plant of the same size, due to this
phenomena (Fearnside, 2009).

2.7
Climate
Human activities like hydropower production and agriculture is deeply reliant on climate conditions
and are vulnerable to changes in precipitation (Arvor et al., 2017). This section will go through the
current climate condition, projected changes and the potential impacts of these
When precipitation falls upon the ground it either stays in the soil as soil-moisture and is then termed
"green water" or it gathers in rivers, lakes and reservoirs and is then termed "blue water" (Lathuillière
et al., 2016). Blue water is what is accessible for humans to use, whereas green water is not accessible for
humans, but is instead the water source of trees and plants. The water is recycled into the atmosphere
through the process of evapotranspiration (ET) from plants, soil and open water bodies. Estimates
suggest that the forest recycles between 30% to more than 50% of the precipitation it receives through
ET (Cox et al., 2008; FAO, 2015a). The water going into the atmosphere through ET later falls again as
precipitation, and in that way ET maintains precipitation in the area (Lathuillière et al., 2016).

2.7.2
Drivers of annual variability
The climate in the region is heavily inﬂuenced by the El Ninõ/Southern Oscillation (ENSO) (Da Silva
et al., 2019; Jiménez-Muñoz et al., 2016). Especially the eastern parts experience decreased river ﬂow
during El Ninõ year and increased ﬂows and ﬂooding during La Niña. Anomalities in the Atlantic
Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO), which is a natural variability in the sea surface temperature of the
Atlantic ocean, has also been found to aﬀect the regional precipitation patterns (Frajka-Williams et al.,
17
==PAGE:30==
Master thesis
Mathias Hermann
2017; Davidson et al., 2012). Together the ENSO in the Paciﬁc and the AMO in the Atlantic are large
scale events that can lead to impacts such as droughts in the basin (Da Silva et al., 2019; Jiménez-Muñoz
et al., 2016). The Amazon experienced severe droughts in 2005, 2010 and 2015 with a signiﬁcant increase
in aﬀected area (Panisset et al., 2018; Jiménez-Muñoz et al., 2016). Increasing Sea Surface Temperature
(SST) in the tropical Atlantic is associated with droughts in the western parts of the basin during austral
winter, whereas increasing SST in the paciﬁc during El Nino is associated with droughts in the eastern
part of the basin during austral summer. The drought of 2005 was thus related to an increasing SST in
the tropical Atlantic, in 2010 to increased SST of both the tropical Atlantic and the Paciﬁc, and in 2015
to increased SST in the paciﬁc. As SST is expected to increase so is the frequency of SST related events
such as droughts. This is one of the reasons that the occurrence of extreme weather events is expected
to increase due to climate change(Da Silva et al., 2019).
The long term changes in precipitation trends might occur due to a combination of global climate change
and local drivers (Llopart et al., 2014).
2.7.3
Local drivers of long term change
Local drivers from deforestation, largely driven by human activities, has been found to play a signiﬁcant
role in changing precipitation patterns and to exacerbate the negative impacts during drought years
(Bagley et al., 2014; FAO, 2015a; IPCC, 2007; Marengo et al., 2011). It has been estimated that around
30-50%, of the water that falls in the Amazon basin stems from recycling of water through cascading
evapotranspiration (Staal et al., 2018; Marengo et al., 2011; Coe et al., 2009; European Parliament, 2020).
It has further been estimated that if the deforestation reaches a tipping point of 40% its eﬀect on the
regional climate condition will be larger than that of climate change. Some simulations have shown that
deforestation has reduced overall ET in the basin by 4.6% and precipitation by 5.5% during dry seasons
(Bagley et al., 2014). Locally in areas turned into pasture the decrease in ET was found to be much larger
at 13-30%. The model further showed that much of the ET stemming from deforested areas fell over the
southern and eastern part of the basin, which is the areas most aﬀected by droughts and areas with a
lot of agriculture and hydropower.
When an area is deforested its ability to detain water decreases which results in an increase in runoﬀ
(Bagley et al., 2014). Initially this leads to an increase in blue water as less water is stored in the soil, but
it also leaves less water to be recycled through ET in the area. In this way deforestation entails changes
in the hydrological cycle. During the dry season when there is little moist air coming in from outside
the basin, the stored green water acts as a buﬀer by maintaining some amount of precipitation driven

by ET. Deep rooted trees have been found to be especially good as they can access soil-moisture down
to 11.5 meters depth (Markewitz et al., 2010). Deforestation and the decrease in ET is thus especially
problematic during the dry season.
Deforestation does not only have local impacts, but has been linked to a decrease in precipitation in
the nearby regions as well (A. B. Eltahir et al., 2016; Davidson et al., 2012; Molina et al., 2019). One
study showed that precipitation increases exponentially following streamlines over forest whereas it
exponentially decreases downwind of forested areas (Molina et al., 2019). This infers that forest both
increases the amount of precipitation and the distance it can reach and according to Antonio Donato
Nobre who is a senior researcher at Brazil’s National Institute of Amazonian Research, the Amazon is
responsible for large parts of South America being green rather than dessert (Antonio Donato Nobre,
2010; Lovejoy and Nobre, 2019). This moisture transport is enabling production of crops large regions
of South America and even in the U.S.A. and reduction or loss of this moisture ﬂux is thus also related
to large economic costs (European Parliament, 2020).
It has further been estimated that continued deforestation could potentially extend the dry season in
the southern and eastern parts of the basin (Costa and Pires, 2010). Other studies have found that the
eastern parts of the basin shows an increasing trend in days with low precipitation and decrease in
days with precipitation above 10mm (Da Silva et al., 2019). This is backed in a paper by Marengo and
Bernasconi (2015) where they ﬁnd a decrease in precipitation and an increase in temperature with water
deﬁcits and a longer dry season, which is expected to increase the arid area of the eastern basin. Thus
the future climate of the eastern basin is expected to be drier and to have a longer dry season. On top
of this, other models shows a shortening of the wet season over the southern Amazon as well (Arvor
et al., 2017).
2.7.4
Future projections
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has in their 5’th assessment estimated the
current risk of reduced water availability in Central and South America to be low/medium, but looking a
little further to 2030-2040 the risk increases to medium/high, and looking yet further again to 2080-2100
the risk becomes high/very high (IPCC, 2014a). The Amazon has experienced increased mean rainfall
over the last 40 years, but although the IPCC predicts that future precipitation patters will still show
an increase over the northern Andes, precipitation over the eastern parts of the basin is expected to
decrease (IPCC, 2007). Other simulations shows a general decrease in precipitation over the Amazon,
but all the projections are associated with a high degree of uncertainty (IPCC, 2007; Llopart et al.,
2014). Some estimates suggests that with the current land use changes precipitation in the amazon
basin will decrease with 12% in the wet season and 21% in the dry season by 2050 due to less water

recycling(Mulligan et al, 2013; Marengo and Bernasconi, 2015)
Following the decrease in precipitation over the eastern basin, some models predict that large areas will
see a change from rainforest to savanna (Marengo et al., 2011).
At the moment the Amazon forest is a net carbon sink, meaning that it takes up more CO2 than it emits,
and contains around 10% of all carbon stored in land ecosystems (Cox et al., 2008; Kalamandeen et al.,
2018). However, due to a combination of deforestation and climate change, this is starting to change
and models predict that if these drivers continue the basin might become a net source of atmospheric
carbon already by 2030 (Hubau et al., 2020).
2.8
Deforestation
The Amazonian ecosystem is facing continuous pressure from deforestation due especially to the ex-
pansion of pasture and soybean, but also due to other activities such as general agriculture expansion,
exploration of natural resources such as minerals, oil and gas, and general development related to pop-
ulation growth (Mulligan et al, 2013; Barona et al., 2010). The deforestation is not just a threat to the
biodiversity, studies have also shown that the loss of vegetation can directly aﬀect the hydrological
cycle on a regional and even global scale, changing precipitation patters and decreasing intensity and
frequency of rainfall (Coe et al., 2013; Lima et al., 2013; Staal et al., 2018; Marengo et al., 2011).
Today the forest cover in Amazonia has shrunk to just over 80% of its original area (Davidson et al.,
2012; Ministry of Environment, 2018). The total amount of deforestation in the basin declined between
2008-2014 compared to 2001-2007 (Kalamandeen et al., 2018). However, looking locally deforestation
increased in Bolivia over this period, from around 300 km2 to around 9560 km2, and in Peru from close
to non to around 2000 km2 . The reason for the great reduction in overall deforestation was due to a
steep decline in Brazilian deforestation. Although overall deforestation declined in this period, small
scale deforestation (< 1 ha) saw an increase of just over 30%, with deforestation happening to the same
degree inside protected conservation areas as outside them. Since granting areas the status of pro-
tected conservation areas is an important tool in order to decrease deforestation, this trend poses a new
deforestation management issue.
From 1996-2005 deforestation averaged 19.500 km2/year in the Brazilian Amazon, but in 2004, in an at-
tempt to reduce deforestation rates, the government of Brazil adopted the Action Plan for Deforestation
Prevention and Control in the Legal Amazon (PPCDAm)(Ministry of Environment, 2018; Government
of Brazil, 2008). The PPCDAm contained three phases which were to decrease the deforestation rate by
70% in the last phase from 2012-2015 and reach a 100% reduction in illegal logging by 2030. The plan
was quite successful and between 2009-2015 the deforestation rate was at around 70% compared to the
historical (TerraBrazilis, 2020; Secretariat for Social Communication, 2016). In 2009 the National Policy

to 20% of the historical rates by 2020 (Ministry of Environment, 2018). However, in 2016 an opposite

2020).
The previous success in reducing deforestation has been linked to governance on soy and cattle prac-

up until 2004 there had been a close coupling between cattle ranching and soybean expansion as drivers
and deforestation, but following the PPCDAm the two drivers had decoupled from deforestation, thus
no longer constituting the main drivers of deforestation (Gollnow and Lakes, 2014). The resent rise in
deforestation is thus likely linked to a re-coupling of ranching and soybean expansion to deforestation
due to a failure in governance of these two areas.
2.9
Water quality
The impacts of water pollution is a severe issue but has generally been contained to the areas around
the pollution sources, as the larger rainfall intensity and river ﬂow quickly dilutes the contamination
(Barthem et al., 2004; FAO, 2015a). Based on this it was in 2004 not deemed to be a signiﬁcant issue
or concern for the region in general. Nonetheless, was it in 2018 identiﬁed in the SAP as the highest
priority transboundary issue within the Amazon, by experts from all 8 countries in the basin (ACTO,
2018).
Chemicals and suspended solids are identiﬁed as being the main pollutants in the Amazon basin, with
mercury being the main chemical pollutant (Barthem et al., 2004; ACTO, 2018). Water pollution and
degradation stems primarily from mining industry and deforestation but is also associated with the
use of pesticides in agriculture, solid waste dumping, hydrocarbon extraction and poor wastewater
treatment from populated areas (Barthem et al., 2004; FAO, 2015a; Castello et al., 2013).
The contamination with mercury from the gold mining industry, has been a severe health hazard for the
riverine population for many years (European Parliament, 2020; Harada et al., 2001; Malm et al., 1995;
Castello et al., 2013). The livelihood of both urban, rural and indigenous people is dependent on the
water of Amazon river and its tributaries and the ﬁsh that lives within them. In the river the mercury
transform from its inorganic to its organic form, methylmercury, which is both highly poisonous and
bio-accumulative. Riverine populations, especially near sources of mercury contamination, thus risk
exposure to dangerous amounts of methylmercury from both their drinking water and from the ﬁsh
they eat.
Mining is not the only source of mercury in the Amazon rivers. Studies have suggested that the soil
in the Amazon is naturally rich in mercury, some of which dissolves in the rivers (Hacon et al., 2008).

The impact from this source of mercury is exacerbated by the large deforestation that have taken place
especially in Brazil, which have lead to an increase of sediment transport in the river systems (Barthem

The use of fertilisers and pesticides in the agricultural industry is also associated with issues of water
pollution. The use of pesticides is the most concerning, as the toxins can move through food chains
and aﬀect the health of the aquatic environment (Castello et al., 2013). The use of fertilizer can lead to
local issues of eutrophication in small streams, but due to the high river ﬂow which quickly removes
the nutrients and the modest use of fertilisers in the basin, this issue is minor.

3.1
The WHAT-IF model
The WHAT-IF is a hydro economic optimisation model which uses a single objective function that takes
externally given problem parameters and constraints related to the water users in the WEF Nexus as in-
puts and maximises the economic beneﬁts by altering decision variables (Payet-Burin et al., 2019). What
the problem parameters, constraints and decision variables are will be explained as they are encountered
through the section. The model can be run on a single scenario to e.g evaluate the sustainability of a
current water management system, or on multiple scenarios to more precisely predict e.g. the potential
beneﬁts of new development projects, such as new hydro power plants or irrigation schemes. To apply
the model a combination of software is used. Geospatial data is viewed, edited and analysed using the
free software QGIS, which is also used to produce images related to the study area. The model itself
is created in the open source programming language Python. The python model extracts data from
a number of excel ﬁles populated with data on water availability, agriculture, hydro power etc.. The
database and python model can be found in Appendix E.6.

Amazon basin contains one of the largest trans-boundary aquifers in the world (FAO, 2015a). It was

3.1.2
Concept explaination
Shadow price
The shadow price, or dual value, denotes the economic gain of loosening a binding constraint one
unit (FrontlineSolvers, 2021). A constraint becomes binding when either its lower or upper bound is
reached during the optimisation process. The optimisation process reaching the bounds of a constraint
indicates, that the model would have preferred to change the variable to a value outside the bounds of
the constrains, and thus that the optimal solution for a non-constrained optimisation would be diﬀerent
to what is currently reached.
Willingness to pay
In situations where there is not enough water to satisfy the demand of all users the module looks
at the Willingness To Pay (WTP) of each user relative to the WTP of the other users in the system
in order to determine which user should be prioritised. The WTP denotes how much any speciﬁc
user is willing to pay to have water allocated to them. For some users it can be assumed equal to the
economic consequence of them not having their water demand satisﬁed, however, some users, such as
the domestic users, do not derive an economic beneﬁt from receiving the water. Still the water have
a value to them which is given an economic value through their WTP. The WTP can be dynamic and
change over time and relative to degree of demand satisfaction. In this model the WTP for each user is
assumed constant. The dynamic WTP can be exempliﬁed by looking at the WTP for a domestic user. A
domestic user is likely willing to pay a very high price for water in order to meet basic needs, and then
willing to pay less and less as urgency of their needs decline. The water demand for basic domestic
needs, could also be given as a constraint in the model rather than a problem parameter.
Consumer and Producer surpluses
The consumer surplus is the diﬀerence between what the consumer is willing to pay for a commodity
and the price the consumer actually have to give and the producer surplus is the diﬀerence between what
the producer would be willing to sell for, at the actual price they get for their commodity (Investopedia,
2019, 2020).

3.3
Water module
The water availability in the individual sub-catchments is dictated by the amount of precipitation and
runoﬀwithin them, as well as river outﬂow from upstream sub-catchments. A sub-catchment can also
contain groundwater aquifers, lakes and reservoirs and in the case of the latter two, as water retardants
and storage. Groundwater aquifers can either be shallow and thus be part of the runoﬀsystem or they
can be deep and thus be a fossil water resources, which can be utilised but is not sustainable in the long
term, as the reservoirs are not reﬁlled.
The precipitation in the basin was determined using the Climatic Research Unit gridded Time Series
(CRU TS) containing monthly data spanning from 1991-2018 (Climatic Research Unit and NCAS, 2020).
The model is run over the last 19 years to take into account anomalies, such as ENSO and AMO years
as described in Section 2.7. These are important to include as years with low precipitation might create
conﬂicts in an otherwise stable water management system, and entail large economic and ecological
costs.
Runoﬀwas estimated using the Global RunoﬀReconstruction (GRUN) database which contains monthly
runoﬀ, predicted based on temperature and precipitation data (ETH Zurich, 2020). The precipitation
data is produced based on observations by using angular-distance weighting (ADW) interpolation and
the runoﬀdata is modelled based on precipitation and temperature. Thus is was expected that inter an-
nual and annual variation would show from the data and this variation was also found when analysing
the data in Section 2.7.
The basin contains various land uses that require water including hydro power plants, agriculture,
thermal power plants, industry, settlements/cities (in this project deﬁned as "domestic") and ecosystems.
Water use can be described as consumptive or non-consumptive depending on whether the water with-
drawn is lost or returned to the river. For users such as thermal power plants where the water is used
for cooling and hydro power plants there is little to no consumption of water, however, the users still
need to withdraw water in order to operate. The ecosystem as a water user will be discussed separately
in the next section.
3.3.1
Ecosystems
The ecosystem is an important water user to account for in the model (Payet-Burin et al., 2019). Flow
deviation and reduction due to dam construction and water withdrawal can have detrimental eﬀects
on the river ecosystems resulting in loss of ﬁsh biomass and biodiversity (Pastor et al., 2013). In or-
der to maintain the ecosystem an environmental ﬂow is required, which was deﬁned in the Brisbane
Decleration in 2007 as
29
==PAGE:42==
Master thesis
Mathias Hermann
"The quantity, timing, and quality of water ﬂows required to sustain freshwater and estuarine
ecosystems and the human livelihoods and well-being that depend on these ecosystems." -
(Arthington AH and S, 2018)
The economics of maintaining the ecosystems is hard to quantify, and so instead monthly environmental
ﬂow requirements (EFR) is estimated and included in the model as a constraint (Payet-Burin et al., 2019).
Including the EFR as a constraint rather than assigning it an endogenous economic variable, circumvents
the issue of assigning an economic value, but is based on the assumption that ecosystem management
is prioritised. The EFR does not take into account ﬂow deviation and river segmentation, which can
hinder ﬁsh migration, but as the river ﬂow is the main driver in determining ecosystem health, setting
an EFR is assumed to be an acceptable approach (POFF et al., 2010).
The EFR was estimated using the Variable Monthly Flow (VMF) method (Pastor et al., 2013). The method
was chosen as it was one of two methods found among 5 methods to have the highest correlation with
locally calculated EFR based on 11 case studies. The VFM method sets a minimum ﬂow requirement for
both low ﬂow, intermediate ﬂow and high ﬂow months, as shown in Table 4.

3.6.1
Agriculture
In the agriculture module interactions with the nexus only occur in areas equipped for irrigation and
in times where the crop water demand is not fully satisﬁed by precipitation alone. If irrigation systems
are installed and water demand is not met by precipitation the model allocates water in order to meet
the demand and increase yield as long as there is water available to use and the beneﬁt of the increase
yield is higher than the costs of irrigating. The value of a crop can either be given as a constant problem
parameter or included as a modelled crop market, where changes in supply and demand can alter the
value of the crop.
3.6.2
Energy
Hydropower plants need water in order to produce power. The hydropower plants have a maximum
capacity they can produce as well as a production factor denoting their ability to convert water into

Mathias Hermann
energy. These two factors determines the amount of water needed for the hydropower plant to run at
full capacity. The model takes the water need together with the production cost and the production
value and maximises the economic beneﬁt. This optimisation is constrained by the amount of available
water. If there is not enough water to satisfy the demand of all users, the model determines if water
should be allocated to the production of hydro power or if the net beneﬁts of allocation would be higher
somewhere else. The production value can either be given as a constant problem parameter, assuming
a constant energy price and that production is always lower or equal to demand, or it can be based on
a modelled power market where price and demand is ﬂuctuating.
The interaction of alternative power production sources with the nexus happens only through water
demand of thermal power production if the power marked is not included. If the power marked is in-
cluded then power production from alternative sources also becomes relevant as it aﬀect the economics
of hydropower production.

Scenarios
This section will brieﬂy introduce identiﬁed scenarios which has been deemed relevant to include in
the model based on the knowledge gained in Section 2.
3.7.1
Hydro
As the amount of hydropower in the catchment is expected to increase in the future, as established in
Section 2.5, a scenario was created where all the catchments already containing hydropower saw a 50%
increase in capacity. This was done in order to evaluate how an increase in capacity would aﬀect the
capacity factor of the plants. As studies were found later in the research for this paper indicating much
larger hydropower capacity expansion, this scenario could be expanded in a future iteration to include
a scenario of the planned hydropower expansion, to evaluate the feasibility of these projects, and a
scenario of large capacity in all catchments, in order to determine where it would be most suitable to
invest in increasing capacity, based on capacity factor.
3.7.2
Irrigation
As irrigation practices in the Amazon basin are very limited but is expected to increase, as mentioned in
Section 2.4.1, a scenario where 50% of all rainfed area is converted to irrigated was modelled. Increasing
yields through irrigation could allow for a maintained crop production using a smaller area, which
would allow for regrowth of forest, given that forest protection is a political priority. It could, however,
also give an incentive for farmers to expand, if they can see a larger potential proﬁt in doing so, as
discussed in Section 2.4.1. Irrigation is often followed by a general intensiﬁcation of the area, which

maximum yield as the rainfed, in order to only see the eﬀects of crop water demand satisfaction.
3.7.3
Climate Change
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) is predicting with high conﬁdence that water
shortage will occur in semi arid regions of South America, as a consequence of future decrease in pre-
cipitation and increase in ET IPCC (2014b). A low conﬁdence prediction of precipitation changes in the
north-eastern Brazil is estimating a 22% decrease by year 2100. The Brazilian Panel on Climate Change
is prediction an even more dire future where precipitation in the Cerrado, a Savannah area bordering the
south and south-eastern part of the Amazon basin, decreases with 50-70% in its north-eastern regions
PBMC (2014). The northern and north-eastern part of the Amazon basin is home to both large amounts
of agriculture and hydropower and thus modelling this predicted decrease in precipitation might show
a large impact on the nexus. The Climate Change (CC) is modelled as three scenarios CC20, CC30 and
CC40 with reduction in runoﬀand precipitation of 20%, 30% and 40% respectively to see the increased
impact of decreasing precipitation.

Discussion
The discussion will start by assessing the implications of the model results on the development of hy-
dropower and irrigation in the basin, as well as the implications of hydropower and agriculture on the
SAP identiﬁed issues. This will be followed by a comparison of the base model results to literature,

5.1
Implications
5.1.1
Hydropower development
An article in Nature includes a map of planned hydropower capacity expansion in the basin (Latrubesse
et al., 2017). Comparing the planned expansion in each of the catchments with the results from the
model, can be used to evaluate the feasibility of the expansion. A table of planned capacity expansion
for the individual catchments can be found in Appendix A.14. It was found that the planned expansion
of the catchments with the highest planned expansions, and which could be assessed, would likely be
infeasible as the capacity would far exceed what could be produced. The assessment of the individual
catchments follow. Catchment 1 in Brazil is planned to see a capacity expansion of 14,609 MW to its
current 579 MW, 94% of this expansion is going to be on the Tapajos river and its tributaries. Catchment
1 has the largest water availability as it contains the mouth of the Amazon river, however, the Tapajos
only receive upstream runoﬀfrom catchment 9. Catchment 9 already see large over capacity at its cur-
rent 2361 MW, and thus the planned capacity expansion in catchment 1 will, according to the model,
likely not be able to achieve the potential production. Catchment 9 itself has a planned expansion of

==PAGE:63==

Mathias Hermann
10432 MW on top of its current 2361 MW, which will likely have the same issue.
Catchment 14 has the largest absolute and relative capacity expansion adding 16,864 MW to its current
233 MW. The model shows no negative impact even in the Combined40 scenario, with 50% increase
capacity and 40% less runoﬀ, however, the expansion is so large that the modelled scenarios are not

5.1.2
Irrigation development
The results on agriculture, water balance and user curtailment, indicates that many areas in the basin
could beneﬁt from irrigation development, and that there is suﬃcient water available to do so without
curtailing water from other users or compromising the environmental ﬂow. However, the model does
not currently give the production of each crop on a catchment resolution, which means that a more
targeted analysis on the spatial issues and potential irrigation beneﬁts in the basin can not be made.
Bolivia would see the largest beneﬁt from irrigation development, and could according to the model
reduce their cultivated area and maintain a larger production than today by equipping some areas for
irrigation.
5.1.3
The Strategic Action Program
Both Hydropower and Agriculture was found to be linked to all three of the top priority issues in the
basin identiﬁed by the Strategic Action Program (SAP), namely water pollution, deforestation and bio-
diversity loss, either directly or indirectly. The implications of the WHAT-IF model results on each of
these issues will be discussed separately.
Water pollution
Both hydropower and agriculture have an indirect impact on water pollution, through their promotion
of deforestation. Deforestation increases the surface runoﬀwhich entails a larger transport of sediment
into the river, leading to pollution with suspended solids. This should be taken into account when as-
sessing the economic feasibility of new projects in these areas or when determining whether to convert
agricultural area back to forest. The agricultural industry also contribute directly to water pollution
through the use of pesticides and fertilisers. These two are, however, are seen as small and local issues
51
==PAGE:64==
Master thesis
Mathias Hermann
compared to the chemical contamination from the extracting industry. Although focus should likely be
on the extracting industry in order to minimise chemical contamination of the rivers, the use of pesti-
cides and fertilisers, should still be regulated and the regulations enforced.
The main water pollution issues associated with hydropower and agriculture is thus deemed to be their
eﬀect on deforestation.
Deforestation
The deforestation from hydropower and agriculture takes place following diﬀerence causal chains. The
construction of hydropower plants leaves infrastructure which facilitates other activities in the area,
which will likely entail deforestation. Agriculture is seen as the main driver of deforestation in the
basin. The largest part of deforested area is used for livestock, but crop production is seen as constitut-
ing the frontier of deforestation. Forest is cut down or burned and the land is used to plant crops. When
the soil is depleted for essential nutrients, the ﬁeld is abandoned and new forest is removed. It is thus
essential to regulate this sector in order to establish sustainable practices, that can mitigate the need
to abandon depleted ﬁelds. Ideally expansion of cultivated area should cease, and some grazing areas
and the lowest yielding ﬁelds should be regrown into forest. The results from the irrigation scenario
indicates that especially Bolivia might be able to decrease the cultivated area and still keep or even
increase their production of crops by investing in irrigation development.
Protected areas and indigenous territories have been found to be eﬃcient barriers to deforestation, espe-
cially in Brazil. Thus governance on these areas should be strengthened across the basin. Hydropower
and agriculture might also in this sense lead to increased deforestation, by creating conﬂicts of interests
in the protected areas and territories which might lead to weakened regulations and governance.
Biodiversity loss
Both activities are connected to biodiversity loss through their impact on deforestation and on water
pollution. Hydropower is further related to the loss of aquatic biodiversity by altering ﬂow patterns,
blocking migration and directly killing ﬁsh upon water release. It is thus important when planning
hydropower projects to also analyse the ecological sensitivity of the river system, taking into account
niche species and migratory ﬁsh. Two studies were found which have tried to analyse the ecological
vulnerability to power plant development in the Amazon basin. One study looking at the environmental
impacts of speciﬁc planned plants in Bolivia, Colombia, Ecuador and Peru, found that 71 would entail a
high ecological impact, 51 a modest ecological impact and 29 a low ecological impact (Finer and Jenkins,
2012). Another study deﬁned the vulnerability of diﬀerent catchments by assigning them a score on
the Dam Environmental Vulnerability Index (DEVI), based on current and planned hydropower plants

5.2
Literature comparison
In order to further evaluate the precision and robustness of the results, the hydropower production,
crop production and water balance of the Current scenario was compared with literature sources.
5.2.1
Hydropower production
The production in each catchment was compared with the estimates made by the GPPD and with a
calculated production based on an estimated average capacity factor from an article in Nature (World
Resources Institute, 2018; Almeida et al., 2019). The table can be seen in Appendix A.15. The results
shows that, as expected, the model predicts a much larger production in catchment 21, Belo Monte,
compared to the estimates by GPPD. Other than that the model estimates are in the same range as the
GPPD estimates and the calculated estimates. If catchment 21 is excluded, the total modelled production
within the catchment is 8% higher than estimated by GPPD and 9% lower than estimated based on the
average capacity factor. The largest diﬀerences between the GPPD estimates and the model estimates
are found in catchments 1, 15, 16 and 17. The model estimates a higher production in catchment 1 and
17. These two catchments has a 100% capacity factor in the model in the Current scenario. Hydropower
in catchment 1 is currently modelled as placed at the month of the Amazon river and thus it is expected
that the production here will be estimated too high. Hydropower in catchment 17 is also modelled with
a water availability that is too high, but the plants are at the same time located in the Andean region
where the head is likely higher than modelled. Hydropower in catchment 15 and 16 have relatively low
capacity factor in the model, and is also mainly located in the Andean region. That is likely why the
model estimates a lower production than the GPPD estimate.
Belo Monte Assessment
The Xingu river basin, where the Belo Monte is situated, spans part of the Mouth and South-east of the
Amazon basin. These two areas are characterised by high seasonality, with the dry season seeing much
less rain than the wet season. The Belo Monte is connected to a large reservoir, which can store water
during the rainy season and release during the dry. The WHAT-IF model estimates a water requirement
of 1.730 billion m3/year to run at maximum capacity, another study found that the plant would require
a discharge of 31.000 m3/s to run at 90% of maximum installed capacity, equal to 978 billion m3/year
(Stickler et al., 2013). This could indicate that the production factor used for the WHAT-IF model is too
low. However, there is only 372 billion m3/year of water available in the Xingu basin according to the

plant still have a large over capacity both with the WHAT-IF estimate and the literature estimate.
5.2.2
Crop production
The WHAT-IF results on crop production under the Current scenario was compared with estimates from
SPAM (IFPRI, 2019). As the crop composition, cultivated area and yield used in the WHAT-IF model is
extracted from SPAM data, the diﬀerence between the estimates relates to the problem parameters Water
response coeﬃcient, PhaseMonth and Crop coeﬃcient. Tables on the comparison on individual crops in
each country and total production can be found in Appendix A.16. Looking at the total crop production
per year the WHAT-IF model estimates lower total production in Bolivia and Peru, higher production in
Colombia and similar productions in Brazil, Ecuador and Venezuela. Looking at the production of each
crop reveals that there are large diﬀerence between the model estimates in Brazil, but since the WHAT-IF
both estimates far below an far above the SPAM estimates, it evens out in the total production. Generally
the WHAT-IF makes markedly higher estimates on Soybean, Maize and Cassava and markedly lower
estimates on Rice, Sugarcane and Potatoes. However, the estimate generally diﬀers between higher and
lower estimates depending on country.
5.2.3
Water balance
The results on water withdrawal for the entire basin and for Brazil and Peru were compared with
estimates from FAO (FAO, 2015a). The comparison showed that the distribution of water withdrawal
between the users in the basin diﬀers a lot between the model results and the literature values. Under
the Current scenario the model estimates that the water withdrawal in the basin is 3% from irrigation
in agriculture and 97% from Domestic (40%), Thermal (33%) and Industry (27%). The literature source
estimated that in 2006 53% of water withdrawal in the basin was from Agriculture and livestock. In the
model livestock is under Industry, but as Industry only constitutes app. 27% of the total withdrawal,
the two are still far apart.
The model estimates that Brazil withdraws 5,291 million m3 a year within the basin, compared to an
estimated 2,108 million m3 by FAO. The water withdrawal in Brazil is according to the WHAT-IF model
almost exclusively from the Domestic, Thermal and Industry users. The model estimates that 1599
million m3 is withdrawn yearly in the Peruvian part of the basin, 10% for irrigation and 90% for the rest
of the users, compared to an estimated 2,360 million m3 by FAO.
This comparison indicates that there might be issues related to the estimates of water withdrawal within
the basin, and that there might also be issues related to the estimates of irrigated area. As the amount
of water consumed in the basin is so small relative to what is available, even in the driest months and

5.3
Identiﬁed model setup issues
5.3.1
Hydropower
General
Most of the hydropower plants in the basin are not placed on the main rivers or at the bottom of their
respective catchments. As they are currently modelled as so, the model can allocate more water to them
than what would actually be available. Furthermore, the hydropower plants located in the Andean re-
gion is likely to have a steeper gradient, or larger head, than in other parts of the basin. Currently all
plants are assumed to have a hydraulic head of 20 meters, however, literature sources descripes heads
of 600 meters in the Ecuadorian Andes (Posso et al., 2015; Verzani et al., 2012). As the larger head would
allow for a higher power production per unit of water, this could have an inﬂuence on the results from
this region. To address this the catchment resolution could be increased for the hydropower plants, so
that each plant would be at the bottom of a smaller catchment, and speciﬁc data on the average head of
each power plant could be obtained.
San Antonio & Jirau
The two major reservoirs in catchment 10, San Antonio and Jirau, are not correctly modelled, as they
are modelled as receiving runoﬀfrom catchment 10, 17, 19 and 20, but should only be receiving from
catchment 17, 19 and 20. Catchment 10 accounts for 36% of the available runoﬀto the two plants, and
thus the CC40 scenario, with 40% reduction in available runoﬀ, can be seen as the most representative
for the current situation. As these two plants account for 90% of the capacity in catchment 10, the ca-
pacity factor for catchment 10 under current conditions is likely closer to 30% than to 49%, see Tabel 5.
These two reservoirs comprise 17% of power produced in the basin, and this issue will thus distort the
results on the economic balance.
Belo Monte
Belo Monte in catchment 21 has a discharge of 2.784.299 Mm3/year, but only 371,823 Mm3/year avail-
able. This indicates that there is an error occurring in the model. As the power production from Belo
Monte constitutes 53% of total power production in the current scenario, this will greatly distort the
results on the economic balance. The estimated available runoﬀwas compared to another study, and
found to be in the same range (Stickler et al., 2013). Based on this it was concluded that the available
runoﬀof 371,823 Mm3/year is likely a good estimate.
Balbina

catchment in the basin as it contains the mouth of the Amazon river. However, Balbina is located on a

5.3.2
Agriculture
The large beneﬁts from irrigation development is likely linked to the high seasonal variation in precipi-
tation in most parts of the basin. However, Ecuador sees a high amount of precipitation throughout the
year, but it still beneﬁts from irrigation and is vulnerable to precipitation reduction. In order to ﬁnd out
if this is due to an issue in the model setup a worst case scenario was constructed. The maximum Kc
value for each of the crops in Ecuador was multiplied with the maximum ET0 over the 20 year period
and compared to the month with the lowest average precipitation. The maximum water demand for the
crops in Ecuador was found to be 138 mm/month and the month with the lowest average precipitation
saw 147 mm/month. This indicates that there is an error somewhere in the model or in the model setup.
5.3.3
Environmental constraints & Curtailment
As the environmental constraints in the low ﬂow months are set to 60% of naturally available runoﬀ, the
model should not allow for any consumption of water in the CC40 and Combined40 scenarios, where
runoﬀis reduced by 40%. This would results in a 100% curtailment of water to the consumptive users
during the dry months, however, no curtailment is seen from the results of the model.
5.3.4
Economics
Energy module
The exclusion of power markets entails that ﬂuctuations in demand are not modelled, which means
that the model reﬂects an ideal marked where power can always be used and should thus always be
produced when possible. This is likely not representative of the actual situation. This does not have
an inﬂuence on the analysis on over capacity in terms of water availability, but should be corrected in
order to get a more accurate picture of the economic beneﬁts.
Agriculture module
The economic value of crops have been approximated based on FAO data which is deemed to be a
good estimate of actual value, although speciﬁc data provided by the respective countries would be
preferable. However, the cost of production is hard to estimate as it is based on many variables, requires
interdisciplinary knowledge and requires data that is not publicly available. The assumptions made in
order to model the cultivation cost for this modelled is deemed a signiﬁcant issue, as it is likely too

5.4
Data uncertainty evaluation
An uncertainty evaluation of the data inputs was conducted based on the speciﬁcity tables of the three
modules Agriculture, Water and Energy, presented in Appendix A.7. Overall many data inputs are found
to have low to very low speciﬁcity, which undermines the robustness of the model results. Improve-
ments to data speciﬁcity could be achieved through collaboration with the governments involved.
Agriculture - Average speciﬁcity: Low
The data used for the Agriculture module is associated with a low degree of speciﬁcity. The data on
the crop composition, cultivated area and yield is based on model estimates of the area, and deemed
to have medium speciﬁcity. The type of crops and their area is the base of the Agriculture module to
which all other inputs relate, thus the speciﬁcity of this data should ideally be improved. It is deemed
that the uncertainty in this module would have to be improved on all data inputs in order to increase
the robustness of the results.
Water - Average speciﬁcity: Medium
The data in the water module is associated with medium speciﬁcity. Some of the inputs such as Runoﬀ,
ET0 and Precipitation are based on model estimates but more precise estimates are diﬃcult to achieve,
and would under all circumstance be based on models rather than actual values. Improvements in speci-
ﬁcity could be achieved by acquiring better data on the reservoirs and the water users and their demand.
This should be possible to acquire from the respective governments. The Environmental ﬂow estimates
are based on best practice, but is still not necessarily representative of the actual environmental ﬂow
requirements. Requirements closer representing the actual situation would require in depth research on
the river systems and all impacts from changes in water ﬂow and volume, which is not deemed feasible.
Energy - Average speciﬁcity: Medium
The data in the energy model is associated with a medium level of speciﬁcity. The power plants found
and their production capacity is deemed to have a high speciﬁcity, but there might be more plants than
what the database contains, as the latest plants are form 2017. The eﬃciency of the plants are based on
literature and estimated to be of a medium speciﬁcity. The economic parameters are generally viewed
as low as they are based on assumptions and literature which is not fully representative. The Production

Conclusion & Recommendations
The aim of this paper was to provide scientiﬁc knowledge on the sustainable water usage in the Amazon
basin to help address the issues identiﬁed in the SAP. The results from applying the WHAT-IF model
showed that some of the catchments in the basin, might currently be experiencing over capacity, show-
ing as reduced capacity factors. For these catchments the over capacity increases under the climate
scenarios, whereas for the ones not currently seeing over capacity the results showed a resilience under
the climate scenario even when capacity was expanded. The catchments experiencing over capacity
also have large inter-annual and annual variability.
The model results on agriculture production showed that some countries had a markedly lower total
production of crops than what could be achieved with full water demand satisfaction. This was gener-
ally improved when converting 50% of rainfed area to irrigated, however, the degree of improvement
varied a lot between catchments and between diﬀerent crops. Here there was also notable annual vari-
ation in total production, which was mitigated to some extend by irrigation development.
The results on water balance and user curtailment showed that the water consumed and lost by the
users in the basin, was insigniﬁcant relative to the water available in the basin, and that little to no
curtailment was seen in any scenario.
The results imply that hydropower development might not be appropriate in some catchments, and
comparison with planned hydropower development plans showed that a lot of the planned expansion
was expected to take place in model identiﬁed unsuitable areas. The results also indicate that there could
be large beneﬁts from developing irrigation in targeted areas, and that there is suﬃcient water available
in the basin to due so. The increased eﬃciency could allow for a potential descaling of agricultural area.
As both hydropower and agriculture was found to negatively impact all of the top three priority issues
in the basin, as identiﬁed by the Strategic Action Program (SAP), expansion of both activities should be
limited and ideally scaled back.
Comparing the model estimates on three indicators with literature showed that the model estimates
diﬀered substantially. This could be due to the issues identiﬁed in the model setup and the uncertainty
relating to the lack of speciﬁcity in the data. These issues in the model setup and the uncertainty, would
have to be addressed in order to strengthen the results of the model.
Recommendations based on the results
• Hydropower developers should conduct initial assessments of the rivers in order to determine if
the projects can run with the expected capacity factor and to estimate to what degree the river
system is vulnerable to the development

• Bolivia, Peru and Brazil should invest in converting targeted areas from rainfed to irrigated, as
the model suggest that this could give a sizeable increase in production
• The increased production from irrigation should be seen as an alternative to expanding the cul-
tivated area, in order to decrease deforestation, and ideally some areas could be regrown with
forest.
Recommendations for future work
• The speciﬁcity of the data input should be improved. This could be done in collaboration with
the stakeholders in the basin.
• An individual map should be made for hydropower with a higher catchment resolution. This
would allow for a more precise model, where each hydropower plant has its own catchment only
containing the water actually available to the plant.
• Two additional scenarios could be made on a) planned hydropower development in the basin, and
b) high capacity in all basins, in order to evaluate the feasibility of planned projects and to locate
the basins most suitable for development.
• The model could be adjusted to give the production of individual crops in each catchment, in
order to better target irrigation development.
• Trouble shooting should be undergone in order to locate where and what in the model causes the
issues identiﬁed with the modelling of Belo Monte and crop production in Ecuador.
• The power market and crop market should be included so that external variations in the market
is taken into account in the model.

A.3
Hydro power database discussion
The Amazon Geo-Referenced Socio-Environmental Information Network (RAISG) provides data on current, under construc-
tion and projected hydro power plants in the basin (RAISG, 2017). However, comparison between the data from RAISG and
from GPPD on current hydro power plants showed a discrepancy. The GPPD comes from a trustworthy source and the data
is transparent as each data point includes a reference to where the information is from. The RAISG data is not transparent
and it was not possible to conﬁrm the location of hydro power plants by comparing with google maps images. Furthermore,
an error was found on all data on current hydro power plants in Brazil, where capacities were a factor 1000 too high. Based
on this it has been decided not to use the RAISG data in this study. It should at the same time be mentioned that while
searching for information on plants, another published paper was found which uses the RAISG data (Lees et al., 2016). As
the data is found to be erroneous this paper might have to be revised.

Assumptions & Estimates
This section describes all the assumptions that have been made in relation to data gathering and explains how diﬀerent
inputs have been calculated or estimated.
A.8.1
Agriculture
Farming zones
- Land capacity: Is assumed the same as the Maximum cultivated area.
- Irrigation costs: Based on irrigation cost/ha, taken from (FAO, 1997a). This needs to be divided by the water need in m3/ha
to arrive at cost/m3. A water need of 0.5 l/s/ha, corresponding to a Humid Tropical Climate, is chosen and it is assumed that
the ﬁelds needs watering 2 hours a day on average (FAO, 1992).
- Water loss rate from allocated: Is based on the water withdrawal and water consumption for irrigation in Brazil following
Figure 4.2 in (OECD, 2017a). It is assumed that the diﬀerence between water withdrawn and water consumed is water lost,
and the water loss rate is thus calculated as the fraction of water withdrawn that is lost.
MaxCultureArea
The Maximum cultivated area is assumed to be the area currently cultivated and is thus taken as the total physical area of
the chosen crops for the study (IFPRI, 2019).
Crops
The crops are found using SPAM data (IFPRI, 2019). Crops are chosen that covers 92% of all irrigated harvested area and
97% of all rainfed harvested area.
Cultures
It is assumed that only Maize and Wheat have more than one season in a year, and thus they are modelled as summer and
winter Maize and spring and winter Wheat.

Water
Reservoirs
- Reservoirs: The reservoirs are found by searching for keywords online, such as ’Country Name + reservoirs’, ’Amazon
reservoirs’ and ’South America reservoirs’. Other reservoirs might thus exist, that have not been found, but it is expected
that the largest reservoirs are included. Some reservoirs around La Paz where identiﬁed and one in northern Peru, but they
were all small and on the outskirts of the basin, so with the current model setup it would have been misleading to include
them.
- Storage capacity: The storage capacity of Jirau, Santo Antonio and Limon was found from literature (Fearnside, 2014a; Iha,
2020; Gobierno Lambayeque, 2009). The storage capacity of the reservoirs connected to Belo Monte and Balbina could not
be found, and this had to be estimated based on an assumed average depth of the reservoir of 15 meters and the reservoir
area.
- Initial and ﬁnal storage: The initial and ﬁnal storage was both set to be equal to half the storage capacity. In this way the
water in the reservoirs can be used during dry periods, but the overall water balance would have to be zero. The reservoirs
might contain more or less water initially, which would aﬀect the extend to which it can initially balance the wet and dry
season, but this is not deemed to have a large inﬂuence on the results, as the water balance = 0 constraint means that any
quantity of water used at time x would have to be stored again at time y.
- Reservoir area: The reservoir area could be found for Belo Monte and Balbina (Fearnside, 1989, 2006). The reservoir area
of Jirau, Santo Antonio and Limon had to be estimated based on the storage capacity and an assumed average depth of the
reservoir of 15 meters.
- Volume to area coeﬃcient: The volume to area coeﬃcient should be calculated as reservoir area/ storage capacity, but as
both of these parameters could not be found for any of the reservoirs, the volume to area coeﬃcient had to be estimated
based on an assumed average depth of 15 meters, yielding a coeﬃcient of 1/15=0.07.
WaterUsers
- Users: Users are assumed to constitute Domestic, Industry and Thermal power plants.
- Return rate: The return rate was estimated based on numbers from Brazil in 2015, as the diﬀerence between the water
withdrawn minus losses and the water consumed, divided by the water withdrawn minus losses (OECD, 2017a).
- Loss rate: The loss rate is based on numbers from Brazil in 2015 (IB-NET, 2009). - Willingness to pay: Domestic willingness
to pay (WTP) per m3 was estimated based on the WTP per month in Manaus, $5.61 USD, and the average water consumption
per month in Brazil, 3.25 m3 (IBGE, 2017; Casey et al., 2006). WTP for Industry was estimated based on numbers from the
USA, where plants were estimated to stop producing power at a water price above $1.7 USD/m3 (Global Water Forum, 2019).
Industry was assumed to be higher than the others, as they would likely switch to an alternative water source in situations
of water shortage.
- Water supply cost: Is based on numbers from Brazil in 2015 (IB-NET, 2009).

following a linear regression increase (Huang et al., 2018). Thermal was calculated based on the capacity and literature esti-
