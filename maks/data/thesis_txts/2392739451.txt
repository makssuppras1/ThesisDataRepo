Preface
This report was prepared by ing. m.ida Morten Beier at the department of Electrical Engineering
at the Technical University of Denmark in partial fulﬁlment of the master’s thesis ’Vision based
localisation and feature detection in underwater environments’.
The report focuses on learnings taken by the student during the exam-project with the aim to
create a system which by the use of a video stream can estimate the localisation of the camera in
an underwater environment. The outcome is intended as a basis for creating a start-up.
The project was conducted in the period from July 5, 2017 - October 5, 2017 and is equivalent to
15 ECTS points.

Abstract
The work described in this report takes it’s starting point in inspection of man made underwater
structures. The end goal is a database where footage from a speciﬁc location on a given structure
can be retrieved. If a structure has been inspected more than once, it will be possible to see a
video, that shows eventual change over time.
The database needs information about location and orientation of the camera/object to keep track
of the footage. For that purpose the work in this report has it’s focus on ﬁnding out if visual
odometry could be a less costly alternative to commonly used technologies like Inertial Navigation
Systems (INSs), Doppler loggers, 3D sonars and Baseline Loggers .
Feature detectors like Harris Corner, FAST, SURF and BRISK are tested on underwater footage
from real outdoor environment, and conﬁned waters.
The Semi-direct Visual Odometry algorithm is tested with the same underwater footage as the
feature detectors.
Semi-direct Visual Odometry (SVO) is originally intended for Micro Aerial
Vehicles (MAVs).
The image enhancing algorithm Visual Saliency is made speciﬁcally for underwater footage and is
applied to the test data and tested with the feature detectors and the SVO algorithm.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Automation of processes and tasks that previously have been carried out by humans is taking place
in many ﬁelds. That is also the case in underwater environments where robots can accomplish
repair and inspection-work, where divers have previously been the only means.
Localisation in underwater environments is more complicated than on-land. There are several
reasons for that; lack of GPS-signal and reduced visibility to name a few. Most of the existing
means to overcome these limitations with adequate precision like Inertial Measurement Units
(IMUs) easily cost more than 100 thousand euros. In this thesis it is therefore investigated if a
computer vision based system is able to provide the necessary information about where each frame
of video is recorded on a known structure.
1.1
Motivation and goal
Oil-rigs are by regulation required to undergo visual underwater inspection at ﬁxed intervals,
depending on the state and age of the rig. Automated underwater inspection is becoming common
for pipelines. Oil-rigs are still mainly inspected manually1. This is also the case for ships and
harbours.
Windfarms, harbours and many other structures are at present not required to undergo visual
inspection. With increasing age of structures and greater easy of inspection due to automation,
that day could soon come. The owners of the structures will also beneﬁt from knowing the physical
state of their investment.
Inspections of oﬀ-shore structures can take weeks and generate vast amount of video which is
reviewed both real-time and after the conclusion of the inspection. Finding the right frames, when
the recording is completed, can be very time consuming.
In the pre-study for this exam-project [1] it is suggested to make a video database, where all video
frames are tagged with a set of coordinates describing pose and orientation of the object and the
time of recording.
By providing footage from an inspection of a structure it will be possible to search the database for
video of a speciﬁc part of the known structure taken at a given point in time, with very limited use

1.2
Problem Statement
Human controlled ROV inspection of man-made underwater structures generates many hours of

3.1
Necessary data
Inspection of a known underwater structure beneﬁts from known position and shape of all elements
of the structure. It will be possible to calculate based on drawings and a known position. In order
to create a database which can be used for inspection it is necessary to have information about
where in the structure the footage is taken, to make it possible to match the footage with the
drawing of the structure.
A camera moving in open water has six Degrees Of Freedom (DOF). It can move freely in the three
dimensional space, and it can rotate freely around the three axis unless the supporting underwater
vehicle sets restrictions. It is therefore not enough to know the location of the camera when each
frame is taken - it is also necessary to know the orientation. All coordinates must be output as
world coordinates.
For basic use of the database time is the last essential piece of information which must be stored
with each frame of the video. This information is essential in order to make retrospective investi-
gations - for example when comparing present inspection with last years inspection.
Additional information can be added, for example water quality or measurements on the structure
based on Eddy currents or ultrasound [1].

considered in this thesis.

3.3
Constraints in underwater environments
The underwater environment sets a number of constraints on the implementation suggested in
this project. Following constraints will be explained brieﬂy below, and some are illustrated in
Figure 3.1.
• no GPS signal
• low visibility
• particles ﬂoating in the water
• poor light conditions
• marine growth
The electromagnetic Global Positioning System (GPS) signal is signiﬁcantly damped under water,
making it useless as soon as the receiving antenna is submerged, therefore other means are necessary
to determine the location.
The visibility underwater varies in the range of 0-100 or even 200 meters, with 20 meters in general
considered very good. For Denmark ﬁve meters is quite common. The visibility is aﬀected by the
presence of zoo- and phytoplankton, sediments and in special cases also layers of water separated
due to diﬀerent densities caused by diﬀerence in temperature or salinity. The zone where the layers
are separated is called the thermocline (caused by diﬀerence in temperature) or halocline (caused
by diﬀerence in salinity) and is characterized by blurry water or a very distinct reﬂective layer. In
general visibility is in most cases a limiting factor.
Other factors are scattering which reduces contrast and chromatic ﬁltering through the water
removing parts of the light spectrum.
Particles ﬂoating in the water, i.e. parts of plants, algae, makes it more diﬃcult to compare video
frames, since the ﬂoating object is detected in spite of not being part of the inspected object and
moves diﬀerently in the frame. This can aﬀect feature detection and matching because the ﬂoating
objects might be detected and matched, but give misleading information about the movement of
the camera compared to the structure. See more about feature matching in Chapter 7.
The light conditions underwater depend on visibility, depth and of course the presence of sun above
water. As illustrated in Figure 3.1 it is necessary to employ lighting systems to bring out details
in the video image in order to improve feature detection in the image.
Marine growth has two major eﬀects on equipment in the underwater environment: The observed
objects can rapidly change appearance, since the marine growth can change the surface structure
- for example mussels covering metal or concrete legs - or it can change the complete shape of the

3.
Analysis
The time stamping of the frames is used for logging the time of inspection in the database, and
the precision provided by the clock of a computer is fully adequate for that.
However, when
taking information from diﬀerent pieces of hardware, like mentioned under subsection 3.4.2, and
the timing between them is important, the requirements are much more strict.
One way to get around the timing issue is the use of Robot Operation System (ROS), which has
functions to keep track of the timing. This is done by having one ROS node running as ROS
master. The master provides all information about timing.
An on board gyroscope of ROVs and AUVs gives information about angular velocities around
the three axis. By integration pitch, roll and yaw can be determined which gives all necessary
information about orientation. In some cases the pitch however is based on a compass and is not
reliable close to constructions with metal. It is important to be aware of since most man-made
underwater structures contain metals.
If the underwater vehicle carries an IMU on board, it will, besides the gyroscope, also carry a
triad of accelerometers which provides the acceleration along the three axis. Integration will give
the speed and double integration will give the travelled distance along the three axis, thus giving
information about the relative location.
3.4.1
Alternative technologies
There are several other technologies to determine the location underwater [1]: Doppler Velocity
Log (DVL), Light Detection and Ranging (LIDAR), multi beam sonars, structured light and Long
Base Line (LBL) - the latter works by triangulation of acoustic signals from known sources mounted
on the seabed.
The above mentioned solutions are expensive with prices measured in at least tens or hundreds
of thousands of euros for high precision devices. From a hardware point of view, camera based
Simultanous Location and Mapping (SLAM) or VO are cheaper solutions.
The SLAM or VO algorithms produces a 3D point cloud, and since the inspection is taking place
on a known structure, the calculated pitch and location can be conﬁrmed by matching the acquired
3D map with the the 3D map of the known structure.
An inspection ROV/AUV can have more than one camera depending on the size and purpose of
the ROV. For vehicles with several cameras it can be considered to use one of them for SLAM or
VO which can provide the missing information about location and pitch. Both methods work with
visual inputs. The input can come from a sonar, a LIDAR or a camera. Another ﬂexible solution
could be to make an add-on piece of hardware providing to the necessary equipment and software
in order to add VO on an existing ROV solution.
Forster, Pizzoli, and Scaramuzza [3] suggests an approach to VO called SVO which is fast enough
to be a part of the on board navigation of an autonomous vehicle. It has been tested on drones
in outdoor environment, ﬂying over grass. The capability to detect very ﬁne features in real time
makes it interesting for the purpose of this thesis. See a more detailed description of the algorithms
in the chapter Visual odometry speciﬁcally under section 8.2 and section 8.4.

3.4.2
Improvement
Due to the nature of monocular VO the output is without scale. The scale can be determined by
using a two-camera VO or by adding information about the distance to the points in the 3D cloud.
Therefore the distance to nearby objects, provided by the sonar, is highly relevant, since it can be
used to set the scale of the output from the VO algorithm.
To increase the precision of a VO-system the information from an IMU or other navigational
information from the ROV can be combined in a Kalman Filter (KF). The KF works by multi-
plying Gaussian distributions of multiple parameters assuming the true value is within each of the
distributions. The result is a smaller and more accurate guess on the reality - i.e. the location.
3.5
Recording of video
Recording of footage of real life structures and storing it in a database system in order to investigate
and document the current state puts strong requirements on the quality of the video. To miss
failures in the construction can lead to great environmental costs, loss of life and ﬁnancial means.
Following is considered in this section:
• lighting
• manual recording or use of ROV/AUV
• choice of camera
• in-action video recording
• image enhancement
Lighting is necessary for most underwater recordings, as mentioned brieﬂy in section 3.3 and
Figure 3.1. The necessary power depends on the sensitivity of the camera and the power of the
surrounding light which must be matched. This means that higher power is needed in shallow
water, where the sunlight is dominant.
Distance is another factor and since light is scattered
and attenuated quickly in water, the need for power increases quickly with distance. However,
it requires very clear water to obtain useful footage at a distance since images are blurred thus
making feature detection diﬃcult or impossible.
Manual recording of video with a diver operated camera or with a camera mounted on an ROV or
AUV each have their pros and cons.
The diver operated camera lacks alternative data inputs like Inertial Navigation System (INS)
data, and all processing must take place after download of the data to a computer. Divers in the
water is a very expensive approach2 which also puts people at risk.
Using an inspection ROV or AUV beneﬁts from on board camera, lighting system and possibly
also INS. Some have additional sensors to provide information to help navigation. Most units
have several cameras and depending on the optics, the applied algorithm and the location of the
cameras, they might be usable for VO.

in this non-commercial project, no big structures are involved and the author has full access to
diving equipment and underwater camera, the manual recording by diver is by far the easiest and
cheapest approach.
In general the best choice of camera for VO is a global shutter camera. Global shutter cameras
work by exposing all pixels at the same time during exposure and then reading out the pixel values.
This is diﬀerent from a more standard approach, where each pixel is exposed and read one after
the other.
Therefore global shutter cameras prevent fast camera movements from causing objects in diﬀerent
location within the frame to shift slightly depending on their location in the frame. However, the
angular movement of the objects within the frame of the underwater robot camera is slow. Thus
it is assumed to have little impact on the subject of this thesis.
In-action the SVO algorithm requires the camera axis to be orthogonal to the plane of the structure
it is covering [3]. This means that measures must be made to either put the VO on hold while
manoeuvring ’oﬀangle’ with the ROV during inspection or have a camera mounted on a 3 DOF
pod to keep the structure at the correct angle at all times. The ﬁrst being the preferred solution
in order to limit complexity and keep risk of entanglement low.
Image enhancement prior to feature detection is recommended by [4]. This is done to de-haze the
images and to improve the contrast in the images, thus giving the feature detector more details to
work with. Since image improvement, feature detection and VO are so essential, these topics are
addressed in separate chapters Image enhancement, Feature detection and Visual odometry.
3.6
Conclusion on analysis
By use of VO, the location and orientation of an underwater camera will be determined while
recording video. The information will be stored in a database, so footage of speciﬁc areas of a
known structure can be found later.
Given the constraints underwater, the camera-rig must have lighting system, which can evenly
light up the Field Of View (FOV). For this project manually recorded video is suﬃcient, but for
application in the industry, recording by ROV/AUV must be used, since it will beneﬁt from more
on-board navigational equipment, which can improve the results of the VO, and to lower the cost
of operation.
In fast moving situations a global shutter camera is preferred, but underwater usage with slow
movements is less demanding than a fast moving MAV. And rolling shutter cameras will be used
in this project.

Chapter 5
Calibration of cameras
The MATLAB calibration tool is used to calibrate the indoor and outdoor cameras. The calibration
is necessary in order to know the respective cameras intrinsic parameters, which are going to be
used for the calculation of the homography in the SVO-algorithm.
There are several ways to calibrate cameras but a typical way is to use a checker board since it
contains very distinct corners in a well known pattern.
In order to make the calibration underwater the checker board is laminated and put inside a photo-
frame with a front glass to keep the pattern as ﬂat as possible. Then an underwater video is made,
covering diﬀerent angles and locations of the checker board in the image.
The Tokina 11-16mm lens has a diagonal FOV of 84-104° in air. With correct placement in the
dome port the lens will have the same FOV underwater, thus eliminating the eﬀects of refraction.
To be sure to get the same focal length each time, the Tokina must be ﬁxed at 16mm thus giving
a FOV equal to 84°, thus keeping it under the 95° required by the calibration tool.
The raspberry pi camera shoots with a FOV similar to a full frame camera with 35mm lens and is
well within the boundary. The FOV is further reduced by shooting through a ﬂat port.
Botelho, Oliveira, Drews, et al. [5] points out that besides the radial and tangential distortion of
a camera system, there is a third kind of distortion added underwater due the diﬀraction. The
distortion is of radial character. In this project the outdoor footage is shot through a domeport,
which corrects the image distortion. In case the image is still not fully corrected the calibration
process will compensate for the rest, since the calibration is done by use of underwater footage.
The Raspberry pi camera, however is shooting through a ﬂat port and is likely to experience
distortion which can not be fully compensated by the calibration. Further details can be found in
Xu and Negahdaripour [6].

6.1
Visual Saliency
Diﬀerent approaches to enhance underwater footage has been suggested and tested. Zhang, He,
Song, et al. [4] suggests to use dark channel prior [10] and on top of that apply Hyper-complex
Fourier Transform (HFT). The combined algorithm is called ’Visual Saliency’. The algorithms
suggested by the authors is implemented in MATLAB within this project and tested in Chapter 7.
The dark channel prior is an algorithm for de-hazing images.
It was originally not made for
underwater images, but the addition of HFT is speciﬁcally for underwater usage. Visual Saliency
is addressed in this project beacause issues with feature detection and matching underwater is
expected to be diﬃcult, and since [4] concludes:"...this algorithm can clearly highlight the visual
saliency objects..." and "feature extraction and matching speed is faster" the hope is that it can
improve the performance of VO.
According to the authors the output beneﬁts from creating:
• a clearer more eye-pleasing contrasty image
• an image on which computer vision algorithms reacts better

I(x) = J(x)t(x) + A(1 −t(x))

’direct attenuation’ and ’air light’ respectively.
The theory is further based the observation that that most local patches in a haze-free outdoor
image contains pixels with very low intensities in at least one color-channel.

7.1
Area Based Method (ABM) and Feature Based Method (FBM)
To track the camera movement it is necessary to track objects in the image. This can either be
done with ABM, where a number of patches are matched between frames based on intensity or
with FBM, where a number of detected characteristics at speciﬁc points in the image are compared
between frames.
7.1.1
ABM
ABM is based on comparison of equal sized patches between two frames, and is also called the
direct method. To create a match, the method requires the patches to be of the same intensity.
Under water natural light, artiﬁcial light and changing shadows as a result of moving marine
growth and changing position of light relative to the structure, creates big intensity changes in the
image. Matching of intensities is therefore expected to be diﬃcult in underwater scenarios.
Joglekar and Gedam [11] concludes: ’when dense disparity map and simple algorithms for less
computational complexity are needed area based algorithms outperform feature based algorithms’.
They also ﬁnd that a combination of ABM and FBM gives the highest accuracy’
29
==PAGE:50==
7.

7.1.2
FBM
Another approach is to use descriptors found by use of feature detection, extraction and matching
algorithms. Features are detected by investigating gradients in small parts of the frame at a time.
The gradients for each feature are gathered in a descriptor which is then matched with the features
found in subsequent frames.
This method is more expensive in terms of CPU time, but depending on the purpose and the
design of the descriptor it can be more robust to variation in light, scale and orientation.
7.2
Feature detectors
Some of the FBM-methods are listed in the subsections below with a short description. Further
details can be found in [12]. Examples of feature matching can be found in section 7.4.
Harris Corner The Harris Corner detector is one of the most used detectors. It looks for features
where there is a gradient in both directions (x- and y-plane) - the characteristics of a corner.
To cancel out noise and jpg-artefacts, the image is averaged (or blurred) by convolution with
a Gaussian ﬁlter. In order to get only the most signiﬁcant feature in a region, non maximum
suppression is used. A threshold is set to detect only the most signiﬁcant features.
Blob Detector A blop is deﬁned as an image location where the second order derivative - also
called Hessian - is large. The numerical value indicating the signiﬁcance of the blob is determined
by either the determinant or the trace (Laplacian) of the Hessian. For 2D images the Hessian is a
2x2 matrix.
Another blob detector is the Diﬀerence of Gaussian (DoG)-detector, which is used in the Scale
Invariant Features Transform (SIFT)-detector. The result of the detection is very similar to the
Hessian, but it operates by convolving the input image with two diﬀerent Gaussian kernels and
then subtracting one from the other. See more under ??
SIFT SIFT builds on a DoG detector. It works by convolving the image with Gaussian kernels at
diﬀerent scales and subtract them from each other. The result is very similar to the Laplacian as
described in ??, thus making a blob detection.
In a scale space environment this is eﬃcient, since the same image already exists in diﬀerent scales.
Instead of working with diﬀerent ﬁlter sizes, the same Gaussian ﬁlter can be applied to the diﬀerent
sized images - this save time when working on the downscaled images. To get the DoG it is just
a matter of subtracting the images. Since the Gaussian ﬁlter works like a low pass ﬁlter in the
frequency domain, the result - after the subtraction - is that of a bandpass ﬁlter.
The SIFT detector is ’scale invariant’ because objects found at one scale can be found at another
scale in a later frame due to the use of the scale space environment.
According to [13], SIFT is considered as one of highest quality detectors at the expense of compu-
tational cost.
FAST Features from Accelerated Segment Test (FAST) is a corner detector and was presented by
Rosten and Drummond [14] in 2005. It was made to aid real time performance, and the authors
30
==PAGE:51==

found that full frame feature detection could be run at 400Hz and describes how this leads to a
system which can track very rapid motions, such as 50° camera shake at 6Hz.
Figure 7.1: FAST feature detection principle. FAST is done at a pixel p by examining a circle
of 16 pixels around p. A feature is detected at p if the intensities of at least 12
contiguous pixels are all above or all below the intensity of p by some threshold. The
test is optimized by examining pixels 1, 9, 5 and 13, to reject candidate pixels more
quickly. The pixel intensities from the 16 pixels are used as a feature vector [14].
In [14] the authors tests, the feature detection runs more than 15 times faster than using Harris
Corner, but ’trade oﬀthe quality of corner detection and matching’. According to Leutenegger,
Chli, and Siegwart [13] it is not robust to image distortions and transformations.
Rosten, Porter, and Drummond [15] made an updated version of the detector in 2010. The new
version includes a machine learning approach where the detector can be optimized for repeatability.
SURF Leutenegger, Chli, and Siegwart [13] describes Speeded Up Robust Features (SURF) as a
fast and reliable blob detector, in order to run real time SLAM algorithms.
BRISK In [13], the authors describe Binary robust invariant scalable keypoints (BRISK) as a novel
way to detect features in a robust and fast way. It is based on a "novel scale space FAST based
detector in combination with the assembly of a bit-string descriptor from intensity comparisons
retrieved by dedicated sampling of each keypoint neighborhood". BRISK is invariant to scale and
rotation.

Studies on feature detection and matching in underwater environments
Oliver, Hou, and Wang [9] have investigated the inﬂuence of underwater blurring on repeatability
and correspondence - corner and blop detectors were represented by Harris and Hessian respec-

object (teddy bear), and an object with geometric shapes (Secchi disk) as illustrated in Figure 7.4.
The results for the teddy bear are shown in Figure 7.5 and the results for the Secchi disk can
be seen in Figure 7.6. The study concludes that to produce high repeatability, the best is the
Hessian detector (blob). When it comes to matching, Harris performs the best on the ﬂuﬀy bear
image - this advantage disappears for the more structured scene with the Secchi disk. It should
be emphasized that both methods struggle with the blurred simulated underwater images.
Also the performance of the descriptors were measured. Here the Hessian based descriptors proved
the best.
The authors conclude that techniques to overcome the murkiness of underwater could be a way
forward. Such a technique is described in Chapter 6 and tested later in this chapter (Chapter 7).
For feature detection and matching in underwater environments with marine growth the results
in [9] indicates that a blob detector is probably the best choice though the corner detector has its
pros in terms of feature matching when it comes to structures full of ﬂuﬀy marine growth. This
assumption will be tested later in this chapter.

Chapter 8
Visual odometry
There are many techniques to keep track of the movement and position of a vehicle, and the best
result is often to combine several of them. The choice of which to use depends on the purpose
(which unknown parameters to determined and at which precision) and the environment (which
available information can be found).
As an example consider a four wheeled car with Ackermann-steering (steering system where only
the front wheels are used to turn, and used in almost all known cars). By using a counter to keep
track of the rotation of two wheels on an axis (the rear wheels) the change in location and pose
can be determined. This is called odometry.
The odometry works ﬁne in the perfect world, but for example sliding and loss of grip due to
imperfect contact between wheels and road leads to erroneous calculations, and with movement
the error increases.
Visual Odometry (VO) is an alternative approach, where the wheels on the road are replaced by
one or more cameras looking at the surroundings as the vehicle moves.
A VO-system detects
features in each image from the camera(s) and matches them image by image. In that way the
system can see an object moving through the frame as the vehicles moves.
In two tutorials Scaramuzza and Fraundorfer [18] and Fraundorfer and Scaramuzza [16] explain
the history and principles of VO and ﬁnd evidence that since VO is not aﬀected by wheel slip it
turns out to be more precise than conventional odometry with position errors of only 0.1 to 2%.
In this chapter following topics will be covered:
• introduction to the theory behind VO
• generic algorithm for VO
• related work and results by others
• description of SVO (Semi-direct VO)
• test of SVO
• comparison with the results in section 7.4

VO is highly relevant in environments with no GPS-signal, and it has reached a level where it is
considered reliable. For example it has been used by two Mars exploration rovers, where VO was
found very useful on slippery slopes [19]. Underwater environments also lack GPS-signal, and like
on slippery slopes, odometry by counting rotations on the thrusters, is of little use.
Monocular VO is able to output 3D data, but if neither the distance to an object in the image and
the scale of the object, nor the physical distance between frames is known; the scale of the output
is unknown.
As the name suggests Simultanous Location and Mapping (SLAM) is a method for a vehicle to
draw a map of the surroundings while ﬁnding out where it is. This is most often done by use of
camera.
VO and SLAM are closely related, but where SLAM has a focus on the global map consistency,
VO focuses on the local map. As such VO can be used as a building block for SLAM, in which
case VO must be developed with a detection of loop closure (the camera comes back to where it
started) and a global optimization of the obtained map. As a consequence SLAM is more precise,
sometimes more robust, more complex and computationally more expensive.
To create a database with video of a known structure, stamped with location and orientation, VO
is suﬃcient, since the map of the structure is known, and the scale can be deducted.
8.3
Related work
Related Work Dunbabin, Corke, Buskey, et al. [20] reach a precision of 3 % in conﬁned waters
with pepplestones as a feature rich sea bed and applying Harris Corner detector and a combination
of vision and inertial-based navigation. Also in conﬁned waters Botelho, Oliveira, Drews, et al. [5]
reach a ’satisfactory’ result during use underwater with VO and SIFT feature detector.
Warren, Corke, Ben, et al. [21] writes that VO is rarely used in AUVs as a pose estimator because of
low frame rates with little image overlap due to energy conservation and data storage requirements.
In the paper they present a novel stereo VO pipeline. In combination with constrained motion
and use of magnetometer and BA, they are able to improve the pose accuracy signiﬁcantly for
trajectories over 300 meters.
Wirth, Carrasco, and Codina [22] tests two diﬀerent VO-algorithms in conﬁned waters and in a
harbour. They conclude that the libvisio2 algorithm using blob and corner detector works better
in the outdoor environment compared to the fovis algorithm using FAST detector.
Bellavia1, Fanfani1, and Colombo [23] Finds that the use of key frames reduce the drift error of
VO.
Forster, Pizzoli, and Scaramuzza [3] presents a novel VO algorithm called SVO, which has a focus
on speed and easily runs real-time. it makes use of a combination of ABM and FBM and uses

8.4
Fast Semi-Direct Monocular Visual Odometry
VO is described in general terms in the ﬁrst part of this chapter. For this project the focus lies on
VO as outlined by Beier [1]. Forster, Pizzoli, and Scaramuzza [3] describes a variety of VO, which
they have called SVO. Though not being developed for underwater use it holds some characteristics
which are desirable for use in an AUV: It is fast, thus making it useful as part of an on board
navigation system and it works well on images with high frequency content. Marine growth is an
example of such content.
One of the claimed strong points of SVO is that it, in the context of monocular VO, uses a mix of
FBM and ABM (see description in section 7.1). This is done to beneﬁt from FBM’s feature-rich
detection, parallel tracking and mapping (see Figure 8.4) and keyframe selection and at the same
time beneﬁt from the accuracy and speed of the ABM.
Forster, Pizzoli, and Scaramuzza [3] cites other articles concluding that ABM can outperform FBM
when it comes to robustness in scenes with little texture and during camera defocus and motion
blur. Which are likely scenarios in marine environments.
As described in subsection 7.3.1, FAST is the most eﬃcient feature detection. SVO as described
in [3] is using this for feature detection of keyframes. Given the results in Chapter 7 it will be
investigated in Chapter 9 if FAST is also a good choice for underwater detection, or if it will be a
better choice to spend more CPU time and in return get a more robust feature detection by using
SURF.
The speed is estimated to be around 55 frames per second on an on board embedded computer
and 300 frames per second on a consumer laptop (estimated in 2014). That means the output
from the algorithm is generated fast enough to be used as a part of the navigation of an AUV.
SVO requires a FOV in the wide angle (64°-84°) to ultra wide angle range (above 84°).
Figure 8.4 shows that two threads are running in parallel in SVO. The camera motion estimations
are output at real-time (50-300fps), while the less time critical mapping thread is not needed to
run hard real time.
In the Motion Estimation Thread following progression takes place: First the sparse model based
image alignment, estimates the new pose of the camera by minimizing the photometric error as
indicated in Figure 8.5(a).
Secondly it reﬁnes the re-projected 2D points by aligning the feature patches with previous frames
illustrated in Figure 8.5(b).
Finally the camera pose and location of 3D points are optimized to minimize the re-projection
error, which was determined during the previous step as shown in Figure 8.5(c).
In the mapping thread new depth ﬁlters are initiated each time a new key-frame is detected.
At every subsequent non key-frame the depth estimate is updated, and when the uncertainty is

9.3.
Conclusion - why does the SVO stop tracking
The best underwater footage produces as many features in the SVO feature detector as the reference
data. The loss of tracking is likely to be caused by poor feature matching, or if the ABM based
matching is not working well in underwater conditions as previously suggested in subsection 7.1.1.
The implementation of SVO in this project uses FAST for feature detection in the keyframes.
Botelho, Oliveira, Drews, et al. [5] are successfully using SIFT-features in their underwater scenario,
and SIFT is known to be more robust compared to FAST Chapter 7 as discussed in subsection 7.3.1.
The SVO algorithm is likely to beneﬁt from a replacement of the FAST detector with a SURF, or
even better a SIFT detector.
The reduced speed by choosing another detector is in order, as long as the output of the SVO is
not going to be used for navigational purposes - in fact the calculations can be done on land after
the footage is downloaded.

Chapter 10
Database
The database for the solution suggested in section 1.2 has not been made within the frames of
this project. Therefore, in this chapter the content, basic structure and user interface of such a
database are discussed on an initial basis.
10.1
Content
The content of this database must consist of all available information of interest during and after
inspection of a structure.
Video is the the most essential part of the database.
It might not be stored directly in the
database. A common advise from fora on this matter, is to store the videos and images in a ﬁle
structure, and refer to them from the database. Metadata can be stored as part of the container for
the video ﬁles(for example .mp4 ﬁles). This can for example be used to store information related
to the exposure of the video: exposure settings, position and power settings for the light system
e.t.c..
The reference to the video from the database is done by use of unique ﬁle naming and a time
indicator.
For example the ﬁlename can including place, equipment ID, date and time of the
beginning of the recording. The time indicator can be run-time or frame number.
The video should be stored in highest possible resolution, to preserve information. Experiments
can be done with the level of compression. It must be set at a conservative, low level to avoid
artefacts.
Location and Orientation tells where, on the known structure, the footage is taken. The output
from the SVO algorithm is the location of the camera, whereas the information in the database
should be the location and orientation of the objects in the images. Spelled out: The user is
interested in pointing out and seeing areas of the structure under inspection. The location of the
camera is of secondary interest.
In the database, Time is only used to keep track of when a certain recording was done. It is
therefore not required to be very precise. Within 1 minute of the correct time is suﬃcient.
The above information is enough to make a basic database, but it must be ﬂexible, so additional
information can be added. For example reference to additional video recordings made by other

Conclusion
In this report the use of monocular Semi-direct Visual Odometry (SVO) in underwater environ-
ments has been investigated with the end goal to make a database which can ease and improve
inspection of man made underwater structures. Such a database with location, orientation and
time stamped video frames will make it possible to search for places of interest on a structure. It
will also make it possible to investigate changes over time.
For this purpose a high quality video source is required, providing enough details to make a thor-
ough visual inspection by use of the recorded video. This requires high quality camera equipment
and lighting system.
This project has worked with rolling shutter cameras (as opposed to global shutter), since the
speed and angular velocities are limited in the test set ups. For the indoor videos a Raspberry pi
type 2 camera has been used and for the outdoor recording a Nikon D500 in underwater housing
and external light has been used.
A real life solution will beneﬁt from more navigational information from on board electronics of
an ROV/AUV. IMUs can be integrated with the SVO algorithm and improve the precision.
Feature detection and matching are key elements when using Visual Odometry (VO). In this
project, indoor and outdoor tests showed that SURF is the best choice for underwater usage. Four
feature detectors were compared: Harris Corner, Features from Accelerated Segment Test (FAST),
Speeded Up Robust Features (SURF) and Binary robust invariant scalable keypoints (BRISK).
SURF has a high output of matched features, the highest match ratio, few errors and the longest
average feature life time.
The Visual Saliency image enhancing algorithm does not improve the performance of the feature
detectors signiﬁcantly - it sometimes makes it worse. One exception is high resolution footage
in well illuminated environment with clear water. Here the FAST feature detector in the SVO
algorithm, beneﬁts to great extend from the enhancement.
The SVO algorithm must provide orientation and location in world coordinates for the camera.
Time is also a necessary output to time stamp the video frames for documentation purposes. If
the VO algorithm should work with other measuring units, timing becomes essential, and use of
Robot Operation System (ROS) as an overall system with a master clock must be used.
When applying the SVO algorithm to the underwater footage it looses features and the ability to
match them. SVO uses a combination of Area Based Method (ABM) and Feature Based Method
(FBM). The former is known for being fast but not so robust. SVO uses FAST (FBM) for the
73

detection of key frames. This project shows that it is not the optimal choice for underwater use.
Changing to a blob detector like SIFT or SURF will improve detection and matching of key frames.
It is also clear that the SVO algorithm performs much better with well lit recordings (HP3971).
This is expected to be due to better conditions for the part of the detection which usesABM.
A more suitable feature detector and better lighting might enable underwater use of SVO. It is
therefore suggested to:
• change feature detector from FAST to SIFT or SURF and try experiments with the well lit
image series HP3971
• if ﬁrst step works; change lighting system to something more powerful, making sure to place
it to avoid backscatter and make new recordings under Munkholmbroen or other places with
murky water and marine growth.
• even if the SVO can be brought to work underwater it must be considered if there are other,
more robust, solutions which can be applied - maybe other approaches to VO.
Future steps to follow:
• investigate potential market and use
• make a prototype of the hardware to be mounted on board a ROV/AUV
• make a pseudo user interface to the database
• test both with users and feedback on relevance, design, use e.t.c.
