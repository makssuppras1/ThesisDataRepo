Abstract (English)
The topic of my thesis is upon the optimization of the clustering process of observations.
More specifically, I will focus on the special case where the input data has the structure of a
graph, however the researched techniques could increase the effectiveness of clustering
within various input­datasets’ formats. In graph theory the clustering methodology can
be called Community detection. Community detection is the notion of dividing a network
into groups. The research focus upon detecting communities has increased over the past
years given the exponentially growing amount of available information and the resulted
complexity. The communities can show significant thematic and/or functional similarities
which can be valuable in many cases to identify patterns and extract insights. In the
hierarchical clustering algorithm the outcome results in a dendrogram which is being cut
according to the optimal value of an objective function in order to extract the clusters.
However, there are cases where cutting the dendrogram on multiple levels could reveal
improved communities detection. Such case could be when the resulted dendrogram is
unbalanced, which is the main hypothesis of the thesis.
In this thesis, I will investigate a number of different techniques with the aim being the
optimization of the objective function and, thus, the improvement of the generated clus­
ters. The baseline method will be Link Clustering [1], which is a variant of hierarchical
clustering. Later, I will compare the level of improvement of each approach in relation to
the baseline method and explore whether there is correlation between the improvement
and the graph and dendrogram’s characteristics (e.g. dendrogram balanceness, network
size, etc.).
One of the implemented techniques is the Greedy algorithm in which I search both by
starting from the root (“top down” approach) and from the leaves (“bottom up” approach)
of the dendrogram to find the optimal value of the objective function. Following that, I de­
veloped a method in which we can tune the single­level cut based on randomly generated
moves in the dendrogram. The final technique corresponds to the inclusion of the Monte
Carlo simulation upon the “tuning the single­level cut” approach. Finally, as part of the
evaluation of the results I developed the Balanceness measure in order to categorize the
networks based on their dendrogram structure.
Keywords
Clustering, Hierarchical Clustering, Network Science, Graph Theory, Community Detec­
tion, Link Clustering, Dendrogram, Greedy Algorithm, Monte Carlo, Balanceness

Abstract (Danish)
Emnet for mit speciale handler om optimering af klyngeprocessen af observationer. Mere
specifikt vil jeg fokusere på det særlige tilfælde, hvor inputdata har strukturen som en
graf, men de undersøgte teknikker kunne øge effektiviteten af dendrogrammer inden for
forskellige input­datasæts formater. I grafteorien kan klyngemetoden kaldes fællesskab­
sdetektion. Fællesskabsdetektion er ideen om at opdele et netværk i grupper. Forsknin­
gens fokus på at opdage lokalsamfund er steget i løbet af de seneste år på grund af den
eksponentielt voksende mængde af tilgængelig information og den resulterende komplek­
sitet. Fællesskaberne kan vise væsentlige tematiske og/eller funktionelle ligheder, som i
mange tilfælde kan være værdifulde til at identificere mønstre og uddrage indsigt. I den
hierarkiske klyngealgoritme resulterer resultatet i et dendrogram, som skæres i henhold til
den optimale værdi af en objektiv funktion for at udtrække klyngerne. Der er dog tilfælde,
hvor skæring af dendrogrammet på flere niveauer kunne afsløre forbedret fællesskabs­
detektion. Et sådant tilfælde kan være, når det resulterende dendrogram er ubalanceret,
hvilket er hovedhypotesen i afhandlingen.
I dette speciale vil jeg undersøge en række forskellige teknikker med det formål at opti­
mere den objektive funktion og dermed forbedringen af de genererede klynger. Basisme­
toden vil være Link Clustering [1], som er en variant af hierarkisk clustering. Senere vil
jeg sammenligne forbedringsniveauet for hver tilgang i forhold til baseline­metoden og un­
dersøge, om der er sammenhæng mellem forbedringen og grafens karakteristika (f.eks.
balanceness, størrelse).
En af de implementerede teknikker er Greedy­algoritmen, hvor jeg både søger ved at
starte fra roden (“top down” tilgang) og fra bladene (“bottom up” tilgang) i dendrogram­
met for at finde den optimale værdi af den objektive funktion. Efter det udviklede jeg
en metode, hvor vi kan tune enkelt­niveauskæringen baseret på tilfældigt genererede
bevægelser i dendrogrammet.
Den endelige teknik svarer til inkluderingen af Monte
Carlo­simuleringen på ”tuning the single­level cut”­tilgangen. Til sidst, som en del af eval­
ueringen af resultaterne, udviklede jeg Balanceness for at kategorisere netværkene ud
fra deres struktur.
Keywords
Clustering, Hierarchical Clustering, Network Science, Graph Theory, Community Detec­
tion, Link Clustering, Dendrogram, Greedy Algorithm, Monte Carlo, Balanceness
Understanding optimal cuts for unbalanced dendrograms

2.1
Cluster Analysis
Cluster analysis or clustering is an unsupervised learning method which means that the
input data do not have labeled outcomes, but are decided by the algorithm dynamically.
It is widely used to identify patterns and meaningful structure in a dataset to improve
decision making in many sectors, such as medicine, biology, industry, etc. It is considered
one of the oldest data exploration models and a common technique for statistical data
analysis. The objective is to divide a set of data points, observations or feature vectors in a
number of groups, which are called clusters. Each cluster must be homogenous, meaning
that the observations belonging to it are similar to each other, and each cluster must be
unique compared to the rest based on some characteristics. The division is being applied
based on a similarity metric between the observations and each methodology proposes
a different set of rules for defining similarity/dissimilarity. One of the most commonly
used is the Euclidean distance function. In other words, the goal is to generate groups
or clusters where data points in the same cluster are more similar compared to the data
points in other groups. The difference of clustering from data classification is that in the
classification technique the groups in which we organize the data are predefined, which
means that the number of groups, their names and identifies are known in advance.
Cluster analysis is a family of classification methods, which can be applied across many
scientific areas. The first applications come from Zoology, where in this case the method
is often called numerical taxonomy, and Biology. Other worth mentioning applications are
in Medicine for grouping patients, symptoms or treatments, in Bioinformatics and Artifi­
cial Intelligence for pattern recognition, on the Internet for grouping related web pages,
etc. Moreover, it is often used in advertising and marketing for separation of consumers
into groups based on their consumption behavior. As an outcome of cluster analysis, a
researcher can gain valuable insights from each cluster like recognising common charac­
teristics and deriving to useful conclusions for decision­making. Moreover, the result can
be used for detecting outliers and by removing such observations we can conclude to an
improved version of the dataset.
The list of the different clustering algorithms is long, however they can be separated in
five popular categories [6]:
• Hierarchical (or Connectivity) methods: These methods are based on the concept
that nearby observations are higher related than the ones further away. Thus, the
similarity metric is associated to the distance between the points in the data space.
It performs a process of continuous cluster splits or merges and they proceed either
with successive divisions or with successive combinations of the observations with
the aim being the desired clustering.

• Partitioning (or Centroid) methods: These methods separate the objects into k clus­
ters and the number of groups (the k) is determined in advance as hyper­parameter.
We iteratively relocate the observations between clusters based on the closeness
of observations to the centroid of clusters until according to a criterion (the value of
which decreases at each iteration) we achieve an optimal partition. One of the most
famous algorithms is K­Means.
• Density­based methods: In these methods, we create clusters by detecting areas
in the data space where there is high density (areas where observations are mostly
concentrated) and sparse regions.
• Grid­based methods: These methods separate the data space into cells, forming a
grid, with the aim to represent the observations as cells and perform the clustering
on them. The number of cells is predefined and the method is known for the fast
processing time given the fact that cells are much fewer than the observations.
• Model­based (or Distribution) methods: In these methods we divide the observa­
tions based on the probability of belonging to a distribution (e.g. Gaussian).

2.2
Hierarchical Clustering
Hierarchical Clustering belongs to the family of connectivity models and its goal is to group
data points into a collection of hierarchical groups. The end result is represented by a hi­
erarchical tree, a dendrogram, in which x axis is the observations and the y axis is the
distances between them. Therefore, Hierarchical Clustering creates a hierarchy of levels
that describe a way of separating observations into clusters. In these methods the number
of clusters is not predefined. There are two different implementation methodologies for it,
the agglomerative (or “bottom up”) approach and the divisive (or “top down”) approach.
The agglomerative approach initially assigns each data point to its own cluster (“bottom”
state) and at each iteration we form clusters with higher hierarchy by merging pairs of
clusters which have less distance between them. The divisive approach begins by con­
sidering that all data points belong to a single cluster (“top” state) and divides recursively
to sub­clusters, in such a way that the observation with the greater distance from the oth­
ers is leaving the single cluster and creates a new cluster by itself. The methodology that
I studied during my thesis and will be discussed further in the following Chapters is the
Agglomerative Hierarchical Clustering as it is the basis of the method I relied on during
my research [1].
From the above definitions we can interpret that hierarchical clustering follows a greedy
approach, since at each iteration we “walk” either following a “bottom up” direction or
“top down” and perform one merge or division at each step based on a similarity/dissim­
ilarity measurement. The task of defining an appropriate similarity/dissimilarity metric is
essential as it measures the distance between the data points. Equally important is the
selection of linkage criterion which defines the method used for measuring the similarity
(or distance) between two clusters. In the following Sections 2.2.1, 2.2.2 and 2.2.3 I will
present the various similarity metrics, linkage criteria and the dendrogram representation
of Hierarchical Clustering outcome. In general, Hierarchical Clustering technique has a
wide range of applications given its flexibility on selecting the similarity measurement or
the linkage criteria and the fact that it is not necessary to define in advance the number

2.2.3
Dendrograms
Dendrograms are a graphical way of representing data and specifically in cluster analysis
we use them to represent the observations and their merges or divisions. In hierarchical
clustering the clusters hierarchy is plotted in the form of a dendrogram. Dendrograms are
defined as tree diagrams which are frequently used in biology to show clustering between
genes or samples. A dendrogram looks like a tree which is composed of stacked branches
which are divided into smaller branches and at the end of the dendrogram we have the
individual elements or data points, which are also called leaves. In the agglomerative
clustering each node with its branches and children represents the merging of the clusters
and the creation of the parent cluster, while in the divisive clustering each node represents
the division of the parent and the creation of the child­clusters. The arrangement of the
branches shows the level of (dis)similarity between them. When two leaves are in the
same branch this means that they are more similar than two leaves in a different branch.
In the y axis we display the value of the linkage criterion, indicating the distance of the
data points or clusters from each other. The larger the gap between two branches, the
further and more different the clusters are.

2.3.1
Graph Theory
Graphs (or networks) are defined as an abstract way to represent relationships between
a set of elements. The term of graph corresponds to a set of nodes (also called vertices)
and the edges which create the connections between some of the nodes. A more specific

definition of graphs would be: graph is a pair G = (V, E), where V = 1, ..., N corresponds
to the set of vertices and E = (i, j) : i, j ∈V is the set of edges in graph G. The number
of nodes in a graph is denoted as n = |V | and the number of edges is represented as
m = |E|. Each edge is defined by two nodes, called endpoints, and when an edge e has
u, v as endpoints we can say that e connects u, v. In such cases the edge is denoted as

Graphs can be separated in two main categories, the directed and the undirected ones.
Directed are the graphs in which edges have orientations, thus when a graph is undirected
it means that there is symmetry between the nodes and the existing edge from node i
to node j entails to the existence of edge ji (Figure 2.5). Another type of graph is the
weighted in which a number is assigned in each edge [9]. The weight can represent

graph G = (V1, V2, E) is called bipartite if the set of nodes V is separated in two disjoint

edges between the nodes of the same subgroup.

A graph’s density is defined by the set of its edges and nodes. If two graphs have the same
order, but different size, then they have different density and the graph with the largest size
is said to be denser than the one with the smallest size. Given an edge (u, v), the node u
is called neighbor of node v, and conversely. The neighborhood of a node u is denoted as
N(u) and it is the set of nodes defined by the equation: N(u) = [u ∈V (G)|(u, v) ∈E(G)].
The number of neighbors incident on the node u is called its degree and is denoted as
d(u). When d(u) = 0 the node is called isolated and when d(u) = 1 the node is called
pendant. A graph in which every node has degree d is called d­normal.
One of the most important concepts in graph theory is Centrality which helps us measure
the importance of each node, or how “central” it is. However, there are different ways to
define importance in a graph. One of the concepts is called Degree Centrality. Degree of
a node in an undirected graph corresponds to the number of connections (edges) a node
has to the other nodes. In the case of a directed graph the degree of a node is divided to
in­degree and out­degree, where the in­degree is the number of edges incident on it and
the out­degree is the number of edges from the node to the other nodes. Accordingly, in
relation to the Degree Centrality metric we consider that the higher the degree of a node,

Community Detection
Modern networks are growing exponentially in size, variety, and complexity which results
in increased complexity in their patterns. These patterns are important to understand
because they can have unexpected effects on various characteristics of the network [10].
One of the basic function of network analysis is community detection.
Community definition
A widely accepted definition of communities is based upon the topology of edges. This
means that the definition of communities is being formed in relation to the differences
in the density of the connections in the network. Consequently, communities in a net­
work are the groups of nodes, which are highly connected to each other than to the rest
of the nodes in the network, and probably share common properties and/or play similar
roles within the graph [11]. This feature called community structure can be seen in sev­
eral systems in the world, for example in society where we have families, working and
friendship circles, common interests, cities, nations, etc. The occurrence of communities
can be identified also in biology, such as in protein­protein interaction networks where
communities are likely to group proteins having the same function within the cell. More­
over, we could consider as communities the groups of websites in the internet referring
to the same topic. Such groupings can be beneficial for better understanding of networks
structure. An example of communities can be seen in Figure 2.6, in which we can see
the three different communities (blue, green, orange) and it is noticeable that the nodes
inside each community have more connections between them compared to the number
of connections with the nodes from the other communities.

Even though the definition for communities stated above is commonly used, there are
several other proposed definitions in the literature [12]: the local definitions, the global
definitions and the definitions based on the vertex similarity.
• Local definitions: The local definitions are focused in a subgraph, including its im­
mediate neighborhood and disregarding the rest of the graph. The logic behind this
is that communities have few connections to the rest of the network, and therefore,
can be considered as separate entities with their own autonomy. Such subgraphs
can be considered the cliques, the n­cliques, the k­plexes, etc. A clique is a maximal
subgraph where every node is adjacent to all other nodes in the subgraph. A k­plex
is a maximal subgraph in which every node is adjacent to all the others except at
most k of them. In contrast, a k­core is a maximal subgraph where each node is
adjacent to at least k nodes in the subgraph.
• Global definitions: Communities consist of a set of nodes and edges, thus it is rea­
sonable to compare the topology of the community with the one of the overall graph.
In this category of community definition we often use the null model [5], which is a
copy of the graph with the same number of nodes and edges but with a different
distribution of edges. In the null model there are no community structures and the
simplest way to create one is to distribute the edges randomly between the nodes.
The null model is used to compare the community structure of the original graph
with the corresponding set of nodes and edges of the null model. In this way, it is
possible to estimate how different the structure of each community is in relation to
a graph structure where there are no communities, for example we can observe the

sumption that communities are groups of nodes that are similar to each other. The
similarity between each pair of nodes can be calculated based on some property,
local or global, regardless of whether there is an edge connecting the nodes or not.
Each node ends up in the groups with the most similar nodes. The use of similar­
ity measures for clustering is the basis of traditional methods, such as hierarchical,
partial and spectral clustering.
Types of communities
It is important to note that according to the type of the data or the morphology of the
network there are different types of communities and some main ones are the following:
• Overlapping Communities: On many real­world networks, communities may share
one or more nodes. For example in social networks, one or more people can belong
to different communities such as work, friends and family, in which he/she is the
common member.
• Directed Communities: Corresponding to the directed graphs, many real­world phe­
nomena are presented by connections which are not reciprocal, such as a hyperlink
on the web that takes a user from one web page to another without a way to return
to the previous one.
• Weighted Communities: Corresponding to graph categories, a set of connected
nodes can be considered a community only if the edge weights are strong enough,
given a threshold.
Popular methods
Several algorithms have been proposed in recent years to approach the problem of com­
munity detection. Many of the classic detection techniques require the definition of specific
properties of the communities before the execution, for example the number of commu­
nities [13], the density [14], etc. Other methods are able to extract communities from
a graph without prior knowledge of information, as they gain this information from the
topology [15, 16]. Several methodologies take into consideration specific characteristic
of communities found in the real­world, such as overlapping [17] and hierarchical com­
munities [18, 19]. Most of the algorithms result to sufficient outcomes, however there are
many cases in which the same algorithms might present some weaknesses. For example,
a famous method called modularity optimization often has issues related to the analysis
of large networks, not being able to detect small communities. Even in these cases, it is
possible to extract valuable conclusion from the results like identifying the nodes which
do not belong to any community (outliers) or the nodes which act as “bridges” between
different communities (hubs).
Some of the most recently published techniques for community detection, also called
node clustering, are the clique percolation method (CPM), the greedy modularity opti­
mization algorithm, the Infomap, etc. Clique percolation method, often called Cfinder,
is successfully used for uncovering overlapping communities. The main concept of this
method is that whereas the intercommunity edges are unlikely to generate cliques, the
internal edges of the community are likely to do so. Cliques are complete graphs, which
means that there are connections (edges) between all nodes in the graph. It is consid­
ered that two k­cliques are adjacent when they have in common k­1 nodes, thus the union
Understanding optimal cuts for unbalanced dendrograms
13
==PAGE:36==
of all adjacent k­cliques forms a k­clique community. The greedy modularity optimiza­
tion technique was proposed by Newman [20] and it iteratively joins pairs of communities
based on whether the move improves the partition’s modularity value. Modularity [21] is
widely used for measuring the quality of each partition and the measures of the density of
intra­community edges as compared to intercommunity edges [10]. The higher the value
of the modularity, the better partitions we have. Modularity is also used as a function for
cutting a dendrogram. Finally, Infomap method [22] is considered one of the best and
most accurate node clustering techniques [19] and the main idea is the optimization of a
quality function (called map equation).
2.4
Previous work on Unbalanced dendrograms
In many cases dendrograms appear with a complicated, asymmetric structure. By asym­
metric structure we refer to the case in which the branches at each level of a dendrogram
do not have an equal number of leaves, but some of them gather a highest proportion
compared to the rest. A commonly used technique for identifying the clusters in a dataset
is cutting the dendrogram in a single­level. For data sets which consist of homogeneous
subsets, deciding a single similarity threshold, which cuts the tree at a uniform height, is
usually sufficient. However, for larger dendrograms, which often consist of heterogeneous
subsets, a more effective approach is needed [23].
Finding such an approach is the main focus of my thesis, however it is worth discussing
some other research publications which explore solutions for cutting an unbalanced den­
drogram. In 2008, P. Langfelder, B. Zhang and S. Horvath published a paper with the title
being: “Defining clusters from a hierarchical cluster tree: the Dynamic Tree Cut package
for R”. In this work a new dynamic branch cutting method is proposed where the clusters
are being detected based on their shape. They present two variants of the method the
“Dynamic Tree”, which is a top­down algorithm relying only on the dendrogram and re­
specting the order of the clustered objects on it, and the “Dynamic Hybrid”, which builds
the clusters from bottom up. Instead of aiming on the optimization of a objective function,
the method finds the optimal combination of clusters based on different goals. In the case
of the first variant they aim for the stability of the number of clusters, while in the second
variant they identify the clusters given four criteria: they contain a certain minimum num­
ber of objects, objects too far from a cluster are excluded from it even if they belong to
the same branch of the dendrogram, each cluster must be distinct from its surroundings
and the core of each cluster, defined as the tip of the branch, should be tightly connected
[24]. However, this method lacks on capturing all pattern variations in a dataset.
Another approach was published by A. Vogogias, J. Kennedy, D. Archambault, V.A. Smith
and H. Currant in 2016, “MLCut: exploring Multi­Level Cuts in dendrograms for biological
data”. The proposed interactive software tool is an effective visualisation approach which
would enable the user to explore different clustering scenarios by providing different levels
of detail [23]. However, this method does not result to one optimal solution, rather provides
the users with an exploratory tool.

Baseline method: Link Clustering
Most real networks are characterised by well defined statistics of overlapping such that
nodes simultaneously belong to several groups. Such a statement can be demonstrated
by the numerous communities each of us belongs to, including those related to our sci­
entific activities or personal life (school, hobby, family) and so on [25] (Figure 3.1). This
is not reflected only on social networks, but we can also identify it in medicine. The same
genes are often implicated in multiple diseases, an indication that disease modules of
different disorders overlap [26]. As noted in Section 2.3.2 most of the networks have a
hierarchical structure which means that communities recursively organized in a hierarchy,
however in reality many networks have communities with extensive overlap and hence it
is not possible to capture the relationships between overlapping communities based on
the nodes’ hierarchy.

The specific challenge of detecting overlapping communities is considered of high atten­
tion in the network science community. One of the first techniques for identifying over­
lapping communities is the Clique Percolation algorithm (CFinder 1) proposed by Tamás
Vicsek and collaborators [25]. The method in which I will focus for the purpose of my the­
sis is a most recent one, called Link Clustering, which in contrast to the other approaches
focuses on the links (edges) hierarchy rather than grouping the nodes. Link Clustering
was proposed by Ahn, Bagrow and Lehmann [1].
The main idea of this Link Clustering is related to the fact that even though nodes belong
to multiple groups (individuals belong to a family, work and friends group), links usually
appear for a unique purpose, for example two people are in the same family, work together
or have common interests. Accordingly the definition of a community is modified to a set

3.1
Limitations
Before moving to actual steps of the method it is important to state that the implementation
is limited only to connected pairs of edges, which means that they share a node. The
reason for this limitation is that it is considered impossible to have higher similarity in
a pair of disjoint edges than in a pair of edges that share a node. Consequently, for a
connected pair of edges eik and ejk we define the shared node k as a keystone node and
i and j as impost nodes.
3.2
Link Similarity measure definition
The link similarity S between a pair of links (edges) is given by the Jaccard index [7]:
S (eik, ejk) = |n+(i) ∩n+(j)|
|n+(i) ∪n+(j)|
where n+(i) is a set of the inclusive neighbors of a node i and is defined as:
n+(i) ≡{x|d(i, x) ≤1}
and d(i, x) is the length of the shortest path between node i and x. A simplified definition
could be formed as: the set of the inclusive neighbors contains the node itself and its
neighbors. Figure 3.2 presents an example illustration of the similarity measure. As we
can see link similarity S measures the relative number of common neighbors i and j have.
Thus S = 1 when nodes i and j have the same neighbors and when the overlap between
the neighborhood of the two edges is less, the smaller the S value will be.

3.3
Apply Hierarchical Clustering
As defined in Section 2.2, hierarchical clustering is a technique in which we aim to group
data points (in this case edges) into a collection of hierarchical groups. Link Clustering
uses the agglomerative (or “bottom up”) approach as implementation methodology, as
linkage criterion the single linkage and the similarity measure described above. Particu­
larly the steps taken are the following:
1. Each edge is initially assigned to its own community.
2. We set the order of the similarity set to be descending.
3. We iterate through each pair of edges (eik, ejk) based on the similarity value. This
means that we begin with the pair of edges with the highest similarity value.
4. At each iteration we merge their respective communities. Also, at the end of each
iteration we store the communities indexes and their similarity value, which is con­
sidered as the strength of the merged community and corresponds to the height of
the dendrogram branch. The stored communities indexes and their similarity value
create a set of all the information of the hierarchical community organization, called
Linkage, and it is the input to the dendrogram.
5. The process is finished when all edges belong to a single cluster.
The outcome of the Link Clustering is the linkage matrix, which shows the merged pairs of
communities, their similarity value and the number of edges the new community contains,
has the following structure:
linkage = [[community ID 1, community ID 2, similarity value, number of edges], . . .]
(3.1)

Greedy Algorithm
This Chapter answers the research question “How Greedy Algorithm can be used for op­
timizing the dendrogram’s objective function?”. Greedy algorithm is a simple and intuitive
technique which is used for optimization problems. Greedy is an algorithm that builds up
a solution piece by piece, always choosing the next piece that offers the most benefit.
The main idea is to identify the globally­optimal solution in a problem by making the best
decision at each stage based on an heuristic. Some of the most well­known and of great
success greedy algorithms are Huffman encoding, which is used to compress data, and
Dijkstra’s algorithm, which is used to find the shortest path in a graph. There are cases
in which greedy algorithm does not produce the globally­optimal solution, nevertheless
it will offer a solution which is close to the optimal in a reasonable amount of time. This
technique has been widely used for optimizing problems in machine learning, artificial
intelligence, networks, etc.
The reason for choosing to investigate the impact of applying a greedy strategy in de­
tecting the optimal combination of clusters in a graph is based on the statement that
is regarded as an easy and straightforward way for solving such problems. In general,
the required steps are to define an objective function that needs to be optimized, either
by maximizing it or minimizing it, and start “walking through” the observations based on
whether taking the next step will lead to a higher or lower value for the objective function.
In the case of our problem, which is the adaptive cut, the objective function (or heuristic)
is the partition density and the data is the various communities in the dendrogram. We
have chosen to both investigate the outcome of “moving” from the top of the dendrogram
to the bottom, as well as the opposite orientation, with the aim to maximize the partition
density.
5.1
Greedy Algorithm “top down” approach
5.1.1
Method
The first approach of greedy algorithm that I will present is the “top down”. The idea of this
technique is that we start from the top of the dendrogram and “move” towards the leaves.
In other words we move from the less similar communities to the most similar ones. For
that we use the list described in Section 3.5 which has the communities at each branch in
a ascending order based on the similarity value. At each step we calculate the partition
density value, with the aim to find the combination of communities with the optimal one. If
the current step does not give a better partition density value we do not drill down further to
the children of the specific branch. After we have walked through the whole dendrogram
we consider as clusters the ones with the best partition density. The pseudocode of this
method can be found in 1.

5.2.1
Method
The second approach of greedy algorithm that we investigated was the “bottom up”. The
main logic is similar to the “top down” one, but in this case we start from the bottom of
the dendrogram and “move” towards the top. In other words we move from the most
similar communities to the less similar ones. Correspondingly, we use the list described
in Section 3.5 which has the communities at each branch in a descending order based
on the similarity value. At each step we calculate the partition density value, with the aim
to find the combination of communities with the optimal one. If the current step does not
give a better partition density value we do not “move” further up to the parent­branches
of the specific branch. After we have walked through the whole dendrogram we consider
as clusters the ones with the best partition density. The pseudocode of this method can

Tuning the single­level cut
6.1
Method
After implementing the greedy algorithm technique, the idea of tuning the single level
cut in the dendrogram formed. This Chapter answers the research question “How the
performance of the single­line cut can be improved by tuning it given randomly generated
moves?”. The purpose of this investigation was to observe how our objective function, the
partition density, could be improved by “moving” one step up or down from the optimal cut
line according to the highest partition density value. This could be beneficial for the cases
of unbalanced dendrograms since we could be able to define an adaptive cut which is
close to the optimal single level cut and captures the unique structure of the dendrogram.
The specific approach starts by identifying the single level cut based on the optimal par­
tition density. Afterwards, we randomly choose one of the branches in the cut points and
randomly select to either “move” one level up or one level down. This method runs for a
predefined number of iterations and similarly to the previous approaches at each iteration
we calculate the partition density value, with the aim to find the combination of communi­
ties with the optimal one. If the current step does not give a better partition density value
we keep the previous set of communities and randomly select the next iteration’s choices.
In order to terminate the method, we used the early stopping concept which is widely
used to prevent overfitting, especially when training neural networks. In general, the main
idea is that we stop the training of the model when its performance is getting worse. In our
case we developed the early stopping by setting a threshold in which we run the model for
the number of predefined iterations (until we are in the threshold step). When the value
of partition density remains stable for a number of continuous loops we stop the model.
The pseudocode of this method can be found in 3.

Monte Carlo simulation
7.1
Method
In this method we aim to improve the method “Tuning the single­level cut” described in
Chapter 6 by including the Monte Carlo method. Monte Carlo method is a widely used
technique which solves mathematical problems with the use of statistical random sam­
pling. This specific approach is an addition to the “Tuning the single­level cut” one, which
means that we begin again from the single­level cut, which is the optimal cut line accord­
ing to the highest partition density value extracted from the Link Clustering method. Also,
similarly we want to observe the impact on our objective function, the partition density,
when “moving” between one level up from the optimal cut and one level down from it.
In the “Tuning the single­level cut” method we were only accepting a change in the cut
when it was improving the objective function. In this method we would like to simulate the
process of “walking” (or “moving”) in the dendrogram including moves which decrease the
objective function. Those moves are being accepted according to the value of a randomly
generated variable. However, our initial implementation was often resulting to a similar
“behavior” as Greedy algorithm “top down”. It would quickly accept a move which results
to the root or to a branch close to it and it would stay in this state throughout the simu­
lations not being able to identify a set of partitions which improve the objective function.
Moreover, there were many cases in which the method would accept a high decrease of
the partition density and it would struggle to maximize the partition density, thus in such
cases the partition density would be much worse than the initial value. In order to surpass
those issues we applied the Simulated Annealing technique which adds stochasticity to
the acceptance criterion of the random variable by using a list of different probability val­
ues in order to approximate the global optimal value of the objective function. Also, we set
a rule in which for the high acceptance criterion values the algorithm could only “move”
with direction down. The pseudocode of this method can be found in 4.
More specifically, the process starts by identifying the single level cut based on the optimal
partition density and defining the number of simulations and the list of probability values
for the acceptance criterion, ϵ. Afterwards, we randomly choose one of the branches
in the cut points and randomly select to either “move” one level up or one level down.
This process runs for a predefined number of simulations and similarly to the previous
approaches at each iteration we calculate the partition density value. If the current step
results to a better partition density value we update the optimal set of communities with
the current one. In case the current calculated partition density is decreased we randomly
select a value a from a normal distribution a ∼N(0, 1). If it is less than the value of the
current ϵ, we accept the current set of communities even though it decreases our objective
function. The simulation terminates when we have reached the total number of iterations.

9.3
Discussion
In the Tables 9.1 and 9.2 we can see that the approaches which reach the average score
on average are the Greedy algorithm “bottom up” and the “Tuning the single­level cut”.
The technique with the worst optimization results is the Greedy algorithm “top down” with
only one case of resulting to the highest partition density. As mentioned in Chapter 5,
Greedy algorithm “top down” is not able to explore deeper the dendrogram in most cases
since it begins from the root and it often has splits where one of the communities is a
singleton which results to a decreased partition density. Moreover, Link Clustering has
the best partition density only for 4 networks, which indicates that the adaptive cut could
provide small improvements to the single­level cut. Finally, we can see that out of the 44
networks used for the experiments only the 4 can be categorized as balanced based on
their dendrogram structure.
The Figures 9.1, 9.2, 9.3, 9.4, 9.5 and 9.6 present the relation between each of the fea­
tures described above and the highest improvement for each network. As we can observe
none of the features show any correlation with the small improvement from the imple­
mented methods. Especially the feature of our main hypothesis, the Balanceness of the
dendrogram, is not impactful towards the optimization of the dendrogram cut. In general,
the network measures do not have a notable strong relationship with the improvement of
the baseline method. However, it is worth mentioning that there is little diversity in regards
to the networks characteristics. As we can see the networks edges, nodes, size and den­
sity are mostly gathered in small values. Also, the balanced dendrograms are few, only 4
out of the 44 networks in total. This results to an analysis of more specific set of networks
and, thus, our conclusions can be limited to that.
The Table 9.3 shows that the dendrogram Balanceness is the least important feature in re­
gards to identifying the best method for partitioning the dendrogram of each network. The
features with the highest importance are the density of the network and its average clus­
tering. From these results we can interpret that for identifying the best cutting technique
the network structure is of greater importance than the dendrogram. This outcome was
unexpected, but there could possible be other characteristics of the dendrogram which
are more salient for detecting the optimal clusters.

Conclusion
10.1
Thesis Review
This MSc thesis investigated various techniques for the optimization of the dendrogram
partitioning by maximizing an objective function. It was focused on the specific case where
the input data are in the format of a graph and the baseline method is Link Clustering, a
variant of Hierarchical Clustering.
Due to the current load of information, networks can be characterized of great complexity
and this has increased the interest of researchers for improving various concepts. One of
these concepts is Community Detection or Clustering. In this thesis a broad theoretical
background of Cluster Analysis, along with the fundamental Network Science concepts
was presented. Part of the theory was an overview of various techniques for clustering,
an in­depth presentation of hierarchical clustering and its application in Network Science.
Along with them, I presented the methodology behind the baseline method (Link Cluster­
ing). Those ideas were the basis for the development of the four optimization techniques
and the dendrogram structure metric. More specifically, the thesis tried to answer five
research questions:
• “Which are the prerequisite information from the dendrogram in order to perform
the optimization techniques?” In Chapter 3, after providing a detailed description of
Link Clustering, I present the information which is needed to be extracted from the
dendrogram in order to perform the optimization and the steps I followed to create
the additional outcomes from Link Clustering.
• “How Greedy Algorithm can be used for optimizing the dendrogram’s objective func­
tion?” In Chapter 5 I discuss the main idea behind Greedy algorithm and how I
implemented both the “top down” and the “bottom up” approach. Along with the
process description, I present an evaluation of the performance of each of the two
techniques based on their ability to improve the baseline method. The Greedy al­
gorithm “top down” performs poorly and shows the least improvements, while the
Greedy algorithm “bottom up” performs better and it is considered one of the best
developed optimization techniques.
• “How the performance of the single­line cut can be improved by tuning it given ran­
domly generated moves?” In Chapter 6 I present the approach of tuning the single­
level cut according to randomly generated moves and branches in the dendrogram.
After the process description, I discuss the main outcome of the experiments when
applying this technique. “Tuning the single­level cut” improves the baseline method,
but slightly in most cases.
• “Does the inclusion of the Monte Carlo simulation enhance the results?” In Chapter
7 I present steps followed for the inclusion of Monte Carlo method in the “Tuning the
single­level cut” approach with the aim being to improve the performance. However,
the specific method did not perform as expected but it was noticed that it requires
the tuning of the hyperparameters which is beyond the scope of this thesis.
• “Is it possible to determine if a dendrogram is unbalanced given a measure?” In
Chapter 8 I present a proposal for identifying whether a dendrogram has a balanced

or unbalanced structure. The chapter goes through the necessary theory and steps
for building the Balanceness measure.
All the above findings are combined and further investigated in Chapter 9 in which I present
the total experiments’ outcome and compare the techniques. Moreover, six network/­
dendrogram features are being analyzed with the aim being to better understand which
characteristics are most important for the optimal partition of a dendrogram. As it was
mentioned, the dendrogram Balanceness measure shows the least importance in com­
parison to the network’s metrics. Nevertheless, the discussed weaknesses and limits of
the presented techniques along with key insights leaves space for improvements.
10.2
Future Work
This thesis was focused on Network science as we believe that there is great growth in
this domain and need for further improvements and research. It is a great achievement
that based on our hypothesis we were able to identify various limitations and insights in re­
lation to the optimization of the dendrogram cut. For example, our core idea regarding the
Balanceness of the dendrogram and its impact upon the improvement of the single­level
cut was rejected, but other network’s features show a greater importance. The possibility
of investigating further techniques would be beneficial, such as the Dynamic Programming
approach, which due to time limitation I was not able to develop. Dynamic Programming
extracts a flat clustering from the dendrogram by combining the methodology of hierarchi­
cal clustering and center­based clustering. Moreover, I believe that it would be important
to perform the same experiment setup and the feature importance steps with more net­
works and with more diversity in regards to their characteristics. Finally, I consider worth
investigating the performance of the developed techniques on input datasets of a different
format and explore their characteristics importance.

A
Experiments Methodology
Given the fact that an investigation of how the methods perform in a wide variety of
datasets was necessary, it was important to develop a script for evaluation. Tt is worth
noting that for the development of the methods I used the Python programming lan­
guage (version 3.8.10) and the implemented scripts can be found in a GitHub Repository:
https://github.com/ElectraZarafeta/adaptive_cut. The challenge in the methods evalua­
tion process is the results storing. For this purpose I used the MLFlow 1 which is a widely
used platform for Machine Learning related projects. This platform allows to track ex­
periments to record and compare parameters and results. The required steps for the
experiments were the following, which are also presented in Figure A.1:
1. For each dataset, I create a new experiment in MLFlow.
2. I run each of the methods: Link Clustering, Greedy algorithm “top down”, Greedy
algorithm “bottom up”, “Tuning single­level cut”, Monte Carlo simulation
3. For each method, I store the name of it as parameter. The partition density value and
the balanceness as metrics, while the plots generated (Dendrogram) as artifacts.
In Figure A.2 we can see an example of the experiments (Les Miserables network) stored
at the MLFlow platform.
