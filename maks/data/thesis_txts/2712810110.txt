Abstract
Current LiôÙion rechargeable batteries are playing a key role in the transition to
renewable energies. While the current battery design has seen many improveôÙ
ments over the years, many aspects of the current design can be iterated over.
This work focuses on solving some of the issues with the current anode design
to achieve higher capacity for the silicon based batteries. Using Reinforcement
Learning to design SiôÙnanostructures that account to the limitations of materials
such as silicon, we can achieve an improvement of up to x2.5 from previous works.
Another approach taken in this project to address the anode structure, is to deôÙ
sign an general tool that allows the automatic joining of graphene structures with
other allotropes, such as carbon nanotubes. This will provide future studies an
accelerated process of finding new materials.

1.1
Background
Energy is one of the foundations in which todayãs society is built, but due to the
extensive usage of fossil fuels used to generate this energy, we are facing a crisis
in our planet[1,2]. There has been a strong shift to green and renewable energies
to try to reduce the impact that our energy generation has on the environment.
From the switch of internal combustion engine vehicles to hybrids and electric
vehicles (EVs), to the switch of power generation using coal plants to renewable
alternatives such as hydropower plants, solar panels and wind turbines.
One of the shortcomings of some of the renewable energies, such as wind and
solar, is that the energy they produce fluctuates through the day. The electric grid
requires a stable and predictable power source in order to provide for all the indusôÙ
try and homes. Different technologies are needed that can fill this gap, overcoming
the limitation from renewable sources and finding a way to store the energy they
produce will lead to an easier adoption and a faster transition to green energies.
With the benefit that they can provide energy at a rate based on demand, instead
of waste the energy they can produce at peak hours by not being able to store
it, and then not being able to fulfill demand during the times they donãt generate
enough power[3].

Current energy storage technologies
Various technologies allow the storage of energy, and they can be categorised by
which process they use to store the energy[4]:
ãÂ Mechanical: It involves the use of pumps or compressors to move liquids

the most notable are pumped hydroelectric energy storage (PHES)[5], comôÙ

ãÂ Thermal: Utilising the thermal capacity and transfer capabilities that some
materials have, we take advantage of that process to store and retrieve enôÙ

gies used are sensible heat thermal[8], latent heat thermal[9], or cryogenic
thermal energy storage[10].
ãÂ Electrical: Making use of some of the properties electrical components
have, we store energy as an electric or magnetic fields. Devices such as
capacitors are candidates[11], as well as using magnetic fields using a suôÙ

ãÂ Chemical: Using energy to generate or convert an element, and then usôÙ

1.3
Batteries
Batteries nowadays can be divided into mainly 4 parts: electrolytes, anodes, cathôÙ
odes, and the interface (separator), as it can be observed in Figure 1.1. Batteries
work in the principle of generating energy due to the reaction between the anôÙ

connected through an electrolyte, though which only ionised particles pass. The
interface, also known as solid electrolyte interphase (SEI), is formed when the
battery is charged for the first time, when the ions contact with the anode mateôÙ
rial, and it prevents contact between the electrons and the electrolyte, but also
have an impact on the battery longevity. Batteries can be divided into two main
categories: Primary (or single use) batteries, in which the process of discharge
irreversibly changes the electrode material, and Secondary (or rechargeable batôÙ
tery), in which the battery can be recharged by applying a current into it.
We are mainly interested in rechargeable batteries, since their ability to be recharged
allows them to be used as a good energy storage system both for portable, e.g.
vehicles and smart devices, as well as static infrastructure such as using them to
provide stabilisation to the energy grid.

1.5.1
Supervised learning
Supervised learning (SL) performs actions based in an inputôÙoutput system, where
based on a given input, certain output is produced. SL relies on a preexisting
data set of inputôÙoutput pairs, known as the input set. The algorithm has the goal
of based on the input set, learn how to properly produce outputs that match the
expected output, so that when unseen data is introduced, it can predict the correct
answer from what it learnt. When building such a ML algorithm, the input set is
divided into train and test datasets. The training set is what the algorithm uses
to build its own function that uses to label unseen data[65]. The test data is used
to evaluate the model, if the same set would be used for training and evaluation,
once the training step is over the score would represent how good it can predict
the input set, not a general answer. This algorithm can be seen as an algorithm
that has a teacher telling it what is right and wrong. The process can be seen in
Figure 1.6. The main drawback with this approach is that all the data needs to
be labelled, usually by hand. It is mainly used when a categorisation of data is
needed, in order to predict unseen data.

1.5.2
Unsupervised learning
Unsupervised learning is similar to supervised learning, but with the main differôÙ
ent that it does not rely on previously labelled data, instead only using the raw
data without any labels. Based on the raw data, it builds its own internal model
based on what data it has seen previously[64]. The knowledge it builds is based
on repeating patterns in the input data, creating its own categories based on the
repeating features. Using this information, when it finds unseen data, it tries to
predict in which category fits based on the list of features it has previously built
from previous data. It is normally used to create clusters of data, where it can help
define which structure the dataset has.

2.1
Introduction
The lithiation of a material is the process of the material interacting with lithium, in
batteries this happens when a battery is charged. Using silicon as an anode was
previously discarded, mainly due to the extreme volume expansion the material
experiences during lithiation (300%), which makes it unstable[67], as well as a low
electronic conductivity. But due to the potential of materials such as silicon, having
theoretically 10 times the storage capacity of graphite, exploring possible solutions
to circumvent those limitations is worth the effort. Using engineered materials and
nanostructures is a possible solution to this problem. The Gravimetric Capacity
is the available energy per unit of mass of a material, it is used to determine how
energy dense a material is. The Coulombic efficiency determines how efficient a
system is in the charge and discharge cycle.

In the first section of this project, inspired by the work of Xia et al.[59], we implement

Xia and coôÙworkers have fabricated a SiôÙarchitecture composed of vertical posts

During lithiation, the beams buckle together forming an inôÙplane sinusoidal pattern
to accommodate the volume change. As a response to the buckling torque, the
poles (or nodes) rotate in opposite directions and the beam has the shape of a
halfôÙperiod sinus. Such a square pattern shows a capacity of 2010 mAh per g of
Si after 50 cycles at C/6[59]. The change in shape happens on the 2D plane and
the final electrode material is obtained by stacking 2D layers with the same layout,
which are connected by the vertical nodes. The RL model agent is designed to
optimise the placement of SiôÙbeams over an area in a virtual environment, in order
to find an optimised layout that maximises the theoretical storage capacity based
on the input constraints, e.g., lengths of the beams and angles formed by two
beams. The virtual environment is designed to include the main elements that
the previous experiment had, that is the beams and nodes, their position and also
checks to avoid collision between beams, both in a delithiated and lithiated states.

2.2
Methods
Basing the model on the structures generated in the paper[59], and on the previôÙ
ous work done by Felix Tim BûÑlle in this PD position, we aim to find an improved
configuration of Silicon nodes and beams by automating the process of creating
these structures based on given parameters. In order to automate the exploration
of new layouts, we will employ a branch of Machine Learning, known as ReinôÙ
forcement Learning (RL).
2.2.1
Reinforcement Learning
Reinforcement Learning is a branch of machine learning which focuses on how
agents perform actions in a given environment. The goal of a reinforcement learnôÙ
ing agent is to find the set of actions that lead to the highest reward at the end of
the training. Its main difference when compared to other machine learning algoôÙ
rithms, is that RL does not require a preset of labeled data, but it works more by
ãtrial and errorã. The main difficulty when designing such a system, is balancing
the exploration and exploitation, where the exploration aspect focuses on trying
new things, and the exploitation focuses on things it already knows. RL has been
successfully used to automate and accelerate the manual work of finding optimal
structures given certain constraints[61,62]. In a standard RL model, an agent interôÙ
acts with the environment in order to maximize a specified reward. The objective
of our agent is to maximise the placement of SiôÙnodes and SiôÙbeams that conôÙ
nect the nodes to create a pattern that changes shape upon lithiation (as shown
in Ref.[59]).
In RL literature, solving this problem usually can be solved with two approaches.
One involves placing the nodes, known as the node placement problem. It has
been successfully used previously in projects such as the design of the floorôÙ
planning of computer chips[68]. After placing the nodes, to connect them we need
to solve the node linkage problem, which has been successful in creating conôÙ
nections between users based on data from two different social networks[69]. The
second approach consists of placing the nodes and edges together using smaller
subgraph structures, e.g. single bonds[70] or larger molecular fragments[71]. In
this work we follow the latter approach. Below, we describe the design of the RL
model in detail.
2.2.2
Environment and Subgraphs
The environment is a simplified representation of the work done in[59], the previôÙ
ous project consists of Silicon pillars with beams connected between them that
bend upon lithiation, having multiple layers of the beams on top of each other,
connected by the pillars that work in parallel. In order to simplify, we consider a
single two dimensional canvas with nodes and beams that represents a layer of
the configuration, since the layout can later be replicated along the vertical pole.
The environment is modeled as an initially empty 2D canvas defined by its width
and height, where nodes and beams will be placed, as shown in Figure 2.3. Since
we are interested in finding a pattern that is repeatable along all directions for maxôÙ
imum volumetric capacity, the canvas has periodic boundary conditions (PBC).
This means that the cell is a representation of a theoretically infinite space, so

subgraphs that allow us to change the environment incrementally with small steps,
for that we have as our graph two nodes connected by a beam. An example list
of subgraphs used can be seen in figure 2.3.
Each subgraph represents an action that can be performed over a node. The paôÙ

2.2.3
Actions
An action consists on the application of a subgraph to a specific node, as illustrated
in figure 2.3. Starting from a selected node, the RL agent picks the next action
to take, among a list of actions available for the selected node, masking out the
actions that are illegal. An illegal action is defined as any action that results in two
beams, either lithiated or not, colliding, or a new node laying too close to already
placed nodes. Since the illegal actions are filtered out, this results in the agent
not learning from them, which has been to found to significantly improve the final
solutions, as shown in Figure 2.11.
In addition to these nonôÙfrustrated configurations, we allow to explore frustration,
i.e., beams that connect nodes rotating in the same direction, which upon lithiation
form beams shaped as fullôÙperiod sinuses. The difference between a frustrated
and a nonôÙfrustrated edge can be observed in Figure 2.4. Two processes have
been implemented to add such edges. This can be done both within the RL model
during training and as a postôÙprocessing after the structures have been optimised.
If using the flag to allow frustrations during training, when the agent is picking an
action, these frustrated edges are added as possible actions to explore when
there is a subgraph that connects two nodes with the same direction of rotation.
On the other hand, when using postôÙprocessing frustration, the training ignores
all possible frustrated edges. When the training is complete, using an the final
generated canvas, we find all nodes of the same directionality that are close to
each other and have no edge, and try to insert a frustrated edge in those positions,
checking for collision with other beams beforehand, this bypasses the requirement
of needed a subgraph for each connection, since it can connect freely any 2 nodes
regardless of angle/length.
2.2.4
Agent and Rewards
The goal of RL agent is to find a set of stateôÙaction pairs, intended as the pair
formed by a given state of the canvas and one action, that maximizes the total reôÙ
ward. The reward, öñ, is defined as the total beam length in the canvas, calculated
as the sum of all the beam lengths before lithiation, divided by the canvas area.
This value represents how much Si can theoretically be added for a given area.
We employ a version of reinforcement learning called QôÙlearning for training the
agent. QôÙlearning uses a table representing all the action and state permutations,
initially all values being, and updating the value for each combination as it trains,

as it can been seen in Figure 2.5. This makes the algorithm a modelôÙfree reinforceôÙ

environment response to pick actions. It follows the trialôÙandôÙerror approach to

The variant of QôÙlearning used in this project is Delayed QôÙlearning[77], which aims
at maximizing the reward by delaying any estimate until there is a statistically
significant sample of observations.
The actions taken by QôÙlearning are based on the Bellmann equation[72], which
defines the value of a state based on the current action and the future actions
available from that state. It uses a function Q to calculate the value of an action
given a state, using the output of the function as a determining factor for the quality
of the action. This allows the algorithm to judge how good of a state they are in,
taking into account the possible states that are available from the current one.
Based on this information, the algorithm can decide which actions to take based
on how good the current state is.
A limitation with QôÙlearning is that when there is a huge amount of possible actions,
representing so many values as a table, which is the default way QôÙlearning works,
is not feasible. In our simulation, we have a huge amount of possible positions for
the nodes, i.e. 2225 for a 2x2 canvas size. We use a neural network to generate
the output of the Q function (QôÙvalue) based on a given state and action. This
procedure turns the agent into what is called a Deep QôÙnetwork (DQN)[75] .

Using a neural network introduces instabilities to the QôÙvalue, since the output of
the neural network is nonôÙlinear, and is hard to converge into a value as well as
it is very computationally taxing[75]. To overcome this limitation, two variants are
introduced. Experience replay is introduced, the experience replay takes a sample
of previously taken actions and uses them to select where to proceed instead of
the most recent action, this introduces variation, which helps the network not get
stuck into a local minimum[78]. Another technique employed to avoid divergent
gradients is double QôÙlearning, which makes use of different policies for evaluating
and for selecting the next action[79,80].

indicated by one or zero, depending on whether the subgraph is used or not in
that state. For dealing with the explorationôÙexploitation problem, we employ the
üçôÙgreedy strategy[81], letting the agent take random actions with probability üç. We
slowly decrease the üç value throughout an episode following an exponential decay,
starting at 0 until it reaches a value of 0.1.

The features include a graph embedding, represented as a nodeôÙtoôÙvector repreôÙ

Table 2.1 shows the reward for different sets of angles. The highest reward is

Performance
The training time highly depends on the number of inputs introduced, as well as
the device used. The problem is CPU bound, when testing a large number of
inputs on an old laptop it proved itself very slow. The tests show that when havôÙ
ing over 10 input angles and multiple beam lengths, the training time could span
hours using an old CPU. Using more modern hardware, the training times were
more manageable, spanning from a few minutes to below an hour for large input
trainings. The number of episodes also played a large role on the training time,
since the more episodes, the longer the training will take, following a linear scale,
that is why finding parameters that allow the agent to converge into the best soôÙ
lution quickly is important. This, however, should be balanced, otherwise we risk
the agent getting stuck in a local minimum. Various optimisations were done such
as the masking of illegal actions mentioned in the previous section.
Most of the experiments were ran using 500 episodes, but the optimal solution
was found usually faster, except in few occasions. The number of episodes value
could be dynamically changed so that the number of inputs determine the amount
of episodes that are executed. The last experiments executed in the project were
using this feature, which lead to faster iterations of testing new parameters.

provide faster exploring of the space, since it can ignore shapes that are not posôÙ
sible to close, leading to faster training times. A possible implementation could be
having closed shapes as the input subgraphs, instead of two nodes and a beam,
this was not done due to the fact that it limits the agent exploration too much, but
other approaches could be pursued to achieve closed shapes.
It is possible that by using a different state representation, and a different RLôÙ
architecture, an agent that can find solutions with a less specific input data can
be found. Exploring the performance of different algorithms and network architecôÙ
tures could be the next step.
Since this program follows the premise of maximise the area given beam lengths
and angles accounting for bending of beams, we can expand its functionality beôÙ
yond silicon. Because of the openness of the methodology, we are not limited to
silicon, it can tweaked and used to find other kind of metamaterials. Any material
that requires to account for a large expansion on some condition can benefit from
this design, such as membranes. With minor modifications, different environment
shapes such as hexagons could be implemented, as well as different bending
amount and shapes.

3.1
Introduction
Graphite as an anode has a few limitations, such as only being able to store up
to one lithium ion for every six carbons, we can use a nanostructured material
based on carbon to overcome this limit. Nanostructured materials have successôÙ
fully managed to make materials display properties that are not present in their
natural form. Works such as the one in Poroelastic metamaterials[88] worked with
a baseline material that showed a positive compressibility when exposed to hyôÙ
drostatic pressure, they created a metastructure that behaves the opposite way,
displaying negative compressibility. This shows promise of designing a carbon
anode which is not limited to only being able to store one lithium ion for every
six carbons. Graphene as a material has been shown to have very remarkable
properties, such as great thermal conductivity, mechanical, optical and electrical
properties[89ã92], being able to retain those properties when expanding from two
to three dimensions can create a very desirable material. Using a nanostructured
graphene structure, we able to overcome the limit of one lithium ion for every
six atoms, it also has the ability to form a more stable interface. Nanostructured
graphene gives more control over the formation of the LiF layer in the SEI, since
the material in contact with the interface is designed. A pillared graphene has no
edges or defects, this affects the formation of the LiF layer, being able to change
the reduction reactions of the electrolyte and remove impurities.
Previous studies have already investigated the use of nanostructured graphene
as battery anodes, most notably the work done by Svane et al.[46], where they
used Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations to check how the formation
of the SEI layer happens in a nanostructured material built using graphene and
carbon nanotubes, as shown in Figure 3.2.
A problem with the manual creation of such structures is that given the parameters
(n and m) of a carbon nanotubes (CNT), selecting the position where the CNT will
be merged with the graphene sheet is a tedious and long process. This issue
is exacerbated when the n and m parameters are large, since there would be
more possible configurations for joining the two structures. So a way to automate
this process based on the input structure could accelerate the generation of such
structures.
Inspired by the tool created in the paper[93], and based on the methodology from
the paper[94], this section aims to create a general tool to join graphene sheets with
different carbon allotropes, such as fullerines or carbon nanotubes. The program
modifies the allotropes to form stable materials, based on the specified input paôÙ

Conclusion
Batteries are playing a key role in the transition to renewable energies. Artificially
designed nanostructures show a lot of promise to provide improvements in the
current rechargeable battery design. The use of materials like Si as an anode
was previously not possible, despite the remarkable properties it has, such as
being able to theoretically hold 10 times more charge than graphite. The main
reason it could not be used was due to the extreme expansion it experiences on
lithiation. With the new advanced and technologies, the design of metamaterials
can provide a solution to overcome such limitations of the material. By designing
a material at such a scale can give us the flexibility to account for behaviours of
the material such as the expansion of silicon. Using machine learning models can
greatly accelerate the process of finding optimal configurations. In this thesis we
have been able to find a pattern that improves upon the previously found material
by up to x2.5. With such promising results, it is worth exploring such options further
in order to find better materials that can lead to the improvement of devices such
as batteries.
On the other hand, carbonôÙbased structures have been already proven to be efôÙ
fective, but have certain known limitations. Using carbonôÙbased metamaterials
we can create carbonôÙbased materials that do not display such limitations, thus
allowing the creation of even better batteries. The use of tools like the one created
in this thesis to generate joint structures, such as graphene layered with carbon
nanotubes, can accelerate the finding of suitable materials to use as improved
carbon anodes. But this does not limit the tool to use in batteries, graphene has
been seen to have ãmagicã properties, and has been used in many other applicaôÙ
tions. The creation of this tool provides a faster way to iterate over carbonôÙbased
metamaterial structures in order to find the best suited to the given use case.
