Abstract
Dimensioning of mould safe interior insulation is what this project aim to address. Dimensioning shall here
be understood as the thickness of a speciﬁc insulation material from a product solution by a commercial
supplier. The product solution can be applied the wall without additional structure and is diﬀusion open.
The focus for application of the insulation is multi-storey building heritage which are subjects for refurbish-
ment and where exterior insulation is found infeasible. The building type can also be referred to as historic
buildings as these follow the prevailing craf tradition in Denmark between 1851 and 1920.
It is the objective of the project to ensure that the most energy saving refurbishment and still moisture safe
can be provided. For this additional methodology have been developed. The methodology suggest that to
provide a well supported advice one must use not only hydrothermal tools but ensure best possible: input
precision, model precision and integrate variability. Each of the three aims above have been separate aspects
- pillars of the project. This pillars can be followed through the discussion of theory, methodology, results
and discussion.
This is found as gap ﬁlling - adjustment of the measurements from experimental mock-up walls at DTU
the input precision got visual improved with continuous realistic data which meant model input ﬂuctuated
(during day) rather than being a interpolated line. On the contrary this visual improvement was not
consistently found to improve the models precision.
The models precision - validation was assessed requiring a deviation less that 5 % from measured. Of two
inspected sensors only the sensor nearest the surface could be concluded valid. Inaccuracy in modelling of
the thermal resistance and capacity accumulated making the sensor in the interface between insulation and
wall to deviate more.
To include variability a Python script, which also beneﬁted calibration of the hygrothermal model was set
up to generate a model portfolio by altering diﬀerent parameters and both simulate a evaluate the mould
index of the result. A comparison between a provided standard dimensioning of the wall and the alternative
dimensioning with use of average maximum mould index from model portfolio was set up. The comparison
showed a large diﬀerence in mould index evaluation where the standard (expected conservative) dimension
advised all insulation thickness to be safe, while the alternative found only 20 mm to be safe.
Keywords: interior insulation, hygrothermal modelling, mould index, massive masonry, mock-up experi-
ment

Objective
Interior insulation is applied to improve existing buildings thermal performance with a reduction of their
energy consumption for heating by increasing the external wall’s u-value which sensibly will be experienced
as an increased interior surface temperature. An increased surface temperature will also improve comfort
as thermal discomfort among other reasons can be caused by asymmetric radiation between an exterior

equivalent to the above thought the eﬀort to reduce energy consumption might be more modest which the
later formulation. This project aim to be continuously ambitious and challenge the propensity to rephrase
energy refurbishment to comfort refurbishment which is seen as a representation of a more modest reduce
in energy consumption.

2.1.1
Local averages
Regression-based gap-ﬁlling is used by Tardivo and Berti (2012) for daily temperature data measured in a
network of more than hundred meteorological stations. With additional surrounding stations these can be
used as regressors. The procedure is the following: (i) identify complete data before and afer a gap (target
station), (ii) identify possible reconstructing stations, before and afer the gap separately, (iii) select the data
- before and afer gap (target station), (iv) select reconstructing station providing the best correlation to
the data, (v) identify the period’s length for data coupling, minimising the reconstruction error, (vi) now
reconstruct. Sequence above is a condensation from list by Tardivo and Berti (2012, section 2.a).
With this procedure it is assumed that a period without gaps in the gap-period itself and in the period
before and afer exists among relevant reconstructing stations. If one gap period is as long as 213 time steps
when the measurement period is 5844 time steps as in the case above, the gap is equivalent to 3.6 % of the
total dataset and substitution following current method may not be possible.
In the measurements above the 5844 time steps present an average of missing data intervals about thirteen
and time steps with missing values about ﬁfy-three. Average is among a total of 114 stations and a gap
length of 213 time steps is an exception as Tardivo and Berti (2012) report 98 % of gaps to be less than twenty
time steps.
Without a network of stations and thereby a pool of use full data Pappas (2014) investigates eﬃcient gap-
ﬁlling for hydrometeorological time series and elaborate that data sets without gaps are oﬀend needed to
complete analysis. In addition to this Pappas (2014) also mention a widespread amount of methods stating

2.2
Energy refurbishment
In this section is presented the construction, measures and requirements to which an energy refurbishment
must be customised to. Historic buildings as those erected 1851 to 1930 was very much prior to any energy
requirements for the building envelope, which was not practiced until 1977 where oil crises motivated an
improvement Rasmussen (2011) so energy improvements are appropriate.
2.2.1
Historic building technology
The knowledge of building methods, the accessible materials, and legislation at the time is all factors that each
play a certain role to why masonry buildings from 1851 to 1920 so conveniently can be treated as one identity
(Byggeforskningsinstitut, 1900). Diﬀerent from present not much commonly acknowledged statically
theory was available thus procedures for designing was to oblige with prescriptive, rather than present
performance, requirements. Prescriptive requirements was declared by the authority of the current area why
some variation exists but within the same framework (Byggeforskningsinstitut, 1900) The requirements

3.3.1.3
Moisture excess
Indoor activities such as cocking, cleaning and generally process that include water but also occupants who
have a metabolism - also pets - all release moisture to the indoor air. The amount of moisture released can
diﬀer widely, some occupants follow recommendations such as dry cloths outside, cook with lid and shower
with a closed door this are measures that reduce the total moisture release, denoted G (kg/h) to indoor
air. The phase moisture excess refer not to the moisture release (production) directly but to the balance
between release and removal of moist air given by G/(nV )
above n is air changes per hour (h−1) while V is the volume (m3) which is changed, equivalent to the room
or building in focus and which imply the unit of moisture excess to be kg/m3 in practice g/m3 is used as
one cubic meter of air does not carry kilos of water.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
This chapter recap the ﬁnding and discussion in each of the project pillars introduced in
ﬁgure 1.3.1 and conclude if the three hypotheses aspects were either approved of falsiﬁed.
Input precision was improved with gap ﬁlling by alternative measurements from a set-up with almost
identical conditions and equipment. The adjusted input parallel with the reference input were simulated
and temperature results in sensor P1 and P3, showing the eﬀect of adjustment versus reference, where
evaluated. Based on two periods with much occurrence of gaps and with at total length of 40 days out the
640 days studied it was found that a too large deviation between measurements and results in areas without
gaps, equivalent to a not too (valid) precise model, made adjustment irrelevant. In the meantime areas with
longer time spans showed much more representative results, such that results before gap, in gap and afer
gap were continuous.
But the ﬁrst hypothesis with a 5 % better alignment to measurements by adjustment was not generally
provided and a one day improved average was as easily identiﬁed as a one day worsened average, due to
lack of model precision.
Above the precision of the model was questioned, without indication of the actual deviation which was
hereafer also investigated with a visual ± 5 % margin to the measured. The modelled temperature of sensor
P1 near the exterior was found within the margins thus valid and imply appropriate boundary condition
implementation while the P3 sensor was slightly outside the margins and right shifed which could be
caused by too high heat capacity or thermal resistance of the modelled brickwork which again suggest to
investigate the eﬀect of the abstract modelling of the brickwork as homogeneous brick material.
The hygrothermal model failed to mimic the relative humidity measurements and deviation is found between
10 and 20 percent point relative humidity for as long as three month for the P1 sensor and continuously
for the P3 sensor. Again the P3 sensor, for which inaccuracy can accumulate thought the mock-up wall,
deviate from measurements the most. Eﬀects of simultaneously temperature deviation is not quantiﬁed but
to validate a hygrothermal model based on temperature and moisture content instead is recommended.
Due the magnitude of deviation in relative humidity investigation of the inaccuracy of measurements and

hereby eﬀects, wind driven rain models and smaller time interval for rain and wind data is suggested.
Finally a comparison between a provided standard dimensioning of the modelled wall and the alternative
dimensioning with use of average maximum mould index from model portfolio was set up. The comparison
showed a large diﬀerence in mould index evaluation where the standard dimension advised all insulation
thickness to be safe, while the alternative found only 20 mm to be safe.
With the complexity of the two compared methods it has not been possible to provide one line of issues

The project has presented several methodological steps to provide well supported advices on interior
insulation thickness, but not succeed in providing a consistent advice as more precision in thermal models
are still needed. The data amount generated and handled in such set ups calls for more detailed methods
and procedural documentation to keep an overview.

0BPreface 
In the period 2013-2014 the project “Upgrade and Extension of the Climate Station at DTU Byg” is 
carried out at DTU Byg. The aim of the project is to renew the hardware and the software for data 
acquisition and monitoring, exchange cables and cable connections in order to avoid interference of 
electrical noise from the surroundings and exchange worn out equipment. Further, the aim is to 
make measured data from the climate station easily available for the users. 
The project is financed by the Bjarne Saxhof foundation.

2. 
1B ackground and objective of the project 
Detailed measurements of weather data are used to study the weather and develop solar radiation 
processing models that are needed in order to simulate the energy consumption of buildings, the 
indoor climate, the thermal performance of solar heating systems and the performance of PV 
systems. The weather varies from one year to another. Therefore design reference years based on 
minimum 10 years of measured weather data are used in simulations. In order to work out reliable 
design reference years, it is needed to know the short term variations in the solar radiation. Hourly 
values of solar radiation are not always sufficient because of the short time constant of e.g. PV 
panels. On this background, a solar radiation measurement station was established on the roof of 
building 119 at DTU Byg. The station was put in operation in 1989 and has been in operation ever 
since [1], see Figure 1.  
The measurements are unique because of the very detailed measurements of the solar radiation, 
especially the diffuse solar radiation and because of the long measurement period of about 25 years.

maintenance, operation and service of users has been very time consuming.  
 
The objective of this project is to renovate and upgrade the Climate station to the standard of today 
in order to minimize the work load related to operating the Climate station and providing service to 
the users.
