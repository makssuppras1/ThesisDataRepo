Abstract
Prediction of neoepitopes is a crucial step in the design of personalized therapies against
cancer like cancer vaccines. MuPeXI is a program designed to extract and inform peptides
from patient tumor data, including tools like NetMHCII so the obtained peptides are known
to be compatible to patient’s MHC class 1 and thus elicit an immone response. Inputs
required for this tool include a variant call format file(VCF), HLA typing and optionally an
expression file. Currently MuPeXI only uses somatic mutations compared to reference
genome to extract the peptides, but in this project germline mutations are added to the
pipeline to asses its impact in the amount and quality of the peptides extracted. To do
so an extra VCF was generated with HaplotypeCaller and merged to the original VCF
containing somatic mutations, and allele phasing was applied to the variants. Filtering
options were added to the program to only select those germline mutations that could be
part of a presented peptide, and the updated pipeline was run for all samples. Results
Show a big overlap in peptides with a higher binding affinity and eluted ligand scores,
indicating that there is not a significant changes in the peptide prediction when adding
germline mutations.

Introduction
In recent years, personalized treatments for cancer have been gaining popularity over
more traditional treatments, which tend to be more invasive[1]. This shift has been fa-
cilitated in part by the increased availability of data, along with the development of new
bioinformatic tools.
Some of these new approaches in cancer treatments heavily rely on identifying neoepi-
topes, which are specific regions of proteins that have been altered due to mutations[2].
These mutated protein regions are newly formed and can be recognized as different from
normal proteins by the immune system.
Cancer vaccines are a promising treatment that leverages neoepitopes[3]. In order to be
targeted by the immune system, one of the strategies of this therapy consist in selecting
peptides derived from these neoepitopes. These peptides are usually 9mers, but can
range up to 11mers. This is because, to be presented to the immune system, these
peptides need to bind to MHC class I molecules.
1.1
Importance of variant calling and neoepitopes
Neoepitopes play a critical role in the immune system’s ability to detect and respond to
cancer cells[4]. One of the immune system’s primary mechanisms for recognizing and
attacking cancer cells involves the presentation of neoepitopes on the cell surface. When
displayed on MHC class I molecules, neoepitopes can be recognized by CD8+ T-cells, a
type of immune cell capable of inducing the death of cancer cells. This selective recogni-
tion is important because it allows the immune system to target cancer cells while leaving
healthy cells unharmed.
In this context, calling somatic variants for each individual is a vital step in the data analysis
required for any personalised treatment. The Genome Analysis Toolkit (GATK) is a soft-
ware package developed by the Broad Institute for analyzing high-throughput sequencing
data which is widely used in several bioinformatic pipelines and it includes robust tools for
both data processing and variant calling[5].
A inside GATK that allows us to extract variants derived from cancer with the use of both
normal tissue samples and tumor samples from a given patient, is Mutect2[6]. In case
the scope of the analysis includes searching for germline variants, Haplotypecaller is an
excellent tool also from GATK that allows the extraction of germline variants from a given
patient data[7]. Both tools can use WES or WGS data depending on the needs of the
study.
One of the most common and useful ways to store variant information is with Variant Call
Format files (VCF), which store DNA annotated polymorphism data, and while several
VCF versions exists depending on the tool used to generate them, all of them contain a
comprehensive compilation of all the variants from a given sequencing data sample[8].

1.2
The peptide-MHC complex
To enable the immune system to recognize and respond to cancer cells, peptides derived
from neoepitopes must be presented on the cell surface in a form that immune cells can
detect. This process involves binding these peptides to MHC class I molecules, which
then form a stable peptide-MHC complex. Once bound, this complex is transported to the
cell surface, where it can be recognized by immune cells, especially CD8+ T-cells. The
interaction between the peptide-MHC complex and T-cell receptors is a key trigger for the
immune response against cells displaying these neoepitopes[9].
MHC molecules themselves are highly variable across populations and even between
individuals. This diversity in MHC, especially within the class I region known as HLA
(human leukocyte antigen), means that each individual may present a unique set of pep-
tides[9]. As a result, accurate neoepitope prediction requires knowledge of the specific
MHC molecules present in an individual. By identifying a person’s unique HLA type, re-
searchers can better predict which neoepitopes are likely to be presented and thus tar-
geted by the immune system.
Advances in HLA typing tools, like OptiType, now allow us to determine an individual’s HLA
alleles with high accuracy[10]. This information is essential for developing personalised
cancer vaccines, as it provides insights into which neoepitopes are most likely to bind
to the patient’s MHC molecules and, therefore, which peptides are suitable targets for
immunotherapy.
1.3
Peptide Binding and Presentation Prediction
Once potential neoepitopes have been identified, the next step is to predict whether these
peptides will effectively bind to MHC molecules and be presented on the cell surface,
where they can be recognized by immune cells. Accurately predicting peptide-MHC bind-
ing is essential for selecting neoepitopes that are most likely to trigger an immune re-
sponse, as only those that form stable complexes with MHC molecules have a high like-
lihood of presentation[11].
One of the primary tools used for this purpose is NetMHCpan, a widely utilized bioin-
formatics tool designed to predict both the binding affinity of peptides to MHC class I
molecules and their subsequent presentation on the cell surface. NetMHCpan uses ma-
chine learning models trained on large datasets of experimentally measured binding affini-
ties and presentation outcomes[12]. This dual prediction—binding affinity and presenta-
tion likelihood—makes NetMHCpan a valuable resource for identifying the most promising
neoepitope candidates for immunotherapy.
By prioritizing peptides with strong binding affinity and high presentation scores, NetMHC
helps researchers focus on those neoepitopes that are most likely to be displayed by
MHC molecules in vivo. This step is critical in personalized cancer vaccine design, as it
increases the chances that selected peptides will engage immune cells effectively.

1.4
MuPeXI
Extracting peptides around somatic mutations is a keystone step in the detection of tu-
moral neoepitope candidates.
The Mutant Peptide Extractor and Informer(MuPeXI) is a specialized bioinformatics tool
designed to identify and characterize neoepitopes derived from somatic mutations in can-
cer. Developed specifically to aid in cancer immunotherapy, MuPeXI focuses on the ex-
traction of peptides containing mutations and evaluates their potential to be presented by
MHC class I molecules[2]. This capability is crucial for neoepitope-based cancer vaccine
development, where the goal is to select peptides that are both immunogenic and specific
to tumor cells.
Several functions inside the program allow to annotate and manipulate each mutation,
allowing to extract peptides both from In-frame and frame-shift variants.
In order to accurately inform about the selected peptides MuPeXI incorporates HLA typing
data and optionally expression data, and uses NetMHCpan to asses both binding affinity
an eluted ligand data from the selected peptides.

Objectives
The objective of this project is to assess the impact of considering patient germline mu-
tations in peptide extraction by implementing the necessary changes in data processing
in MuPeXI, and by modifying its functionalities to handle and annotate this new formatted
data.
The basic MuPeXi version and around half of the currently existing neopeptide-predicting
software (pVACtools, TIminer) extract peptides by looking for somatic mutations and ex-
tracting peptides around them from the reference genome, thus ignoring any nearby
germline that could potentially influence in its binding to the MHC and consequent pre-
sentation.

Materials and methods
Whole exome sequencing data from bladder tumor tissue and normal tissue, as well as
gene expression data from 72 patients was used. This data was previously used in an-
other project that ran MuPeXI, allowing for comparison and assessment of possible im-
provements.
3.1
Input VCF modification
While in the regular MuPeXI pipeline the main input is a VCF file generated with Mutect or
Mutect2, in order to add germline mutations HaplotypeCaller was implemented to obtain
another VCF containing all the germline mutations from the normal samples. Further
modifications to those VCF allowed to obtain a merged file with candidate variants as a
new input for the pipeline.

NGS data QC and alignment
TrimGalore (version 0.4.0) was used to remove adapters from the reads (paired end),
removing reads with a phred score smaller than 20 and a length shorter than 50. Quality
of the newly trimmed reads was assesed with FastQC (version 0.11.2).
For read alignment, BWA MEM (version 0.7.15) was used to align paired-end reads to the
reference genome, with the -M flag enabled for marking shorter split hits as secondary
alignments. Following alignment, the resulting BAM files were sorted using Samtools
(version 1.6) to ensure proper read ordering, and Picard (version 2.9.1) was employed to
mark duplicate reads based on identical mapping positions, with duplicates flagged but
retained for downstream analysis.
The alignment was followed by base quality score recalibration using GATK (version
4.0.1.1). This step involved generating recalibration tables using known variant sites and
applying recalibrated base quality scores to the BAM files. Mosdepth (version 0.3.3) was
then used to calculate coverage depth across the targeted regions. Finally, recalibration
plots were generated using GATK to visualize the accuracy of the recalibrated base quality
scores.
HLA typing
Fastq files were aligned to HLA reference files with razers3 (version 3.4.0) to generate
BAM alignment files that were converted into SAM file with samtools (version 1.2). HLA
typing was performed using OptiType (version 1.3.4) on all the patient SAM files.
Variant calling
VCF (Variant call Format) files were generated for both somatic and germline variants from
all the samples. Mutect2 was used to obtain the VCF file containing somatic mutations
derived from cancer. These VCF where where filtered using VariantFiltration tool from
GATK to remove variants with allele frequency smaller than 0.5.
For germline mutations HaplotypeCaller from GATK was used to generate the VCF files
from normal samples containing germline mutations of each patient. Following variant
calling, GATK VariantFiltration was applied to filter variants based on specific criteria.
Variants with a read depth (DP) of less than 10 were marked as ’LowDepth’, and those
with a quality by depth (QD) score below 2.0 were marked as ’LowQualityByDepth’. Ad-
ditionally, variants occurring in clusters of 3 or more within a 15 base pair window were
flagged to reduce the impact of localized sequencing errors. Finally those flagged variants
were removed from the germline VCFs with GATK SelectVariants.
Allele phasing
HapCUT2 version 1.3.4 was used to perform allele phasing on the VCF merged files.
This process is a two-step process. First the ExtractHAIRS function from HapCUT2 is run
on the merged VCF with the alignment bam file to generate a fragments file, containing

3.2
MuPeXI modification
Several modifications in the MuPeXI code were made to interpret, process and annotate
the new input. MuPeXI first generates a file containing information about the type of
mutation for each variant in the VCF (miss sense, insertion, deletion...) using the variant
effect predictor (VEP) tool from Ensembl. Here a modification was made so it could detect
previously annotated variants as germline, so in the resulting VEP file those were still
annotated as germline.
MuPeXI includes too functionality to create superpeptides around a given variant from the
reference genome, and in this case changes were made so it generated those peptides
only around somatic variants, but also modifying any near position that included a germline
mutation.
Annotation of this newly formed peptides now included information about the presence of
a germline mutation in it and also its respective position in the peptide.
For this purpose, germline mutation inclusion needed different functionalities depend-
ing wether it was a missense mutation, an in-frame insertion, or an in-frame deletion.
Frameshift germline mutations were ignored and not applied in this case, since it added
extra complexity for just some exceptional cases and due to the time constraints in the
project.

Results
Processing the germline variant call files resulted in the removal of many of the variants.
First, allele phasing reduced considerably the amount of available variants to analyze.
Additional filtering of phased germline variants from the VCF files, including those not
proximal enough to a somatic mutation, that is, further than 33 bp in both directions, and
also removing those that where not in the same chromosomal copy resulten in even a
biggest reduction of the number of variants(see appendix).

Discussion
In this project, functionality improvements were made to MuPeXI, a software licensed by
DTU and regularly used in its lab for cancer epitope screening. Originally, MuPeXI couldn’t
include patient-specific germline variations, which might affect the results of neoepitope
predictions. To solve this, I made significant changes to both the WES data processing
steps and the MuPeXI code, allowing it to now take germline variants into account.
We then went back and reanalyzed previous screenings that didn’t use germline data to
see if adding this information made a difference. Our results showed a 90% overlap in
EL rank between the old and new screenings, meaning that while adding germline in-
formation makes MuPeXI more comprehensive, the previous screenings were still fairly
accurate. Overall, these improvements make MuPeXI more useful for future cancer epi-
tope screenings, supporting more precise and personalized cancer vaccine development.
Further improvements in germline mutation implementation, like considering frame-shift
mutations, and comparing with more data could reveal more insights on the role of germline
mutations in peptide predictions.
