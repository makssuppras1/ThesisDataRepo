Abstract 
Objectives: The emergence and rapid spread of carbapenem resistance, mainly among Gram-
negative bacteria is a major global public-healthcare problem. The rationale for this study is to 
design lineage-specific PCR primers for the Enterobacteriales species Citrobacter freundii with the 
sequence type ST18 for rapid identification of environmental samples containing this lineage. C. 
freundii ST18 carrying the carbapenemase gene blaNDM-1 has caused one of the largest outbreaks in 
Denmark and has been associated with colonization of the drainage system at the affected 
hospitals. The idea is to develop a fast PCR method to pin-point blaNDM-1 positive C. freundii ST18 
from drain samples.  
Methods: The dataset consisted of 146 target genomes of C. freundii ST18, where 128 genomes 
were from SSI and 18 genomes were from NCBI. The non-target dataset consisted of 590 genomes 
of other MLST types of C. freundii, where 126 genomes were from SSI and 464 genomes were from 
NCBI. The dataset was used in ssGeneFinder to detect unique targets in C. freundii ST18. The 
unique targets from ssGeneFinder were further investigated by BLAST based in silico methods to 
find a suitable (stable) unique target for primer design for the PCR. The designed primers were 
used in vitro along with a set of specific primers directed against blaNDM-1 to test on known isolates 
and samples from community and hospital environment to detect the specific hospital outbreak 
ST18 strain carrying blaNDM-1. Phylogenetic analysis of the five C. freundii ST18 identified hospital 
environment isolates (HES1/AMA004448 (toilet), HES5/AMA004487 (toilet), HES7/AMA004449 
(toilet), HES9/AMA004450 (toilet), and HES12/AMA004451 (sink)) was investigated in relation to 
the specific hospital outbreak ST18 strain carrying blaNDM-1. Possible blaNDM-1 gene encoded 
plasmid replicon were identified using the IncC – replicon type plasmid pT1 complete plasmid (and 
its derivative pT2b) sequence carrying the blaNDM-1 gene and originating from the C. freundii ST18 
outbreak strain. 
Results: Two primer sets (primer set 1 and primer set 2) were designed based on unique target 
usid000005 where primer set 1 was used for PCR with blaNDM-1 primer set on known isolates and 
unknown samples from community and hospital environment. The PCR for known isolates showed 
results as expected, which verified that primer set 1 along with the blaNDM-1 primer set work well 
together in the multiplex PCR. These primers were used on unknown samples from community 
and hospital environment. The PCR for community environment samples (CES1-26) did not identify 
==PAGE:6==
 
5 
C. freundii ST18 nor blaNDM-1. However, three samples from the hospital environment at Aalborg 
University Hospital (HES5/AMA004487 (toilet), HES7/AMA004449 (toilet), and HES12/AMA004451 
(sink)) were identified as blaNDM-1 producing C. freundii ST18 and two other samples 
(HES1/AMA004448 (toilet), and HES9/AMA004450 (toilet)) as C. freundii ST18. Isolates 
HES7/AMA004449 (toilet), and HES12/AMA004451 (sink) were close related to isolate AMA03588 
from a patient with travel history to Greece and who had previously been hospitalized at Aalborg 
University Hospital. Isolate HES5/AMA004487 (toilet) was related to the ongoing outbreak in 
Denmark since it was identified as having the IncC –replicon type plasmid pT2b, which is also 
found frequently in some of the isolates from the ST18 outbreak.   
Conclusion: From this study it can be concluded that the NDM-1 producing C. freundii ST18 are still 
found in the hospital environment at Aalborg University Hospital, where the main reservoir are the 
toilets. Isolate HES5/AMA004487 (toilet) was the only isolate, which was related to the ongoing 
outbreak in Denmark since it clustered with all the other isolates from the ST18 outbreak and was 
identified as having the IncC –replicon type plasmid pT2b, which is also found frequently in some 
of the isolates from the ST18 outbreak. It is still unclear how isolates HES7/AMA004449 (toilet) 
and HES12/AMA004451 (sink), were epidemiologically related to isolate AMA03588 from a Greek 
patient, which is not genetically linked to the local ST18 blaNDM-1 outbreak.

1 Introduction  
 
1.1 Antibiotics 
The discovery and production of antibiotics have been one of medicine’s greatest achievements. 
The use of antibiotics has reduced morbidity and mortality of humans and contributed to 
increased life span [1]. Antibiotics are drugs that are used to fight infections caused by bacteria in 
humans by either killing the bacteria or making it difficult to grow and multiply [2]. Antibiotics can 
be divided into numerous different classes based on their mechanisms of action. The main groups 
are; antibiotics that inhibit the cell wall synthesis, depolarize the cell membrane, inhibit protein 
synthesis, inhibit nucleic acid synthesis, and inhibit metabolic pathways (Figure 1.1) [3].  
 
 
Figure 1.1: Antibiotic groups based on their mechanism of action [3].  
==PAGE:11==

1.1.1 𝛽-lactam antibiotics  
The first antibiotic discovered was a 𝛽-lactam, i.e., penicillin, by the Scottish scientist Alexander 
Flemming (Flemming, 1929) [1]. He accidentally noticed the production of a substance with 
antimicrobial properties by the mould Penicillum notatum. After that many new 𝛽-lactam 
antibiotics have been developed [1]. All 𝛽-lactam antibiotics are defined as having a common four-
sided 𝛽-lactam ring and act similarly by inhibiting the bacterial cell wall by binding to and 
inactivating the penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs), which are responsible for the formation of 
bacterial cell wall. Consequently, it weakens the cell wall of the bacterium resulting in cytolysis or 
death. 𝛽-lactams are the most used antibiotics worldwide, which include the penicillins and 
derivates, cephalosporins, monobactams and carbapenems [3].  
 
1.1.2 Carbapenems  
Carbapenems are the most effective antibiotic against Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria 
and therefore have a broad spectrum of antibacterial activity 
because of their unique molecular structure, which is the 
presence of a carbapenem together with the 𝛽-lactam ring 
(Figure 1.2) [4]. Carbapenems are considered as the most 
reliable last-resort treatment for bacterial infections [5]. Even 
though there are many different antibiotics that have different 
mechanisms of action, bacteria have, over time, become 
resistant against them all [3].  
 
1.2 Antibiotic resistance  
Antibiotic resistance is a natural phenomenon that occurs when bacteria are treated with 
antibiotic drugs [6]. Antibiotic resistance is a growing public health concern worldwide. The 
problem of antibiotic resistance is associated with high morbidity and mortality. Bacteria have 
become resistant to antibiotics through several mechanisms that fall into four main categories: (1) 
limiting uptake of a drug, (2) modifying a drug target, (3) inactivating a drug, (4) active drug efflux. 
Since Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria have differences in structure, etc., there is a 
variation in the types of mechanisms used by the bacteria [3].   
Figure 1.2: Carbapenem backbone structure [4]. 
==PAGE:12==
 
11
Resistance to 𝛽-lactam antibiotics occurs through three general mechanisms: () preventing the 
interaction between the target PBPs and the antibiotic, which is usually by modifying the ability of 
the antibiotic to bind to the PBPs; () the presence of efflux pumps that can extrude 𝛽-lactam 
antibiotics; () hydrolysis of the drug by 𝛽-lactamase enzymes [3]. Bacteria can exhibit resistance 
to antibiotics either intrinsically or acquired [6]. Intrinsic resistance is natural resistance that is 
always expressed in the species or induced, which means that the genes are naturally occurring in 
the bacteria and are only expressed to resistance levels after exposure to an antibiotic. Acquired 
resistance occurs either by spontaneous mutations in chromosomally located genes or from gene 
exchange through horizontal gene transfer. Horizontal gene transfer is often responsible for 
developing antibiotic resistance [1].  
 
1.2.1 Horizontal Gene Transfer  
The bacteria can carry genes for resistance, which can easily be 
transferred to other organisms thereby spreading the resistance gene. 
Horizontal gene transfer is the process that includes the spread of 
antibiotic resistance genes among bacteria [7]. There are three 
mechanisms of horizontal transmission in bacteria: transformation, 
conjugation, and transduction (Figure 1.3) [7].  
Transformation is a process where the bacteria take up foreign genetic 
material (naked DNA) from the environment. Transduction is the 
process where bacterial DNA is transferred to another bacterium by a 
virus particle called bacteriophage. Conjugation is the process where 
one bacterium transfer genes to another bacterium by direct cell-to-
cell contact [7]. Plasmid-mediated transmission of resistance genes is 
the most common form of conjugation. The plasmid can either be a 
conjugative- or non-conjugative plasmid. Conjugative plasmids are plasmids that can transfer 
themselves since they have an origin of transfer region. Non-conjugative plasmids are plasmids 
that cannot be transferred between bacteria by themselves because they do not encode the 
necessary genes required for them to be transferred. Non-conjugative plasmids may be mobilized 
by other plasmids if they still carry an origin of transfer region [8].

1.2.2 Plasmids  
Plasmids have been classified based on incompatibility since 1970. Incompatibility (Inc) is defined 
as the inability of plasmids to share similar replication and partition systems to be spread stably in 
the same host cell line. Inc groups have been independently classified in three different genera: 27 
Inc groups in Enterobacteriaceae, 14 Inc groups of Pseudomonas, and 18 Inc groups in 
Staphylococcus. Classification into Inc groups is based on the amino acid sequence of the 
replication initiation protein [9]. Plasmids of the incompatibility group A/C (IncA/C) were among 
the earliest plasmids associated with antibiotic resistance in Gram-negative bacteria and have 
been known for more than 40 years. They are large, broad-host-range plasmids with varying 
capacities for conjugation [10].  
 
The combination of plasmid-mediated resistance with chromosomally encoded resistance 
mechanism of multiple drug classes results in strains that are resistant to all the main classes of 
commonly used antibiotics [1]. Horizontal transfer of plasmids carrying carbapenemase genes 
plays an important role in the spread of multidrug-resistant bacteria. The emergence and rapid 
spread of carbapenem resistance, mainly among Gram-negative bacteria is a major global public-
healthcare problem [11].  
 
1.3 Transferable carbapenem resistance  
There are many types of carbapenemases, which are the main mechanism for giving resistance 
towards carbapenems, among which KPC, OXA-48, and NDM-1 are the three major enzyme groups 
found in the Enterobacteriaceae (termed Carbapenemase-Producing Enterobacterales; CPE) 
worldwide [12]. Among the four classes of 𝛽-lactamases defined by the Ambler classification 
system, the carbapenemases that confer carbapenem resistance in Enterobacteriaceae belong to 
three of them: Class A, Class B and Class D. The enzyme KPC belongs to class A, NDM-1 belongs to 
class B, and OXA-48 belongs to class D (Figure 1.4) [12]. The genes encoding the KPC, OXA-48, and 
NDM-1 carbapenemases are called blaKPC, blaOXA-48, and blaNDM-1, respectively. The genes are 
commonly found individually in carbapenemase resistant Enterobacteriaceae (CRE) and are often 
transmitted between organisms by mobile genetic elements, such as plasmids, which contribute 
==PAGE:14==
 
13
to their spread [13]. Bacterial isolates harbouring carbapenemases are often resistant to multiple 
antibiotic classes, which results in limited therapy options [11].  
 
Figure 1.4: Classification of carbapenemases produced by CRE, based on the Ambler classification [12].  
 
1.3.1 KPC 
The carbapenemases of the K. pneumoniae carbapenemase family (KPC) have the most 
widespread global distribution of all carbapenemases associated with Enterobacteriaceae [14]. 
The first KPC-producing CPE in the United States was isolated from a patient in North Carolina 
[15]. Since then, a large number of cases of KPC-producing CPE were reported from New York City 
area hospitals [16]. In Europe, the highest incidence of KPC-producing CPE is found especially in 
Italy and Greece [17].      
 
1.3.2 OXA-48 
Oxacillinase (OXA-48-like) are relative commonly found in Europe, especially in Mediterranean 
countries [18]. The first reported OXA-48 producing Enterobacteriaceae was a K. pneumoniae 
strain, isolated in Turkey in 2001 [19]. OXA-48-like enzymes in Enterobacteriaceae have been 
found worldwide, which include the Middle East, Africa and South America [14].  
 
1.3.3 NDM 
New Delhi metallo-β-lactamase 1 (NDM-1), a metallo- β-lactamase (MBL), was first reported in 
2008 in Klebsiella pneumoniae and Escherichia coli isolated from a patient in Sweden, who had 
received medical care in New Delhi, India [20]. Afterwards, a follow-up study showed that NDM-1-
==PAGE:15==
 
14
mediated carbapenem resistance was widespread in India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh [21]. Since 
then, the blaNDM-1-positive Enterobacteriaceae have become increasingly common in India and has 
rapidly dispersed to various Gram-negative pathogenic species worldwide e.g., in Europe, they are 
commonly found in Romania, Poland and Denmark [14], [22].  
 
1.3.4 CPE cases in Denmark  
The emergence of CPE has been reported in Denmark since around 2008 where most initial cases 
were related to travelling abroad [23]. Since then, there has been an increasing occurrence of CPE 
in Denmark with more and more findings being caused by local and regional outbreaks. Figure 1.5 
shows the number of CPE isolates since 2008, where the occurrence of CPE continued to increase 
throughout 2020 [23].  
 
 
Figure 1.5: Numbers of CPE isolates, Denmark, 2008-2020 [23]. 
 
1.3.5 Healthcare-associated CPE  
CPE are often associated with outbreaks based on healthcare-associated infections, which are 
difficult to treat since they are resistance to multiple antibiotics. CPE outbreaks have increasingly 
been reported worldwide [14]. In Finland, the first carbapenemase-producing C. freundii was 
detected in 2015 and since then, they have detected three clusters of carbapenemase-producing 
C. freundii during 2016-2020, where the cases were different health care facilities [24]. The 
==PAGE:16==
 
15
majority of the carbapenemase-producing C. freundii had the sequence type ST18. The 
epidemiological link was a stay at the same ward with previously detected CPE case, however, 
they suspected that environmental contamination may have played a role, e.g., in one of the 
clusters the link between the cases was a common room, without overlapping stay and a long-
time interval between the cases [24]. Studies have identified that hospital water environment is a 
potential nosocomial source for CPE where sink, toilets and drains were a possible reservoir for 
CPE [25]. Smismans et al. were the first to describe the detection of OXA-48- producing C. freundii 
in toilets in hospital rooms that shared a common waste plumbing, therefrom transmitted to four 
patients in one ward [26]. Another study from USA in 2017, investigated whether CPE can spread 
between toilets through drain water and therefrom transmit patients in hospital. They 
experienced an outbreak of OXA-48-producing K. pneumoniae, which affected five patients who 
stayed in three different rooms. OXA-48-producing K. pneumoniae was detected in toilet water in 
four out of six rooms and drain water between two rooms, which suggested that the strain may 
have spread between rooms by drain water [27]. 
 
1.4 Hospital outbreaks with blaNDM-1 
The spread of NDM-1 producing Enterobacteriaceae in hospitals, where different bacteria carry 
the blaNDM-1 gene, has been investigated in many countries. An outbreak of NDM-1-producing K. 
pneumoniae occurred in an intensive care unit (ICU) in France in 2021, where twelve patients 
tested positive: seven remained asymptomatic whereas five developed an infection. The study 
demonstrated that all strains were related clonally [28].  
The spread of NDM-1 producing Enterobacteriaceae in hospitals has also been investigated in 
Denmark. A study from October 2012 to March 2015, showed 45 isolates of Enterobacteriaceae, 
which were detected in seven patients at the Haematology Department at Aalborg University 
Hospital, Aalborg, Denmark. Thirteen highly clonal ST18 NDM-1 producing C. freundii were isolated 
from the patients [22]. These ST18 NDM-1 producing C. freundii were multiresistant and only 
susceptible to tetracycline, tigecycline, colistin and fosfomycin [22].  
Several reports have shown that the blaNDM-1 gene is often carried on IncC plasmids [29].  
The study from Denmark has also investigated the spread of plasmid mediated resistance genes to 
other Enterobacteriaceae by in vitro conjugation where a 154 kb IncC plasmid with blaNDM-1 (called 
==PAGE:17==
 
16
pT1 plasmid (Figure 1.6) was identified. Twenty-four meropenem-resistant isolates from patients 
(13 C. freundii, 6 K. pneumoniae, 4 E. coli and 1 K. oxytoca) were positive for blaNDM-1 and had the 
IncC replicon. This supported the hypothesis that blaNDM-1 encoded plasmid spread in vivo [22].  
The Danish ST18 NDM-1 producing C. freundii outbreak, which started in 2012 at the Haematology 
Department at Aalborg University Hospital, has since then spread to the Department of Surgical 
Gastroenterology A1, at Aalborg University Hospital where a variant of the pT1 plasmid called 
pT2b (92 kb, Figure 1.7) is found frequently in the isolates from this department. The pT2b plasmid 
cannot conjugate to other Enterobacteriaceae since it has lost most of the conjugative machinery 
(tra genes) but has maintained its origin of transfer region (Figure 1.8) [30].

1.5 Citrobacter freundii 
Citrobacter freundii is an anaerobe gram-negative bacterium of the Enterobacteriaceae family. The 
bacterium is often the cause of a wide spectrum of nosocomial infections, which involves the 
respiratory tract, urinary tract, and bloodstream [32]. The bacteria are found to cause diseases 
such as infections in wounds, respiratory infections, meningitis, and sepsis. They can cause 
healthcare-associated infections, especially in paediatric and immunocompromised patients. 
Reports have shown that the carriage of antibiotic-resistant C. freundii was a mortality risk factor 
for immunocompromised patients with bacteraemia [33].

2 Rationale for this study  
The rationale for this study is to design lineage-specific PCR primers for the Enterobacteriales 
species called Citrobacter freundii having the sequence type ST18 for rapid identification of 
environmental samples containing this lineage. C. freundii ST18 carrying the carbapenemase gene 
blaNDM-1 has caused one of the largest outbreaks in Denmark and has been associated with 
colonization of the drainage system at the affected hospitals [22], [30]. Therefore, it is expected to 
be found in the hospital drainage systems but may be a common inhabitant of drainage systems in 
general. However, finding ST18 isolates in complex samples is difficult as they do not carry a 
specific phenotypic marker, so instead it will be necessary to develop a genotypic method (PCR) to 
identify this specific lineage based on whole genome sequencing data. The dataset consists of C. 
freundii genomes downloaded from RefSeq database as well as genomic data generated at SSI as 
part of the national surveillance of carbapenemase producing organisms (CPO). The BLAST based 
tool called ssGeneFinder will be used to identify unique targets to design PCR primers. These 
primers will be used along with a set of specific primers directed against blaNDM-1 to test on known 
isolates and samples from community and hospital environment, in the lab as well as being able to 
detect the specific hospital outbreak ST18 strain carrying blaNDM-1. The idea is to develop a fast PCR 
method to pin-point blaNDM-1 positive C. freundii ST18 from drain samples.  
 
2.1 Hypothesis  
The main hypothesis of this study is that C. freundii ST18 is everywhere in the environment and 
not only in the hospital drainage systems. As an alternative hypothesis, C. freundii ST18 may only 
be present in the hospital environment specifically at Aalborg University Hospital. The idea is to 
follow a step-by-step process both in silico and in vitro to investigate the validity of the proposed 
hypothesis. The workflow diagram is shown in Figure 2.1, where the validation data and study 
data used for the PCR-setups are shown in Figure 2.2.

ssGeneFinder is a platform for the selection of specific genomic targets for the direct PCR 
detection and identification of bacterial pathogens. This tool utilizes the BLAST method to find 
DNA sequences, which are unique to a given dataset [34]. Target genomes were all the C. freundii 
ST18 strains and the non-target genomes were all C. freundii non-ST18.

3.1.3 BLAST tool  
The Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST) finds regions of similarity between sequences. The 
program compares sequences and calculates the statistical significance of matches. There are 
several types of BLAST searches, which are on NCBI’s WebBLAST [35].  
 
3.1.4 BLASTn (nucleotide BLAST) 
This program compares one or more nucleotide query sequences to a database of nucleotide 
sequences [36], [37]. The program was used to compare the eight unique targets (from 
ssGeneFinder) with the database of nucleotide sequences. A standard BLASTn analysis was 
performed against the entire NCBI nucleotide collection (nr/nt) database (consists of 85203187 
sequences). A FASTA file with all the eight unique targets was uploaded. Default settings were 
chosen.  
 
3.1.5 BLASTx (translated nucleotide against protein) 
This program translates a nucleotide query sequence in six reading frames, which result in six 
protein sequences and compares against a database of protein sequences. It provides combined 
significance statistics for hits to different frames [35], [38]. The program was used to investigate 
the proteins of unique targets usid000001, usid000004, and usid000005 (from ssGeneFinder). 
Sequence for unique targets usid000001, usid000004, and usid000005 were uploaded. Default 
settings were chosen.  
 
3.1.6 BLAST (bioinformatic part) 
With the help of the local bioinformatician Kasper Thystrup Karstensen, a BLAST search of the 
unique targets usid000001, usid000004, and usid000005 with all the positive C. freundii ST18 from 
SSI as well as all the negative C. freundii non-ST18 to verify the results of ssGeneFinder.    
 
3.1.7 MyKMAFinder  
MyKMAFinder (k-mer alignment) is a mapping method that maps raw reads directly against 
redundant database [39], [40]. To find out which of the unique targets should be used for primer 
==PAGE:24==
 
23
designing, MyKMAFinder tool was used. In the tool the database was a FASTA file, which consisted 
of sequences for unique targets usid000001, usid000004 and usid000005. The database was 
aligned to every isolate that had something missing inside their sequences (from the BLAST search 
by Kasper Thystrup Karstensen). Nine isolates (AMA003197, AMA003654, AMA003676, 
AMA003730, AMA003648, AMA003728, AMA004013, AMA004090, and AMA004102 from SSI) 
were chosen to align with unique targets usid000001, usid000004 and usid000005 to investigate 
what was missing inside the sequences of these nine isolates.  
 
3.1.8 MUSCLE 
Multiple Sequence Comparison by Log-Expectation (MUSCLE) is a tool that creates multiple 
alignments [41], [42]. The tool is used for multiple sequence alignment. The input was the 
sequence for unique target usid000005 (chosen for primer design), and all the 11 sequences for 
Salmonella anatum (hit from BLASTn for unique target usid000005). ClustalW was chosen as 
output format.    
 
3.1.9 MLST 
Multilocus sequence typing (MLST) is a web-based method that uses BLAST-based ranking method 
to find the best matching MLST alleles of the specified MLST scheme. The Sequence Type (ST) is 
determined by the combination of seven identified alleles [43]. For Citrobacter spp. the sequence 
type is determined by the combination of these seven identified alleles: aspC, clpX, fadD, mdh, 
arcA, dnaG, and lysP [44], where the combination for ST18 is: aspC10, clpX14, fadD17, mdh12, 
arcA5, dnaG5, and lysP14 [45]. MLST was used under the WGS part to identify sequence types of 
the unknown samples.  
 
3.1.10 cgMLST 
Core genome multilocus sequence typing (cgMLST) is an extension of the traditional MLST where 
genome-wide gene-by-gene allele, which are hundreds/thousands of genes (core genome MLST) 
that are used for genotyping. cgMLST schemes consist of a fixed set of conserved genome-wide 
genes [46], [47]. Two different (tailor-made) cgMLST schemes were used in this study. One 
covering all C. freundii types and containing 2964 core genes and a second specific cgMLST scheme 
==PAGE:25==
 
24
for C. freundii ST18 containing 4056 core genes, both beginning already available before the start 
of this project and used to study the genetic relationship between the available genomes. The 
software SeqSphere+ is used to analyse this as described in section 3.1.13.      
 
3.1.11 ResFinder  
ResFinder is a web-based tool that uses BLAST to identify acquired antimicrobial resistance genes 
in bacteria using whole-genome sequencing data [48]. ResFinder was used under the WGS part to 
identify if the unknown samples were positive for blaNDM-1.  
 
3.1.12 PlasmidFinder  
PlasmidFinder is a web-based tool for bacterial typing based on draft genomes of multi-drug 
resistant Enterobacteriaceae by a rapid detection of replicons and Inc-types of known plasmid 
types [49]. PlasmidFinder was used under the WGS part to identify plasmid replicons and Inc-types 
of the unknown samples.  
 
3.1.13 SeqSphere+ 
SeqSphere+ is a commercial software from Ridom Gmbh that uses the cgMLST typing platform to 
investigate multiple outbreaks. Alleles are used instead of single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNP) 
[50], [51]. The reference genome for the specific ST18 cgMLST scheme was a Chinese C. freundii 
isolate ST18 isolate called “ST2”. 
 
3.1.14 GView Server  
GView Server is a comparative genomics server, a circular and linear genome viewer. A single 
reference genome (in Genbank, EMBL) can be uploaded and the appearance customized, or 
multiple genomes can be uploaded to perform comparative analysis. The server uses BLAST to 
compare the reference genome to the comparison sequences. There are different types of 
comparative analyses [52]. The server was used to investigate the plasmids where the blaNDM-1 
gene can be encoded. For this study the BLAST atlas analysis was used.

3.2 Laboratory methods used in this study 
3.2.1 Polymerase Chain Reaction 
Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is a laboratory technique that is used to make multiple copies of a 
specific sequence of DNA. Two short DNA sequences called primers are designed to amplify a 
specific segment of the genome. The PCR is based on three steps required for DNA synthesis 
reaction. Denaturation of the template into single strands, annealing of primers to each original 
strand for new strand synthesis, and extension of the new DNA strands from the primers. The 
results of the PCR reaction are visualized by gel electrophoresis, which separates the DNA 
fragments according to size. The PCR technique is used for detection and identification of 
infectious disease agents and for typing and characterization of virulence and resistance genes 
[53], [54].  
 
3.2.2 Multiplex Polymerase Chain Reaction  
Multiplex PCR is a powerful technique that empowers amplification of two or more products in 
parallel in a single reaction tube. In order to visually read the results, it is important that the 
individual bands generated from each sup-PCR has different sizes on an agarose gel. The technique 
is widely used in genotyping applications and different areas of DNA testing in research and 
diagnostic laboratories [55].  
 
3.2.3 Primer design 
To get a successful outcome of a PCR trial, appropriate primers are needed. When designing a set 
of primers to a specific region of DNA, which is desired for amplification, one primer should anneal 
to the plus strand, which is oriented in the 5 → 3 direction (known as the sense or non-template 
strand) and the other primer should complement the minus strand, which is oriented in the 3 → 
5 direction (antisense or template strand) [53].  
 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
26
There are some characteristics that should be considered when designing primers [53]: 
• Primer length should be 15-30 nucleotide residues (bases) 
• Optimal G-C content should range between 40-60%  
• The 3’end of primers should contain a G or C to clamp the primer. This increases the 
priming efficiency. The three hydrogen bonds in GC pairs increase the melting temperature 
of the primers.  
•  The 3’ends of a primer set (includes a plus strand primer and a minus strand primer) 
should not be complementary to each other. This can result in formation of primer dimers.  
• Di-nucleotide repeats (e.g., GCGCGCGC or ATATATAT) or single base runs (e.g., AAAAA or 
CCCCC) should be avoided as they can cause hairpin loop structures to form. If this is 
unavoidable due to nature of the DNA template, then only include repeats or single base 
runs with a maximum of 4 bases.   
• Optimal melting temperatures (Tm) for primers range between 52-58 C 
 
Melting temperatures:  
To get an optimal melting temperature the site ThermoFisher Scientific tool was used to calculate 
melting temperatures for the two primer sets. In the settings, the DreamTaq DNA polymerase or 
other Taq-based DNA polymerase was chosen. Other settings were default [56].

commonly found enteric pathogens except Campylobacter. The medium combines growth 
differentiating and selective properties (based on colour variation of colonies) without loss of 
detection (e.g., E. coli will be shown as typical red colonies, Aeromonas spp. will be shown as 
reddish brown colonies with curved edges, Klebsiella spp. will be shown as red colonies, and 
Citrobacter spp. will be shown as dark grey centre and pink edge) [57].

3.2.6 MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry 
Matrix Assisted Laser Desorption Ionization-Time Of Flight (MALDI-TOF) mass spectrometry is a 
potential technology tool for microbial identification and diagnosis of disease caused by bacteria, 
viruses and fungi [59]. During the process, microbes are identified using either intact cells or cell 
extracts. The chemical compounds are ionized into charged molecules and ratio of their mass to 
charge (m/z) is measured. The sample for analysis is prepared by mixing or coating with a solution 
called matrix, which is an energy-absorbent, organic compound. The sample entrapped within the 
matrix co-crystallizes when the matrix crystallizes on drying. The sample is ionized in an 
automated mode with a laser beam. The laser beam generates singly protonated ions from 
analytes in the sample, by desorption and ionization. The protonated ions accelerate at a fixed 
potential, where they separate from each other on the basis of their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). 
The charged analytes are detected and measured using different types of mass analyzers. For 
microbiological applications mainly the time of flight (TOF) mass analyzers are used. The MALDI-
TOF analyzer measures the m/z ratio of an ion by determining the time required for it to travel the 
length of the flight tube. A characteristic spectrum called peptide mass fingerprint (PMF) is 
generated for analytes in the sample, based on the information from the TOF analyzer (Figure 3.2) 
[59].

chromosome rather than on plasmids, which could be lost during replication.

be, that there was not enough material in the pipette while the sample was loaded into the lane 
on the gel, and therefore the multiplex PCR could not detect this isolate.  
 
As mentioned in section 4.8.1-4.8.5 all the known isolates (40 isolates from SSI and 21 samples 
from the Department of Surgical Gastroenterology A1 at Aalborg University Hospital) did show 
results as expected, which confirmed that both primer set 1 and the blaNDM-1 primer set work in 
the multiplex PCR. Therefore, should isolate HES1/AMA004448 also have been detected by the 
PCR. Isolate HES9/AMA004450 was identified as C. freundii by MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry, and 
C. freundii ST18 without the blaNDM-1 gene, which was also observed in the PCR. These three results 
==PAGE:67==

are consistent. Isolates HES5/AMA004487, HES7/AMA004449, and HES12/AMA004451 were also 
identified as C. freundii by MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry and WGS also identified them as C. 
freundii ST18 with blaNDM-1 gene, which was also observed in the PCR. These results are also 
consistent. MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry identified isolate HES13/AMA004488 as C. fruendii as 
best match and C. braakii as second-best match. This was not observed from the WGS results,

match the result observed in the PCR, which showed a band for C. freundii ST18. Isolate 
HES13/AMA004488 may be C. freundii, since MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry also identified it as 
that. The reason for why WGS could not identify it as C. freundii may be that the right colony was 
not picked for whole genome sequencing. The reason why the ST18 band was shown on the gel, 
can be that sample HES13/AMA004488 has been contaminated during the laboratory work or that 
some aerosols from sample HES12/AMA004451, which was ST18, have contaminated the lane on 
the gel where sample HES13/AMA004488 was loaded into, since these two samples were beside 
each other. A third reason may be that samples HES1/AMA004448, and HES13/AMA004488 have 
been switched either by the technician Hanne Donskov Eriksen at Aalborg University Hospital 
when the samples were collected or during the laboratory work at Statens Serum Institut, and 
therefore sample HES1/AMA004448 did not show a clear band for ST18 and sample

usid000005 is not found in samples HES1/AMA004448 and HES13/AMA004488, just like unique 
target usid000001 was not found in some of the known isolates (from SSI) even though they were 
ST18, as mentioned in section 4.5. To investigate if unique target usid000005 was found in 
samples HES1/AMA004448, and HES13/AMA004488 a BLASTn analysis was done. Unique target 
usid000005 was BLAST against samples HES1/AMA004448, and HES13/AMA004488. The results 
from BLASTn showed that unique target usid000005 was found in sample HES1/AMA004448 and 
not in sample HES13/AMA004488 (results not shown). This analysis concluded that there should 
be a band for ST18 for sample HES1/AMA004448, whereas sample HES13/AMA004488 should not 
have a band since it does not have unique target usid000005 in its sequence.    
Isolate HES17/AMA004452 was identified as C. freundii by MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry, and C. 
freundii ST114 by WGS. This result is also consistent. This sample did not show any band in the 
PCR, since it is a non-ST18, thus also confirming the multiplex PCR result.

4.13 Phylogenetic analysis of the identified isolates   
In order to understand the relationship between the identified isolates from community 
environment (CES3/CF18_F3A, CES5/CF18_H1A, CES11/CF18_S3A, CES13AMA004489, 
CES19/AMA004453 and CES23/AMA004454), hospital environment (HES1/AMA004448, 
HES5/AMA004487, HES7/AMA004449, HES9/AMA004450, HES12/AMA004451, 
HES13/AMA004488, HES17/AMA004452) and the clinical isolates from the ST18 outbreak in the 
national surveillance database, a phylogenetic tree was made. The first phylogenetic tree was 
based on a previously (tailor-made) cgMLST schemes covering all C. freundii type, containing 2964 
core genes (Figure 4.19). The tree shows the community environment isolates (CES3/CF18_F3A, 
CES5/CF18_H1A, CES11/CF18_S3A, CES13/AMA004489, CES19/AMA004453 and 
CES23/AMA004454) (Figure 4.19, black rings) and two isolates from hospital environment 
(HES13/AMA004488, and HES17/AMA004452) (Figure 4.19 red rings outside the big green circle) 
distributed in different places, because these were identified as non-C. freundii ST18. Five hospital 
environment isolates (HES1/AMA004448, HES5/AMA004487, HES7/AMA004449, 
HES9/AMA004450, and HES12/AMA004451) are located within the ST18 clade (Figure 4.19, red 
rings inside the green circle), this is because these five isolates were identified as C. freundii ST18.   
Another tree was made based on another previously (tailor-made) cgMLST schemes specific for C. 
freundii ST18 containing 4056 core genes. This was done to investigate the five C. freundii ST18 
identified isolates from hospital environment with the isolates from the ST18 outbreak (Figure 
4.20). The tree shows that isolate HES5/AMA004487 cluster together with the other ST18 isolates 
from the ST18 outbreak (Figure 4.20, red ring), whereas isolates HES1/AMA004448, 
HES7/AMA004449, HES9/AMA004450, and HES12/AMA004451 are clustered together farther 
away (Figure 4.20, black ring).

4.14 Plasmid replicon identification  
The Gview Server showed the whole genomes of each isolate, which are BLAST against the 
reference genome. The IncC – replicon type plasmid pT1 complete plasmid sequence carrying the 
blaNDM-1 gene and originating from the C. freundii ST18 outbreak strain was used as reference 
genome to map the sequencing data from isolates HES5/AMA004487, HES7/AMA004449 and 
HES12/AMA004451 shown in Figure 4.23. This analysis showed that none of the three isolates 
contained DNA that looked like plasmid pT1 as shown in Figure 4.23. Since PlasmidFinder from 
WGS showed that isolates HES7/AMA004449, and HES12/AMA004451 both had the plasmid 
replicon IncN2, and the phylogenetic tree showed that these two isolates cluster with isolate 
AMA003588, another analysis was performed. E.coli (blastn was used to find hits where blaNDM-1 
was encoded on IncN2 (pCRE10.4_IncN2.gb)) was used as reference genome to map the 
sequencing data from isolates HES7/AMA004449, HES12/AMA004451 and isolate AMA003588 
shown in Figure 4.24. The analysis showed that isolates HES7/AMA004449, HES12/AMA004451, 
and isolate AMA003588 looked like the plasmid from E. coli. Sequencing data from isolate 
HES5/AMA004487 did neither cover the pT1 plasmid sequence nor did it have the plasmid replicon 
IncN2, a third analysis was performed, since PlasmidFinder from WGS had identified the IncC 
replicon for isolate HES5/AMA004487. Isolate HES5/AMA004487 was compared to the plasmid 
sequence of a variant of the pT1 plasmid called pT2b (92 kb) normally associated with the 
Haematology Department variant and this sequence was used as reference genome. Isolate 
CPO20190170 sampled from a drain at the Department of Surgical Gastroenterology A1, was used 
as positive control (Figure 4.25). Plasmid pT2b is also found frequently in some of the isolates from 
the ST18 outbreak. The analysis showed that isolate HES5/AMA004487 has the same pT2b plasmid 
as the clinical isolates from the Department of Surgical Gastroenterology A1 (Figure 4.25).

As reports have shown that antibiotic-resistant C. freundii was a mortality risk factor for 
immunocompromised patients, it is important to investigate, which intervention can give best 
result in preventing or eliminating the organism from the water environment.  
Since the NDM-1 producing C. freundii ST18 are mainly found in the toilets and sinks (two sinks), at 
different departments at Aalborg University Hospital, they may try some of the strategies, which 
other studies have tried that succeeded in preventing or eradicating the organism from the water 
environment. Interventions at disinfecting water reservoirs can be, weekly flushing of sink drains 
with acetic acid/hot water, and for drain colonization, bleach can be used to eradicate 
Enterobacteriaceae. UV light was also effective in some studies, while other successful 
interventions included drainage redesign [25].    
 
4.15 Limitations of the study   
Sample collection from the hospital environment (HES) part 2 was only from one hospital, a more 
extended sample collection from other hospitals is required to compare with the samples from 
Aalborg University Hospital. Also, a more extended collection of samples from the community 
environment is required to compare with the findings from the hospitals.  
The results for isolate HES1/AMA004448 in the PCR part 8 showed a weak band for C. freundii 
ST18, and the results for isolate HES13/AMA004488 did not match the results observed in the PCR 
part 8. Another PCR should be run to test isolates HES1/AMA004448, and HES13/AMA004488 
again to see if the band for ST18 will be shown clearer for isolate HES1/AMA004448 and not for 
isolate HES13/AMA004488.

4.17 Further studies  
To supplement this study and especially to discover the NDM-1 producing C. freundii ST18, a more 
extended sample collection is required both from the community environment and also from 
other hospitals. Another PCR should be run to test isolates HES1/AMA004448, and 
HES13/AMA004488 again to see if the band for ST18 will be shown more clearer for isolate 
HES1/AMA004448 and not for isolate HES13/AMA004488. Another possible task could be to 
investigate how abundant C. freundii in general are in the human gut by identifying metagenomics 
data in the ENA database from healthy people and quantify the fraction of C. freundii compared to 
E. coli, K. pneumoniae and Enterobacter cloacae to rule out a reservoir in the gut for C. freundii.

5 Conclusion  
This study suggested one target primer set (primer set 1) based on unique target usid000005, 
specific for C. freundii ST18 together with the blaNDM-1 primer set. This study also suggested that 
the samples need to be cultivated in rich media before PCR to distinguish NDM-1 producing C. 
freundii ST18. All the community environment isolates were neither NDM-1 producing C. freundii 
ST18, nor C. freundii ST18. On the other hand, three isolates (HES5/AMA004487, 
HES7/AMA004449, and HES12/AMA004451) from the hospital environment at Aalborg University 
Hospital were NDM-1 producing C. freundii ST18 and two isolates (HES1/AMA004448, and 
HES9/AMA004450) were C. freundii ST18 without the blaNDM-1 gene.  
Isolate HES7/AMA004449, and HES12/AMA004451 clustered together with an isolate 
AMA003588, which was from a Greek patient who was hospitalized in Aalborg University Hospital, 
but the patient had not been hospitalized in the wards where these two isolates originate from. 
Isolate HES5/AMA004487 clustered together with the other isolates from the ST18 outbreak and 
was identified as having the plasmid pT2b, which is also found frequently in some of the isolates 
from the ST18 outbreak.  
 
From this study, it can be concluded that the NDM-1 producing C. freundii ST18 are still found in 
the hospital environment at Aalborg University Hospital, where the mainly reservoir are toilets 
and sink. It is still unclear why isolates HES7/AMA004449, and HES12/AMA004451 clustered 
together with isolate AMA003588, since the patient has not been on these two wards where 
isolates HES7/AMA004449, and HES12/AMA004451 originate from.
