Abstract
Stay cables on bridges are exposed to dynamic loading from wind, rain, and via motion
from the bridge structure. The cables are prone to vibrations due to their slenderness and
their material. The cables are usually made of high strength steel, which has a very low
material damping. External dampers are often required in the cable design, to mitigate
the large oscillations that occurs due to the aforementioned parameters.
The present thesis concerns the implementation and experimental analysis of an external
electromagnetic damper on a simply supported cable.
The focus in this thesis is to
investigate the eﬃciency of the electromagnetic damper and to obtain eﬀective tuning of
the damper, by an external electric circuit, known as a shunt.
A design is proposed through coupling the theory of electrical components to mechanical
components. The electromagnetic shunt damper (ESD) is tested and designed, to obtain a
hybrid viscous damper, with a variable viscosity from the shunt resistance. The underlying
focus by implementing electromagnetism, is thereby to develop a smart damper with
potential adaptivity.
An experimental setup is build, improved and developed through the project period. It is
demonstrated through initial experiments that the damping of the free cable is dependent
on the amplitudes. Experiments for the free cable is performed for the ﬁrst three modes,
but the measurements from mode two and three is showing errors due to vibrations of the
concrete support. All further experiments are therefore performed for mode 1, where three
types of experiments are performed to measure damping ratios and natural frequencies.
The structural damping is found for mode 1 to be 0.81% for the free decay response and
0.89% for the experimental modal analysis (EMA). It is found that the ESD contributes
with 1.4−2.2% damping for the free decay response, and 1.5−1.8% for the EMA. Newmark
time integration is used to perform numerical simulations for a FE model to support the
experiments. From the Newmark time integration the presence of the ESD contributes
with a maximum of 2.2% damping for the free decay response and with a maximum of
2.4% for the EMA.

Resumé
Kabler på broer udsættes for dynamisk belastning fra vind, regn og via bevægelse fra
brokonstruktionen. Kablerne er udsat for vibrationer på grund af deres slankhed og deres
materiale.
Kablerne er normalt lavet af højstyrkestål, som har en meget lav materi-
aledæmpning. Eksterne dæmpere er ofte nødvendige i designet for kablerne for at undgå
store svingninger, der vil opstå på grund af de ovennævnte parametre.
Denne afhandling vedrører speciﬁkt implementering og eksperimentel analyse af en ekstern
elektromagnetisk dæmper på et simpelt understøttet kabel. Fokuset i denne afhandling
er at undersøge eﬀektiviteten af den elektromagnetiske dæmper og for at opnå eﬀektive
justeringer af dæmperen ved et eksternt elektrisk kredsløb, kendt som en shunt.
Gennem kobling af teorien om elektriske komponenter til mekaniske komponenter foreslås
et dæmper design. Den elektromagnetiske shunt dæmper (ESD) er testet og designet til
at opnå en hybrid viskøs dæmper med varierende viskositet fra shunt modstanden. Det
underliggende fokus ved implementering af elektromagnetisme er derved at udvikle en
smart dæmper med potentiel tilpasningsevne.
En eksperimentel opsætning bygges, forbedres og udvikles gennem projektperioden. Det
demonstreres gennem indledende eksperimenter, at dæmpningen af det frie kabel er am-
plitude afhængig. Eksperimenter med det frie kabel udføres for de tre første modalformer,
dog viser målingerne fra anden og tredje modalform fejl på grund af vibrationer i beton
understøtningen. Alle yderligere eksperimenter udføres derfor for første modalform, hvor
tre typer af eksperimenter udføres for at måle dæmpningsforhold og naturlige frekvenser.
Den strukturelle dæmpning ﬁndes til at være 0, 81% for det frie henfald og 0, 89% for
den eksperimentelle modal analyse (EMA). Det konstateres, at ESD’en bidrager med
1, 4−2, 2% dæmpning for det frie henfald og 1, 5−1, 8% for EMA. Newmark-tidsintegration
er brugt til at udføre numeriske simuleringer for en FE-model for at understøtte eksper-
imenterne.
Ud fra Newmark-tidsintegrationen bidrager ESD’en med maksimalt 2, 2%
dæmpning for det frie henfald og maksimalt 2, 4% for EMA.

1
Introduction
The use of carrying loads by suspending a cable is widely used across the world [1]. The
application of using thin wires in the main load carrying elements causes many problems,
especially in relation to durability. Since cables are highly ﬂexible, due to their slenderness
and the material they are made out of, they are more prone to vibrations induced by
dynamic loads on the cable. Vibrations are primarily induced by forces from wind, rain,
or motion of the supporting deck structure. Cables are usually made of high strength
steel, which has a very low material damping. Full scale experiments have shown that the
structural damping ranges between 0.32 to 0.49 % [2]. Supplemental external dampers
have been widely recognized to be eﬀective in reducing large oscillations from the dynamic
response. The external damping is often provided by mounting a local viscous damper at
a distance between 2 −4% of the span from one of the supports to mitigate these large
oscillations [1]. This closeness to the support limits the amount of additive damping, but
has been found suﬃcient for several structures. An example of this is the Erasmus Bridge
in Rotterdam, seen in ﬁgure 1.1, where ﬂuid-viscous dampers is mounted at a distance of
approximately 2% from the support. Kovacs [3] found that the maximum modal damping
obtained from a viscous damper is about half the relative distance of the damper from
the support. In the Erasmus case the maximum modal damping would then be 1%, which
was enough to reduce the dynamic response.
Figure 1.1: Fluid-viscous dampers installed on Erasmus Bridge in Rotterdam [4].
The problem with a passive damper is that they are unable to adapt to changes in, for
example, loading conditions. In recent times, the interest of optimizing external dampers
on structures has been increasing. Therefore, research and development of a new type of
damper is of high interest. A semi-active damper has the ability to control the damper
properties in real time and is called smart dampers. The scope of this thesis is to analyze
supplemental damping of stay cables on bridges with a smart damper. The smart damper
is an electromagnetic shunt damper (ESD). The ESD consists of a magnetic core and a
coil, connected to a shunt. The experimental setup can be seen in ﬁgure 1.2. The thesis
uses theoretical and experimental analysis of a simply supported cable, with a focus on
designing and implementing an ESD to control the supplemental damping.

The ESD is tested and designed, to obtain a hybrid viscous damper, with a variable
viscosity from the shunt resistance. The underlying focus through implementing electro-
magnetism, is thereby to develop a smart damper with potential adaptivity. The main
goal is to test diﬀerent control strategies of the ESD on the experimental setup of a stay
cable.
Figure 1.2: Experimental setup with a vibration exciter and an ESD installed.
In chapter 2, an introduction to cables and the relevant theory for static and dynamic
analysis. In chapter 3, the procedure of establishment of a FE model with implementation
of an external damping mechanism is explained. In chapter 4, a proposed design of an
ESD is established through the relevant theory. The focus of this design is to understand
the coupling between the electrical components to the mechanical components. Through
the FE model a root locus analysis is made to obtain the desired performance of diﬀerent
types and sizes of an ESD component. An experimental set-up is built during the project
period, and explained in chapter 5. Throughout this period the experimental set-up is
developed and redesigned to achieve the most precise results. The suitable ESD com-
ponent is mounted on the experimental setup of a 12 meter long taut cable. In chapter
6, the experimental results are being presented, discussed and evaluated. Two kinds of
experiments are performed to measure natural frequencies and the damping ratio. In the
ﬁrst experiment the logarithmic decrement is utilized to measure the damping ratio for
a free decay response. The experiment is initially performed without anything attached
to it, in order to understand how the structural damping of the cable behaves at diﬀer-
ent amplitudes. Then the vibration exciter is attached to evaluate the inﬂuence of this
component. Finally the ESD component is attached and control strategies are performed.
The second experiment covers an experimental modal analysis, where the cable is excited
by a white noise signal over a longer period of time. From the measurements a frequency
response function is used to obtain natural frequencies and damping ratios. First, the
cable is excited by a vibration exciter without the ESD component attached. Next, it is
excited again, with the ESD component attached and the same control strategies are used,
as in the ﬁrst experiment. Both experiments are simulated with Newmark time integra-
tion for the FE model to verify the evaluated outcomes. Lastly, a current-displacement
experiment is performed to investigate the hysteresis loop of the ESD.

3
Finite element model
An implementation of a ﬁnite element (FE) model is used to acquire the global stiﬀness
matrix K and global mass matrix M derived from the modal analysis from the work by
Krenk [6]. The cable is discretized into a number of continuous elements ne described
by shape functions. The FE model is used to design the electromagnetic damper and
to compare it with results obtained from the experimental setup through Newmark time
integration. To enable uncoupling of the system, Rayleigh damping is introduced to create
the damping matrix C.

This thesis is focusing on the solution with a transverse damper mechanism mounted to
the cable at 4% of the span from the support, seen in ﬁgure 4.1. The closeness of the

parameters is presented. Subsequently, the procedure of designing an electromagnetic
shunt damper (ESD) for the cable is demonstrated.
Figure 4.1: Electromagnetic shunt damper attached on experimental cable setup.
In general, the function of a damper is to stabilize a system by extracting energy from
the system with a damping force. The tuning of the damper is essential to avoid acting
as a support. The damper cannot be too stiﬀnor to ﬂexible as it fails dissipating the
appropriate energy from the system. With optimal tuning excessive structural vibration
can be reduced to prevent catastrophic structural failure mechanisms. The focus in this
thesis is to imitate a viscous damper by calibrating mechanical properties, the stiﬀness kd
and viscous coeﬃcient cd, respectively from the inductance L and resistance R. Hence the
ESD is an electrical system which is transformed to the coherent mechanical properties
shown in ﬁgure 4.2 according to Chen et al.[8].

This chapter describes the experiments and their results. First, an experiment evaluating
the iterative process of ﬁnding the natural frequencies from response histories is demon-

the mechanism.

6.3.1.2
Fractional Power-law model
The discretized values from experiment 1 are used to obtain a correlation between am-
plitudes and damping ratio of the cable. A fractional Power-law model is established to
create a theoretical coupling to the measurements, as the damping ratio ζ decreases for a
higher vibration level. The estimation of the damping force is obtained with an equivalent
SDOF model, which combines friction and ﬂuid-viscous eﬀects. This model is called a
fractional Power-law model and it is formulated as
f(t) = α sign( ˙u(t)) | ˙u(t)|γ
(6.5)
where α is the damping coeﬃcient, γ represents a Power-law function which interpolates
between pure friction damping (γ = 0) and pure viscous damping (γ = 1). The ability
to interpolate the γ value is an attempt to increase the accuracy of the model.
The
simpliﬁed model for the SDOF system is changed to a more general case to create the

6.4.1
Windows and overlaps
The parameters window length and overlap needs to be established for the function
modalfrf in MATLAB can be computed. The signal (in- and output) according Fourier’s
theorem is presented by weighted sum of sines and cosines according to National Instru-
ments [10]. The experiments are performed by a white noise excitation and is containing a
representation of diﬀerent waveforms corresponding to natural frequencies. The technique
windowing is used to minimize discontinuities between the cycles of signals. Therefore
each signal becomes more distinct and result in continues waveforms. Conceptually a
window consists of a ﬁnite set of data, a window length, and an overlap between the
windows, shown in ﬁgure 6.17. This method ensures reduction of amplitudes for discon-
tinuities. Each window and overlap must have a length, which are evaluated based upon
the experiments in the following sections. This is because the measured signal is not an
integer number of periods sampled in the experiments.
44

Figure 6.17: Conceptual deﬁnition of window length and overlap.
There are diﬀerent examples of typical window functions described in Brandt [11]. The
Hanning window functions are used due to the knowledge about the signal. It is known
that the signal is containing diﬀerent types of sine waves and the Hanning window is found
suﬃcient for these experiments according to National Instruments [10]. The Hanning win-
dow can be seen in ﬁgure 6.17 with the described window length and overlap between the
windows. Due to the uncertainty of capturing an exact number of integer periods for each
signals it is chosen to iterate over the window length to ﬁnd a stabilization window length
to evaluate. On the opposite site it is chosen to divide the window length with a seemingly
arbitrary constant 1.5 for the overlap, rounded to nearest integer. This arbitrary constant
is found representative according to Dahl et. al. [12]. In the following experiments an
EMA is performed based upon a white noise excitation of the cable evaluating on both
natural frequencies and damping ratio for the diﬀerent cases.

Discussion
This section covers the question of validity of the experiments in comparison to the theory
and the numerical time simulations. Clariﬁcation of thoughts and observations under the
experiments is presented, and clariﬁes choices and assumptions.
The ﬁrst results for response histories and fast Fourier transformation, shows the measured
natural frequencies of the free cable, without anything attached to it. Both experimental
methods are found to be suﬃcient to achieve the natural frequencies of the cable. The ﬁrst
natural frequency from the fast Fourier transformation deviates 0.03% from the response
history measurements. The fast Fourier transformation is found acceptable to validate the
measured frequencies from the more robust response histories experiments. The iterative
procedure described in section 6.1, is time consuming because small deviations in the
load frequency causes visible beating phenomena. It is chosen not to perform any further
measurements on mode 2 and 3 for the damping ratio. This is because of the observed
vibrations of the concrete support and the high deviations from measured frequencies to
the numerical model.
The experimental setup is built within the project period. It is discovered that construct-
ing and reﬁning the experimental setup is very time consuming. Based upon this and
more reliable measurements from mode 1 is it chosen to only perform experiments and
numerical simulations for the ﬁrst mode.
From the initial experiment performed with the ESD attached a phenomenon about a
new boundary condition at the damper is observed. The phenomenon is not analyzed
in this project but could be investigated in future projects. The damping mechanism is
creating a support at the damper location, when tuning the shunt resistance from Rs,max
to Rs,min. It is observed that the cable is bending around the damper attachment point
and the potential support causes bending moments inside the cable and increases the risk
of fatigue failure at this location.

EMA. It is assumed that the measured natural frequency from both experiments is the

Conclusion
The main topic of this thesis is the damping of cables by electromagnetic dampers. An
experimental setup is built, improved and developed through the project period. The
experimental setup is a 12 [m] long taut cable simply supported at both ends. A root
locus analysis is performed to design an electromagnetic shunt damper (ESD) as a hybrid
viscous damper. The electrical properties are transformed through the coupling coeﬃcient
to mechanical properties. The mechanical model of the ESD covers a spring and viscous
dashpot in a series connection. A shunt is added to the circuit to adjust the resistance and
thereby control the viscous coeﬃcient. It is thereby designed so the shunt must deliver
positive resistance to attain the theoretical optimal damping by the presence of the ESD.
The initial experiments are performed to understand the cable motion. It is found from
the resonance response, a deviation on 0.5 −6% for the natural frequencies for the ﬁrst
three modes from the FE model.
The FFT analysis, deviates 0.03 −1.81% from the
resonance response and is suﬃcient to validate the obtained frequencies from the resonance
responses. It is observed that the resonance histories for mode 2 and 3 are interrupted by
vibrations of the concrete block support.
The structural damping is found for mode 1 to be 0.81% for free decay response and 0.89%
for the experimental modal analysis (EMA). Based on these two diﬀerent experiment
methods, tuning procedures of the shunt resistance of the ESD is tested to see if an
optimum damping point can be found. It is found that the ESD contributes with 1.4−2.2%
damping for the free decay response, and 1.5 −1.8% for the EMA. For both experiments,
no speciﬁc optimum tuning point is found but the damping ratios is close to the theoretical
predictions. The same experiments are performed with Newmark time integration for the
FE model. The same optimum tuning point is found for both numerical simulations and
are consistent with the root locus analysis. The presence of the ESD contributes with
2.2% damping for the free decay response and 2.4% for the EMA.
It is demonstrated through a current-displacement hysteresis loop experiment that the
mechanical model of the ESD can be questioned. It is found that the ESD does not
necessarily work as a pure hybrid viscous damper. It is recommended to investigate other
types of mechanical models of the ESD to understand the dynamics of the electromagnet.

Further work
Suggestions for further work are based upon observations and experiences from the per-
formed experiments. The suggestions cover changes and improvements of the experimental
setup. Some improvements are performed when discovered, while others are not imple-
mented in the project period due to time constraints.
Most of the improvements for
further work are regarding the experimental setup to remove potential risk of uncertain-
ties. The improvements also covers new investigations of the design and behavior of the
ESD.
• It turned out that the concrete block support is not rigid enough to restrain against
vibrations for especially mode 2 and 3. It is found necessary to replace the concrete
block support with a bosch rexroth support to mitigate these unwanted vibrations.
This could lead to an investigation about adaptivity of the ESD for several modes.
The bosch rexroth support must be mounted to the ﬂoor due to its low weight.
• An investigation of the performance of the ESD at diﬀerent locations along the
cable could be carried out. It is possible for the experimental setup to set the ESD
at diﬀerent distances from the support.
• The measured displacement showed irregularities especially for amplitudes lower
than 10 [mm].
It can be investigated if the irregularities could be mitigated by
replacing the displacements sensors with some more accurate sensors. This is found
especially interesting if more analysis of EMA or OMA should be carried out due to
the measured amplitudes ranging below 10 [mm] throughout the whole experiment.
• An investigation of the ESD in a separate testing machine to clarify a possible
new mechanical model for the ESD and how it performs at diﬀerent resistances in
particular. Further, it would be interesting to implement an adjustable inductance
to control the spring stiﬀness. If possible, it would be interesting to test the ESD
with an adjusted small inductance, hence leading to an inﬁnitely stiﬀspring and to
investigate whether it attains a viscous damper.
• An investigation of designing the ESD as a hybrid tuned mass damper (TMD). The
ESD has relative high moving mass and it is found interesting to take advantage
of this. The spring could be attached on top of the ESD. Potential challenges of
keeping the spring moving vertical is found. A possibility would be attaching three
supporting wheels around the spring to keep it in place.
• Develop an electronic remote control unit for the shunt resistance. This gives the
possibility to control the performance of the ESD and to adapt through live mea-
surements.
• Following this proposed design of a prototype ESD, a development of a full scale
ESD implementation on a cable stayed bridge should be carried out. This covers
the physical dimensions and production of the ESD, housing for the component to
protect from weather, cost estimates and so on.

Introduction
Power Amplifier Type 2718 has been designed to drive
small vibration exciters, particularly Brüel & Kjær Vibra-
tion Exciter Type 4809. It can also be used to drive the
Mini-Shaker Type  4810 to full rating. For this application,
the maximum output current should be limited to 1.8 A.
The power amplifier has a flat frequency response from
10 Hz to 20 kHz (±0.5 dB ). The power output capability
is 75 VA into a 3 W exciter or resistive load and the
maximum voltage gain is 40 dB. This enables the power
amplifier to be used in acoustical measurement setups,
even when third-octave, narrow-band noise is employed.
The use of a transformerless power output stage and high
negative feedback results in very low harmonic distortion.
A balanced preamplifier and rugged solid-state design
results in a stable instrument which can tolerate temper-
ature fluctuations and supply line variations.
Extensive monitoring capabilities include a front panel
multifunction display ( LCD type ) showing output current
and output voltage. Also, the front panel encompasses a
number of LEDs that light up when the instrument is in
the following states: distortion, temperature overload, cur-
rent overload, stand-by status and power on. Monitoring
the output current and voltage can also take place using
two BNC connectors on the rear of Type 2718.

Tension and Compression Load Cell
MODEL 8523
Product description
The tension & compression load cells from the 8523 series are 
designed for a wide range of uses. The sensors feature many benefits, 
including three point-contact mounts for tilt-free installation. Thanks to 
this feature, excellent measurement results can be achieved even with 
a sub-optimum mounting surface.
The force to be measured is applied to the central threaded hole in 
the tension or compression direction. For measuring purely compres-
sive loads, using load buttons from our accessories range saves the 
need for complex sensor-integration mechanisms. Tensile loads in rods 
or chains can also be detected with ease using the optional pull plate.
 
Inside the sensor is an elastic membrane, on which are applied strain 
gages connected in a full Wheatstone bridge. If a tensile or compres-
sive load is applied to the sensor, the ohmic resistance of the measu-
ring bridge changes and detunes the output signal in proportion to the 
measured load in mV/V.

INSTRUCTION SHEET
■ Power Supply and Wiring
• Do not impose voltage exceeding the rated voltage: 10 to 30 VDC, including 10% ripple (p-p).
• When supplying power to the Sensor, make sure that the polarity of the power is correct, and do 
not connect to an AC power supply.
• Do not short-circuit the load for the open collector output. Short-circuiting the load may cause 
ﬁre or damage on the Sensor.
• Connect the load correctly. Short-circuiting the load may cause ﬁre or damage on the Sensor.
• Keep the load within the rated range. Overloading may result in ﬁre or damage on the Sensor.
• Wire the product cable separately from high-voltage or power lines. Placing them in the same 
wiring or the same duct may cause induction, resulting in the product malfunction or damage.
• Always turn off the power of the Sensor before connecting or disconnecting the cable or 
connector.
• Do not use the Sensor for the safety circuits in nuclear power or life-critical applications.
• Implement safety measures e.g. fail-safe circuits.
■ Installation
• Make sure that all installation screws must be tighten securely.
Tightening torque: M3, 0.5N·m (ZX1-LD50□□/ZX1-LD100□□) 
M4, 1.2N·m (ZX1-LD300□□/ZX1-LD600□□)
■ Others
• Do not attempt to disassemble, deform by pressure, incinerate, repair, or modify this product.
• When disposing of the product, treat as industrial waste.
• If you notice an abnormal condition such as a strange odor, extreme heating of the unit, or 
smoke, immediately stop using the product, turn off the power, and consult your dealer.
The ZX1-LD uses a laser as the light source. Lasers are classiﬁed based on EN standard (EN 60825-1)
■ Installation Location
Do not install the product in locations subjected to the following conditions:
• Surrounding air temperature outside the rating
• Rapid temperature ﬂuctuations (causing condensation)
• Relative humidity outside the range of 35 to 85%
• Presence of corrosive or ﬂammable gases
• Presence of dust, salt, or iron particles
• Direct vibration or shock
• Reﬂection of intense light (such as other laser beams, electric arc-welding machines, or 
ultra-violet light)
• Direct sunlight or near heaters
• Water, oil, or chemical fumes or spray, or mist atmospheres
• Strong magnetic or electric ﬁeld
• In the water, rain, or outdoors
■ Power Supply and Wiring
• Do not supply the power to the Sensor before checking the I/O wiring.
• Ground the FG terminal when using a commercially-available switching regulator.
• If the power supply line is subject to surges, connect a surge absorber that meets the conditions 
of the operating environment.
• Do not turn ON the power after wiring before making sure that the power supply is connected 
correctly; there are no faulty connections, e.g. load short-circuits; the load current is correct. 
Incorrect wiring may result in failure.
• Use a ZX0-XC□R Extension Cable (sold separately) to extend the Sensor’s cable. Use only one 
cable. Do not extend the cable for the Sensor to a length exceeding 20 m. 
• The display and indicators turn ON after approx. 2.5 seconds after the power is turned ON.
■ Warming Up
• After turning ON the power, allow the Sensor to warm up for approx. 30 minutes prior to use. 
The circuitry is not stable immediately after turning the power ON, and the values gradually 
change until the Sensor is completely warmed up. When using LD-OFF input for a long period 
of time, perform warmng up for more than 30 minutes after cancelling LD-OFF. 
■ Maintenance and Inspection
• Always turn OFF the power of the Sensor before connecting or disconnecting the cable or for 
making adjustment.
• Do not use thinner, alcohol, benzene, acetone, or kerosene to clean the Sensor.
• If considerable foreign matter or dust collects on the glass surface of the front of Sensor, use a 
blower brush (for camera lenses) to blow off the foreign matter. Avoid blowing it off with your 
breath. For a small amount of foreign matter or dust, gently wipe with a soft cloth. Do not wipe 
hard. The damaged glass surface may result in detection errors.
■ Sensing Object
The product cannot accurately measure the following types of objects: Transparent objects, 
objects with an extremely low reﬂective sensor ratio, objects smaller than the spot diameter, 
objects with a large curvature, excessively inclined objects, etc.

width while the displacement value is varying due to moving workpiece or low

● What is Hysteresis Width?
A point in which a judgment output turns from OFF to ON is called an operating 
point, and a point in which a judgment output turns from ON to OFF is called a return 
point. On this sensor, threshold means operating point, and a distance to the return 
point can be set based on the hysteresis width.
Note that the direction where the hysteresis width is set for the threshold differs 
depending on ON/OFF of the area output.
