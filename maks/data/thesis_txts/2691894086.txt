Abstract
This thesis aims to create a set of day­ahead market bidding strategies for the joint op­
eration of a wind farm with storage. To do so, the French electricity market and a wind
farm in the North­East of the country have been chosen as environment. To analyze it
under different situations, a period in which the balancing market shifted from two­price
to one­price settlement has been chosen.
The strategies performed can be divided into three different groups: Benchmarks, opti­
mization, and analogue approaches. The first one consists of four different simple ref­
erences created for the evaluation of the following groups. The second one consists of
a mathematical formulation that aims to obtain the best possible output in an attempt to
maximize profits. The last one is a data­driven approach that compares every day with a
historical database to obtain the best output. The last two groups have been subjected to
different sensitivity analysis in order to better understand their performance. To analyze
the real behaviour of the strategies, a real time operation approach that sets the working
principles for the storage was created.
The results obtained were promising, showing that the approaches perform differently un­
der the two balancing market settlements. During two­price settlement the benchmarks
showed the best results. However, they were outperformed under the one­price settle­
ment. Therefore, it can be stated that the objective of the thesis was achieved, obtaining
strategies capable of increasing the profits above the theoretical 100%, thanks to the use
of an energy storage system.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The energy system is making a shift from conventional generators to more sustainable
and environmentally friendly alternatives. Among these alternatives, the outstanding ones
are solar and wind energy technologies. These do not only reduce the amount of CO2
emitted, but decrease the price in the electricity markets, making energy cheaper and
cleaner. This technology switch implies huge changes in terms of energy trading in the
electricity markets, creating the need of new offering strategies that can improve the profits
for the generators.
Moreover, the European energy market has been imposed to change from a fossil fuel­
based system to the integration of more renewable energy sources, which in most of the
cases is still on its way. EU member countries expected to achieve a reduction of 20% of
CO2 emissions by year 2020 and 40% by 2030 [1]. This need to install more renewable
sources is a huge opportunity for green generators to increase their profits in the electricity
markets by improving their strategies and gaining participation in the energy mix.
The trading of the energy produced is a complex process that accounts for different stages
and markets in which the energy is bid. The profits achieved by the generators have a
huge dependency on its format, as they can be more or less affected by the penalties
imposed due to imbalances produced. In the same way, they are affected by the energy
mix, which alters the market prices. An electricity market with high penetration of renew­
able sources will have lower prices, as this kind of generator is able to bid at 0 €/MWh
due to its low marginal costs.
Renewable energy generators are also expanding their technology, trying to increase their
profit by new means. In the stochastic energy source, this is reflected as an improvement
of the forecast employed and the inclusion of new additional elements like energy stor­
age systems (ESS). These ESS have increased their focus lately due to the reduction of
prices. Despite hydro representing the greatest rate of energy storage, these small in­
stallations are gaining importance for small generators, like wind or solar. Among all the
different improvements that storage can produce, we can differentiate between market

==PAGE:15==
and technical system benefits. The focus of this thesis lies on the markets ones, there­
fore the technical will be neglected. The market benefits referred to here are the reduction
of imbalance and, as a consequence, that of the penalties. This means that the ESS is
used for storing the energy when it is unnecessary and releasing it when needed. Another
approach given is arbitrage, where the ESS is used for increasing the profits by storing
energy when cheap and releasing it when expensive.
For the computation of this project, different markets were considered at first. However,
due to the market characteristics and the difficulty to gather data, the French electric­
ity market was chosen to work with. This market is characterized by reaching 23% of
renewable energy market share in 2019 [2], slightly above their goals for 2020, which ac­
counted for 22% [3]. The remaining energy generation comes from nuclear power plants,
that covered around 70% of the energy mix, and fossil fuel technologies for the remaining
percentage [4].
The wind farm used for this project is a real wind farm located in the North­East of France
called La Haute Borne, in the small town of Vaudeville­le­Haut. Its commissioning was
finalized the 15th of January 2009. This wind farm offers free available data for a broad
period of time, allowing to select the best study time frame for this thesis. The data was
obtained from the wind farm operator ENGIE [5].
1.2
Objectives
The aim of this thesis is to generate and analyze strategies for wind plus storage producers
in the energy market. These strategies intend to improve the performance of the basic
ones by the use of an ESS, enhancing the profits generated by the wind farm. In order to
succeed at it, the following objectives have been defined:
• Understand the French spot electricity market.
• Understand the French balancing electricity market.
• Understand and implement a fully working optimization approach of a wind farm with
an ESS.
• Understand and develop a data driven approach.
• Analyze the benefits of the implementation of an ESS.
• Evaluate the effectiveness and performance of the different strategies considered.
• Compare and analyze different strategies.
• Evaluate the performance under different balancing market settlements.
• Implement a basic real time operation of the system.
• Analyse the results under the real time operation selected.

CHAPTER2
Problem Statement
This chapter gives a global explanation of the problem. Section 2.1 explains how the
French electricity market works, emphasizing the ones considered for the development
of this Thesis. Section 2.2 follows, providing an overview of the computation of the differ­
ent strategies and the assumptions considered. Section 2.3 explains how the data has
been retrieved from the databases, differentiating between real­life data, including french
market and wind farm data, and synthetic data, composed by wind and price forecasts.
Lastly, to enlighten the understanding of the real­time operation of the strategies, Section
2.4 shows how the storage unit will behave at the dispatch.
2.1
Context
Previous to the explanation of the different sections defined in this Chapter, the reason why
the French electricity market was chosen will be explained. At first, different markets for
the acquisition of the data were considered. Among them, Spain, France, and Denmark
were the ones that revealed the best sets. However, each of these countries showed
different concerns that condition the selection of the final energy market.
Spain’s market data was accessible through REE’s (Red Electrica Española) API, ESIOS
[6]. Downloading it was straightforward compared to the other APIs used in the gathering
process. Also, it allowed the download of both, spot and regulation markets at the same
time. The biggest issue came with the time spent on its download, as a maximum lapse
of one day was allowed to retrieve from the server on an hourly basis. Regarding the wind
farm data, the experimental wind farm of Sotavento was considered [7]. It offered a free
available set from last year’s operation, but due to different shutdowns produced on the
wind farm during 2020, the facility was disregarded and the selection of a different country
was considered.
Denmark’s data proved to be the most accessible among the considered markets, being
Nordpool [8] and Energinet [9] the main web pages for its download. The Danish wind
farms time series is relatively easy to obtain compared to other countries. Despite this,
the problem lays in the low penalizing electricity markets. This was a major determining

Lastly, French data was selected as all the disadvantageous factors from the other coun­

platforms, as will be explained in Section 2.3, leading to an effective working period of 591

To understand the different assumptions taken into account for the development of the
project, an explanation is needed of how the French electricity market works. This means
considering the different stages that will be important for achieving the best possible offer
in the Day­Ahead Market (DAM), generating the highest profits.
As given in [10] and [11], different products are traded during different times before the
dispatch time. The first product consists of futures, buying or selling energy in a time frame
varying from days to years prior to its delivery. It is formed by standardized products, such
as selling 1 MWh of energy. The purpose of this market is focused on hedging risks from
both sides, demand and generation. This means covering the risk that the future prices
of energy may generate.
Then, the DAM comes into play. Here, each market participant has to provide a bid, which
contains both, quantity and price at which each generator is willing to sell the energy bid.
The auction process takes place once a day for the whole year, and in it, all the offers for
both sides ­ demand, and supply ­ are displayed in aggregated curves, ordered by price.
The point at which both curves cross will show the Market Clearing Price (MCP), price at
which energy will be traded during that period. In the case of France, a MCP is defined
every 30 minutes, which causes 48 prices to be considered for the next day [12]. The
auction process takes place until 12:00 (noon) time known as gate closure. In the French
energy market, the prices at which the participants can trade are within the range of [­500
€/MWh, +3,000 €/MWh].
After this stage, the frequency reserve market steps in, as explained in [13]. Here, the
energy needed for reserves is set by the country TSO (Transmission System Operator),
Rte (Réseau de Transport d’Électricité). During the studied period, a change was made,
shifting from weekly auctions and delivery windows with pay­as­bid auction scheme to
day­ahead auctions, four­hours delivery windows, and a pay­as­clear auction scheme,
improving the flexibility of the market. The bids under the last format, have to be submitted
at 16:30 of the day previous to the actual energy dispatch, though, these can be updated
in the different intraday gate closures.
Then, the intraday market, also operated by EPEX, consists of seven gates where the
bids done in the previous markets can be modified. This gate is opened at 14:30 of the
day before dispatch and closes right before the delivery with 30 min length contracts[12].
Lastly, the Balancing Market (BM) is used to reduce the imbalances produced by the dif­

ferent generators between what they offered and what they deliver. The French energy
system currently works through a two­price imbalance settlement, which means that ev­

upward or downward regulation price is fixed to the MCP, depending on the actual imbal­

Benchmarks
As explained previously, this chapter consists of a set of different basic strategies em­

computationally cheap. These benchmarks have been created as blueprints, using them
as a comparison reference to the strategies conceived in this project.
Four different strategies have been defined in this section, studying them with and without

Benchmark 1. Perfect strategy (PS)
This Benchmark reflects the best possible case, in which all the real information is avail­

This strategy aims to check how much the other approaches will differ from the optimal so­

implying 100% efficiency. This strategy represents the maximum profit that the wind farm
can obtain.
It is expected that the case with storage will outperform it, improving the profits. The

Optimization approach
4.1
Description
The optimization approach consists of a stochastic linear problem suitable for obtaining
the best possible bidding strategy of the wind farm plus storage system used in this project.
Both price forecasts, BM and DAM, and the set of equiprobable scenarios and their prob­
ability embrace the formulation time­dependants inputs. The physical constraints of the
system are also used as input. The idea behind this mathematical formulation is to gen­
erate a DAM offer capable of performing arbitrage on an hourly basis.
The BM price settlement shift implies a formulation change, modifying the regulation terms
in the equations. Then, two approaches are created, one for two­price settlement, and
another one for one­price settlement. The main difference refers to the penalty of each
market. In the two­price case, a penalty will be applied regardless of the imbalance di­
rection. However, during one­price, the imbalance will be rewarded if it has the opposite
direction to that of the system.
The number of scenarios considered implies long computational times, as the approach
needs more iterations to achieve the best possible solution. Then, a scenario reduction
algorithm has been implemented to avoid it. This method will reduce the cost of finding
the solution and keep most information for its use in the optimization approach. It will
be explained after the optimization formulation, in Section 4.2.3, so that the role that the
scenarios and their probabilities play in the formulation is clear.

The objective function of this mathematical model, Equation (4.1a), consists on three well
differentiated terms.
First, the system profit, based on [28] [29] [30] [31] [32] [21]. It is the profit generated
by the system. It comprises the DAM income, the BM incomes or costs from penalties,
and the ESS operational costs. As already mentioned, the DAM term does not consider
the set of scenarios. However, the terms on the balancing do, as each scenario will have
different regulation needs given the quantity bid in the DAM. The ESS operation term
consists of the quantity charged or discharged by hour times the degradation cost of the
storage. Both terms, charge and discharge, are added. For every time spam, when the
charge has a positive value, discharge will be 0, and vice versa. This term will also have
different outcomes for each scenario due to its dependency on set S.
The second term represents the value of the energy stored for the next day. It is the
energy stored in the ESS multiplied by the average price of the following day. However,
considering all the energy stored will force it to charge during the last hours of the day.
The whole energy stored times next day’s average price gives a higher result than what
can be obtained from a single hour in that same day. To solve this issue, the energy term
is limited to the maximum discharge in one hour, the ramping rate d. This way, no charge
will happen unless greater profits can be achieved in the first hours of the following day.
The third and last element in the objective function represent the Conditional Value at
Risk (CVaR) [33] [34] [35] [36]. The goal is to modify the final values of variables and
objective function to obtain higher profits while lowering risk. Then, considering the 1 −α
worst scenarios and their probabilities, the DAM offer is modified. As a consequence, the
rest of the scenarios will lower their profit, but in case of the worst ones happening, the
profits will be higher. The application of CVaR implies that uncertainty is considered and

the form of a risk aversion factor β will modify the final solution, giving more weight to the
worst scenarios as it increases. The CVaR working principle is explained in Figure 4.1:

times the self­discharge rate, and adds or subtract the charging or discharging quantities
respectively times its efficiency. Equations (4.1c) and (4.1d) represent the discharging and
charging ramping limits respectively. Lastly, (4.1e) defines the lower and upper bounds

has been considered for the analysis, where α, the confidence level, has been set to 90%
and β, the risk aversion factor, to 1. Though, sensitivity analyses are run on these two
factors to evaluate the performance and the effect that the CVaR may have on the final

From the figures, it can be observed that the offer follows the actual wind trend during the
two­price settlement, Figure 6.6a. However, a negative peak is found to happen on the
9th of November at 20:00. This is because the downward regulation price is equal to the
spot price, meaning that instead of trading the energy in the DAM and possibly incur into
imbalance, it is better to bid nothing in the DAM and obtain the maximum possible in the
BM. The same thing will happen when an upper peak takes place, reaching the maximum
possible capacity. In this case, the upward regulation price is equal to that of the DAM,
implying that bidding everything is the right call to make to maximize the profits. Through
this bid, the excess of energy will be taken away at the same price that it was conceded.
Then, from 6.6b it can be noticed that the DAM offer is not following any of the scenar­
ios, but following the trend of the prices. Then, depending on the regulation price being
higher or lower than the DAM price, the bids will be the maximum capacity or nothing.
This happens as when the regulation price is higher than the spot price, it will be more
interesting to incur into downward regulation, this way, the excess generated will be paid
at the regulation price, obtaining bigger profits than if trading the real amount in the DAM.
On the other hand, if the regulation price is lower than the DAM price, it will be better to
bid at the maximum possible value, this way the revenue from the DAM per MWh bid will
be higher than the cost of regulation per MWh, obtaining a profit from the missing energy.

7.1
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to generate bidding strategies for wind power producers operat­
ing with an ESS that could outperform the most basic strategies currently used. For doing
so, two strategies were applied, an optimization approach and an analogue approach. In
each of them, sensitivity analysis were ran for improving the initial results, which were
found to be unable to outperform the reference benchmarks under a two­price balancing
settlement. However, promising conclusions have been retrieved from the analysis under
one­price settlement, giving ground for future research in this field.
The benchmarks used accounted for a perfect information strategy, that achieved the
maximum theoretical profit under two­price settlement. The use of the ESS and arbitrage
improved those results, increasing the profits of the whole period by a 22%. However,
perfect information is not available at the real time of computing the DAM bids. Then,
three more benchmarks where employed that had good results during two­price settle­
ment, but were outperformed during one­price BM scheme, by the advanced strategies
implemented in this thesis.
Regarding the optimization strategy, it was observed that the model worked in the de­
sired way when the input was the real values, as in Benchmark 1, Perfect Strategy. But,
when applying the forecasts, the imbalance produced and the RTO applied gave worse
quality results, obtaining lower profits than most of the references. Dividing between BM
schemes, under the two­price settlement, bad results were found due to the quality of
the price forecast used. However, and despite the forecast, the results under one­price
are promising, surpassing the efficiencies reached by the benchmarks and the perfect
strategy without an ESS by a 10%. Also, it was noted that the effect of the CVaR was
very low, implying a difference between using it and not using it of around 2,000 €, and
only effective during two­price settlement. This is due to the low importance given to the
Market participation of wind­storage energy systems
67
==PAGE:81==
scenarios under one­price settlement, where the offer made will be based on the price
difference rather than the difference in possible productions. The goal under one­price
is to bid everything or nothing and retrieve money from the BM. Then, due to that small
difference, CVaR can be avoided in future formulations that consider this BM scheme.
The analogue model produced similar conclusions to the ones found in the optimization
approach. Under two­price settlement, the performances are found to be poor, similar to
the worst benchmark considered (FV). Despite this, the results under one­price scheme
are promising, as efficiencies close to 100% were found. In the single case of considering
only the closest day and fixing three outputs, qDAM, qch and qdch, a performance ratio of
108.70% was found. It was noticed from this approach that considering three outputs
increases profits, since the prices follow the behaviour of the market. Therefore, the
direction of imbalance in the market was tracked in a better way than applying a RTO
approach. Also, the more days considered for the computation of the DAM bid, the worse
the final results will be. The best approach was found to be the first of this strategy, using
only the nearest neighbour, followed by the ones that give more importance to the closer
days.
Thus, the conclusions drawn from this study are found to be:
• It is not possible to study a one­price and a two­price settlement together, as results
may be completely opposite in each price settlement.
• The benchmarks used show the best performance under two­price BM settlement.
• The optimization approach is found to work in an outstanding manner under perfect
information. It is specially remarkable under one­price thanks to the perfect track of
the imbalance.
• Under optimization and two­price settlements, the results are worse than the most
basic benchmarks. However, under one­price settlement, the results are found to
be better than the theoretical value without an ESS.
• The analog approach shows promising results, but longer databases will be helpful
at the time of obtaining good results.
• The analog approach showed that under a two­price settlement the results are really
poor. However, under one­price settlement results can reach the values obtained
under optimization outperforming the theoretical maximum without storage.
• Results under analog strategies are found to be good for such a low computational
cost compared to the optimization ones.
• For analog approaches and under one­price settlement, the less days used to com­
pute the DAM offer, the better the results will be.
• If the price estimation follows the market behaviour, the more fixed variables con­
sidered for the analog models at RTO, the better results will be.

• Using a RTO strategy that reduces imbalance, reduces the costs from penalties but
also produces income losses from the BM side. Thus, implying poor profit improve­
ment from the ESS side.
7.2
Future works
The project presented here is subject of future research and investigation by means of
increasing the scope initially considered. To do so, the future works have been divided into
three subgroups. The first one states the technical modifications that can be made to the
approaches considered. The second one checks on ways to improve the performance
of the already considered approaches. Lastly, the third one analyzes how to verify the
correct working of the strategies.
The technical modifications to be made are the inclusion of intraday markets, so that
the offers can be revised throughout the day and there can be a bigger control over the
different variables. Additionally, the application of machine learning will enhance the per­
formance, as well as feature engineering to make a better selection on the data driven
approach parameters. Lastly, another improvement that can be made is the inclusion of
game theory, so that the effect of different wind farms into the market can be considered.
To improve the performance by making slight changes in the already considered formula­
tion, arbitrage can be used during the RTO approach. Also, for the data­driven approach,
bigger databases can be used. This would offer more choices by increasing the number
of comparative elements. Lastly, an imbalance­tracking approach can be considered for
performing better during the one­price period.
Finally, in order to validate the already created and commented strategies, their validation
over different markets and configurations should be performed. This can be done by
checking on markets like the Spanish or the Northern European systems, as considered
in the first steps of the thesis.
