Abstract
Today positron emission tomography (PET) is the go-to imaging modality used in the clinic to
assess cell metabolism e.g. in cancers, but this modality does have it's aws. Hyperpolarized
(HP) magnetic resonance spectroscopic imaging (MRSI) is an emerging modality, because
it is able to measure the molecular transformations of the imaging agent and thus image
downstream cellular metabolism. Especially the conversion of 1-13C]pyruvate to [1-13C]lactate
by the enzyme lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) is of clinical interest, and this project takes a step
back from in vivo imaging to the preceding fundamental in vitro work and investigates this
reaction utilizing HP nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) experiments. From such experiments
knowledge can be gained about the chemical reaction rate kpl, and it is the estimation of
the parameter that this project seeks to improve.
Data acquisition strategies are sought
optimized by evaluating dierent novel ip angle (FA) schemes provided by the development
and implementation of a double Gaussian pulse capable of selective excitation of pyruvate and
lactate. Simulated experiments provide results in accordance with the literature, but results
of actual HP NMR experiments are inconclusive. The project thus has investigated if the HP
state can be utilised more wisely and hence preserve the HP state and increase the lactate
signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). However, evaluation of it this provides increased reproducibility
and a more robust estimation of kpl necessitate further investigation.

Introduction
The current imaging modality used in the clinic to assess cancer metabolism is positron emis-
sion tomography (PET) with the tracer 18F-uorodeoxyglucose (FDG) reporting the local glu-
cose uptake in many cancers as well as inammation and other processes. FDG PET has indeed
been a successful approach, but it lack the ability to assess the downstream metabolism that
is important in understanding cancer and the underlying mechanisms more thoroughly. An
emerging modality is hyperpolarized (HP) magnetic resonance spectroscopic imaging (MRSI)
that possesses the ability to measure the molecular transformations of the imaging agent.
As opposed to conventional proton MRI and MRS, this new method utilizes predominately
13C labelled biomolecules (probes) to quantify dierent steps in cancer metabolism with the
potential to better diagnose and characterize cancer biology, predict cancer progression, and
monitor early response to treatment. [1]
One of the rst HP probes introduced was [1-13C]pyruvate, which remains the most widely
studied in preclinical cancer models.
Some advantages of this probe is that it is easy to
polarize, has a relatively long T1, and is converted via the enzyme lactate dehydrogenase
(LDH) to [1-13C]lactate. The latter thus provide HP [1-13C]pyruvate to depict the so-called
Warburg eect: The elevated lactate production that is an established characteristic of most
cancers. Increased LDH activity has been established as a predictor of tumour progression in
longitudinal studies of liver cancer models, and demonstrates the potential of HP MRSI as a
tool for prognosis and treatment planning. [1]
A way to quantify LDH activity is by estimating the chemical reaction rate kpl within a
voxel. Yet in order to use estimated kpl as a measure of cancer activity, it is crucial that the
estimate is accurate. Therefore eorts have been made into optimizing the data acquisition
and subsequent kinetic tting and parameter estimation of HP MRSI data. For instance by
Harrison et al. [2], Chen et al. [3], Xing et al. [4], Larson et al. [5], and Walker et al. [6], and
most of this work have been done in vivo with appertaining challenges. This project simplies
matters by evaluating an in vitro cellular system, and seek to investigate if the accuracy of the
estimated kpl can be improved by utilizing the HP state perhaps more wisely than the default
metabolic HP nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) experiments.

1.2
Modelling the system
As mentioned, the system under investigation is the conversion of [1-13C]pyruvate to [1-
13C]lactate from which an estimate of kpl as well as the longitudinal relaxation times T1 of
both metabolites can be attained. This is done by metabolic hyperpolarized NMR experiments
tracking the reaction over time until the HP state has relaxed back to thermal equilibrium,
and subsequent tting to the obtained kinetic curves yield estimates of kpl and T1's.
The system also includes some conversion of lactate back to pyruvate at a rate klp, but
things are complicated by the transport of lactate out of the cell: Harrison et al. [2] con-
clude that a three-pool bidirectional model provides the most accurate description of pyruvate
metabolism, but this includes mass spectrometry data to distinguish between intra- and ex-
tracellular lactate. When only evaluating HP NMR data they conclude that the ux through
LDH was well determined by both a bidirectional and unidirectional two-pool model. In this
project, the bidirectional model did not t the reference data and thus the simplied model of
only unidirectional conversion of pyruvate to lactate was chosen; a choice indeed supported by
the literature [3, 4, 6]. Therefore the kpl estimated in this project is an apparent kpl or knet,
and is a measure of the net conversion of pyruvate to lactate.

Background: Standard experiment
1.3
Standard experiment
The current experimental setup utilizes a short 1.5 µs hard pulse at power level 61 dB; cali-
brated to yield approximately α = 15° constant ip angle (CFA) throughout the experiment.
Both the acquisition time (at) and the relaxation delay (d1) are 1 s yielding a total TR = 2 s.
A total of 60 pulses are applied thus the total experimental time is 2 min, and by this time
the HP signals of both metabolites are essentially gone.
In the FTA, the frequency prole of a hard pulse is a SINC, and a short hard pulse ensures
a wide excitation bandwidth, since the rst zero-crossing of a SINC is inversely proportional
to the pulse width (pw) [s] of the hard pulse. In this case, 1/1.5 µs ≈667 kHz bandwidth,
and for such a high bandwidth all metabolites of interest will be ipped by the same amount:
Eq. (1.2) reduces to
α = γ · B1 · pw
(1.8)
that simply states that for a given pulse amplitude B1 the FA is controlled by the duration pw
of the pulse (scaled by γ). A tradeo is made about preserving the HP state, while obtaining
a sucient signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) for a quantiable lactate signal. Or  a new FA scheme
may be employed.
1.4
Proposed optimization
The employed FA scheme is under investigation in this project with the goal of improving the
standard acquisition strategies for metabolic hyperpolarized NMR experiments. For a fully op-
timized data acquisition strategy, all meaningful adjustable parameters should be investigated.
However due to time limitations this project focus on the FA scheme.
The mentioned tradeo between preservation of the HP state and lactate SNR, is sought
addressed by a selective ip angle (SFA) scheme. By exchanging the default hard pulse with
a suitable soft pulse  a shaped pulse that selectively excites distinct frequencies  provides
the ability to apply dierent FAs amongst the metabolites. This approach is supported by the
literature: Larson et al. [5] compares 5° and 10° CFA schemes with a SFA scheme with 1° on
pyruvate and 10° on lactate; in both cases the SNR of lactate improved along with prolonged
signal duration corresponding to preservation of HP pyruvate allowed to be converted to HP
lactate. Chen et al. [3] also compares 5° and 10° CFA schemes with two SFA schemes: One with
4° on pyruvate and 10° on lactate, and one with 10° on lactate, but 0° on pyruvate; denoted
selective non-excitation of pyruvate (SNEP). They nd that the latter SFA scheme decreases
the longitudinal magnetization decay rate compared with the CFA schemes. Simulated time
courses of both SFA schemes demonstrate enhanced SNR coupled with reduced signal decay,
thus illustrating the gain in dynamic range and signal detection over CFA schemes.
Therefore a part of this project has been to design and implement a shaped pulse on the system
available at HYPERMAG. The selective excitation of [2-13C]pyruvate and [1-13C]lactate by a
double Gaussian pulse in Harrison et al. [2] was the inspiration of the selective pulse developed
in this project.
An expansion of SFA is the variable ip angle (VFA) scheme. Here the FA not only diers
between metabolites, but also between pulses throughout the experiment. Many dierent ways
exist to come up with a VFA scheme, and some have been proposed by Xing et al. [4]and Walker
et al. [6]. Besides the iterative pharmacokinetic model for HP signal evolution adapted for
this project, Walker et al. [6] also evaluate three dierent FA schemes by means of simulation: A
broadband CFA scheme with α = 20°, a VFA scheme that attempts to maintain constant (and
maximized) signals for both pyruvate and lactate, and a VFA scheme designed to maximize
total (cumulative) lactate signal. They end up concluding that CFA or the cumulative VFA

Research question
To sum up, this project seeks to investigate if the HP state can be
utilised more wisely by employing a novel FA scheme and hence preserve the HP state and
increase the lactate SNR. The goal is to see if this provides increased reproducibility and a
more robust estimation of parameters kpl, T1,pyr, and T1,lac.

Pulse design
This chapter is about the generation and evaluation of the selective pulse made during this
project in order to employ a SFA scheme. All measurements described in this chapter are made
on an 1 M 13Curea (10% D2O, 15 mM Prohance, T1 < 2 s) standard sample, and performed
on a Varian 400 MHz NMR system with Oxford NMR AS400 Magnet. The software used are
VnmrJ (Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA) that includes Pandora's Box (Pbox), and Matlab
(R2018b, MathWorks, Natick, MA, USA). References will be made to manuals located at the
Agilent.com website: User Guide [9] and CP Ref. [10]. Also, note the distinction made between
a mathematical variable and a spectrometer parameter, e.g. pulse width can be either pw or
pw, respectively.

Methods
2.1.1
Pulse generation
The selective pulse developed was inspired by the double Gaussian pulse used in Harrison
et al. [2], but with some modications: Two Gaussian shapes both with a bandwidth (BW) of
350 Hz were combined to excite [1-13C]pyruvate (171.1 ppm) and [1-13C]lactate (183.4 ppm).
The carbon reference frequency on the spectrometer is approx. 100.56 MHz, and with pyruvate
chosen to be on resonance, lactate will be at an oset given by
(183.4 ppm −171.1 ppm) · 100.562 MHz ≈1237 Hz
(2.1)

a 0 Hz and a 1237 Hz component; this combined pulse was further divided by 3 (in order to
move the 90° pulse to a higher power level; knowledge gained from preliminary pulses). A
detailed explanation of the pulse generation procedure is given in Appendix A.1.

2.1.3
Pulse calibration
For a hard pulse at a given power level, the FA will depend on the pulse width and calibration
of the FA is obtained by arraying the pw parameter at a xed power level tpwr. [9] However
for a shaped pulse, pw must be xed to obtain the proper shape of the excitation prole [11],
and varying tpwr instead will change the strength of the pulse and thus the amplitude B1 and
hence the FA as stated by Eq. (1.2). Therefore the FA calibration of the double Gaussian pulse
is obtained by arraying the tpwr parameter. The array must be chosen such that a peak in the
acquired signals is observed for a value of tpwr; this power level will provide a strong enough
B1 to ip the magnetization into the transverse plane corresponding to a α = 90° excitation.
However, the FA might in practise be ± some degrees of this value.
For technical spectrometer reasons, the power must be changed in integer values, which
imposes a constrain onto the resolution of the resulting FAs; that is, the smallest available

Pulse design: Discussion
2.3
Discussion
The choice of pulse shape for selective excitation in this project stood between the sinc and
the Gaussian. Theoretically, an innitely long sinc pulse would yield a rectangular frequency
response. In practise such a pulse cannot be attained, and alternatively a truncated sinc would
be applied resulting in a somewhat rectangular frequency response still applicable for selective
excitation. However, the frequency response would by marked by ripples at the edges of the
pass band, which is not an issue for the Gaussian pulse and the likewise Gaussian frequency
response. Other advantages of the Gaussian pulse are the rapid fall-o and the lack of side
lobes in the frequency domain. [7] Also, given a sinc and Gaussian pulse of same length, the
Gaussian would in fact be more selective. [15] Therefore the pulse chosen for selective exci-
tation in this project was the Gaussian, and taken as a starting point was the pulse from
Harrison et al. [2].
For the nal pulse, the calibration curves of Figure 2.2 was only acquired once and hence
no statistics about the 90° power level can be made, as well as the FAs calculated from these
curves will be within some undened errorbar. However, calibration of preliminary pulses al-
most identical to the nal one showed similar results also regarding the estimate of L90. Thus
after all, the FAs obtained by the signal approach (Eq. (2.9)) are used as an approximation of
the FAs available with this double Gaussian pulse, and they are used for subsequent evaluation
of novel FA schemes.
The result in Figure 2.5 illustrates eect of violating the FTA: For low tpwr corresponding
to small FA the relationship given in Eq. (2.3) holds, whereas for increasing power and thus
FA non-linearities in the Bloch equation emerges and deviations from Eq. (2.3) occur.
The small constant signal measured at all frequencies at low power which is evident in
Figure 2.5 illustrates a pitfall of using the line reference method for extracting information
from acquired FIDs. If all signals contributing to the model are essentially the same, the
same amount of information will be extracted, which could be okay if all such signals actually
contained some (identical) information to extract. However, if the signals merely contain noise
the information extracted would be of little importance. From Appendix A.2 it is evident that
this is indeed the situation in this case: The FIDs at low power never rises above the noise
level, the calculated model becomes a model of noise, and the information extracted has no
physical value. This pitfall could be worked around by implementing some form of threshold in
the line reference method that discriminates between when to include or exclude a signal e.g. by
evaluating the autocorrelation of the signal which would be a way to exclude measurements
of merely noise.

3.1.2
Simulate magnetization evolution
The iterative pharmacokinetic model for HP signal evolution explained in 1.2 Modelling the
system is implemented in Matlab by the function simulate_2site_model.m developed by
Larson [16]. It is a matrix based approach that calculates the magnetization evolution through-
out an experiment utilizing a matrix of FAs providing a straightforward method for varying
these between metabolites (SFA scheme), and throughout the experiment (VFA scheme).
The function simulates the magnetization evolution in a two-site exchange model with FAs
applied discretely as α1, TR, α2, TR, α3, . . .  and during each TR, relaxation and chemical
conversion takes place. Besides TR and FA scheme, the function takes as input the chemical
conversion rate k and the relaxation rates R1 ≡1/T1 of both metabolites, along with the
initial magnetization M0 of pyruvate and lactate. The output of the function is Mz and Mxy
for both metabolites immediately after each ip. Mxy approximates the measured signal S
(cf. Eq. (1.7)) and thus provides the data for the kinetic curves of the experiment.

Discussion
Ideally, at time t = 0 the lactate signal should be zero corresponding to no HP lactate being
present at the start of the experiment. However, in practise it is quite dicult to start the
data acquisition exactly at the same time as the injection of the HP pyruvate into the cell
mixture. Therefore, data acquisition is started just prior (∼few seconds) to injection in the
hope of catching the rst tiny lactate signal with one of the rst pulses and thus dene t = 0
at this point. If one is lucky, the injection is done in a way such that the reaction starts just
prior to a pulse being applied yielding a tiny signal and thus nearly the full lactate curve can
be obtained, but if the reaction starts just after a pulse is applied (yielding no signal) chemical
conversion will take place during a time interval of TR and the signal measured by the next
pulse will be substantially higher than 0 (depending on k of the system). In practice, the t = 0
of the reaction normally starts somewhere during the interval of TR yielding dierent initial
signal between experiments  as is evident from Figure 3.1. That the rst point of the lactate
curve in data set E6 is much higher (almost 20 a.u.) than the rest (all below 10 a.u.) could
be because the injection was done before the data acquisition was started yielding more time
for conversion to take place before the rst signal was acquired.
The rst measured product signal is substantially higher than 0 and varies across data
sets, which can be translated into the curves having dierent origos. This could be a potential
problem in the kinetic tting if the underlying model included calculations dependant of a
specic time axis, however this is not the case for the implemented model, and therefore it
is not believed to be a problem. However, it might be a good idea to include modelling this
initial delay between rst measured signal and t = 0 of the reaction. On the other hand, the
problem can be  if not xed then certainly reduced  by decreasing the TR which would
correspond to a higher temporal resolution of the signal curve. This is an obvious parameter
to include in optimization of data acquisition strategies, but has not been a part of this project.
The increase of the T1 estimates of both metabolites with increasing FA (Table 3.1) reect
that it is not possible from the data to separate the decay of the HP state into the inherent
relaxation back to equilibrium from the decay due to CFA RF pulsing, and that this coupling
is more pronounced for pyruvate than lactate. This motivates the investigation of novel FA
schemes potentially capable of separating the two.
The simulated SFA experiment supports the idea that such a scheme would preserve the HP
state while boost the product SNR and signal duration. However, only a small improvement
in the estimate of kpl and T1,lac is seen. For the VFA scheme, the signal curves are severely
changed and also provide preservation of the HP state while boosting the product SNR, but
the change in parameter estimate might be for the worse.

4.1.1
Dissolution dynamic nuclear polarization (dDNP)
Various methods exists to achieve the HP state, but the one used in this project is the dDNP
method: The nuclear spins of a sample in the solid state are strongly polarized by means of very
low temperature (≈1.2 K), unpaired electrons, and microwaves. Subsequently the sample is
rapidly dissolved creating a solution of molecules with preserved HP nuclear spins. Transferring
the dissolved sample to a NMR spectrometer provides for NMR signals to be acquired with
an increase in SNR of >10,000 times than normal thermal liquid-state NMR. [20]

4.3
Discussion
Figure 4.2 clearly shows a pitfall of a selective pulse: The importance of the setting the trans-
mitter frequency (tof) at what would correspond to resonance in the selective pulse. The tof
measured from the May 10th CFA data and used for the experiments May 15th, should have
been re-evaluated on May 16th on the rst CFA data set obtained that day. Why this was
not done was solely an ID-10T error, and a lesson has been learned.
Despite adjusting the VFA scheme before kinetic tting, it was not possible to t the
VFA data. Subsequent simulation with the reduced VFA scheme (see Appendix Figure A.26)
did otherwise resemble the actual data curves obtained. This problem has not been solved yet.
In connection with the nal experiments, an experiment measuring T1,pyr to be approx. 42 s
was performed by supervisor Pernille R. Jensen. This is about 10 s faster than the 52 s mea-
sured in connection with the original reference data, and this may call in question the reliability
of the spectrometer or other experimental equipment.
From Table 4.2 it is evident that T1,pyr estimated based on CFA schemes match the new
T1,pyr ≈42 s quite well, whereas T1,pyr estimated based on SFA schemes match T1,pyr ≈52 s
quite well. The rst correspondence is believed to be attributable to the fact that a similar
CFA scheme is used for the T1 experiment; and as seen from Table 3.1 the FA and T1,pyr are
coupled. The estimated T1,lac is higher for both CFA and SFA experiments conducted in this
project than in the original reference data.
When it comes to the estimated kpl, the result obtained from the new CFA experiments
corresponds with the original reference data quite well, whereas the result obtained from the
SFA experiments suggest a slight increase in LDH activity in these cells. However not enough
data have been collected to infer if the dierence in estimates of kpl obtained from CFA or
SFA is of any signicance.

Conclusion
A double Gaussian pulse for the selective excitation of [1-13C]pyruvate and [1-13C]lactate has
been made, implemented and tested in a dissolution dynamic nuclear polarization (dDNP)
nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) experiment. The results show the importance of placing
the transmitter oset frequency (tof) at the frequency corresponding to resonance in the pulse.
Calibrating the double Gaussian pulse at a xed pulse width, but at dierent power levels
provides a way to assess the available ip angles (FAs) of the pulse. These FAs are then the
foundation for novel FA schemes utilising selective ip angle (SFA) and variable ip angle
(VFA) as opposed to the default constant ip angle (CFA) scheme. The line reference method
used for extracting information from the measured free induction decays (FIDs) needs to be
improved such that the method can discriminate between FIDs containing relevant information
and ones that are merely a measure of noise.
The default CFA scheme utilising a 12° FA, have been evaluated against a SFA scheme
with approx. 12° FA on lactate and approx. 1° FA on pyruvate. In agreement with the liter-
ature, simulations of experiments utilizing this SFA scheme show the expected eect namely
preservation of the hyperpolarized (HP) state providing increased lactate signal-to-noise ratio
(SNR) and prolonged signal duration. However, due to an error in the nal experiments the
same eect is not as pronounced in the experimental data obtained in this project. In the
subsequent kinetic tting of the data, the estimated reaction rate kpl was a bit higher for SFA
experiments than CFA experiments. However, not enough data has been collected to infer if
this is a signicant eect to be attributable to the change of FA scheme.
Another novel FA scheme examined in this project is the VFA scheme simply obtained
by arraying the observe transmitter power level (tpwr) throughout an experiment.
More
eort should be put into the exploration of VFA schemes than was managed in this project,
but simulations based on this simple VFA scheme did provide promising results similar to
those obtained for SFA scheme, so it is not a bad suggestion.
However, kinetic tting to
the experimental data obtained utilizing this VFA scheme was not achieved, and therefore not
parameter estimates were attained. The reason for the breakdown of the kinetic tting routine
in this case remains a mystery for now.
In conclusion, this master thesis project has indeed investigated if the HP state can be
utilised more wisely by employing a novel FA scheme and hence preserve the HP state and
increase the lactate SNR. However, evaluation of this provides increased reproducibility and a
more robust estimation of kpl, T1,pyr, and T1,lac necessitate further investigation.

A.1
Pulse generation
VnmrJ Pandora's Box (Pbox) software (Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA) creates shape pat-
tern les for experiments involving e.g. shaped radio frequency (RF) pulses. As stated in
User Guide [9], the goal of Pbox is to simplify generation and use of dierent waveforms in
NMR experiments, and makes the use of complex waveforms as simple as using ordinary rect-
angular pulses. When the shape les are created, Pbox provide all the necessary parameters
such as pulse width and power level needed for 90° excitation. More than 160 dierent shapes
are available from the Pbox library, but for this project only the simple Gaussian shape (see
Figure A.1) was used. The output of Pbox is a .RF le containing header information and
data columns with phase and amplitude information of the generated pulse.
Figure A.1: The basic Gaussian shape le utilized by Pbox.
It is indeed possible to create a double Gaussian pulse in Pbox by adding additional
waveforms to the pulse being created. However, a simple way to obtain a dierent FA between
the two metabolites is to scale the respective amplitude of two single Gaussians before adding
them to obtain a double Gaussian pulse.Therefore the next approach was to create both the
pyruvate single Gaussian (on resonance) and the lactate single Gaussian (at proper frequency
oset) in Pboxthen scale and add the two in Matlab, and thereafter export the data and insert
as new data columns in a .RF le. But two problems emerged: First o, the number of points
in the two pulses diered. This could be worked around by interpolation in Matlab before
addition of the two, but the result looked nowhere near the corresponding Pbox generated
double Gaussian. Secondly, the pulse width provided by Pbox diered for the two generated
pulses. Thus a mismatch between shape and associated pulse width would appear for atleast
one component of the double Gaussian, since changing the pulse width of a shaped pulse will
change the corresponding excitation prole.
Finally, it was decided to generate only one of the single Gaussian pulses in Pbox, and then
make the other  and subsequently the double Gaussian  in Matlab, as will be elaborated
in next two sections.
