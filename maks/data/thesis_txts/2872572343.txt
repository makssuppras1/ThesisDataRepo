Abstract
High Dynamic Range (HDR) imaging aims to present realôÙworld scenes with an extenôÙ
sive range of luminance and color, far exceeding the capabilities of traditional imaging
techniques (by factors of thousands to millions). HDR has not only gained widespread
adoption in the field of photography, but has also expanded into fields such as video proôÙ
duction, game design, and virtual reality. Despite its growing maturity and popularity,
content mastered specifically for HDR remains relatively scarce. Constrained by sensor
technology, most consumerôÙgrade digital cameras capture luminance information solely
within a restricted range, failing to meet the high dynamic range requirements of real
scenes. Consequently, HDR reconstruction algorithms, designed to compensate for this
hardware limitation, have emerged as a research focus. MultiôÙexposure HDR imaging is a
common approach to address this problem. It synthesizes multiple Low Dynamic Range
(LDR) images captured at different exposure values to reconstruct a scene with wider
luminance range and richer detail.
Due to the characteristics of multiôÙexposure images, multiôÙexposure HDR reconstruction
faces significant challenges, including motion artifacts introduced by object movement,
color artifacts from over/underexposure, and slow processing times for highôÙresolution
content. Leveraging advancements in deep learning, many studies have developed neuôÙ
ral networks to reconstruct HDR content. This paper, based on deep neural networks,
implements a series of studies on HDR reconstruction. The main contributions are:
(1) To enhance computational efficiency in video sequence reconstruction, this paper deôÙ
signs an optical flow alignment network incorporating a cache mechanism. The network
utilizes feature cache blocks to accelerate the model in processing video sequence reôÙ
construction tasks, avoiding redundant image reads and feature extractions. Specifically,
a multiôÙscale encoder extracts features from each input LDR imageëƒ features are concateôÙ
nated and merged only prior to the decoder. Meanwhile, features corresponding to the
current and next frames at the reference time are cached. Subsequently, for reconstrucôÙ
tion at the next reference time, only features from new frames require extraction, followed
by concatenation and merging with cached features. Experiments demonstrate that the
algorithm optimizes the processing speed and achieves faster reconstruction with a minor
accuracy tradeôÙoff.
(2) For the fusion stage, this paper introduces a reconstruction algorithm leveraging LaplaôÙ
cian image pyramid and Feature Pyramid Network (FPN). The core contribution lies in
aligning the lowôÙfrequency and highôÙfrequency components of the image derived from
Laplacian decomposition with the multiôÙlevel output of the FPN. This enables comprehenôÙ
sive utilization of hierarchical feature information across different frequency bands. The
fused representation is then reconstructed into the final HDR image using a weight predicôÙ
tion decoder and Laplacian pyramid reconstruction. Experimental results show that the
algorithm can effectively reconstruct HDR images while preserving fine contour details
and mitigating color artifacts.
(3) To further improve reconstruction fidelity and promote multiôÙtask learning, this paper
designs specialized loss functions and introduces a multiôÙscale loss term based on the
Laplacian pyramid decomposition of the input LDR images. This term leverages highôÙ
frequency information (i.e., the residual images from Laplacian decomposition) to guide
the model, enhancing the perceptual quality of the fused HDR result.

1.1
Background
The digital graphics and video, especially those related films, games, simulation and VR
(virtual reality) applications, have the growing demand for more realistic and vivid presenôÙ
tations, which requires higher quality displays and more reliable display software. This is
exceptionally difficult when considering imaging from the real world, which present a wide
variety of colors and intensities, in natural environments, the luminance level could vary
from 10ã3 cd/m2 (starry night) to 106 cd/m2 (daylight) [1]. But the images captured by
traditional photography techniques and equipment are low dynamic range (LDR) imagery
due to the dynamic range limitations of the cameraãs photosensitive elements and disôÙ
play. LDR imagery, a compromise for imaging and display technologies that are not able
to match natural scenes, usually store the color information with 8ôÙbit color depth [2]. As a
result, when the luminance range exceeds its capabilities, LDR images will be truncated
or compressed, resulting in loss of detail in highlights or shadows. Technically, the realôÙ
ization and promotion of high dynamic range (HDR) imagery is not difficult at the digital
level, but the lack of popularization of the supporting hardware market has restricted the
development of related technologies.

that of LDR imagery. It is usually represented by 16ôÙbit or even 32ôÙbit color depth, and
can capture a wider luminance range and richer details and color information. As shown
in Fig. 1.2, an HDR image could be generated by capturing multiple images of the same
scene at different exposure values. However, the most challenging issue is that when
considering nonôÙstatic scenes, the misalignment among multiôÙexposure images may lead
to the ghosting artifacts, reducing the quality of reconstruction. Therefore, extra motion
alignment is an evitable work before the HDR reconstruction, which increases the difficulty
of it.
With the development of artificial intelligence technologies such as deep learning (DL),
HDR reconstruction technology is also being continuously improved and optimized, and
its application areas are also expanding. In the medical field, HDR reconstruction technolôÙ

Challenges
HDR reconstruction is a complex interdisciplinary task that combines various technologies
and algorithms, requiring the comprehensive application in multiple fields such as comôÙ

are many methods for direct obtaining HDR content in practical applications, due to varôÙ

LDR content is easy to capture. The key to the reconstruction is how to reconstruct an
artifactôÙfree HDR version from multiple LDR data while preserving the rich details and
colors. There are some difficulties and challenges in this process, mainly including the
following issues:
ãÂ Motion artifacts: As shown in Fig. 1.3 If there is motion in the camera or the object
being photographed when taking LDR images, the difference between these imôÙ
ages will cause motion artifacts. These artifacts usually appear as blur, distortion,
and unnatural image structures, which will affect the visual quality and realism of
HDR images. To solve this problem, it is necessary to estimate and align the scene
to eliminate the negative effects of motion. Traditional HDR image reconstruction
methods usually use techniques such as pixel matching [4] or patch matching [5]
to erase motion artifacts, but it is difficult to handle the situation where there are
dynamic objects or motion blur in the scene, because these objects or blurs will
cause inconsistencies between images, thereby affecting the reconstruction quality
of HDR images. As a result, a more efficient and robust method is required to solve
these problems.

reconstruction results due to the differences in underexposed/overexposed values

Contributions of The Thesis
Building upon the technical background and challenges outlined in Chapter 1.1, this thesis
aims to explore and develop a multiôÙscale based HDR video reconstruction framework
combined with image pyramid from traditional image processing. The specific objectives
of this thesis are:
1. Cache for optical flow estimation: When performing regressive bidirectional optiôÙ

Related work
The high dynamic range image reconstruction algorithm improves the brightness dynamic
range and detail information of the image by processing and adjusting the pixels of the
LDR image, thereby reconstructing an HDR result with higher image quality. According to
the number of input images and exposure time, the HDR image reconstruction algorithm
can be divided into HDR image reconstruction of single exposure images and HDR imôÙ
age reconstruction of multiple exposure images. The above will summarize the research
status of the two types of HDR image reconstruction algorithms.
2.1
HDR Image Reconstruction
2.1.1
SingleôÙImage HDR Reconstruction
SingleôÙImage HDR reconstruction means utilizing sole one frame of LDR input to gain its
HDR version. Mathematically, it also is considered inverse tone mapping (ITM). Fig. 2.1
illustrates the pipeline of singleôÙimage HDR reconstruction via ITM, which aims to convert
8ôÙbit depth LDR image into 16/32ôÙbit depth HDR image.
Figure 2.1: The pipeline of singleôÙimage HDR reconstruction based on ITM.
Because of the loss of details in underexposed and overexposed areas introduced by
quantization and saturation of the camera or other imaging devices, SingleôÙImage HDR
reconstruction is a challenging task. And it has the following difficulties [7]:
ãÂ Due to the limitation of sensors, most digital cameras are still unable to completely
capture the luminance changes in the real world, leading to captured images sufferôÙ
ing from low dynamic range.
ãÂ When the camera shoots in lowôÙlight conditions, the light sensitivity of camera needs
to be increased to capture enough photons, as a result, captured images may have
more noise and distortion, which will affect the quality of the HDR image. In addition,
lowôÙlight images have a lower signalôÙtoôÙnoise ratio, and the inherent noise accounts
for a higher proportion of the image, so the noise is more significant.
ãÂ When the light intensity received by the camera is too low or too high, the sensor
cannot accurately measure the intensity of this light and thus cannot accurately reôÙ
construct the color of the image, resulting in color distortion.
ãÂ In lowôÙlight conditions, the camera needs to use a slower shutter speed to capture
enough photons, which can cause motion blur, affecting the quality of the image.
However, SingleôÙImage HDR reconstruction also has some advantages. For example,
most of the digital images contain only single exposure information, so SingleôÙImage HDR

3.1.1
Dynamic Range
The dynamic range of image means the luminance range between the maximum value
and minimum value of the image. Dynamic range denotes the ability of a device to capôÙ
ture/display details in darker or brighter scenes, and it also indicates that the camera has a
higher exposure tolerance and stronger resistance to overexposure and underexposure.
Bit depth or color depth usually quantifies dynamic range, that is, how many bits are
used to store and represent luminance information per pixel. For example, a LDR image
normally is 8ôÙbit depth, which means each pixel in this LDR image can represent 28 = 256
luminance levels. Usually LDR is sufficient for portraits or indoor shots, whereas the wide
luminance range scenes make it impossible to capture information of different luminance
areas at the same time. If there is too strong a light source in the scene, the image may be
overexposed to lose details of the bright area, appearing to complete whiteëƒ conversely,
if there is an area that is too dark in the scene, the image will be underexposed to lose
the details of the dark area , appearing complete black. These issues make it hard for
the camera to accurately capture the details of the real scene, limiting the application
and display effect of the image. In order to make up for this shortcoming, high dynamic
range technology came into being. As for a 16ôÙbit depth HDR image, there are totally
216 = 65536 levels, increasingly expanding its range, so that it can retain the rich details
of the scene.

Skip Connection
Skip connection skips some layers in the network and allows the output of one layer to be
added or concatenated directly to a deeper layer, which enables the information directly
flow through some layers. Thatãs to say, for the traditional neural networks, the output of
each layer is the result of a specific transformation of the output of the previous layer. In a
network with skip connections, the information of the previous layers as well as the earlier
layers is contained in the output of a certain layer. And skip connection is a module often
used in many deep learning models.
The motivation of the skip connection is to solve the problem of the vanishing gradients
and training degradation in very deep networks.
For example, the residual networks
build deep models and alleviate the training difficulties by introducing skip connection.
Of course, skip connection is not limited to residual learning. It is also widely used in
architectures such as UôÙNet [56] usually used in image segmentation, where the features
of the encoder are concatenated with features of the decoder to preserve spatial informaôÙ
tion. In addition, it is also used in Transformer [57] models for stabilizing deep attention

layers.
All in all, skip connection is an effective method to solve the degradation problem of the
deep networks and ensure the reutilization of the features.
3.1.7
Residual Networks
Residual Networks (ResNet) [58] represent a breakthrough in the training of deep convoôÙ
lutional neural networks (CNNs). As the depth of neural networks increases, they often
encounter degradation problem. ResNet addresses these issues by introducing residual
learning, which enables extremely deep network training with improved performance and
faster convergence. The key idea of ResNet is the residual block, which reformulates
each layer as learning a residual function with reference to the layer input, rather than
learning a function without reference. Mathematically, instead of directly learning a mapôÙ
ping H(x), the network learns the residual function F(x) = H(x)ãx, and then computes
the output as H(x) = F(x) + x. This is implemented using shortcut connections (or skip
connections) that bypass one or more layers by performing identity mapping and then
adding the output to the output of the stacked layers.
Figure 3.4: Residual learning: a building block. [58]
As shown in Fig. 3.4, each residual block consists of two or more convolution layers
followed by normalization layer and activation layer. The shortcut connection boosts graôÙ
dients flow directly through the network during backpropagation, greatly alleviating the
vanishing gradient problem. This enables the training of super deep networks, such as
ResNetôÙ50, ResNetôÙ101, or even ResNetôÙ152, with stable accuracy improvements.
The architecture of ResNet can be divided into the following stages:
ãÂ the convolution and max pooling layers to degrade the spatial resolution.
ãÂ the residual blocks to maintain a fixed feature map resolution and increase the numôÙ
ber of channels at the same time.
ãÂ the global average pooling and fully connected layer to generate the final output.
In summary, ResNet solves the degradation problem of deep networks through residual
learning, enabling us to train deeper networks. It is a major breakthrough in deep networks
and has been widely applied in many applications, such as the Faster RôÙCNN [59] for
object detection, Mask RôÙCNN [60] for image segmentation and so on.

scales efficiently. By integrating features from multiple levels, the network becomes more
robust to scale variation. Additionally, FPN does not introduce significant computational

Conclusion and Future Work
5.1
Status
In this paper, we first attempt to design a feature cache block in order to speed optical flow
alignment algorithm up (Model I). Then we address the problem of multiôÙexposure HDR
reconstruction under challenging motion, low lighting condition, and underexposure/overôÙ
exposure by proposing a novel, endôÙtoôÙend deep network Model II based on deep learning
based Laplacian pyramid reconstruction and optical flow alignment. Chapter 3 described
our two modelsã architectures, respectively: a flow network that reuses cached features to
accelerate bidirectional alignment, and a multiôÙoutput fusion network that predicts LaplaôÙ
cian residuals at multiple scales. Chapter 4 validated the approach on VimeoôÙ90K, SinôÙ
tel, Cinematic and DeepHDRVideo datasets, demonstrating various metrics like PSNRT ,
SSIMT , HDRôÙVDPôÙ2 and CVVDP scores compared to six baselines, as well as realôÙtime
inference (50ã80 ms per frame) on usual resolution. Extensive ablations confirmed the
value of our novel data augmentations, staged multiôÙscale loss schedule, and threeôÙlevel
pyramid structure.
5.2
Summary of Contributions
Our principal contributions can be summarized as follows:
1. Feature cache block for video processing: We propose a feature cache block
that is capable to reuse the repeat feature extracted from neighboring frame. And
the experiments show that the modifications to the network (singleôÙframe feature exôÙ
tractor) to implement the caching mechanism have made a good tradeôÙoff in speed
and performance.
2. Data augmentation for robust HDR fusion: We examine the effect of mainstream
augmentation pipeline including:
ãÂ Exposure reproduction perturbs the anchor frame intensity via randomly scaled
anchors derived from per frame maximum or highôÙpercentile luminance values.
ãÂ Noise simulation mimics noise in the low lighting area by injecting perôÙpixel
Gaussian noise with randomized standard deviations ü2.
ãÂ CRF variability applies randomized intensity of gamma curves to the inputs
during the training.
Together, these augmentations expand and enrich the diversity of training dataset,
ensuring our network is robust for different exposure value and complex noise disôÙ
tributions, improving generalization on low lighting conditions.
3. MultiôÙscale supervision: We leverages the natural coarseôÙtoôÙfine structure of the
Laplacian pyramid, we devised a multiôÙscale loss schedule. And the experiments
prove that the effect of our multiôÙscale loss function.
4. Comprehensive evaluation: We rigorously evaluated both Model I and Model II
on two standard test datasets (Vimeo and Sintel). Across various metricsãPSNRT ,
SSIMT , HDRôÙVDPôÙ2, and CVVDPãModel I outperforms other methods in terms of
inference time per frame, andModel II obtains fairly good score among six deep
learing based methods. Extended visual comparison shows that our method can
well preserve the edge contour information during reconstruction.

5.3
Limitations
Despite it performance shown in Chapter 4, Model II exhibits several limitations that sugôÙ
gest avenues for future improvement:
1. Objects with large motion in the underexposure/overexposure area: In scenes
containing very bright moving light sources adjacent to deep shadows, optical flow
estimation often fails due to saturation or low signalôÙtoôÙnoise ratio. Also, misalignôÙ
ment errors may cause slight halo artifacts.
2. Sensitivity to noise: While our noise enhancement covers a wide range of simuôÙ
lated Gaussian noise levels, real camera sensors may exhibit nonôÙGaussian noise
that cannot be tackled by our model. In this case, denoising quality and detail preserôÙ
vation may degrade.
3. High memory and computational cost: The multiôÙscale pyramid and optical flow
modules increase GPU memory usage and computational cost. On resource conôÙ
strained embedded devices or mobile platforms, it may not be able to deploy Model
II at a normal resolution (1080P or even 2K) without further optimization.
4. Single frame fusion only: Our network only processes spatial exposures once at
a single timestamp. It lacks the temporal consistency constraints required for HDR
video fusion, leading to flicker and temporal incoherence and reducing the quality
of reconstructed frame sequences.
5. Fixed pyramid level: We fix the number of pyramid to n = 3 based on computaôÙ
tional cost and structure limitation. However, as for extremely large or small images,
it may require deeper or shallower decompositions, thus, static level n is not the best
choice across all resolutions.
5.4
Future Work
Base on the strengths and limitations of our models, we outline the following research
directions:
5.4.1
Robust Motion Alignment
ãÂ Alignment in Feature Space: Considering that the warp operation used by the
optical flow method has obvious losses on highôÙfrequency edge feature (by interpoôÙ
lation), the pixel domain optical flow can be replaced by alignment in feature space.
This can also avoid the need to process two independent networks during training,
greatly simplifying the training difficulty.
ãÂ MultiôÙScale Flow Estimation: The pyramid flow network can be directly integrated
into the fusion pipeline to achieve coarseôÙtoôÙfine motion compensation that reflects
the pyramid decomposition.
5.4.2
Denosing
ãÂ SensorôÙspecific noise distribution.
Combined with camera model calibration to
learn the true noise distribution, including read noise, shot noise, and fixed pattern
noise.
ãÂ Joint denoisingôÙfusion architecture. Design a multiôÙtask network that simultaneôÙ
ously denoises and performs HDR fusion, and uses adversarial or selfôÙsupervised
losses to enforce practical noise suppression.

Temporal consistency of HDR videos
ãÂ Spatiotemporal fusion: Extend the network with RNN [80] to handle short video

flicker.
ãÂ Recurrent or memory enhancement module: Combine recurrent units (ConvLôÙ
STM [81]) or memory banks to propagate HDR features across time for smoother

ãÂ Dynamic Pyramid Depth: Deep adaptive pyramids that select the number of layers
