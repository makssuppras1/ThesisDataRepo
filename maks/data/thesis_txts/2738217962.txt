Abstract
Awareness of climate change has increased in response to global warming, mostly driven
by anthropogenic activities which constitute the primary source of greenhouse gas emisВӯ
sions. To mitigate the impact of these emissions, actions need to be taken. With biomethane
gradually substituting natural gas and its production potential rising, capturing CO2 after
biogas upgrading appears to be a valuable solution for limiting future emissions. The
value chain towards the utilization of CO2 after carbon capture involves the conditioning
and transport of CO2, which are the areas of investigation in this study.
The purpose of this thesis is to examine the energy requirements for the conditioning and
transport of CO2 along with its cost estimation. Therefore, the process design for conВӯ
ditioning CO2 was simulated including the compression, dehydration, desulphurization,
liquefaction, and nonВӯcondensable gases removal to reach the purity specifications. The
scenarios of producing CO2 either in liquid or dense phase were examined with the liquid
CO2 to be transported through tanker trucks and dense through pipelines. In addition,
alternative design layouts were investigated to assess the impact on energy consumption
and cost, considering different design decisions. The pipeline transport was also modВӯ
elled to estimate the pressure drop and heat transfer. The levelized cost of CO2 was used
to estimate the cost for conditioning and transport of CO2 for all process designs.
It was indicated that the cooling duty constitutes 45% of the total energy usage for condiВӯ
tioning, followed by heating and compression with 30% and 25% respectively. The feed
conditions as well as the water content affect the level of cooling duty required while the
regeneration of the desiccant covers the 45% of the total heating duty. Furthermore,
the comparison between the energy requirements for producing liquid and dense CO2
showed that the required level of energy for each utility is similar and that pure liquid CO2
demands 2% more energy due to recycling present in the design.
The design of alternative conditioning layouts proved that the number of compressors and
the absence of precooler have a significant influence on the cooling and heating duties.
The results showed that the highest deviations in the cooling and heating duties were
up to 37% and 55% respectively among the layouts. Compression energy requirements
averaged 0.11 MWh/ton of CO2 across all layouts, with turbines contributing to decreased
differences among the layouts.
Pipeline transport modeling highlighted that the pipe diameter has a determining role in
pressure drop level, estimated at 20 bar, with 6 kW of heat absorbed during transport. It
was also proved that the inlet temperature influences both pressure drop and heat transfer.
Capital investment greatly impacts the levelized cost of conditioning and transporting CO2
(LCOCO2), with pipeline costs elevating the LCOCO2 for dense CO2 significantly higher
than for liquid CO2. Truck transport constitutes 13% of LCOCO2, while the higher conВӯ
ditioning costs compared to literature are attributed to economies of scale. Finally, a
sensitivity analysis revealed that LCOCO2 is highly sensitive to capital expenditure.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Nowadays, climate change is one of the major challenges that humanity must encounter
since its effects put at risk not only humans but all forms of life on planet Earth. Such
effects are absurd weather patterns and phenomena, increased drought, elevated sea
levels, higher temperatures over the globe, survival implications for some species, and
lack of food. The continuously elevating EarthвҖҷs temperature proves that climate change
is an unquestionable reality and actions must be taken to reduce the greenhouse gases,
the primary driver of the global warming rising trend.
Greenhouse gases, vital for maintaining life on Earth, cause disturbances in the EarthвҖҷs
energy balance when present in excessive amounts in the atmosphere [1]. Carbon dioxВӯ
ide, a greenhouse gas among others, accounted for 80% v/v of the total greenhouse gases
in the European Union in 2021, with methane following in more than 12% v/v. Even though
CH4 has a greater global warming potential, it does not remain in the atmosphere for long
[2]. On the contrary, CO2 can remain for 300 to 1,000 years. The primary cause of the
greenhouse gas emissions is human activities, with the higher emissions to be detected
in electricity and heat production, transportation, and manufacturing and construction as
shown in Figure 1.1 [3]. Consequently, restricting greenhouse gas emissions is essential
in order to moderate the mean Earth temperature, which continues rising, currently being
1.1 В°C higher than the preindustrial period [3].
Figure 1.1: CO2e emissions by sector globally from 1990 to 2020[4]
Therefore, carbonВӯnegative technologies are considered a crucial step towards achieving
a lower than 1.5 В°C increase in global temperature by the end of the 21st century based
on the 5th IPCC report [5]. As for biogas, which consists of CH4 (~65% v/v) and CO2
(~35% v/v), an upgrading process is required so that CO2 is removed along with other
impurities. Capturing the CO2 after its removal would mean that biomethane production
will be transformed into a carbonВӯnegative technology [5].

In Denmark, 0.7 billion m3 of biogas was produced in 2021, according to Eurostat, which
corresponds to 32.4% of the natural gas supply in the country [6]. Most biogas facilities
are agricultural plants and are based on manure, located near farms [7]. Biogas was used
for electricity and heat production (96% in Combined Heat and Power plants), and accordВӯ
ing to the European Biogas Association, 78% of the biogas was produced in biomethane
plants, 51 in total. Furthermore, the Danish parliament approved formal conditions to
completely substitute natural gas with biomethane until 2030, while only 25% of the natВӯ
ural gas consisted of biomethane in 2021[6]. The potential of biomethane production in
Denmark as well as the current production and usage of both biogas and natural gas is
depicted in fig. 1.2.
Figure 1.2: Comparison of natural gas supply, biomethane production and potential in
Denmark (2021) [6]
Therefore, integrating CO2 capture technologies with biomethane production in Denmark
could notably contribute to mitigating the accelerating rate of global warming. The capВӯ
tured CO2 from upgrading biomass can later be used for methane production if it reacts
with H2. In particular, when the H2 is derived from PowerВӯtoВӯX (PtX) plants, the soВӯcalled
eВӯmethane, can be integrated into the gas system. As a result, the technical potential of
biogas production when introducing PtX could reach up to 74Вӯ94 PJ to 111Вӯ165 PJ/y by
2040. Moreover, CO2 can be used for producing alternative fuels, such as aviation fuels
or methanol and thus increase the renewable energy share in the energy sector[8].
Consequently, coupling CO2 capture technologies with biogas facilities holds promise in
the fight against climate change. Therefore, it is important to ensure that such a venture
is possible and investigate the means to make it feasible, in terms of both conditioning
and transporting CO2. Moreover, most studies are oriented towards the connection of
CO2 transport with storage rather than its connection with conditioning [9], highlighting
the necessity to bridge the gap in this research field.
1.2
State of the art
In this section, an overview of the stateВӯofВӯtheВӯart technologies and methods for Carbon
Capture and Utilization (CCU) are described. These are included to shape a solid theВӯ
oretical foundation and form the right research question for this study. Consequently,
the literature review focuses on the value chain of CCU, and all steps from capture to
utilization are individually presented.
1.2.1
Value chain of Carbon Capture and Utilization
CCU technologies have become popular in the last decades as they seem an opportunity
to reduce the CO2 emissions and mitigate global warming. Combined with H2 produced

from renewable energy as well as with other components such as water, CO2 can be
transformed into sustainable chemicals and fuels, thereby integrating CO2 into the cirВӯ
cular economy value chain [10]. The CCU value chain comprises six main steps: the
CO2 source characterization, the capture technology, the purification and compression
processes, the transport, and finally the utilization [11] as shown in Figure 1.3.

The source characterization is related to the information about the CO2 source such as the

utilization and finally CO2 is delivered and converted to the final product, comprising the
utilization step [11].
1.2.2
Biogas Upgrading
Taking into consideration biomethane production, biomethane is produced from biomass
undergoing anaerobic digestion. The product following the digestion is biogas, which
primarily consists of CH4, CO2 as well as water and some nonВӯcondensable impurities
such as hydrogen sulfide H2S, hydrogen H2, ammonia NH3, carbon monoxide CO, nitroВӯ
gen N2, oxygen O2 and argon Ar [12, 13]. These impurities, however, vary depending
on the biomass type. The process responsible for separating biomethane from the raw
biogas,CO2 and the impurities, is called biogas upgrading and accounts for 3Вӯ6% of the
biomethane production total cost[13].
The principal contaminant in biogas is the CO2 in a proportion of 30Вӯ40% [13] and it is esВӯ
sential to be removed so that biomethane complies with its sale requirements. However,
the energy demanded to perform the separation is not negligent, leading to lower effiВӯ
ciencies and higher production costs. The most common technologies applied for carbon
capture belong either to the absorption/adsorption or the membrane separation categories
and are depicted in Figure 1.4. However, the selection of the most economically efficient
technology is a strong function of the quality and quantity of the biogas, the final methane
quality and its intended application, the operation of the anaerobic digestion plant and the
stability of the used substrates, and finally of the local conditions at the plant site [13].
Absorption Based Separation
In water scrubbing, CO2 is absorbed by water in an absorption column under high presВӯ
sure. The biogas enters the column from the bottom of the column, which is filled with
packing material so that the gasВӯliquid contact is increased. The principle of this physiВӯ
cal process relies on the much higher solubility of CO2 in water rather than in methane.
The rich in water CO2 is then headed to a desorption column where CO2 is released by
applying air at atmospheric pressure [13].
Another established CO2 capture technology is the Organic solvent absorption/Scrubbing.
The most commonly used organic solvent for CO2 absorption is a mixture of dimethyl
ethers and polyethylene glycol. The principle of this process is the same as water scrubВӯ
bing, but in this case CO2 is 5 times more soluble in organic solvent [13].
However, the most commonly applied current technology is amine absorption/scrubbing,
especially for the absorption of sour gas (that contains CO2,H2S etc). The acidic CO2

Solid Sorbent Technology
The application of solid sorbents for CO2 capture is a conventional method and it is more
beneficial than gas compression in terms of energy, which requires high pressure and
low temperatures. The advantages of the sorbents are the low heat capacity and the high
chemical and thermal stability. Both adsorption and absorption take place simultaneously
as the guest molecules first adhere to the sorbent and then are transferred into its interior.
Regarding the regeneration of the saturated in CO2 solid sorbents, PressureВӯSwing AdВӯ
sorption (PSA) and ThermalВӯSwing Adsorption (TSA) are the two most common practices
for sorbent regeneration [13].
Membrane Separation
The role of the dense membrane is to act as a filter and selectively separate the target
component from the mixture. In particular, in biogas upgrading the membrane allows
CO2 to pass through, while the methane is retained. Contaminants in the biogas are also
separated from methane. The benefit of membrane application in CO2 separation is that
chemicals and solvents are not used and scaling up is feasible without highВӯefficiency
losses. However, its disadvantage is that the CH4 losses are >10% compared to the
aforementioned technologies [13].
1.2.3
Purification and Compression
Following the biogas upgrading, CO2 purity specifications need to be met. The specificaВӯ
tions vary in strictness depending on the means of transport, storage, and final utilization.
These requirements are fulfilled through the CO2 conditioning, in which water and other
impurities are removed. The means of CO2 transport also affects the conditioning design.

Depending on the means of transport, whether by truck or pipeline, the delivered CO2 is
either gaseous, liquified, or in a supercritical state.
The removal of impurities is necessary since they can cause equipment failure in both
conditioning and transport. In particular, water may form hydrates, contribute to corrosion
damage [15], or freeze leading to blockages in equipment and pipelines [16]. HydroВӯ
gen sulphide also has a corrosive nature and its presence can damage the equipment.
Additionally, it might react with other components during compression resulting in both
inefficiencies in the compression process and compromising the quality of the final liqueВӯ
fied CO2 [17]. NonВӯcondensable gases in CO2 can lead to elevated energy consumption,
too [17]. They have the greatest impact on the boundaries of the mixtureвҖҷs phase enВӯ
velope, shifting the bubble and dew point curves to higher pressures [15], thus requiring
higher energy to compress and liquefy CO2. Additionally, nonВӯcondensable gases must
be removed since they contribute to dry ice formation during liquefaction and transport
[9]. Finally, if nonВӯcondensable gases are not removed, larger pipeline diameters will be
used for its transport [18].
In the following paragraphs, technologies for impurities removal are presented.
Dehydration
The saturated water content in CO2 depends on temperature and pressure. At constant
temperature, increasing the pressure reduces the H2O content in the gas. Similarly, under
constant pressure, decreasing the temperature lowers the H2O content [19]. Therefore,
the amount of water in CO2 can be effectively reduced by applying a multistage compresВӯ
sion chain with intercooling. VapourВӯliquid drums are placed before the compressor to colВӯ
lect the condensed H2O. Compression is also a necessary step towards CO2 liquefaction
and reaching the final delivery specifications. However, this method alone is insufficient
for achieving the required water level. The compressed gas will always be saturated in
water at the temperature that it is cooled [19]. Consequently, additional dehydration proВӯ
cesses follow the compression chain to ensure water content in CO2 is present in traces.
Common dehydration processes are absorption using liquid glycols and adsorption using
solid desiccants.
Figure 1.5: Process flow diagram of dehydrationВӯabsorption using TEG [20]
Absorption with triethylene glycol, TEG, is a wellВӯestablished method for dehydrating
gases [21]. The wet gas enters the absorption column from the bottom in counterflow
to the TEG, which enters from the top as illustrated in Figure 1.5. The rich in water TEG

is then regenerated in a desorption column, after being heated up. Vapour leaves the
desorber from the top and dry TEG is cooled down while returning to the absorber [20].
This technology has a lifetime of 3Вӯ10 years and water content in CO2 can be reduced
to 150 ppmv. However, if the TEG concentration is increased, the water content can be
reduced down to 30 ppmv in the dry gas [22].
Similarly to absorption using TEG, the adsorption by using solid desiccants consists of
two columns as shown in Figure 1.6. In one column water is adsorbed while in the other
the desiccant is regenerated. The two columns are switched before the desiccant beВӯ

enters the column from the top, in a downflow direction. Hence, higher gas flow velocities
are developed and bed fluidization is avoided [23]. Regarding the regeneration process,
it is common for a part of the dry gas to be used for regenerating the desiccant. After
leaving the regeneration column, it is recycled back to the adsorber [23]. The regenerВӯ
ative gas is heated up before entering the column in an upВӯflow direction. This direction
accommodates the stripping of contaminants from the top of the bed, thus prolonging the
desiccant lifetime. Dehydration by adsorption can achieve lower water concentrations in
the dry gas. Molecular sieves have higher adsorption efficiency (<1ppmv [22]) than silica
gel (outlet dew point of Вӯ50 В°C [23]) and alumina (outlet dew point of Вӯ68 В°C [23]). The
lifetime of the molecular sieves is around 3Вӯ4 years [22].

However, solid desiccant units are more expensive than glycol units. TEG dehydration
has a higher capital investment cost but lower operating expenses. It is more difficult to
regenerate the adsorbent bed rather than to clean a solvent [20].
Desulphurization
The technologies for H2S are divided into two categories: physicochemical and biological
techniques [24]. However, physicochemical desulphurization is only presented due to
their higher H2S removal efficiency.
Absorption of H2S into a liquid solvent is the dominant technology applied. Absorption can
be either chemical or physical. Chemical absorption is applied at low operating pressures
while physical at high. Therefore, physical absorption is preferred for high H2S partial
pressures or high feed H2S concentrations [24]. As for the regeneration of the solvents,
chemical solvents can be regenerated by heating them in a desorption column, while
Thesis title
7
==PAGE:20==
physical solvents by TSA or PSA, air stripping, or regenerated like chemical solvents
depending on the gas quality requirements. The most common physical solvents are
Rectisol and Selexol due to their high efficiency in H2S removal, 0.1 ppm of H2S with
Rectisol and 1ppm with Selexol [24].
The adsorption process is also applied for H2S removal. In particular, the dry desulВӯ
phurization method has become popular because of its affordability, flexibility, energy
efficiency, and ease of operation. Adsorption is also categorized into chemical and physВӯ
ical adsorption [24]. Common absorbers used are zeolites, activated carbons, porous
metal oxides, and metalВӯorganic frameworks (MOFs). However, carbonВӯbased materiВӯ
als have the highest surface areas. This means that they present the highest adsorption
efficiency, with activated carbon to desulphurize the feed gas down to 0.1 ppmv. AdditionВӯ
ally, activated carbons are a cheaper solution compared to other adsorbents since they
are produced by utilizing easily available carbon sources such as wood, coconut shell,
peat, etc. [24].
NonВӯcondensable gases removal
The nonВӯcondensable gases need also to be removed. However, the specifications imВӯ
posed are so strict that make conventional separation inadequate. If so, a great amount
of energy is required [9].
The volatiles should be removed in a distillation column instead of a flash drum so that
the amount of CO2 lost in the purge stream is reduced. It is noted that an increase in the
condenser duty reduces the amount of CO2 in the purge stream while an increase in the
reboiler duty reduces the nonВӯcondensable gases content in the CO2 product. However,
an elevated operating pressure can lead to higher temperature differences in the reboiler
and condenser [9]. Distillation is considered an efficient method to separate the nonВӯ
condensable gases from CO2, reaching a purity level close to 100%. Its disadvantages,
though, are the high capital investment cost and high energy requirements as CO2 needs
also to be compressed before distillation [25].
Other technologies for nonВӯcondensable gases removal are advanced methods such as
absorption and adsorption towers, membranes, or catalytic processes [9].
1.2.4
Transport
The cost of CO2 transport is optimized when CO2 is in high density phase. These conВӯ
ditions can be achieved by compressing CO2 into a highВӯpressure gas or by liquefying it.
However, the option of solid CO2 is impractical to handle and store, even though its high
density [17].
Transport by tanker truck or ship
The CO2 in the liquid phase is transported by tanker truck or ship. However, trucks are
preferred for shorter distances since their capacity is around 25Вӯ30 tn of CO2. Additionally,
the CO2 emissions for short distances by truck are not significant when compared to the
amount transported. It is estimated that emissions from transporting 30 tn of CO2 through
truck for a distance of 25 km do not overcome 1% of the CO2 for a round trip.
Ship transport is preferred for transport of medium to large volumes of liquid CO2 over
medium to long distances [17]. Typical volumes of CO2 for ship transport fluctuate beВӯ
tween 1000Вӯ2000 m3. Moreover, the advantage of selecting this means of transport lies
in the ability to operate within a network, collecting CO2 from multiple locations [17].
The conditions of CO2 at which it is transported, can range from the triple to the superВӯ
critical point (Вӯ56 to 31 В°C and 5.2 to 74 bara), but it is common practice to be around 15
bara and Вӯ28 В°C [17].

Pipeline Transport
Pipelines are suitable for transporting large volumes of CO2 from large point source emitВӯ
ters to storage sites, export terminals, or CO2 utilization sites. In CO2 transport through
pipelines, it is preferable CO2 not to undergo a phase change, thus setting the minimum
operating pressure for pure CO2 at 80 bar, a pressure a bit higher than the critical (74
bara).
Figure 1.7: CO2 phase diagram[17]
By increasing the operating pressure, density is also increased by around 800Вӯ1000 kg/m3
at typical temperatures for buried pipelines in Denmark [17]. In particular, it is a great adВӯ
vantage operating CO2 in dense phase for pipeline. The dense phase corresponds to
CO2 having a greater pressure than the supercritical pressure and lower temperature
than the supercritical temperature as shown in Figure 1.7. The density is higher than in
the gaseous phase and the viscosity is relatively low compared to the liquid phase [15].
Moreover, small changes in pressure and temperature have a smaller impact on density
in this region. The highest operating pressure is set at 150 bar. This approach balances
a sufficient operating range (taking into consideration pressure drop) while maintaining a
reasonable pipe wall thickness, and thus the piping cost [17].
Pumping or compression stations may be necessary along the pipeline to overcome fricВӯ
tional losses. They are also called booster stations. By using centrifugal or reciprocating
pumps, the cost can be greatly decreased as they are both cheaper and less energyВӯ
consuming than compressors. Usually, the pressure drops down to less than 80 bar
approximately every 70Вӯ140 km [17].
CO2 can also be transported in gaseous phase. However, this option is costВӯeffective for
relatively low mass flows and short distances. It should be noted, though, that a larger
pipe diameter is required as well as the need for recompressing might be more frequent.
Moreover, lower steel grades for gaseous CO2 is adequate than when transporting liquid
CO2 [26].

2.3
Process Design
It can be observed that further processing is necessary so that CO2 complies with the
specifications. In this section, the main processes for the conditioning of the CO2 are
presented. There are two main process designs modelled, one for liquid and one with
dense CO2 produced. The process design of these cases is simulated in the commercial
simulator Aspen Plus V14. The processes that comprise the conditioning are explained
in detail as follows.
2.3.1
Compression Chain
The CO2 conditioning starts with the compression train. In both cases 1 & 2, the stream
is compressed and cooled down so that water is removed. In general, removing water at
an early stage is favourable to prevent CO2 and H2S hydrates [33]. The water collection
is achieved by adding knockВӯout drums right after the cooling of the CO2. At this stage,
most of the water content is expected to be removed as water solubility decreases with
increasing pressure and decreasing temperature [9]. The last free water should leave the
system at pressures between 20Вӯ40 bar and temperatures close to the hydrate formation
curve [9], thus expecting a considerable amount of water to be collected at this point.
In particular, each compression stage comprises a compressor, an interВӯcooler, and a
knockВӯout drum. The compressor is centrifugal and the process is modelled as isentropic
compression with 85% efficiency [34]. The motor efficiency of the compressor is set to
90% [35]. Regarding the compression ratio of the compressors, the ratio varies among
the stages, from higher to lower, starting from 3.5 and ending at 2.5. Such a decision
causes the system to deviate more from the ideal isothermal compression Вӯin contrast to
a fixed pressure ratioВӯ but mitigates capital investment. As for the level of the pressure
ratio, in theory, the pressure ratio a centrifugal compressor can perform depends on the
gas composition and gas temperature. The aid of the intercoolers, however, allows for
higher pressure ratios [36].
14
Thesis title

The intercooler is modelled as a shellВӯandВӯtube heat exchanger with countercurrent flow,
chosen for its ability to handle highВӯpressure fluids and accommodate various fluid phases
[37]. The role of the interВӯcooler is to extract heat from the CO2 and thus approach the
isothermal compression process. In this way, irreversibilities are reduced, and finally, less
energy input is required [38]. In addition, the CO2 is cooled down by the use of cooling
water produced by cooling towers available on the site. The inlet temperature of cooling
water is 20 В°C and is heated up to 40 В°C, falling within the typical return temperature range
of 40Вӯ46 В°C [39]. The minimum temperature difference in the heat exchanger is modeled
as 5 В°C and all intercoolers are set to cool the CO2 down to 25 В°C except the last stage
which is at 30 В°C. Pressure losses in the intercoolers are modelled with 0.4 bar pressure
drop, as intercoolers usually have varying pressure drops between 0.35 Вӯ 1 bar, which
is a function of the cooler design [36]. The heat transfer coefficient U is assumed to be
constant and equal to 1000 W/m2K. This decision derives from a sensitivity analysis on
the heat exchangers presented in subsection 3.7.1.
Finally, the knockВӯout drums are put right before the compressors so that the condensate
produced from the intercooler is accumulated. In this way, not only water is removed,
an impurity in the CO2, but at the same time damage to the compressor is avoided. The
drums are also modelled as there are no heat losses and a pressure drop of 0.2 bar exists
as they usually exhibit quite lowВӯpressure losses [40].
At the end of the compression chain the CO2 heads to the dehydration unit at 30 В°C and
40 bar.
2.3.2
Dehydration Unit
Following the compression chain, the dehydration of CO2 occurs. Even though most
water is removed at the first stage, the water content in the CO2 does not comply with the
final specifications. At this point, it should be noted that dehydration is performed before
desulphurization for the desulphurization technology chosen [41].
The drying technology selected for this project is the Temperature Swing Adsorption (TSA)
with molecular sieves as the adsorbent. Types 3A and 4A are both suitable for CO2 deВӯ
hydration [22]. The 3A is chosen for this project as the 4A molecular sieves are preferred
for static dehydration in closed drying systems [42]. This technology is selected because
of its efficiency ([H2O]<1 ppmv) compared to the other available technologies covered in
subsubsection 1.2.3. Achieving such low water concentrations is essential for preventing
ice formation in the nonВӯcondensable gases removal stage, which is upcoming later in the
conditioning [43]. Usually in processes where cryogenic temperatures are applied, solid
desiccant dehydration is preferred over conventional methanol injection to avoid hydrate
and ice formation [23]. It should be noted that the H2S is corrosive for the molecular sieves
and more resistant materials are used in such cases [33]. Possible damage failure could
be acid degradation of sieve, formation of SO2 in the presence of O2 and free sulphur [22].
However, in this study the H2S concentration in mol in the incoming stream is two orders
of magnitude lower than water, expecting not to have a great influence on the sieves. This
is also a reason to prioritize dehydration in the conditioning as water could cause greater
damage to the activated carbon due to its higher concentration in CO2.
The operational conditions for dehydration are 40 bar and 30 В°C. Low temperatures and
high pressures favour the adsorption process, and specifically molecular sieves have sigВӯ
nificantly higher adsorptive capacity at low temperatures [44]. Typical operating conditions
for adsorption with molecular sieves are 40Вӯ60 bar and 30Вӯ40 В°C [45, 22]. The pressure
drop is calculated to be 0.24 bar as described in subsubsection 2.6.2 and the dry CO2 is
set to contain 1 ppm of H2O at the exit of the adsorber. Furthermore, the operating cycle

2.3.3
Desulphurization
The following process is desulphurization. The technology chosen for H2S removal is
chemical adsorption using impregnated activated carbon as the adsorbent because of its
simplicity and effectiveness ([H2S]<0.1 ppm) [41].
The optimal operating conditions for removing H2S using activated carbon is 7Вӯ8 bar and
50Вӯ70В°C [41]. In this project, the operating conditions are 7 bar and 52В°C. The pressure
drop in the adsorber is considered equal to the corresponding drop in the dehydration proВӯ
cess, as a detailed calculation was not possible. In comparison with the molecular sieves,
it is decided that the activated carbon does not undergo regeneration but is replaced, as
it is a common practice in the industry[47]. However, the breakthrough capacity of the
activated cannot be defined as no specific activated carbon is chosen in this study. ConВӯ
sequently, the replacement of the adsorbent is chosen to be annual as this is a typical
replacement period in case of water filtering in the industry [48]. As a result, no utilities
are required and the only operational cost is the replacement of the adsorbent.
To reach these conditions, however, the dry CO2 undergoes heating and then expansion
through electric heaters and centrifugal turbines. The turbines are simulated with 85%
isentropic efficiency and 90% motor efficiency. Regarding the heaters, they are simuВӯ
lated as simple heaters with a 0.4 bar pressure drop as in the intercoolers. Two heaters
and turbines are required to reach the desulphurization operating conditions. The adВӯ
sorption column is simulated with a separator shortcut by setting the concentration of the
desulphurized CO2 0.05 ppm. The decision upon the H2S concentration is made to enВӯ
sure that the specification of H2S is met in the final pure CO2 as CO2 loss is expected to
occur in the upcoming processes, meaning that the concentration of H2S in the final CO2
stream will rise.
2.3.4
NonВӯcondensable Gases Removal
The last impurities remaining in the CO2 are the N2, O2 and Ar. It is feasible to remove
all impurities in a single cryogenic distillation process. As they all are more volatile than
CO2 [noauthor_co2_nodate] they will be distributed in the distillate. On the contrary,
H2O and H2S are distributed in the bottom product along with CO2. This technology is
selected because the impurities removal is almost 100% [noauthor_co2_nodate].
However, the CO2 firstly needs to be cooled down and liquefied before entering the distillaВӯ
tion column. It is achieved by a precooler leaving the CO2 at 25 В°C and then a refrigeration
cycle to accomplish liquefaction at temperatures below zero. The precooler is modelled
as the corresponding ones in the compression chain and the liquefier as a shellВӯtube heat
exchanger with heat transfer coefficient U=1500 W/Km2 and a pressure drop of 0.4 bar.
It is considered part of an ammonia refrigeration cycle with two compression stages [49].
Regarding the operating conditions, the selection of p,T must be critically taken into acВӯ
count as they strongly influence the relative volatility of the impurities and thus the sepВӯ
aration efficiency. At the same time, the operating pressure has a strong impact on the
power consumption in the refrigeration cycle as at higher liquefaction pressures the COP
of the refrigeration cycle increases leading to lower energy consumption. On the contrary,
lower inlet temperature requires higher energy consumption.
The separation efficiency is focused on the O2 removal. The N2 is the most volatile while
Ar is the least. However, Ar concentration is already below the maximum allowed. The
relative volatility of O2 to the CO2 increases as temperature decrease [50]. Consequently,
low temperatures favour the distillation process. The same phenomenon is true for the
relative volatility of N2 and CO2 [5]. Pressure affects the relative volatility, too. The relaВӯ
Thesis title
17
==PAGE:30==
tive volatility decreases as pressure increases. Moreover, the rate of decrease is a strong
function of temperature. Relative volatility decreases to a greater extent in lower temperВӯ
atures [5]. However, the CO2 recovery ratio increases under higher pressures [51].
In this project, the operating conditions are set to Вӯ35В°C and 28 bar. These conditions have
occurred after optimization of compressing and purifying CO2 according to the lowest
energy consumption possible based on the study of Posch et al. [52]. In addition, a
temperature range of Вӯ45 to Вӯ28 В°C is recommended for better CO2 Вӯ O2 separation [53].
The COP of the refrigeration cycle is 5.54 under this operating pressure [49].
About the design of the distillation column, the feed stage is selected to be the second.
As the impure CO2 passes through more equilibrium stages until it reaches the reboiler,
it leaves the column in a purer state. The pressure drop in the column is also assumed
to be negligible and the condenser is modelled as partial. The number of stages and
the distillateВӯtoВӯfeed ratio are determined through a parametric analysis described in secВӯ
tion 3.8 for both liquid and dense CO2 produced. Finally, the reflux ratio is set at 1.2. The
reflux ratio is calculated by the method suggested by Biegler [35] and multiplied by 1.2
based on a heuristic method [54].

In this section, different design layouts are modelled and compared with the main cases.
These modified layouts aim to analyze the tradeВӯoffs from both an energy requirement and
an economic point of view. The basis of the sensitivity is the original layout for producing
dense CO2.
2.4.1
Layout 1
Layout 1, dense CO2 is produced by separating the nonВӯcondensable gases under highВӯ
pressure distillation. As already mentioned, the CO2 recovery in the bottom product beВӯ
comes higher as the operating pressure increases. At high pressures, the differences in
boiling points of CO2 вҲ’CO2 remain sufficiently distinct, allowing for an effective distillation
[51]. Except for the higher separation ratio of CO2, the higher pressure enables distillation
to be performed under ambient temperatures. Consequently, it is possible to condense
the distillate with cooling water instead of the requirement of the refrigeration cycle. FurВӯ
thermore, the liquefaction will be performed under higher pressure. Therefore, the COP
of the cycle will be increased resulting in less power consumption.
The distillation operating pressure in layout 1 is selected at 50 bar. Higher pressures are
not considered since the purity of CO2 decreases to a greater extent at higher pressures.
The inlet temperature of the distillation column is also reduced to improve the separation
process. The temperature in this case is Вӯ45В°C. Moreover, one more compression stage
is added after the desulphurization. The pressure ratio of the compressor is 4.4 and the
cooler that follows cools CO2 down to 25 В°C before it enters the next compressor. The
process flow diagram is depicted in Figure 2.3.

2.5
Transport of CO2
2.5.1
Pipelines
The CO2 entering the pipeline can be considered pure since the contaminants only appear
in traces in the CO2 stream. Taking this into account, the highest and lowest pressure in
the pipeline are set according to the following approach.
The lowest allowable pressure is defined as the pressure at which the CO2 remains at
the dense phase, above the critical pressure of 75 bar. In this way, a twoВӯphase flow
is avoided. Therefore, the lowest allowable pressure is set equal to 80 bar ensuring a
margin of 5 bar. On the other hand, the highest pressure, which corresponds to the inlet
pressure in the pipeline, is a design parameter, since it coincides with the pumpвҖҷs outlet
pressure. This selection resulted from a sensitivity analysis presented in section 3.4. In
this analysis, the pressure drop and the required work of the pump are expressed as a
function of the inlet pressure of the pipeline. Based on these results, which are illustrated
Figure 3.3 the inlet pressure is set to 110 bar.
It should be noted that some assumptions were made in this analysis. The length of the
pipeline is 30 km and no booster station is required as they are required for larger disВӯ
tances. Consequently, the size of the pipe is determined as the smallest existing that CO2
can be transported without reaching an outlet pressure less than the lowest acceptable.
The maximum allowable stress in the pipe is not neglected. Thickness is the parameter
to ensure that no material failure will occur. The pipe dimensions are based on data for
stainless steel grade TP316 ASTM A312. Furthermore, it is ensured that the velocity deВӯ
veloped in the pipe does not exceed 2 m/s [17]. The roughness of the pipe is set to 45 Вөm,
a typical roughness for welded commercial steel pipes [55]. No inclination of the pipeline
is considered.

By observing Figure 3.3, it can be concluded that in both cases the diameter of the pipe
appears as the dominant variable to affect the pressure drop during CO2 transport. This
loss is strongly influenced by the inner diameter, as it follows a oneВӯtoВӯthird power relaВӯ
tionship. This realization means that a smaller diameter translates to increased velocity
leading to a more turbulent flow and thus to greater pressure losses. Hence, the pressure
drop developed in pipelines of the same size is comparable, with the inlet conditions not
playing a significant role in this context.
In particular, the pressure drop increases as the inlet pressure and temperature decrease
provided the pipe diameter remains constant. This result is primarily due to changes in
the inlet density. As the inlet pressure and temperature in the pipeline decrease, density
decreases leading to an elevated pressure drop. This can be understood by considering
the pВӯlog(v) diagram of CO2. The isotherms of CO2 become less steep in the liquid phase
area as the temperature approaches the critical temperature. Additionally, the inlet temВӯ
peratures do not vary significantly for the inlet pressures examined. So, from one inlet
state to the next, CO2 is located more and more to the right part of the graph, meaning
that the specific volume increases and thus density decreases. This indicates that the
inlet pressure plays a greater impact on density than temperature.
Regarding the power required for pumping CO2, the log(p)Вӯh diagram shows that isotherms
become more distant as the temperature rises above 100 bar. This indicates a larger
34

==PAGE:47==
difference in density from one isotherm to the other, resulting in CO2 adopting a more
gasВӯlike phase. Such a behaviour makes it more difficult for the pump to compress the
CO2. Consequently, the slope of the pump power curve becomes less steep for pressures
above 100 bar reaching higher outlet enthalpies.
The velocity development within the pipeline is also dependent on the density, as velocity
is derived from the volumetric flow rate, a strong function of density. The results indicate
that CO2 reaches the soil temperature, regardless of whether the inlet temperature is
higher or lower. Consequently, for the same pipe inner diameter velocity appears lower
for the higher inlet pressures.
These results are common for both original and layout 1 cases. However, higher pressure
losses occur in the case of layout 1. The distillation is performed at a higher temperature

causes the CO2 to have a lower density, which in turn leads to an increased pressure drop,
around 27% higher. Nevertheless, these temperature differences are minor, causing inlet

3.7.1
Heat Transfer Coefficient of Heat Exchangers
In this subsection, the sensitivity analysis of the heat transfer coefficient U of the heat
exchangers is presented. This analysis is performed to estimate the appropriate heat
transfer coefficient for the heat exchangers and how it affects the heat transfer area.
First of all, the resistances for heat transfer in the heat exchangers are not expected to be
significant. Regarding the conduction present in the metal tube wall, the heat resistance
is expected to be negligible as the metal is highly conductive and thin. Fouling formation
will not be considered relevant as particles are absent in the CO2 stream and the O2
concentration is quite low to contribute to oxides formation on the tube wall. Assuming
a close water network, it would mean that water does not contain contaminants and the
fouling formation in the inner area of the tube is also negligible. Lastly, the flow inside the
Thesis title
39
==PAGE:52==
shell is expected to be turbulent. Most heat exchangers are designed in this way so that
convective heat transfer is enhanced.
The area of the heat exchanger significantly influences the velocity patterns of the flow,
which in turn affects friction development. Consequently, the diffusive patterns of the
CO2 flow within the heat exchanger enhance convective heat transfer. Additionally, the
CO2 conductivity rises with higher temperature, a true case as the outlet temperature
of the compressors is quite high. This convective heat transfer is reflected in the heat
transfer coefficient U. Therefore, the heat transfer coefficient is expected to be high. In
addition, the area of the heat exchanger depends on the chosen value of U. Selecting an
appropriate U coefficient is crucial to ensure the required area for each heat exchanger is
realistic and to minimize uncertainties in the capital cost.

4
Discussion
This chapter aims to highlight the contribution of this study to the field and identify potential
areas for further research. Hence, the limitations and improvements of the models are
discussed. In addition, results are compared to similar studies in the literature. It is divided
into three sections: conditioning, transport, and levelized cost per tn CO2 produced.
4.1
Conditioning
4.1.1
Modelling compression chain
First of all, compression is modelled as adiabatic and reversible. Intercooling could conВӯ
tribute to simulating isothermal compression but would require many more compression
stages with smaller and equal pressure ratios. So, heat losses are present in this process
and are not considered in the modelling.
Heat losses are also neglected in the knockout drums. The equilibrium temperature in
most drums is 25В°C, higher than the 8.3 В°C average ambient temperature in Denmark
[79]. Heat losses will be present if an insulation jacket is not added to the vessels.
4.1.2
Modelling Dehydration and Desulphurization
As for the adsorption processes, they are both modelled after certain assumptions. The
H2O and H2S concentration in the dry and desulphurized stream respectively is modeled
as fixed, which is an ideal case. Adsorption efficiency is also not considered. For example,
some H2S can be removed during the dehydration. In the regeneration process of the
molecular sieves, where high temperature is developed, the sieves act as catalysts with
H2S to form COS, thus depositing sulphur on the bed [44].In addition, a small amount of
CO2 can also be adsorbed in the molecular sieves due to the polar nature of both CO2
and H2O. Similar effects could be present in the activated carbon since CO2 and H2S are
both acid components [noauthor_hydrogen_nodate]. Adsorption, whether it is physical
or chemical, is an exothermic process [noauthor_hydrogen_nodate]. Consequently,
heat losses should also be expected during adsorption. These losses are reflected in
neither of the processes. They are regarded only in the regeneration of the molecular
sieves.
Regarding desulphurization, the adsorption capacity considered is based on activated
carbon that can achieve an H2S outlet concentration close to the target, but not as low as
required. Additionally, the desulphurization process is not based on a specific activated
carbon. As a consequence, it is challenging to accurately calculate the amount of actiВӯ
vated carbon required, the size of the adsorption column, and the time available until the
activated carbon breakthrough.
Moreover, the chemical reaction in the H2S adsorption is disregarded in the simulation.
However, it was not possible to simulate adsorption processes in the software used. AdВӯ
ditionally, studying the H2S adsorption mechanism in detail is not part of the project objecВӯ
tives. The same applies to the H2O adsorption. Nevertheless, the energy requirements
are sufficiently represented as pressure losses are approximated through a slightly eleВӯ
vated inlet pressure in the adsorber.
4.1.3
Modelling NonВӯcondensable Gases Removal
The distillation for the nonВӯcondensable gases removal is not designed in detail, resulting
in many uncertainties in the design parameters. Both the minimum reflux ratio and number
44
Thesis title
==PAGE:57==
of trays are not calculated based on established methods. However, the main drawback is
omitting the pressure loss in the column. HighВӯoperating columns feature higher liquid flow
rates, resulting in a higher liquid holdВӯup on the trays and thus increased weir heights. As a
consequence, the vapour pressure drop will be higher at higher pressures given a vapour
load [64]. Furthermore, pressure drop can have a great impact on the volatility. It is also
important to take notice that even a slight pressure increase may not affect significantly
the fluid dynamics, but can influence the separation efficiency [64]. If the boiling points
are similar, such as of CO2 and O2, even a small change in the relative volatilities could
necessitate more stages and a higher reflux ratio.
The operating conditions involve high liquid and low vapour flow rates. Additionally, there
are significant density differences between the liquid (970 kg/m3) and the vapour flow
rates (70 kg/m3). This results in a high flow parameter Flv as shown in Equation 2.20,
which represents the ratio of the squares of kinetic energies or else momentum fluxes of
the two phases [64]. Hence, a high Flv indicates that the momentum flux of the liquid flow
is higher than that of the vapour, thus allowing for the development of a higher allowable
vapour velocity in the column. This can be alternatively interpreted as high capacity and
suggests that the potential of entrainment is feasible at higher velocities. Consequently,
the maximum allowable velocity results in approximately 1.6 m/s for the original cases
leading to a small column diameter of 0.05m. Although packed columns typically have
smaller diameters than tray columns, they are used in applications with low liquid loads
and pressure drops, which is not the case in this study.
It is noted that the calculated diameter represents the minimum allowable size, allowing
for the use of larger columns. However, in such cases, appropriate measures must be
taken to prevent weeping. Bubble cap trays are most suitable for this purpose due to
their effective design against weeping [59]. Although bubble cups are more expensive
than sieve or valve trays [59], selecting a 65cm column diameter, as done in this analysis,
increases the total capital cost of the distillation column.
Overall, a detailed analysis of the distillation process can provide a clearer understanding
and more accurate results regarding energy requirements in the column. The reboiler
duty is not negligible, and a precise simulation of the phase equilibria in each tray can
reduce uncertainties in both separation efficiency and duty requirements. Additionally,
testing for weeping could also help assess the feasibility of operating the distillation under
these conditions.

4.1.5
Future Eork and Recommendations
Moreover, the energy requirement of liquefying CO2 is calculated based on a COP of
an ammonia refrigeration cycle reported in the literature. However, simulating a refrigВӯ
eration cycle tailored to the systemвҖҷs characteristics would be beneficial for investigating
the impact of design parameters on the overall energy consumption. Firstly, the optimal
operating conditions in the cycle could be found, thus improving the cycle componentsвҖҷ
performance and reducing inefficiencies. Additionally, the integration of the refrigeration
cycle into the overall simulation would enable dynamic interactions with the other comВӯ
ponents used in the conditioning of CO2. Consequently, an optimization process could
be performed to determine decision parameters that could potentially minimize energy
consumption and maximize separation efficiencies. Various design alternatives could be
examined, such as the number of compressors, their pressure ratios (equal or varied),
and the distillation operating pressure.
Performing heat integration in the process design could also contribute to reducing the
energy requirements. Heating CO2 in HEX 4 and HEX 5 could be accomplished by exВӯ
ploiting the heated CO2 stream leaving a compressor. Additionally, using heated CO2
streams to cover the heating required for the regeneration of the molecular sieves would
save a great amount of energy. Moreover, the purge stream leaving the distillation colВӯ
umn is at a temperature lower than zero. This stream has a greater cooling capacity than
water and could be utilized before it is disposed of. Finally, The power for cooling in the
compression chain is 178 kW and could be utilized. Therefore, the waste heat in the coolВӯ
ing water could be used for power production through a Rankine cycle instead of returning
it directly to the cooling tower.
4.2
Pipeline Transport
4.2.1
Size Selection
The CO2 transport through pipelines is modelled assuming that no booster stations are
needed, as they are usually required for longer distances over 30km. However, if a booster
station were installed, both the capital and operating costs would increase. Specifically,
if the inlet pressure were 120 bar or higher, a larger pipeline would be required. As a
consequence, the investment cost for the pipeline would rise along with the capital cost
of the booster station. Operating cost would also increase for pumping and cooling CO2.
For inlet pressures lower than 110, the same pipe diameter and schedule selected for the
analysis could be used. In this case, the purchased cost for the pipeline would remain
the same but the capital cost for the booster station would raise the FCI. In that case,
the capital cost might have been less than in the case without a booster station, but it
would surpass the cost, both capital and operating, calculated in the analysis. As a conВӯ
sequence, the case chosen is considered optimal in terms of energy consumption and
capital cost.
4.2.2
Modelling of Pressure Drop and Heat Transfer
The pressure drop in the pipeline per km is 0.70 bar/km and 0.88 bar/km for the original
case and layout 1 respectively. In literature, it is reported that pressure drop fluctuates
0.5Вӯ1.5 bar/km, with 1.5 bar/km being related to small pipes less than 6in. Consequently,
the pressure drop falls within the reported pressure drop range for CO2 pipeline transport.
However, it should be noted that the design fluid velocities applied for CO2 transport are
in the range of 1Вӯ2 m/s. In this case, the average velocity in the pipeline is 0.47 m/s, and

The cost correlations for calculating the purchased cost of adsorbers and distillation columns

As already mentioned the maintenance cost for producing dense phase is elevated comВӯ
pared to liquid CO2. However, the high maintenance cost does not justify the pipeline

is not high so the pipe can be damaged. Consequently, failures in the pipeline are not
expected to occur frequently and the maintenance cost considered for dense CO2 proВӯ

The cost of molecular sieves is higher than the activated carbon as expected. NevertheВӯ

price for purchasing molecular sieves for industrial use would probably be less expensive
per kg, meaning that the molecular sieve price per kg is likely overestimated.

5
Conclusions
This thesis examines the energy requirements for conditioning and transport CO2 from
a smallВӯscale biogas facility. Two case studies are considered in this investigation. The
first case study included CO2 production in the liquid phase and transporting it through
trucks. As for the second case, dense CO2 is produced and it is transported through
pipelines. The distance is assumed 30 km in both scenarios. Moreover, the conditioning
was simulated in Aspen V14 and the pipeline model in EES. The thermodynamic model
used for predicting the CO2 mixture phase equilibrium is the PPR78 with adjusted group
interaction parameters as suggested by Xu et al. [32]. Liquid CO2 is delivered at Вӯ28В°C
and 15 bar while dense CO2 at 1В°C and 110 bar.
The energy analysis showed that cooling duty occupies most of the total energy consumpВӯ
tion for conditioning CO2, with heating and compression energy to follow. It is observed
that the high inlet feed temperature as well as the water content in CO2 greatly influence
the total amount of cooling duty required. Therefore, the first intercooler, processing the
most impure and hot stream, accounts for 44% of the total cooling duty. In addition, the
energy for regenerating the molecular sieves shapes the level of the heating duty as it
accounts for 45% of the total heat provided. Furthermore, the overall energy requirement
for compressing CO2 is determined by the compressors rather than the pump, despite the
high head developed for pumping the dense CO2.
However, by comparing the energy requirements for producing liquid and dense CO2 it
was concluded that the total duty for each utility did not deviate significantly. The cooling
duty is identical for both cases while the heating and compression duties differ due to
the recycling of the boilВӯoff when producing liquid CO2. The recycling causes the energy
consumption per tonne of CO2 to increase 3% and 2% for heating and compression reВӯ
spectively. Overall, the energy requirement is 0.23 MWh/tn of CO2 for cooling and 0.15
and 0.13 MWh/tn of CO2 on average for heating and compression respectively.
Alternative design layouts for conditioning were also examined to investigate the influence
of process design decisions on the total energy consumption and cost. The production
of dense CO2 was used as the base case. The results showed that the different layouts
have the greatest differences in the cooling and heating duties rather than in compression.
The cause for these deviations in cooling duty originates from the number of compresВӯ
sors used in the conditioning processes as well as the absence of precoolers before the
liquefaction. The highest deviation in cooling duty resulted among the cases is 37%. The
same pattern occurs for the compression as well. Nevertheless, the presence of turbines
offsets the differences in the level of compression energy among the cases. As a result,
the compression energy required is around 0.11 MWh/tn of CO2 for all layouts. Turbines
are also responsible for reducing the heating duty almost to half in the layouts in which
they are not included.
The modelling of CO2 transport through pipelines without considering a booster station
showed that the inner diameter of the pipe is the dominant variable for pressure drop. As
a result, the overall pressure drop was estimated at 20 bar, and around 6 kW of heat was
absorbed from the surroundings during the transport. It was also concluded that the inlet
temperature of the CO2 influences both the pressure drop and the heat transfer.
Moreover, it was proved that the total capital investment had the greatest influence on
the levelized cost of conditioning and transporting CO2 (LCOCO2). It was also shown
Thesis title
51
==PAGE:64==
that the investment cost of pipelines increases significantly the levelized cost for dense
CO2, resulting in around 4 times higher than for liquid. On the contrary, transporting CO2
through trucks corresponds only to 13% of the LCOCO2. In general, the elevated LCOCO2
for conditioning compared to literature stems from the lower CO2 production.
Finally, a sensitivity analysis was performed to assess the extent of uncertainties in formВӯ
ing the LCOCO2. The LCOCO2 was proved sensitive to capital expenditure and not to
the price of adsorbents.
