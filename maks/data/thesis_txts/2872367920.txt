Abstract
In response to the accelerating ’AI race’ and the proliferation of models, interfaces, and
tools, modern knowledge workers face a growing gap between rapidly evolving capa-
bilities and the practical skills needed to harness them effectively. Following the EU AI
Act, AI training is now mandatory for companies that develop AI products and for compa-
nies that use AI tools in their processes. This thesis argues that, while deeply technical
expositions of model architectures can overwhelm non-experts and hinder adoption, a
structured, high-level understanding grounded in explicit prompt engineering principles
empowers end users to interact with generative AI tools more confidently and accurately,
fostering an increase in the conscious use of the tools. Hence, we propose a modular
training curriculum that introduces core concepts of model behavior, codifies best prac-
tices for prompt formulation and model steering, and incorporates hands-on exercises
tailored to typical workplace scenarios. To assess effectiveness, we design both quan-
titative pre-/post-training evaluations and qualitative user-experience interviews. In the
end, we evaluate the qualitative and quantitative outcomes of the training that show how
the user gained confidence after the training and started to use the tools more, while main-
taining a critical mindset.
From both the opinions of the developers and quantitative data we can evince how the
training was impactful in setting the developers on the right course to start using these
tools more often. Based on these findings, we outline strategies to refine the curriculum
such as adaptive learning paths, real-time feedback dashboards, and ongoing analytics
to further boost engagement and retention.

Introduction
1.1
The Emergence of AI in Programming and Its Urgency
Artificial Intelligence (AI) has rapidly become a cornerstone of modern software develop-
ment. In recent years, AI-assisted coding tools have matured from novelty to mainstream,
transforming how developers write code. Generative AI models can now suggest code,
detect bugs, and even generate entire software based on natural-language prompts. As
a result, there is a growing consensus that AI will dramatically augment programmer pro-
ductivity and creativity. A 2023 survey by Stack Overflow found that 70 percent of de-
velopers are already using or planning to use AI tools in their development process [1],
underscoring the widespread adoption of AI in programming. Industry reports similarly
note that generative AI coding assistants offer “a significant leap forward in productivity”
for software teams [2].
The importance of AI in programming has also drawn the attention of policymakers and
regulators. Governments are beginning to establish guidelines to ensure AI is used ethi-
cally and safely. A prominent example is the European Union’s Artificial Intelligence Act
(EU AI Act), which entered into force in 2024 as the world’s first comprehensive legal
framework for AI. The EU AI Act aims to “foster the development and adoption of trustwor-
thy and human-centric AI” while subjecting high-risk AI systems to oversight [3]. Notably,
the Act includes provisions for general-purpose AI models, a category that encompasses
generative code assistants. These regulatory developments underline that AI in program-
ming is not just a technological trend, but a societal concern; developers and organizations
must now focus on responsible AI use, compliance, and upskilling.
1.2
AI Tools in Software Development: From Automation to
Generation
Artificial Intelligence has undergone a remarkable transformation since its inception, evolv-
ing from a theoretical concept to a pervasive force reshaping industries and daily life. This
journey reflects not only technological advancements but also shifting public perceptions
and expectations.
The conceptual roots of AI can be traced back to the mid-20th century, notably with Alan
Turing’s seminal 1950 paper, “Computing Machinery and Intelligence,” which posed the
question, “Can machines think?” [4]. Early AI research focused on symbolic reasoning
and rule-based systems, laying the groundwork for future developments.
A significant milestone occurred in 1997 when IBM’s Deep Blue defeated world chess
champion Garry Kasparov. This event demonstrated AI’s capability to handle complex,
strategic tasks previously thought to be the exclusive domain of human intelligence [5].
Nearly two decades later, in 2016, DeepMind’s AlphaGo achieved a similar feat by defeat-
ing Go champion Lee Sedol, showcasing the power of deep learning and reinforcement
learning in mastering even more intricate games [6].
The advent of generative AI marked a new era, with models capable of producing human-
like text, images, and code. OpenAI’s release of ChatGPT in 2022 brought AI into main-
stream consciousness, as it demonstrated the ability to generate coherent and contextu-
ally relevant text, sparking widespread interest and debate [7].

This progression has consistently challenged public assumptions about AI’s limitations.
Each breakthrough, from game-playing prowess to conversational fluency, has redefined
expectations, often surpassing what was deemed achievable.
Public sentiment toward AI has evolved alongside these technological strides. While early
achievements were met with awe, recent advancements have elicited a mix of excitement
and concern. Surveys indicate a growing awareness of AI’s capabilities, coupled with
apprehension about its implications for employment, privacy, and decision-making [8].
This dichotomy underscores the importance of fostering public trust through transparency,
ethical considerations, and inclusive dialogue. As AI continues to permeate various sec-
tors, addressing these concerns becomes a key need to ensure its responsible and fair
deployment.
Early evidence suggests these AI coding assistants can significantly improve developer
productivity. In a controlled experiment at Stanford University, developers with access
to GitHub Copilot (Copilot) completed a coding task 55.8% faster than those without AI
assistance [9]. Similarly, a field study involving developers in Microsoft and Accenture
showed efficiency improvements ranging from 8% to 21% [10]. Beyond speed, developers
report qualitative benefits: 87 percent of developers in one survey said that AI coding tools
helped them preserve mental energy when dealing with repetitive tasks [11]. However,
AI-generated code must be reviewed carefully, as models can suggest code that contains
subtle bugs or security vulnerabilities [12].
1.3
GitHub Copilot: An AI Pair Programming Assistant
GitHub Copilot, often described as an “AI pair programmer,” was initially little more than
a glorified auto-completion tool. The first version of GitHub Copilot used to cold read the
user’s active file in the IDE, both code and comments, and through the use of a Large
Language Model, it could infer what was the next code edit that you needed. The inno-
vation in the first iteration of the tool was disruptive. It was the first widely available tool
using a large language model to help in real-life work scenarios, and it distinguished itself
from simple code completion because the user could use the comments in the code to
guide the LLM on what code to generate. On top of that the LLM could generate several
lines of code that was coherent with the rest of the file. At first, GitHub Copilot was using
under the hood the OpenAI Codex model, a descendant of GPT-3 fine-tuned on a vast
corpus of source code; it has nowadays evolved and is capable of using several different
AI models developed by different companies, like Claude from Anthropic, OpenAI O1 or
o3, and Gemini from Google. Integrated within IDEs and text editors (e.g., Visual Studio
Code, Visual Studio, Neovim), GitHub Copilot provides real-time code suggestions as de-
velopers type [13]. But alongside the models, GitHub Copilot evolved the feature set of
the tool as well. Nowadays, the tool integrates several ways of interacting with the mod-
els, the concept of context has been expanded from one single file to almost everything
inside the IDE, and the tools available are more sophisticated, like chat participants, inline
chat, context variables, commands, agent mode, and many others. It supports dozens of
programming languages, including C#, JavaScript, Python, and more, making it a versa-
tile assistant across various development contexts. GitHub Copilot’s seamless integration
allows developers to remain in flow, seeing suggestions inline and accepting, editing, or
ignoring them with simple keystrokes.
Studies and industry reports highlight GitHub Copilot’s impact. Developers at Accenture
who adopted Copilot in enterprise settings reported smoother workflows and increased
job satisfaction, with 95% stating they enjoyed coding more with Copilot’s help [14]. Yet,

1.4
Research Hypothesis and Objectives
The central hypothesis of this thesis is that structured training is crucial for developers
to maximize the benefits of AI-assisted coding tools. Specifically, the thesis investigates
whether a tailored educational intervention teaching prompt engineering, key differences
between models, and tool-specific behaviors can increase both the adoption rate and the
proficiency of AI tool usage among software developers.
The research objectives are to:
1. Assess whether training increases the frequency of tool usage among developers.
2. Evaluate if the training framework allows the developers to use the AI tools with
critical thinking, avoiding common pitfalls.
3. Capture qualitative changes in developer attitudes, confidence, and practices post-
training.
These objectives will be addressed through a before-and-after study design, employing
quantitative metrics (e.g., daily engaged users, chat turnovers) and qualitative methods
(e.g., surveys, interviews).
1.5
Case Study Context: Milestone Systems
The setting for this research is Milestone Systems, which serves as an in-depth case
study. Milestone Systems is a notable company in the software industry, specifically in
the domain of video management software (VMS). Founded in 1998 and headquartered in
Copenhagen, Denmark, Milestone Systems is a world-leading provider of open-platform
IP video management software [15].
The company’s flagship product, Milestone XProtect®, is a VMS platform used globally
for managing and analyzing video surveillance feeds. As of recent years, XProtect and
related Milestone offerings have been deployed in over 150,000 installations worldwide
[16], from small businesses to enterprise and city-wide surveillance systems. This promi-
nent market position means that Milestone plays a key role in the VMS industry, often
pioneering new features and setting standards for video management technology.
Milestone Systems provides an excellent case study for examining AI-assisted coding
for several reasons. First, the company’s software products are complex and mission-
critical; failures or inefficiencies can have real-world security implications for customers.
This implies that Milestone’s developers must maintain high standards of code quality,
reliability, and performance. Any introduction of a tool like GitHub Copilot in such an en-
vironment needs careful consideration, making it a relevant testbed for our hypothesis
about training and effective use. Second, Milestone’s development workflows represent
those of a modern, large-sized software enterprise. The development teams are using
contemporary practices such as agile methodologies, code reviews, continuous integra-
tion, and an array of development tools. GitHub Copilot integration in such a workflow
can be observed in a realistic context (as opposed to, say, a purely academic setting or
a hackathon scenario).
Third, Milestone’s software has an open-platform architecture with many integration points.

The XProtect VMS is known for its extensibility – it can integrate with a wide range of cam-

can AI assist our developers? What are the benefits and pitfalls? Do we need to train

at Milestone – both the successes and the challenges – will inform recommendations for
any organization looking to implement AI-assisted coding in a responsible and effective
way.

2
Learning Framework
Understanding and closing the skill gap between advanced AI-assisted development tools
and current industry practices is pivotal for improving developer productivity. Modern AI
coding assistants like GitHub Copilot possess capabilities far beyond what many devel-
opers presently leverage. For example, AI can generate code, explain complex logic,
and even integrate knowledge from documentation, yet many developers use these tools
only for basic code auto-completion. This mismatch between tool capabilities and devel-
oper usage represents a significant skill gap that limits efficiency. Developers unfamiliar
with an AI tool’s full feature set or unsure how to communicate with it effectively cannot
exploit the full productivity gains. As a result, code may be written or debugged slower
than necessary, and opportunities to automate routine work are missed. Bridging this gap
through targeted training is therefore essential. By empowering developers to fully utilize
AI assistance, organizations can improve coding speed, reduce errors, and improve the
developers’ satisfaction, enhancing overall productivity and software quality.
Importantly, the training framework to address this gap is designed to be accessible and
practical. The underlying technologies of AI and large language models (LLMs) are un-
deniably complex, involving advanced machine learning algorithms and deep neural net-
works. However, it is neither necessary nor efficient to turn every developer into a machine
learning expert. Instead, the goal is to equip developers with a working understanding of
how AI coding tools operate and how to use them effectively—without delving into the
intricate mathematics or theory behind the models. In other words, the training provides a
high-level overview of core machine learning and deep learning concepts (such as what
an LLM is, how it learns patterns from code, and what its limitations are) to support in-
formed tool usage, but it deliberately avoids heavy mathematical derivations or in-depth
algorithmic proofs. This approach makes the material accessible to developers from di-
verse software backgrounds, not just those with AI expertise [19]. Thus, the emphasis is
on practical knowledge: understanding when to trust the tool’s suggestions, recognizing
when it might err, and interacting with it effectively, using the full depth of the available
tool-set and workflows, not on building or training AI models from scratch. After a gen-
eral introduction on essential AI topics, the training focuses on specific functionalities of
GitHub Copilot in the IDE, and how to use the tool properly.
2.1
The Skill Gap in AI-Assisted Development
The emergence of AI pair-programming tools has introduced a new paradigm in software
engineering. Just as the shift from low-level assembly language to high-level program-
ming languages transformed how software is built, AI-assisted coding is poised to change
development practices fundamentally [19]. GitHub Copilot and similar AI tools can sug-
gest entire functions, generate code in unfamiliar frameworks, and even anticipate bug
fixes. Yet, in many software teams these capabilities are underutilized. There is often
a lag in adoption and effective usage: developers may stick to traditional coding habits,
using the AI assistant only sparingly or for trivial auto-completion, while more powerful
features remain unexplored.
This skill gap stems from several factors:
• Lack of Awareness: Developers might not fully know what the AI tool can do.

• Trust and Confidence Issues: Some developers are skeptical of AI-generated
code or unsure how to validate it.
• Insufficient Training or Guidance: Many organizations roll out AI coding tools
without comprehensive training, leaving developers to learn by trial and error.
• Workflow Integration Challenges: Effective use of AI often requires changes in
workflow (e.g., writing prompts or describing problems in natural language).
• Complex Company Policies: Companies may have specific internal rules that limit
or completely prohibit the use of specific tools or AI in general.
This gap has tangible consequences for productivity. Developers who use Copilot sub-
optimally might only get minor autocomplete benefits, missing out on larger time savings
in design, documentation, or debugging. Conversely, those who learn to harness the
tool’s full capabilities can delegate repetitive coding tasks to the AI, accelerate complex
implementations, and receive instant answers to questions that would otherwise require
lengthy web searches [13].
Targeted training is the key to closing this gap. A well-designed training program intro-
duces developers to the breadth of the AI tool’s features and provides hands-on practice
in using them. Rather than simply distributing documentation, the training engages de-
velopers with realistic examples and use-cases relevant to their daily work. Over time,
they incorporate the AI into their “mental toolkit” for problem-solving, much as they would
a familiar library or programming technique.
Crucially, this training is tailored to developers’ needs and project contexts. It does not
attempt to make developers into AI researchers, but rather competent users of AI assis-
tance. The distinction is important. Developers need not derive backpropagation equa-
tions or train a transformer network from scratch, but they do benefit from understanding,
for example, that Copilot’s suggestions are probabilistic, that it has been trained on open-
source code, or that it may not have knowledge of very recent library updates. These
insights help developers use the tool judiciously and avoid common pitfalls. The training
thus includes a module giving a high-level primer on machine learning and deep learning
concepts. All of this is done in an approachable manner, without diving into academic de-
tail. The result is that developers from diverse backgrounds, front-end, back-end, junior,
senior, can all grasp the essentials of how their AI assistant works and why it behaves the
way it does, creating a common foundation for tool usage across the team.
2.2
Just-in-Time Learning as a Training Principle
To maximize effectiveness, the training program is built around the concept of Just-in-
Time Learning (JITL). Just-in-Time Learning is a learner-centric approach that delivers
knowledge at the moment of need, rather than far in advance. It has been described as
“anywhere, anytime, anyhow learning” that provides “just enough, just for me, and just in
time” [20]. In other words, JITL focuses on giving learners precisely the information or
skill they require to solve an immediate problem, rather than a broad curriculum that might
cover many topics “just in case” they are needed in the future.
2.2.1
Origins and Rationale
The concept of JITL emerged in the context of workplace training and corporate educa-
tion, especially as organizations recognized that employees need to continuously update
skills without leaving their workflow. Brandenburg and Ellinger [20] note that the demand
for just-in-time learning in the workplace has been steadily growing, driven by fast-paced
6

business changes and the need for on-demand skill acquisition. The rise of digital infor-
mation resources, e-learning platforms, and performance support tools has further en-
abled JITL by allowing employees to instantly find instructional content or experts when
a knowledge gap arises. In essence, JITL aligns training with the flow of work—learning
becomes a continuous, contextual process rather than a discrete event.
Early evidence of its effectiveness can be found in computing education research. Boese [21]
argued that computer science education must adapt to a “Just Google It” era in which stu-
dents expect information on demand; her work demonstrated that Just-in-Time Learning
strategies can be more effective than traditional approaches for today’s learners. It also
minimizes productivity disruptions, as employees spend less time in irrelevant training
and more time practicing skills on the job.
2.2.2
Application in the AI Tool Training
In designing our learning framework for Copilot and AI-assisted development, JITL prin-
ciples are central. The training is structured not as one monolithic course, but as a series
of modules and on-demand resources keyed to specific tasks or milestones in the devel-
opers’ workflow. For example, an introductory session or video might demonstrate basic
Copilot usage when the tool is first introduced. As developers begin using Copilot in real
projects, more advanced topics are introduced at relevant moments.
Additionally, in-situ support is provided in the form of snippets or job aids—cheat sheets
integrated into the IDE, or an internal forum where developers can ask a question and
get quick tips. This approach echoes GitHub’s own recommended strategies for Copilot
adoption, which suggest providing multiple forms of training and knowledge sharing during
the first few months of use [22]. By delivering training at the moment of need, we ensure
that developers immediately apply what they learn, which reinforces the knowledge and
builds practical skill.
Furthermore, JITL in our framework is supported by making learning resources easily
accessible “anytime, anywhere” for the developers. By empowering self-directed, on-the-
spot learning in this manner, we adhere to the JITL philosophy of giving “just enough”
knowledge exactly when it’s needed [20].
2.3
Inductive Teaching: Rationale and Benefits for AI Tools
Training
Inductive teaching [23] is a learner-centered instructional approach where learning begins
with specific observations, practical problems, or case studies, and only later introduces
the underlying theories or principles needed to explain or solve them. This contrasts with
traditional deductive teaching, which starts with general theories and principles before
moving to specific applications. Rather than asking learners to absorb abstract knowledge
for later use, inductive teaching situates new knowledge in authentic, real-world contexts
from the outset. Learners are motivated to discover or construct general rules or concepts
because they perceive an immediate need for them, rooted in the task or problem at hand.
Inductive methods include problem-based learning, project-based learning, case-based
teaching, discovery learning, and just-in-time teaching. All share the goal of engaging
students actively, often in collaboration with others, and encouraging them to connect
new knowledge to their prior experiences and professional needs.
Key characteristics of inductive teaching include:
• Active, collaborative learning: Learners work in groups and discuss questions or
solve problems together.

• Constructivism: Learners build their own understanding by relating new knowl-
edge to what they already know.
• Contextual relevance: Learning begins with authentic tasks, making content im-
mediately useful and engaging.
• Metacognition and self-directed learning: Learners reflect on what they need to
know and monitor their own progress.
Inductive teaching is especially beneficial in this context because it can help us fight the
biases that might hinder the adoption of the AI tools by grounding in the core needs for
the developers the needs for these tools.
Some of the benefits of applying Inductive Teaching for AI Tools training are:
• Direct engagement with real workflows: By starting with realistic use-cases from
developers’ day-to-day work, training becomes immediately relevant, which enhances
motivation and buy-in.
• Bridging the skill gap: Developers experience first-hand how AI tools streamline
their work, creating a natural demand for deeper understanding of the tools’ ad-
vanced features.
• Collaborative, peer-led learning: Inductive methods promote knowledge shar-
ing and collective problem-solving, mirroring real-world software development prac-
tices.
• Deeper learning and retention: Research demonstrates that knowledge gained

• Critical thinking and responsible use: Encountering limitations or pitfalls in realis-

[23]
Inductive training empowers developers to see, in a guided way, how AI tools like Copilot
can transform their workflow, whether by refactoring legacy code, scaffolding new mod-
ules, or automating documentation. As they encounter practical challenges (such as am-
biguous prompts or integration with team workflows), they develop the “need to know”
for underlying concepts, making subsequent instruction on prompt engineering or model
limitations purposeful and memorable.
In summary, inductive teaching ensures that developers not only understand how AI tools
can help them, but are also intrinsically motivated to master their advanced features. This
supports deeper learning, higher engagement, and greater return on investment for orga-
nizations seeking to upskill their workforce in the era of AI-assisted software development.

2.4.1
AI Model Selection and Trade-offs
Modern AI coding assistants may offer a choice of underlying models, each with different
capabilities and limitations. GitHub Copilot, for instance, has been evolving to incorporate
multiple large language models. The training highlights that choosing the right AI model
for a given task can impact the performance and output quality. Different models can
vary in their raw capability, reasoning depth, speed, and even cost of usage [24]. For
example, larger models such as Claude 3.7 tend to offer more accurate and coherent code
suggestions and better chain-of-thought reasoning but come with higher computational
costs and possibly strict usage limits.
Developers are trained to understand these trade-offs in broad terms. The goal is not to
delve into model architecture, but to recognize practical implications: if a task requires an-
alyzing an entire codebase or performing a complex refactoring, a more advanced model
might be needed for reliable output. Conversely, for quick, straightforward code com-
pletions or boilerplate generation, a lighter-weight model might suffice. The training also
touches on the cost aspect: AI services often have a pricing model where using a larger
model consumes more credits or incurs higher fees [24]. By being aware of this, devel-
opers can make cost-conscious decisions.
In summary, model selection is taught as a key skill: developers should know if and when
they can change the AI model, understand in general terms what differences to expect
in output quality and style, and be cognizant of any organizational policies around model
usage.
2.4.2
Prompt Engineering and Effective Communication
Perhaps the most crucial skill in obtaining useful results from an AI coding assistant is
prompt engineering, the art of crafting effective queries or instructions for the AI. Our
training places strong emphasis on teaching developers how to “talk” to the AI to get the
best results, highlighting several proven strategies [25]:
• Start general, then get specific
• Give examples
• Break complex tasks into smaller tasks
• Avoid ambiguity
• Reference relevant context or code
• Iterate and refine
Underpinning all these techniques is the idea that communication matters when working
with AI. The more context and clarity the user provides, the better the response will typ-
ically be. This point is reinforced by numerous success stories and studies on prompt
engineering in large language models; a well-crafted prompt can dramatically increase
the relevance of the AI’s output.
2.4.3
Software-specific Tools and Capabilities
Key to this framework is to streamline the use of the AI tools for the developers. To
facilitate the use of these software applications, it is mandatory to cover the specific details
of the tools at hand, in this case GitHub Copilot. In the two experiments introduced later,
some of the key capabilities are highlighted.
2.4.4
Inline Chat vs Chat View vs Code Completion
GitHub Copilot offers several different ways of interacting with the LLMs; the three main
ways are:

• Auto-completion in GitHub Copilot is the feature that provides real-time code sug-
gestions as the user types within the editor. As he writes code, Copilot predicts and
offers possible completions for the next line or block, allowing the user to quickly
accept, ignore, or cycle through alternatives using the keyboard. This method is de-
signed for fast, seamless coding assistance that integrates naturally into the work-
flow without requiring additional interaction.
• Inline Chat is a more interactive feature that appears directly next to the code in
the editor. With inline chat, the user can select or highlight specific parts of his
code and ask Copilot questions or request specific actions, such as refactoring a
function or explaining what a section of code does. Copilot responds contextually,
often displaying explanations or code changes right where the user is working, and
lets the user preview and apply those changes immediately. This makes inline chat
ideal for targeted, code-specific tasks and understanding.
• Chat view, by contrast, is a dedicated conversational panel or sidebar within the de-
velopment environment. Here, the user can have broader discussions with Copilot
that go beyond a single file or code selection. The user can ask general program-
ming questions, seek help with project-wide issues, or request code examples and
explanations. Chat view can reference the user’s entire workspace and supports
multi-turn conversations, making it well suited for learning, troubleshooting, or get-
ting advice that requires more context than just a snippet of code.
This is just a short list of the different faces of the tools that have been introduced.
A particular note has to be made for the Agent Mode which has only been briefly show-
cased during the training because at that time it was still in preview with limited usability.
Over the few months of work on this thesis, the Agent Mode has had major improvements
and is now a fundamental part of GitHub Copilot. Agentic AI is a term used to refer to AI
systems that can interact with other systems or their environment. In the specific case of
GitHub Copilot, the agent mode allows the LLM to read different files in a project that it
deems useful, edit code in several different files at the same time, run and test the code,
and, if the results are not correct, it can keep iterating without ever needing the interven-
tion of the user. These systems are not perfect and might even get stuck in infinite loops
of incorrect edits. The more powerful the tool becomes, the more important it is to know
how to handle it. This highlights how quickly these tools are evolving and how important
it is to enable the employees to be capable of navigating by themselves this vast and
ever-evolving landscape of AI tools.
2.4.5
Leveraging Chat Variables for Contextual Responses
Another advanced feature introduced in our Copilot training is the use of Chat Variables. In
Copilot’s chat interface, chat variables are a special syntax to pull in context automatically.
By typing the # symbol in a Copilot chat prompt, a developer can access a menu of context
variables (such as the currently open file, selected text, etc.) and insert them into the
query [26]. These act as placeholders that the Copilot system will replace with the actual
content when processing the prompt.
The training emphasizes how chat variables can improve both relevance and efficiency.
Rather than manually copying and pasting code, developers can use chat variables to
swiftly reference exactly the content they mean to discuss.
By training developers to leverage chat variables, we address a common cause of frustra-
tion: irrelevant or hallucinated answers that arise when the AI lacks sufficient context. The
habit of always providing context (and the tools to do so easily) significantly boosts the

is a concrete skill that directly improves their effective use of the tool and thus narrows
the gap between the AI’s capabilities and the user’s results.
2.4.6
Practical Examples Grounded in the Developers’ Daily Experience
A pillar to the outcome of the training is the use of real-life examples to showcase how to
work with the tools and how not to work with the tools. As we will later see in the Results
Chapter, the developers are eager for examples on how to interact with the models to
reduce the ambiguity of the answer, and improve usefulness and reliability of the inferred
response.
The best way possible to structure these examples following the principles of Inductive
Teaching is to start by first taking a problem in the daily developers’ workflow. Once we
can pinpoint a situation where the employee would love to use the AI Tools, if we show
them how to solve their problem using AI, they will be more positively biased towards the
tool, favoring adoption and retention.
This part of the training framework is the one that will have to adapt most to the specific
conditions of the training. So, for example, in an online training we might have to show the
examples but not guide the developers while they work on the examples themselves. In
some cases if the background of the learners is too diverse, we will have to resort to either
using different kinds of examples that might fit better some specific parts of the audience,
or we might choose to use some examples that are generally applicable for most of the
audience but not too specific. This might help to reach more people but at the same time
limit the penetration of the training. It will be the responsibility of whoever is using the
framework to pick the adequate trade-off, but we highly suggest, when it’s possible, to
pick more diverse and specific examples over more broad and general ones.
2.4.7
Common Pitfalls to Avoid
It’s important to show the developers, not only how to interact with the tools,but how not
to interact with the tools as well. There are some common pitfalls that are widespread
when interacting with AI, and making the developers aware of them will help avoid them
and improve their results with the tools.
Some of the most common pitfalls to avoid are:
• Overreliance happens when the developers start depending excessively on an AI
tool to carry out their tasks and lose their capability to perform them without the help
of machine-learning software.
• Overtrust manifests when the developers accept the code suggested by AI without
first critically analyzing it and understanding what it does.
• Poor prompting can lead to lower-quality responses.
• Neglecting biases and limitations can lead to accepting information that at first
sight might seem true but that is based on biased or outdated data.
• Sharing sensitive information can be a critical danger. It’s important for the de-
veloper to know how the data they share with AI are handled and what is safe and
what isn’t.
This list of risks can either be treated as a comprehensive topic, and each pitfall can be
introduced and analyzed one after the other. Instead, we suggest organically presenting
these pitfalls during live examples when possible. This aligns with the ideas behind in-
ductive teaching: by first showing the pitfall in practice, the developers understand the

and then only practically showing how to avoid them poses the risk for the developer to fall

it.

The Company Experiments: Assessing
the Impact of the Training Sessions
This chapter describes the design and implementation of the experiments conducted to
evaluate the impact of a targeted training intervention on developers’ perceptions and
usage of AI-assisted coding tools, specifically GitHub Copilot, within the company. The
experiments aimed to determine whether a structured training, in the format of a Brown
Bag session and a Hackathon could influence both attitudes towards and the actual usage
patterns of such tools. The first experiment consists in an online training in the form of a
Brown Bag session followed by quantitative and qualitative analysis of the AI tool usage
after the training, and will be discussed in Section 3.2. The second experiment consisted
of an in-person training in the form of a training session followed by a Hackathon. In this
experiment as well we analyzed perception and usage data. This is discussed in Section
3.7.
3.1
Objective of the Experiments
The primary objective was to investigate whether a training session can positively affect
developers’ adoption, perceived usefulness, and trust in AI coding assistants. The follow-
ing research questions guided the experiments:
1. Assess whether training increases the frequency of tool usage among developers.
2. Evaluate if the training framework allows the developers to use the AI tools with
critical thinking, avoiding common pitfalls.
3. Capture qualitative and quantitative changes in developer attitudes, confidence, and
practices post-training.

3.6
Limitations
The experiment was subject to several limitations, many of which stemmed from the nec-
essary trade-offs between academic research rigor and the company’s business objec-
tives.
A primary limitation concerned the sample size and composition. The company aimed
to maximize the training’s reach and positive impact by inviting as many employees as
possible, and this resulted in 170 employees attending the online session—a strong in-
dicator of interest despite the training being optional. However, the large audience and
online format necessitated a less interactive training environment, reducing opportunities
for participant engagement and tailored instruction, forcing some deviation from the base
ideas developed in the training framework.
Furthermore, although attendance was high, only 12% of participants (20 employees)
completed the post-training surveys. This low response rate limits the statistical signifi-
cance of qualitative findings and constrains the reliability of inferred trends.
The analysis time frame presented another constraint. Due to data availability, it was not
possible to analyze tool usage prior to January 23, 2025. As a result, the study could not
assess adoption patterns during the initial four months when employees had access to
the tool without any accompanying training.
It is also important to acknowledge that the results of the qualitative analysis may be influ-
enced by biases such as Social Desirability Bias which pushes the participants towards
answers that might be viewed favorably by others[27] or Hindsight Bias, which brings peo-
ple to perceive past events as having been more predictable than they were, altering their
past perceptions of the AI Tools[28].
Finally, the absence of a control group further limits the strength of causal inferences
regarding the training’s impact. Without a comparable group of employees who did not
receive the training, it is difficult to isolate the effects of the intervention from other con-
founding factors.

5
Discussion
5.1
Interpretation of Results
The results presented in the previous chapter provide a comprehensive overview of the
impact that targeted training had on the adoption and perceived usefulness of AI-assisted
coding tools among developers at Milestone Systems.
5.1.1
Quantitative Metrics: Noisy but Suggestive
The quantitative data collected from the GitHub Copilot Metrics API proved to be notably
noisy, affected by several confounding factors, such as incomplete data coverage, limi-
tations of the API, and fluctuations in daily developer activity. Despite this, the analysis
reveals a clear positive trend in usage metrics after the Brown Bag Session. Across all
key indicators like the number of engaged users, daily chat activity, and total chat volume,
there is a marked increase in the post-training period.
While these trends do not constitute definitive proof of a causal link between the training
session and increased usage of GitHub Copilot, one could argue that this positive trend
would have appeared anyway—they nonetheless indicate a possible positive correlation.
The upward trends observed post-intervention suggest that the Brown Bag Session likely
contributed to raising awareness and interest, and potentially encouraged broader exper-
imentation with AI tools within the developer cohort.
5.1.2
Qualitative Insights: Increased Engagement and Comfort
The qualitative data obtained from the surveys supports and enriches the picture drawn
by the quantitative analysis. Developers reported a noticeable increase in interest toward
AI tools following the Brown Bag Session. The intervention appears to have acted as a
catalyst for curiosity and engagement, particularly among those who had not previously
tried the tools.
Comparison of survey responses before and after the training session highlighted several
key findings:
1. Increased Perceived Usefulness: Developers rated the AI tools as significantly
more useful after the session, with the median rating increasing from 4 to 5 on a
five-point Likert scale.
2. Greater Comfort: There was a statistically significant increase in the reported com-
fort level with trying and using AI tools, indicating that the training effectively reduced
psychological barriers to experimentation.
3. Slight Improvement in Perceived Reliability: Although perceived reliability of the
tools increased slightly, there was no evidence of overconfidence or blind trust. De-
velopers maintained a critical approach, which is a positive outcome in terms of
responsible AI adoption.
4. Substantial Growth in Frequency of Use: The frequency of reported usage in-
creased markedly, with daily use rates rising from 48% to 71% among survey re-
spondents. This suggests that the training had a meaningful impact on day-to-day
behavior.
Moreover, what we find very interesting in the surveys are the answers to the question,
“If you’re NOT using GitHub Copilot or other AI tools, what do you think would help you
AI Assisted Coding
39
==PAGE:48==
use them?”, where 14% of the respondents answered that they would use the tools more
if they had more training. Another 60% answered that they would use the AI tools if they
were more reliable and more useful, and while these points can be addressed by having
better and more powerful tools, at the same time, the tools that we already have can be
made more useful and reliable by using them in the proper way through dedicated training.
These results are reflected with comparable statistics in the second survey as well.
5.1.3
Summary and Implications
Taken together, these results indicate that while the quantitative analysis cannot establish
causation beyond doubt due to inherent limitations, the conjunction of quantitative and
qualitative data strongly suggests a positive impact of the brown bag training session. This
intervention helped to raise awareness, reduce apprehension, and foster more regular use
of AI coding tools among developers. These findings provide a solid foundation for further
research and organizational initiatives aimed at maximizing the benefits of AI-assisted
development through training.
5.2
Limitations of the Study
Several important limitations must be acknowledged when interpreting our results:
• Limited Access to Company Data: Restrictions on access to company-wide in-
formation, such as the precise number of GitHub Copilot licenses, hampered the
ability to gather a fully comprehensive dataset. Challenges in obtaining consistent
API dumps further limited the accuracy and completeness of the quantitative data.
• Absence of a Control Group: The study lacked a true control group, as the in-
tention was to expose as many developers as possible to the training for maximum
positive impact. While beneficial for organizational change, this design choice limits
the strength of causal inferences that can be drawn from the data.
• Technical Limitations in Data Collection: The GitHub Copilot Metrics API, while
central to the research, suffered from incomplete documentation and technical is-
sues. Some metrics were unavailable or inconsistently reported (e.g., chat data for
Visual Studio users), which affected data quality and introduced additional noise.
• Reliance on Self-Reported Survey Data: Survey questions relied in part on de-
velopers’ retrospective assessment of their attitudes and behaviors before the in-
tervention, which is susceptible to recall bias. Some comparison questions were
only asked after the training session, relying on participants to recall their previous
opinions.
These limitations suggest that while the evidence points to a positive impact of the training,
the conclusions should be viewed as indicative rather than definitive. The experience and
results described here nonetheless provide valuable lessons and guidance for similar
initiatives in other organizational contexts.
5.3
Future Work
The question of how best to train developers to adopt and effectively use AI tools remains
open for further exploration. Building on the experience and findings of this study, several
avenues for future research and practical improvement are apparent:
• Repeat the study with improved metrics: Await further development and stabi-
lization of the GitHub Copilot Metrics API, then replicate the experiment to obtain
more robust and comprehensive quantitative data.
40
AI Assisted Coding
==PAGE:49==
• Alternative usage tracking: Explore the use of additional tools or methods for
tracking developer interaction with AI tools, supplementing or replacing the limita-
tions of the GitHub Copilot Metrics API.
• Enhanced survey design: Refine survey instruments by asking all comparative
questions both before and after the intervention, to reduce reliance on retrospective
reporting and increase the accuracy of measured changes.
• Inclusion of a control group: Conduct future experiments in settings where a
control group can be established, allowing for more rigorous evaluation of the causal
effects of training.
• Interdisciplinary collaboration: Consider developing future studies with a more in-
terdisciplinary research team, including experts in education sciences, project man-
agement, human-computer interaction, and AI technologies, to broaden the per-
spectives and expertise brought to bear on the research.
• Broader tool coverage: Expand the scope of future studies to cover multiple AI
tools with varying levels of complexity, to understand whether different tools require
tailored training approaches.
5.4
Conclusion
This discussion has examined the impact and implications of a targeted training inter-
vention on AI-assisted coding tool adoption in a real-world enterprise context. Despite
limitations in data quality and study design, the results indicate a positive effect on both
developer attitudes and usage behaviors. While the findings cannot be considered con-
clusive, they offer a strong starting point for continued organizational learning and future
research into the effective training of knowledge workers in the era of AI-assisted software
development.
