Abstract
The pre-feasibility study for ﬂoating oﬀshore wind development at Utsira Nord aims to conclude
whether the given sites should be further developed or abandoned. It requires evaluations based on
technical measures in terms of AEP, technology and execution availability in addition to economic
analysis to assess if the cost level will lead to an LCOE of which is competitive on the wholesale
electricity market and whether the project itself is proﬁtable. As ﬂoating oﬀshore wind is currently
escalating from small pilot projects to large commercial projects, further analysis into the technology
used and factors aﬀecting the net AEP is crucial to address.
The purpose of this Master thesis project is to develop a pre-feasibility study of the three sites for
lease. Adopting weather data from NORSEWInD and NEWA the projects’ AEP was calculated in
WAsP 12. The results were analysed for diﬀerent turbine layouts and wake models. The AEP for the
three sites were further used in an economic evaluation for both socio-economic and private-economic
calculations to obtain the LCOE, NPV and IRR. As anticipated, the economic results gave a non-
competitive LCOE for one site of 495MW of 808NOK/MWh, and NPV of -7.64bnNOK. Hence, the
development of the sites will not be feasible without further ﬁnancial support. Diﬀerent CfD levels and
lump sum payment scenarios were investigated and as the CfD level is not yet decided for the project,
the CfD level for Sørlige Nordsjø was used as a comparison of which was found to not be substantial to
achieve project feasibility. Despite the immense ﬁnancial support needed from the Norwegian State,
ﬂoating wind is a substantial part of the country’s solution to achieve emission reductions, both for
increased power to the national grid, but also for electriﬁcation of oil and gas platforms. In order to
achieve the projected future cost reductions by the industry it is vital that the three sites at Utsira
Nord are built out despite the high cost level. This in order to commercialise the technology and
reduce both the LCOE and CAPEX costs over the next decade.
However, further details and analysis are needed in order to reach the ﬁnal investment decision. This
mainly entails AEP calculations using onsite weather data from the ﬂoating LiDAR campaign now
issued, further analysis on wake eﬀects, array cable layout, transmission strategy from the Norwegian
TSO Statnett and certainty in CfD and other support payments. It was found that all three site areas
will interfere with existing use of the area. They can be summarised to: an overlap of the Military
Zone for Site 1, trespassing corridor for shipping on the border of Site 2 and the overlap of two gas
pipes for Site 3. Taking all the investigated factors into consideration, it seems most feasible to develop
Site 2, but further risk assessment, wake modelling and cable layout to the OSS, once its location is
decided, is necessary to conduct.
Finally, two interviews with employees working in Ørsted within early stage design were conducted in
order to provide inputs from the industry on their perception on the bid criteria for Utsira Nord. It
was made clear that the industry is currently under high pressure and that the current challenges in
the supply chain are necessary to plan for. Thence, a comparison to the ScotWind bid was made and it
was found that it requires a thorough Supply Chain Development Statement (SCDS). Here, 25% of the
project related expenditure must be made in Scotland. Similar requirements could be stated for the
three leased projects at Utsira Nord in order to enhance the focus on local content and combat supply
chain issues to prevent project hindrance during execution. However, a higher percentage should be
aimed for due to the abundant supplier industry of oﬀshore operations from the oil and gas industry.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Wind energy is an essential contributor to the current and future renewable energy mix.
Today,
the largest share of generated wind energy stems from onshore wind farms, but public perception
and local opposition is driving the momentum for oﬀshore development.
Up until now, the only
commercially mature solution for oﬀshore wind farms (OWF’s) is bottom ﬁxed turbines in shallow
waters.
However, the potential in deeper waters is immense and if developed would open up to
signiﬁcantly larger site areas of what is currently analysed for shallow waters. This would however
require a ﬂoating foundation, hence the deep water technology named ﬂoating oﬀshore wind farms
(FOWF). Not only does the concept of ﬂoating wind open up for larger areas, but it allows for areas
of higher wind speeds with lower environmental and social impacts. [1, 2]
Today, ﬂoating wind is still in the pilot phase with 330MW in operation by 2024 in Europe [3]. The
largest FOWF to date is Hywind Tampen in Norway with an installed capacity of 88MW. Predictions
of the energy mix towards 2050 anticipate that ﬂoating wind can make up a share of 15% of all
oﬀshore wind, accounting to 264GW installed capacity in 2050. If this is to be realised, development
of FOWF’s 3000 times the size of Hywind Tampen would need to take place.[2]
In order for ﬂoating wind to reach the above target it is essential that diﬀerent concepts are tested for
the choice of turbines, ﬂoaters, mooring systems, cables, installation and operation and maintenance
(O&M) strategies and their synergies. The learning experience from these small and large scale projects
will be vital to achieve commercially mature and competitive solutions deployed of large scale in 2050.
The current estimation, according to [4], is that there will be 14GW of installed capacity whereof
8GW will be operational in 2030. Challenges in supply chain is the main reason for the assumption
that previously sought to be higher. [4].
One of the ﬂoating leases for 2023 is Utsira Nord in Norway with bid submission in November 2023 and
award in early 2024. The lease is divided into three phases of which the top three ranked consortiums
are awarded an area each of 500MW. This thesis aims to address a preliminary assessment of the
three sites at Utsira Nord, analyse the given qualitative bid criteria from the Norwegian Ministry of
Petroleum and Energy, the bankability and feasibilty of the projects and the design challenges that
will occure for the relatively new technology taken into use. [5]

1.1
Background
Throughout history, mankind has utilized the wind as an energy source. With the invention of the
electric generator in the 1830’s, electricity production became a new means of utilization. The ﬁrst
modern wind turbine was developed in Denmark in 1891, a horizontal axis turbine with a 22.8m tall
tower. However, the modern wind power sector did not emerge before the 1980’s and was pushed
forward in Denmark due to the oil crisis in the 1970’s. Following this, the danish oil and gas company
DONG (now energy company Ørsted), built the worlds ﬁrst oﬀshore wind farm in Vindeby in 1991
and was at the time thought of as an impossible project. On the contrary, Vindeby contributed to
valuable learnings and showed that oﬀshore wind indeed was feasible, marking the start of the wind
industry. Fast forwarding to today, one can see that the development since has been pushed forward
by governments due to an increasing concern over climate change and the need for renewable energy.
Today, OWF’s are fully commercialised with continuous advancements in technology, logistics and
operations but is limited to sea depths shallower than 60m. New concepts and innovative solutions
have been pushed forward to develop technologies that support deep water OWF’s and in 2017 the
ﬁrst ﬂoating oﬀshore wind farm, Hywind Scotland, started its operation of the 5 turbines with a total
capacity of 30MW. Similar to the ﬁrst OWF Vindeby, ﬂoating wind is believed to be an unfeasible mean
of renewable energy. However, the existing pilot projects have shown that although the technology is
not commercialized, it is sought to be a feasible way to produce renewable energy with higher yield,
at greater sea depths.

Norway’s outlook on oﬀshore wind
Norway characterises itself with its mountainous landscape, large coastline and maritime zone of
great sea depths where abundant oil and natural gas reserves were discovered in the 1960’s. Despite
the existence of fossil fuel resources, Norway relies heavily on hydroelectric power which composes the
country’s high level of self-suﬃciency in energy production, leaving it to be the largest energy exporter
in Europe [6]. Even though 98% of the country’s electricity share stems from renewable energy, where
92% amounts from hydropower, further development of renewable energy is necessary in order for
Norway to reach its updated COP27 climate target of 55% emission reduction by 2030 compared to
1990 levels. Due to its deep waters, there are only a scarce amount of areas applicable for bottom-ﬁxed
wind in Norway. Hence, FOW is sought to be the primary solution for wind development oﬀshore and
has led to Norway being in the forefront of FOW with its pioneer projects. Additionally, it homes
a variety of ﬁrms for contractors, system integrators, equipment manufacturers, leading expertise in
support vessels and maritime equipment for oﬀshore installations and close collaboration with research
institutions SINTEF, NTNU and UiB providing research to ecosystems and the oﬀshore wind value
chain.[6, 7]
Obtaining the position as the 11th largest oil producer in the world and the third largest exporter of
natural gas after Russia and Qatar, the Norwegian continental shelf is subject to ﬁnd new solutions
for emission reduction for platforms of which have a set timeline of operation. Mapping of possible
means of emission reduction for oil and gas platforms have entailed the subject of electriﬁcation. As
the platforms are reliant on diesel generators for onboard energy consumption, the possibility to rather
import electricity has sought to be a solution to achieve lower emissions. Given the distances from
shore, a scarce amount of platforms are sought to be feasible for electriﬁcation. Emission reduction of
coupling FOW with the platforms have therefore been a large focus after the development of Hywind
Tampen, of which today is connected directly to the platforms of Gullfaks and Snorre. [8]

2.2
Regulatory framework
The regulatory framework of which oﬀshore wind development in Norway must abide is the Ocean
Energy Act. The aim of the act is stated as follows:
"This Act shall facilitate the utilization of renewable energy resources at sea in accordance with societal
objectives, and for energy facilities to be planned, built and managed in such a way that care for energy
supply, the environment, safety, commercial activities and other interests are taken care of"
The Act was ﬁrst prepared in the early 2000’s as a response to climate change and strategies of
developing further means of renewable energy. It stipulates that all rights to utilize renewable energy
sources oﬀshore belongs to the state and is regulated after the Act. It serves many similarities with
the management of petroleum resources, but where the right is not surrounding the resource itself,
but the utilisation of it. In addition, the aim of the Petroleum Act is rather to secure socio-economic
gains. For all areas announced open for oﬀshore wind development, the general rule as follows is that
the developer shall be chosen through auctions. However, authorities might allow for pilot projects or
projects with a limited time frame to be exempted from the Act. For wind farms of which are directly
connected to oil and gas platforms for electriﬁcation comes the question of which Act that applies. For
these projects, the Ocean Energy Act can be set aside in order for the development to not abide both
the Petroleum Act and the Ocean Energy Act. However, as the laws within each Act diﬀer slightly
for co-use of the area the right to develop under the Petroleum Act has sought some criticism from
the ﬁshing industry for the Hywind Tampen project as it is not as rigid as the Ocean Energy Act.
With Hywind Tampen being the only ﬂoating project in Norway, there is yet to come a developed
project under the Ocean Energy Act and not Petroleum Act in Norwegian waters. Preservation of
areas related to indigenous people is also important and described in the Act as a required area to
consult with the Sami Parliament. [11]
2.3
Societal perception on wind energy in Norway
In Norway, the installation of onshore wind occurred rapidly from 2010, of which there was no onshore
production, to 2020 where 1.53GW was installed. The escalation of development and high rate of
investment resulted in a series of conﬂicts and a temporary halt for new licenses to be granted as
the negative environmental impact, turbine- and farm sizing along with a profound procedural and
distributed inequity was found for the developed projects. Scepticism towards 58% of the projects
being owned by foreigners has additionally reduced the general perception of onshore wind as a suitable
mean of energy production in Norwegian municipalities. The opposition towards onshore wind energy
opened up the discussion of its preference to oﬀshore wind energy. An annual survey done by CICERO,
Norway’s largest research institute for climate research, showed that the public’s support for onshore
wind reduced from a majority of 65% to 33% from 2018 to 2020 and that the support for oﬀshore
wind is thence higher at 58% in 2020 although this share has also decreased over time.[12, 13]
The study showed that although the preference is clear for oﬀshore to nearshore or onshore locations,
the larger concern is rather directed to the ownership and intended use of the wind farms.
The
importance of local or national ownership is so large to the extent that the scenario of Local energy
communities for onshore wind energy is preferred over National alliances for nearshore wind energy,
which additionally is preferred over International consortiums for oﬀshore wind energy. [13]
Despite the opposition, NVE predicts that the electricity consumption in Norway will increase by
23TWh amounting to an increase of 15-20% from the 2020 consumption level to 2040. This was found
in their report to identify possible measures to increase the electriﬁcation rate and its eﬀect on the
national grid [14]. Additionally, Norway has set the mentioned climate goal of emission reduction of
55% in 2040 compared to 1990 levels. With this in mind, it is vital that the negative perception of
wind energy in Norway sees a change if further development of wind energy is to equate a share of the
necessary development of renewable energy in the country.

Utsira Nord
Utsira is an island oﬀthe west coast of Norway belonging to Rogaland county. It is Norway’s smallest
municipality with an area of 6km2 and 193 inhabitants. Despite its size, Utsira aims to be a live
test laboratory for new energy solutions. The island has several research projects and a test centre
connected to smart energy systems with wave energy, onshore wind energy and energy storage of
which the Norwegian power company Haugaland Kraft is responsible for. Even though new and smart
marine technology is tested on the coast of the island it is important to develop the technology in
coexistence with the local ﬁsheries as people on Utsira have lived on and for ﬁsh for centuries.

be opened up for applications for oﬀshore wind production. With the now plan on developing between

also local interventions. One of which will be the construction of one or more land-based substations

traditional oﬀshore substations to eliminate some of the largest cost drivers in oﬀshore wind devel-

Chapter 4
Technology review: Floating Oﬀshore
Wind
The aim of this chapter is to explain the concept behind the diﬀerent technologies used in a FOWF.
As FOW is currently in between the pilot project phase and commercial phase the development of
new technologies and strategies of installation and operation are continuously changing. Hence, the
concepts provided in this thesis is based on the current leading technologies of deployment in the ﬁeld.
4.1
Substructure and Mooring systems
There are currently four main ﬂoating concepts that use diﬀerent theories of stability in their de-
sign. The turbines are almost indiﬀerent to the bottom ﬁxed turbines but the turbine tower is here
deployed on top of substructures, also referred to as ﬂoaters, that are secured by mooring lines to
the seabed. Figure 4.1 depicts the four concepts, a barge platform of which is buoyancy stabilized,
a semi-submersible platform with greater stability achieved from the second water plane area, the
ballast stabilized spar-buoy and a tension leg platform (TLP) which is mooring line stabilized. The
TLP uses taught mooring lines whilst the other concepts use catenary mooring systems. For this case
study the semi-submersible substructure will be used as it can be towed intact to the site and thence
is the easiest to install and withstands greatest wave motion. [20]

The catenary, or taut mooring systems for FOWT’s are often referred to as station keeping systems
as they are crucial for keeping the ﬂoater stabilised and limit movement. The strategy for how many
mooring lines a single FOWT is in need of diﬀers from ﬂoater design. Taking the three legged barge
as an example there are diﬀerent practices and strategies of using one or more mooring lines at each
barge. From a risk analysis perspective, developers prefer additional mooring lines to limit the risk
of capsizing, and with this the idea of shared mooring systems have been investigated to reduce the
mooring costs as applied in [22, 23, 24].
Taut Mooring
As the name indicates, taut mooring refers to a mooring system with limited horizontal movement and
where the mooring force is what creates the stability of the ﬂoater. Taut mooring systems are used
with TLP ﬂoaters where the mooring lines are anchored using anchor piles. Due to the installation
costs related to the anchor piles they are currently the most expensive anchoring option for mooring
lines and drives the cost of taut mooring up. Even though it uses far shorter lengths of mooring line
compared to catenary mooring, the anchoring imposes higher complexity of installation and costs that
the catenary mooring system outperforms. [25]
Catenary Mooring
For catenary mooring systems, the stability of the ﬂoater is dependent on the weight, friction and
tension of the mooring system with a great reduction of load on the anchor. As the semi-submersible
substructure is chosen for this study, catenary mooring lines are used for further analysis.
4.2
Array cables
The term array cables refers to the subsea cables within the wind farm that covers all turbine cable
connections and their connection to the oﬀshore substation. For bottom ﬁxed turbines the array cables
are buried in the seabed between the turbines of connection and raised through the turbine tower to
the gearbox. On the contrary, the FOWT’s don’t have a turbine tower piled into the seabed. Thence,
the cable is hanging freely from the ﬂoater down to the seabed where it is buried before risen to the
next FOWT.
As the cables are subjected to the marine environment it is crucial that they can withstand the mo-
tions forced upon. There are many possibilities of the dynamic cable conﬁguration, also referred to
as umbilical cords, as shown in Figure 4.2.
Of them is arguably the lazy wave conﬁguration the
most suitable and cost-eﬀective solution. The lazy wave conﬁguration is applied for deep water cable
systems in the oil and gas industry and has hence proven applicability. The concept decouples the
motions from the part near the seabed and the hanging part from the ﬂoater by a buoyancy element
attached to the cable causing its shape. [20, 26, 27]

The type of array cable used between FOWTs is a combination of dynamic and static cables, connected

in the water for wet-mate jointing. Even though the technology for the two types of cables exist
and are currently used today, it is possible to only use dynamic cables in the entire layout to avoid
the submarine joints.

5.3
Wind resource assessment in WAsP
The weather data collected lies under two diﬀerent modelling approaches in wind atlas methodology.
This as the NORSEWInD project is based on on-site remote sensing technology using light detection
and ranging (LiDAR). The data is therefore directly measured and lies under microscale modelling and
thus so called "observational wind atlas", whilst NEWA and NORA3 are based on mesoscale models
that provide time series data based on hindcast models, a statistical calculation determining probable
past conditions and thence provides a "numerical wind atlas". Even though the numerical wind atlas
provides reliable data for wind farm planning at regional, national and local levels and a good base
for wind farm design and layout; on-site wind measurements are required for bankable estimates of
power production. Modelling with on-site wind measurements require at least a full calendar year
of measurements and thence numerical wind atlas are often used prior to bid submissions of oﬀshore
wind farms as a preliminary evaluation before long-term measurements in the area are conducted.[36]
The WAsP methodology can derive both a generalised wind climate from met mast data, but also
a predicted wind climate using generalised data for area where no met mast data is available. The
WAsP tool was initially designed for onshore wind resource assessment and parameters diﬀerentiating
for oﬀshore wind must therefore be implemented. One example is the surface roughness of the chosen
area for analysis.
The topography and orography will be of high interest to capture for onshore
analysis, whereas oﬀshore wind farms in open water won’t ﬁnd the same geographical eﬀects due to
no to little surface roughness and elevation. Hence, the value for surface roughness z0 is set to zero
for the oﬀshore wind resource assessment. Internally, WAsP uses converts this to 0.0002 m.
The wind resource assessment in WAsP starts in the WAsP Climate Analyst where each location
with measurements are inserted. The weather data is thence past through a data reading controller
to make sure that columns of wind speed and wind direction are chosen and paired together. All
values are thereafter calculated and the only manual insertion is the calm threshold of which 0.05m/s
is chosen for the calm threshold of the wind direction. The controller lists how many anomalies that
were found that later can be investigated. When the import is complete, an Observed wind Climate
Report(Omwc) is then created to display all statistics, the wind rose with the prevailing wind direction,
Weibull distribution of the wind speed. When this is complete for one site location the procedure is
iterated for the remaining sites.

NORSEWInD
The Northern Seas Wind Index database project (NORSEWInD) was conducted from 2009-2012 as
a reaction to the increase in met mast data for oﬀshore wind developers. The motivation for the
project was to test and validate light detection and ranging (LiDAR) measurements as an alternative
to traditional cup anemometers mounted on met masts. For wind resource assessment of wind farms,
LiDAR’s are preferred as the wind turbines become larger and therefore the hub height of which the
wind speed is measured increases. With increasing hub heights the met mast construction becomes
more expensive and diﬃcult whereas the concept of LiDAR is needless of met masts and operates at
the same position for all height measurements. The project entailed measurements from 14 stations,
whereof 7 were made by met masts and 7 using LiDAR. Only two of the measurements were taken
on onshore stations and the rest mounted on existing oﬀshore structures. At Utsira island an onshore
measurement was taken using LiDAR measurements of the type Wind Cube.

NEWA
Wind atlases have been used for wind energy site assessments since the ﬁrst comprehensive European
Wind Atlas(EWA) was published in 1989. EWA was the ﬁrst wind atlas to use the wind atlas method
based on statistical models, the core of WAsP invented at DTU Wind Energy. After its release, further
development of wind atlases for individual countries were developed and in 2015 the need for a new
update of a European wind atlas gave rise to the 4-year research program to develop NEWA. NEWA
incorporates microscale modelling using the method called downscaling.
Distinctive for mesoscale
models, the horizontal resolution might not capture the local environments with its variations in
surface roughness and orography. For a given site it is vital to know these local features in order to
model the most accurate predicted wind climate. Thence, downscaling from the initial mesoscale data,
derived from weather research and forecasting models(WRF), provides a more accurate estimation for
a given site and would be highly beneﬁcial in more complex terrain. For NEWA, the WAsP microscale
model is used to downscale the mesoscale data where local surface roughness and orography is added to
the vector map of the given area. For the sake of this study, the roughness length chosen is the default
for water bodies in NEWA of 0.0001m and the measurement height set to 150m. The measurement
interval is of 30min and hence further comparison to the NORSEWInD data will be conducted for
valid 30min readings. [39, 40, 41]
NORA3
NORA3 is a 3-km atmospheric hindcast model covering the North Sea, Norwegian Sea, and Barents
Sea from 1984 to 2021. The model, using HARMONIE-AROME, accurately downscales the global
hindcast ERA5 reanalysis, improving wind ﬁeld representation and outperforming its predecessor
(NORA10) in complex terrains. Regular updates are performed on a three-monthly basis, with a few
months’ time lag due to reliance on reprocessed ERA5 ﬁelds. The NORA3 hindcast database is owned
and developed by the Norwegian Meteorological Insititute (MET Norway) and its ability to serve as
a suitable database for wind power estimations has beeen in evaluated by Bergen Oﬀshore Wind
Center(BOW) in [42]. Here they concluded that the database gives slightly conservative estimates for
oﬀshore wind metrics typically 5% lower than observed wind speeds. Hence, a total underestimation
of 10-20% is expected for oﬀshore wind analysis for a selected turbine type and hug height. The
dataset received from MET Norway for the area of Utsira Nord was based on one 10min mean wind
measurement every three hours. Further comparison is therefore done on the NEWA and N0RSEWInD
dataset with more frequent measurement intervals.[43, 44, 42]

6.1
Wind farm layout- Optimization
One of the procedures in wind farm planning is to decide the type of turbine to be used and the layout
and number of turbines that ﬁt the area of lease. It is preferable to choose larger and fewer turbines
over many turbines of smaller size as this increases the power production from each turbine, minimizes

has a great eﬀect on the interaction of each turbine production, cable length needed and maintenance
routing for vessels.

deﬁcit region. This as the wind directly behind the wind turbine is both slowed down and turbulent
compared to the "ideally" laminar and faster wind in the upstream area in front of the turbine sweep

Park wake model
Wake eﬀects are modelled for in WAsP and are based on the Park model of which both Park1 and

The Park1 model is based on the initial development of
(Jensen,1983)[54] and (Katic. et al,1987) [55]. The changes made from the Park1 to the Park2 model
includes a change in the modelling of surface reﬂections as this was included in the initial model but
omitted for Park2 as later CFD studies have proved the wake-interaction with the surface is incorrectly

the two models is that PARK1 is based on a quadratic wake superposition whilst the Park2 model on
the contrary is based on the linear wake superposition[56]. The ﬁnal main diﬀerence between the two
models is the inconsistency of the wind speed deﬁcit formulation for Park1 of which is corrected for
in the PARK2 model.[56].

Internal and external wake eﬀects
The term internal wakes is used for the wake eﬀect from the interference of neighbouring wind turbines
within the same wind farm. In order to avoid lost production from internal wakes, the wind farm layout
should minimize the turbine rows in the downwind direction by increasing the distance in the downwind
direction. There is no uniform standard for the distance to be projected between the turbines in a
wind farm to reduce wake losses, as an increase in the distance limits the project area and increases the
cable length needed. However, using a spacing between 4-11 times the rotor diameter length(4-11D)
is often used for oﬀshore wind projects and in this study a distance of 8D is carried out. [59, 60]
The term external wakes is used to describe the wake eﬀects imposed on a wind farm from a second
wind farm of which is positioned in range where its wakes eﬀect the upward stream of the ﬁrst wind
farm. This is commonly used with the term clustered wind farms of which is used to describe wind
farms of which are situated close to each other. Clustered wind farms may optimize the electrical
infrastructure and require fewer substations and create synergies within operations, but at the same
time eﬀect each others production due to external wake eﬀects. The eﬀects imposed by external wakes
have as mentioned not been of high detail in the Park wake models and hence the eﬀect of external
wakes have been underestimated for wind farm clusters of which are developed using the Park model.
[61]
There is no uniform standard as to what distance a wind farm would need to have to another. In [62],
an investigation was carried out with the conclusion that distances for diﬀerent projects were ranging
between 2-14km for optimal spacing. A recent quantitative assessment for large wind farms using
15MW turbines on the US east coast showed that the wake eﬀects can carry up to 90km downwind
for large lease areas. Investigating further, they concluded that a distance of 7-8D between the wind
individual wind turbines were additionally a minimum distance required to reduce internal wake losses
of which highlights the large utilisation of areas needed for wind farm development [63].

6.3
Array cable layout
The objective behind optimization problems for an array cable layout is to minimize the total cost,
cable length and power losses. There is a great interest in minimizing cable costs as 15-30% of a wind
farm cost arises from electrical infrastructure;leading to immense savings for a single wind farm project
of large scale.[29] The choice of optimisation model varies as more advanced models require increased
computational time. The optimisation models of use are broken down to the three categories below:
Heuristics
The heuristic models are fastest computational algorithms and are rule-based or experience driven, but
might not always ﬁnd the optimal solution. They are commonly used when an initial fast computation
is needed. The models of widely use are the Greedy algorithm which follows a set of rules where e.g
the turbine in shortest distance to the OSS is connected ﬁrst. K-means clustering is another model of
which splits the wind farms into separate smaller clusters and optimizes the cable layout clusters to
achieve fast results.
Metaheuristics
Metaheuristic models take inspiration from natural processes such as the particle swarm optimization
method based on particle movement in the solution space. The genetic algorithm is also widely used
of which utilizes concepts from natural selection methods to ﬁnd the optimal solution.
Mixed Integer Linear Programming(MILP)
The most time-consuming, but precise model is MILP where the model is formulated mathematically
with linear constraints and discrete decision variables.
Common for all methods used for bottom- ﬁxed cable layout optimization are the common constraints
listed below:
• the power ﬂow leaving a turbine must be supported by a single cable
• diﬀerent cables, with diﬀerent capacities and costs, are available for choice
• the capacity of the chosen cable cannot be exceeded
• each substation can accommodate a speciﬁed maximum, say C, of cables
• cable crossing should be avoided

7.1
Stages of development
Similar to all other large infrastructure projects, FOWF’s must undergo several planning, testing and
consenting stages before the operational phase can begin. The lifetime of wind farms diﬀer from type,

included in the analysis but is a practice of which is today commonly seen for both onshore and now

1. Site identiﬁcation and selection
This stage aims to identify potential sites for the wind farm and assessing their suitability.
Factors such as wind speed, topography, and access to the power grid are considered. For open
door projects the site selection is a part of the business strategy for new projects of which areas
of interest are mapped out. For tendering projects framed by the government the site is already
selected but must be evaluated for its suitability in the developers portfolio of projects. Utsira
is a tendering project from the Norwegian Ministry of Petroleum and Energy (OED) supported
by the initial impact assessment by the Norwegian Water Resources and Energy Directorate
(NVE).[65]
2. Bid submission and award
After a site is marked as suitable for development, a bid document needs to be composed and
answer the bid criteria given for the tendering project. The time frame between the diﬀerent
stages diﬀer and thence the complexity of wind farm design, point of operation, installation,
operations and maintenance might only be a ﬁrst draft at this stage of which is developed if the
site is awarded. For Utsira, the sites that are leased give the permission for the developer to
conduct further impact assessments prior to the ﬁnal investment decision must be made.
3. Environmental and Social Impact Assessment
The aim of this stage is to assess the potential environmental and social impacts of the wind
farm. This includes studies on wildlife, noise pollution, and visual impact on the surrounding

community. A strategic environmental assessment(SEA) for the initial lease area for Utsira was

4. Financing and Construction
The aim of this stage is to secure the ﬁnancing for the project and construct the wind farm. This

out to the wind farm location. For bottom ﬁxed turbines the installation of turbines typically
takes place at the location itself using jack up vessels, by impact piling and lifting the hub and
blades from the vessel. Hence, the process for installation and construction deviates strongly
and new strategies are required. State support for Utsira Nord is only given to the two top

5. Operation & Maintenance
When the wind farm reaches operation, maintenance is carried out to ensure safe operations
and ensure that suﬃcient planned and unplanned maintenance for minor and major component
exchanges are conducted. Maintenance schedules for technicians are normally made in advance
in order to plan for the use of service operating vessels (SOV) and crew transfer vessels(CTV).
It is of common practice to lease the vessels for the period of operations.
6. Decommissioning
If the wind farm is not eligible for lifetime extension the wind farm will undergo decommissioning.
Recent attention to landﬁll of decommisioned turbine blades has urged the industry to look into
recyclable blades. This as the blades are made of composite materials of which are challenging
to recycle. Siemens Gamesa are currently leading the research in recyclable blades of which their
ﬁrst recyclable blade was produced in 2021 and installed in 2022 for RWE’s Kaskasi project on
the German coast. [68]
7.2
Auction design
The remaining part of this chapter will focus on the second stage, the auction design of a wind
farm lease. With the current rate of oﬀshore wind development, governments have sought to apply a
competitive allocation mechanism, further referred to as auctions, for the development areas of lease.
The government entities hold auctions with the aim to allocate the right to construct and operate a
wind farm in the area of lease. Additionally, the support scheme of which will be provided from the
government is determined. The relatively new auction procedure replaces administrative allocations
of permit and support schemes and is now of great popularity and use in Europe. In China and the
United States, the procedure diﬀers slightly as areas are leased through competitive solicitation for long
term contracts with public utilities. The bid criteria within the diﬀerent countries and projects diﬀer
from each other as diﬀerent aspects of support, strike price and qualitative criteria are evaluated.[69]
The shift from price-only to multi-criteria bids in oﬀshore wind auctions is driven by several factors
and policy objectives. As oﬀshore wind technology has matured, there is an increasing focus on factors
beyond just the bid price to evaluate the quality and feasibility of projects. Multi-criteria bids allow
authorities to consider various project attributes, such as technological advancements, environmental
impact, project timelines, and local competence development, to ensure the development of high-

quality and sustainable oﬀshore wind projects. Oﬀshore wind auctions are no longer solely focused
on achieving the lowest possible electricity price. European countries have broader policy objectives,
such as increasing renewable energy capacity, promoting innovation, supporting local industries, and
reducing greenhouse gas emissions. Multi-criteria bids enable the integration of these policy objectives
into the project selection process. By considering a range of criteria, countries can identify the most
suitable and beneﬁcial projects that align with their long-term energy and sustainability goals. This

than system integration and the eﬀect on the marine environment and climate.

Oﬀshore wind- a subsidised industry
In Chapter 7 the cost level in 2030 was mentioned as part of the bid criteria. This is equivalent to
the Levelised Cost of Energy (LCOE) of the project of which is both deﬁned as the average cost over
the lifetime of a project, taking into account the time value of money, and the average cost of what
the electricity must be sold at for its lifetime to break even the investment cost. The ﬁrst deﬁnition
is assumed to be the general simple deﬁnition and the latter used by IEA where the diﬀerence lies in
considering all costs that occur for a project developer, including taxes. For the sake of Utsira Nord,
the bid criteria requests a simple LCOE calculation. [78]
In cases where the LCOE is higher than the wholesale market electricity price, measures for economic
support are needed in order for it to be competitive with other means of electricity generation. Support
schemes for renewable energy production has become a big trend in the past decades as they are a
part of governments’ energy transition plan. Their support are often given through auctions where
the auctions designed for oﬀshore wind all have in common that the wind farm operator receives a
guaranteed price for a predeﬁned period called the strike price or bid price of which marks the gap
between the wholesale market price and a guaranteed price. The way in which this is set up diﬀers
from country to country and bid to bid, but speciﬁed for Utsira Nord is that the support scheme
will follow a two sided Contract for Diﬀerence (CfD) of which deﬁnes a lower and upper cost support
limit. This ensures that the developer pays in the diﬀerence from the strike price when higher and
receives state support for the hours of production when the market price is lower than the set lower
limit. However, speciﬁcally for Utsira Nord the CfD support scheme will only be given to the two top
developers in a separate CfD auction. Currently there is not much of which is known regarding the
state support other than that it will have a duration of 15 years and apply to the initial production
amount of 500MW.[79, 80]

Chapter 9
Discussion
The following section will include a general discussion of the results obtained in this study.
The
validation of the study is limited to the parameters and boundaries used for the site assessment and
economic assessment. Hence, the discussion is based on the values achieved based on the set limitation.
9.1
Economic analysis
The achieved LCOE level of 808NOK/MWh for Site 2 is not a competitive price for today’s wholesale
electricity market and as predicted, the sites are not feasible without subsidies of large size. However,
the LCOE achieved at 808NOK/MWh does lie within range of the projection by [70] on what the in-
dustry expects for 2030, with even further cost reductions projected towards 2035. Further assessment
after the CfD level is announced must include the following:
1. How much support is needed after the given CfD level is taken into account?
2. Will the remaining value be feasible to obtain through PPA’s and lump sum support from
ENOVA?
The answers to the above can only be derived knowing the CfD level. As found, a CfD level equal
to Sørlige Nordsjø at 660NOK/MWh, and a lump sump support of 2.3bn from ENOVA based on
the Hywind Tampen 88MW project would leave the project short of 3.7bn NOK to reach economic
feasibility. There is no doubt the three projects will together require a substantial amount of economic
support from the Norwegian State. Further economic evaluation must therefore be conducted once
the CfD allocation round has taken place and will heavily rely on the support level chosen by OED.
The outcome of the NPV and IRR followingly shows a great sensitivity with changes to the electricity
price, AEP, CAPEX and OPEX cost and discount rate used. The investigation made is moreover
limited to the mentioned factors and has not looked into further sensitivity to changes in inﬂation from
the constant 2% used in the calculation or on changes in the depreciation period of 30 years. Further
sensitivity analysis is therefore important to conduct on measures not included in the conducted
analysis.
In addition to the mentioned factors of uncertainty for the 495MW site, the extension to Vestavind F
would prove an additional measure of uncertainty for a 2035 CAPEX of extension. As for this case,
the extension would entail 255MW and would prove feasible for a cost level reduction in CAPEX as
assumed. However, how much the CAPEX cost reduction is aﬀected by the build-out of the ﬁrst
495MW relies on the buildout of Utsira and other earlier projected projects. The certainty of the cost
reduction forecasted for 2035 should be further investigated and conducted together with the supplier
industry. Additionally, the large postponements in delivery due to the current supply chain shortage
might be volatile to any unpredicted changes in the economy, political frameworks or unforeseen
geopolitical incidents.

9.2
Wind resource assessment
The power of data availability and correct measurements are critical for accurate wind resource as-
sessments. By using both LiDAR and mesoscale data the study showed the same prevailing wind
direction. As stated the reported mean wind speed diﬀers from 11.1m/s for NORSEWInd to 10.1m/s
for NEWA. Followingly, analysis for the AEP calculation in WAT presents a 10% diﬀerence for the
net AEP for the two datasets. This is possible as the rated wind speed of the IEA 15MW turbine used
is 10.6m/s of which means that higher wind speeds will not generate higher power. The importance
of further measurement campaigns is therefore evident from the results. Using the NEWA mesoscale
data lead to a higher LCOE due to lower AEP results and thence higher bid price for a developer,
whilst results from NORSEWInD would give a lower bid price but risk a lower actual production of
higher extent. From the bid criteria the developers are asked to show predictions of AEP based on
diﬀerent conﬁdence intervals. For the calculations in this project the P50 values have been further
used, but as the projects entail high uncertainty, it might be discussed whether only the P90 levels are
of relevance for ﬁnal bankability calculations. Table 9.1 depicts the diﬀerence obtained for the three
sites in terms of the two conﬁdence levels investigated.

The resource assessment was conducted using WAsP 12 where set parameters for onshore wind resource
assessment were adjusted to match oﬀshore topography characteristics. It was assumed that FOWT’s
don’t deviate from bottom-ﬁxed and therefore no further corrections were made for. In reality, the
AEP might still be eﬀected heavily by prolonged downtimes during operation and maintenance of
which have not been investigated further in this thesis. Choice of wake model and major changes to
O&M downtimes are therefore vital to investigate to improve the wind resource assessment speciﬁcally
for ﬂoating wind projects. By omitting O&M downtime in the AEP calculation, the actual full load
hours are not presented as such. However, as the O&M is normally conducted in periods of good
weather and lower wind speeds the actual impact of downtime cannot be commented upon without
further analysis.
The turbine layout was chosen to be set as 3x11 initially as it proved to be a simple layout of which
could ﬁt the width of the deﬁned area. In addition it would minimize the number of rows of which
would induce larger wake eﬀects taking the prevailing wind directions into account. However, further
analysis into the third site and its potential need for a deviation in the turbine layout due to the two
existing gas pipes would eﬀect not only the third site but also the external wakes induced from the
new layout. A layout for optimal spacing was used for modelling the TurbOPark model and hence the
results from this model needs to be compared to a WAT calculation of similar layout in order to be
further commented upon.

9.4
Norway’s climate goals and obligations
Whether ﬂoating wind is the preferred renewable technology of which should be given great amount
of subsidies, compared to other renewable energy solutions in Norway, has not been discussed. The
immense future power demand must be met and with FOW it can be met without large intervention
in populated areas. Norway’s coastline is especially exceptional and one could argue that if ﬂoating
wind is not built of large scale outside Norway then there might not be incentives to do so in other
countries.
Even though Utsira Nord is projected to be connected to the onshore grid, one might
discuss the moral behind the use of ﬂoating oﬀshore wind as electriﬁcation of oil and gas platforms
instead of additional power to the grid. The world is still heavily reliant on oil and gas, but as the
world aims to cut its reliance on fossil fuels, enhanced focus on strengthening the transmission grid is
crucial for future energy systems. Developing oﬀshore wind projects supporting both the grid and not
only oil and gas platforms is one way of which the country can help increase the share of renewable
energy in the power system.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
10.1
Concluding remarks
This thesis carried out a pre-feasibility study of the three leased sites at Utsira Nord in Norway for
FOWF development. It entailed both an investigation into the expected AEP for diﬀerent production
scenarios, wind farm layouts and wake eﬀect models and an economic calculation to derive the LCOE
and evaluate the projects from both a socio-economic and private-economic perspective.
Additionally, an insight into the design of the bid criteria was presented. Interviews with two employees
from Ørsted were conducted to get an insight on their perception of the weights given and advantage
and limitation for optimal build out of FOW at Utsira Nord. A comparison was made to the ScotWind
auction to investigate whether the two FOW bid criteria deviates from each other and to pinpoint
any requirements set for ScotWind that Utsira Nord could beneﬁt from. It was found that including
a Supply Chain Development Statement (SCDS) with a speciﬁc required percentage of Norwegian
suppliers could strengthen the Norwegian FOW supplier industry further.
The economic analysis found that per now, it is not feasible to invest in either of the three sites
without knowing the 15-year CfD level of which will be decided 1-2 years after award. This due to
the LCOE commencing to 808NOK/MWh for the development of one site of 495MW. If OED sets the
CfD level to the same level as for the bottom-ﬁxed project Sørlige Nordsjø, of which is to be developed
over approximately the same timeframe, predictions of a strike price of 660NOK/MWh is forecasted
of which would not prove feasible for either of the sites at Utsira Nord. Neither would it be feasible
if a CfD level of 660NOK/MWh and a lump sum payment from ENOVA of the same amount given
to the ﬂoating 88MW project Hywind Tampen, 2.3bn NOK, would be given. The break even CfD
strike price, converted to the nearest NOK is 1001NOK/MWh. If the CfD level nonetheless would be
ﬁxed at 660NOK/MWh then a lump sum support payment of 6bn NOK would be necessary for the
developer to outsource. Without a CfD, a lump sum payment of 8.2bn NOK would be required.
In terms of the AEP, it is expected that the three wind farms will annually produce 2.35 TWh,
2.37TWh and 2.38TWh based on calculations in WAsP of where the PARK2 wake model is used.
The diﬀerence in production level using the TurbOPark wake model should be further iterated as the
analysis was done on a diﬀerent layout than of the one used for AEP calculations in WAT. Due to the
two prevailing wind directions at south-east and northwest with the same frequency, it is expected
that external wake loss will be perceived by all three sites for diﬀerent prevailing wind directions where
the second site will for most directions see a wind speed deﬁcit due to its positioning.
When analysing the AEP at each site and combining them with the geographic restrictions of the sites
and coexistence of area utilisation, it became evident that Site 3 is the most vulnerable in terms of
the two gas pipes that will cross the area. All mooring lines that are to be laid will have to avoid the
area it covers with additional safety margins. With the uncertainty in interaction and eﬀect of the
Assessing the potential of ﬂoating oﬀshore wind at Utsira Nord: A pre-feasibility study
65
==PAGE:79==
military zone for Site 1, further details to the SEA should be conducted before a ﬁnal conclusion on
the preferred site is stated. Additionally, the location of the OSS will aﬀect the cable layout and cable
costs of which should be known before drawing a ﬁnal conclusion. Nevertheless, assuming that the
OSS is positioned at Utsira Nord and that the lowest impact is seen for Site 2, it is further concluded
that Site 2 is the most preferred solely based on the set framework by this study. Despite the current
unfeasible cost level of Utsira Nord and uncertainty of subside scheme, there is a great interest from
the industry to develop the three sites to scale up ﬂoating wind to a commercial scale. From a societal
perspective, an abundant local competence for ﬂoating oﬀshore structures already exists in the oil and
gas industry. OED has clearly communicated that ﬂoating wind is a solution on Norway’s emission
reduction agenda and this is highlightes with the 15 further areas of expansion being assessed. Hence,
if the sites at Utsira Nord are not built out one may question if further build-out of the areas for
screening will occur.
10.2
Further Work
In order to obtain further wind resource- and site assessment it is highly recommended to iterate the
procedure in this study using LiDAR data from the ﬂoating LiDAR measurement campaign in current
operation. This in order to utilize measured data at the speciﬁc site location of which will not be
aﬀected by the orography of Utsira island as the NORSEWInD data was found to be. Additional wake
models should be conducted for optimized layouts for all sites as the developer will have to align with
the military zone, shipping route and gas pipes to ensure safe installation, maintenance and operation.
This may lead to further restrictions in the layout for Utsira Nord and Vestavind F. Once the ﬁnal
layout is decided, it is crucial that further calculations based on certainty of energy yield with factors
calculated speciﬁcally for the uncertainties for BoP and turbine availability are carried out. Further
break downs of diﬀerent conﬁdence intervals and expected down-time for planned and un-planned
maintenance are additionally factors of high importance for further AEP calculations.
The transmission scope is yet to be deﬁned from OED, but when known it will be crucial to analyse its
eﬀect on the array cable layout and to what extent extra project costs in terms of transmission might
be deployed for the developers. Mooring line- and array cable layout for ﬂoating wind is a relatively
new ﬁeld of research and it is recommended to deploy further analysis on the hydrostatic eﬀects and
power loss assumptions and failure rates for the coupling of static and dynamic cables to assess its
aﬀect on the AEP, installation and maintenance cost. An optimization model for the layout of Utsira
Nord should be conducted for both cost minimization and to model for the mooring lines of which
cannot cross the lazy wave projection of the array cables.
Mapping of the supply chain industry in Norway and the possible percentage of national suppliers for
the project would also be of interest for both developers and OED as the auction holder.
As stated in section 1.3, the pre-feasibility study is limited to the wind resource assessment, economic
analysis in addition to regulatory measures. A risk assessment and detailed cost beneﬁt analysis for
the Norwegian State is recommended to conduct. The results from the socio-economic evaluation
conducted in this study can be further used as the measure in detailed cost beneﬁt analysis.

Appendix A
Interview 1
The ﬁrst interviewee works with the planning of operations and maintenance for the development
project in order to develop and ensure that the operating strategies and choice of port will be the
most eﬀective and eﬃcient choice for operations.
What is the main challenge for your scope within the Utsira Nord bid?
We have never developed ﬂoating oﬀshore wind farms before and therefore all concepts must be based
on the best assumption we derive. All costs, assumptions and strategies cannot be benchmarked to an
existing project which leads to a complexity of assessing the extent of realism and feasibility behind.
This includes everything from mooring line strategy to how we plan to execute major component
exchanges.
What are your thoughts towards the bid criteria and the weights given?
Bankability is extremely important and was actually altered by the Ministry as the initial proposal
for bid criteria to the industry did not weigh this as the highest criterion. After feedback from the
big developers they changed the points. The reason behind the feedback is the stress felt by even the
large companies. Therefore, experience and the economic power to deliver such projects are important
especially when its a ﬁrst of its kind. We see already that the industry is challenged, Equinor pulled
out of its own development plan for ﬂoating wind, Trollvind, to power Troll natural gas platform. So
given that, 30 points for bankability seems fair. However, I’m surprised that "Positive local beneﬁts"
and "Sustainability", here especially environmental measures, weren’t weighed higher. I assume that
undermines where the main interest lies; in cost reduction. It seems as if the most important factor
for Utsira Nord is that it turns out to eﬀect the Norwegian citizens’ pockets, part of the Norway’s
welfare system, in the mildest manner possible. In terms of the positive local beneﬁts, I reckon that
the points given are not as high as expected because Norway already has a lot of industries related
to oﬀshore wind rising at the moment. The reason for why I thought it would be more is the large
focus on the use of local expertise, labour and knowledge from the oil and gas industry. In terms of
sustainability they have really only focused on the co-existence of industries and here pinpointed the
ﬁshing industry, but other measures to sustainability is far less weighted than compared to the dutch
bid.
Why did Ørsted bid for a ﬂoating project which will see higher cost levels than bottom-
ﬁxed project?
If we really want to be the leader in oﬀshore wind then we need to be developing ﬂoating wind as well.
If we cannot join this race we will not keep our position as the leader in the oﬀshore wind market.
How do you estimate project costs?
We have dedicated CAPEX and OPEX analysts that make the LCOE for the bid. Diﬀerent scenarios
for the state support contract for diﬀerence is included to analyse the feasibility of diﬀerent support
levels. As most of the competitors are new to ﬂoating wind, apart from small pilot projects, all bidders
will have large uncertainties related to costs and therefore the importance of consortiums and partners
is high as they can help validate the costs for their part which provides a more realistic cost estimation

How do you estimate the cost level for 2035?
We have never been asked to develop cost level ﬁve years in the operation phase for a possible expan-

Other bids are more interested in system integration, but maybe little emphasis is given as the ﬂoating

Appendix B
Interview 2
The second interviewee works with CAPEX costing form development for the diﬀerent screening
projects. Therefore, she doesn’t work with Utsira Nord now, but worked with the Norwegian strategy
when they ﬁrst announced interest in oﬀshore wind some years back. She works in fast business case
analysis where the project narrative is ﬁrst described with the location, WTG size, number of turbines,
harbour location, place of which materials will be sourced from and more factors that are needed in
the project costing form. They use high level tools for smaller packages and elements that are harder
to estimate.
How has your work changed from bottom-ﬁxed projects to ﬂoating projects?
Well we haven’t had a ﬂoating speciﬁc costing form before. Starting it was high level but now quite
detailed with all the diﬀerent activities from mooring pre-lay, hook up, to the integration of WTG.
There are many aspects regarding the assembly that must be taken into account. For example how the
ﬂoater is delivered and what assembly stages it will need. The array cables are now more expensive
because they are dynamic. Changing the vessel duration and cable length then needs to be looked at
as well as an eﬀect of this. In addition it eﬀects the subsea investigations which will diﬀer because you
have anchoring points and not monopiles which must be inspected. All of these factors lead to a higher
DEVEX because new technology and deployment strategies must be carried out. We expect the cost
of the WTG to increase as well because it needs some adjustments from previous bottom-ﬁxed design,
an example is that a new controller will be adopted. We assume the OEM’s will charge extra for this.
When can you be satisﬁed with the level of detail of diﬀerent costs?
The diﬀerence between the level of detail and level of precision is diﬀerent and whether or not the
generic assumptions used are suitable. You can trace all assumptions used, typical forecasted material
rates are not investigated further for ﬂoating but will use the generic forecast numbers. So I would
say there is a medium level of detail as we don’t go into the 30-50 elements per package. We normally
build the CAPEX for screening projects in a week.
How do you achive the cost levels for 2030 and 2035?
We have an own organ in ﬁnance responsible for all pricing estimates forecasts that are then inserted
in the costing form. Vessel rates and cable prices might be based on a past contracts so the impact of
time is diﬃcult to get. The 2035 cost level might also be subject to a choice of a diﬀerent and larger
turbine size which makes the analysis more complex. Cost reduction will be diﬃcult to assume if a
larger turbine is available, so the cost levels are based on the best forecasts and assumptions we have.
The auction committee penalizes assumptions that are not "realistic", how do you ensure
that yours are?
This comes down to technology availability. Are 132kV cables ready to be used by 2030, and would
they be available in 2035? How much industrialisation can we expect. Would manufacturers bring
down the costs because it’s commercial or could something else happen that would alter the expected
LCOE drop? I just spoke with someone about this today in terms of the cable laying rates where it
Assessing the potential of ﬂoating oﬀshore wind at Utsira Nord: A pre-feasibility study
75
==PAGE:89==
looks like the vessel rates won’t go down in the future. So this undermined that just estimating that
everything will be cheaper is not correct.
As you deal with early phase screening of projects, what wind data is used in the site
assessment?
For early stage screening, we order mesoscale nodes from Vortex under the Copernicus programme for
European Union’s Earth Observation Programme. They are basically open to the public as well but
you have to pay a bit for the data. However, it is very uncertain and in some regions very diﬀerent from
reality. Only if we are very interested do we deploy lidars, but measurement campaigns are normally
not held after several milestones in the screening and development stages are overcome. Normally we
only have measurement campaigns when we know that we will bid for a site, but for my scope of work,
the screening phase, the mesoscale data is what we use.
