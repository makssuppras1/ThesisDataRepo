Abstract
Disc brake noise remains to be a matter of great concern in the automotive industry. A common
way to counteract brake squeal is to add damping to the brake system by attaching damping ’shims’
to the brake pad. Those shims are layered structures, consisting of stiff base and constraining layers
and a viscoelastic damping interlayer. For the prediction of using ﬁnite element (FE) methods, the
damping performance of the shim, a full description of the dynamic and damping properties of the
damping material is necessary.
In the study at hand, the frequency and temperature dependent Young’s modulus and loss factor of
the rubber material, used in three layered composite test plates, were determined. The material
description is based on a ﬁve parameter fractional derivative model. The vibrational response
of the test plates was measured in terms of mobility up to 14 kHz and at various temperatures
between -5 °C and 60 °C. From the response curves, natural frequencies and modal loss factors
were determined. A FE model of the test plates was built, including damping by introducing a
complex stiffness. By conducting an eigenvalue analysis, Young’s modulus and loss factor of the
rubber material were ﬁtted to the experimentally found modal frequencies and modal damping loss
factors. Subsequently, the parameters in the ﬁve parameter fractional derivative model were ﬁtted
to the modal data Young’s modulus and loss factor. The procedure was conducted repeatedly for
various temperatures. Eventually, a description of frequency dependent Young’s modulus and loss
factor of the rubber material at separate various temperatures was established.

CHAPTER 1
Background and Motivation
Brake noise has been of great concern in the automotive industry since the 1930s and has been inves-
tigated extensively by many researchers and the industry in the past decades. As customers claim
brake noise to be an indication of performance problems of the brake, not to mention drawbacks in
comfort, it constitutes a major percentage of warranty costs in the automotive industry. However,
factually it has been proven that brake noise has only little inﬂuence on the brake performance
[Dunlap et al., 1999] [Triches et al., 2004].
A distinction is drawn between three categories of brake noise, namely low frequency noise,
low frequency squeal, and high frequency squeal. The former is characterized by a broadband
noise between 100 Hz and 1000 Hz, whereas low frequency squeal and high frequency squeal
are classiﬁed as noise with narrow bandwidths above 1000 Hz and above 5 kHz, respectively
[Dunlap et al., 1999] [Trichês et al., 2007]. The latter are considered to be the more severe cate-
gories, as the human ear is particularly sensitive to noise in that frequency range.
Brake noise is considered to be a consequence of friction induced vibration. There are various
theories about the excitation mechanisms that set the brake system into vibration. Under certain
conditions, ’stick-slip’ motion or ’sprag-slip’ explains how vibration might build up, whereas
’mode coupling’ of the brake components is generally believed to be a requirement for brake
squeal occurrence [Duffour, 2002]. Prior to the thesis work at hand, an extensive literature review
on squeal mechanisms has been conducted and a comprehensive documentation on brake squeal
related phenomena was established. Therefore, for further introductory reading it is referred to
[Hollweck, 2016].
A common way to reduce brake squeal noise in automotive disc brakes is to add damping to
the brake system by means of constrained damping layers. These damping ’shims’ are attached to
the back plate of the brake pad, customarily to the back of the plate, and consist of three components:
a stiff base layer, a stiff constraining layer, and a thin interlayer consisting of viscoelastic damping
material. Structural losses are mainly due to shear deformation within the interlayer and damping
performance depends strongly on the damping and dynamic properties of the damping material.
When a problematic brake system is to be modiﬁed by adding damping shims, the optimal design

of the shim for the particular problem plays a crucial role. Trial and error approaches can be used
to ﬁnd the optimal solution. However, predicting of damping behavior using ﬁnite element (FE)
analysis of the structure becomes more and more attractive, as it uses time and costs. Modeling
damping shims requires a full description of the damping and dynamic properties of the damping
material.
The study at hand describes a method that can be used to determine the damping and dynamic
properties of viscoelastic damping materials, based on the ﬁve parameter fractional derivative
material model established by [Pritz, 2003]. Three layered composite test plates, damped by a thin
viscoelastic interlayer, were the main subjects of the project. Vibrational response measurements
on the test plates were used to determine natural frequencies and modal damping loss factors up to
14 kHz at various temperatures between -5 °C and 60 °C. A ﬁnite element eigenvalue analysis on
the test plates was used to adapt the material properties of the damping material so that simulation
results match the experimental results. COMSOL Multiphysics was used for the ﬁnite element anal-
ysis. Damping was modeled by a complex stiffness, which is described by the material parameters

This chapter covers the theoretical concepts of structural dynamics and vibration damping that

2.1
Fundamental Concepts of Vibration
In this section, the fundamentals of vibration and structure-borne sound are introduced. This is done
following descriptions as in [Cremer et al., 2005] and [Kinsler, 2000], and partly [Mead, 1999].
Detailed derivations are passed up and it is referred to the literature.
2.1.1
Introduction
In the ﬁeld of vibration and structure-borne sound, a ’system’ that is to be investigated is gener-
ally speaking a matter that responds to an input quantity by an output quantity. The response is
determined by the structures particular dynamic behavior which can be described by its ’transfer’
characteristics. For example, a system can be excited by a harmonic force at a certain position (input
quantity), and the structures response is evaluated in terms of displacement at another position
(output quantity). The ratio force over displacement could be considered as the ’transfer function’.
A system might consist just of a few components, as for example the undamped harmonic oscillator
in classical mechanics consists of two components (mass and spring, c.f. Sec. 2.1.2), but can also
be a larger structure like a beams or plates. In the study at hand, the system of interest are damped
test plates. They consist of three layers, a thick base plate, a thin viscoelastic interlayer, and a thin
constraining steel top plate.

The calibration of the laser was done by a B&K vibration calibrator type 4294

Subsequently the velocity channel gain was adjusted to 1 V to obtain the desired velocity level.
3.2.2
Impact Hammer
The setup on-site at MENETA was used to do a set of measurements at ambient temperature using
the impact hammer method. In this setup, the plate under test rests on a foam material block which
represents free boundary conditions. The plate is excited with a short impulse with a B&K force
transducer/impact hammer type 8203, which measures the exciting force. The vibrational response
is measured by a laser vibrometer in terms of acceleration a. The frequency response is evaluated
in terms of accelerance, which is the transfer function A(f) = a(f)/F(f).
Direct and transfer accelerance have been measured. The former was measured at the center of the
plate, denoted by A00, the latter from one end of the plate to the opposite end, denoted as A−ll.
3.2.3
Temperature Dependency
The effect of temperature on the damping and dynamic properties of the damping material was
investigated by performing direct mobility measurements on the test plates using the shaker method
at various temperatures. The plates were heated up in an oven and cooled down in a freezer and
fridge, respectively. Subsequently the setup and method as described in Section 3.2.1 was used.
The generator and FFT analyzer settings were changed to a frequency span of 25.6 kHz base band
and 6400 lines. This yields a resolution of 4 Hz. This change was necessary, as the measurements
had to be carried out in one quick step to prevent the test plates from changing the temperature
while measuring. The frequency span could be adjusted only to certain discrete values. The next
lower value is 12.8 kHz, which is not able to cover the frequency range of interest of 14 kHz. For
this reason the frequency span had to be set to 25.6 kHz.
The temperature of the test plates was controlled by a FLUKE infrared thermometer type 752.
This device determines the surface temperature of the object under investigation by measuring the
infrared radiation, which in turn depends on the emissivity of the surface material. The thermometer
has to be provided with the emissivity value of the surface to be measured, in this case the test
plates. According to the FLUKE information sheet about emissivity values of common materials,
the values for steel vary between 0.24 for freshly rolled steel and 0.96 for rough steel surface.
Measuring the test plates at ambient temperature (i.e. 25°C) and adjusting the emissivity value has

around 7 kHz.
Estimating modulus of elasticity from bending modes.
It can be assumed that the ﬁrst two
distinct resonance peaks in Figure 3.5 correspond to the ﬁrst two symmetric bending modes of
the plate as the plate is driven at its center and no antisymmetric bending modes can be excited
(cf. Section 2.1.3). Furthermore the frequencies are too low that they might correspond to higher
order plate modes. In this range, the plate can be considered factually as a beam. This will be
investigated further and supported in Section 4.2.4, when the mode shapes are determined by ﬁnite
element model. Under that assumption, the Young’s modulus of the steel can be calculated by
means of Equation 2.13. The mass per unit length of the blank steel beam is m′ = M/L = 1.94
kg/m and the area moment of inertia can be calculated to I = WH3/12 = 5.2 · 10−10 m4. Here,
M is the total mass of the plate, W is the width and H is the thickness, introduced in Section 3.1.
With the coefﬁcients Cn corresponding to each of both mode shapes (cf. Figure 2.3), the Young’s
modulus can be calculated from Equation 2.13 as 188.3 GPa for the ﬁrst mode and 185.5 GPa for
the second mode, as summarized in Table 3.3. This ﬁnding might suggest a decreasing Young’s
modulus of the material with increasing frequency. However, the Young’s modulus of metals can
usually be considered as fairly independent on frequency [Cremer et al., 2005]. Other phenomena,
as for example locally changes in stiffness or density might be the reason for the deviations that
have been found. In any case, a mean value Emean = 186.9 GPa of the two values may be taken as
a good initial estimation of the materials modulus of elasticity. This will be investigated further
when implementing the material properties in the ﬁnite element model in Section 4.2.4.

Total Loss Factors
The total modal loss factors (in the following denoted as ηtot) for each plate are determined
by means of the frequency response curves. The 1 dB-, 2 dB-, and 3 dB-bandwidths are used
to determine the total loss factor at each of the four distinct resonance peaks as found in the
measurements, using Eq. 2.17. The standard deviation of the values using different bandwidths
has been calculated, in order to ensure that the results do not deviate from each other signiﬁcantly.
By that it was guaranteed that only the bandwidth of the resonance peak of interest is taken into
account and no neighboring peaks inﬂuence the result. This might be the case particularly for the
two high frequency modes, as many modes are close in frequency here. However, no signiﬁcant
deviation was found between the 3 methods. The maximum standard deviation was found for
the 0.20 mm rubber damped plate at a temperature of -5°C at the lowest mode to be 0.0032 at a
value for the total loss factor of 0.018. In general the standard deviation does not exceed the order
of magnitude of 10−4. For this reason, in the following, the total loss factors are presented with
their mean values determined by the three various bandwidth methods and no standard deviation is
considered. The loss factors for all plates, at all temperatures are summarized in Tables A.3 and

3.4
Summary
The vibrational response has been measured for the blank steel plate and the various damped test
plates at temperatures -5°C, 10°C, 20°C, 30°C, 45°C and 60°C. Two methods have been tested:
using a shaker that excites the plates at the very center, and using an impact hammer exciting the
plates either close to the center or at one end. Direct and transfer response functions have been
investigated. It turned out that direct mobility using the shaker method leads to results of best
quality and are therefore most reliable. For the rest of the report, only direct mobility at the center
of the plates using the shaker method will be considered.
In the measured response curves, four distinct resonance peaks at around 800 Hz, 4000 Hz, 10.5
kHz and 12 kHz could be identiﬁed. The corresponding modes are in the rest of the report referred
to as modes 1, 2, 3, and 4.
The response curve of the blank steel plate measured at ambient temperature was used to estimate
an initial value of the Young’s modulus of the steel to E = 186.9 GPa. Measurement results at
various temperatures suggest a temperature dependent Young’s modulus that is decreasing with
increasing temperature.
From the four resonance peaks in the frequency response curves of the damped plates, modal loss
factors have been determined. The rubber damped plates show consistently increasing damping
with increasing damping layer thickness over all modal frequencies that have been investigated.
Furthermore, a signiﬁcant increase in modal damping can be observed from mode 1 to mode 2,
mode 2 and mode 3 are almost equally damped, and a slight decrease in damping becomes apparent
between mode 3 and mode 4.
The adhesive damped plates have shown a more irregular behavior when it comes to modal damping.
Increasing damping with increasing damping layer thickness was observed only for the ﬁrst mode,
higher order modes show a converse trend.
It has been observed that the damping performance of both rubber and adhesive damped plates
depend strongly on temperature. For both types of test plates, maximum damping was observed
around 20 °C for modes 2 to 4. Mode 1 shows rather a temperature independence for the rubber
damped plates and signiﬁcantly enhanced temperature dependency for the adhesive damped plates.
As the rubber damped plates show an overall more consistent behavior, it is these plates that will be
used in the following to establish a ﬁnite element model of constrained layer damped plates.

Finite Element Analysis
The essential part of the study at hand is based on ﬁnite element analysis of the sample plates. The
material parameters that describe the damping and dynamic behavior of the damping material have
to be adapted in the ﬁnite element model in such a way that the simulation results stand in good
accordance with the experimental results. By adjusting the material properties so that a match
between simulation and experiment can be achieved, a material model of the damping material can
be established. This in turn allows ﬁnite element simulations of a larger system, e.g. a full brake
system, to include damping material models and subsequently to predict vibrational behavior of the
system after modiﬁcation by adding damping.
This chapter covers some basic concepts of ﬁnite element modeling and ﬁnite element vibration
analysis that are fundamental for this study. Furthermore, the modeling of the test plates using
COMSOL Multiphysics is described.
4.1
Fundamentals of Finite Element Vibration Analysis
This section describes brieﬂy the underlying concepts of ﬁnite element vibration analysis that are
important for the following considerations. It is mainly based on descriptions as they can be found
in [Cook, 1981] and [Cook, 1995].
For a ﬁnite element structure, consisting of a ﬁnite number of elements with a certain number of
degrees of freedom (d.o.f.), Equation 2.1 can be rewritten by replacing each component by a vector,
containing information about each nodal d.o.f.. This yields the governing equation

and {d}. These quantities in turn are built by means of the element type speciﬁc shape functions
and the material properties. For an undamped linear elastic solid, Young’s modulus E, Poisson’s
ratio ν, and density ρ are required to build the matrices. If damping is included, an additional
parameter is required to build the damping matrix, for example the material loss factor η. In the

The blank steel plate constitutes the simplest case. No damping is included yet. For this reason, it

In the experiment using the shaker, the plate under test is attached to the exciter by screwing its

Material Properties
Three material parameters are needed to describe the linear elastic behavior of an isotropic solid,
namely density ρ, Young’s modulus E, and Poisson’s ratio ν. With these quantities, the stiffness
as well as the mass matrix can be created and Eq. 4.1 can be solved. In this case, no damping is
included in the model. Furthermore, the material properties of the steel can be considered to be
constant over frequency.

value of the experimentally determined values at mode 1 and 2 (cf. Section 3.3), which yields a
value of 186.9 GPa. Poisson’s ratio was taken as 0.33, which is the default value for ’Structural
steel’ in COMSOL. The density of 7764 kg/m3 is obtained from dimensions and measured total
mass (cf. Section 3.1).
So far all the important aspects that are necessary to build the ﬁnite element model of the blank
steel plate have been discussed. The boundaries of the plate are free, except the inner hole surface,
where an harmonic boundary force is applied. Triangular prisms are used as elements and the mesh
size is reﬁned over the frequency range, as described previously. For the material properties, initial
estimates as described above are used.

The previous ﬁndings from both experiments as well as ﬁnite element analysis can be used to
estimate the ﬁve parameters in the ﬁve parameter fractional derivative model introduced in Section

During this minimization process, focus can be placed either on the response curves or on the
modal behavior in terms of natural frequencies and modal damping. The latter is chosen in the
study at hand, however, also the other method is introduced brieﬂy in the following, at it seems to
be the obvious choice in the ﬁrst place. The process of ﬁtting the parameters is described in detail
in this section and results are presented and compared to the experiments.
5.1

Initially two methods were considered to estimate the ﬁve parameters of the ﬁve parameter fraction
derivative model. One method ﬁts the parameters with respect to the response curves, the other
focuses on the modal damping and natural frequencies. Eventually only the latter was used. For
the sake of completeness, both of them are presented in the following.

5.2.1
Step 1: Solving Equation 5.2
Solving Equation 5.2 with MATLAB using fminsearch
Two approaches were attempted to solve Equation 5.2. For the ﬁrst attempt, COMSOL was
connected to MATLAB via LiveLinkTM for MATLAB and the MATLAB function fminsearch
was used to solve Equation 5.2. The objective function contains two important steps. First, the
ﬁnite element model is solved for eigenfrequencies at one particular desired natural frequency.
Subsequently, the objective function of 5.2 is calculated. This is done for each mode separately.
This is explained more thoroughly exemplary for the ﬁrst mode in the following.
When treating the ﬁrst mode separately, Equation 5.2 is reduced to only one sum of squared
residuals instead of four. The variables to be optimized are Er and ηr. When using fminsearch, an
initial estimate of the variables to be optimized has to be provided. This set of variables is used as a
starting point by the function. In the particular case, Er,0 = 0.1 GPa and ηr,0 = 0.5 might be set
as initial estimates. This choice is based on reconsidering the values that were chosen in Section
4.3.3. A value of Er = 1 GPa seemed too high, however, the choice for ηr is rather arbitrary but
still reasonable. During the ﬁrst iteration of the minimization process, the ﬁnite element model is
the solved for the eigenfrequency around 720 Hz (corresponding to the ﬁrst mode) with the initial
material parameters. Subsequently the summed square of residuals in Equation 5.2 is calculated. In
the subsequent iteration, material parameters Er and ηr are varied according to the derivative-free
algorithm fminserch is based on. This procedure is repeated until the stopping criteria are met.
Eventually a local minimum is found. The stopping criteria were deﬁned in terms of termination
tolerance of the function value as well as termination tolerance on the variable. The former was set
to 5e-5, whereas the latter was set to 0.1. It is important to mention that Er as input variable was
set in units GPa. That means a factor of 109 is included in the objective function and not in the
variable itself. Through that it is achieved that both variables Er and ηr have the same order of
magnitude. This is desirable to make the minimization algorithm work properly. If the variables
cover several orders of magnitude, a small step size for one variable might correspond to a large
step size for the other and vice versa, which might cause problems in ﬁnding the local minimum.

5.3
Summary and Discussion
This section described the parameter ﬁtting method for the ﬁve parameter fractional derivative
material model. Various approaches have been attempted. The method that has been chosen
consists of two steps. First, the modal values were ﬁtted. This is done by ﬁtting the material
properties Er and ηr, so that simulated modal values for natural frequency and loss factor match
the experimentally determined at four distinct modes. Subsequently, the parameters in the ﬁve
parameter model are ﬁtted to the modal values for Er and ηr.
This choice was made based on trying to keep computation cost low. The obvious choice would
be, as mentioned, to implement the ﬁve parameter model in the ﬁnite element model and solve
Equation 5.1 directly. It was expected that this method will be computationally very expensive
as the ﬁnite element model has to be solved in the frequency domain and for many frequencies.
Therefore this method was rejected. However, it could also be that this is the much more accurate
method. The method that has been chosen consists of two minimization processes. This in turn
means, that there are two sources that lead to errors, that add up and might affect the ﬁnal results.
Furthermore, the chosen method is based on modal values. This means, strictly, the obtained modal
results for Er and ηr are only valid at the particular modes with the corresponding mode shapes
of the particular structure. Those values are acceptable for an estimation of the model of a full
frequency dependent description, but are to be treated with care. Considering the purpose of the
damping material, namely to be applied in damping shims for brake systems that have approxi-
mately the same shape and dimensions as the damped test plates, the choice of focusing on modal
values is still justiﬁed. However, it must be kept in mind that for this reason, the estimated material
model is not a generic description of the material properties bit limit to the case of application in
layered structures as the test plates or damping shims.
==PAGE:74==
56 CHAPTER 5. PARAMETER ESTIMATION OF FIVE PARAMETER MATERIAL MODEL
The estimation of the modal values Er,n and ηr,n is based on parametric sweep method as described
above. This means and eigenvalue analysis was carried out for various combinations Er,n and ηr,n
and the one that solves 5.2 was determined by least-squares method. This obviously gives only a
fairly rough estimate of the material properties. However, to use more sophisticated methods as
attempted by solving 5.2 with a MATLAB minimization function, it has been seen that a good initial
estimate of the material parameters is necessary. The parameters estimated with the parametric
sweep method could be used for this purpose in a further study.
Applying the procedure as described on measurement results from various temperatures has
caused some issues. It was not possible to obtain eigenvalue data for mode 3, as with small
variations in material properties the mode shape changed. Furthermore, the resulting modulus and
loss factor curves indeed shows physically reasonable behavior, however, the changes in loss factor
with temperature seem to small compared to literature values and the opposite is the case for the
modulus.
In general the data at temperatures different than ambient temperature must be handled with care,
because the experiment was carried out factually at ambient temperature. That means the test
plates, cooled down or heated up prior to the measurement, may have changed the temperature
when conducting the actual experiment. Even though the surface temperature of the base plate was
controlled with a laser thermometer in any experiment, changes might have happened in the rubber
interlayer which could not be predicted.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The frequency and temperature dependent dynamic Young’s modulus and loss factor of a rubber
material, used in three layered composite test plates as damping interlayer, was determined. The
description is based on the ﬁve parameter fractional derivative model for polymeric damping
materials. Vibrational response measurements and ﬁnite element analysis on the damped test plates
constituted the basis of the study at hand.
The vibrational response of the test plates with various interlayer thicknesses was measured at
various temperatures between -5 °C and 60 °C in terms of mobility. Four distinct resonance peaks
could be determined, which were used to determine the modal loss factors of each plate using
the bandwidth method. A ﬁnite element model of the test plates was build, using the concept of
complex stiffness to introduce damping. In the FE model, the rubber material is then described
by Young’s modulus Er and loss factor ηr amongst others. Conducting eigenvalue analysis on
the four modes determined experimentally, the values for these parameters were optimized so that
corresponding natural frequencies and modal loss factor reﬂect the experimental results. Eventually
the parameters ˆp in the ﬁve parameter fractional derivative model were ﬁt to the modal data of Er
and ηr obtained at the four modes. This procedure was done repeatedly for all temperatures, so that
eventually the frequency dependent Young’s modulus and loss factor could be described at various
temperatures separately.
As the method used in this study is exclusively based on modal values, the established mate-
rial description has to be handled with care. It is assumed that the established model does not
represent a generic description of the rubber material used in the test plates. In fact, the prediction
of dynamic and damping behavior is restricted to the application of the material on plates with
constrained layers in the dimensions similar to those of the test plates. The project arose from
the application of damping shims in automotive disc brake systems. These shims have similar
characteristics as the examined test plates. For this reason, the established material model can
be used for the particular purpose of modeling damping in damping shims applied on brake systems.
The results obtained for temperatures different from ambient temperature might need to be handled
with care as well. The experimental setup was not necessarily suitable to carry out measurements
57
==PAGE:76==
58
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
at various temperatures, because factually they were carried out at ambient temperature. The test
plates were cooled down and heated up, respectively, subsequently transported to the setup and
the measurement was conducted. The surface temperature of the plates was controlled by a laser
thermometer, however, there is no absolute knowledge about the temperature of the rubber material.
This obviously introduces uncertainties that affects the whole procedure of estimating the material
properties.
The estimated parameters were used to implement the ﬁve parameter fractional derivative model in
the ﬁnite element model ot the test plates. For the ambient temperature parameters, a sufﬁciently
accurate reproduction of experimental results in terms of modal loss factors and frequency response
curves was achieved. In summary it can be recorded that, under the previously stated considerations,
a good estimate of the material description was achieved at ambient temperature, and a broad
estimate of the temperature dependency was obtained.
From this point, a few aspects might set the basis for future work:
• The estimated modal values for Young’s modulus and loss factor can be used as initial
estimates for minimization functions that lead to more accurate results as it has been obtained
by the parametric sweep method in this study.
• The estimated temperature dependency needs to be supported by experiments under more
ideal conditions.
• The temperature dependency can be expressed by a shift function, which in turn can be
included in the ﬁve parameter fractional derivative model. Hence, a six parameter model,
including temperature effects, can be established to achieve a comprehensive description of
the material
• A whole different approach, based on the response curves rather than on the modal values,
can be attempted using experimental data obtained in this study.
