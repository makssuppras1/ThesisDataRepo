Abstract
Climate change is expected to significantly impact solar energy potential across Europe,
with complex seasonal and regional dynamics. This thesis investigates future changes
in solar photovoltaic (PV) performance and reliability under the high emissions SSP5­8.5
scenario, comparing the historical period (1980­2014) to projections for the end of the
century (2065­2099). Research focuses on capacity factor trends, temperature effects,
and the evolution of energy droughts.
The analysis is based on an ensemble of 7 CMIP6 climate models and is based on a
fully reproducible and open­source workflow. A thorough double bias correction of ir­
radiance and temperature components is applied, using multiplicative factors to correct
ERA5 reanalysis data with SARAH­3, and CMIP6 outputs with the corrected ERA5. The
methodology also leverages high­frequency modelling of solar capacity factor using a
detailed PV performance model that accounts for sub­daily variability, spectral decompo­
sition of irradiance (including ground­reflected irradiance), module orientation and tilt, and
temperature losses. A dynamic definition of energy droughts is introduced to account for
seasonality in the analysis. Country­level analyses provide actionable insights to support
long­term decision­making by European policymakers and energy system planners.
Results show a robust increase in annual solar capacity factor across most of Europe,
particularly in central regions. These increases are more significant in autumn and spring.
However, temperature­induced efficiency losses partially offset these gains, especially
during summer and autumn in southern and eastern Europe. Energy drought analysis
reveals that while CMIP6 models tend to overestimate the historical frequency of drought
events compared to ERA5, they capture event durations with reasonable accuracy. Future
projections indicate modest but consistent reductions in drought frequency, particularly in
summer and autumn, although the overall impact on energy system reliability remains
limited.
This study addresses several key gaps in existing literature by applying rigorous bias
correction, consistent spacial processing, high resolution PV modelling and a flexible def­
inition of energy droughts relevant to energy system planning. By doing so, it enhances
the understanding of how climate change will affect solar PV deployment across Europe
and emphasizes the importance of integrating climate­informed solar generation profiles
into energy system planning.
Ultimately, the findings underscore the importance of continued PV development beyond
2050 to ensure a resilient and sustainable European energy transition. Incorporating cli­
mate variability and uncertainty into energy modelling will be essential to support long­term
decarbonization, reliability, and energy security under climate change.

1
Introduction
Climate change is no longer a distant projection but a present reality: greenhouse­gas
emissions have warmed the climate system at rates unprecedented in the last 2000 years,
driving rising temperatures, shifting precipitation, and more frequent extremes (e.g., heat­
waves, droughts, intense storms) that already disrupt infrastructure, agriculture, water
resources, and ecosystems. Without rapid, sustained emissions cuts, warming will likely
reach 1.5 °C soon, intensifying concurrent hazards and increasing the risk of abrupt, irre­
versible shifts [1].
In this context, renewable energy, and especially wind and solar, has become central to
decarbonization. Pathways limiting warming to 1.5 °C project renewables supplying at
least 60% of electricity by 2050 [1][2], and decreasing costs (solar PV has dropped over
60% since 2015 [3]) make rapid deployment technically and economically feasible.
The European Green Deal and REPowerEU policies have supported the increase of the
share of renewables in the EU’s electricity mix, which reached 46.9% in 2024, with solar
PV contributing 22% of that share and with 338 GW installed[4]. Despite this support,
there is still a long way to go: long­term scenarios project that solar PV alone must supply
30­40% of electricity by mid­century[2].
Even under current conditions, the natural weather variability, driven by daily and seasonal
fluctuations, already poses a challenge for grid integration of renewable energy[1]. Cli­
mate change will further alter meteorological variables critical to power generation, shift­
ing solar irradiance patterns, modifying wind speeds, changing cloud cover, and affecting
temperatures[1][5]. All of this combined will alter supply[6]. On the demand side, more
extreme temperatures will intensify cooling needs in summer, and more temperature­
related weather extremes (heatwaves, cold extremes), will also provoke sudden demand
surges[6]. These changes will translate into a decrease in the supply­demand match[6].
Together, the growing share of weather­dependent renewables and climate change im­
pacts will further strain the stability and reliability of renewable­based power systems. To
close this gap, energy system planners and policymakers must explicitly account for these
evolving meteorological conditions [7].
1.1
Problem statement
Current integrated energy assessment models provide high­level guidance on decar­
bonization pathways but poorly capture climate­induced variability[8], underestimating
energy droughts and overlooking long­term changes in energy supply. This introduces
significant uncertainty in long­term energy planning [7], threatens grid stability and under­
mines efforts to design resilient, cost­effective decarbonization pathways[7]. Therefore,
there is a need to develop robust methods that quantify the impacts of climate change on
renewables, and to integrate these insights into energy system models for more reliable
and resilient planning of a renewables­based European energy system [9].
To address these challenges, this thesis develops and applies methods to quantify the im­
pacts of climate change on solar photovoltaic power generation, capturing shifts in mean
irradiance and temperature as well as changes in variability and extremes. Insights are
derived to support more reliable and resilient planning for a renewables­based European
energy system.

==PAGE:12==
The central research question is: How will climate change impact future solar energy
generation potential in Europe, and how can this impact be integrated into energy system
modelling? This main research question will be tackled by exploring the following sub­
questions:
• How will projected climate changes affect the characteristics of solar energy poten­
tial across European regions by 2065­2099?
• How will projected climate changes affect the characteristics of solar energy droughts
across European regions by 2065­2099?
• How will changes in temperature impact changes in solar energy potential across
European regions by 2065­2099?
• To what extent do climate models consistently project the direction and intensity of
climate change impacts on solar energy, and how might these changes affect the
reliability and efficiency of renewable energy systems?
1.2
Research Methodology Overview
The methodology is structured in six steps that use a combination of observational and
modelled climate data, advanced Python libraries, and high­performance computing re­
sources to quantify how climate change may alter solar energy potential in Europe.
1. Climate Data Acquisition: Collection of satellite historical solar radiation data from
SARAH­3[10]. Collection of historical (1980­2014) and future (2065­2099) modelled
climate data from CMIP6 models corresponding to RCP8.5 concentration pathway.
Data is stored within the SOPHIA HPC Cluster[11]. ERA5 reanalysis data already
available in the cluster.
2. Data Processing: CMIP6 data standardization using Xarray Python library[12]. Ap­
plication of bias correction to CMIP6 data using ERA5 reanalysis data, which has
been previously corrected with SARAH­3 satellite data. Spatial standardization us­
ing regridding methods integrated in xESMF[13].
3. Power time series generation: Conversion of meteorological high­frequency data
into 3­h power time series using Atlite[14], a Python library for the analysis of weather­
dependent renewable energy time series. Conversion is performed for historical
data from CMIP6 and bias­corrected ERA5 data, and for future data from CMIP6.
Power time series from CMIP6 is also generated removing temperature effects.
Technology is assumed to be constant.
4. Temporal and spatial aggregation: Aggregation of power time series to daily, sea­
sonal and yearly levels; and per­country aggregation.
5. Analysis of solar capacity factor changes: Analysis of solar power capacity factor
between Beggining of Century (1980­2014)) (BoC) and End of Century (2065­2099)
(EoC) at yearly and seasonal level. Analysis of the impact of temperature on the
projected changes. Application of statistical methods for improved robustness.
6. Energy­droughts analysis: Definition of a dynamic energy drought threshold and
evaluation of drought frequency and duration at yearly and seasonal levels. Appli­
cation of statistical methods for improved robustness.
By following these steps, this thesis provides a comprehensive assessment of how cli­
mate change may reshape solar energy potential across Europe and what that means for
reliable, low­carbon power systems.

Data
The following chapter sets a theoretical framework for the three different types of datasets
used in this research. Three main types of data have been used: satellite data, reanal­
ysis data and climate model data. Each dataset has irradiance and temperature data at
different temporal and spatial resolutions.
In section 2.1 the characteristics of satellite­based observations from SARAH­3[10] are
presented, as well as why this is the most trusted dataset that is used to correct the
reanalysis data. After, section 2.2 presents what is ERA5[15], and how this dataset can
be used to bias correct climate model outputs of CMIP6[16]. Finally, in section 2.3 the
concept of climate model is explained, as well as the specific CMIP6 models that have
been used and their characteristics.
2.1
Satellite­based observations dataset: SARAH­3
The third edition of the Surface Solar Radiation Data Set ­ Heliosat (SARAH­3)[10] is a
satellite­derived climate record of solar irradiance covering 01­01­1983 through 31­12­
2020 (extended to the present with a five­day latency). It provides 30­minute, daily, and
monthly means of several variables: daylight; direct normal irradiance (DNI); effective
cloud albedo (CAL); photosynthetically active radiation (PAR); sunshine duration (SDU);
surface incoming direct radiation (SID); and surface incoming shortwave radiation (SIS).
All variables are provided at 0.05° × 0.05° resolution within ±65° latitude/longitude. As a
high­quality, long­term benchmark, SARAH­3’s observations from Meteosat visible chan­
nels define the climatology closest to “truth.”
Data can be accessed freely for academic use via the EUMETSAT CM SAF online por­
tal[17] in NetCDF­4 format[18], which is self­describing, platform­independent, and opti­
mized for storing large, multidimensional datasets; its broad library support and adher­
ence to the Climate and Forecast conventions[19] make it a standard in Earth­science
research.
The process of obtaining SARAH­3 data from CM SAF was time consuming: each 35­year
variable required four separate requests due to size limitations, which arrived by email in
multiple .tar archives of daily 30 min, 0.25º×0.25º NetCDF files. Once all the daily files
were available for the historical period for each variable in the Cluster, then the idea was
to process the data using the cutout function of atlite[14], merging all variables in one
file per year in each period. In atlite, a cutout is a locally stored, spatially and temporally
limited subset of a weather dataset that contains only selected variables, enabling fast
and efficient reuse.However, due to cluster saturation, the process of creating the yearly
.nc files using Atlite was very slow.
To solve this issue, a more practical approach was decided. Hörsch et al.[20] provide
cutouts of European weather data from ERA5 for some variables and SARAH­3 for all
radiation related variables. Data is available for four years inside the historical period:
1996, 2010, 2012 and 2013. The resolution of the data is hourly, 0.3ºx0.3º, slightly more
coarse than original temporal and spatial resolution. In conclusion, four years of 0.3ºx0.3º
hourly SARAH­3 data are used to bias­correct ERA5 reanalysis data instead of the whole
35 year period. The implications from this approach are discussed in the Limitations
section (chapter 6).

2.1.1
Key variables
The key variables used in this study from SARAH­3 are Surface Incoming Direct Radi­
ation (SID) and Surface Incoming Short­wave Radiation (SIS). SID is the radiation flux
(irradiance) reaching a horizontal plane in the 0.2 µm to 4 µm wavelength region at the
surface directly without scattering. SIS is defined as the radiation flux (irradiance) reach­
ing a horizontal plane at the Earth surface in the 0.2 µm to 4 µm wavelength region, in
[W m−2]. SIS combines both direct sunlight and diffuse skylight. In SARAH­3, SIS is
obtained by first estimating an effective cloud albedo (CAL) from visible­channel satellite
images, which capture how much sunlight clouds reflect or scatter. This CAL is converted
into a “clear­sky index” k that tells what fraction of clear­sky radiation actually reaches the
ground given current cloud cover. Independently, a radiative­transfer look­up table, built
with a physics­based model using inputs like aerosol amount, water vapour, ozone, and
surface albedo, provides the clear­sky SIS (what the radiation would be if there were no
clouds). The final all­sky SIS is the clear­sky value multiplied by k. SIS errors are gener­
ally small but can grow when clouds are very thin or aerosols change rapidly.
On the other hand, SARAH­3 retrieves SID using the same CAL and clear­sky lookup to
get clear­sky direct flux, then applies an empirical rule (a fixed formula of k) to extract the
portion that reaches the surface through clouds. If clouds are very thick, SDI drops to zero;
if they are thin, a fraction of the clear­sky direct beam still reaches the surface. SDI tends
to be more sensitive than SIS to small errors in cloudiness, since a slight overestimate of
cloud cover can artificially zero out direct sunlight.
2.1.2
Strengths and limitations
SARAH­3’s strengths include nearly four decades of consistent retrievals, fine 5 km spatial
resolution (capturing microclimate features), frequent temporal sampling, and extensive
validation against high­quality ground observations, with biases below 5 W m−2. Limi­
tations arise from dependence on ancillary aerosol and water­vapour inputs (which can
introduce bias in polluted or rapidly changing regions), potential misclassification of snow
versus cloud at high latitudes or altitudes, and reduced accuracy near the poles due to
geostationary coverage gaps, as shown in Figure 2.1. SARAH­3 is also the dataset with
the more restrictive geographical coverage, as shown in Figure 2.1. Therefore, the geo­
graphical coverage of the current research ranges from longitude −12° to 35° and latitude
from 33° to 64°, with a small triangular area without results in the upper­right corner, ap­
proximately above 25° longitude and 59° latitude, excluding parts of Finland and Russia.

2.2
Reanalysis dataset: ERA5
ERA5[15] is the fifth­generation reanalysis dataset from European Centre for Medium­
Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF), offering hourly estimates of atmospheric, land­
surface, and sea­state variables (together with uncertainty estimates) on a 0.25º×0.25º
latitude­longitude grid and 37 pressure levels for atmospheric data. It stretches back to
1940 and is updated daily, with new data released five days after real time.
Reanalysis combines model data with observations into a globally complete and consis­
tent dataset using the laws of physics. This principle, called data assimilation, is based
on the method used by numerical weather prediction centres, where every so many hours
(12 hours at ECMWF) a previous forecast is combined with newly available observations
in an optimal way to produce a new best estimate of the state of the atmosphere, called
analysis, from which an updated, improved forecast is issued[21].
Reanalysis works in the same way, but at reduced resolution and without the urgency of
real­time forecasting. This approach allows incorporation of historical observations and
improved versions of original observations. ERA5 provides consistent data that repre­
sents the main Earth system cycles[15].
At the start of this work, ERA5 data were already available as yearly NetCDF4 files (hourly,
0.25° x 0.25° resolution) covering the entire European region for 1980–2014, with all rel­
evant variables prepared and stored on the SOPHIA HPC Cluster[11].
2.2.1
Key Variables
The main variables used from ERA5 are Surface direct solar radiation at surface (fdir),
Surface solar radiation downwards (ssrd), Surface net solar radiation (ssr) and 2 metre
temperature (tas).
Surface direct short­wave (solar) radiation (fdir) [J m−2] is the amount of direct solar ra­
diation (also known as short­wave radiation) reaching the surface of the Earth. It is the
amount of radiation passing through a horizontal plane, not a plane perpendicular to the
direction of the Sun.
Surface short­wave (solar) radiation downwards (ssrd) [J m−2] is the amount of solar radi­
ation (also known as short­wave radiation) that reaches a horizontal plane at the surface
of the Earth. This parameter comprises both direct and diffuse solar radiation.
Surface net solar radiation (ssr) [J m−2] is the amount of solar radiation (also known as
short­wave radiation) that reaches a horizontal plane at the surface of the Earth (both
direct and diffuse) minus the amount reflected by the Earth’s surface (which is governed
by the albedo). All irradiance parameters are accumulated over 1 hour, and are converted
to watts by dividing the accumulated values by the accumulation period in seconds.
2 metre temperature [K] is the air temperature measured at 3m above the surface of land,
sea or in­land waters.
2.2.2
Strengths and limitations
The advantages of ERA5 are the wide temporal and geographical coverage of the dataset
(providing measurements also for oceans and remote regions), the physical consistency
of variables that obey the governing equations of the forecast model, together with uncer­
tainty estimates. And finally, the continuous improvements, periodic re­forecasting and
back­revisions of data.
ERA5 inherits biases and parametrization errors from its underlying numerical weather
prediction model, as well as uncertainties arising from the data­assimilation cycle. Al­

though ERA5 provides ensemble­based spread metrics, there is no standardized proce­
dure for quantifying or reporting uncertainties at each grid point and for each variable,
making it difficult to assess the true accuracy of reanalysis outputs[22]. In contrast, ob­
servational datasets can theoretically derive error estimates directly from instrument cal­
ibration and measurement noise. However, purely observational records can also suffer
from poorly quantified errors.
These model and data­assimilation biases vary especially with cloud cover, because
clouds are one of the most challenging components to represent in numerical weather pre­
diction models due to the fact that their characteristics (formation, dissipation, structure)
depend on multiple interacting processes that happen at scales smaller than model grid.
These biases lead to larger errors than the ones from SARAH­3 in many regions. There­
fore, satellite­based data should be used when available to correct ERA5 variables[23].
2.3
Model Projection dataset: CMIP6 Climate Models
Coupled Model Intercomparison Project Phase 6 (CMIP6)[24] is a coordinated interna­
tional effort designed to better understand past, present and future changes in the cli­
mate. The main goals of the project are to provide a common framework for testing
the response of different climate models to the same scenarios, allowing assessment
of model performance, uncertainties and differences; and to support scientific bodies like
the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) in their reports by providing stan­
dardized experiments for historical periods and future pathways. The project has broad
participation from modelling centres across the world and wide data dissemination and
accessibility.
2.3.1
Climate Models
A climate model is a complex computer code that creates a digital analogue to Earth. This
model digitises the processes and interactions between parts of Earth’s climate system:
the atmosphere, ocean, land surface, cryosphere and biosphere. Models are used to
experiment how future changes in human activities will impact the Earth’s future climate,
how much it warms, how floods, droughts and other extremes will change[24]. The main
components and interactions in a Earth system model are shown in Figure 2.2.

The most sophisticated models are General Circulation Model (GCM)s, which represent
the atmosphere and oceans using a 3D grid and solve the equations for fluid motion to
calculate energy transfer between grid points. Individual subsystems (e.g., atmosphere,
ocean, sea ice, land surface) can run independently or be coupled via a “coupler” that
handles fluxes of energy, water, and momentum. Additional components, such as at­
mospheric chemistry (e.g., dust or pollution), ocean biogeochemistry, vegetation, and
carbon­cycle modules, can be integrated to form a complete Earth system model[25].
The CMIP was launched by the World Climate Research Programme (WCRP) in 1995,
and in the first phase (CMIP1) the models were simpler, with Earth­models simulating and
combining atmosphere, ocean dynamics, sea­ice interaction, and a simple land­surface
scheme. A control scenario was used assuming a 1 % yr−1 CO2 increase to document
each model’s systematic errors by comparing mean outputs to observations[26].
Ini­
tially, reduced computational capacity made it necessary to simplify some details of Earth
system’s interactions, simulating each system independently and then inter­linking them
through energy, momentum and mass fluxes. When combining model systems, errors
were introduced in the fluxes. Early GCMs required “flux adjustments” to prevent long­
term climate drift[27].
Over five phases, CMIP has increased the complexity of the climate models, to the point
where CMIP6 no longer requires flux adjustment. Some critical sub­models have been
added in order to be able to understand the response of climate systems to external
drivers such as increasing greenhouse gas emissions, which is one of the main objec­
tives of the current project[28]. CMIP6 models are classified into two categories depend­
ing on how they treat natural CO2 concentrations. In Earth system models (ESMs), the
exchange of CO2 among the atmosphere, oceans, and living organisms is simulated dy­
namically, meaning the model can adjust CO2 concentrations as the climate changes.
In contrast, traditional climate models (also called atmosphere­ocean general circulation
models, AOGCMs or CMs) hold CO2 levels fixed according to a predefined schedule and
do not let them respond to feedbacks from the ocean or biosphere. In both ESMs and
CMs, human emissions of CO2 and other greenhouse gases are specified in advance
rather than generated by the model[29][30].
In CMIP6, historical simulations cover the period 1850­2014, covering emissions of both
short­lived and long­lived greenhouse gases, greenhouse­gas concentrations, gridded
land­use changes, solar irradiance variations, and stratospheric aerosols(e.g., volcano
emissions). Models without ozone chemistry also receive time­varying, gridded ozone
concentrations and nitrogen deposition.
Future simulations cover 2015­2100 and use alternative emissions and land­use path­
ways described in the IPCC Sixth Assessment Report[31]. These pathways are gener­
ated by integrated assessment models[32] and rest on shared socioeconomic storylines
(SSPs)[33] . Each SSP combines a narrative about future societal development with
RCP­style radiative­forcing trajectories (for example, SSP2­4.5 corresponds to roughly
4.5 W m−2 by 2100, while SSP5­8.5 reaches about 8.5 W m−2). The resulting scenarios
span from aggressive mitigation efforts to continued emission growth.

2.3.3
Strengths and limitations
CMIP6 brings together a larger and more diverse set of models than previous phases,
incorporating finer spatial and temporal resolutions that better capture regional features
and extreme events. By integrating interactive carbon, nitrogen, and cryosphere com­
ponents, CMIP6 models simulate key feedbacks, such as CO2 exchange among atmo­
sphere, ocean, and land, rather than prescribing greenhouse­gas concentrations, which
yields more realistic projections of carbon­climate interactions. Standardized experiments
and protocols ensure consistency across simulations, while the adoption of Shared So­
cioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) improve policy relevance. New output variables enable
in­depth evaluation of specific processes. Finally, most CMIP6 models no longer require
artificial flux adjustments to maintain stable pre­industrial climates, indicating significant
improvements in coupled model balance and fidelity [28].
But despite its advances, CMIP6 still has some limitations. Climate models show sys­
tematic biases when compared to real observations, meaning that there are consistent
differences between model predictions and observations that are not due to random vari­
ations or sampling errors but to the simplifications of the models. On top of that, models
still differ in their representation of critical processes, which leads to substantial inter­
model spread in key metrics like climate sensitivity and regional precipitation patterns. In
order to minimize the impact of systematic biases, it is common practice to bias­correct
CMIP6 with ERA5 reanalysis data, ERA5 data corrected with satellite data or directly with
satellite data. These three options are closer to observational ”truth” than the outputs
from the models, and that is why they can be used to ”anchor” models to a more realistic
baseline.
Another source of bias is horizontal resolution. Most models still operate at scales too
coarse to resolve localized phenomena (ranging from around 100 km to 500 km), forcing
dependence on parametrizations that introduce errors and leading to incomplete under­
standing of physical processes. However, multiple research exists on how to reduce these
biases, as presented in section 4.3. A strong assumption is made in this aspect: bias is
considered constant across time. If a model consistently overestimates or underestimates
a certain climate variable in the past, it is assumed that the same overestimation or under­
estimation will occur in future projections.This assumption simplifies the bias correction,
but it is a limitation, given that biases can change over time due to changes in climate
dynamics and other influences[30][34].
Other limitations are the lack of representation or coupling of some complex process (like
dynamic ice­sheet models or biogeochemical cycles), as well as the uncertainties from the
forcing datasets, that can propagate and affect regional outcomes. There is also scenario
uncertainty: SSPs offer improved socioeconomic narratives, but they cannot encompass
all plausible futures, and the translation of SSP storylines into precise forcing time series
can vary by modelling centre.
Consequently, while CMIP6 represents a considerable step forward, these uncertainties
and limitations should be considered when interpreting the projected results

3
Literature Review
The goal of this literature review is to critically evaluate the methods and findings of studies
on European­focused climate impact on solar PV generation. The current review begins
with the identification of main information sources. Afterwards, main techniques used
in previous studies are reviewed. The chapter finalizes with the identification of gaps in
current research, as well as the contributions that this thesis will make.
3.1
Literature Sources
In this thesis, the impact of climate change on solar power generation and on solar energy
droughts is investigated using SARAH­3, ERA5, and CMIP6 model data. For this reason,
two type of climate­change studies are mainly considered: those examining PV power
change (both historical analyses and future projections) and those focused on energy
droughts. In the first category, the reviewed works mainly assess global PV potential
[40][41], followed by regional analyses in Europe [26][42], and a few country­level cases
[43][44]. Among the drought studies, the majority address regional scales [45][46], while
a small percentage examine either local impacts [47][48] or global effects [6].
Most papers use a combination of climate model output, satellite estimates, reanalysis
data and ground observations. For projections of future solar performance, most studies
rely on ensembles from CMIP models: either CMIP5[26][42] or, for more recent stud­
ies, CMIP6[5][41]. A smaller group uses CORDEX [9][49].CMIP provides global climate
projections from GCMs at coarse (100–200 km) resolution, while CORDEX downscales
those outputs with regional climate models (RCMs) to finer (10–50 km) detail over spe­
cific areas. However, recent studies show that most EURO­CORDEX models are poorly
suited to investigate surface solar radiation because aerosols were kept constant in most
of the RCMs[5]. Therefore, this study is based in the CMIP6 datasets.
Across the studies examined, roughly 60% draw on two or more data sources, while
the remainder rely on a single dataset, most often reanalysis or climate model output
in energy­drought work, and CMIP alone in some PV­impact analyses [40][50]. Among
the multi­source papers, the most common approach is to blend reanalysis, satellite, and
ground observations[44][51]; others pair CMIP projections with ground or satellite mea­
surements[43][52], others integrate CMIP (or CORDEX) with reanalysis fields [5][9], and
others use a combination of satellite, reanalysis and CMIP models[41][53].
High­quality ground observations can be unevenly distributed in space and time, while
ERA5 and SARAH­3 provide continuous and gap­free meteorological data. Therefore,
for this thesis it is best to use a combination of satellite, reanalysis and CMIP6[23].
Across all papers, core variables are solar irradiance components (total, direct and diffuse
irradiance), and temperature[42]. Wind­specific data is also included in studies related to
compound energy droughts[54] and in studies examining the impact of wind speed on PV
performance[42].
Temporal coverage is consistent across studies: historical baselines of 20–40 years[55]
(commonly 1980–2019 or 1983–2022) at hourly or daily resolution. Future projections
are segmented into contiguous 20­40­year windows (e.g. 2020–2040, 2041–2060, 2081–
2100). It is common practice to define past and future periods of equal lengths to facilitate
comparison[5][26].

3.2
Data processing
In the following section, the main methods used in climate change impact research are re­
viewed. First, different data preprocessing techniques are examined, including regridding
and bias correction. Next, the methods by which meteorological inputs (like irradiance
and temperature) are translated into capacity factors are examined, with particular em­
phasis on temperature effects. The review then explores temporal aggregation strategies,
explaining why and how hourly time series are summarized at daily, seasonal and annual
scales. The use of per­country aggregates is also considered.
3.2.1
Regridding
Regridding is the process of transferring data from one grid to another while preserving key
data characteristics [13]. It is necessary when comparing datasets with different spatial
resolutions or aligning them to a common analysis grid[56]. Frameworks like xESMF
automate this process and are widely used in CMIP analyses [5][26][42]. However, the
choice of regridding method is not straightforward, as it depends on the purpose of the
analysis and the structure of the underlying variables.
Common interpolation methods include bilinear, conservative, and patch [57]. Each method
comes with trade­offs in terms of smoothness, conservation, computational cost, and ap­
plicability to continuous or discontinuous variables.
According to xESMF [58], when regridding from a coarse to a finer grid (upscaling), the
conservative method is preferred for preserving total quantities. For downscaling from
high to low resolution, bilinear and patch yield similar results, but bilinear is faster and
better suited to continuous variables like temperature and irradiance. For discontinuous
variables such as precipitation, both methods degrade accuracy [30][59].
Although comparisons exist for specific variables and contexts [60][61], comprehensive
inter­comparisons across diverse variables and grid changes remain scarce.
3.2.2
Bias correction
In climate impact studies, systematic model errors (biases) are commonly addressed
through statistical post­ processing[62][63][64]. This is an essential step to avoid the prop­
agation of errors to downstream analyses. Two of the most widely used approaches are
linear bias correction[65] and Inter­quantile mapping (IQM)[66].
Linear bias correction adjusts only the mean of a modelled variable using additive or mul­
tiplicative terms, assuming uniform biases across the distribution and leaving variability
and extremes unchanged [62]. In contrast, quantile­based methods like IQM correct the
full distribution by mapping modelled percentiles to observed ones, addressing mean,
variability, and extremes. Both approaches rely on assumptions such as bias stationarity,
spatial and temporal independence, and sufficient observational data [63][67][68].
Despite its simplicity, linear bias correction often performs well when impact metrics are
driven mostly by mean changes rather than extremes. For example, Zhao et al.[69] found
that linear scaling produced monthly climatologies nearly indistinguishable from those ob­
tained via quantile­based methods. Similarly, Shrestha et al.[65] showed that for hydro­
logical modelling at coarser temporal resolutions, linear scaling matches quantile mapping
performance.
In PV modelling, bias correction improves irradiance inputs. Site­specific linear scaling
can reduce monthly mean biases by 10­15% while improving extreme­value statistics[70].
Benchmarking across Europe demonstrated that both linear scaling and quantile mapping
yield significant bias reductions, with a one­year training period with ground observations

is generally sufficient to calibrate data and preserve physical consistency among GHI,
DNI, and DHI[47]. Likewise, Urraca et al.[23] compared ERA5 against ground and satellite
data, showing uncorrected reanalyses misestimate seasonal cycles by 5–20% and that
bias correction narrows confidence intervals on PV output projections.
Beyond single­model corrections, multi­model ensembles further mitigate bias and en­

Using the full ensemble reduces random spread and yields more stable estimates of mean

usually between 5 and 10 GCMs are used.
3.2.3
Solar capacity factor modelling
When modelling solar power, two choices need to be made: the model used and how to
express the results. In terms of how to express results, most studies use specific yield,
in kW h kW−1
p , which allows to normalize energy production across different system sizes
and facilitate comparison[50][51]. By contrast, other studies with a focus on system­level
impacts or economic modelling provide absolute energy figures in kW h. This requires
making capacity assumptions and introduces additional uncertainties tied to deployment
scenarios[42][55]. For this thesis, specific yield is adopted to provide a clear, scalable
metric of PV performance without additional assumptions, supporting robust comparison
across regions and models.
Regarding PV capacity factor modelling, a wide spectrum of approaches is used in cli­
mate impact studies, from simple, “black­box” approaches to fully detailed, physics­based
workflows, many of which are available as open­source tools. At the simplest end, sev­
eral studies use a DC × PR framework, where plane­of­array irradiance is multiplied by
fixed efficiency and constant performance ratio (PR) to approximate AC output[42][26].
Variants may apply a fixed temperature de­rating [55]. These methods are computation­
ally lightweight and easy to implement but cannot resolve sub­daily ramps or spectral and
angle­of­incidence effects.
On the other end of the complexity scale, physics­based models explicitly simulate cell
temperature, spectral response, and incidence­angle losses at high temporal resolution.
Some examples are the Huld model [73] or the Sandia Array Performance Model (SAPM)
[74], accessible via PVLib­Python [75][76]. These open­source libraries enable detailed
simulations that capture transient cloud events and non­linear temperature de­rating at
high temporal resolution, but at the cost of extensive parameter requirements and com­
putational overhead that make them non­suitable for large datasets.
By contrast, Atlite [14] combines flexibility with ease of use. It takes gridded weather
dataset and applies a Reindl et al. clear­sky transposition [77] before choosing a mod­
ule model for temperature correction (often Huld[73] or Beyer [78]), then converts DC to
AC via inverter efficiency curves. As an open­source package, Atlite has been widely
adopted in energy­system modelling [52], offering a turnkey workflow for high­resolution
capacity factors while still capturing sub­daily, spectral, and thermal effects and allowing
customization, while remaining computationally efficient. that makes Atlite the preferred
method for this thesis.
3.2.4
Temporal resolution and aggregation
It is common in research to derive daily, seasonal and annual aggregated capacity fac­
tors[5][40][42][52]. Aggregating high­frequency raw data (measured at hourly level), al­
lows to smooth out noise from transient weather changes while keeping the underlying

aggregated results are more stable and less sensitive to outliers[80]. Daily aggregates
allow to evaluate energy droughts and filter out hourly fluctuations[46][48]. Annual aggre­

trends and inter­model spread. This facilitates the detection of statistically significant shifts

Aggregating data from sub­daily to daily, seasonal, and annual scales provides a balance
between capturing meteorological variability and providing robust, interpretable metrics
of mean change, variability, and extremes under climate change.
3.2.5
Per­Country and Per­Region Aggregation
Spatial aggregation of capacity factor time series is often performed at the level of political
or geographic areas (per country, per region). Several recent studies illustrate this ap­
proach [42][52][81]. Regardless of the aggregation principle, the process also contributes
to smooth out local noise and allows to obtain more robust conclusions[80].
National­level aggregation allows to present results that can be applied to country­specific
energy policies and targets, and facilitates the communication to the broad public. A
clear case is the European Commission’s reporting strategy for renewable generation
statistics, capacity addition and emission targets, with key figures being aggregated per
country[82]. Country­level aggregates are also used by grid operators and transmission
system planners coordinating across countries and bidding zones, and power statistics
are often also presented by country[83].
Regional level aggregation can be based on different criteria, like market zones or climate
characteristics. In the case of solar energy, regional aggregation can capture shared me­
teorological characteristics that might not be visible through country aggregates[42]. This
method is also useful for high­level, cross­border planning[84]. Finally, climate impacts of­
ten manifest over large spatial scales[40], and aggregating per region can highlight these
changes at continental scale.
3.3
Climate­change Impact findings
In this section, historical analyses and future projections are synthesized to show ex­
pected changes in solar generation mean output and variability. Research on the impact
of climate change on energy drought is also included.
3.3.1
Impact on solar generation
Climate change is expected to influence PV performance mainly by changes in incoming
solar radiation, ambient temperature, and atmospheric composition (for example, aerosol
loading)[42][5]. Jerez et al.[42] used bias­corrected CMIP5 outputs under RCP4.5 and
RCP8.5 to project that Southern Europe could see a modest decline (up to –3% by 2050)
in annual PV yield due to decreased summer insolation and higher module temperatures,
whereas Northern Europe might gain up to +5% from longer sunshine seasons.
Building on CMIP6, Hou et al.[5] found that by 2100 Western Iberia and the Mediterranean
coasts could lose as much as –7% of mean PV potential, while Scandinavia and the British
Isles could gain up to +4%, mainly driven by shifts in cloud cover and atmospheric mois­
ture. Their ensemble of >20 GCM–downscaling combinations highlights the importance
of multi­model spread when assessing regional impacts.

At a global level, Feron et al.[40] concludes that by mid­century under RCP4.5, the multi­
model mean PV power potential changes range from ­4% in the Arabian Peninsula to +5%
in central Europe, with most of the world experiencing changes of ±3%.
Long­term observational analyses[55] (30­year, hourly­validated PV output datasets) re­
veal that inter­annual variability of PV generation in Central Europe is already increasing,
with extreme­low­output years becoming more frequent. These trends correlate strongly
with changes in winter cloudiness and summer heatwaves, which not only reduce irradi­
ance but also degrade module efficiency at elevated temperatures[85].
Finally, most of the literature focuses on the overall impact of climate change without look­
ing into how each meteorological variable drives these changes. Jerez et a.[42] considers
the impact of temperature and finds temperature induced changes in PV potential around
­4%. Liu et al. [53] concludes that in the historical period, rising air temperatures have
already mitigated the increase of PV potential especially over northern Africa and the
Qinghai–Tibet Plateau, and that these areas will also be especially sensitive in the future.
Müller et al. [26] concludes that the increase in temperature reduces PV electricity output
with a magnitude of 1–2% across Europe for the mid­century period under RCP8.5.
Overall, these studies suggest that solar power in Europe could increase slightly in some
areas and decrease in others, depending on the climate scenario, but the change would be
dampened by increases in temperature. The use of accurate bias correction techniques,
proper downscaling methods and a wide model ensemble are crucial for the determination
of the magnitude, direction and robustness of the results.
3.3.2
Impact on energy droughts
Energy droughts are prolonged intervals during which generation falls below a low­output
threshold for a certain duration[46]. Energy droughts are characterized by two metrics:
the duration (the consecutive hours or days below that threshold) and the frequency (the
number of such events per period)[86].
Drought thresholds differ across studies. Some use fixed percentiles (e.g. the 10th per­
centile over ≥3 days[87]), while others apply capacity­factor cut­off’s (which makes sense
for wind)[54]. Another option are climatology­based methods, that scale thresholds to
the mean daily output[48], for example considering 0.2 and 0.5 times the mean daily pro­
duction for moderate and severe droughts[46]. These last methods account for seasonal
variability and identify only unexpected low outputs. Thresholds also vary by technology:
the IEA, for instance, defines a wind drought as ≥4 hours below 10% capacity factor, but
a solar drought as ≥1 day below 30%[88], reflecting wind’s high­frequency variability and
24­h availability versus a more predictable solar diurnal cycle.
Historical single­source droughts have been documented across Europe. Raynaud et
al.[46] found that solar or wind deficits below their 10th percentile for ≥3 days occur reg­
ularly. The paper also shows that compound droughts (wind and solar) can represent up
to 15% of the annual generation shortfall in Northern Europe. In Poland, Jurasz et al. [89]
showed that pairing wind and solar reduces single­source drought frequency by about
40%, yet overlapping deficits still contribute over 10% of total deficit hours, underscor­
ing the limits of resource complementarity under synchronized low­resource conditions.
On a global scale, Lei et al. [90] quantified solar droughts driven by supply–demand
imbalances, finding a 25% expansion since 1980 of areas experiencing at least seven
consecutive days below the 10th percentile of PV potential. Feron et al.[40] concludes
that summer days with PV outputs below 10% are projected to more than double in the
Arabian Peninsula but to decrease by roughly 50% in southern Europe by 2036–2065,

even under a moderate­emission scenario[40].
Looking ahead, Kapica et al. [86] used bias­corrected CMIP6 projections under RCP8.5
to demonstrate that by mid­century, extreme low output events (below the 5th percentile)
could last 20–30% longer in Southern and Central Europe, with smaller increases in
Southern regions. Li et al. [45] framed these challenges within a renewable energy “qual­
ity trilemma,” demonstrating that climate­driven shifts in variability may synchronize wind
and solar deficits, undermining their natural complementarity.
These studies suggest that climate change could increase the duration, the severity, and
even change the frequency of single­source and compound energy droughts, depending
on region and scenario. However, the exact magnitude of these changes remains subject
to model uncertainty, downscaling choices, and regional climate variability. Research on
energy droughts is still relatively novel, especially with a focus on future changes and
seasonality, and there is a lack of a unified definition of energy drought, which difficult
direct comparison across studies.
3.4
Research gap and contributions
Despite advances in assessing how climate change influences solar power and energy
droughts, several gaps remain. In many studies, regridding techniques are applied with­
out distinguishing between upscaling and downscaling, often relying on a single method
[5][42]. Similarly, bias correction lacks a standardized framework: the choice of input
datasets and the correction method vary widely, and ensemble sizes differ without clear
choice criteria. On the modelling side, capacity factor analyses usually rely on relatively
simple PV­performance approaches and daily­averaged aggregates. In the results part,
several papers report regional PV trends, but country­level results presented as annual
and seasonal aggregates are uncommon. In addition, only a few studies explicitly quan­
tify temperature’s separate impact to end­of­century PV outcomes across Europe using
detailed simulations. Finally, in relation to energy droughts, most research uses fixed
thresholds to define low­output events from solar power. There are not many studies fo­
cused on energy droughts at the end of the century, and there is a gap on studies that
combine drought frequency and duration metrics while using statistical tests to assess the
robustness of observed shifts.
By addressing these limitations, this research provides a robust framework for modelling
PV capacity and energy droughts under climate change. First, it implements a robust
bias­correction workflow that uses SARAH­3 satellite data to adjust ERA5 reanalysis be­
fore applying corrections to an ensemble of seven CMIP6 models, ensuring observational
accuracy and a realistic spread of future scenarios. Second, it models PV output using a
complex, high­frequency approach that captures sub­daily patterns, and provides annual
and seasonal aggregates and insight on the temperature impact. Third, it introduces a
dynamic definition of energy droughts, enabling more meaningful metrics of low­output
frequency and duration for grid planners and policymakers. Finally, results are aggre­
gated at the country level, aligning with European policy and offering actionable insights
to guide long­term solar deployment and system resilience.
Together, these advances ensure that resulting PV and drought projections are both co­
herent and statistically rigorous, delivering actionable insights for European energy plan­
ning.

Methodology
The methodology adopted in this thesis includes a sequence of data processing and ana­
lytical steps designed to evaluate the impact of climate change on solar energy between
Beginning of Century (BoC, 1980­2014 ) and End of Century (EoC, 2065­2099).
It begins with the acquisition and processing of data from 7 CMIP6 climate models, with
the goal of standardizing the different variables and facilitating the data analysis later.
Then, a two­step linear bias correction method is applied to remove systematic errors.
First, biases in ERA5 reanalysis data are corrected using high­quality satellite­based ob­
servations (SARAH­3). Subsequently, the corrected ERA5 is used as a reference to bias­
correct the CMIP6 model outputs. This bias correction process requires the use of regrid­
ding methods to standardize spatial resolution across datasets. The method is also used
later in the process to standardize power outputs.
Afterwards, meteorological variables are converted into PV capacity factor (CF) time se­
ries using a physics­based model. The relevant variables include direct and diffuse ir­
radiance, ambient temperature, and surface albedo. Direct irradiance depends on solar
position and hits the panel at a specific angle, while diffuse irradiance contributes from
all directions and is scattered by the atmosphere. Ground­reflected irradiance, or albedo
irradiance, results from the reflection of both direct and diffuse irradiance from the ground.
Their combined projection onto the panel surface determines the effective input to the PV
system, especially under varying sky conditions. Both are needed to accurately compute
the plane­of­array irradiance on tilted PV panels.
Afterwards, meteorological variables are converted into PV capacity factor (CF) time se­
ries using a physics­based model. The relevant variables are direct, diffuse irradiance and
temperature. Direct irradiance depends on solar position and hits the panel at a defined
angle, while diffuse irradiance contributes from all directions. Their combined projection
onto the panel surface determines the effective input to the PV system, especially un­
der varying sky conditions. Both are needed to accurately compute the plane­of­array
irradiance on tilted PV panels.
The process of obtaining PV CFs includes calculating solar position, adjusting for panel
tilt and orientation, and applying the Huld model for PV performance, incorporating also
inverter efficiency. The resulting CF time series from each model are aggregated to dif­
ferent time resolutions and standardized. The main Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)
are yearly and seasonal solar CFs, both with and without temperature impact.
A key step in this study is the identification and analysis of solar energy droughts, which
are characterized by duration and frequency. In this study, a dynamic threshold is used.
For each model, time period and week, the threshold is computed as 50% of the median
daily solar power for that week, using data from all years in the period. Key drought
metrics are yearly and seasonal drought days and P75, P90 drought durations.
Once solar power aggregates and energy drought metrics are available, a comparative
analysis of climate impacts at the BoC and EoC is performed by observing relative differ­
ences in each variable at different temporal aggregations (yearly, seasonal) and different
spatial resolution (grid level, country level). Finally, significance of observed changes is
evaluated using statistical tests for yearly and seasonal results on solar generation and
energy droughts.

4.1
Raw data processing of CMIP6 Climate Models
The data from the CMIP6 models is downloaded from the ESGF Metagrid website [91],
where it can be filtered by model, experiment, time resolution, and variable. Each variable
is stored in separate NetCDF files, and models divide their time axis unevenly: for exam­
ple, ACCESS­CM2 provides ten­year files, whereas CMCC­ESM2 uses five­year chunks.
This combination of variable­by­variable retrieval and model­specific time divisions com­
plicates efficient data processing. As part of this thesis, a standardized workflow was
implemented to manage these differences: all downloaded files are uploaded to the HPC
cluster and processed uniformly using automated routines.
Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Solar Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System 17
==PAGE:28==
The variables downloaded for each model are direct horizontal irradiance, diffuse hori­
zontal irradiance, and 2­meter air temperature, as these are essential for modelling solar
power generation. Direct and diffuse irradiance are required to compute the plane­of­array
irradiance on tilted PV panels, which determines the actual energy input to the system un­
der different sky conditions. Temperature is needed to account for its effect on PV module
efficiency, as higher temperatures typically reduce performance.
The first step of the processing is the application of a geographic filter to extract the Euro­
pean region. Any remaining grid misalignments are resolved during data standardization.
Temperature is then interpolated to match the irradiance time axis. Despite all variables
having a 3­hour resolution, irradiance represents the mean of hourly accumulated irradi­
ance and is timestamped at the centre of the interval (for example, 16:30h for data from
15:00h to 18:00h), whereas temperature is instantaneous and timestamped at the start
and end (that is, at 15:00h and 18:00h). Linear interpolation is used to estimate tempera­
ture at the middle of the interval (that is, 16:30h), aligning the time axes across variables.
After these changes have been applied, a single 3D xarray DataArray (time x lat x lon) is
created for each variable, and then each xarray is merged into one dataset such that all
variables have common time and coordinates. Variable data and attributes are defined.
The dataset is recorded as a netCDF file.
Finally, a loop iterates over all CMIP6 models and both periods (BoC and EoC). For each
combination, the code reads the filenames to identify exactly which netCDF files cover
the same period, concatenates them in chronological order, subsets the target years, and
writes out a single yearly netCDF file containing the three aligned variables.
4.2
Regridding
In this study, regridding is applied at multiple stages to ensure spatial consistency across
datasets with different native resolutions. All datasets use rectilinear, georeferenced grids,
allowing for consistent spatial referencing and straightforward regridding across sources
[13][56].
Regridding involves interpolating data from one spatial grid to another, modifying both
the grid and the associated data values while preserving key characteristics such as spa­
tial patterns or total quantities. This is typically done in two steps: first, computing an
interpolation­weight matrix that quantifies the contribution of each source grid cell to each
destination cell; second, applying this matrix to remap the data [13].
As reviewed in the literature, bilinear interpolation and first­order conservative remapping
are widely used, each with specific advantages [58][60]. Bilinear interpolation computes
values as weighted averages of the four nearest points, producing smooth results for con­
tinuous variables with low computational cost [58][92]. Conservative remapping ensures
global and regional totals are preserved by calculating fractional overlaps between cells
but is more computationally intensive.
Regridding is applied in three key steps of the workflow. First, ERA5 data (0.25ºx0.25º) is
regridded to the coarser SARAH­3 grid (0.3ºx0.3º) using conservative remapping to en­
able consistent bias correction. The process of decreasing resolution is downscaling. The
choice of SARAH­3’s 0.3º resolution rather than its finer native grid of 0.25º is motivated
by practical considerations discussed in the Data section (chapter 2).
Second, the bias­corrected ERA5 data is regridded to the native grids of the CMIP6 mod­
els, all of which have coarser resolutions than ERA5 (downscaling). Here, conservative
18 Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Solar Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System
==PAGE:29==
remapping is used to preserve consistency with the corrected ERA5 fields.
Third, to enable inter­model comparison, the CMIP6 model outputs are standardized to
a common 1ºx1º grid, as recommended by the CMIP6 Guidance for Data Users [93].
The interpolation method depends on whether the model resolution is finer or coarser
than the target grid. When reducing resolution (downscaling), first­order conservative
remapping is consistently applied to preserve total energy quantities. For models with
coarser grids (upscaling), a comparison of interpolation methods was conducted using
annual irradiance fields at 100 km, 250 km, and 500 km resolutions. Bilinear interpolation
produced smoother spatial patterns in these cases and is therefore selected for upscaling.
Models such as ACCESS­CM2, CanESM5, and HadGEM3­GC31­LL are coarser than
1ºx1º and are regridded using bilinear interpolation, while conservative remapping is used
for the rest. Full results of the interpolation comparison are provided in Appendix A.
All steps are implemented with xESMF and displayed in Figure 4.2. Source and target
grids are defined using SCRIP­format descriptors, that contain cell centre and corner co­
ordinates in 1D arrays along with metadata. xESMF uses these to construct a sparse
interpolation matrix, which is applied to flattened source data and reshaped to the target
grid layout. The result is a harmonized dataset with updated coordinates and metadata.

4.3
Bias correction
The bias correction process consists of statistically adjusting one dataset with the objec­
tive of removing systematic model errors and improving data consistency with respect to
observations.
Based on the literature review (chapter 3), the linear bias correction method was selected
for its simplicity, interpretability, and suitability when correcting mean biases in continuous
variables like irradiance and temperature [62].
Bias correction is applied two times. First, ERA5 is bias corrected using SARAH­3, taking
four years of data (1996, 2010, 2012, 2013). After, the corrected ERA5 dataset is used
to bias correct the CMIP6 Climate Models outputs, taking data from the historical period
of 1980 to 2014. The bias factor from the historical period is also applied to the future
period, assuming time­invariant biases. After bias­correcting each dataset and variable,
the results from the historical period of ERA5 and CMIP6 models have been contrasted
with SARAH­3 to ensure coherence before performing the power calculations. Figure 4.3
shows the overall process of bias correction carried out throughout the present work, and
irradiance variable equivalences among datasets are summarized in Table 4.1 to facilitate

As described in chapter 2, hourly ERA5 data was already available for the historical period

As described in chapter 2, ERA5 has been selected as the reference dataset to correct the

the years. This means that 1996, 2010, 2012, 2013 from SARAH­3 are compared to 1996,
2010, 2012, 2013 from ERA5. Besides, it is assumed that the regridding from 0.25ºx0.25º
to 0.3ºx0.3º does not introduce substantial errors that affect this statement.

of using only one year instead of four was evaluated. For direct irradiance, the mean
relative error in the resulting bias factor was just ­0.24%, while for diffuse irradiance it was
higher at 1.03%, reflecting its greater sensitivity to seasonal and inter­annual variability.
Although using four years yields more stable corrections, the results show that even a
single year provides sufficiently accurate bias factors for the intended application.
As explained in section 2.1, SARAH­3 is the limiting factor for geographical coverage, as
it excludes the upper­right corner of the European region. Because SARAH­3 data are
unavailable there and solar irradiance potential is minimal at those high latitudes, the area
is excluded in the study.

4.4
Power time series calculation
Converting meteorological variables into power generation is essential because their rela­
tionship is non­linear. Photovoltaic power generation depends not only on the total amount
of direct and diffuse irradiance but also on their spatial and temporal distribution, panel
orientation, and the impact of temperature on system efficiency. Therefore, studying the
changes in meteorological variables does not provide straight­forward insight on how the
solar power would be affected. Converting them into power provides a more accurate and
system­relevant basis for assessing the effects of climate variability and change.
Power time series is obtained using atlite[14]. In the following section, the power calcu­
lation algorithm is presented. A slightly different process is followed for obtaining power
from ERA5 than for the climate models, and these differences are also explained. The
calculation requires the input of a 3D xarray dataset of dimensions (time x lon x lat) that
contains the irradiance and temperature variables needed for the calculations.
4.4.1
Calendar standardization
This is the first step when working with the climate models. Climate and Forecast conven­
tions define Gregorian as the default calendar. However, from the chosen models, two of
them use the proleptic Gregorian calendar, three of them use the no leap calendar and
two of them use the 260 day calendar.
The proleptic Gregorian is a Gregorian calendar extended to dates before 1582­10­15. A
year is a leap year if either it is divisible by 4 but not by 100 or it is divisible by 400. A
no­leap calendar is a Gregorian calendar without leap years (all years are 365 days long).
22 Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Solar Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System
==PAGE:33==
Finally, in a 360 day calendar all years are 360 days divided into 30 day months. In this
step, no­leap and 360 days calendars are changed into proleptic Gregorian calendars to
facilitate downstream calculations and comparison.
First, leap years are identified for both calendars. For the no­leap calendar, the day 29th
of February is added with the mean values between February 28th and March 1st. For the
360­day calendar, a Gregorian timestamp is created. If the day is in the 1–28 range of any
month, the values from the 360­calendar are directly mapped in the Gregorian timestamp.
For February 29th in leap years in the Gregorian calendar, the 360­day calendar values
at February 28th and March 1st are taken and averaged. For Gregorian days falling on
the 31st, the values from the 30th of the month are repeated.
4.4.2
Solar position
In this step, the solar position, defined by solar altitude and solar azimuth, is calculated
for each time step. Solar altitude is the angle between the Sun and the observer’s local
horizon. Solar azimuth is the clockwise angle from North. For example, azimuth of 180°
faces exactly South. Calculating the solar position is necessary to obtain the angle of
incidence between the incoming solar radiation and the surface of the PV module. This
angle, together with the solar altitude, affects how much direct irradiance is received on
the tilted plane of the array. Recall that the plane of array (POA) refers to the tilted and
oriented plane that faces the sun. Therefore, computing the solar position is critical to
obtain high­frequency and accurate PV CF time series.
The calculation algorithm is taken from [95][96][97]. A time shift is applied if necessary.
For the ERA5 data, the time shift is ­30 minutes, since the accumulated irradiance be­
tween 8:00 and 9:00 is provided at 9:00, and the solar position of the middle of the time
interval is taken. In the case of the CMIP6 models, the time shift is 0, since the time
stamp is already the middle of the measurement interval. Recall that irradiance is mea­
sured as the average hourly accumulated irradiance over 3h, from 15:00h to 18:00h, with
time stamp 16:30h, and temperature was interpolated to be provided also in the middle
of the interval. The output of this step is an xarray dataset containing solar altitude and
azimuth.

results in a simple formulation that uses basic trigonometry to compute each irradiance
component on the tilted plane.

4.5
Temporal aggregation and standardization
After the power CF time series is stored, the next step is to aggregate the results at daily,
annual and seasonal level.
In the case of ERA5, the time resolution is 1h and the irradiance is accumulated over
the period. Therefore, the resulting power capacity factor time series corresponds to the
capacity factor available in each hour of the day. In contrast, the data from CMIP6 is
available every 3h as an average of hourly accumulated irradiance. The capacity factors
time series gives, for each time stamp, how much power could be obtained for one hour.
Each power capacity factor is multiplied times 3 to take into account the capacity factor
representative of the 3 hours. This approach implies assuming that in each hour, the
irradiance will be the same and equivalent to the mean hourly accumulated irradiance.
This is a strong assumption that is discussed in the Limitations section. Once total power
capacity factor is obtained, for each hour in ERA5 and for each hour in the 3­h interval in
CMIP6, daily aggregates are obtained and stored.

4.6
Energy droughts frequency and duration
An energy drought is defined as a period in which the solar power capacity factor is below
a certain threshold. As seen in the literature review, there is not a unique definition for
this threshold. It can be static or dynamic, with the latter varying according to seasonality.
In this case, a dynamic threshold is chosen. This decision is guided by the objective of
evaluating how energy droughts may challenge energy system resilience. A static thresh­
old would incorrectly classify many winter days as droughts, even though low solar output
during that season is expected. In contrast, a dynamic threshold accounts for seasonal
variability and identifies only unexpected or abnormally low solar output, providing a more
meaningful signal of potential stress on the system.
A weekly threshold is defined as 50% of the median daily solar output for each calendar
week, so that any day falling below what is ”normal” for that week is flagged, enabling
a direct comparison between BoC/EoC periods to determine whether there are more or
fewer days with less sun than expected. A lower percentile would isolate more extreme
shortfalls, reducing the number of events, amplifying noise from random weather swings,
and hiding moderate deviations from normal performance under typical operation.
The way of calculating the median daily solar drought for each week is the following:
for each period, from the daily solar capacity factor, the median daily CF is calculated
across all the days in that week for all the period. Let period P ∈BoC, EoC, and week
w be a calendar week, then the set of all days in week w across the years of period P
is DP,w = {d : d falls in week w during period P}. Then, the expression of the weekly
drought threshold is shown in Equation 4.17, with TP,w being the threshold for each week
in each period, and CFd the capacity factor of day d.
TP,w = 0.5 · median{CFd : d ∈DP,w}
(4.17)
Once the threshold has been defined for each week of each period and for each model,
each day in the time series is evaluated. Each drought event is recorded and stored with
the year of occurrence, season, location, start month and duration. An energy drought
has minimum duration of one day. This information allows to analyse number of drought
days and duration of the events, both at yearly and seasonal levels.
For the drought duration, the P75 and P90 durations are calculated. The P75 and P90
are the 75th and 90th percentiles of the distribution of drought event durations for each
grid cell. P75 is the duration below which 75% of all droughts fall (and above which the
longest 25% lie), while P90 marks the point below which 90% of events lie (capturing
only the most prolonged 10%). The median duration would describe the central or most
common event, whereas P75 and P90 highlight how often and by how much unusually
persistent shortfalls occur. This gives a better representation of system stress: it shows
Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Solar Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System 27
==PAGE:38==
how ”bad” the drought can be without being an outlier. This information is critical for
understanding worst­case scenario, and is useful for long­term, reliable energy planning.
4.7
Per­country aggregation
Per­country aggregation is done slightly different for the power time series and for the
energy droughts. For the power time series, both the 1ºx1º grid and the European coun­
try boundaries are reprojected into a common equal­area coordinate reference system
(EPSG:3035). A spatial overlay then “cuts” each grid cell wherever it overlaps a country,
yielding one polygon per cell­country fragment. The area of each fragment is calculated
and divided by the full cell area to give a weight (the fraction of the cell in that country).
Multiplying each cell’s solar power by its country­specific weight and summing across all
fragments inside a country boundary produces an area­weighted per­country aggregate.
A more detailed overview of the method is provided in Appendix B. The per­country ag­
gregates are provided for yearly and seasonal power capacity factors.
For the energy droughts, the approach is slightly different. Now a ”winner­takes­all” rule is
applied. As before, the 1ºx1º grid and the European country polygons are reprojected and
overlaid, and the area of each fragment is measured. Instead of assigning weights, each
cell is assigned to only one country, which is the country of the biggest overlay fragment.
Per­country aggregates of drought frequency and duration are calculated at yearly and
seasonal levels.
The reason behind this method difference is the nature of the variables. Fractional­area
weighting is applied to capacity factors because it guarantees that every fragment of each
grid cell contributes continuously and proportionally to the national total, preserving true
area­weighted sums of solar output. Drought frequency and duration, however, are counts
of discrete events measured in whole days. Assigning each cell’s entire drought count to
the country with the greatest overlap avoids generating fractional or decimal days, and
simplifies computation and interpretation.
The per­country aggregation has been applied for EU27 countries except Finland due to
lack of satellite coverage and Malta due to its size (it only occupies 3% of a grid cell); and
the UK, Norway, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Montenegro, Serbia, Albania, North Macedo­
nia, Switzerland, Turkey, Belarus, Moldova and Kosovo. Sometimes the results are only
shown for some representative countries from this list: Germany, Italy, Spain, France,
the UK and Poland. These countries are chosen because they show geographic diversity
and because they are some of the countries with largest PV development or potential.
Germany has the largest PV capacity in Europe, Italy and Spain have great potential and
rapid growth, France has ambitious solar targets and Poland is one of the fastest­growing
PV markets in eastern Europe. When comparing results across these countries, the CF
is normalized by country size.

The use of relative changes instead of absolute values is motivated by the presence of

conditions are expected to change is more critical than the exact absolute value.
4.9
Statistical tests
Once all relative differences in CFs and drought metrics have been obtained for all mod­
els and temporal aggregations at country level, the agreement between models and the
significance of these relative differences is analysed.
A first approach is to compare how many models agree on the direction of the changes
and the magnitude of the changes. However, this approach does not provide a measure
of uncertainty or significance.
Therefore, a more formal method is developed. The Student’s t­test[100] is applied to
evaluate the significance of differences in mean values between two periods. The number
of models that agree on the significance of the results is recorded and taken as a measure
of the robustness of the result.
Once the significance is evaluated, the median difference is calculated across all models.
Reporting median values instead of means provides robustness to outliers and greater
stability across models that show high internal variability.
The t­test requires that each observation is independent from each other. However, cli­
mate and geophysical data has temporal and spatial autocorrelation. The implications
of this assumption are discussed as a limitation in section 6.4. Another assumption is
that for each group (BoC, EoC samples for each model), data is approximately normally
distributed. To validate this assumption, the Shapiro­Wilks[101] test is applied. Further
detail on these two statistical tests is provided in section D.1.
For cases where the Shapiro­Wilks test does not pass normality, the underlying distribu­
tion is visualized by estimating the probability density function via kernel density estima­
tion[52][102] and with Quantile­Quantile plots. A detailed explanation on this validation
method is provided in section D.2.

Results
This chapter presents a comprehensive evaluation of how solar generation and associ­

Bias factors
First, the bias factors between SARAH­3 and ERA5 are presented for direct and diffuse
irradiation. Afterwards, the bias factors between the corrected ERA5 and the CMIP6

diffuse, total irradiance and temperature.
Bias factor between SARAH­3 and ERA5
It has been observed that the bias factor for direct irradiance is consistently below 1, mean­

Discussion
While the previous section outlined trends in PV power and energy droughts, this chapter
interprets the results presented earlier and discusses its implications for solar energy
generation in Europe and for the energy system overall.
First, the key trends in the main performance indicators are examined. Second, the impli­
cations of these trends for energy planning and policy­making are examined, particularly
in the context of system reliability. Third, the findings are compared with relevant litera­
ture to assess consistency and differences from prior work. Finally, the key limitations of
the methodology and the data are discussed, highlighting the sources of uncertainty and
potential improvements.
6.1
Assessment of Key Indicators
This section evaluates how climate change affects the main indicators of solar energy
performance, photovoltaic power output and energy drought characteristics.

Solar power capacity factor

robustness of these impacts. Afterwards, the role of temperature is discussed.

differences are small or near zero.
In summary, climate change is projected to have the most robust and beneficial impacts on
solar energy during autumn and spring, while winter and summer remain more uncertain
and regionally variable.
The role of temperature
Temperature is projected to increase significantly between BoC and EoC with continental
average of +3­4.5ºC (1%­1.4%) under SSP5­8.5. This leads to a consistent yearly loss
in solar CF of 3­4.5% due to non­linear reduction of PV efficiency with higher cell temper­
ature. It can be concluded that the increase in solar CF under climate change is mainly
driven by irradiance. However, this positive effect is partially offset by the negative impact
of rising temperatures on PV efficiency.
Highest temperature increases are observed in Spain and southern regions, yet these
areas do not exhibit the most significant reductions in solar CF. This is largely because
irradiance gains can partially offset the negative effect of rising temperatures. In these
regions, PV systems may remain efficient despite warming, highlighting the resilience of
southern solar potential. In contrast, eastern European countries (Poland, Romania, Hun­
gary) experience similarly high temperature increases but less irradiance gains, leading
to greater net losses in PV potential increase, especially in summer and autumn. These
regions may face higher climate risk to PV performance due to an unfavourable balance
between irradiance gain and temperature rise.
The seasonal breakdown reveals that losses concentrate in summer, with temperature­
induced CF reductions 4­5 greater than in spring or winter. Autumn also shows significant
losses, particularly in eastern and central Europe. Meanwhile, winter and spring show
minimal temperature­induced losses, both because temperatures are lower and because
irradiance is already reduced in these seasons.

6.1.2
Energy droughts
This section presents insights related to energy droughts. First, the performance of cli­
mate models when reproducing historical energy droughts patterns is evaluated, and then
projected changes in these events between the beginning and end of the century are ex­
amined.
Comparison of ERA5 and CMIP6 model performance
CMIP6 models systematically overestimate the number of drought days per year, esti­
mating 12 to 30 more drought days than the corrected ERA5 dataset (3­8%). This can
be linked to overestimation of cloud cover, inaccurate aerosol modelling, and biases in
irradiance components.
Bias factor analysis shows that many CMIP6 models, specifically MRI­ESM2­0 and the
CMCC family, underestimate diffuse irradiance, leading to lower total irradiance on cloudy
days. Models are more likely to classify these cloudy days as drought days, explaining part
of the overestimation of CMIP6. This highlights the limitations of using absolute drought
statistics, even after bias correction. Instead, relative trends between BoC and EoC offer
a more reliable interpretation, emphasizing the need to address systematic model biases,
particularly in the representation of diffuse irradiance.
At the geographical level, model errors are not spatially consistent: while ERA5 shows
a clear north­south gradient in drought frequency (more frequent in the north) , this pat­
tern is not reflected in the CMIP6 model overestimation. These inconsistencies likely
come from parametrization of physical processes (cloud formation, radiation transfer,
land­atmosphere interactions) and not from errors in regional climate representation.
Nevertheless, CMIP6 models generally capture drought duration more accurately. The
P75 duration in ERA5 ranges from 1 to 2 days, increasing from south to north/east. CMIP6
slightly overestimates duration in southern countries (by 0.75­1 day), but shows no sig­
nificant bias in most of Europe. However, for longer events (P90), CMIP6 models slightly
overestimate duration of long events, mainly in southern Europe(0.5­1 day).
Finally, regarding seasonality, the largest model discrepancies between ERA5 and mod­
els occur in winter and spring. With discrepancy being the highest in winter (6­13 days
overestimation). This reinforces the known difficulty of modelling solar potential in cloud­
dominated or low­irradiance seasons.
Changes in energy droughts between BoC and EoC
Climate change is likely to slightly reduce the number of energy drought days in Europe.
Across models, a modest but consistent decline of 8­11 drought days per year (2­3% of
the year) is projected between BoC and EoC, as shown in Figure 6.3. For context, BoC
projections show that energy drought days account for approximately 8–25% of the year,
depending on the country. While the magnitude of change varies regionally, the overall
impact remains limited, with only a small but robust decline observed.
This decline occurs without a clear spatial pattern, meaning that reductions are not con­
centrated in either southern or northern countries. The statistical agreement is relatively
high (5­6 models). The most robust seasonal improvements in drought frequency are
projected for autumn and summer. Winter remains the most uncertain, with low agree­
ment and mixed signal direction, reinforcing earlier conclusions about its unpredictability
for solar planning.

6.1.3
Uncertainty and variability
Throughout the CF and energy drought assessments, inter­annual and inter­model vari­
ability have emerged as key sources of uncertainty. In particular, the CMCC models con­
sistently deviate from the ensemble, underscoring the importance of using multi­model
ensembles rather than relying on individual models. Including models from different fam­
ilies, each with distinct resolutions and parametrizations, helps mitigate the influence of
Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Solar Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System 49
==PAGE:60==
outliers and structural biases. Besides, to reduce the influence of outliers or skewed dis­
tributions, this analysis reports changes using ensemble medians rather than means.
Many of these uncertainties appear to originate from the parametrizations and simplifi­
cations of the climate models, which are also linked to the coarse horizontal resolution
of the models. From the comparison between CMIP6 and ERA5 drought predictions, it
seems that bias correction alone has not been sufficient to reduce important systematic
errors. The use of the socioeconomic pathways (SSP5­8.5) also introduces uncertainties
from their assumptions. A clear example is the expected reduction in pollution even in the
fossil fuel dominated ”worst­case scenario”[103].
Finally, the application of statistically robust methods helps navigate these uncertainties.
While such methods have their own limitations, they provide a more consistent and ob­
jective framework for interpreting changes, and supporting more informed and resilient
decision­making. The alternative of relying only on model agreement would risk over­
looking the magnitude and significance of changes, potentially underestimating important
signals or overemphasising areas of coincidental consensus.
In conclusion, the results from this research need to be carefully interpreted. When ap­
plying findings to energy planning, adaptability and resilience to all the range of possible
scenarios should be prioritized rather than focusing on mean trends or median changes.
6.2
Implications for the European energy system
In this section, the implications for the future planning and resilience of the European
power system are presented. On one hand, the share of renewables needs to keep in­
creasing to avoid reaching the worst­case scenario of emissions, SSP5­8.5, and avoid or
mitigate climate change impacts on weather variables. To achieve and sustain long­term
decarbonization, PV deployment must continue after 2050[36].
On the other hand, the European energy system needs to prepare for increasing chal­
lenges in system reliability. Climate change is expected to reduce SDM (supply­demand
match) [6] and alter meteorological variables[1][5]. Multiple papers show that a decrease
in wind is expected in Europe at the end of the century under SSP5­8.5[104], with a ro­
bust decline during summer for central and northern Europe[6]. This will be combined
with a decrease in hydropower in Southern Europe[52]. On top of these changes, natural
weather variability will still be a challenge to system stability.
In this context, the findings from this thesis provide valuable input for resilient energy
system planning. The robust increase in solar CF and a modest decrease in energy
drought frequency suggest that solar PV will remain a resilient and increasingly valuable
resource in Europe’s future low­carbon energy mix. The deployment of solar capacity can
help compensate reduced wind in northern and central Europe and reduced hydropower
in southern Europe.
However, Jurasz et al.[89] show that, while combining wind and solar significantly reduces
the frequency of resource­specific energy droughts, their temporal complementarity is
largely limited to the seasonal scale and is negligible at the daily level. This highlights
important limitations of complementarity: short­term variability remains a challenge.
This, combined with current findings on high regional and seasonal variability in solar
changes, reinforces the need for flexible energy systems that incorporate storage, de­
mand response, and diversified generation to maintain stability and reliability under vari­
able renewable output.
50 Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Solar Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System
==PAGE:61==
Besides, from the seasonal analysis, seasonal asymmetries have been observed, with
winter showing low output and high variability. These asymmetries should inform storage
sizing. Importantly, energy drought duration is not projected to increase, with most events
remaining in the 1­3 days range. This reinforces the value of short­duration flexibility
options like batteries over long­duration backup.
Interestingly, regions with currently moderate solar output are projected to experience the
largest relative gains, while already high­performing regions show signs of plateauing.
This suggests a shift in marginal benefit, making emerging regions in central and north­
ern Europe increasingly attractive for solar investments. These trends support a more
geographically balanced deployment of solar PV across Europe, which could improve
system­wide resilience and reduce transmission bottlenecks. They also support the rel­
evance of investing on temperature resilient solar technologies to preserve performance
in southern countries and during the warmest seasons.
One additional remark from this work is the lack of high­frequency, standardized irradiance
data for energy system modelling, and the lack of research on the skill of CMIP6 and
ERA5 datasets to predict solar energy droughts, which limits the accuracy and confidence
of long­term resilience assessments. Improving these points will allow for robust energy
system planning where the impacts of climate change are included in the decision­making
in an accurate manner.
In summary, these findings call for concrete adjustments in energy system design: pri­
oritizing targeted solar deployment in regions with strong projected gains, investing in
temperature­resilient PV technologies, and sizing storage and backup capacity to reflect
seasonal asymmetries in solar availability. Finally, overcoming the disconnect between
energy system and climate modelling is essential to meet ambitious climate mitigation
goals while including risks in decision­making[7].
6.3
Comparison with literature
In this section, the main results obtained are systematically compared with findings from
literature.
6.3.1
Bias factors
Regarding the bias factors, results from this study match those found in literature: re­
analysis data tends to overestimate direct irradiance and underestimates diffuse[105].
Regarding the CMIP6 models, literature shows that GHI is reproduced reasonably well,
but the division between direct and diffuse radiation is less frequently evaluated and is
subjected to systematic biases [7][106], as found in the results.
6.3.2
Findings in annual and seasonal solar power
The literature provides a range of projections on how climate change may affect photo­
voltaic (PV) potential in Europe, with differences in modelling approaches, scenarios, and
variables contributing to the diversity of findings.
Hou et al.[5] used CMIP6 data under SSP5–8.5, and they found a 5–6% increase in so­
lar power, with the strongest gains in Central and Northern Europe and more modest
improvements in the South. These results match the present findings, which show in­
creases of 8–10% in Central Europe and 5–7% in Southern regions. Hou et al. also
analysed seasonal patterns, concluding that summer PV output moderately increases in
Northern Europe, while winter generation declines by 10–20% in those same areas. In
Southern Europe, winter output slightly improves. Although their work does not cover
Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Solar Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System 51
==PAGE:62==
spring and autumn explicitly, both studies agree that non­winter seasons are the primary
drivers of annual PV gains and that seasonal asymmetry is a key consideration.
Feron et al.[40] focus also on the summer and winter seasons. They project a 5–7.5%
summer PV potential increase in Central Europe and a 2.5–5% winter increase in South­
ern Spain. In Northern Europe, winter trends are mixed, with regional increases and
decreases of around ±2.5%. These findings align with the current research, mainly in the
observed variability and uncertainty in winter results.
Müller et al.[26], use CMIP5 and RCP8.5 to project annual PV changes between −6%
and +3% by mid­century, with gains in Southern and central Europe and declines in the
north. Their monthly data show peak increases in autumn (up to 10%), and seasonal
analysis highlights winter variability (positive changes in the south, negative in the north)
along with modest spring and summer gains. They also assess inter­model agreement,
finding the strongest consensus in central Europe during late spring and summer, and the
weakest in northern Europe in winter. Although based on a different time horizon, their
results support this study’s emphasis on regional and seasonal analysis, underscoring
the need to avoid continent­wide generalizations.
In summary, while methodological differences across studies explain variations in mag­
nitude, there is a growing consensus that climate change will generally increase PV po­
tential in Central and Southern Europe, with stronger gains outside of winter and greater
uncertainty in northern regions and in the colder months. The findings in this thesis align
well with the most recent CMIP6­based research and highlight the value of ensemble and
seasonal analysis in assessing solar energy futures.
6.3.3
Impact of temperature on PV potential
Müller et al.[26] investigate the extent to which rising temperatures reduce PV potential
between the periods 2007–2027 and 2060–2080. They conclude that the temperature­
induced losses are largest during the summer and autumn months (June to November),
reaching −1.5% to −2.5% across many parts of Europe. In contrast, the impact is lower
during winter and spring, particularly in southern Europe. Their spatial analysis highlights
that southern and eastern European countries experience the greatest reductions in PV
potential during the warmer months, a pattern that aligns with the seasonal and regional
trends identified in the current study.
Liu et al. [53] conclude that even if the rising air temperatures mitigate the increase in
PV potential in some regions, in southern Europe the rise in radiation dampens the tem­
perature effect. This conclusion matches with the results observed, notably in the Iberian
peninsula. Finally, Jerez et al. [42] find that temperature decreases PV potential in all
Europe in the range of ­1 to ­4%, with special incidence in eastern and northern Europe,
consistent with current findings. In summary, temperature results are consistent with lit­
erature findings. These effects are secondary to irradiance changes but still important to
include in long­term PV impact assessments.
6.3.4
Energy droughts
To the author’s knowledge, no study has yet conducted a systematic evaluation of the skill
of CMIP6 models in reproducing historical solar energy droughts in Europe, especially in
comparison with reanalysis datasets such as ERA5. Wilczak et al.[107] evaluated the
capacity of ERA5 to represent energy droughts against observational data. They observe
small errors for short energy droughts in complete overcast conditions. However, their
findings highlight limitations at longer durations during partially cloudy conditions.This in­
dicates that ERA5 is not properly reflecting long solar energy droughts. Their study does

not assess CMIP6 model performance.
Some literature does examine projected solar energy droughts. For example, Kapica
et al.[86] use EURO­CORDEX RCMs and conclude that most models project a modest
increase in total drought days per year, mainly during winter and autumn, while southern
Europe may experience slight improvements. This contrasts with the slight decrease in
drought frequency found in the present study, likely due to differences between the RCMs
used and the CMIP6 GCMs applied here. CORDEX RCMS show no change in cloud
cover in the RCP8.5 scenario, while this decreases in CMIP5 GCMs. RCMs also show
decreases in total irradiance in many places[26]. Nonetheless, their seasonal findings
partly align: they also project decreases in drought days during spring in southern Europe
and in summer across France, northern Iberia, and parts of Germany.
Liu et al. [6] found slight improvements in the SDM (supply demand match) related to
reduced drought events, but these can be offset by demand changes. Feron et al.[40]
adopt a different drought definition, focusing on days when PV output falls below 10% of
historical values, but similarly report a 25­50% reduction in such extreme­low­output days
in southern Europe during summer, and 0­25% reductions in winter. While not directly
comparable, their results support the broader conclusion of a decreasing trend in the
most extreme low­solar­output events, resonating with the findings of this study.
6.4
Limitations and sources of uncertainty
Despite the robustness of the applied methods throughout this study, several limitations
and sources of uncertainty remain. These come from the uncertainties introduced by the
datasets or the methods used, from simplifications in PV modelling, from the definition
of energy droughts or from the application of statistical testing methods to climate data.
These limitations often arise as a trade­off between the computational costs of the anal­
yses and the available time to achieve the goals of this project.
6.4.1
Bias correction and regridding
Bias correction has been used to correct ERA5 with SARAH­3 and to correct CMIP6
models with ERA5. Both datasets are widely used in literature, but neither of them is free
from uncertainty. SARAH­3 still shows a mean bias of 0.34% mainly due to the use of
ancillary inputs, and ERA5 shows a mean bias of 3.58%, mostly driven by parametriza­
tion errors[23]. The bias correction method can also impact the results. In this case,
the linear method chosen assumes uniform biases across the distribution, without chang­
ing extremes or variability. A different approach to bias­correction could lead to different
results.
The same argument applies to the regridding method. Regridding is necessary for dataset
comparison, but it can distort the data and introduce errors that propagate to the CF
estimates. A thorough analysis of the optimal regridding method to use in each step was
carried out to minimize this errors.
6.4.2
Spatial and temporal resolution
Spatial and temporal detail has been reduced as a trade­off to ensure computational fea­
sibility while maintaining sufficient accuracy for the analysis. Even if solar capacity fac­
tor is calculated at an hourly or 3­hourly level, the main analyses are subsequently con­
ducted using annual and seasonal averages. This approach masks intra­day variability
and smoothens extremes, potentially underestimating the impact of short­term fluctua­
tions on system reliability. In parallel, the standard 1ºx1º grid resolution does not allow for
the identification of regional climates or the assessment of localized phenomena, such as
Analysis of Climate Change­Impacted Solar Energy Generation for a Resilient European Energy System 53
==PAGE:64==
topographically­driven irradiance patterns or small­scale energy droughts, which may be
critical for spatial planning and infrastructure design.
6.4.3
Model Diversity and Outlier Behavior
Among the 7 CMIP6 climate models used, the CMCC models systematically deviate from
the rest of the ensemble mean in solar capacity factor and energy drought projections.
They had significantly higher biases both in the direct and diffuse components than the
rest of the models.
However, these models were kept in the ensemble to keep inter­model diversity. Remov­
ing any would reduce the statistical robustness and representativeness of the analysis.
Keeping all models helps avoid cherry­picking and ensures that a diverse range of plau­
sible climate futures is represented. The results from these models should be interpreted
with caution, as they continue to diverge from the rest of the ensemble even after bias
correction. Their presence contributes to a more comprehensive picture of model uncer­
tainty, which is essential for robust risk assessment in climate impact studies. Finally, the
findings suggest that bias correction of irradiance alone may not be sufficient to harmonize
model outputs when underlying physical processes are substantially different.
6.4.4
PV modelling
Several simplifications and assumptions have been made to model the PV CF. These are
presented here.
Albedo Assumptions
Across the reviewed literature, none of the papers put any special focus on albedo. It is
common practice in solar modelling studies and also in some of the reviewed papers to
utilize a static average value of 0.2 for albedo in Europe[108][42]. Reflected irradiance
from typically contributes 5­10% of the total tilted irradiance [109]. Therefore, it is still
important when evaluating changes in the CF that are in the order of 5­15%, in particular
under high solar angles or snow­covered conditions and in the context of increasing use
of bifacial modules. In this study, the climatological average of ERA5 albedo values for
the historical period was used for each grid cell. This means that a location­specific factor
is used. However, it is still assumed that there will be no change in surface reflectivity due
to land­use change or snow coverage trends between BoC and EoC, which is a strong
assumption. Land use change affected almost one third of global land area between 1960
and 2019[110]. It is to be expected that these changes will continue and increase in the
future.
Constant technology assumptions
The current study does not include potential improvements on PV technology in order to
ensure that the observed differences between periods are attributable to climate effects
alone. However, efficiency of PV cells has been increasing 0.17% per year for crystalline
silicon cells, and this trend will likely continue in the future, leading to higher capacity
factors in the future[26].
While this improvement does not affect the percentage change in solar capacity factor
(CF) due to climate change, it does imply that absolute gains or losses will be larger in
real terms. Moreover, future PV technologies may exhibit different temperature and irra­
diance response characteristics, potentially modifying how climate change affects system
performance.
Negligible impact of surface wind speed
Although surface wind speed can influence PV module temperature through convective
cooling, its impact on long­term solar photovoltaic (PV) potential is generally minimal.

Jerez et al.[42] evaluated the effect of wind speed changes on PV output and concluded
that it has no statistically significant influence on mean PV generation across Europe.
Even in extreme cases, wind speed only contributed ±1% to the total change in PV out­
put. In this study, wind speed has not been included in the calculation of the solar capacity
factor (CF). Given the small expected contribution of wind, its exclusion does not signifi­
cantly affect the validity of the results.
Temporal Averaging of Irradiance
To estimate solar capacity factor, 3­hourly irradiance data was used, with each value rep­
resenting the average hourly irradiance over a 3­hour window. Power generation was
computed using this average and the solar position at the midpoint of the interval. The
resulting power estimate was then multiplied by the interval duration (3 hours) to approx­
imate the total energy generated during that window and not just during 1 hour. This
method assumes relatively stable irradiance and solar geometry over the interval. This
introduces inaccuracies due to rapid changes in solar angles (especially at sunrise and
sunset) and due to cloud cover or atmospheric changes. Despite these issues, the 3­
hourly approach is a practical and consistent solution given data availability, and is justi­
fied in the context of assessing long­term climatological trends.
6.4.5
Energy drought definition
There is no standard definition of energy drought. The choice of threshold was based on
previous literature and the idea of using a dynamic threshold that accounted for seasonal
variability. The results presented in this research could change significantly if threshold
definition changed from 50% of the daily mean power per calendar week to 40% or 30%
or if the daily mean was used for each calendar day. Another limitation is the use of a
daily threshold, which masks sub­daily droughts that could have significant impacts on
the power system. Besides, when looking at duration, only long events were considered,
even if these might be more unusual.
6.4.6
Statistical Limitations in Climate Data Analysis
It is common in research to use the Student’s t­test [100] to assess whether mean values
differ significantly between two periods[42]. Before applying the t­test, researchers often
verify that each sample is approximately normally distributed using the Shapiro­Wilks test
[101]. However, statistical testing in climate and geophysical studies faces two major
challenges: temporal and spatial autocorrelation in the data, and the need to adjust for
multiple hypothesis tests over gridded fields [111][112].
Applying classical t­tests [100] to climate or geophysical data, treating them as indepen­
dent, causes the estimated variance of the mean (and other statistics) to be too low,
meaning that p­values appear smaller than they should. Wilks[111] shows that ignoring
temporal and spatial autocorrelation inflates Type I error rates, so researchers risk declar­
ing false statistical significance.
At the same time, testing each grid point independently without any adjustment means
that, out of hundreds or thousands of tests, many will reject the null purely by chance.
Wilks [112] points out that marking every locally significant result will almost certainly
produce false positives scattered across the map, affecting real patterns of change.
In combination, these two issues can lead to false detection of climate impacts or inflated
claims about model accuracy, and the statistical significance results need to be interpreted
with caution.

7
Conclusion
This study has examined how climate change may alter the performance and reliability of
solar photovoltaic (PV) generation in Europe using an ensemble of CMIP6 climate models
and high­resolution modelling of solar capacity factor (CF). The research addresses future
changes in CF, energy drought frequency and duration, and the influence of temperature.
Results show a robust and widespread increase in annual solar CF across Europe under
the SSP5­8.5 scenario, with the largest relative gains in central Europe. The direction
of change is highly consistent across models. Seasonal analysis reveals that the most
substantial and robust improvements occur in autumn, while spring also shows moderate
but consistent increases.
Temperature­induced losses in PV efficiency are shown to partially offset expected gains
in CF, particularly in summer and autumn and in southern and eastern Europe. This
highlights the importance of considering temperature effects alongside irradiance in long­
term solar energy projections, especially during peak generation periods.
Projections indicate a slight decline in the frequency of energy drought days across Eu­
rope, while event duration remains largely unchanged. However, these signals are weaker
than those observed for capacity factors, and no consistent geographical pattern emerges
in the changes. This suggest that solar­related reliability risks may not intensify, but un­
certainties remain, particularly at regional scales.
Together, these findings provide useful insights for integrating climate projections into
energy system modelling and support the development of more climate­resilient solar
energy strategies through improved methodological rigour.
7.1
Research questions
In this section, the research questions formulated at the start of the thesis are replied to.
How will projected climate changes affect the characteristics of solar energy po­
tential across European regions by 2065­2099?
Climate model projections indicate a robust increase in solar capacity factor (CF) across
Europe by 2065­2099, with most countries seeing annual gains of 4–10%, particularly in
central and eastern Europe. There is high model agreement on the direction of change but
variability in the magnitude. Solar energy emerges as a relatively reliable resource under
climate change, potentially helping to alleviate the impacts of reduced or more uncer­
tain output from other renewable sources. Seasonally, the strongest and most consistent
increases occur in autumn, followed by spring. Winter and summer show greater uncer­
tainty due to climate sensitivity and temperature effects. Inter­model variability remains
significant, reinforcing the need for robust, spatially detailed analysis to interpret regional
impacts accurately.
How will projected climate changes affect the characteristics of solar energy droughts
across European regions by 2065­2099?
By 2065­2099, climate models project a modest but consistent decline (2­3%) in the fre­
quency of solar energy drought days across Europe, while drought duration remains
largely unchanged. The reduction does not seem to follow geographical patterns and
shows the strongest seasonal signals in autumn and summer. However, projections are

affected by high model uncertainty, especially in winter and spring. Importantly, a bet­
ter understanding of how ERA5 and CMIP6 models simulate energy droughts is needed,
especially under cloudy or low­irradiance conditions, as systematic biases in irradiance
components contribute to overestimations. Overall, while droughts may become slightly
less frequent, their structure and modelling remain uncertain, underscoring the importance
of ensemble­based approaches and careful interpretation.
How will changes in temperature impact changes in solar energy potential across
European regions by 2065­2099?
By 2065–2099, rising temperatures are projected to cause a 3–4% annual loss in solar
CF across Europe due to reduced PV efficiency at higher cell temperatures. These losses
are largest in summer and autumn, particularly in eastern and central Europe. However,
they do not outweigh the overall CF gains driven by increased irradiance. In southern
Europe, where temperature rises are highest, strong irradiance gains help maintain solar
performance, highlighting how the impact of temperature varies by region, depending on
whether increases in irradiance are enough to compensate for the losses caused by higher
temperatures.Regions with high temperature increases but lower irradiance increases are
more vulnerable to temperature losses.
To what extent do climate models consistently project the direction and intensity of
climate change impacts on solar energy, and how might these changes affect the
reliability and efficiency of renewable energy systems?
Climate models project robust increases in yearly CF, with all models agreeing in most
countries on the significance of the change. Seasonal changes in autumn and spring
also show 6 or 7 models agreeing for most countries. However, projections also show
significant inter­model and inter­annual variability, especially in the magnitude of changes
and in metrics like energy drought frequency. Inter­model variability has been found to be
significantly higher than inter­annual variability, emphasizing the importance of improving
climate model parametrizations and solar data availability.
In terms of system impact, the rising reliability of solar energy due to increased CF and po­
tentially decreased drought frequency positions PV as a key resource to offset declining
wind and hydropower potential under climate change. Nonetheless, regional and sea­
sonal uncertainties require flexible system design, with a focus on short­term storage,
temperature­resilient PV technologies, and geographically balanced deployment. Ulti­
mately, aligning energy system models with climate data and improving the representation
of solar­related variables will be essential for planning resilient, climate­informed energy
systems.

How will climate change impact future solar energy generation potential in Europe, and

Climate change is expected to alter the spatial and seasonal distribution of solar energy
potential across Europe. The most consistent finding is a robust increase in solar capacity
factor (CF) by 2065­2099, with annual gains of 4­10% projected across most countries.
These improvements are driven primarily by changes in irradiance, though temperature­
induced efficiency losses will partly offset gains in summer and autumn, especially in
Southern and Eastern Europe. Regions with currently moderate solar output (notably
Central and Northern Europe) are projected to experience the largest relative gains, sug­

stronger gains in spring and autumn, may help smooth supply variability, while winter

This findings highlight solar energy as an energy source with great potential to help achieve
the reduction of CO2 emissions in Europe. To ensure climate­resilient system design, en­
ergy modelling must incorporate climate­informed solar generation profiles, temperature
effects, and seasonal asymmetries. At the same time, energy planners and policymakers
need to consider inter­annual and inter­model variability into their assessments, and con­

it is essential to assess the skill of climate models and reanalysis datasets in captur­

analyse them. Advancing these areas will not only strengthen climate modelling but also
significantly improve the reliability of energy system analysis.
Ultimately, integrating more accurate climate information into energy planning is vital to
support the long­term decarbonization, reliability and resilience of the European power
system in the context of climate change.

Future Work
This study highlights several areas where future research could expand, refine, or comple­
ment the current findings. One key recommendation is the application of more advanced
statistical techniques to better address the limitations of classical significance testing in
spatial climate data, increasing the robustness of statistical assessments.
A second improvement area concerns the evolving understanding of solar generation
spatial correlation. While this thesis has focused on inter­model variability and country­
level trends, recent work by Craig et al. [7] shows that climate change may lead to more
synchronized PV output across regions, which suggests that geographic diversification
may be less effective as a risk mitigation strategy in the future. Future studies should
further analyse spatial correlations and their implications for system resilience and storage
planning.
From a modelling standpoint, there is a clear need to evaluate climate model skill in sim­
ulating not only mean solar variables but also rare events like energy droughts. This
thesis provides one of the first comparisons between CMIP6 models and ERA5 for solar
droughts, but broader benchmarking remains limited, especially using high­quality obser­
vational datasets. As highlighted in this work, the lack of standardized definitions and
metrics for energy droughts further complicates cross­study comparisons. Establishing a
community­wide framework for defining, detecting, and reporting energy droughts would
greatly advance the field and facilitate future synthesis efforts. In this work, only daily
drought events have been considered. It would be interesting to also investigate sub­
daily drought events to provide better insights on energy system resilience.
Finally, the climate­energy interface would benefit from the continued improvement of
climate model outputs tailored for energy applications. Following Craig et al.’s[7] recom­
mendations, future CMIP protocols should encourage the release of direct and diffuse
irradiance at sub­daily resolution, along with surface albedo and temperature, to enable
more accurate and flexible downstream energy modelling and bigger model ensembles.
Additionally, a better understanding of the drivers of irradiance changes would be valu­
able. Müller et al.[26] demonstrate the value of decomposing changes in surface radia­
tion into components driven by cloud cover and atmospheric composition. Integrating this
analysis with PV modelling would improve the physical interpretability of projected trends
and strengthen the link between atmospheric drivers and energy system impacts.
In summary, future research should focus on improving statistical treatment of spatial
data, evaluating spatial PV correlation, benchmarking energy drought modelling skills
and standardizing energy drought definitions. These steps are essential for enhancing
the robustness and applicability of climate­driven energy modelling under future climate
scenarios.

A.2
Regridding methods comparison
After literature review on optimal regridding method for data up­scaling (coarse to thin),
no conclusion was reached on best method for continuous variables like irradiance and
temperature between the two considered methods. Therefore, visual comparison of the
performance of both methods is performed.
The study case is total irradiance data from three different climate models with different
resolutions at one specific year. The destination grid is always the standard 1ºx1º grid.
Irradiance data is aggregated at the yearly level. The three compared models are CAN­
ESM5 (500 km), ACCESS­CM2 (250km) and HadGEM3­GC31­MM (100km).
The case of CanESM5, shown in Figure A.1, is the one with the more visible difference.
The resolution change is from 2.81ºx2.79º to 1ºx1º. In Figure A.1b, where the data has
been regridded using the conservative remapping method, the field exhibits sharper, more
abrupt pixel scale gradients. By contrast, Figure A.1c, which uses bilinear interpolation,
displays much smoother transitions between neighboring grid cells. This smoother be­
havior is closer to what irradiance looks like in reality, as observed in the ERA5 data.
It can also be seen that for CanESM5, bilinear and conservative methods both treat the
edges of the grid in a different way. A strip of zeros and Nans is seen at the bottom
of Figure A.1c and Figure A.1b, and not in the other models due to the grid resolution
and the plotted coordinates range in the destination grid. The conservative method sets
to 0 cells that are outside the source grid domain, and assigns a weighted value if the
destination cell is partially in the source grid domain. However, the bilinear method needs
4 neighboring cells in the source grid to generate a value in the new cell. That is why a
wider strip on Nans is observed at the bottom of the bilinear case, and a thinner strip of
zeros is observed in the conservative case.
In the case of ACCESS­CM2 in Figure A.2, the regridding is from 1.25ºx1.88º to 1ºx1º.
The difference between the conservative method and the bilinear method is still visible in
the area of North Africa, but the result is significantly more smooth than in the previous
case. The bilinear method still smoothens the data better.

Normality validation with KDE­based visualization and

The Student t­test[100] is applied to evaluate the the significance of the changes between
BoC and EoC in different variables. This method requires that the data in each group are
approximately normally distributed. Each group contains 35 values of yearly data from
one model and period. To validate this, the Shapiro­Wilks test [101] is applied. How­
ever, some cases do not pass the normality test. For these cases, the underlaying data
distribution is visualized by estimating the probability density function via kernel density
estimation[102][52] and by plotting the QQ­plot.

Kernel density estimation (KDE) smooths data into a continuous curve to visualize its dis­
tribution without assuming a specific model. A QQ­plot compares data quantiles to those
of a theoretical normal distribution; and the alignment of data along the diagonal suggests
normality, while deviations indicate non­normal features like skewness or heavy tails. Un­
like KDE, the QQ­plot directly assesses how well the data match a specific distribution,
making it useful for checking normality assumptions.
In this section, an example of the validation process is provided for the significance of
yearly changes in solar power. Most combinations of county and model pass the normality
test both for the BoC period and the EoC period. From 287 combinations of country and
period, 52 show that one of the two periods does not pass normality. Only in one case
(Hungary, CanESM5) normality is not fulfilled in both periods, and the relative difference
is not significant.
The analysis is shown in Figure D.1a. The first sub­figure (Figure D.1) shows the PDF
estimation. The histograms do not provide any clear conclusions, but the estimated distri­
bution looks normal in both cases, even if the EoC period does not pass the Shapiro­Wilks
test.
The QQ­plots are also observed, with the red line being the ideal fit between the sample
data and the theoretical normal distribution. When observing the QQ­plots (Figure D.1b
and Figure D.1c), it can be seen that the EoC period has significant deviations from the
red reference line, especially in the lower and upper quantiles, with several points falling
well outside the expected range. This suggests heavier tails or skewness, supported by
the low Shapiro­Wilk p­value of 0.001. While the EoC QQ­plot shows some tail deviations,
the central portion of the data still aligns reasonably well with the normal distribution. This
suggests that, despite the presence of outliers or mild skewness, the overall structure is
not strongly non­normal. In such cases, relying on the median instead of the mean is
advantageous because it reduces the influence of outliers and skewed tails, which can
distort the mean and misrepresent the central tendency. Although the EoC distribution
does not meet strict normality criteria, its central structure remains stable, and using the
median allows for meaningful comparisons without being misled by extreme values at the
margins.

difference between median duration per country is very close to 0, being the maximum
0.12 days less. Therefore, it is not possible to say that there is a change in energy drought
P75 duration due to climate change.
