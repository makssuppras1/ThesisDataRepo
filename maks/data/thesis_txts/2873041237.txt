Chapter 1
Summary
This master’s thesis addresses the growing consumer demand for clean-label food products
by investigating natural alternatives to synthetic preservatives and functional additives in
the popular Danish bread, Krydderboller. Conventional formulations of this bread rely on
E-numbers, palm oil, and high salt content, which are associated with significant public
health risks, including cardiovascular disease and metabolic disorders.
This research
aimed to develop and validate a healthier, safer, and more sustainable Krydderboller
prototype by replacing these ingredients with natural antimicrobial agents and functional
hydrocolloids.
The study employed a comparative experimental design to evaluate the efficacy of
rosemary essential oil, oregano essential oil, and chia seed gel as clean-label substitutes.
Physicochemical and microbiological analyses were conducted over a seven-day period to
assess each formulation’s impact on shelf life, microbial stability, moisture retention, and
texture. Key parameters measured included mold onset, total viable microbial counts,
water activity (aw), and pH.
Experimental results demonstrated that rosemary essential oil (0.3%) and a high salt
concentration (2.0%) were the most effective treatments for inhibiting microbial growth,
showing strong, broad-spectrum antimicrobial action and significantly delaying spoilage.
Oregano essential oil also proved highly effective against visible mold but was less ef-
fective against bacteria and yeasts. Furthermore, the incorporation of 3% chia seed gel
successfully extended the mold-free shelf life by three days, primarily by improving mois-
ture retention and enhancing crumb texture. Conversely, powdered natural additives and
the commercial emulsifier E471 were found to be ineffective.
This research successfully identifies viable, natural strategies to replace synthetic ad-
ditives in commercial bread production. It concludes that a combination of rosemary
essential oil and chia seed gel presents an optimised solution, creating a dual-function
system that ensures microbial safety while improving the sensory and nutritional pro-
file of the final product. The findings provide a scientific foundation for developing a
clean-label Krydderboller that meets consumer expectations for health, safety, and qual-
ity, offering a tangible pathway for the bakery industry to innovate toward healthier and
more sustainable products.

Chapter 2
Introduction
2.1
Problem Formulation
Bread is a staple food consumed globally and plays a central role in the Danish diet.
However, to extend its shelf life and preserve quality, commercial bread products—such
as Krydderboller—commonly rely on synthetic chemical preservatives including sodium
metabisulfite (E223), calcium propionate (E282), potassium sorbate (E202), and azodi-
carbonamide (ADA) [1]. Emerging evidence has linked these additives to adverse health
effects such as allergic reactions, endocrine disruption, behavioral disorders, and potential
carcinogenicity [2]. These concerns have prompted growing consumer demand for clean-
label alternatives and regulatory pressure within the European Union to reduce synthetic
additive usage.
At the same time, food producers face significant technical challenges: microbial
contamination, staling, and moisture loss remain critical obstacles to shelf-life extension
in clean-label bread [3]. Specifically, Krydderboller, a popular spiced bread, must maintain
sensory quality and safety without relying on conventional preservatives.
This study seeks to address these dual concerns by exploring natural antimicrobial
agents—such as essential oils and plant extracts—as potential preservatives, alongside
the use of chia seed gel to enhance moisture retention and slow staling. The goal is to
develop a scientifically validated, sustainable, and consumer-acceptable bread formulation
that eliminates harmful additives while maintaining product quality and safety.
2.2
Research Questions
• How do natural antimicrobial agents affect pathogen growth and shelf life compared
to traditional preservatives in the Krydderboller bread?
• How does chia seed gel improve moisture retention and reduce staleness in Kryd-
derboller bread?
• How does increasing salt content impact microbial growth and health risks related
to Krydderboller bread consumption?
• What microbial risks do natural preservatives pose compared to chemical preserva-
tives in Krydderboller bread?

2.3
Aim and Objective
The aim of this research is to develop a healthier, safer, and more sustainable bread
formulation by replacing synthetic preservatives with natural alternatives, while main-
taining product quality and extending shelf life. This study focuses specifically on the
Danish spiced bread Krydderboller, which traditionally contains multiple chemical addi-
tives such as lecithins (E322), mono- and diglycerides of fatty acids (E471), polyglycerol
esters (E475), citric acid (E330), sodium bicarbonate (E500), sodium aluminium silicate
(E550), and pectin (E440). These compounds have been associated with various health
concerns, including allergic reactions, endocrine disruption, behavioural disorders, and
potential carcinogenic effects [2]. Given the growing consumer demand for clean-label
products, this research is timely and relevant for identifying safe, natural, and sustain-
able alternatives.
The specific objectives of this study are to:
• Reduce reliance on synthetic preservatives by formulating a clean-label bread that
ensures microbial safety and quality.
• Enhance food safety through the use of natural antimicrobial agents, including
essential oils and plant extracts.
• Extend bread shelf life by incorporating chia seed gel, a natural hydrocolloid, to
retain moisture and delay staling.
• Develop and optimise a clean-label bread formulation that meets consumer expec-
tations and aligns with EU regulatory trends.
• Compare the preservative efficacy of natural alternatives with conventional syn-
thetic additives using microbiological and physicochemical analysis.
By integrating these natural ingredients, this study aims to deliver a practical and inno-
vative solution that enhances food safety, prolongs shelf life, and supports the growing
consumer demand for preservative-free, sustainable bakery products.

• Moisture Retention: Evaluating the impact of chia seed gel incorporation on delay-
ing staleness.
• Salt Content: Investigating the effect of incremental salt level changes on microbial

Chapter 3
Background and Literature Review
This chapter reviews essential literature concerning food ingredients in bread production.
We first examine E-number additives, discussing their functions and potential health im-
pacts. Next, we analyse common oils like rapeseed and palm oil, highlighting their roles
and associated health risks, alongside concerns about excessive sodium. We then explore
natural alternatives: rosemary and oregano essential oils for their antioxidant and antimi-
crobial properties, and chia seeds for their nutritional and functional benefits. Finally,
we contextualise these elements within the formulation of Krydderboller, illustrating the
practical applications and challenges of healthier ingredient choices in everyday baked
goods.
3.1
E-number Additives
The study by Fødevarestyrelsen (2020) [4] investigated the use of food additives (E-
numbers) in bread, examining their technological functions and potential health effects.
Common additives such as preservatives (E200–E282), emulsifiers (E471, E472), and
stabilisers are routinely used to extend shelf life and improve dough stability. The re-
port highlighted that propionates (E280–E282), especially calcium propionate (E282),
are effective in preventing mold but may impair glucose metabolism and increase insulin
resistance, raising concerns about long-term metabolic health. Emulsifiers like E471 were
also associated with disruptions to gut microbiota and increased intestinal permeability,
potentially contributing to inflammation and the development of metabolic disorders such
as obesity and diabetes.
While these additives are permitted under EU food safety regulations, which set strict
maximum limits based on EFSA assessments, the study emphasised the need for ongo-
ing evaluation of their cumulative health impacts, particularly for at-risk populations.
Fødevarestyrelsen recommends limiting frequent consumption of additive-rich products
and encourages the use of natural alternatives where possible. The report also stresses the
importance of transparent labelling to enable informed consumer choices, aligning with
Denmark’s broader commitment to public health and evidence-based food regulation.
Health Considerations in E-numbers
Food additives labelled with E-numbers are commonly used to enhance the appearance,
texture, and shelf life of processed foods. While most are considered safe within reg-
ulated limits, concerns arise regarding the potential health effects of long-term, daily

consumption. Below is a detailed analysis of selected E-numbers, outlining their func-
tion, associated health risks, symptoms of overconsumption, and regulatory guidelines.
E322 – Lecithin
Lecithin is primarily used as an emulsifier in products like chocolate and dressings, helping
to blend oil and water. It is naturally found in egg yolks and soybeans. Regarding health
risks, excessive daily intake may cause gastrointestinal discomfort, including bloating,
diarrhoea, and stomach cramps [5]. However, no specific Acceptable Daily Intake (ADI)
has been established, as lecithin is generally recognised as safe (GRAS) [6].
E471 – Mono- and Diglycerides of Fatty Acids
E471 acts as an emulsifier in baked goods, margarine, and dairy substitutes, preventing
ingredient separation. Long-term consumption is linked to potential cardiovascular risks,
as a study published in The BMJ indicates a correlation between high emulsifier intake
and an increased risk of heart disease [7]. Additionally, E471 may disrupt gut micro-
biota, leading to digestive discomfort [8]. Despite this, no specific ADI has been set, but
emulsifiers should be consumed in moderation.
E475 – Polyglycerol Esters of Fatty Acids
E475 is an emulsifier and stabiliser used in processed foods like whipped toppings and
desserts. Excessive consumption may lead to digestive issues such as bloating and di-
arrhoea [9]. The compound is approved for use in food, but regulatory bodies suggest
limiting intake to prevent gastrointestinal distress.
E211 – Sodium Benzoate
Sodium benzoate is a preservative used to prevent microbial growth in acidic foods and
beverages. Health risks include allergic reactions, particularly in individuals with asthma,
as well as a potential link to hyperactivity in children [10]. The acceptable daily intake
is up to 5 mg/kg body weight [11].
E330 – Citric Acid
Citric acid functions as an acidity regulator and flavour enhancer in processed foods
and beverages. Frequent consumption may contribute to dental erosion due to its acidic
nature, leading to increased tooth sensitivity [12]. Additionally, high intake may cause
gastrointestinal irritation, exacerbating conditions like acid reflux. While it is generally
recognised as safe, moderation is recommended.
E500 – Sodium Carbonates
Sodium carbonate is a leavening agent and acidity regulator in baking products. Exces-
sive consumption could lead to metabolic alkalosis, a condition characterised by nausea,
muscle spasms, and weakness due to an imbalance in the body’s acid-base levels [13].
No ADI (Acceptable Daily Intake) has been established, but excessive intake should be
avoided.
E450 – Diphosphates
Diphosphates are used as leavening agents and emulsifiers in processed meats and baked
goods. Overconsumption may lead to an imbalance in calcium metabolism, potentially
causing bone demineralisation and increasing the risk of kidney disease [14]. The ADI
for phosphates is 70 mg/kg body weight [15].
E341 – Calcium Phosphates
Calcium phosphates function as anti-caking agents and nutritional supplements in food
products. High phosphate intake has been associated with kidney strain and an increased

3.2
Rapeseed Oil in Bread Production
Rapeseed oil is widely used in bread production due to its neutral flavour, long shelf life,
and favourable nutritional profile, being rich in monounsaturated (MUFA) and polyun-
saturated fatty acids (PUFA) while low in saturated fatty acids (SFA). These qualities
make it a preferred alternative to oils high in saturated fats.
However, several health considerations arise with long-term, daily consumption. To
improve oxidative stability, rapeseed oil is sometimes hydrogenated, a process that can
generate trans fats. These compounds are associated with increased cardiovascular dis-
ease (CVD) risk by elevating LDL and lowering HDL cholesterol levels [18]. Unrefined
rapeseed oil is also high in omega-3 fatty acids, which are beneficial but prone to oxidation
under high heat, potentially contributing to inflammation and oxidative stress [19].More-
over, in its crude form, the oil contains glucosinolates, which may interfere with iodine
absorption and impair thyroid function, though these compounds are typically removed
during refining [20]. Additionally, excessive consumption of vegetable oils, including rape-
seed oil, may disrupt gut microbiota and increase the risk of metabolic disorders such as
insulin resistance and obesity [21].
From a regulatory perspective, the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) considers
rapeseed oil safe when consumed within reasonable limits. The World Health Organisa-
tion (WHO) advises that trans fat intake should not exceed 1% of total daily energy to
reduce CVD risk [22]. While refined rapeseed oil typically contains minimal trans fats,
highly processed products using hydrogenated forms may still contribute to excessive
intake.
3.3
Palm Oil and Salt in Bread Formulations
Both palm oil and salt, commonly used in bread formulations, contribute significantly
to dietary risk factors associated with non-communicable diseases. Palm oil is rich in
saturated fats, notably palmitic acid, which is linked to elevated LDL cholesterol levels.
According to the Global Burden of Disease Study 2019 (GBD 2019), high LDL cholesterol
is a major metabolic risk contributing to cardiovascular diseases worldwide.
Similarly, excessive sodium intake—prevalent in processed foods such as bread—is
a primary contributor to hypertension. High systolic blood pressure was identified as
the leading global risk factor for mortality in 2019, accounting for 10.8 million deaths
[23]. Processed foods that contain palm oil frequently also contain high levels of sodium,
compounding the health risks.

GBD 2019 also highlights that both high sodium and saturated fat intake remain
persistent global dietary risks, with limited progress observed in recent years.
While
palm oil is not explicitly isolated in the GBD analysis, its saturated fat content clas-

substitution of palm oil with flaxseed oil, as investigated in this study [24], offers a health-

presented in the following section.
3.4
Palm Oil
Palm oil is commonly used in industrial bread due to its semi-solid consistency, oxidative
stability, and affordability. It helps provide a desirable texture and extends shelf life.
However, Palm oil’s use in bread production raises significant health concerns due to its
high saturated fat content. From a health perspective, its high saturated fat content and
processing methods pose risks. Palm oil consumption has been consistently linked to
increased LDL cholesterol levels (”bad” cholesterol), a key factor in atherosclerosis and
cardiovascular disease [25, 26, 27]. These adverse effects are largely attributed to the high
saturated fat content of palm oil, particularly palmitic acid (44-45% of its composition),
which contributes to cholesterol buildup, plaque formation, and increased risk of heart
attacks and strokes [28, 29, 27]. A meta-analysis showed palm oil increases LDL choles-
terol by 0.24 mmol/L compared to other vegetable oils [26]. The structure of triglycerides
in palm oil, with saturated fatty acids predominantly at the sn-1 and sn-3 positions, also
impacts how fats are digested and absorbed, potentially raising cholesterol levels.

Processing methods for palm oil also present risks. While partial hydrogenation (which
creates harmful trans fats) is less common now, interesterification has largely replaced it,
with unclear long-term health effects [30, 31]. The World Health Organisation (WHO)

recommends limiting trans fat intake to less than 1% of daily energy [32]. Furthermore, a
lack of labelling transparency often prevents consumers from knowing if interesterification
has been used [31]. The widespread use of palm oil in processed foods, including bread,
underscores the importance of reformulating such products to reduce cardiovascular risk
by replacing palm oil with healthier fats rich in unsaturated fatty acids, such as olive,
canola, or sunflower oil [27].
Health Considerations of Long-Term, Daily Consumption
• Increased Cardiovascular Risk: Palm oil is 50% saturated fat, mainly palmitic acid,
which is linked to atherosclerosis and higher LDL cholesterol [33].
• Potential Pro-Inflammatory Effects: Repeated heating in industrial use generates
oxidised lipids that may promote inflammation and endothelial dysfunction [34].
• Gut Microbiome Alterations: High saturated fat intake from palm oil may reduce
gut microbiota diversity, leading to metabolic issues like obesity and insulin resis-
tance [35].
• Carcinogenic Risk of Processing Contaminants: Refined palm oil contains GE and
3-MCPD, which are potential carcinogens at high doses [36].
Regulatory Guidelines & Recommended Intake
• EFSA warns against excessive palm oil intake due to saturated fats and contami-
nants.
• The WHO advises limiting saturated fats to less than 10% of total daily energy, or
20–25 g/day for an average adult [37].
Given these multifaceted challenges, avoiding palm oil in bread production is a health-
ier and more sustainable choice. Instead, using unsaturated vegetable oils can ensure bet-
ter nutritional quality and product safety. Policy and industry shifts toward clean-label,
heart-healthy formulations are crucial, emphasising the urgency of adopting healthier fat
alternatives in commonly consumed products to mitigate long-term public health risks
[32, 27]. In this thesis, we are exploring natural alternatives, e.g., rosemary and oregano
essential oils, which we will discuss in the upcoming sections.
3.5
Essential Oil Residues: Natural Preservatives
In line with sustainable development and the circular economy, research increasingly fo-
cuses on valorizing agro-industrial by-products. A study [38] on oregano (Origanum vul-
gare subsp. hirtum L.) and rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis L.) essential oil distillation
residues explored their potential as natural antimicrobial and antioxidant agents. These
by-products, being devoid of volatile aromatic compounds, offer a viable, sensory-neutral
alternative to synthetic food preservatives.
The findings indicated that both residues exhibited bioactive properties. Rosemary
residues particularly maintained strong antioxidant potential (Total Phenolic Content of
58.0–60.1 mg GAE/g dry weight, comparable to raw material) and enhanced antibacterial
activity, notably against Bacillus subtilis, Bacillus licheniformis, Bacillus cereus, and

Listeria monocytogenes. This sustained activity is likely due to increased antioxidant
capacity post-distillation.
Oregano residues showed weaker post-distillation antimicrobial activity but remained
effective against L. monocytogenes and Staphylococcus aureus at higher concentrations.
Gram-negative bacteria like Escherichia coli and Salmonella enterica ser. Typhimurium
were resistant, attributed to their outer membrane structure limiting hydrophobic agent

These pioneering studies [39, 40], using a simple and cost-effective methodology, high-

antimicrobials and antioxidants for food safety. Industrially, rosemary residues are more
promising as antioxidants, while oregano residues offer selective antimicrobial effects. Let
us examin them in more detail next.
3.6
Rosemary Oil
Rosemary oil is selected for this research due to its strong antioxidant properties, which
play a key role in preserving bread quality and extending shelf life. The diterpenes present
in rosemary, including carnosic acid, carnosol, rosmanol, and epirosmanol, contribute
significantly to its antioxidant activity. According to Munn´e-Bosch and Alegre [41], these
diterpenes owe their antioxidant capacity to the presence of an aromatic ring (C11–C12)
within the catechol group and the conjugation of the three basic rings in their structure.
By doing so, the electron charge is effectively delocalized, which stabilises free radicals
formed during oxidation.
The catechol group is responsible for scavenging radical electrons. Meanwhile, the car-
boxylic group in carnosic acid enhances conjugation in aqueous environments. In slightly
polar environments, such as fats in bread formulations containing flaxseed oil, the lactone
structure offers greater stability. Consequently, lipid oxidation is inhibited, and the fresh-
ness and quality of the bread are maintained. Furthermore, rosemary diterpenes activate
redox-dependent signalling pathways, such as the Nrf2-dependent transcriptional regula-
tion, which supports the antioxidant response. Wijeratne and Cuppett [41] demonstrated
that carnosic acid and carnosol can inhibit lipid peroxidation by 88–100% and 38–89%,
respectively, under oxidative stress. This is particularly relevant in bread production,
where oxidation contributes to staling and spoilage.
By analysing the chemical structure and mechanisms of rosemary diterpenes, it is
possible to confirm their effectiveness in improving shelf life. Specifically, the catechol
group aids in radical scavenging, while the lactone structure provides stability in fat-
based systems.
Therefore, rosemary oil serves as a natural alternative for enhancing
bread quality while reducing the dependence on synthetic preservatives.
For years, refined rosemary extracts have been applied as natural preservatives, par-
ticularly in food products containing animal and/or vegetable fats [41]. In this study,
rosemary extract is incorporated into a bread formulation containing flaxseed oil, where
it helps delay lipid oxidation. As a result, the quality of the final product is preserved,
and shelf life is extended [41].
In terms of food applications, rosemary has been successfully tested in different ma-
trices. For instance, it has been used to reduce carotenoid color loss and prevent lipid
oxidation in oils [41] and meat products [41]. Studies on meat products have shown that
specific concentrations of rosemary extracts lead to positive sensory outcomes while min-

3.7
Oregano Essential Oil in Bread Preservation
The study “Sliced Bread Preservation through Oregano Essential Oil-Containing Sachet”
[44] evaluated oregano essential oil (OEO) as a natural means to extend the shelf life of
sliced bread by inhibiting yeast and mold growth. Sachets infused with OEO were inserted
into bread packaging and stored under conditions conducive to microbial spoilage. Results
demonstrated that bread treated with OEO consistently showed significantly lower mold
and yeast counts compared to the control group. Sensory evaluations further indicated
that OEO-treated bread maintained better texture, taste, and aroma throughout the
storage period.
These antimicrobial effects are attributed to OEO’s phenolic compounds, particularly
carvacrol and thymol, which disrupt microbial cell membranes and lead to cellular leak-
age and death [45, 46]. In addition to its antimicrobial activity, OEO possesses strong
antioxidant properties that help preserve the quality of bread by reducing lipid oxidation
[47].
Chemical Composition and Mode of Action
Oregano essential oil, primarily derived from Origanum vulgare and Lippia berlandieri
(Mexican oregano), is rich in monoterpenes such as carvacrol and thymol [48, 49]. These
phenolic compounds are hydrophobic, allowing them to penetrate microbial cell mem-
branes, increase membrane permeability, and cause leakage of essential cellular contents
[45]. This mechanism underpins their broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity, which in-
cludes action against bacteria, fungi, and even parasites [46].
Furthermore, the antioxidant properties of carvacrol and thymol are crucial in stabi-
lizing unsaturated fats like flaxseed oil, which are prone to oxidation. By neutralizing
free radicals, OEO helps maintain the bread’s freshness and quality over time [47].
Application and Clean-Label Potential
Although OEO has low water solubility, it can be incorporated effectively into bread
through emulsification techniques, ensuring even distribution throughout the dough [47].
The oil’s ability to enhance food safety and shelf life without negatively impacting texture
or flavor makes it especially suitable for clean-label formulations, aligning with growing
consumer demand for natural, plant-based preservatives [48].
Beyond its role in food, oregano oil has demonstrated therapeutic potential, including
antiparasitic activity against diseases such as malaria caused by Plasmodium species [46].
This highlights its value not only in food preservation but also in health promotion.

Additionally, omega-3s help regulate sodium and calcium channels in cardiac tissue, po-

Risk Assessment
This chapter provides a focused risk assessment of ingredients commonly used in indus-
trially produced bread, with particular emphasis on their public health implications in
the Danish context. It concentrates on the risks associated with excessive sodium intake
and cumulative exposure to synthetic food additives—especially E-numbers—frequently
found in products such as Krydderboller. Drawing on data from global health studies
and food safety authorities, this chapter evaluates the dietary burden these ingredients
pose, particularly among children, and explores the benefits of transitioning to safer,
clean-label alternatives like chia seed gel and essential oils. The goal is to inform future
reformulation strategies and policy recommendations that align with EFSA and WHO
guidelines.
4.1
Natural Preservatives in Krydderboller
Bread is a staple food in Denmark, particularly Krydderboller, a popular variety regularly
consumed by both adults and children. However, frequent consumption of certain bread
ingredients, such as sodium and saturated fats from palm oil, poses notable public health
concerns.
These include an increased risk of cardiovascular disease (CVD) [65], high
blood pressure (hypertension), obesity [66], and metabolic syndrome [67].
This assessment draws on data from the Global Burden of Disease (GBD) Studies [68],
the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) [69], and the World Health Organisation
(WHO) [70] to develop a risk profile. It focuses on the impact of sodium and palm oil
intake, while also examining the potential health benefits of incorporating alternative
ingredients such as chia seed gel, rosemary oil, and oregano oil.

intake for children, which ranges from 1.2 to 2.0 g, depending on age.
Excessive sodium intake is directly linked to hypertension, which in turn increases the
risk of stroke, heart disease, and other cardiovascular issues. The World Heart Federation

Chia Gel and Essential Oils
Natural alternatives like chia seed gel and essential oils (rosemary and oregano) offer
significant health benefits and functional improvements in bread production, providing a
healthier alternative to traditional ingredients.
Chia gel, derived from chia seeds (Salvia hispanica), is a natural hydrocolloid that
serves a dual purpose: it enhances moisture retention in bread and effectively replaces
saturated fat from palm oil. Studies indicate that chia seeds contribute 2–4 g of fiber per
100 g of bread, which is strongly linked to improved cardiovascular health and reduced
hypertension.
By replacing palm oil with chia gel, bread can significantly reduce its
saturated fat content by up to 30–40% [75], thereby potentially lowering LDL cholesterol
and reducing the risk of CVD [76].
Similarly, rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis) and oregano (Origanum vulgare) essen-
tial oils have demonstrated robust antimicrobial and antioxidant properties. These prop-
erties allow them to effectively inhibit microbial growth and extend bread shelf life natu-
rally. Crucially, unlike synthetic preservatives, these essential oils pose no known health
risks and align well with EFSA recommendations for natural food preservation [77, 78].

Conclusion
The cumulative health risks associated with food additives in Krydderboller—especially
synthetic preservatives like E282 and emulsifiers like E471—highlight a significant, though
often overlooked, public health concern. While individual additives are approved within
ADI limits, chronic exposure, especially in children, poses risks ranging from metabolic
disease to behavioral disorders.
Transitioning to natural alternatives such as chia seed gel, rosemary oil, and oregano
oil offers a path toward safer, clean-label bread production in Denmark. To operationalize
this shift, the Danish government could introduce targeted subsidies or tax incentives
for bakeries and manufacturers adopting natural ingredients. Additionally, establishing a
national “Clean Label” certification program could guide consumer choices and encourage
industry compliance.
Enhanced monitoring could be supported through mandatory additive reporting and
periodic assessments of cumulative exposure, especially among vulnerable groups like
children. These efforts would align Danish food policy with emerging WHO and EFSA
recommendations, ultimately reducing DALYs, hospital burden, and premature mortality
associated with dietary risk factors.

This chapter outlines the experimental approach employed in the development and eval-

palm oil with natural alternatives. This aligns with growing consumer demand for clean-

Oregano and rosemary essential oils are known for their potent antimicrobial and
antioxidant properties, making them promising candidates for clean-label preservation
strategies. Their effectiveness in inhibiting microbial growth without significantly im-
pacting sensory characteristics has been supported in recent studies [86].
Chia seed gel was employed as a functional ingredient and natural hydrocolloid, con-
tributing to the texture and stability of the bread while supporting clean-label formula-
tion principles. Its incorporation in bakery products has been previously demonstrated
to improve moisture retention and nutritional quality [87].
A structured and systematic experimental design was implemented to facilitate de-
tailed microbiological and physicochemical comparisons across multiple formulations un-

were conducted to ensure the findings’ scientific rigour and consumer relevance. This ap-
proach aimed to provide a comprehensive understanding of the potential of these natural
ingredients to meet both safety and quality standards, offering an alternative to conven-
tional food formulations.

5.4
Microbiological Analysis
Microbiological analysis was conducted to assess the effectiveness of natural antimicrobial
agents (oregano and rosemary essential oils), chia seed gel, and chemical preservatives
in controlling microbial growth and extending the shelf life of Krydderboller bread. The
primary goal was to evaluate whether clean-label ingredients could match or exceed the
microbial stability provided by traditional additives. Additionally, the impact of min-
imised salt content in most formulations was considered concerning microbial growth.
Sampling Procedure
Bread samples were analysed on days 0, 3, 5, and 7 post-baking. To minimise contam-
ination, a sterile scalpel was used to aseptically extract samples from the core of each
loaf. Samples were stored at room temperature (22 ± 2 °C) in sterile polyethene bags,
simulating typical domestic storage conditions.

Microbial Tests Conducted
1. Total Viable Count (TVC):
• Evaluates the total number of aerobic mesophilic bacteria present in each sam-
ple.
• 10 g of bread was homogenised with 90 mL of sterile peptone water and serially
diluted.
• 1 mL of each dilution was plated on Plate Count Agar (PCA) in duplicate.
• Incubation was done at 30°C for 48 hours, and results were expressed as log
CFU/g.
2. Yeast and Mold Count:
• Enumerates fungal contamination, particularly spoilage yeasts and molds.
• Samples were plated on Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA) acidified with tartaric
acid.
• Plates were incubated at 25°C for 3–5 days, and fungal colonies were counted
and recorded as CFU/g.
3. Optional: Identification of Spoilage Organisms:
• In samples showing visible or rapid spoilage, microbial colonies may be isolated
for identification.
• Basic techniques such as Gram staining and biochemical assays (e.g., catalase,
oxidase tests) may be employed.
• Special attention was given to Penicillium spp., Aspergillus spp., and Bacillus
spp., common spoilage agents in bakery products.
Data Interpretation
• Microbial counts were expressed in log CFU/g for clarity and ease of cross-sample
comparison.
• Results were compared across all groups and time points to evaluate the relative
antimicrobial effectiveness of essential oils, chia seed gel, and synthetic preservatives.
• A microbial load of less than 105 CFU/g was considered acceptable, in line with
EU food safety shelf-life standards.
Replicates and Controls
• All microbiological tests were performed in triplicate for each sample and time point
to ensure statistical reliability.
• Negative controls (uninoculated media) were used to confirm the sterility of mate-
rials and procedures.
• Due to biosafety limitations, positive controls (deliberately contaminated bread)
were not included in this study.

Discussion
The incorporation of powdered natural additives into the krydderboller dough resulted
in pH values ranging from 5.4 to 5.6 and water activity (aw) levels between 0.940 and
0.960 across all test groups. These parameters fall within the optimal range for microbial
proliferation, particularly for molds and yeasts, when stored at ambient temperature
conditions.
Despite slight variations in initial pH among the treated samples, no measurable an-
timicrobial benefit was observed. All bread variants, including those containing powdered
garlic, oregano, and rosemary, showed visible mold growth in day 5 of storage, mirroring
the spoilage timeline of the untreated control group. These results suggest that the ad-
ditives, at the tested concentration of 4% w/w, did not delay or inhibit spoilage under
the specified storage parameters.
The lack of antimicrobial activity can likely be attributed to two main factors:
• Reduced concentration of active compounds:
The powdered forms con-
tain significantly lower levels of critical bioactive components such as carvacrol
(oregano), rosmarinic acid (rosemary), and allicin (garlic), compared to their essen-
tial oil counterparts.
• Limited compound mobility: Unlike volatile essential oils, powdered additives
cannot diffuse throughout the bread matrix, resulting in insufficient dispersion of
antimicrobial agents necessary for effective pathogen control.
Conclusion
Based on the results of this study, powdered garlic, oregano, and rosemary at a concen-
tration of 4% (w/w) were found to be ineffective in extending the shelf life or inhibiting
microbial spoilage in krydderboller under ambient storage conditions. All treated sam-
ples followed the same spoilage trajectory as the control, with no observable preservation
benefit.
Consequently, the use of powdered botanical additives was excluded from further tri-
als. Future experimental efforts were redirected toward pure essential oils, which possess
higher concentrations of antimicrobial constituents and improved functional efficacy in
food preservation applications in bread.

Answers to Research Questions
From the comprehensive analysis of the experimental findings and the supporting litera-
ture review, the following direct answers to the research questions are established
• How do natural antimicrobial agents affect pathogen growth and shelf
life compared to traditional preservatives in the Krydderboller bread?
From the experimental results, it is demonstrated that natural antimicrobial agents,
specifically rosemary and oregano essential oils, have a significant prohibitory effect on
microbial growth and extend the shelf life of Krydderboller. The efficacy of these agents
is comparable, and in some aspects superior, to the traditional preservative method of
using salt. The rosemary oil treatment was of great significance, as it provided a broad-
spectrum antimicrobial action, strongly inhibiting both visual mold development (onset
delayed to Day 6-7) and the overall microbial load recovered during plating. In a similar
manner, the oregano oil treatment completely inhibited any visible mold growth on the
bread surface for more than seven days. However, its action was more specific, as it was
less effective at controlling the growth of certain bacteria and yeasts in the microbiological
analysis. In comparison, the untreated control sample showed extensive spoilage by Day
4-5. Therefore, the natural antimicrobial agents proved to be highly effective at extending
shelf life beyond the standard.
• How does chia seed gel improve moisture retention and reduce staleness
in Krydderboller bread?
The analysis of the chia seed trial shows that the incorporation of 3% chia seed gel
improves moisture retention and reduces staleness primarily through a physical, rather
than chemical, mechanism. The chia seeds form a hydrocolloidal gel that binds a sig-
nificant amount of water within the bread matrix. This action results in a more stable
46
==PAGE:48==
Project Findings
Chapter 7
internal environment, slowing the process of moisture migration and water loss that leads
to staling. The sensory analysis confirmed that the chia-enriched bread remained moist
and possessed a softer, more elastic crumb for up to five days, whereas the control sample
became firm and dry after three days. This improved water retention also indirectly con-
tributes to preservation by delaying the formation of high-moisture zones on the bread
surface that are favorable for mold colonization, thus extending the mold-free shelf life
by three days.
• How does increasing salt content impact microbial growth and health
risks related to Krydderboller bread consumption?
From the analysis of the S1 (medium salt) and S2 (high salt) samples, it is demon-
strated that increasing salt content has a potent impact on inhibiting microbial growth.
This preservative effect is primarily achieved by reducing the water activity (aw) of
the bread, with the S2 sample (aw ≈0.94) being more effective than the S1 sample
(aw ≈0.95). The lower water activity creates an osmotically stressful environment that
is inhospitable to most spoilage microbes, which resulted in strong visual mold inhibition
and a very low microbial load, particularly for the S2 sample. However, this method of
preservation is associated with significant health risks. As detailed in the risk assessment,
high sodium intake is a primary contributor to hypertension and cardiovascular disease.
Furthermore, the higher salt concentration had a negative sensory impact, resulting in
a noticeably firmer and drier crumb texture. Thus, while effective for preservation, in-
creasing salt content poses a considerable health risk and degrades product quality.
• What microbial risks do natural preservatives pose compared to chemical
preservatives in Krydderboller bread?
The microbial risk posed by natural preservatives is primarily related to the speci-
ficity and concentration of their active compounds. As demonstrated by the oregano
oil sample, a natural agent may be highly effective against one class of microbe (mold)
but less effective against others (bacteria and yeasts). This poses a risk that resistant
microorganisms could still proliferate. Furthermore, if the concentration of the natural
agent is insufficient, its action may be merely microstatic rather than microcidal, mean-
ing it only inhibits growth rather than eliminating the microbes. In contrast, the risk
of traditional chemical preservatives (E-numbers), as analyzed in the thesis, is not one
of microbial failure but of long-term consumer health impacts, including potential al-
lergenicity and metabolic disruption. Therefore, the use of natural preservatives shifts
the risk profile from a toxicological consumer health concern to a microbiological control
challenge that must be managed through careful formulation, validation of efficacy, and
good manufacturing practices.
7.2
Limitations
The primary limitations of this study are related to the scope of the experimental design
and the analytical methods employed. Firstly, all trials were conducted on a laboratory
scale, which may not fully represent the conditions and challenges of commercial-scale
bakery production. The sourcing of ingredients was also a limitation, particularly during
the preliminary trials where the use of impure, commercially available additives made it
47
==PAGE:49==
Project Findings
Chapter 7
difficult to control the exact concentration of active compounds and thus to accurately
assess their antimicrobial efficacy. Regarding the microbiological analysis, the assessment
was based on total viable counts and visual identification of mold colonies. The study
did not extend to the specific molecular identification of microbial species. The absence
of such an analysis means that the full spectrum of spoilage microorganisms and the
specific targets of the natural preservatives were not definitively identified. Furthermore,
a significant discrepancy was noted for the E (Silk gel) sample, where the high visible
contamination was not reflected in the plating results, a finding that is considered to be
a result of sampling error and thus a limitation of the experimental execution. Finally,
while the methodology outlined a plan for sensory evaluation, this study primarily focused
on the physicochemical and microbiological aspects of preservation. A comprehensive
quantitative analysis of the bread’s texture using instrumental methods, such as a texture
profile analyser, was not conducted. This limits the objective characterization of the
effects of the additives on staling and crumb structure.
7.3
Future Work
From the conclusions and limitations of the current study, several avenues for future
research are established. It is recommended that a pilot-scale production trial is con-
ducted to validate the efficacy of the most promising natural preservatives—rosemary
oil, oregano oil, and chia seed gel—in a setting that more closely resembles commercial
manufacturing. For future microbiological work, it is of great significance to employ ad-
vanced molecular techniques, such as 16S rRNA and ITS gene sequencing, to precisely
identify the bacterial and fungal species that are inhibited by the natural agents and
those that show resistance.
This would provide a much deeper understanding of the
antimicrobial mechanisms. Furthermore, a dose-response study should be designed to
investigate the effects of varying the concentrations of rosemary oil, oregano oil, and
chia seeds. The objective would be to determine the minimum inhibitory concentration
(MIC) required for effective preservation, which would allow for the optimization of the
formulation for both safety and cost-effectiveness. Moving on, future work should include
a comprehensive sensory analysis performed by a trained panel, as well as a larger con-
sumer acceptance study. This is necessary to fully evaluate the impact of the natural
additives on the flavor, texture, aroma, and overall consumer acceptability of the final
product. To support this, an instrumental texture analysis should be performed to ob-
jectively quantify changes in crumb hardness, springiness, and chewiness over the storage
period. Finally, it would be beneficial to investigate the potential for synergistic effects
by combining different natural agents (e.g., rosemary oil with chia seed gel). It is possible
that a combination of preservatives could provide a broader spectrum of antimicrobial
activity at lower individual concentrations, further enhancing the safety and quality of
the final clean-label product.

half the panel. Feedback included:
”The smell is too much like a kitchen spice rack, not a bakery,”
”It tastes like something you put on bread, not the bread itself,”
”I was expecting a ’bolle’ and this tastes like thyme. It was a shock.”
• Lack of Sweetness (for others):
”It’s a bun, it should have a little sweetness. This was just... bland and herby.”
• Bitterness:
A few panellists noted a slightly bitter aftertaste, likely from the high concentration
of essential oils.
Flavour and Aroma Descriptors:
• Positive: ”Rosemary,” ”savoury,” ”wholesome,” ”earthy,” ”fresh herbs,” ”Mediter-
ranean”
• Negative: ”Too strong,” ”peppery,” ”medicinal,” ”like a pizza,” ”bitter,” ”not like
bread”
Overall Conclusion of Sensory Analysis
The consumer panel confirms that the ”Ultimate Health” prototype is a technical success
in terms of texture but a commercial challenge in terms of market positioning. The use of
chia gel and a high-hydration dough has produced an exceptionally soft and moist crumb
that significantly exceeds consumer expectations for a whole-wheat, no-oil product.
However, the flavour profile is the critical limiting factor.
The assertive, savoury
notes from the rosemary and oregano essential oils, combined with the complete absence
of sugar, fundamentally misalign the product with the established sensory expectations
for a Danish bolle. While it has enthusiastic supporters among a niche group who value
savoury, ”clean-tasting” health foods, it alienates the mainstream consumer.
Recommendation
The prototype should not be marketed as a traditional Krydderbolle. Its success hinges on
repositioning it as a speciality savoury roll or artisanal health bread. Future development
should focus on:
• Optimising the essential oil concentration to soften the flavour profile.
• Creating marketing materials that frame it as an accompaniment for soups, salads,
and cheeses, thereby correctly setting consumer expectations.
