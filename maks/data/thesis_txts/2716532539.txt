Abstract
In this study, an IDA ICE model is built to represent a typical Danish multi-family dwelling.
The aim of the study is to assess the impact of uncertain input parameters and radiator
retrofitting on the operating temperatures and energy consumption of the heating system.
Measured data is used to calculate the minimal operating temperatures of the building
with uniform heating. This heating curve came out 20 °C below the heating curve that
was initially used in the building. This indicates the heating system could be operated at
lower temperatures.
Next, a building simulation model is built in IDA IDE. This model is validated with exper-
imental data from winter 2021-2022. Initially, the model consumed less energy than the
building case. After some modifications to the model, similar energy performance and
operating temperatures were achieved.
To examine the impact of different parameter combinations on the operating tempera-
tures and energy consumption of the building, a parametric analysis was performed using
the Variant Manager in IDA ICE. The investigated parameters were: door openings, uni-
formity of temperature setpoint, airtightness and internal gains. The results pointed out
that the heating curve could be lowered with 10 °C if a uniform temperature setpoint was
implemented and all the internal doors were open, compared to a scenario with a non-
uniform temperature setpoint and closed doors. This was, however, only possible when
the infiltration rate was kept at 2 l/s*m2. In a more leaky building (4 L/s*m2), the examined
heating curves could not ensure thermal comfort, meaning that a higher heating curve was
necessary. Other parameter combinations did not result in a possibility to substantially
lowering the heating curve.
A combination of a non-uniform temperature setpoint and open doors was the only pa-
rameter combination with major influence on the return temperature, raising the return
temperature with approx. 5 °C at 0 °C outdoors.
Finally, two different retrofit strategies were tested to lower the heating curve by changing
up to 5 radiators. The final result showed that the minimal heating curve could be lowered
by 2.5 °C by changing the 3 most critical radiators and by 7.5 °C by changing the 5 most
critical radiators.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Denmark has an ambitious climate policy. Through the Danish Climate Act, it targets to
cut carbon emissions by 70% in 2030 and achieve carbon neutrality by 2050, from 1990
levels [1]. The district heating sector plays a major role in this. Approximately two thirds of
Danish households are connected to district heating networks [2]. At the moment, about
64% of the delivered heat is produced by renewable energy sources and the goal is to
increase this share to 90% in 2030 [3, 4].
To increase this share, the district heating network is currently transitioning from the 3rd
to the 4th generation, phasing out fossil fuel heat generation. This transition consists
of two parts, reorganisation of the energy supply to more renewables and optimization
on the consumer side for a higher generation efficiency. As the generation efficiency of
sustainable sources increases with lower supply and return temperatures, major energy
saving can be achieved by optimizing the consumer-side for lower temperatures.
On building level, heating systems are designed for conditions that rarely occur: an out-
side temperature of -12 degC without internal and solar gains. Many older multi-family
dwellings were retrofitted in the last 20 years, improving insulation to reduce heat loss.
This gives the heating system overcapacity that can be used to lower the heating curve
of the building.
1.2
Problem description
There are different factors that can prevent low-temperature operation of radiator heating
systems:
• The system can have underdimensioned radiators
• There can be inefficient control in the central control station,
• There can be inefficient control by the end-users
Rooms with underdimensioned radiators will be the first rooms where thermal comfort
can not be achieved when the heating curve gets lowered. Hence, these critical radiators
limit the potential for lower supply temperature in the system. Identifying and replacing
these radiators can be a cost-efficient way to facilitate low-temperature operation. It is not
known by how much this retrofit measure can lower the overall operating temperatures of
a typical Danish multi-family dwelling.
Inefficient control in the DH-substation can also obstruct lower supply temperatures. Usu-
ally, the heating curve of the building is set at a temperature level that eliminate the risk
of thermal discomfort. In case of efficient end-user control, the heating curve can usu-
ally be lowered without causing thermal discomfort. However, a common practise is that
residents close some of their radiators hoping to save energy. As a result, the used radi-
ators can’t reach their setpoint temperature because there is extra heat flow towards the
unheated rooms. The used radiators maintain a high flow which results in a high return
temperature.

Aim of the project
The aim of the project is to investigate the influence of uncertain input parameters and
the impact of increased radiator capacity on the minimum operating temperatures and
energy consumption of a multi family dwelling, while maintaining thermal comfort. More
specifically, the following parameters and measures are investigated:
• Radiator capacity - replacing critical radiators
• Radiator usage - uniformity of TRV-settings
• Internal gains
• Building airtightness
• Door openings
Not only will these parameters be tested individually, but also combinations of parameter
changes will be investigated. The parameter changes will be tested with different heating
curves and the resulting thermal (dis)comfort and energy consumption will be examined.
The hypothesis of this thesis is that the operating temperatures of DH-connected multi-
family dwellings can be lowered substantially by replacing critical radiators, while main-
taining thermal comfort in the apartments. This hypothesis will be tested with simulation
models and measurements.
The first objective is to make a simulation model of a typical Danish multi family dwelling
and validate this model with experimental data from winter 2021-2022.
The second objective is to evaluate the effect of uncertain parameters on the thermal
comfort and energy consumption using different heating curves.
The third objective is to investigate the possibility to lower the heating curve by changing
critical radiators.
The work can be subdivided in the following research questions:
• What is the current heating curve of the building and what temperature interventions
have been made?
• What are the minimized operating temperatures of the building considering uniform
heating?
• What is the location of the critical radiators?
• What is the degree of criticality of these radiators?
• How much can the heating curve be lowered by retrofitting selected radiators?

Literature review
There are several recent studies that investigate low-temperature operation strategies of
radiators connected to DH-networks
Østergaard, Tunzi, and Svendsen [5] Investigated 7 single-family dwellings and 3 apart-
ment buildings with low district heating return temperatures. It was found that the single-
family dwellings had return tempertures between 25-30 °C, which is the target in 4th gen-
eration district heating networks. However, return temperatures in the range of 30-40
°C range were found at the best functioning multi-family dwellings. This suggest a re-
turn temperature target of 30-40 °C could be an initial ambition for existing multi-family
dwellings.
Lauenburg and Wollerstrand [6] described an optimized adaptive control method to reduce
the return temperature of DH-connected radiator heating systems. The control method
achieved an additional cooling of the district heating water of 2 °C.
Jangsten et al. [7] did a survey of 109 radiator systems connected to Gothenborg’s district
heating network. Among other things, the result showed that radiator systems with a large
heat transfer area had the largest potential to operate with lower temperatures.
Østergaard et al. [8] describes a method that uses data from digital HCA’s to locate prob-
lematic radiators with a high return temperature. It shows that this method can be used
to indicate which radiators are problematic and result in higher return temperatures.
Benakopoulos et al. [9] investigated two strategies for low-temperature operation of a
radiator system without pre-setting in a multi-family dwelling in Denmark. According to the
results, high flow operation combined with a low supply temperature proved to be the best
strategy because this ensures thermal comfort when there are some problematic TRV’s
in the system. A low flow- high supply temperature operation resulted in a high return
temperature because of the probelmatic TRV’s. Both strategies investigated a system
with a poor hydraulic balance.
Benakopoulos et al. [10] investigated a strategy that uses data from HCA’s to stimulate
the use of all the radiators in an apartment. It showed that hydraulic balance in the ris-
ers is needed to lower the operating temperatures. Using model calculations, an energy
weighted average supply and return temperature of 44 and 30 °C respectively could be
achieved in a typical Danish multi-family dwelling from 1970.
In an ongoing study, Benakopoulos et al. uses digital HCA data to identify the critical
apartments in an apartment block and uses these apartments to calculate the minimum
achievable supply temperature, ensuring thermal comfort. His method to determine the
minimum operating temperatures with uniform heating will be used later on in this study.

4
Case study
The case for this investigation is building block 9 of the Rormosen apartments, located in
Roskilde, Denmark. The buildings are constructed in 1973. Block 9 has 3 floors with a
total of 26 apartments. The building is connected to the district heating network for space
heating and DHW supply. The size of the apartments varies from 30m2 studio’s to 121m2
multi-bedroom apartments.
4.1
Building characteristics
Original construction
Building construction in Denmark was for a large part standardized in the 1970s. Being
build in 1973, the building matches with building type 5 from Danske Bygningsmodeller
[11]. This building type has load-bearing transverse partitions and walls and non-load-
bearing longitudinal walls. The load bearing exterior walls are build with pre-fabricated
concrete sandwich panels, consisting of a concrete outer and inner layer with insulation
in between. It is assumed the thermal transmittance of the original construction is in
accordance with the building regulations from that time, the BR72[12].
Retrofit
The building was renovated in 2004. The roof construction was removed and replaced
with a pitched roof with insulation, a new brick wall with insulation was constructed in front
of the original sandwich panel walls and the longitudinal walls received new aluminium
cladding plates with insulation. The balconies where closed of with a glass facade to
increase the indoor space and the original windows were replaced 2-pane energy glazing.

Heating system
The building is connected to the DH-network. The technical room is located in the base-
ment of the building. The piping and most of the radiators are original from 1973. Some
of the radiators are later changed for newer ones. In figure 4.1, the DHW tank with distri-

Layout
Figure 4.2 shows the radiator plan and piping layout of the ground floor around one stair-
case of building block 9. The principle is copied at the other two staircases. It consists
of a two-pipe system with the pipes installed under the floor. It’s quite common for older
buildings to have a poor hydraulic balance. This system, however, designed in a way that
the sum of the return and supply pipe length to one radiator is equal on all the radiators on
a floor. This creates hydraulic balance without the need of balancing valves or dynamic
radiator valves. The only difference in pressure drop is caused by the height difference
of the floors.

Control methods
Every radiator is fitted with a TRV with pre-setting to compensate the height difference.
Documentation of the system design shows a pre-setting of F=5.0 for every bathroom and
toilet. Other rooms on the ground- and first floor are set with F=5.5 and other rooms on
the top floor are set with F=5.75. This shows the maximum flow to the top floor is slightly
higher than the other two floors.
The circulation pump controls the mass flow that is circulated in the system. The system
is set up with a Grundfos Magna 3 in the proportional pressure control setting, meaning
the pump increases the pressure head proportionally with an increasing heat demand.

5.1
Minimized heating curve
First, the minimized supply and return temperature curves will be calculated assuming uni-
form heating. This will be done according to the method demonstrated by Benakopoulos
et al. [10]. These curves will represent the theoretically minimal operating temperatures
of the building if uniform heating is assumed from the current capacity.
For this calculation, a one month reference period is selected from 12-3-2022 till 13-4-
2022. The temperature difference between each hourly average outdoor temperature
between -12 °C and 12 °C and the indoor temperature is calculated in the reference period
with equation 5.1:
∆T(T) = Tin −T
(5.1)
where Tin is the indoor temperature and T is the hourly average outdoor temperature
according to the temperature sensor on the building. The heating degree hours for every
outdoor temperature between -12 and 12 °C is calculated with equation 5.2
HDH(T) = (T) ∗nhours(T)
(5.2)
where nhours (T) is the amount of hours in the reference period that each outdoor tem-
perature between -12 and 12 °C occurs. The heat loss coefficient L can be calculated
with the following equation 5.3:
L = Eheatloss/HDH
(5.3)
where Eheatloss is the total heat loss of the reference period and HDH is the total amount
of heating degree hours in the reference period. With the HLC (L), the heat loss of the
building for every temperature between -12 and 12 °C can be calculated. The needed
dT between the supply and return temperature for every outdoor temperature can be
calculated with equation 5.4:
(T) = Eheatloss/m ∗cp
(5.4)
where Eheatloss is the outdoor temperature specific heat loss of the building, m is a
constant circulation mass flow and cp is the specific heat of water. The circulation flow
is the observed maximum mass flow of the system of 1.39 kg/s. The minimized supply
temperature curve will be based on a constant circulation flow of this maximum.
The needed logarithmic mean temperature difference LMTD(T) can be derived from the
logaritic mean temperature difference under design conditions with equation 5.5:
LMTD(T) = LMTD0 ∗Q(T)/Qtot
(5.5)
where LMTD0 is the logaritmic mean temperature difference under design conditions
(90/70/20), Q(T) is the outdoor temperature dependent heat loss and Qtot is the total

Critical radiator
The critical radiators are identified by calculating the capacity per unit of heat loss of ev-
ery room. The radiator with the highest relative heat loss is considered the most critical.
The heat loss calculation is based on U-values from table 4.1 in the case study. An in-
door temperature of 23 °C, outdoor temperature of -10 °C and a temperature of 15 °C in
the basement are assumed. This steady state calculation is highly simplified. Heat loss
through windows is neglected and infiltration and ventilation heat loss is not taken into
account. However, it will give an indication if there are critical radiator and where they are
located in the building.
Later in the report, simulation results will give a more accurate overview of the critical
radiators.

Heating curve
The heating curve is set to match the lineair trendline of the supply temperatures mea-
sured during correction period 10, visible in figure 5.2. During this correction period, no
complaints have been received of the residents, so it can be assumed that there was no
thermal discomfort. Also, this curve had enough data point over a large range of outdoor
temperatures.
Figure 5.2: Heating curve input for initial model
Mechanical ventilation
The ventilation system is return air only from the kitchens, bathrooms and the single toi-
let on the second floor. The default air flow of IDA ICE of 2 m/s*m2 is used. This gives
approximately 82 m3/h in a kitchen and 35 m3/h in a bathroom.
Airtightness
The air tightness of older buildings can vary a lot and is difficult to estimate without a
blower door test. Estimates show a infiltration rate between 2 and 6 L/s*m2 (ext. at 50pa)
in renovated Danish single family houses[14]. It is assumed, however, that renovated
multi-family dwellings of this type are relatively air tight. Because of this, an infiltration
rate of 2 L/s*m2 is used in the initial model. The balcony doors are expected to be extra
leaky due to the instalment of energy glazing in the old door frame. For these doors, the
leak area is increased from 0.01 m2 to 0.03 m2.
Openings and indoor climate
All the windows and doors are kept closed in the initial model. It is known that the balcony
has openable windows, but these are assumed to be closed during the winter season.
Indoor temperature measurements have shown that the indoor temperature in the living
rooms is high in Rormosen. Typically, Danish residents prefer their bedrooms to be colder
than other rooms. The heating setpoint is therefore set to 19 °C in the bedroooms and 23
°C in the other heated rooms.

Climate data
To validate the initial model, climate data is retrieved from the DMI (Danish Meteorological
Institute)[16]. A Jupiter Notebook script [17] is used to import the individual data files for
temperature and wind and solar radiation from the weather stations. Temperature and
wind is imported from weather station Roskilde Airport and solar radiation from Tessebølle.
Next, the climate data is merged in excel. To simplify simulation, the last 6 months of 2021
and the first 6 months of 2022 are merged into one file. This makes that the entire winter
2021-2022 a be put into one simulation. Finally, the excel file is converted to an epw.
file using Elements. For the parametric analysis and radiator analysis, the climate file is
changes to DRY 2001-2010 (Design Reference Year) [18] to eliminate atypical weather
conditions.

The model design makes that maximum pump flow and radiator capacity can not be ad-

Discussion and recommendations
Hydraulic balance
It is known that many older building have poor hydraulic balance. Because of the piping
layout of the building, good hydraulic balance is assumed and no further hydraulic calcu-
lations have been performed. It is not known if this heating system design is typical for
the building type and what other (poorly balanced) systems are used. Hydraulic inbalance
would have major impact on the location of a critical radiator and should be addressed
before changing radiators (e.g. riser balancing valves or dynamic valves).
Thermal discomfort
Based on the standard design conditions of the EN-16798, it is predicted that thermal
discomfort occurs when the operational temperature drops below 20 °C. These standard
design conditions assume a metabolic rate of 1.2 and a clo-value of 1. In a building with
elderly, however, a metabolic rate of 1 would have been more in place. Also a clo-value of
0.8 would have been more realistic. These two parameter changes would put the category
II limit at an operational temperature of approx. 22.5 °C. This would result in more hours
with unmet heating and a higher needed heating curve.
Wind compensation
As discussed in chapter 5 section 3, some hours of thermal discomfort have been deemed
acceptable because high wind peaks are not included in the heating curve of the model.
This is something that should have been analysed more thorough. Considering an amount
of hours acceptable might be a too simple way to deal with this issue. In future work, this
is something that could be implemented in simulation models.
Airtightness
As discussed in chapter 6 section 3.1, there is a suspicion that the west facade is very
leaky. It would have been interesting to investigate more heating curves with this leaky
facade, to find the minimal heating curve of the building in it’s current state.
End-user control
In the parametric analysis, two TRV setting scenario’s have been examined. One with the
same setting on every radiator and the other with a lowered setpoint in the bedrooms. In
a real building, there would be more variation in TRV setings, also resulting in a net heat
transfer between different apartments. It would have been interesting to include this in
the analysis.
Heating curve selection
Most of the models examined in the parametric analysis showed either too many high
flow hours and/or too many unmet heating hours. If higher heating curves would have
been examined, the impact of infiltration rate on the operating temperatures and energy
consumption would have been much clearer.

Conclusion
In this report, the impact of uncertain input parameters is tested on the energy consump-
tion and operating temperatures of a multi-family dwelling. In addition, a method is tested
where critical radiators were identified and retrofitted to lower the operating temperatures.
Results have shown that by retrofitting the 5 most critical radiators, the heating curve can
be lowered by 7.5 °C. Changing the 3 most critical radiators can lower the heating curve
by 2.5 °C.
However, an input parameter analysis has shown that these operating temperatures are
highly dependent on some input parameters. It was found that that with the current radia-
tor setup, a combination of a uniform heating setpoint and open internal doors could lower
the heating curve with 10 °C, compared to a scenario with a non-uniform temperature set-
point and closed internal doors. A combination of non-uniform temperature setpoint and
closed doors was the only parameter combination with major impact on the return tem-
perature. A return temperature increase of approximately 5 to 10 °C could be observed
compared to other parameter combinations between outdoor temperatures of 0 and -12
°C respectively. With the examined heating curves, thermal comfort could not be achieved
when the airtightness of the building was increased from 2 l/s*m2 to 4 l/2*m2, meaning
this would need a higher heating curve.
Despite the fact that many parameter combinations have been investigated, realistic end-
user control with more variation in TRV-settings has not been simulated. Also, the results
were based on a hydraulically balanced system, which is not always the case on older
buildings.
