Abstract  
Environmental noise has an impact on the public health and need to be considered when cre-
ating train infrastructure to establish the most optimal conditions for the surrounding areas. 
Consequently, this study investigates a new method to assist the decision-making of the design 
of track constructions of an expansion of the metro in Copenhagen in terms of the least possi-
ble noise impact with the use of simulation modelling.  
With a literature review that contains relevant acoustic theories and existing research and a 
simulation modelling, this study creates a new method to simulate the rolling noise of two dif-
ferent track constructions in two separate simplified models. To verify, that the models were 
reliable, two models based on two previous studies were created and compared with the actual 
results from the studies as a start. The data for the validation models were used to create the 
simulation of the two track construction designs. The comparison of the models suggests that 
the slab track (design 1) has a lower noise impact than the ballast track (design 2). Yet, the 
models require more data than was available. Despite this, the method shows potential to be 
used for simple approximations of rolling noise with the correct data available. In addition, fu-
ture research can be conducted to strengthen the robustness of the models. 
Keywords 
Environmental acoustics, noise, rolling noise, force spectrum, finite element method (FEM), 
boundary element method (BEM), sound pressure level (SPL), force spectrum, frequency re-
sponse function, contact patch filter, roughness, simulation modelling, COMSOL Multiphysics, 
metro.

1.0 Introduction 
Environmental noise has an impact on public health in Europe. Noise is an im-
portant cause of ill health and is characterized as unwanted sounds which come 
from different sources such as roads, railways, airplanes, wind turbines, and 
more. The World Health Organization has evaluated noise as the second highest 
environmental health problem behind air pollution caused by fine particulate 
matter [1]. Moreover, is it determined by the European Environment Agency that 
long-term noise exposure can lead to cardiovascular diseases, impaired cognitive 
learning in children, or cause sleep disorders due to the nuisance of noise. Noise 
caused by traffic or trains is related to feelings of displeasure, discomfort, and 
annoyance [2].   
Railways are an important mode of transportation that connects cities and coun-
tries. If railway transportation is compared to road transportation, then railways 
are often associated with lower levels of air pollution emission and reduced traffic 
congestion. However, railways are contributing to an increase in noise pollution 
and vibration. Furthermore, railway transportation uses less energy per person 
per kilometer than road transport [3]. The noise generated from rails depends on 
the type of train, track type, driving speed and driving mode. Common acoustic 
emission mechanisms are rolling noise, squeal noise and impact noise and they 
are important emissions for any kind of wheel. Furthermore, the noise levels de-
pend on the track and wheel type, the condition of the brake system, the type of 
wagon coupling mode and the vertical load.   
Countries within and outside of Western Europe focus on the technical state of 
the vehicle by investigating its noise. Reducing the noise at the source rather than 
in-stalling trackside barriers is more attractive. This focus led to an attention on 
the noise generation processes such as the source of noise detection, noise iso-
lation and suppression, etc. [3].

1.1 Background 
In connection with the expansion of metro line M4 in Nordhavn in Copenhagen, which is being 
carried out by the Copenhagen Metro (Metroselskabet I/S), the noise impact must be consid-
ered. Nordhavn is one of the largest urban development projects in the entire Nordic region. 
When the area is fully developed, the total number of inhabitants will reach 40,000 and there 
will be jobs for another 40,000 [27]. As mentioned, unwanted noise is a significant problem. It 
is important to create as optimal conditions as possible by reducing the amount of noise from 
the train tracks. Consequently, the Copenhagen Metro are very aware of the challenges they 
face from their future extensions.  
1.2 Scope 
The Copenhagen Metro has expressed a desire to find a way for them to evaluate different 
track constructions in terms of noise and vibration propagation on viaducts. The predominant 
sound source when a train is running is the rolling noise, which is particularly evident when a 
train is traveling straight ahead. When modelling a train that runs straight it will mean that other 
noise phenomena are not as apparent. Consequently, the two track constructions will be based 
on straight track sections of the extended metro line. Hence, the purpose of this study is to 
investigate the rolling noise of two simple track constructions in the software COMSOL Mul-
tiphysics to strengthen the decision-making in the early design phase. The unwanted noise 
needs to be estimated earlier to avoid or accommodate the potential noise challenges.  
It is imagined that the two different track constructions are located on the extension of the M4 
metro, see potential location in Figure 1.

1.3 Objectives 
The study aims to assist the early decision-making when designing track constructions by in-
cluding environmental acoustics considerations in a new way with the use of simulation

modelling. A noise prediction excited by rolling noise for the two track constructions will be 
modelled as a simplified system by using the finite element software, COMSOL Multiphysics. 
The modelling of the noise will focus on the predominant rolling noise coming from the wheel 
and rail interaction caused by the roughness of the wheel and rail. The modelling of the inter-
action will be a simplified modelling based on the well-known Track Wheel Interaction Noise

•

•

•

pressure levels. 
• 
Provide a comparison of results in COMSOL of the two track constructions. 
• 
Evaluate the new method of simulation modelling and provide recommendations for 
future work. 
1.4 Research Question 
The research question of the study is: 
How can the rolling noise be included in the early design phase of the track construction for the 
extension of the metro at Nordhavn in Copenhagen, as a mean to make decisions about the 
construction, based on the construction which will result in the least possible noise impact on 
the surroundings using the software COMSOL Multiphysics?  
1.5 Thesis Structure 
The study is divided into five chapters: Introduction, Methodology and Literature Review, Re-
sults and Analysis, Discussion and Conclusion. The first part, the introduction, creates a frame-
work for the purpose and objectives of the thesis. The next part contains the methods used

the new method. Finally, a conclusion is formed.

2.2 Literature Review 
The literature review states relevant theories and existing research that are connected to the 
study, which will be used to make the simulation modelling [31]. As a start, it is important to 
outline how a train is generating noise. Since the study focuses on metro trains on a straight 
line, noise factors such as squealing noise, wheel flats, joints and more will be elaborated. How-
ever, noises aside from rolling noise were not included in the noise approximation of the way-
side noise.  
2.2.1 Railway Noise 
Two main components that contribute to the total noise generated by a train are the mechani-
cal noise and aerodynamic noise. One, or the other, can be the dominating noise depending on 
the running speed of the train [5]. Aerodynamic noises dominate above conventional speeds 
and high-speed trains. The mechanisms connecting the wheel and rail dominate the noise pro-
duction for conventional speeds and still have a significant impact on high-speed trains. How-
ever, at lower speeds up to 60 km/h, traction noise from motors or fan noise predominates 
over the rolling noise for locomotives and motor coaches. Mechanical noises that contribute to 
the total noise are rolling noise, squeal noise, and impact noise [5]. This study focuses entirely 
on rolling noise; nonetheless the other types of noises will be described in short terms to un-
derstand the basics of the mechanical noises.  
2.2.1a Rolling Noise 
Rolling noise happens when wheels on a rail are rolling along the rails. The noise is generated 
by excitation from unevenness or irregularities of the wheel and rail surfaces. This unevenness 
is commonly referred to as roughness.  An illustration of the rolling noise is seen in Figure 7. 
 
 
Figure 7 - Wheel-rail interaction generates noise due to excitement from roughness [4] p. 520 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
16 
 
This roughness on the surfaces forces a relative displacement in the vertical direction, which is 
illustrated in Figure 7, where it is visible to understand the roughness excites the structural 
vibrations of the wheel and rail. The vibrations turn into radiated noise, depending on their 
respective properties, and this noise then propagates to the nearby field. The roughness be-
tween the wheel and rail excites both the vibration modes of the wheel and the waves in the 
rail. Hence, the wheel vibrations are from 1500 Hz and at higher frequencies and the rail itself 
will resonate around 600-1000 Hz. The sleepers are resonating due to vibrations from the rails 
and that happens in the lower frequencies at 400 Hz. Rolling noise includes frequencies from 
100-5000 Hz. [5][10] The human perception of sound is reduced at frequencies below 100 Hz 
and frequencies above 5000 Hz are decreasing rapidly due to the contact filter effect. [10] In 
the frequency spectrum from 100-5000 Hz, a train driving with a speed of 160 km/h has its 
maximum level around 1000 Hz. When the train speed increases to double, the noise levels 
increase by 8-10 dB(A), and the spectral content tends to move forward at higher frequencies. 
However, the relation between roughness and noise is non-linear as an increase in roughness 
results in a disproportional behavior of the noise. 
A representation of the rolling noise and how the excitation can be modelled is illustrated in 
Figure 8.

W [5]

2.2.1b Impact Noise 
Impact noise can happen when there is a change or discontinuity along the railway. This hap-
pens when there are rail joints, dipped welds, points, or crossings. Impact noises can be con-
sidered an extreme form of rolling noise. Furthermore, wheel flats also account for impact 
noise. A wheel flat appears when an area of the wheel tread is ground down. This can happen 
when the brake system is locked up and the wheel will slide along the rail.  The grounding will 
deform the wheel circumference. An illustration of a wheel flat can be seen in Figure 15(a). A 
clock-updated system occurs when poor conditions between the rail and wheel are present and 
usually during autumn [5]. The length of the wheel flat is typically 50 mm and, in some cases, 
the length is up to 100 mm. When a wheel flat has occurred, the rotation and load of the train 
will deform the wheel back to a circular shape due to large impact forces between the rail and 
wheel. The impact forces are created by the larger size of wheel flats which make a discontinuity 
in the circumference of the wheel. It led to an unloading of the wheel and the impact between 
the rail and wheel creates the impact noise. In the case of small lengths of the wheel flat, the 
excitation can be considered identical to roughness. The overall noise levels for excitation from 
wheel flats are dependent on the speed, start 𝑈 and are roughly described by 20 log 𝑈 and in 
comparison, to excitation from roughness which increases with 30 log 𝑈. Noise from roughness 
starts lower than noise generated by wheel flats. Furthermore, a wheel flat increases with the 
loads of the train [5].

Another typical source of impact noises is from joints between rails. A rail joint is illustrated in 
Figure 15(b). A discontinuity occurs when there is no contact surface between the rail and the 
wheel. The rail ends are perfectly aligned with each other, and this introduces a step height. It 
can either be step-up or a step-down. The discontinuity at rail joints forces discrete inputs to 
the wheel-rail system which creates a large variation in the force contact. Furthermore, a step-
up and step-down with identical height results in different responses.  
A step-down is almost independent of the train speed, whereas the overall noise level from a 
step-up is described by 20 log 𝑈 which is dependent on the train speed. This unbalance response 
from joints is found to have a noise level average of, 30 log 𝑈 like rolling noise. The reason for 
the average noise level is that when the train speed increases, the time between the rails de-
creases.  
2.2.1c Squeal Noise 
Squeal noise is a phenomenon which is caused by lateral excitation between the rail and wheel. 
It occurs in sharp radius curves and is strongly tonal due to vibrations from the wheel near one 
of its resonances [17]. Rolling noise and impact noise are broad-band phenomena with a large 
range of frequencies in the audible range whereas squeal noise is dominating other noises 
when it occurs [10]. Furthermore, squeals may happen in isolated periods with varying ampli-
tudes, and they are not necessarily constant [17]. When the squealing noise occurs, it is often 
considered unwanted. Curved tracks especially encounter problems in this connection, and 
thus more relevant for trams, urban metros, and other light trains [5]. To clarify the mechanics 
behind the phenomenon, Figure 16 is helpful.

2.2.2 Modelling Rolling Noise 
Previously, work has been done investigating different noise sources that contribute to the total 
railway noise. The first comprehensive analytical model of the wheel-rail interaction noise con-
tribution was modelled by P. J. Remington when predicting the noise radiation from a given 
velocity distribution on the wheel and rail [7, 14, 19]. A simplified block diagram of the analytical 
model is illustrated in Figure 17. 
 
Figure 17 - Early block diagram of the analytical model presented by P. J. Remington [19] 
The modelling begins with the roughness of the wheel and rail and at that time no reliable 
method for predicting wheel/rail surface conditions were present. Thus, the roughness was 
generally measured as input parameter for the analytical model. The contact patch, the filter 
effects and contact stiffness are included. The contact stiffness can be represented as local or 
Hertzian deformation. However, the Hertzian stiffness could be neglected for most wheel/rail 
systems due to the Hertzian stiffness is sufficiently high and was kept in the research to explore 
the reducing of the local stiffness for controlling the noise [19]. Furthermore, the wheel/rail 
interaction is split, and the radiation efficiency is estimated and used in the sound propagation 
model.  
The propagation model predicts the average wayside noise when a train is passing by instead 
of using a specific train position during a pass by. The lateral motion from the rail and sleepers 
was not included in the modelling in return of a reduction of the complexity. Moreover, the 
elements selected were considered to influence the noise generation the most.   
Initially, investigations in the characterization of the wheel-rail dynamic system and rolling noise 
[7,14] were completed before the analytical model in Figure 17 was published by the Acoustical 
Society of America. 
Further work was conducted by D. J. Thompson in different literature [4],[8],[9]. The modelling 
of the wheel and rail interaction can be considered as a linear system coupled with non-linear 
contact elements. Thompson has developed a track modelling named Track-Wheel Interaction

2.2.6 COMSOL - Software for Multiphysics Simulation  
COMSOL Multiphysics is a comprehensive simulation software which handles an array of multi-
physics problems. The software allows engineers and scientists to reduce their assumptions 
because the software allows for more accurate results. To eliminate assumptions COMSOL can 
couple related physical applications together to include all necessary factors to complete the 
model. Moreover, COMSOL is more flexible compared to other simulation software. It allows 
coupling any number of physics together and creating user-defined physics and expression di-
rectly in the model. This is performed by designed modules which contain various analyses and 
physics. For instance, modelling acoustic-structure interaction where solutions require elastic 
waves in solids and pressure waves in fluids.  
Since the software was founded in 1986, various product releases have happened and one of 
them is Livelink. As the name suggests, Livelink can connect live to other software and this ap-
plication is mainly used in connection with drawing software such as SolidWorks, AutoCAD, and

(Module), Mesh, Study, and Results. From the top, one can create global definitions and param-
eters such as input variables that could be used readily in expressions or functions. A compo-
nent represents a problem (object) which is of interest. In the Component section, the geometry 
is defined, and material properties can be assigned to the geometry. Next is the module (Solid 
Mechanics) where initial- and boundary conditions are defined mathematically to describe the 
physical laws of the problem. Hence, a problem could be to approximate the displacement of

2.2.6a Finite Element Method  
The method COMSOL uses is the Finite Element Method, (FEM). The method was originally de-
volved to solve problems in solid-state mechanics concerning plate bending [25]. The Finite El-
ement Method is a computational technique that solves the approximated boundary values 
problems typically used in engineering. The method enables to discretize the continuum do-
main into smaller finite parts often called elements [26]. The global continuum domain can be 
estimated by solving local solutions that satisfy the partial differential equations within the 
boundary of the elements. The local solutions for the elements are collected and a global solu-
tion is obtained [25]. 
A common physical challenge in FEM simulations is to represent an infinite domain. A domain 
or a problem is normally limited to a manageable region of interest. The problem of interest 
could be a part of a larger system or domain where details far away from the source, loads, and 
material inhomogeneities may not be of interest. Hence, larger models require more memory 
space and increase the computational time. This led to simulations that defined infinite bound-
aries [24]. To tackle the field problems, in the modelling, it is needed to include sufficient vol-
umes around the external boundaries, and it has to be specified in a way, which does not inter-
fere with the field or problem that is modelled. Hence, it is an artificial truncation of the domain. 
In software as COMSOL this is handled by including special boundary conditions which absorb 
outgoing propagating waves without unwanted or unphysical reflections [24]. For a stationary, 
or for transient problems the effects of an infinite boundary can be applied as a coordinate 
scaling to virtual domains that is stretched out toward infinity, thus infinity elements are cre-
ated.  
In frequency domain problems the virtual domains must be stretched into the complex plane 
to absorb the outgoing waves. By doing so, perfectly matched layers, (PMLs) are created. To 
implement the application of a PML where the same complex coordinate stretching is used 
either in one, two or three directions, a stretching function is needed. The stretching is defined 
as a function of the dimensionless coordinate, 𝜉 that normally various between 0 to 1 over the 
PML. For each frequency simulated the function return a new, complex, and stretched coordi-
nate relative to the typical wavelength [24].  
 
==PAGE:37==

Stretching in a single direction has the complex displacement: 
Δ𝒙= 𝜆𝑓𝑖(𝜉) −Δ𝑤𝜉 
Where, 𝜆 is the typical wavelength and Δ𝑤 is the original width of the PML defined by the geom-
etry. In COMSOL, two stretching functions are available and the polynomial stretching function 
is used in the project: 
𝑓𝑝(𝜉) = 𝑠𝜉𝑝(1 −𝑖) 
 
Where, p is a curvature parameter and s is a scaling factor. The stretching function has a mini-
mum of assumptions for the wave field incident on the PMLs. It is most appropriate to use the 
polynomial function when there are a mix of different wave types, and the model mesh can 
afford a least 8 mesh elements across the PMLs. Moreover, when meshing the PMLs a swept 
mesh is used with the distribution of minimum 8 elements. It is recommended to place the PML 
more than 𝜆/8 away from the source or a scatter because, evanescent waves can interact with 
the PML stretching and produce unphysical reflections. In comparison to other stretching func-
tions in COMSOL, the polynomial function interferes less with the convergence of the iterative 
solver [24].  
 
2.2.6b Boundary Element Method 
Another method COMSOL uses is the boundary element method or the boundary integral 
equations. It is a numerical technique to solve physical problems and it can be tracked back to 
the 1970s [44]. The method is an alternative approach to solve the linear partial differential 
equations if they can be formulated as integral equations. The application of BEM can be used 
to simulate the sound radiation of the surface of an object. The method attempts to fit bound-
ary values from the given boundary conditions whereas FEM fits boundary values through the 
space defined by the partial differential equation [46]. When boundary values are defined, the 
integral equations are used in a post-processing stage where numerical solutions can be calcu-
lated anywhere in the solution domain. BEM models are more efficient than FEM models con-
sidering computational resources for problems where the surface to volume ratio is small. Fur-
thermore, surfaces are meshed by surface elements in BEM models instead of meshing the 
whole continuum thus this happens in FEM models [46]. This BE method only requires the 
boundary data as input. The method does have disadvantages in relation to the finite element 
methods. The BEM matrices are fully populated with complex and frequency-dependent coef-
ficients which reduce the efficiency of the solution. Moreover, singularities can happen in the 
solution and must be prevented [45].

The track length used in this model are approximated to include 10 m.  
After the modifications of the models of the proposed track designs that are made with inputs 
from the validation models, the results and analysis is made, and a conclusion of the recom-
mendations and implementations is formed. It might be necessary, to repeat the steps to 
strengthening the models as more knowledge and data appear.

4.0 Discussion 
In this chapter, the study's key findings, interpretations, limitations, and recom-
mendations are presented.  
4.1 Key Findings 
The research question was to investigate how to include the rolling noise in the 
early design phase of the extension of the metro, as a mean to make decisions 
about the design of the track construction using the software COMSOL.  
The study demonstrates that including a literature review and previous studies 
to support the simulation modelling, can be used to develop a new method, 
shown in Figure 23, to make decisions about the noise impact of simple three-
dimensional models of rolling noise. The comparison of the final design models 
suggests that the slab track (design 1) has a lower noise impact than the ballast 
track (design 2). Nonetheless, it is found that the models require more data than 
was available. Despite this, the method shows potential to be used for simple 
approximations of rolling noise with the correct data available. 
4.2 Interpretations 
The approach of simulating the rolling noise by using COMSOL with two separate 
models suggest a simplified and straight forward approve to estimate the sound 
pressure levels compared to the TWINS model.  
The initial models, model 1, in the four studies presented in the simulations mod-
elling process in Figure 23, show that roughness spectrum measured presented 
according to the ISO 3095:2013 standard is sufficient included as the excitation 
for the simulation. The approximation of the frequency response function for the 
complex point impedance is achievable by the simple Equation (3)-(4). However, 
a more detailed geometry of the wheel or rail are necessary or by including more 
boundary conditions.  
Reference [33] and [40], use measurement for either the track or wheel to tune 
the calculated frequency response to match the measurement until it meets good 
agreement. In reference [33], the resilient wheel is lifted up and excited in various 
positions to identify the wheels modes. The responses were measured by an ac-
celerator. The rubber element inside the resilient wheel was adjusted in a finite 
element model to simulate a better approximation of the response of the wheel.  
==PAGE:97==
 
 
95 
 
Moreover, if representative impedance were collected can COMSOL compare the impedances. 
The force spectrums seemingly in the correct magnitude compared to the finding in reference 
[49] were force spectrum for train speeds of 80 km/h and 120 km/h was below 100 N and above 
1000 N. The uncertainty of the approximated force spectrum is the impedance and roughness 
input and more importantly the neglect of contact stiffness.  
In the paper from Remington [14] it is stated that a neglect of the contact stiffness is judged to 
be tolerable for frequencies below 4000 Hz. In reference [10], A. Pieringer uses various contact 
stiffness models to describe rolling noise and other mechanical noises such as wheel flats. 
Moreover, the work primarily focuses on the time domain models. Thompson include the con-
tact stiffness in the TWINS model. The boundary conditions used for the tracks to approximate 
frequency responses are done differently than reference [33] and [40]. The first reference com-
pares different tram designs. The track designs of the tramps are modelled with discretely sup-
ported Timoshenko beams for the rail. The sleepers are modelled with beam elements and 
sleeper blocks are represented as lumped masses.  
Furthermore, reference [40] investigates the influence of the wheel flats on a crack growth. The 
train-track interaction model simulates the rail as a discreetly supported undamped Rayleigh-
Timoshenko beam using a finite element method. The rail ends are clamped. Sleepers are 
treated as rigid mass and only the half mass is used, since the model is two-dimensional. Each 
rail pad is modelled as a discrete linear elastic spring and a viscous damper. The ballast track is 
modelled with a spring and damper. Moreover, the models use symmetry to include the whole 
track construction.  
The simple method proposed in this study is modeled in three-dimensional and no use of sym-
metry is included. The pads are modelled as stiff pads with a high spring stiffness and damper. 
The stiff pads are behaving according to the illustration shown in Figure 9. Hence, the pads 
dampen the rail vibrations but cause the sleepers to increase the vibration. This assumption is 
likely not present for the pads used for the metro train in Copenhagen. This is an uncertainty. 
Frequency response functions and estimations of sound pressure levels have not been done 
entirely in three-dimensional before. However, reference [36] use a waveguide finite element 
and wavenumber boundary element approach to approximate the sound radiated from rail-
ways and trams. Two-dimensional geometries of the problems take advantage of the finite el-
ements numerical approach. Furthermore, the three-dimensional behavior for the vibrations, 
sound field and the extend of the infinite rail are taken into account. The approach used in 
reference [36], solves the three-dimensional problems with reduced effort compared to the 
three-dimensional modelling from the previous methods.  
 
 
==PAGE:98==
 
 
96 
 
The radiation efficiency used to estimate the sound pressure levels from reference [14] use two 
rigid cylinders vibrating independently with the same levels and diameter from the rail dimen-
sions. Also, an unbaffled rigid disc vibrating normal to its plan has been used to estimate the 
wheel radiation efficiency. The inclusion of the boundary element method and modern soft-
ware provide better approximation for the sound propagation than reference [14].  
4.3 Limitations and Recommendations 
The study presents a new method to make decisions about the rolling noise from track con-
structions using simulation modelling. Further research is needed to strengthen the robustness 
of the model, by examining the boundary conditions to approximate more accurate frequency 
responses in the frequency domain. The solutions found in frequency domains are time invar-
iant, consequently the material properties are not changing. This limits the inclusion of the phe-
nomenon, such as parametric excitation from sleepers, which is due to the pinned-pinned fre-
quently.  
Moreover, the squeal noises are calculated in a time domain, which A. Pieringer [10] with de-
tailed contact models investigates. The rolling noise is dominant for train speeds above 60 km/h 
and at conventional and high speeds. Whereas at the lower speeds other noises can dominate, 
such as traction noise from motors or fan noise. This limits the credibility for sound pressure 
levels for trains speeds below 60 km/h, which is estimated by the method used in this study. 
Correction for noises lower than 60 km/h is necessary to approximate trustworthy noise levels.  
Furthermore, the lack of measurement data has influenced the results. To further develop the 
method used in this study, more data need to be available for the rails and wheels. Still, the 
results indicate that the method has its strengths due to its simplicity and three-dimensional 
modelling (compared to previous studies). Therefore, it is recommended to collect measure-
ments of the resilient wheel and UIC54 rail used at the Copenhagen Metro. Additionally, repre-
sentative roughness data is required to be used in excitation models, such as the model pre-
sented in this study. TWINS model or CRoNoS will likely estimate the noise levels closer to meas-
ured noise levels.  
Additionally, the inclusion of the sound pressure levels in their early design process affects the 
projects involved departments at Copenhagen Metro. Hence, it is recommended for the Copen-
hagen Metro to investigate whether the identified challenges from reference [12] are similar to 
their internal workflows. The reference [12] investigate the workflow between an acoustician 
and an architect. The challenges found are presented by whether they are process-, people-, or 
technical-oriented and eight solutions are weighed in a decision matrix.  
 
 
==PAGE:99==
 
 
97 
 
One of the more influential limitations of the study are the models’ sizes used when predicting 
the response functions and sound pressure levels. Three-dimensional models require enor-
mous numbers of elements and degrees of freedom. This is used to solve the local solutions in 
the finite element method in order to approximate the global solution. Inclusion of symmetry 
of the track or wheel would have reduced the memory demand and computational time. The 
frequency response models used in this study simulated approximately 0.5-1 hour to calculate 
one-third octave band frequencies from 100-5000 Hz. Sound pressure levels from 200-1250 Hz 
simulated roughly 1-3 hours. Further optimization to reduce the three-dimensional models are 
recommended.

5.0 Conclusion 
This research aimed to create a new method for simulating the rolling noise of 
two simple track constructions for the Copenhagen Metro to make decisions con-
cerning the designs based on the noise impact. Based on the literature review 
and simulation modelling, models were made to verify the models of the two de-
signs, a slab track (design 1) and a ballast track (design 2). The sound pressure 
levels of the models indicate that the slab track (design 1) had a lower noise im-
pact than the ballast track (design 2). It can be concluded that this study has 
shown how to create simple tree-dimensional models that can be used for deci-
sion making. The models are in the initial phase and has potential to be used for 
simple approximations of rolling noise in the future if new measured data will be 
available and if the robustness and reliability of the models will be strengthened 
further.
