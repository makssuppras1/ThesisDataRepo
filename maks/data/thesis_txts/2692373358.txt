1
Introduction
The last decade has seen a substantial increase in awareness of citizens on issues related
to climate change. The idea that the current rate of consumption in most industrialized
countries is not on par with a sustainable society model is now widely accepted among
the population.
Thus, the path to follow to ”decarbonize” our society has become a central discussion
topic ­ be it among stakeholders or citizens. At the scale of an individual, a logical first
step towards such objective would be to have a thorough understanding of their individual
impact. This interest in environmental impact assessment has given rise to the emergence
of countless dedicated softwares and websites. As exemplified by the WWF solution
(https://footprint.wwf.org.uk/#/), a straightforward approach consists in asking questions
to the user about their lifestyle habits to provide an estimate of their ”footprint” on the
environment.
However, a limit to this assessment method lies in the discrepancy between the results
obtained and the day­to­day actions that led to them.
The recent developments in open­banking have made it possible to connect to the heart
of a citizen consumption ­ their bank account. By plugging in directly to their flow of
transactions, it is possible to get a clear picture of their consumption ­ making it possible to
identify potential hotspots that should be addressed in priority to maximize their reduction
in footprint.
The aim of this study is twofold. First, capitalize on the work carried out by French com­
pany Greenly in France to design a consolidated database of ”emission factors” across
the whole European Union, that would allow to link expenditures in various sectors of the
economy to the corresponding carbon emissions. Then, assess the correlation between
these calculated factors and some key variables representative of the structure of a coun­
try economy ­ which would allow to investigate if a model can be drawn and generalized
to countries outside of the scope considered.

Background
2.1
Production­ and consumption­based perspectives
The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) is a multi­
lateral organism whose objective is to build a global framework to fight climate change.
Established in 1992, it forms a part of the United Nations (UN).
In its Article 1 the UNFCCC defines climate change as a ”change of climate which is at­
tributed directly or indirectly to human activity that alters the composition of the global
atmosphere and which is in addition to natural climate variability observed over compa­
rable time periods.” (Pachauri et al., 2014). Already present in this axiomatic definition is
the central concept of attribution. All anthropogenic emissions stem from a human activity
that happened at some point in time, and this link needs to be unfold in order to perform
a fair attribution of responsibilities.
Adopted in 1997, the Kyoto Protocol ”sets individual, legally binding targets for industri­
alized countries prepared to take positive steps to curb emissions of carbon dioxide and
other GHGs from sources within their remit” (United Nations Framework Convention on
Climate Change, 2006). It is currently ratified by 192 Parties (UNFCCC, 2021a) which
are divided into 2 groups:
• Annex I members. These are the industrialized countries in charge of leading the
fight against climate change. Following the principle of efforts made on the basis of
”common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities” (Article 3.1),
they are ones who are expected to reduce their GHG emissions. Among these 42
countries is a subset of countries called the Annex II members. It consists of the 24
Annex I Parties that were part of the Organisation for Economic Co­operation and
Development (OECD) in 1992. Annex II members must supply financial resources
to help developing countries access tools and technologies that are necessary to
tackle climate change.
• Non­Annex I members. They mostly consist in developing countries and are not
bound to reporting and mitigation obligations as stringent as Annex I members.
The Kyoto Protocol had two commitment periods ­ 2008­2012 and 2013­2020 ­, after
which it was supplemented by the Paris Agreement. Currently ratified by 197 parties
(UNFCCC, 2021b) it sets a global warming limit of 2°C ­ preferably 1.5 °C ­ compared to
pre­industrial levels.
A central element to the frameworks established by the UNFCCC is the reporting of na­
tional GHGs emissions ­ which is mandatory for all Annex I parties. It constitutes indeed
the basis for all assessment and prospective studies carried out within the context of the
institution. Each government must follow extensive guidelines set up by the Intergovern­
mental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). It is notably mentioned that ”national inventories
include greenhouse gas emissions and removals taking place within national territory and
offshore areas over which the country has jurisdiction. [...] For example, emissions from
fuel use in road transport is included in the emissions of the country where the fuel is sold
and not where the vehicle is driven, as fuel sale statistics are widely available and usually
much more accurate.” (Eggleston et al., 2006).

This type of scope is called territorial­based. Three main geographical approaches are
usually found in the literature (Barrett et al., 2013) (Eurostat, 2021a):
• Territorial­based emission accounts: They are the ones used for reporting pur­
poses to the UNFCCC. They represent all GHGs emissions occurring within the
boundaries of a country. Emissions occurring in international territory (shipping,
flight) are not assigned to any country ­ they are reported as a memo.
• Residence­based (or production­based) emission accounts: Contrarily to territorial­
based accounts, they allocate emissions not according to where they took place but
to the responsible economic entity ­ and subsequently to the country it is based in.
They include emissions related to international shipping and aviation. For example
emissions generated by the Irish airline company Ryanair are allocated to Ireland
irrespective of the countries of departure and arrival. They are generally referred to
as air emissions accounts.
• Consumption­based emission accounts: They attribute emissions to the end­
user of a good or service ­ and subsequently to their country of residence. They
notably include all upstream emissions i.e. emissions that occurred throughout the
whole production chain. For example emissions arising from the production of rice
in Vietnam are allocated to the country where the product is consumed.

Consumption­based emissions can bee seen as production­based emissions adjusted in
international trade (G. P. Peters and Hertwich, 2008a):
Consumption = Production −Exports + Imports
(2.1)
This relationship is key to understand some critiques that have been leveled against the
UNFCCC reporting scheme (G. P. Peters and Hertwich, 2008a). By not taking into ac­
count emissions embedded in international trade, it allows Annex I countries to reduce
their reported emissions by transferring their production means to non­Annex I countries.
This phenomenon ­ often referred to as carbon leakage ­ has been widely covered in the
literature (Ahmad and Wyckoff, 2003 (G. P. Peters and Hertwich, 2008b) (G. P. Peters,
2008). Defining carbon leakage as ”the emissions embodied in trade from non­Annex
I countries divided by the total domestic emissions”, (G. P. Peters and Hertwich, 2006)
finds that Norway’s carbon leakage account for 30%.
The Global Carbon Project initiative (Friedlingstein et al., 2020) provides detailed data
on territorial and consumption emissions of countries, as well as transfer of emissions ­
defined as the difference between the two aforementioned metrics. fig. 2.3 shows that
Annex I countries have become significant emissions importers since the middle of the
1990s.

2.2
The carbon footprint
The concept of carbon footprint has been defined by (Wiedmann and Minx, 2008) as ”a
measure of the exclusive total amount of carbon dioxide emissions that is directly and
indirectly caused by an activity or is accumulated over the life stages of a product”.
However there is no clearly adopted definition. As pointed out by (Weidema et al., 2008)
most definitions do not only take into account carbon emissions and are thus expressed
in CO2­equivalent.
(G. P. Peters, 2010) argues that the sole inclusion of carbon might be too restrictive in
the context of study on climate change ­ which is the core domain of application of the
carbon footprint concept. In their reports, most countries’ statistics offices include at least
the three main GHGs: carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4) and nitrous oxide (N2O).
Other gases are sometimes accounted as well (see e.g. (DEFRA, 2021) which includes
Hydro­flourocarbons (HFC), Perflurocarbons (PFC), Nitrogen trifluoride (NF3) and Sul­
phur hexafluoride (SF6)).
At the scale of a nation, the term ”carbon footprint” refers to GHG emissions embodied in
households final demand. It is generally given in tCO2e/capita.
The carbon footprint of households is composed of 3 elements:
• Direct emissions: They correspond to emissions directly related to households
activities (mainly gas combustion in cars and natural gas/domestic fuel for heating).
• Domestic emissions linked to domestic final demand: They correspond to the

sumed domestically or to produce a good that will be used as an intermediate input

• Foreign emissions linked to domestic final demand: They correspond to the
emissions occurring outside of the country, either to produce a good that will be

2.3
Input­Output Models
2.3.1
Historical background
Developed in the 30s by Wassily Leontief, it was ­ and still is ­ a central tool in policy
planning. Indeed, it allows to assess the impact of a change in final demand on the
economy of the region. Several works in the 70s ­ notably led by Leontief ­ incorporated
environmental extensions to these models. This was the start of what is now commonly
referred as Environmentally­Extended Input­Output Analysis (EEIOA). The interest in this
field has been renewed in the 90s under the influence of the Dutch Office for Statistics.
2.3.2
Input­Output Tables
• Accounting systems and frameworks: 1953, 1968, 1993, 2008 SNA (System of
National Accounts), 1993, 2003, 2012 SEEA (System of Environmental­Economic
Accounting) and 1995, 2010 ESA (European System of Accounts, Eurostat)
Soviet­American economist Wassily Leontief (1905­1999) is often credited for the foun­
dation of Input­Output Analysis (IOA). In his groundbreaking works in the early 1930s
(W. W. Leontief, 1936) he built the first Input­Output Tables (IOTs), which consisted in a
modeling of inter­industries monetary exchanges in the United States. He took notable
inspiration from François Quesnay’s Tableau économique (1758). Interestingly enough,

Quesnay was part of a school of thought called the Physiocrats, who believed that agricul­
ture was the only source of wealth in a society ­ considering manufacturing and commerce
as ”sterile expenditures” with no value added (Miller and Blair, 2009, Appendix C).
Led by British economist Richard Stone (1913­1991), a team of researchers from the
United Nations (UN) has worked during the 1950s and 1960s towards the establishment
of a standardized input­output framework. This materialized in the 1968 version of the
System of National Accounts (SNA, Nations, 1968) which included input­output accounts
in the form of Supply­Use Tables (SUTs). Since then, IOTs and SUTs form a central
component of national accounting systems, the latest iteration of which are the 2008 SNA
and its European counterpart the 2010 European System of Accounts (ESA).
SUTs and IOTs show some key differences. While IOTs provide a symmetric modeling
of the economy (product­by­product or industry­by­industry), SUTs have a product­by­
industry (also called commodity­by­industry) structure.
SUTs are composed of 2 parts:
• A supply table (or make table) showing how products – produced domestically or
imported – are brought into the economy.

• A use table showing how these products are used ­ intermediate consumption, final
consumption, gross capital formation or exports.
Figure 2.7: Simplified structure of the use table (Source: United Nations, 2018)

• For each industry: Output = Intermediate consumption + Gross Value Added (GVA)
• For each product: Output + Imports = Intermediate consumption + Final consump­

Creating IOTs from SUTs involves choosing among a set of assumptions. This choice
results in the computation of 4 different table models referred to as Model A, Model B,
Model C and Model D.
Models A and B are product­by­product IOTs stemming from a technology assumption –

• Product technology assumption: each product is made in a single specific way,
regardless of the industry which produced it.
• Industry technology assumption: each industry has a single specific way of pro­

Models C and D are industry­by­industry IOTs stemming from an assumption of fixed

product sales structure assumption.
• Fixed industry sales structure assumption: each industry has its own specific
sales structure, regardless of its product mix.
• Fixed product sales structure assumption: each industry has its own specific
sales structure, regardless of the industry which produced it.

2.3.4
Environmental extension
IOA provides a flexible framework for various types of analysis. Indeed, the basic Leontief
formulation can be extended to integrate various types of data, such as labour or material
use. One its the main extension consists in an assessment of the environmental impacts
induced by the consumption of a product. This field is known as Environmentally Extended
Input­Output Analysis (EEIOA).
Developed notably by Leontief in the 1970s (W. Leontief, 1972), the idea of extending IOA
with air emission accounts was applied by several statistics divisions in Europe during
the 1980s and 1990s. The Dutch National Accounting Matrix including Environmental
Accounts (NAMEA) is often cited as an early example of this approach put into practice
(De Haan et al., 1993) (De Haan and Keuning, 1996).
National air emission accounts consist in an assessment of the GHG emissions arising
from each sector of a country ­ calculated through the residence principle. They were
integrated in the 1993 System of Environmental Economic Accounting (SEEA), the latest
version of which is the 2012 SEEA (United Nations, 2012). Since 2013, members of the
EU report these accounts to Eurostat on a yearly basis.
Following the notations used in (G. Peters and Hertwich, 2004) and building upon eq. (2.5)
the environmental impact embodied in final consumption is derived by:
E = Fx
= FLY
(2.6)
• E is a row vector of dimensions n×1 referred to as the vector of total environmental
effect. Element Ei represents the total environmental impact of associated with
sector i. In the case of carbon accounting, it is expressed in [kgCO2e].
• F is a diagonal matrix of dimension n × n referred to as the matrix of direct impact.
Element Fii represents the environmental impact observed per monetary unit worth
of output by sector i. In the case of carbon accounting, it is expressed in [kgCO2e/€].
2.3.5
Calculating emissions embodied in trade
In a multi­regional framework, the way to account for imported and exported emissions
­ often referred to as ”emissions embodied in trade” ­ is of primary importance. Until
recently, no consistent database existed to accurately model flows between countries
and regions across the world. Thus, various types of assumptions were employed that
affected the carbon footprint calculation dramatically.
Three main approaches can be distinguished:
• The Domestic Technology Assumption (DTA)
Imported goods and services are supposed to have been produced in the same way as
they would have been if they were produced domestically. In other words, the domestic
technology of the country (direct requirement matrix A and matrix of direct impact F) is
used to calculated emissions contained in imports.
• Unidirectional trade

Monetary Emission Ratios

==PAGE:17==
The carbon content of imported goods and services are calculated from the technology of
the exporting country (direct requirement matrix A and matrix of direct impact F). How­
ever, the imported goods and services used by this exporting country as intermediate
inputs to these goods and services are estimated using the DTA.
• Multidirectional trade
The carbon content of imported goods and services are calculated from the technology of
the exporting country (direct requirement matrix A and matrix of direct impact F). For all
steps of the whole production chain, the technology of the region where the step occurs is
used. Although being the most accurate approach, it has only begun to be used recently.
Indeed, it requires both a comprehensive dataset of IOT and a sizeable computing power.
fig. 2.11 provides a graphical overview of the difference between these three approaches.

following the multidirectional trade approach. As shown by (G. P. Peters and Hertwich,
2006), assuming that imports are produced with the same technology as the importing
country can lead to massive underestimation of the carbon footprint of a country ­ by a

2.5
Greenly
Greenly is a French start­up founded in 2019. It aims at providing precise carbon footprint
estimates to citizens ­ through a free mobile application ­, companies ­ through a dedicated
SaaS ­ and banks ­ through an API.
The company leverages the new possibilities offered by open­banking services to build a
systematic and automatized framework of carbon accounting. In practice for the mobile
application, the user connects to their bank account. The application runs an algorithm
that analyzes each transaction based on its label and categorizes it to one if the sev­
eral Greenly categories (e.g. ”Home hardware” or ”Cultural activities”). The full list of
categories can be found in appendix A. They are labeled according to five general parent
categories: Housing, Shopping, Transport, Services and Food. The Greenly classification
has been created for the sole purpose of expenses categorization and carbon footprint
calculations. This explains its varying level of detail, which is directly linked to the func­
tioning of the categorization algorithm and its ability to analyze transaction labels.

In order to convert expenditure figures (in €) to carbon emissions (in kgCO2e), a Monetary

3
Methodology
The objective of this study is to draw a set of MERs for all 27 EU countries (plus the United
Kingdom) and most Greenly categories, reflecting the carbon footprint associated with the
consumption of goods and services by households.
First, emission ratios are calculated using the EXIOBASE model for all its 200 products.
A mapping procedure is then established to convert these numbers into the Greenly pur­
chase categories classification. Complementary calculations are conducted for some cat­
egories for which the EXIOBASE output is deemed not to fit correctly the Greenly category.
Finally, a correlation analysis is carried out to identify some key explaining parameters to
the difference in results between the countries.

3.1
Calculation of monetary emission ratios
The 2011 product­by­product EXIOBASE v3.8.1 (Stadler et al., 2021 is the primary data
source used to compute the MERs. Computations are performed through Pymrio (Stadler,
2021) ­ a Python open­source tool which implements functions and methods to handle the
parsing, calculation and analysis of various MRIO models (including EXIOBASE, WIOD,
EORA26).
Upon parsing, the tool creates a mrio system object which contains all basic parameters of
the model (technical coefficients, satellite accounts, etc). The calc_all() method can then
be used to calculate all missing parts of the systems ­ notably the impacts footprints. For
this study, the impact category ”GHG emissions (GWP100) | Problem oriented approach:
baseline (CML, 2001) | GWP100 (IPCC, 2007)” is employed.
Among the outputs of the model, product­level impact accounts are calculated for each
region present in the model. They can be divided into two types:
Demand­driven metrics

country. Dimp is a part of Dcba ­ the part that happened abroad. The part that happened
domestically can be obtained by subtraction.

3.2

One of the distinguishing feature of the EXIOBASE model is its high­level of sectoral
detail (Stadler et al., 2018). It exhibits 200 product categories covering thoroughly the
economy of a country. Greenly classification, for its part, employs 76 purchase categories
­ excluding tag values which almost triples this number. It was built following the very

of its label. This means that it is not necessarily aligned on other consumption­driven
classification ­ e.g. from EXIOBASE or COICOP (Classification Of Individual Consumption
by Purpose).

Complementary calculations
For most Greenly purchase categories, the two­fold mapping is considered accurate enough
to give directly operable coefficients. However, this is not the case for some of them, with
various reasons:
• Energy­ and transport­ related data: The impact of a product is not only linked
to its production, but also to its use phase. This is why direct household emissions
(mainly from combustion of heating fuel or car fuel) are added as a separate el­
ement when calculating the carbon footprint of a country using IOA. Although the
EXIOBASE model does contain values for these direct emissions, it does not provide
them at the product level ­ but only aggregated at the level of the country. Moreover,
these are sectors for which comprehensive databases exist at the European level
(mostly available on Eurostat).

(Beylot et al., 2019) and the present study. While (Beylot et al., 2019) deal with absolute
indicators (e.g. expressed in kgCO2e in the case of climate change), the numbers calcu­
lated here are relative to monetary value.
Ultimately, this analysis aims to check if strong linear relationships similar to the ones
found by (Beylot et al., 2019) exist between the calculated MERs and explanatory vari­
ables. If this were the case, this could allow to justify simple approximations to compute
MERs in countries not covered in this study.

A total of seven explanatory variables were selected. They cover various aspects of the
economic structure of a country ­ household consumption and habits, freight, energy, etc.
A description of each variable is given in tables 3.13 to 3.19.
For each explanatory variable, the Pearson correlation coefficient R is used to assess the
strength of its relationship to each MER based on the 28 observations (27 EU countries
+ the United Kingdom).
The set of correlation coefficient is then categorized according to the parent category of
the associated MER (”Housing”, ”Shopping”, ”Transport”, ”Food” or ”Services). A one­way
Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is conducted to check the influence of the parent category
on the correlation coefficient. The null hypothesis H0 can be stated as follows:
”H0: The mean of the correlation coefficients is equal across all parent categories”.
Using a confidence level of 95%, the null hypothesis H0 can not be rejected if the p­value
of the ANOVA model is above 0.05. The assumptions on the normality, equal variance
and independence of the errors are checked graphically through diagnostic plots. These
plots are shown in appendix C for information ­ they are not commented below.
For this study, the api.stats.anova_lm function from the statsmodels Python package is
used to conduct the ANOVA.
The MERs corresponding to ”Specific calculation” and ”International good” ­ as well as the
category ”BOAT” ­ are left outside of this correlation analysis. Indeed, they do not show
any variance across countries, and thus would only add disturbances to the model.

5
Conclusion
Through a combination of input­output calculations and specific studies, it has been pos­
sible to build a consolidated database of MERs at the EU level. These numbers allow
to bridge the gap between expenditures of individuals and their carbon footprint on the
environment.
An analysis of the correlation between these factors and some key representative national
variables has shown that no linear relationship can be directly drawn between them. Only
for the carbon intensity of electricity is there a significant difference in the correlation be­
tween the different parent categories.
This suggest that investigating more advanced modeling approaches (e.g. exploring non­
linear relationships through machine learning techniques) are needed to expect building
a scalable model.
The question of trade and transport margins, as well as taxes has not been covered in
this study ­ which was conducted using the basic price framework of the EXIOBASE. This
could be an valuable follow­up, since taking them into account would allow to represent
more accurately the expenditures of households.
An aspect of particular interest in IO models is their reliance on closed data. Contrarily
to LCAs, the inaccuracy and uncertainty of IO models does not come from omissions
or improper system boundaries, but rather from the aggregation level of the database at
hand. In other words, a MER computed by an IO model is not imprecise because part of
the emissions is concealed, but rather because the level of sectoral disaggregation is not
high enough to take into account the specific features of the supply chain of a product.
The possibility of combining process­based LCA and EEIOA in a hybrid model has been
studied lately (see (Agez et al., 2020)). A close look should be kept on the advancement
of this field, which could help leverage even more efficiently the data available.
