Abstract
In this thesis, we formulate and optimize the campaign planning problem (CPP) and quantify
the impact of applying the proposed optimization approach to the CPP at the Danish Broad-
casting Corporation (DR). The CPP is a complex combinatorial optimization problem that
involves scheduling campaigns of di↵erent priorities over a given time horizon while not exceed-
ing inventories. The campaigns should be scheduled in a way such that metrics reﬂecting the
scope of the priorities and distribution of the campaigns are minimized.
The problem is formulated mathematically as a simple and extended model.
By applying
a state-of-the-art mathematical solver to the two variations, we ﬁnd that only the extended
model is suﬃcient for solving the CPP at DR.
We develop an adaptive large neighborhood search (ALNS) with a simulated annealing frame-
work, and the heuristic design is determined based on experiments using six training instances.
The experiments show that the complexity of the preferred metaheuristic for solving the CPP
is dependent on the instance size, hence a large neighborhood search (LNS) and an ALNS algo-
rithm are proposed. The performance of the metaheuristics and the commercial solver is tested
on nine test instances. For the small and medium sized instances, the commercial solver provides
solutions of better quality, but the solutions provided by the LNS are of comparable quality. For
the largest instances, the solution quality of the proposed ALNS conﬁguration shows substantial
improvement from the commercial solver; on average 6.17-7.4% improvement and up to 11.54%
improvement in the best case. On top of this, the commercial solver requires 120 seconds to
provide solutions we perceive as feasible, while the proposed construction heuristic ﬁnds these
within 0.02 seconds.
Comparing the campaign plan of the proposed ALNS procedure to DR’s current manual cam-
paign plan, we see an objective improvement of approximately 44%. This concludes that the
application of an optimization method that complies with the business requirements can improve
and provide insight into the current campaign planning approach at DR.

Glossary
Advertisement:
A campaign consists of advertisements. An advertisement promotes a
piece of content on a speciﬁc channel.
Brand channel:
A brand channel is the channel that a piece of content is broadcasted
on. The advertisements associated with a piece of content is not nec-
essarily shown on the brand channel.
Campaign:
A campaign consists of a series of advertisements promoting the same
piece of content. A campaign belongs to a priority, and can be broad-
casted on di↵erent channels.
Campaign period:
The campaign period of a campaign refers to the time period where it
is broadcasted.
Channel:
A channel broadcasts advertisements. An advertisement is shown on
one channel, but a campaign can be broadcasted on multiple channels.
Channel consumption:
The amount of the channel inventory that a campaign takes up when
broadcasted.
Channel inventory:
The total capacity of each channel that the campaigns consume from
when broadcasted.
Content:
A piece of content could be a show, program etc. A campaign promotes
one piece of content.
Media:
A media produces advertisements. An advertisement is produced by
one media, but a campaign can be produced by multiple media.
Media consumption:
The amount of the media inventory that a campaign takes up when
produced.
Media inventory:
The total capacity of each media that the campaigns consume from
when produced.
Phase-out period:
The phase-out period is the last time steps of the time period, where
campaigns can not be positioned. The phase-out period is from time
step tstop to |T|.
Priority:
A priority is a categorization of a campaign. It is deﬁned by the brand
channel and the campaign type. A priority could be a campaign of
type Flagskib on brand channel DR1.
Production period:
The production period of a campaign refers to the time period where
it is produced.
Scope:
The aimed number of campaigns of a priority.
Start-up period:
The start-up period is the ﬁrst time steps of the time period, where a
campaign can not be positioned. The start-up period is from time step
1 to tstart.
Week 0:
Week 0 refers to the week of the campaign period where the original
piece of content premieres.

Introduction
Planning problems occur in almost all industries of our modern society and in many di↵erent
variations. Examples could be time-tabling problems, shift schedules, and production planning,
which all are problems that are limited by the amount of available resources. Optimization
techniques are often applied to solve these problems in a way such that the available resources
are utilized in the best possible way. Another example of a planning problem occurs in market-
ing and communication when new products are launched. Marketing communication is often
structured by advertising campaigns, which are a series of advertisements sharing the same
theme and message[2]. The more campaigns a company broadcasts, the more brand awareness
it creates up to a certain point. Therefore it is crucial to consider optimizing the campaign
planning in order to broadcast as many of the desired campaigns as the resources allow.
The Danish Broadcasting Corporation (DR) is Denmark’s primary national public-service unit.
The company is responsible for communicating news, culture, entertainment, and information
through its media[3].
Naturally, DR continually launches new content, and in that matter
campaign planning becomes necessary. The campaign plans at DR have to comply with the
company’s internal capacities regarding production resources and GRP1 inventory on their chan-
nels. Currently, the production and planning of campaigns are done separately and manually.
This can lead to the production of campaigns that are never broadcasted due to lack of in-
ventory or to DR not utilizing their full GRP inventory because their manual approach is not
optimized. Because of this, DR potentially misses out on a lot of exposure and wastes some of
their resources. These challenges at DR form the basis of the problem addressed in this thesis
and are referred to as the campaign planning problem (CPP).
In this thesis, we examine how the CPP at DR can be formalized and solved using operations
research tools. Two versions of the CPP varying in complexity are considered and solved using
a commercial solver. To avoid imposing expensive licenses on DR, a metaheuristic approach is
implemented and tested against the solver. The solution methods are compared in regard to
performance, running time, and reliability.
1.1
The campaign planning problem
In simple terms, the aim of the CPP is to create a clever plan for the scheduled campaigns over
a certain time period given a set of requirements.
An advertisement can be a commercial on TV or on the radio, a post on Instagram, or an ad
on the top of a website that promotes something. In the case of the CPP, the advertisements
promote a piece of content. As one advertisement alone isn’t very e↵ective in creating awareness
of that piece of content, campaigns are created.
Campaigns consist of a series of di↵erent
advertisements promoting the same piece of content for a deﬁned period of time. As di↵erent
content requires di↵erent kinds of advertisements, we deﬁne di↵erent types of campaigns, and
each unique campaign type is referred to as a priority. A priority deﬁnes a campaign in terms

If two pieces of content have similar sizes and similar target groups, the campaigns promoting the
two pieces of content would often also be of the same priority. This means that the campaigns

We imagine that a
piece of content that is directed to the elderly, mainly should be promoted with a campaign

Hence, campaigns targeting the elderly are of a di↵erent priority than campaigns targeting
e.g. teenagers. Likewise, a ﬂagship show would require a campaign consisting of many larger
advertisements and therefore belong to another priority than a small show that only requires
a campaign with fewer advertisements.
As the priority deﬁnes the number and the types
of advertisements a campaign consists of, it also deﬁnes how long it takes to produce the
advertisements for that campaign. For the rest of the thesis, we will consider the campaigns as
a whole and rarely look at individual advertisements.
Each campaign of a priority consumes an amount of the available inventory in a certain time
period depending on when it is scheduled. In the CPP, there are two di↵erent types of inventory:
the channel inventory and the media inventory. There can be multiple channels and multiple
media and these have di↵erent inventories. A campaign of a given priority consumes from the
channel inventory when the campaign is broadcasted and from the media inventory when the
campaign is produced. The period for which the campaign is broadcasted is called the campaign
period and the period for which the campaign is produced is called the production period.
To keep track of the premiere of the content relative to the production- and campaign period,
we deﬁne the term week 0 for each campaign. The term week 0 refers to the time step where the
content premieres. If a campaign is placed at position t, it means that week 0 of the campaign
is at time step t.
The production period of a campaign is placed prior to week 0 of the campaign, and it is in this
period, all the advertisements are produced. The production requires resources from di↵erent
media. For each priority, the media consumption for each media throughout the production
period is predeﬁned. Similarly, the advertisements require inventory from di↵erent channels.
For each priority, the channel consumption for each channel throughout the campaign period is
predeﬁned. For all media and for all channels, it should hold that the total consumption at each
time step is less than or equal to the inventory at that time step. It is only possible to schedule
complete campaigns, which means that we can not schedule a smaller version of a campaign
that only consumes a smaller amount of the predeﬁned channel- and media consumption. Due
to the fact that the campaigns can consume inventory both before and after week 0, we deﬁne
a start-up period at the beginning, and a phase-out period at the end of the time period. The
campaigns can only be positioned in the period between the start-up period and the phase-out
period. The terms presented are summarized in an illustration on Figure 1.1.

1.3
Overview
In this thesis, we investigate the CPP and its solution methods. The problem is positioned in
the operations research (OR) literature and similar well-known problems and their suggested
solution methods are reviewed in Section 2. In Section 3, we initially formulate a simple model
that considers the basic constraints and requirements deﬁned. From this, we formulate a more
complex model that considers the distribution of campaigns and we thoroughly analyze the
cost function. General, exact, and heuristic solution approaches are presented in Section 4, and
a heuristic solution approach for the CPP is proposed. The data is introduced in Section 5,
and the heuristic design regarding parameter values, solution sensitivity, and simplicity of the
proposed solution method is considered. The proposed heuristic and a mathematical solver
are applied for solving the CPP. The computational results are presented in Section 6 and the
performance is evaluated. Finally, the model and its solutions are compared with the current
manual campaign planning approach at DR in Section 7, where the impact of applying an
optimization approach to the problem in practice is quantiﬁed. The decisions, simpliﬁcations,
and assumptions made throughout the thesis are discussed in Section 8, and the ﬁndings are
summarized in a conclusion in Section 9.
The thesis contributes to solving future complex planning problems. It shows how to apply

The KP is a well-known and extensively researched problem within the ﬁeld of combinatorial
optimization. The concept of a KP is to determine which items to pack in a knapsack such that
the total weight of the items is less than or equal to the capacity of the knapsack, and the total
value of the knapsack is as large as possible. The items are selected from a set of items each
with a weight and a value. In (2.1)-(2.3) the standard 0−1 KP is formulated as a maximization
problem. Here xj is a binary decision variable that determines whether or not item j is added
to the knapsack. The parameters wj and pj are the weight and proﬁt of item j respectively,
while c is the capacity of the knapsack. The KP is one of the simplest NP-hard problems in
combinatorial optimization[5], and it is a very investigated problem, hence a lot of variations of
this have been examined.

In the CPP, each item consumes an amount of the inventory in a certain time period, which
makes the problem similar to the temporal knapsack problem (TKP). In the chapter The Tem-
poral Knapsack Problem and Its Solution, Bartlett et al. describe how the TKP can be solved
using a decomposition algorithm[13]. The proposed algorithm involves a non-deterministic pro-
cedure that deﬁnes a search tree, two methods for searching the tree for an optimal solution, and
ﬁnally, linear programming is used to compute cuts, upper bounds, lower bounds, and variable
assignments. Since the time period where an item is active is predeﬁned in the TKP, it varies
from the CPP, as one of the decisions here is to determine when an item should be active i.e.
consume from the inventories. Thus, we cannot use the TKP formulation for the CPP.
We can be inspired by the KP with neighbor constraints in how to model the active period of
an item. The KP with neighbor constraints has been studied mostly in graph contexts and with
uniform weights and proﬁts[14, 15]. Instead of considering the neighbors of an item, the CPP
looks at the neighbors of a knapsack. There is a knapsack for each time step and the neighbors

problem with promising results within a reasonable time.

3.3
Extended model
Since the Baseline model does not consider the distribution of campaigns of the same priority,
we introduce the Extended model as an extension of the Baseline. The di↵erence from the
Baseline is that the Extended model aims for distributing campaigns of the same priority whilst
trying to fulﬁll the scope of all priorities. This extension entails a multiobjective optimization
problem (MOP) with two objectives, which we will get back to. Additionally, it means that we
have to modify and add some model parameters, decision variables, and constraints.

3.3.4
Multiobjective optimization
The Extended model should both aim to fulﬁll the scope of the priorities whilst distributing
campaigns of the same priority. These two aspects can be conﬂicting with each other, hence
the user has to choose how to prioritize them against each other. How the aspects should be
weighed can be determined using methods for MOPs. There are several methods for solving
a MOP. The solution methods are divided into two main categories generating methods and
preference based methods[16]. Generating methods try to ﬁnd the exact Pareto set, which is a
set representing all potential compromise solutions among the di↵erent objectives. The decision-
maker’s preferences are quantiﬁed by their selection of solutions from the Pareto set. These
methods are also referred to as a posteriori methods. Preference-based methods quantify the
decision-maker’s preferences prior to ﬁnding solutions and then try to ﬁnd the solutions that
best satisfy the deﬁned preferences. These methods are also referred to as a priori methods.
In this thesis, we consider a single-objective version of the problem where the objectives are
weighted by a known coeﬃcient, λ.

Solution methods
This section presents exact and heuristic approaches for solving OR problems. The focus is on
methods for solving the CPP. The CPP is a mixed integer linear programming (MILP) problem
since some but not all the decision variables are integers while all features are linear.
4.1
Exact methods
There are multiple examples of exact methods that can be used for ﬁnding exact solutions to
OR problems.
Cutting plane methods can be used for solving integer linear programming (ILP) problems. The
principle behind this approach is to iteratively add constraints (cutting planes), to the prob-
lem such that fractional solutions are removed from the solution space while integer solutions
24
==PAGE:32==
4
Solution methods
4.2
Metaheuristics
remain[18]. If it is possible to deﬁne a set of linear inequalities such that the corner points
of the solution space are integer, then the problem with the added constraints can be solved
using the simplex method. Decomposition methods can also be used to ﬁnd exact solutions to
many ILP problems. The idea behind decomposition methods is to divide the original problem
into smaller sub-problems and solve these separately either sequentially or in parallel. This is
often referred to as divide-and-conquer. As mentioned in Section 2, decomposition methods for
the MKP have previously been investigated and shown successful in combination with di↵erent
approaches such as dynamic programming for solving the subproblems. Since the CPP is a vari-
ant of the MMDKP, it is not straightforward to decompose the problem into single knapsack
problems.
A number of commercial and open-source optimization solvers have been developed over time
such as CPLEX[19], JuMP[20], Gurobi[21] and HiGHS[22]. These solvers apply multiple exact
and heuristic methods to obtain exact solutions faster. In this thesis, the Gurobi optimizer is
used for solving the CPP.
4.1.1
Gurobi
Gurobi is a commercial solver and is the fastest and most powerful mathematical programming
solver available for MILP problems and other OR problems[23]. MILP problems are generally
solved using the branch-and-bound algorithm, but in recent years presolve, cutting planes,
heuristics, and parallelism techniques have contributed to the solver.
First, the Gurobi optimizer applies its presolve algorithm to the model. The presolve algorithm
simpliﬁes the model by removing redundant constraints. The simpliﬁed model is passed to the
MILP optimizer as an initial solution. Gurobi will then apply a root relaxation simplex, in this
case, a dual simplex. After a solution to the root relaxation simplex is found, the branch-and-cut
tree search starts. The branch-and-cut method uses cutting planes that cuts o↵undesired (non-
integer) solutions[24]. This will tighten the problem further and has a huge, beneﬁcial e↵ect
on the process. During the process, Gurobi also performs heuristics on promising non-integer
solutions, in order to ﬁnd good incumbent nodes.
From the log that Gurobi provides, we can follow the search and the evolution of the solution.
We can see the objective of the current node, the best feasible solution found, and the gap.
Besides, it is stated whether the feasible solution is found by heuristic or by branching. Gurobi
is able to run in parallel, which means that the solving can be performed faster and more
eﬃciently for large search trees.
4.2
Metaheuristics
For computationally hard optimization problems, exact methods are challenged in solving the
problem within a reasonable time. For these problems, it is advantageous to use a heuristic
approach to ﬁnd solutions. A metaheuristic usually uses a construction heuristic that builds
a solution from scratch and an improvement heuristic that move from one solution to another
by searching in a neighborhood around the solution[25].
With a well-deﬁned neighborhood
25
==PAGE:33==
4
Solution methods
4.2
Metaheuristics
searching strategy, a heuristic can easily ﬁnd a local optimum.
In order to search more of
the solution space, a metaheuristic uses procedures that move the solution from one basin of
attraction to another. While metaheuristics do not provide optimal solution guarantees, exact
methods can not compete with the solution quality obtained by the leading metaheuristics
for problems with high levels of complexity[26]. Thus, metaheuristics are preferable in many
large-scale optimization problems where time and computational resources are limited.
Many metaheuristics are based on local search (LS), where a neighborhood function designed
for the speciﬁc problem is used to ﬁnd candidate solutions within the local neighbourhood[27].
A neighborhood function can be any kind of smaller change to the given solution. The LS
algorithm continuously searches the neighborhood of the current best solution until no further
improvements are found. In this way, the best solution within the basin of attraction of the
initial solution is obtained.
Therefore, LS on its own is often referred to as a hill-climber
approach that does not have any opportunity to escape local optima.
Metaheuristics that use LS therefore often incorporate some element that prevents being stuck
in local optima. One example is the iterated local search (ILS)[28]. In this metaheuristic, a
perturbation function that moves the solution to another basin of attraction is incorporated.
When LS reaches a local optimum, the perturbation is applied and LS is repeated. Another
example is the greedy randomized adaptive search procedure (GRASP)[29]. In this metaheuris-
tic, a greedy, randomized initial solution is generated every time LS reaches a local optimum.
GRASP is therefore a variation of ILS, where the perturbation is to create a new, greedy,
randomized solution.
These metaheuristics use one neighborhood function, and the performance of the metaheuristic
is therefore very dependent on the quality of this. The neighborhood function has to make
large enough changes to approach the local optimum within a reasonable time, but can’t be
too aggressive, as it in that case can risk missing the optimum. To generate a neighborhood
function of high quality for the CPP would be challenging. Standard neighborhood functions
include swapping items, but since the items in the CPP consume inventory in multiple time
steps, many of these swaps would potentially result in infeasible solutions. Furthermore, the
neighborhood function should also include features for searching through items that are not
already included in the solution.
Another ﬁeld of metaheuristics is evolutionary algorithms (EA), which are inspired by the sur-
vival of the ﬁttest principle[30]. The idea is to ﬁnd multiple solutions of high quality and combine
these to obtain even better solutions. An example of an EA is the genetic algorithm (GA)[31].
In the GA two parent-solutions are chosen from a population of solutions and combined to
generate children, which are then mutated slightly. The mutation is necessary to not create
inbreeding, where all the solutions, in the end, will be very close to identical. The children are
then added to the population of solutions in the next iteration - or generation - of the algorithm.
For the CPP, a natural division of a solution would be by looking at individual priorities or
at individual time steps. Then EA could be applied, and the subsolutions would be combined.
If the solutions were divided by the priorities, a challenge behind combining solutions would

In large neighborhood search (LNS), the search is based on iteratively destroying a part of the
solution and repairing the destroyed solution[32, 33]. This approach moves the solution in each
iteration and thereby decreases the risk of being trapped in local optima. The e↵ectiveness of
the LNS is very dependent on the choice of destroy and repair methods. A standard choice is to
include randomness in the destroy method and greediness in the repair method. However, the
combination of these methods has to be able to move the search around in the solution space.
The adaptive large neighborhood search (ALNS) is an extension of the LNS and was introduced
in 2006 by Pisinger and Røpke[34]. Instead of searching in one neighborhood like LNS, ALNS
adaptively searches multiple neighborhoods using several destroy- and repair methods. In this
thesis, we will implement an ALNS procedure for solving the CPP with a simulated annealing
framework. However, meta-analysis has shown that the adaptive layer does not always add
value to the solution[35]. Therefore we will also investigate if an LNS procedure results in the
same solution quality. The ALNS procedure will be further described in Section 4.3.

The data has been provided in multiple batches and in di↵erent formats and ﬁles. The majority

Parameter tuning
We have appointed seven parameters of ALNS that need tuning. As all the parameters are
used simultaneously, these values can be interdependent, hence a grid search would be the best
method to determine the optimal combination of values. However, with seven parameters and
a range of ﬁve values of each, this would result in 57 = 78, 125 combinations. Due to time
limitations, we will instead use an iterative approach. This involves ﬁxing all parameters except
one and evaluating the results of each parameter value. The process is illustrated in Figure 5.1.
We will start by selecting initial values for all parameters and a range for each. Then, we will
individually adjust each parameter over the range of values and determine the optimal value.
This value will then be set as the ﬁxed value in the next iteration. If the optimal value is at the
boundary of the range, the range will be modiﬁed in the next iteration to center it around the
chosen value. Ideally, this process should continue until all the parameter values have converged,
but due to time limitations, we carry out two iterations to select the ﬁnal parameter values.
The parameters we tune are deﬁned in Table 5.2. For each parameter, we tune using the six
training instances. We deﬁne N = 5 initial solutions for each training instance and run ALNS
for | ¯T| minutes for each initial solution. Thus, ALNS will be run 6 · 5 = 30 times for each
value of each parameter. Then we calculate an average percent improvement from the objective
value of the initial solution, and the standard deviation of the improvement for the N runs for
each training instance. To make fair comparisons, the same N initial solutions are used for all
values of each parameter for each data instance. The parameter resulting in the highest average
improvement is selected with the standard deviation being taken into consideration to ensure
that the objectives do not vary signiﬁcantly from run to run. If necessary, N may be increased
to avoid this. For the destroy fraction δ we assume one value for all of our methods. Ideally, we
would tune the destroy fraction for each destroy method separately, but since this would double
the number of parameters, we start by perceiving the destroy fractions as one parameter. Once
the primary tuning has been completed, we do a mini-tuning of the destroy fractions of the

Input solution sensitivity
The ALNS procedure takes an initial solution as input. To ensure that the quality of the output
solution doesn’t depend on the quality of the input solution, we test the sensitivity of the ALNS
procedure regarding the input solutions.
For each model type, Baseline and Extended, we have created three di↵erent input solutions
for each instance in the training data. The ﬁrst is an empty solution, where no campaigns are
scheduled. The next two are obtained by running the construction heuristic in Algorithm 2,
1,000 times, and storing the worst solution and the best solution for the respective model type.
These are denoted best initial and worst initial. For each training instance, ALNS has been run
on each of the initial solutions N = 5 times for | ¯T| minutes. Looking at Table 5.7, the average
output solution of the Baseline model is slightly better using a worst initial solution, while the
average output solution of the Extended model is slightly better using a best initial solution.
However, for almost half of the instances, it is the other way around. As this experiment does
not result in any indications that the quality of the solution from the construction heuristic
a↵ects the quality of the solution from ALNS, we can conclude that the ALNS procedure is not
sensitive to the input solution as long as it is not an empty solution.

Discussion and future work
Throughout this thesis, we have made a series of choices, simpliﬁcations, and assumptions to
formulate the models and obtain our results. In this section, we discuss some of these choices,
how they a↵ected the work, and which further studies could be taken into consideration.
One aspect to keep in mind is that all of the instances considered in this thesis originate from the
same data. Even though the instances vary in size and consumption density, the distributions
of the consumption and scope for each instance remain the same. I.e. if the scope of a priority
constitutes 10% of the total scope, it will do so in all the instances. This is by itself not a
problem, as the proposed solution method is designed speciﬁcally for DR. However, the reason
the instances are divided into test and training is to avoid overﬁtting, but since there isn’t more
variance in the instances, this can not be completely avoided if the models are only trained on
this data. To make the model more robust toward new inputs, the model could be trained on
random data instances generated by e.g. deﬁning new priorities. Since the aim of this project
is to create a tool tailored to handle the CPP at DR, this has not been a prioritization.
In general, it would be best practice to re-tune the model. The tuning of ALNS was carried out
when it consisted of all 13 heuristics. Since the ﬁnal two models ended up including two and
ﬁve heuristics respectively, the parameters are not optimized for the ﬁnal models. We believe
that the small segment size and the low remembering factor in the ALNS are the e↵ects of this.
With a small segment size, the probabilities in ALNS are updated frequently, and with a low
remembering factor the current solutions a↵ect the probabilities more. In combination, this
creates an ALNS heuristic, where the probabilities can converge very quickly. This can be an
advantage if the heuristic consists of methods that do not contribute positively to the solution
search, and therefore should be excluded from inﬂuence rapidly. If the ﬁnal model was tuned,
we expect the parameters to be di↵erent and we probably wouldn’t see the probabilities of
ALNS converge so quickly. Therefore, we also expect that even better results could be obtained
if the ﬁnal model had optimized parameters. If we selected the ﬁnal destroy and repair methods
before tuning the parameters, the results probably would be di↵erent. However, in that way, we
could have risked discarding some methods due to bad tuning and not due to bad performance.
Throughout this thesis, one of the main focuses has been on modeling the cost function of the
CPP. In Section 3.5 an analysis of the cost function has been carried out. In this section, it is
argued how the distribution term contributes to the schedules and at which cost. However, when
analyzing the computational results of the mathematical models, we encountered an aspect of
the problem that is not modeled in the cost function. In Figure 6.15 and Figure 6.16, we saw
how the distribution of the campaigns of priority Perspektiv (enk.)
on brand channel DR1
was di↵erent in the two solutions, even though the contribution to the objective values for this
priority were equal. For the reader, it might seem obvious that the distribution of this priority
on Figure 6.15 is preferred over the distribution on Figure 6.16. The question is, how would we
model this preference in the cost function? To do so, we have to quantify the preference, and it
can generally be very diﬃcult and sometimes even impossible to quantify human preferences.

In general, a lot of the parameters and assumptions should be discussed with a decision-maker
if the tools should go into production. An example hereof is the freelance budget. In this thesis,
we have assumed that DR has set aside a ﬁxed part of their yearly budget for freelancers. It
could just as well be that the budget is unlimited, which DR’s campaign plan indicates, but
that the number of freelancers should be minimized. In this case, it should be modeled as part
of the cost function. It could also be the case that there is a maximum budget but the amount
of freelance should still be minimized within this budget. This is an example of how important
it is to communicate with the users of the product.
Another aspect of the objective function that could be reconsidered is the scope term. In this
thesis, we assume that reaching the scope of the priorities is equally important, except for the
priorities that are deﬁned as crucial. For the other priorities, this means that if one priority
has a scope of two, while another one has a scope of 100, one campaign of the ﬁrst priority
is equally important as 50 campaigns of the second priority. This might not be the right way
to balance the priorities in the objective. Therefore, we would either need a qualitative and
manual ranking of the priorities from a decision-maker or incorporate other features than the
scope of a priority into the penalty, e.g. the channel consumption.
In addition to this, we have assumed that the maximum number of campaigns of each priority
are those in scope. As each campaign promotes a piece of content, we suppose that the total
scope of the priorities is an estimate of how many pieces of content DR expects to broadcast
during the year. Since it is not possible to promote a non-existing piece of content we therefore,
consider this assumption plausible. However, we cannot be sure if this connection is correct,
and there might be cases where exceeding the scope is beneﬁcial. In Section 7, we saw that DR
scheduled campaigns out of scope in 2021. If the formulation should contain this aspect, it needs
several modiﬁcations. First of all, the cost function should be reconsidered. The cost function
only rewards scheduling campaigns within scope. Additionally, the cost function is penalized
if the minimum idle time is smaller than the ideal idle time and if the ideal maximum number
of campaigns scheduled concurrently is exceeded. The ideal idle time and the ideal maximum
number of campaigns scheduled concurrently are deﬁned based on the scope. Hence, if the scope
is exceeded, at least one part of the distribution term will be penalized in the objective. Second
of all, the dimensions of the x−variable in the Extended formulation have to be reconsidered.
As of now, the scope of a priority determines the size of the third dimension. If we were to keep
the same structure of the variable, the size of the third dimension should be limited by some
number larger than the scope. Therefore, a method for deciding a limitation for each priority
should be considered.
An additional aspect that is not considered in this thesis is to allow partial campaigns or indi-
vidual advertisements. In real life, we imagine that a campaign planner would rather produce
and broadcast half of a campaign than no campaign at all. Again, modeling this preference
requires a closer dialogue with DR. Can we assume that two campaigns downscaled by 50%
are equally good as one full campaign? Is there a minimum threshold for the downscaling of
a campaign such that it still has the desired e↵ect? Can the production time be scaled in the
same way, or will a production team always need a ﬁxed amount of time to start up production?

These are all questions that should be discussed and agreed upon with a potential user of the
model. However, allowing partial campaigns would, besides the above considerations, require a
reformulation of the existing model and an introduction of continuous decision variables. This
would relax the original problem and make it easier for the solver to optimize.
Something that could have been considered as well, is to create a separate construction heuristic
for each of the two problem formulations. In the presented construction heuristic, the priorities
are sorted by their scope penalty and scheduled at random time steps. Dependent on how the
available inventory is positioned, this will result in an objective around the same value for the
Baseline model in every construction as long as all priorities in ¯P are scheduled. However for
the Extended model, the positions of the campaigns matter. Thus, it would be interesting to
consider a semi-random construction heuristic that also considers the position of campaigns of
the same priority. However, in Section 5.3 we examined the input solution sensitivity of ALNS.
From the results, we saw that even though the ALNS solving the Extended model gets a poor
initial solution, it is still able to obtain just as good objectives as if it were given a good initial
solution when running for | ¯T| minutes.
This indicates that constructing an initial solution
ﬁtted for the Extended model, would not have inﬂuenced the results majorly. However, this
experiment has not been carried out on the ﬁnal LNS and ALNS, which means that we can not
reject it completely.
As mentioned, a large challenge when ﬁnding appropriate solution methods to the CPP is the
fact that it in its extended form is diﬃcult to divide into meaningful subproblems.
In the
HorizontalRepair heuristic, the campaign schedule of one priority at a time is optimized. The
limitation of this approach is that the inventory is quickly utilized by the priorities that are
considered ﬁrst.
However, it would be possible to decompose the problem in this way and
deﬁne a limit for the amount of the total inventory each priority can consume.
By doing
so, HorizontalRepair could be run iteratively, which would result in a heuristic decomposition
method. In this way, the subproblems can be solved separately, hence greatly decreasing the
complexity of the Extended formulation of the CPP.
In Section 5.5, the experiments showed that FlexibilityRepair and RegretRepair do not perform
well, even though they are based on well-known methods that are proven eﬃcient for other
problems. A reason for this could be that they are normally used when solving the TSP[42],
which in its original form has to visit all cities.
In the CPP, it is not a requirement that
all campaigns in scope should be scheduled, and scheduling a campaign can both worsen and
improve the objective value.
Therefore, if campaigns are expensive to plan it will often be
beneﬁcial to disregard them.
The idea behind a FlexibilityRepair heuristic is to repair the
solution by considering the cities that have the worst impact on the objective ﬁrst, such that
this impact is minimized. In the CPP, the expensive cities are translated to the campaigns
that can be scheduled in the fewest positions. However, the objective is not considered when
scheduling the least ﬂexible campaign, which means that we in practice could end up worsening
the objective. Because of this and the fact that we do not have to schedule all campaigns in
scope, the best action in some cases would be to disregard scheduling the least ﬂexible campaign.
The general idea behind a RegretRepair heuristic is to evaluate the actions by looking one step
78
==PAGE:86==
8
Discussion and future work
ahead. Thus, the heuristic prioritizes performing actions that it will later regret not having
performed. In the CPP, the RegretRepair ﬁnds the di↵erence in objective between scheduling
two campaigns of a priority one by one against scheduling two campaigns of a priority at once.
It then prefers to schedule campaigns of the priority where this di↵erence is largest. However
similar to FlexibilityRepair, the scope term of the objective is not considered. Therefore, this
heuristic might schedule campaigns that are not beneﬁcial for the overall objective - no matter
if they are inserted one by one or at once.
Finally, we have to keep in mind that the inventories and consumptions in the CPP at DR
are based on estimates. In other problems as the shortest path problems and job allocation
problems, the data is generally associated with little or no uncertainty. However, in problems
such as the CPP where human performance has an inﬂuence the uncertainty is higher and needs
to be considered before being applied to a real-life problem.
The media inventories and consumptions in the CPP are measured in production hours. DR
has estimated how many man-hours it takes to produce a campaign of each priority, and we
have assumed that the media inventory is constant throughout the year. However, we know that
there is a great chance that neither of these estimates will hold in reality. Some campaigns might
take longer to produce than estimated because the producers are ineﬃcient in that production
period, while other campaigns might be produced quicker because the producers suddenly are
super eﬃcient. Likewise, the media inventories are also just estimates. Employees can quit
their jobs, become sick, go on vacation, or suddenly start working overtime, which are all
factors that inﬂuence the inventories. A way to get around some of these insecurities could be
to include stochastic elements in the formulation, which would make the model more robust
towards sudden changes in the data. Another suggestion could be to use a time series model
that predicts in which periods people are absent from work due to sickness, or in which periods
people are most likely to go on vacations. These are solution suggestions outside the scope of
this thesis.
The channel inventory and consumption are also just estimates. As both are measured in GRP,
and since DR for good reasons can’t know the number of viewers, and therefore impressions,
in advance, these must be based on historical data. To avoid these insecurities, the channel
inventories and consumptions could be measured in a more quantitative unit such as seconds
or minutes.
It is important that a potential user of the model knows its limitations. The model generated
in this thesis can create a starting point for a campaign plan but the resulting schedules should
naturally be analyzed further by the campaign planner of the speciﬁc problem.

Conclusions
9
Conclusions
In this thesis, we model and optimize the CPP and provide insight into and benchmark the
performance of the current scheduling approach at DR. To do so, data from DR has been
collected and processed to deﬁne a set of instances of di↵erent sizes, which are split into a test-
and training set. The CPP has been put in KP context, and solution methods for variations of
the KP have been examined.
We have formulated a simple and extended variation of the CPP as minimization problems.
After a thorough analysis of the cost function and the obtained results using a commercial
solver, it is concluded that only the Extended formulation is suﬃcient for solving the CPP at
DR. When the problem size increases, the commercial solver is challenged and the obtained
solutions come with a gap of more than 20%.
An ALNS approach with a simulated annealing framework is implemented, and the heuristic
design is determined by a set of experiments. The ﬁnal parameters of the ALNS are determined
by an iterative tuning approach. Six destroy- and seven repair heuristics are developed, and a
set of meaningful conﬁgurations consisting of subsets of these are generated. The performances
of the conﬁgurations show that an LNS approach performs best on small and medium sized
instances, while an ALNS including only two of each heuristic results in the best method for
large sized instances. A comparison of the metaheuristic and the commercial solver is conducted
with respect to solution quality and running time on instances of di↵erent sizes. For small and
medium sized instances, the LNS in best cases produces solutions of comparable quality. On
average, the solutions di↵er in quality by at most 3.53%. For large instances, ALNS outperforms
the commercial solver; the improvement in solution quality is 6.17-7.4% on average, and up
to 11.54% in the best case. Initial solutions are found within 0.02 seconds using the proposed
construction heuristic, while the solver requires 120 seconds to provide solutions that we perceive
as feasible. In the worst case, the solver fails to obtain feasible solutions within 300 seconds.
Tools for solution validation that quickly allows for identiﬁcation of resource bottlenecks are
generated along with a tool for visualization of campaign plans. These are used to examine
DR’s manually generated campaign plan from 2021, in which we conclude that the demanded
campaigns are impossible to accomplish given the data. By applying an optimization approach
to the CPP at DR with modiﬁed data, the objective of the current manual campaign plan is
reduced by 44%. If the proposed method and tools should go into production, it is necessary to
revisit the simpliﬁcations and assumptions made throughout this work. Primarily, the prefer-
ences of the model deﬁned in the cost function should be considered, advised by a decision-maker
with context knowledge. However, being able to quickly provide optimized schedules to complex
planning problems that comply with business requirements is extremely valuable in practice in
many di↵erent areas.
