Abstract 
 
Microalga–bacterium consortia can sustain CO2 biocapture at elevated gas loadings 
when reactor hydrodynamics and conditional parameters for the growth of the 
microorganisms are optimized. In the current study, a Chlorella sorokiniana–Bacillus 
subtilis coculture was developed on a shared Modified Form Medium (MFM) and a 
modestly enriched variant (MFM+), then evaluated in flasks, sealed bottles, and 
upscaled into a 400 mL flat-panel photobioreactor (PBR) under high-CO2 loading. 
Bottle trials mapped a practical window and 15% CO2 outperforming 30%, consistent 
with pH/Dissolved Inorganic Carbon (DIC) limitations at stronger sparging. In contrast, 
the coculture in the flat-panel PBR converted higher CO2 into sustained capture: in 
MFM+ at 20% CO2 biofixation reached 0.763 g/L/day, compared to 0.108 g/L/day for 
the C. sorokiniana monoculture at the same CO2 level. Simultaneously, rising 
fluorescence, early and constant pH (7.3–7.4 in coculture vs. 5.8–6.1 in monoculture), 
and a transient extracellular  Carbonic Anhydrase (CA) activity support a mechanism 
in which improved gas–liquid transfer and boundary-layer acceleration of CO2→HCO3- 
stabilize DIC supply under high CO2 loading. Together, the results show that pairing 
flat-panel hydrodynamics with a CA-active partner turns conditions that typically inhibit 
chlorophytes into net improvements in carbon capture. Designed guidelines include 
using MFM+, operating within a 15–20% CO2 range only with deliberate pH and 
Volumetric Mass Transfer Coefficient (kLa) control, and exploit algal–bacterial 
complementarity. These findings provide quantitative guidance for scaling consortium 
of algal–bacterial for industrial CO2 biofixation.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1 Global CO2 emissions and Climate change 
Ever since the late 18th century, global emissions of carbon dioxide have had a rapidly 
upward trend, at a rapidly accelerating rate every decade, especially after 1950. The 
world emissions have been recorded in the latest-year data as having reached 6 billion 
tons per year, as opposed to the quadrupled base of 1990, and emissions had climbed 
to levels of over 36 billion tons in 2022 [1]. In contrast, emissions were restrained and 
relatively stable during the pre-industrial period, which starkly reveals the gigantic 
contribution of energy consumption and industrialization to this sharp rise. 
 
During the initial phases of the Industrial Revolution, Europe and North America were 
major emitters of carbon dioxide (CO2), and Europe and the United States alone were 
responsible for over 90% of emissions until the early 1900s [2]. In the past few 
decades, emissions have become less regionally concentrated, and Asia, especially 
China and India, have become the major contributors [3].The increase in emissions 
has been mirrored by a worrying rise in atmospheric CO2 concentrations. Data from 
the longest-running continuous CO2 measurements at the Mauna Loa Observatory, 
which initiated in 1958, show atmospheric CO2 increased from 315 ppm to over 420 
ppm. The annual increment has jumped from around 0.8 ppm/year during the 1960s 
to over 2.6 ppm/year in the last decade, which corresponds to 200 times faster than 
natural post-glacial rates [4]. 
 
Increased CO₂ intensifies the greenhouse effect, trap heat, and disturb the energy 
equilibrium of the Earth. The consequence includes enhanced global warming, melting 
of ice on land and in the ocean, higher sea levels, altered rainfall patterns, unreliable 
ecosystems, and higher extreme weather event frequencies. The implications affect 
agricultural productivity, biodiversity, atmospheric and water qualities, public health, 
and security. A ton of CO₂ emitted nowadays generates future direct warming, which 
stresses the urgency of reducing emissions [5]. 
 
1.2 Current mitigation strategies and their limitations 
Many climate mitigation strategies include afforestation and reforestation, as they can 
sequester vast quantities of CO₂ in biomass and soils. Modeling shows that large-
scale planting of trees, if done in the right places, could pull off hundreds of gigatons 
of carbon during this century [6]. That said, afforestation has trade-offs; shifting arable 
lands could replace food production, which can compromise food security [7], and 
other environmental problems, such as habitat disruption, biodiversity modification, 
soil nutrient removal, and surface modification, particularly at high-latitude locations 
[8]. Lastly, long-term carbon storage has the added risks of fire, disease, and land 
occupancy insecurity [9]. Thus, afforestation since involves the establishing of forest 
on land that has not contained forest, must be carefully planned, taking into account 
regional climate, soils, and ecological suitability, and backstopping socioeconomic 
protection measures [10]. 
 
==PAGE:14==
 
2 
Scaling up renewable energy technologies, such as solar and wind power, is essential 
in reducing CO₂ emissions. Execution, however, has issues of infrastructural deficit, 
supply chain bottlenecks, and intermittency of the latter technologies. On a yearly 
basis, installations continue to rise higher but still require high-volume growth, 
especially in energy storage, to reduce dependency on fossil fuels. Energy efficiency 
upgrades similarly help in reducing emissions but has limits in the form of recover 
effects and sectoral issues, which mean decarbonization alone could be insufficient to 
fill the emission gap [11]. 
 
Engineered carbon capture includes a family of technologies to actively capture CO₂ 
from concentrated emission streams or from the ambient atmosphere to lower 
emissions from renewable energy installations and enhance efficiency [12]. These 
technologies: Direct Air Capture (DAC), Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS), and 
Carbon Capture and Utilization (CCU) are often proposed as possible connecting 
options to reach net-zero and net-negative emissions of greenhouse gases [13]. 
However, they all have unique technical, economic, and environmental issues that 
prevent extensive implementation. 
 
1.2.1 Direct Air Capture (DAC) 
One technology to actively capture CO2 is DAC, which functions by blowing ambient 
air through chemical sorbents, in solid (e.g., functional resins) or liquid (e.g., alkaline 
solutions) forms, which selectively capture CO₂. When saturated, sorbents are 
regenerated via thermal, pressure, or electrochemical methods, releasing 
concentrated CO₂ for storage or use [14]. Spatial flexibility of DAC is unique, plants 
can be located independently of emissions sources and proximate to geologic storage 
reservoirs; however, the intrinsic thermodynamic cost of CO₂ capture from dilute air 
(420 ppm) causes energy demand to be high. Present-scale DAC installations require 
between 1.8 and 8 GJ of heat and 0.5–2 MWh of power, respectively, per ton of CO₂ 
removed, based on the specific technology pathway [15]. Cost continues to be too 
high, at an estimated range of USD 500–1,000/t for small-scale installations, but it is 
possible mid-century reductions to $100–200/t through economies of scale and 
process improvements [16]. Environmental life-cycle analyses indicate that, if heat or 
power derived from fossils is continuously employed, net removal of CO2 would not be 
significantly [17]. 
 
1.2.2 Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) Technology 
There is also, CCS that focuses on CO₂ capture at large point sources, including coal 
and gas power stations, cement furnaces, and steel refines, where the flue gases have 
CO₂ concentrations of 4–15%, much higher than in ambient air. The most developed 
CCS route is post-combustion capture, that uses as a rule amine-based solvents to 
chemically capture CO₂, which can be then stripped off by heat. Oxy-fuel combustion, 
which generates a CO₂ rich in flue gas, and pre-combustion capture in gasification 
power generation are other technologies [18]. Separated CO₂, after compression, 
could be fed into deep geologic formations, such as depleted petroleum and gas 
reservoirs or deep saline aquifers. After three to four decades of pilot and 
demonstration projects, full-scale deployment of CCS on a global basis has so far 
been restricted to less than 40 full-scale installations, which capture roughly 45 Mt of 
CO₂/equivalent annually, much less than the several gigatons of CO₂-eq necessary to 
==PAGE:15==
 
3 
have a significant impact on climate change [19]. Technically, problems include 
degradation of solvents, energy penalties of 15–30% on host power stations, and 
leakage risks from storage locations, though leakage risks in general are assumed to 
be low if the storage locations and monitoring plans are properly selected and carried 
out [20]. Economically, the cost of CCS ranges from USD 50 to USD 150/t for industrial 
applications, which is lowest in gas processing and highest in diluted flue gas streams. 
 
1.2.3 Carbon Capture and Utilization (CCU) 
On the other hand, CCU seeks to convert captured CO₂ to useful products, including 
fuels, chemicals, or construction materials, thereby establishing possible revenue 
streams to offset capture costs [14]. Utilization paths vary; mineralization processes 
calcine CO₂ with aluminosilicate materials to generate carbonates for cement or 
aggregate, chemical synthesis paths generate methanol or hydrocarbons, whereas 
biological paths use microalgae or bioengineered microorganisms to convert CO₂ to 
biomass or bio-products. The climate advantage of CCU, however, is depending on 
product lifetime; fuel or short-lived chemicals release CO₂ to the atmosphere upon 
combustion or degradation, restricting the net removal to transient storage [14]. 
Products of longer lifetime, like carbonates, provide firmer sequestration, but they 
remain restricted by market volume and feedstock supply. Moreover, numerous CCU 
processes require significant energy, so the net climate advantage centers on 
deployment of low-carbon energy. 
 
In general, large-scale engineered carbon capture technologies remains limited due 
to high costs, energy intensiveness, and low deployment rates. Meaning that, without 
radical innovation and policy incentives, would not be able to compensate for more 
than a fraction of today’s emissions in the coming two or three decades. 
 
Cumulative experience of techno-economic evaluations and real world implementation 
reveals that engineered carbon capture, though promising in some niche applications, 
could not be the chief support of overall decarbonization plans [21]. Rather, such 
technologies could be perceived as complementary solutions directed to sectors for 
which there are very little alternatives, such as cement, steel, and aviation, wherein 
process emissions are inherent and remaining emissions should be compensated in 
order to reach net-zero targets. 
 
From a systemic perspective, over dependance on CCS, DAC, or CCU could delay 
the necessary near-term deployment of low-carbon substitutes via the creation of the 
illusion that future negative emissions would compensate for today’s emissions 
(“mitigation deterrence”) [22]. The retrospective analysis has found that many of the 
CCS projects were initiated largely to enable enhanced oil recovery (EOR), literally 
extending fossil fuel uses rather than closing them [22]. That has big issues of 
governance and distributive justice, particularly in relation to the public subsidies 
regularly used to promote such projects. 
 
Policy frameworks will be critical to determining the future role of engineered capture 
in the coming decades. Shaped regulations can tip the scales in favor of permanent 
storage rather than EOR, push cost reduction through public–private R&D 
partnerships and ensure that deployment is powered by renewable energy. 
Simultaneously, research on biological and hybrid, bioengineered approaches, such 
==PAGE:16==
 
4 
as microbial CO₂ fixation, can offer lower-cost, lower-energy alternatives to be 
deployed on a broader basis, particularly in cases where land availability or 
environmental impact limits nature-based alternatives. 
 
While engineered carbon capture is unlikely to be the “silver bullet” for climate 
mitigation, it remains an important component of a diversified portfolio that includes 
aggressive emission reductions, renewable energy expansion, nature-based 
sequestration, and bioengineered CO₂ removal. The challenge lies in aligning 
technological development with climate justice principles, economic feasibility, and 
urgency dictated by the planetary carbon budget. 
 
1.3 CO2 Fixation pathways 
Atmospheric CO₂ constitutes a multitude of fixation pathways, which can be roughly 
divided into geochemical, artificial catalytic, and biological methods. The pathways 
vary in reaction kinetics, energy requirements, scalability, and co-product opportunities 
but, in all cases, involve the end goal of converting CO₂ as a waste product into a 
stable or useful material [23]. To achieve this, it is necessary to comprehend these 
pathways to be able to assess incorporation into large-scale carbon-management 
plans. 
 
1.3.1 Geochemical fixation 
First of all, geochemical fixation defines the conversion of CO₂ into stable carbonate 
minerals through naturally occurring or artificially facilitated reactions. Mineral 
carbonation, for instance, takes advantage of the thermodynamically favorable 
reaction between CO₂ and alkaline earth metal oxides, commonly found in silicate 
minerals like olivine ((Mg, Fe)2SiO4) or serpentine (Mg3Si2O5(OH)4), to precipitate solid 
carbonates such as magnesite (MgCO3) and calcite (CaCO3) [24]. In situ mineral 
carbonation has been shown to be possible in ultramafic rock formations, for instance, 
in the ultramafic rock formations employed in the CarbFix project in Iceland, where 
injected CO2 reacts with basaltic rock and precipitates carbonates in two years [24]. 
Even though mineral carbonation provides permanent storage of CO2, large-scale 
implementation of the method suffers from limitations, which include slow natural rates 
of reaction, high energy input in the mining and grinding of minerals, and high volume 
requirements of waters to aid reactions [23]. 
 
1.3.2 Artificial catalysis 
On the other hand, artificial catalytic pathways for CO2 reduction to value added 
products use electrocatalytic, photocatalytic, or thermocatalytic protocols. First, 
CO2RR (electrochemical CO2 reduction) has been significantly studied because of its 
promising ability to convert CO2 to fuel and chemicals, such as formic acid, methanol, 
carbon monoxide, and ethylene, from renewable electricity [25]. The creation of 
catalysts, particularly nanostructured metals, such as copper, silver, and gold, has 
facilitated greater selectivity and reduced overpotential [25]. Secondly, photocatalytic 
technologies using semiconductor compounds, such as TiO2 or g-C3N4, try to connect 
solar energy directly to drive CO₂ conversion, but efficiencies are too low for industrial 
applications [25]. Thirdly, thermocatalytic CO2 hydrogenation, particularly via the 
Sabatier reaction (CO2 + 4H2 → CH4 + 2H2O), already has niche closed-loop 
==PAGE:17==
 
5 
applications, such as the International Space Station [25]. Nevertheless, technologies 
founded on artificial catalysis in general require high-purity CO2, high energy inputs, 
and complex reactor infrastructures, which restrict scalability. 
 
1.3.3 Biological fixation 
The third CO2 fixation pathway is the biological, which is facilitated by autotrophic 
organisms that have the ability to sequester inorganic carbon into organic matter 
through enzymatic mechanisms. The most common mechanism is oxygenic 
photosynthesis, performed by plants, algae, and cyanobacteria, which employs light 
energy to power the Calvin–Benson–Bassham (CBB) cycle mediated by ribulose-1,5-
bisphosphate 
carboxylase/oxygenase 
(RuBisCO)

[26]. 
Besides 
photoautotrophs, other chemoautotrophic microorganisms fix CO2 based on chemical 
energy from inorganic compounds, for example, the reductive tricarboxylic acid (rTCA) 
cycle, Wood–Ljungdahl pathway, or 3-hydroxypropionate cycle [27]. The benefit of 
biological fixation lies in its ability to operate at moderate conditions, and in many 
cases, generates biomass of possible industrial uses in biofuels, animal feed, and 
bioplastics [27]. Efficiency, however, remains in many cases limited by kinetic 
constraints of RuBisCO, photon availability, and nutrient supply. More recent research 
has shown the use of carbon-concentrating mechanisms (CCMs) and carbonic 
anhydrase (CA) enzymes to increase CO2 uptake and capture rates, making microbial 
and algal systems of greater importance for integrated carbon capture and utilization 
(CCU) concepts [26].

1.3.4 Comparative overview 
A comparative evaluation of the CO2 fixation pathways pointed to the trade-offs in 
permanence, scalability, and economic viability. Geochemical fixation provides 
incomparable long-term permanence but has slow kinetics and stringent material 
handling. Artificial catalysis can be used to convert to valuable products but at present 
experiences high energy input and pure CO2 streams. Biological fixation can be 
conducted at ambient conditions and has renewable biomass output, but optimization 
must be achieved to reach the storage permanence of the geochemical pathways. 
Hybrid systems, for instance, coupling of the mineral carbonation process and 
microbial biomass production or integration of photosynthetic organisms and 
electrocatalytic CO2 reduction, hold promising directions for increased efficiency in 
carbon capture and generation of co-benefits [23]. 
 
1.4 Microalgae (MA) in CO2 biofixation 
1.4.1 Overview of microalgal diversity and physiology 
Microalgae include a taxonomically and physiologically diverse assemblage of 
unicellular photosynthetic microorganisms responsible for much of the world’s primary 
production and carbon cycling. Capable of converting light energy into chemical 
energy in oxygenic photosynthesis, they fix carbon dioxide into organic matter and 
release molecular oxygen activity which underlies aquatic food webs and atmospheric 
composition [29]. Spread into a number of phylogenetic divisions, including 
Chlorophyta 
(green

Physiological variation is equally important. The chloroplasts contain chlorophyll a, 
which is found in all oxygenic phototrophs, and accessory pigments, such as 
chlorophyll b in green algae, carotenoids in the vast majority of taxa, and the 
cyanobacterium-specific phycobiliproteins. The pigment complement yields an ability 
to absorb wide spectra of light, enabling photosynthesis in fluctuating or weak light 
[28]. Significantly, microalgae show metabolic plasticity that allows photoautotrophic, 
heterotrophic, or mixotrophic lifestyles in harmony with environmental conditions, and 
the capability of alternating inorganic and organic carbon sources. The versatility, in 
conjunction with high biomass accumulation rates doubling biomass within as little as 
24 hours in some species makes them promising candidates for industrial applications 
such as CO2 capture, wastewater treatment, biofuel production, and production of 
high-value biochemicals [30]. 
 
The different trophic modes in microalgae cultivation function on major metabolic 
strategies: photoautotrophy, heterotrophy, and mixotrophy, each characterized by its 
energy and carbon acquisition mechanisms as can be observed in Figure 2, but 
numerous species can shift between modes based on light levels, nutrient status, and 
environmental stresses. In photoautotrophy, light energy powers the photolysis of 
water in the thylakoid membranes, which generates adenosine triphosphate (ATP) and 
==PAGE:19==
 
7 
nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) to power the Calvin–Benson 
cycle to fix CO2 into sugars (Figure 1). It is the most sustainable and ecologically 
dominant mode, but efficiency of photoautotrophy is limited by factors as light 
constraint in high-density cultures, suboptimal photoperiod, or environmental stress 
[28]. 
 
Heterotrophy, on the other hand, allows microalgae to thrive in the dark through the 
catabolism of organic carbon substrates like glucose, acetate, or glycerol through 
glycolysis, the tricarboxylic acid cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation. It allows high 
cell densities in dark bioreactors and can boost the production of target metabolites of 
interest such as lipids during nitrogen limitation, but at the cost of higher operational 
costs and stringent contamination control [31]. 
 
Mixotrophy integrates these two paths, enabling simultaneous organic carbon 
assimilation and photosynthetic CO2 fixation. In this two-mode system, recycled CO2 
from respiration is fed into photosynthesis, and O2 produced in photosynthesis 
sustains oxidative metabolism, leading to cooperative increases in growth and product 
yields [31]. Mixotrophy frequently exceeds both modes in productivity and can harness 
multiple resource flows, for example, CO2-rich flue gas and wastewater rich in organic 
matter [30].

1.4.2 Microalgae advantages over plants 
Microalgae possess several intrinsic competitive advantages over plants for CO2 
biofixation, that make them of great interest for industrial carbon capture applications. 
Rates of photosynthetic CO2 capture for microalgae generally fall in the range of 10 to 
50 times higher on a unit area basis, due to mainly higher light conversion efficacies 
and zero energy input to non-photosynthetic structural biomass like stems, roots, or 
woody tissue [28]. Much less cultivation area, then, must be used to obtain comparable 
==PAGE:20==
 
8 
CO2 capture rates, and so microalgae become greatly suited to adoption for land-
limited, urban-industrial settings. 
 
A major advantage of microalgae over land plants is that they can be cultured on non-
arable soils using wastewaters, brackish waters, or seawaters, and thereby avoid 
direct competition in arable soils and freshwater resources for agriculture [28]. Most of 
the strains tolerate 15% concentrated CO2 streams, which are concentrations typical 
of industrial flue gases, without growth inhibition that high-CO2 environments typically 
causes on terrestrial plants [32]. 
 
Moreover, microalgal production units like photobioreactors (PBRs) and open raceway 
ponds can be tightly regulated for temperature, pH, nutrient levels, and light exposure, 
which allows for continuous, persistent production and high areal productivities [28]. 
The metabolic versatility of microalgae increases their industrial applications as well: 
numerous species offer high yields of lipids and carbohydrates for biofuel production, 
whereas other species sequester proteins, pigments, and bioactive compounds useful 
in the food, feed, and pharmaceutical industries [31]. Such complementary features: 
high CO2 fixation efficiency, low land and freshwater requirements, versatility of 
industrial CO2 streams, and cultivation in controlled environments, grant microalgae a 
distinctive edge over terrestrial higher plants in the context of large-scale, sustainably 
based carbon reduction scenarios. 
 
1.4.3 Chlorella sorokiniana as a model of Microalgae species 
Chlorella sorokiniana has become the premier species of microalgae in industrial 
biotechnology due to its robustness, high-rate growth, and metabolic versatility. It is a 
unicellular, immobile green alga, 3–8 µm in diameter, possessing a thick cell wall which 
offers protection against fluctuating environment conditions. It can sustain growth at 
CO2 levels higher than 10% and hence can be suited for direct exploitation of industrial 
flue gases where many other species are not [32]. Physiologically, C. sorokiniana 
possesses high chlorophyll a and b contents, high efficiency of RuBisCO, and an 
efficient carbon concentrating mechanism, which is mediated by active bicarbonate 
transporters and carbonic anhydrases to allow sustained photosynthetic ability in 
limited CO2 or rich O2 environments [32]. It contains a biomass of 50% protein, 20 to 
30% lipid, and 10 to 20% carbohydrate (dry weight), which makes it suitable for biofuel 
production, animal feed, and nutraceutical applications [33]. 
 
Under mixotrophic growth on enriched acetate agro-industrial wastewaters, biomass 
yields of greater than 11 g/L and close to 38% of lipid content are achieved for 
C. sorokiniana, while oxygen-balanced mixotrophy has doubled productivity of 
biomass in the absence of external aeration [33]. Under heterotrophic growth, dark 
fermenters can be run to cultivate the strain on volatile fatty acids or glycerol and 
achieve high-density growth at high lipid or protein content in optimized nutrient media 
[33]. Such versatility makes C. sorokiniana particularly valuable for integrated CO2 
removal and resource recovery technologies. 
 
1.4.4 Microalgae in industrial flue gas capture 
Integration of microalgae into industrial flue gas capture allows a biologically 
controlled, energy-saving alternative to intensive energy chemical scrubbing. In such 
==PAGE:21==
 
9 
technologies, rich CO2 flue gases typically containing 5 to 15% CO2, which is 
supplemented by NOₓ, SOₓ, and particulates bubbled through growth vessels in which 
tolerant microalgae sequester the CO2 in biomass, releasing O2 [34]. Flue gas can be 
toxic to a considerable range of species, but C. sorokiniana can be tolerant of high 
CO2 and contamination if operated in mixotrophic mode on supplemented organic 
carbon from agro-industrial waste streams [32]. 
 
Field tests have shown that in-plant photobioreactors co-located at coal power stations 
can reach CO2 fixations of over 1.5 g/L/day, and lifecycle analyses show that, when 
harvested biomass is converted into fuels or long-lived products, such systems can 
have net negative carbon balances [34]. Co-location of the bioreactor to the power 
station also allows waste heat to be recovered to permit temperature control and to 
use power station effluents as nutrient supply, which improves cost competitiveness 
and sustainability [34]. Oxygen-balanced mixotrophy, in addition, optimizes efficiency 
through carbon dioxide recirculation of respiratory CO2 to photosynthesis and 
minimization of aeration requirements [30]. 
 
Nevertheless promising, the technology has issues of high energy demand in 
harvesting and dewatering, long-term culture volatility, and scalability limitations. 
Current research addresses strain engineering, hybrid cultivation systems, and 
integration of the process with industry infrastructure to enhance performance and 
economy [28]. 
 
1.4.5 Conceptual framework for industrial deployment 
A robust industrial deployment of microalgal CO2 capture is based on five interlinked 
pillars: species selection, metabolic optimization, system integration, product 
valorization, and policy alignment. Strain selection is highest priority, and strain 
C. sorokiniana has emerged as a suitable candidate on the basis of CO2 tolerance, 
metabolic versatility, and high-value biochemical constitution [28]. Metabolic modes 
should be optimized in a dynamic manner: photoautotrophy during high-light CO2-rich 
phases, heterotrophy in low-light closed recirculatory systems, and mixotrophy if CO2 
and organic waste streams become available concurrently. Integration with industrial 
installations allows direct CO2 supply, nutrient input from wastewater, and temperature 
control from waste heat, decreasing costs and environmental effects [28]. Biomass 
valorization via biorefinery strategies can provide fuels, feedstuffs, and specific 
products, offsetting operating costs and promoting economic sustainability [31]. Lastly, 
stringent life-cycle analysis guarantees true net negative emissions, and policy 
incentives like carbon credits, renewable energy incentives, and waste valorization 
policies, can boost adoption and scalability [28]. When integrated, they create a 
circular bioeconomy model where industrial CO2 and organic wastes become valuable 
products through culturing microalgae, closing material cycles and preventing 
greenhouse gas emissions concurrently. 
 
1.5 Carbonic Anhydrase enriched Bacteria (CAB) 
1.5.1 Biology and industrial relevance of Bacillus subtilis 
Bacillus subtilis is a rod-shaped, Gram-positive, endospore forming member of the 
Firmicutes. It is facultatively aerobic, capable of growth under both oxic and anoxic 
==PAGE:22==
 
10 
conditions, and survives severe environmental stresses via the formation of dormant 
endospores [35]. This resilience allows B. subtilis to persist through nutrient limitation, 
desiccation, and fluctuating gas regimes, supported by the unique protective 
structures of its spore coat [36]. In natural settings, it contributes to nutrient cycling by 
degrading complex polymers including cellulose, hemicellulose, proteins, and starch, 
thereby releasing bioavailable nutrients that support other microorganisms, including 
microalgae [37]. 
 
From a biotechnological standpoint, B. subtilis is one of the best characterized 
bacterial platforms: its genome was among the earliest to be sequenced [38], and an 
extensive genetic toolkit supports precise manipulation [39]. Its GRAS (Generally 
Recognized as Safe) status with the U.S. Food and Drug Administration facilitates 
applications across food, feed, pharmaceutical, and enzyme manufacturing. Industrial 
deployments exploit its capacity to secrete extracellular enzymes (amylases, 
proteases, cellulases, lipases) and to produce bioactive metabolites such as surfactin 
and fengycin [37]. These attributes, tolerance to aeration, shear, and pH fluctuations, 
and metabolic activity under elevated CO2, make B. subtilis a strong candidate for 
cocultivation. In that context, it functions not only as a cohabiting microbe but also as 
a biocatalyst, accelerating inorganic carbon conversion and helping to stabilize culture 
conditions that favor algal CO2 fixation [35,39]. 
 
1.5.2 Carbonic Anhydrase Enzymes in Bacillus subtilis 
Carbonic Anhydrases (CAs) are zinc-containing metalloenzymes that catalyze the 
reversible hydration of CO2 to bicarbonate (HCO3-) and protons (H+). Although 
hydration proceeds spontaneously, the uncatalyzed rate is slow (approx. 0.15 s-1), CAs 
can accelerate it by up to six orders of magnitude, rapidly enlarging the bicarbonate 
pool for downstream metabolism [40]. In B. subtilis, at least two CA classes are 
present: β-class enzymes, typically cytosolic and involved in intracellular carbon 
balance and provision of HCO3- for biosynthetic pathways such as fatty acid synthesis 
[40], and γ-class enzymes, which may associate with membranes or periplasmic 
compartments and act on the extracellular environment [41]. 
 
Some B. subtilis strains are capable of secreting proteins, including enzymes, into the 
extracellular medium. Secretion stress regulation has been shown to involve both intra 
and extracellular proteases, supporting the feasibility of extracellular CA activity [42]. 
Such extracellular CA is of particular interest for algal cocultivation, since increases 
dissolved bicarbonate availability, reduces CO2 losses by degassing, and helps 
stabilize pH by speeding equilibration between CO2 and HCO3-. The catalysis 
schematized in Figure 4, involves a metal center, usually Zn2+ coordinated by 
histidines, in which a zinc-bound hydroxide attacks CO2 to form HCO3-, which is 
released, while a proton-shuttle regenerates the active site [43]. Reported bacterial 
CA activity is generally optimal at pH around 7.5 to 9.0 and remains stable across 20 
to 40 °C, overlapping the preferred growth conditions of many microalgae, including 
Chlorella sorokiniana. Adequate supplies of Zn (or Fe for some γ-CAs) are therefore 
important considerations in coculture media.

1.5.3 Role of CA in accelerating inorganic carbon conversion 
In microalgal cultivation, CO2 first dissolves into the aqueous phase, but many species, 
including Chlorella sorokiniana, rely primarily on bicarbonate transported by carbon-
concentrating mechanisms (CCMs) [45]. Because uncatalyzed CO2 hydration is slow, 
rapidly mixed reactors with short gas residence times can lose a substantial fraction 
of supplied CO2 before it converts to HCO3-. Extracellular CA from B. subtilis 
overcomes this bottleneck by catalyzing hydration directly in the bulk liquid, effectively 
coupling the gas-phase supply to the dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) pool [46]. 
 
The consequences of this process are first, a higher steady-state HCO3- concentration 
that allows algal bicarbonate transporters to operate near capacity; second, lower CO2 
losses by outgassing, improving overall carbon retention; and faster CO2 ↔ HCO3- 
interconversion that buffers pH against swings common in high CO2 conditions [47]. 
These effects are especially valuable in flat-panel PBRs, where gas exchange is rapid 
and retention times are short, because CA helps ensure CO2 is converted and 
captured before it exits the system [48]. 
1.6 Microalgae – Bacteria Cocultivation 
Microalgae-Bacteria cocultivation denotes the deliberate pairing of photosynthetic 
microalgae with compatible heterotrophic or mixotrophic bacteria in a shared culture, 
with the aim of increasing productivity, tightening nutrient recycling, and improving 
carbon capture efficiency. In nature, these associations underpin aquatic ecosystem 
function: phytoplankton and their attendant bacteria form tightly coupled 
“phycosphere” in micron-scale habitats around algal cells that host dense bacterial 
populations [49]. These interactions are mediated by exchanges of gases, nutrients, 
and signaling molecules, and they shape algal physiology, stress tolerance, and 
metabolic outputs. 
 
Recreating and optimizing such relationships in engineered systems (e.g., 
photobioreactors) offers routes to both faster and more stable CO2 biofixation. In high  
CO2 experiments, such as those in this thesis, cocultivation can simultaneously 
modulate dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) availability and improve culture quality via 
bacterial effects on pH, nutrient speciation, and dissolved oxygen levels [50].

1.6.1 Natural mutualisms in aquatic ecosystems 
Microalgae and bacteria have evolved together for over a billion years, developing 
complementary metabolisms that sustain mutualisms in planktonic communities [51]. 
Algae release O2 during photosynthesis, heterotrophic bacteria use that O2 for 
respiration and return CO2 that algae can immediately refix. This tight O2-CO2 loop 
reduces diffusive losses and supports both organisms in oligotrophic waters [52]. 
 
Beyond gases, algal cells exude a substantial fraction (often 20-40%) of fixed carbon 
as dissolved organic carbon (DOC), including sugars, amino acids, and organic acids, 
which fuels bacterial growth in the phycosphere [49]. Bacteria, in return, remineralize 
waste and exudates to inorganic nutrients (e.g., NH4+, NO3-, PO43-) and release trace 
metals from organic complexes, thereby sustaining algal growth. Some bacteria also 
synthesize growth-promoting substances (GPS) such as indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), 
cytokinins, and gibberellins that can adjust algal cell division, pigment composition, 
and stress responses [53]. For instance, Azospirillum brasilense, derived IAA has been 
shown to stimulate chlorophyll synthesis in green microalgae [54]. Ecologically, these 
partnerships stabilize community dynamics, in engineered cultures, they can shorten 
lag phases, raise maximum growth rates, and buffer against fluctuations in CO2 supply. 
 
1.6.2 Productivity gains from cocultivation 
Multiple studies report substantial gains in biomass and target metabolites when algae 
are paired with bacteria. It was observed a 46% increase in biomass productivity for 
Chlorella vulgaris co-immobilized with A. brasilense in alginate beads, accompanied 
by higher pigment content [54]. Likewise, it was reported that Scenedesmus obliquus 
grown with Variovorax paradoxus accumulated around 30% more lipids, attributed to 
bacterial provision of vitamins and organic carbon that shifted algal metabolism toward 
lipid biosynthesis [51]. 
 
Productivity can also rise through improved CO₂ mass transfer. For instance, 
coculturing Chlorella vulgaris with Bacillus licheniformis increased chlorophyll content 
and photosynthetic efficiency, while other studies have shown that microalgal-bacterial 
consortia enhance algal carbonic anhydrase activity and CO2 fixation relative to 
monocultures, consistent with faster CO2 hydration expanding the bioavailable HCO3⁻ 
pool for microalgal carbon-concentrating mechanisms [55–57]. Beyond yield, 
cocultures often show greater operational stability: probiotic Roseobacter clade 
bacteria such as Phaeobacter sp., secrete antimicrobials like tropodithietic acid that 
suppress opportunists in microalgal cultures, and outdoor mixed microalgal-bacterial 
systems have demonstrated stable biomass productivity over multi-year continuous 
operation [58–60]. These stabilizing effects are especially valuable under high CO2 
conditions, where pH excursions and dissolved gas gradients would otherwise stress 
microalgal cells. 
 
1.6.3 Nutrient cycling and vitamin provision by bacteria 
Bacterial remineralization is central to both natural and engineered consortia. In dense 
cultures, nitrogen and phosphorus rapidly partition into particulate or organically bound 
forms that are less accessible to microalgae; bacterial decomposition returns these to 
bioavailable NH4+ and PO43-, while siderophore dynamics can mobilize iron from 
complexes [61]. Vitamins are equally fundamental. Many algal taxa are auxotrophic

for vitamin B12 (cobalamin) and depend on external supply [62]; indeed, it was reported 
that roughly half of surveyed algal species require exogenous B12 [63]. Bacteria such 
as Halomonas and Rhodobacter can meet these demands in coculture, alleviating 
otherwise hard growth limits. This is why, under high-CO₂ aeration, when rapid 
microalgal growth can deplete nutrients within hours, continuous bacterial 
remineralization helps maintain steady nutrient availability, sustain photosynthetic 
rates, and delay culture deterioration. 
 
1.6.4 CO2/HCO3- Dynamics in mixed cultures 
In water, CO2 equilibrates with HCO3- and CO32-, with distributions set by pH and 
temperature [64]. Although CO2 can diffuse across membranes, many microalgae 
preferentially import HCO3- via active transport as part of their carbon-concentrating 
mechanisms [65,66]. The uncatalyzed hydration of CO2 to HCO3- is slow, but CA 
accelerates the interconversion [67]. Certain bacteria, such as Bacillus subtilis, secrete 
extracellular CA [68], and similar effects have been demonstrated with CA from 
Bacillus halodurans applied to algal cultures [69], increasing the bicarbonate pool 
available to algae. In cocultures, bacterial partners have also been shown to elevate 
algal CA activity and CO2 fixation [70], more tightly linking the gaseous CO2 supply to 
algal DIC uptake, an advantage in high CO2 experiments that rely on rapid dissolution 
of sparged gas. 
 
These benefits are: 
1. Enhanced inorganic carbon supply: A larger HCO3- pool improves CCM flux 
and carbon fixation rates [66,69,70]. 
2. pH buffering: Rapid CA catalyzed CO2 ↔ HCO3- interconversion inhibits pH 
changes driven by CO2 stripping or consumption, stabilizing the growth 
environment [71,72]. 
 
1.6.5 Integration into photobioreactor systems 
Deploying microalgal–bacterial consortia in PBRs requires coordinated physical and 
biological design. Physically, the reactor must provide sufficient mixing to distribute 
light, nutrients, and gases while avoiding damaging shear [73–75].  Biologically, 
compatible growth rates and environmental tolerances are critical to prevent over-
dominance and to maintain stable interactions [76,77].   
 
Two implementation modes are common, free-suspended systems, both partners 
inhabit the bulk culture, maximizing contact but complicating downstream separation 
[78].  On the other hand, immobilized configurations, microorganism are attached to 
beads, membranes, or surfaces. Standard work shows that co-immobilizing Chlorella 
vulgaris with Azospirillum brasilense in alginate beads increases microalgal growth, 
while immobilized systems simplify harvesting and can stabilize populations over long 
runs [79–81].   
 
In high-CO2 flat-panel PBRs, cocultivation is regulated by gas flow rate, inlet CO2 
fraction, and light intensity to meet the joint demands of the consortium [82–84].  When 
bacterial partners supply extracellular CA, or when is added exogenously, the 
hydration of CO2 to HCO3- is accelerated, increasing the bioavailable DIC pool and 
improving coupling between sparged CO2 and microalgal uptake [85–87].  This

gas sparging rates (i.e., reduced energy input) for an equivalent fixation target. Which 
is a reasonable process inference supported by studies where CA boosts CO2 
capture/transfer efficiency, including membrane-sparger configurations [88]. 
 
1.7 Photobioreactor systems 
Photobioreactors are engineered cultivation systems designed to support the growth 
of photosynthetic microorganisms, such as microalgae, cyanobacteria, and 
phototrophic bacteria, under tightly controlled environmental conditions. Relative to 
open pond systems, PBRs typically enable higher areal productivities, improved 
contamination control, and precise regulation of critical parameters such as light, 
temperature, pH, dissolved gases, and nutrient concentrations [89]. In the context of 
CO2 biofixation, this controllability is advantageous because it permits accurate CO2 
dosing, continuous monitoring of gas exchange, and the generation of quantitative 
data on biomass productivity and carbon uptake efficiency.

There are several types of Photobioreactor designs, between the most common ones 
are the tubular, flat-panel, bubble column and airlift observed in Figure 3, which choice 
of reactor geometry is determined by the target organism, environmental constraints,

1.7.1.1 Tubular photobioreactors 
Tubular PBRs comprise networks of transparent tubes (commonly glass or acrylic) 
arranged horizontally, vertically, or helically to maximize incident photon capture. 
Culture is circulated through the tubes by a pump or an airlift loop, typically passing 
through a central manifold that houses heat exchangers, pH and gas-mixing hardware, 
and sampling points. The defining advantage is the large surface to volume ratio: cells 
experience high specific light availability, and irradiance fields can be modified by tube 
diameter, spacing, and orientation.  
 
In practice, operating performance is governed by three tightly coupled phenomena. 
First, oxygen management is critical. Photosynthetically generated O2 accumulates

strategically placed vent points are therefore integral [91]. Second, temperature control 
is challenging because tubes heat rapidly under insolation, therefore shading 
strategies like coolant jackets at manifolds, intermittent circulation, or burial/partial 
submersion are used to buffer thermal spikes. Third, biofouling on illuminated walls

favor scale-out (more parallel loops) rather than larger diameters, because increasing 
tube diameter quickly deepens the light path and exacerbates internal gradients. 
Pressure drop, pump energy, and mechanical integrity at joints become the principal 
engineering constraints at larger footprints [91]. 
 
1.7.1.2 Flat-Panel photobioreactors 
Flat-panel (or flat-plate) PBRs restrict the culture between two parallel transparent 
plates, producing a centimeter-scale optical path that reduces self-shading even at 
high biomass concentrations. The short light path also permits precise irradiance 
control with external LEDs or fiber-coupled light guides, which is advantageous in 
laboratory and pilot settings. Hydrodynamics are established either by sparged gas at 
the base, by an internal airlift channel, or by gentle rocking/oscillation of the panel [75]. 
Because mixing distances are small, flat-panels achieve short mixing times with 
comparatively low energy input, and the uniform light distribution supports stable 
growth under elevated CO2.  
 
Given this considerations, practical design choices center on plate spacing, gas 
distributor geometry, and materials. Narrower spacing improves light penetration but 
raises the risk of channeling and increases susceptibility to particulates; wider spacing 
eases hydraulics but can reintroduce light gradients. Rigid plastics reduce breakage 
risk and simplify fabrication but scratch more easily and may age under UV; glass 
offers superior optical stability and cleaning but is heavier [75]. Flat-panels are well 
suited to factorial experimentation, meaning that multiple identical modules can run in 
parallel at different CO2 levels or with different consortia, while planar surfaces and 
accessible ports make sampling and cleaning straightforward, reducing contamination 
risk during long campaigns. Thermal management is typically easier than in tubular 
systems because panels present broad surfaces for convective cooling and can be 
jacketed or back-plated for heat exchange [75]. 
 
1.7.1.3 Bubble column reactors 
Bubble columns are vertically oriented cylinders in which gas is injected at the base 
through spargers to provide both aeration and mixing. Their appeal lies in mechanical 
simplicity, no internal moving parts, and in robust, low-maintenance operation. 
Circulation is driven by gas holdup and resistance; the resulting flow field promotes 
bulk mixing and enhances gas–liquid mass transfer. However, performance is 
sensitive to bubble size distribution and coalescence [92]. Coarse bubbles reduce 
interfacial area and shorten residence time, limiting CO2 dissolution and O2 stripping; 
fine-pore diffusers, antifoam management, and column aspect ratio modification are 
therefore central design parameters. Optical performance is the principal trade-off, 
because the cylindrical geometry yields a relatively long and spatially variable light 
==PAGE:28==
 
16 
path, so dense cultures become limited to the light when they are away from the 
illuminated perimeter. As a result, bubble columns are often preferred for moderately 
dense cultures, for species that tolerate lower irradiance, or in settings where low 
capital and operational complexity take precedence over maximum areal productivity. 
For high-CO2 applications, careful control of gas flow prevents excessive pH swings 
and mitigates CO2 depletion via bubble stripping [92]. 
 
1.7.1.4 Airlift reactors (internal and external loop) 
Airlift PBRs introduce a circulation loop that separates “riser” (gassed) and 
“downcomer” (degassed) regions. The density difference between the two zones 
generates a well-defined circulating flow without mechanical impellers, delivering more 
predictable hydrodynamics than a simple bubble column and generally lower shear 
than pumped tubular systems [93]. Internal-loop airlifts house the draft tube within a 
single vessel; external-loop designs connect two columns to form the riser–
downcomer pair.  
 
For phototrophic cultures, this architecture offers several benefits: improved control 
over circulation velocity (therefore residence time in illuminated regions), efficient O2 
disengagement in the downcomer, and energy-efficient mixing at scale [93]. Gas 
distributor design and tube geometry determine bubble residence time, gas holdup, 
and the volumetric mass-transfer coefficient. These in turn set the upper bound on 
achievable productivities at a given CO2 partial pressure. As with bubble columns, the 
main constraint is optical, because large diameters increase the light path and promote 
radial gradients. Consequently, airlifts are ideal for species or consortia that are shear-
sensitive, for moderate biomass densities, and where the energy budget must remain 
low while maintaining reliable gas exchange and pH control [93]. 
 
1.7.1.5 Emerging and hybrid configurations 
Considering the previously mention configurations, a growing set of PBRs designs aim 
to couple carbon capture with resource recovery or to decouple gas transfer from bulk 
hydrodynamics. 
 
One of this, is the membrane-based PBRs that deliver CO2 through gas-permeable 
hollow fibers or flat membranes, providing a large interfacial area without free bubbles. 
This approach reduces CO2 stripping losses, stabilizes pH, and limits foam formation. 
It is particularly attractive for high-CO2 dosing and when precise inorganic carbon 
speciation is needed (e.g., in cocultures with carbonic anhydrase-producing bacteria). 
The primary drawbacks are membrane fouling, pressure-drop management, and 
higher capital cost; cleaning protocols and cross-flow designs are active areas of 
development [94]. 
 
There is also wastewater-integrated PBRs that combines phototrophic cultivation with 
nutrient removal, using municipal or industrial effluents as feed. Flat-panel and tubular 
geometries are common, sometimes augmented with laminar settlers or biofilm 
carriers. These systems influence the nutrient load to offset media costs and generate 
co-benefits (N and P removal), but must manage variable influent quality, competing 
microbiota, and solids that accelerate fouling. When integrated with flue-gas or biogas 
upgrading, they can simultaneously scrub CO2 while polishing effluents [94].

Additionally, thin-layer and cascading systems spread culture as a shallow film over 
illuminated surfaces or stepped channels. The extremely short optical path supports 
very high areal productivities under strong illumination and facilitates rapid heat 
rejection. However, evaporation control, hydrodynamic uniformity, and contamination 
management become dominant design concerns, these systems are often deployed 
outdoors with careful humidity and make-up water control [75]. 
 
Furthermore, rotating and internally illuminated designs (e.g., rotating drum/photo 
wheel, light-rod or fiber-optic reactors) address light limitation by either periodically 
exposing cells to light/dark cycles through rotation or by bringing light into the culture 
volume. They can deliver highly controlled light regimes and short mixing times but 
introduce additional mechanical complexity and, for internally lit systems, non-trivial 
maintenance demands to keep optical surfaces clean [75]. 
 
Across these emerging options, the unifying theme is tighter control of the light and 
carbon nexus: geometry, optics, and gas transfer are co-designed to minimize the self-
shading, stabilize pH and dissolved gases, and reduce the energy drawbacks 
associated with moving fluids and gases. Selection among them depends on the target 
organism(s), CO2 partial pressure and dosing strategy, acceptable shear, fouling 
tolerance, and the degree to which objectives, such as nutrient removal are prioritized 
[94]. 
 
1.7.2 Light management strategies 
Because photosynthesis is light-driven, both irradiance and its spatial, temporal 
distribution strongly influence PBR performance. Excess irradiance can cause 
photoinhibition, whereas insufficient irradiance induces photo-limitation. Strategies to 
manage these constraints include minimizing the light path (as in flat-panel PBRs), 
incorporating internal light guides, and deploying light-emitting diodes (LEDs) with 
adjustable spectra matched to photosynthetic action peaks [75]. Temporal modulation 
via pulsed or intermittent illumination (“light flashing”) can increase photosynthetic 
efficiency by allowing reaction centers to recover between excitation events [95]. 
Under elevated CO2, careful light management becomes even more critical, as 
increased inorganic carbon can only accelerate carbon fixation when photon supply is 
not limiting. 
 
1.7.3 Gas transfer mechanisms 
Efficient delivery of CO2 and removal of evolved O2 are central to PBR operation. Gas 
exchange is commonly achieved by sparging CO2 enriched air through fine diffusers 
at the reactor base, thereby promoting both dissolution and bulk mixing. At high cell 
densities, mass-transfer limitations arise from boundary layers around bubbles and 
cells, necessitating optimization of bubble size, gas flow rate, and hydrodynamic 
conditions [96]. Excess dissolved oxygen is a recurrent challenge because it can 
inhibit RuBisCO and exacerbate photo-oxidative stress [94]. In flat-panel systems, 
inclined or oscillatory configurations enhance bubble sweeping and surface renewal, 
improving gas exchange and stabilizing pH. In coculture settings, gas-transfer 
dynamics also modulate interspecies interactions; for instance, carbonic anhydrase-
==PAGE:30==
 
18 
secreting bacteria can alter inorganic carbon speciation and thereby the effective CO2 
availability to microalgae. 
 
1.7.4 Nutrient Control and Scale-up Considerations 
Microalgal growth requires balanced supplies of macronutrients (e.g., nitrogen, 
phosphorus, sulphur) and micronutrients (e.g., iron, magnesium). In controlled PBRs, 
media composition and feeding strategies should prevent both depletion (growth 
limitation) and chronic excess (waste and undesirable metabolic shifts) [97]. Scaling 
from laboratory to pilot or industrial volumes introduces additional constraints such as 
increased light attenuation, reduced gas-transfer efficiency, a higher propensity for 
biofouling on illuminated surfaces, and escalating capital and operating costs 
associated with lighting, mixing, and temperature control [91]. For CO2 mitigation 
applications, achieving a net positive carbon balance requires that energy inputs for 
fluid handling, illumination, and thermal management remain proportionate to the 
carbon fixed. 
 
1.7.5 Advantages of Flat-panel PBRs Under Elevated CO₂ 
Flat-panel PBRs are particularly well suited to high CO2 experimentation. Their 
geometry imposes a uniform, short optical path, limiting self-shading even at elevated 
cell densities and thereby supporting sustained photosynthetic activity. The modular 
architecture facilitates parallel experimentation across multiple CO2 concentrations or 
microbial consortia, for instance, microalgae paired with carbonic-anhydrase-
producing bacteria, while also simplifying routine operations such as sampling and 
cleaning, which reduces contamination risk during extended runs [98]. Under high- 
CO2 conditions, the favorable mixing and gas-exchange characteristics of flat-panel 
systems help maintain stable pH and dissolved-gas profiles, thereby reducing 
physiological stress and supporting consistent biomass productivity. 
 
1.8 Research Gap and Project Aim 
Despite these advantages, the rapid rise in anthropogenic CO2 has pushed mitigation 
and utilization strategies to the forefront. Classic solutions such as afforestation, large-
scale renewables, and carbon capture and storage (CCS), have slowed but not 
reversed atmospheric accumulation. Biological fixation sits alongside these 
approaches as a practical complement, since it is intrinsically renewable, compatible 
with existing ecosystems, and can generate saleable coproducts. Within this space, 
microalgae consistently stand out. They channel inorganic carbon into biomass at high 
areal productivities and tolerate unconventional media, including saline streams and 
nutrient-rich wastewaters. Chlorella sorokiniana, in particular, combines rapid growth 
with documented tolerance to elevated CO2. Yet even strong performers face familiar 
hurdles by capturing a high fraction of sparged CO2, holding productivity steady under 
shifting light and gas loads, and scaling processes without excessive energy input or 
fouling. 
 
A key rate limitation is kinetic rather than thermodynamic. Many microalgae draw 
carbon primarily as bicarbonate via carbon concentrating mechanisms, but the 
uncatalyzed hydration of dissolved CO2 to HCO3- is slow. Under strong aeration (10-
20% CO2), bubbles pass through faster than CO2 can equilibrate and hydrate, so much 
==PAGE:31==
 
19 
of it strips out before conversion. Thus, even when C. sorokiniana has the transporters 
to exploit bicarbonate, the chemistry can lag the physics. 
 
Carbonic anhydrase offers a direct solution. This zinc metalloenzyme accelerates the 
CO2 ↔ HCO3- interconversion by orders of magnitude, tightening the link between the 
gas phase and the dissolved inorganic carbon pool. Extracellular CA is especially 
relevant to reactors, since unlike intracellular isoforms, it operates where the mass 
transfer occurs, at bubble interfaces and in the bulk liquid. Studies with purified CA 
and CA-producing bacteria show higher growth rates and biomass yields, but most 
tests have been run at lower CO2 (0.03 to 5%). Whether those gains persist, or even 
improve, under very high CO2 typical of flue gas (10 to 20%) remains underexplored. 
 
Co-culturing do more than fix carbon. Around each algal cell (the phycosphere), algae 
produces oxygen and dissolved organic carbon; bacteria use these products, returning 
nutrients through remineralization, and making vitamins that algae can use. Synthetic 
microbial consortia can harness the same exchanges to stabilize pH, recycle N and P, 
and reduce culture crashes. However, much of the literature relies on non-targeted 
communities (e.g., wastewater consortia) or model heterotrophs selected for 
convenience rather than function. Intentionally pairing algae with bacteria that secrete 
extracellular CA, so that inorganic carbon availability becomes a designed variable, 
has received reasonably little attention, particularly in high-CO₂ photobioreactors. 
 
Here Bacillus subtilis is a strong candidate. It is robust, GRAS-listed, and widely used 
in industry, critically, it produces β- and γ-class carbonic anhydrases, with strains 
reported to secrete active enzyme. In a flat-panel reactor, where optical paths are short 
(centimeter scale) and gas exchange is rapid, secreted CA can catalyze hydration in 
the same control volume where bubbles dissolve, boosting the bicarbonate pool 
available to C. sorokiniana. B. subtilis also tolerates the high aeration rates and 
elevated dissolved gases common in these runs. Despite this fit, systematic, controlled 
experiments under high CO2, and in geometries with fast surface renewal like flat 
panels, are still infrequent. 
 
A second gap is operational. Demonstrations in shake flasks and small batch reactors 
do not automatically translate to week-long, contamination resistant campaigns. 
Industrial relevance requires reproducible coculture dynamics, stable CA expression, 
tight pH and DIC control under variable light, and maintenance strategies that do not 
erase energy gains. These are engineering questions as much as biological ones. 
 
Third, success metrics need to move beyond biomass and chlorophyll alone. To link 
mechanism to outcome, datasets should include DIC speciation, gas-phase CO₂ 
profiles across the reactor, and specific uptake rates (qCO2) for mono and cocultures. 
Without these, it is difficult to attribute a productivity increase to CA mediated hydration 
rather than, for example, vitamin provisioning or selection against contaminants. 
 
These considerations motivate the present study: a controlled, mechanistic evaluation 
of B. subtilis assisted CO2 hydration for enhancing C. sorokiniana fixation across CO2 
from atmospheric (0.03%) up to 20%. Flat-panel photobioreactors provide the 
necessary levers, precise gas delivery, short and uniform light paths, defined mixing, 
and tractable heat removal, in a format that already scales in industry. Mapping 
==PAGE:32==
 
20 
performance across this CO2 range identifies operating windows and tolerance limits 
for both microorganisms. 
 
To that end, this study pursues three aims: 
1. Demonstrate that coculturing Chlorella sorokiniana with Bacillus subtilis 
increases the apparent CO2→HCO3- conversion rate in situ via extracellular 
carbonic anhydrase (CA), thereby stabilizing pH and increasing dissolved 
inorganic carbon (DIC) availability. 
2. Quantify how coculture affects growth, specific CO2 uptake, capture efficiency, 
and biomass productivity at high CO2 (15-20%). 
3. Evaluate flat-panel photobioreactors as a controllable, scalable platform for CA-
assisted biofixation under high CO2 sparging. 
 
Taken together, the results are intended to inform reactor design and operating 
strategies for a next generation of biological CO2 capture that is both biologically 
efficient and economically credible, and to clarify when targeted algal–bacterial 
partnerships can be integrated directly with industrial emission streams. 
2. Materials and Methods 
2.1 Microorganisms and Culture Conditions 
Two microorganisms were used throughout: the unicellular green microalgae Chlorella 
sorokiniana and the bacterium Bacillus subtilis. These were selected for 
complementary traits relevant to CO2 capture. C. sorokiniana is well characterized, 
grows rapidly, is easy to cultivate, and can capture CO2, making it a robust candidate 
for carbon biofixation under industrially relevant gas compositions. On the other side, 
B. subtilis is a Gram-positive, genetically tractable organism with GRAS status and a 
long track record in biotechnology. Of particular interest here is its secretion of 
extracellular carbonic anhydrase (CA), which catalyzes interconversion between CO2 
and HCO3-, thereby expanding the dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) pool available for 
microalgae growth. 
 
2.1.1 Chlorella sorokiniana 
The microalgae (MA) Chlorella sorokiniana CCAP 211/8K used in this study was 
purchased from the Culture Collection of Algae and Protozoa, United Kingdom.  
 
Pre-cultures of C. sorokiniana were maintained in Blue Green-11 (BG11) (1.5g NaNO3, 
0.075g MgSO4·7H2O, 0.036g CaCl2·2H2O, 0.02g Na2CO3, 0.0006g C6H8O7⋅xFe3+⋅yN3, 
0.006g C6H8O7, 0.04g K2HPO4, EDTA and traces per liter; pH 7.8). Stock cultures were 
grown in 250 mL Erlenmeyer flasks with 100 mL working volume under 12:12 h 
light:dark cycle under 100 µmol photons m⁻² s⁻¹ light intensity. Temperature was held 
at 25 ± 1 °C, and flasks were shaken at 150 rpm on an orbital shaker to prevent settling 
and to maintain gas and nutrients exchange at the liquid surface. Subcultures were 
prepared every 7 to 14 days by inoculating exponentially growing cells into fresh BG11 
at an initial Optical Density (OD680) of 0.2-0.3.

2.1.2 Bacillus subtilis 
The Gram-positive carbonic anhydrase bacteria (CAB) Bacillus subtilis was obtained 
from the strain collection of Biosustain at Danmarks Tekniske Universitet (DTU), 
Lyngby in Denmark. 
 
The strain was cultivated in Luria-Bertani (LB) agar (10 g tryptone, 5 g yeast extract, 
10 g NaCl, 15g agar per liter; pH 7.0) at 37 °C for 48h. Pre-cultures of B. subtilis were 
maintained in LB broth (10 g tryptone, 5 g yeast extract, 10 g NaCl per liter; pH 7.0). 
Cultures were grown in 250 mL flasks with 50 mL working volume at 37 °C and 150 
rpm to sustain aerobic conditions. For each new experiment, cells from the exponential 
phase were transferred to fresh media. 
 
Maintaining each organism in its optimal medium established reliable baselines for 
growth and physiology. These baselines were essential once both organisms were 
transitioned into a shared coculture medium for side-by-side comparisons. 
 
2.2 Growth correlation curves of microorganisms 
Accurate biomass quantification underpins comparisons across treatments and the 
calculation of kinetic parameters (e.g., specific growth rate, µ). Because OD can be 
influenced by cell size, shape, and pigmentation, calibration curves were established 
for each organism to accurately convert OD values into absolute quantifications, 
including Dry Cell Weight (g/L) and cell concentration (cells/mL). 
 
2.2.1 OD vs. Cell Count (cells/mL) 
The OD for C. sorokiniana was recorded daily at 680 nm (chlorophyll absorption 
region), which provides a robust proxy for algal biomass. The growing cultures of the 
microalgae were serially diluted to yield OD680 values between 0.05 and 1.5. At each 
dilution, OD was measured on a spectrophotometer (Synergy H1 Multi-Mode 
Microplate Reader - Biotek). In parallel, aliquots were analyzed on a particle analyzer 
(oCelloScope - ParticleTech) digital cell imaging system to obtain automated cell 
counts and size distributions. Plotting OD680 versus cells/mL produced a linear 
regression used throughout the study to convert OD to cell number (Appendix A.2.2). 
To avoid non-linearity at high turbidity, any sample with OD680 > 2.0 was diluted back 
into the calibrated range prior to measurement. Debris and clumps were excluded by 
size-based gating in the imaging analysis. 
 
Similarly, for B. subtilis it was transferred 2% of inoculum from a pre-culture of the 
bacteria into a flask of 250 mL containing 100 mL of LB broth, the cultivation was 
performed during 5 hours at 37 ºC and 150 rpm in duplicate, and by taking samples 
every 30 min the OD was measured at 600 nm, the standard for bacterial cultures. In 
parallel, aliquots were analyzed on the oCelloScope in order to obtain the cell density 
(cell/mL) during the growth phases of the bacteria. 
2.2.2 OD vs. Dry Cell Weight (g/L) 
The correlation between optical density (OD) and Dry Cell Weight (DCW) for C. 
sorokiniana was determined using cultures from the same experimental setup used 
for the OD versus cell count correlation (Appendix A.2.1). Specifically, out of a total of 
==PAGE:34==
 
22 
24 flasks, two flasks were harvested each day over a 12-day cultivation period, 
providing duplicate samples for each time point. The entire culture volume from each 
flask was used to measure DCW, ensuring accurate representation of biomass 
accumulation throughout the growth period. The volume was centrifuged at 12000 rpm 
in order to separate the biomass from the supernatant, and then, the pellet was freeze-
dried for 24 hours, and finally, the DCW (g/L) was calculated using the biomass 
recovered and the sample volume. 
 
For B. subtilis, the dry cell weight (DCW) was determined from 5 mL samples taken 
from the cell count correlation cultures at the same sampling intervals. Each sample 
was filtered through a Sartorius cellulose nitrate filter (0.45 µm), which had been pre-
dried in a microwave oven at 180 W for 10 minutes, cooled in a desiccator for 10 to 15 
minutes, and weighed on an analytical balance. 
 
Filtration of the cell suspension was performed (typically 10 mL recommended), and 
the exact filtered volume was recorded. The retained cells were then washed three 
times with demineralized (DEMI) water, each time using the same volume as that 
initially filtered. After washing, the filter was dried again in the microwave oven at 180 
W for 15 minutes, cooled in the desiccator for 10-15 minutes, and reweighed. The 
DCW (g/L) was calculated over the course of cultivation, from the difference between 
the final and initial filter weights with the volume used in the filtration step. 
 
2.3 Development of a common Modified Form Medium 
(MFM) 
Because of the different requirements of C. sorokiniana and B. subtilis, the growth 
media for each is accordingly different. Thus, a Modified Form Media (MFM) was 
developed to support the growth of both microorganisms. BG11 provides a mineral 
framework suited to photosynthesis, whereas LB supplies complex organics that drive 
rapid bacterial growth. A side-by-side comparison of the nutrients and formulations 
highlighted the specific requirements for each organism, summarized in Table 1. 
 
The design goal was a medium that retained BG11’s inorganic nutrient balance 
(supporting algal photosynthesis) while supplying minimal, carefully titrated organic 
carbon and growth factors for B. subtilis. The working formulation included the BG11 
macronutrients and trace elements (NaNO3, K2HPO4, MgSO4, CaCl2, EDTA-chelated 
trace metals), plus yeast extract in the case of bacterial cultivation to sustain its 
metabolism. 
 
Each strain was first cultivated separately in MFM and compared with growth in its 
default medium. Growth was tracked by OD, pH, and cell counts for 7–10 days. C. 
sorokiniana performed similarly to (or slightly better than) BG11, indicating that 
modified macro and micronutrients did not impair algal physiology. B. subtilis, however, 
grew weakly relative to LB, motivating targeted optimization of the organic fraction.

2.4 Optimization of the Common Medium 
Preliminary tests showed that the initial MFM supported C. sorokiniana but did not 
sustain robust B. subtilis growth. Because coculture success requires both partners to 
thrive, glucose and yeast extract concentrations were systematically optimized. 
 
Glucose was screened from 0 to 30 g/L in order to cover low, medium, and high 
concentrations of the carbon source. To perform this, first an overnight pre-inoculum 
of CAB in LB broth was done and inoculum from the exponential phase was transferred 
into the microplates containing MFM + Yeas Extract (5 g/L) + Glucose (0, 2, 10, 20, 
and 30 g/L), while monitoring B. subtilis growth (OD600). Growth improved with 2 g/L 
glucose and at higher concentrations of glucose gains plateaued, and signs of 
overflow metabolism increased. Yeast extract was then screened from 0 to 5 g/L, 
following the same pre-inoculum process, but in this case, the MFM contained the 
optimized level of glucose achieved in the previous step (2 g/L). Growth improved from 
3 g/L and deteriorated below that due to a lack of nutrients. 
 
The optimized selected medium contained BG11 inorganic nutrients plus 2 g/L glucose 
and 3 g/L yeast extract. Validation was performed in order to observe the performance 
of both microorganisms in the common media. C. sorokiniana growth in MFM 
enhances  compared to the BG11 formulation, while B. subtilis growth approached LB 
biomass yields. This optimized MFM was used in all subsequent mono and coculture 
experiments.

2.5 Carbonic Anhydrase (CA) Activity Assays 
The effectiveness of B. subtilis (CAB) as a coculture partner depends on the secretion 
of active extracellular CA. To verify activity in cell-free supernatants from B. subtilis 
grown in optimized MFM, two complementary assays were applied. Because CA 
catalysis relies on a Zn2+ center that deprotonates a metal-bound water to form the 
attacking hydroxide (and uses a proton-shuttle/water-wire to maintain turnover), the 
cultivation medium was supplemented with zinc (as ZnSO4) to ensure sufficient 
bioavailable cofactor (final Zn2+ = 10 μM) [99,100]. This is why, from this point onwards, 
the optimized version of MFM was supplied with Zinc, thus obtaining the final version 
of the common media (MFM+). Detailed media composition of the established MFM+ 
can be found in Appendix A.1 Protocols for Media Preparation. 
 
2.5.1 pH-based (Wilbur–Anderson) Assay 
After the cultivation and adaptation of CAB in MFM+, samples were collected in 
different moments of the cultivation, focusing on the ones that are related with the 
middle of the exponential phase, around 3 to 4 hours. After that, samples were 
centrifuged to obtain the supernatant with the allegedly CA enzyme, then  0.5 mL were 
mixed with Tris buffer (pH 8.3) and finally CO2-saturated water was added as the 
starting reagent, all done at controlled temperature (0 ºC). The decrease in pH was 
tracked with a calibrated electrode and compared with a control: buffer. The time for a 
1.0 unit pH drop was recorded, and Wilbur–Anderson units (WAU) were calculated as 
(T control – T sample)/T sample [101]. Duplicate technical measurements were 
performed for each biological replicate. 
 
2.5.2 Colorimetric Assay 
An alternative and widely used assay for carbonic anhydrase (CA) activity exploits the 
enzyme’s esterase side reaction: CA catalyzes the hydrolysis of p-nitrophenyl acetate 
(p-NPA) to p-nitrophenol, a chromophore that is readily measured at 405 nm [102,103]. 
Building on this principle, it was quantified CA activity using a colorimetric protocol 
adapted from Zhuang et al. (2018). A 0.1 M phosphate buffer at pH 7.2 was prepared 
by dissolving sodium phosphate dibasic (anhydrous) and sodium phosphate 
monobasic (dihydrate) in distilled water. A p-nitrophenol standard curve was generated 
from serial dilutions in the same buffer and read at 405 nm to convert absorbance to 
concentration (Appendix A.2.3). Working solutions were prepared as follows: Solution 
I, 0.0181g of p-nitrophenol (acetic-acid salt) in 0.5 mL of anhydrous ethanol; Solution 
II, 0.1560 g or 147.9 µL of diethylmalonate in 99.5 mL of phosphate buffer; and Solution 
III, the reaction mixture obtained by combining Solutions I and II immediately before 
use. 
 
For each measurement, 1.5 mL of Solution III was mixed with 1.5 mL of cell-free 
bacterial supernatant in a test tube. The initial absorbance at 405 nm was recorded 
and converted via the standard curve to give the starting p-nitrophenol concentration 
(C1). Samples were then incubated at 35 °C for 30 min in a temperature-controlled 
water bath, after which the final concentration (C2) was determined in the same way. 
CA activity was reported in units per liter (U/L), where one unit is defined as the amount 
of enzyme required for releasing 1 µmol p-nitrophenol per minute per liter of reaction 
mixture. Activity was calculated as the following equation:

2.6 CO2 Tolerance Experiments (Monocultures) 
Before coculture, the tolerance of each organism under elevated CO2 conditions was 
examined. Bacillus subtilis examined with both microtiter plates (high throughput) and 
sealed bottle systems (larger volume), and for Chlorella sorokiniana, the results of the 
CO2 tolerance were taken from my prior study of this microalgae in a flat-panel 
photobioreactor [104]. 
 
2.6.1 Microtiter Plates 
B. subtilis (OD600) was cultivated in optimized MFM+ under atmospheres of 0, 3, 5, 10, 
and 15% CO2 (balance air), levels that were chosen based on the report of C. 
sorokiniana previously mentioned. Plates were sealed with gas-permeable 
membranes and incubated in a plate reader (Synergy H1 Multi-Mode Microplate 
Reader - Biotek) with orbital shaking and gas addition through a tube into the reading 
chamber of the equipment, in order to keep a constant atmosphere of the desired level 
of CO2. OD was recorded every 30 min for up to 6 hours of the growth period. Each 
condition included at least three technical wells per biological replicate to account for 
edge effects. 
 
2.6.2 Sealed Bottles 
In order to evaluate the operational CO2 inhibition threshold for B. subtilis in a larger 
volume, higher CO2 levels were tested. For this, 100 mL cultures of CAB were 
prepared in 500 mL bottles fitted with butyl rubber stoppers. The headspace was 
flushed with premixed CO2/N2 to achieve 15% and 20% CO2, before inoculation. 
Cultures were incubated 6 hours at 37 ºC with 150 rpm shaking; two different types of 
samples were taken during the cultivation process, liquid samples for optical density, 
and gas samples were analyzed using Gas Chromatography (TRACE™ 1600 Series 
– Thermo Fisher) for gas composition in the headspace. 
 
Both organisms grew well up to 10% CO2 but showed inhibition from 15%. At 20% 
CO2, B. subtilis growth was strongly reduced, and C. sorokiniana growth was almost 
absent. For subsequent work, 15% CO2 was treated as the operational inhibition 
threshold for both species.

2.7 Coculture Experiments 
Cocultures were inoculated in MFM+ using exponential-phase pre-cultures adapted in 
the common media to avoid shock from a sudden medium change. Initial  
algae: bacteria inoculum ratios were set using the OD versus cell correlation (Section 
2.2.1) to achieve approximately equal starting cell densities. 
 
2.7.1 Bottle Cocultures 
Feasibility was first assessed in sealed, serum bottles at two CO2 levels: 15% (near 
the inhibition threshold identified in monocultures of microalgae) and 30% (above the 
inhibition threshold). Cocultures were run at 30 °C, a temperature that maintains  
C. sorokiniana photosynthetic activity while supporting B. subtilis growth and shaken 
at 175 rpm to promote gas–liquid mass transfer without excessive shear. Each run 
(Figure 5) used a 500 mL serum bottle with a 100 mL working volume (20% fill and 
400 mL headspace) to provide a stable gas reservoir. The medium was the optimized 
MFM without glucose, so that organic carbon for the bacterium derived from algal 
exudates (DOC) produced during photosynthesis, avoiding heterotrophic overgrowth. 
To counter the acidifying effect of CO2 dissolution and to stay within the microalga’s 
preferred range, the initial pH was set slightly alkaline (≥ 8.0), having the 
carbonate/bicarbonate buffering capacity present in the BG11-derived salts. Bottles 
were flushed with premixed CO2/air to the target fraction and sealed with butyl 
stoppers.

2.7.2 Photobioreactor (PBR) Cocultures 
Scalability was assessed in flat-panel photobioreactors (Algaemist-S) with a working 
volume of 400±20 mL. Temperature was maintained at 25 °C via the integrated water 
jacket connected to an external recirculating cooling system on the PBR controller. 
Light intensity was set to 130 µmol photons m-2 s-1 on a 12:12 h light:dark cycle 
(verified at the panel surface with a Photosynthetically Active Radiation meter), a 
regime chosen to balance photosynthetic drive with heat load. Mixing and gas 
exchange were provided by sparging at the bottom of PBR. Two sterile gas inlets (0.2 
µm PTFE filters) supplied dry compressed air and 20% CO2; mass-flow controllers 
were adjusted to achieve the target inlet CO2 fraction at a fixed total flow of 270±5 
mL/min. For reference, the CO2 controller was set to 211 mL/min for 15% (balance air 
63 mL/min) and 266 mL/min for 20% (balance air 0 L/min). Off-gas was vented through 
a condensate trap to limit evaporative losses. 
 
Reactors, lines, and fittings were sterilized before running (clean-in-place with a non-
foaming lab detergent, thorough deionized water rinse, and final sterilization of the 
wetted path; gas lines heat-treated or chemically sanitized as appropriate). Prior to 
inoculation, vessels were filled with the optimized MFM without glucose so that organic 
carbon for Bacillus subtilis would be provided by Chlorella sorokiniana exudates 
(dissolved organic carbon from photosynthesis), limiting heterotrophic overgrowth. 
The initial pH was adjusted to ≥ 8.0 in order to avoid an acidifying effect of CO2 
dissolution and to remain within the preferred range for C. sorokiniana. After aseptic 
inoculation (algae and bacteria in exponential phase), gas flows were ramped to the 
setpoint over 15 min to avoid abrupt pH and DIC shocks. The overall arrangement, 
panel geometry, short optical path, sparging, and active temperature control 
(schematized in Figure 6) ensured steady mixing, even light exposure, nutrient 
sufficiency for batch operation, and continuous monitoring of key culture variables. To 
minimize contamination, aseptic technique was used for all sampling; septa and filters 
were replaced at signs of wear, and reactors were leak-tested before each run.

was followed with technical duplicates for key assays. Results from the flat-panel trials 
were interpreted alongside the sealed-bottle feasibility data to define the operational 
window, quantify gains attributable to coculture at 15% CO₂, and document the 
performance drop at 20% CO₂ relative to monoculture tolerances. 
 
2.8 Analytical Methods 
 
2.8.1 Optical Density (OD) 
Optical density was used as a rapid screen for growth over time. For algal samples, 
readings were taken at 680 nm (OD680), near the absorption maximum of chlorophyll 
a; for bacterial samples, readings were taken at 600 nm (OD600), the conventional 
wavelength for bacterial turbidity. Measurements were obtained on a Synergy H1 
Multi-Mode Microplate Reader (BioTek Instruments, USA). Before each run, wells 
were blanked with fresh medium from the same batch and plates were mixed to 
resuspend cells. Readings outside the linear instrument range were diluted with 
medium and remeasured. Organism-specific correlation curves (Section 2.2.1) 
converted OD into cells/mL or g/L. In cocultures, OD trends were interpreted in 
conjunction with fluorescence and direct counts, as algae and bacteria contribute 
differently to turbidity. 
 
2.8.2 In Vivo Chlorophyll Fluorescence (IVF) 
The photosynthetic status of Chlorella sorokiniana was monitored non-destructively by 
in vivo chlorophyll fluorescence (IVF). Chlorophyll a was excited at 440 nm, and 
emission was recorded at 690 nm on the Synergy H1 reader. To reduce variability from 
non-photochemical quenching, samples were dark-adapted for 5 min and measured 
at consistent times within the light: dark cycle. Duplicate readings were collected per 
==PAGE:41==
 
29 
sample and, where appropriate, compared to OD680 or DCW to facilitate evaluation 
across conditions. 
 
2.8.3 Cell Density (cells/mL) via oCelloScope 
Absolute cell counts in mixed cultures were obtained with an oCelloScope (BioSense 
Solutions, Denmark), which combines time-lapse digital microscopy with automated 
image analysis. In the Particle Analyzer software, size and morphology gates were 
configured to distinguish C. sorokiniana (larger, near-spherical) from Bacillus subtilis 
(smaller rods), as can be observed in Figure 7. Gates were validated against manual 
counts on representative fields, and the pixel-to-micrometer scale was verified with a 
stage micrometer. This approach bypassed the limitations of OD in coculture and 
enabled time-resolved enumeration of both populations from the same sample.

2.8.4 Carbohydrate Concentration 
Extracellular carbohydrates were quantified by the phenol–sulfuric acid method [105]. 
In brief, 200 µL of sample was mixed with 50 µL of 5% (w/v) phenol and 500 µL of 
concentrated H2SO4, incubated at room temperature for 30 min, and read at 490 nm 
on the Synergy H1. A D-glucose calibration curve (Appendix A.2.4) from 0 to 1 g/L was 
prepared in the same matrix to enable quantification (reported as glucose equivalents). 
Medium blanks were included to correct for background. 
 
2.8.5 Biomass Handling 
Biomass destined for downstream analyses was processed under a standardized 
workflow. Cultures were centrifuged at 12,000 g for 10 min, pellets frozen (−20 °C) and 
lyophilized at -108 ºC and 0.07 mbar for 24 hours on Freeze Dryer (LaboGene, 
Denmark). Dried material was stored desiccated at room temperature in sealed tubes. 
The same preparation was applied for DCW determination, amino-acid profiling, and 
other biomass-based assays to minimize sample variability.

Carbon pools in culture samples were measured on a Shimadzu TOC-L analyzer

3. Results and Discussion 
3.1 CAB growth in MFM 
The growth behavior of Bacillus subtilis in the Modified Form Medium (MFM) was used 
to verify that it remains metabolically active without outcompeting the algal partner 
under coculture conditions. Relative to LB, B. subtilis displayed a lower specific growth 
rate in MFM, which is consistent with the formulation (BG11-derived minerals with 
restrained organics) that limits readily assimilable carbon and amino acids. Recent 
work shows that B. subtilis growth is highly sensitive to medium definition: in 
chemically defined or minimal media, rates and yields typically fall below those in 
complex broths, and small compositional shifts (salts, trace metals, or single amino 
acids) can markedly impact doubling time [109,110].   
 
Systematically, restrained growth in MFM aligns with resource-allocation exchange 
observed in B. subtilis: proteome investment into stress survival and biosynthesis 
machinery under nutrient stringency constrains maximal growth capacity [111]. 
Therefore, the moderated trajectories in Figure 8, while lower than LB, indicate 
reproducible adaptation to MFM’s ionic strength and micronutrient profile rather than 
failure to growth. 
 
From a coculture design perspective, this outcome is desirable. Reviews of 
microalgae–bacteria systems emphasize that limiting complex organics helps prevent 
heterotrophic overgrowth and stabilizes positive interactions (e.g., vitamin exchange, 
gas transfer), thereby protecting algal photosynthetic performance [112,113]. Because 
the targeted bacterial function here is continuous extracellular carbonic anhydrase 
supply rather than maximal biomass, maintaining B. subtilis at moderate, steady 
densities in MFM should still deliver CA-mediated acceleration of CO2 interconversion 
to HCO3- and enhance dissolved inorganic carbon availability for algal CCMs (Carbon 
Concentrating Mechanism) [114,115].

3.2 MA growth in MFM 
Alternatively, Chlorella sorokiniana (MA) responded robustly to the MFM observed in 
Figure 9, with trajectories that were comparable to those typically reported in mineral 
media for chlorophytes, indicating that the inorganic macro-/micronutrient balance in 
MFM is sufficient to sustain photoautotrophic growth. Recent studies show Chlorella 
spp., including C. sorokiniana, maintain consistent growth in BG11 or mineral matrices 
when light and nitrogen are adequate, supporting the interpretation that the ionic 
profile of MFM does not impose a strong physiological drawback [116,117]. Moreover, 
the performance of MA in the selected media under controlled light, further illustrates 
its capacity across mineral media backgrounds, reinforcing that growth in MFM reflects 
intrinsic photoautotrophic capacity rather than mixotrophic dependence [118].  
 
The enhancement observed in some replicates can plausibly arise from low-level 
organics (e.g., yeast extract) carried into MFM, which may enable limited mixotrophy 
without shifting the trophic balance. Mixotrophic boosts with glucose/yeast extract are 
well documented for Chlorella spp., and targeted additions to defined media  
(BBM/BG11 families) frequently increase growth rate or chlorophyll content at low 
doses [119–121]. These effects are consistent with transient improvements in 
energy/redox balance or vitamin/peptide supply that do not necessarily destabilize the 
photoautotrophic regime when kept minimal, aligning with the coculture design intent.

3.3.2 Yeast Extract Optimization 
As part of the optimization process, supplementing MFM with yeast extract (YE) 
accelerated Bacillus subtilis growth and increased OD600 (see Figure 11) because YE 
provides assimilable amino acids/peptides, B-vitamins, carbohydrate, and nucleotides 
that reduce biosynthetic burden and shorten lag phase [130]. Across 0 to 5 g/L, the 
response was non-linear: gains rose up to 3 g/L but plateaued at 4 and 5 g/L, with no 
consistent improvement at 5 g/L over 3 g/L at matched time points. This saturation is 
consistent with emerging limitations, such as oxygen transfer, extracellular pH control, 
and carbon-flux regulation (overflow), that limit translating additional complex nutrients 
into biomass [130]. This relays on the fact that richer feeds intensify carbon catabolite 
repression (CcpA–HPr(Ser-P)) and promote overflow metabolism (Pta–AckA → 
acetate), which depress pH and can truncate the exponential window despite signals 
of early growth [123,125]. On this basis, 3 g/L was selected since it captured most of 
the early growth benefit while avoiding the negative effects like, higher acid load, and 
variability important for downstream coculture stability. 
 
Importantly, sampling times differed (0, 3, and 5 h). The 3 h snapshots primarily 
capture early exponential physiology (high specific growth, limited overflow), whereas 
5 h measurements reflect late exponential and early stationary states, where acetate-
linked acidification and resource rearrangement reduce net growth of B. subitlis; 
consequently, 5 h readings are not necessarily higher than 3 h at the same YE dose 
[131]. Taken together these affirmations, Figure 11 supports using 3g/L of yeast extract 
in the MFM, in order to secure reliable B. subtilis activity while avoiding conditions that 
promote overflow and pH stress, which is appropriate for cocultures that prioritize 
extracellular functionality (e.g., enzyme secretion) over maximal bacterial biomass 
[123,130].

3.3.3 MA Growth 
In the optimized MFM, Chlorella sorokiniana sustained robust photoautotrophic 
growth, indicating that the mineral macro-/micronutrient balance is adequate without 
complex organics. This agrees with evidence that BG11-type mineral matrices are well 
suited to freshwater chlorophytes and that trace metals (e.g., Fe, Mn, Zn, Cu, Mo) 
underpin photosystem function and carbon assimilation when kept within bioavailable 
ranges [29,132]. The results observed in Figure 12 reflect a nutritionally competent, 
mineral medium rather than hidden mixotrophy. 
 
Relative to this baseline, low-dose supplementation with glucose and/or yeast extract 
(YE) produced modest but consistent increases in rate and yield, which is consistent 
with mixotrophic facilitation and vitamin/peptide relief. Recent studies show that 
Chlorella spp., including C. sorokiniana, utilize glucose efficiently and often realize 
higher biomass under mixotrophy than under strictly autotrophy, while YE (or peptone) 
additions to defined media can further enhance growth by reducing biosynthetic 
affliction [119,121,133]. The magnitude of the improvement observed here is typical of 
minimal dosing that enhances energy/redox balance without switching the culture into 
full heterotrophy, aligning with reports that small organic carbon inputs can accelerate 
early growth while photosynthesis remains dominant.  
 
On the other hand, at higher organic levels, negative results were expected due to 
light-limited self-shading, respiratory losses, and potential shifts toward overflow or 
community changes; hence, restrained supplementation is commonly recommended 
when preserving phototrophic performance is the goal [132,134]. Taken together, 
Figure 12 supports two design inferences, first, optimized MFM is sufficient for  
C. sorokiniana growth without complex organic addition, and secondly, low 
concentrations of glucose and yeast extract can be used tactically to shorten lag and 
raise biomass productivity while retaining a phototrophic regime, which is useful before 
transitioning to coculture where bacterial organics must be constrained.

3.6.2 Bottle 
To confirm and validate the results obtained in the microtiter plates, another 
experiment has been conducted under two higher levels of CO2 as 15% and 20%. As 
shown in Figure 18, sealed-bottle assays (restricted gas exchange) revealed Bacillus 
subtilis has grown better with the 15% CO2 than 20% over 6 h. OD600 rose from near 
baseline to 0.55 at 5 h under 15% CO₂ (followed by a slight reduction at 6 h), whereas 
cultures at 20% CO2 increased more slowly and plateaued lower (0.46 to 0.47 by 5 to 
6 h). Thus, 20% CO2 did not cause growth collapse; rather, it imposed a modest 
plateau relative to 15% within this time window. 
 
Headspace CO₂ (% v/v) dynamics (Figure 18 B) are consistent with sealed-system 
mass balance: an early dip (0–2 h) likely reflects CO2 dissolution into the medium and 
equilibration, whereas the subsequent rise above the setpoint (peaking 23 to 26% by 
4 to 5 h) reflects respiratory CO2 accumulation as O2 is consumed and CO2 produced. 
These trajectories corroborate that the cultures were metabolically active at both 
settings. The slower slope at 20% is mechanistically in line with hypercapnia acid 
stress: higher dissolved CO2 increases the carbonic-acid load, lowering extracellular 
pH and raising the ATP cost of cytoplasmic pH homeostasis (F1F0-ATPase and 
cation/proton antiport), which diverts resources from biosynthesis and slows growth 
without abolishing it [141,142,144]. 
 
Operationally, 15% CO2 provides a better short-term balance between growth and gas 
burden in sealed bottles; 20% remains tolerable and functional, but with tempered 
kinetics. For downstream coculture work where B. subtilis mainly contributes 
extracellular carbonic anhydrase (CA) rather than maximal biomass, these results 
indicate that moderate CO2 setpoints (e.g., 15%) are preferable for maintaining activity 
while minimizing acidification limitation; nevertheless, CA activity can persist under 
higher CO2 even when net growth is modest, which is advantageous when enzyme-
mediated DIC facilitation is the primary objective.

The coculture (MA + CAB) at 15% in the photobioreactor (see Figure 20), showed an 
earlier and stronger fluorescence rise than the algal monoculture (Figure 20 A): after 
acclimation, IVF440-690 climbed to 3400-3700 by days 4 to 5 in coculture, while MA

(7.5–7.6); MA plunges to 6.1–6.3 with limited recovery, conditions known to depress 
chlorophyte photosynthesis via DIC speciation and H+ homeostasis costs unless 
buffering and kLa are adjusted [145,147,148]. The carbohydrate pool (Figure 20 C)  
dropped to 0.18–0.22 g/L by day 2 and then steadied, indicating early drawdown and 
balanced assimilation afterwards. Extracellular CA is present throughout (Figure 20

outperforms MA (days 2–5) coincides with (i) neutral pH and (ii) detectable 
extracellular CA, both consistent with enhanced interfacial CO2↔HCO3- conversion 
that improves DIC access without raising inlet CO2 [72,115]. Taken together, the 
pattern implicates extracellular CA at near a neutral pH as a coculture advantage. 
 
Consistent with this mechanism, multiple studies support a role for CA in enhancing 
algal CO2 fixation. It has been reported that bicarbonate addition and bacterial CA 
activity significantly improved growth and photosynthetic efficiency of Chlorella 
sorokiniana cultures, with increases in biomass productivity of up to 22% compared 
with untreated cultures under identical aeration and illumination [47]. Similarly, it was 
found that CA-producing bacteria in Dunaliella salina cultures increased 
photosynthetic O2 evolution and reduced the lag phase following CO2 shifts, 
highlighting the importance of bicarbonate conversion to sustain photosynthesis under 
variable carbon supply [149]. Further, it was demonstrated that bicarbonate 
supplementation, mimicking CA-mediated CO2 hydration, elevated dissolved 
inorganic carbon (DIC) pools and enhanced biomass yields in freshwater and marine 
microalgae, with productivity gains linked to species-specific carbon concentrating 
mechanisms (CCMs) [150]. Finally, a review underscored the critical role of 
extracellular CA in aquatic phototrophs, especially under fluctuating CO2 conditions, 
where enzymatic acceleration of CO2 ↔ HCO3- interconversion ensures greater 
retention and assimilation efficiency [29]. Although most experiments were conducted 
at 0.03–5% CO2, these mechanisms predict equal or greater gains at higher CO2, 
where rapid hydration becomes a rate limiting. Under this mechanism, cocultures that 
maintain near-neutral pH and supply extracellular CA, with or without complementary

(µmax = 0.507 day-1; 0.706 g/L/day CO₂ fix).  This behavior is consistent with two 
mechanisms: superior kLa and time control in flat-panel PBRs that stabilize DIC and 
pH under heavy sparging, and secondly, enzymatic acceleration of CO2 hydration to 
HCO3- via bacterial/exogenous carbonic anhydrase (CA), which tightens the coupling 
between gas supply and microalgal CCM uptake [114,169–171]. 
 
Taken together, the table supports a process logic echoed by recent studies: provide 
modest organics to de-risk media limitations (MFM+), operate at 15–20% CO₂ only 
with deliberate pH and kLa control (preferably in flat-panel PBRs), and exploit algal–
bacterial complementarity, including CA mediated CO2↔HCO3- conversion, to 
translate high gas mass into net carbon capture [87,164,169]. The highest single run 
biofixation (0.763 g/L/day at 20% CO2, PBR coculture) reflects the deliberate 
integration of hydrodynamics and biology that mitigated DIC-kinetic constraints under 
elevated CO2. Overall, under high-CO2 operation, reactor design and biological 
partnership are the decisive levers for sustaining carbon capture. Optimizing gas–
liquid transfer, pH/DIC control, and algal–bacterial function can redirect inhibitory CO2 
levels into net performance improvements.

4. Conclusions 
This study set out to optimize and develop a microalga–bacterium coculture that 
sustains high-CO2 biofixation by coupling reactor hydrodynamics with an auxiliary 
inorganic carbon conversion function. In order to achieve that, first, a common mineral 
medium was established, and then monocultures and cocultures were tested across 
flasks, bottles, and a flat-panel photobioreactor (PBR) under elevated CO2. Mutually, 
the results demonstrate that process design and appropriate selection of the 
microorganisms, can convert High CO2 from a stressor into a performance lever. 
 
==PAGE:67==
 
55 
A central enabling step was the development of an optimized, common medium 
(MFM+) that balanced photoautotrophic needs with restrained heterotrophy. MFM+ 
composed BG11 salts supplemented with 2 g/L glucose, 3 g/L yeast extract, and 10 
µM Zn2+. This formula supported robust Chlorella sorokiniana growth, moderated 
Bacillus subtilis proliferation, and preserved extracellular enzyme secretion, the 
targeted function from the bacterial partner. Consistent with that design, supernatants 
free of cells from B. subtilis displayed carbonic anhydrase (CA) activity in both a 
Wilbur–Anderson pH assay and a p-nitrophenyl acetate colorimetric assay, with Zn 
supplementation materially boosting apparent activity, biochemically coherent with the 
Zn-dependent CA active site. 
 
CO2 tolerance screens and bottle cocultures revealed the expected inhibition of green 
microalgae at excessive sparging, but also a practical outcome: 15% CO2 
outperformed 30% CO2 at this scale (µmax 0.487 vs. 0.346 day-1; productivity 0.453 vs. 
0.389 g/L/day), underscoring the need for buffering and adequate gas–liquid transfer 
when operating above ambient. These patterns motivated trials in a flat-panel PBR, 
whose geometry is more alike the setups used in industry that are well suited to 
intensified CO2 operation because of improved kLa, light path, sparging, and pH 
control. 
 
In the PBR, cocultures converted high inlet CO2 into sustained capture. Under 
continuous purging of 20% CO2, the coculture reached 2.35 g/L, 1.040 g/L, and 0.763 
g/L/day biomass, TOC, and CO2 biofixation rate, respectively.  Additionally,  
concurrent signals support tight coupling between hydrodynamics and CA mediated 
DIC supply: in vivo chlorophyll fluorescence (IVF440-690) rose from 2100 to 4800 over 
six days in the coculture while the monoculture remained flat; culture pH separated 
early and durably (coculture 7.3–7.4 vs. monoculture 5.8–6.1); and a transient 
extracellular CA pulse (10–11 U/L at 24 h) accompanied the initial hypercapnic load. 
Overall, the results show that speeding up the conversion of CO2 to bicarbonate right 
next to the cells, and keeping pH and dissolved inorganic carbon stable, prevents the 
high-CO2 inhibition that usually happens at strong sparging. 
 
It is worth mentioning that interpretation should be cautious. The highest biofixation 
value reflects one experiment; therefore, replication at 20% CO₂ with measured kLa 
and on-line pH/DIC would strengthen confidence. The Wilbur–Anderson test was run 
once, and while the colorimetric assay increased throughput, attributing activity solely 
to CA needs inhibitor tests or CA-deficient controls. Also, it wasn’t quantifying kLa or 
tracking how fixed carbon is split between biomass and excreted organics. 
 
Even with those future recommendations, the main conclusions are clear: First of all, 
a lightly supplemented mineral medium with trace Zn (MFM+) enables functional 
cocultures by preventing bacterial overgrowth while preserving extracellular CA 
activity; Secondly, scale and reactor architecture matter, flat-panel PBRs provide the 
pH and gas-transfer control necessary to operate effectively at 20% CO2. Finally, 
pairing C. sorokiniana with B. subtilis that secretes CA can transform high-CO2 stress 
into net advantages in carbon capture, culminating in 0.763 g/L/day biofixation at 20% 
CO2. 
 
Building on these conclusions, and looking ahead to translation and scale-up, the data 
define a practical operating window and a set of engineering levers. Specifically, 
==PAGE:68==
 
56 
reactor choice should privilege high kLa geometries (e.g., flat-panel) paired with 
moderate inlet CO2 (15–20%) and active pH management that holds the bulk phase 
near neutrality; within that envelope, raising interfacial area and alkalinity is preferable 
to further increasing the CO2 fraction. Likewise, media should remain supplemented 
(MFM+: BG11 salts + 2 g/L glucose + 3 g/L yeast extract) with Zn2+ to support early 
extracellular CA activity while restraining bacterial overgrowth. 
 
In order to improve attribution and reduce scale-up risk, the next experiments should: 
First, replicate the 20% CO2 PBR runs with on-line pH/DIC and independent kLa 
mapping. Second, include CA-inhibited or CA-deficient Bacillus controls alongside off-
gas CO2 and TOC/DOC balances to close the carbon budget. Third, evaluate 
robustness to realistic impurities and process upsets (e.g., variable gas flow, light 
fluctuations). In parallel, integrating product analytics (amino acids, lipids, 
carbohydrates) with techno-economic and life-cycle assessments will clarify where 
product valorization can offset gas-handling costs; taken together, these steps convert 
the demonstrated 0.763 g/L/day biofixation at 20% CO2 from a promising result into a 
scalable, well-constrained process blueprint.

A.1. Protocols for Media Preparation 
A.1.1 BG11 
Reagents (1L; pH 7.8): 
• NaNO3 - 1.5 g 
• MgSO4·7H2O - 0.075 g 
• CaCl2·2H2O - 0.036 g 
• Na2CO3 - 0.02 g 
• C6H8O7⋅xFe3+⋅yN3 - 0.0006 g 
• C6H8O7 - 0.006 g 
• K2HPO4 - 0.04 g 
• EDTA + BG11 traces 
 
Method: 
1. Dissolve in 50 mL Fe source + Citric acid. 
2. Dissolve K2HPO4 in 50 mL of water. 
3. Dissolve the remain salts in 900 mL ultrapure water + EDTA and the trace mix. 
4. Sterilize (autoclave 121 °C, 15 min).  
5. Mix the three solutions to complete 1 L; adjust pH to 7.8 if needed. 
6. Store at room temperature in dark conditions. 
A.1.2 MFM+ 
Reagents (1L; pH 7.5): 
• MgSO4·7H2O - 1.0 g  
• KNO3 - 1.0 g 
• NaNO3 - 1.0 g 
• H3BO3 - 0.0029 g 
• CaCl2·2H2O - 0.036 g 
• C6H8O7⋅xFe3+⋅yN3 - 0.006 g 
• EDTA - 0.004 g 
• C6H8O7 - 0.006 g 
• K2HPO4 - 0.272 g 
• Na2HPO4 - 0.284 g 
• Trace Element Solution 
• Yeast Extract  
• Glucose 
• Zinc 
 
Method: 
1. Dissolve in 50 mL Fe source + Citric acid + EDTA. 
2. Dissolve K2HPO4 and Na2HPO4 in 50 mL of water. 
3. Dissolve the remain salts in 900 mL ultrapure water + the trace mix. 
4. Sterilize (autoclave 121 °C, 15 min).  
5. Mix the three solutions to complete 1 L; adjust pH to 7.5 if needed. 
6. Prepare sterile 20% (w/v) glucose and sterile-filtered YE stock (e.g., 300 g/L).
