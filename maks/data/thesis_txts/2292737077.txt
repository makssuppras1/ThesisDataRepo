Abstract
The commercial ﬁnite element software Abaqus has been used to model the compression
behavior of CFRP laminates. The FE models are based on elastic constants calculated from
experimental data. A non-linear material model is used together with the geometry of a test
specimen and a loading similar to that induced by the test ﬁxture. The material model assumes
a linear behavior of the ﬁbers while the matrix is elastic-plastic and follows an actual stress-
strain curve. To capture the dominating failure mechanism - kink band formation - the model
accounts for large rotations. The main investigations are concerned with the inﬂuence of the
clamping effects on the test specimen. Test specimens with different thicknesses have different
compression strengths. The stress state at failure shows signiﬁcant stress concentrations where
the tabs end towards the gauge area. A Tsai-Hill failure index of 1.69 and 1.54 is found for the
thin and thick specimen respectively.
Key words:
Test ﬁxture clamping effects, carbon ﬁber composites, compression strength, ﬁnite element
modeling, non-linear material model, wind turbine blade design, kink band formation

Resumé
Kulﬁberlaminaters opførsel under tryk er blevet undersøgt ved hjælp af ﬁnite element-programmet
Abaqus. Grundlaget for modellerne er elastiske konstanter beregnet fra eksperimentel data.
Den faktiske testemnegeometri er modelleret med en ikke-lineær materialemodel for at kunne
simulere den dominerende fejlmekanisme - kinkbånd. Grips er medtaget i modelleringen i
begrænset omfang for at påføre lasten via indspændingen og derved kunne se den formodede
effekt af spændingskoncentrationer. Modellen tager højde for store rotationer for at approxi-
mere ﬁbrenes position, når disse kinker. Materialemodellen anvender en spændingskurve
for resinen for at kunne håndtere den elastisk-plastiske opførsel. Fibrene er antaget lineære.
Eksperimenter har vist en markant forskel i kompressionsstyrke på testemner med forskellig
tykkelse, og dette fænomen er undersøgt. Der ses rigtigt nok spændingskoncentrationer i ud-
kanten af gaugeområderne. Målt vha. et Tsai-Hill fejlkriterium ﬁndes et index på henholdsvis
1.69 og 1.54 for det tynde og det tykke emne. Dette leder til kinkbånd.

Introduction
The design process of a ﬁber composite structure, such as a wind turbine blade, is a compli-
cated matter. It involves a lot of estimations and numerical simulations based on material
properties from coupon testing as well as aerodynamical restrictions on the geometry and
load envelopes for the speciﬁc turbine. The latter is again based on requirements from the
tower, the nacelle and location of the wind farm. All of the above must be well-documented
and prove that the standards of certifying bodies such as Germanischer Lloyd or Det Norske
Veritas are fulﬁlled. The ﬁnal assessment of the design is full-scale testing of the composite
structure to show that estimations and assumptions are covered by safety factors, and that
material values, design details and production quality are in control.
This report is concerned with a cornerstone of the design foundation, namely the determina-
tion of the compression strength of laminates. The compression strength is often a limiting
factor because it is typically 50-60% of the tensile strength for CFRP’s according to [Fleck, 1997].
It is common practice to handle uncertainties of material data and missing understanding
of failure mechanisms by use of statistical means and safety factors. The compression fail-
ure mechanisms have been described by [Budiansky, 1983] and [Fleck, 1997], among others,
but there are still improvements to be made. Improving the knowledge of the compression
strength would allow for the reduction of the safety factors and thus make a more effective
design of ﬁber composites possible.
This report attempts to investigate in detail the behavior of CFRP laminates in the course of
the compression test. The procedure is the opposite of the design process as ﬁnite element
analysis is used to simulate the test coupons to determine the main inﬂuences on the com-
pression strength. The postulate is that the compression strength is limited by remote shear
stresses which cause stress concentrations in the beginning of the gauge area (i.e. where the
tabs end). The stress concentrations lead to a rotation of the ﬁbers once the matrix starts
to yield, practically allowing single ﬁbers and ﬁber bundles to start buckling. At this point
the load-carrying ability of the ﬁbers, and hence the laminate, is compromised. Total failure
follows quickly hereafter.
For this purpose experimental data has been evaluated from several test series to have a
well-founded material input for the ﬁnite element analysis. Thus minimizing the uncertainty

1 Theory
Generally ﬁber composites are complicated to describe mathematically due to their anisotropic
composition both on a constituent level and as a part or structure. Add to that the many
possible failure mechanisms that occur under different loading conditions and the analysis
becomes lengthy. Experimental studies therefore often accompany any numerical simulation
to create a well-designed component of reinforced ﬁber plastics.
For compression of composites the analysis becomes an instability problem. At a certain point
the test specimen will reach the bifurcation stress where it costs less energy to buckle than to
compress the material further. This behavior is common for metals as well. The bifurcation
stress is not considered the compression strength of the material but actually a consequence
of the geometry. Therefore it is common practice to add a supporting structure around the
test specimen to prevent premature buckling. The test ﬁxture introduced in section 2.1 uses
grips for that purpose but, as can be seen on the experimental results in chapter 2, it comes
with a price.
The last major factor that needs mentioning is the manufacturing process of composites. As
[Zenkert and Battley, 2009] describes there are a number of things going on during manu-
facturing that inﬂuence the quality and thereby the ﬁnal properties of the composite. A few
of these include proper layup and alignment of the ﬁbers, good wet-out during infusion to
prevent the entrapment of air bubbles and the conditions by which the composite is cured.
Precautions must also be taken under the preparation of the specimens to avoid rough surfaces
and delaminations.
1.1
Failure Modes of Composites in Compression
The academic society has been trying to quantify the physically correct compression strength
of ﬁber reinforced plastics since B.W. Rosen in 1965 suggested that the compression strength
was equal to the shear modulus of the composite under the assumption that the constituents
behaved linearly. It was quickly acknowledged that elasticity wasn’t enough to describe the
compression behavior by both [Budiansky, 1983] and later [Fleck, 1997]. The latter mentions
how experimental values were closer to a fourth of the composite shear modulus. A.S. Argon
deserves a mention too for his article from 1972 Fracture in Composites. Together they gathered
evidence for the existence of the six prevailing failure phenomena illustrated in ﬁgure 1.1.
The overall failure mechanism is known as plastic microbuckling. According to [Fleck, 1997]

1.1.1

factors controlling the compression strength, led Argon (1972) to describe it as eq. (1.1). He
furthermore assumed the composite to behave as rigid-perfectly plastic and the kink band to
have no inclination (i.e. β = 0◦).

1.2
Micromechanical Models
The experimental data used in the FE models is converted to elastic constants by means of
micromechanical models. To enable the calculation of all nine elastic constants needed for an
orthotropic material the ﬁber modulus is back-calculated from the CFRP laminate.
Commonly an engineer makes four assumptions when trying to predict the elastic constants
of a laminate. According to [Zenkert and Battley, 2009] these involve the following points.
• The matrix is homogeneous, isotropic and linearly elastic.
• The ﬁbers are homogeneous, isotropic, linearly elastic, regularly spaced and perfectly
aligned. Thus no contiguity of ﬁbers.
• No voids are present in the ﬁbers or matrix, bonding is complete between the con-
stituents and no transitional region between them.
• The lamina is macroscopically homogeneous and orthotropic, linearly elastic and free
of residual stresses.
Obviously these assumptions describe a situation better than the reality when the manufactur-
ing process is factored in. The process is best controlled in a lab when a set of test specimens
are made but even then it is difﬁcult if not impossible to keep the ﬁbers perfectly aligned and
regularly spaced. It is also hard to keep the matrix void free and the ﬁber-matrix interface com-
pletely bonded. This is when considering vacuum assisted resin transfer molding (VARTM).
Using prepregs or other forms of RTM processes and an autoclave will improve the quality
of the laminate but also increase the price and complexity. The improved process will still
not guarantee a perfect laminate. If we consider the manufacturing process on an even larger
scale, such as a wind turbine blade, which can be as large as 75 meters in length, the process
becomes equally harder to control.

adopted in this report. Thus (x, y,z) denote directions in a laminate where (1,2,3) denote the

1.4
The Non-Linear Material Model (UMAT)
The theory of kink band formation developed by Budiansky and Fleck was further developed
into a generalized constitutive model by [Christoffersen and Jensen, 1996] and [Jensen and
Christoffersen, 1997]. The model allows for standard elastic-plastic constitutive equations to
be used for the constituents, and enables the study of initially present imperfections or the
development of a kink band at the bifurcation stress. That means that J2-ﬂow theory can be
used to describe the ﬁbers and the matrix. Therefore, instead of supplying the FE analysis with
the elastic constants of a laminate, it is now given the properties of the constituents in order to
model the specimen as a smeared-out material.
The smeared-out material model saves a lot of computational time but it also means that a
lamina contains evenly distributed ﬁbers and matrix material. Unfortunately that means that
variation of the sizing of the carbon ﬁbers (i.e. the adhesion between ﬁbers and matrix) will
not be apparent in this model. In this model the assumptions from the ’slab model’ carries
over - the stresses in ﬁbers and matrix are identical in the lengthwise direction, and the strains
are identical in both constituents in the transverse direction.
The constituent material behavior is deﬁned by the effective von Mises stress in eq. (1.17) and
the standard power-law in (1.18) as described by [Sørensen et al., 2008].

strength are calculated.

in general. Normal engineering deﬁnitions cannot account for larger deformations. The

3 Finite Element Modeling
This chapter is meant as a manual for recreating the results in chapter 4 based on the theory
and input data of chapters 1 and 2 respectively. Therefore this chapter includes the geometries,
boundary conditions, loads, mesh etc. for the analyzed models. The strategy is to start
off by simplifying the analysis as much as possible and build it up to closely resemble the
real compression test conditions. The ﬁrst FE analysis is concerned with a simple linear
symmetric model of the test specimen with the grip attached to it. Then an elastic-plastic
glue is implemented between the tab and the specimen before the UMAT is also implemented.
Finally everything will be combined in a model of the full-scale test specimen. This last step is
necessary because the failure initiation cannot be predicted nor is it possible to say how it will
grow inside the specimen. The symmetry conditions of the quarter-model simply assumes
that everything is mirrored around the axis of symmetry.
The desired outcome of all the analyses is the stress state in the specimen and the effect of
material deﬁnitions, load distributions and the expected failure mode. Both longitudinal,
transverse and shear stresses are believed to be present when failure initiates so all three are
interesting to quantify. The kink band (i.e. the expected failure mode) is thought to be caused
by stress concentrations around the tabs which further cause the ﬁbers to become misaligned
when the matrix yields. The analyses are conducted for both a 2.8mm and a 5.9mm specimen
because these were the thicknesses chosen for experimental characterization in chapter 2 (i.e.
test series CXG22 and CXG23).
3.1
The Symmetric Model
The test specimen is ﬁrst off modeled as one quarter of the full geometry using the material
elastic constants as deﬁned in section 2.6. For each variation of the FE analysis the model
consists of a specimen with a carbon ﬁber laminate, a glass ﬁber tab and a steel grip. The
geometry, boundary conditions and points of loading are kept constant as well. This is all
illustrated in ﬁgure 3.1. Thickness of the tab is 2mm and the grip is shifted 1mm to the right
from the start of the tab. The width of the specimens is 15mm, but the model will be reduced

The modeled thicknesses are t = 1.4mm for the thin specimen and t = 2.95mm for the thick
specimen. An important thing to notice is that the coordinate system is different on ﬁgure 3.1

Numerical simulations are per deﬁnition approximations of reality. Usually very good approxi-
mations, but to insure that, it is necessary to deﬁne a proper mesh with the right elements.
Seeing as it is a plane problem it is a question of using plane strain or plane stress elements. In
Abaqus these are called CPE4R and CPS4R elements respectively. They are 4-node elements
with reduced integration enabled. A comparison of the two element types is shown in ﬁgure

plane stress and plane strain. The differences are due to the amount of elements. The min-

plane strain element CPE4R is chosen for the analyses. Plane strain makes the most sense
physically because the grips constrain the deformation in the thickness direction of a large
area of the test specimen.
Mesh convergence is determined by increasing the amount of elements on the model. The
size of the elements near the tab end is set to less than half the distance that the path is

5 Discussion
The background for this project was a series of compression tests performed in the lab at
DTU Risø. The experimental data showed a difference in the compression strengths of test
specimens with different thicknesses. Such inconsistency should not exist in material testing,
where the strength is considered a material property. The experimental results didn’t reveal
any indications as to where the difference originated but it is known from experience that
sudden changes in material or geometry leads to stress concentrations. Therefore the decision
was made to try and analyze the testing process with numerical simulations, in order to test
the hypothesis and quantify the magnitude of the stress in the transition from tab to specimen.
The modeling results seem to suggest that the loading through the grips should always be
minimized to the point where the grips only serve to prevent buckling. This is mainly due
to a smaller normal force PG2 on the tabs and secondly due to a little lower shear force PG1.
The impact of changing the normal force is greater for the thick specimens simply because
the applied force is larger than that applied to the thin specimen. This would partly solve
the problem but it is likely not possible to lower the shear stress sufﬁciently because the end
loading must stay below the point where ﬁber crushing becomes the dominant failure mode
though. Another suggestion could be to re-design the test specimen. Chamfering the edges of
the tabs towards the gauge area would provide a smoother force ﬂow from the grips through
the tabs and into the test specimen. A second possibility would be to add a layer of ±45◦
bi-axial ﬁbers on the surface of the CFRP part of the test specimen. It would ﬁt in between the
CFRP and the tabs and contribute with a higher shear stiffness. Both design changes would
minimize the stress concentrations.
A deﬁnitive conclusion is hard to agree upon. The symmetric model with the glue bond shows
relatively believable stresses in and around the stress concentration but there are still many
assumptions involved in the modeling that could be improved or possibly removed.
The ﬁrst source of uncertainties is the material input used in Abaqus. Micromechanical models
are widely used but they are also widely known to be inaccurate in all other directions than
the ﬁber direction. A possibility would be to test all nine elastic constants used for describing
the orthotropy instead of trying to estimate them. Unfortunately the off-axis experiments
are rather imprecise as well. The coefﬁcient of variance between two test series of transverse
characterization can easily be 10-15% on the E-modulus. Thus the engineer is left with the
choice of accepting the micromechanical models as they are or to complete a large test

Looking at the ﬁnite element modeling efforts there are several things to discuss. For one, it
is quite clear that linear materials are not sufﬁcient to describe the compressive behavior of
ﬁber reinforced composites. Adding the elastic-plastic glue bond makes a huge impact on
the stress state inside the model and the magnitude of the stress concentration. The glue is
deﬁned as epoxy which has to mean that the epoxy in the remaining test specimen, i.e. the
resin in the CFRP and GFRP, will also act elastic-plastically in reality. This is not accounted
for. Further studies could also be conducted on the glue bond to determine if the results show
the full impact. For instance a small study was tried where the plastic strain was elongated 50
times instead of 10 as reported in chapter 2. The plastic deformation of the glue bond only
showed a small decrease and it wasn’t pursued further.
It did though raise the question if the strains would be interesting to look into. The focus of
the results were the stresses because the failure mechanism is thought to be governed by the
stress state. An open question for instance is that the asymptotic behavior persists for σ12 on
the models with the glue bond, where it is almost eliminated for σ11 and σ22. Why is that? Is it
due to the fact that discretization errors become more pronounced when moving outside the
elastic range? Allowing the CFRP specimen to shear plastically would likely solve it.
A study that was left out of chapter 4 was the direct comparison of the models with glue under
different remote load distributions. The thin specimens show identical behavior for σ11 and
σ12 and it is almost the same with the thick specimens. The thick specimens show a slightly
larger spread of the stress outside the stress concentration. σ22 is signiﬁcantly lowered for
both thicknesses as expected.

something to dig into for others.
The UMAT subroutine described in section 1.4 should be applied to the model and it should be
run with the full specimen geometry. This way the material behavior would resemble reality a
lot more and it would be possible to follow the progress of the compression test on both sides

6 Conclusion
The overall goal of the work in this report was to investigate the effect of the clamping forces
during a compression test. Clamping forces were suspected to be the reason behind the
different compression strengths found in chapter 2 for CFRP test specimens of different
thicknesses. The test results showed a trend similar to what can be found in the literature.
[Jacobsen, 2001] for instance showed the same counter-intuitive behavior. The strength is in
its deﬁnition independent of the thickness.
As shown in chapter 4 a clamping force is transferred to the test specimen through the tabs as a
shear stress, which in turn causes a stress concentration where the tabs end and the gauge area
begins. The stress concentration has been quantiﬁed with two different models under two
different load distributions. A stress concentration is a very local phenomenon and in this case
it caused a multi-axial stress state in the test specimen. The result was plastic deformation of
the matrix due to the shear component σ12 and buckling of the ﬁbers due to the transverse
component σ22. Together this caused the ﬁbers to rotate and a kink band to form causing the
test specimen to lose its load-carrying ability.
The results of the project conﬁrm the suspicion that stress concentrations should be avoided
when designing a compression test procedure and specimen. It is recommended to limit the
clamping forces as much as possible while still supporting the test specimen sufﬁciently to
prevent premature buckling. This also leads to the conclusion that the compression strength
engineers use to design wind turbine blades is conservative because the test specimen would
likely be able to sustain higher loads if the stress concentrations were not present.
To gain an even deeper understanding of the behavior and failure mechanism it is recom-
mended to model the full test specimen with grips and the non-linear material model de-
scribed in section 1.4.
