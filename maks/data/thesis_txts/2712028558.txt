Abstract  
Oral drug delivery is one of the most progressive routes to deliver encapsulate drug compounds due 
to the pH changing through the gastrointestinal (GI) tract. Previous studies have investigated 
imaging in nanoscale by encapsulating the contrast agent to deliver to the target site, inside the body. 
The studies on radiopaque electro-spun sheet, loaded with drugs are limited. In this study, 
encapsulation of microparticles loaded with barium sulfate, (BS), was investigated in two different 
materials, poly(lactic-co-glycolic) acid (PLGA) and silk fibroin (SF) for tracking X-ray imaging in the 
rat GI tract. Initially, the SF was produced by degumming and dissolving procedures applying 
Lithium Bromide (LiBr). The SF was modified using glycidyl methacrylater (GMA) leading to SILMA 
prototype and then distinct of polyethylene-oxide (PEO) concentrations was employed to obtain the 
required ratio. BS was incorporated at different wt% [0, 10, 20, 30, 40 and 50] and mixed carefully 
with the mentioned materials. For PLGA, a mixture procedure was necessarily applied to ensure the 
homogeneity with BS. Subsequently, the electrospinning was conducted to get uniform distribution 
of fibers. The electro-spun sheets were embossed to obtain microparticles where the shape meet the 
recommendation of the responsible person, therefore the shape was no further considered. 
Subsequently, the microparticles was successfully filled into size 9 gelatin capsules and orally 
administrated to rats, where two euthanasia time points were chosen [1 h; 3h] for two groups, each 
containing three rats. The purpose was to evaluate the transit time of the micro-particles and to get 
an idea of the contrast level obtainable with planar X-ray imaging. The results for PLGA 
microparticles showed challenges in respect to obtaining a homogenous distribution of BS. 
Furthermore, none PLGA microparticles achieved during the µ-cutting. Otherwise, the SILMA in 
vivo evaluation using X-ray imaging showed a repeatedly accumulation in the stomach due to 
unexpected or atypical events that may have occurred pending encapsulation procedure. 
Successively, the tracking of the radiopaque material is obtained, however none exact data are 
obtained.

Introduction 
Nanomedicine is one of the newest technologies to specify the treatment to ensure enhanced patient 
quality of life [1]. Nanomedicine is used to designate diseases at nanoscale, by applying imaging in 
molecular and clinical context. Most molecular and clinical imaging are acquired via the use of 
contrast agents to perceive the differences between tissues. These contrast agents can be capsulated 
to ensure delivery limited to the target site, inside the body [1].  
In vivo imaging techniques used can offer important data related to the site for drug delivery and 
the behavior of formulation. However, it has been reported that formulations can be difficult and 
challenging in a particular case of orally administered bioactive agents targeted to a specific intestine 
segment. To get high efficiency of the tablet, the site of delivery is a crucial parameter [2]. Moreover, 
the efficiency of the active agent cannot be followed by pharmacokinetic or pharmacodynamics 
studies when this active agent is not absorbed [2]. Since intestinal content and peristalsis can 
influence the entire formulation, imaging techniques could be an effective method to monitor 
dosage-form shape during its gastric and intestinal transit letting in vivo-in vitro correlation [2]. 
Scintigraphy and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) are used commonly for monitoring tablet 
transit. MRI is costly, requires a long data acquisition time and necessitates general anesthesia in 
animal models. However, it could clearly observe floating formulation in the stomach, knowing that 
visualization in lower GI tract is challenging because of the superposition of the bowels [3]. 
Scintigraphy has been studied extensively in literature [4] [5] [6] [7] [8]. However, scintigraphy has 
some disadvantages such as [4] [5] 
→ it requires radioisotopes (for example samarium or technetium) that are not easily available. 
→ associated with radiation exposure 
→ potential risks of radiation contamination of facilities 
→ poor anatomical resolution 
→  low contrast 
X ray radiographic techniques are considered beneficial diagnostic tools to monitor any form of oral 
dosage of medication. Compared with MRI and scintigraphy, X-ray radiographic techniques have 
various advantages such as [9] [10] [11] 
→ Radiography is accessible in most research centers or through veterinary teaching hospitals 
==PAGE:11==
11 | P a g e  
 
→ general anesthesia in not required 
→ can be performed without manual restraint avoiding radiation exposure of staff   
→ less expensive than other imaging techniques 
Previous work [12] [13] [14] have studied the interesting features of BS. They reported that 
incorporation of barium sulfate (BS) in a pharmaceutical vehicle permits visual monitoring of the 
passage in the GI tract. In addition, BS is highly suitable for non-invasive radiographic imaging of 
the GI tract for diagnostic due to high atomic number of barium and ideal energy interaction in the 
diagnostic range of X-rays. This method has been used for this purpose for several decades in dogs 
and humans [15] [16] [17] [18] [19] [20] [21] [22]. Moreover, BS has a high density and is insoluble 
in the GI tract. In other words, BS will not interact with other ingredients when incorporated into 
tablets, resulted in orally administered tablets to be monitored in vivo. Concentrations of BS in a 
tablet formulation may vary according to the aim of the study i.e., estimation of floating or efficiency 
of tablet integrity and coating [23]. 
Previous studies [24] [25] [26] [27] [28] have investigated in vivo administration of formulations 
incorporated with only BS. There are limited work concerning the monitoring of drug-containing 
tablets with BS [29] [30]. However, none is studying forms of oral dosages with biological molecules 
i.e. enzymes, proteins. 
When using BS in solid dosage forms, the important step is finding a good balance of its loading 
that should not be too high in order to maintain tablet properties, and not too low to offer a good 
in vivo contrast, resulting in tablet traceability by X-rays.  
It is possible to design matrix formulations with appropriate properties for a certain drug or delivery 
site. In other words, anionic matrices could provide gastric protection by offering an outer layer of 
gel. At the same time neutral matrices as hydroxypropyl methylcellulose (HPMC) could offer a high 
carrying capacity and less interaction with other excipients or active components. 
Recent studies concerning the possibility of derived biopolymers as carboxymethyl starch (CMS) to 
carry and protect biological agents i.e., peptides, enzymes or probiotics when administrated orally as 
solid dosage forms was widely explored [31] [32] [33]. The results showed monolithic tablets based 
on CMS or CMS and chitosan (CHI) obtained by direct compression proved gastric protection and 
targeted release of the drug. Moreover, resent work regarding CMS [34] [35] showed that the CMS 
may modify the dissolution rate by its degree of substitution (DS) affecting the susceptibility to 
==PAGE:12==
12 | P a g e  
 
intestinal pH and αamylase enzyme.  
Calinescu et al. [34] have studied the effect of CHI which is a cationic polymer soluble in acidic 
media. They found that CHI has the ability to form a protective layer of gel in gastric fluid and is 
degradable in the colon, resulted in a targeted drug release. 
Although, in vitro dissolution studies are reproducible, simulating living biota and body peristaltic 
motion is more challenging. Due to physiological and biochemical variability in their GI tract 
features was observed Inter and interspecies differences. Regardless of these differences, a high 
degree of similarity is shown in the ratio between the lengths of different segments of the intestine 
in case of dog, pig and human [36]. As a result, intestinal transit for in vivo model such as dogs may 
link well with humans in terms of diet, organ functions and residence times for forms of solid dosage 
[37] [38] [39]. 
Development of electro-spun (e-spun) nanofibers loaded with drugs are expanded due to its high 
loading capacity, high encapsulation efficiency, ease of administration and the low cost [40]. E-spun 
nanofibers show also mechanical advantages such as its high surface area to volume/mass ratio, small 
inter-fibrous pore size with high porosity and as well as surface functionalization [41]. 
Kenawy et al. published the first studying of e-spun nanofibers constituted of poly (lactic acid) (PLA) 
and poly (ethylene-co-vinyl acetate) (PEVA) loaded with tetracycline hydrochloride [42]. The study 
concluded that e-spun nanofibers have been successfully, used to accomplish different controlled 
drug release profiles. Xu et al. investigate e-spun poly (L-lactic acid) (PLLA) to control co-delivery of 
both hydrophobic drug benzoin and hydrophilic bovine serum albumin (BSA), where the release 
modulation was acquired, by adding the polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) [43]. The ratio of PVP/PLLA 
play a critical role on the release modulation of benzoin and BSA, correspondingly, the cumulative 
release can be avoided.  
According to a literature search, little attention has been paid concerning e-spun nanofiber forms 
loaded with BS, using X-ray imaging. Therefore, the present study, aims to improve the influence of 
BS content on e-spun nanofiber formulations and secondly to target the rat GI tract by link in vitro-
in vivo data using X-ray imaging.

5.1 
The Human gastrointestinal tract 
The digestive system provides the body with a continual supply of water and nutrients by ensuring 
breakdown and absorption of food. The figure 5.1-1 show the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, where it 
divides into three parts and consists as following [44] 
1- The upper part: the mouth and pharynx, the esophagus, and the stomach – the main function 
is an intake source, allow food transportation and determine in which initial digestive 
processes it will take place 
2- The middle part:  the duodenum, jejunum, and ileum of the small intestine where the 
digestion and absorption record  
a. The pancreas, the liver with the gallbladder are the solid organs of the digestive 
system 
3- The lower part: the cecum, colon, and the rectum of the large intestine subsequently the 
storage and elimination occur  
The four main processes of the digestive system are motility, secretion, digestion, and absorption. 
Firstly, the food will be dismantled and then mixed, with saliva with help of the salivary glands. 
Conversely, the nutrients will be absorbed, the unabsorbed wastes will be collected and eliminated

in the large intestine and finally, the vitamins will be synthesized. Saliva contains a digestive enzyme 
called amylase that already begins to digest carbohydrates in the mouth [44].  
The carbohydrates will be split into smaller units, creating a ball known as bolus. The bolus move 
systematically trough the throat by the tongue and finally into the esophagus which propels the bolus 
to the stomach. The esophageal lumen is very flexible, which allows boluses of different sizes to be 
transported. The bolus will be broken down into ions and molecules and absorbed into the blood 
paths and the lymph nodules [44]. 
The stomach wall contains gastric glands - the mucosal and submucosal glands – secrete mucus to 
protect the stomach and aids in lubricating food. One to two liters of gastric juice are produced per 
day. Since the esophagus does not have a protective mucus layer, like the stomach, a sphincter 
separates the stomach and esophagus. Where it relaxes, the bolus is pushed from the esophagus into 
the stomach and then contracts to prevent acid food from going back up. Gastric juice consists 
among of other things of hydrochloric, the enzyme pepsin, the intrinsic factor and lipase for the 
digestion of fats. Hydrochloric acid converts pepsinogen into pepsin. Pepsin can breakdown the 
proteins in the stomach. For vitamin B12 absorption in the small intestine the intrinsic factor is 
needed which is produced by the gastric glands [44].  
The individual boluses are mixed to a semi fluid mass of partly digested food the so-called chyme.  
In the first section of the small intestine, the duodenum bile, and pancreatic secretion are mixed 
with the chyme via the ampulla vater. Pancreatic juice contains numerous digestive proenzymes and 
enzymes. In order for these to do their job, a higher pH value than that in the stomach is necessary. 
For this reason, the pancreatic juice contains sodium hydrogen carbonate. The hydrogen carbonate 
is able to neutralize the acid in the chyme and thus produce the optimum pH value of 7-8 [44] [45].  
In the small intestine, both the duodenum and jejunum have circular folds to increase the contract 
surface with the food. These circular folds extend about a centimeter into the lumen of the small 
intestine. These folds are covered with small finger like projections called villi see figure 5.1-2. Villi 
increase the surface considerably and are about one millimeter long. Each villus contains blood 
capillaries and a lymphatic capillary called lacteal. Each villus is covered by even smaller microvilli. 
The nutrients are absorbed and transferred to the blood capillaries. Some nutrients such as glucose, 
do not require a carrier, they are transported freely, in the blood stream. Other nutrients such as 
iron, require transport proteins transferrin. Fats are transported by chylomicrons, which are

lipoproteins [44].   The ileum is the last part of the small intestine and did not have circular folds 
like duodenum and jejunum.  The ileum absorbs electrolytes, such as calcium for building bones, 
hair and teeth. Trace elements such as zinc for sperm production and immune system, vitamins such 
as B12 for the formation and maturation of red blood cells and remaining bile acid, which is 
transported back to the liver via blood stream. The large intestine is thicker than the small intestine. 
The small intestine is connected to the large intestine by the Bauhin’s valve – it opens when chyme 
will pass. The large intestine does not have any villi like the small intestine because most digestive 
substances have already been absorbed in the small intestine. Conversely, the large intestine has an 
estimated 100 billion bacteria inside, they are important for many other tasks such as the production 
of vitamins and the decomposition of the fiber for the body’s energy production. Many of these 
bacteria are an important part of the immune system [44]. Through peristalsis, the chyme is 
transported from the ascending colon to the transverse colon to the descending colon. On its way 
through the large intestine, water is removed from the chyme. Furthermore, mucus is added for 
proper excretion of waste. Substances that cannot be absorbed by the small intestine or the large 
intestine remain in the rectum and are finally excreted through the anus [44].

5.1.1 
The pH and transit time 
The pH and the transit time are important parameters for understanding the reaction behind 
passaging of drug devices through the GI tract [46]. Figure 5.1-1 illustrates different pH values 
depending on its increasing or decreasing compared to where on the GI tract. The pH ranges 
between one and three on the gastric transit, where it is elevating through the small intestine from 
five up to eight. On colon seems to hit neutral pH value as it varies between six and seven [47] [46]. 
Many factors affect the gastric transit time; accordingly, the values as mentioned are varying. For 
healthy GI tract the transit time estimates for a tablet to deliver through the stomach and the small 
intestine is from two to six hours, on the other hand the gastric transit time estimates to be 30 
minutes [47]. The colonic transit time is 6-70 hours.

5.3.2 
Silk fibroin  
Although, the wide range of the tested materials, the SF has in centuries shown essential properties 
as a biomaterial [59]. Natural materials, like SF has insufficient stiffness, where the rigidity of the 
matrices can be uncontrollable, thereby will affect the adhesion and the proliferation of the cells 
[60]. Researchers have recently studied the effectiveness and influences of SF concentration applied 
on electrospinning. Meehaisue et al. [60] have investigated solution concentration SF 10-40 wt% in 
85 wt% formic acid applied at electrostatic field strength (EFS; 10, 15 and 20 kV/10 cm), the results 
show that low SF solution concentration produced beaded fibers, on the other hand at high SF 
solution concentration smooth fibers was observed [60]. The concentration depends on the shear, 
where affects the beta-sheet structure in the SF. High shear led to premature alignment of the beta-
sheets in the SF [60]. The study concluded that high concentration results in increased shear that 
==PAGE:20==
20 | P a g e  
 
have an influence on the morphology of the SF e-spun [60]. 
In literature, mechanisms of improvement the SF’s stiffness and rheology was proposed by applying 
photopolymerization [61]. Research for photopolymerization of SF are less, with that said there is a 
need for more tested and improved modification method of SF employed of digital light processing 
(DLP) [61, 62]. Photoinitiator is capable to absorb the incident UV radiation and converting the 
amine-containing in SF into a cross-linked network [61, 62]. 
 
 
Figure 5.3-6 Fabricated SF chemically modified with GMA [62] 
Kim et al. [61] has modified SF by glycidal methacrylate (GMA) as illustrated in figure 5.3-6, where 
two GMA molecules react with one free amino group, which maximize the number of vinyl groups 
on the SF. By an increased GMA concentration will lead to an increase in methacrylation of SF, 
which has an impact on the physical and mechanical properties such as stiffness, pore size, and 
durability. Furthermore, the SILMA was investigated by addition of the photoinitiator to 
photopolymerize the SILMA thereby using DLP to improve the crosslinking. In the study was used 
lithium phenyl (2,4,6-trimethylbenzoyl) phosphinate (LAP) on the SILMA where the UV light 
intensity was at 3.5 mW cm-2 for 3 s to coagulate the solution. The examination shows that increasing 
of methacrylation affects the physical and mechanical properties of SF, where higher amount of 
methacrylation lead to stiffer, tougher, and smaller pore sizes. By increasing the SILMA 
concentration will elevate the elastic modulus, were leaded to greater tensile strength and breaking 
elongation. It was tested the cell adhesion and proliferation in SILMA by applying Live & Dead 
assay and Cell counting Kit-8 (CCK-8) assay. The results presented at different time points (3, 5, 7, 
14 days), where the proliferative activity and cell growth was significantly increased during the times 
[61, 62].

5.3.3 
BaSO4 as an x-ray contrast agent 
X-ray contrast agents are important for being able to image devices inside tissues and organs or to 
enhance the signal from the tissues or organs themselves. Barium and iodine are two contrast agents 
that are often used depending on, where in the body the X-ray imaging is applied. They both possess 
a high atomic number and have excessive mass density comparatively to soft tissue [63]. The atomic 
numbers for barium and iodine are 56 and 53, respectively. Because of their high atomic number, 
they become greater contrast agents. Iodine is specified to be used when vascular, renal, and biliary 
imaging are to be carried out [63]. The interesting features of barium sulfate is its insolubility in the 
GI tract. An additional benefit is that it will not interact with other ingredients when incorporated, 
allowing for oral administration in vivo and visibility of the travel of the capsule [2]. 
 
5.4 
Techniques for fabrication 
5.4.1 
Ultra-sonication 
Typically, an ultrasonic bath is used to clean any object by applying energetic ultrasounds trough 
liquid. Cavitation bubbles induce soundwaves by high-frequency pressure approximately 20 kHz 
agitating the liquid [64]. These soundwaves travelling through the water creating small intensive 
vibration allowing break up of organic matter. In this case, ultra-sonication is used to break the BS 
particles inside the PLGA solution in hope to obtain a homogeneous interaction [64]. 
 
5.4.2 
Electrospinning 
Electrospinning (e-spinning) is a technique that produces fibers between approx. 4- 2000 nm in 
diameter [65, 66]. This technique has been used to make nano-equipped scaffolds that can be used 
for tissue engineering to mimic the natural extra cellular matrix [66]. The interesting thing about 
electrospinning is the simplicity of the setup. The setup consists of a syringe pump, a high voltage 
source and a collector. In the production of fibers, a polymer or natural material solution is held in 
a needle tip by a surface tension [67, 66]. Electrostatic field with a high voltage source cause charge 
to be induced in the polymers or the natural material, whereupon a charge repulsion occurs in the 
solution [65, 68]. The surface tension counteracts the electrostatic force, where eventually charge 
repulsion overcomes the surface tension that initiates a jet [65, 66, 68]. When the jet is scattered 
==PAGE:22==
22 | P a g e  
 
from the needle tip, the solution is evaporated, and a suitable collector is used to collect the fibers 
[65, 66, 68].  
The stable electrospinning consists of four regions; base, jet, spreading, and collection regions [68]. 
Electrospinning can be affected by a number of different parameters. Doshi et al. have determined 
the parameters that can control the electrospinning process (see Fig. 5.4-1) [69]: 
→ The properties of the solution include viscosity, conductivity, surface tension and the 
dielectric constant 
→ Controlled variables, include flow rate, electric field strength, distance between the needle 
tip and the collector, the design of the needle tip, and the composition and geometry of the 
collector 
→ The ambient parameters include temperature and humidity 
 
 
Figure 5.4-1 A schematic overview of the set-up of electrospinning, Q=flowrate, d=distance and V=voltage [69] 
 
5.4.2.1 
Viscosity 
Viscosity depends on the concentration of the polymer solution, which has a large influence on fiber 
size and morphology when fibers are spanned [70, 71, 72, 73, 74]. Studies have shown that low 
polymer concentrations cause the polymer fibers to collect as droplets so that they look like they are 
electro-sprayed (this process is called beading), instead of e-spun [71-74]. Beading means that the 
polymer fibers are still wet when they are collected on the collector [71]. Polymeric concentration 
changes also affect the diameter of the polymeric fibers. At a high concentration, the diameter 
increases [71] [75]. 
 
==PAGE:23==
23 | P a g e  
 
5.4.2.2 
Conduction density / Conductivity 
To achieve uniform fibers and minimize beading, one can increase the wire density in polymer 
solution [70] [72]. These can be obtained by adding a saline solution to the polymer solution. 
Conductivity can be increased by adding alcohol, resulting in smoother polymeric fibers [76].  
 
5.4.2.3 
Surface voltage 
The surface tension affects the morphology and the size of the e-spun nanofibers. Addition of alcohol 
to some polymeric solutions, such as poly (ethylene oxide) (PEO) and poly (vinyl alcohol) (PVA), 
results in lower surface tension [76]. Thus, the addition of alcohol to PEO results in less beading. 
 
5.4.2.4 
The molecular weight 
Gupta et al. has investigated the relationship between the molecular weight and the morphology and 
size of e-spun nanofibers by synthesize PMMA in vary molecular weight from 12.47 to 365.7 kDa 
[77] . They found as the molecular weight increased a clearly decreasing of beads and droplets were 
reduced. Otherwise, a narrow molecular weight gave uniform fibers at a lower concentration [69]. 
 
5.4.2.5 
Flow rate 
Low flow rate led to smaller diameter on the fibers, whereas higher flow rate results on beading since 
the fibers did not have chance to dry before reaching the collector [69]. 
 
5.4.2.6 
Electrical field strength / voltage 
Typically, at a low voltage V a drop will be suspended at the needle tip, which form of Taylor cone 
increases [71]. This will eliminate beading in the polymer fibers. At a high voltage, the Taylor cone 
on the needle tip will be reduced in size, which will increase the incidence of beading [71]. 
 
5.4.2.7 
The distance between the needle tip and the collector 
A minimum distance d between the needle tip and the collector must be set [78], accordingly the 
polymer fibers have sufficient time to dry before they reach the collector. For some polymer 
solutions, such as PVA, gelatin, chitosan, and poly (vinylidene fluoride), a short or long distance, 
==PAGE:24==
24 | P a g e  
 
between the needle tip and the collector, has no effect on the size and morphology of the polymer 
fibers [71]. For silk-like polymer, a close distance will allow flat morphology of the fibers vice versa 
producing rounder fibers. 
 
5.4.2.8 
Porosity 
The porosity has valuable properties as they are enabled to modify and control the dousing process, 
adsorption/absorption, and the different release profiles of drugs. Concretely formulation of e-spun 
fibers loaded with drug to obtain high porosity to understand the mechanical behavior is still storage. 
Hence, porous nanofibers have higher drug release from core-shell nanofibers relative to non-porous 
nanofibers [79].  
 
5.4.3 
Scanning Electron Microscope 
The scanning electron microscope (SEM) is considered one of the most versatile tools that 
characterize drug-carrying devices at micro scale. High-resolution images of specimens (around1 nm) 
can be obtained by scanning the samples surface with a focused beam of electrons produced by 
electron gun [80]. Different types of electron guns are accessible but the one used for this project is 
a tungsten filament, which is heated to over 2500°C, resulting in thermal emission of electrons. The 
sample is examined in a chamber, under vacuum environment. 
A typical layout of SEM is shown in 5.4-2(A), where the electron gun produces and accelerates the 
electrons by applying a voltage (1-30 KeV). 
The accelerated electrons pass through a sequence of electromagnetic lenses. The object of these 
lenses is to control the electron beam diameter and focus the electrons over the specimens. To ensure 
high-quality resolution, a high vacuum inside the chamber (0.1-10E-4 Pa) is required. Different types 
of detectors are available to detect the signals produced by the electrons interaction as illustrated at 
figure 5.4-2 (B) [81]. The secondary electrons detector used to detect the signals generated by in-
elastically interaction between the electrons and the atoms of the material. However, backscattered 
electrons are used to detect signals produced by elastic interactions in a deeper penetration depth, 
resulting in contrast information about the surface. In the images, areas with higher atomic number 
appear brighter [80]. Subsequently, the signals will be amplified to record the topography of images 
of the surface.

5.4.5 
Planar X-ray imaging 
As already briefly described, X-ray imaging is a common imaging technique used in various medical 
applications, where it is used to study most areas of the body [83]. X-rays are electromagnetic 
radiation with wavelengths between 5 pm and 10 nm. 
A conventional x-ray uses a fixed tube that sends x-rays in only one direction. There are special digital 
x-ray detectors located directly opposite the x-ray source. These detectors pick up the x-rays 
penetrating the object and transmit the counts to the computer. Image slices can be either displayed 
individually in a two-dimensional form or stacked together to generate a three-dimensional image 
(also known as CT) that can reveal abnormal structures. 
Depending on the density of the tissues between the emitter and the detector at a point, black or 
white images are recorded. Knowing that these images are generated in grey nuances, which give 
contrast information. For low density, the x-ray beams penetrate the object, displaying the black 
nuance on the image. However, the higher density will appear whiter seen from a medical aspect. 
When seen from a physical aspect, the contrast will appear black. 
During the process, the x-ray photons could be either absorbed or scattered out of the specimen.  
Photons that interact with outer electrons result in scattering out of the sample. However, when 
photons interact with inner-shell electrons of an atom in the sample, it will be absorbed. 
Subsequently, X-rays passed through the sample will be detected the x-ray detector. It is worth to 
note that the higher atomic number elements offer more electrons, resulting in more interactions 
==PAGE:27==
27 | P a g e  
 
with the x-rays such as bones, while lower atomic number elements have less electrons, indicating 
low interactions with the x-rays, such as soft tissue [84]. The generated photons can be influenced by 
many parameters; power factor, which affect the amount of x-ray photons produced, and by voltage, 
which directly influences the electrical potential between the anode and the cathode in the x-ray 
generator. It has been reported that an increase in the energy generates a shorter wavelength of the 
x-rays, resulting in an increase in the penetration power through the sample [83].

SILMA was gained by degumming silk cocoons and dissolved using LiBr, and subsequent addition 
of GMA was necessary to obtain more stiffens and stability in the structure. Afterwards, the SILMA 
was investigated with different PEO concentrations followed by microscopy imaging to create an 
overview of the overall morphology of SILMA. Correspondingly, PLGA was studied with two 
different concentrations. The challenges with mixing PLGA with BS was attempted to be solved by 
deriving some mixture considerations.        
All the microparticles in this study were loaded with BS and fabricated by e-spinning. It is important 
to emphasize that the results regarding the e-spinning set-up were not included here. As the studies 
were visually assessed, depending on how the Taylor cone looks like and whether the jet is being 
sprayed instead of spinning. Finally, yet important, if the distance of the collector affected the 
interception of the e-spun jet. The visibility quality of the radiopaque nano-sheets, with the different 
BS percentages were checked by SEM. Afterwards, a pre-study of the fabricated radiopaque 
nanosheets was conducted to create an overview if the contrast agent is visible in rat intestine pieces 
by planar X-ray imaging. Finally, we practiced a pilot study to expand our understanding of µ-particle 
GI behavior, adhesion, and visibility by imaging in X-ray. 
One of the aims for this thesis was quantitative evaluation of the microparticles throughout the rat 
GI tract in in-vivo study to estimate the transit time. According to the obtained results of the in-vivo 
study, we had to redefine the aim to quality check the fabricated radiopaque microparticles. The 
theoretical size of each µ-particle was found to be 50-60 µm. However, this size is an estimate since 
the size can slightly vary between the microparticles from each batch, due to the production process. 
The representation of the X-ray images on figure 7.7-5 and figure 7.7-6 for both 1h and 3h show the 
accumulations of the microparticles, which lead to the questioning of the behavior seen in the 
images. It was now important to understand whether the gelatin capsules degraded inside the GI 
tract, and if the exertion of force on the microparticles resulted in supplementary compaction. It is 
consequently important to emphasize that SILMA and PLGA cannot be compared to the calculated 
BS amounts, since it is different on the scale ratio. Correspondingly, microparticles were not 
achieved for PLGA due the challenge during the µ-cutting application.

8.1 
E-spun radiopaque Nano-sheets 
Fabrication of the radiopaque nano-sheet is obtained by electrospinning. At the beginning of the 
process, the set-up was optimized by applying different parameters, such as increasing and decreasing 
the voltage to observe the drop of the nozzle tip, which were held on to 13.5 kV. In addition, the 
flowrate was initially set at 2 ml/h and decreased down to 0.5 ml/h. We did not gain dry fibers, 
which resulted the fibers to be adhesive on the aluminum foil. The resultant flowrate was set to be 
0.5 ml/l that allowed the fibers to have enough time to arrive at the collector and then dry. 
 
8.1.1 
 Characterization of SILMA 
According to a previous study [62], the concentration of SILMA has to be higher to obtain recovering 
e-spun sheets. In this project, the concentration was workable and allowed remarkable and uniformly 
distribution of the fibers. Previously, the concentration for the SILMA was a constant parameter 
and found to be 5.04 wt %. The crucial concentration of PEO was studied in order to e-spinning 
since an undesirable concentration chain entanglements of polymer are insufficient to stabilize the 
jet and a bead-like structure in the fiber tends to form. Figure 8.1-1 shows the microscopy images for 
the different PEO concentrations blended with SILMA at various ratios. As shown at the prototypes 
acquired the concentration parameter affected the formation of the fibers. Once the concentration 
of PEO was changed from 6.4 wt % to 8 wt % the corresponding e-spun fibers was weakened. An 
explanation could be that higher PEO concentration resulted in a gelified solution. Hence, a series 
of spinning dopes with different PEO concentrations was e-spun, resulting in different fiber 
morphologies, as shown in figure 8.1-1. It detected that the jet can easily break up into droplets and 
the fiber was rarely formed with low concentration solution of SILMA/PEO. Oppositely, the e-
spinning was difficult to perform with higher concentrated solution because the e-spun was easily 
jammed and the flow was unstable. 
SILMA/PEO solutions presented changes in fiber shape, resulting in relatively uniform fiber 
formation of the e-spun fibers, espeically for ratio of 1:3 for 6.4 wt% PEO  as presented on SEM 
images at figure 8.1-2 (1).

9 
Conclusion 
In this study, tracking of microparticles, made of both SILMA and PLGA, by X-ray imaging in the 
rat GI tract was investigated. SILMA was fabricated and various PEO concentrations were examined. 
Low e-spinning solution concentration results in beaded formation of fibers and at higher 
concentration the e-spinning became difficult to perform and lead to unsteady jet. The optimal PEO 
concentration and mixture ratio were concluded to be wt% 6.4 and 1:3, respectively. SILMA had an 
excellent presentation during the examinations. Rationally, the PLGA should provide better results 
accorded to the mentioned evidences; the reason is that by adding the BS into PLGA, the solution 
resulted in an inhomogeneous performance. The performance was caused by numerous challenges, 
therefore no progress obtained by PLGA.  
The in vivo evaluation of SILMA microparticles using X-ray imaging showed a repeatedly 
accumulation in the stomach for both one and three hours. Due to the sticky properties of SILMA, 
microparticles caused a compact mass during the encapsulation, which prevented the degradation 
and release. Considerably, limited number of rats were used to demonstrate feasibility of using X-
ray imaging for GI tracking. The project is reserved for assessment, as the size of the microparticles 
is an important design parameter, which must be reconsidered. Fluorescence imaging would be a 
better replacement if the aim will be qualitative evaluation. An optimization is essential, as this 
information would be useful for design and development of smart microscale devices for oral drug 
delivery applications.

10 Future perspective 
Performing further optimization is required, such as considerations of the engineering design and 
tracking formulation. Certainly, providing the cross-linking of SILMA by photopolymerization could 
be an option if the microparticles should get larger size, so the degradation is supplementary 
manageable. Increasing the concentration of the SILMA by PEG dialysis can be investigated, 
however what we experienced by high concentration of PLGA and mixture of BS lead to difficulties 
in the solvent. Mechanical characterization of these could be required to expand the knowledge of 
mechanical performances of both SILMA and PLGA and compare their similarities and differences. 
Rheology and morphology of both materials will be useful to understand the behaviors and the 
properties of them, this information will help to set up operational process implementation. As 
mentioned earlier, several challenges of accumulation were observed due to the extended force. To 
solve this a setup of degradation test in GI pH environment ex vivo of the capsules would be 
necessary. Fluorescence imaging could be a good option to differentiate between digestive products 
and microparticles. In this case, applying an advantageous statistical analysis for considering the high 
variability in GI behavior is required.
