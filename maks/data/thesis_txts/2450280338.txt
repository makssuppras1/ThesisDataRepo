Abstract
Renewable energy sources play an increasingly important part in the energy mixes of
many countries, with wind power contributing a significant share. Accurate forecasting
of wind power is essential to the economic viability of large scale integration of wind.
The primary source of error in modern wind power forecasting models is from errors
in weather forecasts. The focus of this study is to examine whether higher resolution
weather modeling can improve weather forecasts for wind power forecasting.
The Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model was used to downscale global
weather forecasts in the Western Cape region of South Africa. Forecasts were made using
three different grid spacings and two different physical parameterizations were tested.
The forecasts were validated by comparing with wind measurements from meteorological
research masts distributed throughout the region. The main focus was set on analyzing
correlation between forecast and observed wind speeds, it being the appropriate metric
of performance for the intended use case.
The results indicate that high resolution downscaling can in fact improve the correla-
tion of global weather forecasts. The improvement is not the same for all measurement
sites and also varies by e.g. time of year. This highlights the importance of careful con-
sideration of local and seasonal effects before commissioning high resolution forecasts
with the associated expense.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The motivation for embarking on this study of downscaling weather forecasts is pre-
sented in this chapter, along with a brief overview of wind power forecasting models.
A review of the relevant literature is given with emphasis on high resolution weather
forecasting. A cursory introduction to sub-grid scale processes is given and the physics
parameterizations employed in the study are introduced. Lastly the research question
is defined and the scope of the work herein is presented.
==PAGE:18==
2
1 Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Wind power has become an increasingly important part of the world’s electricity gen-
eration mix in recent years. Denmark, for decades a world leader in the utilization of
wind power, met 43.4% of it’s electricity consumption from wind power in 2017, and
wind power accounted for an estimated 5.6% of the global electricity generation of 2017
(REN21, 2018). Being an intermittent source of power, the ability to accurately predict
fluctuations in wind power generation plays an important part in making wind power
plants economical (Giebel et al., 2016; Gregor Giebel, 2017).
A key tool in successful scheduling of renewable power sources is an accurate forecast
of wind power. On the time scales of seconds to minutes, forecasts are usually statistically
based (Foley et al., 2012). For the short to medium term of hours to days the inclusion of
meteorological information from numerical weather prediction (NWP) models is typically
necessary, and it has been shown that poor quality NWP data is the primary contributor
to forecast error at those forecast horizons (Foley et al., 2012).
Numerous sources of NWP data are available to wind power forecasters today, rang-
ing from global NWP models, typically at spatial grid spacing around 10–50 km, to
limited area models (LAMs) at resolutions up to 1 km or even finer. Meteorological
agencies around the world currently run LAMs for countless regions but the availabil-
ity of this data in a consistent and timely manner is often limited and/or subject to
payments. With the decreasing cost of computational resources the possibility of wind
power forecast providers commissioning “custom-made” LAM forecasts is becoming fea-
sible. (Gregor Giebel, 2017) identify this as an important research issue, going so far as
to call it a “low hanging fruit” for improving forecast skill.
Fine resolution LAMs have been shown to outperform general circulation models
(GCMs) when measuring wind speed bias for example, but can suffer from phase errors
(Mass et al., 2002). Since modern wind power forecasting models typically apply statis-
tical methods to correct the NWP input, bias is not a good measure of the quality of
NWP forecasts as input to these models nor are bias-sensitive metrics such as the root
mean square error (RMSE). Instead metrics such as correlation have been shown to be
more relevant (Rosgaard, 2015; García-Díez et al., 2015).
The focus of this work is to examine whether dynamical downscaling of atmospheric
fields from GCMs with a mesoscale model can provide added value for wind power
forecasting.

1.2 Wind power forecasting models
3
1.2
Wind power forecasting models
Foley et al. (2012) give a review of methods of wind power forecasting.
They note
that wind power forecasting models can be broadly categorized into two ategories, i.e.
statistical or physical. Purely statistical models rely on analysis of historical and on-line
time series of wind and power output (see fig. 1.1). An early example of a statistical
model is described in Nielsen, Madsen, and Tøfting (1998). Physical models rely on a
numerical weather prediction (NWP) model as input, often refining their predictions by
employing knowledge of local conditions such as accounting for orographic speedup and
roughness effects before applying a power curve model to arrive at a forecast of power
output (see fig. 1.2). An early example of a physical model is described in Landberg
(1999). What type of model performs best depends somewhat on the prediction horizons,
with statistical methods performing best for very short horizons (e.g. 0-3 hours)(Lange,
2003). For horizons of the scale of hours or days, Foley et al. (2012) state that input from
NWP models becomes essential. A substantial variety of different models for wind power
forecasting have been developed in recent years, a partial list of which is compiled by
Foley et al. (2012). The majority of models in effect fall in the third category of hybrid
models, which might even be considered a spectrum, with some models relying heavily
on statistical methods and others heavily on physical methods, or even transitioning
between the two as the forecast horizon increases. As already noted by Lange (2003)
well over a decade ago, the borders between statistical and physical models blurs as the
modeling techniques advance.

data assimilation in WRF and found promising results up to a horizon of five hours. Ros-
gaard (2015) examined the explanatory value of six different weather model variables
for wind power forecasting, finding the results to depend on the local terrain. Lange
(2003) related forecast errors to meteorological patterns and found strong relations, the
implication being that it is possible to predict forecast uncertainty. Andrade and Bessa
(2017) apply methods of machine learning to extract information from a spatial field
rather than a single point in an effort to increase forecast accuracy, finding improve-
ments of 15% in terms of MAE for a forecast of wind power. D. M. Siuta and R. B. Stull
(2018) applied a multi-model ensemble approach to increase forecast skill. Conducting
a one year case study of daily 7-day long multi-model ensemble and using hourly obser-
vations of wind speed from four wind farms in complex terrain for verification, they find
the ensemble to outperform any single ensemble member, both in terms of MAE and
correlation.
The goal of this study is to gain insight into the question of whether the downscaling
of a global weather forecast using a LAM can deliver improved input for wind power
forecasts. This is done by performing simulations using the Weather Research and Fore-
casting model (WRF) over a region of South Africa and verifying by using meteorological
measurements near the hub-height of modern on-shore wind turbines from locations on
varying degrees of complex terrain, from flat to mountainous.

1.3 Numerical weather predictions
5
1.3
Numerical weather predictions
1.3.1
Effect of resolution
Weather at a given point in space is a superposition of dynamics across several scales
of motion. The largest of these are planetary scale motions such as Rossby waves or El
Niño, having spatio-temporal scales on the order of several thousands of km in space
and several months in time. Smaller than those are for instance low pressure systems,
spanning hundreds of km and days or weeks. Smaller still are thunderstorms and land-
sea breeze phenomena, spanning tens of km and several hours up to a day, and smaller
than that are turbulent motions down to the microscale (Heinemann, 2018). Mesoscale
models such as the Weather Research & Forecasting (WRF) model are commonly used
tools to resolve the flow down to the range of 1–10 km and even lower. The effective
resolution, defined by William C. Skamarock (2004) as the resolution at which the
kinetic energy spectrum gets steeper than the -5/3 slope predicted by Kolmogorov, has
been shown to be approximately 7 times the grid spacing ∆x. This indicates that flow
features smaller than that should not be interpreted as physical (Knievel et al., 2006;

The accuracy of a weather forecast depends, among other things, on the ability of the
NWP model to resolve the atmospheric processes on a range of spatio-temporal scales
(Rosgaard, 2015). If the terrain is complex, e.g. if there are mountains or valleys in the
vicinity, or complicated coastlines, the grid resolution must be higher than otherwise in
order to be able to adequately resolve the atmospheric processes (Rosgaard, 2015). As
mentioned previously, LAMs are commonly used to increase the spatio-temporal reso-
lution of GCMs. Although LAMs are expected to provide added value when compared
to GCMs in terms of bias in wind speed (Jiménez et al., 2010; Horvath et al., 2012;
Rosgaard, 2015), the question remains open when focusing on error metrics that are
sensitive to phase errors.

1 Introduction
In a landmark study on the subject of high resolution weather forecasting, Mass et
al. (2002) conducted a study of two years of weather forecasts over western Washington
state for forecast horizons up to 48 h. They saw noticeably improved skill as horizontal
resolution decreased from 36 to 12 km but only minor improvements going from 12 to 4
km. They note that the largest improvements are found in regions downwind of a major
topographic barrier (the Olympic Mountains). Filtering to consider only periods with
good synoptic-scale forecasts shows only modest improvements for the high resolution
forecasts. Noting that the cause of poor results for high resolution forecasts are partly
attributed to small timing errors, they suggest that alternative verification metrics are
needed. Following the results of Mass et al. (2002), Rife and Davis (2005) conducted
a study comparing the performances of one full year of wind forecasts for a region of
complex terrain in New Mexico from grids of 30 and 3.3 km resolution respectively.
They find incremental gains in forecast accuracy when scoring using traditional metrics
such as RMSE, instead suggesting that the gains in high resolution forecasts should be
evaluated using metrics with greater tolerance for timing errors such as object-based
verification techniques. Also, similar to Mass et al. (2002), they find that the cross-
mountain component of wind benefits most from higher resolution grids. In an earlier
study of forecasts of wind speed, Rife, Davis, et al. (2004) found a relationship between
anomaly correlation and the ratio of observed sub-diurnal and diurnal spectral power
relative to total spectral power.
Horvath et al. (2012) performed downscaling using horizontal grid spacing from 27
km in the outermost domain, telescoping to 333 m, in a mountainous region to the east
of the Sierra Nevada mountain range. They find that while moment-based accuracy
was improved profoundly for vertical wind shear and temporal variability of wind speed,
phase errors were the primary source of the RMSE and that their contribution increased
with higher resolution. They surmise that the phase errors can largely be attributed
to i) errors introduced by the lateral boundary conditions (given by the GCM), and
ii) lower boundary conditions and physics options. They further conclude that at least
near-kilometer grid spacing is required in regions of complex terrain, but note that some
physics parameterizations might not be appropriate as grid spacing approaches the scale
of turbulent containing eddies.
Hrastinski et al. (2015) conduct a similar study using the ALADIN model, downscal-
ing to a resolution of 2 km over a mountainous region of Croatia. They largely agree
with the findings of Horvath et al. (2012) that the RMSE is largely caused by the phase
errors and that the share of phase errors increases with increased resolution. Smith,
Koračin, and Horvath (2014), in a follow up study to Horvath et al. (2012), attribute
large phase errors in day-ahead forecasts of wind speed on a 1 km grid in a valley west
of Sierra Nevada, to flow features directly related to the terrain such as internal gravity
waves and mountain rotors, and suggest that detailed analysis of local meteorological
conditions is necessary in order to improve forecasts in regions of complex terrain. They
find these phase errors to be the dominant component of error.

was calculated at a horizontal grid spacing of approximately 110 km. In 1998 it was

levels in 2016 (ECMWF, 2019[b]).

1.3.1.1
Summary
Achieving the highest possible correlation with observed wind speed is paramount for
weather forecasts in the context of wind power forecasting. Downscaling coarse resolution
forecasts to higher resolutions is a common way to improve forecast accuracy, but sources
differ on whether high resolution forecasts offer an improvement in terms of correlation
due to them suffering from timing errors. Several studies find that timing related errors
are so severe that coarser resolutions outperform the finer resolution forecasts (Mass
et al., 2002; García-Díez et al., 2015; D. Siuta, West, and R. Stull, 2017; Horvath et
al., 2012; Rife and Davis, 2005), some even suggesting that high resolution GCMs offer
similar performance.
The improvements achievable through downscaling can depend on a variety of factors
including local terrain, weather types, forecast horizon, season and time of day.

Physical parameterizations
Even though mesoscale models can be run at resolutions down to 1 km or in some
cases even finer, there are still important processes occurring at smaller scales of motion
which can not be resolved. These are so-called sub-grid scale processes, arguably the
most important of which (at least when focusing on wind forecasting) are radiation,
clouds/precipitation, and turbulent mixing (Rosgaard, 2015). In the context of wind
energy research turbulent mixing is arguably the one most directly impactful.

instance the incoming solar radiation can greatly affect the turbulent mixing through
the generation of buoyancy effects. This in turn affects cloud formation, which again
has a direct impact on the incoming radation (Rosgaard, 2015).

1.3.3
PBL schemes
Turbulent mixing (of moisture, heat and momentum) is modeled in planetary boundary
layer (PBL) schemes, made necessary due to the scales of motion of PBL turbulence
being smaller than typical resolutions of mesoscale models. Numerous such schemes
have been developed in past decades and are very much an active topic of research. For
this study it is only possible to barely scratch the surface of PBL schemes, a research
sub-field in it’s own right.
As any modern textbook on turbulence theory will explain (see e.g.
R. B. Stull
(1988)), the equations of motion pertaining to turbulent flow will always contain more
==PAGE:27==
1.3 Numerical weather predictions
11
unknown than known terms. This is sometimes referred to as the closure problem of
turbulence.
One common approach to this problem is applying so-called turbulence
closure models, where the unknown term of moment n + 1 is related (empirically) to
lower moment known terms in what is called an n-th order turbulence closure. This is
an important distinction of PBL schemes, with higher order closures generally perceived
as more physically accurate representations (though their performance when ultimately
applied in models can vary).
The other most important distinction between PBL schemes is that of local or non-
local schemes. That is to say, schemes differ by the (vertical) depth over which the
known variables are allowed to affect a given point. A local scheme only allows variables
to affect the levels immediately adjacent to a given point, whereas non-local schemes
can use multiple vertical levels to determine unkown variables at a given point.
The performance of different PBL schemes can rely heavily on a variety of factors.
Yang et al. (2013), focusing on so-called ramp events, concluded that while one scheme
might outperform others under a specific atmospheric condition, no scheme clearly out-
performs the rest under a wide range of atmospheric conditions. D. Siuta, West, and
R. Stull (2017) found when comparing the performance of eight different PBL schemes
that, aggregated over all locations, the Asymmetric Convective Model version 2 (ACM2)
scheme performed best but note that the comparative performance of different PBL
schemes varies by site, grid resolution and season, emphasizing the importance of care-
ful selection of PBL schemes. Utilizing ANOVA analysis they found that the choice of
PBL schemes had more influence on forecast accuracy than grid resolution at two out
of four measuring sites. These influences are shown to be seasonal at three out of four
sites studied, and a diurnal trend is also seen, with the choice of PBL scheme having
most influence on forecast accuracy in midday (12:00 to 18:00). A succinct review of
PBL schemes is available in Cohen et al. (2015).
Two PBL schemes in particular are worth discussing in more detail due to their
relevance to this study; the Yonsei University (YSU) scheme and the Shin-Hong (SH)
scheme.
1.3.3.1
Yonsei University and Shin-Hong schemes
The Yonsei University (YSU) scheme, in the briefest of terms, is a first-order closure,
non-local eddy diffusivity (K theory) approach with explicit treatment of the PBL top
(Troen and Mahrt, 1986; Hong, Noh, and Dudhia, 2006).
Historically, PBL schemes in mesoscale models have been developed for horizontal
resolutions which are unable to resolve any turbulence in the PBL. However with higher
model resolutions becoming more feasible and commonplace the question of whether
these schemes are appropriate for sub-kilometer grid spacings where some turbulent
eddies can be expected to be partly resolved, in what Wyngaard terms the terra incognita

(Wyngaard, 2004; Shin and Dudhia, 2016). Shin and Dudhia (2016) conducted a study
to investigate the performance of common PBL schemes in the sub-kilometer range. Four
schemes were tested, three of which were non-local: YSU, ACM2 and the eddy diffusivity

referred to Shin and Dudhia (2016) or Xu, Y. Wang, and M. Wang (2018) for a succinct
overview.
In light of the known limitations of YSU at terra incognita resolutions, and the
relative recentness and therefore limited experience of SH, it is decided to compare the
performance of these two schemes in this study.

1.4 Scope

1.4
Scope
The central question of this study can be synthesized as:
Can mesoscale models improve the quality of global forecasts for wind power
forecasting models?
Providing a definitive answer to this question is of course not the goal, but rather to
shed light on to what extent mesoscale models offer an improvement over GCMs and
to demonstrate cases where added value is evident or not evident. Since a wealth of
different factors can have drastic effects on the answer to the question, only a handful
of modeling options will be investigated such as the effect of grid resolution and two
different PBL schemes. The effect of external factors will also be investigated to some
extent, i.e. factors such as terrain complexity, seasonality and diurnal variation.
To study the above a forecasting experiment is conducted using WRF. A region of
South Africa is chosen as a test case primarily due to the availability of high quality
measurement data from tall met masts which have been operating in the region for sev-
eral years as part of the Wind Atlas for South Africa (WASA) project. An added benefit
is the extensive mesoscale modeling experiments that have been conducted by DTU as
part of WASA (Hahmann et al., 2017). Furthermore, as previous studies of similar na-
ture have often been done in mid-latitudes in the northern hemisphere, performing the
study in South Africa could offer interesting results.

2.2 Model setup

2.2
Model setup
The model used for the experiment is WRF-ARW version 3.9.1 (William C Skamarock
et al., 2008). The choice of WRF is rather straight forward since it has emerged in
recent years as nearly ubiquitous in studies of this kind and is possibly the most user-
friendly and accessible model currently available, being open-source with a thriving user
community. In addition the model was somewhat familiar to the author at the start of
the study, and entirely familiar to the thesis supervisors.
In this section an overview is given of the configuration(s) of the model used for the
experiment.
2.2.1
Design of domains
As is common practice in downscaling, nesting is applied to reach the desired resolution.
Three nests are used with the coarsest domain at a grid spacing of 25 km (domain 1, d01),
the second domain (d02) at 5 km and the innermost domain (d03) at 1 km. The sizes
are 120 x 120 cells for d01 corresponding to 3.000 × 3.000 km, 220 × 200 cells for d02 or
1.100 × 1.000 km, and 550 × 500 cells or km for d01. Output is saved for all domains
every 10 minutes and one-way nesting is applied which enables a comparison of the
performance of different model resolutions. Figure 2.8 shows the domains graphically.
The rationale behind the design of the domains starts with the innermost domain.
It was desired to capture at least five masts within the highest resolution domain. The
Cape Town area holds the highest density of WASA masts as well as a range of different
terrain features, so it was the natural choice of location, and the size was simply the
minimum size rectangle to fit the number of masts in addition to a buffer zone of at least
50 km to allow flow features to fully develop before reaching the masts. The second and
third domains are made to encompass the inner nests in addition to an adequately large
buffer on each side. In the vertical there are 60 levels up to a pressure level of 5 millibar.
2.2.2
Initial and boundary conditions
The initial state of the atmosphere, or the initial conditions, are provided from a GCM.
Several options are available for GCMs. However most of these are not distributed freely,
and thus the most commonly used GCM for studies such as this is GFS which is freely
available with no delay.
In order to most realistically simulate the performance of a real time operational
forecasting system, the ds084.1 dataset is used1. This dataset includes the operational
GFS analysis along with forecasts at a 0.25 by 0.25 degree global grid. Forecasts are
provided at a 3 hourly interval up to 240 hours and model runs occur every 6 hours.

2.3
Methods of validation
Gauging the performance of a forecast, or a model in general, is a common task of model
developers. Entire textbooks have been written on the subject (Jolliffe and Stephenson,
2012; Wilks, 2006) since there is no single method of model verification that applies to
all cases. Rather the method of verification depends on the type of model output, which
can for example be a time series of wind speed, or spatial fields of precipitation. It also
depends, no less importantly, on the intended use of the model output.
2.3.1
Pearson correlation coefficient
For the current study the primary focus is on verification of time series of wind speed at
individual point locations, with the intended use being as input to wind power forecast-
ing models as discussed in previous sections. A discussion of what performance metrics
are best suited for this type of verification can be found in Rosgaard (2015). The Pear-
son correlation coefficient (PCC) is identified as a suitable metric due to its invariance
to forecast bias and scaling errors, in contrast to metrics that are perhaps more com-
mon in meteorological forecast verification such as root mean square error (RMSE) or
mean absolute error (MAE). The implication of this is that statistical post-processing
techniques (such as those commonly applied to correct for systematic model errors in
the context of wind power forecasting) are unable to improve the PCC (Rosgaard, 2015;
Lange, 2003). It is this quality that makes the PCC so useful, even to the point of being
called the “king of all scores” (Lange, 2003). The PCC is defined thus:

Power curve transformation
Since the power production of wind turbines relates to the wind speed with a highly

the forecasted wind speed is not directly translatable to the performance of a forecasted

3.2
Forecast horizon
As discussed in a previous section, forecast performance is generally expected to decrease
with increasing forecast horizon. Whether this occurs in the same manner for a coarse
resolution global forecast (e.g. GFS) as it does for a high resolution downscaled forecast,
is less clear. One might intuitively expect that high resolution simulations degrade faster
with increasing forecast horizon due to phase errors. This is supported by the findings of
Rosgaard (2015) where it was observed that correlation degraded faster with increasing
forecast horizon for LAM forecasts than it did for GFS forecasts, at three different sites
in Scandinavia. Whether downscaling provides added value over global forecast models
in terms of correlation could then depend on the forecast horizon.
To inspect the forecast performance’s dependence on forecast horizon, five time series
are constructed; the first with a horizon of 0–24 hours, i.e. the first 24 hours of each
48 hour forecast is extracted to form a complete time series. The last 24 hours are also
extracted, forming a time series with wind speeds of a 24–48h horizon. In a similar
fashion three additional series are constructed with forecast horizons of 6–30h, 12–36h
and 18–42h.
Note that these three all share some data with the 0–24h and 24–48h
forecasts so they should not be interpreted as being independent of them or of each
other. Only the 0–24h and 24–48h forecasts have no data points in common. The first
and last 24 h are discarded so that all series cover precisely the same periods.
It should be noted that in an operational setting the first forecast that would realisti-
cally be employed for scheduling or trading would be the 6–30h forecast, since there is an
unavoidable time-delay in distribution of the GCM forecast (GFS in this case) plus the
required time to compute the downscaled forecast and run the wind power forecasting
model itself.

3.5
Decomposition of RMSE
As described in section 2.3.2 the RMSE can be decomposed into three components using
equation 2.3. The result of this analysis is shown in figure 3.16.
Figure 3.16: Decomposition of the RMSE at each site, for GFS and all three WRF
domains. January and July combined, forecast horizon 6-30 h.
In general the RMSE decreases with higher resolution. The dominating component
of RMSE is the dispersion error, followed by the bias. The bias decreases with increased
resolution, and for the 1 km domain it is ≤0.6 ms−1 at all sites, whereas the dispersion
error is > 2 m/s for all sites. The relative contribution of the standard deviation error
is small for all sites and the only case where it exceeds 1 m/s is for the GFS forecast at
WM05. Thus the relative contribution of the dispersion error increases with resolution,
being nearly equal to the RMSE at the highest resolution domain. This is consistent
with previous studies (Horvath et al., 2012; Smith, Koračin, and Horvath, 2014).
Although the dispersion error increases going from GFS to d01 — the 25 km domain
— in all but one site, the RMSE still decreases. In other words, if forecast quality is
measured by RMSE, even the 25 km domain provides added value. Though not shown
here the equivalent plots are done for January and July separately as well as for a horizon
of 24-48h. The same trends are observed for those cases as in figure 3.16 with no clear
difference in terms of season/period or forecast horizon.

Summary
Applying a temporal smoothing can improve the PCC of a downscaled forecast substan-

magnitude, which is expected since it is provided in a temporal resolution of 3 hours.
The magnitude of improvement differs by site, grid resolution and period/season. In
general, the improvement attained by smoothing is larger for fine resolution domains,
and it is larger during January than it is during July. Whether this is a result of seasonal
phenomena or of the specific synoptic patterns dominant during the respective periods
is a point for further study.
The optimal smoothing half-width r also varies by site, grid resolution and peri-
od/season. In general the results suggest that the optimal r is longer for January than
for July, and shorter for fine resolution grids than for coarser grids. This, along with
the variation in optimal r across sites, suggests that it in an operational setting it is
advisable to optimize r for each individual site and to update it regularly, even once
a month. Tuning r to different synoptic patterns might also be worth investigating in
future studies.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion and discussion
We now turn back to the central question of the study, from section 1.4:
Can mesoscale models improve the quality of global forecasts for wind power
forecasting models?
The overarching conclusion of the study is that yes, mesoscale models can offer an
improvement on global forecasts for wind power forecasting uses, but it depends on a
variety of factors. The overall improvement in PCC going from GFS to the 1 km domain,
post-smoothing, is 18% for a forecast horizon of 6-30 h.
Employing high resolution
dynamical downscaling could be worth the computational effort in some sites. The added
value in downscaling can be greater for certain seasons or possibly certain meteorological
situations.
In section 2.3.1 the PCC was introduced and it was argued why it is an appropriate
measure of NWP quality for wind power forecasting uses. Using this as the primary
metric for comparing forecasts the results of the forecasting experiment are analyzed in
chapter 3.
On the influence of grid resolution (sec.
3.3) it is found that higher resolutions
generally result in a better forecast than lower resolutions, as well as performing better
than the driving global model (GFS). The improvement in the downscaled forecasts over
the global model forecast is less in winter than in summer for the specific case studied
here. In sec. 3.2 it was seen that as expected the performance of the forecasts degrades
with increasing forecast horizon. The highest resolution forecast degrades slightly faster
than the global model forecast whereas the coarser resolution forecasts degrade at a
similar pace or slower than GFS. In an attempt to relate forecast performance to local
terrain, the performance was analyzed as a function of wind direction in sec. 3.4. No
obvious relation to local features of the terrain could be identified though further research
is warranted. Rather it is suggested that forecast performance depends more on seasonal
effects or particular synoptic patterns, but further research is warranted on this subject.
Section 3.5 analyzed the decomposition of forecast error, showing that dispersion (or
phase) error is the dominating component, especially for the highest resolution forecasts.
In contrast to previous studies (e.g. Horvath et al. (2012)) this error decreases with
increasing grid resolution.
The propagation of error to forecasts of wind power are
inspected in sec. 3.6. The improvements in using the high resolution forecasts are found
==PAGE:78==
62
4 Conclusion and discussion
to be larger for the wind power forecasts than for wind speed. In sec. 3.7 the effect of
applying a smoothing filter to the wind speed forecasts is analyzed and found to have a
beneficial effect for the downscaled forecasts but not for GFS. Lastly, sec. 3.8 compares
the performance of two physical parameterizations, specifically the YSU and Shin-Hong
PBL schemes. They are found to perform nearly identically.
This study serves as an addition to the body of research on the added value of
dynamical downscaling using mesoscale models for forecasts of wind speed at hub height
in particular, with a focus on wind power scheduling related issues. While not conclusive,
the results are an indication that it is worth investigating further whether, when and
where dynamical downscaling is beneficial for wind power forecasting. A limitation of the
study is the relatively short duration of only two months. Expanding the study would
enable more confidence in the results, and since there are multiple measurement masts
of identical design in the greater region it could also be expanded spatially.
Future
work could also include an analysis of the synoptic patterns during the study period.
Previous studies have shown this to be a strong predictor of errors (Lange, 2003; Bianco
et al., 2019). Assessing whether a relation exists between synoptic patterns and added
value from downscaling could have implications for wind power forecasters. Similarly,
previous studies have suggested a relation between anomaly correlation and ratios of
power contained within certain frequency bands to total spectral power (Rife, Davis,
et al., 2004). It could be of value to see if such a relation exists for added value in hub
height wind speed forecasts, shedding light on when and where dynamical downscaling
offers the most benefit for wind power forecasting.
