Abstract
This thesis has dealt with indoor climate in Danish primary schools and its effect on pupils’
well-being and performance. This was investigated with data from a nation-wide large-scale
indoor climate experiment. Former large-scale experiments from 2009 and 2014 showed, that
the indoor climate, especially CO2-concentration, was above the recommended levels in many
classes. Therefore, it was relevant to conduct a new study.
The indoor environment was measured with six parameters: Air temperature, operative tempera-
ture, CO2-concentration, illuminance, sound pressure level and reverberation time. A well-being
survey and a performance test was also performed by pupils to further evaluate the indoor climate
and its affect on pupils. In total 724 classes participated in the large-scale study.
The large-scale study was a ﬁeld experiment performed by pupils in their classrooms. All measure-
ments were instantaneous measurements performed with simple equipment. The measurements
were conducted on a day where the pupils did as usual, and on a day where the pupils aired out
and left the classroom in the recess prior to the experiment. The data was processed by discarding
duplicates, outliers, and submission-errors. Furthermore, physical data was matched with the
pupils’ well-being and performance-submissions. The relations in the data were explored with
graphs, correlation matrices and regression analysis.
The experiment showed an increase in pupils’ well-being, performance, and indoor air quality
when airing out. The study showed that pupils were capable of evaluating their indoor environment
regarding temperature, CO2-concentration and sound pressure level. From the well-being survey
it was possible to determine a preferred operative temperature of approximately 21°C with
polynomial regression. Furthermore, performance tests showed that the pupils tend to perform
better in low temperature intervals.
An improvement in CO2-concentration was found in this study compared to the previous large-
scale studies. Many of the participating classes stated that they had changed their airing out
habits due to the Covid-19-pandemic. Therefore, the reason for the lower CO2-concentrations
was difﬁcult to establish.
An additional experiment with two classes, also participating in the large-scale experiment,
was conducted. Here, two IC-meters were used for continuous measurements in the classrooms.
Despite differences in measurements, the experiment showed that the instantaneous measurements
in the large-scale study provided an indicator for the CO2-concentration and temperature in the
classrooms.

Resumé
Denne opgave omhandlede indeklima i danske folkeskoler, samt dets påvirkning af elevers vel-
beﬁndende og præstationsevne. Dette blev undersøgt i en landsdækkende indeklimaundersøgelse,
Masseeksperimentet 2021. Tidligere masseeksperimenter fra 2009 og 2014 viste, at indeklimaet i
de danske skoler, særligt CO2-koncentrationen, var i mange klasser over anbefalingerne. Det var
derfor relevant at foretage endnu et studie.
Indeklimaet blev undersøgt ved seks parametre: Lufttemperatur, operativ temperatur, CO2-
koncentration, belysningsstyrke, lydtryksniveau og efterklangstid. En trivselstest og en præsta-
tionstest blev udført af eleverne for yderligere at kunne vurdere indeklimaet og dets effekt på
eleverne. Sammenlagt deltog 724 klasser i masseeksperimentet.
Masseeksperimentet var et feltstudie, udført af eleverne i deres eget klasselokale. Alle målinger
var øjebliksmålinger udført med simpelt måleudstyr. Målingerne blev foretaget på en dag hvor
eleverne gjorde som de plejede, og en dag hvor eleverne luftede ud samt forlod klasselokalet
i frikvarteret inden lektionen. I databehandlingen blev dublikerede aﬂeveringer, afvigerer og
aﬂeveringsfejl kasseret. Fysisk data blev desuden sat sammen med elevernes velbeﬁndende og
præstationstest. Relationen i data blev undersøgt med grafer, korrelationsmatricer og regressions-
analyser.
Forsøget viste en stigning i præstationsevne, velbeﬁndende og luftkvalitet ved udluftning i
frikvarteret. Analysen viste, at eleverne var i stand til at validere deres indeklima med hensyn
til både temperatur, CO2-koncentration, og lydtryksniveau. Fra trivselsundersøgelsen var det
muligt gennem polynomial regression, at fastlægge en foretrukken temperatur på ca. 21°C for
eleverne. Præstationstesten viste desuden, at eleverne havde tildens til at præstere bedere i lave
temperaturintervaller.
I forhold til de tidligere masseeksperimenter blev en forbedring i CO2-koncentration fundet i
dette studie. Mange af de tilmeldte noterede, at de havde ændret udluftningsvaner i forbindelse
med Covid-19 pandemien, hvilket kan have haft en indvirken på eksperimentet.
To klasser, der medvirkede i masseksperimentet, deltog også i en yderligere undersøgelse af indek-
limaet. Her blev to IC-meter-målere anvendt til at måle klassernes indeklima kontinuerligt over
en periode. Forsøget viste, at på trods af forskel i målingerne, så fungerede øjebliksmålingerne
fra masseksperimentet som indikator for klasselokalernes CO2-koncentration og temperatur.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.1
Background
On average Danish pupils spend 200 days a year in primary schools (Børne- og undervisingsmin-
isteriet 2021). Primary schools are mandatory for ten years in Denmark, thereafter the majority of
pupils move to other educational institutes, such as high schools, artisan schools, and universities.
Primary schools are where pupils build their educational foundation. Therefore, a good learning
environment is essential. The quality of the learning environment depends on many aspects. One
of them is indoor climate.
A poor indoor climate decreases the ability to focus and learn. Studies showed that children and
pupils were especially sensitive to poor indoor climate, making the indoor environment in schools
very important (Wargocki and Wyon 2021).
In 2014, 80% of Danish schools were built between 1950 and 1980 (Clausen et al. 2017).
The requirement for mechanical ventilation in schools was introduced in the Danish building
code in 1995 (Traﬁk-, Bygge- og Boligstyrelsen 1995, 11.2.3 stk. 2). The building code
states that mechanical ventilation in classrooms must be installed unless a satisfactory indoor
climate can be achieved another way. In 2010 the maximum allowed CO2-concentration in
classrooms became speciﬁed in the building regulations (Traﬁk-, Bygge- og Boligstyrelsen 2010),
making the CO2-concentration an indicator for the indoor air quality, IAQ, in classrooms. The
maximum concentration was set to 0,1% (<1500ppm). In the current building code the maximum
concentration of CO2 in a classroom is 1000ppm (Bolig- og Planstyrelsen 2018a).
Figure 1.1 shows the total area of schools built in Denmark from 1900-2015 and their related
building code. Most of the Danish schools are built from 1950-1980, before the ventilation
requirements. This means, that many schools were built without a ventilation system. These
schools either have no mechanical ventilation system or a system installed after the schools were
built.
Figure 1.1: The cumulative share of the area of the primary school buildings by age (Clausen et al. 2017, p.7)
In 2014 a new primary school reform became law. Here, the weekly hours in school increased for
all primary schools (Folkeskolen.dk 2013). Furthermore, homework modules became a part of

2.1
Thermal comfort
Thermal comfort is the condition of mind that expresses satisfaction with the thermal environment
(Fanger and Valbjørn 1979). The body measures the temperature through receptors in the skin
which alerts the mind to react to either too warm or too cold environments. The body reacts in
different ways, for instance by sweating or shivering.
People change the thermal environment by changing their clothing, activity level, or the tempera-
ture setpoint for the heating/cooling systems.
Thermal comfort depends on six different parameters; radiant temperature, air temperature, air
velocity, relative humidity, clothing insulation, and activity level (Metabolic rate) (Fanger and
Valbjørn 1979). Two of these factors (clothing insulation and activity level) do not depend on the
building, but the occupants themselves. In the present study, the parameters relative humidity
and air velocity were not measured, furthermore, pupils were not asked to enter their clothing
insulation and activity level were not measured either.
The air temperature [°C] (1) is the temperature of the air
around the human body (Reinhart et al. 2001). The radi-
ant temperature [°C] (2) is the temperature determined
by radiant heat transfer depending on the occupants’
position in the room.
When investigating the thermal environment the mea-
surement points should be within the occupied zones
of the building, where the occupants are expected to
spend most of their time e.g. by the student seats and
the teachers’ desk in a classroom (Toftum 2013).

2.2
Indoor air quality
The indoor air quality, IAQ, deﬁnes the quality of the indoor air in terms of the concentration of
different substances in the air inside and around buildings. The quality of the indoor air is deﬁned
concerning people’s comfort, biological- and psychological health (Vural 2011). Pollutants
inﬂuencing the quality of the indoor air include the outside environment, usage of the building,
and building product (furniture, building materials etc.).
The air in the atmosphere consists of 78% Nitrogen NO2
and 21% oxygen O2 (NASA 2021). The last 1% is a mix
of gasses, water vapor, and other substances including
carbon dioxide. CO2 is often used as the indicator for
indoor air quality, in classrooms, ofﬁces etc where the
main pollutant is humans.
During respiration humans exhale CO2 which increases
the indoor concentration, depending on the number of
occupants, the volume of the room, the inﬁltration, and
ventilation. An average adult has a production rate of
18 L of CO2 pr. hour when performing common ofﬁce
work(4). Production rate of CO2 is greatly affected by
metabolic rate and size. Children often produce less
CO2.
According to the Danish building regulation, BR18, the CO2-concentration in a classroom should
not exceed 1000 ppm (Bolig- og Planstyrelsen 2018a). The outdoor concentration of (CO2) is
in Denmark around ∼400 ppm giving ∼0,04% (1). The concentration of a pollutant in a room

Lighting
The physical deﬁnition of light can be explained by the wave theory and the particle theory
(Tsangrassoulis et al. 2004). This is called the wave-particle-dualism of light. The frequent
deﬁnition of visible light is the part of the electromagnetic spectrum between 380-780 nm. These
wavelengths are visible with human eyes. Below 380 nm the light is ultra-violet and above 780
nm the light is infrared. Neither ultra-violet light nor infrared light is visible to the human eye.
The most fundamental measures of light in buildings
(1) are luminous intensity, luminance, luminous ﬂux and
illuminance (Tsangrassoulis et al. 2004, p. 13).
Luminous intensity [candela] is the quantity describing
the light emission from a light source in a given direction
or a certain solid angle (Tsangrassoulis et al. 2004, p.
13-14). The luminous intensity will vary depending on
the position of the occupants unless the luminaire is per-
fectly diffusing. The luminous intensity for candlelight
is 1 candela and 3·1027 candela for the sun.

2004, p. 14-15). The luminance will vary when changing the viewpoint. The luminance stabilizes
the intensity of the area. The luminous intensity is the product of the luminance and projected
area of the light source.
Luminous Flux [lumen] is the measure for the power of visible light. It is the total of the luminaires
output (Tsangrassoulis et al. 2004). The unit is deﬁned so 1 lumen is 1.46·10−3 W of optical
radiation at a wavelength of 555 nm to match the photopic ﬂux with the human eye, which is
most sensitive to the human eye. An incandescent bulb (100W) has a luminous intensity of 1200
lumen and a Fluorescent of 58W a luminous intensity of 4800 lumen.

Daylight
Daylight is the visible global radiation and the sum of sunlight and skylight (Ruck et al. 2000, p.
8.2). It is described as the direct and indirect outdoor sunlight that is transferred into a building
(3). Daylight is a dynamic light source of light as the illuminance from the sky is inconsistent, and
the variation can be large depending on seasons, local weather conditions, and latitude. Therefore,
an artiﬁcial lighting system is necessary. Daylight provides high illuminance and variable color
rendering throughout the day. To quantify the daylight conditions in an area the metrics daylight
factor and daylight autonomy are often used.
The daylight factor, DF, is the ratio between the amount of daylight available on a plane inside
compared to the daylight on an outside horizontal plane under unobstructed overcast sky. DF is
expressed as a percentage and the daylight is measured in lux (Velux Group 2021a) (Ruck et al.
2000, pp. 8–3). The formula for the daylight factor is shown in Equation 2.14.

Daylighting is inseparably related to solar gain (Ruck et al. 2000). The solar gain is a passive
heating technique and can lead to energy savings in cold climates. During warm periods the
solar gain can lead to overheating and thereby increase cooling loads and the building’s energy
use. To control the solar gains a shading system can be installed (4). Here, both passive and
active solar shadings can be added. A passive solar shade could be an overhang, side shadow or
solar-controlled glass that will narrow the solar gain when the sunlight inters from a certain angle.
Also, the orientation of the building is an architectural passive shading method. An active solar
shading system is a changeable shading system, which could be internal or external blinds. An
active shadings system can be manually or automatically controlled by e.g. the indoor temperature
and/or outside solar radiation.

According to the Danish building code, the ventilation rate in a classroom should be at least 5.0
l/s pr person, and 0.35 l/s pr. m² ﬂoor area (Bolig- og Planstyrelsen 2018a). If a mechanical
ventilation system is chosen, the system must involve both supply and exhaust, and the air must go
through a heat exchanger. Furthermore, the ventilation method shall secure a CO2-concentration
below 1000 ppm.
In requirements from 1980 only reverberation time regulation was stated for classrooms. In 1990
a requirements of 5 L/s pr person was introduced.
2.6
Brief summary of ventilation methods in classrooms
2.6.1
Decentral vs central ventilation system
To fulﬁll the requirements with a mechanical system, both an installation of a decentral or central
ventilation system can be used. A decentral ventilation system has separate ventilation units in
each room or deﬁned area. It has the advantage of little space requirements, individual adjustment
of operating times and a cheaper solution as the investment for a school can be distributed over
a period of time. Compared to a central system the decentral system has a higher service cost
as the aggregates run independently compared to the service cost of a central system (Exhausto
2021). With a central system, the air handling unit delivers fresh air to several rooms or areas,
making it easier to control. The central system requires space, which can be a disadvantage in
renovation projects if the building is not designed with space for ventilation ducts.
2.6.2
Natural ventilation
Natural ventilation is ventilation without a mechanical ventilation system. Buildings and building
parts have become more air-tight over the years. This makes ventilation exclusively with natural
ventilation difﬁcult (Masaaki and Isaac 2010). Especially during winter. The industry guidance
for classrooms outlines the high risk of draught (Vorre et al. 2021).

2.6.3
Other speciﬁc ventilation requirements
For other teaching facilities like chemistry, crafting etc. the removal of hazardous gases, particles
or dust is important. Here local exhaust ventilation placed next to the source is a necessity beside
the regular mechanical ventilation system (Branchefællesskabet for Arbejdsmiljø og Offentlig
administration 2018). The air from the local exhaust must not use the same exhaust ducts as the
central system, and it should not pass heat exchangers.
Other places which often require special ventilation are home economic rooms, kitchens and the
cafeteria. Cooking in general produces a high amount of particles (Sumei et al. 2020).

Chapter 3. The effect of indoor environment in classrooms
3.1
Thermal comfort
Studies have shown that temperature affects concentration and ability to think clearly for humans,
which are essential tools for learning (Lan et al. 2011) (Wargocki and Wyon 2021).
Wargocki and Wyon announced in 2006 a study performed in two Danish schools with pupils aged
10-12 years old. Here, the study conﬁrmed that the thermal environment inﬂuences the children’s
performance. By reducing the temperature from 25°C to 20°C the performance of two numerical
and two language-based tests was signiﬁcantly improved. Generally the improvement in the
performance in the tests was in speed of completion rather than lowering of errors (Wargocki and
Wyon 2006).
A thermal comfort survey based on 4866 responses from Australian primary- and secondary
schools investigated the children’s thermal comfort (Kim and Dear 2017). The data was collected
during two summer seasons and compared to the predicted mean vote, PMV, which is based
on adults. The study showed that under the same thermal environment, the Australian school
children preferred a temperature 2-3°C below the neutrality predicted for adults. This creates two
perceptions of the optimal operative temperature, one for the pupils and one for the teacher. The
inﬂuence of the thermal environment in relation to the quality of teaching was not investigated in
this literature study, but this will inﬂuence the pupils learning as well.
Studies have been conducted to test what temperatures are optimal from a physiological perspec-
tive (Kim et al. 2020). Blood pressure is used as a measurement of physiological health. By
decreasing the temperature from ’warm’ (above 28°C) to ’neutral’ (25°C) the blood pressure of
participants decreased signiﬁcantly. However, this is only the case when the CO2-concentration
is high (>2000 ppm) indicating that the CO2-concentration also affects blood pressure.
Wargocki et al. 2019 revealed a literature study regarding pupils. Pupils, on average, performed
20% better when reducing the temperature from 30°C to 20°C. Furthermore, this study showed
that the optimal temperature was below 22°C. This study is only valid for temperate climate
zones.
3.2
Indoor air quality
T. Honga, J. Kim, and M. Lee performed a study in a laboratory in South Korea testing the
effect of CO2-concentration in relation to the ability to perform certain tasks for adults (Honga
et al. 2018). The tasks performed by the subjects measured their visual reactions, short-term
memory, active working memory, and ability to type. Three scenarios were tested twice with
variations in temperature and CO2 concentration. These tests clearly showed an increase in
performance when the temperature increased from cold to neutral. Likewise, the performance
increased when the temperature decreased from a warm state to neutral. However, by decreasing
the CO2-concentration levels from approx. 2300 ppm to approx. 1000 ppm the performance of
subjects increased more, than in the scenario with just the temperature.
Ventilation studies performed at schools have shown that the concentration of CO2 also inﬂuence
pupils ability to concentrate (Williams et al. 2006) (Wargocki and Toftum 2017) (Petersen et al.

2015). Field studies have the disadvantage of not controlling the surrounding environment, making
it harder to narrow the key elements leading to lower performance, discomfort, etc. However,
with the amount of data collected in schools regarding performance and CO2-concentration,
conclusions can still be drawn.
A study revealed in 2020 (Wargocki et al. 2020) could, based on the available references, conclude
that pupils solve school assignments 12% faster and 2% more correctly when the concentration
of CO2 was reduced from 2100 ppm to 900 ppm. Furthermore, test and exam results were found
to be 5% better when the CO2 concentration went from 2400 ppm to 900 ppm.
3.3
Sound
Sound in buildings is a summation of the sound from the surrounding environment (e.g. trafﬁc
noise), the electrical equipment (e.g. ventilation system), and the sound generated by the
occupants themselves. The acceptable levels and expectations of sound depend on the building
usage. High levels of sound is accepted at concerts but not when entering a library. In a classroom,
acoustic conditions are expected to create a good learning environment.
Studies show, that there is a deﬁnite correlation between cognitive performance and noise (Jafari
et al. 2019). Mental workload, visual attention, and audiotorial attention are all affected by high
levels of noise. This was shown in a study, where participants were asked to solve sudokus, where
the accuracy decreased from 97.0% to 94.4% when the noise increased from 40 dB(A) to 55
dB(A) (Lee et al. 2017).
The ability to concentrate and perform in noisy areas differs from individual to individual. Mind-
wandering and switching skills are predictors of this ability (Massonnié et al. 2020). If a pupil is
more likely to mind-wander, noise becomes more disturbing to the pupils’ concentration. A better
switching skill means that the pupils are better at switching back to the task at hand if disturbed
by noise.
Another risk of excessive noise comes in the health risks, that this can provide. A study done at
the University of London (Stansfeld and Matheson 2003) suggests that there are short-term health
risks such as cardiovascular disease and increased blood pressure. However, this data can be hard
to relate to activities and noise in a class, as pupils do not spend all their time in classrooms. A
more pressing concern regarding health risks is raised by the EPA (EPA 2009), where concerns
such as interference with speech and language, impair learning, impair hearing, and disruption
of sleep are mentioned.
Some studies suggest, that the tonality affects the disturbance factor of the noise (Massonnié et al.
2020).
3.4

Lighting and daylight is also an important factor when characterizing the indoor environment and
studies have shown that lighting and daylight inﬂuence our health and ability to perform well

As most studies have been in ofﬁce buildings Barkmann, Wellelowski, and Schulte-Markwort
decided to perform a study in schools (Barkmann et al. 2011). Here, they compared a variable
lighting system with a regular lighting system at 300 lux constantly. The variable lighting
system consisted of seven different lighting programs. The teacher switched between the lighting
programs depending on the class activities. The pupils’ ability to concentrate, reading speed,
and comprehension were tested during the experiment. During the tests of these parameters the
lighting program concentration was used. The concentration program had an illuminance of 1060
lux and a color temperature of 5800 K (cold light). The concentrating of pupils was improved
by 20.8% and reading speed was improved by 16.8%. Five researchers from the Netherlands
drew the same conclusion from their experiment with a variable lighting system (Sleegers et al.
2011). To validate the pupils’ performance they used a d2 test (test of attention). The pupils
with a variable lighting system performed better and faster indicating the importance of a good
lighting environment in schools.
In 2016 N. Sheishegar and M. Boubekri announced a literature analysis stating the inﬂuence of
IEQ’s with a focus on daylight and how it affects pupils’ performance (Shishegar and Boubekri
2016). They found that the interest in daylight instead of artiﬁcial light has grown as new research
outlines the importance of daylight to obtain a great lighting environment. It was found that
daylight inﬂuences both people’s mental and physical health in relation to mood, sleep, stress, etc.
The wide spectrum of light in daylight and the variation of the spectrum during the day inﬂuenced
humans’ circadian rhythm. Disturbing the circadian rhythm with a constant artiﬁcial lighting
level would have a negative inﬂuence on peoples performance and health. From the study, N.
Sheishegar and M. Boubekri concludes that of all IEQs lighting had the highest inﬂuence on the
human body.

• The accuracy of sudokus decreased from 97.0% to 94.4% when the noise increased from

• Pupils who were more likely to mind-wander were more sensitive to noise during concen-

Chapter 5. The experiment
5.1
Experimental design of the large-scale study
The large-scale experiment lasted from November 1st to December 10th, 2021. Initially the
experiment was planned to last 3 weeks, however this was extended by an addition period of
3 weeks, due to the Covid-19 situation, which made it difﬁcult for the classes to complete the
experiments as planned.
In the large-scale experiment each class performed the experiment twice. One day the class did
’as usual’ in the recess before the experiment, and one day the class ventilated the classroom by
opening the windows and leaving the classroom in the recess before the experiment. The order
in which a class did ’as usual’ or ’aired out’ before the experiment was randomized due to the
advantage of repeating the performance test on the second day. During the physical measurements
of the indoor climate, the windows were kept closed on both days.
In this section, the experiment is divided into three categories; building checklist, physical
measurements, and subjective measurements.
5.1.1
Building checklist
The building checklist was a digital form with questions regarding the physical condition of
the classroom and building e.g. volume of the classroom, window direction and the year of
construction, ventilation methods. The form is shown in Appendix B.1 This form is completed
once by the whole class, as the physical attributes of the classroom did not change from ﬁrst to
second day.
5.1.2
Physical measurements
An experimental protocol for each of the physical measurements was developed by the Technical
University of Denmark, DTU, in collaboration with Astra. The protocols were all gathered in
one manual with descriptions of indoor climate in general and the four indoor climate domains
(Christensen et al. 2021). Furthermore, a separate manual for the teachers was created to ensure
consistency in the experiments.
The classes submitted physical measurements in ﬁve parameters; daily conditions, CO2-concentration,
operative temperature, air temperature, sound pressure level, reverberation time and illuminance.
The daily conditions were entered on the ﬁrst page. The class should ﬁll whether it was a normal
or airing out day, the number of pupils present in the class that day, if the weather was sunny or
cloudy, and if the light was on or off. This was done, for a broader understanding of the physical
measurement.
The operative temperature was measured using a thermometer strip,
ranging from 14°C to 31°C. The strip was plastered on a table tennis ball
(see illustration). The tennis ball was placed approx. 0.6 m above ﬂoor
level. After a few minutes the operative temperature was read.
The air temperature was measured with the same thermometer strip

and table tennis ball. The tennis ball was kept in motion for approx. 30
seconds, hereafter the air temperature was read.
The CO2-concentration was measured with a Kitagawa tube measure-
ment. A Kitagawa tube is a small glass tube with an alkali sodium
hydroxide (NaOH) (Komyo Rikagaku Kogyo k.k. 2006). It is a strong
base that reacts with the CO2 in the air and splits the hydroxides into
hydrons. The reaction is shown in Equation 5.1.
CO2 +2NaOH →Na2CO3 +H2O
(5.1)
The hydrons generate a color change in the tube from pink to yellow. The concentration of CO2
could then be read from where the color stops changing. In the experiment, the pupils ﬁlled a
syringe with 50 ml of air and then pressed it into the tube (see illustration). 50 mL of air is half
the normal amount. Therefore, the pupils multiplied the readings of the concentration by two.
Just one measurement was done each day.
The sound pressure level was measured using the app Noise Exposure
(Arbetsmiljöverket 2011). The app measures the average A-weighted SPL,
over a period of time with the phone’s microphone. In this experiment,
the pupils measured during the whole lesson including the experiment
(approx. 1 hour). Only the average value was collected.
The reverberation time was measured with the app APM Tool Lite
(lorenzo rizzi 2014). The reverberation time was measured in the center of
the classroom with the phone’s microphone. First, the app measured the
background noise and then waits for a strong sound. The app measures
ﬁve short bursts of sounds generated in different points in the room
before revealing the result. The results used in this study was the T20-
measurements. A T20 measurement is a measurement, which measures
the time it takes for the average sound energy energy to decay by 60 dB.
The results are shown in seconds. The pupils popped balloons to create
the ﬁve short bursts of sound.
The illuminance was analyzed by measuring the illuminance at desk-
top level in four quadrants in the classroom. Here, the pupils used the
Swedish app Ljus. The app uses the phone’s back camera to measure the
illuminance level. When measuring, the phone was placed face-down,
with white printing paper blocing the phones back camera. The pupils
divided their classroom into four equally sized squares and measured the
lighting in each of the squares during four different scenarios giving a
total of 17 measurements. The four indoor scenarios were; A) Artiﬁcial lighting turned on and no
solar shading, B) Artiﬁcial lighting turned off and no solar shading, C) Artiﬁcial lighting turned
on and solar shading on, D) Artiﬁcial lighting turned off and solar shading on. Lastly, E) Outside
illuminance measured once.

were send back to the start screen and completed the form again or the screen froze and they
clicked the ’hand in’ button several times. To get around this problem the data went through

class-ID, behavior and time stamp it was considered a duplicate. Each submission had a unique
submission ID. For the physical data the latest data-set was kept. Before the deletion the data was
sorted based on the submission ID and the highest submission ID was kept for each class. The
latest data was kept as it was observed that several teachers had corrected their answers in their
latest submissions. The latest data was kept as the correct submission was prioritised over the
immediate submission.

7.3.6
Summary of physical measurements
The physical measurements show the general indoor climate parameters of schools participating
the large-scale study. The main ﬁndings were:
• Regarding temperatures, most classes complied with the indoor climate requirements stated
in BR18. Newer schools tend to have more overheating, whereas older buildings tend to be
too cold.
• Airing out in classrooms reduces the CO2-concentration notably.
• 39% of the classes did not achieve a lower CO2-concentration by airing out in the recess
prior to the lesson of the experiment.
• The temperature measurements only showed small differences from temperatures measured
in previous large-scale studies.
• The CO2-concentration in classrooms was in general lower in 2021, compared to the
previous large-scale studies.
• The lighting levels of the classrooms were difﬁcult to evaluate. However, even in situation
A (artiﬁcial light on and no solar shading) ∼half of the classes did not reach in average
levels above the requirements of 300 lux.
• A correlation between SPL and the amount of pupil present was found.
• Lower SPL-levels were measured in high school.

DISCUSSION
Initially, 1227 classes signed up for the large-scale study. For a mul-
titude of reasons only 724 (59%) classes ended up taken part in the
experiment and only 30% completed all aspects of the experiment.
In this chapter the ﬁndings and results will be discussed in terms
of weaknesses, errors and improvements. The weaknesses of the
experiment, measuring methods and collected data are explored and
discussed. Furthermore the methods used for data processing are dis-
cussed. Lastly, a brief discussion of the use of the data in future work
and improvements for future large-scale experiments is presented.

Chapter 8. Discussion
8.1
Collection of data
The large-scale study was a ﬁeld experiment performed by pupils in their classrooms. A wide
spectrum of unknown factors, possible errors and mistakes had undoubtedly occurred. In this
section some possible errors in the ﬁeld experiment and data processing are described.
8.1.1
The experiment
This large-scale experiment had many measurements attached to it. Both sound pressure level,
reverberation time, illuminance, operative temperature and air temperature were added compared
to the previous large-scale experiments in 2009 and 2014. Furthermore, two subjective measure-
ments were added. These were conducted by all pupils involved. The subjective measurement
required the ability to read and understand the experiment. Therefore, classes from 0-3rd grade
had trouble completing the subjective measurements, which could explain the low participating
rate.
A teacher at Vesterbro Ny Skole, mentioned that they did not have computers available for 0-3rd
grade, which made it impossible to do the subjective measurements. Instead they decided to let
pupils at age 7-10th grade do the subjective measurement instead. Unfortunately it was unknown
in which classes and if Vesterbro Ny Skole was a single instance.
On top of the many new measurements the Covid-19 pandemic also inﬂuenced the data collection.
During the time of the experiment the new Covid-19 variant, Omikron, was growing throughout
the country. The spreading of the virus was mostly in primary school, as few pupil had been
vaccinated at the time. The situation was time consuming and stressful for teachers making
it difﬁcult to prioritize the large-scale study. According to Astra (Appendix F) more teachers
complained about the extend of the experiment and they felt more stressed, compared to previous
large-scale studies. To accommodate the teachers the experiment was extended for three more
weeks.
The technical solutions behind the experiment were developed by Astra. For some classes a
technical error occurred when the pupils/teachers submitted the measurements. No conﬁrmation
screen showed, saying they completed the measurements. Instead they were send back to the
start screen. This created many duplicate submissions. Furthermore, much of the received data
was received at equal time stamps. It was assumed, that the server receiving the data in batches.
Therefore, it was difﬁcult to distinguish duplicate submissions from re-submissions.
8.1.2
The data submissions
If there were multiple physical submissions at the exact same time the submission ID was used
to determine the newest. For the physical data the newest submissions was prioritised and kept
(Chapter 6), as it was assumed, that the newest submission was corrected. A disadvantage could
be, that some teachers might have been more inattentive in the late submission and made an error.
In this case the corrected data would have been discarded. For the subjective measurements the
ﬁrst submission was kept if a duplicate in class-ID, pupil number, and date. The ﬁrst measurement
was assumed to be the initial feeling (well-being survey) or answer (performance test). If the ﬁrst
==PAGE:105==
Chapter 8. Discussion
92
submission was a test or other, the correct answer would have been discarded.
Another type of error was a class-ID-type error. Each class was assigned a unique class-ID. A
teacher with several classes assigned received individual links for each class. Therefore, confusion
regarding class-ID created errors. Some mismatches were corrected in the data processing, but
the extend of mismatches was unknown. This could have resulted in wrong data matches between
the physical and subjective measurements.
Furthermore, the matches were based on date and time. In the subjective measurements the pupils
did not answer if it was a normal or airing out day. If this question was incorporated in both
submissions, the data matching would have been easier. Initially this was discussed in the ﬁrst
drafts for the well-being survey and performance test but neglected as a higher incorrectness in
data matches was assumed.
A large amount of data was neglected in the data match between physical and subjective data, as
the data was not submitted within the chosen acceptable time frame. It was noted that several
teachers submitted the data for both days at the same time. Therefore, it was impossible to
establish the correct match with the subjective measurements. These mismatches outline that the
message to the teachers regarding time of submission and data match has not been clear enough.
8.1.3
Errors in data processing
The data was processed before analysed. In the data processing the determination of outliers, was
establish before the data was received, to be as objective as possible.
The initial data had many errors, which could have been salvaged by looking through the data
manually. For instance an area measurement of 515 m2, was most likely 51.5 m2, but the teacher
had forgotten the dot. Errors like these were discarded in the measurements, however before
going through the outlier removal one common error were corrected. Many classes had written
an interval in the temperature measurements. These were corrected to the average of the two
numbers e.g. 20-22°C became 21°C. If more time was used on detecting errors before outlier
removal, more common errors could have been detected and more usable data could have been
saved.
During the data match some errors were detected and corrected. The correction of this data was
made subjectively with no contact to the classes in question. Though the change in e.g. class-ID
or behavior seemed obvious the assumptions could be wrong.
8.2
Results
All gathered measurements from the large-scale experiment were instantaneous measurements
and not continuously over a period of time. Therefore, the data did not represent the general
indoor environment of the individual classes. Instantaneous measurements were used to save
time, equipment, and to incorporate the experiment in teaching. Furthermore, all submissions
in the physical measurements were submitted in text-ﬁelds by the teachers. Therefore, some
measurements undoubtedly had typographical errors.
==PAGE:106==
93
Chapter 8. Discussion
The data was collected with simple methods and had a low accuracy in data readings. The
temperature strip and the kitagawa tube was difﬁcult to read accurately, and the other parameters
were measured on smartphones. When measuring the CO2-concentration the classes had to
multiply the reading with two. Therefore, the substance of the data lies in its large amount. The
large amount of instantaneous measurements conducted in classrooms with similar geometry,
usage patterns and daily loads, makes the data more legitimate. Furthermore, the study took place
in November/December, so the ﬁndings are only representative for the individual classes this
time a year.
Even though the study was conducted on schools all throughout Denmark the study can make
no conclusion on the general environment in Danish schools. Every school participating in the
large-scale study had signed up themselves. The classes had an interest in their indoor climate
making them not representative for all Danish schools. Further interest behind their sign up was
unknown.
8.2.1
Building checklist and physical measurements
Two temperature measurements were conducted using a temperature strip. When applying the
strip the temperature rose due to the friction of hands. If the time span between applying the strip
and reading the temperatures was too low, the temperature measurements would have been too
high. This could have effected the temperature data. The small additional study with IC-meters
showed a decrease in temperature compared to the large-scale. However, the change is assumed
to reﬂect the change in outdoor temperature.
As the temperature measurements were done with different methods in the three large-scale
studies the comparison was inaccurate, but the distribution of the temperatures were similar.
Therefore, a comparison was assumed valid.
Regarding the measured CO2-concentrations the same method was used between the previous
large-scale studies and the current. An improvement in CO2-concentration on normal days was
found. The previous studies were conducted roughly a month earlier in the year. Here, the
weather was warmer and airing out may have been more frequent. Even though just 41% of
classes said their behavior had changed due to Covid-19, the media in general had more focus on
airing out.
The pandemic forced pupils to stay home if they showed any symptoms of Covid-19. Therefore,
a decreased amount of pupils present could explain the decrease in CO2-concentration. The
average number of pupils present in classes was 18.7, which seems low compared to the Danish
class-coefﬁcient of 22. The amount of pupils were not counted in the large-scale studies in 2009
and 2014. Therefore, it was difﬁcult to determine if the improvement in CO2-concentration was
due to improvement of the schools’ ventilation systems, change of general behavior or due to the
pandemic.
An average decrease in the CO2-concentration was seen from normal day to airing out day. The
amount of classes measuring CO2-concentrations below the requirement of 1000 ppm was reduced
noticeably. Still, 24 % of classes did not measure a lower concentration when airing out compared
==PAGE:107==

94
to normal days. For further understanding an additional evaluations would be necessary. Schools
with full-automatic building systems perhaps had trouble opening the windows. Multivariable
regression analysis could have been conducted to further understand these results.
In the experiment the classes measured illuminance, sound pressure level and RT using their
smartphones. The ﬁndings from the pilot project together with meetings from Astra, showed that
the smartphones was not an issue (Chapter 5). Furthermore, the apps had high inaccuracy when
comparing to measuring equipment for illuminance and reverberation time. Apps, in general, are
updated consistently and it is not possible to download older versions of apps. Old tests of apps
might show different results. Tests of the apps were done in the spring of 2020. Therefore, a
disparity between versions of apps used in the large-scale experiment and the tested app version
could have occurred. Furthermore, a disparity of data could occur depending on the smartphone’s
features, age and brand.
The pupils measured illuminance in the classroom 16 times during the experiment (17 including
the outdoor measurement). The pupils were asked to repeat scenario A and B, and assign the
measurement to scenario C and D, if no solar shading were installed. By repeating the same
scenarios twice it was impossible to determine whether the classes had solar shading or not.
Furthermore, all analyses were based on assumed conditions, as they did not answer their scenario
during the subjective measurements.
The measured sound pressure level was conducted using a smartphone app, which could create
a difference in data sampling as different phones were used in each classroom. Though the
measurements were measured using an app only few results were determined as outliers and
discarded. Expected results were shown in the data set, e.g more pupil present in the classroom
increased the average sound pressure level.
Regarding the reverberation time, many data points were assumed outliers and removed. In
the initial data processing 885 measurements was discarded as outliers. In the ﬁnal datasheet,
this meant that only 711 reverberation time measurements were used out of 1278 possible
measurements. The measurements in some classes reached extremely high levels. This could
indicate that the app had trouble identifying the background sound and subtract it when the
balloons were popped. Another factor could be that the pupils had trouble staying quiet during
these measurements. Due to the high amount of outliers further analysis of correlation with
building geometry was not prioritized. Furthermore, RT was not assumed to be a problem in
many classes, as humans absorb sound themselves.
8.2.2
Subjective measurements
It was planned that the performance test should record time. If the performance test was timed it
would have been possible to get a more nuanced insight in the pupils’ performance. Furthermore,
the questions in the performance test was equal and in same order both days. This made it
easier for pupils to answer the performance test correctly the second time. The overall change
in performance was countered by having roughly half the pupils start with normal days and the
other half start with airing out days. However, it is possible that the performance test would have
been more distinct if the order of the questions were randomized.

The difﬁcult questions in the performance test were the questions with negated statements.
Unfortunately these questions were all questioned in continuation of each other. This could have
inﬂuenced the high performance score.
8.3
Future work
This thesis examined the physical measurements separately. Findings were used to seek cor-
relations between the physical measurements, the pupils’ performance and well-being. How
the indoor climate parameters effect each other and their connection to the building checklist
was minimally explored in this thesis. These correlations could be relevant for future analysis
and understanding of the indoor climate in classrooms. For construction of new classrooms or
renovations ﬁndings could be used as inspiration and guidance.
No multivariable regression analysis was conducted. All correlations evaluated in this report were
between two parameters at a time. To further investigate the data multivariable analysis could
be conducted. Especially the results regarding the pupils performance could be further analysed
with more parameters, as few relations were found.
The large-scale study faced many problems and obstacles. Some which could have been prevented,
others which were unforeseen. Regarding the physical measurements other sampling methods
could have been used, especially, the collection of light and reverberation time. Regarding the
subjective measurements, the performance test could have been timed and the questions should
have been randomized as initially planned. Some, of the unforeseen errors where the technical
difﬁculties and the Covid-19 situation.
The well-being survey was a success. The pupils seemed to understand their environment and the
questions seemed, from the analysis easy to understand for pupils at all grade-levels.
In this thesis only two out of 49 classes, who used IC-meters, were analysed. Furthermore,
only data from 1 week was used. A large amount of continuous data is available for analysis of
classrooms in Denmark. The IC-meters also measured sound, which has not been investigated in
this thesis.

Chapter 9. Conclusion
In this thesis the indoor environment in the Danish classes participating in the large-scale study
in 2021 was analysed. The effect of six indoor climate parameters on pupils’ well-being and
performance was analysed. Furthermore, the effect of airing out on the indoor climate, the pupils’
well-being and their performance was examined.
From the data collected in the study a change in indoor climate, performance and the pupils’
well-being was found when the pupils aired out.
The measured CO2-concentration was lower when the pupils left their classroom and opened
their windows in the recess prior to the measurements. Concentrations above 3000 ppm were
reduced from 7% to 2%, but the high concentrations were still measured. A relation between low
CO2-concentrations and mechanical ventilation systems was found. Even with both supply and
exhaust in the classrooms values above the requirements were still measured.
When airing out in the recess prior to the measurement the pupils valuated the indoor air quality
higher. They also felt less tired, more motivated and concentrated.
A relation between age and the perception of indoor climate was found showing that pupils at
higher grade-levels were more critical towards the indoor climate. Furthermore, their well-being
was more susceptible to change.
In this thesis several correlations between the felt and the measured indoor climate were found.
The measured sound pressure levels had a correlation with the pupils perception of noise. Further-
more, a small correlation between sound pressure level and the pupils’ experienced concentration
was found. Pupils with high measurements of sound pressure levels had a harder time concentrat-
ing.
A correlation was found between high ratings of the indoor air quality and low measurements
of CO2-concentrations. This indicates that the pupils were able to evaluate the quality of the air.
However, a higher relation between perceived air quality and temperature was found.
Using polynomial regression a preferred temperature, based on the pupils thermal sensation and
the operative temperature, was found to be 21.1°C. This matches ﬁndings from previous studies
performed with children. Furthermore, the best performing operative temperature range was
between 18-20°C in this study.
A low correlation between CO2-concentration and performance was found. However, an improve-
ment in average performance was measured on airing out days compared to normal days for all
grades above the 3rd.
Correlations between the pupils’ performance and well-being were higher than correlations
between performance and the measured indoor climate. The greatest correlation was between
feeling able to concentrate and the pupils performance-score. Regarding indoor climate, a
correlation between thermal sensation and performance was found.
Unfortunately this study found the measurements of light inaccurate and no correlations with
neither well-being nor performance were found.
An additional study was conducted in two classrooms with continuous measurements during a
week. The study showed high correlation between airing out and decrease in both temperature and
==PAGE:111==
Chapter 9. Conclusion
98
CO2-concentration. The CO2-concentrations measured in the large-scale study did not represent
the average value in the classrooms throughout the week, but provided a good indicator for
CO2-concentrations in the two classrooms.
Comparing the large-scale study of 2021 with the previous large-scale studies the temperature
measurements has not changed noticeably, but lower CO2-concentrations were measured in 2021.
Covid-19 has unfortunately made it impossible to state the reason, as 41% of the participating
classes stated a change in behavior due to the pandemic.
The conclusion from the large-scale studies in 2009 and 2014 was that the indoor climate in
the participating classes was unsatisfactory. The same conclusion must be drawn for this thesis,
based on the large-scale experiment of 2021.

Placering af IC-meter 
Jeres IC-meter skal placeres i det klasselokale/undervisningslokale, hvor I vil teste indeklimaet.  
For at dataene kan bruges, er det vigtigt at IC-metret placeres det rigtige sted i lokalet. Nedenfor er en 
liste med krav til placeringen: 
- 
Der skal være en stikkontakt indenfor rækkevidde, da IC-metret skal være tilsluttet for at måle. 
- 
Det skal ikke være lige ved vinduet. 
- 
Det skal ikke placeres på eller nær en radiator eller anden varmekilde. 
- 
Det skal ikke placeres på en arbejdsplads, hvor en person vil kunne udånde på det. 
- 
IC-metret må ikke berøres for meget, når det først er sat i.

Appendix F. Note from Astra
From Lene Christensen, Astra, program leader for the large-scale experiment: 
 
‘’ Den klart største årsag til manglende gennemførsel har været corona situationen. 
Rigtig mange lærere har været sygemeldt eller hjemsendt, og de har derfor haft svært 
ved at gennemføre de uger vi havde lagt op til. Derfor valgte vi at udvide med endnu 
tre uger, og det fik lidt flere til at gennemføre. Men en del har stadig ikke gennemført, 
fordi de simpelthen ikke kunne overskue det, oveni alle de andre ting de skulle 
forholde sig til - skift af klasser, skift af lærere osv. Vi har klart kunne mærke t meget 
højere stressnievau 'derude' end alle de tidligere år, vi har gennemført 
Masseeksperiment. 
 
En del indskolingsklasser fandt eksperimentet for svært at gennemføre. Så de har 
enten meldt fra, eller de har 'givet deres plads' til en kollega med ældre elever. Vi har 
gjort meget for at hjælpe dem i mål, vejledning over telefon og på mail, men det har 
været svært for nogle indskolingslærer, at 'overskue' selve eksperimentet.  
 
Endelig er der nogle som har haft svært ved at svare/gennemføre de to elevtest. Det 
bunder i en 'systemfejl', som var meget svært at gøre noget ved. Hvis rigtig mange 
elever var på, på samme tid, fik de ikke altid et svar ('tak fordi du gennemførte*), og 
så troede de ikke de havde gennemført. Så prøvede de igen, med samme resultat, og 
så gav de op med resten af klassen. Det kan være forklaringen på, I har flere klasser 
der har gennemført de fysiske målinger, end I har klasser der har svaret på de to 
test. ’’
