Abstract
Primary schools in Denmark have a relatively manual process of constructing their best­fitting
timetable. This can be an expensive and time­consuming process. In this thesis, we seek to opti­
mize this process. We define a model framework with 10 sub­objectives, seen as schedule qual­
ities, that is applied to 16 instances from 8 schools. The framework has the goal of automating
the majority of the process when building timetables, without sacrificing quality. The framework
is implemented for a Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) model and two Metaheuristic methods,
Simulated Annealing (SA) and Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search (ALNS), both powered by
a delta evaluation. The results show the MIP achieves the best schedules but with a 24­hour time
frame. Metaheuristics are introduced as a short time frame is essential for a proposed interactive
approach of finding the best weights of sub­objectives in the framework. The Metaheuristic meth­
ods find comparable results for only a 5­minute time frame, and qualitatively could be applied with
the framework to find good timetables for Danish primary schools.
Keywords: Timetabling, Metaheuristics, Simulated Annealing, Adaptive Large Neighbourhood
Search, Denmark, Mixed Integer Programming, Primary school, Gurobi.

Introduction
Education plays a crucial role in modern society, and as the only mandatory part of the educational
system, primary schools play a major role for all of us. This is not only the place where children
learn grammar and basic addition, but also where they are formed into young adults. Primary
school is where we learn a new language, but also the main form of socialization for a big chunk of
our early life. For primary schools to get children through a long list of mandatory learning objec­
tives while thriving socially and mentally, maintaining a balance is essential. This is a tremendous
logistic puzzle that requires a lot of resources. Given its widespread impact, the effective manage­
ment of these resources ­ especially time and personnel ­ is essential to ensure a functioning school
environment.
A fundamental part of this management is timetabling; the process of constructing schedules for
classes, teachers, and rooms that simultaneously ensures that students receive the required educa­
tion while accommodating teacher availability and workloads. Timetables not only shape the daily
routines of student and staff but also affect their school­life balance and overall well­being. With
more than 1000 primary schools in Denmark as of 2022 [1], the task of scheduling is both time­
consuming and recurrent. Many schools even rework their schedules multiple times each year.
Ideally, the process of producing schedules was as streamlined as possible, and while commercial
products do exist, this is not the case
This thesis aims to automate parts of the scheduling process to reduce the tremendous amount
of manual effort needed to build good timetables, without sacrificing quality. It is conducted in
collaboration with Subit, which is a company that aids primary schools in many municipalities
with time­ and workload management ­ particularly for substitute teachers.
To achieve this, we propose a framework that is as general as possible, allowing for customization
to accommodate, ideally, all school structures and preferences. The proposed framework will be
utilized through the implementation of Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) and two Metaheuristic
methods, Simulated Annealing and Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search. Exact methods like
MIP can result in optimal solutions, but Metaheuristic methods may be best suited for practical use
due to their ability to produce better solutions in short time frames.
Following this introduction we have a literature review and description on how primary schools in
Denmark currently construct schedules. In Chapter 2 we present the data from Subit, and how we
use it. In Chapter 3 the MIP is presented, with a brief presentation of its results in Chapter 4. In
Chapter 5 we present the Metaheuristic methods, with all combined results presented in Chapter
6. In Chapter 7 we discuss these results and consider their limitations and implications. Chapter 8
presents several ideas for future work before finally concluding in Chapter 9.
1.1
Litterature Review
Timetabling is considered a challenging and important problem within the field of operations re­
search, and it has been for decades. Timetabling is a broad term to which many types of problems
are associated. Optimization of timetabling dates back to at least the 1960s. For more on early
timetabling we refer to Smidth and Strölein [2].
Educational Timetabling is a sub­genre of timetabling problems and is where our focus will lie.
More specifically, we will in the thesis be working with Primary School Timetabling. Although,
we start here by talking about educational timetabling in general.

==PAGE:8==
According to Kingston [3], extensive research has been conducted in the field of educational
timetabling, especially in the last couple of decades where computing power has soared and the
understanding of the problem has improved. The field is rather significant and many variants have
been proposed in the literature. These can differ on the type of institution and thus the type of con­
straints needed ­ In general, all educational institutions face the problem of educational timetabling
in some form.
Universities must schedule courses within the same curriculum to avoid overlaps. Examinations
for these courses need to be held in rooms and at times that accommodate the courses’ schedules
and capacities. High schools require that all classes meet the required subjects a specific number
of times each week or year.
This leads us to the four main branches of educational timetabling (Schaerf [4] and Holm et al. [5]):
exam timetabling, university course timetabling, high school timetabling, and student sectioning.
Student sectioning, which involves assigning students to sections rather than times, is usually con­
sidered separately (Kristiansen and Stidsen [6]). Therefore, we will not consider student sectioning
as we delve further into the branches of educational timetabling.
University Course Timetabling problem is the task of assigning lectures/courses to time slots,
rooms and other resources, subject to constraints applied for the single student (McCollum [7]).
The large size and structure of universities often makes the problem of course timetabling so com­
plicated it is solved by several experts scattered across different faculties and departments. Due
to the complexity of the problem, it is quite common that the universities re­use the solution from
previous years when solving the problem.
University Course Timetabling is often divided into two general approaches; the Curriculum­based
the Enrollment­based University Course Timetabling (Kristiansen og Stidsen [6]). Courses that can
be taken in combination ­ because they are needed to satisfy the degree rules of the given study ­ are
called curricula. Assigning courses to time slots based on this is called Curriculum­based Course
Timetabling. The problem consists of creating the weekly schedules of lectures where conflicts
between the courses are set according to the curriculum specified by the university. Gaspero et
al. [8] gives a short description of the Curriculum­based Course Timetabling Problem applied for
Track three (problem­type) of ITC2007.
The second university approach is the Post Enrollment­based Course Timetabling. Based on the
enrollment data of each student, courses are scheduled in the timetable to ensure all students can
attend their enrolled events. This approach is also known as the Class Timetabling Problem or
Event Timetabling in the literature. Lewis et al. [9] describe the Post Enrollment­based Course
Timetabling Problem as applied to Track Two of ITC2007.
ITC is the International Timetabling Competition and where the University Course timetabling was
more recently also the subject of ITC2019 but this time on rich real­world data sets. The winners
used a Graph­based MIP formulation (Holm, Stidsen [10]). Exam timetabling is scheduling exams
for students under limited resources. This can vary a lot in terms of complexity. In Schaerf [4]
the difference between exam timetabling and university course timetabling is observed as being
relatively small and the problems could be modeled using the same model.
The most relevant for our thesis is the (High) School timetabling problem. According to Pillay [11]
the High school Timetabling problem essentially involves allocating class, teacher, and room tuples
to timetable slots so as to satisfy the hard and soft constraints of the problem. This is the basics,
but the specific problem can vary a great deal based on specific schools. Also, the studies are often
done in isolation from each other, and general results can be hard to come by. This, combined with
the lack of benchmark data (Pillay [11], Kristiansen and Stidsen 2013 [6]) are the main reasons
that a lot of work within educational timetabling is focused on university course timetabling and

examination timetabling.
Various approaches have been employed to address the problem of educational timetabling, yield­
ing both feasible and effective solutions. These range from generalized methodologies to specific
implementations tailored to solve unique challenges at individual institutions. Examples of so­
lution techniques include population­based algorithms, such as the swarm algorithm applied by
Nothegger et al. [12] for the Post­Enrollment Course Timetabling problem and the evolutionary
algorithm used by Nurmi and Kyngäs [13] for the Curriculum­Based Course Timetabling problem.
Another prominent approach is the use of local search algorithms. Casey and Thompson [14], for
instance, utilized a Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP) to tackle the Exam­
ination Timetabling problem, employing the Toronto benchmark datasets introduced by Carter et
al. [15]. Valouxis and Housos [16] combined local search with constraint programming to address
scheduling challenges in Greek high schools.
Graph coloring algorithms have also been explored. Burke et al. [17] developed a Graph­Based
Hyper Heuristic for Timetabling Problems, while Abdul­Rahman and Bargiela [18] leveraged
adaptive linear combinations of graph coloring heuristics with a heuristic modifier to address
Examination Timetabling problems, utilizing datasets from Carter et al. [15] and the ITC 2007
benchmarks (McCollum [8])
More advanced techniques, such as the Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) commonly
credited to Ropke and Pisinger [19], have been applied in combination with a Mixed Integer Pro­
gramming (MIP) model. Sørensen and Stidsen [20] and Sørensen and Dahms [21] employed ALNS
alongside a commercial solver, Gurobi, to enhance solution quality.
Exact methods have also been used. For example, Qu and Burke [22] applied integer programming
with decomposition techniques for examination timetabling. Similarly, Martin [23] used a brute­
force integer programming approach with CPLEX to solve the University Course Timetabling
problem at Ohio University’s College of Business. And as mentioned, from ITC2019 Graph­based
MIP­formulation (Holm, Stidsen [10])
In this thesis we work with a version of the High School timetabling problem described by Pillay
[11], but where we consider it a hard constraint for schools not to have idle (or free­) periods. This
is what Pillay refers to as a compact timetable. The reason for this is that for Primary Schools in
Denmark, it is not considered a feasible schedule if classes have idle period This is because we
are working with timetabling for primary schools in Denmark, and it is not considered a feasible
schedule to have idle periods.
The most comparable methods to those in this thesis are to some degree the Mixed Integer Program­
ming models used by Sørensen and Stidsen [20] and Holm et al. [5], which incorporate innovative
variations to optimize solutions, but mostly we will be implementing metaheuristic methods in
form of Simulated Annealing and ALNS with similar methods as Carlsson et al. [24], Battistuta et
al. [25], and Ropke and Pisinger [19].
The above listing of different solutions used in education timetabling solely serves as examples of
used methods within educational timetabling, but cannot serve as a comprehensive list of all meth­
ods used. Kristiansen and Stidsen (2013) [6] include a more detailed description of the different
methodologies and implementations.

1.2
Timetabling in Danish primary schools
In Denmark, the most common product used for scheduling in primary schools is called Tabulex,
which was developed in 1991 as part of a master thesis at DTU and later commercialized in 1992.
The process of producing the schedule using Tabulex can roughly be split into four stages: Data,
Preprocessing, Solving and Postprocessing. The process can be seen in figure 1.1. This process
is based on the feedback from the scheduler from the collaborating firm Subit (This company will
be introduced in Chapter 2), the scheduler at one of the participating schools, and from the master
thesis by Hintze and Dybkjær [26].
Figure 1.1: The process of constructing a schedule using Tabulex
Data
In the data stage, the school administration decides which teachers should teach which classes in
the different subjects, and which/if new classes should be made. Meaning that matchings between
teachers, classes, and subjects must be completed. For most schools, this is a relatively easy task,
since they either already have a structure for when classes should switch teachers, or teachers keep
their classes for the next school year. This part of the scheduling is still preferred to be completed
by hand. Student sectioning into classes is normally also pretty simple and rarely re­evaluated.
Especially for younger classes.
Matching of teacher­class­subject is what schools refer to as ”skema­brikker”, which we will refer
to as ”Lessons”. Lessons are often stored in an HR support program such as Tabulex Trio, which
can directly transfer them to Tabulex.
Once all the lessons are in Tabulex, an initial cleaning of the data is conducted, this could be
correcting typos in the data which Tabulex would interpret as errors, or having scheduled to many
lessons for a class, which would make it impossible to make a feasible schedule.
Preprocessing
In the preprocessing stage, the school scheduler defines the individual rules for each class, teacher,
and room. Starting with the classes, the scheduler must define how each lesson should be ”split”.
The meaning of ”splitting” a lesson is deciding how to distribute the lesson throughout the week,
since some must occur several times. An example could be 7A having the subject ”ENG” (English)
5 times a week. Here, the scheduler must specify whether this lesson should occur once as a double
period and once as a triple period, or as two double periods and one single period, or any other
combination. It is not possible to let Tabulex decide this by itself.
The scheduler can also specify the frame for each class, teacher, and room. This can be seen in
figure 1.2. In this figure, we see the schedule frame for a teacher. This layout is identical for
classes and rooms. The scheduler has the option of placing one of four symbols in each time slot;

Restrictions on the subjects are also possible to define. This could be restricting the repetition of a
subject on the same day without them occurring consecutively. It could also be restricting subjects
of the same group from following each other. E.g. language classes such as ”DAN” (Danish),
”ENG” (English), ”TYS” (German), and ”FRA” (French) should not follow each other.
Lastly, it is also possible for the scheduler to define their own rules. The scheduler we talked to
defined his own rule, blocking all double periods from spanning a break. However, it was not
possible for him to define a rule allowing a teacher to have a weekly day off, without specifying
which day it should be.
Once all the rules are defined, Tabulex is ready to produce a schedule. However, the scheduler can
and should set the priorities of each type of rule. This functionality is illustrated in Figure 1.3. The
figure shows rules for classes, teachers, rooms, and subjects, along with a value and red/blue bar
indicating the extent to which each rule is violated. To the left of each rule is a slider, that allows
the scheduler to adjust the priority of the rule: moving the slider to the far left strongly prioritizes
the rule, while having it to the far right assigns it the lowest possible priority.
Once the priorities are set, Tabulex can begin generating the school schedule. Figure 1.3 shows
the rules, and how much they are violated, grouped by classes, subjects, staff/teachers, and rooms.
For classes, the only violated rule is idle periods, with a total of 4 idle periods. For subjects we see
that subject repetition is violated 3 times and that some standard rules are violated twice.
For teachers, there are a total of 43 idle periods. This is influenced by the rule having the the second
to least priority, indicated by the blue bar, and the slider being almost to the far right. Additionally,
teachers are scheduled outside of their frame a total of 3 times and are double booked a total of 3
times. Finally, we note that no rooms have been overbooked.

Solving
In the solving process, all the work is done by Tabulex. While a schedule is being produced,
the aforementioned value of each rule is shown, such that the scheduler can see how good the
current schedule is. Tabulex continues to produce a schedule until the scheduler actively stops the
process. When stopped, Tabulex may have produced a schedule in which some of the rules are
violated, meaning that the schedule is not feasible, as seen in Figure 1.3. The reason for this could
be that Tabulex uses the Simulated Annealing (SA) metaheuristics as described by Dige et al [27],
where the rules are not implemented as strict rules, but as desirable rules not to violate.
Instead of considering rules as hard requirements, Tabulex assigns a certain penalty to each rule,
hoping that the penalty for breaking the rules is high enough to ensure that it is never broken. This
is, as mentioned, not always the case.
Postprocessing
Once the scheduler terminates Tabulex he/she can see how much each rule is violated. However,
Tabulex does not tell where the different violations are. They must be found manually by going
through the entire schedule. Once the violations have been found, the scheduler must manually
find a suitable solution, by moving, locking, or freeing some lessons, before letting Tabulex try
again. An example of this is seen in figure 1.4. Here, the red highlighting of the lessons does
not mean that they violate the constraints but that they are identical to the blue highlighted lesson
on Wednesday morning. The violation is represented by a red box around the teacher’s initials in

scheduler could try to find a new location for the class, by manually moving lessons, or let Tabulex
run again.

The postprocessing stage either terminates when the scheduler has deemed the current schedule to
be of sufficient quality, or by going back to the preprocessing stage and applying changes to the
rules, priorities, etc.
Although Tabulex helps a lot in terms of producing the schedule for a school. Decisions like,
how to split lessons, which days teachers should have a day off, and others, need to be made in
advance, resulting in possible sub­optimal solutions. This thesis will accommodate these decisions
by making a framework capable of make them instead of the school scheduler. Furthermore, a
scheduler we talked to described Tabulex as an excellent tool to digitally visualize timetables for
him to build multiple schedules simultaneously, but that it is terrible at actually automatically
scheduling timetables for the school, which it pretends to be able to. This is the problem that we are
looking to fix in this thesis. Assuming all inputs are adequate, we want to be able to automatically
plan customized schedules for schools.

Data
In this chapter we will begin with a brief introduction to our thesis collaboration company Subit,
which supplied the data on all schools. We will then provide an overview of the data and how we
cleaned it. Following this, we present our formulation of a Lesson and finally, explain how two
instances are constructed for each school.
Subit is a company that helps primary schools in Denmark manage workload through time regis­
tration. They provide each school with a simple and easy­to­use interface that gives the schools
the ability to see and track this for teachers and substitutes individually, with the goal of mini­
mizing excessive expenses. Subit is interested in exploring the possibility of offering automatic
timetabling for its growing municipal customer base. Our thesis can be seen as a proof­of­concept
of that. We will therefore take into account the practical applicability when analyzing the results.
The data received from Subit consists of the current weekly schedules for 8 schools of different
sizes. Spanning from the smallest school with 11 classes and 22 teachers, to the largest with 38
classes and 92 teachers. The school names have been replaced with random colors in this thesis
for anonymity.
The data for all schools follows the same format and has the following attributes. Also, an example
of a raw CSV file with data for the Red school is seen in Figure 2.1
• School: The name of the school considered.
• Initials: The initials of the teacher in the given lessons. This is a unique identifier for each
teacher.
• Starts_at: The date and time a given lesson starts.
• Ends_at: The date and time the given lesson ends.
• Subject: The subject taught in the given lesson.
• Class: The class in the given lesson. This is a unique identifier for each class.

3
MIP model
In this chapter, we present the mathematical Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) models that will
be our first model to produce timetables for primary schools. Section 3.1 introduces the basic MIP
model used to ensure adherence to the most basic schedule requirements. For each of these require­
ments, the variables, parameters, and constraints used in the basic MIP model will be presented.
Section 3.2 introduces the framework with 10 sub­objectives, converting the basic model into the
extended MIP model, to produce high­quality timetables. These sub­objectives will be presented
in the same step­by­step style as the requirements in the basic MIP model. The models presented
are inspired by Hintze and Dybkjær [26].
3.1
Basic model
In this section, we present our Basic Model. This is the simplest model with the only goal of
producing ”feasible” schedules. For a schedule to be feasible, some basic requirements must be
satisfied. Those requirements are the constraints of the Basic model.
Constraints can be either hard (H) or soft (S). Hard constraints are strict requirements ­ if any
are violated, the resulting solutions are not feasible. Soft constraints are requirements for which
violation does not affect feasibility, but fewer violations result in better solutions. Therefore, soft
constraints are handled in the objective function.
The Basic model’s constraints are:
1: All lessons must be placed exactly the right amount of times (H)
2: A class can not have idle periods (H)
3: A class can only have one lesson at a time (H)
4: A teacher can only have one lesson at a time (H)
5: A room must not exceed its capacity (H)
6: A class can only have lessons inside its frame (H)∗
7: A teacher can only have lessons inside their frame (H)∗
8: Locked lessons can not be moved (H)∗
9: Lessons can not be placed in time slots that are marked unavailable (H)∗
Constraints marked with ∗are only active for real­life instances. Constraints 1 and 2 are presented
here as hard constraints as they are essential for any timetable to be feasible, but in practice, they
will be implemented as soft constraints. The reason for this is, as explained in Chapter 2, that we
define lessons directly from the school’s current schedules ­ meaning it may be impossible to fully
comply with our definition of feasibility. Thus, handling constraints 1 and 2 as soft constraints
gives us a ”distance to feasibility” measure which we found very useful. We will get back to this
in the Extended MIP in Section 3.2.

Extensions
The Basic model is the bare minimum. It has the sole purpose of generating feasible timetables
regardless of ”quality”. In this section, we extend the Basic model with 10 objectives, based on
timetable qualities ­ resulting in an Extended MIP model. Each sub­objective will have a scaling,
providing the schools with the possibility to change the scalings according to what they prioritize
in an ideal timetable.
The 10 sub­objectives presented are the result of discussions with Subit and a scheduler from one
of the schools, and represent a set of timetable qualities relevant across most schools.
In general the objective function of the Extended model will be a weighted sum seen in Equa­

schedule quality is therefore having consecutive identical lessons. That leads us to introducing two
complimenting sub­objectives ­ Lesson started and Double lessons.

the same day. By starting a lesson we mean placing a lesson occurences such that it is not adjacent
to an identical lesson occurences. Double lesson focuses on maximizing the number of times a
lesson is placed in two consecutive time slots.
The two sub­objectives compliment each other in term of the possibility of having multiple con­

Metaheuristics
Metaheuristics are used to find the best possible solution within a given time frame. The trade­off
is that, unlike exact methods, they do not guarantee optimal solutions, even with unlimited time.
This is especially relevant in our case, as optimality is not our primary concern. Timetable quality
is ultimately subjective, so achieving good results quickly at the expense of optimality is highly
appealing for this project
In this chapter, we will present our metaheuristic methods and elaborate on how they are imple­
mented. First Simulated annealing in Section 5.1, then Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search in
Section 5.2. For the implementation of both heuristics, there are essential topics to be covered.
In Section 5.3 we elaborate on solution representation and describe the Delta Evaluation which is
vital to the performance of the metaheuristic methods.
5.1
Simulated Annealing
Simulated Annealing is one of the oldest and simplest metaheuristics, and in its simplest form,
is easy to implement for most problems. For an up­to­date exhaustive introduction to Simulated
Annealing and its variants we refer to the work of Franzin and Stützle [29]. Originally, the method
is credited to Kirkpatrick, Gelett, and Vecchi (Kirkpatrick et al. 1983) [30] and is a local search
method based on a probabilistic (usually) acceptance criterion for non­improving moves. Origi­
nally the name Annealing comes from the fusing of metals, where the precise and gradually de­
creasing temperature is critical for constructing the strongest metals. This analogy can be seen as
quite a reach, but temperature is the most important factor in all simulated annealing algorithms.
How the temperature is adjusted can vary a great deal. In Algorithm 1 a general form of the Sim­

Implemented SA Algorithm
For the stopping criterion, we use a time limit, as is standard for metaheuristics. The limiting
factor in finding a solution is almost always time, making it the most practical constraint. We have
chosen a standard time limit of 5 minutes for this project, as this provides a reasonable waiting
period — perhaps enough for a short coffee break. This decision aligns with the experience of
schedulers at schools, who used a similar time frame when waiting for Tabulex to generate its
schedule suggestions. (Although, as mentioned earlier, Tabulex does not produce good schedules.)
An alternative stopping criterion could have been maximum number of iterations, which would
provide a direct input for calculating ns in Equation 5.1. However, in our implementation, the total
number of iterations I will be estimated from an initial run of a simplified version of Simulated
Annealing (SA). This initial run starts from an empty solution and performs many random insertion
attempts. The resulting solution then serves as our initial solution, effectively combining iteration
estimation with a construction heuristic, leading to a fast SA initialization that proves to be a decent
starting point.
This leads us to Neighbourhood. A typical neighbourhood relation in timetabling is swapping two
Lessons in a schedule with each other. We call this neighbourhood SimpleSwap:
SimpleSwap Given two lessons; l1 at position [period, day] = [p1, d1] and l2 at [p2, d2],

⟨
l1, l2, [p1, p1], [p2, d2]

Destroys and Repairs
In this section, we briefly describe the different destroy and repair methods used for the ALNS.
We have a total of 11 destroy functions and 6 repairs. Destroy and repair methods are always very
problem­specific.
First, we have the following three all­purpose destroy functions:
d1 Destroy_all_of_Lesson(x, Π, dp) :
All lessons L from instance Π are drawn at random. For each of them, all occurrences in
solution x are removed with probability dp.
d2 Destroy_one_of_Lesson(x, Π, dp) :
All lessons L from instance Π are drawn at random. For all drawn lessons one random
occurrence is removed with probability dp.
d3 Destroy_Random(x, Π, dp) :
All lesson occurrences from instance Π are removed from schedule x with probability dp.
Secondly, we have object­specific destructors. Each of these destructors target a specific sub­
objective. The idea is that targeting destruction could benefit the sub­objective in the subsequent re­
pair. For objectives to be minimized, the destructors remove penalizing lessons. For sub­objectives
to be maximized, they attempt to make room for benefitting lessons, by removing others. We have
8 such destroy methods, for the following sub­objectives:
• Theme
• Lesson started
• Uniform Days
• Idle Classes
• Idle Teacher
• Preferred Lesson
• Teacher Work Days
• Break Lessons
We will not elaborate on all object­specific destroy methods here, but to demonstrate the idea we
have the following two object­specific destroy methods:
d4 Destroy_lesson_started(x,Π,dp) :
Each single (non­consecutive) period in solution x from instance Π is removed with proba­
bility dp.
d5 Destroy_uniform(x,Π,dp) :
All lesson occurrences in solution x from instance Π, which are scheduled after a class’
perfect uniform day length, are removed with probability dp.
For repair methods we have a mix of fast insertions and different greedy methods. The fast methods
are:
c1 Fast_insert(x, Π) :
All lessons L from instance Π that are not fully scheduled in solution x, are shuffled. For
each, all available time slots are randomly considered, and the lesson is inserted into the first
improving time slot.
c2 Fast_insert_or_swap(x, Π) :
Similar to c1 but additionally checks occupied time slots, and swaps if it is an improvement.
As for the more greedy methods we have:
Primary School Timetabling

==PAGE:57==
c3 Greedy_Insert_1 (x,Π) :
All lessons from instance Π that are not fully scheduled in solution x, are randomly shuffled.
For each, we consider all time slots and insert the lessons in the best improving time slot.
c4 Greedy_insert_2 (x, Π) :
All available time slots in solution x are randomly shuffled. For each, we consider all non­
fully placed lessons from instance Π and insert the best improving lesson.
c5 Big_Lesson_first(x,Π) :
Same as c3, but instead of randomly shuffling lessons, they are sorted by size in decreasing

c6 Super_Greedy(x, Π) :
All lessons from instance Π that are not fully scheduled in solution x. All lessons are con­
sidered in all time slots, but only the best improving lesson occurrence is inserted. This is
repeated until no improving insertions exist.
Note firstly, that c3 and c4 are similar but opposite. c3 matches lessons to best time slot, and c4
matches time slots to best lesson. Additionally, c6 is the slowest repair method but has the greatest
potential. This method is the main reason for the rewards being runtime adjusted.

Delta Evaluation
The delta evaluation is crucial for the performance of the metaheuristics. For large structured solu­
tion representations like our, it would be very time­consuming to calculate the objective value from
scratch each time. When operations are performed to modify the solution schedule, we know that
only the schedules that a lesson belongs to are affected. Thus, only these schedules’ contribution
to the objective value is altered. We can take advantage of this by utilizing delta evaluation.
We use three fundamental procedures as the only operations to modify the timetables. These are:
i check_insert(l,p,d) : Checks if lesson l can be placed in time slot (p, d).
ii insert_lesson(l,p,d) : Inserts lesson l in time slot (p, d). Updates all helper structures
and affected sub­objectives by delta evaluation. Ultimately, updating the overall objective
value.
iii remove_lesson(l,p,d) : Removes lesson l from time slot (p, d). Updates all helper
structures and affected sub­objectives by delta evaluation. Ultimately, updating the overall
objective value.
Note that insert_lesson must be preceded by check_insert , as it does not do any checks.
All delta evaluation is handled in insert_lesson and remove_lesson by updating every
relevant solution representation structure.
We can now describe the basics of how the delta evaluation works. Although the premise is quite
simple, it allows for enormous speed­up of iterations, leading to the metaheuristic being able to
maneuver through a significantly larger portion of the solution space. Given the complexity of the
problem and the multitude of sub­objectives, this section provides only an introduction to how the
delta evaluation works. For a more in­depth explanation, we refer to Section A.5 in the Appendix.
In the following, we briefly go through how each sub­objective is updated through delta evaluation.

Missing Lessons
Starting with the most simple sub­objective, the missing lessons sub­objective keeps track of how
many lesson occurrences are still missing from the schedule. If a lesson is removed from the
schedule, the missing lesson sub­objective is increased by 1, and oppositely, if a lesson is inserted
into a schedule, the sub­objective is decreased by 1.
Idle Periods for class and teacher
An idle period is an empty period (ID = 0), inside the class frame, that has a non­empty period
later the same day. Although classes and teachers have separate sub­objectives for idle periods,
the delta evaluation for them is identical and will therefore be presented as one.
Figure 5.5 shows a made up schedule for a class/teacher. The red 0’s are time slots that are idle
and the black 0’s are empty time slots that are not idle.
The delta evaluation increases the number of idle periods whenever a lesson, placed before a non­
empty time slots, is removed (removing lesson 1 in period 3 on day 4). It will also increase the
number of idle periods whenever a lesson is placed such that it has empty periods earlier on the
same day (inserting a lesson in period 6 on day 5 will make period 1­5 idle periods). On the other
hand, the number of idle periods is decreased if a lesson is placed in an idle period (inserting a
lesson in period 2 on day 4), or if a lesson is removed from a schedule such that empty periods are
no longer after a non­empty periods (removing lesson 1 in period 6 on day 4 removes idle periods
in period 4 and 5 on the same day.).

Discussion
In this thesis, we explored different approaches aimed to automate parts of the scheduling process
to reduce the tremendous amount of manual effort needed to build optimal timetables, without
sacrificing quality. We introduced a framework consisting of 10 sub­objectives, reflecting the most
desirable qualities in timetables. These sub­objectives can be scaled to reflect different schools’
priorities.
All implemented methods contributed to a higher degree of automation when generating solutions
for schools. In the results, we see that when comparing to the school’s current schedules, we
always find improving schedules. However, the improvements are based on a comparison using
the synthetic priorities from Table 6.3. The current schedules are, in reality, not based on our
priorities, resulting in a heavy bias towards the generated timetables.
With the metaheuristic methods, we have achieved comparable results to the exact method of the
Extended MIP model while drastically reducing the time frame. With this, the automation of gen­
erating timetables through a trial­and­error process is now possible. Given adequate scheduling
inputs, this can lead to perfectly tailored schedules for most schools. This would not have been
possible without delta evaluation, as it resulted in a dramatic performance increase, allowing the
SA and ALNS to do 23 and 100 times more iterations respectively on average.
The presented priorities in Table 4.1 were chosen to simulate idle periods and missing lessons as
hard constraints, by always making a solution worse if violating them. In a few cases, the objective
value of infeasible solutions is better than those of the feasible ones, indicating a higher priority on
idle periods and missing lessons should be considered. This was tested in the preliminary trials but
yielded solutions of lower quality, than those presented in the results, without improving distance
to feasibility. This implies that beyond a certain point, increasing the penalty does not improve
the distance to feasibility ­ It will actually diminish the results quality, even if a feasible solution is
found, whereas the penalty does not contribute to the objective value. If no feasible solutions exist,
increasing the penalties obviously will not help, and on the other hand, if good solutions exist, they
become more difficult to find if the penalties are too high. This indicates that there exists a perfect
value for the penalties of ”hard” constraints. This is an opportunity for future work
The results from the 24­hour extended MIP model (Table 4.3), provided feasible solutions for all
open instances. Only for the Gray O and Gray R is it unable to find feasible solutions, even though
their current schedule is feasible. This result indicates that a size limit has been reached for utilizing
the extended MIP model with a 24­hour time frame. Further increasing the running time would
yield a feasible solution, but is undesirable as it solutions are to be provided quickly.
Considering the infeasible results from the metaheuristics, we see that the infeasibility is mainly
due to missing lessons. This indicates that removing a lesson is more beneficial than having an idle
period, probably due to the schedules gaining space for other sub­objectives. Checking the result­
ing timetables, we see that missing lessons are always big lessons (including 4+ classes/teachers),
and it turns out that SA is better at handling these, and reaching feasible results. Big lessons take
up the same time slots but across different schedules, making them difficult to place in partially
completed schedules. To accommodate for this, the neighborhood ForceSwap was introduced for
the SA. This neighborhood made room for the larger lessons when a swap was considered. For
the ALNS, a constructor prioritizing placing big lessons was introduced. However, the constructor
Primary School Timetabling
65
==PAGE:72==
requires the preceding destroy method to remove the exact lessons in the same time slots simulta­
neously. This is unlikely as the destroy percentage upper bound, as a result of hypertuning, is only
8%. This indicates that we could have reached better results with a destroy method targeting big
lessons. Nonetheless, ALNS was the better­performing metaheuristic, being more robust in terms
of mean value. ALNS is a more advanced method than SA, but is very dependent on the problem­
specific destructor­ and constructor methods. SA is simpler to apply, as a relatively simple swap
can lead to good results.
All raw data in this thesis is received as current timetables for the schools, from which we deduced
all needed information. This is a fundamental limitation but does not affect the validity of the
models and framework. If we, for example, have assumed that a maximum day length for class
7B is shorter (more limited) than it actually is, but we still manage to reach acceptable goals, only
underlines the effectiveness of the implemented methods and framework. Additionally, we used
even more limiting specifications of the school’s current schedules to create real­life instances.
This again, underlines the effectiveness of the framework and methods, if we ­ at a minimum ­
reach the solution of their current schedules. However, if their current schedule is infeasible (with
our definitions) the real­life instances most likely result in no feasible solutions existing.
If a school has a good scheduler, the real­life instances are so restricted that the current schedule
might somewhat close be an optimal. This might be the case for Yellow school The scheduler
from Yellow school, was coincidentally, the only scheduler that we talked to during the project. He
talked about having a specific order in constructing timetables, by always starting with the most
limiting special rooms. He would then consider big electable subjects where they for example have
a teacher in ”FRA” (French) that teaches in multiple schools, so was only available for them on
Wednesdays. He has created an excellent personal heuristic for creating feasible schedules. With
this specific instance, the inserted data is too restricted for our methods to find a feasible solution in
5 minutes. His heuristic is better than ours at handling this school, because of the many big lessons.
We could argue that a good scheduler like him would still benefit from using our framework, as he
might have automated more of the process.
The developed framework of using 10 sub­objectives is a balance of customizability and avoidance
of over­complication, to work for not all, but as many schools as possible. Through discussion with
Subit, we reached a set of the most important schedule qualities, but in general, timetable qualities
are subjective to each school. Some schedulers might find certain schedules impractical, while
others may consider them perfectly suitable.
From the presented results and analyses, it is evident that the framework works as an efficient and
objective way to produce timetables for primary schools. The limitation of not having a subjective
consideration of the produced timetables may impact the validity of the findings. The results still
constitute a solid foundation as proof of the potential of the framework.

Future work
Although this report has presented a comprehensive framework for producing schedules for pri­
mary schools, a lot of ideas have been left out. Each idea, and the logic behind, it will be presented
in each paragraph throughout this section.
The ALNS, as mentioned, has a construction method considering the size of the lessons. However,
as considered in the discussion, larger lessons are difficult to insert. We therefore suggest a destroy
method, which would consider the lessons not placed, and then remove the same time slot for all
the members in the respective missing lesson. This would ensure that there is room for a large
lesson
The sub­objective termed Teacher work day was introduced to account for the teachers’ workload
in terms of working days. However, the priority for this sub­objective was applied uniformly across
the entire teaching staff, making it impossible to assign greater importance to certain teachers over
others. For example, some teachers may work part­time. In this case, it should be possible to
prioritize the number of workdays specifically for them. This could be achieved by assigning
individual scalings to each teacher, ensuring that deviations from their desired number of working
days are penalized more heavily. Thereby reflecting their specific needs in the scheduling process.
At the moment, ensuring this is only possible if the teacher frame is modified accordingly, this
would however mean that working days for these teachers would be predefined, and thus result in
sub­optimal schedules.
Continuing with teachers, we also introduced the sub­objective termed idle teacher period, to ad­
dress the issue of teachers having empty periods between lessons. This sub­objective aimed to
minimize the number of idle periods for teachers. However, preferences regarding idle periods
vary significantly among teachers. Some teachers find it overwhelming to have tightly packed
schedules, while others feel bored when their lessons are spread sparsely throughout the day. To
accommodate these differing preferences, we propose that this sub­objective should also consider
individual teacher’s preferences. Implementing this would require splitting the idle teacher period
sub­objective into two separate components. One focuses on minimizing idle periods for teachers
who prefer compact schedules. The other is to maximize idle periods for those who prefer small
breaks during the day. At the moment the best way of accounting for this is to have a high priority
on the sub­objective teacher workday, setting the preferred working days, for the teachers how
like a tightly packed schedule to 0 or 1. This would help pack the working days for these teachers.
Lastly, the objective idle periods for teachers should also have a somewhat low to mid priority, to
ensure a proper distribution of idle periods among the teachers.
At the moment the sub­objective double lessons only considers lessons to be consecutive if they
have the same ID. However, there might be particular cases where the class has e.g. ”MAT” (math­
ematics) with the additional teacher in the first periods. This would not be counted as a double
lesson when in reality it is. To account for this, double lessons could instead consider either the
subject or teacher when deeming lessons to be consecutive. This would, however, introduce am­
biguities in when two or more periods should be considered double periods. To circumvent these
ambiguities one could split the sub­objective, such that there was one for double teacher periods
and another for double subject periods.
The presented schedules do not specify the exact room in which each lesson should take place, as
we did not have detailed room allocation information for each school. If such information were
to become available, it would be a natural extension to develop a secondary model or heuristic.
Primary School Timetabling
67
==PAGE:74==
This model or heuristic would take the produced schedule as input and assign rooms to the various
lessons accordingly.
The presented metaheuristics evaluate a schedule based solely on a single objective value. Requir­
ing the priorities for idle periods and missing lessons to be high enough, such that their violation
renders the solution worse than any other feasible option. However, this strict approach may not
always align with practical priorities. To address this, the metaheuristics could be adapted to work
with two objectives, a feasibility objective (distance to feasibility) and a score objective. During the
acceptance criteria, solutions with a better feasibility objective would always be accepted, regard­
less of their score objective. Additionally, solutions with worse score objective but equal feasibility
objective would be accepted with probability p, and solutions with worse feasibility objective, but
better score objective would be accepted with probability q. Otherwise, solutions will be disre­
garded. This approach ensures that feasible solutions are consistently prioritized over infeasible
ones thus allowing priorities for idle periods and missing lessons to be considered separately. It
is worth mentioning that this method draws significant inspiration from the works of Dige et al.
[27], and would probably have been implemented if this method had been introduced earlier in the
project.

Conclusion
By introducing a general model framework and implementing Mixed Integer Programming we
have achieved and improved upon best known feasible solutions. By then implementing meta­
heuristic methods, we have significantly reduced the timeframe and managed to maintain compa­
rable optimal solutions, by significantly improving computational efficiency through delta evalu­
ation. This addresses the main question of the thesis of automating the process of building good
timetables for Danish primary schools.
The results of this thesis contribute to the field of Timetabling by suggesting a practical trial­and­
error tool for building highly customizable schedules. The resulting methods can not only produce
schedules for schools while saving a tremendous amount of time and resources but also do it for a
wide range of schools based on their priorities on a relatively large set of objectives.
This is based on the quantitative results of being able to still reach acceptable and even good results
despite strict limitations. The limitations are partly intentional because the best quantitative basis of
comparison we have is to create scenarios, and asses results through confirmation of most instances
on the most difficult scenario possible.
Our defined mathematical framework is impacted by subjective scalings but returns a resulting
objective value that describes, explicitly, if one timetable is better than another. However, the
resulting schedules will always be extremely subjective. A school scheduler would never say that
a requirement was ”soft”. Instead, they would compromise each time they realized their idea was
unattainable. This is what makes qualitative results almost impossible to evaluate on a single
schedule or school basis. But as an interactive tool of trial­and­error, we can mimic the process
of finding the perfect set of compromises, resulting in the least un­optimal timetable for a Danish
primary school.
In the end, every final timetable for a school scheduler is a compromise ­ never a perfect solution.

In­depth view on Delta evaluation
An in depth explanation of how the delta function works for the different objectives will be given in
the following sections. The delta function will work differently depending on which fundamental
procedure (insert lesson or remove lesson) is done on the time table. Each of the section following
section will therefore consist of two subsection describing the delta function when inserting a lesson
and removing a lesson.
A.5.1
Missing lessons
The objective ”Missing Lessons” focuses on keeping track of how many lessons still needs to be
placed in the current time table.
Inserting a lesson
When inserting a lesson the total number of missing lessons will go down by 1
Removing a lesson
When removing a lesson the total number of missing lessons will go up by 1
A.5.2
Idle periods for classes/teachers
The objective ”Idle periods for classes” and ”Idle periods for teachers” work in the same way, the
just keep track of idle periods for classes and teachers respectively. The following section will
therefore explain how the delta objective works general, since it is the exact some procedures for
both classes and teachers.
These two objectives focuses on keeping track of how many idle periods a class/teacher has. A
period is considered idle when it is empty (id = 0), not marked as unavailable and has a period later
that day.
Figure A.1: An made up class/teacher schedule with 6 periods and 5 days.
The number in each time slot is the id for the corresponding lesson, time slots
with id 0 are empty periods. 0 are idle periods.
Inserting a lesson
When inserting a lesson the number of idle periods can either decrease, say the same or increase.
In the Figure A.1 below is shown a made up example of a schedule consisting of 4 different lessons
with id 1 to 4. 0 represent idle periods and 0 represent empty periods that are not idle. This schedule
will be used to represent how the delta evaluation works in different scenarios.
Inserting a lesson in period 4 on day 1 will remove the idle period making the total number of idle
periods go down by 1. The same will happen if a lesson is inserted in period 2, 4 or 5 on day 4.
Primary School Timetabling
80
==PAGE:87==
Inserting a lesson in any period between period 2 to 5 on day 5 will make the earlier periods idle,
since they now have a lesson later and they have to start in period 1. That is, if we insert a lesson
in period 3 on day 5, period 1 and 2 will become idle. If we insert a lesson in period 6, period 1­5
will become idle.
Removing a lesson
When removing a lesson the total number of idle periods can decrease, say the same or decrease.
To illustrate this Figure A.1 will be used again.
Removing any lesson that is not in the last period of the day will increase the number of idle periods
by 1. That is, removing the lesson in either period 5 on day 1 or period 6 on day 4 will convert the
above idle periods to empty periods, since these periods no longer has a lesson later the same day.
Removing the last period of the day will decrease the number of idle periods, since a new period
