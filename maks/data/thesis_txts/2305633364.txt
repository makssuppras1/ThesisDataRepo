Summary 
 
Local exhaust ventilation (LEV) is widely used in different workplaces in order to 
control harmful for human/product emissions of gas, vapor, dust, fume and mist in 
surrounding air. One of the most common types of LEV is the articulated extraction arm 
(AEA) which is placed close to the contaminant source to locally capture and exhaust 
the emitted pollutants at the spot of their release. AEAs are used today in many 
industries, i.e. pharmaceutical, food and beverage, machine, etc. While the information 
provided for application of AEA units for certain processes such as welding, grinding, 
etc. is quite broad, not sufficient theoretical and practical knowledge is available at 
present for their performance and application in light industries such as pharmaceutical, 
food and beverage etc., where the pollutants are isothermal and are not released at 
high velocities. Therefore, a thorough study is required for laboratory application of 
AEAs in order to solve some of the existing problems, i.e. needed extraction flows, 
capture velocities, impact of background room movement, etc. The most effective way 
to control the efficiency of the AEAs is attainable by specifying a certain value for the 
capture velocity (exhaust flow). The aim of the present study was to reveal missing 
knowledge on the performance of AEA.  
The thesis consists of two parts. In the first part the real application of the AEAs 
and the possible influencing factors upon the AEA’s efficiency were investigated through 
field survey performed in real laboratories. During this field survey physical 
measurements of background room air velocities and AEA flow rates were performed. 
Sets of questions were given to the laboratory staff members regarding the use of LEV 
systems, the working environment and the working process and on workers’ knowledge 
on how to use the AEA system. According to the measured air velocities in the 
laboratory rooms three values were remarkable, namely 0.15, 0.25 and 0.5 m/s. The 
highest one was measured under the supply diffusers of the background ventilation and 
it had a vertical direction. The AEAs’ flow rate in the different laboratories ranged 
roughly between 70-200 m3/h, and the AEA’s duct had a diameter of 12.5-16 cm. Most 
of the substances used with AEAs were isothermal, i.e. water based substances at 
room air temperature. The majority of the AEAs installed to extract the contaminants 
emitted from working processes which require personnel involvement were not used 
due to the distrust of the personnel in the efficiency of the AEA system. However, the 
personnel knew what the role of the AEA was and how it was working but not as good 
as for the fume cupboards. 
The obtained data from the field studies served as bases for the second part, 
which was a full-scale controlled experiment to measure the pollutant capturing 
efficiency of an AEA unit typically used in laboratories. The experiment was carried out 
in one of the field lab rooms at the International Center for Indoor Environment and 
Energy (ICIEE), Technical University of Denmark. A testing enclosure was built within

the field lab to reduce the unwanted airflows produced by the supply diffuser of the 
background total volume ventilation. In the enclosure a table with the AEA unit installed 
was used to mimic a typical laboratory workstation. Thermal manikin with realistic body 
shape and heat generated as of a real human was used to reproduce a seated 
laboratory worker. The source of pollution for which the AEA was used was mimicked 
by a test tube placed on a stand on top of the table. A so called “wind box” generator 
was used to produce uniform airflow (cross-draught) in the region of the source/ at the 
working zone to recreate background air movement at levels typical in laboratories. 
A typical AEA used in labs of duct diameter of 75 mm was used for the 
measurements having a circular hood with relatively narrow flange. The studied 
parameters that would affect the AEA’s performance to remove pollutants generated 
from an object placed below it (source), namely the capturing efficiency, were: direction 
of the cross-draught, velocities of the cross-draught, position of the AEA’s hood 
compared to the source, magnitude of the AEA’s flow rate, width of the AEA hood’s 
flange, presence of a human body as obstacle and buoyancy flow generator, vertical 
partitions’ distance from the working zone and distance of the AEA’s hood from the 
source. For the AEA three positions relative to the source were considered, namely 
“above”, “45o” and “sideways”. Furthermore, five AEA flow rates were selected for the 
measurement: 60, 87, 105, 134 and 151 m3/h. The passive pollution source was 
mimicked with tracer gas. The distance between the source and the AEA hood was set 
to15.5 cm. The distance was only changed in special cases. By the wind box 0.15, 0.25 
and 0.5 m/s cross-draught was produced horizontally (parallel to the table top) and 
vertically (from above). The capture efficiency of the AEA and the exposure of the 
seated worker to the emitted by the source pollutant were calculated based on 
measurements with tracer gas method. The tracer gas used was nitrous oxide (N2O) 
and was sampled in three points: in the AEA’s duct, at the breathing zone of the sitting 
person and 1 meter from the source at 1 m height from floor. The corresponding velocity 
in front of the hood was measured with low velocity thermal anemometer with omni-
directional velocity probes. Velocity profiles close to the AEA hood were fully 
characterized as well by detailed velocity measurements.  
The case when the hood was placed sideways to the source and close to the table 
top gave in average 18 % higher capture efficiency for horizontal cross-draughts and 
28% for the vertical one compared to the other two studied AEA positions with respect 
to the source. The magnitude of the cross-draught also had a significant impact on the 
capture efficiency which is well represented by the obtained capture velocities. When 
the cross-draught velocity was increased the capture efficiency drastically decreased 
and much higher exhaust (AEA) flow rate/capture velocity was required to overcome the 
capture efficiency decrease. The capture velocities considering the three studied AEA 
positions and both of the studied cross-draught directions were 0.126, 0.196, 0.262 and 
0.435 m/s corresponding to cross-draughts of 0, 0.15, 0.25 and 0.5 m/s respectively.

The distance between the source and the AEA hood had effect on the capture 
efficiency: the capture efficiency decreased from 100% to almost 0% when the distance 
was increased by 5 cm. The presence of thermal manikin mimicking a seated worker 
increased the capture efficiency for low (0.15 m/s) horizontal cross-draughts by 13 % 
while it decreased it by 14 % for vertical cross-draught. The exposure level to the 
emitted tracer gas was inversely proportional to the capture efficiency .For the studied 
system the concentration/exposure was negligible (2mg/m3) above 95% capture 
efficiency. Placing vertical partitions on the table on both sides of the source had almost 
no effect on or slightly worsened the capture efficiency when cross draught was from 
above. 
Additional experiments were done with a hood with wider flange. The results 
showed a much better performance with respect to capturing efficiency than the original 
hood with narrow flange giving 100% at a low exhaust flow rate of 87 m3/h when the the 
horizontal cross-draught was 0.5 m/s. 
Factors such as cross-draught (background ventilation level), distance between 
the source and the hood and AEA position affect the capture velocity and capture 
efficiency substantially. The study shows that reducing the background air movement in 
laboratory rooms as much as possible allow to improve the capture efficiency of AEA 
units for the same flow rates used today. Furthermore, the distance between the source 
and the AEA’s hood has to be indicated on the product or/and specify the capturing 
zone, i.e. the area where the capture efficiency is close to 100%. It is also 
recommended to place the AEA’s hood from the side and as close as possible to the 
table or to any surface on which the source is placed to increase the AEA’s capture 
efficiency.

1. Introduction 
 
Local exhaust ventilation (LEV) is widely used in different workplaces in order to 
control harmful for human/product emissions of gas, vapor, dust, fume and mist in the 
air. In other words, the LEV partly or totally removes the contaminant cloud (normally 
process generated) before it can reach the workplace environment and cause 
harm/damage to the occupants. This can be achievable, if the hood/nozzle or other 
capturing device is capable to create a sufficient flow which will capture and transport 
the contaminants into the hood or it keeps contaminants inside whether they are emitted 
within the hood. By proper LEVs usage, the occupational asthma and other lung 
diseases can be prevented [1]. These diseases are mostly developed due to the high 
volume of inhaled airborne contaminants (dust, fume) at work.  
In general, the requirement regarding the efficiency of LEV can be defined by the 
occupational exposure limit (OEL), i.e. the selected LEV system should be able to 
control specific harmful contaminants and keep their concentration under the value 
predefined by the OEL [2].  
 
OEL definition 
“OEL is the airborne concentration of a compound to which nearly all workers can be 
repeatedly be exposed 8 hours a day, 40 hours a week, without adverse effects [3].”

The OEL is further categorized into the so called occupational exposure bands (OEB). 
The number of bands depends very much on the field of usage, used chemicals and 
company internal rules but normally four to five bands are used [2]. Each band has a 
given lower and upper OEL value (in µg/m3) except the last one which has only an 
upper limitation. The inferior bands always indicate materials with low toxicity 
(sometimes negligible) with a high allowed exposure limit, while the higher bands 
indicates more toxic chemicals with a very low exposure limit. Just to illustrate the 
difference, in the first band the allowed exposure limit can be 10000 µg/m3, while in the 
last band <0.07 µg/m3 [3]. Each band has different requirement regarding the necessary 
LEV system which depends again very much on the company internal rules and the

The most common LEV classification is made based on the hood type. The three basic

While the main application area of the LEVs is still welding, grinding, etc. LEVs are also 
present in light industries such as laboratories. The most frequently used and most 
efficient hoods in laboratories are the enclosing hoods which completely or partially 
encloses the process or contaminant generation (e.g. fume cupboards). In this case the 
inward flowing air through the gap under the fume cupboard’s sash keeps the 
contaminant within the enclosure[5]. For safety reasons, the enclosing hood is preferred

wherever the process configuration/ operation allow. When this is not practicable or/and 
source types allows (low toxicity) other solutions can be used. The other main category 
is the external hoods, which are placed adjacent to the source without any enclosure. 
The usage of external hoods can significantly reduce the energy consumption of the 
system, which roughly means 90% saving compared to the enclosing hoods [6]. The 
one which is considered in this project is called capturing hood. It can be easily mixed 
up with the receiving hood as it can have similar shape and dimension.  However, the 
source type can differentiate these two types of external hood. When the contaminant is 
vapour, gas or fine particle and it is not emitted with high velocity the capturing hood 
can be used and the orientation of the hood is not critical. Otherwise, for contaminant 
with large particles and higher velocity the receiving hood is used and the hood should 
be located in the path of the emission [5]. As the capturing hood has to be placed close 
to the source of contamination to obtain an efficient solution, it is very common to mount 
it on a movable articulated arm called articulated extraction arm (AEA). An example of 
AEA in working position is shown in Figure 3. The AEA is normally limited to the first 
OEB which is prescribed for compounds that are not harmful and/or have low

2.1 Articulated extraction arm (AEA) system 
 
The AEA system is an engineering controlling system which reduces the exposure to 
airborne contaminants such as dust, mist fume, vapour and gas in workplaces. 
Normally, the system has the components presented in Figure 4 but some of these may 
be omitted in particular cases. It is a simple local air exhaust system, although each 
component has to be carefully selected to obtain an overall efficient system [1].

2.1.1 Hood 
 
For the laboratory processes the most common AEA hood is the capturing hood. In this 
case the contaminants are outside the capturing hood and the generated airflow draw 
the contaminants in. It can have any shape according to the required process, but the 
most common shapes for laboratories are presented in Figure 5. The flange is 
necessary since it creates a larger capturing zone in front of the hood although it 
restricts the air capture from behind [1]. The flange size can change from few 
millimeters to over half a meter in diameter, yet it can be efficient if the width is at least 
square root of the opening area [5].

2.1.2 Ductwork 
 
The ductwork can be divided into the following different sections/components 
(depending on design and use some of these may be missing): ducting from the hood 
and between the components, dampers (to adjust the airflow), bends and junction, duct 
transitions, test points, connection to air cleaner and air mover (fan) and ducting to the 
discharge locations [1].  
Obviously, the pressure before the air mover (to the hood) is negative and positive 
afterward. Therefore, it is important to place the air mover/fan close to the discharge to 
prevent the backflow of the contaminant to the workplace in case of duct damage or 
incorrect fitting. 
Flexible ducting has high flow resistance and low resilience, which is a common 
problem in case of the articulated extraction arms. Therefore, to keep the flexible duct 
relatively straight, arms like supports are used on which the flexible duct is put on. Or to 
keep the length of these sections on minimum, rigid sections can be added between the 
flexible joints. These flexible ducts are also easily damaged which is another reason for 
the limited usage. 
The optimal air velocity has to be defined to keep the particles suspended in the 
air. The minimum duct velocities which can keep the particles suspended in the air 
stream are listed in Table 1. For laboratories, the most probable category is the first one 
(gases and non-condensing vapors) and in some extent the second one (Condensing 
vapours, fume and smoke) [1].

2.1.3 Air mover/fan 
 
The fan is the only element which powers the extraction system. The fan is selected 
according to the accumulated pressure losses. Each element has its own pressure loss 
and these are added by using either the ‘American´ or the ´British´ method. In case of 
“American” method, the bends and fittings are treated as pressure losses equivalent to 
certain lengths of straight ductwork. In case of “British” method each component’s 
pressure loss is added and the calculation is made on a stated volume flow rate. Finally, 
the selected fan has to overcome the airflow resistance of each element. From the 5 
general categories (propeller, axial, centrifugal, turbo exhauster and compressed-air-
driven) the centrifugal fan is the most suitable for the AEA system’s requirements. It can 
cover large pressure differences and large airflow, which can overcome considerable 
resistances [1]. 
 
2.1.4 Air cleaner 
 
Considering the laboratories, the extracted air by the AEAs does not require any 
cleaning as the toxicity level or the dosage is very small. If any of these two is higher, 
then a semi-closed/closed system is required, i.e. fume cupboards. Therefore, air 
cleaners are not examined in the current project [1]. 
 
2.1.5 Discharge 
 
The contaminated air must leave the discharge at high speed to make sure it is 
dispersed. Furthermore, the position of the discharge should be selected such that the 
extracted air is not allowed to reenter the building (unless adequate dilution) [1].

2.2.3 Capture velocity 
 
“The minimum hood induced air velocity necessary to capture and convey the 
contaminant into the hood is referred to as capture velocity. This velocity will be a result 
of the hood air flow rate and hood configuration [5].”  
It is important to complete the definition with another property of the capture velocity. 
The external hood has to extract the contaminant directly from the source in order to 
prevent any human exposure, which is normally the shortest path. In this way, the 
exposure only occurs if the worker is wrongly positioned relative to the source and local 
extraction (in the way of contaminant extraction).

2.3 Regulation/legislation 
 
The current legislation/regulation for AEAs is very sparse worldwide, especially in 
smaller countries like Denmark. The single currently available Danish standard [11] for 
AEAs in laboratories is completely based on the German standard [12] as it exclusively 
refers to this standard. According to the prior actions, the Danish legislation tends to 
follow the German one. It is important to examine the English legislation [1] as well 
which has significant impact on the topic of LEVs. Each standard/guidance defines in a 
detailed way the requirements for fume cupboards and similar closed equipment while it 
mentions very generally and shortly the other systems like capturing hoods.  
 
2.3.1 Testing requirement 
 
In order to find out more about the Danish legislation and especially about the 
testing of AEAs the “Arbejdstilsynet” (Danish Working Environment Authority) was 
contacted. One of the executive order provided by “Arbejdstilsynet” is the “Executive 
order on the conditions at Permanent Places of work”-chapter 8 [13]. This describes the 
requirement regarding the general ventilation which is partly related to the current topic. 
The very general requirement summarized is the following: if the working process 
generates gases, dust or similar that are hazardous to health or explosive and cannot 
be avoided, mechanical extraction and fresh replacement air is needed. If the pollution 
cannot be removed effectively from the place of development, the working process shall

capture velocity) has to be reached in the furthest point of the contaminant generation.

2.4 Review of the research on local exhaust hood 
 
In a study performed by the Japan National Institute of Occupational Safety and Health 
[18] the effect of the cross-draught’s direction on the capture efficiency of an exterior 
hood positioned horizontally beside a table was studied in a set of tracer gas 
experiments. The tested cross-draught directions are specified in Figure 11. A cross-
draught of 0.3 m/s normal to exterior hood centerline and coming from the worker’s 
back (right “blower” in Figure 11) did not have influence on the hood capture efficiency 
(>90% in each case) when the capture velocity was 0.2 m/s regardless of the position of 
the hood. The cross-draught had an influence only when it was coming at the angle of 
135o relative to the hood centerline (left “blower” in Figure 11). In this case the capture 
velocity was increased from 0.2 to 0.4 m/s to reach capture efficiencies higher than 
90%.  
 
Figure 11 Location of the cross-draught blower 
  
Remark: The capture efficiency should have been tested with lower capture velocities 
than 0.2 m/s, then the cross-draught would have influenced in the other positions/angles 
as well. The tracer gas was monitored only in the AEA duct. However adding more 
sampling points in the occupied zone (close to the occupant) could give better view of 
the AEA’s capture efficiency. The 90% efficiency may be less than the requirement in 
some application areas. The position of the hood would also have an impact on the 
result which was not investigated in this study. 
 
A laboratory study on capture efficiency of several “Alsident” products was made by The 
Danish Technological Institute (DTI) [19]. The capture efficiency was measured in 
different cases, when the following parameters were changed: hood type, hood position, 
==PAGE:26==
Technical University of Denmark 
2016

distance between the source and the hood, exhaust flow rates. A capture efficiency of 
94-98 % was reached with 60 m3/h exhaust flow rate (AEA’s duct diameter was 50 mm) 
at a distance of 200 mm. Even higher efficiency was reached with higher flow rates 
such as 100, 140 and 300 m3/h (AEA’s duct diameter was 63 to 100 mm) while the 
increased distance (350 mm) decreased the capture efficiency to a range of 64-89%.  
 
Remark: The background cross-draught produced by the background ventilation, from 
opening and closing doors/windows and from people walking by etc. was not 
considered in this study. Although, this may have a significant impact on the capture 
efficiency especially at these studied distances (≥200 mm).  
 
In another study made by Danish Technological Institute (DTI) [20] the capture 
efficiency related to hair treatment processes was measured with tracer gas (SF6) 
method. The hood was placed above a sitting person (manikin) at 0, 200 and 300 mm 
with exhaust flow rate of 62 m3/h when the air velocity in the testing room without the 
extraction arm was 0.06 m/s (without defined direction). For the exhaust hood placed at 
0 mm the reached capture efficiency was 92-97 % while 200 mm gave 66% and 300 
mm gave 60% capture efficiency. 
 
Remark: Again the relatively low and uncontrolled cross-draught may have resulted in 
the higher efficiency value compared to the real situation, since a higher cross draught 
than 0.06 m/s might occur easily in hair salons.  
 
In a previous study three tracer gases i.e. sulphur hexafluoride, nitrous oxide and 
helium were compared for determining the capture efficiency by Institute of 
Occupational Health Vantaa, Finland [21]. The capture velocity at the point of emission 
ranged from 0.2 to 0.5 m/s and a controlled cross-draught of 0.7m/s perpendicular to 
the hood axis was generated in some cases. The heaviest tracer gas (sulphur 
hexafluoride) gave up to 10-15% lower capture efficiency than the other gases when the 
cross draft was present. The lighter tracer gases (nitrous oxide and helium) reached 
close to 100% capture efficiency with 0.5 m/s capture velocity under 0.7 m/s cross-
draught. For the lower uncontrolled cross draft of 0.1-0.2 m/s (naturally found in the test 
space) the lighter gases had close to 100 % capture efficiency on the whole capture 
velocity range while heavier tracer gas had approx. 10% lower capture efficiency around 
0.2 m/s capture velocity and increased  to 100 % within 0.5 m/s capture velocity.  
 
Remark: The cross-draught of 0.7 m/s is very high and hardly occurs in the indoor 
environment. Therefore some other lower velocities would have been more adequate. 
Furthermore, the naturally found velocity of 0.1-0.2 m/s in the test space was also high 
(affected the results).

3. Justification of present study

3. Justification of present study 
 
AEAs are used today in many industries, i.e. pharmaceutical, food and beverage, 
machine, etc. While the information provided for application of AEA units for certain 
processes such as welding, grinding, etc. is quite broad  not sufficient theoretical and 
practical knowledge is available at present for their performance and application in light 
industries such as pharmaceutical, food and beverage etc., where the pollutants are 
isothermal and are not released at high velocities. Therefore, a thorough study is 
required for laboratory application of AEAs in order to solve some of the existing 
problems, i.e. needed extraction flows, capture velocities, impact of background room 
movement, 
etc. 
Since 
normally 
the 
working 
processes 
and 
the 
used 
substances/chemicals in laboratories are not made public, a field survey would be 
needed. Based on the field survey the real use of the AEA units and other factors which 
may influence the AEA’s performance could be identified.  
The most effective way to control the efficiency of the AEAs is attainable by 
specifying a certain value for the capture velocity (exhaust flow). So far this value has 
not been studied for laboratories. This makes it very difficult to design an effective but 
still economical AEA system for laboratories. It would be important to study AEA’s 
performance for the factors already defined in the available literature but with conditions 
for laboratories. The important factors which need to be studied are: velocities of the 
cross-draught, direction of the cross-draught, position of the AEA’s hood compared to 
the source, magnitude of the AEA’s flow rate and distance of the AEA’s hood from the 
source. Since the supply diffusers in laboratories are working with high flow rates the 
most important factor is the disturbing background airflows which has not been 
examined thoroughly. Therefore, it is necessary to study the capture efficiency and 
capture velocity under different cross-draughts with velocities as they occur in reality. 
Another factor which could be important regarding AEA’s performance but it was not 
studied enough is the human body as an obstacle and buoyancy flow generator. 
Furthermore, in cases when the reduction of the background air movement is not 
possible a simple solution to protect the source of contaminant is necessary. For this 
reason, the wider flanges and partitions placed around the working zone needs to be 
tested as well.  
The main purpose of the AEA use is to control the harmful for human emissions of 
gas, vapor, dust fume and mist in the surrounding air. Therefore, studying exposure of a 
working person to the harmful for human emissions around the AEA unit is also 
important topic. It could show how the exposure changes as a function of capture 
efficiencies.

5. Field survey 
5.1 Specific objectives 
 
The specific objectives are: 
1. Determine the flow rate range used for AEAs in laboratories; 
2. Study the air movement in the laboratory rooms and determine characteristic air 
velocity levels;  
3. Determine a characteristic air temperature value for laboratories; 
4. Examine the workers’ knowledge of AEA systems based on questionnaires; 
5. Inspect the working environment and note any factor which can influence the 
performance of the AEAs; 
6. Study the working process and the position of the AEAs during usage; 
7. Study type of the pollution source and determine the boundary conditions for the 
laboratory measurement.

It was difficult to find any place where AEA usage required people involvement. 
Furthermore, as it turned out later the AEAs designed for people involvement were not 
used in most of the cases.

AEAs had lower flow rate when those were used for repeated processes and the hoods 
could be placed relatively close to the source. Considering the diameter of the AEA’s

Most of the substances used under the AEAs or without any LEV system (most of

5.4 Conclusion 
 
The acquired information during the field surveys substantially differ from the 
expected one. While fume cupboards were used in case of toxic, irritating and 
unpleasant substances, AEAs were used only in case of water based isothermal 
substances. Therefore, the most proper source of contaminant for the full-scale 
experiment is an isothermal source which mostly mimics the behavior of the volatile 
compounds.  
Since the AEAs in laboratories were normally used to extract the emission from lab 
devices and other equipment, the presence of human body (manikin) during the full-
scale experiments need to be tested separately and not in each cases. 
The personnel distrust in the efficiency of the AEAs was the main reason for 
choosing fume cupboards or simply none of the LEV systems instead AEAs. Showing 
the real capture efficiency based on the full-scale experiment may change the 
personnel’s opinion.  
The exhaust flow rates of the AEA may differ for the full-scale experiment from the 
measured ones (80-200 m3/h) if the AEA’s duct diameter is different. Furthermore, the 
selected flow rates for the full-scale measurement should satisfy at least the lowest 
category of the required minimum duct velocities (section 2.1.2 Ductwork, Table 1).  
The vertical air velocity in some regions of the laboratory was quite high, produced 
by the supply diffusers of the background ventilation installed on the ceiling. The highest 
velocity measured during the field surveys was roughly 0.5 m/s which have to be tested 
during the full scale experiment. Two more velocities can be tested, one close to the 
measured 0.12 m/s (minimum value) and another which includes the other unwanted air 
movements in the room besides the background ventilation’s disturbance.  
In most of the cases the AEA was used by placing the AEA’s hood above the 
source, however it was not very effective and disturbed the working process. Therefore, 
some other AEA positions should also be considered during the full scale experiment. 
From the two working positions, the standing is more disadvantageous regarding the 
exposure and hence it is enough to test this position during the full scale experiment.

6. Laboratory measurements 
 
6.1 Specific objectives 
 
The aim of the laboratory measurement was to: 
 Study the effect of the following parameters on the capture efficiency and/or 
capture velocity of AEA: 
1. Direction of the cross-draught: from above or sideways; 
2. Cross-draught velocities: 0, 0.15, 0.25, 0.5;  
3. Position of the AEA’s hood compared to the source: from above, sideways 
and at 45o relative to the pollution source placed below the AEA unit; 
4. Magnitude of the AEA’s flow rate: 60, 87, 105, 134, 151 m3/h;  
5. Width of the AEA hood’s flange: narrow and wide 
6. Distance of the AEA’s hood from the source ; 
 Study the effect of the human body as an obstacle and as a buoyancy flow 
generator on the capture efficiency of the AEAs; 
 Study the exposure for a sitting person working with AEA in laboratories; 
 Study the cross-contamination at 1 meter from the source when AEA system is 
used to extract the contaminants; 
 Study the blocking effect of the vertical partitions placed around the working zone 
on the capture efficiency; 
 Visualize the flow at different capture efficiencies; 
 Asses the pressure drop due to the different AEA position

6.2.8 Experimental conditions 
 
Cross draft and background ventilation 
 
It was identified during the field survey that fume cupboards were present in each 
room where AEA was installed. During the background ventilation design this have to 
be taken into consideration. The required face velocity for the fume cupboards is 
normally 0.5 m/s and the background ventilation should be obviously lower not to 
disturb the extraction through the fume cupboards. The supply diffuser has to be placed 
in such a way that the produced air velocity around the fume cupboards should be 
below 30% of the face velocity. According this rule the 30% results in 0.15 m/s however 
some other effects have to be considered (safety factor). People commonly walk at a 
speed of 0.9-1.3 m/s which can produce a wake effect (discussed in section 2.2.5). 
Furthermore, the opening of the doors or windows (if any) can further enhance the 
draught due to the negative pressure in the room (requirement) [22].  
Last but not least, the AEAs may be placed less carefully in the premises than the 
fume cupboards (e.g. in the way of draught caused by supply diffuser) and the fume 
cupboard can have higher face velocity than 0.5 m/s which allows for higher room 
velocities. Considering the previously enumerated factors the final velocity for the 
background ventilation is set to 0.25 m/s. Based on the “Design criteria for spaces in 
different types of buildings “ by DS/CEN/CR 1752:1998 [26], the 0.25 m/s is the worst 
case (category C) for many building types during summer (cooling season). This is 
another explanation why exactly 0.25 m/s was chosen, even if laboratories or similar 
building types are not on the list.  
The field survey (section 6. Field survey) showed quite high background air 
velocities within the working area where the AEA units were installed: up to 0.5 m/s. The 
measured velocity reached the maximum value of 0.5 m/s under the supply diffuser; 
with an average value of 0,231 m/s. Further from the supply diffuser, within a small 
distance (20-30 cm) the value drastically decreased to a maximum of 0,122 m/s and an 
average of 0,030 m/s. Therefore, three different background velocities were tested in 
the current experiment with the AEA, i.e. 0.15 m/s, 0.25 m/s and 0.5 m/s

6.2.10 Procedure 
 
Main experiment 
 
The detailed description of the “main experiment” is presented by its steps below: 
 
Step 1: 
Before the actual measurement started, the disturbance/air velocity produced by the 
total volume ventilation in the room was measured and controlled. For this purpose, a 
spot measurement was done with Swema 3000 and SWA 03 with a measured time of 1 
minute (sampling rate of 0.25 s). The background air velocity was measured on the 
table at height of 10 cm (equal to the height of the source opening) at the central 
(vertical) axes of the source with the wind box turned off. Obviously, 0 m/s or close 
value to it was desired and it was achieved in each case (according to Swema 
instrument).  
 
Step 2: 
The two fan of the “wind box” was connected to a voltage controller which regulates its 
revolutions per minute (rpm). The voltage controller was adjusted until the required 
draught velocity (0.15, 0.25 or 0.5 m/s) was reached above the source opening. During 
the adjustment of the draught velocity the source and Manikin (if used) were removed to 
obtain as uniform cross flow as possible. Again Swema 3000 and SWA 03 were used 
and the average velocity was measured for 1 minute at the same location as in Step 1. 
The value was accepted when the standard deviation was less than 10% of the average 
velocity. Otherwise, the measurement was repeated and the cross flow was readjusted. 
When the obtained value was the same (±2 %) in 3 consecutive measurements, the 
“wind box” was considered adjusted. In the late afternoon (after 17:00) the velocity 
fluctuations increased due to increased voltage fluctuation in the mains, therefore the 
measurements were stopped. The standard deviation in the measured draught velocity 
clearly showed this.  
 
Step 3: 
Both the AEA and the source were placed in the tested position for the particular case 
tested. Normally, the source was positioned on the table 0.25 m from the table’s edge 
and centrally to the body of the dummy.

observed for the position “sideways” together with the highest flow rate (170 m3/h). In 
this case the pressure difference was 10% while the flow rate difference was only 5%. 
Hence, when the AEA is installed and the flow rate is measured/ adjusted, 5% of the 
flow rate (for higher flow rates) has to be added to the initial flow rate to make sure that 
the required flow rate is achieved in the other positions as well. For lower flow rates 
(<120 m3/h), 3% of added flow rate is enough.  
The studied AEA were made up of “rigid sections” which show better 
performance than the flexible ducts as it was discussed in the section 2.1.2. Hence the 
bending of the flexible ducts may show different results.

6.4 Conclusions 
 
The results proved the importance of cross-draught upon the required capture 
velocity. The obtained capture velocities including each of the studied AEA positions 
and both of the cross-draught directions are (0.126), 0.196, 0.262 and 0.435 m/s 
corresponding to the cross-draught of (0), 0.15, 0.25 and 0.5 m/s respectively. These 
capture velocities can be used to design AEA system for laboratories, when the used 
chemicals emit isothermal contaminants with low velocity (e.g. volatile compounds) and 
the disturbance due to the background ventilation does not exceed 0.5 m/s. However, 
the first value (in parentheses) in both cases corresponding to 0 m/s cross-draught 
should not be used as it is hardly possible to create adequate conditions for that in 
normal indoor environment.  
The capture efficiency measured for different AEA positions showed that the 
position (“sideways”) which resulted shorter distance between the AEA’s hood and table 
(3 cm) provided higher capture efficiency/ required lower exhaust flow rate. In average it 
gave 18 % higher capture efficiency for horizontal cross-draughts and 28% for the 
vertical one compared to the other two studied AEA positions with respect to the source. 
This finding has two benefits in the same time. First, the hood positioned sideways to 
the source does not bother the working process, and hence it is allowed to place the 
AEA’s hood closer to the source. On the other hand, it is less sensitive to the cross-draft 
since that is diminished by the table and has less free sides unprotected against cross-
draughts.  
Comparing the two cross-draught directions, it turned out that the vertical cross-
draught positively influence the capture efficiency of the “sideways” AEA position and 
negatively the other two positions compared to the horizontal cross-draught. The impact 
of the thermal manikin on the capture efficiency was different as well for the two cross-
draught directions. While the presence of thermal manikin improved the capture 
efficiency with 13% in average for the horizontal cross-draught, the thermal manikin 
decreased the capture efficiency with 14% in average for the vertical one.  
Obviously, the exposure to the tracer gas at the “mouth” sampling point and the 
cross-contamination at the “1 m from the source” were inversely proportional with the 
capture efficiency. Knowing that substances used with AEAs have a low toxicity level it 
can be concluded that the exposure/concentration of tracer gas in both sampling points 
(“mouth” and “1m from the source”) were negligible (<2 mg/m3) above 95% capture 
efficiency. This can be used to design the system for 95% efficiency and reduce the 
energy consumption.   
The “multi-purpose hood” reached far higher capture efficiencies than the “basic” 
hood under both of the cross-draught directions. Even a low flow rate like 87 m3/h with 
worse studied case (horizontal cross-draught) could extract all the contaminants under 
==PAGE:94==
Technical University of Denmark 
2016 
 
 
84 
 
the highest cross-draught (0.5 m/s). Therefore, it is recommended to use hoods with 
wide flange in each case when the space around the working process allows.  
 Placing vertical partitions on both sides of the working zone did not provide the 
expected improvement for the vertical cross-draught case when the AEA was positioned 
above the source with exhaust flow rate of 134 m3/h. In fact, the vertical partitions were 
neutral to the capture efficiency or decreased it.  
The different methods which investigated the impact of the distance on the 
capture efficiency well complied with the theory. Each one proved how drastically 
decreases the velocity in front of the hood in case of the exhaust flows. The capture 
efficiency decreased from 100% to almost 0% when the distance between the hood and 
the source was increased by 5 cm. Thus, the worker has to be trained to understand 
this property of exhaust flow and keep the recommended distance between the source 
and the hood. Actually, the obtained results from the laboratory measurements, smoke 
visualizations and section 2.3.2 (summary of legislation/regulation) can be further used 
as a basis to formulate such a training/guidance for AEA use.

Appendix G. Background ventilation measurement 
 
The measuring methods to define the background ventilation’s real flow rate:  
1. The system controlled by the “VEE Pro 6.0” obtained the pressure difference from 
the manometer which was placed into the “Control room”. This manometer was 
connected to the two sides of an “Iris 200 damper” which forms a virtually ideal 
measuring orifice [33]. The obtained value from the software was higher than the 
real value. This can be explained with the position of the “Iris damper” which was 
placed close to the fan (in the “control room”). The main duct may had a leakage 
between the “Control room” and the “Field lab” which reduced flow rate in the “Field 
lab”. 
2. The flow rate was also measured with tracer gas by supplying N2O before the “Iris 
damper” and measuring it’s concentration in the “Field lab” approximately 3 
diameters before the supply diffuser (similarly with the exhaust). Due to the 
inappropriate mixing or due to other unknown factors the calculated flow rate by 
tracer gas method was lower than the real value both for supply and for exhaust. 
3. Finally three different methods gave similar results, therefore those were considered 
reliable. “Swema flow 125 D” was used first which was calibrated until 125 l/s though 
it still showed good results. Furthermore, the Swema 3000 with SWA 03 was used to 
measure the velocity on the duct face without the diffuser. The duct face was divided 
equally in 9 points where the velocity was measured 3 times. The results were 
similar with 5% difference. The most accurate results were obtained by the 
“Velocicalc 9565-P” with “Thermo anemometer straight probe 960”.  For the 
measuring purpose a hole was drilled through the duct at a recommended distance. 
The recommended distance means a straight duct section with a length of 3 times 
the duct diameter before (for supply, after for exhaust) any bending/silencer.  The 
probe was inserted into the hole and pushed until the opposite side. Then the 
measurement started (with the adjusted settings) and the probe was pulled for 10 
seconds while the sensor was still inside but on the end of the duct with the duct.  
During these 10 seconds numerous automatic measurements were done and its 
average value was given as a result. This measurement was repeated for three 
times and its average value was the following:

Appendix L. Cross-draught and Optimum hood size 
 
Influence of the cross-draught 
 
By plotting the trajectory of an individual air particle influenced by the suction hood and 
a transverse airstream (draught), the maximum distance between the source and hood 
can be determined. The trajectory (ɸ) has to go through the edges of the hood and the 
obtained thus line will define the suction region from the region which is incapable to 
collect particles (Figure L.92). A particle "A" has to be considered, which is moving 
toward the "sink" with velocity vs, which is acted upon by a lateral stream parallel with 
the plane of the hood, vx (Figure L.93). The other components of the Figure L.93 and the 
exact method of calculation is presented in the provided reference [9]. 
 
 
Figure L.92 Trajectory of particle under the 
influence of suction and a horizontal air 
stream [9]  
 
Figure L.93 Determiantion of critical  
trajectory [9] 
 
For the current theory some simplifications are made which makes it difficult to use it 
precisely in real cases or other cases than those described below: 
 The currents in the building are transverse to the axes of the hood. 
 The currents are constant in velocity from the top to the bottom of room 
 The contaminant is extracted only from the front of the hood, although the 
contaminant can get behind the hood and be extracted afterward.  
 The hood’s central angle should be ≤60o, which assumes an even velocity across 
the hood face (average suction velocity=central velocity).

Optimum hood size and volumetric flow rate 
 
The current theory part is described through an example using Figure L.94. When the 
specific surface emitting the pollutant is given by its length A (square, A=B) located at H 
distance from the hood plane/hood orifice and the desired air speed on the surface is 
given, the optimum hood size and flow rate can be calculated. The maximum face 
velocity is also limited which is normally defined based on the allowed noise level and 
available system performance. The components of the Figure L.94 and the exact 
method of calculation is presented in the provided reference [34] 
 
 
Figure L.94 The parameters and the configuration used  
in the derivion of hood optimisation [34] 
 
The calculation results have been compared with experimental study indirectly. The air 
speed has been measured in points where the air speed has been theoretically 
determined. Then, the flow rate has been adjusted until the air speed specification was 
fulfilled. On average, 10% less exhaust air flow rate was required during the experiment 
than the calculated one. 
 
Similarly to the “2.2.5 Effect of background flows/draught” the current method has some 
limitation as well: 
 The available formula is only valid if the hood plane and the specification plane 
are parallel to each other; 
 Again, uniform velocity is assumed across the hood face (average suction 
velocity=central velocity). This is hardly plausible for hoods with large cross-
sectional area, like the square hood with 19.6 cm side length given in the current 
example.

Appendix M.  Maximum amount of extracted contaminant  
 
Maximum amount of contaminant (in l/s) which can be extracted without changing the 
capture efficiency: 
  
For this reason a low flow rate was considered (70 m3/h) with the worst case 
scenario. By worst case scenario it is meant that the air is extracted from all direction 
(sphere) without flange and the extracted air volume is equally distributed along the 
sphere surface having a diameter of 15,5 cm. Additionally, the contaminant is only 
extracted directly from the source. The ratio between the source’s surface and sphere 
surface (15.5 cm diameter) multiplied with the exhaust flow rate gives the maximum 
allowed dosage/production. For the previously described scenario this is 0.0809 l/s. In 
order to have a reference, the value is compared with the amount of dosed tracer gas 
during the experiments. As a result, the previously described setup is capable to 
exhaust 95 times more tracer gas than the dosed value. In real case disregarding from 
the worst case scenario, the result can even be around 1.23 l/s (roughly 1500 times the 
dosage). The real setup assumes larger area around the source (not only source/tube 
area), flange, higher flow rate and the fact that more air is extracted from the middle 
region if it is well positioned. The simplified calculation is shown in Table L.29.
