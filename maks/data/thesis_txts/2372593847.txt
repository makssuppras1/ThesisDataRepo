Abstract
Power from photovoltaic sources can have wide voltage ranges. When con-
verting this power to another DC source such as a battery, conventional con-
verters would have to reach extreme duty cycles to operate on the whole voltage
range.
In this report a new bidirectional isolated topology, which is eﬃcient at deal-
ing with extreme voltage ranges, is proposed. The operation of this topology is
described and a prototype is designed and tested. The operation of the topology
is then veriﬁed from the tests and eﬃciency measurements are conducted.

Chapter 1
Introduction
During the last ten years, increasing amounts of the electricity consumption
has been covered by sustainable energy, and this is not a trend, which is likely
to stop. One of the types of sustainable power, which is seeing an increased
popularity is PV (PV) power. PV power production has the advantage that
it is not loud, huge or location-dependent, and as such private consumers have
started to install solar panels on their own property for their own consumption.
PV power has the disadvantage of being very dependent on the weather, and
therefore it makes sense to connect it to a storage system such as a battery, so
the power can be produced whenever it is available and consumed whenever it
is needed.
Due to the increase in the number of PV systems in private households and
other renewable energy sources it has become more tempting to include all these
distributed generation systems (GDS) into the shared power grid. This can lead
to a faster movement towards a completely renewable power grid.
The batteries for PV-systems on the market however are of diﬀerent types
and voltage levels and usually each have their own speciﬁc charger. To simplify
the market a hybrid battery charger could be designed, that could handle the
charging and discharging for a wide range of batteries.
In this project a new converter topology for a hybrid charger is proposed,
which can theoretically operate with good eﬃciency at a wide battery voltage
level as well as the various voltage levels of a solar panel. The goal is to verify
the operation of the new topology and to assess its viability for the application.

In this chapter three diﬀerent topologies are evaluated for the application of
the hybrid battery charger. The operation of the converters are explained and
the stress factors are calculated. From the stress factors the chosen topology is
found.
As a rule of thumb, full-bridge topologies are the eﬃcient choice when de-
signing converters for 500 W and above. Since the power level of the hybbrid
battery charger is 1 kW, only full-bridge-conﬁgurated converters are analyzed
in this chapter as they show advantages at power ratings above 500 W[1]. The
three toplogies investigated are the full bridge boost, the EWiRaC and the
four-level EWiRaC.

4.1
Boost Mode Operation
During boost mode the four level I-EWiRaC operates almost like a normal full
bridge boost converter would. During ON-time the converter boosts the current
of the inductor by turning on all the left side switches. The right side switches
are turned oﬀ, as they won’t need to be conducting during this period. During
OFF-time the current of the inductor is channeled into the transformers in
parallel. During the second duty cycle(T to 2T), the inductor is also boosted
during ON-time and then channeled in parallel into the transformers but in
the reverse direction during OFF-time. The switching scheme can be viewed in
ﬁgure 4.1 to 4.4

reason, there are two, is to push a reverse voltage on the transformers so they
won’t saturate. To analyze the operation of the converter in boost mode it is

The MOSFETs also have switching losses. These appear at the switch-on
and switch-oﬀtransients of the MOSFETs. During these transients, the MOS-

To create the ﬁrst six PWM signals, the PWM-channels of the f28335 were
used. Since the complimentary signals are all 180◦shifted from its main signal,
it makes sense to use the up-down-count mode of the f28335, since this allows

Since the deadband is not deﬁned by the analog circuits, this has to be
deﬁned in the code. The MOSFETS on the PV side require a negative deadband,
i.e.
a deadband where both switches are turned on, to make sure that the
current of the inductor always has a path during switching. On the battery side
a positive deadband is required, i.e. a deadband where both switches are turned
oﬀ, in order to not short the output during switching. This is solved using the
DSP’s deadband feature. This requires the PV side signals to be negatively
oriented for the inversion in the deadband generator. This deadband is also
used for the boost mode operation to create the duty cycles above 50% for the
PV side signals and below 50% for the battery side signals.
5.6
Synthesis of PWM-channels
Since the PWM-module of the f28335 only has six channels, two channels had to
be programmed. Since all gate signals switch on or oﬀat either a period(0 or T)
or a duty time (DT or T +DT), it makes sense to deﬁne the two last signals from
these time points. By implementing interrupts from the PWM-module (whose
action qualiﬁers are already set on these time points), this is done rather simply.
However, there can only be one interrupt for each channel, and the interrupts
should be set and not change after the startup. Most channels do a phase shift
in one of the operation modes, so their interrupts will also be aﬀected by this
phase shift.
In order to have a fast and eﬀective code it is optimal to have as few in-
terrupts as possible enabled.
As such it is a good idea to the least amount
of PWM-interrupts to control the last two PWM-channels. The least amount
44
==PAGE:49==
required is four. Since PWM1, PWM2 and PWM5 never change phase, these
are optimal for this purpose. For the fourth interrupt, PWM4 is chosen. This
has a phase shift during HFT mode, however PWM7 and PWM8 are the same
during this operation mode and do therefore only require two interrupts to be
synthesized during this operation mode, so the PWM4 interrupt can be ignored
during this operation mode. PWM1 is used to ﬁnd DT, PWM2 is used to ﬁnd
T, PWM4 is used to ﬁnd 2T (which is not used in HFT mode) and PWM5
is used to ﬁnd T + DT. Using the gate signal diagrams of ﬁgure 5.9 to 5.11.

To create the deadband a timer was introduced. Whenever one of the in-
terrupts is called, the interrupt will only turn oﬀgate signals. It will then set
a global value specifying the switches that should turn on and it will start the
timer. When the timer interrupt is called, it will turn on the switches spec-
iﬁed in the global variable and the interrupt then stops and resets the timer.
The pwm-interrupts will however approach each other, which will create some
timing issues, when the deadbands overlap. At this point the DSP will ﬁrst
handle one pwm-interrupt and then it will handle another pwm-interrupt be-
fore the timer-interrupt occurs. Therefore the timer-interrupts for the DT and
T + DT pwm-interrupt and the T and 2T pwm-interrupts need to be handled

6.1
Testing of parallel full bridges
Initially the circuit was tested with its planned conﬁguration, where the full
bridges of the batter side were connected to each other in parallel. The battery
side MOSFETs were turned oﬀ, so they functioned as diodes. This was done
to simplify the system and to prevent possible shorts from bad timing. The
converter was then tested for power ﬂow from PV to battery. It was measured
that the fourth full bridge would deliver 70% of the battery current for PHT
mode. This increased losses as the currents were not equally balanced between
the power stages, which increased conduction losses. This could be seen as the
MOSFETs on the fourth full bridge became much hotter than the others.
This unbalanced current appeared because the other diodes did not open.
The small variations in turns ratios on the transformers would lead to diﬀerences
in the secondary side voltages if the primary side voltages were the same. The
highest transformer voltage would deﬁne the battery voltage, and since this
would be higher than the other voltages all diodes on the other bridges would
be reverse-biased and not conduct.
This issue was solved by connecting the full bridges in series as seen in ﬁgure

changes the gain function by a factor of 4, and means that it will no longer be

Chapter 7
Discussion and Future
Work
While the four level I-EWiRaC can operate eﬃciently in one direction it does
less so in the other direction. This comes from the fact that one full bridge had
zero voltage during reverse power ﬂow. The reason for this was never really
found, but may be a result of a bad PCB design, as the full bridges did not have
the required clearance. However this seems contradicted by the fact that all full
bridges worked ﬁne with a power ﬂow from PV to battery. Further investigation
into this issue is required.
It was seen that a short was created through the transformers during switch-
ing. This arose from the negative deadband of the PV side MOSFETs. One
way of dealing with this issue is to ensure a certain leakage inductance, which
can suppress the current. However the four level I-EWiRaC already suﬀers from
high ringing from the leakage inductance, so increasing the leakage inductance
would increase this problem further.
Another solution would be to create a
switching scheme, where each half bridge switches with positive deadband, but
just right after eachother.
In order to be used as a battery charger, the circuit will also need a ﬁlter
on both sides to reduce ripple for both battery and PV system. It will also
need a control loop, which may be a challenge in itself, as the circuit has three
operation modes and thereby three transfer functions.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The aim of this project has been to analyze the four level I-EWiRaC and on
this analysis design and assemble a functioning prototype. This should then be
tested to verify the operation of the converter.
The four level I-EWiRac were compared to other topologies through stress
factors, which have earlier been used for handling wide voltage ranges, where it
showed advantages, when looking at semiconductor and capacitor stress. It did
however lose at winding stress compared to the normal I-EWiRaC.
The four level I-EWiRaC was analyzed and described with focus on the three
operation modes which were used for this application.
Their gain functions
were found as well as each mode’s operation limits. A combined proﬁle for the
topology was then found from the operation modes. Three operation modes
which were not used were also brieﬂy mentioned
The circuit was designed and assembled for the testing of the topology.
MOSFETs and capacitors were selected from the stresses calculated and their
losses were estimated to select proper heat sinks. Inductors and transformers
were likewise designed and constructed and their losses were estimated.
A DSP was programmed for an open-loop control of the circuit. Two ex-
tra PWM-channels were programmed into it to create all the signals for the
MOSFETs.
The circuit was tested to verify the operation described in the analysis. The
waveforms were captured and analyzed. The results however were inconclusive
as the circuit only operated as expected with a power ﬂow in one direction.
The circuit however could still transfer power in the other direction. Eﬃciency
measurements were performed on the circuit.
