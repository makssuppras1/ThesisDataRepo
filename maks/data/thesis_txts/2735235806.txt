Abstract
Recently, Sb2S3 has emerged as a promising thin film photovoltaic material because it
exhibits a high absorption coefficient, and is composed of earth-abundant and afford-
able elements. It achieves high crystalline quality at relatively low temperatures and its
band gap of 1.7 to 1.8 eV makes it ideal for the upper cell in silicon-based tandem solar
cells. Even though Sb2S3-based solar cells have high theoretical efficiency, their practical
efficiency lags behind other thin film materials. This work investigates the impact of an-
nealing conditions on Sb2S3 layer properties and optimizes the Spiro-OMeTAD deposition
parameters. The Sb2S3 layers are annealed using either a hot plate or a tube furnace. The
results show that the type of annealing process has a significant influence on the grain
growth process. Moreover, the findings indicate that Sb2S3’s high sensitivity to oxida-
tion is significantly affected by oxygen partial pressure, temperature, and annealing time.
The study further identifies the absence of semiconductor behavior in hot plate annealed
samples due to incomplete crystallization or sulfur deficiencies, in contrast to tube fur-
nace annealed samples that display a band gap of 1.59 eV. Recommendations for future
research include reducing the O2 concentration during hot plate annealing, studying the
Sb2S3 layers using X-ray Diffraction (XRD) and X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS),
and performing the Spiro-OMeTAD layer deposition in a cleanroom. Implementing these
strategies could significantly improve device performance, which is currently limited by
the presence of defects and impurities.

1 Introduction
The severe consequences of climate change have become increasingly evident in recent
years. To reduce the severity of climate change impacts, the global average temperature
increase relative to pre-industrial levels should remain below 2 °C or preferably less than
1.5 °C. Failing to do so would cause increases in multiple climate hazards and present
risks to ecosystems and humans [1]. The most efficient approach to reduce greenhouse gas
emissions is substituting renewable energy sources with fossil fuels in electricity generation
[2]. Given the rising global population, increased electrification, and the ongoing energy
crisis, there is an urgent need to accelerate the deployment of renewable energies to meet
the growing electricity demand [3]. To meet this growing demand, photovoltaic (PV)
technology has a high potential to provide the world with clean and sustainable energy
[4].
To fulfill its potential, the cumulative capacity of PV installations must scale to
multi-terawatt scale by 2030 [5].
PV technologies have demonstrated the fastest cost reductions of all energy technologies
[6]. Consequently, solar power currently represents the cheapest form of electricity in most
regions of the world [7]. Main drivers for future solar cost reductions include technological
improvements, such as efficiency increases [8]. Currently, silicon solar cells have a market
share of over 90 % worldwide [9]. However, the efficiency of silicon solar cells is already
close to the theoretical maximum of this technology [10]. One approach to increase the
performance of PV systems is the use of tandem solar cells. Here, a thin film material
is added on top of e.g. a silicon cell, to maximize the absorption of sunlight and thus
increase the device’s efficiency. While mature and desirable lower cell candidates such
as silicon are already available, the corresponding advanced candidates for wide bandgap
upper cells are still lacking [3].
One promising material for the top cell in silicon-based tandem solar cells is Sb2S3. Its
band gap is in the range of 1.7 to 1.8 eV, aligning well with the required band gap for a

top cell in this configuration. Sb2S3 is an emerging thin film photovoltaic material that
has a high absorption coefficient (α > 10−4 cm−1) with constituents that are both earth-
abundant, low cost and nontoxic [11]. It forms a single stable phase and high crystalline
quality films can already be synthesized at < 350 °C [11].
The favorable band gap alignment with silicon leads to a maximum theoretical power
conversion efficiency (PCE) of over 40% for these tandem cells [9]. For a single-junction
Sb2S3-based solar cell, the Shockley–Queisser limit for the PCE is 28.64% [12].
Even though Sb2S3-based solar cells have numerous desirable characteristics and a high
theoretical efficiency, their practical efficiency has not exceeded 7.5% yet [13], [3]. The
efficiency is primarily limited by the high density of interface and bulk defects and poor
carrier transport [11].
Previously used production processes such as Chemical Bath Deposition (CBD) were
unable to meet these limitations. Therefore, the current thesis investigates an alternative
deposition process and different annealing processes.
The deposition process used in
this work is Radio Frequency (RF) magnetron sputtering on a CdS substrate.
This
process enables the production of high-quality films over large areas and ensures accurate
transfer of the composition from target to film, along with efficient up-scaling for industrial
applications [14].
In addition to the deposition process, the type of annealing process and temperature are
critical parameters because of their impact on the crystallization of the material. Highly
crystalline and large Sb2S3 grains are detrimental to decreasing recombination losses and
improving the performance of the device [13].
Although several publications compare different annealing temperatures of Sb2S3 layers
deposited via sputtering [15], [14], [16], they all rely on tube furnace annealing and do
not compare the obtained results to hot plate annealing.
Previous research compared different annealing temperatures of Sb2S3 layers deposited
via sputtering [15], [14], [16]. However, these studies exclusively utilized tube furnace an-
2
==PAGE:20==
nealing and did not explore hot plate annealing, resulting in a lack of comparison between
different annealing processes. Going beyond previous studies, the following research ques-
tion is investigated: How do different annealing processes and parameters influence the
morphology, crystallinity, and optical properties of the Sb2S3 layer?
Following the deposition and annealing of the Sb2S3 layer, the Hole Transport Layer (HTL)
Spiro-OMeTAD is deposited using spin-coating. HTLs have the purpose of hole extraction
from the absorber layer while blocking the electron flow [17], thereby influencing the
overall performance of solar cells. During the initial spin-coating depositions, it becomes
apparent that the quality of Spiro-OMeTAD layers does not meet expectations, despite
being thoroughly studied in literature [18], [19]. Aiming to obtain high-quality Spiro-
OMeTAD layers, the current thesis investigates how the deposition parameters can be
optimized to enhance its uniformity, and morphology and minimize the occurrence of
comets within the film.
These research questions are answered by analyzing the deposited Sb2S3 and Spiro-

2 Literature Review
This chapter presents a literature review, starting with an overview of thin film photo-
voltaics. It then narrows down to Sb2S3 solar cells, specifically focusing on planar hetero-
junction solar cells and publications using the same device structure as in this work. The
chapter concludes with an overview of the most important material properties of Sb2S3.
2.1 Thin film photovoltaics
Thin-film solar cells have a much higher absorption coefficient (∼105 cm−1) than crys-
talline silicon solar cells (c-Si) [20]. Thus they can be manufactured to be only a few
nanometers (nm) to a few micrometers (µm) thick, which is much thinner than the wafers
used in conventional c-Si-based solar cells. Because of that, they use significantly less
material [21]. This can lead to cost savings, reduced environmental impact, and easier
scalability of production. The production processes of thin-film solar cells typically also
have lower energy consumption, which can potentially reduce the carbon footprint asso-
ciated with manufacturing [22]. They can also be deposited on flexible substrates leading
to a wider range of applications and even roll-to-roll manufacturing, which can reduce the
energy payback time significantly [20].
Thin film solar cells are manufactured by depositing one or more thin layers of photo-
voltaic material onto a substrate, such as glass, plastic or metal [22]. Many different
deposition methods exist for this purpose, these can be categorized into physical and
chemical methods. Physical methods include methods such as sputtering, thermal evap-
oration, and pulsed laser deposition [23].
Chemical methods include Chemical Vapor
Deposition (CVD), Chemical Bath Deposition (CBD) and spin coating [24].
Copper Indium Gallium di-selenide (CIGS), Cadmium Telluride (CdTe), and amorphous-
5

silicon (a-Si) are currently the most adopted types of thin film solar cells [21]. CIGS solar
cells have a high absorption coefficient and a wide spectral response. Their efficiency has
reached over 20 % in recent years. Commonly, glass is used as a substrate, followed by
molybdenum as the back contact and the CIGS absorber layer. Then a cadmium sulfide
(CdS) n-type buffer layer is deposited on top, followed by a zinc oxide (ZnO) layer. At
last, an aluminum-doped ZnO layer is added, which serves as a transparent conducting
oxide [25]. One drawback of CIGS solar cells is that both gallium and indium are relatively
rare and expensive elements. Additionally, the absorber has to be processed at relatively
high temperatures > 450 °C [20].
CdTe solar cells consist of a p-type CdTe absorber layer and an n-type CdS-based window
layer forming a heterojunction, with an intermixed interface region [20]. Its ideal band gap
of ∼1.5 eV matches very well with the peak of the solar spectrum and efficiencies of 22.1
% have been reported. It is chemically simple and exhibits robust stability even in the
presence of high energy-photon and electron irradiation as in space [26]. The deposited
films require a CdCl2 annealing treatment, which recrystallizes the material and helps with
forming a robust interface [20]. CdTe solar cells are ideal for high throughput production
and exhibit the lowest module costs of PV cells on the market. The disadvantages of
CdTe solar cells include the scarcity of Tellurium and the toxicity of Cadmium.
A-Si solar cells have a bandgap of 1.7 eV, have low manufacturing cost, and a short energy
payback time [21]. Their structure is composed of the a-Si absorber layer which itself is
divided into an intrinsic, p-type, and n-type layer, as well as a transparent conductive
oxide (TCO) as the front contact and a metal back contact. The issue of a large number
of dangling bond surface defects in a-Si is solved by doping the material with hydrogen,
which successfully passivates these defects. Silicon is cheap, abundant, and non-toxic, but
a-Si devices have only moderate efficiencies of ∼14 %.
Due to the limitations of the previously mentioned thin film technologies, there is an
ongoing need for research to explore cost-effective, non-toxic, stable, and environmentally
friendly alternatives for absorber materials [27]. Therefore, the semiconducting chalco-
genide Sb2S3 from the V2 −VI3 family has drawn considerable attention from the scientific

2.2 Antimony sulfide (Sb2S3) solar cells
Sb2S3 solar cells were first reported in 1992 by Savadogo and Mandal [29].
In 1993,
they reported the first solid planar heterojunction Sb2S3 solar cell with a PCE of 5.19%
[30]. Planar heterojunction solar cells are one of the two types of Sb2S3 solar cells. The
other type is mesoporous sensitized solar cells. These can be alternatively referred to as
quantum dot-sensitized solar cells, their working principle closely resembles that of dye-
sensitized solar cells [31]. This work will concentrate on the planar heterojunction type,
as it holds greater relevance to the research conducted in this thesis. While Sb2Se3 solar
cells closely resemble Sb2S3 solar cells in device structure and crystallographic properties,
they are excluded in the literature review to avoid exceeding the intended scope of this
work.
After a period of no publications on planar Sb2S3 solar cells, research interest started to
increase again in 2012, when Boix et al. published results on their devices deposited by
CBD with a PCE of 1.43 % [32]. In 2013, Muto et al. reported their Sb2S3 solar cell with
a PCE of 2.56 %, which had an absorber layer thickness of 50 nm [33]. In 2014, Kim et
al. managed to increase the PCE of planar Sb2S3 solar cells to 5.77 % [34]. They used
atomic layer deposition (ALD) to deposit a uniform amorphous Sb2S3 layer. Afterward,
they annealed the sample at 330 °C under H2S atmosphere. The phase purity and denser
morphology resulted in the increased performance of the device [34].
Zimmermann et al.
researched the effects of hole transport layers (HTLs) and their
thickness in 2015, concluding that the HTL contributes to parasitic absorption and that
recombination at the interfaces is still a limiting factor of device performance [35]. They
suggested that using alternative HTLs with a larger exciton diffusion length could im-
prove the short-circuit current.
Their solar cell, which was optimized with a 155 nm
thick absorber and a 50 nm HTL, exhibited a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of
4.06% and a VOC exceeding 700 mV. In 2017, Wang et al.

2.3 Sb2S3 material properties
Sb2S3 has an orthorhombic crystal structure with a Pnma space group [41]. It is formed
of infinite (Sb4S6)n chains (ribbons) along the [001] direction (see figure 2.2) [11]. An
individual ribbon is composed of a pair of trigonal SbS3 pyramids and two square SbS5
pyramids, which together create a foundational unit within the crystal structure of Sb2S3
[11]. Along the ribbon, strong covalent bonds are present, but the ribbons and layers are
linked by weaker van der Waals forces [42]. Similarly, the transport of charge carriers in
Sb2S3 is more efficient along the ribbon axis than in other directions [11]. The conduc-
tivity along ribbons is 100 times higher than along sheets and 1000 times higher than
perpendicular to the sheets [43]. Consequently, it is desirable to control the orientation
of the ribbons such that they are aligned with the desired carrier transport direction [3].
Thus, in a thin film solar cell, the ribbons in the Sb2S3 layer should be oriented vertically
in the [001] or diagonally in the [hk1] orientation [3].
Figure 2.2: Crystal structure of Sb2S3. (A) The perspective view of a single (Sb4S6)n
ribbon. (B) The perspective view of Sb2S3 (Pbnm) crystal structure projected on (001)
plane. Gray spheres represent Sb atoms, while yellow spheres symbolize S atoms. Repro-
duced from Sb2S3 Solar Cells, by Kondrotas et al. 2018.
Sb2S3 has a band gap between 1.7 and 1.8 eV and high crystalline quality films can already
be synthesized at low temperatures due to its low melting of approximately 550 °C [13].
It is also stable in air and moisture and exhibits a relatively high electron diffusion length
of 290-900 nm [13].

2.3 Sb2S3 material properties
The efficiency of Sb2S3 solar cells is mainly limited by the complex defect properties
of the material, which can be classified in bulk and interface defects. Interface defects
include defects due to a mismatch in the crystal lattice, contamination or impurities, and
dangling bonds. Bulk defects include antimony vacancies, sulfur vacancies, interstitials
and anti-sites of antimony and sulfur, dislocations, and grain boundaries [3]. After this
examination of the properties and challenges of Sb2S3 solar cells, the next chapter explores
the methodology used in this thesis to answer the research questions outlined earlier.

3.3 Sb2S3 layer

3.3.1 RF Magnetron sputtering
The sputtering setup shown in Figure 3.5 is used for the deposition of the Sb2S3 layer

3.5 Gold layer
The gold (Au) layer serves as the back contact and collects the charge carriers. Its main
advantages are that it is chemically inert and exhibits a deep work function, necessary to
prevent a high energy barrier for extracting holes at the rear interface in p-type absorbers
and establish ohmic contact.
3.5.1 Layer removal with sandpaper
Since the FTO layer serves as the front contact, but it cannot be accessed from the front
due to the glass substrate, access is required from the bottom. That is why, as shown
in Figure 3.15, sandpaper is employed to remove all layers except the FTO in a section
of the sample. This involves first removing the Spiro-OMeTAD and Sb2S3 layers with

3.5.3 Thermal evaporation
The gold back contact is deposited using the thermal evaporation process, as illustrated
in Figure 3.17. Although the deposition is not carried out by myself, this chapter gives a
summary of the deposition process. Here, gold pellets are heated and melted in a tungsten
boat in a high vacuum chamber, which is depicted in Figure 3.18. It is relatively low-cost
compared to e-beam evaporation or sputtering. Additionally, the particles deposited via
thermal evaporation have lower kinetic energy, resulting in reduced impact speed on the
substrate. This characteristic helps prevent damage to the underlying layers. Further-
more, thermal evaporation exhibits low diffusion into the substrate, enabling precise layer
deposition.
The tungsten boat is used as a hot filament, which is heated using resistive heating, where
a large current is applied to the tungsten boat. The samples are placed above the tungsten
boat in a large sample holder (see Figure 3.17) and covered with an evaporation mask,
which makes sure that the gold is only deposited in certain areas. The sample holder is
rotated to ensure a uniform film on all samples.
The voltage and current applied to the tungsten boat are increased slowly to 1.4 V and
62 A. This ensures a gradual heat-up of the tungsten boat and the gold pellets, which

3.6 Analysis
This section provides a brief explanation of the various analysis techniques used in this
paper. Among these are reflectance, Raman spectroscopy, Scanning Electron Microscopy
(SEM), reflectance measurements, and I-V measurements.
3.6.1 Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is widely used in the field of materials science, as it is
a powerful tool for imaging samples. While light microscopes have a useful magnification
of around 1000x, SEMs can have a magnification of up to 2000000x [47]. This is due
to the lower wavelength of an electron beam compared to visible light.
In an SEM,
the electron beam is focused using electromagnetic lenses and directed onto the sample
surface.
The electrons interact with the sample’s surface and generate backscattered
30
==PAGE:48==
3.6 Analysis
electrons, secondary electrons, and X-rays [48]. These are collected by various detectors
and used to form an image or gain information about the chemical composition of the
sample.
SEMs enable the study of various materials, ranging from metals to carbon nanotubes,
polymers, and biological cells [48]. The sample preparation is relatively simple and it is
a mostly nondestructive analysis technique. The image interpretation is also relatively
straightforward and the large depth of field allows the user to produce 3-D-like images
[47].
In this work, a Zeiss Merlin SEM is used to analyze the samples. It has a theoretical
resolution of 0.8 nm and an acceleration voltage of 15 kV. It features an Everhart-Thornley
secondary electron detector, multiple in-lens detectors for backscattered electrons, and an
EDS-System used for Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy [49].
3.6.2 Raman spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy is a tool to identify molecules based on their unique spectral fin-
gerprints [50]. In this process, a sample is illuminated with a laser that has a specific
wavelength. When the laser light interacts with the vibrating molecules in the sample,
some photons undergo inelastic scattering, resulting in a change in their energy and fre-
quency. This interaction is known as Raman scattering, which involves the transfer of
energy between the incident photon and the vibrating molecule [51]. Here, Stokes and
anti-Stokes scattering can be distinguished, the former occurring when a molecule absorbs
some of the photon’s energy and is promoted to a higher energy state. As a result, the
scattered photon has a lower energy than the incident photon. In anti-Stokes scattering,
energy is transferred to the scattered photon, increasing its energy and causing it to have
a shorter wavelength than the incident photon.
The resulting light spectrum of the Raman scattering is called a Raman spectrum. In
this spectrum, peaks can be identified which correspond to the vibrational modes of the
molecules within the sample. This provides information about the chemical composition
31
==PAGE:49==
3 Methodology
and molecular structure of the sample [50].
In this thesis, a custom Raman setup is used with a 532 nm laser. The scattered light is
guided through a beam splitter and a Rayleigh rejection filter. This filter blocks the light
that is elastically scattered (Rayleigh scattering). Subsequently, the light passes through
the entrance slit and is directed onto a diffraction grating, which disperses the light into
several beams that travel in slightly different directions. Finally, the dispersed light is
captured by a CCD Camera, which records the spectrum.
3.6.3 Reflectance spectroscopy
Reflectance spectroscopy is used to measure the amount of light reflected by a material
across a specific wavelength spectrum. It can provide valuable information about the
optical properties of a sample [52]. It is widely used in materials science to analyze the
surface properties of samples and is employed in this thesis to study the band gap of the
annealed Sb2S3 absorber layers.
In the optical setup that is used for this purpose, a tungsten-halogen light source with
a broad continuum spectrum is connected to an integrating sphere via an optical cable.
This sphere has a white coating inside with a reflectivity higher than 98 %. The light is
focused onto the sample with a lens. Once it hits the sample, part of the light is reflected.
The specular and diffuse reflections are collected at the light port, where it is redirected
via an optical cable into an Ocean Optics spectrometer (QE 6500). This spectrometer is
working in the wavelength range of 200-980 nm. The setup can measure either the total or
the diffuse reflection of samples. To measure diffuse reflection only, the white trap located
at the specular port can be exchanged by a black trap, which absorbs all specular light.
Specular reflection is then obtained by subtracting the diffuse from the total reflection.
At first, the detector is cooled down to -10 °C to reduce thermal noise. Then the system
is calibrated by doing both a dark and white reference measurement. The white reference
material is a ceramic that has 99 % reflectance over a broad part of the spectrum. After
calibrating the system, one sample at a time is analyzed.
32
==PAGE:50==
3.6 Analysis
The band gap of a semiconductor material can be inferred from the reflectance spectrum.
The band gap can be found at the point where the reflectance curve begins to decrease
sharply, marking the onset of absorption of the semiconductor material [53].
3.6.4 I-V measurements
Current-Voltage (I-V) characterization tests are used to assess the performance and ef-
ficiency of solar cells. Here, different voltages are applied to the cell and the resulting
current flowing through the device is measured. The generated I-V curve provides infor-
mation about values such as the open-circuit voltage (VOC), short-circuit current (ISC),
fill factor, and efficiency of the tested devices. Additionally, series and shunt resistance
(RS and RSh) can reveal defects or loss mechanisms within the solar cell. I-V measure-
ment can be conducted in the dark or under illumination. Here, solar simulators are
usually used. In this work, a Newport solar simulator is used along with a Keithley 2400
Source-Measurement-Unit (SMU) for the I-V measurement.
To measure the superstrate Sb2S3 solar cells produced in this work, the cells are posi-
tioned upside down within an elevated holder, allowing for contact with the electrodes
from the underside. This configuration results in a higher illumination level from the
solar simulator, reaching approximately 1400 W/m2, compared to the typical intensity
of around 1000 W/m2. For the I-V measurements, the voltage applied to the devices
is slowly increased from -0.5 V to 0.8 V and the resulting current is measured. These
measurements are conducted in the dark and under illumination. A shadow mask is used
to only illuminate one cell at a time.

5 Discussion
In the following chapter, the results are analyzed and discussed. The first section focuses
on the Sb2S3 layer, comparing the results of the annealing processes and parameters
employed in this study. In the second section, the results from the optimization of the
Spiro-OMeTAD layer are discussed. The third section analyzes the results of the I-V
measurements.
5.1 Sb2S3 layer
In this section, the results from hot plate and tube furnace annealing are discussed and
compared to each other. Comparing the hot plate and tube furnace annealing processes,
the grain growth process seems to be significantly different. The grain growth of the hot
plate annealing process is discussed first. In this process, the amorphous Sb2S3 layer is
first flattened out, before nucleation points can be observed. These nucleation points then
start rapidly growing, forming leaf-like grains as also observed by Zhang et al. [40]. These
grains then start overlapping and turn into a relatively continuous film. The speed of this
process is highly temperature-dependent and is faster for higher annealing temperatures.
While the sample annealed at 250 °C for 10 min shows a relatively smooth surface with no
Sb2O4-crystallites, the sample annealed for 5 min exhibits some crystallites on the surface.
This might be connected to a phenomenon reported by Parize et al. [60], wherein the
crystallization process proceeds via the intermediate creation of a metallic Sb phase and
frequently of a senarmontite cubic Sb2O3 phase.
The two samples annealed at 250 °C for 10 min at different O2 concentrations (Figure 4.7)
exhibit a significant difference in surface morphology. This highlights the sensitivity of
Sb2S3 to oxidation during the annealing process. The oxidation rate is highly dependent
on the partial pressure of oxygen, as shown by Padilla et al. [61]. They also show that
63
==PAGE:81==
5 Discussion
the rate of Sb2O4-crystallite formation depends strongly on the annealing temperature,
which is also observed in the analyzed samples of this thesis. The formation of Sb2O4-
crystallites can already be observed in Figure 4.11 for very short annealing times at higher
temperatures (e.g. 400 °C for 20 s). Higher annealing temperatures (400 °C), as well as
longer annealing times at moderate temperatures (e.g. 300 °C for 10 min), also result in
the formation of bubbles in the Sb2S3 film, which may burst and destroy the film. Because
of the dependence of the oxidation rate on the partial pressure of oxygen, it is essential to
maintain a very low O2 concentration during the annealing process. Thus, the hot plate
annealing process could be further improved by lowering the O2 concentration.
This
adjustment would enable the use of higher annealing temperatures or longer annealing
durations. These longer annealing durations could lead to a more complete crystallization
of the samples. Figure 4.6 shows that longer annealing times at 250 °C lead to a more
complete crystallization and better surface morphology.
The grain growth in the tube furnace annealing process first shows the formation of grain
boundaries, while the surface of the film still has an amorphous surface morphology as
seen in Figure 4.18 (a). While annealing at 300 °C for 1 min still yields an amorphous
surface morphology, extending the annealing time to 5 min results in smoother grains.
However, these exhibit small white crystals on top of the grains, which is the same for
short annealing times at 350 °C. Extending the annealing time to 10 min removes the
crystals on top but shows round structures on top of the grains. Annealing at 300 °C for
30 min also removes the white crystals, but shows larger crystals at grain junctions.
DeAngelis et al. show similar white crystals on top of their annealed Sb2S3 grains for sam-
ples annealed at 250 °C. They do not further analyze their composition but conclude that
these crystal imperfections inhibit electrical conduction, resulting in a lower efficiency [53].
Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) and Raman spectroscopy do not show any
evidence of oxides or other elements. This narrows down the options for the composition
of the white crystals. One option is that there is too much sulfur powder present in the
graphite box. The unreacted evaporated sulfur powder might redeposit and crystallize
on top of the Sb2S3 grains upon cooling. Another option is that the crystals are Sb2S3
or secondary antimony sulfide phases, which might form in the presence of excess sulfur.
64
==PAGE:82==
5.1 Sb2S3 layer
As the annealing time is increased, these then aggregate at the grain boundaries. This
is supported by the needle-like shape of most of the crystals displayed in Figure 4.21,
which can occur in other crystal systems but is often associated with an orthorhombic or
trigonal crystal system as in Sb2S3 [62], [11].
While these are the most likely options, the crystals could also originate from contaminants
in the sulfur powder, the graphite box, or the tube furnace. While there is no evidence
of oxides in the samples’ EDX data or Raman spectra, these can not be completely ruled
out and should be further analyzed with XRD or XPS measurements. While some grains
feature white crystals on top, others do not (see Figure 4.20. The reason for that is not
clear, but could have something to do with the grain orientations. Since the grains are
most likely randomly oriented, some orientations could be more prone to the precipitation
of these white crystals. Different surface energies of the grains could have an influence here
as well. The only samples that do not show any white crystals are the samples annealed at
250 °C for 30 min (Figure 4.18 (a)) and 300 °C for 1 min (Figure 4.19 (a)). This could be
related to the lower temperature or lower sulfur vapor pressure at that temperature, which
might affect the crystal nucleation or growth kinetics. Further studies should investigate
this hypothesis.
Comparing the Raman spectra of the hot plate annealed samples to the tube furnace
annealed samples and literature, the peak at 157 cm−1 (Figure 4.14 and Figure 4.16) has
a higher intensity for the samples annealed on a hot plate than typically observed [55],
[63], [64]. This could be related to sulfur deficiencies of the hot plate annealed samples,
as bulk Sb has a dominant peak at 150-155 cm−1 [65] [66], [67]. The samples with a
high peak at 157 cm−1 could also be showing a lower degree of crystallization as the
height of the peak tends to decrease with longer annealing times (Figure 4.15 and Figure
4.16). The orientation of the crystallites could also affect the amplitude of the Raman
peaks [68]. While the oxide indicating peak at 258 cm−1 is seen for the blue line (300 °C
for 10 min) of Figure 4.15, it can also be seen for the red line (300 °C for 5 min), only
less dominant. This implies that Sb2O4-crystallites already begin forming after just 5
minutes, indicating that the structures observed on top of the grains in Figure 4.8 (c) are
also Sb2O4 crystallites. In the tube furnace annealed samples, no dominant peaks at 258

or 455 cm−1 can be observed, indicating no formation of Sb2O4. This suggests, that due
to the lower oxygen concentration or the added sulfur powder, inside the tube furnace,
oxide formation is reduced.
Additionally, the peak at 158 cm−1 is less dominant in these samples, which is more similar
to Raman spectra of Sb2S3 in literature [55], [63], [64]. A high peak at 158 cm−1 may
indicate the presence of pure antimony (Sb) and, consequently, sulfur vacancies within
the material. Thus, a low peak suggests fewer sulfur vacancies, potentially due to the
sulfur powder that is added to the graphite box before annealing.
The reflectance measurements reveal that there is no drop in reflectance and thus no
increase in absorption for the hot plate annealed samples. Semiconductors typically ex-
hibit an increase in absorption, leading to a corresponding decrease in reflectance in the
proximity of the band gap [53]. As the hot plate annealed samples do not show this char-
acteristic behavior, it may indicate that they do not behave as a typical semiconductor
regarding their optical properties. Since pure antimony has a similarly flat reflectance
spectrum [69], this could again be related to sulfur deficiencies of the hot plate annealed
samples. The tube furnace annealed samples show a drop in reflectance, indicating a cor-
responding band gap of approximately 1.59 eV. However, compared to the literature, the
reflectance is generally relatively low at energies below the band gap [53]. The observed
increase in reflectance for higher energies is also reported by DeAngelis et al. [53]. In
literature, the band gap value for Sb2S3 has not been clearly established [11]. While most
publications report a band gap between 1.7 - 1.8 eV, the calculated band gap value is in
the range of 1.2 - 1.76 eV, and the experimental value ranges from 1.5 - 2.2 eV [70], [71].
These values show a strong dependence on the synthesis methods, annealing conditions,
and thickness of the film and suggest a complex electronic structure of Sb2S3 [11]. Since a
higher thickness of the film leads to a lower band gap [14] and the Sb2S3 layers deposited
in this thesis are thicker than in most publications, this could explain why the value that
is calculated in this work is on the low end for Sb2S3.

5.2 Spiro-OMeTAD layer
In this section, the results of the spin-coating of the Spiro-OMeTAD HTL layer are dis-
cussed. Different approaches are explored to improve the morphology and uniformity of
the Spiro-OMeTAD layer. These approaches yield a uniform layer with good coverage of
the Sb2S3 layer and close to optimal thickness [18]. However, certain challenges persist,
especially the presence of comets within the layer, which remain a significant issue. While
filtering the solution with 0.45 µm PTFE filters reduces the number and size of comets,
other approaches do not. These include rinsing the substrate, using an antistatic pistol,
and cleaning the spin-coater, which do not have a significant impact. Nonetheless, a new
set of optimized spin-coating parameters are obtained from the experiments, which are
presented in Table 4.1 in chapter 4.
Through the experiments, the source of the comets can be narrowed down to a few
options. These include environmental factors such as dust within the glove box or particles
introduced from the syringe, filter, or vial. Another option is that Spiro-OMeTAD crystals
precipitate during the spin-coating, due to a too-high concentration of Spiro-OMeTAD
powder or too-fast evaporation of the Chlorobenzene solvent. However, this option seems
less likely, as the Spiro-OMeTAD concentration and spin-coating speed are derived directly
from established literature, which does not report any comets within their Spiro-OMeTAD
layers [44], [45], [19].
In 2013, Birnie et al. reported a mechanism in which the spinning of the sample during
spin-coating attracts dust from the environment. Here, the rotating sample displaces a
boundary layer of air radially outward, creating a downward flow of air toward the sample.
Consequently, this air movement results in the sample being subjected to airborne particles
present within the environment [59]. This is the most likely source of particles resulting in
the observed comets within the Spiro-OMeTAD layer. It is thus critical, to carry out the
67
==PAGE:85==
5 Discussion
spin-coating process in a completely dust-free environment such as a cleanroom. Another
option would be to conduct the spin-coating in a laminar flow hood equipped with point
suction.
The thickness of the Spiro-OMeTAD layer at approximately 140 nm is close to the optimal
thickness of 200 - 370 nm [18]. However, the thickness could still be increased by reducing
the spin-coating speed, which also decreases the speed of evaporation. This adjustment
would also result in slower air movement caused by the rotating sample, potentially mini-
mizing dust attraction. Therefore, it is advisable to experiment with a lower spin-coating
speed, such as 2000 rpm, and closely monitor the resulting thickness and the impact on
the number of comets.
Figure 4.29 compares two samples that are spin-coated with Spiro-OMeTAD solutions
of different ages. The sample coated with a two-week-old solution exhibits significant
differences from the one coated with a fresh Spiro-OMeTAD solution.
This indicates
that the solution expires over time. This expiration could be attributed to the moisture
absorption from the glove box environment by the hygroscopic dopant Li-TFSI [18]. If
this effect indeed contributes to the expiration, the solution’s shelf life could be prolonged
by ensuring that the H2O percentage in the glove box always remains below 1.5 %.
In this thesis, the thermal annealing of the as-deposited Spiro-OMeTAD layer is carried
out in a nitrogen-filled glove box. However, it is worth noting that oxidation can increase
the conductivity of Spiro-OMeTAD [18].
Therefore, thermal annealing could also be
carried out in air, as done by Wu et al. in 2023 [72].
5.3 I-V measurements
In this section, the results from the I-V measurements (see Figure 4.35, 4.36 and Table
4.2) are analyzed.
The low short-circuit current (JSC) of the fabricated devices could be caused by multiple
68

factors. Insufficient absorption of incident light by the Sb2S3 absorber layer could be one
contributing factor.
Another reason could be defects and impurities within the device, which can act as re-
combination centers for the charge carriers, thereby limiting JSC. The crystals on top
of the Sb2S3 grains are a likely source of such impurities. Grain boundaries within the
semiconductor layers, especially in polycrystalline materials like Sb2S3, can also act as
barriers to charge carrier flow, contributing to series resistance.
Additionally, non-uniform deposition and surface contamination of semiconductor layers
can also result in regions with higher resistance, resulting in poor-quality interfaces. Here,
especially the comets present in the Spiro-OMeTAD layer may represent impurities and
defects within the HTL, which can act as recombination centers, reducing the number
of charge carriers available for collection and hence lowering JSC. Addressing these de-
fects and impurities in the HTL could significantly increase the JSC and improve device
performance.
A moderate VOC but very low JSC is also reported by Gao et al. in their sputtering-
deposited Sb2S3 top sub-cells for silicon tandem solar cells [39]. Other research on Sb2S3
solar cells, fabricated using the sputtering method, also demonstrate relatively low effi-
ciencies. Luo et al. reported Sb2S3 solar cells with 0.49 % efficiency in 2020, while Gao
et al. achieved an efficiency of 2.41 % in 2019 [15], [14].

6 Conclusion
The present thesis investigates the effect of annealing conditions on the Sb2S3 layer and
spin-coating parameters on the Spiro-OMeTAD layer. The results show that the type of
annealing process and the annealing parameters examined in this work significantly influ-
ence the morphology, crystallinity, and optical properties of the Sb2S3 layer. Especially
the grain growth process is drastically different when comparing hot plate annealing to
tube furnace annealing. Hot plate annealing first leads to flattening out of the amorphous
Sb2S3 layer, before nucleation points and grain growth can be observed. The grains then
start overlapping before showing distinct grain boundaries.
On the other hand, tube furnace annealing first leads to the formation of grain bound-
aries, while still maintaining an amorphous surface morphology. Subsequently, the surface
becomes smoother and begins to display white crystals on top of the grains. For longer
annealing times, these white crystals no longer form on the grains but instead aggregate
at the grain boundaries. The source of the white crystals observed during tube furnace
annealing is not clear, the most likely options are sulfur crystals or secondary antimony
sulfide phases. Nonetheless, further investigation through XRD or XPS measurements
is recommended to accurately identify these substances. No oxide formation is observed
for these samples, likely due to the lower oxygen concentration and the added sulfur
powder.
In line with previous research, this work indicates that Sb2S3 is highly sensitive to ox-
idation, which is dependent on the partial pressure of oxygen, as well as the annealing
temperature and duration [61]. Due to the higher oxygen concentration during hot plate
annealing compared to tube furnace annealing, these samples show a higher degree of
oxidation. It is also observed, that hot plate annealing at high temperatures (400 °C)
or at moderate temperatures for extended durations (300 °C for 10 min) results in the
destruction of the Sb2S3 film. The best surface morphology for hotplate-annealed samples
71
==PAGE:89==
6 Conclusion
is achieved under annealing conditions of either 250 °C for 10 minutes or 350 °C for 25
seconds.
The Raman spectra of both annealing processes show good crystallinity for extended an-
nealing durations. However, most hot-plate annealed samples exhibit a dominant Raman
peak at 158 cm−1, indicating the presence of pure Sb and sulfur vacancies within the
crystal structure. During reflectance measurements, hot plate annealed samples fail to
exhibit an absorption edge, indicating the absence of optical semiconductor properties.
This could be attributed to the samples not being fully crystallized or exhibiting sul-
fur deficiencies. Conversely, tube furnace annealing leads to the expected semiconductor
behavior, demonstrating a band gap of 1.59 eV.
Future work regarding Sb2S3 hot plate annealing should focus on lowering the O2 con-
centration within the glove box to delay oxide formation and enable longer annealing
durations. For future studies on tube furnace annealing, it is recommended to conduct
XRD or XPS analysis to determine the origin of the white crystals found on top of the
grains.
XRD should also be used to compare the preferred grain orientation in tube
furnace and hot plate annealed samples.
The optimization of the Spiro-OMeTAD layer involves static deposition and a low-speed
spreading step. Additionally, the amount of solution used for spin-coating is increased
and the solution is filtered before deposition. It is recommended to create the solution
on the same day as the deposition as it has been observed that the solution expires
after approximately two weeks, which can lead to poor depositions. During spin-coating,
the rotation of the sample can attract environmental dust. Due to the spinning sample
displacing air, a downward airflow is created that brings airborne particles onto the surface
of the sample. This phenomenon is described by Birnie et al. (2013) and is the most likely
cause of particle-induced comets in the Spiro-OMeTAD layer [59]. To address this issue, it
is recommended to perform spin-coating in a dust-free environment, such as a cleanroom,
or under a laminar flow hood equipped with point suction.
The solar cells are fabricated using Sb2S3 layers that are annealed in the tube furnace at
350 °C for 5 min. I-V measurements of the fabricated solar cells show a moderate VOC
72
==PAGE:90==
(0.43 V) but a low JSC (0.048 mA/cm2) and thus low efficiency of 0.004 %. The low
JSC is likely caused by defects and impurities within the device, leading to poor-quality
interfaces and recombination of charge carriers. The sources of defects and impurities
are the white crystals observed on top of annealed Sb2S3 grains and comets within the
Spiro-OMeTAD layer. These need to be addressed in future work, which could lead to
significantly improved device performance.
This work contributes to a better understanding of the crystallization process of Sb2S3
under different annealing conditions. This is vital due to the complex defect properties
of the crystallized material. Successfully addressing these defects could pave the way for
developing highly efficient tandem solar cells.
