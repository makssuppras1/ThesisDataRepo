PREFACE
As a part of the requirements to achieve Master of Science degree, thesis "A Dynamic
Study Model for Frequency Stability in the Future Danish Transmission System" was
conducted during period 2nd February to 26th July 2018. The thesis consist of 30 ECTS
points and has been written at the Center of Electric Power and Energy, which is part of
the Department of Electrical Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark.
The project will lead to the construction of a dynamic study model of the Danish
transmission system, suﬃciently detailed to study frequency disturbances in time domain.
The model will be used to investigate participation of HVDC systems in the control of
active power and frequency.

ABSTRACT
Growing share of renewable energy generation in Denmark’s electrical grid creates new
challenges, thus in this project a dynamic model applicable for frequency stability studies
of Danish electrical grid is introduced to study frequency phenomena. The project is
carried out using professional software Siemens PSS-E.
The model relies on Energinet’s published Ten Year Network Development Plan model,
which provides Load Flow model of danish electrical transmission grid. Model does assume
that generation and load from distribution grid are aggregated to appropriate transmission
voltage level. Interconnections to other countries represented as simpliﬁed country models.
The Study model is further supplemented with dynamic data, where synchronous
generators are represented with sixth-order generator model containing standard parameters.
Interconnections to external, Continental Europe and Nordic, systems are modeled from
publicly available data. Afterwards, dynamic study model is validated to theoretical
calculations and known frequency disturbances, available publicly.
After dynamic study model is constructed, analysis of HVDC Emergency Power Supply
control is investigated, subject to predetermined load loss in Western and Eastern grid
areas. The objective of the analysis is to identify how the system reacts to load loss
under diﬀerent wind power penetration levels and how HVDC EPS control could improve
frequency response of the system.
The discussion of eﬀect of wind penetration level in electrical system provides reasoning
for frequency response related to system inertia and stiﬀness. In addition, simulation
results conclude that HVDC EPS control do provide frequency support depending on
HVDC capacity capabilities. Further, it was noted that varying EPS ramp rate shows
improvement in frequency nadir, however under certain conditions, it stress system to
instability.

INTRODUCTION
This chapter is an introduction to the report, which forms a short view of this thesis
content. It focuses on reports focal points, such as - thesis background, key problem and
conditions.
1.1
Background
The initiative to cut CO2 emission had considerable impact on electrical energy systems.
Over the period of 7 years, global cumulative oﬀshore wind energy increased from 4.1 GW
to 18.8 GW. In 2017 Denmark alone had installed 1.27 GW of oﬀshore wind power. The
trend of wind power penetration is forecasted to be positive with 30% increase compared
to 2016 installed capacity levels. [1]
The latter process cause a shift in generation from synchronous generators to electronic-
based non-synchronous generation. System wise, generation shift will cause inertia decrease,
which leaves electrical energy systems prone to disturbances in the grid.
1.2
Formulation of the problem
Grid transformation from synchronous machines to non-synchronous generation causes
inertia decrease in electrical systems. The eﬀect of which have a negative consequences,
such as system sensitivity to smaller disturbance. In order to analyze and examine behavior
of the system in low-inertia electrical system, a model representing Danish electrical grid
for frequency phenomena is required. Based on this the following question will be addressed
during the project:
• What impact wind energy penetration level has on frequency disturbance in the grid?
• How sensitive is the system in relation to diﬀerent wind energy penetration levels?
• What is the potential of High Voltage Direct Current link contribution to support
frequency?
1.3

phenomena

1.4
Methodology, scope and conditions
Project is based on Ten Year Network Development Plan (TYNDP) model from Danish
Transmission system (TSO) operator Energinet, published in Excel format. Thus, project
include data revision and ﬁltering in Excel software. Simulations and system analysis is
carried out with Power System Simulator for Engineering, also known as PSS-E, software.
Data conversion from Excel to PSS-E is done via Python programming language.
The scope of application for dynamic study model is to represent frequency phenomena
of Danish electricity grid. Model should be capable to reﬂect the impact of synchronous
interconnected systems, in particular Continental Europe(CE) and Nordic system. In
consequence, the usage of selected dynamic models should be suﬃcient to represent:
• frequency transients in relation to power imbalance
• CE and Nordic synchronous zones contribution to frequency deviation
• individual transient behavior of central power plants
As frequency can be considered to be global phenomena within synchronous zone, model
is not developed to represent local phenomena like voltage transients, protection of the
system etc.
The model will rely on publicly available data, in particular TYNDP model of the Danish
transmission system for 2020. The data of the system is given as Excel ﬁle of load ﬂow
simulation. Fore mentioned model is reduced order model, meaning that generation and
load are aggregated to transmission voltage levels. As mentioned previously, transient
stability of the system is out of the scope, therefore dynamic system modeling for central
power plants include standard generator model with turbine-governor and excitation models.
For that, dynamic system modeling will rely on Kundur’s book [2] and other available
data.
1.5
Prior Work
Project scope is frequency stability analysis, therefore project essentially based on physics
of frequency stability documented in Kundur’s book. Prior work related to the topic
include Ensto-E report on Nordic system inertia [3], which focuses on estimation of systems
inertia in Nordic countries. Also, it includes frequency response of Nordic power system,
which is used to validate the model. In addition, Entso-E report on frequency stability
criteria in continental Europe [4] conducted in March 2016 analyses impact of reduced
system inertia and evaluation of frequency stability.

THEORY BACKGROUND
Electrical power system objective in a broad sense is to deliver electrical power to the
customers within predetermined quality requirements (i.e. voltage deviation). This is
achieved by maintaining balance between electrical energy generation and demand at any
moment. Formerly mentioned generation-production balance evaluates electrical system’s
stability, deﬁned as ability to remain in operating equilibrium point and regain to same
state after disturbance. The ratio between fore mentioned quantities, power generation
and demand, is called system frequency and no doubly it is one of the most important
references that indicate system’s stability and quality.
Acceptable frequency deviations and limits are stated in grid codes which vary from
country to country. In Denmark during normal grid operation frequency must be in range
of 50Hz ± 0.1Hz. At times of higher frequency deviations, e.g. if frequency drops below
49.9Hz, frequency controlled disturbance reserve, or primary frequency control, is activated.
In particular, this includes generator primary response and HVDC link Emergency Power
Supply (EPS). [5]
System’s operating equilibrium point is changed due to abnormal grid operation, i.e. an
unexpected trip of a large generator or a connection of a load. In latter case, it causes
the imbalance of generation-demand. This results in a sharp change in frequency which is
followed by response from generation to counter act this. In a failed attempt to maintain
frequency within safe range, system might experience a frequency collapse or total electrical
energy system blackout.
Frequency response subject to power imbalance, is followed by response from synchronous
generators, thus generator’s dynamic response cannot be neglected.
To discuss generator’s response, consider a set-up where generating unit is supplying
power to the load. In case of a load change, instantaneous change in electrical torque
will be present, causing mismatch between electrical torque and mechanical torque. The
mismatch, frequently called accelerating torque Ta, will result in generator’s speed deviation.

SYSTEM MODELING
This chapter describes system modeling part of thesis. It covers topics such as Danish
Transmission System Operator (TSO) provided load ﬂow model overview of Danish
transmission electricity grid, presents an overview of data processing for simulation tool
PSS-E.
The pivotal content of this chapter describes a modeling approach used for load ﬂow model
and on top of that - dynamic simulation model for frequency stability phenomena.
Model is done by revising and expanding Excel load ﬂow model data, provided by Danish
TSO, and then converting Excel data to ﬁle format acceptable for simulation tool PSS-E.
Conversion from Excel ﬁle to simulation ﬁle is done with Python programming language.
3.1
PSS-E data processing
PSS-E is system modeling tool for electrical power systems. It has a wide variety of analysis,
including: power ﬂow, dynamics, short circuit, contingency analysis, optimal power ﬂow,
voltage stability, transient stability simulation etc.. To process this vast amount of data, it
uses speciﬁc way of data processing.
Program can be categorized into two structures - working database and master module.
Working data base is a large working ﬁle containing a complete positive-, negative-, zero-
sequence and dynamics representation of the user’s power system. Master module contains
power system analysis functions. Each analysis function contains speciﬁc calculation
algorithms to perform selected analysis. The data used for analysis is taken from working
database and results are presented in either output screen of device or program console.

3.2.1
Model Introduction
Every year Energinet, Danish transmission system operator, publishes static data of
Western and Eastern Danish transmission system electrical grid for 132, 150 and 400 kV
level. This data is available in Excel spread-sheet format online. [cite]
Excel spread-sheet contains information about:

• Bus data
• Branch data
• Transformer data
• Generation data
• Load data
• Shunt data
Each of above mention data types are separated into sheets, containing relevant information
for load ﬂow studies, i.e. Generator data contains rated power,voltage of the machine,
active and reactive power limits and, if machine is central power plant, controlled bus
number and voltage reference. Also, The data comprises a balanced Load Flow case for
the year 2020.
Provided Load Flow data contains reduced-order system model due to assumptions and
voltage limitations. Following assumptions apply for this model:
• Renewable Energy Sources(RES) generation is aggregated to appropriate transmission
system substations based on generation type: Gas, Wind, Solar, Hydro
• Distribution system load is aggregated to appropriate transmission system substation
• Oﬀshore wind farms are modeled as aggregated PQ-controlled generators
• Country interconnections are modeled as PQ loads connected to Danish AC system
busbars
These limitations and assumptions create a simpliﬁed transmission grid model, where
distribution grid is not considered. On the other hand, exclusion of distribution grid reduce
system model and requires less computational capabilities.
3.2.2
Model Overview
The Danish power system comprises of two synchronous areas - Western and Eastern.
The Western part posses transmission lines located in Jutland and Funen. The system is
connected by 400 kV and 220 kV AC lines to Germany, therefore it is synchronized with
Continental Europe (synch. system name - UCTE). The Eastern part posses transmission
lines in Capital Area of Copenhagen (Zealand) and South located islands. The system
is connected by 400 kV adn 132 kV AC lines to Sweden and is synchronized with Nordic
power system (synch. system name - NORDEL). Two synchronized zones are connected
by line commutated HVDC link Storebælt (discussed in ch. 5).
The Western system (DK1) has HVDC interconnections to Sweden, Konti-Skan Pole 1
(360 MW) and Konti-Skan Pole 2 (360 MW), and four HVDC interconnection to Norway,
Skagerrak Pole 1 (250 MW), Pole 2 (250 MW), Pole 3 (500 MW) and Pole 4 (700 MW).
Besides that, in 2019 HVDC interconnection to Netherlands will be commisioned - Cobra
cable (700 MW). The Eastern system (DK2) has one HVDC interconnection to Germany -

Prior dynamic simulation run, two procedures must take place - loads conversion and

Load model conversion
In Load Flow simulation data loads are modeled as static loads, represented with constant
power. The model can be expressed with formulas 3.33 and 3.34.
P = P0(V )a

In static load model, voltage dependency of the load is represent by exponential factor
a and b. Exponential factors are equal to 0, when loads are modeled as constant power.

in system. This load representation leads to numerical errors due to the fact that system
cannot support loads with constant power.

as constant impedance (Z), constant current (I) and constance power P load. The model
is deﬁned by 3.35 and 3.36 equations.

5.4.2
Alternative Modeling Approach
HVDC link was changed by two loads, each load having the power of HVDC Load Flow
model in solved Load Flow analysis.
This was utilized by python automation ﬁle reading HVDC Load Flow data from solved
case with command psspy.a2trmdccplx and obtaining rectiﬁer and inverter complex power.
These values were assigned to the loads and HVDC link was purged from the model with
psspy.purg2dc command.
To initiate HVDC link Emergency Power Supply (EPS) during frequency disturbance, a
controller implemented, which overrides load active power at predetermined time-steps.
PSS-E dynamic simulation time step is 0.008333 s and EPS capacity of Storebælt is 18
MW with 10MW/s ramp rate. Product of simulation time step and ramp rate values give
power increment dP by which load is overwritten each time step, when frequency breaches
predetermined threshold value.

CONCLUSION
The purpose of this thesis was to create a dynamic study model for frequency stability
studies, which would represent frequency phenomena with satisfactory accuracy. Later,
model shall be used for testing Emergency Power Supply of HVDC link, varying capacity
and ramp rates.
8.1
Results Discussion
The dynamic study model is constructed on reduced order Load Flow model from Energinet.
This impose that response of generators is not completely accurate due to generation
aggregation to transmission voltage levels.Further, transmission line reduction could have
eﬀect on transmission losses and result in diﬀerent steady state frequency values. Regardless,
Load Flow analysis results do show satisfactory bus voltage distribution throughout the
model in all distinguished scenarios (see Appendix).
Lack of information on dynamic parameters for generators led to generic modeling instead
of individual generator representation. As a result, inertia in the system is distributed
equally, which eliminates the eﬀect of fault location. Further, detailed generation data for
scenarios was not available, thus generation was distributed based on maximum active
power limit, which create more stable system in terms of power transfer. In HWLL scenario,
the excess of generation , where 2363 MW is transferred to Nordic countries in DK1 area,
creates substantially larger bus voltages in the Load Flow analysis.
Assumptions stated above does not have signiﬁcant eﬀect on frequency stability, however
they are of great importance for voltage and transient stability assessment. This could be
based on model validation, where theoretical calculation reﬂect simulation results with
low error values. However, in DK1 system high oscillatory behavior is observed when
generators do swing against CE generator, presumably due to lack of damping in the
system.
As expected, wind penetration do have an indirect eﬀect on frequency deviation - higher
wind penetration reduces systems rotational energy capacity resulting in higher steady
state frequency and nadir. System’s rotational energy capacity reduces due to smaller
number of synchronous generators connected, in result regulation constant is reduced.
This is justiﬁed by results, where HWLL and LWLL scenarios do have higher number
of disconnected generators, resulting in decreased system stiﬀness and rotational energy,
hence increased frequency nadir and steady-state frequency. On contrary, high load cases
require more power, hence more synchronous generators are connected,thus increasing
rotational energy capacity and system stiﬀness, resulting in more robust systems frequency
wise.
55
==PAGE:66==
8. Conclusion
Result with HVDC EPS control do show improved frequency response. EPS response with
18MW and 10MW/s ramp rate does improve frequency slightly due to 1.5-2% capacity
compared to the fault. To observe more signiﬁcant improvement, capacity must be increased
to 180 MW. With increased capacity, steady-state frequency is decreased due to increased
system stiﬀness. However, frequency nadir is more aﬀected by EPS ramp rate rather than
capacity. It was noted, that EPS with 100MW/s ramp reaches higher capacity values
before nadir, compared to 10MW/s ramp. This minimized the power imbalance seen from
generators and RoCoF. Even with increased EPS capacity, steady-state frequency did not
reach values below 0.0004 p.u., thus frequency restoration to nominal value should be done
by secondary reserves.
During EPS simulation with 180MW and 100MW/s capacity in LWLL scenario, DK1 area
do encounter instability due to increased ramp rate of EPS. Result showed, that CE and
DK1 area generators periodically swinging against each other and lose synchronism. It
was decided that instability occurs due to insuﬃcient damping in the system. It can be
seen that additional damping did improve oscillatory behavior and ramp rate could be
increased to 70MW/s with stable response. These results do suggest, that EPS cannot
be activated or should be activate with lower ramp rates, when generators experience
oscillatory behavior.
Implemented controller did mimic the response of EPS from HVDC link, but some
drawbacks were noted. For instance, with low ramp rates, EPS cannot reach maximum
capacity of 180MW due to small time span when frequency is above 0.001 p.u. on nominal
frequency base. In addition, EPS can be triggered by sharp frequency measurements.
Therefore, EPS controller should contain a moving average ﬁlter, which measures frequency
at several points and when output from ﬁlter breaches the threshold, EPS is activated.
Further, EPS controller should ramp until frequency threshold of 0.0004 p.u. is reached.
8.2
Future work
Load Flow Model Improvement
Size of the model and electrical components modeling approach in PSS-E makes Load Flow
model an intricate part of this project. Results are far from what one might call ideal,
thus in-depth view of Load Flow model could be possible future task. It could analyze,
why Energinet model did not converge in the ﬁrst place and how bus voltage and branch
loading could be kept within limits.
Individual Generator Modeling
Project rely on generic generator parameters used for dynamic modeling. This could be
expanded to represent individual generator model parameters based on generator type
and size. This would diﬀerentiate inertial response and transient response of generator to
one aggregated system’s response. Also, excitation limiters could be provided to represent
more detailed capability curve of generators.
Detailed Scenario Creation
Scenarios were created using aggregated historic data from Danish TSO. Values were
assigned by generation type based on maximum active power limit share to total limit, the
eﬀect of which is localization of generation across the country. To create more realistic
56

scenarios, aggregated historic data could be connected to meteorological data. Further,
economic considerations cannot be disregarded and should be taken into account when
deciding which synchronous plants must be disconnected.
Dynamic HVDC Modeling
During thesis major challenge appeared to be dynamic modeling of HVDC link.
To
represent impact of HVDC link under power imbalance with satisfactory response, dynamic
model must be implemented. Since model is highly dependent on system’s design, this
could be a part of future work. HVDC representation could take one step further and
model all HVDC interconnections.
Future EPS Controller Simulations
Thesis investigated theoretical eﬀect of Storebælt HVDC link adn did not include other
links. It would be of high interest to simulated Emergency Power Supply simulations
using all HVDC interconnections and analyze frequency behavior under these conditions.
Controller could be further improved based on suggestions, provided in conclusions. In
addition, EPS simulation with all HVDC would require an optimization of EPS controller,
otherwise frequency reading might be too time consuming for several frequency channels
in *.out ﬁle.
Investigation of Oscillatory Generators Behavior
In ch. 7 LWLL case with EPS capacity of 180MW and ramp rate 100MW/s did show
oscillatory behavior between CE and DK1 generators. Presumably, this behavior was
caused by insuﬃcient system damping leading to small-signal instability. This could be
validated by adapting model for small-signal stability analysis. As an option, oscillatory
behavior could be improved by introducing Power System Stabilizer and results could be
compared to presented ones.
Wind Power Modeling
In the report, wind power plants were modeled as negative PQ loads instead of generators
with HVDC interconnection.
This does neglect dynamic response from wind farms.
Although, frequency stability results would not be eﬀected much, it would introduce
higher degree of accuracy to the model, especially regarding voltage and transient stability
phenomenas.
