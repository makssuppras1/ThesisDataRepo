Abstract
Retinal diseases such as Retinitis Pigmentosa (RP) and Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)
cause progressive vision loss due to the degeneration of photoreceptor cells. In this context, sub-
retinal photovoltaic (PV) implants offer a promising approach to restore vision by replacing the lost
photoreceptors and converting near-infrared (NIR) light into electrical signals that stimulate the re-
maining retinal neurons. This thesis presents the design and cleanroom fabrication of thin PV arrays
for subretinal implantation and investigates methods for achieving effective pixel isolation.
The fabrication process begins with a preliminary study of KOH etching for device thinning. The goal
is to determine the optimal dimensions of the membrane for easy handling and implantation. Three
membrane sizes (3x3mm2, 1.5x1.5mm2, 0.3x0.3mm2) are evaluated, and 1.5x1.5mm2 is selected as
the most mechanically stable and practical for handling. Devices with two pixel sizes: 40µm and
200µm, are then fabricated with a target thickness of 50µm. These thin devices demonstrate mechan-
ical robustness and satisfy the ﬁrst objective of the project. However, the electrical characterization
of the ﬁnal implants is inconclusive, as the pixels do not behave as expected. Further investigation is
required to identify and resolve the possible issues.
The second key objective of this work is to achieve electrical isolation between adjacent pixels to
reduce crosstalk. To this end, multiple strategies are explored, varying in trench geometry, etching
processes, and oxide ﬁlling methods. The ﬁnal trench layout is based on a compact hexagonal pat-
tern. After parameter testing, three main strategies are implemented in the ﬁnal device: (1) widened
trench vertices with fast etching, (2) widened vertices with slow etching, and (3) no widened vertices
with slow etching. The ﬁrst conﬁguration shows a smoother trench proﬁle, better oxide ﬁlling, and
superior mechanical integrity after thinning. As a result, a thin device with pixel isolation is achieved.
Trench integrity is veriﬁed through optical inspection after thinning. However, electrical characteri-
zation remains incomplete.
Overall, this study demonstrates a viable cleanroom process for producing thin PV retinal implants
and provides a comparative evaluation of trench isolation strategies, laying the basis for future devel-
opment towards fully functional, thin, and pixel-isolated subretinal implants.

Introduction
1.1
Retinal diseases
1.1.1
Background and motivation
Around the world, more than 200 million people are affected by severe vision impairment, which is
deﬁned as the partial or total loss of visual perception [1]. The main leading causes of vision im-
pairment and blindness at a global level include refractive errors (88.4 million people), cataracts (94
million people), age-related macular degeneration (8 million), glaucoma (7.7 million), and diabetic
retinopathy (3.9 million) [2].
It is interesting to highlight the variation in the causes of visual impairment between developed and
developing countries. Historically, retinal diseases, such as age-related macular degeneration (AMD)
have had low priority for blindness prevention in developing countries [3]. That is partially because
AMD is not the main visual impairment condition in these regions. But also, because the technology
needed to restore the vision of AMD patients remains expensive and not yet fully reliable.
Instead of retinal diseases, the primary causes of blindness in developing countries are cataract, glau-
coma, and diabetic retinopathy. When countries grow wealthier, fewer people lose their sight due to
cataract, as cataract surgery becomes more available and accessible to the population [3]. Conse-
quently, in wealthier countries, glaucoma and cataract remain common and relevant conditions, but
most cases of blindness are attributed to retinal diseases.
Given these trends, retinal diseases have become a signiﬁcant and increasing problem worldwide [3].
Therefore, studying possible solutions to retinal diseases is essential for developing effective treat-
ments and making implants and therapies more accessible to everyone.
1.1.2
Retinal degeneration
As denoted by the name, retinal diseases are conditions that affect the retina, the innermost light-
sensitive layer of the eye [4]. When light enters the eye, it gets focused onto the retina by the cornea
and the lens. The central part of the retina is called the macula, which is the region affected in AMD
[5].
The retina is composed of several layers (Figure 1.1) . The primary light-sensing layer contains the
photoreceptors, which are the cells responsible for converting light into electrical signals [6]. These
photoreceptors are placed at the back layer of the retina and come in two types: rods and cones.
Rods are sensitive to low-light and provide a monochromatic (black-and-white) vision; they do not
mediate color. Cones, on the other hand, are responsible for color vision, and they work best under
well-lit conditions [4].
When the light reaches the retina, the electrical signals generated by the photoreceptors are processed
by the intermediate retinal neurons and then transmitted to the optic nerve by the retinal ganglion
cells [7]. Finally, the signals travel along the optic nerve to the brain, where the visual image is recon-
structed.

If the photoreceptors degenerate, light can no longer be converted into electrical signals. As a result,
the visual information can not be processed by the brain, leading to progressive visual impairment.
This process is the underlying mechanism of two major retinal diseases: AMD and retinitis pigmen-
tosa (RP).
1.1.3
Age-related macular degeneration
AMD is the most common retinal disease and a leading cause of vision loss in people over the age of
50. It is characterized by the progressive degeneration of photoreceptors in the macula, the central
region of the retina responsible for sharp, central vision [9][10]. As a result, patients with AMD expe-
rience blurred or distorted central vision, making it difﬁcult to recognize faces or read [11]. Generally,
peripheral vision remains unaffected.
The degeneration of photoreceptors in AMD is mostly associated with aging. However, other con-
tributing factors are genetic predisposition and lifestyle habits, such as smoking or poor diets [9] [12].
There are two primary forms of AMD: dry and wet (Figure 1.2). Dry AMD is the most prevalent type,
accounting for approximately 90% of the cases. It is characterized by the thinning (atrophy) of mac-
ular tissues and the gradual loss of photoreceptors cells [9]. An important factor in dry AMD is the
accumulation of metabolic waste products from photoreceptors, leading to the formation of drusen,
tiny yellow deposits composed by lipids and proteins that accumulate beneath the retina [11]. These
deposits alter the retinal structure and function, contributing to the degeneration of the macula.
In contrast, wet AMD is less common but more severe. It occurs when abnormal blood vessels are
formed beneath the macula [9][11]. These vessels are fragile and prone to leaking blood and ﬂuid into
the retinal layers. This leakage can lead to swelling, bleeding, and scarring in the macula, resulting in
rapid vision loss. Patients with wet AMD may see dark spots in their central vision.

In conclusion, as life expectancy continues to rise, the proportion of elderly people in the global pop-
ulation is increasing. Which in turn results in an increase in AMD cases. Unfortunately, although
some treatment strategies have shown improvement in wet AMD cases (intravitreal injections of anti-
vascular endothelial growth factor), these therapies require repeated administration, are not curative,
and remain too expensive [12].
1.1.4
Retinitis pigmentosa
The second major retinal disease is RP, which is an inherited disorder that typically begins in child-
hood or adolescence and causes the progressive loss of peripheral vision [14]. People with RP experi-
ence tunnel vision, meaning they can only see objects directly in front of them [6].
RP primarily affects the rod cells, which are responsible for vision in lowlight or nighttime conditions.
Therefore, as these cells gradually degenerate, patients experience difﬁculties in seeing in the dark
[14]. Over time, the loss of rods also impacts cone cells, which are responsible for color perception.
Rods normally secrete protective substances that help maintain cone cell function, hence, when rods
are lost, cones begin to degenerate as well. As a result, peripheral vision is lost.
In advanced stages, patients may only have a small area of central vision (Figure 1.3). Moreover, in
some late-stage cases, the macula can swell, a condition known as macular edema, which would
degenerate central vision too [6].
Nowadays, there is no cure for RP. However, ongoing research aims to develop genetic and cellular
therapies to slow the disease progression and restore vision [6].

Overall, in both AMD and RP, the optic nerve and the neural pathways connecting the eye to the
visual cortex in the brain remain intact and functional. As previously discussed, the primary problem
lies in the degeneration of photoreceptors. When the photoreceptors are damaged or lost, the retina
can no longer convert light into electrical signals. This opens the door to the development of retinal
implants.
1.2
Retinal Implants
Retinal implants are devices designed to replace degenerated or lost photoreceptors in the retina.
Their purpose is to detect and convert incoming light into electrical signals, enhancing visual per-
ception by directly stimulating the remaining retinal neurons [16][17]. Retinal implants can stimulate
neurons through two main methods: electrical stimulation or photovoltaic implants [18].
1.2.1
Electrical stimulation
In electrical stimulation prostheses, the system consists of two components: an internal implant and
an external device. As the implanted microelectrode array cannot convert light signals into electrical
impulses on its own, it relies on input from the external component [18][19].
The external device typically consists of a camera-based system, like a small camera mounted on a
pair of glasses, which captures images from the user’s surroundings [18]. This raw image data is pro-
cessed by an external processing unit to enhance the areas of interest and reduce the background
interference [19]. The processed visual information is then converted into electrical stimulation sig-
nals, which are transmitted to the internal implant. Usually, this transmission is done wirelessly via
inductive coupling, eliminating the need for transcutaneous wires.
The internal component, often a periocular or intraocular implant, receives the electrical stimulation
pulses and transmits them to the microelectrode array implanted in the retina [19]. Once the signal

1.2.2
Photovoltaic implants
To avoid the implementation of complex periocular devices, several studies have focused on retinal
stimulation by photovoltaic (PV) implants. Theoretically, in this case, there is no need for two com-
ponents since the photodiode electrode array works as both the light sensor and the electric signal
generator [19]. These photodiodes replace the function of degenerated photoreceptors by detecting
the incident light and converting it into electrical signals used to stimulate retinal neurons.
However, in most designs, the ambient light is not powerful enough to generate a sufﬁcient electrical
signal to stimulate the neurons. Therefore, external components such as a power source, a camera,
a processing unit, or a near-infrared (NIR) light projector are used to amplify the incoming light and
ensure enough stimulation intensity [18][19].
In this project, the designed PV implants are intended to work with a camera mounted on a pair
of glasses, which processes visual scenes and transforms them into NIR light (Figure 1.5). NIR light
(880−940 nm) selectively targets the artiﬁcial photoreceptors while remaining invisible to any resid-
ual natural photoreceptor [22].
Using NIR light offers several advantages. Since it is invisible to the human eye, it is more comfortable
and less distracting for the user. Additionally, NIR light does not stimulate residual photoreceptors,
which prevents interference between natural vision and artiﬁcial stimulation [18][23]. Lastly, NIR has
a higher tissue penetration compared to visible light, making it possible to deliver more energy to the
implant [16].

1.2.3
Advantages of PV retinal implants
PV retinal implants offer several advantages over electric stimulation. One signiﬁcant beneﬁt is that
PV implants do not require implanted cables, thereby reducing the complexity of surgical implanta-
tion and improving overall safety. Additionally, the absence of wired connections helps preserve nat-
ural eye movement by avoiding mechanical constraints or surgical impact on the extraocular muscles
[19][18].
Another advantage, in theory, is that PV implants do not require an external camera, as they are ca-
pable of directly converting incident light into electrical signals. This could preserve the natural co-
ordination between eye movements and visual perception [19]. As a result, the image projected onto
the retina would remain aligned with the user’s gaze direction. However, as previously discussed, PV
implants often require light ampliﬁcation or projection systems, which typically involve an external
camera. These camera-based systems can lead to mismatches, as the eye may be pointing in a differ-
ent direction than the camera [18].
An important consideration is the quality of restored vision in patients. People with retinal implants
perceive light patterns of monochromatic dots or phosphenes. Phosphenes are luminous ﬂoating
stars, swirls, spirals, and other shapes that can appear when the eyes are closed tightly or pressed with
the ﬁngers [24]. In general, the quality of the perceived image depends on several factors, including
the number and density of electrodes and photodiodes on the implants, the stimulation system used,
and the patient’s ability to discern grayscale levels [18].
Finally, it is important to highlight that vision restored by retinal prostheses differs substantially from
natural vision and offers limited resolution and contrast [18]. As a result, rehabilitative training and
extensive practice is needed to optimize functional outcomes.

1.2.4.1
Epiretinal prostheses
In epiretinal prostheses, the electrode array is implanted on the inner surface of the retina, directly
over the ganglion cell layer [17]. This conﬁguration allows for direct stimulation of ganglion cells,
which are the retinal neurons that receive visual information from photoreceptors and transmit it to
the brain via the optic nerve. However, this placement also gives rise to some problems, since direct
stimulation of ganglion cell axons can distort the perceived phosphenes [18].
Epiretinal prostheses are typically used with electrical stimulation, as these implants require an ex-
ternal power battery system [17]. Given that the bipolar cell layer is bypassed, signal processing elec-
tronics are required to convert the camera-captured images into suitable stimulation patterns for the
ganglion cells [25].
The most widely used epiretinal implant is ARGUS II (Second Sight Medical Products, USA) [26],
which consists of a 60-channel microelectrode array. This device has been implanted in over 350
patients worldwide and demonstrated promising clinical results [18].
In 2019, production of ARGUS II ceased as Second Sight shifted its focus to a cortical visual implant
known as ORION. Other epiretinal devices include IRIS II (Pixium Vision) and Epiret (University of
Aachen) [18].

1.2.4.2
Subretinal implants
Subretinal prostheses are implanted between the photoreceptor layer and the retinal pigment epithe-
lium [25]. Since they are placed closest to the damaged or lost photoreceptors they aim to replace,
these implants directly stimulate bipolar cells, which are the retinal neurons that transmit visual sig-
nals from photoreceptors to ganglion cells.
Subretinal implants can be passive, which means that they are powered only by ambient light, or ac-
tive, which use external power and ampliﬁers [17]. As previously discussed, passive implants have
lower effectiveness because of the insufﬁcient energy provided by natural light, resulting in weak
electrical signals that are not strong enough to meaningfully stimulate the retina. Therefore, passive
implants are rarely used.
One of the main advantages of subretinal implants is the fact that they provide a more natural visual
experience [17]. This is because the implants are placed on the site of the damaged photoreceptors
and preserve the natural retinal pathways. As the implants move naturally with the eye, users can
interact with their surroundings ﬂuidly and intuitively. Additionally, subretinal implants offer higher
resolution images than other prosthesis types [17].
The active subretinal implant Alpha IMS (Institute for Microelectronics Stuttgart)[27], was tested in
multiple RP patients, enabling improvements in light detection, motion perception, reading, and ob-
ject recognition [25]. However, only a few patients demonstrated an improvement in visual acuity.
Visual acuity (VA) is deﬁned as the ability of the eye to distinguish shapes and details at a standard
distance. Snellen scale is often used to describe visual acuity, where normal vision is deﬁned as 20/20,
meaning that the patient can see at 20 feet what a person with normal vision sees at 20 feet. An acuity
of 20/200 is deﬁned as blind, meaning that the patient sees at 20 feet what a normal person sees at
200 feet [28]. In the Alpha IMS subretinal implant, the VA ranged from 20/2000 to 20/500 [29].
The most advanced subretinal implant to date is PRIMA, a subretinal PV implant by Pixium Vision
(Paris, France), speciﬁcally designed to treat AMD. The PRIMA implant measures 2x2 mm2 and is
30 µm thick, containing 378 hexagonal pixels [29]. Each pixel is 100 µm wide with a circular active
electrode at the center and a return electrode along the hexagonal perimeter.
In initial feasibility trials, PRIMA implants have demonstrated both safety and efﬁcacy, achieving
prosthetic VAs between 20/438 and 20/550 [29]. In addition, patients reported simultaneous per-
ception of central prosthetic vision added to the natural peripheral vision [30].
Even though these are really promising results, for a broader clinical adoption, prosthetic VA must ex-
ceed the level of residual peripheral vision, which is typically around 20/400 in AMD patients [28][29].

the size of planar bipolar pixels below 75 µm requires NIR light intensities that exceed the safety lim-

Suprachoroidal implants
Finally, sprachoroidal implants are those placed in the space between the choroid, a thin vascular

Photovoltaic theory
Since the main focus of this project is the fabrication of a photovoltaic retinal implant, the following
chapter covers the basic theory of photovoltaics. This background may help understand the working
principle of PV implants and also introduce the characterization methods used to evaluate the ﬁnal

2.1
Semiconductors and doping
2.1.1
Semiconductor models
Diodes, transistors, solar cells, and many other electronic devices rely on semiconductors due to their
unique conductivity properties. Semiconductors can be deﬁned as those materials that behave like
insulators at low temperatures, but become conductive when energy, such as heat or light, is applied
[34][35].
The most widely used semiconductor in the electronics industry is silicon, followed by gallium ar-
senide and germanium [36]. Silicon is a Group IV element, meaning it has four electrons in its outer
shell that can form covalent bonds with four neighboring silicon atoms. Through this electron shar-
ing, the valence shell of each atom is completed, resulting in a stable crystal structure [37][38].
At very low temperatures (close to absolute zero), these bonds remain intact, and silicon behaves as
an insulator. However, at high temperatures, particularly those at which solar cells work, electrons
can gain enough energy to break their bonds and escape [39]. When this happens, electrons are free
to move through the crystal lattice, allowing electrical conduction. The vacant positions left behind
by these electrons are called "holes" and behave like positively charged carriers. This description is
known as the bond model [34].
Alternatively, the band model describes semiconductor behavior in terms of energy levels [34]. In this
model, semiconductors have two energy bands: the valence and the conduction band. The valence
band (EV ) is deﬁned as the outermost electron orbital of an atom that electrons can occupy [40].
While the conduction band (EC) is the band that contains the energy states electrons can occupy
when excited [39]. Electrons in the conduction band are considered free.
The energy difference between the valence band and the conduction band is known as the band gap
(EG) [41]. This concept represents the minimum energy required to excite an electron from the va-
lence band to the conduction band, where it becomes free to contribute to electrical conduction. In
terms of the bond model, this excitation corresponds to breaking the covalent bond [34].
The difference between insulators, semiconductors, and conductors (such as metals) can be explained
by the size of their band gap [33][36]. Insulators have a large gap, requiring signiﬁcant energy to excite
electrons. Conductors have no gap with overlapping EV and EC bands . And semiconductors have a
small gap that allows electron excitation when energy is provided. In semiconductors, the presence
of the band gap enables the conversion of light into electricity (photovoltaic cells) and the emission
of light (LEDs). Both processes rely on energy absorbed or released as electrons transition between
the conduction and valence bands.

It is also important to distinguish between direct and indirect band gaps [33]. In semiconductors, the
minimum energy state in the conduction band and the maximum energy state in the valence band
are associated with a crystal momentum (k-vector) in the Brillouin zone [42]. If the k-vectors from
electrons and holes are different, the material has an indirect gap. If the momentum is the same for
both bands, then the bandgap is direct.
Figure 2.1: Illustration of a (a) direct band gap and an (b) indirect band gap of semiconductor mate-
rials [43].
As shown in Figure 2.1, in a direct bandgap, an electron can directly emit a photon transitioning from
the conduction band to the valence band. On the other hand, in an indirect gap, photon emission
is inefﬁcient because the electron must ﬁrst transfer momentum to the crystal lattice, typically via a
phonon, before recombining with a hole. This distinction is crucial in photovoltaics, as it impacts the
efﬁciency of light absorption and emission [42].
Direct bandgap materials include hydrogenated amorphous silicon, InAs, GaAs, CdTe, and CiGS. In-
direct bandgap materials include crystalline silicon and Ge.
2.1.2
Doping
In a pure semiconductor, known as an intrinsic material, the number of free electrons in the conduc-
tion band is equal to the number of holes in the valence band [44]. These charge carriers are generated
by thermal excitation, and their concentration is known as the intrinsic carrier concentration ni. For
silicon at 300 K, the intrinsic carrier concentration is ni = 9.65·109 cm−3 [33].
Since the carrier generation depends on thermal energy, ni is inﬂuenced by both the band gap of the
material and the temperature. Larger band gaps will lead to lower intrinsic carrier concentrations,
while higher temperatures will increase them [33][44]. This directly affects solar cell efﬁciency, as it
determines how many charge carriers can be generated under illumination.
To achieve higher conduction, impurities (small amounts of atoms) can be intentionally introduced
into the semiconductor; this process is known as doping [34][45]. Doping basically shifts the balance
between electrons and holes in the silicon crystal lattice by adding extra carriers.

2.2
Absorption of light
When light hits the surface of a semiconductor, photons may be reﬂected at the top surface, absorbed
within the material, or transmitted through it [47]. In the context of solar cells, reﬂection and trans-
mission are considered loss mechanisms, since only absorbed photons can generate electrical power.
An absorbed photon can excite an electron from the valence band to the conduction band, contribut-
ing to current generation.
Whether a photon is absorbed or transmitted depends on its energy Eph relative to the semiconduc-
tor’s band gap EG [47]. We can distinguish three cases:
• Eph < EG: photons with less energy than the band gap interact weakly with the semiconductor
passing through as if it was transparent (transmission).
• Eph = EG: photons with the same energy as the band gap are efﬁciently absorbed and can gen-
erate an electron-hole pair.
• Eph > EG: photons with an energy much greater than the band gap are strongly absorbed. In
this situation, however, the excess energy is quickly lost in the form of heat through carrier
thermalization. Hence, for photovoltaic applications, this case is not suitable, as the excess
energy is not converted into useful electrical power.
In absorption and carrier generation, there are two important concepts: the absorption coefﬁcient
and the absorption depth. The absorption coefﬁcient α determines how deeply light of a particular
waverleght can penetrate a material before being absorbed. It has units of cm−1 and depends on both
material properties and light’s wavelength [48].

2.3
Recombination
Any electron in the conduction band is in a metastable state [51]. Hence, when the light is turned off,
the electron-hole pairs generated by the light can no longer be sustained and will eventually stabilize
to a lower energy state [34]. To do so, each electron must move into an available empty state, elimi-
nating both the electron from the conduction band and the hole from the valence band. This process
is known as recombination.
There are three main types of recombination: radiative, Auger and defect-related recombination [51].
In radiative recombination, a photon is emitted as a result of electron-hole recombination. This is the
dominant mechanism in direct bandgap semiconductors [51].
Secondly, there is Auger recombination, which is a three-carrier interaction. In this case, the energy
released from the electron-hole recombination is transferred to a third carrier, usually another elec-
tron, which loses the excess energy through a series of collisions with the lattice, thus dissipating it as
heat [52]. This recombination mechanism typically occurs at high carrier concentrations, for exam-
ple, in heavily doped regions of solar cells, such as the emitter and back surface ﬁeld (BSF) layers.
Lastly, there is defect-related recombination (Shockley-Read-Hall recombination). This interaction
occurs via intermediate energy levels introduced by defects or impurities within the band gap [51][53][54].
A carrier is trapped at the defect site and then recombines with an opposite carrier before it can be

In addition to bulk recombination mechanisms, surface recombination also plays an important role
in semiconductors. The surface of a semiconductor typically exhibits a signiﬁcantly higher recom-
bination rate compared to the bulk. This happens due to the disruption of the crystal lattice at the
surface where the periodic arrangement of atoms ﬁnishes [56]. As a result, unpaired electrons known
as dangling bonds are formed. These dangling bonds and other surface defects, such as vacancies,
interstitials, and impurities, introduce energy states within the band gap, which act as recombination
centers.
Surface recombination is particularly detrimental for solar cells as they have a large surface area com-
pared to their volume. This means that many photogenerated carriers near the surface may recom-
bine before contributing to the current, reducing the overall device efﬁciency. As seen before, this
effect is especially bad for high-energy photons, as the absorption depth is much shorter than in low-
energy photons. Surface recombination can be reduced thanks to surface passivation, which consists
of depositing a thin dielectric layer, such as silicon nitride Si3N4 or silicon oxide SiO2 on the semi-
conductor surface [56]. These layers neutralize the dangling bonds, reducing the density of surface
states. Passivation can occur through two main mechanisms: chemical passivation, where hydrogen
atoms in hydrogenated SiO2 saturate the dangling bonds, and ﬁeld-effect passivation, where Si3N4 or
Al2O3 create an electric ﬁeld that repels carriers from the surface, therefore reducing recombination.

2.4
p-n junctions
A p-n junction is formed by joining a p-type and an n-type semiconductor. This junction is the core
structure of many electronic devices, including solar cells, LEDs, and transistors [33]. When the two
semiconductors are brought into contact, excess electrons from the n-type side diffuse into the p-
type side and excess holes from the p-type side diffuse into the n-type side [34][58]. This diffusion
occurs due to carrier concentration gradients across the junction.
As a result of the diffusion, a region with no mobile charges is created in the interface of both mate-
rials, this is known as the depletion region. Within the depletion region, only ﬁxed positive charges
(on the n-side) and negative charges (on the p-side) remain. These immobile charges create an elec-
tric ﬁeld. This leads to the formation of a built-in potential Vbi which acts as a barrier that prevents
electrons from freely moving from the n-type to the p-type regions, and vice versa [58].

At thermal equilibrium, the drift current (driven by the electric ﬁeld in the depletion region) and
diffusion current get canceled, resulting in a zero current across the junction [59].
As shown in Figure 2.5, under forward bias (positive voltage on the p-side), the built-in electric ﬁeld
is reduced, the energy bands bend closer and the potential barrier is lowered [60]. This facilitates
the injection of carriers across the junction, increasing the diffusion current. As a result, electrons
from the n-type region move into the p-type region, and holes move from the p-type region to the
n-type region. This process is called carrier injection, and makes majority carriers from each side

2.5
The photovoltaic effect
We can now discuss what happens when light hits the junction. As seen in Section 2.2, if the energy
of the incident photons equals or exceeds the band gap energy of the p-n junction, photons are ab-
sorbed, generating electron-hole pairs. This generation can happen anywhere within the junction.
However, electrons (in the p-type material) and holes (in the n-type material) are metastable, mean-
ing that they will exist for only a short time equal to the minority carrier lifetime before they recom-
bine. If recombination occurs, the electron-hole pairs generated by light are lost, and no electrical
current is obtained [62][63].
For that reason, it is crucial that the electron-hole pairs are generated within a distance from the de-
pletion region that is smaller than the minority carrier diffusion length. This ensures that carriers
can reach the depletion region before recombining. In the depletion region, the internal electric ﬁeld
separates the charge carriers in a process called collection. As a result, electrons diffuse toward the
n-type region and the holes toward the p-type region, effectively separating the charges and prevent-
ing recombination [63]. Once separated, each carrier becomes a majoritary carrier in its respective
region.
If the emitter and the base of the solar cell are connected to an external circuit (for example, the cell
is connected to a load like a battery), the separated carriers can ﬂow through the circuit, creating an
electric current (Figure 2.6) [62].
However, the collection of light-generated carriers by itself does not produce power. To generate

the open-circuit voltage (VOC).

to an external circuit power can be extracted from the cell.

As mentioned in the introduction, the two main objectives of this project are to fabricate thinner solar

This chapter describes the characteristics of the implant array and the p-n junction pixel design, as

3.1
Photovoltaic retinal implant design
Firstly, we can start with the implant design. The PV retinal implant consists of an array of microscale
p-n junctions (solar cells), each of them referred to as a pixel [16]. These junctions are built on a
silicon substrate, due to its high quantum efﬁciency, and widespread use in solar cell fabrication [33].
The bulk silicon used is n-type doped, 350 µm thick and has a resistivity in the range of 1−20 Ω·cm.
When designing retinal implants, several parameters must be optimized to ensure the desired device
performance.
3.1.1
Implant thickness
The thickness of the device is an essential parameter, especially when talking about implantation.
Thinner implants offer greater ﬂexibility, allowing the prostheses to adapt more easily to the curvature
of the subretinal tissue [16][79]. Thin and ﬂexible devices also minimize the risk of damaging the
retinal tissue during implantation, which is essential for long-term stability and patient comfort.
Implant thicknesses usually range from 30 to 200 µm [16]. As previously discussed, the main objective
of this thesis is to fabricate PV retinal implants with a target thickness of 50 µm. This thickness was
chosen as it offers a balance between mechanical ﬂexibility and structural integrity. Devices with a
thickness of 50 µm are thin enough to conform to the subretinal tissue, minimizing the mechanical
stress. While still being thick enough to withstand fabrication processes such as lithography or thin-
ﬁlm deposition, as well as handling during surgical implantation.
3.1.2
Implant dimensions
In terms of lateral dimensions, subretinal implants can have widths ranging from 0.3 to 5 mm. The
size of the implant directly inﬂuences the achievable visual ﬁeld, as larger implants can integrate a
larger number of pixels [16][28]. This allows the stimulation of a broader retinal area and can poten-
tially restore a wider ﬁeld of view. However, increasing the implant size can complicate the surgical
procedure, as well as reduce the ﬂexibility of the implant.
In this project, three implant sizes were evaluated: 0.3x0.3 mm2, 1.5x1.5 mm2, and 3x3 mm2 (Fig-
ure 3.1). These dimensions correspond to different animal models: mice (0.3x0.3 mm2), rats (1.5x1.5

3.1.5
Electrode geometry and materials
As previously discussed, the PV retinal implant consists of an array of p-n junctions, referred to as
pixels. Each pixel integrates both a stimulating and a return electrode, connected to the p-type and
n-type regions of the junction, respectively [16]. These electrodes must be made of biocompatible
materials, such as gold, platinum, indium oxide, or titanium nitride [81][82]. The selection of elec-
trode materials and adhesion layers is important to ensure good conductivity and long-term biocom-
patibility. Recent studies, including Pratik Kusumanchi’s PhD thesis, have highlighted the advantages
of carbon-based electrodes, which offer superior chemical stability, durability, and biocompatibility
[16].
To simplify the fabrication process and the device characterization, only 2D planar electrodes were
made in this project. Although 2D electrodes are widely implemented, they present some limitations,
including a larger distance to the target neurons and weaker cell adhesion. These drawbacks can be
improved by using 3D electrode structures. 3D electrodes, such as carbon-based pillars, provide a
larger area for cell interaction and reduce the electrode-neuron distance [83][84]. However, as elec-
trode design was not the primary focus of this project, we opted for 2D electrodes.
Regarding the materials, Titanium (Ti) was used as the adhesion layer due to its excellent biocompat-
ibility and strong bonding properties. Titanium forms stable chemical bonds with substrate materials
like silicon (Si) and silicon dioxide (SiO2), preventing delamination during lift-off or subsequent sur-
gical implantation [85][86].
Thin layers of titanium (10-20nm) also promote the nucleation and adhesion of posterior metal lay-
ers, such as platinum, which adheres poorly to the substrate on its own. In this project, platinum (Pt)
was chosen as the electrode material because of its high electrical conductivity, chemical inertness,
and long-term biostability [81]. Compared to other typically used metals like gold (Au), Pt forms a
lower Schottky barrier with doped silicon, which allows the electrons to easily move from the semi-
conductor towards the metal contact. In contrast, gold, with a higher Schottky barrier, can reduce

transport and lower the efﬁciency of the implant[82][86]. Additionally, both Au and Pt can diffuse
into silicon as dopants, introducing deep-level traps that can affect carrier lifetimes and device be-

As the electrodes have to stimulate the retinal neurons, it is also important to compare the charge
injection capacity (CIC) and charge storage capacity (CSC) of platinum and gold [16]. These two

General pixel design
Each pixel in the PV array consists of a central p+ doped circular region, surrounded by a concentric

central region, while electrons are driven toward the n+ ring and the substrate. As a result, the central
p+ region becomes positively biased relative to the n-type substrate, generating a photovoltage.
When the pixel is connected to neural tissue (in vivo) or an external load (during testing), the photo-

gap states that act like recombination centers, which can lower the carrier lifetime and increase the
dark current. To reduce these defects, the trench sidewalls were passivated using thermal oxidation,
which forms a high-quality silicon dioxide (SiO2) layer that minimizes defect density and suppresses
carrier recombination [90].
3.3.2
Trench ﬁlling
In our case, the objective was not only to passivate the sidewalls but also to completely ﬁll the trenches
with dielectric material (such as SiO2). The trench ﬁlling was crucial as the implant would later be
thinned down to the depth of the trenches. Therefore a complete and uniform ﬁlling was needed, as
any voids or incomplete coverage could compromise the mechanical robustness and stability of the
implant.
Two main trench-ﬁlling strategies were discussed before starting this project: polysilicon deposition
and thermal oxidation. Polysilicon is a conductive material typically used to ﬁll isolation trenches in
CMOS devices (Figure 3.7) [91]. However, it requires chemical-mechanical polishing (CMP) to remove
excess material and planarize the surface. The polishing step must be precisely controlled to prevent
residual polysilicon on the surface that could connect adjacent pixels, introducing variability and
leading to possible short circuits. Moreover, polysilicon alone does not provide sufﬁcient electrical
isolation unless a thermal oxide is grown ﬁrst in the trenches [92][90].
Thermal oxide, on the other hand, is a stoichiometric insulator (SiO2), which is formed by oxidizing

silicon at high temperatures in a furnace [94]. It provides a conformal, high-purity, and high-density
dielectric layer with good insulating properties. Silicon dioxide can also be deposited by Plasma-
Enhanced Chemical Vapor Deposition (PECVD). However, PECVD oxide may suffer from poor side-
wall coverage in high-aspect-ratio trenches and non-uniform stoichiometry [95]. Moreover, PECVD
oxide can exhibit lower ﬁlm quality.
Compared to polysilicon, thermal oxide does not require CMP, simplifying the fabrication process
[90][94]. Moreover, the silicon dioxide layer can be used as a mask during subsequent lithography and
etching steps. Given the need for complete trench ﬁlling, and the compatibility with later processing
steps, thermal oxidation in combination with LPCVD of TEOS was chosen as the preferred technique
for trench passivation and ﬁlling.
Atomic layer deposition (ALD) was also considered as a trench ﬁlling technique using aluminum ox-
ide (Al2O3). However, to fully ﬁll the isolating trenches of our design, a minimum deposition of 1 µm
was required, while the maximum allowed thickness to deposit with the ALD tool was limited to 100
nm. Therefore, even if ALD offered good conformality, the limited thickness made it unsuitable for
this project.
3.3.3
Trench design
In the isolation mask design, each pixel was surrounded by a hexagonal trench pattern [33][90]. Two
trench layout strategies were considered. The ﬁrst one involved individually isolated hexagons for
each pixel, with spacing between adjacent trenches. This option offered mechanical stability, how-
ever, it required more surface area, limiting the number of pixels that could ﬁt in the array.
As shown in Figure 3.8, the second design consisted of a compact layout in which neighboring pixels
had common trench walls. Although this design reduced the mechanical stability and robustness of
the implants due to the shared trenches, it allowed higher pixel density, leading to a better spatial
resolution. The compact design was ﬁnally chosen, given the importance of maximizing the number
of pixels in the implant.

Device fabrication
The fabrication process was carried out through a series of experimental steps (Figure 4.1):
1. KOH etching test: Firstly, a preliminary test was performed on a plain silicon wafer to check if
it was possible to achieve 50µm-thick membranes using KOH etching. And to ensure that the
ﬁnal devices could be safely diced without breaking.
2. Trench etching and ﬁlling test: Secondly, various trench widths and depths were tested using
DRIE and TEOS deposition. The goal was to identify the optimal trench parameters to achieve
the desired trench geometry and ensure a void-free ﬁlling.
3. Pixel array fabrication: Thirdly, the pixels (p-n junctions) were fabricated, and the devices were
thinned down using KOH etching. Achieving the ﬁnal 50µm-thick implants.
4. Integration of KOH and isolation trenches in a PV implant: Finally, the KOH etching process
was combined with trench etching and ﬁlling in the fabrication of p-n junction arrays. The goal
of this integration was to evaluate the structural integrity of thin implants with the addition of
trenches.

4.1.1
KOH etching
Potassium hydroxide (KOH) etching was employed for implant thinning, as it is a widely used tech-
nique for fabricating thin membranes in silicon substrates [96]. KOH offers a high etch rate along
crystallographic 〈100〉direction, precise control over membrane thickness, and exhibits good selec-
tivity against mask materials such as SiO2 or Si3N4 [97]. In this project, silicon nitride was used as the
hard mask.
KOH etching presents several advantages over alternative methods, such as dry etching or the use
of silicon-on-insulator (SOI) wafers. SOI wafers were an interesting option as they include a buried
oxide (BOX) layer that serves as a precise etch stop, which could also be used for pixel isolation [98].
However, these wafers are more expensive than usual silicon wafers and involve more complex pro-
cessing steps, making them less suitable for prototyping and testing.
Dry etching techniques, on the other hand, also had several drawbacks in this context. Plasma-based
etching can induce surface damage due to ion bombardment, introducing defects that may degrade
device performance. Moreover, dry etching methods have poor uniformity over large areas as they
are better suited for creating high-aspect ratio structures [99]. Lastly, using dry etch, wafers must be
processed individually, in contrast to KOH wet etch, which allows multiple wafers to be processed
simultaneously. These limitations made dry etching more time-consuming and less practical for this
project.
As previously mentioned, the desired membrane implants measured 1.5x1.5 mm2 in lateral dimen-
sions and 50 µm in thickness, meaning a 300 µm etch. As our design had no high aspect-ratio struc-
tures, KOH etching proved to be the most suitable and cost-effective technique. In the fabrication
process, the etching was performed using a standard 28wt% KOH solution already prepared by DTU
Nanolab. The solution was heated to 80◦C to promote a fast and uniform etch rate [97].

4.1.2
Process ﬂow
As illustrated in Figure 4.3, the process started with the deposition of a 200 ±100 nm silicon nitride
Si3N4 layer on a double-side polished (DSP) n-type doped silicon wafer. The nitride layer served as a
hard mask for the KOH etching process and was deposited using LPCVD in a furnace, since it allowed
the deposition of stoichiometric Si3N4.
Figure 4.3: Illustration of the process ﬂow steps for the KOH preliminary test
After deposition, UV photolithography was used to pattern the hard mask. The process started by
spin-coating a 1.5 µm thick AZ MIR 701 photoresist on the backside of the wafer. Then, the membrane
pattern for the three tested dimensions was deﬁned using MLA 150 WMII maskless aligner from Hei-
delberg Instruments. After exposure, the wafers were post-exposure baked PEB for 60 seconds and
developed in TMAH-based AZ 726 MIF for another 60 seconds. The developed design features were
ﬁnally inspected in the optical microscope.
An inductively coupled plasma (ICP) tool referred to as advanced silicon etcher (ASE) was employed
to etch 300 ±50 nm of the nitride layer, using the patterned AZ MIR 701 photoresist as a mask. Al-
though the original nitride was only 200 nm thick, over-etching was important to make sure the ni-
tride was completely removed from the membrane areas. Given that the etch rate for Si3N4 in KOH
is less than 0.1 nm/min, any residual nitride layer would have signiﬁcantly slowed down the KOH
etching process.
After hard mask pattern deﬁnition, the photoresist was stripped using plasma asher . The wafers
were then placed in a KOH bath, pre-prepared by DTU Nanolab. To ensure a good etch quality and
uniformity the bath was heated to 80◦C. Having an etch rate of 1.3 µm/min for Si〈100〉, the process
lasted 4 hours. When the KOH etching was done, the wafers were cleaned in a solution of sulfuric
acid (98%) and ammonium persulfate, also heated to 80◦C. The last step before dicing was to strip the
Si3N4 hard mask, which was done by immersing the wafers in a 85 wt% phosphoric acid H3PO4 bath
at 160◦C for 1 hour and 16 minutes.
Finally, the membranes were diced using a Dicing Saw. The resulting membranes were inspected
under an optical microscope to check whether they were still intact or broken due to dicing, and to
see if they were easy to handle when being removed from the dicing tape.

Backside passivation
The next step in the fabrication process was to passivate the backside of the wafers. Backside passiva-
tion was not performed simultaneously with front-side passivation, as the membrane structures on
the backside were really difﬁcult to protect. Even with a thick layer of photoresist, adequate coverage
could not be guaranteed. As a result, the backside passivation layers would have been etched during
contact hole etching.
After an RCA clean, the backside passivation layers were deposited using PECVD. The deposition
times were longer as the passivation layers were signiﬁcantly thicker than those on the front side:
50nm of SiO2 and 600 nm of Si3N4. The thicker silicon nitride was used to protect the silicon dioxide
and ensure that some nitride remained after the HF dip, which was intended to remove the native
oxide on the surface prior to metal deposition.
4.2.2.7
Lithography for electrodes
Following backside passivation, photolithography was used to deﬁne the electrodes on the front side.
In this case, a 4 µm- thick layer of nLOF 2020, negative resist, was manually spin-coated onto the
wafers. The use of negative resist was essential for the metal lift-off process, as it creates an undercut
proﬁle that facilitates the removal of unwanted metal during lift-off. The negative resist was exposed
by MLA, PEB at 110◦C for 120 seconds, and developed for 60 seconds.
Before metal deposition, a short (10-15 seconds) dip in BHF was performed to remove the native
oxide from the silicon surface, ensuring good metal adhesion and proper contact formation.
4.2.2.8
Metal deposition and lift-off
Finally, metal contacts were deposited by electron-beam evaporation (Temescal). As detailed in Sec-
tion 3.1.5, titanium was used as an adhesion layer (20nm), and the contacts were made of platinum
(200nm).
After metal deposition, a lift-off process was carried out without ultrasonic agitation to avoid damag-
ing the membrane structures. Finally, an annealing step was performed at 400◦C for 30 minutes. This
step improved the contact properties by promoting better metal-silicon interaction.

4.3
Trenches - Preliminary test
Once having studied the ﬁrst objective of this thesis, we focused on the second goal: achieving elec-
trical isolation of individual pixels to prevent crosstalk between them. As described in Section 3.3, the
proposed approach consisted of etching hexagonal trenches around each pixel and ﬁlling them with
thermal silicon oxide, forming dielectric barriers between adjacent pixels.
4.3.1
Design
To evaluate the feasibility of this isolation strategy, a preliminary test was carried out on clean silicon
wafers without any doped regions, similarly to the KOH preliminary test. As previously detailed in
Section 3.3, different trench widths and depths were studied. The design layout included test struc-
tures with trench widths ranging from 1 to 10 µm, as well as compact hexagonal array patterns. The
two target depths tested were: 30 or 60 µm.
Overall, the main goal of this test was to determine suitable process parameters to achieve the desired
trench geometry and oxide ﬁlling, before applying the method to the ﬁnal wafers containing the p-n
junction arrays.
4.3.2
Fabrication tools
4.3.2.1
DRIE Pegasus
To deﬁne the deep, narrow isolation trenches (1−10 µm wide and 60 µm / 30 µm deep), Deep Reactive
Ion Etching (DRIE) was used. DRIE is particularly well suited for high-aspect-ratio structures, as it
allows the formation of vertical sidewalls with minimal undercut. This is essential to achieve the
desired trench geometry [97]. Unlike isotropic wet etching methods, DRIE provides highly anisotropic
proﬁles, allowing for precise pattern transfer.
The Pegasus 1 DRIE tool from DTU Nanolab, uses the Bosch process, which alternates etching and
passivation cycles to form deep trenches with well-deﬁned vertical sidewalls [97][102]. The funda-
mental principle of reactive ion etching consists of generating a plasma, which dissociates gases into
reactive species. Simultaneously, directional ion bombardment promotes anisotropic material re-
moval. The volatile byproducts formed during etching are continuously evacuated to maintain clean
processing conditions [102].
As it will be detailed in Section 4.3.5, to achieve smoother walls and reduce the scalloping typically
produced in the Bosch process, a Delayed Reactive Ion Etching Mode (DREM) was used [103]. DREM
is a modiﬁcation of the standard Bosch process, in which the passivation and etching steps are de-
coupled and temporally adjusted. Therefore, in this process, plasma is ﬁrst generated by applying a
coil power that allows the gas to stabilize and produce reactive neutral species (radicals). After a short
delay, bias power is applied to accelerate the ions towards the substrate and start the etching. As
Redesign of p-n junction diodes for photovoltaic retinal implant
51
==PAGE:65==
the radical species have already interacted with the surface during the delay, etching starts gradually
and uniformly. This reduces scallops and results in smoother sidewalls. As described in the following
sections, DREM 2kW Micro was used to get smoother sidewalls and improve trench ﬁlling.
4.3.2.2
Dry oxide and TEOS
For the trench-ﬁlling, a combination of dry thermal oxidation and TEOS-based silicon dioxide depo-
sition was used . Dry thermal oxidation was achieved using horizontal Tempress furnace from DTU
Nanolab [104]. In our process, this furnace was used for dry oxidation at 1050◦C.
The oxidation process consists of exposing the wafers to high-purity oxygen O2 gas at high temper-
atures. As a result, a thin, dense, and high-quality silicon dioxide is grown directly from the silicon
surface. This oxide exhibits excellent interface quality, reducing surface defect density. However, due
to its slow growth rate, the oxide thickness is typically limited to ≤300nm [104].
In this work, a 100 nm thick dry oxide layer was grown after trench etching to passivate the silicon
sidewalls. This allowed the reduction of plasma-induced damage and formed a stable Si-SiO2 inter-
face, minimizing the defect states along the trench boundary [90].
Following the dry oxide growth, a conformal silicon oxide layer was deposited using TEOS-based Low-
Pressure Chemical Vapor Deposition (LPCVD). This process involves the thermal decomposition of
tetrathyl orthosilicate (TEOS) at 725◦C, which results in silicon dioxide SiO2 that coats all wafer sur-
faces [105]. Thanks to its excellent conformality and step coverage, TEOS oxide is well suited for
trench ﬁlling narrow and high-aspect-ratio trenches, such as those in our design.
Initially, the standard TEOS recipe was used for the ﬁrst trench-ﬁlling tests [105]. However, the TEOS
furnace was serviced for more than one month and therefore set out of use. This led to a revision and
update of the initial process ﬂow and recipes, apart from a repetition of the trench-tests made at the
beginning of the project. Originally, trench etching and ﬁlling were planned prior to doping to avoid
exposing doped regions to high temperatures. However, as a result of the extended service time, the
fabrication ﬂow was modiﬁed: doping was processed before trench etching and ﬁlling, which was
resumed once the TEOS furnace became operational again.
As a whole, the combined use of dry thermal oxidation and TEOS deposition was aimed to provide a
good ﬁlling strategy. With the dry oxide ensuring a high-quality interface and good dielectric perfor-
mance, and the TEOS layer enabling trench ﬁlling with minimal void formation.

4.3.6
Results and conclusions
Initially, Solution 4 (tapered trenches) appeared to be the most promising approach due to the near-
complete oxide ﬁlling observed after deposition. However, the presence of large spikes caused by the
etching process made this strategy impractical.
The focus then shifted to the other three approaches. For the ﬁnal device fabrication the three of
them were chosen.
Solution 3 (widened vertices and slow etch) was selected as the most promising strategy, due to the
achieved smooth trench proﬁles, reduced scalloping, and improved oxide ﬁlling. Although some
voids remained, the overall results were better than previous approaches.
Solution 1 (widened vertices and fast etch) was also selected as a complementary approach, since the
scalloped sidewalls might act as achoring points, potentially enhancing the mechanical robustness
of the ﬁlled trenches.
Finally, solution 2 (no vertices and slow etch) was included, despite the less effective ﬁlling, to eval-
uate whether eliminating the wider vertices could lead to improved mechanical durability of the
trenches when thinning down.

Mechanical test on trenched devices
The purpose of this test was to determine whether the oxide-ﬁlled trenches could withstand the KOH
thinning process. And if successful, to identify which trench design and parameters would be the
most suitable for continuing with the fabrication process.
To evaluate this, the wafers were etched in KOH until reaching the base of the trenches. This allowed
us to test whether the oxide alone was sufﬁcient to maintain the pixel structure, or if the implants
would collapse.
Six different wafer conﬁgurations were tested:
• Widened vertices and slow etch 40 µm deep.
• Widened vertices and slow etch 20 µm deep.
• Widened vertices and fast etch 40 µm deep.
• Widened vertices and fast etch 20 µm deep.
• No widened vertices and slow etch 40 µm deep.
• No widened vertices and slow etch 20 µm deep.
All wafers underwent the same KOH process, reaching an etch depth of 310 µm to ensure that the
40 µm trench bottom was reached. As a result, some membranes were fully trenched (40 µm-deep
trench) and some other membranes were only half trenched (20 µm-deep trench). All partially trenched
wafers survived the KOH etch without any structural damage, conﬁrming their mechanical robust-
ness. Therefore, the following study is mainly focused in the fully trenched devices.
In future work, it would be really interesting to assess whether partially trenched structures can pro-
vide sufﬁcient electrical isolation for the PV retinal implants, compared to fully trenched devices. If
the difference is minimal, the partially trenched devices would offer a more mechanically stable and
robust alternative.

some membranes broke, but many remained intact. Optical microscope inspection revealed charac-
teristic square-like patterns typical of KOH silicon etching (Figure 4.31).

KOH-exposed backside, they remain stable, preserving both isolation and structural integrity.

5.2
IV measurements
The test structures from the ﬁnal thinned device (Section 4.1) were electrically characterized using a
four point probe (4PP) station. The objectives were to measure pixel resistivity under bothn dark and
illuminated conditions, to characterize the IV curve of the pixels, and to measure the open-circuit
voltage (VOC) generated under illumination.
Regarding the setup, the 4PP technique consists of applying a known current through the outer two
probes while measuring the voltage drop across the inner two probes, allowing for accurate resistivity
measurements that minimize the effect of contact resistance.
Regarding illumination conditions, light was set by placing a lamp directly above the sample and the
dark state was achieved by turning off the lamp, although some ambient room light was still present.
Both resistivity measurements and IV curves were obtained using the 4PP system. While open-circuit
voltage (VOC) was measured separately using a voltmeter across the contacts under illumination.
5.2.1
Single pixel - 50µm thick
For the single pixel (50µm thick), the measured resistivity changed from 3.055MΩin the dark to 1.937
MΩwhen the light was turned on. This behavior was expected since, under illumination, photogen-
erated carriers increase the conductivity, thereby reducing resistivity.
The measured open-circuit voltage VOC under illumination was 427 mV and 6 mV in the dark. While
the value under illumination was promising, the corresponded IV curve was nearly linear, rather than
the diode-like behavior expected from photovoltaic devices.
Additionally, when repeating the same measurements on other single-pixel structures, the results
were inconsistent. VOC values varied signiﬁcantly, from 427mV to 142mV, indicating a lack of re-
peatability and poor device performance.
5.2.2
5 pixel chain - 50µm thick
For pixels connected in series, no signiﬁcant change in resistivity was observed between light and
dark conditions. Although there was a change in VOC with illumination, the IV measurements again
showed no meaningful results.
These tests were also performed on ultra-thin test structures 15µm thick membranes. But, in those
cases, no change with illumination was perceived, neither on resistivity nor on VOC. These results
further suggested a lack of photovoltaic activity in the pixel.

6
Conclusions
The primary objective of this work was to fabricate thin PV retinal implants with electrically isolated
pixel arrays, suitable for subretinal implantation. This required achieving two main goals: ﬁrst, to
reduce the thickness of the device while ensuring mechanical integrity, and second, to design, etch,
and ﬁll isolation trenches surrounding each pixel array.
Regarding device thinning, the target thickness of 50µm was successfully achieved using KOH etch-
ing. Different membrane sizes (3x3 mm2, 1.5x1.5 mm2, 0.3x0.3 mm2) were evaluated with regards to
their mechanical robustness. As a result, 1.5x1.5 mm2 offered the best balance between structural
stability and ease of handling. While thinner 0.3x0.3 mm2 membranes were more desirable for image
resolution, they proved too fragile to be practically useful. In contrast, larger 3x3 mm2 membranes
remained intact after dicing, but were less suitable for implantation. Therefore, 1.5x1.5 mm2 size was
adopted as the best dimension for ﬁnal devices.
After the initial test, thin p-n junction implants were successfully fabricated, demonstrating that the
designed process ﬂow using KOH etching is an effective method for producing thin implants. More-
over, the ﬁnal devices proved to be robust after dicing, allowing implant handling without breakage.
However, electrical characterization of the ﬁnal diced test structures revealed inconsistent photo-
voltaic performance. Although single-pixel test devices showed a voltage response under illumina-
tion, the IV characteristics deviated from expected diode-like behavior, and the results were not re-
producible. These results suggest processing issues that suppressed the photovoltaic activity. Further
studies and process optimizations are necessary to achieve functional implant behavior.
In terms of trench design, this study evaluated multiple trench widths (ranging from 1 −10µm),
depths, and geometries, targeting a conformal TEOS oxide ﬁlling and structural stability of the ﬁnal
thinned devices. As a conclusion, trenches narrower than 3µm were found to exhibit better ﬁlling.
Due to limitations in the deposition oxide thickness, wider trenches were not successfully ﬁlled.
Among several etching and ﬁlling strategies, three main designs were selected for fabrication. All of
them were aimed at ensuring a proper etching and conformal ﬁlling, avoiding obstruction and air
voids inside the trenches.
• Widened vertices with slow DRIE etch process: This strategy resulted in smooth trench side-
walls, enabling uniform TEOS oxide deposition and almost complete ﬁlling. It also demon-
strated the best mechanical performance, making it the most promising approach overall.
• Widened vertices with fast etching: The faster etch resulted in sidewall scalloping, which led
to partial oxide bridging and small voids within the trenches. While the trenches were not com-
pletely ﬁlled, the oxide bridges were thought to improve mechanical stability by providing an-
choring points within the trench. However, due to issues with the trench etching process, the
results from the KOH thinning were not conclusive.
• No widened vertices with slow etch: This method resulted in worse oxide ﬁlling, with obstruc-
tions and large voids observed in the trenches. Nevertheless, it was included in the ﬁnal thin-
ning process to assess whether the absence of widened vertices could improve the mechanical

==PAGE:87==
stability of the structure during KOH thinning. In the end, most of the membranes broke after
KOH etching, disregarding this hypothesis.
Given the previous results, widened trench vertices and slow etch was considered the most effective
approach for isolation trench fabrication, as it showed a better mechanical stability after KOH etch-
ing. Although electrical characterization of the trenches could not be completed, optical inspection
was carried out to evaluate the condition of the trenches and membranes after KOH etching. This
inspection conﬁrmed that some implants remained intact, demonstrating the successful fabrication
of thin PV retinal implants with trench-isolated pixels.
Overall, this work shows a viable process for fabricating thin, implantable PV arrays. While electrical
performance requires further optimization, the obtained PV retinal implants show good mechanical
stability, and the process ﬂow proves suitable for thinning. Additionally, an extensive trench study for
oxide ﬁlling has been conducted, setting a basis for future progress toward fully functional, thin, and
pixel-isolated PV subretinal implants.
