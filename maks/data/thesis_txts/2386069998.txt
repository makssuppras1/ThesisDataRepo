Abstract 
The species in the genus Aspergillus are central organisms in many aspects, found ubiquitously in nature, 
acting as decomposers in soil, taking part in crucial biogeochemical cycles, producing a variety of 
metabolites. Many species have a significant economic impact in biotechnological, pharmaceutical, and 
food production, producing different metabolites with both positive and negative effects on human health. 
They have the ability to produce both vegetative forms, as well as sexual and asexual reproductive 
structures which can be utilised for the detection and characterisation of the species. 
Species of the section Fumigati, and the Unilateralis clade in particular, have established themselves in a 
variety of habitats that can withstand a range of harsh changes in the environment conditions. Their low 
levels of fertility can be compensated by their asexual reproductive strategies in a wide range of 
environmental conditions. 
Standards for the quality of food and indoor environment are constantly rising and fungi play a significant 
role in the deterioration of such standards. Many can survive through heat processing of food and 
beverages, and others can withstand antifungal treatments with a variety of conventional and 
unconventional products. Hence, characterisation and identification of the species is of great importance. 
To characterise the species in the Unilateralis clade, a variety of assays were designed in order to obtain 
a better understanding of the species and their qualities. By growing them on a variety of media at different 
temperatures, essentially creating a variety of environments, we were able to obtain knowledge of their 
temperature and nutrient preferences. The majority of the species were fonder to grow at the higher 
temperatures (30˚C and 37˚C), however, they produced the most secondary metabolites at 25˚C. Several 
of the produced metabolites, which have great biotechnological potential, included: helvolic acid, austdiols 
and gypsetins. 
Assays to test their mycelial and spore susceptibility to various treatments showed the ability of the 
organisms to survive harsh treatments.  
Heat treatment of the A. hiratsukae spore suspensions resulted in reducing the colonies when subjected to 
more than 10 minutes at 65˚C. However, the longer exposure of 2 minutes to 90˚C had the reverse effect 
and the colony count increased. A second assay with longer periods of exposure showed similar results for 
the A. hiratsukae samples. A. marvanovae appeared unaffected by the changes in temperature and only 
10minute exposure to 65˚C and 2minutes to 90˚C had effects on the organism. Spore treatment with 
ethanol showed inconclusive results as the same concentrations affected the same organism differently 
with each sample. 
Mycelial susceptibility to antifungals showed insufficient inhibition by most antifungal agents used. When 
treated with Rodalone®, A. multiplicatus expressed the highest susceptibility, with an inhibition area of 
227.96 mm2. A. hiratsukae and A. tsunodae were the least affected by both the Rodalone® and ethanol. 
70% ethanol did not provide successful inhibition in most of the organisms, and 90% ethanol successfully 
inhibited in the beginning but as time passed, the inhibition zones significantly reduced size. As an 
alternative, organic fungicide, vinegar was used. It proved unsuccessful to inhibit the growth of most 
organisms, affecting only two A. hiratsukae samples, one A. nishimurae, and one A. unilateralis.  
Reduced water availability assay showed retardation in the growth of the organisms. Enzyme assay for the 
production of cellulases was also unsuccessful. Nonetheless, an assay with limited nutrient media 
following both assays was designed: Paper Agar, Wallpaper Agar, and Wallpaper Glue Agar. A. assulatus 
utilised all three media very well, completely depleting the paper medium. When grown on gypsum boards, 
the A. hiratsukae species established themselves and began production of cleistothecia. 
The Unilateralis clade provided us with deep insight into its potentials, abilities, and characteristics. A. 
marvanovae differed the most in its responses to all assays. We were shown that species in the clade can 
be of interest to further study their biotechnological applications and characteristics.

Thesis outline 
This thesis, entitled “Characterisation of the Aspergillus Unilateralis clade” engaged different approaches 
to the identification and characterisation of species from the Unilateralis clade. A main aim was to 
investigate their phenotypes and growth at a variety of temperatures, their ability to produce secondary 
metabolites, and both mycelial and spore response to various stress.  
The genus Aspergillus is an important group of organisms, capable of producing mycotoxins, organic 
acids, enzymes and bioactive metabolites. New species from the section Fumigati have been described 
from various habitats. Most of these belong to the Unilateralis clade. The oldest described species of the 
clade being A. unilateralis, which is an important research organism. A. hiratsukae is considered to be the 
most common species in the clade, first described in juice and later found in soil and indoor environments. 
Organisms in the clade are becoming model organisms, with potential to be used in various industries. 
They are found in a variety of habitats, thriving after harsh changes in the environmental conditions. Some 
have also been reported to cause opportunistic infections in both humans and animals. 
The knowledge of these organisms is thus important in many aspects of our daily life. Understanding their 
preferences of nutrients, environmental conditions, and susceptibility to treatment gives us the opportunity 
to further explore their possibilities or learn how to control them. 
The overall aim of the study was to achieve a deeper understanding of the organisms’ requirements and 
abilities. The objectives were to generate a matrix of growth specifications and characteristics of each 
individual member of the clade (Ch. 3.1 Culture characterisation) in a variety of created environments and 
conditions (Ch. 3.2), and investigate whether such changes can have an effect on the organisms’ ability to 
produce secondary metabolites (Ch. 3.1.1.1) which can be a mean for the correct identification of the 
species or have any biotechnological potentials; as well as assay the organisms’ resilience when exposed 
to various antifungal treatments (Ch. 3.2). Furthermore, an aim was to follow their ability to proliferate in 
“real life” conditions, by providing the species with limited nutrients and placing them on materials 
commonly used in indoor constructions (Ch. 3.4), as well as media that would induce enzyme production 
(Ch. 3.3).

1. Background 
1.1 Filamentous fungi 
Fungi are ubiquitous in both aquatic and terrestrial environments and can occur in various forms such as 
unicellular organisms (yeasts), polymorphic or filamentous fungi, and can also be free-living or part of 
a symbiotic co-existence. Terrestrial environments are most often associated with fungal activities as the 
organisms have an important role as decomposers, plant pathogens, symbionts with crucial roles in many 
biogeochemical cycles, such as the carbon and nitrogen cycles (Hawksworth et al. 1995). In soil, they 
can be considered as the largest pool of biomass and take part in maintaining soil structure due to their 
filamentous branching growth patterns, as well as their exopolymer production (Hawksworth et al., 
1995; Powers-Fletcher et al. 2016). 
Filamentous fungi grow by apical extension of their filaments, also called hyphae. The hyphal growth 
can occur with or without the presence of septation, which is the cell-wall separation of cellular 
compartments (Khandelwal 2016). Favourable conditions allow for continued growth and formation of 
a complex network of hyphae, also known as a mycelium. The ability to branch while growing and the 
characteristics of branches are one of the many characteristics used to differentiate between the different 
groups of fungi. The mode of growth can also be a strong factor contributing to their pathogenicity (Yu 
& Keller 2005).  
In addition to the ability to produce vegetative forms, fungi can also produce sexual and asexual 
reproductive structures. Depending on the genus, those propagules may be referred to as conidia or 
spores. (Powers-Fletcher et al. 2016) Depending on the phylum, these spores produced sexually, can be 
contained within sacs, known as asci (phylum Ascomata), or externally (Basidiomycota or zygomycota).  
Detecting the presence or absence of asexual/sexual structures under appropriate conditions is a useful 
tool for the identification of the genus, or species. Historically, assigning the appropriate binomial 
nomenclature for fungi has depended on whether the asexual or sexual state was observed, hence each 
state being given its own name (O’Gorman et al. 2009).  
1.2 Aspergillus genus 
Aspergillus is a diverse genus of high economic and social importance. Aspergillus species occur 
worldwide in various environments and habitats. They are known to produce different secondary 
metabolites, spoil food, and have been reported as both human and animal pathogens. Moreover, species 
of the genus are widely used in biotechnology for the production of various metabolites for medicine, as 
agents in food, or organic acids and enzymes. The classification and identification of the genus have 
been based on phenotypic characteristics, but with the advance of modern technology, classification is 
driven by molecular and chemotaxonomic characterisation (Samson et al. 2007).  
The genus was first described by P. A. Micheli (1727), with simple illustrations and a simple description. 
The genus was updated in 1926 by Thom and Church, adding 69 new species, divided into 11 groups, 
based on colony morphology and characteristics of the aspergillum. Classification is primarily based on 
morphological characteristics. Raper and Fennel (1965) have divided the genus into 18 groups and 
introduced over 130 new species. Since 1965, many new taxa have been described, and their validity is 
constantly reviewed.  
1.2.1 Taxonomy 
After the decision to reverse article 59 of the former botanical code in 2011 by the International Botanical 
Congress Nomenclature Section in Melbourne, the principle one fungus : one name  has been 
established. This has led to many implications for Aspergillus and the classification of the genus.

quite useful for approximate species identification (Geiser et al. 2007).

1.2.2 Aspergillus characteristics 
Members of the genus occur in a wide variety of habitats, some common in soil as saprophytes, and 
others on food and feed products, as well as decaying vegetation. They are in abundance in tropical and 
subtropical regions, being one of the most dominant genera found thriving on stored products in 
situations of limited water availability or unfavourable temperatures, and in those areas, they are

Conidia are small and globose structures, that can be either uni- or multinucleate in various colours. 
They have a thick and spiny outer wall known as the epispore, and a thinner, more delicate inner layer, 
the endospore. The conidia can easily be dispersed by air currents and when the conditions are favourable 
they can germinate by growing out a germ tube. The germ tube begins septating and branching itself, 
thus forming a mycelium (Kritartha 2016). Figure 1-3. 
 
 
 
 
 
Sexual reproduction is quite rare as the majority of species in the genus are homothallic. The process 
involves the formation of male and female sex organs. The male organ is known as antheridium, and the

female branch is called Archicarp (Figure 1-5 C). The archicarps develops on the mycelium in the shape 
of a loosely coiled structure which gets into a tighter form as it approaches and finally touches with the 
male. The pollinodium usually grows beside the archicarps and is coiled around the archicarps as it

arches over the apex of ascogonium (Figure 1-5 D, E). At the point of contact, the cell wall dissolves 
allowing for a continuous passage of contents from the antheridium in to the ascogonium. The male and 
female nuclei pair inside the ascogonium. In some species, the contents from the antheridium do not

the pairing of nuclei occurs between ascogonial nuclei (O’Gorman et al. 2009).  
After nuclear pairing (Figure 1-5 F), the ascogonium develops a fruiting body, known as the ascocarp 
and the ascogonium becomes septate. Each segment contains one male and one female nucleus 
(dikaryon). These segments give rise to multicellular ascogenous hyphae that produce asci via crozier 
formation. The terminal bi-nucleate cell of the ascogenouse hyphae elongates and begins to bend, 
forming a crosier structure (Figure 1-5 H, I, J). At the curve position of the crozier, a sub-terminal bi-
nucleate cell occurs, which acts as an ascus mother cell. The nuclei in that cell begin to fuse and form a 
diploid nucleus (Figure 1-5 K). This is followed by a meiotic division which leads to the formation of 
four haploid nuclei. Each of the haploid nuclei divides mitotically, thus resulting in the formation of 
eight haploid nuclei in total (Figure 1-5 N). Each nucleus will then be surrounded by cytoplasm and 
form a cell wall. Hence, a total of eight ascospores are formed in each ascus. With the production of 
large numbers of asci, new hyphae grow around them and form a protective covering, known as 
peridium. The whole structure is called ascocarp, it encappulates many asci and is spherical with no 
openings. Such an ascocarp is known as cleistothecium (Figure 1-5 O) (Kwon-Chung & Sugui 2009; 
Ene & Bennett 2014).  
The asci can be released only after the disbanding of the wall of the ascocarp. Each cell is no larger than 
5µm. When the conditions are favourable, they can germinate by growing out a germ tube (Figure 1-5 
G). The germ tube begins septating and branching itself, thus forming a new haploid mycelium 
(O’Gorman et al. 2009; Khandelwal 2016).

1.2.3 Section Fumigati and its teleomorph Neosartorya 
Aspergillus section Fumigati (subg. Fumigati) and its teleomorphic state Neosartorya are a very 
important group of filamentous fungi in the environment. They are known to cause allergies, food 
spoilage, as well as diseases in humans and animals. (Frisvad & Larsen 2015; Balajee et al. 2005) The 
section Fumigati contains over 50 predominantly soil-borne species (Houbraken et al. 2016; Hubka et 
al. 2016) and 30 Neosartorya species, most with different reproductive strategies. (Giusiano et al. 2017) 
The fungi in the section Fumigati are characterised by uniseriate aspergilli, columnar conidial heads in 
various shades of green, and vesicles in a flask shape (Raper & Fennel 1965; Samson et al. 2007). Some 
species produce thermoresistant ascospores and are ubiquitously distributed in soil, dust, marine 
sediments and sponges, plant tissue, air, food, and organic materials (Hubka et al. 2017). The majority 
of the species are homothallic and produce ascomata, while others are heterothallic or anamorphic with 
unknown sexual state. Many of the anamorphic species are clinically recognised as opportunistic 
pathogens that cause infections in humans and animals, especially the ones belonging to the A. fumigatus 
complex (Balajee et al. 2009; Alcazar-Fuoli et al. 2008). Homothallic species rarely act as pathogens, 
however some infections have been attributed to A. hiratsukae (Guarro et al. 2002), A. thermomutatus 
(Balajee et al. 2005), and A. laciniosus (Malejczyk et al. 2013). It is important to note that these species 
produce heat-resistant ascospores, and have been isolated from heat-treated food products and beverages 
(eg. pasteurised juices), and less commonly from non-processed foods (Pitt and Hocking 2009). Some 
species produce a variety of exometabolites with different biological activities, which increase the 
competitiveness of species in different habitats, or could be of a biotechnological potential (Frisvad et 
al. 2009; Frisvad & Larsen 2015).  
Several of the Neosartorya teleomorph species have been reported to cause human diseases including 
aspergillosis, osteomyelitis, endocarditis, and mycotic keratitis (Balajee et al. 2005; Samson et al. 2007). 
All of the Neosartorya species produce heat-resistant ascospores that are often found in food and drink 
products that have undergone heat treatment (Samson et al. 2007; Frisvad & Samson 1990). Some of the 
identified mycotoxins that they produce can lead to serious health issues in both humans and animals 
(Miao et al. 2015). Some of these teleomorph representatives have been shown to be of value for 
pharmaceuticals production, such as the production of fiscalins by N. fischery, which acts as an inhibitor 
to the binding of substance P to the human neurokinin receptor (Wong et al. 1993), while the A. fumigatus 
strain produces pyripyropenes that act as strong inhibitors of acyl-CoA: cholesterol acyltransferase, 
ribotoxins, and fumagillin that is known to have amoebicidal activity. (Samson et al. 2007). A. fumigatus 
is considered one of the most opportunistic pathogenic fungi (Frisvad & Larsen, 2015; Park & Yu, 2016). 
However, some A. fumigatus strains can be used industrially for the production of cellulase and xylanase 
(Lin, Shen, & Qin, 2016). For example, A. fumigatus N2, an isolate from decaying wood, can produce 
cellulase and xylanase using barley straw as a carbon source (Lin et al., 2016).  
The A. unilateralis clade is a clade within the Fumigati section, closely related to A. fumigatus. The 
clade encompasses six homothallic and two heterothallic species, and three species with an unknown 
sexual state (Hubka et al. 2017). See Figure 1-6 by Hubka et al. (2017). Species of this clade have been 
selected as the main focus of this study. The oldest described species in the clade, A. unilateralis was 
first isolated from rhizospheres of Hibbertia fasciculata and Epacris impressa in Australia. Aspergillus 
assulatus, A. caatingaensis, A. multiplicatus, A. tsunodae, A. marvanovae and A. waksmanii are only 
known from soil or water samples from their type localities (Hubka et al. 2017). A. hiratsukae is 
considered to be the most common species assigned to the clade and was first described in pasteurised 
aloe juice in Japan (1991). It commonly occurs in pasteurised food and drink products (Samson et al. 
2010). The species is distributed worldwide and can sometimes cause opportunistic infections (Miao et 
al. 2015). A. hiratsukae was also described as common and found in abundance in an indoor 
environmental study across the USA between 2008 and 2015 (Jurjevic et al. 2015 unpublished data).

1.2.4 Niches/Habitats 
Several new species within the section Fumigati and its teleomorph Neosartorya have been described 
from soil and marine sponges in the recent years. Hong et al. (2006, 2008) described several species 
including N. assulata, N. coreana, N. laciniosa from soil from North Korea, N. denticulate from soil 
under Elaeis guineensis in Suriname and N. galapagensis from soil in Ecuador. Yaguchi et al. (2010) 
described two new Neosartorya species from soil: N. shendaweii, and N. tsunodae from China and 
Brazil respectively. Eamvijarn et al (2013) described A. siamensis sp. Nov. from forest soil in 
Thailand. Hubka et al. (2013) described A. waksmanii from soil in New Jersey and A. marvanovae from 
contaminated water in the Czech Republic. Nováková et al. (2014) described A. brevistipitatus, A. 
conversis, and A. wyomingensis from reclamation site soils in Wyoming, USA. Matsuzawa et al. (2014) 
described A. caatingaensis and A. pernambucoensis from semi-desert soil in Brazil, and A. huiyaniae 
from desert soil in China. Dethoup et al. (2016) reported A. Similanensis sp. nov. from a marine sponge 
Rhabdermia from a coral reef of the Similan Island, Thailand. 
Soil and marine 
Most commonly, the total population of soil fungi is greatly influenced by the fertility of the soil, its 
type, amount of soluble salts, etc. A study by Warcup et al (1963) showed that most ascospores can 
survive for a lengthy periods of time dormant in soil. It is considered that the majority of spores are 
concentrated near the soil surface at a depth 0 – 20 cm, which would allow for the easier dispersal of the 
spores by various currents (Cardoso et al. 2003). When the conditions in the soil are not favourable, the 
spores can remain dormant until external stimulation in the form of heat, drying, addition of nutrients, 
ethanol (Wicklow 1979). Many fungal species have been isolated from adverse physical and chemical 
conditions in the soil (Aliasgharzadeh et al. 2001).  
Studies have shown that pH, organic matter content, soil P availability, can influence the 
abundance/occurrence of spores in the soil. There is a strong correlation between soil pH and the 
distribution of spores and hyphal growth (Porter et al. 1987), especially at a lower land use intensity 
(Oehl et al. 2010). Smaller spores also have less seasonal variation than larger spores (Palta et al. 2016). 
Many Aspergillus species have been found adapted to dry and hot environments (Giusiano et al. 2017) 
such as desert soils in Brazil (Horie et al. 2011), Kenya (Takada et al. 1974), and Tazhong, central area 
of the Taklimakan desert (Horie et al. 2000). Making them important components of warm and arid 
environmental conditions (Giusiano et al. 2017). They have a significant role in the improvement and 
sustainability of vegetation in forest regions (Horie et al. 2003). In saline soils, sodium and chloride ions 
dominate over other ionic compounds, and it has been shown that sporulation under salt stress can be 
stimulated (Aliasgharzadeh et al. 2001).  
According to Prompanya et al. (2015) Aspergillus species found in marine habitats are one of the most 
prolific source of bioactive secondary metabolites. Aspergillus species have been isolated from marine 
organisms such as sponges, coral, sea fans, and algae (Buaruang et al. 2015). The marine environment 
provides microorganisms with substrates that can differ from soil, fresh water, or plant environments. 
This is the case with algae for example, which not only contain cellulose and xylan, but also a range of 
polysaccharides and monomers of fucose and uronic acid (Wang et al. 2015).

Food and beverage 
Ascospores of Neosartorya species have been isolated from various foods that have gone through heat 
processing. It is considered that the contamination of fruits begins at the fields where the fruit have been 
collected (Salomao et al. 2007; Tournas & Traxler 1994). Ascospores can often survive commercial 
pasteurisation heat treatments, which are usually applied to fruit and fruit juices, thus allowing them to 
spoil said products by germinating and proliferating in such medium. Neosartorya hiratsukae was first 
observed in aloe vera juice by Udagawa et al. (1991). Aloe vera can usually be cultivated on any type of 
soil, with a slight preference towards sandy soil (Surjushe et al. 2008). The soil is usually kept at a lower 
pH. The plant itself provides mono- and polysaccharides, as well as vitamins and enzymes, making its 
juice an ideal medium for proliferation of ascospores (Surjushe et al. 2008). The study by Warcup et al. 
(1963) also showed that exposure of ascospores to temperatures between 50℃ - 70℃ can awake the 
ascospores and stimulate their germination. 
Indoors 
Standards for the quality of indoor environment are constantly rising and fungi play a significant role in 
the overall indoor conditions, be it by musty smells, defacement or structural damage, or causing health 
issues. Moulds have the ability to degrade many man-made materials, especially if they have 
hygroscopic properties, as is the case with many materials used in modern construction (Gravesen et al. 
1994; Frisvad & Samson 1990). A. fumigatus is even known to thrive on inorganic materials (Adan 
1994). 
Wood is highly susceptible to mould growth (Viitanen 1995, Viitanen 1994), especially to Aspergillus 
and Penicillium infestations when that wood material has been modified, such as plywood, OSB, and 
MDF (Chung et al. 1999). In dry wood, water molecules are attracted by hydrogen bonding to hydroxyl 
groups on the carbohydrate macromolecules that make up the cell wall. As the water is being adsorbed 
by the wood, it begins to swell. At ultrastructural level, small capillaries are formed that allow for the 
easier penetration of water, which also enables fungal enzymes to penetrate the cell wall (Smith 1994). 
The wood cell wall is comprised of about 75% cellulose, hemicelluloses, and pectins, making it a 
desirable target for many fungi, such as Fumigati (Wase et al 1984). Often, inside walls are wallpapered, 
which increases the susceptibility of the wall, as paper and glue are great nutrient sources for most indoor 
moulds (Grant et al. 1989). Woodchip wallpapers, common in Denmark, contain cellulosic compounds, 
which can also be a source of nutrition for fungi (Rowan et al. 1999). It has been shown that for 
Aspergillus species, growing on woodchip wallpaper required aw value of 0.98 (Grant et al. 1989).  
Paper is a product of wood materials, and recycled paper is often used as a top layer of prefabricated 
gypsum boards. Common paper-making and recycling processes often underestimate the role of fungi 
and other microorganisms in paper deterioration. Biocides are often used, however they are hardly 
retained until the final product is finished (Tissier & Magne 2001). The majority of fungal organisms 
will survive the paper/paper board manufacturing process (Viitanen et al. 1994), and the majority of 
these organisms will hydrolise cellulose and starch. They have been shown to be highly resistant to 
adverse environmental conditions such as high temperatures (Viitanen 1995).  
Prefabricated gypsum boards are a very common material used in modern Danish construction. Due to 
the paper used to re-inforce the material, the boards can be highly susceptible to mould growth. 
Especially ones with cellulytic activity (Miller et al. 2000, Anderssen et al. 2001; Pasanen et al. 1992). 
The gypsum material is also a good environment for fungal growth and its susceptibility is correlated to 
the relative nutrient content of gypsum (Adan 1994) and the additives used to make it more hygroscopic 
at lower humidity (Andersson et al. 1997; Pasanen et al. 1992). Mineral plasters, based on lime gypsum,

harden as a result of both hydration and carbonation. During carbonation the alkalinity is reduced to

Disease and allergies 
Fungi are not only deteriorative agents, but can also cause illnesses and allergies. Aspergillus species are 
some of the most frequent moulds isolated from clinical samples (Alastruey-Izquierdo et al. 2014). They 
are known to cause a variety of infections in humans, from cutaneous to disseminated, as well as allergies 
and chronic infections (Marr et al. 2002). A. hiratsukae (syn. Neosartorya hiratsukae) from the genus 
Fumigati has been reported in human infections (Koutroutsos et al. 2010; Guarro et al. 2002; Alcazar-
Fuoli et al. 2008). The species has been isolated from respiratory samples, including cases of invasive 
aspergilosis, which have resulted in poor outcomes despite the antifungal treatments (Alcazar-Fuoli et 
al. 2008). A. hiratsukae has been isolated from patients with peritonitis (Koutroutsos et al. 2010), allergic 
fungal rhinosinusitis (Shivaprakash et al. 2009), as well as cerebral aspergilosis (Guarro et al. 2002). 
Studies with antifungals have shown good activity of the antifungals, however most tests have resulted 
in failure (Alastruey-Izquierdo et al. 2014).  
Both biotic and abiotic factors should be considered when assessing factors affecting spore abundance 
and presence in various habitats. Furthermore, environmental conditions are a key factor that affects the 
physio-chemical nature of both the habitat and the fungal organism. 
1.3 Extrinsic factors 
Since members of the genus are filamentous fungi, extrinsic factors such as water, pH, temperature, and 
gas composition can have a profound influence on their growth and synthesis of secondary metabolites. 
Hence, further knowledge of the effects of the mentioned above physio-biochemical parameters can 
provide us with ideas for new methodology for preventing their growth and mycotoxin production. One 
must remember, however, that interactions between several of the previously mentioned factors are often 
of a higher importance than individual factors acting in isolation.  
1.3.1 Water availability  
Water is undoubtedly the most important factor for determining whether mould growth will begin in a 
building (Smith 1994). Water availability is most commonly expressed as Equilibrium Relative 
Humidity (ERH), water activity (aw), or water potential (Ψ). ERH is the relative humidity of the 
intergranular atmosphere in equilibrium with the water in a substrate; aw is the ratio of the vapour 
pressure of water over a substrateto that of pure water at the same temperature and pressure. Many 
moulds have an optimal aw of 0.96-0.98. According to Lacey & Magan (1991) the optimum water 
activity for growth for A. fumigatus is 0.97. Many Aspergillus species differ in their aw requirements. 
aw influences each of the main phases of the growth cycle, and studies on Aspergillus have shown that 
the minimum value required for germination that the one needed for growth sustainability. Likewise, the 
minimum value for linear growth was lower than the minimum required in the asexual state (Adan 1994). 
The germination process requires a slightly higher aw value (~0.02) than the critical value for growth. 
Older spores would also require a longer time to germinate. In addition to water uptake from the 
environment, several processes, such as the breakdown of carbohydrates, in the cell metabolism also 
produce small amounts of water. These can also take part in inducing fungal development (Adan 1994).

1.3.2.1 Heat resistance 
Several factors can influence the growth of heat-resistant fungi. Such include the level of original 
contamination, type of microorganism, product composition, storage temperature, pH, heat treatment, 
degree of ascospore survival, presence of inhibitors, etc. Extensive heating of the product will affect 
many of its sensory attributes, hence most products are subjected to milder treatment. Thus, allowing for 
the survival of ascospores should their count be high prior to the heat treatment.  
Problems caused by heat-resistant moulds were first established in the 1930s in a canning factory in 
England. They had discovered that heat-resistant moulds can survive the pasteurisation of the product 
and thus spoil the finished product (Tournas V., 1994). During thermal processing, it is important that 
the ascospores contaminating the food products should first be activated into vegetative cells in order to 
make them susceptible to the heat inactivation. (Salomao et al. 2007) Food spoilage due to ascospore 
germination can occur if the food or drink products are subjected to a thermal process that can break the 
dormancy of the ascospores, however not severe enough to cause their inactivation (Kikoku et al. 2005). 
Pasteurisation is the process of applying enough heat when treating the product to destroy vegetative 
cells of pathogens (FDA, 2017). The level of thermal tolerance of the ascospores of heat-resistant 
moulds, varies from strain to strain and the composition of the heating medium (Salomao et al. 2007).  
Studies by Kikoku et al. (2009) have shown that the number of germinated ascospores logarithmically 
increased with the prolonged exposure to heat. Spoils by Neosartorya species have been often reported, 
causing spoilage in juices after pasteurisation. In aloe processing, the liquid is treated with temperature 
of 65°C for 15minutes at most, in order not to affect the biological activities of the aloe juice. Extended 
periods of higher temperatures highly reduce the activity levels of certain compounds present in the

1.3.3 Alkalinity 
Unlike temperature, the internal pH is not solely dependent on the environment. The cell has the ability, 
to an extent, to control the passage of ions in and out of the cell. Fungi may also have the ability to alter 
the pH of the environment by releasing alkaline products, or removing certain ions from the 
environment. (Smith et al.1994) Enzyme activity is considered closely related to pH, as each enzyme is 
active and can display maximum activity within a specific and narrow range of pH (Adan 1994). pH is 
also a factor for inhibiting membrane transport. It may influence the configuration of certain membrane 
proteins, thus the activity of the said proteins (Gaudy & Gaudy 1980).  
The control of pH level is quite difficult, especially since the growth of the organism itself results in a 
change in the pH of the substrate. In the case of food and beverage production, the addition of 
preservatives can drastically change the pH. Such changes can affect metabolic processes, germination, 
and fungal growth (Smith 1994). Fungicides of natural origin are increasingly being investigated for the 
use in fungal contamination treatment. The use of organic acids has been widely recommended, as most 
own the status of GRAS (Generally Recognised as Safe) by the FDA. (Alcano et al. 2015). In the 
production of certain foods, several organic acids can be used (e.g., acetic, citric, and lactic acids), 
although some can cause sensory changes when used in large amounts (Taniguchi et al., 1998).  
Vinegar (acetic acid) has been reported to have a remedial effect on fungi when applied in various 
situations. Vinegar vapour application has been shown to prevent the germination of conidia in fruit-
decaying fungi such as Penicillium expansum and Botrytis cincerea (Alcazar-Fuoli et al. 2008) The 
Australian Mould Guidelines even include vinegar and water solution as a remediation of indoor fungal 
contaminations (Kemp & Neumeister-Kemp 2005). A study by Rogawansamy et al. (2015) showed that 
the efficacy of natural antifungal agents such as acetic acid is determined by how well the agent would 
inhibit the growth of the organism, and more ineffective agents would only inhibit the sporulation or 
inhibit the growth for a limited time. The study also suggests that vinegar may not be an effective agent 
against the diverse fungal genera found in the indoor environment (Rogawansamy et al. 2015).  
1.4 Secondary metabolites 
Secondary metabolites are compounds of low molecular weight produced by many different 
microorganisms, including both bacteria and fungi (Madigan & Martinko 2006, Yim et al. 2007; 
Brakhage & Schroeckh 2011). They are often produced in the late growth phase of the producing 
organism, and are usually not crucial for the organism’s reproduction or growth. However, they may be 
useful for intercellular communication or self-defence during the developmental stages of the organism. 
Formation of secondary metabolites highly depends on environmental conditions and are often not 
constitutively expressed (Hoffmeister & Keller 2007) but at advantageous times. Typically, the 
secondary metabolite genes are clustered in the microbial genome. Clusters containing a transcription 
factor (TF), which regulates the expression of the genes within or outside the cluster (Schwab & Keller 
2008; Fox & Howlett 2008; Yu & Keller 2005) Secondary metabolite production can also be regulated 
by more global TFs that allows for the regulation of genes in response to external factors, such as nutrient 
and water availability, pH, temperature, and light (Fox & Howlett 2008; Yu & Keller 2005). 
Many secondary metabolites are of great importance in modern medicine and biotechnological 
production due to their antibiotic and pharmaceutical properties (Newman & Cragg 2012). Many 
==PAGE:23==

secondary metabolites have also been found to cause diseases in both animals and plants (Fox & Howlett 
2008; Newman & Cragg 2012).  
1.4.1 Secondary metabolites from Aspergillus species 
Aspergillus species produce an array of secondary metabolites (Fox & Howlett 2008). Examples include 
many compounds of beneficial use, such as lovastin, known as a cholesterol-lowering drug; and also as 
compounds with adverse effects such as aflatoxins and fumonisins (Nielsen et al. 2009).  
The metabolites often have unknown functions in the fungal organism. However, it may be used as a 
defence mechanism or to compete with other organisms in the fungus’ natural habitat (Yu & Keller 
2005; Fox & Howlett 2008). As mentioned above, the secondary metabolite genes are usually clustered 
together in the fungal genome and Aspergilli do not stray from the path. They have been found to contain 
between 30 to 40 clusters per species (Brakhage & Schroeck 2011). The availability of genomic 
sequences from Aspergillus greatly facilitates the identification of many genes, involved in the 
production of secondary metabolites. In a way, many of the biosynthetic pathways are quite similar, 
however very few metabolite gene clusters are shared between species (Fedorova et al. 2008; Brakhage 
& Schroeck 2011; Inglis et al. 2013). Hence, only few metabolites can be found across various 
Aspergillus species (139). Different species within a section would most commonly produce similar 
metabolites (Larsen et al. 2007; Nielsen et al. 2009). A few selected secondary metabolites are shown in 
Figure 1-7. These extrolites may have a similar function and yet be chemically different. Growth at 
higher temperatures has also been considered as a factor for the pathogenicity of the section Fumigati 
(Abad et al. 2010; Frisvad & Larsen, 2016).  
In the study by Hubka et al. (2017) several specific metabolites of the Unilateralis clade have been 
observed. A number of species were shown to produce aszonapyrones, including A. assulatus, A. 
caatingaensis, A. hiratsukae, A. multiplicatus, A. tsunodae, A. unilateralis, and A. waksmanii. 
Aszonapyrones are common throughout sect. Fumigati and are associated with ascomata. A. turcosus 
was found to produce gliotoxin. Polyketides such as orlandin and siderin have been found exclusively 
in A. nishimurae, thus can be used as potential chemical markers for this species. Helvolic acid was 
found only in A. multiplicatus, and similarly only A. turcosus produced epiaszonalenins. Other exo-
metabolites’ structures have yet to be elucidated. One particular metabolite, SNUT, was only found in 
this set of species from section Fumigati and was detected in A. marvanovae and A. turcosus. 
Interestingly, A. waksmanii, A. marvanovae and one isolate of A. tasmanicus produced an apolar 
indoloterpene that is indicative of a perfect state.

Several extrolites produced by the section Fumigati have been shown to have a role in infection 
processes. However, their roles may differ in their natural habitats. When growing on compost and soil, 
the organisms will need a certain profile of extrolites, which will differ to the one while they act as 
opportunistic pathogens in vertebrates (Miao et al. 2015). Cellulases, for example, are necessary to 
produce while composting, whereas hemolysins would only be important for infections in vertebrates 
(Abad et al. 2010). Similarly, as species in the section Fumigati are mostly thermophilic, they will be 
competing with other thermophilic organisms and will need to produce antifungals and antibiotics to 
overcome competition. Species of the Unilateralis clade, including A. assulatus, A. marvanovae, A. 
unilateralis, and A. waksmanii, produce very few extrolites, hence they would not be expected to produce 
any opportunistic pathogens (Abad et al. 2010; Frisvad & Larsen, 2016).  
1.5 Characterisation

microorganisms. It is known that the number of fungal species continues to grow, therefore there is a 
need for accurate characterisation and identification. Hence, different concepts have be created and used 
over the years to define fungal species. 
In general, identification of Aspergillus species is based on the morphological characteristics of the 
colony along with microscopic identification. According to the American Society for Microbiology

1.5.1 Morphological 
Identification based on colour and appearance of growing colonies and microscopic details of 
morphological structures is the first method of classification. It is especially important for fungal 
contaminations present indoors, in homes or production facilities (Lusta et al. 2003; Russell 1974). 
Ascospores usually take a long time to form and special knowledge and techniques are needed in order 
to identify them rapidly. Issues with morphological characterisation may arise as quite often the 
phenotypes of fungal species can be too sensitive to growth conditions and external factors (Lusta et al. 
2003). 
1.5.1.1 Fungal culture 
The most direct and quite often conclusive method of establishing the cause of contamination/infection, 
is growing the fungus obtained as a sample. There is no single medium or growth condition that permits 
the cultivation of fungal species. Different species have adapted to their specific preferences in culture 
conditions as a result of their nutritional needs (Russell 1974; VanderMolen et al. 2013). Fungi grow 
best, in laboratory conditions, in cultures that best mirror their natural habitat conditions. Their growth 
would significantly improve when gown on media that has been formulated based on the natural 
materials they have been isolated from (Russell 1974).  
Media composition, water activity, pH, and temperature should be taken into consideration when 
selecting the fungal culture media (Russell 1974). Of course, media cannot copy the environment 
perfectly, as in most cases the organisms will be growing under low nutrient conditions. In laboratories, 
artificial enrichment media is most often used. Media selection should always be done in consideration 
with the organism’s needs, their metabolism, and activities such as sporulation, or metabolite production 
(VanderMolen et al. 2013). It is important to remember that when isolating organisms from a surface 
that cannot be sterilised, the chances of bacterial growth are quite high. Therefore, Antibiotic Agar or a 
change of the pH of the agar can create an unfavourable environment for bacterial growth (Walting 1977; 
Russell 1974).  
The energy needs of the organism are often met by the addition of a carbon source, such as sucrose or 
glucose. Most microorganisms require a nitrogen source, in the form of yeast extract, as well as amino 
acids, peptides, etc. Yeast extract is widely used when mineral and vitamins need to be introduced to the 
media (VanderMolen et al. 2013).There are two general types of media for culturing fungi: natural and 
synthetic (Basu et al. 2015).  
Natural media are comprised of natural substrates, much like wood, seeds, leaves, corn meal, wheat 
germ, oatmeal, etc. Natural media are easy to prepare and the combination of extracts and sugars ensures 
the medium is rich in minerals and organic nutrients. In some cases the exact chemical composition is 
unknown. For example the chemical composition of the broth of Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA), is not 
100% known. Some examples include Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA), Corn Meal Agar (CMA), V8 Juice 
Agar, and others (Basu et al. 2015; Russell 1974).  
On the other hand, synthetic media contain known ingredients of known composition. This allows for 
an accurate replication of the media with each preparation, as they are made with a defined amount of 
carbohydrates, nitrogen, and vitamin sources. Such are for example Czapek medium (CYA), Yeast 
Extract Medium (YES), Malt Extract Agar (MEA), etc (Basu et al. 2015). MEA is frequently used when 
culturing fungi from wood and soil. 
==PAGE:26==
16 
 
Studies of Aspergillus species have shown that V8 media is ideal for conidial production (Chang et al. 
2006), which can aid a more confident identification. Enzyme activities can also be assayed using fungal 
media. High purity dyed and cross-linked insoluble AZCL-HE-Cellulose is a perfect example. AZCL 
media are often used for broad enzyme screening, with a focus on endo-activities (Pedersen et al. 2007). 
Progression of the blue zone, occurring due to the hydrolysis of the substrate indicates whether the 
organism is able to produce cellulases and xylanases to effectively hydrolyse cellulose (Pedersen et al. 
2007). Media with reduced aw have been used for many years for the isolation and enumeration of 
moulds. Traditionally, such media are based on NaCl (AŞKUN et al. 2007) A commonly used low water 
activity medium (0.95 aw) containing 18% (w/w) glycerol and 2µg/mL dichloran, is Dichloran-Glycerol 
Agar Base medium, or DG18. Another medium, containing dichloran (aw) is Dichloran-Rose Bengal 
Medium, or DRYES. Studies on ordinary agar media have shown that optimum water activity for rapid 
growth was the lowest when the temperature of incubation was at the maximum for the particular fungus 
(Ayerst 1969). 
The manipulation of environment and nutrition has been shown in several cases to have significant 
impact on the quantity and type of bioactive metabolites produced (Bode et al. 2002; Miao et al. 2006). 
The optimisation of the initial step, the microbial culture, followed by temperature, pH, and water 
activity adjustments may have substantial effects on the success of screening for secondary metabolite 
production (VanderMolen et al. 2013). Studies by Bracarense & Takashi (2014) on Aspergillus species 
have shown that the production of kojic acid was in higher yields when the media contained higher 
amounts of glucose. When the glucose was replaced by sucrose, the production of kojic acid was also 
predominant due to the fact that glucose can be more easily obtained after hydrolysis of sucrose. The 
methodical modification of culture and growth conditions is referred to as an OSMAC (one strain, many 
compounds) approach (Bode et al. 2002). The concept has been shown ideal for exploiting the 
metabolomics diversity of fungal organisms (Bode et al. 2002; Miao et al. 2006).  
1.5.1.2 
Microscopy 
The identification of most fungi also depends on the observation of microscopic characteristics. Many 
can easily be identified to their genus or species by observing their features under direct microscopical 
examination. Fungal tissue (mycelium, fruiting bodies, sporulating structures) can be taken up with a 
sterilised needle, for example, and placed on a microscope slide (McClenny 2005) and a drop of 
mounting fluid is added. It is important that the fruiting bodies are squashed first. Sometimes, taking up 
some of the agar along with the sporulating structures, then gently melting the agar can also be 
performed.  
As Aspergillus species produce many dry conidia, it is recommended that a drop of alcohol is used in 
order to wash the mass of hydrophobic conidia (Samson et al. 2002). In many cases, transparent adhesive 
tape can be used to take up sporulating structures. Mounting fluid is added to a slide and after lightly 
touching the colonies with the tape, it is placed over the slide. Shear’s mounting fluid which consists of 
potassium acetate, water, glycerine, and 95% ethanol is a common mounting fluid (McClenny 2005). 
The described method is suitable for use with a microscope at low power (100 and 400 times). Later, a 
drop of Shear’s mounting fluid can be added followed by a cover glass. Then, immersion oil can be used 
for higher magnification. The “tape” method is also useful when examining contamination of surfaces. 
It would both show the presence of mould growth and presence of other microorganisms and mites 
(Crons et al. 2009).  
1.5.2 Analytical chemistry 
A variety of physiological and biochemical methods for the identification of fungal organisms have also 
been devised (Frisvad & Larsen 2015).  
==PAGE:27==
17 
 
Analytical chemistry is the study of utilising instruments and methods to identify, purify, and quantify 
target compounds (Skoog et al. 2013). It is used when rapid identification of a species is needed, or for 
the identification of compounds that may be present in the original sample. 
Chemical analysis is performed on a small fraction of the original material, thus the sample should 
represent the composition of the material as closely as possible. In most cases, the sample will be 
prepared into solutions using suitable solvents, in which the analytes of the sample should dissolve 
completely (Skoog et al. 2013). The polarity of the solvent should be taken into consideration when 
extracting natural products. Generally, non-polar solvents are used to extract non-polar molecules, and 
polar solvents for polar substances. The pH of the solvent is just as critical and should also be considered 
prior to sample preparation. The pH can be adjusted in order to obtain maximum extraction without 
damaging or breaking down the compound (Houghton & Raman 1998). 
1.5.2.1 Chromatography 
Chromatography is a technique used to separate complex mixtures into individual parts. It utilises the 
selective distribution of various substances in different phases in order to elute the mixtures from 
stationary phase via a mobile phase. Different mixtures will move at different rate along the stationary 
phase, hence separation is eventually achieved.  
Depending on the purpose, chromatography can be analytical or preparative. Analytical refers to the 
process of identification and quantification of relative ingredients of analytes in a mixture by using the 
separation molecules with a small amount of material. In contrast, the aim of preparative 
chromatography is to isolate and purify the target molecules with relatively larger amounts of material 
for downstream use (Hostettmann et al. 1998).  
Chromatography is classified into Gas Chromatography (GC), which utilises gas during the mobile 
phase; and Liquid Chromatography (LC), that uses liquid during the mobile phase. Both types are most 
often carried out in a column as a stationary phase, with the mobile phase passing through. Both can be 
used for analytical or preparative analysis. Based on the separation mechanism of the sample 
components in two phases, chromatography can be classified as adsorption chromatography, partition 
chromatography, 
ion 
exchange 
chromatography, 
size 
exclusion 
chromatography, 
affinity 
chromatography, etc. 
1.5.2.1.1 HPLC 
Nowadays, High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is applied in various disciplines, 
including pharmaceutical, illicit drug detection, laboratory analyses. It utilises high pressure of 50 - 250 
bar. After loading, the liquid sample is pushed by a liquid with high pressure (the mobile phase), through 
a column with absorbent material (the stationary phase). Compounds with higher affinity for the 
stationary phase will attach on it and will be eluted slower than the ones with higher affinity for the 
mobile phase. Hence, separation occurs. See Figure 1-8. According to the polarity of the stationary 
phase, two sub-classes of HPLC exist: normal phase and reverse phase. In normal phase HPLC, the 
stationary phase is more polar than the mobile phase. Silica beads are commonly used as an adsorbent 
material due to the high sensitivity and sample capacity. In reverse phase LC, the hydrophobic stationary 
phase is more non-polar than the mobile phase (Skoog et al. 2013).  
Columns are tubular, with an internal diameter between 0.050 – 4.6 mm, and can vary in length between 
30 – 250mm. They are packed with silica beads (3 - 50µm), and a pore size between 100 to 1000Å, with 
300Å being the most common for peptides and proteins, and 100Å for smaller molecules.  
==PAGE:28==
18 
 
During the mobile phase, two main solvents are typically applied: solvent A and solvent B. A is aqueous, 
and B is organic (methanol, propanol, etc). A is usually an HPLC-grade water with 0.1% acid. Solvent 
B usually is acetonitrile or methanol with 0.1% acid. The acid improves the peak shape outcome and 
acts as a source of protons in RP-LC. Common acids used are: formic, trifluoroacetic, and acetic acid 
(Guzzetta, 2016; SIGMA, Mondello L., 2002).  
1.5.2.2 
Diode Array Detection 
After the separation of compounds in the column, there is a need to determine the compounds separated. 
Most common method of non-destructive detection used nowadays is UV, which Diode-Array-Detection 
(DAD) belongs to. This is due to it giving off a UV spectrum data that can be compared to a database 
(Scott R., 2005; Dewick, 2009). The light from the lamp shines directly onto the flow cell and as it passes 
through it is dispersed by diffraction grating. The amount of dispersed light is estimated for each 
wavelength in the photodiode array. The diode also measures the intensity of the light and whether a 
certain wavelength has been lost in the flow cell (Scott R., 2005). Compared with a UV-VIS detector, 
DAD has multiple photodiode arrays, allowing it to obtain information over a wide range of wavelengths 
at the same time, thus making high speed spectroscopy possible. When analysis complex mixtures with 
little information about absorbance, this is critical. It also confirms whether peak represents a single 
compound or it is a composite peak. It allows for the collection of single targeted compounds in the 
fraction during the purification process (Nielsen et al. 2011).  
The Ultraviolet-Visible (UV) Spectra refers to an absorption spectra generated by the molecular 
absorption of electromagnetic wave in the UV visible spectral region. In general, the wavelength is 
between 200nm- 1000 nm. In UV spectra, the absorption properties of the compound to electromagnetic 
radiation are described by an absorption curve, which displays the position of an absorption peak and 
the absorption intensity of the absorption spectrum. Molecules containing absorbent groups such as 
carbon and carbon double bonds (-C=C-), carbonyl (-COOH), aldehyde (-CHO), carboxyl (-OH), azo (-
N=N-), nitro group (-N=O), have the capacity to generate absorption in UV Vis spectral region.  
1.5.2.3 Mass Spectrometry 
Mass spectrometry (MS) is a powerful technique used to obtain abundant information about the structure 
of unknown compounds, the qualitative and quantitative known or unknown compositions of mixtures.

It ionises analyte molecules, separates the ions based on their mass-to-charge ratio, and outputs an 
electrical signal (Skoog et al. 2013). It is quite commonly performed in conjunction with LC, where the 
compounds would be separated by the LC prior to being directed as a stream to the MS (Russel 1997). 
In order to be able to measure the specificities of individual molecules, an MS converts those molecules 
to ions in order for them to be moved about and manipulated by electric and magnetic fields. An MS has 
three essential functions with the respective components: The ion source- when the sample is ionized, 
usually to cations by losing an electron; The Mass Analyser- when the ions are sorted by mass and 
charge; and The detector- when the separated ions are measured and the results are displayed (Russel 
1997). 
MS is usually presented as a vertical bar graph, where each bar is representing an ion of a specific mass-
to-charge ratio (m/z). The length of the bar indicated the relative abundance of the said ion. The base 
peak represents the most intense ion, which is assigned abundance of 100. Most of the ions in an MS 
have a single mass, therefore the m/z value equals the mass. Different isotopes of a given element can 
easily be distinguished. Results from the MS can be further supported by DAD to determine compounds 
that have similar properties. Data is usually represented in a mass chromatogram (Dewick, 2009; Russel, 
1997).

Aims and objectives 
 
The overall aim of the study was to achieve a deeper understanding of the organisms’ requirements and 
abilities. The objectives were to generate a matrix of growth specifications and characteristics of each 
individual member of the clade (Ch. 3.1 Culture characterisation) in a variety of created environments 
and conditions (Ch. 3.2), and investigate whether such changes can have an effect on the organisms’ 
ability to produce secondary metabolites (Ch. 3.1.1.1) which can be a mean for the correct identification 
of the species or have any biotechnological potentials; as well as assay the organisms’ resilience when 
exposed to various antifungal treatments (Ch. 3.2). Furthermore, an aim was to follow their ability to 
proliferate in “real life” conditions, by providing the species with limited nutrients and placing them on 
materials commonly used in indoor constructions (Ch. 3.4), as well as media that would induce enzyme 
production (Ch. 3.3). 
In order to achieve those endeavors, the project outline was set out to assist with the assay selection, 
their executions and outcomes.  
I. 
Fungal culture characterisation was selected in order to investigate the closely related species 
of the Unilateralis clade and their tolerance towards changes in the environmental conditions. 
a. Their ability to produce secondary metabolites at those different conditions was of 
interest. Hence, chemical analysis using chromatography and UV analysis were selected 
to assay their extrolites. 
II. 
Fungal survival and resilience was assayed in several antifungal susceptibility tests both to the 
mycelium and spores. 
III. 
The ability of the organisms to proliferate in “real life” conditions was tested by introducing 
the different members of the clade to various media with limited nutrition, or media that would 
induce enzymatic production.

2.3 Chemical analysis 
For the chemical analysis, the samples were grown at 15℃, 25℃, and 37℃ on YES and CYA media 
as suggested by literature and (Frisvad & Thrane 1987). 
2.3.1 Plug extraction 
Plugs were taken using a 6mm plug drill from each strain and transferred to Eppendorf tubes. 800µL of 
isopropanol:ethylacetate 1:3 v/v with 1%formic acid were added to each tube for extraction. Then, the 
samples were loaded into an ultrasonic bath for 60min at full effect. Then the extracts were transferred 
to new labelled tubes using a glass pipette and placed in a hood to dry overnight. After the extracts were 
dry, they were re-dissolved using 300µL methanol, and centrifuged at 10RCF for 5minutes. 200 µL of 
the supernatants were transferred to labelled HPLC insert vials for LC-MS analysis. All steps followed 
the Plug extraction protocol provided by DTU Bioengineering, Metabolomics Facility, building 221, 
Document no. CMB0001 01.10.2014. 
2.3.2 Analytical LC 
All HPLC samples were dissolved in HPLC-grade methanol and centrifuged for 3minutes. For each 
sample, 200µL of supernatant was transferred to HPLC vials. The samples were analysed on an 
Agilent I100 system, with a Luna C18 II column, and equipped with a photo diode array detector 
(DAD). All samples were analysed using the method of Frisvad & Thrane (1987).  
2.4 Spore survival & treatment response 
The aim of this study was to assess the relative efficacies of various cleaning agents known to be used 
for indoor fungal remediation as well as response to stress such as heat treatment (pasteurisation) and 
reduced water activity (Rogawansamy et al. 2015). This included the use of a common antifungal 
product on the Danish market Rodalone®, ethanol, vinegar (5.0% acetic acid), subjecting the spores to 
various temperatures, and growth on media with reduced aw.  
2.4.1 Spore count  
Prior to all assays, spore count was performed in order to establish the number of colonies present in 
the spore suspensions and determine whether dilutions would be necessary. This was executed in two 
ways. 
Firstly, a hemocytometer (depth = 0.05mm, volume = 0.0025mm2) was used to count the spores present 
in the suspension. Before each use, the hemocytometer and coverslip were cleaned with EtOH 96%. 
The coverslip was then affixed to the hemocytometer. The flask with spore suspension was gently 
==PAGE:34==
24 
 
swirled to ensure the cells are evenly distributed. 100 µL of the suspension was then pipetted underneath 
the coverslip, allowing the suspension to be drawn by capillary action. The number of cells was then 
counted using a hand tally counter, counting all cells within four, 16corner squares. The total cell 
density per mL was calculated using the formula cell count*5*106, which was also verified by using an 
online algorithm provided by www.hemocytometer.org.  
In order to count the viable spores present in the suspension, the suspension was diluted 1:100 by adding 
100 µL of spore suspension to 900 µL dw. 200 µL of each diluted spore suspension was used to 
inoculate MEA plates which were incubated at 25℃ for 7 days. After 7 days, the number of colonies 
on each plate was counted.  
2.4.2 Water activity assay 
For this assay, media with reduced water activity were used: Dichloran-Glycerol Agar Base medium, 
or DG18, (0.95 aw) containing 18% (w/w) glycerol and 2µg/mL dichloran, as well as Dichloran-Rose 
Bengal Medium, or DRYES, (0.95 aw) containing 2µg/mL dichloran. See Appendix I for media 
specifications. 
Each sample was inoculated using 3 point inoculation on both media and incubated at 25℃ for 14 days. 
Each culture was examined for colony growth, size, and colour after 7 and 14 days. The differences in 
the fungal diversity between the two media were noted.  
2.4.3 pH treatment 
In order to assess response to antifungal treatment with low pH agents, the assay was designed with 
vinegar. Store-bought vinegar (5% acetic acid, pH=2.64) was selected due to the increased interest in 
the use of “natural” products for fungal remediation. Undiluted vinegar was used for the assessment 
due to the fact that the pH will not change drastically with dw dilution. 
The strains were also grown on V8 Juice Agar media (V8) due to the vegetable juices that provide trace 
ingredients necessary for fungal growth.  
2.4.3.1 pH antifungal efficacy assay 
Spore suspensions were prepared by flooding fungal culture plates with 3mL of sterile dw and a sterile 
loop was used to agitate colonies. 200 µL of each spore suspension was used to inoculate MEA plates 
which were then left to dry at room T℃ for 15minutes. The inoculated plates were sectioned in halves 
and a 4mm plug drill was used to punch a hole in the middle of each section. 20 µL of vinegar was 
pipetted directly into one of the holes punched, and 20 µL of dw in the other. DW was used as a negative 
control for fungal growth inhibition. The plates were then sealed with parafilm and incubated at 25℃ 
for 14 days. Observations of fungal growth and formation of inhibition zones around the punched holes 
were observed 7 and 14 days after inoculation. An agent was categorised as having antifungal activity 
when the diameter of the inhibition zone was larger than 4.5mm, 0.5mm larger than the diameter of the 
wells (Rogawansamy et al. 2015). 
2.4.4 Antifungal treatment 
The antifungal agents used in the study are commonly available and commonly used in Denmark. 
Rodalone® is a broad-spectrum disinfectant used indoors. It is a common household product that is also 
used to sanitise surfaces and equipment. It contains <5% Benzalkonium chloride (BZK/BAC), which 
is an organic salt, classified as a quaternary ammonium compound used due to its surfactant properties. 
Rodalone® was prepared at two different concentrations: 10% (undiluted, from the bottle), and 1% 
(diluted with dw). Ethanol was chosen due to its common recommendation in fungal remediation and 
anecdotal support in disinfection of hard surfaces both in home-settings and production facilities. Two 
==PAGE:35==
25 
 
concentrations of ethanol were used in this assessment due to their common recommendation: 70% and 
90%.   
2.4.4.1 Antifungal efficacy assay 
Spore suspensions were prepared by flooding fungal culture plates with 3mL of sterile dw and a sterile 
loop was used to agitate colonies. 200 µL of each spore suspension was used to inoculate MEA plates 
which were then left to dry at room T℃ for 15minutes. The inoculated plates were sectioned in halves 
and a 4mm plug drill was used to punch a hole in the middle of each section.  
For the Rodalone® test, 20 µL of 10% Rodalone® was pipetted directly into one of the holes punched, 
and 1% Rodalone® was added to the other.  
For the EtOH test, 20 µL of 70% EtOH was pipetted directly into one of the holes punched, and 90% 
EtOH was added to the other. 
DW was used as a negative control for fungal growth inhibition on separate plates. The plates were then 
sealed with parafilm and incubated at 25℃ for 14 days. Observations of fungal growth and formation 
of inhibition zones around the punched holes were observed 7 and 14 days after inoculation. An agent 
was categorised as having antifungal activity when the diameter of the inhibition zone was larger than 
4.5mm, 0.5mm larger than the diameter of the well (Rogawansamy et al. 2015). The diameter was 
recorded and converted to area (mm2), subtracting the area of the well. 
2.4.4.2 
Spore survival assay 
In order to test spore susceptibility to treatment, a survival assay was performed. 80 µL undiluted spore 
suspension from each sample was mixed with 100 µL EtOH at 70% and 90% respectively. Each strain 
was subjected to 70% and 90% EtOH for 2, 6, and 10minutes respectively. After the desired time had 
passed 7.2mL dw was added to each sample to reduce the EtOH concentration <1%. 200 µL of each 
spore suspension was used to inoculate MEA plates, which were then incubated at 25℃ for 7 days. 
After 7 days, the number of colonies on each plate was counted.  
2.4.5 Heat treatment 
The aim of this assay was to simulate pasteurisation conditions based on the process description of aloe 
vera juice producers, and asses the survival of fungal spores and their response to heat stress. Based on 
the review by Ahlawat & Khatkar (2011), two sets of tests were performed.  
In the first, 1:100 diluted spore suspensions were used. 1.5mL diluted suspension of each sample was 
added to a 2mL Eppendorf tube and then placed in a dry block incubator at 65℃ for 2, 6, 10, and 
15minutes, and at 90℃ for 30sec, 60sec, and 2minutes (similar to flash pasteurisation). 200 µL of each 
treated spore suspension was used to inoculate MEA plates which were then incubated at 25℃ for 7 
days. Observations of fungal growth were done 7days after inoculation and the number of colonies on 
the plates was counted. 
In the second test, the time needed for the sample to reach the desired temperature was taken and based 
on the FDA standards for heat treatment of raw products, more time was given to the samples while 
being subjected to heat. The samples took 4minutes to reach an acceptable temperature by the FDA 
guidelines (FDA 2001), and then they were treated for 2, 6, 10, and 15minutes at 65℃, and 30sec, 
60sec, and 2min at 90℃. The samples were flash cooled on ice for 8 minutes, similarly to flash cooling 
in production. 200 µL of each treated spore suspension was used to inoculate MEA plates which were 
then incubated at 25℃ for 7 days. Observations of fungal growth were done 7days after inoculation 
and the number of colonies on the plates was counted.

The purpose of this study was to screen the species from the Unilateralis clade for their production of

3.1.1.1 Metabolite production 
The strains were tested for metabolite production on YES and CYA media, after 7 days at 15℃, 25℃, 
and 37℃. The results were then compared in order to observe whether there are substantial differences 
in metabolite production in these environments; and whether the extrolites can be used for species 
identification or biotechnological applications. The profile of extrolites produced by the species from 
the Unilateralis clade has been collected by literature sources, in conjunction with actual metabolite 
profiling. The conclusions about the extrolites detected in this analysis have been reached by comparing 
with the guides by Frisvad & Thrane (1987, 1993), comparing the similarities in the UV spectra and 
literature data. There were considerably fewer extrolites produced by the organisms grown at 15℃ and 
37℃. 
Some of the metabolites discovered, can be considered as primary metabolites, found in the membranes 
of all fungi: such as ergosterol and linoleic acid. Usually, secondary metabolites are quite specific for 
certain species or group of species, making them less common and more desirable for various 
applications.  
Primary metabolites 
Ergosterols (triterpenes) 
Ergosterol is a sterol, found in fungal cell membranes, that serves a similar function to cholesterol in 
mammalian cells. Enzymes involved in its production have become a target in pharmaceuticals as it is 
a provitamin form of vitamin D2. Several ergosterol derivatives have been reported to be produced by 
species in the Fumigati section such as ergosterolpalmitat, ergosterolperoxide, α-ergocryptine, etc 
(Waksman et al. 1942). In this study, α-ergocryptine and ergosterol were found in every sample 
analysed from the Unilateralis clade. Cyclopiazonic acid (CPA) was found in A. assulatus (25℃). 
Fatty acids 
Species of the section Fumigati have been reported to produce series of lipids. Their role in the infection 
process is not very clear, however lipids such as oxylipins, have been shown to be involved in virulence 
(Tsitsigiannis et al. 2005). Linoleic acid was discovered in all samples analysed. It is a carboxylic acid 
which occurs in nature as triglyceride ester. It is often found in Aspergillus species. 
Secondary metabolites 
Helvolic acids (triterpenes) 
Helvolic acid is an antibiotic active against both gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria. Helvolic 
acid has been reported to be toxic, and its toxicity may be due to contamination with gliotoxin (Cole & 
Cox 1981). In the study by Hubka et al. (2017) helvolic acid was isolated from A. multiplicatus grown 
at 37℃. 
Diols 
Austdiol is a fungal compound, known as a gastro-intestinal toxin, found in some Aspergillus species 
(LoPresti et al. 2003). In this study, it was found in all species grown at 15℃ (with the exception of A. 
assulatus), and in all samples of A. hiratsukae.  
Tetronic acids 
Tetronic acid is a substructural element of many natural products such as ascorbic acid, penicillic acid, 
carlosic acid. Its presence in natural products is connected to a variety of biological properties, hence it 
==PAGE:58==
48 
 
makes it a target of interest (Yang et al. 2014). In this study, carlosic acid was found in A. hiratsukae 
species (15℃, 25℃), A. nishimurae (25℃), and in A. tsunodae at all temperatures.  
Fulvic acids 
Fulvic acids along with humic acids are natural components of soil organic matter (Tan 2011). It was 
found in the A. nishimurae sample (25℃). 
Hexaketides 
Ascochitine is derived from a single hexaketide precursor with the introduction of three methyl groups 
from methionine via a quinone-methide structure, similar to the one involved in the citrinin 
biosynthesis. Citrinin is a mycotoxin, often found in food, such as stored grains and plant products 
(Cole & Cox 1981). Both of these extrolites have been found in the A. hiratsukae samples. 
Gliotoxins 
Gliotoxins are mycotoxins produced by several fungi, especially A. fumigatus. Fumigacin has been 
found in mammalian tissue and was later found to be a mixture of gliotoxin and helvolic acid. Gliotoxins 
are claimed to be involved in diseases caused by species in the section Fumigati (Cole & Cox 1981). 
Gliotoxins are best produced on media with low C-N ratio, therefore the media used was not optimum 
for the expression of the metabolite.  
Gypsetin 
Cholesterol regulation is of high priority in drug development, and gypsetin is a potential agent able to 
inhibit acylCoA:cholesterol transferase. It belongs to a complex diketopiperazine compounds, reported 
to have microbial origin (Nuber et al. 1994). In this study, gypsetin was found in the A. marvanovae 
sample. 
Kotanin 
Kotanin has been obtained from Aspergillus species contaminating grains. It has been reported to be 
toxic to mammals (Buchi et al. 1971). Kotanin was found in A. multiplicatus (15℃) and A. nishimurae 
(25℃).

total area of the wells. 
Undiluted Rodalone® (10%) proved the most efficient as the inhibition zones were significantly larger 
than the zones from other agents. For the A. hiratsukae samples (Figure 3-11 B, C, D, E, F, G)

remained the same after 14 days. 10% Rodalone® did not have the same strong effect on the A. 
nishimurae samples. However, the inhibition zones from the different fungicides were very similar in 
size for that organism (Figure 3-11). The antifungal agent had the weakest effect on the A. unilateralis 
sample with a total inhibition area of 31.61mm2.

average total area of inhibition after 7days was 13.3mm2, which after 14 days had reduced to 7.9mm2.

was 40.94mm2, which after 14 days reduced to 6.11mm2 (Table 3-19). In samples like A. multiplicatus 
and A. marvanovae, the inhibition by 1%Rodalone® was not high, however, it remained unchanged 
after 14days.

14 days, the inhibition diameter for sample GvadE12 had increased to 4.8mm. The same sample was

unaffected by the 70% ethanol (Table 3-20). The 90% ethanol had the largest area of inhibition on the

3.3 Enzyme production 
The enzyme production assay was designed in order to observe whether the species from the 
Unilateralis clade produce cellulases which can hydrolyse cellulose and xylose in buildings and 
building materials such as wallpapers, wooden panels, plywood boards, etc. For this assay, AZCL 
medium was prepared. If there is any cellulolytic activity, there would be a defined blue inhibition zone 
around the organism. 
Based on the observations made, none of the organisms from the clade produced cellulases. There were 
no blue inhibition zones present on the media. The organisms, however, expressed interesting 
morphology which can be observed in Appendix IV. 
 
3.4 Establishment on building materials 
As indoor environment safety is a high priority and many fungal organisms are yet to be studied 
extensively, the assay was designed to follow the growth of several species from the Unilateralis clade 
in environments similar to environments in buildings and building materials. For this purpose, two 
studies were designed. In the first one, several species from the clade were inoculated on various media 
prepared from materials commonly found in indoor environments, such as wallpaper, paper, and 
wallpaper glue. In the second study, the A. hiratsukae organisms were inoculated directly on gypsum 
boards and their ability to adapt was followed.  
 
3.4.1 Establishment on limited nutrient media  
Recycled paper is often used for the making of gypsum boards which are commonly found in modern 
Danish homes. Three types of media were prepared Paper Agar (PA), Wallpaper Agar (WPA), and 
Wallpaper Glue Agar (WGA). The samples were three-point inoculated and incubated at 25˚C. Their 
morphologies and abilities to grow were observed. All organisms formed thin layers over the petris, 
making it difficult to make accurate diameter measurements. Hence, such were not made.  
A. assulatus exhibited a more prominent growth than the rest of the organisms on all three media (Fig 
3-16 A, B, C). On WG agar and W agar, the organism expressed a fuzzy white colony and the paper 
media has been almost completely utilized. A. hiratsukae (sample GvadE4) also grew well on all media, 
utilizing the glue and paper media very well. (Fig 3-16 D, E, F). The colonies looked similar in colour 
to the colonies produced on YES and CYA media, white-ish green in colour. A. marvanovae had limited 
growth on the wallpaper and paper media, however it formed colonies on the glue media. (Fig 3-16 G, 
H, I).Opposite to the others, A. multiplicatus did not grow at all on the glue medium (Fig 3-16 J). It 
formed a thin layer on the wallpaper agar, and a more visible one in a white-ish colour on the paper 
medium. The A. nishimurae sample (26500) grew successfully on all three types of media, with a 
preference towards the paper and glue media. The paper medium was almost depleted and the wallpaper 
remained covered with a thin film (Fig 3-16 M, N, O). A. tsunodae formed small colonies that branched 
out along the plate on paper agar, in a similar manner to A. nishimurae. On wallpaper, it grew thin (Fig 
3-16 P, Q, R). A. unilateralis grew in a similar manner to A. hiratsukae with colonies in a greenish-
white colour. On glue and paper, the organism utilized the medium very well, almost depleting the 
paper medium (Fig 3-16 S, T, U). Growth on the WA seemed slower than and not as high as on the 
other media. In most cases, the paper media had been completely utilized. All organisms seemed to be 
thriving well in the conditions provided.

4. Discussion 
A deeper understanding of the Unilateralis clade was achieved based on their requirements and 
characteristics. Growth specifications and requirements of each individual member of the clade were 
observed in a variety of environmental conditions. Their ability to produce secondary metabolites based 
on these environmental changes were observed. Furthermore, the organisms’ resilience to external 
factors and treatments was evaluated as well as their ability to proliferate in “real life” conditions. 
Fungal characterisation & mycelial survival 
The characterization of individual species of the clade, gave a more detailed picture of their growth 
characteristics and preferences. This assay is based on creating a variety of media, which of course 
differ from the organisms’ natural habitat. Nonetheless, the assay allowed for the examination of growth 
patterns, temperature, pH, and water availability preferences for future studies. Previous studies by 
Kwon-Chung & Sugui (2009) have suggested that some species of the clade have relatively low levels 
of fertility, which can be compensated by their proficiency to reproduce asexually in a wider range of 
environmental conditions. Our study confirms these findings, showing that the species in the 
Unilateralis clade can proliferate in a range of temperatures. We have also established a definite 
preference towards higher temperatures, which was exhibited by all analysed samples.  
A greater focus in the study was cast upon the A. hiratsukae organism. It showed a somewhat constant 
growth pattern on all media and temperatures. As a thermophile, its preference towards the higher 
temperatures was prominent. CYA being a go-to medium when analysing unknown fungal samples, 
has proven not optimal for the A. hiratsukae species. Interestingly, their growth on the medium at lower 
temperatures resulted in a considerably slowed down growth and reproduction. The metabolites 
produced were also considerably fewer at the lower temperatures. The species, however, produced 
several metabolites of interest, which were not observed in the other species. For example, sulochrin 
and ascochitine. Ascochitine has been recognised as a metabolite with phytotoxic properties, produced 
by Aspergillus species (Foremska et al. 1990). A. hiratsukae has been reported by Han et al. as the 
causative agent of fungal dermatitis in household hedgehog. Sulochrin is a known vascular endothelial 
growth factor inhibitor with potential for biotechnological uses (Ohashi et al. 1998). It is also known to 
have a role in allergic reactions, such as bronchial asthma and atopic dermatitis (Ohashi et al. 1998).  
Current findings confirm with literature as to A. hiratsukae’s pathogenicity and the fact that it is present 
in indoor environments makes research of that organism even more necessary. 
An organism that stood out during the macro- and micro- studies was A. marvanovae. Its morphology 
was quite different from the rest of the species of the clade. Its responses to environmental changes 
were not that visible. The colonies remained a typical average size of ~10mm diameter. Sporulation 
was abundant. Festuclavine was a metabolite that was only found in the chemical analysis of A. 
marvanovae. The metabolite takes part in the biosynthesis of fumigaclavine C, which is a known 
ergoline alkaloid produced by A. fumigatus. Hence, confirming the species relation. A. marvanovae was 
the only organism analysed that produced gypsetin. Which is known for its potentials in 
pharmaceuticals for its ability to inhibit acylCoA:cholesterol transferase (Cole & Cox 1981). 
A. multiplicatus exhibited similar growth patterns to the rest of the species of the clade. Similarly to 
Lamoth et al. (2016) and Hubka et al. (2017), helvolic acid was discovered after the chemical analysis. 
Interestingly, the acid was only discovered in samples grown at 37˚C.  
A. nishimurae did not differ from its description by Takada et al. (2001). According to their study, the 
organism grows rapidly on CYA medium at 37˚C compared to growth at 25˚C. Similarly, the organism 
grew rapidly at 37˚C on both DG18 and V8 media. However, this was the case only with one of the two 
==PAGE:86==
76 
 
samples analysed. Interestingly, the IBT 26500 sample grew slowly on 37˚C, compared to the other 
temperatures on DG18. The organism did not produce many metabolites at 15˚C, while at 25˚C, it 
produced fulvic acid, commonly found in soils. 
As described by Yaguchi et al. (2010), A. tsunodae is characterised by its rapid growth at 37˚C, similarly 
to the other species in the clade. Interestingly, on V8 medium, the organism grew relatively slow at 
30˚C, compared to the growth at 25˚C and 37˚C. The organism was found producing carlosic acid at all 
temperatures. It is a metabolite derivative of tetronic acids. 
Similarly to the rest of the species of the clade, A. unilateralis grew rapidly at higher temperatures. Like 
A. nishimurae, the organism actually grew slower at 37˚C compared to 30˚C. At 25˚C, it also produced 
fulvic acid derivatives and production of metabolites at 37˚C was limited.  
When grown on medium with limited aw, the organisms’ morphology did not change significantly. 
Their growth compared to growth on MEAox was considerably slower. A. unilatreralis and A. 
multiplicatus grew the least out of all samples and compared to MEAox, their growth was half the 
diameter. A. unilateralis expressed a stressed morphology, with dry colonies. A. marvanovae did not 
seem affected by the water availability levels. As stated by Adan (1994), fungal development could 
have also been slowed down by other processes in the cell metabolism. As the critical aw value for 
growth was reduced, the germination and growth of the organisms may have been slowed down.  
Unlike temperature, the pH of the organisms is not solely dependent on the environment. Passage of 
ions can be controlled by the cells and to some extent the environmental pH could also be altered by 
the organism. Changes in the environmental pH can drastically affect metabolic processes, growth, and 
germination. Vinegar, as a fungicide of natural origin was used to treat the mycelium of the samples 
and observe their response. Study by Rogawansamy et al. (2015) suggests that vinegar may not be an 
effective agent against the diverse fungal genera found in indoor environments. Our study confirms 
their statement. Out of all samples, only four exhibited successful inhibition of growth, two A. 
hiratsukae samples, one A. nishimurae, and A. unilateralis. Vinegar did not seem to have considerable 
effect on germination. 
Fungal survival & spore resilience 
As the species in section Fumigati are not morphologically uniform, other methods for their 
identifications are necessary in order to avoid further misidentifications and classifications. After 
analysing their secondary metabolite profiles and phylogenicity, the next step is to analyse their 
antifungal susceptibility. This is important in clinical or production laboratories, where antifungal 
susceptibility is not routinely performed, often leading to misidentification. Several fungal 
susceptibility tests were designed to assay the resilience of the clade to intrinsic factors in order to 
obtain a better idea of the clade. 
The epidemiology of antifungal resistance in filamentous fungi is characterized by resistance to azoles 
by A. fumigatus and by an intrinsic antifungal resistant pattern of newly emerging species (Pagano et al, 
2006; Alastruey-Izquierdo et al, 2013). The incidence of antifungal resistance in vitro among mould 
isolated from deep tissue samples, blood cultures, and respiratory samples has shown that the most 
common isolates are from the genus Aspergillus. Our assay confirms previous findings. 70% and 90% 
ethanol, which are commonly used to clean indoor surfaces in both clinical and production 
environments, have proven quite ineffective at inhibiting mycelial growth. In only one out of the 6 A. 
hiratsukae samples that were analysed, the inhibition was successful by 70% ethanol. For the rest of 
the species, 70% ethanol was effective in inhibiting growth for A. marvanovae, A. nishimurae, A. 
tsunodae, and A. unilateralis. The inhibition zone remained the same after a longer period of time only 
==PAGE:87==
77 
 
on the A. tsunodae samples. For most other samples, the mycelium had resumed growth over the 
inhibition zones. Interestingly, the one sample of A. hiratsukae that was inhibited at day 7 
measurements, showed an increase of 1.3mm of the inhibition zone after 14days. Similarly, when 
treated with 90% ethanol, two of the A. hiratsukae samples had an increase by 0.3mm of the inhibition 
zones after 14days. Undiluted Rodalone® had the most success at inhibiting the mycelial growth. 
Rodalone® is commonly used in Danish households for antifungal remediation. However, the analysis 
showed that after 14 days, all samples had reduced inhibition zone diameters. A. assulatus remained at 
the same inhibition zone, and A. multiplicatus was most affected by the agent. Diluted Rodalone® was 
used as comparison, as it is often recommended that it be diluted prior to use at home. The 1% 
Rodalone® had less success at inhibiting mycelial growth. Hence, allowing the organisms to proliferate 
after 14days. 
Similarly to the previous assay, spore resilience of the clade was tested. In order to assess their ability 
to survive harsh treatments, different ethanol concentrations were introduced to the spore suspensions. 
The A. hiratsukae organisms responded similarly to the treatment with 70% ethanol. After 2minutes of 
exposure, inhibition of germination was high and exposure to the agent for longer periods- 6min and 
10min, resulted in stimulation of germination. These results are in agreement with the results by Warcup 
et al. (1963). Interestingly, the 90% ethanol induced different responses by the different A. hiratsukae 
samples. GvadE3 and GvadE12 were induced at 2minutes and proliferated after 10. Whereas samples 
GvadE4 and KvadE1 seemed to have equal germination at the various times. Interesting were sample 
GvadE8m which had the least growth at 6minutes, and sample 28630, which had the least growth at 
10minutes. No similar results were found in literature. 
Temperature is considered a very influential factor to fungal growth. In regards to heat treating products 
for human consumption, eliminating microorganisms is of high priority. However, many fungal 
organisms are heat-resistant, and species of the Unilateralis clade also fall into that category. As A. 
hiratsukae is an organism commonly found in juices Udagawa et al. (1991), there is a need to assess 
the ability of all members of the clade to resist subjection to heat. When subjected to 65˚C and 90˚C, 
the A. hiratsukae samples exhibited similar response to what was described in literature by Warcup et 
al. (1963). Shorter periods of exposure to 65˚C, resulted in stimulation of germination, compared to 
exposure at 10 and 15minutes. When exposed to 90˚C, the different samples expressed different 
responses. In most cases, however, growth was not inhibited. Only samples GvadE4 and GvadE12 
showed fewer colonies when treated for 30seconds and 2minutes, respectively. After the second assay 
of heat resistance, the results were similar to the previous assay. Germination was stimulated in samples 
subjected to 65˚C for shorter periods- 2min and 6min. When subjected to 90˚C, A. hiratsukae, A. 
marvanovae, A. tsunodae, and A. unilateralis germinated abundantly. After being subjected to 90˚C for 
30 seconds all of the mentioned samples had the highest numbers of colonies growing. These results 
may be of concern for food and beverage production facilities. In the case of A. hiratsukae, it is quite 
commonly found in aloe juice, which according to Ahlawat & Khatakar (2011) is often not subjected 
to any heat treatment, or is very briefly introduced to heat in order not to inhibit any enzymatic activities 
of aloe. Our study shows that shorter periods of exposure to heat will stimulate germination and fungal 
proliferation. 
Interestingly, after all assays on the spore suspensions, some of the plates were contaminated with 
bacterial growth. Initially, that was brushed off as a malpractice during the inoculation or dilution of 
the samples. Hence, the assays were repeated. However, the bacterial growth was still present which 
led to the conclusion that the original sample may be contaminated. This result, however, led to other 
ideas for future work and perspectives. It seemed that the bacterium was competing with the fungus for 
nutrients and when the fungus was inhibited, the bacterium prevailed. Hence, it would be of interest to 
==PAGE:88==
78 
 
follow the bacterial growth on more fungal media, or its interactions with the fungal organism. Similarly 
to studies with other Aspergillus species and P. aeruginosa.   
Establishment on building materials  
Based on previous studies by Andersen et al. (2011), this assay was designed to assess the association 
of the different mycobiota on different building materials. Literature suggests that species of the 
Unilateralis clade produce cellulases, which makes them detrimental to indoor environments. Hence, 
initially, the organisms were inoculated on AZCL medium in order to check their cellulolytic activities. 
Unfortunately, that assay did not show any such activity. The results, however, do not mean that the 
organisms do no produce any cellulolytic enzymes. Often the organisms produce certain enzymes and 
metabolites when needed for defence or competition, thus in our case, the organism used to inoculate 
the plates may not have found it necessary to produce such enzymes. Nonetheless, species of the 
Unilateralis clade were introduced to a variety of limited nutrition environments comprised of materials 
that are found in modern Danish indoor environments. Some Aspergillus species may need higher water 
activity on gypsum in order to proliferate. Hence, three media were designed to assess three common 
materials that may be affected by fungal growth.  
Woodchip wallpaper is commonly used in Danish household and contain cellulosic compounds, which 
may be a source of nutrition for fungi. Inoculates on medium comprised of woodchip wallpaper seemed 
to establish themselves well. A. assulatus expressed the strongest affinity towards the material, growing 
large and white colonies. Conidiation was also observed. The other species were also able to thrive on 
the medium, however, with less prominent colonies. They formed thin layers over the surface of the 
medium. As wallpaper is commonly put up with wallpaper glue, it was of interest to check the 
organisms’ ability to utilise the glue as a food source. Based on observations, all members of the clade, 
with the exception of A. multiplicatus, were able to proliferate and germinate on the glue medium. 
Recycled paper is often used in construction, as well as into the production of gypsum boards, which 
are quite common in modern construction. Inoculations on paper medium resulted in germination from 
all of the organisms, with A. assulatus showing an affinity towards the medium. It was able to deplete 
the source fairly quickly, compared to the rest of the organisms.  
According to Andersen et al. (2011) and Adan (1994) when installing gypsum plasters during 
construction, the gypsum is hydrated, which causes the temperature of the material to rise, thus inducing 
germination of spores. Often for the production of prefabricated gypsum recycled paper is used. Hence, 
A. hiratsukae species were introduced to prefabricated gypsum boards to assess their ability to 
proliferate. After 14days of incubation at 25˚C, cleistothecia were observed on the surface of the 
gypsum.  
Austdiol was found produced by all species of the clade, raising the potential of these organisms being 
harmful to human health. As it was shown in the study, the species can proliferate in building materials, 
and A. hiratsukae is often found in food and beverage. Which leads us to the conclusion that all species 
of the clade may thrive in building materials and in indoor environments, making them potential threats 
to human health. Hence, further studies of both their extrolites production and their behaviour on 
gypsum and other building materials should be studied.  
In “real life” situations, the organisms can be introduced to the concrete or wallpaper along with dirt, 
dust, or water. The organisms have shown that they can tolerate lower water availability and fluctuations 
in alkalinity, as well as being subjected to environmental stress. Hence, they can remain dormant until 
the materials get wet or heated and then begin germination. Thus reducing the quality of the indoor 
environment.

5. Conclusion 
 
The results presented in this study can aid many aspects of human life. For one, the detailed 
characterisation of the species allows for a faster and more accurate macro- and micro- identification 
of the clade. Based on the metabolites produced, it can be concluded that the species of the Unilateralis 
clade have many potentials in the biotechnological and pharmaceutical industries with their ability to 
produce metabolites of interest. Unfortunately, identification of fungal organisms can be a difficult task, 
quite often leading to misidentification due to lack of assessments or knowledge. Hence, the several 
resilience assays were designed to assist in clinical or production laboratories, where antifungal 
susceptibility is not routinely performed. The building profession in general can aid from our results in 
the selection of materials for construction, especially buildings designed for food and beverage 
production facilities. Manufacturers of commonly used building materials can use our results for the 
design or product specification requirements of their products, in order to achieve fewer numbers of 
surviving spores during manufacturing. Health authorities could benefit from these studies in order to 
facilitate generation of better Good Manufacturing Practice requirements for food and beverage 
production facilities and product specifications. Lastly, the results of this study, can be used by the 
scientific community to focus their research on the potential mycotoxins that the organisms can 
produce; to further follow the interactions between the A. hiratsukae organism and the bacterium found 
growing alongside in the medium; or to aid in the development of new methods of detection and 
identification of fungal growth on building materials.

Heat treatment second assay 
When the 1mL sample was subjected to 65℃, it took 2minutes to reach 50℃, and 5minutes to reach 
60℃.For the 90℃ treatment of the sample, it took 60seconds to reach 51℃ and 4minutes to reach 80℃. 
Based on these observations, it was decided that the 65℃ samples should be treated for 4minutes at 
65℃ prior to turning the timer on for the desired treatment time. Hence, the 2minute sample was in the 
heating block for 6minutes in total, the 5minute sample was heated for 10minutes in total, etc. For the 
90℃ treatment, it was decided that the samples should receive additional 2minutes to their total 
treatment time. This was due to the fact that it took the sample 2minutes to reach 65℃. See Table for 
all temperature measurements.  
When cooling down the 65℃ samples, it took them 20minutes to reach 24℃ (room temperature), and 
3minutes to reach 24℃ when flash cooled. For the 90℃ samples, it took 3.5minutes to reach 24℃ 
when flash cooled on ice. Hence, all samples were flash cooled after heat treatment in order to simulate 
the pasteurisation process which aims to reduce the temperature of the product below 20℃ as quickly 
as possible.
