Abstract
The construction industry is undergoing a transformative shift powered by tech-
nology, and Digital Twins stand out as a possible solution to reshape conventional
practices. This Master’s Thesis delves into Digital Twins’ multifaceted landscape
in construction, investigating applications, benefits, challenges, business model in-
novation, and alignment with client needs. Grounded in a mixed-method approach,
qualitative interviews and quantitative surveys illuminate insights.
The thesis’s objective is to explore Digital Twins’ potential impact on construction,
spanning applications, benefits, challenges, and business model innovation. It also
aims to discern stakeholders’ needs and offer actionable recommendations for bridg-
ing the gap between potential and implementation. Qualitative interviews provide
firsthand insights, while quantitative surveys offer a broader perspective.
The study uncovers diverse Digital Twins applications, from enhancing decision-
making to predictive maintenance and collaboration.
Benefits encompass opera-
tional eﬀiciency, cost savings, prolonged asset lifecycles, and improved communi-
cation. Acknowledging challenges like industry fragmentation and implementation
costs, the thesis unveils avenues for overcoming them.
Stakeholder interviews reveal how Digital Twins catalyze change, reducing depen-
dency on inspections, fostering data-driven insights, and promoting agility. Business
model innovation hinges on transitioning from dependency to autonomy, ignorance
to insight, and inaccuracy to fidelity.
Client needs analysis underscores demand for enhanced decision tools, real-time
insights, and data-driven operations. Recommendations align innovation with client
needs, bridging technological advancements and practical implementation.
This Master’s Thesis delivers a holistic exploration of Digital Twins’ transforma-
tive potential in construction. Illuminating applications, benefits, challenges, and
business model innovation, it charts a roadmap for a digitally transformed future.
Insights and vision merge as buildings evolve into intelligent entities shaped by data,
insights, and innovation.

Introduction
The construction industry is an intricate, expansive ecosystem with numerous facets,
complexities, and unique challenges.
Over the years, digital transformation has
significantly impacted the industry, leading to the evolution of Construction 4.0
and the advent of Industry 4.0.
Technological advancements have given rise to
innovative solutions capable of disrupting traditional practices, enhancing eﬀiciency,
and improving productivity. Among these groundbreaking technologies, the concept
of Digital Twins (DT) has recently come to the forefront, promising a revolution in
the way we plan, design, build, and manage our built environments.
The idea of using Digital Twins - virtual representations of physical assets that
can simulate their behaviour in real-time - is not new. However, its application in
the construction industry is still relatively recent, and its potential impact remains
largely unexplored. Therefore, understanding the current state of the industry and
how DT technology could drive changes becomes paramount. These digital transfor-
mations present opportunities for innovative business models and paradigms, pro-
viding the potential to fundamentally revolutionize the sector.
This Master’s Thesis aims to comprehensively investigate the concept of Digital
Twins within the construction industry, focusing on their definition, applications,
benefits, challenges, and best practices. We aim to explore how National Digital
Twins and Connected Digital Twins could influence the construction industry, driv-
ing innovation and disrupting traditional practices.
The thesis further seeks to understand how Digital Twins can influence business
models within the construction industry, potentially leading to increased eﬀiciency
and productivity. The objective is to dissect the potential of Digital Twins, un-
derstand the change they could bring about, and ascertain their transformative
potential within the sector.
To ensure a comprehensive and grounded investigation, this thesis also includes a

thorough investigation of clients’ needs within the construction industry. This inves-
tigation employs a mixed-method approach, including qualitative analysis through

In pursuit of these goals, this Master’s Thesis provides a holistic exploration of
Digital Twins’ potential impact on the construction industry, from transforming
business models to meeting specific client needs. The ultimate goal is to contribute
meaningful insights and concrete recommendations to the industry, supporting its
journey towards a more eﬀicient, productive, and technologically advanced future.

2
Literature Study
The literature review of this thesis examines the innovative concept of Digital Twins
and their potential within the construction industry. The content includes an ex-
ploration of the definition, possible applications, potential benefits, challenges that
must be addressed, and best practices for implementation.
The review begins with an exploration of the creation and evolution of DTs, il-
lustrating how this technology has transitioned from the manufacturing domain to
construction and infrastructure. As there is no universally accepted definition of
DTs, the first section offers a detailed understanding of what constitutes a DT, its
underlying principles, and unique characteristics.
Next, the applications of DTs within the construction industry are investigated,
with an emphasis on the significant value they present. The literature reveals the
transformative power of National Digital Twins/Connected Digital Twins within
the industry. The objective here is to explore how these technologies can promote
innovative practices, overhaul traditional procedures, and encourage a culture of
eﬀiciency and productivity.
Furthermore, the literature review discusses the influence of DTs on business models
within the construction industry. Special attention is paid to the 4Vs Framework
and the Digital Twin Construction (DTC). The potential for these approaches to
transform the construction industry is assessed, and considerations are given to
challenges in implementing them.

cant obstacles hindering its development, including low innovation levels, workforce
issues, insuﬀicient process standardization, limited collaboration, low productivity,

2.2.1
Introduction to Digital Twins
Digital Twins (DT) has emerged as a powerful tool in recent years, with applications
in a wide range of industries, including in the built environment. The concept was
first introduced by Michael Grieves back in the early 2000s (Grives, 2016), but then
under a different name. There is currently no common definition of DTs, leading
to uncertainty and varied interpretations across different industries (Opoku et al.,
2021). However, a DT is often defined as a virtual replica of a physical object,
system or process that is continually updated with real-time data from sensors and

is similar to the definition put forth by Centre for Digital Built Britain (CDBB),
stating that a digital twin is ”a realistic digital representation of assets, processes,
or systems in the built or natural environment” (Bolton et al., 2018).

Digital Twin Technology (DTT) can be seen as a system consisting of three main
components: The physical part, the virtual models and the data that connects them
(Boje et al., 2020), as shown in Figure 2.2. The connection between the virtual and
physical duality of the system is established by data in various forms. The data from
the physical components to the virtual models is raw and requires processing (Boje
et al., 2020). In contrast, the data in the opposite direction undergoes several trans-
formations and can be stored as processed information and knowledge across digital
models, with higher degrees of meaning. Ultimately, this processed data is reflected
back to the physical components through actuators. The physical components of
a DT collect real-world data, which is sent for processing. On the other hand, the
virtual part applies its embedded engineering models and AI to discover information
that can be used for managing the day-to-day usage of the physical components.
Therefore, the virtual models serve as a tool for simulation and prediction while the
physical components represent the real-world system. The combination of these two

DT. This three-part structure of a DT is crucial for its effective management and

2.2.2
The Digital Twin Maturity Spectrum
The Digital Twin Maturity Spectrum (DTMS) (Table 2.3) was developed to serve
as a comprehensive, industry-agnostic guide to the evolutionary process of a digital
twin in the construction industry (Evans et al., 2020). It was designed with focus on
the purpose and benefits of each stage rather than the appeal of a fully implemented
digital twin. As the digital twin matures, it navigates through different elements,
each representing an increase in complexity, connectivity, and subsequently, value.
The progression through these elements is not necessarily linear or sequential, un-
derlining the diverse pathways to digital twin maturity (Evans et al., 2020). In
essence, the maturity spectrum provides a structured understanding of the Digital
Twin´s development and the value creation at each stage.

Element 0 is the data gathering stage for digital twin creation, utilizing reality
capture techniques like point cloud scanning. This stage develops an accurate as-
built data set, providing insight into an asset’s geometry, condition, and location.
It increases asset certainty, spatial context and understanding.
Element 1 involves creating surface and shape models of the asset without meta-
data. 2D or 3D models are derived from reality capture techniques, providing valu-
able insights for design and asset optimization, as well as coordination between
teams.
Post-construction, these models are updated to accurately represent the
asset.
Element 2 involves attaching metadata to the 2D or 3D models, connecting them
with data like design information and inspection reports.
This creates a single,
comprehensive reference point, reducing errors, cost and enabling better decision-
making and collaboration. Element 2 helps answer important questions about the
asset, such as whether the project is on schedule and budget and where the highest
risk items are located.
Element 3 is about incorporating real-time operational data from the physical asset
through IoT technology. This allows for predictive behavior analysis and near-real-
time decision-making. This element boosts asset performance and eﬀiciency.
Element 4 allows for the state and condition of the physical asset to be changed
through the twin, with feedback and updates to the twin. This level of integration
requires additional sensors and mechanical augmentation of the asset. The inte-
gration can also extend to other digital assets, such as other twins or engineering
systems and applications. This provides full integration between human-to-machine
and machine-to-machine interactions. Overall, Element 4 enables more advanced
control and optimization of the physical asset through the digital twin.
Element 5 involves developing a fully autonomous digital twin capable of self-
correcting anomalies. Achieving this ambitious level of maturity would require sig-
nificant advancements in AI and IoT. While only partially feasible today, the future

The DTMS and the 3-Tier Paradigm both offer unique pathways to digital twin
maturity, yet they diverge in their level of detail and focus. The Maturity Spectrum
outlines a clear-cut, five-step procedure starting from data gathering and culminat-
ing in autonomous operations and maintenance (Evans et al., 2020). In contrast, the
3-Tier Paradigm presents a more incremental and gradual approach, emphasizing
the evolution of intelligence and semantic understanding in the digital twin, and
concluding with a socio-technical platform that is responsive to user needs (Boje
et al., 2020). Each pathway presents distinct strengths and considerations, making
them suitable for different applications and contexts according to specific needs and
requirements.

2.2.3
Digital Twins in the Built Environment
In this section, the potential of DTs in the built environment will be investigated.
The application of this technology in the built environment enables a greater un-
derstanding, monitoring, and management of construction projects, consequently
paving the way for optimized outcomes. The implications of digital twins on the
architecture, engineering, and construction (AEC) industry will be unpacked by un-
derstanding the challenges that lie ahead, the manifold benefits they could provide,
and the best practices that lead to successful implementation.
The power of DTT lies in its ability to create a virtual model of a building or infras-
tructure project even before the actual construction commences. This process opens
avenues to identify and rectify potential issues, saving considerable time and re-
sources, and ensuring an improved overall quality of the project (Bolton et al., 2018;
Evans et al., 2020; Khajavi et al., 2019; Halmetoja, 2022). Notably, the utility of
digital twins extends beyond project completion; monitoring the performance of the
infrastructure via digital twins allows for continuous identification of improvement
areas and adjustments for enhanced eﬀiciency and performance (Opoku et al., 2021;
Bolton et al., 2018; Evans et al., 2020; Halmetoja, 2022).
While the DTT is garnering popularity in various industries, the adoption rate in
the construction sector is still not as high as it could be. This slower adoption can
be attributed to several challenges and barriers surrounding its implementation.

National Digital Twin - System of System
The notion of a digital twin within construction projects is more than just an iso-
lated entity; it’s part of a larger ecosystem, which is akin to a federation of DTs
(Bolton et al., 2018). This interconnected network promotes a secure and valuable
exchange of information between various entities. For example, DTs of construc-
tion equipment, like tower cranes, can interact with the DT representing the overall
project, exchanging valuable data (Bolton et al., 2018).
The Centre for Digital Built Britain (CDBB) underscores the necessity of industry-
wide synchronization for fully realizing the potential of digital transformation, as
expressed in their Gemini Principles (Figure 2.4) (Bolton et al., 2018). Consequently,
the concept of the National Digital Twin (NDT), crafted by CDBB, expands on
these principles to ensure that DTs from different domains harmonize and can be
integrated into the larger NDT ecosystem.
Figure 2.4: The Gemini Principles (Bolton et al., 2018)

higher echelons, including digital representations of local transportation networks
and concrete batching plants. However, not every DT needs to connect to all oth-
ers. Selective connections are established where they offer value, improving project

Innovation in Construction Industry
The digital transformation remains a challenging goal for many engineering and
manufacturing companies as they struggle to emulate the success of platform-based
businesses like Facebook. Despite the availability of new technologies, achieving a
comprehensive digital revolution has proven to be more challenging than anticipated.
Researchers believe that digital twins could be the solution and have investigated
which opportunities these digital twins could present (Pärn et al., 2021).
DTT has already made a significant impact on the construction industry. Software

1. From Dependency to Autonomy
Autonomy is becoming increasingly important in various engineering fields like au-
tomotive, aviation, and manufacturing.
Digital twins, a technology that creates
virtual replicas of physical objects or systems, have a unique capability to under-
stand, learn, and react on their own. This ability of the DT is considered extremely
valuable and is seen as a major achievement in their development.
2. From Ignorance to Insight
The DTs can provide continuous insights into the performance of the physical object
it is mirroring, and by that make real-time improvements to services possible. It is

3. From Inaccuracy to Fidelity

Additionally, Pärn identifies three key forces that contribute to driving business

1. From Products to Services

2.3.1
The 4Vs Framework
The ”Four Values” (4Vs) framework for DTs offers a more streamlined approach
compared to the Business Model Canvas. It originated from a research study led by
Dr. Erika Pärn from the Cambridge Service Alliance (Pärn et al., 2020), with the
aim of assisting executives in gaining a clearer understanding of the business model
possibilities associated with DTs and by this discovering new revenue streams. The
framework is a vital tool for B2B service providers as it aids in comprehending the
optimal configuration of their business model and identifying significant obstacles
that may hinder their success.
The 4Vs framework encompasses several essential aspects.
This includes value
proposition, value architecture, value network and value finance.
Value Proposition
The value proposition outlines how an organization generates value not only for itself
but also for its customers and other stakeholders, including supply chain partners.
It encompasses the products and services provided, the benefits and value they
deliver to customers and businesses, and the ownership structure that underlies the
offerings. In essence, it articulates the unique value that the organization brings to
the table and how it satisfies the needs and desires of its target audience.
Value Architecture
The value architecture focuses on the foundational elements of the business model.
It encompasses five crucial components that revolve around establishing and main-
taining the infrastructure necessary for generating sustainable revenues. These com-
ponents involve integrating technological, operational, and organizational elements.
An Investigation of Digital Twins in the Construction Industry
24
==PAGE:32==
Essentially, the value architecture ensures the robust structure required to support
the successful implementation and long-term viability of the business.
1. Value control focuses on how an organization manages and controls value
within the ecosystem. It determines whether the organization operates ex-
clusively within its own digital twin services ecosystem or collaborates and
intersects with other ecosystems.
2. Value delivery pertains to how the DTs are distributed and provided to
customers. It considers whether the delivery is centralized, decentralized, or a
combination of both (hybrid). Additionally, it examines any potential barriers
that may hinder the delivery of DTs to customers.
3. Interactions refer to the ways in which customers engage and interact with
the digital twin. This can involve various methods such as desktop or mobile
apps, virtual reality, augmented reality, and other forms of interaction that
enable users to engage with the digital twin.
4. Data collection is fundamental to the value proposition of DTs. It involves
the gathering of data, which can come from sensors, as well as data that is
acquired or purchased from external sources.
This combination of sensor-
based and supplied/purchased data contributes to the overall value of the
digital twin.
5. Boundary resources are the resources provided to enhance the network
effects and scalability of digital twin services. These resources typically in-
clude APIs (Application Programming Interfaces), hackathons, software de-
velopment toolkits, and forums. They enable collaboration, integration, and
expansion of the digital twin services to achieve greater value and impact.
Value Network
The value network represents the recognition and comprehension of the intercon-
nected relationships and collaborations among various parties, organizations, and
stakeholders. In the realm of DT-services, this understanding is crucial since the de-
livery mechanism relies on the involvement of multiple organizations, technological
infrastructure, and stakeholders. It acknowledges the intricate web of interactions

and interdependencies necessary for the successful implementation and operation of
digital twin services.
Value Finance
This aspect encompasses how organizations approach the costing, pricing methods,
and revenue structure associated with DTs. The revenue model for DTs commonly
revolves around outcomes-based revenue streams and data-driven revenue models.
Pricing models for DTs can include various approaches such as freemium and pre-
mium models, subscription models, value-based pricing, and outcome-based pricing
models.

Introduction to DTC
Digital Twin Construction (DTC) is a transformative approach, introduced by Sacks
et al., 2020, that integrates techonological advancements and innovative production
management methods. DTC begins by acknowledging that the real-time data flow-
ing from the construction project allows for the creation of a closed-loop control
model in construction, something that hasn’t been achievable until now (Sacks et
al., 2020). It leverages the principles of Lean Construction, BIM and Digital Twin
Technologies. The motivation for DTC arises from the need for a comprehensive
conceptual framework that harnesses these disparate innovations, enabling effective
data utilization and defining an integrative method for managing and executing
construction processes (Sacks et al., 2020).
The DTC utilizes the real-time information generated from construction projects
in order to form a closed-loop model of construction control, and is facilitated by
Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) cycle shown in Table 2.12. The distinctiveness of DTC
is notable in the ”Check” phase when compared to traditional construction man-
agement approaches. In this phase, the data collected is automatically interpreted
to generate comprehensive and accurate information in a significantly short time
frame. This, in combination with the project BIM models, can enable evaluation of
alternate product designs and production plans (Sacks et al., 2020).
Furthermore, DTC promotes a shift in construction management from being reac-
tive to proactive by offering enhanced situational awareness. It enables managers
and workers at all levels to anticipate future actions’ potential consequences through
digital simulations and analysis tools. Ultimately, DTC aims to improve productiv-
ity and eﬀiciency, reduce waste, and support continuous learning, resulting in more
reliable design information and production planning (Sacks et al., 2020).

Workflow and Lifecycle of DTC
The DTCs lifecyle operates through three interlinked cycles as shown in Figure 2.6.
The workflow (Figure 2.7) of this concept offers a comprehensive approach to con-
struction, from inception to post-completion analysis (Sacks et al., 2020).
In the initial phase, a project starts with the design of a product based on the
owner’s specifications.
The designers use a Project Intent Information (PII) to
plan the construction and simulate the design’s performance. This iterative process
results in both ”as-designed” and ”as-planned” information (Sacks et al., 2020).
As the project transitions into the construction phase, real-time monitoring tech-
An Investigation of Digital Twins in the Construction Industry
32
==PAGE:40==
nologies begin accumulating data, marking the birth of the physical twin, also known
as the Child Physical Twin (Sacks et al., 2020). The information gathered, known
as Project Status Information (PSI), provides insights into the ”as-built” and ”as-
performed” states of the project (Buchmann & Koldehofe, 2009). This information
is compared with the intended outcome (PII) in an evaluation process, leading to
the formation of the Project Status Knowledge (PSK) (Sacks et al., 2020).
Real-time feedback plays a crucial role in ensuring safety and quality control during
the construction process. The PSI delivers real-time information to site managers
and workers, alerting them of deviations from the plan or safety concerns (Cheng &
Teizer, 2013). This direct feedback fosters swift correction of anomalies, preserving
the quality and safety of the construction project (Sacks et al., 2020)
Post-construction, a broader cycle exists wherein each completed project is archived
and subsequently used for organizational or industry-level learning. This ”long-term
feedback for design and planning” is crucial for improving future projects (Sacks
et al., 2018). The data accumulated throughout the project serves as a resource
for future learning, such as training machine learning applications and conducting
empirical research (Sacks et al., 2020).
In essence, the DTC workflow presents a holistic system that goes beyond construc-
tion, integrating the process with real-time adjustments and long-term learning. It
facilitates a comprehensive understanding of the project, thereby refining construc-
tion practices.
Platform Business Model
Platform business models have proven effective in various industries due to their
ability to offer a wide group of products or services while also taking advantage of
economies of scale. A similar structure can be observed in the construction industry
where leading contractors, particularly the ones in the UK, act as a platform organi-
zation (Sacks et al., 2020). These organizations maintain a lean core of management
and administrative personnel while relaying on subcontracted supply chain to pro-
vide construction personnel, equipment and materials. Platform business model in
the construction industry has been proposed (Mosca et al., 2020), including adop-
tion of platform organizations in the context of management services delivered på
An Investigation of Digital Twins in the Construction Industry
33
==PAGE:41==
”software-as-a-service” solution (Laine et al., 2017)), and product platform models
in building (Jansson et al., 2014).
The DTC system integrates monitoring hardware, cloud-based data storage, and
advanced information processing. While its implementation and operation require
substantial resources, beyond the reach of most general contractors, it offers an
invariant solution, adaptable to a variety of construction project types.
Conse-
quently, it holds the potential to serve multiple projects simultaneously, offering
an integrated service of hardware and software (Sacks et al., 2020).
The DTC,
therefore, lends itself well to a platform business model. Here, a comprehensive
DTC platform company would supply all the requisite management, planning, and
production control infrastructure for general contractors (Sacks et al., 2020).
As the DTC platform evolves, it could centralize procurement from the supply chain
by handling the everyday coordination of subcontractors and supplier deliveries
across a range of projects.
In line with the Portfolio-Project-Operations model
posited by (Sacks et al., 2018), a DTC platform could achieve significantly improved
production flow by coordinating operations across a substantial portfolio, a feat that
is challenging for general contractors handling individual projects.
The growing impact of digital information has been steadily revolutionizing the de-

the next wave of this digital transformation, potentially revolutionizing commercial
practices in construction and paving the way for a robust platform economy within
the industry. Such DTC platforms can potentially outpace the capabilities of tradi-
tional general contractors by offering superior design and construction management
services, leveraging the industry’s most valuable resources: data and information.
This digital shift could render the need for on-site production management personnel
obsolete, challenging the relevance of traditional gatekeeper organizations, such as
classic general contractors. In effect, the DTC model presents an opportunity to sig-
nificantly restructure the construction industry, offering a scalable, technologically
advanced platform that can streamline operations and greatly enhance eﬀiciencies

Detailed Representation
The Community Architecture Framework (CAF) as proposed by McGinley and
Nakata, 2015, signifies a transformation of the conventional Zachman Framework,
a well-respected standard in enterprise architecture for systematically planning, im-
plementing, and managing intricate systems. While the Zachman Framework has
been highly praised for its role in correlating business requirements with the capa-
An Investigation of Digital Twins in the Construction Industry
38
==PAGE:46==
bilities of information systems, it doesn’t inherently account for the distinct features
and necessities intrinsic to community-based systems.
Identifying this shortcoming, McGinley and Nakata, 2015 put forward the CAF (Ta-
ble 3.2), an adaptation of the Zachman Framework, customised to resonate with the
unique aspects of community-focused contexts. This adaptation primarily involved
substituting the term ”business”, used in the Zachman Framework, with ”commu-
nity”, creating more community-specific terms like ”community process model” and
”community logistics model”.
In effect, the CAF harnesses the architectural prowess of the Zachman Framework,
supplementing it with additional context-specific layers designed for community set-
tings. This ensures a comprehensive understanding and representation of the diverse
perspectives, interests, and needs of a community’s stakeholders. Furthermore, the
CAF concept includes the idea of a ”community vision”, aiming to encapsulate the
collective ambitions and objectives of a community, thus fostering a sense of common
purpose and unity (McGinley & Nakata, 2015).
Consequently, the CAF serves as an invaluable tool for understanding, handling, and
manoeuvring through the complex dynamics of community-based systems. It ex-
tends the solid principles of the Zachman Framework and integrates changes tailored
to community environments, providing a more nuanced and effective management
strategy.

The Community Architecture Development Methodology (CADM), proposed by

on the Community Architecture Framework (CAF). The methodology mirrors the
principle of design science research methodology (DSMR), applied to information
systems (Peffers et al., 2007). It leverages the practical application of ”the use of
artefact” during design and development to tackle one or more specific problems
(McGinley & Nakata, 2015).

Discussion
In the landscape of the AEC-industry, the integration of Digital Twins stands as
a beacon of transformative potential. As organizations grapple with the challenges
and opportunities presented by this technological paradigm, it becomes impera-
tive to contextualize their experiences within the broader academic and industry
discourse. This chapter delves into a discussion, comparing the insights from the
stakeholders experiences with Digital Twins to the foundational knowledge obtained
in the literature review (chapter 2).
By aligning the stakeholders real-world experiences with established industry knowl-
edge and best practices, this chapter aims to bridge the theoretical and practical
aspects of Digital Twin adoption. This comparative approach provides a comprehen-
sive understanding of the challenges, aspirations, and strategies that organizations
face, and how these align with broader industry trends and knowledge.
Through this analysis, the chapter offers insights into the practical implications of
Digital Twin adoption, the challenges encountered by organizations like stakeholder
1, and the overarching trends shaping the future of the AEC-industry.

Stakeholder 1’s Alignment with the
DTC-Framework
The DTC-framework, introduced by Sacks et al., 2020 in subsection 2.4.2, repre-
sents a transformative approach that combines technological advancements with
innovative production management methods, offering a pivotal lens for assessing
Stakeholder 1’s aspirations and challenges. This framework not only revolutionizes
the construction sector but also resonates with the ”pathways for change” proposed
by Pärn, 2020 in section 2.3. These pathways, when compared with the DTC prin-
ciples, provide a comprehensive roadmap for organizations like Stakeholder 1 in
the evolving AEC-industry. Such a synergy emphasizes a future in construction
that’s integrated, data-driven, and innovative, highlighting the significance of both
frameworks in shaping Stakeholder 1’s strategic direction.
Dependency to Autonomy
Stakeholder 1’s current reliance on traditional data collection methods, such as phys-
ical inspections, finds resonance with the initial stages of the DTC framework. The
DTC’s emphasis on harnessing real-time data, termed Project Status Information
(PSI), to create a closed-loop control model in construction is particularly perti-
nent to Stakeholder 1’s challenges.
By integrating Digital Twins, Stakeholder 1
can transition from a model heavily dependent on manual methods to one that is
autonomous, leveraging real-time PSI for proactive decision-making. This is evi-
dent in the DTC’s PDCA-cycle (Table 2.12), where the ”Check” phase, powered by
real-time data, becomes pivotal.
Plan (Model)
In this phase, the DTC emphasizes digital modeling of a facility using BIM technolo-
gies and processes. For Stakeholder 1, this means a shift from manual, drawing-based
planning to a digital, data-driven approach. This not only enhances accuracy but
also allows for simulations, predictions, and optimizations even before the actual
construction or maintenance process begins.

==PAGE:72==
Do (Build)
The actual execution phase, where the construction or maintenance takes place.
With the DTC framework, this phase is continuously informed by real-time data,
ensuring that any deviations from the plan are immediately flagged. For Stakeholder
1, this could mean fewer errors, reduced rework, and more eﬀicient use of resources.
Check (Monitor and Interpret)
This is where the DTC truly shines and offers the most significant value for Stake-
holder 1. Instead of periodic, manual checks, the DTC framework promotes contin-
uous digital monitoring. The data collected is automatically interpreted, providing
comprehensive insights in a significantly reduced timeframe. For Stakeholder 1, this
could translate to rapid evaluations in emergency situations, faster response times
to anomalies, and a more proactive approach to maintenance and repairs.
Act (Evaluate and Improve)
The insights derived from the ’Check’ phase are used to evaluate and improve the
design, construction, and operation. For Stakeholder 1, this means a continuous
cycle of improvement, where lessons learned from one project can be immediately
applied to the next, ensuring that the same mistakes are not repeated.
Ignorance to Insight
The DTC framework underscores the importance of transitioning from a state of
ignorance to one of insight.
Stakeholder 1’s challenges, especially those related
to data standardization and interoperability, can be addressed through this lens.
The DTC framework emphasizes the generation of Project Intent Information (PII)
during the planning phase, which represents the ”as-designed” and ”as-planned”
states of a project.
As the project progresses, real-time monitoring technologies
accumulate PSI, providing insights into the ”as-built” and ”as-performed” states.
This PSI is then compared with the PII in an evaluation process, leading to the
formation of Project Status Knowledge (PSK). Stakeholder 1’s aspirations to harness
real-time data align with this principle, suggesting that by integrating the DTC’s
approach, they can achieve granular insights, optimize solutions, and anticipate
challenges.

By integrating a high-fidelity Digital Twin that aligns with the DTC’s principles,

to more informed decision-making.

up to third-party developers and service providers, allowing them to offer specialized

Conclusion
The construction industry, a dynamic and fragmented ecosystem, has undergone
substantial shifts due to the infusion of digital technologies. With Industry 4.0 and
Construction 4.0 driving innovation, eﬀiciency, and technological advancements, the
emergence of Digital Twins as a transformative concept holds promise to reshape
how we conceive, construct, and manage built environments. This thesis has gone
through a comprehensive exploration, aligning with the research goals that were
introduced in the introduction.
Applications, Benefits, and Challenges
The exploration into the applications, benefits, and challenges of Digital Twins in
the construction industry unveiled a landscape teeming with potential and complex-
ity. The applications of Digital Twins extend far beyond their initial conceptualiza-
tion, encompassing areas such as improved decision-making, enhanced visualization,
predictive maintenance, and streamlined collaboration among stakeholders. Stake-
holder interviews and surveys highlighted the real-world impact of Digital Twins,
demonstrating their role in optimizing asset management, accelerating project time-
lines, and facilitating eﬀicient resource allocation.
The benefits derived from Digital Twins are as diverse as they are transformative.
Enhanced operational eﬀiciency emerged as a common thread, with stakeholders
reaping the rewards of reduced physical inspections, data-driven decision-making,
and agile response to emerging challenges. Improved asset management, coupled
with predictive maintenance, translated into cost savings and prolonged asset life-
cycles. The visualization prowess of Digital Twins allowed stakeholders to communi-
cate complex concepts with ease, fostering collaboration and shared understanding
across multidisciplinary teams.
Yet, the path towards reaping these benefits was not devoid of challenges. The frag-
mentation of the construction industry, with its multitude of stakeholders and data
silos, posed hurdles to seamless data integration. Cultural resistance to change and

that required attention.
Business Model Innovation
Delving into the realm of business model innovation, the thesis illuminated the
potential of Digital Twins to reshape traditional paradigms and catalyze change.
Through the distinct lenses of stakeholders representing railway and water infras-
tructure, the thesis shed light on how Digital Twins can drive change from depen-
dency to autonomy, from ignorance to insight, and from inaccuracy to fidelity.
Stakeholder perspectives unveiled a spectrum of possibilities. For Stakeholder 1,
real-time asset monitoring and proactive maintenance emerged as game-changers,
ushering in a new era of operational eﬀiciency. Stakeholder 2, leveraged real-time
monitoring and data-driven agility to optimize decision-making and operation. The
fusion of Digital Twins with AI-powered insights opened pathways for autonomy,
empowering stakeholders to transcend traditional limitations and embrace data-
backed operations.
Client Needs and Recommendations
The thesis embarked on an exhaustive exploration of clients needs within the con-
struction industry, shedding light on the intricacies of diverse requirements. Em-
ploying a mixed-method approach, blending qualitative interviews with quantitative
surveys, the research navigated the nuanced landscape of expectations and aspira-
tions.
The findings resonated profoundly, uncovering the multifaceted essence of clients re-
quirements. Ranging from the call for improved decision-making aids to the desire
for up-to-the-minute insights and actionable data, the stakeholders in the construc-
tion industry showcased a strong appetite for innovation that surpasses traditional
limits.
Consequently, the suggestions arising from this study provide a guiding
beacon, proposing approaches to customize Digital Twins to address these precise

INTERVIEW RESULTS BANEDANMARK
Unraveling Customer Needs for Successful Digital Twin Implementation
Introduction
This research project is being conducted on behalf of Niras, which is aiming to integrate
Digital Twin technology in their service offerings. The main objective is to understand the
needs and expectations of the clients in the AECO industry regarding the adoption of Dig-
ital Twin technology. The insights gained from this interview will help shape the approach
to implementing this technology and providing relevant services to the customers. The
following definition of a digital twin is used as a basis for the following questions below:
A Digital Twin is a digital replica of a physical asset, process, or system. In the
construction industry, it could represent a building or infrastructure, serving as a
dynamic tool for visualization, monitoring, and performance optimization.
Interview Questions
1. Introduction
• Could you briefly introduce your company and describe the field of work your
company operates in?
Banedanmark
• How familiar are you with the concept of digital twins?
A) Good knowledge and working with a DT. Don’t know exactly what is in
the field (what structures are there and what condition they are in). DT
effort: How can one quickly find out what is in the field. That is, existing
conditions and how these are registered, visualized and maintained. If er-
rors are registered at e.g. structures, a project is created which ensures the
error/damage is rectified (also minor renovations).
B) Raise BDK’s digital deliveries to the use of data in full life cycle (Example:
drainage - ditches must be cleaned every other year as they grow, so these
are in accordance with the original project).

B) Track location - a need to maintain the geographic location (track loca-
tion changes over time). Check with Troels - Curve and longitudinal profile
register.
• Are there processes within your asset management that you think could be made
