Abstract
The evolution towards inverter­based renewable power generation in the Nordic Power System (NPS)
contributes to power systems having lower inertia, thus weakening the frequency response. As inertia
decreases other measures can be taken to secure frequency stability. Electrolysers have proven their
ability to handle small imbalances acting as primary frequency reserves by rapidly changing their active
power consumption. Combining the frequency stability advantages with hydrogen production imposes
a new case study, where the facilitators of electrolyser projects might increase their profit by mixing
the two revenue streams. This thesis investigates the above assertion by dividing the statement into four
parts: (i) Investigating state­of­the­art power system frequency stability and electrolyser technologies, (ii)
dynamic modelling of two electrolyser models, (iii) a case study that tests the capabilities of the modelled
dynamic models, and (iv) and economic evaluation of mixing hydrogen production with participation in
the reserve market. In the end limitations and possible extensions are discussed along with an evaluation
of the overall results of the thesis.
A review of power system stability describes the impact of Converter Interfaced Generation (CIG) in the
system and the different ancillary services in the NPS to cope with instabilities. CIG mainly influences
the frequency response of the system as inertia is reduced, incentivising the need for other ways of sta­
bilising the frequency. A state­of­the­art review of electrolyser technologies revealed that the Alkaline
Electrolyser (AEL) and Polymer Exchange Membrane Electrolyser (PEMEL) are the most promising
technologies for providing Frequency Containment Reserve Normal operation (FCR­N), allowing them
to aid in frequency stabilisation in a system highly penetrated by CIG. Furthermore, this part creates a
foundation for the main parameters needed to provide FCR­N, such as voltage/current behaviour and
dynamic response.
Dynamic models for the AEL and PEMEL are built in PowerFactory using DIgSILENT Simulation Lan­
guage (DSL) based on characteristics and mathematical models from the literature. The electrical dynam­
ics of the electrolysers are implemented as a controlled DC voltage source and the active power control
is modelled through a PWM converter. Validating the models against similar existing experimental data
proved a sufficient U­I characteristic, but implementing an additional dynamic delay was necessary to
simulate realistic ramp rates.
The impact of the electrolyser models was investigated in an existing Nordic Power System Model
(NPSM) created in PowerFactory for analysing frequency response [1]. Through simulations of various
types of system disturbances, it was found that the electrolysers were able to handle minor active power
imbalances by providing frequency support. In the case of larger disturbances, such as the dimension­
ing incident, the impact of the electrolysers was limited as they quickly reached their maximum power
provision. The main improvements in the frequency response involved an improved post­contingency
set­point and frequency nadir. Challenges regarding small signal stability and tuning of the control were
detected and their occurrence discussed.
The economic evaluation of a single electrolyser project introduces three scenarios used to compare the
differences between pure hydrogen production, reserving capacity, and participation in the balancing
market. A Net Present Value (NPV) analysis performed to evaluate feasibility proved that none of the
scenarios was feasible when based on power price data from 2017­2022. Comparison between the sce­
narios showed that participation in reserve markets positively impacted the project NPV. A sensitivity
study concluded that a hydrogen price of 4.53 €/kg and 5.53 €/kg for the AEL and PEMEL, respectively,
the project would be feasible for pure hydrogen production. These numbers decreased to 4.11 €/kg and
4.91 €/kg if the operation allowed participation in the reserve market.
To set this thesis in perspective to adjacent research areas, a discussion of assumptions, results, and
potential extensions to the modelling was made.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
The operation of the power system is a crucial area within society, ensuring that power is safely and
reliably transmitted throughout the entirety of the system, thus always keeping a strict balance between
consumption and production. This balance is mainly reflected through the power system frequency, which
is directly connected to the speed, at which large synchronous generators are spinning.
The inertia of these spinning generators is what counteracts deviations in frequency through stored me­
chanical energy, thus stabilising the balance between consumption and production in the event of distur­
bances [2]. As to this day, these generators are still the main contributors to keeping a stable frequency
level, but as more and more renewable power generation is introduced, the disposal of conventional
generators is inevitable. As most renewable power generation is pure Converter Interfaced Generation
(CIG), and therefore not possessing any stored mechanical energy, the power system will gradually tend
towards a scenario with less system inertia. This becomes a crucial reality seen from an operating perspec­
tive since system stability can no longer be supported by the inertial response of synchronous generators
being replaced by inverter­driven ones. The severity of disturbances within the system will thus increase,
jeopardising the system stability, and hence the overall operability [2].
1.1
Motivation
Various measures can be taken to secure frequency stability in a high inverter­based power grid, such
as implementing external spinning reserves or having expensive backup gas turbines, which can pro­
vide frequency response when needed. With the sole purpose of stabilising frequency at any cost, other
technologies have more prosperous business opportunities, while still supporting the frequency during
disturbances [3]. Electrolysers have proven their ability to rapidly change their active power consumption
[4], thus showing the necessary characteristics, which could allow them to provide frequency services
to the power grid, specifically referring to the Frequency Containment Reserve (FCR). That being said,
electrolysers are still in an early stage in regards to being directly grid­connected in the size of megawatts
[5, 4]. Therefore, the dynamic behaviour of grid­sized electrolysers is essential to validate their impact,
when providing frequency response in a large interconnected power system, where various stability phe­
nomena are present.
High costs and low efficiencies have historically been a huge barrier for electrolysers to simply enter the
market as an alternative energy storage compared to other similar technologies such as batteries [5]. Pre­
viously, electrolysers have mainly been producing hydrogen from fossil­based power generation, but due
to the recent impact of renewable power generation technologies, the notion of green hydrogen produc­
tion has spiralled [6]. In essence, hydrogen can be used as an intermediate energy carrier to decarbonise
intensive energy sectors, by producing hydrogen from renewable resources instead of carbon­heavy fossil
fuels, thus making it a carbon­neutral supplement for other sectors than the electricity sector. This would
mean that the development of electrolysers will be still emerging, and possibly reach a grid­connected
level [6, 7]. The increase in the green hydrogen market has been forecasted to grow from $0.3 billion
in 2020 to $9.8 by 2028 [8]. Furthermore, the recent increase in gas prices also improves the business
opportunity for green hydrogen production [9].
To summarise, the increasing amount of renewable electricity sources and the possible increase in grid­
connected electrolysers will highly impact the power grid, and the need for more flexibility will be crucial
in terms of the stability of the whole power system [7]. Electrolysers have the possibility to solve two
of the main problems in modern days energy crises: Produce a secondary energy carrier, in the form of
hydrogen, allowing for the decarbonisation of sectors, where electrification is difficult or even not possi­
ble. Secondarily, the opportunity of aiding the stability within the power system by providing frequency
response can secure stable power transmission when inverter­based generation is present.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.2
Problem Statement
Given the evolution towards a power system with a higher share of renewable power generation, and
consequently fewer conventional generators to handle frequency instability, this thesis aims to analyse
the technical possibilities that lie within stabilising the frequency utilising grid­sized electrolysers. As
electrolysers are representing system load, having them provide frequency response comes with the dis­
advantage of continuously adjusting hydrogen production. Therefore, this thesis will additionally analyse
the economic perspective of actually engaging in frequency reserve markets.
The technical analysis will be based on building and simulating the dynamic behaviour of the most appro­
priate types of electrolysers for the purpose of providing frequency response. Modelling these as dynamic
loads with proper control action, which enables them to rapidly change their active power consumption
set­point, will be the cornerstone of the technical evaluation. Furthermore, the dynamic behaviour of the
models will be thoroughly tested in a larger interconnected system, where they will be subject to various
simulated disturbances to evaluate the extent of their capabilities of providing frequency response.
The economic analysis will be conducted to showcase opportunities and risks connected to the engage­
ment of providing frequency services. As revenues are regularly obtained from selling hydrogen, this
analysis takes the perspective of an electrolyser operator to evaluate the feasibility of establishing a new
facility, where revenue provisions are now consisting of both hydrogen sale and payments for reserving
capacity in the FCR market.
To analyse the above, the following research questions are established.
1.3
Research Questions
• How is the current Nordic Power System (NPS) affected by the implementation of large renewable
energy sources in terms of frequency stability?
• How can grid­sized electrolyser plants contribute to frequency stability by participation in the re­
serve markets?
• What are the design requirements for an electrolyser model, to provide primary frequency response
in a dynamic model of the future Nordic Power System to maintain frequency stability?
• How will participation in the reserve market impact the economical feasibility of utilising electrol­
ysers to stabilise frequency alongside the production of hydrogen?
1.4
Thesis Contributions
In relation to the problem statement and the specific research questions, the contributions of this thesis
to the field of frequency stability and implementation of electrolysers are highlighted in the following:

1.5
Research Approach
To answer the above stated research questions, this section will provide a description on the research
approach of the thesis. This aims to highlight the thoughts behind the work, and how different consider­
ations have played an important role in the development of the thesis.
Furthermore, it should be emphasised that the goal of the thesis is revolving around two partial objectives:
• Technical Feasibility: From a technical perspective the thesis aims to evaluate the operability
of having electrolysers provide dynamic frequency response within a power system. Taking a
broader perspective on the evolution of the power system, whether it would be technically possible
that electrolysers in the present and near future could support the grid during contingencies, is the
question that is to be answered.
• Economic Feasibility: Narrowing the perspective to a potential investor in an electrolyser facility,
the economic objective is to evaluate, whether a business case can be built upon having electrolysers
partially produce hydrogen and provide frequency services.
Both of these objectives are necessary to evaluate the overall possibility of having electrolysers sup­
port frequency stability by participating in the reserve markets since one cant suffice without the other.
Technical capabilities alone are not providing an incentive to implement electrolysers for the purpose of
frequency stability alone, if the economic incentives are lacking, and vice versa.
Literature Review:
First and foremost, in chapter 2 a literature study is undertaken to establish the scope of frequency stability
in the relation to the overall stability of a power system. A break­down of the frequency stability is further
conducted to (i) explain the impact on frequency response with the current evolution towards more CIG,
(ii) establish the operational boundaries of different types of frequency response, and (iii) highlight the
requirements to be able to engage in providing frequency response. Lastly, other concepts of system
stability are described as they will be present throughout the work of this thesis.
Extending the literature study, state­of­the­art electrolyser technologies are reviewed in chapter 3 to un­
derstand the working principles and showcase the technical capabilities of having these engaging in pro­
viding frequency response. Based on the technical review and especially the flexibility in response, the
AEL and PEMEL are chosen as the most promising technologies regarding this work.
Model Establishment:
In chapter 4 different approaches on modelling the dynamic behavior of the two types of electrolysers are
reviewed. Due to the fact that modelling electrolysers in the size of megawatts are not deemed a state­of­
the­art, as various research articles are building their models based on either confidential material, or case
specific expression estimated through testing. As this thesis aims to assess the impact of implementing
electrolysers in a large interconnected system from a system point­of­view, more general mathematical
models for voltage­current relationship of the AEL and PEMEL cells are used.
Having established the dynamic models for the electrolysers, these are implemented in DIgSILENT Pow­
erFactory using DSL. In addition, frequency and power control are implemented in relation to the elec­
trolyser dynamics to simulate a frequency response. In PowerFactory, the dynamics are modelled as an
externally controlled DC­voltage source, and the frequency control through a Pulse Width Modulation
(PWM)­converter, which are allowing the DSL control to be reflected within the grid configuration.
The models are validated against the experimental and theoretical behaviours in the literature to ensure
appropriate behaviour. Furthermore, the models are validated against the requirements for entering the
FCR­N market in the NPS.

System Stability
The frequency of an electric power grid is determined by the balance between the generation and con­
sumption of power. When there is a sudden change in the balance, the frequency will deviate as well.
This can impose problems on the power grid, such as equipment damage or power outages if the im­
balance is not restrained. In system stability, frequency is not the only term able to cause harm to the
system components, and it is therefore important to consider all aspects. In 2004 a task force was set up
by committees in IEEE and CIGRE to define and classify power system stability. The overall definition,
which has since been broadly accepted and cited was:
”Power system stability is the ability of an electric power system, for a given initial operating condition,
to regain a state of operating equilibrium after being subjected to a physical disturbance, with most
system variables bounded so that practically the entire system remains intact” [10]
Following this definition was a categorisation of stability in the following; frequency stability, rotor
angle stability, and voltage stability. Since 2004, power grids around the world has evolved, and new
concepts and technologies such as the CIGs has emerged. Therefore, the IEEE established an additional
task force, which published a paper [11] in 2021 with extensions and reassessment of the definitions
and classification of power system stability. The total power system stability can according to [11] be
categorized into five categories as seen in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Classification of power system stability. From [11]
The classification of the power system, seen in 2.1, provides a convenient way to analyse a complex
stability problem as the categories has specifically developed tools to analyse each specific problem.
However, the overall stability of the system should always be prioritized, and one stability category
should not be at the expense of another. It is crucial to ensure all stability requirements are met. Hence,
when focusing on frequency stability it is necessary to understand the remaining concepts, which might
influence the frequency response analysed. Therefore, this chapter will focus on describing the concept
of frequency stability, but also explain the rotor angle stability and voltage stability as they might affect
later results in relation to the frequency response.
2.1
Frequency Stability
Power system frequency stability refers to the ability of a power system to maintain a constant frequency
over time. It is a critical parameter to power system stability as it affects the operation of Synchronous

Generator (SG), motors and other frequency dependent equipment. The SG speed reflects the system
frequency and should be kept at a constant level. Large frequency deviations can cause power outages,
load shedding, damage of power system equipment and voltage imbalances [10]. Frequency excursions
are mainly happening due to disturbances in the active power balance between consumption and produc­
tion. Over the last century, one of the main problems related to frequency has been the slow mechanical
dynamic of boilers, making the system inertia a major factor in keeping the system stable. With the intro­
duction of CIGs this has changed significantly, mainly due to the fact the CIGs do not provide an inertial
response. However, the CIGs have the possibility to provide primary frequency response faster than
conventional generation [11]. As future power systems will have a higher share of CIG, the frequency
response of these systems will tend towards putting greater emphasis on the tuning of the controls con­
cerning the primary frequency response, to allow participation in the FCR. Here the focus should be on
faster­acting controllers to arrest frequency drops as grids with lower inertia tend to have instabilities
occurring earlier [11].
After an imbalance, the frequency stability is often divided into three distinct periods. Figure 2.2 shows
the three periods, which are: (i) inertial response, (ii) primary frequency response, and (iii) secondary
frequency response [11]. For each of these periods, ancillary service guidelines are often set up to enable
additional generation to support the electric power system to have a reliable system. These services can
differ significantly between electrical systems and even between Transmission System Operator (TSO)s.
The TSOs from the Nordic countries have set up a framework that explains the tender conditions of the
ancillary services to be delivered in the NPS [12]. This framework is investigated to explain how each
of the periods in Figure 2.2 is handled. The framework for handling frequency disturbances is important
to understand how the later implementation of electrolyser can aid the system stability by providing
demand­side flexibility.

2.1.1
Inertial Response
In the first period in Figure 2.2, from 0 ­ 10 seconds, the rotational inertia of the system resists changes
to the speed of all rotational devices and thereby the frequency [10]. This resistance is defined as the
amount of energy required to alter the current state of the system. The result is a gradual frequency
deviation, known as the inertial response. A measure of this initial frequency drop is called Rate of

Change of Frequency (RoCoF), which is defined as the frequency deviation within the first 500 ms after
the disturbance [13]. A lower inertia will cause a higher RoCoF and reduce the damping of the frequency
oscillations. Another important term is the frequency nadir which is the evaluation of the maximum
frequency deviation. It is dependent on the inertial response, but also the primary frequency response.
2.1.2
Primary Frequency Response
Primary frequency reserve, also known as primary control or frequency regulation, is the next period in
Figure 2.2 [11]. In this period, the frequency is to be contained within a specific range from the nominal
frequency, which is handled by the deployment of various control actions. Primary frequency reserve
is used to automatically adjust the generation of power in response to changes in the power demand. It
helps to maintain a stable frequency and prevents potential issues caused by frequency deviations. This
is typically done by adjusting the output of large power plants or hydroelectric dams, which can quickly
increase or decrease their generation of electricity. Primary frequency reserve is an important part of the
overall control and stability of a power grid, and it is essential for ensuring a reliable power supply [11].
Figure 2.3: Activation frequencies for FCR­N and FCR­N
In the NPS the primary frequency response is commonly divided into two zones depending on the size of
the deviation, namely the FCR­N and Frequency Containment Reserve Disturbance operation (FCR­D).
As seen from Figure 2.3, the threshold separating the two zones are located a 100 mHz deviation in the
frequency in both directions.
FCR­N: This type of reserve is used in the event of smaller frequency deviations mainly reflected in
power imbalances, in the 49.9­50.1 Hz range. It ensures that the equilibrium between active power supply
and demand is kept [12]. The market structure for participation in the FCR­N market is focused on
bidding a certain amount of reserve capacity for the TSOs to utilise in frequency balancing. The technical
requirements of providing this reserve can be summed as follows:
• Upwards­ and downwards­reserve bids must be symmetrical
• Linearly supplied between ­/+ 0­100 mHz deviation
• Activation within 150 seconds
• Requirement in NPS is approximately 600 MW
• Procurement is daily with hourly bids
• Procurement is proportionally to the size of the corresponding grid
The FCR­N reserve should be supplied regardless of the size of deviation, and both consumption and
production units are recognised as being able to provide automatic FCR­N regulation. This means that
demand units, such as electrolysers have the possibility to enter the FCR­N market. However, these units
cannot operate at 100% capacity all the time, as they should be able to increase production as well given
the requirement on symmetry in the response.
There is a joint responsibility from the TSOs in the NPS to supply FCR­N. The ENTSO­E RG Nordic
grid is requiring a total of approximately 600 MW in FCR­N [12, 14]. The Danish, Swedish, Norwegian,
and Finnish TSO is obliged to supply a proportionate share that changes from year to year. In this thesis,
the share will be based on their share in 2021.

In the NPS, FCR­N is procured collaboratively and symmetrically at daily auctions by the Nordic TSOs.
Suppliers are providing hourly bids, meaning that they reserve their capacity for one whole hour per
bid. Bids must state an hour­by­hour volume and price for the day of operation. However, a bid can be
composed of several consecutive hours, referred to as a block bid, but these must have the same price
for all quantities in the given block. Pricing is contracted in a pay­as­bid framework, meaning that all
participants submit a bid to the FCR­N market, and the corresponding TSO optimises the amount of
needed capacity along with the price. This also implies that having electrolysers submitting a bid for
providing FCR­N reserve, not necessarily means winning the bid due to market mechanisms.
FCR­D: The second type of ancillary service for frequency stability is used as a fast reserve in the event of
a major system disturbance [12]. FCR­D is divided into separate up and down­regulation products. FCR­
D is mainly focused on upward reserve to handle larger outages of major transmission lines or generation
units since a high increase in the generation is less plausible. The reserve is activated automatically when
the system frequency drops below 49.9 Hz or increases above 50.1 Hz and remains active until manual
Frequency Restoration Reserve (mFRR) takes over or the frequency has been restored. Units providing
FCR­D must apply to the following technical requirements.
• Supply power at frequencies between 49.9 and 49.5 Hz
• Reduce supply at frequencies between 50.1­50.5 Hz
• Supply 50% of the response within 5 seconds
• Supply the remaining 50% of the response within an additional 25 seconds
The total volume of FCR­D is determined by the size of the fault of the largest operating nuclear power
station at Oskarshamn, referred to as the dimensioning incident. Deliveries to the FCR­D can be made
from several generation or consumption units, collectively delivering the required response, again creat­
ing the possibility for electrolysers to access the reserve market as demand side reserve. In this thesis,
the scope will not include the possibility of having electrolysers participate in the FCR­D market, but the
necessity of understanding its framework is essential to understand the overall system response.

aid the FCR­D. In situations where there are major system disturbances such as loss of large generators or

Therefore, FFR is necessary for these situations as the FCR­D cannot by itself maintain frequency at the
specified threshold values in the event of outages. Units providing FFR must apply to the following
technical requirements

Secondary Response
Secondary response through automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve (aFRR) is used to restore the fre­
quency. This response is often activated between 30 seconds to 10 minutes as seen in Figure 2.2. In
Eastern Danish Transmission Grid (DK2), units bidding in the aFRR market must be able to deliver a full

Additional Stability Concepts
As explained in the research approach, section 1.5, this thesis involves the implementation of electrolysers
in a larger interconnected system. Therefore, additional stability issues will evidently be present during
simulations. To understand the impact of implementing electrolysers in regard to frequency stability, it
is crucial to distinguish between stability concepts.
2.2.1
Rotor Angle Stability
The concept of angle stability is important for this thesis, as the frequency response of the system will
vary depending on the rotor angles. Therefore, some of the results in chapter 6 might be explained by
the theory provided in this chapter. When studying the frequency response of a synchronous area such as
the NPS, the rotor angle stability concept of synchronous generators is an important aspect to understand.
Especially when inspecting the transient response of the system, the synchronous generators can have a
great effect on the system response. This is relating to their ability to remain in synchronism under normal
operating conditions and regain synchronism after disturbances. When a system disturbance happens, a
change in the power flow will inevitably happen. This will force the power output of the generators to
change, which will interrupt the torque balance of the SGs [17].

2.2.2
Voltage stability
Voltage stability refers to the ability of the system to always maintain a steady voltage level at all busses
within the system, whether this is during normal operation or under the impact of a system disturbance
[10]. As for the rotor angle stability, the voltage stability can also be explained from Equation 2.2, where
δ is corresponding to the voltage angle difference between two busses. The sign of the angle defines the
direction of the power flow. Voltage angle displacement between buses generally occurs due to the flow
of reactive power within the AC transmission lines of the power system. AC lines are imposing reactive
power losses due to their inherently inductive reactance X′, thus influencing the overall power flow along
with the voltage stability of the power system. This consequently means that disturbances influencing
the reactive power flow impact the voltage stability, such as the loss of reactive power generation or AC
lines.
Therefore, implementing electrolysers could affect the reactive power flow by imposing additional power
transfer along the system, which should be considered from a system point of view. Being DC loads, the
electrolysers are not able to provide reactive power compensations on their own, but may however be
able to support the voltage stability through the control of their corresponding power converter [7, 15].
As this is not directly in relation to the scope of this thesis, the voltage control of the converter will not
be modelled, but is subject to later discussions on the matter.
2.2.3
Small Signal Stability
Another stability issue that might occur when running tests in a large system are small signal instabilities.
Generally, there are three different types of small signal instability [18]:
• Oscillations between a single generator or a group of generators and the whole system
• Oscillations between generators in the same plant
• Oscillations between a group of generators against another group of generators.
The instability can occur in two variations; the rotor angle increases due to lack of synchronising torque
or rotor angle, and oscillations increase in size due to lack of damping torque [18]. In this thesis, the
small signal instabilities might also be caused by insufficient tuning of the control, which could impose
oscillations in the other generators. Small signal stability can be validated in PowerFactory using eigen­
value/modal simulations, however, this is out of the scope of this thesis to investigate.

Electrolyser Characteristics
This chapter will examine the characteristics of electrolysers and whether they can contribute to providing
the grid balancing services explained in chapter 2. More specifically participate in the FCR­N market,
as demand response contributors. Acting as a dynamic load, key characteristics in regard to the active
power response are presented throughout this chapter in order to shed light on the technical applicability
of electrolysers when they are providing frequency response and producing hydrogen simultaneously.
Characteristics for various types of electrolysers will be presented in order to compare their individual
capabilities for providing frequency response in order to determine which type is more appropriate for
providing frequency response.
3.1
Working Principles
Electrolysers are characterised by their ability to separate water molecules into hydrogen and oxygen by
applying an external electrical current through the stack of the electrolyser. The electrolyser stack is built
by combining electrolyser cells, and it is within each cell that the actual separation of water is happening
[7]. The composition of a cell is varying depending on the type of electrolyser. The overall composition
for all of the types is that they consist of an anode and a cathode, which are separated by an electrolyte.
The chemical reaction happening within the cell is based on which type of electrolyte is used [7], as
depicted in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Diagrams and chemical reactions showing the working principles of AEL, PEMEL, and
SOEL. Illustrations from [19].
Alkaline Electrolyser (AEL): This type of electrolyser is composed of a liquid alkaline solution, which
the electrodes and separating diaphragm are submerged into. Being the simpler way of constructing elec­
trolysers, the AEL is also the most commercialised technology. The high degree of commercialisation
entails that the efficiency is in the interval of 63­70% based on Lower Heating Value (LHV) [7]. The
stack is typically larger compared to the other types, reaching into the range of meters, where PEMEL
and Solide Oxide Electrolyser (SOEL) are typically in the range of centimetres which also influences the
current density of the cells, being rated between 0.2 −0.8 A/cm2 [19]. The high level of commercial­
isation and the simplicity of the stack constitutes that the associated Capital Expenditure (CapEx) and
Operational Expenditure (OpEx) are low in comparison to the other electrolyser technologies.

Polymer Exchange Membrane Electrolyser (PEMEL): Supplying water to the cathode allows for the
separation of electrodes through a proton exchange membrane. Being less commercialised than the AEL,
advanced designs of the exchange membranes still allow for high values of efficiency, which is rated as
61­70% based on LHV [7]. Having significantly higher current densities compared to AEL, stretching
from 1.5 −2.3 A/cm2, the PEMEL technology makes up for a smaller footprint. A smaller footprint
might be positively reflected in the CapEx, but due to oxidative environment leading to high corrosive
effects on materials, it is a requirement for the PEMEL to use noble metals, which constitutes an even
larger increase in costs [7].
Solide Oxide Electrolyser (SOEL): The SOEL is the type of electrolyser, which can reach the highest
efficiency, rated above 80%. This is due to the high operating temperatures compared to those of the
AEL and PEMEL. Operating at temperatures higher than 500°Celsius also gives the SOEL the advantage
of being able to operate in a reverse configuration, thus producing power. Even though this sounds like
a valuable property in terms of providing frequency response, the SOEL is still in comparison a highly
immature technology [7]. Therefore, the SOEL is also associated with the highest CapEx, and many
economic risks are associated with a potential investment in the technology.
Based on the above discussion of the electrolyser types, it has been decided that the AEL and PEMEL are
the most relevant technologies to further study. Therefore, the remaining thesis will only concern itself
with parameters, modelling and analysis regarding these. The main reason for precluding the SOEL is
due to the maturity of the technology, which will have a too high impact in terms of costs combined with
too much technical uncertainty.

sponse, the stack size is probably the most important factor, giving a state­of­the­art capacity that a single
stack can provide. The reason is simply that if electrolysers should be able to participate in reserve mar­
kets, they need to reserve capacity in the megawatt range to have any impact on a grid level. The AEL
and PEMEL are presently in the same range in terms of stack capacity and are both forecasted to be
increasing towards a stack size of 10 MW capacity in 2030, based on future planned facilities [7].
Current density, temperature and pressure are mainly connected to the performance of the electrolyser, and
more specifically to the efficiency of the whole facility [20]. All three parameters are therefore important
in regard to the electrical performance of the electrolyser facility, where various trade­offs would need
to be made, for example, the increase of efficiency at higher temperatures with the potential drawback of
faster material degradation, thus shorter lifetime of the stack [20]. Thickening the materials, alternative
coating of the materials, or simply replacing the stack after the end of its lifetime are all options that
could cope with degradation, but on the other hand, increases the overall cost of the facility. The same
considerations should be made for the choice of current density and operating pressure. More information
about the impact of key parameters in terms of operating an electrolyser facility is investigated in [20,
21].
Flexibility: The flexibility parameters shown in Table 3.1, are as compared to sizing not as much re­
lated to the hydrogen output nor design choices of the electrolysers. The flexibility refers to the dynamic
constraints of operating an electrolyser [7]. These parameters are the most important aspects regarding
operating the electrolyser to provide frequency response. Especially start­up times and ramp rates are
of great importance, since these are in direct relation to the requirements of participating in the FCR­N
market, as explained in section 2.1. Long start­up times should hinder the electrolysers from being turned
off if they should change their set point quickly. This means that to provide reserve capacity, the electrol­
yser should already be operating. Comparing the AEL and PEMEL in terms of flexible operation is also
constituting the most obvious differences, where the PEMEL are overall showing better performance.
Looking specifically at the ramp rate, the PEMEL can change its power consumption by 100% within a
second, thus being up to five times faster than the AEL.

active power/frequency characteristic is defining the new set­point.

4.3
Active Power Control & Converter Topology
The power control block contains the logic responsible for controlling the converter connecting the elec­
trolyser with the AC grid. According to [15, 33] the most commonly used converter topology for this
type of connecting, is by implementing a two­stage back­to­back converter (DC­DC­AC). This topology
is created for grid­connecting various types of storage units, which is also representing the key connection
features of an electrolyser. In this converter topology, the DC­DC converter controls the active power
output to be operating at a stable level, and the DC­AC side ensures a stable DC­link voltage [33].
Due to the scope of this thesis, which is only concerning itself with the control of the active power
response, the DC­AC converter side is not further modelled. This means that the converter will not
have any reactive power compensation to keep a stable voltage level. Modelling a PWM­converter in
PowerFactory as operating in PQ­control mode, allows it to maintain a constant active and reactive power
set­point, while the voltage can vary over a limited range [27], which will be determined by the overall
power flows of the system in regard to the remaining components. As it will be explained in chapter 5,
the electrolyser model is later implemented in a larger model, resembling the NPS, where other control
systems are providing the necessary voltage control. Therefore, the electrolyser model itself will not
control have its reactive power set­point controlled, but simply keep it at constant zero due to the PQ
mode. The active power set­point, on the other hand, is determining the initial state of operation, and will
consequently change when the grid is exposed to frequency deviations due to the control system.

representing the outer power control, whereas the second is representing the inner current control loop.

the d­axis current, due to the choice of using the internal current controller of the converter, thus leaving
no option to constraint the final control signal. This will consequently have the same effect in terms of
not overloading the converter, which is why the limits are to be applied in the first place. The limits are
also important in terms of start­up times of the electrolysers since start­up can take up to a minute, as
explained in chapter 3. The limits are crucial to ensure that the electrolyser is always operating since it
would not be possible in a real­life scenario to provide a frequency response if a cold start­up was engaged
before a fault.

CHAPTER 6. CASE STUDY ­ IMPACT OF DISTURBANCES
6
Case Study ­ Impact of Disturbances
In the previous chapter, the outline of the NPSM has been drawn, where various system components
have been slightly modified to allow the implementation of the electrolyser units without impacting the
stable initial conditions of the systems upon doing so. Furthermore, frequency response which is already
acting within the system has been highlighted to distinguish between the original NPSM and the case of
implementing electrolysers.
Having made the changes, as discussed in chapter 5, this chapter seizes to assess the impact on the FCR­N
contribution by the implementation of electrolysers. Therefore, a variety of simulations cases have been
made, which are specified in the following:
• Loss of Generation
• Loss of Load
• Dimensioning Incident
• Loss of HVDC connection
• Loss of HVAC connection
Firstly, imbalances and minor disturbances are tested regarding the loss of a low­capacity load and gen­
erator. These types of tests will constitute simulations within the frequency limits of FCR­N operation.
These tests are relevant in the light of providing FCR­N given that the term is directly referring to nor­
mal operation, and thus the ability to handle continuously changing imbalances in power production and
consumption.
Secondly, the dimensioning incident will showcase whether the electrolysers would be able to provide
additional assistance during events of larger disturbances. The dimensioning incident is referring to the
loss of the largest generation unit within a power system and is supposed to be handled by other units
contributing to the FCR­D market or even the FFR. Nevertheless, the relevance towards the electrolysers
stays the same, since it would be relevant to look into the difference in overall response during the di­
mensioning incident with and without electrolysers activating their primary frequency response. Mainly
due to the fast reaction times of the electrolysers, the maximum deviation in frequency would be assisted,
hence lower frequency nadir.
The loss of a HVDC connection is referring to a scenario where an interconnector between two system

Preliminary Summary
It has been shown that the electrolysers are capable of providing a sufficient response to comply with
FCR­N requirements. This is proven as the electrolysers improve the key performance characteristics of
the frequency response, such as stable post­contingency set­point, during minor imbalances in production
and consumption. Additionally, it has been shown that the electrolysers are also capable of assisting in
frequency stabilisation under the occurrences of larger disturbances.
Regarding the loss of a HVAC line, it was assessed whether the dead band of the power error within the
Power Control­block should be increased for the controller to not react upon small changes in the power
measurements even though the frequency deviations was appropriate. Since the power measurement
reacted upon voltage changes, this will lead to a further discussion on voltage stability in chapter 8.
The control does not affect the RoCoF as no inertia is changed. However, both the frequency nadir and
steady­state frequency can be improved. A shortcoming of the electrolyser control was found in cases
where small signal stability issues might occur. Here the control does not have sufficient tuning to reduce
the oscillations occurring.
As a last remark, the overloading capabilities were not taken into account in the above analysis, since
the focus of this chapter was to evaluate the frequency response of the AEL and PEMEL in regards to
the technical requirements for participating in the FCR­N market. Since overloading capabilities do not
invoke any different type of response, apart from increasing the amount of power supplied per unit. This
concludes that from a system perspective, overloading capabilities would not impose any changes, since
there will always be a fixed amount of FCR­N capacity needed, regardless of the facilities reserving it.
On the contrary, from the perspective of an electrolyser facilitator, this would have an impact on the
incentive to bid in the FCR­N market. This perspective will lay the foundation for the following chapter,
where the economical feasibility of establishing an electrolyser facility is analysed.

Economic Evaluation ­ Project Level
In the previous chapters, the technical feasibility of utilising electrolysers for providing primary frequency
response was assessed. chapter 6 showed that both the AEL and the PEMEL were able to fulfil the
requirements for participating in the reserve market and achieve a fast response to frequency deviations,
with the PEMEL being the favourable choice from a purely technical point of view. Having determined
that electrolysers are technically able to provide primary frequency response, the question now lies with,
whether an operator of an electrolyser facility should invest in it.
This chapter aims to showcase the difference between the two types of electrolysers in terms of eco­
nomical investment. Therefore, this chapter will present reasonable figures for the costs of building a
grid­sized electrolyser to enlighten the question of, whether participation in the reserve market could pro­
vide an additional revenue stream. Thus, serving as an incentive to invest in more electrolyser facilities
seen from an investment point of view. Three cases for both types of electrolysers are analysed:
• Scenario 1: Revenues are only gained from the sale of hydrogen to the gas grid.
• Scenario 2: Revenues are gained from a partial sale of hydrogen, but also from reserving capacity
for the FCR­N market.
• Scenario 3: An extension to Scenario 2, where frequency measurements are used to determine the
amount of capacity activated in the balancing market.
To simplify the economic analysis to a point, where other market participants are not involved, this thesis
assumes that the operators of the electrolyser units will always win the bid, they offer. This means that the
risk of being underbid and not being able to reserve capacity is eliminated. It also means that there will
be no need to conduct a bidding strategy involving market optimisation as a price taker. This assumption
is discussed later in chapter 8. This particular assumption is also implying that there will always be a
demand for FCR­N, even though there might be times when TSOs do not need FCR­N provision.
In light of the technical evaluation in the previous chapters, it is also assumed that both electrolyser units
are capable of fulfilling the requirements for participating in the FCR­N market. This implies that they
can offer a capacity at the reserve market, which can be activated if needed in the balancing market. The
bidding constraints presented will be discussed throughout this chapter.

The cornerstone of the economical assessment is the NPV analysis. The NPV is used to capture the total
value of the investment opportunity, as it accounts for the time value of money. It is calculated by the
net cash inflow and outflow during the lifetime of the electrolyser. The CF will be subject to a yearly
discount rate, given in Table 7.1.
A positive NPV indicates an economically feasible project, where the rate of return on the project is higher
than the discount rate. This means that an investor can earn more money by investing in the specific
project, than by alternative investments such as government bonds or investment funds. A negative NPV
indicates in­feasibility, meaning that it would be more beneficial to invest in alternatives.
The NPV analysis can also be used to estimate prices and costs of the project that would result in a net­zero
investment where the project reaches break­even at the end of its lifetime. This could i.e. be finding the
minimal hydrogen selling price for the project to be feasible, which would yield an NPV = 0. Finding
the discount rate that yields the NPV = 0 would provide the Internal Rate of Return, which is simply
another measure of the feasibility of the project. The relationship between the NPV and the CF is given
in Equation 7.1.

electrolyser should be able to be provided in both directions. This means that the electrolyser should
have an operating set­point where it allows up and down­regulation without reaching its operating limits.
Otherwise, this could lead to a case where the electrolyser is not able to provide the proper response, thus
violating the symmetry in response. As a result, a bid­size parameter δ is used to determine the amount
of capacity bid.

to increase nor decrease its power set­point, but is constantly just producing the same amount of hydro­

8.1
Electrolyser Modelling
Due to a high scarcity of literature that accurately explains and models the electrical­chemical behaviour
of an electrolyser stack in the size of megawatts, it has been difficult to develop and implement a model
that could be fully validated against a real­life grid­size electrolyser. Furthermore, trying to implement
different stack models, the AEL and the PEMEL, has complicated the literature research. This has conse­
quently entailed that various assumptions and design choices had to be made. It is important to acknowl­
edge that changing these will most likely affect the results and this section will focus on assessing the
impact of these assumptions.
One of the major assumptions made was in terms of how the electrolyser stacks are modelled. In this
thesis, it was an active choice to base these models on theoretical electrochemical relations concerning a
single cell of the electrolyser and then scale the number of cells to get a nominal power equal to 5 MW.
This has resulted in exceptionally larger numbers for the cells inside the AEL and PEMEL, as seen in
chapter 4, with 2700 and 62000 cells, respectively. The assumption that there were no losses between
cells or stacks in series ensured that the mathematical model could linearly be extended to the number
of cells needed in the electrolyser module. This way it was possible to theoretically estimate the U­I
characteristic of the large­scale electrolysers. However, currently, there are simply not enough empirical
tests on electrolysers on the scale of megawatts to prove the relationships in these models.
The high number of cells in series would cause small ohmic losses between each cell, causing an extra
voltage drop. This extra voltage drop might seem insignificant if only connecting 21 cells for a 26 kW
electrolyser as in [26], but with a least 1000 cells in series, this could be a relevant factor to take into
account. The higher voltage drop would however mainly affect the composition stacks and cells in the
electrolyser as the slope of the U­I characteristic would have been the same. It is important to recognize
that the extra ohmic loss would have caused lower efficiency in the electrolyser. In the technical part
of the thesis, the efficiency does not affect the frequency response that the electrolysers can deliver, and
therefore it would not affect this part of the thesis. In the economical part, the efficiency was found
through other literature, with large­scale electrolyser and hence this would not be affected as well.
Having acknowledged this, the cell U­I characteristics for both types were evaluated in section 4.4, where
the final stack models were implemented with a capacity of 5 MW and were simulated in a NPSM also in
the megawatt scale. The results proved that the U­I characteristics produced from this test were similar to
the ones from corresponding models in the literature. The U­I characteristic was then validated as being
within the acceptable operating limits for each of the models. Since the main focus of this thesis is on
providing active power response, it was simply assumed that the U­I characteristic could be disregarded
in the following assessments.
Implementation of the electrolyser models in the NPSM proved that when a larger interconnected model
and power transmission were taken into account, the voltage levels at the busbars of the electrolysers
did not suffice. Therefore, the need of implementing additional shunt reactors at different busbars was
necessary to keep the voltage within operational limits. Since reactive power compensation in real life

8.2
Control Limitations
This section aims to provide a overview of the limitations of the implemented control and discuss how
changes in the control strategy might affect results for the better. This will mostly touch upon topics
closely related to the scope but also direct the research into topics just outside of the scope.
Voltage Control
As concluded in the previous section, the voltage at the electrolyser terminals differs throughout the
operation and might affect the overall stability of the system. In this thesis, a shunt reactor is used to
automatically provide reactive power response at the high voltage busbars at the electrolyser end of trans­
mission lines. This solves the problem of voltage instability, but having the electrolyser units solve the
instabilities through their control, might prove a better solution.
This was due to the assumption that a facilitator engaging in building an electrolyser plant would not have
to pay for any component beyond the 10 kV connection. With this assumption, it would be the responsible
TSO that would have to pay for these. In a real­life scenario, there might however be some regulation, for
voltage stability, on connecting facilities to the grid. For example, the Danish TSO Energinet has require­
ments for the connection of new demand facilities, which includes voltage operating limits and quality
[50]. Thus, voltage control deems a necessary extension to the model for an electrolyser in general, but
especially if the goal is to participate in primary frequency regulation. The possibility of having the elec­
trolysers provide voltage control on their own is possible by the utilisation of the power electronics within
the corresponding grid converter [15, 33, 51] was mentioned in section 4.3. The possibility of improving
the voltage quality at the POC, shown in [51], by the introduction of a coupled grid converter that can
level out reactive power fluctuations without affecting the efficiency of the electrolyser significantly.
It is important to note that the NPSM, was originally constructed to investigate the frequency dynamics
of the NPS. The model was therefore ideal for a study of frequency response by electrolysers. To under­
stand how voltage stability might have affected the results of this thesis, a study on the NPSM concerning
voltage stability should be undertaken. This would show how the U­I characteristics might have influ­
enced the operability of the system during disturbances, especially in connection to the implementation
of additional load reflected through the electrolysers. So far, voltage stability has been handled in the
system by tap changing transformers and shunts. This method was also used as the voltage increased
heavily at the electrolyser busses, due to the long cables attaching the facilities to the rest of the system.
Inertial Control
In addition to the considerations on implementing voltage control to cope with voltage stability issues,
other aspects of stability could also be investigated such as inertial stability. In chapter 6 it was frequently
noticed that the electrolysers do not provide any inertial response. This meant that the RoCoF of most
responses was not affected by the implementation of the electrolysers, thus highlighting the opportunity
of extending the frequency control to include measures to provide an inertial response. This would also
propose a highly relevant topic to discuss due to the current development in power systems. The phasing
out of conventional generators, and hence system inertia, imposes new challenges in the field of providing
inertial frequency response. The ability to individually provide virtual inertia to the system is also seen
in other areas of implementation, such as wind turbines [52].
This thesis has solely based this part of the control on a standard proportional droop controller, where the
active power is linearly increasing with the amount of capacity bid until the full frequency deviation of

8.3.1
Locations and Plants
In the base case of the system, in chapter 6, the amount of electrolyser capacity was implemented to
deliver the exact required amount of FCR­N for the NPS, hence having all electrolyser units bid the same
amount of their capacity, namely 40%. A way to avoid this would be to implement more electrolysers in
the system, where each facility could then bid a lower amount. Doing this would have been possible as
a full market review was undertaken to understand the pipeline and future outlook of real­life hydrogen
production. In this review, all projects with expected commissioning before 2030 were evaluated in
tables in Appendix B and a few were chosen to be implemented in this thesis. Technically all of these
plants could be introduced as a participator in the FCR­N control, thus allowing for smaller bids per
electrolyser unit. Different scenarios regarding the implementation of the electrolyser facilities would
provoke different results.
In a scenario where all projects from the market review were to be implemented in the NPS, but the same
facilities delivering FCR­N, the total load of the system would increase with 6.12 GW. As the load is
increased in the system, so should the generation. Assuming that the increased generation comes from
CIGs the inertia won’t be affected significantly. Secondly, none of the electrolysis facilities would provide
any inertia to the system, which means that the robustness and the inertial response of the system would
stay approximately the same.
One thing that would be affected significantly would be the voltage stability of the system. As all facilities
would be bounded by the U­I characteristics described in chapter 4. Figure 4.11 and Figure 4.12 show
that changes in the output of electrolyser current will change the voltage of the electrolyser cells leading
to a voltage change at facility terminals. Having a large amount of production from the electrolysers
along with some of it reserved for active power control will contribute to high changes in voltage. In
the future development of the power grid involving electrolyser facilities, it would be beneficial to add
voltage control to the controller abilities, as this would be one of the large factors that the facilities affect.

To conclude, implementing all electrolyser facilities as loads in the system would not have any effect on
the frequency control, however, it could play a significant role concerning voltage stability.
In a scenario with the full amount of electrolyser capacity implemented, but where the FCR­N capacity
is split out between the facilities, again with the assumption that production is mostly coming from CIGs,
the inertia would not change significantly. As the amount of FCR­N reserve is not changing, the overall
active power balance, hence the frequency response during a contingency would most likely stay the same.
Nevertheless, by distributing the FCR­N response within the system, the distance of the active power flow
would potentially be minimised following a disturbance. This could relieve individual transmission lines
from single handily transporting larger amounts of power by limiting the active path from reserve to the
disturbance. Smaller amounts of bidding from each plant could also ensure that no interaction between
synchronous generators and electrolyser control are electrically close to each other as the damping of the
synchronous generator would eliminate this instability.
The reason why all the facilities were not implemented in the NPSM can be summed up in two conclusions:
(i) Firstly that this thesis was about the ability of electrolysers to participate in the FCR­N market and that
implementing all of the electrolysers acting as pure loads, would not have changed the frequency response
significantly enough for the authors to engage with the time­consuming activity of implementing these,
(ii) it can be argued how realistic it is that all of the projects in the market review will be realised. Many
of these projects are in a very early stage and have not received any funding, but are simply investigating
the possibilities of creating the plants.

Conclusion
The was found that the integration of large­scale renewable has a significant impact on the power system
both due to their variable and intermittent electricity generation which causes active power imbalances,
but also because most of the renewable energy sources are Converter Interfaced Generation (CIG)s that
does not naturally provide any inertia, which causes a worse inertial response in the system. As CIGs
has a faster response than conventional generation, a possible shift in inertial response towards having
more primary frequency response could occur. Using converter­based response, the frequency nadir can
be improved, however, the rocof will consequently worsen.
Electrolysers can provide primary frequency reserve (Frequency Containment Reserve Normal operation
(FCR­N)) in the Nordic Power System (NPS). The units can control their power consumption through
their converter and have multiple advantages that indicate possibilities of using them as participants in
the FCR­N market. This includes their wide flexibility, which enables them to increase or decrease pro­
duction considerably. Furthermore, both Alkaline Electrolyser (AEL) and especially Polymer Exchange
Membrane Electrolyser (PEMEL) have quite fast ramping rates that qualify the units for participating
in providing primary frequency response. Additional literature has been undertaking similar studies on
utilising electrolysers for frequency control, but have been limited to smaller grid models resembling
mostly the Central European Grid. These make up for interesting comparisons, but do not include as
much complexity as the NPS modelled in this thesis.
It was found that to deliver primary frequency reserve to the NPS, the electrolysers should be able to
provide a linear symmetrical response for a 100 mHz frequency deviation in both directions within 150
seconds. With ramp rates of 20% and 100%/s and load ranges from 10­110% and 0­160% for the AEL
and PEMEL, respectively, this was deemed possible.
Validating the U­I characteristic of the dynamic cell of the electrolysers showed appropriate behaviour,
apart from a small downwards shift in the voltage for the AEL cell, due to a lower pressure used. The
desired capacity of the electrolysers was gained by linearly scaling the electrolyser cells. A basic droop
control was used to change the active power set­point depending on frequency deviations in the system,
which was validated against previous literature study on electrolysers, and it was found that additional
dynamic delay had to be added to model the real ramping time of the electrolyser types.
Implementing a total electrolyser capacity of 1315 MW in the NPSM enabled 526 MW FCR­N provision.
The frequency response of the system with electrolysers was tested in five different types of contingencies,
which showed that both types of electrolysers are capable of improving the key performance parameters
of the response such as the post­contingency steady state and the frequency nadir. This was true even on
occasions where the frequency excursion is larger than 100 mHz.
In all cases, the response from the synchronous generators still impacts the response, which is observed
by small damped oscillations during the first 5 seconds after the disturbance. These oscillations do not
have a major impact on the response, however, they reveal a deficiency in the electrolyser response as
small signal instabilities were observed due to these oscillations. Here, the synchronous generators in the
system and some of the electrolyser facilities started acting in reverse of each other, thereby cancelling out
each other’s response. Lastly, it was shown that it is possible for the electrolysers units to simultaneously
decrease their bidding amount without causing major problems in the grid.
In the economic Net Present Value (NPV) analysis, three scenarios were developed to be able to compare
the strategy of only producing hydrogen versus bidding on the FCR­N market and utilising the reserve
for system balancing. The NPV analysis found that with current power and hydrogen prices, no scenarios
would generate a positive NPV and thereby generate returns. Furthermore, it was found that the small
difference in efficiency between the two electrolysers types had a major impact on the results, where the
PEMEL with a lower efficiency was a lot less feasible compared to the AEL.

It was found that the lack of bidding strategy in our modelling has consequences for the economic analysis.
Due to a hydrogen selling price that is lower than the electricity buying price, increasing the overloading
capability will decrease the NPV of the project. However, the opposite effect was shown when the hy­
drogen selling price was greater than the electricity price.
By solving the total Cash Flow (CF) for a NPV = 0 it was found that a realistic decrease in electricity
prices would make the projects feasible. This highlights why most electrolyser projects are undertaken
in combination with renewable energy projects where the electricity will be cheaper.
A sensitivity study showed that the AEL will comprise a feasible project at lower hydrogen prices com­
pared to the PEMEL. However, it also showed that due to PEMELs overloading capability this type
of electrolyser would provide larger revenue at high hydrogen prices, above 7.6€/kg. The sensitivity
study furthermore, revealed that even though the AEL has a better NPV result it is also more sensitive to
changes in prices, whereas the PEMEL with its larger overloading capability can mitigate prices increases,
by changing its power set point while still maintaining high reserve market bids.
9.1
Future Work
As this thesis has examined the particular study on frequency stability in connection with the integration of
electrolysers in the power system, there are still various stability issues there should be taken into account
to properly evaluate every aspect that follows the integration. As touched upon regularly throughout the
report and discussed in more detail in chapter 8, the following points should be included in future works
following this thesis to fully evaluate the feasibility of integrating electrolysers capable of participating
in frequency services.
• Voltage control implemented in the dynamic model of the electrolysers. This could show the possi­
bilities of electrolysers contributing to keeping a stable voltage set­point at the Point Of Connection
(POC), and potentially omit the use of additional shunt reactances.
• Inertial control added to the base frequency control. By enabling the electrolysers to act on the
RoCoF, it might be possible for the electrolysers to provide an emulated inertial response.
• Include an optimisation algorithm for the economical model. This would make it possible to couple
the economics with renewable energy production by including i.e. wind production, power prices,
and frequency measurements to establish an optimised bidding schedule for the electrolyser on
yearly basis.
As a final remark, it would be relevant to perform other types of studies in PowerFactory than the RMS
stability study, as this only enables the study of stability in a larger interconnected system based on
simplified network equations.
As the electrolysers are connected with the remaining power system through converters, it would be
proposed to perform an EMT (Electromagnetic Transient) study on the converter behaviour during con­
tingencies. In an EMT study, the network equations are not converted to the phasor domain but reflected
through differential equations. This of course constitutes higher computational effort but will allow one to
study the converter regarding phenomena such as power quality, harmonics of the converter, and switch­
ing of its IGBTs.
Furthermore, as the electrolysers are implemented alongside other generators simulating dynamic re­
sponse, the eigenvalue/modal analysis can be utilised to study the small signal stability of the system.
Since the eigenvalues of a power system represent the natural frequency, where post­contingency oscil­
lations will occur, these can be used to identify potential stability issues in the system.

C.2
Direction of Disturbance
This short chapter tests the inherent symmetrical properties of the FCR­N control of the electrolysers.
In chapter 2 it was mentioned how the FCR­N market is symmetric. This mean that the electrolysers
implemented and the rest of the grid, should react the same way, no matter the direction of the disturbance.

In Equation F.1a, PH2/η is the hydrogen price divided by the efficiency η, which constitutes the selling
price of hydrogen converted to the power consumption side, thus given in €/MW. Ctotal are the total
electrolyser capacity given in MW, Pre is the payment in €/MW for reserving a percentage δ of the total
capacity, p0 are the percentage set­point of the total capacity, and Pel are the cost of power in €/MW. All
terms are summed over the time T, thus evaluated in each time­step t given in an hourly resolution.
The constraint in Equation F.1b, are ensuring that the sum of the bid δ and set­point p0 are always greater
than the minimum allowed capacity Cmin. Equation F.1c are allowing the sum of the bid and set­point
to reach the maximum overloading capability Cover, meaning that bids are still allowed for higher power
set­points.
The constraints in Equation F.1d and F.1e are constraining the decision variables. δ are only constrained
to be larger than 0, since it is naturally constrained by the previous constraints. p0 is constrained between
the minimum allowed capacity Cmin since the electrolyser should always be running, and Cmax which
is basically given as 1, being the percentage of running at nominal capacity.
