PREFACE 
This thesis is a study on how to advise setting up PV applications in Maniitsoq and oth-
er arctic regions. The thesis is mainly concerned with PV installations in Maniitsoq, but 
also contains some explanations of issues occurring when introducing lots of solar ener-
gy to a small energy system.  
Two cameras have been installed in Maniitsoq to monitor four different PV installations 
in terms of snow cover. Also an Elspec power quality analyser has been installed in the 
Kilaaseeraq School, to analyse the grid and one of the four PV installations on the 
school roof. The two camera systems which were installed were made in Denmark. The 
Systems was based on the Raspberry Pi platform, which is mainly Linux programming.

ABSTRACT 
This thesis contains an analysis of the different factors contributing in one way or an-
other to the power production of PV installations in Maniitsoq and the rest of Green-
land. The analysis is made to provide knowledge about snow shedding and how to ad-
vise on building new PV-installations. The analysis is based on photo series taken by 
four Raspberry Pi setups, two in Maniitsoq and two in Sisimiut. Apart from that, the 
report is using data from the Maniitsoq CHP plant, from an Elspec and various weather 
data. A model from Truckee California has been calibrated to give the monthly PV pro-
duction loss due to snow. The model clearly shows a declining loss due to snow when 
the tilt angle is increased and another less considered factor is the snow build up under 
the array edge which has a huge effect on the snow loss. The optimal tilt angle is a 
complex thing to give because it depends on a variety of factors including the plane of 
array insolation, but it has been estimated that providing only little information an opti-
mal angle can be given with some uncertainty. The essential factor is the height from 
the array edge to the roof or ground. In the case that it is high enough, the optimal tilt

The report has also been analyzing data, to determine in what degree the large amount 
of solar energy in Maniitsoq‘s utility grid, has a negative effect on the Maniitsoq CHP 
plant and whether quick power ramping caused by fluctuating insolation is resulting in 
frequency drops. The report found no clear evidence of any grid problems.

INTRODUCTION 
There is no united utility grid in Greenland, because of the terrain and long distances, 
thus most towns and settlements have their own energy supply consisting of some hydro 
plants but mostly it is based on fossil fuels. The settlement Maniitsoq receives energy 
from a central diesel power plant, but more and more private households begin to con-
tribute to the energy system by installing PV. The change in production profile might 
affect the power quality, because the diesel generators have to adjust quickly to sudden 
changes that will occur when lots of PV installations are simultaneously affected by 
passing clouds. 
Diesel generators are running most efficiently within a range of 50-75% rated power 
(Supply Side). When there is much solar energy in Maniitsoq‘s utility grid, the diesel 
generators might be forced down in a very inefficient production area, thus making the 
PV applications less environmentally friendly. If that is the case in Maniitsoq, an opti-
mum amount of produced solar energy may be found, both an optimum in terms of en-
ergy economy and environmentally friendliness. The issue arises because Greenland as 
well as Denmark is drastically moving the energy production from the production side 
to the load side. The increased amount of private PV applications is distinctly more pro-
nounced in Greenland because of the high electricity prices, and the fact that PV appli-
cations gain approx. 0.3 % efficiency to its baseline efficiency, per degree Celsius under 
baseline temperature, appendix B, Kinve. As it is rather cold in Greenland, as opposed 
to the 20◦ test temperature, the PV applications are very efficient. An issue with the low 
Greenlandic temperature is that snow will pile up on the modules preventing any pro-
duction. A hypothesis (Marion et al,. 2013) is that the snow will slide off the modules 
when the angle is steep enough, this will be analyzed in this project using an already 
made model from Truckee which calculates the production-loss, of PV installations, due 
to snow. The model is used to calculate the ―snow loss‖ in Maniitsoq. 
 
 
 
1.1 
Background 
 
There is no united utility grid in Greenland, thus all the cities and smaller settlements 
have to have a utility grid and power plant on their own. Some of the cities have hydro-
==PAGE:16==
 
Introduction 
14 
power plants while still others use old diesel generators. The energy system in 
Maniitsoq is primarily based on fossil fuel, but Maniitsoq has seen an increasingly 
greater number of private PV investments. Not addressing this issue, at some point the 
diesel engine will have trouble adjusting to the relative fast power fluctuations which 
can be caused by solar power; this will be reflected in the frequency and the power qual-
ity, and in worst scenarios cause brownouts or blackouts. Furthermore the efficiency of 
the diesel generator will be lowered as more sun production will push the diesel genera-
tor towards the lower edge of rated power. It needs to be assessed if there is already a 
problem with the amount of solar power in the system today in order to find a solution 
in time to accommodate even more solar power.  
Maniitsoq is south of the Arctic Circle, but is still considered to be in the arctic region 
climate wise. When building PV setups in the arctic, one has to be concerned with snow 
accumulation on the modules as a part of the factors influencing the optimal installation. 
Another concern is the snow piling up under the lower end of the array, thus hindering 
the snow shedding. This thesis takes use of an already made model from Truckee which 
calculates the production-loss, of PV installations, due to snow. The model is used to 
calculate the ―snow loss‖ in Maniitsoq. 
 
1.2 
Formulation of the problem 
 
Finding the right tilt angle for an arctic PV setup is a complex problem. One has to con-
sider if the angle should be optimized in terms of annual production, or optimized in 
terms of achieving as many production hours as possible during a year. This thesis is 
concentrated on finding an optimum angle in terms of optimizing annual production. 
There is a lot of software such as PVsyst that calculates the annual production for a pro-
posed setup with any given tilt angle at locations all over the world, but those calcula-
tions do not include a snow model. One would expect that the optimal tilt angle is high-
er when snow is introduced, thus making the snow more likely to shed (Marion et al,. 
2013), but there is a trade-off between having optimization for insolation and snow 
shedding, and finding the combined optimum is dependent on a lot of different condi-
tions. 
 
• What effect, does the solar power production have on the efficiency of the diesel pow-
er plant in Maniitsoq? 
 
• How is the angle correlated to the snow loss, and can this be modelled to optimize the 
angle in terms of minimum snow loss? 
 
• Is it possible to get full production during the winter, and how large must the tilt angle 
be to achieve that?

1.7 
Method, scope and conditions 
 
This section presents the Truckee model, and show how it is calibrated and what data is 
used in the calibration. The data handling in general is also described here. 
 
1.7.1 
Applying the Truckee model 
 
The Truckee Model is from ―Photovoltaics and Snow: An update from two winters of 
measurements in the Sierra‖ (Tim Townsend and Loren Powers). The model is made 
analytically to calculate the annual production-loss, of a PV installation, due to snow.

1.7.4 
Finding Color 
 
The Raspberry Pi setups have taken over 40000 photos, which are tough to go through 
manually, so two methods for finding POI (Points of Interest), has been developed. The 
points of interest are when the insolation on the solar panels changes from 0 % to 100 % 
or from 100 % to 0 % in few minutes. The first method is making time lapses out of the 
photos, thus making them easier to go through. With a frame rate of 24photos/second 
there is about 30 min of time lapse to analyze. The second method is determining the 
brightness of a wall besides the PV installation, to give a way of assessing the 
Maniitsoq insolation with clouds included in the assessment. The reason the wall was 
chosen as an indicator of insolation, is that the panels themselves are reflective, and 
therefore reflects white clouds thus giving a false indication of the actual insolation. 
Figure 7 shows the reflecting panels at the right side of the school roof. The ―mean pho-
to brightness‖ is also influenced by clouds that make the overall photo brighter when the 
panels are receiving less irradiation as seen in Figure 7. When looking at Figure 7 and 
Figure 8, it‘s easy to see that the yellow wall has a very wide range of brightness be-
tween the two photos, and is why it is the best indicator for insolation on the panels. The 
problem with the wall is that is has another angle towards the sun, and is not located 
directly beside the panels, thus it is expected to see a slight offset between the bright-
ness and the measured power output at that time. The offset and correlation between 
power and brightness can be seen in results, Figure 17 Production Comparison. 
 
A third way of assessing the insolation is looking at the different temperatures of the 
two setups in Maniitsoq. One of the setups is bolted to an electricity pole and is thus

DISCUSSION 
 
4.1 
Problems with power ramps 
 
Relative steep ramp ups or ramp downs can cause severe problems in the utility grid. 
The steepest ramp can occur under snow shedding, in which case the ramp up will hap-
pen in a few seconds, but in case of snow shedding, the steep ramp up is likely to be less 
of a problem in Maniitsoq because Maniitsoq has no large-scale PV-installations, and 
snow shedding is a local phenomenon that occurs on a single array at a time. Another 
problem is cloud induced power ramps, which is more likely to cause problems in 
Maniitsoq, because the PV installations are that close to each other in a compact town. 
A passing cloud could cast shadow on the whole town at once, and pass by within 
minutes, inducing a common power ramp for all the PV installations. Although calcula-
tions around Figure 39 Drifting Fog in Maniitsoq, shows only 5.5% power drop, this is 
something to be considered when installing more PV. A way to address the problem 
could be to set a power ramp restriction for bigger installations of e.g. 1/10 of peak 
power per minute. This can be achieved with battery storage. 
 
4.2 
Truckee Model Limitations 
A problem occurred when applying the Truckee model to Maniitsoq. The problem is the 
Truckee Model having negative loss output, which happens when Git assumes negative 
numbers in the nominator in Equation 1. This problem is traced back to γ1 being a too 
large negative number in Equation 2. The number γ1 gets negative in Equation 3, when 
the monthly rolling average snowfall SE1 exceeds the height from the array edge to 
ground H, which in Maniitsoq is a short height because the array is roof mounted. The 
original Truckee setup was not likely to encounter that problem as frequently, as the 
Truckee array edge was a bit higher from the ground.  
The Truckee model is made to give a loss output of 100 % as soon as the snow can‘t 
shed. This effect is modeled in the denominator in Equation 3. (T, Townsend) states that 
the model is designed for rooftop mounted installations, as the lower edge to ground 
height is similar to rooftop mounted installations.  
==PAGE:67==
 
 
65 
I find that the ground interference part of the model is too aggressive when used on in-
stallations which is mounted directly or few cm off the roof. If the model is applied to 
the installation like the KNI house in Sisimiut the loss would be 100 % which is incor-
rect as can be seen on Figure 30 lost vs potential production Sisimiut. This is because 
the Truckee model does not include a melting effect, but only shedding, and does not 
take into account that an angled roof also sheds.  
The temperature included in the model is a monthly average temperature, which is like-
ly to be negative because of cold nights, but in the daytime it might be positive and 
cause some melting and shedding on the arrays, but only one average temperature is 
included in the model and that is a shame. 
 
4.3 
Sisimiut Insolation 
The Sisimiut calculations are made with DNI insolation, thus the KNI house and Stef-
fan‘s house is assumed to receive the same amount of insolation per square meter. This 
does bias the results in Steffan‘s favor as his house would likely receive less POA inso-
lation. If this was done more accurately I expect that the results would have been slight-
ly more to the KNI house‘s favor, though I believe the results would still prove that 
there is little advantage of having production in winter months as the winter insolation is 
very scant north of the Arctic Circle.  
 
4.4 
Regrets 
The Elspec did not prove as useful as hoped. Because the Elspec data could not be ex-
ported to a CSV file, thus no Fourier analysis was made. Also because the Elspec was to 
be brought back to Denmark again, the measurement period is very short, making it 
difficult to spot trends on the long scale. 
 
The Raspberry Pi setups in Maniitsoq were installed late in April, thus taking measure-
ments the whole summer. If the Raspberry Pi setups were to be installed in the winter 
instead, a lot more relevant data would have been captured.  
 
The insolation data assumes the PV installations are facing south, which is the optimal 
azimuth for the northern hemisphere. None of the PV installations is directly south 
however, thus introducing a small error in the POA insolation. 
 
The final model from Truckee was computed in Matlab, but is not in any means user 
friendly. I hoped for it to have a guided user interface where the user would be prompt-
ed for key factors, and the model would give recommendations based on that. The mod-
el would have to incorporate POA insolation for any angle and azimuth, and have syn-
thetically snowfall years to base its calculations on.  
==PAGE:68==
 
 
66 
 
4.5 
Kilaaseeraq School Setup 
 
The Kilaaseeraq School PV setups surprised many in Maniitsoq, when the accumulated 
production in the summer showed that 56◦ degree was the least effective angle. And the 
36◦ angle the most effective. The Kilaaseeraq school setups was meant to find what an-
gle is best in the harsh weather conditions in Maniitsoq, but as the ground interference 
was not considered, and all of the setups endured heavy ground interference, the snow 
shedding had almost no relation to the tilt angle. Thus the setup which had the best an-
gle in terms of POA insolation in the summer months would prove itself as the optimal 
angle. The optimal tilt angle for summer production in Maniitsoq is 41◦. As the 36◦ set-
up is the closest one to 41◦ of the three differently angled setups, it is expected to have 
the highest accumulated production. Figure 15 Accumulated Production, and Figure 16 
Real Montly Production, shows that the 56◦ setup is behind the 16◦ setup in accumulated 
production, because the 16◦ setup has the highest summer production. According to Fig-
ure 9 POA Insolation, the 16 degree setup should have the lowest production in the 
summer months, so this is indeed unexpected. An explanation could be that in the POA 
insolation, it is assumed that the Kilaaseeraq School setups have an azimuth of 180◦ 
while in reality they have an azimuth of 140◦. The assumption does bias the 56◦ setup 
with a small but very dissatisfying error.   
 
4.6 
Normalizing the Plane Of Array Insolation 
 
In order to calibrate C1 in the Truckee model, the expected production was to be calcu-
lated via POA insolation. The POA insolation was multiplied by 14% as the expected 
efficiency and multiplied by the array area of 45.46m2. This calculation should in theory 
equal the real production of the Kilaaseeraq School PV installation, but it did not. This 
was no surprise, because clouds were not taken into account in the calculation. The 
problem was solved by normalizing the POA insolation to the summer production. The 
normalization could have been done without the 14% efficiency and 45.46m2, but it has 
been left in the calculation to help with understanding.

5.2 Conclusion 
 
The power production from the many PV-installations was expected to have some nega-
tive effect on the Maniitsoq CHP plant‘s efficiency. The impact on the Maniitsoq CHP 
plant‘s power production cannot be pointed out with any certainty and the CHP plant 
efficiency is around 40% for the electricity production, see Table 6 Maniitsoq CHP 
plant , so this is clearly not the case at this point. 
 
 
The Truckee Model has been successfully applied to the Maniitsoq PV installations and 
has given very realistic results. The model takes a variety of factors into account one of 
which is the angle - that is just one of the two factors which can be adjusted during in-
stallation, the other being the ground interference factor. The snow loss does vary with 
the angle so that a higher angle means less loss due to snow, but the angle is far less 
important than the ground interference. 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
68 
 
 
In terms of annual production it is not recommended to optimize for maximum winter 
production, but according to the Truckee model, it is possible to have almost full pro-
duction in winter time if there is no ground interference and tilt angle at 90◦. If heavy 
ground interference is present though, the tilt angle has little to no impact on the snow 
loss according to the Truckee model. 
 
This project aimed at finding an optimal tilt angle for Maniitsoq. This, however, has not 
been calculated as a result, because it also depends on the ground interference. On Fig-
ure 9 POA Insolation, it can be seen that the 56◦ setup has the largest annual POA inso-
lation; however the higher insolation is located in the spring and autumn where there is 
a potential snow loss. The 36◦ setup receives the highest POA insolation in the summer 
months but receives a bit lower annual POA insolation due to slightly less POA insola-
tion during winter, spring and autumn. Thus the ground interference is the crucial factor 
that determines what angle is optimal in terms of annual production. If ground interfer-
ence is present, the 36◦ setup will produce more due to its high production in summer 
time. If ground interference is not present, the 56◦ setup will produce more, due to its 
higher production in spring and autumn. The PV setup on the Kilaaseeraq School roof is 
an example of ground interference making the snow loss almost independent of tilt an-
gles. 
 
There are a large number of factors contributing to the snow loss in one way or another, 
but there are two factors that one can adjust to optimize the setup –the height from the 
array edge to ground or to roof, and the array tilt. The most important of these factors 
being the height. The recommendation when installing new PV installations is to ensure 
that snow will not pile up under the array edge. This can easily be achieved on roof 
mounted installations by placing the array edge on the roof edge. On ground mounted 
installations, and roof mounted installations with more than one array, it is recommend-
ed to build the racks high enough so that the snow will not pile up and interfere with the 
shedding. An important factor which is not included in the Truckee model is the separa-
tions between the arrays on directly roof mounted installations. The KNI house is a 
good example of a high angled setup with a separation between the arrays. The snow 
will anchor on all chinks and hinder shedding; therefore it is recommended to keep the 
surface as smooth as possible. 
 
Cases of installations in Maniitsoq: 
If the ground interference has not been dealt with, one should expect heavy snow loss in 
the winter, and thus the angle should be optimised for summer production. This means 
an angle around 41◦ see Figure 44 Optimization thumb rule.

dark that the difference in annual production, based on DNI, between the low angled 
installation, on Steffans‘s house, with heavy ground interference and a high angled in-
stallation, on the KNI house, with almost no ground interference is only 9.8%. The op-
timal angle for a Sisimiut PV installation without ground interference is 53◦ while one

There is a slight correlation between inverter power and grid voltage in the Elspec data,

5.3 
Perspectives 
 
The results from this project are the advice on how to install PV in Greenland. The ra-
ther simplified advice is based on latitude which makes it applicable to all of Greenland. 
When mounting the modules facing another way than south, the optimal angle might be 
lower than stated in this project, but in terms of annual production, north faced modules 
are not recommended. 
 
5.4 
Future work

see the Kilaaseeraq setup production for 2017 to compare it to the Truckee model. In 
small settlements without a diesel generator, it might be preferable to maximize winter 
production, instead of annual production, to keep a steady power all year, without a 
large battery bank. Another interesting project would be to model Maniitsoq in Power

700787;

realistiv value when runing 
% the code. because the code starts in janu-

Loss12 = 0; 
  
for n = 1:12 
    SE = (1/2)*S(month)*(1+1/month); 
    SE1 = SE +(1/3)*SE2; 
    gamma = R*SE1*cos(tilt)*(2*tan(P))/(H^2-
SE1^2); 
    Git = 1-c2*exp(-gamma); 
    Loss = 
7.39*10^7*SE1*cos(tilt)^2*Git*RH(month)/(Tair(

    % the code in the for loop is the main al-

    if Loss < 0     % the if else keeps the

        Loss = 100;

        Loss = 100; 
    end

    LossV(month) = Loss; % updating the final 
loss vector 
    SE1V(month) = SE1; 
    month = month+1; 
    SE2 = SE1; 
    Loss12 = Loss12+Loss; % Loss 12 is not

Requirements Specifications 
 
 
- 
The Raspberry Pi unit should be able to collect data and store it locally. 
- 
The programming should be robust and fault tolerant thus no technical service is 
needed 
-

All the code should be run from RAM thus the coded schedules will happen at 
boot, after a power outage.  
- 
The RPi should be built into a junction box, to protect it against the harsh Green-
landic climate. 
- 
The RPi should be sealed, as to prevent moisture from fogging the lense.

5.15.8 
Setting up the Digital Humidity and Temperature Sensor 
DHT  
 
―The DHT sensor from Adafruit is an open source project mainly for experimentation 
and hacking, and is therefore not guaranteed to work‖  
(DHT Humidity Sensing on Raspberry Pi or Beaglebone Black with GDocs Logging) 
 
 
 
==PAGE:118==
 
 
116 
How to connect: 
 
Figure 75 DHT22 Connection 
 
The DHT sensor was chosen among others because it was very cheap and already com-
patible with the RPi. The cheap sensor comes with a price, there is no dedicated hard-
ware to perform the demodulation and setting and sampling the pins. Instead the DHT 
sensor utilizes Manchester coding and is bit banging the serial communication. The 
Manchester coding uses a higher bandwidth compared to other USART (Universal Syn-
chronous/Asynchronous Receiver Transmitter) like the I2C utilized in the RTC module 
also added to the RPi. The I2C communication is a master/slave method that uses 1 data 
wire and 1 clock wire, compared to the Manchester communication which uses only 1 
data wire also including the clock signal, thereby saving a pin. The very fast data poll-
ing which is needed for the DHT can only be carried out by a C library, but the RPi has 
to dedicate a lot of processor power to this task. If the RPi is running other task along-
side with the DHT, e.g. the camera, it can cause jitter and glitches in the signal, which 
can also be observed by looking at the Adafruit test code in Figure 78. Therefore the 
DHT will be called from the camera.sh shell script after the raspistill function has fin-
ished executing, thereby ensuring processor power for the DHT. 
 
 
Edit the camera.sh shell script to include the DHT2302 (digital humidity and tempera-
ture sensor) as seen in Figure 76 Camera.sh
