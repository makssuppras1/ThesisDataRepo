Abstract
The appearance of medical imaging as a diagnostic tool has revolutionized medicine.
Suddenly, it allowed non-invasive, fast and accurate imaging of the inside of the body.
Medical imaging techniques are a versatile way of health conditions diagnostics, as-
sessment of disease development and the eﬀectiveness of treatment. Among many
methods, x-ray computed tomography (CT) is widely used, allowing non-invasive,
real-time three-dimensional imaging of body structures. It is used in the exami-
nations of head, abdomen, angiography, as well as is extremely helpful in tumor
diagnostics. However, to obtain maximum eﬃciency imaging, it is frequently re-
quired to use a contrast agents.
These types of agents are designed to enhance
contrast between diﬀerent tissues, consequently improving the resolution and qual-
ity of visualization. The principle of their activity is the presence of high molecular
number (Z) atoms, such as iodine, barium, gold or lanthanides. The most popular
contrast media are those based on iodine, because they are relatively inert and non-
toxic, although their major disadvantage is the short circulation in the system and
rapid clearance. This may result in degradation of image quality as well as is highly
unfavourable for long-term examinations.
The project was aiming to develop novel contrast agent based on iodine, which
would provide a signiﬁcant improvement of imaging quality, show the extended cir-
culatory times while maintaining bio-compatibility. Contrast media present in the
market are mainly based on microemulsions of iodinated oils (for example, Lipi-
odol). In the following project, the approach as been modiﬁed and apply the solid
lipid nanoparticles (SLNs) dispersons instead of liquid oil emulsions. Nowadays, it
is extensively studied suspension type, particularly in drug delivery systems. SLNs
are characterized by a prolonged circulation times, hence the idea for the formula-
tion of dispersion of solid iodinated phenol-fatty acid ethers stabilized by medically
approved surfactants.
The study involved synthesis and initial formulation of SLNs dispersions, in or-
der to select the most promising starting materials. Out of three synthesized lipids,
2,4,6-tiiodophenol-hexadecyl ether was chosen and subjected to series of experi-
ments designed to determine the optimal homogenization and formulations condi-
tions. Eventually, two SLNs preparations stabilized with TWEEN R
⃝80 and BRIJ R
⃝
O20 have been completed with success and thoroughly characterized. Their stabil-
ity under both physiological conditions and upon storage has been assessed showing
excellent results. Moreover, preparations were analyzed for iodine load, structure
and thermodynamics. Results from the experiments are promising and further in-
vestigation of the idea of iodinated SLNs might lead to creation of novel contrast
agent for x-ray CT.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction and motivation
Medical imaging’s history begun in 1895, when Wilhelm Roentgen ﬁrst discovered
X-rays. Short after, radiography was developed which opened new way of diagnos-
ing [1]. Together with other medical imaging techniques, such as ultrasonography,
magnetic resonance tomography and computed tomography it allows complete ex-
amination of patients in non-invasive manner. Aforementioned methods are versatile
tools for diagnosing and monitoring diseases as well as planning and assessing imple-
mented therapy. There are over 5 bilion imaging examinations conducted reported
worldwide [2]. To increase imaging robustness, contrast agents are frequently em-
ployed which enhances contrast of body tissues and ﬂuids resulting in higher quality
images and better diagnostics. Although there are number of such agents available
on the market, they all suﬀer drawbacks such as large injection volumes and rapid
clearance. This Master’s project was aiming to create a novel, more eﬃcient contrast
agent based on those available on the market.
1.2
Existing background
Even though appearance of medical imaging techniques initially improved ﬁeld of
diagnostics of internal conditions, it was soon realized that it often gives poor qual-
ity images with hard to distinguish structures. In order to face this issues, contrast
agents for improving visualization of body were developed. First contrast agents for
radio-imaging developed at the beginning of 20th century were based on heavy metal
salts and have been exhibiting signiﬁcant toxicity. Nowadays there are numbers of
non-toxic and well tolerated contrast media present on the market [3]. All those
agents vastly enhance contrast in x-ray images owing to presence of high atomic
number atoms with great attenuating properties.
The most popular contrast media since 1920s are based on iodine, since they are
safe to use, inert and cost eﬀective [4]. However, small-molecule based agents suﬀer
disadvantage of short circulatory time and potential adverse eﬀects.
Contempo-
rary approaches focus on development of nano-sized contrast agents [5]. Among
recently investigated iodine containing formulations, most promising ones regarding
their contrasting properties are nanoemulsions, liposomes, polymers, dendrimers and

metal-organic frameworks. Other approaches use metal based contrast media, such
as gold nanoparticles or chelated lanthanides.
Several studies have been exploring iodine-containing nanoemulsions consisting of
liquid lipid stabilized in aqueous medium with surfactants [5]. However, to date no
research among solid lipid nanoparticle based contrast agents has been conducted,
which potentially leaves a wide scope.
1.3
Scope of the project
When it comes to diagnostics, medical imaging is the most powerful tool for non-
invasive internal body examinations. X-ray computed tomography (CT) is widely
used, since it provides three-dimensional images of body’s insides. However, proper
CT scan requires use of contrast agents for enhancement of tissue contrast. Require-
ment for contrast media is that they contain high atomic number atoms, such as
iodine, gold or gadolinium. Iodinated contrast agents are the most frequently ap-
plied, since they exhibit low toxicity, high biocompatibility and show little adverse
eﬀects [4]. Howbeit, the major disadvantage of such media is rapid clearance from
the body, which leads to numbers of issues. It can cause poor resolution of images
and impedes real-time screening of disease development or implemented treatment.
The aim of the project was to obtain nanoparticulate iodine containing contrast
agent, that would possess good contrasting properties due to high iodine load, long
circulatory times and low toxicity.
In this project, solid lipid nanoparticles (SLNs) of iodinated phenol-fatty alcohol
ether aqueous dispersion stabilized by surfactants was developed as contrast media.
Project was divided into several stages:
• Synthesis of 2,4,6-triiodophenol-fatty alcohol ethers
• Formulation and optimization of SLNs dispersions using various surfactants
and homogenization methods
• Analysis and characterization of SLNs preparations
• In-vivo CT scan as conﬁrmation of development of eﬀective CT contrast agent
Fulﬁlment of aforementioned goals would lead to achieve novel computed tomogra-
phy contrast agent, competitive to those present on the market.

Chapter 2
Theoretical background
2.1
Medical imaging
Medical imaging is a general term that refers to various technologies used for visu-
alizing insides of the human body without surgical intervention. It is powerful tool
used in health conditions diagnostics, monitoring as well as treatment [1]. Depend-
ing on the technique applied, diﬀerent type of information can be obtained.
History of medical imaging has started with discovery of x-Ray radiation by Roent-
gen in 1895 [6]. Its multiple applications has been rapidly investigated and within a
few years, ﬁrst modalities of this type were built and started to be commonly used
in hospitals. It allowed obtaining information about tissue speciﬁcity and planar
images of insides of the body [7]. At the beginning, equipment had poor sensitivity
and was rather harmful for patients as well operators due to high radiation doses
produced during examination.
However, constant improvements resulted in safe
and robust imaging technique [8]. For next half a century it was the only imaging
method, until invention of sonar arrived. In 1962, ﬁrst ultrasound imaging equip-
ment was applied for medical examinations [6]. Ten years later, two methods that
allowed obtaining cross-sectional pictures of the body were developed: magnetic
resonance imaging (MRI) and computed tomography (CT) x-ray. Further develop-
ments resulted in positron emission tomography (PET) and single photon emission
tomography (SPECT) techniques. Nowadays, modalities are often combined, in-
creasing sensitivity and giving high resolution images [6].
Diﬀerent technologies are suitable for diﬀerent types of examinations [9]. For in-
stance, conventional planar x-ray is used for imaging bones, blood vessels and lungs,
but also swallowed objects; ultrasound serves to visualize internal organs, joints and
pregnancy; MRI can show diﬀerences between healthy and diseased tissue within
soft tissues.
CT/PET/SPECT are useful for real-time imaging of blood vessels,
tumors, heart conditions etc [6].
2.2
X-Ray Computed Tomography Imaging
X-Ray computed tomography (CT), also called computer-aided tomography or computed-
axial tomography (CAT) is a three-dimensional imaging method [10]. Planar x-ray
examination results in 2D pictures, in which diﬀerent tissues are superimposed and

overlapping, which might give poor visibility of object of interest and limits its ap-
plications[11]. Unlike conventional x-ray, CT is creating cross-sectional images of
nearly all parts of the body in high detail. Godfrey Hounsﬁeld in late 1960s realized
Figure 2.1: Computed tomography of human brain. Reproduced from [12]
the limitations of conventional x-ray and developed technique which would allow
obtaining 3D instead of 2D image using the same type of radiation[10]. He assumed
that measuring x-ray transmission while scanning the subject across a slice under
diﬀerent angles might result in attenuation of the signal suﬃcient to diﬀerentiate
between tissues.
Operating principle of CT scanner is the same as for standard x-Ray [1]. The most
crucial parts of apparatus is an x-ray tube and scintillation detector. The x-ray tube
is a vacuum cylinder with cathode and anode placed inside. Electrons emission from
cathode is induced thermally via its heating by ﬁlament (thermionic emission) and
electrons are accelerated in electrostatic ﬁeld between electrodes. Anode is typically
made of metal with high heat conductivity such as copper, with a target spot made
of rhodium or tungsten. It is installed at an angle, so x-ray radiation is emitted per-
pendicular to electron beam. When electrons from the cathode reach anode, they
collide with metal atoms. Such phenomena causes deceleration of electrons called
‘bremsstahlung’. Collisions with atom’s nucleus are rare and most of electrons are
deﬂected in nucleus’ ﬁeld or collide with inner shells and partially lose their energy.
The energy loss is further emitted from anode in form of x-ray photon. Due to
randomness of collisions and diﬀerences of emitted photons’ energies, the x-ray ra-
diation emitted has a continuous energy spectrum instead of one particular energy.

X-ray photons are further radiated on examined object (patient) and when they
hit the matter, they interact with it mainly via:
• Rayleigh scattering, which causes photons lose small part of energy through
elastic collision.
• Compton scattering or deﬂection of the x-ray.
• Photoelectric eﬀect or absorption, which is the most crucial phenomena when it
comes to medical imaging. In this interaction type, x-ray photon’s energy while
colliding with an atom is fully utilized by ejection of K-shell electrons causing
ionization.
When vacancy gets ﬁlled, characteristic radiation is generated.
The rate of the absorption is dependent on matter’s density.
Aforementioned processes contribute to phenomena called attenuation of x-rays.
Since variations in matter’s composition causes diﬀerent absorption of photons, it
is possible to diﬀerentiate between tissue types in the body based on contrast [1].
Those tissues, which are dense are attenuating photons more and resulting in white
areas on image (i.e. bones), whereas more permeable attenuate weaker and appear
darker. As mentioned before, standard x-ray image can be blurry due to diﬀerent

2.3
Contrast Agents for X-Ray Computed Tomog-
raphy
Multiple tissues are visualized using x-Ray CT and it is based on their x-rays at-
tenuation ability [4]. This is measured in Hounsﬁeld Units (HU) and is based on
attenuation coeﬃcient of the matter relative to water. Arbitrary assumptions are
that water has CT number of 0 HU whereas air is -1000 HU. The bones, which are
very dense, exhibit value of 1000 HU [14]. Majority of soft tissues have attenuation
value of 40-80 HU, therefore it can raise a challenge in imaging two adjacent tissues
of similar CT number, for example organ and tumor. Thus, it is required increase
the diﬀerence in x-rays attenuation to ensure qualitative visualization. This is done
by applying contrast agents that enhance sensitivity of CT examination [5]. The
principle of contrast agent is based on presence of an atom which attenuating prop-
erties increase with their atomic number (Z) and binding energy of K-shell electron
(K-edge value). Therefore high atomic number atoms, such as iodine or metal atoms
are used.
There are various contrast agents, both on the market and under development,
which diﬀer in properties and applications. In principle, such an agent needs to
fulﬁll certain requirements to be applied clinically [4]:
• It should increase x-rays attenuation two times between neighboring tissues
for improved imaging
• Agent should be rich in element responsible for attenuation (high atom per
mole content)
• Circulatory time after intake should be long enough for CT scan completion,
however the contrast agent should not accumulate in the body and ought to
be excreted within reasonable time-frame
• Both agent and it’s metabolites should be non-toxic
• Contrast agent should be bio compatible and stable under physiological con-
ditions
Due to their internal application, it is also required that contrast media formulation
consist of small particles, typically within micron and sub-micron size. Here most
common contrast agents are brieﬂy presented.

2.3.1
Iodine-based Contrast Agents
Iodine has high atomic number (Z=53) and was historically the ﬁrst atom chosen
for x-Ray CT contrast agents and still remains widely used [4]. Initially, lithium
and sodium iodides were used for CT imaging, however due to their high toxicity
they had to be replaced. Group of 1,3,5-triiodobenzene derivatives was developed,
with amine and carboxylic acid groups attached to make them more biocompatible
and water-soluble [5]. Two categories of such iodine-base agents can be separated-
ionic and non-ionic. Whereas ionic compounds have high water-solubility, they also
possess high osmolality that might cause several adverse eﬀect [16]. Moreover, due to
their ionic nature, they are more likely to interact electrostatically with biological
molecules, such as peptides. To address this issues, group of non-ionic oil-based
iodinated contrast media has been developed, among which ethiodized poppyseed
oil (Lipiodol) is the most popular.
Such non-ionic agents have lower osmolality
and toxicity, however they need additional groups attached to increase their water-
solubility.
Among main concerns with iodine-based contrast agents is fast renal excretion,
allowing only short time examination after injection, and nonspeciﬁc distribution,
which might result in poor quality CT images [5]. Development of sub-micron size

agents, such as micelles, liposomes and polymers is addressing to aforementioned
problems.
2.3.2
Gold-based Contrast Agents
Atomic number of gold (Z=79) together with its better x-ray attenuating properties
than iodine, make it better base for CT contrast agents [5]. Gold atom exhibits bet-
ter attenuation for high energy x-rays which is also independent on the environment
(whether present in water or air). Gold nanoparticles (AuNPs) are investigated as
contrast agents, since they are known for good biotolerability and non-toxicity[4].
Moreover, due to their small size (2 nm) they possess long blood circulation times
and do not accumulate in mononuclear phagocyte system organs, such as spleen or
liver [5]. Intravenous injection of AuNPs resulted in obtaining high-quality images
of such minute biological structures as blood vessels like capillaries.
Facility of synthesis as well as possible surface modiﬁcations allow to tailor AuNPS’
properties. This might include targeting contrast agents, which can be achieved by
introduction of speciﬁc receptor or tissue targeting moieties on the AuNPs. As an
example, conjugation of gold nanoparticles with heparin polysaccharides resulted
in liver speciﬁc contrast agent. What is more, AuNPs can be used in multimodal-
ity contrast agents [17]. By conjugation with gadolinium chelates they can serve
as agents for simultaneous CT and MRI imaging, allowing greater visualization
quality in both used techniques.
Another utility is combining CT imaging with
drug-delivery- this was successfully conducted by loading AuNPs, conjugated by
prostate-cancer speciﬁc moiety, with doxorubicin [18].
Gold nanoparticles are potentially excellent candidates for biocompatible, non-toxic
and target-speciﬁc contrast agents, however gold’s high cost seems to be the most
limiting factor for further development of such for clinical applications.
2.3.3
Lanthanide-based Contrast Agents
Lanthanide atoms have high atomic numbers- gadolinium Z=64, dysprosium Z=66
and ytterbium Z=70. As free ions, lanthanides exhibit very high toxicity, possibly
due to their radius similar to Ca2+, which makes them competitive in biological sys-
tems [4]. Nevertheless, they form non-toxic and stable complexes with polyaminocar-
boxylic acids and are widely applied for MRI imaging [19]. As x-Ray CT contrast
agents, Gd- and Yb-chelates are attenuating x-rays better than iodinated agents,
but their circulation times are just as short, since they are excreted through kidneys
[5]. Rapid clearance combined with high cost “per dose”, makes lanthanide-based
contrast agents not being investigated as replacement of iodine-based, but rather as
alternative for patients with contraindications to iodinated agents [19].
2.3.4

Bismuth is pointed as cost eﬀective alternative for AuNPs contrast agents. Bi atom
has high atomic number (Z=83), good x-ray attenuating properties and exhibits
low toxicity. It is too reactive in metallic form for in vivo use, therefore bismuth
sulﬁde (Bi2S3) coated with polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) were developed as a CT

2.3.5
Xenon Gas as Contrast Agent
Xenon gas can be also applied as contrast agent. It has relatively high atomic number
(Z=54) and attenuating properties similar to iodine [23]. Due to its inert nature,
it is highly biocompatible and non-allergenic. It can diﬀuse into blood from tissues
and cross blood-brain carrier, which makes it great contrast agent for cerebral blood
ﬂow imaging. Moreover, Xe is applied in lung examinations regarding perfusion
and regional ventilation. Another application is liver and pancreas diagnostics since
certain conditions exhibit changes in blood ﬂow in these organs. However, xenon is
anesthetic, therefore it cannot be used in patients with respiratory problems.
2.4
Iodinated contrast agents
Historically, iodine is the ﬁrst element to be applied for contrast agents and still
remains the most widely used [14]. It is due to its relatively high attenuation proper-
ties, inertness and facile chemistry behind iodine incorporation into molecules. Gen-
erally, iodinated agents can be divided into extracellular/unspeciﬁc, mainly small
molecule agents, and targeted/speciﬁc.

2.4.1
Small molecule iodine-based contrast agents
As mentioned before, they can be either ionic or nonionic. Whereas ionic are not
used anymore, nonionic agents exhibit a few favorable properties, such as high hy-
drophilicity, osmolality within acceptable window (<1500 mOsmkg-1) and low tox-
icity considering high dosages used. They are very stable and are excreted from the
body unmetabolized. Iodinated imaging agents’ pharmacokinetics proﬁle consists
of two phases: distribution (3-10 min) and elimination (1-2h) [14]. Fast excretion
through kidneys is one of major disadvantages, that prevents small molecule con-
trast agents from being helpful in real-time monitoring of conditions or treatment
progress [4]. Moreover, non-targeted nature of those contrast media is a limiting
factor for their application. Therefore, area of iodine-containing nanoformulations
is being widely investigated.

2.4.2
Micellar nanoemulsions
Amphiphilic surfactants in aqueous media, when above critical micelle concentration
(CMC), spontaneously form micelles. These are spherical species with hydrophilic
heads in contact with solvent and hydrophobic tails in the centre, which can entrap a
lipophillic iodinated compound inside [5]. Such micellar nanoemulsions allow immis-
cible liquid media (e.g. water and oil) to form a stable dispersion [4]. Emulsions of
ethiodized oil (known as Lipiodol/Ethiodol) stabilized with surfactant is one of the
ﬁrst and still clinically applied formulations for CT imaging of e.g, liver and spleen.
Moreover, several studies are carried out to combine imaging and drug delvery by
simultaneous incorporation of Lipiodol and hydrophobic drugs into micelles. An-
other example of successful nanoemulsion development is 1,3-Bis-[7-(3-amino-2,4,6-
tiiodophenyl)-heptanoyl]-2-oleoyl glycerol (DHOG), iodinated triglyceride known as
Fenestra LC. Since its structure mimics certain liver transporters, they can be ap-
plied for liver imaging [5].
2.4.3
Liposomal contrast agents
Similar to micelles are another self-assembled spherical vesicles called liposomes [5].
However, unlike micelles, they are build up of lipid bilayer, thus they can carry

lipid bilayer. Their properties can be easily tailored, due to variety of phospholipids
that can be used for their preparation as well as facility of surface modiﬁcations (e.g.
PEGylation, antibody anchoring etc.). Initially, liposomes containing iodinated con-
trast agents in the core had extremely short circulatory time, therefore PEGylated
liposomes (“stealth liposomes”) have been developed, which are circulating in blood
signiﬁcantly longer [4]. Alternatively, liposomes with contrast agent incorporated
in lipid bilayer have been investigated. Advantage of such is empty core, in which

2.4.4
Polymeric contrast agents
Another approach to tailored contrast media is synthesis of iodinated polymeric
nanoparticles [4]. They are consisting of iodinated phenols bound covalently with
amines or hydroxyl groups of polymers. Such formulation is extremely stable, es-
pecially compared with aforementioned liposomes or micelles (which may “leak”)
[5]. Synthesis of polymers can be easily controlled regarding size and additional
functionality groups incorporation for desired properties. Several studies over dif-
ferent polymeric structures are conducted, however there are no iodinated polymeric
contrast agents yet medically applied.
2.4.5
Dendrimeric contrast agents
Dendrimers consists of branches build with repeated units surrounding inner core
linker. It is symmetric and therefore has ordered structure and excellent monodis-
persity [5]. Number of units in branches is referred to as generation. In case of
contrast media, 4th generation dendrimers are usually studied. Application of poly-
l-lysine branches with PEG core, conjugated with iodinated benzene rings have
shown promising results with good attenuating properties of the contrast agent,

Iodinated Metal-Organic Frameworks
Relatively new and therefore uninvestigated type of contrast agents are Metal-
Organic Frameworks (MOFs) [5]. These species are metal-based coordination poly-
mers which consist of polydentate ligands and typically Zn2+ or Cu2+ as bridging
ions [26]. Study over MOF build from 2,3,5,6-tetraiodo-1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid

trast agents and its biodegradability. However, no data regarding pharmacokinetics
or toxicity is yet available.
2.5
Solid Lipid Nanoparticles
Since 1990s solid lipid nanoparticles (SLNs) have been receiving growing interest as
an alternative to other micro- and nanoparticulate formulations such as emulsions,
polymers and liposomes [27]. They are successfully investigated as a potential drug
delivery system, which is advantageous to aforementioned, since SLNs do not suﬀer
problematic questions such as:
• Limited routes of administration
• Both hydrophobic and hydrophilic drugs loading capacity
• Cytotoxicity
• Production upscaling
• Stability upon storage
Solid lipid nanoparticles size lies in submicron range and since incidence of micropar-
ticles in formulation is negligible, unlike polymers or emulsions can be administered
in both oral and parenteral routes, e.g. intravenously [28].
Dispersions of SLNs are technically slightly modiﬁed lipid nanoemulsions [27]. In

the latter case, liquid lipid is dispersed in aqueous media and stabilized by phos-
pholipids or surfactants. When it comes to drug delivery, loaded compounds are
released rapidly from liquid media which is unfavourable in controlled drug release.
Exchanging liquid lipid into a solid one (e.g. long chain fatty acid) decreases mo-
bility of drug carried and thus allow prolonged release. Long chain fatty acids used
as solid lipids are physiological and therefore very well tolerated and do not exhibit
neither acute nor chronic toxicity. As carriers, they are stable and allow large pay-
loads of drug. Moreover, PEG coated SLNs possess long blood circulation times.
What is important for medical applications, process of upscaling production is facile
since all of possible formulation methods are well established and commonly used.

2.5.1
Methods of solid lipid nanoparticles manufacturing
High shear homogenization/ultrasonication
High shear mixing and ultrasonication were methods of choice in ﬁrst attempts
of solid lipid nanoparticles formulations since they are easy and widely used [30],
[31]. These methods, however, suﬀer incidence of microparticles being present in
dispersion. Ahlin et al. applied mixing with rotor-stator homogenizer at diﬀerent
mixing rates and times achieveing nanoparticles with size range of 100-200nm [32].
Interestingly, no clear pattern on used parameters has been observed.
Diﬀerent
studies found similar results while using ultrasonication technique, also without
clear setup dependence [31], [33].
High pressure homogenization (HPH)
Primarily used for nanoemulsions formulation, high pressure homogenizers has gained
interest as an eﬃcient method of production of SLNs dispersions [27], [34]. They
oﬀer low sizes and polydispersity of nanoparticles and can have large capacity for
potential upscaling. Principle of operation of high pressure homogenizer is that high
pressure (up to 2000 bar) is pushing liquid through a few-micron narrow gap, thus
liquid accelerates extremely at a short distance. Present shearing and cavitation

==PAGE:33==

forces are so strong that they disrupt particles to form nano-sized range. Depending
on requirements, such as temperature sensitivity, there are two techniques of HPH.
• Hot homogenization
Mixture of lipid melt, surfactant/emulsiﬁer, water and possibly a drug is high-
shear stirred to form pre-emulsion. Pre-emulsion undergoes HPH above melt-
ing temperature of the lipid and this step would be typically repeated up to 5
times. Finally, formed nanoemulsion (since lipid is melted) is cooled at room
temperature or below to form SLNs dispersion.
• Cold homogenization
This technique is used when lipid particles are meant to be loaded with tem-
perature sensitive drugs. First step would include dispersion of drug in lipid
melt with subsequent rapid cooling , i.e. in liquid nitrogen. Obtained solid is
further milled to reach micron size range and mixed with emulsiﬁer aqueous
solution. Mixture undergoes high pressure homogenization at room tempera-
ture or lower. This method, however, yields nanoparticles of larger size than
using hot homogenization.

Solvent emulsiﬁcation/evaporation
Another approach is based on dissolution of lipid and emulsiﬁers in organic solvent
with subsequent emulsiﬁcation of the solution in water, importantly mixture has to
be immiscible [35]. Nanoparticles of narrow distribution size are formed during evap-

Microemulsion –based preparation
Microemulsions are prepared by mixing lipid, surfactants and water at temperature
above melting point of the lipid and dispersing the mixture in cold water (4◦C)
under stirring [36]. The method yields particles with a sub-micron size, however
with relatively high polydispersity (up to 0.7). Similar study with improved SLNs
characteristics has been conducted recently [37]. Researchers obtained 100-200nm
nanoparticles as follows: ﬁrst, standard microemulsion was prepared and maintain-
ing high temperature, it was dropped into cold water (2◦C) under vigorious stirring.
As a puriﬁcation step, SLNs dispersions were centrifuged for large diameter particles
to precipitate. Applied technique gives satisfying results with adequate microemul-
sion composition.
2.5.2
Hydrophilic-Lipophilic Balance and Critical Packing
Parameter Theories
Manufacturing of lipid emulsions and dispersions requires use of stabilizers: sur-
face active agents, commonly known as surfactants [38]. They are amphiphilic in
nature and are composed of hydrocarbon chain hydrophobic part (‘tail’) and po-
lar hydrophobic part (‘head’). The head strongly interacts with water molecules via
dipole-dipole (or ion-dipole) interactions or hydrogen bonding whereas the tail avoids
interaction with water. Depending whether hydrophilic part is ionic, zwitterionic
or non-ionic, surfactants are classiﬁed correspondingly. Principle of surfactant as
stabilizer is its rendering in dispersion by cooperative forces of interactions between
hydrophilic head and water, and dispersed particles. One of early developed pa-
rameters to characterize surfactants was hydrophilic-lipophilic balance (HLB) value
[39].
It is estimated based on proportion between hydrophilic and hydrophobic
parts of the molecule. This approach is rather simplistic and there are no strong
physicochemical foundations of the theory. HLB value is purely empirical, however
it is a considered good starting point for emulsion preparations and is widely used.
Typically, HLB <9 is assigned to lipophilic and >11 to hydrophilic surfactants.

Ultrasonication is a method of many applications among which homogenization of
micro- and nanoemulsions is common [27]. Technique’s principle is use of high fre-
quency (above 20 kHz) sound waves for disruption and dispersion of the liquids. The
process is a continuum of pressure-altering cycles that can be divided in two phases:
rarefaction (low-pressure phase) and compression (high-pressure phase). During the
rarefaction, small void areas are created in the liquid which later collapse during
compression. Shear forces created in during ultrasonication are strong enough to
overcome intermolecular forces, yielding ﬁnely dispersed emulsions and suspensions.
Lipid was put in glass vial and melted at 87◦C. Surfactant and milliQ water were

into vial and it was placed in ultrasonic bath (Ultrasonic Cleaner, VWR, USA) for
20 minutes at 80◦C. After that time, vial was removed from the bath and cold milliQ
water (4◦C) was immediately put into the mixture. All of preparations have been
analysed with DLS in order to establish particle size. Those with the smallest par-
ticle diameters have been chosen for further characterization as the most promising
ones. Moreover, reproducibility of these preparations has been tested.

3.2.3
Determination of size of solid lipid nanoparticles
Dynamic Light Scattering is a rapid and simple method for particles size distri-
bution determination [45]. The principle of the method is based on scattering of
monochromatic and coherent laser beam which is pointing at sample. Once the light
hits the particles of sub-micron size range, it gets scattered (Rayleigh scattering),
however scattering intensity ﬂuctuates due to Brownian movements of molecules in
the solution. Diﬀraction of the light from the particles causes its interferences (either
constructive or destructive) and since the distance between scatters changes with
time, it is possible to create a pattern, which further analyzed gives distribution of
diﬀusion coeﬃcient of particles. This can be applied in Stoke-Einstein equation for
calculation of hydrodynamic diameter, namely particle size.

Samples for DLS analysis were prepared as follows: in plastic cuvette, washed with
milliQ water prior to use, 2000µL of ﬁltered milliQ water was pipetted. Next, 10µL
of SLN formulation was added to cuvette, mixed gently to avoid air bubble forma-
tion and degassed for 3 minutes. Prepared samples were measured in ZetaPALS

3.2.4
Determination of ζ- potential
ζ-potential is a measure of electrostatic potential of solid particles dispersed in aque-
ous medium [46]. Particles are forming a net electrical charge on the surface which
causes rearrangement of ions in the dispersion. Counterions are forming a ﬁxed layer
on the solid-liquid interface and those are further surrounded by cloud of ions, neu-
tralizing the charge: this is called electric double layer (EDL). ζ–potential is electric
potential at the boundary of the EDL, called slipping plane. Value of ζ–potential
is commonly used as a measure of colloids stability- it is considered that the higher
potential’s absolute value, the more stable the dispersion. However, this measure
cannot be treated as fully reliable, since there are factors that can inﬂuence colloids
stability, mainly dependent on suspending medium’s properties [47]. Method used
for obtaining ζ-potential is Electrophoretic Light Scattering (ELS). ELS is based on
Dynamic Light Scattering principle- laser light is going through the sample, where
it is scattered.
Diﬀerence in frequency between scattered and reference beam is
detected and analyzed based on Doppler Eﬀect. However, unlike in DLS, which is
based on Brownian motion of molecules, in ELS there is electric ﬁeld applied, which
causes electrophoresis and therefore particles movements are directed.
For ζ–potential determination of SLNs formulations, at ﬁrst 1700µL of milliQ water
and 10µ of sample were put in plastic cuvette and mixed gently. Second measure-
ment was conducted with 1700µL of ﬁltered 10mM HEPES buﬀer and 10µ of sample.
Electrode was put in cuvette and samples were analyzed using ZetaPALS Analyser
(Brookhaven Instruments Corporation, USA).
3.2.5
Determination of iodine content
To determine iodine content in SLNs formulations, Inductively Coupled Plasma-
Mass Spectroscopy (ICP-MS) was applied. It is an eﬀective and robust technique
used for trace element analysis. Known for its good accuracy and low detection and
quantiﬁcation limits makes this method reliable and widely used tool for quantiﬁ-
cation and speciation purposes.
Principle of the method is that initially, sample is nebulized into plasma, where
charged species are created by colliding with Ar+ ions and once they reach mass
spectrometer, they can be distinguished based on their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z)
[48]. To avoid instrumental drift and background noise inﬂuence on the measure-
ments, internal standard is often used and it is chosen so it has similar mass and
properties to analyzed element. Addition of properly chosen internal standard re-
sults in more accurate and precise measurements.
Most commonly used diluent used for sample preparation is mixture of nitric acid
and hydrogen peroxide. However, this could cause disattachment of iodine from

3.2.6
Structure analysis
X-Ray Diﬀraction (XRD) is popular and powerful tool for structural and crystal-
lographic analysis.
It provides information about size and shape of basic units
comprising crystal lattice of examined non-amorphous materials [51], [52]. Char-
acterization of compounds is possible based on how they diﬀract x-ray light beam.
Principle of x-rays generation was previously discussed in introductory part. Once
the incident x-ray beam hits the sample, it gets scattered by electrons of atom build-
ing up a crystal. If the scattering is coherent and scattering centers form a speciﬁc
pattern, beam undergoes diﬀraction. Information about three-dimensional structure
of analyzed sample can be obtained by measuring diﬀraction intensity and angles.
However, in case of Powder X-Ray Diﬀraction (PXRD), ﬁnely ground sample has
polycrystalline structure and therefore, diﬀraction will happen only from the planes
that are oriented at certain angle. During PXRD analysis, incident laser beam hits
sample’s surface at angles from 0 to 90◦. Data about intensity and position is further
analyzed to give spectra, which are characteristic to every material.
Prior to analysis using Powder XRD technique, SLNs formulations have been freeze-
dried with EPSILON 2-4 LSC (Martin Christ, Germany). Dry SLNs and C16-TIP
were ground in a mortar and put on ﬂat plate in such amount to ﬁll in the sample
space. Raw material, namely C16-TIP became highly static after grinding which
prevented transferring it on sample holder, therefore sample was prepared from non-
ground C16-TIP. Formulations and raw material were analyzed with MiniFlex 600
(Rigaku Corporation, Japan).

3.2.8
Solid lipid nanoparticles imaging
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is versatile tool for visualization of material
and its topographic analysis [54]. Unlike in optical microscopy, which uses visible
light for scanning a sample, SEM is based on scanning with electron beam under
vacuum. Wavelength of accelerated electrons is signiﬁcantly smaller than of visi-
ble light, which provides much higher resolution and magniﬁcation up to 3 000 000
times. Operating principle is rather simple: electrons from the electron gun are pass-
ing through system of electromagnetic lenses to focus beam on the sample. Once
they collide with surface, secondary electrons are produced and detected. Signals
are further transformed to give an image of the sample.
In order to conduct SEM analysis on the SLNs preparations, samples had to be
freeze-dried. First, samples have been up-concentrated, as mentioned earlier and
extracted using 5% glucose solution into glass vials. Glucose has served as a cry-
oprotectant, to ensure that SLNs will not collapse while drying. Samples in vials
have been frozen in liquid nitrogen and placed in freeze-dryer over 48 hours to ensure
complete drying. Such prepared samples were provided for SEM imaging. Obtained
images were analyzed using ImageJ software in order to obtain data about particle
sizes.

4.3.3
Structural analysis
In order to determine structural properties of both SLNs samples compared to raw
C16-TIP, Powder X-Ray Diﬀraction was used. Since composition of all samples is
known, analysis has been conducted mainly to establish whether raw material and
SLNs formulations are crystalline. Powder XRD analysis of C16-TIP yielded spec-
trum with multiple sharp peaks, which corresponds to highly organized, crystalline
structure of lipid. Both solid lipid nanoparticles preparations’ spectra are compara-
ble to the raw material. Peaks are present at the same angle, however signals are
less intense. Intensity most likely have been decreased by presence of surfactants.
Overlapping of SLNs’ and lipid’s spectra indicates that material does not undergo
structural transitions nor become amorphous upon formulation, therefore remains
crystalline. What is worth to mention, is that palmitic acid (C16H32O2), which is
corresponding species to C16-TIP, is also crystalline [57]. Thus presented XRD data
is in agreement with initial assumptions about crystallinity of iodinated phenol-fatty
alcohol ether.

4.4
Future Perspectives
When it comes to solid lipid nanoparticles preparation method, ultrasonication have
shown satisfying results, howbeit it would be highly recommended to optimize the
homogenization process. Applying ultrasonic probe instead of bath could increase
eﬃciency of dispersing of lipid, leading to formation of smaller and more monodis-
perse particles. Moreover, investigating various ultrasonication parameters could
also improve those properties [31].
What is more, preparation of SLNs using, by far the most reliable reported method,
namely high pressure homogenization, is recommended [34]. Regardless following
hot or cold preparation process, particles created with this technique possess very
narrow size distribution and low diameter. Distinctive feature of HPH compared
to mixing or ultrasonication is large capacity that yields in >10g batches. On one
hand, this is favorable once method is well established and applicable for SLNs pro-
duction. However, it means that big amounts of nanodispersion components would
be required also during initial preparation studies. That leads to question of ef-
fective up-scaling of iodinated lipid synthesis process, although it is believed that
this is fairly feasible. The reaction does not require additional heating or cooling,
44
==PAGE:63==
CHAPTER 4. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
therefore only by proportional increase of materials and setup equipment, product’s
amounts could be increased.
Furthermore, it is crucial to create eﬀective and reliable method for iodine con-
tent determination. The ICP-MS analysis should be optimized. In case of SLNs,
the digestion stage appears to be limiting for accurate determination, therefore it
needs to be investigated more extensively. One of the possible modiﬁcations could
include microwave induced digestion, which is commonly used in sample preparation
step for ICP-MS analysis [49]. Another possibility is to test more aggressive organic
solvents in SLNs decomposition are release of iodine. Achieving complete digestion
of samples could improve iodine determination with ICP-MS or allow application
of spectrophotometric methods [58]. Spectrophotometric analysis, however, usually
impairs accuracy, so this would not be preferred technique. There are also researches
using ion chromatography [59] for iodine levels determination. What is more, there
are several studies reporting use of X-Ray Fluorescence Spectroscopy for iodine mea-
surements in biological samples [60] and this could be another promising approach.
In order to obtain data complementary to DSC, further thermal transitions study
over SLNs using thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) is suggested. TGA can be used
for determinig loss of mass and thereby provide additional information about nature
of transitions occuring in the sample.
Moreover, what requires further exploration is structure of SLNs. It remains un-
resolved which factor is leading to creation of long crystals instead of lipid spheres
and what is the nature and properties of crystals formed. Surely, freeze-drying of
samples leads to particles agglomeration and therefore alternative drying method
needs to be applied. For SEM imaging purposes, it would be worth to investigate
slow drying of highly diluted sample under atmospheric conditions or even at de-
creased temperature, to avoid collapsing of the SLNs.
Moreover, as for another
method of imaging, it might be beneﬁcial to analyse SLNs preparations with atomic
force microscopy (AFM) which can be applied for imaging of liquids. AFM could
provide information about structure of SLNs in the dispersion without any invasive
preparatory method.
The last investigation that is highly recommended is to conduct cytotoxicity and
in vivo studies. It was not carried out during the project due to time restrains.
However, those studies would reveal vital information about applicability of the
SLNs formulations as contrast agents, such as bio-compatibility, circulatory times
and attenuating properties of media.

Conclusions
The main focus of the project was to manufacture solid lipid nanoparticles that could
serve as contrast agents for X-Ray Computed Tomography. The aim was to create
an alternative to contrast media currently used for medical imaging, that would
be easy to produce and possess better contrast properties and longer circulatory
times than those present on the market. In order to achieve this, SLNs preparations
should be sub-micron sized species, highly loaded with x-ray attenuating iodine as
well as exhibit long shelf-life and good stability under physiological conditions. An-
other crucial requirement is neutral charge to avoid interaction between tissues and
contrast agent.
Initial formulation attempts employed mixing as homogenization technique for creat-
ing SLNs suspensions from 2,4,6-triiodophenol-hexadecyl ether (C16-TIP). Particle
size achieved was around 500nm, which was acceptable yet not satisfactory. In the
end, ultrasonication became the method of choice in order to obtain ﬁne dispersion
of surfactant stabilized lipid in aqueous medium. Homogenization of C16-TIP in
water with either TWEEN R
⃝80 or BRIJ R
⃝O20 yielded particles of below 200nm
diameter and low polydispersity, and method has been proven to be reproducible.
Furthermore, iodine load of solid lipid nanoparticles preparations have been analysed
with ICP-MS, howbeit results cannot be considered reliable, due to issues encoun-
tered during sample preparation. Iodine content measured has been distinctively
lower than theoretical, but there is no certain proof what the reason was.
Studies on formulations stability over storage time (above month at 5◦C) and under
heating to 37◦have shown no changes in particle size nor polydispersity, what proves
the stable and therefore applicable as pharmaceuticals. Moreover, ζ-potential mea-
surements exposed that charge carried by SLNs negligible while analysed in HEPES
and can readily be considered as neutral for internal applications.
Remaining concern is structure of prepared SLNs revealed by powder XRD and
SEM analysis. Hypothetically, solid lipid nanoparticles should be spherical which is
not the case. Since particles appear as easily agglomerating nanocrystalline species,
which is infavourable for pharmaceuticals, this might be constraining factor for ap-
plicability. Although, formation of crystals might have occurred during preparation
