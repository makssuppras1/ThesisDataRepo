RESUMÉ 
IV 
RESUMÉ 
Der er i mange studier fundet en klar sammenhæng mellem indeklimaet i 
klasseværelser og præsentation, helbred og fravær, som alle påpeger at bedre 
indeklima i klasseværelser forsøger præsentationen og velværen og nedbringer 
sygefraværet. Elever i danske folkeskoler bruger per år 24 % af deres vågne tid på 
skolen, og når folkeskolerne er et springbræt for elevernes fremtidige uddannelse 
og karriere, er det meget vigtigt at tilbyde det bedste undervisningsmiljø som 
muligt.  
60 danske folkeskoler er i dette projekt blevet undersøgt i opvarmningssæsonen. 
Sammenhænge mellem målte indeklimaparametre og registreret 
bygningskarakteristikas blev undersøgt, samt variationen af 
indeklimaparametrene indenfor og i mellem skoler. De målte indeklima 
parametre var CO2 koncentration, relativ luftfugtighed, temperatur, lysforhold og 
lydniveauer. Resultatbehandlingen foregik ved hjælp af valgte deskriptive værdier 
og i regressionsanalyser.  
Signifikante sammenhænge blev fundet mellem CO2 koncentration og ventilations 
metode, hvor CO2 koncentrationen var lavere i klasseværelser med balanceret 
ventilationssystem end i klasseværelserne med mekanisk udsugning og klasser 
med automastik eller manuel vinduesåbning. Luftkvaliteten var utilfredsstillende i 
mere end 50 % af brugstiden med en CO2 koncentration højere end 1000 ppm. 
Den relative luftfugtighed var lavere i klasseværelser med balanceret ventilation 
end i klasseværelser med mekanisk udsugning og klasser med automastik eller 
manuel vinduesåbning. 
Temperaturen var inden for den acceptable grænse i ~80 % af brugstiden, men 
med en tendens til at være i den høje ende. Belysningsstyrken på bordene var 
under 300 lux i ~50 % af brugstiden, men ingen signifikante sammenhænge 
mellem temperature og lysstyrken og bygningskarakteristika blev fundet. 
Lydniveauet var i ~40 % af brugstiden over 70 dBA, som blev karakteriseret som 
et højt lydniveau. Det blev fundet, at det ækvivalente lydniveau steg med stigende 
CO2 koncentration, samt med et stigende antal elever i klassen. Der var dog ingen 
af de variable, som havde en signifikant sammenhæng med det ækvivalente 
lydniveau.

ABSTRACT      V
ABSTRACT 
Several studies have found a clear association between the indoor environment in 
classrooms and the performance, well‐being and absenteeism of the pupils. All of 
them suggesting that better Indoor Environmental Quality in classrooms leads to 
increased performance and well‐being and to lower absenteeism. Since the pupils 
spends annually ~24 % of the time in schools, and since primary schools is the 
stepping‐stone of the pupil’s further education and professional life, it is essential 
to provide the best educational environment as possible.  
 
In this study, the indoor environment in 60 Danish primary schools was 
investigated in the heating season. The measured indoor environment parameters 
were CO2 concentration, relative humidity, temperature, lighting conditions and 
sound pressure level. Associations between selected indoor environment 
parameters and registered building characteristics was studied. Furthermore, the 
variability of these parameters within and between schools was studied. The 
recorded data was processed and described with selected descriptive values and 
analyzed in univariate and multivariate linear regression analyses.  
 
Significant associations between CO2 concentration and ventilation type was 
found, suggesting that classrooms with balanced mechanical ventilation systems 
had a lower CO2 concentration than classrooms with mechanical exhaust and 
classrooms with automatic and manual openings of windows. Furthermore, 
surface age was a significant effect on the CO2 concentration, indicating that the 
CO2 concentration decreased, the newer the surface age was assessed to be. The 
Indoor Air Quality was in more than 50 % of the occupied time insufficient with a 
CO2 concentration higher than 1000 ppm. The relative humidity tended to be 
lower in classrooms with balanced mechanical ventilation than classrooms with 
mechanical exhaust and classrooms with automatic and manual openings of 
windows. 
The temperature tended to be high, but was within the acceptable range of 20‐
24°C in ~80 % of the time. The illuminance was below 300 lux in ~50 % of the 
occupied time indicating too dark conditions. No significant association was found 
between temperature, illuminance and building characteristics.  
The sound pressure level tended to be high with a sound pressure level above 70 
dBA in ~40 % of the occupied time. The SPLeq increased with increasing CO2 
concentration and furthermore the SPLeq increased with and increasing number 
of pupils, but none of them were significant in a regression analysis.

INTRODUCTION      1
1 INTRODUCTION  
In Denmark, the first 10 years of education are compulsory for children at the age 
from around 6 to 16 years. The new school reform in from 2014, makes the pupils 
spend 1,200‐1,400 hours per year in school, depending on grade 
(Undervisningsministeriet 2014). This corresponds to 21 % ‐ 24 % of the awaking 
hours, assuming 8 hours of sleep at night.  
Several studies have found a clear association between the performance of the 
pupils and the indoor environment in the classrooms (Haverinen‐Shaughnessy et 
al. 2011; Wargocki & Wyon 2011; Kjeldsen et al. 2013). All of them suggesting that 
better Indoor Environment Quality (IEQ) in classrooms leads to increased 
performance, well‐being, health and lower absenteeism.  
Since primary school is the stepping‐stone of the children’s further education and 
professional life, it is essential to give the children, as well as the teachers, the best 
educational environment as possible, on the background of the proven association 
between IEQ and performance/well‐being.  
1.1 OBJECTIVE 
In this Master thesis, the indoor environment in 60 Danish primary schools was 
investigated. The thesis was limited to consider public schools.  
Part 1: Comprehensively study in 10 Danish Primary Schools 
In 10 schools selected according to building typology and ventilation method, 
indoor environment parameters were measured in a large number of classrooms 
on each school during the heating season to characterize the variability of these 
parameters within and between schools. 
This part of the study was a continuation of an investigation done by Stine Rolle 
Hansen in the thesis Evaluation of the Indoor Environment in Seven Primary 
Schools (2016).  
Part 2: Representative study in 50 randomly selected Primary Schools 
In 50 randomly selected schools, a cross‐sectional survey was made to study the 
association between selected indoor environment parameters and certain 
buildings characteristics such as ventilation type, materials, location, 
surroundings, windows etc.

2 LITERATURE REVIEW  
Pupils and their teachers are exposed to the indoor environment in schools a 
significant amount of time each day. To optimize pupil’s performance and well‐
being, it is essential to know the indoor environment exposures as well as gain 
knowledge of IEQ’s effect on the pupil’s health and performance. The parameters 
focused on in this thesis will be described as in this section as a background of the 
project. 
2.1 INDOOR ENVIRONMENTAL QUALITY (IEQ) 
The quality of the indoor environment is influenced by several parameters. The 
most important parameters that define the IEQ are; Air quality, thermal 
conditions, lighting conditions and acoustics, as seen in Figure 2.1.

However, numerous non‐measurable factors influence the perception of the

 INDOOR AIR QUALITY 
The CO2 concentration is often used as an indicator of the quality of the air in 
rooms, where occupants are the main source of pollutions, e.g. in classrooms. CO2 
itself is not harmful, unless it is found in high concentrations, (Clausen et al. 2014), 
but it is used as an indicator of other bio effluents from occupants.  
Other pollutants from e.g. building materials, furniture or radon, even hundreds of 
different chemicals, can be present in the indoor environment, and if the room is 
not sufficient ventilated, the concentration of these will increase as well.  The 
occupants may inhale these chemicals and pollutions as a cocktail (Fanger 2006). 
It has been suggested that it is the cocktail effect of chemicals that can be harmful 
to occupants health and even cause cancer (Syberg et al. 2015). Therefore, in an 
inadequately ventilated classroom high CO2 concentrations may not be the only 
problem. It may also be the increased concentration of other pollutants, but for 
the purpose of this thesis, the focus will be on CO2 concentration. 
 THERMAL INDOOR ENVIRONMENT 
Thermal conditions are described by air temperature, radiant temperature, air 
velocity and relative humidity. Too high or too low temperature can cause 
discomfort, which can affect the pupil’s ability to learn and perform well. A study 
suggests that in classrooms with poor thermal environment it will be cost‐
effective to provide better thermal conditions (Wargocki & Wyon 2013a), since 
the pupils performed better in a classroom with the right temperature. Too high 
air velocity can cause draught. The relative humidity is as well an important 
parameter, since too low humidity (< 25 %) can cause discomfort by e.g. dry 
mucous membrane, throat and eyes. Too high relative humidity (> 80 %) 
increases the risk of fungus and mold.  
In this thesis, the thermal environment in the classroom will be characterized by 
air temperature and relative humidity.  
 LIGHTING CONDITIONS 
The human body has been developed to live in an environment with natural 
daylight. In modern lifestyle, most people spend more than 90 % of the time 
indoors (Jacobs 2001) and it is therefore essential to ensure sufficient lighting 
conditions, both daylight and artificial lightning when the daylight conditions is 
insufficient. Lighting conditions are, among others, characterized by illuminance 
and color temperature. Insufficient lighting can cause symptoms as headache, 
fatigue or even make you fall asleep. The hormone melatonin, which helps us fall 
asleep, is produced under dark conditions. Therefore, darkness in the classroom 
may effect on the pupils learning ability. In this thesis, the illuminance on desks in 
the classroom will be measured, and several factors that can affect the lighting 
conditions will be registered.

 ACOUSTIC ENVIRONMENT 
Sound can be characterized by the Sound Pressure Level (SPL). There is a 
difference between sound and noise, which is very important, when assessing the 
acoustic conditions in classrooms. Noise is unwanted sound, and a sound can be 
assessed as noise in both high and low SPL.  
In primary schools, the SPL may vary throughout the day, depending on the 
activities. When measuring the SPL, it is therefore difficult to distinguish between 
what is noise and what is acceptable sound relative to the activity.  
Another parameter to describe a room’s acoustical condition is the reverberation 
time (RT). RT describes the time from a sound is emitted until it has decreased by 
60 dB. A low reverberation time can be ensured by sufficient sound absorbing 
materials in the classroom. 
In this study the acoustical environment will be assessed by reverberation time 
and sound pressure level, and any building characteristics and location that may 
affect the acoustical environment will be registered.   
2.2 PERCEPTION OF THE INDOOR ENVIRONMENT 
Human beings experience the indoor environment very differently. The 
requirements and design values used to evaluate the IEQ are typically based on an 
‘average person’ and it is important to have in mind that some groups of people 
can be more sensitive to impacts of the indoor environment than others, hereof 
for example children (Kjeldsen et al. 2013; Wargocki & Wyon 2013a).  
Children breathe a larger volume of air compared to their body mass (Bennett et 
al. 2008) and they are not fully developed, which allows easier entry of toxic 
substances that can affect their organs and development (Firestone et al. 2008). 
In 2012, Børnerådet has investigated the perception of the IEQ among 5th‐ 6th 
graders distributed around in Denmark. 927 children answered a questionnaire 
regarding how they experienced the IEQ. In Figure 2.2, it can be seen that the 
highest prevalence was draught and too high temperature related to IEQ on a 
daily basis and poor classroom air quality on a weekly basis. (Børnerådet 2012).

The respondents were also asked if they found the different parameters 
problematic and 44 % and 36 % found that draught and bad IAQ were

The consequences of insufficient IEQ reported by the children was mostly

Finally, a study found significant impact of temperature and humidity on the 
perception of indoor air quality. The air was perceived as less acceptable with 
increasing temperature (Fang et al. 2000). 
2.3 INDOOR ENVIRONMENT IN DANISH ELEMENTARY SCHOOLS 
Numerous studies have been conducted over the past years to assess the indoor 
environment in primary schools. The Danish law on educational environment § 1 
states that ‘Pupils, students and other participants in public or private teaching has 
a right to a good educational environment, so that the teaching can take place in a 
fully safe and healthy environment’ (dcum 2011).  
Unfortunately, it was found in the Mass Experiments from 2009 and 2014 that the 
conditions were insufficient in more than 50 % of the participating classrooms 
(Andersen et al. 2009; Clausen et al. 2014).  
 MASS EXPERIMENTS 2009 AND 2014 
To investigate the currents conditions in the Danish primary schools and high 
schools, two comprehensive studies were carried out in 2009 and 2014 in the so 
called Mass Experiment.  
In 2009, 796 classes distributed on 330 schools participated in the study  
(Andersen et al. 2009). In 2014, 785 classes from 262 Danish schools participated 
(Clausen et al. 2014). During the experiment in 2014, teachers and pupils 
measured the CO2 concentration and temperatures at the end of a lesson twice 
with a one‐week interval. The first measurement was made with the windows 
closed during the lesson and the second time, when the pupils has been outside 
the classroom before the lecture, where the classroom was well vented. In 2009, 
only the scenario with closed windows during the lesson were made.  
The results from both 2009 and 2014 on the first comparable measurement with 
closed windows can be seen in Figure 2.3. In 2009, 44 % of the classrooms had a 
CO2 concentration below the required 1000 ppm and this percentage was 
decreased to 40 % in 2014, despite the focus on the importance on sufficient 
venting in the classrooms.

2.4 IEQ EFFECT ON PUPILS PERFORMANCE AND WELL-BEING 
The association between the well‐being and performance of the pupils and the 
indoor environment has been investigated in several studies (Petersen et al. 2015; 
Haverinen‐Shaughnessy et al. 2011) etc. An overall finding was that poor 
classroom environment leads to decreased performance and well‐being of the 
pupils. In most studies, the CO2 concentration and/or the temperature was used to 
characterize air quality exposures as input to assessment of pupil performance.  
 INCREASED VENTILATION 
A study on the effect of increased classroom ventilation, on the performance of 
schoolwork (Petersen et al. 2015), concluded that by increasing the ventilation 
from 1.7 l/s per person to 6.6 l/s per person, the performance of the pupils was 
increased with 3.2 % (grammatical reasoning) and 7.4 % (reading and 
comprehension). The study was performed in four classrooms with children in 
the age of 10‐12 years.  
Also Haverinen‐Shaughnessy et al. (2011) found that increased ventilation rate 
gives a higher performance. In their study a linear association between increased 
ventilation rate and students’ academic achievement, within the range of 0.9‐7.1 
l/s per person was found. For every unit (1 l/s per person) increase in the 
ventilation rate within this range, the pupil’s performance increased by 2.9 % for 
math and 2.7 % for reading.  
A comprehensive study of European schools called SINPHONIE underlined the 
importance of IAQ in schools and found impacts on the health, of IEQ in 
classrooms (Sinphonie 2014). The study provided evidence similar to several 
other studies; That bad IAQ leads to health symptoms like headache, cough and 
fatigue.  
==PAGE:23==
 
LITERATURE REVIEW      9
Furthermore, a higher absence rate of the pupils was found to be correlated with 
inadequately ventilated rooms with high person density, as a result of increased 
risk of transmission of airborne infections between the occupants (Daisey et al. 
2003). A study in 28 Californian schools showed that the absence rate due to 
illness could be lowered with approximately 10 % by increasing the ventilation 
rate from 4 l/person to 9.4 l/person (Mendell et al. 2013). Lower absence rate will 
naturally cause to higher performance and better conditions for the social aspects 
in the classroom.   
2.4.1.1 
VENTILATION TYPE 
Ventilation systems are mostly classified as; Natural ventilation (opening of 
windows by pupils and teachers), mechanical exhaust and balanced mechanical 
ventilation (mechanical supply and exhaust). 
A study on the association between classroom ventilation type and performance 
in Danish schools, concluded that pupil scores in a national test scheme was 
higher in mechanically ventilated classrooms than in classrooms where windows 
had to be opened manually (Toftum et al. 2015). This study also concluded that 
the CO2 concentration was often higher than the allowed 1000 ppm in classrooms 
with natural ventilation, which was not the case in classrooms with mechanical 
ventilation.  
In the Mass Experiment 2009 in both Denmark, Norway and Sweden, the results 
also indicated that the CO2 concentration in all three countries was higher in 
naturally ventilated classrooms compared with mechanical ventilated classrooms 
(both mechanical exhaust and balanced ventilation) (Andersen et al. 2009).  
 LIGHTING CONDITIONS 
Illuminance adjusted to the activity improves the perception of the IEQ and 
increases the ability to concentrate (Barkmann et al. 2012). In this study, it was 
found that lighting adjusted to ‘concentrating mode’ instead of ‘standard mode’, 
reduced the number of errors for an average student by 20.8 %, the reading speed 
by 16.8 % (the students read 16.8 % more word in the same time period) and 
reading comprehension improved as well. Improved concentration with ‘Focus 
light’ settings (1000 Lux and 6500 K) compared to standard settings (650 lux and 
12000 K) was as well concluded in a Dutch study (Sleegers et al. 2012).  
Better illuminance is associated with improved Subjective Indoor Air Quality 
(SIAQ) according to a Swedish study (Wang et al. 2015). Thus lighting conditions 
are not only suggested to affect the performance, they as well have a positive 
impact on the perception of the IEQ.

 NOISE 
The most frequent factor causing daily inconvenience in classrooms was found to 
be noise in a study in 355 primary schools in Finland (Turunen et al. 2014). The 
study concluded that the pupils’ auditory working memory and comprehension 
were negatively affected by noise. According to the study by Børnerådet (2012), 
traffic noise had a significant influence on the performance and concentration, 
where irrelevant speech did not have significant influence on the reading and 
mathematical performance.  
2.5 DANISH PRIMARY SCHOOL BUILDINGS AND RENOVATION 
The majority of the Danish primary schools have reached an age where 
renovations are necessary. 90 % of the primary schools were built before (Dam‐
Krogh & Berg 2015).  
The Danish government established in 2007 a quality improvement fund of 50 
billion DKK, to be invested in the period of 2009‐2018. 22 billion were dedicated 
the physical environment in primary schools (among others) (www.fm.dk 2008). 
Since it was found in the Mass Experiment from 2014 that only 40 % of the 
participating classrooms had a satisfying IAQ, it may seem that the indoor 
environmental quality has not been in focus on so far. Furthermore, the 
investments in primary schools increased in the period from 1995‐2014, but has 
decreased since 2014 (Dam‐Krogh & Berg 2015). 
As it can be seen in Figure 2.5, the most common renovation initiatives were 
renewable energy and lighting, which can be classified as renovation initiatives 
with a higher financial viability than renovation initiatives improving the air 
quality or thermal conditions (Dam‐Krogh & Berg 2015).

2.6 SCHOOL TYPOLOGIES 
The Danish school buildings are divided into 6 different typologies, based on year 
of construction (Hviid et al. 2014). The typologies will be summarized in this 
chapter and used as a basis of classifying the school’s typology.  
The study performed by Dam‐Krogh & Berg (2015), showed significant 
associations between typology and different parameters; A higher percentage of 
newer schools had the energy‐efficient energy labels (A‐C) and a higher 
percentage of the pupils in newer schools were satisfied with the IEQ. Though, it 
may be, that the typology based on construction year may not provide a precise 
definition for all schools, since renovation, reconstruction, extension and

2.7 RECOMMENDED INDOOR ENVIRONMENT IN DANISH SCHOOLS 
Buildings in Denmark have to fulfill The Danish Building Regulation (BR) current 
at the time of construction. In the case of extensive renovation or change in 
building use, the building has to fulfill the current Building Regulation at the time 
of change.  
Indoor environment in schools has not been in focus until the latest versions of 
BR. BR95 was the first version to have a specific chapter with requirements to the 
indoor environment. BR95 had references to the Danish standard DS 474, 
describing indoor thermal requirements. Before this version, only requirements 
of sound and ventilation were included, and only a few were specific 
requirements for schools.  
In the versions after BR 1995 the focus on indoor environment increased. In 
today’s building regulation, BR15 (as well as BR10), the requirements of thermal 
and atmospheric air quality, acoustic environment, lighting conditions, and 
multiple guidelines from the Danish Building Research Institute have been made 
to ensure a sufficient and healthy indoor environment in buildings, including 
schools. (www.bygningsreglement.dk 2016). The Building Regulation refers to 
DS474 for specification of requirements of thermal indoor environment.  
Other authorities have recommendations regarding the indoor environment in 
schools as well. The Danish Law on Education Environment (Undervisnings‐
miljøloven) states that all pupils are entitled to a good and healthy educational 
environment. Unfortunately, this is weakly formulated (Heebøll 2015), compared 
with the Working Environment law (Arbejdsmiljøloven), which ensures the 
teachers right to a healthy working environment. Therefore, the pupils are mostly 
indirect covered by the Working Environment Law (Hillig 2013). 
The Working Environmental Law states that the level of noise should not exceed 
85 dB(A) integrated over the working day. It should be noted that these limits are 
in relation to hearing damages. For comparison the SPL for assessment of the 
levels, 60 dB corresponds to ‘Talking’, 40 dB corresponds to the SPL at a library 
and 80 dB corresponds to traffic noise if located close to the traffic 
(www.acoustic‐glossary.co.uk).

Working Environment Law.

ISSUES WITH THE INSTRUMENTS AND SOURCES OF ERRORS 
UNPLUGGED VAISALAS AND PEAKTECHS 
In some classrooms, the instruments were unplugged by the time of pickup. The 
Vaisala suitcase stores the logged data from before the Vaisala was unplugged. In 
cases, where a few days were measured before it was unplugged, the data was 
used. If unplugged the same day as the equipment was installed, the data was not 
taken into account in the analysis. The PeakTech soundmeters did not start 
recording again if unplugged and plugged again, and the recorded data was not 
saved. 
SHADED OR MOVED HOBO LIGHT LOGGERS 
In some rooms, the HOBO light loggers had been moved during the measurement 
period. Either the logger was moved to a shelf or the tables were rearranged. It 
was seen that the loggers were shaded by paper, books etc. At one school, the 
pupils had the loggers in their hands at the time of pickup. Such data was deleted 
prior to analysis.

breaks were not removed since the outdoor lighting conditions were not changing 
as a result of breaks. Furthermore, sufficient lighting is essential in the breaks as 
well and it would not be correct to assume that the pupil would always be outside 
the classrooms in the breaks.  
For data on CO2 concentration, temperature and relative humidity, recordings 
were removed if the CO2 concentration was below 500 ppm, as it then was seen as 
uncopied time. 500 ppm was chosen on the basis of a study on the CO2 
concentration for several classrooms. It was very rarely that the CO2 
concentration was below 500 ppm in the any of the classrooms studied in random 
samples. Furthermore, if the CO2 concentration decreased more than 78 ppm 
within 5 minutes, it was seen as the pupils had left the classroom. The 78 ppm was 
found by comparing the class schedule and a subjective assessment of the 
increase and decreases of the CO2 concentration, to find how much the CO2 
concentration decreased when it was certain that all the student had left the 
classroom. Random samples were made and the value was adjusted to fit all 
samples as good as possible.

 UNI- AND MULTIVARIATE LINEAR REGRESSION ANALYSIS 
Regression analysis were used to study associations between the measured 
parameters and different observed building characteristics, e.g CO2 concentration 
vs. ventilation type and temperature vs. orientation.  
For the 10 schools, the regression models was based on slightly modified models 
used in the pilot phase. To select important and significant variables, a 
combination of forward and backward model selection was used.  
For the 50 schools, Univariate linear regression analysis analyses with several 
more characteristics was made. The procedure to find the final multivariate linear 
regression models was:  
 
I. 
Univariate linear regression (ULR): Initially, ULR analysis were made 
for each of the five measured parameters with all the observed 
characteristics that could have an influence on the IEQ. Those without 
significance were removed from the analysis.  
II. 
Uni/Multivariate regression: For some of the characteristics, that were 
found to be non‐significant, two independent variables were analyzed 
together to see if the two variables would be significant with both in 
consideration.  
III. 
Multivariate regression (MLR): All the characteristics selected in the 
forward selection process were analyzed in a MLR analysis. The 
independent variables being non‐significant in the MLR analysis were 
removed and a final MLR model was determined.  
In the pilot phase, a significance level was predefined to be p ≤ 0.2 in the selection 
process of independent variables, which is much higher than the usually p ≤ 0.05. 
The arguments were a relatively limited number of independent variables and 
since relevant information on outdoor weather conditions and detailed user 
behavior have not been accessible. Furthermore, an argument was that a 
significance level of p ≤ 0.2 has been used in published scientific papers where 
similar analysis were performed (Bekö et al. 2011). 0.05 was used as the 
maximum significant level in the MLR models. 
Subsequently, the procedure after defining the models, was to check for factors 
that potentially could influence the robustness and to validate the assumption 
behind the model. The procedure is summarized below:

4.2 MULTIVARIATE LINEAR REGRESSION ANALYSIS  
A MLR analysis was made to investigate significant associations between the 
indoor environment parameters and registered building characteristics.  
From the pilot phase, three regression models were defined for mean CO2 
concentration. The models contained the following independent variables, and the 
output from Stata of the models can be found in Appendix C.1: 
- 
Model 1 contains the two independent variables VolPup (volume to pupil 
ratio) and venttype (type of ventilation).  
- 
Model 2 contains VolPup, venttype and the presence of trickle vents.  
- 
Model 3 contains VolPup, venttype, trickle vents and typology 
Model 1 was the strongest model with R2= 0.69. This model was suitable for 
maximum CO2 concentration as well with an R2 = 0.68. Model 1 had a weakness 
with heteroscedasticity, but clustering of the ventilation types solved this 
problem.  
In this thesis, it was chosen to focus on model 1 and model 3. Model 2 was 
removed partly because it in the pilot phase was concluded that it has some issues 
with the normality of the residuals and partly because it was found, for all 10 
schools, that the presence of a trickle vent had a tendency to give a higher CO2 
concentration, which should not be true. The trickle was removed from model 3 
as well.  
In the pilot phase no significant regression model could be made for temperature, 
illuminance and sound pressure level. It was chosen not to make regression 
models for these parameters for the 10 schools, but it will be tested in the MLR for 
the 50 schools in chapter 5, to investigate if a larger number of observations can 
give a significant regression model on these parameters.

4.3 SUMMARY OF RESULTS OF THE 10 SCHOOLS 
CO2 CONCENTRATION: 
The CO2 concentration was below 1000 ppm in 45 % of the occupied time. The 
mean CO2 concentration was 886 ppm in classrooms with balanced ventilation, 
974 ppm in classrooms with mechanical exhaust, 1134 ppm in classrooms with 
automatically naturally ventilation, and 1587 ppm in classrooms with manual 
window opening. Furthermore, it was found that in balanced ventilated 
classrooms the percentage of time with CO2 concentration below 1000 ppm was 
71 %, where it was 58 % in classrooms with mechanical exhaust, 42 % in 
classroom with automatic natural ventilation and 26 % for manually naturally 
ventilated classrooms.  
No association in the variation of the mean CO2 concentration was found between 
different typologies, except that the newer typology, project work schools tended 
to have a lower CO2 concentration due to balanced ventilation system. The 
variation of the CO2 concentration within each school was highest in schools with 
manual natural ventilation, and for schools with balanced ventilation, the 
variation was small.  
A significant MLR model was made on mean CO2 concentration with ventilation 
type and volume to pupil ratio as independent variables, with significance levels 
below 0.05.  
RELATIVE HUMIDITY: 
The relative humidity was in 95 % of the time, the relative humidity was within 
the acceptable range of 25‐60 %. Balanced ventilated classrooms tended to have a 
lower relative humidity than naturally ventilated classrooms. There was found no 
association in the variation of the relative humidity within and between the 
schools. The only association found was that the relative humidity tended to be 
==PAGE:71==
 
RESULTS OF THE COMPREHENSIVELY STUDY IN 10 DANISH PRIMARY SCHOOLS      57
lower in classrooms with balanced mechanical ventilation, than manually 
naturally ventilated classrooms.  
Furthermore, it was also found that the mean relative humidity tended to 
decrease with an increasing openable window area except for classrooms with 
mechanical exhaust.  
TEMPERATURE: 
The mean temperature was 22.6 °C and during 83 % of the time the temperature 
was within the acceptable range of 20‐24°C. No meaningful association between 
the temperature and ventilation type was found.  
No association in the variance of the temperature between typologies was found. 
The temperature varied widely within schools. No clear association between the 
temperature and the orientation of the windows was found, however classrooms 
facing south with flexible interior curtains as solar shading tended to have a 
higher temperature than the other orientations.  
LIGHTING CONDITIONS: 
The illuminance was found to be below 2000 lux in 99 % of the occupied time, 
which indicates no critical conditions with glare.  
The classrooms were divided into 4 squares. In the two squares closest to the 
windows, the illuminance was below 300 lux in 60 % of the occupied time. The 
two squares furthest away from the window had an illuminance below 300 lux in 
66 % of the occupied time.  
No association was found between the mean illuminance and school typologies. 
Furthermore, it was found that classroom with windows facing south tended to 
have a higher illuminance than north, east and west facing classrooms.  
SOUND PRESSURE LEVEL: 
It was found that in only 1 % of the occupied time, the SPL were above 85 dBA, 
which indicate no critical SPL in regards to hearing damages. In 38 % of the 
occupied time, the SPL were above 70 dBA, indicating a tendency of loud SPL.  
No association between the SPLeq and the typology was found. The SPLeq varied 
widely both within and between the schools. Furthermore, it was found that the 
SPLeq increased linearly with increasing CO2 concentration for all ventilation 
types except from manually natural ventilation. The SPLeq tended to be lower in 
classrooms with mechanical balanced ventilation.  
Additionally, most of the classrooms had a reverberation time below 0.6. One 
school without sound insulation as the ceiling had a higher reverberation time.

5.8 SUMMARY OF RESULTS FOR THE 50 SCHOOLS 
CO2 CONCENTRATION 
In the 50 randomly selected schools the CO2 concentration was below 1000 ppm 
in 62 % of the occupied time with a mean value of 1026 ppm.  
Classrooms with balanced mechanical ventilation had a lower CO2 concentration 
than classrooms ventilated by mechanical exhaust and manual window openings. 
In classrooms with balanced ventilation, the CO2 concentration was below 1000 
ppm in 70 % of the occupied time with a mean value of 930 ppm. Classrooms with 
mechanical exhaust had a CO2 concentration below 1000 ppm in 54 % of the time 
with a mean CO2 concentration of 1139 ppm. Naturally ventilated classrooms had 
a mean CO2 concentration of 1219 ppm and the CO2 concentration was below 
1000 ppm in 46 % of the time.  
Classrooms with balanced ventilation systems with heat recovery tended to have 
a lower CO2 concentration than balanced systems with no heat recovery.  
Region Nordjylland were below 1000 ppm in 42 % of the occupied time, where it 
in Region Sjælland was 75 % of the occupied time.  
A MLR model was made with mean CO2 concentration as dependent variable with 
ventilation type and surface age as independent variables. The assumptions of the 
MLR analysis was not completely validated, but the model can be used to identify 
the building characteristics that mostly affect the CO2 concentration.  
==PAGE:99==
 
RESULTS OF THE REPRESENTATIVE STUDY IN 50 DANISH PRIMARY SCHOOLS      85
Furthermore, the volume to pupil ratio did have a significant influence on the 
mean CO2 concentration in naturally ventilated classrooms.  
RELATIVE HUMIDITY 
In 96 % of the occupied time, the relative humidity was within the range of 25‐60 
%. Classrooms with balanced ventilation tended to have a lower relative humidity 
than manually naturally ventilated classrooms.  
It was found in a URL analysis that naturally ventilated classrooms differed 
significantly from classrooms with balanced ventilation. This independent 
variable was the only significant variable that influenced the mean relative 
humidity. 
TEMPERATURE 
In 73 % of the occupied time, the temperature was within the acceptable range of 
20‐24°C with a mean temperature of 23°C. There was found no meaningful 
association between the temperature and ventilation type.  
In a ULR analysis it was found that none of the tested independent variables had a 
significant association with the mean temperature, except from the floor levels 3rd 
and 7th compared to ground floor. However, there was only one observation at 
both 3rd and 7th floor which made the association invalid.  
LIGHTING CONDITIONS 
In 5 % of the occupied time, the illuminance was above 2000 lux for the two 
squares closest to the windows and in ~ 1 % for the two squares away from the 
windows. In 31 % of the time, the illuminance was below 300 lux in the squares 
closest to the windows and in 47 % of the time furthest away from the windows.  
It was furthermore found that classrooms with west, south and east facing 
windows tended to have a higher illuminance than north facing classrooms.  
In a ULR analysis the mean illuminance was found not to depend on any of the 
included variables, since the p‐values of all the independent variables were >> 
0.05 and also > 0.2.  
SOUND PRESSURE LEVEL 
In less than 1 % of the occupied time, the SPL was above 85 dBA indicating no 
critical conditions for hearing damages. In 25 % of the occupied time, the SPL was 
above 70 dBA. No meaningful variation of the SPL between classrooms with 
different types of ventilation was found.  
The SPLeq increased slightly with an increasing CO2 concentration and 
furthermore, the SPLeq increased with an increasing number of pupils in the 
classroom. In agreement with results from the 10 schools, no obvious association 
between the SPLeq and the class levels was seen. Only the number of pupils in the 
classroom affected the SPLeq.

In this chapter the main findings and associated sources of errors are discussed. 
The study was divided into two parts with different objectives and approaches. 
Some of the findings will therefore be discussed in relation to the specific 
objective, whereas other findings will be discussed across the study parts when 
appropriate.  
TYPOLOGY 
The project work schools tended to have a lower CO2 concentration than the other 
schools, but otherwise no association between buildings typology and the 
measured indoor environment was found. However almost all project work 
school had balanced ventilation and classrooms in schools with another typology 
than project work schools that also had a good IAQ also had balanced ventilation 
systems. Assessment of the IAQ based on typology was therefore not applicable, 
except for project work schools. If typology should be used to categorize the IAQ 
in other schools than project work schools, renovation year and renovation 
initiatives would be necessary to include. This applies for temperature, 
illuminance and sound pressure level as well, since no association between 
typology and these parameters were found.  
The investigated schools were assigned a typology based on the definitions by 
Hviid et al. (2014). The definitions both construction year and different other 
characteristics. This thesis used the construction year, since it was very difficult to 
assign the typology according to building characteristics, as many schools had 
been renovated and therefore changed characteristics. It was therefore not 
possible to generalize the main characteristics as e.g. in Gungehusskolen, which on 
the basis of construction year was a comb school, but was in 2 storeys due to an 
extension. Comb schools according to Hviid et al. (2014) were typically 
characterized as one‐storey buildings.  
RANDOMLY SELECTED SCHOOLS AND CLASSROOMS IN THE 50 SCHOOLS 
One of the most important preconditions for Part 2 of the study was that the 50 
schools should be randomly selected. The investigation was intended to give an 
indication of the conditions in the randomly selected schools as well as study the 
associations between the indoor environment and building characteristics. The 
number of schools selected from each of the five regions was balanced as it was 
assessed that the most representative results would be achieved if the percentage 
of schools investigated in each region compared to the total number of schools in 
the regions was relatively evenly distributed. It was managed to select the schools 
randomly. However, the selection of the classrooms at the schools was not 
random. Four criteria were used when selecting the classrooms; Classrooms in 
different buildings, with different orientations, different grades and different 
ventilation types. These criteria were not possible to fulfill in all cases. Partly 
because some schools already had chosen which classrooms they wished to have 
==PAGE:102==

88 
investigated and partly because it was not always possible to access all the 
classrooms, since some teachers did not like the interruption. If the classrooms 
were selected by the school beforehand, two scenarios often occurred; 1) The 
school had classrooms with a very poor indoor environment that they wanted 
documented and used as proof to give to the municipality. 2) The school had a 
newly installed ventilation system or other initiatives for improving the indoor 
environment. In such cases, the school typically wanted documentation for the 
improved IEQ after renovation. No statistics on the occurrence of the two 
scenarios were made, but the ‘randomly selected’ criteria may have been affected 
slightly by this distortion. 
OUTDOOR WEATHER CONDITIONS 
Measurements at the last 9 of the 50 schools were carried out in the beginning of 
May where the outdoor temperature rose. This could have influenced the 
measurements significantly, since the windows might have been opened more 
frequently, which may have lowered the CO2 concentration compared the first 
period of measurements.  
The changing outdoor conditions may also have affected the temperatures in the 
classrooms. Partly because of the possible increased frequency of openings of the 
windows and partly because of potentially more solar heat gain, that could higher 
the temperature. It was found in the URL analysis that the mean temperature did 
not depend significantly on any of the registered building characteristics. Because 
of the increase of the outdoor temperature, the mean indoor temperature may as 
well have increased in the period, which could affect the analysis. If the outdoor 
weather conditions were approximately the same throughout all the 
measurements, it may have been possible to detect some associations between 
buildings characteristics and temperature. However, the control of the radiators, 
continuous measurements of the radiator’s surface temperature and data on user 
behavior in terms of frequency of regulating the thermostat etc. would be factors 
that needed to be known to investigate the variability of the temperature between 
classrooms and schools in the heating season.  
If leaving aside this study and focus on the pupils, the pupils was exposed for a 
higher temperature in the classroom, which in this study was found to increase in 
the period from December‐May. This was unfortunate as the literature study 
found that the pupil’s performance is affected by too high temperatures. However, 
the fact that no significant association was found may be a relevant finding 
anyway, since the outdoor weather conditions always will be changeable, 
indicating that different building characteristics affects the temperature in the 
different seasons of the year.  
 
The mean illuminance increased from the 10 schools to the 50 schools This 
indicates that the 50 schools, especially the last 7 schools, were investigated at a 
time of the year, where the solar radiation was in general higher. The literature 
study found that it was essential to ensure sufficient lighting conditions, since 
insufficient lighting can cause headache, fatigue or make you fall asleep. The low 
==PAGE:103==
 
DISCUSSION      89
illuminance found in this study may therefore affect the pupils well‐being and 
performance, since several studies suggested that illuminance adjusted to the 
activity increased the ability to concentrate (Barkmann et al. 2012). Furthermore, 
the perception of the IEQ increased in classrooms with sufficient illuminances 
(Wang et al. 2015), which substantiates the importance of sufficient lightings. 
However, the recommendation of 300 lux at the work desk is from the new 
Danish Buildings Regulation 2015, which only became effective in the beginning 
of 2016. Before this, the recommendation was 200 lux. Since none of the 
investigated schools was built since January ’16, none of the schools was entitled 
to fulfill 300 lux.  
Furthermore, the mean illuminance may not be representative of the illuminance 
in a classroom. The solar radiation can change throughout the measuring period 
and detection of associations between the illuminance and buildings 
characteristics may need another approach to be studied.  
OPENABLE WINDOW AREA 
In the analyses of the CO2 concentration for both the 10 and the 50 schools, the 
mean CO2 concentration decreased with increasing openable window area, except 
for manually naturally ventilated classrooms. It was surprising that classrooms 
with manual natural ventilation did not have this decreasing association, as a 
larger openable window area was hypothesized to let more fresh air into the 
room. However, a source of error that might have influenced this tendency was 
that the measured openable area was not the actual openable area, but the size of 
the glazing of the openable window. Furthermore, a larger actual openable area 
need not mean that the fully openable window area would be used, especially 
since it was in the heating season. 
No matter of the size of the openable window area, it is in naturally ventilated 
classrooms difficult obtain a good IEQ in many periods of the year. In seasons with 
an outdoor temperature much lower than the desired indoor air temperature, 
opening of the windows can cause discomfort by draught. Draught was in a study 
by (Børnerådet 2012) found to have one of the highest prevalence’s of 
discomforts among 5th‐6th graders, leading to concentration difficulties. On the 
other hand, a low frequency of opening of the windows leads to a higher CO2 
concentration, which also decrease the pupil’s ability to concentrate and may 
cause headache and other inconveniences. Another perspective of the difficult 
balancing in naturally ventilated classrooms, is that noise from the outside may be 
inconvenient for the pupils, especially for schools located in the inner cities or 
other densely populated area, when the windows are opened. It was found in a 
study performed by (Børnerådet 2012) that traffic noise affected the ability to 
concentrate and the reading comprehensive. It was in this present study found 
that the SPL increased with increasing CO2 concentration, which would be the 
case if the naturally ventilated classrooms did not open the windows. This 
illustrates the problematics of naturally ventilated classrooms, which may argue 
why balanced mechanical ventilation systems is the best solution to provide the 
best indoor environment in classrooms, since the CO2 concentration would be 
lower, contemporary with closed windows.

ACCEPTABLE RANGES FOR THE INDOOR ENVIRONMENT PARAMETERS 
The temperature was within the acceptable range of 20‐24°C in 83 % of the 
occupied time in the 10 schools and in 73 % of the occupied time in the 50 
schools. This temperature interval is the acceptable range for the operative 
temperature and not the air temperature, which was measured in this study. 
Therefore, some percentage of the time, where it was assessed to be in the 
acceptable range, may not have been acceptable if the radiant temperature and air 
velocity were taken into account. The Working Environment Law recommends a 
temperature between 20‐22°C. The temperature was above 22°C in 71 % of the 
time in the 10 schools and in 76.2 % of the time in the 50 schools. This underlines 
the assumption that the temperature could be categorized as high even though it 
was inside the requirement.   
The literature study found that providing better thermal conditions would be 
cost‐effective (Wargocki & Wyon 2013b), since the pupils performance increased 
in classrooms with the right temperature. 24°C may be too high for classrooms, 
since pupils are assumed to have a relatively high activity several times a day. 
Especially since the new school reform from 2014 stated that the pupils should be 
more active throughout the day and the teachers are therefore entitled to 
incorporate higher activities in the lessons.  
Furthermore, the 20‐24 °C is based on an average person and as each individual 
perceive thermal conditions individually, it may not always be acceptable for 
everybody in this range, especially kids may be more sensitive to for too high 
temperatures.  
ISSUES WITH THE EQUIPMENT  
The equipment for measuring the SPL was not reliable for recordings below 63 
dBA. It was therefore not possible to analyze potential associations between the 
SPL at the lower levels and buildings characteristics, which may also be a reason 
that no significant associations between SPLeq and buildings characteristics were 
found.  
It was also not possible to study the association between the surrounding traffic 
intensity and the SPL since it was assessed that the background noise from traffic 
would be below 63 dBA. It was hypothesized that an association in the SPL 
between heavy traffic vs. light traffic might have occurred if the recordings were 
valid below 63 dBA. The investigation performed by Børnerådet (2012) found 
that traffic noise had a significant influence on the pupils ability to concentrate. 
The association between SPL and the traffic density would therefore have been 
relevant to study, if the recordings below 63 dB were reliable.   
Furthermore, it was not possible to analyze the background noise level from 
mechanical ventilation systems. This was unfortunate since some janitors claimed 
that noise from the ventilations system was so disturbing that the systems were 
turned off during the lessons and running only in the breaks.

COMPARISON WITH MASS EXPERIMENT 
To simulate a ‘worst case scenario’ of the air quality in the 50 schools, an average 
of the recordings from one day in each classroom between 10.30‐11 am was 
extracted. The CO2 concentration was lower in the 50 schools than in the Mass 
Experiments. However, some of the differences in the conditions may explain 
different results on the two studies.  
First, only 26 % of the classrooms investigated in the 50 schools, had natural 
ventilation by manual window opening. In the Mass Experiment it was 56 % of the 
classrooms. 68 % of the classrooms in the 50 schools were ventilated by balanced 
ventilation, as opposed to 35 % in the Mass Experiment.  
Second, the CO2 concentration in the Mass Experiment was measured at the end of 
a lesson, where the pupils were told not to open windows during the lesson. In the 
extracted data from the 50 schools, it was not known if the room had been vented. 
Final, the time interval of 10.30‐11 was chosen to be completely sure that the 
lessons had not ended in any of the classrooms. In some schools, the lunch break 
may not start until e.g. 11.30, were the CO2 concentration could have reached to 
an even higher concentration.

CONCLUSION      93
7 CONCLUSION 
In 60 Danish primary schools, five indoor environment parameters were 
measured in the heating season to study potential associations between the 
indoor environment and the buildings characteristics of the school buildings.  
The air quality in the 10 schools was insufficient in more than 50 % of the 
occupied time. In the 50 schools, the air quality was insufficient in just below 40 % 
of the occupied time. 
The variability of the measured indoor environment parameters; CO2 
concentration, relative humidity, temperature, lighting conditions and sound 
pressure level was large within and between school building typologies. 
Therefore, building typology was not applicate for characterization of the IEQ.  
Two characteristics found to have a significant association with the CO2 
concentration. Ventilation type affected the CO2 concentration significantly, where 
classrooms with balanced ventilation had a lower CO2concentration than 
naturally ventilated classrooms. Classrooms with mechanical exhaust had in 
general a higher CO2 concentration than classrooms with balanced ventilation, but 
lower than classrooms with natural ventilation. The variation of the CO2 
concentration in manually naturally ventilated classrooms was large, but smaller 
in classrooms with balanced ventilation. The second characteristics significant 
was the subjective assessment of the surface age. The CO2 concentration 
decreased the newer the surface age was assessed to be. The volume to pupil ratio 
significantly affected the CO2 concentration in naturally ventilated classrooms.  
The relative humidity was generally in the recommended range. The only 
association influencing this parameter was the ventilation type. The relative 
humidity was lower in classrooms with balanced ventilation than classroom with 
mechanical exhaust, automatic and manual natural ventilation.  
The temperature in the classrooms at all 60 schools was in the acceptable range of 
20‐24°C in approximately 80 % of the time, but with a tendency of being in the 
high end of the range. In both parts of the study, the temperature tended to be 
higher in classrooms facing south, which was the only association found between 
temperature and buildings characteristics.  
The illuminance was low in ~50 % of the occupied time. North facing classrooms 
had a lower illuminance than south, east and west facing classrooms. This was the 
only association found between illuminance and building characteristics.      
The SPL tended to be high in ~25‐40 % of the occupied time with a SPL above 70 
dBA. The SPLeq increased with increasing CO2 concentration and furthermore, the 
SPLeq increased with an increasing number of pupils in the classroom. Lastly, the 
reverberation time was below the requirements of 0.6 s in all classrooms with 
sound insulation as ceiling material and only classrooms with no sound insulation 
had a higher reverberation time.

8 FUTURE STUDIES 
Several data recorded and collected at the schools, was delimitated from the 
analyses in this study. Suggestions for future relevant studies are listed below.  
MANUALLY VS. AUTOMATICALLY NATURAL VENTILATION 
In the 10 schools the user behavior of window openings was measured with state 
loggers. The janitor and teachers at two of the schools with automatically natural 
ventilation, made us aware of the automatically openings of the windows were 
often overruled by the teachers and pupils due to discomfort from draught. It 
could therefore be relevant to study the difference in the frequency of windows 
openings in the classrooms with automatically openings of the windows and 
manually openings of the windows and the difference of the CO2 concentration in 
those classrooms. Optionally, the actually openable window area could be taken 
into account as well.  
ARTIFICIAL LIGHTING AND DAYLIGHT 
In the 10 schools, the user behavior of artificial lighting was also measured with 
state loggers, where the frequency and duration of the use of artificial lighting was 
measured. Furthermore, the effect of the artificial lighting was measured with the 
spot measurements of the illuminance in four different scenarios. A suggestion of 
a relevant study could be to investigate the use of artificial lighting in association 
to the daylight for an energy‐ and economical perspective to see how often the 
artificial lighting was unnecessary on due to sufficient daylight. 
Furthermore, in future studies the association between different types of solar 
shading, illuminance and temperature may be a relevant study, especially outside 
the heating season as it was found in this study that the illuminance in general 
increased from December‐May due to changing outdoor weather conditions.  
RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FUTURE MEASUREMENTS 
In future measurements of the illuminance in classrooms, we would recommend 
to define some criteria of the distance to the windows to obtain the most equally 
performed measurements.  
The outdoor weather conditions may for many studies be an important parameter 
to know in studies of the association between the indoor environment parameters 
and buildings characteristics. It would be recommended to record the outdoor 
temperature, relative humidity and the solar radiation. Furthermore, the user 
behavior and surface temperature of the radiators would be beneficial to measure 
to study the variability in the temperature.  
Furthermore, if more reliable equipment was acquired, study on the associations 
between SPL and surroundings may be relevant to investigate.
