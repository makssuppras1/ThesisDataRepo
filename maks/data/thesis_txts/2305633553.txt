Abstract
In hostile environments, such as scientiﬁc facilities or industrial plants, where high temperatures, ionizing
radiation, oxygen deﬁciency and high electromagnetic ﬁelds present big hazards for humans, the increase of
robotic operations for minimizing human intervention is becoming a fundamental need. CERN, the European
Organization for Nuclear Research, has more than 50 km of underground experimental facilities where robots
can help the maintenance and the survey of the structures and machinery. Different remote controlled robotic
solutions are currently under development at CERN. Many challenges have to be considered for robotic
applications, such as the harsh and unstructured underground tunnel environment, where the communication
with the robot could be unstable. Furthermore, the robots must not, in any case, interrupt the accelerators
operation or damage any material. The development of autonomous or semi-autonomous platforms is a
fundamental requirement for CERN. The Train Inspection Monorail (TIM) is a CERN product designed to
provide unmanned actions in the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) tunnel. By ﬁtting on a monorail located on
the ceiling of the tunnel, the TIM train can run big distances performing visual inspections and monitoring
radiation levels, among other measurements. It is designed to be able to accept various payloads depending
on the needs.
As well as inspection, survey measurements of LHC machines are fundamental for the success of the
experiments. In the past, a prototype of a remote survey train was built. Consisting of a robotic system to
position targets on a reference, the survey train included a photogrammetric system that would measure the
LHC machinery alignment. This system was controlled manually and was never put in operation. A novel
control solution for the existing survey robotic system, designed to perform photogrammetric measurements
remotely and autonomously, is presented in this master’s thesis. The proposed control system has been
implemented and validated after a series of reliability runs. The work of this master’s thesis is conceived as
the ﬁrst step in the integration of the survey wagon system into the TIM train for remote measurement of the
LHC machinery.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction to CERN
1.1
European Organization for Nuclear Research
CERN, the European Organization for Nuclear Research, is considered one of the world’s largest and
most respected laboratories for scientiﬁc research. Located in the Northwest suburbs of Geneva on the
Franco-Swiss border, it currently has twenty European member states, as well as Israel which is the only
non-European country granted full membership. Nevertheless, scientists coming from non member states
can collaborate with CERN, making it one of the most multicultural and diverse environments in the World.
Established in 1954 and evolving ever since, CERN has been at the forefront of science research and several
important discoveries have taken place in it, leading among others to two Nobel prizes in physics and the
invention of the World Wide Web.
Currently, more than 12000 scientists (belonging to 120 different nationalities and representing 608
universities and research facilities in the world) are collaborating at CERN. CERN’s main function is to
provide the particle accelerators and other infrastructure needed for high-energy physics research. As a result,
numerous experiments have been constructed at CERN following international collaborations. Around
the construction and the maintenance of the accelerators, a lot of research is done involving medicine,
engineering and computer science in order to provide the best research facilities all over the world.
The main instruments used at CERN are purpose-built particle accelerators and detectors. Accelerators
boost beams of particles to high energies before the beams are made to collide with each other or with
stationary targets. Detectors observe and record the results of these collisions, from which physicists study
the basic constituents of matter, the fundamental particles. This process gives the physicists clues about how
the particles interact, and provides insights into the fundamental laws of nature [1].

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION TO CERN
1.2
Introduction to CERN’s accelerators
The accelerators complex at CERN is a succession of machines that accelerate particles to increasingly
higher energy. It is located near Geneva at an average of 100 meters underground (50 m near Geneva’s Lake,
175 m near the Jura Mountains). The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) [2] is the biggest particles accelerator in
the world and it is the last accelerator of a long chain that increases the energy of the particle to 7-8 TeV per
beam.
As soon as the particles are produced from the hydrogen ionization, protons are injected in the Linear
Accelerator 2 (LINAC 2) [3], the ﬁrst in the chain, that increases the energy of the particle to 50 MeV. The
beam is then injected in the Proton Synchrotron Booster (PSB) [4] that increases the energy to 1.4 GeV.
Before the Booster received its ﬁrst beams on May 26th 1972, protons were injected directly from the LINAC
into the Proton Synchrotron (PS) [5], where they were accelerated to 26 GeV. The low injection energy of 50
MeV limited the number of protons the PS could accept. The Booster allows the PS to accept over 100 times
more protons, which greatly enhances the beam’s use for experiments.
The Booster is followed by the PS, which pushes the beam to 25 GeV. It is a 628 meters circular
accelerator with 277 magnets (room-temperature), including 100 dipoles to bend the beam around the ring.
Particles are sent to the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS)[6]: it measures 7 km circumference and
accelerates the beam to 450 GeV using 1317 room-temperature magnets. In 1983, through protons-
antiprotons collisions, SPS experiment discovered two new particles, W and Z, bringing the Nobel Prize to
CERN [7].

vaporized lead and enter LINAC 3 [19] before being collected and accelerated in the Low Energy Ion Ring
(LEIR) [20]. They then follow the same route to maximum energy as the protons.
After the ﬁrst long shut-down from the beginning of 2013 to the end of 2014 [21], all the accelerators
have been improved, allowing LHC to increase the maximum energy from 4 TeV to 7-8 TeV per beam, with
a collision energy of 15 TeV. Also LINAC 4 has been built [22], replacing the LINAC 2 and increasing the
injection energy to the PS Booster to 180 MeV.

LHC Layout
The LHC is composed by several components which accomplish distinguished tasks. Although vast in
number, the main components of the LHC can be summarized in the following fashion. The detectors, of
course, are the key elements. Four particle detectors have been constructed and placed in huge underground
caverns, corresponding to the four collision points of the two beams. These are ATLAS (A Toroidal LHC
ApparatuS), CMS (Compact Muon Solenoid), ALICE (An LHC Ion Collision Experiment) and LHCb (LHC
beauty) (Figure 1.4). ATLAS and CMS are designed to study as broad a variety of physics as possible and
they are considered to be general-purpose experiments. ALICE is dedicated to heavy ions collisions and it
recreates conditions similar to those just after the Big Bang, in order to study and analyse the properties
of the quark-gluon plasma. The LHCb is dedicated to B-physics phenomena; in other words, it focuses on
B-hadron decays which should give better insight into the differences between matter and anti-matter as well
as possibly providing indirect evidence for SM-violating processes. In addition there are also the TOTEM
(Total Cross Section, Elastic Scattering and Diffraction Dissociation at the LHC) [27], LHCf (Large Hadron
Collider forward) [28] and MoEDAL (the Monopole and Exotics Detector At the LHC)[29].
Other important elements in the LHC operation are the superconducting magnets (Figure 1.5(a)). They

are responsible for keeping the beam stable and precisely aligned. Speciﬁcally dipole magnets are some of
the most complex parts of the LHC and they are used to bend the trajectory of the particles. This is absolutely
necessary as the LHC is a circular accelerator, which means that the beam circulates many times in closed
orbit before reaching its peak energy level, as opposed to a linear accelerator, where charged particles travel
along a straight trajectory and go through a number of accelerating structures. There are 1232 main dipoles
installed in the LHC, each 14.3 metres long and weighing in at 35 tonnes.
The quadrupole magnets are vital elements as well. They help to keep the particles in a tight beam; in
other words, they focus the beam. The idea behind this is that when particles are bunched together, they
are more likely to collide in greater numbers when they reach the LHC detectors. In addition to the dipole
and quadrupole magnets, there are also smaller sextupole, octupole and decapole magnets to correct small
imperfections in the magnetic ﬁeld at the extremities of the dipoles.
Radiofrequency (RF) cavities (Figure 1.5(b)) [30] are also of great importance. An RF cavity, whose
primary purpose is to accelerate charged particles, is a metallic chamber containing an electromagnetic
ﬁeld. The RF cavity is constructed in a speciﬁc shape and size so that the electromagnetic waves become
resonant and build up inside the cavity. The result is that the charged particles passing through the cavity
feel the overall force and direction of the resulting electromagnetic ﬁeld, which transfers energy to push
them forward along the accelerator. One important detail is that the ﬁeld in an RF cavity is made to oscillate
(change direction) at a given frequency, so timing the arrival of particles is critical.
Another critical element of the LHC is the beam dump system [31]. Beams do not circulate inside
accelerators indeﬁnitely. As particles collide with the sides of the beam pipe or with each other, the beams
degrade becoming less likely to give collisions of interest. Accelerator physicists can choose to dump the
beams, removing them from the accelerator and sending them to a beam dump (usually a radiation-shielded
block deep underground) for a safe absorption. In order to understand the necessity of the beam dump
system, the nominal LHC beam contains an unprecedented stored energy of 350 MJ, contained in 2808
bunches with a beam sigma of the order of 0.3 mm. The extremely high destructive power of such a beam
imposes an external dump, where the beam must be extracted completely from the LHC, diluted to reduce
the peak energy density and then absorbed in a dedicated system.

the LHC relies on storing, accelerating, and colliding beams with unprecedented intensities. The transverse
energy density of the nominal beam is 1000 times higher than previously achieved in proton storage rings.

parts of the accelerators. “Quenching” occurs when any part of a superconducting cable goes from the
superconducting to the normal resistive state; it occurs when the critical temperature, the critical current or
the critical ﬁeld is by-passed, or in case of movement of the superconductor by several mm (friction and

Robotic solutions at CERN
2.1
Motivation for tele-robotics
CERN’s experiments involve colliding particles beams together or into stationary targets. When this
happens some of the particles release radiation. Radiation only occurs at CERN when the particle beam is on,
and turning it off stops the emissions immediately. Radiation is sometimes causing some of the components
surrounding the collision points and the components interacting with the particles throughout the accelerators
to become radioactive.
Since the LHC experiments are underground, this provides a natural barrier to radiation. CERN is very
committed to ALARA (As Low As Reasonably Achievable) implying a job and dose planning for works in
CERN’s radiation areas aiming to minimise the dose to the workers. Ionising radiation, along with oxygen
deﬁciency and high magnetic ﬁelds, are the main hazards to which maintenance personnel are exposed to
when working in the LHC tunnels or other experimental areas at CERN [33].
Remotely controlled mobile robots, able to carry out maintenance work and inspections, are nowadays
considered in applications in hostile and hazardous environments in order to reduce the human intervention.
CERN has identiﬁed this need, and is in the process of developing several devices for remote inspection,
radiation monitoring and machinery maintenance in order to minimize the personnel exposure to hazards.
These remote operation robotic systems possess the autonomy capabilities for easing part of the workload
for human operators conducting the remote interventions or inspections. Radiation surveys and visual
inspections from a remote location are of great beneﬁt to the Radio Protection personnel at CERN. Generally,
electronics can take a radiation dosage of up to 100 Sievert (Sv) [34], which is 5000 times the annual dose
allowed by the European Union, and 50000 times the dose allowed by CERN for personnel as internal rule,
making possible tele-robotics in radiation areas. From an analysis of ALARA interventions, about 50% of
the dose to personnel was originated from very simple actions, such as visual inspections. By using robots
the processes of measuring and inspecting can be automated, decreasing the radiation dose of the personnel
and improving the quality of the obtained data. At the moment, the objectives for the robotic projects at
CERN include radiation inspection, remote machine measurements, automated visual detection of anomalies
in the underground areas and autonomous and tele-operated manipulation for machine maintenance.

3.1
Survey of LHC components
The LHC is composed of more than 2000 components the are interconnected, in which protons circulate
in two tubes and collide in the four experiments. In order to allow the particle beams to circulate, all the
components in the beam line must be perfectly aligned. The initial alignment of the LHC components was
done using a geodetic reference network that was determined from the position of the magnets of the LEP
(Large Electron Positron), that was located in the same tunnel of the LHC and run from 1989 until 2000.
The alignment of the LEP components had been improved during the years and its position, before the
dismantling in 2000, was considered the best achievable [36].
The geodetic network is composed of about 500 points sealed in the ﬂoor, which position was determined
from the alignment of the main quadrupole magnets of the LEP. Levelling measurements, horizontal angles,
offsets with respect to a stretched wire, gyroscopic measurements, etc. were combined using the least square
method in order to determine the coordinates of the LHC geodetic network. For the ﬁrst alignment of the
LHC, all the components were aligned with respect to the geodetic network, achieving a very high accuracy
[37].
In order to minimise residual misalignments, global surveys have been done during the shut-down
periods. The instruments and technologies used for the surveys are the same as the ones used for the ﬁrst
LHC alignment, with the addition of some new techniques in every shut-down, like the use of stretched
wires for alignment in the horizontal direction, achieving a precision and accuracy never reached before [38]
during the ﬁrst long shut-down period in 2009.

The ability to carry out visual inspection, measurement and handling activities remotely during the

measurement system for the alignment of the collimators was needed. Conventional measurements of an

team of 3 people. The high radiation level at this area (up to 4 mSv/h) makes vital the remote execution of
these measurements. Based on the monorail-mounted TIM train, the ﬁrst prototype called "LHC Collimator

4.1
Survey robotic systems
Surveying can be deﬁned as the science, art and technology of determining the relative positions of
points above, on or beneath the earth’s surface, and of establishing such points. Surveying is a discipline
which encompasses all methods for measuring and collecting information about the physical earth and the
environment, processing that information, and disseminating a variety of resulting products to a wide range
of clients. Surveying is mainly used in locating property boundaries, construction layout and map-making,
and involves work with elements of geometry, trigonometry, regression analysis, physics, engineering and
metrology, among others [40].
Underground surveying presents many differences from surveying on the surface. In tunnelling or mining
operations the conditions may complicate it, like high or low temperatures, humidity, darkness, dust, dirt
and dangerous hazards [41]. For these reasons, improvements in efﬁciency in surveying work carried out
in underground areas are of high difﬁculty, as well as the installation of surveying instruments and the
collection of data, which are still done manually in many places, and are very likely to cause errors in the
measurement results.
Mobile robots for industrial inspection are the most suitable solution for remote survey and inspection
in underground areas, for better access to tight or remote places and for replacing humans in dangerous
tasks. In the case of underground surveying, robotic solutions aim as well at improving the efﬁciency and
saving time. Survey robots can perform tasks such as transportation and installation of surveying equipment,
recording data and calculating measurements [42].

4.2
Photogrammetry
Photogrammetry is deﬁned as the science of obtaining reliable measurements, by the use of photographs,
in order to determine characteristics such as size, shape and position of photographed objects. Three-
dimensional photogrammetric calculations make possible to measure an object in three dimensions, using a
set of photographs taken from different angles [48].
The applications of photogrammetry are widely spread. It is mainly used for object interpretation and
object measurement. Aerial photogrammetry is mainly used to produce large scale topographical maps and
digital terrain models, representing the terrain relief. Among the users of close-range photogrammetry are
architects and civil engineers (supervising buildings, document their current state, deformations ordamages),
archaeologists, geophysicists, geodesists or police departments (documentation of trafﬁc accidents and crime
scenes).
The two fundamental elements (measurements) used to derive spatial positions are: direction and distance.
These elements can be obtained either by direct observation, or indirectly, by measuring a quantity which is
a function of these elements. Images can be used for spatial positioning, and measurements made in images
are also considered indirect observations with respect to the two fundamental elements [49].
Images are created by projection, a process or technique of reproducing a spatial object upon a plane
or curved surface by straight lines, which originate at the object, and are intersected by the surface. If two
points are joined by a straight line, one point is said to be projected upon the other. Projection is a subset of
transformation, which means a change in position, size, shape and orientation. Projection is also considered
mapping, which means to assign to every element of a set a corresponding element in another set. Projections
can be constructed mathematically, graphically and physically. The physical construction of projections is
performed by various optical or mechanical devices, such as a photographic camera.
Images provide much more information than what is required for spatial positioning. In fact they
comprise three distinct kinds of data:
• Geometric data (direction and distance), which indicate the spatial relationship between image and
object points, and are used to reconstruct spatial positions.

Robotic arm control
A robotic arm is considered one component in an overall robotic system. A complete robotic system is
composed by the robotic arm or manipulator, the external power source, the end-of-arm tooling, the external
and internal sensors, the computer interface, the control computer and the programmed software (Figure

The survey system used in the development of this project consists of two robotic arms that must be

position control, velocity control, forwards and inverse kinematics and trajectory or path planning.

4.3.3
Forward and inverse kinematics
Robot kinematics refers to the analytical study of the motion of a robotic arm or manipulator. In order to
analyse the behaviour of industrial manipulators, the suitable kinematics models for the robot mechanism
have to be formulated. Two different spaces can be used in kinematics modelling, cartesian space or
quaternion space. The transformation between two cartesian coordinate systems can be decomposed into a
rotation and a translation. Some ways of representing rotation are Euler angles, axis and angle, orthonormal
matrices and quaternions, among others. Homogeneous transformations are the most used in robotics.
Denavit and Hartenberg showed that a transformation between two joints require four parameters (DH
parameters), which have become the standard for describing robot kinematics.
Robot kinematics are divided into forward and inverse kinematics. Forward kinematics problem is to
determine the position and orientation of the end-effector given the values for the joint variables of the robot.
The calculations are straightforward and there is always a forward kinematic solution for a manipulator.
Inverse kinematics problem is to determine the values of the joint variables given the end-effector’s position
and orientation.
A robotic arm or robot manipulator is composed of a set of links connected by joints. Simplest joints are
rotational or prismatic joints, and more complex can be a ball and socket joint. Rotational or revolute joints
allow a relative rotation about a single axis, and prismatic or translational joints permit a linear motion along
a single axis. Both rotational and translational joints have only one degree-of-freedom of motion. In the
case of the complex joints, like a ball and socket, the joint has two degrees of freedom [50]. In this master’s
thesis, the robotic arms used were composed of one rotational joint and one translational joint.
There are two main solution techniques for the inverse kinematics problem, analytical and numerical
methods. In the ﬁrst type, the joint variables are solved analytically according to given conﬁguration data. In
the second type of solution, the joint variables are obtained based on the numerical techniques. There are
==PAGE:45==

two approaches in analytical method: geometric and algebraic solutions. Geometric approach is applied to
the simple robot structures, such as two degree-of-freedom robotic arms, as the arms involved in this project.
For the manipulators with more links and whose arms extend into three dimensions or more the geometry
gets more complex, and an algebraic approach is more beneﬁcial for the inverse kinematics solution [51].
Only the geometric solution approach has been used in the development of this project, due to the

The geometric solution approach is based on decomposing the spatial geometry of the manipulator

The ﬁrst approach to the survey wagon system consisted in inverse engineering for retrieving all the

5.1
Speciﬁcations and constraints
The survey wagon is composed of two units, the wagon carrying the measurement equipment and the
robotic arms with the laser sensors, and the wagon carrying the electronics, such as the PLC CPU and
modules, and the motor controllers.
The robotic arms are composed of two joints each: one translational joint and one rotational joint. For
each joint there is a motor and an incremental encoder. The four motors are controlled with four Maxon
Motors 4-Q-DC Servoampliﬁers [52]. The controller’s inputs are connected to the digital and analogue
outputs of the PLC modules; therefore the entire control of the arms has been deployed on the PLC system,
which also guarantees a level of safety mandatory in this kind of applications.
Each joint is limited by end-stop switches for safety reasons. The arms must never reach the LHC
equipment for avoiding any possible damage, and must not harm themselves by reaching the physical ends
without stopping the motors.
For the development of this project, the conﬁguration of the wagon containing the electronics, the survey
control wagon, was re-designed. The main reason for this choice was the dimensions of the wagon, not
suitable for the space constraints imposed by the LHC (30 by 30 cm for passing sector and ventilation doors).
In fact, although the current arm system is too big to allow the door passage, the control wagon must also be
used for serving as control of other possible payload wagons for TIM, such as multi-axis robotic arms or ﬁre
extinguish wagons. Since the current survey measurement system is only necessary in limited sectors, it
does not need to travel all around the LHC and therefore will not need to pass any sector or ventilation door.
Since the survey system will take advantage of the battery, the communication, the positioning and the
safety of TIM, most of the electronics from the survey train prototype were not needed and only the PLC
CPU and modules, the motor controllers and the electrical protection components were re-used for the new
system.

5.1.1
Motor controllers
The system features four motor controllers, Maxon Motors 4-Q-DC Servoampliﬁers, one for each motor
of the arms. The four present controllers are connected to each motor following the same conﬁguration.
The motor controller is powered constantly in the same line as the rest of the system. In order to control
the motor it receives the following inputs from the PLC:
• Enable for the controller, sent from the PLC as a digital output bit and received from the controller as
24 V.
• + Set value, voltage between -10 and +10 V, sent from the PLC analogue output.
• - Set value, connected to the ground of the PLC.
When (+ Set value) > (- Set value) there is a positive set value and the motor shaft turns counter clockwise.
The same way, when (+ Set value) < (- Set value) there is a negative set value and the motor shaft turns
clockwise. The voltage received is ampliﬁed by the controller and sent to the motor through the + Motor
and - Motor outputs (Figure 5.1). By changing this voltage, the speed of the motor is changed. Since the
potentiometers of the motor controllers were found to be adjusted in different positions on each controller,
the voltage received by each motor is not the same if sending the same value from the analogue outputs of
the PLC. For this reason, the adjustments were made later in the PLC software.

Precision requirements
The former requirements for the photogrammetric system were a precision of the arm position on the

5.2
Joint position control
Once the encoders could be read by the counter modules of the PLC, the next step was the design of
the joint position control. By selecting a target position, the joint should move in the correct direction until
reaching the desired position, and then stop. The velocity of a joint depends on the + Set value voltage sent
to the motor controller form an analogue output of the PLC. This output is set from the PLC program as a
decimal value, which sign given in the program determines the direction of the motor, and therefore, the
direction of the joint. From now on, the decimal value on the low level of the PLC will be referred to as
velocity. For both translational joints, the positive value of velocity sent from the PLC will move the joint
towards the rail, upwards. The negative value of velocity will therefore move the joint towards the wire,
downwards (Figure 5.2(a)).
In the case of the rotational joints, due to the fact that the motors are physically inverted, the direction of
the movement is different for each joint if the velocities sent from the PLC have the same sign. Despite this
fact, the encoders for the rotational joints count both in the same direction, positive when rotating towards
the wire and negative when rotating towards the wall.
• For the left arm, the positive value of the velocity sent from the PLC will move the arm towards the
wall, backwards. The negative value of the velocity will move the arm towards the wire, frontwards.
• For the right arm, the positive value of velocity send from the PLC will move the arm towards the
wire, frontwards. The negative value of the velocity will move the arm towards the wall, backwards

5.5
Velocity controller
Up to this point, the velocity of the motors is sent from the PLC as a voltage, that the motor controllers
receive and convert into another voltage, sent to the motor according to the conﬁguration of the controller.
Nevertheless, the velocity of a joint (in m/s for the translational joints and rad/s for the rotational) is not
directly proportional to the assigned voltage, but depends on the arm position, direction of the movement and
controller conﬁguration, so a constant voltage does not guarantee a constant speed of the joint. In order to
guarantee a higher precision and to develop a trajectory planner for the arm, a velocity regulator is necessary.
A feedback control system has been designed and implemented for this purpose.
Given a reference velocity for a joint, the velocity controller receives in input the difference ev between
the reference velocity vref and the actual joint velocity vout in m/s or rad/s, multiplies the difference by a
constant gain and adds it to the actual controlled voltage Vcon (Figure 5.10).
Figure 5.10: Block diagram of the velocity control.
The actual velocity of the motor Vmotor is not directly measurable, but it is obtained through discrete
derivation from the encoder position Eout of the joint.
This velocity controller allows to set speciﬁc velocities for the joints, that is a fundamental requirement
for a trajectory planner.
5.6
Trajectory planning
In order to reach the wire with the robotic arms, the arms should follow a trajectory that would position
the laser sensor ﬁeld around the reference wire. Damaging or breaking the wire must be avoided, so this
trajectory must be followed without the arm touching it at any time.

The position of the wire in Arm coordinates was obtained by performing tests with the survey wagon in a
mock-up of the LHC tunnel. A reference wire was placed in front of a collimator, in the same position as the
wire should be in the LHC. Even if the rail on the tunnel ceiling differs in height from some LHC sectors
to others, for the areas of interest for the survey wagon, the height of the rail stays the same. This means
that the survey wagon will be always positioned at the same distance from the wire, with less than 2 cm of
difference between machines to measure. With the help of the laser sensors, the ﬁnal position for measuring
can be corrected if the wire is already in the sensor ﬁeld of view, that is of 2 by 2 cm.

With the wire position in Arm coordinates as input, the trajectory function (Figure 5.11) computes
the difference between the wire and the retracted position in Xarm and Yarm. After different trials of
trajectories, it was found that if the point Ohand follows a straight line until the wire position, the wire would
be approached successfully, since the hand rotates linearly at the same time, making the laser sensor ﬁeld of
view be smoothly positioned around the wire. For this reason, in the trajectory function, the distances to
move in Xarm and Yarm directions are divided by a number of steps that the arm must follow, and all the
divisions are stored in an array as target positions.
The target positions in Arm coordinates are converted into encoder positions through the inverse kine-

6.2
Program structure
The survey wagon software has been designed as a robust, re-usable program. The main reason for the
re-usability is the fact that in the future, more measurement or tele-manipulation wagons will be added to
TIM. With an already designed control wagon and software, the integration of a new measurement unit
will be straightforward, and only the low-level control of the speciﬁc measurement unit will have to be
implemented, either in the PLC software or in another type of PC communicating with the PLC. The new
measurement unit would then take the advantage of the safety, robustness and feasibility of the program
designed for the survey control wagon.
There are currently ﬁve different programming languages for Siemens PLC [54]. LAD (Ladder logic)
and FBD (Function block diagram) are graphic programming languages. STL (Statement list) textual
programming language enables the creation of hardware level runtime and memory optimized software
programs. GRAPH, also called SFC (Sequential function chart) is used for describing procedures with
alternative or parallel sequencers. And lastly, SCL (Structured control language), that is particularly suitable
for the programming of complex algorithms and arithmetic functions, as it is a simple, short and clear code
to produce and handle. For this reasons, the most used language in the development of this software was
SCL, complemented with some functions in LAD.
State machine
The program is divided in states. The main state machine contains ﬁve states: initialization, conﬁguration,
idle, measurement and error. The survey wagon system will be always in one of those ﬁve states (Figure 6.4).
• Initialization (INIT): This is the ﬁrst state for the survey wagon when the PLC is initialized. In this
state, the program checks the status of TIM and if it is ready for a measurement, the status of the
sensors and the motor controllers. The calibration of the encoders is also executed in this state. When
the arms are in the retracted position, and the status checks are positive, the program passes to the
Conﬁguration state.
==PAGE:68==
50
CHAPTER 6. SYSTEM IMPLEMENTATION
• Conﬁguration (CONFIG): In this state, the program is waiting for an input from the operator. Many
different conﬁgurations can be featured in this state (machines to measure, area to measure, etc.), but
since no measurement missions have been determined yet, the operator will only be able for now to
select the type of control of the arms, manual or automatic. When the control is selected, the program
passes to Idle state.
• Idle (IDLE): In this state the program is waiting until it receives the command to start a measurement,
when it passes to Measure state.
• Measurement (MEASURE): This is the state in which the complete measurement is taken. From the
movement of TIM, to the position of the arms on the wire and the triggering of the photogrammetric
system. Containing another state machine inside, the Measure state involves the complete control of the
robotic arms, as well as the management of the photogrammetric measurement and the communication
with TIM.
• Error (ERROR): Warnings and errors can occur at any part of the program. The possible errors have
been identiﬁed and coded, so if at any of the previous states an error occurs, the program passes to
error state until it is solved. The coding of the errors makes them identiﬁable, so the operator can
receive feedback about them and act in consequence.
Figure 6.4: State machine diagram.
Measurement state machine
The Measure state machine contains ﬁve states: idle, initialization, TIM moving, move arms to the wire and
perform photogrammetric measurement. Inside the main Measure state, the survey wagon program will be,
at the same time, in one of these ﬁve states.

• Idle (MeasureIDLE): In this state, the program is waiting for a measure to start. Even though it is
not needed in the ﬁrst measurement of a series, it is useful when one measurement will be followed by
another in what is called a mission. In between measurements, the program will be in MeasureIDLE

• Initialize measurement (MeasureINIT): When a measurement starts, the program performs the

6.4
Safety
The integration of a new system to the TIM train always has the advantages of TIM’s safety, thanks to
the laser scanners and safety bumpers for avoiding collisions. Therefore, a safe communication with TIM
has to be established. However, the safety needed for the survey wagon system must be taken into account
as well. The two robotic arms must under any circumstance damage any of the equipment in the LHC. For
this reason, many features of the program in the survey wagon ensure a safe use with limitations on the
movements and velocities.
==PAGE:71==
6.5. ERROR HANDLING
53
Limitation of the arm joints
End-stop limit switches are installed at both ends of the rotational joints, and at the minimum end of the
translational joints. In the automatic arm control, the movement is limited to this switches in the joint
position control. For the manual arm control, where the velocity can be selected and commanded by the
operator, if a limit switch is activated the operator will only be able to send the velocity for the joint to move
in the opposite direction. In the case of the lower limit of the translational joint, since there was no physical
possibility of installing a limit switch, the limit is done in the hardware by setting a maximum reachable
length of the translation, in order to avoid possible contacts or collisions with the LHC components, such as
the collimators.
Limitation of the velocity
The velocity controller function includes limitations on the velocities that can be sent from the PLC to the
motors. This way, possible damages of the arm motors are avoided, as well as sudden movements that can
break the reference wire.
Fail-safe PLC program
The fail-safe Siemens PLC are used to guarantee the functional safety of machines or plants. With only
one CPU, both the standard and the safety program can be handled simultaneously. In the case of the
survey wagon, the safety program was included for preventing the components to get electrical damages.
The fail-safe digital input module detects wire breaks and the fail-safe digital output module triggers the
contactors if necessary, so the system can be protected.
6.5
Error handling
When the survey wagon is performing a measurement remotely, different type of warnings and errors
can occur. The error handling is part of the standard program and not coupled with the fail-safe program.
The states in the state machines of the program described in Section 6.2 include an error or warning code
that is activated in the different cases that can be considered errors or warnings. When an error or warning
occurs in any state, the program passes to the ERROR state, in which the errors are handled. Every type of
error in every state has an error code, which is an integer variable created for each state, and different values
for the variable correspond to the different possible errors. Therefore, when the program passes to the error
state, the error code variables are read and, if one or more are different than zero, the value is compared in
order to identify the type of error that has occurred, being each variable an indentiﬁer of the previous state of
the program as well.
After being identiﬁed, different type of errors have different ways to be handled. Examples of errors and
how they are handled are listed below:
• The calibration is executed in the initialization of the program. A counter measures the time that the
calibration takes until all the joints have arrived to the zero position. If this time exceeds 100 seconds
then a warning would be sent to the operator, stating that the calibration was not successful in the
ﬁrst attempt, and the calibration will be initiated again. If the warning is repeated three times, it will
be considered an error and the operator will have to intervene. When it is solved, the operator can
initialize the system again, and if the calibration succeeds, the error code will be zero.
• When a measurement is commanded to start, the system checks ﬁrst the status of the communication
with TIM, the status of the laser sensors and the status of the robotic arms. When all the status are ok,
the system is ready to measure. But if a measurement is commanded to start, and any of the conditions
==PAGE:72==
54

are not met, the program will go to error state and generate the corresponding warning for the operator.
Then, the MeasureINIT will be restarted, and if the conditions are not met again, the program will
go to error state and generate a second warning. If this is repeated for a third time, the operator will
receive the error feedback and the program will stay in error state until the corresponding error is
solved.

7.1
Test for velocity control
The velocity control has been tested for both joints of one arm. The purpose of this test was to verify
how the voltage sent to the motor is adjusted by the controller depending on the reference velocity, and how
the system responds to variations of the reference velocity.
Different velocities were tested for each joint, in order to evaluate the different responses of the controller.
In addition, two more tests were performed: the response to step variation of the reference and the
response to ramp variation of the reference.

7.1.2
Step variation response
For this test, the input reference velocity was being varied and the response of the controller was analysed,
ﬁrst for the translational joint and later for the rotational joint.
The reference velocity was varied from 0.7 cm/s to 1.5 cm/s for the translation, and from 7 mrad/s to
15 mrad/s for the rotation. The results can be seen in Figure 7.5(a) and in Figure 7.5(b), where it can be
observed that the response time is quite long. This is an effect that has been taken into consideration for the
design of the higher level controls, in which not sudden changes in velocity should be necessary, unless for
starting to move the joint. Nevertheless this effect can be decreased by increasing the running frequency of
the controller, that at the moment is executed every 150 ms. This limit is imposed by the maximum scan
cycle of the PLC. Even if the average scan cycle of this program is much lower than the maximum, it must
not be allowed that the PLC skips the velocity controller for one cycle.
7.1.3
Ramp response
For this test, the input reference velocity was linearly increasing from zero to the maximum value, and
the response of the controller was analysed.
The results for both joints are shown in Figure 7.6(a) and Figure 7.6(b). The reference velocity was
linearly increased in order to check if the response of the system could follow it. From the results it was
observed that if the reference changes in a smoother way, the controller is able to adjust better and faster the
output velocity.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In this master’s thesis, an automatic control system for precise alignment of LHC components has
been presented. The system uses two robotic arms for positioning the references for a remote sensing
photogrammetric system, to measure precise alignments in the LHC machinery. It has been proved in this
thesis that this automatic system allows to perform the operation in a more safe and user-friendly way than
the previous prototype, that was controlled manually by an operator. It also allows to perform the operation
during the running periods of the LHC machine in case of extraordinary misalignment cases. This system
allows to reduce the human exposure to hazards, by permitting the measurements to be done remotely,
without exposing the personnel to high radiation levels.
It has to be noted that for the development of this thesis, only existing hardware has been used, thanks to
a big inverse engineering process which allowed to make the previous choices compatible with the current
project goals. No documentation of the already existing system was available, and the system was found
in a non working status. Restoring the main functionalities of the system has been a huge challenge that
allowed also to set the roadmap of the future steps in the development. Once the functionalities were restored,
the features and limitations of the system were detected. The encoders, for example, allow very accurate
positioning of the joints, while the weight of the arms, together with non appropriate motors, do not allow
perfectly smooth movements. For this reason a velocity controller has been developed over the already
existing motor controllers, in order to stabilize the velocity of the joints, a necessary feature for an accurate
trajectory planning.
A study of the mechanics of the system was required in order to develop precise forward and inverse
kinematics. Furthermore, a system integration with the already existing PXI, used for managing the
photogrammetric system and for reading the laser sensors, has been done. This integration plays an
important role in correcting the ﬁnal position of the robotic arms, so they can be precisely allocated on the
reference wire for photogrammetric measurement of collimator misalignments.
Finally, the solution has been tested in order to validate the control system proposed in this master’s
thesis. Even though the limitations of the existing hardware were conﬁrmed, such as the effect of the gravity
that limits the possible velocity of a joint, a very high precision in position was achieved for both types of
joints, and therefore, for the complete robotic arm. With the trajectory planner and the position correction
with the laser sensors, the system can position the arms on the reference wire with the expected precision.
The work on the survey wagon project for this master’s thesis will be continued at CERN with the goal
of achieving remote and autonomous inspection of hazardous environments.

CHAPTER 9
Further work
The main objective of this master’s thesis was to design and implement a safe and precise control system
for the survey wagon measurement unit, by controlling the two robotic arms in order to position the laser
sensors on the reference wire achieving very high precision in the ﬁnal position. Being the objective achieved,
the work for this master’s thesis was the ﬁrst step in the implementation of the survey measurement system
in the LHC. The future steps on the continuation of this project at CERN are summarized in the following
list:
• Test the ﬁnal solution of this master’s thesis with the photogrammetric system attached to the survey
wagon, and correct the orientation of the robotic arms with the help of inclinometer sensors and
according to the speciﬁcations of the protogrammetry.
• Test the complete measurement in front of a collimator and reference magnet and update the pho-
togrammetric measurement.
• In order to make the system more robust to height variation of the wire, due to imperfections on the
rail, an automatic recognition of the position of the wire with respect to wagon using camera could be
done.
• Build the new survey control wagon as a 30 by 30 cm standard TIM wagon.
• Integrate the complete survey wagon (including control unit and measurement unit) to TIM, conﬁguring
a safe PLC to PLC communication, and test in the LHC tunnel mock-up. In addition, merge the survey
wagon GUI with the TIM GUI.
• Once the system is integrated with TIM, the movement on the rail of the wagon can be added to the
arm control system as an additional degree of freedom
• Design a measurement solution with multi-axis robotic arms.
