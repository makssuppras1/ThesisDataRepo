This master thesis is written as the ﬁnal requirement in order to obtain the Master of Science degree
in Biomedical Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. The thesis is conducted together with

Abstract
Cardiovascular diseases are one of the leading causes of death each year, creating a desire and need
for better diagnostic tools and treatments. Therefore, it is desirable to investigate the ﬂuid dynamics
and solid mechanics of the human heart. Due to the complex anatomy and physiology of the heart,
previous literature has focused mainly on investigating one isolated area of the heart. Therefore, several
studies examine either the left or right ventricle with the use of either computational ﬂuid dynamics or
ﬂuid-structure interactions, but only a few full cardiac models exist. Therefore, a bi-ventricular ﬂuid-
structure interaction model has been created and investigated in this thesis.
Through segmentation of clinical data in MATLAB R2021b, meshes have been extracted corre-
sponding to the anatomically correct ventricles of the phantom by Shelley Medical Imaging Technolo-
gies. A computer-aided design model has successfully been created in SolidWorks® based on the ex-
tracted meshes. Inlets, outlets, and the solid domain surrounding the ventricles have been created in
SolidWorks®. In COMSOL Multiphysics®, the CAD model was converted into an ﬂuid-structure inter-
action model with applied material parameters extracted from the literature. The constitutive relations
are based on the assumptions of the solid domain being modelled as a nearly-incompressible neo-Hookean
material and the ﬂuid domain governed by a laminar homogeneous ﬂow and the Navier-Stokes equation.
The boundary layer is solved with the use of ﬁnite element method and the arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian
formulation.
The ﬁnal results showed similarities with reported phenomenons and values in the literature for sev-
eral ﬂuid and solid parameters.
The ﬁnal model is proposed as a duplication of the DHP-01 phantom. The ﬁnal model creates a
foundation for future experiments and anatomically correct model adjustments. Furthermore, additional
improvements of the model design and limitations of this thesis have been provied.

1.1. Motivation
1.1
Motivation
The heart is responsible for the circulation of blood and maintenance of life from creation to the end.
Each year, cardiovascular disease (CVD) are the leading cause of death with 874,613 registered deaths
in 2019 in the United States of America (USA) alone [11][12][13]. CVD covers conditions aﬀecting both
the heart as well as the blood vessels ranging from atherosclerosis to damages of the heart and other
organs[12]. With a global health problem like CVD, it is desirable to further investigate and understand
the heart and its mechanisms [14].
Advancements have been made within the ﬁeld of cellular and molecular biology in order to understand
the physiology and structures inﬂuences of the heart at a microscopic level, but only few contributions
have been made to the further understanding of the importance and complexity of the pump function. As
the main purpose of the heart is to pump and circulate the blood, ﬂuid dynamics plays a crucial role in
understanding and modelling of the human heart. By knowing how the heart ﬁlls and eject comes a pos-
sibility of predicting new physiological phenomena and disease inﬂuence in the clinic [15][16]. Diﬀerent
cardiac models have been proposed through the past 30 years, which include diﬀerent assumptions and
ways to model the human heart. A great advantage is to use computer aids in the modeling of the human
heart. With computer simulations investigating computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) or ﬂuid-structure
interaction (FSI) models, it is possible to investigate the the ﬂuid dynamics of the heart, as well as the
solid mechanics of the surrounding tissue [17]. The CFD model allows for a thorough investigation of
the ﬂuid domain and the material parameters surrounding it. The FSI model poses the opportunity to
include the solid domain and, thereby, investigate the moving boundary between the domains.
Previous models presented in the literature have focused primarily on the left ventricle, as this is the
ﬁnal chamber before the oxygenated blood reenters the systemic circulation. Furthermore, the asym-
metric lunar shape of the right ventricle has created a challenge in medical imaging and model creation,
compared to the elongated left ventricle [18]. As both ventricles take part in the CVD and following
diagnosis, it is of great advantage to include both ventricles in cardiac studies. However, due to the
anatomically diﬀerences between subjects, the electrodynamics and the diﬃculties of doing in vivo stud-
ies, cardiac phantoms are used for research purposes of the ﬂuid and solid dynamics.
This thesis will contribute to the ongoing research of the Shelley Medical Imaging Technologies’ bi-
ventricular phantom at the Center of Fast Ultrasound (CFU). A computer aided design (CAD) model
is replicated from scans of the phantom, and a FSI model will be evaluated based on the ﬁnite element
method (FEM) and appropriate boundary conditions. The ﬁnal FSI model will work in addition to
the physical phantom and can help facilitate the further investigation of the ﬂuid dynamics and solid
mechanics for future cardiac studies.
1.2
Thesis Deﬁnition and Objectives
This master thesis revolves around the poly-vinyl alcohol (PVA) in-house bi-ventricular heart phantom
model at the CFU at the Technical University of Denmark. As the heart phantom enables full control of
the motion, a simulation model based on the phantom can be created. A computational geometry is cre-
ated based on ultrasound images of the phantom, and the simulation software COMSOL Multiphysics®
is used to study the ﬂuid dynamics and solid mechanics of the simulated phantom. The following objec-
tives create the foundation and framework of the thesis:
I. Perform experiments and collect data from the in house heart phantom model.
II. Investigation of the biomechanical properties of the in house heart phantom model and formulation
of constitutive equations related to this.
III. Quantiﬁcation and application of boundary conditions for the created simulation model.

2.1. The Human Heart
2.1
The Human Heart
2.1.1
Anatomy and Physiology
The heart consists of the four chambers, the right and left atria and the right and left ventricles, as
illustrated in Figure 2.1. The right atrium retrieves deoxygenated blood from the body from the inferior
vena cava and superior vena cava as well as deoxygenated blood from the myocardium of the heart from
the coronary sinus. From the right atrium, the blood ﬂows into the right ventricle through the tricuspid
valve. The right ventricle contracts and eject the deoxygenated blood through the pulmonary semilunar
valves and into the pulmonary trunk towards the lungs as part of the pulmonary system.
Through
the pulmonary veins, the left atrium receives the oxygenated blood and transfers it to the left ventricle
through bicuspid valve. The left ventricle ejects the oxygenated blood from the heart into the aortic
arch through the aortic valves. The interventricular septum separates the right and left ventricles from
one another with a muscular part at the apex of the heart and extending into a membranous part when
reaching the atria. When examining the ventricles, the left ventricle accounts for the largest volume in
the heart and the wall thickness of the left ventricle is larger compared to the right ventricle as visible
in Figure 2.2. This allows for greater contractions and distribution of the blood with a higher pressure,
compared to the right ventricle [1][19].
Figure 2.1: Cross-sectional illustration of the human heart and anatomy from an anterior view. Illustration from
[1]
The heart wall is composed of three main layers as illustrated in Figure 2.3.
The epicardium is
the outer layer and acts as a protective membrane for the heart with its 100 µm thickness. It consists
primarily of epimysal collagen and some elastin. The center part of the heart wall, the myocardium, is
the thickest part of the heart wall and is composed of cardiac muscle cells, which are responsible for the
contractility of the heart. The cardiac cells constitute the muscle ﬁbers, which are arranged in parallel
bundles with diﬀerent orientation depending on the location. As it can be seen in Figure 2.4, the ﬁber
orientations of the atria are towards the longitudinal axis of the heart, whereas the ﬁbers travel downward
clockwise towards the apex of the right ventricle, only to turn and travel upwards counter-clockwise of
the left ventricle.[20] The lining of the inner wall of each of the heart chambers is called the endocardium
and is approximately 100 µm in thickness. It consists of epimysal collagen, elastin and endothelial cells.
The endothelial cells line the boundary between the heart wall and blood of the heart [1][19].

2.1.2
Cardiac Cycle
Two phases create the cardiac cycle; the systole and diastole. The systole phase represents the period
of ventricular contraction and outﬂow of blood, whereas the diastole refers to the period of ventricular
relaxation and inﬂow of blood from the atria. As illustrated in Figure 2.5, each of the phases in the
cardiac cycle can be further divided into subphases. The isovolumetric contraction is the ﬁrst part of
the systole, where the tricuspid and mitral valves close and the pulmonary and aortic valves remain
closed. With all valves closed, the rise in ventricular pressures result in the ventricular ejection caused
by cardiac contractions, once the pressures are higher than those of the pulmonary artery and aorta.
The isovolumetric relaxation commence the diastole, where the intraventricular pressures drop below the
atria pressure, resulting in the closing of the pulmonary and aortic valves and opening of the tricuspid
and mitral valve. With a higher atrial pressure, the pressure gradient will facilitate the passive ventric-
ular ﬁlling, and atrial contractions will then terminate the diastole and ensure the atria are completely

2.2
State of the Art
The reviewed articles originates from the investigation presented in the pre-thesis project Simulation
Methods and CAD Modelling of the Heart: A Structures Literature Search on Computational Models
of Fluid and Solid Mechanics of the Human Heart [10]. The project examined how the numerical car-
diac models, the moving boundary and boundary conditions had previously been used and deﬁned. An
overview of the reviewed articles can be seen in Appendix C, Table C.1.
The investigated numerical simulations vary in level of anatomically correct details included and
their way of modeling the heart. In 2000, McQueen et Peskin [20] created a three-dimensional (3D)
cone-shaped model of the human heart, where the focus was to implement the ﬁber structure of the
cardiac wall based on the geodesic paths shown in Figure 2.4. This implementation of ﬁbers created
interpretation problems, when the ﬂuid dynamics were investigated. A clear interpretation of the veloc-
ity ﬁeld could not be easily conducted. Even with the assumption that the cardiac wall is only created
7
==PAGE:27==
2.2. State of the Art
by one layer, the geodesic paths disturbed the interpretation of the ﬂuid dynamics. Both Watanabe et
al. [17] and Sugiura et al. [16] investigated the incorporation of a multilayer cardiac wall consisting of
ﬁbers and sheets with diﬀerent orientations. Results were not disturbed by this incorporation. Holzapfel
et al. [19] investigated a numerical method for the incorporation of the passive myocardium, where
the myocardium structure of ﬁbers and sheets were modelled and created a theoretical foundation for
future models. In the 2005 paper by Cheng et al. [21] the cardiac wall of the left ventricle is modelled
as thin-walled, isotropic and homogeneous as well as the time-varying Young’s modulus is accountable
for the nonlinear changes in the wall. This is disproved in [19], who characterizes the myocardium as
orthotropic based on the orientations of the ﬁbers and sheets.
Most of the studies simplify the inlet and outlet of the model by neglecting the cardiac valves
[15][16][17][20][21].
McQueen et Peskin [20] argued, that the valves could be neglected as these are
passive structures without any muscle. The valves are only attached to the cardiac muscle and their mo-
tion is based on the interaction with the blood. Watanabe et al. [17] neglected the cardiac valves as well,
but argued that they should be investigated in future models, as they are part of the atrio-ventricular
valve plane and inﬂuence the blood ﬂow and velocity. Furthermore, in the clinic, the displacement of the
cardiac atrio-ventricuar valve plane provides an indication of the cardiac function.[17]
The diﬀerent studies evaluated the CFD and FSI models with similar constitutive relations and
boundary conditions. For the CFD models, only constitutive equations of the ﬂuid domain are solved
with the applied boundary conditions. The movement of the boundary can then be formulated by ex-
tracted medical imaging data [22] or by implementing mathematical theories. Kovács et al. [15] and
McQueen et Peskin [20] conducted a CFD analysis with the implementation of the theoretical immersed
boundary condition as the moving boundary. FSI models have primarily used the arbitrary Lagrangian-
Eulerian (ALE) formulation to describe the moving boundary as seen in [16], [17] and [21]. Common for
all studies are the assumption that the ﬂuid is Newtonian and governed by the Navier-Stokes equation
as the constitutive equation. Contrary, diﬀerent methods have been used to describe the cardiac wall
and solid mechanics. Watanabe et al. [17] assumed it was a hyperelastic material an used the passive
part of the Lin-Yin model [23] to describe it.
Cheng et al.[21] describe it as an isotropic, homoge-
neous, elastic material with a zero density and incorporated a time-varying Young’s modulus to account
for non-linear changes in elasticity. Zhu et al. [8] uses the neo-Hookean model as the constitutive relation.
Due to the complex anatomy and physiology of the heart and the problems and considerations de-
scribed in these previous paragraphs, only a limited number of cardiac models representing the whole
heart exist. Alone the inclusion and modeling of both ventricles and a simpliﬁed myocardium in a FSI
model are a complex and demanding process. Therefore, it is the intention to create a fully functional
cardiac model representing the ventricles with the implementation of the relevant physics. Implementa-
tion of assumptions such as, a single homogeneous layer representing the myocardium, exclusion of the
valves and simpliﬁcation of the inlet and outlet of each ventricle can facilitate the creation of a converging
cardiac FSI model. Ideally, this model will be representative within the given simpliﬁcations and create
the foundation for incorporating more anatomically correct details in future models.

2.3. Constitutive Relations
2.3
Constitutive Relations
This section presents the constitutive relations concerning the creation of the phantom simulation. The
governing constitutive relations and theory regarding the ﬂuid dynamics and solid mechanics of the ven-
tricles and the solid part of the phantom will be presented in the following sections.
2.3.1
Solid Dynamics
The solid dynamics describe the behavior of the solid part of the phantom and are derived from the
continuum mechanics.
Previous master student at CFU Morgane S. D. Garreau investigated the most beneﬁcial governing
equations of the phantom’s solid dynamics in her thesis Biomechanical Modelling of the Right Ventricle
[5]. Through two diﬀerent studies, the solid part of the phantom was modeled as either a linear elastic
homogeneous isotropic (LEHI) material or a hyperelastic nearly incompressible neo-Hookean material.
The study of the LEHI material showed that the chosen constitutive relations caused problems with the
simulation where the linear strains had to be forced in Comsol Multiphysics without suﬃcient results.
When examining the hyperelastic nearly incompressible neo-Hookean material, the study showed that
these constitutive relations provided the best results and fewest problems with the implementation in
Comsol Multiphysics compared to the LEHI material model. Following the results presented by Morgane
S. D. Garreau, it is decided to model the solid part of the phantom as a hyperelastic nearly incompress-
ible neo-Hookean material.[5][24]

incompressible hyperelastic material, the stress is characterized by being derived from the strain energy
function  and only depending on the deformation gradient tensor ¯¯F, as seen in (2.1).

Vorticity and the Formation of Vortex Rings
As the outer layers of the ﬂuid ﬂow engage with the wall of the solid domain, a boundary layer will
occur. Through the boundary layer, the eﬀect of the wall is decreased towards the ﬂuid. Thus, as the
wall velocity is equal to zero, the maximum ﬂuid ﬂow velocity at the center of a vessel will rapidly
decrease, when moving through the boundary layer towards the wall. The boundary layer is considered
a shear layer, where an elongated distribution of vorticity occurs. [18] The vorticity is deﬁned as the
curl of the velocity ﬁeld and is a measure of the velocity gradient ∆v between adjacent layers. Vorticity
is given by (2.19),

near sharp edges, the shear layer will curl back up towards the inﬂow, forming spiraling structures. Due
to the circular shape of the oriﬁces of the ventricles, vortex rings will form. The vortex rings are a
axis-symmetrical, three-dimensional vortex torus shaped vortex and is usually seen in the left ventricle,
due to the inlet ﬂow.[18]

[29] The λ2 criterion deﬁnes the vortex rings by parting the velocity gradient into a symmetrical and

2.4
FEM and the ALE Formulation
The FEM is used to solve complex physical problems by discretization of the continuum and solving the
problem numerically. The process is illustrated in Figure 2.6 with additional comments regarding the
implementation within this thesis. The method subdivides the model into non-overlapping continuous
elements of ﬁnite size, thus discretizing the continuum. The physical problem is then solved with ei-
ther the strong or the weak formulation. The strong formulation must satisfy the governing diﬀerential
equations through the entire model, resulting in a complex problem. Contrary, the weak formulation
implies a numerical approximation of the exact solution within each ﬁnite element. Neighboring elements
connected by nodes share respected continuities and overall identical boundary conditions. Thereby, the
complex physical problem is converted from inﬁnite degrees of freedom to a mesh consisting of elements
with ﬁnite degrees of freedom. A degree of uncertainty is introduced in the model with the use of FEM
and is dependent on the number of elements in the mesh. The uncertainty can be lowered by the in-
clusion of additional elements. However, the number of elements inﬂuences the computational time and
therefore, the ﬁnal number of elements are a compromise of model accuracy and computational time.
[4][30]
13
==PAGE:33==
2.4. FEM and the ALE Formulation
Figure 2.6: The process of the FEM. Illustration inspired by [4].
The complex physical problems are described diﬀerently depending on the applied mathematical ap-
proach. The partial diﬀerential equations are either formulated in spatial or material coordinate systems.
The spatial coordinate system is referred to as the Eulerian formulation, where coordinates are ﬁxed in
space. The material coordinates are referred to as the Lagrangian formulation, where the material co-
ordinates are ﬁxed to the material and follow the deformation of the material as well. The Eulerian
formulation is usually used to simulate ﬂuid behavior, as the quantitative parameters rather than spe-
ciﬁc points are of interest. Contrary, the Lagrangian formulation is applied to solid materials and is
especially advantageous in the simulations of stress and strain.[31][32]
As described in most literature[14][16][17][21], the moving boundary between the domains is modeled
with the ALE method. This method implements a mesh coordinate system, which follows the implemen-
tation of ﬂuid dynamics as well as solid mechanics. It creates a mesh coordinate system that accounts
for the boundary between the Eulerian and Lagrangian formulation and does not require the mesh to
follow the material.[32]
In COMSOL Multiphysics®, the FEM is solved with the use of the weak formulation. Here, the
ALE formulation is used to discretize the continuum and the constitutive relations are solved with the
implemented boundary conditions at each node of the ﬁnite elements.

placed behind the ﬁrst. The stacked images create a 3D matrix, where the dimensions are based on

each ventricle. The active contour (AC) algorithm uses the energy constraints and forces to separate
regions and create boundaries. The AC algorithm is applied to the three 2D masks to improve the matrix
segmentation and create a 3D volume as presented in Figure 3.5 and 3.6 for the left and right ventricle
respectively. The MATLAB script for the segmentation of the left ventricle can be found in Appendix

3.4.5
Mesh Construction
When considering a FSI simulation, the implemented mesh is required to move with the boundary of
the model. A deformed mesh allows for the boundary to move with time or deform with the prescribed
physics. COMSOL Multiphysics® provides two options for interfaces of the deformed meshes; a moving
mesh or a deformed geometry. The deformed geometry does not allow for the material to change shape,
and will therefore correspond to either adding or removing layers of the boundary. This mesh type is
usually applied to studies, where it is desired to investigate the diﬀerent shapes of a model through
diﬀerent studies. Contrary to this, the moving mesh provides the opportunity for the solid domain to
deform symbiotic with the deformation of the moving mesh. This mesh type is applied to the studies,
where the deformation is physics controlled and the deformation of the moving boundary, therefore can
inﬂuence the ﬂuid domains as well. [32] The moving mesh is chosen as the ideal mesh interface for this
simulation with the mesh smoothing type of hyperelastic. The interior nodes of the mesh solve the ALE
formulation of the Navier-Stokes equation as described in Section 2.4.
To mesh the diﬀerent domains diﬀerently, user-deﬁned meshes of diﬀerent combinations have been
examined. As the ﬂuid domain provides the most information about the model, it is decided to mesh
the solid domain with a coarser mesh compared to the ﬂuid domain. This is done with regards to the
computational time and to help the model converge. The predeﬁned mesh sizes Extra Coarse, Coarser
and Coarse are chosen for the solid domain. The ﬂuid domain is modelled with the predeﬁned meshes
Coarser, Coarse, Normal and Fine. It is decided not to investigate ﬂuid mesh sizes which are coarser than
the selected mesh of the solid domain. Both meshes are modelled as free tetrahedral elements composed
mainly of tetrahedrons as illustrated in Figure 3.18 and the boundary layer is deﬁned by Comsol as
prisms (colored gray) and pyramids (colored blue) as visualised in Figure 3.19. Furthermore, the ﬂuid
mesh composed of tetrahedrons are illustrated in redin Figure 3.19.

3.4.6
Solver Conﬁguration
Two diﬀerent solver settings can be applied in COMSOL Multiphysics®. The segregated solver creates
a system of subdivided physics solves these steps in sequences within each iteration. Contrary the direct
fully coupled solver creates a single system, which solves across the diﬀerent physics.[38].
As the DHP-01 is a FSI model, a linear direct MUMPS solver with a fully coupled model is used for
the computation. The number of iterations is increased from automatically selected 4 to 7 for all studies.
The studies are computed as time-dependent in the interval from t = 0 to t = 5.4 s with a ﬁne stepping
of ∆t = 0.01 s.
As both combinations of Study 1 and 2 with Q1 and Q2 are investigated using diﬀerent mesh combi-
nations for the FSI models, the ﬁles are computed on the HPC cluster to reduce the computational time
of each study.

6.1. The Comparative Analysis
6.1
The Comparative Analysis
Study 1 represents the isolated scenario, where the ﬂuid domain is the only deforming factor of the solid
domain. This is not a real phenomenon but is presented to give an overview of ﬂuid behavior without
any other inﬂuences. Study 1 also demonstrates how the implementation of the moving boundary aﬀects
the solid domain as seen in Figure 5.5 and 5.5. Study 2 creates a more realistic description of the move-
ment with the inclusion of compression and stretching, but it is still a simpliﬁcation of the real heart
movement. When evaluating the movement and stress distribution visualised in Figure 5.6, the highest
stress magnitude is distributed around the apex during compression and stretching. Similar results of
stress distributions location and propagation are reported in [8] and [21].
From the velocity ﬁelds in Figure 5.1 and 5.2, it can be seen that the deﬁned Γi inﬂuences the start of
the velocity ﬁeld through the inlet tube. The applied mean velocity at the inlet is derived from the ﬂow
rates used in the data collection. The low ﬂow rate of 1 L/min contributes to the small displacements
of the solid domain together with the assumptions and material properties of the solid domain as visible
for Study 1 in Figure 5.1 and 5.2. If the material parameters are changed to resemble the soft tissue, it
is expected that larger deformations caused by the ﬂuid domain will occur in Study 1.
The constant initial pressure of 0 Pa is applied as the Γo and aﬀects the pressure throughout the
outlet as visible in Figure 5.3 and 5.4. The Γo is applied to mimick the aortic semilunar valve, but it
is not a representative boundary condition for the real heart. As the heart is not an isolated organ, the
initial and boundary conditions are not a realistic representation of the environment in the heart. The
initial conditions aﬀects the ﬂuid dynamics and should be further investigated [22].
The ﬂuid is assumed to be laminar with a Re < 2300. With the given ﬂow rates, the Re and needed
Le of this model is calculated in Section 3.4.2. Here, the Re of the two ﬂow rates Q1 and Q2 are de-
termined to be 533.2 and 1066.1 which veriﬁes the laminar assumption. The needed Le is determined
to be 0.32 m and 0.64 m for the respective ﬂow rates. The tubes modelled in the FSI model are only
0.035 m, however it could be argued that the tubes extend further than the base plate of the phan-
tom, thereby fulﬁlling the needed Le. When comparing the calculated Re for both ﬂow rates with the
literature [27] [28], it is noticed that both values remain in the interval of reported vena cava values
of 600 to 3300. Couch et al.[27] investigated the inferior vena cava and vena cava ﬁlters with a ﬂow
rate corresponding to 1 L/min and a time-averaged Re of 600. The reported value of the aortic valve
is 3400, indicates that turbulence occurs in the left ventricle. Khalafvand et al. [40] investigated the
Re at diﬀerent time instances with the change of velocity throughout the ventricle, providing Re as
high as 5684 indicating a turbulent ﬂow. Both Couch et al.[27] and Khalafvand et al. [40] argued that a
decrease in Re correlates with pressure drops and cold give an indication of location of vortex formations.
Vortex ring formations are expected to occur as a result of the inlet ﬂow from the mitral valve at
the beginning of the diastole. The inlet ﬂow will create a clockwise and counterclockwise vortex ring
formation [22]. Local pressure minimums are indicated around the connection between the outlet and
the ventricle, as illustrated in the pressure ﬁelds in Figure 5.3 and 5.4. In Figure 5.1 and 5.2, the velocity
ﬁelds and associated velocity arrows visualized indicate the formation of vortex rings forming after the
inlet ﬂow is distributed in the ventricles. This is due to the Γb and the boundary layer as described in
Section 2.3.2. Previous studies have successfully shown this formation as presented in [22], [40] as well
as in the thesis [6] for the left ventricle. It would be desirable to illustrate the vortex ring formation in
this thesis, but due to the chosen geometry as a CAD kernel, it is not possible to connect the model with
MATLAB through LiveLink™. Due to the time frame of the thesis, it was not possible to investigate
the consequences of converting the model to a Comsol kernel and reproduce the results as well as the
vortex ring formation. Thus, future work with the model should investigate this conversion, as the λ2
and vortex ring formation is a known and quantitative measure of the ﬂow in the ventricles. It is a
desired measure for comparisons with state of the art research.

6.2. Model Limitations and Possible Improvements
6.2
Model Limitations and Possible Improvements
6.2.1
Model Creation
Several simpliﬁcations and limitations are applied to the model, which could aﬀect the simulated results.
The CAD model is the base of the investigations, thus it is important that the CAD model resembles the
DHP-01 in the best way. As illustrated in Figure 3.3 and 3.4, the original ﬁles were a lot more detailed
but also deﬁcient, compared to the segmented meshes. The uneven structures of the ventricles from the
original ﬁles would cause problems for the converging in Comsol, thus the smoothed meshes are used.
With the segmented meshes, the inlets and outlets of the two ventricles are removed together with the
original placement. Therefore, the current straight shape of the used tubes, does not fully represent the
shape of the inlet and outlets of the ventricles. The outlet of the left ventricle should not be placed on
the top of the ventricle but should instead be created with a bend part and placed just below the top.
Thus, the ﬂow of the left ventricle will be impacted slightly diﬀerently with a diﬀerent outlet shape.
Furthermore, as visible in Figure 3.12, the tubes are not placed identically to the positions registered in
Figure 3.11, and the positions could be further adjusted in future models.
When examining the mesh, especially the connection between the tubes and the ventricles causes
meshing problems.
As demonstrated with the mesh quality plots in Figure 4.6, especially this area
causes a decrease in the average element quality and is also accountable for some of the minimum ele-
ment quality as well. Therefore, it is suggested to smooth the assembling to avoid sharp edges between the
parts. Furthermore, the tubes extended a bit into the ventricles, which can inﬂuence how the inlet ﬂow
is distributed. Adjustment and elimination of additional tube length stretching beyond the ventricle sur-
face connection should be conducted. Within the time frame of this thesis, it is not possible to eliminate
this additional edge completely, but future work on the model should examine this assembly arrangement.
6.2.2
Model Simulation
The DHP-01 is created in PVA hydrogel, which exhibits other material parameters and behavior com-
pared to the myocardium of the real heart. PVA hydrogel is a more stiﬀmaterial, which will inﬂuence
the material behavior and ﬂuid motion [8]. The material parameters are identiﬁed based on the average
of multiple experiments conducted by [8]. If the exact parameter values of the material were listed by
Shelley Medical Imaging Technologies, this could change the results of the simulated model. This might
change the observed results from Study 1, where only ﬂuid behavior causes deformation in the solid
domain. A material with parameters closer to the myocardium would demonstrate bigger deformations
caused by the ﬂuid domain. However, the material parameters are not as important for Study 2, as the
deformation in this study is caused by the servo movement.
6.3
Future Work
Even though, great cardiac simulations are based on 3D models, constitutive relations, and boundary
conditions, the mesh and solver conﬁguration inﬂuence the model simulation. As the main purpose of
this thesis is to create a simulation model based on the in-house DHP-01, the model is veriﬁed within its
limits of mesh combination and solver choice. Future work should investigate of the mesh construction
and contributions from the diﬀerent mesh elements and sizes as well as diﬀerent combinations of ﬂuid
and solid mesh types to identify the ideal mesh combination. With the ideal mesh combination, the
solver conﬁguration should be tested as well.
It is a choice to recreate the simulation as close to the real in-house DHP-01, instead of the real
human heart.
Therefore, it is also expected, that the results deviate from in vivo studies.
As it is
desired to replicate the DHP-01, a validation of the simulation model against in vitro data is necessary
to quantify the quality of the simulated model. This is currently not possible with the DHP-01, as it is
no longer functional. Otherwise, if a more realistic model is desired, the validation of the model should
be conducted against in vivo data of the ﬂow and movement of the soft tissue. Future cardiac simulation
models should investigate the incorporation of a more detailed anatomically correct description of the
myocardium and the cardiac wall. Inclusion of the passive myocardium as described by [19] could be

Conclusion
The project focused on the creation of a duplicated model representing the in-house bi-ventricular
DHP-01 phantom by Shelley Medical Imaging Technologies. Based on clinical data, segmentation was
used to create meshes of the ventricles.
These meshes provided the foundation for the CAD model
resembling the DHP-01 phantom. Through the investigation of previously published literature, the con-
stitutive relations, boundary conditions as well as material properties were determined and successfully
implemented in the FSI model. Based on the combination of these assumptions as well as a well designed
CAD model, a converging FSI model was created and veriﬁed in this thesis. Unfortunately, the model
could not be validated against the in-house phantom, as the in-house phantom is no longer functional, but
the FSI provided results similar to those reported in the literature. However, the ﬁnal model and corre-
sponding results presented some limitations of the conducted study. Due to the time frame of this thesis,
it was not possible to accommodate these limitations, thus future work should examine these possibilities.
Overall, it is concluded that the constructed model provides a great foundation for further experiments
and future implementations of more anatomically correct assumptions.

Appendix E
SolidWorks Protocols
This appendix contains a compilation of protocols for all CAD procedures completed in SolidWorks
for this thesis. Each protocol represent a diﬀerent part of the ﬁnal CAD model with a step by step guide
of how to construct the diﬀerent parts. The protocols are presented in the same order by which they
should be completed.
I
Creation of the Ventricle
This protocol goes through the process of converting the segmented mesh volumes of the ventricle(s) into
surfaces and solid bodies.
Step 1
Open the SolidWorks application and select Part in the pop-up win-
dow. Once the interface of SolidWorks is loaded, go to the SolidWorks
Add-in tab and click on ScanTo3D. This enables all the add-in tools
and functions available with the ScanTo3D package.
Step 2
On the interface of SolidWorks click File, then select Open. In the
ﬁle format choose the ScanTo3D Mesh ﬁles and select the one of the
segmented meshes of the ventricles. Click on Options button and
inspect the dimensions in the File format tab matches those of the
mesh and DICOM images. The Units should be in Millimeters. The
segmented volume of the ventricle will now appear on the interface
of SolidWorks.
The segmented volume of the ventricle is now imported as a mesh and with the ScanTo3D
add-in enabled, it is possible to transform the mesh into a surface and a solid part. This is
done by using the ScanTo3D functions Mesh Prep Wizard and Surface Wizard.
Step 3
The Mesh Prep Wizard function.
Go to the Tools tab and click on ScanTo3D. All build-in functions for
ScanTo3D is now available. Select the Mesh Prep Wizard function.
With this function it is possible to adjust and alter the imported
mesh through a series of guided options within the function.

==PAGE:115==
I. Creation of the Ventricle
In the ﬁrst step of the function, the Welcome part, the mesh of the
ventricle is chosen. When the mesh is selected, SolidWorks provide
a count of faces for the mesh for future reference when adjusting the
mesh with these functions. The left ventricle consisted of 24276 faces

The second step is the Mesh Orientation. This step allows for addi-

ventricle, where the superior part of the ventricle is perpendicular to
the Front plane in SolidWorks. Therefore, the option None is chosen

The Extraneous Data Removal step provides the option for the user
to delete part of the mesh. This function is not used on the mesh of
the ventricle.
With Simpliﬁcation it is possible to simplify the mesh by reducing
its size. This can be helpful if the mesh presents a lot of noise, but it
is important to remember, that the function eliminates data points
from the mesh. Thus, the function is not applied to the mesh of the
ventricle.
The Smoothing option allows for global smoothing of the mesh or
local smoothing only aﬀecting selected parts of the mesh. Opposite
to Simpliﬁcation, Smoothing does not remove data points or reduce
the size of the mesh. It uses a sine curve to smoothing the height
of the mesh curvatures to even out the diﬀerences. The maximum
global smoothing is applied to the mesh.
The ﬁnal part of the Mesh Prep Wizard function is Mesh Completion.
This part enables the launch of the Surface Wizard directly or to
complete the Mesh Prep Wizard and exit the function if desired. It
is chosen to complete the function and exit without launching the
Surface Wizard. This allows the user to return to the SolidWorks
interface and save the progress before continuing the conversion of
the mesh into a solid body.

The Surface Wizard function.
Go to the Tools tab and click on ScanTo3D and select the Surface
Wizard function. With this function it is possible to convert the mesh
into a surface and solid body.

mesh of the ventricle is selected. Once again SolidWorks provide a
count of faces for the mesh for future reference as well as illustrates
how the changes made with Mesh Prep Wizard have aﬀected the
mesh. The left ventricle still consisted of 24276 faces and the right
ventricle of 20092 faces.

1
Introduction
This project plan is conducted in order to outline the purpose, time frame and learning objectives of
the master thesis Investigation of Flow and Material Properties in a Heart Phantom Model. The thesis
accounts for a workload of 35 ECTS points and covers a period starting the 30th of August and is to
be submitted at the 8th of March 2022. It ﬁnalizes the Master of Science in Engineering within the
ﬁeld of Biomedical Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. The thesis is conducted at the
Department of Health Technology under the supervision of Marie Sand Traberg and Matthias Bo Stuart.
1.1
Motivation
The heart is responsible for circulating blood and maintaining human life from creation to the end.
But each year, approximately 92.1 million adults are aﬀected by cardiovascular diseases in the United
States of America (USA) alone. Therefore, it is desirable to investigate and understand the heart and
its mechanisms.[1] As the main purpose of the heart is to pump and circulate the blood, ﬂuid dynamics
plays a crucial role in understanding and modelling the human heart. With the knowledge of knowing
how the heart ﬁlls and ejects comes the possibility of better disease diagnosis and management for the
patient. The ﬂuid dynamics and solid mechanics of the heart can help predict these new physiological
phenomena and disease occurrences. The computational simulation models of the heart help facilitate
the study of ﬂuid dynamics and solid mechanics in healthy and diseased hearts in order to improve the
diagnostic tools and medical devices. [2][3]
2
The Project
2.1
Problem Deﬁnition and Goals
The master thesis Investigation of Flow and Material Properties in a Heart Phantom Model revolves
around the in-house poly-vinyl alcohol (PVA) bi-ventricular heart phantom model at the Department of
Fast Ultrasound and Biomechanics. As the heart phantom enables full control of the motion, a simulation
model based on the phantom can be created. A computational geometry is created in SolidWorks based
on recorded ultra sound images of the heart phantom and the simulation software COMSOL Multiphysics
is used to study the ﬂuid dynamics and solid mechanics of the simulated phantom. The following project
objectives create the foundation and framework of the thesis.
I. Perform experiments and collect data from the in house heart phantom model
II. Investigation of the biomechanical properties of the in house heart phantom model and formulation
of constitutive equations related to this.
III. Quantiﬁcation and application of boundary conditions for the created simulation model.
IV. Performance investigation of the ﬂuid dynamics and solid mechanics of the in house heart phantom
model

5
Evaluation
In general, the process of writing this thesis has been a great experience. The work process has been
comprehensive with lots of learning opportunities. It has been learned, that working with a computa-
tional model requires additional time to ﬁne-tune speciﬁc model parts as well as extra time for rerunning
of the simulations.
Some of the elements were delayed due to extraordinary circumstances. The death of my grandmother
in the middle of October delayed the travel plans to California as well as the start of the simulations
in Comsol. Furthermore, being isolated with Covid-19 after the exam in December, delayed the further
investigation of the ﬂuid dynamics and solid mechanics.
Besides these delays, it has been a successful process with a great outcome and satisfying results.
