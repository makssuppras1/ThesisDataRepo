Abstract
Performing hearing tests using hearing aids (in-situ) makes the fitting session more con-
venient and can potentially increase accuracy compared to the manufacturer’s initial fit.
The purpose of this study is to investigate the underlying algorithms of in-situ audiometry
fitting modules and the validity of in-situ audiometry relative to conventional audiometry.
To this end, data was collected from hearing instruments of six different brands in order
to study and generalize the effects that may occur under clinical conditions.
Differences in real-ear-to-dial difference (REDD) between conventional and in-situ thresh-
olds should be corrected for, either manually by the clinicians, or automatically in the
fitting software. In this study, it was investigated whether REDD-corrections are auto-
matically applied by the fitting software, and if in-situ air conduction thresholds measured
with open domes are valid relative to conventional thresholds measured with insert ear-
phones without additional manual corrections.
Evidence was found that Widex and Phonak hearing aids correct prescription targets
based on in-situ thresholds measured with standard occluded domes, and that in hearing
aids from Widex, Phonak, Rexton, Signia, and Oticon in-situ stimuli presented with open
domes are corrected to account for low-frequency leakage through the vents. ReSound
hearing aids were found to be an exception in this regard. However, it is very clear from
the instructions in the ReSound Smart Fit in-situ module that in-situ audiometry should
be measured with an occluded dome or earmold to obtain valid results.
Hearing thresholds of eleven adults with known symmetric sensorineural hearing impair-
ment were measured with conventional insert earphones and through top-technical RIC
hearing aids with standard open domes from four different brands. Across all hearing aid
brands, the average interparticipant difference between conventional and in-situ thresholds
was within ±5 dB for frequencies of 250-6000 Hz. No significant differences in thresholds
were observed for Rexton and Widex hearing aids, whereas statistical significant differ-
ences was observed for Phonak and Oticon hearing aids. However, individual differences
for frequencies of 250-6000 Hz were within ±10 dB and were thus within expected clinical
variation. At frequencies 125 Hz and 8000 Hz, the Oticon in-situ thresholds were in all
cases overestimated compared to the conventional thresholds. In-situ thresholds at 125
Hz were only obtained for two participants and they differed by respectively 15 dB and 20
dB from conventional thresholds. At 8000 Hz, the differences ranged between 0 dB and
15 dB.
Overall, hearing thresholds measured in-situ with open domes at frequencies 250-6000
Hz are as valid as conventional thresholds and no manual REDD-corrections should be
applied.

Introduction
The sense of hearing allows us to experience the world through sound. Humans are able
to detect and interpret sounds without conscious effort, and the sense of hearing allows us
to interact and communicate through speech. The amount of information that we receive
from our surroundings through our sense of hearing is enormous, and diﬀiculties can arise
when we are not able to rely on this sense.
The partial or complete inability to perceive sound due to malfunction or damage to
the ear is called hearing impairment. Hearing impairment is diagnosed through auditory
tests, and conventional pure-tone audiometry is the gold standard of auditory tests for
determining hearing impairment. The result of such a test is called an audiogram. Any
potential treatment of the patient’s hearing impairment depends closely on the cause of the
hearing impairment; some patients may need medical or surgical treatment, while other
patients will benefit from hearing aids. A hearing aid is a medical device that amplifies
sound that is not otherwise heard by the user. The amount of amplification (gain) that the
hearing aid provides is calculated based on information on individual characteristics of the
hearing loss and physiological properties of the ear through evidence-based prescription
rules (Dillon, 2012).
When the hearing aid is fitted, it is considered good practice to ensure its performance
through validation and verification. One way of verifying that the hearing aid provides
the appropriate amount of gain is by measuring hearing aid output directly in the ear
canal of the patient. Such measurements are called real-ear measurements (REM) and are
recommended by several oﬀicial auditory bodies world wide (Valente, 2006; ASHA, 2015a).
However, it is estimated that only half of the world’s audiologists perform REM (Abrams
et al., 2013). In a literature review on REM from Almufarrij, Dillon, and Munro (2021)
it was concluded that, in general, the benefits from REM are statistically significant, but
that additional research are necessary to investigate whether the benefits of REM that are
also of clinical importance are worth the costs of providing REM.
In 1996, Widex A/S introduced a technique that allowed for hearing tests to be performed
through the hearing aid itself when placed in the ear of the user. The technique is called in-
situ audiometry and was introduced with the aim of increasing accuracy of the hearing aid
fitting (Ludvigsen and Tøpholm, 1997). Performing the hearing test through the hearing
aid allows for considerations of individual conditions that may influence the perceived
sound (Block, 2008). In-situ audiometry is attractive, as it requires less equipment and
resources than e.g. REM (Keidser, Yeend, et al., 2011). One major shortcoming of the
technology is that the variety of special tests available with in-situ audiometry, such as
bone conduction and masking, is currently limited. This means that the technique for the
time being is limited to hearing aid fitting and not suitable for diagnostics.
In modern hearing aid fitting software it is possible to report two types of audiograms:
a conventional audiogram, an in-situ audiogram, or both.
In many cases, the fitting
software also allows the clinician to choose which audiogram type that should be used for
the hearing aid fitting. It is however not clear whether this means that the conventional
audiogram is completely disregarded when in-situ is used for hearing aid fitting. It is even
more unclear how information from the conventional audiogram is used in cases where
the choice of audiogram type is absent. Understanding how the conventional audiogram

influences the prescribed hearing aid gain when in-situ is used for hearing aid fitting will
contribute to making the fitting process more transparent.
Studies have shown that test-retest reliability of in-situ audiometry is equivalent to that
of conventional audiometry (Smith-Olinde et al., 2006; O’Brien et al., 2010). The validity
of in-situ audiometry, however, is debated. Significant differences have been reported in
audiograms obtained by using in-situ audiometry as compared to audiograms obtained us-
ing conventional audiometry (DiGiovanni and Pratt, 2010; O’Brien et al., 2010; Kiessling
et al., 2015). O’Brien et al. (2010) found that the differences could be explained by the
difference in sound intensity in the ear of the patient between the two measurement meth-
ods. When sound is presented to a patient through either headphones, insert earphones,
or a hearing aid, there is a difference between the intensity indicated by the audiometer
dial setting and the intensity measured in the real ear. This difference in sound intensity is
denoted the real-ear-to-dial difference (REDD). The study concluded that in-situ audiom-
etry is as valid and reliable as conventional audiometry if appropriate REDD-corrections
are made. However, Kiessling et al. (2015) concluded that REDD-corrections were not
included in the fitting software modules of any manufacturers involved in their study.
Based on daily practice, clinicians at Audiological Clinic at Bispebjerg Hospital (BBH)
have reasons to believe that in-situ technology has advanced since these studies were per-
formed in 2010 and 2015 respectively. This hypothesis is supported by Vercammen (2020)
in a retrospective analysis of 162,772 conventional in-clinic assessments compared to mea-
surements performed with Phonak’s in-situ audiometry fitting module ”AudiogramDirect”.
This analysis found that the mean difference between conventional and in-situ thresholds
was less than 1 dB and negligible from a clinical point of view.
In broad terms, the aim of this study was to obtain a better understanding of in-situ
technology with the aim of making the fitting process more transparent and guide clinicians
to the best practice when performing in-situ audiometry. Part of the experiments are
performed on artificial ears, and part of the experiments are performed on real ears.
High-end hearing aids from six major brands were fitted for artificial ears based on either

Human hearing and hearing loss
Hearing is the ability to perceive sounds. It is a product of many underlying mechanisms,
and a dysfunction in any of these mechanisms leads to hearing impairment: the partial or
complete inability to hear. The cure to the hearing impairment is linked to the cause and
depends on the very nature of the hearing impairment.
In this chapter relevant background on hearing impairment and various audiometry pro-
cedures are reviewed. The core-concepts are conventional audiometry and in-situ audiom-
etry, and based on these techniques, current audiometry practice is discussed.
2.1
Hearing thresholds and hearing impairment
The objective measurement of sound intensity, measured in decibel (dB), is sound pressure
level (SPL). Sound pressure, measured in pascal (Pa), is the average variation in atmo-
spheric pressure caused by a sound wave. Sound pressure level is a logarithmic measure
of the effective pressure of a sound relative to a reference value. It is given by

In air, the commonly used reference sound pressure is p0 = 20µPa (What is sound pressure

The weakest audible sound level to an individual is called the hearing threshold (Poulsen,
2008). Below this sound level, the individual is not able to detect any sound. In the
remainder of this thesis, thresholds always refer to hearing thresholds. Hearing thresholds
are frequency-dependent and humans do not hear equally well at all frequencies. The
range of frequencies that can be detected by humans is denoted the audible spectrum. It is
commonly given as 20 Hz to 20 kHz (Pujol, 2018), although there is considerable variation
between individuals. Thresholds are reported in a graph that displays hearing threshold
for all tested frequencies, the audiogram. Typically, the audiogram shows thresholds at
frequencies 125, 250, 500, 750, 1000, 1500, 2000, 3000, 4000, 6000, and 8000 Hz, and the
range of frequencies is oftentimes divided into three: the low frequencies (125-750 Hz), the
mid frequencies (1000-3000 Hz) and the high frequencies (4000-8000 Hz).
In practice, when reporting thresholds, it is convenient to report only the deviation from
normal hearing rather than the absolute hearing threshold. A new scale is thus desirable,
in which the zero value corresponds to the threshold of the average normal hearing adult,
also denoted the reference thresholds. This scale is referred to as the hearing level (HL)
which is also measured in decibel. Any deviation in hearing thresholds from the zero-
line in the dB HL scale indicates a hearing loss (Bauman, 2012) relative to the reference
threshold. However, an individual is said to have normal hearing if the thresholds do not
exceed 20 dB HL at any tested frequency.
Hearing thresholds are evaluated in an audiometric test, see Section 2.2, with typical
transducers being headphones or insert earphones, and hearing level is specific to the
transducer used to obtain the thresholds. Thresholds included in this study are obtained
using insert earphones or hearing aids. The amount of dB SPL needed to obtain 0 dB
HL for a certain transducer is called the reference equivalent threshold sound pressure

Determination of hearing thresholds establishes the degree of the hearing loss, that is
how severe the hearing impairment is.
The categories are normal hearing, mild loss,
moderate loss, moderately severe loss, severe loss, and profound loss, see Figure 2.2. A
full explanation of the hearing loss furthermore includes specification of the type of the
hearing loss and several additional information including (ASHA, 2015b):
1. Configuration: the shape or pattern of hearing loss across frequencies,
2. Unilateral/Bilateral: hearing loss in one ear or in both ears,
3. Symmetrical/asymmetrical: whether or not the degree and configuration of hearing
loss are the same in both ears, and
4. Progressive/sudden: whether or not the loss becomes worse over time.
The type of hearing loss is determined by which part of the ear that causes the hearing
impairment.
The nature of a hearing loss can be conductive, sensorineural or mixed
(ASHA, 2015b).
A conductive hearing loss is defined as damage to or malfunction of
the outer and/or middle ear. The pathway through the outer and middle ear is not free
and this leads to a reduced transmission of sound to the cochlea in the inner ear.
A
sensorineural hearing loss, on the other hand, is caused by malfunction of the structures
in the inner ear or the auditory nerve. Sensorineural hearing loss is the most common type
of permanent hearing loss (Eyvazzadeh, 2020), and it is oftentimes caused by loss of or
damage to the outer and/or inner hair cells of the cochlea. The damage to the hair cells
can be genetic or due to aging or exposure to loud sounds. A conductive hearing loss can
oftentimes be treated medically or surgically whereas sensorineural hearing impairment is

often treated with hearing aids. A mixed hearing loss is a combination of a conductive
and sensorineural hearing loss.
Age-related hearing loss (presbycusis) commonly presents as a symmetrical sensorineural
hearing loss, and such hearing losses will be the primary focus of the present thesis. Patien-

followed by lower frequency sounds as the condition progresses. Presbycusis appears as an
overall down-sloping line on the audiogram (sloping hearing loss) with worse thresholds in
the high frequencies relative to thresholds in the lower frequencies.
2.2
Conventional audiometry
Measurements of hearing for variations in sound intensity and frequency is called audiom-
etry. Audiometric tests are used to diagnose hearing loss and in the process of fitting a
hearing aid. Hearing thresholds at a given frequencies can be determined using stimulus
consisting of only that particular frequency. This type of stimulus is called pure tones,
and conventional pure-tone audiometry is the gold standard test for measuring hearing
status (Beck et al., 2014). It is the main hearing test used to identify hearing threshold
levels of an individual.
In pure-tone audiometry, two types of hearing thresholds are measured: air conduction
(AC) thresholds and bone conduction (BC) thresholds (Saunders, Stein, and Shuster,
1990). The air conduction thresholds are measured for pure tones of frequencies from 125
Hz to 8000 Hz using either headphones or insert earphones. The term ”air conduction”
refers to the sound traveling from the headphones or insert earphones through the air
into the outer ear canal, hitting the eardrum and from there passing on through the ear
6
==PAGE:19==
system. Afterwards, BC thresholds are measured with pure tones for frequencies of 250 Hz
to 4000 Hz using an oscillating headband. Usually, the oscillating part of the head band is
placed on the mastoid bone, which is the rear part of the temporal bone of the skull and
is located just behind the inner ear (Cunha, 2020). This headband is designed to deliver
tones directly through the skull, hence the name, to the cochlea, bypassing the outer
and middle ear, and transmitting the sound directly to the inner ear. Bone conduction
is perceived in both ears, and to test each ear individually, white noise is played into
the non-test ear. This technique is called masking. Masking is also used for measuring
asymmetric AC thresholds to prevent the better hearing ear from influencing thresholds
in the other ear (Masking in audiology, everything you might not know, 2020). If AC
thresholds are measured through headphones, masking should in general be used when
the difference between hearing level of the test ear and non-test ear exceeds 40 dB. AC
thresholds measured through insert earphones should include masking when the difference
in hearing level between the ears exceeds 60 dB. The results of the pure-tone audiometric
examination are reported in the audiogram.
AC thresholds are used to describe the degree of hearing loss, and when combining them
with BC thresholds, the type of hearing loss can be determined. If the patient suffers
from a conductive hearing loss, the audiogram will show a gap between the AC and the
BC thresholds, see Figure 2.3a. The audiogram reflecting a purely sensorineural hearing
loss, see Figure 2.3b, will show a non-zero hearing threshold for both air conduction and
bone conduction, and these two types of thresholds will be clinically equivalent, see e.g.,
Margolis (2010), meaning that in general the difference is no more than 15 dB at any
tested frequency. Note that the typical human ear can hear frequencies that are both
lower and higher than the ones in the audiogram, but all frequencies of usual speech are
covered by the audiogram.

2.3
The hearing aid fitting session
Once a hearing loss is diagnosed, one should investigate whether the patient would benefit
from hearing aids. According to Hørenedsættelse hos ældre (2020), there is evidence that
a patient can benefit from hearing aids when the average threshold exceeds 30 dB over the
frequencies of 1000, 2000, and 4000 Hz. In some cases, patients with less severe hearing
impairment can also benefit from hearing aids. Sundhedsstyrelsen (2019) states that, with
referral from an ear doctor, hearing aids provided by the public health authorities or
subsidies to hearing aids provided by private clinics are provided in Denmark.
A patient is provided with hearing aids in a hearing aid fitting session. At the initial
hearing aid fitting session, the proper hearing aid for the patient is found and adjusted to
the individual hearing loss. The different procedures and components of the hearing aid
fitting session are elaborated in the following sections.
2.3.1
Selection of hearing aids
There are many different types of hearing aids, and which one if them is the right one for
a patient depends on the degree of hearing loss and the patient’s preferences concerning
appearance and lifestyle. There are two overall categories of hearing aids: in-the-ear (ITE)
hearing aids, and behind-the-ear (BTE) hearing aids (Winkler, Latzel, and Holube, 2016).
ITE hearing aids are worn inside the ear canal and are usually custom-fit. The depth
within the ear canal where the hearing aid fits depends on the specific style. BTE hearing
aids on the other hand sit behind the outer ear with tubing or wires from the hearing aid
instrument to the dome or custom-fit earmold in the ear canal.
A hearing aid dome is a small plastic piece at the end of the tube or wire that is placed
inside the ear canal (What is the difference between open and closed hearing aid domes?,
2014). The different types of hearing aids come with different advantages and disadvan-
tages. In general, the smaller the hearing aid, the more discrete it is. On the other hand,
the larger the hearing aid, the longer the battery life and the more amplification can be
provided by the hearing aid.
A subcategory of the BTE styles are the receiver-in-the-ear (RITE) or receiver-in-canal
(RIC) hearing aids, see Figure 2.4. In a RIC hearing aid, the speaker is placed with the
ear dome in the ear canal, as opposed to being placed in the hearing instrument behind
the ear. The hearing aid speaker is inside the ear, while the microphone and processor
are placed within a small box behind the ear. The hearing instrument and receiver are
connected by a thin wire. A RIC hearing aid is discrete and allows for both closed and open
fits. RIC it the most popular style of hearing aids, especially for people with symmetrical
sensorineural hearing loss, and all major hearing aid brands manufacture RIC hearing
aids. For this reason, the RIC type hearing aids are the focus of this thesis.

2.3.2
Closed and open fittings, occlusion and vent effects
Having found the proper hearing aid, one should select the preferred acoustic coupling to
the ear, i.e., the dome or earmold. As already mentioned, BTE hearing aids sit behind the
outer ear with tubing or wires from the instrument to the dome or custom-fit earmold in
the ear canal. The dome can be occluded or more or less open. According to the patent
of Fretz, Stypulkowski, and Woods (1998), open-fit hearing aids, as opposed to closed-fit
hearing aids, are defined as “An open ear canal hearing-aid system comprises an ear canal
tube sized for positioning in an ear canal of a user so that the ear canal is at least partially
open for directly receiving ambient sounds.” An open style dome has openings for sound
to pass through from outside the ear. An occluded dome is meant to reduce the leakage
of sound from the hearing aid (What is the difference between open and closed hearing aid
domes?, 2014) and is needed to amplify low frequency sounds.
In this study, measurements are conducted with the most occluded standard dome, power
domes or double domes, and standard open domes, see Figure 2.5. In the remainder of this
thesis, an open-fit hearing aid refers to a hearing aid fitted with a standard open dome,
and a closed-fit hearing aids refers to a hearing aid fitted with a standard occluded dome.
(a) Open dome.
(b) Power dome.
Figure 2.5: A standard open dome and a standard power dome from Phonak.
With the ears covered, or occluded, self-generated sounds such as speech, chewing, and
swallowing can appear louder than usually. This sensation of increased loudness, which
is most pronounced for low frequencies, is denoted the occlusion effect (Staab, 2003).
Sound passes through the ear canal and is transmitted to the middle and inner ear via
the tympanic membrane. Self-produced sounds are transmitted both via air and via bone
vibrations through the skull. The sounds can escape the ear, if ear canal is open, but an
occluded dome causes the bone conducted sounds to be retained inside the ear (Winkler,
Latzel, and Holube, 2016).
The amount of occlusion can be quantified by objective measurements and subjective
descriptions, here denoted the objective and subjective occlusion effects, respectively. The
objective occlusion effect was defined by Dillon (2012) as the increase in SPL between the
occluded and open ear canal during self-vocalization. The subjective occlusion effect is the
hearing aid users own perception of the increased loudness. For both measures it holds
that the effect is mainly present at low frequencies (Winkler, Latzel, and Holube, 2016).
To reduce the occlusion effect, vents can be introduced into the earmold or dome. Kuk and
Keenan (2006) described that an increase in vent diameter by 1 mm leads to a reduction
of the objective occlusion effect in low-frequencies of 4 dB. They also found that the
subjective occlusion effect was significantly improved with this increase in vent diameter.
Venting improves own-voice perception and sound quality.
According to Winkler, Latzel, and Holube (2016), the occlusion effect is especially promi-
nent for listeners with normal hearing or a mild hearing loss at lower frequencies. Open-fit
9
==PAGE:22==
hearing aids might be the ideal solution for these patients, as some sound still reaches
through the ear unimpeded.
The downsides of the open fit are reduced benefits of directional microphones and noise
reduction, less compression, and less available gain before feedback (Winkler, Latzel, and
Holube, 2016). Furthermore, the effect of venting on the acoustic output of a hearing aid
is well documented; venting causes real-ear low-frequency output to decrease (Kuk and
Keenan, 2006). This effect is called the vent effect, and it has a huge effect on the gain
characteristics of a hearing aid. The openness of the ear canal affects the achieved SPL,
due to leakage of low frequencies out of the ear canal and due to reduced maximum stable
gain at high frequencies before feedback occurs (Winkler, Latzel, and Holube, 2016). This
means that closed-fit hearing aids are preferable for hearing aid users suffering from a
more severe hearing loss at low frequencies.
Kuk and Nordahn (2006) describes that the vent effect is affected by various parameters
besides the actual vent, such as unintentional venting (leakage between the hearing aid
and ear canal wall), residual volume, and middle ear compliance. Low frequency reduction
is larger for smaller ears, and high compliance leads to less vent effect. Formally, Kuk and
Nordahn (2006) define the vent effect as the decrease in SPL measured at the eardrum of
the hearing aid user consequent to a vent (intentional or unintentional).
Vents can occur in both ITE and BTE style hearing aids. In ITE hearing aids, both the
microphone and receiver are placed in the ear close to each other. In BTE style hearing
aids where the receiver is inside the ear canal, RIC hearing aids, the distance between
the microphone and receiver is larger, and the transmission of sound from receiver to
microphone is thereby reduced. The reduced transmission in turn allows for larger vents
and higher gain before feedback in RIC hearing aids than in ITE hearing aids (Winkler,
Latzel, and Holube, 2016).
2.3.3
Prescription targets
Once the hearing aid and dome have been selected, the process of fitting begins. The
main function of a hearing aid is to improve sound quality and amplify sound intensity at
frequencies where the hearing aid user has diﬀiculties in perceiving sounds. A hearing aid
is a medical device that requires careful adjustment to the individual ear. The amount
of amplification necessary depends on the individual hearing loss as well as individual ear
characteristics and the intensity and frequency of the incoming signal. In practice, the
required amplification as a function of frequency is calculated based on this information
using a prescription algorithm or prescription procedure, and is often referred to as the
prescription targets.
Various prescription procedures exist, and they each offer their take on how auditory
attributes such as comfort and speech intelligibility should be weighted in order to provide
the best experience for the hearing aid user. The first prescription procedures proposed
provided the same gain over a wide range of input levels (Dillon, 2012). Hearing aids
relying on such prescription procedures are called linear hearing aids. Today non-linear
(compression) hearing aids, in which gain prescription decreases with increasing input
level, are almost universal. Non-linear hearing aids, providing more amplification for soft
sounds than loud sounds, avoid amplification of loud sounds above what is comfortable
for the hearing aid user.
One of the most popular prescription procedures for compression hearing aids today is the
NAL-NL2 from The National Acoustic Laboratories (NAL) and all hearing aid fittings in
this study used this prescription procedure. It is the second generation of prescription
10
==PAGE:23==
procedures from NAL used for fitting non-linear hearing aids. The underlying rationale is
to make speech intelligible and overall loudness comfortable (Keidser, Dillon, et al., 2011).
The optimal gain was found in a computer simulation based on a speech intelligibility
model, a loudness model, and more than hundreds of audiograms covering a wide range
of degrees and configurations of sensorineural hearing loss (Dillon, 2012).
Extensive empirical data show that other parameters than hearing thresholds affect the
preferred amplification provided by the hearing aid. For example, it is well established
that females, regardless of other factors, in general prefer less gain than males (Keidser,
Dillon, et al., 2011). It has also been shown that inexperienced hearing aid users with
moderate hearing loss prefer less gain than more experienced hearing aid users with a
similar degree of hearing loss (Keidser, Dillon, et al., 2011). Consequently, gender specific
gain is prescribed by NAL-NL2, and a gain adaption for new hearing aids users is recom-
mended. Additional parameters such as age, unilateral vs bilateral fitting, venting, and
tubing also affect the NAL-NL2 prescriptions targets (Keidser, Dillon, et al., 2011).
A default hearing aid fitting based on hearing thresholds, user profile information, specific
information on hearing instruments, and prescriptions targets calculated in the fitting
software, is called manufacturer’s initial fit or simply initial fit.
2.3.4
Validation and verification
In a hearing aid fitting session, the primary objective is to ensure that the hearing aid
user is given the correct amplification such that maximal benefit from the hearing aids is
obtained. The validation procedure serves to confirm performance with the hearing aids.
Real-ear-measurements (REM), using a probe microphone to measure the sound at the ear
drum, are a means of verifying that the desired hearing aid output is obtained. Professional
associations such as the American Audiological Association and ASHA recommend the
use of REM as best practice (Valente, 2006; ASHA, 2015a). REM allows audiologists to
objectively measure and verify the sound that is being delivered at the ear drum. It helps
overcome the differences in type of hearing aid prescribed, the unique ear canal of the user,
and the method with which sound is passed from the hearing aid to the ear. According to
ASHA (2015a), a more accurate fitting can be obtained using REM.
The SPL that is delivered through a specific transducer to a patient’s ear drum depends
on both the transducer, input stimulus, and individual characteristics such as venting and
residual ear canal volume. There are different ways to measure the real-ear SPL at the
ear drum, and in the following only a couple of possible ways to do it will be described.
A hearing instrument is most conveniently calibrated in an ear simulators or a coupler, a
cavity connected to a microphone in one end and with the hearing instrument in the other
end. The real-ear-to-coupler difference (RECD) is a measure of the difference between SPL
in the real-ear and SPL as measured in the coupler as a function of frequency. According
to ANSI standards, RIC hearing aids should be calibrated in the HA1 2cc type coupler,
see Figure 2.6. SPL in the real-ear is measured subsequent to an otoscopic examination
by placing a probe tube and insert earphones in the ear canal (Pumford and Sinclair,
2001). RECD values enter the NAL-NL2 prescription target to convert targets to real-
ear targets.
If no RECD measurements are performed, average age-specific RECD is
oftentimes automatically used by the software. However, the use of individual RECDs
increase accuracy compared to the use of average RECDs (Watts et al., 2020).
A similar, although slightly different, measure, is the real-ear-to-dial difference (REDD)
which is the difference in decibels between SPL in the real-ear (dB SPL) and the au-
diometer dial setting (dB HL) that produced it. Like the RECD, the REDD is used to
convert thresholds from the dB HL scale to the dB SPL scale, but REDD can be used

olds. Later, Kuk and Nordahn (2006) argued that in-situ audiometry would minimize the
need for real-ear verification. It is worth investigating whether in-situ audiometry offers
a shortcut to an accurate hearing aid fitting, and the technology will thus be carefully
explained and evaluated in the next section.
2.4
In-situ audiometry
Most modern hearing aids and most hearing aid fitting software have in-built audiometers
to obtain hearing thresholds with the hearing aid in the ear. The hearing aid receiver
is used as the transducer for delivering sound, and signal presentation can be controlled
using the manufacturer’s fitting software. The technique is denoted in-situ audiometry.
”In situ” is a Latin phrase that directly translates to ”in place”. In-situ audiometry refers
to measurements made with the hearing aid in its natural place, correctly fitted in the
ear. As for conventional audiometry, the hearing aid user indicates to the clinician when
the sound is heard during in-situ audiometry. The technology was invented by Widex
in 1997 (Ludvigsen and Tøpholm, 1997), with the intention of increasing accuracy of the
hearing aid fitting by including real-ear acoustics to the thresholds. Block (2008) explains
that when the hearing thresholds are measured in-situ, the influence of individual residual
ear canal volume, the insertion depth of the hearing instrument in the ear canal, venting
effects, etc. are naturally taken into consideration. This can possibly lead to an increased
precision of the hearing aid fitting.
In-situ audiometry is attractive, as it requires less equipment and resources than e.g.,
REM (Keidser, Yeend, et al., 2011). Furthermore, it does not require special training
in positioning probe tubes.
Besides this obvious convenience, in-situ audiometry also
offers a comfort when patients suffering from a sensorineural symmetric hearing loss later
want their hearing aids adjusted, and a new AC hearing test is necessary. In practice,
conventional audiometry and hearing aid fitting are oftentimes done in two separate rooms
and require two separate appointments at the hearing clinic. In-situ audiometry can be
performed in the fitting room, and this procedure is thus time-saving as the patient would
only need one session instead of two.
Importantly, though, in-situ audiometry is currently restricted to hearing aid fitting. It is
not suitable for diagnostics as masking and bone conduction thresholds cannot be obtained.
2.4.1
Reliability of in-situ audiometry
Studies by Smith-Olinde et al. (2006) and O’Brien et al. (2010) have shown that test-retest
reliability of in-situ audiometry is equivalent to that of conventional audiometry. Smith-
Olinde et al. (2006) measured hearing thresholds of 43 normal hearing individuals at four
randomized frequencies using Widex Diva SD-9 BTE hearing instruments connected to
insert earphone tips. Each threshold was measured twice with the ER-3A earphone tip
13
==PAGE:26==
completely removed and reinserted between trials. The test-retest reliability of in-situ
thresholds measured in the study was equivalent to that of current accepted audiometric
procedures. This conclusion was confirmed by O’Brien et al. (2010) who measured in-situ
AC thresholds of 24 participants with known symmetrical and sensorineural hearing loss
with Siemens Centra HP BTE hearing instruments using both occluded and open domes
and conventional thresholds with ER-3A insert earphones at two appointments spaced
three weeks apart.
2.4.2
Validity of in-situ audiometry
Significant differences between in-situ thresholds and thresholds obtained with conven-
tional audiometry are reported by DiGiovanni and Pratt (2010), O’Brien et al. (2010),
and Kiessling et al. (2015). O’Brien et al. (2010) found that thresholds obtained in-situ
resulted in higher thresholds in dB HL for both open and closed fits, and the differ-
ences were especially prominent for low frequencies, see Figure 2.7a. Similar deviations
in low- and mid-frequency thresholds between conventional audiometry performed with
Supra-aural headphones and in-situ audiometry was reported by Kiessling et al. (2015)
across four hearing aid brands in a study of 30 adults with varying degree of symmetri-
cal sensorineural hearing loss. O’Brien et al. (2010) found that the main reason for the
discrepancies in hearing thresholds were the differences in REDDs between the insert ear-
phones, the open dome, and the occluded dome, see Figure 2.7b, and as such was a result
of the vent effect. Vents and leakage will allow ambient low-frequency noise to directly
enter into the ear canal and low-frequency sound to leave the ear canal.

O’Brien et al. (2010) concluded that in-situ audiometry using either occluded or open
domes yielded valid audiometric results when individual REDD-corrections were added.
Kiessling et al. (2015) however concluded that REDD-corrections were not included in the
fitting software of any manufacturers involved in their study. This was concluded based on
the significant deviations in thresholds between conventional and in-situ audiometry, and
the fact that indicated target curves by the software were identical regardless of whether
thresholds were entered as being measured with conventional or in-situ audiometry. The
considerable threshold differences found would thus directly affect the prescription targets,
calculated based on conventional thresholds, and hence the hearing aid output.
In addition to sound drain-off through vents and leaks, the degree of the hearing loss

was also found to affect the in-situ thresholds (DiGiovanni and Pratt, 2010; Kiessling
et al., 2015). DiGiovanni and Pratt (2010) compared in-situ thresholds to conventional
thresholds for 10 people with normal hearing and 10 people with known sensorineural
hearing loss. For both groups, in-situ thresholds differed significantly from conventional
audiometric thresholds at several frequencies important for speech intelligibility, see Figure
2.8. However, the differences were well within ±5 dB for the normal hearing group and
thus not of any clinical importance. The differences from the hearing-impaired group were
both statistically and clinically significant. Conventional thresholds were obtained with
ER-3A insert earphones, and in-situ thresholds were measured using a Widex Inteo I-19
BTE hearing aid coupled to an ER-3A earphone tip to match the amount of occlusion
in the two tests. Any effect of venting would thus be equally reflected in both sets of
thresholds. As in-situ stimuli were only higher than intended for the hearing-impaired
group, the differences in thresholds were assumed to be caused by a non-linear transducer
or amplifier.

In the study by Kiessling et al. (2015), the effect of hearing loss on in-situ thresholds
was explained partly by the changing degree of venting, which was lower for more severe
hearing losses, and partly as a boundary effect caused by the limited fitting-range of in-
situ audiometry. Variance-based deviations in thresholds of up to 10 dB were expected in
the measurements. If the conventional thresholds were below the boundary of the in-situ
range, and the in-situ thresholds were at the edge or above this range, an overestimation
of in-situ thresholds would follow. If in-situ thresholds on the other hand were below the
boundary of the in-situ range, then the data-point vanished.
Differences between brands were also observed and explained by differences in calibration
approaches. Unlike previous studies, Kiessling et al. (2015) found that hearing loss was
underestimated by in-situ at 4000 Hz for most devices. As an attempt to explain this
phenomenon, the authors considered participant ear canal volume and boundary effects
15
==PAGE:28==
caused by in-situ ranges. However, there was no reason to believe that the test subjects in
the study would have exceptionally small ear canals leading to this underestimation, and
only very few participants had hearing losses at the edge of the in-situ ranges.
Consequences of the deviations in hearing thresholds on hearing aid gain were studied
by Keidser, Yeend, et al. (2011) based on data from O’Brien et al. (2010). In the main
study, the differences in thresholds between conventional thresholds and in-situ thresholds
with an open dome were so significant that they were assumed to severely affect the
perceived sound through the hearing aid. Keidser, Yeend, et al. (2011) studied the effect
of the differences between conventional thresholds and thresholds obtained in-situ with an
occluded dome. NAL-NL1 targets for 50, 65 and 60 dB SPL inputs were calculated for the
two sets of thresholds. One third of the participants from the original study had targets
that differed by more than 3 dB root mean square (RMS) across all frequencies at one or
more of the input levels and by more than 10 dB averaged across the low frequencies 250
and 500 Hz. Seven of the eight participants for whom this difference was significant agreed
to participate in another appointment where hearing aids were fitted using either sets of
thresholds for comparison. It was concluded that the auditory experience was generally
perceptually different between the two fits, and that REDD-corrections should be applied
to in-situ thresholds either by the manufacturers in the software or by the clinician.
In summary, in previous studies there had been observed differences between conventional
and in-situ thresholds. The differences were mainly explained by difference in venting.
Other factors such as difference in audiometric ranges between the two test types and
differences in calibration approaches amongst manufacturers were also considered to in-
fluence the differences in thresholds. The differences due to venting leads to perceived
difference in the hearing aid output and should be corrected.
Clinicians at the Ear-Nose-Throat and Audiology Department at Bispebjerg Hospital
(BBH) have reasons to believe that in-situ technology has advanced. In current clini-
cal practice at BBH, both tests are currently conducted, and thresholds obtained in-situ
seem to correlate more with conventionally obtained thresholds today than what was
found in the reviewed studies.
This hypothesis is supported by Vercammen (2020), a
retrospective analysis of 162,772 conventional audiometric sessions that were compared to
measurements performed with Phonak’s in-situ audiometry module, ”AudiogramDirect”.
In that study, 95% of the examined thresholds fell within a range of ±15 dB HL, and
the average difference between thresholds obtained with AudiogramDirect and thresholds
obtained with conventional audiometry was less than 1 dB HL. The obtained results were
frequency-dependent, with a trend towards lower variation for middle frequencies.
2.4.3
Implementing REDD-corrections
The potential convenience of in-situ audiometry relies on the fact that no probe tubes
should be used. Thus, O’Brien et al. (2010) further investigated if average REDDs could
be used to accurately convert from dB HL to dB SPL at the eardrum. Interparticipant
variability in REDD was high for the closed condition, presumably due to variations in
the extend of leakage that occurred around the tip rather than variability in ear canal
volume. Consequently, average REDD-corrections are adequate for in-situ with an open
fit, but individual measurements of REDD should be used for in-situ with a closed fit.
The need for REM for the closed fit means that in-situ audiometry no longer has the
intended advantages.
Furthermore, as individual REDDs are usually not included in
clinical practice, this implies that careful consideration should be shown when performing
in-situ audiometry with a closed-fit hearing aid; especially if the conventional audiogram
is overwritten when an in-situ audiogram is reported in the fitting software. Note that
16
==PAGE:29==
in-situ audiometry with a closed fit can still be performed in cases where conventional
audiometry is not available.
It can be argued that the use of average REDD-corrections for in-situ with an open fit
means that the in-situ thresholds are no longer individualized. Indeed, some individual-
ization is lost when average REDD-corrections are implemented. However, as discussed in
Section 2.3.4, there might be need for a a cost-effectiveness analysis to evaluate the ben-
efits against the costs of providing REM. Furthermore, some degree of individualization
remains even with the use of average REDD-corrections.

2.5
Company philosophies
This study includes hearing aids from manufacturers Widex, Phonak, Oticon, Signia,
Rexton, and GN ReSound. The hearing aid manufacturers have different philosophies
towards in-situ audiometry; some manufacturers encourage the use of in-situ thresholds for
all hearing aid fittings while others use it as a tool in cases where conventional audiometry
is not an option. Importantly, though, all manufacturers recognize that in-situ thresholds
cannot replace conventional thresholds, and that conventional audiometry is necessary for
diagnostics and rehabilitation. In-situ audiometry has shown to be very relevant under
the current circumstance with COVID-19 as it allows for remote fitting (Phonak, 2020;
Cooling, 2020). This section briefly goes through each hearing aid manufacturer and their
philosophies towards in-situ audiometry.
Widex: As already mentioned, the use of in-situ thresholds was introduced by Widex to
increase accuracy of the hearing aid fitting (Ludvigsen and Tøpholm, 1997), and Widex
are strong advocates for in-situ audiometry. In-situ thresholds measured through Widex
hearing aids are denoted ”sensogram”. Kuk (2012), vice president of clinical research at
Widex USA, argues that the sensogram can increase the accuracy of the hearing aid fitting
and simplify the task of verifying audibility.
Since 2005, the Widex sensogram includes a patented vent effect compensation algorithm
called Assessment of In-Situ Acoustics (AISA) (Kuk and Nordahn, 2006). As REDD-
corrections are mainly needed due to vent effects (O’Brien et al., 2010), this can be consid-
ered as an implementation of REDD-corrections into the fitting software. The maximum
gain before feedback is directly related to the acoustic characteristics of the hearing aid
earmold or dome, and the AISA algorithm is based on that relationship. The AISA algo-
rithm predicts the overall vent effect based on the maximum gain of the hearing aid before
feedback. When thresholds are measured with the sensogram, internally in the fitting soft-
ware, the vent effect is subtracted, and the prescription targets are calculated based on
these internal thresholds. These internal thresholds, with the vent effect subtracted, are
called the ”true hearing loss” by Widex. After prescription targets are calculated, the loss
due to the vent effect is added. According to Kuk (2012), no external real-ear measure-
ment devices are necessary, when using the Widex sensogram. This is based on the fact
that all individual acoustic conditions such as tubing length, vent diameter, impedance,
residual volume etc. that could affect the intensity of the stimulus within the individual’s
ear canal are already taken into account. Importantly, though, the AISA algorithm is
based on average ears, and may thus not be reliable for atypical ears and ears that have
undergone surgery (Kuk and Nordahn, 2006).
Phonak: Phonak’s in-situ audiometry fitting module is called AudiogramDirect. It was
introduced into their fitting software for in-situ hearing tests in 2011 to optimize the
hearing aid fitting process (Miller et al., 2020). Similar to Widex, Phonak argues that the
method provides a fast and accurate point for a successful fitting when used in conjunction
with a diagnostic hearing test. AudiogramDirect also includes a vent estimation algorithm
that predicts the overall vent effect based on the feedback analysis. It is possible that vent
estimation is done by other manufacturers too, but of the hearing aid brands involved in
the study, only Widex and Phonak state this explicitly.
Phonak are careful not to encourage in-situ audiometry too much. They state that vari-
ations are expected when comparing thresholds between AudiogramDirect and a clinical
audiological test (Phonak, 2020), but if the thresholds are significantly different from the
conventional result (exceeding ±15 dB), a new hearing evaluation at the hearing clinic is
recommended.
18
==PAGE:31==
Oticon: Oticon introduced in-situ audiometry into the Genie 2 software with the 2017.2
release (Oticon, 2020b). With this update, in-situ audiometry was available for Oticon
Opn hearing aids. The aim was to improve accuracy of hearing aid fittings and patient
satisfaction (Oticon, 2020a).
Oticon views in-situ audiometry as an expansion to the
fitting that provides a lot of flexibility, a quicker “follow-up” on the thresholds and a
tool that enables a personalized hearing experience for the patient (internal communi-
cation over email, 2021). Oticon focuses on the strengths of in-situ audiometry, that it
enables convenient measurements outside the clinic (e.g., in the patient’s home) and it
enhances the quality of demonstration fittings. The hearing aid fitting is based on only
one type of audiogram and not a combination of the two types (internal communication
over email, 2021). The in-situ audiogram will thus overwrite the conventional audiogram.
The conventional audiogram will not be deleted, but nor will the fitting be based on this
audiogram. In the Genie 2 in-situ module, compensation for the selected vent or dome is
made, such that the clinician does not have to adjust the gain relative to a potential leak-
age (internal communication over email, 2021). Therefore, it is crucial that information
about fitting level, earpiece, and vents is correctly entered in the fitting software before
conducting in-situ audiometry.
Signia and Rexton: In-situ audiometry is available in the Connexx fitting software for
Signia and Rexton as an optional feature that considers the individual acoustics of the
patient’s ear canal as well as any venting effects when wearing the hearing aid.
GN ReSound: GN ReSound calls their in-situ audiometry module for insitu fitting.
Contrary to Widex, GN ReSound primarily base their hearing aid fitting on conventional
audiometry.
According to Pedersen (2012), in-situ audiometry is relevant only if the
measured thresholds are equivalent to the thresholds obtained by conventional audiometry.
To help generate results more similar to those of a conventional audiometer, the clinician is
guided to use an occluding coupling to the ear in an environment with minimal background
noise. The recommended coupling to the ear is independent of whether the actual desired
fitting is open or occluded, and this indicates that no REDD-corrections are added.
The GN ReSound philosophy behind providing insitu fitting is to offer flexibility to the
clinician to obtain the thresholds necessary to generate prescription targets (internal com-
munication over email, 2021). It is especially advantageous, in the case where providing
conventional audiometry is not an immediate option. In this case, they recommend an
in-oﬀice visit at first opportunity within a suggested time frame of 30 days. In the email
it was also stated that ”While insitu can produce thresholds quite close to those obtained
using conventional audiometry, it really depends on the hearing instrument used during
insitu. An audiometer is calibrated to a particular transducer, and insitu is calibrated
to the receiver of the hearing instrument type in a similar way.
However, variability
is introduced by the coupling to the ear, which is not standardized like in conventional
audiometry (headphones or insert earphones). With insitu fitting you might be using a
custom earmold for one patient and an occluded dome for another. Even within the same
ear coupling type there can also be variability.”
In-situ audiometry modules of all six manufacturers show in-situ tones in dB HL. No
information from any of the manufacturers is provided on how or how often calibration or
software checks are performed.
In summary, all companies except Widex offer in-situ audiometry as a means of making
the fitting process more flexible as well as including some individual parameters to the
thresholds. They are very careful to state that in-situ can neither replace conventional
audiometry, nor REM. Widex on the other hand consistently argue that in-situ thresholds

REM.

3
Methodology
The aim of this thesis was to give an answer to the research questions by an experimental
approach. All analyzed data was collected during the thesis project period. Experiments
were carried out by myself in the CHBC lab from April-July 2021.
To address the four research questions, four individual experiments were designed. Ex-
periment 1 measured hearing aid output where an in-situ audiogram was reported in the
fitting software in combination with different conventional audiograms. In Experiment 2,
hearing aid output was measured when only an in-situ audiogram was reported in the soft-
ware and compared to hearing aid output measured when only a conventional audiogram
was reported in the software. In-situ stimuli were measured in Experiment 3, and finally,
in experiment 4, conventional audiometry using insert earphones and in-situ audiometry
with hearing aids from four brands were performed on test subjects
3.1
Experiment 1: Effect of the conventional audiogram
The aim of these measurements was to investigate if the conventional audiogram was
overwritten when an in-situ audiogram was reported. To evaluate this, both a conven-
tional audiogram and an in-situ audiogram were reported in the software when fitting the
hearing aids. The reported in-situ audiogram was the same in all measurements, whereas
the conventional audiogram varied between measurements and simulated either a milder
or more severe hearing loss as compared to the in-situ audiogram. If the conventional
audiogram was disregarded when in-situ is used for hearing aid fitting, the hearing aid
output would be the same regardless of which conventional audiogram was reported.
For comparison, measurements were also performed on hearing aids fitted using the con-
ventional audiograms exclusively. The purpose of fitting the hearing aids using the con-
ventional audiograms exclusively was to determine whether an actual significant difference
between hearing aid output for these hearing losses exists. Furthermore, it served as in-
dication of how the conventional audiograms would affect the output-curves (SPL as a
function of frequency) if the fitting were in fact (partly) based on these.
The term ”audiogram type” will be used to describe whether the hearing loss is reported
in a conventional audiogram or in an in-situ audiogram. The term ”acoustic coupling”
will be used to describe the specific dome selected, i.e., whether a standard open dome or
standard occluded dome was used.

hence output of the hearing aid, and these parameters were therefore kept the same for
all the measurements. However, this information is not expected to influence whether or
not the conventional audiogram is overwritten by the in-situ audiogram. In general, all
parameters in the fitting software were kept the same, except for the degree of hearing loss,
the audiogram type, and the acoustic coupling. The prescription target was NAL-NL2,
see Section 2.3.3.

3.1.3
Procedure
Each hearing aid was connected to the corresponding manufacturer’s fitting software con-
trolled on a PC. The Widex and Phonak fitting software clearly indicate that vent-effect is
estimated based on the feedback analysis. Therefore, it was decided that realistic feedback
analysis was needed for all measurements. Before measurements of hearing aid output were
commenced, feedback analysis was performed using the HATS. Domes used in the feedback

power/double domes). After the feedback analysis, the hearing aid was connected to the
Aﬀinity Compact. Note that the de facto coupling between the hearing aid and coupler
was closed in this setup. This was however not a problem, since output was compared
within groups.

trolled in Aﬀinity Compact Suite. Particularly, a specific protocol in Aﬀinity Compact
Suite was used which included RECD and measurements of REAR (speech 65 dB SPL).

In all measurements RECD was set to zero in Aﬀinity Compact Suite, such that the Aﬀinity
Compact Suite would act more as a sound level meter. In the REAR-test, hearing aid
output (dB SPL) was measured in the frequency range 250-8000 Hz for each combination of
hearing aids, hearing loss, audiogram type, and acoustic coupling with international speech
test signal (ISTS) stimuli for 15 seconds. Hereafter, the resulting curves are referred to as
output-curves.
3.1.4
Measurement error and noise
Errors that could affect the measurements were ambient noise, hearing device effects,
microphone location effects, and hearing aid and coupler connection. Each of these effects
will be discussed in the following.
The Aﬀinity Compact is designed with a special sound-absorbing silicone base. It is not a
sound proof test box, but in relatively quiet surroundings, the ISTS-stimuli at 65 dB SPL
provided by the box dominate ambient noise.
There might be inaccuracies in measurements due to hearing device performance. Mea-
suring hearing aid output with the same hearing aid setting did not result in exactly the
same output-curve every time. Fluctuations due to ambient noise and noise due to hearing
device effects were estimated by conducting ten measurements of output-curves for each
configuration of hearing aid, hearing loss, audiogram type, and acoustic coupling, and
then reporting the mean and standard deviation as a function of frequency.
Hearing aid output was compared within groups, and thus errors due to coupling effects
were minimized by performing measurements with a given hearing aid and acoustic cou-
pling with the same connection between the hearing aid and the coupler for all hearing
losses and for both audiogram types.
To minimize errors due to microphone location effects, the position of the coupler, hearing
aid, and external microphone in the Aﬀinity Compact box was kept as still as possible
during measurements. For measurements with Oticon, Rexton, Signia, and Widex this
meant that the box was kept closed during measurements. To obtain connection to Phonak
and ReSound hearing aids, it was necessary to turn the hearing aids off and on. Thus, to
estimate errors due to microphone location for these hearing aids, the Aﬀinity Compact
was opened between each measurement and the hearing aid was turned off and on. The
coupler and external microphone were kept as fixed as possible in the box using putty.
3.1.5
Data assessment and statistics
After data collection, the data was saved as an XML file and analyzed in Python 3.8.3,
see Appendix C.1.1.
As a means for determining whether output-curves were significantly different, the method
by Keidser, Yeend, et al. (2011) was adopted. Output-curves that differed by more than 3
dB root mean square (RMS) across all frequencies, or by more than 10 dB at a single fre-
quency, were considered to be significantly different from each other. The RMS deviation
is a measure of the overall level of the signal.

Calibration
The Audacity recordings were saved in Waveform Audio File Format (WAV) in 32-bit
floating-point format and analyzed in Python 3.8.3. The 32-bit floating-point WAV-file
takes dimensionless values from −1 to +1. The data was converted to a relative dB-scale
by the command 20 log10(peak_height), see Equation 2.1. Calibration was performed to
convert the amplitude of the waves to dB SPL, for detailed Python script see Appendix C.2.
For calibration, a Brüel and Kjær Type 4230 Sound Level Calibrator 94 dB 1000 Hz was
used. The tone was played in the ear of the HATS for 10 seconds, recorded with Audacity,
and saved as a WAV file. The estimated wave amplitude at 94 dB SPL, peak_height, was
estimated as the mean of all wave period maxima during the time that the sound level
calibrator was turned on. The correction factor, that converts from dimensionless to dB
SPL, was then found by subtracting the mean of the converted peak heights from 94 dB.
The standard deviation of this estimate was added to additional measurement errors using
the law of error propagation.
To verify calibration, it was investigated whether 1) the relative intensity difference was
preserved through calibration, and 2) the resulting SPL-curves were comparable with
conversion of dB HL to dB SPL scale using RETSPLs for insert earphones, see Figure 2.1.

3.4.6
Data assessment and statistics
Threshold data was analyzed using ”analysis of variance” (ANOVA) tests. This type of
statistical test is used to study whether two or more group means are equal. The null
hypothesis being that the means of the groups are equal. Two different types of ANOVA
were performed: the two-way mixed design ANOVA (Mixed ANOVA using SPSS statistics,
2018), and the two-way repeated measures ANOVA (Repeated measures ANOVA, 2018).
The primary purpose of these tests was to study how patient thresholds data (dependent
variable) was effected by the independent variables test-ear, frequency, and transducer
(conventional, Rexton in-situ, Phonak in-situ, Oticon in-situ, Widex in-situ).
Most powerful is the two-way repeated measures ANOVA, where each subject is compared
to itself between independent variables. To test the effect of transducers, data must be
collected at all tested frequencies for all transducers for all participants included in the
ANOVA. As the tested frequencies varied between hearing aid brands, the comparison was
done pairwise; in-situ thresholds against conventional thresholds for each brand. Hence, a
total of four repeated measures ANOVAs were calculated, see Appendix C.3. A schematic
of the repeated measures design for Oticon is shown in Figure 3.5. Similar designs were
made for Phonak, Rexton, and Widex for relevant frequencies. As the repeated measures
ANOVA did not allow missing values, subjects, for whom thresholds were outside in-situ or
insert earphone range, were omitted from this particular analysis, and individual threshold
data was instead investigated in these few cases. On this background, one participant was
omitted from ANOVAs with Oticon, Rexton, and Phonak, and three participants were
omitted from the Widex ANOVA.
The repeated measures ANOVA was not suitable for determining whether test-ear in this
study was significant, as thresholds were only measured on one ear. Instead, a mixed
design ANOVA was calculated on the entire data set to test for significance in test-ear
using the pingouin.mixed_anova function included in the Python Pingouin library.
The ANOVA outputs a p-value and an F-ratio. According to Understanding analysis of
variance (ANOVA) and the F-test (2016), an ANOVA partitions variability of a data
set into two components: variability between groups (variation due to transducers), and
variability within groups (variation for a given transducer). The F-ratio is the ratio of
variation between group means to the variation within groups. Under the null-hypothesis,
these two quantities are expected to be roughly equal, and this produces an F-ratio of
approximately 1.

The p-value is the probability, given that all groups have the same mean, that random
sampling would result in means as least as far apart as observed in this experiment. A large
p-value indicates that the group means do not differ, and that the observed differences
between groups are likely caused by chance. A small p-value, on the other hand, indicates
that the group means in fact differ. As a general rule, a p-value less than 0.05 indicates that
the null hypothesis can be rejected. To identify differences, post-hoc tests are necessary.

5
Discussion
In the following, the meaning and implications of the obtained results are explored and
discussed. The results for each research question are interpreted individually and followed
by a joint discussion on the general potential of in-situ audiometry that combines results
from all four research questions. Recommendation for clinical practice is debated.
5.1
Effect of the conventional audiogram
In modern hearing aid fitting software, two types of audiograms can be reported: the
conventional audiogram and/or the in-situ audiogram. Oftentimes the clinician indicates
in the fitting software which audiogram type that should be used for the hearing aid
fitting. For the brands tested in this study, Oticon, Phonak, Rexton, Signia, and ReSound
allowed the clinician to make this choice. In the Widex COMPASS GPS software, no
such choice was presented. This indicates that the conventional audiogram is completely
disregarded when an in-situ audiogram is reported for Oticon, Phonak, Rexton, Signia, and
ReSound, but that the Widex software may still include information from the conventional
audiogram. To bring transparency to the hearing aid fitting process, it was investigated,
for all brands, how the conventional audiogram influenced the prescribed hearing aid gain
when in-situ was included in the hearing aid fitting session.
It was expected that the hearing losses N1, N2, and N3 would require different levels of
amplification. For all hearing aids fitted using conventional audiograms with both open
and occluded domes, it was shown that these hearing losses lead to differences in REAR
exceeding 3.6 dB RMS and up to 17 dB RMS. The differences found were significant and
are expected to result in different listening experiences for the hearing aid user.
The expectations were that the observed differences would be reduced when not only the
conventional audiogram was reported in the software, but also an in-situ audiogram was re-
ported. REAR was measured for in-situ audiogram N2 combined with either conventional
audiogram N1, N2, or N3. A comparison was made to the REAR for in-situ audiogram
N2 without reported conventional audiogram. The differences in REAR between in-situ
audiogram N2 and in-situ audiogram N2 combined with any of the conventional audio-
grams were below 3 dB RMS for all tested brands and domes, except Phonak with an
open dome. For Phonak with an open dome, a 3.1 dB RMS difference was observed and
the maximal difference exceeded 10 dB for frequencies around 1500 Hz. However, the
observed differences in REAR between in-situ audiogram N2 and in-situ audiogram N2
combined with conventional audiogram N1 (N3) were not correlated with the observed
differences in REAR between conventional audiogram N2 and conventional audiograms
N1 (N3).
For measurements with the Phonak hearing aid, the Aﬀinity Compact was
opened between each measurement and the hearing aid turned on/off possibly changing
the position of the hearing aid. In addition to random errors, this measurement technique
might introduce a systematic error due to the microphone position over time affecting the
REAR measurements.
Adding a conventional audiogram when an in situ audiogram was reported in the software
did not affect the REAR measurements. The measurements thus show no evidence that
the conventional audiogram affects the prescribed gain when in-situ is used for hearing
aid fitting for any of the tested hearing aids.
A possible explanation to why the in-
situ audiogram overwrites the conventional audiogram in these cases, is that the fitting
In-situ Audiometry
47
==PAGE:60==
software assumes that the conventional audiogram is outdated when an in-situ audiogram
is reported. Based on company philosophies, see Section 2.5, this is the reasoning behind
the choice for GN ReSound and Oticon.
Their philosophies behind providing in-situ
audiometry is to offer flexibility. For GN ReSound, this is in particular in the case where
providing conventional audiometry is not an immediate option. Another explanation is
that manufacturers expect in-situ thresholds to be more precise; this might very well
describe the strategy of Widex.
An additional comment on Widex is appropriate here. In these measurements, all hearing
losses reported in the conventional audiograms were sensorineural with identical AC and
BC thresholds. In the Widex GPS COMPASS software, the clinician is given an additional
choice: If the conventional audiogram includes an air-bone gap, it can be chosen whether
or not this air-bone gap should be ignored. It is thus possible that the COMPASS GPS
software does not completely overwrite the conventional audiogram in cases where an air-
bone gap is present. Further investigations are required to determine if this is in fact the
case, and also, in case BC thresholds affect prescription targets, to determine whether the
original BC thresholds are used or the original air-bone gap is used to calculate prescription
targets.
5.2
REDD-corrections
According to O’Brien et al. (2010), in-situ thresholds are as valid as conventional thresholds
when proper REDD-corrections are made. It is natural to ask whether such corrections
are implemented in modern fitting software. Two possible implementations of REDD-
corrections were investigated: REDD-corrections
(1) as an adjustment of prescribed gain for in-situ thresholds compared to prescribed
gain for conventional thresholds, and
(2) as compensation directly in the in-situ stimuli.
In the following, these two implementations will be referred to as (1) and (2) respectively.
Based on O’Brien et al. (2010), the expected REDD-corrections are frequency-dependent
and largest for low frequencies. Furthermore, implementation (1) would imply that output-
curves based on an in-situ audiogram were lower than output-curves based on a conven-
tional audiogram.
This would not be the case with implementation (2), instead this
implementation would imply that in-situ stimuli for open fits were higher than in-situ
stimuli for closed fits.
5.2.1
Implementation 1: REAR difference between audiogram types
In Experiment 2, hearing aids were fitted based on only one audiogram type: either a
conventional or an in-situ audiogram was reported in the software. In general, it was
found that REAR was unaffected by audiogram type.
Exceptions to this general rule
were found for Phonak and Widex closed-fit hearing aids, and these measurements will be
discussed in detail in the following.
The differences seen for the Phonak closed-fit hearing aid agreed with the expectations for
implementation (1) type REDD-corrections. REAR based on in-situ audiogram was lower
than REAR based on conventional audiogram, and the differences were mainly observed for
frequencies below 1000 Hz. Furthermore, the magnitude of the difference was comparable
with REDD3, the average difference in REDD between insert earphones and in-situ closed
fit hearing aids reported by O’Brien et al. (2010), see Table 2.1.
A significant difference in REAR between audiogram types was also found for the Widex
48
==PAGE:61==
closed-fit hearing aid for hearing loss N3. The Same significant difference was not found
for hearing loss N2. In the Widex COMPASS GPS fitting software, a ”sensogram EAT”
(equivalent adult threshold) was computed based on the thresholds obtained in-situ and
the estimated vent effect. Figure 5.1 summarizes fitting sessions where in-situ audiograms
N2 and N3 respectively were reported to fit a Widex hearing aid with an occluded dome.
The estimated in-situ vent effect was 2.4 in both cases. The sensogram EAT deviated
significantly from the sensogram at frequencies below 750 Hz for both hearing loss N2 and
N3. However, no gain was prescribed at low frequencies for N2, and thus no significant
difference in REAR between the two audiogram types was observed. Based on Figure
5.1b, the correction factor for in-situ thresholds were -25 at 250 Hz and -10 dB at 500 Hz.
The corresponding differences in hearing aid output were 23.1±0.9 dB at 250 Hz and 5±1
dB at 516 Hz (measured frequency closest to 500 Hz).

The differences in REAR between audiogram types found for Phonak and Widex closed-fit
hearing aids matched the criteria for REDD-corrections quite well and are likely an at-
tempt to provide ”individualized” REDD-corrections. As discussed in Section 2.5, venting
algorithms are based on average ears, and will thus be less accurate the more the patient’s
ear deviates from the average ear. Based on the magnitude of the observed differences in
REAR between the audiogram types, a larger gain adjustment was made for Widex than
for Phonak. The discrepancies in gain adjustment between Phonak and Widex closed-fit
hearing aids are consistent with the study by O’Brien et al. (2010), where large deviations
in REDD were found for in-situ through standard occluded domes. The same ear, the
ear of the HATS, was used to perform feedback analysis and fit the hearing aids for both
Widex and Phonak, but different occluded domes where used. A medium sized dome was
used for Phonak, and a large sized dome was used for Widex, and this can possibly affect
the amount of occlusion provided by the domes.
In summary, evidence was found that both Widex and Phonak hearing aids include im-
plementation (1) type REDD-corrections with closed fits. Based on these measurements,
none of the tested hearing aids included implementation (1) type REDD-corrections with
open fits, and Signia, Rexton, Oticon, and ReSound did not include implementation (1)
type REDD-corrections with closed fits either.

5.2.2
Implementation 2: Compensation in in-situ stimuli
If any REDD-corrections were made when using in-situ with open domes, they were nec-
essarily implementation (2) type REDD-corrections with compensation in in-situ stimuli.
Indeed, evidence was found that in-situ stimuli had higher intensities at low frequencies
for open domes as compared to in-situ stimuli for occluded domes for all tested hear-
ing aids except ReSound. The five brands, Signia, Rexton, Phonak, Oticon, and Widex,
recommend that in-situ audiometry is performed with the acoustic coupling, i.e., specific
dome or earmold, chosen for the patient. GN ReSound stands out amongst the hearing
aid manufacturers as the only company that recommends performing in-situ audiometry
with a closed fit, even when the patient is fitted with an open dome. The choice made
by GN ReSound is likely related to their philosophy towards in-situ audiometry. As dis-
cussed in Section 2.5, GN ReSound prefers conventional audiometry and considers this
method more reliable and accurate. Measuring in-situ thresholds with a closed fit ensures
that the obtained thresholds are as close as possible to conventional thresholds. In-situ
audiometry is merely included in the software to allow for some flexibility and is mostly
recommended in cases where conventional audiometry cannot be conducted (COVID-19,
patient illness, lack of equipment in developing countries, etc.). The reluctance to in-situ
could be based on the fact that prescription targets are based on conventional thresh-
olds and that discrepancies between conventional and in-situ thresholds were previously
reported.
As expected, a loss of intensity was observed as a consequence of the open dome for all six
hearing aids. The effect was frequency-dependent and most severe for low frequencies. The
amount of low-frequency loss from the open dome can be quantified as the difference in SPL
between the open/closed and open conditions. In Figure 5.2, this difference is depicted
along with REDD1 and REDD2, the average differences in REDD between transducers
obtained by O’Brien et al. (2010), see Table 2.1. These results suggest that the amount
of low-frequency loss was consistent across all tested hearing aids except Widex, and the
difference in SPL between the open/closed and the open conditions was of magnitude
comparable with REDD1, the average difference in SPL between insert earphones and
in-situ through open domes reported by O’Brien et al. (2010). At 500 Hz, the difference
in SPL in the ear of the HATS between the open dome and the occluded dome was 9 ± 3
dB for Widex, and exceeded 20 dB for all other hearing aids. At 500 Hz, the Widex
sensogram was limited upwards by 30 dB HL and this made the estimated SPL in the
ear of the HATS less accurate. Furthermore, larger domes were used for Widex, and it
is possible that this affected the amount of venting from the open dome. For an exact
comparison between hearing aids, it is recommended that the same dome sizes be used in
all measurements.
Comparison of SPL for the open/closed and closed conditions showed significant deviations
for Signia, Rexton, Phonak, Oticon, and Widex. The deviations were frequency-dependent
with an increased difference at low frequencies. As intensity of in-situ stimuli were depen-
dent on the acoustic coupling indicated in the fitting software, these measurements show
that implementation (2) type REDD-corrections were made in fitting software for Oticon,
Rexton, Signia, Phonak, and Widex. Differences in SPL between open/closed and closed
conditions for ReSound were within ±5 dB, indicating that no implementation (2) type
REDD-corrections were made in the ReSound Smart Fit software.
The magnitude of SPL differences between open/closed and closed conditions varied be-
tween hearing aids even when disregarding ReSound. Discrepancies between Oticon, Rex-
ton, Signia, Phonak, and Widex may be due to either measurement error, deviations in
unintentional leakage from the occluded domes, differences in the measured in-situ stimu-

5.2.3
Implementations of REDD-corrections in modern fitting software
According to O’Brien et al. (2010), in-situ thresholds are as valid as conventional thresh-
olds if appropriate REDD-corrections are applied. The present study showed that REDD-
corrections were implemented in current fitting software for Signia, Rexton, Phonak, Oti-
con, and Widex.
Fitting software from all five brands included REDD-corrections for
open fits implemented as stimulus compensation in the in-situ modules. In addition, ev-
idence was found that Phonak and Widex implemented REDD-corrections for closed-fit
hearing aids as adjustments of the prescription targets for in-situ thresholds compared
to conventional thresholds.
ReSound did neither provide any implementation (1) nor
implementation (2) type REDD-corrections.
The possibility remains that in-situ stimuli presented through occluded domes are adjusted
in modern fitting software to correct the difference in REDD between insert earphones and
in-situ through occluded domes found by O’Brien et al. (2010). This was not investigated
in the present study, and whether such closed-fit REDD-corrections are implemented in
modern fitting software cannot be investigated by measuring in-situ stimuli, as there is
nothing to compare the closed-fit in-situ stimuli to; it is not an option to choose ”occluded
foam tip” as acoustic coupling in the fitting software, and of the six manufacturers included
in this study, only two (Phonak and ReSound) allowed the use of in-situ audiometry with
a dome/earmold different from the selected acoustic coupling.
As a side note however, given the evidence that Phonak and Widex implements closed-fit
REDD-corrections otherwise, it is unlikely that additional closed-fit REDD-corrections are
implemented in fitting software for Phonak and Widex, and as the recommendation in the
51
==PAGE:64==
GN ReSound in-situ audiometry module is to establish conditions as similar as possible to
those of conventional audiometry, there is no reason to believe that ReSound compensates
the in-situ closed-fit stimuli either.
It could be investigated whether such REDD-corrections are implemented in modern fit-
ting software by restudying how conventional thresholds compare to in-situ thresholds
measured through occluded domes and compare to the results obtained by O’Brien et al.
(2010). Measurement of REDD could also be included to compare REDD between the two
transducers. Such a study would also illuminate whether the REDD-corrections imple-
mented for Phonak and Widex closed-fit hearing aids are valid compared to conventional
thresholds. It is recommended that further research on in-situ through occluded domes is
conducted.
The study by Kiessling et al. (2015) clearly stated that REDD-corrections were not in-
cluded in any software modules in their study. This is in direct contrast to the results
obtained in the present study. In-situ technology has evolved since 2015. Besides imple-
mentations of REDD-corrections, the frequency and intensity range in which hearing aid
manufacturers offer to perform in-situ audiometry has expanded. In the study by Kiessling
et al. (2015), some in-situ implementations only allowed for measurements above 40 dB
HL. This is no longer the case, current in-situ ranges can be seen in Appendix A.3. These
technological developments in turn might mean that in-situ nowadays is much more accu-
rate, and that there may not be any (major) differences in prescribed gain for thresholds
obtained conventionally and in-situ for patients suffering from a symmetrical sensorineural
hearing loss.
5.3
Validity of in-situ audiometry through open domes
In this study, it was observed that the average interparticipant differences between con-
ventional and in-situ thresholds were within ±5 dB for all tested hearing aids across all
frequencies from 250-6000 Hz. The average observed differences are thus not of any clin-
ical importance, as thresholds were measured with a measurement error of 5 dB due to
the Hugson Westlake procedure. This suggests that implementation of REDD-correction
in the in-situ audiometry modules have led in-situ thresholds to be far more comparable
to conventional thresholds today.
However, for both Phonak and Oticon, the thresh-
olds obtained using in-situ audiometry were statistically different from the conventional
thresholds.
The individual differences between conventional thresholds and Phonak in-situ thresholds
were within ±10 dB across all tested frequencies. These deviations are within what is
expected in clinical practice confirming the validity of Phonak in-situ thresholds as com-
pared to conventional thresholds. This is in agreement with observations by clinicians at
BBH and the analysis of Vercammen (2020) that concluded that the mean differences in
thresholds were negligible from a clinical point of view based on a much larger dataset.
Large individual differences in thresholds between conventional and Oticon in-situ thresh-
olds were observed at 125 Hz. However, only two participants had thresholds within the
boundary of in-situ range at 125 Hz and they differed (conventional — in-situ) by 15 dB
and 20 dB respectively from conventional thresholds. Even though these may seem like
large differences, it could also be a coincidence for these two particular participants. No
general effects can be determined solely on these two measurements. The individual in-
situ thresholds were within ±10 dB of conventional thresholds at frequencies 250-4000 Hz,
and within ±15 at 8000 Hz. The general significant overestimation of in-situ thresholds
at low frequencies reported by O’Brien et al. (2010) and Kiessling et al. (2015) was not
52
==PAGE:65==
observed. To my knowledge, the REDD-corrections proposed by O’Brien et al. (2010) are
the only publicly proposed REDD-corrections, and thresholds at exactly 125 and 8000 Hz
were not measured due to limitations in in-situ range.
In summary, all observed differences, average and individual, between conventional and
in-situ thresholds obtained with any of the hearing aids from Rexton, Phonak, Oticon, and
Widex were within clinically expected deviations confirming the validity of AC thresholds
for real test subjects obtained using in-situ audiometry with standard open domes against
conventional audiometry using insert earphones.
5.4
Clinical implication
Clinical implication for firstly in-situ audiometry with standard open domes and secondly
in-situ audiometry with standard occluded domes will be discussed in the following.
Patients fit with standard open domes
In modern fitting software for Signia, Rexton, Phonak, Oticon, and Widex, REDD-
corrections are implemented to compensate for low-frequency sound leakage due to an
open dome. For all five brands, the in-situ stimulus levels depend on the selected acoustic
coupling to the ear. It is thus crucial that information on acoustic coupling is correctly re-
ported in the fitting software before performing in-situ audiometry, as was already pointed
out in Section 2.5. If all settings and selections are correctly reported in the software, ap-
propriate REDD-corrections are automatically made and the clinician should not worry
about correcting thresholds manually.
This study found, in accordance with the con-
clusion by O’Brien et al. (2010), that thresholds obtained in-situ through standard open
domes in patients with purely sensorineural hearing losses are as valid as conventional
thresholds.
A mistake in reported acoustic coupling, when e.g. a closed fit is chosen but the actual fit
is open, will lead to significantly different (in this example less) amplification for especially
low frequencies.
In software for Oticon and Widex, the audiologist is forced to review settings on the
acoustic coupling. In software for Signia, Rexton, and Phonak, the audiologist should
manually find and adjust the acoustic coupling. Additionally, in software from Signia,
Rexton, Oticon, and Widex, the selected acoustic coupling is indicated on the screen
while performing in-situ audiometry, to remind the clinician which acoustic coupling the
fitting software expects.
ReSound does not make compensation for the open dome. It is thus important to either
follow the recommended steps in the in-situ module of the ReSound smart fit software
or to perform in-situ with the open dome and manually correct the in-situ thresholds.
This could be done either by measuring individual REDD or by applying average REDD-
corrections, see REDD1 in Table 2.1.
Patients fit with standard occluded domes
The recommendation from O’Brien et al. (2010) was that in-situ thresholds obtained with
occluded domes should be corrected using individual REDD. Keidser, Yeend, et al. (2011)
confirmed that in-situ thresholds obtained with occluded domes in general led to perceived
differences in hearing aid output compared to conventional thresholds. It was however not
investigated if average REDD-corrections applied to the closed-fit in-situ thresholds would
lead to the same perceived difference in hearing aid output.
The present study did not investigate the validity of in-situ thresholds obtained through
occluded domes and thus provides no solid basis for discussing whether in-situ thresholds
53
==PAGE:66==
obtained through occluded domes with modern hearing aids should be accompanied by
REM. There are reasons to believe that Phonak and Widex provides REDD-corrections
also for the occluded dome, however additional research is necessary to investigate if these
implemented adjustments result in valid in-situ thresholds as compared to conventional
thresholds. The analysis by Vercammen (2020), concluding that AudiogramDirect thresh-
olds and conventional thresholds were strongly correlated, did not specify the acoustic cou-
plings of the included observations. Thus, potentially in-situ thresholds obtained through
closed-fit hearing aids were included in the analysis and this would indicate that also in-
situ thresholds obtained through occluded domes are more correlated with conventional
thresholds today than was the case in previous studies.
Until validity of in-situ thresholds through occluded domes is confirmed, careful consider-
ation should be taken when performing in-situ audiometry with a closed-fit hearing aid.
It is recommended that thresholds are either obtained conventionally, or that REM are
conducted to ensure that the proper gain is provided by the hearing aid. Furthermore,
in-situ with occluded domes can still be performed in cases where conventional audiometry
is not available.
For patients fit with standard occluded domes, Phonak and Widex software estimate
REDD-corrections based on the feedback analysis.
In the fitting software, the chosen
acoustic coupling, receiver, and tubing length should be selected. With all settings in place,
feedback analysis should be performed before in-situ audiometry is commenced. During
the various software fitting steps, the clinician is guided to perform feedback analysis
before in-situ audiometry.
If the acoustic coupling is later changed in the software, a
warning message will show in the software for both Phonak and Widex, and the measured
in-situ thresholds will be reset.
Fitting software for Signia, Rexton, Oticon, and ReSound prescribe the same amount
of hearing aid gain based on in-situ thresholds as based on conventional thresholds. It
is still important that the correct acoustic coupling is chosen before performing in-situ
audiometry, but there is no evidence that feedback analysis affects the in-situ prescription
targets any differently than it affects conventional prescription targets.
5.5
Potential of in-situ audiometry
The initial idea of in-situ audiometry was to increase accuracy of the hearing aid fitting. By
including individual characteristics directly to the thresholds, the ambition was to bypass
the need for REM. The level of individualization is however diminished by the need for
REDD-corrections. These average corrections are however the best qualified estimates on
appropriate corrections for the individual unless REM are made.
The standard best practice is to accompany thresholds with real-ear acoustics using REM.
Studies on the benefits of REM on hearing outcomes in adults in general show that REM
have a statistically significant positive effect on both speech intelligibility and user pref-
erence (Almufarrij, Dillon, and Munro, 2021). However, further research is necessary to
determine whether the benefits are worth the cost of providing REM. It might be this
argument that is reflected in the consultant responses from HørNu and PAKS on the pro-
posal from Sundhedsstyrelsen discussed in Section 2.3.4. For the time being, REM is not
required in Denmark, and is oftentimes not performed. The present study indicates that
in-situ audiometry through open domes gives the same hearing aid output compared to
conventional audiometry in patients who have a sensorineural symmetrical hearing loss
when no REM are conducted. In practice, this leads to a necessity to do conventional
audiometry at least at the first audiology session but not, or less, in follow-up sessions.
54
==PAGE:67==
In principle, in-situ audiometry could be made accessible for patients suffering from an
asymmetric hearing loss by implementing masking into in-situ audiometry; white noise
played in the non-test ear from another hearing aid or hearing device.
However, this
does not eliminate the need for BC thresholds that cannot be obtained in-situ through
air-conduction hearing devices. Thus, whenever there is a need for measuring BC thresh-
olds, in-situ audiometry should be abandoned and conventional audiometry should be
performed.

6
Conclusion
In the present study it was shown that a conventional audiogram reflecting a purely
sensorineural hearing loss is overwritten by the in-situ audiogram for all manufacturers
included in the study. As a consequence, the test should be redone or erased from the
fitting software if there is any doubt about the the in-situ thresholds.
Based on results of the present study, it can be concluded that REDD-corrections are
implemented in current fitting software for Signia, Rexton, Phonak, Oticon, and Widex.
The fitting software from all five brands includes REDD-corrections for open fits imple-
mented as stimulus compensation in the in-situ modules. As long as the selections and
settings entered in the fitting software are correct, in-situ audiometry through standard
open domes is as valid as conventional audiometry in patients who have a sensorineural
symmetrical hearing loss without any additional manual corrections.
In addition, evidence was found that Phonak and Widex implement REDD-corrections
for closed-fit hearing aids as adjustments of the prescription targets for in-situ thresh-
olds compared to conventional thresholds. Further investigations on the validity of in-situ
thresholds through occluded domes with modern hearing aids and fitting software is recom-
mended. And as such, clinicians should be careful when reporting an in-situ audiogram in
the software when using occluded domes, especially since the in-situ audiogram overwrites
the conventional audiogram.
