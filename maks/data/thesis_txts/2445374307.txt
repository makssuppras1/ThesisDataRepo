Abstract 
The purpose of this thesis is, firstly, to develop processes for fabricating electrodes, and 
especially cathodes; and secondly, to use the fabricated electrodes in a Solid Acid Electrolytic 
Cell (SAEC), operating at 300 OC, for the electrochemical conversion of carbon dioxide (CO2) 
to methane (CH4) or to other organic fuels. The solid acid electrolyte used, was a CDP-SiC-
based electrolyte and the anodes were IrO2-based. Processes for fabricating copper-zinc-based 
(brass) cathodes were developed, due to the potential of brass to electrocatalyse efficiently the 
reduction of CO2. A one-step, electroplating method that could fabricate brass cathodes, 
consisted of the Cu0,65Zn0,35 phase, was established. Based on this method, stainless-steel felts 
were electroplated at 25 OC, 12 mA/dm2, for 50 minutes, using an electroplating solution 
containing: 30 g/L CuCN,  9 g/L Zn(CN)2, 55 g/L NaCN, 10 g/L Na2CO3 and 10 g/L. It was 
also achieved to develop a method for fabricating Nickel-pellets and brass pellets of specific 
dimensions and 40% porosity. Regarding the IrO2-based anodes, it was shown that the rolling 
compression of their felts, increased the deposition rate of IrO2 and increased the IrO2 load, 
from 3,3g/cm2 to 5,1 g/cm2. Electrolysis tests conducted with the fabricated Ni-cathodes at 8 
bars, produced CH4 with a maximum current efficiency of 43,7% at 11,3 mA/cm2, whereas the 
maximum current efficiencies of CO and H2 were 13,3% and 1,6% at 17 mA/cm2. The decrease 
of pressure to 4 bars, enhanced the CH4 formation and increased its maximum, current density 
to 60%, at 11,3 mA/cm2. Electrolysis tests conducted with the fabricated brass cathodes, at 
300OC and 8 bars, produced CH4, H2, CH3OCH3 (DME) and traces of CH3CHO and HCHO. 
Using an electroplated brass cathode with thick brass layer, rough surface and the Cu0,65Zn0,35 
phase as main phase, was achieved to produce DME at 10,6 mA/cm2 and 1,8 V. However, tests 
with cathodes consisted again of the Cu0,65Zn0,35 phase, but with thinner and smoother brass 
layers, produced only CH4 and H2. The testing of a brass-pellet cathode that was consisted 
mainly of the CuZn phase, indicated the formation of CH4, H2 and traces of CH3CHO and 
HCHO, at 11,3 mA/cm2 and  1,7 V. Cathodes consisted of the Cu0,75Zn0,25 phase, with thick 
brass coatings (≅13μm) and either smooth or rough surfaces, produced CH4 and mainly H2, at 
35,5 mA/cm2 and 1,9 V. This increased production of H2 can be attributed to the enhancement 
of the water’s oxidation reaction, due to the increased IrO2 load of the anode (5.1 g/cm2). The 
XRD analysis on the tested brass cathodes, before and after their testing, did not detect any 
Cu2O phase that could lead to the formation of CH3OH, but it verified the phase transition of 
the brass samples towards richer in Cu phases which may increase the possibilities to form 
metallic Cu. The cathode that showed the higher resistance to this transition was the MEK, 
which had produced DME, and its phase changed from Cu0, 65Zn0,35 to CuZn.

Preface 
This thesis is submitted as a partly fulfillment of the requirements for the Master of Science 
(M.Sc.) in Sustainable Energy of the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The experiments 
of this thesis were conducted at the laboratories of the Electrochemistry Section, which is one 
of the sections of DTU Energy Department, and were supervised by Erik Christensen, Aleksey 
Nikiforov, Irina Petrushina and Niels J. Njerrum. The experimental process started at 1st of 
September 2018 and completed at the 31ST of January 2019. The project was a collaboration 
between DTU Energy, DTU Mechanical Engineering, Danish Power Systems ApS and Elplatek 
A/S.

1. Introduction 
 
Among the greenhouse gases, carbon dioxide (CO2) is considered the most common in planet 
Earth. CO2 is of vital importance for the survival and the development of the Earth’s flora, 
while it is also a raw material for various, important, chemical processes of the industry. It can 
be produced by natural processes, such as volcano activities, soils, inland waters e.t.c ([1,2], 
but it is mainly produced by the various industrial (e.g cement industry) [3] and transport 
processes of humans (cars, shipping, airplanes e.t.c) [4,5]. These processes are based on the 
combustion of fossil fuels, like coal and oil and as a result they produce CO2, which is either 
accumulated in the Earth’s atmosphere, causing the global warming effect (GWE), or it end 
ups to the ground and oceans, altering the carbon balance of the planet. The current CO2 
concentration in atmosphere is 400 ppm and is expected to surpass the 750 ppm in 2100 [6]. 
In order to decrease the amount of CO2, two different, technological approaches are under 
technical investigation and development the last years. The first approach suggests the 
capturing of CO2 and its pumping deep under the ground (carbon sequestration), whereas the 
second one proposes the conversion of CO2 to high-value hydrocarbons. The increasing 
demands of human societies for energy resources, seem to promote the idea of producing fuels 
from CO2, fuels that can be used in transportation sector and for electrification purposes. In 
that way, CO2 can be part of an energy circle of zero carbon footprint (Fig.1).  
 
Figure 1: Schematic representation of a CO2- neutral energy cycle including production of synthetic fuels, via 
electrolysis, and CO2 capture from the atmosphere. Taken by reference [7]. 
==PAGE:15==

However, various factors prevent the establishment of this approach as a valid and sustainable 
solution for the limitation of the CO2 emissions and the mitigation of the negative effects of 
global warming. One of these factors, is the insufficient level of the existing CO2-purification 
technology, which is mainly responsible for the current, high costs of capturing, separating and 
purifying CO2. Another factor is the great amounts of energy that are required for the chemical 
or electrochemical processes of CO2 conversion. Furthermore, the absence of a proper-
designed market for CO2-based products and the insufficient commitment of the international, 
political and industrial committees to promote carbon-neutral fuels, delay the development of 
the CO2-electroreduction field [8]. 
Regardless these barriers, the electrocatalytic conversion of CO2, also called electroreduction 
of CO2 (ERC), has many assets that have attracted the interest of the scientific community [9]. 
These assets include, the fact that it is a process that can be easily controlled by varying the 
ratio of the supplied electric and thermal energy, while the electric energy can be supplied by 
renewable sources. Also, in electrolyzer systems, the electrolyte can be recycled after its use, 
limiting the use of materials and decreasing the OPEX cost of the process. Furthermore, it is a 
technology that can be used for the storage of surplus renewable energy (R.E),  promoting the 
integration of R.E in the grid,  and it can also help industries to reduce their carbon footprint, 
without changing completely their technology, by just leading their purified exhausts to a CO2-
electrolyzer. The main disadvantage of the electroreduction of CO2 is the sluggish kinetics of 
the electroreduction that give rise to the minimum voltage that should be applied to the 
electrolytic cell. The increase of the voltage decreases the energy efficiency of the process. In 
general, it is believed that the main drawbacks of the CO2 electroreduction are related to the 
limited catalytic activity and the instability of the electrodes. 
The content of this thesis is related to the development and the electrochemical testing of 
electrodes, and especially cathodes, for the co-electrolysis of CO2 and H2O, in the intermediate 
temperature range of 200 OC - 400 OC. The main goal was to produce hydrocarbons larger than 
CH4, using brass electrodes. The electrolyte of the cell was a CDP-based electrolyte, mixed, 
mainly, with silica carbide (SiC). In previous experiments, conducted in the Electrochemistry 
section of DTU Energy, CDP based-electrolytes had been mixed with ZrO2, whereas also  
KH2PO4-based electrolytes had been tested, exhibiting high conductivities [10,11].

2. Theoretical Part 
 
2.1 Electrolysis 
 
Electrochemical reactions are heterogeneous reactions that occur in a galvanic or in electrolytic 
cell. The basic working principles of these two devices are identical, and their main difference 
is that in the former, the reactions take place spontaneously (batteries, fuel cells), whereas in 
the latter, it is necessary to provide electrical energy to the device in the form of applied voltage 
or current, in order to activate the reaction. The main parts of an electrolytic cell are: the 
electrodes, where the electrochemical reactions are occurred, and the electrolyte, which 
conducts the positive charges from the anode chamber to the cathode chamber. In an 
electrolytic cell, the anode is the positive terminal (+) and where the oxidation reaction takes 
place, whereas the cathode is the negative terminal (-) and where the reduction reaction takes 
place. Both electrodes are connected electronically by an external conductor that allows the 
electrons to flow from the anode to the cathode, when an external voltage is applied on the cell 
[12]. The value of the applied voltage should be higher than the open-circuit voltage of the cell 
in order to create the energy conditions that would favor the transfer of electrons from the 
anode to the cathode. The reactions that are taking place on the electrodes, are called half-cell 
reactions and combined, they constitute the total electrocatalytic, redox reaction. Based on the 
type of the electrolyte, there are, mainly, three types of electrolyzers: a) the alkaline 
electrolyzers, b) the solid oxide electrolyzers, and c) the acidic electrolyzers [7] (Fig.2). Water 
electrolysis has been studied intensively for many years and it can be considered a simpler and 
more understandable process compared to the carbon dioxide electrolysis. In Figure 2, are 
presented schematically the three different types of electrolyzers, in case of water electrolysis. 
==PAGE:18==
 
5 
 
Figure 2: a) Working principle and a sketch of an alkaline electrolysis cell b) Working principle and a sketch of 
a PEM electrolysis cell c) Working principle and a sketch of a SOEC cell [7].  
The CO2 electrolysis process that is studied and developed in this thesis, is a combination of 
conventional H2O electrolysis and CO2 electroreduction in acidic media and as a result, it can 
be called, co-electrolysis of CO2 and H2O in a single step. For these reasons, it is considered 
important to present briefly the principles of water electrolysis only in acidic media, before 
elaborating more on the various aspects of CO2 electroreduction. 
 
2.1.1 Water Electrolysis using acidic-based electrolytes 
 
Water electrolysis is one of the most studied electrochemical processes, due to its great 
potential to produce hydrogen-fuel using renewable energy. The water electrolysis in acidic 
media is conducted either in a polymer electrolyte membrane electrolyzer cell (PEMEC) or in 
a solid acid electrolyzer cell (SAEC). The PEMEC systems usually have Nafion@-based 
electrolytes and they operate at relative low temperatures (40-80 OC) in order to avoid the 
decomposition of the electrolyte. Their electrodes are usually made by noble metals, such as 
platinum and iridium oxide for cathode and anode, respectively [7]. Regarding the SAEC 
systems, their electrolytes are made by alkali-metal salts and they can function properly at 
relatively high-temperatures, allowing the use of non-noble metals, such us nickel and copper, 
as cathode materials. In these electrolyzers, water-steam is supplied to the anode of the cell, 
where it is oxidized and split to molecular oxygen (O2) and protons (H+). The oxygen molecules 
flow out of the cell from the exit of the anode chamber (Oxygen Evolution Reaction, OER), 
==PAGE:19==
 
6 
whereas the protons are transfer through the proton-conductive electrolyte to the cathode, 
where they are reduced to molecular hydrogen (Hydrogen Evolution Reaction, HER).   
The half-cell reactions of the water electrolysis using acidic electrolytes, are presented below:  
• Anode (+) 
Oxidation: 2 H2O (g) ↔ O2 (g) + 4 H+ + 4 e- 
• Cathode (-) 
Reduction: 4 H+ + 4e-↔  2 H2 (g) 
The addition of the two half-cell reactions leads to the total reaction of water electrolysis, 
presented below: 
• Total 
Redox: 2 H2O (g)↔ 2 H2 (g) + O2 (g) 
The half-cell reactions of the alkaline water electrolysis and solid oxide water electrolysis are 
different compared to the respective reactions of the acidic water electrolysis. This difference 
is attributed to the fact that their electrolyte materials are not similar, and consequently, their 
conduction mechanisms and reactants are not the same. [13].  
 
 
2.1.2 Co-electrolysis of CO2 and H2O using acidic-based electrolytes 
 
Water electrolysis and CO2 electrolysis using acidic electrolytes are two processes that have 
many similar characteristics. The purpose of CO2 electrolysis is to reduce the CO2 to a 
hydrocarbon with commercial value. PEMEC systems are not considered suitable systems for 
the electroreduction of CO2, because their low-operating temperatures allow the CO-poisoning 
of cathode. On the other hand, neither  the high operating-temperatures of a SOEC (600OC-
800OC) are suitable, because at this temperature range many hydrocarbons are unstable [7]. 
The electroreduction of CO2 cannot either be conducted in a conventional alkaline electrolyzer, 
which uses a potassium hydroxide solution (KOH) as an electrolyte. The reason is that CO2 
reacts with KOH, forming the carbonate KHCO3, based on the reaction presented below [15]: 
KOH (aq) +CO2 (g) → KHCO3  
==PAGE:20==
 
7 
The electrolysis experiments of this thesis were conducted with a SAEC electrolyzer at 300OC, 
an operating temperature which is quite higher compared to the operating temperature of the 
conventional PEM electrolysis (40OC-80OC). At 300 OC the kinetics of the reactions are 
improved, while also the formed hydrocarbons are not decomposed. In order to achieve the 
CO2 reduction, is necessary to have a source of protons that can reduce the CO2 and its various 
adsorbed intermediates, promoting the formation of hydrocarbons. Hence, it is necessary to 
feed the anode chamber of the electrolysis cell with water-steam that its oxidation will produce 
the protons (H+) required for the reduction of CO2. The cathode chamber is fed with CO2 and 
in the three-phase boundary of the cathode side, the electroreduction of CO2 takes place (Fig.3). 
In the cathode, the CO2 is possible to be reduced to carbon monoxide (CO) and/or 
hydrocarbons-alcohols, while it is also possible to form hydrogen due to the recombination of 
protons.

Methane is the main product evolved at the cathode; steam is considered as the reactant at the anode [14] 
 
As it will be discussed later, various products can be formed by the co-electrolysis of CO2 and 
H2O in acidic media, depending mainly on the properties of the electrodes and the electrolyte. 
Furthermore, it is possible to produce more than one organic products at the same time and that 
makes quite challenging the establishment of a general rule that could describe the half-cell 
reactions of this co-electrolysis. For this reason, it is decided to present only the half-cell 
reactions that lead to 100% formation of CH4, which is considered the simpler, possible

of CH4, are presented below:  
• Anode (+)

• Cathode (-)  
Reduction: CO2 (g) + 8 H+ + 8e-↔ CH4 (g) + 2 H2O (g) 
• Total

2.1.3 Thermodynamics and kinetics of electrolysis 
 
Thermodynamics 
The thermodynamics of the CO2 electroreduction is directly related with the properties of the 
CO2 molecule. The formation of the CO2 molecule is a thermodynamic process during which 
two different systems (carbon and oxygen element), interact microscopically and reach a final, 
macroscopic, energy state. As a thermodynamic process, the formation of CO2 follows the 
second law of thermodynamics which implies that any thermodynamic system tries to reach its 
lowest energy state. Consequently, any process that decreases the energy of a system is favored 
and leads to stable state. The formation of CO2 at standard conditions has a negative value of 
Gibbs free energy, around -394.6 kJ/mol [16], which means that the initial energy state of this 
reaction is higher than its final (ΔG<0 ↔ Gfinal<Ginitial). Therefore, the CO2 formation is a 
reaction that is energetically favored, because it decreases the energy of the system, and the 
formed CO2 molecule is a very stable molecule with linear structure (O=C=O). However, this 
conclusion also implies that in order to ΄΄loosen΄΄the chemical bonds in the CO2 molecule and 
reduce it to another useful chemical, it’s necessary to increase its Gibbs free energy to positive 
values by providing energy [16,17].

Kinetics 
The thermodynamic considerations described above, are valid only when no voltage is applied 
on the electrolytic cell. However, in real electrolysis experiments, a voltage is applied on the 
cell, causing an electric current to flow from the anode to the cathode. The value of this applied 
voltage is not equal to the value of Erev, but it is always higher due to kinetic losses. These 
kinetic losses are attributed to the electric resistance of the various parts of the electrolytic cell. 
Hence, the actual total voltage of an electrolytic cell can be expressed by the equation 
mentioned below (9): 
==PAGE:27==
 
14 
Ecell = Erev + Eohm + Eact = Erev + (I × Rohm) + ηan + ηcat                                                                                  (9) 
where: 
• Erev = The reversible voltage of the cell (V) 
• EOhn = The voltage required for overcoming the ohmic resistance (V) 
• Eact = The voltage required for the activation of the electrodes (V) 
• I = The value of the applied current density (A/cm2) 
• Rohm = The ohmic electric resistance of the cell (Ω) 
• ηan = Τhe voltage required for the activation of the anode (V) 
• ηcat = The voltage required for the activation of the cathode (V) 
 
Regarding the ROhm, it is an additive parameter which includes various types of electric 
resistance. For example, the electric resistance caused by the non-proper contact of the 
electrodes with the electrolyte and the internal, electric resistance of the current collector, are 
included in the ROhm. However, the most important contribution to the ROhm is coming from 
the electrolyte resistance [11]. The ohmic resistance, as indicated by its name, follows the 
Ohm’s law and as a result, is related to the EOhm with the equation: EOhm = I × Rohm. 
The Eact is the sum of the ηan and ηcat. Especially in steam electrolysis, the value of ηan is much 
larger compared to the value of ηcat due to the nature of the water oxidation reaction that takes 
place in the anode. In order to visualize the actual performance of an electrolytic cell and try 
to explain its behavior, it is common practice to plot the voltage of the cell against its current 
density. This kind of graph is called polarization curve. The typical form of a polarization curve 
is presented in figure 6, depicting also the contribution of the various types of overvoltage to

2.1.4 Electrolysis Cell 
 
The cell tested in that thesis was designed in order to function properly in the intermediate 
temperature range of 200 OC-400 OC and under acidic conditions. As it was mentioned above, 
the core of an electrolysis cell are its electrodes and its electrolyte. In the cell of this project, 
the electrolyte membrane is ΄΄sandwiched΄΄ between the anode and the cathode, constituting a 
structure called membrane electrode assembly (MEA).  
==PAGE:29==
 
16 
Besides the MEA, an electrolysis cell is consisted of other additional parts that are necessary 
for the proper function of the cell. These parts are: the flow plates, the end plates and the gasket 
materials. These parts together with the MEA constitute an electrolysis cell. A simple 
schematic representation of such a cell is presented in figure 7. 
 
Figure 7: Schematic representation of the main components of the tested cell and their relative positioning. In 
this representation are not presented the gaskets placed between the flow-plates and the end plates. Taken by 
reference [11] 
The flow plates have channels that allow the flowing of the gaseous reactants to the surface of 
the electrodes. From their one side, the flow plates are in direct contact with the surface of the 
electrodes. The flow plates used in this project, are electron-conductive and they have specific 
metallic ‘’arms’’, which are connected to the potensiostat. When the potensiostat applies a 
voltage to these ΄΄arms΄΄,  the flow-plates are polarized and because they are electron 
conductive, and in direct contact with the electrodes, the electrodes are also polarized. The 
polarization of the electrodes is necessary for the conduction of electrolysis. From their other 
side, the flow plates are in contact with the end plates. The end plates provide mechanically 
stability to the cell, while through them the gaseous reactants are flowing in and out of the cell. 
Regarding the electrodes used in this project,  they are considered gas-diffusion layer electrodes 
(GDL), because they allow the penetration of the gaseous reactants inside them. This kind of 
==PAGE:30==
 
17 
electrodes are loaded with an electroactive material and should also be electron-conductive and 
porous. The higher the porosity of the electrode, the easier the diffusion of the reactants to the 
electrode-electrolyte interface. The electrode should be designed in such a way that would 
increase the three-phase-boundaries of the cell. The three phase boundaries of the cell are the 
regions where the gaseous phase of the gaseous reactants, the electrolyte phase and the 
electrode phase are in direct contact. For example, in the three phase boundaries of the anode, 
the gas is oxidized in the surface of the electrocatalyst, while the generated cations or protons 
are transferred through the electrolyte phase to the cathode of the cell.   
Finally, the gasket materials, Kapton® and Papyex® are used for the electric insulation of the 
cell and for increasing its tightness, respectively. Kapton® is a polymer, non-electron 
conductive material. Layers of Kapton® are placed: between the metallic flow plates and the 
metallic end plates, and also between the electrodes, in order to prevent any undesired short 
circuit. Regarding the Papyex®, they are flexible, graphitic materials that are not destroyed 
under great compression and at the temperature range of 200 OC-400 OC. The cell is a structure 
consisted of various layers and as result, gases can leak through the possible gaps that exist in 
the contact regions of these layers. In order to limit these gaps, the cell is strongly tightened, 
and gasket layers are placed in various positions in the cell structure. The thickness of the 
gasket layers had to be chosen in such a way that they would allow the great compression of 
the cell structure without destroying the MEA.  
 
 
2.2 Steps in CO2 electroreduction 
 
The electroreduction of CO2, as any other electroreduction process, is consisted of various steps 
that correlate to each other. The most important steps of the electroreduction are the interfacial 
charge transfer, surface reactions, the mass transfer of the reactants to the surface of the 
electrode and the bulk and surface diffusion of the products. In figure 8, is presented 
schematically, the most probable mechanism of  mass transfer towards the electrode’s surface, 
during CO2 electroreduction. The bonding of the CO2 to the surface of the electrode is greatly 
affected by the chemistry and the surface morphology of the electrode [20,21]. When the 
absorbed on the electrode’s surface CO2, called CO2(ads),  receives an electron, it transforms 
to the absorbed radical species, .CO2-(ads), which is the initial reactant in several, different 
==PAGE:31==
 
18 
paths that produce different hydrocarbons. Regarding the rate constants of the CO2 
electroreduction steps, there are no valid values yet, whereas the fact that these values are 
greatly affected by the characteristics of the electrolyte (e.g size, charge), deteriorates the 
understanding and the controlling of the CO2 electroreduction process.

The fact that the carbon atom of CO2 molecule has the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital 
(LUMO) among the other atoms of CO2, explains why the majority of the electroreduction 
mechanisms, found on literature, occur through the formation of the .CO2-intermediate [22], 
which is formed after the addition of an e- to the C atom of the CO2(ads) species. The fact that 
84% of the unpaired electron density of the highest occupied orbital of .CO2- is at the carbon 
atom, promotes the nucleophile behavior of that carbon atom [23]. It is believed that the change 
from the linear CO2(ads) form to the bent .CO2-(ads) form (Fig.9), is the most important step

2.2.1 Electroreduction of CO2 at Cu electrodes in Aqueous Media

Based on this mechanism, the CO2 is adsorbed in the surface of the electrode, forming 
CO2(ads), before getting reduced to .CO2-(ads). The value of the standard potential of this 
reduction is around  2,21 V (vs SCE) in dimethyl-formamide (DMF) solution and -1,9 V, or -
1,85 V [20] vs SHE in aqueous solution (table 2). Following the path C, the .CO2-(ads) receives 
a proton and transforms to .COOH (ads), an intermediate which is usually produced when the 
electroreduction is conducted with electrodes made from Cu or another metal that belong to 
the d-block of the periodic table. However, the path E is of greater interest because the 
formation of the CO(ads) is the necessary step for the formation of high value hydrocarbons, 
such as CH4, C2H4, C2H6. More specifically, the addition of protons to the CO(ads) (step F) 
leads to the formation of the reactive methylene group .CH2, which can either accepts more 
protons and convert to CH4 or it can react with other .CH2 species and transform to C2H4 and 
C2H6.

2.2.1.4 Effect of surface morphology and structure of Cu electrodes on the

Various studies have shown that the surface conditions of the Cu-electrode affect significantly

The same study investigated the effect of the surface’s morphology of Cu electrodes. The 
results showed that  rough Cu-electrodes have an overall better electrocatalytic performance 
compared to smooth electrodes. More specifically, electrolysis experiments conducted with

in all the tests the faradaic efficiency of H2 was increasing during the running of experiments, 
due to the deactivation of the electrode. This increase was more rapid in the case of smooth 
electrodes compared to the rough electrodes.

2.2.3 Degradation of the electrocatalytically activity at Cu electrodes  
 
Τhe activity degradation mechanisms of Cu electrodes during the electroreduction of CO2 are 
not clear yet, due to the absence of a valid description of its reaction mechanism.

2.3 IrO2 anode 
 
The performance of a solid acid electrolyser that works in the intermediate range of 
temperatures, is greatly affected by the behavior of the anode electrode and specifically from 
its resistivity towards corrosion. The relative high temperatures of 200 OC- 400OC, combined 
with the acidic environment of the electrolyte, affect negatively the structure of the anode, 
decreasing the reaction rate of steam’s oxidation. As a result, the number of protons that are 
generated in the anode and are conducted through the electrolyte to the cathode is also 
decreasing, limiting the reduction of CO2. 
Based on the abovementioned, it is very important to fabricate an anode with materials that 
will combine great electrocatalytic and electron conductive behavior, with high resistivity to 
corrosion. A material which has been proved to have high resistance in corrosion, while it is 
also electron conductive, is Tantalum (Ta), but its high price does not allow its extensive use 
in the anode. The coating of the anode material and the respective flow plate with Tantalum, is 
==PAGE:44==
 
31 
an approach that can compensate the high price issue, while improving the corrosion resistance 
of the anode and increasing the life time of electrolysis [50]. 
Beside its high price issue, the electrocatalytic activity of tantalum is not high enough and as a 
result, Ta is not a material that could be considered suitable for the energy demanding oxidation 
reaction. Furthermore, the reduction of CO2, includes various steps and is taking place in the 
cathode, hence, in order to understand the reduction mechanism and improve the electrolysis 
process, it is important to simplify the system by making sure that the water oxidation reaction 
is capable of supplying the cathode with the number of protons required. Noble metals and 
their respective oxides and alloys were expected to be both corrosive resilient and 
electrocatalytically active [51]. Among them, iridium-ruthenium, metal-based alloys are 
considered the best electrocatalysts for the oxygen evolution reaction [52]. However, RuO2 
tends to corrode easier compared to IrO2, especially in acidic environment, like the

Mechanism of oxygen evolution at IrO2 electrode 
The study of Krasil’shikov proposes that the reaction mechanism of the oxygen evolution at 
IrO2 surface is the one presented below [58]: 
• Krasil’shchikov mechanism 
1. S + H2O ↔ S – OH(ads) + H+ + e- 
2. S – OH(ads) ↔ S – O- + H+ 
3. S – O-↔  S – O + e- 
4. 2S – O → 2S + O2(g) 
where: S: Active site of the electrode’s surface 
Based on this mechanism, the first step of the water oxidation includes the splitting of water to 
H+, e- and OH- species and the adsorption of the OH- species to the surface of the electrode, 
where they formed tthe OH(ads) species. Then, the addition of electrons and protons leads to 
the formation of metal oxide (S – O-) and protons (H+). The protons are conducted through the 
proton-conductive membrane to the cathode, while the created oxide is quite unstable, due to 
the increased valence state of its metal ion, and as result, the oxygen is easily subtracted from 
it, forming molecular oxygen (oxygen evolution). Depending on the affinity of the metals  
towards oxyganed species, the rate determing step (rds) can be varied. For example, in case of  
metals with high affinity to oxygenated species, the oxygen ion is strongly absorbed to the 
metals surface, forming a stable metal-oxide, and the rds is the subtraction of oxygen from the 
metal oxide (step 4). In case of metals with low affinity, the adsorption of OH- is too weak and 
as a result, the  initial, water-discharging step becomes the rds (step 1). 
Kotz et al. also investigated the oxygen evolution reaction at IrO2 surface, in acidic conditions 
and in the mechanism that they proposed, they stated that in its first step the surface of the 
oxide is covered by a layer of hydroxides (-OH), forming Ir(OH)3. The Kotz mechanism is 
presented below [55]: 
1. Ir(OH)3  ↔ IrO(OH)2 + H+ + e-

Although, the majority of the inorganic oxides are non-electtron conductive, the rutile oxides 
show electrical-conductive behavior which is attributed to the different type of bonds that 
connect their atoms, compared with other metallic oxides. The electrical resistivity of IrO2 has 
been measured at 28 OC and found equal to 6× 10-5 Ω.cm [60]. Relevant studies have shown 
that the borders of grains are electrical insulating regions and as a result, it is believed that the 
electrons are transferred inter-molecularly from one grain to another (hoping mechanism), as 
they cannot pass through the intergrain areas [59]. The success of this type of transfer is greatly 
affected by the size of the crystal and the size of the intergrain regions, because the greater the 
distance that the electron has to overpass, the less probable is for the electron to achieve a 
successful inter-molecular transfer.

Solid acids differ from regular acids because they combine characteristics of both salts and 
acids, but the main and more interesting difference between them, is the fact that there is no 
dissolvement of the solid acid is taking place during a reaction process [63].  
A typical example of solid acid that has combined characteristics of an acid and salt, is the

Solid acids have a general chemical formula of AxByCOz, where A can either be an alkali metal

Due to their high stability in the presence of hydrogen and d-metal catalysts and also their high 
proton-conductivity values at high temperatures, solid acid phosphates have concentrated a lot 
of scientific interest, due to their potential to be employed as electrolyte material. Other types 
of solid acids like solid acid sulfates and solid acid selenates are lacking this property and as a 
result, they are not considered proper candidates for electrolyte materials. 
 
2.4.1 CsH2PO4 (CDP) 
 
Various studies have shown that one of the most suitable electrolyte-materials for solid acid 
electrolytic cells is the cesium dihydrogen phosphate (CsH2PO4, CDP) [64]. CDP has several 
advantages compared to other alkali metal-based salts, such as high proton conductivity, 
mechanical stability, high melting point (345 OC) and long-term functionality at high 
temperatures. These advantages are attributed to the ability of CDP to change its crystal 
structure,  depending on the temperature [65]. More specifically, at standard conditions the 
crystal phase of CDP is consisted of monoclinic unit cells and shows limited ability to conduct 
ions. In these conditions CDP behaves like a paraelectic material, which means that it can be 
polarized under the imposition of an electric potential. The imposition of an electric field can 
alter the electron orbitals of CDP’s bonds, causing the redistribution of elementary charges in 
CDP’s crystal and that redistribution can lead to CDP’s polarization. However, at temperatures 
below -100 OC, CDP loses its paraelectric properties, although it retains its monoclinic crystal 
structure. At temperatures higher than 230 OC, the monoclinic unit cell of CDP’s crystal is 
conversed to cubic and specifically, to a body centered cubic unit cell (bcc) (Fig.21). This 
transition is accompanied with a very interesting change to the properties of CDP that justifies 
its superiority as an electrolyte material. That change is the increase of the proton conductivity 
of CDP. More specifically, at 223 OC the value of conductivity is 8,5 10-6

OC the conductivity is four orders of magnitude larger, reaching 2,5 10-2

possible, different orientations of the phosphate unit. Taken by reference [67] 
Due to that great change in proton conductivity, this phase transition is called superprotonic, 
and is mainly attributed to the difference in the available space that each unit cell provides to 
the phosphate groups. On one hand, the phosphate groups in the monoclinic unit cell are 
restricted by the relatively small volume of that cell and as a result, they are not capable of 
performing any other movement, besides local vibrations. On the other hand, the bcc cell 
provides more space to the phosphates to move and rotate randomly, to all directions inside the 
stable cubic structure formed by the Cs ions [7]. This random rotation of the phosphate groups 
increases the possibility of a successful proton transfer and consequently, increases the proton 
conductivity of CDP [7]. In other words, the superprotonic transition transforms a steady, low-
vibrating, ordered system to an active, high-rotating, disordered system [7]. The disorder 
system is constituted by a stable cubic structure of Cs+ that ΄΄surrounds΄΄ an active phosphate 
group which is capable of rotating fast, towards any direction. The conduction of the protons 
is carried out through the rotation of the phosphate groups which are called, transport vehicles. 
More specifically, the protons can be attached to the oxygen atom of the tetrahedral, phosphate 
groups and transferred from one group to another (hopping mechanism). This transfer is 
achieved by the reverse rotating of adjacent phosphate groups. The mechanism of the proton-
conduction process that uses transport vehicles, is called Grotthus mechanism. The steps of the 
Grotthus mechanism are presented in figure 22, below.

However, it was not clear from the beginning if the conduction of protons at high temperatures, 
was achieved through the Grotthus mechanism or through the observed dehydration of CDP. 
High pressures and high levels of humidity are two factors that can limit the dehydration of a 
system and increase the value of the lowest dehydration temperature. Experiments conducted 
under these two limiting conditions and under high temperatures, showed that the proton-
conductivity of CDP was still increasing and as a result, they verified that this increase was 
owe to the Grotthus mechanism [7]. 
However, in dry conditions CDP is transformed to solid cesium metaphosphate (CsPO3) which 
is not as proton conductive as CsH2PO4 [7]. This transformation includes the formation of an 
intermediate pyrophosphate (Cs2H2P2O7) species based on the chemical reaction presented 
below:

The presence of water in the reaction steps, highlights the importance of providing the system 
with the necessary amount of H2O in order to shift the reaction towards CDP, based on the 
equilibrium law of Le Chatelier. It is of vital importance to preserve CDP in its superprotonic 
phase, when it is used as an electrolyte material in solid acid electrolysis, because the high

2.5 Electroplating of alloys 
 
The principles of alloys electroplating are the same principles with the single-metal 
electroplating. In an electroplating process, the sample that is going to be electroplated, is 
soaked in an aqueous solution that contains ions of the metal (M+) that is desired to be plated 
at the surface of the soaked sample. The electroplating is taking place in an electroplating cell, 
which is consisted of an anode, a cathode and an electrolyte (Fig.24). An applied voltage is 
polarizing negatively the cathode and positively the anode. The electrolyte is usually a salt 
dissolved in water, or a molten salt of the material that is desired to be deposit on the surface 
of the cathode. During electroplating, the positive charged anions of the solution are attracted 
by the negative charged cathode, forming the desired layer of material around it. The anode 
should be made of the material that is being deposit on the cathode otherwise the electrolyte 
solution will be diluted by other metal cations. More specifically, when the anode is polarized 
positively, electrons flow from the anode to the cathode through an external circuit and as a 
result, the material of the anode is oxidized and produces the respective metal cations, which 
then transferred to the electrolyte.

sides of the bath, and opposite to each other (anodes). 
• A heating element (ROTKAPPE-L-TG 200/0, 315-230Ws), a thermocouple and an 
electronic heating controller (Temperaturregler TS 125 by HTRONIC).

• Three baths of 10 L volume, containing distilled water and one bath of 10 L volume that 
contains acetone, (CH3)2CO, (>99% (GC) provided by Sigma Aldrich). 
 
All this system, except the power source, was placed inside a fume hood for safety reasons 
(Fig.34).

The steel felts were cut in smaller pieces in order to create the samples that would have been 
coated. These samples should always have had smaller size compared to the size of the metal 
plates of the bath, otherwise the brass coating of the samples would not be homogeneous (see 
2.8 paragraph). Firstly, the felt sample was immersed in the acetone bath for 2-3 minutes in 
order to remove any organic deposits or impurities from its surface. Then, then acetone was 
washed out by soaking the sample in one of the distilled water baths, for 4-5 minutes. 
In between, the pump had been already turned on and was pumping the electroplating solution 
of the bath through a specific filter, and then back to the bath. This filter was able to remove 
==PAGE:67==
 
54 
any solid impurities from the bath, impurities that could had stacked in the surface of the under-
coating samples, preventing the Cu and Zn ions to reach these surface-areas and consequently, 
decreasing the electroactive surface of the brass electrode. In addition, the flows caused by this 
pumping were stirring the brass solution, increasing its homogeneity and the rate of 
dissolvement of the various salts. Also, the heat-controller had been turned on and was set to 
the desired temperature. It was of vital importance that the heating element was covered 
completely by the brass solution during the heating, in order to avoid the local overheating of 
the galvanic bath that could lead to local melting of the plastic bath. 
The brass electroplating of the sample could start only when: a)the temperature of the galvanic 
bath had reached the desired value, b) the pump was already working for more than 15 minutes 
and finally, c) the sample was clean. The sample was placed on thick, electron-conductive, 
copper hook, and was  immersed in the galvanic bath. This hook was hanged from a 
rectangular, moving metal part of the bath, which was fixed on the top of the galvanic bath. I 
order to achieve a homogeneous coating of the sample, the sample should had been placed 
exactly in the middle of the distance, separating the two metal plates (anodes), also parallel to 
them, and its center should be fully aligned with the centers of the two metal plates. In that 
way, the flows of the brass ions, caused by the polarization of the sample (cathode) and the 
metal plates (anodes) were distributed uniformly along the surface of the sample and as a result, 
a homogeneous brass coating was formed. 
Then, the device that controls the motion of the metal part was turned on and the metal part, 
carrying the sample, would start to move back and forth. This motion  was removing any 
hydrogen bubbles that could had been possibly absorbed in the surface of the sample and they 
could had been coated with brass. These hydrogen bubbles are produced during the 
electroplating, due to the splitting of the water of the brass solution.  It was necessary to prevent 
the brass coating of these bubbles, because possible, later blows of them, would create uncoated 
areas in the electrode’s surface, decreasing the electroactive surface of the electrode. However, 
a rather intense motion of the metal part would may alter the ion flows around the surface of 
the sample, affecting also negatively the homogeneity of the coating. The motion speed was 
related to a voltage indication in the relevant controlling device, and after some tests, it was 
decided to set it to 7,38 V. It was observed that this value, was not only preventing the 
absorption of the hydrogen bubbles, but it was promoting the formation of a homogeneous 
brass coating layer 
==PAGE:68==
 
55 
The next step was to turn on the power source and apply a potential difference between the 
sample (cathode) and the two steel plates (anodes). The negative terminal of the power source 
was connected to the copper hook, whereas the positive terminal was connected to the steel 
plates of the bath. The power-source device gave the possibility to control either the applied 
voltage or the applied current. In general, the electroplating is based on the electrical current 
values and that approach was also followed in this series of brass electroplating. Τhe 
polarization of the electrodes was initiating the brass plating of the sample, producing an 
intense turbulence close to the surface of the under-coating sample. 
When the power source was turned off and no electric current was running between the 
electrodes, the electroplating of the sample was terminated. The brass coated sample was 
removed from the bath and was soaked for 1 minute in the second distilled-water bath, followed 
by a 10 minutes soaking in the third distilled-water bath, in order to wash away any cyanide 
salts from the sample’s surface. Finally, the brass coated sample was removed from the bath 
and was let to dry naturally, before its further mechanical processing (cutting, straightening) 
and physicochemical characterization (XRD, SEM, EDX).

3.4 
Assembly of the Electrolytic Cell 
 
As it was mentioned in paragraph 2.1.4, the electrolytic cell was consisted of different layers 
of materials, placed on the top of each other. All the cells were assembled manually following 
a standard, simple procedure, in which the cell was built from the bottom to the top. Firstly, a 
polymer ring layer of 0,075 mm thickness (called Kapton®) and on the top of it, a flexible 
graphite ring-layer of 0.2 mm or 0.5 mm thickness (called Papyex®), were placed on the surface 
of the end plates. The end plates were made by an aluminum-brass alloy, the CuAl10Fe5Ni5 
==PAGE:77==
 
64 
(JM7-20, Johnson-metal) [11]. A flow plate was placed on the top of these two layers, followed 
by the placement of an electrode, which was surrounded by several ring-layers of Papyex®. 
The flow-plate was made by stainless-steel AISI 316. The number of the Papyex® layers is 
related to their thickness and to the electrode’s thickness. After the first experiments, it was 
decided that the total thickness of the layer around the electrodes (Papyex® and Kapton®) 
should be 20% thicker than the electrode’s thickness (+20% rule) (Fig.43a). On the top of these 
Papyex® ring-layers, it was placed another Kapton® ring-layer, followed by the electrolyte, 
which was surrounded by Papyex® ring-layers. These Papyex® ring-layers should create a layer 
that has the same thickness with the electrolyte (Fig.43b). After the addition of the electrolyte, 
the order of the next layers that had to be added up to the upper end plate, was the same that 
was followed up to the addition of the electrolyte, but on reverse. The cell was stabilized by 
using bolts that were holding together the two end-plates of the cell.

3.5 
Electrolysis setup 
 
The electrolytic cell was placed inside an autoclave for safety and technical reasons (Fig.44). 
More specifically, it is a typical laboratory approach that high temperature systems should not 
be exposed, especially when they are located in laboratories, where different experiments are 
conducted, and many people work. For this reason, the autoclave was equipped with a spiral 
heat-exchanger that was decreasing the temperature in the surroundings of the cell, facilitating 
the controlling of the whole experimental setup and eliminating the possibility of any burning 
accident. 
 
Figure 44: The electrolysis experimental set up with the autoclave open. 
==PAGE:79==
 
66 
Also, unpleasant odors, produced by the degradation of various part of the setup due to the high 
temperature, were isolated inside the autoclave and were led to the exhaust hood. From 
technical perspective, the autoclave was used in order to create a pressurized environment 
around the cell that could prevent any possible leak of the cell’s gases. In other words, the 
pressure inside the autoclave equalizes the pressure inside the cell, eliminating any possible 
pressure gradient. To achieve this, the autoclave was filled with N2 (N2 bottle provided by 
Strand Møller Air Liquid 5.0, Un 1066), until its pressure would reach the pressure of the gases 
inside the cell, which was usually 8 bars. 
 
• Pre-run of the Experiment: 
Before starting the electrolysis experiment, it was necessary to check some technical 
parameters of the setup.  
1. Checking for an electrical short circuit between the anode and cathode using a multimeter. 
2. Check the system for any leakages by blowing it with a Lugaspray. 
3. If there is still some residual water inside the system from previous runs, it should be 
removed by blowing the system with N2, while having fully open exits.  
4. Checking that all N2/CO2-inlet valves are closed, except from the cathode entrance which 
must be open. 
 
• Start of the Experiment: 
When the Pre-run steps had been completed, the experimental setup was ready to be used. 
Firstly, the cathode outlet was fully open and then the CO2-pressure was rising gradually (CO2- 
provided by Strand Møller Air Liquid, UN2013). When the desired pressure was reached, the 
cell is flushed for a minute. The flow out of the system was adjusted by the cathode exit valve. 
Then, all three heating elements were turned on. The temperatures values used were 305OC, 
180OC, 160OC for the cell, the valve and the evaporator, respectively. Specifically, for the 
temperature controller of the cell, the temperature was first set to 180 OC, then to 280 OC and 
finally to 305 OC. The cooling-water flow of the heat-exchanger was initiated, followed by the 
flushing of the autoclave with N2 or CO2 and the pressurization of the autoclave. When the 
heating elements had reached temperatures above the boiling point of water, the HPLC water 
pump was turned on and the value of the flow was set to 0.01 mL per minute.  
==PAGE:80==
 
67 
 
Figure 45: Schematic illustration of the experimental set up, drawn by Erik Christensen and Aleksey Nikiforov. 
The electrolysis experiment could start, when the temperatures of the cell, evaporator and valve 
had reached the desired values. Figure 45 gives an overview of the various parts of the 
electrolysis system. 
 
• Running of the Experiment (Chronopotentiometry) 
There are various electrochemical methods that can be used in order to conduct an 
electrochemical experiment. All of them are related either to the applied electrical current or to 
the applied voltage. The electrical current is related to the number of electrons that are provided 
to the electrochemical reaction, and they can be considered as reactants, whereas the applied 
voltage is related to the energy required to move a number of electrons from the anode οf the 
cell to its cathode. This series of experiments was mainly focusing on the possible products 
that can be produced by the co-electrolysis of CO2 and H2O and as a result, it was of vital 
importance to have a steady flow of reactants, including electrons. Consequently, it was chosen 
to apply a steady current in all the electrochemical experiments and try to identify the produced 
chemicals by using a Gas-Chromatograph (GC). 
The electrochemical method during which the cell potential is recorded, while the value of the 
applied electrical current is steady, is called Chronopotentiometry.  
In order to conduct the Chronopotentiometry measurement a potensiostat (VersaSTAT 4, 
provided by AMETEK Scientific Instruments) was used. The potensiostat was not only 
providing the required electric power to the electrolysis reaction, but it was also capable of 
==PAGE:81==
 
68 
controlling the duration and the development of the electrolysis, while recording all the 
important electrochemical parameters, such as the voltage, the current and the resistance of the 
system. The anode of the cell was connected with the union of the Working Electrode cable 
(WE) and the Sense Electrode cable (SE) of the potensiostat, whereas the cathode was 
connected with the union of the Counter Electrode (CE) cable and the Reference Electrode 
(RΕ) cable.  
Every different cell was considered a different experiment and every different experiment was 
including a series of runs that were conducted under different values of applied current, 
producing different polarization curves. It was observed that in the first run of all the 
experiments, the potential tended to increase slowly until reaching a steady value, whereas in 
the next rounds the time-response of the potential was much quicker.

4. Results 
 
This section is separated in two different parts. In the first part, are presented the results of the 
fabrication procedures that were developed and more specifically, the characteristics of the 
electrodes fabricated in each method. It is very important to be able to create the desired 
electrodes in a fast and controllable way and for this reason, it was considered necessary to 
understand the way that the parameters of each method affect the structural properties of the 
fabricated materials, as these properties are strongly related with their electrocatalytic 
performance. The fabricated samples were analyzed using XRD, SEM, EDX and by applying 
some simple mathematical considerations (e.g. porosity calculations). The performance of the 
one-step, brass electroplating method is mainly investigated, due to its potential to create 
simply and fast, brass electrodes of various compositions and crystal phases. The investigated 
parameters of the other methods are fewer and as a result, the type and the amount of the 
respective results are also limited. 
In the second part, are presented the results of the electrolysis experiments, conducted with the 
fabricated electrodes. The experiments are separated in two groups, based on the material of

4.1 Development of electrode’s fabrication processes 
 
4.1.1 Cu-Zn electroplated cathodes 
 
The brass samples were fabricated by using an electroplating set-up (provided by Elplatek A/S) 
and following the process described in paragraph 3.1.2.3. However, due to technical issues, the 
chemical concentration of the electroplating solution was not the same among the 
electroplating experiments and as a result, the electroplating process was conducted using four 
different electroplating solutions. These four different solutions were called Galvanic Bath 1, 
Galvanic Bath 2, Galvanic Bath 3 and Galvanic Bath 4. Brass samples created with the same 
==PAGE:85==
 
72 
one-step electroplating method, were provided by Elplatek A/S. In addition to these samples, 
οne more brass sample, made by the  Mechanical Engineering Department of the Technical 
University of Denmark (DTU), was examined and used in an electrolysis experiment. 
However, this brass sample was fabricated following a two-step electroplating method, during 
which a stainless-steel felt was coated, firstly, with a zinc layer and then, this zinc-coated felt 
was coated with a copper layer. Finally, this double-layer sample was heat-treated, following 
the same process described in paragraph 3.2, in order the two layers to diffuse to each other 
and the sample to obtain a homogeneous brass layer. It was not possible to repeat this method 
and create a similar sample with a homogeneous brass coating. 
The initial directive was to develop an electroplating-based method (including heat-treatment) 
that would be able to produce brass samples with a relative mass composition (wt%) ratio of 
60/40% (Cu=60% and Zn=40%). For this reason, some very Cu-rich, brass samples were 
neither tested in the electrolysis cell nor further treated and thoroughly investigated. XRD 
analysis conducted to all the brass samples that had a homogeneous brass coating in order to 
identify their crystal phases and their respective weight ratios (WPPF analysis). It was possible 
to calculate the total mass compositions of Cu and Zn in each investigated sample, using the 
WPPF results and more specifically, the number of the crystal phases, the ratio of Cu and Zn 
in each phase, and the weight ratios of each phase. These compositions were compared with 
the respective mass compositions (wt%) identified by the EDX analysis. It is important to 
mention that the presented Cu and Zn composition values of the EDX analysis, are always 
relative to each other, therefore, all the other detected chemical compositions had been 
excluded by the calculations. SEM images and graphs are presented in order to identify and 
clarify the effect of the electroplating current density, heat-treatment and concentration of 
electroplating solution on the chemical and structural properties of the brass samples.

4.2 Electrolysis Tests 
 
In this section, are presented the results of the electrolysis tests conducted in this thesis. The 
polarization curves of the experiments and the most important results of the Gas 
Chromatography (G.C) measurements are presented, accompanied with brief discussion of the 
total performance of the cell. The degrading performance of the cells, in terms of voltage 
response, can mainly be attributed to the melting of the electrolyte and its penetration deeply 
into the electrodes. One hand, this penetration was desired to some extent because it was 
forming the three-phase boundary in areas close to the interface of electrode-electrolyte. One 
the other hand, the penetration into the bulk regions of the electrode, was limiting the three-
phase boundary of the cell by blocking the paths that were available for the gas in order to flow 
through the electrodes and reach the electroactive material. It was decided that the tests would 
be terminated when the voltage of the electrolysis would be above 2 V, because higher voltages 
were expected to promote the oxidation of the flow plate of the cathode and decrease its 
electrical-conductivity.  
The electrolysis tests are separated in two sub-sections: The tests conducted with Ni cathodes 
and the tests conducted with Cu-Zn-based cathodes. The ability of the Ni-electrodes to produce 
CH4 had been already proved in previous thesis and the main purpose of these experiments was 
to test various new electrolytes and various approaches in the cell assembly. Regarding the 
tests conducted with Cu-Zn cathodes, the main interest was not the total performance of the 
cell, but their potential to reduce the CO2 to hydrocarbons more complex than CH4. For the 
detection of the products were used two different computer programs of the G.C, called the 
short-run and the long-run, respectively. Only the long-run was capable of detecting 
hydrocarbons larger than CH4, but it was only the short-run that had been calibrated properly 
and its data could be used for the calculations of the current efficiencies. Below, is presented a 
table that includes the most important parameters of the conducted  electrolysis tests (table 21). 
Regarding the electrodes, more details about their characteristics can be found in section 4.1, 
whereas about the electrolytes, it was decided to present only their initial composition and the 
different silica carbide (SiC) types that were used for their fabrication, because the content of 
this thesis was relevant with the development of the electrodes and of not the electrolytes

blocking of the flow channels from the electrolyte, because this would decrease the time that

long time. Hence, in order to avoid blocking, it was decided to increase the thickness of the 
electrodes. Regarding the cathode, two pieces of the GB1-NH sample were placed on the top 
of each other, constituting a cathode of 1,1 mm thickness, while the same approach was 
followed in the case of anode, which was consisted of one heavy IrO2 anode and one piece of 
the light, tantalum coated, steel felt. The total thickness of the anode was 1 mm, instead of the

5 Conclusion-Discussion 
 
It was achieved to develop a one-step, brass-electroplating method that could fabricate brass 
electrodes, mainly consisted of the Cu0,65Zn0,35 phase. The successful electroplating conducted 
at 25 OC, 12 mA/dm2, for 50 minutes, using an electroplating solution containing: 30 g/L 
CuCN,  9 g/L Zn(CN)2, 55 g/L NaCN, 10 g/L Na2CO3 and 10 g/L NaOH. The composition of 
the main, brass phase was found to be determined from the concentration of the electroplating 
solution and especially, the ratio between the concentrations of CuCN and Zn(CN)2. Although, 
the value of the applied current density did not affect the total, relative Cu/Zn mass composition 
(wt%) of the brass samples, increase of the current density from 2 to 12 10 mA/dm2, promoted 
the formation of  single-phase brass samples. Heat-treatment of the electroplated, brass samples 
at 470 OC and then at 670 OC, increased the roughness of their surfaces and decreased their Cu 
mass content (wt%). A method for fabricating both Ni and Cu/Zn-pellet electrodes of 1,1 mm 
thickness, 7,06 cm2 surface area and 40% porosity, was also established. Furthermore, it was 
also shown that the rolling compression of the  tantalum coated, stainless steel felts, from 0,56 
mm thickness to 0,45 mm thickness, enhanced the  deposition of IrO2 on their surfaces, 
increasing the IrO2 load of the anodes, from 3,3 g/cm2 to 5 g/cm2. 
Electrolysis tests conducted with Ni-pellet cathodes, IrO2-based anodes and CDP-SiC-based 
electrolytes, at 300 OC and 8 bars, produced CH4, CO and H2, at voltages below 2V. Imposition 
of current densities above 17 mA/cm2, limited the formation of CH4 and promoted the 
formation of CO and H2, verifying the mechanism proposed by Kudo et al [96]. The higher 
value of current efficiency of CH4 was 43,7%, at 11,3

(0,1 g/cm2).

conclusion about the effect of the thickness and roughness of the brass coating on the formation 
of large hydrocarbons. Regarding the large signal of the H2 peak that was observed in the last-
mentioned experiment (Test 15), it can be attributed to the use of the rolled anodes that had 
more IrO2, compared to the unrolled anodes that had been used in other tests. More specifically, 
the increase of the IrO2 load could have increased the active sites for water oxidation, increasing 
the amount of protons that were generated and conducted through the electrolyte to the cathode. 
The higher the number of the protons that were reaching the cathode, the higher the possibility 
for protons to be recombined and form molecular hydrogen. The fact that the increase of 
molecular hydrogen corresponded to increase of the CH4 peak,  indicates that the CO2 may 
react chemically with the molecular hydrogen, at the surface of the Cu0,75Zn0,25 cathode, 
forming CH4.The change of the surfaces color of the brass cathodes after their testing, shows 
that the cathodes were may subjected to phase transition during the electrolysis. Indeed, the 
phases of the cathode’s surface that was in contact with the electrolyte (Ca-El surface) had 
become richer in Cu, whereas the surface of the cathode that was in contact with the flow-plate 
(Fl-El surface) did not subject to such and intense phase transition, indicating that the 
conditions of the cathode-electrolyte interface were responsible for this change. The MEK 
sample, which was the only electroplated sample that produced a large hydrocarbon (DME), 
did not follow that rule and its main phase transformed from Cu0,65Zn0,35 to CuZn. Hence, brass 
cathodes that are more resistive to phase transition during electrolysis, seem to favor the 
formation of large hydrocarbons. The XRD analysis of the brass samples before and after their 
testing, did not detect any Cu2O phase that could promote the formation of CH3OH. In general, 
the color change of the yellowish brass samples (before their testing) to brownish (after their 
testing) can be considered as an indication of Cu-rich phases formation and that formation may 
increases the possibilities to form metallic-Cu phase that does not promote the formation of 
large hydrocarbons, such as DME and CH3OH.
