Abstract
The spherical wave expansion is a ubiquitous computational tool in antenna engineering, and efficient means of
obtaining the expansion coefficients are desirable. In this thesis we apply a fast O(N log(N)2) algorithm to approx-
imate the expansion coefficients to negligible errors, using different quadrature rules that are exact for spherical
harmonics up to some degree. We use quadrature rules with uniform sampling, Gauss-Legendre qaudrature rules,
and rotationally invariant quadrature rules. We compare the efficiencies of the quadrature rules and the execution
times of their node-distributions. We find that the execution time depends negligibly on how the quadrature nodes
are distributed, and therefore conclude that the most efficient quadrature rules are best suited for computing the
expansion coefficients.

Introduction
Harmonic analysis on the 3-dimensional unit sphere S2 =

x ∈R3 : |x| = 1
	
appears in many fields of science and
engineering. It is, for example used, used in weather forecasting, in geo- and astrophysics, in quantum chemistry,
and wherever data is most naturally represented on a sphere. The application considered here is antenna engineering.
The (time-harmonic) electromagentic field radiated by an antenna is frequently represented by a spherical wave
expansion. This expansion provides simple means of computing the electromagnetic field and its far-field pattern.
A number of important antenna characteristics may furthermore be computed from knowledge of the expansion
coefficients [10, Sec 2.4]. Effective means of obtaining the expansion coefficients is therefore of great interest.
The expansion coefficients are given by inner product on a sphere. These inner products are in practice discretized
and approximated by means of quadrature rules on the sphere. The effectiveness of this approach is two-fold:
• There are different quadrature rules on the sphere with different efficiencies.
• There are different algorithms for utilizing the quadrature rules, with different complexities.
A trade-off between efficiency and complexity has traditionally had to be made. One could use an quadrature rule
with equispaced nodes, and then utilize the celebrated Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm to accelerate com-
putations. But quadrature rules with equispaced nodes are known to be inefficient. They require a much greater
number of quadrature nodes, i.e, samples, to achieve the same accuracy as more efficient quadrature rules. On the
other hand, the non-equal spacing between the nodes of more efficient quadrature rules prevents us from dividing and
conquering the computation with FFT. Computations have to be done directly, at a high computational complexity.
Recent developments in non-equispaced fast Fourier algorithms suggest that we may have the best of both worlds:
Efficient quadrature rules, and the low computational complexity of FFT. In this thesis we show that we may indeed
have this, and we explain why and how we may have this.
We initiate our analysis with a derivation of the spherical wave expansion and its key properties in Section 2.
Our focus will be the expansion of solutions to the scalar Helmholtz equation, rather than the expansion of elec-
tromagnetic vector fields, i.e, solutions to Maxwell’s equations. This is without loss of generality, which of course
will be shown.
There is a standard notion of efficiency for quadrature rules on the sphere, which we introduce in Section 3.
It depends on the degree up to which the quadrature rule exactly integrates spherical harmonics. We explain how
this notion is relevant and useful to consider when approximating the expansion coefficients.
An overview of the fast Fourier algorithms used to accelerate the computation of the approximate expansion
coefficients is given in Section 4. In Section 5 we conduct numerical experiments to verify the performance of the
methods we propose. We discuss the results there, and make concluding remarks in Section 7.
In summary: This thesis aims to present the means for using fast algorithms and efficient quadrature rules to
compute spherical wave expansion coefficients.

for download at Burkardt’s website (link ←). In our numerical experiments we exclusively test these octahedrally

Numerical experiments
In this section we test the validity of the theoretical predictions made in the previous sections. We recall them here.
In Section 2 we derived the spherical wave expansion of radiating solutions to the Helmholtz equation and de-
scribed how the expansion coefficients are found by computing integrals on the sphere. We predicted that radiating
solutions to the Helmholtz equation are band-limited in practice, i.e, only a finite number of expansion coefficients
account for (practically) all the radiated energy.
In Section 3 we discussed three quadrature rules on the sphere of varying efficiencies, that are exact for spher-
ical harmonics up to some degree. We predicted that these quadrature rules can recover the expansion coefficients
of band-limited functions exactly, provided that they are exact for spherical harmonics up to degree twice the
bandwidth of the function.
In Section 4 we discussed a fast algorithm for computing the expansion coefficients, using any desired quadra-
ture rule.

5.3

The experiment above illustrates the advantage of using efficient quadrature rules. It does, however, not indicate
whether they can compete computationally with the equispaced quadrature rules that accelerate computations with
FFT. Even with the use of NFFT, it is possible that the more efficient quadrature rules fall behind in terms of
speed, due to computational overhead.
In this experiment we compare the execution times of the FSHT algorithm for the different quadrature rules
and for different input sizes, i.e, for different values of Npts. This is in order to test whether the execution time
depends on how the quadrature nodes are distributed. We also test how the algorithm scales with the input size,

Other considerations
Our experiments have shown it most advantageous to use the rotationally invariant quadrature rules, since these
have the greatest efficiency. Despite their optimal efficiency, the applicability of these quadrature rules is severely
limited by the fact that we only know ones that are exact for spherical harmonics up to fairly small degrees. We see
in Figure 17 that the most accurate Lebedev rule that has been tabulated, which is exact for spherical harmonics
up to degree 131, only gives a decent relative error for rmin < 10. Gauss-Legendre quadrature rules are better suited
for larger problem. They are still fairly efficient, and they can be constructed to most needed sizes more or less
instantaneously.
To construct rotationally invariant quadrature rules that are exact for larger degrees of spherical harmonics, we
need to solve the non-linear system of equations (37), e.g, via Newton’s method. If we were to invest resources
into solving these non-linear systems of equations, it is better to look for quadrature rules that are invariant wrt.
the icosahedral group with inversion. This is because the group has larger order than the octahedral group with
inversion, so amount of work we need to put in is reduced to a smaller fraction. Another way to think of this is
that an equivalent amount of work lets us construct quadrature rules that are exact for a larger number of spherical
harmonics.
Another benefit of the rotationally invariant quadrature rules, in addition to optimality, is that their nodes are
very evenly distributed across the sphere. See e.g, Figure 10. A feature of tensor product quadrature rules on the
sphere is an unreasonably dense clustering of nodes near the poles θ = 0 and θ = π, see Figure 5 and 6. This is,
for instance, detrimental for the performance of finite difference methods when solving time-dependent problems
on the sphere, such as weather forecasting. This is because the largest stable time increment is bounded by the
smallest distance between nodes [4, Sec 18.10, p 398-399].
Computing spherical wave expansion coefficients from samples at set of quadrature nodes is in many places through-
out the literature considered to be the adjoint problem. The forward problem is instead to evaluate the spherical
wave expansion at a desired set of points on a sphere, given a set of expansion coefficients. There are analogous fast
algorithms for this forward problem, see [14] and [16]. These forward and ajoint algorithms, together with efficient
quadrature rules, are powerful tools for analysis and synthesis of electromagnetic fields.
In practice, when making spherical antenna measurements, the time it takes to make the measurements may greatly
outweigh the time is takes to process them. Efficient quadrature rules therefore not only reduce the computation
time, but also greatly reduced measuring time, by requiring fewer measurement to obtain a desired accuracy.
Uncertainties and noise are present when making antenna measurements in practice.
In such cases it may be
undesirable to compute the spherical wave expansion coefficients by means of quadrature rules. One would want to
regularize the noise in some way. If we try to obtain the expansion coefficients by e.g, solving a regularized system
of linear equations in the least-squares sense, then a fast way of evaluating the spherical harmonic expansion is
desirable. This is especially true, if the system is solved using iterative methods, where numerous spherical har-
monic expansions have to be evaluated. The forward algorithms mentioned above help reducing the computational
complexity of evalating these expansions.

Conclusion
The goal with this thesis was to investigate effective means of computing spherical wave expansion coefficients. We
first derived the spherical wave expansion. It is a special case of the Helmholtz representation for radiating solutions
to the Helmholtz equation, and a special case of the analogous Stratton-Chu representation for radiating solutions
to Maxwell’s equations. We derived the spherical inner products for computing the expansion coefficients. These
can be used to compute the far-field patterns and radiated energies of radiating solutions.
We then discussed three types of quadrature rules on the sphere for discretizing the inner products and approxi-
mating the expansion coefficients numerically. Quadrature rules with uniform sampling have explicit constructions
and enable the use of fast algorithms like Fast Fourier Transform to accelerate computations. However, their low
efficiencies E = 1
3 means that a large number of quadrature nodes is required to achieve the same accuracy as more
efficient quadrature rules. The Gauss-Legendre quadrature rules with E = 2
3 only require half as many nodes, and
the rotationally invariant quadrature rules with E = 3
3 only require a third as many nodes. The construction of
the more efficient quadrature rules is not explicit. The most efficient quadrature rules are constructed by solving
a (possibly) large system of non-linear equations. Only a small number of such quadrature rules have been con-
structed and tabulated to be used.
We have shown numerically that the exact expansion coefficients can be computed by using quadrature rules
with sufficiently many quadrature nodes. We have in turn verified that the sufficient number of quadrature nodes
is much lower for the efficient quadrature rules. This means, in practice, that efficient quadrature rules can save a
lot of cost by requiring fewer samples.
Using the more efficient quadrature rules with non-equispaced nodes has traditionally been at the cost of increased
computational complexity. We have here described a fast algorithm for computing the expansion coefficients, using
quadrature rules with non-equispaced quadrature nodes. It has the same asymptotic complexity as the algorithm
that computes them using quadrature rules with equispaced nodes, namely O
 Npts log(Npts)2
operations. We have
shown that the non-equispaced algorithm outperforms the equispaced algorithm (in the particular implementation
we considered). We also saw that the execution time does not depend considerably on how the quadrature nodes
are distributed. In other words, it takes more of time to run the algorithm on a set of equispaced nodes, than it
takes to run the algorithm on a set of non-equispaced nodes that can achieve the same accuracy.
This confirms what this thesis sought to investigate: Efficient quadrature rules are overall better to use than
quadrature rules with equispaced nodes, when computing spherical wave expansion coefficients. This is because an
increase in efficiency does not come at a cost of an increase in computational complexity.
