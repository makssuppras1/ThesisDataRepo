Abstract
There have been several cases of ﬁre-induced collapses of concrete buildings in the last
couple of decades. The knowledge about concretes reaction to elevated temperatures is
still limited. This stresses the need for further studies in the compressive and tensile
properties, as well as reassessing the currently used models.
This study investigates the mechanical properties of concrete exposed to elevated temper-
atures. The results are compared to theoretical models and others experimental results.
This study investigates both the compressive and tensile mechanical properties.
The
tests are performed in both transient and non-transient conditions. The transient tests
simulates real life conditions, as the concrete specimens are applied certain constant loads
during heating. In non-transient tests the concrete specimens are heated to a certain
temperature and subsequently loaded.
Results from the compressive experiments showed general more reduction in strength
compared to the models. The obtained strain values at peak stress showed closest simi-
larities to the model by Hertz[13], but signiﬁcant lower values than the standard model
by DS/EN-1-2[6]. Giving reason to believe that the standard design model by DS/EN-
1-2 gives overestimated values. In comparison between transient and non-transient tests,
results showed conservative values from transient tests, opposite the general believe.
The models describing the tensile mechanical properties of concrete elevated to high tem-
peratures, shows general signiﬁcant diﬀerences. Values obtained in this study were more
conservative than the existing models. However it showed the closest compliance with the
model from DS/EN-1-2, the most conservative of the models.
Several diﬃculties were encountered in testing for direct tension. Thus several suggestion
are made for further studies.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Concrete is often regarded as a very good material against ﬁre. However, several are
the cases of ﬁre-induced collapses of concrete buildings witnessed in the last couple of
decades: the collapse of half a building in Alexandria, Egypt, in 2000, the roof collapse
of a car park in Gretzenbach, Switzerland, in 2004, the Windsor Tower, Spain, in 2005,
the Architectural faculty building of Delft University, Netherlands, in 2009, and recently
the collapse of a high-rise building in Teheran, Iran, 2017, are just a few non-exhaustive
examples of such collapses.
There is still limited knowledge about concretes reaction to elevated temperatures, espe-
cially experimental studies. Several models describe the change in strength and strain
at peak stress. Such as the current design method described in DS/EN-1-2[6], and the
model by Hertz[13] which both takes into account the type of aggregate. Other models
such as Terro[23] and Lie[18] do not take the type of aggregate into account. Between
the models diﬀerences are seen in predicting strain and loss of compressive strength. Es-
pecially concerning the strain at peak stress diﬀerences are seen. As DS/EN-1-2[6] and
Lie[18] predicts considerable higher values than Hertz[13], Terro[23] and results by An-
derberg & Thelandersson[1]. Most of the studies which are performed are non-transient,
meaning that they were heated to a certain temperature and then exposed to stress. In
most life-like situations it would be more correct to perform transient tests, as a concrete
element is typically loaded upon elevated temperature exposure. The common belief is
that non-transient test, results in a conservative compressive strength when comparing
with the results obtained from transient tests[4]. This study will aim to compare the
results from these two test methods.
Experiments with concrete exposed to elevated temperatures have usually only been tested
for compression with less focus on the tensile strength of concrete. Existing research re-
garding the tensile strength for concrete at elevated temperatures, is mostly based on
indirect tensile tests, like split-cylinder test [24] [10] and ﬂexure tests. However, indirect
tensile strength tests are known to overestimate the tensile strength. Furthermore, sev-
eral discrepancies can be observed by comparing the literature models and the Eurocode
model [6]. Nevertheless, experiments with direct tensile load applied to concrete at el-
evated temperatures are very limited and only related to non-transient test conditions
[16], where the specimen is ﬁrst heated and then subjected to mechanical loads, thus not
properly representing the condition of a real structural element in ﬁre.
For this reason additional compressive and tensile experiments needs to be conducted
both in non-transient and transient conditions. Damkjær[5] and Zawadowska[26] have
previously investigated concrete exposed to elevated temperatures under compression, for
both non-transient and transient conditions. This study will therefore to the extend it is
possible, be build on the experimental results and test setups from their studies.

1.2
Objects and methods
The main aim of this project is to obtain new experimental data of the compressive and
tensile strength of concrete exposed to elevated temperatures, both for transient and non-
transient scenarios.
The results of the experiments are compared to the reduction in
strength and strain at peak stress from existing models and experimental results.
Several preliminary experiments are performed, investigation the aging of concrete, aleatory
and batch diﬀerence, and the eﬀect of changing the specimen size.
Concrete specimens are exposed to compression and tension in an oven simulating tran-
sient and non-transient conditions. It is a cylindrical oven by Scandia Ovne model 5F868D
with a capability of reaching temperatures around 800oC. The casting method and recipe
will simulate the one used by Zawadowska [26], with some possible improvements of the
mould. The specimen size will remain the same for all the tests, 70x470mm. Finally spe-
cial attention is paid to the description of the experimental setup and how it may aﬀect
the variability of the results, including future improvements.
The experimental setup for this study is modiﬁed from previous studies by Zawadowska
due to a number of changes. Primarily the diameter of the concrete specimen is changed
as well as the machine and tools used to conduct the experiments. In addition, a new
experimental setup is used to conduct the direct tensile experiments.

2
Theory
2.1
Heating of Concrete
When concrete is exposed to elevated temperatures the concrete starts to deteriorate.
This occurs at diﬀerent temperature steps according to Hertz [13]. What deteriorates
the concrete is the evaporation of water. At the ﬁrst stage the free water evaporates at
around 100 to 140oC. This does not aﬀect any of the concrete properties as it is the free
excess water which is evaporated. If the temperature is increased further the chemically
bound water starts to evaporate. This starts from around and above 150oC where the
chemical bound water in hydrated calcium silicate (C-S-H) starts to evaporate.
This
process has its peak at 270oC. Along with the dehydration of the concrete matrix the
aggregates expands which causes internal stresses. As a result of this micro-cracks occurs
at around 300oC. If the temperature is not increased any further and the micro-cracks
had not occurred yet the concrete can regain its strength by absorbing water from the
air. Above 300oC the concrete takes permanent damage as the strength of the concrete
is reduced. From 400 to 600oC the dehydration of calcium hydroxide proceeds as crystals
of calcium hydroxide becomes calcium oxide and water, Ca(OH)2 →CaO + H2O, which
impairs the concrete even further. From 600oC the decomposition of hydrated calcium
silicate continues. Above 800oC the concrete is so weakened that it can be crumpled by
hand. Above 1150oC feldspar is melting and the minerals which are left turns into a glass
phase.
2.1.1
Aggregate
The aggregates expand due to thermal expansion. Therefore the type of aggregate inﬂu-
ences the concrete at elevated temperatures, due to diﬀerences in the thermal expansion.
Siliceous aggregates is the form of aggregate which expands the most when heated [13].
Siliceous aggregates will therefore naturally give rise to larger damage of the concrete
than calcareous aggregates which has a smaller thermal expansion. It is therefore natural
to use siliceous as a conservative estimate, if the aggregate type is unknown. Fletcher [11]
states that quartz-based aggregates will expand at 575 oC and calcareous aggregates such
as limestone and sea-gravel will begin to decompose at 800oC.
2.1.2
Heat Rate
The heating rate is important to consider, as a too high heating rate may result in
spalling. The two main mechanism leading to spalling is large pressure due to moisture
evaporating and a rapid heating rate [11]. A large heating rate results in temperature
diﬀerence between the core of the concrete and the surface, thereby also a diﬀerence in
thermal expansion. This is seen as large internal stresses which may damage the concrete
before testing. A heating rate limit of 3oC/min is recommended by Fletcher[11] although
it can depend on the type of concrete. To be on the safe side a slightly lower heating rate
of 2oC/min was used, the same as used by Zawadowska [26].
2.2
Testing of Concrete at Elevated Temperatures
In general their are two diﬀerent type of test used for testing concrete at elevated temper-
atures: transient and non-transient. When testing concrete there are also some general

dimensions which needs to be considered.
2.2.1
Non-Transient
This type of test is the most commonly used, probably because the experimental setup
is easier than for transient tests. For this type of test the specimen can be heated up
in a oven separate to the actual test setup, as long as the desired temperature is kept.
The non-transient test is used to determine the strain-stress relationship by heating the
specimen to a certain temperature followed by the loading. Contrary to the transient test,
the non-transient test will follow a gradually increase of loading, thus making it possible
to plot a strain stress curve. The disadvantage with this test, is that the heat is applied
at zero loading, which is an unrealistic scenario considering that bearing structures will
have an applied load in the case of a ﬁre scenario. Another disadvantage is that transient
strain is not present, as the concrete will have free thermal expansion during heating.
The ultimate strain result will be aﬀected by this, making it questionable whether or not
to include the thermal strains in the ﬁnal strain-stress curve. A strain-stress curve that
assumes the thermal strains as negative, meaning that they are added to the mechanical
strains, is called a shifted strain-stress curve.
2.2.2
Transient
The essence of the transient test is to preload the specimen with a constant loading
before heating. This require higher demands for the experimental setup, compared to
non-transient test, as the specimen needs to be heated while placed in a loaded setup.
It simulates a realistic scenario since bearing structures are exposed to stress before a
ﬁre accident occur. The test states the temperature-strain relationship in a preloaded
specimen. The disadvantage with this test is that no information about the stress-strain
curves are available due to the constant loading. Therefore, this realistic scenario can
not be used to determine the compressive strength degradation neither can it be used to
calculate the stiﬀness of the specimen.
2.2.3
Specimen Dimensions
There are recommendations by DS/EN-1-1[7] when testing hardened concrete. The di-
ameter of the specimen should be at least three and a half times the maximum aggregate
size used in the concrete. The nominal sizes by DS/EN-1-1 are 100, 113, 250, 200, 250,
and 300mm for the diameter, and the length of the cylindrical specimen should be twice
the diameter. However for this study the nominal sizes has not been possible to comply
due to the experimental setup, see section 3.
2.3
Thermal Properties
The thermal properties of the concrete states its attributes during heat. These properties
depends entirely on the concrete type such as the type of aggregate, the age of the concrete,
and the general concrete recipe.

2.4
Temperature Distribution
As the concrete is heated up, diﬀerent temperature gradients will occur through out the
radial direction of the concrete specimen. The concrete is heated up from the outside
and inwards. Due to the high density and low thermal conductivity which is found in
normal-weight concrete, the penetration time is high and it takes time for the core of

2.5
Mechanical Properties
2.5.1
Strain
The strain of concrete exposed to elevated temperatures consist of four diﬀerent compo-
nents. These are the stress-related strain εload, the creep strain εcreep, the transient strain
εtr, and the thermal strain εth. The sum of these gives the total strain of concrete at
elevated temperature:
ε = εload + εcreep + εtr + εth
(8)
2.5.1.1
Stress-related Strain
The stress-related strain is basically the instantaneous strain from an applied load to the
concrete. The strain is the value at peak stress at a given temperature [25]. Anderberg &
Thelandersson [1] shows that the total strain increases monotonously with the temperature
if it is unstressed.
However if the concrete is pre-loaded, the stress-related strain is
counteracting the thermal expansions. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 5. Thereby the preload
must be taking into account when determining the total strain.

3.1
Concrete recipe
The concrete recipe chosen for this project is identical to the concrete recipe used by
Damkjær [5] and Zawadowska [26]. There are several reasons why the same recipe is cho-
sen. Damkjær was able to test three concrete recipes with diﬀerent water/cement ratios.
From the obtained results it was concluded that the recipe with a water-cement ratio of
0.7 had the lowest variation in compressive strength. There is a rule of thumb saying that
the tensile strength is approximately 10% of the compressive strength. Since there is a
relationship between the compressive and tensile strength, it is concluded that the recipe
with a water-cement ratio of 0,7 also have the lowest variations in tensile strength.
Another very important reason why the same recipe is chosen is that this study aims to
further develop the test results from, Damkjær[5] and Zawadowska[26], and is compared
with the test results from these .
The concrete recipe provides a compressive strength of 27.86 MPa at 28 days according
to the Bolomey’s formula. The compressive strength will be measured in the preliminary
tests to ensure the true values. The recipe is presented in table 5 and in appendix A. For
the concrete, sea gravel aggregates and Rapid Portland Cement (CEM 52.5R) was used.

This section deals with the possibility of comparing the transient and non-transient results

investigation is provided in ﬁgure 51.
Figure 51: Strategy for investigation of the D/L ratio test
Step 1: The ﬁrst step is to test 5 specimens with dimensions of 100mm x 200mm and
compare with results from Zawadowska [26]. This is to examine the diﬀerence in mean
and standard deviation, to conclude whether the results are comparable or not. If the
results have been proven to be comparable, the next step is to be performed.
Step 2: The second step is to test 5 specimen with dimensions of 100mm x 644mm and
compare with the results from step 1. This is to study if there is a diﬀerence in the
compressive strength when increasing the length of the specimen. Theoretically, there
should not be any diﬀerence, but practically there will be a some deviations.
If the
results have been proven to be comparable with the result in step 1, the next step is to
be performed.
Step 3: The third step is to test 5 specimens with dimensions of 73mm x 470mm. These
specimens have the exact same D/L ratio as the specimen in step 2. This step investigates
the diﬀerence in compressive strength when decreasing diameter and length, but keeping
the same D/L ratio. If the results have been proven to be comparable with the results
in step 2, it can be concluded that the transient and non-transient tests of this project is
comparable with the transient and non-transient tests performed by Zawadowska [26].

4.2
Non-Transient
For the non-transient compression tests specimens were tested at 400, 500 and 600oC. For
each temperature three specimens were tested. All the specimens was loaded until failure.
The strain values are collected upon loading, meaning that the thermal expansion is
included in the given strains. In the ﬁgures, strain values from both the extensometer(Ex)
and piston position(Pos) are illustrated. The strain from the piston position is calculated
over a length of 360mm, corresponding to the height of the oven.
4.2.1
400oC Test
From the preliminary experiments an average strength of 21.5MPa was observed at ambi-
ent temperature. From ﬁgure 56 it can be seen that peak stress has been reduced to 15.8,
18.7 and 16.6MPa. The reduction in strength is expected to be because of the hydration
of the concrete matrix and the expansion of aggregates. Temperatures above 300oC are
known to cause permanent damaging micro-cracks.
None of the tested specimens indicate that that they have reached an expected plateau.
It was expected that the stress-strain curves would reach a plateau before failure occurs.
Therefore it indicates that all the specimens have had early failure.
Even though the specimens have diﬀerences in peak stress and strain, they all follow
the same tendency. The standard deviation is 1.4MPa at ambient temperature with a
mean value of 21.5MPa, this equals a standard deviation of 6.7%. The mean peak stress
at 400oC is 17MPa, a 6.7% standard deviation equals a range of 15.9-18.1MPa.
The
diﬀerences of the peak stresses are just outside of the standard deviation for the general
strength of the concrete. Therefore this can not be the only answer to the diﬀerences.
A reason could be an increase in the standard deviation as the concrete is exposed to
elevated temperatures, though it can not be conﬁrmed from these results.

Additional test
This section deals with the validation of the compressive strain-stress relationship for the
particular specimen used in this project. When the non-transient tests were performed,
the validation of the results were questioned.
This additional test were performed to answer following questions:
1. How does the position and force control aﬀect the strain-stress curve and what is
the diﬀerence?
2. How does the length of the specimen aﬀect the strain-stress curve?
3. Is the plastic plateau in concrete reached for the specimen used for non-transient
and transient?
These 3 question were answered by preparing 6 specimen with the dimensions illus-
trated in ﬁgure 79. The specimen have a D/L ratio for 0.5, 0.33 and 0.15.

position controlled. Specimen a,b,c and d are all tested with a high precision extensometer,
where specimen e and f are tested in the machine used for transient and non-transient tests.
The piston position of the machine will be compared with the high precision extensometer
to validate the strain values.

The strain-stress curves for all 6 specimen are presented in ﬁgure 80. Curve (a) shows

As mentioned, curve (e) and (f), describe the 70mm x 470mm, are tested with the machine

strain-stress curves. As illustrated, the compressive strength for both specimens are 18.5

around 3.1×10−3 for both specimens. From this it can be concluded that the elongation of
specimen length decreases the compressive strength a bit, but gives a larger strain at peak
stress. Concerning the modulus of elasticity, it can be seen that curve (e) and (f), have a
smaller value than curve (a),(b),(c) and (d) in the beginning of the curve. This is caused
by a rubber layer in the spherical bearing that deform at low stress. This means that
the piston position registers bigger strains that what it should. The descending branch of
the concrete is highly depended on where the strain is measured. It can be seen that for
curve (e) and (f), descending branch of the curve is highly decreasing. Since the strain is
measured over the whole specimen for curve (e) and (f), the strains that is caused by the
failure is divided over a longer length. This makes the strain values much smaller. This
is a well known phenomena and is discussed further by Mier[19].

Experimental Development
6.1
Extensometer
As mentioned, there were several problems with the extensometer both in compression and
tension. The strain values obtained by the extensometer were generally double the values
obtained by the piston position. This is not possible unless the extensometer record with
an error. Since the whole machine is able to deform, the strains obtained by the piston
position should be the same or higher than the those of the extensometer. This is however
only applicable on the non-transient method, as the machine deformation increase with
the applied load increase. For as transient setup, the stain values should be the same for
extensometer and piston position, as any deformation will be applied before the recording
of strain. The error with the extensometer are not related to a calibration error, since
the extensometer were calibrated several times. The results from the calibration revealed
that the calibration were done correctly.
The error with the extensometer were present due to a number of problems.
As
the specimen was applied a load, only the bottom piston were moving.
Thereby the
deformation of the specimen would happen with respect to the top point of the specimen.
This create a movement of the center, moving both rods upwards or downwards. This
created more friction on one of the rods and less friction on the other, resulting in possible
loss of contact of one of the rods. This is seen in the ﬁrst two illustrations in ﬁgure 108.
Besides this, only one of the rods moves in reference to the other. As the hole specimen
moves with reference to the top, the extensometer will not only measure the relative
deformation between the rods, but also include an unknown deformation ’x’ by the entire
specimen, seen in the last illustration in ﬁgure 108
Figure 108: Movement of the extensometer rods in compression
It should be noted that it is not possible to get strain values after the load has reached
the ultimate strength, in other words the top of the plateau, due to the experimental
setup of the extensometer.
The extensometer record the strain in the middle of the
specimen over at span of approximately 25mm. As the specimen gives in at peak stress,
the extensometer will no longer record the strain unless it is precisely within its span.
Therefore the curve could theoretically continue at the same or lower stress values without
it being recorded. This is both seen in ﬁgure 57 and 58, the extensometer simply can not
record the strain as it reaches the plateau or gets very close to it.
Another problem that aﬀected the results were the alignment between the oven and
the specimen. As the extensometer is attached to the oven, any tilt between these two
111
==PAGE:113==
would possible results in the extensometer obtaining wrong values. The alignment of the
oven were very unpredictable and imprecise.
6.1.1
Attachment in Tension
The rods make contact with the concrete and is held in place by friction from a applied
load. This means that if the concrete moves, the rods will loose contact, and the mea-
surement will not be reliable. It is commonly known that when a material is aﬀected by
tension, a phenomena called the poisson ratio takes place. That is where the thickness
of the material decrease while the length increase. The decrease of the thickness of the
specimen, increases the distance between the specimen and the rods, this will make the
strain measurements less reliable. The probelem is illustrated in ﬁgure 109.
Figure 109: Problems with extensometer
A solution to this problem is to use an extensometer described in the next section.
Since the cross section is decreasing, a higher contact surface area is needed to still have
enough friction for the extensometer to measure the strains. By creating an extensometer
that are ﬁrm around the specimen, it is possible that the extensometer still is able to
measure the strains regardless of the poisson eﬀect.
112
==PAGE:114==
6.1.2
Alternative Extensometer
The solution for the extensometer problems is ﬁrst of all to extend the distance between
the rods. A distance of 25 mm is simply not enough to represent the strain for a specimen
of 470 mm. The chance that the crack appears between the rods is almost non-existent,
which were also demonstrated since none of the specimen had a crack between the rods.
The width of the rods were only 4 mm resulting in very small contact surface between
the rods and the specimen. The edge shape of the rods were straight which decreases the
surface contact even more, as the specimen is round. A solution is to make a more ﬁrm
attachment, such that no thermal expansion or friction loss can aﬀect the attachment.
The attachment of the extensometer should surround the specimen such that the whole
cross section is participating in the deformation. The extensometer should have a much
larger distance between the rods. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 110. A similar extensometer
were used by Zawadowska[26] and Damkjær[5].
Figure 110: Solution to the extensometer problem

6.2
Heating Area
As described in the experimental setup, the oven have three heating elements in total,
placed in the top, mid and bottom of the oven. The oven will thus heat a length of 360mm
of the specimen. Since the cracks of the concrete are so unpredictable in ﬁre, it is suggested
that only the heating element in the middle are used for the heating. This is creating a
scenario with more control over the position of the cracks which is highly desirable when
measuring the strain. However, the heating area should never be signiﬁcantly smaller
than the extensometer length. Figure 111 scenario A shows a heating area close to the
extensometer length which is desirable. Scenario B shows a heating area smaller than the
extensometer length which is undesirable. Scenario B will result in an underestimation
of the strains, because the displacement of the heated area will be divided by a longer
distance (extensometer distance). The heating and extensometer length should thus be
sought to have a similar length.

6.3
Dimensions of The Specimen
It should be clear so far, that the dimension of the specimens have caused some problems
to the experiments. A diameter of 70 mm were a requirement due to the inner diameter of
the oven. The results obtained in this report, that represent the aﬀect of ﬁre for concrete
with a diameter of 70 mm, is partly deviating from the results obtained by concrete with
a diameter of 100 mm. Eurocode suggests a minimum diameter of 3.5 times the biggest
aggregate size which is still fulﬁlled. The results that is obtained in this study should
not be seen as wrong results, as they are still representing the aﬀect of concrete with a
diameter of 70 mm. But since the results are partly diﬀerent from results with bigger
diameters, and a diameter of 70mm is rarely seen in real life, the diameter should be
increased such that the results are representing a larger range of real-life construction
scenarios.

Conclusion
The main aim of this study was to obtain new experimental data for concrete exposed
to elevated temperatures in both compression and tension. This is done in transient and
non-transient scenarios, as these are limited. For the compressive part, a modiﬁed ex-
isting method was used from Zawadowksa[26]. In tension, special focus was paid to the
experimental setup, as few others have made direct tensile tests.
The obtained data was compared to existing models and experimental results. The com-
pressive results were compared to models by Hertz[13], DS/EN-1-2[6], Terro[23], and
Lie[18], and experimental results by Anderberg & Thelandersson[1], Damkjær[5], and Za-
wadowska[26]. For tension comparisons were made with models by DS/EN-1-2, and Lam
& Fang[16], and experimental data by Faiyadh & Al-Ausi [10] and Thelandersson[24].
When data were compared, special attention was paid to the used design methods by
DS/EN-1-2.
The experimental setup for the compressive test were a modiﬁed setup of the one used
by Zawadowska[26], as a diﬀerent oven was used. For the direct tensile test a completely
new setup was made. Both test setups and the prior steps to completing the test, are
described in full detail for others to continue the work or replicate the test. Further more,
the issues and solutions for the used experimental setups were examined.
Prior to the compressive and tensile test at elevated temperatures, several preliminary
experiments were made to obtain reference values at ambient temperatures. In addition,
the tests gave reasonable validation to the obtained results, from factors such as age and
batch diﬀerences, as well as aleatory diﬀerences. This was specially needed in tension, as
it was a completely new setup, never tested before. Preliminary temperature tests were
made to validate the function of the oven and to determine the time for the concrete
specimens to reach a constant homogeneous temperature within the cross section. The
temperature results were also validated by the analytically model by Hertz[12].
Several specimens were tested for compression at both transient and non-transient con-
ditions. Non-transient results showed closest similarities to models by Hertz and Terro,
regarding the strain at peak stress. The reduction in ultimate stress were generally lower
than the compared models, due to generally early failure.
The transient results were compared with experimental result by Anderberg & Thelander-
sson[2], Zawadowska[26], and Damkjær[5]. The results showed closest similarities with
Anderberg & Thelandersson for the given test parameters, as expansion was seen. This
is opposite the results by both Zawadowska [26] and Damkjær[5], which showed com-
pression. Further inconsistency between results by Anderberg & Thelandersson and this
study, regarding the exact peak strain and critical temperature, is probably a consequence
of diﬀerence in testing and curing conditions, as this seems to have a signiﬁcant impact.
As transient conditions simulates real life conditions, the non-transient and transient re-
sults were compared to the extend it was possible. Opposite to the common belief, results
showed that non-transient experiments have a tendency to overestimate the compressive
strength, the same conclusion as Zawadowska[26] derived. As a conclusion, DS/EN-1-2[6]
may give non-conservative values for concrete in ﬁre. This is considered that the design
method by DS/EN-1-2 is based on the non-transient models and it predict higher strain
values at peak stress than the results of this study.
116
==PAGE:118==
With regards to the tensile conditions, specimens were tested in both transient and non-
transient scenarios. The results were compared with non-transient models by DS/EN-1-2
[6], Faiyadh & Al-Ausi [10], Thelandersson [24] and Lam & Fang [16]. The models for
non-transient tensile values are in in general showing signiﬁcant variabilities. The most
conservative of the compared models are DS/EN-1-2. The results from this study showed
closest similarities to DS/EN-1-2, however with even more conservative values. This is
despite that DS/EN-1-2 is most likely based on split cylinder tests, which generally are
know to overestimate the tensile strength. In the comparison between transient and non-
transient results, it proved diﬃcult to conclude a diﬀerence, as no directly comparable
results were obtained. Only by a small indication, a tendency of similar results was seen
between transient and non-transient results in tensile conditions.
The validity of the results obtained from the direct tensile test, are however probably
with some larger inaccuracy compared to the compressive. This was discovered as several
diﬃculties was seen with the tensile test, during the progress of this study.
Several problems were faced during the experimental phase both in compression and
tension. Specially the measurement of strain resulted in many challenges that required
some problem solving. The strain measurement throughout this project revealed an er-
ror in the combination of the experimental setup and the extensometer. Thus, resulting
in using the machine position instead of the extensometer for the strain measurements.
Therefore, the results obtained in the non-transient compression have a larger uncertain-
ties, due to the higher stresses and thus larger deformations of the machine. The results
obtained in the transient tests, both for compression and tension, do not have uncertain-
ties in the strain used by the piston position, because the measurements were without an
increasing load. The specimens for tensile test were designed with a pull bar for the test
machine to attach and apply a direct tensile load. The results in non-transient tension
revealed that the pull bars used to perform the tensile experiments, are not optimal due to
an external moment applied to the system thus creating a scenario without pure tension.

B
The Function of The Oven
The oven used for these experiments is a ’SFL, Model 5F868D’ cylindrical oven. Three
heating elements are distributed to the top, centre and bottom, each controlled individ-
ually by a Instron 902/3/4 controller. The layout of the controller is seen in ﬁgure 114.
It is an analog input, which means a lot of pressing through menus and parameters, and
double pressing. The controller basically works by setting a ’SetPoint(SP)’ which is the
desired temperature one wants to reach. In the normal start-up mode the oven will then
try to reach this temperature as fast as possible by setting its ’OutputPower(OP)’ to
100%. The controller is seen in ﬁgure 114. The SP is set by pressing button 6, until ’SP’
is displayed, it is then adjusted by pressing button 4/5. There are mainly two function,
the ramp function and the manual control function.
Figure 114: Top zone controller for the oven
At the start of the project the ramp function was not activated in the controller. This
is the function which allows the user to set a certain temperature ramp rate, such as
2oC/min. This function should be activated know for future use as well as the tuning of
the oven/controller. This process will not be explained any further.
The Manual function is pretty self explanatory. It allows the user to manual set the
’OutputPower(OP)’. First the function is activated by pressing button 3 this will turn on
a ’MAN’ icon in the upper right corner of the display. The parameters which the user
wants to change is circle through by pressing button 6, and ﬁnally changed by button
4/5. The oven will immediately function at the chosen OP. To turn oﬀthe manual con-
trol function, button 3 is pressed again.
The Ramp function works by the controller targeting a certain temperature within a
small amount of time until it reaches the desired SP. The ’SetPointRate(SPr)’ determines
this rate. It is changed in the ’Operator Menu (OPEr). To do so one has do the follow.
1. Access the ’Main Scroll’ by pressing and hold button 6.

2. When entering ’Main Scroll’ OPEr should be shown as the ﬁrst option.
If not,
repeatedly press button 6 until it is shown, possibly starting at item 1 again.
3. Enter OPEr by pressing button 1.
4. When entering OPEr, SPr should be the ﬁrst parameter if not button 6 is pressed
repeatedly to circle through the diﬀerent parameter until SPr is shown.
5. The SPr is then changed by pressing button 4/5.
6. To leave OPEr button 1 is pressed and afterward button 6 repeatedly until you are
back at the starting screen (Short Scroll)
The ramp function will ramp up until the set SP is reached and hold the oven at this
temperature, indicated by a ’E’ icon on the display. To activate the ramp function, ones
the SPr is set, button 2 is pressed. This will turn on a ’RAMP’ icon on the display and
the ramp function will immediately start. By pressing button 2 again the oven will hold
the current temperature, indicated by the ’HOLD’ icon turning on. To carry on the ramp,
press button 2 again and the ’HOLD’ icon will turn oﬀ. To turn oﬀthe ramp function
button 4 and 5 is pressed simultaneously.
It should be noted that there is a diﬀerence in turning the controller and the oven
on. The oven is ﬁrst turned on when pressing the dedicated button on the controller but
the controller will at all times act and think like the oven is constantly on. For the most
precise results the oven should be turned on whenever the ramp function is activated.
