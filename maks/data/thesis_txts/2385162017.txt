INTRODUCTION 
 
1.1 
Formulation of the problem 
 
The aim of this study is to determine the best course of action in producing the Valde-
mar Resevoir of the North Sea by gas injection. The considered options are miscible and 
immiscible injections of produced gas. 
 
1.2 
Previous work on Valdemar  
 
In a previous work on Valdemar field the analytical solution comparing gas and 
waterflooding proved gas injection to yield lower ultimate oil recovery, however with a 
significantly shorter response time which makes it a promising candidate for implemen-
tation in the field. Subsequently the reservoir model was built and  gas injection simula-
tions were performed assuming hydraulically fractured wells: 1st scenario was an injec-
tion at 3500psi for which capacity restrictions enabled drilling 10 wells, the 2nd scenario 
was an injection at 5500psi which is around MMP for MCM displacement with 5 wells. 
Both solutions turned out to yield similar RF, while 2nd option being much cheaper 
(Suicmez & al, 2015) 
 
 
 
1.3 
Immiscible gas injection 
 
Immiscible gas injection into oil reservoirs is a method of increasing oil recovery by 
immiscible displacement. The definition proposed by Muskat has characterized the 
pressure maintenance by gas injection as a process of  “injection of gas into an oil reser-
voir during  the course of its primary-production history” (Muskat, 1949). Its purpose is 
to retard the pressure decline An ideal case assumes complete prevention of the decline, 
maintaining current pressure by injecting sufficient amount of fluids, with the ultimate 
==PAGE:10==
 
10 
goal of extending the flowing production period of the well and thus increasing the cu-
mulative oil recovery.  
The abovementioned term is differentiated from the secondary-recovery gas injection 
which is applied to the operations after the primary depletion in reservoirs which have 
already been considerably depleted (Muskat, 1949). 
 
The first immiscible gas injection projects were launched in 1930s for pressure mainte-
nance and used lean hydrocarbon gas, which is the dry gas that is obtained at the surface 
after separation from liquids or condensate.  Afterwards water injections projects were 
also initiated. Both fluids remain the most commonly used injection fluids.  
 
Availability of gas supply is a requirement for a gas injection project to be evaluated. 
The gas  usually comes from produced solution gas, gas from the gas-cap or from an-
other gas field in the vicinity (Warner Jr. & Holstein, 2007). Several types of gas injec-
tion operations exist, however they can be classified in two main categories, based on 
the location of injection, relative to the oil zone. 
 
1.3.1 
Dispersed gas injection  
 
Dispersed gas injection is also referred to as pattern injection, it typically utilizes some 
geometric layout of gas injectors in order to obtain a uniform distribution of gas 
throughout the producing section of reservoir. However, in reality the layout often dif-
fers from the usual 5-point or 9-point patters, it is due to the irregularities in reservoir 
geometry, permeability or porosity changes and relative positions to existing wells. This 
method is best suitable for the low-relief and low-permeability reservoirs and it provides 
fast pressure response allowing for faster reservoir depletion.  The recovery efficiency 
of this method is reduced, compared to external gas injection method, by little or no 
gravity-drainage contribution, lower areal sweep efficiency and  gas “fingering”, fur-
thermore high investment costs associated with drilling and operation of higher number 
of new wells  additionally reduces the profitability (Roebuck Jr., 1987). 
 
1.3.2 
External gas injection 
 
External gas injection is the other type and is sometimes called crestal gas injection. 
This concept is based on performing injection operations in the high parts of the reser-
voir, mainly in the primary or secondary gas cap. It is most efficient in reservoirs with 
substantial structural relief and good vertical permeability. Injection wells arrangement 
aims at maximizing areal distribution and the contribution of gravity drainage effect. A 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
factor that strongly influences the total number of wells is the injectivity of individual 
wells. (Roebuck Jr., 1987)  
Due to the presence of gravity drainage effect the external gas injection method is more 
efficient than dispersed gas injection method when a reservoir fulfills required condi-
tions. The gravity force enhances the downdip displacement of oil towards the produc-
tion wells, by stabilizing the movement, keeping the gas phase on top of the oil phase. If 
the production is performed at a rate below the critical rate the gas/oil contact will move 
at a uniform rate and this will yield greater volumetric sweep efficiency. The critical 
rate is given by the following equation (Warner Jr. & Holstein, 2007).

1.3.4.1 
Swanson River Field (Alaska, United States) 
 
An example of a successful immiscible gas injection is the Swanson River Field 
project. The field is composed of an anticlinal reservoir cut by several faults , perpen-

dicular to the anticline.  The payzone interval consists of interbedded fine to coarse 
sandstone, conglomerate, siltstone and coal with numerous impermeable stringers. The 
stringers have been confirmed to be barriers to the vertical flow around the producing 
wells.  The reservoir fluid system was a highly undersaturated oil with     API gravity 
with initial formation volume factor of 1.21 at initial pressure of 5580 psi. Its 
bubblepoint pressure was at 1350 psi.     
        The reservoir exhibited rapid pressure decline upon the beginning of production. It

maximize recovery factor  and oil rate performance.  Dry gas for reinjection was sup-

Haft Kel Field (Iran)

1.4 
Miscible Gas Injection  
 
 
Miscible injection processes use gas either of designed composition or at high enough 
pressure to reach miscibility of given phases. Such  processes increase the oil recovery 
factor by eliminating the interfacial tension between oil and displacing fluid, thus reach-
ing the reserves that are trapped in the porous media by capillary forces and decreasing 
residual oil saturation. Fully miscible displacement process assumes that  injected and 
displaced fluids mix in all proportions without creating two phase regions. In such case 
the interfacial tension is eliminated completely and the displacement efficiency ap-
proaches 100%. Most solvents in oilfield use however, form two phases over a broad 
range of mixtures and pressures when mixed with reservoir oils and it is necessary to 
inject the solvent at high enough pressure or rich enough composition to reach miscibil-
ity (Holstein & Stalkup, 2007).These required conditions can be determined by finding 
the value of minimum miscibility pressure (MMP) or minimum miscibility enrichment 
(MME) through experimental measurements, empirical correlations or equation-of-state 
(EOS) calculations.

1.4.1 
Miscibility 
 
As previously mentioned, full miscibility is achieved when fluids form a single phase in 
all proportions. This phenomenon is called first contact miscibility (FCM). It occurs at 
the first contact of phases at the investigated conditions of pressure and temperature. 
 
However it is not the only way two fluids may become miscible. Another way to devel-
op miscibility is through dynamic mixing of fluids, which is called multiple contact 
miscibility (MCM). In this process injected gas exchanges components with the reser-
voir oil until the fluids form a single phase.  In  a vaporizing drive, light and intermedi-
ate hydrocarbons from the oil come into the gas phase. The opposite happens in a con-
densing drive, where intermediate components from the gas phase enter the reservoir 
oil. The two processes may be combined.  
 
1.4.2 
Screening criteria 
 
Before implementation of a miscible flooding process, an initial screening of 
thereservoir must be conducted. According to Stal up’s (1   ) guidelines for identi-
fying oil reservoirs with potential for miscible process,  the desired viscosities for 
these processes are at 1cp or less with practical upper limit of 5cp. He has also es-
tablished that crude oils with mid API-gravities  are most suitable for first contact 
miscible and condensing drive processes, the former requiring pressures in the range 
of 900 psia to 1300 psia to reach miscibility, equivalent to reservoir depths between 
1500 ft to 2500 ft and the latter requires pressures between 1500 and 3000 psia with 
the reservoir depth of minimum 2000 ft. High API gravity oils, on the other hand, 
are suitable for vaporizing drive processes and require pressure of at least 3500 psia 
which makes it applicable only to deep reservoirs ( see

1.4.3.1 
The Sleipner Ty Field (Norway) 
 
 he Sleipner  y field is a structural stratigraphic trap  It’s a gas-condensate sand-
stone reservoir with strong underlying waterdrive and  permeabilities of 100 to 
1000mD. Reservoir’s initial pressure was slightly above dew point and condensate

Dry gas and CO2 rich dry gas was reinjected in order to minimize decrease in res-

Prudhoe Bay Field (United States) 
 
The Prudhoe Bay reservoir is a structural trap, its largest producing horizon has an 
average thickness of 500ft and consists of series of clastic zones made of sandstones and 
conglomeratic braided-stream deposits. The reservoir rock has an average permeability 
of 500md, porosity of 22% and water initial saturation of 35%. The original fluid gravi-

Some parts of the vast reservoir operated initially under gravity drainage other 
under waterflood. After initial waterflooding and immiscible gas injection project, an 
enriched-hydrocarbon miscible WAG injection project was started in 1983 with me-
thane with addition of propane and butane. 
Originally all the injection patterns received a solvent injection of 1% of total pore 
volume per year, however this approach has been changed after some time. The new 
distribution approach used field data to control the distribution of miscible injectant, 
directing more of it to the most efficient patterns and has increased amount of oil being 
mobilized by up to 50%. Estimated ultimate recovery of oil in place is 60% and the 
miscible injection production increase constitutes 10% of oil originally in place.

1.4.3.3 
Intisar “D” Field (Libya) 
 
It is a carbonate reservoir with 22% porosity and permeability of 200 mD. It has a 
very good vertical communication. The reservoir fluid was highly undersaturated with 
saturation pressure at 2224 psig and original reservoir pressure of 4257 psig.  
The field has been developed with water injection low in the structure for pressure 
support and crestal enriched gas injection with gas supplied  from other fields in the 
area. Performance was simulated with a modified black oil model.  
In the course of studies on the reservoir performance it was found that residual 
saturations in the gas-swept zone were successfully decreasing with time because of 
vaporization and gravity drainage effect. Performance was expected to be dominated by 
coning and completion intervals were advised to be lowered as the gas front advances. 
Ultimate recovery factor of the project was estimated to be 67.5% and was better than 
waterflooding performance.

RESERVOIR’S DESCRIPTION 
 
 
2.1 
Valdemar Field  
 
The Valdemar Field is located in the Danish Sector of the North Sea and is operated by 
Maersk Company. It is comprised of the southern reservoir called Bo and the northern 
reservoir called North Jens, both at 38 m water depth. The Bo area of the field was dis-
covered in 1977, followed by discovery of North Jens area in 1985, the two reservoirs 
were put in production in 2007 and 1993, respectively. 
 
Production Facilities  
 
The North Jens area of the Valdemar Field has been designed as a satellite to the Tyra 
field, it consists of two bridge-connected, STAR type, wellhead platforms, Valdemar 
AA and AB with 16 producing wells in total  S AR stands for “slim tripod adapted for 
rig installation”, which is a platform technology that aims at minimi ing costs and safe-
ty hazards by introducing small, unmanned, multi-well platforms suitable for shallow 
waters. The design can be utilized as a temporary platform in the early stages of field 
development or in projects with reservoir uncertainties.  Separation of the production 
takes place at Valdemar AB platform. The produced liquids are subsequently transport-
ed by 20 km pipeline to Tyra East for processing and export, while the produced gas is 
sent to Tyra West. The chemicals for the Valdemar AA and AB platforms are provided 
from Tyra East and power from Tyra West. 
 
The Bo area of the Valdemar Field facility consists of an unmanned, STAR type, well-
head platform, Valdemar BA with 7 producing wells. Produced fluids are transported by 
a 16” multiphase pipeline to Tyra East, via Roar.

Review of Geology 
 
Both parts of the Valdemar Field are anticline chalk structures associated with tectonic 
uplift. Significant amounts of oil have been identified in Upper and Lower Cretaceous 
chalk formations in several separate accumulations, however the extremely low-
permeable layers of the Lower Cretaceous chalk induce production difficulties in some 
parts of the Valdemar Field. 
 
The gas permeabilities obtained during measurements conducted on non-fractured Low-
er Cretaceous samples range from 0.01 to less than 4mD with an average around 
0.4mD. Fracture permeability, however, could increase the effective permeability of the 
reservoir rock fivefold (Frykman, 2002).

Production Strategy 
 
The oil is produced by means of natural depletion. Both Upper and Lower Cretaceous 
layers have been developed in the Valdemar Field. The field is developed with long 
horizontal wells drilled in a parallel pattern, oriented in a north- south direction.  The 
process of fracturing of the low permeability Lower Cretaceous layer and subsequent 
filling the fractures with sand has enabled economic production from the layer.

ANALYTICAL SOLUTION 
 
3.1 
Description of Buckley-Leverett  theory 
 
The Buckley-Leverett theory first presented in the paper “Mechanism of fluid displace-
ment in sands” (Buckley & Leverett, 1941) describes the process of immiscible oil dis-
placement in one dimensional reservoir  It’s a useful tool for estimating the rate at 
which the injected fluid advances through a porous medium  In this study it’s going to 
be utilized for comparison of performance of water and gas injections. 
 
3.2 
Derivation of Buckley-Leverett equation 
 
The equation governing the movement of displacement front has been derived based on 
the following assumptions: 
 Flow is linear and horizontal 
 Both  the displacing and the displaced fluids are incompressible 
 The two fluids are immiscible 
 Capillary pressure effects are negligible

3.3.3 
Comparison 
 
The process of gas injection yields almost two times lower recovery than waterflooding, 
30.9% compared to 58.4%, however its response time is much shorter than in case of 
water injection. Abandonment time was assumed to be equal to the point were the frac-

injection occurs much later, at 3.37 of pore volumes injected and the recovery factor at 
this point amounts to 50.84%  which is only 21% less then ultimate recovery by water-
flooding. 
 
There are additional factors that can increase ultimate recovery by gas injection like 
reduction of interfacial tension and possibility of developing miscibility with the reser-
voir oil, that are not accounted for in the Buckley-Leverett solution. The above argu-
ments make the gas injection a good prospective way of increasing oil recovery and 
prove further research worthwhile.

4.3 
Ternary plots 
 
In a vaporizing gas drive the injected lean gas vaporizes the light hydrocarbon compo-
nents from the oil. After multiple contacts with the reservoir oil it can develop miscibil-
ity. In such process the composition of a new created gas moves along the dew-point 
line towards the plait point.  The plait point is located where the bubble-point line and 
the dew-point line meet and it corresponds to the critical point. 
 
Miscibility is developed when the line connecting the composition of the enriched gas 
and the reservoir fluid does not cross the two phase region. To facilitate the process of 
determining whether or not the gas can reach the miscibility with the oil a line has been 
constructed. This is a line that is tangent to the two phase region and stretches from the 
plait point to the bottom axis. It represents the richest composition that the gas can reach 
in contact with oil.  It divides the rest of the ternary plot into two areas, one to the left 
from the line, in which the miscibility cannot be developed, and the second area to the 
right from the line in which the miscibility will be developed. 
 
The ternary plot below, see Figure 4.5, was constructed for the reservoir fluid. It con-
firms that the original conditions represented by the dot, place the reservoir oil in a sin-
gle phase region, since the dot is outside the two phase region. However, the construct-
ed line proves that the injected gas wouldn’t develop miscibility at these conditions  
 
 
Figure 4.5 – Ternary plot for the reservoir fluid under initial reservoir conditions. 
 
The second plot was constructed for the minimum miscibility pressure. The higher pres-
sure causes the two phase region to shrink and thus puts the dot representing oil compo-
==PAGE:42==
 
42 
sition directly at the path of the tangent line drawn from the plait point to the bottom 
axis. It means that at this conditions the gas will develop multiple contact miscibility. 
This method, however, does not fully explain the processes that occur in the reservoir, 
since the development of the miscibility in the reservoir is a combination of vaporizing 
and condensing drive.

4.4 
First contact miscibility pressure 
The first contact miscibility pressure is a pressure at which the gas and oil are miscible 
in all proportions. Its value has been determined using the PVTi simulation program and 
is equal to 10463 psi. The determination of this value can be explained with the Figure 
4.7. It shows phase envelopes for the original oil as well as oil with 50% and 65% of 
lean gas injected. When one considers the value of reservoir temperature (363K), the 
first contact miscibility is the lowest pressure at which a point representing reservoir 
conditions will never be inside the two phase region. No matter in what proportions are 
the two phases mixed.

4.5 
Conclusions 
 
The minimum miscibility pressure was found to be equal 5500 psi, the first contact mis-
cibility pressure was found to be equal 10463 psi. The first contact miscibility pressure 
value is too high to be considered as a potential injection pressure. Thus in the further 
investigation only pressures around minimum miscibility pressure will be taken into 
account. It has been decided that the injection simulation will be performed at pressures 
below and above the MMP and then the results will be compared. The lower pressure 
will of the simulations will be 4000 psi. The higher pressure will be 6210 psi, which is 
15% higher than the original reservoir pressure. It will allow to maximize the miscibility 
processes and simultaneously it will be low enough to avoid accidental formation frac-
turing.

CONCLUSION 
 
 
6.1 
Results 
 
In the the course of this study the pressure conditions required for developing miscibil-
ity were determined. The first contact miscibility pressure turned to be too high to be 
considered a prospective option for injection, thus a multiple contact miscibility option 
was studied. A 3D model representing a part of the Valdemar field was build, with two 
injection wells at the sides and one production well in the center. Subsequently a series 
of simulations was conducted: simulation of production without injection, production 
with the injection well bottom hole pressure of 4000 psi, and 6210 psi, corresponding to 
values below and above the minimum miscibility pressure, and lastly a simulation of a 
production from hydraulically fractured well with the bottom hole injection pressure of 
6210 psi. 
 
The obtained results allowed to conclude that the most viable option of production, that 
requires moderate amount of injection and yields satisfactory levels of recovery factor is 
injection of produced gas, under a pressure higher than minimum miscibility pressure 
allowing for development of miscibility, which in this study was taken to be 6210 psi. 
This pressure is 15% above the original reservoir pressure. The gas for injection can be 
supplied from produced lean gas and from nearby fields. Injection of 0.72 pore volumes 
of lean gas would yield 41% recovery factor after 5 years under the abovementioned 
conditions. In the same period production without injection would result in 12% recov-
ery factor. 
 
The fractured model offered little additional recovery but significantly quicker produc-
tion time, reducing the time needed for reaching 41% recovery from 5 to 3 years. 
 
If the abandonment time for the high pressure injection scenario was assumed to be at 
the point where the oil constitutes 2% of the produced fluids, then the abandonment 
time would occur after injecting 3.6 pore volumes and the recovery factor at this point 
would be 60.3%  It is a significantly higher value than the one obtained in analytical 
==PAGE:59==
 
59 
solution for gasflooding. That solution yielded 50.8% of recovery and the abandonment 
time occurring after injecting 3.37 pore volumes. It didn’t however ta e into account 
vaporizing and condensing processes occurring in the reservoir. Thus the result obtained 
in the Eclipse simulator should be deemed as more reliable.
