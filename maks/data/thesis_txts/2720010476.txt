Abstract
The adverse effects of emission of greenhouse gasses, as well as the growing demand
for plastics and other chemicals, call for an alternative to production of chemicals
from fossil resources. Chemical production from biomass feedstock is a promising
alternative to petrochemical production. Furfural, which is derived from lignocel-
lulosic biomass, is considered a promising platform chemical for biobased chemical
production. Selective oxidation furfural yields 1,4-diacids, which are important pre-
cursors for production of polymers, paint, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals.
The
focus of this project was liquid phase selective oxidation of furfural to maleic acid
with TS-1 based catalysts using hydrogen peroxide as the oxidant. The activity
of the TS-1 was enhanced by introducing mesopores and nickel oxide nanoparticles
in TS-1. The promotional effect of nickel oxide was discovered by screening several
transition metal oxides impregnated hierarchical TS-1 materials. A maleic acid yield
of 58% was obtained with the novel NiO/HTS-1 catalyst during a 24-hour reaction at
50℃. The catalysts were characterized by XRD, TEM, N2-physisorption, XPS and
NH3/CO2-TPD. An in-situ 13C-NMR study of the NiO/HTS-1 catalyzed furfural
oxidation confirmed that furfural peroxide and furan-2-yl formate were intermedi-
ates in the maleic acid formation. Based on the time series a reaction pathway for
maleic and succinic acid formation was proposed.

Introduction
For decades, gas, oil and coal have supplied the world with energy, fuels, and petro-
chemicals. Petrochemical is a term describing chemicals isolated by crude oil refine-
ment or derived from natural gas. Derivatives of these petrochemicals are found in
everyday life, as detergents, plastics, synthetic fibers and dyes [1].
As the reserves of fossil resources are decreasing, and the effects of global warming
due to emissions of greenhouse gasses become more adverse, alternatives to fossil
resources are vital. In addition, the demand for petrochemicals, especially plastics, is
expected to grow as a result of growing economies and increasing populations. The
International Energy Agency projects that petrochemical production will rapidly
become the largest driver for oil consumption, accounting for nearly half the demand
for oil by 2050 [2].
A promising alternative to petrochemical production is biobased chemical produc-
tion. In this process, biomass, which is the most abundant source of carbon on
earth, is used as a feedstock. Biomass is defined as ”biological material from living
or recently living organisms” such as algae, crops, trees and waste from food and
paper production. It is estimated that 170 billion tonnes of biomass is produced
annually on earth. Currently 3.5% of the biomass on earth is utilized by humans.
In order to generate a complete biobased economy, it is estimated that 25% of the
biomass annually produced is needed [3]. Lignocellulosic biomass, found in trees,
plants and some crops, is of special interest for bioeconomy since it cannot be used
for food or feed.
Simplified life cycles of petrochemicals and biobased chemicals are compared in
figure 1.1. The beginning of the cycles are similar, both feedstocks are processed by
chemical industry to yield fuel, chemicals and materials. The combustion of fuel or
waste from used products, releases CO2 into the atmosphere. The regeneration of the
feedstocks after CO2 release differs, especially on a timescale. Biomass is regenerated
in a so-called short-term carbon cycle, in which plants sequesters and converts CO2
into biomass via photosynthesis. In contrast oil is formed in the long-term carbon
cycle, a process which takes millions of years, where plant biomass under specific
conditions is transformed to oil. Due to this difference in regeneration time, carbon
from biomass is denoted as renewable in contrast to carbon from fossil resources [4,
5]. The short regeneration time and the large amounts of biomass available to us,
makes biobased chemical production an advantageous alternative to petrochemical
production. Other drivers for implementing biobased chemical production includes
the projected near-future production peak of fossil fuels as well as decreasing the
dependency of fossil fuel import [6].

Theoretically most chemicals derived from fossil resources can be synthesized from
biomass, however there are several technical and financial limitations [7]. In order
to compete with petrochemical production from a economical point of view, the
processes must be energy and cost efficient. From a chemical point of view, crude
oil and biomass are very different feedstocks. Although both contain carbon, crude
oil mainly consists of hydrocarbons and aromatics, while biomass contains various
polymers with a high oxygen content. Therefore, development of new and efficient
processes is vital for bioeconomy. The use of biomass as a feedstock, which itself is
considered carbon neutral, set high demands for designing environmentally benign
processes for utilization and upgrading of biomass [8].
The 12 principles of Green Chemistry provide guidelines for developing green
chemical processes. Developed by Anastas and Warner, they outline the impor-
tance of reducing waste and maximizing atom economy [9, 10]. A corner stone of
green chemistry is catalysis.
Catalysis is processes where a chemical reaction is
accelerated by the addition of chemical species, a catalyst, which is not consumed
during the reaction. A catalyst lowers the activation energy of a reaction by provid-
ing another and usually more selective reaction pathway [11]. By designing processes
with catalysts, one can reduce or even remove the need for reagents and solvents,
thus lowering the amount of waste produced by the reaction. Since catalysts lower
the activation barrier of the reaction, the reaction can run at milder and less energy
intensive conditions [10].
Furfural, derived from lignocellulosic biomass, is regarded as a promising platform
chemical for bioeconomy. A total of 80 chemicals, is directly or indirectly derived
from furfural [12]. Selective oxidation of furfural can yield C4 carboxylic acids. A
schematic representation of the process is shown in scheme 1.1. Maleic acid is used
as a precursor for polyester resins, surface coatings, copolymers and agricultural
chemicals.
In addition maleic acid is a platform to obtain other important C4
diacids such as fumaric and succinic acid [13].

The focus of this project is the selective liquid phase oxidation of furfural to maleic
acid using hydrogen peroxide as the oxidant. The titanium silicate zeolite, TS-1,
has shown unique catalytic activity for oxidation reactions. For furfural oxidation
TS-1 has shown good selectivity towards maleic acid production [14–17]. The major
drawback of the TS-1 catalyst is the microporous structure causing diffusion lim-
itations, thus restricting access to catalytic sites. In addition poor mass transfer
can lead to gradual deactivation due to polymerization of furfural and it can lower
selectivity due to side reactions [18]. The structure of TS-1 allows for reactants
with a kinetic diameter of 5.5 ˚A to enter the pores, making it difficult for bulky
molecules, such as furfural to diffuse into the zeolite structure. In order to overcome
the diffusion and steric limitations of TS-1, hierarchical TS-1 has been synthesized
[16, 19, 20].
A study of furfural adsorption on TS-1 showed weak adsorption of the carbonyl
group of furfural compared to other catalysts [21].
By facilitating selective and
stronger adsorption of either the furan ring or carbonyl group of furfural the reaction
pathway and thus the product selectivity could be influenced. From other studies
of furfural conversion, mainly hydrogenation reactions, it is known that various
transition metal (oxides) provide adsorption sites for furfural [22, 23]. Studies found
that homogeneous metal catalyst promoted furfural oxidation by formation peroxo
complexes with the carbonyl group of furfural [24, 25]. In addition for other TS-
1 catalyzed oxidation reactions, introduction of metal species has yielded higher
catalytic activity due to synergistic effects [26–29]. Furthermore transition metal
catalysts have successfully been applied for Baeyer-Villiger oxidation reactions [30].
The aim of the project is to improve the catalytic activity of TS-1 for furfural ox-
idation by (i) introducing mesopores in order to reduce diffusion limitations and (ii)
introducing metal oxides species which could provide adsorptive sites. Metal oxides
are introduced in two different synthetic ways by incipient wetness impregnation
in hierarchical TS-1 and encapsulation in TS-1. The catalyst are characterized by
XRD, N2-physisorption, NH3-TPD, CO2-TPD, XPS, XRF and TEM. The activity
of the catalysts for furfural oxidation is examined at various conditions using H2O2
as the oxidant. The furfural oxidation reaction was studied in-situ by 13C-NMR and
based on the observed intermediates a reaction pathway for maleic and succinic acid
production was proposed.

Background
2.1
Furfural - a platform for biobased chemical
production
Biorefineries combine various mechanical, biological and chemical processes in order
to generate energy and chemicals from biomass. Platform chemicals derived from
biomass are versatile building blocks for production of commodity and fine chemicals
[5]. In contrast to fossil fuels, biomass is considered a renewable resource since the
it can be regenerated within a time interval which is consistent with our resource
consumption. Lignocellulosic biomass is found in agricultural waste, wood residues
and energy crops. It is the most abundant type of biomass feedstock, and it does
not enter the human food cycle. In addition, lignocellulosic biomass grows faster
and is cheaper to produce than edible biomass. Therefore it is of great interest for
biomass upgrading [31].
The main components of lignocellulosic biomass are cellulose, hemicellulose, and
lignin. The distribution of these components, as well as water, acid and alkali metal
content vary depending on the biomass feedstock, region and season. While lignin
utilization remains a challenge due to the complexity of the polymer, there is great
potential in converting cellulose and hemicellulose into platform chemicals. From
these platform molecules various chemicals and biofuels can be derived [32].
Furfural is considered a promising platform chemical for biobased production since
it is a highly functional molecule which allows for many different derivatizations [12,
31]. It has been produced from biomass on commercial scale since 1921. Furfural is
produced from pentosans i.e., xylans. Pentosans are found in hemicellulose, which
is a significant component of lignocellulosic biomass. The hydrolysis of xylan is acid
catalyzed and is followed by a cyclodehydration of xylose [12], shown in scheme 2.1
.

Oxidation of furfural to C4 1,4-dicarboxylic acids is of great interest for biobased
chemical production. 1,4-diacids, such as maleic and succinic acids are important
building blocks in the chemical industry for production of polyesters, polyurethanes,
resins, paint, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals. In addition 1,4-diacids also have the
potential of replacing other fossil fuel derived chemical precursors such as phthalic
acid and adipic acid [21].
Maleic and succinic acid are currently produced predominantly from crude oil
feedstock but there is some industrial scale production of succinic acid by fermen-
tation of glucose [33]. Another pathway for producing 1,4-diacids is by selective
oxidation of furfural. Due to the high functionality of furfural, there are many dif-
ferent oxidation products as shown in scheme 2.2. Therefore, selectivity is a key
factor in catalyst design and evaluation.
Maleic acid is of special interest since it can act as a platform for obtaining
other diacids. As shown in scheme 2.3 it can be converted into fumaric acid by acid
catalyzed isomerization and to succinic acid via hydrogenation [13].

Oxidation of furfural can be conducted in liquid or gas phase. The reaction in gas
phase with O2 as the oxidant yields up to 75% maleic anhydride over vanadium
oxide based catalyst [34, 35]. The major drawback of this process is the high energy
requirements making it difficult to design an economically viable commercial process.
Furfural oxidation in liquid phase allows for milder conditions and the use of
other oxidants. Biorefineries produces furfural in aqueous solution, and thus it is
advantageous to design a catalyst system where the biorefinery product can be used
directly thereby rendering distillation steps obsolete [14].
Hydrogen peroxide and oxygen are the most commonly used oxidants for liquid
phase furfural oxidation. Oxygen has successfully been applied as an oxidant for
metal (oxide) catalyzed furfural oxidation [36–41]. The process requires tempera-
tures of 90-110℃and a pressure of 10-20 bar. Catalyst deactivation by furfural
polymerization is often observed for systems using O2 as an oxidant [42].
Hydrogen peroxide, H2O2, is a widely used oxidant for furfural oxidation. The
use of H2O2 allows the reaction to proceed under atmospheric pressure at 30-100℃
[7]. H2O2 has a high oxygen content and is considered a green oxidant [43]. Hydrogen
peroxide is also a potent oxidant and when combined with the vast reaction network
of possible products (see scheme 2.2), selectivity control is key.
2.2.1
Catalytic systems for liquid phase oxidation
Liquid phase furfural oxidation with H2O2 as the oxidant have been achieved using
homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysts. Even catalyst-free systems, where the
oxidant, H2O2, is activated by high frequency ultrasound resulted in high maleic
acid yields [44].
Li et al. reported a metal catalyst free method of producing maleic acid by using
organic acids as solvent systems. A system with formic acid was most successful,
and a maleic acid yield of 95% was achieved at 60 ℃[45]. Another study found
that formic acid in a biphasic system will catalyze selective production of 2(5H)-
furanone (6). The high yield, 62%, was attributed to the biphasic system, allowing
for a reservoir of product in the organic phase inhibiting further oxidation of 2(5H)-
furanone to diacids [42]. An alternative to homogeneous mineral or organic acid
catalysts, is betaine hydrochloride. The homogeneous catalyst is easily recycled by
a precipitation method. A maleic acid yield of 61% was obtained and the catalyst
could be recycled 5 times without loss of activity [46].
Catalyst recyclability is a key advantage of using heterogeneous catalysts. For fur-
fural oxidation several different types of solid acid catalyst have been successfully
applied. In table 2.1 the performances of some important catalyst are summarized.
Sulphonic acid resins such as Amberlyst-15 (entry 1) and Smopex-101 have shown
selectivity towards succinic acid production. The benzene rings of the resin catalyst
stabilize furfural by π −π interactions with the furan ring. Other Brønsted acid
catalyst such as SO3H-carbocatalysts [47, 48] and sulphated zirconia [49] also show
affinity for succinic acid production.
The sulphonic acid carbocatalyst with nitrogen functionalities, SO3H–C–tepa
(entry 3), reported by Thubsuang et al. showed superior succinic acid yield com-
pared to other sulphonic acid solid catalyst tested under the same conditions [47].

The pathways have been proposed based on identification of intermediates

In the
furfural/H2O/H2O2 system, furfural is readily converted into furfural peroxide (10)

and unlike other aldehydes, furfural is not hydrated in aqueous solution [54, 55].
Based on proposed mechanisms for oxidation of furan (11) in a H2O2/TS-1 system
[56, 57], pathway A has been suggested. The formation of 13 and subsequently, 17
from furan was rationalized by direct epoxidation of the furan ring forming short-
lived 12 which has not been experimentally confirmed [56].
Furan (11) can be
formed from furfural (1) by oxidation of the aldehyde to carboxylic acid (4) and
the subsequent release of formic acid. Intermediates of pathway A (4), has been
observed for furfural oxidation in basic organic media and in aqueous solution at
pH> 5[55].
Pathway B has been proposed by Alonso et al. for TS-1 catalyzed furfural oxidation
[14]. This pathway suggest that the furan ring is epoxidized directly as the first step
of the reaction. The rearrangement of 14 and subsequent Baeyer Villiger oxidation
yield 16. The ester is hydrolyzed forming 17 and formic acid. Based on experimental
results, it is suggested that the equilibrium between 17 and 18 is shifted towards 18
and that maleic acid formation occurs by oxidation of the compound. Badovskaya et
al. has argued that pathway B is implausible since the direct epoxidation of furfural
is unlikely, due to the lowered electron density compared to furan and the disruption
of the conjugated system [55].
Pathway C, proposed by Kul’nevich et al., is a widely accepted reaction mechanism
for the production of succinic acid over sulphonic acid resin catalysts [47, 48, 52].
Furfural is first converted via the Baeyer Villiger oxidation to 19 with furfural
peroxide (10) as an intermediate. 19 hydrolyses rapidly forming 20 and formic
acid. The lactones, 6 and 21, are in equillibrium with the tautomer 20 [54]. 21
is not stable under aqueous acidic condition and reacts readily to form 22.
22
undegoes keto-enol isomerization (23) and is oxidized to succinic acid (3).
6 is
more stable than 21 under the acidic conditions due to the conjugated system [54,
58]. It is proposed that pathway C also results in maleic acid formation. Whether
the formation occurs via oxidation of 6 to 17 or through oxidation and dehydration
of 20 to 18 (not depicted here), is still debated. It is however clear that coproduction
of maleic and succinic acid often occurs over sulphonic acid catalyst. The presence
of this tautomeric equilibrium between 6, 20 and 21 explains the coproduction
and why selectivity can be tuned by changing the H2O2/furfural molar ratio [14].
Malonic acid (24) is also thought to be a (by)product of this reaction pathway, but
how it is formed is unclear. Reaction pathway C was also proposed for pure Lewis
acid zeolite, Sn-beta, selectively producing succinic acid [21]. 21 was identified as
an intermediate by NMR. The affinity for producing succinic acid was explained by
the inability to activate H2O2 to form active peroxo sites on the catalyst [21].

2.3
Zeolites
Zeolites are microporous (<2 nm) crystalline (metal)silicates with three dimensional
frameworks. Zeolites consists of tetrahedral [TO4] units connected by corner sharing
oxygen atoms. Typically, zeolites are aluminosilicates, thus consisting of Si and Al T
atoms, however other T atoms include Ge, Ga and Ti. The tetrahedral [TO4] units
are the primary building blocks of the zeolite structure, see figure 2.2. Tetrahedrons
can be combined in different geometric shapes ie. rings, cubes and prisms yielding
different types of secondary structures with up to 16 T atoms. By combining the
secondary building blocks a three-dimensional structure is created. The many pos-
sible combinations of building blocks lead to over 240 different zeolite structures [59,
60]. Zeolites are characterized by well-defined structures, high surface areas, shape
selectivity and high thermal stability. In addition the chemical properties of zeolites
are easily tuned by post synthetic treatments. The unique properties of zeolites
makes the materials ideal for catalysis [59, 61].
Figure 2.2: The building blocks of zeolite structures, exemplified by the MFI structure
2.3.1
Hierarchical zeolites
One major constraint for zeolites as catalytic materials is mass transport limitations.
Due to the microporous structure, diffusion of reactants and products inside the
pores is limited. This results in poor utilization of the catalyst material since only
the outer shell of the zeolite is effectively used in the catalysis. In addition, the
microporous structure also limits applications within chemical transformations of
bulky molecules [59].
One solution to the mass transport limitations is hierarchical zeolites. They are
characterized by possessing more than one level of porosity, often micro- and meso-
porosity. Mesopores are defined by a pore size of 2-50 nm. In hierarchical zeolites
mesopores are interconnected with micropores while maintaining the catalytic prop-
erties and stability of conventional zeolites. By introduction of larger pores, there
is space in the zeolites for deposition of catalytically active phases ie. metals and
metal oxides [62].
Synthesis of hierarchical zeolites are categorized into two approaches; (i) bottom-
up, where a template is added in the zeolite synthesis and (ii) top-down, where meso-
pores are created by post synthetic treatments including desilication, dealumination
Page 11 of 74
==PAGE:24==
and dissolution-recrystallization [63]. With dissolution-recrystallization technique
it is also possible to synthesize hollow zeolites with mesoporosity in the remaining
shell. Due to the thin zeolite shells, the diffusion limitations are further reduced [63,
64]. Hollow zeolites are categorized as a subset of hierarchical zeolite structures.
2.3.2
Titanosilicate-1
The synthesis of titanosilicate, TS-1, reported in the 1980s, is regarded as a milestone
within the fields of zeolite materials and heterogeneous catalysis [65]. TS-1 is a
unique catalyst with high activity and selectivity for several oxidation reactions in
H2O2/H2O systems.
With TS-1 it is possible to catalyze epoxidation of olefins,
hydroxylation of phenols, ammoxidation of ketones as well as selective oxidations
of primary and secondary alcohols respectively to aldehydes and ketones (see figure
2.3). Oxidation reactions with hydrogen peroxide are often denoted green oxidations
since the only byproduct from H2O2 is water. In addition, the TS-1 catalyst allows
the reaction to run at mild conditions. This has led to several industrial applications
and the advancement of green oxidations [66].
TS-1 is a microporous zeolite with a framework consisting of [SiO4] units where
a little fraction of Si atoms is replaced by isomorphous substitution of Ti.
The
incorporation of Ti(IV) in the framework results in a redox active molecular sieve.
TS-1 is a unique catalyst for oxidation due to a combination of three properties (i)
the isolated tetrahedral Ti(IV) sites, (ii) the strong hydrophobic nature from the
silica framework and (iii) the microporous structure providing shape selectivity [67].
Applications in industry
The unique catalytic activity of TS-1 has led to some commercial applications, see
figure 2.3. The earliest industrial application of TS-1 was for the hydroxylation
of phenol to hydroquinone and catechol (reaction e) which lead to increased yields
compared to the iron(II) catalyst used previously. Using TS-1 and hydrogen peroxide
in a biphasic system yielded 20-30% phenol conversion with 90-95% selectivity at
80-100°C [68].
TS-1 is also used to produce cyclohexanone oxime, a precursor for ε-carprolactam,
by the ammoxidation (reaction c). First ammonia is oxidized to hydroxylamine in
with H2O2 in the presence of TS-1, and then the generated hydroxylamine species
reacts with cyclohexanone to form cyclohexane oxime. The reaction proceeds at 80-
90°C with nearly full conversion of cyclohexanone and a 98% yield of cyclohexanone
oxime [69].
Another example of TS-1 catalyst used in industry is the epoxidation of propylene
to produce propylene oxide (see reaction d). The HPPO (hydrogen peroxide propy-
lene oxide) process, which was commercialized in 2008, provides a technology for
propylene oxide production with a significantly smaller environmental impact than
conventional technologies. Compared to the non-catalytic production of propylene
oxide via chlorohydrin, the HPPO technology reduces energy usage by 35% and
wastewater generation by 70-80% [70]. Every year 1 million tonnes of propylene
oxide is produced by the HPPO processes, accounting for approximately 14% of the
total production [71]

Deactivation of the TS-1 catalyst in liquid phase oxidation reaction mainly occur by
coking and leaching. Coking of TS-1 is a reversible deactivation mechanism since the
catalyst can be regenerated by calcination without significant activity loss or change
of morphology [68]. Leaching of Ti species in TS-1 is an irreversible deactivation [15,

Framework of TS-1
The zeolite has an MFI type framework, known from zeolites such as ZSM-5.
The MFI framework, shown in figure 2.2, consists of pentasil units.
The three-
dimensional structure has a bidirectional pore system where straight channels are
interconnected with sinusoidal channels running perpendicular to the straight chan-
nels. The opening of the channels has a diameter of 5.1-5.6 ˚A and therefore MFI
type zeolites are categorized as medium pore zeolites [67].
The composition of TS-1 is defined as xTiO2 · (1 – x)SiO2, where x is between 0
and 0.025, corresponding to a Ti content of less than 2wt% [67, 73]. Three different
Ti species have been identified in the zeolite; tetrahedral and octahedral Ti(IV)
as well as anatase, TiO2. The tetrahedral Ti(IV) species, also denoted framework
titanium, are incorporated in the zeolite framework. Extra framework Ti species
includes amorphous octahedral Ti(IV) and crystalline TiO2. Although described as
tetrahedral the framework Ti(IV) species are not perfectly tetrahedral but rather
pseudo tetrahedral. Insertion of Ti in the MFI structure leads to an increased unit
cell due to the larger ionic radii of Ti4+ which also causes distortion of the TO4 units
[67].

Several studies indicate that all framework Ti may not be tetrahedrally coordinated.
Using various spectroscopic methods evidence of pentahedral [74] and mononuclear
octahedral Ti species [75, 76] have been found and linked to increased catalytic
activity. Spectroscopic investigations suggests that there several defective or non-

The role of defects in the TS-1 catalyzed
reaction is still discussed [67, 77, 78].
The unique catalytic activity of TS-1 is attributed to framework Ti while extra
framework Ti is considered inert. It is suggested that extra framework Ti promotes
homolytic side reactions, such as decomposition of hydrogen peroxide [73]. There
has been several attempts over the years to optimize the catalytic activity of TS-1,
by synthesizing TS-1 with a higher Ti content. It has proved difficult to incorpo-
rate more Ti in the framework without generating extraframework Ti. Fan et al.
found that lowering the pH by addition of (NH4)2CO3 to the starting gel, slowed
down the crystallization process matching it with the rate of incorporation of Ti in
the framework. With this method a Ti-rich TS-1 zeolite without extraframework
species was obtained. The higher activity of the Ti-rich TS-1 for various oxidations
was ascribed not only to the higher Ti content but also to the increased hydropho-
bicity [79]. Other parameters to optimize the catalytic performance of TS-1 includes
improving the distribution and thus accessibility of Ti atoms [80],
Active sites of TS-1
The isolated tetrahedral framework Ti(IV) ions of TS-1 is commonly regarded as
the active center for oxidation reactions. Due to the low coordination of Ti(IV) in
the framework it is able to expand its coordination sphere upon interaction with
H2O2, water or other solvents [81].
The structure of active species, generated in the TS/H2O2 system is still widely
debated. Several different species have been proposed, see figure 2.4. Hydroperoxo
and peroxo species are often proposed as active species but adsorbed species and
terminal oxo species may also play a role [67, 82]. A proposed epoxidation mech-
anism with TS-1 suggests that hydroperoxo sites are the active species. Although
the hydroperoxo species in figure 2.4 are depicted as bidentate, η2, monodentate, η1,
hydroperoxo species have also been proposed as active species [72, 83].
The yellow η2 peroxo species have been identified as the dominant species in
XAS and Raman spectroscopy of TS-1 in contact with H2O2/H2O. The spectro-
scopic investigations show that an equilibrium between η2 peroxo species and η1
hydroperoxo species exists.
In addition water is thought to be a key factor for
shifting the equilibrium [84].

3.1
Synthesis of catalysts
A schematic overview of the catalysts synthesis is shown in figure 3.1. The dissolu-
tion-recrystallization method was used as a post-synthetic method to introduce
mesopores into a commercial TS-1 zeolite. The dissolution-recrystallization method
is governed by the structure directing agent tetrapropylammonium hydroxide,
TPAOH. During the hydrothermal treatment TPA+ (0.84 nm) remains outside the
zeolite framework, since the ion is too big to enter the pores (0.55 nm). OH– ions
diffuse into the zeolite structure and dissolves the framework from the inside. The
diffusion of OH– ions, and thus the dissolution, is restricted by charge balance ef-
fects. Recrystallization of the dissolved species occurs, at the exterior surface of the
zeolite particles directed by TPA+ ions [18, 19].
The dissolution-recrystallization method was utilized in two different ways, with
impregnation of metal precursors after or before the hydrothermal treatment. In
the latter case, harsher synthesis conditions i.e., longer reaction times and higher
concentration of the structure directing agent, was used.

3.1.2
Synthesis of hierachical TS-1
A post-synthetic method, reported by Wang et al. was used to create hierarchical
TS-1, HTS-1 [19]. TS-1 (10 g) was mixed with TPAOH (0.15 M, 40 mL) and stirred
for 15 min. The following dissolution-recrystallization process was carried out in
an autoclave under autogenous pressure at 150 ℃for 24 h. The white powder was
collected by centrifugation, washed and dried overnight. The powder was calcined
at 550 ℃for 5h (6h ramp).
3.1.3
Metal oxides on HTS-1
Metal oxides were introduced into the pores of HTS-1 by incipient wetness impreg-
nation with a 1 wt% metal loading. In a typical synthesis, the metal acetate salt
was dissolved in 0.45 mL H2O. The solution was added slowly to a container with
0.7 g HTS-1 while the powder was stirred to ensure homogeneous distribution. The
powder was kept static for 18 h before drying for 24 h. The powder was calcined at
600 ℃for 5h (7h ramp). The catalysts are denoted MeO/HTS-1 where Me = Fe,
Co, Ni, Cu, Zn, Zr & Mn.
3.1.4
Nickel oxides encapsulated by HTS-1
The catalysts were synthesized using a method reported by Liu et al. for embedding
for Ni NPs in silicate-1 [85].
First, nickel was introduced into TS-1 by wetness
impregnation. An aqueous solution (6 mL) of nickel nitrates was added slowly to TS-
1 (3 g) while stirring. The mixture was stirred overnight so all water had evaporated.
The powder was added to an autoclave with TPAOH (0.3 M, 140 mL) and place in
an oven at 170 ℃for 72 h. The powder was collected by centrifugation, washed, and
dried overnight. The powder was calcined at 600 ℃for 5h (7h ramp). The catalyst
was synthesized with varying loading from 1-10wt%, denoted as xwt%NiO@HTS-1.
A blank synthesis, denoted as HollowTS-1, with no impregnation was also carried
out.

4.1
Effect of mesoporosity
Hierarchical TS-1, HTS-1, is synthesized by a post-synthetic dissolution-recrystal-
lization method. In the following TS-1 and hierarchical TS-1 are characterized and
the catalytic activity of the two materials is compared.
4.1.1
Characterization
X-ray diffraction patterns (figure 4.1) confirms that the zeolite framework is retained,
after the dissolution-recrystallization process yielding hierarchical TS-1, HTS-1. The
characteristic peaks of TS-1 at 2θ angles of 7.9◦, 8.8◦, 23.1◦, 23.9◦and 24.4◦are un-
changed after the post-synthetic treatment. Non-framework Ti species are charac-
terized by 2θ diffraction angles of 25.3◦and 27.3◦[86]. These peaks are not apparent
in the patterns of TS-1 and HTS-1 indicating a low concentration of extra framework
Ti. No obvious amorphous peaks were observed in the 20◦area, indicating that if
any amorphous species are formed during the post-synthetic treatment, they are of
low concentration. This is in accordance with the calculated crystallinity index for
HTS-1 and TS-1. After the treatment of TS-1, crystallinity decreased by less than
10% compared to untreated TS-1. The X-ray diffraction peaks of the two materials
have similar peak width (FWHM) indicating that the crystal size did not change
after the treatment.

N2 physisorption results confirmed the introduction of mesoporosity in HTS-1, see
table 4.1 and figure 4.2. After the post-synthetic treatment of TS-1 the mesoporous
volume in the material increased by 23%.
BET calculations show that the sur-
face area increased slightly after the post-synthetic treatment of TS-1. A summary
of additional structural information obtained from N2-physisorption is included in
appendix, table A.2.
Interestingly, the adsorption-desorption profile and the pore size distribution
(figure 4.2) are almost identical for TS-1 and HTS-1. They both exhibit a type
IV isotherm with a H4 hysteresis loop. A large initial uptake of N2 at p/p0=0.01
shows large micropore filling for both materials. The abrupt closing of the hysteresis
loop at p/p0=0.45 due to the tensile strength effect, indicates that the mesopores
are constricted possibly because the mesopores are accessed via entrance pores of
smaller diameter [87, 88].
The type IV isotherm has been observed for mesoporous zeolites and was ex-
pected for HTS-1 [88]. In contrast it was expected that the isotherm of microporous
TS-1 would exhibit less characteristics of mesoporosity, i.e., a smaller slope after
p/p0=0.1 or more narrow or non-existent hysteresis loop. Others report that TS-1
has a type I[16, 89] or type IV isotherm showing small hysteresis loops[19]. The
mesoporosity of TS-1 could be attributed to interparticle voids arising from agglom-
eration of nanoparticles.

XPS analysis of the materials, is used to examine the titanium species, see figure 4.3.
The Ti 2p spectra is characterized by spin orbit coupling of titanium, giving rise to
two peaks for 2p1/2 and 2p2/3 separated by 6 eV. The most intense peak, 2p2/3, is
used for characterization of the titanium species. The Ti 2p2/3 peak has a binding
energy of 460.0 eV, which is characteristic of tetrahedral framework Ti(IV). TS-1
showed no peak at 458.0±0.4 eV which is attributed octahedral extra-framework
Ti(IV) [90, 91]. Peak deconvolution of Ti 2p signals of HTS-1 show that 24% Ti
species are extra framework species, see table 4.1. The formation of extra-framework
titanium during the dissolution-recrystallization process is also reported in literature
[16, 19, 20]. The Ti/Si molar ratio determined by XPS is significantly higher than
the ratio determined by XRF (see table A.4), indicating that the surface of the
zeolite contains less titanium than the bulk [80].
In the O1s region (see figure A.9) both materials show a characteristic peak
at 533.4±0.2eV, which is attributed to Si-O species. In addition, a shoulder peak
at ∼530eV is present which can be attributed to Si-O-Ti species and TiO2.
In
the Si 2p region, (see figure A.10) a major peak, attributed to SiO2 is observed at
104.7±0.1eV, any contributions from Si-O-Ti species is masked by this intense peak
[91].

1 and HTS-1. After 1 h the conversion of furfural is 69% and 68% for TS-1 and
HTS-1 respectively. The similar conversion rates as well as the similar maleic acid
yields regardless of the reaction conditions, indicate that the diffusion rate has not
been significantly improved by using hierarchical TS-1. XRD patterns indicate that
the crystal size has not changed and thus the surface diffusion barrier has not been
affected [18].
One probable explanation is that the mesoporosity formed by the post-synthetic
treatment are voids within the catalyst connected to the catalyst surface by smaller
entrance pores. These ink-bottle pores, indicated by the shape of the N2 isotherm,
could explain why the catalytic activity of zeolite is not significantly improved by
the post-synthetic treatment. Another explanation is that while the treatment in-
troduces mesopores into the material, it also generates extra framework Ti species.
The removal of catalytically active framework Ti sites could counteract any positive
effects of introducing mesopores. The maleic acid yields obtained in these experi-
ments are somewhat lower than results reported in literature for TS-1 and HTS-1
catalyst (see table 2.1). These differences are mainly explained by the use of differ-
ent catalyst loadings as well as different reactor set-ups. Other factors like particle
size and content of impurities also influence the catalytic activity of TS-1[67, 94].

X-ray diffraction of the metal oxide impregnated HTS-1 confirmed that the MFI

4.3.3
Mechanistic study
The furfural oxidation is studied in detail by an in-situ 13C-NMR time series study.
NiO/HTS-1 is used as the catalyst at conditions similar to the conditions where the
highest maleic acid yield of 58% has been obtained. The time series is shown in
figure 4.14 and a possible reaction pathway derived from the time series is seen in
scheme 4.1.
Figure 4.14: Time series of 13C-NMR of furfural oxidation with NiO/HTS-1 catalyst at
50℃. Conditions: 0.12 mmol furfural, 0.6 mg NiO/HTS-1, 300µL D2O and 180µL 30%
H2O2, corresponding to H2O2/FF ratio of 15
Scheme 4.1: Reaction pathway for furfural oxidation catalyzed by NiO/HTS-1
Initially two species are present in the reaction mixture, furfural (1) and furfural
peroxide (2). As time progresses the intensity of the peaks for (1) and (2) decreases
as the species are converted. The formation of furfural peroxide has been observed as
intermediate in previous experiments with short reaction times. A sample, without
catalyst, subjected to the same conditions, also shown formation of furfural peroxide,
indicating that the furfural peroxide formation is autocatalyzed.
Page 37 of 74
==PAGE:50==
The time series shows the formation of furan-2-yl formate (3), which is formed by
a Baeyer-Villiger oxidation of furfural peroxide (2). The low intensity peaks of (3)
indicates that the intermediate is continuously and quickly converted. Two other
intermediates, 5-hydroxy-furan2(5H)-one (4) and furan-2(3H)-one (6) is identified
from the NMR experiment. Furan-2-yl formate (3) will likely hydrolyze to form
formic acid (8) and 2-hydroxyfuran. 2-hydroxyfuran was not detected in 13C-NMR,
presumably since it readily undergoes oxidation to form 5-hydroxy-furan2(5H)-one
(4) which is oxidized yielding maleic acid (5). 2-hydroxyfuran could also undergo
tautomerization to form furan-2(3H)-one (6). It has been proposed that (6) can
undergo keto-enol isomerization followed by oxidation to yield succinic acid (7).
While 5-hydroxy-furan2(5H)-one (4) is regarded as an intermediate, it does not
disappear completely after the reaction time of 40 hours, as is the case for (3) and
(6), indicating that the oxidation of 5HFO to maleic acid (5) is slow.
The proposed reaction pathway indicates that the branching point of the re-
action, with the formation of 2-hydroxyfuran, is key to controlling the selectivity.
Besides maleic and succinic acid, malic acid (9) was also formed in the NMR study.
Malic acid can be derived from maleic acid via isomerization to fumaric acid followed
by hydration, see scheme 2.3. However, no peaks of fumaric acid were identified in
the pseudo 2D NMR study. Alternatively, malic acid could be formed directly via
another intermediate in the furfural oxidation.
Assignment of peaks is made difficult by drifting of the chemical shifts during the
reaction. This is a result of the pH changing during the reaction due to formation of
acids, especially formic acid. The pH of the reaction mixture changes from neutral
to a pH of 1.6-2 at the end of the reaction. Since pH of the reaction media passes
through pKa points of the acids, the chemical shifts are subject to change due to
gradual protonation.
The acidity of the reaction seems to play a vital role and formic acid is regarded
by some as a co-catalyst which enhances oxidation [21, 45, 99]. A catalytic test
conducted in a buffer solution showed reduced furfural conversion and product yields,
indicating that the reaction is partly driven by the acidic environment (see table A.6,
entry 19).
Efforts were made to design the experiment in a way that closely mimics the reaction
conditions, such as catalyst loading and furfural concentration, used in the catalytic
testing set-up. The product distribution obtained in the time series experiment is
significantly different than the similar test conducted in ACE pressure tubes (see
table A.6, entry 67). The time series experiment also shows slower conversion of
furfural than in ACE pressure tubes.
Stirring of the reaction mixture is one major difference between the NMR study
and the catalytic testing set up. Mechanical spinning of the NMR tube was not
possible and thus mixing in the reaction mixture was not ideal. However, gas de-
veloped from H2O2 decomposition might assist in mixing and thus preventing the
catalyst from settling at the bottom of the tube. Furfural is not miscible with the
water phase and thus stirring is an important factor in the reaction.
The work of Palai et al.
suggests that maleic acid is formed when a Lewis
catalyst, such as TS-1, which can activate H202 is used. In contrast when succinic
acid is formed activation of H2O2 on the catalyst surface does not occur [21]. This
Page 38 of 74
==PAGE:51==
could partly explain why succinic acid formation is observed in the NMR study, but
not in the catalytic test of NiO/HTS-1 in ACE tubes. Since mixing is not ideal in
NMR, it is likely that some furfural conversion occurs away from the catalyst surface
and thus succinic acid formation is favored.
Another explanation for the different product distribution and slower conversion
is the lack of pressure generated in the NMR tube compared to the ACE pressure
tube. Due to the pressure generated from decomposition of H2O2, an NMR tube cap
modified with holes was used for the experiment. The previously described addition
test (see section 4.1.2) indicated that the pressure generated within the reaction
vessel might influence the reaction and the product distribution.
Based on in-situ 13C-NMR study over time, it was possible to elucidate a possible
reaction pathway for furfural oxidation over NiO/HTS-1. The intermediates ob-
served in NMR, has previously been proposed in reaction pathways published in
the literature, see scheme 2.1. No evidence of the proposed pathway A with furan
as an intermediate, or evidence of epoxidation of the furan ring (pathway B) has
been observed. From the NMR study, the TS-1 catalyzed furfural oxidation largely
follows pathway C, which has been proposed for sulphonic acid catalysts.

4.4
Encapsulated catalysts
In an effort to further optimize the nickel catalyst and explore the effects of metal
loading, while achieving good metal oxide dispersion a different synthetic approach
was used. By impregnating TS-1 with a nickel precursor before the dissolution-
recrystallization process, HTS-1 catalysts with encapsulated NiO nanoparticles were
obtained. Catalyst with nickel loading of 1-10wt% was synthesized. The previous
synthesis of HTS-1 material yielded a moderate increase in mesoporosity, which
did not significantly improve diffusion limitations. Therefore, a concentration of
TPAOH and a longer synthesis time was used for synthesis of encapsulated catalyst,
to dissolve the framework further.
4.4.1
Characterization
X-ray diffraction patterns (see figure 4.15) of the synthesized catalysts show that the
MFI zeolite structure is preserved. Diffraction peaks have shifted slightly towards
lower 2θ values, indicating unit cell enlargement due to incorporation of NiO in the
framework [85]. The crystallinity of the materials compared to TS-1 is reduced by
15-20% as a result of the harsher conditions used for the dissolution-recrystallization
process. Despite the harsh conditions, no peaks which can be attributed to extra
framework anatase species are observed.
In addition, no diffraction peaks at 37.2° and 43.3° which are attributed to NiO
phases are observed in XRD (JCPDS no.
00-043-0664).

the metal loading from 1 to 10 wt%, no peaks attributed to NiO are observed.

show no evidence of phyllosilicate formation with none of the characteristic peaks
at 2θ values of 19.5, 33.7 and 60.9 present (JCPDS no. 00-043-0664).

figure 4.22). The two peaks at 23.1° and 23.3° has merged into one diffraction peak
for the spent catalysts. In addition, in the XRD of spent HTS-1 a diffraction peak
at 13.2° has disappeared. However, XRD analysis shows that the zeolite framework
of spent catalysts is preserved to a great extent.
The Si/Ti mol ratio estimated by XPS has significantly increased after 24h of
reaction for both HollowTS-1 and HTS-1, indicating that Ti species have leached
from the surface of the catalysts (see table A.4). The XPS spectra of Ti 2p, show
that the 2p3/2 peak of the spent catalyst have shifted towards higher binding energies
compared to the fresh catalysts (see figure A.16). Deconvolution of the Ti 2p3/2 peak
show that the relative amount of octahedral, extra framework Ti have decreased
compared to the fresh catalysts.
This indicates that the Ti leaching during the
reaction is primarily from extra framework Ti species, which is consistent with other
reports [14, 15].
In conclusion, the synthesis of nickel oxides encapsulated in HTS-1 was successful,
as evident from XRD and TEM analysis. The synthesized catalysts exhibited a
high surface area and mesoporous volume. The harsh dissolution-recrystallization
conditions resulted in a high fraction of Ti species outside the zeolite framework, as
determined by XPS. The lowered activity of the encapsulated catalysts compared
to the previously described catalysts, was largely attributed to the lower fraction
of Ti(IV) within the zeolite framework. XRD analysis of spent catalysts revealed
that the zeolite framework was largely retained. XPS analysis showed evidence of
leaching of Ti species, mainly of the extra framework type. The highest maleic acid
yield (19%) was reached with the nickel free HollowTS-1 catalyst. Introduction of
nickel oxides promoted succinic acid production. Generally, the nickel oxide con-
taining encapsulated catalyst gave a broader product distribution. Any correlation
between nickel oxide loading and catalytic activity could not be elucidated due to
the overshadowing effect of extra framework species on the catalytic performance.

5
Conclusion
In this project furfural oxidation with hydrogen peroxide, H2O2, as the oxidant over
TS-1 based catalysts was examined. The TS-1 zeolite has a unique ability to catalyze
oxidation reactions with H2O2, due to the active tetrahedral Ti(IV) framework sites,
hydrophobicity and shape selectivity. However, the major drawback of the catalyst
is diffusion limitations due to the microporous zeolite structure. The goal of the
project was to enhance the activity of the TS-1 catalyst by introducing mesopores
to alleviate diffusion limitations and explore the promotional effects of introducing
metal oxides species into the pores of TS-1.
The TS-1 zeolite was modified by a post-synthetic dissolution-recrystallization met-
hod to enhance mesoporosity. The treatment resulted in a modest increase of meso-
pore volume and the formation of extra framework titanium species. The hierar-
chical TS-1, HTS-1, retained the MFI zeolite structure and the acidity of TS-1.
Surprisingly, the introduction of mesopores did not improve the catalytic activity
significantly. This is attributed to the fact that diffusion within the zeolite is still
limited since mesopores are accessed through smaller entrance pores. Another ef-
fect which could be counteracting any positive effects of increased mesoporosity, is
the removal of framework Ti species during the dissolution-recrystallization process.
Catalytic activity tests of TS-1 and HTS-1 were conducted with varying reaction
time and H2O2/furfural ratios. The experiments showed that conversion of the in-
termediate 5-hydroxy-furan2(5H)-one to maleic acid was slow and thus low maleic
acid yields of up 20% were obtained after 8 hours of reaction. Additionally, a clear
correlation between increasing H2O2/furfural ratios and maleic acid yield improve-
ments was observed. It was attempted to boost utilization of H2O2 by adding the
additional H2O2 during the reaction, however this did not result in improved maleic
acid yields.
Metal oxides were introduced into the HTS-1 catalysts in an effort to enhance cat-
alytic activity, for example by providing adsorption sites for furfural. Oxides of zir-
conium, manganese, cobalt, iron, nickel, copper and zinc were introduced in HTS-1
by incipient wetness impregnation. This caused a change in the acidic and basic
properties of HTS-1 while the structure and porosity were preserved.
Catalytic
testing revealed that introducing iron oxides promoted furfural conversion but re-
sulted in a wide product distribution due to radicals generated by Fenton chemistry.
HTS-1 materials with copper, cobalt and manganese oxide nanoparticles showed
lower maleic acid yield than HTS-1. Zirconium, zinc and nickel oxide containing
catalyst showed lower conversion rates, but similar or higher maleic acid yields com-
pared to results for HTS-1. Based on several catalytic tests it was determined that
NiO/HTS-1 was the most promising catalyst, as it under several different conditions
Page 49 of 74
==PAGE:62==
showed higher maleic acid yields than TS-1 and HTS-1. Additional characterization
of NiO/HTS-1 by XPS and TEM, yielded no obvious reason why the addition of
nickel oxide nanoparticles improved the catalytic activity of the HTS-1 material.
In a in-situ 13C-NMR study of furfural oxidation over NiO/HTS-1, furan-2-yl for-
mate, furan-2(3H)-one and 5-hydroxy-furan2(5H)-one was identified as intermedi-
ates. Based on the time series experiments a reaction pathway was proposed. These
results were in agreement with one of the mechanisms proposed in literature. The ex-
periment gave insight into how the maleic and succinic acid production are coupled,
however the pathways for malic and malonic acid production was not determined.
Another synthetic strategy was employed to further enhance mesoporosity, and
achieve high dispersion of nickel species when increasing the metal loading. The
encapsulated catalysts had a significantly higher mesoporous volume than TS-1 and
large intraparticle voids, as evidenced by TEM images. XPS analysis of the catalysts
showed that most Ti species were no longer incorporated in the zeolite network. The
XPS analysis also showed a higher-than-expected binding energy of Ni, indicating
that nickel species are incorporated in the zeolite framework. The low amount of
framework Ti species resulted in significantly reduced conversion rates as well as low
product yields. Analysis of the spent catalyst showed that the structure was retained
after testing, but XPS analysis revealed leaching of Ti species from the catalyst sur-
face. No conclusion about the effects of Ni metal loading could be drawn, since
extra framework Ti species was the dominant cause for the low catalytic activity,
regardless of Ni content.
In conclusion, introducing mesopores by using the structure directing agent TPAOH,
yielded restricted mesopores. The catalytic activity results indicate that diffusion
was not significantly improved after the post syntehtic modification of TS-1. In-
troduction of metal oxides changed the acidity and the catalytic activity of HTS-1.
The highest maleic acid yield of 58% was obtained using the NiO/HTS-1 catalyst
at 50℃for 24 h. An in-situ 13C-NMR study of furfural oxidation over NiO/HTS-1
confirmed the presence of intermediates suggested in the literature.

6
Future work
In this project the introduction of nickel oxides on HTS-1, improved the catalytic
activity of the zeolite. However, the reason why NiO/HTS-1 improved maleic acid
yield could not be elucidated by the chosen characterization methods. Any creation
of adsorption sites by introducing nickel in the catalyst could be examined by FTIR.
Acidity is an important parameter in furfural oxidation, and thus distinction of Lewis
and Brønsted acidic sites by pyridine-TPD could also give valuable information
about the catalyst.
Diffusion limitations of TS-1 could be addressed by applying other synthetic strate-
gies for creating mesopores [18, 64]. Using another structure directing agent, for
example CTAB, could also help to avoid creating ink bottle mesopores, which was
observed when using TPAOH [107]. Another approach to increasing diffusion in the
catalyst is the synthesis of layered or pillared TS-1 materials [108].
The activity of the TS-1 catalyst for furfural oxidation could also be optimized
by increasing the number of active sites. Several modified synthesis methods for
increasing the content of framework Ti species have been reported [76, 79, 86].
Further study of the mechanisms of furfural oxidation could be beneficial for better
catalyst design. Regulation of the interconnected maleic and succinic acid pathways
is key to achieving high product selectivity.
The pathways for formation of the
byproducts malic and malonic acid were not elucidated in this project. Malic acid
could be formed either via maleic acid or directly in furfural oxidation. Studying
the transformation of maleic acid under the acidic and oxidizing conditions, similar
to the reaction mixture, could help determine how malic acid is formed.
