Abstract 
 
Phosphorus (P) is a necessary nutrient for all life and is supplied as inorganic P from a few 
countries, mainly Morocco with estimated >70 % of global P reserves. Research indicate that the 
inorganic P reserves will be extinguished within a few decades to 100 years, although some 
scientists disagree (Rosemarin, 2016). The aim of this report was to investigate the potential of 
using electrodialysis (ED) for extracting P from waste water sludge, which can alleviate the 
problem of supplying P and provide a more soluble P-product for the agriculture. Waste water 
sludge was provided by Lynetten waste water treatment plant (WWTP), Copenhagen. The 
investigations firstly determined if P could be released from the microbes that accumulate it 
(Phosphorus Accumulating Organisms, PAOs) by addition of a Volatile Fatty Acid (acetate) and 
curing at anaerobic conditions. Afterwards, the P solution (liquid phase of sludge containing P) was 
subjected to a 3-compartment ED cell with altering parameters; current (10-100 mA), 
filtered/unfiltered P solution and duration (hours). 7 experiments were conducted on 350 mL P 
solution (30-80 mgP/L) with 500 mL anolyte and catholyte (0.01 M NaNO3, pH~2) on each side of 
the P solution.  
 
The results of the release to the liquid phase were successful of a ~38-39 % release after 2-8 hours 
at 4.4-7.7 g acetate/gP. The ED experiments were successful at all currents applied in terms of 
recovery, removal ratio (removal of P from P solution) and transfer to the anolyte. The highest 
removal ratios (>96.7 %) were obtained at currents 10-25 mA, which corresponded to energy 
consumptions 16-57 kWh/m3. These experiments had a duration of 20-49 hours. The transfer of P to 
the anolyte compared to end content was >95 % for five out of seven experiments, at 100, 25 and 10 
mA. Three experiments concentrated P in a smaller volume, one of 0.40 % and two of 0.10 % of 
original volume. The concentration factors obtained were 2.0-3.9. The lowest energy consumption 
obtained was 3.1 kWh/m3 with a concentration factor of 3.9 and a transfer to the anolyte of 36.5 %, 
with a recovery of 180 %, that presumably was due to unclean pumps and tubes.  
 
It could be concluded that acetate was efficient in facilitating a dissolvement of P from the sludge to 
the liquid phase and that subjection of ED to this P solution was able to remove 100 % and achieve 
a maximum transfer of 96.8 % to the anolyte. Implementation at a WWTP will not need a 100 % 
recovery but energy efficiency and stable conditions, which presumably will be a faster process 
than the cell experiments. The membranes became very dark during the concentration experiments, 
indicating a need for investigation of efficiency of a stack of membranes instead of cell 
experiments, which also would enhance the energy efficiency. The results have documented 
feasible fast and efficient electrodialytic extraction of phosphorus from waste water sludge.

Sammenfatning 
 
Fosfor (P) er et nødvendigt næringsstof for alle planter og levende organismer. Den nuværende 
forsyning stammer fra minedrift af uorganisk P fra nogle få lande, hovedsageligt Marokko med 
anslået >70 % af de globale P reserver. Forskning tyder på, at reserverne vil løbe tør inden for få 
årtier og op til 100 år, selvom der hersker uenighed (Rosemarin, 2016). Formålet med denne rapport 
var at undersøge potentialet for at bruge elektrodialyse (ED) til at udvinde P fra spildevandsslam, 
hvilket kan afhjælpe forsyningsproblemet samt skaffe et mere lettilgængeligt P-produkt til 
landbruget. Spildevandsslammet blev stillet til rådighed af Lynetten spildevandsrensningsanlæg, 
København. Den første del af opgaven undersøgte om P kunne frigives fra mikroberne, som 
akkumulerer P (fosforakkumulerende organismer, PAOer) ved tilsætning af let optageligt organisk 
materiale (en flygtig fedtsyre, acetat) og påføring af anaerobe betingelser. Derefter blev P-
opløsningen (flydende fase af slam indeholdende P) benyttet i en tredelt elektrodialytisk celle med 
parametre; strømstyrke (10-100 mA), filtreret/ufiltreret P opløsning samt varighed i timer. 7 
eksperimenter blev udført på 350 ml P opløsning (30-80 mgP/L) med 500 ml anolyt og katolyt 
(0,01 M NaNO3, pH~2) på hver side af P-opløsningen.  
 
Resultatet af frigivelsen til væskefasen var vellykket med en overførsel på 38-39 % efter 2-8 timer 
ved 4,4-7,7 g acetat/gP. ED-eksperimenterne var vellykkede ved alle de undersøgte strømstyrker i 
forhold til genfindingsprocent, procentvis fjernelse P fra P-opløsning og overførsel til anolytten. De 
højeste procentvise fjernelser (>96,7 %) blev opnået ved strømstyrker 10-25 mA, hvilket svarede til 
et energiforbrug på 16-57 kWh/m3. Disse eksperimenter havde en varighed på 20-49 timer. 
Overførslen af P til anolytten sammenlignet med slutindholdet var >95 % for fem ud af syv forsøg 
ved 100, 25 og 10 mA. Tre eksperimenter opkoncentrerede P i et mindre volumen, et på 0,40 % og 
to på 0,10 % af det oprindelige volumen. De opnåede koncentrationsfaktorer (KF) var 2,0-3,9. Det 
laveste energiforbrug, der blev opnået, var 3,1 kWh/m3 med en KF på 3,9 og en overførsel til 
anolytten på 36,5 %, dog var genfindingsprocenten på 180 %, hvilket formodentligt var på grund af 
urene pumper og rør.  
 
Det kunne konkluderes, at acetat var effektivt til at facilitere en overførsel af P fra slammet til 
væskefasen, samt at elektrodialytisk behandling af denne P-opløsning var i stand til at fjerne 100 % 
og opnå en maksimal overførsel på 96,8 % til anolytten. Ved implementering på et rensningsanlæg 
er det mere ønskværdigt at have stabile forhold og energieffektivitet, end at fjerne 100 %, hvilket 
ligeledes formentligt ville være en hurtigere proces. Membranerne blev meget mørke under 
opkoncentreringsforsøgene, hvilket indikerer et behov for undersøgelse af effektiviteten af en stak 
af membraner i stedet for celleforsøg, som også ville forbedre energieffektiviteten. Resultaterne har 
dokumenteret muligheden for hurtig og effektiv elektrodialytisk ekstraktion af fosfor fra 
spildevandsslam.

1 Introduction 
 
Phosphorus is necessary as a nutrient for humans as well as natural processes, and is used 
excessively on farmland (FAO, 2004; Jensen et al., 2015). For many years the norm has been to 
extract phosphorus from mining. Research indicate that the phosphorus extraction will peak within 
a few decades to ~100 years, and that the phosphorus resources will decrease in the following 
centuries, others however disagree that there will ever be a phosphorus peak (Van Kauwenbergh, 
2010; Schröder et al., 2010; Oberender et al., 2013; Rosemarin, 2016). Estimations made by 
Rosemarin (2016) indicate that within a few decades Morocco will become the main exporter of 
phosphorus, and that the phosphorus will contain much higher amounts of cadmium. This will 
constitute a problem for the countries that do not allow the high content of cadmium in their 
fertilizer (Jørgensen & The ecological council, 2018).  
 
The extraction of phosphorus from waste water has in many years had the primary aim to reduce the 
nutrient load to the environment and the consequent issues with eutrophication in aquatic 
ecosystems. The focus has not been on extracting phosphorus as a resource from the waste water. 
Therefore, the current technology overall relies on binding phosphorus with precipitation agents, 
which consequently makes it hardly soluble and unavailable for plants if placed on farmland 
(Jensen et al., 2015). Focus on using biological removal has increased in the later years, which 
means that phosphorus can be accumulated and removed in sludge without use of precipitation 
agents. The phosphorus enriched sludge can possibly substitute the mined phosphorus; however, 
there is still a conception of sludge being too contaminated to be placed directly on farmland  
(Jensen et al., 2015). This may however be due to extreme precaution as studies have shown that the 
current used fertilizers (inorganic phosphorus and manure from swine and cattle) contain more 
organic pollutants and heavy metals than waste water sludge, with zinc and copper in 
concentrations that should be landfilled (Magid, 2018; Eriksen et al., 2009; Hansen, 2016; Bak et 
al., 2015). In some countries the use of sludge on farmlands is completely forbidden, and in EU it is 
not allowed in ecological farming, though manure from conventional farming, containing high 
amounts of organic pollutants, is allowed (Dijk, 2014; Bresters et al., 2007; Terkelsen, 2016; 
Magid, 2018). Therefore, this project set out to evaluate the potential of using electrodialysis (ED) 
as the technological method to extract and concentrate phosphorus from sludge without extracting 
the contaminants, ensuring the most soluble state for the highest availability if placed on farmland. 
Another advantage is that the waste water treatment plants (WWTP) will be rid of the precipitated 
phosphorus compound struvite which clogs their pipes and causes extra costs.

2 Aim 
 
The aim of this report is to investigate the potential for electrodialytic recovery of phosphorus from 
waste water sludge, which could provide a phosphorus product free from contaminants and easily 
soluble to use as nutrient in agriculture. It is assumed that it is too expensive to treat all the inlet 
waste water with ED due to low phosphorus concentrations. This project will therefore investigate 
phosphorus recovery of a small part of the waste water, the concentrated secondary sludge, to make 
the process technologically and economically efficient. This project is therefore dependent on using 
sludge from a waste water treatment plant that has an advanced biological phosphorus recovery 
system; an Enhanced Biological Phosphorus Recovery configuration.  
 
The project is divided into three main parts. The first part consists of determining the potential of 
bringing phosphorus into solution from waste water sludge by addition of sodium acetate, which 
facilitates the release of phosphorus from the microbes to the sludge. This phase ensures a more 
efficient process in part II. The second part consists of evaluating the potential of transferring 
dissolved phosphorus from part I by ED. The potential will be evaluated by the parameters current, 
duration, energy consumption and fouling on membranes. Part III evaluates concentration of 
phosphorus into a smaller volume using ED.  
 
2.1 Key investigations 
 
1) How can most phosphorus be dissolved into the water phase available for electrodialytic 
concentration, and what is the maximum achieved availability? 
2) How should the key parameters of ED be managed to extract most phosphorus? 
3) How effectively can phosphorus be concentrated? 
 
The project will add to the technical solutions available for mitigating the eutrophication of Danish 
streams, lakes and coastal waters, the rising challenge with providing phosphorus for the Danish 
agriculture and the nuisances and extra costs for removal of struvite at the Danish waste water 
treatment plants.

3 Background 
 
Phosphorus can be acquired from different sources, whereof the most prevalent are mining of 
inorganic compounds and reuse of organic compounds from waste or manure. As an inorganic 
compound phosphorus is mined as phosphorites, also called rock phosphate (RP), which primarily 
comes from sedimentary deposits with high amounts of phosphate minerals (Jasinski & U.S. 
Geological Survey, 2016). Phosphate minerals are the combination of phosphate/orthophosphate, 
(PO43-), and for example calcium, chlorine or fluorine, e.g. fluorapatite (Ca5(PO4)3F). Since 
phosphate is very reactive it does not exist as a free ion but instead binds with cations to balance its 
negative charge. An orthophosphate is the ion of the phosphoric acid, and both can be seen in 
Figure 3.1. Orthophosphate’s anions H2PO4- and HPO42- are the water-soluble ions found in soils.  
 
Orthophosphoric acid (Wikipedia, 2017) 
Orthophosphate (phosphate) (Socratic, 2016) 
Figure 3.1: The structure of orthophosphoric acid and phosphate.  
 
The organic compounds of phosphorus (organophosphates) are called esters of phosphoric acids, 
and consist of long chains. Figure 3.2 illustrates this by R’s indicating attached alkyl groups instead 
of -H (Orenda Technologies, 2017). In general organophosphates must be broken down into 
orthophosphates before they can become available for plants, because the organic compounds 
consist of large molecules. The breakdown is done by microbes and is highly dependent on 
temperature and moisture. The organophosphates origins from manure or waste water 
(SpectrumAnalytic, n.d.; Jensen et al., 2015).

In the following sections it will be described why phosphorus is important, why new extraction 
methods are needed, whether waste water sludge can substitute the current sources and in that case 
how to extract it from the sludge.

3.1 Importance of phosphorus   
 
Phosphorus is necessary for both plants and humans. For animals and humans phosphorus is needed 
to maintain normal metabolic processes, and humans should consume 600-900 mg/day (FAO, 2004; 
Møller et al., 2009). Phosphorus is used in several industries and while the fertilization industry 
accounts for the majority of the use only 10 % is used in other industries, e.g. laundry detergents 
and toothpaste (FAO, 2004; Bostrup et al., 2018). For plants it is necessary together with other key 
nutrients such as nitrogen and potassium. Plants require phosphorus for growth, respiration and 
photosynthesis (FAO, 2004). Plants can only take up inorganic phosphorus, and organic phosphorus 
needs to be mineralized before it can be utilized (Pagliari et al., 2017). The cycle of phosphorus can 
be seen on Figure 3.3. The natural cycle has been broken for many years since the input of mineral 
fertilizers has increased and caused unnecessary runoff and accumulation instead of being taken up 
by the plants.  
Figure 3.3: Cycle of phosphorus, edited from: Wikipedia (2018b). 
 
In order to produce food to meet the societal demands fertilization of the soil is needed, which is 
why 90 % of the mined phosphorus is consumed by the fertilization industry (FAO, 2004). Plant 
roots take up phosphorus as either the primary or secondary orthophosphate anion (H2PO4- and 
HPO42-, respectively) dependent on pH. The phosphate exists primarily as the HPO42- anion at 
pH>7, where H2PO4- is the dominant anion at pH<7 and H3PO4 is dominant at pH<2. Difficulties 
with addition of phosphorus on farmland can be to ensure continuous availability for the plants 
during the entire growth cycle as well as ensuring correct timing of availability (FAO, 2004). The 
washout of phosphorus is more prevalent when it originates from organophosphates. The sludge is 
==PAGE:13==
Page 13 of 63 
 
applied in the growth season but is mineralized after the growth season. The nutrients are not 
utilized and are washed out of the oversaturated soil (SpectrumAnalytic, n.d.; Jensen et al., 2015).  
 
Often lots of the nutrients added to the farmland in Denmark are washed away and end up in 
streams, lakes or coastal waters where they cause eutrophication (Madsen, 2018b). Eutrophication 
is the bloom of algae due to excess of nutrients which causes lack of sunlight for the deep aquatic 
vegetation. Furthermore, the bacterial degradation of the algae can cause oxygen depletion, which 
leads to less aquatic life as some species migrate away and the ones that cannot migrate perhaps die 
(Hunding, 2018).  
 
3.2 Phosphorus; a scarce resource 
 
3.2.1 Inorganic phosphorus resources 
 
It has been estimated that the available and known reserves of RP vary from 15,000 Mt to above 
1,000,000 Mt. The numbers are larger for the estimated but unknown amounts (resources) where it 
has been estimated that there exist between 91,000 Mt to over 1,000,000 Mt (Van Kauwenbergh, 
2010). The estimates are rough and there is great disagreement to whether there will be a peak of 
phosphorus production soon, or whether it will even occur or has already occurred. Some state that 
the production of phosphorus will peak around 2030-2040 and unavoidably decrease afterwards due 
to lack of phosphorus (Van Kauwenbergh, 2010; Schröder et al., 2010; Rosemarin, 2016).  
 
The mining of RP has risen since 2008 from 160 Mt to 223 Mt in 2015. The RP mined was divided 
between United States of America (read: 2008; 2015) (18.7 %; 12.4 %), Morocco (15.5 %; 13.5 
%), China (31.5 %; 44.8 %), Russia (6.4 %; 5.6 %) and others (27.9 %; 23.7 %) (Van 
Kauwenbergh, 2010; Jasinski & U.S. Geological Survey, 2016; Rosemarin, 2016; Oberender et al., 
2013). Most of the countries use the phosphorus they mine themselves and do not export much, e.g. 
China that does not export very much, therefore, Morocco is the main supplier on the world market. 
It has also been estimated that Morocco contains 70 % of the phosphorus reserves of the world, 
which is likely to make them the most influential party on the market as they will become the single 
supplier. This is based on an annual growth of 2.5 % by the Food and Agriculture Organization of 
the United Nations (FAO) (Rosemarin, 2016; Oberender et al., 2013). Another aspect is the 
worsening quality of the RP due to increasing content of the heavy metal cadmium (Jørgensen & 
The ecological council, 2018). If no alternative phosphorus source is found the quality criteria for 
cadmium in fertilizer must be adapted to the new cadmium concentrations for agriculture to 
continue.   
 
The amount of phosphate in ores varies a lot and is often expressed as phosphorus pentoxide (P2O5). 
Before exporting, the phosphate is often enriched to around 30 % P2O5 by use of different 
separation techniques. P2O5 is a uniform description of different phosphate minerals, and it is not 
the actual mineral being mined (SpectrumAnalytic, n.d.). Inorganic phosphorus can be easily 
dissolved and is suitable for application directly on farmland (FAO, 2004).

3.2.2 Alternative phosphorus resources  
 
Phosphorus exists in large quantities in manure from farming and in smaller amounts in waste water 
and other sources. Manure is utilized by placing it directly on farmland, but it is only applied in the 
nearby area since transportation costs are the limiting factor. Utilization of phosphorus in waste 
water also consist of direct placement of sludge on farmland after stabilization. It is necessary to 
concentrate phosphorus in bacteria, and if not possible, to precipitate it before it can be applied. If 
precipitated, the availability for the plants is lowered significantly.  
 
3.2.3 Phosphorus consumption in EU 
 
In 2009 it was estimated that the 27 EU member states consumed 1.5 Mt P/yr corresponding to 8.8 
% of the global consumption of 17.2 Mt P/yr (Schröder et al., 2010; Van Kauwenbergh, 2010).  
 
The disposal methods for sludge in each member state is seen in Figure 3.4 by Dijk (2014) and 
supported by results from Gendebien et al., (2010). The average for all the member state can be 
seen to the left in the figure, from which the preferred method is application on land, followed by 
incineration. Other destinations of sludge could be gasification or composting (Bresters et al., 
2007). The current trend is that use of sludge on farmland is exposed to stricter regulation, and it is 
expected that incineration of sludges will become more frequent as the requirements for agricultural 
disposal cannot be met (Bresters et al., 2007). 
 
The use on agricultural soil is restricted by legislation in all EU member states. The regulations 
differ as some countries only place regulations on content of heavy metals, and others have 
restrictions on crop type, access conditions to farmland with sludge on, micropollutants and more. 
All member states do however agree on the following: The sludge must be pre-treated, there are 
restrictions on content of heavy metals as well as restrictions on amount allowed to spread in

It is likely that there also exists an aspect of problems with utilization of the large quantities of 
nutrient rich sludge in WWTPs produced in urban areas where the demand for nutrients is low due 
to lack of farmland. The farmers in Denmark are not eager to use sludge as nutrient supply, 
therefore they receive a compensation of cash for using sludge (Jensen et al., 2015). Furthermore 
there are restrictions on how much phosphorus to apply on farmland whereof the maximum is 30 kg 
P/ha/yr. (Jensen et al., 2015). Another aspect of the phosphorus debate is that recirculation of sludge 
is not allowed in ecological farming according to EU legislations due to risk of heavy metal and 
pathogen contamination. The quality of the sludge is however becoming better and contain e.g. less

cadmium than phosphorus from mines in Morocco which sometimes contain >500 mg Cd/kgP2O5,

3.3 Phosphorus in waste water 
 
In this section the components of water treatment at WWTPs in relation to removal of nutrients and 
especially phosphorus is accounted for. It addresses the difficulties of balancing the quality criteria 
to avoid eutrophication and extracting phosphorus.  
 
3.3.1 Waste water treatment plants

amounts of organic matter and nutrients, but also pathogens, heavy metals and other unwanted 
compounds. The quality criteria for discharge of phosphorus, organic matter/Chemical Oxygen

There are three overall processes, mechanical, biological and advanced. Mechanical treatment uses 
screens and grates for removal of sand and grit. It includes the primary settling tank which removes 
30-50 % of the organic content and 10-20 % of the nitrate and phosphorus (Madsen & Henze, 
2017). The sludge from the primary settling tank is called primary sludge and has a high biogas 
potential (ClearCove, 2014). Biological treatment removes organic matter by converting it to water, 
carbon dioxide and sludge by oxidation. It occurs in aerated tanks with activated sludge that contain 
many different microbes which can degrade the organic matter. The excess sludge is called 
secondary sludge and is sent to biogas production or another handling (Madsen & Henze, 2017; 
Henze et al., 2014).

the alteration between oxic and anoxic conditions ensure a constant carbon source for the 
nitrification and denitrification which produces molecular nitrogen (Madsen & Henze, 2017; Henze 
et al., 2014; Veolia, 2018).

3.3.3 Enhanced Biological Phosphorus Recovery configuration 
 
This section describes the optimization of biological recovery of phosphorus with implementation 
of an EBPR configuration. It requires a functioning microbial community of Phosphorus 
Accumulating Organisms (PAOs). It is an essential technique to utilize if phosphorus should be 
extracted at a soluble state and without large costs. It is a natural process that allows for circulation 
of important nutrients instead of disposal of them as insoluble precipitates.  
 
The process of an EBPR configuration contains two stages with anaerobic and aerobic conditions 
following each other. This is indicated by the bio-P and aeration tank in Figure 3.5. It is essential 
that PAOs dominate the microbial community, and to ensure this the nitrogen removal must occur 
first and activated sludge is returned to the bio-P tank. 
 
==PAGE:19==
Page 19 of 63 
 
In the first stage of EBPR anaerobic conditions dominate. Any phosphorus accumulated within the 
PAOs as polyPs is degraded to generate energy which is used to take up Volatile Fatty Acids (VFA) 
and binding them in long chains known as Poly-B-hydroxyalkanoates (PHAs). Therefore, the 
phosphate concentration increases in the solution. This formation requires energy which is produced 
by degradation of intracellular glycogen. The process is illustrated in Figure 3.6. It can be seen that 
the VFA acetate is produced by fermenting bacteria and afterwards used for the formation of PHAs 
(Gary et al., 1999; He & McMahon, 2011).  
 
 
Oxygen present, such as bound in nitrate, will result in utilization of the ready biodegradable COD 
by ordinary bacteria and lower the amount of VFA available to the PAOs. The lower amount 
available of VFA to the PAOs will result in lower phosphorus accumulation later in the process 
(Gary et al., 1999). Therefore, the nitrification and consequent denitrification to remove nitrogen is 
necessary to implement before the EBPR system.   
 
In the second and aerobic stage the PAOs can utilize the PHAs as energy and carbon source which 
they use for energy production, growth and generation of glycogen, see Figure 3.7. By consuming 
the PHAs as energy source the PAOs can take up phosphorus from the sludge and generate polyPs. 
In total during the two stages there is a net removal of phosphorus from the sludge, which is 
illustrated on Figure 3.8 where the two stages are shown in comparison (Gary et al., 1999).  
 
In general, there are some difficulties with maintaining EBPR systems, e.g. the necessity of nitrogen 
removal beforehand. More generally, it can be difficult to manage microbes as their metabolism is 
difficult to identify. It can also be difficult to maintain good conditions for them to thrive, as sudden 
changes in waste water composition can be lethal for the microbes. In a successful EBPR system 
the phosphorus bound in the sludge can be increased from 0.02 mgP/mgVSS to 0.06-0.15 
mgP/mgVSS (Gary et al., 1999).

Investigations by the Danish EPA have shown that 5 WWTPs in Denmark that uses enhanced

investigations used a weak acid as solubility facilitator, and may yield higher percentage than a

To manage an EBPR system many parameters must be in balance and the difficulties with this has 
caused it to remain an upcoming technology. The development of EBPR could alleviate the issue 
with clogging pipes and loss of phosphorus to insoluble compounds.   
 
3.4 Extraction methods for phosphorus  
 
Mining in the absolute most common extraction method, but it is not sustainable. The current 
methods for extracting from waste water only recover part of the phosphorus, and it is slowly 
soluble for the plants as either organophosphates or precipitated with iron/aluminium. Other 
methods include extraction of struvite (NH4MgPO46H2O) from WWTPs. For WWTPs, the 
==PAGE:21==
Page 21 of 63 
 
precipitation of struvite results in extra costs as it clogs pipes and pumps. Thus by extracting the 
phosphorus the nuisances are removed as well as a new source for phosphorus is provided 
(Herningvand, 2018). The limiting factor is often the concentration of phosphorus as more than 100 
mgP/L is needed to effectively precipitate struvite, which is why the use of ED for concentrating is 
interesting (Xie et al., 2016). Struvite is however not easily soluble as it takes approx. 9 months to 
release the phosphorus, and usually it is not possible to extract all the phosphorus as struvite. It is 
possible to reduce the content of heavy metals in struvite, although they will accumulate in the 
remaining sludge to be dealt with (Jensen et al., 2015; Ronteltap et al., 2007). Phosphorus can also 
be extracted from sewage sludge that has been incinerated. One phosphorus extraction method from 
ash is the crystal-P method that is driven by addition of acid and afterwards precipitation of 
phosphoric acid with calcium chloride, which can be converted to calcium phosphate by heating 
(Ottosen et al., 2018). Another technique is to use ED directly on ashes mixed with water without 
pre-treatment (Ottosen et al., 2015). Most of these alternative methods are not implemented 
anywhere due to difficulties and costs, but struvite precipitation is implemented in 10+ locations 
around the world by the company Otara, as well as in Denmark by Herningvand (Jensen et al., 
2015; Herningvand, 2018; Geerts et al., 2015; Ostara, n.d.).  
 
3.4.1 Electrodialysis (ED) 
 
ED is the technique of utilizing the charges of ions to get them to separate into different liquids by 
use of membranes and applied electric field. The technique can include Anion Exchange 
Membranes (AEM) and Cation Exchange Membranes (CEM), each only allowing anions and 
cations through, respectively. In general, dialysis is controlled by the difference in concentration 
which will facilitate a movement. Electro kinetic is overall the technique of facilitated movement of 
ions or charged particles through a soil with applied electric field. The electro migration functions 
by the negative electrode, the cathode, attracting the cations and the positive electrode, the anode, 
attracting the negative anions. ED uses membranes and applied electric field to facilitate a 
movement through the membranes and is commonly used to recover acid or metals from different 
processes. Electro kinetic utilize the acid formed by the anode, see eq. (3.2), which assists the 
desorbing of heavy metals from soil particles. In ED the reactions at the electrodes, eq. (3.1) and 
(3.2), causes changes in pH in the electrolyte solutions at the electrodes, which are separated from 
the middle compartment by membranes. These changes must be attended with addition of base and 
acid, respectively (Dahl & Løkkegaard, 2000; Andersen, 1998). For heavy metals is has been shown 
by several that it is possible to obtain high removals of 70-99 % by use of ED (Viader et al., 2015; 
Guedes et al., 2014; Nystroem et al., 2007).

Regarding waste water sludge the EBPR configuration can accumulate large amounts of phosphorus 
within the sludge, but there is still an issue with heavy metals that also have accumulated in the 
==PAGE:22==
Page 22 of 63 
 
sludge. ED is a favourable technique for this problem since phosphate is an anion and heavy metals 
are cations and differentiate to their respective electrolyte solution. It should therefore be feasible to 
extract a phosphorus solution without heavy metals, and a concentrated heavy metals solution, 
making it easier to dispose of the metals. The applied electric field does not facilitate a movement 
of non-ionized compounds, e.g. pathogens, although movement can occur due to concentration 
dispersion. The size of compounds is also a factor and pathogens such as bacteria are presumably 
much larger than the pores of the membranes and cannot penetrate or transfer to the electrolyte 
solutions. The electric field can be applied as fixed current or fixed voltage. Anything from 1-2000 
mA or 1-140 V is common (Liu et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2015; Červenková et al., 2017).  
 
The setups used at DTU Civil Engineering are built of cells of plexiglass and commonly contain a 
middle compartment of 350 mL for e.g. waste water, with a 500 mL electrolyte chamber on each 
side separated by an AEM and CEM, respectively. This is illustrated by Figure 3.9. In that case the 
cell measures approx. 20 cm in length with a diameter of 10 cm and an inner diameter of 8 cm, 
which corresponds to an area of ~50 cm2 of AEM. The electrolyte solutions consist of 500 mL of 
0.01M NaNO3 (pH~2), and is named anolyte at the anode and catholyte at the cathode. The 
membranes are produced by GE Water and Process Technologies in Belgium. The electrolyte 
solutions are continuously circulated from the compartments to the flasks by aquarium pumps, 
partly to make sure that produced gasses from the reactions can escape the system, see eq. (3.1) and 
(3.2). Stirring can also be applied. The setup can be altered with more chambers and more 
membranes which can be used to concentrate the desired ions in a smaller volume. The current 
densities applied commonly vary between 0.2-2 mA/cm2. 
 
Figure 3.9: Setup of three compartment cell.  
 
Issues with ED can be fouling which often occur at the AEM and is due to organic matter or 
sediment particles that often are charged negatively and migrate towards the anode. Another issue 
related to fouling is miscolouring of membranes that often occurs but not always in an inhibiting 
manner. Other challenges can be management of the pump pressure and chemical pressure to avoid 
osmosis.

3.5 Summarization for this project 
 
It is essential for the project that Lynetten WWTP has EBPR implemented in order to extract sludge 
with a high amount of phosphorus bound within bacteria and not bound with precipitation agents. 
The project utilizes the microbes’ metabolism by inducing anaerobic conditions and adding the 
VFA sodium acetate which should increase the release of the phosphate ions to the waste water 
solution. The solution is then subjected to ED. The ED treatment will reveal whether it is possible 
to move phosphate ions at an efficient rate considering energy consumption and transfer percentage, 
and if it is possible to concentrate the phosphate in a smaller volume with little or no heavy metals. 
Consequently, it could provide an alternative to the inorganic phosphate or slowly mineralized 
manure as fertilizer, which both have their disadvantages. It will not be investigated if pathogens 
can penetrate the membranes.

6 Discussion 
 
The experiments with acetate addition revealed that an average of 39 % of the phosphorus was 
transferred to solution. Compared to the investigations made by Jensen et al. (2015) for the Danish 
EPA, the extraction with a weak acid yielded the results of 40 % bioavailable phosphorus. This 
demonstrates that the VFA acetate is as efficient as a weak acid. At a WWTP it is possible to 
produce VFA within the system from hydrolysis of sludge which means that there is not necessarily 
a need for import of this component (Gidstedt, 2017).  
 
The electrodialytic extraction of phosphorus from waste water proved to occur relatively fast 
compared to similar ED cell experiments with incinerated sewage sludge, fly ash or contaminated 
soil of ~14 days duration (Ottosen et al., 2006; Merdoud et al., 2016; Guedes et al., 2014). The 
results of 25-UF-25h revealed that 96.8 % of the phosphorus in the solution could be extracted 
within 25 hours at a current of 25 mA, and 96.0 % could be extracted in 6 hours in experiment 25-
F-20h. The durations are in accordance with other investigations of electrodialytic recoveries of a 
phosphorus solution from phosphate rich water in stack, though the concentrations were lower than 
what was used in stack experiments (Wang et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2017). The fast development 
makes it suitable for appliance at WWTP where the volumes are too large for a long retention time 
for ED.  
 
6.1 Efficiency of ED 
 
Compared to other investigations with waste water in ED cells the removal ratios and recovery rates 
were very similar, as described in the following. Ebbers et al. (2015a) made experiments with 3-
celled ED subjecting secondary waste water to a constant voltage of 135 V for 24 hours which 
resulted in phosphorus extraction of approximately 20 %. Similar experiments were conducted by 
Guedes et al. (2016) in a cell that found a higher phosphorus extraction of 55 % within the 24 hours 
at 2 mA/cm2.  
 
Investigations of waste water in a stack of membranes are described in the following. In 2017 
Červenková et al (2017) used the liquid discharge of a WWTP in a stack of membranes with an area 
of 344 cm2 at a constant 14 V and with a start volume of 5.68 L. Within maximum 4 hours the 
desalination was 96-98.9 % (Červenková et al., 2017). The results by Červenková et al (2017) were 
obtained without the use of a facilitator to release phosphorus to the water phase, but only filtration. 
Ebbers et al. (2015b) investigated the use of acetate as VFA to bring phosphorus into solution and 
consequent ED subjection, and thus maybe avoiding fouling due to the big particles in sludge. By 
use of 1 g/L acetate concentrations the liquid phase went from 10 mg/L to almost 80 mg/L. 
Phosphorus solution with 16 mg/L was used in a stack with 322 cm2 of membranes and a current 
density of 0.25 and 1 mA/cm2, respectively. After 5-6 hours the experiments with a flow rate of 800 
mL/min and 0.25 mA/cm2 had a removal of 90-100 %. The lowest achieved energy consumption 
was 12 kWh/m3 (Ebbers et al., 2015b). Compared to this report, it was not examined if the cell setup 
could have removed all the ions from the 5 L experiment in part III, since it was not the purpose 
==PAGE:48==
Page 48 of 63 
 
with only 50 cm2 of membranes and not more than 300 as in Ebbers et al. (2015b) and Červenková 
et al (2017). Perhaps that is the reason for a lower energy consumption of 3 kWh/m3 since the 
removal ratios varied from 73 % to -30 %.  
 
Experiments on pig manure with bipolar membranes in a stack have also shown promising results 
with a removal of 77 % of phosphate after 9 hours at a steady current of 3 A (Shi et al., 2018). 
Other investigations such as Liu et al. (2017) and Wang et al. (2013, 2015) examine the efficiency 
of transferring ions through a stack, though it is on synthetic waste water solution. They found 
recovery rates of 80-115 % with removal rates of 86-96 % at 5-62 V after 4-8 hours (Liu et al., 
2017; Wang et al., 2013, 2015). The results from these experiments with synthetic show similar 
results to what was obtained in this report. The results from this report document the potential for 
electrodialytic recovery on actual waste water with efficiencies as high as experiments on synthetic 
waste water.  
 
Other experiments conducted at DTU Civil Engineering with sewage sludge ashes in an 
electrodialytic cell have shown phosphorus transfer to the anolyte of 69 % (Guedes et al., 2014). 
The transfers obtained by use of phosphorus solution in this project were higher, but in general 
higher initial concentrations are available when working with ashes, e.g. 134 g/kg in the case of 
Guedes et al. (2014). However, the cost savings from not having to incinerate the sludge before ED 
treatment would probably outweigh the advantages of larger concentrations in the ashes.  
 
The study by Guedes et al. (2016) investigated a few organic contaminants in the sludge (caffeine, 
bisphenol A, 17a-ethinylestradiol (hormone in birth control) and oxybenzone (used in sunscreens)) 
from which it was found that 79-96 % were degraded, but some were found in the anolyte (Guedes 
et al., 2016). This is very interesting as it usually is assumed that larger compounds cannot penetrate 
the membranes’ pores, but the organic contaminants investigated by Guedes et al. (2016) are larger 
molecules. Perhaps bacteria and other pathogens still cannot penetrate due to being larger than 
caffeine.  
 
6.2 Concentrating phosphorus  
 
Liu et al. (2017) concentrated phosphorus from 9.8 mgP/L to 21.2 mgP/L (factor 2.2) which was 
similar to what Ebbers et al. (2015b) obtained with a CF of 2. Another investigation documented 
concentration of 5.68 L of ~79 mgP/L to ~674 mgP/L in 500 mL with a CF 8.4 (Červenková et al., 
2017). Wang et al. (2015) documented a CF of approx. 10, where Shi et al. (2018) documented a 
CF of 4. Therefore, experiments on both synthetic and real waste water corresponded in CFs of 2-8. 
Compared to the results of part III, the obtained concentration factors were 2.0-3.9 with start 
concentrations of 31-59 mg/L. The results documented possibility for concentrating phosphorus in a 
smaller volume even without stack, and presumably the CF would be higher if experiments were 
conducted in a stack.

6.3 Energy consumption 
 
The energy consumptions vary from 1-12 kWh/m3 as the lowest (Liu et al., 2017; Ebbers et al., 
2015b), to around 100 kWh/m3 (Wang et al., 2015; Shi et al., 2018) and up to 2700 kWh/m3 (Wang 
et al., 2015; Ebbers et al., 2015a; Guedes et al., 2016). All the numbers were estimated from data 
provided in the journals except Liu et al. (2017) who specifically reported it. The low consumption 
by Liu et al. (2017) is not due to low transfer, as 86 % of phosphorus was transferred from a start 
concentration of 9.8 mg/L. Compared to the results in this project the lowest energy consumption 
was found to be around 3 kWh/m3 but with a lower transfer to the anolyte of 38 % of the original 
content of phosphorus, where some might have come from old precipitates in pump or tubes. It can 
be noted that two of the three experiments with the highest estimated energy consumption were the 
ones conducted on real waste water and not synthetic solution. It is probably because the synthetic 
solution does not contain the same particles that might clog the membranes’ pores and cause more 
resistance. The experiments in this report with 25 mA also showed promising low energy 
consumptions of 3.6-4.8 kWh/m3 compared to removals of 69-76 % of the phosphorus.  
 
If all the sludge processed at Lynetten in one year should be treated with electrodialysis at an energy 
consumption of 3 kWh/m3 as in experiment 40-F-C-47h the costs would correspond to approx. 1 % 
of their annual electricity demand (BIOFOS Lynettefællesskabet A/S, 2013). The calculations are 
estimates based on one treatment of all the sludge and not recirculation, the calculations can be 
found in Appendix Data Part II Subfolder Exp. 10. It is very likely that it could be done more cost 
efficient with a stack of membranes instead.  
 
6.4 Fouling and filtration 
 
It has been documented by many that the main issue with concentrating phosphorus in ED is 
fouling, and especially fouling on the AEM (Xie et al., 2016; Lindstrand et al., 2000; Oztekin & 
Altin, 2016; Thompson, 2017). The AEM took colour in all the experiment but without causing 
problems. In part II the membranes all became their original colour again after acid treatment, 
which means that it must have been a layer of something which was on them. The AEMs in part III 
took more colour and perhaps burned during the experiments since it did not come off again with 
acid treatment, see Appendix C for photos. The miscolouring in the 5 L experiments may simply be 
due to the smaller area for transferring ions compared to a stack of membranes. It was unsolved 
why the membranes in the 5 L experiments burned since other experiments with ED extract heavy 
metals in much larger concentrations than in this project (Abo-Ghander et al., 2006; Merdoud et al., 
2016). Ebbers et al. (2015b) found that for 6 hours experiment in a stack 70 % of the phosphorus 
entering the system accumulated on the AEMs. It was difficult to determine if a higher voltage or 
current corresponded to fouling since many does not report if they had fouling or not. Some of the 
other investigations had high transfers which indicate that the major part of the phosphorus cannot 
have accumulated on the AEM, though these experiments were conducted on synthetic waste water 
which probably does not clog the membranes as real sludge would (Wang et al., 2013; Liu et al., 
2017; Wang et al., 2015).  
==PAGE:50==
Page 50 of 63 
 
 
None of the experiments in this report had the typical problems that fouling causes; drop in current 
yet with a high conductivity, which made it difficult to determine the colouring as fouling. Perhaps 
the pre-treatment of centrifuging and filtration was the reason for no clogging of the AEM. 
Implementation at a WWTP would probably not be able to filter the solution as effectively, which 
could be investigated by using unfiltered solution in a stack of membranes. It is also a factor that 
stacked ED cannot be stirred which might increase the necessity of filtrating beforehand to reduce 
the fouling, though the turbulence may be enough to avoid the accumulation on the AEM, as a 
higher accumulation was seen in experiment 100-UF-C-2h without stirring. 
 
Increased recovery with unfiltered sludge could be due to desorption of phosphorus from organic 
matter in the phosphorus solution. Phosphorus sorption decreases with pH, so as the pH drops in the 
middle compartment it makes sense that more could be desorbed and increase the recovery (Bai et 
al., 2017). The effect of this was also seen regarding the heavy metals results. The lack of 
acidification of start samples resulted in recoveries of more than what was in the sample initially, 
though it probably just was bound within organic matter. Overall the unfiltered solution provided 
more phosphorus to the solution, but it might also have been the contributing factor to osmosis. 
Although the osmosis may just have been due to it being a liquid sample which is more sensitive for 
difference in pump flow. The results indicated possibility for performing experiments on sludge 
instead of only the water phase.  
 
The transfer to the anolyte was also expressed compared to total end content of phosphorus, from 
which the experiments with 25 mA and 10 mA had the highest transfers of all the experiments. In 
general, the unfiltered samples had high transfers to the anolyte compared to the filtered samples. If 
neither fouling nor lowering of transfer to the anolyte occur due to unfiltered sludge the 
implementation at a WWTP is further feasible based on these results.  
 
6.5 Implementation at WWTP 
 
The results indicated that the pH decrease mainly was a problem with small waste water volumes in 
cell experiments. It may be possible to mix the low pH waste water with other waste water from the 
system to increase the pH if it constitutes a problem. The more stable tendency with larger volumes 
indicate that the pH problem may not be an issue at an actual WWTP, especially if it is not 
necessary to completely remove the ions but perhaps 50 % for each circulation.  
 
The transfers of 95 % or more of the phosphorus to the anolyte in five out of seven cell experiments 
are promising results for the development of phosphorus recovery. The results provide a more 
realistic potential for use in a WWTP than other investigations that examined synthetic waste water.  
At a WWTP the recovery of phosphorus would probably occur continuously with a stream of P 
enriched sludge that is recirculated to the bio-p tank. In a such design the microbes in the sludge 
and the remaining phosphorus would recirculate back to the bio-p tank to strengthen the microbial 
community and make sure that all phosphorus eventually is recovered. The process would require

either addition of a VFA, e.g. acetate, or a tank for production of VFAs by hydrolysis of sludge with 
retention time of ~24-48 hours (Gidstedt, 2017), and a tank or pipe with retention time 4-6 hours for

It is likely that more difficulties with fouling on membranes will occur as the sludge would not be 
centrifuged and filtered to same extend as in this project. Instead, a drum filter could be applied that 
would remove many of the large particles after phosphorus has been released to the water phase. 
The membranes will however probably have to function with more organic matter present.  
 
If the concentrated phosphorus solution should be used to precipitate struvite it would be relevant to 
investigate if the heavy metals bind within the struvite. It is probably not an issue since the results 
in this project found concentrations of heavy metals in the anolyte lower than the quality criteria. 
This is supported by research which have shown that the content of heavy metals in manure and 
urine are lower than that of commercially available fertilizer, and that only 20-63 % re-appear in 
precipitated struvite (Ronteltap et al., 2007).

7 Conclusion 
 
Electrodialytic concentration of phosphorus from waste water to a small volume was documented 
successful at relatively low costs, with concentrations of heavy metals in the finished product lower 
than the Danish restrictions.  
 
It was found in the first and second sampling that a maximum average 38-39 % of the phosphorus 
in the sludge could be transferred into solution by addition of acetate. The optimal concentration of 
acetate was 1 g/L of sludge and 4 hours curing. It is necessary to account for ratio between acetate 
and phosphorus where it could be concluded that an optimal relation is 4.4-7.7 g acetate/gP.  
 
Three compartment cell ED on phosphorus solution was successful with maximum 100 % removal 
and 96.8 % transfer to the anolyte. Recoveries >77.6 % were achieved for all experiments. The 
most consistent high removal ratios (R>96.7 %) were found for 25 mA and 10 mA experiments, 
which all had >95 % of the phosphorus in the anolyte compared to end content. These experiments 
also had the lowest energy consumption. It was concluded that unfiltered samples contained 
phosphorus bound within organic matter, bacteria etc. which was released and caused recoveries 
>120 %. Also experiments with 100 mA had transfers to the anolyte of >95 %.  
 
Low currents of 10-25 mA applied for 6-50 hours on filtered/unfiltered phosphorus solution 
documented best results of cell experiments and are advised for new experiments if further 
optimization is wanted. The voltage did not increase due to particles in the unfiltered samples, nor 
did particles cause fouling on the membranes if stirring or turbulence by pumps was applied. 
Unfiltered solution could easily be used in ED with transfers to the anolyte of >95 %, though it may 
have an increased tendency of osmosis which can be adjusted with flow restrictions.  
 
The concentration experiments found that concentration factors (CF) of 2-3.9 were obtained, though 
the 3.9 CF might be assisted by dissolved precipitates of phosphorus from the pumps and tubes 
used. The end concentrations were 98-120 mg/L in the three concentration experiments. The 
transfers to the anolyte obtained were 12.2 and 36.5 % for the concentrating experiments with a 5 L 
middle compartment, with energy consumptions of 25.5 and 3.1 kWh/m3, respectively. The results 
were disturbed by precipitation of phosphorus, causing a high loss in the first experiment, and 
dissolvement of precipitates in the next experiment, causing a high recovery.  
 
Based on the results of this project implementation of ED at WWTPs seems feasible. Further 
experiments in a stack of membranes with phosphorus solution from waste water sludge should be 
investigated. The CF of 3.9 with the corresponding energy consumption of 3.1 kWh/m3 indicate the 
possibility for higher CF with high energy efficiency in a stack of membranes. The sludge samples 
from Lynetten all contained available phosphorus, indicating functioning EBPR, and feasibility of 
ED implementation. Utilizing the phosphorus at the WWTP by electrodialytic recovery is a 
sustainable technology for both removal of the nutrient and its nuisances, and providing a soluble 
phosphorus product for the agriculture.

8 Outlook 
 
The successful electrodialytic phosphorus recovery could be applied to another area lacking 
attention; the manure from farm animals. All over Europe there are issues with oversaturation of 
phosphorus in some regions and subsaturation in others (Oberender et al., 2013; Tóth et al., 2014). 
In Europe it was estimated in 2005 that there annually is an accumulation of approx. 924,000 tonnes 
P/yr (van Dijk et al., 2016). This is due to the poorly exploitation of the phosphorus in feed for farm 
animals which comes out again in the manure in high quantities and is spread in the surrounding 
areas. The washout of the accumulated phosphorus is now a much larger issue than the quantities 
coming from WWTPs and eutrophication remains a problem (Madsen, 2018b, 2018a; Poulsen & 
Rubæk, 2005; Jørgensen & The ecological council, 2018; Kronvang et al., 2001). Another 
perspective is that the manure contains more pollutants than sludge, e.g. zinc, copper and oestrogen 
in concentrations whereof the manure should have been landfilled (Bak et al., 2015; Eriksen et al., 
2009).  
 
The overall dilemma is to transport the liquid manure to the areas lacking nutrients without too high 
costs or CO2 emissions doing so, and removing the pollutants. ED could be applied which would 
remove the heavy metals and large contaminants (pathogens) whilst concentrating the nutrients and 
allowing for cheap and CO2 friendly transport. The lack of restrictions for manure in regards of 
contaminants and quantities to apply is the reason for the lack of focus on this dilemma.  
 
8.1 Future work with cells 
 
It could be interesting to investigate whether the cells could run with the sludge filtered in a manner 
comparable to what would be implemented at a WWTP. Important factors for future studies are 
clean membranes and tubes, ensure this by using personal equipment and reusing personal 
membranes that have not been in contact with more contaminated sediments. Pumps with same 
flow capacity is a necessity or osmosis will occur with liquid samples. Experiments with pH neutral 
electrolytes would be interesting, especially to see if the transfers are as high and if acidification of 
the middle compartment can be avoided. This would also ease the implementation at a WWTP.
