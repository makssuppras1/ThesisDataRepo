Abstract
Excessive heat poses a threat to human health, as it can have negative effects on both
physical health and work productivity. With climate change exacerbating the problem,
heat waves and rising temperatures are expected to increase the risks. This study in-
vestigates mitigation interventions for reducing overheating and improving the indoor
climate in regard to cost and environmental impact, by comparing different combina-
tions of interventions for a Zimbabwean healthcare facility.
Six criteria were established to evaluate potential solutions: Time above an indoor
temperature health-risk limit, a temperature comfort assessment, the incorporation of
adapted predicted mean vote comfort models, air quality assessment, and mould growth
risk. For each intervention, life cycle cost and life cycle assessment calculations were
made to compare the feasibility of the different measures and combinations of measures.
The current conditions were investigated, which showed excessive overheating and po-
tential risk of mould growth in the Maternity Ward building at Mt. Darwin District
Hospital with indoor operative temperatures reaching up to 40°C. The effects of in-
dividual intervention measures were tested, though no single measure could remove
overheating without causing poor indoor climate in regard to other parameters, such
as air quality and potential mould growth. By utilizing passive measures, the overheat-
ing could be significantly reduced, though only by using active measures all evaluation
criteria were within an acceptable range, but at the cost of high energy consumption.
Lastly, the study revealed that in the absence of any interventions, rising temperatures
would lead to a more significant rise in overheating indoors, caused by climate change,
compared to a scenario where any of the potential proposed interventions were adopted.

Abstract (Dansk/Danish)
Ekstrem varme udgør en trussel for menneskers sundhed, da det kan påvirke både fy-
sisk sundhed og arbejdsproduktivitet negativt. Med klimaforandringer, der forværrer
problemet, forventes hedebølger og stigende temperaturer at øge risiciene. Dette pro-
jekt undersøger tiltag, der kan reducere overophedning og forbedre indeklimaet med
henblik på omkostninger og miljøbelastning. Dette gøres ved at sammenligne forskel-
lige kombinationer af tiltag på en hospitalsbygning i Zimbabwe.
Seks kriterier blev etableret til at evaluere potentielle løsninger: Tid over en græn-
seværdi for indetemperatur ift. sundhedsrisiko, vurdering af termisk komfort for tem-
peraturer, inkorporering af tilpassede modeller til at forudsige opfattelsen af det ter-
miske indeklima, vurdering af luftkvalitet, samt risikoen for skimmelsvampvækst. For
hvert indgreb blev der foretaget beregninger af omkostninger og miljøpåvirkning for
levetiden for at sammenligne gennemførligheden af de forskellige tiltag og kombina-
tioner.
Undersøgelser af de nuværende forhold viste en ekstrem grad af overophedning, samt
potentiel risiko for skimmelsvampvækst i fødsel- og barselsbygningen i Mt. Darwin Dis-
trikt Hospital, hvor indetemperaturer flere gange nærmede sig 40°C. Effekten af tiltag
blev evalueret, men intet enkelt tiltag kunne fjerne overophedning uden at forværre
andre indeklima parametre som fx. luftkvalitet og risikoen for skimmelsvampvækst.
Ved at anvende flere passive tiltag kunne overophedningen dog reduceres betydeligt,
men kun ved hjælp af aktive tiltag var alle evalueringskriterier inden for acceptable
grænser, dog med et højt energiforbrug.
Endelig viste undersøgelsen, at uden nogen tiltag, ville stigende temperaturer, som
følge af klimaforandringer, lede til en mere markant stigning i overophedning indendørs,
sammenlignet med et scenarie hvor nogen af de potentielle tiltag blev implementeret.

1.1
Introduction
Excessive heat poses a significant danger to human health, especially for sensitive occu-
pants, and can lead to mistakes during work tasks. Due to climate change, heat waves
and global temperature rise will further worsen the situation, especially in already hot
areas. Presently, energy used for space cooling accounts for 20% of emissions from
buildings, and as this is a preventive measure against overheating, it is increasing.
Without any preventive measures, the energy used to cool buildings will more than
triple by 2050. Therefore, it is vital to find viable and sustainable solutions to address
this issue [International Energy Agency, 2018].
UN Sustainable Development Goal No. 3 emphasizes the enhancement of health and
reduction of mortality rates, especially for pregnant and postpartum women and new-
borns.
Goal 3.1 ceases to reduce the maternal mortality ratio to less than 70 per
100,000 live births by 2030. However, in sub-Saharan Africa there were more than 500
maternal mortalities per 100,000 live births in 2015 [UNICEF, 2015], some of which can
be related to being exposed to too high indoor temperatures [HIGH Horizons, 2021].
The UN estimates that approximately 40% of all newborns will be born in Africa by
2050, emphasising the importance of constructing more energy-efficient buildings there
[UNICEF, 2015]. With significant population growth, there will be greater demand for
housing and infrastructure. And by prioritizing energy-efficient building practices, it
will be possible to meet the rising demand, while minimizing the environmental impact
and promoting sustainable development in Africa. This includes implementing designs
and technologies that optimize energy usage, enhance thermal insulation, utilize re-
newable energy sources, and incorporate sustainable materials. This will heighten the
quality of life and result in more sustainable building practices.
The construction industry currently constitutes 37% of all global warming potential
(GWP) and 36% of all energy-related emissions. While these percentages might be
lower in Africa, it is crucial and more feasible to drive sustainable practices in the
building sector now, rather than delaying it [UN, 2021].

1
Background
The prevalence of extreme heat is increasingly becoming a significant issue in various
regions worldwide, posing a risk of overheating within the built environment and en-
dangering the occupants. For a poorly designed building, this problem can be even
worse, as the inside of a building can become hotter than outdoors in some cases. Par-
ticularly in healthcare facilities, overheating can contribute to elevated mortality rates
among pregnant women and infants, while reducing productivity among healthcare
staff.
This problem is amplified by climate change and the heightened occurrence of heat
waves and droughts. Addressing this concern, the European HIGH Horizons project
aims to enhance conditions in healthcare facilities by leveraging data from multiple

The objective of this master thesis is to create a comprehensive indoor climate and
lifecycle study of an existing healthcare facility within the HIGH Horizons project,
with the aim of establishing best practices. An indoor climate simulation model will
provide a reference point for identifying potential areas of improvement. A wide range
of building-related solutions will be tested and evaluated, considering factors such as
viability, efficiency, cost, and sustainability. Furthermore, the simulation will incorpo-
rate the anticipated rise in global temperatures in the coming half-century, to assess
the effectiveness, resilience, and scalability of the proposed solutions.

The HIGH Horizons project is sponsored by the European Union’s Research and Inno-

Theoretical framework
2.1
Building design in tropical and subtropical climates
One of the main functions of buildings is to protect its occupants from the weather
conditions.
Traditionally, buildings use a lot of energy to create comfortable con-
ditions, whether it is by heating or cooling indoor spaces. As countries experience
economic progress, a growing use of air conditioning was found around the world.
In developing countries, especially in Africa, cooling presents a challenge due to the
high temperatures, and the need for comfortable indoor environments will further in-
crease energy consumption. This could increase expenses and carbon emissions. The
need for sustainable building design is thus critical for both new and existing projects
[Butera et al., 2014].
Building design is influenced by a variety of factors, such as geographic location, tradi-
tions, and available resources. Geographic location determines the local climate, which
has a big influence on the measures taken to keep buildings comfortable. Some of
the parameters to take into consideration are solar radiation, air temperature, relative
humidity and wind [Butera et al., 2014].
2.1.1
Sun position and radiation
The radiation emitted from the sun is one of the main drivers of climate differences
around the world. The angle of incidence of the sun’s rays affects the temperature, as a
high angle of incidence will increase the amount of energy reaching the ground. Starting
from the Equator, the angle of incidence decreases towards the poles, where less energy
reaches the ground, resulting in lower temperatures, see Figure 2 [Butera et al., 2014].
Page 8 of 136
==PAGE:23==
2
Theoretical framework
Figure 2: Sun path at the equator and angular sun coordinates. [Butera et al., 2014].
The altitude of the sun varies due to the offset rotation of the earth, which creates
different climate zones and seasons across the globe. At the Equator there is a very
high sun altitude, see Figure 2, resulting in high solar radiation, and mostly high
temperatures, over the year.
2.2
Heat transfer
Due to the second law of thermodynamics heat will flow from hot objects towards
cooler ones, to try to achieve balance. Transfer of heat, or energy, can occur in various
ways, for which the important ones for buildings and occupants, are explained in the
following sections [Thermal Edge, 2023].
Radiation
Radiation is one way for heat to transfer from one object to another as all objects
emit radiation heat through electromagnetic waves. Solar irradiance is the radiation
of solar power hitting a surface area at any point in time, measured in W/m2. When
a building is exposed to solar irradiance, the sun’s rays will hit objects and the heat
will be absorbed or reflected. In most cases, the absorbed energy will be converted into
thermal energy and heat up the object. An object with thermal energy, will then also
emit radiation, to reach a balance with the surrounding objects. [Butera et al., 2014].
The amount of solar radiation received is made up of direct, diffuse and reflected ra-
diation. This means that an object does not have to be in direct line-of-sight with the
sun, to receive solar radiation energy.
Page 9 of 136
==PAGE:24==
2
Theoretical framework
Conduction
When objects or substances are in direct contact, heat transfer through conduction
occurs as heat flows through the objects, to achieve balance. This can be expressed
through heat flux, which is the transfer of heat through a substance per unit area
and unit time. Besides the temperature difference, the heat flux is dependent on a
material’s thermal conductivity [SIMSCALE, 2023]. Building materials such as insula-
tion have a low thermal conductivity, which means that less heat transfers through it,
thus an insulating barrier will slow the heat transfer between two substances over time.
Convection
Convection describes heat transfer by the movement of fluids. One type of convection
is natural convection, which is caused by the buoyancy effect that pushes warmer fluids
upwards. Forced convection is caused by the movement of fluid with fans, pumps or
wind [SIMSCALE, 2023].
Evaporation
People often have to adjust their body temperature to feel comfortable. As a way to
cool down, the body can transfer heat through sweating. Many ways of heat transfer
already explained are involved when sweating cools the body, but one additional is
evaporation. As sweat, which is made up of mostly water, has contact with the air, a
number of molecules will be released from the liquid and phase change into gas form.
As energy is needed to break the bonds holding the water into liquid form, the phase
change will result in a net heat loss and thus provide cooling [USGS, 2019].
2.3
Passive climatisation of buildings
When optimizing any building regarding indoor climate, passive climatisation measures
are an efficient method. A passive measure could be the geometry of the building, spe-
cific construction materials, window types etc., affecting both heat gains and heat
transfer of the building. These properties can contribute to how the indoor climate of
a building is experienced. Using passive measures that fit a given climate is important,
as different climates require different indoor climate strategies. The purpose of pas-
sive building design is to improve design decisions before implementing active solutions
that use energy. As active solutions can be expensive, passive design is a way to both
reduce costs and improve sustainability. Many different building codes, certifications,
Page 10 of 136
==PAGE:25==
2
Theoretical framework
and design strategies focus on passive design, or at least have some minimum require-
ments. An example is the Passive-house strategy, with a design goal of very low energy
consumption for a building [Sifferlen, 2017].
2.3.1
Natural ventilation
Many modern buildings make use of either mechanical ventilation systems or air con-
ditioning systems, with electricity-powered fans as well as coils to heat or cool the
air. Over time, the use of mechanically operated systems has increased, leading to
higher energy consumption and higher annual costs. More than 50% of a commercial
building’s energy consumption can be allocated to an air conditioning system in the
tropics. Thus, natural ventilation can be a very sustainable alternative, if incorporated
correctly. [Wong et al., 2019].
Natural ventilation can be obtained by naturally generated air flows through the build-
ing, often created by openings in the building e.g., windows, doors, or other intentional
devices. The driving forces for airflow can either be due to pressure differences often
caused by wind or by temperature differences [CEN, 2019a]. Both affect the result-
ing airflow, though one will often be more dominant depending on the specific situation.
The size, placement and number of windows all affect the airflow pattern and veloc-
ity. A common type of natural ventilation is single-sided natural ventilation, which
relies on airflow through openings on a single facade, see Figure 3. Single-sided nat-
ural ventilation is not as efficient as cross-flow ventilation, because the air is entering
and leaving the room through the same aperture. Cross-flow ventilation can have an
increased airflow due to wind pressure, driving air through the building. Even though
single-sided natural ventilation is less efficient [Awbi, 2003], it is commonly used due
to the general layout of buildings.

2.3.2
Cool roofs
When a material is hit by solar rays, specific material properties can explain the re-
sulting absorbed heat and heat transfer of a given material. Thermal emittance and
solar reflectivity are some of the key parameters influencing surface temperature and
surrounding ambient air heat transfer [Cool Roof Rating Council, 2022a]. Solar reflec-
tivity describes the fraction of solar radiation not being absorbed by a surface to the
total amount of radiation striking that surface, thus being a value between 0 and 1. A
surface with low reflectivity will absorb a significant amount of solar radiation. Some
of the absorbed energy is conducted into other building materials, while some of the
energy is lost to the air through convection and radiated to the sky or underlying ma-
terials. Thermal emittance is defined as the ratio of the emissivity of a material to
the emissivity of a perfectly emitting and absorbing black object, also being a value
between 0 and 1. Surfaces with a low emissivity struggle to effectively radiate heat
back to the environment, which causes them to become hot.

Consequently, a roof cladding material that efficiently emits heat and reflects solar
radiation reduces the heating potential of said material, resulting in less heat trans-

2.3.3
Thermal mass
It can be difficult to control the temperature in a building in an environment with large
temperature fluctuations e.g. between day and night. Materials with high thermal mass
can be incorporated into the building, which will lower indoor temperature fluctuations.
Thermal mass is a description of a material’s ability to store heat, which concrete and
brick are good examples of. These materials will absorb heat from the sun during the
day and release it to the surrounding environment during the night. Thus, the indoor
air will receive less heat during the day, lowering the indoor operative temperature,
and more heat during the night [Butera et al., 2014]. This effect can make thermal
mass a good passive design measure in climates with large temperature fluctuations,
especially if the night temperatures cause the indoor temperature to be below comfort
levels. However, the correct use of thermal mass is highly dependent on when a building
is occupied.

2.4
Active climatisation of buildings
Many modern buildings make use of active solutions to achieve a comfortable indoor
environment due to the controllable and predictable effects. An active solution can pro-
vide additional temperature adjustment, which can not be otherwise gained by passive
design solutions. Some buildings might even need both an active heating and cooling
system, especially if the interior heating loads are high. An active system could also
focus on providing better air quality by providing fresh air to a zone or dehumidifying
indoor air. Active measures are often expensive compared to passive measures, in both
construction and maintenance costs. However, this adds controllability, which passive
measures do not offer in the same way. Some building types, such as hospitals, often
require a controlled environment due to risks of contamination and similar health-
related risks. Furthermore, some patient groups in hospitals might be more sensitive
to changes in the indoor environment.
2.5
Thermal comfort
It is evident that the definition of overheating and its physical effects on the human
body is established so that building design solutions are evaluated with appropriate
benchmarks.
2.5.1
Thermal comfort definition
Thermal comfort relies on multiple measurable and non-measurable factors and thus,
no absolutely fixed approach can be derived. The internationally accepted definition,
defined by [ISO, 2005a], is to have an environment in which its occupants find the
thermal environment satisfactory. Evaluation of thermal comfort can be done using
the predicted mean vote(PMV) index, which predicts satisfaction with the thermal
environment from the mean value of votes in a large group of occupants. Experiments
studying the PMV index from individual responses create the basis for these predic-
tions, where the thermal environment was rated using the thermal sensation scale, see

2.8
Overheating in buildings
It has been proved that elevated indoor temperatures can have negative effects on
well-being and productivity, as well as health [Zukowska et al., 2019]. With multiple
ways of defining thermal comfort, overheating has no precise definition either, though
heat strain indexes have been developed. However, standards such as EN16798-1 sug-
gest a simplified method by using a maximum and minimum allowed temperature
[CEN, 2019a]. In hot environments where thermal comfort indices such as PMV are
exceeded for longer periods of time, this might not be adequate as a stand-alone vali-
dation parameter. Consequently, WHO advises indoor temperatures to be kept below
32 ◦C during the day to avoid serious health risks [World Health Organization, 2018].
2.8.1
Body heat balance
The body has several systems that must work synchronously for it to perform any
physical activity. When exposed to environments which cause stress to these systems,
the body possesses regulatory mechanisms and can thus adjust, to ensure internal sta-
bility. Body temperature is regulated within a narrow range by the thermoregulatory
system through sensors, signal integrators and effector organs [Tipton et al., 2008].
A healthy human body has a core temperature between 36.1◦C and 37.8◦C. To maintain
a stable core body temperature, the body has to be in a state of thermal equilibrium
with the surrounding environment, also called heat balance, which requires the body
to gain and lose heat at the same rate. The heat left is the difference between lost
heat and generated heat, which consequently controls if the body temperature rises or
drops. Heat can be emitted from the body through different routes. These include
heat loss through convection, conduction, radiation, sweating and respiration. This
will vary from each individual as everyone responds differently to heat and develop
signs and symptoms of heat illness differently [Dengel and Swainson, 2012]. It should
be noted that a body in equilibrium does not necessarily mean that thermal comfort
is achieved [CIBSE, 2006].
2.8.2
Health hazards
Overheating of the body can cause both mild and more severe health impacts, whereas
mild health effects include heat oedema, dehydration, heat syncope and heat rashes
among others. Research show a clear relation between excessive heat exposure and re-
Page 20 of 136
==PAGE:35==
2
Theoretical framework
duced mental concentration and productivity, which can potentially lead to an increase
in mistakes or even accidents [Dengel and Swainson, 2012].
Mental health consequences, exhaustion and heat stroke are some of the severe health
effects caused by excessive heat exposure. Extreme heat exposure can lead to excessive
sweating, which can cause two types of heat exhaustion. Firstly, a fluid loss and thus
a reduction in the circulatory blood volume. Secondly, a reduction of sodium levels
through sweat. Improper treatment of heat exhaustion can in severe cases lead to heat
stroke, which is the most serious illness caused by heat exposure. Heat strokes have a
high fatality rate and require clinical treatment [Dengel and Swainson, 2012].
Heat strokes and other health effects can affect all populations but some groups are
more vulnerable to physiological stress and increased risk of death from excessive
heat exposure.
Effects differ across demographics, as elderly, infants and children
are at higher risk [World Health Organization, 2018]. Furthermore, pregnant women
are increasingly recognized as being vulnerable to excessive heat exposure, as epi-
demiological evidence shows a correlation between high ambient temperatures and
a variety of pregnancy complications including adverse fetal and neonatal outcomes
[Samuels et al., 2022].
A meta-analysis on the associations between high temperatures during pregnancy and
risk of adverse fetal outcomes demonstrated an increased risk of stillbirths and preterm
birth [Chersich et al., 2020]. The risk of preterm birth rose 1.05-fold per 1 ◦C increase
in ambient temperature and was found to be higher during heatwaves. Several of the
studies pointed to a threshold temperature of 25 ◦C, above which increased risk would
occur. These associations were found to be largest among women at age extremes
and lower socioeconomic groups. This link suggests an increased risk in low-income
countries. Consequently, global warming and rising temperatures could have major
health implications in these regions in the future [Chersich et al., 2020].

2.10
Climate data
One of the key components of predicting building performance is the location and cor-
responding weather data, as this heavily influences optimal building design. Weather
data can be derived from measurement sites all around the world, measuring hourly
values for solar radiation, wind speed and direction, air temperature, humidity and
other metrics. One of the most common file formats to store weather data is the EPW
(EnergyPlus Weather) comma-separated (CSV) format. The data stored in EPW files
are based on a climate file format, which dictates what data is used, often following
TMY/ISO 15927-4:2005 [ISO, 2005b].
The latest dataset made available by Ener-
gyPlus is TMY3 (Typical Meteorological Year, version 3), which uses weather data
collected in the period 1991-2005. The TMY format tries to identify the most typical
weather conditions by determining the most representative of each month during the
entire measurement period. Thus, the dataset will be made out of data from different
years [Ladybug Tools, 2021].
However, more recent datasets were made available through the TMYx weather file
format using typical meteorological data obtained from weather data
[Lawrie and Crawley, 2022]. The datasets are derived from the same data and method-
ology used in the TMY3 files but use more recent data (2007-2021). It should be noted
that by using typical meteorological data, extreme weather events are intentionally re-
moved from the datasets. Thus, the increase in heat waves and other extreme weather
events are not necessarily included. Besides being based on more recent data, the TMYx

2.11
Life cycle assessment
A part of climate change is caused by a high amount of CO2 emissions, which is lead-
ing to the rise of global temperatures [Eckstein et al., 2021]. Environmental impacts
associated with buildings or other products can be evaluated through a life cycle assess-
ment (LCA). By making use of LCA, opportunities for improvement of environmental
performance can be identified and can further enhance an informed decision-making
process [ISO, 2006]. LCA identifies potential environmental impacts and different en-
vironmental aspects in addition to CO2 emissions, such as resource use, toxicity and
more. ISO 14040 describes the four phases of an LCA study. The objective of the
goal and scope definition phases is to determine the use and requirements of the study,
as well as define the system boundaries. The inventory analysis phase is the selection
of relevant data. The impact assessment phase and interpretation phase are the last
phases, which aim to better understand the environmental significance of the results.
As seen by Figure 5 LCA is made up of several phases describing the life cycle of a
building or product, from extraction of materials to end-of-life scenarios.

2.12
Life cycle costing
Economic sustainability is also an important aspect of building design, a way to assess
if an investment is economically responsible. To find whether or not this is the case,
a Life Cycle Costing (LCC) analysis can be performed. LCC analysis can be used to
calculate the total cost of building ownership, where both construction and operations
and maintenance can be considered. Thus, the cost of different material solutions or
systems may be compared over a predefined period of time, [Fuller, 2016]. These costs
are usually within the following categories:
• Initial investment costs
• Operation, maintenance, and repair costs
• Replacement costs
• Residual values - resale or salvage values or disposal costs
• Finance charges - loan interest payments
• Non-monetary benefits or costs
To add and compare the defined costs, the value of money has to be equal, both at
the point of investment, but also for future costs. When calculating an LCC, costs are
discounted to fit the theoretical value at a specific point in time to find the present
value. This is done by applying an interest rate, which discounts the value to a degree
that matches the project owner’s minimum acceptable rate of return. When all costs
have been identified and estimated throughout the pre-defined period of time and
recalculated to present value, the total life cycle costs can be derived from Equation
12,
LCC = I + Repl −Res + E + W + OMR + O,
(12)
where I is the present value (PV) investment costs, Repl is the PV capital replacement
costs, Res is the PV residual value, E is the PV of energy costs, W is the PV of water
costs, OMR is the PV of operating, maintenance and repairs, and O represents the PV
of other related costs.

As thermal comfort sensation is different depending on climate and building type,
different adaptive comfort models were used, see Section 2.5.2. The ’adaptive PMV’
was used for free-running buildings, while the ’newPMV’ was used for cooled buildings.
As both models were based on adaptive methodologies, they were applicable for hot
and humid climates. Results based on both models will be referred to as aPMV and
PPD.

3.5.4
Indoor CO2-concentration above 1000 ppm
Due to the incentive of natural ventilation through window openings, air quality could
be highly affected, in addition to being especially important for a hospital build-
ing. Consequently, air quality was assessed by the number of hours having a CO2-
concentration above 1000 ppm. This level was chosen based on recommendations from
various standards and studies, though no specific requirements of how much time should
be allowed above 1000 ppm were defined [Küçükhüseyin, 2021].
3.5.5
Risk of mould growth
In various buildings, indoor humidity is a significant factor that poses a risk to the
indoor environment and can lead to property damage, particularly through the growth
of mould. This contamination of indoor climate poses a significant concern, especially
in healthcare facilities where maintaining a clean and healthy environment is crucial.
The Finnish mould growth model, presented in Section 2.7 was used to assess the risk
of mould growth. Mould growth during the reference year used for the simulations was
assessed for all room surfaces. The highest recorded mould growth index value was
used as the evaluation criterion.
3.6
Evaluation of life cycle cost and life cycle assessment
In addition to assessing the indoor climate, each solution was evaluated based on its
sustainable and economic aspects. To achieve this, calculations were performed to esti-
mate both the Life Cycle Cost (LCC), which considers the monetary cost and the Life
Cycle Assessment (LCA), which evaluates the environmental impact. The assessment
period for these calculations was defined as 50 years, aligning with commonly used
practices for LCA and LCC calculations of buildings [DGNB, 2020].
Material quantities were derived from a Revit model using the building data obtained
from Mt. Darwin District Hospital. In the case of active solutions, the energy con-
sumption of the building was integrated into the analysis through the corresponding
IDA ICE model results. Despite Zimbabwe having a large amount of hydro-energy
which usually would imply a very sustainable grid, it was found that Zimbabwe faces
issues with running the systems due to increasing droughts [Mambondiyani, 2023]. This

The lifetime of each building material or product was estimated based on Danish

3.6.1
LCC evaluation
The total life cycle cost was estimated in EUR/m2 floor area by using the software
LCCbyg (v3.6.18), which allowed for easy incorporation of interest rates. The interest
rate used was 5% in general and 3.5% for electricity. The LCC results did not include
the residual value of a solution, in accordance with practices observed in the DGNB
certification system [DGNB, 2020], meaning that the value added from e.g. replace-
ments that exceed the assessment period is not subtracted. The data collected from
Zimbabwe included an electricity price of 0.214 EUR/kWh. The total life cycle cost
was estimated in EUR/m2 of floor area.
The construction material costs were based on local prices found online from either
Zimbabwe or South Africa with the exception of the air handling unit. As no appropri-
ate source for an AHU was found in Zimbabwe or South Africa, data were estimated
from a Danish manufacturer, see Appendix G. Labour prices were estimated based on
a percentage of the construction material cost. The total cost for a project was esti-
mated to be made of 70% material costs and 30% labour costs [Kamau, 2013]. Thus,
all material costs have been multiplied by 1.43 to account for labour costs.

5.2.1
Air handling unit system
The first active solution that was investigated was an air handling unit solution due to
the recommended high air change rate in hospitals. Overheating issues would not be
solved with a realistic amount of fresh air, pushed through a ductwork, as the outside
ambient temperature would be too high. Thus, a cooling coil was implemented in the
IDA ICE model for the air handling unit, with a COP of 3. This will cool the air down
to around 20◦C. However, it could cause problems since the ambient hot and humid air
would cause the supply air to reach the dew point. This would cause the indoor air to
be very humid, causing further discomfort and possible mould issues. Thus, dehumidi-
fication was implemented by cooling the air further down to 14 ◦C. This caused the air
to release water, if cooled past hitting its dewpoint, which would then be reheated by
using the heat generated by the compressor from the cooling coil with a heat exchanger.
The dehumidification process would only be active when the ambient air absolute hu-
midity was registered to be above 0.013 kg of water per kg of dry air as indicated by the
psychrometric chart showing the dehumidification design, see Appendix G, Figure G16.
An additional heat exchanger was installed on the incoming and outgoing air streams,
with an efficiency of 80%, lowering the energy consumption of the system as it was
able to reuse some of the cooling and heat up incoming air in the winter. Each room
was modelled with a supply inlet and an exhaust outlet, which would be connected
through ducts to an air handling unit, located in the attic or outside of the building.
The fans driving the airflow were modelled with a specific fan power of 1 kW/m3/s,
while the supply temperature was chosen based on minimum temperature differences
between design temperatures in winter and the rest of the year. Thus, the supply
temperature setpoint during the winter (June-August) was 21 ◦C. The rest of the year
had a supply temperature setpoint of 20 ◦C, which was the lowest allowed temperature
based on the temperature difference being 8◦C lower than the design maximum of 28
◦C. The maximum air change rate for the room was set to 3 h−1, which equalled 70
l/s. This value was recommended from The Center for Disease Control for hospitals
[CDC, 2003]. This could be held constant if wanted, but to reduce energy use, the min-
imum air change rate was set to 2 h−1 to counter accumulation of CO2-concentration
above 1000 ppm in the room based on Equation 7. To account for the reduced load
during the night, when the healthcare staff were not present, the fan power was reduced

Summary of results
The study compared combinations of active and passive measures in terms of their effect
on indoor climate, environmental impact and life cycle costs. Active Combinations (3
and 4), had much higher global warming potential and costs due to their high energy
consumption. However, they also provided very good thermal comfort. Additionally,
Combination 4 also had good air quality as well. The incorporation of photovoltaics

contrast to the combinations including active measures, Combination 1 had the lowest
impact and costs with minimal interventions.
Adding insulation in Combination 2

which could occur with Combination 1, and any mould issues. To avoid mould issues
for Combination 1, new ceiling boards could be implemented, which however would
double the costs and quadruple the environmental impact. However, both parameters
were still lower than using insulation (Combination 2). Passive measures generally had
a much lower environmental impact and lifecycle costs.

8
Discussion
Designing and renovating hospital buildings presents various challenges. In hot cli-
mates, the indoor temperatures in buildings can surpass outdoor temperatures, leading
to discomfort and potential health implications. The continuous occupancy in hospitals
limits the methods by which the building can be cooled. If no people were not present
at night, the building could be cooled below the comfort level by using cold ambient
air during the night, which could limit the accumulation of heat.
The studies conducted in this thesis focused on the Maternity Ward of Mt. Darwin Dis-
trict Hospital, where significant issues with overheating were found. Various measures
were explored to mitigate excessive overheating, though some impacted the remaining
evaluation criteria in a negative way. Consequently, it is important to consider the
trade-offs and interdependencies between different design measures to find the most
effective and balanced solutions. The findings of this thesis highlight the complexity
of addressing multiple performance factors in healthcare facilities and the need for
integrated approaches to optimize the indoor climate.
8.1
Individual and combinations of measures
All the measures were developed to reduce overheating. However, most also led to
issues regarding different aspects. CO2-concentration was heavily affected by the drop
in temperature, as not only the peak temperatures were reduced. Instead, the entire
range of temperature fluctuations was lowered, which led to reduced natural ventila-
tion, whenever temperatures were low. This was due to the window control and chosen
setpoints, which prevented the windows from opening if the outdoor temperatures were
below comfort levels. In some cases, the lower temperatures, typically occurring during
winter nights, could lead to potential mould growth, as the relative humidity was very
high.
This was especially true for Combination 1, as the temperature fluctuations between
day and night were very similar to the ones found in the baseline model, though the
average temperature was lowered because of the reflective paint reducing the heat en-
tering through the roof. The number of hours with a CO2-concentration above 1000
ppm was reduced by incorporating a small air change with ventilation grills, which
prevented the CO2-concentration from accumulating way past the setpoint. However,
Page 93 of 136
==PAGE:108==
8
Discussion
as the setpoint for the window control was defined at 3000 ppm, the number of hours
above 1000 ppm was still very high. Most combinations experienced some time with
poor air quality, except when implementing an air handling unit to actively ventilate
the room with fresh air. Having windows with a larger effective opening area increased
the natural ventilation rate, though not to an extent that prevented poor air quality.
Furthermore, whenever overheating was reduced in the north-oriented room, the south-
oriented room was prone to high CO2 and a high mould growth index value, since
temperatures were generally found to be lower when facing south because of fewer
external heat loads. As the main focus was to reduce overheating, the north-oriented
room was chosen as the basis of the study due to higher temperatures. Consequently,
the north-oriented room was used to develop the building design measures, whereas
the south-oriented room was solely used to check the effects of the given measure. By
including the results of the south-oriented room at first, the design decisions might
have been different to optimise both orientations at the same time.
Implementing insulation gave the opportunity to minimize temperature fluctuations
between day and night, which would prevent the winters from getting too cold, min-
imise heat transfer and allow windows to stay open for longer periods. Despite some
of the heat accumulating, the indoor climate was improved and also resulted in less
energy consumption when incorporated into the active measures. Additionally, shades
were initially considered important for all solutions to reduce overheating. However,
due to the position of the sun, an overhang was only implemented to reduce energy
consumption in active combinations, as it only prevented additional heat gains during
the winter period.
The two active combinations used cooling coils to cool the supply air, though the supply
air in the air conditioner was recirculated air from the room, whereas the air handling
unit provided cooled fresh air. Of the tested solutions, these systems were the only
ones to provide the necessary cooling to comply with the PPD requirements for IEQ
category III (<15%). In general, the active solutions proved very efficient in removing
overheating with no hours with temperatures above 32◦C and only a small number
of hours outside of the allowed range in IEQ category III, due to high temperatures.
However, by using the air handling unit (Combination 4) the south-oriented room
was outside of the category limits for almost a sixth of all hours in a year, which was

==PAGE:109==

largely due to cool temperatures during the winter period. Thus, the supply air was po-
tentially cooling the room unnecessarily, increasing the energy consumption of the unit.
8.1.1
Disregarded measures
Additional measures than those tested in this study were initially thought of as having
good properties and effects for cooling a building. However, they were disregarded for
various reasons.
Ceiling fans were investigated, which would have increased the air velocity in the room
and reduced the perceived air temperature, by increasing the convection heat transfer
of the occupant’s skin. Additionally, on hot days it could also feature a misting spray
of water, which would increase evaporation from the skin. However, increasing the air
velocity and spreading water droplets could spread germs and viruses more efficiently,
which was not ideal in a healthcare setting [Wong and Chong, 2010].
Furthermore, by installing a duct in the ground, the ground’s thermal properties could
cool the supplied air. A method which does not require large amounts of excavating
would be to implement an air supply duct with a fan, which would go through the
ground before entering the building. This could potentially cool the indoor operative
temperature without using additional power to cool the air. However, it also features
some downsides. The most prominent is the risk of mould growth in the ground duct,
which would then be supplied indoors, and put sensitive patients and staff at further
risk [Bergsøe and SBI, 2012].
Finally, a solar chimney was investigated. But as the building is only one floor, the
chimney would have had to be very high compared to the building. The structure
would have to be changed to accommodate such, and due to the building layout many
solar chimneys would be required, as the rooms were not stacked, resulting high costs
and reduced efficiency.
8.2
Sources of error in the models
The accuracy of the findings heavily relies on the quality and accuracy of the input used
for the simulation model. Without any on-site indoor climate measurements, there was
Page 95 of 136
==PAGE:110==
8
Discussion
no way to validate the results. Consequently, the actual temperatures experienced in
practice may vary from the simulated results, though the general tendency of the find-
ings should provide a fairly accurate picture of causes and solutions.
Based on the received data, it was assumed that the building would be occupied both
day and night, which was further supported by the fact that babies are born anytime.
With two beds in the labour room, two occupants were defined as being present at all
hours of the day, while a single healthcare staff would occupy the room for half a work-
day on average. Given the size of the building and the reality of occupying a labour
room 24/7, this might result in a vast overestimation of the actual internal heat loads.
If the occupancy had been reduced, it would have been easier to reduce overheating,
while cool winter nights would have gotten cooler.
Trying to imitate user behaviour with only two parameters (Operative temperature
and CO2) creates a lot of uncertainties as different physical and psychological aspects
also contribute. Actions related to ventilation are neither a reflex nor a rational act
[Fabi et al., 2012] and it is unlikely that people would open and close their windows
to keep the desired air quality setpoint. During the day, it might not be comfortable
for the occupants to have the windows open all the time due to health, privacy or too
high air velocity. Furthermore, the control in IDA ICE opens the windows gradually,
as it tries to keep the setpoint. A lot of variables not included in the study affect
user behaviour, and thus the results represent the indoor environment under strictly
defined conditions. Despite the uncertainties, the results still indicate that poor air
quality was likely to occur. Therefore, the sensitivity of the setpoint was tested with
1000 and 3000 ppm. This only changed the temperature slightly, though air quality
was considerably improved. In retrospect, a setpoint of 2000 ppm might have been a
better estimate.
Furthermore, the overheating of the building was mainly driven by sun irradiance,
which the reflectance and emissivity of the materials decreased. These values were
estimated based on generic values but might not be entirely correct. The values made
a big difference in the external heat load and especially affected the baseline model,
which had a high heat transfer through the envelope.

8.3
Thermal comfort for patients and healthcare staff
Despite the different evaluation methods used between active and passive solutions,
it was evident that patients and healthcare staff have different preferences regarding
thermal comfort, due to their distinct roles and needs within the healthcare environ-
ment. Their different preferences were defined through a higher clothing insulation
(clo) interval for the patients and a higher activity level (met) for the healthcare staff.
From the patient’s perspective, a slightly warmer temperature may be preferred to
reduce discomfort.
Both patients and staff may also value improved air quality to
promote respiratory health, reduce the risk of infections and provide a better work
environment for the staff which should result in fewer mistakes. Additionally, occu-
pants may appreciate personalized control over their immediate surroundings, such as
opening windows to get fresh air. Healthcare staff need an indoor climate to support
their work efficiency, concentration and overall well-being. Temperature control plays
a significant role, as the staff may be engaged in physically demanding tasks, wearing
special equipment or working in different hospital areas with varying environmental
conditions. Due to this, the staff might prefer a cooler environment than patients,
with ventilation and air quality being crucial for maintaining a healthy and safe work-
ing environment, reducing the spread of airborne pathogens and minimizing odours.
While patients generally prioritize their own comfort and well-being, healthcare staff
need an environment that facilitates their duties and ensures optimal conditions for
patient care.
Providing a satisfactory indoor climate, balanced between the different preferences
between patients and staff, proved to be a challenge. Having the patients present both
day and night was another aspect that complicated the design, because of the large
temperature fluctuations. This was especially true when there was no insulation, as
removing the excessive heat during the day further reduced the temperature at night,
increasing the number of hours outside of IEQ category III. Though both IEQ category
III temperatures and PPD were often improved simultaneously, this was not always the
case. When comparing the baseline model and Combination 2, the number of hours
outside of IEQ category III temperatures was reduced by 86% and a reduction of 20
percentage points for patients, while the PPD for the healthcare staff was only reduced
by less than 4 percentage points, due to the temperatures still being too high.

8.4
Evaluation criteria review
The evaluation criteria, presented in Section 3.5, were defined to allow comparisons of
solutions with large variations. The baseline model suggested severe overheating with
little to no mitigation interventions. By testing a variety of measures that significantly
reduced the average operative temperature, more ambitious evaluation criteria were
necessary, and thermal comfort criteria were added based on commonly used indoor
climate requirements e.g. ISO 7730 [ISO, 2005a]. Using the comfort indices PMV and
PPD, newer methods were used to better fit the climate conditions in Zimbabwe. The
PPD of the different combinations were compared one to one, even though two different
adaptive thermal comfort models had been used for free-running buildings (adaptive
PMV) and actively cooled buildings (new PMV).
In itself, this was not a concern because expectations for free-running buildings are
generally found to be lower and are incorporated into the method. However, when
applying the same data to the two methods, this expected result was not found. When
using the baseline model (free-running) to compare the different methods, the resulting
PPD for the newPMV method was notably lower. The percentage of hours with a PPD
above 15% for the patients was 78% using the regular PMV method, 67% using the
adaptive PMV method, and 33% using the newPMV method. This difference indicated
that the expectations of thermal comfort should be lower in actively cooled buildings,
contrary to what was expected and suggested by the literature [Yao et al., 2009].
Furthermore, the reduction in the percentage of hours above the PPD limit (regular
PMV to newPMV) was quite high (-45%). If the adaptive method for free-running
buildings had been used on Combination 4 (AHU), the percentage of hours above the
PPD limit would have been 5%, whereas the newPMV method resulted in 0% with
an average PPD of 6.3%. This would explain the large difference in thermal comfort
between the passive and active combinations, all of which might suggest an error in
the newPMV model or in the incorporation thereof. The expected results of occupants
having higher thermal expectations of actively cooled buildings were supported by the
IEQ thermal comfort temperatures, which are less strict for free-running buildings.
The high humidity of the outdoor air led to the use of The Finnish mould growth
index. By using the index, the problematic moisture conditions and causes could be
Page 98 of 136

found, and thus be mitigated. It would not have been so definite if only the indoor rel-

However, it could not specify the potential health implications caused by particles in

8.5
Sustainability of the solutions
A lot of assumptions were made for both the LCA and LCC. The processes used in
the LCA were mostly from a library of ’Global’ processes, meaning that the climate
impact related to each process was derived from a global average. This creates even
more uncertainties for the results, as the production of construction materials in Zim-
babwe might deviate from this.
Furthermore, because of the high energy demand, the active combinations had a much
higher global warming potential than the passive combinations. The results were es-
pecially high due to the chosen calculation method, which did not take future energy
production into consideration. In sustainability certifications such as DGNB, energy
is included with a projection of the environmental impact of future energy production
[The Danish Energy Agency, 2020]. These projections have a big impact on the global
warming potential targets of different countries. For instance, the new Danish build-
ing code requires a GWP below 12 kg CO2-eq/m2/yr [Bolig- og planstyrelsen, 2022].
As projections were not used for the LCA in this study, comparisons to such targets
can not be made, as the global warming potential would have been reduced almost
70% when using the Danish projections. It can be difficult to predict future energy
production, especially considering the current state of the grid in Zimbabwe. Thus,
projections could have led the results to appear much more sustainable, based on pro-
jections in countries with more developed electricity grids. However, it might also lead
to an overestimation of the total life cycle environmental impacts, as the actual energy
production should be improved going forward, based on Zimbabwe’s National Climate
Change Response Strategy [Ministry of Environments Water and Climate, 2016].

solution would require. Due to the cost of labour being calculated as a percentage
of the cost of materials, a very expensive material will also be very expensive in cost
of labour. While this is generally true, it might not be as linear as this assumption
describes. Additionally, the sources used for prices were online stores from both Zim-
babwe and South Africa. Consequently, the overall cost was a major uncertainty in the
study, as the prices could not be validated and seemed very low. The interest rates used
were also subject to uncertainty, as it is impossible to predict economic development.
Thus, an estimate was used instead.
As the LCC and LCA calculations have been made, one can access each combination
and compare. However, this does not mean that the combination with the least envi-
ronmental impact is sustainable. Determining whether a solution is truly sustainable
is difficult, as a plethora of factors can be involved. One approach could be to evaluate
what amount of positive measures it brings to the environment and if it is within the
allocated amount of resources available. Absolute sustainability tries to allocate how
much of a given resource can be used for a given quantity of output, e.g. a building in
Zimbabwe providing healthcare for a given amount of population [Hauschild, 2022].
8.5.1
The potential of photovoltaics
The addition of a photovoltaic system for active systems can have both economic and
environmental benefits. Zimbabwe can utilize a lot of solar power because of its lo-
cation. If a battery is added to a photovoltaic system, the power can be used more
effectively both day and night. Furthermore, a battery would act as a power backup
during the frequent power outages. As photovoltaics (PVs) become more popular, it
could help resolve Zimbabwe’s energy problem in a sustainable way. Additionally, other
solar-powered equipment could be very beneficial, e.g. generating hot water from a so-
lar thermal panel.
Incorporating PVs in Combination 4 with a battery was essential to be able to cover
the energy use during the night. For Combination 3 (air conditioners), PVs would also
be beneficial, even though the energy consumption is lower. Consequently, the solar
system would be smaller and a battery would not provide the same benefits, as the
energy is generated and used simultaneously.

==PAGE:115==
8
Discussion
The improvements in environmental impact will be slightly reduced over time, as the
electricity grid in Zimbabwe becomes more sustainable. However, incorporating PVs is
essential for the feasibility of any active solutions due to regular power outages.
8.6
Climate scenarios
As previously described, extreme weather events were removed from the weather files
to best represent the average weather conditions. Consequently, the increase in heat
waves and drought are not included in the data.
When using the future climate data, an increase in overheating was found, which was
greater for the baseline model than for the combinations. This suggests that by not
implementing any measures, overheating will be relatively worse. This was seen as the
number of hours above 32◦rose by 15% compared to 7% for Combinations 1 and 2. As
the findings were based on projections, uncertainties of the results were higher than
for simulations using weather station data. A lot of assumptions had to be made for
the methodology to predict future weather scenarios. Furthermore, the analysis of the
future scenario was entirely based on simulations using weather data from Harare and
comparisons can not be made to Mt. Darwin one to one. Despite this, the results still
highlight a tendency that should be similar between the two locations.
Furthermore, the increase in cooling demand to avoid overheating aligns well with the
projection of increased energy consumption for cooling globally. Consequently, passive
measures could be an efficient way to reduce energy consumption and avoid unnecessary
carbon emissions.
8.7
Additional measures to consider
This study included eight different measures which were each evaluated, and multiple
combinations were found to have a very positive influence in reducing the overheating
issue for the building. Some measures were not included due to being unfit for the case
or going beyond the scope of the study.
An issue with the air conditioners included in Combination 3 is that they do not bring
in fresh air. A potential solution could be a window AC or another type of de-central
air conditioner which also brings in a part of fresh air, instead of only relying on recir-
culation.
Page 101 of 136
==PAGE:116==
8
Discussion
To bring in some part of the passive exhaust of the hottest air of the zone, at the ceiling
level, was to implement the ventilation grills in the exterior wall. However, a possible
improvement could be to implement ventilation shafts going through the attic and out
above the roof. The top of the shaft could feature a turbine which would drive the air
up and out, when driven by wind, in addition to the buoyancy effect it would create.
8.8
Context for future building development
This study provides a good indication of possible mitigation interventions that can be
implemented in buildings in hot and humid climates to improve the indoor climate,
especially in regard to overheating. The evaluated composition of measures each pro-
vided different levels of improvement, cost and environmental impact. To prevent the
increasing health implications from overheating, similar buildings in hot and humid cli-
mates could benefit from the findings in this study. Installing an air conditioner might
be the single measure that reduces overheating the most, however, it comes with a
large lifecycle impact and cost. The necessity for more sustainable building operations
is crucial for reducing global environmental impacts and highlights the importance
of considering passive measures, renewable energy sources and the entire lifecycle of
buildings. Most of the passive measures were simple and relatively cheap, and can also
be considered for smaller buildings and budgets.

9
Conclusion
9
Conclusion
The research conducted in this study aimed to investigate possible mitigation interven-
tions to reduce overheating in healthcare facilities in subtropical climates. As a part of
the HIGH Horizons project, the study focused on heat exposure of healthcare staff and
pregnant and postpartum women. Thus, data were provided from the Maternity Ward
at Mt. Darwin District Hospital in Zimbabwe, which was used to create a building
information model and an indoor climate and energy simulation model.
The establishment of evaluation criteria provided a framework for assessing the success
of different strategies in achieving acceptable conditions within the indoor environ-
ment, as well as determining the effects on life cycle costs and environmental impacts.
The criteria evaluated overheating and additional indoor climate parameters to further
investigate the impacts of different measures. However, criteria regarding predicted
thermal comfort proved difficult to assess correctly in hot and humid climates.
The results for the current state of the building showed that extreme overheating would
occur frequently, with 41% of the year having an indoor operative temperature above
32◦C. The sun was the driving factor, heating up the roof and attic space to extreme
temperatures, which had a direct impact on the rooms below.
The study found that only a combination of measures could yield satisfactory results
in reducing overheating and improving indoor climate. Implementing a single measure
proved insufficient in improving across all evaluation criteria, highlighting the com-
plexity of the problem. Through numerous tests and analyses, four combinations of
measures were highlighted as effective in reducing overheating. Two combinations of
passive measures, and two with one active and multiple passive measures.
Upon evaluating the findings, it became evident that establishing a solid foundation
of passive measures is imperative, as most of them can be integrated relatively easily,
compared to their effect. With only passive measures, it was possible to reduce over-
heating to almost no time with temperatures above the health-risk limit and to comply
with IEQ thermal comfort category III.
The incorporation of an active system can provide exceptional indoor conditions.
By combining an air conditioner with passive measures, good thermal comfort was
Page 104 of 136
==PAGE:119==
9
Conclusion
achieved. However, achieving both good thermal comfort and air quality simultane-
ously, required a ventilation system. As the associated costs and environmental impacts
of the active measures were considerably greater than the passive, the implementation
of photovoltaics should be considered.
Furthermore, the study identified varying levels of thermal comfort among the differ-
ent occupant groups in the building. Due to their activity level and limited presence
during the cooler hours of the day, the healthcare staff demonstrated a higher suscepti-
bility to thermal discomfort in response to elevated temperatures. Conversely, patients
remained in the building day and night with a low activity level, while being able to
adjust their clothing insulation to a greater extent. Achieving a balance between ade-
quate cooling during the day and warmth during the night proved challenging. Despite
this, the study indicated that the implemented measures had a more significant posi-
tive effect on the comfort of the patients than the healthcare staff.
Given the increasing, and already high temperatures, there is a pressing need to enhance
the indoor environment within healthcare facilities in hot climates. The projection for
the year 2080 showed a 15 percentage point increase in yearly hours above 32◦C for
the current building conditions. However, by implementing any of the four presented
combinations, future overheating was diminished. Combinations incorporating active
measures demonstrated minimal change compared to the current achievable indoor
climate, though consuming a significantly higher amount of energy.

M
Photovoltaic calculations for inclusion in Combination 4
To dimension the photovoltaic system, the energy consumption over the year was found
for each month. Then in order to not have an overproduction on a day-to-day basis,
the amount of installed power had to be limited to around when a daily overproduction
would happen.
To find how much power would be able to be generated a standard 400 Wp panel was
used, with each panel having a cost of 292 EUR, excluding VAT, but including a 20%
price increase to take the connections and inverters into account, and also including
labour price [MIKMA, 2023a].
A tool was used to see how much a panel will generate at this location and position,
for both a north and south-facing panel:
Figure M25: Photovoltaic electricity production for a north and a south facing 400 Wp panel on the
Maternity building. Including a 14% system loss.
Therefore 1 panel, located on the north-orienting 19.3 degree roof slope will generate
658 kWh per year.
The least energy-intensive months are June, July, and August, averaging an electricity
consumption of 769 kWh per month. For these months each PV panel will average an
electricity production of 56.7 kWh per month. Thus the minimum amount of panels
needed can be found: 769 kWh / 56.7 kWh = 13.5

To allow a slight overproduction, to get more electricity covered, the amount is rounded
up to 16 panels, to get a number better suited for a grid, but still allowing very little
overproduction, See Figure 37.
Each panel is 1.134x1.722 m, which corresponds to 1.95 m2. Thus, this system will
use:
16*1.95 m2 = 31.2 m2
of the roof surface
In order to also be able to use the energy during the night, a battery, a battery has to be
used. The battery size is found by estimating the nightly energy use. This calculation is
based on 1/3 of the average power to run the air handling unit without cooling for a day.
The percentage of consumption used for running the AHU, without cooling: (4.523kWh
+ 21.36kWh) / 21.36kWh = 83%
The electricity consumption for June for the AHU per day:
(763 kWh * 0.83) / 30 = 20.98 kWh per day.
Estimating 1/3 of the use for the night:
20.98 kWh / 3 = 6.99 kWh
Thus a standard house battery of 6.5 kWh capacity, with a cost of 3754 EUR including
labour, and excl. VAT, was used [MIKMA, 2023b].
This system will generate 10528 kWh over a year, of which 10112 kWh, is estimated
to be able to be used. This amounts to roughly 40% of the total energy used by the
AHU incl. cooling.
Energy sold back to the grid is not included in the LCC or LCA calculations, which
only amounts to 416 kWh yearly and is thus negligible.
