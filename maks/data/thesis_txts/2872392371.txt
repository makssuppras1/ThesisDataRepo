Abstract
To achieve zero GHG emissions when producing fertilizers and fuels for the maritime
industry a PowerбґtoбґAmmonia plant powered by renewable energy sources is essential.
The ammonia production using green hydrogen from electrolysis and nitrogen obtained
from the air is modeled and simulated in DWSim.
The designed process leads to a production of 100 tones of ammonia per day. The proбґ
cess achieves a 27 % singleбґpass and 98 % overall conversion while using 8.6 MWh tБ┬▓1,
which most of it is drawn by the electrolyser and internally recovering most lowбґgrade
heat.
Technoбґeconomic analysis estimates total CAPEX at US$ 74 M and annual OPEX at
US$ 30 M, 92 % of which is electricity. Assuming an NH3 price of US$ 800 tБ┬▓1, the
plant delivers a 14.5% ROI and a 5.4бґyear payбґback, yet shows a negative NPV at a
20% discount rate, highlighting the need for cheaper green power and electrolysers. The
design matches greyбґammonia energy efficiency, but profitability hinges on further cost
declines and premium markets for lowбґcarbon NH3.
Therefore, the production of ammonia using renewables sources is possible alternative
to the traditional path. However, in terms of profitability the point hasnБ─≥t been reached yet.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Ammonia (NH3) is among the most widely produced and utilized chemicals globally beбґ
cause of its critical role in various industries. It is primarily used in the production of fertilбґ
izers, but is also has applications in refrigeration, pharmaceuticals and water treatment,
etc. Approximately, 80% of the Ammonia produced worldwide is used in agriculture as a
fertilizer and the remaining 20% is used in other industrial processes.
The synthesis of ammonia is considered one of the most significant achievements in inбґ
dustrial chemistry. The pracess was first developed in the early 20th century, commonly
known as the HaberбґBosch, has been improved over time. However, the conventional
method has a substantional enviromental impact, as the technique applied is steam reбґ
forming of fossil fuels such as matural gas or coal. Due to this approach the Ammonia
synthesis is responsible for 1% of the global CO2 emissions, making it a notable contribбґ
utor to the climate change.
In light of the targets set by the 2015 Paris Agreement to reduce greenhouse gas emisбґ
sions, alternative methods for ammonia production are being studied. One of the exбґ
plored possibilities involves the use of renewable Hydrogen and Nitrogen, called Powerбґ
toбґAmmonia, which yields Green Ammonia. In addition to its role in decarbonizing indusбґ
trial processes, ammonia is also being considered as a lowбґcarbon fuel for the maritime
transport as it has easier storage, high volumetric energy density and existing infrastrucбґ
ture.
1.2
Objective
The main purpose of this thesis is to simulate and design a PowerбґtoбґAmmonia plant and
also do an economic evaluation of it. This involves the following steps:
Б─╒ Review technologies used for the different units of the plant
Б─╒ Model and simulate a PowerбґtoбґAmmonia plant on DWSim
Б─╒ Economical evaluation of the plant
1.3
Scope
This thesis focuses in small to medium scale plants as it is wanted t be done the more
realistic possible, and it is not possible to make anything comparable on size with the
traditional Ammonia plants. One of the main reasons behind is because the PtA plants
are powered by renewable energy sources such as wind and solar. The scope of the
thesis are the following:
Б─╒ Air separation unit, electrolysis and ammonia synthesis
Б─╒ Process flow diagrams of each unit
Б─╒ Mass and energy balances

2.1
Hydrogen production
Hydrogen can be obtained through different methods and from different sources. Hydroбґ
gen is a clean and nonбґtoxic energy source however hydrogen production processes are
not so environmentally friendly. Therefore, hydrogen can be classified into three different
colors depending on the feedstock, energy inputs and waste emissions during its whole
life cycle. The colors used are grey, blue and green. So, grey Hydrogen is the one that
uses fossil fuels as a feedstock but if there is CO2 capturing and storing unit is added
then that Hydrogen is called blue. Lastly, the green Hydrogen is the one that relays in
renewable energy to be produced.[1]
Traditionally, fossil fuels like natural gas, oil or coal have been used as the primary feedбґ
stock for hydrogen production. The techniques used with these feedstock to produce the
hydrogen are steam methane reforming (SMR), oil reforming and coal gasification. Even
though electrolysis has gained some weight in the recent years, the conventional routes
have remained dominant. There is a growing interest in producing hydrogen using renewбґ
able energy sources (RES). Despite the interest, these new technologies often struggle
to compete when their costs and efficiencies are not equal to traditional process. [1] [2]
Water electrolysis is an electrochemical process. The water is separated into H2 and
O2, they are produced at the cathode and anode respectively. They are immersed in the
electrolytes and when the electrical current is applied the separation takes place. This
technique allows to produce high purity H2 as there is no presence of carbons and sulfur.
[3]
This technology allows the hydrogen to be produced close to the users due to the comбґ
pactness needed and it helps on losing dependency on third countries. Electrolysis is
an energy intensive process but it can be coupled with RES to achieve green hydrogen.
Since grid electricity is not necessarily derived from renewable energy, there is no assurбґ
ance of clean energy input. [3]
There are different electrolyzers currently in the market and under development that can
be used[4][3] :
Б─╒ Alkaline Electrolysis (AEL)
Б─╒ Polymer electrolyte Membrane (PEM)
Б─╒ Solid Oxide Electrolysis (SOEL)

Polymer electrolyte Membrane
In 1966 a new electrolyzer was developed, it was done to overcome the drawbacks from
the AEL, the new technology was named as Polymer Electrolyte Membrane (PEM). It
was invented by Grubb in the early 1950Б─≥s and later in 1966 it was developed by General
electrics Co. This technology highlights because it has a solid polysulfonated membrane
as an electrolyte. [6]
PEM differs from the AEL because the water is fed into the anode only, where it is split
into oxygen (O2), protons (H+) and electrons (eБ┬▓).These protons travel via proton conбґ
ducting membrane to the cathode side. The electrons leave the anode and pass through
the external power circuit, which supplies the driving force (cell voltage) needed for the
reaction at the cathode side the protons and electrons recombine to produce hydrogen.
Hydrogen and oxygen are separated by the membrane and therefore the gases are sent
to further treatment and storage. Equations (2.7) and (2.8) show the chemical reactions
taking place and in figure 2.2 a representation of the PEM electrolyzer can be seen.[8]

Cryogenic distillation
Cryogenic distillation is a separation process that utilizes extremely low temperatures to
liquefy and separate components of gas mixtures based on their boiling points. Comбґ

production of high purity gases like oxygen, nitrogen and argon [15]. The process opбґ

Air compression and preбґtreatment
The preбґtreatment stage is critical in cryogenic distillation, even more in ASU, because
impurities like water vapor, CO2, and hydrocarbons can freeze at cryogenic conditions
and clog or damage the distillation columns and heat exchangers. This way it is ensured
that only clean, dry air enters the cold section of the process.
1. Filtration and compression: Ambient air is captured and passed through fine filters to
remove dust, particulates and aerosols. Then it is compressed in multiбґstage comбґ
pressors with interбґstage cooling to remove the heat generated during compression.
This step purpose is to remove physical impurities and increase the pressure for a
more efficient downstream cooling and liquefaction [17].
2. Afterбґcooling: after compression, the air is cooled in afterбґcoolers to condense and
remove much of the moisture.
3. Removal of water vapor and CO2: this is a critical step. Where usually knockбґout
drums or demisters are used to separate the water vapor from the air and for the
removal of CO2 and hydrocarbons the most common technology is molecular sieve
adsorbers [18].
Heat exchange
The main heat exchanger is a critical unit in the ASU. It accomplishes a thermal energy
exchange between the incoming warm stream to the distillation unit and the products
of the separation unit. This allows the system to reach the extremely low temperatures
required to be working in cryogenic conditions.
The heat exchanger cools down the compressed inlet to near its liquefaction temperature
and minimizes the external energy input into the system. As using the products streams
from the distillation columns leave colder and with the exchange of heat there is no need
of external energy.
Distillation column
The distillation columns are the core of equipment and process happening inside the ASU
process. They are used to obtain separated streams of nitrogen, oxygen and in some
cases argon. The process is based in the differences in their boiling temperatures under
cryogenic conditions.
Alternative designs on the distillation columns for cryogenic air separation units have been
reported in literature differentiating between 1, 2 or 3 columns. The 1 column design is
the simplest design to produce highбґpurity oxygen and nitrogen from air. However, it is
rarely used in industrial practice. The threeбґcolumn air separation process is a relatively
complex design and employed only when a part from highбґpurity oxygen and nitrogen,
separation of argon is also required. The most widely used design in industrial scale is
based on 2 columns. They are operated at different pressures, the first one operates
at high pressure so it is named after this like High Pressure Column, HPC. The second
column operates at lower pressure which leads to its name Low Pressure Column (LPC)
[16].
In this configuration the air is compressed up to 5бґ6.5 bar as its the pressure operation
in the HPC. In the HPC nitrogenбґrich vapor ascends to the top, while oxygenбґenriched
liquid collects at the bottom. This oxygenбґrich stream is then circulated to the LPC, which
operates at 1.2бґ1.3 bar, where further separation takes place obtaining a much purer
oxygen stream at the bottom and pure nitrogen at the top [16] [17].

Pressure Swing Adsorption (PSA) is a nonбґcryogenic process used widely to separate

cryogenic distillation, PSA operates at nearбґambient temperatures and uses the different
speed of adsorption to separate the nitrogen from the rest of components[19].

process. This phase uses part of the nitrogen separated to detach the oxygen molecules
and carries them to a new adsorption phase. As a consequence, the filter is cleaned and
able to go to a next adsorption phase. [20][21]
To ensure that it is a continuous process it is always designed with at least two adsorption
columns, where they would alternate their position between adsorbing and regeneration.

Ammonia reactor type
As said previously the HaberбґBosch process is highly exothermic, thus managing heat is
crucial for optimal reactor performance. To prevent overheating of the catalyst and to drive
the reaction towards higher ammonia yields, lower temperatures favor the equilibrium,
ammonia converters use a multiбґbed design with interбґcooling. The most common design
are [32] [33]:
Б─╒ Adiabatic Quench Cooled Reactor (AQCR): it uses multiple adiabatic catalysts beds,
and the cooling is accomplished by quenching, in other words injecting cold fresh
feed reaction gas between beds to lower the temperature of the mixture after the
bed [32] [33].
Б─╒ Adiabatic Air Cooled Reactors: as the previous design this one also uses many
catalytic beds but uses indirect cooling between them. The cooling technique in this
reactor is done by using heat exchangers, the hot reaction gas is cooled down with
a fraction of the initial cold feed. The beds are adiabatic, which means that there is
no heat removed within the bed [32] [33].

Both designs aim to control the reaction temperature profile and to improve the single
pass conversion. Below a comparison between these two reactors would be done in
the following topics: Heat management, catalyst utilization, conversion efficiency, design
complexity and industrial use.
Heat Management Strategy
In the AQCR the quench gas absorbs heat by mixing, this lowers the temperature of the
combined stream that enters the next catalyst bed. In this case, the heat is not removed
from the stream but rather redistributed. The direct cooling method, is simpler as no
additional equipment is needed.On the other hand, the degree of cooling achievable is
limited by the temperature and amount of the quench gases injected [33].
In the AICR the heat removal is obtained though indirect cooling between the beds by
using a heat exchanger. After each bed, the hot stream exits and enters the heat exбґ
changer before entering the next bed. This strategy removes heat from the hot stream
and is recovered by a cold fraction of the feed [33].
The key difference between them is that the AICR removes the heat indirectly via the heat
exchanger. But, AQCR retains the heat within the gas by mixing it with cooler gas. The
indirect cooling on the AICR can achieve a larger temperature drop than by quenching cold
gases. Since quench cooling is limited by the quench gas temperature and the feasible
quench ratio [33].
Catalyst Utilization and performance
In the ACQR systems, the feed gas is splitted among the beds. As a fraction of the feed
skips the first bed and another fraction skips the first two beds, meaning that fraction of gas
that goes through the beds only see the fractions that go in and not the added afterwards
[32]. This means that there would be beds that do not encounter the whole feed stream,
which can be seen as a less efficient use of catalyst. Overall the AQCR tends to obtain
a lower conversion per unit of catalyst of the total feed as part of the catalyst capacity is
bypassed by a fraction of the feed. Due to the high temperatures in the AQCR catalysts life
can shorten and need more frequent rejuvenation [33][24]. In this design some catalysts
regions might be hotter than others, leading to a nonбґuniform aging.
In the AICR design all the feed goes through every catalyst bed. Which means that every
catalyst is used for the entire flow. The distribution of bedБ─≥s catalyst is different for each
Process design, simulation and technoбґeconomic analysis of a PowerбґtoбґAmmonia plant
20
==PAGE:30==
bed, as the feed for each is not the same in each case. Therefore, usually the first bed has
a smaller volume, while the later have a larger volume to handle lower reactions rates. In
this design the utilization of catalysts is more effective as all the flow goes through every
catalyst. Also, the more controlled temperature in the AICR helps keep the catalyst in a
favorable temperature window. Because if catalysts are working in excessive temperaбґ
tures it will deactivate faster, thus indirect cooling contributes to a consistent and longer
performance of the catalyst.[32][33][24]
Conversion performance
Conversion per pass is the most direct metric to know the effectiveness of each design.
AICR achieves a higher percent conversion of H2 and N2 compared to ACQR under similar
conditions.[32][33][34]
Many studies have compared these deigns[32][33][34] [35], and it has shown that in the
AICR a 30% conversion of reactants can be achieved but the AQCR would reach a 20%
conversion. The conversion rate per single pass is significantly lower in the AQCR. The
reasons behind the lower conversion rate of the AQCR are for example that they have
higher temperatures and just a fraction of the feed gets to react in all beds.
On the following paper [34] it was found evidences to suggest that AICR benefits more
from load variations, which results in a smaller bed, allowing even lower temperatures
and higher equilibrium conversions. Whereas the AQCRБ─≥s conversion does not improve
as much when load is reduced. This means that the AICR is better at capitalizing on
improved equilibrium conditions when given the opportunity, however the quench designБ─≥s
inherent limitations still cap its performance even at lower rates.[34]
Reactor design complexity
AQCR is mechanically simpler, since all the cooling is done by injecting cold gas streams.
So, there are no heat exchanger connection between beds. This reactors consists of a
single pressure shell with multiple beds with spacer gaps for injections. This simplicity
means fewer highбґpressure joints and no internal tubing for heat exchangers, thus imбґ
proves the reliability and reduce fabrication difficulty. Designing a AQCR also means a
lower cost as it doesnБ─≥t have to design heat exchangers just distributor nozzles for quench
gas.[24][33]
When incorporating the interбґbed heat exchangers makes the reactor system more comбґ
plex mechanically and operational. Each bed has an internal heat exchanger or the reбґ
actor outlet has to be forwarded to an external heat exchanger and back in. These heat
exchanger must stand high pressures which is challenging engineering wise. Moreover,
as a consequence of adding more equipment the capital cost of the reactor is much bigбґ
ger.[24][33]
Industrial relevance
Both designs have been employed in the ammonia industry, but there has been a shift
due to technological advances and upбґscaling of plants.
In the early stages, it was simple design for the quench type due to equipment limitations.
In the 1960sбґ1970s where using the ACQR design because they would avoid the comбґ
plexity that AICR brings. These designs proved an effective and easier construct with the
means of the time.
When the old plants updated and modern plants were also constructed, energy efficiency
was important and exchanger technology improved. This cause that the AICR where the
standard reactor in large scale ammonia plants. Today, most of the worldбґscale ammonia
reactors ar AICR, furthermore industry data shows that the indirect cooling are preferred
for large scale as a consequence of their efficiency.

3.2
Thermodynamic models
The selection of the thermodynamic models is fundamental for an accurate simulation, esбґ
pecially in systems involving phase changes, high pressures or nonбґideal behavior. Each
unit of the PtA design has unique thermodynamic requirements based on the nature of
the chemical compounds involved and operating conditions. Thus, different equations of
state (EOS) were selected for each unit, since a reliable prediction wants to be obtained.
In this work, EOS were chosen over activity coefficient models due to the wide range of
pressures and temperatures across the different units. EOS models provide consistent
framework for predicting thermodynamic properties across gas, liquid and twoбґphase sysбґ
tems. Which is essential for simulating ASU, electrolysis and ammonia synthesis. While
activity coefficients models are well suited for nonбґideal liquid mixtures at low pressures,
they lack of robustness and applicability across this process conditions. Different EOS
were selected for each unit, since a reliable prediction wants to be obtained.
3.2.1
Thermodynamic modelбґASU
For cryogenic air separation, the PengбґRobinson (PR) equation of state is widely regarded
as an appropriate and accurate thermodynamic model. As the key components are N2,
O2 and Ar, which are are nonбґpolar gases, PR is accurate because PR was intended for
them. In addition, in literature and industry sources consider PR an Б─²ideal thermodynamic
model to predict phase equilibria during cryogenic air separationБ─². In other words, the PR
can reproduce the vaporбґliquid equilibrium (VLE) behavior of air components at very low
temperatures.[43]
At 6 bar and temperatures below бґ150б╨ Celsius air components condense and boil in close
proximity, so accurate VLE are vital. The PR EOS have been validated against cryogenic
VLE data for N2/O2 mixtures, showing it can match distillation behavior when appropriate
binary interaction coefficients are used. [44]
The operating window of the cryogenic ASU lies within the validate range of PR. This
ensures that the model can predict liquid oxygen/nitrogen yields, reflux ratios... with conбґ
fidence. Since бґ150б╨ Celsius is far below the normal range for many equations of state,
PR robustness and enhancements allows it to maintain reasonableness predictions at this
temperatures.[44][43]
As said at the beginning of this section main constituents of air are nonбґpolar and simple
molecules, which means no strong dipoles or hydrogen bonds that complicate interacбґ
tions. Cubic equations of state like PR excel for such systems because they primarily

==PAGE:39==
account for dispersion forces. Indeed, the PR model is specifically recommended for
applications comprising nonpolar gases and vapors like O2 and N2 [43] . So, this lack
of polarity justifies the use of PR over polar models. The argon is also noble gas and
nonбґpolar gas, so it is also well within PRБ─≥s capabilities.
Using PR ensures thermodynamic consistency between enthalpy, entropy and phase
equilibrium calculations, which is important for the process energy balances and column
design. From an industry point of view, PR is the standard EoS in most process simulaбґ
tors for cryogenic separations. Its success in industrial ASU design and operation is well
documented, which means engineers trust it to obtain an accurate phase split and comбґ
position data. To sum up, PR has proven accuracy for nonбґpolar mixtures, applicability to
low temperatures and suitable for VLE make it an excellent choice for the air separation
unit.[44]
3.2.2
Thermodynamic modelбґElectrolyser
The electrolyzer unit involves a mixture of water, hydrogen and oxygen with both gas and
liquid phases. Selecting the SoaveбґRedlichбґKwong (SRK) EOS for this system provides a
convenient single model to handle VLE between a highly polar component (H2O) and non
polar gases (H2 and O2). The reason why SRK is a good selection is because a balance
between accuracy and simplicity is wanted for the model. SRK is a wellбґestablished model
with a better record for the vapor pressure prediction of light components compared to PR.
[45]
In the electrolyzer water can evaporate into hydrogen and oxygen gases, and have limited
solubility in the liquid water. So, a model that can handle this twoбґphase equilibrium is
needed. The SRK is capable of predicting the vaporбґliquid split qualitatively correctly, and
show that the hydrogen and oxygen have only trace solubility in liquid water. In practice,
engineers often use SRK for such gasбґwater systems, it is widely applied in modeling
H2бґH2O.[45]
The operating conditions are well in the range where SRK performs reliably. SRK can
handle moderate temperature and pressure without numerical issue. Since the pressure
is low nonбґideal gas compressibility effects are small, and SRK will predict compressibility
factors for H2 and O2 close to unit. [45]
Water is a highly polar compound which is a modeling challenge due to its strong inбґ
termolecular attractions. Hydrogen and oxygen, by contrast, are nonpolar and interact
weakly. So, using SRK here requires careful parameter calibration, this is a common
practice in many simulators. Moreover, SRK is a trusted model in the industry for gasбґ
liquid involving light gases. By applying the same model in all the streams and equipments
in the process allows achieving better results than swapping the models inside the same
unit.[45]

temperature it is done to prevent temperature cross in the main heat exchanger.

streams after being cooled are mixed together again, the temperature is бґ166б╨C.

column, it will use a Chemsep plugбґin since the columns on the palette of DWSim are to
simple or not able to simulate this separation. The HPC has 45 stages and is fed on the
bottom, from the top the distillate leaves the column in a liquid phase and rich in nitrogen,
and from the bottom another product stream leaves as liquid with a mix of nitrogen and
oxygen. These outlet streams would go through a subcooler, where they would exchange
heat with the pure nitrogen and pure streams of the LPC. The LPC, has two feeds one at
the top and another one in the middle, respectively they are the distillate from the HPC
and the bottom product. The operating pressure of the column is 1.3 bar and the outlets
from this column would be the products that will later be used or sold but before that they
need to go through the main heat exchanger and the sub cooler to exchange hat with the
inlets. The distillate has a flow rate of 3,294 kg/h of N2 and the bottom a 1,009 kg/hr of

4.1.2
Electrolyser results
For the simulation of the electrolyser, the flowsheet can be seen on the Appendix Figure
X2. The flowsheet has been designed based on the PFD 3.2 saw in the methodology and
by internal data from Rambц╦ll. The inlet of water to obtain the desired amount of green
hydrogen is 7,100 kg/h of purified water, which will allow us to obtain 750 kg/hr of green
H2.
The water purification hasnБ─≥t been modeled in DWSim, so it is assumed that the initial
water is already treated and fulfills the conditions needed for the PEM. These flow will
be pumped to an oxygen separator, which in DWSim is a gasбґliquid separator since the
oxygen that is produced in the electrolyser comes in a gaseous phase. Here, the water
recirculated to the electrolyser, but the water has to be within the work temperature range
therefore it will go through a heat exchanger where it will be assured that these stream
enters at 60б╨C and at 3.5 bar.
The electrolyser unit in DWSim is not accurate as it will generate two outlet streams with
pure oxygen and pure hydrogen. Thus, it is not a realistic simulation as in reality these
streams have also unreacted water. To solve this issue Rambц╦ll provided an internal
design for DWSim where it would imitate the work of a PEM, as it can be seen in the figure
A.2 there is a conversion reactor and a compound separator and they would imitate the
working process of the electrolyser. In the conversion reactor is where the electrolysis
is taking place, the conversion of this reaction is calculated through a model provided
by Rambц╦ll. This model uses the electrical consumption of the electrolyzer to produce a
kilogram of hydrogen and the total amount of hydrogen expected to be produced, which
will tell us the electrolysis capacity and the conversion rate.
There are two streams leaving the electrolyser, one is rich in oxygen gas and the other
in hydrogen gas. First, the stream with gaseous oxygen is recycled back to the oxygen
separator, where Vбґ1 will separate the oxygen in gas state. After, the outcome with just
gases is cool down to 40б╨C so if there was any vapor is condensed and converted into
liquid for its post separation. Once, the water droplets have been separated we have a
pure Oxygen stream that can be sell as a byбґproduct. It has a 92% purity and a mass flow
of 6,026.39 kg/h.
The other streams that leaves the electrolyser is the one rich in hydrogen. This one is
forwarded to a hydrogen separator, which in DWSim is simulated as a gasбґliquid separator
where the unreacted water is separated from the gases and recirculated to the oxygen
separator. The gases are then cooled down to 40б╨C to remove the water droplets that
could be present in the gaseous phase. The clean gases are now heated up to 80б╨C
before going into the deoxidizer, which is simulated as a conversion reactor where the
water formation reaction takes place. Thus, the amount of oxygen that could be present
disappears leaving just hydrogen and water as compounds present in the stream. Before
it is ready for storage there is a last unit, the dryer. On the dryer water is separated from
the hydrogen and obtaining a stream with a 99.9999% of purity and a mass rate of 750.08
kg/h.
This high purity nitrogen reflects the requirements of the ammonia synthesis process,
where impurities like oxygen,moisture and argon can either poison the catalyst or accuбґ
mulate in the recycle loop. Which will reduce the process efficiency since even trace
amounts of oxygen can lead to irreversible catalyst deactivate. Also, argon and other
inerts cannot be consumed and need to be purged, which causes losses of hydrogen.
So, highбґpurity nitrogen is used in ammonia plants to ensure maximum catalyst life and
minimal purge losses.

obtain a 24% conversion when a single pass occurs.

point is reached at 40б╨C, so at this conditions the first step of the ammonia recovery takes

separation. The gases will be expanded to 80 bars and cooled at бґ25б╨C, to condensate the
remaining ammonia present as gases. Again, a gas бґliquid separator has been added to

All those gases that did not condensate, the unreacted gases and ammonia will go through

Conclusions
This project has detailed the process modeling and simulation of a ammonia production
in a plant powered by renewable sources. Where the three main units were designed
and simulated, these are: the ASU, hydrogen production by electrolyser and ammonia
synthesis loop. In addition, a sensitivity analysis and a technoбґeconomical analysis was
also performed.
The ammonia reactor has been an important subject of this project, since the type of reacбґ
tor had to be chosen before simulating the kinetics. Since the ammonia process has been
implemented for a long time, there are many developments and upgrades on the origiбґ
nal kinetics. But when doing the design on DWSim many errors where encountered with
the kinetic terms, therefore after unsuccessfully developing the Langmuir Hinshelwood
kinetics it was decided to proceed with Temkin & Pyzhev. As said previously during the
design of the ammonia synthesis the reactor was the main focus since at the beginning it
was not possible to proceed with a PFR due to the errors encountered with the kinetics a
conversion reactor was chosen, and given a fixed conversion rate. With deeper research
it was finally modeled with the kinetics as if not we would not have been able to see how
the temperature and pressure affect the conversion.
When comparing the single pass conversion with the traditional plants of ammonia it was
identified that the conversion rate per single pass was about 10% lower than with reбґ
newables. The reason that can be behind this is the fact that methane and chemical
compounds that derivate from the natural gas are not present anymore, and they can
inhibit the catalyst.
The electrolyser was also really important on the design of the plant, as the one selected
needed to be able to work with the uncertainty that renewable energy bring. Therefore,
the selection of PEM was then done based on its fast response, efficiency and production
of pure hydrogen. A 99.99% of purity was achieved since it is required for the ammonia
process but the conversion rate is pretty small and would need a lot of water to produce
the hourly demand.
Lastly, the ASU design was also really important as it needs to be checked that the nitroбґ
gen product reaches the requirements to be used as a feedstock. The main issue was
with the argon since only a limited amount of it can go into the ammonia reactor so if the
limit of 0.8% was passed a third column should have been implemented. It was also disбґ
covered that with one column it can also be obtained high purity in the Nitrogen product
stream but the mass obtained per hour is much more lower.
To finish with the process design, the selection of the reactor is also a big part of the
design as it can make a difference on the conversion rate obtained on a single pass. If
AQCR was selected then the conversion decreases from 27% to 20%. Which will affect
the recycle stream as there would be more unreacted reactants.
The economic assessment estimated a total capital investment of 73,9046,47.65 $ for
a medium size plant of 100 t/day of ammonia. The primary costs contributors were the
electrolyzer and ASU unit. Operational expenses were dominated by the price of elecбґ
tricity is 103 $/MWh. Under this scenario the levelized cost of ammonia was estimated
at $/ton ammonia, which is significantly higher than the levelized cost of ammonia when
using fossil fuels as feedstock,
