Abstract
Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (MRS) is a powerful technique that provides unique
information about brain metabolite concentrations in vivo. An important form of this tech-
nique is Functional Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (fMRS), which allows to study the
neurotransmitters variation linked to neural activity [1].
An inherent characteristic of fMRS signals is the low Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR), primar-
ily caused by the relatively low concentration of the metabolites of interest in comparison
to water. The traditional approach to this problem is to average the spectra obtained from
different temporal recordings, as a result, the SNR is increased but the temporal reso-
lution is badly affected [2]. For this particular reason, the solution currently used is not
always feasible for fMRS since its main interest is detecting the small changes in metabo-
lite concentrations between the spectra acquired at different time points.
Therefore, it was thought that significant improvement in detection sensitivity could be
achieved by applying denoising strategies before the metabolites quantification proce-
dure.
The aim of the Thesis project is to devise a methodology for processing and analyzing
fMRS data that includes denoising, as well as to compare the efficacy of different denois-
ing methods implemented.
The denoising methods are implemented and optimized in Matlab and evaluated to sim-
ulated data first and subsequently to in vivo spectra. The methods comprise Spectral
Improvement by Fourier Transform (SIFT), Wavelets, Spline, Sliding and Gaussian win-
dow, Marchenko Pastur - Principal Component Analysis (MP-PCA), and L + S or robust
PCA, and their optimization is done to preserve the balance between removing as much
noise as possible without distorting the signal.
The optimized methods are applied first to the static simulated spectra, and then to the
dynamic ones. The initial analysis based on applying the techniques on static spectra
reveals that the denoising methods yield superior outcomes when applied to the DIFF
spectrum rather than the OFF. Among these methods, the Sliding and Gaussian window
techniques demonstrate the most effective results.
When the methods are applied to the dynamic simulated spectra, the main focus is the
improvement of the detection sensitivity to the simulated metabolic changes of the neuro-
transmitter GABA. From this analysis results that all seven methods, except for the SIFT,
are able to correctly detect the metabolic concentration changes, however only the MP-
PCA technique is actually improving the GABA detection sensitivity.
As previously recalled the interest of fMRS experiments relies on metabolites changes
detection. Therefore, the L + S or robust PCA method is of particular interest, since it is
the only technique that utilizes the inherent information of the fMRS signal to separate the
spectrum into two matrices: one, called sparse matrix S, containing only the simulated
changes and the random noise from the original signal, and the other, known as low-rank
matrix, storing the time-invariant part of it.
Finally, the denoising methods are applied to the in vivo data acquired during the HiRe-
Spect project at the Danish Research Centre for Magnetic Resonance (DRCMR). Here
the techniques are evaluated according to the CRLB values obtained when quantifying
the denoised spectra.

1
Introduction
Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (MRS) is a powerful technique that provides unique
information about brain metabolite concentrations in vivo. A relevant form of MRS when
considering dynamic changes is Functional Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (fMRS),
which permits to study the variation in neurotransmitters linked to neural activity. The
technique is a rapidly growing area of interest due to its ability to measure quantitative
changes in neurochemicals in response to brain activity non-invasively [1].
Despite notable advancements in the field, there persists a necessity to enhance the
detection sensitivity of fMRS, primarily because of the relatively lower concentration of
the metabolites of interest in comparison to water, resulting in a low Signal-to-Noise Ratio
(SNR) [2].
For this particular reason, the main objective of the Thesis is to address the challenges
inherent to fMRS by analyzing and comparing different denoising techniques applied to
the spectroscopy signal. The aim is to enhance the SNR of the signal and improve the
detection sensitivity to metabolic changes to maximize the benefits offered by this valuable
technique.
The Thesis project is part of the HiReSpect project carried out at the Danish Research
Centre for Magnetic Resonance (DRCMR) at Hvidovre Hospital, where data acquisition,
data analysis, and supervision took place. The project has its main interest in GABA
detection. This neurotransmitter plays an important role as a potential biomarker in neu-
rological and neuropsychiatric disorders such as cancer, multiple sclerosis, Alzheimer’s
disease, epilepsy, schizophrenia, and autism, however, it could greatly benefit from de-
noising since is present in approximately one millimolar concentration in the human brain
[3].
1.1
Motivation
All the Magnetic Resonance (MR) techniques inherently exhibit low SNR, with fMRS be-
ing particularly affected due to the metabolites of interest having lower concentrations
compared to the water signal employed in Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) [4].
The traditional approach currently employed is to average the spectra from various tem-
poral recordings, allowing for an increased SNR. For the fMRS data on the other hand,
this technique is not always feasible since it will reduce the signal’s temporal resolution
obscuring the minor changes in the metabolite concentration between different repeated
acquisitions. [5].
For the reasons stated above, denoising strategies used before the metabolites quantifi-
cation procedure can greatly help the techniques.
Although a number of techniques for noise reduction and sensitivity enhancement have
been used in the past, the MRS community has not reached an agreement on any of them
as a standard practice to be added to the processing pipeline.
In order to maintain a high SNR and good sensitivity in the signal, the primary aim of
the Thesis is to implement, analyze, and compare several denoising algorithms and ap-
proaches to be used to both simulated and in vivo fMRS data.
All the available methods are implemented in Matlab in a way that they can be integrated
into the already existing fMRS pipeline used in the HiReSpect project, explained in Section
3.5.1.

1.2
Background
In the field of chemistry, Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (MRS) is a useful analytical
method that allows for the non-invasive identification and quantification of metabolites
in complex samples, such as the living brain. This technique, along with MR imaging,
originates from Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR), with MRS being the specific term
used in a biological or medical context [6].
The NMR technique began to be used in vivo after the introduction of the gradients in
the magnetic field, which allowed for the location of the emitted signal to be determined
and reproduced in an image or spectrum. Subsequent to the introduction of gradients in
the magnetic field, improvements in MR technology, especially with regard to higher field
strengths, have been made [7].
Currently, MRS has become a valuable tool in clinical and diagnostic settings due to its
non-invasive ability to detect and quantify metabolites in-vivo complex samples, such as
the human brain [6], and it is widely recognized as the only non-invasive technique capa-
ble of quantifying neurometabolites in the brain in a single acquisition [1].
While resting-state MRS provides information about the baseline levels of neurometabo-
lites in the brain, it does not capture how these levels change in response to internal or
external stimuli. Functional MRS, on the other hand, measures metabolites during tasks
or stimulation with high temporal resolution, providing a method to record dynamic neu-
rometabolic alterations.
The fundamental principle of fMRS involves acquiring data during various phases of a task
to capture dynamic metabolic changes due to stimulation. Unlike traditional MRS, which
averages transients across a complete scanning session to provide a static measure,
fMRS studies typically average a lower number of transients to achieve better temporal
resolution. However, this approach involves a trade-off between temporal resolution and
Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR), which leads to additional limitations [1].

erty, and because of it they are able to align along the axis of a magnetic field [8]. Figure

The property of nuclear resonance is observed due to the presence of a magnetic mo-
ment in the nuclei of most isotopes of elements. This magnetic moment arises from the
combination of nuclear spin and the electric charge of the nucleus. The concept of nuclear
spin can be visualized as the rotation of the atomic nucleus around its own axis.
When exposed to a constant magnetic field B0, the nuclei with a spin can be excited, and
the magnetic moment energy depends on the alignment of the nucleus in relation to the
magnetic field. By applying electromagnetic radiation at a specific frequency, the nuclei
transition between high- and low-energy states can be induced [8].
In the presence of a magnetic field, the nuclei magnetic moments align pointing to the
direction relative to the applied field, and are determined by the nuclear spin quantum
number, I. The resonance frequency, denoted as υ, is directly proportional to the strength
of the external magnetic field B0, as described in equation 1.1 [8].
υ = γ
2πB0
(1.1)
The phenomenon of resonant absorption in nuclear spin occurs when electromagnetic
radiation of a specific frequency, known as the Larmor frequency, is applied to match the
energy difference between the levels of nuclear spin in a constant magnetic field of suit-
able strength.
When Radio Frequency (RF) pulses are applied, the nuclei spins absorb energy and pre-
cess in phase around the direction of the magnetic field, following the Equation eq. (1.2),
called the Larmor equation:
ω = γB0
(1.2)
where ω is the field-dependent Larmor frequency, γ is the gyromagnetic ratio, a constant
for each nuclear species, and B0 is the strength of the main magnetic field.
Each nucleus experiences a unique chemical environment, determined by the surround-
ing electron environment, which affects the total magnetic field experienced by the nu-
cleus. This leads to different spin energy levels and resonance frequencies, known as
chemical shifts. The chemical shift creates a distinct resonance signal for each metabo-
lite, which can be used for their identification and quantification [1].
While the primary influence on nuclei comes from the static magnetic field strength (B0)
and the field (B1), they are also subject to a local magnetic field resulting from the electron
fields in their immediate chemical surroundings. Consequently, the extent of shielding or
amplification of the local magnetic field by electron currents relies on the specific electronic
environment, which is determined by the molecular’s precise chemical structure.[8].
As previously mentioned, MR spectra can be obtained using a variety of nuclei, but for in
vivo applications in the human brain, protons (1H) are the most commonly used due to their
high sensitivity and abundance. Moreover, proton Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (1H
- MRS) is particularly favorable because it only requires standard radiofrequency (RF)
coils and dedicated software. In contrast, even if non-proton MR spectroscopy offers
potential advantages in certain applications, it requires more extensive and specialized
hardware compared to 1H - MRS [7].
For traditional clinical MR imaging and spectroscopy, various field strengths, spanning
from 0.2 to 3T, are used. Higher field strength units benefit from better resolution, faster
acquisition times, and higher signal-to-noise ratios (SNR). A field strength of up to 7T can
be employed in research settings [7] and it is used in this specific project.

1.2.2
Techniques
Various methods can be used to acquire the spectroscopy signal, such as single- and
multi-voxel spectroscopy. To achieve a sufficient SNR, data are acquired repeatedly and
the resulting signals are averaged to obtain a single spectrum.
In spectroscopy, the Repetition Time (TR) refers to the time interval between each re-
peated measurement, and the transient corresponds to the signal acquired within each
TR. The Echo Time (TE) instead, is defined as the time between the centre of the excita-
tion pulse and the start of acquisition [9].
In the Project, the data are acquired with the Single-Voxel spectroscopy technique.
Single-Voxel spectroscopy
Single Voxel Spectroscopy (SVS) involves obtaining a signal from a pre-selected voxel.
The voxel is acquired using slice-selective excitations in three dimensions of space, ob-
tained by applying an RF pulse while turning on a field gradient. This process generates
three orthogonal planes whose intersection corresponds to the volume of interest (VOI).
Figure 1.2 provides a visual representation of the slice selection process in single-voxel
spectroscopy.
Figure 1.2: Process of slice selection in single-voxel spectroscopy: the three RF pulses
(rf) are used to select three orthogonal planes (Gx, Gy, Gz), which intersect at the Volume
of Interest (VOI) where the signal is acquired. The time between the center of the RF
pulse and the center of the echo is referred to as the Echo Time (TE). [10]

==PAGE:19==
SVS spectra are commonly acquired using the Pointed-REsolved Spectroscopy Sequence
(PRESS). PRESS is the most frequently used technique, in which the spectrum is ac-
quired using a sequence of radiofrequency (RF) pulses and field gradients. The first RF
pulse is a 90° pulse, followed by two 180° pulses applied at different field gradients, but at
the same time, resulting in a spin echo signal emitted by the selected volume of interest
(VOI). Spoiler gradients are used to restrict the acquired signal to the VOI by dephasing
the nuclei outside the VOI and reducing their signal.

time, rf, radiofrequency pulses. [10]
1.2.3
Spectrum
In MR spectroscopy, the spectral information about metabolites is represented in a two-
dimensional plot displaying the chemical shift of metabolites in ppm on the horizontal (x)
axis from right to left, and the arbitrary signal amplitude on the vertical (y) axis. The height
of the metabolic peak corresponds to the relative concentration of the metabolite, and
the area under the curve is proportional to the metabolite concentration in the sample.
In general, a spectrum represents the resonance interaction intensity as a function of
frequency[6].
Knowing the typical spectrum obtained from 1H - MRS in a healthy subject and how it
may differ based on factors such as acquisition technique, patient age, and brain region
is crucial. This is because metabolic changes may occur prior to structural abnormalities
and 1H - MRS can reveal abnormalities associated with various neurological disorders
earlier than conventional MR imaging.

1.2.4
MRI and MRS: similarities and differences
MRI and MRS share a common physical background based on NMR. However, while MRI
is an imaging technique, MRS is a spectroscopy technique that uses NMR.
MRS has a major advantage in its use of chemical shift information as an additional di-
mension to express the shielding degree of nuclei within one molecule. In contrast, a
normal MRI image is taken at one chemical shift of water protons at 4.7 ppm and pro-
vides anatomical structure information through two or three different spatial dimensions.
MRS provides biochemical information through chemical shifts of metabolites, thus adding

another dimension of data. By detecting chemical shift differences, MRS can quantita-
tively assess various metabolites and lipids in living tissue that cannot be separately de-
tected by MRI.

end of the processing pipeline, the resulting spectra from MRS, display different metabo-

1.2.5
functional Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy
Functional Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (fMRS) is a technique used to investigate
changes in metabolites during an acquisition. The basic principle is that data are acquired
during different phases of a task or after the application of an external stimulus, in an
attempt to capture dynamic brain state changes.
In fMRS, two approaches are used mainly: block designs and event-related designs [1].
Block design
Block design fMRS involves acquiring a single MRS scan for a specific duration or dividing
continuous resting-state acquisitions into smaller transient blocks, a schematic represen-
tation of the paradigm employed in this kind of fMRS design is shown in Figure 1.8. The
advantage of this approach is that it provides a high Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) by av-
eraging across a large number of transients. However, a major drawback is that it is not
sensitive to fast or transient changes in neuro metabolites. Any rapid changes in metabo-
lite levels will be averaged out in the block design and may not be reliably detectable. The
temporal resolution may be increased involving event-related fMRS designs [1].
7
==PAGE:22==
Figure 1.7: Schematic representation of the block design experiment in fMRS. The stimuli
are collected in a block and then the spectra are acquired between the stimuli blocks. Here
there are two stimuli blocks and two resting states in which the spectra are acquired.
Event-related design
Event-related approaches rely on locking the stimulus onset within a single MRS acquisi-
tion in the time domain and thus, are advantageous because they allow for the detection
of transient changes in metabolite levels that can occur in response to a stimulus [1].
Figure 1.8: Schematic representation of the event-related design experiment in fMRS.
The stimuli are present for a short period of time and the spectra are acquired allowing a
rapid transient change. Here two stimuli are represented with a resting state in between.
Sensitivity
The sensitivity to variations in neurometabolites levels presents a problem for fMRS.
Despite the fact that fMRS employs fewer transients than static MRS, it could be said that
the sensitivity to neurometabolites quantification and variability is in the same range for
both of them and that low SNR may not detect subtle changes in metabolites. This may
be much more difficult for the GABA signal since it is overlapping with other metabolites,
such as NAA and Cr which are more abundant.
Solutions such as higher field strengths and increasing voxels size can be employed to
mitigate the problem, however, the latter would decrease regional specificity necessitat-
ing longer scan times [1].
Obtaining many repetitions of the same sequence and averaging them is the normal pro-
cedure to increase SNR, which will aid in having improved sensitivity. This approach in
fMRS has the disadvantage of losing temporal information, which results in a trade-off
between spectral and temporal feature characterization.

1.2.6
Measuring GABA in MRS
As previously stated, the HiReSpect project has its main interest in GABA detection which
can greatly benefit from the denoising methods employment.
γ-Aminobutyric acid (GABA) is an amino acid that acts as an inhibitory neurotransmitter in
the Central Nervous System (CNS). During the GABA-glutamine cycle, the neurotrans-
mitter is formed by glutamate and released into neurons’ post-synaptic terminals. The
GABAa and GABAb receptors are considered the main inhibitory receptors for the central
nervous system and while the first is considered in the fast synaptic inhibition, the second
has an important role in the slow inhibition [13].
Although GABA can be detected at different field strengths, it has been demonstrated that
spectra that are acquired at higher field strengths show superior fitting precision, better
SNR, and simplified spectra. As a result, ultra-high field MRS is largely adopted for its
high sensitivity and specificity when measuring this metabolite.
The sequence used primarily to address the challenge of quantifying low-concentration
metabolites, such as GABA, is called MEGA editing [3].
Essentially, the MEGA-PRESS sequence uses the spin-spin coupling properties of GABA
to selectively invert spins at 1.9 ppm with a frequency-selective ”editing pulse”. This pulse
then affects the 3 ppm GABA spins through coupling, allowing for the selective refocusing
of the GABA triplet at 3 ppm. By acquiring MRS data both with the editing pulse (edit-
ON) and without it (edit-OFF), the resulting difference (DIFF) between these two spectra
allows for the visualization of signals only affected by the editing pulse at 1.9 ppm [1]. A
schematic example of how the edit-ON, edit-OFF, and difference spectra look is schemat-
ically represented in Figure 1.9
Figure 1.9: MEGA-PRESS sequence scheme and the edited ON, OFF, and difference
spectrum DIFF representation[1]
The PRESS sequence explained in Section 1.2.2 is not generally used with ultra-high
fields, because of the low bandwidth of the RF pulses which causes a chemical shift dis-
placement error.
The introduction of adiabatic refocusing pulses, which have high bandwidth and are in-
sensitive to the B1 field inhomogeneity, allows the sLASER sequence to function more
effectively, which results in a less chemical shift displacement error (CSDE) [3].

1.4
Problem Statement
As explained in Section 1.2.5, in Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (MRS), achieving
a sufficient Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and a good sensitivity to metabolites changes,
poses a significant challenge, mainly due to the low concentration of many of the metabo-
lites under investigation. The standard method to mitigate this issue is by acquiring multi-
ple iterations of the same sequence and averaging them, resulting in an increasing SNR
proportional to the square root of the number of repetitions. However, in functional MRS,
the primary interest is to monitor the metabolic variation across successive spectra, which
is compromised when multiple repetitions are combined into one data point, leading to re-
duced temporal resolution. Therefore, a trade-off arises between characterizing spectral
features and temporal ones [2].
Following these reasons, fMRS signals can take advantage by applying a denoising al-
gorithm on the raw data in terms of temporal resolution, since it would not be necessary
anymore to average a large set of spectra in order to have a sufficiently high SNR, losing
the important information about the metabolite levels varying between different acquisi-
tion.

1.5
Goal and methods
To date, a vast number of denoising methods in MRI and MRS have been proposed.
However, each denoising algorithm is often tailored to a specific application or dataset
meaning that its performance has to be confirmed when applied with different data or
methods. In order to estimate this, a comparison with ”ground truth” is necessary. There-
fore, in this Thesis project, each method, after being optimized, was tested on static and
functional simulated data where was possible to generate a noiseless ground truth spec-
trum and consequently make a comparison with it. In addition, in vivo performance was
tested on in vivo 1H fMRS data acquired within the HiReSpect project.
The main references for the choice of the methods are the studies by Rowland et al. [2],
which comprises a description of the majority of the techniques, Mosso et al. [5], and
Berrington et al. [15] and they are:
1. Spectral Improvement by Fourier Thresholding (SIFT)
2. Wavelets
3. Splines
4. Sliding window
5. Gaussian window
6. Marchenko Pastur - Principal Component Analysis (MP-PCA)
7. Robust PCA or L + S
A first comparison of the methods is done by the spectra appearance and employing
different metrics for both the static and functional scenarios. After that, the accuracy of
metabolite quantification was taken into consideration. The focus is especially on quan-
tifying the neurotransmitter GABA and determining if the denoising method improves the
accuracy of its quantification by reducing the CLRB! (CLRB!).
A recent study [16] proves that the GABA detection may be more robust when using only
the OFF spectrum, which will lower the acquisition time, and so also his aspect was con-
sidered by analyzing the CRLB values.

Related works
This section aims to give an overview of the related works and studies taken as an in-
spiration for both the methods implementation and the work organization in the methods
comparison.
The study by Rowland et al. [2] delved into the utilization of the indirect time domain
denoising technique on dynamic MRS data as a substitute for the conventional repeti-
tion averaging approach, which is similarly done in the thesis project. The aim here is
to enhance spectral SNR and enhance quantification, evaluating six different denoising
techniques and comparing their results for various SNR values.
Pseudo-synthetic data was constructed to mimic MRS 31Phosphorous spectra from exer-
cising muscle. To assess the performance of various denoising methods, it was neces-
sary, to begin with a known, noise-free ground truth that could be used as a reference to
which noise is added to obtain test data. In this way, after the application of the denoising
method, it was possible to compare the result to the original ground truth to determine
the amount of remaining noise. In this study, the ground truth consisted of time courses
that describe the concentration of each metabolite across repeated acquisitions. These
time courses were then used to generate idealized artificial FIDs for each acquisition in
the series, to which Gaussian noise was added.
This simulated dataset was then processed using the indirect time domain denoising tech-
nique. For each frequency in the spectrum, a time series was created from all repeated
acquisitions. The time series was then denoised using the selected denoising method and
the data points were reorganized back into their original spectra. The resulting spectra,
with reduced noise, were quantified to obtain metabolite concentration estimates. The es-
timated concentrations from the processed noisy data were then compared to the ground
truth concentrations over the time course to evaluate the effectiveness of the denoising
method in recovering the original time course. On the other hand, the denoising methods
are also applied to the so-called pseudo-synthetic data. These data are obtained from
a linear combination of noisy metabolite time courses denoised by previously known de-
noised methods.
Lastly, recalling that each denoising method must find a balance between removing as
much noise as possible and ensuring that the true signal is not distorted, a Monte Carlo
approach was used in order to determine the optimal values of the tuning parameters for
each method, at each SNR.
The denoising methods investigated in the study [2] are six and comprise SIFT, Hankel
Singular Value Decomposition (HSVD), Spline, Sliding and Gaussian window, and Wavelets.
This work was taken as a reference for the denoising methods implementation, but also
for the scores used to compare the techniques’ performance. The main differences are,
however, the kind of spectra that are employed (31P - MRS in the study [2] and 1H - MRS
in the thesis) and the specific methods used.
For the implementation of the MP-PCA method, the studies by Mosso et al. [5] and Ver-
aart et al. [17] are taken as a reference, where both of them have the purpose to assess
the effectiveness of this method. The first one used synthetic and experimental datasets
of single-voxel 1H - MRS diffusion-weighted MRS data, while in the second diffusion-
weighted MRI data were used.
13
==PAGE:28==
Both studies consist of decomposing the signal using the Principal Component Analysis
and then analyzing which components contain a significant part of the signal and which
contain the noise. The studies also describe the missing objective criterion to set an
appropriate threshold that separates the noise from the signal and propose a solution
based on the Marchenko Pastur - Principal Component Analysis (MP-PCA) technique.
The MP-PCA method takes advantage of the fact that the eigenvalues of the noise follow
the asymptotic universal Marchenko-Pastur distribution, which is derived from the random
matrix theory for noisy covariance matrices [17] and offers a data-driven method for sep-
arating noise from signal components in SVD, which is determined by iteratively fitting the
Marchenko-Pastur distribution to the tail of the eigenvalues. This study [5] was used in
order to get inspiration for the PCA method, and the available code from the Veraart et al.
[17] study was adapted to be used on spectroscopy data.
In order to implement the L + S method the studies by Berrington et al. and Liu et al.
[18] were taken into account. The study conducted by Berrington et al. [15] is used as
a valuable point of reference for the incorporation of the L + S method but also in the
functional data simulation. In this study, the algorithm is implemented and applied to both
simulated block design paradigms and in vivo data. In the simulated spectra, the STEAM
sequence was utilized, and the glutamate concentration was increased by 5% during the
ON periods.
Six fMRS experiments were simulated, with random Gaussian Noise (2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64
dB) added to each transient. The in vivo data were obtained from 13 healthy volunteers
who underwent visual stimulation, employing the semi-LASER sequence. The study [15]
was valuable in order to get some information about the L + S method implementation and
the main differences stay in the simulated data sequence and in the choice of glutatamate
as metabolite to be varied in concentration.
The algorithm described in the study and the available code developed by Liu et al. [18]
was adapted to be used in spectroscopy data since the code was first thought to be used
in video recording data.

Methods
The methods section of the thesis describes the comprehensive workflow of the project
which was divided into several key steps.
Firstly, the spectra simulation was performed to generate representative static and func-
tional data for the analysis. Real data acquisition was then carried out to gather actual
experimental data, according to the HiReSpect project acquisition design.
The different denoising methods were then implemented following the guidelines found in
the literature and changing them accordingly to be applied to the particular data employed
in the Thesis project. The denoising methods were optimized by determining the most ef-
fective parameters which maintain the significant signal information while removing the
noise, and then they were applied to the simulated spectra.
Finally, a thorough comparison was conducted to evaluate the performance and efficacy
of the implemented denoising methods.

3.2
Denoising methods implementation
The denoising methods are implemented in Matlab and are the following:
3.2.1
Spectral Improvement by Fourier Transform (SIFT)
In fMRS, it’s typical to have different spectra acquired at different time points. This kind of
acquisition can be called time series, and the domain in which the spectra are collected
indirect time domain. The method SIFT transforms the indirect time domain in the Fourier
domain, in which the signal is focused in a few numbers of coefficients. By threshold-
ing the Fourier transform of the time series, the single static spectrum results less noisy
and the noise along the time series is reduced. The performance will be impacted by the
precise threshold decision, with higher values suppressing more noise but perhaps elimi-
nating signal components. Thresholds are typically measured in noise standard deviation
[2], and it is executed employing the Matlab function wthresh which can softly or hardly
threshold the input signal [19]. In this project, soft thresholding is used.
3.2.2
Wavelets
In the wavelets denoising method, the signal is transformed into the wavelet domain,
which has a more sparse representation of the data. Similar to SIFT, also in this case, the
small noise coefficients are separated from the larger signal coefficients using a threshold
[2]. The advantage with respect to the Fourier thresholding is that the latter uses a low
pass filter in order to get the denoised signal, which means that high-frequency signals
will be smoothed with the possibility to lose features such as spikes and peaks.
In order to implement this method the Matlab function wdenoise is employed. This function
denoises the input using an empirical Bayesian method with a Cauchy prior and by default
the wavelet transform is used with a posterior median threshold rule [20].
3.2.3
Spline
In the smoothing approach of the splines method, a baseline set of cardinal B-splines is

method. The application taking two input vectors is capable of creating, plotting, and com-

3.2.4
Sliding window
The sliding window can be considered the simplest technique implemented. The selected
point of the signal becomes equal to the average of the points neighboring it [2] following
the equation 3.1.
ys(i) =
1
2N + 1(y(i + N) + y(i + N −1) + ... + y(i −N))
(3.1)
where ys(i) is the smoothed value for the ith data point and N is the number of neighboring
data points on either side of ys(i).
The Matlab function called filter was employed, which works by filtering the input signal by
applying a rational transfer function defined by the numerator and denominator specified
[23]. The numerator was defined as 1 and the denominator as a vector of the window
width.
3.2.5
Gaussian window
Similar to the sliding window method, in the Gaussian window technique the signal points
are weighted. In this case, the Gaussian function is used such that those points nearest
to the point being calculated have a higher impact on the outcome [2].
As used for the sliding window in section 3.2.4, the filter Matlab function is employed. In
this case, while the numerator is still 1, the denominator is equal to the Gaussian window
generated using the Matlab function gausswin [24].
3.2.6
Marchenko Pastur - Principal Component Analysis (MP-PCA)
The Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) is employed in this method to decompose the
signal into components that represent the signals and components that represent the
noise. In fact, it is demonstrated that the PCA for data with more acquisitions shows that
the greatest part of the signal-related variance is represented by few components, while
the noise is spread over all of them. Meaning that thresholding the number of components
is possible to get a denoised signal.
The missing step in this practice is the application of an objective criterion to determine
the correct threshold, which could be solved by exploiting the fact that the noise-only
eigenvalues are expected to follow the Marchenko-Pastur Law.

3.5
Testing the Denoising Methods on HiReSpect MRS data
3.5.1
MRS processing pipeline
The pipeline for the real data analysis, developed in Matlab, reads the Philips RAW data
using FID-A functions and also prepares the dataset for LCModel use.
FID-A is an open-source software package for the simulation and processing of MRS data
[28]. The pipeline comprises different steps including:
1. Load the data in the RAW (Philips) format and converting in the FID-a struct format
2. Separate the spectrum according to the timing vector describing the delay system
used in the data acquisition. In this section, the ONs and OFFs spectra are sepa-
rated, and from now on the steps are applied separately to the ONs and OFFs.
3. Remove the bad averages. This optional section is disabled in this project.
4. Correct the frequency drift, in order to avoid incoherent signal averaging, which
leads to artifactual line broadening, lineshape distortion, and reduced SNR [29].
5. Apply the denoising methods. It was decided to apply the denoising methods after
the averages alignment in order to avoid distorted values in the residuals caused by
the misalignment of the spectra.
6. Additional phase correction using the creatine peak in the signal
7. Combine the OFFs and ONs spectra in order to get the DIFF spectrum

Implementation of L + S or robust PCA
As explained in Section 3.2.7, this method is the only one that, by using the intrinsic infor-

pose the signal according to the temporally correlated components of it. This means that
this specific method allows to represent the signal components that are changing in time,
i.e. the metabolites changes, in the sparse matrix S, while the time-invariant components
of the signal are represented by the low-rank matrix L.
These characteristics of the method are important in the analysis of the fMRS signals
since it allows to detect the small variation in the metabolite changes, which is the main
interest of this technique.

5
Discussion
The main goal of the Thesis project is to develop a methodology for processing and an-
alyzing functional MRS that includes the denoising of the signal prior to the metabolites
quantification.
The inclusion of a denoising step is believed to be important because of the fact that
functional MRS is characterized by an inherently low SNR. This characteristic will lead to
difficulties in the detection of the changes in the concentration of the metabolites in which
the technique is mainly interested. Therefore, seven denoising techniques were imple-
mented and optimized in Matlab before being applied to simulated datasets mimicking
static and dynamic MRS spectra.
The objective was to determine which technique was effective in removing noise while
preserving the significant components of the signal. In addition, testing the denoising
methods on a dataset of spectra in which the GABA concentration is varying allowed
to investigate the ability of each of the methods in improving the metabolites detection
sensitivity.
5.1
Data simulation
The complexity of evaluating denoising methods for MRS arises from the lack of knowl-
edge about the actual concentration of metabolites in in vivo datasets. As a result, it is
often necessary to simulate data with a known concentration in order to assess the effec-
tiveness of denoising techniques [30].
To achieve this, the simulated metabolite spectra have to be combined with appropriate
weights depending on their in vivo concentration [31]. In this project this was done by
considering the concentration values found in literature [9].
Moreover, two sets of spectra mimicking functional MRS experiments were simulated by
alternating static spectra and spectra in which the GABA concentration is increasing as
done in Berrington study [15]. The specific increment was suggested by recent studies
[7] indicating an observed GABA change of more than 20%.
5.2
Testing the Denoising methods on static MRS data
5.2.1
Denoising methods optimization
The optimization of the denoising methods was a fundamental step before their application
to the signal of interest [2]. The optimal parameter value will vary considerably according
to the signal being analyzed. Proof of this concept it’s already visible from the trend
differences when the parameters are employed for denoising the DIFF and OFF spectra.
Because of that, the optimization of the method results in an important step to complete
before any application. However, even though the trends are different the optimal values
selected for both the spectra are very similar.
This could be caused by the fact that the graphs are not showing a sharp transition in
the RMSE values for almost all the methods, meaning that there isn’t a precise value of
the tuning parameter for which the RMSE metric decreases first and then increases with
either too much residual, or inducing signal distortion. This kind of behavior can be seen
only in the case of the application of the Spline method on the DIFF spectrum, where the
smoothing parameter equal to 0.9455 represents the precise value for which the method
is considered to be optimal.
43
==PAGE:58==
5.2.2
Denoising methods application on the simulated data
Conducting this test before evaluating the performance on the dynamic spectra is crucial
since the dynamic dataset can benefit from the denoising of the singular static spectra.
Overall, the implemented methods are enhancing the spectrum visualization reducing
most of the random noise. However, there are noticeable signal distortions when using the
SIFT and Spline methods. The SIFT may be impacting the high-frequency components
more than the low-frequency due to the Fourier transform used for denoising [2]. On the
other hand, the Spline method, since is defined as a smoothing technique [2], smooths
both the noise and the significant signal, which could result in these distortions.
5.2.3
Denoising methods comparison
The results regarding both the RMSE and the standard deviation are suggesting that all
the denoising methods are more effective when applied to spectra with high SNR and high
line-broadening factor values, as expected. It is also worth noticing that the RMSE values
are lower when the techniques are applied to the DIFF spectrum. Since the DIFF spectrum
is less complex than the OFF this is also an expected finding. The outcomes differ from
what is reported by Rowland et al. [2], where the Spline method performs the best. A
possible explanation for this behavior may be the different data employed, highlighting the
importance of the optimization stage before the application of the techniques to different
signals. On the other hand, the performance of the MP-PCA technique reinforces the
findings of Mosso et al. [5].
5.3
Testing the Denoising Methods on Functional MRS data
The final objective of the project involves not only the denoising process but also the
detection sensitivity improvement of the dynamic spectra acquired during a functional
MRS experiment. For this reason, the second step of the denoising methods evaluation
and comparison comprises a test on the spectra simulating a functional MRS experiment.
5.3.1
Denoising methods comparison
When applied to both of the two functional MRS experiments, all the denoising methods,
except for SIFT, are successfully able to detect the GABA change induced by the dy-
namic experiment. However, since the main aim is to improve the detection sensitivity to
metabolites changes, only the MP-PCA application results in a more accurate detection
when compared to the GABA quantification of the noisy spectrum.
The results confirm the recent findings by Dziadosz et al. [32] study. The study stated
that even though the spectra appearance results greatly improved when the denoising
method is applied, the quantified metabolite values are not more accurate compared to
the ground truth, similar to this Project’s findings.
The reason for this behavior may be due to the fact that the noise detected and removed
by the techniques is in the signal-free part of the spectrum [32], resulting in a subsequent
better visual appearance but not in a more accurate metabolite quantification. F
5.4
Implementation of L + S or robust PCA
Despite the poorly effective results obtained by the denoising methods applied as a pre-
processing step, the L + S method shows promising results in separating the varying
signal from its static background.
The decomposition in low-rank and sparse matrices is usually used in moving object de-
tection in video [33], but the previous study by Berrington et al. [15] also shows its potential
when applied in functional MRS signals.
44
==PAGE:59==
In this Project, the technique successfully distinct the temporally correlated and uncor-
related components of the signal, meaning that the simulated GABA detection is well
recognized. When no metabolite changes are simulated, the results are still promising,
even though a residual of the static signal is represented in the sparse matrix.
The reason for that could stay in the choice of the regularization parameter lambda, re-
sponsible for the decomposition weighting, and the tolerance parameter, which instead
regularizes the number of iterations to solve the optimization problem.
Future works will comprise a better understanding and a more precise parameters choice.
5.5
Testing the Denoising Methods on HiReSpect MRS data
The last test comprises the implementation of the denoising methods in the processing
pipeline employed in the HiReSpect project and their application to the in vivo data.
It is important to realize that in this analysis the data are treated as static since only the
baseline is considered, but also that is not possible to do a comparison with the noiseless
ground truth since in the in vivo scenario the real concentration of the metabolites is un-
known. From a first visual inspection of the spectra’ appearance is possible to observe
how some of the methods, like Wavelets, Spline, Sliding and Gaussian Window, highly
distort the signal, probably due to the denoising methods’ simplicity, which even though
promise good results in the simulated data can’t handle the complexity of the in vivo spec-
tra.
However, even the methods that visually best perform, such as SIFT, MP-PCA, and L
+ S, don’t allow a more accurate GABA detection. Indeed, the CRLB computed em-
ploying these methods results in similar ranges of the ones computed by employing the
noisy spectrum, as confirmed by the statistical analysis. Diminished CRLB values are
detected only by employing the Sliding and Gaussian Window, however, since these are
the methods that most distort the signal they cannot guarantee improved detection of all
the metabolites of interest.
Future works will delve into this aspect involving more subjects and more metabolites
detection.

6
Conclusion
The Thesis aim is to develop a methodology for processing and analyzing functional
MRS data, comparing different denoising techniques. The denoising methods function
is twofold, the goal is to denoise the spectra while improving the metabolite detection dur-
ing functional MRS experiments.
Based on the results obtained all the seven tested denoising methods show a visual im-
provement, especially in the simulated data, proven by the RMSE and standard devia-
tion metrics. However, their application doesn’t allow a more accurate detection of the
metabolites of interest reflected by the CRLB and bias analysis. Almost all the denoising
methods underperform when compared with the results obtained by quantifying the noisy
and noiseless spectra with the exception of MP-PCA and L + S, which, however, don’t
show a significant improvement.
In the project, the focus is mainly on GABA detection which is a difficult metabolite to
quantify [7], especially in functional MRS experiments, due to its low concentration, but
similar results are obtained when the majority of the metabolites are quantified as reported
in the study by Dziadosz et al. [32], in which a deep learning algorithm is tested. Similar
to the cited study [32] the motivation for the poor results obtained may be that the noise
detected by the denoising methods is mainly focused on signal-free areas of the spectrum
leading to a good result in terms of total RMSE but not in the quantification analysis.
A recent study [16] showed that the GABA quantification may be more robust and effective
if done by quantifying the OFF spectrum instead of the DIFF. This finding is interesting
from a practical point of view because in this case the acquisition time could be limited
to half. Usually, the quantification of the OFF spectrum results in higher CRLB values
compared to the one obtained by the DIFF, therefore diminished values when the denois-
ing methods are applied would indicate the possibility to involve only the OFF spectrum
acquisition, however, this was not observed for any of the tested methods.
Even though the denoising methods have shown to be ineffective in their application as
a preprocessing step, there is great promise in applying techniques such as the L + S or
robust PCA to separate the varying signal in time from the static background [15]. This
aspect of the methods is important when considering functional MRS where the main
interest is in the small metabolites changes between different time acquisitions. The re-
sults obtained show that the method, when applied to simulated DIFF spectra resembling
GABA changes in time, successfully separates its time-varying component from the static
spectrum.
Further work will focus on improving the algorithm by optimizing it with the proper param-
eters and testing it in in vivo data.
