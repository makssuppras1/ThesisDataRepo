Abstract
This thesis investigates single-channel feedforward active noise control in a ventilation
duct using the Filtered-x LMS (FxLMS) algorithm. A one-dimensional duct model in the
plane-wave region is developed, including frequency-dependent losses and reflections
at the flanged terminations, to derive analytical transfer functions for the primary and
secondary paths. A MATLAB simulation framework is then used to study the FxLMS
controller for both idealized pure-delay / downstream-only secondary paths and more
realistic, reflection-rich bidirectional paths. The results show that, when the secondary
path is well behaved and dominated by early components, broadband attenuation of
several tens of decibels can be achieved in the 50–1000 Hz band. However, strong reflec-
tions and bidirectional radiation lead to long, notch-rich impulse responses that severely
limit controllability and reduce the attainable attenuation, even with long adaptive filters.
Measured impulse responses from a physical duct setup are used in offline ANC tests,
which qualitatively confirm the simulation trends but yield only modest noise reduction
in practice. Finally, latency constraints in the available hardware are shown to prevent a
fully causal real-time implementation.
Keywords: Active noise control; Feedforward FxLMS algorithm; Adaptive filtering; Duct
acoustics; Broadband attenuation

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Heating, ventilation, and air-conditioning (HVAC) systems are indispensable to maintain
comfortable indoor environmental conditions for habitation and occupational activities,
yet their operation introduces a pervasive acoustic burden in many buildings. Unlike
intermittent environmental sounds, HVAC noise tends to be continuous, dominated by
low frequencies, and broadly distributed through duct networks [1]. Its principal sources
include rotating machinery (e.g., fans and motors) and turbulence at fittings and dif-
fusers [2]. Such low-frequency noise is often more intrusive than higher-frequency, in-
termittent transients, prolonged exposure has been linked to increased annoyance and
psychophysiological stress, while also degrading speech communication, concentration,
and perceived acoustic comfort [3,4].
Given these consequences, effective HVAC noise control is essential for acoustic comfort
and overall indoor environmental quality, and it is achieved through a combination of
passive and active strategies that are complementary rather than mutually exclusive [5].
Passive noise control (PNC) typically relies on low-noise equipment selection and vibra-
tion isolation, optimization of fittings and duct geometry to suppress turbulence [6], and
acoustic treatments such as porous absorbers [7–9], micro-perforated panels, and dis-
sipative or reactive silencers [10]. Among these passive options, porous absorbers are
widely used, offering their greatest effectiveness in the mid- to high-frequency range.
They dissipate sound primarily through viscous boundary-layer losses. Viscous shear in
the sub-millimetre boundary layer near the pore walls converts kinetic energy into heat.
The local power dissipated per volume is proportional to the square of particle veloc-
ity and its gradients, so where particle velocity is larger, more energy is dissipated [11].
Near a rigid boundary, particle velocity is minimal—approaching zero at the wall—and

to go a considerable distance from the wall to reach a point where the particle velocity

with porous treatments necessitates substantial layer thickness, yielding geometrically
large, bulky constructions that constrain the practicality in most HVAC duct systems.

is required and their bandwidth remains intrinsically limited. A common example is the
Helmholtz resonator (HR), which consists of a rigid-walled expansion chamber coupled
to the duct through a narrow neck that behaves like a lumped mass–spring–damper. At

In summary, these constraints, namely the thickness-driven inefficiency of porous linings
and the narrowband, tuning-sensitive nature of reactive silencers, limit what purely pas-
sive means can achieve at low frequencies in space-constrained HVAC ductwork. This mo-
tivates the use of active noise control (ANC), which employs compact secondary sources
and sensor-based feedforward/feedback control to synthesize a cancelling field with neg-
ligible added pressure drop. In ducted applications, ANC generates a secondary sound
field via small loudspeakers that destructively interfere with the primary noise, while
adaptive signal-processing algorithms update the control signal in real time as operating
conditions change. Compared with passive treatments, ANC is compact, making it fea-
sible in ductwork with tight spatial allowances and enabling broadband low-frequency
attenuation. For existing installations, ANC is readily retrofittable within shafts, plenums,
and equipment housings. In addition, it imposes minimal flow resistance, thereby avoid-
ing fan-power penalties associated with high pressure drop. Hence, ANC can serve as a
complementary not substitutive approach to passive noise control, retaining good perfor-
mance for mid-to-high frequency mitigation while targeting the low-frequency band with
compact, adaptive control.

1.2. Previous Research
1.2
Previous Research
The concept of active noise control was first proposed by Paul Lueg in Germany in 1932.
And then he registered a patent in 1934 with uses including three of the common concepts
of ANC: suppression of noise in ducts, cancellation by interference at points in space,
and creation of protected "zones of quiet". However, early analog implementations were
constrained by hardware latency and stability, as the available detection, processing, and
sound generation systems of the time were inadequate for practical realization, leaving
ANC confined to theoretical studies and analog circuit experiments.
In the 1950s, Harry Olson tackled the topic of ANC systems, using an "electronic sound
absorber" to test the effectiveness of a device designed to provide a quiet area around
the head of a person exposed to a sound field [16]. Reducing the level of noise at the
passenger positions in vehicles was one conceptual application of the system. Although
the system worked, it (and its descendants) later turned out to be sensitive to the listener’s
head position; even slight adjustments to the head location would result in noticeable
variations in the noise that the listener perceived. In the late 1950s, active noise control
was first applied to headphones, marking the emergence of noise-reducing headsets. Their
first notable civilian use occurred in 1986, when Dick Rutan and Jeana Yeager employed
active noise control headsets supplied by Bose Corporation during their around-the-world
flight.
With the advent of digital signal processors (DSPs) and microprocessors, active noise con-
trol entered a new stage in which practical engineering implementation and widespread
application became feasible. The computational capability of DSPs enabled efficient real-
time signal acquisition, processing, and adaptive control, bridging the gap between the-
oretical models and practical systems. This technological advancement directly led to
the emergence of digital ANC systems based on adaptive algorithms, which became the
core of ANC systems, enabling real-time adjustment and adaptation to varying noise
conditions in complex acoustic environments.
Among these algorithms, the Least-Mean-Square (LMS) algorithm proposed by Widrow
and Hoff in 1960 became a representative method due to its simplicity, stability, and capa-
bility for real-time implementation [17]. Its principle is to continuously update the filter
coefficients based on the acquired error signal so as to minimize the mean square error
between the output and the reference signal, thereby achieving effective noise cancella-
tion. Extensive research has been conducted to evaluate the convergence and tracking
performance of the LMS algorithm [18–21]. It has been found that LMS employs a fixed
step size to iteratively approach the optimal filter coefficients, resulting in a fundamental
trade-off between convergence speed and stability. A small step size ensures stability but
results in slow convergence and low computational efficiency, making it difficult for the
system to adapt to changing noise conditions in time. Conversely, an excessively large
step size may cause oscillations or divergence. Moreover, the LMS algorithm assumes that

the input signal statistics remain relatively stationary. When the noise spectrum varies
significantly over time, the algorithm must reconverge to adapt to the new conditions,
often exhibiting transient instability or lag, which degrades its overall noise reduction
performance.
To address these limitations of the LMS algorithm, researchers have developed several
variants to overcome specific drawbacks of the conventional approach. The Variable-
Step-Size LMS (VSS-LMS) algorithm, for instance, improves upon the standard LMS by
adaptively adjusting the step size parameter [22]. Based on the magnitude of the residual
error and the desired signal, the algorithm increases the step size when the error is
large to accelerate convergence toward the optimal solution, and decreases it when the
error becomes small to avoid overshooting. In such variable step-size filtering schemes,
a relatively large step size is initially used to achieve rapid convergence, followed by a
smaller step size near the steady-state phase to enhance stability. This adaptive adjustment
compensates for the slow convergence and limited tracking ability of the conventional LMS
algorithm, enabling faster and more efficient adaptation to dynamic noise environments.
The Normalized Least-Mean-Square (NLMS) algorithm is also an extension of the con-
ventional LMS method [20,23]. Its main difference lies in normalizing the step size by the
instantaneous power of the input signal, allowing adaptive adjustment according to sig-
nal energy. This normalization overcomes the LMS algorithm’s sensitivity to variations in
input power. NLMS is particularly suitable for scenarios with large fluctuations in signal
energy, such as speech signals, and is widely applied in acoustic echo cancellation, where
it effectively improves the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of speech in noisy environments.
Although the LMS algorithm and its variants perform effectively in general adaptive
filtering tasks, their direct application to active noise control systems faces inherent lim-
itations. In ANC, the control signal 𝑦[𝑛] generated by the adaptive filter is not directly
compared with the error signal 𝑒[𝑛], but rather passes through the so-called secondary
path, which comprises the power amplifier, loudspeaker, acoustic propagation channel,
and error microphone. This path introduces additional delay, phase shift, and frequency-
dependent distortion due to the finite sound-propagation time and the non-minimum
phase characteristics of acoustic transmission between spatially separated transducers.
As a result, the inverse of secondary path 𝑆−1(𝑧) would be non-causal and thus cannot be
physically realized. The observed error is not an instantaneous linear function of the filter
output but rather a delayed convolution of the control signal with the secondary-path re-
sponse, rendering the control-to-error relationship inherently time-delayed and coupled.
Without proper compensation for these dynamics, conventional LMS-type algorithms
may become unstable or fail to converge. To ensure stable adaptation in the presence of
such a secondary path, researchers developed the Filtered-x Least-Mean-Square (FxLMS)
algorithm [24–26].
The FxLMS algorithm compensates for the dynamics of the secondary path by pre-filtering
the reference signal with an estimate of its transfer function before the weight-update op-

Building upon these algorithmic advancements, the field of active noise control has con-

actuators, and digital signal processing has greatly enhanced its stability, adaptability,
and implementation efficiency. Today, ANC is widely integrated into various commercial
and industrial applications, including aviation and automotive cabins, consumer head-
phones, ventilation ducts, and exhaust systems, where it provides compact and effective
low-frequency noise reduction that cannot be achieved by passive means alone.

Thesis Objectives
In ducted environments such as HVAC ventilation systems, where the dominant acoustic
energy lies in the low-frequency plane-wave region, the sound field can be accurately
approximated as one-dimensional. Under these conditions, a single-channel feedforward
FxLMS controller can achieve effective broadband attenuation with minimal hardware
complexity. Compared with passive absorptive materials, whose performance deterio-

uation of several to more than ten decibels in real time, providing an effective solution for
low-frequency HVAC noise mitigation.

3.1
Feedforward and Feedback Configu-
rations in ANC
The physical implementation of an active noise control system constitutes an electro-
acoustic system, in which a secondary sound field is generated to achieve destructive
interference with the primary noise. Depending on how the control signal is derived
and updated, ANC systems can be generally classified into feedback and feedforward
configurations. However, their sensing strategies, stability characteristics, and practical
applications differ significantly.
3.1.1
Feedback Control Strategy
In a feedback system, the control sound is synthesized based solely on the error signal 𝑒(𝑛)
measured by an error microphone located near the point of desired quieting, as illustrated
in Fig. 3.1.
Specifically, the microphone captures the residual difference between the
primary noise and canceling sounds generated by secondary loudspeaker, and this error
serves as the feedback signal for the adaptive filter. Based on the instantaneous value
of 𝑒(𝑛), the controller updates its filter coefficients and produces a new output 𝑦(𝑛) in
real time, thereby continuously adjusting the secondary signal to minimize the remaining
disturbance.
Because it does not require a separate reference sensor, feedback ANC is particularly useful
in applications where a reference signal is unavailable or impractical to measure, such as
18
==PAGE:28==
3.1. Feedforward and Feedback Configurations in ANC
Figure 3.1: Schematic of single-channel feedback ANC system configuration. The solid
curves represent acoustic waves propagating downstream toward the duct outlet, whereas
the dashed curves denote waves traveling upstream in the opposite direction.
in headphones or enclosed acoustic spaces. However, since the feedback controller reacts
only to the residual error that has already occurred, the control signal cannot be updated
until the disturbance has already produced an error at the microphone. This makes the
structure inherently reactive rather than predictive. As a consequence, if the disturbance
changes rapidly, the resulting error signal also varies quickly, and the controller cannot
update its coefficients fast enough to track these variations, leading to delay-induced phase
errors and even potential instability. Therefore, feedback ANC is best suited for periodic
or narrowband noise with known and slowly varying frequency characteristics.
In addition, feedback ANC is highly sensitive to the dynamics of the secondary path.
Because the phase response of the secondary path lies directly in the closed loop, any
significant phase delay introduced by the secondary path can severely compromise stabil-
ity. At higher frequencies, the accumulated phase shift may approach ±180◦, causing the
intended negative feedback to behave effectively as positive feedback. In this situation,
the control will amplify rather than attenuate the residual disturbance, and if this ampli-
fied signal is repeatedly fed back through the loop, it can grow over time and ultimately
destabilize the system. For this reason, feedback ANC can maintain stable operation only
when the secondary path introduces sufficiently small phase delay. This condition is eas-
ier to satisfy in small acoustic cavities, for instance in headphones or earcups, where the
inherent secondary-path delay is minimal and feedback control is correspondingly more
feasible and stable.
It is precisely this strong dependence on the secondary-path phase delay that has mo-
tivated researchers to develop several modified feedback structures. A representative
example is the virtual earth feedback architecture [33,34], which introduces an internally
generated reference-like signal to reduce the effective loop delay seen by the controller.
These architectures improve robustness and allow stable control even when the physical
secondary path exhibits non-negligible delay.

3.1.2
Feedforward Control Strategy
In contrast, a feedforward ANC system utilizes an additional upstream reference micro-
phone to capture the primary noise before it reaches the error microphone. As illustrated
in Fig. 3.2, the reference signal 𝑥(𝑛) provides advance information about the disturbance,
allowing the controller to predict the noise that will later arrive at the error sensor. The
adaptive filter then processes this reference signal to generate a compensation signal that
propagates through the duct and interacts with the primary noise. The resulting residual
pressure at the error microphone is fed back to the adaptive algorithm to update the
control filter coefficients.

than reactive. The spatial placement of the reference microphone must ensure a sufficient
causal interval to compensate for the inherent delays in the digital signal processing

delays originated from the computer’s audio subsystem, the computational latency of the
adaptive algorithm, as well as digital-to-analog conversion (DAC) time. In other words,
the secondary loudspeaker must emit sound earlier than the arrival time of the primary
noise at the secondary source location, mathematically expressed as

Furthermore, in a feedforward ANC system, the controller is driven by a reference signal
rather than by the error signal, in contrast to feedback structures where the controller input
directly depends on the residual error generated by its own output. For the feedforward
case, the error microphone serves only to update the adaptive filter coefficients, a process
that will be explained in detail in the following section. As a result, the feedforward
configuration forms an open-loop control structure, meaning that the secondary path
does not reside inside a closed feedback loop. Consequently, feedforward ANC cannot
exhibit closed-loop instability induced by the secondary path dynamics. Nevertheless,
the system may still encounter forms of adaptive instability, such as coefficient divergence,
high-frequency oscillations or overshoots due to secondary path mismatch or excessive
phase lag, and a general degradation of noise attenuation performance.
These characteristics make feedforward ANC particularly suitable for pipework systems,
automotive and other scenarios where the noise travels along a well-defined path and can
be measured upstream before reaching the error sensor.
In the remainder of this thesis, we focus exclusively on the feedforward ANC architecture.
The adaptive algorithms underlying this configuration, in particular the FxLMS method,
will be introduced in the following section.
3.2
Adaptive Algorithms
In signal processing, filtering refers to a linear operation that modifies the spectral content
of an input signal in a specific way. Conventional filters are linear and time-invariant.
They perform a constant set of linear operations on the input sequence 𝑥[𝑛], producing an
output with predetermined magnitude and phase characteristics. In the case of adaptive
Page 21 of 77
==PAGE:31==
3.2. Adaptive Algorithms
filters, this restriction of time invariance is removed. Adaptive filtering means that filter
parameters such as bandwidth and resonant frequency vary with time. This is achieved
by continuously updating the filter coefficients using an algorithm, which usually ac-
complish the modification (adaptation) automatically. As a result, adaptive filters can
operate effectively in situations where the required filtering operation is unknown or is
nonstationary.

3.2.2
Filtered-x Least-Mean-Square Algorithm
In a practical feedforward ANC system, as illustrated in Fig. 3.2, it’s evident that the
adaptive filter output is not directly linked to the error signal as implied in Eq. (3.6).
Instead, it must propagate through the entire secondary path before it contributes to the
residual error detected at the error microphone.
Moreover, the signal flow becomes considerably more complex by the fact that the elec-
trical reference and error signals must be obtained from the acoustic pressure using
microphones.
Likewise, the control output generated by the adaptive filter must be
converted back into an acoustic signal by driving a loudspeaker. Consequently, several
additional electroacoustic transfer functions associated with the sensors, actuators, and
acoustic propagation paths must be incorporated into the system model.
Therefore, the secondary path comprises not only the acoustic propagation between the
secondary loudspeaker and the error microphone, but also all intermediate electronic and
electroacoustic components, including the D/A converter, reconstruction filter, power
amplifier, secondary loudspeaker, error microphone, the preamplifier, anti-aliasing filter,
and the A/D converter.
Each element in the secondary path contributes a non-negligible processing or prop-
agation delay.
For example, the D/A and A/D converters require several sampling
periods, the reconstruction and anti-aliasing filters exhibit finite group delay, and the
loudspeaker–microphone acoustic path has an inherent propagation time determined by

influence of the adaptive filter output. If a standard LMS update rule in Eq. (3.17) were

3.3
Update Schemes
3.3.1
Sample-by-Sample Processing
In the sample-by-sample or sample-based update scheme, the adaptive filter coefficients
are updated once for every new input sample. At time index 𝑛, the filter output 𝑦[𝑛], the
secondary-path filtered reference signal ˜x[𝑛], and the error signal 𝑒[𝑛] are computed, and
the coefficient vector is immediately updated according to the adaptation rule in Eq. (3.25).
This approach offers the fastest possible adaptation rate because the filter reacts to each
incoming sample without delay. For broadband or rapidly time-varying disturbances,
such immediate updates allow the controller to track changes in the signal statistics
and maintain good cancellation performance. Moreover, sample-by-sample processing
provides the lowest algorithmic delay, which is beneficial in ANC systems where excessive
latency directly reduces achievable attenuation due to causality constraints.
However, this scheme requires that all computations—reference filtering, secondary-path
convolution, and coefficient updates—be completed within one sampling interval. As
a result, the computational load grows linearly with the adaptive filter length L and
the secondary-path model length S. For high sampling rates or long filters, real-time
implementation may therefore become challenging, especially on embedded systems with
limited processing power.

Streaming Block-Based Processing
In ANC systems, the anti-noise output 𝑦[𝑛] must be generated immediately at each
sampling instant to maintain the required causal relationship between the reference,

For this reason, real-time ANC implementations typically use a streaming block-based
approach, in which the controller still computes the anti-noise output sample-by-sample,
but accumulates data over a block and performs the adaptive weight update only once

1. Computes the anti-noise output 𝑦[𝑛] and sends it immediately to the loudspeaker.

3. Apply one coefficient update for the entire block once the buffer is complete.
This streaming formulation preserves the real-time output path while retaining the com-
putational advantages of block updates. Therefore, it is adopted in this thesis for the
implementation of the adaptive controller.

4.4.1
Truncation of Impulse Responses
The impulse responses of the primary and secondary path often contain long trailing
portions with extremely low energy, originating from high-order reflections. These weak
tail components contribute negligibly to the physical propagation of sound, yet they sub-
stantially increase the computational burden of the FxLMS algorithm. Each additional
sample in h[𝑛] or s[𝑛] participates in the convolution used to compute the desired signal
d[𝑛] and the filtered-x signal ˜x(𝑛), thereby increasing the number of multiply-accumulate
operations, the algorithm’s effective memory, and the real-time latency. Moreover, al-
though their amplitudes are insignificant from an acoustic perspective, these samples still
influence the adaptation process. The algorithm attempts to "learn" the near-zero tail
coefficients, which slows convergence and wastes computational effort. For these reasons,
it is both physically justified and computationally advantageous to truncate the impulse
responses to their effective duration that contains the dominant energy of the acoustic
path without compromising the fidelity of the underlying model.
In practice, the impulse responses are typically truncated according to an energy-based
criterion, retaining the portion that contains approximately 90–99% of the total accumu-
lated energy. This strategy preserves the dominant physical characteristics of the acoustic
paths while discarding the insignificant late reflections that contribute little to the control
performance.
If the cutoff is chosen too aggressively, the truncated impulse response may become
shorter than the true effective duration of the acoustic path. In such cases, important
propagation features such as late but non-negligible reflections may be removed. This
loss of information leads to a mismatched path model, which degrades the accuracy of
h[𝑛] and s[𝑛]. Consequently, the FxLMS algorithm receives an incomplete or distorted
representation of the physical system, which may impair convergence, reduce achievable
noise attenuation, or even destabilize the adaptation process. Therefore, truncation must
be applied carefully: the window should be long enough to capture all acoustically
meaningful components of the impulse response, yet short enough to avoid unnecessary

computational overhead and long tail portions that do not contribute to physical sound
propagation.
4.4.2
Determination of Convergence Time
In an adaptive ANC system, convergence does not mean that the instantaneous error
becomes zero. Instead, it denotes the stage where the residual error has settled to a stable
minimum given the modeling errors, secondary-path approximation, and the inherent
noise floor. This stable region indicates that the adaptive filter has stopped making large
changes and ANC performance has settled, extending the experiment duration no longer
improves the average noise-reduction performance.
To determine when convergence occurs, we monitor how the error signal evolves over
time using a moving RMS measure. First, a 1-second sliding window is used to compute
the RMS of the error signal, providing a smooth representation of how the sound-pressure
amplitude changes throughout the entire experiment. Then, to assess whether the system
has stabilized, we evaluate the RMS over a trailing window of fixed duration. This trailing
window is defined as a backward-looking interval that ends at time 𝑡and extends several
seconds into the past; therefore, the stability test is causal and depends only on past and
current samples.

for improving ANC performance. Several strategies have been proposed to address this
issue.
If only a single secondary loudspeaker is used, one possible approach is to adjust its
position in order to shift the locations of the notches in the secondary-path transfer
function. However, this strategy offers only limited practical benefit: eliminating a notch
at one frequency inevitably introduces a different notch at another frequency, making it
impossible to achieve broadband attenuation simply through geometric adjustment. In
other words, with a single actuator, one can only “move” the uncontrollable frequencies
rather than remove them.
A more effective solution is to employ multiple secondary sources whose transfer functions
do not share common zeros. As demonstrated in [38], the joint use of multiple actuators
expands the controllable frequency range, since the zeros of one secondary path may be
compensated by the non-zero response of another, thereby restoring the ability of the
adaptive algorithm to update in those bands.

Another approach is to introduce an equalizer that shapes the secondary-path frequency
response to be more uniform [39]. By reducing the depth and irregularity of the notches
in |𝑆( 𝑓)|, equalization preserves the energy of the filtered-x signal across the spectrum, en-
abling more reliable adaptation and often yielding significantly improved noise-reduction
performance.

Experimental Verification
Although the initial objective was to conduct real-time active noise control experiments,
practical limitations of the current hardware made such implementation infeasible for
the present setup. The duct used in this study has a relatively short physical propagation
distance, with only about 2.2 m between the reference microphone and the secondary loud-
speaker, corresponding to an acoustic travel time of approximately 6.4 ms. To maintain
stable and effective real-time control, the total digital latency, comprising audio interface
buffering, ADC and DAC delays, and computational processing time, must remain shorter
than the acoustic delay. However, preliminary testing showed that the available audio
interface and computing hardware could not meet this requirement without introducing
buffer underruns or excessive latency.
Therefore, all experimental validation in this chapter is conducted in an offline manner.
Instead of running the adaptive algorithm in real time, the impulse responses of primary
and secondary path were recorded first, and the FxLMS algorithm is subsequently applied
offline to evaluate the achievable control performance. This offline framework retains
the essential physics of the system, including the true acoustic propagation paths and
hardware-induced delays.

Offline ANC Test
In this section, the measured impulse responses are used to replace the physically modeled
paths developed in the previous chapter. As before, the analysis is carried out entirely in
MATLAB simulations rather than in real-time experiments.
Before incorporating these measured paths into the ANC framework, several preprocess-
ing steps are applied to the impulse responses to ensure that they accurately represent the
underlying acoustic propagation and are suitable for numerical simulation.
Because of the substantial delay introduced by the signal-processing chain, the measured
impulse responses contain a long segment of leading zeros before the arrival of the
first physical peak. These artificial delays do not represent acoustic propagation; rather,
they would significantly increase the computational load and memory requirement of the
FxLMS algorithm, since every additional sample contributes to the convolution operations
for both the desired signal and the filtered-x signal. To avoid unnecessary computation
while preserving the true physical response, the impulse responses are trimmed. The
truncation is aligned to the first significant peak of the secondary-path impulse response,
with a small safety margin retained, and the same number of leading samples is removed
from both the primary and secondary paths so that their relative delay is preserved.
Then, the first significant peak of the primary-path response occurs at sample 388, while
the secondary-path peak appears at sample 51.
It should be emphasized that this trimming of the leading samples is only possible in
the fully simulated environment where all signals are generated and processed digitally,
it is not valid in real experiments, even when the noise waveform is known beforehand.
Although the broadband noise used in the experiment is generated by MATLAB, once
it is played through a loudspeaker it becomes a physical signal and must pass through
the complete causal chain consisting of DAC delay, loudspeaker group delay, acoustic
propagation, microphone response, ADC delay, and buffering. These delays collectively
determine the true physical timing of the ANC system. Consequently, the leading de-
lay present in the measured impulse responses cannot be removed, because doing so
would artificially shorten the physical propagation path and incorrectly imply that the
secondary sound reaches the error microphone earlier than is physically possible. Over-
all, the physical propagation and hardware delays must be strictly respected in real ANC
implementations.

Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
This thesis has investigated the feasibility and performance of single-channel feedforward
active noise control in ventilation ducts using the FxLMS algorithm. Motivated by the
need for effective low-frequency noise reduction in HVAC systems, where traditional
passive treatments become bulky and inefficient, the work focused on a one-dimensional
duct configuration in the plane-wave regime and examined both idealized and realistic
acoustic conditions.
The starting point was a theoretical review of duct acoustics, including plane-wave prop-
agation, modal cut-on frequencies, frequency-dependent losses, and reflection effects at
duct terminations. These fundamentals provided the basis for deriving analytical trans-
fer functions for the primary and secondary acoustic paths in a circular duct. Special
attention was given to modeling the reflection coefficient at the flanged open ends and
the impact of multiple reflections on the frequency response of the paths. In parallel, the
thesis reviewed feedforward and feedback ANC architectures and introduced the FxLMS
algorithm together with a streaming block-based update scheme, which balances real-time
suitability with computational efficiency.
Building on this foundation, a comprehensive simulation framework was developed to
model the primary and secondary paths and to implement the single-channel feedforward
FxLMS controller. The secondary path was studied under two configurations: an idealized
downstream-only radiation (Case A), and a more realistic bidirectional radiation (Case B).
The simulations also incorporated practical processing strategies, such as energy-based
truncation of impulse responses and an automated convergence-time detector based on
the coefficient of variation of a moving RMS measure.
71
==PAGE:81==
6.1. Conclusion
The simulation results first confirmed that, for purely delayed acoustic paths without
reflections, the FxLMS implementation operates correctly and can achieve near-complete
cancellation of broadband noise. When realistic reflections and losses were included,
the behavior became strongly dependent on the characteristics of the secondary path. In
the downstream-only configuration with relatively short, 90%-energy–truncated impulse
responses, the controller achieved very high broadband attenuation in the plane-wave
region (about 44 dB for white noise and 47 dB for pink noise over 50–1000 Hz). These
results demonstrate the potential of single-channel feedforward FxLMS control when the
secondary path is well behaved and predominantly downstream-radiating.
However, as more reflections were incorporated into the secondary-path model, the
impulse response became longer and more dispersive, and the corresponding transfer
function developed a comb-like spectrum with deep, irregularly spaced notches. Un-
der these conditions, the filtered reference signal lost energy at the notch frequencies,
severely limiting the ability of the FxLMS algorithm to update the coefficients at those fre-
quencies. As a consequence, low-frequency attenuation deteriorated and the broadband
attenuation in the 50–1000 Hz band dropped to roughly 10–13 dB, despite using much
longer adaptive filters. The situation worsened further in Case B, where bidirectional
radiation introduced upstream-propagating components and additional reflections. The
secondary-path impulse response became even more complex, the maximum stable step
size was reduced, convergence slowed, and frequency-dependent attenuation degraded
across multiple bands. These findings highlight that the secondary-path zeros and overall
spectral structure fundamentally constrain the performance of single-channel ANC.
In the experimental part of the work, a physical duct setup was constructed. The primary
and secondary paths were measured using logarithmic sweeps, and the resulting impulse
responses were preprocessed by removing non-radiated very-low-frequency components
and truncating based on cumulative energy. These measured paths were then imported
into MATLAB for offline FxLMS tests. The offline experiments confirmed the qualitative
trends predicted by the simulations but achieved only modest noise reduction in practice.
In the low-frequency plane-wave region, the attenuation fluctuated between 0 and roughly
18 dB, with a local maximum of about 30 dB near 1 kHz and negligible suppression above
the duct cutoff frequency, where higher-order modes dominate. The limited improvement
is attributed to several factors: the strong spectral irregularities of the measured paths,
the truncation of long, reflection-rich primary-path tails (which leaves uncontrollable
residual components), and the inherently multimodal, multi-path interference field at
higher frequencies that cannot be matched by a single secondary source and a one-
dimensional control model.
Finally, the thesis examined the practical feasibility of real-time implementation. The
short physical distance between the reference microphone and the secondary loudspeaker
yielded an acoustic delay of only about 6.4 ms, whereas the available audio interface and
computing platform introduced comparable or larger digital latency when operated with
stable buffer sizes. This prevented the realization of a causal real-time feedforward ANC

system within the current hardware constraints.
Overall, these findings provide both a validated simulation framework and an experimen-
tal reference for understanding the capabilities and limitations of single-channel feedfor-
ward ANC in ventilation ducts, and they inform the design choices for more advanced
multichannel or hybrid systems in future work.
6.2
Future Work
The present study suggests several directions for future research aimed at overcoming the
identified limitations and moving toward practical real-time ANC solutions in ventilation
systems.
First, the limitations of single-channel control in multimodal and strongly reflecting
ducts point naturally toward multichannel ANC architectures. Future work could in-
vestigate systems with multiple secondary loudspeakers and multiple error microphones
distributed along the duct. If the individual secondary paths are designed so that their
transfer functions do not share common zeros, the combined actuator–sensor set can
restore controllability across previously uncontrollable frequency bands and enable at-
tenuation of higher-order modes above the duct cutoff frequency. This would require
extending the FxLMS algorithm to its multichannel form and carefully addressing inter-
channel coupling and stability issues.
Second, further improvements could be pursued at the algorithmic level. One promising
direction is to employ variable-step-size variants of the FxLMS algorithm. By starting
with a relatively large step size and gradually reducing it as the error decreases, such
schemes can combine fast initial convergence with a finer steady-state solution, leading
to smaller misadjustment than the fixed-step-size FxLMS used in this thesis. In addition,
normalized variants of the FxLMS algorithm could be explored to account for the highly
non-uniform energy distribution of the filtered reference signal across frequency.
By
scaling the adaptation step with the local signal power, such normalized schemes prevent
high-energy bands from dominating the update while still allowing low-energy regions
to be adjusted, which is particularly beneficial for coloured reference signals such as pink
noise. Finally, leaky variants of the FxLMS algorithm, where a small leakage factor is
introduced in the coefficient update, could be investigated to improve robustness against
modelling errors and slow time variations of the acoustic paths. Such leakage helps to
limit unbounded coefficient growth, reduce sensitivity to poorly excited frequency bands,
and facilitate tracking of slowly time-varying duct and loudspeaker characteristics.
Finally, the modelling framework could be extended to account for additional physical
effects that were neglected or simplified in this work. In practical ventilation systems, a
mean air flow is almost always present, which modifies the phase speed and attenuation
Page 73 of 77
==PAGE:83==
6.2. Future Work
of acoustic modes and introduces flow–acoustic interaction and flow-generated noise;
incorporating convective effects and realistic flow profiles into the duct model would
therefore make the predictions more representative of real HVAC conditions. Further
extensions could include more detailed loudspeaker and microphone radiation models,
wall compliance and structural vibration of the duct, as well as three-dimensional or mul-
timodal simulations that explicitly represent higher-order modes, bends, and junctions in
branched duct networks. Such extensions would provide a more complete understand-
ing of ANC performance in realistic HVAC systems and support the design of robust
controllers for complex duct configurations.
