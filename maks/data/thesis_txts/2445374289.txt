Abstract
The traditional process of diagnosing antennas relies on a static receiver with a well
calibrated position. In the case of cell towers, this results in a slow and expensive pro-
cess. This work studies the possibility of imaging a static system in the presence of
systematic and random errors and the robustness of such imaging method, which could
be applied in order to measure cell tower antennas using a drone.
Interferometric inversion is suggested for the recovery of the excitation of an active
system, for which measured ﬁeld values and a ﬁeld propagation model are given. The
advantage of interferometric inversion in contrast to the ordinary least squares inversion
is its inherent robustness to systematic errors in the ﬁeld propagation model; these could
for example be a relative movement between the measured system and the probe, or a
wrongly assumed propagation speed. Two formulations of the interferometric inversion
are considered, and the selection of diﬀerent data-pairs is investigated and found to be
important for inversion.
Both formulations are compared to the ordinary least squares inversion in terms of the
errors in the recovered excitation, which arise as a consequence of random or systematic
errors. The suitability of this method for ﬁeld values measured from a drone, which are
associated with both random and systematic errors, is discussed as a practical example.
keywords: interferometric inversion, antenna measurements, drone measurements, least
squares inversion

1. Introduction
Modern day society depends heavily on fast and reliable communication. The demand
for higher quality and speed is rising, and with it the maintenance of the infrastruc-
ture for mobile communication. An important part of mobile communication is the cell
towers, at which antenna arrays are receiving and transmitting the information sent.
Diagnosing these antenna arrays in a fast an eﬃcient manner would be very desirable
improvement for the mobile industry, since the process today is expensive and time-
consuming. Measuring the cell tower antennas with drones would be a solution to this
problem.
In its essence, this thesis is about improving imaging on a static object with system-
atic errors, by exploiting the basic principle of interference of electromagnetic waves;
how this relates to drone measurements will become more clear. The method used is a
certain way of combining data, and has been reported to give a more robust recovery,
when the errors are the movement of a physical object during imaging, or some wrongly
estimated parameters of the surrounding medium. Historically, the characterisation of
antennas often requires large chambers and expensive equipment, because the measure-
ments should be without disturbances from changing outside environments with noise
from other communication devices. But since antennas are often deployed in outside en-
vironments, the measurements are taken in a much more ideal scenario than the actual
application, and in many cases, on site measurements might be more practically useful.
Thus recent research has gone into ﬁnding good ways to characterise antennas on site
with much less cost - using e.g. drones. A special discipline is the reconstruction of ﬁelds
or equivalent currents close to the radiating elements. Some applications are detecting
faulty elements in antenna arrays and non-destructive testing in general.
The ﬁeld close to an antenna is called the near-ﬁeld, and equivalent currents are currents
mapped on a certain geometry around the antenna, that would produce the same mea-
sured ﬁelds. The reconstruction of these are always a matter of solving inverse problems,
meaning that the equations that govern the radiation from the antenna array have some
variables in them, diﬀerent from the ones that are measured, which we would like to
solve for.
This thesis is a documentation of work done to test, whether quadratic combinations
of data could yield more robust recovery for inverse problems with systematic errors.
The method is known as interferometric imaging, which comes from the fact that the
quadratic data combinations create a matrix which entities is the correlation of intensities
between measurements.

1.2. Interferometry
Interference arises when two waves interact, and the superposition principle enables us
two understand this interaction to be a resulting wave as a sum of the two. As with
any wave, this wave can be characterised by its amplitude and phase information, and
herein lies the problem to be solved. Our eyes do not see oscillating ﬁelds when they
register the light around us, meaning that this example of our eyes, like many physical
systems, only measures intensities, where the absolute phase information is lost. This is
known in imaging, signal processing and similar ﬁelds as the phase problem. Interfero-
metric inversion tries to circumvent this problem, not by trying to calculate the absolute

and altitude mapping as in Google Maps.

2. Theory
Interferometric inversion is a combination interferometry and inverse problems. To pro-
vide the reader with suﬃcient background knowledge, the ﬁrst sections will be a theoret-
ical overview of the main points from the topic, starting from the concept of interference
of waves, through interferometry, inverse problems, optimisation and noise in inverse
problems. The theory behind creation and use of interferometric data might not be par-
ticularly advanced or diﬃcult, while the physical interpretation is less intuitive. From
an engineering perspective, the physical interpretation is of utmost importance since it
is crucial to whether the method is applicable or not, and how to design the system if it
is.
2.1. Interference
Interferometry is based on the concept of interference, which is the resulting pattern from
the superposition of two waves interacting. The interference is said to be constructive if
the waves are in phase at a certain point in space, and destructive if the waves are out
of phase in a certain point in space, which respectively are the peaks and troughs in the
pattern. Figure 2.1 shows an interference pattern of ﬁve isotropic sources placed on a
line.
2.2. Measuring electromagnetic waves
The interference pattern in Figure 2.1 is in fact generated by the equations governing
the electric ﬁeld from ﬁve small antennas. When several antennas are considered a part
of the same conﬁguration, it is called an antenna array. The physical equivalent to what
is shown is the real part of the phasor of the resulting electric ﬁeld, which is the same
as the oscillating electric ﬁeld in time-domain, at t = 0. From this picture we could
easily extract phase and magnitude of the electric ﬁeld at any point. But unfortunately
physical measurement systems do in many cases not measure the magnitude and phase
of a system, but rather the intensity as shown in Figure 2.2.
The star in Figure 2.2 represents a measurement point, which would be a receiving
antenna of any kind. The equations used in this thesis are based on the far-ﬁeld ap-
proximation of the electric ﬁeld of the antenna array. Far enough from the antenna the
radiation seems to some degree to radiate from a single point, from where the far-ﬁeld
has an absolute phase reference, and the magnitude decays as one over the distance.
In inverse problems the absolute phase is important for recovering the solution, and

for the main-beam, if the target was a directive antenna-array.
An optimisation process is continuously taking place in a scanning antenna array. In
mobile communications, signal power and thus signal quality is improved by establishing
directive channels between user and base-stations and masts. Then a query is sent to
run the optimisation loop with every ping from a mobile device.

3. Benchmark
Evaluating the performance of an algorithm for engineering purposes requires a setup
that translates easily to the real world. Measuring antenna arrays and characterising
them using drones and other Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) is a possible real life
application for interferometric inversion, since the application requires some of the most
extreme robustness to shifting conditions for the inversion, such as wind gusts altering
the position of both receivers and AUTs, and rain altering the background velocity of
the radiated waves. Whether or not interferometric inversion can aid in solving these
problems turns out to depend much on the formulation of the problem in terms of how
the solver interprets the problems to be solved. Thus in this section we will present the
speciﬁc test case, and brieﬂy discuss the challenges of choosing the best method.

5.2. Selector Matrices
Including all quadratic data combinations for the interferometric recovery is very com-
putationally expensive, and hence it would be advantageous to reduce redundant infor-
mation, and still ensure the same optimal recovery. The study by Demanet and Jugnon
in [4], shows that recovery is stable as long as the selection of elements in the large
matrix B = ⃗b⃗b
H is ”characterised by a well connected graph”. What this means in their
deﬁnition is a graph for which the graph Laplacian has a second eigenvalue that is large,
since the they prove that the error of the recovery in the inverse problem is inversely
proportional to the square root of this eigenvalue of the weighted graph-Laplacian (a
41
==PAGE:42==
matrix calculated on B solely).
They propose that one could generate random well-connected graphs by generating
Erdos-Renyi random graphs.
Furthermore they also show that by selecting the ”nearest-neighbours” in B, they achieve
better than the ordinary least squares recovery in the case with a 10% error in the back-
ground velocity of the wave. By nearest neighbours is meant the closest measurement
points in terms of the geometric euclidean norm.
Demanet and Jugnon also mention in [4] that the choice of selector needs further study.
They therefore don’t state more signiﬁcant considerations regarding this matter, al-
though, they do mention that there exists a trade-oﬀbetween the robustness to what
is here called systematic errors, and the number of quadratic pairs needed for stable
inversion. Which might mean that using less quadratic pairs leads to greater robustness
to systematic errors.
Demanet and Jugnon chose to combine the nearest neighbouring measurement points,
for reasons we ﬁnd unclear. Therefore it is not known whether this would work in our
case. To shed some light on the choice of the right selector for this given setup, eight
diﬀerent selectors will be studied here. Note that the notation for the selection of ele-
ments in B is sometimes written as a set El = {(i, j)|si,j ̸= 0}, and the speciﬁc selector
matrix Sl ∈M×M. The subscript l denotes the selector number in this case. In this
small problem the matrix notation gives a more visual impression. The set notation
is preferably used in literature for the sake of larger problems than the one considered
here. The selector-matrices considered are:
• Selector 1: Full matrix
• Selector 2: Self-interactions only
• Selector 3: Full matrix except diagonal
• Selector 4: Nearest Neighbours with diagonal
• Selector 5: Erdos-Renyi Graph
• Selector 6: Erdos-Renyi Graph with diagonal ensured elements
• Selector 7: Upper triangular elements
• Selector 8: Nearest Neighbours without diagonal

5.6. Reformulation
The reason why the interferometric solver did not perform better in any type of error
is not completely clear, though one explanation might be interdependent measurements.
The quadratic system is considering M2 equations and N2 unknowns. This would look
like a highly over-determined system for M2 = 121 and N2 = 25 for our setup. But
the M2 equations are not unique, supported by the fact that ⃗b⃗b
H is a symmetric rank
1 matrix. On the other hand, X is relaxed to a matrix of possible maximum rank ˜X,
therefore having independent columns and rows. This might cause the system to be
under-determined even though M2 ≫N2. If this is the case, then the system would
need regularisation in order to recover the original excitation.
Formulation A is very slow, it takes up to 14 seconds to run (full matrix M2 × N2 =
121 × 25), compared to a mere 0.03 seconds for the OLS. Simulation time is further
increased when the ﬁt parameter σ is too low for the problem to be feasible, since the
program then decreases the ﬁt (increase in σ), and runs the optmisation again. This is
not inherently a downside of the interferometric problem nor CVX. In fact CVX has a
default precision of the solver at √ϵ ≈1.49 · 10−8, where ϵ is the machine precision, and
this is quite suﬃcient for our case. Furthermore CVX has processes that automatically
try to increase the precision of a solution if more is to be gained, than the precision spec-
iﬁed. But the precision has to be ”internally speciﬁed”, where our precision is speciﬁed
as a constraint. Other formulations have thus been investigated to see if better results
were to be gained from here, hence also the name ”Formulation A” - indicating that
more than one formulation is considered.
The formulations can be seen in the appendix in Section A. Since we cannot know if
certain selectors perform better for certain solvers, all solvers for all formulations are
tested in the recovery with ideal measurements where SNR = ∞and no error in the
forward model operator so ˜
A = A. Figure A.1, A.2 and A.3 does not show selector 2
for any of the formulations, since it failed at recovering the ideal case with a recovery
error below 10%, and is therefore not expected to do any better with errors introduced.
Therefore selector 2, which only includes the diagonal elements, is ruled out as a viable
selector for interferometric inversion. For the same reasons, the curve for selector 8 is
not shown for formulation C. Selectors 5 and 6 do not show great recovery either, but
since they are random graphs, this particular result might not be representative.

system to a precision close to the machine ϵ, most of the blue and red curves displays
recovery errors around the precision of CVX, and the green curve displays recovery er-

6. Results and comparison of the methods

been presented. The assessment of these methods depends on the application. On one
hand, when deciding on faulty elements in an array, the error can be quite high as seen

exact values of the excitation or other parameters like contrast or permitivity, such as
in tomography. On this note, the results will then be evaluated ﬁrst with the scope on
achieving the lowest possible error, and next with faulty element detection in mind.

7. Conclusions
7.1. Discussion
This project set out to study, test and understand the method of interferometric inver-
sion. In particular its validity for the measurement of cell tower antenna arrays. The
results presented in this report show that interferometric inversion is a suitable method
for solving inverse problems in the framework of antenna measurements. Over the nu-
merous experiments and tests, the choice of diﬀerent quadratic data pairs has been found
to have a considerable inﬂuence on the inversion. Moreover, these experiments show how
least squares inversion is sensitive to noise, random errors and systematic errors. These
have been documented and presented in Chapter 4 for the OLS case and in Chapter 5
for both formulations of Interferometric Least Squares.
One the formulations for interferometric inversion has almost the same recovery as or-
dinary least squares, which could practical advantages, since interferometric inversion
uses only relative phases. Relative phases which can be measured by using several mea-
surement antennas at the same time. Another formulation, using less quadratic pairs,
and thus have smaller simulations times, provides an increased robustness to an error in
the velocity in the background medium.
7.2. Future work
This work has been constrained by time and though the literature research provided great
insights into further development of these methods, they are left for future continuation
of this work.
Zhi-Quan et.
al in [8] investigate several methods for extracting the
solution, and one method with potential is what they call randomisation. The method
is in principle to generate random solutions to the QCQP, then evaluate which ones are
feasible, and then optimise only within the set of these solutions.
On the other hand, in order to better understand the practical uses of interferometric
inversion, more study should be given to the computational cost and complexity of the
algorithms.

C. Generating uncertainties
In physical measurement system, as for the drone measuring an antenna, the location of
the probe can only be known down to a given uncertainty depending on the system. As
an example the uncertainties for the system used in [3] was between 1 and 4 centimetres
in every direction (x, y and z). These uncertainties will aﬀect the inversion depending
on how large they are, and also of what distribution they have. In many measurements
the errors, noise and uncertainties are uncorrelated, or modelled as such if the sources
and characteristics of the errors are unknown, and they are therefore characterised by a
normal/Gaussian distribution.
Complex measurements are constituted by normally distributed real part, and a normally
distributed imaginary part. Now say that these errors are later evaluated as errors in
the magnitude and phase (polar form of a complex number), then one should not expect
a Gaussian distribution to appear. As you convert to polar form, the magnitude and
phase will have Rayleigh-, and uniformly- distributed data respectively. The same holds
for polar coordinates. So when uncertainties is speciﬁed as angular deviations in the
angular φ-component and metric deviations in the radial ρ-component, they must also
respectively have a uniform distribution and a Rayleigh distribution, in order to obtain
normally distributed perturbations in the x- and y-coordinates, while the z-coordinate
has uncertainties with a Gaussian distribution from the start. Same rule applies to spher-
ical coordinates, which means that if uncertainties in the two angular components are
speciﬁed as uniformly distributed and the uncertainties speciﬁed as Rayleigh-distributed
in the radial component, the uncertainties will be seen as Gaussian in x- y- and z.
