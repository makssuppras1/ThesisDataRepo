Abstract 
Intumescent coatings are widely employed in fire protection for structural steel due to their ability to expand 
under heat exposure, forming an insulating char layer that mitigates temperature rise. This study investigates 
the thermal performance of three commercially available intumescent coatings—water-based (PPG 
Steelguard 651), solvent-based (Sherwin-Williams Firetex FX2003), and epoxy-based (Akzo Nobel 
Interchar 212)—under varying heat flux conditions (25 kW/m², 50 kW/m², and impinging flames). The 
research aims to evaluate the influence of heat flux on the expansion behavior, char layer integrity, and 
thermal protection efficiency of these coatings. 
Results indicate that both heating conditions and initial coating thickness strongly influence expansion 
behavior. For coatings with an initial dry thickness of 1 mm, the water-based coating exhibited the highest 
expansion factor (23.64) under a lower heat flux of 25 kW/m², while the solvent-based coating expanded the 
most at higher heat fluxes, reaching a maximum expansion factor of 44.65 at 50 kW/m² and 27.33 at 
impinging flame exposure. In contrast, the epoxy-based coating demonstrated limited expansion at lower 
heat fluxes, but it can be seen that performance improved as heat flux increased. Pore size analysis further 
shows that coatings with finer, uniformly distributed pores tend to provide improved thermal insulation, 
while large, interconnected pores compromise heat-shielding performance.  
The findings contribute to a deeper understanding of the relationship between intumescent coating 
composition, expansion dynamics, and thermal resistance. Comparisons with previous studies highlight the 
importance of balancing the expansion factor and char stability in optimizing fire protection efficiency. 
Furthermore, error analysis identifies key experimental uncertainties, such as coating thickness variations 
and environmental factors affecting combustion stability. These insights are valuable for improving 
intumescent coating formulations and tailoring fire protection strategies for different fire exposure scenarios. 
Keywords: Intumescent Coatings, Thermal Protection Efficiency, Heat Flux, Fire Resistance, Char 
Layer, Radiation Heat Flux, Flame Impingement.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Project Background 
In recent years, the safety and sustainability of construction have been increasing concerns, especially fire 
safety. The rise of modern infrastructure and high-rise buildings leads to the need for fire protection. For 
example, one of the important fire incidents in modern history was the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire in 
1911 in New York City, which caused the death of 146 workers [1]. This tragedy highlighted the importance 
of proper fire exits, sprinkler systems, and fire-resistant materials, leading to significant concern for fire 
safety worldwide. The lack of fireproof materials and poor building regulations were identified as key 
contributors to the high death toll. 
 
Figure 1.1: Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire in 1911 in New York City. 
Similarly, the Grenfell Tower fire in London in 2017 is also a reminder of the fire risks posed by inadequate 
fire protection. The use of combustible cladding materials, combined with insufficient fire barriers, allowed 
the fire to spread rapidly and resulted in the loss of 72 lives [2]. The Grenfell Tower fire highlighted the 
importance of using fire-retardant materials in high-rise buildings. Steel is an important material that is 
widely used in modern construction for its strength and versatility. However, it becomes vulnerable when 
exposed to fire. According to studies, unprotected steel in buildings can reach temperatures as high as 500℃ 
or even higher during fire incidents. And steel loses much of its structural integrity when its temperature is 
more than 500℃, which means the risk of collapse of structures is relatively high [3]. The fire at Madrid’s 
==PAGE:11==
Windsor Tower in 2005 can prove the risk. During the fire incident, the skyscraper was damaged severely 
despite the extensive efforts of the firefighters [4]. To decrease such risks, passive fire protection systems 
have become an essential part of building design. Among these systems, intumescent coatings stand out as 
one of the most effective fire protection methods for steel structures. These coatings are reactive fire 
protection materials that expand significantly when exposed to high temperatures, forming a protective char 
layer that insulates the underlying structure from heat. Their efficiency is attributed to a synergistic reaction 
between acid donors, carbon sources, and blowing agents, which creates an expanded, thermally stable 
barrier that slows down heat transfer. Due to their lightweight nature, ease of application, and high efficiency 
in fire scenarios, intumescent coatings are widely used in modern construction and industrial fire protection. 
Building upon this, this research aims to further investigate the performance and behavior of intumescent 
coatings by analyzing their thermal and mechanical properties under realistic fire scenarios. 
Specifically, this study focuses on three distinct types of coatings—water-based, solvent-based and epoxy-
based intumescent coatings. These coatings are provided by Steelguard, Sherwn-Williams Firetex, and Akzo 
Nobel, respectively. These coatings represent distinct formulations tailored for various fire protection 
applications. Understanding their performance under controlled conditions allows for a direct comparison of 
their thermal and mechanical properties, providing insights into their strengths and limitations. For 
consistency, the three coatings will be referred to as water-based, solvent-based, and epoxy-based throughout 
this study. 
Moreover, the combination of radiant heaters and impinging flame burners in this study ensures a 
comprehensive evaluation of the coatings: 
1. Samples coated with water-based, solvent-based, and epoxy-based coatings are tested under heat fluxes 
of 25 kW/m² and 50 kW/m² to simulate different kinds of fire intensities. Radiant heaters provide a baseline 
performance measure under controlled and predictable conditions. 
2. Impinging flame burners introduce more challenging, localized heat exposure, enabling the study of 
coating integrity and char formation, such as relevant for jet flame scenarios that may occur in gas 
technology-related accidents. 
By using these different heating methods, this research offers a comprehensive understanding of the thermal 
and mechanical behavior of intumescent coatings under diverse fire scenarios. 
1.2 Fire development 
Fires progress through several stages which are shown in Figure 1.2, and each is characterized by unique 
thermal and chemical dynamics that impact the behavior of materials. Understanding these stages is critical 
for evaluating the effectiveness of fire protection systems, particularly for steel structures.  
1）Incipient Stage: 
==PAGE:12==
This initial phase involves ignition and the generation of a localized heat source. The heat release rate (HRR) 
is relatively low, and the temperature rise in the surrounding environment is minimal. During this stage, steel 
structures experience negligible thermal effects, as the fire has yet to generate sufficient energy to heat the 
substrate significantly [6]. 
2）Growth Stage: 
The fire begins to expand as combustible materials ignite, and the HRR increases exponentially. Hot gases 
accumulate at the ceiling, creating a thermal stratification effect that drives heat transfer to surrounding 
structures through convection and radiation. In this stage, steel components exposed to direct flame or 
radiative heat begin to absorb energy, leading to a gradual temperature rise. During this stage, the fire may 
transition into flashover, a critical point at which most combustibles in the compartment ignite almost 
simultaneously. Flashover marks a rapid escalation in HRR and temperature. It is strongly influenced by 
factors such as ventilation, fuel load, and compartment geometry [6]. 
3）Fully Developed Stage: 
The fire reaches its peak intensity, consuming the maximum available fuel. Temperatures can exceed 1000°C, 
and the rate of heat transfer to steel structures intensifies. Without adequate fire protection, steel components 
may deform or collapse, jeopardizing the overall structural stability. For this stage, ventilation can intensively 
influence the maximum temperature. Changes in airflow, which may be caused by windows breaking, doors 
opening or mechanical ventilation, can alter oxygen levels and lead to peaks and dips in combustion intensity. 
In addition, Inconsistent fuel availability within the compartment can result in temporary reductions in HRR. 
For example, when fuels in certain areas are fully consumed, HRR may temporarily drop. If new fuel sources 
are ignited, HRR may spike again [6]. 
4）Decay Stage: 
As fuel is depleted, the HRR declines, and the fire subsides. However, residual heat can continue to weaken 
steel structures, which increases the risk of collapse. 
The transition between these stages is influenced by factors such as fuel type, ventilation conditions, and 
compartment geometry. Intumescent coatings are particularly critical during the growth and fully developed 
stages, where they serve to delay heat transfer to steel [6]. 
 
==PAGE:13==
 
Figure 1.2: Four Stages of Fire Development [6]. 
Jet-Flame: Jet-flames are not typical compartment fires but can significantly influence fire behavior in 
industrial settings. This placement allows for a natural transition from general fire growth mechanisms to 
specific hazards posed by gas leaks and jet flames. Therefore, part of the jet flame is supplemented. Jet flame 
accidents pose a significant risk in industries utilizing gas-based technologies, such as natural gas processing, 
hydrogen storage, and Power-to-X (PtX) applications. These incidents typically result from high-pressure 
gas leaks, leading to highly directional and intense flames. In pipeline systems, jet flames can result from 
unintended ruptures or failures in containment. Such severe incidents highlight the importance of fire 
protection measures, including intumescent coatings and fire-resistant barriers, to mitigate the impact of jet-
flame accidents and enhance safety in critical infrastructures. 
1.3 Steel Performance in Fire 
Steel is widely used in construction for its strength, versatility, and durability under normal conditions. 
However, its performance deteriorates significantly when exposed to fire due to its high thermal conductivity 
and rapid loss of mechanical properties at elevated temperatures. 
Thermal Properties: 
Steel exhibits high thermal conductivity, allowing heat to transfer quickly through the material. This 
characteristic, while advantageous in dissipating localized heating under normal conditions, becomes a 
liability in fire scenarios. Rapid heat conduction accelerates the temperature rise throughout the steel 
structure, reducing its load-bearing capacity. 
Critical Temperature: 
Steel begins to lose its mechanical strength at approximately 300°C and experiences a 50% reduction at 
==PAGE:14==
550°C. At temperatures beyond 750°C, steel retains only 10% of its original strength, making it highly 
susceptible to deformation and collapse under load [7][8][8]. The exact critical temperature varies depending 
on the steel grade and the load conditions. 
Failure Mechanisms: 
As steel reaches critical temperatures, several failure mechanisms may occur: 
Thermal Expansion: The expansion of steel components under heat can lead to buckling, displacement, and 
increased stress at connection points. 
Softening: At high temperatures, the modulus of elasticity decreases, resulting in reduced stiffness and an 
inability to resist applied loads. 
Creep: Prolonged exposure to high temperatures accelerates creep deformation, further compromising 
structural stability. 
1.4 Fire Protection System 
Effective fire protection systems are critical in preserving structural integrity and ensuring the safety of 
occupants during fire incidents. These systems are generally divided into two categories: active fire 
protection systems and passive fire protection systems. 
Active Fire Protection Systems 
Active fire protection (AFP) systems are designed to detect, suppress, or control fires as they occur, which 
are shown in Figure 1.3. These systems rely on mechanical or electronic interventions and include sprinklers, 
fire alarms, smoke control systems, and fire suppression systems. Active systems are vital in the early stages 
of a fire to limit its spread and provide occupants with sufficient time to evacuate. It is for this reason that 
automatic sprinkler systems are designed to work on the principle of releasing water or other fire 
suppressants when high temperatures are detected. Fire detection systems, which may come in the form of 
smoke or heat detectors, serve to notify both occupants and emergency response personnel of a fire. 
==PAGE:15==
 
Figure 1.3: Active Fire Protection Systems. 
Although active fire protection systems play a critical role in enhancing fire safety, their effectiveness often 
depends on external factors, such as stable power supply, adequate water pressure, and regular maintenance. 
This reliance emphasizes the importance of integrating complementary fire protection systems that can 
provide uninterrupted protection without requiring manual activation or external triggers [6]. 
Passive Fire Protection Systems 
Passive fire protection (PFP) systems are designed to contain fires and prevent structural failure by 
incorporating fire-resistant materials and design strategies into a building’s structure. Unlike active systems, 
PFP does not require activation, offering continuous and reliable protection throughout a fire event. Common 
examples include fire-resistant walls, floors, doors, and fireproof coatings applied to structural components. 
Figure 1.4 shows an example of fire-resistant wall systems. 
==PAGE:16==
 
Figure 1.4: Example of Passive Fire Protection Using Fire-Resistant Wall Systems [5]. 
In the context of my project, intumescent coatings play an important role in passive fire protection. These 
specialized coatings expand upon exposure to high temperatures, forming a protective char layer that 
insulates the underlying material from heat. This insulation is particularly crucial for steel structures, which 
are highly heat-conductive and prone to rapid strength loss at elevated temperatures. By creating a thermal 
barrier, intumescent coatings help preserve the structural integrity of steel during a fire, thereby reducing the 
risk of collapse and providing critical time for evacuation and firefighting efforts [6]. 
The effectiveness of passive fire protection systems depends on various factors, including the properties of 
the materials used, the thickness of the coatings, and the environmental conditions during application and 
curing. This study, which examines the performance of three distinct intumescent coatings—water-based, 
solvent-based, and epoxy-based—provides valuable insights into their comparative effectiveness under 
different fire scenarios. This research makes a meaningful contribution to understanding how these systems 
perform in diverse conditions, aiding in the selection of appropriate solutions for enhanced fire safety. 
However, due to the limitation of time, this research did not focus on the effect of thickness. The thicknesses 
of all three coatings were 1 mm before curing. 
1.5 Intumescent Coatings 
Intumescent coatings are designed to provide passive fire protection by undergoing a controlled chemical 
reaction when exposed to high temperatures. The intumescing process forms an insulating char layer, which 
effectively delays heat transfer to the substrate. The key components of intumescent coatings include acid 
source, carbon source, blowing agent, and binding resin [9]. The efficiency of an intumescent coating is 
influenced by its formulation[9]. Each component plays a distinct role in producing a stable, voluminous 
char with excellent thermal insulation properties.  
==PAGE:17==
Key Components and Their Roles 
1. Acid Source: 
The acid source is the catalyst in the intumescing reaction. When exposed to heat, usually 
above 200°C [11], it decomposes to release acids (e.g., phosphoric acid), which acts as a 
catalyst to promote the charring of the carbon source such as pentaerythritol. The carbon 
source then forms a carbon-rich char which provides thermal insulation and protection for 
the underlying material. This char layer is essential for creating an effective barrier that 
reduces heat transfer to the structural substrate. 
Common acid sources: ammonium polyphosphate (APP) and phosphate-based compounds. 
Reaction:

Released phosphoric acid interacts with the carbon source to promote char formation and 
stability. 
2. Carbon Source: 
The carbon source in intumescent coatings is responsible for forming the carbonaceous 
char so it’s necessary for a high carbon source to be typically rich in carbon and decompose 
under heat to produce a stable carbonaceous residue. The efficiency of a carbon source in 
forming a char depends on its carbon content and hydroxyl groups. In other words, the 
carbon content directly influences the quantity of char produced, while the hydroxyl groups 
determine the dehydration rate, thereby affecting the speed of foam formation during the 
intumescence process [11]. 
Common carbon sources: pentaerythritol (PER), pentaerythritol, or other polyols [20].

3. Blowing Agent: 
The blowing agent is used to release gases such as carbon dioxide, ammonia, nitrogen oxides, and 
water vapor, which help expand the coating into a foam several times its original thickness [11]. The

to solidify [13]. The common sources of no-flammable gases are melamine, guanidine, glycine, urea, 
and so on, and the use of them depends on the need for decomposition temperatures.

4. Binding Resin: 
Thermoplastic properties are necessary for binding resin. Binding resin plays a crucial role in 
intumescent coatings by ensuring that the gases released during the thermal decomposition of the 
blowing agent are trapped effectively within the coating. These resins prevent the dispersion of 
gases, which enables the formation of a uniform and stable foam. The process of foam formation 
requires a careful balance between gas release and the resin’s ability to hold the gases within the 
expanding structure. 
Thermal Behavior of Intumescent Coatings 
Intumescent coatings undergo a complex chemical and physical transformation when exposed to elevated 
temperatures, offering passive fire protection by forming an insulating char layer that shields the underlying 
substrate. The behavior of these coatings can be divided into several distinct stages, each characterized by 
specific chemical reactions and material changes. 
Stages of Intumescent Coating Behavior: 
Intumescent coatings are designed to expand under high temperatures, forming a protective char layer that 
insulates the underlying material from fire damage. This process involves a sequence of complex physical 
and chemical transformations, which are illustrated in Figure 1.5, showing the four key stages of expansion. 
The reaction mechanism behind intumescent coatings is discussed extensively in various studies ([14], [15])  
 
Figure 1.5: Schematic of the various stages of the intumescent process [16]. 
 
At first, when the coating is exposed to heat, it absorbs most of the thermal energy. During this initial stage, 
the coating’s temperature gradually rises, but it still looks unchanged on the surface. Internally, however, 
molecular activity is increasing. As the heat continues to go up, the coating starts to soften, transitioning 
==PAGE:19==
from a solid state to a more flexible, heat-responsive material that sets the stage for further chemical reactions. 
Once the temperature reaches a critical range—typically between 100°C and 250°C—the coating’s acid 
source, such as ammonium polyphosphate (APP), begins to break down. This breakdown produces 
phosphoric acid [17], which acts as a catalyst to start the charring process. At the same time, the coating’s 
polymer matrix softens and turns into a viscous, gel-like state, allowing the various components to interact 
more effectively [18]. 
As temperatures climb higher, usually between 280°C and 350°C, the blowing agent—often melamine—
decomposes and releases gases like ammonia and nitrogen. These gases become trapped in the softened 
polymer, causing the coating to expand significantly [16]. This stage is crucial because it creates a 
lightweight, highly porous char layer that slows down heat transfer to the underlying surface. Additionally, 
carbon sources such as pentaerythritol (PER) contribute to forming a strong, stable structure that maintains 
the foam’s integrity. 
When temperatures exceed 350°C, the coating reaches its final transformation stage. The molten material 
begins to solidify, forming a rigid, low-conductivity char layer that acts as a barrier against further heat 
penetration. This insulating layer plays a key role in delaying heat transfer and protecting the structure 
underneath. 
The effectiveness of an intumescent coating depends on the right combination of ingredients—acid source, 
carbon source, and blowing agent—all working together to achieve the best expansion and a durable char 
layer. Additives like titanium dioxide and silica can also enhance the char’s strength and thermal resistance, 
further improving the coating’s fire protection capabilities.

2 Objectives and Experimental 
2.1 Research Objectives 
The primary objective of this research is to evaluate and compare the performance of three distinct types of 
intumescent coatings—water-based, solvent-based, and epoxy-based—under various thermal conditions. By 
examining their fire-resistant properties, this study aims to achieve the following specific goals: 
1) Analyze Temperature vs. Time Performance: 
Generate and analyze temperature versus time plots for each coating under controlled fire conditions to 
assess their ability to prevent heat transfer. These plots will quantify the thermal barrier effectiveness of each 
coating by tracking the rate and extent of temperature rise in the steel substrate. 
2) Evaluate Thermal Insulation Properties: 
Determine the coating's capacity to insulate steel substrates by delaying heat transfer during fire exposure. 
This includes identifying the time taken to reach critical temperature thresholds that may compromise 
structural integrity. 
3) Assess Material Characteristics: 
Examine the core properties of the coatings, such as density and char expansion factor, to establish a 
correlation between material properties and fire resistance performance. 
4) Determine Fire Protection Efficiency: 
Compare the coatings' ability to maintain steel substrates below the critical temperature of 550°C, beyond 
which steel loses significant load-bearing capacity. This evaluation involves varying heat flux levels to 
simulate different fire scenarios.

To achieve the planned objectives, the research process includes:

2.2.2 Coating Application 
The application of intumescent coatings is a critical step in ensuring the accuracy and reliability of the 
experimental results. For this study, the thickness of all samples is standardized at 1 mm, ensuring 
consistency across all coating types: water-based, solvent-based, and epoxy-based. The use of a plywood 
mold during the application process ensures precise control over the coating thickness. Before coating, 
the surface of each steel sample was thoroughly cleaned using ethanol to remove any residual dust, 
grease, or other contaminants. 
The coating material (water-based, solvent-based, or epoxy-based) was prepared as per the 
manufacturer’s guidelines. For epoxy-based coatings, the two components were mixed thoroughly using 
a mechanical stirrer to achieve a homogeneous mixture. Wooden sticks were used to dip into the coating 
material, and the material was then applied manually to the surface of the steel sample. The wooden 
stick was moved methodically across the sample surface to spread the material evenly. Excess material 
was carefully removed to maintain the desired thickness of 1 mm. The coated samples were placed in a 
controlled environment of stable humidity and ventilation to cure for a specified period based on the 
type of coating: 
• 
Water-based coatings were left to cure for 16 hours, but they were left for 24 hours in this project. 
•

2.2.4 Fire Tests: Cone Calorimeter and Impinging Flame 
The fire-testing phase of this study was designed to evaluate the thermal performance and fire resistance 
==PAGE:25==
properties of the coated steel samples. Two methods were employed: Cone Calorimeter Testing and 
Impinging Flame Testing, each serving specific purposes to simulate different fire scenarios and assess 
the coatings’ ability to mitigate heat transfer. For all fire tests, two thermocouples were embedded into 
each steel sample: one placed 3 mm beneath the surface and the other 7 mm beneath the surface. These 
thermocouples provided crucial temperature data, enabling a detailed assessment of heat transfer through 
the coatings. 
2.2.4.1  Cone Calorimeter Testing 
The cone calorimeter test was conducted to quantify the heat transfer through steel samples under 
controlled radiant heat. A cone calorimeter, as shown in Figure 2.7, applies a uniform heat flux to the 
sample's surface. The steel samples were securely mounted on an insulating block to ensure thermal 
isolation and minimize heat loss from the backside. 
 
Figure 2.7: Cone Calorimeter Setup. 
Setup of the Cone Calorimeter 
The Cone Calorimeter shown in Figure 2.8, as described by Babrauskas [19], is a widely used instrument 
for bench-scale fire testing. The device conforms to standards such as ISO 5660 and ASTM E1354, 
ensuring reliable and repeatable fire behavior assessments. The setup consists of several key components 
designed to ensure precise measurement of heat release, mass loss, and smoke production under 
controlled conditions. The specimen, typically 100 mm × 100 mm in size with a thickness between 6 
mm and 50 mm, is placed in a low-loss insulating ceramic holder to minimize conductive heat losses. 
The standard horizontal orientation (face up) is used for most tests, though a vertical orientation is 
available for exploratory studies. Above the sample, a conical radiant heater provides heat flux levels 
ranging from 0 to 110 kW/m² [19], with uniform radiation controlled to within ±2% in the horizontal 
position. The heater in the cone calorimeter can reach temperatures up to approximately 950°C, with the 
actual operating temperature adjustable based on experimental requirements, simulating real fire 
exposure. During testing, a gas analyser was used at the same time, while combustion gases pass through 
an exhaust system where oxygen (O₂), carbon dioxide (CO₂), and carbon monoxide (CO) are 
==PAGE:26==
continuously recorded and the data waw exported to files. This gas composition data is essential for 
calculating heat release rate (HRR) [19]. 
 
Figure 2.8: Schematic view of the cone calorimeter [19]. 
Calibration 
Before the fire test, a calibration process is necessary to determine the relationship between the cone 
calorimeter’s temperature and the resultant heat flux. A heat flux meter was positioned precisely at the 
location where the sample was placed during the actual fire test. This ensures the measured heat flux 
accurately reflects the conditions experienced by the sample. The distance between the cone heater and 
the sample's surface was fixed at 60 mm. The setup for calibration is shown in Figure 2.9. 
 
Figure 2.9: Experimental Setup of the cone calorimeter for heat flux versus temperature calibration [19]. 
This standard distance was maintained throughout the calibration and testing procedures to ensure 
consistent results. The cone heater temperature was incrementally increased across a predefined range 
to generate varying heat flux levels. At each temperature setting, the heat flux meter recorded the 
corresponding heat flux value. These measurements were exported to a calibration file

derive the relationship between the cone heater's temperature and the resultant heat flux. 
Fire Test 
After the calibration of the cone calorimeter, the installation of the samples for fire testing is a critical 
step to ensure accurate and reproducible results. The prepared samples, each with a uniform coating 
thickness of 1 mm, were carefully positioned within the testing apparatus to facilitate consistent 
exposure to the heat flux. 
The specialized sample holder was used to secure the specimens during testing. The holder was 
constructed from non-combustible materials to prevent unintended thermal interactions. The back and 
sides of the holder were insulated to minimize heat loss and ensure that the heat flux was directed solely 
onto the exposed surface of the sample.

The placement of thermocouples in the experimental setup was directly aligned with the pre-drilled 
holes in the test samples to ensure precise and consistent temperature measurements. Each sample was 
carefully prepared with two drilled holes at depths of 3 mm and 7 mm from the surface, which are shown 
in Figure 2.10. During the installation process, the thermocouples were inserted into these designated 
holes, ensuring direct contact with the material at the specified depths. The thermocouples were 
connected with the data logger by which the temperature with time could be recorded and exported to 
files. The sample is securely fastened within the holder to prevent movement or displacement during the 
test. Clamping mechanisms were used to ensure a tight fit without applying excessive pressure that might 
alter the coating’s integrity. The sample was positioned at a fixed distance of 60 mm below the cone 
heater, as determined during the calibration process.

Figure 2.11: Setup for Data Acquisition and Processing. 
 
The first step in the fire test was to set the temperature of the cone calorimeter to the desired level, such 
as 603°C or 793°C, which were selected to correspond to heat flux levels of 25 kW/m² and 50 kW/m² 
by the result of calibration. Simultaneously, it is crucial to ensure that the data logger is functioning 
correctly to record temperature readings accurately throughout the experiment. Once the display of the 
cone calorimeter confirms that the temperature has reached the predetermined value and remains stable, 
the sample, securely mounted within the holder, is carefully positioned at a distance of 60 mm beneath 
the cone calorimeter. 
Following the proper placement of the sample, the protective shutter is opened to initiate exposure to 
the applied heat flux. As the heat flux is applied, the thermocouples embedded within the pre-drilled 
holes of the sample, positioned at depths of 3 mm and 7 mm, continuously monitor and record the 
temperature evolution within the material. Observers closely monitor the surface of the sample for any 
visible changes such as bubbling, expansion, or char formation, which indicate the onset of the 
intumescent reaction. 
Once the heating phase is completed, the sample is left to cool gradually to avoid thermal shock (shown 
in Figure 2.12), which could influence post-test evaluations. The char layer formed during the test is 
then carefully examined, and its thickness is measured at nine designated points across the sample 
surface to assess the expansion behavior and thermal insulation effectiveness of the coating. Additionally, 
visual documentation is conducted to capture any significant observations for further analysis.

After the completion of the fire test, the cone calorimeter temperature was set to 100°C, allowing it to 
cool down gradually before turning off the power. This controlled cooling prevents sudden temperature 
drops that could potentially damage the heating elements and ensures the system is safely brought back 
to a manageable state. 
During this cooling phase, the shutter was closed to isolate the sample exposure area, and the system 
was monitored to ensure a steady reduction in temperature. Once the calorimeter reached the preset 
cooling temperature of 100°C, the equipment was turned off, followed by shutting down the exhaust 
hood to prevent unnecessary energy consumption and exposure to residual emissions. 
Finally, the collected data was exported for post-processing and in-depth analysis, aiming to evaluate 
the effectiveness of the intumescent coatings in terms of thermal protection and fire resistance. The 
sample, once fully cooled, was labeled and stored for any further examination, and the test environment 
was cleaned and prepared for subsequent experiments. Moreover, microscopic analysis was conducted 
to gain deeper insights into the microstructural changes occurring within the char layer after exposure 
to high temperatures. This step is crucial for understanding the internal morphology and structural 
integrity of the intumescent coatings post-heating, which will be described then. 
2.2.4.2 Impinging Flame Test 
For the impinging flame test, samples are positioned within a designed sample holder, which is elevated 
on insulating blocks to minimize heat dissipation to the surrounding environment. Thermocouples are 
==PAGE:30==
carefully inserted into the pre-drilled holes within the sample to monitor internal temperature variations 
during the flame exposure. The setup is placed on a non-combustible, heat-resistant platform to provide 
stability and safety during the test. The gas cylinder valve is slowly opened to allow gradual 
pressurization of the system. Prior to ignition, the gas supply system is thoroughly checked for leaks and 
flow consistency. Once confirmed, the flame is ignited, and adjustments are made to make sure the gas 
flow be maximum every time to ensure a steady heat flux to the sample. Upon completion of the test, 
the flame is extinguished, and the sample is allowed to cool naturally before further analysis. Post-test 
evaluation involves measuring the extent of char formation, delamination, and thermal penetration depth, 
which is similar to the evaluation of cone calorimeter. 
 
Figure 2.13: Setup of Impinging Flame Experiment. 
2.2.5 Data Processing 
To ensure the accuracy and reliability of the experimental data, a suitable data processing approach is 
necessary. Each test condition for each type of coating was repeated three times to minimize the 
influence of random variations and avoid coincidental results. To further enhance data consistency, 
multiple measures are taken to refine the recorded data before analysis. 
Firstly, the initial portion of the recorded temperature data was removed to ensure an accurate 
representation of the heating process. This is because environmental temperature fluctuations, such as 
changes in room temperature or variations before the sample was properly positioned, caused 
inconsistencies. Additionally, before the shutter was opened, the sample temperature did not start rising 
immediately, leading to discrepancies across datasets. To eliminate this influence, temperature data 
recorded before the shutter was opened was deleted. Furthermore, minor adjustments were made to the 
initial temperature values to improve comparability. For example, if the sample temperatures at the 
==PAGE:31==
moment the shutter was opened were 15°C, 23°C, and 27°C, they were all adjusted to start from 27°C 
to maintain consistency. Specifically, all data points recorded before reaching 27°C were removed to 
ensure a uniform starting temperature across samples. This step allows a more objective evaluation of 
the thermal response of each coating.  
To further reduce measurement uncertainties, the temperature data from the three repetitions under each 
heating condition (25 kW/m², 50 kW/m², and impinging flame) are averaged. This averaging method 
smooths out experimental inconsistencies and provides a more representative dataset for evaluating the 
thermal protection performance of each coating. Moreover, any datasets that exhibited significant 
deviations from the expected trend were discarded to prevent outliers from distorting the results. For 
example, the data from samples with coatings of wrong thicknesses will not be used in further analysis. 
The reason is that the molds used in the coating application process are manually made by workers. A 
defective mold can be identified through the measured coating thicknesses before curing, as it may result 
in samples with thin coatings. 
For the impinging flame test, additional selection criteria were applied due to the nature of the heat 
source. Since the gas flow was maximized to maintain a stable heat flux throughout the test, only the 
dataset with the most stable heating conditions and the best data quality was used for analysis. In some 
cases, the gas flow was not fully opened, leading to fluctuations in heat input. Consequently, datasets 
from these tests were excluded to prevent inconsistencies from affecting the overall findings. 
This comprehensive data processing approach ensures that the results accurately reflect the thermal 
performance of each intumescent coating under different heat flux conditions. By refining the data 
through normalization, averaging, and calculating deviation as well as the coefficient of variance, the 
analysis maintains a high degree of precision, enabling meaningful comparisons between coatings and 
across different heating scenarios. 
2.2.6 Gas Analysis 
To further analyze the thermal behavior of the intumescent coatings under impinging flame conditions, 
a gas analysis was conducted to measure the concentrations of carbon monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide 
(CO₂), and oxygen (O₂) using a gas analysis machine shown in Figure 2.14. The primary objective of 
this measurement was to evaluate the heat release characteristics during the test. 
Before conducting the gas measurements, the system underwent a calibration process to establish the 
relationship between the sensor's electrical signal (VDC) and the actual gas concentration. This was 
done by using reference gas cylinders with known concentrations placed near the machine. First, the 
baseline electrical signal corresponding to 0% gas concentration was recorded. Then, the analyzer was 
exposed to gases with known percentages to determine their respective voltage outputs. These 
calibration data points were used to create a conversion relationship between the sensor's voltage 
readings and actual gas concentrations. 
==PAGE:32==
During the experiment, the gas analyzer continuously recorded the electrical signal (VDC) over time. 
These raw data files, similar to temperature-time curve data, were exported to Excel for post-processing. 
The recorded voltage values were converted into corresponding gas concentrations over time using the 
established calibration relationship. Finally, the obtained gas concentration-time data were processed 
using Janssen’s method to calculate the heat release rate (HRR), which is shown in Appendix 8.1. This 
approach allows for a direct comparison of the combustion characteristics of different coatings under 
varying heat flux conditions. 
Unlike the cone calorimeter, which provides a well-controlled and relatively stable heat flux 
environment, the impinging flame system exhibits localized variations in heat intensity due to 
fluctuations in gas flow and flame impingement angles, which influence the thermal degradation 
mechanisms of the coatings and the development of the char layer. By monitoring the concentrations of 
CO, CO₂, and O₂, it becomes possible to correlate the heat release rate (HRR) with the behaviors of the 
coatings. 
The collection of gas concentration data is performed simultaneously with temperature measurements, 
both of which are recorded and exported to files for further analysis. Subsequently, a programmatic 
approach using Python and Mathcad is applied to process the gas concentration dataset and convert it 
into heat release rate (HRR) documents. These documents allow for the estimation of heating conditions, 
particularly assessing whether the heat flux remains steady, which is crucial for evaluating the 
consistency and reliability of the impinging flame as a heating source.

4.5 Pore Size Distribution and Porosity 
In this study, the pore size distribution of intumescent coatings was analyzed under three different 
heating conditions: 25 kW/m², 50 kW/m², and direct flame exposure. The results provide valuable 
insights into how heat flux influences the formation and structure of pores in the three coatings used, 
ultimately affecting their thermal protection efficiency. 
Water-based 
Figure 8.1, Figure 8.2, and Figure 8.3 of Appendix 8.3 show the pore size distribution of the water-based 
coating under different heat conditions. Under a lower heat flux (25 kW/m²), the majority of the pores 
are concentrated in the smaller size range (20–40 μm), while percentages of larger pores (> 100 µm) are 
relatively low. As the heat flux increases to 50 kW/m², the distribution shifts slightly toward larger pores, 
but small pores (<60 µm) still dominate. Under direct flame exposure, the distribution changes 
==PAGE:65==
significantly. It can be seen that there is a sharp increase in the number of large pores (>100 µm). For 
porosity, Table 8.7 shows that porosity analysis further supports the trend of pore size distribution. The 
porosity is 39.20% under the heat flux of 25 kW/m².As the heat flux increases to 50 kW/m², the porosity 
rises to 49.58%, suggesting an increase in void spaces as larger pores begin to form. Under direct flame 
exposure, the porosity further increases to 56.07%.  
Solvent-based 
Based on Figure 8.4, Figure 8.5, and Figure 8.6 of Appendix 8.4, the pore size distribution of the solvent-
based coating shows a clear shift toward more and larger pores as the heat flux increases. Under a lower 
heat flux of 25 kW/m², the majority of pores are concentrated in the 20–40 µm range, meaning a high 
proportion of small pores. As the heat flux increases to 50 kW/m², the proportion of small pores 
decreases, while mid-sized pores (60–120 µm) become more prevalent, suggesting a more porous char 
layer. This corresponds with the observed porosity (Appendix 8.5) increase from 63.24% at 25 kW/m² 
to 70.38% at 50 kW/m², confirming that higher heat exposure results in a more porous structure. Under 
direct flame exposure, the porosity slightly decreases to 60.06%. 
Epoxy-based 
For the epoxy-based coating, the char layer formed at 25 kW/m² was too thin to allow meaningful pore 
size analysis, so only the distributions under 50 kW/m² and direct flame exposure are presented in 
Appendix 8.3 (Figure 8.7 and Figure 8.8). The pore size distribution remains relatively stable, with the 
majority of pores concentrated in the 20–60 µm range under both 50 kW/m² heat flux and direct flame 
exposure. Under direct flame exposure, a noticeable increase in large pores (>200 µm) is observed, 
indicating localized char expansion. This is supported by porosity data which can be seen in Table 8.7 
of Appendix 8.5.

5 Discussion 
5.1 Performance of Intumescent Coatings Under Different Heat Flux 
Conditions 
The performance of the three intumescent coatings—PPG Steelguard 651 (water-based), Sherwin-
Williams Firetex FX2003 (solvent-based), and Akzo Nobel Interchar 212 (epoxy-based)—varies 
significantly under different heating conditions (25 kW/m², 50 kW/m², and impinging flame), 
highlighting the complex interplay between expansion behavior, char structure, and thermal protection 
efficiency. The discussion below is based on the performance of 1mm coatings before being cured, as 
the different thicknesses of coatings can vary the result, which means if the thickness changes, the result 
and comparison can be different. A study by Jimenez et al. (2006) investigated the effect of external heat 
flux and coating thickness on the fire resistance of intumescent coatings applied to stainless steel [22]. 
The research demonstrated that thicker coatings exhibited more pronounced swelling, resulting in a more 
effective thermal barrier. In contrast, thinner coatings showed less expansion, which compromised their 
insulating capabilities. The study concluded that insufficient coating thickness might lead to inadequate 
char formation [22].  
5.1.1 Influence of Heat Flux on the Development of Intumescent Coatings 
The development of intumescent coatings under different heating conditions provides insight into their 
thermal activation, expansion behavior, and structural transformation. The recorded observations of 
color changes and expansion dynamics reveal critical differences in the reaction mechanisms of water-
based, solvent-based, and epoxy-based coatings, particularly when exposed to 25 kW/m², 50 kW/m², 
and impinging flames. 
5.1.1.1 Color Evolution  
Color changes in intumescent coatings provide a visual representation of chemical transformations 
occurring during heating. The observed variations in discoloration across different coatings correlate 
with the onset of pyrolysis, char formation, and final degradation stages. 
Water-based coating  
At low heat flux (25 kW/m²), the gradual transition from white to dark gray/black over several minutes 
suggests a stepwise thermal decomposition, which is consistent with prior studies on water-based 
coatings [15]. This progressive degradation allows for sustained gas release and expansion, producing a 
==PAGE:67==
highly aerated char layer. The final char layer is predominantly black, indicating slower decomposition 
and reduced oxidation due to the lower heat flux. 
At 50 kW/m², a more rapid darkening is observed, likely due to accelerated binder decomposition and 
gas production. Similar results have been documented by Zhang et al. (2015) [12], who noted that higher 
heat flux conditions lead to earlier char oxidation, making the protective layer more prone to shrinkage. 
The final char exhibits a two-tone appearance, with the central region turning white while the edges and 
deeper layers remain black, suggesting accelerated gas release and enhanced expansion but localized 
oxidation effects. 
Under impinging flames, localized reddish oxidation occurs, possibly indicating surface overheating. 
Previous studies suggest that high-intensity flames can lead to premature char burnout, reducing 
insulation efficiency over prolonged exposure [7]. The final char layer is almost entirely white, 
demonstrating extensive thermal decomposition and complete oxidation, likely due to prolonged high-
intensity exposure. This result differs from previous studies where partial black residues were observed 
in similar water-based coatings under standard heat flux conditions [15], which will be talked then. 
Solvent-Based Coating  
Compared to water-based coatings, solvent-based coatings exhibit a more uniform darkening, with a 
gradual shift to grayish-black before significant expansion occurs. This aligns with findings from Hu & 
Ji (2024), who reported that solvent-based binders tend to promote a more controlled degradation 
process, delaying char formation but improving final efficiency [7]. At higher heat flux, darker and more 
compact char layers form earlier, suggesting that the decomposition kinetics are influenced by the 
solvent-based formulation’s ability to withstand higher heating. 
Epoxy-Based Coating  
Epoxy-based coatings differ significantly from the other two formulations by not exhibiting immediate 
expansion but undergoing early color transformation. The release of gases can be observed early during 
experiments of the epoxy-based coating. At 50 kW/m² and under impinging flames, the coating develops 
a dense, reddish-black char, confirming that epoxy-based systems primarily rely on high-strength 
carbonaceous residue rather than volumetric expansion for fire resistance. This is following the findings 
of Maluk et al. (2019), who demonstrated that epoxy-based coatings tend to form ceramic-like protective 
layers that resist further degradation at extreme temperatures [21].   
Hypothesis of the Differences with Previous Studies 
In this study, the water-based coating exhibited an almost entirely white residue under impinging flame 
conditions, which contrasts significantly with the black residue observed under low heat flux conditions. 
This color transition is primarily attributed to carbon oxidation and the presence of aluminum and 
titanium compounds at high temperatures. 
==PAGE:68==
Carbon Oxidation: Under low heat flux conditions, the intumescent coating forms a carbon-rich char 
layer, which appears black due to the presence of residual carbon. However, at higher temperatures, such 
as those experienced during impinging flame exposure, this carbonaceous char undergoes further 
oxidation, resulting in the formation of carbon dioxide and a subsequent reduction in carbon content. 
This process leads to a lighter-colored residue. 
Formation of Aluminum Oxide: Many water-based intumescent coatings incorporate aluminum-based 
additives, such as aluminum hydroxide, which serve as flame retardants and char stabilizers. Upon 
exposure to high temperatures, aluminum hydroxide decomposes to form aluminum oxide (Al₂O₃), a 
white, thermally stable compound. The presence of aluminum oxide contributes to the white appearance 
of the residue after intense heat exposure. Research indicates that "Industrial mineral fillers such as 
titanium dioxide, magnesium hydroxide and aluminium hydroxide are vital flame-retardant ingredients 
added into IC to enhance the fire-resistance performance."[24] 
Therefore, the observed color change from black to white in the char layer of water-based intumescent 
coatings under varying heat flux conditions can be explained by the oxidation of carbon components 
and the formation of aluminum oxide at elevated temperatures. 
5.1.1.2  Expansion Characteristics and The Appearance of The Char Layer 
The expansion dynamics of intumescent coatings are highly dependent on their heat exposure conditions 
and formulation chemistry. 
Water-Based Coating  
The observed expansion trends align with prior studies indicating that water-based coatings (PPG 
Steelguard 651) exhibit rapid initial swelling but suffer from shrinkage over time due to char fragility. 
At 50 kW/m², the faster expansion but weaker char integrity suggests that increased heat flux accelerates 
gas release but weakens structural cohesion. Hu et al. (2024) documented similar results, showing that 
highly porous chars formed under intense heating tend to collapse under prolonged exposure[7]. Under 
impinging flames, expansion is significantly reduced, possibly due to flame-induced surface oxidation, 
which depletes organic material before full expansion occurs. 
Solvent-Based Coating 
Compared to the water-based system, expansion occurs later under lower heat flux. Under higher heat 
flux, the expansion is sustained for longer durations, and the char layer remains intact despite thermal 
stress. This suggests that solvent-based coatings balance volumetric growth with enhanced structural 
stability, leading to better fire resistance over prolonged exposure. Under impinging flames, the 
expansion is limited but produces a more compact char layer, consistent with Zhang (2015), who noted 
that some formulations prioritize char reinforcement over high expansion[12]. 
==PAGE:69==
Epoxy-Based Coating  
At a heat flux of 25 kW/m², the Epoxy-Based Coating exhibits limited expansion. Moreover, due to the 
limited number of samples with this coating and the unclear behavior observed, a single test was 
conducted using the epoxy-based coating under a heat flux of 25 kW/m². Under impinging flames, 
minimal expansion occurs, but the char layer remains intact even under extreme heat exposure, 
reinforcing prior research that suggests epoxy-based coatings are better suited for high-intensity fire 
scenarios [7]. 
 
5.1.2 Influence of Heat Flux on Temperature Evolution and Thermal Protection Efficiency 
Under lower heat flux conditions (25 kW/m²), the water-based coating exhibits the highest thermal 
protection efficiency, achieving a peak efficiency of approximately 32%. This is attributed to its high 
expansion factor (23.64), which produces a thick, porous char layer that effectively reduces heat transfer. 
This is followed by the solvent-based coating, which reaches a peak efficiency of 27% with an expansion 
factor of 13.88. For heat flux of 25 kW/m². It can be seen that the performance of epoxy-based coating 
is the worst, with a peak efficiency of 15% and a minimal expansion factor of 0.66, which highlights the 
importance of expansion in reducing heat penetration at lower heat fluxes. 
As the heat flux increases to 50 kW/m², the performance hierarchy shifts. The solvent-based coating 
becomes the best performer, achieving the highest peak efficiency (45.24%) and final efficiency 
(44.52%), driven by a significantly increased expansion factor (44.65). This result highlights that the 
solvent-based coating responds more effectively to higher heat flux, forming a thicker and more stable 
char layer that better resists heat. The water-based, despite achieving a slightly lower peak efficiency 
(45.12%), experiences a more rapid decline over time, ending with a lower final efficiency (38.94%), 
which may be caused by the formation of a highly porous but mechanically weak char layer. This 
fragility can lead to structural collapse or shrinkage under prolonged heating, reducing its ability to 
maintain thermal insulation. Meanwhile, the epoxy-based coating, though still the weakest, improves 
significantly compared to its 25 kW/m² performance, reaching a peak efficiency of 35.00% with an 
expansion factor of 5.27, suggesting that higher heat flux enhances gas release and char formation. 
Under impinging flame conditions, which simulate the most severe heating, the solvent-based coating 
exhibits exceptional thermal performance, achieving both the highest peak (58.59%) and final efficiency 
(41.70%) with an expansion factor of 27.33. Despite its lower expansion factor than at 50 kW/m², the 
coating maintains high thermal protection, indicating that char density and strength are more important 
under extreme heating conditions. The water-based coating, with an expansion factor of 18.37, peaks at 
56.59% efficiency but suffers the most severe decline, with its final efficiency dropping to 28.09%, 
suggesting that the water-based char is more susceptible to structural degradation under extreme thermal 
stress. Remarkably, the epoxy-based coating demonstrates unexpected resilience, with its final 
==PAGE:70==
efficiency (37.58%) surpassing that of the water-based coating despite having the lowest expansion 
factor (10.74). This performance highlights the advantage of dense, compact char structures in providing 
stable thermal insulation under high intensity. 
5.1.3 Relationship Between Expansion Factor and Thermal Protection Efficiency Across Heat 
Fluxes 
The expansion factor directly impacts thermal protection efficiency by influencing the thickness and 
density of the protective char layer. However, the results of this project highlight that this relationship 
becomes more complex as heat flux increases. 
Low Heat Flux (25 kW/m²): At this level, coatings with higher expansion factors generally exhibited 
superior thermal protection efficiency. In my experiments, the water-based coating demonstrated the 
highest expansion factor (23.64) and the highest peak efficiency (31%), consistent with findings by 
Zhang et al. (2022) that coatings with greater expansion provide superior thermal insulation under 
moderate heat fluxes. The solvent-based coating, with a lower expansion factor (13.88), achieved 27% 
peak efficiency, while the epoxy-based coating performed the worst, correlating directly with its minimal 
expansion factor (0.66) and efficiency (15%). These results align closely with research indicating a linear 
relationship between expansion and thermal efficiency at lower heat flux levels [25]. 
Moderate Heat Flux (50 kW/m²): Under increased heat flux, my experimental data revealed a shift in 
performance trends. The solvent-based coating exhibited the highest expansion factor (44.65) and 
surpassed the water-based coating in final efficiency (44.5% vs. 38.9%). This result is different from the 
expected pattern observed at lower heat fluxes but aligns with findings from (Wang et al., 2021), who 
reported that coatings forming denser char structures with moderate expansion outperform highly porous 
but less stable char layers [26]. 
High Heat Flux (Impinging Flame): At extreme heat flux, my results showed a clear difference in coating 
performance. The solvent-based coating maintained the highest expansion factor (27.33) and final 
efficiency (41.7%), which is better than both the water-based (18.37, 28.1%) and epoxy-based (10.74, 
37.6%) coatings. It can be seen that the final efficiency of the solvent-based coating exceeds that of 
water-based coating, despite the latter exhibiting a higher expansion factor. This observation indicates 
that the expansion factor alone is not the sole determinant of a coating’s final efficiency, suggesting the 
importance of other factors again, such as thermal stability, char structure integrity, and chemical 
composition. 
5.1.4 Relationship of Thermal Protection Efficiency with Pore Size Distribution and Porosity 
The pore size distribution of the three intumescent coatings exhibits a consistent trend, where low heat 
flux promotes the formation of a dense char layer with dominating small pores, while higher heat flux 
==PAGE:71==
leads to increased pore size and increases of large pores.  
When these findings are compared with previous studies, both similarities and differences are observed. 
For example, Gadaparthi & Prakash (2025) investigated porous alumina and zirconia-toughened 
alumina structures, where a higher porosity leads to lower thermal conductivity because air has a lower 
thermal conductivity than solid materials [27]. While this principle also applies to intumescent char 
layers, my results present a nuanced relationship between porosity and thermal insulation efficiency. It 
can be seen that the porosities of water-based and epoxy-based coatings increase when the heat fluxes 
increase from 25 kW/m² to 50 kW/m², and this is accompanied by an improvement in thermal protection 
efficiency. The same trend is seen in the epoxy-based coating, where porosities rise when heat conditions 
change from 50 kW/m² to direct flame, and its thermal protection improves at the same time.  
However, the trend changes when moving from 50 kW/m² to direct flame exposure. The water-based 
coating continues to show a rise in porosity, but its thermal protection efficiency declines. On the other 
hand, a drop can be seen for the porosity of the solvent-based coating, and its thermal protection 
efficiency also decreases.  
This difference can be explained by the role of pore size distribution, porosity and connectivity in 
thermal protection. In intumescent coatings, not just porosity but also pore morphology and network 
structure significantly influence heat resistance. Small, well-distributed, and non-connected pores 
contribute to a dense char structure that slows heat transfer, while large, interconnected pores facilitate 
convective and radiative heat transport, reducing insulation efficiency. According to Figure 4.7, the pores 
formed within the char layer appear to be interconnected, penetrating through the entire char thickness. 
This suggests that the expansion process of the water-based coating leads to a highly porous and open-
structured char, where the pores extend continuously across the material rather than being confined to 
isolated regions. The presence of through-thickness pores allows hot gases and heat to penetrate more 
easily, reducing the coating’s ability to act as an effective thermal barrier. The presence of through-
thickness pores allows hot gases and heat to penetrate more easily, reducing the coating’s ability to act 
as an effective thermal barrier. The continuous growth of large, interconnected pores indicates that the 
char layer loses its structural integrity over time. This could explain why the water-based coating tends 
to exhibit lower thermal protection efficiency under flame with higher porosities. 
Overall, these findings support the argument that a fine-pored, compact char layer with high porosity 
can contribute to enhanced heat protection. At 50 kW/m², increased porosity appears to improve thermal 
efficiency, reinforcing prior research on the insulating effects of porous materials. However, under direct 
flame exposure, excessive pore connectivity and structural degradation may lead to a decrease in thermal 
resistance. Thus, my results further prove that while high porosity can enhance thermal protection in 
intermediate heat flux conditions, the stability and connectivity of the pore network play a crucial role 
in maintaining insulation performance under extreme conditions [28].

5.2 Error Discussion 
Every experimental setup is subject to inherent limitations and sources of error that can affect the 
accuracy and consistency of results. In this study, several factors—ranging from sample preparation 
inconsistencies to operational challenges—may have influenced the measured expansion, thermal 
efficiency, and overall fire resistance of the tested intumescent coatings. Identifying and addressing these 
sources of error is essential to refining experimental methodologies and ensuring the reliability of 
conclusions. 
5.2.1 Variability in Coating Thickness Due to Mold Limitations 
A significant source of error stems from the use of a plywood mold for the coating application. Since 
the coatings were applied at a thickness of just 1 mm, even slight imperfections in the mold could 
introduce variations in the final coating thickness. This is particularly relevant for intumescent coatings, 
as small differences in thickness can have a substantial impact on expansion behavior and thermal 
insulation efficiency. According to the thicknesses of coatings after being cured, it can be seen that at 
least one of the ten sample slots in the mold is significantly inaccurate, which produced notably thinner 
coatings. For example, Water 7 and Solvent 6 (shown in Appendix 8.2) exhibited much lower thicknesses 
compared to other samples, which could have directly influenced their expansion and fire protection 
performance. Additionally, even among the remaining samples, the variation in thickness was relatively 
clear, suggesting that mold inconsistencies contributed to deviations in the final coating application.  
Since intumescent coatings rely on a precise balance of char expansion and structural integrity, uneven 
initial thickness may lead to inconsistent thermal performance, where thinner coatings experience earlier 
degradation or insufficient expansion, while thicker coatings may have better insulating properties. 
These variations, introduced during sample preparation, may explain some of the deviations observed 
in fire test results. 
5.2.2 Inconsistencies in the Impinging Flame Test 
The impinging flame test presented additional challenges due to the fluctuating nature of the combustion 
process. Unlike the cone calorimeter, which delivers a controlled and steady heat flux, the stability of 
the flame in the impinging flame test was influenced by multiple external factors, including: 
1) Gas Flow Variations – As the fuel in the gas cylinder was consumed, the gas pressure and flow rate 
gradually decreased, which may have led to changes in flame intensity over time. 
2) Environmental Oxygen Levels – The oxygen concentration in the testing environment was not 
constant, as it was affected by the opening and closing of the laboratory doors. These changes in 
airflow could have influenced combustion efficiency, leading to minor variations in heat flux. 
==PAGE:73==
3) Temperature of the Gas Cylinder – Over the course of the experiments, the gas cylinder itself cooled 
down. Since gas flow is temperature-dependent, this cooling effect likely contributed to slight 
fluctuations in flame intensity. 
While these factors did not introduce drastic inconsistencies, they could have contributed to variations 
in heating conditions between tests, potentially affecting the repeatability of results. 
5.2.3 Sample Limitations and Experimental Constraints 
1) Limited Epoxy-Based Samples – Unlike the water-based and solvent-based coatings, the epoxy-
based coating could not be tested three times for each heat source due to its longer curing time and 
limited sample availability. As a result, the epoxy-based data lacks the same level of statistical 
reliability as the other coatings. 
2) Pre-Used or Modified Samples – Some steel samples had been previously used or contained pre-
drilled holes from earlier experiments. These structural modifications may have influenced heat 
transfer behavior, leading to potential inconsistencies in thermal measurements and char expansion. 
5.2.4 Operator-Induced Errors and Procedural Inconsistencies 
Beyond sample preparation and heat flux variations, certain procedural inconsistencies may have 
introduced errors: 
1) Incomplete Gas Cylinder Opening – In some trials, the gas valve was not fully opened, leading to 
variations in flame intensity. While adjustments were made where possible, this inconsistency could 
have affected heat transfer rates. 
2) Minor Errors in Thermocouple Placement – Although thermocouples were carefully positioned, 
small differences in placement within the pre-drilled holes could have introduced slight variations 
in recorded temperatures. However, this potential error is minimized by the redundancy of 
measurement—each sample was equipped with two thermocouples at different depths (3 mm and 7 
mm), ensuring that any inconsistencies in individual sensor readings could be cross-validated 
against expected trends within the same sample group. Additionally, during the experiments, the 
temperature gap between the two thermocouples remained relatively consistent across samples 
exposed to the same heat flux. This consistency suggests that any placement errors were small and 
did not significantly impact overall results. In cases where anomalous discrepancies were observed 
between the readings from the two thermocouples—such as an unexpectedly large deviation from 
established trends—the experiment was immediately halted, and the setup was checked for potential 
issues before being restarted. This quality control measure further ensured that errors related to 
thermocouple positioning did not substantially affect the accuracy of recorded temperature profiles. 
==PAGE:74==
3) Handling and Contamination Effects – Variations in sample handling, surface cleanliness, or coating 
application technique could have influenced char formation and expansion behavior. 
5.2.5 Possible Errors in Pore Size Distribution and Porosity Analysis 
One potential source of error in the pore size distribution analysis arises from the image processing 
techniques used for char layer identification. ImageJ was applied to analyze the pore structure. The 
potential source of error in the process comes from the determination of thresholding values for pore 
segmentation. The accuracy of the results depends heavily on the selection of an appropriate threshold 
value, which can be challenging due to variations in contrast and material texture. If the threshold is too 
low, some pores will be ignored (Figure 5.1), while pores will be overlooked if it is too high (Figure 
5.2).

6 Conclusion 
This study set out to evaluate the fire protection performance of three different intumescent coatings—PPG 
Steelguard 651 (water-based), Sherwin-Williams Firetex FX2003 (solvent-based), and Akzo Nobel Interchar 
212 (epoxy-based)—when exposed to varying heat flux conditions (25 kW/m², 50 kW/m², and impinging 
flame). Through detailed analysis of their expansion behavior, thermal protection efficiency, and char layer 
formation, valuable insights were gained into how these coatings respond under fire exposure. 
The results clearly show that heat flux has a significant impact on the way intumescent coatings expand and 
insulate the steel substrate. Under lower heat flux (25 kW/m²), the water-based coating exhibited the highest 
expansion factor (23.64) and achieved the best thermal protection, maintaining its char structure effectively. 
The solvent-based coating, while expanding less, still provided good insulation due to its denser and more 
stable char. In contrast, the epoxy-based coating showed very limited expansion (0.66) and lower thermal 
efficiency, indicating that it relies more on its inherent material properties rather than an expanding char 
layer to provide protection. 
At higher heat flux (50 kW/m²), performance trends shifted. The solvent-based coating surpassed the water-
based formulation, achieving the highest expansion (44.65) and the best final efficiency (44.5%). This 
suggests that under more extreme heat conditions, a more compact and stable char structure plays a greater 
role than expansion alone. The water-based coating, which initially performed well, experienced faster 
degradation, leading to a drop in efficiency over time. Interestingly, the epoxy-based coating, despite its 
lower expansion factor (5.27), performed better than at lower heat flux, likely due to the formation of a 
denser, more heat-resistant char. 
At higher heat flux (50 kW/m²), performance trends shifted. The solvent-based coating surpassed the water-
based formulation, achieving the highest expansion (44.65) and the best final efficiency (44.5%). This 
suggests that under more extreme heat conditions, a more compact and stable char structure plays a greater 
role than expansion alone. The water-based coating, which initially performed well, experienced faster 
degradation, leading to a drop in efficiency over time. Interestingly, the epoxy-based coating, despite its 
lower expansion factor (5.27), performed better than at lower heat flux, likely due to the formation of a 
denser, more heat-resistant char. 
Under impinging flame exposure, which represents the most extreme fire condition, the solvent-based 
coating again demonstrated the strongest performance. It maintained both high expansion (27.33) and the 
highest final efficiency (41.7%). The water-based coating initially showed strong efficiency but degraded 
significantly, ending at only 28.09%. The epoxy-based coating, despite its minimal expansion, retained 
relatively good efficiency (37.58%), reinforcing the idea that while expansion is important, the density and 
integrity of the char layer are equally critical for fire resistance in high-intensity exposure scenarios. 
The color changes observed in the char layers provided further insights into the coatings' behavior. Initially, 
==PAGE:77==
coatings darkened due to the decomposition of polymer binders and carbonization. However, under intense 
heat, particularly in the impinging flame test, some samples turned almost completely white. This suggests 
that the high temperatures led to the oxidation of carbon residues into CO₂, leaving behind aluminum oxide 
and other non-combustible inorganic components. Notably, the water-based coating, which was expected to 
form a darker char, resulted in a nearly white final char, likely due to differences in its formulation compared 
to other studies.  
While the experiments were carefully designed, some sources of error were identified. Small variations in 
coating thickness, caused by inconsistencies in the mold, likely influenced the expansion and efficiency 
results. The impinging flame test introduced additional challenges, as gas flow fluctuations and 
environmental oxygen levels varied slightly depending on room conditions. Additionally, not all epoxy-
based samples were tested in triplicate due to curing time constraints, which could have introduced 
variability in results. Nevertheless, these errors were minimized through repeated trials, careful monitoring, 
and validation of data. In conclusion, this study reinforces the idea that intumescent coatings do not perform 
the same way under different fire scenarios, and their effectiveness is highly dependent on expansion 
behavior, char integrity, and heat exposure conditions. While water-based coatings offer strong initial 
performance at moderate heat flux, solvent-based coatings show superior long-term stability under high heat. 
Epoxy-based coatings, despite their limited expansion, provide reliable protection due to their mechanically 
robust char structure. These findings underscore the importance of selecting the right type of coating based 
on expected fire conditions rather than assuming that greater expansion always results in better protection. 
For future research, it would be beneficial to explore the impact of coating thickness variations on fire 
resistance, the long-term durability of char layers under cyclic heating conditions, and the potential for nano-
fillers to enhance char stability. Additionally, computational modeling of intumescent expansion could 
provide deeper insights into optimizing these coatings for real-world fire protection applications.
