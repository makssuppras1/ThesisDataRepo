Preface
The master thesis was carried out at the Department of Health Technology at the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU) between September 2021 and February 2022 in partial ful-
ﬁllment for the degree Master of Science in Engineering. The thesis has a workload of 32.5
ECTS points.
This project was done under the supervision of associate professor Ramneek Gupta, post-
doc Rikke Linnemann Nielsen and PhD student Marianne Helenius at the research group
Disease Data Intelligence at DTU.
The thesis deals with development of polygenic risk scores which are applied on several
genetic data types, to determine a potential genetic predisposition of childhood cancer. One
aspect includes whole genome sequencing of germline DNA and RNA-sequencing of so-
matic tumor RNA, which we did only manage to touch superﬁcially and it requires more
time to investigate its full potential in depth. The thesis consists of an introduction and
background with concepts to understand the scope of the thesis followed by the work of the
thesis itself.

Developing polygenic risk scores of childhood cancer predisposition
by Naja Stolberg Bahrenscheer & Mikkel Damsgaard Mortensen
Acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) is a disease with an unknown etiology. Though, a
theory suggests ALL to be a polygenic trait with inherited predisposition arising from mul-
tiple single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) contributing with small individual effects.
The aim of this study is to generate a polygenic risk score (PRS) to examine the associa-
tion between genetic variation and ALL. The analytical samples included a patient cohort of
386 Danish individuals diagnosed with ALL ranging from 1-45 years of age, while the con-
trol cohort consisted of 1158 healthy participants chosen from the retrospective study of UK
Biobank with the age spanning from 40-69 years old. The genetic risk of individuals were as-
sessed by the weighted PRS model based on 15 SNP related to B-cell ALL identiﬁed through
the article “Identiﬁcation of four novel associations for B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukaemia risk”.
Patients with ALL had signiﬁcantly higher means of PRS compared to the controls (2.69 vs.
2.32, p-value: 8.46 ⇥10−15). The simple logistic regression showed an OR = 1.74 (CI 95% =
1.51 to 2.01, p-value: 5.87 ⇥10−14). However, the PRS was not suitable for clinical utility.
The results indicate that use of PRS could identify individuals with higher predisposition of
ALL.
In a parallel study 171 cancer patients under the age of 18 had their germline DNA se-
quenced by whole genome sequencing and their somatic tumor RNA sequenced by RNA
sequencing. An unweighted PRS model was applied and showed that ALL patients had
higher PRS than non-ALL patients and further investigation showed the PRS based on
germline DNA was signiﬁcantly higher than the PRS of somatic tumor RNA.

1 Introduction
During the past decades, the amount of big data has increased exponentially. This also in-
cludes the amount of genetic data stored in public gene banks and biobanks, as the costs of
different sequencing techniques have decreased dramatically. With access to all this data,
there is a need to apply it in a useful manner to discover hitherto unknown disease mecha-
nisms and thereby gain understanding of more complex diseases [1]. Acute lymphoblastic
leukemia (ALL) is a rare and complex cancer which predominantly occurs in young chil-
dren, being the most frequent cancer type among children below the age of 15. The etiology
behind the development is until now more or less unknown, while there are suggestions
towards a genetic predisposition in the children who develops ALL [2]. With the increasing
numbers of genome-wide association studies, more and more genetic ﬁndings are being as-
sociated with, at ﬁrst common diseases, and now investigating rare diseases as well. Since
one genetic mutation cannot be ascribed as the causal agent of many complex diseases, a
method has been developed to implement multiple genetic variants into one score summa-
rizing a total risk – namely the polygenic risk score (PRS). This score has previously shown
to be a promising, yet simple, tool to apply and generate results which are easier interpreted
compared to complex biological mechanisms. PRS might be the key, or at least a step on the
way, to solve unresolved challenges, such as the etiology behind ALL. However, it requires
knowledge of implementation, proper interpretation and most importantly, being aware of
its potential, strengths, and limitations [3].
1.1
Motivation
The etiology behind the development of ALL is the foundation of the motivation behind
this project. Only a modest fraction of the cases can be explained by known predisposing
factors, while the rest remain unresolved in terms of why these children develop exactly
ALL, and why does it appear signiﬁcantly more frequent in children – when other cancers
normally do not.
The broad hypothesis of this thesis is as follows:
Is there a genetic predisposition of acute lymphoblastic leukemia since it occurs predominantly in
children?
This hypothesis is based on multiple considerations and thoughts. One is that assuming
these children have only experienced low exposure to well-known lifestyle and environ-
mental risk factors of cancer in general – why the development of ALL cannot be explained
by this in most cases. Assuming the children are not affected by external factors, or at least
to a lesser degree compared to adults, it suggests that they were born with a predisposing

factor related to their hereditary material. Another aspect to support this hypothesis is, that
genetic abnormalities are detected in the majority of the patients [2], which independently
are related to different prognosis.
If we manage to discover or map some of the etiology and mechanisms behind the develop-
ment of ALL and increase the understanding of this complex and rare disease, it might be
possible to treat the patients even more effective and precise than today, and thus limiting

This thesis makes use of the PRS implemented in several ways on different genetic data –
always returning to whether these children did have a genetic predisposition to develop-
ing ALL. Additionally, the clinical utility of the PRS is investigated, trying to ﬁnd relations
explaining various clinical ﬁndings.

2 Background
2.1
Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia
Acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) is a malignant neoplasm of cells originally derived
from hematopoietic precursor cells of either B- or T-lymphocytes (often referred to as lym-
phoblasts) in the bone marrow [4]. ALL occurs predominantly in children with an incidence
peak from 2-7 years old and is the most common pediatric cancer type accounting for 25%
of all cancers before the age of 15 [5, 6]. However, ALL is a rare disease with an average an-
nual incidence of 68 between 2015-2019 in Denmark (across all age groups) with a declining
incidence as age increases [7].
ALL can be divided into subtypes by the immunophenotype which is determined based
upon the origination of the precursor cells – whereas B-cell precursor (BCP) accounts for
approximately 80-85% of the cases and appears to be by far the most common immunophe-
notype [8]. T-ALL on the other hand accounts for 15-20% of the cases. In this project, focus
will be on the two most common immunophenotypes, namely BCP-ALL and T-ALL. Within
these subtypes, a subclassiﬁcation can be done by assessing the cytogenetics of the cells to
determine chromosomal abnormalities, which are often related to the regulation of expres-
sion and function of transcription factors required for normal hematopoietic cell develop-
ment [4]. These chromosomal aberrations are used as a prognostic indicator of ALL, where
e.g. high hyperdiploid (> 50 chromosomes (chr)) is related to a favorable prognosis while
hypodiploid (44 chr) is related to a poor prognosis [8]. Cytogenetics available for patients
included in this project and their corresponding relation to prognosis can be seen in table
2.1 [9, 10].

2.1.1
Treatment strategy and survival
With a ﬁve-year survival rate approaching 90% in childhood ALL the overall survival rate
has increased remarkably during the last decades due to better and more speciﬁed treat-
ment strategies based upon individual risk stratiﬁcation [11]. The treatment strategy has
recently been updated and changed to a common European protocol called ALLTogether.
The patients included in this project has been treated by the previous protocol in Denmark,
which followed the Nordic Society of Pediatric Hematology and Oncology (NOPHO) ALL-
2008 protocol - why the description of the treatment and risk stratiﬁcation is of the NOPHO
ALL-2008 protocol.
The treatment extends over a time frame of 2-2.5 years and includes three overall phases
(regardless the individual risk stratiﬁcation) with different purposes. Initially, an intensive
remission induction phase (⇠4 weeks) is carried out with the aim of obtaining remission by
eradicating malignant lymphoblasts in the blood and bone marrow [12]. This is followed
by a consolidation phase (⇠7-9 weeks) which aims to eliminate potential remaining lym-
phoblasts by introducing multiple chemotherapeutic agents to minimize drug resistance
[12]. The last and longest phase of the treatment is the maintenance phase (> 18 months or
until 2.5 years after diagnosis [8]). This phase has the purpose of preventing relapse and
is less intensive and invasive for the patient as it is typically administered orally [12]. The
phases of the treatment can be seen in ﬁgure 2.1. A fourth option is a hematopoietic stem cell
transplantation (HSCT), however it is only used in cases of poor initial treatment response
(induction failure) or in the high-risk group as it is the most intensive therapy of ALL [12].
Throughout the treatment period, the different phases is of varying intensity which corre-
sponds to the risk stratiﬁcation - e.g. the higher risk group the more intensive treatment and
vice versa.

2.1.2
Risk stratiﬁcation
The speciﬁc treatment strategy and drugs used for each patient is based upon an individual
risk stratiﬁcation. A clinical measurement which the risk stratiﬁcation partly relies on, is the
minimal residual disease (MRD). It is used as a measure to describe the number of remain-
ing leukemic cancer cells after different phases of the treatment and can provide an insight
in the current situation. According to the NOPHO ALL-2008 protocol, patients are strati-
ﬁed four times during the treatment, though it is an ongoing assessment, and are placed in
either of the three groups: standard risk (SR), intermediate risk (IR) or high risk (HR) [13].
The ﬁrst stratiﬁcation is at diagnosis (or beginning of induction phase) and based on the im-
munophenotype and the white blood cell (WBC) count at diagnosis – this determines which
induction therapy will be used; dexamethasone or prednisolone. The second assessment is
solely based on a MRD measurement on day 15 of the induction phase to see the intial re-
sponse to the given treatment and possibly make adjustments. The third stratiﬁcation is to
one of the three groups (SR, IR or HR) and is in the beginning of the consolidation therapy.
It relies on the cytogenetics, bone marrow MRD and central nervous system (CNS) involve-
ment. The last stratiﬁcation is prior to maintenance therapy and is based on bone marrow
MRD [13]. The different treatment courses depending on the risk stratiﬁcation can be seen
in ﬁgure 2.1.
2.1.3
Etiology and predisposition of ALL
Over the last decades, the knowledge within the biology of how ALL develops as a disease
and thus how to treat ALL the best way possible, has increased signiﬁcantly. Despite the im-
provements, the etiology behind ALL remains unresolved [14]. The etiology is considered
being multicausal, combining environmental risk factors, individual genetic susceptibility,
and other potential risk factors (e.g. previous chemotherapy). These are proposed not only
to be environmental or genetic but rather an interaction between those as gene-environment
interactions (such as chemicals, radiation etc.) or gene-gene interactions, which are chal-
lenging to determine [2]. A well-known predisposing factor of developing ALL is having
certain types of genetic syndromes (e.g. Downs syndrome), why patients who also have
Downs syndrome has been excluded from the data used in this project. Possibilities of new
discoveries and improvements of this area is of great interest as less than 10% of the ALL
cases can be explained by the known risk factors, and the remaining 90% is left with an un-
known causation [2]. Even though the etiology is unclear, genetic abnormalities are detected
in 70-75% of the patients - why it is interesting to look into an individual predisposing risk
score based upon genetics [2].

2.2
Genomics
Genomics refers to the study of an organism’s full complement of hereditary material, fo-
cusing on a combination of sequencing and analyses referring to the structure and function
of the genome.
In humans, the haploid genome consists of ⇠3.2 billion base pairs in a double-stranded
structure of deoxyribonucleic acids (DNA) which are stored in pairs of 23 chromosomes
(22 pairs of autosomes and a pair of sex-chromosomes) [15]. The human genome encodes
approximately 20,000 genes, constituting the coding region which accounts for 1-2% [16].
The coding region is transcribed into messenger-RNA (mRNA) and translated into proteins
while the non-coding region is encoding RNA molecules of structural and functional rele-
vance, and not proteins [16].
In relation to the completion of the Human Genome Project in 2003, a reference of the human
genome became available. Many studies, primarily as a Genome-Wide Association Study
(GWAS) (explained in section 2.3) have arisen to identify and understand the genetic vari-
ations and the possible associations to disease predisposition, prognosis and personalized
treatments [17]. The human genome across two individuals are 99.9% identical, and studies
such as the 1000 Genome Project and the HapMap Project have complemented the Human
Genome Project in order to produce an extensive catalogue of the 0.1% of human genetic
variation [16, 18].
Genomic variations can vary from large-scale variation to a single nucleotide polymorphism
(SNP). The most abundant type of variation is a SNP [19]. SNPs are primarily being non-
pathological but can contribute to the development of diseases with varying degree of effect
[20]. A SNP is a single base-pair variation at a speciﬁc position in the DNA-sequence, oc-
curring in ≥1% of a given population [21, 22], otherwise commonly called a mutation. Each
individual usually inherits one copy of each SNP position from each parent, why the as-
signed genotype for any SNP position can be assigned as major allele homozygote (Hom.
major), heterozygote (Het.) or minor allele homozygote (Hom. minor) [23].
2.2.1
Germ and somatic cell lineage
At fertilization, the haploid sperm and egg genomes fuse to form a diploid zygote. The zy-
gote contains germline DNA inherited from each parent and represents the ﬁrst stage of the
development of a genetically unique organism. The zygote develops through mitosis into
multiple cells which carry the original germline DNA, since they originate from the same
gametes [24]. These formed diploid cells are called somatic cells and when differentiated
they turn into different parts of the body each with a speciﬁc function. Somatic cells include
all cells in the human body except the gametes [25].
==PAGE:17==
2 Background
7
The germ cells are set aside in the early development from all somatic cells in the embryo
by forming a fringe and thereby can traverse through the development of the somatic cells
[26]. Once the gonads are formed, the germ cells containing the germline DNA will undergo
meiosis to produce the haploid egg or sperm cells, and a new organism can be produced by
sexual reproduction.
Mutations and cellular differentiation of somatic cells changes the DNA and makes it differ-
ent from the DNA sequence in germ cells, which can possibly lead to damage of the cells and
make the cell cancerous [27, 28]. However, mutations in somatic cells are localized and will
disappear when the cell dies and further not be inherited by any offspring [27]. Mutations
in the germline cells on the other hand, can be inherited in children where all of their DNA
will carry this mutation as the zygote is formed using the DNA including the mutation. The
somatic mutation rate is almost two orders of magnitude higher than the germline mutation
rate, why the germ cells are kept apart from the somatic cell division [29].
2.2.2
Genotyping platforms
SNP genotyping platforms are required for the purpose of detecting SNPs in humans and
map the genotypes across the genome. The following section will describe the genotyping
platforms used for obtaining data related to this thesis, which includes DNA microarrays,
whole genome sequencing (WGS) and RNA sequencing (RNA-seq).
DNA microarray
A DNA microarray provides a cost-effective ($28-$90) and rapid method to genotype vari-
ants in the human genome [30, 31]. DNA microarrays are designed to have a high density of
short pieces of single stranded DNA (ssDNA) attached to the solid surface on the chip. The
ssDNA is called a probe and is complementary to the targeted SNP in the sample sequence.
The selected probes need to be chosen based on prior knowledge of detected variants in or-
der to target the desired SNPs. The genotype of the SNPs is detected by labelling the targets
with ﬂuorescence and once hybridized the genotype can be measured as it exhibits different
colors.
In relation to genome-wide studies, there will be missing data due to the limited number
of SNPs tagged on the chip. However, imputation is applied in order to estimate missing
genotypes from a genotype reference panel.
The DNA microarray of use in this thesis is the Illumina Inﬁnium Omni 2.5 Exome chip (re-
ferred to as chip) performed on germline DNA. This chip tags > 2.5 million SNPs combined
from the HapMap and 1000 Genomes project with > 2.5% minor allele frequency (MAF) [32].
The Illumina Inﬁnium Omni 2.5 Exome has a large coverage with relatively high MAFs,
which is beneﬁcial when searching for common variants and for the imputation process as
it is affected by factors as SNP coverage and density [33].
==PAGE:18==
2 Background
8
Whole Genome Sequencing
Whole genome sequencing (WGS) sequences the entire human genome of 3.2 billion base
pairs of DNA. WGS uses next generation sequencing (NGS) which relies on fragmentation
of a DNA sample into multiple smaller pieces, adding adapters for binding to the ﬂow cell,
sequencing the DNA segments and at last aligning them into a complete sequence of DNA
[34]. The NGS sequences millions of fragments in a massively parallel manner improving
speed while reducing the cost (>$500 per sample), though it is the highest cost of the intro-
duced sequencing techniques [31, 34].
As WGS sequences the entire genome, including both coding and non-coding regions, it
is suitable for SNP detection and SNP genotyping. In contrast to DNA microarrays, both
rare and common SNPs will be detected and not be limited to tagged SNPs, however, WGS
delivers huge amounts of data to be processed and stored [35].
RNA sequencing
RNA sequencing (RNA-Seq) shares the overall techniques as WGS. RNA-seq uses NGS and
can detect both common and rare variants as the sequencing is base-by-base [36]. The differ-
ences from WGS the RNA-seq only sequencing the transcriptome which accounts for <5%
of the human genome [37, 38]. This entails a cost reduction for RNA-seq ($260 per sample)
and also a corresponding reduction of the amount of data [31].
2.3
Genome-wide Association Study
Since the ﬁrst GWAS was published in 2005 much has happened in this area [39]. Several fac-
tors have contributed to an exponential increase in the numbers of GWA studies performed.
Firstly, the costs of conducting genome-wide genotyping arrays, as the ones described in
section 2.2, have decreased dramatically, now being more than 20 times less expensive than
15 years ago. Secondly, as the number of variants tested have increased from ⇠500,000 to
⇠10 million variants of the newest GWA studies, thus allowing not only to focus and detect
common variants and traits, but also rare variants and traits [40]. As of February 2022, close
to 5600 publications and more than 195,000 SNPs have been found to be associated with
some disease or traits [41].
GWA studies aim to identify associations between genetic variants, most commonly SNPs,
and a given trait. This is done by testing the allele frequency in the genetic variants in groups
who are phenotypically different [42]. Linear models are usually used to test for associations
of a continuous traits (e.g. height, blood pressure), while logistic models are used to test for
associations of a binary trait (e.g. presence or absence of a given disease - in this case ALL).
The SNPs are statistically tested for association with the phenotype, usually with Bonferroni
correction to ensure a stringent multiple-testing threshold to avoid false positives. A p-value
is deﬁned as the probability of obtaining a test statistic which is at least as extreme as the
==PAGE:19==

9
actual observed test statistic under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true. In other
words, a small p-value indicates that it is unlikely to observe an association between, in this
case, a SNP and a trait by chance. This will infer that there is a relationship between the SNP
and a trait and the null hypothesis will be rejected [43]. Commonly accepted is a genome-
wide signiﬁcance level of P < 5 ⇥10−8, which results from studies (International HapMap
project) showing that the average number of independent common genetic variants across
the human genome are approximately 1 million, thus leading to a Bonferroni corrected p-
value threshold presented in equation 2.1 (assuming a signiﬁcance level of 0.05) [42].

The genome-wide signiﬁcant SNPs are considered as effect alleles, thus meaning that a car-
rier of a given effect allele in a speciﬁc SNP (also called minor allele) will have an increased
or decreased risk of having the phenotype associated with the SNP. Effect size is a measure
of the strength between, in this case, ALL and an effect allele. Typically for logistic regres-
sion, this is reported as an odds ratio (OR), where OR > 1 is associated with an increased
risk and OR < 1 is associated with a decreased risk [44]. When considering a binary trait,
the OR is typically found using logistic regression. To obtain the effect size (OR), the logistic
regression coefﬁcient b1 can be converted into an OR as it is the estimated increase in the log

Skepticism about the clinical utility of GWAS ﬁndings has arisen as the found genetic vari-
ants may only explain a modest proportion of the possible heritability of a complex trait
[45]. With difﬁculties related to integrating gene-gene interactions and gene-environment
interactions into GWAS ﬁndings, it is proposed to explain some of the ’missing’ heritability
as complex diseases often are multi-factorial [46]. Despite challenges, GWAS ﬁndings can
lead to discovery of novel biological mechanisms and contribute to a development within
the area of precision medicine [45] and thus the implementation of GWAS ﬁndings into a
polygenic risk score has the possibilities to promote the clinical utility (further elaborated in
section 2.4).
2.4

Human genetic diseases can be of widely differing character. Some are monogenic diseases
where a few mutations with large effects causes the disease, while others are complex and
caused by hundreds to thousands of genetic variants, all with modest contributions which
collectively gives insights to the disease [47]. Genetic tests for monogenic diseases have been
in use for a decade, however it still remains challenging for complex diseases.
A polygenic risk score (PRS) is a genetic test for measuring the predisposition of polygenic
diseases, which indicates a person’s risk of developing a disease compared to others with a

SNP selection
The SNP selection for the PRS model follows two approaches during this thesis. The ﬁrst
uses summary statistics for SNPs derived from GWA studies performed on an external co-
hort in relation to ALL. These summary statistics derived from the external cohort include
among other the b1, OR and p-values for SNPs which is necessary to perform the PRS. Those
values are applicable to the PRS model, as they have gone through the necessary steps of
quality control.
The other approach for SNP selection is by performing the GWAS based on the case-control
cohorts and the genotyped SNP data. When performing the GWAS, summary statistics for
each SNP position becomes available, and quality control approaches are applied for ﬁlter-
ing and ensuring only SNPs fulﬁlling certain criteria are included in the PRS model. The
ﬁrst criteria for SNP selection from the gathered summary statistics are p-value selection
threshold approach. As SNP effects are estimated in the GWA studies using logistic regres-
sion, uncertainty will be attached to each estimate of effect sizes. The p-value is used as a
criteria to exclude SNPs with too high uncertainty, why PRS calculations will be based solely
on SNPs with p-values below a deﬁned threshold (usually P < 5 ⇥10−8) [3]. This approach
effectively shrinks all effect sizes of SNPs with too high uncertainty to zero, while it does
not perform any shrinkage of the effect size estimates of included SNPs [3].
The next step of SNP selection refers to the fact that many genetic alleles are correlated and
inherited together [51]. In many cases, a region of DNA includes several correlated alleles
which includes one independent associated SNP to a trait while the others are found signif-
icant due to their genetic link to the true causal SNP [51]. For the purpose of excluding the
dependent SNPs, linkage disequilibrium (LD) and pruning are induced. LD expresses the
genetic linkage as a measure of the correlation between an amount of SNPs, while pruning
will only report the single SNP with the strongest statistical evidence of association to a trait
in each correlated region identiﬁed by LD [52, 53].

3.2
Control cohort - UK Biobank
3.2.1
Data description
Individuals from the UK Biobank project, who fulﬁll chosen criteria described in section
3.2.3, represent the control group throughout this project. The UK Biobank began in 2006
and is a large prospective epidemiological resource consisting of ⇠500,000 participants from
the United Kingdom, aged 40-69 at recruitment. For each participant, a variety of deep ge-
netic and health-related data is available, creating a large-scaled biomedical database where
genetic predisposition and environmental exposures are of highest priority [55].
The genome-wide genetic data are obtained from 480,000 UK Biobank participants using
purpose-designed arrays with 805,426 markers for identiﬁcation of genotypes [56]. The en-
vironmental and linked health-related data is obtained primarily through medical history,
questionnaires and tests.
3.2.2
Quality control
Genotypes of the participants in the UK Biobank was called using Affymetrix on two ar-
rays – UK BiLEVE Axiom array and UK Biobank Axiom array. The former accounting for
⇠50,000 participants and the latter for the remaining ⇠450,000 participants. The quality
control done for the UK Biobank includes in general the same steps as described in the pa-
tient cohort. The imputation process used the Haplotype Reference Consortium as reference
panel and IMPUTE v4.0 for the imputation. The result after imputation was a dataset with
⇠96 million SNPs [57].
3.2.3
Data processing
The control cohort consists of people who are available in the UK Biobank and participated
in the process described in section 3.2.1. The age distribution of the patient and control
group will not be similar, since the patients are primarily children and young adults and the
participants from UK Biobank are 40-69 years old at recruitment. It is assumed that the pa-
tient group have only experienced low exposure to lifestyle and environmental risk factors
known to be related to cancer, mostly because of the age of the patients. When generating
the control group, the aim is to make two cohorts that are as similar as possible in every
aspect except for the primary area of interest, namely the genetic predisposition for devel-
opment of ALL. This is why the criteria, especially related to lifestyle and environmental
risk factors, has to be fulﬁlled in order for a person to be included in the control group.

infectious agents, physical inactivity, diet, occupational exposure, alcohol, ultraviolet light,

clude people who are exposed to factors that could potentially give rise to mutations and

3.3.2
Data processing
As previously mentioned in section 2.2.2, the WGS sequences the entire genome, hence the
data will consist of a lot more compared to the RNA-seq data, which only includes the
transcripted parts of the genome. Data was provided in Variant Call Format (VCF) for both
WGS and RNA-seq which only contains a certain position in the genome if it varies from
the reference genome applied. In that way it is possible to decrease the amount of data and
only have variants included in the data. Despite this advantage, it has a problem related
to the search for the SNPs related to ALL. If a SNP is not found in the VCF ﬁle (hence not
a variant), it is not possible to conclude whether it results from not being sequenced as it
might be located in an intron (in RNA-seq), or if the reference genome uses another allele as
a reference to what the European population uses. A way to solve this issue is by creating
a ﬁle that contains every position sequenced whether it is a variant or not. This is called a
Genomic Variant Call Format (GVCF) and can be made using Sentieon Haplotyper which
can take a binary BAM ﬁle (which is also available for both WGS and RNA-seq) and create
the GVCF ﬁle. Before applying Sentieon Haplotyper to create the GVCF ﬁles, it was ensured
only to include patients that had both the germline DNA (from WGS) and somatic tumor
RNA (from RNA-seq) data available.
3.3.3
Sentieon Haplotyper
Sentieon Haplotyper has several quality settings which can be applied when generating
VCF ﬁles. These includes a conﬁdence setting that can be set to a certain threshold - variants
with quality below the conﬁdence threshold will not be added to the output VCF ﬁle. This
setting is possible for both calling and emitting variants. To process RNA reads an option
can be applied that determines whether soft clipped bases in the reads should be excluded
from the variant calling - this means that bases in 5’ and 3’ of the read are not part of the
alignment. These settings would be applied if VCF ﬁles were the wanted output - while
making GVCF ﬁles these quality settings are ignored. Due to the fact that a GVCF should
include every position sequenced, it would not make sense to apply quality settings that
would exclude parts of the sequenced data.
In the GVCF ﬁles it is possible to ﬁnd quality measures for each position, which can then be
used to assess whether a genotype call should be included or not. This includes measures
of e.g. genotype quality and mapping quality.

3.4.1
Imputation and quality control
An imputation of the genotype data has been completed prior to this project. Thus, a brief
description of the imputation process will follow. When gathering genotype data, it is com-
mon that only a subset of SNPs is genotyped and not the whole reference panel thus leading
to a varying amount of missing data and a lack of power in the study. When imputing
genotype data, it is possible to predict genotypes that are not directly assayed in a sample
of individuals [62]. Each predicted genotype call is given an information metric (INFO)
score ranging from 0 to 1. It represents the certainty related to the genotype call, and thus a
higher INFO score the higher certainty about the predicted genotype. This imputation pro-
cess leads to a decrease in the amount of missing data and the INFO score makes it possible
to choose a threshold to maintain a high quality of the predicted genotypes [63].
The imputed SNPs in this thesis are all with an INFO score greater than 0.96, which is a
decent score as INFO scores higher than 0.4 is often approved as acceptable well-imputed
variants [63]. The imputed SNPs provide a probability for each possible genotype, where
the PRS model will call the most likely genotype for that speciﬁc SNP. To only include SNP
genotypes of acceptable probability, a hard-call threshold of 0.51 is implemented, meaning
that SNPs are used in the PRS if its genotype can be called with a probability above 0.51,
which is quite strict and conservative but provides greater certainty of the genotype calls.

3.7
Statistical testing and study design
Statistical testing is primarily applied to reduce subjective decision making of the null hy-
pothesis [65]. In this thesis a patient group of 386 patients and ten control groups of each
1158 individuals needs to be statistically tested for differences. A description of the study
design and statistical tests applied to examine the difference between the PRS of the patient
and control groups (if any), is elaborated through this section.
3.7.1
Two-sample t-test
The two-sample t-test is applied for the purpose of testing the values of a statistic between
two independent groups [65]. This thesis contains distinct cohorts of case-control design,
why the two-sample t-test is suitable for testing the null hypothesis:
H0: µpatient = µcontrol
The patient cohort and control cohort have the same mean values of PRS
With the alternative hypothesis being:
H1: µpatient 6= µcontrol
The patient cohort and control cohort does not have the same mean values of PRS
The hypothesis is tested with an arbitrary a value of 0.05. This indicates that we accept a
maximum of 5% chance of making a type 1 error if the null hypothesis is rejected in favor
to the alternative hypothesis. Type 1 errors occur when rejecting the null hypothesis, and it
is actually true [8]. However, in this study ten subsamples of the control cohort are tested
versus the patient cohort, called multiple testing. If an a value of 0.05 is used in each of the
tests, inﬂation of the a value will occur and the risk of making a type 1 error will blow up.
The event is called family wise error rate (FWER) and the calculation can be seen in equation
3.1:
FWER = 1 −(1 −a)n = 1 −(1 −0.05)10 = 40%
(3.1)
This states that in the series of testing, 40% risk of making at least one type 1 error occurs.
Instead, we induce Bonferroni correction of the a value, by dividing with the number of
tests, which results in the corrected a value of 0.005, seen in equation 3.2.
aBon f erroni = a
n = 0.05

(3.2)
Equation 3.3 shows the new risk of at least one type 1 error:

The p-value, when tested against the Bonferroni corrected a value, has to be lower than
0.005, in order to reject the null hypothesis at a 5% signiﬁcance level and claim different
means between the patient cohort and control groups.
The b value is referring to the type 2 error, which is deﬁned by failing to reject a null hy-
pothesis, when it is actually false [66]. The b value is not arbitrary determined in this study;
however, the study design has focused on its relationship to the power of the study. The
power is calculated by (1-b) and states the ability to observe an effect, when there in fact is
one [66]. The power is affected by many aspects of the study design, which among other
will be described in the following.
Multiple conditions and assumptions regarding the data has to be satisﬁed for a rigorous
methodology when performing a t-test [67]. There are checked assumptions concerning the
data, variance, normality and the sample size. The assumptions for the data and sample
selection are checked by simply looking at the data and the underlying calculations. The
data for the patient and control group possess independent and continuous PRS values in an
ordinal scale, as the PRS is ranked and with no degree of difference between the two cohorts.
Also, the sample selection is performed by the simple random sample principle, where each
individual of the control cohort is retracted with equal probability, and collectively being an
unbiased representation of a population, recall the selection described in section 3.2.3.
Normality
The importance of normality check in relation to a t-test, lies in the fact that the t-test com-
pares the means of two groups. If the data is normally distributed, the means become rep-
resentative for the cohorts as the data points are gathered around the mean value and vice
versa [67].
The data is tested for normality by a density plot for an overall assessment of the distribu-
tion of the data. Furthermore, a normal probability plot was used as a graphical technique
to approximately check for a normal distribution of given data [68].
The central limit theorem states that when sample sizes become large enough, the distri-
bution will follow a normal distribution [69]. The sample size however, does have a large
impact on the t-test, why it is not reasonably favorable to increase the sample size heavily
[67].
Sample Size
It is ideal to have adequate sample sizes with a ratio between the case-control group of 1:1,
as increased power of the study will follow [70]. In this thesis, there is an occasion of ALL
being a rare disease, why we have a limited size of the patient group of 386 individuals. The
control group on the other hand is stored in a retrospective study, containing the possibility
==PAGE:34==
3 Data & Methods
24
of a huge sample size of 142,205 individuals. As mentioned, a 1:1 study between the case-
control is possibly ideal but would in this case lead to very small sample sizes and poor
power of the statistical t-test. As the size of the patient group is ﬁxed, it is decided to increase
the sample size of the control group to 1158 individuals, meaning a 1:3 ratio. By doubling the
control group the t-statistics will be increased by a factor of 1.15, which increases the power
as well [67]. However, it is possible to include too big sample sizes. When sample sizes
are too big, the power of the study will increase to a state where the insigniﬁcant becomes
signiﬁcant and an exaggerated tendency to reject the hypothesis occurs, as the t-statistics
becomes larger [71]. Another effect of sample size ratio is that more attention should be
committed to the assumption of variance [67].
Homogeneity of variance
The two-sample t-test assumes homogeneity of the variance between the two groups and is
important as unequal variances can induce type 1 errors and lead to false positives [72]. The
violation of this assumption is checked simply by calculating the variances of each group.
Though, the sample size ratio of 1:3 in this study, leads to a focus of this area. Generally,
each data point in a small sample size will contribute in a larger extent to the variance than
a data point in a group with larger sample size. This can lead to larger variability between
the samples and the variance will be checked as an absolute value in this study.
3.7.2
Logistic regression
Binary logistic regression is a statistical technique applied to predict the relationship be-
tween a number of independent variables and a dependent variable with a binary outcome.
In this thesis, there are two purposes of the logistic regression. First off, to test if there is a
relationship between the continuous independent variable PRS and the dependent binary
variable ALL. Secondly, to conduct a candidate SNP approach of the 43 SNPs of interest in
this study.
To apply logistic regression some basic assumptions has to be fulﬁlled about the data, how-
ever, as most assumptions are related to the connection between multiple independent vari-
ables, only a few assumptions have to be checked [73]. First assumption states the depen-
dent variable to be binary and coded as 1 for ALL and 0 for no ALL for each individual. This
is related to the fact that logistic regression assumes that P(Y=1) is the probability of an event
occurring and therefore should run between 0 and 1, which is also why logistic regression
is applied in contrast to linear regression [74]. Second assumption is that each observation
has to be independent, which is fulﬁlled as the study includes two distinct groups of pa-
tients and controls. Third assumption requires linearity of the independent variables and
the outcome log-odds (logit). This assumption relates to equation 3.6, in order to use the
linear space transformed from the logistic regression. Finally, the sample sizes have to be
of appropriate size as the procedure for calculating coefﬁcients is by maximum likelihood
estimates (MLE). The MLE technique should be given more than 20 cases per independent

the 386 patients along the control group of 1158 individuals. This data was decided to be
randomly but uniquely distributed by 50% into the training set and 50% into the test set [79].
The training set is used to make the classiﬁer model, with the purpose to predict the ALL
status of each individual in the test set.
The ROC performed using ‘sklearn’ in python, will basically make a confusion matrix for
each data point, (or threshold), and calculate the TPR and FPR to be plotted on the curve.
When the ROC curve is calculated, the Area Under the Curve (AUC) can be interpreted and
used as a performance parameter to evaluate the model skill. The AUC is respresented by
the 2 dimensional area underneath the ROC curve which provides an aggregate measure of
performance across all possible classiﬁcation thresholds [80]. A model with no skill at each
threshold will have an AUC equal to 0.5, which in a ROC curve will result in a linear line
going from point (0,0) to point (1,1), and the AUC of a perfect classiﬁer will be 1 [81].
Another approach is the precision recall. The precision recall is often used when there is
imbalance in the dataset, which there is in this study as ALL is a rare disease. As mentioned,
we have 386 patients and 1158 controls. This ratio can induce many true negatives in the
ROC, and will therefore increase the speciﬁcity resulting in a decrease of the FPR which
will indicate a skilled model [78]. For avoiding this scenario, a precision-recall curve is
implemented, which has the same approach as the ROC, but has the precision on the y-axis
and recall on the x-axis. The recall is the same as the sensitivity and TPR, but precision
differs from FPR and is shown in equation 3.10:
precision =
TP
TP + FP
(3.10)
By replacing the FPR with the precision parameter, the focus is shifted from both the minor-
ity and majority, to mainly the minority group of ALL patients [81]. The recall and precision
will likewise the ROC be calculated at different thresholds and plotted on the curve where
the AUC will be calculated as the performance parameter and compared to the random
classiﬁer. The random classiﬁer in a precision recall curve is calculated by using the last
threshold value where all values are predicted as positives and therefore either TP or FP. By

using equation 3.10, the random classiﬁer is calculated as the number of patients (TP) di-
vided by the sum of patients and controls (FP). The ratio is 1:3 in this thesis, and the random
classiﬁer will be a horizontal line of precision equal to 0.25, and therefore also an AUC of
0.25.
3.8
SNP selection from GWAS and PRS calculation (preliminary)
Another part of this thesis concerns the issue of selecting SNPs by performing a GWAS on
the accessible chip data of 386 patients and 1158 controls. As this part is still ongoing, the
methods presented will be preliminary and describing what has been done until hand-in
of the thesis. The preprocessing of the chip data includes a ﬁltering to only include SNPs
with an INFO score above 0.7 to secure high imputation quality. Then a removal of very rare
SNPs with a MAF below 0.01 was done, in order to avoid having potentially blown up effect
sizes occurring from rare SNPs rather than actual being strongly associated with ALL. SNPs
with more than 2% missing data were removed as well. Lastly, LD and pruning was done to
remove dependent SNPs. In order to be able to include principal components as covariates
in the logistic regression, a principal components analysis allowing only LD pruned variants
to be included. This was carried out using plink2 obtaining summary statistics of the SNPs
which have the required quality.

5 Discussion
5.1
Weighted PRS - Predisposition and clinical utility
The weighted PRS approach has previously proven to be relevant when measuring an indi-
vidual’s risk of developing a complex disease and partly relevant when implementing the
PRS calculations into clinical utility, e.g. in diseases as breast cancer, chronic lymphocytic
leukemia and coronary artery disease [82, 83, 84]. In this thesis, we will use this potential of
the weighted PRS and apply it with the purpose of measuring the predisposition of ALL in
patients. To test our main hypothesis of ALL being a genetic disease with inherited predis-
position we performed weighted PRS calculations of the Danish children with ALL relative
to a control cohort of European ancestry using data for 15 SNPs found related to ALL.
The weighted PRS showed a signiﬁcant difference between the patient and control cohort.
This indicates that the patients were born with an inherited predisposition of ALL relative
to the control cohort, and basically that the SNPs were more frequent in the patients. How-
ever, the performance of the test evaluated by the ROC and precision-recall curve were only
slightly better than due to random classiﬁcation. The logistic regression showed an OR of
1.74, which states the PRS being positively related to the predisposition of ALL where an
increase of 1 unit in the PRS leads to the odds of ALL being multiplied by 1.74. Further
calculations showed the odds of having ALL in the highest 5% percentiles were increased
4-fold compared with the middle quantile and reduced to approximately half the odds in
the lowest 5% percentiles compared with the middle quantile.
While the weighted PRS was able to distinguish ALL status, it generally did not show signs
of clinical utility. We chose to test if a higher PRS was associated with an earlier onset of
ALL, which was not the case. Furthermore, a link between PRS and severity of ALL, based
on WBC count, were tested but likewise did not show any ﬁndings. However, a test of the
PRS’ ability to distinguish between the immunophenotypes (BCP-ALL and T-ALL) showed
signs of a distinction.
When the ﬁndings of our weighted PRS are reﬂected upon a theoretical perspective, we ﬁnd
that the model is highly related and dependent of what is given as input. The 15 SNPs
included were found to be associated with a higher risk of B-cell ALL which complement
the majority of our patients diagnosed with BCP-ALL (319 of 386). This match makes our
weighted PRS model able to signiﬁcantly discriminate between the patients and controls,
in this exact scenario. However, the SNPs were not related to the clinical measures age of
onset or MRD, so one might ask why the weighted PRS should be able to ﬁnd any con-
nection to clinical utility and if we try to use the PRS measurements out of context. It is
==PAGE:55==
5 Discussion
45
earlier described that the 15 SNPs included are from different genes and pathways and then
summarized by the PRS, why the PRS may not be associated to anything but genetic predis-
position of speciﬁcally B-cell ALL [85]. The PRS showed signs of a discrimination between
the immunophenotypes of ALL, which is likewise a cause of the 15 SNPs being associated
to speciﬁcally B-cell ALL. The effect is that the SNPs are more frequent in the the BCP-ALL
patients why the calculated PRSs are higher than the patients diagnosed with T-cell ALL.
In our strive to ﬁnd relations between the PRS and clinical utility, a connection to the prog-
nosis of ALL were tested. Again no connection were found, and were again as a cause of the
SNP selection. If a model was based on 15 hypothetical SNPs related to hyperdiploid ALL,
which has a favorable prognosis, then a high PRS would be related to a favorable progno-
sis as well. However, if the 15 hypothetical SNPs were related to hypodiploid ALL, then a
higher PRS would instead lead to an poor prognosis (table 2.1). This scenario is stated as
it explains the connection between what we try to measure, and the capabilities of the PRS
solely based on the SNP selection.
The performance of the weighted PRS, evaluated by the ROC and precision-recall curve,
indicated the PRS as a classiﬁer performing slightly better than random classiﬁcation. The
lack of performance might be due to the method of SNP selection and belonging effect sizes.
By including SNPs and its weights from GWA studies, we are assuming them to have the
same impact in all populations of the same ethnicity, which seems unrealistic [85]. We chose
to only include SNPs from the same study in order to not mix populations when using esti-
mated effects, but the estimated effects could be very different from the reality and therefore
also in our population and lead to alternative results.
The weighted PRS model did show potential for being a measurement of inherited predis-
position of ALL, which can be beneﬁcial in the section of health care. As the PRS could pos-
sibly be implemented as a screening technique, it could lead to early diagnosis of ALL and
decrease the burden of the disease, however, previous implementations showed issues as
over-diagnosing and increased costs [82, 86]. Also, as ALL is a rare disease, the cost-beneﬁt
of screening every newborn child would be too expensive, and would require a more ac-
curate model than seen in this project. The PRS model could alternatively be a link in the
current diagnosis to pinpoint individuals with known risk factors e.g. family history of can-
cer or siblings of ALL patients and calculate the PRS as an additional measurement [86].
Another limitation of the implementation of the PRS, is that even though an individual has
high odds of developing ALL, it is a relative risk and one might live fearing for ALL al-
though the absolute probability of developing ALL is very low. Also, an individual who
knows to be predisposed of ALL, cannot reduce the risk as the current knowledge about the
etiology is not speciﬁed for lifestyle/environmental risk factors.

During this thesis, we found that the PRS is a two-edged sword. The biggest limitation as
being very speciﬁc of what is given to the model and what we can expect it to deliver as out-
come and thus being narrow in implementation. This has also the possibility of being the
greatest advantage, it just requires extensive knowledge of what you include in the model
and doing a correct interpretation of the outcome – which showed to be closely related to
the input.
If we should do it all over again with the knowledge acquired during the process of this the-
sis, and knowing the capabilities and limitations of the PRS, we would take advantages of
its speciﬁcity. Instead of only looking at a broad perspective of a PRS based on genetic pre-
disposition for ALL, and trying to apply that to other clinical ﬁndings (e.g. risk group, WBC
count etc.), it would be interesting to ﬁnd SNPs associated to certain prognosis. For exam-
ple, calculating a weighted PRS for each of the three risk groups (standard, intermediate and
high risk) with one based on SNPs related to high risk, one based on intermediate, and one
based on standard. In that way it would be possible to see if the PRS could distinguish be-
tween subgroups within the patients and could potentially be used as a prognostic measure.
Another interesting aspect could be to look into severe side effects from the treatment, re-
lapse and survival of the patients. Again, with the perspective of being even more speciﬁc
in SNP selection to have a greater possibility to obtain relevant and signiﬁcant results that
could increase the quality of life while having a deadly disease. Both suggestions would
require even more patient data – possibly looking outside Denmark’s borders, since ALL is
a rare disease, and the number of patients are limited.
5.2
Unweighted PRS - Germline DNA and somatic tumor RNA
The results from the germline DNA and somatic tumor RNA showed signiﬁcantly differ-
ences in the developed unweighted PRS (ﬁgure 4.10), with the somatic tumor RNA being
distributed around 4 (average of ALL and non-ALL) and the germline DNA with a four-
fold increase and thus distributed around 16 (average of ALL and non-ALL). An interesting
ﬁnding is that the ALL patients have a slightly higher PRS, both in somatic tumor RNA and
in germline DNA. This indicates, that the 43 SNPs used in the unweighted PRS indeed are
related to ALL (as expected) - though it is not a huge difference. As this part of the project
solely relies on patient data and hence not a case-control study, together with an even more
simple PRS model compared to the weighted PRS, it is not necessarily obvious why the dif-
ferences between the PRS of germline DNA and somatic tumor RNA are of that proportion.
Aspects of this will be elucidated in the following section, though that we only managed to
scratch the surface of these data types and its potential.
As stated earlier (section 2.1.3), ALL is considered or at least proposed to have genetic fac-
tors contributing to the development of the disease – even though the etiology remains un-
resolved. Based on this knowledge, a hypothesis was that, because ALL primarily occurs
==PAGE:57==

in young children there could potentially be inherited mutations in the germline DNA that
drives the development of the disease. Why it would be expected that the results would
show a higher PRS distribution of the germline DNA compared to the somatic tumor RNA.
Despite the results being consistent with the expectations there are several factors that could
contribute to the obtained results, and thus not necessarily be directly ascribed to a clear re-
lation between germline DNA ﬁndings and the early development of ALL.
First off, some of the differences could remain from the fact that the data arise from different
genetic material, namely DNA and RNA. This results in a very small overlap of the genetic
data in each patient, as the RNA only constitute less than 5% of the whole genome. This
leading to an expectation of, in general, ﬁnding each SNP more frequently in the germline
DNA compared to the somatic tumor RNA. This was indeed the case showed in ﬁgure 4.11,
where it could be seen that more or less each of the 43 SNPs was more frequently appearing
in the germline DNA – especially in the heterozygote genotype. This leads to the choice of
PRS model, as it was chosen to do an unweighted PRS in order to include all 43 SNPs across
the studies. When implementing the found genotypes in the PRS with the additive method
– it is the same as counting how many times the minor allele occurs in a patient’s genetic
data. So, when there is a huge difference in the frequency, most likely due to the different
data types, it is directly affecting the PRS score, which is surely one of the reasons of the
much higher germline DNA PRS.
The unweighted PRS is an even more simple method of summarizing the polygenic con-
tributions based on a patient’s genetic data, why it has certain limitations. Again, it solely
depends on the SNPs included in the model – in this case the 43 SNPs that have previously
been reported as associated to ALL. This is not a large amount compared to size of the data
included, which is another obstacle. Even though this huge amount of data has the potential
to discover novel genetic ﬁndings, it requires some knowledge or new methods in terms of
how to handle the data. When only looking at such small subset of 43 SNPs, you do not
exploit its full potential.
An attempt to implement and make use of the amount of data was to calculate a mutational
burden for each patient. In the calculation of this measure, it was investigated in a more
general setting – and not necessarily with ALL as focus point. By taking each patient’s
sequenced data of the germline DNA by WGS and the number of variants found in both the
germline DNA and the somatic tumor RNA, it was possible to make a measure which could
provide an indication of mutational burden. This held together with the developed PRS
could possibly provide an insight if patients with a higher PRS would have a corresponding
larger number of variants in their somatic tumor RNA or in their germline DNA. Thus,
the results did not show any interesting ﬁndings to look further into (see appendix D, ﬁgure
D.3). Ideally, it would have been preferred to apply both the PRS and the mutational burden
calculations to a larger patient cohort than 171 persons. This would increase the certainty of
which the ﬁndings could be accepted.

5.3
Candidate SNP approach
The purpose of conducting a candidate SNP approach was to test the already known SNPs
associated with ALL on the cohorts in this project – which consist of the 386 danish patients
diagnosed with ALL, and a control cohort consisting of 1158 people from UK Biobank who
met certain criteria. All 43 SNPs (across studies - appendix A) was individually tested with
logistic regression and thereby obtaining a new effect size and p-value for each of the SNPs
based on our population. By doing this, it was possible to identify which SNPs that were
found to be signiﬁcant with the data used throughout the project and address them as can-
didate SNPs. As visualized in ﬁgure 4.12 and in table 4.4, six SNPs had a lower p-value than
the Bonferroni threshold and thus considered as candidate SNPs. Five out of the six candi-
date SNPs originated from Identiﬁcation of four novel associations for B-cell acute lymphoblastic
leukemia, and are potentially associated with the majority of our patient cohort being diag-
nosed with BCP-ALL as the GWAS investigated B-cell ALL.
In order to verify and test these six SNPs, it was chosen to perform a weighted PRS cal-
culation together with a ROC curve and precision-recall curve. This was done to make a
comparison between the SNPs that are known to be signiﬁcant based on our population
and the 15 SNPs, along with their corresponding effect sizes, from the article 2019 which
was found on another population and then applied to the cohorts in this project. An expec-
tation was that the difference between patient and control in the density plot would be more
distinct, and that the PRS would perform better as a predictive model. This was indeed the
case, both in terms of distinction and as a predictive model (ﬁgure 4.14). When looking at
the obtained effect size from the logistic regression (table 4.5), it showed a higher OR value
of 1.96 compared to 1.74 based on the weighted PRS based on the 15 SNPs, which implies a
stronger association between this weighted PRS model and ALL compared to the other.
Though one should keep in mind, that during this approach, the identiﬁcation, and ﬁndings
of the six candidate SNPs were based on the cohorts used in this project. So, when imple-
menting the effect sizes in the weighted PRS on the same population, the results are almost
certain to appear better and more predictive since it is already known that the SNPs were
signiﬁcant between the patient and control cohort. It becomes a circular event of verifying
and applying on the same population, which ideally should be on independent populations.
Once again, the PRS model is solely relying on the SNPs decided to be included and, in this
case, it is enhancing the possibility of directing the outcome towards a desired signiﬁcant
result.

6 Conclusion
In this thesis, polygenic risk scores were developed both as a weighted and unweighted
score using the additive method using SNPs found to be associated with ALL in GWA stud-
ies. This was done to investigate whether children with ALL did have a genetic predispo-
sition of developing the disease, with the purpose of exploring the etiology behind ALL.
Additionally, the clinical utility of the PRS was tried to be implemented on accessible clini-
cal data to increase the usability of the PRS. A candidate SNPs approach was carried out to
examine the SNPs on our own population and determine signiﬁcant SNPs and their effect
size based on the data used during this project. Finally, a GWAS was conducted to ﬁnd
genome-wide signiﬁcant SNPs based on the patient and control cohort.
The weighted PRS based on 15 SNPs from “Identiﬁcation of four novel associations for B-cell
acute lymphoblastic leukaemia risk” did show a signiﬁcant difference in the PRS between the
patient cohort and the control cohort, indicating a genetic predisposition of developing ALL
if a person does have these speciﬁc SNPs. Logistic regression was applied to study this
relation and found an OR of 1.74 implying a positive relation between PRS and a genetic
predisposition. It is important not to interpret this result as an absolute risk of having ALL.
This result suggests that a 1 increase in PRS leads to a 1.74 higher likelihood of having ALL
compared to the control cohort, and hence implicit resulting in a genetic predisposition be-
cause the PRS is based on exactly this.
Germline DNA and somatic tumor RNA was implemented in an unweighted PRS on a pa-
tient group consisting of children diagnosed with ALL and children diagnosed with another
type of cancer. The PRS was based on the 43 SNPs across GWA studies, and showed a sig-
niﬁcant difference in the PRS, where the germline DNA showed a four-fold increase in PRS
compared to somatic tumor RNA. A heatmap showed that each SNP occurred remarkably
more frequent in the germline DNA, which could imply that the higher PRS could result
from the SNPs being more frequent sequenced rather than appearing more frequent.
The candidate SNP approach found six of the 43 SNPs to be signiﬁcant using our popula-
tion. All six had an OR above 1, thus indicating a positive association between each of these
SNPs and ALL. When implementing the newly found effect sizes into a weighted PRS, we
found a slightly stronger association between the PRS and ALL with a corresponding OR
being 1.92. Both the candidate SNP approach and the PRS used the same population, why
the result was expected to ﬁnd a stronger association as there is no validation population.
==PAGE:61==
6 Conclusion
51
The ﬁrst steps were taken towards conducting a GWAS on the data used previously in this
thesis. We did only manage to identify that there was a problem within the population,
most likely from having two datasets merged into one, and we did not solve the appearing
substructures in time.
In general, applying the PRS to clinical data did not show any signiﬁcant ﬁndings during
this project. The most important take-home message from working with the PRS, is that SNP
selection is alpha and omega in what you can expect the results from the PRS to be. A speciﬁc
example from this thesis is, that we tried to see if the PRS (based on genetic predisposition)
could be linked to the risk stratiﬁcation of each patient – which surely it could not. Instead,
the PRS should have been based on SNPs speciﬁcally related to prognosis of ALL and then
the chances of a signiﬁcant result would increase. The PRS has potential as a simple and
easily applied tool to examine complex genetic diseases, but the user should be aware of the
input (SNP selection) in the model and the interpretation of the results.
6.1
Future work
During this project, we found that the PRS is very dependent on the SNPs used as input.
As discussed earlier, this has the potential of calculating very speciﬁc PRS regarding clini-
cal features - not only for ALL but for all kinds of diseases in which genetics are suggested
to contribute in some way. So instead of developing one PRS for each person, you could
implement several PRS each describing different aspects of the disease based on the SNPs
contributing to the PRS. This requires a more comprehensive and precise SNP selection, and
that each PRS should be interpreted differently.
Another interesting aspect could be to go more general instead more speciﬁc. If a PRS was
developed on SNPs known to be associated with cancer in a broader perspective instead of
only ALL, it could be possible to investigate whether the ALL patients or childhood can-
cer patients seem to have a genetic predisposition towards development of cancer, and thus
having a higher risk of developing cancer already as a child.
As mentioned, a further processing of the data used to the GWAS based on chip data would
be a good idea to do in order to carry out the GWAS with appropriate adjustment of the
found subcultures in the population. If obtaining this result, it would be possible to identify
genome-wide signiﬁcant candidate SNPs based upon the two cohorts used for this thesis -
the 386 patients diagnosed with ALL, and the 1158 controls from the UK Biobank. Using
these summary statistics from the GWAS and the relevant candidate SNPs, we would like
to do a PRS calculation on the foundation of these ﬁndings. Ideally, this PRS would be
developed on another population than the one used for the GWAS, as there would be no
validation if it was to be developed on the same population.
