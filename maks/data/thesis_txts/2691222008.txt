Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to develop a system, which can be used by a company to sug-
gest new incremental product concepts to their current portfolio based on user’s preferences
and requirements. This was achieved by combining methods from systematic design, prod-
uct customization, and function modelling. The project was based on a case scenario with
a kitchen stand mixer through investigation of the current market and the market opportu-
nity through statistics. A new potential customer segment was deﬁned based on this analysis,
which was veriﬁed through qualitative and quantitative interviews with the potential customer
segment. Furthermore, the most important values (price, usability, etc.) for the customer of
kitchen equipment was also investigated during this survey. The kitchen stand mixer was then
analyzed and the diﬀerent modules of the mixer was mapped in a product variant master.
Furthermore, the product was decomposed into it’s principle structure, parts, forbidden areas,
and functional surfaces. Product requirement (from a user’s perspective) and their relation to
the diﬀerent parts of the product was translated into functions based on function modelling.
This includes price, durability, eﬀectiveness, overall size, and maintenance. These requirements
(and requirements in terms of weight and design) are then translated into quantiﬁable perfor-
mance indicators. A 3D model was created in Creo Parametric based on top-down design and
resilient modelling to make the 3D model as ﬂexible as possible. This model was integrated
with calculations of the engineering requirements in the integration software ModelCenter. It
became clear that this software was not suitable for analysis of product modulization. Only
the motor was therefore created as a module in the 3D model. The model was used in a DoE
analysis to decide which variables has the largest inﬂuence on the engineering aspects. The
most sensitive variables was used for further optimization analysis of the product. A bug in
the chosen software made it impossible to perform large analysis, which made the results less
reliable. The outcome of the analysis’ was a range of designs, which score diﬀerently in the
engineering aspects. The best designs are rendered in Creo. These designs were then evaluated
by 6 potential customers. Half of the participants preferred the same design, which suggests
that this tool can be used to specify a design, which a group of users prefer. Furthermore, three
design engineers were asked to provide feedback on the usefulness of the tool. They stated that
aspects of the project was useful, however, the conﬁgurator was too tightly constrained. The
project has some limitations, for example that the interviews were performed with few people
and the design space of the model had been tightly constrained due to technical diﬃculties in
the integration software.

Introduction & Methodology
Some consumer products reuses the same components to create multiple series of the same
product. Some of these consumer products are produced based on a portfolio of product family
build on product platforms and product architectures (e.g. Black and Decker’s consumer power
tool product portfolio). These consist of a platform, for which diﬀerent types/series of the same
product can be developed. The design of this product is based on market research, focus groups,
and service design [1]. Some ﬁrms also use a module-based product range to build product
conﬁgurators, which customers or sales persons can build mass-customized products based on,
e.g. American Power Conversion’s (APC) production of infrastructure systems for computer
centers [2]. Both approaches require an understanding of the customer’s and user’s needs and
how the diﬀerent subsystems in the platform contributes to these. However, it is assumed
that concept development of new additions (both modules and products) to the product family
is based on the regular design process. One simple model of such a process is the analyze,
synthesize, and evaluate phases [3]. This project investigates whether the development of a
new concept to an existing product family/series based on modularzation can be automated
based on a conﬁguration tool and 3D model.
The new concept should be developed with
incremental design improvements in mind.
The question which this project sought to answer was therefore whether the process of con-
cept development of incremental changes to an existing product based on user
preferences can be automated?
3.1
Aim
The project aimed to develop a system (product conﬁgurator), which could suggest new product
concepts based on user’s preferences and requirements. The system should be used during
concept development of incremental changes to an existing product which can be divided into
modules. The project was based on producing new concepts of a kitchen stand mixer inspired by
the Kenwood kitchen stand mixer. On this company’s webpage, they have a broad assortment
of diﬀerent kitchen stand mixers which are very similar [4], but is assumed to fulﬁll diﬀerent
user needs (e.g. by varying the bowl size of the product). Their products therefore seems to
be based on modules.
3.2
Research questions
During the beginning of the project, diﬀerent research questions were formulated to deﬁne the
direction of the project. These are presented in this section along with a short answer to each
of them and an indication of in which section of this report they are investigated.
An overall question, which the project had to ﬁnd an answer to was also developed:
How might we do concept development based on user-interaction with a conﬁgu-
rator?

1. What deﬁnes a regular design process?
The design process often consists of three major stages: Analysis of the problem and
requirements, synthesise of diﬀerent solutions, and evaluation of the solutions based on
requirements.
(a) How is the design process currently being automated?
Ongoing research investigates automatization of all three stages of the design process.
A lot of the research is focused on automating the synthesise stage by creating
computer generated designs (often focused on the visual aspect) and predicting the
score of these based on big data of the market.
(b) How are conﬁgurators used in the design process?
The conﬁgurators are used to automate the customization design process
(c) How can the design process be further automated?
It seems that it has not been investigated how the concept development can be
automated with a mix of conﬁguration and design optimization
2. What software is currently used to structure products?
Diﬀerent types of software is used based on the stage of the design process. CAD software
is often used during the concept development of the product.
(a) What (accessible) software would work well together to make a conﬁgurator based
on CAD drawings?
Creo Parametric and Phoenix Integration was chosen, since Creo is very robust
when it comes to top-down design, and Phoneix Integration is capable of integrating
diﬀerent ﬁles and has powerful analysis algorithms (which will be needed to specify
the best designs)

3. What market opportunity currently exists for kitchen aid machines?
(a) What are currently the top sellers on the market?
It was not possible to ﬁnd this information, however, based on reviews of kitchen
stand mixers it seems that Kitchen Aid, Hamilton Beach, Smeg, Cuisinart, Crux,
and Kenwood are top seller brands
(b) Who are the buyers/users of these machines?
Based on statistics of e.g. food habits it is assumed that the main users are upper-
or middle class women (30-50 years) living with their family
(c) What do the buyers/users value in the product?
Based on reviews on kitchen stand mixers it seems that the most important values are
price, size, weight, accessories, design, eﬃciency, noise, maintenance, and usability
(d) Where are there gaps in the market?
Based on statistics it seems that either young Danes (18-30 years old) living alone or
with others or elderly Danes (65-90 years old) living alone are potential new niche

Section 6
4. What is the general structure for kitchen aid machines?
(a) What are the objectives of the machine?
The main objective is to mix ingredients. However, the product consists of many
sub-objectives. These were deﬁned in an objectives tree
(b) What functions and means does it consist of?
The main function is to mix ingredients. However, the product consists of many
sub-functions. The diﬀerent functions of the product were deﬁned in an objectives-
function-mean tree.
(c) What parts does it consist of?
Based on a PVM it seems that the product consist of a bowl, paddle, housing, motor,
locking mechanism, gearbox, gear chain, and accessories.
(d) How are the values represented in the diﬀerent parts of the machine?
Many of the values are present in all parts, e.g. weight and price. However, dura-
bility is only present in the gearbox and bowl. Eﬀectiveness is based on the bowl.
Maintenance is based on housing, bowl, paddle, and planetary hub. Design is only
present in parts visible from the outside. This relation is mapped in the objectives-
functions-means tree
(e) How are the diﬀerent parts related to each other in terms of functional surfaces?
The product consists of many functional surfaces, where the user or parts interact.
As well as many banned surfaces between parts, which must not collide. One critical
area is the void, where the bowl is placed.

5. How can the machine be modelled in a CAD software?
(a) Which parts should be modelled? What should the level of detail be?
The level of detail was kept at a minimum and the parts chosen were therefore the

planetary hub, gearbox, and the two buttons. The locking mechanism and electronics
were black-boxed. The accessories were excluded due to restriction in terms of time.

3.3
Learning Objectives
The learning objectives were also deﬁned during the initial phase of the project, these are
deﬁned below. These will be the reviews during the reﬂection 9.
1. Scope the size of the project according to the time frame
2. Deﬁne research area based on relevant literature
3. Argue for choice of theory and methods and their limitations
4. Deﬁne relevant objectives, needs and values of the chosen type of product based on market
research (both ”hard” and ”soft” criteria)
5. Analyze and evaluate on the most important product objectives, user needs and values
based on user interaction and analyze how they are reﬂected in the diﬀerent parts of the
product (based on an architecture model) and how they can be translated into the 3D
model
6. Deﬁne chosen product in terms of which parts it consists of, which functions these parts
have, and how the parts are structured inside the product using a systematic design
approach and an architecture model
7. Assess how to keep the ﬂexibility of the 3D model and conﬁgurator within a feasible
design space
8. Create ﬂexible 3D model of the product and conﬁgurator of the product and link these
through the chosen objectives, needs, and values
9. Plan and conduct user interviews with conﬁgurator and create 3D model based on the
user’s decision in conﬁgurator during the interview
10. Summarize and interpret user-interaction and other data collected

The current market for the chosen product (kitchen stand mixer) was investigated based on

Milestone 2 (section 6)

modes through a FMEA and furhter translation of the most critical ones into design require-
ments. Eﬀectiveness and maintenance was based on further surveys with potential customers,
specifying how they would deﬁne these.
Design was based on specifying diﬀerent possible
designs based on a review of existing designs. The connection between customer needs and
engineering aspects were visualised in a house of quality.

top down design. The model did not encompass all parts down to the last detail. Instead, the
most important parts (aﬀecting the engineering aspects speciﬁed in the previous section) were
modelled. The conﬁgurator was constructed in ModelCenter based on the 3D model, scripts
and excel sheets with calculations of engineering aspects, and scripts limiting the design space.
Milestone 4 (section 8)
DoE analysis’ were performed to specify which dimensions and other variables had the largest
eﬀect on the engineering aspects. The most inﬂuential variables and design variables were then
used in an optimization analysis, which produced diﬀerent designs. The best designs (based on
engineering aspects) were used to discuss preferences with 6 potential customers. Furthermore,
the potential of this type of tool developed during the project was discussed with three design
engineers.

4.1
The Current Design Process
"Design process" is a very broad term and can be used to describe the process of many types
of projects. In this case, the focus is on the design process used during product development
of consumer products from an engineering perspective.
The design process is often divided into two types: The creative processes and more systematic
approaches. Design processes can also involve both types of processes. Engineering design de-
velopment is mainly based on a more structured approach: The design process for engineering
product development described in literature encourages the designers to work systematically.
Most of these are based on the basic design process structure of analyze-synthezise-evaluate
[3]. The analyze phase constitutes the deﬁnition of requirements and performance indications
of the product. The synthesis phase involves concept development, where a solution to each
performance indication is explored and a set of concepts made based on this. In the evaluate
phase the diﬀerent designs are evaluated in terms of how well they fulﬁll performance require-
ments for operation, manufacturing, and sales before the ﬁnal design is selected. Examples of
deﬁnitions of more detailed design processes are the four stage model, French’ design process
model, the engineering design process from VDI 2221 (Verein Deutscher Ingenieure), and the
Double Diamond.
The four stage gate model[3] includes an exploration phase (similar to the analysis phase),
generation phase (similar to the synthesis phase), evaluation phase (similar to the evaluation
phase), and communication phase (where a ﬁnal design is produced).
This is an iterative
process, meaning that the Generation and Evaluation phase might be repeated.
French’ design process model [6] consists of a need, which is analyzed and formalized into a
problem statement. Then, diﬀerent designs are created and detailed. Throughout some of these
phases, feedback from e.g. users or other stakeholders might be necessary.
The engineering design process from VDI 2221 (Verein Deutscher Ingenieure) with seven diﬀer-
ent stages each has an output, which is used as an input for the next stage (see illustration 1).

It should be noted, that this type of process is not linear but iterative, and the designer might
therefore go back and forth between phases until the results are satisfactory. Especially since
the Synthesis phase might be further divided into concept development and detailed product

the design world since they focus on the problem deﬁnition and is diﬀerent from the designers

The Double Diamond [8] process consists of four phases: 1. Discover (where problems are

and 4. Deliver (one concept is chosen and ﬁnalized). The ﬁrst and third phases are divergent,
meaning the problem space and solution space are explored, while the second and fourth phases
are convergent, meaning the problem area and solution space is narrowed/deﬁned.
These
processes are all centered around deﬁning the problem, which the product should solve, and
ﬁnding a solution.
The design processes described in literature (above) seemed rather extensive for companies
where a new version or series of a product would need to be developed. In these cases the
problems, which the product is solving, would already be know. Furthermore, the exploration of
diﬀerent solutions would also assume to be within the company’s knowledge. In these projects,
the goal would therefore be to make a product, which would be an upgrade compared to the
last of it’s kind. It’s assumed that the company would have an interest in reusing the same
components in the new designs in order to minimize the amount of diﬀerent versions of a part.
One structured way of achieving this is through the deﬁnition of moduralization and product
architectures.

4.2
Automating The Design Process
It seemed worthwhile to investigate whether the process of developing a new version or series
of the product could be automated. This is investigated through all three main stages of the
design process.
Analyze The ﬁrst part of the design process has been automated through the use of AI and
deep learning to analyze large amounts of data on consumer behaviour to determine things
such as design gaps in the market [9].
Synthesise The second part of the design process (developing concepts) can be automated
based on CAD tools and algorithms. This phase can be automated to diﬀerent degrees and
through diﬀerent methodologies. Three of these are topology optimization, generative design,
and design conﬁguration. These processes are used for diﬀerent purposes.
Topology optimization is a wide area of research, consisting of diﬀerent sub-categories like
shape design. The basic structure is the use of a mathematical formulation or algorithms of a
design problem to support selection of the optimal design among many alternatives. This can
be integrated into the design process through CAD models [10]. These methods seems usable
for this project, in aspects, where new modules have to be generate.
Generative design has become trendy during resent years, which has only previously become
commercialized (e.g. the ﬁrst chair made using generative design was produced in 2019 [11]).
Generative design is also a type of design exploration (like topology design). However, more
powerful algorithms are used, which can explore even further corners of the design space. The
research on the area is often focused on optimizing one speciﬁc aspect of the design, like the
visuals, and not on automating for multiple factors of the product. This includes research by
Barros et. al. [12] and Oh et. al. [13]. Both papers mainly focus on exploring diﬀerent designs
by changing diﬀerent parameters of the product and then analysing physical aspects of the
design, like mass or force.
Product conﬁguration is based on a computer-aided systems which can produce custom prod-
ucts based on rules in the system and requirements from users. Product conﬁgurators are often
used in businesses utilizing mass-customization [2]. These can also be used to automate the
early design phase by choosing from and interactively working with automatically generated
alternative solutions [14]. These methods seems highly usable for the parts of the product,
which are modules.
Other research in the area of optimizing the synthesis phase is research by Komoto and
Tomiyama [15], which aims at the development of a CAD system, which is used to support
conceptual design of multi-disciplinary complexity products through hierarchical system decom-
position. SA-CAD oﬀers a collaborative environment for the diﬀerent engineers of the diﬀerent
areas of the domain. The approach in terms of decomposition of the system from functions
to parts seems worthwhile to incorporate into this project. However, creating a collaborative
environment is not part of this scope, since the chosen product is a simple system.
Evaluate Many of the above speciﬁed techniques to automate the synthesis phase also involves
some form of evaluation of requirements like mass and force. Evaluation also often consist of

4.3
Closing the gap between problem and solution
The connection between the problem, diﬀerent requirements of the product, possible solutions,
and the ﬁtness of these solutions had to be quantiﬁed if a computer should be able to do the
process. Deﬁning how the diﬀerent parts of the system/product aﬀects the requirements of the

One beneﬁcial method of closing this gap is through function modelling (FM). However, other
systematic design methodologies for closing this gap also exists, like axiomatic design [18] and

4.3.1
Function modelling
Function modelling is a key step in the product design process. [19]. It can be used in various
ways, but one of the key values is it’s ability to divide the product into functions, which can
be connected with the components and deﬁne modules for e.g. architecture models[19]. In this
thesis it will be used to deﬁne the boundaries and the structure of the CAD model.
Function modelling (FM) is used to keep an overview of how details of the products (often
produced at the end of a design process) contribute to the overall goal (often produced at the
beginning of the process) of the product. A functional model can show how the general goal
of a system is achieved by realization of subgoals via the subfunctions in the system. This

is achieved through functional concept ontology (as opposed to device ontology and process
ontology), which focuses on answering what the device and its components do or what the
purpose of the device and its components are. [20]
In order to create a structure of functions and goals and subfunctions and subgoals one has
to deﬁne what a Goal or Function is. In FM literature the deﬁnition of these words vary,
some being more subjective than others. During this thesis it was decided to consider function
as a cluster of words, which subjectively categorises the linkage between the human inten-
tions/purposes (resident in the subjective realm) to the behaviors and structures (resident in
the objective realm). This is based on the conception of Umeda et al. [21] and Chandrasekaran
and Josephson [22]. The subjective realm is the abstraction and mental conception of humans
(e.g. their needs), while the objective realm is the physical relations and processes applied to
an object [20]. In other words, a function is considered as the action on the input ﬂow to change
its state and then obtaining the required output ﬂow.
The functions are mapped through a translation from one realm to the other as shown on
illustration 2 where the need is the customer’s/users need. The environment centric function
is an intermediary step between the needs and device-centric function.
The device-centric
function describes what the device needs to be able to do. This is further decomposed in order
to connect them to speciﬁc components, which perform these functions. [20]

Quality function deployment

5.7
Result: Deﬁnition of automation of design process and market
opportunity
From the literature study it became clear that the majority of design processes for development
of engineering products were based on three stages: Analyze (specify needs and requirements),
synthesise (make diﬀerent concepts), and evaluate (evaluate concepts based on requirements
and user feedback). Ongoing research on automating all three stages exists. This was mostly
based on optimizing one aspect automatically, e.g.
aesthetics, often via the use of a large
amount of data on similar designs.
However, it seemed that not much research had been
focused on automating the process while focusing on multiple aspects of the design at once.
Function modelling and quality function deployment was going to help close the gap between
the needs speciﬁed by the user, the functions of the product, and the diﬀerent parts of the
product.
It was decided to use Creo Parametric and Phoenix integration as basis for the construction
of the conﬁguration and optimization tool, due to it’s robustness and capabilities in terms of
integration with many other ﬁle types.
The market research assumed based on multiple statistics and assumptions that the average
user of kitchen stand mixers were women in their 30-50’s who were upper- or middle class and
living with their family. Based on statistics, it was suggested that three diﬀerent other groups
of people showed potential as customers of a new kitchen stand mixer design. These were young
Danes (18-30 years old) living alone or together with others or elderly Danes (65-90 years old)
living alone. It was decided to focus on young Danes living together, since this user group
showed most potential. However, this will be a niche market.
A questionnaire was constructed to conﬁrm this. It’s focus was the participants’ relationship
to food and kitchen equipment. The questionnaire was sent as an online survey to appropriate
groups on social media. 19 people within the customer segment participated in the survey. 8
people were interviewed as qualitative interviews (face-to-face) as well. The survey conﬁrmed a
need/want for a kitchen mixer among the participants. This was not a great number of people
and the results might therefore not prove as reliable as one might have hoped. The online
survey showed that when participants were asked what their next piece of kitchen equipment
should do, the majority chose either whisk/mix, fry, or chop as one of their three wishes. From
the qualitative survey it also seemed that multi-functionality was important to be worthwhile to
have such a large machine in their kitchen. It therefore seemed worthwhile that both mixing and
chopping should be part of the function of the product. The majority in the online survey valued
durability, price, eﬀectiveness/productivity, maintenance, and design as the most important
values when choosing kitchen equipment (in prioritized order). These should therefore be the
main focus of the concept.

The goal of this chapter was to deﬁne the kitchen stand mixer. This was performed through

6.1
Basic Structure of the mixer
The basic structure of the current kitchen stand mixer was deﬁned through methods of product
range analysis based on methods from Hvam et. al [2], since this provided an understanding
of the diﬀerent parts of the product. Furthermore, systematic design of industrial products by
Eskild Tjalve [5] was used to provide an understanding of the physical structure of the product
and how the parts were constrained in the three dimensional space.
6.1.1
Product Range Analysis
The Kenwood kitchen stand mixer consisted of a lot of parts. The main parts, however, were the
bowl, paddle, gearbox with planetary hub, motor, housing, opening/closing mechanism with

[51]. However, it still seemed that it contained the same type of parts, like the bowl, motor,
electronics. One major diﬀerence was that the gears are integrated into the planetary hub.

6.1.2
Systematic design analysis
The order in which the systematic design analysis was performed is the opposite of a regular
design process, since this was a revers engineering process.
Functional surfaces
In order to be able to generate new designs, one has to deﬁne what parts of the machine serve
a functional purpose and therefore have restrictions in terms of geometry and what can change
based on preferred design. The quantiﬁed and functional surfaces are therefore deﬁned. There
exist two types of functional surfaces: (i) External surfaces, which have an active function in
relation to surroundings. (ii) Internal surfaces, which have an active function in relation to
other elements of the product.[5]
The mixer contained external functional surfaces on the inside of the bowl, where food would
be mixed with the end of the paddle, which therefore also consisted of functional surfaces.
However, it was debatable whether these were internal or external surfaces, since the surfaces
have an active function in relation to each other in terms of how well they ﬁt together and
thereby how well they mix external ingredients. The bowl also consisted of external functional
surfaces around the top of the bowl, where the user would have to hold the bowl when moving
it. There was an opening at the front of the top part of the mixer, with an external functional
surface, where extra equipment can be inserted. The side of the speed button was also an
external functional surface, since it was used to rotate the speed button, changing the speed of
the machine. The top of the opening button consisted of an external functional surface, since
it was pressed to unlock and lift/open the upper part of the machine. The top of the blender
also consisted of external functional surfaces around the top, where it was opened. Lastly, the
handle on the blender was an external functional surface, which the user used when moving the
blender.
There were internal functional surfaces around the outer gears of the gearbox and the motor
and the rubber gear chain. There were mating surfaces between the bowl and the housing,
paddle and planetary hub, planetary hub and gearbox, housing and food processor, the parts
in the food processor (the blade, lid, and bowl), locking mechanism and housing, and housing
and table.
Banned areas
Another important aspect of the geometry was banned areas, which were areas in space that
must not be obstructed either due to structural conditions, functional conditions, or operational
conditions. [5]
Structural banned areas included the areas around the outer gears (in order for these to move
and convert their mechanical power). While functional areas included the area between the

functions (based on the current kitchen stand mixer). The wording for the functions followed
Stone and Wood’s function classes [19]. It was deemed unnessesary to translate the objectives

needs to be cleaned. The smoothness of the surface of the parts was assumed not to have a
signiﬁcant eﬀect on the cleaning, since all parts were made of either metal or plastic and thus
they have a relative small surface roughness. It was assumed that "easy to clean" dependent on
the geometry of the parts: Large roundings would make it easier to clean than corners. Also,
narrow openings or a combinations of narrow openings and long objects would make it very hard
to clean, since it would be hard to get a sponge or other objects through this area. Evaluat-

identify narrow areas, sharp corners, etc. This was too big a scope for this project and therefore
discarded. However, a simpliﬁed performance indicator of maintenance based on the cleaning

6.10
Result: Deﬁnition of mixer and performance indicators
In this section the basic structure of the Kenwood Kitchen Stand mixer was investigated through
methods from systematic design of industrial products [5] and mass customization [2]. The anal-
ysis showed that the product consists of many functional and forbidden areas in and around the
bowl, which suggested that this area would need to be tightly constrained to avoid unrealistic
designs. The main classes of the product was estimated to be paddle, housing, bowl, motor,
gearbox, locking mechanism, gear chain, and accessories. Furthermore, the overall objectives
and their sub-objectives were mapped in a tree structure. The objectives were based on the
market research from the previous section. It became apparent that some aspects of durabil-
ity, maintenance, and eﬀectiveness needed further research to be translated into sub-objectives.
Functional modelling was then used to translate the objectives into functions and specify which
parts are responsible for this. This technique was not very fruitful in some categories of objec-
tives (e.g. pricing and maintenance), which are not direct functions, which the product has to
perform. It was concluded that FM was not well suited method for this purpose. However, it
created an overview of which parts were responsible for the majority of the functions (is most
important in the model). These were chosen as the main parts, which should be modelled in
the 3D model. Furthermore, a couple of the other parts from the class diagram was also deemed
important to the model, since they were responsible for the main functions of the part.
The performance indicators of price, durability, eﬀectiveness, maintenance and design was spec-
iﬁed into quantiﬁable performance indicators, which could be added to the model/conﬁgurator.
Price was based on an estimation of the manufacturing of purchased and in-house products.
Only the direct cost which would be aﬀected by the design was considered. The ﬁnal cost was
therefore not an estimation of the actual price of the product for the consumer.
Durability was further speciﬁed through a fault tree and failure mode and eﬀects analysis. The
most critical fault modes were chosen to be translated into measurable performance indicators.
This was based on investigating how to ensure that the kitchen stand mixer does not move
during use. As well as ensuring that the motor can withstand the maximum force from the
ingredients during mixing.
Eﬀectiveness was speciﬁed as the volume of the bowl, since it became apparent that the other
objectives would not be feasible to include in the model.
Maintenance was speciﬁed based on the cleaning time of the parts in contact with food based
on their surface area.
Design was tricky to estimate, since preferred design can vary from person to person. A survey
was constructed, where participants had to rate diﬀerent similar design. This was unfortunately
not fruitful, due to a lack of participation. The designs will instead be varied in the model and
then investigated during the ﬁnal interaction with the customer at the end of the project.
Three diﬀerent designs were constructed in the 3D modelling software to uncover the Kenwood
kitchen stand mixer’s current diﬀerent designs.

7.1.2
Deﬁning the structure of the 3D model
The kitchen stand mixer consisted of many parts, which aﬀected each other if they change in
size. Some of the parts would therefore constrain the other’s. It therefore seemed, that there
were two approaches to this: (i) Focusing on the technical parts of the inside (gearbox and

ﬁt this. (ii) Or deﬁning the design/outside (housing, weight, size, design and maintenance) and
then generating the technical parts based on the space left. However, it also seemed possible
to do something in between, e.g. deﬁning the gearbox, then the housing, and then the bowl.

• Housing
• Bowl
• Motor
• Gearbox (gears)
• Paddle
• Buttons, cord, etc.
This order was based on what deﬁnes the diﬀerent performance indicators: Eﬀectiveness was
based on the size of the bowl, durability was based on the motor and the gear ratio, and the
paddle was part of the core functionality of the product.

Limitations of the model
After creating the model, it became apparent that the model had some limitations.
After
varying the dimensions of the part it became clear that some dimensions were sensitive to
change: The diameter of the bottom housing sketch determined where the bowl and paddle
was placed (center of diameter). Consequently, the combination of the diameter and the length
of the platform in the housing greatly aﬀected how much space there was for the bowl and the
paddle - if a large diameter was chosen, then the center point was moved closer to the vertical
part of the housing, leaving less room for the bowl and paddle (see illustration 55). The space
of the void might in this case not be used optimal, since there might be room for the bowl
and the paddle, if it was moved further away from the center of the arc of the bottom sketch.
However, this would add complexity to the model, since further inspection would be needed to
ensure that the bottom of the bowl was smaller than bottom housing diameter. The height of
the void was also a sensitive dimension, since it determines the height of the bowl, paddle, and
planetary hub.

7.3
Model Structure
The structure of the model in ModelCenter is described during this section. The model was
constructed with a focus on the maintainability and robustness to changes (e.g. if a later version
shouldn’t take price into account, but instead CO2 emission). The centerpiece of the model
was the 3D drawing (Creo ﬁle), since this generates the diﬀerent concepts. The performance
indicators would therefore be calculated based on values from the CAD tool.
Creo Parametric had multiple diﬀerent features, which was deemed useful during modelling
e.g. reference dimensions, and family tables. However, unfortunately, these features were not
transferable into ModelCenter (at leased not with the standard wrapper made by ModelCen-
ter). Only input parameters of the top level and driving dimensions were transferable into
ModelCenter. These were all based on common data types like arrays (no matrices), Boolean,
integers, double, String, etc.
Furthermore, it became apparent during introduction of the ﬁrst modulized part, the motor,
that ModelCenter did not handle modurilazation very well: It was necessary to create a sep-
arate script, which assigned the right values of each dimension of the module. Furthermore,
if the module contained features, which had to be suppressed/unsupressed, then this had to
be speciﬁed in the programming window of Creo by manually writing an if-statement associ-
ated with a Boolean variable, which then would control whether the feature was suppressed or
unsuppressed (further explanation in appendix A 7.1). Lastly, the modules could not be part
of the DoE analysis (further explained in section 8). It was therefore decided to discard the
option to modulate anything but the motor, since this had to be diﬀerentiated based on the
diﬀerent assortment of power (watt).
Conﬁgurators often consist of diﬀerent rules regarding what modules can and cannot be as-
sembled together. This was assumed to be part of the ModelCenter model. However, due to
the technical diﬃculties, only constraints in terms of changes in dimensions depending on the
other dimensions will be considered.

7.3.4
Limitations of the model
During the development, it became clear that there exists some limitations between the inte-
gration tool and the CAD software, which aﬀected the ﬁnal model: ModelCenter only receives
the dimensions from parts, which are active/unsuppressed in the model. This made it very
important, that all major dimensions were stored in the skeleton model, which were always ac-
tive. Features with dimensions, which were suppressed in some cases, e.g. the diﬀerent designs,
were stored as parameters as well. However, using parameters should be limited, since these
will have to be stored as both a parameter in the local part (e.g skeleton) and the assembly
in order for ModelCenter to access it as an input, which adds unnecessary complexity to the
model. Another downside of using the 3D model of the kitchen stand mixer to both produce
a visual representation of the parts AND volumes were that a circular reference was created
when the on/oﬀof the suction cups where based on the ﬁnal weight of the product, since the
weight depended on what parts were turned on/oﬀ, while the suction cups were turned on/oﬀ
dependent on the weight. The suction cups were therefore not physically turned on/oﬀin the
3D model. During testing it was troublesome to ﬁgure out why an error occurred in one of
the 3D models, since Creo only provided one general error message no matter what fails in the
model.
Even though multiple scripts had been created to make sure that the changes in the dimensions
of the model did not result in other parts of the model failing (or correlating), errors could still
occur in some scenarios. This was especially true for the roundings and shell thickness of the
parts, since it seemed impossible to determine when these had to be restricted, since it depended
on the overall geometry of the product.
Determining the size of the gears was very complex and the optimization of this did not ap-
pear to be the best solution, since certain patterns in terms of relative size between gears
would probably appear. A tailored optimization algorithm for this scenario would therefore be
preferable.
There was a trade oﬀin terms of how well the dimensions of the diﬀerent parts should be
restricted, since it is beneﬁcial if the majority of the designs created through analysis were
successful designs. However, this was ensured through restricting the design space based on
simpliﬁed rules. Making too many rules might therefore result in very similar designs, where
scaling would be the main diﬀerence.

8
Analysis
8.1
Intro
The purpose of this part was to determine the best design(s) of the kitchen stand mixer based on
the engineering aspects. The detail of the analysis was speciﬁed: (i) Should diﬀerent designs be
generated and a ﬁnal design be determined based on feedback from potential customer (based
on their view on price, design, etc.). (ii) Or should the potential customers provide feedback
to a ﬁnal design (which has been evaluated and chosen as the best design)?
If only objective parameters had been chosen, then approach no. 2 would be most beneﬁcial.
However, since one of the parameters involve design, it seems that approach 1 was most bene-
ﬁcial. Consequently, the analysis was be twofold: First, diﬀerent designs had to be generated
and the best had to be chosen, which could then be further evaluated by potential customers.
Two types of analysis needed to be performed in ModelCenter:
• DoE analysis’ (Design of experiments), determining which variables aﬀects the diﬀerent
design goals the most (e.g. cost, mass, etc.)
• Optimization analysis’ where the inﬂuential variables were varied and the design with the
lowest score was determined based on which designs scores lowest/highest in the diﬀerent
design goals
8.1.1
Gearbox optimization
Through initial analysis it became clear that the optimization of the sizes of the gears in the
gearbox could be optimized for almost all box sizes (since they can be scaled) and that the gears
could be optimized in a way, which would make the watt needed in the motor very small (less
than 10 watt). This was assumed to be due to the extensive sequence of gears in the gearbox or
a calculation error. It was therefore determined that the gear ratio wont be the deﬁning factor
of the size of the motor. The gearbox optimization was therefore not part of the analysis, since
this also severely slowed the analysis down. The durability/reliability performance indicators
was therefore not part of the analysis.
8.1.2
DoE
The 3D model and calculations of the diﬀerent parameters consisted of many dimensions and
variables, which all aﬀected the design. The design space for the machine was determined by
many variables. It was not obvious which variables had the largest aﬀect on the outcome, since
the variables were dependent on each other and had diﬀerent upper and lower limits. E.g.
the sizing of the bowl depended on the size of the void. It was therefore important to get an
overview of this during the initial phase of the analysis. Design of experiments (DoE) was used
for this purpose.This was achieved through varying the diﬀerent variables and observing how
this aﬀected the outcome of the diﬀerent parameters (weight, price, overall size, maintenance,
bowl size). The DoE algorithm would calculate the sensitivity of each variable (to what degree
the variable aﬀects the outcome). Diﬀerent algorithms existed in ModelCenter, which chooses
MSc project - Design Inoovation, DTU
93 of 118
==PAGE:95==
Camilla Strunge Nielsen
June 4, 2021
appropriate data sets during the DoE. It was therefore important to specify what the algorithm
needed to be able to handle:
• A large amount of variables
• Both integers and doubles as variables
• There will be scenarios where the model will throw an error/break
• The analysis should be time eﬃcient (should not take days)
It was assumed that the variables, which had a large eﬀect on the majority of the parameters,
were the variables deﬁning the outside of the housing: The lengths, heights, diameter of bottom,
and diﬀerence between size of top and bottom. Since the upper and lower bound of many of
the other variables were restricted based on these variables.
8.1.3
Optimization analysis
The most inﬂuential variables were chosen for further analysis in order to create optimized
designs based on the engineering aspects. This was based on using appropriate algorithms,
which chooses increasingly appropriate data, which was assumed to be a best ﬁt for the design.
It was therefore important to specify, what the algorithm needed to be able to handle:
• Multi-objective analysis (there are multiple parameters)
• Both integers and doubles as variables
• There will be scenarios where the model will throw an error/break
• Handle a moderate number of variables
8.1.4
Upper and lower bound of variables
Each variable would need to be deﬁned in terms of it’s upper and lower bound. The shells’
values were based on trial-and-error modelling on the 3D model (what upper and lower limit
of the shell would break the model?). The upper and lower bounds of the housing geometry

8.2.1
DoE
Some variables, which had to be used as inputs in the DoE were integers and not double (e.g.
motor no. and design no.). The only algorithm available for this scenario was Parameter Scan.
This was therefore initially chosen.
However, this algorithm works by creating all possible
combinations of the variables’ upper an lower limit. Having over 30 variables this resulted in
so many designs, that the program would not allow it to run. Even having half the amount
of variables would result in the analysis running for days. This algorithm was therefore not
suitable for this situation.
This poses a problem, since some of the most important variables are integers, like the design,
motor type, and materials. It was decided to do the same DoE of the diﬀerent designs (design
1-4) with the double variables. The algorithm used was latin hypercube sampling in order to
equally uncover all parts of the design space while still controlling the amount of runs (in order
for the analysis to be eﬃcient as well). ModelCenter had a bug (veriﬁed by the ModelCenter
support), where the Creo-ﬁles were not closed properly during analysis. Consequently, only
about 80 runs can be performed before Creo had too many ﬁles open. It was not possible to
closed these manually during the analysis in the taskManager. 3 DoE’s were performed per
design in order to minimize any false positives due to the low number of runs. The variables,
which consistently had a signiﬁcantly (by eye) larger sensitivity compared to the other variables
were chosen as variables worth using in the optimization analysis.
A list of the diﬀerent variables which were varied, their range, and the results can be found in
appendix C 8.1

8.3
Limitations
One great limitation was the discovery of the bug in the integration software used, where only
80 runs could be performed with each analysis. This limited the size of the design space, which
had been explored, and might create unintended biases. Furthermore, the integers could not
be integrated into the DoE analysis. Consequently, the sensitivity of the integers was based
on the optimization analysis, which used a diﬀerent algorithm, which is not intended for DoE.
The resulting sensitivity might therefore not be correct.
There might be situations where it did not matter to the potential customer e.g. whether the
mixer weights 1 or 2 kg, as long as it is less than 3 kg. This consideration had not been taken
into account during the selection of best designs.
Many of these designs were very similar (which was partially also the scoping of the project).
Some of the relative diﬀerences between engineering aspects were therefore not that great,
e.g. the value of maintenance was very similar. However, the relative diﬀerence between e.g.
price and weight was much larger due to the diﬀerences in material, shell, and overall size of
parts. The diﬀerence between the designs visually was therefore also not that great. It was
therefore interesting whether potential customers were going to ﬁnd a speciﬁc design preferable
and whether it was going to be based on the engineering aspects or the design.

9
Reﬂection on project and results
This section reﬂects on the diﬀerent learning objectives (LO) of the project.
9.1
LO1: Scoping of project
The project plan was not followed as strictly, as anticipated. One of the reasons being that
quantifying the diﬀerent objectives took more time than anticipated. Consequently, there had
been a disconnect between the initial analysis of the diﬀerent user need categories (price, dura-
bility, eﬀectiveness, maintenance, design, weight, size) and the chosen performance indicators:
Each segment had been thoroughly investigated, however, only a few of the objectives deﬁning
each category had been translated into quantiﬁable performance indicators, which could be
used in the model. Furthermore, constructing the model also took longer than anticipated due
to limitations and errors in ModelCenter during integration of the 3D model.
9.2
LO2: Deﬁnition of research area
The research area was based on two diﬀerent processes of automating the design space: Topo-
logical optimization and conﬁguration. As well as the use of function modelling to translate
needs into functions. The analysis of these areas, however, could have been more in depth.
This is possibly one of the reason why the use of FM did not provide very good results.
9.3
LO3: Choice of theories and methods
Diﬀerent systematic concept development and mass customization methods have been used
during the initial phase of the project. Most methods have proven very beneﬁcial. However,
only the parts view of the PVM proved beneﬁcial during the project, the engineering- and
customer view was mapped in a house of quality, instead. This seemed to provide a good
overall understanding of the product. However, it would also have been beneﬁcial to do the
Parts Deployment of the four-matrix framework of QFD [24] in order to map the relation
between engineering aspects and parts. Function modelling provided a good overview of which
parts were most important to include in the model based on which products had the greatest
number of functions. However, some functions were more important than others, which was
lost during the analysis. Furthermore, the result of the class diagram and the FM in terms of
which parts should be modelled was very similar. One of the two methods could therefore have
been eliminated. The FM analysis, furthermore, was not beneﬁcial during quantiﬁcation of the
objectives. A structured method of quantifying the diﬀerent functions/objectives were missing,
consequently, methods from diﬀerent areas of engineering was used. This broadened the project
to a degree which might not be beneﬁcial. Methods of quantifying design was especially vague.
This should be better deﬁned during a similar project. The method of resilient modelling used
during the modelling of the CAD model did not ﬁt very well with top-down design. The method
of constraining the diﬀerent parts in a robust, but ﬂexible way was generally missing (especially
in terms of dimensioning). The structure of the model in ModelCenter was not based on any
methods or literature, there might therefore be a more optimal way of doing this, which the
author had not found. The choice of software gradually proved not to be beneﬁcial for this

beginning of the project. This search was mainly focused on choosing the right CAD software.

LO4 + NO5: Deﬁnition of objectives, needs, and values
The deﬁnition of the engineering aspects were very vague due to the lack of data regarding the

on their product. Furthermore, a better and more structured method of translating the user
needs into measurable objectives would be needed, since the use of function modelling was not
fruitfully for all categories. These categories instead had to be translated into performance

e.g. the legal department is not accounted for.
The performance indicator are only based on dimensions, volume, and surface area from the

Deﬁning the product through systematic design methods and customization methods proved
very beneﬁcial, since it provided an overview of the product. However, the architecture model
was not deemed appropriate since many of the performance indicators were not based on op-
timization of one part but of multiple parts (e.g. pricing and cleaning time). Furthermore, it
was decided to focus on optimizing rather than conﬁguration of the product due to limitations
in the chosen software. The architecture model therefore became unnecessary.
9.6
LO7: Flexibility of model
Keeping the model ﬂexible, but within a feasible design space, was ensured through resilient
modelling and top-down design of the 3D model and the scripts in ModelCenter. During the
project, it became clear that the scripts were not a very maintainable solution. Furthermore,
the design space might also have become too tightly constrained, which was a point often raised
during the interview with engineers.
It became clear during analysis that the way the 3D model was constructed was not optimal
in terms of avoiding failed runs/versions. In hindsight it became clear that it would have been
easier to assure this if the model had been constructed with a bottom-up approach, where the
inner parts (e.g. motor and gearbox) deﬁnes the parts on the outside (e.g. housing). It should
have been better deﬁned what parts needed to vary greatly in design and which parts would
be sensitive to change.

LO8: Creation of model
The 3D model was hard to manage in ModelCenter even though it contains very few parts.
A product with many parts are therefore not advisable. Another solution to this problem,
however, might also be to make a hierarchical structure, dividing the concept into multiple

Furthermore, it became clear from the interviews with design engineers, that this type of tool
would only be valuable in businesses, where the product do not change too much each year due

Multiple technical diﬃculties had been encountered during this project, the model therefore
only contains one conﬁgurable part, the motor. The optimization algorithms were also not
suitable for this project, partially due to the vague deﬁnition of requirements for the software.
These requirements are therefore created in hindsight.
9.7.1
Software
During this thesis it became clear that although ModelCenter provided many of the necessary
capabilities, some aspects were not optimal. This section therefore deﬁnes what an integration
tool would need to be capable of in order to create a similar optimization/conﬁguration tool.
The integration software needs to be capable of:
• Integrating 3D models with the possibility of importing volumes and surface areas of all
parts and dimensions from all parts in the model (also dimensions in features which are
turned oﬀ)
• Integrating programming language suitable for advanced calculations (e.g.
MatLab,
Python, Java)
• Integrating lists of initially chosen variables and ﬁnal variables (e.g. Excel sheets)
• The possibility of transmitting data between 3D model, scripts, and lists both as doubles,
integeres, Strings, booleans, arrays (one or two dimensions)
• Performing a DoE and optimization analysis with:
– Multiple variables as both doubles and integers which can be speciﬁed to be lower
than 1
– Multiple outputs
– Algorithm, which can deal with a model, which might fail/break/contain errors in
some instances/runs.

9.8
LO9: User interview
From the user interviews it seems that the 3D model produced by the conﬁgurator was useful as
a boundary object communicating diﬀerent possible designs and the consequences in terms of
e.g. price. One important take away was that the potential customers in the online survey in the
market research in section 5 did not ﬁnd weight to be important. However, during interaction
with other potential customers with the concepts and weight speciﬁcation, this suddenly seemed
to matter. However, this might also be due to a bias in terms of who was asked. The amount of
participants in user interviews during the project has generally been very small. It is therefore
not assured that the results of these interviews reﬂect the whole customer segment.
9.9
L11: Use of project in design process
The method was developed in order to automate the initial concept development of designs,
which had to be incrementally changed. It was assumed during the beginning of the project,
that it would not be beneﬁcial to use this type of tool during novel design development, since
the requirements, upper and lower limits of dimensions, and geometries would be too vague and
need to be too ﬂexible, which would be hard to achieve with this type of model. During the
development of the conﬁgurator, where the 3D model had to be relatively tightly constrained
to avoid errors, this point still seemed reasonable. However, this would have to be tested in
order to know for certain. It also seemed from the interviews with design engineers, that a
conﬁgurator, which can suggest more novel designs would be more beneﬁcial.
The basic structure of the design process can be seen on the left side of illustration 70 and on the
right is the new design process with this tool implemented. The illustration does not suggest

much smaller, since the creation of new designs would be automated. However, the designer
would still have to look at the data and decide which designs are most interesting and do

10
Final conclusion
The conﬁgurator was created in ModelCenter from Phoenix Integration based on diﬀerent cal-
culations in scripts and excel sheets and a ﬂexible 3D model assembly made in Creo Parametric.
This was constructed based on market research, and a systematic approach to the analysis of
the kitchen stand mixer and it’s parts. The model could estimate diﬀerent performance in-
dicators: Production cost, minimum amount of watt of motor, weight, size of bowl, cleaning
time, weight, and overall size. These aspects seemed to be important factors for the user based
on potential customer interviews suggesting that eﬀectiveness, durability, maintenance, price,
and design were important factors to potential customers. A DoE and optimization analysis
could be performed, which resulted in new designs of the mixer with indications of their perfor-
mance indicator score. The concepts could then be used during user interviews. The aim of the
project was therefore researched. However, the model was more limited than what was initially
scoped: The model only contained one conﬁgurable part (the motor) and the assortment of
diﬀerent parts/modules were very limited due to the limitations of the integration software
chosen. Furthermore, the model needed time to do the analysis and can therefore not create
concepts right away during a user interaction. Three design engineers were asked to provide
their insight regarding whether this model could be useful in their work. They all agreed that
diﬀerent aspects could be interesting, especially if the model was more ﬂexible. Further research
of a more ﬂexible version of the model therefore seems worthwhile. The motivation behind the
project was to minimize the time spend on concept development and thereby making it more
eﬃcient for the engineer/designer. It is uncertain whether this would be achieved with this
type of tool, since the development time of the tool and maintenance is uncertain.
