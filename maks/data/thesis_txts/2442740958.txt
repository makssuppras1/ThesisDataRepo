Abstract 
Discovery of new antibiotics against multi-drug resistant gram-negative bacteria is important for 
prospectively curing life-threatening infections. In this project, actinobacterial strain NBC00162, 
which had shown activity against a multi-drug resistant gram-negative bacterium Acinetobacter 
baumannii, was investigated. The active compound was isolated with a two-step bioassay-guided 
high-pressure liquid chromatogram separation. The isolate was analyzed using mass spectrometry 
and nuclear magnetic resonance and identified as Pyracrimycin A, which was first discovered in 
1971. No biosynthetic gene cluster have been connected to the production of the active compound.  
The genome of NBC00162 was sequenced and analyzed using AntiSmash software. Three 
biosynthetic gene cluster candidates which could be responsible for the synthesis of the bioactive 
compound were chosen for investigation.  One biosynthetic gene cluster was chosen as proof-of-
concept, because of its smaller size. The biosynthetic gene cluster for proof-of concept and one of 
the candidates were cloned into a backbone vector pXj157.  The candidate was conjugated into 
several heterologous host. The proof-of-concept biosynthetic gene cluster was transferred using 
conjugation to several heterologous expression hosts, and integration of the biosynthetic gene 
cluster was confirmed using PCR in Streptomyces griseofuscus DSM40191 and Streptomyces 
coelicolor M512.

Introduction 
Antibiotics have and has historically had a huge impact on public health, some even claim that it is 
the most important contribution of medical science to date [1]. During the 19th century, a lot of 
products from secondary metabolites were discovered with different bioactivities, for instance, 
antibiotic activity. It is believed to be a major influence for the increased average life span of 
Americans from 47years to 74-80years [2]. The discoveries have contributed greatly to the 
treatment of a broad range of common diseases caused by bacteria. The 1950’s and 1960’s are often 
described as the golden ages of antibiotic discovery due to the massive number of new antibiotic 
drugs discovered during that period [1]. Unfortunately, over the last couple of decades a dramatic 
decrease in the discovery of new antibiotics has been observed. Simultaneously, the number of 
multi-drug-resistant (MDR) bacteria, that have evolved, has increased [3],[2]. This combination 
results in an urgent demand for the development of new antibiotics targeting MDR bacteria. This 
project has been focused on discovering antibiotics targeting MDR gram-negative bacteria. MDR 
gram-negative bacteria caused infections are especially difficult to treat. Common broad-spectrum 
antibiotics are not able to accumulate in gram-negative bacteria due to the outer membrane, which 
is not present in gram-positive bacteria and, hence, are not active against gram-negative bacteria 
[4]. For this reason, new antibiotics against gram-negative pathogens are in high demand.  
 
Clinical antibiotics are usually developed from scaffolds derived from natural compounds produced 
in organisms as secondary metabolites. In 2011 more than 22.000 microbial bioactive compounds 
were known, and that number is constantly increasing. 45% of the antibiotic compounds have been 
found in Actinomycetes species, which are bacteria known to produce many secondary metabolites 
[5]. It was previously believed that almost all the secondary metabolites had been characterized. 
However, genomic sequencing has revealed that many of the potential secondary metabolites have 
yet to be characterized. A lot of biosynthetic gene clusters (BGC), which were predicted are 
probably not expressed in the cells and hence there is a great potential in uninvestigated BGCs 
[6],[7]. Knowledge about BGCs and how these kinds of compounds are synthesized is important for 
discovering new compounds.    
 
This project builds on prior work performed in the group New Bioactive Compounds. Soil samples 
were collected from different locations primarily in Denmark. Different Actinomycetales have been 
grown from the samples and stored in a collection. Sample, that was used for isolation of 
NBC000162 was collected on 24th of August 2016 in Hørsholm. NBC000162 is the main object of

To identify the BGC responsible for the biosynthetic pathway of the molecule, at first the genome 
of strain of interest is sequenced. The genomic sequence is analyzed in order to determine whether 
the organism is known and its respective phylogenetic position. Furthermore, the genomic sequence 
is analyzed with AntiSmash software [9], which identifies possible BGCs for secondary 
metabolites. Target BGCs are generally identified based on the knowledge of secondary metabolites 
and chemical properties of the target compound. BGCs, which are matching these properties are 
considered as candidates for further analysis. The candidates can be cloned into expression 
vector(s) and subsequently transferred to a heterologous expression host in order to determine 
whether any of them is responsible for the activity. If the heterologous expression host gains the 
activity, which was previously seen in NBC00162, and it can be verified to come from the same 
molecule, with the use of analytical tools, it can be concluded that the BGC lies within the borders 
of the cloned fragment. If no activity is observed, it can be because the wrong candidate cluster was 
chosen for cloning. However, there is also the risk that, the chosen BGC is not expressed under 
selected conditions. In the case, when no new compounds are produced by the heterologous host, 
carrying cloned BGC, different growth conditions are verified. In addition, other host organisms 
can be used [7], [10],[11].  
 
Besides cloning, clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) based systems 
can be used to induce knockout of biosynthetic genes in the BGC with use of the CRISPR 
associated protein 9 (Cas9) or it can be used for deletion of BGC. If NBC00162 loses its bioactivity 
and the active peak is removed, after use of CRISPR-Cas9 tools, it indicates that the BGC is 
responsible for the biosynthetic pathway of the active compound [12]. To verify this, other types of 
genetic engineering of the BGC should be performed. Cas9 has a high risk of causing off-target 
effects making the confirmation of activity loss because of mutations in the target BGC less certain. 
Another risk, is that the organism loses the activity for other reasons than mutations in the target 
BGC [13],[14]. The workflow of identifying the BGC responsible for the bioactivity observed in 
NBC00162 is illustrated in Figure 3.

Background 
During the project theory of different subjects have been applied and the most important subjects 
will be described in this section. The main subjects have been: HPLC, MS, NMR, Secondary 
metabolites, Gene cloning and CRISPR-Cas9. As described in the introduction HPLC, MS and 
NMR have been used to identify the active compound. These subjects will be described in the first 
part of the background. Knowledge of secondary metabolites, gene cloning with CRISPR-cas9 have 
been used to locate secondary metabolites, which could be responsible for the biosynthetic pathway 
of the active molecule and these subjects will be described in the second part of the background 
section. 
Identification of active molecules 
In this section, the basic principle of HPLC is described, the preparation of the sample for analysis 
and the development of the methods for separation of a complex sample with unidentified 
compounds. Furthermore, the basic principles of MS are described and how the data can be used to 
identify the compound. Lastly, the NMR techniques used for elucidating the structure of the 
molecule are presented.  
Sample separation  
During this project, the separation was performed using HPLC. In general, chromatography is a 
separation system with two phases, a stationary phase and a mobile phase see Figure 4. In liquid 
chromatography (LC) the mobile phase is liquid and usually consists of a water phase and an 
organic phase. In this project, the water phase consisted of buffer systems with different pH values 
and the organic phases was either methanol (MeOH) or acetonitrile (ACN). The stationary phase is 
a column most commonly with solid silica beads with different surfaces packed in the column. 
Another method for separation is gas chromatography (GC) which has a gaseous mobile phase and 
a stationary phase which is either liquid or solid. GC was not used during this project, therefore it is 
not described further, for an introduction, please refer to reference [15].

Samples can be separated based on different properties using a HPLC system. The compounds can 
be separated by size, adsorption, ion-exchange, partition or affinity see Figure 5. The method of 
choice typically depends on the sample. In this project, the main property, used for separating the 
sample, was an adsorption. How the sample is separated is highly dependent on the surface of the 
column. If the compounds are separated by size, the silica is designed with pores, which retain 
small molecules longer than large molecules. For adsorption the molecules are separated based on 
their chemical interactions for instance compounds can be separated based on their polarity. The 
surface of the beads is retaining compounds which interacts with the surface. The surface can be 
non-polar, polar or have functional groups which interact with compounds. Ion-exchange happens 
when the surface contains ions, if ions are present in the sample the ions might interact causing 
different retention times [15].

Method development 
In this project the structure and nature of the compound of interest is unknown. This poses a 
challenge for the separation method development. In order to find the correct separation technique, 
different methods have been developed for initial characterization of the compound’s chemical 
properties. One such method is explorative solid-phase extraction, which can give fast information 
about the compound from a relatively small extraction, and is described further in reference [17]. In 
this case common characteristics for antibiotics active against gram-negative bacteria have been 
analyzed by Richter et al. in 2017 [18] giving the necessary information about the compound. 
Gram-negative bacteria have a second outer membrane. The outer membrane of gram-negative 
bacteria increases low permeability of the membrane, which means a lot of molecules can not 
accumulate in the organisms. Furthermore, efflux pumps remove a broad range of antibiotics when 
they are accumulated in the cell. Properties resulting in good accumulation was investigated based 
on known antibiotics active against gram-negative bacteria. What was noted in the analysis was that

the molecules, likely to accumulate in the gram-negative bacteria, were: (1) Small molecules (below 
600Da) (2) Molecules containing a positive charge often amines and preferably primary amines (3) 
Molecules with amphiphilic and rigid regions (4) Molecules with low globularity [4], [18]. These 
common features and some of the features noticed during the first dereplication, are used as 
guidelines for the method development. Prior to the beginning of this project, the sample was run 
on a C18 column, with methanol as a mobile phase and a buffer of pH 6.5. The eluent was collected 
each minute and the active sample was dereplicated by MEDINA. The report based on this analysis 
can be found in Appendix A. 
 
The choice of column has a great influence on the separation and knowledge about the analyte 
should be used to choose the column. Besides the column, other factors might influence the binding 
of the molecules, retention time of the molecules and how well the sample is separated. The choice 
of buffer, its pH, temperature, organic solvent and mixture of buffer and organic solvent have an 
impact on the separation as well. A decision tree can be used to choose the optimal column and 
method. In Figure 6 a decision tree is presented, which first differentiates the target molecule based 
on size. Large molecules with a mass above 2000 Da can be divided into molecules which dissolve 
in organic solvent and aqueous solvent. Large molecules which dissolve in organic solvent can be 
separated based on size. Large molecules which dissolve in aqueous solvent can be divided based 
on size, charge or hydrophobicity. Molecules with a mass below 2000 Da are also differentiated 
into groups based on whether the molecule is dissolved in organic or aqueous solvent. If the analyte 
is dissolvable in aqueous solution it is divided into ionic analytes and nonionic analytes. Analytes 
which are dissolvable in organic solvent can be differentiated based on solubility in different 
organic solvents. Based on these differentiations columns and methods are suggested in the figure 
[15]. In this project separation of small molecules is most relevant as one of the characteristics of 
antibiotics against gram-negative bacteria are sizes below 600 Da.

Sample preparation 
In this project, the analyte or target compound was the active compound produced by NBC00162. 
Sample preparation for chromatography is important not only in order to get a good chromatogram, 
but also to retain the system. The first step in isolating the molecule is extracting the molecule from 
the media, in which the cells were grown. In order to do this an extraction is performed. The 
extraction from bacterial cultures can be done in two ways. In the first case, the cells can be 
==PAGE:19==
Julie Bang Nielsen  
Master Thesis 
July - December 2018

19 
cultivated in liquid media and a liquid-liquid extraction is performed. In a liquid-liquid extraction it 
is important that the liquid used is immiscible in water, as water is the solvent for liquid media. 
Because the two solvents are immiscible it is possible to mix them and end up with a 2-phase 
system one with the water-soluble compounds and one with the compounds soluble in the chosen 
solvent. Another type of extraction, which was used in this project, is an extraction from solid agar 
plates. In this case, cells are grown until they reach the stationary phase. Typically, secondary 
metabolites, produced by Actinobacteria, are accumulated in the stationary growth phase of the 
strain. Therefore we assumed that it holds true in the case of NBC00162 [14]. Then growth media, 
collected from plates, is chopped into small pieces in order to give a large surface area extracted 
with organic solvent. If different solvents are used, a broad range of molecules can be extracted.  
 
The crude extract, prepared in this way, has to be concentrated and purified before its separation on 
the HPLC column. The solvent is removed by evaporation and the sample is dissolved in a smaller 
volume. It is important that the final solvent matches the initial solvent in the HPLC method. For 
instance, if the HPLC method has an initial mobile phase with 10% water and 90% MeOH the 
sample solvent should be as close as possible. Of course, the analyte also has to be dissolvable in 
the solvent. Furthermore, residuals in the sample should be removed to avoid clogging the system 
[15].  Fraction collection of the eluent from chromatography result in a less complex sample with 
the active compound or isolation of the bioactive compound.   
MS 
LC-diode array detector-MS (LC-DAD-MS) system was used in this project. A LC-DAD-MS 
system is a liquid chromatography separation with MS and DAD as detectors. In general, the mass 
spectrum shows the number of ions detected for each mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). Two types of 
analysis can be performed with an MS: (1) is a MS scan (2) MS/MS scan. The MS scan gives 
information regarding the molecular ions and some easily fragmented fragments. The MS/MS 
results in the fragmentation pattern of each ion from MS [15], [19]. The MS is divided into different 
parts. For instance, the ionization of the sample when it comes from liquid which is most commonly 
performed with an electrospray ionization (ESI) or a matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization 
(MALDI) method. The advantages of these two methods are that both are soft ionizers, which 
means the compounds less likely to fragmentate during ionization. Fragmentation should be 
avoided for the first analysis and a different approach is used such as collision induced dissociation 
(CID) for the MS/MS scan where fragmentation is important to obtain the fragmentation pattern 
[20]. Different strategies for gaining the mass are available. One of them is a quadrupole system 
==PAGE:20==
Julie Bang Nielsen  
Master Thesis 
July - December 2018

which consists of four metal rods. Voltage is applied to the rods, one of which is constant and the 
other with oscillating radio frequency. The quadrupole is used as a filter for only obtaining a certain 
mass range of ions for higher quality. The frequency of the ion in the electric field can be used to 
measure the mass in a detector. In this project an orbitrap quadrupole fusion spectrometer was used. 
For more information, please refer to [19]. Another popular method, used to obtain the mass of 
ions, is the time-of-flight (TOF) method, which separates ions based on the time the ion uses to 
travel the flight tube given the same amount of energy [15], [19], [21].  
To detect a high-quality mass spectrum, it is necessary to choose the mass range of interest. The rest 
of the ions are filtered and not detected in the spectrum. In this project the focus was small 
molecules and the mass range was primarily molecules below 1000 Da. Furthermore, it is possible 
to detect either positively charged ions or negatively charged ions depending on the settings of the 
instrument.             
 
Dereplication of activity guided fractionation was performed to get information about the masses 
and the molecular formula of the compounds found in the sample. From a precise mass the 
molecule formula can be suggested, and isotope patterns can help reveal information about the 
molecule formula. The isotope pattern is a pattern of peaks with different masses from the same 
main structure. The difference comes from the isotopes of the different atoms. The intensity of the 
isotope pattern follows the percentage of isotopes found in nature. For instance, if chloride is 
present in the molecule a +2 peak with an intensity of 1/3 of the molecular ion should be present 
[22]. This information was used to check the databases, containing molecules, that were isolated as 
secondary metabolites. In this project the databases used was; Antibase wiley 2017 [23], 
StreptomeDB [24] and Dictionary of natural products (DNP). If the mass and formula match a 
known molecule, different other parameters are considered, such as the UV-VIS absorbance and 
biological activity of the sample. Besides the mass and molecular formula, one can gain information 
from the fragmentation pattern of the molecule. The fragmentation pattern gives information about 
the structure and in this case, it can be used for comparing with literature of known compounds. If it 
matches a known compound, while also the rest of the parameters fit, there is a great chance that the 
compounds are the identical. For the structure elucidation of the compound NMR spectroscopy is 
used for analyzing the sample.

NMR 
NMR spectroscopy is one of the most versatile analytical techniques, which provides information 
about a molecule’s structure and dynamics. For evaluation of whether a compound is known, or 
novel, structural information is important for verification even though dereplication using a wide 
variety (here mainly MS) have provided important information and often enough to avoid isolation 
of known compounds. Different types of NMR experiments can be performed to gain information 
about the compound. 1D NMR are commonly performed for 1H and 13C. From 1H-NMR the 
chemical shifts of the protons can be gained and provide information about the chemical 
surroundings of the proton and the integral can give an idea of the number of equivalent protons 
with a given chemical shift. The integral is most useful in cases, when the formula is known prior to 
the analysis with the help of HR-MS data. Spin-spin couplings between protons can be found in 
order to verify which protons are coupled. The multiplicity of the peaks, resulting from spin-spin 
couplings can give an idea of the number of coupled protons [22]. One of the disadvantages of 1D 
1H-NMR is that the peaks can be overlapping and hard to distinguish, especially if the J-coupling is 
equal between different protons in this case 2D NMR can help the assignment of peaks. 13C-NMR 
can give information about number of different Carbon atoms in the structure, the chemical shift. 
Disortionless enhancement by polarization transfer or (DEPT) is experiments which allows to 
determine whether the Carbon is tertiary, secondary or primary, one method DEPT-135 gives 
negative signals for CH2 and positive signals for CH/CH3. NMR spectra can also be acquired as 
multidimensional for example 2D NMR where two axes are found, usually the axes are the 
chemical shifts. 2D NMR is divided into homonuclear NMR and heteronuclear NMR[25]. 
Homonuclear NMR is for instance 1H, 1H NMR. Different methods are used such as Correlation 
spectroscopy (COSY) [26]or double quantum filtered (DQF) COSY which is methods used to find 
correlations between neighboring protons. It can be used for assigning coupled peaks and the fine 
structure can give the coupling constants J, which is helpful if overlapping peaks are found in the 
1D 1H NMR. Total correlation spectroscopy (TOCSY) is a method that may be used to find 
couplings between all protons in a spin-system. A spin system is a system of coupled proton. Both 
COSY and TOCSY are used to determine couplings through bonds whereas Nuclear Overhauser 
effect spectroscopy (NOESY) is used for finding correlations for protons close in space[25]. 
Heteronuclear methods can be used for finding correlations between for instance 1H and 13C 
(different heteronuclear correlations can be found too). Heteronuclear single quantum correlation 
spectroscopy (HSQC) can give information about one bond correlations this may be acquired in an 
edited version with respect to CH/CH3 being one phase and CH2 the opposite phase[27].

Heteronuclear multiple bond correlation (HMBC) is also a 2D heteronuclear experiment that can be

Identification of BGC 
Identification of the BGC, responsible for the active compound, firstly requires a high-quality 
genomic DNA sequence. For this project two types of sequencing were performed. At first, PacBio 
sequencing technique was applied. PacBio method relies on the single molecule real time (SMRT) 
sequencing [29]. In addition, in-house Illumina MiSeq dye sequencing was used, in order to obtain 
the highest possible quality of genomic DNA sequence [29]. When the genomic sequence is 
assembled it can be analyzed with the help of different bioinformatic tools. In this project, 
AntiSmash was used to identify potential secondary metabolites BGCs [9]. Please, refer to the next 
section for introduction to secondary metabolites for more information. AntiSmash tool identifies 
BGCs and their respective types [9]. Furthermore, the AntiSmash database is used to predict the 
type of structure, synthesized from BGC and the length of the BGC of interest. At last, it also 
suggests the role of the genes, present in the cluster. This information combined with the 
information, received regarding the chemical nature of active compound makes it possible to 
identify target clusters for cloning and CRISPR-Cas9 induced knockout (Figure 7). CRISPR-Cas9 
can be used to introduce double strand breaks (DSB) in specific sites of the genome, which can 
result in mutations. For more information, please refer to the section regarding CRISPR-Cas9-based 
engineering [12].  
 
BGCs are large clusters which can be hard to clone because, typically clusters or their fragments are 
too big to be amplified using PCR. The solution to this problem is to use gel for isolation of 
genomic DNA to avoid the DNA shearing. After that, CRISPR-Cas9 protein, assembled with 
respective sgRNA, can be used to restrict the target BGCs. Gibson assembly can be used to 
incorporate the fragment into a vector plasmid. The new plasmid is transformed into an expression 
host which typically has simplified biosynthetic background. The compound can be isolated and 
investigated from the expression host [11].

Secondary metabolites 
Most known antibiotics belong to secondary metabolites of bacteria and fungi. Actinomycetes are 
known to produce a wide range of secondary metabolites. 76% of the scaffolds used for clinically 
used antibiotics originate from secondary metabolites of Actinomycetes [10]. Secondary 
metabolites are in general described as non-essential metabolites as they are not absolutely required 
for bacterial growth. Primary metabolites, on the other hand, are essential and crucial for the growth 
of the organism. Primary metabolites are largely similar over wide ranges of organisms. Secondary 
metabolites vary from strain to strain and result in a wide range of different chemical structures 
with a plethora of different activities. One of them, is the antibiotic effect, other activities include, 
but are not limited to: pheromones, pigments and agents for symbiosis. Secondary metabolites are 
used widely in the pharmaceutical industry, e.g. as anticancer agents and immunosuppressants [14].      
  Introduction of main classes of natural compounds that belong to secondary metabolites 
Secondary metabolic pathways are typically derived from the primary metabolic pathways, 
however they tend to branch off the usual pathways. The primary metabolic pathways provide a lot 
of different building blocks for macromolecules which are important for the cell’s growth and

secondary metabolites. From the glycolysis pathway the shikimate pathway is synthesized into

Most secondary metabolites are in the size range below 1500 Da, except of lanthipeptides, which 
are in the size range 3000-4000 Da [14]. The building blocks for lanthipeptides are amino acids and 
their structures often contain thioether bridges. A lot of lanthipeptides are known to be active 
against gram-positive bacteria but are too large to accumulate into gram-negative bacteria cells. The 
lanthipeptides belong to the family of polycyclic peptides which belong to the large class of 
ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified peptides [31]. 
  
Polyketides are different, as long as they are not necessarily reduced, and a wide range of 
modifications can happen to the chain with the help of additional tailoring enzymes. Overall, it 
results in very diverse group of compounds. Polyketides are often very rich in Oxygen. The 
proteins, responsible for synthesizing polyketides, are called polyketide synthases (PKS). The group 
can be divided into type 1 PKS (t1pks), type 2 PKS (t2pks) and type 3 PKS (t3pks); all three types 
can be found in genomes of Streptomyces species [7], [30]. For more information about PKSs and 
their different types, please refer to [30], [32], [33].

Genetic manipulation and cloning 
There are different ways of getting knowledge about the BGC responsible for the biosynthesis of 
the active compound of interest, two of which are considered in this section. One is using a 
CRISPR-Cas9 plasmid system which is designed to manipulate actinomycetes. The system can be 
designed to induce DSB in a gene, coding for key structural enzyme, in the target BGC. The other 
method is cloning the BGC into a vector and transferring it to a heterologous expression host.  
CRISPR based approach 
The CRISPR-Cas9 system was considered as a method of choice, because it is a relatively simple 
approach and in principal it can be used to target several BGC simultaneously. The system consists 
of a plasmid with gene, coding for the Cas9 protein, and the main structure for the single-guide 
RNA (sgRNA) which is necessary for the protein to recognize a restriction site. Cas9 and sgRNA 
are joined in a complex for inducing DSB at specific sites. Compared to other methods such as 
zink-fingers and transcription activator-like effector nuclease (TALEN), CRISPR is a simpler and 
faster approach [35]. Apramycin resistance gene aac3(IV) is used as a selection for the plasmid. The 
plasmid is designed with restriction sites which makes it possible to insert a protospacer sequence. 
The protospacer sequence is usually 20 base pairs (bp) long and matches a sequence in the target 
gene. The protospacer sequence has to be next a protospacer adjacent motif (PAM). The PAM 
sequence is “NGG”. The Cas9 protein induces DSB three bases upstream to the  from PAM [36]. 
The sgRNA is further combined assembled with the protein and is used as a guide for performing 
the DSB in specific location. The protospacer is inserted by designing primers for the sgRNA 
sequence, one of the primers contains a so called “tail” with the protospacer sequence. When the 
fragment with the additional 20bp sequence is amplified, it is introduced into the plasmid (Figure 
9)[37]. When the DSB is induced in the cell, different repair mechanisms can be recruited by the 
cell for its repair. Based on what kind of mutation is required in the experiment, different plasmid 
systems can be used. Three repair mechanisms are generally possible: (1) homologous 
recombination (HR), (2) non-homologous end-joining (NHEJ), (3) imperfect NHEJ, which has been 
observed to occur in some Streptomyces [12], [38].

Cloning based approach 
Cloning of BGC into a heterologous expression host can help identifying the gene cluster 
responsible for the biosynthetic pathway resulting in the active compound. Currently, cloning of 
large fragments from streptomycetes genomes is considered to be a challenge. Therefore, different 
approaches are being developed to solve it. The most common approach is amplifying the region 
with PCR, which is typically limited to fragments below 10 kbp in size [11]. In addition, high GC 
content of Streptomyces genomes, contributes to the hurdles of establishing proper amplification 
protocol. As long as all of the BGCs in this project are larger in size, a different approach is 
required. Another popular cloning method is construction of large insert bacterial artificial 
chromosome (BAC) libraries. This method relies on random restriction of genomic DNA, thus 
creating different fragments, that are subsequently cloned into a vector. This approach is considered

To avoid some of these challenges, Cas9-assisted targeting of chromosome segments (CATCH) 
approach was developed from previously described method [11], [45]. Because of the large size of 
the fragments, that are cloned, the genomic DNA, that is used in this approach, has to be of high 
quality. Therefore, in order to avoid disruption of the genomic DNA, it is captured in agarose gel. 
Cas9 protein and in vitro transcripted sgRNA pairs are used to perform the cleavage of the genomic 
DNA in order to get the target fragment, as described in the previous section [46]. When the 
fragment is obtained, the agarose gel is removed and a Gibson assembly is performed with a 
previously prepared vector plasmid [47]. Gibson assembly is performed with two fragments or 
more if needed with overlapping regions. A 5’ exonuclease digest the DNA strand resulting in a 
single strand region. The two regions overlapping can be matched and polymerase extension fills 
the gaps. The overlaps are sealed with Taq ligation from E. coli (Figure 12) [48].

the size of the insert in order to verify the absence of large rearrangements in the cloned region.

BGC 1 and 5 were successfully cloned into pXJ157 vector 
In this section, results regarding cloning of potential BGC candidates for the biosynthetic pathway 
of pyracrimycin A will be described and discussed. No known BGC, that is coding for the 
biosynthetic pathway of pyracrymycin A, was found in the literature search. Therefore, 
identification of the BGC responsible for pyracrimycin A biosythesis was the aim of this part of the 
project. The results from the AntiSmash software analysis and process of choosing BGC candidates 
will be further presented and discussed. Detailed procedures, that were the part of cloning, such as 
preparation of sgRNAs and amplification and purification of the backbone DNA for the cloning 
will be presented. Furthermore, the results from cloning of each of the BGC candidates will be 
presented and discussed. 
3 BGC candidates were identified using AntiSmash software 
Cluster 5, 7 and 13 were all considered as BGC candidates, possibly responsible for the formation 
of the active compound. The primary choice of BGCs was based on the compounds, described in 
the MEDINA dereplication report. The work of cloning BGCs was performed sidelong with the 
identification of the active compound. Hence, all the details regarding the nature and properties of 
the compound were not available in the beginning of the BGC search. All of the compounds which 
were detected as “unknown” in MEDINA report, were small and contained Nitrogen atoms. One of 
them was furthermore suggested to contain a Sulphur atom. The summary of results from analyzing

Prior designing the CRISPR-Cas9 plasmid for knockout it is important to know if the organism is

Material and methods 
Media and growth 
For sporulation, International Streptomyces project media 4 (ISP4) plates, poured by hand, have 
been used with incubation time of approximately 10 days at 30 °C. For conjugation ISP4 plates 
were poured by hand with addition of10mM MgCl2 and/or MS plates with 10mM MgCl2. For the 
liquid culture, liquid International Streptomyces project media 2 (ISP2) was used with 1:3 tap water 
and Milli Q water. Recipes can be found in Appendix F. For check plates ISP2 plates poured by 
hand with 50 µg/mL apramycin was used. For extraction ISP2 plates with 12mL of media on each 
plate was used.  
Liquid lysogeny broth media (LB media) with appropriate antibiotics have been used for growing 
E. coli ET12567 and Dh5a. LB solid plates poured by hand has also been used. The recipe of LB 
media can be found in Appendix F. 
Electro-competent cells E. Coli ET12567 and Dh5a. 
E. Coli ET12567 (or Dh5a) was streaked onto an LB plate with 50 µg/mL kanamycin and 25 
µg/mL chloramphenicol and incubated at 37 °C overnight. From the overnight plate a starter culture 
was prepared. A colony was inoculated in 10mL liquid LB media with the same concentration of 
antibiotics. The starter culture was placed in an incubator with shaker at 37 °C overnight. The 
starter culture was inoculated into 1L of LB with the same concentrations of antibiotics and placed 
on a shaker and incubated at 37 °C until the OD600 reached 0.38. The shake flask was placed on ice

Preparation of genomic DNA for cloning  
A liquid culture with a volume of 50mL was prepared and grown for 48 hours at 30°C. The liquid 
culture was plated on a LB plate and incubated at 37°C over night, to check for contamination and 
the liquid culture was frozen at -20°C over night. 2% CleanCut agarose solution was melted using a 
hot water bath at 70°C. The solution was cooled to 50°C. In the meantime, the liquid culture was 
centrifuged at high speed until the cells were collected as residual. The supernatant was removed, 
and the cells were resuspended in equal amount of cell suspension buffer. The cell suspension was 
gently mixed with the CleanCut agarose solution in a ratio of 1:1. The mixture was slowly moved 
with a pipette, to avoid bubbles, to a plug mold with a volume of 180µL. The gels were allowed to 
solidify. Lysozyme 2mg/mL solution was mixed, and the two gels were added to each of the 
solutions. The blocks were incubated with the lysozyme solution for 2 hours at 37°C. After 
incubation the lysozyme was gently removed without disturbing the gel plugs. The plugs were 
rinsed with sterilized Milli Q water after Proteinase K solution 1mg/mL was added and the plugs 
were incubated at 50°C over night. After incubation it was no longer possible to see cells in the 
gels, the gels were transparent. The plugs were washed with TE buffer pH8 four times to ensure that 
no proteinase K solution was left in the tubes. The plugs were stored in TE buffer pH8 at 4°C until 
use.

Preparation of Cas9 with sgRNA 
Templates for in vitro RNA synthesis were prepared using PCR. Two primers were used one with 
the customized protospacer sequence and one which was standard for all amplifications. The 
primers are presented in Appendix D. It was performed with Phusion master mix with GC buffer 
and addition of DMSO as recommended by the manufacturer. No template DNA was added to the 
solution. The PCR program was carried out as follows: First denaturation at 98°C in 10s, after 
cycles was performed, 32 cycles with denaturation 98°C for 5s and annealing at 55°C for 15s. Final 
extension at 72°C for 1min. The PCR products was checked on a gel TriTrack loading dye was used 
and a 1kb ladder. The products were run on a 1% agarose gel 18min at 90V. Thermo Scientific 
TranscriptAid T7 High Yield Transcription kit (k0441) was used for in vitro synthesis of RNA. 5µL 
of PCR product for each template were used. The reaction time was 3 hours and for each primer 
three replicates were performed. After the reaction 1µL of DNAseI was added to each of the 
reactions, the content was mixed by pipetting up and down. The reactions were incubated half an 
hour at 37°C. The mixes were purified with use of the GeneJet RNA Purification kit and the final 
RNA was eluted using RNAse and DNAse free water. The concentration of the final elution was 
measured using Nanodrop and at least one replicate for each primer was tested using Agilent 
Nanochip. 
Transparent gel blocks with genomic DNA was washed prior to use with 5mL of 0.1TE buffer pH8. 
Afterward the blocks were incubated in 1X Cas9 cleavage buffer from NEB, for around half an 
hour. sgRNA pairs for each cluster were mixed separately forward sgRNA and reversed sgRNA 
mixed with Cas9 and buffer mix for 30min at 37°C for assembly of the sgRNA-Cas9 complex. The 
following mixtures were prepared and divided with one sgRNA in each half: 30µL 10x Cleavage 
Cas9 buffer, 9000ng sgRNA forward and 9000ng sgRNA reversed, 20µM Cas9 3µL, RNAse and 
DNAse free water was added to a total volume of 300µL. The solution was added to the gel blocks 
after incubation time and incubated for 4 hours at 37°C. Afterwards the solution was removed from 
the blocks and they were washed using 1mL 0.1TE buffer pH8. The blocks were incubated with 
500µL b-agarse buffer for an hour at room temperature. The solution was removed from the blocks 
and the blocks were incubated in a 70°C water bath for around 10min, or until all samples turned 
liquid. The tubes were moved to a 45°C water bath and equilibrated for 10min. 1.5µL b-agarase 
was added to the samples and gently mixed. The mixture was incubated at 45°C for 40min and 
afterwards the solution was cooled to room temperature. 4-time volume of room temperature 
absolute ethanol was mixed into the solution and incubated for 2 hours at room temperature. The

Gibson assembly 
First the vector backbone was amplified with the 40bp overhangs for the Gibson assembly. The 
primers are found in Appendix D. The amplification was performed using Q5 polymerase with high 
GC enhancer. The mixtures were as follows: 5µL buffer, 0.5µL dNTPs, 2.5 primer mixture, 1µL 
DNA matrix, 5µL enhancer, 10.5µL water and 0.5µL polymerase, with at total volume of 25µL. 8 
replicates were performed for each of the primers and vector backbones. The DNA matrixes used 
was pXj157 with an actinorhodin cluster with a concentration of 48.9ng/µL and pXj158 with a 
concentration of 26.7ng/µL. A touchdown PCR program was performed as follows: An initial 
denaturation at 98°C for 1min. 11 cycles was performed with decrease in temperature for each step 
by 1°C with the following program; denaturation at 98°C for 10s, annealing at 72°C for 15s and 
extension at 72°C for 2min for pXj157 and 4min for pXj158. Another 35 cycles were performed as 
follows; denaturation at 98°C for 20s, annealing at 63°C for 15s and extension at 72°C for pXj157 
for 2min and for pXj158 for 4min. A final extension was performed at 72°C for 10min. 3µL of the 
PCR product was checked on a 1% agarose gel with a 1kb ladder, the products were dyed using 
TriTrack and the gel was run 20min at 90V. Afterwards 1µL of DpnI fast digest was added to the

Conclusion 
 
The aim of the project was to characterize the nature of bioactive compound or compounds, 
synthesized by NBC00162 strain. One of the compounds, produced by this strain, namely 
pyracrimycin A, was found to be responsible for the bioactivity against A. baumannii. A bioassay-
guided HPLC was used to isolate the crude extract and to isolate the active compound. The final 
separation was a two-step separation: at first separation with a C18 column with subsequent 
Xbridge Amide and Accucore PFP second separation. A literature search did not result in a known 
BGC responsible for the formation of the bioactive compound pyracrimycin A. Three candidate 
BGCs, which could be responsible for the biosynthesis of the bioactive compound, were identified. 
Protospacer sequences were designed both for CRISPR-Cas9 induced knockout and for CRISPR-
Cas9 induced fragment digestion for BGC cloning. CATCH cloning technique was successfully 
applied for the cloning of BGC 1 and BGC 5 using the p15A-based vector pXJ157. The resulting 
plasmid constructs were sequenced in order to verify the presence of both BGCs. The plasmids with 
BGC 1 were successfully transferred through the process of intrageneric conjugation to a range of 
selected heterologous expression hosts. Exconjugant colonies were selected for the following 
heterologous expression hosts: S. coelicolor M1146, S. coelicolor M512, S. albus J1074, S. 
coelicolor M1152 and S. griseofuscus DSM40191. The presence of BGC 1 in the cells of S. 
griseofuscus and S. coelicolor M512 was verified with the help of colony PCR.

Future prospects  
The cloning of the CRISPR-Cas9 plasmid will be finished and positive clones will be identified for 
each of the designed protospacers. The plasmids then will be transferred through conjugation to 
NBC00162. The exconjugants will be checked to see if any of them loses activity against A. 
baumannii. The test would give an indication of whether any of the candidate BGC is responsible 
for the biosynthesis of pyracrimycin A. If any of the candidates are selected in this experiment, the 
corresponding BGC will be cloned and heterologously expressed in order to verify it. 
The heterologous expression hosts, that contain plasmids with BGC 1 and 5, will be further 
analyzed to identify the possible new compounds expressed in the hosts. First of all, the conclusive 
proof of the presence of both plasmids in the host strains has to be obtained. Further on, all host 
strains will be cultivated in different growth media under the set of conditions in order to determine 
the perfect conditions for the analysis of the new compounds. New cloning-experiments will be 
designed for BGC 7 and 13. Similarly to BGC 1 and 5, the BGC 7 and 13 will be expressed in the 
range of heterologous hosts.
