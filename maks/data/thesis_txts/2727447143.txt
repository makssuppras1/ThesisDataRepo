Abstract
The dark web, a hidden and encrypted part of the internet accessible only through speôÙ
cialised software, has been the subject of growing interest due to its securityôÙfocused
nature. The dark web protects its users through the onion router (Tor) which directs the
traffic through an encrypted circuit that improves privacy and anonymity compared to the
clear web. These features have facilitated the rise of illegal activity on the hidden serôÙ
vices deployed on the Tor network. The hidden services are identified through v3 onion
addresses which hosts forums and marketplaces that have become a hub for criminals
to thrive. This illegal content contains valuable information that is interesting for several
parties to collect and analyse to mitigate threats. The dark web hosts a large number
of hidden services making it challenging to monitor the content. To overcome this chalôÙ
lenge, an automated monitoring system can be utilised, however, the hidden services
are actively implementing antiôÙmonitoring techniques such as CAPTCHAs to prevent the
collection of their content. This thesis attempts to identify the antiôÙmonitoring techniques
implemented on the hidden services through a black box test with the aim of triggering the
antiôÙmonitoring backôÙend logic. A hidden service is hosted to analyse the data received
for any differences that potentially reveal an automated monitoring system. This thesis
improves the process of bypassing CAPTCHAs on the dark web by presenting a trained
AI model to detect and classify different CAPTCHA types. Finally, an automated monitorôÙ
ing system is designed to collect v3 onion addresses, providing a framework to bypass
CAPTCHAs and crawl through the hidden services to collect the content. The content is
then once stored and made accessible via a search interface, laying the groundwork for
the initial stages of comprehensive data analysis on the dark web.

Introduction
For many years, the dark web has been home to hidden services that host forums, marketôÙ
places, and other websites. The dark web is accessed through the Tor network providing
encrypted internet traffic that improves the anonymisation and privacy of the users comôÙ
pared to the regular clear web [1]. The clear web is accessible through search engines
such as Google and Yahoo, whereas the dark web in contrast is not indexed and relies
on sharing of onion addresses. The onion addresses represent the domain of a hidden
service and are commonly found on directories and forums [2].
The anonymisation and privacy features of the dark web have allowed citizens of countries
with strict internet censorship to bypass the restrictions and access banned sites including
news websites and social media [3]. These features have also encouraged the rise of
illicit markets and services, which is determined to cover 57% of the content on the dark
web [4]. The content commonly includes unlawful discussions, cyber threats, terrorist
communication, illegitimate drugs, weapons trafficking, child pornography, and more.
The illicit content is openly available for security agencies, law enforcement, and threat
analysts. However, the challenges of retrieving content from the dark web make it a
difficult process. Being able to monitor the content provides valuable insights that can
be subject to data analysis [5]. The analysis potentially shows data trends and patterns
which can have a significant impact on mitigating threats from the dark web.
The challenges of monitoring the dark web stem from a continuous battle between hidôÙ
den services and crawling techniques that are actively getting more sophisticated to surôÙ
pass one another [6]. The hidden services have deployed antiôÙmonitoring techniques to
prevent crawlers from accessing and retrieving the content. The antiôÙmonitoring techôÙ
niques include rate limiting and CAPTCHAs, which drastically complicates the process
for crawlers to get access to the hidden services [7]. On the contrary, algorithms are deôÙ
signed and developed to solve CAPTCHAs on the dark web making it possible to bypass
some of the antiôÙmonitoring techniques.
The quantity of illegal content on the dark web shows the need for an intelligent automated
monitoring system that is able to bypass antiôÙmonitoring techniques and store the content
efficiently and accessible for data analysis.
1.1
Scope
The ultimate goal of this thesis is to design an automated monitoring system that provides
the possibility to bypass antiôÙmonitoring techniques on the dark web. To achieve this goal,
several tasks need to be fulfilled and are broken down into the following:

to prevent crawling and automated scraping.

ãÂ Design a collection system that can fetch active v3 onion addresses and access the

ãÂ Propose a framework to bypass the antiôÙmonitoring techniques during automated
monitoring of hidden services.

Background
A subset of the dark web is hosted on the Tor network, short for the onion router, which
was publicly released in 2006 and consists of openôÙsource software to provide anonymous
communication on the internet [8]. The dark web has gained increasing attention in recent
years due to its anonymity and the potential for illicit activities, leading to a growing interest
in understanding its structure and content through various forms of monitoring and data
analysis. The hidden services are hosted on the Tor network and are used by nearly 3
million daily users on average in the year 2023 according to the Tor project [9].
Attempts on improving the performance and reducing the threats to the network are acôÙ
tively implemented. Thus the infrastructure and features of the Tor network are continôÙ
uously modified and one recent drastic change is the onion addresses upgrading to V3
being more challenging in a monitoring context [10].
The dark web is a constantly evolving digital landscape, shaped by the changing needs
of its users and the adoption of new methods by criminals. It is relevant to present an
upôÙtoôÙdate definition of the Tor network and the possibilities of monitoring the dark web,
which is presented in the following sections.
2.1
The Tor Network
In this thesis, the initial step of monitoring dark web forums is identifying their addresses
to access and crawl the forums. However, due to the structure of the Tor network, this task
is rather challenging. Thus, a brief overlay of the Tor network is presented by describing
the fundamental setup of the underlying structure, which is followed by an elaboration of
the onion addresses [11].
2.1.1
Structure of Tor Network
The structure of the Tor network consists of a decentralised network of servers, also known
as nodes, which are run by volunteers around the world [12]. When a user connects to the
Tor network, their internet traffic is routed through a number of three randomly selected
nodes referred to as a circuit, as visualised in Figure 2.1. Each node only has knowledge
of the identity of the next nodes in the circuit. This process of routing internet traffic through
multiple nodes is known as onion routing.
The Tor network is designed to provide users with anonymity by making it difficult for
anyone to trace the internet activity back to the user. This is achieved by encrypting
the internet traffic of users and routing it through the circuit such that interception of the
traffic contains no valid information from the perspective of an eavesdropper. The traffic
encryption is depicted in Figure 2.1, where the green arrows represent encrypted traffic.
For the hidden service to be able to read the traffic the last request is sent unencrypted
and represented by a red arrow. Furthermore, as the network is decentralised there exists
no single entity or organisation which can monitor all the network traffic.
The circuit on the Tor network, shown in Figure 2.1, has three types of nodes: entry nodes,
relay nodes, and exit nodes [13]. Entry nodes are the first nodes where the internet traffic
of a user is sent when they connect to the Tor network. Relay nodes are intermediate
nodes that route the traffic between the entry and exit nodes. Exit nodes are the final
nodes that the traffic passes through before leaving the Tor network where the traffic is
decrypted.

For users to access the dark web on the Tor network, the Tor browser is needed, where
the correct configurations of setting up proxies are done by default. These configurations
enable the traffic to be routed through the Tor circuit. After the connection to an entry
node is set up, the next step is to identify the onion address of hidden services. The
following section goes into the details of hidden services and their onion address based
on the latest specification by the Tor project [2].
2.1.2
Accessing Hidden Services
The onion addresses have recently been through a major update from v2 to v3, which
makes the onion addresses more difficult to identify. The reason that onion addresses
are difficult to identify is the fact that the Tor network does not utilise IP addresses as
seen in the DNS protocol.
A server on Tor is a hidden service which generates a public and private key for asymôÙ
metric encryption, more specifically the EdDSA25519 publicôÙkey signature system. The
hidden service then chooses some relays from the Tor network to act as their introduction
points and creates a hidden service descriptor which contains the public key and location
of the introduction points. The hidden service descriptor is then signed with the private key
and uploaded to a distributed hash table (DHT), which is stored on a hidden service diôÙ
rectory (HSDir). The HSDir relay is a partially decentralised storage system that provides
a lookup for keyôÙvalue pairs from the DHT. By utilising HSDir relays there is no central
authority that knows about all hidden services as their information is distributed among the
relays in the network. However, all HSDir relays are listed in the Tor consensus, hence
it is not fully decentralised and can be viewed as the DNS of Tor. Furthermore, the DHT
only stores the first half of the public key from the hidden service. For a user to access
the hidden service, knowledge of the full EdDSA25519 public key is needed as there is
no central service with knowledge or index of all public keys. For that reason, the public
keys are only accessible by sharing, which is typically done on the clear web on social
media, such as Reddit and directory sites dedicated to sharing onion addresses [14].
When a user requests an onion address, the DHT checks if it exists and returns the inôÙ
troduction point. To enable communication, a random relay is chosen and both the user
and the introduction point agree on a new relay called the rendezvous point, which both
the server and user connect to. When the circuits are built to the rendezvous point, a
functional connection between the user and the hidden service has been established.

The traffic between the user and the hidden service is endôÙtoôÙend encrypted and the renôÙ
dezvous point is only responsible for relaying the traffic back and forth. Each router along
the circuit only knows what router it is relaying the traffic to, but it has no knowledge of
where the traffic originates from, the final destination, or the previous relay.
Onion address versions
The hidden services are identified by a 56ôÙcharacter long EdDSA25519 public key folôÙ
lowed by the domain .onion and are named a v3 onion address [10]. This change was
fully implemented in 2021 when the v2 onion addresses were deprecated. It is relevant to
include the differences between these two versions, as several studies have shown how
v2 onion can be exploited [15, 16]. This is the main reason why the Tor project updated
the onion address and led to a comprehensive document [2], elaborating all details of v3,
which this section is partially based on1. Exploits of the onion address can lead to identiôÙ
fying relevant forums which are not publicly available on hidden service directories [17].
However, these exploits are potentially relevant to also explore in the v3 onion address.
The obvious difference between v2 and v3 is the length of the onion address, where v2
was a 16ôÙcharacter long hash of the public key, and v3 is the full public key. For v2, the
information uploaded to the HSDir is in plain text, which implies that Tor relays with the
HSDir flag can exploit the system by obtaining significant details about active v2 onion
services, including the onion address. It should be noted that collecting onion addresses
through exploits via HSDir relays is considered malicious behaviour, and a dedicated badôÙ
relay team from the Tor project is sanctioning these relays.
To address the issue of plain text data uploaded to the HSDir in v2 onion services, v3 uses
encryption and key derivation. In v3 onion addresses, the address itself is a public key,
allowing all data uploaded to the HSDir to be encrypted, which can be decrypted by using
the public key itself. However, the issue of mass collection of onion addresses still exists
if the public keys are stored on the relays. This issue is prevented by providing a blinded
key of the public key to the HSDir relays which are stored. The blinded key is determined
through a key derivation scheme, which uses an identifier that is rotated daily. From the
perspective of the user, the Tor client calculates the blinded public key after receiving the
onion address and then requests it from the HSDir.
The user requests a v3 onion address from the HSDir such as:

The blinded key is then sent to the HSDir and then the process continues.
Potential attacks on v3 onion addresses
As a result of updating the whole onion address scheme, the v3 onion addresses expose
less sensitive information. One obvious benefit is the reduced bruteôÙforce possibilities by
identifying onion addresses through 56 characters, in contrast to the 16 characters in v2
onion addresses. Nonetheless, these modifications on v3 onion addresses do not entirely
prevent the hidden service directory from disclosing significant metadata.
An attack proposal by Hoeller et al. in [18] describes that when the HSDir relays receive
a blinded public key, it translates to exactly one onion address. Thus, it is possible to
estimate the number of running onion services by counting the number of blinded pubôÙ
lic keys. The HSDir relays have knowledge of when blinded public keys are uploaded

2.2
Monitoring on Tor
Monitoring and scraping data from forums on Tor makes it possible to collect valuable inôÙ
formation including discussions and user profiles that are potentially eligible for data analyôÙ
sis. This process can provide insights into the inner workings of these hidden communities
and help uncover potential threats. Both on the dark web and clear web researchers have
evaluated several crawling techniques to iterate through pages on a website. However,
collecting data from the forums hosted on hidden services includes several challenges
with different solutions.
2.2.1
Crawling techniques
Depending on the goal of an application, different crawling techniques are more suitable.
The general aim of a crawler is to automate a browser to follow scripted commands. The
current crawlers offer several opportunities including HTTP crawling, browserôÙcontrolled
crawling, or controlling a headless browser, which are the most common options [19].
HTTP crawling are sent to web servers and extracting data from the responses. When
a crawler sends an HTTP request, it typically includes information such as the URL of
the destination being requested, the user agent, and any cookies or other data needed
for authentication or tracking [6]. The web server then sends back an HTTP response,
which contains the requested content along with metadata such as the HTTP status code,
content type, and other headers. It is possible to start sessions using HTTP requests
frameworks, in which cookies and session ids are stored in the headers.
Browser crawling is running a web driver which interacts with an actual browser by sendôÙ
ing commands and spawning a GUI, which is similar to a user browsing on a computer
[20]. The process of browser crawling makes it possible for the scripted commands to
mimic human behaviour such as taking screenshots, filling out forms, scrolling through
pages, and loading and running Javascript. Browser crawling enables the user to follow
the process visually in real time.

2.2.2
Challenges of monitoring
It is relevant to include the general challenges related to monitoring, as different crawling
methods are suitable for specific monitoring purposes. The presented challenges of monôÙ
itoring cover both the dark web on Tor and the clear web, as they potentially are relevant
for the dark web. An automated monitoring system that crawls data from the dark web
encounters several challenges including the following:
ãÂ Speed is a relevant factor when crawling on Tor due to the circuit connection which
needs to hop through three different relays before reaching the destination [6]. Thus
it can be relevant to optimise the time efficiency of a crawler on the dark web.
ãÂ Availability of onion services suffer from technical challenges such as bandwidth
limitations, which leads to an outage on the sites making crawling operations unfeaôÙ
sible as the web server does not respond [6].
ãÂ Dynamic content such as JavaScript or AJAX scripts, can be challenging to crawl
because the content may not be available in the initial HTML response [22]. Web
pages may require user interaction or additional requests to load images, videos, or
other media content. Dynamic content can also be loaded after the user scrolls the
page.
ãÂ Scalability of the crawlers can be necessary to compete with the fast pacing nonôÙ
stopping evolvement of the internet [6]. One way to address this challenge is to
employ distributed crawling, which involves running the crawler on multiple devices
and dividing the URL space among them so that each device is responsible for a
subset of URLs.
These challenges mainly concern the infrastructure of the monitoring system. Other chalôÙ
lenges of monitoring include the implementation of antiôÙmonitoring techniques. An exôÙ
ample of an antiôÙmonitoring technique is the Completely Automated Public Turing test
to tell Computers and Humans Apart (CAPTCHA), which forces the user to pass a test
before entering the hidden service [23]. CAPTCHAs prevent simple crawlers to access
hidden services and therefore constitute a significant challenge in monitoring the dark
web. Browsing the dark web shows various types of CAPTCHA, where each of these
types is presented in different levels of obfuscation, typically including some sort of image
overlay or tilting of characters. The different types can be combined, often mixing text
with numbers or images inside a puzzle. Examples of CAPTCHAs from the dark web are
shown in Figure 2.2.

Related Work
The relevant theoretical perspectives behind this thesis have been presented. In the
following sections, a deeper examination is conducted of the automated monitoring and
antiôÙmonitoring techniques on the dark web by exploring the existing literature in the field
of this study. The aim is to provide knowledge of the existing results as well as illustrate
the yet unexplored research topics.
Threat intelligence within the dark web has long been a topic of interest for researchers
and industry experts in the fields of cyber security and information retrieval. Studies conôÙ
ducted explore the challenges and opportunities associated with accessing and moniôÙ
toring content on the dark web [24, 25, 26]. Previous research has focused on various
aspects of dark web monitoring, including collecting onion addresses leading to hidden
services [15, 14, 27], the use of machine learning techniques for bypassing antiôÙbot proôÙ
tection [28, 7], the effectiveness of different crawling strategies [20, 6], and the opportuôÙ
nities of data analytics on the dark web [29, 11].
3.1
Gathering Intelligence from The Dark Web
In this section, relevant work on gathering intelligence from the dark web is reviewed, and
the key contributions and limitations are highlighted.
Studies worth mentioning include [24] from 2019 where Pannu et al. propose a design of a
dark web search engine consisting of a database populated by scraping onion URLs and
archiving a searchable snapshot of the web pages. The study highlights that CAPTCHA,
being an antiôÙbot measure, is becoming more common on hidden services, which prevents
the designed crawlers from retrieving data.
Another study from 2021 by Dalvi et al. implements a primitive monitoring tool in [26] that
crawls onion URLs through a seed list of addresses provided by the user. The presented
framework offers keyword search, however, upon examining the source code, it becomes
apparent that the process of conducting keyword searches employs a commercial search
engine, rather than searching through the data that has been acquired [30]. The study
mentions two challenges that the developed crawler encounters: 1) accessing hidden
services that require login credentials to enter 2) formatting the unstructured data to do
further analysis. However, the study does not mention bypassing CAPTCHAs or other
antiôÙmonitoring techniques.
Besides the research, commercial and public applications exist, where ahmia.fi is one of
the most used solutions, funded by the Tor project and used for keyword search in the
mentioned article [26]. A common factor of the publicly available search engines is the
seed lists used and crawling methods that follow the instructions of the hidden services
provided in the robots.txt files as well as the passive nature of the crawlers. None of the
publicly available search engines shows signs of bypassing antiôÙmonitoring techniques.
Thus, a large part of the dark web remains uncovered by these search engines.

Collecting Onion Addresses

3.3
Bypassing AntiôÙmonitoring Techniques
Hidden services has implemented several methods to prevent bots from accessing their
services and crawling through the sites [6]. Bots can consume large amounts of the
resources on the hidden services which can lead to DoS attacks or bots being used for
malicious activities such as spamming and launching other cyber attacks [31]. In this

services and crawling to collect data. Automated monitoring of hidden services is the first
step towards developing the resources needed for cyber threat intelligence and mitigation
of cybercrime through analysis of patterns and trends on the dark web.
Different techniques can be implemented to prevent bots from monitoring the content of
hidden services, however, a common technique is employing CAPTCHAs to force the

been seen to fail at preventing bots from passing the test [7]. Thus, additional measures
may have been implemented including digital fingerprints and flagged IP addresses as

3.3.2
Solving CAPTCHA
Several studies show that it is possible to solve different types of CAPTCHA. A study from

method used is convolutional neural network (CNN), which is a deep learning algorithm,

is more reliable and accurate during the tests. Other studies have focused on using either
deep learning methods or nonôÙmachine learning algorithms, which are presented in the

Deep learning
A modern approach to solving CAPTCHAs is using a CNN, a class of artificial neural
networks within deep learning [35]. The CNN algorithm takes images as input and assigns
different values to various features on the image. The goal of CNN is to convert the
features into forms that differentiate the images and enable accurate classification. In the
case of CAPTCHAs, the CNN can be trained on a data set of CAPTCHA images and
their corresponding labels being the correct answer to the respective CAPTCHAs. The
CNN learns to recognise the unique features of each type of CAPTCHA and classify them
correctly.
One advantage of using a CNN for CAPTCHA solving is that it can be trained on a wide
range of CAPTCHA types, including textôÙbased, numberôÙbased, and imageôÙbased CAPTôÙ

design, making it more resilient in solving modified CAPTCHAs.

On the dark web, it is significant for an automated monitoring process to bypass detection

CAPTCHAs can be detected and classified using an AI model. The relevant methods for
this study includes collecting a dataset, expanding the dataset, and training an artificial
intelligence model. Other methods of detecting and classifying CAPTCHAs can include
OCR scans, however, these are not as effective as deep learning algorithms, given the
complexity and variety of CAPTCHAs designed as described in [34].

a label defining the location of the CAPTCHA, it is possible to extract only the CAPTCHA
from the screenshot. The CAPTCHA can then be inserted into other pages with different
backgrounds and located randomly on the page with the respective label representing the
new location of the CAPTCHA. Thus, it is possible to expand the dataset with CAPTCHAs
present on other websites, such that the AI model has a better chance of identifying the
CAPTCHA.

term frequency (TF) and inverse document frequency (IDF). TF represents the number of
occurrences of the searched word, such that if the word appears more often the score is

6.2
CAPTCHA Detection and Classification
The results from exploring the antiôÙmonitoring techniques and the related literature shows
the extensive use of CAPTCHAs on the dark web as a primary antiôÙmonitoring technique
[28, 7, 33]. It is also common that hidden services which implement antiôÙmonitoring techôÙ
niques are more interesting to monitor. Several studies have designed and demonstrated
how CAPTCHAs can be solved by algorithms, as presented in Section 3.3.2. The studôÙ
ies solving CAPTCHAs target one type of CAPTCHA, which leads to the definition of the
different types of CAPTCHA on the dark web. As there exists no algorithm that solves all
types, a CAPTCHA detection and classification algorithm is needed.
As already mentioned, the aim of this thesis is to train an AI model to detect the presence
of a CAPTCHA and classify the CAPTCHA type. The methods utilised included gatherôÙ
ing CAPTCHAs by requesting the hidden services, expanding the dataset through image
manipulation, and training the AI model.

To gather valuable intelligence from the dark web an automated monitoring system has

7
Conclusion
In this thesis, an automated monitoring system that targets hidden services on the dark
web is presented.
The hidden services host forums, marketplaces and other pages
which hold valuable information. However, the services are commonly protected by antiôÙ
monitoring techniques, which block crawlers, and thus the automated monitoring system
was expanded to allow access to hidden services despite their antiôÙmonitoring tools.
Tests are conducted to explore what antiôÙmonitoring techniques are deployed on the dark
web. Through an extensive black box and stress test of selected hidden services, it is
shown that the primary effort of blocking crawlers is the use of CAPTCHA, which needs to
be solved to gain access. A hidden service is hosted to analyse the data received by the
server. The test shows the additional request that are sent through the Tor network from
the SeleniumôÙcontrolled crawlers. This additional request is potentially used to implement
antiôÙmonitoring techniques which can be bypassed by extending the requests sent from
the crawlers.
The findings from exploring the antiôÙmonitoring techniques showcase the need for an
algorithm, that is able to detect the CAPTCHAs encountered when accessing hidden serôÙ
vices. The algorithm is developed using a CNN model with a dataset consisting of different
CAPTCHA types. The trained model is able to detect and classify the CAPTCHAs, which
can be combined with various CAPTCHAôÙsolving algorithms. The evaluation shows that
the model performs fairly when encountering new textôÙbased and numberôÙbased CAPTôÙ
CHA. The limitation of the trained model has shown to be the detection of imageôÙbased
CAPTCHAs due to their drastically different visuals.
The automated monitoring system is provided with a dynamic seed list that supplies 904
unique onion addresses and is reduced to 278 active v3 onion addresses. The content of
each active hidden service is stored and provided through a search interface. The content
is crawled to iterate through the pages and analysed for other onion addresses. Through
the references of each source, a network graph is presented that reveals the relationships
between hidden services and the seed list.
As a final remark, it is concluded that the project is a success as it is shown to be possible
to deploy an automated monitoring system which covers the dark web and provides a
framework that is able to bypass the antiôÙmonitoring techniques on hidden services.
7.1
Limitations
It is essential to recognise certain limitations that have been present throughout this thesis.
These limitations will be listed in the following:
ãÂ The tests conducted on the dark web revealed that the Tor network differs sigôÙ
nificantly from the clear web, presenting unique challenges.
Server issues are
frequently encountered when experimenting with antiôÙmonitoring techniques, often
causing outages in the hidden services during tests. Monitoring hidden services
on the Tor network is a challenging task due to these frequent outages, causing
services to be temporarily unavailable. It is unclear whether a hidden service is
inactive temporarily, permanently shut down, or has never been active in the first
place. Thus, a limitation in this study is that the continuous monitor of the hidden
services has only been possible for a limited time span, namely during the months
of creating this project.

ãÂ The onion addresses targeted when exploring antiôÙmonitoring techniques are manôÙ
ually identified and used. This can result in only a small fraction of all the hidden
services on the dark web being targeted. The hidden services are selected based on
the assumption of having implemented antiôÙmonitoring techniques, which potentially
provide a biased snippet of the dark web.
ãÂ The crawling setups designed and utilised to trigger antiôÙmonitoring techniques is
mainly divided into SeleniumôÙbased and HTTP crawlers. A relevant factor is the
speed differences of these crawlers as the Selenium crawlers can be slower than
the HTTP crawlers. This variation in speed could potentially impact the obtained
results, given that HTTP crawlers tend to be more aggressive in sending requests.
ãÂ An antiôÙmonitoring technique is suspected on the dark web when the status codes
returned by the hidden services is not 200. While a status code of 200 is generally
interpreted as a successful request, this assumption can potentially be misleading.
It is possible that the backôÙend logic redirects the request to a blocked page, despite
initially indicating a successful connection. This limitation is particularly relevant for
HTTP crawlers, which tend to analyse only the first response received from the tarôÙ
get. Therefore, a 200 status code could lead the crawler to inaccurately show sucôÙ
cessful access. Additionally, there exists another complexity where a response may
return a successful status code, but the server of the hidden service may block the
Tor identity internally. This indicates that despite receiving a successful response,
the access to the content could be limited or blocked. These potential issues conôÙ
tribute to the limitations of the test and could potentially introduce inaccuracies in
the results.
ãÂ The process of collecting a CAPTCHA dataset is restrained by the rate limits on the
access page for hidden services. Dread forum and other hidden services use the
Endgame project, described in Section 3.3.2, which provides a imageôÙbased CAPTôÙ
CHA generator and other antiôÙDDOS methods, such as rate limits on the CAPTCHA
page. Thus, it is only possible to generate three CAPTCHAs before the hidden serôÙ
vice blocks the Tor identity. This implies that the CAPTCHA dataset in this thesis is
restricted by the rate limits, and as a consequence only a limited quantity of imageôÙ
based CAPTCHAs are collected. This is also seen in the distribution of CAPTCHA
types from the dataset in Figure 5.2.
ãÂ The imageôÙbased CAPTCHA generator can be used to generate a CAPTCHA colôÙ
lection. This is done in [7] from 2022 by Audran et al., where the Endgame project is
targeted. The Endgame project is openôÙsource and the code for generating CAPTôÙ
CHAs is used to create CAPTCHAs for the dataset. However, at the time of writing
this thesis, the hidden services have changed their CAPTCHAs to an image puzzle
CAPTCHA. The source code is kept unchanged, generating an outdated CAPTôÙ
CHA, as seen in Figure 3.1. Thus, the approach of generating CAPTCHAs using
the openôÙsource code is irrelevant in this study.
ãÂ The number of onion addresses fetched from Google is very limited due to the
antiôÙmonitoring techniques implemented by Google, that are not possible to bypass
within the scope of the thesis, as the techniques are more comprehensive. Other
search engines are examined for mirror links with no success.
