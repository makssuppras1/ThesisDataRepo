Abstract
This thesis presents a novel approach for learning observation models in automated planning. It
is relevant to deterministic, partially observable domains in which observability is dependent on an
inherent observation function that is deterministic with regard to the state of the problem.
Our approach approximates an observation function by creating a relation-based model from
partial states to atomic propositions. We show that it is possible for such a model to provide the same
output as the observation function. Initially, we present an optimistic naïve learner that produces a
complete observation model, albeit with an infeasible space complexity. We iterate upon this basic
approach, splitting the model into two sets, suﬃcient and insuﬃcient conditions. This approach
gives a sound but not complete observation model with better space bounds. To further reduce
space consumption we introduce a reduction method that removes relations based on contradictions.
The ﬁnal revision of the model adapts it to contingent planning by adding another set of conditions
that are possibly suﬃcient. We have a reasonable expectation that such a condition is relevant for
observing some atomic proposition because it has been present at least once before while the agent
observed that atom. Conditions from possibly suﬃcient conditions are removed whenever the agent
can verify that they are either suﬃcient or insuﬃcient. Suﬃciency requires the agent to be certain
about the current world state. The proposed learning method is identiﬁable in the limit, but can be
made ﬁnitely identiﬁable if the entire state space is computed and veriﬁably visited.
We show through an implementation that the observation model learning algorithm is feasible.
Further, we provide benchmarks of various test domains, in which correct and complete observation
models are eventually learned.
The next logical step would be a higher degree of generalisation in terms of error recovery,
determinism, and degree of knowledge along with application in contingent planning.

Introduction
This report is the master thesis of Kasper Lederballe Sørensen and Thomas Søren Henney and was
completed from August 26, 2019 until January 26, 2020 at the Technical University of Denmark
(DTU). The work is equivalent to 30 ECTS points per student (60 in total) during the thesis period.
The work presented is the result of rigorous literature research culminating in novel research with
a proof-of-concept implementation.
Our initial intent was to study state-of-the-art action model
learning and eventually develop new methods or alter current methods and ﬁnally compare these
through benchmarks.
We concluded that action model learning was very well-studied, but were
instead drawn to the concept of observation models. Agents operating in the real world are rarely
able at all times to observe everything regarding the systems in which they operate. This concept,
partial observability, is frequently used, but most literature on automated planning reduces the
observability to randomness, usually attributed to faulty sensors. We conjecture that agents rarely
obtain observations uniformly at random, and that an underlying observation function dictates the
perceptual input to the agent. This thesis aims to study the ability to learn to mimic such functions.
We begin this report by giving a thorough introduction to the automated planning framework
we use for our descriptions in Chapter 2. We focus on traditional deterministic STRIPS actions
with the addition of conditional eﬀects. We study all domains in contingent planning that adhere
to such action descriptions under the assumption that agents have full knowledge of action models.
Thus, contingent planning problems can contain incomplete knowledge of the initial state and partial
observability.
Chapter 3 describes common practices when deﬁning partial observability in automated planning
and continues by describing some current state-of-the-art contingent planners, i.e. partial observ-
ability planners. Finally, the chapter provides thorough insight into the foundations of action model
learning as well as some state-of-the-art approaches.
In Chapter 4 we introduce novel observation model data structures in an iterative process. The
aim of each of these observation models is to be able to correctly predict the output of the observation
function from the above conjecture about observations given the knowledge of an agent. Initially, we
introduce a naïve data structure that over-approximates the observability of atoms in a system with
known initial state. We revise that data structure by creating a sound observation model, which
always correctly predicts that an atom can be observed or not observed through a series of suﬃcient
and insuﬃcient conditions, but cannot always provide an answer.
The ﬁnal iteration introduces
an observation model in contingent planning that, along with the above suﬃcient and insuﬃcient
conditions, contains conditions that are likely to be suﬃcient for observing some atom.
We created a Java implementation of an entire generic planner as well as the ﬁnal observation
model described above, which we explain in Chapter 5. Using the implementation, we tested the
applied capabilities of the described observation models, and the results are shown in Chapter 6.
We created observation functions for the test domains that emulate realistic systems as closely as
possible. We show that we can reasonably expect to learn to predict observations for these domains
under certain assumptions.
Finally, we use Chapter 7 to discuss the applications of observation models in general, how

2.1
Artiﬁcial Intelligence
The ﬁeld of artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) is a very broad research topic, which, especially with recent
breakthroughs in deep learning, has gained a lot of traction in both industry and media.
The
academic ﬁeld of AI is, however, generally accepted to have been founded in 1956 [30] (pp. 111–136)
[37] (p. 17) and has since seen many applications in today’s society, these days solving problems such
as natural language processing and interpretation, self-driving vehicles, image recognition, playing
games, and automated planning.
Some categorise subﬁelds of AI according to approach and others according to end goal.
A
common distinction between approaches is the symbolic1 versus subsymbolic2 AI research domains.
Symbolic AI refers to machine intelligence created by means of representing problems logically in
a human-readable manner, which can be exhaustively searched in order to ﬁnd solutions to the
given problems. Subsymbolic AI approaches, on the other hand, create abstractions from the human
understanding of the problems. Instead, they mathematically model all input variables while verifying
a satisfactory model through means of cross-validation [41].
The above approaches were previously researched by almost disjoint groups of researchers. Re-
cently, however, ﬁelds of research have merged in order to solve open problems by applying AI
approaches from both areas [20][7]. A prime example of this is the AlphaGo AI based on Google
DeepMind. AlphaGo is the ﬁrst and only computer program to defeat a professional player at the
game of Go, previously thought by many players of the game to be impossible for computers to learn
as it requires some level of intuition. AlphaGo uses state search as typically used in symbolic AI
with underlying layers of deep learning to guide the search [39].
2.1.1
Automated Planning within Artiﬁcial Intelligence
Within the past 50 years, automated planning (AP) has become a well-studied ﬁeld. In AP, one or
several agents in some domain must ﬁnd a plan that achieves certain goals. These agents operate
within speciﬁc domains, which can be thought of as a limited part of the world of which they
keep track of an internal model. Using this internal representation, they attempt to solve planning
problems within the domain. An example of a domain is Blocks World, which is often used in research.
A plan can be thought of as an ordered sequence of actions where each action has preconditions and
eﬀects3. Preconditions are requirements on the state of the world before the action is executed,
whereas eﬀects are requirements on the state after the action has been executed, i.e. what the action
changes in the world [37]. A more formal introduction will be given in Section 2.3.
The ﬁrst computer to solve planning problems was the Stanford Research Institute Problem
Solver (STRIPS) developed in 1971. It gave name to the ﬁrst formal language for describing planning
problems because the language was part of the software it used. Its intended use was as software

2.2
Automated Planning in Society
Many areas in modern society require some form of planning or scheduling.
A common use is
within logistics, where several diﬀerent people and vehicles must transport goods between locations
in possibly very complex systems. Performing calculations of good routes while considering available
resources, schedules, size of goods, etc. is very diﬃcult for humans to do feasibly. This gives rise
to the use of AP in order to calculate routes taking these variables into consideration with minimal
human eﬀort.
Other uses of AP include automated warehouses, composition of web services, and hospital robots
[9] [36] [12] [49]. Planning in web services often involves composition of several diﬀerent services in
order to provide functionality not supported by any single service alone.
While very unlike the
other domains, web service planning has already found widespread use. Automated warehouses have
become increasingly common lately and show how AI is already replacing humans at rather complex
tasks [13].
While applications of AP already exist in society, most research is initially performed on much
smaller domains (toy domains) with limited numbers of agents, objects, and actions. Approaches are
later scaled to proper domains when they have promising outlooks. As mentioned, a common toy
domain is Blocks World, which we will introduce below and later describe formally in Section 2.3.3.
Additionally, we describe an adapted version of the Light Switch domain introduced by Shahaf and
Amir [38], which we shall use later on.
2.2.1
Blocks World
In this planning domain, a set of uniquely identiﬁable blocks are placed on a table. They can be on
top of each other, but any block can have no more than one other directly on top of it. The goal in
the domain is to move the blocks to place them in some desired conﬁguration. Blocks can be moved
either on top of other blocks or onto the table. Movement has two restrictions: only one block may
be moved at a time, and a block can only be moved if there is nothing on top of it. Figure 2.1 shows
a simple example problem.

2.3
Formal Description of Planning Problems
Many attempts at creating concise descriptions of planning problems have been made. The STRIPS
language mentioned earlier deﬁnes problems in terms of an initial state, goal literals, and a set of
actions. Other languages such as the Action Deﬁnition Language (ADL), based on STRIPS, have
found some use in certain areas. The most common language used for planning is Planning Domain
Deﬁnition Language (PDDL), which takes elements from both STRIPS and ADL, but also allows
additional functionality. PDDL has gone through many iterations since its introduction in 1998.
Russell and Norvig [37] use an adapted version of PDDL that lifts the language from its syntax
restrictions. Much like them, we will create an abstraction from the formal syntax of PDDL and
instead stick to describing the semantics of the languages in a legible way. In the following, we base
our description of an extension of STRIPS with conditional eﬀects loosely on the description by
Russell and Norvig [22].
2.3.1
Logic
PDDL uses mathematical logic as the foundation for describing problems. It is assumed that the
reader has ﬁrm knowledge of propositional logic as well as ﬁrst-order logic (FOL). However, to
establish common ground, we brieﬂy introduce some fundamentals of each.
In logic, an atom is an atomic formula, i.e. a formula that is a terminal symbol in the grammar for
that logic. In AP, an atom expresses a statement about the domain, for instance that some speciﬁc
light is turned on. In propositional logic, this corresponds to a proposition such as LightBulbOn
or BlockOnTable, whereas it is a predicate in FOL, e.g. OnTable(Block1) or On(LightBulb2).
A literal is an atom or its negation, and it is used to denote the truth value of some proposition
(propositional logic) or predicate (FOL). In FOL, literals can be ground or not. An example of a
non-ground literal is On(item1, item2), where the predicate in this literal is On, and the literal
describes that some object item1 is on item2. Here, item1 and item2 are function symbols and not
actual objects in a domain. In natural language, we would simply say that item1 is on item2. Literals
are ground by using speciﬁc constants in place of these variables, e.g. if we have two constants in a
domain, Box and Table, a ground literal could be On(Box, Table). In general, we can distinguish
between constant and function symbols in that constant symbols begin with an uppercase letter, and
function symbols begin with a lowercase letter. If a predicate has cardinality 0, or its literal is ground,
it is equivalent to a proposition in logic. We can combine several literals with logical connectives,
e.g. On(Block, Table) ∧¬On(Table, Block).
2.3.2
Applying logic in Planning
A state is a conjunction of ground literals, i.e. the statements that hold true at some certain point
in time. All states must contain at most one literal of each atom in the problem. Ground literals
that are not explicitly part of the state are implicitly FALSE in that state, i.e. a state never contains
a negative literal. This is also known as database semantics or as the Closed-World Assumption.
In a simple Blocks World setting as the left one seen in Figure 2.1, the state is OnTable(B) ∧
OnTable(C) ∧On(A, B) ∧Clear(A) ∧Clear(C). This implicitly means that B is not clear, and
A is not on the table, i.e. ¬Clear(B) ∧¬OnTable(A) is also part of the state.
Often, it is more convenient to consider a state to be a set of ground literals rather than a

conjunction of the same. That means that only the literals contained in the set hold true in the
state.
There is isomorphism between the two representations of states, and this holds in gen-
eral for sets and conjunctions of literals. So in the above example, the state could be written as
{OnTable(B), OnTable(C), On(A, B), Clear(A), Clear(C)}. By the semantics of conjunction,
the two deﬁnitions of state representations are equivalent. We use both and thus identify any set of
atoms or literals with the conjunction of its members. It should be clear from context which repre-
sentation is being used, usually by application of a certain operator such as entailment or subset.
Deﬁnition 2.3.1 (State). A state is a conjunction of the literals that are TRUE in that state of some
world. The same state can also be a set of those same literals according to the isomorphism between
conjunctions and sets. The Closed-World Assumption is used for states such that negative literals are
implicitly part of the state unless their positive counterpart is explicitly given.
The constant symbols with which we ground literals are also called objects in PDDL. Any object
may have a speciﬁc object type such as block or car. Some objects are considered domain constants,
i.e. they exist in every problem in the domain and can be contained in ground literals.
An action is a function that takes some world state s and transforms it into some other state
s′. In PDDL, an action is deﬁned by its action schema. We allow actions to be conditional in the
sense described by [25].
That is, the action schemata we are concerned with contain two parts,
preconditions and eﬀects. The preconditions for action schema a are written as Pre(a) and are a
conjunction of non-ground literals. Action a is said to be applicable to state s given that s satisﬁes
all preconditions in a, that is s ⊨Pre(a). The behaviour of performing an inapplicable action is
undeﬁned.
Action eﬀects are written as Effects(a) and are a set of tuples where each element in the
set describes a conditional eﬀect. An eﬀect e contains two conjunctions of literals: the conditions,
Con(e), and the conditional eﬀects, ConEffects(e). In practice, we write these as Con(e)) ⇒
(ConEffects(e). For instance, if Con(e) = a∧b and ConEffects(e) = c∧¬d, we write (a∧b) ⇒
(c ∧¬d).
We deﬁne the add set for a conditional eﬀect, Add(e), as the set of literals that occur positively
in ConEffects(e) and the delete set, Del(e), as those that occur negatively. Con(e) describes the
literals required in the state in order for the eﬀects in ConEffects(e) to take place. If there are no
conditions, the conditional eﬀects always occur. Add(e) and Del(e) describe that the literals are
added and deleted from the state, respectively, if the action can be performed (i.e. the preconditions
are satisﬁed), and the conditions are satisﬁed for that eﬀect.
An action schema can have zero or more parameters. An action is an instance of an action schema
where all literal parameters have been ground by constants under uniﬁcation with the literals of the
preconditions and eﬀects. An action can have conditional eﬀects whose parameters are quantiﬁed
by the conditions and therefore not ground in the action instance. An action schema may deﬁne
object type restrictions on its parameters as well as condition parameters such that it can only be
instantiated with objects of a certain type, and conditions have to be satisﬁed with objects of a
certain type. For instance, if some object Truck1 has object type vehicle, we write Truck1 : vehicle.
When we perform an applicable action in a state, the action changes the truth values of literals
in the world and thus changes the world state. We deﬁne this change through the Result function.

Planning Domains and Problems
Finally, we can formally describe a planning domain and problem in the following way:
Deﬁnition 2.3.3 (Planning Domain). A planning domain is a tuple ⟨C, P, A, T⟩where
• C is a possibly empty set of constant symbols.
• P is the set of non-ground literals.
• A is the set of action schemata.
• T is a possibly empty set of object types.
Propositional planning domains are a special case in which C = ∅, and the arity of all literals
and action schemata is 0. That is, all literals are equivalent to propositions, and action schemata
correspond to actions since the are no function symbols.4
Deﬁnition 2.3.4 (Planning Problem). A planning problem in a given domain is a tuple ⟨D, O, I, G⟩
where
• D is the domain described as above.
• O is a set of objects that exist in the problem deﬁnition and their object type if they have any, e.g.,
∀i ∈1..10 (Blocki : block) in blocks world.
• I is the initial state of the problem, i.e. the set of ground literals that hold initially.
• G is a set of ground literals called the goal literals. Every relevant ground literal should be part of
the goal literals, even if negated. That is, there is no Closed-World Assumption on the goal state.
A goal state is any state s for which s ⊨G. The planning problem is considered solved once a goal
state is reached.5
In propositional planning, O = ∅.
The output of a planner is a solution to a planning problem, unless the planner is unable to
solve the problem, either due to lack of soundness or completeness or because of time limitations. A
solution is a plan P, which is an ordered list of actions performed from the initial state that results
in a state satisfying the goal literals. A planner is said to be sound if it only produces plans whose
actions do in fact reach a goal state from the initial state. A planner is said to be complete if, given
inﬁnite time and resources, it will eventually solve any solvable planning problem in its domain.

2.3.5
Types of Planners and Planning Domains
AP captures a wide variety of problems in the sense that there are many variable parameters of a
domain that can drastically change the quality and inherent structures of plans. We will list some
of them below.
Number of agents
Part of most planning domains is some deﬁnition of agents, which are the
actors that perform actions. Some AP problems only have a single agent. Others allow several to
cooperate in order to achieve a common goal, which could either be impossible for a single agent to
achieve or much less eﬃciently solvable. This creates the distinction between single- and multi-agent
systems, many aspects of which have been very thoroughly researched. In this thesis, we will focus
solely on single-agent domains and planners.
Non-determinism
In the above description of action schemata, all actions were assumed to be
deterministic. That is, the execution of a speciﬁc action a from some state s has exactly one possible
resulting state, which is the return value of Result(s, a). If actions were instead non-deterministic,
the agent would not know the return value of Result(s, a) before execution. This introduces an
unpredictable layer of complexity.
Oﬀ- or online
Oﬄine planning refers to calculating the entirety of a plan before performing a
single action. For deterministic, fully observable problems, this is an ideal way of planning since the
outcome of all actions can be fully predicted by the agent(s), and they can therefore simply simulate
the domain before performing any steps. However, oﬄine planning can seem somewhat Utopian.
The real world is unpredictable in many ways, even in limited environments such as train networks
or automated warehouses; and often, especially in multi-agent systems, it is infeasible to precompute
every possible outcome because of an exponential increase in the size of the state space. Planners
that have to operate in these types of domains often use online planning to some extent. Online
planners are complementary to oﬄine planners. That is, they do not necessarily recalculate their
plans in every step, but they consider what they perceive throughout execution of a plan, and, if
need be, they attempt to replan. In partially observable and non-deterministic environments, this is
more or less a necessity because agents have no guarantees as to how the state of the world will be
after applying any part of the plan.
Russell and Norvig [37] actually deﬁne online planning in terms of the agent not having knowledge
about the output of Result(s, a) unless it is in state s and performs a.6 This is equivalent to either
being in a partially observable or a non-deterministic environment.
Domain-dependent and -independent planners
We distinguish between planners that are
restricted to operating within a limited number of domains, i.e.
they are domain-dependent, or
those that can operate within any domain. Generally, domain-independent planners are less eﬃcient
because almost all domains contain some inherent biases that are not represented very well by only
the available literals and must instead be accounted for in some other way, often by heuristics (see
Section 2.4.2). In Blocks World, we as humans know that blocks should be stacked on top of one
another in some certain order. As an example, assume that some goal state requires blocks A, B,
C to be placed in that order from the table up. A domain-independent planner may think that it

Observability
A planning problem is said to have full observability if the agents receive full knowl-
edge about the truth values of all literals in the domain at every time step. Many planners use this
assumption. In reality, agents rarely have full knowledge of everything happening in the environment.
A simple example in a Euclidean domain would be a robot observing the world through sensors or
cameras that cannot look around corners, and the robot will therefore not have knowledge about
anything in areas that are out of sight. Contingent planning problems are a group of planning prob-
lems in which the initial state and action eﬀects are (partially) unknown, and there is only partial
observability. If a planning problem only allows for partial observability, agents must somehow reason
about the possible worlds that they could currently be in. This is done by means of belief states, i.e.
sets of states of which the agent only knows that one of them is the actual current state of the world,
but not which one [23]. Partially observable problems are not considered solved unless every state in
the belief state fulﬁls every goal literal. Explicitly enumerating all states in the belief state requires
exponential space in the number of literals with unknown truth assignment. Instead, we can describe
belief states in terms of logical formulae such as in Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) or Disjunctive
Normal Form (DNF). A special case of contingent planning is conformant planning, in which there
is no perceptual information at any point [37] [24]. This requires planners to compute an entire
plan prior to any execution, resulting in a belief state that is certain to satisfy the goal conditions.
Contingent planners typically provide conditional plans through a decision tree that depends on per-
ceptual input during execution. Clearly, partially observable domains are much harder to control
and solve planning problems within than classical planning domains. Section 3.2 introduces current
state-of-the-art research within contingent planning.
2.4
Solving Planning Problems
2.4.1
Search Algorithms
Planners ﬁnd problem solutions by means of searching through the state space. Naïve search algo-
rithms include Breadth-First Search (BFS) and Depth-First Search (DFS). Generally, planners use
some sort of informed search, often Best-First search, which introduces an evaluation function f(n)
that guides the search. We expect the reader to know these, so they will only be explained brieﬂy.
While exploring, an informed search algorithm will explore node n for which an evaluation func-
tion, f(n), has the lowest value. A state is considered a node. This evaluation function is an estimate
of the amount of steps required to reach a goal state from the initial state. Typically, it is made up
of the two functions: g(n), which measures the amount of steps taken from the initial state to reach
n, and the heuristic evaluation function, h(n), which estimates the length of a plan from node n to a
goal state. Best-First Search can be split into three strategies that evaluate f(n) slightly diﬀerently:
• A⋆: f(n) = g(n) + h(n)
• Greedy: f(n) = h(n)
• Weighted A⋆: f(n) = g(n) + k · h(n) for some k ∈N. Notice that k = 0 corresponds to BFS and

k = 1 to A⋆.
Intuitively, Greedy Best-First Search completely disregards how many steps are required to reach
an explored node, but simply places it at the front of the priority queue if it is closer to the goal
state according to the heuristic function, while A⋆attempts to ﬁnd a shorter path by considering the
solution length so far. Weighted A⋆puts itself somewhere in between the two depending on its bias
k.
2.4.2
Heuristics
Several generalised techniques for constructing good heuristics have been created.
They rely on
relaxations of the problem. When we relax a problem, we remove some restrictions from the domain.
A relaxation is always computationally more feasible to work with than the actual problem while
still keeping most resemblances to the actual domain. We can do this in many ways. For instance,
a relaxation on Blocks World could allow agents to move blocks even if they are not clear.
Heuristics are often based on relaxed versions of problems. A commonly used relaxation-based
heuristic is delete-relaxation, which is a domain-independent relaxation performed by disregarding
all negative eﬀects from action schemata. That is, ∀a (Del(a) = ∅).
Relaxations are also easily applied to domain-dependent planners, which tend to have very speciﬁc
heuristic functions that can account for implicit domain knowledge that is not represented otherwise.
In a Euclidean domain, using Manhattan distance as (part of) the heuristic function is an example
of a domain-speciﬁc relaxation. If agents cannot move through walls, however, Manhattan distance
would consider two spots next to each other separated only by a wall to be very close, showing that
even domain-dependent heuristics can be diﬃcult to construct.
A heuristic is admissible if it never over-estimates the cost of reaching a goal state. Formally,
if h⋆(n) denotes the optimal cost of reaching a goal node from node n, then if ∀n (h(n) ≤h⋆(n))
the heuristic is admissible. Manhattan distance is admissible for Euclidean domains. A⋆search is
guaranteed to ﬁnd optimal solutions with an admissible heuristic. It is very common to sacriﬁce
admissibility for faster computation [37].
2.4.3
Decomposition of Tasks
Many planning problems become infeasible to solve using only informed search. Therefore, many
planners decompose the tasks of the problem into smaller tasks. There are several approaches, the
most straightforward one being goal decomposition. Each goal literal is considered a subgoal and is
solved individually through informed search. Once a subgoal has been solved, the planner attempts
to leave this subgoal fulﬁlled while achieving the remaining ones. Other task decompositions include
hierarchical task networks, in which tasks are recursively broken down into subgoals until a search
can fulﬁl them.

3.1
Action Model Learning In Planning
Creating plans for problems in AP is generally done by reasoning about states through correct and
complete domain knowledge, for instance about action models. However, deﬁning such models can be
a tedious, time consuming, and error-prone task, even for domain experts. An alternative approach,
which part of the AI community has been pursing for exactly these reasons, is to learn these models
through machine learning instead of hand-coding them.1 The diﬀerent approaches being researched
can be classiﬁed by degree of domain observability (full or partial observability) and stochasticity of
action eﬀects (deterministic or probabilistic). The interleavings of these diﬀerences pave the way for
categorising learning models into four areas.
The scenario with full observability and deterministic action eﬀects, also called classical plan-
ning, is the simplest of these four, and therefore learning in this setting is also simpler. Every state
s is fully observable, i.e. we have full knowledge of every literal at all times, and every action a
behaves consistently. Simplicity of the scenario stems from bounded theoretical complexity and ef-
ﬁcient planning algorithms. Classical planning does, however, suﬀer from poor expressiveness. By
this we mean that the requirements are extremely strict and are therefore hard to adhere to in many
real-world applications.
In the scenario with partial observability and deterministic action eﬀects, we cannot fully
observe every literal in every state, but actions still have consistent behaviour.
This makes the
action models of a greater set of real-world domains eligible for learning. Learning these models is
a greater challenge since the eﬀects of applied actions cannot be observed. Walsh and Littman [43]
work within this scenario. They assume parts of the action model are given initially, for instance the
preconditions, and then they reason about determining the remaining parts. The ARMS algorithm
described by Yang et. al. [46] assumes no initial knowledge about action schemata but instead uses
the initial state and the goal literals to set up a max-satisﬁability (MaxSAT) problem.
Action model learning in full observability with probabilistic action eﬀects is researched by
Pasula et. al. [35] who learn a relational planning rule representation that can model noisy, non-
deterministic action eﬀects.

Partially Observable Non-Deterministic (POND) [17], Fully Observable Non-Deterministic (FOND)

problems into classical planning problems to be solved by generic classical planners [15] [3].
As previously mentioned, explicit representation of all possible states in belief states is infeasible,
so all of the above mentioned planners use some form of propositional logic representation based on
the literals of the problem as belief state instead and revise beliefs through novel algorithms of their
own. The known and unknown literals of a belief state can be inferred by means of satisﬁability
(SAT) solving. Say some belief state represented propositional formula ϕ, and we want to check
whether we know literal l, we can SAT solve the formula ϕ ∧¬l. If the result is UNSAT, l holds in the
belief state. If the result is SAT, the truth value of l is either unknown, or l has value FALSE in the
state. Testing the latter would require another call to the SAT solver. SAT is a known NP-complete
problem, and creating eﬃcient solvers is an entire ﬁeld of research in itself. The International SAT
Competition has promoted research into eﬃcient solvers, and the competing solvers are generally
considered the current state-of-the-art.3 Frequently used open source solvers include lingeling and
its numerous versions such as plingeling and treengeling [10].4 It is preferable to attempt simple SAT
solving internally. Calls to external solvers are often more computationally expensive than internal
SAT solving by several orders of magnitude because a large string representing the current formula
has to be written to an external process, which needs to be started. Therefore, measures are taken
to ensure as few of such calls as possible. In the following, we will not go into detail about how
each planner does so, but since most of their belief state formulae are in CNF, the techniques are
similar. Firstly, it is often possible to reason about known literals between belief states through
the action schemata, so the truth value of some literals in the state can be cached. Secondly, unit
clauses in CNF provide direct knowledge of some truth values, and unit propagation in CNF formula
representations is straight-forward to implement, so most decide to do so. Unit propagation allows
the planner to extract knowledge from larger clauses by utilising unit clauses in the formula [50].
The above contingent planners generally contain some measure, often a heuristic search, guiding
them towards the goal, which predicts and reasons about known literals in neighbouring belief states
in order to decide the next actions from some state. As with most classical planners, admissibility of
heuristics is often sacriﬁced for runtime performance. In the following, we describe two of the current
state-of-the-art approaches.

3.3
Learning Techniques
In learning systems in general, learning algorithms vary greatly in applicability. In the following, we
describe diﬀerent learning techniques in the context of action model learning.
Oﬄine learning
is when the system learns prior to the planning phase and is usually performed
by analysing traces of complete and correct plans. It allows the system to completely decouple the
learning and planning phases, thus not adding extra overhead to already time-consuming planning.
One drawback of oﬄine learning is that since all learning is done before plan execution, if a poorly
deﬁned domain is given, or the traces do not cover a variety of actions, the planner may get stuck
and will not be able to recover. That is, the planner is not informed enough about the domain, and
thus cannot reasonably make a plan [5].
Online learning
is learning during the execution phase. The system learns every time an action
is applied throughout the entire execution. Such learning can keep going indeﬁnitely and thus have
continuous learning and improvements.
This is especially valuable when a domain can undergo
changes, or if no good plan traces are available from the get-go. The signiﬁcant drawback to this is
that, since learning is done simultaneously with the planning, the cost of learning is added to the
planning cost, increasing the duration of the execution. On the contrary, if action models have very
low granularity prior to planning, learning during execution may be necessary to feasibly generate a
plan [5].
Another way to look at diﬀerences in learning in AP is to look at what type of learning is
performed.
Inductive Learning is learning from experience.
This could be learning a general
function or a rule among other things. Such learned knowledge is not guaranteed to be correct.

3.5
Macro Actions
A macro action, as commonly deﬁned in planning, is an ordered sequence of speciﬁc atomic actions
within the domain. Macro actions are identiﬁed by ﬁnding actions that frequently occur together
in that speciﬁc order. One area of action model learning focuses on this. Choosing eﬀective macro
actions can lead to signiﬁcant performance increases in planners, while ineﬀective macro actions have
the opposite eﬀect due to the increased branching factor stemming from the additional actions. This
relationship leads to the utility problem that is described in Deﬁnition 3.5.1 [26] [51].
Deﬁnition 3.5.1 (Utility Problem). The utility problem is the trade-oﬀbetween the reduction in the
depth of a search and the increased branching factor that results in a negative payoﬀ.
Macro action learning has been widely researched. Initial work in macro actions started with
an extension of the STRIPS language, known as ABSTRIPS by Fikes and Nilsson [21], that uses
previously completed plans as macro actions in solving larger subsequent plans. This idea is extended
by Minton [31], who introduces the concepts of S- and T-macros with the planner MORRIS. S-macros
(script macros) are frequently used sequences, while T-macros (trick macros) are action sequences
that model some weakness in the search heuristic of the problem. While not used as much, Minton
found that T-macros oﬀer a greater increase in planning eﬃciency.
More recent work by Coles et.
al.
[18] introduces the heuristic planner MARVIN that uses
hill climbing algorithms and a combination of Least-Bad-First-Search and macro actions to escape
plateaux.

3.7
Traces and Teachers
A trace is a plan that has already been performed by a planner. It details the sequence of actions
performed from the initial state until the goal state is reached. A plan trace can either be ideal or
noisy.
• An ideal plan trace shows the world exactly as it was when the plan was executed, with no
discrepancies from the real world.
• A noisy plan trace is not ideal in the sense that it can have missing or wrong state information.
Such plan traces relate closely to partially observable domains.
Learning by using a plan trace is a type of oﬄine learning as described above.
Walsh and Littman [43] propose an algorithm that has access to a teacher that can be queried for
a correct plan trace. Their algorithm can use the trace to learn preconditions and eﬀects of actions
available in the domain.
3.8
Simultaneous Learning and Filtering
Amir and Chang suggest a unique framework for action model learning in deterministic, partially
observable domains with their Simultaneous Learning And Filtering (SLAF) algorithm [4]. SLAF
outputs the set of possible transition relations when given a sequence of actions and partial obser-
vations. Such a sequence is usually the trace of another agent. SLAF is online and continuously
updates a propositional logical formula that represents the possible transition relations and world
states at every time step. Each component of the formula is updated separately in linear time for
every time step, thus making every time step update take linear time in the size of the input formula.
Amir and Chang present diﬀerent versions of SLAF that work under particular assumptions. An
assumption across all versions is that actions have no conditional eﬀects. The SLAF algorithm works
by ﬁrst applying progression on actions followed by ﬁltering based on what is observed. The assump-
tions that diﬀer between algorithm versions include knowledge of action preconditions, observation
of action failures, and guaranteed action success.
The SLAF semantics are deﬁned by Amir and Chang as in Deﬁnition 3.8.1.

2. SLAF[o](ρ) = {⟨s, R⟩∈ρ | o is TRUE in s};

Learning Observation Models
In AP, the usual approach for providing percepts in partial observability, especially in action model
learning, is to pick a number of observations uniformly at random from the full set of possible
observations [4] [1]. While this generally models what partial observability is, it does not correctly
encapsulate a lot of real-world applications where observations are not random. The literature often
considers partial observability to be an error resulting from poor hardware such as faulty sensors.
The perspective we introduce is that real scenarios that contain partial observability often do so
in the form of some function. A common example would be a robot whose sensors only allow it
to sense the environment immediately within its line of sight. This means that such an observation
function is deterministic given a speciﬁc state of the world, so it should be possible to learn it through
experience. We refer to the act of learning to predict or model these observations as Observation
Model Learning. We claim that most real-world planning problems under partial observability are
subject to some observation function.
Knowledge of an observation model is relevant in several diﬀerent cases. Imagine a shop assistant
robot that has to ensure that every item in stock is displayed in the shop. To do so, it wanders
around the shop and observes which items are missing on the shelves. When an item is missing, the
robot should go to the stock to ﬁgure out whether there is any stock left. One solution to this may
be to hardcode the location of every item’s shelving unit and placement in the shop. Such a solution
is volatile because placements are rarely static since stock items change, and the shop employees may
move things around for ease of access or any number of reasons. If the robot were able to learn the
locations of items dynamically, it could run more or less indeﬁnitely without software updates.
4.1
Observation Function
Let us return to the Light Switch domain introduced in Section 2.3.4. Assume that the agent knows
the action schemata of the domain. Learning the observation model corresponds to the agent ﬁguring
out what it is actually able to observe according to which room it is in. Speciﬁcally, the intent is to
learn which atoms are perceivable in each state, in this case whether switches and lights are on or
oﬀ. In Light Switch, the underlying observation model is intuitive: the agent can perceive everything
in the room that it is in, including the states of light bulbs and switches. In order for a planner
to solve a problem, it should be certain that the agent is in a goal state. Unless it knows how to
observe the truth values of literals, it can either wander aimlessly until it eventually observes them
or intentionally perform actions that guarantee certain truth values of literals either through eﬀects
or through knowledge inferred from the belief state as described in section 3.2.1. With knowledge of
the observation model, the agent can speciﬁcally aim to observe the goal literals without necessarily
performing convoluted plans and may be able to take this into account in its guiding heuristic.

4.1.1
Considerations
When we discuss learning observation models, we seek to generalise our methodology as much as
possible. Our main assumption is that all action schemata are known.
Plan traces
Some action model learning methods seek to learn during planning, while others use plan traces
provided by some expert or teacher where the initial state and goal state are known, for instance.
We can do either in observation model learning as well, but since we have known actions, the learning
method does not have to diﬀer between the two. Taking advantage of plan traces can be very useful
for learning observation models, as such traces are complete plans in a usually fairly direct path

towards a goal state. It may, however, provide more information to learn the observation models

that has idle time, during which it could be exploring in order to improve its internal observation

Limitations of Traditional Contingent Planning
We now introduce an additional domain that highlights the weakness of traditional contingent plan-
ning, which is that such planners do not take actions into account whose eﬀects are purely observa-
tional in practice.
Example 4.1.2 (Contingent n × n grid). Let the Grid GPS domain be deﬁned by a grid of n × n
cells, in which the agent knows the size of the grid but has no knowledge of its own initial position in
the grid. The goal is for the agent to be in a speciﬁc cell.
To solve this problem, the agent would move n steps to either the right or left followed by n steps
either up or down to make sure that the agent is in one of the four corner cells of the grid. From
there, it can navigate to the desired goal cell. No matter the approach and actual initial position, the
agent has to perform at least 2n actions to be certain that it has indeed solved the problem.
Example 4.1.2 shows that conformant planners are in fact able to solve certain problems by
performing some number of actions ensuring the agent to be in a speciﬁc set of states even though
there is no perceptual input.
Assume now that this domain also has an action check-gps(), which ensures that the location
of the agent is included in the next percept. In classical PDDL speciﬁcations, there is no way to
model that this action provides the agent with its position aside from deﬁning a literal that tells the
agent that it has looked at the GPS such as gps-on. check-gps() has no preconditions, so it can
be performed from any position on the grid.
The check-gps literal still does not actually give the agent its current position, but merely
informs it that is has looked at the GPS. With observation model learning, we can learn that whenever
we know this literal to be TRUE, the current position of the agent is observed with the next percept,
which enables us to use that knowledge during planning.
Preliminary Deﬁnitions
Learned observation models are unique to domains and problem settings.
Deﬁnition 4.1.3 (Problem Setting). A problem setting is deﬁned by the objects and the truth values
of the rigid literals in that problem. Two problems are said to have the same problem setting if and
only if they have the same set of objects, and they have the same truth values of all rigid literals in
the initial state.
For instance, in Light Switch, a certain light bulb may be in Roomx in one setting, but in another
in Roomy, and thus two diﬀerent observation models have to be learned for these.
Deﬁnition 4.1.4 (Observation Model Scope). An observation model is unique to its problem setting.

In the next sections, we will need the following deﬁnitions.
Deﬁnition 4.1.5 (Complete Term). A complete term over a ﬁnite set of propositional literals P is
a conjunction of literals from P such that every literal from P appears either positively or negatively
exactly once.
Every state can be represented as a corresponding complete term of the propositional literals, P,
of its problem.
Deﬁnition 4.1.6 (Partial State). Let s be a state deﬁned by a set of literals that is a complete term.
A partial state ϕ ⊆s is a subset of state s, i.e. a set of literals that does not necessarily have to
be a complete term. Thus, the Closed-World Assumption does not apply to partial states. As is the
case with states, a partial state may also be represented as a conjunction of those same literals.
Deﬁnition 4.1.7 (Overloading Entailment). Given belief state b and partial state ϕ represented as
a conjunction of literals, let b ⊨ϕ denote that ϕ is TRUE in all states in belief state b.
Furthermore, given some set O containing relations between partial states and atoms, for some

4.2
In Classical Planning
In this section we show how to naïvely learn an observation model for any classical planning domain
with known action models. We remind the reader that in classical planning – as opposed to contingent
planning – the agent has full knowledge of the initial state. This, along with known action eﬀects,
means that percepts do not provide additional insight into the state of the problem, but since we
are attempting to learn observation models, we assume that they are still provided. For the sake of
generalisation, the agent represents the state it is in at any given time as a belief state even though
it will always be a singleton in this setting.
We will iteratively introduce complexity to the following observation model learning technique.

Space Complexity
The worst case space complexity for each of SC and IC in the sound observation model are the same
as the space bounds for the entire complete observation model, so the space complexity of the sound
model remains the same, at least expressed in terms of the input. However, we can instead attempt
to express the size of SC in terms of the minimal size it can achieve.
We refer to Section 4.4.2 for a proof that any observation function for a given setting can be
expressed as a collection of formulae in DNF where each formula expresses conditions required in
order to observe some atomic proposition. Each clause of such a formula corresponds to a single
observation condition in an element of SC. Say there are |P| of these formulae, one for each atomic
proposition, for a minimal representation of the observation function (if all propositions can be
observed), and on average they have n clauses of average length m. Let M be the minimal set of
suﬃcient conditions that corresponds to such a formula. That is, M contains n sets of literals, and
each set of M consists of m literals on average. Thus, the minimum size of suﬃcient conditions that
capture the observability of a single atomic proposition is n · m where n ≤2|P|, m ≤|P|.
For simplicity, assume now that we have explored all reachable states, and that we are not
performing reduction by contradiction on any elements of SC. This means that the size of every
observation condition is |P|, where P is the set of all atomic propositions in the problem. There are
at most 2|P| of such conditions for each observation atom p in SC. Therefore, the total size used to
represent the observability of a single atom is |P| · 2|P|. For now, we stick to considering the size of
all conditions for a single proposition.
In summary, the size of all the conditions for a single atom in SC that we generated is larger than
the minimal representation by a factor no greater than |P|·2|P |
n·m . However, suﬃcient conditions are
only ever added if the partial state contains all m literals necessary to observe the atom according to
a condition from M. This means that if we know that all conditions of M contain at least k literals,
our representation is at most |P|·2|P |−k

4.3

The previous sections assumed a completely known initial state and known action schemata. In
reality, knowing the initial states and the action schemata completely removes uncertainty from the
belief states of agents in single-agent domains with determinism, which means that knowledge of an
observation model provides little to no additional insight. To improve upon the previous sections,
we now introduce the generalised observation model that encompasses observation knowledge given
uncertainty about belief states.

4.4.1
Observation Model Updates Maintain Soundness
The observation model aims to maintain soundness for suﬃcient and insuﬃcient conditions. Infor-
mally, by soundness, we mean that we do not add observation elements to the suﬃcient conditions
that are not always suﬃcient in order to observe the observation atom. Similarly, we do not add
observation elements to the insuﬃcient conditions whose observation conditions are in some cases
suﬃcient for observing their observation atom. We claim that this also holds in the classical case, but
show it speciﬁcally for the contingent model using the deﬁnitions of updates from Deﬁnition 4.3.3
and determinism of observations from Deﬁnition 4.1.2.
Theorem 4.4.1 (Insuﬃcient Conditions can Never be Suﬃcient). For a problem with initial state
I and an observation element (ϕ, p) ∈IC, it holds that there exists some state s reachable from I
where ϕ ⊆s and p /∈Observation(s).
Proof. Assume that there exists an observation element x = (ϕ′, p′) ∈IC but no state s′ reachable
from I where ϕ′ ⊆s′ and p /∈Observation(s′). According to UpdateIC, when adding x to IC at
time ti, either:
1. x was not already in IC: Let the agent’s belief state be b at time ti when the actual state was si.
It holds that b ⊨ϕ′, so by Deﬁnition 4.1.7, ϕ′ ⊆si. It also holds that p′ /∈Observation(si). In
summary, the agent reached si from I, ϕ′ ⊆si, and p′ /∈Observation(si). This contradicts our
assumption that no such state exists.
2. x was already in IC: x was added to IC at time tj, j < i under the above conditions. Inductively,
x was never added to IC at time tj.
Theorem 4.4.2 (Suﬃcient Conditions are Never Insuﬃcient). For a problem with initial state I and
an observation element (ϕ, p) ∈SC, it holds that there exists no state s reachable from I where ϕ ⊆s
and p /∈Observation(s), assuming no reduction by contradiction.
Proof. Assume there exists a state s′ reachable from I and some x = (ϕ′, p′) ∈SC such that ϕ′ ⊆s′
but p /∈Observation(s′). According to UpdateSC, when adding x to SC at time ti, either:
1. x was not already in SC: The agent had a singleton belief state b such that all literals were known
at time ti; all literals in ϕ′ were known from b; and p′ was observed in s′. From determinism of
Observation, Observation(s′) always contains p′. This is a contradiction.
2. x was already in SC: x was added to SC at time tj, j < i under the above conditions. Inductively,
x was never added to SC at time tj.

Reduction by Contradiction Maintains Soundness
We now prove that the proposed algorithm for reducing the size of suﬃcient conditions maintains

Theorem 4.4.4 (Reduction by Contradiction Maintains Soundness). Reduction by contradiction
applied to any sound suﬃcient condition set CS will return a new sound condition set CS′.
Proof. Say reduction by contradiction has been applied to a condition set CS, producing the new con-
dition set CS′ by removing the observation elements (ϕ1, p) and (ϕ2, p) where RBC((ϕ1, p), (ϕ2, p)) =
(ϕnew, p) and replacing them with (ϕnew, p). Assume also that CS was sound, but CS′ is not. For
that to be the case, the following must hold:
The addition of (ϕnew, p) or removal of either (ϕ1, p) or (ϕ2, p) has made CS′ become unsound,
i.e. an observation query on CS that previously returned TRUE or FALSE now returns the opposite
for CS′. Let P be the set of literals with the same truth assignments in both ϕ1 and ϕ2 and b the
literal of which each of the two sets contains the complement of the other.
As any condition set can be rewritten to formulae in DNF (this is trivial as the inverse trans-
formation of the one performed in the proof to Theorem 4.4.3), let ψ denote the conjunction of all
literals in ϕnew. The rewrite of CS is {(ψ ∧b) ∨(ψ ∧¬b), p}, and equivalently, CS′ can be rewritten
as {(ψ), p}.

4.4.4
Identiﬁability
When a program learns online, it has to be able to assess what has been learned and, if possible, when
its learning is complete. This type of learning categorisation can be split into ﬁnite identiﬁability
and identiﬁability in the limit. Bolander and Gierasimczuk [14] explore both of these notions.
Finite identiﬁability is a property of a learning algorithm. The property says that in ﬁnite time,
the correct model will be learned, and it can be identiﬁed as the correct model. The certainty of
recognising a correct model can be deﬁned as the ﬁnite identiﬁablity function being once-deﬁned, i.e.
it is only allowed to output a model once.
Identiﬁability in the limit is a weaker property. An algorithm with this property is not able to
identify a correct model. However, when a correct model is reached after a ﬁnite number of steps, it
is guaranteed that the algorithm will not make any further changes to the model.
We claim that the updates of suﬃcient conditions proposed in Section 4.2.2 and 4.3, using the
reduction from Section 4.2.3, ensure ﬁnite indentiﬁability if the entire state space is veriﬁably visited
(the agent has explored every state while in a singleton belief state).
Theorem 4.4.5 (Finite Identiﬁability of Suﬃcient Conditions). Given a deterministic domain with
known action models, reversible actions, and known complete state space, the learner will output a
model with ﬁnitely identiﬁable suﬃcient conditions.
Proof. Assume that a deterministic domain with known deterministic action models, reversible ac-
tions, and known complete state space is given. For any problem in such a domain, a singleton belief
state will be reached after a ﬁnite sequence of actions and stay as such because the action models
are known, and therefore no new uncertainty can be added to the domain.
Assume that a ﬁnite sequence of actions that visits every state in the state space has been
performed while the agent has a singleton belief state, and that the learner returns an incorrect
model of suﬃcient conditions SC. Let S denote the full state space. Determinism dictates that any
proposition p observed in a state s ∈S will be observed for every visit to s. This means that for
SC to be incorrect, a relation (s′, p) must have been added to SC when p was not observed in s′.
Theorem 4.4.2 proves that this is not possible, and thus SC must be a correct model when it is
returned.
Although ﬁnite identiﬁability is an excellent quality to have in a learner, having to calculate
the state space of the domain and ensure that it is visited in its entirety is something that quickly
becomes infeasible for anything other than toy domains. For this reason, we consider identiﬁability
in the limit instead.
Identiﬁability in the limit allows inability to recognise a correct model, and in our case, that
means that we do not have to calculate the entire state space of the domain to verify that the model
is correct. It does, however, require that a correct model is achieved after a ﬁnite number of steps
and once reached, it is never altered.
Theorem 4.4.6 (Suﬃcient Conditions are Identiﬁable in the Limit). Given a deterministic domain
with known action models and reversible actions, the learner will produce a model with suﬃcient
conditions that are identiﬁable in the limit.

Proof. A problem in a deterministic domain with known action models and reversible actions is
given. A singleton belief state is reached after a ﬁnite sequence of actions following the same logic
as for the proof to Theorem 4.4.5. As the state space is ﬁnite, every state is eventually visited in
a ﬁnite sequence of actions, and a correct model with suﬃcient condition set SC is produced, once
again following the proof to Theorem 4.4.5. This model is, however, never returned as there is no
veriﬁcation that the entire state space has been visited.
Assume that after a ﬁnite sequence of actions, after having visited every state while in a singleton
belief state, a state s is visited that modiﬁes SC. For that to happen, one of two things must be the
case:
1. s is a new state not previously seen. This is impossible as the entire state space has been visited.
2. The agent observes a new atom p not previously observed in s. For p to be a new propositional
atom observed in s, it has to not have been observed when s was initially visited, which cannot
happen in a domain with deterministic observations.
This shows that SC is identiﬁable in the limit.

Implementation
We have created a Java implementation that we use to learn observation models. We parse PDDL
domains and problems as Java objects for which our two planners ﬁnd plans. During plan execution,
we simulate the problem in the background and return the percepts that should be available to the
planner in the state based on a speciﬁc observation function from the given domain. Observation
functions are unique for every domain, and we decided to simply create Java objects for each and
provide these to the problem simulator. With observation functions and a simulator in place, we built
an observation model learning framework that any contingent planner can use to learn observation
models in the sense that we describe them in Chapter 4.
This chapter describes the major parts of our implementation, considerations behind some crucial
choices, and descriptions of the most important algorithms.
5.1
Planning Framework
Our planning framework parses planning domains with typical STRIPS actions and conditional eﬀects
as well as negative preconditions. We use the standard PDDL format to parse domains, but a custom
format for parsing the planning problems. The two are represented by Domain and Problem Java
objects, respectively. We use uniﬁcation with objects to initialise all ground literals from their non-
ground counterparts. We do the same for all available actions. It is important to note that we do
not perform full uniﬁcation on action eﬀects because their conditions may introduce new arguments
that are not scoped by the parameters of the action itself. Because we only consider deterministic
actions, it should only be possible to ground eﬀect condition arguments in one way from any given
state, but the grounding may diﬀer between states. Uniﬁcation of arguments whose scope is created
by an eﬀect condition is therefore done during action execution.
5.2
Planners
We intended to create an eﬃcient contingent planner based on CFF [25] that would learn observation
models during planning. We found that the resources spent implementing the heuristic search based
on the relaxed version of the problem, including comprehensive algorithms and data structures, were
better spent on our novel approach. Additionally, in order to run a satisfactory amount of benchmarks
and be able to rerun them multiple times, it was preferable to be able to create plans as quickly as
possible. Thus, we settled on creating two planners of our own, one classical and one contingent.
5.2.1
Classical planner
The ﬁrst planner is an intentionally ineﬃcient, generic classical planner that uses DFS. We use DFS
rather than an informed search such as (weighted) A⋆Best-First Search in order to mitigate the
advantages of having full observability and thus better simulate the plans that would be created by
a contingent planner, i.e. longer plans that explore larger parts of the search space before ﬁndinng
a solution. In order to further simulate partial observability planners, the search has two ﬂags that
45
==PAGE:47==
CHAPTER 5. IMPLEMENTATION
5.2. PLANNERS
can be toggled:
• Only state-altering actions: With this ﬂag set, only actions that change the state are added to
the frontier.
• Exploration mode: In exploration mode, the search continues even if the agent achieves all goal
literals.
With the ﬁrst ﬂag disabled, actions whose conditional eﬀects are not applicable are still executed,
but with no change to the state. However, to an agent with partial observability, performing such
an action would change its belief state.
Domains may have irreversible actions, and in those cases, there must not exist a path from a
goal state s of a problem to a diﬀerent state s′ such that there is no path from s′ back to s. If
one such path exists, the DFS will potentially trap the planner in an area of the state space from
which it cannot reach the goal. If the planner’s search attempts to backtrack an irreversible action,
which is not possible, it simply resets its frontier and begins a new search from the state it is in. In
exploration mode, assuming no irreversible actions, the planner explores all states before eventually
stopping.
5.2.2
Contingent planner
Our contingent planner represents belief states in CNF as the CFF planner does [25], i.e. with ground
literals time stamped with the step in which we can infer knowledge about them through axioms. This
is described thoroughly in Section 3.2.1. Its search algorithm is based on the Online-Dfs-Agent
algorithm described by Russell and Norvig [37] (p. 150). Online-Dfs-Agent assumes known action
eﬀects unlike some of the contingent planners mentioned in Section 3.2.1. We perform no relaxation
on the problem, and the search algorithm works almost exactly like the DFS of our classical planner
in terms of plans generated. Upon execution of an action from the planner, a problem simulator
changes the actual state of the problem, and the planner can request a percept from that simulator
in the state and thus update its own belief state, but very importantly also its observation model.
Because the two planners generate similar plans, and we are not really interested in the plans
themselves, but rather how we can update an observation model during plan execution, we decided
to use the classical planner to create plan traces in full observability and afterwards let the contingent
planner simply execute the plans. This type of learning is referred to as learning through plan traces
in the action model learning literature as described in Section 3.3. Only planning with the classical
planner meant that the search algorithm of our contingent planner became somewhat obsolete, and
the main resource is now instead its belief state representation. In contingent planning in general,
planners opt to perform only actions that they know are applicable in the current state since the
behaviour of performing an inapplicable action in general is undeﬁned. By executing plan traces,
we may be performing actions that a contingent planner itself at the given time step would not be
certain were applicable.1
Unit propagation
Every time new knowledge is added to the belief state, we perform unit prop-
agation on the entire CNF formula in order to compactly describe the belief state, allowing for more
eﬃcient SAT solving and thus faster planning. In short, if some unit clause contains ground literal l,

In practice, we can perform unit propagation in O(c · n) time, where c is the number of clauses, and

the other clauses in the set.
SAT solving
In order to eﬃciently test satisﬁability of belief state formulae, we opt to use two
layers, the ﬁrst being a quick test for satisﬁability with unit clauses, and the other being a call to an
evidently good SAT solver. We use the parallel version of lingeling, plingeling, which has performed
well in The International SAT Competitions for several years and is frequently updated [10]. Even
though the overhead in calling a remote process is not insigniﬁcant, plingeling performs very well
in our implementation. We knew that our belief state formula would contain many unit clauses
because they are added by every percept. Because of this, we implemented unit propagation, and
our tests show that the planner runs much faster with unit propagation enabled, mostly because
we can often avoid external process calls, but in large because it allows plingeling to solve problems
almost instantly in most cases whenever called.
5.3
Observation Model Learning
We use a Java class named ObservationModel to store observation models. It contains the three ﬁelds
insufficientConditions, possiblySufficientConditions, and sufficientConditions, each of
which is a HashMap that maps from GroundLiterals to sets of Observations.
The Observation class has a condition ﬁeld, which is a set of GroundLiterals, and a ﬁeld,
observationAtom, which is a GroundLiteral (that always has value true). Additionally, it contains
an entails method taking another Observation that returns true if and only if the two observations
have the same observation atom, and all the conditions of the other observation are contained in this
observation’s condition set.
We update the observation model with its update method that takes the current BeliefState
as well as a Percept. We propose Algorithm 1 that encapsulates the deﬁnitions from Section 4.3 in
order to update the model. We completely omit rigid literals from all observation conditions in our
representations such that diﬀerent problem settings require entirely diﬀerent models.
For performance, all equality, entailment, etc. checks are optimised to perform O(1) time com-
plexity operations ﬁrst, which minimises time spent on unnecessary time-consuming operations such
as equality of sets.

Discussion
This research expands previous research in contingent planning by providing a framework for reason-
ing about observations in systems where partial observability is systematic as opposed to randomised.
In the following, we discuss the results as well as natural next steps in order to apply observation
model learning in practice and improve upon our approach.
Looking at the results of the benchmarks from Section 6, it is clear that our proposed learner has
been able to learn observation models. For all problem settings, the set of possibly suﬃcient conditions
grows initially. Once the agent reaches a singleton belief state, the set of suﬃcient conditions starts to
grow instead, and the possibly suﬃcient conditions are emptied rapidly. When an agent has explored
the full state space with certain knowledge of its state, learning immediately stops.
In most of these toy domains, we are able to quickly explore the full state space through plan
traces. These traces were created by an explorative planner that simulates a robot taking advantage
of its idle time to freely roam its domain in order to learn how observations can be understood.
Although the story of rapid learning followed by an immediate stop is the clear case for the
Light Switch and the Grid GPS domains, the same cannot be said for the Big Vacuum domain.
Here, it proves diﬃcult to completely learn the observation model because the reachable state space
is signiﬁcantly larger. This is mainly due to the high number of permutations of clean tiles, each
enabling the existence of a new state.
This results in very large sets of insuﬃcient conditions,
which require many computational resources. For instance, with a grid size of 4 × 4, the number of
insuﬃcient conditions grows to almost 350.000, corresponding to roughly 350 times the size of SC
and PSC combined. This is before the observation model has even been fully learned. Even if IC is
disregarded, the growth of SC shows no sign of stagnating within the ﬁrst several thousand actions.
If we look at the number of non-rigid literals in each of the domains, it is not immediately clear
why an observation model in the Big Vacuum domain is much harder to learn. The reason seems
to purely be that the action schemata of the domain cause a much larger area of the total state
space to be possible to visit. The same is not the case for any of the other domains, in which several
literals are clearly connected in a trivial pattern through the action schemata, e.g. OnSwitch and
OnLight.
Reduction
Given that IC ends up growing to the size that it does, and the fact that it provides
little to no insight into the inherent observability of atoms, there is a reasonable argument for not
maintaining such a set. It may be possible to reduce the size of IC slightly by performing a reduction
by contradiction similar to the one we perform on SC. We presume that the soundness proof of such
a reduction could be done in a nearly identical manner. Since we did not implement reduction by
contradiction for SC, reduction of IC was never a priority.
Reduction of PSC also seems feasible. The diﬃcult part of such a reduction would be to distin-
guish between two observation elements, one of which was added directly through observation, and
the other of which was added through reduction of two more speciﬁc conditions. Whether or not the
distinction is important is not entirely clear to us. However, since PSC in general seems to remain
the smallest set, the priority of developing a process for this reduction is low.
While the proposed reduction of SC from Section 4.2.3 is correct and in theory seems applicable,
we found that in most domains, it can rarely be applied. Domains have dependencies and relations

literal, so the diﬀerence between two states is almost never merely the diﬀerence in truth value of a

Suﬃciency prediction
In general, it seems that few, if any, possibly suﬃcient conditions end
up being insuﬃcient. This is merely deduced from the fact that the negative slope of PSC in the
graphs of Chapter 6 is never greater than the positive slope of SC at the same time, meaning that
we never remove a large amount of possibly suﬃcient conditions without adding them as suﬃcient
conditions. This result indicates that the existence of PSC has some merit. However, we have no
benchmarks that can show unambiguously that the existence of an element in PSC is actually a
reasonable prediction of it belonging to SC eventually, but doing so would be a strong argument for
the raison d’être of PSC.
Exploration
If a system has actions that are costly to execute, making it too expensive to simply
explore while an agent has no tasks at hand, it is completely possible to learn and train observation
models in parallel with regular planning. This means that there are not necessarily any drawbacks to
adding observation model learning to any system aside from the additional computational resources
used to learn the model. Since good plans rarely explore the entire state space, concurrent learning
might never learn the entire observation model. This is not critical since the part of the would-be
observation model that remains unknown is also seemingly irrelevant to the agent.
Planner integration
It would have been relevant to use a state-of-the-art contingent planner to
generate the plan traces used for learning observation models. Because integration would become
a time-consuming aspect, and because developers of open source planning software such as CFF
explicitly mention that they have no support and scarce documentation, we opted to not pursue this.
Since the initial tests with diﬀerent conﬁgurations of our own planner showed little to no diﬀerence
in results, we expect that the same would have been true with an external planner.
7.1
Relation to Other Work
Our initially proposed approach builds the naïve observation model that incrementally decreases in
size as more observations are received. This is strongly reminiscent of the technique used by Walsh
and Littman in their optimistic algorithm for learning action preconditions [43]. As done in this
thesis, they overestimate the reach of their model initially and proceed to incrementally reduce it
as knowledge is acquired. Their work was our initial inspiration for approximating the observation
model through an over- and an underestimation.
We would have liked to work on using learned observation models in conjunction with a con-
tingent planner, but integration of the approach in a heuristic proved at quite an early stage to be
outside of the scope of this thesis. However, in KACMBP [8], described in Section 3.2.1, so-called
knowledge acquisition conditions are used to guide the heuristic search. Loosely described, a belief
state is a knowledge acquisition condition if it is the immediate neighbour of another belief state that
satisﬁes some condition. We believe that our work with observation models has a close connection
to knowledge acquisition in the sense used by KACMBP. Therefore, observation models can be inte-
grated as a type of knowledge acquisition in order to improve the heuristic used by KACMBP. Such

an extension requires additional research.
Finally, we note that while our work is within automated planning, partial observability also exists
in other domains. Especially POMDPs, described by Kaelbling et. al. [27], have been researched
extensively. POMDPs have a much stronger notion of partial observability, and observation functions
are an inherent part of such systems. Actions are non-deterministic with some probability distribution
for reaching neighbouring states, and observations are similarly non-deterministic with a probability
distribution that describes the observations that will be received in a state. Additionally, conditional
observation probabilities in POMDPs are based upon a state and the action performed to get there,
whereas we assume that an observation depends only on the state.
Therefore, such observation
models only seem somewhat related to the work we do, and we have not pursued them any further.
7.2
Future Work
The proposed approach to learning observation models is the foundation for a lot of additional
research on the topic. We considered adding many of the following ideas to the work of this thesis,
but due to time limitations we did not pursue them. However, we believe that they have interesting
prospects.
Planning algorithm
One obvious choice for future work on our approach of building observation
models is to develop a planning algorithm to take advantage of the observation model and create
better plans. One possibility is an alteration to KACMBP as previously described. Another is that
with a learned observation model, a planning algorithm could use the model to assist with updates of
belief states. For instance, if SC is complete and contains some set of observation conditions Σ that
are suﬃcient for observing atom p, and we reach state s1 where p is observed, we know that at least
one of the observation conditions in Σ are part of the current state. Conversely, if we do not observe
p, we know that none of the partial states in Σ are part of the state. This could be used to describe
the belief state with a probability distribution rather than having no assignment of probability to
its states. We note that POMDP belief states are probability distributions, but have not found AP
research that deals with belief states in this way.
Probing
An algorithm that probes literals could also be a useful application. The intended meaning
of probing is that if the truth value of a literal in the goal literals is unknown, the planner attempts
to check its truth value rather than perform a (possibly long and complicated) plan to ensure the
truth value of the literal. For instance, if a sequence of 10 actions is required to ensure that the
literal is TRUE, but it already holds initially and checking this only requires a single action, it would
be a signiﬁcant improvement to probe the literal ﬁrst. This sort of problem is clearly quite speciﬁc,
but some classes of problems have a structure that could beneﬁt from such an algorithm, examples
being shopping for supplies or ﬁnding a parking spot.
Improved reduction
Reduction of the condition sets in our approach did not work with the
intended eﬀectiveness, and we consider it a shortcoming of our work.
If we continue to assume
known action models, the eﬀects of each action dictates the sets of literals that change truth values
simultaneously. It might be possible to replace such sets with single literals if they are unaﬀected by
other actions. This would ensure that only a single literal is changed between neighbouring states.
The removal of one or several literals would also cause a general improvement for both planning and
observation model learning. Alternatively, somehow providing the action performed between two
states to the reduction function may enable a sound reduction by contradiction to be performed,

which removes multiple literals from observation conditions. It seems very likely that one or both of
these approaches could work.
Condition probability
If, on the other hand, the assumption of known action schemata is
dropped, it would not be possible to reach a singleton belief state.
This means that suﬃcient
conditions could never be added with assurance of soundness, giving us a much less certain model.
Our learner could in that case be combined with an action model learning framework, and it may be
possible for the two learners to provide mutual beneﬁt. Assuming unknown action schemata, it would
be worthwhile to introduce probabilities to observations in the PSC set to indicate the likelihood of
an element actually being a suﬃcient condition. Such a probability distribution could be dependent
on the frequency of occurrence of the observation element as well as the degree of knowledge in the
belief state in which they were observed.
Lifted models
A possible approach for improving the space eﬃciency would be to lift parts of the
observation model to ﬁrst order logic by coalescing learned relations. This is similar to the discipline
of action schema learning, and we believe that the framework we provide is suﬃcient for applying
inverse substitutions in order to make estimations of generalised observation models.
PDDL description
A natural extension of our implementation and of PDDL would be the ability
to provide observation functions in some input format. As described in Section 4.4.2, any proposi-
tional logic formula can be rewritten to DNF, so observation functions could be provided as a set of
tuples of observation conditions and observation atoms as in our observation model description.
Faulty observations
Another interesting thing to work further on would be to look at how
faulty observations are handled by the learner. A faulty observation could be categorised as non-
deterministic, but it could nevertheless be interesting to keep the assumption of determinism while
still being able to recover from occasional faulty observations.
Compression
The ﬁnal idea for future research is to reduce the size of the model through com-
pression methods while allowing it to remain queryable, sacriﬁcing query time for space. Ideally,
the compression should allow both queries and updates, but settling for either has beneﬁts as well.
Allowing queries would be especially useful for models that have been deemed fully learned but re-
quire a signiﬁcant amount of space. Compression of the model that allows correct updates is also
extremely useful, as it can reduce the space requirement while learning the model. Compact and
queryable data structures such as the one described by Bille et. al. [11] already exist for similar
purposes.

Conclusion
Throughout this thesis, we have introduced a planning framework consisting of STRIPS actions with
conditional eﬀects in a contingent planning setting where action models are assumed to be completely
known. We have presented some of the currently best techniques within action model learning and
summarised current state-of-the-art contingent planners.
The original intent of developing new action model learning techniques instead led us to research
the possibilities of deﬁning a method for agents to identify and reason about what they are able to
observe given their knowledge in a certain state. We refer to this as observation model learning and
present three diﬀerent ways of building such models. We base learning on the assumption that there
is in fact some deterministic observation function that governs what can be observed by an agent
in a certain state. The most obvious such function would be line of sight – agents cannot observe
anything that is not directly within line of sight of their sensors (or, for humans, eyes). All three
observation models operate similarly, in that when queried with a set of ground literals and an atomic
proposition, they return whether or not that atom is observable in a state that satisﬁes these literals.
Observation models are shared among problems in the same problem setting, that is, the rigid literals
of the problems have the same truth values.
The ﬁrst model is almost trivial and works as a building block for the remaining two. We frame
it in a planning setting with known initial state. The idea is based on the naïve assumption that any
atom in a problem can be observed no matter the truth values of the input literals until the opposite
has been proven. This leads to a model that has a quite infeasible size initially, but slowly reduces
in size. This model is complete in the sense that it captures all the connections between literals and
the observable atoms when those literals are TRUE. It is not sound because it may incorrectly predict
the observability of some atom when it cannot in fact be observed. In most domains, while able
to eventually achieve soundness, the model can never reach a minimal size necessary for sound and
complete queries, and we cannot know when soundness is achieved.
We adapt the ﬁrst model to aim for soundness rather than completeness and at the same time
achieve better space bounds. For this model, we assume that nothing is observable unless proven
otherwise, that is we ﬁnd conditions that are suﬃcient to have satisﬁed for observing a certain atom.
A condition is a set of literals. Similarly and simultaneously, we keep track of all conditions that are
insuﬃcient for observing atoms of the problem. The new model is sound rather than complete. We
also give a method for losslessly reducing the size of the model slightly.
Finally, we turn to the contingent case where we do not have knowledge of the initial state, so
agents operate according to their belief state rather than certain knowledge. Since we have known
action models, it is possible for agents to reach certainty of states. We extend the second observation
model by adding the notion of possibly suﬃcient conditions. Those are conditions that we continually
revise during planning and that we have a certain suspicion are relevant for observing their respective
atoms.
Our implementation of the ﬁnal observation model allows us to benchmark the extent to which we
can expect to learn in three diﬀerent toy domains where we created our own deterministic observation
functions. In two of the three domains, possibly suﬃcient conditions are slowly added until certainty
about the state is reached, at which point they start being replaced by suﬃcient conditions. This
shows that possibly suﬃcient conditions have merit in applied learning. While the set of possibly
60
==PAGE:62==

suﬃcient conditions grows, insuﬃcient conditions are steadily added.
Eventually, the size of all
condition sets plateaus, and at this point we consider the models learned. No plateaus are ever
reached in the benchmarks of the third domain. We attribute this to the presence of irreversible
actions in this domain. This means that for new problems in the same problem setting, previously
undiscovered states keep being reached. This is a result of the reachable part of the state space being
much larger.
In conclusion, we have shown that it is possible to learn observation models and reason about
the observations that agents can receive given certain knowledge. We propose many ways in which
our work can be continued. The natural application would be to attempt to use the framework in
a contingent planner, either an existing one or one created speciﬁcally for the purpose. We also see
many possibilities for extension, including, but not limited to: further reductions in model size by
using action eﬀects, learning with unknown action schemata, and lifting models to ﬁrst-order logic.
The models we have created show a novel approach to thinking about observability in contingent
planning, and we hope that our work provides opportunity for future work by ourselves or others.
