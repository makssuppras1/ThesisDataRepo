Abstract
The increasing demand of more and cleaner power production is pushing wind energy
industry to colder and harsher environment where ice accumulates on wind turbine
blades. The research in this thesis is focusing on the effect of three ice geometries
(rime, glaze and horn) located on the leading edge of the NREL S826 airfoil. Further-
more, the DU 06-W-200 airfoil has been used for investigating generalization of horn
ice geometry. The airfoils tested in the wind tunnel is modelled in Autodesk Fusion
360 and 3D printed with FDM method.
The research was conducted by wind tunnel experiments at DTU, Lyngby in
addition to numerical simulations in CD-Adapco’s CFD package StarCCM+. The
glaze ice increased the lift coefficient compared to clean airfoil at Reynolds numbers
60,000 and 80,000. In general for Reynolds numbers 60,000 to 300,000, rime and
glaze ice caused a decrease in lift coefficient and increase in drag coefficient. The
horn ice geometry on the other hand, showed significant change in the aerodynamics
for all Reynolds numbers, in addition a time dependency was detected for horn ice
aerodynamics due to high fluctuations from both experiments and CFD.
From CFD, rime ice and clean airfoil simulations agreed well with the experiments.
Glaze and horn ice had a tendency to underpredict lift coefficient. For angle of attack
at 0 and 4 degrees for horn ice geometry, the solver was not capable of converging
the simulation. Through flow study, the time dependency of horn ice geometry was
found to arise from the large vortices formed at leading edge horn tip and translated
downstream along the chord.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction to Iced
Airfoils
1.1
Cold Climate
In 2017 the wind energy industry accounted for 52% [11] of the total new gross energy
production in Europe. As the power demand increases, the available conventional sites
are getting scarcer and the industry have to turn to more and more unconventional
sites. Conventional sites, typically areas with good wind measurement data, open and
lack of obstacles. In contrast, unconventional sites can be characterized by obstacles,
mountains and harsher environments such as the cold climate (CC) environment.
CC areas are defined by the wind energy industry as areas with low temperatures
with a high possibility of icing conditions. Icing can be a severe problem when ice
accumulate on power lines, wind measurement devices and on the wind turbine (WT)
itself. Ice accumulated on the WT can generate increased loads resulting in serious
fatigue damage.
Additional problems such as lift reduction and increase in drag
resulting in unwanted power degradation can also be observed. This effect has been
documented by several researchers, for instant in the paper Hudecz et.
al.
[13],
where they report that a change in surface roughness after only five minutes in icing
condition for a NACA 64-618 can cause a significant degradation in lift coefficient. A
more favourable aspect to CC areas, is that remote areas with few inhabitants the
noise and visible damage to neighbours is less of a concern. Another factor that can
make CC areas lucrative is the increase in air density caused by the cold air and the
increase in wind speed.
CC conditions can be separated into two subcategories: icing climate (IC) and
low temperature climate (LTC). Icing climate is typically defined by a climate where
accretion of ice and/or snow occurs on structures and equipment exposed to the
atmosphere and temperatures down to -20◦C. LTC on the other hand is comprised
by temperatures below -20◦C where icing rarely occurs, but can still have a negative
effect on materials on the WT, maintenance work, viscosity change in oil and fluids,
start up of the WT can be problematic and any heating components will have a
larger power consumption which will affect the efficiency of the wind farm [16]. An
illustration of how low temperature climate and icing climate makes up cold climate
is presented in Figure 1.2.

1.1.1
Ice types
Rime ice
Rime ice is formed by super cooled liquid water droplet hitting the surface of the
airfoil where the droplets freeze immediately upon impact. Rime ice can be divided
into soft rime and hard rime. Soft rime is formed by fairly small water droplets, and
hard rime by larger ones. Rime ice is commonly formed at temperatures between 0◦C
and -20◦C with densities up to 600 kg/m3 forming an irregular surface. Soft rime is
more snow-like and rather easy to remove from the surface. While hard rime would
stick rigidly to the surface, which makes is more problematic to remove. [16], [24].
==PAGE:19==
1.2 Scope of the thesis
3
Glaze ice
Opposite from rime ice, glaze ice is formed by freezing rain which makes a smooth
and transparent surface. Not all of the water droplets freeze immediately on impact,
therefore there will be some run-back towards the trailing edge where it can
potentially freeze. The glaze ice has a very inherent bond on the structure which
makes it hard to remove. This type of ice is normally formed in temperatures
between 0◦C and -6◦C. The density of glaze can be up to 900 kg/m3 which makes it
the heaviest ice type and can cause a severe increase in weight on the WT blades
[16], [24].
Horn/mixed ice
Mixed ice is occurring in the regime between rime and glaze ice. In the proximity of
the stagnation line the mixed ice has a clear/transparent surface similar to glaze ice
and a more rough and obscure rime ice like surface on both sides of the stagnation
line. Horn ice accretion is normally formed in glaze ice condition but also form in
the mixed ice regime and can be characterized by its horn height in addition to its
angle pursuant to the chord line. The critical aerodynamic feature arising from horn
ice is the formation of a separation bubble downstream of the horn [4], [1].

1.2
Scope of the thesis
The scope of this master thesis is to study the aerodynamic effect on a NREL S826
airfoil at 14 % thickness due to ice geometries. The ice geometry in question is rime,
glaze and horn ice. These ice geometries are 3D printed with the airfoil as one with
a chord length of 150mm, at DTU through the mechanical department (MEK). In
addition a clean airfoil is printed with 100mm chord and a 150mm chord DU 06-W-
200 airfoil with the horn geometry for increasing the knowledge of the effect of horn
on different airfoils. A set of computational fluid dynamic (CFD) simulations are
then performed to recreate and validate a few of the wind tunnel experiments.

CHAPTER 2
Methodology
The airfoil used for this experimental study is the NREL s826 airfoil which have a
thickness of 14 percent. This airfoil is an invention by D.M Somers for the National
Renewable Energy Laboratory for use as blade tip on a 20- to 40-meter horizontal
axis wind turbine, to ensure high lift properties on a variable speed and variable pitch
wind turbine [23]. The blade tip airfoil is interesting to investigate due to its higher
relative velocities and thus more ice accretion on the blade.
2.1
Modeling
The dimensions for modelling the airfoil, is obtained from the website of National
Renewable Energy Laboratory [18]. A non dimensional 2D sketch of the airfoil is
presented in Fig.
2.1a.
The dimensions was imported to the software Autodesk
Fusion 360 for modelling.
Various design options were carried out with different
designs before landing on the best options defined by the mounting mechanism of
the airfoil in the wind tunnel. Further, 30 and 32 pressure channels for clean and ice
cases respectively, were included in the design for taking pressure measurement on
the surface of the airfoil.
The internal pressure channels starts from the end of the airfoil and follows the
span until it reaches 38 % of the span. At this point the pressure channels penetrates
the surface where the pressure measurement is taken.
The pressure holes on the
surface is located with 25 degree shift to each other for the airfoil with 100mm chord,
to avoid flow disturbance from one to another ( Figure 2.4a).
In Fig 2.4a it can also be seen how the airfoil is divided into 3 pieces, 198mm,
150mm and 150mm respectively. This is due to the limitation of the 3D printer used
for printing the pieces. For connecting the 3 pieces of 100mm chord, 2 guide pins
is made for connecting each piece. A hollow channel of 10mm in diameter, is going
through the whole span from end to end, which is used for mounting the airfoil to
the wind tunnel and thus the force gauge.
Due to its longer chord width, the airfoil with 150mm chord has the pressure holes
located with 20 degree shift instead of 25 degree as the one with 100mm chord. This
reason for this is that for a larger chord, the pressure holes would appear to close to
the wind tunnel wall if 25 degree was chosen and thus the wall effect would have an
impact on the pressure readings. In the end, the pressure channels showed a leakage,

2.2
3D-printing
The 3D printing of the airfoils is carried out at FABLAB at the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU), Lyngby. The printing technique used at FABLAB is Fused Deposit
Modeling (FDM) which melts and extrude thermoplastic filament through a nozzle
and builds the model layer by layer. This is a widely used and cheap technique to

CHAPTER 3
Experimental
Investigation of Iced
NREL S826 Airfoil
In this chapter the airfoil NREL S826 with 14 % thickness is investigated for clean
version, with rime ice, glaze ice and horn ice geometry. In addition a DU 06-W-200
airfoil of 20 % thickness, with the same horn ice geometry is experimentally tested
to investigate how the horn ice shape effects different type of airfoils. All the airfoils
with its ice is 3D printed at DTU with a span of 498mm to fit the tunnel section of
500mm x 500mm. All airfoils with ice geometry has a chord length of 150mm and
the clean airfoil has a chord of 100mm.
This chapter gives an overview of the experimental investigation of the geometry
effect of the different ice shapes. Further, the wind tunnel set-up and execution is
explained in respective sections. Reynolds number dependency and generalization of
the horn ice geometry is also investigated. The the clean airfoil is not in the center
of interest, when its well documented by others, such as Sarlak [8], Sagmo et. al. [20]
and Prytz et. al. [19]
3.1
Previous work
During the past years, there has been an significant increase on the focus of ic-
ing on wind turbine airfoils. However, the aircraft industry is the research leader
when it comes to this matter, which usually includes high Reynolds number cases
(Re>1,000,000). Even though the study on iced wind turbine airfoils have increased,
more extensive studies is needed to fully understand the phenomenons that follows
with icing on WT blades in addition to understanding how icing effects in the lower
range of Reynolds numbers (Re).
==PAGE:28==
12
3 Experimental Investigation of Iced NREL S826 Airfoil
In a study by Jasinski et. al. [14], they show that the rime ice on the NREL S809
airfoil used in horizontal axis wind turbine (variable pitch) caused an power loss of
20 percent for wind speeds below the WT’s rated wind speed. For an stall regulated
turbine a rime ice horn located at the leading edge resulted in 16 percent increase in
the peak power from delay of stall, which is a unwanted scenario where this can lead
to burnout of the turbine generator. Further, it was found that the Reynolds num-
ber and Mach number dependency were rather minimal for a ice contaminated airfoil.
Adriana Hudecz [13], found in her experimental investigation on the effects of
rime, glaze and mixed ice on a NACA 64-618 airfoil, that the degradation of lift coef-
ficient could be as much as 64 % for mixed ice and 42 % and 50 % for rime and glaze
ice respectively.
In the experimental investigation of aerodynamic performance on an iced NLF-
0414 airfoil, Ebrahimi et. al. [10] found that the separation bubble increases on the
suction side, while it decreases on the pressure side of the airfoil. Their investigation
consisted of various angle of attack from -2 to 6 degree with leading edge glaze horn
ice.
In Ebrahimi et.
al.
[10] it was found that the static pressure in proximity
of the leading edge was fairly constant over a certain distance downstream on both
suction and pressure side.
With these readings, Ebrahimi et.
al.
[10] concluded
that this constant pressure could indicate the presence of a separation bubble, due to
the relative constant pressure inside a separation bubble, shown as pressure plateau
in Broeren et.
al.
[6] and in Bragg et.
al.
[4].
Bragg et.
al.
[10] visualized
the reattachment zone as the end of the pressure plateau, caused by the separation
bubble. Further, in Bragg et. al [4] it was found that a horn ice located far forward
on the leading edge of a NACA-23012 is favourable in terms of pressure field recovery.
In addition, Bragg et.
al.
found that roughness had little to no impact on the
performance of the airfoil with leading edge horn ice. This is an interesting finding,
when Broeren et. al. [7] observed that built-up ice roughness had a noticeable impact
on the performance for NACA 23012, NLF 0414 and NACA 2415 with a performance
degradation in CL,max of 28, 11 and 12 percent respectively. Further, for glaze horn
ice the largest penalty in terms of CL,max were for NACA 23012 airfoil with a loss
in CL,max of nearly 80 percent. All of the experiments in Bragg et. al. [7] were
done with Reynolds numbers ranging from 1,000,000 to 10,5000,000. The effects of
Reynolds number were small to insignificant on the iced airfoils, however, the Mach
number had a small effect.

Reynolds Number Effect
In Figure 3.10 Reynolds number dependency is investigated for rime ice (3.10a), glaze

3.4.3
Horn ice geometry generalization
In Figure 3.11 the DU 06-W-200 airfoil is presented with the same horn ice shape as
used for the NREL s826 airfoil. The DU 06-W-200 airfoil is an invention from TU
Delft University of Technology and is designed for usage on small vertical axis wind
turbines in the range of Re=150,000 to Re=700,000. The airfoil has a thickness of 20
% and is designed to have high lift coefficients up to around 1.5.
It can be seen in Figure 3.11, that in the lower angles of attack (AoA), the horn
geometry doesn’t affect the airfoil performance much, however the stall point is moved
from AoA≈15◦to AoA≈8◦. The drag coefficient also seems to be rather unaffected
by the Reynolds number, as seen for the other ice cases in section 3.4.2.
3.5
Discussion
At Re=60,000 and Re=80,000 the glaze ice Cl was found to be larger then Cl for the
clean airfoil.This can also be seen for Re=100,000 in Figure 3.9a. The increase in
Cl were also documented in Bragg et. al.[4], where the ice shapes were acting as a
leading edge flap on a NACA 63A415. Kim and Bragg [15] did also document this
effect for a horn ice shape on a NLF 0414 airfoil.
The severe lift penalty for horn ice geometry and the recovery after AoA=11◦, was
also documented by Bragg et. al.[4] on a NACA 0012 airfoil for Re=1,800,000. From
their flow study, they found that the flow separates from the tip of the horn as the
boundary layer can not recover the pressure around this geometry. For this reason
the flow does not reattached and vortices is formed downstream in the shear layer
and move upwards where even larger vortices is formed. These vortices is highly un-
stable and the separation bubble changes in size and location which lead the forces
to be time dependent. This time dependency can be seen as the standard deviation
in Figure 4.8d.

3.6
Conclusion of wind tunnel experiments
In this chapter an experimental study on NREL S826 airfoil were conducted for a
number of AoA and Reynolds number and lift coefficients from force gauge up-stroke
and drag coefficients from wake rake up-stroke were presented. The experiments were
performed in the Red Wind Tunnel at DTU, Lyngby. For Re=60,000 and Re=80,000
the glaze ice experienced a higher lift coefficient compared to the clean airfoil. In
general the rime and glaze ice geometry resulted in a increase in drag and decrease
in lift.
However.
the horn ice geometry showed severe penalty in regards of lift
reduction and increase in drag. Further, time dependency of the lift coefficient for
horn ice geometry were seen due to large fluctuation in lift force measurements. The
horn ice geometry effect were in addition investigated for the DU 06-W-200 airfoil for
possible generalization. The same behaviour as seen for horn ice on NREL s826 were
seen for the DU 06-W-200 airfoil. The results could be directly compared to several
references, which documented the same effect.

4.1
Introduction to numerical investigation
In Sagmo et. al. [20] several turbulence models were tested out in StarCCM+, for
the reason of checking their validity on numerically simulate the flow for the NREL
S826. The numerical investigation was compared to available experimental data for
NREL S826 airfoil. Given the spread in available experimental data, it was concluded
that k-ω SST turbulence model gave fair prediction of both lift and drag coefficient,
with a tendency to slightly under predict both lift and drag.
Recently Hudecs.
[13] performed a numerically and experimentally study on ice
accumulation on a NACA 64-618. In Hudecs. study experiments were conducted in
a climate tunnel at FORCE Technology, Lyngby. The ice geometries and its aero-
dynamic penalty were compared with the numerical ice accretion model, TURBICE
from the Technical Research Centre of Finland. Further, Hudecs. used Ansys Fluent
for investigating the impact of ice accretion on the aerodynamics of the airfoil and
the flow behaviour. The numerical study agreed with the experimental, however, the
influence of tunnel walls were captured and needed to be corrected. For the investi-
gated ice types, rime, Glaze and mixed, the rime ice was found not to alter the flow
pattern and its velocity, instead it was similar to the clean airfoil. The glaze ice on
the other hand, had a significant speed up at the sharp edges of the ice geometry
which caused large separation bubbles. For all three ice cases, small vortices were

4.2
Numerical set-up
The 2D domain used in StarCCM+ is set up to match the tunnel experiments, with
a 2D test section, height x length of 0.5m x 1.3m respectively. The top and bottom
wall are set as symmetry planes for simplicity. All the domains were constructed with
polygonal grid with 15 boundary/prism layer on the airfoil. A study of the mesh re-
finement is a presented in section 4.2.2. The roughness of the airfoil is not considered
in the simulations, thus the airfoil is simulated as smooth surface, which is not true
for the real airfoil that has an unknown surface roughness.
The left side of the fluid domain is set as the velocity inlet and the right side is set
as outlet with 1 atm. Further, the Reynolds Average Navier Stoke (RANS) solver is
used with the k-ω SST (shear stress transport) turbulence model, with the measured
turbulence intensity (TI) (table 4.1) of the empty red wind tunnel. Using the k-ω
turbulence model in StarCCM+ was found to give good estimation for lift and drag
coefficients in Sagmo et. al. [20], when considering the spread in available experi-
mental data for NREL S826. The simulated medium is set to air, as ideal gas and
isothermal flow with its automatic selected properties, except for reference pressure
which is set to 101,325Pa and reference density set to 1.2047 kg/m3, which are the
atmospheric properties at 20 degrees Celsius. The fluid was solved as compressible,
even though the simulations were done for low Reynolds numbers and incompressible
flow could have been assumed. The timestep used for the simulations are changing
with respect to Courant number equal to 1:

4.2.2
Mesh validation
In order to ensure high quality simulations in Star CCM+, different mesh settings
are compared to the experimental data obtained in the experiments in section 3 for
the clean NREL s826 airfoil at Reynolds number 100,000 and zero degrees angle of
attack. For the mesh validation the number of mesh cells in the proximity of the
airfoil is increased until the lift coefficient is of certain precision. Eight different mesh
settings have been compared to each other, surface growth rate (sgr) 1.3, 1.2, 1.1,
1.1, 1.08, 1.05, 1.03 and 1.02 respectively. In this range of mesh settings, the number
of meshing cells is increased from 21655 (sgr=1.3) to 131631 (sgr = 1.02). By going
from the coarse mesh of sgr = 1.3 to the finer mesh of sgr = 1.02, the simulation time
increase as well, which can be seen by the CPU time per iteration in table 4.2. For
all the 8 different mesh operations, the prism layer setting has been kept constant.
In Figure 4.2a, 4.2b and 4.2c, the mesh comparison is presented for Aoa = 0o,
AoA = 7o and AoA = 10o respectively. In Figure 4.2a, we can see how the curve
representing the increase in mesh refinement (x-axis), have a quite constant decrease
in lift coefficient until it reaches sgr = 1.03 and sgr = 1.02 where the curve flattens
out. This trend is also noticeable in Figure 4.2b and Figure 4.2c, however not so
clearly. For AoA = 7o 4.2b and AoA=10o 4.2c, it’s clear that the finer the mesh is,
the closer the solution gets to the experimental data obtained in section 3 for the
clean arifoil at Re=100,000. Even though the graph indicating the experimental data
(green line) in Figure 4.2a lies between sgr = 1.08 and sgr = 1.05, the difference in
percentage for sgr = 1.05 and sgr = 1.03 is only 0.55 percent at AoA = 0o.
By analyzing the mesh comparison for AoA = 7o and AoA = 10o, it can be seen
that with surface growth rate of 1.02 the precision of the obtained results is rather
minimal 0.47 % and 0.2 % respectively, compared to the results obtained with surface
growth rate of 1.03. With this in mind, sgr = 1.03 were chosen for the simulations
when this saved more on simulation time compared to the increase in simulation
precision.

4.4
Discussion of numerical investigation
From the results one can see that the simulations of clean and rime ice airfoil pre-
dict fairly well compared to the obtained experimental results. The glaze and horn
ice simulations have a tendency to under predict, which is easily seen in Figure 4.8.
Adriana Hudecs [13] too, found irregularities when comparing numerical and exper-
imental studies, which also is true for this study. Hudecs, concluded that the 3D
effects arising in tunnel experiments along with unknown surface roughness could
be the reason to cause the difference in modeled and measured values. The surface
roughness arising from ice accumulation is not present in the experiments for this
thesis, however, an unknown roughness from the 3D print is. Broerent et. al. [7],
documented the noticeable negative impact from ice surface roughness on the aero-
dynamic characteristics.
The numerical simulations of the horn ice shape, shows a great number of produced
vortices at the horn tip and travelling downstream disrupting the flow. These vortices
are causing the lift force acting on the airfoil to fluctuate over time. which make Cl
time dependent. This effect were also documented by Bragg et. al.[4], and can in
addition be seen in section 3.
It is clear from Figure 4.15, that the rime and glaze ice geometry does not alter the
streamlines around the airfoil significantly, although the point of separation occurs
earlier. The growth in trailing edge separation for the iced cases is clearly indicated
in Figure ?? and Figure 4.15, when separation starts earlier. The local separation
after the rime ice and glaze ice geometry was also documented by Hudecz. [13], which
caused a reduction in lift coefficient.
The increase in suction peak for the Cp curves (Figure ??) are deviating from the
findings in Hudecz [13] and in the work of Brandrup and Krogenes [5], where pressure
coefficient decreased for the respective iced airfoils.
The difference in CFD and experiments for the horn ice could arise from the time
dependent lift force as explained in section 3.4.1, where the produced eddies at the
horn tip translate downstream and interrupt the attachment of the flow, [4].
All of these illustrations of vortices and separation bubbles for horn ice (4.13, ??
and 4.15), can be used to explain the time dependency for lift force on the airfoil as
discussed in section 3.4.1.

4.5
Conclusion of numerical investigation
In this chapter a numerical investigation in 2D-domain was conducted on the NREL
s826 airfoil using CD-Adapco’s StarCCM+. It was found that the simulations of clean
and rime ice airfoil agreed well with experimental data obtained in section 3. Simu-
lations of glaze and horn ice had a tendency to under predict the lift coefficient and
thus gave more variation in lift coefficient compared to the clean airfoil. Simulations
of horn ice at AoA=0◦and AoA=4◦at Reynolds numbers 60,000 and 80,000 did not
converge and the corresponding mean value can not be trusted. By conducting a 2D
simulation, 3D effects such as transverse vortices is not captured and will influence
the lift coefficients. In addition, the experimentally tested airfoil had an unknown
surface roughness, while the simulation set-up was with smooth airfoil. Perhaps 3D
simulations including surface roughness would agree more with experiments. The
time dependency of horn ice lift coefficient was proven in this chapter in addition to
section 3, for large fluctuation in flow around the horn iced airfoil.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion and Future
work
Conclusion
In this master thesis the aerodynamics of a NREL S826 airfoil were investigated
through experimental and numerical study for three different leading edge ice geome-
tries. The ice geometries in mind were rime, glaze and horn ice. The experimentally
tested airfoil were 3D printed with FDM method. Hollow channels inside the airfoil
were included for measuring surface pressure, this method proved to be unsuccessful
when the 3D printer were not capable of printing the airfoil in such way it would be
airtight.
The experiments were conducted in the Red Wind Tunnel (low speed open-loop)
at DTU, Lyngby at a range of Reynolds numbers from 60,000 to 300,000 measuring
lift and drag polars. In general rime and glaze ice geometry caused a decrease in lift
coefficient and increase in drag coefficient. On the other hand, for Reynolds number
60,000 and 80,000 the glaze ice resulted in a increase in the lift coefficient compared
to clean airfoil. Horn ice geometry produced a significant change in both lift and
drag values, in addition to behavior of the polars. Further, a time dependency of lift
coefficient were established for horn ice geometry. Further, TU Delft’s DU 06-W-200
airfoil was tested with the same horn ice geometry for investigating generalization.
Same behavior of the lift and drag polars were fond for both airfoil types, in addition
to NACA 3415 investigated by Broeren et. al. [7] and NACA 63 415 investigated
by Seifert and Richert [22]. Having surface pressure measurements would have given
great value for determine these effects with certainty.
Furthermore, computational fluid dynamic simulations were performed using CD-
Adapco’s CFD package StarCCM+. The same airfoil with its ice geometries were
simulated using this software for validating the experiments. Simulations of glaze
and horn ice had a tendency to under predict lift and drag coefficients, while rime
ice agreed quite well with the experiments. For Re=60,000 and Re=80,000 the horn
ice at AoA=0◦and AoA=4◦did not converge, which resulted in a mean of large fluc-
tuations. The simulations were performed with assumption of smooth airfoil which

Future work
Being able to capture the surface pressure on the airfoil would bring great knowledge
when investigating iced airfoils. By 3D printing with a method called SLS/SLA in-
stead of FDM will give the possibility of utilizing the method in this thesis for surface
pressure measurements. It could be worth trying to pour Acetone liquid through the
”pressure channels” of an FDM print for ”welding” the printed layers together.
Capturing the flow structure around the airfoil is also of interest when analyzing
this topic and analyzing the uncertainties that follows with experiments and CFD.
Furthermore, it will be of interest comparing 3D simulations with the experiments to
investigate if the 3D effects can be captures numerically.
For the last topic I will suggest of implementing surface roughness in addition to
the ice geometry, perhaps with grit size paper (sand paper) to quantify the effects of
both geometry and surface roughness.
