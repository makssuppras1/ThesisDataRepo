Abstract
High atomic number and density of metallic implants leads to major problems at pro-
viding an accurate dose distribution in radiotherapy and contouring tumors and organs
caused by the unknown degree of attenuation from the implants.
The main objective of this study is to contribute toward a more exact dose planning for
radiotherapy treatment of patients with metallic implants, by material decomposition of
metallic elements, compounds, and mixtures of relevance to clinical implants regularly
seen in patients receiving radiotherapeutic treatment. The material decomposition is
performed by material decomposition (MD) models based on a six fitting parameter
least square fit (LSF) model gathered from basis material sets of; (1) aluminum and
stainless steel and (2) titanium and stainless steel.
MD models were established using kilo voltage (kV) dual-energy computed tomogra-
phy (DECT) and single-energy CT (SECT) scans with an extended CT scale range on
a General Electric (GE) Revolution™CT scanner. A subsequent MD model was estab-
lished using a mega voltage (MV) Varian TrueBeam linear accelerator (LINAC).
The CT model proved as viable in estimating density levels of three different hip im-
plants. These estimates were used to determine a correct material to assign the hip
implants during a dose calculation process. Further work and improvements is needed
on the MV model to gain acceptable density estimates for clinical use.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Metals are routinely encountered in radiotherapy patients in the form of orthopedic im-
plants, dental fillings, spinal implants, and subcutaneous medical devices. For patients
with metallic hip implants, treatment planning has to be performed with consideration
of the implant material as well as its position and form of photon beams administered
through the implant. Knowledge about the implant material may be missing, or the
actual geometry may deviate from the assumed one, resulting in under- or overdosage of
the tumor, thus compromising tumor control and producing severe collateral problems
for the patient [2]. Overestimation of target dose by 14% have been found for patients
with metallic hip implants undergoing radiation treatment [24]. Although results have
shown some cases of sufficient compensation of the disturbing effects caused by the pres-
ence of femur and hip metallic implants by the treatment planning system (TPS) when
treating prostate cancer patients [2].
The human body is composed of various inhomogeneous materials. Therefore, to calcu-
late the attenuation of radiation during the diagnostic phase and absorption during the
treatment phase, inhomogeneity correction should be done based on the densities/elec-
tron densities of various materials, which are converted from the Hounsfield unit (HU)
values (or CT values) obtained from computed tomography (CT) images. The CT im-
ages of patients with metallic implants contain artifacts disturbing the overall visibility,
thus contaminating the HU values in the CT images. The TPS needs an established re-
lationship between HU values and densities, a relationship covered by calibration curves
empirically obtained from CT images of a phantom containing several materials of dif-
ferent density [54]. Treatment planning in the presence of metals can be handled by
contouring the high-density material and artifacts, and by manually assigning a CT
number to those regions corresponding to the desired relative electron density (RED).
Metals are commonly causing disturbances in the form of artifacts in CT images and to
counteract this, metal artifact reduction (MAR) algorithms are commonly implemented
in modern CT scanners, reducing major image disturbances. Regardless of how artifacts
are handled, the CT number of the metal itself must accurately correspond to a RED in
the TPS for proper handling of dose calculation. High density materials have HU values
above the standard CT scale limit, resulting in all high density materials to be assigned
the maximum HU value available. High density materials are then indistinguishable
from one another. Entrance dose is avoided in areas of unknown density such as metal
implants, limiting the entrance angle of the treatment. For these reasons, errors of dose
distribution could occur.

1 Introduction
1.1
Dual Energy CT and Extended CT scale
Conventional CT images, which represent x-ray attenuation by shades of gray, are a
map of tissue attenuation for each voxel1 represented in HU. Two materials with similar
attenuation coefficients have similar HU numbers, even if they have different chemical
compositions. Therefore, conventional single energy CT (SECT) images provide limited
information on material composition of tissues or materials under investigation.
Recent technological advances allow nearly simultaneous acquisition of CT scans us-
ing more than one polychromatic x-ray energy spectrum, enabling the adoption of dual
energy CT (DECT) into clinical practice. Image computation using CT data from dif-
ferent energies permits generation of datasets that provide information about tissue
composition, providing richer information than that available from SECT.
Photon attenuation in diagnostic x-ray imaging results from scatter and absorption
events that occur within the tissues [58, 13, 12]. The specific mechanisms that govern
the attenuation of a beam of x-ray photons vary depending on the incident beam energy
and on the density and atomic number (Z) of the material (the absorber) that interacts
with the x-ray photons. For the energy range and materials encountered in clinical CT,
there are two dominant phenomena that cause attenuation of x-ray photons: Compton
scattering and the photoelectric effect [58, 13, 12]. Compton scattering is proportional
to the effective atomic number but has little dependence on the energy of the x-rays,
whereas the photoelectric effect increases strongly with increasing effective atomic num-
ber, and with decreasing energy of the x-rays [58, 13, 12]. Photoelectric interaction
depends highly on the unique binding energy of any innermost shell (the K-shell) elec-
tron for a particular material. An abrupt increase in attenuation is observed at x-ray
energy levels just greater than the binding energy (K-edge) of different materials [58, 13,
12]. The K-edge varies for each material and is higher with increasing atomic numbers.
Evaluating material specific attenuation behavior at different x-ray energy levels reveals
information about tissue composition. This is done by either consecutive CT acquisi-
tions with different photon spectra, modification of the x-ray source to emit two different
polychromatic x-ray energy spectra, or spectral separation at the detector level [22, 55].
These methods are all different approaches to DECT. Spectral analysis offers the oppor-
tunity to distinguish clinically relevant materials with different elemental composition
on the basis of unique K-edge characteristics and differences in attenuation at different
energy levels, as illustrated by Figure 1.1 [22, 55]. This technique lays the foundation
for decomposing a scanned object of unknown composition between a set of materials
with a well known composition, hence the name; material decomposition.

1.1 Dual Energy CT and Extended CT scale
5
Figure 1.1: Mass attenuation as a function of photon energy for a few clinically relevant
materials.
The standard CT scale ranges from -1024 to 3071 HU on 12-bit2 images acquired at
typical CT scanners - many metals and in general materials of high atomic number
and density have HU values exceeding the 12-bit image CT scale limit of 3071 HU.
However, data can also be reconstructed using extended CT scale techniques, resulting
in 16-bit images with a HU limit exceeding the 12-bit image by more than a tenfold.
Using extended CT scale techniques for improving efficiency in presence of metallic im-
plants in the treatment planning process of clinical workflow and eliminating a potential
source of human error by manual CT number assignment has been proven useful [40, 53].
DECT in combination with an extended CT scale offers the opportunity to generate
material specific images for metals and other high density materials on the basis of
the atomic number and the unique attenuation coefficient of a particular material at
different x-ray energies. Material specific images provide qualitative and quantitative
information about tissue composition and contrast media distribution, highly needed for
CT images of patients with metallic implants planned for radiotherapy treatment.
Metals attenuate to a great extent due to their much higher atomic number, when com-
pared to the surrounding tissue. Standard 12-bit CT images are not able to fully capture
the attenuating values of metals, resulting in them all appearing with the same HU value
rendering them indistinguishable from one another. Using an extended scale provided
by 16-bit images provides unique HU values to metals exceeding the attenuation limit
of 12-bit images. Combining this extended insight with DECT images paves the way
for classification and material decomposition of metals with subsequent application to
metallic implants in patients.

1 Introduction
1.2
Project
The initial motivation and basis of this thesis lies in the references Gascho et. al and
Skaarup et al. [29, 65], where material decomposition of metals is used for identification
of unknown objects of otherwise unknown composition. Both articles use a separate set
of basis materials each with a different purpose in the subsequent material decomposi-
tion. Gascho et. al [29] proves that bullet type identification is possible, while Skaarup
et. al [65] proves that material decomposition is possible for cone-beam CT.
This thesis sets out to establish a material decomposition model using aluminum/stain-
less steel and titanium/stainless steel as basis materials, thus creating two models able
for comparison. The material decomposition models are then to be used for calculating
material thickness and density maps of metallic implants with special application to
hip implants. The material decomposition approach will be tested using an extended
CT scale DECT scanner and a megavoltage photon accelerator, to display functionality
during both the diagnostic and subsequent treatment phase for cancer patients.
1.3
Structure
The thesis in its entirety is organized into four parts; First part serves as an introduc-
tion. Second part explains the theoretical background of DECT and MV projection
radiography. Third part conducts the equipment and experimental approaches followed
by presentation and analysis of the gathered results. Fourth and final part is dedicated
to final remarks in the form of a discussion and conclusion, summarizing the gathered
results from the previous two parts and linking this to the purpose of the thesis. The
fourth part is finalized with an examination of possible future prospects.

CHAPTER 2
Medical Diagnostic
Applications of Ionizing
Radiation
Ionizing radiation is a form of energy, either in the form of particles (neutrons, beta
or alpha) or electromagnetic waves (gamma or x-rays), that acts by removing electrons
from their bound state in atoms or molecules of materials. Removing the tightly bound
electrons from their atomic orbit causes the atom to become charged or ionized.
Ionizing radiation in medicine is grouped into two applications: diagnostic and ther-
apeutic. Diagnostic applications consist of use in radiology and nuclear medicine. In
radiology, the radiation administered is external to the patient, whereas, in nuclear
medicine, the radiation is internal. Therapeutic applications are likewise grouped into
categories based on whether the source is external or internal, e.g. oncology (external)
and therapeutic nuclear medicine (internal).
This chapter presents the theory necessary for understanding the basic principles of
diagnostic radiology applications of ionizing radiation through x-rays. This includes a
description of diagnostic scanners such as conventional x-ray imaging and single and
dual-energy CT scanners, beginning with a description of the physics of ionizing radia-
tion. Subjects important for further understanding and argumentation will be explored
in depth, whereas those of less importance will be covered briefly.
Key words:
• Attenuation: The reduction of the intensity of a beam as it passes through a
medium.
• Absorption: Some of the energy from radiation transferred to a medium.
• Scattering: A change in the direction of a photon with or without the loss of
energy.

2 Medical Diagnostic Applications of Ionizing Radiation
2.1
Ionizing Radiation - Types and Interactions
X-rays are electromagnetic (EM) waves with frequencies much higher than those of
visible light. They are a form of ionizing capable of ejecting electrons from atoms, which
has proven very useful in both medical diagnostic and therapeutic purposes. Once x-
rays reach a high enough energy, they can traverse relatively thick objects without being
significantly absorbed or scattered, making them optimal to image the inside of visually
opaque objects.
Electron beams, which may be considered a particulate form of radiation, are used in the
production of x-rays. The electron beam is comprised in an x-ray tube projecting the
x-rays necessary for imaging in both projection imaging and CT imaging. This section
will describe the fundamental physics of x-ray, covering the creation and interaction
processes.
2.1.1
Ionization
Ejection of an electron from an atom, creating a free electron and an ion is the definition
of ionization [12, 58]. Radiation with enough energy to ionize an atom is called ionizing
radiation. Ionizing radiation covers a broad range of physical phenomena, may it be
high energy EM waves such as x-rays or particles such as electrons.
Electrons orbiting the nucleus of an atom are organized into orbits or so-called shells.
The shell closest to the nucleus is defined as the K shell - the first shell, L shell is the
second, then the M shell as the third and so forth. It is energetically more favorable
for an electron to be bound in a atom rather than to be free [12, 58]. Each electron is
bound by a certain electron binding energy depending on the atomic number (Z) and the
distance from the nucleus (inner shell electrons have higher binding energy than outer
shell electrons), usually specified in electron volts (eV). One electron volt is defined as
the kinetic energy gained by an electron when accelerated across a one volt potential.
The ionization process is depicted in Figure 2.1, where ionizing radiation is shown to
free an otherwise bound electron from its shell orbit.
A single ionizing radiation emission may ionize many atoms before all of its energy is
exhausted. The ionizing radiation typically used in medical imaging has energies ranging
from about 25 keV to 500 keV [58]. If an ionizing particle of EM wave transfers some
energy to a bound electron but less than the corresponding binding energy, then the
electron is raised to a higher energy state, an orbit further away from the nucleus, but
not completely ejected from the atom. Such a process is called an excitation. During
both the ionization and excitation process, an electron shell is left with a ”hole” that
need be filled in order to return the atom to a lower energy state - the lowest possible
energy state may be considered nature’s preferred arrangement of electrons in a given
atom. Filling of these holes is done by electrons residing in shells of higher shell number
than where the hole is left. These electrons ”jump down” to fill the hole, emitting energy
in the form of x-rays in the process.

2.1.2
Types of Ionizing Radiation
Ionizing radiation may be split into two broad types: particulate and electromagnetic.
The electromagnetic ionizing radiation is the only type used throughout this project
but in order to understand how electromagnetic radiation, namely x-rays, are created,
a brief explanation of particulate radiation in the form of electrons will be introduced.
Particulate Radiation
Nuclear medicine considers modes of radioactive decay that then give rise to different
kinds of particulate ionizing radiations [17]. In projection radiography and computed
tomography (CT), as is the concern of this project, however, the only particulate radia-
tion of concern is that of electrons not bound in an atom and have gained kinetic energy.
These electrons emerge from a heated filament and a potential difference between a cath-
ode and an anode within an x-ray tube [12]. The energies of these electrons are known
by the manner in which they were created. Electrons accelerated across e.g. a 100 kV
potential will have energies equal to 100 keV.
Electromagnetic Radiation

2.1.3
Brehmsstrahlung and Characteristic X-Rays
The only particles of outcome to the formation of medical images are electrons and
positrons. Positrons are of main concern in nuclear medicine and will only briefly be
discussed. Electrons are, on the other hand, the major contributor to the generation of
x-rays.
Electrons interact and transfer energy to an absorbing medium by two processes: colli-
sional transfer and radiative transfer. Collisional transfer is the most common type of
interaction in the case of medical imaging [58]. A fraction of the electron’s kinetic en-
ergy is transferred to another electron in the target medium with which it collides. The
incident electron’s path may be changed because of the collision, both collisional and
radiative energy transfer may subsequently occur, until the incident electron’s energy is
depleted.
In radiative transfer, the energetic electron’s interaction with an atom produces x-rays.
This can occur in two different ways: characteristic radiation or bremsstrahlung radia-
tion. Both interactions are depicted in Figure 2.2. In the generation of characteristic
radiation, the incident electron interacts with a K-shell electron, exciting or ionizing the
atom, and briefly leaving a hole in that shell. The hole is then filled by an electron re-
siding in one of the outer shells. Because electron binding energy of the K-shell is higher
than that of the outer shells, a loss of energy as the vacancy is filled is to be expected.
This specific loss of energy creates an EM x-ray photon, called a characteristic x-ray
(Figure 2.2a). The energy is exactly equal to the difference in electron binding energy
between the vacant shell and the outer shell from which the substitute electron comes
from. These distinctive characteristic x-rays varies with the atomic number, making
the atoms easy to identify based on the measured x-ray energy [12, 58]. Characteristic

Coherent Scattering
Coherent scattering (also known as Rayleigh, Thomson, classical, or elastic scattering)
is a type of scattering interaction that occurs between a photon and an atom as a whole.
Because of the great mass of an atom (e.g., in comparison to the recoil electron in the
Compton scattering process), very little recoil energy is absorbed by the atom. The
photon is therefore deflected with essentially no loss of energy - a change in direction
(mainly in the forward direction) but not in wavelength [12].
The coherent scattering types Rayleigh and Thomson scattering should, however, not
be mistaken for the same form of interaction. The Thomson scattering effect relies on
an incident x-ray photon being temporarily absorbed by an orbital electron resulting in
excitation. The x-ray photon is then re-emitted with its original energy in a arbitrary di-
rection. Rayleigh scattering instead relies on the photon being absorbed and re-emitted
by electron cloud as a whole [15]. Shortly said, Rayleigh scattering is the elastic scat-
tering of a photon off an entire atom. Thomson scattering is the elastic scattering of a

Although occurring within the energy range of diagnostic imaging, coherent scattering
is not a major interaction process encountered in radiography and will thus be regarded
as a small negligible contribution.

Pair Production
Pair production results in the complete attenuation of the incident photon. This mode
of interaction for x-rays with an absorber involves conversion in the Coulomb field of
the nucleus of an x-ray into an electron and a positron - a pair of particles, as shown

x-ray must have a minimum energy value of 1.022 MeV to interact by pair production.

pair production increases [18] (see Figure 2.7 and 2.8). The excess energy (above the

2.3
Computed Tomography
X-ray computed tomography (CT) is extremely popular since it generates images of
slices of the body, thereby eliminating the artifacts of overlaying structures that domi-
nate conventional planar radiographs [12, 58]. One way to understand the fundamental
mechanism of CT is to imagine taking a series of conventional chest x-rays, where the pa-
tient is rotated slightly around an axis running from head to foot between each exposure.
Each acquired image then contains a 2D projection of the 3D body. These projection
data are then used to reconstruct cross-sectional images of the scanned object.
The arrival of x-ray CT in the mid-1960s and its advancement all the way up to the
present day has created a significant change in the role of diagnostic imaging in medicine.
Diagnosis and monitoring of many diseases are conceivable through CT alone, thus re-
ducing the amount of exploratory surgery previously required for such diseases. Today,
through standard calibration practices, CT scanners measure CT numbers in Hounsfield
units (HU), which are constant across scans and across different scanners, thereby mak-
ing quantitative analysis available [13].
Modern advancements in CT imaging have lead to a standard of multiple cross sec-
tions being collected rapidly using helical CT and multiple-row detector CT, making
3D data sets rapidly available. Many modern scanners also come with a dual-energy
scanning ability, scanning the patient with more than one x-ray energy for optimized
image contrast.

2.3.4
Artifacts
Artifacts are commonly encountered in clinical computed tomography (CT). Knowledge
of these artifacts is important as they may resemble pathology or can reduce image qual-
ity to non-diagnostic levels [7]. To optimize image quality, it is necessary to understand
why artifacts occur and how they can be prevented or suppressed. There are many dif-
ferent types of CT artifacts, those most relevant to this thesis are metal artifacts caused
by beam hardening and photon starvation. Beam hardening and overall metal artifacts
can be reduced using dual and multi-energy CT providing better overall contrast [8].
This can be done by combining images acquired at different energy levels where the
attenuation varies.
Beam hardening
Beam hardening is the interaction occurring when an x-ray beam comprised of polychro-
matic energies passes through an object, producing selective attenuation of lower energy
photons. This effect is considerably similar to a high-pass filter, as only higher energy
photons are left in the beam and thus the mean beam energy is increased; low energy
==PAGE:43==
2.3 Computed Tomography
29
photons are preferentially attenuated, which in return increases the effective energy of
the beam directly impacting the measured intensity as governed by Eq. 2.15. As a con-
sequence, the probability of photon interaction decreases, i.e. the beam becomes more
penetrating - hardened [7, 8, 50, 72].
This is the same concept behind the use of metal filters in x-ray tubes in order to min-
imize the low energy photons not able to penetrate the patient that would otherwise
solely contribute to an increased dose.
In CT, beam hardening comes from a very dense target, such as implants made of various
metal alloys, which may result in characteristic artifacts. CT beam hardening artifacts
have two distinct appearances; streaking and cupping artifacts [7, 8, 50, 72]. An exam-
ple of the streaking and cupping effect can be seen in the reconstructed CT image of a
patient with a hip implant in Figure 2.12. Streaking artifacts are a result of the poly-
chromatic x-ray beam being hardened at different rates according to rotational positions
of the tube/detector. Cupping artifacts are seen as a falsely bright appearance along
the periphery of an object. Because the x-ray beam is hardened by passing through
dense material, the mean photon energy will be higher upon exit of the material. Since
higher energy photons are attenuated less by less dense materials, the resulting image
will appear with dark line as if nothing was in its way. Accordingly assigned correct CT
numbers will lead to major errors, as the surroundings may appear as too attenuating
or not attenuating enough, resulting in few options to distinguish materials apart [7, 8,
50, 72].
Photon Starvation
Photon starvation is another source of streaking artifact which may occur in CT. It is
seen in highly attenuating areas, particularly trailing metallic implants. Because of high
attenuation, an insufficient amount of photons reach the detector. During the recon-
struction process, the noise is highly amplified in these areas leading to characteristic
streaks and distorting noise in the resulting image [52].
In certain applications, particularly low dose CT procedures, the increased noise due
to photon starvation is typically encountered as a compromise between low patient ra-
diation dose and acceptable image quality. The artifacts can be reduced by automatic
tube current modulation or generally higher tube current and adaptive filtration. Use of
iterative reconstruction techniques can likewise considerably reduce image noise caused
by photon starvation [72].
2.3.5
Dual-Energy CT
Dual-energy imaging is a way to gather more information about tissue, or in the interest
of this thesis, metal, characteristics since the linear attenuation of all materials is a func-
tion of energy. The principles of SECT carry over to dual energy CT (DECT) with the
exception of the x-ray source functionality and opposing detector system, as depicted
in Figure 2.15. Image computation utilizing CT data from various energies allow for
==PAGE:44==
30
2 Medical Diagnostic Applications of Ionizing Radiation
generation of datasets that present information about tissue composition, resulting in
richer information than that available from SECT.
Elements with a low atomic number are relatively affected more by Compton scatter,
whereas those with a higher atomic number are affected more by the photoelectric effect
(see Figure 2.7 and 2.8).
The photoelectric effect is energy dependent, and its likelihood increases as the energy
of the incident photon approximates the K-shell binding energy of an electron [8, 12, 13,
58]. The K-shell binding energy varies for each element, and it increases as the atomic
number increases. The term K-edge then refers to the spike in attenuation that occurs
at energy levels just greater than that of the K-shell binding because of the increased
photoelectric absorption at these energy levels (see Figure 2.3). K-edge values differ for
each element, and they increase as the atomic number increases. This energy depen-
dence of the photoelectric effect and the variability of K-edges form the basis of dual
energy techniques [8, 12, 13, 58].
In clinical practice, there are several methods to acquire dual or multi-energy CT data
[41]. Most commonly, two different x-ray spectra are used in combination with standard
CT detectors. Several different techniques available for commercial use have been intro-
duced: (1) Performing two subsequent scans with different x-ray tube voltages with a
single source CT scanner, (2) Rapidly switching the x-ray tube voltage during scan, (3)
Introducing a split filter into the tube collimator housing of a single source CT scanner,
(4) Using dual source CT systems, and (5) Using a single x-ray spectrum in combination
with an energy sensitive detector [21, 22, 41]. With current dual energy CT technology,
the two energies most frequently used are 80 kVp and 140 kVp [21]. Fast switching DECT
between energies of 80 - 140 kVp of similar type as the one shown in Figure 2.15B has
been used throughout this project.
Among all DECT methods, fast kVp-swithcing is advantageous in terms of system cost
and imaging performance [75]. Another advantage of rapid kV-switching is the almost
simultaneous acquisition of low-energy and high-energy projections with a time delay of
less than 0.5 ms, which prevents registration problems due to organ motion or contrast
agent dynamic [22]. However, as a disadvantage, fast switching of the x-ray tube current
synchronously to the fast switching of the x-ray tube voltage is technically not doable,
and at identical x-ray tube current the x-ray flux at 80 kV is much lower compared to
140 kV [22]. To adjust the radiation dose of the low kV and the high kV data, two
or more low kV projections are collected for every high kV projection. However, the
total number of high kV projections during one rotation is reduced, thereby resulting
in possible sampling problems and limiting the maximum attainable spatial resolution
[22].

2.4
Material Decomposition
DECT offers the ability to produce material-specific images based on the atomic number
Z and the unique mass attenuation coefficient of a specific material at various x-ray en-
ergies. Material-specific images supply qualitative and quantitative information about
tissue composition and contrast media distribution [14, 55, 65, 75].
Fast kilovoltage switching DECT scanners perform two-material decomposition analysis
in the projection domain to produce material-specific images from low and high energy
projection data, before tomographic images are generated [55, 75]. These algorithms
take the low and high energy sinograms/projection data and convert them into material-
specific projections, from which the tomographic images of the materials are produced.
Such images are also concerned as material-specific images, since these images symbolize
a mass density or concentration value [55, 75].
These algorithms estimate that the entire volume is purely made of two preselected ma-
terials. The algorithms assume every voxel imaged is comprise of these two materials in
various amounts. The amount of these two materials within a voxel is computed on the
basis of the attenuation characteristics of these materials at two different energies. This
is viable since the energy-dependent attenuation of each material has been precomputed
on the basis of its mass attenuation coefficient and atomic number [55, 75]. The materi-
als comprising the basis for two-material decomposition are often considered as the pair
of basis materials.
In the diagnostic x-ray energy range, the attenuation coefficient of a material can be
decomposed into two major components, a photoelectric component and a Compton
scatter component. Because these two components are complicated to measure indepen-
dently, they are practically approximated with two basis materials. One material with
relatively high atomic number Z and the other material with relatively low Z. At a given

More than half of all people with cancer receive radiation therapy as part of their cancer
treatment, and some patients are receiving other cancer treatment methods alongside
radiation, such as chemo therapy. Medical personnel use radiation therapy to treat a
wide variety of cancer types and locations throughout the body. Radiation therapy are
even used in treating some noncancerous (benign) tumors [39, 60].
Radiation therapy is the use of high-energy radiation to damage cancer cells’ DNA and
destroy their ability to divide and grow further. Cancer cells whose DNA is damaged
beyond repair stop dividing or die. When the damaged cells die, they are broken down
and removed by the body itself [39, 60]. Radiation therapy may be delivered externally
using machines called linear accelerators (LINACs) with an MV energy beam or inter-
nally via radioactive sources placed inside the patient on a temporary or permanent basis.
Preparation for radiation therapy is concentrated on targeting the radiation dose to the
cancer as accurate as possible to reduce side effects and refrain from damaging healthy
cells. Diagnostic images taken prior to treatment, e.g. a CT scan, may be used to help
determine the exact shape and location of the patient’s tumor and define its size and
boundaries. Before receiving treatment, a cone beam CT (CBCT) scan is, as common
standard procedure, performed to ensure the position is correct and complies with the
treatment plan customized for that specific patient. CBCT source and detector is a part
of the LINAC machine allowing for a quick and easy verification of the treatment plan
and the diagnostic CT from which the treatment plan was conducted [67].
Key words:
• CBCT: Cone Beam CT, x-ray emission in a cone shape.
• MV: High energy emission in the mega voltage range. Well suited for treatment.
• LINAC: Device producing high energy x-rays used for treatment.

beam between positions equidistant from the central axis of the beam [57, 70].
Final beam shaping is done by making use of the substantial secondary collimation sys-
tem. Modern LINACs use multileaf collimation (MLC) with leaf widths at the isocenter
ranging from 4 mm to 10 mm. This has provided the opportunity for simplified adjust-
ment to the shaping of the beam to the target as well as for changing the beam intensity
by rapidly changing the beam shape within the overall field [57, 70].
A standard conventional method of changing the intensity of the beam has been the use
of metal wedges1 placed within the beam to modify the uniformity of the beam profile in
a regulated manner. This change of the beam intensity has been found to be very useful
in producing uniform dose distribution within the target [57, 70]. This method allows
for adjustable treatment of specific area of the body, while simultaneously irradiating
the cancerous tumor.
Positioned opposite of the LINAC MV beam source is a detector plate able to detect MV
photons and construct images from the detected photons. This detector plate has proven
to be of great use for positioning the patient correctly and performing dose verification
[57, 67, 70].
3.2
Electronic Portal Imaging Devices
Precise patient positioning and beam placement is vital for achieving the desired treat-
ment outcomes.
However, patient setup variations and errors can occur during the
course of treatment [39, 60, 67]. To ensure appropriate patient positioning relative to
the treatment beam image-guided radiotherapy, which corrects for any variations in
patient position and target localization before each treatment session is adopted. Elec-
tronic Portal Imaging Devices (EPIDs) mounted on a LINAC using robotic arms have
been implemented into clinical practice. EPIDs produce digital images that allow for
online treatment verification and automatic analysis [57, 67, 70].
Three different design variations of EPIDs are available.
These include video-based,
scanning liquid-ion-chamber-based, and hydrogenated amorphous silicon (aSi: H) based
devices. Due to the high quantum efficiency (70-80% more than video-based systems)
of aSi: H flat panel EPID, satisfactory image quality is obtained and the aSi: H EPID
is now dominant [67].

Since EPIDs use LINAC beams in the MV energy range, poor contrast images are
obtained due to the dominance of Compton scattering (see Figure 2.7 and 2.8) [67].
LINACs are therefore also typically equipped with a kV CBCT source and detector
plate, making it possible to acquire images in the diagnostic energy range (40 - 140 keV)
by normal CBCT and in the high-energy MV range by employing the EPID as detector
plate. Both kV and MV imaging instruments mounted on the LINAC are of cone beam
shape, making the subsequent reconstruction procedures nearly identical.
3.3
Cone Beam Computed Tomography
It is common practice to have a kV CBCT scanner mounted into the LINAC. The built-
in CBCT scanner serves as an effective image-guided radiotherapy tool for confirmation
of patient position and also gives the option of real-time re-optimization of treatment
plans [10, 65, 67].
CBCT differs from conventional CT in that the x-rays are not collimated into a fan
beam but rather into a wide field of view creating a cone of x-rays, as can be seen by the
comparison of the two in Figure 2.9. A 2D array of detectors, rather than a 1D array as
with conventional CT, is required. CBCTs are well suited for mounting on the LINAC
and they have have resolution and other advantages, such as a decreased examination
time and decreased patient movement artifacts [10, 67]. Some of the disadvantages in-
clude an increase in scattered radiation and a potential for cone beam artifact if an
inappropriate reconstruction algorithm is used [10]. The algorithms used for a normal
fan-beam reconstruction CT scan may very well not suit the different geometry of a
CBCT scan. Reconstruction algorithms suited for CBCT are then used instead. Some
of these reconstruction algorithms do show similarities with the ones used for conven-
tional fan beam CT, among those are: filtered backprojection suited for a cone beam
geometry, iterative reconstruction techniques, and Feldkamp-Davis-Kress (FDK) as a
commonly used algorithm [12, 13, 58].
MV and kV CBCT both provide verification of patient position.
However, the soft
tissue contrast and thus the accuracy of delineation is superior with kV CT imaging.
Images provided by kV CBCT display an improved high contrast resolution due to the
dominance of the photoelectric effect at kV energies. Since the dominant interaction at
MV energies is Compton scattering (inversely proportional to photon energy and almost
independent of the atomic number, Z), the image contrast for MV CBCT images is then
reduced for several tissue equivalent (or tissue-like) materials [69]. However, MV CBCT
may be used for dose distribution verification and improved resolution of metal implants
due to a reduced soft tissue contrast. The MV range of CT has been proven to enable
imaging of anatomical structures in the presence of dental metal or orthopedic implants
[69].

The most significant difference between kV and MV (may it be cone beam or fan beam)
CT is in the dose required to achieve the same image quality, where much higher doses

radiation dose from both kV CBCT and MV CBCT is significant although the dose is

advanced imaging techniques.
3.3.1
FDK Reconstruction
Feldkamp, Davis, and Kress (1984) describe an approximate reconstruction algorithm
for circular cone-beam tomography - named the FDK reconstruction method. It is ap-
proximate in the sense that the reconstruction result will deviate somewhat from the
measured object regardless of the measurement resolution. However, for moderate cone-
angles, these differences are small and often acceptable. The simplicity of the FDK
reconstruction method has made it the most used algorithm for cone-beam reconstruc-
tion [13].

Performing MD in the projection domain based on raw CT scan data in projection
domain instead of the image domain has been proven to benefit from intrinsic beam-
hardening correction, while doing it in image domain is easier, faster and more ap-
proachable but may be prone to suffer from artifacts and noise [29, 48, 31, 65, 75]. Since
focus of this thesis is on research of metallic implants prone to considerable artifacts,
this has served as the reasoning for primarily performing MD in the projection domain
throughout this project. Attempts to do it in the image domain has likewise been done
to test the differences but proved to be inconclusive due to a big distorting impact by
the presence of artifacts. Further research of an image-based MD has therefore not been
pursued.

Initial motivation for this project came from radiotherapeutic treatment of cancer pa-

An approach to accomplish this is by MD, the act of recognizing the material composi-

This chapter will present measurement results of SECT and DECT scans conducted

of the viability of image domain MD will be presented. Second, an MD model with alu-

5.3
Imitating the DECT Concept by SECT
Considerable access to a wide variety of thicknesses of aluminum and stainless made it
possible to set up several configurations resulting in combinations covering a number
of different material thicknesses. Metal pieces either solely composed of titanium or of
a simply composition with titanium comprising the majority was scarce. Using a large
number of material thicknesses when creating a measurement data table provides a bet-
ter model for material decomposition - the more reference material thicknesses with a
corresponding measurement value, the more accurate the model [29, 48].
Using GE’s fast-switching DECT GSI technology resulted in a considerable amount
of inconsistent ˆIL,H values, mainly due to photon starvation. The reconstruction al-
gorithms utilized by the GE scanner were confidential and could not be accessed for
further insight into the reconstruction process. Instead of continuing along the lines of
confidential DECT technology, a simpler process with known reconstruction algorithms
was pursued. Instead, performing two SECT scans at different tube voltages, 80 kVp
and 140 kVp, to imitate scans at low and high energy, would sustain and imitate the
necessary dual-energy concept needed for material decomposition. One simple manual
SECT scan at 80 kVp followed by another at 140 kVp served as the replacement of the
fast-switching DECT technology. This approach minimized unknown acquisition proce-

Aluminum/stainless steel (Al/SS) and titanium/stainless steel (Ti/SS) comprised the
two sets of basis materials used to conduct an accompanying set of fitting parameters
and MD model. The data tables for each material set were used for calculating the
fitting parameters as described in Eqs. 2.28-2.31 for each basis material. Fitting param-
eter values are attached in Appendix C, Table C.1.
To test the accuracy of the model, the same material thickness data values used for
training the model were used as input. The output should then yield the same material
thickness value as the reference given. Figure 5.3 displays the accuracy of the Al/SS and
Ti/SS three fitting parameter MD model with input data identical to reference material
thicknesses. These figures follow the same increment numbers, meaning that at e.g. in-
crement number 12, reference material thickness of aluminum is 1.58 mm and 11.25 mm
for stainless steel, i.e. a mixture of basis materials added up a total of 12.83 mm of metal.
Determining the material thickness of aluminum using the Al/SS model is only accurate
at around reference thicknesses of 3 mm, whereas determining material thickness values
below and above the 3 mm level are of great deviation. All computed material thick-

Al/SS nor the Ti/SS model is found to be well trained in the vicinity of 0 mm material
thickness, i.e. when metal is absent. The accuracy of the computed material thickness

follow a pattern of increasing computed material thickness following the reference mate-

compared to aluminum. Compared to the other metals, stainless steel is fitted much
better in both models. In general the difference between reference and computed value
is around a few mm, the only exception being about 0 mm (and 6 mm in the case of

in the form of an almost constant material thickness estimate within a short interval as

higher for stainless steel than for aluminum. Significant difference in increasing attenua-
tion values can be seen in Figure 5.4, where Figure 5.4b shows additional extrapolation of
attenuation values for aluminum to match the the same reference material thicknesses of
stainless steel. Stainless steel is clearly the dominating compound regarding measured
attenuation values, whereas aluminum does not contribute any noticeable difference.
As a consequence of this, amount of aluminum would be overall underestimated if the
combined amount corresponds to more than 3 mm aluminum or overestimated if be-
low, i.e. no acceptable correlation between reference thickness and computed thickness,
thus leading to higher density estimates as the contribution of stainless steel is favored
nonetheless.

and high intensity values (ˆIL ˆIH) or a six parameter model, including the previous fourth
parameter and two second order parameters (ˆI2
L,H) could prove a viable solution to the
extensive off-set of the material thickness of aluminum compared to stainless steel. The
equations explained in Section 2.4.2 was implemented into a Matlab script using the
same measurement data as that used in the case of three fitting parameters to calculate
the new fitting parameters. Applying these additional fitting parameters to the same
measurement data produced a new set of material thickness estimates presented in Fig-

parameter MD model. No apparent improvement in computed estimates of aluminum
are found compared to the estimates of the three parameter model. All estimates were
found within the same mm range as was found for the three fitting parameter model.
Adding the additional term in the form of a mixed ˆIL ˆIH term thus proved no significant
accuracy of material thickness estimates for aluminum, based on the results gathered
in this thesis. Material thickness estimates for stainless steel are, similar to the three
fitting parameter model, very accurate. Subsequent density estimates would then suffer
by underestimates of aluminum content in favor of stainless steel if the combined alu-
minum content corresponded to thicknesses above 3 mm or overestimates of aluminum
content if below; results would nevertheless be an overall overestimate of density level.
Just as when using the three fitting parameter model, this model constantly gave the
same aluminum thickness as output value. Altering the density ratio in a similar fashion
as in Section 5.4.1 produced the same improved accuracy of the simulated test material
at the expense of stainless steel as the three parameter MD model.
Implementing a six parameter fitting model on the measured aluminum and stainless
steel data provided the material thickness estimates presented in Figure 5.7c & 5.7d.

overestimates in the lower levels, similar to that seen for three and four fitting param-

MV Projection
Measurements
The dual-energy CT material decomposition was performed in the projection domain,
due to several previous studies proving that this was the best approach regarding metals
[29, 31, 48, 65, 75]. The data obtained by the CT scanner was transferred to the com-
puter in projection domain format and hereafter processed by the computer by iterative
reconstruction methods transforming the data into the image domain. Raw projection
data was not available and a forward projection had to occur before further data pro-
cessing in the projection domain. This was an extensive work process compared to just
performing the material decomposition process in the image domain or working directly
with raw data.
This thesis does not only concern physics within the diagnostic energy range of CT,
but also radiation physics within the MeV energy range of radio therapeutic treatment
methods.
To compare the work process, liability, and convenience, the material de-
composition for the MV images was performed in the projection domain but using an
image-based MD approach. The acquired data consisted of projectional cone-beam im-
age projections similar to what a well-known 2D x-ray scanner provides except that this
is in the MeV energy range. Venturing into the MeV x-ray energy range results in a loss
of photoelectric influence, while maintaining the dominating interaction of Compton
scattering and more frequent occurrence of pair production, as seen by Figure 2.7 and
2.8. Compton scattering is, without the influence of the photoelectric effect, directly
proportional to the material density (see Eq. 2.8). Beam hardening and photon starva-
tion are thus significantly reduced, leading to a major decrease in artifacts and noise in
the resulting images - image-based MD is thus a viable approach.
Limited time was available at the MV Truebeam LINAC used for the experiments, since
all work had to be started and ended under supervision and outside of the clinical work
hours. Performing the MD with an image-based approach would then be a reasonable
choice to conserve work time and make sure that a proper MD model could be estab-
lished.
Images taken at two different energies is a requirement for MD. While several MV
energies were available, 6 MV and 15 MV was chosen. These two energies represent a
huge difference in energy, while simultaneously displaying a different type of dominating

6.2
Reduced Metal Artifacts
Beam hardening and photon starvation play a significant role in x-ray physics within
the energy ranges of diagnostic x-ray imaging. These two effects generate disturbing
metallic artifacts. The lower the x-ray energy gets, the more artifacts will be seen. The
80 kVp and 140 kVp CT scans suffered from this, as artifacts were present and caused
distortion in the resulting images. This issue would have been partly resolved by in-
creasing the x-ray tube current [8, 29]. Figures 6.4a and 6.4b display the 80 kVp and
140 kVp scans CT images of the hip implants from a coronal viewpoint, respectively.
Distortion in the form of artifacts and noise in the images directly lead to an increase
in measurement errors and uncertainties. Increasing the x-ray tube voltage leads to a
higher on-average x-ray energy, directly leading to a decrease in artifacts and noise as
the photons are able to penetrate the object to a greater extent due to a lesser mass
attenuation of the materials [29, 34, 71]. Figures 6.4a and 6.4b show a clear improve-
ment in image quality already at an increase of 60 kV, corresponding to an increase of
∼18 keV. Images produced at an MV LINAC were thus expected to contain minor to
no artifacts or noise compared with the keV CT scans. Figures 6.4c and 6.4d, 6 MV and
15 MV images, respectively, confirm this hypothesis to its fullest. Compared to figures
6.4a and 6.4b no artifacts or noise are present and the insides of the hip implants are
visualized in greater detail. The x-ray energy increase between the 6 MV and 15 MV
scan is much greater than between the two CT scans, resulting in even greater penetra-
tion abilities of the photons. Further reduction of artifacts and noise should be present
in the 15 MV scan compared to that of 6 MV but is not observable, instead the hip im-
plants appear more faint due to this lowered mass attenuation and increased penetration.
MV x-ray images are thus proven to significantly reduce artifacts and noise at the disad-
vantage of losing information from lower density constituents, leading to higher density
materials being favored in the MD. Density levels of objects containing several lower
density constituents are thereby overestimated.

7.1
Basis Materials and Hip Implant
Composition
The sets of basis materials, Al/SS and Ti/SS, used for establishing two MD models
were chosen due to their frequent use in the medical industry. The overall assumption
of the hip implants being composed of either a combination of these three materials or
something with an attenuation degree in between, was based on this reasoning. Manu-
als/instructions for the hip implants proved that both titanium and stainless steel are
present, but in unspecified amounts [56, 20].
External references [73, 49, 64] define
the material distribution in the Corail® hip implant. While this may be true for some
models, it is unknown if this distribution matches the Corial® hip implants used in this
thesis, thus rendering the overall material composition of all the test implants unspeci-
fied. Measurements of the densities (Table 5.2) of all implants and materials used then
serve as the most reliable source of density reference. While these measurements were
simple and accurate, determining the density in separate areas, such as the Corail® hip
implant head and stem, was not possible. The hip implants were borrowed with cour-
tesy of the department of Orthopedics, Herlev Hospital, and had to be returned in the
same condition as received, thereby limiting the option of separating regions from one

7.2
Model Accuracy and Material
Composition
The accuracy of the Ti/SS model was found slightly better than that of the Al/SS model
due to greater flexibility in determining the amount of titanium (Figure 5.3c) compared
to aluminum (Figure 5.3a). The density measurements listed in Table 5.2 shows the
titanium tweezers used to establish the Ti/SS MD model to clearly deviate from the
reference density value of titanium listed in Table 4.2, while a good correlation to the
reference value was found for the other titanium insert. The Ti/SS MD model was estab-
lish using setups containing both the titanium insert and tweezers under the assumption
that they shared the same density. This may have be the reason for such great uncer-
tainties, while the favorable fit may be a coincidence of the wrongful assumption that
both ”titanium” objects are of the same density. Using the conducted Ti/SS model for
any clinical implementation were thus assessed as unfit. Further use of the Ti/SS model
was seized, hence the lack of material thickness and density maps for the MV images.
However, using titanium and stainless steel as basis materials with consistent use of
authentic titanium components could lead to an improved MD model accuracy. The
difference in mass attenuation between the two materials would not be as significant as
that found between aluminum and stainless steel. The attenuating contribution from
titanium would be more significant, and variations in titanium thickness would thus be
more apparent in the measured intensity values. Increased contrast between the two

7.3 Additional Reference Material Thicknesses

basis material would be obtained and thereby improved material thickness estimates of
both titanium and stainless steel was expected to be achieved.
The MD was performed under the assumptions that all components of the same material,
aluminum, stainless steel, and titanium had the exact same density. This assumption is
obviously not correct, especially in the case of stainless steel. Stainless steel is an alloy
and comes in many different types depending on its ability to avoid corroding, compat-
ibility with the human body and so on. The measured density of the stainless steel
components (Table 5.2) matched well with the most common medical type (SAE 316L)
but not all medical metal implants containing stainless steel may include exactly this
type, which could lead to additional mismatches in the estimated content by the MD
model. Albeit the density differences between different stainless steel types are minor,
this is something worth considering.
7.3
Additional Reference Material Thicknesses
Previous studies [65, 48, 29] conducting MD have used a wide range of material thick-
nesses as references when creating their MD model, which formed the basis for obtaining
viable MD models. This thesis has used rather few material thicknesses as references.
These have therefore been configured in varying settings to achieve an adequate range of
thicknesses for the MD model. Simple setups were scanned, altered, then to be scanned
again repeatedly. Figure B.2 shows two of these setup variations. Creating an MD model
with such an experimental approach limits the amount of available reference thicknesses,
while simultaneously taking an extensive amount of time. Limited setup possibilities
similarly limit the precision of the MD model. Ample amounts of basis materials would
have allowed for the possibility of molding a more comprehensive set of reference thick-
nesses into a lower number of setups, e.g. by creating a metal grid with squares of
different thicknesses similar to the approach used in Hao et. al [48]. The time needed
for altering the setups would be substantially reduced and the precision of the model
would be increased.
The maximum reference material thickness achieved with the metal pieces in the ex-
perimental setups did not reach the maximum thickness of the Corail® hip implants. It
is impossible for the model to accurately estimate material thicknesses outside the range
of material thicknesses used when creating the model, hence the incorrect estimates of
the material thickness in the rotational heads of the Corail® hip implants. Extrapo-
lating the measurement data to increase the reference material thicknesses of the MD
model demonstrated improved accuracy in determining material thickness estimates of
stainless steel in the rotational heads, whereas aluminum estimates were still inaccurate.
Optimizing the MD model would thereby require an expansion of material thicknesses
used when creating the model in order to estimate a wide range of material thicknesses
if a truly viable and reliable MD model for clinical applications is to be pursued.

7.4
Number of Fitting Parameters
The linear three parameter MD model used in this thesis, found in the initial motiva-
tion and basis article, Skaarup et. al [65], proved to work unsuccessfully in determining
aluminum material thickness and thereby density of hip implants when using an LSF
method. The work performed by Hao et al [48] proves that an additional amount of fit-
ting parameters lead to promising results. The three parameter model was thus expanded
to test out if four and six fitting parameters would improve the model. Applying four pa-
rameters to an LSF model saw no significant improvements on the results of aluminum
estimates (Figure 5.3), while six fitting parameters led to considerable improvements.
Aluminum thickness estimates were still found to be unreasonably high in areas of high
density and material thicknesses. The MD model could then benefit from an implemen-
tation of either additional fitting parameters or a different fitting approach than an LSF
method. Time did not allow for further testing of different fitting approaches during
this work and will be left for future improved research.
7.5
Artifacts
Metal artifacts as a consequence of beam hardening and photon starvation had a sig-
nificant impact on the error propagation calculations and the HU values of the various
materials used. The standard deviation values of the average HU values of the materials
are too high for proper use and should thus be considered as guiding values rather than
useful and quantitative.
The forward projection algorithm originally created for MD of CBCT scans did, however,
compensate for metal artifacts resulting in usable ROI assignment. Despite this, arti-
facts had already impacted the acquired CT data, resulting in high standard deviations
when dealing with high thicknesses and low scan energy, as seen in Table 5.3. The ad-
ditional compensation of photon starvation by integrating over an increased number of
detectors, thus increasing the overall photon count, did not lead to any distinct decrease
in uncertainties. This could be a consequence of having data already ”infected” by metal
artifacts when reconstructed by the CT scanner. Subsequent forward projection would
then have no effect on predetermined ”polluted” data, resulting in no additional mea-
surement improvements. Working directly in the projection domain with data extracted
from the CT scanner, prior to reconstruction, would ultimately lead to improved metal
artifact reduction and a bypass of the hidden reconstruction algorithms implemented by
the manufacturer, yielding more accurate MD estimates.
Initial CT scans showed inconsistent intensity values for the same reference material
thicknesses, e.g. 3.75 mm of stainless steel showed different intensity values across scans
acquired at the same energy and experimental setup. To check if something was wrong
with the scanner, a glass of water was put next to various experimental setups. Water
is known to have a consistent CT number of 0 HU. Air calibration steps were likewise

7.6 Projection Algorithms

performed prior to each scan session to check if the scanner suffered from calibration
errors.
Water content in all of the scans was found to have 0 HU, and calibration prior to scan
sessions showed no improvements - inconsistent results were still present. It was there-
fore decided to avoid as many manufacturer specific reconstruction algorithms (such as
MAR) and advance with the simplest reconstructions available. First step of every CT
scan consisted of scout scans, where x-ray tube current to be used was determined by
the computer based on the objects scanned and how much they attenuated the beam.
Modern techniques allowed for varying tube current depending on the scanned material
at every instance, e.g. higher tube current for metals and lower tube current for tissue.
To make sure as few inconsistencies as possible were present during the CT scans, a
constant tube current of 315 mA (high enough to avoid major artifacts) and the sim-
plest reconstruction techniques were chosen. Consistent intensity values followed as a
consequence of this choice.
7.6
Projection Algorithms
The forward projection algorithm optimized for CBCT was adapted for this thesis but
no additional information besides it being a FDK-reconstruction algorithm came with
it.
Results gained through this forward projection did, however, prove to be useful
and matched well with the DECT data, allowing for proper MD. Lack of knowledge
about the precise reconstruction algorithms implemented in the GE scanner, left the
option to use the implemented MAR algorithms in favor of the common backprojected
reconstructed without additional enhancement algorithms to avoid a many unknown
contributing factors as possible.
Future research would benefit from avoiding these
unknown algorithms altogether by applying self-designed reconstruction algorithms or
working directly with raw scan data in the projection domain.
7.7
Patient Data
Applying the six fitting parameter Al/SS model on patient data showed that some form of
tissue compensation is required to achieve any form of material thickness estimates from
patient scan data. The images generated in Figure 5.17 were based on monochromatic 44
keV and 62 keV images, as these values, based on reference literature [36] were determined
to be the approximate mean energy of 80 kV and 140 kV scans. This is not necessarily
true for the CT scanner used in this thesis. Furthermore, patient CT scn acquisitions
did not comply with the x-ray tube settings used in establishing the MD model. The
DECT scan of the patient utilized the automatic x-ray tube current setting, in which the
current was adjusted accordingly based on the attenuating properties of the insides of
the patient. The same applies to the patient data borrowed from Rigshospitalet. Future
patient data studies would show a greatly improved MD if acquired with the exact same

7.8
Dealing with Errors
None of the measurements taken are without uncertainty. Uncertainties occur when
the reference thickness of the scanned materials is measured, just as there is a certain
standard deviation to the measured intensity values. This affects the end result as errors
in the gathered measurements, and thereby errors in the calculated results.
7.8.1
Fear of Being Ill-Posed
Having small variations in problems where the solution is found by solving a linear equa-
tion of the form b = Ax, could potentially lead to large variations in the result. Such
an ill-posed solution with great variations in the resulting material thicknesses and den-
sities, would ultimately prove the results useless to real life implementation.
The solution to the linear equation does, however, satisfy the requirements of a well-
posed1 problem. (1) a solution exists to the linear equation, (2) it is unique, (3) the
solution depends continuously on the input data, and (4) the determinant of the mea-
surement matrix (”A” in Eq. 2.30) is non-zero. Meeting these four requirements renders
the data well-posed instead of the horrific ill-posed alternative. The solution does how-
ever not come completely without errors. These errors or uncertainties are all calculated
by error propagation techniques adjusted to the LSF approach. Explanations of the tech-
niques used for the error propagation calculations are attached in Appendix A.
A possible solution to this would be to factorize in a weighting factor upon the sec-
ondary basis material with the lowest attenuation properties. Regularization methods
would serve as a great option to solve this, namely Tikhonov regularization. Applying
such weight factors through a Tikhonov regularization method was pursued briefly but
without any significant success. Considering regularization as part of an MD model
would be worth considering in future work.
Despite the considerable access to stainless steel and aluminum during the work for
the thesis, many of the metal pieces were found unsuitable for CT scanning, mostly
due to being either too big, oddly shaped or too thin for proper measurement of the
thicknesses. Creativity was therefore needed when organizing experimental setups. This
resulted in a lot of scan data with only few alterations to the setup between each scan,
while still covering a variety of different material thicknesses.

7.9
Image-Based MV MD
Simple and fast image-based MD from projection images acquired in the MV range of
a LINAC proves that the concept also applies to scans outside of the diagnostic energy
range. Similar to the MD in projection domain performed off of CT scans, MV scan pro-
jections prove that working in this domain will provide MD opportunities. The approach
and concept used in the MV projections are similar to the approach of image domain
MD of CT scans. Instead of establishing the MD model from intensity values (as in the
case of the MV projections), one would establish the MD model from HU values when
working with CT images in the image domain. Utilizing HU values of CT scans in the
image domain was not pursued in the work of this thesis, but would work just as well as
the MD of the MV projections. Image domain MD is assessed as possible from the MV
findings but whether it is more accurate than the projection domain MD done in this
thesis is left unknown. In depth discovery of this hypothesis is left for future research.
Density values estimated by the MV MD model show distinct overestimates compared
to the reference values in Table 5.2. The overestimates are most likely caused by the
model failing to recognize a sufficient contribution of aluminum content compared to
stainless steel, which seems favorable based on the accuracy estimations presented in
Figure 5.3a.
Metal artifacts are reduced significantly in the MV energy regime, resulting in projection
images of clear resolution. Enhanced resolution will improve the treatment executioner’s
ability to confirm location and composition of the metal implant prior to treatment. Two
MV projections at two different energies prior to treatment are sufficient for an MD,
which will verify the density estimates previously measured through diagnostic DECT
images, thus confirming the correct dose calculated in the TPS.
Unfortunately, time did not allow for thorough MV testing since the LINAC used in
this work had limited availability due to the machinery only being available during clini-
cal hours - a time in which the instrument is mainly used for patient treatment. During
this time an attempt was made to imitate MV CBCT by acquiring a projection image
then to rotate and repeat. In order to properly imitate such an MV CBCT scan, a wide
number of images at a varying angle had to be acquired. The service window in which
the acquisitions were acquired during this work allowed for varying projection angles but
the process of changing the projection angle was lengthy, hence time did not allow for
an attempt at imitating MV CBCT.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
DECT technology provides visual and quantitative information about composition of
metallic implants. Material decomposition techniques utilize this technology to a large
extent through CT scans with an extended CT scale. Data processed in the projection
domain proves accurate estimates of material thicknesses and density values of hip im-
plants of various size and composition.
CT scans using aluminum and stainless steel as basis materials acquired at tube volt-
ages of 80 kVp and 140 kVp proved to be viable for a material decomposition model of
hip implants through a six parameter least squares fitting method. Density estimates
of three different hip implants were were found to agree with measured density values.
Based on the density values by both the MD model and measured ones, titanium would
be assigned as designated material for the three hip implants during dose calculations.
MV scans using identical basis material setup and material decomposition model ac-
quired at 6 MV and 15 MV showed overestimated density levels but with a significant
decrease in artifacts. Assigning a designated material during dose calculations based
on the MV MD model could erroneously lead to a choice of stainless steel instead of
titanium. MV MD with the current six fitting parameter Al/SS model requires improve-
ment before acceptable applications to clinical practice.
Implementing material decomposition as part of the clinical routine in cancer treat-
ment of patients with metal implants is evaluated as viable with aluminum and stainless
steel as basis materials if tissue compensation is included in the model. Nevertheless,
titanium and stainless steel are assessed as a more accurate alternative as basis ma-
terials in combination with additional fitting parameters or with an entirely different
fitting model, a customized experimental setup of various metal thicknesses, and data
processing of raw projection scan data.

CHAPTER 9
Future Prospects
The work done in this thesis proves that successful MD is possible with very few tools
at hand, but if utmost precision is a requirement for proper clinical implementation,
additional work has to be done. Future improvements would include a wide range of ref-
erence material thicknesses of basis materials used for creating the MD model. Stainless
steel and aluminum are both abundant metals suitable for pre-made experimental con-
figurations, e.g. creating a set of ”stairs” with increasing thicknesses capable of stacking.
Examples of such are previously seen in Skaarup et. al [65] and Hao et. al [48], both
with promising results.
Expanding the amount of fitting parameters from three to six lead to more accurate
estimations of material thicknesses. Hao et. al [48] have successfully implemented an
MD model with seven fitting parameters, although they did not use a LSF, nor a linear
model. Further improvements upon a model with several fitting parameters would in-
evitably result in more accurate material thickness and density estimations.
Avoiding disturbances from metal artifacts causing of beam hardening and photon star-
vation in CT images would lead to smaller degree of uncertainties - a problem which
may be addressed by scanning at an increased tube current or working directly with
raw data extracted from the CT scanner. A research agreement with the manufacturer,
GE, was required for accessing raw data. Applying for such was outside the time frame
of this thesis. However, future MD implementation would benefit greatly from such an
agreement.
Implants do usually not consist of only two sets of materials and assuming such could
potentially prove too crude an approximation in some specific situations. Expanding
an MD model to consider three or more materials is previously seen [55] and something
worth considering in the case of patients with metallic implants of very different com-
position situated close to each other, allowing the radiologist to separate implants from
bone or tissue, or to simply provide a more in depth analysis.
The need for more efficient cancer treatment of patients with metallic implants is only
getting more and more relevant as the human implant industry keeps evolving. MD is a
technique far from being perfected, but it has already shown that it is worth considering
and will undoubtedly play a significant role in the future of cancer treatment to come.
