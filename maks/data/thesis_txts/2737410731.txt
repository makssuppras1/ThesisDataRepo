Abstract
The global dissemination of antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) is a serious
concern.
And
one
important
reason
behind
their
widespread
is
the
plasmid-mediated ARGs spread.
Advanced sequencing technologies and bioinformatics tools have played a
crucial role in revealing the complex genetic networks and mobile genetic
elements that facilitate the spread of ARGs in different bacterial species and
environments. The traditionally used metagenomic sequencing targets the entire
genetic material in a specific environment. Compared with this method, single-cell
sequencing technology can study the differences between cells at the level of
individual cells, thereby improving our understanding of the heterogeneity of cell
populations. Here, my research focuses on developing and using bioinformatics
methods to analyze microbial single-cell genome sequencing information and
reveal the differences and heterogeneity between individual cells in microbial
populations.
In this project, I mainly designed a bioinformatics pipeline for the analysis of the
High-throughput single-cell sequencing data. Including data demultiplexing,
tracking the coverage of single-cell data following demultiplexing, determining the
copy number and distribution of plasmids in single-cell samples, and investigating
drug resistance gene distribution are a few examples. Subsequent research has
demonstrated the superiority of single-cell sequencing technology over other
sequencing methods for analyzing the variations among individual cells.

1 Introduction
1.1 Common ARGs
The spread of antibiotic-resistant bacteria (ARB) and antibiotic resistance genes
(ARG)(Zhuang et al. 2021) in the microbiota is an important global public health
issue. Many environmental habitats are under threat due to the overuse of antibiotics
and evolutionary changes in resistance genes. ARBs and ARGs can accumulate in
water, soil, and air in waste water from farms, cities, hospitals, as well as from
wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) or surface runoff. The spread of these ARGs
factors increases the risk of ARGs spread and poses a huge challenge to the public
health system.
For example, the spread of beta-lactamase depends on multiple factor (Livermore D
M.1998), including antibiotic use, plasmid-mediated spread of -lactamase, and
bacterial genetic variation. Widespread among different bacteria, the spread of these
enzymes is mainly through plasmid delivery. Genetic variation of bacteria is also an
important factor in the spread of drug resistance. Therefore, the spread of drug
resistance is a complex process involving the interaction of multiple factors.
Therefore, the purpose of this article is to use single-cell sequencing technology to
explore the distribution of ARGs in plasmid genes. Provide technical reference for
exploring the spread of ARG gene.

1.2.1 Escherichia coli
Escherichia coli is a well-known pathogen in the intestines of humans and animals,
mainly parasitic in the large intestine. It is a short, motile, spore-free Gram-negative
bacterium with blunt ends. It is an important pathogen in humans and certain animals
that has an enhanced ability to overcome or subvert host defenses and cause
extraintestinal disease in healthy hosts (Erb et al. 2007). Infection with pathogenic E.
coli strains can result in three common clinical syndromes: enteric/diarrheal illness,
urinary tract infection, and sepsis/meningitis.
The genetic structure of E. coli is influenced by multiple host and environmental
factors, and genetic determinants related to bacterial virulence reflect adaptations to

1.3 Plasmid
Plasmid is a small circular DNA molecule that is independent of the host cell's
chromosome and is widely found in bacteria, archaea and some eukaryotes (such as
yeast) . Unlike the host genome, plasmids are able to replicate independently and
are passed to daughter cells during cell division through a variety of mechanisms
(Salje 2010).These plasmids usually carry genes that are beneficial to host survival
and reproduction, such as antibiotic resistance genes, heavy metal resistance
genes, virulence factors, and enzyme genes for specific metabolic pathways, thereby
enabling bacteria carrying these plasmids to compete under specific environmental
conditions (Koutsoumanis et al. 2021).
Plasmids also play an important role in studying antibiotic resistance. Because
plasmids can carry and spread antibiotic resistance genes, their role in the spread of
resistance has attracted much attention. Through plasmid-mediated horizontal gene
transfer, antibiotic resistance genes can spread rapidly between different bacterial
populations, leading to the spread of drug-resistant strains (Hull et al. 2021).
Through single-cell sequencing technology (Zheng et al. 2022), we can analyze in
detail the behavior of bacteria carrying drug-resistant genes in different environments
and reveal the distribution and expression of drug-resistant genes in bacterial
populations. These studies help to understand the dynamics of resistance spread
and provide scientific basis for formulating antibiotic use strategies and developing
new antibacterial drugs.
The aim of this research in this project is to study the distribution of ARGs
(antimicrobial resistance gene) at the single cell level. However, to control cell
distribution within droplets (see Single Cell Sequencing section below), we need to
determine the copy number of the plasmid gene in each Single Amplified Genome
(SAG) to ensure the accuracy of the distribution data obtained.
1.4 Illumina Sequencing
Compared to Sanger sequencing, Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) offers a
better throughput, faster speed, and lower cost. Large-scale genomics research is
made feasible by this technology, which has numerous applications in target region
sequencing (Mamanova et al. 2010), transcriptome sequencing , whole-genome
sequencing (Dey et al. 2015), and epigenomics (Hirst and Marra 2010). NGS's
primary function is to read many DNA fragments concurrently and simultaneously in
order to produce comprehensive genomic information via effective sequence reading
and data analysis.

Worldwide, Illumina is a leading developer and producer of next-generation
sequencing technology, with its sequencing systems being utilized extensively.
Reversible end termination technology is the foundation of Illumina sequencing,
which reads bases that have been fluorescently labeled (Proudnikov et al. 1996).
Sample preparation, library building, bridge amplification, sequencing reaction, and
data analysis are all included in the procedure. Bridge amplification is one of the
most important ones: o. On the sequencing flow cell's surface, it creates high-density
DNA clusters that produce enough fluorescent signals for further sequencing.

1.5 Single-cell sequencing
A new wave of technologies in the fields of genomes, transcriptomics, proteomics,
and epigenomics is being created by ongoing advancements in next-generation
sequencing technology. With the growing ability to target individual cells, these
sequencing-based technologies will make it possible to answer a great deal of
unanswered issues.
As opposed to metagenomic sequencing, which focuses on all of the genetic
material (DNA) inside a particular setting. This project's research reveals the
heterogeneity and variations among cells by concentrating on individual cells. With a
technique called single-cell sequencing, individual cells from a metagenomic
sample—such as sewage or excrement—are encapsulated in droplets. The DNA
from each cell is then extracted and sequenced independently. Similarly, even
though WGS makes it simpler to evaluate SNPs and can access all of an individual's
genes. Nevertheless, WGS required culture because the isolates' individual cell
sequences could not be identified. Consequently, single-cell sequencing will allow us
to barcode label various cells without the necessity for cell culture. Consequently,
this permits a more in-depth examination of certain isolates. Single-cell sequencing
is generally a more precise technique that works in conjunction with the other
sequencing techniques discussed above.
Bacterial resistance genes are currently the subject of extensive research, and the
corresponding technologies are fairly developed. Nevertheless, there isn't much
research on the newly popular single-cell sequencing, and most of them concentrate
on 16s RNA and the transcriptome. This time, we employed a technique called
Microbe-seq(Zheng et al. 2022), which involves encapsulating individual cells into
SAGs, lysing and amplifying them, and then barcoding. After that, Sequencing the
whole Mixed isolates. The collected sequencing data is then demultiplexed using
these barcodes to provide whole-genome data for individual cells. Lastly, data
visualization and additional analysis are conducted using bioinformatics tools.
1.6 Bioinformation analyses
1.6.1 Alignment
In bioinformatics, sequence alignment is a method of arranging DNA, RNA, or
protein sequences to identify similar regions that may be the result of functional,
structural, or evolutionary relationships between sequences. to find the similarities
and differences between them. This technology is of great significance for revealing
the evolutionary relationships, functions, and structures of sequences.
==PAGE:11==
In the comparison of two sequences, if there is a difference in the same region of
alignment, if it is a difference at a single site, it can be called a point mutation. In
addition, insertion and deletion also exist. There are many tools for alignment at
present. In this article, BWA (Burrows-Wheeler Alignment)(Li 2013) and BLAST
(Basic Local Alignment Search Tool)(Korf, Yandell, and Bedell n.d.)are mainly used.
Apart from that, in resfinder, The KMA(K-mer Alignment)(Clausen n.d.) tool was used
to finde the ARGs in the data.
These tools can perform global alignment (Global Alignment), that is, find the best
match within the entire sequence; they can also perform local alignment (Local
Alignment), that is, find the optimal matching region in certain fragments of the
sequence. Alignment results are usually presented in the form of alignment scores,
similarity percentages, and conserved regions.
1.6.2 BWA
An approach for aligning DNA sequences called BWA-MEM (Burrows-Wheeler
Aligner - Maximal Exact Matches) works particularly well with longer readings (>70
bp). It locates the corresponding locations of these seeds in the reference genome
by first extracting fixed-length seed sequences from the reads. Subsequently, every
maximum exact match (MEM) discovered is chained; that is, MEMs that are near to
one another and grouped in a certain sequence are joined to create a chain.
Mismatches, insertions, and deletions are considered in this stage. Subsequently,
the chain undergoes local alignment, the reads and reference genome are precisely
aligned using the Smith-Waterman technique, and the alignment score is computed.
The alignment with the highest score is chosen as the final alignment after the
alignment quality is computed based on the scores.
1.6.3 NCBI- BLAST
NCBI BLAST (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool) is an algorithm used to compare
biological sequences (DNA, RNA, or proteins) to find similarities and differences
between them. The core idea of ​the BLAST algorithm is to quickly find locally similar
regions for sequence comparison. First, the query sequence is divided into a series
of fixed-length subsequences (k-mers or words), and these short k-mers are
searched in the database to find seeds that match the query sequence. BLAST then
expands on both ends of these seed matches, looking for longer matching regions.
The expansion process works by calculating a match score, which is based on the
similarity of matching bases or amino acids. BLAST will filter all found high-scoring
matching sections and only retain matches whose scores exceed a certain threshold.
Subsequently, the significance of the match is evaluated using statistical methods
such as E-value, which represents the expected number of random matches in a
==PAGE:12==
given database. The smaller the E-value, the more significant the match. Finally,
BLAST outputs the alignment results, including matching sequences, scores, E
values, and alignment details.
1.6.4 KMA
KMA (K-mer Alignment) is an efficient sequence alignment algorithm based on
k-mer, specially designed to process large-scale genome data. The core idea of
​KMA is to use fixed-length k-mer fragments to quickly perform sequence alignment.
First, the reference sequence and query sequence are split into subsequences of
length k, which are called k-mers. Then, KMA creates an index table for each k-mer.
Through this index table, the position of the k-mer in the query sequence in the
reference sequence can be quickly found. Next, KMA performs local expansion and
alignment around the found k-mer matching positions to identify longer similar
regions, and calculates a score. The advantage of KMA lies in its speed and
processing power, and it can effectively handle repetitive regions and variations in
the genome, such as single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) and insertions and
deletions .

Density Gradient Function is a tool used to estimate data distribution and is
commonly used in statistical analysis and data visualization. Unlike the frequency
histogram, the density gradient function smoothes the data to provide a continuous
distribution curve showing the probability density of the data. Its core is Kernel
==PAGE:30==
Density Estimation (KDE), which uses kernel functions to smooth data to generate
smooth probability density curves.
Then X is a continuous random variable, and and 𝑓𝑋(𝑋) is its probability density function.
This function smoothes the data to generate a continuous density curve to illustrate
the characteristics of the data distribution. The horizontal axis represents the value
range of the data, and the vertical axis represents the density value, that is, the
relative frequency of each value in the data distribution. After normalization, the area
under the curve is equal to 1. The height of the curve reflects the density of the data
near the value. The higher it is, the more concentrated the data is; it does not
represent the proportion of the data in the total data. The width of the curve indicates
the degree of dispersion of the data distribution. The wider it is, the more dispersed
the data is. The peak position of the curve represents the most common values ​or
ranges in the data, while the tails can reveal extreme values ​or outliers in the data.

3.1.5 Comparison
Here we used a specialized demultiplexing approach to searching barcodes that we
designed ourselves. When compared to the second method (Ultraplex), three
barcodes can be found. because the final result can be obtained in one step, there is
no need to generate many intermediate results like Ultraplex, thus saving memory.
However, the fuzzy search results led to a lot of data being classified into many
SAGs in the data if the levenshtein distance become larger because of the rise in
barcodes and the fact that many barcodes are quite close. Since then, numerous
SAGs have shown a significant quantity of duplicate data. It significantly lengthens
the program's runtime, thus we look for more effective demultiplexing techniques.
One of the reasons for the lengthy search time in fuzzy search is that we do not
specify where the barcode should be placed in the sequence.
Our demultiplexing algorithm searches based on the input data. It not only needs to
read a large amount of data, but also builds different barcode lists, and then
searches each fastq reading based on this list. It consumes a lot of time and
memory. During this period, It took about 5 hours to complete the demultiplexing of a
species single-cell sequence. In this regard, we can partially optimize.
Ultraplex uses an algorithm that first generates all possible barcodes with the same
length as the barcode. After that, each sequence is compared to every user-defined
barcode to determine which match is the best (reads containing multiple best
matches are ignored). An approximate O(1)(Chivers and Sleightholme 2015) efficiency
can be achieved by matching each read to the correct barcode or barcode pair by
saving these precomputed best matches in a Python dictionary.
In contrast, in my program, the dfs function is a recursive function, which calls itself
at runtime. In this function, we refer to a third-party package (fuzzy search), and its
function is to retrieve the input sequence from the beginning. Each search only
advances one position at the beginning, which greatly increases the complexity of
our calculations and consumes more time.
Relatively speaking, using a third-party Python package can indeed better customize
the demultiplexing process and standardize the format of the output file. Compared
with the programs we wrote ourselves, the Ultraplex has advantages in running
speed, memory usage, demultiplexing success rate, etc.Its algorithm has lower
complexity and higher performance, thus an efficient algorithm can significantly
improve computational efficiency and the success rate of demultiplexing.
However, due to Ultraplex's strict requirements on data, even if we remove all data
with excessive wildcards. Ultraplex still cannot demultiplex successfully, and only a
very small number of data can find a barcode. In contrast, the program we designed
is more specific and can meet the requirements of demultiplexing this data.

3.1.6 Optimizing
In fuzzy search, we consider whether we can find inspiration from the experimental
process to optimize the program. For example, we have linkers betwen barcodes,
but the actual linker or when the barcode mutates. We tend to know what kind of
mutation occurs, such as A to T. We set different mutations to obtain different scores
and filter out the sequences with the highest scores to save energy and reduce data
duplication, that is, to avoid one data entering multiple SAGs’ lengths being different.
From now on, we can consider setting filter conditions during the search process,
such as our preference and the interval betwen two barcodes. Similarly, we can
consider multi-threading processing, which will reduce our time, but require more
computing power.

3.3.1 Calculation method 1
In this part, we used a target gene as a template and use BLAST to match the reads
in the data. To ensure that there is only one cell in a SAG, we match the replication
origin site in the chromosome as the quotient of the two values, that is, the number
of copies in this SAG.
The problem is that if there are multiple replication origin sites in the sequence, it will
cause us to repeat multiple matches when using BLAST. That is, one read can input
multiple results, so the plasmid copy number we get will also be Multiplied.
Therefore, we need to determine the number of sequences of each origin of
replication we found in their respective references and divide by it when drawing to
get the approximately correct plasmid copy number. We only found 5 sites that can
match the replication origin site gene on the reference of E2_p2. So the rest of the
data remains unchanged, we only need to divide the E2_p2 data by 5.
Another problem is that not all reads that successfully match the replication start site
can match all 5 start sites. Due to time constraints, we can only simply divide by five
to reduce duplications. The actual number of matches should be smaller than 5
times. If we still have time, we can classify the matching data in the BLAST report by
its position in the original sequence to get a more accurate plasmid copy number.
==PAGE:49==
3.3.2 Calculation method 2
In addition to this method, we also used another method to calculate the copy
number of plasmids:
First, we classify the files according to the output results of BLAST. This is the data
on the chromosomes and plasmids of each cell after demultiplexing. We then
counted the corresponding reads from the plasmid to the chromosome in each cell.
From here we recode Rc (Reads for Chromosome) and Rp (Reads for Plasmid) for
each SAG data.
After that, we queried the reference size for both chromosome and plasmid, and
recorded them as Sc (Size of Chromesome) and Sp (Size of Plasmid). Then we
calculated the copy number of plasmid in each SAG
The following is a density plot of the plasmid copy number of Salmonella calculated
by this method.
