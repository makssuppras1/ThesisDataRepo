Abstract 
 
This assignment has investigated different energy systems to supply the energy needed in a 
single-family house. A typical single-family house has been studied. 
For that, a research has been done to find the energy demand in terms of space heating, DHW and 
electricity appliances for a single-family house. An investigation of the energy production has 
been done as well for each case. Electricity prices have been calculated from Nordpool prices 
adding taxes. Finally, accumulation has been studied in order to get the best way to store energy. 
Calculations have been done for the 24 hours of an average day of each month and for the 12 
months of the year.  
 
Four cases have been investigated in order to supply the energy demand of a single-family house. 
First case is based on an electric unit for space heating and an electric water heater for DHW; 
second case consists on a PV system for space heating, DHW and electricity appliances; third 
case is formed by a heat pump for space heating and a heat pump for DHW and last case is based 
on a solar thermal system for space heating and DHW.  
 
A feasibility study has been carried out considering energy production costs first and then also 
product, installation and service cost of the different products of each system. Regarding the 
feasibility study when only energy costs are considered, both systems with and without heat 
storage losses have been studied in order to see the difference not only in the price, but also in the 
behavior of the system. Then, when also product, installation and service costs are taken into 
account tank heat losses are considered in order to get a more realistic study.  
 
Finally, taking all the costs in consideration, an optimal system is found. A CO2 study has been 
done in order to compare also the different systems in terms of environmental friendliness.  
 
It can be concluded that heat pump for space heating and DHW gives the best performance 
regarding costs. It is considerable the difference of more than 10,000 DKK/year between a 
system based on a heat pump and the rest of the studied systems. However, heat pump and floor 
heating system show better performance when no tank is considered because the storage tank cost 
increases the total cost more than the savings on energy costs. It can be seen that there is a 
striking difference in the total cost for different system sizes.

1. Introduction  
 
One of the main society problems is the global warming and its consequences. Higher 
temperatures are driving many disasters including storms, floods, heat waves and droughts. And 
it is considered that human is the number one causing global warming, especially carbon 
pollution by burning of fossil fuels [1]. Besides, Denmark as a country aware of the 
environmental problems and its importance has the ambitious goal of cover the electricity and 
heat of the whole country from renewable energy by 2035 and being free of fossil fuels by 2050 
[2]. Therefore, other sources of energy than fossil fuels have been studied in order to supply the 
energy needed to lower pollution to the environment. Solar power is considered one of the top 10 
renewable energy sources [3].  
 
The objective of this assignment is to investigate the economy for different energy systems to 
supply the energy needed in a single-family house. First of all, a research has been done in order 
to get the data needed regarding a single-family house demand for a whole year, as well as the 
study of accumulation, electricity prices and energy production by the different systems.  
Regarding the energy demand, space heating, DHW and electricity appliances have been 
investigated for a single-family house. The software used to calculate the energy production of a 
solar thermal system for DHW is Spiralsol. Energy production of a solar thermal system for 
space heating and DHW has been calculated with Polysun, software that allows the creation of a 
solar thermal system among others with the demanded characteristics. It gives the possibility of 
creating a whole system from element to element applying the wanted design. A solar thermal 
system has been created with Polysun and investigated in order to find an optimum system. 
Different forms of accumulation have been investigated. Regarding the electricity prices, 
electricity cost has been found in Nordpool prices and taxes have been added in order to get the 
total electricity price for 24 hours of an average day of the month and the 12 months of the year. 
The study has been done for 24 hours of an average day of each month and for 12 months of the 
year.  
 
The different energy systems can be used today in single-family houses. A feasibility study has 
been done in order to get a complete study of the energy supplied by the system as well as the 
costs of the different systems. Therefore, 4 different systems have been studied so that the most 
optimum option can be found. First case consists of a floor heating system for space heating and 
an electrical heated water heater for DHW, first without heat storage losses and then considering 
them and also with and without a storage tank for space heating. Second case is based on a PV 
system for space heating, DHW and electricity appliances with and without a storage tank with 
heat storage losses. The third case contains a heat pump for space heating and a heat pump for 
DHW and the study has been done with and without storage tank for space heating and also for 
different tank volumes and heat pump capacities for DHW again considering heat storage losses. 
And finally, a solar thermal system for space heating and DHW with different tank sizes and heat 
storage losses is studied.

1.1 Space heating 
Annual space heating demand is assumed to be 72.8 kWh/m2 in a single-family house from 2010 
in Copenhagen and taking into consideration a single-family house of 165 m2, final heating 
demand is around 12,000 kWh/year [4]. 
Space heating varies considerably from one month to another due to the ambient temperature 
difference.  
Now, it is possible to calculate the space heating demand for each month and therefore for each 
day of the selected month taking into account the degree-days exposed for 2015 [5]. The degree-
days contain also domestic hot water so the degree-days found in the reference have to be 
subtracted by 100 degree-days/month, which is the corresponding value for domestic hot water. 
Consequently, the heating demand is calculated for each month proportionally to the degree-days. 
January, for example is calculated multiplying the space heating demand (12,000) by the degree 
days in January (485) and divided by the degree days during the year (2,706):

2 Energy production 
Regarding the energy production, different options have been investigated to produce the energy 
needed for space heating and DHW to analyze both costs and energy savings.  
2.1 PV 
PV production is calculated with an excel sheet given by Bengt Peters based on the weather data 
from the Danish Design Reference Year. It calculates the solar radiation on the PV surface for 
each hour of the year taking into consideration different parameters that define PV energy such as 
direct and diffuse radiation, panel dimensions and PV tilt. PV module efficiency is defined as 17 
%. The influence of the tilt has been investigated for 45º and 30º tilts south and the yearly output 
is slightly better for 45º. South has been implemented to prevent grid perturbations at noon. 
Appendix B shows in Table 20 the hourly PV production of the average day of each month. The 
balance sheet (Appendix D) shows PV for space heating and DHW. To get energy or output from 
the PV system, input or energy is needed in order to operate the system. The input is electricity 
bought from the grid, which makes the system working.  The output is the performance from the 
PV system used for space heating, DHW and electricity appliances. The input needed for PV is 
assumed to be 3% of the PV output.

2.2 Solar thermal 
Solar heating production for space heating and DHW is calculated by Polysun [8]. It allows the 
implementation of different elements with specific parameters and gives you the energy output 
and input to supply the space heating demand and hot water consumption.  
 
 
Figure 6. Sketch of the system by Polysun 
Figure 6 displays the system designed in Polysun for space heating and DHW. It contains a solar 
collector and an auxiliary electrical system, which provides the rest of the energy needed. An 
electric heating element is placed in the middle of the heat storage. That is half of the heat storage 
volume can be heated by the electric heating element. The top of the heat exchanger spiral is 
located at 30 % from the bottom of the tank upwards. It also contains a floor heating system. A 
600 l tank has been used. A 2.2 m2 collector panel SK500-ECO-AL is selected from 
SONNENKRAFT. The solar collectors are tilted 45º and facing south. Collector panel has a 
maximum efficiency of 0.81, an incidence angle modifier of 0.96 for an incidence angle of 50º 
and 1st and 2nd order heat loss coefficients of 3.365 W/m2K and 0.021 W/m2K2.

3.2 Taxes 
Nordpool electricity prices are among the highest in OECD (Organization for Economic 
Cooperation and Development) due to the high taxes. End-user prices are based on four different 
elements: production, transmission, distribution elements and PSO (a tax to support renewable 
energies). 
 
Grid tariff is the electricity distribution cost and covers developing, operating and maintaining 
the utility grid and it corresponds to 30.3 øre/kWh. Taxes tariff is 83.3 øre/kWh and it is a tax to 
the state including costs relating to reserve capacities and system operation among others. On the 
other hand, PSO includes costs relating to public service obligations in order to research and 
develop environmental friendly electricity including biomass and wind generation and it is 16.1 
øre/kWh. Subscription is assumed to be 30 øre/kWh and includes IT costs, energy management, 
billing and customer management. All are Danish costs for 2014 [13]. 
VAT is 25% in Denmark [14]. 
 
3.3 Sales back prices 
During low-demand hours, PV producers are selling the excess of electricity at a price lower than 
the market price. It is established as 0.6 DKK/kWh for the first 10 years and 0.4 DKK/kWh for 
the following years [15]. This policy is not true any longer due to the government decision last 
May of stopping it. The reason is the increased number of planned PV systems due to the 
decreased PV price.

4.2 Batteries 
Batteries are used in PV systems to store electricity. For optimal use, it should be discharged for 
less than half of its capacity every time it is used.  In case of daily use, its capacity must be equal 
to the energy consumption of 2.5-5 days [17].  
Traditional PV batteries are made of Lead Acid and stand out due to its high energy and power 
capacity as well as its cheap price.  
The energy content of the battery is given by the charge in Ah (Ampere hour) and the voltage of 
the battery in Volts, which ends in kWh. This form of storing energy will not be investigated 
deeply in this assignment. 
4.3 Phase change material (PCM) 
Phase change storage is a heat storage in which part of the heat storage is stored during the phase 
change of the heat storage material. It is an efficient way of storing thermal energy due to its 
high-energy storage density as well as the isothermal nature of the storage process. There are 
many options of PCM at an extensive range of temperatures, which allows for using it in different 
applications [18]. 
A main problem of this type of phase change material is the risk of supercooling and it occurs 
when heat of fusion materials are cooled below the melting point with no start of solidification. It 
should be avoided so it can be assured that heat can be discharged in periods with a heat demand, 
unless long-term heat storage is aimed for. It won’t be considered in this assignment.

Tank for DHW 
Regarding the DHW, the water heater also needs electricity to heat the water required. Heating 
elements of 0.53, 1, 1.2, 1.4, 2, 3, 4, 5, 10 and 11 kW have been investigated to convert the 
electricity from the grid into heat. 
 
In the first investigation, the electricity for DHW supply is bought at the cheapest time, at the 
beginning of the day and it is accumulated in the tank for later use along the day. Tapping 
finishes at the end of the 22 h so after 22 h a new cycle starts which means that the energy 
content in the tank is zero. Heat storage is made in a hot water tank with the temperature interval 
10 ºC - 65 ºC and heat loss is disregarded in this first case.  
 
The idea is to calculate the energy content in the tank comparing the energy accumulated in the 
tank with the demand accumulated. The demand accumulated is the energy needed accumulated 
from the beginning to the end of the cycle so at the end of the cycle will be the total demand for 
the day. The difference of the energy accumulated in the tank and the demand accumulated 
cannot be negative and the closest to 0, the better so that the hot water consumption is covered 
with a minimum of energy consumption. 
 
The cost of the electricity for DHW is calculated multiplying the electricity price by the energy 
bought at that time for the heating element. The minimum tank volume required is related to the 
energy required to cover the DHW supply. Therefore the minimum tank volume can be

The minimum tank volume for each month is calculated by taking into account the maximum 
energy stored in the water tank for the average day of each month. The maximum energy stored 
in the water tank is the maximum energy content at the hour where the difference between the 
energy accumulated and energy demand is at its greatest. The maximum energy content is 
influenced by the price of electricity. When the electricity price is at its lowest (usually at night), 
the most energy is bought and hence the maximum energy content is reached. After the 
calculations for each month, the highest tank volume over the year is taken so the tank will be 
able to supply the demand during the whole year.

5.3 Heat pump for space heating and DHW and a heat exchanger 
This system consists on a heat pump for DHW including a heat exchanger, which recovers part of 
the ventilation losses to the system and another heat pump for space heating. The heat pump 
needs electricity to move heat from the outside air to the water use for DHW and space heating. 
Compared to the water heater, which needs the same amount of energy that is needed in the 
system, heat pump has a COP from 2.5 to 3.6 depending on the month of the year which means 
that less input is needed to use the same amount of energy.  
 
Electricity is bought from the grid when needed at a price given in Table 29 hour by hour for each 
month of the year. Heat pump capacity needs to supply the required demand. The demand is 
divided by the COP so the input needed is calculated and it is the electricity bought to the grid. 
To supply the demand during the whole year without buying additional electricity from the grid a 
heat pump capacity of 1 kW is needed for DHW and 4 kW for space heating.

cost for the lifetime of the product so this cost is divided equally for the product lifetime.

8 Discussion 
PSO dropping 
Denmark will discard the PSO tariff that has been funding renewable energy development since 
1998 by the end of the year [22]. This means an 11 % drop of the electricity price. The fact of an 
electricity price reduction will obviously affect this assignment study with a decrease in the total 
cost for all the systems. However, the reduction will be proportional to the electricity price so the 
trend will be kind of the same. But it is important to consider that as electricity cost drops, the 
electricity consumption would increase in some cases so there will be changes regarding the final 
option for each case. First case corresponding to the floor heating for space heating and electric 
water heater for DHW will benefit the most of the PSO dropping because more electricity is 
bought to the grid. Then, the systems that benefit the most are in order, solar thermal system and 
PV system. The system which benefits less of this price drop would be heat pump because is the 
one that buys less electricity to the grid.  
 
Electricity prices variation 
It can be observed in Table 29 in Appendix C that there is not a huge variation in the electricity 
prices from hour to hour. It can be seen that for example when heat losses are considered, 
sometimes it is cheaper to buy electricity close to its use even though the electricity price is more 
expensive than buying it when it is cheaper and store it in the tank because at the end it leads to 
higher costs. Assuming that the electricity prices would vary in a greater way, this strategy would 
change completely because if prices are much cheaper at night, total costs will be lower if the 
electricity is bought at night when it is cheaper and stored in the tank instead of buying it when is 
going to be used.  
 
Changes on selling back the electricity to the grid 
Due to the drop in the price of PV, its economy is now equivalent to onshore wind turbines and 
cheaper than offshore wind turbines and biogas [23].  
So the government decided to stop the subsidies to prevent a huge drain of government funds 
[24].  
This leads again to an increase of the price of PV and therefore a higher total cost of the PV 
system compared with the other studied systems. The only system which is more expensive than 
PV is the floor heating for space heating and the electric water heater for DHW. It can be 
observed that if there are not earnings of selling the electricity back to the grid (1,194 DKK/year 
for a 200 l tank), the total cost of the PV system is still cheaper (41,571 DKK/year) than the cost 
of floor heating for space heating and electric water heater for DHW (44,840 DKK/year). Then, 
PV system is still worth it even though it will be less attractive than when electricity is sold back 
to the grid. PV area will not be influenced at all by the changes on selling back the electricity to 
the grid because energy is not produced with the purpose of selling back the electricity to the grid 
even though it is an advantage. Besides, the energy is mostly stored at night and used it later 
during the day.

Change in the price of the storage tank 
It is interesting that when the tank is considered in the case of heat pump for space heating, the 
total cost increases approximately 250 DKK/year while it is 450 DKK/year in the floor heating 
system. This means that the storage tank product or installation cost should decrease these 
amounts, making it economically efficient. In the case of the floor heating system, storage tank 
product cost is 324 DKK/year and the installation cost 188 DKK/year, which means that the 
decrease of 450 DKK/year does not make sense at all. Regarding the heat pump for space 
heating, storage tank product cost and installation cost are 99 DKK/year and 188 DKK/year 
respectively. Therefore, it is not viable at all either to decrease 250 DKK/year.  
Then, both systems show better performance when no storage stank is considered because the 
storage tank price has to decrease considerably compared with the price itself.  
 
Lower taxes 
Electricity final price in Denmark, compared with other countries, contains more than two thirds 
(69 %) of taxes. However, United Kingdom or Malta contains the lowest tax contribution (less 
than 5 %) and no other taxes are charged [25]. 
Assuming that the taxation policy in Denmark would change and decrease the tax contribution, 
this would result in less environmentally friendly choices and therefore an increase in the CO2 
emissions. Besides, if the taxes were significantly reduced (to near 0) the electricity final cost 
would be lower than the average electricity cost in EU [26]. The system that benefits the most of 
the lower taxes is the floor heating for space heating and electric water heater for DHW because 
is the one that buys more electricity to the grid. Next one will be solar thermal system and then 
PV system and the last one will be heat pump because is the one that takes less electricity to the 
grid and therefore is not affected as much as the others by lower taxes.  
It would be also interesting to think about a new taxation policy that implies taxes to the use of 
fossil fuels and lower taxes to the electricity. 
 
 
Variable taxes 
Assuming that instead of fixed taxes, Denmark would have taxes proportional to the electricity 
cost, this would result into more differences from the cheapest times (usually at night) to the rest 
of the times. Therefore this would lead into the strategy of buying electricity at night and storing 
it to use it during the day instead of buying it when needed even though it guides into higher 
storage heat losses. Then, it results in bigger tanks.  
Variable taxation results in the use of more renewable energies.

9 Conclusions 
The assignment consists on the study of energy systems for a single-family house. For that, a 
research has been carried out in order to determine the energy demand, energy production, 
electricity prices and accumulation of a single-family house. Energy demand covers space 
heating, DHW and electricity appliances. Energy production has been calculated for all the 
different systems and different sizes. Electricity prices have been calculated from the Nordpool 
electricity cost for 2014 and the taxes given by the government. And finally, different ways of 
accumulation have been studied. The study has been done hourly for the average day of each 
month of the year.  
Once the research is completed, different systems have been studied in order to find the option 
that gives the best performance. A system based on a floor heating system for space heating, a 
water heater for DHW has been analyzed, with and without heat storage losses and also with and 
without tank for space heating. A system consisted on a PV system for space heating, DHW and 
electricity appliances have been investigated with and without accumulation taking into 
consideration heat storage losses. A heat pump for space heating and a heat pump for DHW is the 
third considered case and includes an exhaustive investigation first without accumulation for 
space heating and then with accumulation for both space heating and DHW for different heat 
pump capacities and tank volumes. Also heat storage losses are considered. And finally, a solar 
thermal system for space heating and DHW has been analyzed for different tank volumes and 
solar collector areas with heat storage losses.  
 
After the energy costs study, a deeply investigation has been done taking also into account 
product, installation and service cost of each system element. Then, according to the total costs 
an optimum system has been found. A system based on a heat pump for space heating and a heat 
pump for DHW displays the best performance among the studied systems.  
Also the CO2 has been studied for the different systems in order to show also the environmental 
impact of all the different options. 
 
To conclude, it is interesting the huge difference of more than 10,000 DKK/year from the heat 
pump system to the rest of the systems. It is also observed that the PV system and the solar 
thermal system can only work when a tank is considered because even though there is an implicit 
product, installation and service cost for the storage tank, most of the energy can be stored and be 
used during the day. Otherwise, most of the energy would be sold back to the grid or lost. 
However, the other two cases, heat pump and floor heating and water heater displays better 
performance without tank because the tank implies higher product costs than the savings on 
energy costs. It can also be seen that the total costs vary considerably with the heating element 
power, heat pump capacity, system area and tank volume so it is interesting to study all the 
possibilities to get the best possible system. It is also important to consider the heat storage losses 
in order to get a more realistic study even though the total cost difference is less than 500 
DKK/year for all cases. And finally, it is also pronounced the weight of the energy costs for all 
the systems in the total cost. Energy costs percentage is always higher than 70 % of the total cost. 
So it is noticeable that it would be interesting to invest on the energy cost reduction or electricity 
price instead of the system cost. Energy cost reduction means to increase for example the 
==PAGE:70==
 
60 
efficiency of solar thermal and PV system as well as the heat pump COP. Electricity price 
reduction would be the discard of some taxes.  
It is interesting that about 60 % of the households in Denmark are heated with district heat from 
district heating plants and co-generation plants and they have not been studying in this 
assignment [27].  
The possibility of combine the different studied systems has not been studied deeply. For instance 
a combination of a heat pump for DHW and a solar thermal system for space heating could be an 
option.
