Abstract
This thesis presents a comprehensive study on optimizing the control strategies for hy┬¡
brid power plants that integrate renewable energy sources with hydrogen production via
electrolysis. The research addresses the challenges posed by the intermittent nature of
renewable energy sources such as wind and solar power and examines their impact on
the performance and reliability of hydrogen production systems. An optimization model
simulating a control system for a hybrid power plant is developed to capture the activity
and behavior of an electrolyzer, considering all its characteristics to ensure realistic simu┬¡
lations that adhere to operational. By using power generation data averaged over different
time resolutions, the model demonstrates adaptability in maximizing hydrogen production
even under fluctuating renewable power conditions. The study evaluates the performance
of the optimization model both with and without power imports from the grid. Results in┬¡
dicate that limited grid imports can significantly enhance operational flexibility, stabilize
production during low on┬¡site generation periods, and improve overall economic viability.
The modelÔÇÖs robustness in handling intermittent renewable energy sources and its ability
to maintain optimal hydrogen production levels underscore its practical applicability.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The European Union (EU) has set ambitious goals to achieve carbon neutrality by 2050.
This means the EU aims to balance any remaining greenhouse gas emissions with ac┬¡
tions that remove an equivalent amount of carbon from the atmosphere [1]. These efforts
include increasing the use of renewable energy, improving energy efficiency, and devel┬¡
oping new technologies for energy storage and grid management to support a reliable
and resilient energy system. Several target have been set. By 2030, the EU plans to cut
emissions by at least 55% compared to 1990 levels [2], and by 2040, the target is a 90%
reduction [3]. Finally, around 90% of the electricity generated by 2050 will be based on
renewable sources, with wind and solar accounting for 70% [4].
In this context, the challenge of intermittency associated with power sources such as so┬¡
lar and wind requires innovative solutions for reliable energy production. Although solar
and wind energy are abundant and clean, they are inherently intermittent due to varying
weather conditions [5]. As a result, the energy production can vary from zero to the max┬¡
imum capacity within minutes. This unpredictability complicates efforts to match supply
with demand in real┬¡time energy markets. This challenge underscores the critical impor┬¡
tance of developing integrated energy systems capable of effectively managing, storing,
and using these renewable energy resources. As we transition towards renewable domi┬¡
nance, the electrification of various sectors such as transportation, heating, and industry
becomes imperative [6]. This transformation demands versatile energy storage solutions
to balance supply and demand in real┬¡time and support the evolving energy landscape.
Hybrid power plants incorporating hydrogen production through electrolysis represent
a groundbreaking approach to address the challenges of renewable energy integration.
These plants have the potential to use renewable sources such as solar and wind energy
to generate electricity that will be used to produce hydrogen. Hydrogen, known for its ver┬¡
satility and potential as a clean fuel, can be used directly in fuel cells for power generation
or stored for later use [3]. As an energy carrier, hydrogen has great potential for clean,
efficient power in stationary, portable, and transport applications. It is considered as a
significant element of the future fuel mix for transport, allowing for energy security while
simultaneously reducing oil dependency, greenhouse gas emissions [7]. Several compa┬¡
nies have the ambition to adopt this technology, with some already in late development
stages. This is notably the case for NamX which is currently advancing its projects for
hydrogen SUVs, indicating a future where hydrogen┬¡powered vehicles could become a
significant component of the green transportation ecosystem [8]. This ongoing develop┬¡
ment highlights the potential of hydrogen to contribute substantially to reducing carbon
emissions and advancing the transition to sustainable energy.
1
==PAGE:10==
As a pivotal energy storage technology, hydrogen offers promising solutions to address
the intermittency of renewable power sources. By converting surplus renewable electric┬¡
ity into hydrogen via electrolysis, energy can be stored for later use or transported for
consumption in various sectors. The ability to produce hydrogen from renewable sources
contributes to the decarbonization of energy┬¡intensive industries and facilitates the transi┬¡
tion towards cleaner energy alternatives.
Furthermore, hybrid power plants can enhance grid stability by providing flexibility through
their ability to switch between different energy sources and storage options [5]. The inte┬¡
gration of hydrogen production within these systems not only optimizes the use of renew┬¡
able energy but also creates a buffer against the volatility of renewable power generation.
This synergy between hydrogen production and hybrid power plants is crucial for maxi┬¡
mizing the efficiency and reliability of the energy supply, making it possible to meet peak
demand without relying on fossil fuels.
Recognizing the potential of hydrogen, the EUÔÇÖs Renewable Energy Directive has estab┬¡
lished clear guidelines for green hydrogen production. According to the EUÔÇÖs Renewable
Energy Directive, green hydrogen is defined by the source of the energy used in the elec┬¡
trolysis process. It qualifies as ÔÇØgreenÔÇØ when the electricity used for the electrolysis of
water is obtained from sources considered renewable. This focus on renewable energy
sources ensures that the hydrogen production process itself is sustainable and does not
contribute to additional carbon emissions. This is in alignment with the EUÔÇÖs goals to
significantly increase the share of renewable energies in all sectors, ensuring that the
production process contributes to a substantial reduction in greenhouse gas emissions
compared to conventional hydrogen production, which primarily uses fossil fuels. The
EUÔÇÖs strategy on hydrogen, part of the European Green Deal, specifically targets an in┬¡
crease in green hydrogen production as a means to decarbonize industrial processes and
economic sectors where reducing carbon emissions is both urgent and hard to achieve.
Furthermore, the EU has initiated the ÔÇØHydrogen Strategy for a Climate┬¡Neutral Europe,ÔÇØ
which aims to scale up green hydrogen production significantly. By 2024, the EU plans
to install at least 6 gigawatts of renewable hydrogen electrolyzers and produce up to one
million tonnes of renewable hydrogen [9].
The optimization of operations in hybrid power plants, relies on effective energy manage┬¡
ment strategies and storage capabilities. Battery energy storage systems play a crucial
role in balancing supply and demand, optimizing production schedules, and enhancing
grid stability. However, due to their limited number of charge┬¡discharge cycles before
their efficiency starts to degrade, and their high cost they directly influence the levelized
cost of hydrogen. Therefore, in the absence of battery storage, hybrid power plants may
face limitations in adapting to fluctuating energy demand and maximizing renewable en┬¡
ergy utilization. All the aforementioned points underscore the importance of investigating
2
==PAGE:11==
solutions to reach effective operations in hybrid power plants, ultimately resulting in cost┬¡
effective sustainable energy systems.
1.2
Motivation
Hybrid power plants with behind┬¡the┬¡meter configurations face significant challenges due
to restrictions on power import imposed by current grid codes, limiting them to operated
exclusively on the on┬¡site renewable production. These limitations affect operational effi┬¡
ciency and economic potential. However, the EU┬¡directive has allowed for some import
allowance depending on the GHG intensity of the grid. These power imports can be used
by the power plant to improve its operational flexibility and stabilize production, particu┬¡
larly during periods of low on┬¡site generation.
This thesis aims to contribute in the investigation of optimization models for control strat┬¡
egy in hybrid power plants including solar panels, wind turbines, and a Power┬¡to┬¡X (PtX)
unit with a slow ramp rate (10% per minute). The primary objective is to develop an
optimization model that effectively simulates the operations of a hybrid power plant for
hydrogen production. Initially, the model will simulate operations without relying on grid
imports, ensuring efficient resource allocation and maximizing production. Subsequently,
regulatory directives will be analyzed to identify conditions under which power imports
can be allowed. This analysis will result in an updated model that allows for limited power
imports.
The thesis will also explore changes in the electrolyzer across several time resolutions
to better understand its dynamic behavior. Given the slow ramp rate, we are particularly
interested in analyzing behaviors on a minute and intra┬¡minute basis. For this study the
10 seconds time resolution will be considered as the real┬¡time. Additionally, given that
forecasts can have resolutions ranging from 15 minutes to 1 hour [10], a higher time res┬¡
olution of 15 minutes will be considered to enable meaningful comparisons.
Beyond optimizing plant operations, the thesis aims to determine whether allowing im┬¡
ports can enhance adaptability. Specifically, we will examine if this approach can lead
to a higher and more stable hydrogen output while ensuring compliance with European
Commission regulations.

In the context of developing power control strategies aimed at maximizing hydrogen pro┬¡
duction by integrating electrolyzers into hybrid power systems, it is common for some
studies to simplify the modeling of these devices by considering only their rated power
and hydrogen production rate. This is notably the case in the works [15] [16], where the
authors analyze the integration of electrolyzers for hydrogen production without detailed
modelling of how these devices behave under varying conditions of power availability.
This approach, although simple and straightforward, significantly underestimates the com┬¡
plexity of the dynamics of an electrolyzerÔÇÖs operations. This is particularly noticeable when
integrating the electrolyzer within a renewable energy system where there are constant
fluctuations in power supply. Such oversimplifications can result in models that are not
only less accurate but also less effective in predicting real┬¡world performance and opera┬¡
tional strategies [17].
A critical limitation of modeling electrolyzers by only considering their production rate is
the neglect of dynamic operational states, which reflect more accurately the electrolyzerÔÇÖs
response to changes in energy supply and demand. Each state has unique energy re┬¡
quirements and operational impacts that are essential for optimizing system efficiency
5
==PAGE:14==
and hydrogen output. When focusing on dynamic operations, the main operational con┬¡
troling the flexibility of an electrolyzer are the ramp rate, the load range and the startup
time [23] [24]. For example, ignoring phases where the electrolyzer must turn off due to
insufficient power availability (i.e. a power flow being lower than the operational thresh┬¡
old) leads to overly optimistic predictions of hydrogen production. Therefore, models in
[15] [16] would fail to account for energy losses and operational inefficiencies when the
the power is not sufficient to actively producing hydrogen. Furthermore, such simplistic
models do not adequately capture the efficiency variations with different load conditions,
which is crucial for optimizing energy use and minimizing operational costs.
The works from Matute et al. [18] [14] elaborate on the operational dynamics of an elec┬¡
trolysis plant by categorizing it into several distinct states. These states are production,
standby, and idle. They play a pivotal role in optimizing the dispatch of hydrogen produc┬¡
tion, particularly in scenarios of dynamic operation, thus significantly contributing to the
optimization of plant performance and resource utilization. The operating details offered in
this paper facilitate a comprehensive framework for managing transitions between states,
crucial for maximizing efficiency and reducing operational costs. Specifically, the paper
describes transition times and costs between production, standby, and idle states, which
are essential for determining the optimal dispatch strategy. By incorporating these details
into the model, it is possible to improve the accuracy in the representation of hydrogen pro┬¡
duction under dynamic conditions. Understanding and accurately modelling the behavior
of electrolyzers is fundamental for optimizing their performance. By developing precise
mathematical models, we can simulate electrolyzer behaviors under different operating
conditions, identify potential inefficiencies, and propose design modifications to enhance
overall performance and efficiency of the energy management system.
Additionally, the electrolyzerÔÇÖs production is influenced by numerous factors═¥ however,
two primary physical characteristics need to be taken into consideration: The number of
operating states an electrolyzer can enter, and the electrolyzer efficiency [17]. Opera┬¡
tional characteristcs are also to be taken into consideration as they directly influence the
two factors cited above. The main characteristics to consider when modeling an AEL in┬¡
tegrated to a system using essentially renewable energy are load range, ramp rates as
well as start┬¡up and shutdown costs [19] [25] [26].
Varela et al. [19] and Bartels et al. [20] introduced an innovative approach for model┬¡
ing the operation of an alkaline water electrolysis (AEL) as a mixed┬¡integer program using
the three states described previously. The focus of the study centers on the mathemati┬¡
cal description of the main operational characteristics of an AEL. Especially the amount
of hydrogen produced, the description of the different states, and the transition from one
to another.
The authors of [17] investigated the impact of choosing different number of operating
6
==PAGE:15==
states through a comparative study of electrolyzer models with two and three numbers of
states: Production┬¡Idle┬¡ Stand┬¡by, Production┬¡Idle, and Production┬¡ Stand┬¡by. The study
has shown that choosing a model including only production and idle states results in the
most underestimated profit compared to models incorporating production and standby
states. On the contrary, modelling only production and standby states yields similar profit
and dispatch decisions as a three┬¡states model. However, as we need to model cases of
emergency shutdowns, where the electrolyzer will enter idle mode, a three┬¡state model
has been chosen to model the activity of the hybrid power┬¡plant. These studies constitute
the basis of our work for modelling the states of the electrolyzer.
As described previously, another important aspect to describe is the electrolyzerÔÇÖs per┬¡
formance. In the analysis found in [14] [18], the authors used a constant value for the
energy requirement for producing 1Kg of hydrogen regardless of whether the electrolyzer
runs at full load or not. It is indicated that this method was used as the increase when the
electrolyzer is at partial load is so low that it can be neglected. Ulleberg [27] proposed a
current┬¡voltage model. This model is vastly adopted to represent the relationship between
voltage, current density, and temperature in electrolyzers. It is a fundamental approach
used to predict the performance and operational characteristics of these systems under
different conditions. This model is particularly relevant in the field of electrolysis where
understanding the electrochemical processes is crucial for optimizing the efficiency and
output of hydrogen production. Based on the latter, the authors of [19] formulated an input┬¡
output correlation for the production rate of the electrolyzer as a function of the load. A first
and second order approximation were made at given conditions of temperature,pressure
and load. It is discussed that using a quadratic function to represent the hydrogen pro┬¡
dution, although offering a more accurate representation, will result in a non┬¡linear model.
This will result in an increasing model complexity and decreasing computational efficiency.
Therefore, the authors ended up using the linear model due to its computational efficiency,
which is crucial for handling large datasets and optimizing operations in real┬¡time, despite
the trade┬¡off in detailed accuracy with a low but non┬¡negligeable root mean square error.
Other approaches were also explored, and although they obtained more precise results,
the difference in computational effort required compared to the linear model is significant.
This is notably the case for the two following papers. In [17], a piecewise linearization of
the hydrogen production curve is used. In [21], the hydrogen production is represented
using a quadratic function, resulting in a non┬¡convex mixed┬¡integer non┬¡linear program
(MINLP). The authors developed a heuristic to simplify the MINLP into a set of convex
non┬¡linear programs(NLP). Although this approach reduces the complexity of the prob┬¡
lem, it still requires significant computational resources as the NLPs still need to be solved
using iterative numerical methods.

As stated previously, the hybrid power plant proposed in this thesis has a behind the meter
configuration. It includes two sources of renewable energy: wind turbines and solar panel
arrays. The central component of the power plant is its electrolyzer, which uses the elec┬¡
tricity generated from these renewable sources to produce hydrogen through electrolysis.
The electrolyzer has a rated load of half the capacity of the renewable sources as this was
demonstrated to be the most optimal configuration [19]. it has a slow ramp rate of 10%
per minute. To increase the reliability, the power plant is connected to the electrical grid,
allowing for the import of electricity when the renewable sources do not generate sufficient
power to meet demand (represented by the yellow arrow). However, the control system
is designed to limit the maximum contribution from the grid to a specific percentage of the
total electricity mix. This feature ensures that the plant remains predominantly reliant on
8

renewable energy, thus maintaining the sustainability that will be discussed in Section 3.3.
Finally, the excess electricity produced by the renewable sources is sold to the electricity
market, however these will not be considered in this project.
3.2
Behind┬¡the┬¡meter
To comply with the EU directives mentioned above, it is essential to monitor and man┬¡
age the energy available. A BTM system refers to a small┬¡scale energy solution installed
on the customerÔÇÖs side of the utility meter. By generating and consuming the energy on┬¡
site, the power plant globally reduces its dependence on external energy suppliers, gains
greater control over energy costs, and enhances energy independence. Although gen┬¡
erating and consuming renewable energy onsite promotes better utilization of renewable
sources, which are crucial for reducing carbon footprints, the inherently intermittent nature
of renewable energy sources can pose challenges.
Behind┬¡the┬¡meter systems address this variability by combining the potential of onsite re┬¡
newable generation with the flexibility of grid connectivity. This configuration allows the
power plant to import power from the grid whenever onsite generation is insufficient, en┬¡
suring consistent operations.
However, the main challenge currently experienced by hybrid power plants with behind the
meter load is that current grid codes often restrict them from importing power from the grid,
forcing them to operate solely based on their renewable generation. Nevertheless, the
European energy directive allows for limited imports under the specific criteria discussed
in the next section. These import allowance are contingent upon the emissions associated
with the electricity taken from the grid, aligning the operation of such systems with stringent
environmental standards.
3.3
Directives and regulatory frameworks
To ensure that hydrogen production is based on renewable energy sources and achieves
at least 70% greenhouse gas emissions savings, the European Commission has adopted
specific directives. These directives include two delegated acts that define the detailed
rules under which hydrogen can be considered renewable within the EU. The first del┬¡
egated act specifies the conditions under which electricity used for hydrogen production
can be classified as renewable [30]. The second act outlines the methodology for calculat┬¡
ing the life┬¡cycle greenhouse gas emissions for Renewable Fuels of Non┬¡Biological Origin
(RFNBOs)[31] [32]. The first delegated act further elaborates on two types of criteria to
guarantee that hydrogen is produced renewably:
ÔÇó Additionality: This criterion mandates that the electricity used for hydrogen produc┬¡
tion must come from installations that did not exist before the commencement of
hydrogen production. Specifically, installations must be commissioned no earlier
than 36 months before the start of the operations of the electrolyzer .
ÔÇó Temporal and Geographical Correlation: This criterion emphasizes the need to pro┬¡
9
==PAGE:18==
duce hydrogen at the same time and place where renewable energy is available.
For this to be fulfilled, the hydrogen production needs to happen at the same type
and in the same bidding zone as the electricity generation.
The precise value for the threshold that greenhouse gas emissions should not surpass is
provided in the second delegated act. Since the threshold for GHG emission intensity is
set at 70% of the fossil fuel comparator, or 94 gCO2eq/MJ, the greenhouse gas emissions
from the production of hydrogen must not exceed 28.2 gCO2eq/MJ in order to qualify as
RFNBO. Under all these requirements, it is detailed in [33] [34] that there are four differ┬¡
ent configurations for sources the electricity that are recognized to be suitable for these
regulations:
The first option is be to connect the electrolyzer to the grid and use this as the exclusive
source for hydrogen production. In countries like France, Norway, Sweden, this would be
possible as the carbon intensity of the grid is computed to be sufficiently low to produce
hydrogen that would match the EU criteria regarding emissions as show in Figure (A.1).
However, in most European countries this would result in a production of hydrogen that
will not be considered as RFNBO due to the GHG intensity in the power being too large.
The second option is to connect the electrolyzer directly to a fully renewable electricity
source. This approach ensures that the hydrogen produced is considered renewable,
provided the production installation is off┬¡grid or equipped with a meter to prove compli┬¡
ance with the criteria. However, this option is limited by the intermittency of renewable
energy sources, making it less viable.
The third option involves securing Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) to ensure that
the electricity used for hydrogen production is renewable. However, for this to be possi┬¡
ble the renewable power plant producing electricity must comply with the additionally and
temporalty/geographical criterias.
Finally, the fourth option combines renewable local production with grid imports or or
power purchase agreements. In this configuration, the power plant uses locally produced
renewable electricity and imports electricity from the grid when local generation is insuf┬¡
ficient. This configuration allowed the plant to utilize on┬¡site electricity production while
supplementing with grid imports, provided the energy mix meet the 70% greenhouse gas
emissions savings. This approach would allow to import electricity from the grid, ignor┬¡

4.1
Considerations and assumptions
4.1.1
Wind and Solar
In several studies, power outputs of wind turbines and solar panel arrays at any moment t
were computed using the methods described in [35][36]. However, in this study, the power
generated using wind turbines and solar panel arrays is taken from real world data from
SYSLAB [37]. The data used includes the power generation of a 10 kW wind tubine and
2 solar panel arrays of 10 and 7 kW. In this data, the power produced by one wind turbine
and two solar panel arrays are recorded at 1┬¡second intervals. The total power generated
is then scaled up to align with twice the capacity of the electrolyzer, as describe previously.
It is however important to take into account that doing this does not fully simulate real┬¡life
power plants production. This is particularly the case for wind farms, where individual
turbine output varies mainly due to the wake effect. Wind turbines reduce the wind speed
of the air passing through them, leading to a slower, turbulent flow known as the wake.
This wake can then affect surrounding turbines, diminishing their energy production [38]
[39].
4.1.2
Electrolyzer
Description and assumption of the physics of the electrolyzer in the system under
study
The aim of this part is to build a model describing the activity and behaviours of an elec┬¡
trolyzer under specific conditions. As a starting point, the three states of the electrolyzers
will be described:
ÔÇó Production: In this state, the electrolyzer is an active hydrogen producer. This phase
involves consuming power to facilitate the electrolysis process. Electricity is used
to split water molecules into hydrogen and oxygen gases. According to some work,
The power load ranges from 15% to 100% of the nominal capacity of the electrolyzer
[40]. However, more recent technologies have made it possible to increase the
operating range, reducing the minimum load to as little as 10% of the electrolyzerÔÇÖs
rated capacity [41].
ÔÇó Standby: In this mode, the electrolyzer is not actively producing hydrogen but re┬¡
mains powered and operational. This state requires continuous energy input to
maintain internal conditions such as temperature and pressure necessary for quick
activation when needed. The power consumption during this state is significantly
lower than for the production state. As the value is not explicitely given by manufac┬¡
turers, values between 1┬¡5% of the rated load of the electrolyzer are adopted in the
literature [19] [14] [17] [18]. In this study, we will therefore assume a value of 5% of
the rated power with the ability to transition into production state within seconds.

ÔÇó Idle: This state corresponds to a period of inactivity where no hydrogen production
takes place. The temperature and pressure within the electrolyzer drop to lower
levels. In this state, the electrolyzer consumes negligible power.

4.3
Methodology for the simulation
This part of the report will focus on the simulation of optimization results in real time. This is
an important aspect of this project because as discussed previously, traditional forecasting
techniques provide a forecasting horizon in high timescales [46] [47]. However, one of the
main challenges for the integration of wind power into renewable energy systems is the
occurrence of rapid and strong changes in wind in very short period of time [47]. This
problem can also be encountered when using solar radiation data, as discussed by the
authors of [48]. In the paper ÔÇØTime┬¡averaged wind power data hides variability critical to
renewable integrationÔÇØ by Kate R. Ward et al.[49], the authors discuss the overestimation
of the hydrogen production and energy storage capabilities of alkaline electrolysers when
basing the control strategy on a low frequency data such a half┬¡hourly wind data. As the
averaging timescale increases, the precision of the data decreases, resulting in a growing
gap between the averaged values and the actual power generated. This issue can notably
be observed when comparing power production data averaged over different timescales,
as illustrated in the following plots.

This algorithm simulates the operation of an electrolyzer system over a given time period,
adjusting power usage, production states, and import limits based on various conditions.
When the electrolyzer is on but not actively producing hydrogen and the power level drops
below a minimum threshold (105 in this case), the electrolyzer turns off. The Shutdown
function ensures the electrolyzer will only reactivate if 180 consecutive power values ex┬¡
ceed the production threshold. In production mode, several conditions are evaluated. If
the power level falls below the production threshold (210) but remains above the critical
threshold, the electrolyzer enters standby mode. Otherwise, it enters idle mode, again
through the Shutdown function. If the difference between the current and previous power
levels exceeds the ramp rate, the electrolyzer also enters idle mode. If the current power
level is greater than the sum of the previous power level and the ramp rate (denoted by
RR), the current power level is decreased, and hydrogen production is calculated using
the HydrogenProduction function. If none of these conditions are met, the state and the
power level remain unchanged, and hydrogen production is computed. It is important
to note that, similar to the optimization model, the power used for production equals the
current power minus the power required to keep the electrolyzer on.

Production of hydrogen with no import allowance
In this part of the analysis, no import allowances are considered. The daily operation
and state of the electrolyzer are optimized using an optimization model that leverages the
perfect foresight of power generation. The main goal is to assess how the control system
performs in an environment with limited power availability and constant fluctuations in
power generation.
The model runs with predefined timescales to generate an optimal operation plan for the
electrolyzer. Three different timescales are used in this analysis: 10 seconds, 1 minute,
and 15 minutes. This approach is justified by the fact that the ramp rate of the electrolyzer
is given in minute (10% per minute). We were therefore interested in observing how the
model performed specifically at the 1┬¡minute interval to directly align with the electrolyzerÔÇÖs
ramp rate. By using 10┬¡second intervals, we aim to capture rapid fluctuations in power
generation and assess the modelÔÇÖs ability to make precise adjustments in real┬¡time. Fi┬¡
nally, the 15┬¡minute intervals offer a more generalized view, potentially smoothing out
critical fluctuations and providing insight into how the system performs under less granu┬¡
lar data conditions. This higher time resolution simulates the outputs of the optimization
model if applied to a realistic forecast, reflecting how well the system can handle variations
in power availability within its operational constraints.
In this analysis, two main elements will be studied. The first element is hydrogen produc┬¡
tion, which involves examining the amount of hydrogen produced under different opera┬¡
tional conditions and timescales. The second element is the duration of active hydrogen
production by the electrolyzer.
By studying these elements, we aim to gain insights into the optimal operational strategies
for electrolyzers in dynamic power environments.
5.3

As discussed in Section 4.3, the high fluctuations in power generation present significant
challenges for the stable and efficient operation of the electrolyzer. These fluctuations can
disrupt the electrolyzerÔÇÖs activity, making it difficult to adhere to the designed production
plan. Consequently, the electrolyzer may not be able to consistently follow the optimal op┬¡
eration schedule, leading to potential inefficiencies and variations in hydrogen production.

with the power generation data, will serve as input for our simulation. Moreover, the 10┬¡

then be compared to the resampled data. At every 15 minutes interval, it will be possible
to observe how much of the planned production was realized considering a ramp rate of
35kW per unit of time (10 seconds).

averaged values, which smooth out short┬¡term fluctuations. In contrast, the simulation
considers a 10┬¡second time resolution, capturing more detailed fluctuations in renewable
energy generation. This higher resolution reveals the variability that the 15┬¡minute aver┬¡
aged data misses.
Secondly, the response of the electrolyzer to changes in power availability plays a signifi┬¡
cant role. Indeed, the electrolyzer has a ramp rate of 10% of its rated capacity per minute,
which is equivalent to 210 kW per minute. When the production plan was made using the
optimization model, this ramp rate constraint was not accurately represented. In fact, the
model used a ramp rate that was 900 times higher than the actual rate used in the simula┬¡
tion (35kW). This discrepancy is due to the fact that the optimization model could assume
instantaneous adjustments to power changes, while in reality, the electrolyzer can only
39
==PAGE:48==
adjust its output gradually. However, as the high frequency data presents frequent fluc┬¡
tuations, there are situations where the decrease in renewable generation between two
times is faster than the ramp rate, resulting in an interruption of the production.
Due to these factors, the simulation reflects more realistic operational constraints and
variability, leading to a significant deviation from the planned production. The real┬¡time
operation, influenced by detailed short┬¡term fluctuations and realistic ramp rates, presents
the practical challenges in meeting an optimized schedule that doesnÔÇÖt fully account for
these dynamics.

from the grid. When there is a drop in renewable energy that would cause an interruption

Conclusion
This thesis provides a comprehensive analysis of optimizing hybrid power plants that inte┬¡
grate renewable energy sources with hydrogen production via electrolysis. The research
addresses the critical challenges posed by the intermittency and fluctuating nature of re┬¡
newable energy sources, such as wind and solar power, and explores how these fluctua┬¡
tions impact the performance and reliability of hydrogen production systems.
For this purpose, an optimization model simulating a control system in a hybrid power
plant has been developed. This model aims to capture the activity and behaviour of an
electrolyzer when integrated into the system. The model meticulously considers ramp
rates and various operational states of the electrolyzerÔÇöproduction, standby, and idle.
Furthermore it also incorporates transitions between these states to accurate simulate
its dynamics. This detailed approach allows for a more exact representation of the real┬¡
world operational challenges and dynamic behavior of the electrolyzer. The model also
includes various constraints related to power balance, ramp rates, and start┬¡up and shut┬¡
down cycles, ensuring that the simulations are both realistic and adhere to operational
and regulatory requirements. By incorporating these factors, the model can offer a simu┬¡
lation of the performance of hybrid power plants that is closer to reality.
To evaluate the performances of the optimization model, the latter uses power gener┬¡
ation data averaged over several time resolutions (10 seconds, 1 minute, and 15 min┬¡
utes), capturing different degree of fluctuations in renewable power generation. Despite
high fluctuations in renewable production, the model was able to adapt and maximize
hydrogen production effectively when imports from the grid were not considered. This
adaptability was even more noticeable when using the 10┬¡second model, which has a sig┬¡
nificantly lower ramp rate and captures higher fluctuations in the power generation. This
model had to adopt a conservative approach to avoid cases of power deacresing faster
than the ramp rate. This ability to respond swiftly to rapid changes in power generation
ensured that the electrolyzer operated at optimal levels, maximizing hydrogen output and
demonstrating the modelÔÇÖs robustness in handling intermittent renewable energy sources.
The model including power imports was also able to optimize the resource utilization
further. By allowing limited power imports from the grid, the hybrid power plant could
stabilize production during periods of low on┬¡site generation, leading to higher and more
stable hydrogen output. The inclusion of grid imports provided additional flexibility, which
enhanced the overall economic viability and operational efficiency of the power plant. This
was particularly evident in scenarios where high┬¡resolution data was used, allowing the
system to better manage the intermittent nature of renewable energy and maintain a con┬¡
sistent hydrogen production level.
55
==PAGE:64==
Finally, when comparing the outputs of the optimization models incorporating imports and
the ones without, it was observed throughout the analysis that imports from the grid can
significantly enhance the operations of a hybrid power plant. Indeed, the production using
power import considerably increased compared to cases where no power was imported.
As in previous sections, this is due to the fluctuations in power generations being com┬¡
pensated by grid imports, ensuring a steady and reliable power supply to the electrolyzer.
This compensation allows for continuous hydrogen production, maximizing output and im┬¡
proving overall system efficiency and profitability.
The value added by grid imports was even more visible when simulating the operations of
the electrolyzer in real┬¡time (here 10 seconds intervals) using a planning made with low
frequency data. As the planned was based on lower frequency data (15 minutes intervals),
the sudden drops in power were smoothed out, leading to the electrolyzer entering idle
state as it was not able to follow the plan. Therefore, imports from the grid were necessary
to compensate for these sudden drops to allow for a stable hydrogen.

Future work
Three main extensions of this thesis can be discussed. First, It was mentioned previously
that the renewable local production could be combined with imports from the grid and
PPAs. This analysis was focused on simple imports from the grid. However, the latter
showed that imports are constrained by electricity prices and the composition of the elec┬¡
tricity mix. Given that the maximum allowable contribution from the grid is related to its
CO2 intensity, it is anticipated that as the global energy landscape becomes increasingly
renewable, this constraint will become more flexible with time until it no longer limits op┬¡
erations. Nevertheless, there are inherent limitations in relying solely on grid imports, the
main one identified in the analysis to be the electricity prices which can impact operational
stability and cost┬¡efficiency.
Considering the use of Power Purchase Agreements (PPA) instead of simple grid imports
for electricity can offer several significant benefits and considerations. PPAs provide a
stable and potentially cost┬¡effective supply of renewable energy, depending on the type
of contract, which can substantially influence hydrogen production and overall profitability.
With a PPA, the hybrid power plant can secure an unlimited amount of renewable electric┬¡
ity, ensuring that the electrolyzer operates at full capacity without the constraints of limited
grid imports. This stability and predictability can lead to higher overall green hydrogen pro┬¡
duction. Moreover, PPAs come with an agreed price, often cheaper to spot market prices,
reducing operational costs and increasing profitability. Furthermore, utilizing renewable
energy through a PPA enhances the sustainability profile of the hydrogen production pro┬¡
cess, which is crucial for regulatory compliance, investor relations, and market positioning,
especially in green┬¡conscious markets.
However, the impact of PPAs on profitability largely depends on the contract terms. The
specific type of PPA contract, whether fixed┬¡price, variable┬¡price, or a combination, will
influence cost savings. Fixed┬¡price contracts offer stability, while variable┬¡price contracts
can provide savings if market prices drop. Cheaper prices under a PPA compared to
grid electricity can significantly reduce the cost of hydrogen production, making it more
competitive in the market. Additionally, power purchased via a PPA might not be fully
used dependent on weather conditions, but there remains a commitment to purchase a
certain quantity, which can sometimes lead to excess power during periods of low imports
need, leading to increasing costs.
Grid imports, on the other hand, offer the flexibility to purchase electricity as needed with┬¡
out long┬¡term commitments, allowing the plant to respond to immediate operational de┬¡
mands and market conditions. This flexibility is crucial for optimizing costs and ensuring
a reliable power supply, especially during periods of low renewable generation. However,
the unpredictability of grid prices and availability can pose challenges.
57
==PAGE:66==
Therefore, while PPAs present a promising solution for securing stable and cost┬¡effective
renewable energy, the inherent flexibility of grid imports remains a valuable asset. Balanc┬¡
ing the use of PPAs and grid imports could provide the optimal strategy for ensuring both
the reliability and cost┬¡efficiency of hydrogen production in a hybrid power plant. Further
research and analysis can be done in this area to find the perfect mix between simple grid
imports and PPAs to maximize the revenue while minimizing the risks of being limited in
the power supply.
The second extension is directly related to the assumption we made about perfect fore┬¡
sight of the daily renewable generation. It involves incorporating uncertainty into the model
to better reflect real┬¡world conditions. By adding this element of unpredictability, we aim
to investigate how variations in renewable energy availability impact hydrogen production
and whether allowing imports can enhance the systemÔÇÖs adaptability. This approach will
help us assess the potential for achieving a higher and more stable hydrogen output while
still adhering to the regulations established by the European Commission. The authors
of [52] have notably contributed in the investigation of this topic by developing a robust
optimal scheduling for isolated microgrids with PtX storage.
An additional factor that could be incorporated into the model is the inclusion of electricity
sales to the grid within the objective function. This aspect was not addressed in this
study, as the primary focus was on hydrogen production. However, it is anticipated that
incorporating electricity sales could significantly enhance the modelÔÇÖs profitability.
Particularly on days when renewable generation is low, the electricity that would other┬¡
wise be used for hydrogen production could be diverted to the market for sale. This
strategic shift is expected to generate higher financial returns due to the typically higher
market prices of electricity during periods of low renewable generation. By optimizing
the use of electricity based on market conditions and the availability of renewable energy,
the model could balance between hydrogen production and electricity sales, maximizing
overall profit. This dual┬¡focus approach could make the energy system more flexible and
economically resilient, ensuring that resources are utilized in the most profitable manner
based on real┬¡time market dynamics and production capacities.
