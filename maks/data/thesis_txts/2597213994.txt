Abstract
Renovation of buildings have increasingly become area of interest, in Denmark,
due to the potential for reduced energy consumption, improvement to indoor
environment and the need to increase attractiveness for future residents. There-
fore, this thesis investigates two recent renovation projects in Denmark: Svend-
borg and Korngården.
The aim of the renovation project in Svendborg was
1) to improve the indoor environment and 2) to increase the attractiveness of
the neighborhood. The aim for Korngården was to increase energy eﬃciency
through renovation and by installing monitors in each apartment with measure-
ments of residential heat, electricity and hot water consumption. This thesis uses
data from both renovation projects to analyse energy consumption and indoor
environment. The Svendborg case is used to analyse the economical diﬀerence
between two heath allocation methods, assess the energy consumption over four
years using two diﬀerent methods, assess the indoor environment (temperature,
CO2 concentration and humidity), and to compare 15 apartments before and
after renovation.
The Korngården case looks at the reduction in residential
consumption after the renovation, in order to assess whether the installment of
screens has an impact on consumption. Rstudio is used to illustrate the ﬁndings.
The many diﬀerent aspects investigated are found to illustrate interesting ﬁnd-
ings. For Svendborg, there is a more evenly distribution of economic cost be-
tween the residents when utilising the new heat allocation method.
The es-
timated energy consumption based on multiple assumptions yields reasonable
results when compared to a second method, except for one of the four years
estimated. Moreover, the indoor environment including temperature, CO2 con-
centration and humidity, are found to be within the recommended ranges of the
Danish Standards for the majority of the apartments. However, malfunctioning
of the IC-meters were found to be the cause of unrealistic low CO2 concentra-
tions in two apartments, and high temperatures were caused by a combination
of residents behaviour and external factors in other apartments. Lastly, a com-
parison of the indoor environment in 15 apartments before and after illustrated
a diﬀerence between the two periods, however, the diﬀerence was not consistent
for all apartments.
In Korngården a reduction in heat consumption was identiﬁed in a previous com-
pleted study when comparing the winter season in 2016/2017 and 2018/2019 -
both after renovation.
A similar reduction was found in the hot water con-
sumption and a smaller reduction in the electricity consumption was identiﬁed.
However, the hypothesis that only the visibility of monitors incentives residents
to reduce their consumption could not be conﬁrmed, as other reasonable expla-
nations were found.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Denmark is a country known for setting ambitious energy targets. They are a
pioneer in striving towards developing new solutions on how to utilise variable
renewable energy (VRE). The main drivers of progress are the country’s ambi-
tious aim for renewables to cover 50 % of the country’s total energy consumption
by 2030, and the goal to be completely independent of fossil fuels by 2050. Over
the past two decades, the country has made signiﬁcant developments within the
electricity generation from wind energy and bioenergy, while coal dependency
has been gradually reduced. As a result, Denmark is globally recognised as a
leader in integrated VRE, while managing a secure and reliable electrical-power
grid, and for developing their heating sector in a sustainable direction. [10]
Though, an increase of VRE is in focus and constantly improving, there are still
other areas where the energy consumption can be reduced. Residential buildings
represent about 23 % of the total energy use in the European Union. [14] Solely
in Denmark, the energy consumption in buildings reach up to 35-40 % of the
country’s overall energy consumption. [15] The main energy consumption in
buildings is divided by heating, ventilation and artiﬁcial lighting. [8] As a result,
Denmark has for the last decades focused on energy eﬃciency with sustainable
and energy-eﬃcient solutions in new and renovated buildings, oﬃces and public
institutions across the country. [17] One of the reasons why the development has
come this far is due to incentives and regulations set by the Danish government.
[8]
"Energistyrelsen" is the organisation which has the main responsibility for im-
plementing the European building directive, in addition too, a wide range of
national incentives that supports energy eﬃciency. The aim is to spread in-
formation on how to choose energy eﬃcient solutions in both of new-built and
renovated buildings. Some of the incentives and regulations implemented are the
EU Building Directive, building law and building regulations and measurement
and billing of consumption. [8] Even though the main focus is a reduction in
energy consumption in buildings there is also another associated aspect to con-
sider when reducing the energy consumption, namely the indoor environment.
This area is becoming increasingly important as the two focus areas intertwine.
Indoor environment quality is a focus area that has come secondary to energy
eﬃciency for many years. However, the importance of the subject comes to light

when a strategy only focused on energy eﬃciency often results in a poor indoor
environment. [13] As the indoor environment is highly dependable on the occu-
pants’ behavior and usage, it can be diﬃcult to distribute enough information
so that the inhabitants can gain the knowledge needed to contribute to the best

In Denmark, the ﬁrst building law was registered in 1965, which has later been
developed to the latest building code BR18 and BR20. These regulations are
requirements and restrictions for constructing a new building or renovation of
an old building. This is done to ensure progress in energy eﬃciency and con-
struction of buildings with a long lifetime. [3] Even though the building laws,
regulations and guidelines are encouraging sustainable development when dis-
cussing indoor environment and energy consumption in buildings, it is still not
the main reason why buildings are being renovated today. The purpose of ren-
ovations is in general due to the need of updating the old existing buildings
and neighborhoods to make them more desirable for potential residents. It is a
constant development, where improvements are needed to increase the attrac-
tiveness of the neighborhood. Moreover, the deeply implemented welfare society
in Denmark ensures housing based on needs for the older residents, as well as,
residents with special needs.
Furthermore, a new suggested way of billing energy consumption is currently
being tested in Denmark where the focus is transferred from heating to the
indoor environment. Lastly, it has been discovered that occupants’ behavior and
knowledge of indoor environment play a key role when evaluating both energy
consumption and the indoor environment. Therefore, multiple experiments have
been conducted to see if the occupants change their behavior when given access
to data on their indoor environment and energy consumption.
This report aims to investigate and assess two residential renovation projects
in Denmark. The majority of the report is based on a renovation project com-
pleted in Svendborg for multiple apartments. This includes an assessment of the
economical diﬀerences in two heat allocation methods, the energy consumption,
the indoor environment, and a comparison of indoor environment before and
after renovation are completed.
Furthermore, the close relationship between
occupants’ behavior and indoor environment is illustrated, and unexpected re-
sults are investigated further. Lastly, another residential renovation project in
Korngården has been investigated as previous ﬁndings suggested that further
discoveries within residential consumption should be completed. The purpose
of this report is to provide insight into both energy consumption and the indoor
environment in renovated apartments.

Chapter 2
Background
Renovations of buildings are constantly completed in Denmark. Thus, there
is often an opportunity to investigate the results after renovation in terms of
energy consumption and indoor environment. Moreover, the indoor environment
is aﬀected by occupants behaviour, where the residents contribute to internal
gains, interact with systems, and modify indoor conditions with their behaviour.
[4] Therefore, this chapter will look further into these topics, in addition too,
presenting information on two diﬀerent renovation projects where data has been
utilised for analysis on energy consumption and indoor environment.
Occupants behaviour have a large eﬀect on energy consumption and the indoor
environment in residential buildings. The residents modify the environment by
both their adaptive actions and their presence. [9] Manual control of building
instruments allows each resident to regulate their indoor environment to their
preferences. As a result, the amount of energy utilised and the indoor tem-
perature may vary signiﬁcantly. Area, number of occupants, resident’s habits,
and location are also parameters that will have a direct eﬀect on the energy
consumption and indoor environment. For example, when gathering people in
a room for a longer period, they exchange heat with the surroundings and they
release CO2 and other wastes that impact the indoor environment. Moreover,
actions like switching on a light or opening a window, increase the residences
inﬂuence considerably. [9]
Indoor environment quality (IEQ) is a topic directly connected to occupants
behaviour. The importance of good IEQ has gained an increased focus in re-
cent decades. Since residents are directly aﬀected by the indoor environment
it means that the residents sense of welfare and well-being is the main result
of either adequate or inadequate IEQ. Moreover, IEQ is determined by many
factors, including lighting, air quality, and damp conditions. It is a crucial area
of research for oﬃce buildings, schools, universities and other places where a
large amount of people is gathered with the need to completed tasks and be
productive. However, it is also important in residential buildings, where occu-
pants can be motivated by comfort and economy. Despite uncertainty about
what to measure and how to interpret what is measured, research shows that
building-related symptoms are associated with building characteristics including
dampness, cleanliness, and ventilation. [5]

Background
Thermal comfort and indoor air quality can be classiﬁed into certain recom-
mended ranges according to the European Standard EN15251 and the Danish
Standard DS/EN1678-1:2019. European Standard EN15251 includes diﬀerent
categories of values, where category 2 represents a normal level of expectation
and should be used for new buildings and renovations. Category 3 includes an
acceptable, moderate level of expectation and may be used for existing build-
ings.
The Danish Standard DS/EN1678-1:2019 has categories depending on
the level of expectancy of indoor environmental quality. Category 2 represents
medium and is assumed to be the most suitable category for renovated residen-
tial buildings. The recommended values for temperature, CO2 concentration
and humidity are presented in Table 2.1 and Table 2.2. [20, 21] The standards
give guidelines on design values and recommended ranges for energy calculations
and thermal environment, therefore it is regarded as guidance in this report.

2.1
Svendborg
The renovation project is located in the municipality of Svendborg in the South-
Denmark region. Toftemarken, Klintemarken and Frilandsvej are included in the
7502 department and consist of multiple buildings that have been renovated.
The main builder and owner of the project is the housing association BSB

Chapter 5
Discussion
A large number of various ﬁndings for the residential renovation projects in
Svendborg and Korngården have been presented and described in chapter 4. As
a result, this section will discuss the ﬁndings from both renovation in section 5.1
and section 5.2 separately.
5.1
Svendborg
In Svendborg, the economical diﬀerence between two heat allocation methods
illustrated a large reduction in payment for some residents when utilising the
shadow account instead of a dynamic account. Moreover, the estimated energy
consumption for 4 years was found to vary between the years with a reduction
in 2019, and the method was tested due to assumptions. The ﬁndings for the
indoor environment indicate an overall good indoor environment for the majority
of the apartments. However, apartments with unexpected high temperatures
or unrealistic low CO2 concentrations were investigated further or identiﬁed
as malfunctioning of the IC-meters. Furthermore, the ﬁndings from occupant
behaviour and indoor environment indicate diﬀerences between building Ids and
weekdays by illustrating ﬂuctuating patterns. Lastly, a comparison of the indoor
environment before and after renovation shows that the renovation has made
some improvements within the indoor environment. Hence, a discussion of the
mentioned ﬁndings is presented in this section.
5.1.1
Economical diﬀerence between dynamic and shadow
account
The comparison between the two heat allocation methods illustrated large diﬀer-
ences in payment for many residents in 2019. As the two methods are based on
very contrasting methods, it raises the question of which method is the most op-
timal and for who. From a solely economic perspective, the results indicate that
for the residents the better option is the dynamic heat account, as the amount
per apartment was decreased substantially when utilising the dynamic account
==PAGE:82==
74
Discussion
for multiple residents. Furthermore, it is found that the variation of payment
between residents is drastically reduced with an increased number of residents
who pay almost the same amount. However, some residents had to pay more
with the dynamic account but this amount is far less than the amount saved
for other residents. Moreover, by utilising the dynamic account the question
of unfairness between apartments is removed. The shadow account is mainly
based on the heat consumption, where location and orientation of apartments
play a key role. An apartment that is located with a higher amount of external
walls, or with a limited amount of sunlight has a higher risk of increased cost
when compared to an apartment with fewer external walls and a large amount
of sunlight. Moreover, heat transfer between apartments is another advantage
or disadvantage, depending on the neighbours behavior. By removing these fac-
tors with the introduction of the dynamic account, the external factors that are
diﬃcult to control can be reduced.
In addition to more equally distributed payments among the residents, there is
also the beneﬁt of an improved indoor environment for all apartments. By intro-
ducing a system that forces the occupants to focus on the indoor environment
with economical incentives, it is apparent that the residents change their area
of focus. Also, with an upper and lower limit for temperature and humidity and
an upper limit for CO2 concentrations it automatically reduces the diﬀerences
in the indoor environment between apartments. The result is a more evenly dis-
tributed consumption among apartments and an improved indoor environment.
Since the majority of the residents are assumed to be economically motivated,
a reduction in heat consumption is expected as a temperature above the upper
limit means increased cost. However, there is still a chance that residents who
spend a little amount of time in the apartment or is not economically motivated,
will still prefer e.g high temperatures. But no such evidence was found when
solely investigating the economical diﬀerences in 2019.
Despite the clear advantages of the dynamic allocation method, there are also
some disadvantages from the residential point of view. The ranges of the in-
door environment that decides how much the residents pay are decided by the
heat allocation companies. The ranges are decided based on an optimal indoor
environment to ﬁt the majority of the residents’ preferences, but the compa-
nies still need to get an income for the system installed. Also, if an occupant
prefers temperatures outside the decided range it means increased economic
cost.
Moreover, if the occupants spend a lot of their time abroad, it could
be diﬃcult to adjust the radiators to always be within the temperature range
instead of turning everything on low value or oﬀ, before leaving the apartment.

like outdoor temperatures were not found to be the obvious explanation. [12]
Moreover, the screens installed in the apartments were not optimised completely
in 2016/2017 but were assumed to work properly in 2018/2019. As both periods
of the investigation were after renovation, it would be intriguing to discover if
the reduction could be found in other areas. As a result, a similar reduction
was found in the hot water consumption, while for the electricity consumption
a smaller reduction was found.

that the measures completed during renovation are not the most reasonable

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis is separated by two renovation projects, Svendborg and
Korngården. The Svendborg case was used to analyse the economical diﬀerence
between two heath allocation methods, assess the energy consumption over four
years using two diﬀerent methods, assess the indoor environment (temperature,
CO2 concentration, and humidity), and to compare 15 apartments before and
after renovation. For Korngården, previously completed studies revealed a dras-
tic reduction between 2016/2017 and 2018/2019 within the heat consumption.
As a result, further research was completed within electricity and hot water
consumption to investigate if the same reduction could be identiﬁed, and to ﬁnd
explanatory reasons.
The ﬁndings in Svendborg show that the dynamic method for payment for heat
is found to have a more evenly distribution of economic cost between residents.
Moreover, the assumptions and estimations of energy consumption in Svend-
borg are found as reasonable as two methods conﬁrmed very similar results,
with the exception in 2017, method one. For the indoor environment, the ﬁnd-
ings illustrate that the majority of the apartments are within the recommended
limits when compared to the Danish Standards. Further investigations identi-
ﬁed malfunctioning IC-meters within the CO2 measurements, as average CO2
concentrations per apartment were observed below assumed background con-
centration, and therefore removed from later investigations. Unexpected high
average indoor temperatures observed were found in two apartments were ex-
ternal factors for e.g sunlight and orientation were assumed to be the most
reasonable explanations. Lastly, correlations between the indoor environment
and occupants’ behavior are found.
The results from Korngården illustrated a similar reduction within the hot wa-
ter consumption, as found in the heat consumption, and a minor reduction in
electricity consumption when comparing 2016/2017 and 2018/2019. The reduc-
tion found could be explained by several reasons, where for e.g a new roof with
isolation was conﬁrmed, but readjusted mechanical ventilation was not possible
to conﬁrm. Therefore, it can only be assumed that the visibility of the screens
can be one of many reasons for the reduction found. Further research, for e.g in-
terviews with residents, questioners, and conﬁrmed changes within the building
between 2016/2017 and 2018/2019 should be completed.
