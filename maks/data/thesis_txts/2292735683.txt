Abstract 
The aim of the present research is to identify human subjective response (health and comfort) to 
the micro-thermal environment established by integration of individually controlled localized 
chilled beam and chilled ceiling (LCBCC) system and to compare its performance with the 
performance of mixing ventilation combined chilled ceiling (CCMV).Experiments were carried 
out in mock-up of an office (4.1m × 4.0m × 3.1 m, L× W× H) with one person under two 
summer temperature conditions (26 °C and 28 °C). To mimic direct solar radiation in the room, 
five radiative panels on the wall together with electrical sheets on the half of the floor were used. 
The test room was set-up with two desks, as two workstations, and one laptop on each table. The 
main workstation (WS1) was located close to the simulated window. The second work station 
(WS2) was placed in the opposite side of the room. The room was equipped with two types of 
ventilating and cooling systems.  The first system consisted of a localized active chilled beam 
(LCB) unit together with chilled ceiling (CC) panels. The LCB was installed above the WS1 to 
create micro-environment around the occupant sitting at the desk. The supply flow rate from the 
LCB could be adjusted by the occupant within the range of 10 L/s to 13L/s by means of a desk-
mounted knob. The integration of mixing ventilation (MV) system and chilled ceiling panels was 
the second ventilating and cooling strategy. Twenty- four subjects (12 female and 12 male) were 
exposed to different indoor environment established by two cooling systems. Each experiment 
session lasted 120 min and consisted of 30 min acclimatization period and 90 min exposure 
period. The performance of the systems was identified and compared by physical measurements 
of the generated environment and the response of the human subjects. 
The study showed that perceived air quality (PAQ), overall thermal sensation (OTS) 
acceptability and local thermal sensation (LTS) acceptability clearly improved inside the micro-
environment by using LCBCC system. Moreover, at the main workstation, OTS and LTS votes 
were close to “neutral” thermal sensation (ASHRAE seven point scale) when LCBCC system 
was used. However, OTS and LTS votes increased to the “slightly warm” side of the scale by 
applying CCMV system which implied the better cooling performance of the LCBCC system. 
Acceptability of work environment apparently increased under the room condition generated by 
LCBCC system.  
==PAGE:5==
iv 
 
In agreement with human subjective study, the results from physical measurements and thermal 
manikin study showed that uniform thermal condition is generated all over the room. Air and 
operative temperature distribution was almost uniform with no difference higher than 1°C 
between the measured locations in the room. Thus, both LCBCC and CCMV systems performed 
equally well outside of the micro-environment region. The use of the chilled ceiling had impact 
on the airflow interaction in the room and changed the airflow pattern. It can be concluded that 
the combination of convective and radiative systems can be considered as an efficient strategy to 
generate acceptable thermal condition in rooms. 
 
Keywords: Localized chilled beam, Chilled ceiling, Thermal comfort, Individual control, Micro-
environment, Human response

1. Introduction  
1.1. Thermal Comfort and Indoor Air Quality 
Like other mechanisms following the first law of thermodynamics, the produced heat by humans 
must be dissipated by exchanging heat with the surroundings. In the standard thermal condition 
the net heat from body core towards the skin can be measured and defined as metabolic rate. 
Temperature of different body parts varies and depends on the metabolic rate [1]. In addition, the 
surface temperature of body is 4°C or 5°C colder than the inner part [2]. Human body 
exchanges heat mostly by convection and radiation and the balance achieves by evaporation 
from body skin. Therefore, the heat loss from the body takes place in two ways: sensible heat 
loss and latent (evaporative) heat loss. 
Sensible heat exchange is associated with the temperature gradient between the mean skin 
temperature and the ambient temperature. Since the surrounding space cannot usually be 
assumed as homogenous, the sensible heat exchange depends on the variation between 
convective and radiative heat transfer, see Figure 1. The dominant parameters in latent heat 
exchange from the body are saturation pressure of water vapor at the skin surface and the 
saturation pressure of water vapor of the ambient air. The higher the gradient between these two 
items, the more evaporation from the skin. Skin wetness is a function of sweat rate which itself is 
one the natural body physiological cooling processes. In addition to the latent heat released by 
sweat evaporation, respiratory evaporation is another type of latent heat loss. Human body 
perceives heat exchange variations by thermal receptors in the skin. Thermal receptors are 
sensitive to both temperature fluctuations and the rate of change of temperature. For instance, in 
the case of sudden changes in temperature, some drastic physical and physiological actions such 
as clothing modification and vaso-motion are done at the beginning in order to improve the body 
thermal condition. But the magnitude of changes fades gradually by approaching the steady state 
condition under the experienced environment [3]. Complicated network of several thermal 
receptors, multiple feedbacks loops, various inputs, etc. results in a non-linear behavior of human 
body thermoregulation, which makes it difficult to simulate and predict [4]. 
Berglund [5] reported that six factors were affecting thermal parameters which were divided into 
physical factors and personal parameters. The physical factors were: air velocity, air temperature, 
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
relative humidity of air and mean radiant temperature of air. The personal factors are: activity 
level and clothing level. The perception of surroundings is also affected by other factors such as 
individual, cultural, social, and physiological and some other physical factors of the room, e.g. 
indoor air quality, acoustic, visual condition, etc. [6]. Considering the effect of each factor or 
their combination, thermal balance of the body can be easily changed. However, 
thermoregulation of the body is controlled by two separated mechanisms: behavioral 
thermoregulation mechanism and autonomic thermoregulation mechanism. Whereas the first 
mechanism is attributed to clothing adjustment and activity level, the latter one is mostly 
involved with physiological reactions like adjustment of internal and external thermal resistance, 
heat production, skin wetness, etc. 
 
Figure 1. Heat exchange between human body and surrounding 
As it is addressed in ASHRAE standard 55 [7] and ISO 7730 [8], thermal comfort is “the 
condition of mind which expresses satisfaction with the thermal environment”. The first 
conclusion that is drawn is that the thermal comfort is not a physical definition but physiological 
concept. By accepting this statement, the influence of other individual parameters such as 
cultural and environmental habits [9], mood [10], gender [11,12], age [12], and many non-
measurable factors have to also be taken into account as the reason for the different perceptions 
on the surrounding thermal conditions. 
==PAGE:12==
3 
 
Several thermal indices have been developed to define human thermal behavior [13–17]. The 
precision of a standard is related to how it describes the energy exchange between the man’s 
body and its surroundings as well as how the human body adjusts its internal temperature at a 
fairly constant condition by means of different physiological actions [18]. Among these various 
methods, two approaches have become more applicable and popular in terms of thermal comfort. 
The first approach which is so called rational or heat balance approach has been established by 
Fanger and it has been developed based upon climate chamber studies under steady-state 
conditions. The second model is known as adaptive approach which is based on field study in 
real buildings [19, 20]. Each approach has some advantages and disadvantages. However, since 
the present study is underpinned by the heat balance approach, this method is going to be 
described further. 
As it was mentioned, the heat balance approach was developed based on Fanger’s work [14] and 
has been proved over the past decades by data acquired from climate chambers’ subjective 
experiments. The key factor about this approach is that it combines the theory of human body 
thermoregulation with heat exchange parameters to define the condition under which human 
feels comfortable. Body, as a control volume, perpetually regulates its heat balance with 
surrounding by applying physiological methods such as vaso-motion, shivering, controlling the 
sweat rate, etc. or spontaneous reactions like clothing modification and/or doing some physical 
activities in order to provide pleasant condition to the body. For the purpose of predicting the 
comfort of occupants in a space by this method, an index which is known as Predicted Mean 
Vote (PMV) is introduced. PMV index combines four physical parameters, i.e. mean radiant 
temperature, air temperature, air velocity and relative humidity of air, and two personal 
parameters, i.e. clothing and activity level, to predict the thermal comfort level of occupants. 
Nevertheless, it is emphasized that heat balance condition due the thermoregulation adjustment 
of human body could be fulfilled in a broad range of environmental variables under which 
comfort may not exist [14]. This approach is evaluated as a precise method for simulations of 
thermally neutral resting subjects in casual clothing style [21]. 
Thermal discomfort is a state under which the heat balance of the body either as a whole being 
(overall thermal sensation) or at some part of the body (local thermal sensation) loses its balance. 
==PAGE:13==
4 
 
Reported by Winslow et al. [22] and McIntyre [23], warm discomfort is linearly proportional to 
the skin wittedness while the cold discomfort is in association with the mean skin temperature.  
Local thermal discomfort is a complicated physiological phenomenon which is sensed by the 
difference between skin temperature and core body temperature [24]. Local thermal discomfort 
is defined by ISO 7730 [8] as “thermal dissatisfaction caused by unwanted cooling or heating of 
one particular part of the body”. Four discomfort conditions are known as the causes of local 
thermal discomfort: Draught, vertical air temperature difference, warm and cool floors, and 
radiant thermal asymmetry, see Figure 2.  
 
 
Figure 2. Different thermal discomfort conditions 
Among mentioned local thermal sensation (LTS) factors, draught is denoted as the most common 
complain among office workers. Draught, itself, is undesirable local cooling of the human body 
caused by air movement [25]. Draught increases the convective local heat transfer from the 
exposed to the air movement body part and therefore, the temperature gradient between the 
surface of the skin and the body core increases.  Griefahn [26] and Toftum [27] showed that the 
sensitivity of people towards draught discomfort decreases with increasing the activity level. 
This could be the reason why draught has been found as one of the two most pronounced thermal 
discomfort factors in workplaces and office premises [28–30], where people are spending their 
time mostly seated. According to Fanger et al. [25], mean air velocity, air temperature and 
turbulence intensity are the three major factors playing the dominant role in causing draught 
discomfort. Apparently, air movement characteristics like magnitude, direction, continuity, etc. 
==PAGE:14==
5 
 
can significantly change the Percentage of Dissatisfied (PD) due to draught. PD rises with 
increment of air velocity [25,31]; air flow fluctuations cause more discomfort than uniform air 
flow [32] and the direction of airflow towards body can change the comfort experience [33]. In 
addition, some parts of the body like head, neck, face and hands, not covered by clothing, are 
obviously more sensitive to air movement and its fluctuations and hence the most complaints are 
referred to these parts. Radiation from cold surfaces produces the same effect as draught, i.e. 
sensation of discomfort on particular part of the body because of different temperature between 
the skin and the body core. However, in the case of draught the air movement causes pressure on 
the skin which is felt by the pressure receptors when the air velocity is higher than 0.5 m/s. 
Vertical temperature gradient discomfort usually takes place by exposing to cooler flow of air 
delivered to a room by means of floor or wall-mounted diffusers, e.g. displacement and 
underfloor ventilation systems.  Vertical temperature difference between ankles and head is 
limited by ISO 7730 [8] to 4°C. 
In addition to the effect of indoor environment studies on improving human body comfort, 
thermal comfort indices and standards could be useful tools to boost sustainability in buildings 
[34] and reduce fossil fuel and energy consumption in buildings as well [35,36]. However, these 
fields are not the points of interest in the present study. 
Dissatisfaction with indoor condition can be also a result of poor Indoor Air Quality (IAQ). Over 
the past decades, the focus on reducing energy consumption in non-industrial buildings is 
growing. Since the energy crisis in 1970s, more effort has been made to construct energy 
efficient buildings. Although making the buildings air-tight is a good strategy to reduce energy 
losses via infiltration air, lack of fresh air or low ventilation rates also lead to health problems for 
occupants [37]. Therefore, problems which are associated with low supply airflow rate into the 
occupied spaces have become widespread. As it was correctly noted by Maroni et.al [38], the 
concentration of pollution in a ventilated space is related to outdoor air pollution concentration, 
the rate of pollution production in the room, the rate of pollution removal from the room and the 
supply rate of fresh air in to the room. Many field studies have proved the fact that some 
discomfort conditions such as headaches, respiratory problems, eye discomfort and feeling of 
extreme tiredness (also classified as SBS symptoms) have resulted from long exposure to air 
pollution from non-healthy building materials [39–41]. 
==PAGE:15==
6 
 
Taking the above considerations into account, three main strategies are suggested by different 
guidelines and standards to improve the indoor air quality status. Whereas some standard [42] 
offers source control method for decreasing concentration in the room, the other focuses 
predominantly on higher ventilation rates (via dilution) to avoid high concentration of pollution 
in the room[43], and some of them suggest both methods [44].  
In addition to the impact of suspended particles, pollutants, odors, etc. on Perceived Air Quality 
(PAQ), thermal parameters of air such as temperature, velocity, relative humidity, turbulence, 
etc. can affect human’s judgment on the quality of ambient air as well as their health and 
performance. 
Unsurprisingly, due to the direct influence of temperature and humidity, the lower temperature 
and humidity of air produces better perception for occupants [45]. Fang et al [46] showed that 
simultaneous increment of temperature and humidity of facial airflow had a considerable reverse 
effect on PAQ. The study was repeated for the whole body exposure and the same result but with 
different magnitude of acceptance was obtained [47]. Noted by Melikov and Kaczmarczyk [48] 
and Melikov et al [49], PAQ improves by increasing facial air movement. They also found that 
the magnitude of the improvement became more significant in warm and humid condition. 
However, it was mentioned that recirculation of room air in order to generate the air movement 
didn’t decrease Sick Building Syndrome (SBS) symptoms. As suggested by some studies, 
increasing the air flow rate could be an efficient way to enhance PAQ which is polluted by 
human sources, building materials etc. [50,51]. Consistent with the results of mentioned works, 
Wargocki et al. [52] found that overall productivity and PAQ increased and SBS symptoms 
decreased by raising airflow rate. 
 
1.2. Methods for generating indoor environment 
The main purpose of applying air-conditioning system is to provide (extract) heat to (from) a 
room. The primary purpose of using ventilation system is to supply clean air and remove the 
polluted air and reduce contaminants in the room. In some cooling systems these functions are 
combined. Agreed by several standards and guidelines, a good ventilated condition is achieved 
==PAGE:16==
7 
 
by creating low air velocities, low contaminant concentration and small thermal gradients in the 
room. Nowadays, various kinds of ventilation and air-conditioning strategies are employed based 
on the different demands and heat transfer principles. They are classified based on 
cooling/heating fluid, e.g. all-air or all-water systems, distribution method, e.g. centralized or 
decentralized supply and exhaust systems, ventilation principles, e.g. displacement, mixing and 
localized ventilation systems, and/or the combination of systems. 
Since the aim of the present study is to investigate the capability of two common ventilation 
strategies which are known as total volume ventilation and localized ventilation, the focus in the 
following sections will be specifically on these two methods. 
 
1.2.1. Total volume environment 
1.2.1.1. 
Convective systems 
From heat transfer point of view, energy can be transferred by conduction, convection and 
radiation. Convection is a process in which the heat is exchanged between two locations by 
movements of fluid. In ventilation, however, convective systems are known as air-conditioning 
systems which remove/supply heat to spaces by means of forced air movement. Systems are 
categorized based upon on the method by which air is supplied and extracted from a space, see 
Figure 3. Supplying and extracting strategies of air are also considered as important factors in 
pollution control in rooms. Mixing ventilation system and Active Chilled Beam are very well-
known systems. These will be described more in the following sections. 
==PAGE:17==
8 
 
 
Figure 3. Different ventilation strategies: A) Mixing B) Confluent jets, C) Displacement, D) Impinging 
 
1.2.1.1.1. 
Mixing ventilation  
Mixing ventilation system is the most widely used method for ventilating and both cooling and 
heating in buildings. In this method, supply air with relatively high initial momentum is 
discharged into the room by means of air diffusers. Due to the high initial speed of the supply air 
and its interaction with buoyancy flows from heat sources, the supply air and old-room air mix 
within the ventilated space. Consequently, both temperature and pollution concentration are 
evenly distributed in the room. Thus, typically only negligible vertical and horizontal 
temperature gradients exist in the ventilated space. Moreover, except in proximity with the 
pollution source, the concentration of pollution should be the same all over the room. The reason 
for uniform distribution of temperature and pollution in the room relies on the interaction 
between the ventilation flow and the buoyancy flows as dominant flows in the room. This will be 
discussed further in section 1.3. Since usually supply air with high speed is delivered to the room 
at increased air volumes, the concentration of pollution is relatively lower close to the ceiling 
than within the occupied zone.  
==PAGE:18==
9 
 
Despite the fact that uniform indoor condition and low gradients are desirable in rooms, mixing 
strategy basically has some potential problems. Spreading pollution, smell and infection all over 
the room could raise the risk of disease, reduce the thermal comfort and IAQ. Furthermore, the 
probability of having stagnation regions with high concentration of pollution and fairly high 
temperature than other parts of the room is possible. Similarly, in the case of improper design of 
the position of inlets and outlets in the room, short-circuit could become problematic for the 
thermal and the air exchange efficiency of the ventilation system, and can also increase the risk 
of creating stagnation regions. Documented by Melikov [53], other inefficiencies of mixing 
systems are complicated interaction between supply air and internal heat sources which makes 
the supply jet difficult to control, long distance between the supply inlet and the occupants which 
causes the supply air warm and polluted by the time it reaches to people, low possibility for 
satisfying a majority of occupants due to the individual differences.  
 
1.2.1.1.2. 
Chilled beam 
Chilled beam is one of the Water-Air systems widely used in offices and commercial buildings. 
Acceptable thermal comfort and air quality have been provided by employing this system [54]. 
Moreover, due to the rather high temperature of the water used in CB systems compared to 
conventional cooling systems, it is categorized as one of the energy efficient ventilation systems 
among available systems in the market [55,56]. 
From the design point of view, there are two types of chilled beams, active and passive chilled 
beams, see Figure 4. The similarity between active and passive chilled beams is the way that they 
cool down a space. However, the active chilled beam (ACB), which is also called induction 
diffuser, has higher cooling capacity due to the induction effect [57]. The room air is induced to 
the device due to the pressure difference between the inside of the unit and the surroundings. 
Then, the warm and polluted room air is mixed with the supplied cool and clean air and 
discharged back into the room from side slots. Downfall flow from the supply slots, which 
causes down-draught, and high air velocity near the floor are known as two possible local 
thermal discomfort problems taking place in rooms ventilated by chilled beams [58,59]. 
==PAGE:19==
10 
 
The effect of other factors such as positioning of chilled beams in the room [60,61], the layout of 
the room [58] and the influence of induction control on diminishing the draught discomfort based 
on human response [62] have also been studied.

1.2.1.2. 
Radiant systems 
Nowadays, the sensible heat load in offices is increasing due to use of large glass facades, high 
number of electrical equipment and growing the number of occupants. Removing heat by only 
applying air systems is not an effective way of saving energy and could cause thermal discomfort 
for occupants due to the risk of creating down-draught regions within the occupied zone [63]. 
Therefore, using radiant systems which can remove considerable amount of the generated 
sensible heat from the room has become attractive by consultants and investors. By proper 
design of radiant cooling systems, not only desirable thermal comfort condition can be achieved 
but low operation costs and flexibility can result in more useful space in the room can be fulfilled 
[64]. In fact several configurations of radiant cooling systems are used in buildings but in the 
following only Chilled Ceiling (CC) system will be discussed since it is used in this study. 
In cooled ceiling panels both radiation and convection methods are used to condition the room, 
see Figure 5. Radiation takes place due to the temperature difference between the warm surface 
of the objects in the room and cold surface of the chilled beam. The room air in contact with the 
==PAGE:20==
11 
 
chilled ceiling gets cold and as a result its density increases. It gradually drops down towards the 
floor. Therefore, the convection occurs due to the air motion in the room. Therefore, these types 
of cooling systems require rather high supply water temperature and low water flow rate which 
are decisive parameters for reducing the energy consumption. Moreover, since internal heat is 
extracted not only by means of convection, low temperature gradient and local discomfort may 
prevail [65]. Nevertheless, the temperature pattern in radiant cooling system is not uniformly 
distributed in the room and it has been investigated that it is sensed more close to the walls than 
the middle of the room [65]. 
 
Figure 5. Heat exchange in the presence of Chilled Ceiling system 
In radiant ceiling systems with low supply flow rate, the major source of air movement in the 
room is convective plume generated by internal heat loads. Thus, the distribution of the loads as 
well as chilled panels’ layout could affect the air distribution pattern in the room. Fredriksson et 
al. [66] mentioned that negative buoyancy plume generated by CC was sensitive to the presence 
of heat sources and it had a tendency to  be pulled towards surfaces with higher temperatures. 
The depth of the affected area by downward plume is dependent on the buoyancy force, because 
the driving force and thermal plumes from internal sources could deeply change the air 
==PAGE:21==
12 
 
distribution in the surroundings [66–69]. In addition, configuration of CC panels have been 
considered as an important factor in terms of air flow pattern inside the room [70]. 
It is worth mentioning that the energy and overall performance of CC system is also attributed to 
the climate condition. In other words, applying this method would be problematic in hot and 
humid conditions due to the risk of condensation [71,72]. Thus, the lower limit for CC surface 
temperature is a function of the dew point of the supplied air. Another disadvantage of pure 
radiant conditioning systems is that because there is no air supplied to the room, the quality of air 
is getting worse by either occupants or other pollutions sources in the room. 
 
1.2.1.3. 
Combined radiant and convective methods 
Integration of convective and radiant heat exchange methods could be viable alternative to 
overcome their individual disadvantages and to get better result. Apparently, based on heat 
balance mechanism of human body, the portion of heat exchange between the body and the 
surroundings by convection and radiation is not the same as under radiant systems compared to 
conventional convective systems. Generally speaking, the portion of radiation by applying 
radiant cooling systems increases significantly and the convective heat flux decreases, 
respectively [65,73]. With this ventilation strategy, greater part of the sensible heat is extracted 
by radiant part while the convective part is responsible to remove the latent heat and provide 
clean air to get better IAQ in room, see Figure 6. 
In the case of integration of convectional system with CC, the flow pattern in the room is 
affected by air and buoyant flow from heat sources as well as the ventilation strategy, e.g. mixing 
system or displacement system. Unlike mixing ventilation system, in displacement ventilation 
system air with low velocity and 2-4 °C cooler than the room air temperature is supplied to the 
room by means of floor-level inlets. By integrating the convective and radiant systems, since the 
amount of supplied air to the room regardless of the ventilation strategy, is little, the convective 
plumes still have important influence on forming of air flow pattern. It is observed that by 
combining displacement ventilation and CC the typical characteristics of flow pattern in 
displacement method vanish [74]. Strictly speaking, the stratified flow pattern is not very well 
==PAGE:22==
13 
 
established in the room because of the downward flow close to the surfaces which are cooled 
down by radiation from CC. Thus, more mixing condition than the stratified flow pattern in 
rooms is expected. As a result, the contaminant distribution is also changed due to this effect. In 
the case of simultaneous usage of mixing and CC systems, the level of air mixing is attributed to 
the area of ceiling covered by chilled panels. It has been reported that ideal mixing can be 
achieved by covering the entire ceiling [75]. 
In addition to the impact of supply air on removing heat and contaminants from the ventilated 
space, the velocity of the supply air could enhance the cooling capacity of the radiant panel [70]. 
However, increasing supply air could bring thermal comfort problems, especially draught, if the 
limit is exceeded [76].

1.2.2. Micro-environment 
As depicted by Int-Hout [77], total indoor environmental quality depends on parameters such as 
acoustics, indoor air quality, thermal comfort and air distribution. However, to some extent, it is 
neither easy nor profitable to provide all mentioned items within recommended ranges in a 
building. One strategy to approach the desirable condition is to separate the total environment 
into smaller zones: micro-environments (µenvironment). This method can be more efficient and 
==PAGE:23==
14 
 
simple way to get the desirable indoor conditions. It can also be used to delegate individual 
control over the µenvironment. 
µenvironment for ventilation and conditioning is defined as that particular part of the room 
where the thermal condition is assumed to be different from other parts of the room, see Figure 
7. The idea for creating the environment in a small scale inside an occupied space have stemmed 
from the fact that total ventilation system usually cannot make all occupants satisfied with the 
generated indoor climate [77, 78]. In addition, ventilating and conditioning the whole room 
would be an inefficient method from energy point of view in places, i.e. office premises, where 
the occupants don’t change their location most of the time. Self-control over physical parameters 
of supplied air such as: direction, temperature, airflow rate, etc. are providing extra advantages 
compared to total volume ventilation systems.

1.3. Airflow interaction in rooms 
1.3.1. Ventilation flow 
The air jet is released from the diffuser could either flow into the room as a free jet or attach to 
an adjacent surface and continue flowing as a wall jet. The jet glides over the ceiling and after 
reaching an opposite wall it is deflected downwards and then spreads over the floor. Then, it 
reaches internal heat sources in room and as a result, the convective thermal plume is generated 
due to the temperature difference. It has been investigated that the highest velocity in the 
occupied zone in rooms ventilated by wall jets is over the floor [80] and it is a function of the 
inlet height from the floor, initial velocity of the jet and room length. Another important 
parameter affecting the flow pattern in the room is the separation length of the jet from the 
surface.. After some distance from the slot, the jet is detached from the surface and penetrates the 
occupied zone. A jet with rather high momentum could cause thermal discomfort problems to 
occupants. Archimedes number is a dimensionless number which defines the motion of fluids 
because of differences in density. High local Archimedes number could be the cause of short 
separation length [81–83]. Other parameters such as design of the diffuser and its location [84], 
room layout [85,86], physical characteristics of the supplied air flow [87,88], etc. have been 
considered as important factors in flow and concentration pattern in a room ventilated by this 
method. 
1.3.2. Buoyancy flows 
Buoyant plumes in a room are generated as the result of temperature difference between a 
relatively warm surface of the internal heat sources and the surrounding air. Convective heat 
sources in the room can be defined either as internal heat sources such as electrical equipment, 
lightings, human body, radiant heaters, etc. or surfaces with high thermal mass exposed to direct 
or indirect solar radiation, e.g. window and room floor exposed to sun light. Since convective 
heat sources in the room give rise to a large internal volume flow, they can establish a flow 
pattern locally or in the room scale. The magnitude of a convective flow depends on the 
temperature difference between the warm surface and the ambient air temperature at the same 
height. The amount of entrained air into the plume and the elevation height of the plume flow are 
==PAGE:25==
16 
 
also function of the temperature gradient between the room air and the surface temperature of the 
heat source [89].  
In homogenous environment, because the temperature difference between the plume and ambient 
air is constant, the plume keeps developing towards the ceiling. However, in reality, irrespective 
to interaction between air movement and the plume, there is always a positive vertical 
temperature gradient which further affects the development of the plume. The gradient becomes 
more pronounced in stratified ventilated spaces. In this case, since colder air is supplied to the 
room in the lower part, the temperature difference as a driving force is stronger. The turbulent 
flow which is generated above the heat source entrains ambient air and transports it upwards. 
Thus, the airflow rate in the plume increases with height [90]. Because the flow rate in the plume 
is usually larger than the extracted flow rate from exhaust, some part of the mixed room and 
convective flow comes back towards the floor. The amount of downward flow becomes equal to 
upward flow at a certain height, known as the neutral height [91].  However, as the buoyant 
airflow increases with height, the driving force of the plume becomes weaker. But the plume 
keeps propagating upwards due to the momentum. It was showed that the convective flow rate 
and the temperature gradient created by plume were strongly dependent on the height of the heat 
source in the room [89,92]. 
1.3.3. Airflow interaction around human body- impact on occupants’ thermal 
comfort and inhaled air quality 
Human body has two effects on surrounding air movement: obstruction effect and rising effect 
[93]. The first impact is caused by the physical presence of human body in the room whereas the 
latter one is due to the heat exchange between the body and its surrounding, see Figure 8. 
Depending on several physical and thermal factors in the ventilated room, each of these two 
could affect locally the ambient air close to the body. Results from several studies have shown 
that human body generates a plume with a velocity of 0.2- 0.3 m/s. Therefore it can potentially 
be considered as an important factor in mapping local flow patterns specially in stratified 
conditions [94–96]. 
==PAGE:26==
17 
 
The obstruction effect of body predominantly changes the velocity pattern within the vicinity of 
the body and is independent of the natural convection phenomena [97,98]. Myers et al, [98] 
showed that the blocking effect of the human body was observed down to a distance of 0.15 m 
behind it.  
Convection flow generated by the human body is playing role both in thermal comfort and local 
IAQ. As was already mentioned in the section of thermal comfort, thermal receptors within the 
skin surface are sensitive to air fluctuations. Apart from the air fluctuations, unsteady non-
uniform upward air current is established around the body by means of convective flow. 
Therefore high air velocity fluctuations around the body can presumably results in increasing the 
risk of draught. Licina et al. [99] demonstrated that  increase in air temperature fluctuations close 
to the body affect the convective plume. It was also mentioned that increasing the velocity of the 
Convective Boundary Layer (CBL) leads to large air temperature fluctuations. One parameter 
that has a profound effect on the formation of CBL is the posture of the body. Due to the 
influence of thighs and lower legs positioning, the thickness of CBL is higher in seated posture 
than in standing and in supine postures [96]. The plume, however, can be easily distracted by the 
surrounding air. In this case, velocity and direction of the air are considered major parameters. 
Melikov [100] showed that if the velocity of downward flow is not high enough, it couldn’t 
prevail over the warm upward flow and diminish the plume. Moreover, because of undeveloped 
and thin CBL in front of body, transverse flow could easily penetrate the plume and reach the 
body surface. Generally, the expansion rate, the thickness and the velocity of CBL are affected 
by several factors such as the direction of the invading flow, room temperature, clothing, etc. 
which makes it difficult to study.  
==PAGE:27==
18 
 
 
Figure 8. Convective plume around human body 
From IAQ point of view, the thermal plume is known as one of the major transport mechanisms 
of pollutants around the human body. Since air is entraining mostly from below, the convective 
plume is able to change the air quality in the breathing zone. Findings from Novoselac [101] 
revealed that the thermal plume from an occupant is important in transporting suspended 
particles especially in stratified ventilated rooms. In a study on displacement ventilation, it was 
found that thermal plume could enhance inhaled air quality if the lower region of the ventilated 
room was less polluted than the mouth region, and vice versa [102].

2. Justification 
Nowadays, decreasing the energy consumption in residential buildings and office premises 
becomes important. It is documented that energy consumption in these buildings is much more 
compared to the energy used for transportation and in industry [103]. Thus, a trend to optimize 
the energy consumption by applying active and passive design energy saving strategies is 
growing rapidly. Heating, ventilating and air conditioning (HVAC) systems play major role in 
terms of decreasing the energy consumption in buildings. Decreasing supply airflow rate from 
ventilation systems is not efficient strategy to save energy: several studies show the decreasing 
the supply flow rate has a negative impact on indoor air quality affecting negatively human 
performance and health [37,104]. Therefore, more advanced technologies based on different heat 
exchange methods between the human body and surroundings and also more efficient to remove 
heat from indoor environment have taken considerable notice. Among various methods for 
cooling and heating the spaces, high-temperature water cooling and low-temperature water 
heating systems have become more popular. Water is 4000 times more efficient to remove the 
generated heat in a space compared to air for the same volume of both liquids (water and air) 
used. Chilled ceiling panels and chilled beam system are two cooling systems working on this 
principle. As previously discussed, radiant systems utilize both the radiative and convective heat 
exchange to remove the generated heat loads in the room. However, unacceptable indoor air 
quality due to the absence of supply air takes place.  
Since ventilating the whole room would be an inefficient method from an energy point of view, 
another alternative for reducing the energy consumption in the room is creating a micro-climate 
within those parts of the room where the occupants spent most of the time, i.e. their workstations 
in an office environment. The thermal conditions within the micro-climate are assumed to be 
different from other parts of the room in terms of thermal sensation and perceived air quality. 
Another benefit of establishing micro-climate is the advantage of providing individual control 
over the physical parameters of supplied air, e.g. direction, temperature, airflow rate, etc. One 
example as already mentioned is the PV system. However these systems need to be close to the 
occupant and they are more intended on providing better air quality than thermal comfort. 
Localized chilled beams on the other hand can establish a micro-climate around the seated 
person via individual control. Non-uniformity in the environment out of the occupied zone (out 
==PAGE:29==
20 
 
of the micro-climate region) could cause thermal discomfort to the occupants when they have to 
move out of the workstation. Reported by Nygaard and Uth [105] occupants were satisfied with 
the generated thermal micro-environment established by the LCB. The individual control of the 
primary air was assessed as a beneficial for the cooling system. However, the results of their 
study show that the conditions outside of the micro-climate were evaluated as unacceptable by 
the subjects. 
In personalized ventilation system physical properties of supply air plays a key role in terms of 
perceived air quality as well as local and overall thermal discomfort. Hence, the velocity, 
temperature and direction of the supply jet should be within the acceptable range for the 
occupants.  By integrating the LCB with CC it is expected to improve the micro-environment 
generated by the LCB as well as the thermal environment in the occupied zone. Thus more 
acceptable conditions compare to the conditions obtained with LCB only [105] will be obtained. 
 The major effect of using chilled ceiling system is to take away the generated heat load out of 
the occupied zone and keep more homogeneous thermal environment between the occupied zone 
and the surroundings by means of radiation. However the performance of such combined cooling 
system consisting of LCB and chilled ceiling panels with regards to the generated indoor 
condition has not been studied. The present thesis investigates the impact of such combined 
system (Localized Chilled Beam with Chilled Ceiling) on the generated indoor environment 
under cooling conditions at 26 and 28 oC in a single office by physical and human subjective 
experiments.

4. Objectives 
The objectives of the present study are as follows: 
 To study human subjective response (health and comfort) to the micro-thermal 
environment established by integration of localized chilled beam and chilled ceiling 
(LCBCC) system and to compare it with the thermal environment generated by the 
integration of mixing ventilation and chilled ceiling (CCMV) system.

and Energy (ICIEE) at Technical University of Denmark (DTU). Both chambers were

its own supply and exhaust system, which offered the possibility to control the rooms’ thermal

direct sun light and therefore surrounding temperature was fairly stable all the time. 
5.1.2. Test room 5 
Test chamber 5 was built in 2001 and it is used for different long-term exposure purposes. Broad 
range of airflow within the limit of 12-170 L/s at 10-40 °C can be supplied to the test chamber by

used to produce heat on the floor (Figure 9) and mimic direct solar radiation. In order to make 
sure that all produced heat from the electrical foils was delivered to the room, thermal insulation

5.2.1.4. 
Test room preparation 
Since getting the steady state thermal condition in the room was taking long time, the preparation 
process of the test rooms was started just after finishing the previous experiment. The most 
important point which should be followed in order to prepare chamber 5 was to generate the 
same amount of heat load as during the experiment with the human subject in. Therefore, a 
==PAGE:45==
36 
 
heated dummy with 100 W heat production was used to mimic the human subject. In addition, 
one laptop was set to generate a constant power during the night. These additional heat sources 
were turned off about 10 minutes before the first participant arrived in the morning.  
When the condition of CC combined with CB had to be changed to the condition CC combined 
with MV system, the CB device was dismounted and removed from the room and its supply duct 
was blocked. As for the MV system, the inlets were blocked by tape when the CB system was 
used. The other components, such as chilled ceiling panels and exhaust outlets, were not changed 
during the human subject study. 
5.2.2. Experimental procedure 
The subjects were divided into 6 groups of 4 people. The number of male and female participants 
in a group was not necessarily equal. Every group member came on the same fixed day and time 
during the whole study period of 9 weeks, Appendix 2. The ventilation strategy could be 
different from session to session for each group. The conditions were arranged randomly to 
avoid repetition, bias and other environmental influences which could affect participants’ votes 
during the experiments.  
The experiment was divided into two main parts, acclimatization period and exposure period. 
Every person was exposed for two hours to the environment generated in the five cases. First, 
every subject entered into the chamber 6 (acclimatization) and spent half an hour there. Then, the 
subject proceeded into chamber 5 for starting the experiment, see Figure 19. During the 
exposure, the subjects were expected to do different tasks such as: filling in questionnaires, 
giving tear film samples, moving from one work station to another, etc.

5.2.3. Data analysis 
As mentioned previously, data from human subject experiment was obtained by paper 
questionnaires (during acclimatization period) and computerized questionnaires (during exposure 
period). The program for computer questionnaires was developed so that all results for each 
group were organized at the end of the day in Microsoft Office Excel format. The acquired data 
from paper-based questionnaires were scaled in the same way as with the computer 
questionnaires and also prepared in Excel format. All acquired data were then processed and 
analyzed by using statistical software STATISTICA. 
The analyses started by investigating the normality distribution of data by applying the Shapiro-
Wilcoxon test with significance level of p<0.05. If the p value was less than 0.05 the data was 
not normally distributed. In this case non-parametric tests were used to analyze the results. Since 
in the present study the same group of people experienced different conditions in the test room, 
variables in the experiment have dependent relationship. Thus, two groups of results were 
compared as dependent variables by using Wilcoxon matched pairs test. In this test, if the p-
value is less than the significance level of 0.05, it suggests that the compared variables are 
different. The level of significance in the present study is categorized based on Motsulky [112], 
i.e. p<0.05 is significant, p<0.01 is highly significant, and p<0.001 is extremely strongly 
significant.

5.3.1.5. 
Thermal manikin 
In order to calculate the Manikin Based Equivalent Temperature (MBET) a thermal manikin, 
named Kirsten, was used. MBET is defined as “the temperature of a uniform enclosure in which 
a thermal manikin with realistic skin surface temperature would lose heat at the same rate as it 
would in the actual environment” [117]. The manikin is shaped to resemble a typical 
Scandinavian woman, 1.70 m in height. The manikin consisted of 23 body segments and made of 
a 0.003 m fiberglass polystyrene shell. Each body segment was equipped with temperature and 
heating measuring nickel wiring which were separately controlled by a computer program. The 
temperature of each segment closely resembled that of a real human and the power consumption 
was controlled so that the surface temperature remained constant all the time at a given activity 
level. Therefore, the amount of heat exchange between the manikin body and the surroundings 
==PAGE:57==
48 
 
could be measured. The control strategy of the manikin is illustrated in details by Tanabe et al, 
[117]. 
To calculate the MBET in an accurate way the manikin had to be calibrated under similar 
temperature conditions, clothing and posture as in the test chamber. The calibration was done 
within the temperature range of 22°C 30°C at intervals of 2°C. As mentioned before, the manikin 
was shaped like a woman. Therefore, the average Clo value of all female participants was 
derived from the questionnaires and used to dress the manikin. The total Clo value was 0.52. The 
clothing items are shown in Figure 26. The manikin was sat at the same chair and table as it was 
used for subjects during the exposure period, Figure 26. After getting steady state condition in 
the calibration room, manikin was logged for at least 5minutes with an interval of 10 sec. As can 
be seen in Figure 26, a white plate was placed underneath the manikin to avoid the impact of 
ambient air movement on it.

In the present study the human subjective response to combined radiant and convective cooling 
by chilled ceiling combined with localized chilled beam is investigated. Since the thermal 
sensation of human is very much dependent on the heat exchange between the body and 
surroundings the results of the performed physical measurements are explained first and then the 
findings of the studied human subject response are discussed.

6.1.2. Localized Chilled Beam combined with Chilled Ceiling (LCBCC) - 26 - 10  
Apparently, due to the presence of warm window on the left side of the room, the difference 
between air and operative temperature at the points close the window can be distinguished, see 
Figure 28. The effect of the thermal plume from windows prevailed in the upper part of the room 
compared to the lower part. Thus, operative temperature at the points close to the windows 
remains constant whereas the air temperature decreases with height from 1.1 m to 1.7 m. The 
reason for the rather high operative temperature in the points close to the floor is due to the 
heated floor, (see Methods section). 
The warm windows and floor besides increasing the operative temperature in nearby points 
produced strong upward thermal plume. In cases with LCBCC the impact of the plume can be 
seen in the supply jet trajectory. The generated thermal plume always pushes the supply jet from 
the LCB towards point 10, Figure 21. Hence, in the velocity distributions’ layouts higher 
velocity at point 10 can be distinguished.  
Points 6 and 10 with rather low air temperature and high velocity at the heights of 0.1m and 0.6m 
are observed in Figure 28 and Figure 29. The operative temperature of the mentioned points is 
not different from the nearby points. Therefore, the reason for low air temperature and rather 
high velocity at points 6 and 10 is due the effect downward supply flow which is discharged

6.1.4. Localized Chilled Beam combined with Chilled Ceiling (LCBCC)- 28 - 10  
The radiative cooling effect of CC is more apparent in this case than in the cases at 26°C. In a 
conditioned space, where there are several internal heat sources, normally the operative 
temperature is higher than the air temperature. However, at measuring points positioned high 
above the floor and far from the warm floor and/or the simulated window and close to the 
opposite wall, i.e. points 4, 8, 12 and 16, the difference between operative and air temperature is 
either negligible or reverse, i.e. the air temperature is higher than the operative, see Figure 32. 
The reason is most probably due to the cooling effect of the radiant ceiling panels. Since the 
mentioned points, i.e. [points 4, 8, 12 and 16, are located close to the wall, the cooling effect of 
the ceiling panels is more dominant. 
Comparing the air velocity at the lower heights at points 6 and 10 between the present case and 
case LCBCC- 26°C- 10 L/s shows that the supply jet trajectory tends towards point 10 in case 
with 28°C, see Figure 33. This is more noticeable at the heights of 0.1m and 0.6m from the floor 
than higher heights most probably because of the propagation form of the jet which is previously 
mentioned. 
Averaged air temperature values for the measured horizontal planes at different heights shown in 
Table 8 do not show any substantial variation which suggests rather smooth pattern in the room.

at certain point the jet with higher initial momentum has lower temperature than the jet with 
lower initial momentum. This phenomenon can be seen in Figure 40 and Figure 41. In both cases 
with higher air flow rate, i.e. higher initial momentum, the air temperature at a certain point is 
considerably lower than under the reduced air flow rate case.

forward posture.

6.1.10.5. Left - Right side of the body investigation 
The equivalent temperature difference between the right and left side of the upper part of the 
manikin body for different thermal conditions and for the two studied systems, LCBCC and 
CCMV, is shown in Figure 56, Figure 57, Figure 58 and Figure 59. Generally, the left side of the 
body is warmer than the right side as a consequence of the simulated warm window close to the 
seated manikin’s left side at WS1. In addition, none of the differences in all tested cases violates 
the recommended ranges in EN 7730 [8].  
Comparing with the results from the effect of the body posture in each condition one can notice 
that the lower parts of the body, i.e. foot and lower legs, is less affected by radiation from the 
simulated window because the window started 65cm above the floor. Thus, the temperature 
difference is small. In addition, front thighs have the highest value in all cases but LCBCC 28°C- 
13L/s which is the second highest. The reason could stem from the fact that different postures 
change the air flow pattern on a micro-level, i.e. around the body. Furthermore, changing the 
posture causes changing the view angle of the surface of the body to the source of radiation 
which could change the heat exchange between body and the radiation source. In the case of 
sitting leaned forward when exposed to the LCB, the torso could prevent the air reaching the 
thighs through the gap between the torso and the table. 
Surprisingly, the results from the equivalent temperature of the arms and face show that the 
temperature difference between the left and right side of the mentioned body parts are higher 
with LCBCC system than CCMV system. The reason could be the stronger radiant cooling effect 
of the CC panels with CCMV system. By installing the LCB device on the ceiling, some part of 
the radiation from the CC panels is blocked. Thus, the cooling effect of the panels on the 
occupant body at WS1 decreases.  
By comparing the two conditions for the LCBCC system at similar temperature but under 
different airflow rates of 10 and 13 L/s, almost the same values can be seen, which suggests that 
the supplied flow rate has a minor effect on cooling performance in comparison with radiation.

Median value of PAQ for all tested conditions is presented in Figure 60. The basic statistical data

room under both thermal conditions with LCBCC and CCMV systems. PAQ in all cases is 
improved during the acclimatization period. By comparing the values acquired upon arrival and 
after 3 min of staying in the acclimatization chamber, the improvement in PAQ can be clearly 
seen, Figure 60. This is attributable to the effect of adaptation. Then, the improvement continues 
during the acclimatization period. Given the values of the beginning and the end of exposure 
period for all cases except case 4, PAQ improves over the exposure period at both work stations. 
The increase in votes in cases with LCBCC system at WS1 is caused by the combined effect of 
cold air and adaptation. By comparing the last votes after 30 min of exposure under LCBCC at 
WS1 with the same votes at WS2, the effect of cooler air on PAQ at WS1 is observed. In cases 
with CCMV system the increase in PAQ votes could be regarded as the effect of adaptation.  In 
addition, the value for the last 10 min of staying at WS1, both before and after moving away to 
WS2, is similar in all cases with LCB with p>0.05 which suggests that the final vote after the 
exposure period would be the same even though there are some differences at the beginning of 
the exposure period, see Figure 60. Although the values in Table 13 are calculated for the whole 
experiment, the values for 75 percentiles in cases 1, 2 and 3 don’t have considerable difference.   
Due to the lower temperature in the room, unsurprisingly, PAQ for case 1 is higher than other 
studied cases regardless of the ventilation strategy. By taking the ventilation system into account, 
better PAQ is obtained in cases with lower room temperature under the same ventilation system, 
either CCMV or LCBCC.

6.2.3. Overall and Local Thermal Sensation (OTS & LTS) 
As shown in Figure 62, all votes for OTS can be classified within the range of neutral and 
slightly warm. Due to the micro-climate condition generated by LCB, thermal sensation in cases 
with LCBCC system at WS1 is lower than cases with CCMV system. Statistical analysis in 
Appendix 8 shows that cases under the same room temperature but with different cooling system 
are significantly different (p<0.05), i.e. cases 2 and 3 with case 4; and case1 with case 5. Among 
cases with LCBCC, subjects felt warmer in cases 2 and 3 than under case 1, which is related to 
the lower room temperature in case 1 (26°C). However, by comparing the last votes at WS2 and 
first votes at WS1-2 it can be concluded that the value for the thermal sensation upon moving 
back to WS1 is lower in case 3 than that in case 2. In addition, the last vote at WS2 for case 3 is 
less than case 2. Both mentioned issues are attributed to the airflow rate of the LCB, which was 
set to maximum (13 L/s) in case 3 and to minimum (10 L/s) in case 2 when subjects were away 
from WS1 and sat at WS2. 
A slight decrease in TS votes over the acclimatization period is observed for all cases. The 
reason for higher values of the first votes could be the effect of higher metabolism rate upon 
arrival to the acclimatization chamber. In addition, since the experiment was done during winter, 
the effect of winter clothing and the difference between cold outdoor air and warm room air has 
to be also taken into account, even though the question of OTS was asked 3-4 min after clothing 
modification. 
Moreover, statistical (p>0.05) and analytical comparison between the last votes at WS1-1 and 
WS1-2 show no change in subjective votes for OTS. By taking the OTS votes at WS2 into 
account, no substantial changes between votes at WS1 and WS2 is observed. The reason could 
be the uniformity of air and operative temperature fields throughout the room. This is in 
agreement with the results from the physical measurements. Air and operative temperature 
distribution in the room (physical measurements) show no difference larger than 1°C among the 
measured locations in the room. This pattern implies that the effect of simulated window and 
warm floor at WS1 was either diminished by the cooling system or the thermal impact of them 
was not strong enough to be sensed by the subjects. The calculated average values for the 
operative and air temperatures in the room are shown in Table 16. Comparing the air and 
operative temperature distribution in the room and the vertical air temperature distributions at

7.2. Thermal manikin 
The thermal manikin measurements show the better cooling performance of LCBCC system 
compared to the CCMV system. At room temperatures of 26°C and 28°C the equivalent 
temperature of the manikin’s body segments in the case of LCBCC was much lower at the WS1 
than at WS2 while in the case of CCMV it was the opposite. This reveals that the LCBCC could 
cool the body and counteract the effect of the radiant heating from the warm window while in the 
case of CCMV the equivalent temperature increased due to the radiant heat exchange with the 
warm window, see Figure 46 and Figure 47. Some body segments such as hands and arms were 
cooled more than others. This was because these segments were outside of the convective plume 
existing around the body and the supply flow from LCB could easily reach them. 
The cooling performance of the CCMV was almost the same as the cooling of the LCBCC 
system when operated at minimum airflow rate, Figure 85. Therefore, it may be concluded that 
the LCBCC system created the same thermal condition at the WS1 with less airflow rate than the 
CCMV system. This can be regarded as a major benefit of the LCBCC system over the CCMV 
system from the energy saving point of view. 
The interaction of the buoyancy flow from the warm window with the ventilation flow had 
impact on the cooling performance of the CCMV system. As already discussed the buoyancy 
flow discharged the ventilation flow toward the wall opposite to the window resulting in lower 
temperature and higher velocity at WS2. Therefore with this system the equivalent temperature 
was lower at WS2 than WS1 where the radiant heating effect from the warm window was 
present, Figure 46 and Figure 47. This is in agreement with the results of the human subject 
response revealing significantly cooler LTS at WS2 compared to WS2. 
The radiant temperature asymmetry generated by the warm window lead to higher equivalent 
temperature at left side of the manikin (facing the window). However as identified during the 
human subject experiment the measured temperature difference between the left and right side of 
the body did not cause big difference in the LTS and is acceptability.

7.3. Human subjective response 
7.3.1. Perceived Air Quality (PAQ) 
PAQ increased by generating micro-environment at WS1 by the LCBCC system. The PAQ 
reported by the subjects was almost the same in the rest of the room under both thermal 
conditions with LCBCC and CCMV systems. Thus the use of the LCBCC was advantageous 
compared to the CCMV. An important result of this study was that with the LCBCC PAQ in the 
generated micro-environment remained unchanged when the room air temperature increased 
while with the CCMV system PAQ considerably decreased when the room air temperature 
increased.  The velocity range of the airflow supplied from the LCBCC at height 1.1 m with the 
maximum (13 L/s) and minimum (10 L/s) airflow rate at both room temperatures (26 and 28°C) 
was measured as 0.3 m/s and 0.2 m/s, respectively. The temperature of the jet at the same height 
of 1.1 m was within the range of 25.2°C to 25.8°C at 26°C background room air temperature and 
between 26.9°C and 27.9°C at 28°C background room air temperature for the maximum and the 
minimum airflow rates, respectively. Based on the vertical velocity distribution measurements at 
WS1 shown in Figure 42, it is likely that the supply jet was pushed by the convective flow. This 
pattern of the airflow interaction with the LCBCC is supported by the results from the subjective 
response, namely there was considerable number of subjects, who didn’t perceive air movement 
at head, face and neck, see Figure 67. The PAQ reported at WS1 during the first stay (WS1-1) 
and during the second stay (WS1-2) at 26°C and 28°C indicated that subjects were affected more 
by the velocity of the incoming jet than its temperature. This concurs with findings reported in 
the literature [48] that facially applied air movement improved PAQ and diminishes the negative 
impact of the elevated temperature. Nevertheless the interaction of the thermal plume above the 
occupant at WS1 with the airflow supplied from the LCBCC needs to be study in detail in the 
future.

system more significant correlation are found compared to the cases with CCMV. Furthermore, 
there can be seen no apparent difference among the cases with LCBCC under different room 
temperatures, i.e. cases 2 and 3 with case 1 in Table 23. This is probably due to the fact that the

rate or they simply managed easily to set the optimal flow in order to achieve favorable thermal 
conditions. The results are shown in Appendix 12. 
Comparison of the adjusted airflow rates at WS1 before and after moving to WS2 for subjects 
who preferred “no change” in flow rate reveals that a higher airflow rate was adjusted before 
moving to WS2 than after coming back to WS1. Although higher OTS values for the last votes at 
WS1-2 than WS1-1 were expected due to the lower adjusted airflow rate, slightly decrease in 
votes is observed. The lower votes at WS1-2 could be related to the effect of the radiation from 
CC systems or adaptation. The first reason is more likely because in spite of the convection 
cooling effect which has a sudden impact on the subject, the effect of radiation takes time to be 
prevailed. However, the average of OTS at WS1-1 and WS1-2 doesn’t show significant 
difference.  
Most of the subjects preferred no change in the airflow towards the body parts and only few 
people wished for more or less airflow. Among the cases with LCBCC system, one subject in 
case 1 at WS1-1with OTS vote of -1.14 and adjusted airflow of 11.6 L/s  felt slightly cold in 
neck (LTS= -1.0) and left arm (LTS= -1.3) which could be interpreted as draught in those parts. 
In addition, 4 subjects in case 2 at WS1-2 with adjusted airflow rate at the maximum wanted 
more air for the left arm, and felt warm in neck (LTS= 1.52). In addition, one person in case 1 at 
WS1-1 who set the airflow rate at maximum preferred more air towards the head as he felt warm 
(LTS= 1.95) in left and right side of the face. These finding could be a result of the poor cooling 
performance of LCBCC system for those mentioned subjects. 
There were more people who perceived air movement at WS1 in cases with LCBCC system in 
comparison to CCMV system in Figure 67. This indicates the lack of supply air in cases with 
CCMV system. This is in agreement with the observations of the performed smoke visualizations

that the rising plume from the windows pushed away the supply jet from the mixing diffuser. 
However, at WS2 more people perceived air movement with CCMV system due to the 
downward airflow pattern at this work station, Figure 67. In addition, given the number of people 
who felt and didn’t felt air movement in cases with LCBCC, one can conclude that even though 
the air was supplied with rather high velocity at WS1, considerable number of subjects couldn’t 
feel it. This implies that the supply jet was reached to the subject with proper temperature and 
velocity.  
Generally speaking, the OTS votes of subjects who wanted “no change” in airflow rate is within

7.3.5. SBS symptoms 
Given that the exposure period in the test room was 90 minutes for each tested system, SBS 
symptoms could not prevail in such a short time compared to every day conditions in offices 
where people spend 8 hours on average. The short exposure time may explain why no difference 
in the SBS symptoms with the two systems was found. 
Comparison of the prevalence of tiredness and headache among the subjects doesn’t reveal any 
specific effect of the cooling systems in terms increasing or decreasing these symptoms. 
However, concentration ability is slightly increased by using LCBCC system. In addition, the 
performance of both cooling systems was almost similar in terms of eye, nose and throat dryness.  
Statistical analyses show that changes for improving thermal comfort parameters didn’t affect the 
mentioned SBS symptoms prevalence in this experiment, see Appendix 15. Thus, changes for 
improving thermal comfort parameters didn’t affect the SBS symptoms prevalence in the short-
term run of this experiment. The positive influence of cold and fresh outdoor air on people 
causes low votes at the beginning of the very beginning of the exposure period. Thus, the first of 
in each case was lower than the last vote after the exposure period. The influence of low air 
temperature is observed in terms of improving the headache, concentration ability and headache 
level.

9. Conclusions 
 The PAQ generated by both studied systems was within the acceptable range, i.e. 
between just acceptable and clearly acceptable. PAQ inside the micro-environment was 
considerably increased by using LCBCC system.  PAQ votes showed the equal 
performance of both studied cooling system in the rest of the room. 
 Votes on overall thermal sensation (OTS) indicated better performance of LCBCC 
system compared to CCMV system at WS1. The thermal sensation reported during the 
exposure to the two systems was within the range of “neutral” and “slightly warm” 
thermal sensations for the whole body.  
 Previous study showed that using LCB without CC panels generated unacceptable 
thermal environment outside of the microclimate. However, OTS and OTS acceptability 
votes in the present study clearly showed that combination of CC panels with LCB not 
only generated acceptable thermal environment in the micro-climate region but also rest 
of the occupied zone was evaluated as acceptable thermal environment. 
 Air and operative temperature distribution was almost uniform with no difference higher 
than 1°C between the measured locations in the room. The chilled ceiling diminished the 
effect of the buoyancy flow from the simulated warm window and warm floor resulting 
in better distribution of the flow in the room and less discomfort due to radiant 
temperature asymmetry at WS1. The results from physical measurements and manikin 
study were in good agreement with human subjective study. 
 Under the same ventilation system, OTS and OTS acceptability of cases with lower room 
temperature were evaluated as more acceptable than those with higher room temperature. 
 LTS and LTS acceptability votes indicated better performance of LCBCC system 
compared to CCMV system. The reported LTS votes were in the range of “neutral” and 
“slightly warm”. The votes revealed no local thermal discomfort. Warmest LTS was 
reported at WS1 were at 28°C with CCMV system. 
 There were more people who perceived air movement at WS1 in cases with LCBCC 
system in comparison to CCMV system. This indicated the lack of supply air in cases 
with CCMV system. However, more subjects felt air movement at WS2 in cases with 
CCMV system compare to cases with LCBCC system due to the established flow pattern 
in the room with CCMV system. 
==PAGE:144==
135 
 
 No significant differences were observed in terms of SBS symptoms prevalence for the 
exposure to the environment (inside the microclimate and in the rest of the room) 
generated by the systems. 
 Work environment conditions generated by the LCBCC in general were assessed by the 
subjects as more acceptable than the one by the CCMV system. 
 Analysis of individual control of the supply flow rate indicated that the subjects either 
didn’t participate actively in adjusting the airflow rate or they simply managed to set the 
optimal flow in order to achieve favorable thermal conditions. 
 The difference between the LCB jet temperature at the vicinity of the subject and the 
average room temperature was small due to the entrainment of the convective plume 
from the simulated window. 
 Results from the thermal manikin showed that cooling performance of the LCBCC 
system increased with increasing the supply flow rate but the increase was smaller at high 
room temperature and for the body parts not directly exposed to the flow.
