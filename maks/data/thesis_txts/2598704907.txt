Abstract
This work introduces stochasticity to the Inhomogeneous Helholmtz equation through
the medium. The eﬀects of the stochastic medium on the forward and inverse
problem is investigated numerically. The numerical investigation of the Stochastic
Partial Diﬀerential equation is followed by a derivation of a forward operator in
the stochastic setting, and the spectral decomposition of the derived operator is
analyzed. The investigation of the forward problem relies on Circulant Embedding
and Finite Elements, combined into the Monte Carlo Finite Element method. The
inverse problem studied is the inverse medium problem and is solved based on a
Recursive Linearization Algorithm, and the reconstruction error given the random
ﬁeld ﬂuctuation is assessed. For the forward operator, it is found that the variance in
the waveﬁelds seems to be dependent on the wavenumber. Furthermore, hints toward
non-convergence to the mean are seen for wave-numbers larger than 2π. The inverse
medium problem is solved using Recursive Linearization. The problem turned out to
be very sensitive towards the regularization parameters, but reconstructions were
possible. The derivation of the forward operator is based on the Lippmann-Schwinger
equation. The derivation yields an integral operator when interpreted as a ﬁrst-order
expansion of a Neumann series. Spectral decomposition of the forward operator is
derived, and a spectral leakage is found when the stochastics medium is white noise
based. The spectral leakage does not only introduce changes in the amplitude of
the spectrum, but it turns out complex, meaning a dispersion is happening. The
spectral decomposition leaves open questions about the spectrum of the spectral
leakage, and the direct eﬀect is still subject for further research.

Introduction
Partial diﬀerential equations are used in many ﬁelds of study within science such as
physics, engineering, and biology to model reality. Problems within ﬂuid dynamics,
the stress of air-plane shielding, and medical imaging, among others, are the typical
examples. This has lead to extensive literature regarding the mathematical back-
ground of partial diﬀerential equations [1] [2] [3] [4] [5] and many more, and for many
years the focus has been on the deterministic setting or minimal focus regarding
the stochastics. This is partly due to the irregularities of stochastic processes, and
the noise has often been assumed Gaussian noise. Recent developments both within
mathematics itself but also the rise in computational power has spiked an interest in
investigating the stochastic setting. The newfound interest lead to a mathematically
sound stochastic calculus, numerical methods for stochastic ordinary partial diﬀer-
ential equations, and applications of these ordinary stochastic diﬀerential equation
has rooted in e.g. ﬁnance, chemistry and population dynamics [6]. The introduction
of stochasticity to the ﬁeld of partial diﬀerential equations, especially in regards
to uncertainty quantiﬁcation of large scale systems, has also seen a recent rise in
interest . The work presented here evolves around acoustic and electromagnetic
scattering modelled by the Inhomogeneous Helmholtz equation. In [7] this model
was studied as an inverse medium problem with stochasticity entering in terms
of a stochastic source. A solution scheme for the inverse medium problem for a
stochastic source was proposed and illustrated. A slightly diﬀerent setting, also based
on the Inhomogeneous Helmholtz equation, was considered in [8] where the inverse
medium problem was solved numerically for a random medium with diﬀerent level of
information at hand.
A more classical treatment of the Helmholtz equation in vacuum is presented in [9]
where a complete singular value decomposition of the forward operator is proven,
and bounds for the stably recoverable information is shown.
The goal is to introduce stochasticity to the Inhomogenous Helmholtz equation and
investigate the eﬀect a random medium has on models of Helmholtz type, for the
forward and inverse scattering problem in the two cases:
1. where no source is present but an incoming wave is.
2. where a source is present with no incoming wave.
The investigation of the eﬀects a random medium has on the Inhomogeneous
Helmholtz equation will be done through following milestones:
1. Introduce and develop the needed mathematical tools
2. Implement proper sampling techniques and Partial Diﬀerential Equation (PDE)
solver
3. Implement Recursive Linearization and solve the inverse problem with known
techniques
4. Derive the Lippmann-Schwinger equation for the stochastic setting
5. Based on the derived Lippmann-Schwinger equation deﬁne the forward operator
and calculate the spectral decomposition
The outline of the work is structured as follows. The ﬁrst section introduces the
model considered and how the stochasticity is being modelled in addition to the

The stochastic aspects
Before returning to (1), another most essential factor in this work is discussed, namely
the notion of stochasticity used. To be more speciﬁc random ﬁelds. These objects
are the ones making it possible to introduce the needed stochasticity over the domain
considered, here the plane. The framework presented is based on the axiomatic
introduction of random variables and introduced only for real-valued continuous
process and random ﬁelds. Few remarks regarding discrete or complex-valued cases
might be mentioned, but will not serve any purpose regarding the model (1). Two of
the most fundamental constructions for the stochastic analysis are white noise and
Brownian motion [13].

Deﬁnition 6 (Stochastic process). Let T be an ordered set. Let F be a ﬁltration

3.3
Mathematical tools
To assess the similarity or relatedness of two random variables, the covariance is
used. For random ﬁelds, this function yields the covariance of two points in the
domain. The covariance is a symmetric positive semi-deﬁnite function, it is given by
the mean, and is deﬁned as:
Deﬁnition 9 (Covariance function). Let Ψ be a random ﬁeld or stochastic process,
and denote by η = E [Ψ] the mean of Ψ. Then the covariance is given by:
C(x, y) = COV [Ψ(x), Ψ(y)] = E [(Ψ(x) −η(x)) (Ψ(y) −η(y))] ,
x, y ∈D
With the notion of covariance, stationarity for second-order random ﬁelds can be
formulated:
Deﬁnition 10 (Stationary random ﬁeld). A second-order random ﬁeld is said to be
stationary if the mean is constant and the covariance is of the form C(x, y) = c(x−y),
for some function c.

3.5
Sampling
A signiﬁcant class of methods used in uncertainty quantiﬁcation to solve problems
where the solution might not be tractable in general, is the Monte Carlo Methods
[25]. These methods heavily rely on multiple samples of the sporadic part and thus
both numerical precision and speed factors in. Simulation of a stochastic process
or random ﬁeld can be done by direct application of Theorem 3.3, but this requires
knowledge of the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of the covariance operator (5). This
knowledge might not always be at hand. Even then it can be acquired numerically
through various method, e.g. Galerkin, Nyst¨om, Collocation etc. [24]. However, this
introduces a numerical error in the estimation of the eigenvectors and eigenvalues, in
addition to the inescapable truncation error. The truncation arises dealing with the
inﬁnite series as the expansion of the stochastic process. In [17] an algorithm based
on Cholesky factorization is stated and can be summarized as:
1. Deﬁne the grid
2. Construct the covariance matrix from the grid, and calculate it’s square root
by Cholesky factorization such that C = ΛΛ⊤
3. Construct a vector v of desired length of i.i.d. entries vi ∼N(0, 1)
4. Construct the sample as Ψ = Λv
which has a computational cost of O(n3). This method works for all Gaussian
processes and ﬁelds but is computationally expensive. Having said that, it is possible
to obtain exact samples of stationary Gaussian random ﬁelds, as shown in [26].
This method is more complicated than the one presented in [17] based on Cholesky
factorization but much faster. The method of [26] utilizes the fact that circulant
matrices (Deﬁnition 13) take a special form under the discrete Fourier transform.
Circulant matrices are deﬁned as matrices with the following property:
Deﬁnition 13 (Circulant Matrix). Consider an n1 × n1 real valued Teoplitz matrix
(deﬁned in 14), then such a matrix is called circulant if each column is a circulant
shift of the elements of the preceding column.

Turning the attention to (1) once more. This time concerned with the forward
problem. The forward problem is simply the solution us to the diﬀerential equation
(1) when the source, scatterer, boundary condition and the incoming wave is known.

component in the later discussed algorithm for solving the inverse problem. The
forward problem is, in most cases, well-posed, which means it fulﬁls the three criteria

1. A solution exists
2. The solution is unique
3. The solution depends continuously on the data

The inverse problem
Having seen how the random ﬁeld aﬀects solutions to the forward problem, the
inverse problems are considered. Inverse problems arise in a variety of situations
and deal with problems such as determining the model coeﬃcients, reconstruction of
initial data, estimation of source functions etc. [7]. But unlike the forward problem,
the inverse problems are often ill-posed, which means one or more of the items listed
for a problem to be well-posed is violated (item 1 - 3 in section 4). Lack of stability,
among others, is one of the situations often encountered in inverse problems, making
them severely ill-posed, especially at ﬁxed frequencies [32]. This, unfortunately,
means that small changes in the measurement data can lead to large reconstruction
errors. One way of dealing with the ill-posedness of inverse problems are though
regularisation [10] which seeks to make the recovery of data as stable as possible.
The inverse problem of interest here is the so-called inverse medium problem (IMP),
which is to reconstruct the inhomogeneous medium given the right-hand side and
measurements of the scattered wave ﬁeld. The IMP turns out to be non-linear in
addition to ill-posed.
In article [7] they introduced the Recursive Linearization Method (RLM) to solve the
IMP when the source rather than the medium is assumed stochastic. The method
solves the IMP for each basis function appearing in the Karhunen-Lo´eve of the source
and reconstruct the scatterer based on these solutions. It is concluded that when
the medium is stochastic, the method does not work properly and is subject for
longer-term research where new methods are needed. In the article by [8] the IMP
with an incoming wave rather than a source is considered. This turns out to be
better conditioned, and the RLM is successfully applied to reconstruct a scatterer
in the presence of a random medium when diﬀerent levels of knowledge about the
random medium is known. A combined approach of the method presented in [7] and
[8] is implemented. This is done to assess the impact of the random ﬁeld’s inﬂuence
on the inverse problem.

Discussion
7.1
Forward problem MCFEM
Inspecting the results from section 4 obtained by the MCFEM, for the random
ﬁelds having White noise and Orhnstein-Uhlenbeck structure, it is seen that as the
wave-number increases so does the deviation from the deterministic solution. These
observations already start to show for the wave-numbers larger than k ≥2π. For the
smaller wave-numbers, it seems that for enough samples, the mean converges to the
deterministic solution. But on ﬁgure 5D, relating to the white noise, and for ﬁgure
6B to 6D, relating to the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck like noise, it is no longer clear whether
or not the mean and deterministic solution will eventually coincide. What is more,
is that the relative ﬂuctuations in the solutions seems to become increasingly more
vivid for an increasing wave-number. Numerically this implies that more realizations
will be needed for better estimates. Aforementioned becomes computationally more
expensive. It also indicates that the variance depends on the wave-number. However,
these results were obtained for 50 samples at each wave-number for each noise
structure, and it is possible the mean of the sampled solution will still converge to
the deterministic solution. However, the most interesting part is the hint towards
a wave-number dependent variance. Thus it is seen that a computational eﬃcient
sampling method becomes important using the MCFEM method to get precise
estimates. Figure 5 and 6 shows the correlation structure of the underlying random
ﬁeld plays a major role. This is expected and does underline the modelling part
concerned with deciding on the random ﬁelds best describing the real-world problem
being modelled. As the method used was MCFEM the random ﬁelds did not pose
huge numerical implications for the PDE solver, but rather on the sampling, and as
the method used for sampling introduced in section 3.5 only evolved around second
order stationary random ﬁelds, it limited the range of randomness possible to assess.
7.2
Inverse problem RLM
Regarding the inverse problem, it is clearly seen on ﬁgure 7 that the reconstruction
error in the cases where the propagation is done in a stochastic medium is converging
slower than the deterministic case with added Gaussian noise. It is also seen that
the error starts to diverge at a fairly large error compared to what is possible to
obtain in the classical setting. This divergence of the error could be solved by setting
the step size to a smaller value; this would result in a slower convergence altogether,
and become more computationally ineﬃcient.
Another explanation for this could be the dependency on the wave-number of the
variance found for the forward problem in section 4. The algorithm outlined in 1
relies on reconstructing more and more information about the scatterer/medium in
each iteration for an increase in the wave-number, having the uncertainty change the
higher the wave-number without a proper way to deal with the increase in variance.
It is also noted that the algorithm in 1 relies on the Fr´echet derivative of the forward
operator, which in this case is non-linear, and a linearization of the problem is done,
but as the medium is now irregular and bends in every point (not diﬀerentiable) this
linearisation might not be suﬃcient.

7.3
On spectral decomposition
The spectral decomposition from section 6 yielded a lot of interesting results, but
it should be noted that these are for the ﬁrst-order approximation of the Neumann
series, and not the entire forward operator itself. Adding higher-order terms is
possible, but will require many more cumbersome manipulations. It is conjectured
that a general expansion for arbitrary order can be obtained. Another thing to keep
in mind is, the results presented here assumes the existence of an inverse to the
forward operator in terms of the Neumann series, as shown this limits the choices of
k as a direct function of the stochasticity, which is only true under some assumptions
on the boundedness of the random ﬁelds within the domain. This, of course, has its
limitations.
The ﬁrst interesting thing to note is the variance of the intrinsic random variables
arising in the calculation of the normal operator Υ; these random variables has a
variance not only depending on the index of the expansion but is also depended on
the radius of the domain D, in addition to the wave-number. This, not only, shows
that the conjectures forming when considering the forward solution in section 4 to be
true, but gives a direct relationship between the wave-number and variance. What
can be seen from ﬁgure 9 is that the stochasticity ”fades out” rather quickly for
small wave-numbers as the variance has a cut-oﬀat small values for m and for a
wave-number of size π the cut-oﬀis seen already within the ﬁrst m = ±25 terms. For
a higher wave-number, the variance decreases for a ﬁxed term, m, in the expansion,
but the cut-oﬀis shifted. This means that each term varies a little less, but more
”information” is leaked to multiple projections. Hence the subpart of the operator
Υ seen in (24) is deemed the spectral leakage. This name is given as it seems that
when stochasticity is introduced, the forward normal operator of the ﬁrst-order
approximation, will have a more complicated spectrum which can be seen from ﬁgure
10 and 11 . The spectral leakage (24) does not only alter the singular values for V1
from [9] it also adds an imaginary part. From the closure property (9) one would
expect the mean to still be zero, which is also true for the real and imaginary parts
by themselves as is seen on ﬁgure 14A and 14B. Despite this, the modulus of the
spectra is no longer normally distributed, and thus (9) no longer holds instead each
spectral value will have its magnitude follow a Rayleigh distribution [41], this eﬀect
is clearly seen on ﬁgure 11A. The addition of the imaginary part also introduces
a spatial frequency dependent non-uniform phase-shift. For the spectra observed
with no stochasticity present the phase-shift or angle of the spectral values are zero,
however, for the spectral values found for (24) the mean of the phase shift appears
to exhibit a very distinctive behaviour as is clear from ﬁgure 11. These observations
carry over to Υ with a slight modiﬁcation being the addition of the mean term (22)
which adds a more varying structure to the real part of the spectrum, 10A leaving
the observations about the modulus and angle. The spectrum of Υ is unfortunately
not the sought after spectral decomposition of the forward operator VqV1, and as the
spectral leakage of (24) refrain one from writing the expansion on a standard form,
no direct linkage between the operator Υ and VqV1 can be calculated. The best thing
that can be done is discretize (25) and estimate the spectrum of VqV1 numerically
when a source is known. This was done for the projection used throughout f = ψn,
and the result is seen on ﬁgure 15.

8
Conclusion and outlook
Conclusion
Stochasticity in terms of second order stationary Gaussian random ﬁelds was success-
fully introduced in the Inhomogeneous Helmholtz equation as part of the medium,
and with the eﬃcient sampling method, Circulant Embedding, for these types of
random ﬁelds the implementation of the sampling based Monte Carlo Finite Element
method proved suﬃcient and computationally tractable. Based on this method it
was possible to obtain numerous forward solutions and estimate moments of the
underlying distribution to the solution. This was possible for multiple structures
on the covariance of the random ﬁelds and solutions was obtained for White noise
and Ornstein-Uhlenbeck like correlation structure. Using the implemented solver for
the forward problem, the Recursive Linearization method was implemented when
an incoming wave rather than a source was present. It was possible to reconstruct
the scatterer within the medium to some precision, but ﬁnding the parameters for
optimal convergence of the error proved diﬃcult. The implementation did not expand
on the Recursive Linearization method in a notable way and only the reconstruction
of the mean behaviour or strong smooth structures was possible.
The mathematical investigation of the forward operator, mapping a source function
to measurement boundary was carried out as a spectral analysis when the medium
was white noise driven. It was possible to determine the spectrum for the normal
operator to the normal equations of the system. For this operator it was found
that the introduction of the simple random ﬁeld considered here lead to a spectral
leakage. This spectral leakage did not only alter the amplitude of the singular value,
it introduced a dispersion as the angle of the spectral values were no long purely
real and positive. It was also shown that this spectral leakage had a well-deﬁned
non-trivial very distinct mean structure w.r.t. the angular behaviour. However, this
was not directly for the forward operator itself, and due to the spectral leakage,
analysis showed that the normal operator when the medium is white noise driven
can not be written on normal form and a direct link between the two operators is
not possible. Instead a numerical calculation of the spectrum related to the ﬁrst
order expansion of the Neumann series was successfully calculated for the forward
operator. Showing if a speciﬁc source and orthonormal basis is known, it is possible
to numerically estimate the spectrum for the forward operator in that given case.

Outlook
Forward problem
There are many directions in which to take the above work. A ﬁrst step could be
the implementation of a more precise or computationally eﬃcient method than the
MCFEM, or a completely new, more sophisticated method of solving or approximating
the solution to the forward problem could be considered. Such method could be,
applying the Wick product based on a calculus dependent on Hermite polynomials.
Inverse problem
Another direction could be to derive an algorithm to properly deal with the increase
in the relative variance as the wave number grows, or come up with a totally diﬀerent
approach than the Recursive Linearization Method. This could for example be
methods reliant on the spectral decomposition of the forward operator.
Spectral decomposition
For the point of view of the spectral decomposition there are multiple directions.
The entire framework used to obtain the results were based on the derivative of
the two-sided Brownian sheet, and the integral deﬁned was introduced using the
eigenvectors and eigenvalues of the covariance operator relating to the two-sided
Brownian sheet. This also means that the spectral decomposition of section 6 will not
hold true if the underlying noise is not white noise. Therefore the same calculations
can be done for diﬀerent type of integrals or a more general approach could be
deployed maybe generalizing the above results.
The scatterer q considered in the spectral decomposition was also for constant mean
and variance, and generalization to the cases where the mean and variance themselves
behaved diﬀerently.
When it comes to the results achieved in section 6, the eﬀects of the spectral
leakage needs investigation. The implications and interpretation of the spectral
leakage for physical situations has not been investigated. The physical interpretation
of the relationship between the wave-number and maximum ﬂuctuations of the
random ﬁeld is also of interest, or maybe a generalization, if possible, dampening
the restriction of the stochasticity could be interesting. It is also possible that the
spectral decomposition could be used to derive eﬃcient solvers for inverse medium
problems where the noise structure are assumed to be white noise.
