Abstract
The ongoing electrification and integration of renewables in Denmark’s distribution grids
pose challenges, including lack of reserve capacity, component damage, voltage insta-
bility all leading to increased investment needs. This master’s thesis develops a compre-
hensive framework for activating demand-side flexibility (DSF) through dynamic tariffs to
optimize local grid operations within the Cerius/Radius grid.
Objectives include quantifying flexibility potential across customer segments, forecasting
price-responsive consumption, predicting voltage profiles, and optimizing tariffs to bal-
ance grid stability, revenue neutrality, and regulatory compliance.
The methodology integrates data-driven flexibility assessment, regime-sensitive time se-
ries modeling (MS-SARX) for consumption forecasting, graph neural networks (ST-GNN)
for spatio-temporal voltage prediction, and multi-objective optimization for tariff design.
Evaluation employs real-world data from smart meters, SCADA, and grid topology, vali-
dated against baselines like XGBoost.
Three case studies demonstrate the potential: (1) A large infrastructure facility deferring
10 million DKK in reserve capacity reinforcements via load shifting; (2) a residential area
mitigating EV-induced overloading, reducing peaks by over 15%; and (3) a rural network
improving voltage stability in extended lines, potentially postponing 50 million DKK invest-
ments.
Findings indicate significant economic savings, voltage improvements >5%, and peak
reductions >15%, enabling investment deferrals and enhanced resilience. Implications
underscore DSF’s role in sustainable grid management, though limitations in data gran-
ularity and customer adoption warrant further research on scalability and multi-purpose
flexibility markets.

1
Introduction
The ongoing green transition and the electrification that accompanies are fundamentally
reshaping the operational dynamics of electricity distribution grids. This evolution intro-
duces substantial challenges, including voltage instability, elevated substation loading,
and the imperative for improved grid resilience amid high penetration of renewables like
wind and solar. In Denmark, these issues are particularly acute due to aggressive elec-
trification targets and increasing demand from electric vehicles (EVs), heat pumps, and
other flexible assets. For instance, projections indicate that with sustained policy support,
the EV fleet could grow significantly by 2030, exacerbating grid constraints[1]. However,
demand-side flexibility (DSF) — the ability to adjust customer demand in time to mitigate
system constraints — offers significant benefits, such as reducing peak loads by up to
58% in local grids and achieving up to 30% savings in electricity consumption through
smart devices, as demonstrated in pilot projects like Roskilde Energy Centre and inter-
national initiatives such as Flexi4Home and FED. Challenges include consumer adoption
barriers, high setup costs, data privacy under GDPR, and regulatory hurdles to ensure
equitable tariffs [2].
This thesis investigates the incentivization and activation of multi-purpose DSF in Danish
distribution grids, with a focus on the operations of Radius/Cerius, a prominent DSO. By
developing a comprehensive framework for flexibility assessment, forecasting, and opti-
mization through price signals, the research aims to support efficient grid management,
sector coupling (e.g., with PtX technologies), and reduced greenhouse gas emissions.
The following sections provide background on these challenges, formulate the research
problem, and outline the objectives and scope.
1.1
Background and Challenges
Contemporary distribution grids face mounting pressures from electrification trends, the
intermittency of renewable energy, and the need for sector coupling at all levels. In Den-
mark, where renewables constitute a significant portion of the energy mix, voltage fluctu-
ations, substation overloads, and power losses are recurrent issues, particularly in areas
with high EV and heat pump penetration. These challenges compromise grid stability and
necessitate investments in expansions.
The issues arising from increased consumption include maintaining a voltage frequency
of 50 Hz, ensuring correct voltage magnitudes at all times and locations, guaranteeing
sufficient reserve capacity without damaging components. DSF can be categorized into
explicit flexibility, where consumers respond to direct signals from the distribution sys-
tem operator (DSO), and implicit flexibility, driven by incentives like dynamic tariffs that
encourage shifts in consumption (e.g., charging EVs during periods of high renewable
output). In Denmark, this approach is supported by high smart meter penetration (>99%)
and regulatory frameworks like Begrænset Netadgang (BNA) for limited grid access, with
emerging concepts like Market-Based Activation of Flexibility (MAF) under development
to incentivize multi-purpose flexibility.
For example, projects like CITIES have shown potential savings throughout a variety of
industries, such as district heating, data centers and heat pumps [3].

1.2
Motivation and Research Gaps
Building on these challenges, the motivation lies in leveraging DSF to bridge current gaps
in grid management. DSF, enabled by digitalization, AI, IoT, and dynamic tariffs, provides
a cost-effective alternative to grid expansions. The motivation for this research stems
from the need to quantify multi-purpose flexibility potential across customer segments,
activate it via incentives to address grid constraints, and integrate advanced modeling
linking price to demand, for informed decision-making. This can reduce risks of reserve
shortages, enhance grid planning precision, promote equitable cost allocation, and sup-
port a weather-driven energy system where DSF acts as a key enabler for stability and
de-carbonization.
1.3
Problem Formulation
To address the identified gaps, this thesis tackles the following research question and
sub-questions. This thesis addresses the following central research question:
How can dynamic and localized modeling of electricity consumption optimize grid utiliza-
tion in Danish distribution networks?
To explore this question, the following sub-questions are examined:
1. How can the flexibility potential of large customers be quantified and selected based
on their capacity for temporal load shifting?
2. How can flexibility be mobilized to generate insights into constraints within critical
customer segments, emphasizing daily and annual profiles alongside meter data,
to benefit both customers and the DSO?
3. Can advanced models enhance capacity forecasting and flexibility assessment,
thereby determining the viability of flexibility-based solutions?
4. What requirements must be fulfilled to establish a sufficient pool of market-based
flexibility, and what tools and data are essential for optimizing grid planning?
This formulation is grounded in the observed variability of consumption patterns in mod-
ern grids, where capacity often fails to align with demand during peak periods. A dynamic
management approach can empower large customers to contribute to load balancing,
thereby diminishing the reliance on expensive grid expansions and fostering rapid, accu-
rate evaluations of operational constraints.
1.4
Thesis Objectives, Scope, and Limitations
Guided by this problem formulation, the thesis pursues the following objectives within
defined boundaries. The primary objective of this thesis is to develop and implement a
framework that enables demand-side flexibility in distribution grids through price signals.
This framework encompasses:
• Flexibility assessment through multi-layered analysis of voltage quality, consump-
tion patterns, substation loading, and customer segmentation.
• Forecasting the change of customers’ behavior with varying price signals.
• Optimization of flexibility via dynamic tariff design, incorporating graph-based volt-
age predictions and economic incentives.
The framework will be evaluated through case studies in diverse Danish grid contexts,
including a large infrastructure facility in a stressed grid area, a rural voltage constrained

Technical and Economic Foundation
This chapter will go through the technical foundations for understanding the structures
behind the actual implementation of a demand-side flexibility framework. This is neces-
sary to fully grasp the mechanism and issues there are with the rise of electrification and
sustainable transition happening in Denmark. Furthermore, the economical benefits and
issues will be discussed, to further explain why demand-side flexibility is an advantage
for modern distribution grids.
2.1
The Danish Electricity Grid
Denmark’s electricity system operates through a multi-level hierarchy designed to trans-
port electricity safely and efficiently from large-scale generation facilities to end consumers.
The grid is formally split into two operators: Transmission System Operator (TSO) and Dis-
tribution System Operator (DSO), where each operator maintain their own set of voltage
levels within the grid.
TSOs are operating the high voltage lines at 132kV and above, creating a regional trans-
mission systems that connect major generation facilities and provide bulk power transport
between regions. In Denmark Energinet is the only TSO and they maintain the upper volt-
age levels and tasks such a ensuring correct frequency.

since some days the production might be high, leading to fine voltage and good reserve
capacity, whilst other days these sources might not be able to lift those issues, creating a
potential vulnerable grid.
2.1.1
Technical Challenges Driving Flexibility Needs
To further dive into the technical challenges caused by increased electrification, the issues
addressed in this thesis will be presented:
Reserve Capacity and System Reliability
Electrical systems require continuous reserve capacity to handle unexpected demand
increases, generation outages, or equipment failures. In Denmark’s distribution networks,
this requirement creates particular challenges due to the ”N-1” reliability criterion where
the system must remain stable even if any single major component fails.
For many distribution substations, particularly those serving rural or industrial areas, main-
taining adequate reserve capacity requires significant infrastructure investments that may
be economically unjustified given the limited customer base served. The Cerius/Radius
network faces numerous situations where substations operate near capacity limits, creat-
ing vulnerability to service interruptions.
Peak demand correlation exacerbates these challenges. When multiple customers simul-
taneously increase consumption during cold winter evenings when heat pumps activate
across a service area, distribution transformers may exceed their rated capacity, leading
to accelerated aging or component failure. Demand-side flexibility addresses these chal-
lenges by enabling temporary load reduction during critical periods, effectively creating

”virtual” reserve capacity without physical infrastructure investments.
Voltage Stability in Distribution Networks
Voltage stability represents one of the more difficult technical challenges in modern dis-
tribution systems. Unlike transmission networks where voltage control relies primarily on
large, centralized equipment, distribution systems must manage voltage with limited con-
trol resources while serving highly variable loads. Throughout the thesis, the upper and
lower limits for voltage acceptance is ±5% of the nominal voltage at 10.4kV, even though
the standard presented in ”DS/EN 50160: Voltage characteristics of electricity supplied by
public electricity networks”, allows a ±10% range [4]. The underlying thought about this is
to adopt a more conservative methodology in voltage regulation during grid planning and
analysis, thereby ensuring enhanced stability, reduced risk of deviations under dynamic
conditions such as fluctuating loads or distributed generation, and superior overall power
quality for end-users in the Danish distribution system managed by operators like Cerius
and Radius
Substation transformers in distribution systems are frequently equipped with on-load tap
changers that enable adjustments to the voltage ratio for regulation purposes; however,
this mechanism may prove insufficient for managing extreme load variability or rapid fluc-
tuations from distributed generation, potentially resulting in voltage deviations that directly
affect end-users.
End-of-line voltage problems occur frequently in rural areas served by long distribution
feeders. As electrical current flows through cables and overhead lines, voltage naturally
drops due to impedance. When demand increases or distributed generation decreases,
customers at the end of distribution lines may experience voltage below acceptable limits.
Reverse power flow challenges emerge when distributed generation exceeds local con-
sumption. Solar installations and small wind turbines can cause a voltage rise that propa-
gate back toward distribution substations, potentially causing over-voltage conditions that
damage equipment or trigger protective disconnections. Traditional solutions involve ex-
pensive infrastructure investments such as installing voltage regulators, upgrading trans-
formers, or reinforcing distribution lines. Demand-side flexibility offers an alternative ap-
proach by modifying consumption patterns to maintain voltage within acceptable ranges.
Fast electrification and Load Growth
Denmark’s aggressive electrification strategy, particularly the widespread adoption of elec-
tric vehicles and electric heat pumps, creates unprecedented load growth in distribution
networks originally designed for much lower consumption levels. It is forecasted that
within the next 10 years, overall consumption at a majority of primary substations will
double [5].
Electric vehicle charging presents particular challenges due to its concentration and tim-
ing. Many EV owners charge their vehicles simultaneously during night hours through
price optimizing schedules, creating new peak demand periods that can overload local
distribution transformers. The coincident charging behavior creates much higher peak-to-
average demand ratios than traditional residential loads. Heat pump deployment similarly
affects distribution network loading, particularly during cold weather when heat pumps op-
erate at maximum capacity. Unlike traditional electric heating, heat pumps create larger
but more efficient electrical loads that can significantly impact local distribution systems.
In addition to these residential and transportation-driven trends, Denmark’s industrial sec-
tor is experiencing a parallel push toward electrification as part of broader de-carbonization
efforts. Many industrial processes, especially those involving low-temperature heating
(where more than half of process heat demand is below 80°C), are shifting from natural

Traditional solutions involve extensive infrastructure upgrades with long implementation

nation.
2.1.2
Current Regulatory Structure
The Danish Utility Regulator (Forsyningstilsynet) oversees the electricity sector, ensuring
compliance with EU directives while promoting efficiency and sustainability. Denmark’s
regulatory landscape is part of the broader EU framework, including the Clean Energy
Package (2019) and Electricity Market Design Reform (2024), which emphasize market-
based flexibility, dynamic pricing, and aggregator roles to integrate renewables and man-
age grid variability. At the national level, Denmark adopts a ”flexibility-first” approach,
leveraging the Nord Pool market for cross-border balancing and extensive hydropower
resources in the Nordic region [7]. Key initiatives include Tariff Model 3.0, which intro-
duces dynamic and time-of-use (ToU) tariffs to reflect real-time grid conditions, incentiviz-
ing demand-side flexibility through cost-based price signals that encourage load shifting
during peaks. This aligns with EU mandates under the Electricity Directive (EU 2019/944),
requiring tariffs to be cost-reflective and non-discriminatory, while supporting non-firm flex-
ible connection agreements for areas with capacity constraints [8].
The overall landscape balances consumer protection with innovation, mandating smart
meter rollouts (nearly complete in Denmark) to enable dynamic pricing, where consumers
can adjust usage based on hourly spot prices. Demand-side flexibility is core, turning
assets like EVs, heat pumps, and data centers into grid stabilizers through aggregation
and incentives, potentially reducing peak demand and deferring investments. Denmark-
specific measures include locational incentives in network charges (e.g., lower fees in
demand-surplus areas) and, from 2023, injection charges for distribution-level produc-
ers to promote co-location of generation and consumption. Expected changes in 2025
and beyond include accelerated implementation of dynamic tariffs, with Denmark exper-
imenting further to achieve 100% fossil-free electricity, supported by EU-wide flexibility
needs doubling by 2030 (to 24%of demand, or 288 TWh). By mid-2025, the EU’s Action
Plan for Affordable Energy will propose tariffs rewarding load-shifting, clarify State aid for
non-fossil flexibility, and issue guidance on retail contract remuneration [8]. In Denmark,
this may expand Tariff Model 3.0 with more granular elements, aligning with projections
for sevenfold flexibility growth by 2050 and investments of EUR 730 billion in distribution
grids EU-wide by 2040 [7].

2.1.3
Economic Benefits of Demand-Side Flexibility
Recent research from the ”Danish Utility Regulator’s anthology on better regulation in the
energy sector” provides quantitative evidence supporting the economic advantages of
demand-side flexibility implementation. In their analysis of multi-purpose flexibility activa-
tion, Madsen et al. (2024) demonstrate substantial potential savings through demand-side
management strategies
Simulation and pilot studies indicate that residential electricity consumption can be re-
duced by up to 30% through smart device integration and automated control systems.
National and international projects, including Center FOr IT-Intelligent Energy Systems
(CITIES), Flexible Energy Denmark (FED), have shown that smart IoT devices can achieve
significant reductions in peak consumptions while maintaining user comfort.
A case study focusing on Renewable Energy Communities, revealed that targeted flex-
ibility measures could reduce local grid stress by up to 58%, potentially deferring costly
infrastructure upgrades. The analysis further suggests that sector coupling through tech-
nologies like power-to-X (PtX) and integrated energy systems can yield efficiency gains,
resulting in reduction of greenhouse gas emissions. [9]
The 2022 energy price spikes highlighted flexibility’s value, where consumers shifting EV
charging and appliance usage to low-price periods reduced system peaks during low re-
newable output. Economic modeling indicates that proper tariff design, including dynamic
components tied to grid losses and investment needs, could minimize expansion costs
while ensuring fair cost allocation.
Overall, the study estimates significant system-wide savings: investment needs for low-
and medium-voltage grids until 2030 could be substantially reduced through flexibility ac-
tivation, with savings spanning from 27% to 80% of all planned investment, resulting in
up 2.7 billion EUR annually for Europe
These findings underscore the economic rationale for prioritizing demand-side flexibility
over traditional infrastructure expansion, aligning with Denmark’s energy transition goals
while minimizing costs to consumers and operators [2].

Flexibility: Concepts, Scope, and
Application
This chapter explores the concept of demand-side flexibility as a strategic solution to the
challenges faced by Denmark’s electricity distribution networks, with a specific focus on
Radius/Cerius. It examines the current state of flexibility implementation, defines key
concepts, and establishes the theoretical and practical framework for this research. The
discussion begins with foundational definitions, progresses to sources and characteristics,
distinguishes between explicit and implicit mechanisms, reviews Danish implementations,
and concludes with technical, economic, and regulatory justifications.
3.1
What is Demand-Side Flexibility
Demand-side flexibility refers to the ability of electricity consumers to deliberately modify
their consumption patterns in response to external signals, such as price incentives, direct
control mechanisms, or grid operation requirements. This encompasses both voluntary
responses to market signals and contracted flexibility services that can be dispatched by
system operators. At its core, demand-side flexibility transforms passive consumers into
active participants in grid management, enabling more efficient use of existing infrastruc-
ture.
The value proposition of demand-side flexibility is multifaceted. Economically, it can de-
fer or avoid costly grid reinforcements by optimizing existing infrastructure during peak
demand periods, leading to better long-term investment planning and synergies. Techni-
cally, it acts as a virtual grid asset, addressing voltage regulation, managing component
stress, and enhancing infrastructure utilization. Figure 3.1 provides an overview of the
general structure of demand-side flexibility, illustrating how various sources and mecha-
nisms interact to support grid operations.

3.1.1
Sources of Flexibility
Flexibility potential varies across customer segments and technologies, making it essen-
tial to identify and leverage diverse resources within the Cerius/Radius context. In the res-
idential sector, for instance, electric vehicles (EVs) offer significant opportunities through
smart charging strategies that shift consumption to off-peak hours or even enable vehicle-
to-grid services. Similarly, heat pumps equipped with thermal storage can pre-heat build-
ings during periods of low electricity prices, maintaining user comfort while reducing peak
loads.
The industrial sector provides another rich source of flexibility, where large consumers can
adjust process scheduling, utilize energy storage systems, or activate backup generation
to align with grid needs. This interest extends beyond traditional industries to major market
actors such as Google, whose power-intensive data centers benefit from demand-side
flexibility programs that enhance grid reliability, thereby ensuring scalability and secure
connections [10].
Finally, the infrastructure sector—including district heating systems, water treatment plants,
and pumping stations—benefits from inherent thermal or operational inertia, allowing for
load shifting without disrupting essential services. By tapping into these varied sources,
DSOs like Cerius/Radius can ensure a robust portfolio of flexible resources tailored to
local grid dynamics.

3.1.2
Technical Characteristics of Flexibility
The effectiveness of flexibility resources depends on several key parameters that deter-
mine their value and applicability in real-world scenarios. Response time, for example,
measures how quickly consumption can be modified, ranging from near-instantaneous
adjustments in industrial processes (on the order of seconds) to more gradual shifts in
thermal systems (spanning hours). Duration refers to how long the modified consumption
pattern can be sustained before a ”payback” effect occurs or normal operations must re-
sume, which is crucial for matching flexibility to the length of grid stress events. Capacity
quantifies the magnitude of power adjustment available, while energy constraints account
for the total shiftable energy over a given period, incorporating limitations from storage or
operational boundaries.
Additionally, price elasticity captures the sensitivity of demand to price changes, defined
as the percentage change in consumption per percentage change in price. These char-
acteristics collectively guide the selection, deployment, and integration of flexibility re-
sources, ensuring alignment with specific grid requirements such as peak shaving or volt-
age support.
3.2
Explicit and Implicit Flexibility
A key distinction in demand-side flexibility lies between explicit and implicit mechanisms,
each offering unique approaches to consumption modification with varying implementa-
tion challenges and benefits.
3.2.1
Explicit Flexibility
Explicit flexibility involves direct contractual arrangements where customers commit to
providing specific services in exchange for compensation. Distribution System Opera-
tors (DSOs) can dispatch these resources with high reliability, as customers agree to
constraints during grid stress periods. Compensation may include lower connection fees
or payments for outages. While this ensures operational certainty, it requires advanced
control systems and active customer participation.
3.2.2
Implicit Flexibility
Implicit flexibility uses price signals and tariffs to encourage behavior changes without
direct control. The reactions are purely based upon interaction with economic signals
voluntarily. There is no contractual obligations and it purely relies on market dynamics
rather than dispatch. This makes is scalable to larger groups with minimal customization.
3.3
Danish Flexibility Mechanisms for Distribution Systems
Denmark’s DSOs, including Cerius/Radius, are advancing flexibility solutions within a sup-
portive regulatory environment. These mechanisms blend explicit and implicit approaches
to address practical network constraints.
3.3.1
Limited Grid Access (Begrænset Netadgang - BNA)
BNA exemplifies explicit flexibility, offered by major DSOs like Radius, Cerius, N1, and
Konstant Net under Green Power Denmark’s framework. Customers connecting at 10 kV
or higher receive reduced fees in exchange for accepting dynamic capacity limits (0-100%
of nominal). DSOs enable remote disconnection and require communication interfaces.
Capacity is allocated proportionally in real-time with a warning. Customers pay only setup
costs but bear limitation risks, making BNA ideal for highly flexible operations [11].
3.3.2
Local Collective Tariff Structure
This implicit mechanism, based on the Capacity-Subscription Method, applies shared tar-
iffs to customer groups in defined zones. Costs are split between energy and capacity,

Market-Based Activation of Flexibility (MAF) Proposal

• Aggregator Integration: Bundling small-scale resources for scalability.
• Incentive Structures: Financial rewards balanced with operational needs.
Aligned with EU and Danish regulations, MAF reduces reinforcement needs while boost-
ing reliability and engagement.

Data-Supported Flexibility Assessment
This chapter focuses on evaluating how flexibility can be scaled in the Cerius/Radius
grid, analyzing substations and customers across dimensions like voltage stability and
consumption patterns.
4.1
Data Overview
The available data is directly retrieved from the Cerius/Radius databases through query-
ing. The Cerius/Radius distribution grid serves over 1.5 million customers via smart me-
ters that provide live measurements. It encompasses approximately 212 primary substa-
tions, more than 22,000 secondary substations, and a total cable length exceeding 44,000
km. All of this data will be used together to create a digital twin, which enables simulations
for demand response and voltage estimation at future time-steps.
• Consumption Data: Point-of-delivery (POD) level meter measurements for +1.5
mil customers, with indicators for production/consumption switching.
• SCADA data: Live measurements straight from components within the grid. Data
that can be retrieved is for transformers, radials and secondary substations with
switchgear.
• Grid data: Consist of topological correct data from SAP and GIS systems, includ-
ing features such as topology, transformer capacities, cable resistance, reactance,
capacitance and lengths.
• Voltage Data: True measurements from primary transformers, supplemented by
simulated values using power flow models based upon aggregated POD measure-
ments per substation. The data is validated up against true voltage measurements
from secondary substations with switch-gear.
• Spot-price data, Energinet’s Energi Data Service API for ”Elspot Prices” is used to
retrieve the spot price for the desired period [14].
The following sections provide a preliminary analysis of these data components, validating
their utility and application in modeling flexibility in distribution grids.

To help reason the potential for all the inelastic clients to shift over to an elastic consump-

Arbitrage here refers to temporal price arbitrage: Shifting consumption from high-price to

It is calculated by assuming that 20% of the consumption occurring during the highest 25%
of price periods can be shifted to the lowest 25% of price periods, with savings normalized
per MWh of total consumption.

5
Dynamic Tariff Design Framework
This chapter presents a comprehensive framework for developing dynamic tariffs that en-
hance demand-side flexibility within electrical distribution networks. The approach inte-
grates advanced predictive techniques to anticipate grid conditions and create tariff struc-
tures that promote consumption behaviors aligned with grid stability and operational effi-
ciency.
Modern distribution networks face unprecedented challenges due to the integration of
renewable energy sources, electric vehicle charging, and heat pump adoption. These
factors create volatile consumption patterns with abrupt shifts between distinct behavioral
regimes, such as net consumption and net production in prosumer-dominated areas. Tra-
ditional static tariff structures fail to provide adequate price signals for managing these
dynamic conditions, necessitating intelligent tariff design that responds to real-time grid
states.
5.1
Framework Overview
The proposed framework operates through a three-stage pipeline that transforms grid
topology and historical consumption data into optimized dynamic tariff signals:
Stage 1: Consumption Forecasting with price as an exogenous - A regime-sensitive
time series model predicts electricity consumption at both aggregate and localized levels,
using electricity prices as exogenous signals to capture demand response behaviors.
Stage 2: Voltage Prediction - A graph-based neural network forecasts voltage levels
across the distribution grid, leveraging both temporal dynamics and spatial interdepen-
dencies captured through the network topology.
Stage 3: Tariff Optimization- A multi-objective optimization process generates dynamic
price signals that balance grid stability, load management, and regulatory compliance
requirements.

and exogenous variables.
This model, referred to as the Markov-switching seasonal
autoregressive exogenous (MS-SARX) model, allows for endogenous identification of
regimes, capturing varying responses to price signals across different operational states.

5.1.2
Graph-based Voltage Prediction
To predict voltage levels under varying consumption patterns in the distribution grid, we
employ a spatio-temporal graph neural network that integrates graph attention mecha-
nisms with recurrent processing in an encoder-decoder framework. This model captures
spatial dependencies through multi-head graph attention layers (GAT) and temporal dy-
namics via gated recurrent unit (GRU), enabling accurate forecasting of voltage profiles
across the network topology. This approach adapts principles from spatio-temporal graph
neural networks, which have been widely used in physically similar domains like traffic
forecasting where spatial relations are defined by connectivity and temporal evolution by
dynamic patterns [24].
In power systems, similar architectures have demonstrated superior performance in ap-
plications such as voltage stability assessment and state estimation, leveraging the graph
structure of electrical networks to propagate information effectively [25, 26, 27].
Input Feature Engineering
The input features per node include scaled voltage (in volts), consumption (in kW), cycli-
cal encodings of hour, month (sine and cosine pairs for each), a binary weekday/weekend
indicator, and main station consumption measured directly on the transformers (in kW),
yielding F = 9 features. For multi-step forecasting over a horizon H = 24 hours, the

model processes historical sequences of length L (lookback hours) and incorporates fu-

Handling Partial Forecasts with Historical Baselines
In distribution grid forecasting, complete consumption projections may be unavailable for
all substations, particularly when focusing on critical nodes or facing computational lim-
its. For unforecasted nodes, we impute consumption with the previous 24 hour window.
Temporal features and main consumption are broadcast uniformly across all nodes. This
strategy maintains the full-graph structure required for graph neural processing while pro-
viding reliable priors, avoiding the need for complex imputation models that could intro-
duce additional errors.
This imputation aligns well with the model’s design: The encoder GRU treats imputed
sequences as stable temporal inputs, while the GAT layers propagate information from
known (forecasted) nodes to imputed ones via attention-weighted aggregation, effectively
smoothing uncertainties across the topology. This method is efficient, avoiding additional
modeling overhead, and has been validated in spatio-temporal forecasting tasks with in-
complete data. In traffic networks (analogous to power grids in graph structure), average-
based imputation combined with graph attention achieves significant error reductions on
datasets with missing values [29]. For power systems, similar imputation in GNN-based
predictors improves voltage forecast accuracy due to the localized nature of load varia-
tions [26].

5.2
Tariff Optimization
Building on the consumption forecasts from the MS-SARX model and the voltage predic-
tions from the ST-GNN, the tariff optimization step generates dynamic price signals that
incentivize demand-side flexibility while ensuring grid stability. This process integrates
the predictive capabilities of the previous stages to create tariff structures that reflect
anticipated grid conditions, promote load shifting among elastic customers, and minimize
violations of operational criteria such as voltage limits, reserve capacity constraints, and
component capacity limits.
The optimization draws inspiration from hierarchical control approaches in demand-side
management, where price signals serve as indirect controls to activate flexibility without
direct intervention in customer operations by exploiting the price elasticity of the cus-
tomers’ consumption [32]. The approach aligns with Danish regulatory requirements,
including revenue neutrality under the Danish Utility Regulator framework, by penalizing
significant deviations from baseline revenue [33].

7.1
Consumption Forecasting with Regime-Sensitive Time
Series Analysis
The Markov Switching Seasonal ARX (MS-SARX) model, as detailed in Chapter 4, has
been trained using historical price and consumption data for the specified primary or
secondary substation.
This model captures regime-switching dynamics in electricity
demand, where different regimes represent distinct consumption patterns influenced
by factors such as time of day, day of the week, and price fluctuations. Each regime
maintains its own set of SARX parameters, with transitions between regimes governed
by a Markov chain estimated via the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.
To ensure stable regimes, a transition regularization was introduced through:
Pregularized = 0.6 · I + 0.4 · P
where I is the identity matrix emphasizing persistence, and P is the empirical transition
matrix. This shrinkage approach biases the transitions toward greater regime stability,
reducing overfitting to transient patterns, whilst substantially enhancing forecasting
performance.
To optimize forecasting performance for the substation, a grid search was conducted over
the model hyperparameter. The search prioritized lower complexity to avoid overfitting,
information criteria such as AIC and BIC were used to select a modest values of na
and nb.
Limiting k to 3 regimes balances model expressiveness with interpretability,
preventing excessive complexity while allowing for key behavioral shifts (e.g., peak vs.
off-peak and low price vs. high price periods). This configuration can be extended in
future iterations if additional regimes prove beneficial.
The training and evaluation strategy employed a time series cross-validation approach
with an expanding window to respect the temporal nature of the data and simulate
real-world forecasting conditions.
Once optimal hyper-parameters were selected, the final model underwent evaluation
using a longer initial training window of 432 days (10,368 hours), followed by 108 test
evaluations. In each evaluation, the model was retrained on the expanding historical
data up to the current point, and forecasts were generated for the next 24 hours. This
expanding-window validation ensures robust assessment of out-of-sample performance
while incorporating progressively more data.

and residential consumer domains when evaluated using metrics such as mean absolute
error (MAE) and mean absolute percentage error (MAPE). This approach outperforms
simpler baselines, including naive persistence and historical averages, thereby providing
a robust reference for comparing forecasting models in grid management applications
[35].
7.2
Training the Graph-Based Voltage Prediction
The Spatial Temporal Graph Convolutional Neural Network (ST-GNN) model, as detailed
in Chapter 4, has been trained using historical voltage and consumption data from
secondary substations, along with main station consumption, to predict future voltage
profiles across the grid network.
To optimize the model’s performance, hyperparameters such as hidden dimensions
(ranging from 16 to 64) were tuned based on a grid-search, and a fixed learning rate
at 0.001, batch size of 128, with a lookback of 24 hours, prioritizing configurations that
minimize overfitting while capturing both short-term fluctuations and seasonal patterns.
The model complexity is balanced to handle the graph’s scale, with the number of nodes
determined by the sub-grids bus-structure.
The training and evaluation strategy utilizes a temporal cross-validation approach with
fixed validation and test splits to maintain chronological order and prevent data leakage.
The dataset, spanning historical hourly observations up to the day before the forecast
period, is split into training (70%), test (15%) and validation (15%), sets based on time
sequence.
For each training run, the model is fitted using an expanding window of
historical data, with predictions evaluated on held-out future periods.
Training employs Adam optimization with a ReduceLROnPlateau learning rate scheduler,
gradient accumulation for effective larger batches, and early stopping based on validation

deployment.

8.1.5
NPV Calculation for Case 1
Assuming that the annual cost to resolve this issue with a flexibility solution is about
150,000 DKK, based upon the loss of revenue from expected from the forecast window
presented above scaled to an annual sum. We operate with a horizon of five years,
where the upgrade cost is 10 million DKK, and we apply an interest rate of 5%. Thus, the
base is an upgrade investment now of 10 million DKK, i.e. NPVcase1,trad = −10 million
DKK.
The NPV is being calculated for two scenarios:
a The flexibility solves the reserve capacity constraint for the following five years
b After three years it is not sufficient with the flexibility scheme, and an upgrade is
needed.
For the flexibility only scenario (a), an NPV of:
NPVcase1(a) = NPVcase1,flex(a) −NPVcase1,trad = −0.65 + 10 = 9.35mil DKK
For the hybrid scenario(b), where the investment is deferred through flexibility
NPVcase1(b) = NPVcase1,flex(b) −NPVcase1,trad = −9.29 + 10 = 0.71mil DKK

8.2.5
NPV Calculation for Case 2
The compensation is estimated to be 11,000 DKK over a horizon of 5 years. The upgrade
cost is 0.75 mil DKK and an interest rate of 5%. Thus, the base case is an upgrade
investment now at 0.75 mil DKK, i.e. NPVcase2,trad = −0.75mil DKK.
The NPV is being calculated for two scenarios:
a The flexibility solves the capacity constraint for the following five years
b After three years it is not sufficient with the flexibility scheme, and an upgrade is
needed.
For the flexibility only scenario (a), an NPV of:
NPVcase2(a) = NPVcase2,flex(a) −NPVcase2,trad = −0.05 + 0.75 = 0.7mil DKK
For the hybrid scenario(b), where the investment is deferred through flexibility
NPVcase2(b) = NPVcase2,flex(b) −NPVcase2,trad = −0.70 + 0.75 = 0.05mil DKK
For this case, the NPV gains are not of significant size, it is in most cases easier and more
responsible to just perform the investment immediately. However, the results shouldn’t be
neglected either, they show a small business case that does justify it, but the solution has
to be short term, for it to be of any significant value.

9
Discussion of Findings and
Implications
The central problem motivating this thesis is ensuring that the rapid electrification
occurring as Denmark transitions to green and sustainable energy, does not compromise
grid reliability or capacity. While one solution involves installing buffer utilities, such as
batteries, in vulnerable grid locations, demand-side flexibility treats customers as virtual
buffers by providing monetary incentives to shift their consumption patterns, thereby
optimizing grid operations indirectly.
This chapter elaborates on the underlying assumptions and key insights derived from
developing a framework for demand-side flexibility in a real grid, grounded in actual
data. It reflects on the theoretical foundations, methodological approaches, empirical
findings, and practical implications of demand-side flexibility in Danish distribution
grids. By analyzing the interplay among data-driven assessments, advanced modeling
techniques, and real-world case applications, we evaluate the framework’s strengths,
limitations, and broader significance.
Although full case results are forthcoming, this
discussion draws on the established background, data analysis, model implementations,
and preliminary outcomes to offer a comprehensive reflection on demand-side flexibility
in the Cerius/Radius grid through localized and dynamic tariffs.
9.1
Key Findings
The achieved results demonstrate that demand-side flexibility represents a viable and
increasingly essential tool for managing the evolving challenges in distribution grids,
particularly in the context of Denmark’s aggressive electrification and integration of
renewables. A central finding is the clear distinction between explicit and implicit flexibility
mechanisms, as outlined in Chapter 3. Explicit flexibility, such as through Limited Grid
Access (BNA) or Market Access Flexibility (MAF), provides direct control and reliability
for DSOs like Cerius/Radius but requires contractual commitments and infrastructure
investments.
In contrast, implicit flexibility driven by economic signals like dynamic
tariffs offers scalability and minimal (if any) upfront costs, relying on voluntary consumer
responses to price incentives. This duality underscores the need for hybrid approaches
tailored to customer segments, with implicit mechanisms showing promising results for a
wider array of users, extending further than the customers able to comply with constraints
forced through explicit flexibility.
Data analysis in Chapter 4 reveals significant untapped flexibility potential within the
Cerius/Radius grid. Consumption patterns exhibit strong seasonal and daily variations,
with winter peaks increasing by up to 50% and constantly increasing making the data
highly non-stationary. Price elasticity assessments indicate that while many industrial
customers are inelastic, 5.5% are considered elastic, who take up 13% of all industrial
consumption. These findings validate the data’s utility for flexibility modeling, confirming
non-stationarities (e.g., growing electrification trends) that necessitate regime-sensitive
approaches over traditional static models.
The dynamic tariff design framework in Chapter 5 emerges as a key innovation, inte-
grating regime-sensitive forecasting (MS-SARX) with graph-based voltage prediction
Demand-Side Flexibility in Distribution Grids
69
==PAGE:78==
(ST-GNN) and multi-objective optimization. Preliminary model evaluations show that MS-
SARX outperforms baselines like XGBoost by capturing regime shifts in consumption,
achieving up to 28% better accuracy in out-of-sample forecasts for substations, where
the underlying joint variable space of consumption and price takes the shape of bi-modal
distribution. This is most often caused by having local electricity production, or variable
loads closely connected to the spot-price. In uni-modal distributions—typical of stable
residential loads without significant renewables—the performance gap narrows, with
both models yielding similar accuracy (e.g., RMSE differences <5%). This suggests that
MS-SARX provides significant value in complex non-linear and variable environments,
while a simpler and easier to implement model as XGBoost may suffice as a solution for
a less dynamic substation.
The ST-GNN’s integration of spatial and temporal dependencies achieved fine voltage
prediction, effectively mimicking load-flow calculations while requiring significantly less
computational overhead. The model’s ability to propagate information through the grid
topology via graph attention mechanisms proved essential for capturing voltage devia-
tions at end-of-line substations, where traditional time series models would fail to account
for network effects and spatial tendencies.
The general predictions are too smooth,
so for further refinement, one has to consider how to leverage the well-performing
attention mechanism, to create more radical and correct prediction that is not smooth
and mean-like.
However, something not showcased in this thesis, is its ability to do
these predictions for an entire grid at a time, throughout the cases, only the targeted
substations voltage profile was selected, but one could easily apply the model in a case
consisting of multiple substations.
Tariff optimization results further affirm the framework’s efficacy, with dynamic signals
yielding peak reductions of 9-15%+ and voltage lifts >5% in Cases 2 and 3, respectively.
NPV analyses indicate savings up to 47.68 million DKK, though constrained by data
non-stationarities and adoption risks.
Overall, these models enable proactive tariff structures that align consumer behavior with
grid needs, potentially deferring investments while maintaining neutrality under Danish
regulatory caps.
9.2
Effectiveness of Demand-Side Flexibility Framework in
Case Studies
The framework’s effectiveness is evident in its application to diverse grid contexts, as
defined in Chapter 6.
In Case 1, involving a large infrastructure facility facing reserve capacity constraints,
the framework demonstrated strong effectiveness through targeted flexibility activation.
The MS-SARX model captured binary consumption patterns with an MAE of 76.67
kWh at the secondary level, enabling accurate forecasting of price-sensitive shifts.
Coupled with ST-GNN voltage predictions, dynamic tariffs reduced peak loads by 90.3
kW, alleviating 48.9% of the primary substation’s reserve capacity exceedance.
This
resulted in substantial NPV savings of 9.35 million DKK under a flexibility-only scenario
or 0.71 million DKK for deferring the investment for 3 years, validating the approach for
implementing a local and dynamic tariff in the case of deferring a 10 million DKK primary
substation reinforcement.

Case 2, focusing on EV integration issues in a residential substation with coincident
charging peaks exceeding 200 kVA, highlighted the flexibility framework’s ability to miti-
gate component stress. The MS-SARX model effectively forecasted nocturnal patterns
with an RMSE of 19.48 kWh, outperforming XGBoost by 20% in capturing regime shifts.
ST-GNN voltage predictions, supported optimization, where dynamic tariffs achieved a
9% peak reduction (20 kWh), or 13% (31 kWh) with extended negative bounds. NPV
analysis showed savings of 0.7 million DKK in a flexibility-only approach, justifying
complete deferral of investment, however with the hybrid approach and reinforcement
after 3 years, the potential lowers to 0.05 million DKK, making it less appealing. These
results do not undermine the potential of demand-side flexibility at this residential level,
but instead showcases how it could be implemented to temporarily solve local issues or
postpone investments for later consideration and thus gain time.
In Case 3, addressing voltage regulation challenges in extended lines with drops exceed-
ing 5% (up to 8% below 10.4 kV) and reserve capacity shortages, the framework proved
effective in stabilizing end-of-line conditions. MS-SARX forecasting yielded an MAE of
14.82 kWh at the secondary level, capturing seasonal spikes from vacation homes and
medium-sized loads with regime-sensitive accuracy outperforming baselines by 15-20%.
Integrated with ST-GNN, which propagated spatial dependencies to predict voltages
with RMSE <0.1 kV across the 118-node sub-grid, optimization enabled consumption
shifts aggregating 150-200 kW (8-10% of primary load) from elastic small industry and
residential users. Dynamic tariffs, ranging from 0.1 to 1.5 DKK/kWh with selective nega-
tive bounds (-0.1 DKK/kWh during surplus), lifted voltages by >5% (reducing deviations
to <3% below nominal) and mitigated reserve exceedance in 1.85% of hours through
targeted off-peak incentives. Economic analysis shows savings of 47.68 million DKK in a
flexibility-only approach, avoiding the 50 million DKK reinforcement entirely by leveraging
aggregated elasticity. Even with a hybrid or deferred reinforcement after 10 years, the
potential is still showing an attractive NPV of 13.84 million DKK, though limitations in
rural data granularity and lower elasticity density between customers suggest the need
for aggregators to enhance scalability.
Across the cases, flexibility activation on elastic customers showcase the potential,
with Case 1 demonstrating a 90.3 kW shift from a highly elastic facility (6% of primary
load), Case 2 achieving 9-13% peak reductions through EV charging adjustments,
and Case 3 aggregating small industry and residential elasticity to improve voltage by
mitigating 1.85% hourly violations, highlighting the framework’s excellence in utilizing
price-sensitive behaviors for grid optimization.
The expected cost savings from implementing the flexibility framework are substantial,
as evidenced by the NPV analyses across the cases. For instance, in scenarios where
flexibility defers reinforcements, savings range from 0.05 to 47.68 mil.
DKK. Where
the investments are, for now, only deferred, the savings are expected to go from 0.05
to 13.84 mil. DKK, assuming different investment horizons and an interest rate of 5%.
These numbers underscore the framework’s potential to optimize capital allocation, and
maybe just as important, allowing the DSOs to gain time by stretching the implementing
of the transition over a longer period of time, and in some cases, eradicating any need
for reinforcements in the grid.

9.3
Impact on Grid Stability and Investment Planning
The economic and technical justification for DSO-led flexibility is robust, offering a cost-
effective alternative to immediate reinforcements. By deferring investments, vulnerable
areas can mature, enabling larger, more impactful projects—such as comprehensive
primary substation enhancements—that improve return on investment while maintaining
grid resilience during transition periods.
Regarding tariff design, the framework allows for a broad range of price signals, including
negative tariffs to further incentivize consumption during surplus periods, though this
raises regulatory and economic consideration.
Under the Danish Utility Regulator
guidelines and EU directives (e.g., Electricity Directive 2019/944), tariffs must remain
cost-reflective and non-discriminatory [36]. With Market Model 3.0 approved, DSOs may
procure flexibility services to mitigate local bottlenecks, provided they are cost-effective,
reduce investment needs, and enhance grid reliability [13]. Negative tariffs align with this
by implicitly compensating customers for cooperation and providing a service by changing
consumption patterns. Expanding on this, the framework’s tariff range includes tariffs
down to -0.2 DKK/kWh during periods of surplus renewable generation, which incentivize
increased consumption. To mitigate potential customer concerns over increased spot
price exposure during such shifts, an additional compensation mechanism funded from
operational expenditures, will be ensuring revenue neutrality while implicitly subsidizing
participation. However, this introduces risks, such as over-reliance on OPEX budgets
that could strain DSO finances if adoption exceeds projections, or unintended rebound
effects from aggressive negative pricing.
Effective communication via public channels could further encourage consumers to adapt
consumption patterns beyond current expectations and thereby amplify the benefits even
further.
While EV charging currently represents 3-5% of Denmark’s daily electricity consumption
in 2025 about 1.5-1.7 TWh annually from a fleet of 400,000-450,000 battery electric
vehicles averaging 3,800 kWh each this is projected to rise to around 10% by 2030 as
the fleet exceeds 1 million under national electrification targets [1]. Major operators like
Clever, controlling one-third of the national network, could serve as aggregators respond-
ing to dynamic tariffs from DSOs such as Cerius/Radius. These tariffs target specific
constraints, benefiting both parties: DSOs achieve load balancing, while aggregators
gain optimized pricing. A modest 10% shift in EV loads achievable via automated smart

9.4
Limitations
Some limitations temper this thesis’ findings.
Data quality and availability pose chal-
lenges: Smart meter readings from Cerius/Radius, while comprehensive (over 1.5 million
customers), exhibit non-stationarities and missing values, potentially biasing data. Fur-
thermore, the sparse pre-processing approach to data in this study highlights additional
considerations. The decision to maintain relatively raw pre-processing of sparse data in
this study was deliberate, aiming to replicate real-world operational scenarios faced by
distribution system operators like Cerius/Radius, where data from SCADA systems and
smart meters often arrive incomplete or noisy without immediate cleansing capabilities.
This approach preserved authenticity in model training, allowing for direct assessment
of robustness against non-stationarities and missing values, as encountered in practical
deployments. However, it introduces potential inaccuracies, such as biased elasticity
estimates from unhandled outliers or imputation errors in graph-based predictions, which
did amplify forecast deviations leading to high MAPE for a lot of the AR-modeling on the
primary substation. Critically, this underscores the need for balanced strategies in future
implementations, where minimal intervention ensures deployability but may necessitate
supplementary techniques like robust imputation (e.g., graph-based interpolation) to
mitigate risks without deviating from operational realism.
Methodologically, the MS-SARX and ST-GNN models, though advanced, rely on
hyperparameter tuned to specific cases, limiting the possibility for generalizing.
The
EM algorithm for regime estimation assumes Markovian transitions, which may not
capture long-term trends like climate change impacts on loads. Optimization assumes
perfect consumer response, ignoring participation risks or gaming behaviors. Regulatory
constraints, such as tariff neutrality, could cap incentive strength, while computational
demands (e.g., graph processing for large grids) is challenging real-time deployment.
Customer participation risks pose significant challenges, particularly low adoption among
inelastic segments (94.5% of industrial customers, as per Figure 4.6), where manufac-

Conclusion
This thesis has investigated the implementation of dynamic tariffs to activate demand-side
flexibility (DSF) in Danish distribution grids, with a focus on optimizing grid utilization amid
rising electrification and renewable integration. The central research question addressed
is: How can dynamic and localized modeling of electricity consumption optimize grid
utilization in Danish distribution networks?
To answer this, four sub-questions were
examined:
1. Quantifying flexibility potential in large customers for temporal load shifting.
2. Mobilizing flexibility to generate insights into constraints within critical segments us-
ing meter data.
3. Enhancing capacity forecasting and flexibility assessment to evaluate solution via-
bility.
4. Identifying requirements for a market-based flexibility pool, including essential tools
and data for grid planning.
The primary objective was to develop and implement a framework enabling DSF
through price signals, encompassing multi-layered flexibility assessment (voltage quality,
consumption patterns, substation loading and customer segmentation), forecasting
of customer behavior under varying prices, and optimization via dynamic tariffs with
graph-based voltage predictions. The scope was limited to Cerius/Radius operated grids
at the 10 kV level, evaluated through three case studies: A stressed infrastructure facility,
a rural voltage-constrained area with seasonal patterns, and a residential zone with high
electrification. Success was measured against metrics such as net present value (NPV)
savings exceeding 10% for investment deferral, voltage stability improvements over 5%
in simulations, and peak load reductions above 15%.
The framework demonstrated substantial potential for optimizing grid operations through
DSF. In the flexibility assessment, analysis of 150,000 industrial customers revealed that
94.5% exhibited inelastic demand, with only 0.9% highly elastic (elasticity coefficient ≤
-0.25) and 4.6% moderately elastic (-0.25 to -0.1). However, elastic customers accounted
for 12.9% of total consumption, indicating significant arbitrage opportunities (up to 140
DKK/MWh) for shifting loads, this could be a stepping stone for inelastic customer to
transition into elastic behavior
Economic benefits were quantified through NPV comparisons, assuming a 5% discount
rate and 10-year horizon. Flexibility-only scenarios yielded savings of 9.35 million DKK
(Case 1), 0.70 million DKK (Case 2), and 47.68 million DKK (Case 3) relative to traditional
reinforcements (10 million DKK, 0.75 million DKK, and 50 million DKK, respectively).
Hybrid scenarios (deferred investments) provided 0.71 million DKK, 0.05 million DKK,
and 13.84 million DKK, respectively, highlighting deferral potential. Thus the cases show
significant economic potential of DSF vis-a-vis the full scale expansion of the grid with
surplus capacity.
The findings underscore DSF’s role in supporting Denmark’s green transition, enabling
DSOs like Cerius/Radius to manage electrification pressures cost-effectively. This aligns
with regulatory frameworks like Market Model 3.0 and Tariff Model 3.0, promoting cost-
reflective dynamic pricing and multi-purpose flexibility.

Benchmark Model: XGBoost
Forecasting Methodology
This appendix details the methodology employed in the benchmark model for multi-step
forecasting of substation energy consumption using the XGBoost algorithm.
The ap-
proach serves as a comparative baseline for evaluating the performance of the primary
MS-SARX model developed in this thesis (as detailed in Chapter 7). The methodology is
structured to ensure reproducibility and transparency, focusing on data preparation, model
training, and comprehensive evaluation, while aligning with the time-series-specific strate-
gies used for MS-SARX (e.g., expanding-window retraining to handle non-stationarity and
regime shifts).
C.1
Data Preparation
The dataset spans October 2023 to April 2025 ( 540 days or 12,960 hourly observations),
matching the period used for MS-SARX training.
To address non-stationarity (e.g.,
seasonal trends and regime-like shifts in consumption), preprocessing includes:
(1)
Yeo-Johnson transformation for variance stabilization and normality approximation; (2)
automatic differencing (regular order d up to 2, seasonal order D up to 2 with period
s=24) based on ADF and KPSS tests to ensure stationarity, similar to MS-SARX.
The data is initially partitioned with an expanding-window approach: the first 432 days
( 80% of the data) form the initial training window, with the remaining 108 days used for
rolling evaluation (matching MS-SARX’s 108 test evaluations).
To facilitate multi-step forecasting, a sliding window approach is utilized:
for each
sample, a lookback period of 24 hours is employed to construct input features, while
the subsequent 24 hours form the prediction horizon for the target variable (substation
consumption). This results in input features derived from historical values of selected
co-variates (e.g., electricity price) and the target, flattened into a single feature vector
per sample. The output for each sample is a vector of 24 future consumption values
(inverse-transformed to original scale post-forecasting).
This preparation yields multiple evaluation windows, each representing a distinct 24-hour
forecast period, enabling robust assessment across various temporal segments while re-
specting chronological order to prevent data leakage.
C.2
Model Architecture and Training
The forecasting task is addressed using XGBoost, a gradient boosting framework
renowned for its efficiency and effectiveness in regression problems. Given the multi-
output nature of the prediction (24 horizons), a multi-output regressor wrapper is applied
around a base XGBoost regressor.
The base model is configured with the following hyperparameter:
• Objective function: Squared error minimization.
• Learning rate: 0.1.
• Number of boosting rounds: 100.
• Maximum tree depth: 6.
• Subsample ratio: 0.8 (fraction of samples used per tree).
• Column subsample ratio: 0.8 (fraction of features used per tree).
84
Demand-Side Flexibility in Distribution Grids
==PAGE:93==
• Random seed: 42 for reproducibility.
Training follows an expanding-window strategy to match MS-SARX: The model is initially
trained on the first 432 days. For each of the 108 evaluation days, the model is retrained
on the expanding historical data up to the day before the forecast, then used to predict the
next 24 hours. This simulates real-world deployment, incorporates all available history,
and handles evolving patterns (e.g., increasing winter consumption). The model learns
to map the flattened historical feature windows to the multi-horizon output vectors.
C.3
Evaluation Metrics
Model performance is quantified using same metrics applied to the MS-SARX evaluations:
• Overall Metrics: Aggregated across all predictions and evaluation windows.
• Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE): Measures the standard deviation of residuals.
• Mean Absolute Error (MAE): Captures the average magnitude of errors.
• Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE): Expresses errors as a percentage of actual
values, with safeguards against division by zero.
• Coefficient of Determination (R2): Indicates the proportion of variance explained by the
