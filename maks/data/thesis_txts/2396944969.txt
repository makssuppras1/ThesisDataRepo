Executive Summary 
 
Mariners operate in the most extreme and harsh environmental circumstances humankind can 
get in touch with, which results in extremely hazardous working conditions. Crew members are 
exposed to both Repeated Shocks (RS) as well as Whole Body Vibrations (WBV), which can cause 
both short- and long-term adverse physical health effects. These conditions have been shown to 
be correlated with a decreased working lifespan of crew members, with high medical costs as a 
derivative and to cause material fatigue of the structure of the craft eventually. 
 
The market share for High Speed Crafts (HSC) increases year by year, but the current 
development has mainly focused on improving the operational capabilities of the craft rather 
than concentrating on the safety aspect of crew members and passengers. Since the human 
factor is considered to be a high-cost investment, there exists a gap in the market for an 
investment decision evaluation method based on analysis how alternative craft seakeeping 
characteristics affect occupants at sea. There have been numerous research studies performed 
on suitable methods to assess seaworthiness, but there are limited resources on what the most 
suitable tool to evaluate HSCs seaworthiness is based on predetermined operational 
requirements. 
 
All relevant factors that occupants are exposed to, and their effects, along with assessment 
methods, were studied with a structured literature review. The findings from the review, as well 
as quantitative and qualitative data, were analyzed to create and present a potential investment 
decision-making framework. This research paper was carried out as an exploratory case study to 
use real-world data to confirm the applicability of the framework. 
 
The suggested framework presented in the paper is intended to assist HSC investors to select and 
decide, based on necessary input and personal requirements, which craft among the portfolio of 
alternatives might fit their needs optimally. The results are presented as a ratio, between crafts, 
of potential severe shock that occupants are exposed to during a sea trip, or in less intense 
working conditions, the lifespan of the occupants is estimated. The framework is designed to take 
into consideration operational weather circumstances the customer operates in, either for 
professional or private reasons. Simulation is part of the framework to generate and iterate the 
optimal solution and to decrease the uncertainty of the final decision. It is taken into account 
that occupants of the crafts can either be seated or standing during a seatrip. Therefore, the 
relevant method of assessment for potential risk effects they might be exposed to is chosen.  
 
The framework offers a wide range of assessment methods to evaluate seaworthiness based on 
input requirements, including its applicability to a wide variety of circumstances. The proposed 
framework is suitable for HSC investors/customers or others wanting to analyze such crafts. 
Simulation allows assessors to implement a range of variability to uncertainty factors for further 
establishment of alternative crafts. Furthermore, the framework application conducted with a 
case study showed a significant difference, of the tested crafts, of exposed hazardous wave 
shocks. The overall difference indicated a 78% - 89% variance in shock impact.

Abstract 
 
Purpose – Mariners operate in the most extreme weather conditions humans can encounter, due 
to constant vibration and repeated wave collision which can be as drastic as a car collision. Craft 
owners need to take into consideration how the motion of the sea affects their crew members. 
Therefore, the purpose of this paper is to develop an investment decision tool to allow customers 
interested in buying crafts to establish the seakeeping behaviour of alternative vessels, in 
correlation with its effects on occupants.  
 
Design/Methodology/Approach – The following research was accomplished through structured 
literature research, and the case study was conducted as an exploratory case study. The emphasis 
on literature research was to identify useful and applicable tools and methods to assess a craft’s 
seakeeping behaviour and its connection to risks on onboard occupants, with the focus on 
investment decision-making. The framework’s functionality was validated with a real-world case 
and further with an in-house workshop. 
 
Findings – The paper presents a decision making framework that allows HSC clients to select the 
most suitable option of vessels under research, based on the amount of hazardous exposure to 
potential injury related scenarios as a result of operational circumstances, with uncertainty 
factors taken into consideration. 
 
Research limitations/implications – The proposed framework is suitable for any firm 
investigating investment options with a focus on the human performance of an HSC. Limitations 
apply to the framework, since it does not indicate all possible dangers. Further, it requires 
detailed personal based input variables. 
 
Practical implications – The presented information of seakeeping assessment tools, injury 
assessment tools, human performance factors, uncertainties and evaluation methods form the 
developed investment framework for practical decision-making. Practitioners can apply the 
framework to their specific operational requirements and determine how the circumstances 
affect their crew members and passengers. 
 
Originality/value – There is a significant amount of data available that discusses the topic of this 
thesis, i.e. has the aim of presenting seakeeping and injury assessment methods. However, 
according to literature research, no paper exists that proposes an investment decision-making 
framework to analyze the behaviour of a craft and takes into account human performance factors 
and the variation of uncertainty factors. Therefore, this paper aims at filling the existing research 
gap by combining seakeeping and injury assessment tools, human performance factors and 
uncertainty simulation with a novel framework composed of theory.  
 
Keywords: High Speed Craft, Injury Assessment (Risk Assessment), Discomfort Assessment, Whole 
Body Vibration, Repeated Shock, Motion Induced Fatigue, Motion Induced Interruptions.

1 Introduction 
 
In today's environment, an operation of a HSC has become more widespread, especially in 
Europe, which has led to the rise of new safety issues to protect crew members and passengers 
(Antao and Guedes Soares, 2008), by improving seakeeping ability. On the one hand, occupants’ 
safety has been very well integrated into the automotive and aircraft industry, while on the other 
hand, it has lacked in some fields of the fast craft industry, as in HSC (Dobbins et al., 2015). High-
speed marine crafts are typically 6-15 meters in length and are capable of speed up to about and 
above 30-40 knots/hour (Dobbins et al., 2008). HSC are usually operated in rough weather 
conditions, which is arguably one of the harshest working environments humans can encounter 
(Dobbins et al., 2010). Marines and members of the Coast Guards have to train in similar 
conditions as real-life scenarios may occur in, or "train as you fight” (Peterson et al., 2004). 
Occupants of these crafts are exposed to an extremely uncomfortable and non-linear motion, 
that causes a high level of fatigue, both physically and mentally, as well as chronic and acute 
injuries in the long run (not exhaustive: Martire et al., 2017; Myers et al., 2011; Peterson et al., 
2004; Townsend et al., 2012). Fatigue can appear in both physical and mental states, as in feelings 
of tiredness, physical discomfort or chronic and acute musculoskeletal injuries as a reflection of 
exposed prolonged activity (Leung et al., 2006; Peterson et al., 2004). The evolution of safety and 
comfort for crew members and passengers has not kept up with the innovation of HSC speed and 
capabilities, and therefore it has become a higher priority to focus on the protection and comfort 
of onboard members due to the long-term effects of Repeated Shock (RS) or transient mechanical 
shocks, and Whole Body Vibration (WBV) or low frequency environmental vibration (not 
exhaustive: de Alwis, 2014; Dobbins et al., 2008; Dobbins et al., 2010; Peterson et al., 2004). Being 
exposed to RS and WBV for an extended period of time appears in lack of physical capabilities, 
such as neck and back pain and even a damaged musculoskeletal system (not exhaustive: 
Department of Defense, 2013; Dobbins et al., 2010; Martire et al., 2017; Myers et al., 2011). 
 
The EU Physical Agents Directive aims to limit exposure to WBV, which will lead to a revolutionary 
change in design and operations of HSC, since WBV daily exposure limit can be reached in minutes 
because of the contemporary craft's design (Dobbins et al., 2008). According to the Department 
of Defense (2013), "medical health care and military operational costs of lower back pain (LBP) 
are enormous. Interventions that could reduce the prevalence and recurrence of LBP could be 
extremely cost-effective and should be a high priority for military research” (p.4). In a survey that 
the U.S. Naval Health Research Center conducted, for the U.S. Special Operation craft crewmen, 
62% were hospitalized due to one or more injuries in a craft operation (Bass et al., 2008; Peterson 
et al., 2004). Crew members and passengers are exposed to repeated slamming, i.e., rough wave 
slam, which is as dangerous and severe as craft collision (Dobbins et al., 2015). The most common 
reported injuries are in the lower back, knee, shoulder, neck, upper back, and ankle, along with 
severe trauma, vertebral fractures, and intervertebral disc injuries (Bass et al., 2008; Terai, 2016). 
Therefore, interventions that could lead to fewer vibrations and slamming impacts, RS and WBV, 
will improve operational performance, as well as reduce the growing, long-term medical and 
disability expenses of occupants who operate in such environments (Department of Defense, 
2013). Hence, establishing how beneficial an HSC design with improved seakeeping capabilities

is as a better investment, compared to currently operated crafts that have not yet been analyzed 
with a focus on the Life Cycle Cost (LCC) from the customer’s perspective. 
 
Consequently, this paper aims to create a framework of tools that allows investors (operating 
customers) to compare alternative crafts, based on potential risk-effective exposures directly 
connected to seakeeping capacity. Uncertainty Factors (UFs) will be introduced and included in 
the framework to demonstrate real-life scenarios as accurately as possible, and for investors to 
optimize their potential return on investment. Hence, a case study will be conducted to apply the 
developed framework, to test the applicability in reality. The study is conducted in co-operation 
with a company which operates, designs and manufactures HSCs. The motivation to analyze this 
industry is based on how undeveloped HSCs are when it comes to safety requirements, due to 
many small players in the market which operate on a limited budget (Dobbins et al., 2008). 
Therefore, a research gap exists to analyze alternative craft vessels for craft operators’ risk-
effectiveness based on seakeeping capabilities. Hence, this paper addresses the following two 
research questions: 
 
RQ1: What correlation exists between seakeeping characteristics and operational efficiency with 
regards to a high-speed marine craft crew member? 
 
RQ2: How does the correlation between expected operational efficiency and seakeeping 
characteristics of high-speed marine crafts impact investment decisions? 
 
This paper is structured as follows: Chapter 2 introduces the outline of the thesis and research 
methodology. Chapter 3 presents the findings from literature review, limitations to seakeeping 
and injury assessments, the human factors related to seaway exposure, uncertainty factors and 
if a sensitivity analysis and/or if Monte Carlo simulation should be conducted. Chapter 4 discusses 
the framework input values and a reflection on the case study findings. Chapter 5 validates the 
applicability of the developed framework. Chapter 6 presents implications on theory and 
practice, and lastly, chapter 7 consists of conclusion as well as the limitations of the proposed 
framework and ideas for further development.

A/S. Shipper A/S, has the vision to redefine the accepted fundamentals of seakeeping abilities of 
HSCs which face the most extreme and harsh environment mariners can experience (Dobbins et

2.3 Structured literature review 
 
The literature review documentation for this paper was carried out in two stages to identify a 
point of departure, an exploratory literature review and a refined literature review, which are 
further categorized into four steps. The exploratory literature review consists of the first two 
steps, (1) initial- and (2) exploratory literature review. The drive for the initial literature review is 
the interest and curiosity about the topic, used to seek resources that lead to an unexplored gap 
in the market, to form a potential research question(s). This step was used to get an overview of 
the HSC market, understanding how it works and how it is operated. The exploratory literature 
review is used to dig deeper into the topic and get an overview of the terminologies (such as 
drivers, limitations, purpose, etc.) within the topic area. The primary human factor performance 
drivers were discovered, along with identifying methods used to illustrate seakeeping 
capabilities. The refined literature review consists of the latter two steps, (3) focused- and (4) 
refined literature review. For the focused literature review the aim was to form a refined research 
question(s), based on analysis of the terminologies. The last step, refined literature review, 
focuses on finalizing the research question(s), through further analysis built on the prior level. 
The guidelines followed in the literature review are built on a model developed by Kathleen 
Liston, known as Point of Departure (POD) (Liston, 2006). Table 2, presents the results of the 
literature research and displays the primary keyword search term as well as the secondary 
keyword. 
 
The primary search tools employed were DTUfindit, the DTU Library database, Google scholar 
online free search engine, and the case company‘s database for more detailed literature 
throughout the process.

2.4 Data Collection 
 
The data has been compiled over a five-month period, to analyze and develop the aim of the case 
study along the way and to ensure that the gathered information is reliable. The data was 
collected through both internal and external meetings, documents, informal conversations, e-
mails, measurements and further data processing, to establish and evolve a more holistic 
framework to be applicable for a wider range of HSC investors.  
 
The qualitative data gathering can be classified into three categories; open, open-ended and 
close-ended, where they are considered to be unstructured, semi-structured and structured, 
respectively (Flick, 2009). When the meetings were held, they were constructed according to one 
of the previously mentioned categories with a specific purpose in mind. At first, the meetings 
aimed to get a broad overview of the company’s portfolio and their current vision and mission 
based on their products and to understand where and how the research topic could become 
useful. Once the core understanding had been established, more structured (semi-structured) 
interviews were held to dig deeper into the matter of interest, but with an open mind to 
encourage derivative discussions, and to get an idea of the case company vision on the topic. 
Furthermore, to confirm certain predetermined assumptions and uncertainties, structured 
interviews were carried out to clarify those factors, along with the structure of the framework 
and the included methods.

National Rescue Team (NRT), for confidentiality). The quantitative data was gathered, based on 
collected qualitative data, in order to answer the proposed research questions and to validate if 
there is a correlation between seakeeping, occupants safety and investment decision.

2.4.1 Data Analysis 
 
As can be seen in Table 3 the qualitative data was aggregated as notes during meetings. 
Afterwards the notes were analyzed and the useful data was subtracted and further investigated. 
The collected notes were used to find symmetry between prior meetings and literature research. 
If no balance was established with prior work, the notes were used as a source to find reliable 
references through further literature research. 
The quantitative data was collected and analyzed based on the relevant input needed to perform 
the framework‘s steps. The operational data had to be inspected to identify each mission that 
took place at sea and coordinated to the weather condition for the specific date. The measured 
data, performed by the author, needed some work to deduct the relevant data since it was set 
up to create input for the framework workability.  
 
2.4.2 Data Validation 
 
It is highly essential to make sure that the gathered data through the qualitative collection is 
credible and reliable in harmony with the literature to carry out the research (Flick, 2009). Most 
of the interviewees within the case company, all seemed to keep the company‘s vision in mind 
when the framework‘s development took place, and therefore the validity of the provided 
information needed to be confirmed by external partners. The author sought meetings with 
external unbiased sources, both professors within the academic field of relevance, as well as 
professionals that welcome highly reliable results for their university or company‘s benefit. The 
professors who donated their knowledge for research purposes came from two different

This chapter summarizes tools and theories used to identify how a particular HSC operates. 
Subchapter 3.1 introduces the tools used to assess the seakeeping characteristics of a particular

Subchapter 3.2 describes tools used to evaluate hazardous conditions, conditions that can cause

3.1 Seakeeping Assessment Tools 
 
Comfort and reliability are essential targets for the seaworthiness of a vessel, as well as to obtain 
passenger satisfaction (Nahvi et al., 2006; Payne, 2005), due to human factors that can cause 
decreased workload performance (further discussion in subchapter 3.3). The foundation of 
factors affecting the operability of a seaway is threefold; motion characteristics of the vessel, the 
uncontrollable and unpredictable sea behaviour (weather, wind, and current), and the 
operational requirements (skills of the crewmen) (McCreight and Stahl, 1985; www.marin.nl, 
2017). Seakeeping assessment needs to take into consideration these factors, to identify the 
behaviour of a vessel at sea. In the hydrodynamic field, a floating object is exposed to six motional 
degrees of freedom (x-, y-, z-axes and rotational degrees for each axis, rx, ry, and rz) (Janson, 2014), 
which increases the complexity of recognizing a craft‘s behaviour. Therefore, the naval architect 
community needs to apply various criteria when assessing the HSC seakeeping qualities through 
motion and acceleration data, which is either obtained with seakeeping simulations, tow tank 
tests, or full-scale prototype sea trial (Gollwitzer & Peterson, 1995; Peterson, 2004).  
 
According to the author of this paper, seakeeping assessment tools can be classified into two 
categories, Discomfort Assessment (DA) and Injury Assessment (IA) (subchapter 3.2). DA, takes 
into consideration WBV over a period, to establish if the exposure is beyond comfortable limits. 
IA focuses more on the RSs caused by wave collisions, and the potential severe physical effects 
of such shocks over a certain period. As put forward by the EU Physical Agents Directive, the 
limiting exposure to WBV for an uncomfortable level is 0,5m/s2, and for a hazardous level 1,15 
m/s2 and above (according to an 8 hour work day) (Pelmear & Leong, 2002). Table 4 summarizes 
the most common HSC seakeeping assessment tools, used by naval architects, and the limitations 
of the tools are presented to determine their situational applicability.

3.2 Injury Assessment Tools 
 
In the marine industry, there are many forms of hull shape, each one depending on the 
operational purpose of the boat or a ship. Each hull has its seakeeping characteristic, ranging 
from good to inadequate capabilities. The human body is an extremely complex system, and the 
capabilities and functionality differ greatly between individuals, and therefore the human factor 
plays a big role in identifying the hazardous conditions it is exposed to (Dobzhansky et al., 1972). 
The seakeeping data measured for a specific boat/ship can be used to perform an IA (Gollwitzer 
& Peterson, 1995). Risk Assessment (RA) tools can be classified into two categories; assessment  
tools that can predict severe discomfort (Figure 1), which can further be subcategorised into 
Displacement-Response Methods (DRM) and Acceleration-Duration Methods (ADM), and then 
Moderated discomfort and performance loss, which covers International Standards (Gollwitzer 
& Peterson, 1995; Marrison et al., 1998). The two former categories are built on limited data. 
These tools can predict whether or not the exposure to shock and vibration are hazardous or 
within injury boundaries (Bass et al., 2008), based on an abrupt change in acceleration that 
results as an impact (Glaister, 1975). As described by Gollwitzer and Peterson (1995) DRM use an 
established human biodynamics model to predict the maximum displacement of the central 
predicted or measured seat kinematics time history, and the resulting maximum displacement is 
correlated with the probability of injury and that ADM are based on waveform analysis of an 
individual acceleration time history, where the incidence of injury or effect on performance is 
correlated with acceleration waveform characteristics. Therefore, it is highly critical to identify 
the duration of the acceleration, because the longer the force, the greater the effects (Glaister, 
1975). DRM tools are usually used for seated occupants, where their spinal displacement is 
measured according to the craft‘s behaviour, while ADM tools are used for standing occupants 
(Gollwitzer & Peterson, 1995).

For both DSM and ADM methods, the human body is thought of as a simple mass-spring system, 
which has specific impact limits before potential injury occurs (Brinkley, 1984; Glaister, 1975). 
The duration, magnitude, and axis of exposure impact the severity level of injury significantly 
with short-term and transient accelerations on humans (Kumar and Norfleet, 1992). Glaister 
(1975), also discusses that the acceleration impact can be defined with high accuracy, but the 
effect of forces acting on the body internally is often a matter of guesswork. Table 5 summarizes

3.3 Human Performance Factors 
 
Vibration exposure mainly depends on a complex combination of interactions to motion 
direction, duration, frequency, magnitude, biodynamic, psychological, physiological, pathological 
and intra- and inter-subject changeability (Townsend, 2012). Drivers, aviation employees, sailors 
and other workers in unstable environments are constantly affected by their surroundings in the 
form of an interruption in their daily work routine. These interruptions can create a demanding 
working environment, and impact safety. During sailing, there are several factors affecting crew 
members and passengers, which all fall under one category, Shock and Vibration (S&V), and are 
classified as performance parameters for seakeeping ability (Pattison & Sheridan, 2004). These 
factors are the following: Repeated Shock (RS), Whole Body Vibration (WBV), with the 
subcategories, Situational Awareness (SA), Motion Induced Fatigue (MIF), Motion Induced 
Interruptions (MII) and Motion Sickness Interruptions (MSI) (not exhaustive: Dobbins et al., 2010; 
Dobbins et al., 2008; Janson, 2014; Pattison & Sheridan, 2004). Antao (2008) states that 80% of 
all ship accidents are contributed to human factors, while only 20% are related to equipment and 
other factors.  
==PAGE:23==
14 
 
 
Short-term exposure to both RS (severe slamming) and WBV have shown to result in fatigue, and 
long-term exposure increases the risk of musculoskeletal injuries (Dobbins et al., 2008; Dobbins 
et al., 2010). Shocks are typically large impulsive forces that act over a short period and at high 
amplitude, and vibration is considered periodic (Gollwitzer & Peterson, 1995). Reported injuries 
or physical states from these factors are, e.g., annoyance, sleepiness, discomfort, anxiety, 
nausea, loss of visual and hand-eye coordination, broken ankles and legs, damaged vertebrae 
and on internal organs (list not exhaustive) (Townsend, 2012). Those causes demand HSC builders 
to implement human performance criteria in the design phase to gain a competitive advantage 
for future investment decision making. 
 
SA determines the applicability to be alert and for crew members and passengers to maintain 
enough awareness of system status to track the development of events occurring in the 
surroundings as it gradually unfolds (Stanton et al., 2001). The enhanced speed capacity of small 
HSC has reduced SA due to increased exposure to RS and WBV as a result of improvements in 
propulsion capabilities (Dobbins et al., 2008). Therefore, SA needs to be improved, through 
seaworthiness, to avoid hazardous situations that could occur unexpectedly and potentially harm 
occupants.  
 
MIF reduces occupants’ physical and cognitive performance, due to repeated impacts which are 
caused by an unstable surface (Townsend et al., 2012). Fatigue influences the amount of energy 
needed to perform a certain task. The amount of effort needed is usually measured through 
oxygen intake (Pattison & Sheridan, 2004).  
 
MII, or loss-of-balance, is the definition of how comfortable it is to move around the vessel during 
transportation (Dobbins et al., 2008; Janson, 2004). MII incidence varies considerably and is 
highly dependent on location on the ship, where the least effect is deep in the hull and aft of 
amidship (Graham, 1990). This performance factor is measured in interruptions per minute, and 
according to Janson (2004), one disruption per minute is acceptable for crew members but 
slightly less for passengers, or one intrusion per every two minutes. Table 6 indicates the range 
of comfortable MII‘s situation to extreme conditions.

3.4 Uncertainty Factors 
 
Uncertainty is an element of a decision problem, and almost all decisions are made under some 
degree of uncertainty, where the solution (the expected value) is almost always an approximation 
to the real world problem (Wallace, 1998). An uncertainty factor (UF) is classified as all inputs 
that can drive a variation in the output of a specific model (Saltelli et al., 1999). Gathered in Table 
8 are UFs that can affect the optimal outcome of the proposed framework, and further 
categorized based on their relation. Furthermore, Table 8 categorizes UFs that are present in the 
framework, and UFs that should be included in the framework to assess the real effects of shock 
and vibration. Generally UFs can either be rated as quantitative or qualitative factors, based on 
their format of data (Integrated-assessment.eu, unknown). As described by Firestone et al. (1997) 
„Uncertainty includes parameter uncertainty, model uncertainty, or scenario uncertainty“ (p.9), 
which displays that uncertainty can be positioned anywhere. Quantitative UFs give information 
about the quantity that can be measured in numbers, while qualitative UFs are measured in 
words (Yin, 1994). This paper focuses on the parameter uncertainty accounted for by quantitative 
data.  
 
As the proposed framework is built to assess the behaviour of HSC at sea and analyzes how the 
seakeeping affects humans located on the craft, the HF should be taken into account in a more 
detailed way for a more comprehensive solution. However, since the HF varies greatly between 
individuals, it would be nearly impossible to include the physical capabilities of each person based 
on their past life (Dobzhansky et al., 1972). Hence, it can be said that for the most part, the HF is 
generalized in the model. Therefore, the model mainly focuses on UFs accounted for by the 
seaworthiness in the application by each tool. All factors that are formularized as constants 
within the tools are kept unchanged, and not considered as UFs. UFs are only taken into 
consideration for the RA, as they account for the result of the optimal solution.

3.5 Sensitivity Analysis and Monte Carlo Simulation 
 
Sensitivity analysis is used to identify, create and evaluate alternative strategies, which might 
occur during the process of implementation or execution of a process or a project for more 
comprehensive decision making and to decrease the uncertainty of a possible negative outcome 
(Pannel, 1997). Management decision making should depend on sensitivity analysis for all 
possible variation in the parameter’s value, to better understand the risk involved for each 
alternative along with the uncertainty (Rappaport, 1967).  Rappaport (1967) also discusses that 
==PAGE:28==
19 
 
the importance of sensitivity analysis varies with the degree of uncertainty, less certainty 
increases the acuteness of possible parameter variation. There are various sensitivity analysis 
methods, where the selected approach should be adjusted to suit the decision-making problem, 
(Pannel, 1997). These methods can be classified into either local or global methods, which can 
furthermore be grouped as quantitative or qualitative methods (Borgonovo & Plischke, 2016). 
Local methods focus on identifying the changes of one uncertainty parameter at a time, while 
global methods take into consideration changes of all uncertainty factors, which can be simulated 
with methods such as Monte Carlo Simulation due to size and complexity (Saltelli et al., 2008; 
Borgonovo & Plischke, 2016; Saltelli et al., 1999). This paper focuses on simulation due to the 
extremely large number of UFs that are included in the analysis of the best and worst possible 
outcome. However, Monte Carlo Simulation does not track the sensitivity of changes for each 
variable, but creates randomly distributed values over a specific determined range (Mooney, 
1997). Furthermore, Table 9 indicates whether Local or Global methods should be used to 
identify the sensitivity of the input variables for the appropriate technique, and if the simulation 
is suitable to increase the plausibility of the optimal result.

Since the UFs that are present in the framework, are the dominating factors that account for

particular prescribed confidence range to explore the possibilities of the framework (Firestone et

sensitivity analysis and/or simulation. The rhythm of the sea moves in a stochastic motion, which 
is almost impossible to establish with high confidence. Therefore, it is recommended to simulate 
the motion of the sea, and related factors, to determine the possible outcomes HSC investor 
might expect their occupants to be exposed to during operations of its craft. Due to the extremely 
large amount of UFs (e.g. every single wave over the measured period, counts for one UF) the

4.2 Framework 
 
When performing the assessment it is important to place the measurement equipment where 
the action takes place on the HSC since the shock magnitude varies with different locations. 
Furthermore, measurement equipment needs to be placed around the spine of a person, in the 
vessel. The person should be located close to the destination of the craft‘s measurement 
placement. 
 
The framework is indicated for high speed craft investors to analyze alternative options and to 
determine if the operational conditions, according to historical data, expose crew members and 
passengers to dangerous and/or severe shocks for comparison. The framework, Figure 3, has 
been structured and developed based on findings from literature review, case company data and 
further information gathered from specialists in the field. Before the assessment takes place 
through the framework, the customer or the consultant needs to determine a portfolio of HSCs 
of interest to be assessed and compared. It is recommended to keep the framework guidelines 
(Appendix C) at hand once calculations are being performed, or for more detailed information. 
Following is an explanation of how to execute the HSC RA comparison according to the proposed 
framework.  
 
Step 1 The first step is dependent on the gathered data from the end user‘s database. The input 
factors are: (1) The HSC portfolio needs to be determined. (2) The number of crew members. (3) 
The age of all onboard members. (4) Operational conditions: analyze past data for weather 
conditions most operations take place in. If data does not exist, establish the most likely weather 
conditions that might occur during operations. (5) Mission duration: analyze historical data for 
operational time duration. (6) Declare if the assessment should be performed for seated or 
standing occupants, or a mix of both. (7) Where on the vessel most activity takes place. 
Furthermore, one of the following seakeeping trial measurement methods, (1) Tow Tank test, (2) 
Sea trial, or (3) Seakeeping simulation, is chosen based on personal requirements and executed 
to determine the behaviour of the HSCs. It is recommended that the measurement equipment 
collects data at the frequency of 160 Hz. 
 
Step 2 The Seakeeping data is reviewed to get an overview of exposed shocks. It is recommended 
the data is analyzed with an ICI, to establish the range of shocks and counts of each shock range 
==PAGE:32==
23 
 
for the measured data. However, this is just a recommended approach to visualize the measured 
data, but it can be performed in other ways if required. Subsequently, either DA or IA is chosen 
based on the results from ICI. DA is suitable for shock range of 0G to 4G for seakeeping behaviour 
evaluation that affects crew members’ long-term exposure to WBV and RS, while IA is 
recommended to estimate the hazards and severity of shocks of higher magnitude.  
 
Step 3.1 DA is based on discovering the discomfort level over the crew members’ lifespan by 
determining the seakeeping characteristics with an International Standard ISO 2631:1 from 1997, 
to see if the established data fits the requirements for the next step/s of the framework. The 
International Standard ISO 2631:1 includes three assessment methods, each one applicable to a 
specific motion of the HSC. These methods all have a checkpoint which evaluates whether the 
current method is relevant to the specific case. The first method, Root Mean Square (RMS), is 
applicable for the motion of the vessel which is considered to be balanced. The next method, 
Running RMS, is suitable if an occasional shock occurs in the measurement. The last method, 
Vibration Dose Value (VDV), is practicable for measurement including frequent shocks. However, 
it is recommended that both of the later methods are performed, since there is no proposed 
criteria to fulfill in order to determine if the data includes occasional or frequent shocks. If DA 
does not satisfy its requirements to exceed to the recommended assessment method (ISO 
2631:5), the evaluation is directed to the other IA assessment techniques. 
 
Step 3.2 IA is used to evaluate if the individual shocks of the measured data exposed to the crew 
members are of the level of severity and can cause potential physical injury. Before the exposure 
evaluation, the input data needs to be reviewed, to determine if the occupants are in a seated or 
standing position.  
In case of IA for seated occupants, DRM is relevant to analyze the compression level of the spine 
caused by the collision of a seated person versus the occupant's seat. The DRM method, Dynamic 
Response Index (DRI), is used to calculate every single shock that occurs over the measurement 
period; each DRI value is then classified, and compared to the DRI index, Figure 9  (seen in 
Appendix C), to assess if the exposure can cause severe damage to the occupants. 
Hence, if occupants are in a standing position, an ADM should be used to assess the shocking 
exposure. The ADM method, Maximum Tolerable Impact Loading (MTIL), takes into account all 
shocks and evaluates their duration compared to an MTIL index, Figure 10 (seen in Appendix C), 
to analyze if the shocks are considered to be at a severe level for the crew members. 
However, if DRI or MTIL (depending on the input data) result in zero shocks, it is recommended 
to turn to other IA assessment techniques (ISO 2631:5). 
 
Step 4 Based on either step 3.1 or 3.2, UFs are discovered to identify the factors that count for 
changes in the outcome of each vessel under view. Upper and lower limits (Confidence Level) 
need to be defined for each UF, based on personal opinion. It is recommended the range between 
upper and lower limits is kept wide (±10%) due to the stochastic motion of the sea. Table 8 
indicates the relevant UFs present in each tool for assessment. Furthermore, the real value of 
each UF is simulated with a Monte Carlo Simulation, where each UF takes a random value within 
the CL to establish credible value.  
 
==PAGE:33==
24 
 
Step 5 The results of the assessment will, therefore, indicate the number of severe shocks, or the 
lifespan of each crew member. These results should be used to evaluate which alternative vessel 
out of the tested crafts is the optimal option based on the assessment. Furthermore, the 
customer/investor then needs to assess personally which option is more likely to fulfill further 
requirements, if required.

5 Validation of the Framework 
 
The framework was validated through a workshop, which was carried out by four employees 
from the case company. The feedback session took place at the company’s headquarters and 
lasted for 50 minutes. The intention was to determine the validity of the framework for the 
company’s future product developments.  
 
First, the workshop began with an introduction to the research conducted by the author, and 
furthermore, the framework and its required input parameters were introduced. Pursuant to the 
presentation, a discussion arose around the included assessment tools for each possible 
evaluation direction, along with additional input requirements that could become valid for the 
establishment of craft’s seakeeping behaviour and RA. It was also mentioned by participants that 
research had been performed in the past, where only the behaviour of their vessel had been 
measured and compared, without evaluating its effects on humans. 
 
The feedback from the workshop proved to be very constructive. To begin with, there was a 
consensus among participants that involving the customer requirement of a minimum 
operational speed should be implemented since it usually plays a big role when potential buyers 
examine alternative HSC investment options. Furthermore, three participants addressed their 
enthusiasm about the framework and mentioned that it is highly applicable to their current and 
future work, as well as for other competitors in the market. They find it relevant for customers 
to include this type of information when reviewing alternative options. In addition, the point was 
also made that there is a significant gap in the market for future development of this kind of work 
and that a tool, such as this one, can be a big step of implementing future RA standards within 
the HSC industry and further establish competitive advantage and proof of concept. 
Subsequently, it was discussed that decision makers usually look at the initial price of the craft, 
and make decisions based on that. However, those workers are usually not informed about how 
the craft can affect their crew member’s performance, and that this framework might help them 
understand that concept better with scientific proof. Only one participant expressed doubt 
concerning the IA based on the fact that some of the evaluation tools have not been established 
as standards within the industry. The rest of the participants did not find it to be a limitation to 
the framework, since there are no current official assessment methods that can indicate if shocks 
are severe or within the comfort zone, according to their knowledge. Lastly, one participant 
talked about the framework’s applicability for the case company, and its potential of becoming a 
standardized work process to assess their crafts, to see if their future product development is 
increasing or decreasing safety at sea. 
 
It is worth mentioning that the company had not been informed about the results of the case 
study prior to the workshop, to keep them unbiased and in a neutral position for validation of 
the proposed framework. Furthermore, the framework has only been validated with a single case 
study, which currently indicates just a single aspect of its applicability. It would be useful to 
conduct other case studies to see its real functionality, based on different input data and 
requirements.

6 Implications for theory and practice 
 
This chapter covers how the study contributes value to theory for further research on the topic, 
as well as its utility for practitioners adopting the framework for deployment.   
 
6.1 Implication for theory 
 
In principle, HSC has been used to travel as fast as possible to complete a certain task (Dobbins 
et al., 2008), and therefore, with that as a priority, other highly important factors have been left 
out. Seakeeping has been gaining more and more attention due to an awakening in correlation 
with performance of the crew members (Dobbins et al., 2008; Dobbins et al., 2015). The main 
contribution to theory of this research paper is the composed framework which allows HSC 
investors to select the best suited RA technique, based on personal requirements, to analyze the 
operational effects on crew members, along with its usefulness of comparing alternative vessels 
under investment investigation. The framework includes guidance matrixes throughout the 
assessment process to determine the best suited evaluation technique to choose for the optimal 
and most applicable results. Table 4 and Table 5 further present the limitations of each method 
to allow the practitioner to be aware of their applicability. Other researchers have identified the 
technique’s limitations and developed the assessment techniques, without composing a useful 
framework to guide assessors to determine the most suitable evaluation technique for diverse 
conditions. 
Additionally, UFs were investigated, which were connected to the rhythm of the sea due to its 
random motion. The analysis includes many UFs, since each single wave is classified as one UF, 
and therefore a simulation method needs to be used to determine their overall effects.  
Lastly, the framework contains a Monte Carlo simulation technique that establishes the random 
variation of the measured input data, with a predetermined upper and lower value, and enables 
the practitioner to reflect on the potential risk range. The results then allow the investors to 
choose which among the tested crafts best fits their needs. 
 
6.2 Implication for practice 
 
Implications for practice of this research are the ability to evaluate dangerous shocks that 
occupants at sea are exposed to in relation to their body posture once impacted, and to use that 
evidence as a comparison of alternative investment options. Thus, it allows decision makers, with 
little knowledge about the performance of crafts in general, to understand in more detail the 
true effects on a company’s most costly asset (humans), and potentially increase the return on 
investment in the long run. Further, different scenario evaluation techniques establish the 
framework’s applicability to different circumstances and for more personalized results. Also, 
Monte Carlo allows practitioners to simulate the craft’s behaviour with personally determined 
CL to increase the credibility of the chosen alternative. Implications for practice also include the 
validity when the framework was utilized in correlation with the case company, which resulted 
in a big difference of severe impact shocks on occupants, compared to the other craft.  
==PAGE:37==

The framework is structured around gathered qualitative and quantitative data from members 
with divergent background to create a holistic and credible tool for assistance of decision making.  
For practitioners to take full advantage of the proposed RA framework in correlation to 
seakeeping behaviour, Figure 4, recommends following the undermentioned steps:  
 
- 
Step 1: Ask the following questions to determine if comparison of alternative vessels is 
needed before investment decision making, 
- 
Is the current Life Cycle Cost of the shipyard too high? 
- 
Are the crew members not performing according to full potential? 
- 
Is the lifespan of the crew members low? 
- 
Is this the first vessel investment? 
- 
Is the assessment being conducted for professional purposes? 
 
- 
Step 2: Have an unbiased person carry out the assessment to increase the accuracy and 
keep the results according to synonymous methods of measurement. Hence, outsource 
the measurement and assessment execution.  
 
- 
Step 3: Evaluate the CL for UFs, and determine the applicable variation they are likely to 
take.  
 
- 
Step 4: Conduct a Monte Carlo Simulation to determine more accurately the potential 
outcome due to uncertainty exposed by the environment. Reflect on the UFs advised, and 
take them into account in the simulation. 
 
- 
Step 5: Base the investment decision on the true operational factors: 
- 
Is the investment for extreme weather condition purposes? 
- 
Is the investment based on luxury and/or for fun? 
- 
Does Life Cycle Cost matter in the long run? 
- 
Will this increase the return on investment?

7 Conclusion 
 
Papakirillou (2002) claims that most HSC change ownership or operators many times throughout 
the operational life of the vessel, and therefore the owner and the manufacturer is unlikely to be 
interested in the LCC of their product. However, efficient performance increases competitive 
advantage, and furthermore, it depends on which industry sector this statement is aimed at. 
Therefore, this research paper focuses on analyzing different vessels’ seakeeping characteristics 
to evaluate potential correlation to hazardous shocks occupants are exposed to during operation. 
The main seakeeping and IA techniques, along with their required input and applicational 
limitations were gathered. The overall human performance factors were analyzed with focus 
placed on the most relevant factors. UF analysis was conducted by the author (due to limited 
research papers) to spot and evaluate those factors to identify their variance on the method‘s 
outcome. Due to the rhythm of the sea, each wave is considered to be a single UF, and therefore 
the number of UFs depends on the measured time. Subsequently, a sensitivity analysis was 
compared to Monte Carlo simulation for variance in the outcome, and it was determined that 
applying a sensitivity analysis would be too time-consuming. The paper presents a holistically 
developed framework, applicable to assess seakeeping characteristics directly related to its 
effects on human health based on input requirement.  The framework is a novel approach to 
estimate the number of hazardous shocks that occupants are exposed to during operation, or for 
less extreme circumstances evaluates the lifespan of crew members. The execution of the case 
study through the framework, indicates a large overall difference in counts of severe shock 
exposure between tested vessels. Shipper 11 showed a 78% - 89% decrease in hazardous wave 
slamming incidences, with 80% CL, compared to Þórður over an operational time of 5 minutes 
and 34 seconds according to the NRT analyzed data. This difference indicates that seakeeping 
characteristics are related to hazardous shocks exposed to occupants (RQ1). However, 
investment decision-making is highly dependant on the input requirement of each customer. 
Therefore the framework can be used as guidance to choose between alternative options, and 
for further awareness about expectations of the investment (RQ2). The key contribution to 
theory is the composed framework that allows HSC investors to evaluate the risky shocks their 
crew members experience during a sea trip, based on the best suited RA technique which fits the 
pre-established input variables. Furthermore, the key contribution to practice is to incorporate 
hazardous shocks to evaluate alternative options in investment decision making purposes. 
 
7.1 Limitations & Further Development 
 
The developed framework is limited to guiding practitioners in evaluating alternative HSC and to 
determine the investment option based on seakeeping capabilities exposure to crew members. 
It may require an additional case study for further adaptation, and to verify in more detail its true 
applicability. The case study addresses evaluation for standing occupants at the bow of the craft 
and shows a significant difference between measured vessels. Therefore, studies for seated 
occupants placed at other locations on the craft are needed for validation of other evaluation 
techniques, along with implementation of true physical capabilities of each individual, which will 
indicate more detailed information regarding seakeeping of an occupant’s performance. 
==PAGE:39==
30 
 
Furthermore, more holistic research on UFs, and sensitivity analysis is required so practitioners 
can identify the realistic variation of a calculated result. 
Subsequently, to expand the applicability of the framework and add value to identifying the 
overall effects due to WBV and RS, shock evaluation for mechanical equipment and electronic 
devices can be implemented, along with an assessment of structural fatigue.

Appendix C 
Framework Guidelines 
 
This guideline is intended to help the reader to understand every step of the framework, for 
the best and most accurate results for comparison of alternative crafts for investment decision 
making when it comes to acquiring a High Speed Craft. 
 
Tools needed to follow the framework 
- 
2x Accelerometers  
o 1 attached to the craft, where the most activity takes place, 
o 1 located around the spine of a crew member, 
o Data is recommended to be gathered at 160 Hz. 
 
The input needed to execute the framework assessment are the following: 
1. Portfolio of crafts under investigation, 
2. Number of crew members, 
3. Age of all members, 
4. Average weather conditions missions take place in, 
5. Average mission duration per day, 
6. Location on the craft where the most activity takes place, 
7. Positioning of crew members: standing, seated or mix of both. 
 
1. Data Gathering 
Data gathering for the framework can be performed in three different ways: Tow tank test, Sea 
trail or Seakeeping simulation. The chosen method is optional.  
 
1. Tow Tank Test 
Tow tank test is a physical simulation test, to establish the ship’s performance. It is used to 
identify its behaviour for further development. 
2. Sea trial 
Sea trial is a trial at sea. The boat should be equipped with accelerator measurement equipment, 
placed where most activity takes place on the boat, where occupants are either standing or 
seated, according to requirements.  
3. Seakeeping Simulation 
Seakeeping simulation is performed in a simulation software, where the simulation is based on 
the boat hull model. 
2. Data Analysis 
2.1 Impact Count Index (ICI) 
All peak values from the measured data are categorized into groups. Each group has a range of 
0,2G, starting from the peak value of 1G, since lower values are not considered as shocks to the 
vessel. Therefore, the first group should have the range [1G;1,2G], the next one (1,2G:1,4G], etc. 
Furthermore, the groups should be presented in a histogram for visualization.
