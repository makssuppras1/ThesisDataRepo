Abstract (ENG) 
TP901-1 is a temperate bacteriophage that has lactic acid bacteria as its natural host. This 
means that it is a virus which can follow one of two lifecycles, lytic or lysogenic, and that it uses 
bacteria as its host. The lifecycles differ in the way that the lytic cycle rapidly replicates the 
phage before destroying the cell, while the lysogenic cycle integrates the phage’s genome into 
the bacterial genome and makes the viral genes lie dormant as the phage spreads when the 
host propagates. When the integration is complete, the host is immune to the lytic cycle from 
the same species. To determine what lifecycle to follow, TP901-1 uses a bistable switch and 
95% of the time the lytic cycle is followed. In this study, the focus is to see if the lytic switch 
frequence is tied to the strength/activity of the lytic promoter.  
Old experiments were conducted prior to this study, but the attempts to verify and replicate 
them and their results have not been successful. Instead, new mutants have been created with 
the Golden Gate technique. They have been tested for altered switch frequency and promoter 
activity; however, no conclusive answers have been found.

Abstract (DAN) 
TP901-1 er en bakteriofag, hvis naturlige vært er mælkesyrebakterier. Den har evnen til at vælge 
mellem at gå ind i det lytiske stadie eller det lysogene stadie. I det lytiske stadie kopierer fagen 
sig selv hurtigt hvorefter den ødelægger værten så den kan sprede sig til flere værter. I det 
lysogene stadie integrerer fagens DNA sig i værtens eget DNA og spreder sig når værten deler 
sig. I det lysogene stadie er værten også immun overfor det lytiske stadie fra den samme art af 
bakteriofager.  For at vælge hvilken stadie den skal gå ind i, bruger TP901-1 en genetisk switch og 
den følger det lytiske stadie i 95% af tilfældene. Dette projekt undersøgte om der var et 
sammenhæng mellem den lytiske promotors styrke og fagens valg af stadie, samt promotorens 
overordnet aktivitet.  
Sammenhængen blev undersøgt før, men det var ikke muligt at genskabe eller verificerer disse 
eksperimenter og deres resultater kunne ikke lade sig gøre. Der blev i stedet skabt nye 
mutationer med ”Golden Gate” teknikken. De blev testet for om der var en ændring i det lytiske 
stadies frekvens og aktivitet, men der kunne ikke konkluderes noget.

Introduction 
One of the most common causes of illness in humans, plants, and animals, is viral infection. 
Viruses adapt quickly, are hard to combat, and can spread at an incredible rate. They can be 
split into various categories depending on which type of organisms they use as host. A virus that 
targets a bacterium for instance is a Bacteriophage.  
Viruses are also an issue for many industries, such as the dairy industry. Lactic acid bacteria are 
used in the fermentation process to create products such as cheese and yoghurt. A viral 
infection can both destroy an entire culture of bacteria and contaminate the batch of product 
afterwards. For that reason, looking into how we defend against these viral infections is 
paramount. 
One way to approach this is by preventing the cell death in the first place. An example of this 
could be how to potentially protect L. lactis from the bacteriophage TP901-1. This virus infects 
Lactococcus Lactis and causes them to burst 95% of the time by following the lytic lifecycle. 
However, in 5% of cases, the virus integrates its genome into the bacterial DNA. In this case, 
other TP901-1 phages can’t cause cell death in the same host. This happens when the virus 
follows the lysogenic life cycle. This lifecycle will eventually give way to the lytic cycle, however, 
if that could be prevented or the condition could be artificially created to make it seem like the 
bacteria is infected, it would effectively be immune to phage-induced cell death. 
It is not exactly known how the lifecycle of the virus is decided, however, if the frequency of the 
lytic cycle could be altered, it would be a step towards combatting viral infections in the dairy 
industry, and potentially elsewhere too.

Bacteriophages 
Viruses are the most abundant type of organism with a ratio of 10 phages for every bacterium 
[1]. They are unique because they can’t replicate on their own. Instead, they must infect a host, 
and use the host’s replication process to multiply and spread further. A bacteriophage is a type 
of virus that targets Bacteria. 
Phage structure 
Physically, phages mainly consist of a head capsule where the phage stores its genetic material, 
and then a tail that it uses to attach itself to the hosts’s surface. The specifics of the structure 
can change depending on the species of phage [2]. The tail consists of a long tail segment, and 
a tail tip [3]. The tail segment can be divided into two groups: contractable and not contractable. 
If the tail is contractable, it has a sheath around its tail tube that will contract upon infection. 
This allows the tube underneath to penetrate the host and inject the phage’s DNA. An example 
of the contractable tail segment is the phage T4 of the Myoviridae family [4]. If the phage’s tail 
isn’t contractable, then the overall shape of the tail is retained during infection. This is the case 
with the Lambda phage from the Siphoviridae family [4].  
The tail tip enables the phage to attach itself to its host’s surface and inject the genetic material. 
Therefore, the tail tip is unique to the species [3]. This can, for instance, be seen when 
comparing the tail tip of the TP901-1 phage and the tip of the p2 phage. Both are from the 
Siphoviridae family as well and are visibly different when studied under an electron microscope 
[3]. This can be seen in Figure 1 below.

Phage Lifecycle 
Two lifecycles have been observed in bacteriophages: Lytic, and Lysogenic. All phages must 
follow the lytic cycle to replicate, and those phages that can only follow the lytic cycle are called 
the lytic phages. The other lifecycle is the lysogenic lifecycle which is a dormant state some 
phages can adopt instead. These phages are called temperate phages. The difference between 
the cycles can be seen in figure 2. 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
 
Figure 2 - The lifecycles of a bacteriophage: The phage first makes contact and attach itself to a host. It then injects 
its genome into the cytoplasm. The phage will now follow either the lytic or lysogenic cycle. In the lytic cycle the host 
will start to express the phage’s genome. Through this, the host produces phage proteins and phage components 
which are assembled into new phages. When enough phages have been made, the cell is lysed, letting the phages 
spread to new hosts. In the lysogenic cycle, a large amount of the CI protein (shown as orange spots) is synthesized, 
and the phage genome integrates in the host’s genome via an attP site. In this state, the phage remains dormant and 
spreads with the host as it propagates. Eventually, if the phage detects an SOS response in the host, it will start 
following the lytic path. All phages follow the lytic path eventually, those that can follow the lysogenic path are called 
temperate phages. 
Lytic lifecycle 
To complete the lytic lifecycle, a phage will attach to a host’s surface, then inject its genetic 
material into the host. The host will then replicate the viral proteins and components needed for 
a new phage to form. Once enough phages have been created, the cell is lysed, killing the cell, 
and letting the phages spread to find new hosts and repeat the cycle [5]. 
Lysogenic lifecycle 
In the lysogenic lifecycle, the phage integrates its genome into the host’s chromosome. In this 
state, the viral proteins needed for the lytic cycle are repressed by other proteins, such as the CI 
protein found in multiple phages. Now, instead of replicating itself, the phage replicates when 
the host proliferates [5].  
The lysogenic phage can therefore remain undetected until a specific condition is met, due to 
either internal or external factor, where the phage will change to follow the lytic lifecycle. This 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
can, for instance, be when the Lambda phage, which infects Escherichia coli, detects an SOS 
response in the host [6]. TP901-1 can also respond like this. It has been observed that, when 
exposed to Ultraviolet light, TP901-1 will switch to the lytic lifecycle [7].  
When a temperate bacteriophage, such as TP901-1 or Lambda, infects a cell and integrates 
itself in its host’s genome, the host become immune to the lytic cycle from the same kind of 
phage [8]. In the case of TP901-1, the production of CI protein during the lysogenic lifecycle will 
act on and repress any other infections from the same type of phage, meaning it won’t be able 
to follow the lytic path [9]. This is also why such immunity only works on the same species, they 
need to interact with the same proteins.  
How a temperate phage’s lifecycle is chosen can vary from species to species. For instance, in 
the Lambda phage, the choice is regulated by a bistable switch.  
Lambda Switch 
The Lambda phage is the most studied bacteriophage as of now. As stated earlier, it is a 
temperate phage. The decision between the lytic and lysogenic lifecycle is decided by a bi-
stable switch. The switch is controlled by the repressors CI and Cro. The Cro repressor is the 
one that represses the lysogenic cycle, while CI represses the lytic cycle [10].  
To follow the lytic life cycle, the Cro repressor first binds to operator site OR3, which represses 
the promoter PRM, which represses the synthesis of CI. Cro will then bind to the operator site OL 
(All three sites), which shuts down promoter PL. Then it finally binds to the rest of the OR-sites, 
which represses the promoter PR, repressing the expression of itself [10].   
For the Lambda phage to follow the lysogenic cycle, the protein CII is expressed and enables the 
promoters PRM and PI. CI, the repressor of the lytic cycle, can be expressed from two promoters: 
PRM and PRE. The former is controlled by the CII protein, and the latter is controlled by CI through 
a self-inducing loop. CI binds to the OL and OR operator sites to activate the expression of itself 
and block the transcription of early phage construction and lytic genes. From there CI can 
maintain its own concentration through PRM. Meanwhile PRE transcribes anti-sense Cro mRNA, 
which mixes with the Cro mRNA and prevents it translation [10]. The ways CI, CII, and Cro 
interact with the Lambda switch can be seen in figure 3. 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
 
Figure 3 - The Lambda Switch: The two proteins of note are CI, the repressor of the lytic cycle, and Cro, the repressor 
of the lysogenic cycle. To follow the lytic pathway, Cro binds to Operator site OR3 to repress the promoter PRM. This 
blocks the expression of CI. Cro then binds to all the OL sites to repress promoter PL. Finally, it binds to the rest of the

The Lambda switch is a good place to start, but the switch of interest for us is the switch in the 
phage TP901-1. 
Lactococcal Bacteriophage TP901-1 
TP901-1 is a bacteriophage whose natural host is Lactococcus lactis. It is a temperate phage, 
meaning it can follow either of the two life cycles: Lytic and Lysogenic. In over 95% of the cases, 
TP901-1 will follow the lytic cycle [11], [12]. The phage consists of a capsule for the DNA dubbed 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
the Major Head Protein, a non-contractable tail and a tail tip that can attach the phage to the 
host surface [2].  
TP901-1’s Switch 
Upon attaching itself to a L. lactis bacterium TP901-1 will inject its genome into the cytoplasm. 
It is then assumed that the choice between the lytic or lysogenic cycle is made. The exact 
timeframe of the choice is unknown. TP901-1 makes that decision with a bistable switch that 
can produce two proteins: CI and Modulator of Repression (MOR) which are responsible for 
choosing the lysogenic and lytic lifecycle respectively [5]. In terms of complexity, the TP901-1 
switch is a lot simpler than the Lambda switch. A map of the TP901-1 switch can be seen in 
figure 4. 
 
Figure 4 - A topological map of the TP901-1 switch: Compared to the Lambda Switch, the TP901-1 switch is a lot less 
complex. It is only the concentrations of MOR and CI that determine the outcome of either Lytic or Lysogenic 
respectively. The CI repressor can bind to the operator sites: OR, OL, and OD to repress the promotor PL and the mor 
gene. The MOR repressor can bind to the CI repressor and then the CI:MOR complex can bind to OM1, OM2, and OM3 in 
order to repress PR. Modified from [13]. 
The TP901-1 switch encodes for the repressors: CI and MOR. These two repressors compete to 
repress each other. When infecting L. lactis, the switch has been observed to favour the lytic 
lifecycle as the wildtype (WT) follows it 95% of the time [11]. 
Operator sites (OR, OL, OD) 
The switch also codes for the 3 operator sites: OR, OL, and OD. These 3 sites are palindromic 
sequences as can be seen above in figure 4. OR and OL are located 63 basepairs apart, and they 
flank the PL promoter. For optimal repression of the lytic promoter, a CI dimer complex must 
bind to both OR and OL [14]. The third operator site, OD, is located much further downstream, 
247 bp from OL, inside the mor gene. Given the flexibility of DNA, the distance involved doesn’t 
affect the ability for the CI dimers to form a trimer complex (see figure 5) [14]. Partial repression 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
of the PR promoter while retaining full repression of the PL promoter is also possible, if OR is 
unbound while the other two operator sites are bound [14].  
The CI protein 
The CI protein is 180 amino acids long. It first forms a dimer with another CI-protein and this 
dimer can interact with one of the three operator sites: OR, OL, and OD. The 3 dimers can then 
form a trimer of dimers. This trimer causes the switch area to curl up and prevents the RNA 
polymerase from binding the lytic promoter, PL. This represses that lytic cycle [11]. A model of 
how CI is assumed to operate can be seen in figure 5. 
 
Figure 5 – The CI protein and its complexes: The protein CI consists of a NTD that binds to DNA, a connection-
segment, and a CTD that can form complexes with other CI dimers. The Connector-segment can form a dimer with 
another monomer CI protein. Dimer CI complexes can bind to the operator sites OR, OL, and OD, and they can form 
trimer complexes of dimers using the CTDs. When the Trimer of dimers has been formed, it causes a loop in the DNA, 
repressing the promoter PL. 
The protein contains a Helix-turn-helix (HTH) motif at the N-Terminal Domain (NTD), which is 
where the DNA-binding properties are also located [14]. The motif can be seen in figure 6. The 
C-terminal domain (CTD) is responsible for the formation of CI2 trimers [15].

site. The grey areas of the HTH motif are the CI-NTD, and the yellow section is the proposed MOR interaction site. The

The MOR protein 
The other protein, MOR, is the repressor of the lysogenic lifecycle. It consists of 72 amino acids, 
and contains a HTH motif [11]. It has the ability to form a complex with a CI dimer and the HTH 
motif serves as a DNA binding motif. Through mutations, it has been found that the ability to 
bind with CI plays a big part MOR’s ability to repress the lysogenic cycle [16]. The interaction 
between CI and MOR can be seen in figure 7.

The CI:MOR complex 
For MOR to repress the PR promoter, it must form a complex with a CI-N-Terminal Domain (CI-
NTD). The exact formation of this complex isn’t fully understood, but it is known MOR must bind 
to the DNA binding motif on the CI protein. Then more MOR proteins will join the already bound 
MOR proteins. Two models for how the CI:MOR complex looks have been proposed (see figure 
8). The difference between the two proposed models is that in model 1 the MOR proteins bind 
on the outer side of the CI2-complex, two on the left and one on the right [16].  
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
 
Figure 8 - The two proposed models for how MOR and CI2 interact to form CI:MOR complex and how they bind 
with the operator sites: Model 1 theorizes that MOR binds on the outside of the CI2-complex. This means all the 
operator sites can be bound; however, it doesn’t prevent the binding to the operator sites tied to repressing the lytic 
cycle. Model 2 proposes that the MOR proteins bind on the inside of the CI2 complex. This model makes it harder for 
the CI-MOR complex to bind to the lysogenic repressing operator sites, however, the benefit is that it completely 
prevents the binding to lytic repressing operator sites. It does mean that one MOR protein has to bind with operator 
site OR. Taken from [16]. 
This creates the configuration MOR-MOR-CI2-MOR. The issue with this model is that it isn’t likely 
to prevent the CI2-complex from binding to the operator sites it normally would. Model 2 
proposes that the MOR proteins bind on the inside of the CI2-complex. This creates the 
configuration: MOR-MOR-MOR-CI. The benefit of the second model is that it prevents the CI2-
complex from fully binding to the operator sites tied to Lytic repression. But it faces the issue 
that one of the MOR proteins must use an operator site that is meant for a CI-protein (OR) [16]. 
The models can be seen in figure 8. 
The CI2-part of the complex binds to an operator site named OR and the MOR part binds to the 
other operator sites. These sites are designated as OM1, OM2, and OM3. Through mutations, it has 
been seen that the OM2 site isn’t as crucial for establishing the lytic state compared OM1 and OM3 
[16].  
Switch frequency vs. promoter strength. 
As mentioned earlier, TP901-1 follows the lytic cycle 95% of the time. This is called the Switch 
Frequency. The frequency is measured as the number of colonies in a sample that follows the 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
lytic cycle. This is done by inserting a reporter gene in after the mor gene. When TP901-1 makes 
its choice between the lytic or lysogenic lifecycle, it depends on the promoter strength/activity 
of PR and PL. PL being the lytic promoter, and PR being the lysogenic promoter. A way to alter the 
strength of a promoter is to mutate the basepairs in the intermediate area between the 
promoter’s -10 and -35 domains. The sequence between the two is not a part of consensus, 
however, they do play a role in how strong a promoter is [17]. 
In 2012, DTU Master student Nick Olesen tested if the switch frequency was tied to the strength 
of the lytic promoter, PL. To test this, he used localized mutagenesis to alter the area between 
the -10 and -35 domains of the PL promoter. He did this with a mutated primer and Polymerase 
Chain Reaction (PCR). The switch was replicated in two segments. The mutagenesis was done 
with the primers MK653 and MK540 (See figure 9). The other segment was made with the 
primers MK594 and MK654. Both segments were ligated together with Blunt-end ligation. Then 
the mutated switch was amplified with the primers MK638 and MK639. The mutated switch was 
then ligated into the vector, pAK80 [18]. The workflow can be seen in figure 9 and a picture of the 
specific area of the genome with primer MK653 and MK654 annotated can be seen in figure 10. 
 
Figure 9 – Workflow of Nick Olesen and Jesper Tvenge: The switch was mutated with the primers MK653 & MK 540 
in a PCR reaction. The other part of the switch was amplified with MK594 & MK654. Both segments were ligated 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
together via blunt-end ligation. The complete switch was amplified with primers MK638 and MK639. It was then 
ligated into the vector pAK-80 using the restriction-sites HindIII and PstI, and vector pLB86 using the sites XhoI and 
PstI. Taken from [19].  
To establish a baseline, the WT switch was transformed into the E.coli strain MC1061. The 
switch had a lacZ gen positioned in front of PL, so if the switch followed the lytic path, the colony 
would turn blue when put on plates containing X-Galactose (Xgal). It was found that the plated 
WT colonies turn blue around 32% of the time [18]. 
When the mutated switch was introduced, a good half of the plated mutants started to have a 
switch frequency of 100%, while the overall switch frequency never went below 50%, except 
one of the plasmids which was all white [18]. 
Afterwards, a new reporter plasmid was constructed for each mutated promoter. Here the 
promoter PL was cloned into an E.coli-vector. The mutants also contained the lacZ gene and its 
purpose was to test the strength of the PL promoter. Through a β-galactosidase assay it was 
possible to determine the strength of the PL promoter and then put it into a graph with the 
promoter strength on the X-axis and the switch frequency on the Y-axis. From that, a curve was 
derived by fitting the values [18]. The graph can be seen in figure 11. 
One thing to note about Nick Olesen’s work is that it was all done in E. coli, which isn’t one of 
TP901-1 natural hosts, and with a high copy number vector. Therefore, in 2015, DTU Master 
Student Jesper Tvenge also tested if the switch frequency could be impacted by mutations in 
the PL-promoter but this time within a L. lactis host.  
To mutate the switch, Tvenge used the same PCR primers and procedures as Nick Olesen did 3 
years prior [19]. In addition to this, he also created another series of mutants where he used the 
plasmid pLB86[8] as a vector. This series was made with the purpose of measuring the PL 
activity. The lacZ for a lacLM gene [19].

The new mutant plasmids were transformed into L. lactis strain MG1363. The colonies were

were counted. It was found that with the mutations, the switch frequency was between 0 and

The purpose of this report 
Due to an interest in getting these findings published in the future, it’s important that these 
results and experiments could be replicated. Therefore, the plan was to try and verify the strains 
Jesper Tvenge had created, and then recreate the promoter fusion experiment. Because the 
experiments couldn’t be replicated, new mutations had to be produced, and tested. Based on 
this, the following hypotheses have been set up: 
1. Jesper Tvenge's results can be replicated and verified. 
2. Mutating the switch can be done quickly and effectively with Golden Gate. 
3. Mutating just two base pairs in the region between the -35 and -10 domains of the PL-
promoter will affect the switch frequency.

Inoculating from stock cultures 
The original strains were brought up from -80°C cold storage and inoculated in GM17 medium 
with 5 μg/mL Erm and 150 μg/mL Xgal added in. The cultures were streaked onto agar plates 
with said medium and left overnight at 30°C.  
Additionally, the JT-161 strains were inoculated into a liquid GM17 medium with Erm5 as well. 
They were also left to incubate at 30°C overnight. The liquid cultures were then turned into 
pellets by gathering 2ml of culture, centrifuge it for 5 minutes at 13300 RPM, discard the 
supernatant, and repeat for a total of 4ml worth of culture pellets.

Cuvettes were placed on ice. 40 µL of electro-competent cells were thawed from -80C, while 
still being on ice. 2 µL of the plasmids were mixed with the competent cells and then placed in 
the cuvettes at the bottom. The mixture is electrocuted and afterwards the cells were 
suspended in pure LB medium for 2 hours before 100 µL were plated to agar plates with 
selective markers. The remaining cells were spun down, resuspended, and plated as well. Then 
the plates were left to incubate overnight.

Electroporation of L. lactis 
Cuvettes were placed on ice. 40 µL of electro-competent cells are thawed from -80°C, while still 
being on ice. 1 µL of the plasmids were mixed with the competent cells and then placed in the 
cuvettes at the bottom. The mixture was electrocuted by 2 kV for around 5 msec. Afterwards the 
cells were suspended in pure SGM17 medium and left on ice for 5 to 10 minutes before being 
moved to 30°C for 2 hours. 100 µL were plated to agar plates with selective markers. The 
remaining cells were spun down, resuspended, and plated as well. Then the plates were left to 
incubate at 30°C overnight. 
Results and Discussion 
Verification of Jesper Tvenge’s strains 
To ensure that Jesper Tvenge’s experiments could be replicated, and the results reproduced, the 
strains he used had to be verified. To accomplish this, the first step was to find the strains used 
in the experiments. Within the report was a table of strains with their frequency and PL activity. 
Two series of plasmids were mentioned: pJT9 and pJT11. The PJT9 series of mutants was used to 
measure the Switch frequency when the intermediate area between the PL’s -35 and -10 
domains, while the pJT11 series was used to measure promoter activity with the same 
mutations. A second table showed the mutations present within the two series, as, the 
plasmids that share the last same number share the same mutation. So pJT9-4 shares the same 
mutation as pJT11-4 and so on. Through the Method and Materials section, it was deduced that 
the plasmids were transformed into competent cells. The pJT9 plasmids were transformed into 
a series of strains dubbed JT161-x, and pJT11 were transformed into the series JT165-x. In both 
cases, the x refers the series number. Figure 13 shows the plasmids mentioned in the report.

Once the sequencing results were received, it was found that very few of the strains matched

Designing new FW primer mutants with PCR 
Once it was concluded that the experiment couldn’t be replicated, a new approach was 
devised. New mutations had to be made and tested. When compiling the mutations listed in 
Tvenge’s report, a trend was found. There were certain areas that were nearly always mutated. 
As such, 3 different primers were designed, each having two base pairs mutated to reflect a set 
of mutated primers. The three new primers, named MK1155, MK1156, & MK1157, can be seen in 
table 1, and figure 16. The rationale behind the choices can be seen in figure 15. 
==PAGE:30==

In addition, three other primers were designed: MK1158, MK1159, MK1160 (See figure 14 for

be seen in figure 16. The 59 and 60 primers are located on the other side of the pAK80 plasmid 
(see figure 14). The primers were used in PCR with the template pMAP50, a modified pAK80 
plasmid. Each of the mutated primers were paired with MK1159 to create one half of the 
plasmid while MK1158 was paired with MK1160 to create the other half. 
During the PCR, it was found that the primers behaved differently when annealing was done at 
50°C. As such, various temperatures were tested to find the optimal temperatures as well as 
different concentrations. In the end, all the templates were diluted 100 times. In terms of 
temperature, it was found that the Golden Gate mixes containing MK1155 and MK1157 worked 
best when the annealing temperature was set to 51°C while MK1156 and MK1158 + MK1160 had 
the annealing temperature set to 47°C. The details of the procedure can be found in Methods 
and Materials. 
Creating mutants with Golden Gate 
To create new mutants that would be nearly identical to the wildtype, Golden Gate was 
exploited. Golden Gate hinges on the sequence having a recognition site that leads to a 
separate restriction site. When the site is clipped, the entire golden gate segment with 
recognition site is cut off, leaving only the part of the fragment that is native to the plasmid to 
form the new plasmid. This can be seen in figure 16 below. 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
 
Figure 16 - The primer design for the golden gate procedure: The wild type shows the area of the PL-promoter 
between the -35 and -10 domains. The domains are also bracketed with red. The -10 domain is also marked in the 
primer as an underscored, bold segment. On the left is the Golden Gate attachment. The bold segment is the 
recognition site, with an “anchor” behind it to allow the restriction enzyme to bind. The bold italics is where the 
enzyme cuts, which is always 1 base to the right of the recognition site. The bold italics with an underscore are where 
the random basepairs are located.  
The new fragments of the plasmids were put together in a Golden Gate Mix for each of the 
mutants. After the procedure was completed, a portion of the Golden Gate Assemblies were 
diluted 10 and 100 times, before they, and the undiluted assembly mixes were tested with 
Agarose gel to verify the presence of the switch. The results can be seen in figure 17. From the 
figure, it can be seen that the switch is present in all the undiluted (the first three bands) and 10x 
dilutions (second set of three bands). First at 100 times dilution did the band start to vanish.

Conclusion 
To recap, the purpose of this project was to verify the experiments and results produced by DTU 
master student Jesper Tvenge, with the intent on publishing the findings in the foreseeable 
future. The experiments couldn’t be reproduced as most of the mutations mentioned in the 
report couldn’t be verified and the documentation was lost, meaning that it wasn’t possible to 
properly recreate the mutations. 
Instead, the focus shifted to creating new mutations that were based on the most common 
mutations specified in Jesper Tvenge’s report. The mutations were created from mutated 
primers and the mutated plasmids were created via Golden Gate. Through sequencing it was 
found that Golden Gate can be used to assemble new mutants, but finding mutants with the 
switch did take a long time, with only 4% of the mutant strains having the switch. However, it 
was not possible to determine whether these mutants altered the switch frequency of the 
mutants as the transformants weren’t able to grow on plates once transformed. 
Therefore, more research must be done to see whether these mutants do alter the switch 
frequencies of TP901-1, or if they aren’t viable at all.
