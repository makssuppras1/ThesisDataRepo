Abstract
As sequencing technologies has improved over the last decades, the possibility of using
whole genome sequencing for diagnostic purposes in healthcare has become a reality. In
order to facilitate rapid detection of pathogenic outbreaks a novel surveillance system,
Pathogenic Real Time Typer (PRTT), is designed. PRTT is able to process both short-
and long sequencing reads and outputs the identiﬁed clonal cluster of the query isolate.
The entire system is fully automated and self-updating, thus ensuring a quick analysis
and constant evolution of its reference databases.
The accuracy of PRTT is prospectively compared against four bacterial outbreak datasets
from published studies. In all four cases, PRTT is able to estimate the same phylogenetic
structure as those published in the corresponding articles. Reference ﬁnding, with the
purpose of identifying the clonal cluster of the query read, is found to be very eﬀective for
isolates that had closely reference related sequences and less eﬀective for distantly related
sequences.
In this thesis it is demonstrated that by using PRTT, it is possible to quickly, precisely
and automatically determine if an unknown isolate is a part of a speciﬁc cluster. For the
vast majority of isolates, for whom related references exist in the database, it is possible to
complete a full phylogenetic analysis in under ﬁve minutes, thus opening up the possibility
of performing real time surveillance, as well as diagnostics.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Since the completion of The Human Genome project in 2003, major advancements in the
ﬁeld of sequencing technology have been made in terms of speed, accuracy and cost [1].
Previously, investigation of pathogenic bacterial DNA sequences was mostly carried out in
research settings for academic purposes. However, due to major innovations over the past
two decades, modern sequencing technologies have now become an essential part of clinical
microbiology in most healthcare systems [2]. Today, whole genome sequencing (WGS) of
pathogenic isolates causing infectious diseases has become a powerful and widely used
screening tool [3].
The aim of this project was to developed an end-to-end solution for rapid and eﬀective
pathogen detection, which can be easily implemented in large scale at several healthcare
facilities. When a patient is admitted with an bacterial infection, pathogenic DNA is
puriﬁed and sequenced. Based on these query sequencing reads, the detection system
will rapidly and accurately determine the epidemiological cluster of the pathogen. This
determination can then be accessed by medical professionals, and relevant healthcare
actions may be taken, such as patient isolation, if there is cause for concern.
1.1
Outbreaks in Human History
Throughout history, pathogenic outbreaks have always been one of the biggest existential
threats to human kind. The Black Plague wiped out one-third of Europe’s population [4],
The Spanish Flu claimed upwards of 50 million lives [5], and in today’s modernized world
SARS-CoV-2 has become a pandemic. Even though we are able to develop treatments
and vaccines faster than ever before, containment of initial outbreaks has proven to be
one of the most eﬃcient weapons against new pandemics.
1.2
Whole Genome Sequencing
In the past, surveillance and typing of pathogens have relied heavily on both pulse-ﬁeld gel
electrophoresis and serotyping. However, these techniques are far too slow to implement
in any large-scale surveillance solution [6] and today WGS is often preferred. Further,

contrary to pulse-ﬁeld gel electrophoresis, WGS is organism agnostic, and thus the same
method of sequencing can be used regardless of the organism type [7]. The introduction of

1.2.1
WGS-based Epidemiologic Analysis
Variability between genomes of the same species can be caused by several things, including
point mutations, homologous recombination and diﬀerences in genome content [8]. The
occurrence SNPs, insertions and deletions varies between species [9] [10], as well as the
rate of recombination events [11]. Several approaches to index accessory genome variations
have been developed, including typing of either individual SNPs or gene-by-gene [8].
The typing of SNPs can be performed via reference-based mapping of either reads or
larger assembled contigs. This approach can produce a high phylogenetic resolution, but
it does require a closely related reference genome to the sequence samples [8]. The larger
the phylogenetic distance between the reference and the query reads is, the greater the
risk of producing mismappings [8].
Another approach to determine genetic relatedness is to perform either whole genome
(wg) or core genome (cg) MLST [12].
In this approach, a large number of genes are
compared to the alleles in a curated set of predeﬁned core genes. Depending on which
variations are observed between the query and the curated genes, a sequence type (ST)
can be assigned to the pathogen [13].
1.2.2
Oxford Nanopore
Since its founding in 2005, Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) has greatly contributed
to the innovation of sequencing technology. Rather than following the well established
sequencing-by-synthesis techniques, which other companies use, a fundamentally diﬀerent
system was devised. By measuring the change in electrical current across a synthetic
membrane as a denatured DNA strand passes through it, long strands of DNA can be
sequenced quickly [14]. ONT sequencing reads have continuously struggled with high
error rates and wrong interpretation of homopolymers [15]. However, with improvements
in the sequencing approach and base calling algorithms, these problems have been much
reduced [16]. The ONT platforms’ output is long read sequencing data, which has speciﬁc
advantages such as enabling closing genome assemblies [14].
Real Time Diagnostics
In 2014, ONT introduced a pocket-size sequencer: MinION. What separates it from
other sequencing machines is the relatively low unit price of just $1,000 (USD). The
low price facilitates the distribution of sequencers to several remote locations. This is
critical since, for an outbreak surveillance system to function eﬀectively, sequencing cannot
just take place in few central locations.
Otherwise, once isolates have been sampled,

transported and sequenced, the current outbreak situation could have completely changed,
thus rendering the phylogenetic analysis useless.
Further, the eﬀectiveness of outbreak surveillance increases with the decrease in time from
sampling to a completed analysis. In certain cases, such as when a patient is infected with
a drug resistant pathogen, the diagnostic time is of great importance since the patient
needs to be put into isolation. ONT’s MinION sequencer sequences with a median speed
of 0,5 giga bases per hour using a R9.4 ﬂowcell [17], and thus takes about 6 hours to reach

of an isolate’s phylogeny during sequencing, rather than having to wait until the entire
process has completed which is required with second generation sequencing platforms
[20]. Naturally, the sequencing still needs be completed eventually, because an eventual
diagnosis will require an analysis based on trustworthy, accurate sequencing reads. The
preliminary estimation should primarily serve as an early warning to medical professionals
in case the patient’s pathogen is associated with a known cluster of concern. Additionally,
this would also free up additional hospital resources as non-concerning infections are
rapidly identiﬁed and easily dealt with, thus prevent unnecessary isolation of patients.
1.2.3
Illumina and Other Sequencing Technologies
Although ONT’s MinION platform is the ideal sequencer to widely employ in a real-time
surveillance system, it should still be recognized that a variety of Illumina sequencing plat-
forms have dominated the market over the last decade. Contrary to the ONT MinION
sequencer, the Illumina platforms produce short read sequencing data which cannot be ac-
cessed until the sequencing process has ended [20]. Short reads have lower error rates than
long reads [21], but often cannot close genome assemblies by themselves [14]. Because a
lot of institutions already have Illumina sequencing platforms, an eﬃcient surveillance sys-
tem should also be compatible with short read sequencing data. Naturally, this logic also
applies to all other commercially available sequencing platforms, including technologies
such as PacBIO sequencers (Long reads) and IonTorrent sequencer (short reads).
1.3
Existing Surveillance Systems
Previous research has been carried out on the subject of a surveillance system for monitor-
ing pathogen outbreaks. As a result, several such systems exists, and diﬀerent countries
and organizations have developed their own solutions.
1.3.1
NCBI Pathogen Detection Pipeline
The NCBI Pathogen Detection project is a centralized system designed to detect the
presence of foodborne pathogens. NCBI collaborates with a wide range of agencies, who
are responsible for sequencing and submitting data to NCBI’s Sequence Read Archive

11
(SRA). The system only accepts short read sequences as input, and when an isolate
is submitted, the nearest known reference genome is identiﬁed and a reference-assisted
assembly is performed. Additionally, a phylogenetic distance tree is created based on
k-mers [22].
GenomeTrakr Network
The GenomeTrakr network is a distributed network of laboratories across several coun-
tries, who collaborate on identifying pathogens related to foodborne illnesses [23]. The
laboratories perform whole genome sequencing in house and submit their data to the
shared platform hosted by NCBI. The data is then processed in the NCBI Pathogen De-
tection Pipeline. Since the launch of the network in 2012, it has resulted in more than
709 actions intended to protect consumers from foodborne illnesses [24].
1.3.2
SnapperDB
SnapperDB is an analytical pipeline developed by Public Health England with the goal
of performing high throughput screening of outbreaks of infectious diseases. SnapperDB
takes a set of short read sequences, combined with a reference genome or a Variant
Call Format (VCF) ﬁle, as input. SnapperDB is designed to capture single nucleotide
polymorphisms (SNP) variation relative to a reference genome and then cluster these
variants against relevant clonal isolates found in the SnapperDB database [25].
1.3.3
Evergreen
Evergreen is an online pipeline developed at the Center for Genomic Epidemiology at
the Technical University of Denmark.
Every day, it retrieves and processes publicly
available sequencing data from foodborne bacterial pathogens. It takes short sequencing
reads as input and searches among 151 reference genomes for a match with greater than
99% identity. If a reference is found, the reads are aligned and the consensus sequence is
placed into the corresponding phylogenetic tree. Originally, Evergreen primarily retrieved
public data of ﬁve major foodborne pathogens (E. coli, Campylobacter jejuni, Listeria
Monocytogenes, Salmonella enterica and Shigella spp.), but today it also screens for minor
pathogens [26].
1.4
Parallelization Challenges
When sequencing throughput reaches a certain level, it may be necessary for a surveillance
system to analyze multiple isolates in parallel. Doing so presents several inter-process
communication (IPC) challenges, such as The Readers Writers Problem [27], synchronous
identiﬁcation of a new unique reference, localized corrupt readings and potential deadlocks
[28]. These all need to be addressed to ensure smooth and eﬃcient usage by multiple users
concurrently.

1.4.1
Semaphores
Shared memory semaphores can be used to solve IPC challenges, such as those mentioned
above. A semaphore is an abstract data type which has an integer value s greater or equal
to 0, and can be allocated to the shared memory between sub-processes [28]. Semaphores
possesses two operations: wait() and signal(). If s = 0 then the semaphore will either
wait() until s > 0 or time out after a designated period. Whilst waiting, if s > 0, then the
semaphore may be acquired and the inner value decremented (s = s −1). Any desired
sub-process can now be run and upon completion the semaphore is released via signal(),
thus incrementing s (s = s + 1). Through correct implementation of semaphores, it is
possible to synchronize sub-processes thereby preventing IPC clashes [29].
1.5
K-mer Based Mapping and Alignment of Query
Reads
One of the most fundamental questions in bioinformatics is the determination of identity
between two genomic sequences. Two approaches to looking at this question can be taken:
mapping and alignment. Mapping is estimating the approximate identity between a query
and a template, and alignment is determining the exact identity. Computationally, it is
much faster to performing mapping rather than alignment [30].
Today, several diﬀerent mapping and alignment methods, such as MiniMap2 and KMA,
are able to directly map sequencing reads by splitting the reads into k-mers [31] [30]. An
identity score can be estimated from the number of overlapping k-mers between the query
reads and the template sequence, thus allowing for quick identiﬁcation of closely related
reference sequences from large databases [30].

Chapter 2
Materials and Methods
In this thesis a novel analytical software tool for conducting real-time outbreak surveil-
lance, Pathogen Real Time Typer (PRTT), is presented. The PRTT system was developed
to be able to analyze both short and long sequencing reads and produce a report contain-
ing an epidemiological breakdown of the sequenced isolate’s clonal cluster. PRTT may
serve as a stand-alone, localized surveillance system, but should ideally be implemented in
a multi-user setup for regional or national surveillance. Several remote systems can syn-
chronize their ﬁndings, thus allowing for an eﬀective outbreak surveillance system across
multiple geographical locations.
Upon completing an analysis of a set of query reads, the results are integrated into the
database system, thus constantly expanding the volume of genomic information available
for future analyses. This speciﬁc database structure was developed to ensure computa-
tional scalability and to enable rapid and precise sub-typing of clusters.

To benchmark and test the PRTT system, four diﬀerent datasets were chosen. These
datasets were selected based on their clinical relevance, prevalence, diversity and epidemi-

Outbreak of Shiga toxin-producing E. coli of Serotype
O104:H4

France [32]. Patients primarily experienced bloody diarrhea and hemolytic uremic syn-

found that the outbreak originated from contaminated sprout seeds which had been im-
ported to Germany from Egypt. The seeds had been repacked into smaller packages that
were then sold to garden stores in both Germany and France [32].

2.1.3
Outbreak of S. enterica serovar Enteritidis
Fifty-ﬁve epidemiologically characterized clinical and environmental S. Enteritidis isolates
originating from a series of outbreaks in Minnesota and Ohio (US) from 2000-2013 were

Since the ﬁrst detection of S. enterica serovar Enteritidis in the United States in the 1970,
it has led to a high number of outbreaks [36]. The peak of infections was during the mid
1990’s, and since then improvements in the production of eggs have led to a signiﬁcant
decrease in cases. Although their appearances are rare today, outbreaks still continue to
occur.
The 55 isolates in this data set were sequenced on an Illumina MiSeq sequencer and
subsequently uploaded to SRA [35].

2.2
Pathogen Real Time Typer
In broad terms, the complete PRTT pipeline can be broken into ﬁve steps:
1. Template ﬁnding
2. De novo assembly of newly identiﬁed references
3. Reference alignment
4. Distance matrix calculation and phylogenetic tree estimation
5. Database update and remote synchronization
Template Finding
Identifying a matching template from a given database can be time consuming. In order
to speed up the process, a template ﬁnding algorithm using KMA mapping [30] was
designed. The algorithm only searches for k-mers beginning with the start codon ATG
and with length k = 16. By computing the k-mer overlap between reference sequences
and the query reads, it is possible to determine a match score based on the co-occurring
k-mers. Additionally, by using KMA’s -sasm function, the scoring equation rewards k-
mer overlaps from multiple k-mers originating from the same read. The top three scoring
references are considered, and if none of them has a template identity greater than 90%
the algorithm returns null, thus beginning de novo assembly of the query reads. If one
of the top three candidates does have an identity above 90%, the pipeline will proceed to
reference alignment [30]. If not, it will proceed to de novo assembly.
De novo Assembly of Newly Identiﬁed References
If template ﬁnding returns null, de novo assembly of the sequenced reads will take place us-
ing the assembly pipeline Unicycler [37]. Unicycler uses SPAdes [38] for short-read assem-
bly and minimap2+miniasm for long-read assembly [31]. In order to ensure cross system
stability, a dockerized version of Unicycler was used with tag: nanozoo/unicycler:0.4.7-
0–c0404e6. Post-assembly, the contigs of the draft genome are concatenated and indexed
into the KMA reference database. No removal of plasmids are conducted during the as-
sembly process, as that exclusion of plasmids are accounted for later in the PRTT pipeline
during consensus sequence generation.
Reference Alignment
If template ﬁnding successfully identiﬁes a reference candidate, alignment is carried out
using KMA [30]. A series of diﬀerent parameters are used depending on whether or not
==PAGE:16==
2.2. PATHOGEN REAL TIME TYPER
16
short reads or long reads are given as input. Of these parameters the most notable once
are -ca (make circular alignment) and -dense (do not allow insertions in the consensus
sequence). Before the query consensus sequence is accepted, the SNP distance to the
reference is calculated. If it is greater than 300 SNPs the isolate sample is determined
to not be a part of the reference’s cluster, and the input reads are instead assembled
and indexed as a new reference as previously described. However, if the query consensus
sequence are within 300 SNPs of the template, then the consensus sequence is indexed in
the isolate SQL database as a part of the template’s cluster.
It should be emphasized, that the 300 SNPs threshold does not deﬁne an actual outbreak,
and that not all isolates clustered under the same reference originate from the same source.
Instead, this should rather be considered a systematic storage system, which allows for
fast query searches.
By greatly limiting the number of references, and then selecting
a speciﬁc subset of related isolates based on the identiﬁed reference, sub-typing can be
performed at much greater speed than otherwise.
Distance Matrix and Phylogenetic Tree Estimation
After alignment, the consensus sequence is included in the cluster and a new distance
matrix is calculated using CCphylo [34]. In the distance matrix calculation, all positions
across all samples are compared against each other. Only positions present across all
samples are included in the in the SNP analysis, and if chosen by the user, options for
recombination SNP pruning and motif masking are possible. CCPhylo v0.2.2 identiﬁes
SNP diﬀerences between the isolates, and performs a subsequent hierarchical clustering
using the Neighbor-Joining algorithm [34]. Finally, the phylogenetic tree is visualized
with a dockerized version of Figtree (tag: biocontainers/ﬁgtree:v1.4.4-3-deb_cv1).
Database Update and Remote Synchronization
Throughout the entire PRTT pipeline several local database updates are performed.
These are quite simple to carry out and as long as no local IPC challenges arise the
process is straight forward. However, when the database is updated with remotely re-
trieved data, or when results from analyses performed locally are pushed to a remotely
hosted server, synchronization becomes more complicated.
Within the PRTT system exists two synchronization ﬁles; referenceSync.json and iso-
lateSync.json. All additions, deletions and modiﬁcations of isolates and references are
stored in these two ﬁles. Additionally, in each ﬁle, a timestamp is found containing the
epoch time since January 1, 1970. When the data in the synchronization ﬁles have been
sent to the remote server these ﬁles are wiped and the timestamp updated, thus ensuring
only new, local results are included in the next round of synchronization.
At the remote server at a speciﬁc point of time, such as midnight every day, the synchro-
nization takes place: N number of remote PRTT systems sends the local results to the
main server, and N isolateSync.json and N referenceSync.json ﬁles are compiled into two
large, concatenated ﬁles. Homology reduction of the two ﬁles is then carried out in order

to ensure that similar isolates sequenced at diﬀerent locations are not included multiple
times. The remaining, reduced data is then inserted into the database, and the ﬁnalized
database changes are sent to the remote PRTT systems for local update.

Analysis of Associated clusters
In case a medical professional in a clinical setting might want to perform a wider analysis of
several neighboring clusters, an association analysis function was developed. The reference
and consensus sequences of the associated clusters are aligned against the outbreak of
interest’s reference and subsequently included in the calculated distance matrix.
This calculation is slightly less precise compared to aligning the original sequencing reads
to the reference of interest, however it is several orders of magnitude faster. Rather than
performing new alignments of the neighboring clusters’ original reads, their consensus
sequences are aligned against the new reference. Additionally, all insigniﬁcant positions
are excluded from the distance matrix [30]. The associated analysis should only serve as
a quick estimation for discovery purposes, as it is not as precise as performing alignment
with the original query reads.

Preliminary Estimation of an Isolate’s clonal cluster
When sequencing depth with a MinION sequencer reaches the required threshold for
producing the preliminary estimation, the reference ﬁnding algorithm from the PRTT
pipeline is run. If a reference associated with a serious risk factor, such as multi antibiotic
resistance, is found, a warning is raised.
Otherwise, if either a non-dangerous or no
reference at all is identiﬁed no warning is raised and there should be no immediate reason
for concern.

2.2.4
Pruning and Motif masking
As described previously, pruning and motif masking are additional available options which
can be employed to analyze isolates. Recombination events may occur in an isolate which
could aﬀect the result of the phylogenetic analyses.
This eﬀect can be mitigated by
prune closely located SNPs and the genome segments between them [40]. Additionally,
speciﬁc motifs in some species have shown to result in incorrect basecalling for some
sequencing platforms [34], and thus to increase accuracy these positions could be masked,
thus excluding them from the distance matrix.
In this thesis neither motif masking or pruning was used in any analyses, as the optimal
pruning values and masking motifs varies between species. They where excluded in order
to give the best assessment of PRTT’s automatic analytical capability. Ideally, an index
should be made for all species in the reference database, and upon identiﬁcation of a
reference candidate a speciﬁc pruning value and masking motifs are chosen.

4.1.2
Outbreak of Carbapenemase Producing E. coli.
The CPO outbreak had previously been studied in Roer et al. [33]. In this study the
Northern Arizona SNP Pipeline (NASP) v1.0.0 was used to estimate the molecular epi-
demiology [47]. In NASP duplicate regions of a chosen reference are masked out using
MUMmer4 [48], the raw reads are then aligned using BWA-MEM [49] and ﬁnally the
SNPs are called using GATK.
Although no cluster analysis of the subset of 24 isolates were performed by Roer et al. [33]
the epidemiology was known to a certain extend. Twelve of the isolates originated from
patients with no connection to one another and of unique ST-types. The remaining 12
isolates were all of ST410, and 6 patients were known to have been infected in Denmark.
In the PRTT analysis the ST410 isolates divided into 3 clusters of sizes 9, 2 and 1.
NZ_CP024801.1, the reference identiﬁed for the largest cluster, originates from the same
2015 danish outbreak as 6 of the ST410 isolates, which a validation of the accuracy of the
reference ﬁnding algorithm.
All identiﬁed template candidates for the CPO datasets, no matter how closely related to
the query sequence, were identical for the Illumina-based analysis and the ONT MinION-
based analysis. However, the SNP distances to the reference did vary slightly between
the platforms (Table 3.2). For the majority of the isolates the deviation is less than 10

The overall structure of the phylogenetic tree generated in the association analysis closely

Taylor et al., but it was not the one used in the association analysis. Instead, because 7
clusters were identiﬁed, 7 diﬀerent reference templates were used.
The association analysis took 4 minutes 38 seconds to perform, compared to the original
analysis, in which the query reads are used, which for all 55 isolates took 3 hours 40
minutes and 21 seconds. This is a 48 fold run time improvement compared to performing

4.1.4
Outbreak of VRE
Since the VRE data did not originate from a published outbreak, this experiment should
only serve as a comparison between the results generated with short reads and long reads.
Contrary to the other 3 datasets, Enterococcus is a Gram-Positive organism, which might
have implications for the analytical performance of PRTT. The same ten isolates, that
were a part of the NZ_020488.1 cluster, identiﬁed the correct reference with either data
type, but the number of nucleotide diﬀerences varied between 1 to 90 SNPs. This is quite
a bit larger, than the diﬀerences observed between the two read types in the CPO data
set.
Additionally, both VRE3350 and VRE3280 identiﬁed a diﬀerent reference depending on
the read type. This same result was encountered in the CPO data set when queries with no
closely related reference sequences tried to estimate the best match. For the performance
of the PRTT system, the identiﬁcation of a slightly incorrect reference, which is very
distantly related, does not matter. In these cases the distance to the query consensus
sequence is much more than 300 bp, and so de novo assembly and reindexing as a new
reference happens regardless of the template candidate.
SNP Deviation Between Short- and Long Sequencing Reads
The higher deviation in SNPs between MinION and Illumina reads within the VRE data
set is noteworthy, because situations might occur in which reads from diﬀerent sequenc-
ing platforms are included in the same cluster. A possible explanation for this diﬀerence
is the base calling algorithms available for Nanopore sequencers. When a Nanopore se-
quencing is completed the output is a FAST5 ﬁle which does not contain the actual
nucleotide sequence. Instead it is processed by a basecalling algorithm, which is a neural
network trained to interpret the measured signal and convert it into a nucleotide sequence.
However, as are true for all neural networks, the performance of the network is highly
dependent on the data used to train it [54]. The majority of basecall training has been
conducted using E. coli data, thus creating unknown fall groups when applying the same
networks on other species’ genomes [16].
This problem is very solvable, and as time goes on more and more bacterial Nanopore
data from a wide array of diﬀerent species will be used as training sets. However, it

widely used, or if perhaps the usage of PRTT initially should be limited to speciﬁc species

4.2
PRTT Compared to Existing Surveillance Systems
There are several signiﬁcant diﬀerences between PRTT and existing surveillance systems.
As shown in Table 4.1 one of the factors which sets them apart is PRTT’s ability to process
long read sequencing data. Additionally, it is open-source and decentralized which is a big
must-have for real time typing. If multiple users can’t install the software and participate
in surveillance it heavily limits the eﬀectiveness of the system. This is one of the short-
comings observed with the NCBI Pathogen Detection pipeline and GenomeTrakr where
the query data has to be submitted to NCBI and analyzed server-side. Although the
speciﬁc analysis run time for NCBI’s Pathogen Detection Pipeline and Genometrakr is
unknown, it usually takes a couple of days from submission of query reads before a cluster
analysis is available [23].
It is diﬃcult to conduct a run time comparison of PRTT and the analyses published in
the corresponding articles of the datasets presented in this thesis. Naturally, it is possible
to measure the run times for each individual software tools used and sum them up, but
this would only represent the theoretically optimal run times. It does not account for
factors such as manual assessment of results, manual execution of software commands
and manual database updating. It is entirely possible, that the time saved by simply
automating everything in between isolate sequencing and clinical results represented the
most signiﬁcant run time upgrade that epidemiological analysis pipelines could receive.
An example of the impact of automation is seen when comparing the PRTT system to
SnapperDB which does not have automated template ﬁnding. Instead, it relies on the
user identifying a proper reference themselves. This might take a long time and the user
might unknowingly choose an incorrect or less optimal template.

The database system in PRTT is built to be scalable and to have greater diversity than
established screening systems.
This leads to faster query searches and more accurate
sub-typing of localized outbreaks, and by default naturally limits the number of isolates
included in each sub-cluster. This is contrary to the approach used by Evergreen which
has clusters that include tens of thousands of isolates. When clusters become this large it
leads to much slower analyses and ultimately diminishes the eﬀect of a real time diagnosis.

can quickly and precisely be merged together, if a wider epidemiological picture is re-

thus adding another dimension to the potential for wide scale implementation of the
surveillance system and inclusion of archive data.

The PRTT system showed great potential in processing both short- and long read se-
quencing data. As described in the introduction of this thesis, the system is primarily
designed with the intend to used MinION reads as input. This naturally raises the ques-
tions as to why only two of the four datasets chosen for bench marking included long
read sequencing data. The answer to this question is quite simple; there exists nearly no
published outbreak analyses based on long read sequencing data. This is not surprising
when considering that most/all of the current outbreak investigation pipelines only accept
short reads as input.
As more long read sequencing data becomes available in the future, the PRTT system’s

Chapter 5
Conclusions
Pathogen Real Time Typer (PRTT) represents a fully automated surveillance system that
rapidly can determine the clonal relationship of an isolate. It is able to process both short-
and long sequencing reads from most commercially available sequencing platforms, and
when using Oxford Nanopore Technologies’ MinION sequencer it can produce a prelimi-
nary estimation of an isolate’s clonal cluster during the sequencing process. The PRTT
system has been built to scale with large data quantities, as well as being operational by
multiple users in diﬀerent locations.
PRTT showed great promise in correctly clustering the isolates of four outbreak datasets
with both short- and long sequencing data. The only minor issue was in identifying the
same reference across both read types for remotely related query sequences, but since
these queries were to be assembled regardless of the template choice, it did not have any
impact on the epidemiological result.
When comparing PRTT to existing pathogen surveillance solutions it showed to be fast,
accurate and oﬀer additional features, which are crucial to the success of a real time
screening system, such as the ability to process long read sequencing data.
5.1
Future Work
Although PRTT has already shown great potential in rapidly identifying clonal clusters of
pathogens, there are still room for further development. The current two layer database
system is currently working ﬁne at small scale, but it is likely that eventually some cluster
could become problematically big if the system is rolled out at large scale. This problem
could be solved by introducing a third layer, thus storing the data in a "Reference >
Single Cluster > Multiple Outbreaks" scheme, rather than the current "Reference >
Single Cluster" scheme. Additionally, an automated species speciﬁc approach to motif
masking and pruning will need to be developed. Finally, proper benchmarking with long
sequencing reads should eventually be conducted when enough outbreak related isolates
are sequenced on those platforms.
