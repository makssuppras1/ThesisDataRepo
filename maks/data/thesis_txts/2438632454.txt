Introduction
Aurorae, or polar lights, is one of nature‚Äôs most spectacular phenomena. As
such many are interested in knowing the time and location to best experience
this naturally occurring light-show, or to conÔ¨Ārm a past observation. The
aurorae is also a visual indication of precipitating particles and movements
in the magnetosphere. As such it is of interest to both scientist and ordinary
people alike.
The objective of this study is to examine if a connection between the location
of aurorae and the magnetic Ô¨Āeld can be determined, and if the boundaries
of the auroral oval could be calculated based on magnetic Ô¨Āeld satellite ob-
servations alone.
To this eÔ¨Äect, the known auroral boundaries from the Defence Meteorological
Satellite Program (DMSP) was compared to the magnetic Ô¨Āeld observations
from the Challenging Minisatellite Payload mission. The two dataset was
searched to locate information on when the satellite orbits where at roughly
the same location at the same time.
These corresponding, or matched orbits, was used to examine potential pat-
terns in the magnetic Ô¨Āeld data in relation to the auroral boundaries. Two
proxy variables was made in order to recreate the limits of the auroral oval
from the magnetic data alone. Their correlation with the known boundaries
was examined to Ô¨Ānd the precision and any statistical errors.
The proxy variables was then be used to create a statistic model for the
auroral oval depending on magnetic activity, calculated from a solarwind-
magnetosphere coupling function.

Background
This study is not an extensive examination of physical parameters or coupling
functions but rather a statistical approach of determining the aurorae using
only magnetic data. As such a detailed theory section is not included but
rather a brief, simpliÔ¨Āed, presentation of the most important physical aspects

3.1
The solar-terrestrial environment
The term solar-terrestrial environment encompasses all the regions where the
interactions between the Sun, galactic cosmic rays, and the Earth‚Äôs magnetic
Ô¨Āeld takes place. SpeciÔ¨Ācally this refers to the magnetosphere, the ionosphere
and the upper atmosphere of the Earth.
The Sun‚Äôs inÔ¨āuence is dictated by the physical conditions of the Sun‚Äôs core,
atmosphere and the solar wind. The interactions between the solar wind
and the magnetosphere governs the coupling processes taking place in the
ionosphere, and the parameters of the resulting aurorae.
3.1.1
The Sun‚Äôs inÔ¨āuence
The Sun emits a contentious stream of energy and charged particles into the
surrounding area, called the solar wind, which originates from the outer part
of the Sun‚Äôs atmosphere, the corona. The Sun‚Äôs corona is so hot that the
Sun‚Äôs gravity cannot prevent matter from evaporating into the interplanetary
medium. This stream of charged particles, primarily electrons, protons and
alpha particles, is the dominant force out to the border of the heliosphere.
Embedded in the escaping plasma is the solar magnetic Ô¨Āeld, which becomes
the interplanetary magnetic Ô¨Āeld (IMF) as it is carried out into space. At a
distance of about 2 solar radii the solar magnetic Ô¨Āeld is reduced to a radially
direct structure frozen into the solar wind plasma.
The sun has a rotation period of 27 days, and this spin causes the solar wind
magnetic Ô¨Āeld to take the shape of an Archimedean spiral, named the Parker
spiral, and often is depicted as or likened to a ballerina skirt. Variations in
solar activity are transmitted trough the solar wind, both the small recur-
ring variation from the 27 days rotation period to the sporadic disturbances
caused by events such as Ô¨āares, coronal mass ejections and shock waves. The
frequency of these events can be linked to the solar activity cycle, a periodic
cycle of approximately 11 year. [2]

3.1.2
The magnetosphere
The Earth is shielded from the solar wind and other cosmic rays by the
magnetosphere. It is powered by the planet‚Äôs magnetic Ô¨Āeld and acts as a
protective bubble, deÔ¨āecting the stream of charged particles emitted from
the Sun. If Earth‚Äôs magnetic Ô¨Āeld is assumed to be a dipole, and no outside
forces were present, then the magnetosphere would resemble a sphere around
the planet.
As the solar wind impinges on the magnetosphere, the day-side becomes com-
pressed due to the pressure, and the night-side elongated into a tear-shaped
tail. Most of the solar wind is deÔ¨āected but some of the charged particles
penetrate the protection via a process called magnetic reconnection. Upon
reaching the magnetosphere the supersonic solar wind is abruptly decelerated
creating a Bow Shock region. An illustration of the magnetosphere and the
magnetic Ô¨Āeld lines can be seen in Figure 4.

3.1.3
The ionosphere
The ionosphere is the in-between or transition layer between the magne-
tosphere‚Äôs fully ionized plasma and the more neutral plasma of the lower
atmosphere, as such the ionosphere itself consists of partially ionised plasma.
Along with the charged particles from the magnetosphere, the ionosphere is
also where solar radiation, in the ultraviolet and extreme ultraviolet wave-
lengths cause photoionization.
The dynamics of the ionosphere, and the magnetosphere, is dominated by
various current systems. The Birkeland currents, also called Ô¨Āeld aligned
currents (FAC), is a set of currents that follow the magnetic Ô¨Āeld lines. In a
quiet periode the currents occurs as two pairs of current sheets, each with a
upward and a downward Ô¨āowing current - an upward current sheet contains
downward Ô¨āowing electrons. An illustration can be seen Figure 2.
The current sheets pairs stretches from noon towards midnight, one through
the dawn sector and one through the dusk sector - see section 4.0.1 for an
illustration of the magnetic coordinates. Of the pairs, the current sheet clos-
est to the pole is referred to as Region 1 current sheet and the second at a
lower latitude as the Region 2 current sheet. At the boundary between the
two regions, for both pairs, Ô¨āows the Polar Electrojet (PEJ) in the direction
perpendicular to the local magnetic Ô¨Āeld.
In the case of high geomagnetic activity, such as under storm conditions, the
magnetic Ô¨Āeld becomes more erratic. As such several current sheets might
be present, with less clearly deÔ¨Āned placements, though they always appear
in pairs and with an accompanying electrojet between them. [3, Ch. 8] [4]

3.1.4
The Aurorae
The aurorae are a naturally occurring phenomena, primarily observable in
the polar regions - referred to as Aurora Borealis and Aurora Australis in the
Northern and Southern hemisphere, respectively. The aurorae are visible to
the naked eye, but easiest to observe at night-time.
Aurorae occur when charged particles, accelerated by the processes in the
magnetosphere, travel down the Ô¨Āeld aligned currents and precipitate into
the planet‚Äôs upper atmosphere. There they excite the neutral atoms and
molecules, causing them to emit electromagnetic line radiation, in the range
from ultraviolet to infrared. The colour produced depends on the atomic
number of the element that is exited, the most commonly seen green aurorae
is caused by energetic electrons interacting with atomic oxygen.
The auroral oval refer to the region where aurorae is most likely to be ob-

the edges propagate towards lower latitudes, making it possible to observe
aurorae in regions well below the poles. The shape will change to an increas-

The discrete aurorae are the most distinctive auroral arcs with their curtain-

motion of lines as the the auroral arcs shifts and fractures due to small-scale
changes in the magnetic Ô¨Āeld. An arc will consist of multiple vertical rays that

3.3.1
The Defence Meteorological Satellite program
The Defence Meteorological Satellite Program (DMSP) is developed by the
United States Department of Defence and tasked with long-term monitoring
of meteorological, oceanographic, and solar-terrestrial processes. SpeciÔ¨Ācally
designed to collect and disseminate worldwide cloud cover data on a daily
basis to aid the US military in planning operations at sea, on land and in
the air, the program was started in the 1960s and saw the Ô¨Ārst launch of the
F-1 satellite, in 1971.
The program depends on several satellites for information at any given time,
with the satellites in LEO polar orbit with an altitude of about 830 km and
an orbital time of 101 minute, giving global coverage twice a day.
From 1974, with the launch of F-6, the six satellite constellation was achieved,
always with two designated as primary, and the satellites readily replaced
when exceeding their 3-5 year life time. This coverage scheme was maintained
until 2016, with the failure of the F-19 and the destruction of the yet-launched
F-20 due to storagecosts. As such the next DMSP satellite isn‚Äôt scheduled
until 2023, which exceeds the expected lifetime of the current satellites. No
data is available for download after the beginning of 2016. [7], [8], [9]

The Challenging Minisatellite Payload (CHAMP) satellite mission was launched

The objective of the matchingOrbit-function is to Ô¨Ānd orbits where the
CHAMP and a DMSP satellite run parallel or cross each other over a pole
area at approximately the same time, in order to compare the data from the
two dataset. The process is divided into two parts, Ô¨Ārst a rough sorting to
Ô¨Ānd potential matches followed by a more comprehensive comparison of the
two dataset. The MATLAB code for both sorting processes can be found in
Appendix C.1 and C.2.
5.1.1
Matching Orbits, Ô¨Ārst sorting
The Ô¨Ārst sorting is divided into years, because the DMSP matrices are gener-
ated that way, but also because the amount of data to be processed is quite
large, as such it was easier to monitor if the code was working correctly by
dividing it into smaller runs.
The script cross-references each CHAMP orbit with each DMSP passage,
with the Ô¨Ārst threshold being the time; a comparison between the Ô¨Ārst times-
tamp in the CHAMP orbit with the time given for the DMSP end-point. If
the diÔ¨Äerence is greater than 100 minutes then the algorithm proceeds to the
next iteration, as a CHAMP orbit takes about 1.5 hours. This is primarily
used to weed out all the DMSP passages that isn‚Äôt on the same day as the
CHAMP orbit.
If a DMSP passage is in time window that is the CHAMP orbit, then the
program will examine if any of the 10 DMSP electron and ion boundaries
have a corresponding CHAMP observation, that fulÔ¨Āls the following three
requirements:

‚ÄĘ DiÔ¨Äerence in MLT is less than 1 hour.
‚ÄĘ DiÔ¨Äerence in UT is less than 30 minutes.
If any DMSP boundary fulÔ¨Āls these thresholds, the orbit information is stored
along with the pole distribution and the diÔ¨Äerence in Mlat, MLT and UT

5.1.2

that this sorting process considers each DMSP separately and makes a Ô¨Ānal
decision depending on the number of boundary points that passes the thresh-

The script gathers the results from the Ô¨Ārst sorting, comparing each of
the boundary points, speciÔ¨Āed in Section 4.1, individually to the matched
CHAMP orbit and only saves the result if four or more boundary points ful-
Ô¨Āl the requirements. This is to minimize the number of matches where the
two orbits are perpendicular, and instead favouring those that are somewhat

5.2
Calculating the oval using CHAMP
Using the patterns garnered by comparing the DMSP and CHAMP matched
data, two proxy variables are created to estimate the beginning and end of
the auroral oval using the magnetic data. An estimate of the polar electrojet
location and intensity is also included.
5.2.1
The beg-end function
The beg-end function, however strangely named, does as the title suggests
and determines the beginning and end of the magnetic Ô¨āuctuations. The
desire was to create a simple but robust estimate, independent of intensity.
The end result being a function who‚Äôs input is an orbit vector Ô¨Āle and re-
turns four sets of start and end limits - henceforth referred to as beg and
end, respectively.
The CHAMP orbit is Ô¨Ārst separated into four regions, deÔ¨Āned from 45 de-
grees Mlat to the most poleward observation, and from that observation to 45
degrees Mlat - and similarly for the South Pole. Due to the way the orbit Ô¨Āles
are constructed, the sequence of regions is always: from equator approaching
the North Pole, from the North Pole towards equator, from equator towards
the South Pole and from the South Pole approaching equator again. During
the work the polar regions have been denoted PN1, PN2, PS1 and PS2, for
easy identiÔ¨Ācation.
A set of running means were created and compared with the gradient of the
observations of the external BŌÜ Ô¨Āeld, seen in Figure 15, with the horizontal
line being the mean calculated in each scenario. It was found that the 30
second running mean was the best candidate, with its mean as a suitable
threshold to determine the limit of the magnetic Ô¨āuctuations.
Using the
mean should help to ensure that the proxy variable takes the Ô¨Āeld intensity
into account.

The function that determines the limits works in a similar manner as a run-

considered to Ô¨Ānd the locations where the mean-threshold is crossed. In or-

to be above/below of the threshold and two or less above/below after the
point. The distinction between a beginning and end point is determined by
the direction the threshold is being crossed and the knowledge of which re-
gion is being considered.
In most cases this results in multiple candidates for both the beg and end
limits, as can be seen in Figure 16.
Because of the construction with a
window-search, a threshold crossing often returns two adjacent candidates
for the same passing, where one is removed. The way this is done is that
the script checks if two candidates are with one or two indices of each other
and removes the second one if that is the case. This introduces a small er-
ror, as it is not necessarily the one furthest from the threshold that is being
removed, but the Mlat diÔ¨Äerence between two observations is approximately
0.06 degrees and as such it was considered a negligible error.

5.4
Method summary and discussion
The sorting processes Ô¨Ānd orbits where the CHAMP and DMSP satellites
are overlapping in time and location, in order to Ô¨Ānd comparable data. De-
spite requirements for the number of DMSP boundary points that have to
match, some cases where the two orbits are anti-parallel still occurs, when
the DMSP boundaries are located closely together. The second sorting pro-
cess‚Äôs thresholds were determined with this in mind, in order to minimise the
number of matches of that constellation.
It should be noted that the Ô¨Ārst sorting process includes the DMSP ion
boundaries in its search, and excludes the b0, and the second sorting process
could easily be expanded to include the ion data, if it was desirable.
Two proxy variables to characterize the limits of the auroral oval was made
and found to correlate with the DMSP boundaries b1e and b5e. The proxies
are determined by the same method utilizing a 30 second running mean of
the magnetic Ô¨Āeld and uses the mean of the same to calculate the limits. The
equatorwards boundary was found to recreate the DMSP boundary pretty
well, with a statistical error of 0.69 and -0.05 degrees, for the North and
South Pole respectively. The polewards boundary performed worse, despite
attempts to optimize the function, and it shows a statistical error of -3.24
and -3.83 for the North and South Pole respectively.
