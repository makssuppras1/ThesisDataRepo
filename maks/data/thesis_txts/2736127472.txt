Abstract
Streamers are thin ionized plasma channels that form the early
stages of lightning discharges. Both in nature and in industry appliВӯ
cations, multiple streamers are initiated simultaneously and interВӯ
act with each other. The interaction of two or more streamers plays
an essential role in the progression of lightning, as it is through the
collision and consecutive merge that the lightning discharge moves
through space. Additionally, multiple studies suggested that elecВӯ
trons are accelerated by the impulsive, enhanced field generated
in the gap between two streamers soon to collapse. In turn, these
electrons produce bremsstrahlung radiation. These two cases exВӯ
emplify the importance of understanding the process of streamer
encounters. For this project, an inВӯhouse fluid model, a wellВӯknown
technique to study streamersвҖҷ physics, is developed using AMReX
framework. AMReX provides multiple functionalities for numeriВӯ
cal simulations, that were used as key components in the model.
The model solves the set of conservation equations that govern
the change of the density function and makes use of AMReX tools
to solve the Poisson equation to obtain the Electric field. The phoВӯ
toionization term it is also considered in the model, providing more
accurate results. The model was benchmarked successfully with
external studies to test its capabilities in simulating known cases.
The model is used to study streamer encounters, for cases when
streamers move towards each other in the same axis, but also for
cases where they still move towards each other in parallel axis.
Cases with different distances between the parallel axis of the traВӯ
jectory are presented, showing examples where the mutual attracВӯ
tion between the streamers is in effect and others where the beВӯ
havior of the streamer mimics the behavior of single streamers.

Introduction
1.1
Lightning and streamers
Lightning has been present on Earth since before humans set foot
on the planet.
At any point in time around 2000 thunderstorms
are roaming the EarthвҖҷs atmosphere striking the Earth with lightВӯ
ning discharges about 100 times per second. Hence, lightning has
had some influence on human history: from the idea that lightning
flashes may have caused the first chemical reactions that led to
the basic building blocks of life in primordial EarthвҖҷs atmosphere
[1]Нҫ the access of humanity to fire, the presence in old mythology,
old and modern literature, and modern technology and engineering
sciences [2].
The lightning flash as it is known involves a multitude of steps,
which even now are not completely understood. One of the early
stages of lightning is the streamer discharge, which is the focus of
this work.
1.2
What are streamers?
Streamers are rapidly growing channels of weakly ionized plasma
that penetrate in a previously nonВӯionized region under the action
of an electric field [3, 4]. Streamers start as a single or a group
of electrons in motion, which is enhanced due to the presence of
an external electric field. In some cases after multiple collisions
with neutral molecules and atoms in the medium, the number of
free electrons undergoes an avalanche multiplication and becomes
what is known as an electron avalanche. If the motion continues
the electrons charge, space charge, will generate an additional
electric field, and if this electric field becomes comparable to the
external field a streamer emerges.[1, 5, 6]

Inside the streamer channel, the electric field is largely screened
by the space charge especially at the tip of the streamer, known as
the streamer head, which consists of a positive or negative curved
layer of space charge. At the same moment, the head enhances
the external field in the area in front of the head, producing addiВӯ
tional ionization that propagates the streamer forward through the
impact of accelerated electrons onto neutral atoms and molecules.
[4, 7]
Streamers can be classified as positive and negative depending
on the direction of their motion. Negative streamers propagate in
the same direction as the electron drift, while positive streamers
propagate against it. [8] Streamers evolve in timescales insufficient
for the background gas to be significantly heated. Even with the
increase of electron energy due to ionization and kinetic collisions
the temperature of the ions and neutral species remains almost
unchanged, making a streamer a plasma that is far out of thermal
equilibrium.[9]
Streamer discharges occur in nature and have varied industrial apВӯ
plications. Being a key step in lightning and related phenomena,
occurring in the limited volumes of plasma technological devices,
appearing in corona reactors, and have applications in the treatВӯ
ment of polluted gases and water in highВӯvoltage technology. [1, 6,
10] Additionally, as wind turbines increase in height (> 200)m they
are exposed to strong local electric fields under thundercloudsНҫ preВӯ
senting favorable conditions for the initiation of streamers which
will normally evolve into what is known as upward lightning. The
damage produced by lightning exposure is becoming one of the
largest drivers of downtime and repair costs. Additionally, it can be
dangerous to personnel working onВӯsite at the wind farms.[11, 12,
13] Hence, having a good understanding of the physical process
involved in the formation and evolution of streamers is of great imВӯ
portance in research and industry. But while the various stages of
electrical breakdown are well known, streamers are difficult to inВӯ

Numerical simulations have become an important tool to study these
complex phenomena. Two types of numerical models can be used
to describe the motion of electrons as well as the inception and
propagation of streamers: particle and fluid models.
In particle models, the main idea is to follow the dynamics of single
electrons through the formation of avalanches and streamers. DurВӯ
ing the free flight of the electron, collisions with neutral atoms and
molecules occurНҫ therefore particle models focus on the dynamВӯ
ics of the electrons and the collisions. The collisions depend on
the crossВӯsection, which indicates how probable is the collision to
take place and can be: i) Calculated in laboratories, ii) determined
through ab initio quantum theoretical calculations, or iii) through the
inversion of swarm experimental data [4]. Once these values are
obtained, the probability of the collision can be calculated in each
time step of the simulation. Due to this process, particle models
can follow the full physics of the streamer, giving a more complete
description. The main problem with these models is that such high
detail is too computationally expensive to do on a large scale.
Fluid models are computationally less expensive than their counВӯ
terparts making them attractive despite their approximate nature.
The focus of this kind of model is to approximate the particle distriВӯ
bution by density functions for electrons and ions. [4, 17] A set of
conservation equations that govern the change of the density funcВӯ
tion is derived from the Boltzmann equation. The density functions
are then used to obtain the electric field. Fluid models are less
affected by the constrain of computational power, hence it is posВӯ
sible to have a description of streamers longer in time than those

1.3
Motivation and structure of the thesis
Both in nature and industry applications, multiple streamers are iniВӯ
tiated simultaneously and interact with each other electrostatically,
so the study of this interaction achieves a better understanding of
the discharge characteristics observed in the real world than studyВӯ
ing isolated streamers. Streamer collisions are responsible for the
progression of lightning, where streamers that emanate from the
hot conducting lightning leader channel collide with streamers of
opposite polarity that emerge from bright and locally warmer segВӯ
ments that appear ahead of a discharge channel known as space
stem.[1, 18, 19] Additionally, streamersвҖҷ encounters could be conВӯ
nected with the emission of XВӯrays observed in lightning and elecВӯ
tric discharges. Cooray et al. (2009) [20] proposed that the high
electric fields produced due to compression of the potential difВӯ
ference in the short distance between two colliding streamer tips
exceed the soВӯcalled thermal runaway threshold (вүҲ260kV/cm at
atmospheric pressure). Hence, the study of streamer encounters
offers the possibility of shedding some light on different and interВӯ
esting types of phenomena that happen around the world.
The method proposed for this project is the development of a fluid
model, that in the future will complement a DTU inВӯhouse particle
model, to study how the distance between the sources of streamers
affects the interaction. As far as the author knows, the studies
of streamer encounters have been done only for cases where the
streamers move toward each other in a straight line. Hence the
interest in studying cases where this is not true.

Streamer Theory
The streamer discharge starts when an electron gains energy due
to the presence of an electric field, and as it is accelerated it interВӯ
acts with the atoms and molecules in the surroundings producing
more electrons, which in turn are also accelerated. [1, 5] After
some conditions are met, section.Вӯ2.3, this avalanche of electrons
evolves into what is known as a streamer discharge. To truly underВӯ
stand the evolution of single electrons into a streamer discharge,
the reader needs to have basic concepts from electron motion in
gases. Once the fundamentals are understood, a description of
streamersвҖҷ formation can be comprehended.

5
Conclusions and outlook
The purpose of this project is to present the application of the fluid
model, built during the projectвҖҷs duration, in the study of streamВӯ
ersвҖҷ encounters. Streamers, rapidly growing channels of weakly
ionized plasma that penetrate a previously background gas under
the action of an electric field, can be found frequently in nature and
industrial applications (Section.Вӯ1.2) They start as single electrons
that are accelerated by a background electric field, through interВӯ
actions with the molecules and atoms of the medium the number of
electrons grows generating an electron avalanche. The avalanche
will evolve into streamers once the RaetherMeek criterion, is fulВӯ
filled (Section.Вӯ2.2 and Section.Вӯ2.3).
In reality, electric discharges consist of multiple streamers, e.g.
lightning propagation, hence the need to understand the interaction
between two or more of them. Computational models have proven
useful when studying streamer physics, the fluid model being one
of the most used in the scientific community. In the fluid model, the
particle distribution is approximated by density functions, which in
turn are used to calculate the electric field. The greatest advantage
of this model is the low computational cost. (Section.Вӯ3.2) The fluid
model proposed in this project was built using the AMReX frameВӯ
work, a software framework containing functionalities that facilitate
the parallelization and the use of GPU for a plethora of tasks. AddiВӯ
tionally, AMReX provides numerical solvers for wellВӯknown equaВӯ
tions, e.g. the Poisson equation, and a direct way to implement
boundary conditions. (Subsection.Вӯ3.2.3)
The fluid model was built to include photoionization, which is an adВӯ
ditional source of positive ions. Photoionization represents the posВӯ
itive ions produced by UV or Bremsstrahlung photons (Section.Вӯ
2.4, Subsection.Вӯ3.1.1)
